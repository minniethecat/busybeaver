## Role in the Narrative

Lefebre Desnouettes appears in the narrative through retrospective reference: he commanded the French assault on Saragossa during the first [[siege-warfare|siege]] (June–August 1808). His tactical approach — attacking through the [[portillo|Portillo]] gate and [[castle-aljaferia|Castle Aljafería]] — is discussed by the defenders as a strategic error that [[napoleon|Napoleon]] himself later criticized. His failed assault serves as narrative contrast to the second [[siege-warfare|siege]], where the French, learning from his mistakes, would attack through [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] instead.

> "Do you know that Napoleon in Paris, knowing the resistance shown by this city in the first siege, was furious with Lefebre Desnouettes because he assaulted the plaza by the Portillo and the Castle Aljafería? He called for a plan of Saragossa, and they gave it to him, and he showed that the city should be attacked by Santa Engracia." (010-004)

## Biography

Lefebre Desnouettes (1773–1822) was a French cavalry general who served in the napoleonic Wars. During the Peninsular War, he was tasked with taking Saragossa in the summer of 1808. His assault focused on the western approaches to the city — the [[portillo|Portillo]] gate and the [[castle-aljaferia|Castle Aljafería]] — but the Spanish defenders, led by [[palafox|Palafox]], repelled the attack in what became a celebrated victory of Spanish resistance.

The failure of the first [[siege-warfare|siege]] became a matter of strategic analysis. When [[napoleon|Napoleon]] reviewed the campaign from Paris, he reportedly examined a map of Saragossa and determined that the correct approach was to attack through [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] — the strategy that [[marshal-moncey|Marshal Moncey]] and later [[marshal-lannes|Marshal Lannes]] would employ with devastating effect during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]]. Desnouettes's experience illustrates the learning curve the French army faced in confronting urban guerrilla-style [[resistance|defense]].

## Historical Context

As a historical figure, Lefebre Desnouettes represents the broader context of the Peninsular War (1808–1814), in which [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s Grande Armée, despite its reputation for battlefield supremacy, encountered fierce [[resistance|popular resistance]] in Spanish cities. Saragossa became an emblem of this resistance. The contrast between Desnouettes's failed approach and the successful second [[siege-warfare|siege]] underscores the brutal learning process of [[siege-warfare|siege]] warfare against a determined civilian population.
