# Convent of San Francisco

The **Convent of San Francisco** was one of the most important military positions in Saragossa during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]]. Its location on the [[coso|Coso]] and its formidable structure made it the linchpin of the Spanish defensive line during the siege's final phase.

## Strategic Importance

The possession of San Francisco was widely understood to determine the outcome of the siege:

> "The possession of San Francisco would decide the fate of the city. That vast edifice, situated in the middle of the Coso, gave an incontestable superiority to the side which occupied it." (028-001)

The French, established in the [[convent-of-jerusalem|Convent of Jerusalem]] opposite, recognized that they could not take the convent by direct assault. They instead resorted to extensive [[french-mine-warfare|mining operations]].

## The Cloister and Surroundings

The convent complex extended beyond the main edifice. Its cloister served as a rallying point where defenders regrouped between sorties; Gabriel and his companions were "stationed again in the cloister of San Francisco" at a critical juncture (027-008). The building's position — "close by the Convent of San Francisco, and opposite the college of San Diego" (027-024) — placed it at the center of a network of fortified religious buildings whose possession determined control of the Coso.

Behind the convent lay the garden of [[san-diego|San Diego]], and defenders fortified positions "back of San Francisco, next to the garden of San Diego" (026-048). The French had occupied the buildings farther down the Coso, including the Convent of Jerusalem directly opposite, creating a close-range artillery and mining duel across the width of the city's main thoroughfare (025-002). The street fighting that engulfed the convent began in the quarters near the hospital and the Convent of San Francisco, where citizens including Don Santiago Sas and Don Vicente Marraco "fearlessly attacked the French" (002-025).

## Underground Warfare

Beneath the convent lay a network of vast wine-cellars that became the setting for underground combat. Both sides dug galleries through the earth, attempting to place explosives beneath each other's positions:

> "The Convent of San Francisco had vast subterranean wine-cellars under its choir. The edifices which the French occupied farther down had these also, and it was unusual for a house not to have a deep cellar." (026-007)

Spanish engineers worked frantically to counter French mining from the direction of [[san-diego|San Diego]]. Despite their efforts to detect and neutralize enemy tunnels, the convent was ultimately destroyed by a massive explosion. Gabriel described descending into these subterranean passages — "I went to the San Francisco, and going down into the cellars…" (027-028) — where the underground war against French sappers continued day and night.

## The Explosion and Aftermath

The mining culminated in the detonation of a large section of the convent, killing many defenders. The destruction marked a turning point in the siege's final hours. [[gabriel-araceli]] and [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] rushed to the scene, finding the Coso filled with debris and the fate of the city sealed.
