# Santiago Sas

## Overview

Don Santiago Sas was a parish priest of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]] and one of the most prominent warrior-clergymen of Saragossa. In Galdós's novel, he commands a company of fusileers drawn from his own parishioners, embodying the fusion of religious [[faith|devotion]] and military resistance that characterized the Spanish defence against [[napoleon|Napoleon]].

## Role in the Narrative

Santiago Sas appears as a figure of both martial and moral authority. His company of fusileers, drawn exclusively from the residents of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]] parish, represented the neighborhood-based organization of the defence. [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], one of the novel's principal characters, served as captain in Sas's company (003-006).

The company held to a strict local identity — [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] notes that [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] could not serve under Sas because the battalion accepted only residents of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]] parish (004-012).

Sas's reputation as a fighter was widely recognized throughout Saragossa. A friar later lists him among the great warrior-churchmen:

> "Here we have great warriors — Don Santiago Sas, Don Manuel Lasartesa; the incumbent of San Pablo, Don Antonio La Casa; the parish priest of San Miguel, Don José Martínez; and also Don Vicente Casanova, who is famous as the first theologian of Saragossa." (012-008)

## Key Relationships

Santiago Sas led the fusileers of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]] parish, with [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] serving as a captain in his company. He fought alongside other prominent defenders including [[mariano-cereso|Don Mariano Cereso]] and Father Basilio [[father-boggiero|Boggiero]]. His company's exclusivity to [[san-pablo|San Pablo]] parishioners created a tight-knit fighting unit bound by neighborhood loyalties.

## Historical Context

Santiago Sas was a historical figure who genuinely fought in the defence of Saragossa. Like many Spanish clergy during the Peninsular War, he took up arms against the French, representing the deep entanglement of Church and resistance in Spanish society. The novel's portrayal is consistent with the historical record: Sas was one of several priests who led armed parishioners during [[siege-warfare|the siege]].

## Death

Sas met a treacheroismus end after the city's fall. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] learns of his fate in the novel's closing pages:

> "They were Santiago Sas, commander of those brave musketeers of the parish of San Pablo, and Father Basilio Boggiero, teacher, friend, and counsellor of Palafox. They say that they went and called up Father Basilio at midnight, pretending that they wished to entrust an important commission to him; and then they took him on their treacherous bayonets to the bridge, where they pierced him through, and flung him into the river. And they did the same with Sas." (031-026)

This account — the midnight summons, the pretext of a commission, the bayoneting on the bridge, and the disposal of the bodies in the Ebro — is presented as testimony of French perfidy, and it contributed to the enduring memory of both Sas and [[father-boggiero|Boggiero]] as martyrs of Saragossa.
