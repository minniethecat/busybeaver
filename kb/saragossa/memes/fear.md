# Fear

## Overview

Fear in *Saragossa* is treated with remarkable psychological honesty. Unlike conventional war narratives that suppress or sanitize fear, Galdós allows his characters—including [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]]—to admit terror openly. Fear is not portrayed as the opposite of couragey but as its precondition: couragey is meaningful precisely because fear must be overcome.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The most direct exploration of fear comes from [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]], who contrasts his earlier fearlessness with his present state:

> "Frankly, I do not wish to die in this terrible circle which the French have drawn about us. In the other siege, however, all the students of the Seminary took arms, and I confess that I was more valiant then than now. A peculiar zeal filled my blood, and I threw myself into places of greatest danger without fear of death. Today does not find me the same. I am timid and afraid, and when a gun goes off, it makes me tremble." (005-007)

This confession—that a [[heroism|hero]] of the first [[siege-warfare|siege]] is now afraid—is a striking departure from heroismc convention and grounds the novel in psychological realism.

## Key Passages

Fear also operates at the social level. [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] dismisses [[epidemic|the epidemic]] as a manifestation of fear rather than a medical condition:

> "I don't know what to think of this thing which the doctors call the epidemic. I call it fear, sirs, pure fear. They take a chill; then they have spasms and a fever; then they turn green, and they die. What is all that but the effect of fear?" (014-024)

The narrator's own experience during the counter-assault reveals how fear and couragey can coexist in the same moment:

> I do not know why we were cowards, nor why we were brave a few moments later. (010-024)

## Historical Context

The recognition of combat psychology—what would later be called "shell shock" or PTSD—was rare in 19th-century literature. Galdós's willingness to depict fear honestly, including in [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]]-[[heroism|hero]], anticipates modern psychological realism in war fiction.
