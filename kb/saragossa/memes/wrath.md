# Wrath

## Overview

**Wrath** in the novel is distinguished from blind [[fury]] by its moral dimension — it is anger that believes itself justified. Where fury is impersonal and directionless, [[fury|wrath]] has a target and a grievance. In the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]], [[fury|wrath]] is primarily directed at [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]], [[don-jeronimo-candiola|the miser]] whose hoarded grain represents everything the starving citizens despise: profit extracted from collective suffering.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Wrath first erupts in the confrontation between [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] and [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]. The wealthy citizen arrives to demand grain at a fair price, but the encounter quickly escalates:

> He came face to face with Montoria, his ugly eyes glowing with wrath. (012-034)

The scene escalates as [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]]'s righteous anger gives way to physical violence:

> He was already choking with wrath, lost his stirrups, as the saying is, and became incapable of reason. (012-054)

[[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s response — stammering defiance — only intensifies [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]]'s [[fury|rage]]:

> The miser, stammering thickly in his wrath, could barely form words of protest. (012-057)

The distinction between [[fury|wrath]] and fury matters precisely because [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]] believes his anger is justified: [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] is hoarding food while the city starves. But the novel refuses to endorse this self-justification. [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]]'s "righteous" [[fury|wrath]] leads to the same brutality as blind fury — he beats an old man senseless. The concept of [[fury|wrath]] thus functions as a critique: the moral high ground does not sanctify violence.

The populace's collective [[fury|wrath]] is described separately, as an impersonal force:

> The wrath of the populace was not to be reasoned with. (020-003)

This mob [[fury|wrath]], directed at perceived traitors and profiteers, mirrors [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]]'s individual [[fury|rage]]: both believe themselves righteous, and both become indiscriminate once unleashed.
