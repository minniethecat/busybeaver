## Role in the Narrative

General Butron appears as one of the key Spanish commanders during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] of Saragossa. He led one arm of the major sortie on December 31 — a coordinated two-pronged counterattack against the French [[siege-warfare|siege]] works, sallying from the Bernardona gate with infantry and cavalry through the gates of Sancho and [[portillo|Portillo]]. Butron's force operated in tandem with [[renovales|Renovales]]'s column, which attacked the French center from Torrero to the Muela road.

> "The morning of the thirty-first arrived, and my battalion was charged to be ready to march upon orders from Renovales to attack the enemy in their centre, from the Torrero to the Muela road, while General Butron did the same by the Bernardona, that is to say, by the French left, sallying with sufficient forces of infantry and cavalry by the gates of Sancho and Portillo." (008-009)

The sortie was a tactical success: both columns inflicted substantial damage, destroyed entrenchments, captured tools and prisoners, and withdrew before French reinforcements could arrive. Butron's column, like [[renovales|Renovales]]'s, had its deswrath — the defenders struck hard and retwrathd in good order.

> "We who were with Renovales had had our desire, the same as those with Butron, and were not obliged to wait for those horsemen who arrived at the end of the action." (008-015)

## Biography

Butron's most notable contribution was the reconnaissance aspect of the December 31 sortie. He took official engineers with him on the daring venture, and they had time to examine the French [[siege-warfare|siege]] works, measure them, and deliver detailed descriptions to the commanding general upon their return. This intelligence-gathering made the sortie more than a morale-boosting raid — it provided actionable information about the enemy's progress and dispositions.

> "The official engineers that Butron took with him on that daring venture had had time to examine the works of the besiegers, and measure them, and could give descriptions of them to the commanding general." (008-020)

In the latter phase of [[siege-warfare|the siege]], Butron is named as one of the four principal advisors to [[palafox|Palafox]] — alongside Father Basilio [[father-boggiero|Boggiero]], General O'Neill, and [[general-saint-march|General Saint March]] — who effectively governed the [[resistance|defense]]. The narrator observes that while [[palafox|Palafox]] reigned as a symbol, these four men, including Butron, did the practical work of command.

> "Father Basilio governed, with O'Neill, Saint March, and Butron, the first, an ecclesiastic, the other three noted generals." (023-018)

## Historical Context

Butron's role reflects the nature of the Spanish high command during the Peninsular War: a mix of professional officers and charismatic figures coordinating an improvised but determined [[resistance|defense]]. The December 31 sortie was one of the most significant offensive actions taken by the defenders during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]], demonstrating that the besiege-warfared garrison retained both fighting spirit and tactical initiative even as the French tightened their encirclement.
