# Hunger

## Overview

**Hunger** is one of the dominant physical and thematic forces in the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]]. More than a mere condition of deprivation, hunger functions as an active antagonist — a weapon wielded by the besieging French and a crucible that tests the moral character of every inhabitant. Throughout the novel, the struggle for bread reveals heroism, selfishness, and the raw calculus of survival in a city under [[siege-warfare|siege]].

## Historical Context

Famine was a deliberate component of [[siege-warfare|siege]] warfare in the Napoleonic era. At the historical [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]], [[french-army|French forces]] encircled the city to prevent food convoys from entering. Contemporary accounts describe civilians reduced to eating cats, dogs, and leather. Galdós's fictional portrayal draws on documented eyewitness testimony — [[siege-warfare|the siege]] claimed more lives through disease and starvation than through direct combat. The moral tension between those who hoarded and those who shared food was not literary invention but a documented feature of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s social dynamics.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Hunger is introduced not as an abstract concept but as a bodily affliction that defines [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]]'s experience from the outset. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] and his companions describe it in explicitly medical terms:

> We four are suffering from a disease called hunger, which cannot be cured by listening to stories. (002-022)

The condition pervades every social stratum. [[don-roque|Don Roque]]'s comic lament underscores the universality of the crisis:

> Don Roque, yawning with hunger. "How much I should enjoy hearing Mass now — after breakfast!" (002-028)

Hunger becomes a weapon that the French wield deliberately:

> It was the destruction of human life which hunger would quickly bring — hunger, that ally of the invader. (008-005)

The defenders acknowledge its power over them:

> If it were not for Father Busto, we should die of hunger. (009-021)

The moral dimension of hunger crystallizes in the conflict between [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] and [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] over grain supplies. [[don-jose-montoria|Don José]], representing the patriotic demand for shared sacrifice, confronts [[don-jeronimo-candiola|the miser]] who hoards food for profit:

> "The people of Saragossa must not die of hunger, porra! and all citizens must contribute." (012-042)

[[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s cold reply — through [[father-mateo-del-busto|Father Busto]] as intermediary — exposes the brutal economics of [[siege-warfare|siege]]:

> "Candiola says that who wants bread must pay for it with money, and not with heroics." (012-024)

In [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s terminal phase, hunger and thirst become indistinguishable from the physical wounds of war:

> In addition to all this, hunger was demoralizing us. We could scarcely stand. (018-010)

The narrator's most harrowing memory combines hunger with the sight of the dying:

> What terrible mental dejection! What hunger! What thirst! I saw many running about, telling me of their dreadful hunger and thirst, had terrible wounds. (024-005)

In the final pages, hunger, fatigue, and wounds converge to reduce the defenders to near-death:

> By the wound lately received, and by hunger and fatigue, my comrades almost dead about me. (031-024)
