# French Retreat from Saragossa

## Overview

The French retreat from saragossa was not a single event but a recurring pattern during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]]: the besieging [[french-army|French forces]], commanded by Marshal [[marshal-lannes|Lannes]], repeatedly advanced into the city's outer resistances only to be driven back by the determined Spanish defenders. These tactical withdrawals punctuated the two-month [[siege-warfare|siege]] and demonstrated the costliness of urban warfare for an army accustomed to set-piece battles.

## Narrative Depiction

Galdós describes the initial French advance and its repulse at the [[castle-aljaferia|Castle Aljafería]]:

> The French had assaulted with great vigor the positions of the Castle Aljafería. (006-001)

But the defenders held, and the French pulled back under pressure:

> We saw the French slowly and in the best order, retreating by the Puente de América. (006-001)

The pattern recurred throughout [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. After the [[december-31-sortie|December 31 sortie]], the French found themselves unable to maintain their forward positions:

> Having no more need to protect our retreat, and the Castle Aljafería alone remaining, we contented ourselves with keeping the enemy at bay. (008-022)

Spanish counter-sorties persistently disrupted French advances:

> The two sorties combined had done damage enough to make the enemy think twice before extending their lines. (008-020)

In the city's final days, even as French [[french-mine-warfare|mines]] and bombardment broke the Spanish defensive line, individual positions held out until overwhelmed:

> We retreated to the chapels. The first defenders of the pulpit, those who had held out longest, fell one after another. (022-009)

The last organized retreat came as the city fell:

> With a still louder noise, the retreat began with plenty of disorder. (023-010)

Even at the very end, the defenders' determination nearly broke French morale:

> The Coso was hers, but Spain retreated only to the opposite pavement. (029-003)

And in one remarkable passage, Galdós notes that the French themselves came close to breaking:

> We were almost on the point of retreating. Tears streamed from their eyes… (030-019)

## Historical Background

In the historical [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]] (December 1808 – February 1809), [[marshal-lannes|Marshal Lannes]] commanded approximately 40,000 [[french-army|French troops]] against a city defended by some 30,000 Spanish regulars and armed civilians. The French tactic of methodical advance through mining and bombardment was consistently disrupted by Spanish sorties — sudden forays from the defensive line that inflicted casualties and delayed the inexorable French progress. These tactical retreats were not signs of French weakness but testimony to the effectiveness of Spanish urban guerrilla tactics, which turned every street, house, and convent into a contested position. The pattern described by Galdós — advance, counterattack, withdrawal — was historically accurate and characteristic of the grinding, positional nature of [[siege-warfare|siege]] warfare in the napoleonic era.
