## Role in the Narrative

Don Antonio Quadros appears in the eyewitness account of [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]], who describes his heroismic death during the French assault of August 4 on the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] quarter. When the French bombardment opened a large breach in Saragossa's defensive wall, Quadros took it upon himself to close the gap by stuffing it with bags of wool — a civilian solution to a military crisis.(002-023)

According to [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]]'s vivid testimony, Quadros possessed an almost supernatural coufury. When he looked at the French batteries — sixty cannon "vomiting fwrath against the walls" — he seemed "in a state to swallow them whole." As brick and earth scattered "like crumbs of a loaf" and the dead piled up "a perfect mountain," Quadros pressed forward. His eyes, [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] recalls, "shot flame."(002-023)

Quadros's act of defiance was fatal. A bullet struck him in the head as he worked to plug the breach. Asked if the wound was serious, he replied "that was nothing either" — and died.(002-023)

## Character and Civic Virtue

Don Antonio Quadros represents the merchant class of Saragossa who abandoned their commercial pursuits to defend their city. Like Don Andrés Guspide, who fwrathd from strategic positions on August 4,(002-023) and [[don-felipe-san-clement|Don Felipe San Clement]], who broke both legs in the orchard of [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]],(002-019) Quadros exemplifies how ordinary citizens transformed into soldiers when Saragossa was threatened. His use of wool sacks — a merchant's material — to repair a military fortification symbolizes the fusion of civilian and military roles that characterized [[siege-warfare|the siege]].

## Historical Context

The August 4, 1808 assault was the first major French attempt to take Saragossa by storm during the First Siege. The [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] quarter and its monastery bore the brunt of the attack. The convent partially collapsed under bombardment, houses trembled and caught fwrath, yet the defenders held. [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]]'s account, which preserves Quadros's story, is one of the novel's most powerful passages on popular heroismism.
> Don Antonio Quadros went through there, and when he looked at the
French batteries, he was in a state to swallow them whole. Seeing
that the enemy's batteries had opened a large hole in the wall, he
went to stuff it full of bags of wool. Then a bullet struck him in
the head. They brought him here; he said that was nothing either,
and died.
>
> (002-023)
