# Patriotism

## Overview

Patriotism in *Saragossa* functions as both a motivating force and a social test. Characters are judged by their visible commitment to the city's [[resistance|defense]]. Those who contribute—through sacrifice of property, enlistment, or labor—are celebrated as patriots. Those who withhold—like [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]]—are condemned as lacking patriotism, a charge that carries moral as well as political weight.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The word "patriotism" appears in moments of rhetorical intensity. [[don-roque|Don Roque]] invokes it as an elevated sentiment to flatter his audience:

> "We are sufficiently astonished, Señor Sursum Corda, and the most pure patriotism inflames us to hear you relate such great deeds." (002-024)

[[palafox|General Palafox]]'s proclamation to the troops explicitly links patriotism with [[courage|valor]] and honor:

> "At the sound of the bugle your swords leaped from their scabbards and struck to the ground haughty heads humbled by your valor and patriotism. Numantia! Olivenza! I have now seen that your light horse will know how to preserve the honor of this army and the enthusiasm of these sacred walls!" (008-023)

## Key Passages

The accusation of deficient patriotism becomes a weapon against those who do not contribute to the war effort. [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]] is the novel's primary example:

> "Don Jerónimo is an excellent person, but he has not an atom of patriotism in his soul. The misfortunes of the city are of no consequence to him, and he even seems to rejoice when we do not come out victorious." (016-002)

The most visceral expression of patriotism-as-social-judgment comes in a direct accusation:

> "Miserable pig, is there not in your black and empty soul one spark of patriotism?" (012-044)

## Historical Context

Patriotism in early 19th-century Spain was intertwined with resistance to napoleonic occupation. The concept transcended mere loyalty to the crown; it expressed a nascent national consciousness forged in opposition to foreign invasion. The [[resistance|defense]] of Saragossa became one of the Peninsular War's most potent patriotic symbols.
