# Courage

## Overview

Courage is the defining virtue of the defenders of Saragossa in Galdós's novel. It manifests not as the absence of fear but as the will to act despite it. The narrative repeatedly contrasts the material weakness of the city's defenders—half-naked peasants with rope sandals—with their extraordinary couragey, which astonished Europe and earned them the title "the Numantines of modern times."

## Manifestation in the Novel

Courage appears in the novel as both individual and collective phenomenon. The citizens of Saragossa display couragey across all social classes: from noblewomen like the Señora Maria Consolación Azlor, who led peasants into battle, to the common soldiers who faced French assaults with what Galdós describes as "audacious courage":

> Saragossa displayed in the suburb such audacious courage that that day is known as one of the most brilliant of all her brilliant history. (004-014)

The novel also explores how couragey can be lost and regained. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]], [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]], honestly depicts moments of cowardice followed by inexplicable transformations:

> I do not know why we were cowards, nor why we were brave a few moments later. What I do know is that, moved by an extraordinary power, immense and superhuman, we hurled ourselves into the breach. (010-024)

This psychological honesty—showing couragey as fluctuating rather than constant—is one of the novel's strengths.

## Key Passages

The French attack on the entrenchments by the Barcelona road reveals couragey as a shared quality between enemies:

> Their courage lay in striking audacious blows upon the batteries, and their tenacity produced a veritable hecatomb. They fell in great numbers; the ranks were broken, and, being instantly filled by others, they repeated the attack. (006-017)

[[don-roque|Don Roque]]'s exaggerated introduction of [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] illustrates how couragey became a form of social currency in besiege-warfared Saragossa:

> He said that the junta had made me a great offer of a concession in Peru, and that during the siege of Madrid I had performed prodigies of valor at the Puerta de los Pozos, my courage being so great that the French found it convenient after the capitulation to rid themselves of such a fearful foe. (003-005)

## Historical Context

The defenders of Saragossa became legendary across Europe for their refusal to surrender despite overwhelming odds. The comparison to Numantia—the Celtiberian city that chose mass suicide over surrender to Rome—was made by contemporaries and reflected the perception that Saragossa's resistance transcended ordinary military calculation.
