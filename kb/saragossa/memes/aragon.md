Aragon (Aragón) is a historic region and former kingdom in northeastern Spain, with [[saragossa|Saragossa]] as its capital. The region gave its name to the Crown of Aragon, a medieval Mediterranean power, and its people — the Aragonese — were renowned for their tenacity and independence.

## The Aragonese Character

The character of the Aragonese people is frequently invoked in the narrative of the siege. [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], one of the wealthiest citizens of Saragossa, embodied the ideal of Aragonese virtue: loyal, brave, and open-handed.

As [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] was told, "Like every good Aragonese, he is all heart. We will go to his house, and I am sure he will receive us well" (001-008).

The physical strength of the Aragonese was equally celebrated. When [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] was seized by an enraged citizen, it was the "muscular strength of the Aragonese patriot" that shook him off and compelled him to answer (012-055).

## Resistance Against Napoleon

During the [[peninsular-war|Peninsular War]], Aragon became a symbol of Spanish resistance against [[napoleon|Napoleon]]. The [[first-siege-of-saragossa|First]] and [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second]] sieges of Saragossa transformed the Aragonese capital into an emblem of national defiance.

The courage of the Aragonese defenders went beyond ordinary valor: "The Aragonese nature stood forth" in moments of supreme crisis, when rational calculation would have dictated retreat (017-005). They held positions that were no more than "a shapeless mass of earth, bricks, and corpses," refusing to abandon what had become a graveyard.
> The Aragonese nature stood forth, and it is one which does not know how
to be conquered. The living took the places of the dead with a sublime
aplomb. Death was an accident, a trivial detail, a thing of which no
notice should be taken.
>
> (017-005)


Even as conditions deteriorated, the Aragonese displayed extraordinary resilience. Those who lacked "the steel nerves of the Aragonese" dragged themselves through the ruins searching for bread and water, while others ran swiftly to continue the fight (024-005).

## Legacy

The sacrifices of Saragossa were understood as contributions of the entire Aragonese nation. The city gave its lives "to those of the millions of all the nations which have accomplished something in the world" (030-022). In death as in life, the defenders of Aragon joined the ranks of those who shaped history through collective sacrifice.
