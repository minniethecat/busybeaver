The Plazuela de San Felipe is a small plaza in Saragossa, best known as the site of the [[torre-nueva|Torre Nueva]], the city's iconic leaning tower. The plaza also contains the belfry of San Felipe, a small bell tower that stands beside the great tower, in a spatial relationship that the narrative captures vividly.

> "You arrive at the Plaza de San Felipe where the tower is. Look yonder! Do you see there near the great pile there is another tower, a little belfry? It seems like an acolyte before his lord the canon, which is the great tower."
> (005-014)

During the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]], the Plaza de San Felipe served as a significant landmark in the [[calle-de-anton-trillo|Antón Trillo]] neighborhood.

From this plaza, the narrow Calle de Antón Trillo leads to the back of the church, where the house and garden of [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo de Candiola]] were located. The directions to reach Candiola's house were given with the plaza and belfry as the primary reference points (005-018).

## Role in the Narrative

The plaza is central to the romance between [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] and [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]]. Augustine described to [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] the precise route from the Torre Nueva through the Plaza de San Felipe to Mariquilla's window overlooking the garden wall.

During the later stages of the siege, Augustine was impelled by emotion to direct his steps towards the Plazuela San Felipe, following a great crowd that was hastening towards that place, though he was detained by his father and forced to continue on (014-029).

The plaza also became a gathering point during critical moments of the defense. [[friar|Friar]] Mateo del Busto stopped there to speak with officers, announcing the safety of Augustine Montoria, who was one of those occupying the Torre Nueva (028-042).

As the siege reached its final stages, [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] was led through the plaza on his way to execution. The French had mined the Plazuela de San Felipe, and the defenders feared the earth would leap under their feet at any moment (029-019).

## Significance

The Plazuela de San Felipe encapsulates the layered geography of besieged Saragossa — a public square that was simultaneously a place of romance, a military command post, a route to execution, and a mined death trap.

Its proximity to both the Torre Nueva (the city's tallest landmark) and the Candiola residence (the home of its most reviled citizen) made it a crossroads where the personal and the epic dimensions of the siege intersected.
