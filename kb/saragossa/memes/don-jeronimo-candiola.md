# Don Jerónimo Candiola

## Overview

Don Jerónimo Candiola is the novel's most morally complex figure. As the wealthiest man in Saragossa, his refusal to contribute to the war effort makes him the target of public scorn and accusations of deficient patriotism. Yet Galdós refuses to reduce him to a simple villain: by the novel's end, Candiola has lost everything—his house, his fortune, and his daughter—and his grief is rendered with genuine pathos.

## Biography / Background

Candiola is established as the wealthiest citizen of Saragossa, a man whose fortune has been accumulated through usury and shrewd business dealings. He lives in a large house on the Calle de Antón Trillo with his daughter [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]]. His social position is paradoxical: his wealth commands a certain deference, yet his avarice and refusal to participate in communal sacrifice makes him universally despised. Unlike the landed gentry represented by [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], Candiola's wealth is liquid and portable—a fact that shapes his calculus throughout both sieges. His wife is deceased; Mariquilla is his sole heir and his only emotional attachment.

## Role in the Narrative

Candiola is introduced as a counterpoint to the self-sacrificing patriotism of characters like [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]]. While others give freely, Candiola hoards:

> "Don Jerónimo is an excellent person, but he has not an atom of patriotism in his soul. The misfortunes of the city are of no consequence to him, and he even seems to rejoice when we do not come out victorious." (016-002)

The accusation becomes more visceral in direct confrontation:

> "Miserable pig, is there not in your black and empty soul one spark of patriotism?" (012-044)

Candiola embodies a critique not merely of individual cowardice but of a worldview that places private wealth above collective survival. His opposition to his daughter's romance with [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]]—the son of his enemy—adds a Romeo-and-Juliet dimension to the plot. When the siege destroys his house, his psychological collapse reveals the limits of wealth as a shield against the chaos of war:

> Candiola's distress and moral suffering made it seem as if he had a nervous disorder. It was plain to be seen that terror and grief had completely upset him. (019-013)

The destruction of the Candiola household during the second siege marks a turning point. The man who refused to give anything to the war loses everything to it. His final scenes—wandering the ruins of his house, clutching at the rubble as if his gold were buried beneath—are among the novel's most potent images of war's indifference to wealth and status.

## Key Relationships

- [[mariquilla-candiola]] — his daughter, whose love for [[augustine-montoria|Augustine]] he opposes. Candiola's paternal love, though possessive and ultimately destructive, is the one redeeming emotion Galdós grants him.
- [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] — his enemy and foil. Montoria represents patriotic self-sacrifice rooted in landed wealth and communal identity; Candiola represents the rootless logic of capital. Their enmity is personal as well as ideological, culminating in the violent confrontation at Candiola's house.
- [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] — his daughter's lover, whom Candiola despises as both a Montoria and a rival for Mariquilla's affection.

## Historical Context

Candiola is a fictional character, not based on a specific historical figure. However, he represents a recognizable social type in Napoleonic-era Spain: the urban rentier whose wealth, accumulated through lending and property, placed him outside the traditional hierarchies of aristocratic honor and peasant loyalty. Galdós uses Candiola to explore the tension between liberal individualism and the demands of national solidarity—a debate that would dominate Spanish politics throughout the nineteenth century.
