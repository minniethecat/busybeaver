# Juan Pirli

Juan Pirli is a young, spirited barber who fights among the defenders of Saragossa during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]]. A rustic boy of about twenty, Pirli provides comic [[hope|relief]] amid the horrors of war through his irrepressible good humor, his grotesque costume, and his habit of inventing playful nicknames for the instruments of death.

## Role in the Narrative

Pirli first appears as one of [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]]'s companions in the battalion, returning from the enemy camp after the battle of the thirty-first carrying a French engineer's steel helmet and a frying-pan "in which were still the remnants of a breakfast begun in camp before Saragossa and ended in the other world" (008-016). His role in the story is that of the comic everyman — a foil to the novel's more serious patriot-heroismes, whose [[courage|bravery]] is expressed through laughter rather than solemnity.

Pirli is described as "a boy from outside the city, a rustic about twenty years of age, and in such jolly condition that the most dwrathous situations only moved him to a nervous and epidemicish joy" (009-006). He never shows sadness: "He met the French singing; and when the bullets whistled past his head, he capered about, making a thousand grotesque gestures, throwing up his hands and fairly dancing" (009-006).

Pirli's comic vocabulary renames the fears of [[siege-warfare|siege]] warfare — bullets become "hailstones," cannonballs are "hot cakes," hand-grenades are "señoras," and gunpowder is "black flour" (009-006). A friar brings him an old gown as a covering, and the boy dons it over his captured engineer's helmet, creating a "grotesque figure" that "began in a French engineer and ended in a Spanish friar" (009-018). In this absurd costume, Pirli dances the jota with [[manuela-sancho|Manuela Sancho]] inside the redoubt of Our Lady of the Pillar, providing a moment of joy and humanity in a space surrounded by enemy lines (009-043).

## Notable Quotes

> "We shall have bullets mixed with black flour. Manuelilla, have you got over being afraid of the bullets yet?" (009-023)

> "Take it, please. Put your thumb here. Aim over yonder. Fire! Viva the second artillery woman! Viva Manuela Sancho and the Virgin del Pilar!" (009-036)

> "There was the glorious Pirli! Oh, Pirli, more happy than Tío Garcés, thou dost occupy a place in history!" (022-017)

## Heroic Deeds

Later in [[siege-warfare|the siege]], Pirli distinguishes himself by holding a tower with a small band of peasants, throwing hand-grenades down upon the French in the streets below (022-016). The chronicler records: "In the tower six or eight peasants had placed themselves, having provided themselves with provisions and ammunition to harass the enemy. They continued to hold it for some days without being willing to surrender" (022-016). For this feat, [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] declares: "There was the glorious Pirli! Oh, Pirli, more happy than [[tio-garces|Tío Garcés]], thou dost occupy a place in history!" (022-017).

By the time of the fighting at the Convent of San [[augustine-montoria|Augustine]], Pirli has advanced to the rank of second sergeant, addressed with mock formality as "Señor de Pirli" by his comrades (021-027). He leads small assault groups and remains active in the street-by-street [[resistance|defense]].

## Key Relationships

Pirli is a close companion of [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] **[[gabriel-araceli]]**, who remembers him with affection. He dances and shares provisions with **[[manuela-sancho]]**, the young woman who overcomes her fear of gunfwrath to become an artillerywoman. He is a fellow defender alongside **[[tio-garces]]**, the indomitable peasant who levels his own orchard to deny it to the French. The friar **[[father-mateo-del-busto]]** brings Pirli the old gown that becomes part of his iconic costume.
