# Father Luengo

## Overview

Father Luengo was a friar who appears in the later stages of Galdós's narrative as a companion to [[father-mateo-del-busto|Father Mateo del Busto]] and a spiritual counsellor to [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]]. His role, though limited in prominence, illustrates the pervasive involvement of the clergy in Saragossa's defence.

## Role in the Narrative

Father Luengo first appears alongside [[father-mateo-del-busto|Father Mateo del Busto]] as they make their way through the embattled streets:

> "We had reached the parish church of San Pablo when we were met by a friar, Father Mateo del Busto, who was coming with much fatigue, forcing his feeble steps, and accompanied by another friar whom they called Father Luengo." (012-016)

His narrative function is primarily as a foil and interlocutor for [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], the novel's central paternal figure. In a key exchange, Luengo praises the younger generation's coufury, remarking that [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] "has performed prodigies of [[courage|valor]]" and noting that "the greenest laurels of this campaign belong to the brilliant fighters of the Church" (026-016).

Luengo also serves a practical function as guide and escort. When the elderly and desperationing miser [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]] enters a convent gate, it is Father Luengo who "took [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]] down the [[coso|Coso]]," steering the grieving father away from confrontation (026-061).

## Key Relationships

Father Luengo's most significant relationship is with [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]]. He appears at moments of crisis — when [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]] is weighed down by grief over his lost son and grandson, when the Captain-General has fallen, and when the city's fate hangs in the balance:

> "'Eh, Father Luengo,' said Montoria, calling to the friar of that name, 'what is it? Where is the Captain-General? Has he perished in the ruins?'" (028-041)

## Historical Context

While Father Luengo is likely a fictional character, he represents the many unnamed friars and clerics who served as chaplains, messengers, and morale-keepers during [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. The novel portrays the Saragossan clergy not as a monolithic institution but as a diverse body of individuals — some taking up arms like [[santiago-sas|Santiago Sas]], others, like Luengo, serving in supporting roles of counsel and care.

His presence alongside [[father-mateo-del-busto|Father Mateo del Busto]] also reinforces the network of religious figures that Galdós weaves through the narrative, from the scholarly [[father-boggiero|Boggiero]] to the warrior-clergymen of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]].
