# San José

## Overview

The fortress of San José, one of the key defensive positions during [[siege-warfare|the siege]], defended by [[renovales|Renovales]] and the [[volunteers-of-huesca|Huesca volunteers]] against sustained French artillery fire.

## Location and Strategic Significance

San José is situated in Eastern Saragossa. Along with the redoubt of El Pilar, it was one of the two best positions in the Mediodía sector, and "the radius of our fire was considerable, crossing that of San José" (009-002). The French recognized its importance early: "The right batteries opened fire upon San José and the Pilar" (010-009) as the January assault began.

## The January Assaults

The attack on San José began as a probing action. Gabriel observed the movement before it happened: "Either I am greatly deceived, or they are now going to attack San José" (006-003). A first attempt was quickly repulsed: "Such was the attack upon San José, a futile attempt quickly punished" (006-008).

By January 10–11 the assault had become full-scale. The French attacked "at the same time, and with equal fury, the redoubt del Pilar and the fortress of San José" (010-014). [[renovales|Renovales]] commanded the defenders — Huesca and Valencia volunteers, Walloon guards, and Soria militia (010-014). Despite heavy casualties, the position held through the day of the 10th.

## The Fall

The fortress could not withstand sustained artillery indefinitely. By the 11th, San José was "converted into a heap of ruins, and with most of its garrison dead" (010-017). The French entered only "when their artillery had reduced it to powder, and all of its defenders had fallen, one by one, to lie among its fragments" (011-001).

The loss of San José was strategically devastating. Together with the redoubt of El Pilar, "gaining this position and San José, the French would have enough protection to open their third parallel and to demolish at their leisure the whole circuit of the city" (011-005). After its fall, Las Tenerías became "the weakest point in the city since the destruction of San José" (013-005).

## The Fall of San José

> "The fortress of San José had surrendered, or rather the French had entered it when their artillery had reduced it to powder, and all of its defenders had fallen, one by one, to lie among its fragments. The Imperial soldiers, on entering, found heaps of bodies and stones matted together with blood."
