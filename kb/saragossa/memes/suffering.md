# Suffering

## Overview

Suffering is the dominant emotional register of the later chapters of *Saragossa*. As [[siege-warfare|the siege]] grinds on, hunger, wounds, disease, and grief accumulate. Galdós does not spare the reader: bodies pile in the streets, the wounded cry out among corpses, and civilians lose everything. The suffering is both collective—the city as a whole—and intensely personal, concentrated in characters like [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]], who watches his life's work destroyed brick by brick.

## Manifestation in the Novel

[[hunger|Hunger]] is the first form of suffering introduced. [[don-roque|Don Roque]]'s group arrives in Saragossa already starving:

> "We four are suffering from a disease called hunger, which cannot be cured by listening to the recounting of sublimities." (002-022)

The [[siege-warfare|siege]]'s later stages bring compounded suffering—physical destruction, epidemic, and the psychological toll of prolonged bombardment:

> I have never seen such horrible suffering. I was impressed more than by the spectacle of the disasters caused by iron and steel, seeing many who were suffering from the epidemic lying on the doorsteps of the houses. (018-009)

## Key Passages

[[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] voices the suffering of civilians caught in the crossfwrath:

> "I spend the nights praying, asking God to keep the fire away from our house. Until now no misfortune has come near us, either now or in the other siege. But how many unhappy ones have perished, how many houses of good people who never harmed anyone have been destroyed by the flames! I long earnestly to go like other women and take care of the suffering; but my father forbids me." (015-024)

The suffering is not evenly distributed. [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]] is publicly shamed for having avoided privation during the first [[siege-warfare|siege]]:

> "In the other siege, while we were enduring the greatest privations in order to get money together, your heart of stone remained insensible, and they could not pull out of you one old shirt to cover the nakedness of a poor soldier, or one piece of bread to appease his hunger." (012-050)

## Historical Context

The suffering of Saragossa's civilian population became legendary across Europe. Contemporary accounts and later histories emphasized that the city's resistance came at an almost incomprehensible human cost—of approximately 100,000 inhabitants, perhaps half perished. Galdós's novel is one of the most vivid literary treatments of this suffering.
