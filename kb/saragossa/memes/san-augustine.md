# Convent of San Augustine

## Overview

The **Convent of San Augustine** was a convent in the [[las-tenerias|Las Tenerías]] quarter of Saragossa that became one of the most fiercely contested positions during the Second Siege. Adjacent to the [[las-monicas|Convent of Las Mónicas]], it served as a key defensive bastion whose possession was vital for control of the suburb.

## Location

The convent was situated next to the Convent de las Mónicas in the Las Tenerías district. The defensive line ran "from the Puerta Quemada, past the battery of Palafox, through the mill, the garden of San Augustine, to the mill of Goicoechea, then to the orchard of Las Mónicas, and on to those of San Augustine" (013-012).

## Role in the Siege

### Fortification and Strategic Value

Both San Augustine and [[las-monicas|Las Mónicas]] were described as "veritable fortresses" within the defensive network of Las Tenerías (013-011). The convent's position made it critical for preventing the French from advancing through the quarter toward the Plaza de la Magdalena.

> As the possession of San Augustine and Las Mónicas permitted them to threaten that central point by the easy way to the Calle de Palomar, they already considered themselves masters of the suburb. (023-001)

### The Battle of San Augustine

When the French captured Las Mónicas, the defenders of San Augustine faced the full weight of the attack. The battle for the convent became a desperate, close-quarters struggle:

> It was inevitable that the French, now masters of Las Mónicas, should show great perseverance in the effort to gain possession of this monastery, in order to establish themselves firmly and definitely in that quarter. (021-025)

The defenders' grim humor is captured in one soldier's remark:

> "They will give us a spoonful when the battle of San Augustine is over. It would be too bad if they should finish us off before we know what color the stuff is which they are going to pass around tonight." (021-032)

### The High Altar

The convent contained an enormous high altar filled with gilded wooden sculptures, "extending from the floor to the arch above, and from wall to wall, and represented in row upon row the celestial hierarchies" (022-004). This altar, with its interior passages for the sacristan, became part of the battlefield itself.

### The Fighting

After the French penetrated the breaches at San José, their infantry advanced "towards the gardens of San Augustine, finishing those who were already dying" (017-010). The defenders fought tenaciously but were eventually compelled to leave:

> "We therefore left San Augustine. While we were passing through the street of the same name, parallel with the Calle de Palomar, we saw that they were throwing hand-grenades among the French." (022-016)

### The Tower

Even after the main convent was lost, a handful of peasants held out in the tower for days:

> "In the tower six or eight peasants had placed themselves, having provided themselves with provisions and ammunition to harass the enemy. They continued to hold it for some days without being willing to surrender." (022-016)
