# Calle de Antón Trillo

## Overview

The Calle de Antón Trillo is a narrow, secluded street in Saragossa that serves as the location of [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s house and, by extension, the home of [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]]. Though an unremarkable lane in peacetime, it becomes a site of intense personal and military drama during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]], hosting scenes of love, death, and destruction.

## Location

The street is described as a quiet, out-of-the-way passage:

> That street, narrow and retired, called Antón Trillo. You follow this until you reach the garden wall… (005-018)

It lies off the beaten path of Saragossa's main thoroughfares, far from the [[coso|Coso]]'s bustle, lending it an air of seclusion that contrasts sharply with the violence that eventually engulfs it. Its proximity to a garden wall marks it as being at the city's edge, where built structures give way to cultivated land.

## Role in the Siege

The Calle de Antón Trillo is the setting for some of the novel's most emotionally charged scenes. It becomes a recurring destination for characters seeking the [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] household:

> We hid ourselves quickly in the Calle de Antón Trillo; and Montoria said to us… (012-026)

As [[siege-warfare|the siege]] intensifies, the street transforms from a place of domestic refuge to a site of violence. After a devastating [[french-artillery-bombardment|bombardment]], [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] describes its desolation:

> There were few people, but the Calle de Antón Trillo was deserted. We stopped close to the garden wall. (015-003)

The street's fate is sealed in [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s final days:

> Upon returning to the Calle de Antón Trillo. But as she scarcely had strength to walk… (018-032)

And the climactic visit:

> I hurried to the Calle de Antón Trillo. The house of Candiola had been destroyed by a bomb. (019-001)

## Historical Notes

The street name evokes a real personage — Saint Anthony (Antón) and possibly a local Trillo family — following the Spanish custom of dedicating streets to saints and notable citizens. Like most of the novel's locations, Galdós anchors his fiction in Saragossa's actual urban geography. The Calle de Antón Trillo represents the many narrow medieval streets of Saragossa's old quarter that were devastated during the historical [[siege-warfare|siege]] of 1808–1809, when house-to-house combat reduced entwrath blocks to rubble.
