# Glory

## Overview

Glory is the reward that the defenders of Saragossa sought in exchange for their suffering and death. Unlike honor, which operates in the present, glory is oriented toward the future—toward posterity, legend, and historical memory. The novel's opening epigraph from Napier frames this explicitly: "When the other events of the Spanish war shall be lost in the obscurity of time… the story of Zaragoza, like some ancient triumphal pillar standing amidst ruins, will tell a tale of past glory."

## Manifestation in the Novel

[[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]], upon enlisting, expresses the allure of glory as a motivating force:

> The spirit of the men who were enlisting filled me with such enthusiasm that nothing seemed to me so noble as to follow glory, even afar off. (004-008)

The Saragossan defiance of the French surrender demand explicitly substitutes glory for material comfort:

> "We do not know how to surrender," they made answer; "and our bodies shall be clothed with glory." (004-010)

## Key Passages

The narrator explicitly links glory to the international perception of Saragossa's resistance. The city's fame spread across Europe, transforming half-naked peasants into mythic figures:

> In England and Germany, where they were considered the Numantines of modern times, those half-naked peasants, with rope sandals on their feet and the bright Saragossan kerchief on their heads, became like figures of mythology. (004-008)

## Related Characters

- [[gabriel-araceli]] — narrator who sought glory through enlistment
- [[Palafox]] — general who promised his troops "bloodstained swords as the sign of your glory"

## Historical Context

The pursuit of glory through martial sacrifice was a central theme of napoleonic-era culture, influenced by both classical models (Roman virtue) and emerging Romantic ideals. Saragossa's defenders understood their actions as contributions to an enduring legacy that would outlast their individual deaths.
