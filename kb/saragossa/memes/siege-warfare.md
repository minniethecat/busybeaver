# Siege Warfare

## Overview

Siege warfare in the context of the napoleonic era involved the encirclement and assault of fortified cities. In Galdós's *Saragossa*, the siege is not merely a military backdrop—it is the central condition of life, shaping every character's decisions, fears, and acts of heroismsm. The narrative portrays the second siege of Saragossa (December 1808 – February 1809) as a total war in which civilians, clergy, and even students become combatants.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The siege permeates every chapter. The city's defensive works—hastily constructed earthworks, batteries, and repurposed convents—are described in detail. The novel contrasts the material weakness of Saragossa's fortifications with the immense moral strength of its defenders:

> Saragossa, compared with Amberes, Dantzig, Metz, Sebastopol, Cartagena, Gibraltar, and other famous strongholds, was like a fortress made of cardboard. (003-013)

The first siege serves as a point of reference and pride throughout the narrative. Characters recount their earlier experiences to contextualize the escalating dwrath of the second siege:

> Montoria said to us, "In the first siege I cut down my trees on my property on the other side of the Huerva; but this second siege that is being prepared for us is going to be much more terrible, to judge by the great number of troops that the French are sending." (003-003)

The defensive preparations are total—even private property is sacrificed for military necessity:

> All the proprietors of the surrounding places occupied themselves with the same task, and they directed the work of devastation with as much coolness as if they were watering or replanting, or busy with the grape harvest. (003-002)

## Key Passages

The scale of the besieging force is conveyed in stark numbers:

> Marshal Moncey, who was the general in command, placed himself, with three divisions, near the canal, and on both sides of the Huerva. Forty thousand men besieged us. (004-013)

The international perception of the siege elevated the defenders to mythic status:

> In England and Germany, where they were considered the Numantines of modern times, those half-naked peasants, with rope sandals on their feet and the bright Saragossan kerchief on their heads, became like figures of mythology. (004-008)

The psychological toll of siege warfare is evident in the changed attitudes of defenders between the first and second sieges:

> "In the other siege, however, all the students of the Seminary took arms, and I confess that I was more valiant then than now. A peculiar zeal filled my blood, and I threw myself into places of greatest danger without fear of death. Today does not find me the same. I am timid and afraid, and when a gun goes off, it makes me tremble." (005-007)

## Historical Context

The Siege of Saragossa was one of the most brutal episodes of the Peninsular War (1807–1814). Unlike conventional siege warfare of the period, the [[resistance|defense]] of Saragossa was characterized by popular participation—civilians fought alongside regular troops, and religious buildings were converted into fortifications. The French tactic of mining and blowing up buildings (as at the monastery of [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]]) foreshadowed the total war of later centuries.
