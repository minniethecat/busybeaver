# Don Miguel Salamero

Don Miguel Salamero was a prominent citizen of Saragossa and a member of one of the city's leading families during the Peninsular War. He is remembered in Galdós's chronicle of [[siege-warfare|the siege]] principally for his decisive intervention during the brutal hand-to-hand fighting in the orchard of the Convent of the Incarnation.

## Role in the Narrative

Salamero is first mentioned by the beggar-narrator [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] during his sprawling account of the first [[siege-warfare|siege]]'s heroismisms. Describing the slaughter in the orchard behind the Convent of the Incarnation — "There are at the least two hundred bodies in that orchard" — [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] notes that a prominent merchant named [[don-felipe-san-clement|Don Felipe San Clement]] broke both his legs there. He then adds a pregnant, unfinished sentence: "Indeed, if Don Miguel Salamero had not been present — don't you know anything about that?" (002-021). The implied narrative is that Salamero's presence at a critical moment saved the position from being overrun, though the beggar's account is cut short by his listeners' hunger and impatience.
> There are at the least two hundred bodies in that orchard; and there
Don Felipe San Clement, a merchant of Saragossa, broke both his legs.
Indeed, if Don Miguel Salamero had not been present — don't you know
anything about that?
>
> (002-021)


The Salamero family is later cited among the patrician houses that formed Saragossa's moral and military backbone. When "the influential men of the city, the don-jose-montorias, the Ceresos, the Sases, the Salameros, and the San Clementes were returning to Las Mónicas," they displayed "such tremendous coufury and uttered such contempt of the enemy that it roused the spirits of all who saw and heard them" (018-014). This passage places the Salameros in the highest tier of Saragossa's citizen-defenders, alongside the families whose names recur throughout the chronicle.

## Historical Context

Don Miguel Salamero belongs to the class of prominent Aragonese citizens — merchants, landowners, and minor nobility — who assumed military leadership during siege-warfares in the absence of a regular army. Like [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], [[mariano-cereso|Don Mariano Cereso]], and [[santiago-sas|Don Santiago Sas]], Salamero represents the fusion of civic and military authority that characterized Saragossa's resistance. These men were not professional soldiers; they were citizens who organized, led, and fought alongside their neighbors in the desperate street-by-street [[resistance|defense]] of their city.

The Salamero family name carries weight in the narrative alongside the don-jose-montorias, Ceresos, Sases, and San Clementes — a pentad of families whose collective presence at Las Mónicas after a night of heavy fighting served to reanimate the spirits of the exhausted defenders (018-014).
