# Don Roque

## Overview

Don Roque escapes French captivity with [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] and makes his way to Saragossa. He serves as a comic foil and social facilitator, using his gift for rhetoric and connections to secure shelter and food. His exaggerated praise of Gabriel to [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] (003-005) is a highlight of the early chapters.

## From Escapee to Guide

Don Roque's defining trait is his social resourcefulness. "I know Don José de Montoria, one of the richest citizens of Saragossa" (001-008), he reveals as the group enters the city. His existing connections give the escapees a crucial foothold.

## The Comic Foil

### Hunger and Heroism

Don Roque's most memorable scenes arise from the tension between physical hunger and patriotic rhetoric. When [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] regales the group with tales of heroism, Don Roque exclaims with enthusiasm — then yawns: "How much I should enjoy hearing those tales of heroism told on a full stomach!" (002-028).

His repeated attempts to redirect the conversation toward finding Don José's house form one of the novel's most sustained comic sequences.

### The Exaggerator

Don Roque's gift for embellishment is both a strength and a flaw. When introducing Gabriel to Montoria, "he overdrew the picture so much that he made me blush, particularly as some of his announcements were stupendous lies" (003-005). Yet this same rhetorical skill — leavened with "circumlocution and graceful ellipses" — secures food for the starving group (003-007).
> Don Roque said to me, "I know Don José de Montoria, one of the richest
citizens of Saragossa. We were both born in Mequinenza. We went to
school together, and we played our games together on the hills of
Corregidor. It is thirty years since I have seen him, but I believe
that he will receive us well. Like every good Aragonese, he is all heart."
>
> (001-008)


## Role in the Siege

After the escapees settle in Saragossa, Don Roque transforms from comic companion to active participant. He joins the sanitary service, displaying "a feverish activity before there were any wounded" and preparing lint for dressings (010-013).

During the dark later chapters, he remains a constant presence. He weeps at burials (025-041), searches for food in a starving city (025-048), and stands by Gabriel in the final evacuation: "A friendly arm held me up, and I recognized Don Roque" (030-012). He stays with Gabriel as the column departs, stopping "to keep me company for a little time" (031-029).

## Role in the Narrative

Don Roque functions as both guide and comic [[hope|relief]]. His impatience with endless tales of heroism — "How much I should enjoy hearing those tales of heroism told on a full stomach!" (002-028) — grounds the novel in physical reality. He introduces [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] into [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]]'s household, setting the plot in motion.
