# Father Mateo del Busto

## Overview

Father Mateo del Busto is a Franciscan friar who serves as a spiritual anchor among the defenders of Saragossa during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]]. A "reader and qualifier" of the Franciscan order, his knowledge and pastoral presence make him a valued mediator between the city's feuding families and a source of comfort to the starving and wounded. Though a secondary figure, his interventions at critical moments illuminate the role of the clergy in sustaining civilian morale.

## Role in the Narrative

Father Busto appears at pivotal moments in the domestic and military crises of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. He is first introduced as a confidant of [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]], mediating between the [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]] household and the wider community:

> Don José was accompanied by a friar, Father Mateo del Busto, reader and qualifier of the Franciscan convent of Saragossa. (009-012)

His presence signals moral authority; characters turn to him for guidance when the city's social order frays. During a tense exchange about [[don-jeronimo-candiola|the miser]] [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]], Busto acts as an intermediary between the enfuryd [[don-jose-montoria|Montoria]] and the reviled moneylender:

> "I will tell you, Señor Don José," said Busto; "Candiola says that who wants bread must pay for it with money, and not with heroics." (012-024)

This moment crystallizes the conflict between sacrificial patriotism and self-preservation that runs through the novel. Busto's role as truth-teller — reporting [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s words without softening them — underscores his integrity in a city riven by distrust.

## Ministry Amid Suffering

During [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s final phase, Busto's ministry shifts from mediation to direct care for the wounded:

> That little friar called Father Busto takes it to the wounded and sick. (016-019)

His selflessness becomes most evident in the Convent of San Francisco, where, exhausted and unable to move, he still ministers to those around him. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] finds him there among the debris:

> I recognized at once Father Mateo del Busto, seated himself beside me, silent and motionless, with his eyes fixed on the floor. (024-011)

When [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] attempts to rescue him, Busto refuses to leave the wounded:

> "Father Mateo del Busto is over there and cannot move, for his legs are broken." (024-016)

[[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] and others carry him out:

> I remembered Father Mateo del Busto, and with some others went to rescue him. (024-017)

## Historical Context

While Father Mateo del Busto appears to be a fictional creation, Franciscan friars were deeply embedded in the life of early 19th-century Saragossa. The Franciscan convent (whose ruins feature prominently in the novel) was a center of learning and pastoral care. Galdós's portrayal of Busto — learned yet humble, mediating civic disputes while tending the dying — reflects the historical reality of religious orders caught up in the Peninsular War's urban warfare.
