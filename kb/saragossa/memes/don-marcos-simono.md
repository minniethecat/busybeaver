# Don Marcos Simono

Don Marcos Simono was a distinguished Spanish commander of engineers who fought in the desperate street-by-street [[resistance|defense]] of Saragossa during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]]. He died in action on February 3, 1809, during the French assault on the Convent of Jerusalem.

## Role in the Narrative

Simono is among the notable defenders named by the garrulous beggar [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] during his recounting of the first [[siege-warfare|siege]]'s street fighting. When the French broke through the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] quarter and advanced into the [[coso|Coso]], "[[santiago-sas|Don Santiago Sas]], [[mariano-cereso|Don Mariano Cereso]], Don Lorenzo Calvo, Don Marcos Simono, [[renovales|Renovales]], Martin Albantos, Vicente Codé, Don Vicente Marraco, and others fearlessly attacked the French" (002-025). This roll call places Simono in the company of Saragossa's most prominent defenders, a mark of his standing in the city's resistance.

Later, during [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]]'s service in the redoubt of Our Lady of the Pillar, [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] notes with pride: "In the corps of engineers, we had the great Simono, high official of that distinguished branch" (009-003). The epithet "the great Simono" signals the respect he commanded among the defenders and his reputation as an engineering officer of the first rank.

Simono's death is recorded with solemn brevity amid the bloody fighting of early February: "Don Marcos Simono, the distinguished commander of engineers, was one of those who died there" (026-002). This occurred during the French capture of the Convent of Jerusalem, a position between [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] and the hospital, in a battle described as "as bloody as those of [[las-tenerias|Las Tenerías]]" (026-001). His death, alongside the gradual loss of key defensive positions, marked the inexorable tightening of the French grip on the city.
> Don Marcos Simono, the distinguished commander of engineers, was one
of those who died there. In the suburb, the besiegers had advanced but
little; and in six or seven days' effort, they had not gained possession
of the Calle de Puerta Quemada.
>
> (026-002)


## Historical Context

As commander of engineers, Simono would have been responsible for the construction and maintenance of defensive works — barricades, trenches, [[french-mine-warfare|mines]], and counter-[[french-mine-warfare|mines]] — that defined Saragossa's resistance. The [[siege-warfare|siege]] was characterized by intense subterranean warfare, with both sides digging [[french-mine-warfare|mines]] and counter-[[french-mine-warfare|mines]] beneath the streets and convents. The loss of an experienced engineering commander was a severe blow to the defenders' technical capacity.

Simono appears to be a historical figure whose name is preserved in Galdós's narrative among the roll of Saragossa's martyred defenders. His inclusion in the chronicle-like passages of the novel reflects Galdós's documentary approach to the *Episodios Nacionales*, in which real historical actors appear alongside fictional characters.
