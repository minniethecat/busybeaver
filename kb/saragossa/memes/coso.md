# Coso

## Overview

The main avenue of Saragossa, a broad boulevard that served as the city's central artery. Throughout the siege, the Coso was simultaneously a thoroughfare, a rallying point, a battlefield, and a place of refuge — "the central place of reunion, and at the same time, park, hospital, and general cemetery of the besieged" (026-008).

## Location

Coso is situated in Central Saragossa, forming the wide road that outlines the boundary between the old part of the town and the eastern quarters (013-006).

## Role in the Siege

### A Central Artery Under Fire

From the earliest days of the siege, the Coso was a focal point of urban warfare. The fighting "began in the quarter of the Coso, and in the streets thereabouts" (002-025).

As the bombardment intensified, the avenue became a place of constant danger: "We ran swiftly into the Coso, where we saw that innumerable bombs were being hurled upon the unhappy city" (016-032).

### Place of Daily Life Amid War

Even under bombardment, the Coso remained the center of civilian and military life. The commissary distributed rations there (018-013), and open-air masses were celebrated: "in the morning we heard mass in the Coso" (020-001).

Citizens gathered seeking food and news. Gabriel met a hungry, weeping child near the Coso (024-016), and later "directed my steps towards the centre of the Coso, because they said that there they were giving out something to eat" (024-017).

### The Cross of the Coso and Popular Resistance

One of the most vivid episodes of popular fervor occurred at the cross in the Coso, where a man "climbed the cross in the Coso, where he began a harangue, saying that he was the River Ebro, and he would run over the city and put out the fire" (002-025). This moment captures the blend of desperation and theatrical defiance that characterized Saragossan resistance.

### The Final Barricade

In the closing stages of the siege, the Coso became the last line of defense. The vast edifice of San Francisco, "situated in the middle of the Coso, gave an incontestable superiority to the side which occupied it" (028-001). Don José de Montoria fought at the barricade of La Cruz del Coso until wounded (028-003).

When the French finally advanced across it, the fighting was house-to-house: "Half the Coso was hers, but Spain retreated only to the opposite pavement" (029-003). The final survivors filed along the Coso in the last hours: "along the Coso filed the last combatants, one for every thousand of those who had faced the bullets and the epidemic" (030-019).
