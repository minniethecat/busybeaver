# French Army

## Overview

The **French Army** in the novel is the besieging force that encircles saragossa during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]]. Commanded by [[marshal-lannes|Marshal Lannes]], it represents the might of napoleonic France — disciplined, well-equipped, and relentless — but also the moral blindness of imperial conquest applied to urban warfare. Throughout the narrative, the French Army is present as a constant, impersonal threat: artillery fire, [[french-mine-warfare|sapping]] operations, and the tightening ring of encirclement that slowly starves the city.

## Composition

The French force at Saragossa numbered approximately 40,000 troops, drawn from various corps of [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s Grande Armée. Galdós does not enumerate specific regiments, but alludes to the presence of infantry, artillery, and engineering units — sappers, in particular, feature prominently in the [[french-mine-warfare|mining operations]]. The army's composition reflects napoleonic military organization: professional soldiers complemented by specialist engineers whose expertise in [[siege-warfare|siege]] warfare proved decisive. The novel also references French-allied troops from various European territories, reflecting the multinational character of [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s forces.

## Historical Context

The French Army that besieged Saragossa was part of [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s Peninsular campaign, which aimed to subdue Spain and place Joseph [[napoleon|Bonaparte]] on the Spanish throne. [[marshal-lannes|Marshal Lannes]], one of [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s most capable commanders, was assigned to take Saragossa after an earlier [[siege-warfare|siege]] (summer 1808) had failed. The [[siege-warfare|siege]] of December 1808 – February 1809 was notable for the ferocity of the French assault and the stubbornness of Spanish resistance. While Galdós depicts the French primarily from the Spanish perspective — as an implacable, dehumanized threat — his novel also contains moments of recognition: the French soldiers who admired Spanish courage, the individual soldier Lindener who is remembered as a man rather than an enemy. This ambivalence reflects the complex Spanish memory of the Napoleonic Wars, in which French occupation was brutal but also introduced liberal ideas that would transform Spanish society.

## Role in the Siege

The French Army's presence in the novel is felt before it is seen. From the opening pages, the bombardment is a constant:

> The bombs fell like hail; the sick, seeing them fall at every step, went singing, laughing, and dancing. (002-023)

The French assault begins with methodical pressure on Saragossa's outer resistances:

> The French had assaulted with great vigor the positions of the Castle Aljafería. (006-001)

As [[siege-warfare|the siege]] progresses, their tactics evolve:

> The French, impatient to defeat us, began the great bombardment. (004-014)

The narrator acknowledges the French Army's skill even while condemning its purpose. A notable moment of respect occurs when the French admiration for Spanish courage is recorded:

> "I will give you clothes," said the French in the first siege, admiring the naked defenders. (004-009)

The French advance through the city is imagined as a tightening circle:

> This terrible circle which the French have drawn about us. In the opening of the siege… (005-007)

The novel captures the French Army's psychological evolution — from confidence to frustration to grim determination. The approach of the main army is a moment of existential dread:

> And talk of the approach of the French army; but it was not an incontrovertible fact. (004-013)

In a final twist, Galdós humanizes the enemy through the figure of an individual soldier — [[don-carlos-lindener|Carlos Lindener]], a German serving in French ranks — suggesting that even the imperial army is composed of men caught in forces larger than themselves:

> "Don Carlos Lindener, is in the French army? I am going to see him. I remember him well." (027-008)
