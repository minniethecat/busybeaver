# Resistance

## Overview

Resistance in *Saragossa* refers to the city's organized and spontaneous defense against the French [[siege-warfare|siege]]. Unlike conventional military resistance conducted by professional soldiers, Saragossa's defense involved the entwrath population—peasants, clergy, students, women, and children—transforming the city into a single fighting organism. The resistance was so effective that it infuriated [[napoleon|Napoleon]] himself.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel depicts resistance at multiple scales: from the strategic decisions of commanders to the street-by-street, house-by-house fighting that characterized the later stages of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s personal attention underscores the international significance:

> Napoleon in Paris, knowing the resistance shown by this city in the first siege, was furious with Lefebre Desnouettes because he assaulted the plaza by the Portillo and the Castle Aljafería. He called for a plan of Saragossa, and they gave it to him, and he showed that the city should be attacked by Santa Engracia. (010-004)

The physical infrastructure of resistance—fortifications, batteries, redoubts—is described with technical detail, but the novel emphasizes that the true strength of the defense was human:

> After four hours of incessant fire by powerful artillery, they were not able to open a breach. (010-014)

## Key Passages

As resistance becomes untenable, the novel depicts the heartbreaking moment when holding out ceases to be meaningful:

> "Manuela Sancho," I exclaimed, placing my hand upon the head of the heroic girl, "resistance is no longer of any use. The next house is already destroyed by the batteries of San José, and the balls are already beginning to fall upon the roof of this. Let us go." (017-017)

The house-to-house fighting in the [[calle-de-pabostre|Calle de Pabostre]] shows resistance reduced to its most elemental form:

> We had no time for rest. The two cannons that raked the Calle de Pabostre and the angle of the Puerta Quemada were left entirely without men. Some of us ran to serve them, and the rest occupied houses in the Calle de Palomar. (018-003)

Even as defeat becomes certain, resistance continues through civilian infrastructure—barricades of rubble, furniture thrown from balconies, and the conversion of every building into a fortress:

> We toiled with frenzied ardor in these various tasks. The fighting was least difficult of all. From inside the houses we threw down over the balconies all the furniture and movables. (018-003)

## Historical Context

The resistance of Saragossa became a foundational myth of Spanish national identity during the Peninsular War. It demonstrated that popular resistance—not just professional armies—could challenge napoleonic forces. The urban warfare tactics pioneered in Saragossa prefigured later guerrilla strategies.
