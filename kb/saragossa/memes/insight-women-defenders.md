# Women in the Defense of Saragossa

## Thesis

One of the most striking features of Galdós's *Saragossa* is the prominence it gives to female actors in a military narrative that, by the conventions of its genre, should belong overwhelmingly to men. From [[agustina-de-aragon|Agustina de Aragón]] firing the cannon to [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] defying her father, from [[manuela-sancho|Manuela Sancho]]'s battlefield inspiration to the [[countess-de-bureta|Countess de Bureta]]'s organizational leadership, the women of *Saragossa* are not peripheral figures but central agents of the city's resistance. This essay argues that Galdós uses his female characters to challenge the gendered assumptions of heroic narrative — and that the novel's treatment of women reveals the deepest tensions in its vision of patriotism, sacrifice, and community.

## Agustina de Aragón: The Canonized Exception

[[agustina-de-aragon|Agustina de Aragón]] is the novel's most famous female figure — and the one most easily assimilated to traditional heroic narrative. Her act of firing the cannon after the male gunners have fallen is framed as a moment of individual valor that transcends gender. Galdós presents Agustina as proof that patriotic [[courage]] knows no sex.

Yet the very exceptionalism of her portrayal — she is "the one glorious exception to her sex" (009-039) — contains an implicit limitation. By framing Agustina as exceptional, the narrative risks reinforcing the norm she ostensibly challenges: women can be heroes, but only by becoming honorary men, by stepping into the roles that men have vacated.

## Manuela Sancho and Collective Female Heroism

[[manuela-sancho|Manuela Sancho]] represents a different model of female heroism — one rooted in collective action rather than individual exception. Her inspiration of Gabriel in battle is not a moment of solitary glory but an instance of the diffuse, distributed courage that characterized the city's defense. Where Agustina fires the cannon, Manuela embodies the less spectacular but equally essential work of sustaining morale, nursing the wounded, and maintaining the social fabric under bombardment.

The novel's references to women fighting alongside men — sometimes unnamed, mentioned only in passing — suggest a broader reality that Agustina's individual fame obscures. The women of Saragossa carried ammunition, tended wounds, brought food to the barricades, and in many cases took up arms themselves.

## Mariquilla Candiola: Love as Resistance

[[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] occupies the novel's most complex negotiation of gender and agency. As the daughter of the despised miser [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]], she is doubly constrained — by her father's authority and by the social expectations placed on young women. Her love for [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] is an act of defiance that carries political as well as personal significance: in choosing a Montoria, she chooses the side of patriotic sacrifice against her father's hoarding individualism.

Mariquilla's tragedy — she dies in the destruction of her father's house — is arguably the novel's most devastating commentary on the cost of war. Unlike Agustina, whose death would be heroic, Mariquilla's death is simply wasteful. She is collateral damage, a victim of the collective [[wrath]] that her father's selfishness helped provoke. Through Mariquilla, Galdós insists that the siege's casualties include not only soldiers but the innocent, not only men but women, not only bodies but the possibility of love itself.

> Different from most women, like few among them, that soul would not readily change, except for just and righteous reasons.
>
> (007-012)

## The Countess de Bureta and Elite Women's Patriotism

The [[countess-de-bureta|Countess de Bureta]] represents yet another model: the aristocratic woman whose patriotism takes organizational form. Her leadership in sustaining the city's morale — through charity, through example, through the visible performance of shared sacrifice — demonstrates that female agency in the siege was not limited to physical combat. The Countess's patriotism is expressed through the traditionally feminine domains of care and community, but Galdós presents these as no less essential to the defense than military action.

> And behind a barricade made by herself, awaited them, furious, gun in hand, the Countess de Bureta.
>
> (002-025)

## Conclusion

The women of *Saragossa* collectively challenge the assumption that war — and war literature — is an exclusively masculine domain. Galdós's inclusion of female characters across the social spectrum reflects the historical reality of the siege, in which women participated at every level. But it also reflects a novelist's understanding that the siege's human meaning cannot be captured through military narrative alone. The women of Saragossa are not ornaments to a masculine story; they are its moral center.
