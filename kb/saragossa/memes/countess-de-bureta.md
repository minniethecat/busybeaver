# Countess de Bureta

## Overview

The Countess de Bureta was a Spanish noblewoman who became a celebrated figure in Saragossa's defence. Galdós presents her as an exemplar of aristocratic patriotism — a woman who set aside the privileges of her class to fight alongside common citizens in the street battles against the French.

## Role in the Narrative

The Countess appears at key moments in the novel, illustrating her fierce commitment to the resistance. In the first [[siege-warfare|siege]], she is described as a combatant who built her own barricade:

> "And behind a barricade made by herself, awaited them, furious, gun in hand, the Countess de Bureta." (002-025)

This image of a noblewoman wielding a firearm at a street barricade is one of the novel's most striking vignettes. It appears in a passage where the beggar [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]] catalogues the defenders who fought in the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] quarter, listing her alongside [[santiago-sas|Don Santiago Sas]], [[mariano-cereso|Don Mariano Cereso]], [[renovales|Renovales]], and other celebrated patriots (002-025).

Her second appearance reinforces her civic virtue. During the desperate house-to-house combat, [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] rebukes citizens for failing to transport furniture to barricades, using the Countess as a benchmark of selfless action:

> "Look! In the street below is the Countess de Bureta with a bed on her shoulders, and her two maids carrying a wounded soldier on a cot." (012-012)

The image is carefully crafted: a titled lady physically carrying a bed — heavy, awkward, and demeaning labour for a woman of her station — while her servants tend to the wounded.

## Organization of Aid

Beyond her personal feats of arms, the Countess used her household as a center of support for the [[resistance]]. A character delivering supplies to the defenders mentions receiving gifts "in the house of that most excellent lady, the Countess de Bureta" (009-013). Her home became an informal commissary and infirmary, channeling provisions and medical care to the defenders — a role that was as vital to the city's endurance as the fighting on the walls. This dual contribution — both martial and logistical — made her a symbol of the total mobilization that characterized Saragossa's defense.

## Key Relationships and Legacy

The Countess was part of a network of women who sustained the siege effort. Her generosity extended to all ranks, and her willingness to perform physical labor alongside commoners dissolved the social barriers that normally separated the aristocracy from the populace. She stands alongside [[manuela-sancho|Manuela Sancho]] and [[agustina-de-aragon|Agustina de Aragón]] as one of the women whose courage defined the Saragossan resistance.

## Historical Context

The Countess de Bureta is one of several historical women who participated in the defence of Saragossa. The presence of upper-class women fighting and labouring alongside commoners was a significant element of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s mythology, illustrating how the emergency dissolved social hierarchies and united all classes against the invader.
