# Volunteers of Huesca

## Overview

The **Volunteers of Huesca** were a battalion of militia drawn from the Aragonese city of [[huesca|Huesca]], who fought as part of the Spanish [[resistance|defense]] of Saragossa during the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege]]. Under the command of [[villacampa|Don Pedro Villacampa]], they became one of the most active and celebrated units in the city's [[resistance|defense]], repeatedly distinguishing themselves in counter-sorties, defensive stands, and the grim house-to-house combat of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s final phase.

## Role in the Siege

The Volunteers of Huesca were citizen-soldiers — men drawn from the towns and villages of the [[huesca|Huesca]] region, not professional soldiers. Their composition reflected the broader pattern of the Peninsular War, where local militias supplemented and often outperformed regular army units. The battalion also included volunteers from the broader Aragón region:

> The volunteers of Aragón and Huesca, taking the chance of advantage which the confusion of the French offered. (008-007)

They are first mentioned among the forces awaiting French assault:

> Under his commands, the volunteers of Huesca, of whom Don Pedro Villacampa is leader. (006-002)

They distinguished themselves in the counter-sorties that checked French advances:

> While the volunteers of Huesca, the grenadiers of Palafox, and other troops, who were there assembled, attacked the enemy's works. (008-013)

During the pivotal [[resistance|defense]] of the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] monastery, they bore the heaviest burden:

> To tremble. The volunteers of Huesca defended it vigorously; and, when they could no longer hold the ruins, they fell back fighting. (018-003)

The [[assault-on-santa-monica|assault on Las Mónicas]] saw their finest hour:

> While they were doing this, the men of Huesca who still survived, placed themselves in the most dangerous positions. (020-006)

Their commander's praise, delivered amid the carnage, carries the weight of earned respect:

> "The volunteers of Huesca have not borne themselves badly today." (020-009)

In [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s final days, the battalion was reduced to a remnant:

> "The few soldiers of the Huesca battalion who survive think they can still hold this position." (021-030)

Their last stand at Las Mónicas became legend:

> The conduct of the volunteers of Huesca, who defended Las Mónicas until not a man remained standing. (022-013)

## Historical Notes

The Volunteers of Huesca were a historical unit that participated in the actual [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]]. The city of Huesca, located approximately 70 kilometers northeast of Saragossa, contributed militia to the [[resistance|defense]] in response to [[palafox|Palafox]]'s call for volunteers from across Aragón. Galdós's portrayal — the unit's courage, its gradual attrition, and its commander [[villacampa|Villacampa]]'s laconic praise — reflects documented accounts of militia performance during [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. The battalion's near-total destruction at Las Mónicas exemplified the fate of many volunteer units that entered Saragossa's [[resistance|defense]] with high patriotic fervor and were consumed by [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s attritional brutality.
