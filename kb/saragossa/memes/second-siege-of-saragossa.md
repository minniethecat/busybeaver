# Second Siege of Saragossa

## Overview

The second siege of Saragossa (December 21, 1808 – February 20, 1809) was the decisive phase of the French campaign to capture the city. After failing to take Saragossa in the summer of 1808, Napoleon dispatched Marshal Moncey and later Marshal Lannes with over forty thousand troops to reduce the city. The defenders, numbering approximately thirty thousand including armed civilians, held out for two months before epidemic typhus, starvation, and relentless bombardment forced capitulation. The siege remains one of the most celebrated episodes of Spanish resistance during the Napoleonic Wars (006-021) and forms the dramatic backbone of Galdós's novel *Saragossa: A Story of Spanish Valor* (003-002).

## Historical Background

The second siege was part of Napoleon's broader campaign to subdue the Iberian Peninsula. The first siege (June–August 1808) had ended in French withdrawal, a rare humiliation that stiffened Spanish resolve. Napoleon, recognizing Saragossa's symbolic importance, committed overwhelming force to the second attempt. The French employed systematic approaches — mining, parallel trenches, and concentrated artillery — that reduced the city block by block.

The defenders' experience anticipated the urban warfare of later centuries. Barricades were constructed from rubble and furniture; communication relied on runners and coded signals; disease spread rapidly in the crowded, unsanitary conditions of a city under bombardment. Typhus killed more defenders than French action, and by February 1809 the garrison was too weakened to continue (003-003).

## Narrative Depiction

Galdós's novel focuses almost exclusively on the second siege, using it as both setting and subject. The narrative follows [[gabriel-araceli]] through the city's progressive destruction, from the initial confidence of the defenders to the harrowing final weeks when epidemic and hunger became deadlier than French bombardment.

The author depicts the siege as a total war that erased distinctions between soldier and civilian. Women carried ammunition and fought on the barricades; priests administered last rites in collapsed churches; children ran messages through artillery fire. The novel's most powerful passages describe the incremental ruin of the city — streets reduced to rubble, convents turned into makeshift forts, and the relentless attrition of hope.

Key episodes include the [[december-31-sortie]], the [[assault-on-monte-torrero]], the destruction of [[santa-engracia]], and the defense of [[san-jose]]. Each location becomes a character in its own right, its fate mirroring the city's trajectory toward surrender (009-001).

## Outcome and Aftermath

Saragossa capitulated on February 20, 1809. Approximately fifty-four thousand of the city's inhabitants — over half the pre-siege population — perished from combat, disease, and starvation. The French took control of the ruined city, but their victory was pyrrhic: the siege had consumed troops and time that Napoleon could ill afford, and the legend of Saragossa's resistance inspired further uprisings across Spain.

Within the novel, the fall of the city marks the culmination of the narrative and the breaking point for many characters. [[gabriel-araceli]]'s survival and escape provide the frame for his retrospective narration, while the fate of [[mariquilla-candiola]] and her father crystallizes the human cost of the siege.
