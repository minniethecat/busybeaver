Valencia is a major city and historic kingdom on the eastern coast of Spain. During the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|Second Siege of Saragossa]], volunteers from Valencia fought alongside the Aragonese defenders in the city's desperate resistance against the [[french-army|French]] besiegers.

## The Valencia Volunteers

The Valencia troops were commanded by [[renovales|Don Mariano Renovales]], a distinguished veteran of the [[first-siege-of-saragossa|First Siege]] who "distinguished himself so highly in the other siege" and now commanded "the troops of Orihuela and of Valencia" (006-005).

Renovales and his men were stationed at the fortress of [[san-jose|San José]], defending the position alongside the Huesca volunteers, the [[walloon-guards|Walloon Guards]], and militia from Soria. The composite force at San José reflected the diverse regional origins of the defenders who converged on Saragossa (010-014).

## San José Fortress

The fortress of San José, though more formidable in aspect than the [[redoubt-del-pilar|redoubt del Pilar]], had less power of resistance because it presented a broader target for the enemy's fires. The French attacked it with the same fury they directed at the redoubt del Pilar, subjecting both positions to simultaneous assault.

Under Renovales's command, the Valencia and Huesca volunteers held San José through some of the heaviest fighting of the siege. Their stand exemplified the broader Spanish solidarity against [[napoleon|Napoleon's]] invasion — soldiers from distant regions converging to defend a city that was not their own.
> With some of the soldiers of Olivenza and part of those of Valencia, we
advanced by the Madrid road straight towards the French lines. The
skirmishing parties were on both sides of the road when the enemy
became aware of our presence, and now we were quicker than deer in
doing up the first troop of French infantry which came to meet us.
>
> (008-010)


## Significance

The presence of Valencian volunteers at Saragossa showed the national character of the resistance. Though Valencia was far from the immediate theater of operations, its citizens answered the call to defend a sister city.

The troops of Valencia and Orihuela, fighting alongside [[aragon|Aragonese]] militia under a commander tested in the first siege, embodied the pan-Spanish unity that Napoleon's invasion had paradoxically forged. Their stand contributed to the legendary defense that made the siege a European cause célèbre.
