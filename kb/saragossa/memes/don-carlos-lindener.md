## Role in the Narrative

Don Carlos Lindener is a Swiss captain whose shifting allegiances illuminate the complex international dimensions of the Peninsular War. He served in Saragossa during the year before [[siege-warfare|the siege]], where he came to know [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]], don-jeronimo-candiolaly merchant who is [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]]'s father and the novel's most ambivalent civilian figure.(027-008)

During [[siege-warfare|the siege]], Lindener reappears — but now in the French army. [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]], fleeing harassment from his fellow Saragossans, crosses the French lines seeking him out. He tells his daughter [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]]: "Do you know that my friend who served last year in Saragossa, the Swiss Captain Don Carlos Lindener, is in the French army? I am going to see him. I remember that he owes me a certain amount."(027-008)

## Candiola's Crossing

[[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s journey to find Lindener is one of the novel's most morally fraught episodes. [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]] fears that her father will be branded a traitor for crossing into French lines, though she cannot bring herself to believe him capable of treason: "I am [[fear|afraid]] that if his enemies know that he crossed over into the French lines, they will call him a traitor. I do not know whether it is the great affection that I have for him, but he seems to me incapable of such action."(027-008)

The claim that Lindener "owes me a certain amount" is characteristically [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] — reducing even a wartime border crossing to a matter of accounts receivable. Whether [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] genuinely seeks repayment, or uses the debt as a pretext for collaboration, remains ambiguous.

## Swiss Soldiers in the Napoleonic Wars

Lindener's presence in both Saragossa and the French army reflects the historical reality of Swiss mercenaries and officers serving across European armies during the napoleonic era. Swiss regiments fought on multiple sides of the Peninsular War, and individual officers frequently moved between employers. Lindener's trajectory — from Spanish service to French — would not have been unusual for a Swiss professional soldier of the period.
