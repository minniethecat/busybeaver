# Las Mónicas

**Las Mónicas** (the Convent of Santa Mónica) was one of the principal convent-fortresses in the Las Tenerías suburb of Saragossa during the Second Siege. Together with San Augustine, it formed the backbone of the eastern defensive perimeter and was the scene of some of the most ferocious fighting of the siege.

## Fortification

The convent was converted into a military stronghold alongside [[San Augustine]]. As the siege progressed, both edifices became "veritable fortresses" (015-005). The defensive line running through Las Tenerías passed through the battery of Palafox, the mill of Goicoechea, the garden of San Augustine, and onward to the orchard of Las Mónicas (015-007).

## The Assault

The French directed their eastern artillery against Las Mónicas, discharging "solid balls upon the weak walls of Las Mónicas, and the fortifications of earth and brick of the oil mill, and the battery of Palafox" (019-009). The bombardment opened three great breaches in the walls, and an assault became imminent. The French had already seized the Goicoechea mill the day before.

## Fall of Las Mónicas

The convent became the focal point of extraordinary resistance. After intense fighting, the influential citizens of Saragossa — the Montorias, Ceresos, Sases, Salameros, and San Clementes — gathered at Las Mónicas and "showed such tremendous courage and uttered such contempt of the enemy that it roused the spirits of all who saw and heard them" (019-022).

Despite the heroic defence, the position eventually became untenable. [[Don José de Montoria]] himself acknowledged that "defence was no longer possible in Las Mónicas" (020-003). The convent passed into French hands, and the defenders shifted their focus to defending [[San Augustine]] next door:

> "In this way the convent of Las Mónicas passed into the hands of the French." (020-004)

## Aftermath

After taking Las Mónicas, the French used it as a base to assault San Augustine, blowing up "the middle walls on the side towards Las Mónicas" (021-013) to gain entry into the neighbouring convent.
