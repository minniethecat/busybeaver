# Surrender

## Overview

Surrender in *Saragossa* is not a military option but a moral impossibility. The novel repeatedly dramatizes the rejection of surrender as a defining characteristic of the city's identity. When the French offer terms, the Saragossans respond with defiance that transforms their material poverty into spiritual superiority. Surrender becomes the line that separates Saragossa from every other [[siege-warfare|besieged city]] in history.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The theme of surrender appears early, when [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] recounts the fall of Madrid and [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]]'s reaction:

> We told him the story of the surrender of Madrid, and as this seemed to depress him very much, we praised the deeds done at Saragossa. (003-004)

The contrast between Madrid's surrender and Saragossa's resistance establishes the city's exceptionalism. The novel's most famous exchange distills this ethos:

> "Surrender, and we will give you clothes," said the French in the first siege, admiring the constancy of a few poor countrymen dressed in rags. (004-009)

## Key Passages

The Saragossan response to the French offer is iconic:

> "We do not know how to surrender," they made answer; "and our bodies shall be clothed with glory." (004-010)

As [[siege-warfare|the siege]] wears on, the inevitability of surrender becomes harder to deny, yet the defenders hold out:

> In this situation, surrender was inevitable sooner or later. Indeed we were at the mercy of the French arms as a ship at the mercy of the waves of the ocean. (011-003)

## Related Characters

- [[Don José de Montoria]] — disturbed by Madrid's surrender, resolute about Saragossa
- [[gabriel-araceli]] — narrator who brings news of Madrid's fall
- [[Palafox]] — general who refused all terms of surrender

## Historical Context

Saragossa's refusal to surrender, despite overwhelming odds, made it a symbol of Spanish resistance during the Peninsular War. The phrase "We do not know how to surrender" became legendary, encapsulating the city's defiance. The historical reality was grim: the city eventually fell in February 1809 after disease and [[hunger|starvation]] made further resistance impossible.
