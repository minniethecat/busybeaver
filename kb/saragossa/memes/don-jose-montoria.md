# Don José de Montoria

## Overview

Don José de Montoria is one of the central supporting characters in *Saragossa*. A wealthy landowner and citizen of Saragossa, he embodies the patriotic sacrifice demanded by [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. He cuts down his own olive trees for the city's [[resistance|defense]], hosts strategists in his home, and shames those who shirk their duty. His character arc culminates in profound personal tragedy.

## Biography / Background

Don José is a landowner of substantial means, with properties on both sides of the Huerva River. He is married to Doña Leocadia and is the father of [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]], whom he has destined for the priesthood. His social standing places him among the civic elite of Saragossa, and his home becomes a gathering point for defenders and refugees during both sieges. His wealth is rooted in land—olive groves and orchards—rather than commerce or usury, distinguishing him from figures like [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]].

## Role in the Narrative

Don José serves as a moral anchor in the novel. He is introduced as the host whom [[don-roque|Don Roque]] seeks out upon the group's arrival in Saragossa. His generosity is immediate—he offers money and shelter—but his true character is revealed through his uncompromising commitment to the city's [[resistance|defense]].

He personally destroys his own property for military necessity:

> Montoria was occupied, when we arrived, in cutting down olive-trees on his place, a proceeding demanded by the military exigencies of the plan of defence. (003-002)

> "In the first siege I cut down my trees on my property on the other side of the Huerva; but this second siege that is being prepared for us is going to be much more terrible." (003-003)

His view of [[epidemic|the epidemic]] as psychological rather than medical reveals his belief in the primacy of will:

> "I don't know what to think of this thing which the doctors call the epidemic. I call it fear, sirs, pure fear." (014-024)

Montoria's most dramatic moment comes when he leads the mob to Candiola's house, demanding the miser contribute to the defense. This confrontation—between patriotic sacrifice and private hoarding—is one of the novel's central moral tableaux. Montoria's righteousness, however, is complicated by his role in the destruction of the Candiola household and the eventual death of Mariquilla.

## Key Relationships

- [[augustine-montoria]] — his son, destined for priesthood but drawn to battle and love. Don José's patriarchal authority is tested by Augustine's defiance.
- [[dona-leocadia]] — his wife, who mourns their son's death and represents the domestic cost of patriotic sacrifice.
- [[don-roque]] — old friend who brings [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] to his household.
- [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]] — his ideological and personal enemy. Their conflict embodies the novel's central tension between communal sacrifice and individual self-interest.

## Historical Context

Don José de Montoria is a fictional character, but he represents the class of Spanish landowners whose property was directly impacted by the Peninsular War. The practice of cutting down trees and demolishing buildings to create fields of fire was a standard defensive measure in siege warfare of the period. Galdós uses Montoria to explore the patriotic ideal of the *vecino honrado* (honorable citizen)—the property-owning male whose willingness to sacrifice private wealth for public defense was the ideological foundation of Spanish liberal nationalism.
