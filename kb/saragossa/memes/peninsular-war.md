# Peninsular War

The Peninsular War (1807–1814) was the great conflict fought on the Iberian Peninsula between Napoleonic [[french-forces|France]] and the allied forces of Spain, Portugal, and the United Kingdom. The [[first-siege-of-saragossa|two sieges of Saragossa]] were among its most famous episodes.

## Historical Context

Napoleon Bonaparte's invasion of Spain in 1808 triggered a war that would bleed French resources for six years and earn the name "the Spanish Ulcer." What began as a French attempt to place Napoleon's brother Joseph on the Spanish throne became a protracted struggle marked by conventional battles, guerrilla warfare, and iconic urban resistance.

The author of *Saragossa*, [[benito-perez-galdos]], opens his narrative with a reflection on the war's place in history:

> "When the other events of the Spanish war shall be lost in the obscurity of time, or only traced by disconnected fragments, the story of Saragossa will remain." (P02-005)

## The Saragossa Campaign

Saragossa occupied a strategic position on the Ebro River, commanding the route between Madrid and the French border. Its resistance became a symbol of Spanish determination. The [[first-siege-of-saragossa|First Siege]] (June–August 1808) saw the city repel a French assault under General Verdier, while the [[fall-of-zaragoza|Second Siege]] (December 1808–February 1809) ended in the city's destruction but cemented its legendary status.

## Legacy

The Peninsular War devastated Spain but also forged a national identity rooted in popular resistance. The events at Saragossa — where civilians, clergy, and soldiers fought house-to-house — became emblematic of this spirit, memorialized in art, literature, and national memory.
