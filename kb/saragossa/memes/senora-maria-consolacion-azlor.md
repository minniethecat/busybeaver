# Señora María Consolación Azlor y Villavicencio

Señora María Consolación Azlor y Villavicencio was a Spanish noblewoman of Saragossa noted for her public encouragement of the city's defenders during the siege. She is associated with the Countess of Bureta in the narrative.

## Role in the Siege

The beggar who guides [[gabriel-araceli]] through the city mentions her among those who raised civilian morale:

> "The Señora Maria Consolación Azlor y Villavicencio, who lives near the Ecce Homo, also walked through the streets, saying words of good cheer to those who were discouraged." (002-027)

## Social Position and Residence

Azlor belonged to the upper ranks of Saragossan society. Her full name — Señora María Consolación Azlor y Villavicencio — marks her as a member of the Spanish nobility, and her association with the Countess of Bureta places her within the circle of aristocratic women who remained in the city during the siege rather than fleeing to the countryside.

The reference to her living "near the Ecce Homo" (002-027) situates her within a specific neighborhood of the besieged city — the Ecce Homo was a shrine or convent in Saragossa. This detail suggests that she chose to remain at her residence rather than evacuate, making her rounds of encouragement from her own doorstep while the French bombardment continued around her.

Unlike [[agustina-de-aragon]], who took up arms at the Portillo battery, Azlor's contribution was moral rather than military. Her act of walking the streets and "saying words of good cheer to those who were discouraged" (002-027) represents a form of civilian leadership — sustaining the morale that allowed Saragossa to resist for months against overwhelming French forces — no less vital than combat service.

## Historical Significance

She represents the active participation of Saragossan women of all social classes in the city's defense. Alongside figures like the [[countess-de-bureta|Countess of Bureta]] and [[agustina-de-aragon|Agustina de Aragón]], she contributed to the distinctive character of Saragossa's resistance, where civilians — and particularly women — played vital roles in sustaining morale.
