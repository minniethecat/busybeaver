# Grief

## Overview

Grief is the emotional aftermath of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]—the private cost paid by individuals for the collective glory. While the novel celebrates heroismism and resistance, it does not shy away from the devastating personal losses that accumulate as [[siege-warfare|the siege]] grinds toward its conclusion. Parents mourn children, widows lose husbands, and the city itself becomes a landscape of grief.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The most concentrated depiction of grief comes near the novel's end, when [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] confronts the death of his son. The grief is physical, almost beyond language:

> Doña Leocadia, who had no life left except for weeping, was kissing the cold body of her son. Don José, trying to subdue the manifestations of his own grief, rose and said in a firm voice. (025-028)

The grief ripples outward to the widow of Manuel, whose cry is "heartrending":

> At the same moment we heard a heartrending cry not far from there, and a woman came running in anguish towards us. It was the wife of the unfortunate Manuel, now widowed and childless. (025-031)

## Key Passages

[[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]]'s grief takes a different form—a nervous overflow bordering on madness, as his life's possessions are destroyed:

> Candiola's distress and moral suffering made it seem as if he had a nervous disorder. It was plain to be seen that terror and grief had completely upset him. (019-013)

## Related Characters

- [[Don José de Montoria]] — father who loses his son
- [[dona-leocadia]] — mother consumed by grief
- [[don-jeronimo-candiola]] — miser whose grief is for lost possessions as well as lost family

## Historical Context

The civilian death toll of the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] of Saragossa was catastrophic—perhaps 50,000 of the city's 100,000 inhabitants perished. The scale of grief was unprecedented in modern European urban warfare, prefiguring the civilian casualties of 20th-century total war.
