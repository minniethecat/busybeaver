## Role in the Narrative

Sursum Corda is a crippled patriot who serves as an eyewitness narrator of the heroismic [[resistance|defense]] of Saragossa. He first encounters [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] and [[don-roque|Don Roque]] when they arrive in the city, introducing himself by his given name Pepe Pallejas, though he is universally known by his ecclesiastical nickname "Sursum Corda" from his twenty-four years as sacristan of the Church of Jesus, where he used to sing the liturgical phrase.(002-019)
> For you must know that I am Pepe Pallejas, and they call me Sursum
Corda, as twenty-four years ago I was sacristan of the Church of Jesus,
and I used to sing — But this is not coming to the point, and I was
going on to say I am Sursum Corda, and perhaps you have heard about
me in Madrid?
>
> (002-019)


Despite his physical disability — he walks on crutches — Sursum Corda possesses remarkable agility and a fierce [[patriotism|patriotic spirit]]. He guides newcomers through the devastated streets of Saragossa, narrating the deeds of its defenders with vivid, breathless enthusiasm. [[don-roque|Don Roque]], though impatient with his digressions, acknowledges having heard of him in Madrid, indicating his [[glory|renown]].(002-020)

During the August 4 attack, Sursum Corda personally witnessed the [[resistance|defense]] of the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] quarter. His account describes the bombardment as a "furnace" where bombs and hand-grenades "rained down" but the patriots "did not mind them any more than so many drops of water." He recounts how [[don-antonio-quadros|Don Antonio Quadros]] stuffed bags of wool into a breach in the wall before being fatally shot, and how [[pepillo-ruiz|Pepillo Ruiz]] calmly walked up to spike an enemy cannon "as if he was promenading in a garden among butterflies and may flowers, only here the butterflies were bullets, and the flowers were bombs."(002-023)

Sursum Corda reappears throughout [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] recalls "the phrase of the beggar Sursum Corda" that souls were "turned into bullets" during the street fighting.(023-002) In the later stages, [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] dwrathctly asks Sursum Corda to lend him his crutches, recognizing the patriot's eagerness to contribute but his physical limitations.(028-034) In the final chaotic days, [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] encounters him again, asking what is happening.(030-008)

## Character and Patriotism

Sursum Corda embodies the popular patriotism of Saragossa's common citizens. Though a beggar with no military rank or social standing, his soul is consumed with the [[resistance|defense]] of his city. [[don-roque|Don Roque]] acknowledges that his tales inflame "the most pure patriotism" in his listeners.(002-024) His narrative function is to transform the chaos of battle into legend, celebrating the ordinary Spaniards who performed extraordinary deeds.
