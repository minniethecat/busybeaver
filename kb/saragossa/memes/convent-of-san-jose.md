The **Convent of San José** was a religious building in Saragossa that became a key defensive position during the second siege (1808–1809). It was the target of a major French assault early in the siege.

## French Attack

The French launched a concentrated attack on the convent during the early stages of the siege. Gabriel de Araceli anticipated the assault: "Either I am greatly deceived, or they are now going to attack San José" (006-003). The prediction proved correct: "the convent of San José was attacked by a strong column of French infantry" (006-007).

## Defensive Response

The initial assault was repulsed. The narrator describes the outcome plainly: "Such was the attack upon San José, a futile attempt quickly punished" (006-008). The convent held firm against the French column.

## Position in the Defense Line

San José formed part of the defensive perimeter. Celebratory signals spread from the convent after successful actions: "demonstrations of rejoicing, and from San José all the way to the Trinitarios" (008-021). The convent's position linked it to the broader defensive network.
> From the house in the Calle de Pabostre, close to the Molino of the
city, we were firing upon the besiegers, when behold the batteries of
San José, formerly occupied in demolishing the wall, directed their
cannons against that ancient edifice. We felt that the walls were
trembling; the beams were cracking like the timbers of a ship tossed
by a tempest.
>
> (017-006)
