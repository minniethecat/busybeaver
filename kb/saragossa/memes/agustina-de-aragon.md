**Agustina de Aragón** (born Agustina Zaragoza Doménech, 1786–1857) was a Spanish heroine who gained fame during the [[first-siege-of-saragossa|First Siege of Saragossa]]. She is referenced in the narrative as an exemplar of female courage in the defense of the city.

## Legend

According to legend, during the assault on the [[portillo|Portillo]] gate on July 2, 1808, Agustina saw the Spanish artillerymen fall to French bayonets (003-013). Taking a lighted match from the hand of a dying gunner, she fired the cannon at point-blank range into the advancing French column, breaking their assault (003-013). This act made her an instant symbol of the city's popular resistance.

## Recognition

For her bravery, Agustina was commissioned as a sub-lieutenant in the Spanish army by General [[palafox|Palafox]]. She later saw action at the Battle of Vitoria (1813) and was decorated with the Shield of Honor. Her story was celebrated across Europe — [[napoleon|Napoleon]] himself, on learning of her feat, reportedly remarked on the ferocity of Spanish women in defense of their homes. Lord Byron immortalized her in *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage* as "the Maid of Saragossa." (023-008)

## In the Narrative

Agustina is mentioned alongside other figures of female heroism in the siege, including the [[countess-de-bureta|Countess de Bureta]]. Her example served as inspiration for the defenders during the second siege, embodying the spirit of total [[resistance]] that made Saragossa legendary. Her legacy stands alongside that of [[manuela-sancho|Manuela Sancho]], another woman who distinguished herself at the redoubt of [[redoubt-del-pilar|del Pilar]].
