# General Saint March

General Don Philip Saint March was a Spanish general of Irish descent who served as one of the principal military commanders during the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] of Saragossa. He commanded the forces at the Torrero redoubt at the outset of [[siege-warfare|the siege]] and later formed part of the inner council of military advisers to Captain-[[palafox|General Palafox]].

## Historical Background

Like many Spanish officers of the Napoleonic period, Saint March was of Irish descent — part of the "Wild Geese" diaspora of Irish Catholics who served in continental European armies after the Treaty of Limerick. The presence of generals named O'Neill and Saint March in the Spanish high command reflects the long tradition of Irish military service to the Spanish Crown, stretching back to the sixteenth century.

## Role in the Narrative

Saint March first appears at the beginning of the French assault, commanding — alongside General O'Neill — the ten thousand men defending the fortified positions of the Torrero: "The French had assaulted with great vigor the fortified positions of the Torrero. Ten thousand men defended them, commanded by Don Philip Saint March and by O'Neill, both generals of great merit" (006-001). The Torrero position fell after a determined [[resistance|defense]], with the Spanish troops retreating in good order across the Puente de América, carrying away all but one of their artillery pieces.

Saint March's practical judgment as a seasoned commander manifests later in [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. When the defenders of the Convent of San [[augustine-montoria|Augustine]], inspwrathd by the heroismic example of the volunteers of Huesca at Las Mónicas, are resolved to fight to the death within the crumbling building, an order arrives from outside: "An order from outside stopped us, dictated doubtless by the practical good sense of General Saint March" (022-014). The order reads: "The convent cannot be held. Instead of sacrificing men with no advantage to the city, let all go out to defend the points attacked in the [[calle-de-pabostre|Calle de Pabostre]], and the [[puerta-quemada|Puerta Quemada]]" (022-015). This intervention spares lives by preventing a futile last stand.

Saint March is named among the three generals — with O'Neill and [[general-butron|Butron]] — who, alongside Father Basilio [[father-boggiero|Boggiero]], formed the de facto governing council of Saragossa during [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. The chronicler observes: "In reality, the illustrious commander did not govern, he reigned. Father Basilio governed, with O'Neill, Saint March, and [[general-butron|Butron]], the first, an ecclesiastic, the other three noted generals" (023-018).

In the final days of [[siege-warfare|the siege]], Saint March signs the death warrant for [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]], convicted of treason for revealing a secret passage to the French. [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]], appointed to carry out the execution, cries out in [[despair|anguish]]: "Do you see this order? It is signed by General Saint March" (029-026). This act — the execution of a civilian by military authority — represents the grim terminus of command responsibility in a city pushed to extremity.
