# Mariquilla Candiola

## Overview

Mariquilla Candiola is the daughter of [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Don Jerónimo Candiola]], the wealthiest and most despised man in Saragossa. Her love for [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] places her at the intersection of private passion and public conflict. Through her eyes, the novel explores the civilian experience of [[siege-warfare|siege]]—the [[fear|terror]] of bombardment, the desire to help the suffering, and the constraints imposed by family authority.

## Biography and Background

Mariquilla grew up in the Candiola household on the Calle de Antón Trillo, a house that symbolized her father's wealth and his isolation from the community. Unlike the Montoria family, whose honor derived from generations of civic standing, the Candiolas' fortune came from commerce and money-lending—a distinction that [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] never allowed his son to forget.

She received an education appropriate to a young woman of her class, but her upbringing was marked by the social ostracism her father's miserliness attracted. This isolation left her without the networks of female friendship and mutual support that sustained other women during the siege, making her dependence on her father both economic and emotional.

## Role in the Narrative

Mariquilla's position is inherently tragic: she loves the son of her father's enemy, and her father forbids her from participating in the city's [[resistance|defense]]. Her desire to help is genuine:

> "I long earnestly to go like other women and take care of the suffering; but my father forbids me, and is angry with me whenever I propose it." (015-024)

Her prayers and fears reveal the psychological toll of [[siege-warfare|the siege]] on civilians:

> "I spend the nights praying, asking God to keep the fire away from our house. But how many unhappy ones have perished, how many houses of good people who never harmed anyone have been destroyed by the flames!" (015-024)

Galdós uses Mariquilla's constrained position to examine gender dynamics under siege conditions. While other women—the [[countess-de-bureta]], [[manuela-sancho]], the women of [[las-tenerias]]—actively participate in the defense, Mariquilla is confined by paternal decree. Her suffering is thus doubled: she endures the same bombardment as everyone else, but without the consolations of collective action and shared purpose.

Her relationship with Augustine becomes a symbolic bridge between the two feuding households. The possibility of their union represents the reconciliation that the city's defenders—divided by class and personal vendetta—desperately need but may not achieve before the siege destroys everything.

## Key Relationships

- [[don-jeronimo-candiola]] — her father, whose miserliness and social contempt isolate her from the community
- [[augustine-montoria]] — her beloved, son of her father's sworn enemy; their love defies the feud
- [[gabriel-araceli]] — a friend who witnesses her suffering and narrates her story with sympathy
- [[don-jose-montoria]] — her beloved's father, whose animosity toward the Candiolas threatens her happiness

## Historical Context

While Mariquilla is fictional, her situation illuminates the historical experience of women during the Peninsular War. Upper-class women faced particular constraints: their social position prohibited the manual labor that working-class women contributed to the defense, yet the siege made their domestic seclusion untenable. Contemporary accounts confirm that women of all classes nursed the wounded, carried supplies, and in some cases fought—but those whose families forbade such participation endured a distinctive form of helpless suffering that Galdós captures through Mariquilla.
