# Convent of Jerusalem

The **Convent of Jerusalem** was one of the principal French strongholds during the final phase of the Second Siege. Its position directly opposite the [[Convent of San Francisco]] made it a focal point of the underground mine warfare that defined the siege's closing weeks.

## French Operations Base

After capturing the convent, the French established it as their forward base for operations against San Francisco. From Jerusalem, French engineers dug extensive mining galleries beneath the intervening streets and buildings:

> "They would not be able to get here by the galleries under the Calle de Santa Engracia from the Jerusalem and the hospital, because our mine has made theirs useless." (020-007)

## Underground Warfare

The Convent of Jerusalem was the starting point for French mining operations that sought to place explosives beneath the Spanish defensive positions. The Spanish responded with counter-mines, creating a subterranean battlefield of parallel galleries. [[gabriel-araceli]] and his comrades were ordered to "guard San Francisco — a vast edifice which offered good positions for our guns against the French, who were established in the Convent of Jerusalem" (010-012).

The French also used Jerusalem to mine towards the hospital, threatening another critical Spanish-held position. The convent's capture represented a significant strategic gain for the besieging forces.

## Modern Site

Today the Convent of Jerusalem still exists in a restored condition, its façade facing towards the Hall of Independence [Footnote, Chapter 32].
