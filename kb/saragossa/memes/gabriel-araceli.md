# Gabriel Araceli

## Overview

Gabriel Araceli is the first-person narrator and protagonist of *Saragossa*. At the opening of the novel, he is one of four Spanish patriots who have escaped French captivity between Lerma and Cogollos. He arrives in Saragossa on the evening of the eighteenth, just as the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] is about to begin. Through his eyes, the reader experiences the full arc of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]: the initial enthusiasm of enlistment, the daily violence of bombardment, the camaraderie of the defenders, and the eventual devastation.

## Role in the Narrative

Gabriel serves as both participant and witness. He enlists in the city's [[resistance|defense]] and fights in major engagements, including the [[resistance|defense]] of the redoubt and the house-to-house combat in the later stages of [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. His narrative voice is distinctive for its psychological honesty—he admits fear, cowardice, and uncertainty alongside courage and resolve.

The novel begins with his arrival:

> It was, I believe, the evening of the eighteenth when we saw Saragossa in the distance. As we entered by the Puerta de Sancho we heard the clock in the Torre Nueva strike ten. (001-001)

His enlistment is driven by the infectious enthusiasm of the city's defenders:

> The spirit of the men who were enlisting filled me with such enthusiasm that nothing seemed to me so noble as to follow glory, even afar off. (004-008)

## Biography

Gabriel is a young man of uncertain social standing—[[don-roque|Don Roque]]'s exaggerated introductions claim he is "of one of the highest families of lower Andalusia" and fought at Trafalgar (003-005), though the narrator's own modesty suggests these claims are embellished. He is resourceful, having engineered the escape of his companions from French custody, and observant, with a gift for rendering the chaos of battle in vivid, psychologically acute detail.

His narrative perspective is shaped by his status as an outsider: he is not a native of Saragossa, and his gradual absorption into the city's collective identity mirrors the reader's own immersion in the world of the siege. By the novel's end, he has been transformed from a callow youth into a veteran who has witnessed the full spectrum of human behavior under extreme duress.

## Key Relationships

- [[don-roque]] — fellow escapee and traveling companion, whose bluster and loyalty provide comic relief and moral grounding
- [[augustine-montoria]] — close friend and fellow defender, whose tragic love story with [[mariquilla-candiola]] forms the novel's romantic subplot
- [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] — protector and host in Saragossa, who embodies the ideal of patriotic sacrifice
- [[manuela-sancho]] — heroic woman who inspired Gabriel in battle and represents the active role of women in the city's defense

## Historical Context

Gabriel Araceli is a fictional character, but he is also the narrative vehicle for the entire first series of Galdós's *Episodios Nacionales*. His trajectory—from anonymous youth to battle-hardened veteran—mirrors the coming-of-age narrative that Galdós uses to dramatize Spain's national awakening during the Peninsular War. The first-person retrospective narration (Gabriel tells his story from old age) frames the siege not merely as a historical event but as the formative experience of a generation.
