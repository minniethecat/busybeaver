# Hospital of Saragossa

## Overview

The Hospital of Saragossa was a large public hospital building situated between the Convent of [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] and the [[convent-of-san-francisco|Convent of San Francisco]]. During the Second Siege of Saragossa (1808–1809), it became one of the most bitterly contested positions in the city. The French captured the hospital early in February and used its ruins as a fortified position from which to threaten San Francisco and push deeper into the city center.

## Location

The hospital was located on a line running from Santa Engracia toward the [[coso|Coso]], Saragossa's central thoroughfare. It stood adjacent to the [[convent-of-jerusalem|Convent of Jerusalem]] and the Convent of San Francisco, forming part of a chain of large institutional buildings that became the frontline of the siege.

> "On the third of February, the French gained possession of the Convent of Jerusalem, which was between Santa Engracia and the hospital." (026-001)

The modern site of the hospital is occupied by the Hôtel de l'Europe.

> "The hospital occupied the place where the Hotel de l'Europe stands." (032-001)

## Role in the Siege

### French Capture

The French seized the hospital, along with the Convent of Jerusalem, in early February as part of their systematic advance through the city's religious and institutional buildings. The capture of these positions gave them a foothold from which to threaten the Convent of San Francisco — one of the most important Spanish defensive strongholds.

> "They took possession of the hospital and of the Convent of San Francisco." (002-025)

### Mining and Counter-Mining

After capturing the hospital, the French began mining operations toward San Francisco, digging underground galleries to plant explosives beneath the Spanish positions. The Spanish defenders responded with counter-mines of their own.

> "After the fourth day, the French began mining towards the hospital and San Francisco, in order to take it; for they knew well that it would be impossible in any other way." (026-006)

> "They would not be able to get here by the galleries under the Calle de Santa Engracia from the Jerusalem and the hospital, because our mine has made theirs useless." (027-029)

### The Hospital as a Fortified Ruin

By the later stages of the siege, the hospital had been reduced to ruins, but these ruins became a fortified French position. Spanish defenders, including groups of peasants, fired upon the hospital ruins from neighboring houses. The French used the hospital as a base from which to launch assaults on surrounding positions.

> "While this was going on, some twenty peasants came into the house to fire from the windows upon the ruins of the hospital." (028-013)

> "Don't let them have time to breathe over there at the hospital." (028-014)

> "Those in the hospital have been repulsed three times." (028-020)

The French defenders of the hospital ruins eventually shifted their assault through [[san-diego|San Diego]], quickly occupying further ruins while Spanish attention was fixed on the hospital.

## Wartime Hospitals

Beyond its role as a military position, the hospital was part of a network of improvised medical facilities across Saragossa. Wounded defenders were carried to hospitals throughout the city, and the civilian population — particularly women — played a crucial role in staffing them.

> "The men to the walls, the women to the bloody hospitals, the children and priests to carry ammunition!" (010-011)

The hospitals, however, were overwhelmed by the scale of casualties: "The charity of women, the zeal of patriotic citizens, the multiplied activity of the hospitals, really availed nothing." (021-023)
