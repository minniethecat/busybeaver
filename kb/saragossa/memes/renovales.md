# Renovales

## Overview

Renovales (Mariano Renovales) commanded the Huesca and Valencia volunteers who defended the fortress of [[san-jose|San José]] during the January assaults. His presence at the redoubt is credited with sustaining its [[resistance|defense]] despite overwhelming artillery fire. Galdós mentions him alongside the Walloon guards and Soria militia.

## Sorties and Offensive Operations

Before the January assaults, Renovales led aggressive sorties beyond the walls. On December 24, he "made one with the troops of the fortress of San José, and cut down an olive grove which hid the works of the enemy" (008-006). On December 31, Gabriel's battalion was ordered to support a larger operation led by Renovales, attacking the enemy centre from Torrero to the Muela road (008-009).

When the battalion hesitated at a critical moment, "Renovales dashed forward and led us on" (008-010), demonstrating the personal courage that earned him his reputation. After the sortie, the returning troops were greeted "from San José all the way to the Trinitarios" with cheers from citizens who "climbed upon the walls" to welcome them (008-021).

## Defense of San José

Renovales's defining moment came during the January 10–11 assaults on San José. The French batteries opened "a fire such that the whole army seemed behind the cannon" (010-009). Renovales held the fortress alongside his volunteers, the Walloon guards, and the Soria militia (010-014).

Fresh troops were repeatedly sent because "the battalions working there since morning were decimated" (010-015). Despite the fortress being reduced to ruins, Renovales held through the night — only relinquishing the position when "their artillery had reduced it to powder" (011-001).

## Later Actions

Even after the fall of San José, Renovales remained active. During the desperate final days, Gabriel is sent with an urgent message: "Run this instant to the convent. Go down into the cellar, and if you hear the noise, tell Renovales what is going on" (027-027), underscoring his continued importance to the defense.

## Role in the Narrative

Renovales appears during the [[resistance|defense]] of [[san-jose|San José]]:

> Renovales was there with the Huesca and the Valencia volunteers, the Walloon guards, and various members of the militia of Soria. (010-014)

Despite the fortress being "more formidable in aspect" but having "less power of resistance," his leadership kept it from falling on January 10–11.

## Historical Note

Mariano Renovales (1774–1820) was a historical figure who fought in both sieges of Zaragoza. He later served in the Spanish War of Independence and in South American campaigns, eventually dying in Havana.
