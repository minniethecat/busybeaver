# Tío Garcés

## Overview

Tío Garcés was a forty-five-year-old peasant from Garrapinillos, a village near Saragossa, and one of the most memorable supporting characters in Galdós's narrative. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]], [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]], describes him as a man of extraordinary [[courage|bravery]] and physical resilience:

> "He was a man of forty-five years, a native of Garrapinillos, very brave, bronzed, sawed-off looking, with limbs of steel; there was no one so active or so imperturbable under fire." (009-007)

## Role in the Narrative

Tío Garcés embodies the common Spaniard's fierce resistance to [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s forces. He appears in the second half of the novel, serving as a model of self-sacrificing patriotism. Before [[siege-warfare|the siege]], he deliberately destroyed his own property to deny the enemy any advantage:

> "He had a small estate in the environs, and a very modest house; but he had levelled it with his own hands, and cut down his pear-trees, so that the enemy could not use them." (009-007)

He fought with an almost mythical fearlessness that inspired those around him. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]] notes that he "slept without shelter, and ate less than an anchorite," sustaining himself on "two pieces of bread and a couple of bites of dried beef" per day (009-007). He wore a decoration on his sleeve commemorating the sixteenth of August, earned during the first [[siege-warfare|siege]].

During the house-to-house fighting, Tío Garcés led a band of defenders through attics, over rooftops, and into the church of [[san-pablo|San Pablo]], where he mounted a desperate last stand from the pulpit.

## Key Relationships

Tío Garcés commanded a small group of defenders during the street combat in the city centre. [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel Araceli]] fought alongside him and witnessed his final moments. His relationship with the men he led was marked by a rough, paternal authority, and he inspired fierce loyalty. Though a peasant by birth, he earned the respect of soldiers and citizens alike.

## Historical Context

Tío Garcés represents the thousands of unnamed peasants who participated in the defence of Saragossa. While fictional, his characterization draws on the historical reality that [[siege-warfare|the siege]] was fought not only by regular soldiers but by the entire population, including peasants who levelled their own fields and homes to deny resources to the French. The narrator notes that men like him inspired [[palafox|Palafox]]'s famous phrase that the defenders' "bodies were clothed only in glory" (009-007).

## Death at the Pulpit

His final moments are among the novel's most vivid scenes. Defending the pulpit of the church, he stood upright directing his men amid the chaos:

> "Meantime the pulpit was still held bravely, and in that hell of flame I saw Tío Garcés standing erect, directing the men, and looking like a preacher screaming impudently with a hoarse voice." (022-008)

He was killed when the French broke through:

> "This could not last long; and Tío Garcés presently fell, screaming hoarsely, pierced by a hundred balls." (022-009)

The narrator contrasts him with another figure named [[juan-pirli|Pirli]], who found a place in history while Tío Garcés remained anonymous: "Oh, [[juan-pirli|Pirli]], more happy than Tío Garcés, thou dost occupy a place in history!" (022-017)

## Notable Quotes

> "Truly Tío Garcés should have worn a tablet on his brow like that on the bridge, reading, 'An unconquerable man.'" (009-011)
