# Fernando VII

## Overview

**Fernando VII** (Ferdinand VII) was the King of Spain during the Peninsular War, whose name became a rallying point for Spanish resistance against Napoleon. Though absent from Spain — imprisoned by Napoleon at Valençay — his symbolic authority inspired the royalist forces that fought against the French occupation.

## Name and Authority in Saragossa

In Saragossa, Fernando's name was invoked by military units that fought in the city's defense. The [[sharpshooters-of-fernando-vii|Sharpshooters of Fernando VII]] (Tiradores de Fernando VII), an elite light infantry unit, bore his name into battle at [[monte-torrero]] during the opening engagement of the Second Siege on 21 December 1808. They were among the ten thousand defenders who held the Torrero heights:

> "The volunteers of Bourbon, Castile, Campo Segorbino, of Alicante, and of Soria, the sharpshooters of Fernando VII, the Murcia regiment, and other bodies that I do not remember, answered the fire." (007-006)

## Historical Context

Fernando VII ascended the throne in March 1808 after the Tumult of Aranjuez forced his father, Carlos IV, to abdicate. Napoleon summoned both to Bayonne, where he compelled them to renounce their claims, placing his brother Joseph Bonaparte on the Spanish throne. Fernando remained a prisoner in France until 1814, while the Spanish people fought in his name during the War of Independence.

## Legacy

The invocation of Fernando's name by Spanish forces during the Peninsular War illustrates how royalist sentiment served as a unifying force against the French occupation. His eventual return to the throne in 1814 marked the restoration of the Bourbon monarchy, though his subsequent absolutist policies foreshadowed the political turmoil of 19th-century Spain.
