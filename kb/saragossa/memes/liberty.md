# Liberty

## Overview

**Liberty** in the novel is not a political abstraction but an intimate, urgent demand: the plea to release a captive father. In [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s final hours, [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] confronts [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]] and demands he free her imprisoned father, [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]]. This personal crisis of liberty — one prisoner, one captor, one desperate daughter — becomes the novel's final moral test, compressing [[siege-warfare|the siege]]'s larger questions of power, justice, and mercy into a single room.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Liberty enters the narrative late, not as a rallying cry but as a whispered plea. [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]], dying from wounds and exhaustion, finds [[augustine-montoria|Augustine]] and demands he release her father:

> "Set him at liberty. No one can see. We will flee together." (029-038)

The scene unfolds in the ruins of [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]]'s house, where [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] is held captive. [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]] repeats her demand with increasing [[despair|desperation]]:

> "Set him at liberty this instant. Augustine, if you love me, set him free." (029-040)

[[augustine-montoria|Augustine]] hesitates, torn between love for [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]] and duty to his father, who imprisoned [[don-jeronimo-candiola|Candiola]] for hoarding grain. [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]] invokes their shared past:

> "Augustine, set him at liberty! We will hide where no one can find us." (029-049)

When [[augustine-montoria|Augustine]] resists, [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]] questions the very foundation of his hesitation:

> "What harm can it cause, your setting my father at liberty? Do you not remember me?" (029-064)

The scene reaches its climax when [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla]], in [[desperation|despair]], appeals one final time:

> "Yes, he will set him at liberty," said Mariquilla, in despair. (029-069)

The resolution of this plea — whether liberty is granted — shapes the novel's closing moral judgment. The demand for individual liberty, made in the shadow of a collapsing city, becomes the test by which all the novel's claims about honor, patriotism, and justice are measured.
