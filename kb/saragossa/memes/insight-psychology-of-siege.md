# The Psychology of Siege Warfare in Galdós's Saragossa

## Thesis

Galdós's *Saragossa* is not merely a military chronicle but a sustained psychological study of how human beings — individually and collectively — respond to the extreme duress of siege warfare. Through the first-person narration of Gabriel Araceli, the novel traces a psychological arc that moves from the initial enthusiasm of enlistment through cycles of [[courage]] and [[fear]], [[hope]] and [[despair]], to the ultimate transformation of ordinary citizens into figures of [[heroism]] and [[wrath]]. This essay argues that Galdós presents siege psychology not as a collection of individual emotional states but as a collective phenomenon — a shared atmosphere that permeates the besieged city and reshapes its inhabitants' understanding of themselves.

## The Arc of Enthusiasm

The novel opens with a mood of defiant optimism. Gabriel's arrival in Saragossa coincides with the city's mobilization, and the narrator captures the infectious quality of martial enthusiasm:

> The spirit of the men who were enlisting filled me with such enthusiasm that nothing seemed to me so noble as to follow glory, even afar off. (004-008)

This initial enthusiasm is not merely individual but contagious — it spreads through the city like the epidemic that will later devastate it. Galdós is careful to show that the boundary between [[courage]] and its dark twin, [[wrath]], is thin from the outset. The same energy that drives men to enlist drives mobs to attack the houses of suspected traitors.

## Fear as a Collective Condition

As the siege intensifies, [[fear]] becomes the dominant psychological register. But Galdós's treatment of fear is notably sophisticated: it is presented not as individual cowardice but as an environmental condition, as inescapable as the bombardment itself. When Don José de Montoria dismisses the epidemic as "fear, sirs, pure fear" (014-024), he articulates a view in which psychological and physical suffering are indistinguishable — fear *is* the disease.

The narrator himself admits to fear without shame. Gabriel's psychological honesty — his willingness to record his own moments of terror — distinguishes *Saragossa* from simpler patriotic narratives. Fear in this novel is not the opposite of [[courage]] but its precondition: courage is not the absence of fear but the decision to act despite it.

## The Epidemic of Despair

The typhus epidemic that ravages the city during the second siege serves as Galdós's master metaphor for the psychological cost of prolonged warfare. [[Epidemic|The epidemic]] blurs the boundary between physical illness and psychological collapse. Characters waste away not only from disease but from the accumulated weight of [[suffering]], [[hunger]], and [[despair]].

[[Despair]] in *Saragossa* is rendered as a kind of contagion — it spreads from person to person, from household to household, until it becomes the city's dominant emotional climate. The novel's most haunting passages describe not battles but the silent, interior collapse of those who have simply endured too much:

> Candiola's distress and moral suffering made it seem as if he had a nervous disorder. (019-013)

## Heroism as Transformation

Against this background of collective fear and despair, Galdós places acts of extraordinary [[heroism]]. But his treatment of heroism is notably demystified. Heroes in *Saragossa* are not born but made — transformed by circumstances that they neither chose nor fully understand.

[[agustina-de-aragon|Agustina de Aragón]] fires the cannon after the men around her have fallen. [[tio-garces|Tío Garcés]] dies defending the pulpit in San Pablo, not because he is fearless but because he has found something worth dying for. The novel's [[heroism]] is always specific, always grounded in particular moments and particular choices. It is heroism stripped of glory and reduced to its essence: the refusal to stop fighting when stopping would be easier.

## Madness and the Dissolution of Self

The siege's ultimate psychological destination is [[madness]] — the point at which the accumulated weight of fear, despair, and violence overwhelms the self's capacity for coherent response. Galdós depicts madness not as clinical pathology but as a rational response to irrational circumstances. When the city's defenders fight on amid the ruins, when they laugh at incoming bombs, when they treat death as a familiar companion — these behaviors occupy the ambiguous territory between heroic resilience and psychological disintegration.

The French soldiers' own growing horror at what they have been ordered to do — recorded in passing moments of recognition — suggests that the madness of siege warfare afflicts attackers and defenders alike.

## Conclusion

Galdós's great achievement in *Saragossa* is to have written a war novel that is, at its deepest level, a study in collective psychology. The siege is not merely the setting for individual emotional dramas but the agent that produces them. [[courage|Courage]], [[fear]], [[despair]], [[heroism]], [[madness]] — these are not properties of individual characters but states of the besieged city itself, as real and as deadly as the French bombardment. In tracing the psychological arc from enthusiasm to exhaustion, Galdós offers not a celebration of war but an unflinching examination of what war does to the human mind.
