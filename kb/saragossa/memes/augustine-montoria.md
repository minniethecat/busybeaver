# Augustine Montoria

## Overview

Augustine Montoria is the son of [[don-jose-montoria|Don José de Montoria]] and one of the novel's most psychologically complex characters. Destined by his father for the priesthood, he instead takes up arms in the city's [[resistance|defense]] and falls in love with [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] — the daughter of his father's enemy. His candor about fear and his inner conflicts make him one of Galdós's most modern characters.

## Biography and Background

Augustine was raised under the strict expectations of his father, who intended him to enter the Seminary and pursue an ecclesiastical career. He received a classical education befitting a young man of his social standing in late eighteenth-century Saragossa. When the [[second-siege-of-saragossa|French siege]] began, Augustine abandoned his father's plans and volunteered for the city's defense — a decision that set him on a collision course with both paternal authority and his own conscience.

His upbringing in a wealthy household stands in contrast to the poverty of the Candiola family, a tension that underlies his relationship with [[mariquilla-candiola]]. The Montoria household represents established honor and civic standing, while the Candiolas inhabit the margins of Saragossan society. The Montoria-Candiola feud mirrors broader social tensions in late Bourbon Spain: the conflict between established families of honor and those whose wealth derived from commerce rather than lineage.

Although Augustine is a fictional character, his predicament reflects the historical reality of young Spaniards forced to choose between traditional vocations and the urgent demands of national defense. The Napoleonic invasion disrupted every institution in Spanish society, including the Church and the family. Sons who had been groomed for clerical or professional careers found themselves on the barricades alongside peasants and artisans.

## Role in the Narrative

Augustine's most striking quality is his honesty about fear, which distinguishes him from conventional heroic characters. He admits that his courage has waned between the first and second [[siege-warfare|sieges]]:

> "In the other siege I was more valiant then than now. A peculiar zeal filled my blood, and I threw myself into places of greatest danger without fear of death. Today does not find me the same. I am timid and afraid, and when a gun goes off, it makes me tremble." (005-007)

This frank admission of fear is unusual in the literature of the period and marks Galdós's commitment to psychological realism. Augustine does not perform heroism as spectacle; instead, he enacts what [[courage]] truly means: persisting in duty despite terror.

His love for [[mariquilla-candiola|Mariquilla Candiola]] creates a secondary plot line that humanizes [[siege-warfare|the siege]] narrative:

> "I am often afraid that you will be killed; but I know not what voices I hear in the depths of my soul telling me that they will not kill you." (016-019)

The romance unfolds against the destruction of the city, intertwining personal and collective catastrophe. Augustine's devotion to Mariquilla forces him to confront his father's bitter feud with [[don-jeronimo-candiola]], revealing fractures within the Spanish resistance itself:

> "My father is a good man, but he will not consent to my loving the daughter of one he hates." (011-008)

During the bombardment, Augustine reflects on the blurred line between courage and madness:

> "I do not know if it is courage or madness that keeps me at this post. Perhaps there is no difference." (020-005)

## Key Relationships

- [[Don José de Montoria]] — his father, who intended him for the priesthood and whose rigid sense of honor Augustine must ultimately question
- [[mariquilla-candiola]] — his love interest, daughter of his father's enemy; their bond symbolizes reconciliation across class and factional divides
- [[gabriel-araceli]] — his close friend and confidant, who provides a foil through his own more pragmatic approach to war and loyalty
