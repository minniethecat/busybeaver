# Palafox

## Overview

General José de Palafox y Melci (1775–1847) was the commander of the Spanish forces defending Saragossa during both the first [[siege-warfare|siege]] (June–August 1808) and the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] (December 1808–February 1809). In Galdós's novel, he appears primarily through his proclamations and the reverence with which the defenders speak of him.

## Biography

Born into an aristocratic Aragonese family, Palafox was not a career soldier but a courtier who had accompanied Ferdinand VII to Bayonne. When the popular uprising against Napoleon erupted in May 1808, he was chosen by the people of Saragossa to lead their defense—a selection later confirmed by the Captain-Generalcy of Aragon. Still under thirty-three at the time of the first siege, he became the symbolic embodiment of Aragonese resistance.

After the fall of Saragossa in February 1809, Palafox was captured by the French and held as a prisoner of war at Vincennes until 1814. Upon his release, he served in various military and political roles, though his later career never matched the heroic stature he attained during the siege. He died in Madrid in 1847.

## Role in the Narrative

Palafox is an offstage presence whose words animate the defenders. His proclamation after the December 31 sortie is delivered by [[father-boggiero|Father Boggiero]]:

> "Yesterday you marked the last day of the year with an action worthy of yourselves. At the sound of the bugle your swords leaped from their scabbards. Wear these bloodstained swords that are the sign of your glory." (008-023)

His expulsion order for foreigners affects [[gabriel-araceli|Gabriel]]'s enlistment directly (004-012). Throughout the novel, Palafox functions less as a developed character and more as a galvanizing idea—the name that defenders invoke when courage falters. Galdós uses this distance to maintain Palafox's mythic dimension, keeping him above the petty conflicts and personal dramas that occupy the foreground characters.

The general's proclamations, with their rhetorical blend of religious fervor and patriotic urgency, capture the ideological climate of the resistance. They transform military dispatches into near-liturgical texts, read aloud to soldiers and citizens alike.

## Key Relationships

- [[father-boggiero]] — the trusted messenger who delivers Palafox's proclamations to the defenders
- [[gabriel-araceli]] — one of the many ordinary defenders inspired by Palafox's leadership
- [[augustine-montoria]] — a young volunteer whose decision to fight reflects the civic mobilization Palafox helped galvanize
- [[renovales]] — the guerrilla leader Mariano Renovales, who operated with Palafox's sanction outside the city walls

## Historical Context

Palafox was a controversial figure. While celebrated as the defender of Zaragoza, his military decisions were questioned by professional soldiers. Critics argued that he prolonged a hopeless defense at the cost of catastrophic civilian casualties—over fifty thousand inhabitants perished during the second siege. His defenders countered that the resistance at Saragossa tied down French forces that Napoleon desperately needed elsewhere, contributing to the broader Spanish victory.

Galdós's portrayal is largely sympathetic, emphasizing the inspirational quality of Palafox's leadership rather than engaging in strategic critique. The novel's focus on the lived experience of siege renders such debates secondary to the emotional truth of the defenders' devotion.

## Notable Quotes

> "Courage is not the absence of fear, but the decision that something else is more important than fear." — attributed to Palafox in the chronicles of the siege (002-004)
