# Heroism

## Overview

Heroism in *Saragossa* is examined at two levels: the raw acts of couragey performed by the city's defenders, and the mythologization of those acts as they traveled beyond Spain. Galdós shows how the reality of Saragossa's resistance was transformed into legend, with the defenders becoming "like figures of mythology" in the eyes of Europe.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel repeatedly shows characters self-consciously invoking heroism as a narrative frame. [[don-roque|Don Roque]], the group's guide upon arrival in the city, uses the word as hungry praise—both admiring and impatient with endless tales:

> "Oh, what sublime heroism!" exclaimed Don Roque, yawning with hunger. "How much I should enjoy hearing those tales of heroism told on a full stomach!" (002-028)

This ironic juxtaposition—sublime heroism alongside mundane hunger—is Galdós's signature technique for grounding elevated concepts in human reality.

The international [[glory|fame]] of the Saragossans is a recurring motif:

> In England and Germany, where they were considered the Numantines of modern times, those half-naked peasants, with rope sandals on their feet and the bright Saragossan kerchief on their heads, became like figures of mythology. (004-008)

## Key Passages

After battle, citizens rush to the field to witness the scenes of heroism firsthand, blurring the line between participant and audience:

> Night came, and when a part of our troops fell back upon the city, all of the people hastened to the suburb to look at the field of battle from near at hand, and to gladden their imaginations by going over, one by one, the scenes of heroism. (007-001)

The heroism of specific individuals is invoked throughout, including Agustina de Aragón and [[antonio-laste|Antonio Laste]]:

> "We have already heard of the heroism of that noble woman," said Don Roque. (002-016)

## Historical Context

The term "Numantines of modern times" linked Saragossa to the ancient Celtiberian city of Numantia, which resisted Roman [[siege-warfare|siege]] until its inhabitants chose death over surrender. This classical parallel elevated the Saragossan [[resistance|defense]] from a military event to a moral exemplar in European imagination.
