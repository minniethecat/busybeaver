# Calle de San Gil

## Overview

The Calle de San Gil was a street in Saragossa that formed part of the approach to the bridge over the [[ebro|Ebro River]]. During the Second Siege of Saragossa (1808–1809), its narrow entrance was fortified with cannon, and together with the archway of Cineja it served as a defensive barrier against the French advance toward the bridge.

## Location

The Calle de San Gil was located near the Ebro bridge, running alongside the Calle de San Pedro and the Calle de la Cuchillería — a cluster of streets that together formed the approaches to the city's main river crossing. These streets were densely built and became nearly impassable during the siege due to rubble, barricades, and the movement of civilians and soldiers.

> "The streets of San Gil, San Pedro, and La Cuchillería, which lead to the bridge, were almost impassable." (006-014)

## Role in the Siege

### Defensive Position

The narrow entrance of the Calle de San Gil was one of the choke points where Spanish defenders placed cannon to block the French advance. The nearby archway of Cineja was similarly fortified, creating a layered defense at this end of the city.

> "In the narrow entrance of the Calle de San Gil, and in the archway of Cineja, there were cannon to restrain the enemy's advance." (028-002)

### Street Fighting

As the French pushed deeper into Saragossa during the later stages of the siege, the Calle de San Gil became part of the zone of house-to-house combat. The narrator records having to detour around the Calle San Gil because the direct route was blocked — likely by rubble, barricades, or active fighting.

> "But we had to go around by the Calle San Gil, because we could not get through directly." (028-040)
