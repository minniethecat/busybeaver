# Desperation

## Overview

Desperation in *Saragossa* is coufury pushed past the point of hope. It is the psychological state reached when defeat is certain but surrender is rejected. Characters act not from confidence in victory but from a desperate refusal to [[surrender|yield]]—fighting because there is nothing left to lose. Desperation is the dark twin of coufury: born of the same circumstances but stripped of illusion.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The narrator describes the strategic situation in terms that make [[despair|desperation]] inevitable:

> In this situation, surrender was inevitable sooner or later. Indeed we were at the mercy of the French arms as a ship at the mercy of the waves of the ocean. (011-003)

Yet even with surrender inevitable, the defenders continue. [[augustine-montoria|Augustine Montoria]]'s darkest fantasy expresses pure [[despair|desperation]]:

> "If this immense quantity of powder were as big as Saragossa I should like it still better. Yes, and in such a case I should like to be the only inhabitant of this great city. What a pleasure! I would myself set fire to it, and fly into the clouds, torn to pieces in the horrible explosion." (013-004)

## Key Passages

Desperation also characterizes the French soldiers during house-to-house combat:

> The Imperial soldiers, panic-stricken with this violent assault, looked quickly for a way out of the house which had been taken room by room. We pursued them through passages and halls... We finished them wherever we could find them, and some of them escaped, dashing in desperation out through the courtyards. (021-021)

The [[despair|desperation]] of the French soldiers mirrors that of the defenders—both sides pushed beyond strategic calculation into primal survival.

## Historical Context

The final weeks of the second [[siege-warfare|siege]] of Saragossa descended into a desperate struggle that shocked contemporary observers. With two-thirds of the city in ruins, epidemic raging, and food exhausted, resistance continued not from strategic calculation but from a collective refusal to accept defeat. This [[despair|desperation]] became central to the city's legend.
