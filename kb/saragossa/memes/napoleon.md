# Napoleon Bonaparte

## Overview

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821), Emperor of the French, appears in *Saragossa* as a distant but personally invested antagonist. His fury at the failure to take the city — and his tactical intervention — elevates Saragossa's resistance from a provincial [[siege-warfare|siege]] to an event of European significance.

## Napoleon's Tactical Intervention

The most striking episode involving Napoleon occurs through reported speech. During the first siege, the French commander Marshal Moncey had attacked through the Portillo and Castle Aljafería without success (010-004). When news of this failure reached Paris, Napoleon reacted with characteristic fury:

> Do you know that Napoleon in Paris, knowing the resistance shown by this city in the first siege, was furious with Lefebre Desnouettes because he assaulted the plaza by the Portillo and the Castle Aljafería? He called for a plan of Saragossa, and they gave it to him, and he showed that the city should be attacked by Santa Engracia. (010-004)

This personal intervention — an emperor in Paris studying a map of a Spanish provincial city — testifies to the strategic and symbolic weight Saragossa had acquired. The defenders recognized the gravity: "A black day is indeed dawning for us if the orders of Napoleon are carried out" (010-005).

## Napoleon's Understanding of Saragossa

Galdós suggests that Napoleon, despite his distance, possessed an unusual insight into the Saragossan character. Gabriel reflects that "Napoleon, with his extraordinary penetration, would have comprehended the Saragossan character" (006-020).

The implication is that Napoleon understood the Spanish defense better than his own generals — recognizing that the city's strength lay in the ferocity of its people, not its fortifications.

## Role in the Narrative

Napoleon appears only through reported speech, but his involvement carries immense symbolic weight. He is the unseen force driving the siege, the distant emperor whose decisions determine the fate of thousands.

Galdós uses him to situate Saragossa within the larger framework of the Peninsular War, making the city's resistance not merely a local affair but a challenge to imperial power itself.

## Historical Context

Napoleon's Peninsular War (1807–1814) was one of his most costly campaigns. The resistance of Spanish cities like Zaragoza contributed to the "Spanish ulcer" that drained French resources and morale, bogging down hundreds of thousands of troops that Napoleon needed elsewhere in Europe.
