## Role in the Narrative

Don Felipe San Clement is a Saragossan merchant who joins the armed [[resistance|defense]] of his city during [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. He is first mentioned in [[sursum-corda|Sursum Corda]]'s account of the fighting around the [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] monastery on August 4, where he sustained severe injuries: "Don Felipe San Clement, a merchant of Saragossa, broke both his legs" in the orchard at the back of the convent, where at least two hundred bodies lay.(002-019)

His participation is striking because the orchard of [[santa-engracia|Santa Engracia]] was a particularly deadly sector. The same location saw the death of Lieutenant Don Miguel Gila, and the survival of anyone wounded there — let alone a civilian merchant — marks San Clement as both coufuryous and fortunate.

## Family and Social Standing

The San Clement family is listed among the "influential men of the city" alongside the don-jose-montorias, Ceresos, Sases, and Salameros — families who provided leadership during [[siege-warfare|the siege]]. After a night of intense fighting in which the French failed to gain ground, [[gabriel-araceli|the narrator]] records that these prominent families "showed such tremendous coufury and uttered such contempt of the enemy that it roused the spirits of all who saw and heard them."(023-002)

## Civic Contribution

Like [[don-antonio-quadros|Don Antonio Quadros]] and Don Andrés Guspide, San Clement represents the Saragossan merchant class that converted commercial energies into military ones during the crisis. While the novel does not detail his mercantile activities, his inclusion in the honor roll of influential families and his presence on the battlefield — sustaining grave injuries — testify to the totality of civilian mobilization in Saragossa.
> When the influential men of the city, the Montorias, the Ceresos, the
Sases, the Salameros, and the San Clementes were returning to Las
Mónicas, the scene that night of great prodigies of valor, they showed
such tremendous courage and uttered such contempt of the enemy that it
roused the spirits of all who saw and heard them.
>
> (018-014)
