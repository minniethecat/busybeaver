Captain Amery is a sea captain who appears in the climactic final chapter of the story, commanding the vessel used in the pursuit of the Belgian steamer [[the-lescaut|L'Escaut]] in the English Channel.

## The Pursuit of the L'Escaut

The hands of French's watch had just reached the quarter hour after six, when Captain Amery, who had once again been sweeping the horizon with his telescope, said quietly: "There she is" [020-013]. He handed the glass to French, directing him to look about three points on the starboard bow, where very faint and far off could be seen what looked like the upper part of a steamer's deck, with a funnel and two masts [020-013][020-014]. Amery explained that the vessel was still hull down and would be more visible in a few minutes, estimating they would be up with her in about three-quarters of an hour [020-014].

As the pursuit vessel closed the distance, the dramatic climax approached and the minds of the actors in the play became charged with a very real anxiety [020-016]. Captain Amery, under almost any circumstances, would have to deal with a very ticklish situation: he had to get the gold if it was salvable, and the fact that they were not in British waters would be a complication if the Belgian had already recovered it [020-016]. French had to ascertain if his quarry were on board and see that they did not escape — also a difficult job outside the three-mile limit [020-016]. Their several anxieties were reflected on the faces of the men as they stood in silence, watching the rapidly growing vessel [020-016].

## Identifying the Target

Presently an exclamation came from Captain Amery as he made a key observation [020-017]. He exclaimed "By Jove! this is a rum business," noting that he could now see the vessel's flag — and it was the British red ensign [020-018]. Amery puzzled over what a Belgian boat would be doing with a British flag, and observed that it was flying jack down — a flag of distress [020-018]. The captain voiced a tactical concern: if the quarry were armed, it would be a likely enough ruse to get them close by so as to make sure of hitting them in a vital place [020-018].

Amery continued his assessment, noting that he saw no signs of life aboard the vessel — no smoke from either boilers or galley, no one on the bridge, and no movement on deck [020-020]. He concluded that it did not look well: "it looks as if they were lying low and waiting for us" [020-020].

## Confirmation and Approach

When they were within a mile of the stranger and her details were clear even to the naked eye [020-021], Captain Amery confirmed the identification: "It's the L'Escaut anyway. I can see the name on her bows" [020-022]. However, he admitted he did not like the flag and the silence, and decided to see if he could wake her up [020-022]. He put his hand on the foghorn halliard and blew a number of resounding blasts [020-023].

The signal produced an immediate response: two figures appeared at the deckhouse door and rushed up on the bridge, waving furiously — they were both women [020-023]. Amery then rang his engines down to half speed and, slightly porting his helm, headed for some distance astern of the other vessel. Then starboarding, he swung round, and bringing up parallel to her and some couple of hundred yards away, he dropped anchor [020-025]. Without loss of a moment a boat was lowered for the boarding party [020-026].
