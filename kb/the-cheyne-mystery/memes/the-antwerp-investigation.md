## Summary

The Antwerp Investigation was Inspector French's pursuit of the conspirators' trail to Belgium. After discovering that James Dangle had made frequent visits to Antwerp, French traveled there to trace his movements. Working in a foreign country without knowledge of French or Flemish, French methodically searched hotels, railway stations, and the docks, eventually uncovering evidence that Dangle had organized a maritime expedition — the *L'Escaut* voyage — from the port of Antwerp [2697-2781].

## Context

Following his taking charge of the case after Joan Merrill's disappearance and the attempted murder of Cheyne, Inspector French systematically gathered evidence. A crucial breakthrough came from the stationmaster at Ghent, who identified "Carillon" as the hotel name Cheyne had partially deciphered from a torn receipt. Further investigation revealed that Dangle had been paying frequent visits to Antwerp for several weeks [2749-2761].

## Details

French arrived in Antwerp and immediately began a methodical search. At the Grand Hôtel du Carillon in the Place Verte, near the Cathedral, he confirmed that Dangle had stayed there and had recently departed — just one day before French's arrival [2703-2709].

Reasoning that Antwerp was the nearest major foreign port to London and ideal for a secret maritime expedition, French deduced that Dangle might have left by water. He focused his investigation on the docks, working through the handicap of language barriers without police assistance [2761-2781].

With the help of a local contact named Renard, French searched the harbours and boatmen along the Scheldt River, eventually reaching the Tête de Flandre. There he obtained crucial information about the vessel and its departure [2895]. The investigation confirmed that the conspirators had chartered a ship — the *L'Escaut* — and had sailed with a crew of twenty-eight, including divers and equipment for recovering Otto Schulz's sunken gold [3436].

## Consequences

The Antwerp investigation was a critical success for French. He wired his findings to Scotland Yard, which in turn contacted the Moroccan Government and confirmed that the conspirators' cover story (chartering for agricultural samples) was fabricated [2975-2979]. French's work in Antwerp provided the essential intelligence that set the stage for the Admiralty's salvage operation and the final confrontation at sea. The investigation also demonstrated French's ability to operate effectively in foreign territory, using only his professional skills and determination.
