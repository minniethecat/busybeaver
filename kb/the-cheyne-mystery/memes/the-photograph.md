# The Photograph

**The Photograph** played a critical role in the Cheyne mystery as the primary means of copying and preserving the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]] and the [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price cipher]]. Both the conspirators and the investigators used photography to duplicate key documents.

## The Conspirators' Photographs

[[dangle|James Dangle]] and [[sime|Sime]], having obtained the original tracing and cipher document, needed copies for their operation. They employed a photographer named Parkes to create the copies:

> "We've got to get these photographs taken and then we've got to get them developed," Dangle said. [006-018]

The work was done with careful precision:

> …proceeding with the photographs in the most careful and deliberate manner. [006-027]

> …presently three photographs had been taken. [006-028]

### The Photographic Session

The conspirators set up their camera in the sitting room of the house at [[hopefield-avenue|Hopefield Avenue]], with the tracing pinned to a cupboard door as the subject. The discussion about how many negatives to make revealed their meticulous approach:

> "Do you think two will be enough or should we take four?" said Price when he had arranged the camera to his satisfaction. "Two, I should say," Parkes answered. "Even if we lost the tracing, two negatives should be an ample record." [006-024]

It was while eavesdropping on this session that Cheyne learned the true identities of the conspirators — and that Dangle's story about the Hull barony was entirely false [006-026].

> Sime? Cheyne thought perplexedly that the man who had run the motor on the Enid had been introduced to him as Lewisham. Sime, was it? [006-011]

## Joan Merrill's Photographs

[[joan-merrill|Joan Merrill]], using her own photographic equipment, independently documented the evidence at the [[the-hopefield-avenue-discovery|Hopefield Avenue house]]:

> …having photographed them with her half-plate camera. [008-002]

This gave the investigators their own copies to work from, independent of the originals held by the conspirators. Joan had originally taken up photography for her architectural studies, using a half-plate camera and flashlight apparatus to document moldings, string courses, and other details for her decorative panel work [008-002]. This professional skill proved unexpectedly crucial to the investigation.

## The Tracing Photograph at Scotland Yard

When [[inspector-french-takes-charge|Inspector French took charge]] at [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], the photograph of the tracing became a central piece of evidence:

> …the tracing pinned up to be photographed in the house in Hopefield Avenue. [013-019]

It was from studying this photograph that Inspector French eventually deduced the meaning of the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]] and the goal of [[the-lescaut|the L'Escaut]].

### The Critical Insight

The photographic evidence proved decisive when French made a key connection that he and Cheyne had both overlooked. French realized that Joan Merrill, having photographed the tracing herself with her half-plate camera, must possess her own set of prints. "He had even specially noticed the girl's camera and the flashlight apparatus which she used for her architectural details when he was searching her rooms, but he had then, and since then up till this moment, entirely and completely forgotten the arrangement made between the partners" [018-053]. The photographer produced three excellent prints, which French had enlarged to match the original tracing's dimensions [018-053], [018-055].

> He took tracing and photographs home with him, and spent the greater part of the evening making a minute comparison between the two. [018-057]

## Significance

Photography in *The Cheyne Mystery* serves a dual narrative function. On the practical level, it is the technology that democratizes the secret — once the tracing is photographed, it can no longer be monopolized by whoever holds the physical original. Dangle and Cheyne each possess copies, which transforms the conflict from a simple chase for a physical object into a race to be the first to solve the cipher. On the thematic level, photography represents the modern investigative method that French embodies: careful, systematic, reproducible, and ultimately more reliable than the romantic improvisations of both Cheyne and the conspirators.

The photographic subplot also underscores the value of Joan Merrill's contribution. Her seemingly unrelated hobby — architectural photography — proves essential to the investigation's success, demonstrating the novel's broader theme that apparently marginal skills and perspectives can be decisive in solving complex problems.
