## Background

Schulz served as a U-boat captain in the German Imperial Navy during the First World War. During his service, he made a discovery of extraordinary value — a secret that would later attract the attention of criminals and investigators alike [011-056]. The exact nature of Schulz's discovery is gradually revealed: it involves a hidden asset whose location he alone knew.

During a naval engagement, Schulz's U-boat was involved in action that left him severely wounded. He was transferred to a hospital ship along with other injured crewmen [011-046]. There, in the hospital, he met Arnold Price, a British merchant navy officer recovering from his own wounds [011-048].

## Role in the Story

Otto Schulz is the origin point of the entire mystery. As a German U-boat captain during the First World War, Schulz made a discovery of immense value — the nature of which drives the plot of *The Cheyne Mystery* [011-045]. Although Schulz dies before the events of the novel, his secret, encoded by [[arnold-price|Arnold Price]] into the [[arnold-price-cipher|cipher]] and preserved in the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]], becomes the object of a desperate criminal pursuit led by [[dangle|James Dangle]].

The chapter titled "Otto Schulz's Secret" (Chapter XI) is devoted to the revelation of Schulz's story, underscoring his importance to the narrative despite his absence from the novel's present action [011-001].

## The Hospital Encounter

The meeting between Schulz and Price in the hospital is the pivotal moment that connects the secret to the novel's present. Schulz, aware that his injuries might be fatal, decided to share his discovery with Price [011-055]. Before revealing the secret, Schulz administered an oath of secrecy to Price — a binding promise that reflected both the value of the information and Schulz's determination to control its dissemination [011-056].

> Schulz began by telling Price that he had a secret of immense value. But before revealing it, he required an oath of secrecy. [011-055]

Schulz described his discovery in detail. The blow to Schulz's head had caused serious injury, and his condition remained grave [011-050]. Despite his declining health, Schulz ensured that his secret would not die with him — entrusting it to Price, who encoded it into the cipher system.

The dramatic circumstances of the hospital encounter are heightened by the fact that a third party — James Dangle — lay in the next bed, feigning sleep while overhearing everything Schulz said.

> Schulz began by telling Price that he knew he wasn't going to live. Then he went on to say that he felt it cruelly hard luck, because he had accidentally come on a secret which would have brought him an immense fortune. [011-055]

> Schulz was obviously growing weaker and he now spoke with great difficulty. But he made a final effort to go on; 'The key to the cipher —' he began and just then the sister came back into the room. Schulz stopped, but before she left he got a weak turn and fell back unconscious. He never spoke again and next day he was dead. [011-058]

## Death and Legacy

Schulz died of his injuries, never recovering from the head wound sustained during the U-boat action [011-050]. His death, followed by Price's own death, left the secret accessible only through the cipher and tracing — a locked puzzle that would eventually draw Cheyne, Dangle, and others into a deadly game of pursuit and deception.

Schulz's legacy extends beyond the secret itself. His widow, [[frau-schulz|Frau Schulz]], remains entitled to a share of the secret's value [011-065]. The ethical question of how the secret's benefits should be distributed among Schulz's widow, Price's associates, and those who risk their lives to protect it is a recurring subtext of the novel.

> Frau Schulz would get her eighth and the others would each receive a share. [011-065]

## The Manganese Alloy

Schulz's discovery was a process for hardening copper into an alloy as strong as high-carbon steel — a material with enormous commercial value for electrical engineering and manufacturing. The discovery was made accidentally during experiments in the German Submarine Research Department.

> He tested it and found it was, roughly speaking, as hard as high carbon steel and with an even greater tensile strength! Unintentionally he had made a new and unknown alloy. [011-056]

The knowledge that the hardened copper would be worth "nearly its weight in gold" [011-057] explains the lengths to which all parties — Dangle's gang, Cheyne, and eventually the Admiralty — were willing to go to obtain it.
