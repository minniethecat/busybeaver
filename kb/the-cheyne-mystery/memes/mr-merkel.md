# Mr. Merkel

**Mr. Merkel** (referred to as **M. Merkel** in the text) is the senior partner and head of Merkel & Lowenthal, a shipping firm based in Antwerp. His company's freighter, the [[the-lescaut|L'Escaut]], was used by [[dangle|James Dangle]] and his associates to transport gold bullion illegally.

## Role in the Story

Merkel first comes to Inspector French's attention in Chapter 17, during French's investigation in Antwerp. While tracing the movements of Dangle, French visited the offices of several shipping companies. Merkel & Lowenthal's office, located in the Rue des Tanneurs, was described as a modern establishment with plate glass windows, teak furniture, and polished brass fittings—a contrast to the older, more modest offices nearby [017-011].

When French arrived, he was informed that M. Merkel, the head of the business, was away, and he was seen instead by the junior partner, [[mr-lowenthal|M. Lowenthal]] [017-013]. Merkel himself does not appear in person during French's visit.

> "She was owned by Messrs. Merkel & Lowenthal, whose office was farther down the Rue des Tanneurs, and five minutes later he had pushed open the door and was inquiring for the principal." [017-011]

## Significance

The L'Escaut, owned by Merkel & Lowenthal, played a central role in the conspiracy. The vessel sailed in ballast from Antwerp, ostensibly on a routine voyage, but was in fact transporting the stolen gold. French's investigation revealed that Merkel might have been personally involved—one theory considered was that Merkel was "playing a double game," acting as a legitimate shipowner while facilitating the smugglers' operations [017-066–071].

The connection between Merkel's firm and the conspirators was established through [[otto-schulz|Otto Schulz]] (alias Blessington), who had entered into business relations with Merkel. The gold was ultimately recovered from the L'Escaut's wreck site, confirming the vessel's role in the scheme [017-037].

Merkel does not appear in person in the narrative; his role is entirely behind the scenes, communicated through Lowenthal and French's investigative findings.
