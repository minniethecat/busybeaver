Lord Hull is the present Baron Hull, a wealthy aristocrat whose title and fortune play a significant role in the backstory of Arnold Price's claim to nobility.

## Background

The present Lord Hull is a man "well on to eighty and is in failing health" [004-014]. He is described as "a man of enormous wealth" [004-014]. He has no known heir, and unless a claimant comes forward, the title will become extinct upon his death [004-014].

## Role in the Story

Lord Hull does not appear directly in the novel, but his position and legacy form a key element of the Price family's motivation. St. John Price claimed that he, his cousin Arnold Price, and another family member were all descended from the eldest son of Francis, the fifth Baron Hull — a man who led "an evil, dissolute life" and fled to South Africa in the early nineteenth century [004-014]. After the fifth baron's death, the title passed to his second son, Alwyn, because the eldest could not be located [004-014].

## Cheyne's Assessment

Maxwell Cheyne, after hearing Price's account of the Hull inheritance claim, found the story convincing in its facts. "It certainly explained the facts adequately, and Cheyne believed that the statements about Lord Hull were correct" [004-021]. However, Cheyne remained suspicious of Price's motives and declined to discuss the matter further until he received "satisfactory replies to one or two personal questions" [004-021].
