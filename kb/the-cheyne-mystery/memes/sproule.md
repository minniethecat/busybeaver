Mr. Sproule is a resident of 17 Colton Street, Putney, who appears as a crucial witness in the investigation led by Inspector French. His testimony, along with that of his invalid wife, proved valuable in establishing the timeline of events involving a yellow motor-car outside Sime's house. Unlike many other witnesses who were interviewed proactively by French, Sproule came forward voluntarily after seeing a newspaper appeal.

## Role in the Investigation

Mr. Sproule was interviewed by Inspector French during the investigation of the Colton Street area [013-071]. He lived at number 17, adjacent to Sime's residence at number 12 Colton Street [013-071]. His invalid wife, Mrs. Sproule, was also a key witness, having observed a yellow car stopping near Sime's house from her window [015-016].

> "Mr. Sproule, 17 Colton Street. I should be glad to see you if you are in this district, but I couldn't think of taking you out of your way." [013-071]

French's polite tone in addressing Sproule reflects the inspector's methodical and courteous approach to witness interviews, treating even minor informants with professional respect.

## Witness Testimony

Mrs. Sproule, described as "the invalid of Colton Street," provided testimony about the time at which a motor-car had stopped in the street [015-047]. Her observations helped French piece together the movements of the suspects. The yellow car she witnessed was later determined to have left Sime's house during the critical timeframe [015-047].

> "Then I think Mrs. Sproule saw her wearing it today." [016-060]

This observation by French, referring to an item of Joan Merrill's clothing, demonstrated how the Sproules' testimony connected to the larger abduction timeline. French was able to deduce from Mrs. Sproule's sighting whether Joan had been taken directly to Sime's house.

## Significance to the Timeline

French used the Sproules' testimony to reconstruct a critical segment of the abduction timeline. From Horne Terrace to Sime's house in Colton Street was about a ten-minute run. If the car left Horne Terrace about 11:15, it should reach Colton Street a minute or two before the half-hour.

This worked in with the time at which the invalid lady, Mrs. Sproule, had heard the motor stop in the street. To French it seemed clear that Joan Merrill had been taken direct to Sime's house, and kept there until 1:45 p.m. on the following day [017-005].

> "From the statement of Mrs. Sproule, the invalid of Colton Street, the yellow car had left Sime's house at about 1:45." [017-007]

This timing was cross-referenced with the testimony of Hackworth, who saw the car reach the garage at a minute or so before two. From Colton Street to the garage was a ten or twelve minutes' drive, confirming that Blessington must have gone practically direct [017-007]. The discrepancy in the car's occupants — Joan Merrill and other gang members were in the car when it left Colton Street, but Blessington was alone when he reached the garage — became a key puzzle that French had to solve [017-007].

> This worked in with the time at which the invalid lady, Mr. Sproule, had heard the motor stop in the street, and to French it seemed clear that Miss Merrill had been taken direct to Sime's, and kept there until 1:45 p.m. on the following day. [015-016]

## Colton Street Setting

Colton Street itself was a modest location that underscored the working-class character of Sime's hideout:

> "It was like its neighbors, a small two-storied single terrace house of old-fashioned design. Indeed the narrow road, with its two grimy rows of almost working-class dwellings, seemed more like one of those terrible streets built in the last century in the slum districts of provincial towns, than a bit of mid-London." [015-043]

The Sproules' residence at number 17 was typical of this unremarkable street, making their detailed observations all the more valuable to the investigation.
