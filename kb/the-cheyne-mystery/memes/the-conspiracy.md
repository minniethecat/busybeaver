# The Conspiracy

**The Conspiracy** was a criminal enterprise organized by [[dangle|James Dangle]] to obtain the [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price cipher]] and use it to recover [[the-treasure|Otto Schulz's hidden treasure]]. The conspiracy involved multiple members, used violence and deception, and drove the central conflict of the novel.

## Origins

The conspiracy began when [[arnold-price|Arnold Price]]'s letter, containing information about the cipher, fell into Dangle's hands:

> …searched by other members of the conspiracy, and it was for Price's letter that they were looking. [004-001]

Dangle recognized the potential value and assembled a team to pursue it. As Cheyne later realized, every crime against him was connected:

> "The drugging in the hotel in Plymouth, the burglary at Warren Lodge, and now his kidnapping on the Enid were all part and parcel of the same scheme." [004-001]

## Organization

The conspiracy was well-organized and ruthless. [[speedwell|Mr. Speedwell]], a private inquiry agent, was recruited to handle the operational aspects. [[sime|Sime]] served as Dangle's right-hand man. [[susan-dangle|Susan Dangle]], James's sister, acted as an impersonator and accomplice.

[[inspector-french|Inspector French]] later assessed its scope:

> "This business looks uncommonly like conspiracy for criminal purposes." [014-031]

## Methods

The conspirators employed increasingly desperate measures:
- Drugging [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]] at the [[plymouth-drugging|Plymouth hotel]]
- Burglarizing [[warren-lodge|Warren Lodge]] in the [[warren-lodge-burglary|Warren Lodge burglary]]
- Operating from a hideout on [[the-hopefield-avenue-discovery|Hopefield Avenue]]
- Crossing the English Channel in pursuit of [[the-lescaut|the L'Escaut]]

Dangle financed the operation through promises of vast reward:

> "I have paid them twenty pounds on account and they will get a thousand guineas each if my claim is upheld." [004-026]

## Downfall

The conspiracy unraveled when Cheyne enlisted [[inspector-french-takes-charge|Inspector French]] of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]. The professional investigation exposed the gang's operations and led to their arrest. [[sime|Sime]]'s confession provided crucial evidence:

> …were in the conspiracy to get whatever you had. [014-012]

### Speedwell's Betrayal

The conspiracy was further undermined from within. [[speedwell|Mr. Speedwell]], the private detective originally engaged by the Edgecombe Hotel manager to investigate the drugging, was in fact a disgraced former police officer working both sides. Inspector French privately confided to Cheyne that Speedwell had been "dismissed from the force on suspicion of taking bribes to let a burglar get away" and that he "would do anything to curry favor" with the Yard [014-033]. Speedwell's inside knowledge of the conspiracy's movements proved instrumental to French's investigation.

## Significance

The conspiracy in *The Cheyne Mystery* exemplifies the organized criminal networks that feature prominently in Freeman Wills Crofts's fiction. Unlike the lone villains of sensationalist crime fiction, the Dangle gang operates as a coordinated enterprise with specialized roles: Dangle as the strategic leader, Sime as the enforcer, Blessington as the operative, and Susan Dangle as the infiltrator. This structure made the conspiracy more dangerous than a single criminal — and ironically more vulnerable, as each member constituted a potential weak point once Inspector French began applying systematic investigative pressure.

The conspiracy's sophistication also underscores the novel's thematic concern with deception. Every member of the gang presents a false identity to Cheyne at some point — Dangle as Lamson and then as Price, Blessington as Parkes, Sime as Lewisham. The cumulative effect is a world in which trust is impossible and truth must be painstakingly reconstructed through evidence, a theme that resonates with the cipher's role as a puzzle requiring systematic solution.
