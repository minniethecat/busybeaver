# Arnold Price's Cipher

## Origin

Arnold Price was a junior officer serving on a ship of the Blue Funnel Line. Before his death, he entrusted a sealed envelope to his friend [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]], with instructions to open it only if Price did not return from his next voyage. [003-084] Price's ship was subsequently lost, and Cheyne became the keeper of the mysterious document.

> "The sealed envelope was of a size which would hold a foolscap sheet folded in four, and was fairly bulky. It was inscribed: 'To Maxwell Cheyne, of Warren Lodge, Dartmouth, Devonshire, from Arnold Price.'" [003-085]

## Contents

Price had discovered, through the dying confession of a man named St. John, that he was the rightful heir to the barony of Hull. The document contained a cipher or code that would prove his claim — but one crucial document, a birth register, was missing. [004-014] [004-015]

Beyond the inheritance, the cipher was believed to contain the location of a treasure buried by a German submarine commander during World War I. [[blessington|Blessington]] and his gang dedicated themselves to obtaining the document for this reason. [006-026]

:::image fig="Illustration" src="images/illustration-3.png" caption="The cipher document — dozens of circles containing letters and numbers, arranged without apparent plan, with the phrase 'England expects every man to do his duty' written in a clockwise loop through the entire image."
:::

## Role in the Story

The cipher is the MacGuffin driving the entire plot. Every crime committed — the [[plymouth-drugging]], the [[warren-lodge-burglary]], and the kidnapping — was motivated by the conspirators' desire to possess it. Cheyne realized this connection only gradually:

> "A valuable document, this of Price's must surely be, if it was worth such pains to acquire!" [004-002]

## The Kidnapping Ruse

The conspirators went to extraordinary lengths to obtain the cipher. Dangle, posing as an inventor named Lamson, lured Cheyne aboard the launch [[the-enid|Enid]] by promising a demonstration of a "position indicator" for coastal navigation. The entire story was a fabrication:

> "The story of my invention was a fabrication. So far as I am aware no apparatus such as I have described exists: certainly I have not made one. The truth is that you can do me a service, and I took the liberty of inveigling you here in the hope of securing your good offices in the matter." [003-061]

The extent of the deception revealed the conspirators' skill:

> "And what inventive genius they had shown! They had now told him three yarns, all convincing, well-thought-out statements, and all entirely false." [006-026]

## The Demand

Held captive aboard the Enid, Cheyne was presented with a simple ultimatum: hand over Price's letter or face death by starvation. Dangle's threat was explicit:

> "For you see there is neither food nor drink in the cabin and we don't propose to pass any in. You won't get any, Mr. Cheyne, no matter how many days you remain aboard." [003-074]

Dangle later gloated to his confederates about how the tactic worked:

> "It was keeping him without water that did the trick. When I offered him another day to think it over he collapsed like a pricked bubble." [006-013]

## Resolution

The cipher's secret is ultimately uncovered through the investigation of [[inspector-french|Inspector French]], leading to the climactic confrontation with Blessington's gang and the recovery of the buried treasure.
