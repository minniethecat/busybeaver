# The Plymouth Drugging

## Summary

On a damp March day in 1920, [[maxwell-cheyne]] visits Plymouth on business. While resting in the lounge of the [[edgecombe-hotel|Edgecombe Hotel]], he is approached by a stranger calling himself Hubert Parkes -- later revealed to be [[blessington]], the leader of a criminal conspiracy. After sharing lunch and coffee laced with a drug, Cheyne falls unconscious. When he wakes hours later, Parkes has vanished -- but not before searching Cheyne's pockets for a vital document: the sealed envelope from Arnold Price. [001-018] [001-056] [004-001]

## Context

Before the drugging, Cheyne was living a quiet country life at [[warren-lodge|Warren Lodge]] in Devonshire. He had recently received a sealed envelope from Arnold Price, third officer of the S.S. *Maurania*, with instructions to keep it unopened until Price claimed it or authentic news of his death arrived. [003-085] Unknown to Cheyne, this envelope contained the key to a hidden fortune -- and [[blessington|Blessington]]'s conspiracy was determined to obtain it.

The incident began what the author calls "The Cheyne Mystery": "the strange episode which took place in the Edgecombe Hotel in Plymouth, and from which started that extraordinary series of events." [001-005]

## Details

The sequence of events unfolds as follows:

1. **The Approach**: A page announces "Gentleman to see you, sir," presenting a card reading "Mr. Hubert Parkes, Oakleigh, Cleeve Hill, Cheltenham." A short, stoutly built man with fair hair and a toothbrush mustache greets Cheyne, who does not recognize him. [001-014] [001-015] [001-017]

2. **The Conversation**: Parkes claims to be a stranger seeking business advice, engaging Cheyne in polite conversation over lunch. [001-018]

3. **The Drugging**: After coffee, Cheyne feels increasingly drowsy and collapses -- the coffee had been drugged.

> "No?" the manager answered. "I dare say not. I'll tell you what has happened to you, Mr. Cheyne, though I'm sorry to have to admit it could take place in my hotel. You've been drugged. That's what has happened." [001-052]

The manager later confirms: "there was brandy in those cups, but no drug: no trace of a drug in either," deepening the mystery of how Cheyne was incapacitated. [001-080]

> The doctor was quite emphatic and ridiculed the idea of any other explanation. He was physically perfectly sound, and his sleep of the afternoon was not the result of disease or weakness. He had been drugged. [001-065]

### The Trick Flask

:::image fig="Illustration" src="images/illustration-1.png" caption="Diagrams of the trick flask used by Blessington — divided down the middle by a diaphragm, forming two chambers. By inverting the flask from different sides, either drugged or undrugged liquid could be poured at will."
:::

The method of the drugging was revealed later by Dangle himself. The flask was divided down the middle by a diaphragm, forming two chambers holding different liquids. A delicately fitted flap valve would close the passage to either chamber depending on which way the flask was tipped. When pouring from one side, the drugged liquid emerged; from the other, the undrugged. Blessington's convenient fit of coughing gave him the opportunity to turn the flask around between pours. [012-056]

4. **The Search**: While Cheyne lies unconscious in a private room, Parkes searches his pocketbook for Price's letter. [004-001]

> The discovery that his pockets had been gone through while he was under the influence of the drug reduced Cheyne to a state of even more complete mystification than ever. What had the unknown been looking for? [002-001]

5. **The Aftermath**: Cheyne wakes disoriented. A woman claiming to be his sister arranges for him to stay overnight. A phone message is sent to [[warren-lodge|Warren Lodge]] claiming he will remain in Plymouth. [001-079]

> Cheyne stared incredulously. He could not believe he had been drugged. "But," Cheyne objected, "that's all right, only I wasn't drugged. I know exactly what I ate and drank, and Parkes had precisely the same. If I was drugged, he must have been also, and you say he wasn't." [001-055]

## Consequences

The drugging is the first act of the conspiracy. When it fails to yield Price's letter, [[blessington|Blessington]] escalates -- leading to [[warren-lodge-burglary|the burglary at Warren Lodge]] that very night. The hotel manager, fearing scandal, initially resists involving the police: "It would not do the hotel any good if it became known that a visitor had been drugged." [001-086] He later employs the investigation firm of Speedwell and Co. to look into the matter privately. [009-010]

Cheyne himself refuses to let it rest: "But you surely don't suggest that I should take the thing lying down? If I have been drugged, as you say, I must know who has done it, and why." [001-088]

> But in spite of the analysis, the coffee still seemed the doubtful item. Cheyne's sleepy feeling had come on very rapidly immediately after drinking the coffee, before which he had not felt the slightest abnormal symptoms. [003-011]

## Analysis

### Narrative Function

The Plymouth drugging serves as the inciting incident of the novel, establishing several key narrative elements in a single chapter. It introduces the central McGuffin -- Arnold Price's sealed envelope -- without yet revealing its contents, creating sustained mystery that drives the plot through twenty chapters. It also establishes the antagonist's modus operandi: Blessington relies on charm, deception, and chemical means rather than overt violence, a pattern that defines the gang's approach throughout the story.

### Mystery Construction

Crofts employs a layered mystery technique in the drugging scene. The reader, like Cheyne, initially assumes the incident is a random crime. Only gradually does the connection to Price's envelope become apparent, and the full significance -- the hardened copper discovery -- is not revealed until Chapter XI. The drugging thus functions as the first of several revelations that progressively deepen and complicate the central puzzle.

### Character Establishment

The drugging scene introduces Maxwell Cheyne as a protagonist who is competent but vulnerable -- a man of action (war veteran, yachtsman) who is nevertheless outmaneuvered by a more cunning adversary. His response to the drugging -- indignation, determination to investigate, refusal to "take the thing lying down" -- establishes the tenacity that will characterize his behavior throughout the novel.

### Technical Detail

The scientific impossibility of the drugging -- no trace of drug found in any analyzed food or drink -- is a hallmark of Crofts's approach to mystery writing. Rather than glossing over this difficulty, he foregrounds it, making the method of drugging a subsidiary puzzle that engages the reader's analytical faculties. This devotion to technical plausibility, even when it complicates the plot, is characteristic of the "scientific detective" school that Crofts helped pioneer.
