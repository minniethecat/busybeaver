# St. John Price

**St. John Price** is a pivotal backstory figure in *The Cheyne Mystery*. A British diplomat and amateur genealogist, he is the uncle of [[arnold-price]] and cousin of the man impersonating Arnold Price (who uses the alias "Lamson"). His genealogical research into the Hull barony succession forms the foundation of the conspiracy that entangles [[maxwell-cheyne]] throughout the novel — only to be revealed in a dramatic twist as a complete fabrication.

## Role in the Story

St. John Price serves as the **absent catalyst** of the novel's central deception. Though he never appears directly in the narrative (having died before the story begins), his purported discoveries about the Hull succession provide the gang with their most elaborate cover story. The tale of his genealogical work is used to manipulate Cheyne into cooperating with the conspirators.

In [008-004], [[inspector-french]] confirms through Admiralty contacts that "No such diplomat as St. John Price had ever existed," revealing him as a fictional construct of [[dangle]]'s "nimble brain." This revelation marks a turning point in Cheyne's understanding of the conspiracy.

## Background

According to the fabricated story told by "Lamson" (the false Arnold Price) in [004-009], St. John Price was a British diplomat who served in the Foreign Office and traveled to South Africa on diplomatic business during the First World War. He allegedly spent years tracing the lineage of Francis, the fifth [[lord-hull]], believing that the eldest son — a disreputable figure who had fled England for South Africa in the early 19th century — had legitimate descendants who were the rightful heirs to the barony.

> "[St. John] had after many years' labor traced what he believed was a lineal descent from the scapegrace, and he had utilized his visit to South Africa to make further inquiries. There he had unearthed the record of a marriage, which, he believed, completed the proofs he sought." — [004-014]

## The Maurania Incident

The most dramatic element of the St. John Price story involves his death aboard the RMS *Maurania*. According to [004-012], the ship was struck by another vessel while running without lights in the English Channel near the end of a wartime voyage. [[arnold-price]], serving as third officer on the same ship, attempted to rescue a passenger standing at the rail — only to discover afterward that the passenger was his own uncle, St. John Price.

St. John lingered for several hours after the accident, conscious and able to communicate. During this time, he told Arnold about the Hull succession claim and handed over an attested copy of a marriage certificate, making a new will leaving all genealogical documents to Arnold. As recorded in [004-014]:

> "St. John lingered for some hours, during most of which time he was conscious, and it was then that he told Arnold about his belief, that he, Arnold, was heir to the barony of Hull."

## Revelations and Exposure

The truth about St. John Price emerges gradually. In [006-026], Cheyne reflects on the three elaborate lies the gang has told him, including "the story of the Hull succession, including the interesting episode of the attempted rescue of the uncle St. John Price." He marvels at the "trouble which had been taken in making each story watertight."

The definitive exposure comes in [008-004] when Inspector French confirms through his Admiralty contact, a clerk named Hake, that St. John Price was entirely fictional. French also learns that the real Arnold Price had disappeared from his ship in Bombay — a detail that deepens the mystery. In a further confrontation in [011-034], Cheyne sarcastically dismisses any further "lies about St. John Price and the Hull succession."

The final confirmation appears in [011-062] when Dangle himself admits: "I learned at the same time that I learned St. John Price was a myth."

## Significance

St. John Price represents one of the novel's most sophisticated narrative devices. As a character who is **fictional within the fiction**, he demonstrates the elaborate lengths to which [[dangle]]'s gang goes to deceive Cheyne. The St. John Price fabrication is notable for its internal consistency and detail — including diplomatic service, genealogical research, a dramatic sea rescue, and a deathbed revelation — qualities that make it one of the most memorable elements of the conspiracy.

The St. John Price subplot also connects to the novel's broader themes of identity, deception, and the blurring of truth and fiction. The fact that the real [[arnold-price]] (the actual third officer of the *Maurania*) had indeed served on that ship — and had subsequently vanished — adds a layer of authenticity that makes the fabrication more credible and more disturbing.
