## Origin

The linen tracing was created by [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price]] as the physical medium for encoding [[schulzs-secret|Otto Schulz's secret]]. Using a sheet of tracing linen approximately fifteen inches high [006-037], Price overlaid the cipher grid in such a way that the tracing, when properly aligned, would reveal the hidden message. The choice of tracing linen — translucent, durable, and portable — was deliberate: it allowed the document to be concealed, transported, and aligned with other materials to decode the message.

> It was a sheet of tracing linen some fifteen inches high, covered with an intricate pattern of lines and letters. [006-037]

## Role in the Story

The tracing is the central MacGuffin of *The Cheyne Mystery* — the object that every party seeks to possess. Its importance is established early, when [[dangle|James Dangle]] orchestrates the [[warren-lodge-burglary|burglary at Warren Lodge]] specifically to obtain it [006-044]. The tracing changes hands multiple times throughout the narrative, each transfer escalating the conflict and driving the plot forward.

### The Burglary

Dangle and his associate Sime break into [[warren-lodge|Warren Lodge]] to steal the tracing. During the burglary, Cheyne hears Dangle directing the search: "Keep guard of the house, Sime. I'll search the room" [006-044]. The theft is successful, and Dangle folds the tracing and conceals it in his pocket [006-038].

> He heard Dangle's voice: "Keep guard of the house, Sime. I'll search the room." [006-044]

### The Loss and Recovery

The tracing does not remain in Dangle's possession for long. In a dramatic reversal, the document is taken from him, and the cry goes up: "It's gone, James! The tracing's gone!" [006-044]. This moment marks the beginning of an extended pursuit, as Dangle desperately tries to recover what he has lost and Cheyne races to find it first.

> Sounds indicative of a struggle reached him, followed by Susan Dangle's voice shouting: "It's gone, James! The tracing's gone!" [006-044]

### The Hopefield Avenue Search

Cheyne's search for the tracing leads him to the house in [[hopefield-avenue|Hopefield Avenue]], where he recovers the document after a tense search of likely hiding places [010-041], [010-043]. The moment of recovery is visceral: Cheyne recognizes the familiar lilac-gray color of the tracing paper, seizes it, and escapes [010-049], [010-050].

> His eye caught a gleam of the familiar lilac gray of the tracing. He plunged in his hand, seized the tracing, thrust it into his pocket. [010-049]

## Significance

The tracing's significance extends beyond its physical value:

1. **Cryptographic Key**: The tracing is essential for solving the [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price cipher]]. Without it, the cipher message cannot be decoded [007-085].
2. **Proof of Ownership**: Possession of the tracing establishes a claim to [[schulzs-secret|Schulz's secret]], making it a document of legal and financial significance.
3. **Leverage**: Control of the tracing gives its holder power over others — Dangle uses it to manipulate, while Cheyne uses it to bargain for [[joan-merrill|Joan Merrill]]'s freedom.

> "And you have no idea what the tracing was?" — The question underscores how the tracing's true nature remains mysterious even to those who possess it. [007-085]

## Physical Description

The tracing is described as a sheet of tracing linen, distinguished by its lilac-gray color [010-049]. It measures approximately fifteen inches in height [006-037] and bears an intricate pattern of lines and letters. The document is folded for concealment and transport [006-038]. Its distinctive color makes it recognizable at a glance to those who have seen it before.

## Decoding

The tracing's cipher proved impenetrable to the conspirators, despite exhaustive efforts. Dangle admitted to Cheyne: "I wouldn't like to say how many hours we've spent — all of us — working at it. And I don't think there's a book on ciphers in the whole of London that we haven't read. And not a glimmer of light from any of them!" [012-046] The solution came only through [[inspector-french|Inspector French]]'s systematic approach, combining the tracing with photographic enlargements to overlay proportionate grids [018-086]. This breakthrough revealed the tracing as a map — most likely of England — that pointed the way to the [[the-lescaut|L'Escaut]] at sea [019-007].

> "My word, if we aren't a set of blithering idiots! Of course! 'England expects' — and the tracing is a map of England!" [018-085]

## In the Public Eye

The tracing and its significance attracted attention beyond the immediate circle of investigators. When [[joan-merrill|Joan Merrill]] first heard Cheyne's story of the tracing, she declared breathlessly: "It was a cipher telling where there was buried treasure! Isn't that all that is wanted to make it complete?" [007-086] The document's role as the key to a wartime treasure gave the affair an air of romantic adventure that captivated all who learned of it. The conspirators themselves — [[blessington|Blessington]], [[dangle|Dangle]], [[sime|Sime]], and [[susan-dangle|Susan Dangle]] — had been drawn into the plot not merely by greed but by the compelling mystery the tracing represented [006-026].

> "What fun! And you have no idea what the tracing was?" [007-085]
