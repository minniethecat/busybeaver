## Summary

Speedwell's Intervention refers to the point in *The Cheyne Mystery* when Mr. Speedwell, an agent of Horton and Lavender's Private Inquiry Agency, enters the case and transforms the investigation from an amateur pursuit into a professionally conducted operation [009-001]. His methodical approach, investigative resources, and willingness to take calculated risks provide [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]] with the support he needs to counter [[dangle|James Dangle]]'s criminal enterprise and ultimately rescue [[joan-merrill|Joan Merrill]].

## Context

### Before the Intervention

Prior to Speedwell's involvement, Cheyne's investigation was hampered by limited resources and the need to act alone. The [[plymouth-drugging|Plymouth drugging]] in [[plymouth|Plymouth]] had demonstrated the ruthlessness of his adversaries, while the [[warren-lodge-burglary|burglary at Warren Lodge]] showed their reach. Cheyne's attempts to decode the [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price cipher]] and recover the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]] were those of a determined amateur -- courageous but lacking systematic support.

### Speedwell's Introduction

Speedwell first appears in connection with Horton and Lavender's, a private inquiry agency. When Cheyne visits their offices and asks for Mr. Speedwell, he finds him available -- "by good fortune Mr. Speedwell was within" [009-001]. This moment of luck proves decisive, as Speedwell's professional skills complement Cheyne's personal knowledge of the case.

> Cheyne called at the offices of Horton and Lavender's Private Inquiry Agency and asked if their Mr. Speedwell was within. By good fortune Mr. Speedwell was within. [009-001]

## Details

### The Decisive Meeting

Speedwell's method is characterized by a calm, business-like demeanor that contrasts with Cheyne's emotional investment. When Cheyne bursts out with frustration, Speedwell responds with professional composure [009-022]. His questioning is pointed and strategic, designed to extract the maximum information while revealing minimal reaction.

> "Well, Mr. Speedwell, what of it? If you are so well informed, perhaps you can tell me what to do?" Cheyne burst out. [009-022]

### The Breaking and Entering Decision

One of Speedwell's most consequential decisions is to endorse what he calls "the breaking and entering" -- a calculated illegal act designed to recover the tracing from Dangle's possession [009-021]. This decision demonstrates Speedwell's pragmatic approach: he is willing to operate at the edge of legality to achieve justice, recognizing that conventional methods are insufficient against an adversary like Dangle.

> "The breaking and entering." Speedwell spoke now in a decisive, business-like tone. [009-021]

### The Horton and Lavender Ruse

A comic interlude reveals that the impressive-sounding "Horton and Lavender" firm is entirely fictitious:

> "Not today, I'm afraid," she answered. "You see Mr. Horton has been dead over ten years and Mr. Lavender at least five." [009-035]

Cheyne, storming out to complain to the principals, discovers that Speedwell himself is the sole proprietor. This revelation is characteristic of Speedwell's modus operandi: the appearance of institutional weight concealing a one-man operation.

### Strategic Counsel

Speedwell provides more than operational support; he offers strategic guidance that shapes the entire approach to the case. His "melancholy voice" delivers assessments that are sober and realistic, tempering Cheyne's impulses with professional judgment [009-047]. He advises on the timing of actions, the management of information, and the assessment of risk -- particularly where Joan Merrill's safety is concerned.

> "I sell my wares in the best market, Mr. Cheyne," he declared. "You couldn't blame me for that; it's only business. But I don't want to drive a hard bargain with you. I would rather have an amicable settlement. I'm always one for peace and goodwill." [009-039]

## Analysis

### The Ambiguous Professional

Speedwell occupies a morally ambiguous position in the novel's economy of justice. He is neither a villain like Dangle nor an official upholder of the law like Inspector French. Instead, he operates in the gray zone of private detection, selling information to the highest bidder while maintaining a rough code of professional ethics. His willingness to take money from both sides -- offering to withhold information from Dangle's gang if Cheyne pays more -- would be corruption in a police officer but is presented as normal business practice for a private agent.

### Comic Relief and Social Commentary

The Horton and Lavender revelation is one of the novel's few genuinely comic moments, and it serves a dual purpose. On the surface, it provides relief from the mounting tension of the conspiracy plot. At a deeper level, it offers quiet social commentary on the pretensions of the professional classes in post-war London -- the gap between institutional appearance and individual reality, the necessity of puffery in a competitive marketplace.

### The Information Economy

Speedwell's intervention introduces the theme of information as commodity. Throughout the novel, knowledge is the true currency: who knows what, when they know it, and what they can do with that knowledge. Speedwell is the novel's purest embodiment of this principle -- he produces no physical goods, performs no physical acts (beyond investigation), and creates value solely by acquiring and trading information. His dealings with Cheyne make explicit what is implicit throughout the novel: that the mystery's resolution depends less on physical courage than on superior intelligence.

### Speedwell and the Detective Tradition

Speedwell belongs to a tradition of private inquiry agents in British detective fiction that stretches from Sherlock Holmes's Baker Street Irregulars through the seedy operatives of Graham Greene. Unlike the gentleman-amateur detective of the Golden Age tradition, Speedwell is a working professional who charges by the question and haggles over fees. His presence in the novel provides a bridge between the amateur world of Cheyne and the official world of Inspector French, and his mercenary pragmatism serves as a foil to both Cheyne's impulsive honor and French's institutional rectitude.

## Consequences

Speedwell's intervention has far-reaching consequences for the investigation:

1. **Professional Resources**: The agency's network and expertise are brought to bear on tracking Dangle's movements and uncovering his associates.
2. **Tactical Shift**: The investigation moves from reactive defense to proactive offense, including the calculated risk of "breaking and entering."
3. **Moral Support**: Cheyne gains a capable ally, reducing the psychological burden of fighting alone.
4. **Escalation**: Dangle recognizes the increased threat and responds with greater ruthlessness, including the abduction of Joan Merrill.
