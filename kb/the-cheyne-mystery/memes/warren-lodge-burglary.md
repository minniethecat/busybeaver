# The Warren Lodge Burglary

## Summary

The Warren Lodge burglary was the second criminal act in the conspiracy to obtain [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price's secret document]]. Following the failed [[plymouth-drugging|Plymouth drugging]], the conspirators broke into [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne's]] home while he was away, cracked his safe, and searched for the document.

## Events

Cheyne was returning from Plymouth on the night of the burglary, riding his motor bicycle along the deserted road. "The night was fine, the mists of the day having cleared away, and a three-quarter moon shone brilliantly out of a blue-black sky." [002-027]

Upon arriving at [[warren-lodge|Warren Lodge]], Cheyne found the household in disarray. His mother and the chauffeur James discovered that the safe had been broken open and its contents rifled through. [002-077]

> "As soon as we were satisfied the burglars had gone James went home to call up the police. Then he came back and we began a second search to see what had been stolen." [002-081]

The conspirators had sent a forged telegram to lure Mrs. Cheyne and Agatha away from the house:

> You and Agatha please come without fail to Newton Abbot by 5:15 train to meet self and Ackfield about unexpected financial development. Urgent that you sign papers today. [002-083]

Cheyne was staggered by how much the criminals knew about his family. "Not only had he known of Cheyne's war experiences and literary efforts and of his visit that day to the Edgecombe, but now it seemed that he had also known his address, of his mother and sister, and, most amazing thing of all, of the fact that Mr. Ackfield of Plymouth was their lawyer and confidential adviser!" [002-083]

## Investigation

Cheyne interviewed the cook, described as "an elderly woman, small and plain and stout," who had been with the family since they purchased the house. [002-086] He also consulted the Plymouth police and his banker in the days following.

The safe was built into the wall of Cheyne's study. When he examined it, the doors were standing open, the papers tossed about:

> There was very little in the safe and it did not take Cheyne many seconds to go through it. The papers were tossed about -- he could swear someone had turned them over -- but none seemed to have been removed. [002-069]

Sergeant Kirby, stooping over the opened safe, greeted him grimly. "Good evening sir," he said in an impressive tone. "This is a bad business." [002-067] Yet nothing had actually been stolen -- not the Treasury notes, not the silver trophies, not even Mrs. Cheyne's jewelry:

> "Nothing!" Mr. Cheyne's tone conveyed the wonder she evidently felt. "Nothing whatever! Or at least we can't find that anything is missing." [002-065]

The two maids, cook and Susan, had been found bound and gagged in cook's bedroom. Cheyne questioned them and learned that two men -- "one was tall and powerfully built, with dark hair and clean shaven, the other small and pale" -- had overpowered them at the front door before searching the house. [002-088]

Inspector [[inspector-french|French]] of Scotland Yard was later called in to investigate. He noted the professional nature of the break-in and connected it to the earlier Plymouth drugging. [003-023]

## Significance

The burglary convinced Cheyne that the criminals were after something of great value -- Arnold Price's sealed envelope. As he reflected: "A valuable document, this of Price's must surely be, if it was worth such pains to acquire!" [004-002] The realization that the drugging and burglary were connected to the same object marked a turning point in the investigation.

The burglary also revealed the full scope of the conspiracy's knowledge. The forged telegram demonstrated that the criminals knew Cheyne's address, his family members' names, his lawyer, and his movements. This level of intelligence was later traced to [[susan-dangle|Susan]], the parlormaid, who had been planted in the household as a spy.

The episode also established a pattern that would recur throughout the novel: the conspirators used precision and insider knowledge rather than brute force, yet they were repeatedly thwarted because they could not locate the document they sought.
