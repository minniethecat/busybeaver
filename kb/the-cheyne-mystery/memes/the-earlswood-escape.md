## Summary

The Earlswood Escape was Maxwell Cheyne's audacious night-time break-in at James Dangle's house, "Earlswood" on Dalton Road in Wembley, to recover the stolen linen tracing. After locating the tracing hidden inside a desk panel, Cheyne was discovered by Dangle and Susan. He made a dramatic escape, hurling a chair at Dangle to break free, fleeing through the back garden and lane while Joan Merrill recovered the tracing he threw over a wall [1791-1799].

## Context

Following the discovery of the conspirators' hideout through information from Speedwell's investigation, Cheyne and Joan Merrill had identified "Earlswood" — a secluded house at a crossroads in Wembley — as Dangle and Blessington's residence [210051-210684]. After reconnaissance missions to study the layout and the surrounding lanes, they devised a plan: Joan would ring the front doorbell as a diversion while Cheyne would break in through the back to search for the linen tracing [219271].

## Details

On the appointed evening, Cheyne and Joan approached Earlswood under cover of darkness. The house was in darkness and appeared empty. Joan rang the front doorbell to confirm it was unoccupied while Cheyne slipped through the back garden [219271-219791]. Using a jemmy, Cheyne forced his way into the house and began searching for the tracing.

After an extensive search, he discovered the document hidden inside a desk panel. As he was working to extract it, the front door opened — Dangle and Susan had returned unexpectedly [1791]. Cheyne desperately pried the panel free, seized the tracing, and prepared to flee.

Dangle rushed upstairs to intercept him. In a moment of quick thinking, Cheyne seized a light cane chair from near the stairhead and hurled it at Dangle's head as he charged down. Dangle threw up his arms to protect his face, giving Cheyne the precious seconds needed to reach the hall [1795]. Both Dangle and Susan clutched at him, but Cheyne twisted free and sprinted for the back door.

Following their pre-arranged emergency plan, Cheyne threw the tracing over a wall at a pre-designated hiding place where Joan would later recover it [1797]. He then raced through the garden gate and along the lane. Though Dangle gave chase, Cheyne's lighter build and athletic training gave him the advantage, and he successfully escaped [1799].

## Consequences

The Earlswood escape successfully recovered the linen tracing — a crucial document in the mystery of Otto Schulz's secret. However, the operation had unintended consequences: Joan Merrill, who had been waiting to recover the tracing thrown over the wall, was captured by Dangle. Her disappearance prompted Cheyne to seek help from Inspector French, bringing the official police investigation into the case. The escape also escalated the conflict — Dangle later admitted that the conspirators now faced the choice of either murdering Cheyne and Joan or recruiting them as partners [1947].
