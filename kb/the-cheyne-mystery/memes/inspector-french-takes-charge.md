# Inspector French Takes Charge

**Inspector French Takes Charge** marks the turning point in the Cheyne mystery when the investigation transitions from [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]]'s amateur efforts to a professional police inquiry led by [[inspector-french|Inspector French]] of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]].

## Background

After the events at [[the-hopefield-avenue-discovery|Hopefield Avenue]] and the conspirators' escape, Cheyne realized that his personal resources were insufficient. He decided to enlist professional help at Scotland Yard.

## The Meeting at Scotland Yard

Cheyne visited Scotland Yard and was introduced to Inspector French:

> ...he was told that Inspector French would see him. [012-079]

> "I am Inspector French. You wish to consult us?" [013-002]

Inspector French listened attentively to Cheyne's complete account:

> Inspector French listened to his recital with close attention. [013-007]

He also met [[joan-merrill|Joan Merrill]], who accompanied Cheyne:

> Inspector French bowed to her. [013-033]

## The Professional Investigation

Under Inspector French's direction, the investigation became systematic and methodical. He began each day by organizing the evidence:

> ...reaching the Yard next morning Inspector French began his day by compiling a comprehensive summary of the case. [015-001]

His quiet, observant manner concealed a sharp analytical mind:

> Inspector French had been sitting quite still, his eyes fixed on the speaker. [014-016]

## The Resolution

Inspector French followed the trail across the English Channel to [[the-lescaut|the L'Escaut]], where he made the final arrests and recovered the treasure:

> "I am Inspector French of Scotland Yard," he announced. [020-032]

His work led to the successful conclusion of the case and the distribution of the recovered fortune:

> ...£1,000 went to Inspector French -- also Joan's gift... [020-046]

## Analysis

### The Professional Enters the Narrative

Inspector French's arrival in Chapter XIII represents a structural shift in the novel's investigative methodology. Up to this point, the investigation has been conducted by amateurs -- Cheyne's determined but impulsive efforts, Joan Merrill's resourceful collaboration, and Speedwell's mercenary information-trading. French brings to the case what none of them possess: institutional authority, systematic method, and the resources of Scotland Yard.

### The Crofts Formula

Inspector French is Freeman Wills Crofts's series detective, appearing in multiple novels. His introduction here follows a pattern that became Crofts's trademark: the amateur investigator reaches an impasse, the professional step in, and the resolution proceeds through methodical police work rather than brilliant deduction. French's approach -- exhaustive searches, patient questioning of witnesses, careful cross-referencing of timelines -- exemplifies the "police procedural" avant la lettre that Crofts helped pioneer.

### The Speed of Professional Investigation

The chapters following French's assumption of command demonstrate the acceleration that professional resources bring. Within hours, French has surrounded Earlswood, searched Sime's house, interviewed the invalid witness Mrs. Sproule, and circulated descriptions of the yellow car. The contrast with Cheyne's weeks of solitary effort is stark and deliberate, emphasizing the novel's implicit argument for professional policing over amateur vigilantism.

### Character Dynamics

French's relationship with Cheyne is notably different from his relationship with Speedwell. With Cheyne, French is patient, sympathetic, and avuncular -- he puts the young man at ease and takes his extraordinary story seriously. With Speedwell, French adopts a tone of ironic amusement ("Your story, as I told you, sounds like a novel"). This modulation of tone demonstrates French's social intelligence and his ability to extract information from varied sources -- a skill as important to his success as his analytical abilities.

### The European Dimension

French's investigation crosses national boundaries, following the gang from London to Antwerp and ultimately to the Atlantic. This international scope -- unusual for detective fiction of the 1920s -- reflects the post-war reality of a Europe in which borders had become more permeable and criminal enterprises more transnational. French's comfort operating across jurisdictions marks him as a modern detective for a modern world.
