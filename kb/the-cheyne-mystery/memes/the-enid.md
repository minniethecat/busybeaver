# The Enid

## Description

The *Enid* was a steam launch or small yacht used by [[blessington|Blessington]] and his accomplices as a base of operations. It was aboard the *Enid* that Blessington held [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]] prisoner and confessed to the earlier crimes.

The vessel was registered at Devonport and described as "a fair-sized motor launch" that was "not a graceful boat, but looked strongly built, showing a high bluff bow, a square stern and lines suggestive of speed" [003-020]. Cheyne, himself an experienced yachtsman, assessed it approvingly as "a sea boat" [003-020].

> As he spoke he pulled round the stern of the launch upon which Cheyne observed the words "Enid, Devonport." [003-020]

## The Abduction

The conspiracy to obtain [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price's cipher]] reached its most brazen stage when Dangle (posing as the inventor Arthur Lamson) lured Cheyne aboard the *Enid* under the pretense of demonstrating a new "position indicator" for coastal navigation [003-019], [003-020]. Once Cheyne was in the forward cabin examining the supposed apparatus, Dangle locked the steel-plated door and revealed his true purpose:

> "I'm sorry, Mr. Cheyne, for this incivility. The story of my invention was a fabrication." [003-061]

## Role in the Conspiracy

After the failed [[plymouth-drugging]] and [[warren-lodge-burglary]], Blessington escalated his tactics. He abducted Cheyne and brought him aboard the *Enid*, where Cheyne was held captive while the conspirators attempted to extract information about [[arnold-price-cipher|Arnold Price's cipher]]. [006-018]

It was aboard the *Enid* that Blessington made his confession, admitting to Cheyne:

> "I succeeded in drugging you." [012-046]

## The Ordeal

Cheyne's captivity aboard the *Enid* was a harrowing experience. Locked in a cabin with the deadlights shipped (preventing any signal for help), denied food and water, he endured a night he would never forget:

> "Then began for Cheyne a period which he could never afterwards look back on without a shudder. Never could he have believed that a night could be so long, that time could drag so slowly." [004-004]

The deprivation was psychological as much as physical:

> "His whole being cried out for water. He could not have described the sensation, but it was very intense, and with every hour that passed it grew stronger." [004-004]

Dangle later admitted to his confederates that it was "keeping him without water that did the trick. When I offered him another day to think it over he collapsed like a pricked bubble" [006-013].

## Key Events Aboard

Blessington explained his motives to Cheyne while on the *Enid*. He revealed that he sought the German submarine commander's buried treasure — a fortune hidden during World War I — and that Arnold Price's sealed envelope held the key to its location. [006-026] [003-085]

The *Enid* was one of several vessels used during the conspiracy. As [[inspector-french|Inspector French]] closed in, Blessington transferred to another vessel, the *L'Escaut*, for the final sea chase. [006-044] [006-047]

## Fate

The *Enid* was eventually abandoned or seized as part of the investigation by [[inspector-french|Inspector French]] and [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]. Its crew — Blessington, Dangle, and Sime — were captured in the climactic confrontation. [020-032]

## Significance

The *Enid* serves as the setting for the novel's most intense psychological ordeal. Unlike the other vessels in the story — Cheyne's yacht, or the tramp steamer L'Escaut — the *Enid* is a place of confinement and helplessness. The locked cabin with its steel-plated door represents the conspirators' complete dominance over their victim at this stage of the narrative, making Cheyne's eventual escape and triumph all the more satisfying. The *Enid* also marks the point at which the conspiracy transitions from surreptitious methods (drugging, burglary) to open criminality (kidnapping, false imprisonment, threatened murder), a crucial escalation that eventually draws the attention of Scotland Yard.
