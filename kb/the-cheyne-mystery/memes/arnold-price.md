## Background

Arnold Price was a junior officer in the merchant navy, serving as third officer aboard the S.S. Maurania [003-085]. During the First World War, he was wounded and sent to a hospital where he met Otto Schulz, a German U-boat captain [003-084]. It was there that Schulz shared his discovery with Price, who swore an oath of secrecy before receiving the information [011-055].

Price's background gave him the technical skills to create a sophisticated cipher system. His address was in Dartmouth, Devonshire [003-085], connecting him to the maritime world of the Devon coast — a geography that would later prove significant in the pursuit of the [[the-lescaut|L'Escaut]].

> Arnold Price was a junior officer in one of the merchant navy vessels. [003-084]

## Role in the Story

Arnold Price is a pivotal but largely off-stage figure in *The Cheyne Mystery*. As the creator of the [[arnold-price-cipher|cipher]] that bears his name, Price is the link between [[schulzs-secret|Otto Schulz's original discovery]] and the events that entangle [[maxwell-cheyne|Maxwell Cheyne]]. Although Price himself does not appear directly in the narrative (having died before the story begins), his actions — encoding Schulz's secret into cipher form and creating the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]] — set the entire plot in motion.

> "We want Arnold Price's letter." [003-080]

## The Cipher

Price's most significant act was encoding Schulz's secret into cipher form. He created both the [[arnold-price-cipher|cipher system]] and the [[the-tracing|linen tracing]] — a physical document that, when overlaid on the cipher text, would reveal the hidden message. The cipher and tracing together constitute a two-factor security system: neither alone is sufficient to decode the message.

Price's motivations were protective. By encoding the secret rather than writing it plainly, he ensured that only someone with both the cipher text and the tracing — and the knowledge of how to use them — could access Schulz's discovery. This precaution proved prescient, as the tracing and cipher became separated after Price's death, setting up the conflict that drives the novel.

## The Rescue of Otto Schulz

Price's connection to Schulz began with an act of extraordinary courage. During the U-boat engagement that saw the Maurania shell a German submarine, Price volunteered for the dangerous boat party sent to rescue survivors.

> Price was the first man to offer, and they got a boat lowered away and pulled for the submarine. [011-045]

As the rescue unfolded, the U-boat captain — Otto Schulz — was sinking beneath the waves when Price dived in after him.

> The captain was there clinging on to a lifebelt, but just as the boat came up he let go and was sinking, when Arnold Price jumped overboard and caught him and supported him until they got a rope round him and pulled him aboard. [011-045]

This rescue, performed at the risk of Price's own life, created the bond of gratitude that led Schulz — dying in hospital — to entrust Price with his secret.

> But all the same Price had saved his life, and what was more, had saved it at the risk of his own. [011-045]

## Death and Legacy

Price died before the events of the novel, along with Schulz. Their deaths left the secret locked in code, with no living person knowing its full contents. The tracing and cipher passed through various hands until they came to the attention of [[dangle|James Dangle]], whose determination to decode them drives the criminal conspiracy.

Price's legacy is ambiguous. His cipher protected the secret from casual discovery, but it also created the conditions for the violence and abduction that follow. The question of whether Price's elaborate precautions were ultimately helpful or harmful is one of the novel's implicit themes.
