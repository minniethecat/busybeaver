# Magic Pitcher

## Description

The Magic Pitcher is a wish-granting vessel that can produce any food or drink its owner desires. It is introduced as a mysterious gift to the woodcutter Subha Datta, who discovers it in the forest. The pitcher is described as unique: *"It's a magic pitcher—the only one there is in the"* [004-012] world, establishing it as a singular artifact whose value cannot be replicated.

The pitcher's mechanism is simple and absolute: whatever the owner wishes for, the pitcher provides. This boundlessness is both its gift and its danger. The woodcutter's family initially uses it prudently, but the temptation of unlimited provision proves difficult to manage. The narrator poses the question directly: *"what would you have wished for if you had had a magic pitcher?"* [004-023], inviting the reader to reflect on the moral weight of unbounded desire.

## Magical Properties

The pitcher's magic operates through verbal command or silent wish — the text does not specify an incantation, suggesting the magic is inherent to the object rather than activated by ritual. Its primary power is the conjuration of food: *"he wished for"* sustenance and it appeared. Unlike many magical objects in folklore, the pitcher has no apparent limitation on frequency or volume of use. It never runs dry, never refuses a wish, and never demands payment.

This unlimited nature distinguishes it from more constrained magical artifacts like the magic staff (which grants invisibility) or the magic shoes (which grant speed). The pitcher's power is creative rather than transformative — it produces rather than changes.

## Story Role

The Magic Pitcher is the central narrative engine of the first tale in the collection. Its discovery by Subha Datta sets the entire plot in motion: the woodcutter's initial secrecy, his disappearance into the forest to *"go a very long way into the forest"* [001-019] where the pitcher was found, the gradual revelation to his family, and the sons' eventual access to its power.

The pitcher represents a test of character. Subha Datta's instinct to hide it from his family reflects a mix of protectiveness and possessiveness. When his sons learn of it, their reaction drives the story's second act: *"I will have the magic pitcher"* [009-002], one declares, introducing the theme of inheritance and entitlement. The pitcher thus functions as both a blessing and a source of moral tension within the family.

## Symbolic Meaning

The Magic Pitcher embodies the folkloric theme of the wish-granting object, but with a specifically Indian moral framing. Unlike the European cornucopia or Aladdin's lamp, the pitcher's danger lies not in malicious genies but in the human heart: the more it gives, the more its owners want. *"By slow degrees the woodcutter"* [014-007] changed, suggesting that unlimited provision erodes character gradually rather than suddenly. The pitcher is thus a meditation on contentment — the Sanskrit virtue of *santosha* — and the danger of desires that grow to match their means of satisfaction.
