## Description

Jihva is a young servant girl working in the royal palace, introduced in "A Clever Thief." Her name is explicitly glossed in the text: "a young servant girl in the palace whose name was Jihva, which is the Sanskrit word for tongue."[^1] The name proves fitting, for it is Jihva's inability to keep silent about the hidden treasure that sets the story's climax in motion.

Jihva had been told by a man where the king's stolen money and jewels were hidden. She then "slipped away to see if she could get that guard to let her see the prisoner,"[^2] intending to bribe the supposed holy man Hari-Sarman not to reveal her involvement. The guard allowed her to listen at the keyhole, where she overheard Hari-Sarman — who was actually talking to himself about a completely different matter — repeating her name: "Jihva! Jihva! Thou art the cause of this suffering."[^3]

## Role in the Narrative

Terrified that her secret had been supernaturally discovered, Jihva "threw herself at his feet and, clinging to his knees, began to weep and moan,"[^4] confessing everything. She offered to give Hari-Sarman all her money if he would not expose her,[^5] and hurried away to fetch it. When Hari-Sarman's cleverness was tested again by the king, Jihva was brought forward: she was asked to confirm whether Hari-Sarman truly knew about the theft, and her terrified reaction inadvertently validated his reputation as a seer.

Jihva functions as the unwitting instrument of Hari-Sarman's success. Her guilty conscience — born of complicity in the theft — transforms Hari-Sarman's accidental muttering into apparent divine knowledge. Without Jihva's fear-driven confession, the story's resolution would be impossible.
