# Rupa-Sikha

## Role in the Stories

Rupa-Sikha appears in "The Jewelled Arrow" as the daughter of the powerful and dangerous magician [[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]]. She is introduced when prince [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]], fleeing into the forest after a disastrous hunting expedition, stumbles upon her home. The narrator introduces her simply: "the girl, whose name was Rupa-Sikha" (052-035). Her role is that of the helper — a clever young woman who uses her intelligence and insider knowledge of her father's ways to protect the prince she comes to love. She is the classic fairy-tale figure of the magician's daughter who defies her villainous parent for love.

## Key Actions

When Sringa-Bhuja arrives at her home, Rupa-Sikha "did not take long to decide what was the right thing to do" (053-001). She immediately warns him of the dangers her father poses, demonstrating her quick judgment and moral clarity. She leads the prince far away from the magician's dwelling to a place of temporary safety (053-027), then returns to face her father alone.

Rupa-Sikha's most cunning action comes when she realizes her father suspects she has hidden something from him. When Agni-Sikha demands she send for the stranger, she replies cleverly: "I cannot send, for none knows where I have gone" (053-064). She then extracts a binding vow from the magician — forcing him to swear that not a hair of the stranger's head will be harmed — before she reveals the prince's whereabouts. She makes him say the exact words: "Not a hair of his head will be hurt" (053-072), after which "Rupa-Sikha sped away, as if on the wings of a bird" to bring the prince back (053-078).

Her cleverness continues throughout the prince's ordeal with Agni-Sikha. When Sringa-Bhuja follows the servants to what appears to be a trap, "Rupa-Sikha managed to whisper to him" a crucial piece of advice (055-001). She later sends him a secret message of warning that proves prophetic: everything "happened exactly as Rupa-Sikha described" (055-020).

## Relationships

Rupa-Sikha's defining relationship is with her father **Agni-Sikha**, the magician who "loves not strangers" (052-032). She knows her father's true nature intimately — his hatred of outsiders, his deadly temper, his magical power — and yet she is not afraid to defy him. Her bond with Agni-Sikha is one of familial loyalty strained to breaking by moral conviction.

Her relationship with prince **Sringa-Bhuja** evolves from protector to beloved. Sringa-Bhuja "loved Rupa-Sikha so much that he was ready to do anything she told him" (054-001), and this trust proves to be his salvation. When the prince finally triumphs and she returns to him, Rupa-Sikha also shares in his joy: "Rupa-Sikha also was very delighted" (054-018).

## Character Traits

Rupa-Sikha embodies intelligence used in service of love and justice. She is quick-witted, resourceful, and courageous — willing to deceive her powerful father to protect an innocent stranger. Unlike many heroines who must be rescued, Rupa-Sikha is the rescuer: she anticipates dangers, prepares escape routes, extracts binding oaths, and delivers crucial messages at the right moment. Her cleverness is never malicious; she deceives only to prevent harm. She exemplifies the virtue of using one's wits for righteous ends, even when that means opposing one's own family — a quality she shares with other clever figures in the tales, such as the quick-thinking [[animal-mouse|mouse]] who must outwit his predators.
