# Cleverness

## Definition

Cleverness in the Hindu Tales is a quality of practical intelligence distinct from the more contemplative virtue of wisdom. A clever character is quick-witted, resourceful, and capable of devising effective strategies — but is not necessarily good. The tales maintain a careful distinction: cleverness is instrumental (good at achieving goals), while wisdom is normative (good at choosing the right goals). This distinction allows the tales to celebrate cleverness in virtuous characters while warning against its misuse by the selfish or deceitful.

Several characters in the tales are described as "clever" in contexts that reveal different facets of the quality: the clever father who provides for his family, the clever daughter who outsmarts an evil magician, the clever thief whose ingenuity runs ahead of his ethics, and the clever vizier who serves a just cause. By examining these varied instantiations, the tales teach that cleverness is a tool whose moral value depends on the hand that wields it.

## Exemplifying Stories

### Cleverness in Service of Love

Patala, the princess in "The Magic Shoes and Staff," is described as *"as clever as she was beautiful"* (048-018). Her cleverness is presented as a complement to her beauty — an active quality that allows her to participate in the lovers' escape rather than being a passive object of rescue. The tales consistently pair cleverness with beauty in their ideal female characters, suggesting that intelligence is an essential component of desirability.

Rupa-Sikha in "The Jewelled Arrow" is similarly characterized as *"very strong and very clever"* (049-011). Her cleverness is the quality that enables her to outmaneuver her own father, the powerful magician Agni-Sikha. A discussion question validates her strategy: *"Do you think it was clever of Rupa-Sikha to make up this"* (059-058). The magician himself acknowledges his daughter's intellectual gifts, referring to her as *"that too clever child of mine"* (057-020) — the adjective carrying both admiration and resentment.

### The Clever Vizier

"The Beetle and the Silken Thread" celebrates cleverness in the service of justice. The vizier, described as *"the clever vizier, who knew that all"* (066-020), uses his intelligence to execute a rescue plan that saves an innocent man. This is cleverness at its most admirable — instrumental intelligence harnessed to moral purpose, achieving results that brute force could never accomplish. The wife who recognizes *"what a clever husband she had"* (068-042) expresses the domestic gratitude that cleverness properly deployed can earn.

### The Ambiguous Cleverness of Hari-Sarman

"A Clever Thief" presents the most morally complex treatment of cleverness. Hari-Sarman devises a scheme to pass himself off as a wise man: *"he cannot have been very clever"* (076-022). The tale's irony lies in the gap between Hari-Sarman's self-assessment and reality — he thinks himself clever for devising the deception, but the narrative reveals that true cleverness would have recognized the long-term unsustainability of the fraud. The genuinely clever characters in the tale are those who see through him, using their intelligence in service of truth rather than deception.

### Cleverness in the Animal Kingdom

The animal fables extend cleverness beyond the human. The hunter believes *"he had hit upon a very clever"* (070-005) plan to trap birds, but the wise old crow's counter-strategy proves more clever still. This episode demonstrates that cleverness is not species-specific — it is a quality of mind that can manifest in any creature and that must be evaluated by its results rather than its pretensions.

## Contrasting Examples

Cleverness without moral grounding slides into cunning — and the tales recognize this distinction. Characters who use their intelligence to deceive, manipulate, or exploit are not celebrated for their cleverness but condemned for their lack of wisdom. The genuinely admirable clever characters — Patala, Rupa-Sikha, the vizier — use their intelligence in service of love, justice, and truth.
