[089-001]The wicked woman went back to the palace, thinking all the way to

[089-002]herself, “How can I get a proof of what is not true?” At last an idea

[089-003]came into her head. She knew that the queen loved to wander in the

[089-004][[place-forest|forest]], and that she was not afraid of the wild creatures, but seemed

[089-005]to understand their language. She would tell the barber that

[089-006][[person-kadali-garbha|Kadali-Garbha]] was a witch and knew the secrets of the woods; that she

[089-007]had been seen gathering wild herbs, some of them poisonous, and had

[089-008]been heard muttering strange words to herself as she did so.

[089-009]Early the next morning the [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] woman went to see the barber, and

[089-010]promised him a reward if he would tell the king what she had found out

[089-011]about his wife. “He won’t believe you at first,” she said; “but you

[089-012]must go on telling him till he does. You are [[concept-cleverness|clever]], enough,” she

[089-013]added, “to make up something he will believe if what I have thought of

[089-014]is no good.”

[089-015]The barber, who had served the king for many years, would not at first

[089-016]agree to help to make him unhappy. But he too liked money very much,

[089-017]and in the end he promised to see what he could do if he was well paid

[089-018]for it. He was, as the wicked woman had said, clever enough; and he

[089-019]knew from long experience just how to talk to his master. He began by

[089-020]asking the king if he had heard of the lovely woman who was sometimes

[089-021]seen by the woodmen [[event-journey|wandering]] about alone in the forest, with wild

[089-022]creatures following her. Remembering how he had first seen

[089-023]Kadali-Garbha, [[person-dridha-varman|Dridha-Varman]] at once guessed that she was the lovely

[089-024]woman. But he did not tell the barber so; for he was so proud of his

[089-025]dear wife’s beauty that he liked to hear her praised, and wanted the

[089-026]man to go on talking about her. He just said: “What is she like? Is she

[089-027]tall or short, fair or dark?” The barber answered the questions

[089-028]readily. Then he went on to say that it was easy to see that the lady

[089-029]was as clever as she was beautiful; for she knew not only all about

[089-030]animals but also about plants. “Every day,” he said, “she gathers

[089-031]quantities of herbs, and I have been told she makes healing medicines

[089-032]of them. Some even go so far as to say she also makes poisons. But, for

[089-033]my part, I do not believe that; she is too beautiful to be wicked.”

[089-034]The king listened, and a tiny little doubt crept into his mind about

[089-035]his wife. She had never told him about the herbs she gathered, although

[089-036]she often chattered about her friends in the forest. Perhaps after all

[089-037]it was not Kadali-Garbha the barber was talking about. He would ask her

[089-038]if she knew anything about making medicines from herbs. He did so when

[089-039]they were alone together, and she said at once, “Oh, yes! My father

[089-040]taught me. But I have never made any since I was married.”

[089-041]“Are you sure?” asked the king; and she answered laughing, “Of course,

[089-042]I am: how could I be anything but sure? I have no need to think of

[089-043]medicine-making, now I am the queen.”

[089-044]Dridha-Varman said no more at the time. But he was troubled; and when

[089-045]the barber came again, he began at once to ask about the woman who had

[089-046]been seen in the woods. The wicked man was delighted, and made up a

[089-047]long story. He said one of the waiting women had told him of what she

[089-048]had seen. The woman, he said, had followed the lady home one day, and

[089-049]that home was not far from the palace. She had seen her bending over a

[089-050]fire above which hung a great sauce-pan full of water, into which she

[089-051]flung some of the herbs she had gathered, singing as she did so, in a

[089-052]strange language.

[089-053]“Could it possibly be,” thought the king, “that Kadali-Garbha had

[089-054][[concept-deceit|deceived]] him? Was she perhaps a witch after all?” He remembered that he

[089-055]really did not know who she was, or who her father was. He had loved

[089-056]her directly he saw her, just because she was so beautiful. What was he

[089-057]to do now? He was quite sure, from the description the barber had given

[089-058]of the woman in the forest, that she was his wife. He would watch her

[089-059]himself in future, and say nothing to her that would make her think he

[089-060]was doing so.
