# Road

## Description

The road in the Hindu Tales is a space of transit, hazard, and transformation — the liminal corridor between destinations where characters are most vulnerable and magic most readily intrudes upon the mundane.

Roads are described as physically demanding: the Brahman merchant travels a *"long distance on the rough roads"* [021-005], the roughness itself an index of the journey's difficulty. But roads are also where the supernatural erupts into the narrative. The most dramatic road event occurs when Putraka's path is blocked: *"immediately a great mountain rose up, barring the road"* [058-011], a magical obstruction that halts all progress. Moments later, a pursuer is seen *"coming over the top of the mountain"* [058-013], transforming the blocked road into a scene of confrontation.

The road also hosts more quotidian features: *"a great pile of wood by the road-side"* [059-033] provides fuel for a needed fire, and a dangerous stretch of road is marked by *"the burning charcoal on the road"* [058-028] that threatens to ignite the surrounding grass. Characters are seen *"hurrying along the road as if not a moment was to be lost"* [060-011], capturing the urgency that road travel carries in the narrative economy — nobody lingers on the road; it is purely a means to an end.

## Narrative Role

The road in these tales occupies the narrative role of the **passage** — the space between stable locations where characters are exposed. Unlike the palace (power), the forest (mystery), or the city (society), the road is purely transitional. This very lack of rootedness makes it dangerous: a road can be blocked by magic, lined with hazards, or used as the scene of pursuit. The magical mountain that rises up *"barring the road"* [058-011] literalises the idea that the path forward can vanish in an instant.

The road also serves a structural function in the stories: it stitches the geography together, linking Sravasti to the wider world, the palace to the prison, the forest clearing to the city. Without roads, the spatial logic of the tales would collapse into a series of disconnected settings.
