## Summary

Hari-Sarman is a Brahman living in a little village, but he is no holy man.
Lazy and resentful of his poverty, he "used to send his children out" to beg
(076-010) rather than work himself. When his wife Vidya grows
tired of their destitution, Hari-Sarman conceives a plan: he will go to "the
chief house" of a nearby town and present himself as a seer with supernatural
knowledge (076-019). The wealthy householder, deceived by the
story Hari-Sarman tells him (076-023), takes him in and treats
him as an honoured guest.

What follows is a high-wire act of deception. Hari-Sarman, who possesses no
actual mystical powers, must maintain his fraud by luck, quick thinking, and
his wife's behind-the-scenes assistance. A series of thefts at his patron's
estate put him to the test: he must "discover" the stolen goods without any
means of actually knowing where they are. Through a combination of brazen
bluffing, observational cunning, and improbable coincidence, he survives
each crisis -- until the lies multiply beyond his ability to control them.
The story is both a comedy of errors and a cautionary tale, asking the
reader when a clever deception crosses the line into unforgivable fraud.

## Characters

- **Hari-Sarman** — The protagonist (and anti-hero), a Brahman whose intelligence
  is genuine but whose moral compass is broken. He is the story's central
  question embodied: is it better for a clever person to be poor and honest,
  or comfortable and fraudulent?
- **Vidya** — Hari-Sarman's wife, whose name means "knowledge." She is the
  practical force behind her husband's scheme, feeding him the information he
  needs to sustain his pretense. The story asks whether her complicity makes
  her as guilty as he.
- **the Wealthy Householder** — The patron who is "quite taken in by the story
  Hari-Sarman told him" (076-023). His gullibility is in part a
  critique of those who value reputation over genuine wisdom.
- **the Real Thieves** — Their actions provide the engine of the plot, forcing
  Hari-Sarman into ever more elaborate fabrications.

## Moral Themes

The story is a meditation on the ethics of deception. Hari-Sarman is not a
malicious figure -- he is lazy and desperate, and his frauds harm no one in the
immediate sense. Yet the story asks whether the endpoint of deception is
inherently corrupting. Each lie requires another to sustain it, and by the
tale's end, Hari-Sarman has become trapped in a prison of his own making.

A secondary question concerns class and worth. Hari-Sarman is a Brahman by
birth but a fraud by character. The story contrasts social status (birthright)
with genuine merit (wisdom, integrity), asking whether it "would have been
better for Hari-Sarman and Vidya if their" honesty had matched their position
(076-032).

## Setting

The story moves between a humble village hut -- the site of honest poverty --
and the wealthy merchant's estate -- the site of dishonest comfort. The
contrast between these two settings mirrors the story's moral axis: the harder
it is to return to honest poverty, the further Hari-Sarman falls into deception.
