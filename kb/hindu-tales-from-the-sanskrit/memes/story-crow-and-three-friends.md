## Summary

A "wise old crow" named Laghupatin lives comfortably in a great forest tree,
where he takes "a great interest in the affairs of his neighbours"
(069-001). One day he witnesses the king of the pigeons
being caught in a hunter's net, and through his quiet observation, sees how
collective action saves the flock.

The crow's curiosity leads him to befriend Hiranya the mouse, who has been
helping the pigeon king by nibbling through nets with his sharp teeth
(070-016). Despite the natural enmity between birds and
mice, Laghupatin and Hiranya forge a bond. They eat together and meet daily.

When the crow grows restless and proposes a journey
(072-009), they travel to meet Mandharaka the tortoise, "a
thoroughly good, trustworthy fellow" (072-014). The three
companions then encounter a deer hunted by men and pool their different
abilities to save him (073-045). The story shows how
friendship across species boundaries proves stronger than the instincts that
rule the forest.

## Characters

- **Laghupatin the Crow** — The protagonist, "a wise old crow" (069-001)
  whose curiosity and openness to friendship drive the story. He is fond of
  adventure (072-009) and serves as the group's strategist.

- **Hiranya the Mouse** — The crow's first friend, whose sharp teeth
  (070-016) prove essential to survival. Despite his fears, he
  learns to trust the crow and tortoise (073-034).

- **Mandharaka the Tortoise** — "A thoroughly good, trustworthy fellow ...
  though rather slow and cautious in his ways" (072-014).
  He is the steady anchor of the friendship.

- **the Deer** — A hunted creature whose rescue tests the fellowship and
  expands it from three to four.

- **the Pigeon King** — His leadership under the net demonstrates collective
  action, inspiring the crow's own approach to friendship.

## Moral Themes

The story's thesis is that friendship multiplies strength. The crow begins
alone and ends as part of a fellowship. Each friend contributes something
distinct: the crow offers perspective and flight, the mouse brings sharp teeth,
the tortoise provides grounded wisdom. Alone they are prey; together they are
formidable.

A second theme is breaking natural enmities. Crow and mouse are natural
adversaries, yet Laghupatin's friendship offer is genuine and Hiranya's
acceptance is courageous (071-008). The story argues that
mutual aid can transcend instincts -- a clear parallel to human society.

## Setting

The story moves through the crow's great tree (069-001), the
mouse's underground passages (070-019), and the tortoise's
riverside home (072-013). This range mirrors the moral
journey: leaving one's territory to enter another's is the geography of
friendship.
