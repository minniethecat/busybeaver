# Temple

## Description

Temples appear across multiple stories as sites of worship, refuge, and supernatural encounter. The most richly described temple is the one near Putraka's palace, where *"he often went to a beautiful temple near his palace"* [039-007]. This temple is not merely a shrine — it becomes the stage for a dramatic confrontation between the young king and the heavenly guardian, who *"never"* abandoned his sacred post [039-024].

A very different temple appears in the story of Sringa-Bhuja: *"a deserted temple a few miles from here"* [057-027], occupied by the malevolent Dhuma-Sikha. This temple is described in precise detail — Sringa-Bhuja must *"rein up your steed opposite"* the entrance [057-028] and recognize it by *"the images of three of"* the gods at its gate [058-002]. The temple's architecture is specific enough that Rupa-Sikha can give *"such an exact account of the temple, naming the"* identifying features [058-061].

Later chapters mention temples among the civic amenities of a well-planned settlement, alongside *"happy inhabitants, with temples in which they might"* worship [048-026], suggesting temples are markers of civilized life in the world of the tales.

## Narrative Role

The temple serves a dual narrative function. As a sacred space, it represents divine order — the heavenly guardian in Putraka's temple embodies moral authority, passing judgment on those who enter. As a deserted temple, it becomes a place of danger and trial: Dhuma-Sikha's temple is where Sringa-Bhuja must prove his courage to win Rupa-Sikha. The contrast between the two temples mirrors the broader moral arc of the tales — sacred spaces can shelter both protection and peril, depending on who occupies them.
