[019-001]When the cat heard what [[animal-mouse|the mouse]] said, she could hardly believe her

[019-002]ears. She was of course ready to promise anything to anyone who would

[019-003]help her, so she said at once:

[019-004]“You dear little mouse, to wish to help me. If only you will nibble

[019-005]through that string which is killing me, I promise that I will always

[019-006]love you, always be your friend, and however hungry I may be, I will

[019-007]starve rather than hurt your tender little body.”

[019-008]On hearing this, the mouse, without hesitating a moment, climbed up on

[019-009]to the cat’s back, and cuddled down in the soft fur near her neck,

[019-010]feeling very safe and warm there. The owl would certainly not attack

[019-011]him there, he thought, and the cat could not possibly hurt him. It was

[019-012]one thing to pounce down on a defenceless little creature running on

[019-013]the ground amongst the barley, quite another to try and snatch him from

[019-014]the very neck of a cat.

[019-015]The cat of course expected the mouse to begin to nibble through the

[019-016]string at once, and became very uneasy when she felt the little

[019-017]creature nestle down as if to go to sleep, instead of helping her. Poor

[019-018]Pussy could not turn her head so as to see the mouse without drawing

[019-019]the string tighter, and she did not dare to speak angrily lest she

[019-020]should offend him. “My dear little friend,” she said, “do you not think

[019-021]it is high time to keep your promise and set me free?”

[019-022]Hearing this, the mouse pretended to bite the string, but took care not

[019-023]to do so really; and the cat waited and waited, getting more miserable

[019-024]every minute. All through the long night the same thing went on: the

[019-025]mouse taking a little nap now and then, the cat getting weaker and

[019-026]weaker. “Oh,” she thought to herself, “if only I could get free, the

[019-027]first thing I would do would be to gobble up that horrid little mouse.”

[019-028]The moon rose, the stars came out, the wind murmured amongst the

[019-029]branches of the banyan tree, making the unfortunate cat long to be safe

[019-030]in her cosy home in the trunk. The cries of the wild animals which

[019-031]prowl about at night seeking their food were heard, and the cat feared

[019-032]one of them might find her and kill her. A mother tiger perhaps would

[019-033]snatch her, and take her to her hungry cubs, hidden away in the deep

[019-034][[place-forest|forest]], or a bird of prey might swoop down on her and grip her in his

[019-035]terrible claws. Again and again she entreated the mouse to be quick,

[019-036]promising that, if only he would set her at liberty, she would never,

[019-037]never, never forget it or do any harm to her beloved friend.
