[083-001][[person-hari-sarman|Hari-Sarman]] thought when he looked at [[object-magic-pitcher|the pitcher]]: “Alas, alas, it is

[083-002]all over with me now! Never can I find out what is in it. Would that I

[083-003]had left this [[place-city|town]] with the money I had from [[person-jihva|Jihva]] before it was too

[083-004]late!” Then he began to mutter to himself, as it was always his habit

[083-005]to do when he was in trouble. It so happened that, when he was a little

[083-006]boy, his father used to call him frog, and now his thoughts went back

[083-007]to the time when he was a [[concept-happiness|happy]] innocent child, and he said aloud: “Oh,

[083-008]frog, what trouble has come to you! That pitcher will be the death of

[083-009]you!”

[083-010]Even Deva-Jnanin was astonished when he heard that; and so were all the

[083-011]other [[concept-wisdom|wise]] men. The king was delighted to find that after all he had

[083-012]made no mistake; and all the people who had been allowed to come in to

[083-013]see the trial were greatly excited. Shouting for joy the king called

[083-014]Hari-Sarman to come to the foot of [[object-throne|the throne]], and told him he would

[083-015]never, never doubt him again. He should have yet more money, a

[083-016]beautiful house in the country as well as the one he already had in the

[083-017]town, and his children should be brought from the farm to live with him

[083-018]and their mother, who should have lovely dresses and ornaments to wear.

[083-019]Nobody was more surprised than Hari-Sarman himself. He guessed, of

[083-020]course, that there was a frog in the pitcher. And when the king had

[083-021]ended his speech, he said: “One thing I ask in addition to all that has

[083-022]been given me, that I may keep the pitcher in memory of this day, when

[083-023]my truth has been proved once more beyond a doubt.”

[083-024]His request was, of course, granted; and he went off with the pitcher

[083-025]under his arm, full of rejoicing over his narrow escape. At the same

[083-026]time he was also full of fear for the future. He knew only too well

[083-027]that it had only been by a lucky chance that he had used the word Jihva

[083-028]in his first danger and [[animal-frog|Frog]] in the second. He was not likely to get

[083-029]off a third time; and he made up his mind that he would skip away some

[083-030]dark night soon, with all the money and jewels he could carry, and be

[083-031]seen no more where such strange adventures had befallen him. He did not

[083-032]even tell his wife what he meant to do, but pretended to have forgiven

[083-033]her entirely for the way she had neglected him when he was poor, and to

[083-034]be glad that their children were to be restored to them. Before they

[083-035]came from the farm their father had disappeared, and nobody ever found

[083-036]out what had become of him; but the king let his family keep what had

[083-037]been given to him, and to the end believed he really had been what he

[083-038]had pretended to be. Only Deva-Jnanin had his doubts; but he kept them

[083-039]to himself, for he thought, “Now the man is gone, it really does not

[083-040]matter who or what he was.”
