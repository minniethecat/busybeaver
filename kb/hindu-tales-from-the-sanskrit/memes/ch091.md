[091-001]When the barber was shaving the king the next morning, he told him he

[091-002]had heard that people were saying the beautiful woman had been seen

[091-003]again one night, gathering herbs and muttering to herself. “They talk,

[091-004]my lord,” said the man, “of your own name having been on her lips; and

[091-005]those who love and honour you are anxious for your safety. Maybe the

[091-006]woman is indeed a witch, who for some reason of her own will try to

[091-007]poison you.”

[091-008]Now [[person-dridha-varman|Dridha-Varman]] remembered that [[person-kadali-garbha|Kadali-Garbha]] had left him the night

[091-009]before, “and perhaps,” he thought, “at other times when I was asleep.”

[091-010]He could scarcely wait until the barber had finished shaving him, so

[091-011]eager was he to find out the truth. He hurried to his wife’s private

[091-012]room, but she was not there; and her ladies told him she had not been

[091-013]seen by them that day. This troubled him terribly, and he roused the

[091-014]whole palace to seek her. Messengers were soon hurrying to and fro, but

[091-015]not a trace of her could be found. Dridha-Varman was now quite sure

[091-016]that the woman the barber had talked about was Kadali-Garbha, the wife

[091-017]he had so loved and [[concept-trust|trusted]]. “Perhaps,” he thought, “she has left

[091-018]poison in my food, and has gone away so as not to see me die.” He would

[091-019]neither eat nor drink, and he ordered all the ladies whose duty it was

[091-020]to wait on the queen to be locked up till she was found. Amongst them

[091-021]was the wicked woman who had done all the mischief because of her

[091-022]jealousy of the beautiful young queen, and very much she wished she had

[091-023]never tried to harm her.
