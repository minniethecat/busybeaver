# Trust

## Definition

Trust in the Hindu Tales is portrayed as a fragile and precious bond that, once broken, is nearly impossible to restore. The tales explore trust across the full spectrum of relationships — between parent and child, husband and wife, ruler and subject, predator and prey. Unlike abstract virtues such as [[concept-wisdom|wisdom]], trust is inherently relational: it exists only between beings, and its health or sickness reflects the state of those relationships.

The tales are notably unsentimental about trust. They acknowledge that misplaced trust can be fatal — a theme most starkly dramatized in the animal fables involving [[animal-cat|the cat]] and [[animal-mouse|the mouse]] — while also insisting that a life without trust is impoverished and fearful. The wisest characters calibrate their trust to the trustworthiness of the other party, a skill the tales encourage readers to develop through their discussion questions.

## Trust Relationships Across Stories

| Story | Trust Relationship | Outcome |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| The Cat, Mouse, Lizard and Owl | Mouse → Cat (predator) | Tested — the cat's behavior after rescue determines the verdict |
| The Jewelled Arrow | Vira-Bhuja → Son (father-son) | Vindicated — the son is innocent despite slander |
| The Beetle and the Silken Thread | King → Minister (ruler-subject) | Shattered by death — "the one man he could trust" |
| A Crow and His Three Friends | Crow → Companions (friends) | Sustained — trustworthiness redeems other flaws |
| The Hermit's Daughter | Husband → Wife (spousal) | Broken, then supernaturally restored |

## Exemplifying Stories

### Trust and Danger in the Animal Fable

"The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl" presents trust as a life-or-death calculation. When a cat, trapped and helpless, appeals for help, the mouse must decide whether to trust a natural predator. The discussion question embedded in the text sharpens the dilemma: *"If you had been the mouse, would you have trusted to what the cat said in her misery?"* (019-039). The cat's subsequent behavior raises a further question: *"was it wise to trust a cat when she is free to kill..."* (020-039). These questions do not offer easy answers — they invite the reader to weigh compassion against prudence, mercy against self-preservation.

### A Father's Trust

"The Jewelled Arrow" presents trust in its most idealized form — the bond between father and son. When a malicious courtier slanders the prince, Vira-Bhuja dismisses the accusation: *"he trusted his son as much as he loved him"* (050-018). The father's trust is portrayed as noble and well-founded — the son is indeed innocent — but the episode also illustrates the vulnerability of trust to deliberate sabotage by third parties.

### The Trusted Servant

In "The Beetle and the Silken Thread," the death of a trusted figure precipitates crisis: *"the death of the one man he could trust"* (068-002). The singular article — "the one man" — emphasizes how rare and precious trustworthy individuals are in the world of these tales. A similar figure appears in "A Crow and His Three Friends," where a character is described as *"a thoroughly good, trustworthy fellow he is, though rather..."* (072-014). Trustworthiness is presented as a cardinal quality that redeems other flaws.

### Trust Broken and Rebuilt

"The Hermit's Daughter" explores the devastating consequences of broken trust between spouses. A husband who *"no longer trusts his wife, who loves him with..."* (090-031) sets in motion a chain of suffering that only supernatural intervention can repair. The wife's ultimate goal is *"to make him love and trust me again"* (092-022) — the restoration of trust is presented as the highest form of relational healing. The tale that opens the collection asks readers explicitly: *"What do you think it was which made the animals trust Kadali-Garbha?"* (084-024), suggesting that trustworthiness is a quality perceptible across species boundaries.
