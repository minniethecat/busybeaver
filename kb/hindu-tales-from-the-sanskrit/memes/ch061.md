[061-001]Having really got rid of [[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]], [[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]] and her husband were

[061-002]very soon out of his reach and in the country belonging to

[061-003][[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]]’s father, who had bitterly mourned the loss of his

[061-004]favourite son. When the news was brought to him that two strangers, a

[061-005]handsome young man and a beautiful woman, who appeared to be husband

[061-006]and wife, had entered his capital, he hastened forth to meet them,

[061-007]hoping that perhaps they could give him news of Sringa-Bhuja. What was

[061-008]his [[concept-happiness|joy]] when he recognised his dear son, holding the [[object-jewelled-arrow|jewelled arrow]],

[061-009]which had led him into such trouble, in his right hand, as he guided

[061-010]Marut with his left! The king flung himself from his horse, and

[061-011]Sringa-Bhuja, [[concept-generosity|giving]] the reins to Rupa-Sikha, also dismounted. The next

[061-012]moment he was in his father’s arms, everything forgotten and forgiven

[061-013]in the happy reunion.

[061-014]Great was the rejoicing over Sringa-Bhuja’s return and hearty was the

[061-015]welcome given to his beautiful bride, who quickly won all hearts but

[061-016]those of the wicked wives and sons who had tried to harm her husband

[061-017]and his mother. They feared the anger of the king, when he found out

[061-018]how they had [[concept-deceit|deceived]] him, and they were right to fear. Sringa-Bhuja’s

[061-019]very first act was to plead for his mother to be set free. He would not

[061-020]tell any of his adventures, he said, till she could hear them too; and

[061-021]the king, full of remorse for the way he had treated her, went with him

[061-022]to the [[event-imprisonment|prison]] in which she had been shut up all this time. What was

[061-023]poor [[person-guna-vara|Guna-Vara]]’s joy, when the two entered the place in which she had

[061-024]shed so many tears! She could not at first believe her eyes or ears,

[061-025]but soon she realised that her sufferings were indeed over. She could

[061-026]not be quite happy till her beloved husband said he knew she had never

[061-027]loved any one but him. She had been accused falsely, she said, and she

[061-028]wanted the woman who had told a lie about her to be made to own the

[061-029]truth.

[061-030]This was done in the presence of the whole court, and when [[event-trial|judgment]] had

[061-031]been passed upon Ayasolekha, the brothers of Sringa-Bhuja were also

[061-032]brought before their father, who charged them with having deceived him.

[061-033]They too were condemned, and all the culprits would have been taken to

[061-034]prison and shut up for the rest of their lives, if those they had

[061-035]injured had not pleaded for their forgiveness. Guna-Vara and her son

[061-036]prostrated themselves at the foot of [[object-throne|the throne]], and would not rise

[061-037]till they had won pardon for their enemies. Ayasolekha and the brothers

[061-038]were allowed to go free; but Sringa-Bhuja, though he was the youngest

[061-039]of all the princes, was proclaimed heir to the crown after his father’s

[061-040]death. His brothers, however, never ceased to hate him; and when he

[061-041]came to the throne, they gave him a great deal of trouble. He had many

[061-042]years of happiness with his wife and parents before that, and never

[061-043]regretted the mistake about the jewelled arrow; since but for it he

[061-044]would, he knew, never have seen his beloved Rupa-Sikha.
