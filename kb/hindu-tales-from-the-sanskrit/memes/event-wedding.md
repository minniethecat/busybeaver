# The Wedding

## Description

Weddings in the Hindu Tales are never merely ceremonial. They function as narrative hinge points where multiple story threads converge and pivot toward resolution. The royal wedding in "The Jewelled Arrow" is announced with the king's declaration: *"The wedding shall take place"* [055-027], a performative utterance that sets irreversible events in motion. Preparations are elaborate, spanning days: *"whilst the preparations for the wedding were going on"* [077-023], characters scheme, secrets threaten to surface, and the social order is both celebrated and tested.

A wedding in these tales is a public spectacle that draws characters from across the social spectrum. In "The Girl Who Married," the wedding provides the occasion for a poor family's clever daughter to transform her circumstances. The event brings together the powerful and the powerless, the knowing and the deceived, creating the conditions for dramatic reversals.

The wedding feast is an essential component: *"he would get after the wedding"* [077-019] refers to the food and celebration that follow the ceremony, and characters' anticipation or dread of the feast reveals their true attitudes toward the marriage itself.

## Narrative Significance

The wedding functions as the classic comedic resolution in the narrative sense: the union that restores order after chaos. But the tales complicate this simple arc. In "The Jewelled Arrow," the wedding is nearly prevented by mistaken identity; in "The Girl Who Married," the wedding is a calculated gamble; in "The Lost Camel," the wedding's approach creates urgency that drives the final act.

A character's attitude toward an approaching wedding reveals their moral nature. The eager groom who thinks only of the feast [077-019] contrasts with the bride who plans for her future security [077-010]. The wedding thus serves as a moral X-ray, exposing each character's priorities at the moment of maximum social pressure.

The narrator in "The Lost Camel" captures the king's impatience: *"eager as he was for the wedding to be soon"* [087-023]. This eagerness is both human and dangerous — impatience drives the plot forward but also creates the conditions for error and misunderstanding.

## Consequences

When the wedding is concluded, the narrative world is permanently altered: *"After the wedding was over, the king mounted his"* [088-001] — the story moves immediately from ceremony to aftermath. Characters ascend to new roles (bride becomes queen), secrets are either buried or exposed, and the social order is reaffirmed or subtly subverted. The wedding's consequence is the new status quo from which subsequent tales may begin.
