## Summary

Prince Sringa-Bhuja is the beloved son of King Vira-Bhuja, a fact that fills
his stepbrothers with consuming envy. They "hated Sringa-Bhuja" because their
father loved him more (051-004), and they conspire with their
mothers to get rid of him -- "prevent this by getting rid of Sringa-Bhuja"
(051-008). Their plot involves the king's prized possession: a
jewelled arrow of legendary power.

The arrow is the key to a contest designed to determine the heir. When
Sringa-Bhuja wins the match with the jewelled arrow (051-023),
his stepbrothers' hatred intensifies. But the arrow proves to be more than
a weapon -- it is a magical guide that leads Sringa-Bhuja through enchanted
lands, past supernatural guardians, and ultimately to the woman he is destined
to love. Along the way, he must solve riddles, defeat magical foes, and prove
that the love in his heart is stronger than any enchantment devised against him.

## Characters

- **Sringa-Bhuja** — The protagonist, a prince whose genuine goodness attracts
  both love and envy. His name means "the one with the jewelled arm," linking
  him to the magical arrow that defines his destiny.
- **Vira-Bhuja** — The king and father, a well-meaning but blind ruler whose
  favouritism inadvertently endangers his favourite son.
- **the Stepbrothers** — The antagonists within the royal household, driven by
  jealousy of "their father's love for Sringa-Bhuja" (051-006).
  Their hatred -- and its ultimate futility -- is the story's shadow theme.
- **the Princess** — The object of Sringa-Bhuja's quest and love, held captive
  by a supernatural enchanter. She is more than a prize: her own character and
  choices shape the outcome.
- **the Enchanter** — The magical antagonist whose power over the princess
  must be overcome through love rather than force.
- **the Jewelled Arrow** — Not a character but a character-like force: a
  sentient magical object that guides, tests, and ultimately rewards.

## Moral Themes

The story's central question is posed explicitly: "Is true love ever jealous?"
(049-057). The stepbrothers' jealousy is presented as the
antithesis of love -- a possessiveness that destroys rather than creates.
Sringa-Bhuja, by contrast, proves his love through selfless action and
willingness to face danger for another's sake.

A second theme is the idea that destiny cannot be thwarted by malice. The
stepbrothers' plot to get rid of Sringa-Bhuja (051-008)
backfires, driving him toward the very fate they sought to deny him. The
jewelled arrow, which they meant as a trap, becomes his salvation.

## Magical Elements

The jewelled arrow is the story's central magical artifact: a weapon that
chooses its wielder, guides him across impossible distances, and reveals
hidden truths. Its magic operates at the intersection of fate and character --
it helps those who are worthy, not merely those who are strong.

## Setting

The story moves from the opulent court of Vira-Bhuja's kingdom into an
enchanted forest realm where the rules of ordinary reality do not apply. The
enchanted palace where the princess is held represents a space where only
genuine love can penetrate the illusions.
