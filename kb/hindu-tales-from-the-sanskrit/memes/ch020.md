[020-001]It was not until the moon had set and the light of the dawn had put out

[020-002]that of the stars that [[animal-mouse|the mouse]], made any real effort to help the cat.

[020-003]By this time the hunter who had set the snare came to see if he had

[020-004]caught the cat; and the poor cat, seeing him in the distance, became so

[020-005]wild with terror that she nearly killed herself in the struggle to get

[020-006]away. “Keep still! keep still,” cried the mouse, “and I will really

[020-007]save you.” Then with a few quick bites with his sharp teeth he cut

[020-008]through the string, and the next moment the cat was hidden amongst the

[020-009]barley, and the mouse was running off in the opposite direction,

[020-010]determined to keep well out of sight of the creature he had kept in

[020-011]such misery for so many hours. Full well he knew that all the cat’s

[020-012]promises would be forgotten, and that she would eat him up if she could

[020-013]catch him. The owl too flew away, and [[animal-lizard|the lizard]] went off to hunt flies

[020-014]in the sunshine, and there was not a sign of any of the four

[020-015]inhabitants of the banyan tree when the hunter reached the snare. He

[020-016]was very much surprised and puzzled to find the string hanging loose in

[020-017]two pieces, and no sign of there having been anything caught in it,

[020-018]except two white hairs lying on the ground close to the trap. He had a

[020-019]good look round, and then went home without having found out anything.

[020-020]When the hunter was quite out of sight, the cat came forth from the

[020-021]barley, and hastened back to her beloved home in the banyan tree. On

[020-022]her way there she spied the mouse also hurrying along in the same

[020-023]direction, and at first she felt inclined to hunt him and eat him then

[020-024]and there. On second thoughts however she decided to try and keep

[020-025]friends with him, because he might help her again if she got caught a

[020-026]second time. So she took no notice of the mouse until the next day,

[020-027]when she climbed down the tree and went to the roots in which she knew

[020-028]the mouse was hidden. There she began to purr as loud as she could, to

[020-029]show the mouse she was in a good humour, and called out, “Dear good

[020-030]little mouse, come out of your hole and let me tell you how very, very

[020-031]grateful I am to you for saving my life. There is nothing in the world

[020-032]I will not do for you, if you will only be friends with me.”

[020-033]The mouse only squeaked in answer to this speech, and took very good

[020-034]care not to show himself, till he was quite sure the cat was gone

[020-035]beyond reach of him. He stayed quietly in his hole, and only ventured

[020-036]forth after he had heard the cat climb up into the tree again. “It is

[020-037]all very well,” thought the mouse, “to pretend to make friends with an

[020-038]enemy when that enemy is helpless, but I should indeed be a silly mouse

[020-039]to trust a cat when she is free to kill me.”

[020-040]The cat made a good many other efforts to be friends with the mouse,

[020-041]but they were all unsuccessful. In the end [[animal-owl|the owl]] caught the mouse,

[020-042]and the cat killed the lizard. The owl and the cat both lived for the

[020-043]rest of their lives in the banyan tree, and died in the end at a good

[020-044]old age.
