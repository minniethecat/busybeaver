# Jealousy

## Definition

Jealousy in the Hindu Tales is portrayed as a uniquely intimate vice — it arises not between strangers but between those who are close: siblings, courtiers, spouses. Unlike cruelty, which flows downward from power, jealousy flows sideways through relationships of approximate equality. A sibling envies a father's favorite; a courtier envies a rival's preferment; a palace woman envies a younger rival's beauty.

The tales consistently show jealousy as self-destructive. The jealous character gains nothing by their envy; they only poison their own peace and ultimately bring about their own downfall. The discussion question embedded in one tale — *"Is true love ever jealous?"* (049-057) — invites readers to consider whether jealousy can coexist with genuine affection, suggesting that it cannot.

## Exemplifying Stories

### Jealous Uncles

"The Magic Shoes and Staff" features jealousy as a family dynamic. When Putraka, raised by three uncles, begins to demonstrate superior abilities and virtue, the uncles' response is predictable: *"Presently they became jealous of Putraka, and began to wish"* (038-007). Their jealousy is a response to his excellence — it is not that Putraka has wronged them but that his very existence as a superior person threatens their sense of their own worth. The uncles' jealousy drives the plot: it motivates their cruelty, isolates Putraka, and sets him on the journey that leads to his ultimate triumph.

### The Jealous Sisters

"The Jewelled Arrow" explores jealousy within a sibling constellation. Of a father with many daughters, the tale notes that *"they were very jealous indeed of their father's love"* (049-013). The youngest daughter, Rupa-Sikha, is the father's favorite, and her sisters' jealousy toward her creates the conditions for her alliance with the outsider Sringa-Bhuja. The tale embeds a discussion question that probes the emotional logic: *"Is true love ever jealous?"* (049-057). The implied answer — no, true love is generous and secure — frames jealousy as incompatible with the highest form of human connection.

### Courtly Jealousy

"A Royal Thief-Catcher" grounds jealousy in the political domain: *"many of the Raja's subjects were jealous of Dhairya-Sila"* (062-022). When a person of merit rises in the court, those who lack merit resent the ascent. This form of jealousy is particularly dangerous because it operates through institutional channels — jealous courtiers spread rumors, manipulate the ruler, and attempt to destroy the object of their envy through political means rather than direct confrontation.

### The Jealousy That Destroys a Marriage

"The Hermit's Daughter" presents the most devastating portrait of jealousy: *"only because the woman was jealous of the beautiful young queen"* (088-031). This jealousy is explicitly linked to beauty and youth — the older woman cannot bear the younger woman's radiance. The narrative traces the full arc: from *"jealousy of the beautiful young queen"* (091-022) through the plotting and execution of a scheme to destroy her, to the eventual supernatural revelation of the truth. A palace servant warns that *"many would be jealous of her"* (087-029), suggesting that exceptional beauty or virtue inevitably attracts the envy of lesser souls.

## Contrasting Examples

The tales offer implicit counter-examples to jealousy in the form of characters who rejoice in others' good fortune. Kadali-Garbha's animals feel no jealousy toward each other or toward the humans who come into her life. The loyal servant who is *"a thoroughly good, trustworthy fellow"* (072-014) demonstrates the opposite of jealousy — genuine pleasure in another's success. These characters embody the ideal that the jealous fail to reach: security in one's own worth that needs no comparison to others.
