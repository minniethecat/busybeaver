[046-001]It was very difficult to persuade [[person-putraka|Putraka]] to go, but at last he flew

[046-002]away. Every night after that, however, he came to see [[person-patala|Patala]], spending

[046-003]the days sometimes in one place, sometimes in another, and using his

[046-004][[object-magic-bowl|magic bowl]] to supply himself with food. Alas, he forgot all about the

[046-005]dear old woman to whom he owed all his happiness, and she slowly gave

[046-006]up hope of ever seeing him again. He might quite easily have flown to

[046-007]her cottage and cheered her with his presence; but he was so wrapped up

[046-008]in his love for Patala that everything else went out of his head. This

[046-009]selfishness on his part presently got him into serious trouble, for he

[046-010]became careless about making himself invisible when he flew up to the

[046-011]princess’ window. So that one night he was discovered by a guardian of

[046-012]the palace. The matter was at once reported to the king, who could not

[046-013]at first believe such a thing was possible. The man must have seen a

[046-014]big bird, that was all. The king, however, ordered one of his

[046-015]daughter’s ladies to keep watch every night in an ante-room, leaving

[046-016]the door open with the tapestry, in which there was a slit, drawn

[046-017]carefully over it, and to come and tell him in the morning if she had

[046-018]seen or heard anything unusual.

[046-019]Now the lady chosen loved the princess, and, like many of her

[046-020]fellow-attendants, thought it was very [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] of the king to punish his

[046-021]own child for being so beautiful, by shutting her up as he did. It so

[046-022]happened that the very first night she was on guard, Putraka had flown

[046-023]a very, very long way, not noticing where he was going, because he was

[046-024]thinking so earnestly of Patala. When at last he flew in at her window,

[046-025]he was so weary that he sank down on a couch and fell fast asleep. The

[046-026]princess too was tired, because she had lain awake talking to her lover

[046-027]so many nights running that she had had hardly any rest. So when the

[046-028]lady peeped through the slit in the tapestry, there, by the light of

[046-029]the night lamp, she saw the young king lying unconscious, whilst the

[046-030]princess also was asleep.

[046-031]Very cautiously the attendant crept to the side of Putraka, and took a

[046-032]long, long look at him. She noticed how handsome he was, and that he

[046-033]was dressed in beautiful clothes. She especially remarked the turban he

[046-034]wore, because in [[place-india|India]] the rank to which men belong is shown by the

[046-035]kind of turbans they wear. “This is no common man,” she thought, “but a

[046-036]prince or king in disguise. What shall I do now? I will not raise an

[046-037]alarm which might lead to this beautiful young lover being killed and

[046-038]the heart of my dear mistress broken.”
