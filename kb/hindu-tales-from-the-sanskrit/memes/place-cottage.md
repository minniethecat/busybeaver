# Cottage

## Description

The cottage is the humble family dwelling at the edge of the wood where the opening chapters of the Magic Pitcher story unfold. It is the home of Subha Datta's father, a woodcutter, positioned deliberately near the forest: there was a *"tree in the wood near his cottage, for he did not mean any one"* [011-004] to discover his secret observation post. The cottage's proximity to the forest clearing — where the fairies gather — is the geographical fact on which the entire plot depends.

The cottage is characterised by domestic intimacy and want. After the family's fortunes change, Subha Datta returns to the *"cottage"* [012-052], and his wife *"begged him to let"* her share in his new prosperity. The cottage is also vulnerable — someone *"managed to get into the cottage"* [013-006], suggesting it offers little in the way of security. In a later story, a different cottage appears, where a visitor *"cheered her with his presence"* [046-007], suggesting cottages throughout the tales are modest but emotionally charged domestic spaces.

## Narrative Role

The cottage represents the **domestic origin point** from which the protagonist departs into a world of magic and adventure. It is unremarkable by design — a woodcutter's dwelling on the forest's edge — but its very ordinariness anchors the supernatural elements that unfold nearby. The fact that the magic pitcher is discovered within walking distance of this humble cottage underscores the tales' democratic imagination: enchantment lies not in distant palaces but in the woods beside a poor man's home.
