[027-001]After the king had seen the big empty hole, and noticed exactly where

[027-002]it was, and the nearest way to it from the [[place-city|town]], he returned to his

[027-003]palace, first telling the Brahman to go back to the house he lived in,

[027-004]and wait there till he received a message from him. He promised to see

[027-005]that he wanted for nothing, and sent one of his attendants to a rich

[027-006]merchant of [[place-sravasti|Sravasti]], who had already done a good deal for the Brahman,

[027-007]to order him to supply the holy man with all he needed. Very [[concept-happiness|glad]] that

[027-008]after all he was not going to die, the Brahman obeyed willingly, and

[027-009]for the next few days he was taken care of by the merchant, who

[027-010]supplied him with plenty of food.

[027-011]As soon as [[person-prasnajit|Prasnajit]] was back in his palace, he pretended that he was

[027-012]taken suddenly ill. His head ached badly, he said, and he could not

[027-013]make out what was the matter with him. He ordered a proclamation to be

[027-014]sent all round the town, telling all the doctors to come to the palace

[027-015]to see him. All the doctors in the place at once hastened to [[concept-obedience|obey]], each

[027-016]of them hoping that he would be the one to cure the king and win a

[027-017]great reward. So many were they that the big reception room was full of

[027-018]them, and they all glared at each other so angrily that the attendants

[027-019]kept careful watch lest they should begin to fight. One at a time they

[027-020]were taken to the king’s private room, but very much to their surprise

[027-021]and disappointment he seemed quite well and in no need of help from

[027-022]them. Instead of talking about his own illness, he asked each doctor

[027-023]who his patients were in the town, and what medicines he was [[concept-generosity|giving]] to

[027-024]them. Of course Prasnajit’s questions were carefully answered; but the

[027-025]king said nothing more, just waving his hand to shew that the interview

[027-026]was at an end. Then the attendants led the visitor out. At last however

[027-027]a doctor came, who said something which led the king to keep him longer

[027-028]than he had kept any of the others. This doctor was a very famous

[027-029]healer who had saved the lives of many of Prasnajit’s subjects. He told

[027-030]the king that a merchant named [[person-matri-datta|Matri-Datta]] was very ill, suffering

[027-031]greatly, but that he hoped to cure him by giving him the juice of a

[027-032]certain plant called nagaballa. At the time this story was written,

[027-033]doctors in [[place-india|India]] did not give their patients medicine, or write

[027-034]prescriptions for them to take to chemists to be made up, because there

[027-035]were no chemists in those days, such as there are in all the towns of

[027-036]Europe, who keep the materials in stock for making medicines. A doctor

[027-037]just said to his patient, “you must take the juice of this or that

[027-038]plant”; and the suffering person had to go into the fields or [[place-forest|woods]] to

[027-039]find the plant or else to send a servant to do so.

[027-040]When the king heard that the doctor had ordered Matri-Datta to take the

[027-041]juice of the nagaballa plant, he cried “No more doctors need come to

[027-042]see me!” and after sending away the one who had told him what he wanted

[027-043]to know, he gave orders that Matri-Datta should be sent for at once.
