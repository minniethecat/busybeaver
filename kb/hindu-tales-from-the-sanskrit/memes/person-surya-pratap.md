# Surya Pratap

## Role in the Stories

Surya Pratap appears in "The Beetle and the Silken Thread" as the king whose name bears a grand meaning — "Powerful as the Sun" (062-008) — yet whose character is defined by cruelty and jealousy rather than radiance or justice. He is the antagonist of the tale, the sovereign whose vanity and insecurity drive him to unjustly persecute his wisest and most loyal servant. His role illustrates the danger of absolute power in the hands of a ruler who values his own pride above truth and justice.

## Key Actions

Surya Pratap's first significant action is his jealous response to the courtiers' rumors about Dhairya-Sila. The king allows himself to be swayed by the envy of others: "for one day Surya Pratap ordered him to be taken outside the city and put to death" (062-028). This order — to execute his own vizier without trial or evidence — reveals the king's fundamental unfitness to rule. He reacts not as a judge but as a tyrant, punishing wisdom because it makes him feel inferior.

When Dhairya-Sila speaks brave words in his own defense, the king's response is rage: "On hearing these brave words, Surya Pratap flew into a terrible rage" (062-034). His fury is the fury of power challenged — the vizier's calm dignity is itself an affront to a king who cannot tolerate anyone being better than himself.

The king's most revealing moment comes at the story's end, when Dhairya-Sila miraculously reappears. Initially, Surya Pratap is eager to see his old vizier: "'Bring him in at once,' cried Surya Pratap" (068-007). When Dhairya-Sila explains that God had protected him, the king chooses to believe this miraculous account without question: "Surya Pratap asked no further questions, but at once restored Dhairya-Sila to his old place as chief vizier" (068-029). This ready belief suggests not genuine faith but convenience — the king needs to believe whatever allows him to escape responsibility for his own murderous actions.

## Relationships

Surya Pratap's defining relationship is with his vizier **Dhairya-Sila**. The king's jealousy of his servant's wisdom and composure drives the entire plot. The narrator explains the king's motivation succinctly: "the Raja would never forgive Dhairya-Sila for being wiser than himself" (065-005). This is not a rational grievance but a deeply personal one — Dhairya-Sila's very existence wounds the king's pride.

His relationship with his **courtiers** is one of mutual corruption. The jealous courtiers exploit the king's vanity to destroy a rival, and the king uses his power to act on their malicious whispers. The court is a closed system of envy, flattery, and injustice.

## Character Traits

Surya Pratap's name — "Powerful as the Sun" — is ironic. The sun brings light, warmth, and life; Surya Pratap brings darkness, cruelty, and death. His defining trait is his inability to tolerate anyone being wiser or better than himself. This insecurity manifests as rage, as when he "flew into a terrible rage" at Dhairya-Sila's calm words. He is easily manipulated by flatterers and quick to believe the worst of the best of his servants. His final act — restoring Dhairya-Sila without asking questions — reveals not repentance but a willingness to accept any convenient narrative that absolves him of guilt.

## Moral Significance

Surya Pratap illustrates the dangers of unchecked royal power. A king who cannot bear to be outshone by his own servants will inevitably destroy the very people who could help him rule wisely. His story serves as a cautionary tale about the relationship between power and character: one who rules like the sun should illuminate, not burn.
