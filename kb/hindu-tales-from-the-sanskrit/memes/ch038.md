[038-001]The three wives very soon had good reason to wish their husbands had

[038-002]stayed away. Instead of being grateful for all [[person-putraka|Putraka]]’s generosity,

[038-003]they were very unkind and exacting, never pleased with anything; and

[038-004]whatever they had given them, they were always trying to get more. In

[038-005]fact, they were silly as well as wicked; for they did not realize that

[038-006]this was not the way to make the king love them or wish to keep them

[038-007]with him. Presently they became [[concept-jealousy|jealous]] of Putraka, and began to wish

[038-008]to get rid of him. His father hated to feel that his son was king,

[038-009]whilst he was only one of that king’s subjects; and he made up his mind

[038-010]to kill him, hoping that if he could only get rid of him he might rule

[038-011]over the country in his stead. He thought and thought how best to

[038-012]manage this, and did not at first mean to tell his brothers anything

[038-013]about it; but in the end he decided he had better have them on his

[038-014]side. So he invited them to go with him to a secret place to talk the

[038-015]matter over.

[038-016]11. What qualities did Putraka’s father show in this plot against his

[038-017]son?

[038-018]12. Was there any other way in which the king’s father could have

[038-019]gained a share in governing the land?
