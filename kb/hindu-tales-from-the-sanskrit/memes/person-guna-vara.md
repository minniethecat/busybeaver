# Guna-Vara

## Role in the Stories

Guna-Vara appears in "The Magic Pitcher" as the beloved wife of King Vira-Bhuja and the mother of his favourite son. The narrator introduces her as one of the king's many wives: *"the one named Guna-Vara, and of all his sons her youngest, Sringa-Bhuja, was his favourite"* [049-004]. She is distinguished not only by her beauty — *"Guna-Vara was not only very beautiful"* [049-005] — but by the singular devotion the king shows her. This very devotion, however, becomes the source of her tragedy: the king's other wives, consumed by jealousy, conspire to destroy her.

Her story is one of unjust suffering, maternal love, and eventual vindication. Guna-Vara endures what no person should: imprisonment by her own husband, based on fabricated evidence, with no opportunity to defend herself. Yet she never loses her dignity, and her son Sringa-Bhuja ultimately proves her innocence and restores her rightful place.

## Key Actions

Guna-Vara's actions in the narrative are defined more by what she endures than what she does — she is a victim who maintains her innocence with quiet dignity. When her jealous co-wives fabricate a story that she does not truly love the king, they exploit the king's insecurity: *"The very fact that Vira-Bhuja loved Guna-Vara so much made it easier to persuade him to believe their evil stories"* [049-024].

The co-wives escalate their campaign by accusing Guna-Vara of plotting against the king's life, claiming that *"the son of Guna-Vara and some of the chief men of the kingdom were going to kill him, so that Sringa-Bhuja might reign in his stead"* [050-013]. Vira-Bhuja, despite his love for her, succumbs to suspicion. The narrator notes the psychological manipulation at work: *"the very fact that Vira-Bhuja loved Guna-Vara so much made it harder for him to doubt the lies"* [049-024].

When confronted, Guna-Vara offers no dramatic protestations of innocence. She simply denies the charge — *"Guna-Vara is not true"* [050-009] — and accepts her fate with a composure that her accusers did not anticipate. Her silence is not weakness but dignity: she knows that protest would be futile against a king already convinced of her guilt.

The king's punishment is absolute: he orders Guna-Vara *"shut up in prison for the rest of her life"* [049-040]. She is confined to a *"strong prison"* [050-002], separated from her son and everything she loves. The narrative emphasizes the pathos of her situation: *"even to think of going to see Guna-Vara in her prison was considered an act of disloyalty to the king"* [050-023]. But *"something held him back, and poor Guna-Vara was left"* to suffer alone [052-011].

Her vindication comes through her son Sringa-Bhuja, who, aided by the magic pitcher and his own courage, exposes the conspiracy. The moment of reunion captures the depth of her ordeal: the narrator describes *"poor Guna-Vara's joy, when the two entered the prison and told her she was free"* [061-023]. In the final reconciliation, the plotters *"pleaded for their forgiveness. Guna-Vara and her son were generous enough to grant it"* [061-035], and she was restored to her position alongside the repentant king.

## Relationships

Guna-Vara's defining relationship is with her husband **King Vira-Bhuja**. Their marriage begins with genuine love and devotion — she is his acknowledged favourite among many wives — but is shattered by the king's credulity. Vira-Bhuja's love paradoxically makes him more vulnerable to manipulation: the intensity of his feeling for Guna-Vara becomes the very channel through which his other wives destroy her. The tragedy is that Vira-Bhuja is not a cruel man but a weak one — *"Vira-Bhuja simply could not believe this"* denial of her guilt [050-018], yet he fails to act on his doubt. Their relationship is ultimately restored through the son who bridges the gap between them, but the story leaves a lingering question about whether such a breach can ever fully heal.

Her relationship with her son **Sringa-Bhuja** is the narrative's emotional anchor. Even in prison, cut off from everyone, her maternal bond endures: the king *"could not refuse"* her plea *"to let her see Sringa-Bhuja again"* [049-046], though this concession is granted only once. Sringa-Bhuja, who *"resembled his mother in her beauty and her goodness"* [049-010], becomes the instrument of her deliverance — the son redeeming the mother, as the mother once nurtured the son.

Her relationship with **the king's other wives** is purely adversarial. They *"hated Guna-Vara"* [049-017] not for anything she had done but for the love the king bore her. Their conspiracy — fabricating a story of infidelity, then escalating to a false treason charge — is the engine of the narrative's tragedy. Notably, Guna-Vara never retaliates; after her vindication, she forgives them, demonstrating a moral stature that her persecutors wholly lacked.

## Character Traits

Guna-Vara embodies the virtue of patient endurance. Unlike more active heroines in the Hindu Tales — such as Buddhi-Mati, who orchestrates an elaborate escape — Guna-Vara's heroism is passive: she survives. She does not break under injustice, does not curse her fate, and does not seek revenge. Her eventual forgiveness of the women who destroyed her life is the story's most challenging moral moment: it presents a standard of generosity that even the reader may struggle to accept.

Her beauty is repeatedly emphasized, but the narrative makes clear that her true worth lies in her character. The fact that her son *"resembled his mother in her beauty and her goodness"* [049-010] links physical beauty with moral goodness — a common Sanskrit literary convention. Guna-Vara's tragedy is that her very virtues (her fidelity, her forbearance) make her an easy target for those who lack them.

## Moral Significance

Guna-Vara's story illustrates several interconnected moral themes. First, it is a cautionary tale about the destructive power of jealousy and credulity: the king's willingness to believe falsehoods about the woman he loves most nearly destroys his family. Second, it demonstrates the redemptive power of forgiveness — both Guna-Vara and her son choose reconciliation over revenge, breaking the cycle of harm. Third, it affirms that truth, however long suppressed, will eventually emerge: the conspiracy that seemed airtight unravels completely when confronted with courage and integrity.

The story also raises uncomfortable questions about power and gender. Guna-Vara has no agency in her own defense: she is accused, judged, and condemned by men (and, indirectly, by women wielding male authority). Her only recourse is to wait — and trust that her son will succeed where she cannot act. The tale thus functions both as a portrait of unjust suffering and as an implicit critique of the structures that enable it.
