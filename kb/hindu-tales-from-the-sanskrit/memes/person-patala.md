# Patala

## Role in the Stories

Princess Patala appears in "The Magic Shoes and Staff" as the daughter of a king and the love interest of the protagonist Putraka. She is introduced when Putraka, using his magic staff to render himself invisible, enters her chamber in the palace and beholds her for the first time. The narrator identifies her simply: "she is a princess, and her name is Patala" (044-006). Her role is that of the beloved — the romantic prize that motivates Putraka's daring actions — but she is far from passive. Patala makes her own choices, choosing to trust the mysterious stranger who appears in her room and ultimately fleeing her father's palace to be with him.

## Key Actions

When Putraka first reveals himself to her, Patala's response is one of quiet wonder rather than fear. Upon waking and finding the strange young man present, the text describes her initial reaction: "thinking she had been dreaming, Patala lay down again, and the king's son began to praise her beauty" (045-024). Once she realizes the situation is real, she listens to him, and his gentle manner wins her over: "it seemed to Patala so wonderful" (045-029). After a long conversation, Patala "begged him to leave her for fear of danger" (045-037), showing both her growing affection and her concern for his safety.

Patala's most decisive action comes when Putraka is discovered in her room by a lady-in-waiting. When the servant hesitates to betray the princess, Patala's loyalty to Putraka is tested — and proven true. The king discovers the truth, and the couple's escape by flight becomes necessary. When Putraka uses his magic shoes to carry them both away, Patala's trust in him is absolute. Upon their reunion after danger: "great indeed was the delight of Patala when her beloved Putraka once more stood beside her" (048-001).

## Relationships

Patala's primary relationship is with **Putraka**, the exiled prince who becomes her lover and eventual husband. Their bond is forged in the secrecy of her chamber, where Putraka visits her nightly using his magic staff: "he came to see Patala, spending the hours until daybreak talking of the happy future which was before them" (046-002). Patala weeps when she learns of the dangers they face (048-008), revealing the depth of her emotional attachment.

Her relationship with her father the king is one of tension — she is his daughter and subject, yet she has secretly given her heart to a stranger. The king's discovery of Putraka creates the crisis that drives the story's climax. She maintains her loyalty to her father's household through the lady who discovers Putraka but refuses to immediately betray her mistress.

## Character Traits

Patala exhibits courage and trust that belie her sheltered upbringing as a princess. She does not scream or call guards when a stranger appears in her bedchamber — instead, she listens, judges his character by his words and manner, and makes a clear-eyed choice. Her love for Putraka is neither frivolous nor impulsive; it grows through repeated visits and conversation. She also demonstrates wisdom in knowing when to keep secrets and when to act, and her devotion is unwavering even when faced with the prospect of leaving everything she has known behind. Patala's character embodies the virtue of faithful love guided by sound judgment.
