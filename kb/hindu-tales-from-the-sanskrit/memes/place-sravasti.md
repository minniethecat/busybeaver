# Sravasti

## Description

Sravasti is introduced as one of the *"smaller cities of India called Sravasti"* [021-001], a provincial urban centre where a significant portion of the story of [[person-hari-sarman]] unfolds. The city is characterised by a close-knit community where news travels fast and reputations are built on public opinion.

The Brahman Hari-Sarman *"became very well known in Sravasti"* [022-001] after his clever handling of a situation that impressed the local people. His fame spread quickly: *"the news spread very quickly through Sravasti"* [025-001], and soon *"one after another of the chief men of Sravasti"* [025-010] came to seek his counsel. The city thus functions as the stage on which Hari-Sarman's false reputation as a wise man is constructed through the credulity of its citizens.

The city also has a practical, mercantile character — one of its notable residents is *"a merchant of Sravasti, who had already done a good"* deed for Hari-Sarman [027-006], highlighting the economic relationships that underpin the social fabric of the town.

## Narrative Role

Sravasti serves as the primary urban backdrop for Hari-Sarman's arc within "A Royal Thief-Catcher." It represents the provincial Indian city — smaller than a royal capital, but large enough to have a merchant class and a lively public sphere where reputations rise and fall. The city's communal nature, where *"the people of Sravasti thought"* [022-028] collectively and news *"spread very quickly"* [025-001], creates the conditions for Hari-Sarman's scheme to flourish: his fame depends not on genuine wisdom but on the speed and credulity of public opinion.

The city also provides a contrast with the royal court — when Hari-Sarman is eventually summoned by the king, the shift from Sravasti's provincial society to the demands of the throne drives the story's central tension.
