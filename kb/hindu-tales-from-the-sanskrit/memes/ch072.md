[072-001][[animal-mouse|Hiranya]], on hearing the last remark of Laghupatin, hesitated, and in

[072-002]the end he agreed that he would have supper with the crow that very

[072-003]evening. “There is plenty of rice here,” he said, “which we can eat on

[072-004]the spot. It would be impossible for you to get into my hole, and I am

[072-005]certainly not disposed to visit you in your nest.” So the two at once

[072-006]began their meal, and before it was over they had become good friends.

[072-007]Not a day passed without a meeting, and when all the rice was eaten up,

[072-008]each of the two would bring something to the feast. This had gone on

[072-009]for some little time, when the crow, who was fond of adventure and

[072-010]change, said one day to the mouse: “Don’t you think we might go

[072-011]somewhere else for a time? I am rather tired of this bit of the [[place-forest|forest]],

[072-012]every inch of which we both know well. I’ve got another great friend

[072-013]who lives beside a fine river a few miles away, a tortoise named

[072-014]Mandharaka; a thoroughly good, [[concept-trust|trustworthy]] fellow he is, though rather

[072-015]slow and cautious in his ways. I should like to introduce you to him.

[072-016]There are quantities of food suitable for us both where he lives, for

[072-017]it is a very fruitful land. What do you say to coming with me to pay

[072-018]him a visit?”

[072-019]“How in the world should I get there?” answered Hiranya. “It’s all very

[072-020]well for you, who can fly. I can’t walk for miles and miles. For all

[072-021]that I too am sick of this place and would like a change.”

[072-022]“Oh, there’s no difficulty about that,” replied Laghupatin. “I will

[072-023]carry you in my beak, and you will get there without any fatigue at

[072-024]all.” To this Hiranya consented, and very early one morning the two

[072-025]friends started off together.
