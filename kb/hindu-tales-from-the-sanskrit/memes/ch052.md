[052-001]As [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]] sped along after the crane, the beggar made some

[052-002]strange signs in the air with [[object-magic-staff|the staff]] he used to help him along; and

[052-003]such clouds of dust arose that no one could see in which direction the

[052-004]young prince had gone. The brothers and Ayasolekha were very much

[052-005]dismayed at the way things had turned out, and greatly feared that the

[052-006]king’s anger would vent itself on them, now that Sringa-Bhuja had

[052-007]disappeared. [[person-vira-bhuja|Vira-Bhuja]] did send for them, and asked them many

[052-008]questions; but they all kept the secret of how Sringa-Bhuja had got the

[052-009]arrow, and promised to do all they could to help to get it back. Again

[052-010]the king thought he would go and see the mother of his dear youngest

[052-011]son; but again something held him back, and poor [[person-guna-vara|Guna-Vara]] was left

[052-012]alone, no one ever going near her except the gaoler who took her her

[052-013]daily food. After trying everything possible to find out where

[052-014]Sringa-Bhuja had gone, the king began to show special favour to another

[052-015]of his sons; and as the months passed by, it seemed as if the young

[052-016]prince and the [[object-jewelled-arrow|jewelled arrow]] were both forgotten.

[052-017]Meanwhile Sringa-Bhuja travelled on and on in the track of the drops of

[052-018]blood, till he came to the outskirts of a fine [[place-forest|forest]], through which

[052-019]many beaten paths led to a very great city. He sat down to rest at the

[052-020]foot of a wide-spreading tree, and was gazing up at the towers and

[052-021]pinnacles of the [[place-city|town]], rising far upwards towards the sky, when he had

[052-022]a feeling that he was no longer alone. He was right: for, coming slowly

[052-023]along one of the paths, was a lovely young girl, singing softly to

[052-024]herself in a beautiful voice. Her eyes were like those of a young doe,

[052-025]and her features were perfect in their form and expression, reminding

[052-026]Sringa-Bhuja of his mother, whom he was beginning to fear he would

[052-027]never see again.

[052-028]When the young girl was quite close to him, he startled her by saying,

[052-029]“Can you tell me what is the name of this city?”

[052-030]“Of course, I can,” she replied, “for I live in it. It is called

[052-031]Dhuma-Pura, and it belongs to my father: he is a great [[concept-magic|magician]] named

[052-032][[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]], who loves not strangers. Now tell me who you are and whence

[052-033]you come?”

[052-034]Then Sringa-Bhuja told the maiden all about himself, and why he was

[052-035][[event-journey|wandering]] so far from home. The girl, whose name was [[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]],

[052-036]listened very attentively; and when he came to the shooting of the

[052-037]crane, and how he had followed the bleeding bird in the hope of getting

[052-038]back his father’s jewelled arrow, she began to tremble.

[052-039]“Alas, alas!” she said. “The bird you shot was my father, who can take

[052-040]any form he chooses. He returned home but yesterday, and I drew the

[052-041]arrow from his wound and dressed the hurt myself. He gave me the

[052-042]jewelled arrow to keep, and I will never part with it. As for you, the

[052-043]sooner you depart the better; for my father never forgives, and he is

[052-044]so powerful that you would have no chance of escape if he knew you were

[052-045]here.”

[052-046]Hearing this, Sringa-Bhuja became very sad, not because he was afraid

[052-047]of Agni-Sikha, but because he knew that he already loved the fair

[052-048]maiden who stood beside him, and was resolved to make her his wife. She

[052-049]too felt drawn towards him and did not like to think of his going away.

[052-050]Besides this, she had much to fear from her father, who was as [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] as

[052-051]he was mighty, and had caused the death already of many lovers who had

[052-052]wished to marry her. She had never cared for any of them, and had been

[052-053][[concept-contentment|content]] to live without a husband, spending her life in wandering about

[052-054]near her home and winning the love of all who lived near her, even that

[052-055]of the wild creatures of the forest, who would none of them dream of

[052-056]hurting her. Often and often she stood between the wrath of her father

[052-057]and those he wished to injure; for, wicked as he was, he loved her and

[052-058]wanted her to be [[concept-happiness|happy]].
