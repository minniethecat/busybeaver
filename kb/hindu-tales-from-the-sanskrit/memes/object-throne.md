# Throne

## Description

The throne is the most politically charged object in the Hindu Tales — a seat that transforms whoever occupies it into an embodiment of royal authority. Its physical description is minimal but its symbolic weight is immense. When a character approaches the throne, the narrative marks the moment with ritual gravity: *"he fell upon his face at the"* [029-015] foot of the throne, the act of prostration establishing the throne as an object before which even powerful men abase themselves.

The throne is also a site of observation and judgment. Attendants standing *"behind the throne looked on, wondering what would"* [029-023] happen, and the king *"on his throne, surrounded by his court"* [047-046] receives reports and issues commands. The throne is not merely furniture — it is the physical anchor of the court, the fixed point around which the machinery of royal justice revolves.

The throne's reach extends beyond the palace walls: the king *"on his throne to the beggars in the street"* [032-016] represents a spectrum of authority that connects the highest and lowest in society. Prasnajit *"still sat on his throne, and the attendants still waited"* [031-012], the word "still" suggesting the permanence and patience of royal power — the throne endures while petitioners come and go.

## Magical Properties

The throne possesses no supernatural magic. Its power is entirely institutional — derived from the social contract of kingship rather than enchantment. This distinguishes it from objects like the magic pitcher or magic staff, whose powers are inherent. The throne's authority is performed: it works because everyone in the narrative agrees that it does.

## Story Role

The throne serves as the **seat of narrative resolution**. When a character is brought before the throne, the story is reaching its climax — judgment will be rendered, truth will be revealed, or punishment will be decreed. The throne scenes in the tales follow a consistent pattern: approach, prostration, petition, judgment. This ritual structure gives the throne a dramatic gravity that no other object in the tales possesses. Even the magic pitcher, for all its wish-granting power, cannot match the throne's hold over human fate.
