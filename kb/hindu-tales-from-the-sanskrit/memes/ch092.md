[092-001]In her trouble about the loss of the king’s love [[person-kadali-garbha|Kadali-Garbha]] longed

[092-002]for her father, for she felt sure he would be able to help her. So she

[092-003]determined to go to him. With the aid of the [[concept-wisdom|wise]] woman who had given

[092-004]her the packet of mustard seed, and who had been her best friend at

[092-005]court, she disguised herself as a messenger, and, mounted on a strong

[092-006]little pony, she sped along the path marked out by the young shoots of

[092-007]mustard, reaching her old home in the [[place-forest|forest]] before the night fell.

[092-008]Great indeed was the [[concept-happiness|joy]] of [[person-mana-kanaka|Mana Kanaka]] at the sight of his beloved

[092-009]child, and very soon she had poured out all her sorrow to him. The

[092-010]hermit was at first very much enraged with his son-in-law for the way

[092-011]in which he had treated Kadali-Garbha, and declared that he would use

[092-012]all the powers he had to punish him. “Never,” he said, “shall he see

[092-013]your dear face again; but I will go to him and call down on him all

[092-014]manner of misfortunes. You know not, dear child, I have never wished

[092-015]you to know, that I am a [[concept-magic|magician]] and can make the very beasts of the

[092-016]field and the winds of heaven [[concept-obedience|obey]] me. I know full well who has made

[092-017]this mischief between you and your husband, and I will see that

[092-018]punishment overtakes them.”

[092-019]“No, no, father,” cried Kadali-Garbha; “I will not have any harm done

[092-020]to my dear one, for I love him with all my heart. All I ask of you is

[092-021]to prove to him that I am innocent of whatever fault he thinks I have

[092-022]committed, and to make him love and trust me again.”

[092-023]It was hard work to persuade Mana Kanaka to promise not to harm the

[092-024]king, but in the end he yielded. Together the father and daughter rode

[092-025]back to the palace, and together they were brought before

[092-026][[person-dridha-varman|Dridha-Varman]], who, in spite of the anger he had felt against his wife,

[092-027]was overjoyed to see her. When he looked at her clinging to Mana

[092-028]Kanaka’s arm, as she had done the first time they met, all his old love

[092-029]returned, and he would have taken her in his arms and told her so

[092-030]before the whole court, if she had not drawn back. It was Mana Kanaka

[092-031]who was the first to speak. Drawing himself up to his full height, and

[092-032]pointing to the king, he charged him with having broken his vow to love

[092-033]and protect his wife. “You have listened to lying tongues,” he said,

[092-034]“and I will tell you to whom those tongues belong, that justice may be

[092-035]done to them.”

[092-036]Once more Kadali-Garbha interfered. “No, father,” she said; “let their

[092-037]names be forgotten: only prove to my lord that I am his loving faithful

[092-038]wife, and I will be [[concept-contentment|content]].”

[092-039]“I need no proof,” cried Dridha-Varman; “but lest others should follow

[092-040]their evil example, I will have vengeance on the slanderers. Name them,

[092-041]and their doom shall be indeed a terrible one.”

[092-042]Then Mana Kanaka told the king the whole sad story; and when it was

[092-043]ended the wicked woman who had first thought of injuring the queen, and

[092-044]the barber who had helped her, were sent for to hear their doom, which

[092-045]was—to be shut up for the rest of their lives in [[event-imprisonment|prison]]. This was

[092-046]changed to two years only, because Kadali-Garbha was [[concept-generosity|generous]] enough to

[092-047]plead for them. As for the third person in the plot, the old witch of

[092-048]the cave, not a word was said about her by anybody. Mana Kanaka knew

[092-049]well enough what her share in the matter had been; but magicians and

[092-050]witches are careful not to make enemies of each other, and so he held

[092-051]his peace.

[092-052]Dridha-Varman was so grateful to his father-in-law for bringing his

[092-053]wife back to him, that he wanted him to stop at court, and said he

[092-054]would give him a very high position there. But Mana Kanaka refused

[092-055]every reward, declaring that he loved his little home in the forest

[092-056]better than the grand rooms he might have had in the palace. “All I

[092-057]wish for,” he said, “is my dear child’s happiness. I hope you will

[092-058]never again listen to stories against your wife. If you do, you may be

[092-059]very sure that I shall hear of it; and next time I know that you have

[092-060]been unkind to her I will punish you as you deserve.”

[092-061]The king was obliged to let Mana Kanaka go, but after this he took

[092-062]Kadali-Garbha to see her father in the forest very often. Later, when

[092-063]the queen had some children of her own, their greatest treat was to go

[092-064]to the little home, in the depths of the wood. They too learnt to love

[092-065]animals, and had a great many pets, but none of those pets were kept in

[092-066]cages.
