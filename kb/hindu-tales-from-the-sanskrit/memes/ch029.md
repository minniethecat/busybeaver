[029-001]When [[person-matri-datta|Matri-Datta]] told his servant that he was to go to the palace to

[029-002]see the king, the man was dreadfully frightened, and begged his master

[029-003]not to make him go. This made Matri-Datta pretty sure that he had done

[029-004]something wrong and was afraid of being found out. “Go at once,” he

[029-005]said, “and whatever you do, speak the truth to the king. That will be

[029-006]your only chance if you have offended him.” Again and again the servant

[029-007]entreated Matri-Datta not to insist, and when he found it was no good,

[029-008]he asked him at least to come with him to the palace and plead for him

[029-009]with [[person-prasnajit|Prasnajit]]. The merchant knew then for certain that something was

[029-010]seriously wrong, and he consented to go to the palace with his servant,

[029-011]partly out of curiosity and partly out of fear for himself. When the

[029-012]two got to the palace, the attendants at once led the servant to the

[029-013]presence of the king, but they would not let the master go with him.

[029-014]Directly the servant entered the room and saw the king sitting on his

[029-015]throne, he fell upon his face at the foot of the steps, crying, “Mercy!

[029-016]mercy!” He was right to be afraid, for Prasnajit said to him in a loud

[029-017]voice: “Where are the gold and the jewels you took from the hole in the

[029-018]roots of a tree when you went to find the nagaballa plant for your

[029-019]master?” The servant, who really had taken the money and jewels, was so

[029-020]terrified when he found that the king knew the truth, that he had not a

[029-021]word to say at first, but just remained lying on the ground, trembling

[029-022]all over. Prasnajit too was silent, and the attendants waiting for

[029-023]orders behind [[object-throne|the throne]] looked on, wondering what would happen now.
