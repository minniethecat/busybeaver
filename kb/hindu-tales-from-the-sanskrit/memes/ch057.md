[057-001]The [[concept-magic|magician]] was very angry indeed when he heard that the field was

[057-002]ploughed and the seed sown. He knew at once that some magic had been at

[057-003]work, and suspected that [[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]] was the cause of his

[057-004]disappointment. Without a moment’s hesitation he said to the prince:

[057-005]“No sooner were you gone than I decided not to have that seed sown. Go

[057-006]back at once, and pile it up where it was before.”

[057-007]This time [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]] felt no fear or hesitation, for he was sure of

[057-008]the power and will to help him of his promised bride. So back he went

[057-009]to the field, and there he found the whole vast space covered with

[057-010]millions and millions of ants, busily collecting the seed and piling it

[057-011]up against the wall of the [[place-city|town]]. Again Rupa-Sikha came to cheer him,

[057-012]and again she warned him that their trials were not yet over. She

[057-013]feared, she said, that her father might prove stronger than herself;

[057-014]for he had many allies at neighbouring courts ready to help him in his

[057-015]evil purposes. “Whatever else he orders you to do, you _must_ see me

[057-016]before you leave the palace. I will send my faithful messenger to

[057-017]appoint a meeting in some secret place.”

[057-018][[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]] was not much surprised when the prince told him that his

[057-019]last order had been obeyed, and thought to himself, “I must get this

[057-020]tiresome fellow out of my [[place-kingdom|domain]], where that too [[concept-cleverness|clever]] child of mine

[057-021]will not be able to help him.” “Well,” he said, “I suppose the wedding

[057-022]must take place to-morrow after all, for I am a man of my word. We must

[057-023]now set about inviting the guests. You shall have the pleasure of doing

[057-024]this yourself: then my friends will know beforehand what a handsome

[057-025]young son-in-law I shall have. The first person to summon to the

[057-026]wedding is my brother Dhuma Sikha, who has taken up his abode in a

[057-027][[place-temple|deserted temple]] a few miles from here. You must ride at once to that

[057-028]temple, rein up your [[animal-horse|steed]] opposite it, and cry, ‘Dhuma Sikha, your

[057-029]brother Agni-Sikha has sent me hither to invite you to witness my

[057-030][[event-wedding|marriage]] with his daughter Rupa-Sikha to-morrow. Come without delay!’

[057-031]Your message given, ride back to me; and I will tell you what farther

[057-032]tasks you must perform before the [[concept-happiness|happy]] morrow dawns.”

[057-033]When Sringa-Bhuja left the palace, he knew not where to seek a horse to

[057-034]bear him on this new errand. But as he was nearing the gateway by which

[057-035]he had gone forth to sow the field with seed, a handsome boy approached

[057-036]him and said, “If my lord will follow me, I will tell him what to do.”

[057-037]Somehow the voice sounded familiar; and when the guards were left far

[057-038]enough behind to be out of hearing, the boy looked up at Sringa-Bhuja

[057-039]with a smile that revealed Rupa-Sikha herself. “Come with me,” she

[057-040]said; and taking his hand, she led him to a tree beneath which stood a

[057-041]noble horse, richly caparisoned, which pawed the ground and whinnied to

[057-042]its mistress, as she drew near.

[057-043]“You must ride this horse,” said Rupa-Sikha, “who will [[concept-obedience|obey]] you if you

[057-044]but whisper in his ear; and you must take earth, water, wood and fire

[057-045]with you, which I will give you. You must go straight to the temple,

[057-046]and when you have called out your message, turn without a moment’s

[057-047]delay, and ride for your life as swiftly as your steed will go, looking

[057-048]behind you all the time. No guidance will be necessary; for Marut—that

[057-049]is my horse’s name—knows well what he has to do.”

[057-050]Then Rupa-Sikha gave Sringa-Bhuja a bowl of earth, a jar of water, a

[057-051]bundle of thorns and a brazier full of burning charcoal, hanging them

[057-052]by strong thongs upon the front of his saddle so that he could reach

[057-053]them easily. “My father,” she told him, “has given my uncle

[057-054]instructions to kill you, and he will follow you upon his swift Arab

[057-055]steed. When you hear him behind you, fling earth in his path; if that

[057-056]does not stop him, pour out some of the water; and if he still

[057-057]perseveres, scatter the burning charcoal before him.”
