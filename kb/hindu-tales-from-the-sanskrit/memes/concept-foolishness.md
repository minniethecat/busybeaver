# Foolishness

## Definition

Foolishness in the Hindu Tales is not mere ignorance or lack of intelligence but rather a failure of practical judgment — knowing what one *should* do and doing the opposite. The tales' embedded discussion questions repeatedly ask variations of *"Was it wise or foolish...?"* — a framing that treats foolishness as the mirror opposite of wisdom and equally subject to moral education.

The tales are remarkably generous toward foolishness. Unlike cruelty or deceit, which are condemned, foolishness is treated as a corrigible flaw — a mistake that can be learned from. Characters who act foolishly are not villains but students who have failed a test, and the narrative itself functions as the teacher who administers the corrective lesson.

## Exemplifying Stories

### Subha Datta's Foolish Choice

The opening tale pivots on a moment of foolishness. Subha Datta, having discovered the magic pitcher and secured his family's prosperity, makes a disastrous decision: *"he was doing a very foolish thing"* (012-049) in revealing the pitcher's existence to his wife. The narrative frames this as a failure of judgment — not wickedness but imprudence. The embedded question — *"Would Subha Datta have been wise if he had told his wife about..."* (012-056) — turns the character's mistake into a teaching opportunity for the reader.

### The Foolish Miser

"A Royal Thief-Catcher" diagnoses a particular form of foolishness: the folly of hoarding. When an adviser sees a man becoming a miser, *"they told him it was foolish to care so much"* (025-015) about wealth that brought no benefit. This is the foolishness of misprioritization — investing emotional energy and moral identity in something that cannot reciprocate. The miser's folly is particularly pointed because it represents a failure to understand what wealth is *for*.

### Foolish Promises

"The Jewelled Arrow" features a classic folktale pattern: the foolish promise. A character is told: *"very foolish were you to promise that"* (055-010) without first ascertaining what the promise entails. The fool in this pattern is not malicious but incautious — his foolishness consists in failing to anticipate that a powerful adversary will exploit a blank check. The tale teaches that some commitments should never be made without full knowledge of their terms.

### The Fool's Tongue

"A Clever Thief" offers a memorable treatment of self-incriminating foolishness. A character laments: *"It is all my foolish tongue which has"* (081-003) caused his trouble, and is asked: *"didst thou behave in such a foolish manner, just for the sake of..."* (081-021). The foolish tongue — speech without forethought — is a recurring motif. The discussion questions press the point: *"Was it wise or foolish of Hari-Sarman to remain in the..."* (082-063). The tales treat verbal imprudence as one of the most common and most avoidable forms of foolishness.

### The Owl's Folly

In the animal fables, foolishness is dramatized through the natural world: *"Was the owl wise or foolish to wait before he caught the..."* (017-023). The owl's hesitation — waiting so long that the prey escapes — represents foolishness as excessive caution, a failure to act when action is needed. This complements the more common portrayal of foolishness as impulsivity, showing that the concept encompasses both extremes of the judgment spectrum.
