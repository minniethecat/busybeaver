# Ganges

## Description

The River Ganges is the most exalted geographic feature in the Hindu Tales, treated with religious reverence by the characters who inhabit the stories. It is introduced as *"the River Ganges, which the Hindus worship"* [024-021], immediately marking it as more than a waterway — it is a sacred entity. A character speaks of going on *"pilgrimage by the banks of the river"* [024-013], reflecting the Ganges' role as a destination for spiritual practice and ritual purification.

The river is described in aesthetic as well as spiritual terms: it is *"the grand and beautiful river which the people of India love and worship"* [048-014], combining natural majesty with cultural devotion. The narrator also calls it one of the *"sacred rivers of India, the Ganges, which the Hindus lovingly ca"* [062-004] — the incomplete sentence at the chapter boundary suggesting the reverence is so self-evident it needs no elaboration.

Unlike the generic "river" that appears as a landscape feature throughout the tales, the Ganges carries specific religious weight. It is not simply crossed or followed; it is worshipped, pilgrimaged to, and invoked as a symbol of Indian spirituality.

## Narrative Role

The Ganges functions in the tales as a spiritual anchor — a fixed point of sacred geography that lends the narratives cultural authenticity. When characters speak of pilgrimage to the Ganges, they invoke a shared religious framework that the original audience would recognize immediately. The river is never merely background; each mention carries the weight of worship. Its presence across three different stories also demonstrates how the tales share a common cultural universe — the Ganges is the same holy river whether encountered by Matri-Datta, Putraka, or Dhairya-Sila.
