# The Palace

## Description

The palace is the architectural embodiment of royal authority in the Hindu Tales. Every named city — Vardhamana, Sravasti, Chinchini — has one, and it is invariably the destination of anyone seeking justice, favor, or audience with the king. Its physical layout follows a consistent pattern: outer gates guarded by soldiers [031-010], inner chambers where the king receives visitors [029-012], and upper rooms described as being *"as high as the summits of the mountains"* [044-009].

Entry to the palace is a formal, mediated process. Attendants *"at once led them"* [029-012] inside, suggesting a protocol of admission. Those without proper standing must plead: a servant begs his master *"at least to come with him to the palace and plead for him"* [029-008]. The palace gates are a social filter, separating those who belong from those who must petition.

Like the forest, the palace interior is rarely described in detail — it is the actions that occur within that define it. The Brahman is brought in to treat the king [027-014]; Putraka defies a hostile monarch [047-051]; Dhairya-Sila is brought before King Surya Pratap to face judgment [062-034]. The palace is where power is made visible through speech, gesture, and decree.

## Narrative Role

The palace is the stage for the exercise and contestation of power. Every scene set there involves a power dynamic: a petitioner and a granter, an accuser and a defender, a subject and a sovereign. Prasnajit uses his palace as a testing ground for the Brahman's medical skill [027-011]; Putraka must pass the palace gates — and the soldiers who guard them [031-010] — to reclaim his rightful place; Dhairya-Sila's brave words before the king [062-034] transform the palace from a space of judgment into a space of truth-telling.

The palace also functions as a container for the vulnerable. The princess is imprisoned in its high rooms [044-009]; a baby is abandoned at its entrance [032-019]. In each case, the palace walls both protect and confine. This ambivalence — sanctuary and prison — reflects the dual nature of royal power itself, which can shelter its subjects or crush them depending on the character of the ruler within.
