[026-001]In spite of all that any one could say to him, the Brahman was quite

[026-002]determined that he would not live any longer. He set off to the place

[026-003]of pilgrimage he had chosen, taking no notice of any one he met, but

[026-004]just marching steadily on. At first a number of people followed him,

[026-005]but by degrees they left off doing so, and soon he was quite alone.

[026-006]Presently however he could not help noticing a man approaching from the

[026-007]direction in which he was going. Very tall, very handsome, very

[026-008]dignified, this man was one whom no one could fail to admire, even if

[026-009]he had been only an ordinary person. But he was the king of the whole

[026-010]country, whose name was [[person-prasnajit|Prasnajit]]; and a little distance behind him

[026-011]were a number of his attendants, waiting to [[concept-obedience|obey]] his orders. Everybody,

[026-012]even the Brahman, loved the king, because he took such a very great

[026-013]interest in his people and was always trying to do them good. He had

[026-014]heard all about the loss of the money, and was very much vexed that

[026-015]such a thing should have happened in his land. He had also heard that

[026-016]the Brahman meant to kill himself, and this distressed him more than

[026-017]anything else, because he thought it a very wicked and terrible thing

[026-018]to do.

[026-019]The king stood so exactly in the path of the Brahman that it was

[026-020]impossible to pass him by without taking any notice of him, and the

[026-021]unhappy man stood still, hanging down his head and looking very

[026-022]miserable. Without waiting for a moment, Prasnajit said to the Brahman:

[026-023]“Do not grieve any more. I will find your treasure for you, and give it

[026-024]back to you; or if I fail to do so I will pay you as much as it was

[026-025]worth out of my own purse: for I cannot bear to think of your killing

[026-026]yourself. Now tell me very carefully where you hid your gold and

[026-027]jewels, and everything about the place, to help me to make sure of it.”

[026-028][[person-brahman-jewelled-arrow|The Brahman]] was greatly delighted to hear this, because he knew full

[026-029]well that the king would keep his word, and that, even if his own

[026-030]treasure was never found, he would have plenty of money given to him by

[026-031]the king. He at once told Prasnajit exactly where he had put his store,

[026-032]and offered to take him there. The king agreed to go with him at once,

[026-033]and he and the Brahman went straight away to the big hole in the

[026-034][[place-forest|forest]], the attendants following them a little way behind.
