[058-001]Away went the prince after he had received these instructions; and very

[058-002]soon he found himself opposite the temple, with the images of three of

[058-003]the gods worshipped in [[place-india|India]] to prove that it had been a sanctuary

[058-004]before the [[concept-magic|magician]] took up his abode in it. Directly [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]]

[058-005]shouted out his message to Dhuma-Sikha, the wicked dweller in the

[058-006]temple came rushing forth from the gateway, mounted on a huge horse,

[058-007]which seemed to be belching forth flames from its nostrils as it

[058-008]bounded along. For one terrible moment Sringa-Bhuja feared that he was

[058-009][[event-disappearance|lost]]; but Marut, putting forth all his strength, kept a little in

[058-010]advance of the enemy, [[concept-generosity|giving]] the prince time to scatter earth behind

[058-011]him. Immediately a [[place-mountain|great mountain]] rose up, barring the road, and

[058-012]Sringa-Bhuja felt that he was saved. He was mistaken: for, as he looked

[058-013]back, he saw Dhuma-Sikha coming over the top of the mountain. The next

[058-014]moment the magician was close upon him. So he emptied his bowl of

[058-015]water: and, behold, a huge river with great waves hid pursuer and

[058-016]pursued from each other. Even this did not stop the mighty Arab horse,

[058-017]which swam rapidly across, the rider loudly shouting out orders to the

[058-018]prince to stop. When the prince heard the hoofs striking on the dry

[058-019]ground behind him again, he threw out the thorns, and a dense wood

[058-020]sprouted up as if by magic, which for a few moments gave fresh hope of

[058-021]safety to Sringa-Bhuja; for it seemed as if even the powerful magician

[058-022]would be unable to get through it. He did succeed however; but his

[058-023]clothes were nearly torn off his back, and his horse was bleeding from

[058-024]many wounds made by the [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] thorns. Sringa-Bhuja too was getting

[058-025]weary, and remembered that he had only one more chance of checking his

[058-026]relentless enemy. He could almost feel the breath of the panting [[animal-horse|steed]]

[058-027]as it drew near; and with a loud cry to his beloved [[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]], he

[058-028]threw the burning charcoal on the road. In an instant the grass by the

[058-029]wayside, the trees overshadowing it, and the magic wood which had

[058-030]sprung from the thorns, were alight, burning so fiercely that no living

[058-031]thing could approach them safely. The wicked magician was beaten at

[058-032]last, and was soon himself [[event-escape|fleeing]] away, as fast as he could, with the

[058-033]flames following after him as if they were eager to consume him.

[058-034]Whether his enemy ever got back to his temple, Sringa-Bhuja never knew.

[058-035]Exhausted with all he had been through, the young prince was taken back

[058-036]to the palace by the faithful Marut, and there he found his dear

[058-037]Rupa-Sikha awaiting him. She told him that her father had promised her

[058-038]that, if the prince came back, he would oppose her [[event-wedding|marriage]] no longer.

[058-039]“For,” he said, “if he can escape your uncle, he must be more than

[058-040]mortal, and worthy even of my daughter.” “He does not in the least

[058-041]expect to see you again,” added Rupa-Sikha; “and even if he allows us

[058-042]to marry, he will never cease to hate you; for I am quite sure he knows

[058-043]that you shot the [[object-jewelled-arrow|jewelled arrow]] at him when he was in the form of a

[058-044]crane. If I ever am your wife, he will try to punish you through me.

[058-045]But have no fear: I shall know how to manage him. Fresh powers have

[058-046]been lately given to me by another uncle whose magic is stronger than

[058-047]that of any of my other relations.”

[058-048]When Sringa-Bhuja had bathed and rested, he robed himself once more in

[058-049]the [[object-robe|garments]] he had worn the day he first saw Rupa-Sikha; and together

[058-050]the lovers went to the great hall to seek an interview with [[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]].

[058-051]The magician, who had made quite sure that he had now got rid of the

[058-052]unwelcome suitor for his daughter’s hand, could not contain his rage,

[058-053]at seeing him walk in with her as if the two were already wedded.

[058-054]He stamped about, pouring out abuse, until he had quite exhausted

[058-055]himself, the lovers looking on quietly without speaking. At last,

[058-056]coming close to them, Agni-Sikha shouted, in a loud harsh voice: “So

[058-057]you have not obeyed my orders. You have not bid my brother to the

[058-058]wedding. Your life is forfeit, and you will die to-morrow instead of

[058-059]marrying Rupa-Sikha. Describe the temple in which Dhuma Sikha lives and

[058-060]the appearance of its owner.”

[058-061]Then Sringa-Bhuja gave such an exact account of the temple, naming the

[058-062]gods whose images still adorned it, and of the terrible man riding the

[058-063]noble steed who had pursued him, that the magician was convinced

[058-064]against his will; and knowing that he must keep his word to Rupa-Sikha,

[058-065]he gave his consent for the preparations for the marriage on the morrow

[058-066]to begin.
