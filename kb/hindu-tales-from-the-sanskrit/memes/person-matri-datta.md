# Matri-Datta

## Role in the Stories

Matri-Datta appears in "A Royal Thief-Catcher" as a merchant and subject of the king. He is introduced when news of his illness reaches the palace: "the king learned that a merchant named Matri-Datta was very ill, suffering from a strange disease" (027-030). His role is that of the reluctant subject — a sick man caught in the crossfire of royal suspicion. The king, obsessed with catching a thief, takes an interest in Matri-Datta's medical condition, learning that "the doctor had ordered Matri-Datta to take a medicine so rare and expensive that only the king himself could provide it" (027-040). This medical detail becomes the king's pretext for summoning the merchant.

## Key Actions

Matri-Datta's defining action is his reluctant obedience to a royal summons he dare not refuse. Though "suffering though he was, Matri-Datta did not dare disobey the king's command" (028-001), and presents himself at court despite his illness. His physical condition and visible suffering mark him as a sympathetic figure caught in the machinery of royal paranoia.

When questioned by the king, Matri-Datta's response reveals his fear: "To this Matri-Datta replied trembling with fear" (028-008). He is clearly terrified of royal displeasure, his trembling a physical manifestation of the terror that the king's arbitrary power inspires in his subjects. His fear is well-founded — the king's interest in him is not benevolent but investigatory, and Matri-Datta realizes he has become a suspect in the theft investigation.

The crisis deepens when Matri-Datta confides in his servant about the royal summons. "When Matri-Datta told his servant that he was summoned to the palace, the servant tried to persuade him not to go" (029-001). The servant's desperate attempts to dissuade him only heighten Matri-Datta's suspicion: "This made Matri-Datta pretty sure that his servant had done something wrong" (029-003), perhaps even that the servant was the thief. The servant "entreated Matri-Datta not to insist" on investigating further (029-007), and when Matri-Datta persisted, events took a decisive turn.

## Relationships

Matri-Datta's relationship with **the king** is one of subject to sovereign, marked by fear and the absolute power differential between them. The king's concern for Matri-Datta's health is purely instrumental — a means to an investigative end. Matri-Datta understands this and responds not with the confidence of an innocent man but with the trembling fear of one who knows that royal justice can be arbitrary.

His relationship with his **servant** forms the subplot of his story. What begins as routine master-servant interaction transforms into mutual suspicion. The servant's attempts to prevent Matri-Datta from going to the palace suggest guilt, while Matri-Datta's growing certainty that his servant is the thief drives the resolution of the theft investigation.

## Character Traits

Matri-Datta is characterized by his vulnerability: he is sick, frightened, and trapped between his duty to his king and his growing suspicion of his own servant. Unlike protagonists who act boldly, Matri-Datta illustrates the experience of the ordinary person caught in extraordinary circumstances. His trembling fear before the king is not cowardice but a realistic response to unchecked power. His persistence in uncovering the truth about his servant, despite his own fear and illness, shows a latent courage — the determination of a wrongfully suspected man to clear his name.
