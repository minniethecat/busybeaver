# Wisdom

## Definition

Wisdom in the Hindu Tales is presented as a quality distinct from mere intelligence or cunning. It is associated with age, experience, and above all with moral clarity — the ability to judge not only what is effective but what is right. The tales feature "wise men" as recurring archetypes who serve as royal advisors, village elders, and moral commentators. A wise person in these stories is one who can perceive hidden truths, foresee consequences, and guide others toward virtuous action.

Crucially, the tales distinguish wisdom from cleverness. A clever person like Hari-Sarman in "A Clever Thief" may succeed temporarily through wit, but the narrative consistently privileges the deeper, more reflective wisdom of characters like the wise old crow or the wise woman who counsels Kadali-Garbha. In the moral economy of the tales, wisdom is a virtue earned through suffering, reflection, and alignment with dharma.

## Exemplifying Stories

### The Wise Old Crow

"A Crow and His Three Friends" opens with a portrait of seasoned wisdom: *"in a forest in India, lived a wise old crow"* (069-001). The crow — watchful, perceptive, and unhurried — serves as a mentor and protector to his three companions. His wisdom lies in his ability to read danger before it materializes and to counsel restraint. When he advises caution, the narrative frames his hesitation not as cowardice but as the fruit of long experience (070-008).

### Wise Advisors in the Royal Court

The tales are populated with "wise men of the court" who serve kings as truth-finders and justice-keepers. In "A Clever Thief," when Hari-Sarman falsely claims to possess wisdom, the court's genuine wise men detect his deceit through patient questioning: *"the wise men knew he was not speaking..."* (082-043). This episode illustrates that true wisdom recognizes counterfeits of itself — the genuinely wise are not easily fooled by pretenders. A wise old man lectures a character at length on proper conduct: *"a specially wise old man gave him a long lecture"* (025-017).

### The Wise Woman's Counsel

"The Hermit's Daughter" introduces a female embodiment of wisdom: *"amongst these ladies was a very wise woman who could see what was"* (087-027). Unlike the public, institutional wisdom of the court advisors, the wise woman's knowledge is intimate and prophetic — she perceives the emotional truth of Kadali-Garbha's situation and warns her of approaching danger. The narrative validates her counsel by having the predicted trouble materialize (087-039).

### Wisdom and Folly Contrasted

Several tales pose the question directly to the reader, suggesting their origin as pedagogical tools: *"Would Subha Datta have been wise if he had told his wife about..."* (012-056), *"Was the owl wise or foolish to wait before he..."* (017-023). These questions embed wisdom as an active interpretive exercise — the tales are not merely about wisdom but are instruments for cultivating it in their audience.
