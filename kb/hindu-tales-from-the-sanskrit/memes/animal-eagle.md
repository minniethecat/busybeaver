## Description

The eagle appears in the Hindu Tales in two strikingly different roles. In one context it is a genuine predator — "the claws even of a tiger, a wild cat or an eagle, could not penetrate" the tortoise's armour.[^1] In another, it is the invention of a storyteller: the vizier Dhairya-Sila, rescued from the tower by a beetle, fabricates a tale of being saved by "a great and noble eagle, which appeared above me, hovering with outspread wings."[^2]

The eagle thus becomes a narrative device for exploring truth and reputation. The vizier chooses the eagle as the hero of his false account because an eagle is a suitably grand and dignified rescuer — unlike a humble beetle. His wife Buddhi-Mati "could never understand why he made up the story about the eagle, and constantly urged him to tell the truth."[^3] The Raja's credulous acceptance of "the lie about the eagle"[^4] reveals more about human nature than about any actual bird.

## Appearances

- **The Beetle and the Silken Thread** — The eagle features as the star of the vizier's fabricated escape story. Dhairya-Sila, embarrassed to admit a beetle saved him, invents a tale of an eagle that "appeared above me, hovering with outspread wings, as if about to swoop down upon me."[^2] The lie is accepted without question by the Raja, highlighting the gap between the modest truth and the grand fiction people prefer to believe.[^4]
- **A Crow and His Three Friends** — The tortoise Mandharaka, boasting of his natural armour, cites the eagle's claws alongside the tiger's as an example of the harm from which his shell protects him.[^1]

## Narrative Function

The eagle's narrative function is unique: it is simultaneously real and fictional within the world of the tales. As a real predator, it represents genuine danger. As the subject of Dhairya-Sila's lie, it represents the human preference for grand narratives over humble truths. The eagle is what people wish had happened — a noble rescuer — in contrast to the beetle, which is what actually did happen.
