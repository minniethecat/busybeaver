[051-001]Now the fact of the matter was that Ayasolekha, who had told the wicked

[051-002]story about [[person-guna-vara|Guna-Vara]], knew where the king kept [[object-jewelled-arrow|the arrow]], had taken it

[051-003]to her private rooms, and had sent for her own sons and those of the

[051-004]other wives, all of whom hated [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]], to tell them of a plot to

[051-005]get their brother into disgrace. “You know,” she said to them, “how

[051-006]much better your father loves Sringa-Bhuja than he does any of you; and

[051-007]that, when he dies, he will leave the kingdom and all his money to him.

[051-008]Now I will help you to prevent this by getting rid of Sringa-Bhuja.

[051-009]“You must have a great shooting match, in which your brother will be

[051-010]delighted to take part, for he is very proud of his skill with the bow

[051-011]and arrow. On the day of the match, I will send for him and give him

[051-012]the jewelled arrow belonging to your father to shoot with, telling him

[051-013]the king had said I might lend it to him. Your father will then think

[051-014]he stole it and order him to be killed.”

[051-015]The brothers were all delighted at what they thought a very [[concept-cleverness|clever]]

[051-016]scheme, and did just what Ayasolekha advised. When the day came, great

[051-017]crowds assembled to see the shooting at a large target set up near the

[051-018]palace. The king himself and all his court were watching the scene from

[051-019]the walls, and it was difficult for the guards to keep the course

[051-020]clear. The brothers, beginning at the eldest, all pretended to try and

[051-021]hit the target; but none of them really wished to succeed, because they

[051-022]thought that, when Sringa-Bhuja’s turn came, as their father’s youngest

[051-023]son, he would win the match with the jewelled arrow. Then the king

[051-024]would order him to be brought before him, and he would be condemned to

[051-025]death or [[place-prison|imprisonment]] for life.

[051-026]Now, as very often happens, something no one in the least expected

[051-027]upset the carefully planned plot. Just as Sringa-Bhuja was about to

[051-028]shoot at the target, a big crane flew on to the ground between him and

[051-029]it, so that it was impossible for him to take proper aim. The brothers,

[051-030]seeing the bird and anxious to shoot it for themselves, all began to

[051-031]clamour that they should be allowed to shoot again. Nobody made any

[051-032]objection, and Sringa-Bhuja stood aside, with the jewelled arrow in the

[051-033]bow, waiting to see what they would do, but feeling sure that he would

[051-034]be the one to kill the bird. Brother after brother tried, but the great

[051-035]creature still remained untouched, when a travelling mendicant stepped

[051-036]forward and cried aloud:

[051-037]“That is no bird, but an evil [[concept-magic|magician]] who has taken that form to

[051-038][[concept-deceit|deceive]] you all. If he is not killed before he takes his own form

[051-039]again, he will bring misery and ruin upon this [[place-city|town]] and the surrounding

[051-040]country.”

[051-041]You know perhaps that mendicants or beggars in [[place-india|India]] are often holy men

[051-042]whose advice even kings are [[concept-happiness|glad]] to listen to; so that, when everyone

[051-043]heard what this beggar said, there was great excitement and terror. For

[051-044]many were the stories told of the misfortunes Rakshas or evil magicians

[051-045]had brought on other cities. The brothers all wanted to try their luck

[051-046]once more, but the beggar checked them, saying:

[051-047]“No, no. Where is your youngest brother Sringa-Bhuja? He alone will be

[051-048]able to save your homes, your wives and your children, from

[051-049]destruction,”

[051-050]Then Sringa-Bhuja came forward; and as the sun flashed upon the jewels

[051-051]in the stolen arrow, revealing to the watching king that it was his own

[051-052]beloved son who had taken it, the young prince let it fly straight for

[051-053]the bird. It wounded but did not kill the crane, which flew off with

[051-054]the arrow sticking in its breast, the blood dripping from it in its

[051-055][[event-escape|flight]], which became gradually slower and slower. At the sight of the

[051-056]bird going off with the precious jewelled arrow, the king was filled

[051-057]with rage, and sent orders that Sringa-Bhuja should be fetched to his

[051-058]presence immediately. But before the messengers reached him, he had

[051-059]started in pursuit of the bird, guided by the blood-drops on the

[051-060]ground.
