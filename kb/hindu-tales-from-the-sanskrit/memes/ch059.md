[059-001]The [[event-wedding|marriage]] was celebrated the next day with very great pomp; and a

[059-002]beautiful suite of rooms was given to the bride and bridegroom, who

[059-003]could not in spite of this feel safe or [[concept-happiness|happy]], because they knew full

[059-004]well that [[person-agni-sikha|Agni-Sikha]] hated them. The prince soon began to feel

[059-005]home-sick and anxious to introduce his beautiful wife to his own

[059-006]people. He remembered that he had left his dear mother in [[event-imprisonment|prison]], and

[059-007]reproached himself for having forgotten her for so long. So he said to

[059-008][[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]]:

[059-009]“Let us go, beloved, to my native city, [[place-city|Vardhamana]]. My heart yearns

[059-010]after my dear ones there, and I would fain introduce you to them.”

[059-011]“My lord,” replied Rupa-Sikha, “I will go with you whither you will,

[059-012]were it even to the ends of the earth. But we must not let my father

[059-013]guess we mean to go; for he would forbid us to leave the country and

[059-014]set spies to watch our every movement. We will steal away secretly,

[059-015]riding together on my faithful Marut and taking with us only what we

[059-016]can carry.” “And my [[object-jewelled-arrow|jewelled arrow]],” said the prince, “that I may give

[059-017]it back to my father and explain to him how I [[event-disappearance|lost]] it. Then shall I be

[059-018]restored to his favour, and maybe he will forgive my mother also.”

[059-019]“Have no fear,” answered Rupa-Sikha: “all will surely go well with us.

[059-020]Forget not that new powers have been given to me, which will save us

[059-021]from my father and aid me to rescue my dear one’s mother from her evil

[059-022]fate.”

[059-023]Before the dawn broke on the next day, the two set forth unattended,

[059-024]Marut seeming to take pride in his double burden and bearing them along

[059-025]so swiftly that they had all but reached the bounds of the country

[059-026]under the dominion of Agni-Sikha as the sun rose. Just as they thought

[059-027]they were safe from pursuit, they heard a loud rushing noise behind;

[059-028]and looking round, they saw the father of the bride close upon them on

[059-029]his Arab [[animal-horse|steed]], with sword uplifted in his hand to strike. “Fear not,”

[059-030]whispered Rupa-Sikha to her husband. “I will show you now what I can

[059-031]do.” And waving her arms to and fro, as she muttered some strange

[059-032]words, she changed herself into an old woman and [[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]] into an

[059-033]old man, whilst Marut became a great pile of wood by the [[place-road|road-side]].

[059-034]When the angry father reached the spot, the bride and bridegroom were

[059-035]busily gathering sticks to add to the pile, seemingly too absorbed in

[059-036]their work to take any notice of the angry [[concept-magic|magician]], who shouted out to

[059-037]them:

[059-038]“Have you seen a man and a woman pass along this way?”

[059-039]The old woman straightened herself, and peering, up into his face,

[059-040]said:

[059-041]“No; we are too busy over our work to notice anything else.”

[059-042]“And what, pray, are you doing in my wood?” asked Agni-Sikha.

[059-043]“We are helping to collect the fuel for the pyre of the great magician

[059-044]Agni-Sikha,” answered Rupa-Sikha. “Do you not know that he died

[059-045]yesterday?”

[059-046]The Hindus of [[place-india|India]] do not bury but burn the dead; so that it was quite

[059-047]a natural thing for the people of the land over which the magician

[059-048]ruled to collect the materials for the pyre or heap of wood on which

[059-049]his body would be laid to be burnt. What surprised Agni-Sikha, and in

[059-050]fact nearly took his breath away, was to be quietly told that he was

[059-051]dead. He began to think that he was dreaming, and said to himself, “I

[059-052]cannot really be dead without knowing it, so I must be asleep.” And he

[059-053]quietly turned his horse round and rode slowly home again. This was

[059-054]just what his daughter wanted; and as soon as he was out of sight, she

[059-055]turned herself, her husband and Marut, into their natural forms again,

[059-056]laughing merrily, as she did so, at the thought of the ease with which

[059-057]she had got rid of her father.
