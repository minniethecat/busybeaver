# Cave

## Description

The cave appears in the final chapters of the Hindu Tales as the dwelling of Asoka-Mala, a woman of insight who *"lived in a cave not far from the town"* [088-022]. Unlike the forest, palace, or temple — settings defined by their relationship to society — the cave is a place set apart, a residence chosen for its remove from the busy life of the settlement.

The cave is not described in physical detail, but its function is clear: it is a destination for those seeking wisdom. Visitors come to the cave bearing gifts — *"fifty pieces of gold to the cave"* [088-038] — and Asoka-Mala receives them there, dispensing the knowledge for which she is known. The cave thus serves as both home and consulting-chamber, a liminal space where the ordinary rules of town life give way to something closer to oracular truth.

At the story's conclusion, when *"not a word was said about"* [092-048] certain matters back at the cave, the silence itself is telling — the cave, having served its narrative purpose as a place of revelation, recedes into the landscape.

## Narrative Role

The cave functions as the **wise woman's sanctuary** — a natural space appropriated for human wisdom rather than human society. Its location *"not far from the town"* [088-022] is significant: Asoka-Mala is accessible but not embedded; she lives at the boundary between civilization and wilderness, and it is precisely this marginal position that gives her counsel its authority. The cave, unlike the temple or palace, carries no institutional weight — its power derives entirely from the person who inhabits it.
