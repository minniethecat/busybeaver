# Mountain

## Description

Mountains in the Hindu Tales are ambiguous landforms — at once natural and supernatural, obstacles and destinations. They appear primarily in the story of Putraka, where the mountain plays two contrasting roles.

First, the mountain is a **refuge of knowledge**. Putraka encounters a hermit who lives among the mountains, and when the youth expresses a desire to learn, the hermit tells him of *"the top of the highest mountains"* [044-013] — the literal and metaphorical summit where wisdom might be found. The phrase *"mountains"* [044-010] is spoken with reverence, suggesting an ascetic realm far above the plains of ordinary life.

Second, the mountain becomes a **magical barrier**. As Putraka flees with his bride, *"immediately a great mountain rose up, barring the road"* [058-011] — conjured into being by supernatural agency to block his escape. This mountain is not a static feature of the landscape but an active intervention: it rises in response to pursuit, and the narrative tracks a pursuer *"coming over the top of the mountain"* [058-013], transforming it from barrier to battlefield.

The mountain's dual nature — sanctuary in one context, prison wall in another — reflects the tales' broader treatment of landscape as morally responsive to character and circumstance.

## Narrative Role

The mountain operates as a **narrative hinge** in Putraka's story. In the earlier passage, it represents the vertical axis of spiritual aspiration — the hermit's mountain is a place to climb toward, a geography of enlightenment. In the later passage, the mountain inverts this symbolism: instead of being climbed toward, it rises to block; instead of opening a path upward, it closes the path forward.

This reversal mirrors Putraka's own arc: the mountain that once symbolised the wisdom he sought becomes the obstacle he must overcome. The fact that a pursuer comes *"over the top"* [058-013] of the magically raised mountain also suggests that no barrier, however supernaturally imposed, is absolute — every mountain has a far side.
