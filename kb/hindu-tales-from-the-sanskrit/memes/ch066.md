[066-001][[person-buddhi-mati|Buddhi-Mati]], though her hands shook and her heart beat fast as she

[066-002]realized all that depended on her, kept the silk from becoming

[066-003]entangled; and when it was nearly all unwound, she heard her husband’s

[066-004]voice saying to her: “Now tie the cotton thread to the end of the silk

[066-005]that you hold, and let it gradually unwind.” She obeyed, fully

[066-006]understanding now what all these preparations were for.

[066-007]When the little messenger of life reached the top of the tower,

[066-008][[person-dhairya-sila|Dhairya-Sila]] took it up in his hand and very gently unfastened the

[066-009][[object-beetle-thread|silken thread]] from its body. Then he placed [[animal-beetle|the beetle]] carefully in a

[066-010]fold of his turban, and began to pull the silken thread up—very, very

[066-011]slowly, for if it had broken, his wonderful scheme would have come to

[066-012]an end. Presently he had the cotton thread in his fingers, and he broke

[066-013]off the silk, wound it up, and placed it too in his turban. It had done

[066-014]its duty well, and he would not throw it away.

[066-015]“Half the work is done now,” he whispered to his faithful wife. “You

[066-016]have all but saved me now. Take the twine and tie it to the end of the

[066-017]cotton thread.”

[066-018]Very happily Buddhi-Mati obeyed once more; and soon the cotton thread

[066-019]and twine were also laid aside, and the strong rope tied to the last

[066-020]was being quickly dragged up by the [[concept-cleverness|clever]] vizier, who knew that all

[066-021]fear of death from sunstroke or hunger was over. When he had all the

[066-022]rope on the tower, he fastened one end of it to the iron railing which

[066-023]ran round the platform on which he stood, and very quickly slid down to

[066-024]the bottom, where his wife was waiting for him, trembling with [[concept-happiness|joy]].
