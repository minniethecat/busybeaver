# Robe

## Description

Robes appear throughout the Hindu Tales as garments that mark significant transitions — from poverty to wonder, from travel to ceremony, from danger to authority. The most enchanted robes are those encountered in the fairy realm: Subha Datta, upon entering the supernatural space, saw that the trees *"hung beautiful robes"* [009-017] — garments suspended like fruit, available for the taking. His companions *"got down the robes"* [009-024] from the branches, and later asked about the *"robes"* [009-029] alongside jewels, treating the fairy robes as treasures equal to precious stones.

In the mortal world, robes serve as markers of status and moments of transformation. Putraka, in a moment of crisis, found the magic *"bowl in the pocket of his robes"* [047-040] — the robe's pocket serving as the unassuming container for magical power. Sringa-Bhuja, after bathing and resting, *"robed himself once more in"* [058-048] finery, the act of robing signalling his return to princely dignity. At the story's conclusion, the king *"sat in his royal robes"* [082-056], the robes visually establishing the restoration of legitimate authority.

The robes range from supernatural (the fairy garments that grow on trees) to ceremonial (the king's royal attire), unified by their function as external markers of internal states — wonder, power, legitimacy, or transformation.

## Magical Properties

The fairy robes possess a passive enchantment: they appear in the fairy realm as natural growths, available without cost or quest, and their beauty is self-evident. They are not described as conferring powers upon the wearer, but their origin in the supernatural realm marks them as fundamentally different from mortal garments. The contrast with the royal robes is instructive: one set hangs on magic trees, the other adorns a throne room — different sources of authority, expressed through the same medium of cloth.

## Story Role

Robes function across the tales as **vestimentary signals** — clothing that communicates status change. Subha Datta's acquisition of fairy robes marks his passage from mortal woodcutter to beneficiary of supernatural favour. Sringa-Bhuja's robing marks his transition from fugitive to prince. The king's royal robes mark the restoration of order. In each case, the robe is not merely clothing but a narrative punctuation mark — the moment when a character's changed circumstances become visible.
