# Bravery

## Definition

Bravery in the Hindu Tales is portrayed as a virtue of action rather than contemplation. Unlike wisdom, which is cultivated through reflection, bravery is demonstrated in moments of crisis when a character must choose between retreat and advance, silence and speech. The tales consistently show that bravery does not require physical strength — it can be exercised by the young, the weak, and the outnumbered — but it does require moral clarity about what is worth risking oneself for.

The tales also draw an implicit distinction between bravery and recklessness. The brave character acts with purpose and often with strategy; the merely reckless character acts on impulse and typically comes to grief. Bravery is thus a form of wisdom in action — the judgment that a cause is worth the risk.

## Exemplifying Stories

### The Brave Woodcutter's Sons

In the opening tale, the two sons of Subha Datta display physical courage that the narrator explicitly notes: *"had it not been that the two boys were brave, plucky"* (007-006). Their bravery is presented as a natural complement to their father's initial simplicity — where the father was cautious to a fault about revealing the magic pitcher, the sons are willing to act. The ellipsis in the PN excerpt suggests that their bravery was instrumental in a positive outcome, though the tale also implies that bravery alone is insufficient without wisdom.

### Putraka's Courage Before the King

"The Magic Shoes and Staff" presents bravery as moral defiance in the face of unjust power. When Putraka is brought before the hostile king who opposes his love for Patala, he *"looked bravely into the face of his dear..."* (047-051). This is not the bravery of battle but the bravery of bearing — the courage to stand before authority and refuse to be cowed. Putraka's bravery is validated by the narrative: he wins his beloved and eventually the kingdom, proving that moral courage is as powerful as any magic object.

### Brave Words Before a Tyrant

"A Royal Thief-Catcher" offers the most politically charged instance of bravery. When Dhairya-Sila, unjustly condemned, speaks before the cruel King Surya Pratap: *"On hearing these brave words, Surya Pratap flew into"* (062-034) a rage. The king's fury at being challenged confirms that the words were indeed brave — they risked death. Dhairya-Sila's bravery here is that of the truth-teller who refuses to flatter power, a figure celebrated across Indian literary traditions from the Puranas to the modern tale.

## Contrasting Examples

The tales present implicit contrasts to bravery in characters who surrender to fear. The mouse in "The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl" must decide between brave action and prudent retreat. The owl's hesitation — waiting so long that opportunity passes — represents the failure of courage in the other direction. Bravery in these tales occupies a middle ground between foolhardy recklessness and paralyzing caution.
