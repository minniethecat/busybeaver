[068-001]All this time the Raja was feeling very unhappy, for he thought he had

[068-002]himself caused the death of the one man he could trust. He was too

[068-003]proud to let anybody know that he missed [[person-dhairya-sila|Dhairya-Sila]], and was longing

[068-004]to send for him from the tower before it was too late. What then was

[068-005]his relief and surprise when a message was brought to him that the

[068-006]vizier was at the door of the palace and begged for an interview.

[068-007]“Bring him in at once,” cried [[person-surya-pratap|Surya Pratap]]. And the next moment

[068-008]Dhairya-Sila stood before his master, his hands folded on his breast

[068-009]and his head bent in token of his submission. The attendants looked on,

[068-010]eager to know how he had got down from the tower, some of them anything

[068-011]but [[concept-happiness|glad]] to see him back. The Raja took care not to show how delighted

[068-012]he was to see him, and pretending to be angry, he said:

[068-013]“How dare you come into my presence, and which of my subjects has

[068-014]ventured to help you to escape the death on the tower you so richly

[068-015]deserved?”

[068-016]“None of your subjects, great and just and glorious ruler,” replied

[068-017]Dhairya-Sila, “but the God who created us both, making you my master

[068-018]and me your humble servant. It was that God,” he went on, “who saved

[068-019]me, knowing that I was indeed guiltless of any crime against you. I had

[068-020]not been long on the tower when help came to me in the form of a great

[068-021]and noble eagle, which appeared above me, hovering with outspread

[068-022]wings, as if about to swoop down upon me and tear me limb from limb. I

[068-023]trembled greatly, but I need have had no fear; for instead of harming

[068-024]me, the bird suddenly lifted me up in its talons and, flying rapidly

[068-025]through the air, landed me upon the balcony of my home and disappeared.

[068-026]Great indeed was the joy of my wife at my rescue from what seemed to be

[068-027]certain death; but I tore myself away from her embraces, to come and

[068-028]tell my lord how heaven had interfered to prove my innocence.”

[068-029]Fully believing that a miracle had taken place, Surya Pratap asked no

[068-030]more questions, but at once restored Dhairya-Sila to his old place as

[068-031]vizier, taking care not again to ill-treat the man he now believed to

[068-032]be under the special care of God. Though he certainly did not deserve

[068-033]it, the vizier prospered greatly all the rest of his life and as time

[068-034]went on he became the real ruler of the kingdom, for the Raja depended

[068-035]on his advice in everything. He grew richer and richer, but he was

[068-036]never really happy again, remembering the lie he had told to the master

[068-037]to whom he owed so much. [[person-buddhi-mati|Buddhi-Mati]] could never understand why he made

[068-038]up the story about the eagle, and constantly urged him to tell the

[068-039]truth. She thought it was really far more wonderful that a little

[068-040][[object-beetle-thread|beetle]] should have been the means of rescuing him, than that a strong

[068-041]bird should have done so; and she wanted everyone to know what a very

[068-042][[concept-cleverness|clever]] husband she had. She kept her promise never to tell anyone what

[068-043]really happened, but the secret came out for all that. By the time it

[068-044]was known, however, Dhairya-Sila was so powerful that no one could harm

[068-045]him, and when he died his son took his place as vizier.
