## Summary

Dhairya-Sila -- whose name means "the Patient One" -- serves as vizier to
the Raja, advising the king with rare wisdom and loyalty (062-015).
But the Raja's court is a den of intrigue, and "many of the Raja's subjects
were jealous of Dhairya-Sila" (062-022). His enemies conspire to
turn the king against him, and the Raja, in a fit of rage, orders the faithful
vizier thrown into a high prison tower and condemned to death
(062-035).

Dhairya-Sila's response is extraordinary: he "kept quite calm"
(062-040) and accepts his fate with dignity, walking "to the tower
as if he were" going to a celebration (063-005). From his
prison cell, he devises one of the most ingenious rescue plans ever recounted:
using a beetle, a dab of honey, and a long silken thread. He asks his wife to
fetch these seemingly trivial items, and by patiently coordinating their use,
he escapes certain death -- proving that "if you only obey" the dictates of
patience and intelligence, no prison can hold you (063-022).

## Characters

- **Dhairya-Sila** — The protagonist ("the Patient One," 062-015),
  a vizier whose defining virtues are loyalty, calm, and ingenuity. His
  patience is not passivity but strategic composure in the face of death.
  He embodies the idea that intelligence and faithfulness are the truest forms
  of strength.
- **the Raja** — A ruler susceptible to flattery and intrigue. His quickness
  to condemn an innocent man is a cautionary portrait of power unmoored
  from wisdom.
- **Dhairya-Sila's Wife** — An essential partner in the escape, she brings the
  requested items despite not understanding "what share the beetle and the
  honey were to take" in the plan (064-013).
- **the Jealous Courtiers** — The antagonists, whose envy of Dhairya-Sila's
  reputation (062-022) drives them to fabricate charges
  against him.
- **the Beetle** — The unlikely hero, a tiny insect whose instinctual
  behaviour (climbing toward honey) becomes the mechanism of salvation.

## Moral Themes

Patience is the story's crown jewel. Dhairya-Sila's name is not merely a label
but a thesis: the patience that keeps him calm while others rage is the same
patience that allows him to orchestrate a beetle's slow climb up a tower wall.
The story argues that the quiet virtues -- calm, perseverance, methodical
thought -- are more powerful than the passionate vices of envy and rash judgment.

A subsidiary theme is the dignity of loyalty. Dhairya-Sila never turns against
his king, even when the king has betrayed him. He simply proves his innocence
and leaves the moral reckoning to the truth itself.

## Setting

The tower prison is the story's central image: a vertical fortress where the
difference between death and freedom is measured in the slow crawl of a beetle
up a stone wall. The court below, where "many of the Raja's subjects were
jealous" (062-022), contrasts the corrupt world of human
ambition with the pure logic of nature that the beetle represents.
