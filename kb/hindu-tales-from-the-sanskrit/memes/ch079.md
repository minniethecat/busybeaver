[079-001]On hearing what [[person-vidya|Vidya]] said, the merchant at once told her to go and

[079-002]fetch her husband. But to her surprise [[person-hari-sarman|Hari-Sarman]] refused to go back

[079-003]with her. “You can tell the master what you like,” he said, angrily.

[079-004]“You all forgot me entirely yesterday; and now you want me to help you,

[079-005]you suddenly remember my existence. I am not going to be at your beck

[079-006]and call or anyone else’s.”

[079-007]Vidya entreated him to listen to reason, but it was no good. She had to

[079-008]go back and tell the merchant that he would not come. Instead of being

[079-009]made angry by this, however, the master surprised her by saying: “Your

[079-010]husband is right. I have treated him badly. Go and tell him I

[079-011]apologise, and will reward him well, if only he will come and help me.”

[079-012]Back again went Vidya and this time she was more successful. But though

[079-013]Hari-Sarman said he would go back with her, he was very sulky and would

[079-014]not answer any of her questions. She could not understand him, and

[079-015]wished she had not left him to himself for so long. He behaved very

[079-016]strangely too when the master, who received him very kindly, asked him

[079-017]if he could tell him where the mare was. “I know,” he said, “what a

[079-018][[concept-wisdom|wise]] and [[concept-cleverness|clever]] man you are.”

[079-019]“It didn’t seem much like it yesterday,” grumbled Hari-Sarman. “Nobody

[079-020]took any notice of me then, but now you want something of me, you find

[079-021]out that I am wise and clever. I am just the same person that I was

[079-022]yesterday.”

[079-023]“I know, I know,” said the merchant, “and I apologise for my neglect;

[079-024]but when a man’s daughter is going to be married, it’s no wonder some

[079-025]one gets neglected.”
