# City

## Description

Cities in the Hindu Tales are centers of wealth, power, and social drama. The most vividly drawn is Vardhamana, described as a place of prosperity and intrigue. The narrator situates key events *"In the city of Vardhamana in India"* [049-001], establishing it as a named urban anchor. Cities are characterized by their architecture — a *"beautiful city with towers and pinnacles"* [043-005] — and their role as destinations: *"ten paths led to a very great city"* [052-019], marking it as a nodal point in the geographic imagination of the tales.

A traveler arriving at a city gate might ask, *"tell me what is the name of this city?"* [052-029], underscoring that each city has a distinct identity. Cities are also sites of danger: outside the walls lies *"the field outside the city"* [056-014] where the guards patrol, and within, a person might be forbidden to *"remain in the city"* [082-063] as a form of exile.

The idea of founding a new city is presented as a mark of royal ambition: *"Would it not be a good thing to build a new city in this lovely place?"* [048-020], linking urban development to kingship. Cities also serve as destinations for those seeking fortune — *"some big city where we can pick up a living"* [076-014] — reflecting their economic draw.

## Narrative Role

Cities in the Hindu Tales function as stages for pivotal narrative events: royal weddings, public judgments, banishments, and homecomings. The named city Vardhamana anchors the geography, while unnamed "great cities" serve as generic urban destinations. The city is both sanctuary and threat — a place of opportunity for the ambitious and a place of exile for the disgraced. Its walls separate the ordered urban world from the wild forests beyond, mirroring the tales' broader tension between civilization and nature.
