[015-001]This is the story of four creatures, none of whom loved each other, who

[015-002]lived in the same banyan tree in a [[place-forest|forest]] in [[place-india|India]]. Banyan trees are

[015-003]very beautiful and very useful, and get their name from the fact that

[015-004]“banians,” as merchants are called in India, often gather together in

[015-005]their shade to sell their goods. Banyan trees grow to a very great

[015-006]height, spreading their branches out so widely that many people can

[015-007]stand beneath them. From those branches roots spring forth, which, when

[015-008]they reach the ground, pierce it, and look like, columns holding up a

[015-009]roof. If you have never seen a banyan tree, you can easily find a

[015-010]picture of one in some dictionary; and when you have done so, you will

[015-011]understand that a great many creatures can live in one without seeing

[015-012]much of each other.

[015-013]In an especially fine banyan tree, outside the walls of a [[place-city|town]] called

[015-014]Vidisa, a cat, an owl, a lizard and a mouse, had all taken up their

[015-015]abode. The cat lived in a big hole in the trunk some little distance

[015-016]from the ground, where she could sleep very cosily, curled up out of

[015-017]sight with her head resting on her forepaws, feeling perfectly safe

[015-018]from harm; for no other creature, she thought, could possibly discover

[015-019]her hiding-place. The owl roosted in a mass of foliage at the top of

[015-020]the tree, near the nest in which his wife had brought up their

[015-021]children, before those children flew away to seek mates for themselves.

[015-022]He too felt pretty secure as long as he remained up there; but he had

[015-023]seen the cat prowling about below him more than once, and was very sure

[015-024]that, if she should happen to catch sight of him when he was off his

[015-025]guard seeking his prey and obliged to give all his attention to what he

[015-026]was doing, she might spring out upon him and kill him. Cats do not

[015-027]generally attack such big [[animal-bird|birds]] as owls, but they will sometimes kill a

[015-028]mother sitting in her nest, as well as the little ones, if the father

[015-029]is too far off to protect them.

[015-030]The lizard loved to lie and bask in the sunshine, catching the flies on

[015-031]which he lived, lying so still that they did not notice him, and

[015-032]darting out his long tongue suddenly to suck them into his mouth. Yet

[015-033]he hid from [[animal-owl|the owl]] and the cat, because he knew full well that, tough

[015-034]though he was, they would gobble him up if they happened to be hungry.

[015-035]He made his home amongst the roots on the south side of the tree where

[015-036]it was hottest, but [[animal-mouse|the mouse]] had his hole on the other side amongst

[015-037]damp moss and dead leaves. The mouse was in constant fear of the cat

[015-038]and the owl. He knew that both of them could see in the dark, and he

[015-039]would have no chance of escape if they once caught sight of him.

[015-040]1. Which of these four creatures do you think was most to be pitied?

[015-041]2. Do you think that animals ever hate or love each as human creatures

[015-042]do?
