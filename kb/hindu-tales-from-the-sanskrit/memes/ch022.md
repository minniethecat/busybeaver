[022-001]As time went on, the Brahman became very well known in [[place-sravasti|Sravasti]]. His

[022-002]fame indeed spread far beyond the [[place-city|town]], and people came from far away

[022-003]to consult him about all sorts of things, and he gave them good advice,

[022-004]for he was a very [[concept-wisdom|wise]] man. Those who wanted him to tell them what to

[022-005]do paid him for his advice, and as some of them had plenty of money and

[022-006]were [[concept-happiness|glad]] to help him, he soon became quite rich. He might have done a

[022-007]great deal of good with all this money by helping the poor and

[022-008]suffering, but unfortunately he never thought of doing so. Instead of

[022-009]that, he got to love the money for its own sake. At night, when all

[022-010]those who had come to see him had gone to rest, and there was no fear

[022-011]of his being found out, he used to steal away into the [[place-forest|forest]], and

[022-012]there he dug a deep hole at the root of a great tree, to which he took

[022-013]all his money and jewels.

[022-014]In [[place-india|India]] everybody has a siesta, that is to say, a sleep in the middle

[022-015]of the day, because the heat is so great it is difficult to keep well

[022-016]and strong without this extra rest. So, although it is quite light at

[022-017]the time, the streets are deserted, except for the dogs who prowl

[022-018]about, trying to find something to eat. Now the Brahman loved his money

[022-019]and other treasures so much, that he used very often to do without this

[022-020]siesta and go to the forest to enjoy the pleasure of looking at them.

[022-021]When he got to the tree, he would bend down, clear away the earth and

[022-022]leaves with which he had hidden his secret hole, take out the money and

[022-023]let it slip through his fingers, and hold up the jewels to the light,

[022-024]to watch how they gleamed and glistened. He was never so happy as when

[022-025]he was alone with his riches, and it was all he could do to tear

[022-026]himself away from them when the time came to go back to his shady

[022-027]corner. In fact he was becoming a [[concept-selfishness|selfish]] miser instead of the holy man

[022-028]the people of Sravasti thought he was. By the time the siesta was over

[022-029]he was always back again in his place beneath the tree, holding out his

[022-030]bowl and looking as poor and thin as ever, so that nobody had the least

[022-031]idea of the truth.

[022-032]3. Why was it wrong for the Brahman to hide away his money and jewels?

[022-033]4. Can anyone be a miser about other things as well as money and

[022-034]jewels? If so, what other things?
