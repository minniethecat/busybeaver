[010-001]Even when they were all back again in the clearing the fairies did not

[010-002]quite give up hope of keeping their pitcher. This time they gave other

[010-003]reasons why [[person-subha-datta|Subha Datta]] should not have it. “It will break very

[010-004]easily,” they told him, “and then it will be no good to you or any one

[010-005]else. But if you take some of the money, you can buy anything you like

[010-006]with it. If you take some of the jewels you can sell them for lots of

[010-007]money.”

[010-008]“No! no! no!” cried the woodcutter. “The pitcher! [[object-magic-pitcher|the pitcher]]! I will

[010-009]have the pitcher!”

[010-010]“Very well then, take, the pitcher,” they sadly answered, “and never

[010-011]let us see your face again!”

[010-012]So Subha Datta took the pitcher, carrying it very, very carefully, lest

[010-013]he should drop it and break it before he got home. He did not think at

[010-014]all of what a [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] thing it was to take it away from the fairies, and

[010-015]leave them either to starve or to seek for food for themselves. The

[010-016]poor fairies watched him till he was out of sight, and then they began

[010-017]to weep and wring their hands. “He might at least have waited whilst we

[010-018]got some food out for a few days,” one of them said. “He was too

[010-019][[concept-selfishness|selfish]] to think of that,” said another. “Come, let us forget all about

[010-020]him and go and look for some fruit.”

[010-021]So they all left off crying and went away hand in hand. Fairies do not

[010-022]want very much to eat. They can live on fruit and dew, and they never

[010-023]let anything make them sad for long at a time. They go out of this

[010-024]story now, but you need not be unhappy about them, because you may be

[010-025]very sure that they got no real harm from their generosity to Subha

[010-026]Datta in letting him take the pitcher.
