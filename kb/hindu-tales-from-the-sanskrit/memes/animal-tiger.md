## Description

The tiger is mentioned in the Hindu Tales as one of the most dangerous predators of the forest. It appears only in passing — as a creature that other animals rightly fear — rather than as a character with a speaking role. In "The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl," the cat, stranded outside at night, trembles at the thought that "a mother tiger perhaps would snatch her, and take her to her hungry cubs, hidden away in the deep forest."[^1]

In "A Crow and His Three Friends," the tortoise Mandharaka boasts of his natural armour, declaring that "the claws even of a tiger, a wild cat or an eagle, could not penetrate it."[^2] The tiger thus serves as a benchmark of lethal force against which other creatures measure their safety.

## Appearances

The tiger is invoked in two separate stories as a symbol of mortal danger:

- **The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl** — When the cat is trapped outside the banyan tree at night, she imagines being seized by a mother tiger and fed to her cubs.[^1]
- **A Crow and His Three Friends** — The tortoise, explaining his confidence in his shell, cites the tiger's claws as an example of the harm from which his armour protects him.[^2]

## Narrative Function

The tiger functions as an emblem of the forest's danger. It never appears directly in any tale — it exists only in the imaginations of the smaller creatures, a spectre of violence that gives weight to their fears and their boasts alike. The tiger's narrative role is to define the stakes of survival: for the cat, it represents the deadly consequences of being caught outside at night; for the tortoise, it provides a measure by which his impenetrable shell proves its worth.
