## Role in the Stories

Sringa-Bhuja is the youngest son of King Vira-Bhuja and his favorite child (049-005). He is the central figure of "The Jewelled Arrow," a story in which his father's preference for him creates jealousy among his half-brothers and their mothers, leading to a plot that tests Sringa-Bhuja's courage and character. Sringa-Bhuja, the son of Guna-Vara, resembled his mother in her beauty (049-010). The story follows his journey after being sent away from the palace through the scheming of those who resented the king's love for him.

## Key Actions

The pivotal moment in Sringa-Bhuja's story comes when a crane steals the jewelled arrow during a hunting expedition with his brothers. When none of his elder brothers could recover it, their father demanded: "Where is your youngest brother Sringa-Bhuja? He alone will be able to retrieve the arrow" (051-047). Sringa-Bhuja then came forward, and as the sun flashed upon the jewels, he stepped up to attempt the recovery (051-050).

Sringa-Bhuja sped along after the crane, determined to retrieve what was lost (052-001). His brothers, who had all kept the secret of how Sringa-Bhuja had got the jewelled arrow, watched as he alone succeeded where they had failed (052-008). Sringa-Bhuja travelled on and on in the track of the drops of the magic staff, his quest taking him far from the known world (052-017).

## Relationships

Sringa-Bhuja's relationship with his father, King Vira-Bhuja, is the emotional anchor of the story. The king's obvious preference for his youngest son created a dangerous dynamic at court. The king's other wives, all of whom hated Sringa-Bhuja, conspired to remove him from the line of succession, telling the other sons how much better their father loved Sringa-Bhuja than any of them (051-004). His mother Guna-Vara, desperate to see Sringa-Bhuja again after the plot separated them, simply bowed her head in sorrow when she appealed to Vira-Bhuja (049-046).

Sringa-Bhuja's half-brothers, influenced by their mothers, participated in the scheme to get rid of Sringa-Bhuja (051-008). When Sringa-Bhuja was about to be struck down by their carefully planned plot, fate intervened (051-027). Sringa-Bhuja stood aside, the jewelled arrow in his possession, and his brothers could only watch as their plot unraveled (051-032).

## Character Traits

Sringa-Bhuja embodies the qualities of patience, courage, and quiet determination. When his brothers and their mothers conspired against him, he did not retaliate or seek revenge. Instead, he faced each trial as it came. The chief qualities of Sringa-Bhuja are his resilience in adversity and his uncomplaining acceptance of hardship (051-064). His success in retrieving the jewelled arrow — a feat none of his brothers could accomplish — demonstrates that true worth cannot be obscured by the schemes of the envious.
