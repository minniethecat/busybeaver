# Dridha-Varman

## Role in the Stories

Dridha-Varman appears in the later chapters of the Hindu Tales as a king and seeker of truth. The narrator introduces him as "the king of the country, whose name was Dridha-Varman" (085-017). His role is that of the determined investigator — a ruler who acts on his convictions, pursues hidden truths, and ultimately plays a pivotal role in reuniting characters separated by tragedy. Unlike the passive kings who populate other tales, Dridha-Varman is an active agent of discovery and justice.

## Key Actions

Dridha-Varman's first significant appearance occurs when he arrives at the hermitage of Mana Kanaka. Upon seeing the young girl Kadali-Garbha, the text notes that he "was filled with wonder when he saw how Dridha-Varman looked at the girl" (086-002). His intense gaze is not predatory but investigative — he recognizes something in the girl's appearance that sparks a chain of realizations.

When he speaks with Kadali-Garbha, Dridha-Varman demonstrates keen perception: "Dridha-Varman at once guessed that she was someone of high birth" (089-023). His ability to read beyond surfaces — to see nobility in a girl raised in a forest hermitage — marks him as a man of unusual insight. Though he presses his questions, "Dridha-Varman said no more at the time" (089-044) when he sees the limits of what she can tell him, showing restraint and patience.

His most decisive action comes when he pursues the truth about Kadali-Garbha's parentage. He remembers a crucial detail: "Now Dridha-Varman remembered that Kadali-Garbha had been warned never to go near the pool" (091-008). This memory triggers his investigation. When she disappears, he searches for her relentlessly: "no trace of her could be found. Dridha-Varman was now quite sure that his worst fears were true" (091-015).

At the story's climax, Dridha-Varman demonstrates both righteous anger and the capacity for restraint. When the full truth of the abduction is revealed, he declares: "I need no proof; but lest others should follow this bad man's example, let him be punished" (092-039). His justice is measured — he seeks punishment not for personal vengeance but as a deterrent to others. The text notes that "Dridha-Varman was so grateful to his father" (092-052), indicating the filial piety that grounds his character.

## Relationships

Dridha-Varman's relationship with **Kadali-Garbha** evolves from investigator to protector. Initially drawn to her by the mystery of her origins, he becomes her champion, seeking to restore her to her rightful identity and position. His relationship with **Mana Kanaka** is one of mutual respect between men of different worlds — the king and the hermit, the active and contemplative lives meeting in shared purpose.

His relationship with his **father** provides the emotional anchor of his character. His gratitude and respect for his father shape his own sense of duty and justice, grounding his kingly authority in filial devotion.

## Character Traits

Dridha-Varman is characterized by the marriage of action and insight. Unlike kings who rule from a distance, he rides into the forest himself, questions witnesses directly, and pursues truth personally. His perception — his ability to see nobility and hidden identity — suggests a wisdom beyond ordinary rule. Yet he also demonstrates restraint: he knows when to stop pressing, when to let events unfold, and when to act decisively. His justice is firm but not cruel, motivated by duty rather than anger. He represents the ideal of the kshatriya king: a ruler whose strength is guided by wisdom and whose authority is exercised in service of truth and right.
