## Summary

Kadali-Garbha is born in a hermitage deep in the forest, where her father the
hermit raises her in solitude and wisdom. She "learnt a great deal through"
her father's teaching and through the trust of the animals who share her home
(084-015). Her childhood is idyllic and innocent -- she "never
even thought about other" people or the world beyond the forest
(085-001) -- until the day a young king, hunting nearby, discovers
the hermitage and sees her for the first time.

What begins as a love story quickly deepens into something more complex. When
Kadali-Garbha enters the king's court, she finds herself surrounded by jealousy
and intrigue. Courtiers who resent her sudden elevation plot against her,
whispering lies that test the king's trust. The hermit, hearing of his daughter's
suffering, is "at first very much enraged" (092-010), but the story's resolution
turns not on vengeance but on forgiveness. Kadali-Garbha must choose whether
to return hatred for hatred -- or to model the wisdom her father taught her
in the forest.

## Characters

- **Kadali-Garbha** — The protagonist, a hermit's daughter whose journey from
  forest innocence to court sophistication is the story's arc. Her name evokes
  the plantain tree (kadali), suggesting natural beauty and hidden depth. She
  embodies the ideal that true wisdom comes not from experience with the world's
  corruption but from maintaining purity in the face of it.
- **the Hermit** — Kadali-Garbha's father, a holy man of deep wisdom and fierce
  protectiveness. He teaches his daughter everything she knows about the natural
  world (084-015), but her encounter with the king forces him to
  reckon with human nature, which is less easily tamed.
- **the Young King** — The ruler who falls in love with Kadali-Garbha. His
  susceptibility to court gossip makes him a morally complex figure -- not a
  villain, but a man failing a test of character.
- **the Jealous Courtiers** — The antagonists, representing the corrupting
  influence of envy that Kadali-Garbha "would be" wise to fear
  (087-029).
- **the Forest Animals** — Not characters in the conventional sense but essential
  to Kadali-Garbha's identity; their trust in her is the measure of her
  character (084-024).

## Moral Themes

The story is a study in jealousy and its antidotes. The courtiers' envy of
Kadali-Garbha mirrors the jealousy that drives conflict in several other tales
in this collection -- but this story goes further by exploring the victim's
response. Kadali-Garbha does not become bitter or vengeful; she clings "closely
to" the values of her upbringing (087-001) and models a
counter-intuitive approach to injustice.

Forgiveness is the story's highest value. The hermit's transformation from rage
(092-010) to acceptance -- prompted by his own daughter's example --
is the narrative's moral climax. The story argues that forgiveness is not
weakness but the most demanding and transformative of human capacities.

Trust is the third thread. Kadali-Garbha must trust the king (who has doubted
her), the king must trust Kadali-Garbha (against his courtiers' whispers), and
the hermit must trust his daughter's judgment. The story suggests that trust,
once broken, can be rebuilt -- but only through the deliberate, difficult work
of forgiveness.

## Setting

The story's dual setting is its thematic engine. The hermitage is a world of
natural wisdom, where "all that live in it" coexist in harmonious trust
(084-015). The royal court is its opposite: a world of artifice,
ambition, and hidden motives. Kadali-Garbha's journey between these two worlds
is the story's central movement, and her ability to carry the forest's wisdom
into the court -- rather than being corrupted by the court -- is the measure
of her character.
