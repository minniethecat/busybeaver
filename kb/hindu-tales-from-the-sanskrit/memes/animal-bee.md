## Description

Bees appear indirectly in "The Beetle and the Silken Thread" through their honey, which is the final and essential item in the vizier Dhairya-Sila's escape plan. When imprisoned atop a high tower, the vizier instructs his wife Buddhi-Mati to bring "one drop of the purest bees' honey"[^1] along with a beetle, silk thread, cotton thread, twine, and rope.

The honey serves as bait: rubbed on the beetle's nose, it motivates the insect to climb the tower wall in pursuit of what it believes to be "the hive from which that honey came."[^2] The plan succeeds — the beetle, "fond of honey,"[^3] crawls upward carrying the silk thread, enabling the vizier to haul up progressively stronger cords and ultimately a rope for his descent.

## Appearances

The bees and their honey feature throughout the tower-escape narrative:

- **The Beetle and the Silken Thread** — The bees' honey is the crucial lure in the multi-stage escape devised by Dhairya-Sila. The wife selects a beetle she "knew to be fond of honey"[^3] from her garden, and the drop of honey on its nose drives the entire mechanism of escape.[^4]

## Narrative Function

The bee and its honey function as a critical plot device. Without the honey, the beetle would have no incentive to climb the sheer tower wall, and the entire escape would fail. The bees are never directly present as characters — they exist only through their product — yet their role is indispensable. The story also invites reflection on natural instinct: the beetle is said to be unlikely to "imagine it was on the way to a hive of bees when it began to creep up the tower,"[^5] highlighting how simple creatures can unknowingly perform heroic acts.
