[082-001]Without waiting a moment [[person-jihva|Jihva]] hurried away to fetch the money; but

[082-002]when she got back with it, the man on guard, who had heard everything

[082-003]that had passed between her and [[person-hari-sarman|Hari-Sarman]], would not let her in to

[082-004]the [[event-imprisonment|prison]] again till she gave him ten gold pieces. Thinking that

[082-005]Hari-Sarman really knew exactly how much money she had, Jihva was

[082-006]afraid he would be angry when he missed some of it; and again she let

[082-007]out the truth, which he might never have guessed. For she began at once

[082-008]to say, “I brought all I had, but the man at the door has taken ten

[082-009]pieces.” This did vex Hari-Sarman very much, and he told her he would

[082-010]let the king know what she had done, unless she fetched the [[event-theft|thief]] who

[082-011]had taken the money and jewels. “I cannot do that,” said Jihva, “for he

[082-012]is very far away. He lives with his brother, Indra Datta, in the [[place-forest|forest]]

[082-013]beyond the river, more than a day’s journey from here.” “I did but try

[082-014]you,” said the [[concept-cleverness|clever]] Hari-Sarman, who now knew who the thief was; “for

[082-015]I can see him where he is at this moment. Now go home and wait there

[082-016]till I send for you.”

[082-017]But Jihva, who loved the thief and did not want him to be punished,

[082-018]refused to go until Hari-Sarman promised that he would not tell the

[082-019]king who the man was or where he lived. “I would rather,” she said,

[082-020]“bear all the punishment than that he should suffer.” Even Hari-Sarman

[082-021]was touched at this, and fearing that if he kept Jihva longer, she

[082-022]would be found in the prison by messengers from the king, he promised

[082-023]that no harm should come to her or the thief, and let her go.

[082-024]Very soon after this, messengers came to take Hari-Sarman once more

[082-025]before the king; who received him very coldly and began at once to

[082-026]threaten him with a terrible punishment, if he did not say who the

[082-027]thief was, and where the gold and jewels were. Even now Hari-Sarman

[082-028]pretended to be unwilling to speak. But when he saw that the king would

[082-029]put up with no more delay, he said, “I will lead you to the spot where

[082-030]the treasure is buried, but the name of the thief, though I know it, I

[082-031]will never betray.” The king, who did not really care much who the

[082-032]thief was, so long as he got back his money, [[event-disappearance|lost]] not a moment, but

[082-033]ordered his attendants to get spades and follow him. Very soon

[082-034]Hari-Sarman brought them to the pomegranate tree. And there, sure

[082-035]enough, deep down in the ground, was all that had been lost.

[082-036]Nothing was now too good for Hari-Sarman: the king was greatly

[082-037]delighted, and heaped riches and honours upon him. But some of the [[concept-wisdom|wise]]

[082-038]men at the court suspected that he was really a [[concept-deceit|deceiver]], and set about

[082-039]trying to find out all they could about him. They sent for the man who

[082-040]had been on guard at the prison, and asked him many questions. He did

[082-041]not dare tell the truth, for he knew he would be terribly punished if

[082-042]he let out that Jihva had been allowed to see his [[place-prison|prisoner]]; but he

[082-043]hesitated so much that the wise men knew he was not speaking the truth.

[082-044]One of them, whom the king loved, and [[concept-trust|trusted]] very much, whose name was

[082-045]Deva-Jnanin, said to his master: “I do not like to see that man, about

[082-046]whom we really know nothing, treated as he is. He might easily have

[082-047]found out where the treasure was hidden without any special power. Will

[082-048]you not [[event-trial|test]] him in some other way in my presence and that of your

[082-049]chief advisers?”

[082-050]The king, who was always ready to listen to reason, agreed to this; and

[082-051]after a long consultation with Deva-Jnanin, he decided on a very clever

[082-052]puzzle with which to try Hari-Sarman. A live frog was put into a

[082-053]pitcher; the lid was shut down, and the man who pretended to know

[082-054]everything was brought into the great reception room, where all the

[082-055]wise men of the court were gathered together round [[object-throne|the throne]], on which

[082-056]sat the king in his [[object-robe|royal robes]]. Deva-Jnanin had been chosen by his

[082-057]master to speak for him; and coming forward, he pointed to the small

[082-058]pitcher on the ground, and said: “Great as are the honours already

[082-059]bestowed on you, they shall be increased if you can say at once what is

[082-060]in that pitcher.”
