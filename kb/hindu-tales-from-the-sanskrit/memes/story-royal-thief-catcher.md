## Summary

The king of a prosperous realm is plagued by thefts from the royal treasury.
Furious and desperate, he issues a command that an ailing merchant named
Matri-Datta be summoned to catch the thief -- despite the merchant being a
holy man who has renounced wealth and "was suffering" from a serious illness
(027-030). The doctor had already declared what medicine
Matri-Datta required (027-040), and dragging a sick man before the
throne seems the height of injustice.

Matri-Datta, "trembling with fear" (028-008), has no choice but
to obey. Through a series of clever stratagems, he navigates the king's
court, exposing the real culprits while protecting himself from the tyrant's
wrath. The story is a masterclass in using intelligence rather than force --
the merchant cannot fight the king's power, but he can outwit it. By the
tale's end, justice is served, the thieves are caught, and Matri-Datta's
reputation as honest and wise is secured, though the experience leaves scars.

## Characters

- **Matri-Datta** — The protagonist, a merchant-turned-holy-man who, despite being
  "very ill" (027-030), uses his wits to catch the real thief
  and prove his own innocence. He embodies quiet courage and intellectual
  resourcefulness.
- **the King** — A ruler whose rage blinds him to justice. His willingness to
  bully a sick man reveals how power can corrupt judgment.
- **the Doctor** — A physician who prescribes for Matri-Datta's illness
  (027-040), inadvertently revealing the merchant's genuine suffering.
- **the Servant** — Matri-Datta's own servant, whose actions force his master to
  confront uncomfortable truths (029-001).
- **the Real Thieves** — Those who actually stole the king's treasure, caught
  not by force but by the logic of a sick man's clever trap.

## Moral Themes

This story is fundamentally about the triumph of intelligence over brute force.
Matri-Datta cannot fight the king, nor can he flee -- he is too ill. His only
weapon is his mind, and by using it well, he not only saves himself but delivers
justice. The tale implicitly critiques the abuse of power: the king becomes "a
selfish miser" (022-027) rather than a righteous ruler, while
the supposedly powerless merchant becomes the agent of justice.

A secondary theme concerns the dignity of the weak. The story's suspense comes
from watching a sick man, who should be convalescing, forced into a deadly
scenario -- and watching him prevail. It is a tribute to the idea that
character and cleverness matter more than physical strength or social status.

## Setting

The royal court serves as the primary arena, a place of arbitrary power where
the king's word is law and the innocent suffer alongside the guilty. Matri-Datta's
humble home, where he lies ill, provides a stark contrast -- a place of
simplicity that the king's summons violently disrupts.
