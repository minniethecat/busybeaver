[004-001]When the fairies saw how astonished [[person-subha-datta|Subha Datta]] was at the way they

[004-002]laughed, it made them laugh still more, and they seized each other’s

[004-003]hands again and whirled round and round, laughing all the time.

[004-004]Poor Subha Datta, who was very tired and hungry, began to get unhappy

[004-005]and to wish he had gone straight home after all. He stooped down to

[004-006]pick up his axe, and was just about to turn away with it, when the

[004-007]fairies stopped their mad whirl and cried to him to stop. So he waited,

[004-008]and one of them said:

[004-009]“_We_ don’t have to bother about fetching this and fetching that. You

[004-010]see that big pitcher. Well, we get all our food and everything else we

[004-011]want out of it. We just have to wish as we put our hands in, and there

[004-012]it is. It’s a [[object-magic-pitcher|magic pitcher]]—the only one there is in the whole wide

[004-013]world. You get the food you would like to have first, and then we’ll

[004-014]tell you what _we_ want.”

[004-015]Subha Datta could hardly believe his ears when he heard that. Down he

[004-016]threw his axe, and hastened to put his hand in the pitcher, wishing for

[004-017]the food he was used to. He loved curried rice and milk, lentils, fruit

[004-018]and vegetables, and very soon he had a beautiful meal spread out for

[004-019]himself on the ground. Then the fairies called out, one after the

[004-020]other, what they wanted for food, things the woodcutter had never heard

[004-021]of or seen, which made him quite discontented with what he had chosen

[004-022]for himself.

[004-023]7. What would you have wished for if you had had a magic pitcher?

[004-024]8. Would it be a good thing, do you think, to be able to get food

[004-025]without working for it or paying for it?
