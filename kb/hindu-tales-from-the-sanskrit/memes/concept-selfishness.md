# Selfishness

## Definition

Selfishness in the Hindu Tales is the foundational vice from which other moral failings spring. It is portrayed not merely as a personality flaw but as a spiritually corrosive force that isolates the selfish person from family, community, and ultimately from happiness itself. The tales trace a consistent arc: a character becomes increasingly focused on their own desires, loses the regard of those around them, and either reforms through suffering or is narratively punished.

The opposite of selfishness is *unselfishness* — a quality celebrated in several tales as the highest form of moral beauty. Unselfish characters, particularly women like Buddhi-Mati and Kadali-Garbha, are rewarded with love, loyalty, and supernatural aid. The tales thus construct a moral universe in which selflessness is not merely virtuous but practically and spiritually efficacious.

## Exemplifying Stories

### Subha Datta's Descent into Selfishness

The very first story introduces selfishness as a warning. Subha Datta begins as a contented woodcutter but becomes gradually corrupted by the magic pitcher: *"becoming every day more selfish and bent on"* (008-002). His transformation is incremental — he does not wake up selfish but slides into it through the seduction of wish-fulfillment. His selfishness is diagnosed by those around him: *"selfish to think of that"* (010-019). The tale traces a complete moral arc from contentment through corruption to eventual loss and potential redemption.

### The Miserly Holy Man

"A Royal Thief-Catcher" presents selfishness in its religious guise — a man who was supposed to be a renunciant instead becomes a hoarder: *"he was becoming a selfish miser instead of the holy man"* (022-027). The tale is particularly damning because it contrasts what the man *should* have been (a holy man, devoted to spiritual practice and generosity) with what he became (a miser, devoted to accumulation). The selfishness here is a betrayal not just of other people but of sacred duty.

### The Selfish Uncles

"The Magic Shoes and Staff" provides a family drama of selfishness in which three uncles who raised Putraka reveal their true nature: *"the three selfish men, who were now quite old"* (037-014). Their selfishness is a long-established character trait — they are "now quite old," implying a lifetime of self-centered behavior. The tale shows how Putraka's own return forces them to confront their past selfishness: *"when he knew how selfish they had been"* (036-012).

### Unselfishness as Counterpoint

Against these negative examples, the tales set portraits of unselfishness. Kadali-Garbha in "The Hermit's Daughter" is described as *"unselfish that she always thought of others before herself"* (049-007), a quality that earns her the devotion of animals and humans alike. The tales explicitly moralize the contrast: *"reward for the unselfish way in which they had behaved"* (034-015). Selfishness leads to isolation and loss; unselfishness attracts love, loyalty, and supernatural favor. The discussion questions embedded in the text — *"Do you think it was selfish to think of that?"* — indicate that the tales were designed to cultivate the reader's capacity for moral self-examination.

## Cultural Context

The tales' treatment of selfishness resonates with the Hindu concept of *moha* (attachment, delusion) — the selfish person mistakes their own desires for the center of the universe and suffers as a consequence. The ideal of *nishkama karma* (selfless action) is implied in the portrait of unselfish characters who act for the good of others without calculation of personal benefit.
