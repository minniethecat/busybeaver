[033-001]Far, far away in a [[place-city|town]] of [[place-india|India]] called Chinchini, where in days long

[033-002]gone by the ancient gods in whom the people believed are said sometimes

[033-003]to have appeared to those who called upon them for help, there lived

[033-004]three brothers of noble birth, who had never known what it was to want

[033-005]for food, or clothes, or a house to live in. Each was married to a wife

[033-006]he loved, and for many years they were all as [[concept-happiness|happy]] as the day was

[033-007]long. Presently however a great misfortune in which they all shared

[033-008]befell their native country. There was no rain for many, many weeks;

[033-009]and this is a very serious thing in a hot country like India, because,

[033-010]when it does not rain for a long time, the ground becomes so parched

[033-011]and hard that nothing can grow in it. The sun is very much stronger in

[033-012]India than it is in England; and it sent forth its burning rays, drying

[033-013]up all the water in the tanks and changing what had been a beautiful

[033-014]country, covered with green crops good for food, into a dreary desert,

[033-015]where neither men nor animals could get anything to eat. The result of

[033-016]this was that there was a terrible famine, in which hundreds of people

[033-017]and animals died, little children being the first to suffer.

[033-018]Now the three brothers, who had none of them any children, got

[033-019]frightened at the state of things, and thought to themselves, “If we do

[033-020]not escape from this dreadful land, we shall die.” They said to each

[033-021]other: “Let us flee away from here, and go somewhere where we are sure

[033-022]of being able to get plenty to eat and drink. We will not take our

[033-023]wives with us; they would only make things worse for us; let us leave

[033-024]them to look after themselves.”
