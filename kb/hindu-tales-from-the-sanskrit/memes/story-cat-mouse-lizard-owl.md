## Summary

In a fine banyan tree outside the walls of Vidisa, four natural enemies share an
uneasy home: "a cat, an owl, a lizard and a mouse, had all taken up their abode"
in the tree's separate quarters (015-014). Each creature lives in
perpetual fear of at least one of the others: the mouse dreads the cat and the
owl, the lizard fears the cat, and the cat watches the mouse. Their fragile
coexistence is upended when a hunter arrives, threatening them all.

What follows is a delicate negotiation of trust. The wise little mouse
(018-001) becomes the unlikely hero, persuading the others to set aside their
predator-prey relationships for the sake of mutual survival. Through a shared
plan of action, they drive the hunter away and discover that cooperation can
thrive even among those whom nature has set against one another

## Characters

- **the Mouse** — The protagonist and the story's unlikely hero. Small and
  vulnerable, the mouse is also the linchpin of the alliance, able to move
  where the others cannot. He emerges as the most courageous of the four.
- **the Cat** — A predator who must overcome her deepest instincts to
  participate in the alliance. Her hunger for the mouse is never entirely
  forgotten.
- **the Lizard** — A sun-loving creature who shares the mouse's diurnal
  vulnerability and brings cautious pragmatism to the group.
- **the Owl** — Perceived as helpless, the owl is revealed to be a "wise little"
  strategist (018-001) whose wisdom is crucial to the
  plan's success.
- **the Hunter** — The external antagonist whose arrival forces the four
  natural enemies into their improbable alliance.

## Moral Themes

The central theme is that even the deepest enmity can be set aside in the face
of a common threat. The story asks whether trust can exist between those whom
nature has made enemies -- and answers that it can, at least temporarily, when
survival depends upon it. The owl's question -- "was the owl wise or foolish to
wait before he" acted (017-023) -- invites the reader to
consider patience and timing in moral decisions.

A second theme is the power of the small and weak. The mouse, whom none of the
others would consider a threat, becomes the only creature capable of action
(016-006). The story celebrates the underdog and the value of
courage regardless of size.

## Setting

The entire story unfolds within and beneath a single banyan tree outside
Vidisa, an ancient Indian city. The banyan, with its multiple trunks and vast
canopy, serves as a microcosm of society -- separate dwellings sharing a common
roof, much like neighbours in a village who must learn to coexist.
