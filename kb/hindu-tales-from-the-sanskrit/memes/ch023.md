[023-001]For many months the Brahman led this double life; until one day, when

[023-002]he went as usual to his hiding-place, he saw at once that some one had

[023-003]been there before him. Eagerly he knelt down, full of fear of exactly

[023-004]what had actually happened. All his care in concealing the hole had

[023-005]been wasted, for it was quite empty. The poor man could not at first

[023-006]believe his own eyes. He rubbed them hard, thinking that there was

[023-007]something the matter with them. Then he felt round and round the hole,

[023-008]hoping that after all he was mistaken; and when at last he was obliged

[023-009]to believe the terrible truth that there really was not a sign of his

[023-010]money and jewels, he became almost mad with misery. He began to run

[023-011]from tree to tree, peering into their roots, and when there was nothing

[023-012]to be seen, he rushed back again to his empty hole, to look into it

[023-013]once more. Then he wept and tore at his hair, stamped about and cried

[023-014]aloud to all the gods he believed in, making all kinds of promises, of

[023-015]what he would do if only they would give him back his treasures. No

[023-016]answer came, and he began to wonder who could have done such a terrible

[023-017]thing. It must, he felt sure, have been one of the people of [[place-sravasti|Sravasti]];

[023-018]and he now remembered he had noticed that a good many of them had

[023-019]looked into his bowl with longing eyes, when they saw the money and

[023-020]precious stones in it. “What horrible, wicked people they are,” he said

[023-021]to himself. “I hate them. I should like to hurt them as they have hurt

[023-022]me.” As he thought in this way he got more and more angry, until he

[023-023]became quite worn out with [[concept-generosity|giving]] way to his rage.
