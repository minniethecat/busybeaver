# Happiness

## Definition

Happiness in the Hindu Tales is the emotional dividend of moral living. Unlike concepts such as wisdom or obedience, which are cultivated through effort, happiness is portrayed as something that *results* from right action rather than something that can be directly pursued. The tales consistently show that characters who chase happiness directly — through wealth, power, or wish-fulfillment — become unhappy, while those who act virtuously find happiness arriving unbidden.

The tales embed a distinctive philosophy of happiness within their narrative structures. Happiness is communal rather than individual — the happy ending involves not just the protagonist's satisfaction but the restoration of proper relationships across families, kingdoms, and even species. Happiness shared is happiness magnified; happiness hoarded, like Subha Datta's magic pitcher, breeds discontent.

## Exemplifying Stories

### Happiness and the Magic Pitcher

The opening tale is fundamentally a study in the paradox of happiness. Subha Datta begins with genuine happiness: his family *"were all very happy together"* (001-002). The introduction of the magic pitcher — which should increase happiness by eliminating material want — instead creates dissatisfaction: the children *"began to spoil the happiness of the home"* (012-008). The tale's moral is clear: happiness is a function of contentment and family harmony, not of wish-fulfillment. The pitcher, far from being a gift, is a test — and Subha Datta fails it by wanting more than he needs.

### Desert and Happiness

The question of whether characters *deserve* their happiness is posed explicitly in the end-of-chapter review questions. For Putraka, the pedagogical question asks: *"Do you think Putraka deserved all the happiness which came to him?"* (048-043). Similarly, for Rupa-Sikha, the reader is asked: *"Do you think Rupa-Sikha deserved all the happiness that came to her?"* (061-050). These review questions reflect a pedagogical approach that invites readers to evaluate characters' moral standing — the tales' implicit theory of happiness is meritocratic: happiness should flow to the deserving, and the reader's task is to learn how to judge desert.

### The Happiness of Others

"The Hermit's Daughter" reaches its emotional climax in a father's benediction: *"This," he said, "is my dear child's happiness. I hope you will..."* (092-057). The magician-father, who began the tale as an obstacle, ends it as the guarantor of his daughter's joy. His pronouncement reframes happiness as a gift that the powerful can bestow upon the deserving — and as the ultimate measure of whether a parent has fulfilled their duty.

### Happiness Lost and Found

"The Magic Shoes and Staff" traces a happiness arc. Putraka forgets *"the dear old woman to whom he owed all his happiness"* (046-005) — a lapse that temporarily costs him his moral bearings. The tale implies that happiness has a source (in this case, the kindness of others) and that forgetting the source imperils the happiness itself. Gratitude is the emotional mechanism that preserves happiness; ingratitude, the mechanism that destroys it.

## Cultural Context

The tales' treatment of happiness resonates with the Hindu concept of *ananda* (bliss) — not mere pleasure but the deep satisfaction that arises from alignment with dharma. The happiness that the tales celebrate is not the temporary excitement of wish-fulfillment (Subha Datta's initial thrill with the magic pitcher) but the enduring contentment of characters who have fulfilled their duties, honored their relationships, and achieved harmony with their social and cosmic order.
