# Crow

## Description

The crow in the Hindu Tales is the central figure of "A Crow and His Three Friends" (ch069–ch075), a wise old bird who lives in a forest and watches over a community of animal companions. Unlike the trickster crows of some folk traditions, this crow is portrayed as sagacious, perceptive, and protective — qualities more typically associated with owls in Western fable but here given to the crow with deliberate purpose.

The crow's domain is established in the story's opening: he lives *"in a very comfortable, well-built nest"* (069-002) in a great tree, from which vantage point he observes the forest below. This elevated position is both literal and metaphorical — the crow sees what others miss and thinks before acting. His age is emphasized: he is a *"wise old crow"* (069-001), and his wisdom is presented as the fruit of long experience rather than innate cleverness. When a hunter enters the forest, the crow immediately suspects danger: *"That fellow is up to some mischief, I'll be bound," thought the crow* (069-008), demonstrating his characteristic wariness.

## Appearances

The crow appears exclusively in "A Crow and His Three Friends" (ch069–ch075), where he functions as the story's moral and strategic center. The narrative opens from his perspective: *"thought the crow"* (069-008) as he observes a hunter setting a trap. From his vantage point, *"the crow was watching"* (069-012) the events unfold below. His watchfulness is his defining trait — he is never the first to act but is always the first to see.

When the hunter captures a group of pigeons, *"the crow and the man behind the tree kept very quiet, watching them"* (069-019). After the pigeons are released by their king and the hunter retreats, *"the wise old crow was greatly interested in the pigeons"* (069-030). Days later, he flies down to meet the mouse and begins building the friendship network that will sustain the story's action.

## The Friendship Network

The crow is the architect of the multi-species alliance at the heart of the tale. He initiates the friendship with the mouse Hiranya, overcoming the mouse's initial distrust. When the mouse *"began, when he knew the crow could not get at him, to cry, 'You be off to your nest'"* (074-038), the crow is deeply offended: *"The feelings of the crow were very much hurt at this speech"* (074-039). Yet he persists, and his persistence pays off — the mouse eventually agrees to dine with him, and a bond is formed.

The crow subsequently introduces the mouse to the tortoise Mandharaka and the deer Laghu-Patinaka. When the deer is caught, *"the crow was the first to speak. 'Whatever you tell us, dear Laghu-Patinaka, we will do'"* (077-009). He flies *"up to the highest tree near and eagerly sought for"* (077-014) the trapped deer, locates him, and coordinates the rescue plan. The crow's role as coordinator is underscored when he tells the deer: *"I can do nothing for you myself," said the crow, "but I know who can"* (077-017) — he is the connector who makes collective action possible.

## Narrative Function

The crow serves as **protector** and **mentor** within the animal community. When a hunter traps a group of pigeons, the crow does not intervene directly but watches and learns: *"The crow and the man behind the tree..."* (069-025). His role is that of the strategic observer who intervenes at the decisive moment rather than rushing into danger. This distinguishes him from the more impulsive animal characters in the tales.

His narrative function extends beyond the immediate plot to embody the tale's pedagogical purpose. The crow's decisions — when to act, when to wait, whom to trust — become discussion questions for the reader. The crow is not merely a character but a model of prudential reasoning that the tales invite their audience to emulate.

In the rescue of the tortoise, the crow executes the plan's aerial component: *"perch on his head and bend over as if you were pecking out his eyes"* (080-014), instructed by the deer. This final sequence demonstrates the crow's willingness to subordinate his own judgment to the group's strategy — wisdom, in the tale's framework, includes knowing when to follow as well as when to lead.

## Personality

The crow is not a flat paragon of wisdom. He is *"fond of adventure and change"* (075-010), restless and curious. After befriending the mouse, he flies *"for several hours"* until *"the crow began to feel very tired"* (075-020, 075-028). His boldness can shade into rashness — he lands near the mouse without thinking of how a mouse might perceive a crow. He is also sensitive: when insulted by the mouse's initial rejection, his feelings are *"very much hurt"* (074-039). This vulnerability humanizes him and makes his wisdom feel earned rather than inherent.

The story's closing reunion shows the crow in his natural element: among friends, ready to tell *"their adventures,"* and *"the crow always had the most to say"* (078-003). He is, after all, the eyes of the group — the one who sees, and therefore the one who has stories.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Hindu tradition, crows (*kaka*) occupy a complex symbolic position. They are associated with ancestors (*pitrs*) and are fed during *shraddha* rituals, yet they are also seen as clever and somewhat opportunistic. The tale's choice of a crow as the wise leader — rather than the more conventionally wise owl — may be read as a deliberate revaluation, suggesting that wisdom is found in unexpected places and that the seemingly common may possess uncommon insight.

The friendship network the crow builds also echoes a broader theme in the Hindu Tales: that moral community transcends natural enmity. A crow befriending a mouse, a tortoise, and a deer is no less remarkable than a cat seeking shelter with a mouse in the banyan tree story. The crow embodies the possibility that watchfulness — his defining trait — leads not to suspicion but to solidarity.
