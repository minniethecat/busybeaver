# Pigeon

## Description

The pigeons in "A Crow and His Three Friends" are introduced in the story's opening scene as a collective entity whose behavior sets the narrative in motion. Unlike the individually characterized crow, mouse, or deer, the pigeons function as a group — their strength lies not in individual distinction but in coordinated action under competent leadership.

When a hunter casts his net, the pigeons are trapped but not helpless. At their leader's command, they execute a remarkable coordinated escape: *"all the pigeons, who were accustomed to obey their leader, did as they were bid; each little bird seized a separate thread of the net in his beak and up, up, up, they all flew"* (070-001). This collective action — each bird lifting a single thread so that together they lift the entire net — is a powerful metaphor for the strength that emerges from coordinated obedience to wise leadership.

## Appearances

The pigeons appear in the opening of "A Crow and His Three Friends" (ch069–ch070). Their escape from the hunter's net is the event that catches the crow's attention and sets the story's moral machinery in motion.

## Narrative Function

The pigeons serve as an **exemplary model** of collective action. Their escape demonstrates a principle that the rest of the story will elaborate: individual weakness can be overcome through coordinated effort guided by wisdom. The pigeons' reliance on their leader — *"accustomed to obey their leader"* — is presented as a virtue, not a failing. Their obedience is the mechanism through which their collective strength is activated.

The crow, watching from his tree, *learns* from the pigeons. Their example shapes his own approach to leadership within his community. The pigeons thus function as teachers despite never directly addressing the crow — their actions speak, and the wise observe.

## Interactions

The pigeons interact with their leader (who issues the command to lift the net) and with the hunter (whose trap they collectively defeat). Their relationship with the crow is one-sided — the crow watches them, and they are unaware of his observation. This dramatic irony adds depth to the scene: the pigeons act to save themselves, unaware that their example will shape the moral reasoning of a far more complex character.

## Symbolic Meaning

Across cultural traditions, pigeons symbolize peace, community, and homing instinct. The tale draws on the communal dimension of pigeon symbolism — these are not solitary birds but creatures whose very survival depends on their willingness to act as one.
