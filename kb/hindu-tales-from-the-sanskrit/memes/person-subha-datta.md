# Subha Datta

## Role in the Stories

Subha Datta is the protagonist of the opening story cycle (ch001–ch014), a poor woodcutter whose accidental discovery of [[concept-magic|a magic pitcher]] transforms his life and tests his character. He begins as a contented family man — his household is described as one where all *"were all very happy together"* (001-002) — but the introduction of magical wish-fulfillment gradually corrupts his contentment and reveals the selfishness lurking beneath his simple exterior.

His narrative arc follows the classic folktale pattern of the wish-granting object that proves to be more curse than blessing. Where a wiser or more disciplined character might use the magic pitcher to secure a modest improvement in circumstances and then set it aside, Subha Datta's desires expand to fill the pitcher's unlimited capacity. He becomes *"every day more selfish"* (008-002), and his growing dissatisfaction is noted by those around him: *"selfish to think of that"* (010-019). The tale tracks his moral decline with precision, showing how each fulfilled wish creates the appetite for the next.

## Key Actions

Subha Datta's defining action is his treatment of the magic pitcher. Initially, he uses it to provide for his family — a legitimate application of magical aid in the tale's moral framework. But he quickly moves beyond sustenance to luxury, demanding rare foods and wines. His most consequential decision is to conceal the pitcher's existence from his wife — a choice the tale frames as a turning point: *"Would Subha Datta have been wise if he had told his wife about..."* (012-056). This decision isolates him within his own family and sets the stage for his eventual downfall.

A related action is his neglect of his woodcutting trade. Once the pitcher provides unlimited food, Subha Datta no longer goes to cut wood (012-002), abandoning the work that gave his life structure and daily rhythm. This shift from active livelihood to passive consumption marks a turning point in his character arc — from provider to dependent on magical abundance.

## Relationships

Subha Datta's primary relationships are with his wife and children. The tale's opening emphasizes the harmony of the household — a harmony that the magic pitcher progressively destroys. A review question asks: *"Would Subha Datta have been wise if he had told his wife about the pitcher?"* (012-056) — inviting reflection on whether transparency within the family would have changed his trajectory. His decision to exclude her from knowledge of the pitcher is therefore a failure of [[concept-trust|trust]] and a missed opportunity for the accountability that family relationships provide.

His relationship with the fairies who possess the pitcher is that of a beneficiary who fails to honor the spirit of the gift. The fairies weep at losing the pitcher yet make no attempt to reclaim it, suggesting that magical abundance functions as a test of the recipient's character. Subha Datta fails the test, and his failure is instructive: the tale is designed to teach readers what not to do when fortune unexpectedly smiles.

## Moral Significance

Subha Datta's story illustrates the Hindu teaching that *moha* (attachment, delusion) is the root of suffering. His attachment to the pitcher's products — the food, the wine, the luxury — is not materially wrong (the pitcher's gifts are real, not illusory) but spiritually corrosive. By desiring more than he needs, he loses what he had: the contentment and family harmony of the tale's opening scene. His story is a parable of the Second Noble Truth in narrative form: suffering arises from craving, and the cessation of craving is the path to peace. In this, he stands as a cautionary counterpoint to characters like [[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]], whose relationship with magical power is defined by restraint and purpose rather than unlimited appetite.
