[080-001][[person-hari-sarman|Hari-Sarman]] now thought it was time to take a different tone. So he put

[080-002]his hand in his pocket, and brought out a map he had got ready whilst

[080-003]waiting to be sent for, as he had felt sure he would be. He spread it

[080-004]out before the merchant, and pointed to a dark spot in the midst of

[080-005]many lines crossing each other in a bewildering manner, which he

[080-006]explained were pathways through the [[place-forest|forest]]. “Under a tree, where that

[080-007]dark spot is, you will find the mare,” he said.

[080-008]Overjoyed at the good news, the merchant at once sent a [[concept-trust|trusted]] servant

[080-009]to [[event-trial|test]] the truth; and when the mare was brought back, nothing seemed

[080-010]too good for the man who had led to her recovery. At the wedding

[080-011]festivities Hari-Sarman was treated as an honoured guest, and no longer

[080-012]had he any need to complain of not having food enough. His wife of

[080-013]course thought he would forgive her now for having neglected him. But

[080-014]not a bit of it: he still sulked with her, and she could never feel

[080-015]quite sure what the truth was about the mare.

[080-016]All went well with Hari-Sarman for a long time. But presently something

[080-017]happened which seemed likely to get him into very great trouble. A

[080-018]quantity of gold and many valuable jewels disappeared in the palace of

[080-019]the king of the country; and when the [[event-theft|thief]] could not be discovered,

[080-020]some one told the king the story of the stolen mare, and how a man

[080-021]called Hari-Sarman, living in the house of a rich merchant in the chief

[080-022]city, had found her when everyone else had failed.

[080-023]“Fetch that man here at once,” ordered the king, and very soon

[080-024]Hari-Sarman was brought before him. “I hear you are so [[concept-wisdom|wise]], you can

[080-025]reveal all secrets,” said the king. “Now tell me immediately who has

[080-026]stolen the gold and jewels and where they are to be found.”

[080-027]Poor Hari-Sarman did not know what to say or do. “Give me till

[080-028]to-morrow,” he replied in a faltering voice; “I must have a little time

[080-029]to think.”

[080-030]“I will not give you a single hour,” answered the king. For seeing the

[080-031]man before him was frightened, he began to suspect he was a [[concept-deceit|deceiver]].

[080-032]“If you do not at once tell me where the gold and jewels are, I will

[080-033]have you flogged until you find your tongue.”

[080-034]Hearing this, Hari-Sarman, though more terrified than ever, saw that

[080-035]his only chance of gaining time to make up some story was to get the

[080-036]king to believe in him. So he drew himself up and answered: “The wisest

[080-037]magicians need to employ means to find out the truth. Give me

[080-038]twenty-four hours, and I will name the thieves.”

[080-039]“You are not much of a [[concept-magic|magician]] if you cannot find out such a simple

[080-040]thing as I ask of you,” said the king. And turning to the guards, he

[080-041]ordered them to take Hari-Sarman to [[event-imprisonment|prison]], and shut him up there

[080-042]without food or drink till he came to his senses. The man was dragged

[080-043]away, and very soon he found himself alone in a dark and gloomy room

[080-044]from which he saw no hope of escape.

[080-045]He was in despair and walked up and down, trying in vain to think of

[080-046]some way of escape. “I shall die here of starvation, unless my wife

[080-047]finds some means of setting me free,” he said. “I wish I had treated

[080-048]her better instead of being so sulky with her.” He tried the bars of

[080-049]the window, but they were very strong: he could not hope to move them.

[080-050]And he beat against the door, but no notice was taken of that.
