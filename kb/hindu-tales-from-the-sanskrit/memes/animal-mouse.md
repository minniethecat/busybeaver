# Mouse

## Description

The mouse appears in two major story cycles — "The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl" and "A Crow and His Three Friends" — and in both, despite his small size and vulnerable position in the food chain, he exercises significant moral agency. The mouse is consistently the character through whom the tales explore questions of [[concept-trust|trust]], courage, and the ethics of helping those who might harm you.

In the banyan tree story, the mouse shares the tree with a cat, an owl, and a lizard: *"a cat, an owl, a lizard and a mouse, had all taken up their"* (015-014) dwelling. He is *"in constant fear of the cat"* (015-037) and must navigate a world where nearly every other creature — including [[animal-cat|the cat]] and [[animal-owl|the owl]] — is a potential predator.

## Appearances

The mouse appears in "The Story of a Cat, a Mouse, a Lizard and an Owl" (ch015–ch020) as the pivotal moral decision-maker, and in "A Crow and His Three Friends" (ch069–ch075) as one of the crow's companions. In the first story, he is the center of the ethical drama; in the second, he is part of a community of equals alongside [[animal-deer|the deer]] and [[animal-tortoise|the tortoise]].

## Narrative Function

The mouse serves as the **moral protagonist** — the character whose decisions carry the tale's ethical weight. When the cat is trapped and helpless, it is the mouse who must decide whether to help: *"the mouse was the only one of the four..."* (016-006) capable of freeing her. His deliberation — and the discussion questions that accompany it — transform a simple animal fable into a sophisticated exercise in moral reasoning.

## Interactions

The mouse's relationship with the cat is the story's central tension. The cat is his predator, and yet when the cat is *"in her misery"* (019-039), the mouse must decide whether compassion transcends the predator-prey relationship. His choice — and the cat's subsequent behavior — provides the tale's moral resolution.

In "A Crow and His Three Friends," the mouse is one of the crow's three companions and participates in the collective strategies the crow devises. The discussion questions extend the mouse's moral arc: *"Would it have been wise or foolish for the mouse to agree to be..."* (071-034).

## Speaking Role

The mouse speaks, reasons, and makes decisions. His small size belies his moral stature — in the ethical universe of these tales, physical weakness is no barrier to moral strength. This theme resonates with the broader narrative pattern in the Hindu Tales where the humble and seemingly powerless — like the [[concept-magic|magically aided]] hero [[person-putraka|Putraka]] — prove to be the most consequential actors.
