# Vira-Bhuja

## Role in the Stories

Vira-Bhuja appears in "The Jewelled Arrow" as the king and father of prince Sringa-Bhuja. He is introduced at the very start of the story, described in terms of the customs of his time: "Vira-Bhuja, who, as was the custom in his family, had two sons by different wives" (049-002). His role is that of the loving father caught between his natural son and his adopted son, Guna-Vara. Unlike the jealous kings who appear in other tales, Vira-Bhuja is a fundamentally decent ruler whose tragic flaw is excessive love — a love that inadvertently creates the jealousy that drives his adopted son to destruction.

## Key Actions

Vira-Bhuja's most consequential action is his public affection for Guna-Vara, his adopted son. The narrator notes that "the very fact that Vira-Bhuja loved Guna-Vara so dearly made his other courtiers jealous" (049-024). The king's love is genuine but politically naive — he fails to recognize that favoritism, even when well-intentioned, can generate dangerous resentments.

When suspicions about Guna-Vara's intentions arise, Vira-Bhuja's response reveals the depth of his attachment. To his wife's "sorrow and surprise, Vira-Bhuja went on to tell her that he feared Guna-Vara was plotting against his own son" (049-037). The king's willingness to suspect his adopted son represents a painful internal conflict — his love for Guna-Vara warring with his protective instinct toward his natural heir. His wife begs "Vira-Bhuja to let her see Sringa-Bhuja before anything was done" (049-046), trying to stay the king's hand.

A dark thought then enters Vira-Bhuja's mind: "She is afraid to look me in the face" (049-054) — the king's suspicion spreads from son to wife, revealing how jealousy can poison all relationships. When his wife is led away, "Vira-Bhuja returned to his own palace and shut himself up alone" (050-004), isolated by his own suspicions.

Later, when evidence emerges that seems to clear Guna-Vara, "Vira-Bhuja simply could not believe this" (050-018). His skepticism here cuts both ways — it reflects both his desire to believe in his adopted son's innocence and his fear of being deceived. When the truth is finally established and Guna-Vara's treachery is confirmed, Vira-Bhuja "did send for them, and asked their pardon publicly" (052-007), showing that despite his flaws, he is capable of admitting error.

## Relationships

Vira-Bhuja's most defining relationships are with his two sons: **Sringa-Bhuja**, his natural heir, and **Guna-Vara**, his adopted son. The king's love for Guna-Vara — genuine but politically dangerous — creates the triangle that powers the story's opening tragedy. His relationship with his **wife** (the queen) is strained by the crisis; his suspicion of her shows how distrust, once planted, spreads like a disease.

## Character Traits

Vira-Bhuja is a study in the dangers of good intentions. He loves his adopted son, and this love is not wrong in itself — but its expression, in a court of competing interests, becomes destructive. He is trusting to a fault, slow to see evil in those he loves, and when he finally does see it, he is crushed. His willingness to apologize publicly for his errors reveals a humility rare among kings in these tales. Unlike Surya Pratap, Vira-Bhuja is not cruel — he is merely human, a father who loved unwisely and paid the price.
