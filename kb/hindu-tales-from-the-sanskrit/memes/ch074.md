[074-001]The deer was so touched by the kind way in which he had been received,

[074-002]that he agreed to stop with the three friends; and for some weeks after

[074-003]his arrival all went well. Each member of the party went his own way

[074-004]during the day-time, but all four met together in the evening, and took

[074-005]it in turns to tell their adventures. The crow always had the most to

[074-006]say, and was very useful to [[animal-deer|the deer]] in warning him of the presence of

[074-007]hunters in the [[place-forest|forest]]. One beautiful moonlight night the deer did not

[074-008]come back as usual, and the other three became very anxious about him.

[074-009]The crow flew up to the highest tree near and eagerly sought for some

[074-010]sign of his [[event-disappearance|lost]] friend, of whom he had grown very fond. Presently he

[074-011]noticed a dark mass by the river-side, just where the deer used to go

[074-012]down to drink every evening. “That must be he,” thought the crow; and

[074-013]very soon he was hovering above the deer, who had been caught in a net

[074-014]and was struggling in vain to get free.

[074-015]The poor deer was very [[concept-happiness|glad]] indeed to see the crow, and cried to him in

[074-016]a piteous voice: “Be quick, be quick, and help me, before the terrible

[074-017]hunters find me and kill me.”

[074-018]“I can do nothing for you myself,” said the crow, “but I know who can.

[074-019]Remember who saved [[animal-pigeon|the pigeons]]!” And away he flew to fetch little

[074-020][[animal-mouse|Hiranya]], who with [[animal-tortoise|the tortoise]] was anxiously awaiting his return. Very

[074-021]soon Laghupatin was back by the river-side with the little mouse in his

[074-022]beak; and it did not take long for Hiranya, who had been despised by

[074-023]the deer and the tortoise as a feeble little thing, to nibble through

[074-024]the cords and save the life of the animal a hundred times as big as

[074-025]himself.

[074-026]How happy the deer was when the [[concept-cruelty|cruel]] cords were loosed and he could

[074-027]stretch out his limbs again! He bounded up, but took great care not to

[074-028]crush the mouse, who had done him such a service. “Never, never,

[074-029]never,” he said, “shall I forget what you have done for me. Ask

[074-030]anything in my power, and I will do it.”

[074-031]“I want nothing,” said Hiranya, “except the joyful thought of having

[074-032]saved you.”

[074-033]By this time the tortoise had crept to the riverbank, and he too was

[074-034]glad that the deer had been saved. He praised the mouse, and declared

[074-035]that he would never again look down upon him. Then the four started to

[074-036]go back to their usual haunt in the forest; the deer, the crow, and the

[074-037]mouse soon arriving there quite safely, whilst the tortoise, who could

[074-038]only get along very slowly, lagged behind. Now came the time for him to

[074-039]find out that armour was not the only thing needed to save him from

[074-040]danger. He had not got very far from the riverbank before the cruel

[074-041]hunter who had set the net to catch the deer, came to see if he had

[074-042]succeeded. Great was his rage when he found the net lying on the

[074-043]ground, but not exactly where he had left it. He guessed at once that

[074-044]some animal had been caught in it and escaped after a long struggle. He

[074-045]looked carefully about and noticed that the cords had been bitten

[074-046]through here and there. So he suspected just what had happened, and

[074-047]began to search about for any creature who could have done the

[074-048]mischief.

[074-049]There was not a sign of the mouse, but the slow-moving tortoise was

[074-050]soon discovered, and pouncing down upon him, the hunter rolled him up

[074-051]in another net he had with him, and carried him off, “It’s not much of

[074-052]a prize,” said the hunter to himself, “but better than nothing. I’ll

[074-053]have my revenge on the wretched creature anyhow, as I have lost the

[074-054]prey I sought.”
