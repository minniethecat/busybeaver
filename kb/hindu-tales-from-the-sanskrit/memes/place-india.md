# India

## Description

India serves as the universal geographic frame for every tale in the collection. The opening words of the first story establish this immediately: *"Long ago there lived far away in India a woodcutter called Subha Datta"* [001-001]. Throughout the narratives, India is characterized by dense forests, city-states ruled by kings, riverside towns, and a society structured around caste and trade. The narrator occasionally draws contrasts with England, as when observing that *"nowhere in India is there so much rainy weather as there is in England"* [033-012], framing India as warmer and more temperate.

The India of these tales is a patchwork of named cities: Sravasti [021-001], Chinchini [033-001], Benares [024-020], Vardhamana [049-001], and Ikshumati [084-001]. Each city houses a king's palace and is surrounded by agricultural land and wilderness. Benares in particular is singled out as *"a very famous place of pilgrimage"* [024-020], indicating the religious significance embedded in the geography.

Daily life reflects a recognizably Indian rhythm: *"In India everybody has a siesta, that is to say, a good sleep"* [022-014] during the hottest hours. Merchants called *"seths"* gather in public spaces [015-004], and clothing signals rank — *"in India the rank to which men belong can be known by the clothes they wear"* [046-034]. These details accumulate into a coherent portrait of pre-modern Indian society.

## Narrative Role

India functions less as a character in its own right and more as the indispensable backdrop against which all action unfolds. The narrator treats India as the default world — it is never introduced or explained, only invoked through local color: banyan trees, siestas, seths, pilgrimage sites, and the caste-marked clothing that *"the people of India love and worship"* [048-014].

This taken-for-granted quality gives the tales their internal coherence. When a character walks from one city to another through a forest, the reader is expected to understand that this is normal Indian geography — no distance is specified because none is needed. The forest is always nearby; the palace is always at the city center; the river is always accessible. India provides the spatial grammar within which the narratives operate.
