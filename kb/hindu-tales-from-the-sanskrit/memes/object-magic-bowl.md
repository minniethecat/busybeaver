# Magic Bowl

## Description

The Magic Bowl is one of three magical objects obtained by Putraka, described alongside the staff and shoes as a trio of enchanted items: *"A bowl, a stick and a pair of shoes"* [041-022]. The bowl's defining property is made explicit: *"Whoever gets the bowl will find plenty of food in it"* [041-029] — it is a vessel of inexhaustible provision, ensuring its owner never goes hungry.

The bowl is compact enough to be carried in a pocket: Putraka carried the *"bowl in the pocket of his robes"* [047-040], suggesting it is a modest-sized vessel whose magic is independent of its physical dimensions. The hermit who originally owned the objects *"taking his staff and bowl with him"* [043-020] carried them as everyday travelling gear, and later *"taking with him his bowl and staff"* [045-002] — the bowl's portability is a recurring detail that emphasises how unassuming magical objects appear.

Putraka uses the bowl practically, relying on the *"magic bowl to supply himself with food"* [046-004] during his travels. The bowl is also invoked as one of the objects whose whereabouts are questioned: *"the wand, the shoes and the bowl"* [048-044] are all sought after, cementing the bowl's place in the trio.

## Magical Properties

The bowl's magic is straightforward: it produces food on demand, without apparent limit. Unlike the [[object-magic-pitcher]], which grants any wish for food and drink, the bowl is described specifically as a food source — its magic is narrower but equally reliable. It requires no incantation or ritual; possession alone is sufficient to access its power. The bowl is never described as running empty, suggesting the magic is perpetual.

## Story Role

The Magic Bowl is the most practical of Putraka's three magical objects. The staff grants invisibility and the shoes grant flight, but the bowl addresses the most basic human need — hunger. Its presence allows Putraka to undertake long journeys without concern for provisions, and its understated magic (compared to the spectacle of flight or invisibility) grounds the fantastical elements of the story in domestic reality. The bowl also serves as a narrative marker of the hermit's generosity: by giving away all three objects, the hermit equips Putraka not just with power but with sustenance.
