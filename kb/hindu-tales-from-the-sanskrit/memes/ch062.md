[062-001]The strange adventures related in the story of the [[animal-beetle|Beetle]] and the

[062-002]Silken Thread took place in the [[place-city|town]] of [[place-allahabad|Allahabad]], “the City of God,”

[062-003]so called because it is situated near the point of meeting of the two

[062-004]sacred rivers of [[place-india|India]], [[place-ganges|the Ganges]], which the Hindus lovingly call

[062-005]Mother Ganga because they believe its waters can wash away their sins,

[062-006]and the Jumna, which they consider scarcely less holy.

[062-007]The ruler of Allahabad was a very [[concept-selfishness|selfish]] and hot-tempered Raja named

[062-008][[person-surya-pratap|Surya Pratap]], signifying “Powerful as the Sun,” who expected everybody

[062-009]to [[concept-obedience|obey]] him without a moment’s delay, and was ready to punish in a very

[062-010][[concept-cruelty|cruel]] manner those who hesitated to do so. He would never listen to a

[062-011]word of explanation, or own that he had been mistaken, even when he

[062-012]knew full well that he was in the wrong. He had a mantri, that is to

[062-013]say, a chief vizier or officer, whom he greatly [[concept-trust|trusted]], and really

[062-014]seemed to be fond of, for he liked to have him always near him. The

[062-015]vizier was called [[person-dhairya-sila|Dhairya-Sila]], or “the Patient One,” because he never

[062-016][[event-disappearance|lost]] his temper, no matter what provocation he received. He had a

[062-017]beautiful house, much money and many jewels, carriages to drive about

[062-018]in, noble horses to ride and many servants to wait upon him, all given

[062-019]to him by his master. But what he loved best of all was his faithful

[062-020]wife, [[person-buddhi-mati|Buddhi-Mati]], or “the Sensible One,” whom he had chosen for

[062-021]himself, and who would have died for him.

[062-022]Many of the Raja’s subjects were [[concept-jealousy|jealous]] of Dhairya-Sila, and

[062-023]constantly brought accusations against him, of none of which his master

[062-024]took any notice, except to punish those who tried to set him against

[062-025]his favourite. It really seemed as if nothing would ever bring harm to

[062-026]Dhairya-Sila; but he often told his wife that such good [[concept-fate|fortune]] was not

[062-027]likely to last, and that she must be prepared for a change before long.

[062-028]It turned out that he was right. For one day Surya Pratap ordered him

[062-029]to do what he considered would be a shameful deed. He refused; telling

[062-030]his master that he was wrong to think of such a thing, and entreating

[062-031]him to give up his purpose. “All your life long,” he said, “you will

[062-032]wish you had listened to me; for your conscience will never let you

[062-033]rest!”

[062-034]On hearing these [[concept-bravery|brave]] words, Surya Pratap flew into a terrible rage,

[062-035]summoned his guards, and ordered them to take Dhairya-Sila outside the

[062-036]city to a very lofty tower, and leave him at the top of it, without

[062-037]shelter from the sun and with nothing to eat or drink. The guards were

[062-038]at first afraid to touch the vizier, remembering how others had been

[062-039]punished for only speaking against him. Seeing their unwillingness, the

[062-040]Raja got more and more angry; but Dhairya-Sila himself kept quite calm,

[062-041]and said to the soldiers:

[062-042]“I go with you gladly. It is for the master to command and for me to

[062-043]obey.”

[062-044]1. What is the best way to learn to keep calm in an emergency?

[062-045]2. Why does too much power have a bad influence on those who have it?
