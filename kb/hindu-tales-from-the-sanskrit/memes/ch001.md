[001-001]Long, long ago there lived far away in [[place-india|India]] a woodcutter called Subha

[001-002]Datta and his family, who were all very [[concept-happiness|happy]] together. The father went

[001-003]every day to the [[place-forest|forest]] near his home to get supplies of wood, which he

[001-004]sold to his neighbours, earning by that means quite enough to give his

[001-005]wife and children all that they needed. Sometimes he took his three

[001-006]boys with him, and now and then, as a special treat, his two little

[001-007]girls were allowed to trot along beside him. The boys longed to be

[001-008]allowed to chop wood for themselves, and their father told them that as

[001-009]soon as they were old enough he would give each of them a little axe of

[001-010]his own. The girls, he said, must be [[concept-contentment|content]] with breaking off small

[001-011]twigs from the branches he cut down, for he did not wish them to chop

[001-012]their own fingers off. This will show you what a kind father he was,

[001-013]and you will be very sorry for him when you hear about his troubles.

[001-014]All went well with [[person-subha-datta|Subha Datta]] for a long time. Each of the boys had

[001-015]his own little axe at last, and each of the girls had a little pair of

[001-016]scissors to cut off twigs; and very proud they all were when they

[001-017]brought some wood home to their mother to use in the house. One day,

[001-018]however, their father told them they could none of them come with him,

[001-019]for he meant to go a very long way into the forest, to see if he could

[001-020]find better wood there than nearer home. Vainly the boys entreated him

[001-021]to take them with him. “Not to-day,” he said, “you would be too tired

[001-022]to go all the way, and would lose yourselves coming back alone. You

[001-023]must help your mother to-day and play with your sisters.” They had to

[001-024]be content, for although Hindu children are as fond of asking questions

[001-025]as English boys and girls, they are very [[concept-obedience|obedient]] to their parents and

[001-026]do all they are told without making any fuss about it.

[001-027]Of course, they expected their father would come back the day he

[001-028]started for the depths of the forest, although they knew he would be

[001-029]late. What then was their surprise when darkness came and there was no

[001-030]sign of him! Again and again their mother went to the door to look for

[001-031]him, expecting every moment to see him coming along the beaten path

[001-032]which led to their door. Again and again she mistook the cry of some

[001-033]night-bird for his voice calling to her. She was obliged at last to go

[001-034]to bed with a heavy heart, fearing some wild beast had killed him and

[001-035]that she would never see him again.
