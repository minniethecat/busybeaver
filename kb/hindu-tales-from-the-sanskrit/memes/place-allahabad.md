## Description

Allahabad, whose name means "the City of God," is the setting for "The Beetle and the Silken Thread."[^1] It is a royal city, governed by a ruler with absolute authority over life and death. The Raja of Allahabad, Surya Pratap, is described as "a very selfish and hot-tempered Raja,"[^2] whose arbitrary exercise of power drives the story's central conflict: the imprisonment of the wise vizier Dhairya-Sila atop a high tower.

It is in Allahabad that Buddhi-Mati, the vizier's faithful wife, gathers the beetle, silk thread, and honey needed for her husband's ingenious escape. The city's tower — the instrument of the Raja's punishment — becomes the stage for one of the collection's most memorable feats of cleverness.

## Significance

Allahabad is one of the few specifically named cities in the Hindu Tales. Most stories unfold in unnamed forests, palaces, and towns; Allahabad's identification by name — and by its honorific title — signals its importance as a centre of royal power and justice, however imperfectly administered.
