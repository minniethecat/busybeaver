[075-001]When [[animal-tortoise|the tortoise]] in his turn did not come home, [[animal-deer|the deer]], the crow and

[075-002][[animal-mouse|the mouse]] were very much concerned. They talked the matter over

[075-003]together and decided that, however great the risk to themselves, they

[075-004]must go back and see what had become of their friend. This time the

[075-005]mouse travelled in one of the ears of the deer, from which he peeped

[075-006]forth with his bright eyes, hoping to see the tortoise toiling along in

[075-007]his usual solemn manner; whilst the crow, also on the watch, flew along

[075-008]beside them. Great was the surprise and terror of all three when, as

[075-009]they came out of the [[place-forest|forest]], they saw the hunter striding along towards

[075-010]them, with the tortoise in the net under his arm. Once more the little

[075-011]mouse showed his wisdom. Without a moment’s hesitation he said to the

[075-012]deer: “Throw yourself on the ground and pretend to be dead; and _you_,”

[075-013]he added to the crow, “perch on his head and bend over as if you were

[075-014]going to peck out his eyes.”

[075-015]Without any idea what Hiranya meant by these strange orders, but

[075-016]remembering how he had helped in other dangers, the two did as they

[075-017]were told; the poor deer feeling anything but [[concept-happiness|happy]] lying still where

[075-018]his enemy was sure to see him, and thereby proving what a noble

[075-019]creature he was. The hunter did, see him very soon, and thinking to

[075-020]himself, “After all I shall get that deer,” he let the tortoise fall,

[075-021]and came striding along as fast as he could.

[075-022]Up jumped the deer without waiting to see what became of the tortoise,

[075-023]and sped away like the wind. The hunter rushed after him, and the two

[075-024]were soon out of sight. The tortoise, whose armour had saved him from

[075-025]being hurt by his fall, was indeed pleased when he saw little Hiranya

[075-026]running towards him. “Be quick, be quick!” he cried, “and set me free.”

[075-027]Very soon the sharp teeth of the mouse had bitten through the meshes of

[075-028]the net, and before the hunter came back, after trying in vain to catch

[075-029]the deer, the tortoise was safely swimming across the river, leaving

[075-030]the net upon the ground, whilst the crow and the mouse were back in the

[075-031]shelter of the forest.

[075-032]“There’s some magic at work here,” said the hunter when, expecting to

[075-033]find the tortoise where he had left him, he discovered that his

[075-034][[place-prison|prisoner]] had escaped. “The stupid beast could not have got out alone,”

[075-035]he added, as he picked up the net and walked off with it. “But he

[075-036]wasn’t worth keeping anyhow.”

[075-037]That evening the four friends met once more, and talked over all they

[075-038]had gone through together. The deer and the tortoise were full of

[075-039]gratitude to the mouse, and could not say enough in his praise, but the

[075-040]crow was rather sulky, and remarked: “If it had not been for me,

[075-041]neither of you would ever have seen Hiranya. He was my friend before he

[075-042]was yours.”

[075-043]“You are right,” said the tortoise, “and you must also remember that it

[075-044]was my armour which saved me from being killed in that terrible fall.”

[075-045]“Your armour would not have been of much use to you, if the hunter had

[075-046]been allowed to carry you to his home,” said the deer. “In my opinion

[075-047]you and I both owe our lives entirely to Hiranya. He is small and weak,

[075-048]it is true, but he has better brains than any of the rest of us, and I

[075-049]for one admire him with all my heart. I am glad I [[concept-trust|trusted]] him and

[075-050]obeyed him, when he ordered me to pretend to be dead, for I had not the

[075-051]least idea how that could help the tortoise.”

[075-052]“Have it your own way,” croaked the crow, “but I keep my own opinion

[075-053]all the same. But for me you would never have known my dear little

[075-054]Hiranya.”

[075-055]In spite of this little dispute the four friends were soon as happy

[075-056]together as before the adventure of the tortoise. They once more agreed

[075-057]never to part and lived happily together for many years, as they had

[075-058]done ever since they first met.
