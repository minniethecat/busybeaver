# Dhairya-Sila

## Role in the Stories

Dhairya-Sila is the protagonist of "The Beetle and the Silken Thread." He serves as vizier to King Surya Pratap, and the narrator introduces him by explaining his name's significance: "the vizier was called Dhairya-Sila, or 'the Patient One,' because he never got angry" (062-015). His role is that of the unjustly persecuted wise man — a counselor whose very wisdom and composure become the source of his master's jealousy and the catalyst for his persecution. Throughout the tale, Dhairya-Sila endures attempts on his life with unshakeable calm, embodying the virtue of patience under trial.

## Key Actions

When the king's other subjects grow jealous of Dhairya-Sila's influence and spread rumors about him, Dhairya-Sila does not retaliate or defend himself. Instead, "he often told his wife that he feared he could never win against those who did not want him to live" (062-026). His prescience is confirmed when King Surya Pratap orders the jealous courtiers to take Dhairya-Sila to be executed. Yet even in the face of death, Dhairya-Sila remains composed: "holding himself very upright, Dhairya-Sila walked to the tower as if he were going to a feast, not to his death" (063-005).

His most significant action is his calm instruction to his executioners. When they hesitate, Dhairya-Sila tells them quietly: "if you only obey every order you are given, and do what you are told to do, you will never find yourself in trouble" (063-022). This patience earns him their sympathy, and they agree to spare him. Later, through the help of his devoted wife Buddhi-Mati and a beetle with a silken thread, he escapes captivity.

When the king eventually regrets his actions and summons Dhairya-Sila back, the vizier returns without bitterness. Standing before his master, "his head held high, but with no anger in his eyes" (068-008), Dhairya-Sila speaks not of his own suffering but of divine providence: "the God who created us both has protected me" (068-017). The king, moved by this display of grace, immediately restores Dhairya-Sila "to his old place as chief vizier" (068-030).

## Relationships

Dhairya-Sila's most important relationship is with his wife **Buddhi-Mati** ("the Sensible One"), whose cleverness proves essential to his survival. While Dhairya-Sila provides the patience and moral compass, Buddhi-Mati provides the practical intelligence and quick action needed to effect his escape. He entrusts her with his secrets and follows her guidance, showing a partnership of mutual respect.

His relationship with **King Surya Pratap** defines the story's dramatic arc. The king, described as a ruler who "would never forgive Dhairya-Sila for being wiser than himself" (065-005), orders his vizier's death out of jealous rage. Yet Dhairya-Sila never responds with anger or vengeance. Instead, he forgives his master, returns to serve him, and becomes so powerful afterward that "no one could harm him again" (068-044).

## Character Traits

Dhairya-Sila's defining quality is his name itself — patience. He does not react to provocation, does not curse his fate, and does not seek revenge. His composure under unjust persecution makes him a model of stoic virtue. Yet his patience is not passive; it is an active force that transforms those around him, from the executioners who spare his life to the king who eventually repents. He also demonstrates humility, giving credit to God rather than to himself or his supporters. His character illustrates the moral that true strength lies not in power or retaliation but in enduring injustice with dignity and forgiveness.

## Moral Significance

Dhairya-Sila embodies the ideal of patient endurance in Hindu ethics — the notion that remaining calm and faithful under trial ultimately leads to redemption and justice. His story teaches that wisdom and composure can overcome even the deadly jealousy of kings. The fact that he returns to serve the king who tried to kill him speaks to a deeper theme: the duty of the wise to continue serving, even when that service is not appreciated or rewarded.
