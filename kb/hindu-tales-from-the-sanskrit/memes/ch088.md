[088-001]After the wedding was over, the king mounted his beautiful horse, and

[088-002]bending down, took his young wife up before him. Holding her close to

[088-003]him with his right arm, he held the reins in his left hand; and away

[088-004]they went, soon leaving all the attendants far behind them, the queen

[088-005]scattering the mustard seed as she had promised to do. When they

[088-006]arrived at the palace there were great rejoicings, and everybody seemed

[088-007]charmed with the queen, who was full of eager interest in all that she

[088-008]saw.

[088-009]For several weeks there was nobody in the wide world so [[concept-happiness|happy]] and

[088-010]light-hearted as the bride. The king spent many hours a day with her,

[088-011]and was never tired of listening to all she had to tell him about her

[088-012]life in the [[place-forest|forest]] with her father. Every day he gave her some fresh

[088-013]proof of his love, and he never refused to do anything she asked him to

[088-014]do. But presently a change came. Amongst the ladies of the court there

[088-015]was a beautiful woman, who had hoped to be queen herself, and hated

[088-016][[person-kadali-garbha|Kadali-Garbha]] so much that she made up her mind to get her into

[088-017]disgrace with the king. She asked first one powerful person and then

[088-018]another to help her; but everybody loved the queen, and the wicked

[088-019]woman began to be afraid that those she had told about her wish to harm

[088-020]her would warn the king. So she sought about for some one who did not

[088-021]know Kadali-Garbha, and suddenly remembered a [[concept-wisdom|wise]] woman named

[088-022]Asoka-Mala, who lived in a cave not far from the [[place-city|town]], to whom many

[088-023]people used to go for advice in their difficulties. She went to this

[088-024]woman one night, and told her a long story in which there was not one

[088-025]word of truth. The young queen, she said, did not really love the king;

[088-026]and with the help of her father, who was a [[concept-magic|magician]], she meant to

[088-027]poison him. How could this terrible thing be prevented, she asked; and

[088-028]she promised that if only Asoka-Mala would help to save [[person-dridha-varman|Dridha-Varman]],

[088-029]she would give her a great deal of money.

[088-030]Asoka-Mala guessed at once that the story was not true, and that it was

[088-031]only because the woman was [[concept-jealousy|jealous]] of the beautiful young queen that

[088-032]she wished to hurt her. But she loved money very much. Instead

[088-033]therefore of at once refusing to have anything to do with the matter,

[088-034]she said: “Bring me fifty gold pieces now, and promise me another fifty

[088-035]when the queen is sent away from the palace, and I will tell you what

[088-036]to do.”

[088-037]The wicked woman promised all this at once. The very next night she

[088-038]brought the first fifty pieces of gold to the cave, and Asoka-Mala told

[088-039]her that she must get the barber, who saw the king alone every day, to

[088-040]tell him he had found out a secret about the queen. “You must tell the

[088-041]barber all you have already told me. But be very careful to give some

[088-042]proof of your story. For if you do not do so, you will only have wasted

[088-043]the fifty gold pieces you have already given to me; and, more than

[088-044]that, you will be terribly punished for trying to hurt the queen, whom

[088-045]everybody loves.”
