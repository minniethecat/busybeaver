## Description

The bear is not present as an animal character or symbol in the Hindu Tales from the Sanskrit. Although bears feature prominently in broader Hindu mythology — most notably Jambavan, the king of bears in the *Ramayana* — none of the twelve tales in this collection mention a bear. Every occurrence of the word "bear" in the corpus is the English verb meaning to carry, endure, or give birth to.[^1]

This absence is notable given the collection's rich animal cast: the mouse, crow, tortoise, deer, cat, lizard, owl, crane, pigeon, beetle, and others all play speaking or plot-critical roles. The bear's exclusion may simply reflect the particular stories selected for this anthology rather than any cultural or symbolic omission.

## Appearances

The bear does not appear in any of the twelve tales. The word "bear" occurs in the corpus in the following verbal contexts:

- "could not bear the thought" — a character's emotional response
- "bear him on this new errand" — to carry or transport
- "bear all the punishment" — to endure or suffer

None of these instances refer to the animal.

[^1]: A full-text search of `corpus/raw/doc_final.md` confirms zero occurrences of "bear" or "bears" as a noun referring to the animal.
