# Mana Kanaka

## Role in the Stories

Mana Kanaka appears in the later chapters of the Hindu Tales as a holy hermit who has withdrawn from worldly life. The narrator introduces him simply: "there was a man named Mana Kanaka, who spent a great part of his life in a lonely forest, devoting himself to prayer and meditation" (084-003). His role evolves over the course of the narrative — he begins as a seemingly peripheral holy figure, but is gradually revealed to have a profound connection to the central characters and their hidden histories. He is the keeper of secrets, the wise elder whose knowledge bridges the world of the past and the present.

## Key Actions

Mana Kanaka's first significant interaction comes when a young girl — the lost princess Kadali-Garbha — is brought to his hermitage. When she arrives, frightened and displaced, "Mana Kanaka was with her, and she got over her fear" (085-024). His calming presence provides the first safety she has known since her abduction.

His wisdom is demonstrated when a stranger arrives seeking the girl. "Mana Kanaka knew at once that the man on the horse was Dridha-Varman" (086-001), recognizing the king before any introductions are made — a sign of his spiritual insight. When Dridha-Varman questions him about the girl's identity, Mana Kanaka reveals a crucial piece of information: "Her mother was indeed what you say; and her father is the King of the country to the north" (086-009).

His most tender action is his farewell to Kadali-Garbha. "Mana Kanaka stroked her hair, and said in his gentle voice that she must go with the stranger" (087-004). Though he is "sad to lose his beloved ward" (087-018), he does not keep her for himself — he recognizes that her destiny lies elsewhere and releases her with blessing.

In the story's resolution, Mana Kanaka reappears. "Great indeed was the joy of Mana Kanaka at the sight of his beloved former ward" (092-008), and it is at this reunion that his true identity and past are revealed. He tells the king "the whole sad story of his early life" (092-042), which explains his withdrawal from the world. The king offers him "a very high position there" (092-054), but "Mana Kanaka refused all these offers" (092-054), choosing to continue his life of contemplation despite the temptation of worldly honor. "The king was obliged to let Mana Kanaka go" (092-061), respecting the hermit's spiritual vocation.

## Relationships

Mana Kanaka's most significant relationship is with **Kadali-Garbha**, the lost princess whom he shelters and raises in his hermitage. Their bond is that of guardian and ward, but also of two people linked by a hidden family connection that is only revealed at the story's climax. His relationship with **Dridha-Varman** begins with wariness but develops into mutual respect, as the king comes to appreciate the hermit's wisdom and selflessness.

## Character Traits

Mana Kanaka embodies the ideal of the forest-dwelling sage — the vanaprastha who has renounced worldly life to pursue spiritual truth. His wisdom is not merely intellectual but intuitive; he knows things without being told. His gentleness, particularly with Kadali-Garbha, reveals a deep compassion beneath his ascetic exterior. Most importantly, he demonstrates the virtue of detachment: when offered wealth and position by a grateful king, he refuses without hesitation. His joy lies not in power or recognition but in the fulfillment of his spiritual duty and the happiness of those he loves.

## Moral Significance

Mana Kanaka represents the ideal of renunciation — the ability to love without clinging, to guide without controlling. His refusal of royal honors at the story's end illustrates the teaching that true fulfillment comes from inner peace, not external status. His hidden past — a sad story that drove him to the forest — suggests that wisdom is often born from suffering transformed through contemplation.
