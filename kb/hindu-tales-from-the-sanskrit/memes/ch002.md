[002-001]When [[person-subha-datta|Subha Datta]] started for the [[place-forest|forest]], he fully intended to come back

[002-002]the same evening; but as he was busy cutting down a tree, he suddenly

[002-003]had a feeling that he was no longer alone. He looked up, and there,

[002-004]quite close to him, in a [[place-clearing|little clearing]] where the trees had been cut

[002-005]down by some other woodcutter, he saw four beautiful young girls

[002-006]looking like fairies in their thin summer dresses and with their long

[002-007]hair flowing down their backs, dancing round and round, holding each

[002-008]other’s hands. Subha Datta was so astonished at the sight that he let

[002-009]his axe fall, and the noise startled the dancers, who all four stood

[002-010]still and stared at him.

[002-011]The woodcutter could not say a word, but just gazed and gazed at them,

[002-012]till one of them said to him: “Who are you, and what are you doing in

[002-013]the very depths of the forest where we have never before seen a man?”

[002-014]“I am only a poor woodcutter,” he replied, “come to get some wood to

[002-015]sell, so as to give my wife and children something to eat and some

[002-016]clothes to wear.”

[002-017]“That is a very stupid thing to do,” said one of the girls. “You can’t

[002-018]get much money that way. If you will only stop with us we will have

[002-019]your wife and children looked after for you much better than you can do

[002-020]it yourself.”

[002-021]3. What would you have said if you had been the woodcutter?

[002-022]4. Do you think the fairies really meant that they could do as they

[002-023]offered?
