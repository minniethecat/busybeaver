# Cruelty

## Definition

Cruelty in the Hindu Tales is consistently associated with the abuse of power. It is not random violence but the deliberate infliction of suffering by those who hold authority — kings, fathers, magicians — upon those who depend on them. The tales construct a clear moral geography in which cruelty is the defining sin of the powerful, just as selfishness is the defining sin of the ordinary person.

The tales' critique of cruelty is also implicitly a theory of good governance. A cruel king like Surya Pratap is not merely unpleasant but *illegitimate* — his cruelty voids the obedience his subjects would otherwise owe him. By contrast, kings who rule with mercy and wisdom, like the reformed rulers in several tales, earn the love of their people and the favor of the gods.

## Exemplifying Stories

### The Cruelty of Bad Kings

King Surya Pratap in "A Royal Thief-Catcher" embodies institutionalized cruelty. His name — "Powerful as the Sun" — already signals the fusion of absolute power with a burning, destructive energy: he was *"ready to punish in a very cruel manner those who hesitated to"* obey (062-010). His cruelty is structural — it is not a momentary lapse but a governing principle. The narrative condemns him through its plot: his cruelty generates the very deceptions and evasions he seeks to punish.

In "The Magic Shoes and Staff," the king's cruelty is directed at his own family. When Putraka falls in love with the princess Patala, the court observes *"it was very cruel of the king to punish his"* (046-020) daughter for loving a worthy suitor. The king's cruelty drives the lovers to flee — and their eventual return, transformed by magical aid, forces him to confront the folly of his harshness.

### The Cruelty of Magicians

Agni-Sikha in "The Jewelled Arrow" is described by his own daughter as *"her father, who was as cruel as"* (052-050). The magician's cruelty takes the form of impossible tasks and mortal threats directed at his daughter's suitor. Sringa-Bhuja suffers *"many wounds made by the cruel thorns"* (058-024) during his flight through the magician's enchanted forest. The magician's cruelty is linked to his isolation — he has no allies, only servants and victims, and his power is ultimately broken by forces of love and loyalty.

### Cruelty in the Animal World

The tales extend their meditation on cruelty to the animal kingdom. A discussion question challenges the reader: *"can you call it cruel for a cat or an owl to kill a mouse?"* (020-051). This question cuts to the heart of the concept — is predation cruelty, or is cruelty a distinctively human capacity to inflict suffering beyond what nature requires? The trapped deer is described with *"the cruel cords were loosed"* (074-026), and a hunter is warned: *"before the cruel..."* (074-040). These passages suggest that cruelty involves the intentional infliction of suffering beyond necessity — nature is harsh, but only humans, magicians, and tyrants are cruel.

### The Cruel Woman

"The Hermit's Daughter" presents the cruelest individual character — a woman whose jealousy drives her to orchestrate a young queen's suffering: *"Early the next morning the cruel woman went to see the barber"* (089-009). Her cruelty is portrayed as methodical and patient — she plans, she waits, she executes. The adjective "cruel" becomes almost a title, used as a narrative shorthand for her entire moral character. Her eventual exposure and defeat at the hands of the magician-father represents the triumph of protective love over destructive cruelty.

## Cultural Context

The tales' treatment of cruelty aligns with the Hindu concept of *ahimsa* (non-harm) and the ideal of the *rajarshi* — the king who is also a sage, ruling with compassion rather than force. The cruel king who expects *"everybody to obey him without a moment's delay"* is the antithesis of the dharmic ruler who governs through moral authority rather than fear.
