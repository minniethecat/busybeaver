[081-001]When it got quite dark in the [[event-imprisonment|prison]], [[person-hari-sarman|Hari-Sarman]] began to talk to

[081-002]himself aloud. “Oh,” he said, “I wish I had bitten my tongue out before

[081-003]I told that lie about the mare. It is all my [[concept-foolishness|foolish]] tongue which has

[081-004]got me into this trouble. Tongue! Tongue!” he went on, “it is all your

[081-005]fault.”

[081-006]Now a very strange thing happened. The money and jewels had been stolen

[081-007]by a man, who had been told where they were by a young servant girl in

[081-008]the palace whose name was [[person-jihva|Jihva]], which is the Sanskrit word for tongue;

[081-009]and this girl was in a great fright when she heard that a revealer of

[081-010]secrets had been taken before the king. “He will tell of my share in

[081-011]the matter,” she thought, “and I shall get into trouble,” It so

[081-012]happened that the guard at the prison door was fond of her, as well as

[081-013]the [[event-theft|thief]] who had stolen the money and jewels. So when all was quiet in

[081-014]the palace, Jihva slipped away to see if she could get that guard to

[081-015]let her see the [[place-prison|prisoner]]. “If I promise to give him part of the money,”

[081-016]she thought, “he will undertake not to betray me.”

[081-017]The guard was [[concept-happiness|glad]] enough when Jihva came to talk to him, and he let

[081-018]her listen at the key-hole to what Hari-Sarman was saying. Just imagine

[081-019]her astonishment when she heard him repeating her name again and again.

[081-020]“Jihva! Jihva! Thou,” he cried, “art the cause of this suffering. Why

[081-021]didst thou behave in such a foolish manner, just for the sake of the

[081-022]good things of this life? Never can I forgive thee, Jihva, thou wicked,

[081-023]wicked one!”

[081-024]“Oh! oh!” cried Jihva in an agony of terror, “he knows the truth; he

[081-025]knows that I helped the thief.” And she entreated the guard to let her

[081-026]into the prison that she might plead with Hari-Sarman not to tell the

[081-027]king what she had done. The man hesitated at first, but in the end she

[081-028]persuaded him to consent by promising him a large reward.

[081-029]When the key grated in the lock, Hari-Sarman stopped talking aloud,

[081-030]wondering whether what he had been saying had been overheard by the

[081-031]guard, and half hoping that his wife had got leave to come and see him.

[081-032]As the door opened and he saw a woman coming in by the light of a

[081-033]lantern held up by the guard, he cried, “[[person-vidya|Vidya]] my beloved!” But he soon

[081-034]realized that it was a stranger. He was indeed surprised and relieved,

[081-035]when Jihva suddenly threw herself at his feet and, clinging to his

[081-036]knees, began to weep and moan “Oh, most holy man,” she cried between

[081-037]her sobs, “who knowest the very secrets of the heart, I have come to

[081-038]confess that it was indeed I, Jihva, your humble servant, who aided the

[081-039]thief to take the jewels and the gold and to hide them beneath the big

[081-040]pomegranate tree behind the palace.”

[081-041]“Rise,” replied Hari-Sarman, overjoyed at hearing this. “You have told

[081-042]me nothing that I did not know, for no secret is hidden from me. What

[081-043]reward will you give me if I save you from the wrath of the king?”

[081-044]“I will give you all the money I have,” said Jihva; “and that is not a

[081-045]little.”

[081-046]“That also I knew,” said Hari-Sarman. “For you have good wages, and

[081-047]many a time you have stolen money that did not belong to you. Go now

[081-048]and fetch it all, and have no fear that I will betray you.”
