# Garden

## Description

Gardens in the Hindu Tales oscillate between two poles: the humble domestic plot and the magnificent palace enclosure. The domestic garden is a place of sustenance: a father is urged *"to work in their garden in which all their own food"* [084-019] was grown, framing the garden as the foundation of household self-sufficiency. In a quieter moment, a character is found *"in the garden of her home"* [064-015], where private life unfolds away from the pressures of court and quest.

At the opposite extreme stand the palace gardens, described with unmistakable admiration. A character marvels at *"the lovely gardens all round the palace"* [090-023], and another, denied permission to leave, declares *"I will be content with the gardens"* [090-028]. But even paradise has its limits: one dweller *"longed to go outside the palace gardens"* [090-037], and another ventures *"into the gardens, hoping that the fresh air"* [090-052] would bring relief — revealing that the garden, however beautiful, can become a gilded cage.

The garden is also a place of discovery. Buddhi-Mati *"led her husband to the garden where she had found the"* [067-011] evidence that would change their fate, making the garden a threshold between the ordinary and the extraordinary.

## Narrative Role

The garden serves as a liminal space between the wild forest and the civilized interior. In domestic settings, it represents honest labor and self-reliance; in palace settings, it represents beauty that can tip into confinement. The garden is also where secrets surface — Buddhi-Mati's discovery in the garden drives the plot forward, while Kadali-Garbha's longing to leave the palace gardens foreshadows the story's central conflict. Across the tales, the garden is never neutral: it is always a site of meaning, whether of sustenance, pleasure, discovery, or constraint.
