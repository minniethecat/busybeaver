[047-001]After hesitating a long time, the lady made up her mind that she would

[047-002]only put some mark in the turban of [[person-putraka|Putraka]], so that he could be known

[047-003]again, and let him escape that night at least. So she stole back to her

[047-004]room, fetched a tiny brooch, and fastened it in the folds of the

[047-005]turban, where the wearer was not likely to notice it himself. This

[047-006]done, she went back to listen at the door.

[047-007]It was nearly morning when Putraka woke up, very much surprised at

[047-008]finding himself lying on the couch, for he did not remember throwing

[047-009]himself down on it. Starting up, he woke [[person-patala|Patala]], who was terribly

[047-010]frightened, for she expected her ladies to come in any minute to help

[047-011]her to dress. She entreated Putraka to make himself invisible and fly

[047-012]away at once. He did so; and, as usual, wandered about until the time

[047-013]should come to go back to the palace. But he still felt too tired to

[047-014]fly, and instead walked about in the [[place-city|town]] belonging to Patala’s father.

[047-015]The lady who had been on guard had half a mind to tell her mistress

[047-016]that her secret was discovered. But before she could get a chance to do

[047-017]so, she was sent for by the king, who asked her if she had seen or

[047-018]heard anything during the night. She tried very hard to escape from

[047-019]betraying Patala; but she hesitated so much in her answers that the

[047-020]king guessed there was something she wanted to hide, and told her, if

[047-021]she did not reveal the whole truth, he would have her head shaved and

[047-022]send her to [[event-imprisonment|prison]]. So she told how she had found a handsome man,

[047-023]beautifully dressed, fast asleep in Patala’s room; but she did not

[047-024]believe her mistress knew anything about it, because she too was

[047-025]asleep.

[047-026]The king was of course in a terrible rage, and the lady was afraid he

[047-027]would order her to be punished; but he only went on questioning her

[047-028]angrily about what the man was like, so that he might be found and

[047-029]brought before him. Then the lady confessed that she had put the brooch

[047-030]in the turban, comforting herself with the thought that, when the king

[047-031]saw Putraka and knew that Patala loved him, he might perhaps relent and

[047-032]let them be married.

[047-033]When the king heard about the brooch, he was greatly pleased; and

[047-034]instead of ordering the lady to be punished, he told her that, when the

[047-035]man who had dared to approach his daughter was found, he would give her

[047-036]a great reward. He then sent forth hundreds of spies to hunt for the

[047-037]man with a brooch in his turban, and Putraka was very soon found,

[047-038]strolling quietly about in the [[place-market|market-place]]. He was so taken by

[047-039]surprise that, though he had his staff in his hand and his shoes and

[047-040]bowl in the pocket of his [[object-robe|robes]], he had no time to write his wishes

[047-041]with [[object-magic-staff|the staff]], or to put on [[object-magic-shoes|the shoes]], so he was obliged to submit to

[047-042]be dragged to the palace. He did all he could to persuade those who had

[047-043]found him to let him go, telling them he was a king and would reward

[047-044]them well. They only laughed at him and dragged him along with them to

[047-045]the palace, where he was at once taken before the king, who was sitting

[047-046]on his throne, surrounded by his court, in a great hall lined with

[047-047]soldiers. The big windows were wide open; and noticing this, Putraka

[047-048]did not feel at all afraid, for he knew he had only to slip on his

[047-049]shoes and fly out of one of the windows, if he could not persuade the

[047-050]king to let him marry Patala. So he stood quietly at the foot of the

[047-051]throne, and looked bravely into the face of his dear one’s father.

[047-052]This only made the king more angry, and he began calling Putraka all

[047-053]manner of names and asking him how he dared to enter the room of his

[047-054]daughter. Putraka answered quietly that he loved Patala and wished to

[047-055]marry her. He was himself a king, and would give her all she had been

[047-056]used to. But it was all no good, for it only made the king more angry.

[047-057]He rose from his throne, and stretching out his hand, he cried:

[047-058]“Let him be scourged and placed in close confinement!”

[047-059]Then Putraka with his staff wrote rapidly on the ground his wish that

[047-060]no one should be able to touch him, and stooping down slipped on his

[047-061]magic shoes. The king, the courtiers and the soldiers all remained

[047-062]exactly as they were, staring at him in astonishment, as he rose up in

[047-063]the air and flew out of one of the windows. Straight away he sped to

[047-064]the palace of Patala and into her room, where she was pacing to and fro

[047-065]in an agony of anxiety about him; for she had heard of his having been

[047-066]taken [[place-prison|prisoner]] and feared that her father would order him to be killed.
