[018-001]The mouse, small and helpless though he was, was a [[concept-wisdom|wise]] little

[018-002]creature. He saw [[animal-owl|the owl]] fly up into the tree, and knew quite well that

[018-003]if he did not take care he would serve as dinner to that great strong

[018-004]bird. He knew too that, if he went within reach of the claws of the

[018-005]cat, he would suffer for it. “How I do wish,” he thought to himself, “I

[018-006]could make friends with the cat, now she is in distress, and get her to

[018-007]promise not to hurt me if ever she gets free. As long as I am near the

[018-008]cat, the owl will not dare to come after me.” As he thought and

[018-009]thought, his eyes got brighter and brighter, and at last he decided

[018-010]what he would do. He had, you see, kept his presence of mind; that is

[018-011]to say, he did not let his fright of the cat or the owl prevent him

[018-012]from thinking clearly. He now ventured forth from amongst the barley,

[018-013]and coming near enough to the cat for her to see him quite clearly, but

[018-014]not near enough for her to reach him with her claws, or far enough away

[018-015]for the owl to get him without danger from those terrible claws, he

[018-016]said to the cat in a queer little squeaky voice: “Dear Puss, I do not

[018-017]like to see you in such a fix. It is true we have never been exactly

[018-018]friends, but I have always looked up to you as a strong and noble

[018-019]enemy. If you will promise never to do me any harm, I will do my best

[018-020]to help you. I have very sharp teeth, and I might perhaps be able to

[018-021]nibble through the string round your beautiful neck and set you free.

[018-022]What do you think about it?”
