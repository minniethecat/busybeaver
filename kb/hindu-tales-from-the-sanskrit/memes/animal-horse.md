# Horse

## Description

The horse in "The Jewelled Arrow" is among the most striking animal presences in the Hindu Tales — not a character in its own right but a gift of extraordinary power that enables the hero's quest. When Rupa-Sikha, the magician's daughter, equips Sringa-Bhuja for his escape and his trials, she provides him with a horse of supernatural quality: *"a noble horse, richly caparisoned"* (057-041).

This horse is no ordinary steed. It is described with the language of magnificence — *"richly caparisoned"* suggests both practical equipment and ceremonial grandeur. More significantly, the horse possesses specialized knowledge that transcends ordinary equine capability: *"'You must ride this horse,' said Rupa-Sikha, 'who will...'"* (057-043). The horse's name — and by extension its identity — is intimately tied to its function: *"is my horse's name — knows well what he has to do"* (057-049). The horse knows; the horse understands its mission without being told.

## Appearances

The horse appears in the middle section of "The Jewelled Arrow" (ch057–ch058) as the mount that carries Sringa-Bhuja through the magician's enchanted forest and toward his ultimate destination. A contrasting equine presence appears at the magician's gate: the magician himself is *"mounted on a huge horse"* (058-006), suggesting that horses in this tale serve as markers of power and status, whether aligned with the hero or the villain.

The two horses — Sringa-Bhuja's magical gift and the magician's imposing mount — form a visual and symbolic pair. One represents supernatural assistance given freely; the other represents power wielded as threat. Their juxtaposition heightens the sense that Sringa-Bhuja's quest takes place in a world where the boundaries between natural and supernatural are permeable, and where the right mount can mean the difference between escape and capture.

## Narrative Function

The horse functions as a **magical aid** — one of several supernatural gifts (along with the jewelled arrow itself) that enable the hero to overcome obstacles beyond ordinary human capacity. In the grammar of folktale, the magical horse is a familiar type: the steed that knows the way, that outruns pursuit, that carries the hero across boundaries that cannot be crossed on foot. Rupa-Sikha's instructions make clear that the horse is not merely fast but *knowing* — it possesses intelligence that makes it an active partner in the escape, not a passive vehicle.

The horse's narrative role also includes the function of **transportation across thresholds**. Sringa-Bhuja must traverse the magician's domain, and without the horse this traversal would be impossible. The horse carries him not merely across physical space but from one state of being to another — from fugitive to hero, from supplicant to victor. This threshold-crossing function aligns the horse with a universal folktale motif: the magical steed that enables passage between worlds.

## Interactions

The horse interacts primarily with its rider, **[[person-sringa-bhuja|Sringa-Bhuja]]**, and implicitly with **[[person-rupa-sikha|Rupa-Sikha]]**, who knows its capabilities and bestows it as a gift. The dynamic between horse and rider is one of trust — Sringa-Bhuja must trust the horse's knowledge and speed, just as he must trust Rupa-Sikha's strategic wisdom. The horse, in turn, fulfills its role without hesitation, embodying the ideal of the loyal mount whose capabilities are perfectly matched to its rider's needs.

The relationship between Rupa-Sikha and the horse is also significant. She does not merely hand over an animal; she provides a mount whose name encodes its purpose, who *"knows well what he has to do"* (057-049). Her knowledge of the horse's capabilities reflects her own supernatural wisdom and her intimate understanding of the dangers her beloved will face. The horse is thus not just a gift but a communication — Rupa-Sikha's love and strategy made tangible.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Hindu tradition, the horse (*ashva*) carries profound symbolic weight. The *Ashvamedha* (horse sacrifice) was the supreme royal ritual, signifying a king's claim to universal sovereignty. While the tale does not invoke these specific associations, the horse's portrayal — noble, knowing, supernaturally capable — resonates with the broader Indo-European tradition of the horse as a creature of power, prestige, and connection to the divine.

The contrast between Sringa-Bhuja's *"noble horse, richly caparisoned"* (057-041) and the magician's threatening mount creates a symbolic opposition between two modes of equine power: power that serves and power that intimidates. This opposition maps onto the larger moral structure of the tale, in which the magician's coercive magic is ultimately overcome by the collaborative magic of love and assistance — the horse being one of its principal vehicles.
