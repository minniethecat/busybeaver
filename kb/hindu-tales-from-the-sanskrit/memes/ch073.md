[073-001]After flying along for several hours, the crow began to feel very

[073-002]tired. He was seized too with a great desire to hear his own voice

[073-003]again. So he flew to the ground, laid his little companion gently down,

[073-004]and gave vent to a number of hoarse cries, which quite frightened

[073-005][[animal-mouse|Hiranya]], who timidly asked him what was the matter.

[073-006]“Nothing whatever,” answered Laghupatin, “except that you are not quite

[073-007]so light as I thought you were, and that I need a rest; besides which,

[073-008]I am hungry and I expect you are. We had better stop here for the

[073-009]night, and start again early to-morrow morning.” Hiranya readily agreed

[073-010]to this, and after a good meal, which was easily found, the two settled

[073-011]down to sleep, the crow perched in a tree, the mouse hidden amongst its

[073-012]roots. Very early the next day they were off again, and soon arrived at

[073-013]the river, where they were warmly welcomed by [[animal-tortoise|the tortoise]]. The three

[073-014]had a long talk together, and agreed never to part again. The tortoise,

[073-015]who had lived a great deal longer than either the mouse or the crow,

[073-016]was a very pleasant companion; and even Laghupatin, who was very fond

[073-017]of talking himself, liked to listen to his stories of long ago.

[073-018]“I wonder,” said the tortoise, whose name was Mandharaka, to the mouse,

[073-019]“that you are not afraid to [[event-journey|travel]] about as you have done, with your

[073-020]soft little body unprotected by any armour. Look how different it is

[073-021]for me; it is almost impossible for any of the wild creatures who live

[073-022]near this river to hurt me, and they know it full well. See how thick

[073-023]and strong my armour is. The claws even of a tiger, a wild cat or an

[073-024]eagle, could not penetrate it. I am very much afraid, my little friend,

[073-025]that you will be gobbled up some fine day, and Laghupatin and I will

[073-026]seek for you in vain.”

[073-027]“Of course,” said the mouse, “I know the truth of what you say; but I

[073-028]can very easily hide from danger—much more easily than you or

[073-029]Laghupatin. A tuft of moss or a few dead leaves are shelter enough for

[073-030]me, but big fellows like you and the crow can be quite easily seen.

[073-031]Nobody saw me when [[animal-pigeon|the pigeons]] were all caught except Laghupatin; and I

[073-032]would have kept out of his sight if I had not known that he did not

[073-033]care to eat mice.”

[073-034]In spite of the fears of Mandharaka, the mouse and the crow lived as

[073-035]his guests for a long time without any accident; and one day they were

[073-036]suddenly joined by a new companion, a creature as unlike any one of the

[073-037]three friends as could possibly be imagined. This was a very beautiful

[073-038]deer, who came bounding out of the [[place-forest|forest]], all eager to escape from the

[073-039]hunters, by whom he had been pursued, but too weary to reach the river,

[073-040]across which he had hoped to be able to swim to safety. Just as he

[073-041]reached the three friends, he fell to the ground, almost crushing the

[073-042]mouse, who darted away in the nick of time. Strange to say, the hunters

[073-043]did not follow [[animal-deer|the deer]]; and it was evident that they had not noticed

[073-044]the way he had gone.

[073-045]The tortoise, the crow and the mouse were all very sorry for the deer,

[073-046]and, as was always the case, the crow was the first to speak. “Whatever

[073-047]has happened to you?” he asked. And the deer made answer:

[073-048]“I thought my last hour had come this time, for the hunters were close

[073-049]upon me; and even now I do not feel safe.”

[073-050]“I’ll fly up and take a look ’round,” said Laghupatin; and off he went

[073-051]to explore, coming back soon, to say he had seen the hunters

[073-052]disappearing a long distance off, going in quite another direction from

[073-053]the river. Gradually the deer was reassured, and lay still where he had

[073-054]fallen; whilst the three friends chatted away to him, telling him of

[073-055]their adventures. “What you had better do,” said the tortoise, “is to

[073-056]join us. When you have had a good meal, and a drink from the river, you

[073-057]will feel a different creature. My old friend Laghupatin will be the

[073-058]one to keep watch for us all, and warn us of any danger approaching; I

[073-059]will give you the benefit of my long experience; and little Hiranya,

[073-060]though he is not likely to be of any use to you, will certainly never

[073-061]do you any harm.”
