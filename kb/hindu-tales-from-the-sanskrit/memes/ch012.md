[012-001]Life was now, of course, completely changed for the family in the

[012-002][[place-forest|forest]]. [[person-subha-datta|Subha Datta]] no longer went to cut wood to be sold, and the boys

[012-003]also left off doing so. Every day their father fetched food for them

[012-004]all, and the greatest desire of each one of the family was to find out

[012-005]where it came from. They never could do so, for Subha Datta managed to

[012-006]make them afraid to follow him when he went forth with his basket. The

[012-007]secret he kept from the wife to whom he used to tell everything soon

[012-008]began to spoil the happiness of the home. The children who had no

[012-009]longer anything to do quarrelled with each other. Their mother got

[012-010]sadder and sadder, and at last decided to tell Subha Datta that, unless

[012-011]he would let her know where the food came from, she would go away from

[012-012]him and take her little girls with her. She really did mean to do this,

[012-013]but something soon happened to change everything again. Of course, the

[012-014]neighbours in the wood, who had bought the fuel from the boys and

[012-015]helped them by [[concept-generosity|giving]] them fruit and rice, heard of the return of their

[012-016]father and of the wonderful change in their lot. Now the whole family

[012-017]had plenty to eat every day, though none of them knew where it all came

[012-018]from. Subha Datta was very fond of showing off what he could do, and

[012-019]sometimes asked his old friends amongst the woodcutters to come and

[012-020]have a meal with him. When they arrived they would find all sorts of

[012-021]good things spread out on the ground and different kinds of wines in

[012-022]beautiful bottles.

[012-023]This went on for some months, Subha Datta getting prouder and prouder

[012-024]of all that he could do, and it seemed likely that his secret would

[012-025]never be discovered. Everybody tried to find it out, and many followed

[012-026]him secretly when he set forth into the woods; but he was very [[concept-cleverness|clever]]

[012-027]at dodging them, hiding his treasure constantly in a new place in the

[012-028]dead of the night. If he had only been [[concept-contentment|content]] with getting food out of

[012-029]his pitcher and drinking pure water, all would most likely have been

[012-030]well with him. But that was just what he could not do. Till he had his

[012-031]pitcher he had never drunk anything but water, but now he often took

[012-032]too much wine. It was this which led to the misfortune of losing his

[012-033]beloved pitcher. He began to boast of his cleverness, telling his

[012-034]friends there was nothing they wanted that he could not get for them;

[012-035]and one day when he had given them a very [[event-feast|grand feast]], in which were

[012-036]several rare kinds of food they had asked for, he drank too much

[012-037]wine—so much that he no longer knew what he was saying.

[012-038]This was the chance his guests wanted. They began teasing him, telling

[012-039]him they believed he was really a wicked robber, who had stolen the

[012-040]food or the money to buy it. He got angry, and at last was actually

[012-041]silly enough to tell them all to come with him, and he would show them

[012-042]he was no robber. When his wife heard this, she was half pleased to

[012-043]think that now at last the secret would come out of where the food came

[012-044]from, and half afraid that something terrible would happen. The

[012-045]children too were greatly excited, and went with the rest of the party,

[012-046]who followed their father to the last hiding-place of the precious

[012-047]pitcher.

[012-048]When they all got very near the place, however, some idea began to come

[012-049]into Subha Datta’s head that he was doing a very [[concept-foolishness|foolish]] thing. He

[012-050]stopped suddenly, turned round facing the crowd that followed him, and

[012-051]said he would not go a step further till they all went back to the

[012-052]cottage. His wife begged him to let her at least go with him, and the

[012-053]children all clamoured not to be sent back, but it was no good. Back

[012-054]they all had to go, the woodcutter watching till they were out of

[012-055]sight.
