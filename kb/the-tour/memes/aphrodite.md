# Aphrodite

## Overview

Aphrodite is the Greek goddess of love, beauty, desire, and fertility, identified with the Roman Venus. According to Hesiod, she was born from the sea foam (aphros) after Cronus castrated Uranus and threw his genitals into the sea. In *The Tour*, Aphrodite serves as a framing deity — the patroness of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s ship (001-006), the subject of haunting hymns sung by the slave [[cora|Cora]] (001-005, 002-024), and a recurring motif linking love, loss, and the sea voyage that opens the narrative.

## In the Narrative

Aphrodite permeates the novel from its very first pages. The quadrireme that carries [[lucius|Lucius]] from Italy to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] is named the *Aphrodite*, and a silver statue of the goddess stands at the prow (001-006). The opening scene is set to the sound of harps and "a hymn to the goddess Aphrodite, whose name constantly rang back through the measured cadence of the verse" (001-005). The closing phrases of [[cora|Cora]]'s song — "Aphrodite! … Aphrodite!" (001-028, 001-030) — echo across the night sea, establishing the mood of longing and unfulfilled passion that drives the plot.

The ship is repeatedly identified with its divine patroness: "the *Aphrodite* heaved to" (003-004) and the travellers stand "beside the silver figure of Aphrodite" (003-006). As the barge glides toward [[canopus|Canopus]], the temple of Aphrodite on an island in [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]] is described in romantic terms, with its pillars "like torches of white fire" against the sunset (008-002). The silver statue of the goddess at the prow is flanked by burning incense (008-004), treating the goddess as a living presence on the journey.

[[cora|Cora]]'s *Hymn to Aphrodite* is the most sustained poetic passage in the novel (chapter VII). Sung as the barge passes the temple of Aphrodite Arsinoe (008-017, 008-022), the hymn is a prayer for love reciprocated, its refrain — "Mother of Eros, hear thy slave! … Aphrodite, look down from above!" (007-003, 007-026) — echoing [[lucius|Lucius]]'s own desperate longing for the vanished [[ilia|Ilia]] (008-036). [[cora|Cora]] recounts that she has "served the sacred goddess Aphrodite" since childhood (012-034), giving the hymn a personal dimension of lifelong devotion.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Aphrodite's worship was widespread across the Greek world and was adopted into Roman religion as Venus. In Hellenistic [[alexandria|Alexandria]], Aphrodite was associated with Arsinoe II, the deified Ptolemaic queen, giving rise to the cult of Aphrodite Arsinoe — the specific manifestation mentioned in the novel (008-017). Temples to Aphrodite dotted the islands of [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]] (003-044), and her cult was particularly associated with seafaring, fitting for a novel whose protagonist arrives by ship. The town of [[canopus|Canopus]] itself was "consecrated to Aphrodite" and "peopled by none but hieroduli" (015-006).
