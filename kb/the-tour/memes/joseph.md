## Overview

Joseph, the son of Jacob and Rachel, is a central figure in the Book of Genesis whose story — sold into slavery by his brothers, rising to become vizier of Egypt through his gift of interpreting dreams — made him a foundational figure in Jewish tradition. In *The Tour*, Joseph is mentioned alongside Moses and [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]] as one of the great figures who walked the land of Egypt and transmitted its ancient [[philosophy|wisdom]] [015-029]. He is remembered specifically as "the Jewish interpreter of dreams" [016-008], and his presence in the narrative links the [[jewish|Hebrew]] scriptural tradition to the Egyptian spiritual landscape, presenting Egypt as the crucible where [[jewish|Hebrew]] prophecy and Egyptian occult knowledge first met.

## In the Narrative

Joseph is invoked as part of the deep history of Egypt — not a living character but a revered ancestor whose memory sanctifies the land. When the travellers contemplate the antiquity of Egyptian civilisation, Joseph is named in the same breath as Moses and [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]]: "here Joseph had wandered, the interpreter of dreams" [015-029]. The phrasing — "had wandered" — captures Joseph's dual identity as both [[jewish|Hebrew]] patriarch and Egyptian vizier, a man who belonged to two worlds and mediated between them.

A Jewish character in the narrative identifies himself as being "of the tribe of Joseph himself, who took to wife Asenath" [018-013], drawing a direct genealogical connection to the patriarch that spans centuries. This declaration of tribal identity places Joseph not as a distant legend but as a living presence in the self-understanding of Jewish characters in the novel. The tutor [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] reads about Joseph and Moses together, linking their stories through the theme of Egyptian [[philosophy|wisdom]] received and transmitted [016-012]. The pairing of Joseph and Moses — the dream-interpreter and the lawgiver — suggests a tradition in which access to Egyptian knowledge was seen as preparatory to divine revelation.

## Joseph as Dream-Interpreter

The epithet "interpreter of dreams" connects Joseph to a specific Egyptian tradition. Dream interpretation (*oneiromancy*) was a well-developed practice in ancient Egypt, with priests trained to decode the symbolic language of dreams. The biblical Joseph's interpretation of [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]]'s dreams — the seven fat and lean cows, the seven full and withered ears of grain — draws on this Egyptian context while transforming it for a [[jewish|Hebrew]] theological purpose. In the novel, Joseph's dream-interpretation is not merely a personal gift but part of a broader tradition of Egyptian [[philosophy|wisdom]] that the travellers encounter. His skill places him alongside [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]] as a transmitter of the occult knowledge for which Egypt was famous in the Roman world.

## Historical Context

The biblical Joseph is not attested in Egyptian historical records, but his story reflects elements of the Hyksos period (c. 1650–1550 BCE) when Semitic peoples held power in Egypt. The narrative of a foreigner rising to high office in the Egyptian court has parallels in Egyptian literature, such as the Tale of Sinuhe. In Hellenistic Jewish tradition, Joseph was seen as a precursor to Moses — both were [[jewish|Hebrew]]s who gained access to Egyptian [[philosophy|wisdom]] and used it for divine purposes. The Jewish community of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], which produced works like the *Wisdom of Solomon* and Philo's philosophical treatises, saw in figures like Joseph a model for synthesising Egyptian and [[jewish|Hebrew]] traditions.
