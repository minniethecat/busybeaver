# Lake Mareotis

## Overview

Lake Mareotis (modern Lake Mariut) was a large brackish lake lying immediately south of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] in the Nile delta. In antiquity, it was connected to the Nile by canals and to the Mediterranean through [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s harbours, serving as both a commercial waterway and a recreational setting. In *The Tour*, Lake Mareotis is the route of the barge journey to [[canopus|Canopus]], its illuminated villas and island temples creating one of the novel's most atmospheric sequences.

## In the Narrative

The lake is first glimpsed during the [[alexandria|Alexandria]] city tour, when [[caleb|Caleb]] points it out beyond the city walls: "Lake Mareotis: it lay spread out like a sea, but was divided by isthmuses into smaller inland lakes; there were islands often bearing some temple to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]; and along the margins of the lake rose villa after villa, in royal pomp of marble-coloured villas, casting their reflections into the limpid water" (003-044). This description establishes the lake as a landscape of beauty, wealth, and sacredness.

The night barge journey across the lake toward [[canopus|Canopus]] is the novel's most visually sumptuous passage: "The night fell and the stars shone brilliantly over Lake Mareotis and the [[canopus|Canopus Canal]]" (008-002). The illuminated temples and villas lining the route create an atmosphere of mystical festivity: "The splendid villas on either side of the canal were ablaze with balls of fire; the temple of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], on the eyot, was silhouetted against gold and chrysolite" (008-002).

## Historical Context

Lake Mareotis was an important economic resource for ancient [[alexandria|Alexandria]], supporting papyrus cultivation, fishing, and wine production (the famous Mareotis wine praised by [[ghizla|Ghizla]]). The canal connecting [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to [[canopus|Canopus]] ran along or across the lake, lined with pleasure-gardens and villas belonging to wealthy [[alexandria|Alexandria]]ns. [[strabo|Strabo]] describes the lake region as a place of recreation and the canal journey to [[canopus|Canopus]] as a popular diversion.
