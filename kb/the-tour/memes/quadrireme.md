# Quadrireme

## Overview

A quadrireme (Latin *quadriremis*) was a large oared warship of the Hellenistic and Roman periods, driven by four levels of rowers and supplemented by sails. In *The Tour*, the quadrireme *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* serves as the vessel that transports [[lucius|Lucius]], [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] from Italy to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], and later carries them homeward at the novel's conclusion. It is far more than a means of transport — it is a floating microcosm of Roman wealth and culture, a stage for the novel's opening scene, and a recurring symbol of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s fortune and its eventual dissipation.

## In the Narrative

The novel opens with the quadrireme gliding through a windless, star-strewn night on the Mediterranean: "The great quadrireme glided evenly and softly, as though upon a lake, under a wide firmament of stars" (001-001). Cousijns lavishes descriptive attention on the vessel, personifying it as a living creature: "she was like the legs of some graceful sea-animal; the ship herself, with her swanlike raised prow, suggested an elegant monster swimming through the lake-calm waters of that silvery night-world, a monster with a swan's neck and hundreds of slender, evenly-moving legs and winged with two rose-yellow sails" (001-003).

The ship is explicitly named the *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]*, dedicated to the goddess [[aphrodite]], whose silver statue stands on the prow with an alabaster lamp burning before it (001-006). The ship's musical character is important to the opening atmosphere: three hundred rowers sing "the constantly repeated melodious phrase … soft and monotone, in a melancholy minor" (001-002), while sailors sing "the blither song" from the rear half-deck and a Greek songstress accompanies herself on a "little four-stringed Lesbian harp" singing a hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] (001-005). The sudden silencing of all music when [[lucius|Lucius]] cries out in grief for [[ilia]] establishes the ship as a resonant emotional space (001-025).

The quadrireme's arrival in the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]] of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] is a spectacle in its own right: "though numbers of vessels entered the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]] of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] daily, it was not every day that the quay was visited by so impressive a quadrireme as this; and the beautiful ship aroused curiosity" (003-005). The [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n crowd presses forward to stare, and the ship "with swanlike elegance turned and lay to beside the great quay, at the place where she was expected" (003-004).

In the novel's concluding movement, the quadrireme becomes entangled in the financial ruin that overtakes [[lucius|Lucius]]. [[vettius|Vettius]] the steward reveals that had he not acted swiftly, "your quadrireme also would have been seized at [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and you would now have been without your ship" (027-040). The ship's legal ownership becomes uncertain: "even my title to the quadrireme is questionable" (028-004), and [[lucius|Lucius]] is forced to sell "the quadrireme with all her contents" (028-018) to raise funds. The vessel that had been a symbol of Roman magnificence becomes a commodity to be liquidated, marking the protagonist's transformation from wealthy tourist to penniless seeker.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Quadriremes ("four-oared") were developed in the fourth century BCE, larger than the ubiquitous trireme but smaller than the massive quinqueremes that dominated major naval battles. By the Roman imperial period, they served primarily as flagships, pleasure-craft for the wealthy, and escort vessels rather than frontline warships. The *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* carrying three hundred rowers and additional crew, with luxurious appointments including a Tyrian silk pavilion on the prow, is consistent with the private luxury ships known from Roman sources.
