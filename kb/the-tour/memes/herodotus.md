## Overview

Herodotus of Halicarnassus (c. 484–425 BCE) was the first Greek historian, author of the *Histories* — a vast account of the Greco-Persian Wars that includes extensive descriptions of the lands and peoples of the ancient world, including a detailed account of Egypt in Book II. In *The Tour*, Herodotus functions as a classical authority on Egypt, repeatedly quoted by the travellers and particularly by [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], who is both charmed by and sceptical of the old historian's marvellous tales.

## In the Narrative

Herodotus enters the novel as a touchstone of classical knowledge. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], the learned Greek tutor, finds his mind charmed by "the wonderful, fantastic stories of Herodotus" (004-013) — an affection that suggests both genuine admiration and a faint condescension, as though the historian's tales belong to a more credulous age than the tutor's own.

The historian's authority is invoked directly at the pyramids: "Herodotus says that the pyramid of Cheops…" (016-006), and his most celebrated phrase — "Egypt, that gift of the Nile" — is quoted by the narrator as the party contemplates the Egyptian landscape, the very words "as Herodotus had called it long ago" (024-001) lending the scene the weight of centuries. At [[syene|Syene]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] quotes Herodotus on the sources of the Nile — "Herodotus tells us that the mysterious sources of the Nile ought to be here, near [[syene|Syene]] and Elephantina, and that the canal which leads to them is an abyss and a bottomless sea" — before immediately dismissing the claim: "But Herodotus often tells us fairytales!" (025-007).

This pattern — quotation followed by qualification — defines Herodotus's role in the novel. He is the indispensable authority, the Greek who saw Egypt and wrote it down for posterity; but he is also the fabulist, the teller of "fairytales" whose accounts must be checked against the evidence of Roman eyes. The tension between reverence and scepticism mirrors the novel's broader treatment of classical *paideia*: the travellers carry Herodotus with them as mental baggage, a lens through which they see Egypt — but the lens is acknowledged to be imperfect, and occasionally they must set it aside.

## Historical Context

Herodotus travelled to Egypt around 450 BCE, during a period of Persian rule. His account in *Histories* Book II covers Egyptian geography, religion, customs, and history, drawing on conversations with Egyptian priests at [[memphis|Memphis]], [[thebes|Thebes]], and Heliopolis. While some of his statements were later challenged (notably by Plutarch in *On the Malice of Herodotus*), his work remained the primary Greek source on Egypt for centuries and was still widely read in the Roman period. His characterisation of Egypt as "the gift of the Nile" became proverbial throughout antiquity — appearing in the novel as a phrase so familiar it needs no attribution beyond the historian's name (024-001).
