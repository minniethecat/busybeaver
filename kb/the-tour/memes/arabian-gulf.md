## Overview

The Arabian Gulf — called the Erythraean Sea by the Greeks and the Red Sea in modern geography — is the narrow body of water separating the eastern coast of Egypt from the Arabian Peninsula. In the Roman period of the first century AD, it was a vital corridor for trade between the Mediterranean world and the East, connecting Roman Egypt to Arabia Felix (modern Yemen), the Horn of Africa, and ultimately India. The gulf appears in *The Tour* as the maritime setting for the later stages of the travellers' journey, where the quadrireme *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* sails its waters.

## In the Narrative

The Arabian Gulf features in the novel's logistical geography during the latter portion of the journey. After the party's Nile barge reaches [[apollonopolis-magna|Apollonopolis Magna]], [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes an alternative route: the thalamegus will take [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] through the canal system to [[berenice|Berenice]] on the Bay of Acathantus, while the quadrireme *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* sails up the Arabian Gulf to meet them at the Pillars of [[sesostris|Sesostris]] [004-02187]. This avoids the arduous overland journey through Ethiopia.

Later, the gulf serves as the backdrop for the dramatic revelation of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s ruin. [[vettius|Vettius]], the steward, explains how he kept [[tiberius|Tiberius]]'s displeasure secret "by continuing to drift about in the Great Sea and the Arabian Gulf," and subsequently navigated the [[nechao-canal|Nechao Canal]] to Arsinoe to escape the reach of imperial seizure [006-2415]. The gulf is thus both a route of escape and a liminal space between safety and exposure.

At [[cape-dire|Cape Dire]], [[cora|Cora]] and [[lucius|Lucius]] look out from the forest and see "the line of the sea, the Arabian Gulf, the Erythraean or Red Sea" gleaming darkly on the horizon — a moment of longing and melancholy in the starlight [005-2293].

## Historical Context

In the first century AD, the Erythraean Sea was the primary maritime corridor for Roman trade with the East. Under Augustus and [[tiberius|Tiberius]], the ports of Myos Hormos and [[berenice|Berenice]] on the Egyptian coast served as the Roman Empire's gateways to the incense and spice routes of Arabia Felix, the Horn of Africa, and the pepper and silk of India. The anonymous Greek navigational guide known as the *Periplus Maris Erythraei* (circa AD 40–70) documents the ports, goods, and sailing conditions of this sea in detail — attesting to the sophistication of Roman-era navigation. The Arabian Gulf's monsoon winds dictated the seasonal rhythm of this trade: southward voyages departed in summer with the etesian winds, while return voyages rode the winter monsoon. This trade enriched [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and fuelled the luxury consumption of Imperial Rome — making the gulf not merely a geographic feature but an economic lifeline of the early Roman Empire.
