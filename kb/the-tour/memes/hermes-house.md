# Hermes House

## Overview

The Hermes House, formally known as the Great Diversorium, is the guesthouse near the Canopian Gate of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] where [[lucius|Lucius]], [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] take up residence upon arriving in Egypt. Owned and operated by Master [[ghizla]], a [[saba|Saba]]ean entrepreneur, and staffed by his younger brother [[caleb|Caleb]], the Hermes House is more than a lodging — it is a hub of commerce, intrigue, and cross-cultural encounter, named for and dedicated to [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]], the patron god of travellers, merchants, and messengers.

## In the Narrative

The Hermes House is introduced in elaborate detail as the travellers arrive after their eventful tour of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The procession "trotted home through the gardens of [[bruchium|Bruchium]], [[bruchium|the palace quarter]], and along the Hippodrome to [[ghizla|Master Ghizla]]'s great diversorium" (003-092). The establishment's dedication to [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]] is made explicit: a "winged head of [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]], in marble, crowned the marble architrave of the door," while "caducei, or [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]]'-wands, with winding snakes, were carved on the pilasters of the door; for the diversorium was dedicated to [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]] and known in the quarter as the Hermes House" (003-092).

The building itself "consisted of several low buildings" and "harboured Arabian and Phoenician merchants, who looked out curiously, squatting on mats or lying at their meal, served by black slaves" (003-097). The travellers are given a row of three suites with private gardens, described by [[ghizla|Ghizla]] as reserved "only for princely nobles like your lordship" (003-013), boasting every modern convenience: marble basins with taps providing hot and cold water, kitchens, and a cellar of exotic wines.

[[ghizla|Ghizla]]'s self-promotion is a running motif: "My diversorium boasts every possible comfort, my lord, and all the latest conveniences … which visitors like your lordship demand in these days" (003-098). The [[saba|Saba]]ean brothers operate as a tag-team — [[ghizla|Ghizla]] the dignified proprietor, [[caleb|Caleb]] the vivacious guide and fixer — and their guesthouse functions as a total service operation for wealthy foreigners, providing lodging, dining, tours, introductions to local beauties, and access to merchants selling exotic wares.

The diversorium serves several key narrative functions. It is the base from which the travellers launch their [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n adventures, including the secret nocturnal expedition to the sibyl of [[rhacotis|Rhacotis]]. Its palm-garden and galleries provide a setting for quieter moments, such as [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] "reading his Egyptian guidebooks at a table under a palm-tree" (004-013) and the display of goods by "Indians, [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]], Arabs, Phoenicians" (004-015). During the Nile flood season, "the palm-garden of the diversorium was inundated" (011-004), a detail that grounds the narrative in the actual geography of [[alexandria|Alexandria]].

The Hermes House also has a branch establishment at [[cape-dire|Cape Dire]] (027-002), where [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] awaits the travellers on their return journey, and appears in the novel's final pages when [[lucius|Lucius]], now impoverished, lodges "in a small apartment in the Hermes House" (029-007) rather than the princely suites of his arrival.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Roman *diversoria* (from *divertere*, "to turn aside") were inns or guesthouses catering to travellers. Unlike *cauponae*, which were rough taverns frequented by the lower classes, a *diversorium* of the quality described in the novel would cater to wealthy merchants and travelling officials. The dedication to [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]]/Mercury is fitting, as the god was patron of both travel and commerce. The multicultural character of the establishment — [[saba|Saba]]ean proprietors, Arabian and Phoenician merchant guests, Libyan bearers, and a Roman clientele — reflects the cosmopolitan reality of first-century [[alexandria|Alexandria]].
