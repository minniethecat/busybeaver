# Serapeum

## Overview

The Serapeum (or Sarapeion) was the temple dedicated to the syncretic Greco-Egyptian god [[serapis|Serapis]]. The most famous Serapeums stood at [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — where the temple complex included a vast library second only to the Great Library of [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] — and at [[canopus|Canopus]], the popular pilgrimage destination. In *The Tour*, the Serapeum appears both as an architectural landmark of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and as the ritual setting for the climactic dream-incubation ceremony at [[canopus|Canopus]].

## In the Narrative

The [[alexandria|Alexandria]] Serapeum is first glimpsed as the travellers trot through the city: "past the immemorial temple of [[serapis|Serapis]], the Serapeum: sombre and grey it lay with its heavy hyeroglyphed pylons" (005-013). The grim, ancient appearance of the [[alexandria|Alexandria]] temple contrasts tellingly with its current state of decline: "The Serapeum is deserted … for the temple of [[serapis|Serapis]] at [[canopus|Canopus]]" (005-014), indicating that the cult's centre of gravity has shifted eastward.

The Serapeum's intellectual role is noted when [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] proposes visiting "the great libraries of [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] and the Serapeum" (006-002), acknowledging the temple's function as a centre of learning. The subsidiary library at the Serapeum was said to hold some 42,800 scrolls, making it one of the most significant repositories of knowledge in antiquity.

At [[canopus|Canopus]], the Serapeum is described in ecstatic detail across chapter IX. The temple's "immeasurable empty space, without image, without altar, without anything" (009-006) conveys the awe-inspiring architecture of the pronaos (forecourt) and the holy of holies, where the statue of [[serapis|Serapis]] remains invisible behind "close-drawn hyacinth curtains" (009-006). The terraces where pilgrims sleep under their dreaming-nets form the temple's highest levels, open to the starry sky.

The Serapeum's power over the imagination is emphasised even negatively: "notwithstanding the Serapeum, notwithstanding the dreams of [[canopus|Canopus]], which die away in orgies" (013-003), the temple's spiritual efficacy proves limited against human weakness.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The [[alexandria|Alexandria]] Serapeum was built under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] III Euergetes (r. 246–222 BCE) and was considered one of the most magnificent temples of the ancient world. It was destroyed in 391 CE during the Christianisation of the Roman Empire, when a mob led by the patriarch Theophilus demolished the temple and its famous cult statue. The [[canopus|Canopus]] Serapeum, though less architecturally famous, was more popular as a pilgrimage site due to its association with healing dreams.

Rufinus of Aquileia's account of the Serapeum's destruction notes that its walls were inscribed with [[hieroglyph|hieroglyphics]] and that it contained a subsidiary library. Both details appear in Couperus's description, suggesting he drew on ancient sources for his depiction.
