# Amulets

## Overview

Amulets are protective charms worn to ward off evil, bring good fortune, and invoke supernatural protection. In the ancient Mediterranean world, amulets were ubiquitous across all social classes — from simple knots of coloured thread to intricately engraved gemstones set in gold. In the narrative, amulets — particularly those of [[saba|Saba]]ean origin — play a significant role as tangible objects of faith and superstition. [[caleb|Caleb]], the [[saba|Saba]]ean merchant, is a fervent believer in their power and supplies them to his master [[lucius|Lucius]]. The amulets are described as "wonderful" objects that "bring luck and blissfulness" [004-021], and their efficacy is repeatedly asserted by [[caleb|Caleb]], who wears them "everywhere, on his chest and round his waist and even on a narrow gold bangle round his ankle" [005-005]. Yet the novel also allows a counter-voice: [[cora|Cora]]'s fear that "it is perhaps because of one of your amulets that my mistress [[ilia|Ilia]] disappeared" [011-022] introduces a note of dread, suggesting that the power attributed to these objects may be real but not necessarily benevolent.

## In the Narrative

Amulets first appear when [[caleb|Caleb]] displays his wares in the garden, where "the [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] displayed wonderful amulets, which bring luck and blissfulness" [004-021]. [[lucius|Lucius]] purchases and wears them on [[caleb|Caleb]]'s advice: "Are you wearing your Sabaean amulets?" [[caleb|Caleb]] asks as they venture into [[alexandria|Alexandria]] at night [005-004]. [[lucius|Lucius]] confirms he has them, and [[caleb|Caleb]]'s confidence in the talismans is unwavering — "[[caleb|Caleb]] himself wore amulets everywhere" [005-005]. Later, [[caleb|Caleb]] attributes [[lucius|Lucius]]'s boldness to the amulets' influence: "your Sabaean amulets have no doubt inspired you with courage" [010-012].

Throughout the story, characters kiss and press their amulets in moments of fear or supplication [017-015][024-026], treating them as conduits of divine protection. But the novel complicates the amulets' benevolent reputation through [[cora|Cora]]'s dark suspicion: "I fear the Sabaean amulets. It is perhaps because of one of your amulets that my mistress [[ilia|Ilia]] disappeared" [011-022]. This accusation — that the same talismans [[caleb|Caleb]] touts as protective might have caused [[ilia|Ilia]]'s disappearance — cuts to the heart of the novel's ambivalence about magic and foreign religious objects. Are the amulets genuine protectors, or are they dangerous, even malevolent, imports from an alien religious world?

## Historical/Cultural Context

In the Roman Empire of the first century CE, amulets (Latin *amuleta*) were widely used across all levels of society. They took countless forms: engraved gems depicting [[egyptian-deities|deities]], folded metal lamellae inscribed with magical formulae, animal teeth, and coloured stones. Pliny the Elder devoted sections of his *Natural History* to the purported properties of various amuletic substances. Egyptian amulets — particularly scarabs and the *udjat* eye of [[horus|Horus]] — were exported throughout the Mediterranean and imitated by local craftsmen.

The Sabaean amulets in the novel belong to this broader phenomenon but carry a distinct cultural charge. [[saba|Saba]] ([[saba|Sheba]]), in southern Arabia, was known to the Roman world as the source of exotic luxuries — frankincense, myrrh, gold, and precious stones. [[caleb|Caleb]]'s amulets participate in this aura of Arabian exoticism. They are not merely protective objects but markers of [[caleb|Caleb]]'s foreignness: his insistence on their power is both a genuine belief and a commercial strategy, part of his performance as the exotic Eastern merchant who traffics in the marvellous.
