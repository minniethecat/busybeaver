## Overview

The Great Harbour (Megas Limēn) was the main port of ancient [[alexandria|Alexandria]], situated between the island of [[pharos|Pharos]] and the mainland. It was one of the busiest harbours of the Roman Empire, serving as the gateway for Egyptian grain exports to Rome and as a hub for trade between the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean via the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] canal. The harbour was dominated by the [[pharos|Pharos]] lighthouse, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, which guided ships into its waters day and night.

## In the Narrative

The Great Harbour is the first sight that greets visitors arriving in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] by sea. As characters enter the harbour, they see the [[pharos|Pharos]] lighthouse rising on the left and the city spread before them beneath the nine-storeyed tower, with the royal palaces and temples visible along the waterfront [003-001]. It is described as welcoming a great number of vessels daily [003-005]. [[lucius|Lucius]], the narrator's steward, looks out across the Great Harbour from a higher vantage point, observing the ships and activity below [005-006]. The harbour also serves as the entry point where the steward first sees his master's house upon arrival [003-020].

## Historical Context

In the first century AD, [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s Great Harbour was the primary maritime hub of Roman Egypt. The harbour complex included the royal harbour (for official and military vessels), the commercial harbour, and extensive dockyards. A chain could be raised across the entrance to control access. The harbour's prosperity was closely tied to the annona system — the grain fleet that sailed annually from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to Puteoli and later Portus, supplying Rome with Egyptian wheat.
