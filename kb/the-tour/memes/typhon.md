# Typhon

## Overview

Typhon (the Greek name for the Egyptian god Set or Seth) is the deity of chaos, storms, disorder, and violence in Egyptian mythology. He is most infamous as the murderer of his brother [[osiris|Osiris]], whom he killed, dismembered, and scattered across Egypt. In the cosmic drama central to Egyptian religion, Typhon represents the forces of destruction eternally opposed by [[horus|Horus]], [[osiris|Osiris]], and the forces of order. In *The Tour*, Typhon appears as an ever-present threat — the grinning embodiment of evil whom the beneficent gods must continually subdue.

## In the Narrative

Typhon is introduced in the tomb of the Ptolemies, where among the sacred images, the narrator describes how "[[horus|Sacred Horus]], son of [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[isis|Isis]], the radiant redeemer of mankind, who descended out of pity on a sinful world, bestrode Typhon, the grinning spirit of evil" (003-073). This visual depiction — [[horus|Horus]] triumphantly standing over the defeated Typhon — is a classic Egyptian iconographic motif symbolising the triumph of order over chaos.

The theological opposition between Typhon and the benevolent [[egyptian-deities|deities]] is made explicit during the [[serapis|Serapis]] feast: "He opposes Typhon, even as [[osiris|Osiris]] combated Typhon. Victory falls to him in the end, even as it did to [[osiris|Osiris]]" (009-015). The Feast of [[isis|Isis]] is described as commemorating "the night on which [[isis|Isis]] had collected the scattered limbs of her brother and husband [[osiris|Osiris]], murdered and quartered by Typhon" (014-031), making Typhon's murder of [[osiris|Osiris]] the mythic foundation for the ritual.

In the desert journey, Typhon's menace extends beyond mythology into the physical landscape: travellers report "awful visions of wrath with which Typhon lures the caravans into Hell" (020-012), and some have "seen Typhon's awful giant head appear above the sand" (025-008). A curse invokes "the awful jaws of Typhon, whose tongue is a serpent" (028-025), showing how the god's destructive power haunts the Egyptian imagination.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Set (Typhon) was originally a more ambivalent deity in early Egyptian religion — associated with storms and the desert but also a protector of Ra in his nightly battle against the serpent Apep. Over time, particularly after the Hyksos period, Set became increasingly demonised and was eventually identified as the embodiment of evil. In the Hellenistic period, the Greeks equated Set with Typhon, the monstrous giant of Greek mythology who challenged Zeus.

By the Roman period of *The Tour*, Typhon/Set was firmly established as the antagonist in the [[osiris|Osiris]]-[[horus|Horus]]-[[isis|Isis]] divine narrative. His association with the desert, chaos, and the colour red made him a natural symbol of everything opposed to Egyptian cosmic and social order.
