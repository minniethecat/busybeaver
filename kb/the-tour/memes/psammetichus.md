# Psammetichus

## Overview

Psammetichus I (Psamtik I, reigned c. 664–610 BCE) was the founder of the Twenty-Sixth (Saite) Dynasty and the pharaoh who reunified Egypt after the fragmentation of the Third Intermediate Period. Originally one of twelve kings of the Dodecarchy who divided Egypt among themselves after the death of the Kushite pharaoh [[typhon|Seth]]os in 671 BCE, Psammetichus — in obedience to oracular prophecy — defeated and expelled his eleven fellow-kings and reigned alone, first at [[memphis|Memphis]] and afterwards at [[sais|Sais]] (015-001). His reign inaugurated a period of stability, cultural renaissance, and renewed contact with the Greek world. In *The Tour*, Psammetichus appears not in person but through his tomb and oracle at [[sais|Sais]] — a sacred site that [[lucius|Lucius]] consults during his desperate pilgrimage through the Delta, seeking answers about the lost [[ilia|Ilia]].

## In the Narrative

The travellers arrive at [[sais|Sais]] after a long journey through the flooded Delta. There they visit the temple of Athene and the tomb of Psammetichus, described in a passage dense with historical detail:

> "The travellers had left Sais, after visiting the temple of Athene and the tomb of Psammetichus, son of Necho, founder of the twenty-sixth dynasty, one of the twelve kings of the Dodecarchy, who divided Egypt among themselves after the death of Sethos in BC 671. Psammetichus, in obedience to the oracles, defeated and expelled his eleven fellow-kings and reigned alone at Memphis and afterwards at Sais. Here was his tomb; it was sacred; there was an oracle attached to it; and Lucius had consulted that oracle." (015-001)

This single passage encodes the entire arc of Psammetichus's political career: his origin among the twelve kings (Dodecarchy), his oracular mandate, his military triumph, and his dual capitals at [[memphis|Memphis]] and [[sais|Sais]]. The tomb is described with three significant attributes — it is "sacred," it has "an oracle attached to it," and [[lucius|Lucius]] consulted it (015-001). The visit to Psammetichus's tomb marks the beginning of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s intensive oracle-consulting phase.

After consulting Psammetichus's oracle, [[lucius|Lucius]] proceeds to a series of other oracular sites across the Delta: "the [[oracle|manteum]], or oracle, of Latona at [[butos|Butos]], on an island in the lake," followed by "Xois, Hermopolis, Lycopolis, Mendes, and all the Sebennytic nome, which contained numberless [[oracle|oracles]] and shrines" (015-001, 015-002). The tomb of Psammetichus thus serves as the threshold — the first and most historically weighty oracle in a chain that leads [[lucius|Lucius]] deeper into the sacred landscape of the Delta, reflecting his desperate search for answers about [[ilia|Ilia]]'s fate.

## Historical Context

Historically, Psammetichus I was the son of Necho I, a local ruler of [[sais|Sais]] under Assyrian suzerainty. After the Assyrian withdrawal from Egypt, he emerged as the dominant power among the Delta principalities. According to [[herodotus|Herodotus]] (*Histories* 2.147–152), an oracle at Buto foretold that whichever of the twelve kings poured a libation from a bronze helmet would rule all Egypt. When Psammetichus inadvertently used his bronze helmet for a libation during a sacrifice, his fellow-kings interpreted it as the fulfillment of the prophecy and exiled him to the marshes. Psammetichus, in turn, consulted another oracle and was told to take revenge with "bronze men from the sea" — interpreted as Greek and Carian mercenaries, with whose help he defeated the eleven kings and unified Egypt.

His reign marked a turning point. Psammetichus moved the administrative capital from [[memphis|Memphis]] to [[sais|Sais]] in the western Delta, promoted trade with Greek city-states, and employed Ionian and Carian mercenaries — policies that brought Egypt into closer contact with the Mediterranean world but also created tensions between native Egyptians and foreign settlers. He initiated an archaising artistic revival that looked back to Old Kingdom models, and his building programme at [[sais|Sais]] included the temple of [[neith|Neith]] and [[soma|the royal tombs]] within its precinct — the very tomb that [[lucius|Lucius]] visits in the novel (015-001).
