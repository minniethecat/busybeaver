## Overview

A sarcophagus (from the Greek *sarkophagos*, "flesh-eating") was a stone coffin used for the burial of the wealthy and powerful in ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome. Egyptian sarcophagi were typically decorated with painted images of gods, hieroglyphic inscriptions, and funerary spells to protect the deceased in the afterlife. In *The Tour*, the travellers enter a mortuary temple where rows of sarcophagi line the walls, their painted faces staring with ghostly intensity, and one extraordinary sarcophagus — made of transparent crystal — becomes the focal point of a scene charged with awe, historical reverence, and a sense of the uncanny.

## In the Narrative

The sarcophagi first appear in an [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n necropolis, where "the painted images on the sarcophagus-lids stared, ghostly and supernatural" [003-072] — their decorated faces watching the living with unsettling intensity. The scene is deliberately uncanny: the dead seem to look back at the living, their painted eyes following the travellers through the dim temple. This reversible gaze — the dead observing the living — is one of the novel's recurring motifs.

Amid this gallery of the dead, "on a basalt pedestal stood a sarcophagus of transparent crystal, polished" [003-076], a miraculous object that renders the body within visible. The transparency of the crystal creates a paradox: it preserves while revealing, hiding nothing while protecting everything. The guide explains that the body originally "lay in state in a massive gold sarcophagus" and that "this sarcophagus was stolen" [003-078], replaced by the crystal one — a detail that weaves Ptolemaic history into the funerary architecture and hints at the dynastic violence and tomb-robbing that marked the period.

The motif of the sarcophagus recurs later in the narrative, now freighted with melancholy. At a temple site, the travellers learn that "once upon a time it contained a sarcophagus of azure granite, with the emerald eyes of the goddess upon the lid" [017-017], but the sarcophagus is gone — its presence now only a story told by a guide. The disappearance extends beyond the sarcophagus itself: "the scented [[mummification|mummy]] vanished; her sarcophagus vanished. But the sandal, which had fallen from her foot, remained" [017-027]. This haunting image — a single sandal surviving the destruction of everything around it — crystallises the novel's meditation on loss and survival. Empires fall, tombs are looted, sarcophagi are stolen, but the smallest, most intimate objects endure.

## Funerary Art and Belief

Egyptian sarcophagi were not merely containers but active participants in the funerary ritual. Their painted surfaces depicted [[egyptian-deities|deities]] — [[osiris|Osiris]], [[isis|Isis]], Nephthys, the four sons of [[horus|Horus]] — who would protect the deceased. [[hieroglyph|Hieroglyph]]ic inscriptions contained spells from the Book of the Dead, guiding the soul through the perils of the afterlife. By the Ptolemaic and Roman periods, [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n workshops produced sarcophagi blending Egyptian iconography with Greek naturalism, creating hybrid forms that reflected the city's cosmopolitan culture.
