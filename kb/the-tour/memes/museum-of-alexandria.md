# Museum of Alexandria

## Overview

The Museum — or Mouseion — of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] was the pre-eminent centre of learning in the Hellenistic and Roman worlds, an academy housing philosophers, mathematicians, astronomers, poets, and critics, sustained by state funding and attached to a library of celebrated fame. In *The Tour*, the Museum appears as one of the obligatory sights of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], visited by [[lucius|Lucius]], [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] on their first morning in the city, and later revisited by [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] alone during the period of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s incapacitating grief.

## In the Narrative

The visit to the Museum is conducted by [[caleb|Caleb]], the ever-energetic [[saba|Saba]]ean guide, who parades the travellers through the city at a brisk trot and announces their arrival: "We are now coming to the Avenue of Pillars and to the Museum" (003-049). The building itself is described impressionistically: colonnades loom aloft, statues commemorate famous men of learning, and "an immense rounded exedra" serves as a lecture hall from which "lectures were delivered at frequent intervals" (003-059).

[[caleb|Caleb]]'s characteristically irreverent commentary shapes the reader's first impression. He describes the scholars as "poor as rats" who "do not, all told, possess … *that!*" (003-056), explaining they "live on a fund provided by the State" and "have more in their heads … than in their pockets" (003-060). Yet even [[caleb|Caleb]] concedes: "you won't find their equals for cleverness anywhere." He acknowledges the library's fame: "The books they collect! Their library is quite famous" (003-058).

The travellers enter the cenaculum, or refectory, described as "wide, lofty and very long," where philosophers and scholars sit eating together (rather than reclining in the Roman aristocratic fashion) (003-059). An aged philosopher with "a clawlike hand" approaches them begging, and upon receiving alms from [[lucius|Lucius]], delivers an ironic maxim: "The highest philosophy is … to be satisfied with little" (003-062).

[[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] provides the correcting voice of learning against [[caleb|Caleb]]'s glibness: when [[caleb|Caleb]] claims the Museum was "founded by the beautiful [[cleopatra|Cleopatra]]" (003-054), [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] whispers to [[lucius|Lucius]] that it was actually "founded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] the First" (003-055). He also supplies the roll-call of the Museum's great alumni: "Euclid, Erasistratus and Diophantus; then there were the poets Theocritus, Aratus, Callimachus; and among critics Aristarchus; and among philosophers more than I could name" (003-057).

Later, during [[lucius|Lucius]]'s period of grief-stricken withdrawal, [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] "consoled himself by visiting the libraries of the Museum and the [[serapeum|Serapeum]]" (006-002). The Museum thus serves a double narrative function: as a tourist spectacle that allows Cousijn's satirical eye to rest on the vanities of intellectual life, and as a genuine seat of [[philosophy|wisdom]] where the scholarly [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] finds solace.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Museum (Greek *Mouseion*, "shrine of the Muses") was founded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy I Soter]] around 300 BCE as part of the broader palace complex in the [[bruchium|Bruchium]] quarter. It was not a museum in the modern sense but a research institute with salaried scholars, communal dining, lecture facilities, gardens, and the attached Library of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — the most ambitious collection of texts in the ancient world. The scholars [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] names span the third and second centuries BCE, from Euclid (geometry) to Aristarchus (textual criticism of Homer). By the Roman period in which *The Tour* is set, the Museum remained active but its golden age of original scholarship had passed; historians debate whether the Library had already suffered damage by the first century CE.

## Key Passages

> "The Museum has produced great scholars," Thrasyllus expounded, more appreciatively, "such as Euclid, Erasistratus and Diophantus; then there were the poets Theocritus, Aratus, Callimachus; and among critics Aristarchus; and among philosophers more than I could name." [003-057]

> The colonnades of the Museum loomed aloft; there were statues to commemorate famous men of learning; and there was an immense rounded exedra, from which lectures were delivered at frequent intervals. [003-059]

> "The highest philosophy is … to be satisfied with little," he then said, plainly, in pure Greek. And he bowed, ironically, and turned away with the movement of a long dry stalk, in his dirty cloak. [003-062]
