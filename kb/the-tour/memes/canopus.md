# Canopus

## Overview

Canopus (modern Abu Qir) was an ancient Egyptian coastal town located east of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], renowned throughout the Greco-Roman world for its temple of [[serapis|Serapis]]. The town served as a major pilgrimage destination where worshippers practised incubation — sleeping within the sacred precinct to receive oracular dreams from the god. In *The Tour*, Canopus is the destination of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s nocturnal barge journey, the setting for the most spiritually charged episode of the narrative: the great feast of [[serapis|Serapis]].

## In the Narrative

The journey to Canopus dominates chapters VII through IX of *The Tour*. The trip is proposed suddenly: "We are going to Canopus, my lord, tonight!" (007-042), and the entire household embarks with festive excitement. [[caleb|Caleb]] explains: "One and all were going to Canopus that night" (007-055), conveying that the feast draws worshippers from across [[alexandria|Alexandria]].

The approach to Canopus is by barge along the canal, past illuminated villas and the temple of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite Arsinoe]] (008-002). The rich description of the barges, the singing, and the lantern-lit water creates an atmosphere of sacred festivity. At Canopus itself, the town "rose amid its slender [[obelisk|obelisks]] and its spreading palm-trees" (009-001), transformed by night into a realm of mystery and divine encounter.

The temple ritual is described in elaborate detail from 009-001 through 009-021: the avenue of sphinxes, the four propylaea, the immeasurable sanctuary, the processions of hieropsalts and hierogrammats, the sacred gong-strokes, and finally the thousands of pilgrims lying motionless under their dreaming-nets as [[serapis|Serapis]] rises from the underworld to bring the dreams. [[lucius|Lucius]] participates "covered with a gold network, like a precious [[mummification|mummy]]" (009-019), seeking a vision of the lost [[ilia|Ilia]].

## Historical/Cultural Context

The temple of [[serapis|Serapis]] at Canopus was one of the most famous oracular centres in the Roman Empire. [[strabo|Strabo]] (Geography 17.1.17) describes Canopus as a place of pilgrimage and revelry, accessible by a canal from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] lined with pleasure-gardens. The cult of [[serapis|Serapis]] at Canopus was particularly associated with healing dreams — the practice of *incubatio* — where sick or troubled pilgrims slept in the temple and received divine prescriptions in their dreams.

The Canopus canal and the festive night-barge trips were a well-known feature of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n life, combining religious devotion with the pleasure culture for which Canopus was equally famous. Couperus's description closely follows ancient accounts of the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] and its rituals.

## Key Passages

> "In the strange bright summer night of light, lit by the sheen of the stars and the glow of the lamps, Canopus rose amid its slender obelisks and its spreading palm-trees. The barges lay moored to the long quay, one beside the other. One solemn train of pilgrims after another flowed down the street to the temple of Serapis." (009-001)

> "We are going to Canopus, we are going to Canopus!" cried Cora, like a child. "I am to sing the Hymn to Aphrodite before the temple, as we row past! The lord has been so kind and promised me a beautiful new veil to throw over him for the dreams!" (007-057–058)

> "One and all were going to Canopus that night. What a surprise! And what a feast! It was the feast of Serapis! The feast of dreams!" (007-055)
