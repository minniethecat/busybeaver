# Bruchium

## Overview

Bruchium (Greek *Broucheion*) was the royal quarter of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], occupying the eastern and central portion of the city along the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]]. It contained the former Ptolemaic palace complex on the promontory of Lochias, the [[museum-of-alexandria|Museum]], the Gymnasium, [[soma|the Soma]] (the royal burial complex housing the tomb of [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]]), and the [[heptastadium|Heptastadium]]. In *The Tour*, Bruchium serves as the travellers' primary geographical frame of reference — the district through which they pass, linger, and retreat after each excursion into the more exotic reaches of the city.

## In the Narrative

Bruchium makes its first appearance as the travellers, having visited [[soma|the Soma]], pass "through the public gardens of Bruchium, along the Paneum — an artificial little rocky mountain built up in the shape of a top or pineapple" (003-069). The gardens of Bruchium, with their "silver-green shadows between waving tamarisks and rustling sycamore-leaves" (003-088, implied context), provide a cool, shaded counterpoint to the sun-blasted streets of the commercial quarters.

After [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] visit and the tour of [[soma|the Soma]], the procession "trotted home through the gardens of Bruchium, the palace quarter, and along the Hippodrome to [[ghizla|Master Ghizla]]'s great diversorium" (003-092), locating the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] as situated on the edge of Bruchium near the Canopian Gate. The quarter thus functions as the travellers' home territory — the familiar, elegant, and safe district from which they venture out and to which they return.

On the night of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s secret journey to the sibyl of [[rhacotis|Rhacotis]], "the bearers scurried through Bruchium and past the Gymnasium and [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]], as though they had an enemy at their heels" (005-006), the hurried transit through the elite quarter emphasising the illicit nature of the expedition into the common districts. The contrast between Bruchium — lit, patrolled, respectable — and the dark, narrow streets of [[rhacotis|Rhacotis]] is a defining spatial opposition in the novel's [[alexandria|Alexandria]].

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Bruchium district occupied approximately one-third of ancient [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s area and contained most of the city's monumental public buildings. The Ptolemaic palace complex, begun under the first Ptolemies and expanded over three centuries, was among the largest royal residences of the Hellenistic world. After Egypt became a Roman province in 30 BCE, the legate (governor) resided in the former palace, as [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] notes: "since Egypt had become a Roman province, the legate resided there, surrounded with royal honours" (003-002). The Bruchium was eventually destroyed during the upheavals of the late third century CE.
