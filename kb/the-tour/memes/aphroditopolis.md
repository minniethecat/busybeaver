## Overview

Aphroditopolis was the Greek name for an ancient Egyptian city (Egyptian: Per Hathor, "House of Hathor"), so named because the Greeks identified the Egyptian goddess Hathor with their own [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]. It was the capital of the Aphroditopolite nome, one of the administrative districts of Upper Egypt. Two [[cities-and-settlements|towns]] bore this name in antiquity — the novel distinguishes them, referring to "the second town of the name" on the Arabian bank of the Nile.

## Historical Background

The Egyptian city of Per Hathor (modern Atfih) stood on the east bank of the Nile in the 22nd nome of Upper Egypt. As a nome capital, it served as the administrative and religious centre for the surrounding district, housing the governor's residence, granaries, and the principal temple devoted to Hathor. The nome system, established during the Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE), divided Egypt into roughly 42 administrative provinces, each with its own capital, standard weights and measures, and local cult — and Per Hathor retained this status through the Ptolemaic and Roman periods. Under the Ptolemies, Greek settlers hellenised the name to Aphroditopolis, following the established pattern of mapping Egyptian [[egyptian-deities|deities]] onto the Greek pantheon. The town appears in several geographical papyri of the Roman era, including tax registers and itinerary lists, confirming its continued importance as a regional centre into the second century CE [PN:001].

## Religious Significance

Hathor, the goddess of the city, was one of the most widely venerated [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in the Egyptian pantheon — a complex figure associated with love, music, motherhood, and the afterlife. The Greeks' identification of Hathor with [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] was based on these shared attributes of love and femininity, though the equation simplified the much broader scope of Hathor's cult. The temple at Aphroditopolis would have been the focus of local religious life, hosting festivals, oracular consultations, and the daily ritual of caring for the goddess's cult statue. Votive offerings, including faience figurines and inscribed stelae, attest to the popularity of Hathor's cult among both Egyptian and Greek residents of the nome during the Ptolemaic and Roman periods [PN:002].

## In the Narrative

Aphroditopolis is encountered as the travellers journey along the Nile. [[lucius|Lucius]], the narrator's steward, goes on shore when the boat reaches the city, which is described as the capital of the Aphroditopolite nome [015-006].
