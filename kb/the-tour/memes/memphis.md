## Overview

Memphis was the ancient capital of Egypt, located on the west bank of the Nile south of modern Cairo. Founded around 3100 BC, it served as the administrative and religious centre of Egypt through the Old Kingdom. In the Roman era of "The Tour," Memphis was a vast, half-empty city — a monument to its own past, with colossal decaying temples, avenues of sphinxes, and the sacred cult of the [[apis|Apis bull]] still maintained mainly for the benefit of travellers (015-040).

## In the Narrative

The travelling party approached Memphis in Chapter XV, arriving by barge after an extensive tour of the Delta region's [[oracle|oracles]] and shrines (015-001, 015-002). The [[pyramid|Pyramid]]s on the horizon announced the city before it came into view, and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], [[lucius|Lucius]], and [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] each exclaimed "Memphis!" upon sighting it.

The city made a profound impression on [[lucius|Lucius]] as a place of "eternal past." The old Egyptian capital lay "Cyclopean, like some extinct monster, with heavy lines of squat, bleaching sanctuaries and, on the river, a portico of giant pylons" (015-021). Once a metropolis of six hundred thousand, it now held only a few thousand inhabitants, "dwarfed and lost in the spaces of the ancient, mystic capital" (015-040). The party visited the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] along an avenue of six hundred sphinxes — "six rows of a hundred sphinxes, the ever-silent incarnations of [[philosophy|wisdom]]" (015-028) — and witnessed the [[apis|Apis bull]], the living incarnation of [[osiris|Osiris]], in its sacred secos. A young, cheerful priest who spoke Latin guided them, sharing the details of the [[apis|Apis]] cult: the god incarnates in a bull for twenty-five years, after which the bull is drowned, embalmed, and a new incarnation is sought throughout Egypt (015-040).

Memphis appears in the narrative as a place of haunting, mystic significance — "thrice-sacred Memphis, profaned long centuries ago" by the Persian [[cambyses|Cambyses]], now "dead and sinking in the devouring sands of the desert" (015-029). The narrator describes the city's desolation in apocalyptic terms: "The city would be swallowed up by the sands! That past would sink back into the lap of the earth!" (015-029). Yet even in this terminal decay, Memphis retains its power over the imagination — the travellers are moved not despite the ruin but because of it.

## Memphis and the Aesthetic of Ruin

Memphis provides the novel's purest expression of what might be called an aesthetic of ruin. Unlike [[alexandria|Alexandria]], which is alive with commerce and spectacle, Memphis is a city of absences — its squares empty, its pylons heaven-high but tenantless, its sphinxes silent (015-028). The novel's descriptive language shifts from the sensory richness of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] (wine, perfume, noise, colour) to the austere vocabulary of scale and silence (Cyclopean, endless, devouring, sunk). This is not a failure of description but a deliberate calibration: Memphis is the point where Egypt's past becomes visible not as heritage but as loss.

The contrast with [[thebes|Thebes]] is instructive. At [[thebes|Thebes]], [[lucius|Lucius]] finds a living priest and a living god. At Memphis, the travellers find only the cult of [[apis|Apis]] — maintained "only because all the travellers came to see the sacred bull" (015-040), a religious practice reduced to tourist attraction. If [[thebes|Thebes]] represents Egyptian religion's surviving heart, Memphis represents its commodified surface — the difference between pilgrimage and sightseeing.

## Historical Context

In the 1st century AD, Memphis was well past its zenith. Already superseded by [[alexandria|Alexandria]] as Egypt's primary city, it retained religious importance through the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] and the [[apis|Apis]] cult. The population had declined drastically from its Old Kingdom peak. [[strabo|Strabo]], a contemporary geographer (c. 64 BC – 24 AD), described Memphis as Egypt's second city after [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The temple complexes, though decaying, continued to function as pilgrimage sites well into the Roman period. The city was eventually abandoned after the Arab conquest in the 7th century AD, its stone reused for the construction of Cairo.

## Key Passages

> "The old Egyptian capital lay Cyclopean, like some extinct monster, with heavy lines of squat, bleaching sanctuaries and, on the river, a portico of giant pylons. Behind these age-old, massive buildings the Pyramids showed spectrally." (015-021)

> "Ye gods, what dimensions! What lines, what spaciousness of deserted squares, what heaven-high rows of pylons! The Serapeum yonder, at the endless end of an avenue of six hundred sphinxes, six rows of a hundred sphinxes, the ever-silent incarnations of wisdom, the lion-women who were the wisdom of Neith!" (015-028)

> "This was Memphis, thrice-sacred Memphis, profaned long centuries ago and now dead and sinking in the devouring sands of the desert, which approached from the west, out yonder! The city would be swallowed up by the sands! That past would sink back into the lap of the earth!" (015-029)

> "For in the huge dead city of Memphis, inhabited by hardly a few thousands, who were dwarfed and lost in the spaces of the ancient, mystic capital of ancient, mystic Egypt, in truth the worship of Apis was still maintained only because all the travellers came to see the sacred bull." (015-040)
