## Overview

[[alexander-the-great|Alexander III]] of Macedon — Alexander the Great — was the conqueror who founded [[alexandria|Alexandria]] in 331 BCE and whose body was interred in the [[soma|Soma]], the royal mausoleum of the Ptolemies. Declared the son of the god [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Ra]] by the oracle at the Siwa Oasis, Alexander occupied a unique position in Hellenistic-Egyptian religious imagination as both historical figure and divine being. In *The Tour*, his presence radiates through the entire journey: from the travellers' visit to his crystal coffin in [[alexandria|Alexandria]], to [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s recounting of the crows that guided him through the desert, to the climactic scene at the ruined sanctuary where [[lucius|Lucius]] beholds the fading of the god Alexander once crossed whirlwinds and sandstorms to seek.

## In the Narrative

The travellers' first encounter with Alexander comes during their tour of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], when [[caleb|Caleb]] leads them to [[soma|the Soma]], the burial place of the Ptolemies. "These distinguished strangers wish to see the burial-places of the Ptolemies," [[caleb|Caleb]] announces, adding that they "will also be interested in the tomb of Alexander the Great" [003-070]. The priest in charge shows them the crystal coffin containing Alexander's remains and recounts the dramatic history of how [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]], son of Lagus, seized the body from Perdiccas, who had been transporting it from Babylon to Macedon [003-078].

The priest details the theft: Perdiccas was bringing the body back with the royal family — Alexander's pregnant widow Roxana and her young children — when [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] intercepted the procession. Perdiccas perished at the hands of his own soldiers on an island surrounded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]]'s troops. Alexander's body was carried to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and buried in a massive gold sarcophagus, later stolen by a pretender, [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] Parisactus, but the body itself was rescued and laid in the crystal coffin the travellers now behold [003-078]. The priest solemnly intones: "Here lies Alexander the Great. The warrior, the conqueror, the king of kings, the son of the sacred sun-god, [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Ra]], descended upon earth" [003-081].

[[lucius|Lucius]] reflects on the contrast between the legendary conqueror and the chrysalis-like remains before him: "Was this chrysalis all that remained of the great Alexander, whom the [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] had declared to be the son of [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Ra]], son of the sun-god?" [003-079]. [[caleb|Caleb]], jaded from having conducted many distinguished foreigners through the tomb — including "Kardusi of Persia and Baabab of Mesopotamia" — remains indifferent, with "an incredulous little laugh at the genuineness of Alexander's body" [003-080].

Later, during discussions of the [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] recounts how Alexander made his way from Paraetonium through the desert to the Siwa Oasis: "Two crows flew beside the great Alexander and guided him to the oasis" [020-014]. At the temple itself, the travellers learn that "the deity addressed [[alexander-the-great|Alexander of Macedon]] with the sound of his lowing voice and told him, in words plainly audible to the king and all his following, that he was the son of the Sun, the son of [[ammon-ra|Jupiter Ammon]]-Ra" [021-013].

The narrative closes the [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n circle in the ruined sanctuary, where [[lucius|Lucius]] beholds the fading of the forgotten Egyptian god — "whom, centuries ago, [[alexander-the-great|Alexander of Macedon]] had travelled through whirlwinds and sandstorms to seek" [021-015]. The image of the conqueror who once braved the desert to hear the god's voice frames the novel's central meditation: the death of the old gods and the uncertain dawn of whatever comes next.

## Key Passages

> "These are the sacred remains of the great Alexander. History teaches us that Ptolemy, son of Lagus, took the body of the hero and conqueror from Perdiccas, who was bringing it back from Babylon to Macedon." [003-078]

> "Here lies Alexander the Great. The warrior, the conqueror, the king of kings, the son of the sacred sun-god, Ammon-Ra, descended upon earth." [003-081]

> "Was this chrysalis all that remained of the great Alexander, whom the oracle of Ammon had declared to be the son of Ammon-Ra, son of the sun-god?" [003-079]

> "When Alexander founded Alexandria, the oracle of Ammon was the most celebrated in Egypt; and Callisthenes and Plutarch relate how the great Macedonian started from Paraetonium, on the coast, to make his way through the desert by way of the oasis. Two crows flew beside the great Alexander and guided him to the oasis." [020-014]

> "The deity addressed Alexander of Macedon with the sound of his lowing voice and told him, in words plainly audible to the king and all his following, that he was the son of the Sun, the son of Jupiter Ammon-Ra." [021-013]
