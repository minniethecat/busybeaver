# Tarrar

## Overview

Tarrar is [[lucius|Lucius]]'s young Libyan slave, a minor but vivid presence in the household. Initially introduced in a state of injury — bandaged and recovering — he reappears at the [[canopus|Canopus]] night in a dramatic transformation: dressed in a "Libyan festive garment" with a girdle and headdress of feathers, looking "like a little cannibal" (007-041). Tarrar embodies the novel's attention to the full human texture of a Roman household, where even the smallest slaves have moments of vivid presence.

## In the Narrative

Tarrar first appears when [[lucius|Lucius]] summons [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]: "Tarrar … find [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] for me" (001-009). He is later one of the slaves serving at the [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n luncheon, alongside "three girl-slaves" (003-102). His most memorable scene is his transformation for the [[canopus|Canopus]] journey: "Tarrar, no longer bandaged, looked like a little savage: he wore his Libyan festive garment; a girdle of feathers hung round his waist; he was crowned with a headdress of feathers" (007-040-041). [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] reacts with comic alarm: "Great gods, Tarrar! … You look like a little cannibal! You frighten me!" (007-041).

Tarrar's jubilant announcement — "We are going to [[canopus|Canopus]], my lord, tonight!" (007-042) — sets the festive mood for the novel's climactic sequence.

## Key Passages

> "Tarrar, no longer bandaged, looked like a little savage: he wore his Libyan festive garment; a girdle of feathers hung round his waist; he was crowned with a headdress of feathers. And he stood grinning." (007-040-041)

> "We are going to Canopus, my lord, tonight!" cried Tarrar, jubilantly. (007-042)

## Relationships

- **[[catullus|Catullus]]**: The uncle who reacts to Tarrar's costume with comic alarm
- **[[cora|Cora]]**: Fellow slave, both part of the household travelling to [[canopus|Canopus]]
