# Pharos

## Overview

Pharos was a small island off the coast of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], made famous by the monumental lighthouse built upon it in the third century BCE. The Lighthouse of Alexandria, standing over 100 metres tall, was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World and the symbol of the city. In *The Tour*, Pharos is the first landmark the travellers pass as their quadrireme enters the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]], marking the threshold between the Mediterranean sea and the Egyptian world.

## In the Narrative

The approach to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] is described with the Pharos as the gateway: "The ship had glided past the monumental, marble, nine-storeyed Pharos into the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]]" (003-001). The lighthouse is both a physical landmark and a symbolic boundary — beyond it lies [[alexandria|Alexandria]], Egypt, the east, and everything that is "very different from Rome in every way" (003-041).

The [[heptastadium|Heptastadium]], the great promenade-jetty that connects Pharos to the mainland, is described in the city tour: "The [[heptastadium|Heptastadium]], [[heptastadium|the promenade-jetty]] which stretched out to unite the city with the island of Pharos, whence the lighthouse took its name" (003-003). The causeway divides the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]] from the harbour of Eunostus.

## Historical Context

The Lighthouse of Alexandria was built during the reign of [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] II Philadelphus (r. 280–247 BCE) and designed by the Greek architect Sostratus of Cnidus. It stood approximately 100–130 metres tall and was constructed in three tiers: a square base, an octagonal middle section, and a cylindrical top where the beacon fire burned. The lighthouse was damaged by earthquakes over the centuries and was finally destroyed in the 14th century. The name "Pharos" became the origin of the word for lighthouse in many languages (French *phare*, Italian *faro*, etc.).
