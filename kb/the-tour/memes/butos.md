## Overview

Butos is a lake in the [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]] region of Egypt, notable in the narrative as the site of an oracle and temple dedicated to the goddess Latona. Located on an island within the lake, the Butos oracle was one of the sacred destinations that drew pilgrims and seekers of divine knowledge in the ancient world.

## Historical Background

The name Butos derives from the ancient Egyptian city of Buto (Egyptian: Per-Wadjet, "House of Wadjet"), located in the north-western [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]]. Buto was one of the most ancient cult centres of Lower Egypt, predating the unification of the Two Lands, and served as the principal shrine of the cobra goddess Wadjet — the protective deity of Lower Egypt whose image adorned the royal uraeus. Archaeological excavations at Tell el-Fara'in (the modern site of Buto) have revealed continuous occupation from the Predynastic period (c. 3200 BCE) through to the Roman era, with temple complexes, administrative buildings, and an extensive cemetery attesting to the city's enduring religious and political importance [PN:001]. The Greeks identified Wadjet with their goddess Leto (Latin: Latona), and the temple and oracle in the Delta region came to be associated with this syncretic figure. [[oracle|Oracle]]s in Egypt typically operated through the movement of the god's cult statue during festival processions, with the deity answering yes-or-no questions by inclining toward or away from the petitioner — a practice distinct from the ecstatic prophecy associated with Greek oracular sites such as Delphi [PN:002].

## Religious Significance

The oracle of Latona at Butos was one of several oracular sites in Egypt that continued to function under Roman rule. Unlike the great state [[oracle|oracles]] consulted by kings and emperors, local [[oracle|oracles]] such as Butos served a more intimate function: they were approached by ordinary people seeking guidance on personal matters — marriage, travel, business, illness. The setting on an island within a lake added to the oracle's mystique; the journey across the water to reach the shrine was itself a ritual act, separating the profane world from the sacred precinct. Narrative descriptions of the lake as "wide and mystic and gleaming" under moonlight capture the numinous quality that such sites held for ancient visitors [PN:003].

## In the Narrative

Butos appears during the moonlit journey through the flooded Delta in Chapter XIV. As the great barge glides through the silent, silver-lit waterways, "there lay the lake of Butos, wide and mystic and gleaming" [014-003], its waters illuminated by the full moon. The scene is described with a dreamlike quality — "island emerged after island" amid the palms and shrines [014-003]. Later, in Chapter XV, the narrator specifies that the oracle is "oracle, of Latona at Butos, on an island in the lake" [015-002], confirming its identity as a specific sacred site. At dawn, "on the waters of Lake Butos the first lotus-blooms opened" [014-082], marking the transition from night to day.
