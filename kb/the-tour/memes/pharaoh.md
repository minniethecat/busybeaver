## Overview

Pharaoh was the title of the rulers of ancient Egypt, who were regarded not merely as monarchs but as divine beings — living incarnations of [[horus|Horus]] and intermediaries between the gods and humanity. In *The Tour*, the pharaohs represent the antiquity and continuity of Egyptian civilisation. The travellers encounter their monuments — the pyramids they built as tombs and the palaces they inhabited — and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] recounts the history of specific pharaohs, including Amenophis, in connection with the biblical narratives of Moses and [[joseph|Joseph]] [016-012].

## In the Narrative

The pharaohs are invoked as part of the deep history that permeates the Egyptian landscape. When the travellers contemplate the age of Egypt, they imagine how "the Pharaohs themselves had passed in sacred procession" through the same spaces centuries before [015-029] — a vision that collapses the temporal distance between the Roman present and the pharaonic past. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] points out "the old palaces of the Pharaohs" [016-008], using the visible ruins to connect the present journey to the distant past. The physical remnants of pharaonic power — crumbling walls, fallen columns — become a visible index of time's passage.

The tutor also discusses "Pharaoh Amenophis, who was the father of the princess who found Moses" [016-012], weaving Egyptian royal history into the biblical narrative. This Amenophis is likely Amenhotep III, the great builder of the New Kingdom, whose monuments were still visible throughout Egypt in the Roman period. The pharaohs thus serve as markers of temporal depth — reminders that Egypt was ancient even to the Romans visiting it.

## The Divine Kingship

The pharaonic ideology of divine kingship was one of the most enduring political and religious concepts in human history. The pharaoh was not merely a king ruling by divine right but was himself a god — the living [[horus|Horus]], son of [[osiris|Osiris]]. This theological status carried profound implications: the pharaoh was responsible for maintaining *maat* (cosmic order), and his ritual actions — temple offerings, foundation ceremonies, sacred processions — were understood to sustain the universe itself. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s narration of "the Pharaohs themselves" passing "in sacred procession" [015-029] captures this ritual dimension of pharaonic rule.

## Historical Context

The title "pharaoh" (from Egyptian *per-aa*, "great house") originally referred to the royal palace and only later came to designate the king himself. By the Roman period, Egypt had been ruled by the Ptolemies (who adopted pharaonic titles and imagery) and then by Roman emperors, who were depicted as pharaohs on Egyptian temple reliefs. The Amenophis referenced in the novel is likely Amenhotep III (c. 1390–1353 BCE), one of the great builders of the New Kingdom and the father of Akhenaten. The identification of Amenophis as the father of "the princess who found Moses" [016-012] reflects the later Jewish and Christian tradition that sought to synchronise biblical and Egyptian chronologies.
