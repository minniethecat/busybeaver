# Magic

## Overview

In the Roman world of the early first century CE, magic (*magia*, *ars magica*) occupied a fluid space between religion, philosophy, medicine, and popular superstition. Educated Romans like [[lucius|Lucius]] studied magic alongside astronomy and philosophy as part of a comprehensive pursuit of knowledge. In *The Tour*, magic is named explicitly as "the favourite passion of that period" (003-020), and the novel's Egyptian setting — long associated in the Greek and Roman imagination with ancient magical [[philosophy|wisdom]] — provides a natural context for exploring the intersection of the mystical and the rational.

## In the Narrative

Magic is introduced as one of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s intellectual pursuits. The narrator notes that after briefly participating in the orgies of young Roman nobles, [[lucius|Lucius]] "had soon devoted himself to many branches of science, to astronomy in particular, philosophy, magic, the favourite passion of that period" (003-020). This places magic on equal footing with astronomy and philosophy as a legitimate field of inquiry, reflecting the Roman elite's genuine interest in esoteric knowledge.

The Egyptian setting of the novel amplifies the magical atmosphere. Egypt was regarded in the Roman imagination as the homeland of magic — the land of [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]], of hieroglyphic [[philosophy|wisdom]], of priests who could interpret dreams and read the stars. When [[lucius|Lucius]] goes to the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] to seek a divinely sent dream, he is participating in a practice that blurs the line between religion and magic: incubation is a religious ritual, but its goal — obtaining hidden knowledge through supernatural means — is essentially magical.

The novel's magical worldview is most concentrated in the marketplace scene, where the Indian vendors display "tame, trained little snakes, which bring luck and blissful dreams" (004-021). These creatures — at once exotic merchandise and magical [[amulets|talismans]] — embody the commodification of magic in Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The dream-ritual itself can be seen as the highest form of magical practice: a systematic attempt to access divine knowledge through ritual means.

## Magic as Character Motivation

[[lucius|Lucius]]'s interest in magic (003-020) is not incidental to his character but constitutive of it. Unlike his Roman peers who dissipate their energies in "mad orgies," [[lucius|Lucius]] channels his inherited wealth and restless intellect into the systematic pursuit of hidden knowledge. Magic, astronomy, and philosophy form a triad of inquiry that defines the novel's protagonist as a seeker — someone for whom the visible world is never sufficient. His journey to Egypt is, in one sense, a magical pilgrimage: the pursuit of the occult [[philosophy|wisdom]] that the ancient land was believed to harbour.

This framing distinguishes [[lucius|Lucius]] from the simple tourist. He does not visit Egypt merely to see the sights but to access the power that the sights represent. The distinction is implicit but crucial: [[caleb|Caleb]]'s guided tour moves through the exoteric Egypt of monuments and spectacles; [[lucius|Lucius]]'s magical interests draw him toward the esoteric Egypt of hidden chambers, oracular dreams, and divine secrets.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Magic in the Roman Empire encompassed a wide range of practices: curse tablets (*defixiones*), love spells, healing charms, divination, astrology, and the summoning of spirits. The Roman state periodically attempted to suppress magic — the Twelve Tables prohibited harmful incantations, and Augustus banned magical texts — but these laws were inconsistently enforced, and magical practice flourished at all levels of society.

Egypt held a special place in the Roman magical imagination. The Greek Magical Papyri (Papyri Graecae Magicae), a corpus of magical texts from Roman Egypt, reveal a syncretic magical tradition combining Greek, Egyptian, Jewish, and Babylonian elements. The figure of [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]], the legendary Egyptian sage, was thought to have authored the Hermetic corpus of philosophical and magical texts.
