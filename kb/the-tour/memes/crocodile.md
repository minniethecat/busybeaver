## Overview

[[nile|The Nile]] crocodile (*Crocodylus niloticus*) was both feared and venerated in ancient Egypt. As the embodiment of the god Sobek (called Suchos in Greek), sacred crocodiles were kept in temple lakes, adorned with jewellery, and fed by priests. In *The Tour*, the travellers visit a sacred lake where a living crocodile named Such is maintained as an object of worship, offering a striking illustration of Egyptian theriolatry — animal worship — that fascinated and sometimes scandalised Roman observers. Along the Nile, the travellers encounter "[[cities-and-settlements|towns]] at which crocodiles, fishes and falcons were worshipped" [024-031], revealing the pervasiveness of animal cults across the Egyptian landscape.

## In the Narrative

The encounter with the sacred crocodile occurs at a temple precinct with "a sacred lake where the sacred crocodile, named Such, was held in veneration" [024-006]. The crocodile is described in terms that suggest its role as an oracle or divine intermediary, and a story is recounted about a Persian who was swallowed by the creature — an event the priest recounts with evident satisfaction at the divine retribution visited upon a foreigner [024-011].

The priest who guides the travellers explains that the bull-calf and the crocodile "were after all the only ones who had preserved their original forms completely pure" [024-016] — a reference to Egyptian beliefs about the purity of certain [[sacred-animal|sacred animals]] as vessels for divine presence. This theological explanation does little to comfort [[catullus|Catullus]], who declares bluntly: "honestly, I'm not going to feed any more sacred crocodiles, nor any sacred goats or cats or beetles either" [024-017]. His exasperation mounts as the tour continues through "every conceivable sort of crocodile, goat, dog, rat, falcon and baboon" [024-022], culminating in his weary admission: "I'm sick of falcons, fishes and crocodiles which are gods" [024-032].

## Historical Context

The cult of Sobek was widespread in Egypt, with major temple centres at Kom Ombo and in the Fayyum region (called Crocodilopolis by the Greeks). Sacred crocodiles were mummified after death and buried in special necropolises. The Greek geographer [[strabo|Strabo]] described the Fayyum crocodile cult in detail, noting that the animals were fed bread, meat, and wine by pilgrims. Roman authors like Juvenal and Pliny the Elder expressed a mixture of fascination and contempt for [[sacred-animal|Egyptian animal worship]]. Intriguingly, not all Egyptians venerated the crocodile — those who "worship [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]" actively "hunt the crocodile, which they destroy wherever they can" [024-020], reflecting the complex and locally variable nature of Egyptian religious practice.

## Catullus and the Limits of Curiosity

[[catullus|Catullus]]'s refusal to continue feeding [[sacred-animal|sacred animals]] marks a turning point in the travellers' engagement with Egyptian religion. While [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] maintains scholarly detachment, [[catullus|Catullus]] embodies Roman scepticism pushed past its limit. His enumeration of the animals — culminating in "I'm sick of falcons, fishes and crocodiles which are gods" [024-032] — expresses a weariness that the novel itself courts: how much Egyptian detail can the reader absorb before sharing [[catullus|Catullus]]'s fatigue? This tension between scholarly curiosity and sensory overwhelm is one of the novel's central dynamics.
