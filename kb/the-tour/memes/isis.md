# Isis

## Overview

Isis is one of the most significant goddesses in the ancient Egyptian pantheon and, by the Roman period of *The Tour*, one of the most widely worshipped [[egyptian-deities|deities]] across the Mediterranean world. She is the sister-wife of [[osiris]], the mother of [[horus]], and the divine embodiment of magic, healing, motherhood, and faithful devotion. Her myth — the search for and reassembly of the murdered [[osiris|Osiris]]'s body — was one of the most emotionally resonant narratives of antiquity. In *The Tour*, Isis appears as a living presence in [[alexandria|Alexandrian]] religious life, invoked by merchants, priests, and ordinary worshippers alike. Her mystery cult, with its veiled statues and nocturnal rites, parallels and complements that of [[serapis]], together forming the twin pillars of Egyptian religion as depicted in the novel.

## In the Narrative

Isis is woven throughout the everyday fabric of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] as depicted in the novel. On the bustling quay of the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]], "priests of [[serapis|Serapis]] and Isis" mingle with the crowd of merchants and soldiers (003-005). Master [[ghizla]], the [[saba|Saba]]ean guesthouse owner, praises his [[lake-mareotis|Mareotis]] wine as "fragrant as the own lotus of our Lady Isis, blessed be her name!" (003-018), showing how the goddess's name suffuses ordinary commercial speech.

A major episode centred on Isis occurs in chapter XIV, where the travellers visit the temple of Isis-Neith (014-008). The temple is the setting for the Feast of Isis, a nocturnal ritual commemorating "the night on which Isis had collected the scattered limbs of her brother and husband [[osiris|Osiris]], murdered and quartered by [[typhon|Typhon]]" (014-031). The description of the veiled statue of Isis within the holy of holies (014-041) provides a parallel to the veiled [[serapis|Serapis]] statue at [[canopus]], linking the mystery cults of both [[egyptian-deities|deities]].

The theological pairing of Isis with [[serapis|Serapis]] appears throughout: "[[horus|Sacred Horus]], son of [[osiris|Osiris]] and Isis, the radiant redeemer of mankind" (003-073), and the priest offers comfort by invoking her myth: "There is comfort. Isis seeking for [[osiris|Osiris]] recovered all the pieces of his mangled body" (010-047). At the feast, worshippers adore Isis "with hands uplifted to the moon" (014-075), connecting the goddess to the night sky.

The initiate's theology is expressed in one of the novel's most mystical lines: "And great Isis has appeared to them as the splendour of truth, for she lifted her veil for their delight, so that they saw her. Life is but a dream, death is a bridge and eternity is life" (003-071). This passage encapsulates the Isiac promise — that the veil may be lifted and truth revealed — a theme that resonates with [[lucius]]'s own quest for meaning after the loss of [[ilia]].

## Historical/Cultural Context

The cult of Isis originated in Egypt but spread throughout the Hellenistic and Roman worlds, becoming one of the most popular mystery religions of the early Imperial period. Temples (*Isea*) existed in Rome, Pompeii, Athens, and across the provinces. Isis was identified with numerous other goddesses — Demeter, [[aphrodite]], [[neith]] — a syncretism reflected in Couperus's use of the compound name "Isis-Neith" (014-008).

The Isis myth provided a powerful narrative of devotion, suffering, and ultimate triumph. Her annual festivals involved dramatic reenactments of the search for [[osiris|Osiris]], nocturnal rites, and displays of the goddess's veiled statue — all features depicted in the novel's treatment of both the Isis and [[serapis|Serapis]] cults.
