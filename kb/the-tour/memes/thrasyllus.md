# Thrasyllus

## Overview

Thrasyllus is [[lucius|Lucius]]'s Greek tutor and freedman, a scholarly companion who accompanies the young Roman on his Egyptian journey. Well-read in the geographers and historians of the Hellenistic world — [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]], [[strabo|Strabo]], and others — Thrasyllus serves as the novel's voice of learning, providing historical and cultural context for the sites the travellers visit. His relationship to [[lucius|Lucius]] is both that of teacher and friend: he eats at [[lucius|Lucius]]'s table but, as a freedman, sits on a stool rather than reclining on a couch.

## In the Narrative

Thrasyllus is introduced on the ship, summoned by [[lucius|Lucius]] to provide comfort through conversation. When [[lucius|Lucius]] calls for him, the slave [[tarrar|Tarrar]] fetches "Thrasyllus, the Greek freedman, [[lucius|Lucius]]' tutor" (001-010). Immediately, Thrasyllus demonstrates his learning, discoursing on the approach to Egypt: "We are now sailing towards Egypt, the birthplace of all [[philosophy|wisdom]]" (002-002).

His role as cultural interpreter is most evident during the [[alexandria|Alexandria]] tour. When [[caleb|Caleb]] claims [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] was founded by "the beautiful [[cleopatra|Cleopatra]]," Thrasyllus quietly corrects him: "It was founded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] the First" (003-055). He provides the catalogue of [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]]'s achievements, naming "Euclid, Erasistratus and Diophantus; then there were the poets Theocritus, Aratus, Callimachus; and among critics Aristarchus; and among philosophers more than I could name" (003-057).

At the Sphinx and pyramids, Thrasyllus's philosophical nature deepens. He is the first to identify the Sphinx with [[neith|Neith]]: "The great Sphinx, the immense [[neith|Neith]], the ever-silent [[philosophy|wisdom]]!" (015-023). His meditation on vanity — "Thy [[philosophy|wisdom]] alone, O [[neith|Neith]], is needful to man!" (017-017) — represents the novel's most sustained philosophical reflection.

Physically, Thrasyllus rides a donkey during the [[alexandria|Alexandria]] tour — a detail that distinguishes his humble status from [[lucius|Lucius]]'s litter and [[caleb|Caleb]]'s showy horse (003-026). He is moderate in all things: "Thrasyllus was moderate as always" at the meal (003-104).

## Historical Context

Thrasyllus is likely fictional, though his name evokes the historical Thrasyllus of Mendes, an Egyptian Greek scholar and astrologer who became a friend of the emperor [[tiberius|Tiberius]]. The character represents the type of the learned Greek freedman who served as tutor (*paedagogus*) to wealthy Roman youths. [[crocodile|Such]] figures were central to the transmission of Greek learning in Roman society.

## Key Passages

> "We are now sailing towards Egypt, the birthplace of all wisdom. … Egypt is the oldest country in the world, the mother of all civilisation, the source of all knowledge." (002-002)

> "The Museum has produced great scholars, such as Euclid, Erasistratus and Diophantus; then there were the poets Theocritus, Aratus, Callimachus; and among critics Aristarchus; and among philosophers more than I could name." (003-057)

> "The great Sphinx, the immense Neith, the ever-silent wisdom! Next, the pyramids, the tombs of Cheops, Chephren and Mycerinus!" (015-023)

## Relationships

- **[[lucius|Lucius]]**: Thrasyllus's pupil and patron; he treats [[lucius|Lucius]] with loyal affection and intellectual companionship
- **[[catullus|Catullus]]**: [[lucius|Lucius]]'s uncle; Thrasyllus engages with him in good-natured conversation, balancing respect with quiet superiority in matters of learning
