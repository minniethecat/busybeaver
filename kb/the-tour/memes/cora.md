# Cora

## Overview

Cora is a Greek slave from the island of Cos, serving in [[lucius|Lucius]]'s household as a harpist and singer. Her voice and her music provide the emotional soundtrack to the journey, from the opening *Hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* sung on the ship at night to the procession hymn at the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] in [[canopus|Canopus]]. Cora's music bridges the novel's worlds — Greek, Roman, and Egyptian — and her songs of longing and love mirror [[lucius|Lucius]]'s own emotional state.

## In the Narrative

Cora is introduced on the ship at night: "On a low chair sat Cora, the Greek slave from Cos. Her harp stood beside her" (002-007). She has been singing hymns to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] for three months, since the voyage began, as [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] notes: "She has been singing hymns to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] for three months now, ever since we sailed out of the [[baiae|Bay of Baiae]]" (002-024).

Her *Hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* in chapter VII is the novel's most sustained poetic passage. The lyrics — "Mother of Eros, hear thy slave! … Child of the foam, great goddess of love, [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], look down from above!" (007-001–002) — are sung as the barge glides past the temple of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite Arsinoe]] toward [[canopus|Canopus]]. Cora's voice is described as "clear voice, tinkling as though with little golden bells, at first soft and hushed, rose throbbing in passion and then broke like a crystal ray and melted in mournfulness and plaintive prayer" (007-031).

Cora's personal feelings are briefly glimpsed. She is diffident in conversation with [[catullus|Catullus]], declining to call him "uncle" as [[ilia|Ilia]] did: "I am not [[ilia|Ilia]], my lord" (007-038). At [[canopus|Canopus]], she becomes childlike with excitement: "We are going to [[canopus|Canopus]], we are going to [[canopus|Canopus]]! … I am to sing the Hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] before the temple!" (007-057), and she also sings the hymn to [[serapis|Serapis]] during the procession (009-005).

## Historical Context

The island of Cos, Cora's birthplace, was famous in antiquity for its fine textiles (the "Coan vestments" mentioned by Roman poets) and as a centre of the cult of Asclepius. Greek slave women skilled in music were highly valued in Roman households, where they performed at symposia and religious occasions. Cora's role as both entertainer and participant in religious ritual reflects historical practice.

## Key Passages

> "Cora's song rang through the falling night. Her clear voice, tinkling as though with little golden bells, at first soft and hushed, rose throbbing in passion and then broke like a crystal ray and melted in mournfulness and plaintive prayer." (007-031)

> "She has been singing hymns to Aphrodite for three months now, ever since we sailed out of the Bay of Baiae. Her voice is as beautiful as her person, to divert Lucius a little." (002-024)

> "I am not Ilia, my lord," said Cora, smiling. (007-038)

## Relationships

- **[[lucius|Lucius]]**: Cora's master; her music is performed for his entertainment and consolation
- **[[catullus|Catullus]]**: Treats Cora with paternal kindness, invites her to call him "uncle"
- **[[ilia|Ilia]]**: The absent slave whose place Cora cannot fill, though she occupies a similar role
