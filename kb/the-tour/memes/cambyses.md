# Cambyses

Cambyses II was the Achaemenid king of Persia who conquered Egypt in 525 BCE, deposing the last Saite pharaoh [[psammetichus|Psammetichus I]]II. In *The Tour*, he appears only as a remembered destructive force — the conqueror whose sacrilegious hordes destroyed [[memphis|Memphis]] and [[thebes|Thebes]], reducing the greatest cities of antiquity to the ruins that [[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions encounter centuries later.

## In the Narrative

Cambyses is first mentioned during the travellers' visit to [[memphis|Memphis]]. As [[caleb|Caleb]] leads the party along the sphinx avenue, the narrator evokes the great figures who have passed this way:

> "Here Moses had walked and Hermes Trismegistus; here Joseph had wandered, the interpreter of dreams; here, lastly, Cambyses, with his Persian hordes, had ridden sacrilegiously! This was Memphis, thrice-sacred Memphis, profaned long centuries ago and now dead and sinking in the devouring sands of the desert." (015-029)

Later, at [[thebes|Thebes]], the destruction is even more vivid. The great city of a hundred gates lies in ruin, and the narrator explicitly attributes this to Cambyses:

> "The gloom of a fatally waning glory lay like a haze over all this architectural immensity, which Cambyses, with his Persian hordes, had in past ages destroyed beyond repair, as with gigantic hammer-strokes." (024-023)

Cambyses thus functions as a historical symbol of sacrilege and irreversible destruction — the antithesis of the [[philosophy|wisdom]] and reverence embodied by [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]], Moses, and [[joseph|Joseph]]. His Persian horde is the force that severed Egypt from its pharaonic past.

At [[thebes|Thebes]], the travellers' local guide — a man whose livelihood depends on catering to visiting Persians — offers a glimpse of how the memory of Cambyses persists even in Egypt's commercial present. When Persian lords arrive at the Nile bank, the guide bows deeply before them, performing elaborate salaams (024-009–012). The irony is sharp: the descendants of the destroyers are now the honoured guests, while the descendants of the destroyed earn their bread by serving them.

## Historical Context

Cambyses II (r. 530–522 BCE) was the son of Cyrus the Great, founder of the Achaemenid Empire. He conquered Egypt at the Battle of [[pelusium|Pelusium]] (525 BCE), ending the 26th Dynasty and incorporating Egypt into the Persian Empire as a satrapy. Ancient sources, particularly [[herodotus|Herodotus]], portray him as a mad tyrant who desecrated Egyptian temples, killed the [[apis|Apis bull]], and mocked Egyptian religion — though modern scholarship suggests some of these accounts were exaggerated by later Egyptian and Greek propagandists seeking to portray Persian rule as illegitimate. What is certain is that the Persian conquest marked a decisive break in Egypt's history, ending the last period of native Egyptian rule until the 28th Dynasty briefly restored it. Cambyses died in 522 BCE under mysterious circumstances — possibly by accident, possibly by suicide — while returning to Persia to suppress a revolt.

## Legacy and Assessment

In the novel's moral geography, Cambyses occupies the role of the archetypal foreign destroyer — the conqueror who, unlike [[alexandria|Alexander the Great]], brought not fusion and learning but ruin and desecration. The narrator's choice of the word "sacrilegiously" (*sacrilegiously* in the original) is deliberate: Cambyses's crime is not merely military destruction but a violation of the sacred order. His Persian hordes serve as a narrative counterweight to the reverent, if melancholy, tourism of the Roman travellers — they represent what happens when the foreigner comes not to learn but to dominate.

This symbolic role is reinforced by the contrasting treatment of Persian visitors in the narrative present. While Cambyses destroyed [[thebes|Thebes]], the modern Persian lords who visit the ruins are treated with obsequious deference by the local guides — a bitter historical irony that the novel presents without explicit commentary, allowing the reader to register the moral distance between ancient destruction and present accommodation.
