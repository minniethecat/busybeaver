# Lucius

## Overview

Publius Lucius Sabinus is the protagonist of *The Tour*, a wealthy young Roman nobleman who travels to Egypt seeking distraction from grief. The sole heir of various relatives, Lucius has devoted himself to astronomy, philosophy, magic, sculpture, and collecting antiquities — a man of refined tastes and genuine intellectual curiosity. His journey is motivated by the mysterious disappearance of [[ilia|Ilia]], his beloved slave, who vanished from his villa at [[baiae|Baiae]]. Throughout the novel, Lucius oscillates between aesthetic appreciation of Egypt's marvels and the consuming grief of loss, making him a vehicle for the novel's meditation on love, death, and the search for meaning.

## In the Narrative

Lucius is introduced on the prow of his quadrireme, the *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]*, as it approaches [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. "He lay on the prow in a languid, limp attitude, his head resting against the silver statue of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], the patroness of his ship" (001-006), his posture communicating the grief that has consumed him for three months since [[ilia|Ilia]]'s disappearance. His full name is given when the slave [[tarrar|Tarrar]] addresses him: "Publius Lucius Sabinus" (001-010).

The narrator provides a detailed portrait: "He was a tall, comely fellow, with an athletic frame developed by wrestling exercise; and his dark eyes, though now veiled with melancholy and longing, gleamed with a deep spark of intelligence" (003-020). Lucius's intellectual breadth is notable: he has studied astronomy, philosophy, and magic — "the favourite passion of that period" (003-020) — and as a collector he "loved everything that was beautiful: pictures and statues, old coins and old glass; and his Etruscan antiquities were famous all over Rome" (003-020).

Lucius's journey through Egypt is structured as a series of ever-deepening encounters. In [[alexandria|Alexandria]], he is the sightseeing Roman tourist, visiting [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]], [[soma|the Soma]] tombs, and the [[serapeum|Serapeum]]. At [[canopus|Canopus]], he becomes a pilgrim, dressing in Egyptian finery — "an Egyptian robe of striped byssus, with a border of [[hieroglyph|hieroglyphics]] worked in heavy embroidery and precious stones" (007-044) — and participating in the sacred dream-ritual. In the desert, he becomes a seeker, contemplating the Sphinx and confronting the vastness of the Egyptian landscape.

His emotional arc is defined by the tension between engagement with the world and the undertow of grief. At [[soma|the Soma]], seeing Alexander's [[mummification|mummy]], he is immediately transported back to his loss: "Death! … Death! … She is perhaps dead. … She is drowned, perhaps, in the sea … and we shall never recover her exquisite body, to embalm it" (003-090). This pattern — wonder at Egypt followed by collapse into grief — defines his character throughout.

## Historical Context

Lucius is a fictional character, but he represents the type of wealthy, educated Roman aristocrat of the early Imperial period (the reign of [[tiberius|Tiberius]], 14–37 CE). His intellectual interests — astronomy, philosophy, magic, art collecting — reflect the genuine pursuits of Roman elites in this period. His journey to Egypt follows a well-established pattern of Roman tourism and pilgrimage, with [[alexandria|Alexandria]], the [[pyramid|Pyramid]]s, and the oracular shrines as standard destinations.

> "He was a tall, comely fellow, with an athletic frame developed by wrestling exercise; and his dark eyes, though now veiled with melancholy and longing, gleamed with a deep spark of intelligence. Immensely rich, the sole heir of various relatives who had died childless, he had joined for but a short time in the mad orgies of the young Romans of his own rank and had soon devoted himself to many branches of science, to astronomy in particular, philosophy, magic, the favourite passion of that period." ## Relationships

- **[[catullus|Catullus]]**: Lucius's penniless uncle, a cheerful parasite who provides comic relief and avuncular advice
- **[[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]**: Lucius's Greek freedman tutor, companion and intellectual guide through Egypt
- **[[cora|Cora]]**: Lucius's Greek slave from Cos, a harpist and singer whose hymns frame the emotional tone
- **[[ilia|Ilia]]**: Lucius's vanished beloved slave, the absent centre of his grief and the quest's motivation
- **[[caleb|Caleb]]**: The [[saba|Saba]]ean guide in [[alexandria|Alexandria]], who flatters Lucius as a "princely noble"
