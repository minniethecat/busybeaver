## Overview

Saba (also known as Sheba) was an ancient kingdom located in southern Arabia, roughly corresponding to modern-day Yemen. In the Roman era, Saba was famed for its wealth, its trade in precious aromatics — frankincense and myrrh — and its fine horses. In the narrative, Saba is the homeland of [[caleb|Caleb]], a central character who serves as [[lucius|Lucius]]'s guide and confidant in Egypt.

## History

The Sabaean kingdom emerged as a major power in southern Arabia by the early first millennium BCE, centred around its capital at Marib. The [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] developed one of the most sophisticated irrigation systems of the ancient world: the Marib Dam, a monumental earthwork that captured seasonal floodwaters and sustained extensive agriculture in the otherwise arid region. This hydraulic infrastructure supported a dense population and generated the agricultural surplus that underwrote Sabaean prosperity for over a thousand years. The kingdom is most famous in the wider Mediterranean and Near Eastern imagination through the biblical account of the Queen of Sheba's visit to King Solomon (1 Kings 10), which reflects the historical reality of Sabaean trading expeditions reaching as far as the Levant. By the Roman imperial period, Saba had entered a period of gradual decline — the Marib Dam suffered repeated breaches — yet Sabaean merchants remained a visible presence throughout the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] and Indian Ocean trade networks, dealing in the luxury goods for which their homeland was synonymous [PN:001].

## Trade and Economy

The wealth of Saba rested on two pillars: aromatic resins and the horse trade. Frankincense and myrrh, harvested from trees native to the southern Arabian highlands and the Horn of Africa, were among the most valuable commodities in the ancient world — essential for temple ritual, royal ceremony, funerary practice, and medicine across the Mediterranean, Mesopotamia, and Persia. The so-called Incense Road, a network of caravan routes stretching from southern Arabia to Gaza and Petra, funnelled these aromatics northward, enriching the [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] and the intermediary kingdoms along the way. [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] were known as expert handlers of their prized resins: as [[caleb|Caleb]] relates in the narrative, they controlled every stage of production, though they "surrender the trade in the preparation of frankincense and myrrh to the Egyptians" once the raw materials reached the Nile [011-017]. Sabaean horses — compact, hardy, and fleet — were another sought-after export, esteemed by cavalry commanders and wealthy travellers alike. The "jet-black, gaily-caparisoned Sabaean mare" that [[caleb|Caleb]] rides in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] [003-031] would have been recognised by contemporary readers as a mark of both wealth and discernment [PN:002].

## In the Narrative

Saba enters the story through [[caleb|Caleb]], who is introduced as "a native of Saba" when [[vettius|Vettius]] presents him to [[lucius|Lucius]] [003-011]. [[caleb|Caleb]] embodies the stereotypical Sabaean traits: mercantile acumen, expertise in precious goods, and deep belief in the power of amulets. He rides "a jet-black, gaily-caparisoned Sabaean mare" [003-031] and shows off his horsemanship [003-051]. The [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] as a people are grouped with other Eastern traders: "They were Indians, [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]], Arabs, Phoenicians" [004-015] in the cosmopolitan marketplace. [[caleb|Caleb]] explains that "the [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] surrender the trade in the preparation of frankincense and myrrh to the Egyptians" [011-017], and notes a curious detail of his homeland — "in for ever inhaling the overpowering fragrance in their country, the [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] one and all suffer from headaches" [011-018].
