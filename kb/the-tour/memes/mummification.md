# Mummification

## Overview

Mummification was the elaborate process of preserving the human body after death, practised in ancient Egypt for over three thousand years. The ritual involved removing internal organs, dehydrating the body with natron salts, anointing it with aromatic oils and resins, and wrapping it in linen bandages — all accompanied by religious ceremonies intended to prepare the deceased for eternal life. In *The Tour*, mummification is encountered in its completed form: the entombed mummies of the Ptolemies and [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]], whose preserved bodies evoke both wonder and existential unease in the Roman travellers.

## In the Narrative

The travellers encounter mummification at [[soma|the Soma]], the Ptolemaic burial complex in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The tomb chamber is described with precise sensory detail: "standing lamps burned on their tripods, perfumes rose in a cloud from vases and dishes; and daintily-coloured glass vessels, filled with oil, honey and fruit, stood on low bronze tables" (003-072). These grave goods — food offerings for the dead — are part of the mummification ritual's continuation into eternity.

The mummy of [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] is the novel's most vivid depiction of the embalmer's art. Within a sarcophagus of transparent crystal, "a mummy lay visible. It was like the chrysalis of a gigantic moth. The face was stained brown with balsam and salve and stared with eyes of beryl. The hair and short beard were painted gold. Many-coloured bandages wrapped the body in a close sheath; and the legs also were closely fastened together in a case of gold filagree" (003-076). The image of the mummy as a "chrysalis" — a pupa awaiting transformation — captures the Egyptian belief in resurrection through preservation.

The priest's commentary frames mummification within Egyptian theology: "The soul has departed from this house embalmed with precious ointments" (003-081), and the mummy rests on "a mattress of striped byssus, the head on a byssus pillow" (003-077). The scarlet lips that "seemed to grin" and the beryl eyes "full of amazement at what they saw in eternity" (003-077) suggest the uncanny strangeness of the preserved dead for Roman visitors. After the visit, one character exclaims: "We have seen the mummy of Alexander!" (003-091) — the experience of viewing the preserved conqueror becoming a kind of tourist trophy.

The imagery of the wrapped body recurs throughout the novel. A character is described lying "like a precious mummy, straight out, his arms beside him" (009-019), and in the Delta tombs the travellers see how "the mummies of the proud rulers" have been reduced, over centuries, to "grains of sand" (017-017) — the very preservation that mummification promises mocked by the entropy of deep time. Rhodopis' "scented mummy vanished; her sarcophagus vanished" (017-027), the elaborate rituals of preservation undone by the ages. In a "Titanic ruin, in which not a mummy remained" (024-025), the travellers confront the ultimate failure of the embalmer's art against the scale of geological time.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Egyptian mummification evolved over millennia, from simple sand burials in the Predynastic period to the elaborate seventy-day ritual of the New Kingdom and later periods. The process involved evisceration (removal of lungs, liver, stomach and intestines into canopic jars), dehydration in natron for forty days, anointing, and wrapping. The heart was typically left in place as the seat of intelligence needed for judgment in the afterlife.

By the Roman period of *The Tour*, mummification had become somewhat commercialised, with different "packages" available at different price points. The mummies seen by [[lucius|Lucius]] — Ptolemaic royalty and Alexander himself — represent the highest tier of preservation, with gold, crystal, and precious unguents. The description of the mummy as a "chrysalis" anticipating transformation reflects a genuine Egyptian theological concept: the mummy was not a corpse but a transformed, eternal body (the *sah*).
