# Catullus

## Overview

Catullus is [[lucius|Lucius]]'s uncle, a penniless, potbellied Roman who serves as the novel's primary source of comic relief. He accompanies [[lucius]] on the Egyptian tour as a *parasitus* — in the Roman social sense of a dependent who earns his keep through wit and companionship. His character provides a grounded, worldly counterpoint to [[lucius|Lucius]]'s romantic grief and [[thrasyllus]]'s philosophical musings. His preoccupations are simple and constant: good food, good wine, and amusing commentary on the strange customs of Egypt. Where [[lucius|Lucius]] sees mystery and transcendence, Catullus sees a meal; where [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] sees cosmic order, Catullus sees an opportunity for a nap.

## In the Narrative

Catullus is first described on the ship: "On a pile of cushions lay Catullus, [[lucius|Lucius]]' penniless uncle, potbellied and bald-headed, with a wreath of roses round his bald head" (002-007). This image — the fat, cheerful uncle crowned with roses — establishes his character immediately. When [[lucius|Lucius]] is consumed with grief over [[ilia]], Catullus offers practical philosophy: "By all the gods, try to forget her: she had thick ankles and large feet" (003-091).

His response to [[alexandria]] is characteristically focused on sensory pleasures. Upon arriving, he immediately asks about kitchens: "Are there kitchens to the suites, kitchens where our trusty cook can prepare us this or that simple fare?" (003-015). He is delighted by Master [[ghizla]]'s description of the wine cellar: "That's better than water" (003-019), smacking his lips.

Catullus's commentary provides a deflating counterpoint to pretension. When the philosophers at [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] beg, he cries "The shameless rascal!" but [[lucius|Lucius]] only laughs (003-064). After the overwhelming morning of sightseeing, he declares: "My brains are soaked like an overfull sponge: they can contain no more this morning. That sated condition of the head makes my stomach feel empty" (003-091) — a perfect distillation of his character.

He is a good-natured presence, calling [[cora]]'s singing beautiful and inviting her to "call me uncle" (007-035). His warmth extends to all, creating a sense of familial affection that balances the novel's darker themes. He is "tired of Rome and [[baiae|Baiae]]" (002-027) and embraces the novelty of Egypt with the enthusiasm of a man who has nothing to lose and everything to enjoy.

## Character Analysis

Catullus embodies the Roman social type of the *parasitus* — a poor relation or dependent who earned his keep at a wealthy patron's table through flattery, wit, and companionship. The type appears throughout Roman literature, most famously in the comedies of Plautus and Terence. But Couperus deepens the type: Catullus's simple [[philosophy|wisdom]] is not merely comic but genuinely humane.

| Trait | Manifestation | Narrative Function |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Epicureanism | Obsession with food, wine, and comfort | Counters [[lucius|Lucius]]'s ascetic grief with life-affirming pleasure |
| Practical [[philosophy|wisdom]] | "She had thick ankles and large feet" | Punctures romantic idealism with comic deflation |
| Good nature | Warmth toward [[cora|Cora]], loyalty to [[lucius|Lucius]] | Provides emotional grounding for the travelling party |
| Physical comedy | Potbelly, rose wreath, lip-smacking | Lightens the novel's philosophical and religious gravitas |
| Honest philistinism | "My brains are soaked like an overfull sponge" | Validates the reader's own possible reaction to the tour's intensity |

## Relationships

- **[[lucius]]**: Catullus's wealthy nephew, whom he accompanies and whose grief he tries to alleviate with humour
- **[[thrasyllus]]**: [[lucius|Lucius]]'s tutor; Catullus respects his learning but gently mocks his seriousness
- **[[cora]]**: The slave singer; Catullus treats her with grandfatherly warmth
- **[[ilia]]**: Called Catullus "uncle"; her disappearance saddens him in his own uncomplicated way
