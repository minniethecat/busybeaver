## Overview

The ibis — specifically the sacred ibis (*Threskiornis aethiopicus*) — was a bird revered in ancient Egypt as the embodiment of Thoth, the god of writing, [[philosophy|wisdom]], and the moon. With its distinctive curved beak and white-and-black plumage, the ibis was mummified in vast numbers as votive offerings. In *The Tour*, ibises are among the first living details of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] that the travellers notice upon disembarking, marking the city's exotic Egyptian character even at its bustling harbour. Unlike the stone monuments that dominate the travellers' later impressions, the ibises represent Egyptian religion as something alive — birds moving freely among the crowds, sacred and mundane at once.

## In the Narrative

The ibises are introduced as a vivid sensory detail of the [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n waterfront. As the travellers arrive, [[lucius|Lucius]]'s companion urges him to look "at all those ibises walking about on the quay" [003-007] — the first direct address to [[lucius|Lucius]] about an Egyptian sight, and a signal that the familiar world of Rome has been left behind. The ibises are not confined to temples but move freely through public space.

Later, when street-boys scatter, "the ibises scattered with outstretched wings" [003-033], their movements woven into the rhythm of street life — the sacred birds reacting to the same urban disruptions as the human inhabitants. [[catullus|Catullus]] exclaims with delight at the sheer number of them: "What crowds of ibises!" [003-034], a moment of spontaneous wonder that prompts [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] to provide an explanation situating them within Egyptian religion. The ibises thus serve as an entry point for the Roman visitors — and the reader — into the unfamiliar world of Egyptian sacred zoology, where the boundary between the divine and the everyday is permeable in ways that challenge Roman sensibilities.

## Thoth and the Sacred Ibis

The ibis was sacred to Thoth (called [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]] by the Greeks), the ibis-headed god of writing, [[philosophy|wisdom]], magic, and the moon. Thoth was the divine scribe who recorded the weighing of the heart in the afterlife, and his cult at Hermopolis Magna was one of the most important in Egypt. The ibis's curved beak was thought to resemble the crescent moon, linking the bird to Thoth's lunar aspect. Pilgrims purchased mummified ibises as votive offerings, and archaeological excavations have revealed millions of ibis mummies in underground catacombs — the scale of the cult was industrial. The Greek and Roman observers who saw ibises freely roaming [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s streets were witnessing an ancient tradition in which the sacred and the quotidian coexisted without tension.

## Historical Context

The sacred ibis was associated with Thoth at Hermopolis Magna and throughout Egypt. Pilgrims purchased mummified ibises as votive offerings, and millions of ibis mummies have been found in Egyptian catacombs — a scale of religious production that astonished archaeologists. The birds were also valued for their practical role in controlling water snails and other pests along the Nile. Ibis mummification continued into the Roman period, and the birds remained a protected species under Egyptian law. The ibis became extinct in Egypt only in the 19th century, making the novel's description of living ibises on [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s quays a historically accurate portrait of a species now vanished from the region.
