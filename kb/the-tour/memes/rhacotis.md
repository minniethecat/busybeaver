# Rhacotis

## Overview

Rhacotis was the original Egyptian settlement that predated the foundation of Greek [[alexandria|Alexandria]] by [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] in 331 BCE, later absorbed into the western quarter of the Ptolemaic and Roman city. In *The Tour*, Rhacotis appears as the "old" or "common" quarter — distinct from the marble splendour of the [[bruchium|Bruchium]] and the intellectual prestige of [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] — a district of narrow streets, sailors' taverns, and nocturnal mystery. It is here that [[caleb|Caleb]] leads [[lucius|Lucius]] on a secret nighttime expedition to consult the sibyl, an old sorceress said to know everything.

## In the Narrative

Rhacotis is first mentioned by [[caleb|Caleb]] during the travellers' whirlwind tour of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] on their first morning. As they pass the Moon Gate and enter the High Street, [[caleb|Caleb]] gestures dismissively: "behind it lies the Rhacotis quarter, which is very interesting at night, my lord, most interesting for anyone like your lordship to roam through in disguise" (003-039). [[caleb|Caleb]]'s knowing wink establishes Rhacotis as the shadow-side of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — the place where wealthy Romans go slumming.

Later, when the party visits the more genteel shores of [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]], [[caleb|Caleb]] draws an explicit contrast: "You must come here again in the evening, my lord … This is the place for one like your lordship to enjoy himself: at Rhacotis there are only the common women and the houses where the sailors go" (003-047). The [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]] villas house the "rich hetairae"; Rhacotis serves the masses.

When [[lucius|Lucius]], consumed by grief for [[ilia]], demands to consult [[oracle|oracles]], [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes "the sibyl of Rhacotis, an old sorceress who knows everything" (004-010). The nocturnal journey that follows takes them through the deserted temple quarter — past the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] and "numbers of other shrines, also sombre, grey and mysterious" (005-013) — before arriving at Rhacotis. [[caleb|Caleb]]'s commentary underscores the district's religious marginality: "These shrines are deserted … and no longer find worshippers. Even the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] is deserted … for the temple of [[serapis|Serapis]] at [[canopus|Canopus]]" (005-014).

The sibyl of Rhacotis proves, in [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s later assessment, to have "merely guessed your own thoughts" (013-003). Yet the district's atmospheric power — dark, labyrinthine, suspended between the derelict sacred quarter and the sailors' taverns — makes it the natural setting for [[lucius|Lucius]]'s first, disappointing encounter with Egyptian divination.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Egyptian settlement of Rhacotis (Egyptian *Râ-kedet*) existed on the site of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] for centuries before Alexander's conquest. After the foundation of the Greek city, it became its westernmost quarter, retaining an Egyptian character distinct from the Hellenistic grid-plan of the central districts. The ancient sources describe it as a working-class area, home to fishermen, sailors, and the native Egyptian population. The sibyl — a prophetic old woman — belongs to a long tradition of Egyptian and Graeco-Roman folk [[oracle|oracles]] operating outside the institutional temples.
