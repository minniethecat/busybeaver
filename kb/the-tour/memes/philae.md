## Overview

Philae was a small island in the Nile, just south of the first cataract near [[syene|Syene]], renowned throughout antiquity for its Temple of [[isis|Isis]]. As the southernmost point of Egypt proper, it marked the boundary between the civilized world and the mysterious lands of Ethiopia beyond. The island's temple complex, built during the Ptolemaic period, was one of the last active centres of ancient Egyptian religion, surviving well into the Roman era — indeed, the cult of [[isis|Isis]] at Philae persisted until the sixth century CE, long after most other Egyptian temples had fallen silent. In *The Tour*, Philae is reached by the travellers after a long overland cart journey from [[syene|Syene]], and it is here that [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes an audacious change of plan: abandoning the barge to continue southward through Ethiopia by caravan, with "carts, camels, elephants and tents" (024-033).

## In the Narrative

After the party's barge reaches [[syene|Syene]] — the southernmost Roman garrison — the travellers continue overland to Philae by cart. The journey is austere: "There was an end to any luxurious comfort; the road led for hundreds of stadia through a level plain with strange big rocks, like statues of [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]] in a Greek city" (025-006). The strange cylindrical rock formations — "round and cylindrical, like polished black stones, three on top of one another, from large to small" — make the landscape feel almost sculptural, as if the desert itself had been arranged by an ancient artist (025-006). The approach to the island is made on "a raft of laths and wickerwork, on which the water lapped over their feet" (025-006).

At Philae, [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes a dramatic new itinerary. [[lucius|Lucius]] recounts the moment: "[[caleb|Caleb]] did suggest that we should leave the barge at Philae, where we shall soon be arriving, and go through Ethiopia with carts, camels, elephants and tents, go hunting on elephants and ostriches and travel on to [[meroe|Meroe]]" (024-033). The proposal — to venture beyond the boundaries of the Roman world into the African interior — marks Philae as the hinge point between the Egyptian journey and the Ethiopian adventure that will occupy the latter chapters of the novel.

Near Philae, at Elephantina, the travellers also witness the Little Cataract and watch watermen from the island shoot the rapids in light boats: "the watermen come up from Philae in light boats and then shoot, with the powerful, brawling stream, over the steps and raise themselves over the rocky wall and slip, boat and all, with joyful cries, down the waterfall" (025-005). The spectacle of the watermen — joyful, fearless, utterly at home in the dangerous current — offers a fleeting image of a world beyond Roman order, anticipating the wilder journey to come.

## Philae as Threshold

Philae functions in the novel as a narrative and symbolic threshold. Geographically, it is the last island of Egypt — beyond it lies Ethiopia, a land the Romans regarded as semi-mythical. Thematically, it is the point where the travellers' mode of movement changes from water to land, from the known Nile to the unknown caravan routes (024-033). The Temple of [[isis|Isis]] at Philae — the southernmost major sanctuary of the goddess — embodies the boundary between civilization and wilderness, order and adventure. [[caleb|Caleb]]'s proposal at Philae to abandon the barge and travel overland represents a decisive break with the comfort and predictability of the Egyptian journey. From this point forward, the novel enters a different register: less pilgrimage, more expedition.

## Historical Context

The Temple of [[isis|Isis]] at Philae was begun under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] II Philadelphus (3rd century BCE) and expanded by successive Ptolemaic and Roman rulers. The island became a major pilgrimage centre, attracting worshippers from across Egypt and Nubia. The cult of [[isis|Isis]] at Philae was the last bastion of traditional Egyptian religion: the temple was not closed until the reign of Justinian I (c. 535–537 CE), and the last dated hieroglyphic inscription — carved in AD 394 — was found at Philae. The island was largely submerged after the construction of the [[syene|Aswan]] Low Dam (1902) and completely flooded by the High Dam (1970), though the temple complex was famously relocated to the nearby Agilkia Island as part of the UNESCO Nubian Salvage Campaign.
