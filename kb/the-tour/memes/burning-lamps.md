## Overview

The Feast of the Burning Lamps — also called the Night of the Glowing Lights — is an Egyptian religious festival celebrated at [[sais|Sais]], the cult centre of the goddess [[neith|Neith]]. The festival is characterised by nocturnal illuminations: thousands of lamps are lit and set adrift or displayed, creating a spectral, flickering landscape of light across the city. In *The Tour*, the feast provides the setting for one of the novel's most atmospheric sequences, blending Egyptian religious practice with the personal drama of the Roman travellers.

## In the Narrative

The travellers arrive at [[sais|Sais]] during the festival. The city is transformed: the women of [[sais|Sais]] are "all arrayed in long, white-linen robes," and [[cora|Cora]], [[lucius|Lucius]]'s slave and beloved, is similarly dressed, wearing "a wreath of wheatears and [[lotus|lotus-flower]]s at her temples" — for "it was the Night of the Glowing Lights, the Feast of the Burning Lamps" (014-011). [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], characteristically unimpressed, declines to go ashore, declaring:

> "The Night of the Glowing Lights and the Feast of the Burning Lamps leave me cold. It is colourless and cheerless; it will be a spectral orgy. I am too old and fat, Lucius." (014-014)

[[lucius|Lucius]], however, does go ashore, and the festival setting becomes the backdrop for a night charged with tension and desire. The lamps themselves — burning with fragrant oils, casting shadows across the temple precinct — create an atmosphere that is at once sacred and sensuous. As the night wanes, "the twinkling of the Burning Lamps died away" (014-081), marking the passage from nocturnal ritual to the greyness of dawn, and with it a turn in the emotional register of the journey.

The imagery of burning lamps recurs earlier in the novel as well, when [[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions enter the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]: "Again, burning lamps shed their fragrance" (003-076), establishing the motif of lamp-light as a marker of sacred or liminal space.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Festival of Lamps (*Lychnocaia*) was a well-attested Egyptian religious practice, particularly associated with [[sais|Sais]] and the cult of [[neith|Neith]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] (Histories 2.62) describes a "Feast of Lamps" at [[sais|Sais]] where thousands of lamps filled with oil and salt were burned throughout the night. The ritual likely symbolised the triumph of light over darkness, the goddess [[neith|Neith]]'s creative illumination, and possibly funerary associations — guiding the dead through the darkness of the underworld. By the Roman period, such festivals had absorbed Hellenistic and syncretic elements while retaining their essential Egyptian character. Couperus draws directly on the Herodotean account, adapting the ancient festival as a richly sensuous backdrop for the emotional tensions among his Roman travellers.
