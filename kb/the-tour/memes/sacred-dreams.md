# Sacred Dreams

## Overview

Sacred dreaming, or incubation, was a central ritual practice at the temple of [[serapis|Serapis]] at [[canopus|Canopus]]. Pilgrims would travel from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] by barge, process through the temple's monumental pylons, and lie down on the temple terraces under consecrated dreaming-nets and veils, awaiting the god's oracular dreams. In *The Tour*, the feast of [[serapis|Serapis]] is explicitly called "the feast of dreams" (007-055), and the entire ninth chapter is dedicated to this ritual, which [[lucius|Lucius]] undertakes in the hope of discovering what has become of his lost beloved, [[ilia|Ilia]].

## In the Narrative

The dream-ritual dominates the novel's central chapters. The journey to [[canopus|Canopus]] is described with mounting anticipation: "One and all were going to [[canopus|Canopus]] that night. What a surprise! And what a feast! It was the feast of [[serapis|Serapis]]! The feast of dreams!" (007-055). The festive atmosphere of the barge journey — with singing, dancing, and [[cora|Cora]]'s hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] — gives way to solemn ritual as the pilgrims enter the temple.

The incubation itself is depicted in precise detail. After the midnight gong-strokes and the processions of priests (hieropsalts, hierogrammats, pastophors carrying images of [[anubis|Anubis]]), the pilgrims lie down on the granite terraces: "On the vast terraces the multitude of the thousands of pilgrims lay motionless under nets and veils, their eyes closed" (009-014). Temple-keepers cover the pilgrims with dreaming-nets while zacori swing censers: "A heavy, intoxicating perfume of almost stifling aromatic vapour was wafted through the air" (009-011).

The theological rationale is that [[serapis|Serapis]] rises from the underworld during this sacred hour: "He rises in a cloud of dreams, out of the sacred, subterranean Hell, where he reigns even as [[osiris|Osiris]] reigns in high Heaven" (009-015). The god "makes the dreams hover like butterflies around the foreheads of those who believe in him" (009-017), offering healing, advice, and prophetic visions.

[[lucius|Lucius]] himself participates: "In the silence the young Roman lies, covered with a gold network, like a precious [[mummification|mummy]], straight out, his arms beside his body, his eyes shut" (009-019). The gold net echoes his earlier transformation into an Egyptianised figure, dressed in byssus and gold tissue (007-044).

## Historical/Cultural Context

The practice of incubation (Greek *enkoimesis*) was widespread in the ancient Mediterranean. The most famous incubation sanctuaries were the Asclepieia (temples of Asclepius) in Greece, but the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] at [[canopus|Canopus]] was renowned as a centre for dream-[[oracle|oracles]]. [[strabo|Strabo]] mentions [[canopus|Canopus]] as a place of pilgrimage and revelry, with the canal from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] lined by pleasure-gardens. The practice involved ritual purification, offerings, and sleeping within the *abaton* (forbidden area accessible only to the pure).

The "dreaming-nets" and "veils" described in the novel may be Couperus's imaginative elaboration, but the use of incense, darkness, silence, and the presence of temple attendants during incubation is well attested in ancient sources, including the practice of *temple-sleep* at Epidauros recorded in inscriptions.
