# Baiae

## Overview

Baiae was a fashionable resort town on the Bay of Naples, renowned in the Roman world for its thermal springs, luxurious villas, and hedonistic atmosphere. It was the preferred retreat of wealthy Romans seeking escape from the capital — a place where the pleasures of the body took precedence over the duties of civic life (002-027). In *The Tour*, Baiae is the point of departure — both literally and emotionally — as the site of [[lucius]]'s villa and the place from which [[ilia]] vanished. If [[alexandria]] is the gateway to Egypt and [[thebes]] the spiritual destination, Baiae is the paradise lost, the world left behind at the novel's opening (001-022) that can never be fully returned to.

## In the Narrative

Baiae is referenced throughout the novel as the lost paradise from which [[lucius|Lucius]] has been exiled by grief. [[ilia|Ilia]] vanished "one inauspicious morning, from his villa at Baiae, without leaving a trace behind her" (003-020). The villa itself has passed into imperial ownership: it is now "the property of our gracious [[tiberius|Emperor Tiberius]]" (003-011), a detail that transforms [[lucius|Lucius]]'s personal loss into an emblem of broader historical change.

The contrast between Baiae and Egypt is repeatedly drawn. Uncle [[catullus]] declares himself "tired of Rome and Baiae" (002-027), seeking novelty in the east. [[cora]] has been singing hymns to [[aphrodite]] "for three months now, ever since we sailed out of the Bay of Baiae" (002-024), using Baiae as the temporal marker of the journey's beginning. Baiae functions as the "before" in the novel's before-and-after structure: the world of Roman Italy, of youth and love and certainty, before the transformative Egyptian journey.

The memory of Baiae haunts [[lucius|Lucius]] just as the memory of [[ilia|Ilia]] does. When he curses his melancholy in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — "The first charm of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] was past" (006-003) — it is Baiae's lost happiness he is measuring against (006-003). The villa, the bay, the vanished beloved: these three elements form a single node of nostalgic loss that Egypt can illuminate but never heal.

## Historical Context

Baiae (modern Baia) was one of the most famous resorts of the Roman Empire, located on the Cumaean peninsula west of Naples. Its natural hot springs and pleasant coastal setting attracted emperors and aristocrats. Julius Caesar, Nero, and Hadrian all had villas there (003-011). The town was also notorious for its luxury and moral licence — the poet Propertius warned his girlfriend against visiting Baiae's corrupting pleasures.

Much of ancient Baiae now lies underwater due to volcanic bradyseism — a physical sinking that mirrors the novel's theme of irretrievable loss.
