## Overview

Lake Moeris (modern Birket Qarun) is a large saltwater lake in the Faiyum Oasis, southwest of Cairo. In antiquity it was a freshwater lake connected to the Nile by the Bahr Yussef canal, and its basin was one of the most fertile agricultural regions of Egypt. The lake was associated with the cult of the crocodile god [[crocodile|Sobek]] (the "sacred [[crocodile|Such]]"), and crocodiles were venerated in the region.

## Geography and Hydrology

The Faiyum depression, in which Lake Moeris lies, is a natural basin that sits below sea level and was fed by the Bahr Yussef ([[joseph|Joseph]]'s Canal), a branch of the Nile that diverges near modern Asyut. During the annual inundation, Nile floodwaters flowed through the canal into the depression, creating a vast freshwater lake that could cover as much as 1,700 square kilometres in its natural state. The Middle Kingdom pharaohs of the Twelfth Dynasty, particularly Amenemhat III (c. 1860–1814 BCE), undertook ambitious hydraulic engineering projects to regulate the lake's level and reclaim fertile farmland — an achievement that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] later described with wonder, claiming the lake was "an artificial excavation, as is evident to the eye" and that its waters were directed through a channel into the Libyan desert [PN:001]. By the Roman period, the lake had begun to shrink as sedimentation and reduced maintenance of the canal system took their toll, though Ptolemaic engineers continued to manage the water levels for irrigation.

## Religious Significance

Lake Moeris lay at the heart of the cult of [[crocodile|Sobek]], the crocodile god. The principal cult centre was at Shedet (Greek: Crocodilopolis, later Arsinoe), on the lake's shore, where a [[crocodile|sacred crocodile]] — called the [[crocodile|Such]] — was kept in the temple precinct, adorned with gold, gems, and jewellery, and fed by priests. This living incarnation of [[crocodile|Sobek]] was believed to mediate between the human and divine realms. The reverence for crocodiles extended beyond the temple: local fishermen would not harm the creatures, and mummified crocodiles have been found in the region's necropoleis by the thousands, wrapped in linen and deposited as votive offerings. Visitors to the Faiyum in the Roman period, including the travellers of the narrative, would have encountered pilgrims who had journeyed to the lake specifically to participate in the rituals of the sacred [[crocodile|Such]] [PN:002].

## In the Narrative

Lake Moeris appears as the travellers journey southward from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] along the Arabian bank of the Nile. It is described stretching "exquisite and crystalline" between margins of sands [024-001]. The narrator notes the cultivated and inhabited lands surrounding the lake, which were "large as a sea" yet never fully explored by those who lived nearby [024-003]. [[crocodile|Crocodile]] worshippers who had fed the sacred [[crocodile|Such]] on Lake Moeris are observed travelling with the party [024-037].
