## Overview

Ptolemy, son of Lagus — later known as Ptolemy I Soter ("the Saviour") — was one of [[alexander|Alexander the Great]]'s most trusted generals and the founder of the Ptolemaic dynasty that ruled Egypt for nearly three centuries. After Alexander's death in Babylon in 323 BCE, Ptolemy secured the governorship of Egypt and, in a masterstroke of political symbolism, intercepted the funeral procession carrying Alexander's body back to Macedon. He brought the remains to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where they were interred in the [[soma|Soma]], establishing the new city as the spiritual and dynastic centre of his kingdom. Under Ptolemy and his successors, [[alexandria|Alexandria]] became the intellectual capital of the Hellenistic world — home to the Great Library and the [[museum-of-alexandria|Museum]], where scholars like [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] flourished under royal patronage. In *The Tour*, Ptolemy appears through the history of the tomb he established, through the library of his descendant Ptolemy Evergetes, and even through the local currency that still bears his name.

## In the Narrative

Ptolemy's most significant presence in the story is at [[soma|the Soma]], the royal mausoleum in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The priest in charge of Alexander's tomb recounts the dramatic history to the travellers: "History teaches us that Ptolemy, son of Lagus, took the body of the hero and conqueror from Perdiccas, who was bringing it back from Babylon to Macedon, but was passing through Egypt" [003-078].

The priest details the military confrontation that followed: "Ptolemy marched against him; Perdiccas had hardly set foot in Egypt when he perished at the hands of his own soldiery on an island which had been surrounded by Ptolemy's troops" [003-078]. With Perdiccas dead, the royal family — Alexander's pregnant widow Roxana and her children — were allowed to embark for Macedon, while Alexander's body was carried to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and buried in a massive gold sarcophagus.

The narrative notes that the original gold sarcophagus was later stolen by "Ptolemy Parisactus, a pretender to the Egyptian throne, who invaded the country from Syria with a host of troops," but Alexander's body was rescued and placed in the crystal coffin the travellers now see [003-078]. This layered history — Ptolemy the founder, Ptolemy Parisactus the pretender — intertwines legitimate and illegitimate claims to Alexander's legacy, a theme that echoes through the entire tomb episode.

The Ptolemaic dynasty's intellectual legacy also surfaces through [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s reading. He takes pleasure in "the more succinct descriptions of the learned [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]], Ptolemy Evergetes' librarian, who lived some two hundred years after [[herodotus|Herodotus]]" [004-013] — a reference to Ptolemy III Euergetes, under whose patronage the Library of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] reached its zenith and [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] calculated the circumference of the Earth.

The dynasty's name persists even in the local currency: when the travellers need to pay a guide, "the fee is half a ptolemy, in gold" [005-059], and [[caleb|Caleb]] later mentions slipping "a gold ptolemy" into a guide's hand [024-015]. The coinage is a small but telling detail — centuries after Ptolemy I, his name remains stamped on the gold that changes hands in the marketplace of his city.
