## Overview

The Sabaeans are the people of [[saba|Saba]] (biblical [[saba|Sheba]]), the wealthy kingdom of southern Arabia — Arabia Felix, modern Yemen. In the Roman imagination of the first century AD, Arabia Felix was a land of fabulous wealth, scented with myrrh, frankincense, and cinnamon, whose inhabitants lived "as gods" amid the perfumed breezes of their blessed country. In *The Tour*, the Sabaeans appear primarily as merchants and traders of exotic goods, and most prominently through the character of [[caleb|Caleb]], the [[saba|Saba]]ean guide whose vivid descriptions of his homeland form one of the novel's most lyrical passages.

## In the Narrative

The Sabaeans are first introduced at [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s harbour, where a "dark-skinned [[saba|Saba]]ean" — later revealed to be the elder brother of [[caleb|Caleb]] — assists [[vettius|Vettius]] the steward in securing lodgings for the Roman party (003-008). This [[saba|Saba]]ean speaks a language that draws from the cosmopolitan trading world of the eastern Mediterranean, moving fluidly between Latin, Greek, Phoenician, and Arabic as the situation demands.

[[caleb|Caleb]] himself becomes the novel's principal [[saba|Saba]]ean voice. He presents himself with characteristic flair, mounting "a jet-black, gaily-caparisoned [[saba|Saba]]ean mare" and parading through [[alexandria|Alexandria]] "like a young conqueror, always smiling and sparkling with black eyes and white teeth" (003-031). Throughout the journey, [[caleb|Caleb]]'s [[saba|Saba]]ean identity is repeatedly invoked — his horsemanship on the [[saba|Saba]]ean mare (003-032, 003-051), his amulets, his trading connections, and his deep knowledge of Arabian luxury goods.

The Sabaeans appear collectively as merchants in a memorable scene at the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] in [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where "Indians, Sabaeans, Arabs, Phoenicians" arrive bearing their wares (004-015). Among them, "the Sabaeans displayed wonderful amulets, which bring luck and blissful dreams" (004-021). These amulets become a recurring motif, worn by [[lucius|Lucius]] and referenced throughout the journey as objects of both protection and ambiguous power.

[[caleb|Caleb]]'s rhapsodic description of [[saba|Saba]] — delivered to [[cora|Cora]] as a promise of the life they might share — forms the novel's most extended portrait of the [[saba|Saba]]ean world:

> "Saba, dear Cora, is the mightiest kingdom of Arabia; Saba is Arabia Felix, Cora. The Sabaeans surrender the trade in the precious products of their country to the men of Syria and Mesopotamia. They themselves, Cora, are rich and as gods." (011-017)

This vision of effortless, fragrant wealth is undercut by [[cora|Cora]]'s dry observation:

> "I have heard say that, because of all that fragrance in their country, the Sabaeans one and all suffer from headache." (011-018)

[[caleb|Caleb]]'s reply reveals another layer of [[saba|Saba]]ean life: "When we suffer from headache, [[cora|Cora]], we burn asphalt and the smoke drives away the pain" (011-019). And later, he mentions, with characteristic nonchalance, the "[[saba|Saba]]ean law which prescribes that a married woman is also the wife of all her husband's brothers" (011-032) — a detail that foreshadows the complications of his relationship with [[cora|Cora]].

## Historical/Cultural Context

The historical Sabaeans ([[saba|Saba]]ʾ) flourished in southern Arabia from approximately the eighth century BCE, controlling the trade in frankincense and myrrh along the Incense Road. Their capital, Marib ([[mariaba|Mariaba]] in the novel), featured the famous Marib Dam, an engineering marvel of the ancient world. The biblical Queen of [[saba|Sheba]] (Balkis in Islamic tradition, whom [[caleb|Caleb]] invokes) visited Solomon bearing gifts of spices, gold, and precious stones. By the Roman period, [[saba|Saba]]ean trade networks extended across the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] to Egypt and the Mediterranean, and [[saba|Saba]]ean merchants were a familiar presence in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s markets. The novel's depiction of the Sabaeans as wealthy traders dealing in amulets, perfumes, and exotic goods accurately reflects their historical role as the primary intermediaries of the incense trade, though Couperus freely embellishes their customs and laws for narrative effect.
