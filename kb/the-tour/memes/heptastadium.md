# Heptastadium

## Overview

The Heptastadium (Greek *Heptastadion*, "seven stadia") was the great causeway that connected the mainland city of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to the island of [[pharos|Pharos]], site of the famous lighthouse. Measuring approximately seven stadia in length (roughly 1,200 metres), it divided the harbour into two basins — the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]] to the east and the harbour of Eunostus to the west — and functioned simultaneously as a bridge, a breakwater, and, in the novel's depiction, a fashionable evening promenade for the citizens of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. In *The Tour*, it serves as one of the defining landmarks of the [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n cityscape, a monument of Hellenistic engineering that frames several key scenes.

## In the Narrative

The Heptastadium is first pointed out by [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] as the quadrireme enters the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]]. Surveying the panorama from the ship's prow, the tutor "pointed with his finger, along the harbour … to the Heptastadium, the promenade-jetty which stretched out to unite the city with the island of [[pharos|Pharos]], whence the lighthouse took its name" (003-003). [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] notes the architectural richness of the structure: "On the one side of this pier, with rostra and statues on the marble balustrade and gates, lay the harbour of Eunostus and the naval docks of Cibotus" (003-003).

[[caleb|Caleb]], ever attentive to the opportunities for diversion, draws [[lucius|Lucius]]'s attention to the Heptastadium's social function: "Do you see the Heptastadium? The great bridge leading to the [[pharos|Pharos]]? Do you see that tall-masted ships are able to sail under it? It is an interesting walk there of an evening, my lord: all the beauties of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] go there; and a great nobleman like yourself need but make his choice and any hetaira in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] will fall at his feet!" (003-039).

The Heptastadium reappears that very evening, as [[lucius|Lucius]] and [[caleb|Caleb]] make their secret nocturnal journey to the sibyl of [[rhacotis|Rhacotis]]. From a square overlooking the harbours, [[lucius|Lucius]] watches the lighthouse's rotating beams sweep across the causeway: "Sometimes the wide sheaves of light struck the high marble bridge of the Heptastadium, which led to the lighthouse itself and which at this hour was crowded with women and idlers" (005-006). The image of the lighthouse beam illuminating the crowded promenade captures the dual character of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]: monumental engineering and human vitality.

Later, when the travellers visit [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]], the contrast is drawn between the two waterfronts: "It is so very different here, my lord, from the Heptastadium and [[lake-mareotis|Lake Mareotis]]. Here the people are common, my lord!" (005-018, implied context), underscoring the Heptastadium's association with [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s fashionable elite.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Heptastadium was constructed under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] I or II in the early third century BCE, simultaneously with or shortly after the [[pharos|Pharos]] lighthouse. It was both a causeway — carrying a road and an aqueduct to the island — and a breakwater protecting the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]]. Over the centuries, silt accumulated on either side of the causeway, gradually widening it until, in the medieval and modern periods, it became the basis for the land on which much of central [[alexandria|Alexandria]] now stands. The marble balustrade, rostra (ship's rams mounted as trophies), and statues mentioned by Cousijns are consistent with ancient descriptions. Ships passing from the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]] to Eunostus would have sailed under arched openings in the causeway.
