## Overview

Apis was the sacred bull of Memphis, venerated in Egyptian religion as the living incarnation of [[osiris|Osiris]] (or, in the syncretic Graeco-Roman context, [[osiris|Osiris]]-[[serapis|Serapis]]). The cult centred on a living bull, selected by priests according to sacred markings — a white crescent on the forehead, a white mark on the flank, and other prescribed signs. The Apis bull lived in a dedicated sanctuary (secus) at the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] in [[memphis|Memphis]], where it received offerings and oracular consultations from pilgrims. The incarnation was believed to last twenty-five years, after which the bull was ritually drowned in the Nile, embalmed, and a new Apis — identified by the priests — succeeded it.

## In the Narrative

The visit to the Apis bull at [[memphis|Memphis]] is one of the most vivid and humorous episodes in "The Tour." In Chapter XV, [[lucius|Lucius]] and his party are received at the [[serapeum|Serapeum]] by priests who maintain the Apis cult "only because all the travellers came to see the sacred bull" — the fees forming the brotherhood's principal revenue [015-040].

A young, cheerful priest who speaks a little Latin guides the visitors. He first shows them Apis' mother, a handsome white cow with gilded horns and one black foot, whom the priest kisses fondly on her muzzle [015-057]. The party then visits Apis himself — a splendid young bull-calf, black-coated, with the sacred white moon-mark between his gilded horns. The little priest lets Apis out "for a trot" in honour of the distinguished visitors, and the young bull races around his grass-plot, tearing up clods of earth, while the priests, the travellers, and even [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] all laugh at his playful energy [015-068].

The encounter is both comic and revealing: the priest is "a landowner's son" whose father's farm happened to produce the new Apis, and he was therefore dedicated to [[osiris|Osiris]] to care for the god [015-046]. The entire sacred apparatus, the narrative implies, is sustained by tourism rather than genuine piety.

After the visit, the travellers continue to joke about Apis. In Chapter XVI, [[caleb|Caleb]] "rubbed his hands at the thought of dear little Apis trotting round his secus for the benefit of strangers" [016-002].

## Cultural Context

The Apis cult was one of the oldest and most enduring in Egyptian religion, dating back to the First Dynasty (c. 3100 BC). The bull was associated with Ptah (the creator god of [[memphis|Memphis]]) and later with [[osiris|Osiris]], and after death was identified as [[serapis|Osiris-Apis]] (Oserapis), which in the Ptolemaic period was Hellenized into the syncretic deity [[serapis|Serapis]]. The [[serapeum|Serapeum]] at Saqqara, the burial place of the Apis bulls, contained massive stone [[sarcophagus|sarcophagi]] for the embalmed animals. In the Roman period, the cult continued to function as a tourist attraction, as vividly depicted in "The Tour," though the traditional Egyptian religion was increasingly marginalized by the spread of Christianity.

## Key Passages

> "The priests arranged themselves in processional order ... And they sang the Hymn of Apis, as they were wont to do when visitors came. For in the huge dead city of Memphis ... in truth the worship of Apis was still maintained only because all the travellers came to see the sacred bull. The fees which the travellers paid to the priests formed the principal revenue of their brotherhood." [015-040]

> "It was Apis; and the priests lifted their hands in adoration as they sang. But, if his mother was stately and aware of her dignity, Apis himself carried his divinity with the recklessness of his hot youth. He ran across the lawn, glad to have escaped from his stable; and the pleasant priest, laughing, ran after him." [015-065]

> "He was beautiful and playful and most delightful, the visitors granted. He was a splendid bull-calf. His coat gleamed black as jet; and he was painted in accordance with the sacred prescript without which there is no incarnation: a white moon, like a snowy little crown, shone like a sickle between his gilded horns." [015-068]
