# Lesbian Harp

## Overview

The Lesbian harp (Greek *barbitos* or *lyra*) was a small stringed instrument associated with the island of Lesbos and, in Greek musical tradition, with the lyric poetry of Sappho and Alcaeus. In *The Tour*, a "little four-stringed Lesbian harp" appears in the novel's celebrated opening scene, played by an unnamed Greek songstress aboard the [[quadrireme]] *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]*. Its delicate, bright sound — described as "golden beads" falling from "twanged harp-strings" — weaves through the contrasting musical textures of the shipboard night and becomes, together with the hymn to [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] it accompanies, a recurring motif in the novel's treatment of music, desire, and the consolations of art.

## In the Narrative

The Lesbian harp makes its first and most richly developed appearance in the novel's opening pages. As the quadrireme glides through a windless, star-strewn night, three distinct musical ensembles sound simultaneously from different parts of the ship: the three hundred rowers' "melancholy minor" chant, the sailors' "blither song" from the rear deck, and, cutting through both, "a delicate female voice" accompanied by the harp. Cousijns describes the instrument with tactile precision: "while as it were golden beads tinkled from twanged harp-strings: those very bright gold beads which tinkle from the strings of the little four-stringed Lesbian harp" (001-005). The song is "a hymn to the goddess [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], whose name constantly rang back, plaintively and wantonly, in the singer's Greek, exotically soft against the harder Latin of the men's joy-song" (001-005).

When [[lucius|Lucius]] cries out in anguish for [[ilia]], the music falls silent: "suddenly, because of his grief, all the music fell silent: the melancholy chant of the rowers, the joy-song of the sailors and the hymn to the goddess, sung to the twanging Lesbian harp" (001-025). The silence is only temporary: moments later, "the boatswain's voice made itself heard … Bright, golden beads from the four-stringed harp fell like clear drops through the night; and the Greek hymn of the songstress pined away with love and tenderness" (001-027). This pattern — music interrupted by grief, then resuming — establishes the harp as a symbol of art's resilience against suffering.

The harp reappears in connection with [[cora|Cora]], the Greek slave from Cos who is one of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s household musicians. [[cora|Cora]]'s harp is described as standing "like a rounded bow by her side" (002-007) in the shipboard pavilion. At the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]], [[lucius|Lucius]] is greeted "with the music of harps" (003-101) and [[cora|Cora]], with two other harpists, "struck a soft melody from their strings" and "danced a little ballet" (003-102) during the travellers' first [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n repast. The harp thus bridges the shipboard and [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n settings, tying the musical life of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s household to the broader sound-world of the novel.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The *barbitos* was a deeper-voiced variant of the lyre, with longer strings and a lower pitch, particularly associated with Lesbos and the Aeolic poetic tradition. Sappho is frequently depicted in Greek vase-painting holding a barbitos. By the Roman period, the instrument had a slightly archaic or literary association. The description of the harp as "four-stringed" is consistent with some variants of the Greek lyre family, though the standard lyre had seven strings. The contrast between the "harder Latin" of the men's voices and the "exotically soft" Greek of the singer reflects the historical linguistic landscape of the Roman Mediterranean, where Greek remained the prestige language of music and poetry.
