# Mariaba

Mariaba is the capital of [[saba|Saba]] ([[saba|Sheba]]), the kingdom in Arabia Felix (modern Yemen). It is the native city of [[caleb|Caleb]], who describes it in rhapsodic detail to [[cora|Cora]] as a fairy-city of perfume, gold, and divine luxury. [[caleb|Caleb]]'s full formal designation in the Postscript is "[[caleb|Caleb]] of Mariaba ([[saba|Saba]])."

## Caleb's Description

During the rainy season at [[alexandria|Alexandria]], while the travellers are confined indoors, [[caleb|Caleb]] sits with [[cora|Cora]] in the porch of the slaves' quarters and paints an elaborate picture of his homeland (011-017):

> "Saba, dear Cora, is the mightiest kingdom of Arabia; Saba is Arabia Felix, Cora. Saba is the sweet land where the balsam-trees grow and the precious spices are gathered: myrrh and frankincense and cinnamon. All the herbs and flowers, Cora, are scented in Saba; there is no herb and no flower that is not scented. Under the sky, which is transparent as empty blue space, the clouds of perfume waft up and rise to the feet of the gods, who always glance down smilingly upon my country, upon my happy country."

[[caleb|Caleb]]'s description blends genuine Arabian geography with extravagant fantasy — the spices myrrh, frankincense, and cinnamon were indeed Arabian trade goods, but the image of a land where every herb and flower is perfumed belongs to the realm of nostalgic idealization by an exile.

## The City

Mariaba, [[caleb|Caleb]] tells [[cora|Cora]], "is situated on a hill, like the palace of a god" (011-017). Its streets "are strewn with golden sand." The temples are "of chrysolite with domes of blue crystal, which imitate the firmament."

## The King

The [[saba|Saba]]ean king is a descendant of Balkis, the great Queen of [[saba|Sheba]] who brought Solomon the treasures of Ophir. He "lives at Mariaba in a palace walled with gold," where the walls of his apartments "are like blue mirrors and he treads on carpets that are woven of flowers and hourly renewed." The king "does not eat, but lives on perfumes" (011-017).

A peculiar constraint governs [[saba|Saba]]ean royalty: "He is sacred, but he may not leave his palace; for an oracle has commanded his people to stone him the moment that he comes out" (011-017). This divine captivity recalls the Ethiopian custom at [[meroe|Meroë]] and suggests a broader theme of sacred kingship in the novel's depiction of peripheral civilizations.

## Sabaean Society

The [[sabaeans|Sabaeans]] themselves are wealthy and "as gods" — they surrender the trade in the precious products of their country (myrrh, frankincense, cinnamon, balsam) to Syrian and Mesopotamian merchants, and do no business themselves. This is the reason [[caleb|Caleb]] and his brother [[ghizla|Ghizla]] had to leave [[saba|Saba]]: "we do no business in [[saba|Saba]]" (005-012). They intend to return only when they have grown sufficiently rich abroad.

## Sabaean Law

[[caleb|Caleb]] mentions a [[saba|Saba]]ean marriage custom: "a married woman is also the wife of all her husband's brothers" (011-032). He tells [[cora|Cora]] the story of a king's daughter who was the wife of fifteen brothers, to reassure her that his brother [[ghizla|Ghizla]] "would not dare to touch even the hem of your garment" (011-034).

## In the Postscript

In the [[Postscript]], [[caleb|Caleb]] signs his letter to [[lucius|Lucius]] as "[[caleb|Caleb]] of Mariaba ([[saba|Saba]])" and reports that during a temporary stay in his beloved country he bought goods for trade. By this time, he appears to have achieved the wealth needed to reside at Mariaba as a man of substance (030-001–030-011).
