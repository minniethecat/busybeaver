## Overview

Hermes Trismegistus ("Thrice-Great Hermes") is a syncretic figure combining the Greek god Hermes with the Egyptian god Thoth, regarded in Hellenistic and Roman antiquity as the author of the Hermetic writings — a body of texts on philosophy, alchemy, astrology, and the occult. In Egyptian tradition, he was associated with Moses, who was said to have learned the occult [[philosophy|wisdom]] of Egypt from him [016-008]. The figure represents the fusion of Greek philosophical thought with ancient Egyptian priestly knowledge that characterised the intellectual culture of Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]].

## In the Narrative

Hermes Trismegistus is referenced as part of the deep Egyptian spiritual tradition that the travellers encounter. He is named alongside Moses as one of the great figures who walked and taught in Egypt [015-029]. The narrative presents him as part of a chain of [[philosophy|wisdom]] transmission — Moses learned from him, and subsequent generations of Egyptian priests and philosophers are the heirs of both Moses and Hermes Trismegistus [024-024]. His presence in the story underscores the novel's theme of Egypt as the source of primordial [[philosophy|wisdom]], predating and informing both [[philosophy|Greek philosophy]] and Jewish revelation.

## Historical Context

The historical Hermes Trismegistus is a legendary rather than historical figure. The *Corpus Hermeticum*, a collection of Greek treatises written in Egypt between the 1st and 3rd centuries AD, was attributed to him. These texts blend Platonic philosophy with Egyptian religious thought and were highly influential in the Renaissance. The association with Moses reflects a broader Hellenistic Jewish tradition that identified Moses with the Egyptian sage Thoth-Hermes, exemplified by the Jewish writer Artapanus (2nd century BCE).
