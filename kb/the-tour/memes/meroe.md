# Meroë

Meroë is the capital of Ethiopia, situated on an island of the same name in the upper Nile. It is the southernmost point reached by [[lucius|Lucius]] and his party in their expedition beyond the Egyptian frontier, at what the travellers regard as the very edge of the known world — a place where Mediterranean civilization gives way to the exotic and the unknown.

## In the Narrative

The travellers reach Meroë after passing through [[napata|Napata]] and the Ethiopian emerald-mines and topaz-rocks (025-014). At [[napata|Napata]] they saw "their first entirely native, barbarian town" where no Latin was spoken (025-015). From there they pressed farther south "to the capital of Ethiopia, Meroe, on the island of the same name" (025-016).

[[caleb|Caleb]] had earlier proposed the Ethiopian expedition during the barge journey, suggesting they "go through Ethiopia with carts, camels, elephants and a numerous armed escort by [[napata|Napata]] to Meroe" (024-033). The journey took them through forests, past emerald-mines and topaz-rocks, and among exotic tribes including the Elephantophagi and Struthophagi (025-008–025-014).

## The City

At Meroë the travellers discovered a civilization strikingly different from the Mediterranean world:

- The Ethiopians wore "no clothing save the skin of some animal round their waist" and surprised the travellers by "the smallness of their stature" — everything about them was small: their houses of palm-leaves and bamboo, their oxen, goats, and sheep. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] speculated that "the legend of the Pygmaei, or nations of dwarfs, had originated because of Ethiopia" (025-015).

- The natives subsisted mainly on vegetables, fruits, young shoots of trees, reed-stalks, and [[lotus|lotus-flower]]s, "but they also took blood and milk and cheese; and there was no other food" (025-016).

- Fabulous vegetation grew luxuriantly: "huge palm-trees, the ebony-trees, the ceratia and persea, under whose gigantic domes of thick foliage the green villages of little plaited wicker huts disappeared from view" (025-017).

- In the marshes round Lake Psebo, the travellers hunted the terrible boa, "which even ventures to attack the elephant," and the natives staged a fight between a boa, an elephant, and a hippopotamus (025-017).

- The travellers visited "the goldmines, the copper-mines, the jewel-mines, the temples of Hercules and Pan and of a strange barbaric deity" (025-018).

## The Golden Temple

In the centre of the town stood the Golden Temple, where the king dwelt "in sacred mystery." It featured "slabs of gold between bamboo columns" (025-018).

The Ethiopian monarchy was bound by a striking custom: formerly, priests had elected and deposed kings at will, but a certain king "had caused all the priests to be strangled." Thereafter a law was passed decreeing that "if the king were maimed or lost a limb, all the people of his court had to inflict the same injury on themselves." For this reason, the king's person was guarded with great care and was "divine and sacred" — and the travellers did not see him (025-018).

## The Dead

Burial customs at Meroë differed from Egyptian practice: the dead were either buried in the Nile or kept in the houses "under a mica slab of human form" (025-018). This detail highlights the cultural distance between Ethiopian and Nilotic civilizations, even as both share the great river.

## Significance

Meroë represents the farthest reach of the tour — the boundary where "the civilized world ended" and the temptations of the unknown began. [[caleb|Caleb]] had warned of travellers who ventured beyond and saw [[typhon|Typhon]]'s "awful giant head appear above the edge of the world, with gaping mouth" (025-008). Beyond Meroë lay "the secret of the world's end, the secret of the sources of the Nile, the secret of the horizons of the earth, the secret of the endless sea surrounding the world" (025-008).
