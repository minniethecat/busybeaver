# Ghizla

## Overview

Master Ghizla is a [[saba|Saba]]ean merchant and the owner of the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]], the largest guesthouse (diversorium) in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. Tall, lean, and dignified, he represents the cosmopolitan commercial class of Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — fluent in a linguistic mix of Latin, Greek, Phoenician, and Arabic (003-011), and skilled in the elaborate courtesies of Eastern hospitality. He provides [[lucius|Lucius]]'s party with "princely suites" that have previously housed the Persian Prince Kardusi and "Baabab, Satrap of Mesopotamia" (003-013), and serves as the fixed commercial anchor to his brother [[caleb|Caleb]]'s more performative, outward-facing role as guide and entertainer.

## In the Narrative

Ghizla is introduced when he greets [[lucius|Lucius]] at the quay with "many salaams and respectful assurances, uttered in a language that fluctuated between Latin, Greek, Phoenician and Arabic" (003-011). His polyglot speech is the first marker of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s cosmopolitan character — a city where commerce requires fluency across cultures and where identity itself is fluid.

His commercial instincts are refined and his patter well-rehearsed. He boasts of the baths with "very cold water and taps with very hot water" (003-013), the "old [[lake-mareotis|Mareotis]] wine … thick as ink, dark-purple as molten princely sealing-wax" (003-018), and "all the Ethiopian liqueurs" (003-018). When [[catullus|Catullus]] inquires about the kitchens, Ghizla diverts him with wine-cellar bragging: "We have old [[lake-mareotis|Mareotis]] wine in our cellars, my lord, wine thick as ink, dark-purple as molten princely sealing-wax and fragrant as the own lotus of our [[isis|Lady Isis]], blessed be her name!" (003-018). The rhetoric is florid, the hospitality lavish, and the prices — one suspects — commensurately inflated.

The [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] itself is a monument to Ghizla's commercial vision. He leads his "princely guests" through the diversorium (003-097), showing off "the latest conveniences" (003-098) and the triclinium — "the dining-room which Master Ghizla described by this high-sounding name" (003-100–101). [[lucius|Lucius]]'s party arrives at the guesthouse along the Hippodrome, where Ghizla stands beside a statue, bowing "low, with his hands stretched groundwards" (003-092–093). When the party returns from their [[alexandria|Alexandria]] tour, Ghizla has dinner "prepared by Master Ghizla's care" (003-021), and he pours wine himself "out of a jar still warm from the sun" (003-103) — the personal touch that elevates commerce to hospitality.

## Ghizla and Caleb: Two Faces of Sabaean Enterprise

Ghizla and his brother [[caleb|Caleb]] form a complementary pair. Ghizla is the dignified businessman — bowing, pouring wine, managing the guesthouse — while [[caleb|Caleb]] is the vivacious performer, prancing on horseback and guiding tourists through [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s monuments (003-093, 003-030). Together they represent the [[saba|Saba]]ean commercial diaspora at work in Roman Egypt: one brother creates the infrastructure of hospitality, the other animates it with narrative and entertainment. Ghizla's occasional deference to [[caleb|Caleb]] — pointing to his brother to lead the tour (003-030) — suggests a deliberate division of labour rather than any real subordination.

The figure of Ghizla also embodies a distinctively Roman-Egyptian type: the *hospes* or *hospitator* — the innkeeper who is not merely a service provider but a cultural mediator, translating [[alexandria|Alexandria]] for Roman visitors through food, wine, architecture, and the ritualised language of Eastern courtesy. His polyglot speech and eclectic guest list (Persian princes, Mesopotamian satraps, Roman nobles) make the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] a microcosm of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] itself.
