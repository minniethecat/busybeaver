# Vettius

## Overview

Vettius is [[lucius|Lucius]]'s Roman steward (*vilicus* or *procurator*), who travels ahead of his master to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to secure suitable accommodation. Along with [[rufus|Rufus]] the under-steward, Vettius represents the efficient domestic machinery that makes a wealthy Roman's travel possible — the invisible labour of slaves and freedmen that enables [[lucius|Lucius]]'s leisured tourism.

## In the Narrative

Vettius meets the travellers at the [[alexandria|Alexandria]] quay, coming "hurrying across the gangway" alongside [[ghizla|Ghizla]] the [[saba|Saba]]ean (003-008). He is described as "an obvious Latin" (003-008) — his Roman identity immediately legible. His report is confident: "I am convinced that, when we have put in our own furniture, they will afford a fit residence for you and the honourable [[catullus|Catullus]]" (003-011).

Vettius has worked efficiently alongside [[rufus|Rufus]] to prepare the suites: "They had worked to good purpose, conveying furniture, boxes and packing-cases on camels and mules" (003-097). The result is an apartment that reproduces the comforts of a Roman home in Egypt, complete with Babylonian carpets, bronze and marble statues, and all the "brushes, tweezers and unguent-sticks, in gold adorned with agates" (003-097) essential to the Roman toilet.

[[lucius|Lucius]]'s approval — "[[lucius|Lucius]] assured Vettius and [[rufus|Rufus]] that he was really pleased; indeed it was as though he were at home" (003-102) — speaks to the steward's success in transplanting domestic comfort to foreign soil.

## Key Passages

> "Well, Vettius!" said Lucius, welcoming the Latin, who was his steward. "I am glad to see you again and I hope that your voyage was as prosperous as ours!" (003-009)

> "Vettius and Rufus received the travellers on the threshold. They had worked to good purpose, conveying furniture, boxes and packing-cases on camels and mules." (003-097)
