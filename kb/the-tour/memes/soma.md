# Soma

## Overview

The Soma (Greek *Sōma*, "body") was the mausoleum complex in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] that housed the embalmed body of [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] alongside the pyramid-shaped tombs of the Ptolemaic dynasty. Located in the [[bruchium|Bruchium]], or royal quarter, and approached by a long avenue of recumbent sphinxes, it was one of the most significant pilgrimage sites of the ancient Mediterranean. In *The Tour*, the visit to the Soma provides one of the novel's most densely theological passages — a sustained meditation on death, [[mummification|embalming]], Egyptian eschatology, and the perishability of earthly power — and triggers [[lucius|Lucius]]'s renewed anguish over the disappearance of [[ilia]].

## In the Narrative

The approach to the Soma is described with ceremonial gravity: "A long avenue of recumbent sphinxes, male — bearded — and female — high-breasted — led to the [[pyramid|pyramid tombs]]" (003-069), set in "the cool shade of sycamores and tamarisks" (003-069). The old priests in charge greet the travellers and [[caleb|Caleb]] announces them as "princely nobles" who "will also be interested in the tomb of [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]]" (003-070).

The custodian priest delivers a formal theological exposition that blends Egyptian mortuary doctrine with commercial pragmatism. He describes the cosmic destiny of the dead monarchs: "Death is but a slumbering and a twilight transition to the halls of eternal sunshine. Earthly greatness is the perishable step to the imperishable palace of [[osiris|Osiris]], where our dead monarchs now sit enthroned around him, their heads circled with the pschent and their hands grasping the scarab sceptre" (003-071).

The interior of the tombs is rendered as a phantasmagoria of Egyptian funerary iconography: "Through the mist of the aromatics the golden, winged suns gleamed in the embrace of the snakes coiled tail in mouth. Sacred [[horus|Horus]], son of [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[isis|Isis]], the radiant redeemer of mankind … bestrode [[typhon|Typhon]], the grinning spirit of evil. There were images of the god [[apis|Apis]], of the god Râ, of Thoth and [[anubis|Anubis]], with the heads of an ox, an ibis, a dog" (003-073).

The climax of the visit is the tomb of Alexander himself. Behind a bronze railing, "on a basalt pedestal stood a sarcophagus of transparent crystal, polished and engraved" (003-076). Inside, "in a green watery light, where the flame of the lamps was mirrored in the glass, a [[mummification|mummy]] lay visible … like the chrysalis of a gigantic moth" with "eyes of beryl" and "hair and short beard … painted gold" (003-076). The beryl eyes are described as "full of amazement at what they saw in eternity" (003-077).

The priest recounts the body's turbulent history — how [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] I seized it from Perdiccas, how it was originally interred in a gold sarcophagus, and how that was stolen by a pretender before the body was rescued and placed in the crystal coffin (003-078) — before delivering the punchline: "Even to your excellencies the charge is only one gold stater a head …" (003-083). The sudden shift from eschatological sublimity to commercial transaction is characteristic of Cousijns's ironic method.

The emotional effect on [[lucius|Lucius]] is immediate: stepping out of the sepulchre, he turns pale and says, "Death! … Death! … She is perhaps dead. … She is drowned, perhaps, in the sea" (003-090). The sight of the embalmed conqueror becomes a mirror for his own loss.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Alexander died in Babylon in 323 BCE. His body was being transported to Macedon when [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] I intercepted the funeral cortège and diverted it to Egypt, first to [[memphis|Memphis]] and later to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where a magnificent tomb was constructed. The body became a dynastic relic and tourist attraction for centuries. Roman emperors including Julius Caesar, Augustus, and Septimius Severus are recorded as having visited the tomb. The crystal sarcophagus described in the novel appears to be a later replacement for the original gold coffin. The exact location of the Soma remains one of the great unsolved mysteries of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n archaeology.
