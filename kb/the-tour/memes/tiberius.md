# Tiberius

## Overview

Tiberius Julius Caesar Augustus was the second Roman emperor, ruling from 14 to 37 CE. The stepson and successor of Augustus, Tiberius was a capable but increasingly reclusive ruler who spent his final years on the island of Capri. In *The Tour*, Tiberius appears only in one crucial reference: the villa at [[baiae|Baiae]] — [[lucius|Lucius]]'s former property where [[ilia|Ilia]] lived — is now "the property of our gracious Emperor Tiberius" (003-011).

## In the Narrative

The single reference to Tiberius occurs when [[vettius|Vettius]] the steward describes the accommodations: "Of course, when travelling, all conveniences are but temporary and not to be compared with your insula at Rome or your villa at [[baiae|Baiae]], now the property of our gracious Emperor Tiberius" (003-011). This brief mention serves two narrative functions: it dates the story precisely to the reign of Tiberius (14–37 CE), and it hints at the political context — the concentration of property in imperial hands under the Julio-Claudian dynasty.

Later, a passing reference to "our gracious emperor, Augustus Tiberius" (027-038) reinforces the dating and the deferential form of address expected toward the emperor.

## Historical Context

Tiberius succeeded Augustus in 14 CE after a long career of military command. His reign was marked by competent administration but also by political purges, the growing power of the Praetorian Guard, and the increasing authority of the *delatores* (informers). By the time of *The Tour*'s setting, Tiberius had largely withdrawn from Rome to Capri, leaving governance in the hands of the Praetorian Prefect Sejanus. The transfer of aristocratic villas to imperial ownership, mentioned in the novel, was a real phenomenon of this period.
