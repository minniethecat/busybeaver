# Naucratis

Naucratis was a Greek trading colony in the [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]], founded in the 7th century BCE under the Saite pharaohs as a controlled port of trade. It was the primary point of contact between Greek merchants and Egypt for centuries. In *The Tour*, Naucratis appears as a waypoint on the travellers' journey through the Delta and as the legendary home of the courtesan Rhodopis, whose story is told at the black pyramid.

## In the Narrative

The barge carrying [[lucius|Lucius]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], [[caleb|Caleb]], and [[cora|Cora]] departs Naucratis in the afternoon, travelling along the canals of the Saitic nome towards [[sais|Sais]]:

> "The barge that afternoon had left Naucratis along the canals which seam the Saitic nome, or province. They were now nearing the capital, Sais, the capital of all Lower Egypt." (014-007)

As they approach [[sais|Sais]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] points out the [[obelisk|obelisks]] and the temple of [[isis|Isis-Neith]], where the [[burning-lamps|Night of the Glowing Lights]] — the [[burning-lamps|Feast of the Burning Lamps]] — is about to be celebrated.

Naucratis is also the setting of the Rhodopis legend, told to the travellers by the old priest at the black pyramid near [[memphis|Memphis]]. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] identifies the black pyramid not as the tomb of [[cleopatra|Cleopatra]] (as [[caleb|Caleb]] claims) but as the tomb of Doricha, a courtesan of Naucratis:

> "The historians speak of Doricha, an hetaira mentioned by Sappho, the famous poetess, as the mistress of her brother Charaxus, who was a wine-merchant at Lésbos and travelled constantly to Naucratis." (017-011)

The priest corrects both of them, telling the story of Rhodopis:

> "The pyramid of Rhodopis. She lived at Naucratis and was incomparably beautiful and chaste. One day, when she was bathing, an eagle flew through the open ceiling of the bathroom and plucked from her maid's hands the sandal which she was just about to lace on her mistress' foot." (017-025)

The eagle carried the sandal to [[memphis|Memphis]], where it dropped it into the king's lap. The king searched all Egypt for the woman whose foot fit the tiny sandal, found Rhodopis at Naucratis, married her, and upon her death built a great pyramid in her memory (017-027). The priest shows the travellers the very sandal, preserved in a crystal shrine (017-028).

This story deeply affects [[lucius|Lucius]], who trembles "in every limb" — the tale of a lost beloved and a sandal as the sole surviving token echoes his own obsession with [[ilia|Ilia]]. Later, the spirit of Rhodopis is invoked by the old priest as he gives [[lucius|Lucius]] a vision of [[ilia|Ilia]]'s fate (017-036, 017-053).

## Historical Context

Naucratis was historically the first and, for much of its early history, the only Greek settlement in Egypt. It was granted monopoly rights over Greek trade by the pharaoh Amasis (Ahmose II) of the 26th Dynasty. The city was home to several Greek temples, including the Hellenion, and was famous for its hetairai (courtesans). The story of Rhodopis — widely recognised as an ancient precursor to the Cinderella tale — is recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] (Histories 2.134–135) and [[strabo|Strabo]] (Geography 17.1.33), who identify her with the pyramid of Mycerinus at Giza. Couperus adapts this tradition freely, relocating the story to the small black pyramid near [[memphis|Memphis]] and weaving it into the emotional fabric of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s quest for [[ilia|Ilia]].
