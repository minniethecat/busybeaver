## Overview

In ancient Egyptian religion, animals were not merely symbols of the divine but were believed to be actual incarnations of the gods. Each nome (district) held particular animals sacred: the ibis embodied Thoth, the falcon [[horus|Horus]], the cat Bastet, the crocodile [[crocodile|Such]], and the bull [[apis|Apis]] — the most famous of all — incarnated [[osiris|Osiris]]. In *The Tour*, the travellers encounter this practice in multiple locations: the [[ibis|ibises]] on [[alexandria|Alexandria]]'s quays, the [[crocodile|sacred crocodile]] at [[lake-moeris|Lake Moeris]], and the [[apis|Apis bull]] at [[memphis|Memphis]]. The cumulative effect of these encounters is a growing fatigue — the sheer number and variety of sacred animals overwhelms the Roman visitors, who find the Egyptian blurring of the boundary between animal and deity alternately wondrous and wearisome.

## In the Narrative

The travellers encounter sacred animals from the moment they disembark. The [[ibis|ibises]] on the [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n quays [003-007] are the first living emblem of Egyptian religion that [[lucius|Lucius]] observes — sacred birds moving freely among the crowds, unconfined by temple precincts. At [[memphis|Memphis]], the [[apis|Apis bull]] is the pilgrims' principal destination; the priests explain that "the incarnation of the god in the sacred bull lasts a quarter of a century" before the bull is drowned and a new [[apis|Apis]] sought [015-044]. The travellers find the [[apis|Apis]] impressive, his black coat gleaming and adorned with sacred markings [015-068].

The mood shifts by the time they reach [[lake-moeris|Lake Moeris]], where they encounter the [[crocodile|sacred crocodile]] [[crocodile|Such]] [024-006]. [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], initially curious about Egyptian animal worship, openly rebels: "honestly, I'm not going to feed any more [[crocodile|sacred crocodile]]s, nor any sacred goats or cats or beetles either" [024-017]. He enumerates the list of sacred animals — "cats and falcons, sheep and wolves, baboons and zebus, eagles and lions, goats and spiders" [024-016] — with the weariness of someone who has seen too many divine beasts. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], by contrast, remains the patient expositor, explaining the theology behind each local cult.

## Theological Background

Egyptian animal worship was not zoolatry in the crude sense Romans sometimes assumed. Animals were not worshipped for their own sake but as vessels for divine manifestation. The god chose to dwell temporarily in a particular animal, rendering that creature sacred. The incarnation was marked by specific physical signs: the [[apis|Apis bull]] had to be black with a white triangle on the forehead, a white crescent on the flank, and other prescribed markings [015-068]. When the animal died or the period of incarnation ended (25 years for [[apis|Apis]] [015-044]), the god departed and the priests sought a new vessel — searching "throughout sacred Egypt" [015-044] for the next animal bearing the required marks. The mummified bodies of sacred animals were interred with ceremony in vast catacombs; millions of ibis mummies have been discovered by archaeologists.

## Catullus's Fatigue and Roman Skepticism

[[catullus|Catullus]]'s growing exasperation with sacred animals serves a narrative function beyond comic relief. His complaint — "I'm sick of all these sacred animals" [024-032] — expresses a distinctively Roman scepticism toward Egyptian religion. Where [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] approaches each new sacred creature with scholarly curiosity, [[catullus|Catullus]] experiences the proliferation of animal cults as an assault on his senses and on Roman notions of the proper separation between the mundane and the divine. His fatigue mirrors the reader's own potential saturation: the novel itself, through [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s endless knowledge, risks overwhelming its audience with Egyptian detail, and [[catullus|Catullus]] provides a valve for that pressure.

## Key Passages

> "And who drew the plough through the fields of sacred Egypt when he disguised himself with the other gods, under the forms of animals.…" [015-037]

> "The incarnation of the god in the sacred bull lasts a quarter of a century" the young, pleasant, smiling priest explained. "After being incarnated in the bull for five-and-twenty years, the god disappears out of the bull and the bull is marked down for death. The priests drown him solemnly in the Nile and embalm his sacred body…" [015-044]

> "Cats and falcons, sheep and wolves, baboons and zebus, eagles and lions, goats and spiders: all the animals were worshipped in one or other town or village; all the animals had their temples and priests…" [024-016]
