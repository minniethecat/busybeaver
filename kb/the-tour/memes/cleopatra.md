# Cleopatra

## Overview

Cleopatra VII Philopator was the last active ruler of the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Egypt, reigning from 51 to 30 BCE. A figure of immense historical and legendary significance, she was renowned for her intelligence, political acumen, and relationships with Julius Caesar and Mark Antony. In *The Tour*, Cleopatra appears as a recurring point of reference — invoked by [[caleb|Caleb]] with his characteristic blend of historical inaccuracy and local folklore, and debated by the travellers as they encounter the Delta monuments that popular tradition associates with her name.

## In the Narrative

Cleopatra enters the novel through [[caleb|Caleb]]'s guided tour of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. He describes [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] with his usual grandiosity: "The Museum is, as your lordships know, [[museum-of-alexandria|the Academy of Alexandria]], founded by the beautiful Cleopatra" (003-054). [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] immediately corrects him in a whisper: "That is not true. It was founded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] the First" (003-055). This small, almost comedic moment encapsulates [[caleb|Caleb]]'s character — eager to please, historically unreliable — and the distance between popular legend and scholarly knowledge.

Cleopatra returns during the travellers' exploration of the Delta monuments. At a site of ancient tombs, [[caleb|Caleb]] points to a pyramid-shaped monument and declares it "the pyramid of Cleopatra" (017-022). The claim sparks debate: one of the party objects that "the historians speak of Doris" and that Cleopatra "died in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]" and "was buried in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]" (017-009, 017-011). But [[caleb|Caleb]] insists — "Cleopatra, Cleopatra!" (017-010) — his voice rising with the conviction of the tour guide who cannot afford to be wrong. A nearby black stone monument is said by [[caleb|Caleb]] to have been "adored by all the lovers of Queen Cleopatra" and to mark the place where "she is buried" (017-008). These exchanges reveal how Cleopatra's historical reality has been overlaid with layers of folklore, wishful thinking, and touristic invention — the Queen of the Nile becoming, in death, whatever her admirers need her to be.

## Cleopatra in the Novel's Historical Imagination

Cleopatra functions in *The Tour* as a barometer of historical reliability. Each time her name is invoked, the novel stages a collision between three modes of historical consciousness: [[caleb|Caleb]]'s folk-traditional version (Cleopatra the beautiful founder, Cleopatra of the pyramid), [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s learned scepticism (the textual correction), and the material evidence of the monuments themselves (the tombs, the black stone, the pyramid). None of these modes triumphs absolutely — [[caleb|Caleb]]'s "pyramid of Cleopatra" is demonstrably wrong by scholarly standards (017-009, 017-011), but it reflects a truth about how history lives in popular memory, attaching itself to visible monuments regardless of archaeological fact. The novel treats this not as simple error but as a genuine feature of historical consciousness: the past is always, to some degree, invented by those who visit it.

## Historical Context

Cleopatra VII was a member of the Ptolemaic dynasty, descended from [[ptolemy|Ptolemy I Soter]], one of [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]]'s generals. Unlike her predecessors, she learned the Egyptian language and presented herself as the reincarnation of the goddess [[isis|Isis]]. After her defeat by Octavian (the future Augustus) and the suicide of Mark Antony, she took her own life in 30 BCE, ending the Ptolemaic dynasty and the Hellenistic period. By the time of the novel's setting (early Roman imperial period), Cleopatra's reign was living memory for older citizens — and already passing into legend.
