# Alexandria

## Overview

Alexandria (*Alexandria ad Aegyptum*) was the greatest city of the Hellenistic world and, by the Roman period of *The Tour*, the second city of the Roman Empire after Rome itself. Founded by [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] in 331 BCE and developed under the Ptolemaic dynasty, Alexandria was a metropolis of dazzling cultural hybridity — Greek in its institutions, Egyptian in its religious life, and Roman in its political administration. In the novel, Alexandria serves as both the literal gateway through which [[lucius]] enters Egypt and a symbolic space where Roman rationalism encounters Egyptian mystery. Its temples of [[serapis]] and [[isis]], its bustling quays, and its magnificent palaces form the backdrop for the novel's most vivid urban scenes.

## In the Narrative

The arrival at Alexandria is described as one of the novel's most visually spectacular passages. As the quadrireme glides past the [[pharos|Pharos]] lighthouse into the [[great-harbour|Great Harbour]], Alexandria appears "shining pink through diaphanous, mother-o'-pearl gleams and a slowly-lifting silvery mist, like a city of magic and fairytale" (003-001). The "long, long row of white palaces, with irregular gables" (003-001) conveys the city's architectural magnificence.

The detailed city tour that follows introduces Alexandria's key landmarks: the [[heptastadium|Heptastadium]], the Caesareum, [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]], [[soma|the Soma]] (royal tombs), the Acropolis, the Canopic gate, the diversorium of Master [[ghizla]] (the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]]), [[lake-mareotis]], and the [[canopus|Canopus Canal]]. Each location is layered with historical and cultural significance, creating a composite portrait of the city.

The contrast between Rome and Alexandria is repeatedly drawn. [[lucius|Lucius]] reflects: "It is very different from Rome in every way. It is the east" (003-041). The narrator elaborates: "Rome was monotonously white and colourless beside it" (003-042), with Alexandria characterised by "a motley whirl of colours" — citrons, oranges, melons, exotic fruits "scarlet and vermilion." The city's energy is captured in its perpetual motion: "It was as though everyone were pressed for time and hurrying feverishly" (003-042).

Master [[ghizla]] arranges for two "Alexandrian litters, with twelve Libyan bearers, and ten running Nubian slaves" (003-024), and the travellers begin their exploration. The ibis — "Alexandria's scavengers" (003-035) — wanders the streets, a detail that grounds the city in vivid realism. The travellers' guide reminds them that "all the beauties of Alexandria" promenade at the Canopic way, where "a great nobleman like yourselves would certainly enjoy a stroll" (003-039).

When the travellers prepare to leave Alexandria for the journey south, the narrator notes "the metropolitan press and throng of Alexandria, the world's marketplace" (015-025), reinforcing the city's commercial vitality.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Alexandria was founded on the site of the Egyptian village of [[rhacotis|Rhacotis]] and designed by the architect Dinocrates. It became the capital of Ptolemaic Egypt and the intellectual centre of the Hellenistic world, home to the Great Library and the [[museum-of-alexandria|Museum]]. Under Roman rule (from 30 BCE), Alexandria remained Egypt's administrative capital, governed by a prefect appointed by the emperor.

The city's population in the Roman period has been estimated at 300,000–500,000, making it one of the largest cities of the ancient world. Its population was diverse: Greeks, Egyptians, [[jewish|Jews]], Romans, and traders from across the Mediterranean and Indian Ocean. The division of the city into five quarters (Alpha, Beta, Gamma, Delta, Epsilon) reflected its enormous size.

## Key Passages

> "Alexandria lay before the eyes of the delighted travellers, Lucius, Thrasyllus, Catullus, shining pink through diaphanous, mother-o'-pearl gleams and a slowly-lifting silvery mist, like a city of magic and fairytale." (003-001)

> "It is very different from Rome in every way," thought Lucius. "It is the east." (003-041)

> "The quay, between the obelisks, was alive with a maddening concourse of people: sailors and merchants, vendors of fruit and water and vegetables, chattering women, screaming children, Ethiopian beggars, Greek students, priests of Serapis and Isis, Roman soldiers." (003-005)
