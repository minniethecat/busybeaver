# Thebes

## Overview

Thebes (modern Luxor) was one of the most important cities of ancient Egypt, serving as the capital during the New Kingdom period (c. 1550–1069 BCE). Located in Upper Egypt on the east bank of the [[nile]], Thebes was the cult centre of the god [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]] and the site of the great temple complexes of Karnak and Luxor. In *The Tour*, Thebes is the destination of [[lucius]]'s journey into Upper Egypt, where his encounter with the [[high-priest-of-ammon-ra]] provides the novel's most profound spiritual exchange. Unlike cosmopolitan [[alexandria]] with its hybrid Greek-Egyptian-Roman culture, Thebes represents the ancient, austere, authentically Egyptian religious tradition — a direct conduit to the primordial sources of the Nile's spiritual life.

## In the Narrative

Thebes is invoked early as a destination of hope. [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes sailing "up the Nile, to Thebes, to Ethiopia" (013-006), and even as the travellers face disillusionment at [[memphis|Memphis]], Thebes remains a beacon: "But there is [[memphis|Memphis]], there is Thebes. I still have hope" (014-080). The hope persists into later chapters: "And yet I have hopes. There is Thebes; and there are the secrets of…" (023-005). Thebes functions as the promise that sustains the journey — the destination where ancient [[philosophy|wisdom]] might still be found, even when other sites disappoint.

The city itself arrives with visual drama. After passing "Coptos and [[apollonopolis-magna|Apollonopolis]], Thebes loomed into view, with its hundred-gated walls" (024-020). Known as "Greater Diospolis" to the Greeks (024-021), Thebes "spread itself as an immense" city (024-023), rivalling even [[memphis|Memphis]] in scale. But after Thebes, the journey grows stranger — "to [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]]' despair" (024-031) — as the travellers press beyond the city into the unfamiliar south.

The temple of [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]] at Thebes is where [[lucius|Lucius]] meets "the [[high-priest-of-ammon-ra|centenarian high-priest]] of [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]]" (021-008), who receives him with paternal warmth and guides him into the deeper mysteries of Egyptian religion. The high priest ordains [[lucius|Lucius]]'s path: "Go, my son, to the House of the Sun" (021-011). The encounter with the [[high-priest-of-ammon-ra]] — "the high-priest of [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]], accompanied by eight pastophors" (021-007) — is described with ceremonial gravity, marking a shift from the sensory spectacle of earlier chapters toward a more interior, spiritual register.

Thebes represents a different kind of Egyptian experience from cosmopolitan [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — more ancient, more austere, more authentically connected to the primordial sources of Egyptian religion. The contrast between the commercialised temple cults of the Delta and the ancient sanctity of Thebes is implicit throughout these chapters. At Thebes, [[lucius|Lucius]] finds not the veiled mysteries of [[isis]] and [[serapis]] but a direct, unmediated encounter with a living representative of the divine.

## Historical Context

Thebes reached its zenith during the New Kingdom (18th–20th Dynasties, c. 1550–1069 BCE), when it was the religious and political capital of the Egyptian empire. The temple of Karnak, dedicated to [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]], was the largest religious complex in the ancient world, covering over 100 hectares. On the west bank of the Nile lay the Valley of the Kings, the burial place of New Kingdom pharaohs including Tutankhamun and Ramesses II.

| Period | Status of Thebes |
|--------|-----------------|
| Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE) | Minor provincial town |
| Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE) | Rising importance as cult centre of Amun |
| New Kingdom (c. 1550–1069 BCE) | Imperial capital; Karnak temple complex at its height |
| Third Intermediate Period | Declining political power; remains religious centre |
| Ptolemaic/Roman (332 BCE onward) | [[alexandria|Alexandria]] dominates; Thebes a pilgrimage site of ancient sanctity |

By the Roman period depicted in *The Tour*, Thebes had declined politically but remained an important religious centre, exactly as Couperus uses it — a place where the ancient truths are still accessible to the sincere pilgrim (021-007).
