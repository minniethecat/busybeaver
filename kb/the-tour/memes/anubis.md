# Anubis

## Overview

Anubis is the ancient Egyptian god of mummification, [[mummification|embalming]], and funeral rites, typically depicted as a man with the head of a jackal or dog. He presided over the weighing of the heart ceremony in the afterlife and was the guardian of tombs and cemeteries. In the Hellenistic theology depicted in *The Tour*, Anubis serves as "watchman, servant and comrade" (009-016) of [[serapis|Serapis]], bearing the sacred water vessels whose waters flood the Nile.

## In the Narrative

Anubis first appears in the novel as one of the divine images in the Ptolemaic tombs, listed alongside "the god [[apis|Apis]], of the god Râ, of Thoth and Anubis, with the heads of an ox, an ibis, a dog" (003-073). This visual catalogue establishes the Egyptian pantheon's theriomorphic character — gods with animal heads — which fascinates the Roman travellers.

At the [[serapis|Serapis]] feast, Anubis plays a specific liturgical role. The pastophors, a class of priests, "carried the images of Anubis, with the dog's head, in silver boats" (009-008) in procession through the temple. More significantly, the sacred waters that [[serapis|Serapis]] pours in summer rains come from "the sacred vessels wherewith the dog's-head of Anubis, his watchman, servant and comrade, is crowned" (009-016). This establishes Anubis as an intimate attendant of [[serapis|Serapis]], the keeper of the life-giving waters.

Worship of dogs and dog-headed [[egyptian-deities|deities]] is further contextualised when the travellers pass through "Cynopolis, where the dog is worshipped as Anubis" (024-016), demonstrating that Anubis had his own dedicated cult centres throughout Egypt.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Anubis was one of the most ancient and consistently worshipped [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in Egypt, predating even [[osiris|Osiris]] in some funerary contexts. In the earliest periods, Anubis was the primary god of the dead; as the [[osiris|Osiris]] cult rose to prominence, Anubis was incorporated as [[osiris|Osiris]]'s son (in some traditions) and the god who performed the first mummification on [[osiris|Osiris]]'s body.

In the Greco-Roman period, Anubis was identified with [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes]] in the composite figure of Hermanubis, combining the Egyptian funerary god with the Greek psychopomp who guides souls to the underworld. The image of Anubis being carried in barques or boats (silver boats in the novel's description) was a standard feature of Egyptian temple ritual.
