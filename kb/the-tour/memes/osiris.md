# Osiris

## Overview

Osiris is one of the most important [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in the ancient Egyptian pantheon, presiding over the afterlife, death, resurrection, and the annual flooding of the Nile. According to myth, he was murdered and dismembered by his brother [[typhon|Typhon]] ([[typhon|Set]]), then reassembled and revived by his sister-wife [[isis|Isis]], becoming the ruler of the underworld (003-071). In *The Tour*, Osiris is invoked repeatedly as the divine judge of the dead and is theologically identified with [[serapis|Serapis]], the syncretic Greco-Egyptian deity whose dream-oracle at [[canopus|Canopus]] forms the spiritual centre of the narrative.

## In the Narrative

The cult of Osiris permeates *The Tour* from the moment [[lucius|Lucius]] arrives in [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. At [[soma|the Soma]], the burial place of the Ptolemies, the priest explains that the dead monarchs now sit enthroned around Osiris "in the imperishable palace of Osiris" (003-071), linking Egyptian royal funerary tradition to the god's cosmic reign. The tomb description includes images of "[[horus|Sacred Horus]], son of Osiris and [[isis|Isis]], the radiant redeemer of mankind" (003-073), establishing the divine triad central to Egyptian religion.

The theological identification between Osiris and [[serapis|Serapis]] is stated explicitly during the feast of dreams at [[canopus|Canopus]]: "He is Osiris himself; between him and Osiris there is no difference. He is two. While Osiris is the benevolent Almighty above, he is the benevolent Almighty below" (009-015). Later the text affirms: "He is everything, even as Osiris is" (009-017). This syncretic theology reflects the Ptolemaic fusion of Egyptian and Greek religious concepts.

Osiris also appears in consolation contexts. When the characters discuss suffering, one remarks: "[[isis|Isis]] suffered because of Osiris. All the gods suffer" (023-020), suggesting that divine suffering provides a model for mortal endurance. At a temple near Acanthus, [[lucius|Lucius]] encounters a temple of Osiris set "in a spreading wood of Theban palm-trees" (024-001), demonstrating the god's widespread worship throughout Egypt.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Osiris was worshipped in Egypt from the Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE) through the Roman period. His myth — murder by his jealous brother [[typhon|Set]], the search by [[isis|Isis]], and his resurrection as ruler of the underworld — was one of the most enduring narratives in Egyptian religion (010-047). The Osiris cult centred on Abydos, where an annual festival reenacted his death and resurrection, but temples to Osiris existed throughout Egypt (024-001).

In the Hellenistic and Roman periods (the setting of *The Tour*), Osiris became increasingly identified with [[serapis|Serapis]] through a deliberate Ptolemaic policy of religious syncretism. The composite deity [[serapis|Osiris-Apis]] (Osorapis) merged the funerary aspects of Osiris with the living bull-god [[apis|Apis]], creating a Hellenised deity accessible to both Greek and Egyptian worshippers (009-015). Couperus's text faithfully reflects this theological synthesis.

## Key Passages

> "Death is but a slumbering and a twilight transition to the halls of eternal sunshine. Earthly greatness is the perishable step to the imperishable palace of Osiris, where our dead monarchs now sit enthroned around him, their heads circled with the pschent and their hands grasping the scarab sceptre." (003-071)

> "He is Osiris himself; between him and Osiris there is no difference. He is two. While Osiris is the benevolent Almighty above, he is the benevolent Almighty below. He opposes Typhon, even as Osiris combated Typhon. Victory falls to him in the end, even as it did to Osiris." (009-015)

> "There is comfort. Isis seeking for Osiris recovered all the pieces of his mangled body." (010-047)

> "Isis suffered because of Osiris. All the gods suffer." (023-020)

> "He is Serapis Osiris, who loves his Egypt and but seldom sends universal plagues." (015-044)
