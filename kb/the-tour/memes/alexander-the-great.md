# Alexander the Great

## Overview

Alexander III of Macedon (356–323 BCE), known as [[alexander|Alexander the Great]], was one of history's most extraordinary conquerors, creating an empire that stretched from Greece to India before his death at age thirty-three. In *The Tour*, Alexander appears not as a living character but as a mummified presence — his crystal sarcophagus at [[soma|the Soma]] in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] is one of the novel's most powerful images, provoking in [[lucius|Lucius]] a meditation on mortality, greatness, and the persistence of the body beyond the soul (003-076, 003-079).

## In the Narrative

The visit to Alexander's tomb occurs in chapter III, when the travellers tour [[soma|the Soma]], the Ptolemaic burial complex. The priest in charge leads them to a gleaming marble pyramid and announces: "The tomb of Alexander of Macedon" (003-075). Inside, the crystal sarcophagus holds a [[mummification|mummy]] "like the chrysalis of a gigantic moth. The face was stained brown with balsam and salve and stared with eyes of beryl. The hair and short beard were painted gold" (003-076). The [[mummification|mummy]] rests on "a mattress of striped byssus, the head on a byssus pillow" (003-077), and the scarlet lips "seemed to grin in the crisp golden beard" while the beryl eyes were "full of amazement at what they saw in eternity" (003-077).

The priest provides a detailed history: Alexander's body was seized by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] from Perdiccas, who was bringing it from Babylon to Macedon; it was buried in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] in a gold sarcophagus, later stolen by a pretender named [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] Parisactus, then recovered and placed in the crystal coffin where it now lies (003-078). The body is "nearly three centuries old" (003-079). After the visit, one character exclaims: "We have seen the [[mummification|mummy]] of Alexander!" (003-091) — the encounter reduced, in retrospect, to a tourist trophy.

[[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions are "greatly moved" (003-079). The question posed — "Was this chrysalis all that remained of the great Alexander, whom the oracle of [[ammon-ra|Ammon]] had declared to be the son of [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Râ]]?" (003-079) — connects Alexander to the Egyptian religious framework while posing the novel's central existential question: what survives of greatness after death? The visit prompts [[lucius|Lucius]]'s anguished cry about [[ilia|Ilia]], linking the conqueror's mummified eternity to the beloved's unknown fate (003-079).

## The Chrysalis and the Conqueror

The image of Alexander's [[mummification|mummy]] as a "chrysalis" (003-076) is the novel's most concentrated meditation on the relationship between body and soul, mortality and transformation. A chrysalis is a transitional form — not the larva that entered it, not yet the imago that will emerge. By describing the [[mummification|mummy]] as a chrysalis, the novel suggests that [[mummification|embalming]] is not an end but a pause: the body awaits transformation, and the conqueror who once ruled the known world now waits, in gilded stillness, for a resurrection that may or may not come.

The juxtaposition of Alexander's earthly conquests with his present immobility is devastating. The man who marched armies across Asia, who was declared a god by the [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] (003-079), now lies "closely fastened together in a case of gold filagree" (003-076), his lips grinning, his beryl eyes staring at a heaven he cannot reach. The novel offers no consolation for this — only the image, set in green watery light, of power reduced to preservation.

## Historical Context

Alexander died in Babylon in 323 BCE. His body was indeed seized by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] I and brought to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where it became a major attraction. The tomb ([[soma|the Soma]], meaning "body" in Greek) was visited by Roman emperors including Julius Caesar, Augustus, and Septimius Severus. The body's ultimate fate is unknown; the tomb was likely destroyed during the Christianisation of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. Couperus's description of the crystal sarcophagus follows ancient accounts of the elaborate presentation of Alexander's remains.
