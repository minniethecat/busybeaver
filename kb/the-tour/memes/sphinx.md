# The Sphinx

## Overview

[[neith|The Great Sphinx]] of Giza is a monumental limestone statue with the body of a lion and the head of a human, carved from a single outcrop of bedrock during the reign of [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]] Khafre (c. 2558–2532 BCE). It is the largest monolith statue in the world and one of the most iconic monuments of ancient Egypt. In *The Tour*, Couperus makes a striking interpretive move: he identifies the Sphinx not as a royal portrait but as a manifestation of the primordial goddess [[neith|Neith]], the embodiment of eternal [[philosophy|wisdom]].

## In the Narrative

The travellers approach the Sphinx and the Giza pyramids in chapters XV to XVIII of the novel. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], the learned Greek tutor, is the first to articulate the [[neith|Neith]] interpretation: "The great Sphinx, the immense [[neith|Neith]], the ever-silent [[philosophy|wisdom]]!" (015-023). He elaborates: "Sand-women who were the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of [[neith|Neith]]! What colossal statues, hewn out of the rock!" (015-028), suggesting that the Sphinx and similar desert sculptures are not merely monuments but vessels of divine [[philosophy|wisdom]].

The encounter with the Sphinx prompts a sustained philosophical meditation. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] reflects: "A shroud covered up the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of [[neith|Neith]]. One day the shroud will cover up all and nothing will remain. All is vanity: thy [[philosophy|wisdom]] alone, O [[neith|Neith]], is needful to man!" (017-014–017), echoing the language of Ecclesiastes. The [[philosophy|wisdom]] of the Sphinx-[[neith|Neith]] is presented as the one true value in a transient world.

The novel's most mystical passage involves the Sphinx at night, when [[lucius|Lucius]] experiences it alone under the stars: "The Sphinx loomed before him in the night" (018-007), and he contemplates "the immense [[neith|Neith]], the omniscient [[philosophy|wisdom]]" (018-007) across the shimmering silver sea of moonlit sand. The Sphinx becomes a gateway to deeper consciousness: "It was not the veiled [[isis|Isis]] or the hidden [[serapis|Serapis]]: it was a vision of the truth" (018-008).

## Historical/Cultural Context

[[neith|The Great Sphinx]] of Giza was carved during the Fourth Dynasty and likely bears the face of [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]] Khafre. In Egyptian texts, the Sphinx was associated with the solar god Hor-em-akhet ("[[horus|Horus]] of the Horizon"). During the New Kingdom, Thutmose IV erected the Dream Stela between its paws, recording a dream in which the Sphinx promised him the throne in exchange for clearing away the sand that buried it.

By the Greco-Roman period, the Sphinx had acquired layers of reinterpretation. Greek travellers identified it with various beings. Couperus's identification of the Sphinx with [[neith|Neith]] is a literary innovation — [[neith|Neith]] was more commonly associated with [[sais|Sais]] rather than Giza — but it serves the novel's thematic interest in silent, eternal [[philosophy|wisdom]] as a counterpoint to human vanity.
