## Overview

Hieroglyphs (from the Greek *hieroglyphika*, "sacred carving") were the pictorial writing system of ancient Egypt, used primarily for religious and monumental inscriptions. Consisting of over 700 signs representing sounds, words, and determinatives, hieroglyphs were already an ancient and partly mysterious script by the Roman period. In *The Tour*, hieroglyphics appear as decorative and sacred elements on temple walls, pylons, sarcophagus lids, and even embroidered textiles — visual markers of Egyptian antiquity that the Roman travellers observe without being able to read. The script functions as a metaphor for Egypt itself: formidably ancient, visually magnificent, and ultimately impenetrable to outsiders.

## In the Narrative

The travellers encounter hieroglyphics first on the [[sarcophagus|sarcophagi]] in an [[alexandria|Alexandria]]n mortuary temple, where "marble, inscribed with hieroglyphics, rose like temples, pyramid-shaped" (003-072) — the script is associated with death, mystery, and ancient sanctity. The carved signs on tomb architecture create a visual register of permanence: the dead speak forever in a language the living cannot understand.

Later, hieroglyphics appear in a strikingly different context: a character wears a garment of "striped byssus, with a border of hieroglyphics worked in heavy embroidery" (007-044). This transposition of sacred script onto luxury fabric shows how hieroglyphs had entered the realm of fashion and decorative art — emptied of their religious meaning, they become exotic ornament for Roman consumption. The tension between the script's sacred origins and its commodification as decoration is characteristic of the novel's treatment of Egyptian culture under Roman rule.

The approach to a major temple is described through "heavy pylons, painted with hieroglyphics: a veritable forest of pylons" (009-004), the sheer density of inscribed signs overwhelming the eye. The pronaos, or inner porch, gives admission to the sanctuary "by the roof, with its painted hieroglyphics" (009-006) — the script extends from ground to ceiling, turning the temple interior into a total textual environment. The metaphor of a "forest" suggests both profusion and illegibility — one cannot see the trees for the wood. The travellers are surrounded by a script they cannot decipher, reinforcing their position as outsiders to Egypt's deepest secrets.

Hieroglyphics possess a sacred authority that extends beyond decoration. The sacred scrolls in the temple, "whose hieroglyphics gave answer to all things" (010-004), represent the script in its most potent form — not merely inscription but divine revelation, a writing system that contains within itself the answers to cosmic questions. This claim, reported without irony, captures the awe with which hieroglyphs were regarded even by those who could not read them: the signs were believed to encode not just language but truth itself. The image of [[isis|Isis]] in the temple is described with her "great robe, which had a silvery film, seamed with hieroglyphics" (014-041), the script adorning the body of the goddess herself — a union of word and divinity that elevates hieroglyphs to the status of sacred adornment.

The travellers encounter fallen colossal statues "mysteriously carved with hieroglyphics" lying "as felled Titans" (017-001), while nearby they see "gigantic figures of gods and with hieroglyphics" (017-015) covering every surface. The image of hieroglyph-covered colossi toppled and broken adds a melancholy dimension — the script outlasts the monuments that bear it, but even the signs cannot prevent the fall of empires. The [[obelisk|obelisks]] also bear witness: "there reigned, according to the hieroglyphs on the [[obelisk|obelisks]], over Scythia" (024-024), a tantalising reference to historical records encoded in the ancient script, hinting at lost chronicles of conquest accessible only to those who could read the signs.

## The Silence of the Signs

A central irony of the novel's treatment of hieroglyphs is that the travellers stand before one of the most information-rich writing systems ever devised, yet they receive nothing from it. The hieroglyphs encode royal decrees, hymns to the gods, funerary spells, and historical annals — but to Roman eyes they are merely decorative patterns. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], for all his learning, cannot read them; the knowledge the signs contain remains sealed. This silence mirrors the broader theme of Egypt as a repository of [[philosophy|wisdom]] that the travellers can glimpse but never fully possess.

## Historical Context

By the Roman period, the ability to read hieroglyphs was increasingly restricted to a small class of priests. The last known hieroglyphic inscription dates to AD 394 at [[philae|Philae]]. The script was not deciphered until Jean-François Champollion used the Rosetta Stone in 1822. For Romans, hieroglyphs were objects of fascination — seen as repositories of lost Egyptian [[philosophy|wisdom]] accessible only to initiates (010-004). The Emperor Hadrian reportedly collected Egyptian antiquities, and [[obelisk|obelisks]] inscribed with hieroglyphs were transported to Rome as trophies of empire (024-024).
