## Overview

Eratosthenes of Cyrene (c. 276–194 BCE) was a Greek polymath who served as the chief librarian of the Library of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] III Euergetes. He is most famous for calculating the circumference of the Earth with remarkable accuracy, but his geographical writings — describing the lands of Egypt and beyond — were equally influential. In *The Tour*, his works serve as a travel guide for the Roman visitors to Egypt, whose tutor [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] has studied Eratosthenes's descriptions in preparation for the journey [003-025].

## In the Narrative

Eratosthenes appears only through his writings, not as a living character — he lived two centuries before the novel's events. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], the learned tutor accompanying the travellers, has read the works of Eratosthenes and [[strabo|Strabo]] before the journey and uses their geographical descriptions to guide the party [003-025]. Eratosthenes is described with admiration as "[[ptolemy|Ptolemy Evergetes]]' librarian" [004-013] and as "the most respectable guide" for those travelling through Egypt [004-014]. His knowledge provides the intellectual framework through which the Roman visitors understand the geography and history of the land they are exploring.

## Historical Context

The real Eratosthenes was born in Cyrene (modern Libya) and educated in Athens before being summoned to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] III. As head of the Library, he had access to the accumulated knowledge of the ancient world. His calculation of the Earth's circumference — based on the angles of shadows at different latitudes — was within 2–15% of the true value. He also created a system of latitude and longitude, wrote on chronology and literary criticism, and composed the *Geographica*, which [[strabo|Strabo]] later used as a source.
