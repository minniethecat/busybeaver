## Overview

Pelusium was an important fortress city and port at the easternmost mouth of the Nile, marking the traditional frontier between Egypt and Syria-Palestine. It stood at the Mediterranean entrance to the [[nechao-canal|Nechao Canal]], the ancient waterway connecting the Nile to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]]. In *The Tour*, Pelusium is a brief but crucial waypoint — the point where [[vettius|Vettius]] bribed the authorities to save [[lucius|Lucius]]'s last remaining asset.

## In the Narrative

Pelusium is mentioned twice in the novel, both in connection with the [[quadrireme]]'s movements and the network of canals that connect Egypt's waterways.

[[caleb|Caleb]] first mentions Pelusium when proposing the alternative travel route for [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]]:

> "At Berenice you will meet the quadrireme, which has gone by Pelusium and the Nechao Canal and is ascending the Arabian Gulf to fetch us at the pillars of Sesostris." [024-032]

The quadrireme's route from the Mediterranean through Pelusium into the [[nechao-canal|Nechao Canal]] and down to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] traces the full length of Egypt's canal infrastructure.

Later, when [[vettius|Vettius]] recounts how he saved the quadrireme from imperial confiscation, Pelusium reappears as the critical point of escape:

> "By bribing the authorities at Pelusium with the money that remained in my hands, I managed secretly to pass through the Nechao Canal to Arsinoe; and at Berenice we met your Uncle Catullus and informed him of the terrible news." [027-040]

[[vettius|Vettius]]'s bribe at Pelusium is the decisive act that preserves the quadrireme — and with it, [[lucius|Lucius]]'s last financial hope after [[tiberius|Tiberius]] confiscates all his other possessions. The passage through Pelusium into the canal system represents the thin line between ruin and survival.

## Historical Context

In the Roman period, Pelusium was a city of considerable strategic and commercial importance. Located at the Pelusiac mouth — the easternmost branch of the Nile — it was known in antiquity as the "key of Egypt," guarding the land route from Syria-Palestine into the Delta. Its name derived from the Greek *pelos* (mud), a reference to the silty waters of its harbour. The city was the site of several decisive battles: [[cambyses|Cambyses II]] of Persia defeated the Egyptians here in 525 BCE, and it featured prominently in the campaigns of Alexander's successors and later Roman civil wars. The [[nechao-canal|Nechao Canal]], which began near Pelusium, made the city a vital transit point for goods moving between the Mediterranean and the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]]. In the early Roman Empire, Pelusium housed a garrison and served as a customs station for goods entering and leaving Egypt from the east.
