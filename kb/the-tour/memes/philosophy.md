# Philosophy

## Overview

Greek philosophy permeates the intellectual culture of Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]] depicted in *The Tour*. The Museum, with its colonnades and cenaculum (refectory), houses "philosophers and scholars in every branch of science" surrounded by "thousands of disciples from all countries" (003-056). Philosophy in the novel serves as a secular counterpoint to the religious mysticism of the [[serapis|Serapis]] and [[isis|Isis]] cults — a different path to wisdom, though one that the novel treats with both respect for its achievements and gentle irony about its practitioners' poverty.

## In the Narrative

The travellers visit [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] in chapter III, where they encounter the philosophical community at its midday meal. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] provides the scholarly perspective, praising [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]]'s historical achievements: "Euclid, Erasistratus and Diophantus; then there were the poets Theocritus, Aratus, Callimachus; and among critics Aristarchus; and among philosophers more than I could name" (003-057). This catalogue establishes [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]]'s intellectual prestige.

[[caleb|Caleb]], the worldly guide, provides the comic counterpoint: "they live on a fund provided by the State: they're no use for anything, these learned gentry; but they are certainly clever" (003-058). His observation about their "black broth" and the fact that "they are sitting, instead of reclining" (003-059) contrasts the philosophers' asceticism with Roman luxury.

The most poignant philosophical moment comes when an aged philosopher approaches the strangers: "The highest philosophy is … to be satisfied with little," he says "in pure Greek," then bows "ironically" and turns away "with the movement of a long dry stalk, in his dirty cloak" (003-062–063). This encounter distils Greek philosophy to its essence — and simultaneously questions its worldly value.

[[lucius|Lucius]]'s own intellectual interests include "astronomy in particular, philosophy, magic" (003-020), showing that for educated Romans, philosophy was one branch of a broader pursuit of knowledge that encompassed both rational inquiry and esoteric practice.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Museum ([[museum-of-alexandria|Mouseion]], "shrine of the Muses") of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] was founded by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy I Soter]] and developed under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] II Philadelphus as a research institution modelled on Aristotle's Lyceum. It housed scholars in various disciplines, supported by a state stipend, and was associated with the famous Library of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The philosophers and scientists who worked there — including Euclid (geometry), [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] (geography), Aristarchus (astronomy), and Herophilos (anatomy) — made foundational contributions to Western knowledge.

By the Roman period (the setting of *The Tour*), [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] was still functioning, though its golden age had passed. The philosophers described by Couperus, eating their "black broth" as state-supported intellectuals, reflect historical reality: [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]]'s scholars were proverbially poor despite their learning.

> "The highest philosophy is … to be satisfied with little," he then said, plainly, in pure Greek. And he bowed, ironically, and turned away with the movement of a long dry stalk, in his dirty cloak. (003-062–063)

> "Lucius had joined for but a short time in the mad orgies of the young Romans of his own rank and had soon devoted himself to many branches of science, to astronomy in particular, philosophy, magic, the favourite passion of that period." (003-020)
