# Strabo

## Overview

Strabo (c. 64 BC – 24 AD) was a Greek geographer, philosopher, and historian from Amaseia in Pontus (modern-day Amasya, Turkey). His monumental work, the *Geographica*, in 17 books, is a comprehensive description of the known world in the early Roman Empire and remains one of the most important geographical texts from antiquity. Book 17 of the *Geographica* covers Egypt and Libya in detail, describing [[alexandria|Alexandria]], the Nile, and the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] coast — precisely the regions traversed by the travellers in the novel. Strabo's chronological proximity to the narrative (he died around 24 AD, during the reign of [[tiberius|Tiberius]]) makes him a uniquely relevant contemporary witness: he was composing the *Geographica* during the very period in which the novel is set.

## In the Narrative

Strabo is mentioned twice in the novel, both times as a scholarly authority. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] cites "the writings of [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] and Strabo" as the basis for his geographical knowledge of Egypt and the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] coast [003-025]. In a later conversation, Strabo is referred to as a contemporary — "the writings of his contemporary, Strabo" — whose accounts can verify what the travellers see [004-014]. These passing references do more than name-drop: they establish the scholarly framework within which the educated characters of the novel understand the geography of Egypt, and they ground the novel's own descriptive passages in real ancient geographical literature. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s reliance on Strabo also characterizes him as a man of Hellenistic learning — the kind of Roman-era intellectual who navigates the world through books before experiencing it directly.

## Historical Context

Strabo came from a wealthy family in Pontus and studied under several prominent teachers, including the Peripatetic philosopher Xenarchus and the grammarian Aristodemus. He travelled widely — through Asia Minor, Egypt, and Italy — and spent considerable time in Rome. His *Geographica* was not merely a catalogue of places but a work of cultural geography that described the customs, histories, and economies of the peoples of the Roman world. His account of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] in Book 17 is one of the most detailed surviving descriptions of the city from the early Roman period, covering its layout, monuments, and the workings of [[museum-of-alexandria|the Museum]] and Library — many of the same sites visited by [[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions.

Strabo's presence in the novel also raises an interesting temporal question. Since he was alive and writing during the Tiberian period, the characters' citation of him as an authority is historically plausible — unlike their citation of [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] (third century BC), which is a reference to an older authority. The pairing of the two geographers creates a timeline of Greek geographical knowledge: [[eratosthenes|Eratosthenes]] the pioneer, Strabo the contemporary synthesizer.
