# Horus

## Overview

Horus is one of the most ancient and significant [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in Egyptian religion, depicted as a falcon or falcon-headed man and associated with kingship, the sky, and protection. As the son of [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[isis|Isis]], Horus represents the triumph of order over chaos — avenging his father's murder by [[typhon|Typhon]] ([[typhon|Set]]) and restoring legitimate divine rule. In *The Tour*, Horus is invoked as a divine youth, "the radiant redeemer of mankind, who descended out of pity on a sinful world" (003-073), his image sanctifying the temple spaces through which the pilgrims move.

## In the Narrative

Horus appears at significant moments in the novel's depiction of Egyptian religious art and architecture. In the tombs of the Ptolemies at [[soma|the Soma]], among the images of [[apis|Apis]], Râ, Thoth and [[anubis|Anubis]], there appears "Sacred Horus, son of [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[isis|Isis]], the radiant redeemer of mankind, who descended out of pity on a sinful world" (003-073). This passage portrays Horus in distinctly salvific terms, echoing the language of mystery religion redemption.

At the barge procession to [[canopus|Canopus]], the [[saba|Saba]]ean guide [[caleb|Caleb]] flatters [[lucius|Lucius]] by invoking Horus: "Image of Horus, the son of [[osiris|Osiris]]! May [[serapis|Serapis]] send you good dreams!" (008-008). This juxtaposition of Horus with [[serapis|Serapis]] reflects the syncretic religious environment of Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where Egyptian and Greco-Roman [[egyptian-deities|deities]] coexisted and merged.

Later, at a humble dwelling, the worshippers see the divine child as resembling both "Habel and Horus" (018-015, 018-031), drawing a perhaps deliberate parallel between the [[jewish|Hebrew]] Abel and the Egyptian divine son — both figures of youthful sacrifice. This syncretic impulse runs through the novel's treatment of Egyptian religion (003-073).

## Historical/Cultural Context

Horus was worshipped from the Predynastic period through the Roman era. His myth — the posthumous son of [[osiris|Osiris]] who avenges his father and defeats [[typhon|Set]] — was central to Egyptian royal ideology: each living pharaoh was considered a manifestation of Horus (003-073). Temples throughout Egypt contained Horus chapels, the most famous being the Temple of Horus at [[apollonopolis-magna|Edfu]].

By the Roman period, Horus was often identified with Apollo in Greek interpretation, and his iconography as a divine child held by [[isis|Isis]] influenced early Christian depictions of the Madonna and Child. The novel's description of Horus as "the radiant redeemer of mankind, who descended out of pity on a sinful world" (003-073) reflects this Hellenistic reinterpretation of the Horus myth in terms familiar to a Roman audience (008-008).

## Key Passages

> "Sacred Horus, son of Osiris and Isis, the radiant redeemer of mankind, who descended out of pity on a sinful world, bestrode Typhon, the grinning spirit of evil." (003-073)

> "Image of Horus, the son of Osiris! May Serapis send you good dreams! May Serapis thrice holy fill your night with happy visions!" (008-008)

> "The Child was like Habel and like Horus. When they came before the miry water, the Child walked with Moses. But I do not know, unless He was all at once." (018-015)
