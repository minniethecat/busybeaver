# Neith

## Overview

Neith is one of the most ancient [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in the Egyptian pantheon, a primordial goddess associated with creation, [[philosophy|wisdom]], war, and weaving. She was considered the mother of the gods, existing before all others, and her cult centre at [[sais|Sais]] in the [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]] was a major religious site. In *The Tour*, Neith appears in both her independent aspect — as the immense Sphinx embodying eternal [[philosophy|wisdom]] — and in her syncretic identification with [[isis|Isis]] as "[[isis|Isis-Neith]]" (014-008), representing the feminine divine principle that underlies all Egyptian religion.

## In the Narrative

Neith's presence is evoked at several key moments. During the [[serapis|Serapis]] theology exposition, the narrator explains that [[serapis|Serapis]] "is feminine, Neith, the beginning, and masculine, [[ammon-ra|Ammon]], eternity" (009-017). This identification of Neith as the primordial feminine principle places her at the foundation of cosmic reality — she is the "beginning" from which all existence emerges.

The most powerful depiction of Neith comes in chapter XV, where the travellers encounter the [[sphinx|Great Sphinx]]. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] exclaims: "The great Sphinx, the immense Neith, the ever-silent [[philosophy|wisdom]]!" (015-023), interpreting the Sphinx itself as a manifestation of Neith. He reflects on the colossal statues: "Sand-women who were the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of Neith! What colossal statues, hewn out of the rock!" (015-028), and a recurring meditation runs through the later Egypt chapters: "Thy [[philosophy|wisdom]] alone, O Neith, is needful to man!" (017-017, 017-018).

A deeper theological observation is offered: "A shroud covered up the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of Neith. One day the shroud will cover up all and nothing will remain. All is vanity: thy [[philosophy|wisdom]] alone, O Neith, is needful to man!" (017-014-017). This echoes the biblical language of Ecclesiastes ("vanity of vanities") while rooting it in Egyptian divine [[philosophy|wisdom]]. Near the pyramids, "the immense Neith, the omniscient [[philosophy|wisdom]]" looms over the silver sea of sand (018-007).

## Historical/Cultural Context

Neith's cult centred at [[sais|Sais]] (modern [[sais|Sa el-Hagar]]) in the western [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]]. She was a war goddess — her symbols included crossed arrows and a shield — but also a creator deity who wove the world into existence on her loom. In the Late Period, Neith gained prominence as a primordial mother goddess, described in inscriptions at Esna as the one who "made the Nile to flow and created all that exists."

The identification of the Sphinx with Neith in *The Tour* is a distinctive literary interpretation rather than standard Egyptian theology (the Sphinx was more commonly associated with solar gods), but it serves Couperus's theme of silent, eternal [[philosophy|wisdom]] standing in contrast to human vanity and transience.
