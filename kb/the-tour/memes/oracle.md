## Overview

Oracles were sacred institutions in the Hellenistic and Roman world where individuals sought divine guidance through dreams, prophetic utterances, and ritual consultation. In *The Tour*, oracles form a recurring motif — from the famed oracle of Ammon in the Libyan desert to the local oracles of Egypt — shaping the spiritual journeys of characters and serving as narrative devices for revelation and mystery.

## In the Narrative

The oracle of Ammon is a pivotal destination in the story. [[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions undertake a desert journey to consult the oracle at the oasis of Siwa (020-002, 020-003, 020-004). Although by this period the oracle had declined — "the oracle of Ammon was no longer visited" (021-008) — it retains symbolic power as the oracle that once declared [[alexander|Alexander the Great]] a son of the god (003-079).

Oracles also appear closer to [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. The manteum (oracle) of Latona at [[butos|Butos]] is mentioned as a site where [[psammetichus|Psammetichus]] once consulted the goddess (015-001, 015-002). [[lucius|Lucius]] himself visits this oracle, seeking answers about his fate (015-006). Throughout the narrative, oracles provide a through-line of divine communication — [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] observes that "all the oracles now never speak except of one" (015-012), hinting at a convergence of prophetic traditions toward a singular revelation.

The motif of oracular dreams runs alongside the formal oracles. [[ilia|Ilia]] confides that "She has taught me, through oracles and dreams, the utter secret" (012-034), suggesting that in the world of the novel, divine communication is not confined to temple precincts but extends into the private realm of sleep and vision. This blurring of institutional and personal revelation is characteristic of the religious syncretism of Roman Egypt.

[[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], the philosopher, serves as a sceptical counterpoint to the enthusiasm for oracles. He reflects that "the oracles, even though they never satisfy the intellect, yet bring a certain gratification to the will" (015-010) — a sophisticated observation that separates the epistemic value of prophecy from its psychological appeal. People seek oracles not because they reliably predict the future, but because the act of seeking itself provides comfort.

## The Desert Oracle of Ammon

The journey to the oracle of Ammon represents one of the narrative's most symbolically charged episodes. [[lucius|Lucius]] travels "through the desert to the oracle of Ammon, with [[caleb|Caleb]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], and the whole caravan" (020-003), a pilgrimage that strips away the comforts of civilisation and reduces the travellers to their essential selves. The oracle of Ammon, once consulted by [[alexander|Alexander the Great]], had by [[lucius|Lucius]]'s time fallen into obscurity — "the oracle of Ammon was no longer visited" (021-008) — yet [[lucius|Lucius]] undertakes the journey anyway. The oracle's decline mirrors the novel's broader meditation on Egypt's faded glory: the gods, like the monuments, retain their majesty but have lost their audience.

## The Function of Oracles in the Novel

Oracles serve a dual narrative function in *The Tour*. Structurally, they provide the motivation for travel — [[lucius|Lucius]]'s journey from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to Siwa, his visits to [[butos|Butos]] and other oracular sites, all arise from the desire to consult the divine (015-006, 015-002). The oracle is a plot engine, converting spiritual yearning into geographical movement. Thematically, oracles embody the novel's central question about the accessibility of hidden knowledge. Can the gods speak to mortals? And if they do, can mortals understand them? [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s scepticism (015-010) and [[ilia|Ilia]]'s visionary certainty (012-034) represent two poles of response, and the novel refuses to resolve the question — the oracles may speak, or they may be silent, but the human need to consult them persists regardless.
