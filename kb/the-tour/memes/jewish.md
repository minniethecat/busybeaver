## Overview

The Jewish community formed one of the most significant ethnic and religious minorities in Hellenistic-Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. By the first century, [[alexandria|Alexandria]] housed one of the largest Jewish populations outside Judaea, with entire quarters of the city given over to Jewish life, synagogues, and scholarship. This community was the cultural milieu that produced the Septuagint — the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible — and the philosophical works of Philo of [[alexandria|Alexandria]]. In *The Tour*, the Jewish presence weaves through the narrative on two levels: as the deep past of [[joseph|Joseph]] and [[moses|Moses]] — figures [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] recounts as part of the Egyptian sacred history — and as the living present of a mysterious hermit-seer who dwells in a cave near the Sphinx, a figure of giant stature and prophetic vision who claims to have witnessed the dawn of a new divinity.

## In the Narrative

The Jewish tradition first enters the story during the Nile journey, when [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] discusses the old palaces of the [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]]s. He recounts how "[[joseph|Joseph]], the Jewish interpreter of dreams, was a powerful governor" under a [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]], and how "Moses, who knew [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]] and learned the occult [[philosophy|wisdom]] from him," was saved as an infant by [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]]'s daughter [016-008]. [[lucius|Lucius]], absorbed in his reading, reflects on "[[joseph|Joseph]] and Moses, about [[jahve|Jahve]] and [[pharaoh|Pharaoh Amenophis]], who was the father of [[sesostris|Sesostris]]" [016-012]. The passage establishes a genealogy of occult [[philosophy|wisdom]] running from Moses through [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]] to the Egyptian priestly tradition, a chain of secret knowledge that echoes throughout the novel.

The living Jewish presence in the narrative appears later, when [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes taking [[lucius|Lucius]] to visit the Jewish hermit near the Sphinx: "My noble lord, before the night has quite fallen, I should like to take you to the great [[neith|Neith]] ... for the sake both of the statue itself and of the Jewish prophet, a hermit, who dwells in a cave hard by" [018-002]. [[caleb|Caleb]] fetches the hermit from his cave opposite the Sphinx and warns [[lucius|Lucius]] that the man may be mad, but notes that "all distinguished foreigners, such as your lordship, listen to him ... because he says strange things" [018-010].

The hermit emerges in the fallen night — "of giant stature and incredibly old; his beard fell in waves down to his waist. His grey robe trailed over the sand" [018-012]. He proclaims his lineage: "I am of the tribe of [[joseph|Joseph]] himself, who took to wife Asenath the daughter of Potipherah priest of On! In me was the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of [[joseph|Joseph]], who interpreted dreams, and the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of the priests of On!" [018-013]. But this ancient [[philosophy|wisdom]], he declares, has died within him since he beheld a vision: "Then, O [[jahve|Jahve]], I knew that I had beheld Thy Son; and this happiness was my last [[philosophy|wisdom]]. Since then I know nothing more, O [[jahve|Jahve]], be praised! Since then the knowledge of [[joseph|Joseph]] has died away within me and that of the priests of On! For I have seen [[jahve|Jahve]]'s Son, there, there, in the lap of [[neith|Neith]]" [018-015].

Later, reflecting on the fading Egyptian temple at the end of the journey, the narrative observes that "the Jewish seer, who lived in the cave of [[neith|Neith]], had haply seen the new god, the Son of [[jahve|Jahve]], crowned with light for days and days" — while [[lucius|Lucius]] beholds the fading of the forgotten Egyptian deity whom [[alexander|Alexander of Macedon]] had once crossed deserts to seek [021-015]. The juxtaposition is the novel's most explicit theological statement: the old gods are dying, and a new one has been glimpsed by a Jewish visionary in the Egyptian desert.

The connection between Jewish and Egyptian [[philosophy|wisdom]] is further underscored at the abandoned temples, where "the lost [[philosophy|wisdom]] especially had been cultivated by the omniscient priests, the heirs of Moses and of [[hermes-trismegistus|Hermes Trismegistus]]" [024-024].

## Key Passages

> "Joseph, the Jewish interpreter of dreams, was a powerful governor under one of them; Moses, who knew Hermes Trismegistus and learned the occult wisdom from him, all the wisdom that can be known, was saved, as a babe, by the daughter of a Pharaoh." [016-008]

> "The Jewish hermit came up to them in the fallen night. He was of giant stature and incredibly old; his beard fell in waves down to his waist. His grey robe trailed over the sand." [018-012]

> "I am of the tribe of Joseph himself, who took to wife Asenath the daughter of Potipherah priest of On! In me was the wisdom of Joseph, who interpreted dreams, and the wisdom of the priests of On! But all wisdom is dead in me, Jahve be praised, since I beheld Him!" [018-013]

> "Then, O Jahve, I knew that I had beheld Thy Son; and this happiness was my last wisdom. Since then I know nothing more, O Jahve, be praised! Since then the knowledge of Joseph has died away within me and that of the priests of On!" [018-015]

> "The Jewish seer, who lived in the cave of Neith, had haply seen the new god, the Son of Jahve, crowned with light for days and days." [021-015]
