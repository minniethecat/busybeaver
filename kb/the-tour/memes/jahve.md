## Overview

Jahve (Yahweh) is the God of the [[jewish|Hebrew]]s, referenced in *The Tour* within the context of Egyptian religious history. The narrative presents Jahve alongside the Egyptian gods, acknowledging the [[jewish|Hebrew]] tradition as one of the ancient spiritual forces that shaped the land. The story invokes the memory of the ten plagues sent by Jahve upon Egypt [016-008] and connects Jahve to the patriarchs [[joseph|Joseph]] and Moses, who are remembered as bearers of divine [[philosophy|wisdom]] in the Egyptian landscape. In the cosmopolitan religious world of Roman [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — where Egyptian, Greek, Jewish, and emerging Christian traditions coexisted — Jahve represents the ancient claims of [[jewish|Hebrew]] monotheism within a polytheistic empire.

## In the Narrative

Jahve is mentioned primarily in the context of Jewish characters and their heritage. When discussing the history of Egypt, the travellers recall "the ten plagues sent over Egypt by Jahve, the God of the Jews" [016-008] — a reference that situates the biblical Exodus narrative within the Egyptian setting. The plagues are not dismissed as myth but treated as historical events that left their mark on the Egyptian landscape and collective memory.

[[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], ever the scholar, reads about "[[joseph|Joseph]] and Moses, about Jahve and [[pharaoh|Pharaoh Amenophis]], who was the father of the princess who found Moses" [016-012], linking the [[jewish|Hebrew]] God to Egyptian royal history and treating the biblical narrative as part of the shared history of the region. This conflation of Egyptian dynastic chronology with [[jewish|Hebrew]] scripture reflects the syncretic intellectual culture of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where the Septuagint translation had made Jewish texts available to Greek readers for centuries.

A Jewish character's exclamation — "all [[philosophy|wisdom]] is dead in me, Jahve be praised, since I beheld Him" [018-013] — marks a moment of spiritual surrender and transformation, showing the living presence of [[jewish|Hebrew]] worship within the novel's world. The phrase captures the tension between intellectual [[philosophy|wisdom]] and revelatory experience that runs through the narrative.

## Theological Dimensions

Jahve's presence in the novel raises questions about the relationship between Egyptian and [[jewish|Hebrew]] religious traditions. The ten plagues [016-008] are presented not as theological polemic but as historical memory — events that shaped Egyptian identity as much as [[jewish|Hebrew]]. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]]'s scholarly approach treats Jahve alongside [[ammon-ra|Ammon-Ra]] and [[serapis|Serapis]] as one of the divine powers acknowledged in the Egyptian landscape. This inclusivism reflects the religious pluralism of the Roman Empire, where local gods and foreign [[egyptian-deities|deities]] coexisted and were often identified with one another.

## Historical Context

In the first century AD, the Jewish community in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] was one of the largest and most influential in the diaspora. The translation of the [[jewish|Hebrew]] Bible into Greek (the Septuagint) had been completed in [[alexandria|Alexandria]] centuries earlier, making Jewish scripture accessible to the Hellenistic world. The Egyptian priest Manetho and the Jewish historian [[joseph|Joseph]]us both engaged in polemics about the Exodus narrative, and Egyptian anti-Jewish literature sometimes portrayed Moses as a renegade Egyptian priest. The novel's references to Jahve reflect this complex religious landscape where Egyptian, Jewish, and Greco-Roman traditions intersected.
