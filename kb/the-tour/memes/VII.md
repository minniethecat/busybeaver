## VII

[007-001]> “Mother of Eros, hear thy slave!

[007-002]> “Child of the foam, great goddess of love,

[007-003]> [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], look down from above!

[007-004]> Thou, who dost madden the gods with desire,

[007-005]> Thou, who fulfillest men’s hearts with thy fire,

[007-006]> All but the heart of my lord that I crave,

[007-007]> Hark to thy slave!

[007-008]> “Spill this hot blood that courses in vain for him,

[007-009]> Darken these eyes that are heavy with pain for him,

[007-010]> Smite the parched lips that he sees but to spurn them,

[007-011]> The hands stretched in love … take them, break them and burn them!

[007-012]> “Then, in the place where lately he strode,

[007-013]> Mingle mine ash with the dust of the road;

[007-014]> Thus, though I win not a glance from his eye,

[007-015]> Thus, though as ever he pass me by

[007-016]> Careless, unseeing, at least my lord’s heel

[007-017]> Cannot but touch me, at least I shall feel

[007-018]> The embrace of his foot; and his sandall’d sole

[007-019]> Shall kiss my dust and make me whole.

[007-020]> “Then let the heart that he has press’d,

[007-021]> The ashen lips by him caressed

[007-022]> Sink low in the lowly dust of the road

[007-023]> Lest another tread where late he trod.

[007-024]> “Mother of Eros, hear thy slave!

[007-025]> “Child of the foam, great goddess of love,

[007-026]> Aphrodite, look down from above!

[007-027]> Thou, who dost madden the gods with desire,

[007-028]> Thou, who fulfillest men’s hearts with thy fire,

[007-029]> All but the heart of my lord that I crave,

[007-030]> Hark to thy slave!”

[007-031][[cora|Cora]]’s song rang through the falling night. Her clear voice, tinkling as though with little golden bells, at first soft and hushed, rose throbbing in passion and then broke like a crystal ray and melted in mournfulness and plaintive prayer.

[007-032]The shadows lay heaped under the palm-trees. Outside the doors of their apartments, in the galleries of the diversorium, sat the travelling merchants, squatting or lying on mat or rug, listening. [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] lay in a hammock and [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] sat beside him and looked up at the stars, which were beginning to show like silver daisies in wide, blue meadows.

[007-033]“You have sung beautifully, Cora,” said Uncle Catullus to the slave, who was sitting on the ground with [[lesbian-harp|the four-stringed harp]] before her.

[007-034]“Thank you, my lord,” said the slave.

[007-035]“Why not call me uncle?” said Catullus, good-naturedly.

[007-036]“I should not dare,” said Cora, smiling.

[007-037]“[[ilia|Ilia]] used to call me uncle.”

[007-038]“I am not Ilia, my lord.”

[007-039][[tarrar|Tarrar]] appeared in the pillared portico.

[007-040]But his appearance was a surprise. For Tarrar, no longer bandaged, looked like a little savage: he wore his Libyan festive garment; a girdle of feathers hung round his waist; he was crowned with a headdress of feathers. And he stood grinning.

[007-041]“Great gods, Tarrar!” cried Uncle Catullus, with a start. “What have you done to yourself? You look like a little cannibal! You frighten me! What’s happening?”

[007-042]“We are going to [[canopus|Canopus]], my lord, tonight!” cried Tarrar, jubilantly. “My Lord [[lucius|Lucius]] lets you know that we are all going to Canopus this very night! Here is his lordship himself!”

[007-043]And Tarrar pointed triumphantly to Lucius, who appeared upon the threshold. Cora had risen and now curtseyed low to the ground, with outstretched arms.

[007-044]Lucius looked like a young Egyptian god. He wore an Egyptian robe of striped byssus, with a border of [[hieroglyph|hieroglyphics]] worked in heavy embroidery and precious stones; his legs were encased in hose of gold tissue; about his head was an Egyptian coif, like that of a sphinx, with broad, projecting, striped bands, which fell to his shoulders; he glittered with strange jewels and was wrapped from head to foot in fine gold net like a transparent cloak, like an immaterial shroud. And he approached with a smile, brilliantly, superhumanly beautiful.

[007-045]“Great sacred gods! Great sacred gods!” exclaimed Uncle Catullus.

[007-046]He rose; Thrasyllus rose too; and the merchants gathered round and, in salaam upon salaam, showed their admiration for the dazzling stranger.

[007-047]“Lucius, what possesses you? What is happening? Have you turned into [[serapis|Serapis]] himself?”

[007-048]“No, uncle,” smiled Lucius, “I am merely clad in ceremonial raiment because I want to go to Canopus and dream on the roof of the temple of Serapis. It is the great feast; and [[caleb|Caleb]]”—he pointed to Caleb stepping forward—“has persuaded me to go this night in state to Canopus. You are coming too, uncle; you also, Thrasyllus; we shall all go, all my freedmen and slaves. Caleb will see about a boat.”

[007-049]A violent and feverish excitement followed. Slaves, male and female, streamed from every side of the diversorium, rejoicing and clasping their hands in amazement.

[007-050]“When any princely noble, such as his lordship,” Caleb explained, “goes to Canopus, to the feast of Serapis thrice holy, he goes in the greatest state, with all his household to accompany him.”

[007-051]“So I am going too, as I belong to the household?” exclaimed Uncle Catullus. “Only … am I to rig myself out like that? And where shall I find such a sumptuous raiment?”

[007-052]“My lord,” said Caleb, “you will find everything ready in your chamber. You too, Master Thrasyllus.”

[007-053]Uncle Catullus hurried away, clasping his fat stomach in his two hands. You never knew where you were with that Lucius! For days and days he had been mourning and sobbing and lamenting; he had remained invisible and had eaten nothing … and there, there he appeared, decked out like a young god, and wanted to go to Canopus, to dream on the roof of the temple!

[007-054]“And I had just been reckoning on a quiet evening, because I feel that I’ve overloaded my stomach!” moaned Uncle Catullus. “Egypt will be the death of me!”

[007-055]Lights everywhere, links and torches; fever and gaiety everywhere, because one and all were going to Canopus that night. What a surprise! Their lord was no longer sick! It was the great feast! It was the feast of Serapis! The feast of dreams! The water-festival and the boat-festival! It was the summer festival of Canopus!

[007-056][[vettius|Vettius]] and [[rufus|Rufus]], the two stewards, gave orders here, there and everywhere. One and all, they said, were to deck themselves in festive garb. Ione, the old female slave, who had charge of the harpists and dancers, was given leave to buy from the merchants whatever she needed, veils and ornaments.

[007-057]“We are going to Canopus, we are going to Canopus!” cried the women, in joyful chorus. “Quick, Ione, hand me the poppy-rouge! Here, a stick of antimony! I want a blue veil, Ione, and blue lotus-flowers for my hair! Quick, quick, Ione! The master is ready!”

[007-058]“We are going to Canopus, we are going to Canopus!” Cora cried, joyfully, with the rest. “My lord was like a young god, he looked like Serapis himself! Ione, I must have a net of gold thread and a dreaming-veil of gold thread and pink water-lilies for my hair! I want a wreath of pink water-lilies!”

[007-059]Lucius from afar beheld this stir, in the reflection of the lamps and torches in the night. Slaves were running to and fro; litters were prepared. He thought only of Ilia. He wanted to wrap himself in the dreaming-veil and to lie on the temple-roof and dream where Ilia was, where she had been carried … by the pirates. And he stood like a priest, gazing solemnly before him.
