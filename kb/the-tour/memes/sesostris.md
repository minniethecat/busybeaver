## Overview

Sesostris was a legendary Egyptian pharaoh, conflated by Greek and Roman writers from the historical Senusret (Senwosret) kings of the 12th Dynasty. Classical sources — including [[herodotus|Herodotus]] and Diodorus Siculus — credited him with conquering vast territories from Arabia to Bactriana to India over a nine-year campaign. In "The Tour," Sesostris is primarily known through his monuments: the "pillars of Sesostris," inscribed [[obelisk|obelisks]] at [[cape-dire|Cape Dire]] on the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]], which mark the furthest destination of the travellers' expedition.

## In the Narrative

Sesostris is a recurring geographical reference point rather than an active character. From the earliest planning stages of the Nile journey, [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] proposes visiting "the pillars of Sesostris" as the ultimate goal [013-006]. The destination serves as the turning point of the travellers' expedition: they journey up the Nile to the first cataract, then across the desert to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] coast to reach [[cape-dire|Cape Dire]], where the [[obelisk|obelisks]] of Sesostris stand.

When the travellers finally reach the pillars in Chapter XXVII, they find inscriptions whose "sacred writings immortalized the remembrance of the passage of the Egyptian world-ruler who for nine years had linked conquest to conquest, even to Arabia, even to Bactriana, even to India" [027-001]. At [[cape-dire|Cape Dire]], [[caleb|Caleb]]'s guesthouse — a small branch of the [[hermes-house|Hermes House]] — provided the travellers their first proper lodging in weeks. Here they awaited the arrival of [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] aboard the quadrireme.

Sesostris is also referenced as the son of the legendary king Menophis (or Amenophis) [016-008, 016-012].

## Historical Context

The historical basis for Sesostris lies in the Senusret (Senwosret) kings of Egypt's 12th Dynasty (c. 20th–19th centuries BC), particularly Senusret I and Senusret III. These pharaohs conducted military campaigns into Nubia and built fortifications, though their actual conquests were far less extensive than the Greek and Roman legends suggest. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] (5th century BC) greatly embellished the Sesostris legend in his *Histories*, crediting him with conquering all of Asia and erecting commemorative pillars wherever he went. This legendary version was transmitted through Hellenistic and Roman literature, including Diodorus Siculus and [[strabo|Strabo]], and forms the basis for the references in "The Tour."
