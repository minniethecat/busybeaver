# Sais

## Overview

Sais (Ancient Egyptian: Zau; modern Sa el-Hagar) was an ancient Egyptian city in the western [[nile-delta|Nile Delta]] that rose to prominence as the capital of Lower Egypt during the 26th (Saite) Dynasty (664–525 BC). It was the cult centre of the goddess [[neith|Neith]], a creator-deity associated with war, hunting, and weaving, whom the Greeks identified with Athena. In the Roman period, Sais remained a significant regional centre, known for its [[obelisk|obelisks]], the temple of [[neith|Neith]] (called Athene by Greek visitors), the tomb of [[psammetichus|Psammetichus]], and — most famously — the colossal veiled statue of [[isis|Isis]] in the temple precinct, which bore the inscription "I am all that has been, and is, and shall be; and no mortal hath lifted my veil." In *The Tour*, Sais appears as the travellers' destination after [[alexandria|Alexandria]] — a city where the weight of Egypt's past presses against the diminished present, and where the novel's themes of [[philosophy|wisdom]], concealment, and cultural decline find their most concentrated expression.

## In the Narrative

Sais is introduced as the party's destination: they "were now nearing the capital, Sais, the capital of all Lower Egypt" (014-007). The city receives the travellers and provides lodging (014-032), serving as a base for a sequence of significant events. The "[[obelisk|obelisks]] of Sais" are noted by one of the travellers (014-006), their presence signalling the city's ancient importance even as the monuments themselves stand aslant or neglected. [[cora|Cora]] is invited to accompany the party to Sais (014-030), though her presence there is uncertain.

The travellers visit the temple of Athene ([[neith|Neith]]) and the tomb of [[psammetichus|Psammetichus]] after departing the city proper (015-001). These visits ground the narrative in the actual sacred topography of Sais, where the temple of [[neith|Neith]] was one of the largest in the Delta and the burial of the 26th-Dynasty kings was a site of historical pilgrimage. The figure of [[nemu-pha|Nemu-Pha]], the priest whom [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] consults, is associated with the temple at Sais (014-012, 023-016), reinforcing the city's role as a centre of oracular and esoteric knowledge. [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] recalls consulting him there: "As [[nemu-pha|Nemu-Pha]] said to me, at Sais. I paid for it in gold" (023-016) — a bitter acknowledgment that even sacred [[philosophy|wisdom]] has its price.

The most resonant passage associated with Sais, however, concerns the veiled [[isis|Isis]]: "It was not the veiled [[isis|Isis]] of Sais; it was the unveiled, silent…" (018-008). The narrator later delivers the novel's most elegiac verdict on Egypt's decline through the city: "Egypt is Egypt no longer. Sais is Sais no longer. If [[philosophy|wisdom]] once dwelt in Egypt, it has fled" (014-080). The night over Sais is described as waning, with "the twinkling of the Burning Eyes" visible from afar (014-081). Sais thus becomes a synecdoche for Egypt itself — a once-great capital now fading, its [[philosophy|wisdom]] veiled or fled, its monuments sinking, its priests selling knowledge for gold.

## Historical Context

Sais reached its political zenith during the 26th Dynasty, when the Saite pharaohs (including [[psammetichus|Psammetichus I]], Necho II, and Amasis) ruled a reunified Egypt from the Delta. The city was famed for its medical school, its temple of [[neith|Neith]] (described by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] in Book 2 of his *Histories*), and the tombs of the Saite kings within the temple precinct. By the Roman period, Sais was still a significant city but had long since ceded its political importance to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] (014-007).

The veiled [[isis|Isis]] of Sais was one of the most famous cult images of antiquity. Plutarch, in his *On [[isis|Isis]] and [[osiris|Osiris]]*, records the inscription on the statue's base. The image of the veiled goddess — whose truth is hidden from mortals — became a powerful symbol in Western esotericism and philosophy, influencing thinkers from the Renaissance to Kant and beyond. In the novel, the distinction between the "veiled [[isis|Isis]] of Sais" and the "unveiled, silent" [[isis|Isis]] (018-008) marks a theological crux: the difference between a goddess who conceals truth and one who embodies it directly.
