# Serapis

## Overview

Serapis is a syncretic deity worshipped in Hellenistic and Roman Egypt, combining elements of the Egyptian gods [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[apis|Apis]] with Greek divine attributes. Created under the Ptolemaic dynasty to unify Greek and Egyptian religious traditions, Serapis became the patron god of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and was venerated at grand temples, or [[serapeum|Serapeum]]s, throughout the Mediterranean world. In *The Tour*, Serapis and his feast at [[canopus|Canopus]] form a central spiritual episode in the narrative, where the protagonist [[lucius|Lucius]] seeks divine guidance through the ritual of sacred dreaming.

## In the Narrative

The cult of Serapis appears prominently in *The Tour* when [[lucius|Lucius]] and his companions travel from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to [[canopus|Canopus]] for the great feast of the god. The [[serapeum|Serapeum]] at [[alexandria|Alexandria]] is noted as "immemorial" (005-013), suggesting an ancient and imposing presence in the city, though the local temple had been largely deserted in favour of the more famous temple of Serapis at [[canopus|Canopus]] (005-014).

The feast of Serapis is described as "the feast of dreams" (007-055), a nocturnal pilgrimage where thousands of worshippers gather at the temple to receive sacred dreams sent by the god. [[lucius|Lucius]] participates in this ritual, hoping Serapis will reveal the whereabouts of [[ilia|Ilia]], his beloved slave who has mysteriously vanished. The chapter dedicated to this episode (IX) provides a detailed depiction of the temple architecture, the procession of priests, and the mystical atmosphere of the sacred incubation ritual.

During the preparation for the feast, [[caleb|Caleb]], the guide, flatters [[lucius|Lucius]] by comparing him to the god himself: "Have you turned into Serapis himself?" (007-047), and later remarks that [[lucius|Lucius]], in his Egyptian finery, "looked like Serapis himself" (007-058) and (008-006). This association elevates [[lucius|Lucius]]'s appearance to divine status in the eyes of the locals.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Serapis was a deliberately created deity, introduced by [[ptolemy|Ptolemy I Soter]] (r. 305–282 BCE) as part of a policy to integrate Greek and Egyptian populations under Ptolemaic rule. The name derives from a fusion of [[osiris|Osiris]] and [[apis|Apis]] (Osorapis → Sarapis → Serapis), and his iconography adapted the Greek image of Zeus or Hades — a bearded, mature man enthroned, often accompanied by Cerberus — which resonated with both Greek and Egyptian religious sensibilities.

By the Roman period (the setting of *The Tour*, c. early 1st century CE), Serapis had become one of the most widely worshipped [[egyptian-deities|deities]] in the Mediterranean. His cult was especially associated with healing, prophecy through dreams (incubation), and the afterlife. The [[canopus|Canopus]] temple, located east of [[alexandria|Alexandria]], was renowned as a pilgrimage site where the faithful slept within the temple precinct to receive oracular dreams — a practice vividly portrayed in chapter IX.

The theological framework in Couperus's narrative aligns Serapis with [[osiris|Osiris]], as both ruler and benefactor: "He is [[osiris|Osiris]] himself; between him and [[osiris|Osiris]] there is no difference." (009-015). The text also identifies Serapis with both masculine and feminine principles — "He is feminine, [[neith|Neith]], the beginning, and masculine, [[ammon-ra|Ammon]], eternity" (009-017) — reflecting the syncretic and all-encompassing theology of the Hellenistic period.

## Key Passages

> "In the strange bright summer night of light, lit by the sheen of the stars and the glow of the lamps, Canopus rose amid its slender obelisks and its spreading palm-trees. The barges lay moored to the long quay, one beside the other. One solemn train of pilgrims after another flowed down the street to the temple of Serapis." (009-001)

> "The earth splits and Serapis rises from the subterranean Hell. He is everything, even as Osiris is. He is feminine, Neith, the beginning, and masculine, Ammon, eternity. He is what the last will be. And he cannot be other than the benefactor. He makes the dreams hover like butterflies around the foreheads of those who believe in him." (009-017)

> "He is Osiris himself; between him and Osiris there is no difference. He is two. While Osiris is the benevolent Almighty above, he is the benevolent Almighty below. He opposes Typhon, even as Osiris combated Typhon. Victory falls to him in the end, even as it did to Osiris." (009-015)
