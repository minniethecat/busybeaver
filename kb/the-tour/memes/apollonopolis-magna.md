## Overview

Apollonopolis Magna — the "Great City of Apollo," known to the Egyptians as Edfu — was a significant settlement in Upper Egypt, situated on the west bank of the Nile. In the Greco-Roman period, it was the capital of the Apollonopolite nome and was renowned for its temple dedicated to [[horus|Horus]]-Apollo, one of the best-preserved temples from antiquity. In *The Tour*, the city marks a pivotal moment in the journey: it is here that [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], weary of Egypt, confesses his exhaustion, and [[caleb|Caleb]] proposes an altered itinerary.

## In the Narrative

The travellers' barge approaches Apollonopolis Magna in the latter stages of their Nile journey. It is at this point that [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]], "old and ill," confesses to [[lucius|Lucius]] that he is tired of "falcons, fishes and crocodiles which are gods" and craves "a few succulent oysters and a young roast peacock" [004-02179]. [[caleb|Caleb]] seizes this opportunity to propose an alternative route: rather than continuing through Ethiopia — which the party increasingly dreads — [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] can take the thalamegus from Apollonopolis Magna through the canal to [[berenice|Berenice]] on the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]], while the quadrireme circles around to meet them [004-02187].

This plan is accepted, and the party splits at Apollonopolis Magna: [[catullus|Uncle Catullus]] boards the thalamegus with [[rufus|Rufus]] and a retinue of slaves, bound for [[berenice|Berenice]], while [[lucius|Lucius]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]], and [[caleb|Caleb]] take a simple barge southward to [[syene|Syene]] [004-02195]. The city thus functions as a narrative hinge, marking the transition from the Nile journey to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] phase of the expedition.

## Historical Context

In the first century AD, Apollonopolis Magna was the capital of the Apollonopolite nome, the second nome of Upper Egypt. Its great Temple of [[horus|Horus]] — begun under [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] III Euergetes in 237 BCE and completed in 57 BCE — was among the largest and most architecturally sophisticated temples of the Ptolemaic period, and its pylons and reliefs remain the best-preserved of any Egyptian temple. The city sat on the west bank of the Nile roughly midway between [[thebes|Thebes]] and [[syene|Syene]], making it a natural stopping point for river traffic. Under Roman administration, the nome retained its traditional governance structure, and the temple cult of [[horus|Horus]]-Apollo continued to flourish — a fusion of Egyptian and Hellenistic religious traditions reflected in the city's very name, which pairs the Greek Apollo with the Egyptian [[horus|Horus]].
