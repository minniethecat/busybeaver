# Ammon-Râ

## Overview

Ammon-Râ is the supreme sun-god of the Egyptian pantheon as it appears in *The Tour*, a syncretic deity combining the ancient Egyptian Râ (the sun) with Ammon of [[thebes|Thebes]] and identified in the Hellenistic and Roman periods with Zeus-Ammon or Jupiter Ammon. His remote desert oracle in the oasis of Siwa, deep in the Libyan desert west of the Nile, was one of the most celebrated oracular sites of the ancient world — it had famously declared [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] to be the son of the god. In the novel, [[lucius|Lucius]] journeys to this oracle as the climactic episode of his Egyptian spiritual quest, where he encounters the aged [[high-priest-of-ammon-ra|High Priest of Ammon-Râ]] and receives the enigmatic counsel that reshapes his understanding.

## In the Narrative

The [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] is first mentioned in the context of Alexander's tomb at [[soma|the Soma]] in [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where the priest describes Alexander as "the son of the sacred sun-god, Ammon-Râ, descended upon earth" (003-081). The travellers marvel at "this chrysalis all that remained of the great Alexander, whom the [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] had declared to be the son of Ammon-Râ son of the sun-god?" (003-079). This early reference establishes the oracle's prestige in the reader's mind well before [[lucius|Lucius]] decides to seek it.

The journey to Ammon is undertaken only after the travellers have exhausted the [[oracle|oracles]] of [[alexandria|Alexandria]] and [[canopus|Canopus]]. "After he had left [[alexandria|Alexandria]], [[lucius|Lucius]] went to the [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]], through the barren desert" (020-002). The journey is arduous: "The sun had not set and was still blazing cruelly," and the party travels "with [[caleb|Caleb]], [[thrasyllus|Thrasyllus]] and [[tarrar|Tarrar]]" (020-003). Their guides warn them that the oracle "is quite ruined and deserted" (020-008), a victim of the decline in pilgrimage since the quinquennial games were moved to [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]] (005-014, implied).

At the temple, "whose walls lay spread like a giant's limbs on the sand" (021-001), the high-priest appears — "very old, frail and grey, with long white locks and a long white beard" (021-007) — accompanied by eleven priests. The high-priest laments the oracle's diminished state: "The [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] was no longer visited as it had been, since the games had been instituted at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]" (021-008). Before the "enormous statue of Ammon-Râ, old as time, the sun-god of Egypt" (021-012), [[lucius|Lucius]] receives his oracle.

The theology of Ammon presented in the novel draws on syncretic identifications: the god is associated with the creative word ("He is what the last word shall be that creates the world anew," 009-017) and his priest declares [[lucius|Lucius]] "the son of the Sun, the son of Jupiter Ammon-Râ" (021-013). The climactic counsel — "Be a god unto yourself!" — becomes the philosophical turning-point of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s journey.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The [[oracle|oracle of Ammon]] at the Siwa Oasis gained international fame in 331 BCE when [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] made the perilous desert journey to consult it. The priest reportedly greeted Alexander as the son of Ammon, and thereafter the conqueror was depicted with ram's horns on coinage. By the Roman period, the oracle had declined significantly; [[strabo|Strabo]] reports that it was largely abandoned by his time. The novel's depiction of a ruined temple with a handful of remaining priests, lamenting the diversion of pilgrims to [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]], accurately reflects this historical trajectory. The identification of Ammon with Râ (the sun-god) and with Zeus/Jupiter was a characteristic feature of Hellenistic and Roman religious syncretism.
