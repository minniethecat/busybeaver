## Overview

The Nechao Canal (or Canal of Necho) was an ancient Egyptian waterway connecting the Pelusiac branch of the Nile to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] at [[arsinoe|Arsinoë]]. Originally begun by [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]] Necho II (610–595 BCE) of the Twenty-Sixth Dynasty, it was later completed by the Persian king Darius I and subsequently maintained and renovated under the Ptolemies and Romans. In the first century AD, the canal served as a vital link between the Mediterranean trade networks and the Erythraean/[[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] routes to Arabia and India. In *The Tour*, the canal appears as both a logistical artery and an escape route central to the novel's final act.

## In the Narrative

The Nechao Canal is first mentioned in [[caleb|Caleb]]'s proposed itinerary: the quadrireme *[[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]* is to sail "by [[pelusium|Pelusium]] and the Nechao Canal" and ascend the [[arabian-gulf|Arabian Gulf]] to meet the party at the Pillars of [[sesostris|Sesostris]] [004-02187]. This establishes the canal as a known navigation route that circumvents the overland journey through Ethiopia.

The canal's most dramatic narrative function comes during the revelation of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s ruin. [[vettius|Vettius]], the steward, explains how he fled from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] after learning of the imperial seizure of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s property: "By bribing the authorities at [[pelusium|Pelusium]] with the money that remained in my hands, I managed secretly to pass through the Nechao Canal to Arsinoe" [006-02415]. The canal thus becomes a route of clandestine escape — the narrow waterway through which [[vettius|Vettius]] preserved what remained of [[lucius|Lucius]]'s movable wealth.

Later, in a letter from [[caleb|Caleb]], the canal is referenced as a point where legal complications might have arisen during the sale of the quadrireme, "previous to the passage through the Nechao Canal (which would probably have been liable to legal objections)" [006-02523]. This suggests the canal represented a jurisdictional boundary of some significance.

## Historical Context

The canal connecting the Nile to the [[arabian-gulf|Red Sea]] has a long and interrupted history. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that Necho II abandoned the project after losing 120,000 workers, and it was Darius I who completed the waterway, commemorating the feat with granite stelae along its banks. [[ptolemy|Ptolemy]] II Philadelphus (r. 284–246 BCE) undertook a major renovation, installing a navigable lock system at the junction with the Nile. Under Roman rule, the canal was still functional in Augustus's time, though subject to silting; Trajan (r. AD 98–117) restored and expanded it, extending the northern intake to the Nile at Babylon (modern Cairo). In the Roman period, it was sometimes called "Trajan's River" (*Amnis Trajanus*), and it remained a critical conduit for Egyptian grain and Eastern luxuries until it fell into disrepair after the Arab conquest.
