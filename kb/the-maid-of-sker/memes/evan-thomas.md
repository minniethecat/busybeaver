# Evan Thomas

The keeper of the rabbit-warren at Sker House, whose assertion of territorial rights over the shoreline creates an early obstacle for Davy — and whose household becomes an unexpected refuge.

## Character Overview

Evan Thomas lives in "the ancient house" at Sker, keeping the rabbit-warren and claiming the shore as his domain. "He had a grant from the manor, he said, and the shore was part of it" (003-002). A Welshman with "scarce any English," he is suspicious of outsiders fishing his waters — especially "one vile Scotchman" (003-005). Though initially adversarial, Evan Thomas is not malicious; he is a man of narrow vision and fixed habit whose obstinacy is more comic than threatening.

## Role in the Novel

Evan Thomas provides the first obstacle Davy encounters, establishing the pattern of conflict over coastal resources that runs through the novel. His territorial claims at Sker set up Davy's discovery of Bardie: it is precisely because Davy must fish secretly that he is alone when the derelict boat appears. Later, Evan's household — including his wife Moxy — becomes a reluctant refuge for the children, complicating the initial portrait of him as mere obstruction.

## Character Analysis

Evan Thomas represents the older, Welsh-speaking rural order that exists alongside — and sometimes in tension with — Davy's more cosmopolitan naval Englishness. Blackmore treats him with the ethnographic curiosity characteristic of Victorian regional fiction: his Welsh speech, his suspicion of outsiders, his rootedness in local custom are rendered with attention and a degree of sympathy. He is not a villain but a type — the man whose world is small and fiercely defended.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Evan Thomas is a fictional creation, but he represents the class of Welsh smallholders and estate workers whose lives were shaped by manorial tenure and customary rights. The conflict over shore access that opens the novel reflects real legal disputes over foreshore rights that were common in coastal communities throughout the 18th and 19th centuries.
