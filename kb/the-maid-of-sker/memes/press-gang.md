# Press-Gang

The Royal Navy's practice of forcibly recruiting men into service, which serves as both a background threat throughout the novel and a decisive plot mechanism in its later chapters.

## Definition

Impressment was the Royal Navy's legal power to compel men — particularly seamen — into naval service. In *The Maid of Sker*, the press-gang embodies the violent reach of the state into coastal communities. Davy, a navy veteran himself, understands its logic from the inside, yet the novel treats impressment as a moral hazard that tests character and community.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The press-gang materializes most vividly in the later chapters, where Davy participates in the impressment of his old foe the tailor and the cowardly Hezekiah. The operation is rendered with dark comedy: "we made them both so entirely drunk, that we were compelled, for their own sake, to weigh anchor while having their bodies on board" (053-027). The threat of impressment hangs over coastal men throughout: "golden guineas on the one hand, impressment on the other; for a press-gang was in the neighbourhood" (065-006).

> We impressed not only my foe the tailor, but also Hezekiah! That is to say, it was not quite what might be called impressment; because, with no war raging yet, we could not resort to violence: but we made them both so entirely drunk, that we were compelled, for their own sake, to weigh anchor while having their bodies on board.
>
> (053-027)

Davy's own naval past — "the many sprees ashore, and the noble fights with Frenchmen" (001-004) — frames his participation in impressment as a continuation of his service identity, now turned against his own community.

## Critical Context

The press-gang was a defining institution of 18th-century British naval power, legally sanctioned yet widely resented. Blackmore's treatment — mixing violence with farce — captures the moral ambiguity of a system that both sustained naval supremacy and terrorized coastal populations. The novel's use of impressment as a plot device reflects broader literary engagement with the institution in maritime fiction.
