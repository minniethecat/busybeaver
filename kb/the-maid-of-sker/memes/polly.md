# Polly

A child whose presence in the novel's domestic scenes contributes to the texture of ordinary life against which the Gothic plot unfolds.

## Character Overview

Polly appears in the domestic chapters of the novel, part of the constellation of children — Bunny, Bardie, and others — whose presence humanizes Davy's world. She is defined by her youth and her place within the web of family and neighborhood relationships.

## Role in the Novel

Polly contributes to the novel's domestic register. Like Bunny, she grounds the extraordinary events of the plot in the ordinary rhythms of childhood — meals, play, sleep. Her presence reinforces the stakes of the central mystery: it is a world of children, and therefore a world worth protecting.

## Character Analysis

Polly is a minor domestic figure, significant less for her individual characterization than for her contribution to the novel's texture of familial warmth. In a Gothic narrative that includes shipwreck, madness, and revenge, characters like Polly provide the ordinary happiness that is threatened — and ultimately preserved.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Polly is a fictional creation. Children in Victorian fiction often function as symbols of innocence and vulnerability; Blackmore uses his child characters in this way while also giving them — especially Bunny — a lively particularity that resists pure symbolism.
