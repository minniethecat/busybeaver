# The Inquest

The coroner's inquest convened after the Sker shipwreck, which attempts to establish facts about the disaster while revealing the limits of official procedure in the face of mystery.

## Event Overview

Following the great shipwreck at Sker, a formal inquest is held under the authority of Colonel Lougher. Witnesses are called, evidence is presented, and the legal machinery of the 18th-century magistracy grinds into motion. Yet the inquest's formal procedures sit uneasily with the community's informal knowledge: the bailiff's question — "how shall we certify anything about this terrible shipwreck?" (013-028) — acknowledges the gap between legal process and lived reality.

## Key Developments

- **The coroner and the coronet**: Chapter XIII's title signals the intertwining of death and social rank in the proceedings
- **Testimony gathered**: Davy and others give evidence; the community's oral knowledge is translated into legal record
- **The limits of inquiry**: The inquest establishes some facts but cannot penetrate the central mystery of Bardie's identity

## Key Participants

- [[colonel-lougher]] — The presiding magistrate
- [[davy-llewellyn]] — Key witness; his testimony shapes the official record

## Significance

The inquest represents the novel's engagement with the relationship between official truth and lived experience. Legal process can establish facts but cannot resolve mysteries — a theme that resonates through the novel's treatment of identity, justice, and narrative itself.
