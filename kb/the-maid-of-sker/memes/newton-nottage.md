# Newton Nottage

The Glamorganshire village where Davy Llewellyn makes his home — "Newton-Nottage, fisherman and old sailor" (002-002) — a real coastal settlement that Blackmore populates with the novel's domestic and communal life.

## Location

Newton Nottage (now part of Porthcawl, Bridgend County Borough) was a distinct coastal parish in the 18th century, situated between the dunes of Kenfig and the Bristol Channel. Blackmore renders it as a tight-knit community of fishermen, artisans, and small traders — a world where "sixpence never entered twice into the thoughts of anyone" (025-019).

## Significance in the Novel

Newton Nottage is Davy's home ground — the place he returns to after fishing expeditions, the community whose opinion he values, and the social world that the novel's extraordinary events disrupt and ultimately re-confirm. The village represents the ordinary life that Gothic events threaten: neighbors helping neighbors, children growing up, the rhythms of work and worship.

> I, David Llewellyn, of Newton-Nottage, fisherman and old sailor, was in great distress and trouble.

## Historical Note

Newton Nottage was historically a distinct parish with a medieval church (St. John the Baptist). By Blackmore's time in the 1870s, it was still a recognizable coastal community, though it has since been absorbed into the larger settlement of Porthcawl.
