# Davy Llewellyn

The first-person narrator of the novel, an aging fisherman of Newton-Nottage whose voice — colloquial, digressive, worldly, and warm — is the book's defining achievement.

## Character Overview

David "Davy" Llewellyn introduces himself as "an ancient fisherman upon the coast of Glamorganshire" (001-002), fifty-two years old in the summer of 1782. A veteran of the Royal Navy who served under Captain Howe and Sir Edward Hawke (002-015), Davy has returned to shore life as a widower caring for his orphaned granddaughter Bunny. His discovery of the shipwrecked child Bardie at Sker sets the novel's central plot in motion.

## Role in the Novel

Davy is both narrator and actor — the story is his memoir, told in old age, of the strange events that began on "that blessed 10th of July 1782" (003-021). As narrator, his voice mediates every event; as character, his decisions — to rescue Bardie, to confront Parson Chowne, to participate in naval impressment — drive the plot. His combination of plain honesty and narrative artfulness makes him one of Blackmore's most memorable creations.

## Key Scenes

- **Ch. I–III**: Introduces himself, his poverty, and his fishing life; sets out to fish at Sker despite hunger (003-021)
- **Ch. IV–V**: Discovers the derelict boat and the shipwrecked child Bardie (004-012, 005-018)
- **Ch. XI**: Confronts the wrecker on the shore (011-007)
- **Ch. XXIV**: Forms a fishing association with his patron, marking his social rise (024-040)
- **Ch. LIII**: Participates in the impressment of the tailor and Hezekiah (053-027)

## Character Analysis

Davy's narrative voice is the novel's greatest strength. Blackmore crafts a narrator who is at once artless and artful — a man who claims "the work of writing must be very dull to me" (001-004) while producing prose of remarkable vitality and humor. His worldview combines naval discipline with folk superstition, genuine piety with worldly tolerance. As a protagonist, he occupies an unusual position: neither gentleman nor peasant, but a skilled laborer whose dignity is asserted through competence rather than rank.

> I am but an ancient fisherman upon the coast of Glamorganshire, with work enough of my own to do, and trouble enough of my own to heed, in getting my poor living.
>
> (001-002)

## Historical / Biographical Context

Davy represents the type of the 18th-century British naval veteran — men who served in the wars against France and returned to civilian life with skills, stories, and a distinctive worldview. Blackmore's portrayal draws on the oral traditions of coastal communities where such figures were living repositories of local history.
