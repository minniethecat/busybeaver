# The Parish

The Anglican parish system as it functioned in late-18th-century Glamorganshire — simultaneously a spiritual community, an administrative unit, and a theater for clerical corruption.

## Organization Overview

The parish in *The Maid of Sker* is the basic unit of communal organization. It provides the church where Davy attends "with all my topmost togs on" (007-011), the school "by deputy commission from the parson of the parish" (051-012), and the framework of poor relief, moral oversight, and social identity. The parish is also, through Parson Chowne, the site of institutional corruption — the system that enables his schemes.

## Role in the Novel

The parish functions as both setting and theme. Its ordinary operations — baptisms, marriages, burials, services — provide the rhythm of communal life. Its corruption, embodied in Chowne, provides the novel's central critique of institutional authority. The tension between the parish as spiritual community and as corrupt institution structures much of the novel's moral inquiry.

## Historical Note

The 18th-century Anglican parish was the primary institution of local governance as well as worship. Clergy might be resident pastors or absent pluralists; the quality of ministry varied enormously. Blackmore, the son of a clergyman, writes with insider knowledge of the system's strengths and failures.
