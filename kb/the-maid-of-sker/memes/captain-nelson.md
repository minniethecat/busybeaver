# Captain Nelson

Captain Horatio Nelson, the future hero of Trafalgar, who appears in the novel during Davy's Caribbean service — a figure of naval authority whose decisive action against smuggling establishes the moral standard of honorable command.

## Character Overview

Nelson enters the narrative in command of HMS *Boreas*: "And now we first met Captain Nelson in command of the Boreas, a proud ship, and a prouder captain" (045-010). Blackmore's portrait is admiring but not hagiographic: Nelson is decisive, principled, and impatient with corruption, enforcing the Navigation Act even when colonial authorities object.

## Role in the Novel

Nelson's function is to embody naval honor at its best. His enforcement of law against smuggling — "Nelson set his face against it; and whenever he set his face, his hand came quickly afterwards" (045-011) — provides a counterpoint to the corrupt authority of Parson Chowne. Davy's service under Nelson is a source of lifelong pride and a reference point for his own moral judgments.

## Character Analysis

Blackmore's Nelson is a figure of uncomplicated virtue — decisive where Chowne is devious, principled where others are compromised. The portrait reflects the post-Trafalgar cult of Nelson that had fully developed by Blackmore's time. In the novel's moral economy, Nelson serves as the standard against which lesser authorities are measured and found wanting.

> Nelson set his face against it; and whenever he set his face, his hand came quickly afterwards. We soon cut up that bit of smuggling.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Horatio Nelson commanded the *Boreas* in the Leeward Islands from 1784 to 1787, enforcing the Navigation Acts against American trade — precisely the period and activity Blackmore depicts. The novel's Nelson is historically accurate in outline, though his role in the narrative is brief and emblematic rather than developed.
