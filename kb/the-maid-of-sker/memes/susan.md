# Susan

A woman of quiet competence who appears in the novel's resolution, embodying the domestic virtues that the novel endorses.

## Character Overview

Susan appears in Chapter LXIV as a figure whose actions help to resolve complications and restore order. Blackmore characterizes her through action rather than description: she is defined by what she does rather than what she says.

## Role in the Novel

Susan represents the domestic sphere that the novel's Gothic elements threaten and its resolution restores. Her competence and moral clarity provide a counterpoint to the chaos sown by Chowne and the uncertainty surrounding Bardie's identity.

- **Ch. LXIV**: Susan "quite acquits herself" — her actions contribute to the novel's resolution (064)

## Character Analysis

Susan, like several of the novel's minor female characters, is rendered with economy rather than depth. Blackmore's interest is in her function — restoring domestic order — rather than her psychology. This is characteristic of the Victorian novel's treatment of minor female figures, who often serve as embodiments of domestic virtue rather than fully realized characters.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Susan is a fictional creation, representative of the capable women of the laboring classes whose competence was essential to household survival in 18th-century Britain but whose stories were rarely told in their own voices.
