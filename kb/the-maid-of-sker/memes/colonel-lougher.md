# Colonel Lougher

The magistrate of Candleston Court, whose official inquiries into the Sker shipwreck and its aftermath represent the workings of local justice — imperfect but fundamentally decent — in the novel's world.

## Character Overview

Colonel Lougher of Candleston Court is introduced as a figure of local consequence: Davy worries that "even that Colonel Lougher of Candleston Court might see" him (007-011). As magistrate, he presides over the inquest into the shipwreck, hearing evidence from Davy and others. His bailiff, Mr. Lewis, carries out the practical work of investigation, but Lougher's authority frames the legal process.

## Role in the Novel

Lougher represents the formal apparatus of justice — the magistracy that was the backbone of 18th-century local governance. His proceedings provide the forum in which evidence about the shipwreck is officially recorded, and his authority constrains the more lawless impulses of characters like the wrecker. Unlike Chowne, who corrupts institutions for private ends, Lougher embodies institutional authority exercised within its proper bounds.

## Character Analysis

Colonel Lougher is a background figure — more institution than character — but his presence is important to the novel's social architecture. He represents the legitimate face of authority against which Chowne's corruption is measured. Blackmore, who served as a magistrate himself later in life, treats the magistracy with respect: it is imperfect, class-bound, but fundamentally oriented toward justice.

> Colonel Lougher, of Candleston Court, was one of the most active amongst them.
>
> (019-002)

## Historical / Biographical Context

The 18th-century magistracy was the primary instrument of local government in England and Wales, combining judicial, administrative, and police functions. Magistrates were drawn from the gentry and served without pay — a system that ensured they had independent means but also tied justice to class interest. Blackmore's portrait, while idealized, reflects the real importance of magistrates in maintaining order in rural and coastal communities.
