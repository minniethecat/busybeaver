# Smuggling

The clandestine import and export of goods along the Glamorganshire coast, which Blackmore depicts as an established feature of maritime life, testing the boundaries between subsistence, criminality, and community loyalty.

## Definition

Smuggling — or "free-trade" as its practitioners called it — was the untaxed movement of goods by sea, a pervasive economic activity on the Welsh coast in the late 18th century. In *The Maid of Sker*, it appears as a known and semi-tolerated practice, entangled with legitimate fishing and shipbuilding industries. The narrator Davy observes it with the knowing eye of a local rather than the censure of an outsider.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Smuggling threads through the novel as part of the coastal economy's fabric. Davy recognizes smuggled goods by sight: lace "that made me think of smuggling" (025-019). The practice reaches as far as the colonial sphere, where Captain Nelson enforces the Navigation Act against illicit trade: "We soon cut up that bit of smuggling, although the Governor of the Islands was himself against us" (045-011). The shipwright at Ilfracombe proves to have "made more money, I doubt, by the smuggler's keg than the shipwright's adze" (067-006), showing how deeply smuggling penetrated legitimate trades.

> We soon cut up that bit of smuggling, although the Governor of the Islands was himself against us. Captain Nelson's orders were to enforce the Navigation Act; and we did it thoroughly.
>
> (045-011)

The parson Chowne's involvement as "Satan's own chaplain" and "supervisor of smugglers" (049-003) reveals how even clerical authority could be compromised by the smuggling economy, blurring the line between moral leadership and criminal enterprise.

## Critical Context

Smuggling on the Glamorganshire coast was endemic in the 18th century, driven by high duties on commodities like tea, brandy, tobacco, and lace. Coastal communities often regarded it as a legitimate "free trade" rather than crime, and local authorities frequently colluded. Blackmore's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: smuggling is presented as wrong in principle but understood in practice, part of the moral complexity of maritime life.
