# Burial and Death

The thematic treatment of mortality — from physical death and burial to spiritual death-in-life — which Blackmore uses to create Gothic atmosphere and explore the moral condition of his characters.

## Definition

Death in *The Maid of Sker* operates on multiple registers: as physical fact (drownings, shipwreck deaths, natural mortality), as Gothic atmosphere (graves, corpses, ghostly presences), and as moral metaphor (the "dead shipwrecks of ourselves" (052-033)). Blackmore's treatment reflects both the omnipresence of death in 18th-century coastal life and the Victorian Gothic fascination with mortality.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Death shadows the narrative from the outset. Davy's wife is "lately gone to a better world" (002-002); the orphan Bardie is discovered as if "dropped into the grave of the waters" (005-048). The fear of drowning without Christian burial haunts the coastal community. Davy confronts bodies "scared out of death's manufacture" (060-012) and meditates on the absence of proper rites for those "buried at sea rather than Christian burial" (010-014). Death is both intimate and institutional, personal and communal.

> So many of us are not the waifs and strays, and salvage only, but the dead shipwrecks of ourselves; content with the bottom of the great deep, only if no shallow fellows shall come diving down for us.
>
> (052-033)

## Critical Context

Victorian Gothic fiction is saturated with death, and Blackmore's maritime setting intensifies this preoccupation. Coastal communities lived with the constant reality of drowning; the absence of a body for Christian burial was a genuine spiritual terror. Blackmore draws on this cultural anxiety while transforming it into literary metaphor — the sea becomes at once a grave, a source of livelihood, and a figure for spiritual condition.
