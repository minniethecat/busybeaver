# Sir Philip

The "fine and pure old gentleman, the much-troubled Sir Philip" (062-016), whose wealth enables Davy's memoir and whose concealed relationship to Bardie provides the novel's ultimate revelation.

## Character Overview

Sir Philip is present in the narrative from its opening sentence — "a very wealthy gentleman, whose name will appear in this story" (001-003) — yet appears in person only near the novel's end. He is the patron who has "promised to pay all expenses, and £50 down" for Davy's book, an act of patronage that frames the entire narrative as a commissioned work. His delayed appearance and eventual revelation tie together the novel's threads of identity, class, and justice.

## Role in the Novel

Sir Philip is the novel's *deus ex machina* — the figure whose wealth and authority resolve what the efforts of humbler characters cannot. His connection to Bardie, when finally revealed, explains the mystery that has driven the plot and restores the child to her proper station. As a character, he is more symbolic than developed: he represents the ideal of aristocratic virtue — benevolent, honorable, and quietly effective.

## Character Analysis

Sir Philip represents the novel's conservative ideal: aristocracy as benevolent stewardship. Unlike the corrupt Chowne (clerical authority gone wrong) or the obstructionist Evan Thomas (local authority gone narrow), Sir Philip exercises power with wisdom and restraint. His structural position — absent yet powerful, revealed only at the climax — makes him a narrative device as much as a character.

> A very wealthy gentleman, whose name will appear in this story, has promised to pay all expenses, and £50 down (if I do it well), and to leave me the profit, if any.
>
> (001-003)

## Historical / Biographical Context

Sir Philip may be loosely based on Sir C. P. Bampfylde, a Devonshire baronet of Blackmore's acquaintance, or he may be a composite of the benevolent-patron type that appears throughout 18th- and 19th-century fiction. His role as the offstage benefactor whose concealed paternity resolves the plot echoes similar figures in Fielding and Scott.
