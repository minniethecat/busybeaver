# Royal Navy

His Majesty's Navy as it appears in the novel — not the admiralty of strategy and politics but the lived experience of common sailors: discipline, danger, comradeship, and the pride of service.

## Organization Overview

The Royal Navy in *The Maid of Sker* is both a specific institution and a moral reference point. Davy's years of service — "the many sprees ashore, and the noble fights with Frenchmen" (001-004) — have shaped his character, his language, and his standing in the community. The Navy appears directly through Captain Nelson's command and indirectly through the press-gang, naval pensions, and the web of maritime loyalty that connects characters.

## Role in the Novel

The Navy provides the novel's institutional counterweight to the corrupt parish system. Where Chowne's church is venal and self-serving, the Navy — at its best — is honorable and effective. Nelson's enforcement of the Navigation Act (045-011) demonstrates institutional integrity; Davy's naval pension (018-015) represents the service's care for its veterans.

## Historical Note

The Royal Navy of the late 18th century was the largest industrial organization in the Western world, employing tens of thousands of men. Blackmore's depiction captures both its institutional culture and its place in the lives of ordinary seamen.
