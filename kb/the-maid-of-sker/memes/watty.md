# Watty

A boy of the Sker household whose partial knowledge of the shipwrecked child and local events makes him an informant — unreliable but useful — in Davy's early investigation.

## Character Overview

Watty appears as a source of local knowledge whom Davy questions about the shipwrecked child: "And now, Watty, my boy, come and talk to me" (008-025). His knowledge is fragmentary — "about the shipwrecked child Watty knew scarce anything" (008-027) — but his talkative nature and local connections make him a useful conduit of information.

## Role in the Novel

Watty is a minor functional character — the boy who knows things but not everything, whose partial testimony advances the plot while maintaining the mystery. His presence also adds texture to the Sker household, populating it with the kind of servant-class figures who would have filled a real country estate.

## Key Scenes

- **Ch. VIII**: Davy questions Watty about the shipwrecked child (008-025, 008-027)
- **Ch. XI**: Watty reports on the movement of people toward the great shipwreck (011-019)

## Character Analysis

Watty exemplifies Blackmore's Dickensian attention to minor characters — figures who appear briefly but vividly, marked by a speech pattern or a mannerism, and who collectively create the novel's densely populated social world. His function is informational but his presence is atmospheric, contributing to the sense of Sker as a place with its own internal life.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Watty is a fictional creation, representative of the servant boys and estate workers who populated country households in 18th-century Wales. His type — the talkative, not-entirely-reliable informant — is a staple of mystery narratives from the Gothic novel onward.
