# Candleston Court

The Glamorganshire estate of Colonel Lougher — a seat of local authority whose drawing-rooms and legal proceedings represent the formal governance that the novel's wilder characters evade or exploit.

## Location

Candleston Court is situated near Merthyr Mawr in Glamorganshire, a gentry house of some antiquity. In the novel, it appears as a place of official business: the inquest into the Sker shipwreck is conducted under Colonel Lougher's authority, and "Mr. Lewis, bailiff to Colonel Lougher" (013-028) carries out the practical work of investigation.

## Significance in the Novel

Candleston Court represents the legitimate authority that stands in contrast to Chowne's corruption. Lougher's magistracy operates from his estate, blurring the line between domestic and judicial space in a way characteristic of 18th-century governance. Davy is acutely conscious of Lougher's social gaze: "even that Colonel Lougher of Candleston Court might see" (007-011).

> Mr. Lewis, bailiff to Colonel Lougher, "if we hold no quest on the black men, how shall we certify anything about this terrible shipwreck?"

## Historical Note

Candleston Court (or Candleston Castle) is a real location near Merthyr Mawr, a 14th-century fortified manor house later remodeled. By Blackmore's time it was a recognizable local landmark. The real Candleston family were prominent in medieval Glamorgan, though the house was tenanted by others by the 18th century.
