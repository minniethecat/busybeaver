# Money and Commerce

The circulation of money, goods, and obligations that constitutes the economic substructure of the novel — from Davy's fishing income to the patronage of wealthy gentlemen, from market prices to guineas risked and debts incurred.

## Definition

Money in *The Maid of Sker* is never invisible. Davy's narration is unusually frank about economic realities: the price of fish, the cost of keeping a child, the guineas risked on a journey, the "£50 down" (001-003) promised by a patron. This material specificity grounds the novel's romantic and Gothic elements in the daily calculations of subsistence. Blackmore presents an economy in which cash, credit, patronage, and barter coexist, and in which moral value and market value are in constant negotiation.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The novel opens with an economic proposition: the wealthy gentleman's promise of "£50 down (if I do it well)" (001-003) — the book we are reading is itself a commercial venture. Davy risks "five guineas out of my savings" (065-006) on a dangerous journey. Market transactions are reported with precision: the price of fish, the "half nothing a pound" (012-012) that middlemen offer. Even neighborly assistance is measured in economic terms — "sixpence never entered twice into the thoughts of anyone" (025-019).

> A very wealthy gentleman, whose name will appear in this story, has promised to pay all expenses, and £50 down (if I do it well), and to leave me the profit, if any.
>
> (001-003)

The novel's economic world is one of precarious subsistence occasionally relieved by patronage. Money does not corrupt in *The Maid of Sker*; rather, its absence exposes character, and its presence — when accompanied by generosity — enables the plot's movement toward resolution.

## Critical Context

The explicit presence of money in fiction was a Victorian innovation, part of the novel's commitment to representing social reality. Blackmore participates in this tradition while rooting it in the particular economy of the Welsh coast: neither agrarian nor industrial, dependent on fish, salvage, and patronage. The novel's economic frankness — Davy never pretends to be above money — is a form of narrative honesty that anticipates later realist fiction.
