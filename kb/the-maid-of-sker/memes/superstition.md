# Superstition

The web of folk beliefs, ghostly apparitions, and providential signs through which the characters — particularly the narrator Davy — interpret the strange events that befall them on the Welsh coast.

## Definition

Superstition in *The Maid of Sker* operates at the intersection of folk tradition, religious faith, and Gothic imagination. Davy's worldview is shaped by the superstitions of a seafaring community: he reads omens in the weather, perceives apparitions in the play of light on water, and interprets coincidence as providence. Blackmore neither endorses nor dismisses these beliefs, instead using them to create the novel's distinctive narrative texture.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Supernatural apprehension pervades Davy's narration. The derelict boat appears "sudden as an apparition" (004-005), gliding toward him with an almost supernatural grace. In the darkened hall at Sker, he believes "the ghosts of the wrecked child's father and mother" (009-003) have come to challenge him. Yet Davy's superstition is tempered by experience: "I called to mind how many strange things had befallen me, both at sea and on the coast" (009-003).

> As I gazed at all this beauty, trying not to go astray with wonder and with weariness, there, in the gateway of black rock, with the offing dark behind her, and the glittering waves upon their golden shoulders bearing her — sudden as an apparition came a smoothly-gliding boat.
>
> (004-005)

The spiritual register extends beyond mere ghostliness. Davy describes moments of transcendence: "my heart went up to God, and all my spirit and soul was lifted into something purer" (010-018), fusing superstition with genuine religious feeling in a way characteristic of Blackmore's narrative voice.

## Critical Context

Blackmore writes in the tradition of the Victorian Gothic, where superstition serves not as naive belief but as literary atmosphere. The Welsh coastal setting — with its isolation, treacherous seas, and Celtic folk traditions — provides fertile ground for supernatural suggestion. Unlike the urban Gothic of Dickens or Stevenson, Blackmore's supernaturalism is tied to landscape, weather, and the ancient superstitions of maritime communities.
