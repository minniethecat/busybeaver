# The Impressment

The roundup of reluctant recruits — including Davy's old foe the tailor and the sanctimonious Hezekiah — for naval service, a scene that moves from comedy to moral complexity.

## Event Overview

In the novel's later chapters, Davy participates in a press-gang operation targeting local men for naval service. The tailor and Hezekiah are "made so entirely drunk, that we were compelled, for their own sake, to weigh anchor while having their bodies on board" (053-027). The operation is presented as rough justice — the tailor who cheated Davy, the pious Hezekiah who bargained in bad faith — yet Blackmore registers the violence beneath the comedy.

## Key Developments

- **The tailor impressed**: Davy's old adversary is overcome by drink and carried aboard
- **Hezekiah's fate**: The sanctimonious fish-buyer shares the tailor's fate — "we impressed not only my foe the tailor, but also Hezekiah!" (053-027)
- **Moral ambiguity**: The operation is technically illegal — "with no war raging yet, we could not resort to violence" — making the impressment a form of extra-legal rough justice

## Key Participants

- [[davy-llewellyn]] — Participant in the press-gang, settling old scores
- [[hezekiah]] — Victim; the comic hypocrite gets his comeuppance

## Significance

The impressment scene encapsulates the novel's treatment of power: it is wielded with humor and a sense of rough justice, but the underlying violence is never entirely obscured. Davy's participation complicates his moral standing even as it satisfies his sense of poetic justice.
