# Glamorganshire

The coastal county of South Wales whose shoreline — treacherous, beautiful, and economically marginal — is the novel's geographical and imaginative territory.

## Location

Glamorganshire (now divided into several unitary authorities including Bridgend, Vale of Glamorgan, and Swansea) was one of the historic counties of Wales. Its southern coast faces the Bristol Channel, a body of water with one of the highest tidal ranges in the world. Blackmore's rendering of this coast — its sandhills, its rocky points, its dangerous reefs — is precise and knowledgeable.

## Significance in the Novel

Glamorganshire is more than setting; it is condition. The coastal economy — fishing, wrecking, smuggling — is shaped by the county's geography. The novel's social world — Welsh-speaking smallholders like Evan Thomas, Anglicized fishermen like Davy, English gentry like Colonel Lougher — reflects the county's cultural complexity. Blackmore's Glamorganshire is a place of particularity: not "the coast" in the abstract, but this coast, with its specific tides, fish, churches, and customs.

## Historical Note

Glamorganshire was historically one of the most populous and economically diverse Welsh counties, with a coastline that supported fishing, shipping, and — by Blackmore's time — the early industrial development of the South Wales coalfield. The novel captures the county on the cusp of transformation, before industrialization had fully remade its coastal communities.
