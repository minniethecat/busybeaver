# John

A representative figure of the common British sailor — one of the many "Jacks" whose collective presence in the novel evokes the maritime world that shaped Davy and his generation.

## Character Overview

John (or Jack, as he is variously called) appears not as a single developed character but as a recurring type: the ordinary seaman, competent and unpretentious, whose loyalty and skill form the backbone of naval power. Davy's memories of serving "with half a hundred Jacks ashore" (002-015) establish the type that individual Johns later embody.

## Role in the Novel

The various Johns and Jacks of the novel represent the maritime working class from which Davy comes and to which he returns. They are witnesses, helpers, and occasional comic figures — the chorus of ordinary seamen against which the more developed characters stand out.

## Character Analysis

John/Jack functions as a type rather than an individual — the common sailor whose virtues of loyalty, competence, and good humor the novel celebrates. This typological characterization is characteristic of Blackmore's treatment of working-class figures: they are rendered with respect and affection but without the psychological depth accorded to Davy.

> Half a hundred Jacks ashore, at Naples, or in Bermudas, or wherever the luck might happen.
>
> (002-015)

## Historical / Biographical Context

"Jack Tar" was the personification of the British common sailor in the 18th and 19th centuries — a figure celebrated in ballads, caricature, and fiction. Blackmore's treatment belongs to this tradition, presenting sailors as rough, honest, and essential to national identity.
