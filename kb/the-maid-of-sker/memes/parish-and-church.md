# Parish and Church

The institution of the Anglican parish church in late-18th-century Glamorganshire, which Blackmore depicts as both a spiritual anchor and a site of social tension, clerical ambition, and communal identity.

## Definition

The parish church in *The Maid of Sker* is more than a place of worship: it is the framework around which community life is organized. Baptisms, marriages, burials, poor relief, and moral oversight all flow through the parish system. Blackmore portrays the clergy with characteristic ambivalence — some devout, some worldly, some outright corrupt — reflecting the uneven quality of rural Anglican ministry in the period.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Davy's relationship with the church is pragmatic and social. He attends "with all my topmost togs on" (007-011), treating churchgoing as much a communal display as a spiritual act. The parson Chowne represents the dark extreme of clerical character — "Satan's own chaplain" (049-003) — while other clergy appear as background figures of variable virtue. The parish structure extends into education: the schoolmaster operates "by deputy commission from the parson of the parish" (051-012), showing the church's reach into daily life.

## Critical Context

The 18th-century Anglican parish was the basic unit of local governance as much as religious life. Blackmore's portrayal — from devout churchgoing to clerical corruption — mirrors historical realities of a system in which clergy might be sincere pastors, political appointees, or gentlemen of leisure. The Welsh context added linguistic and cultural dimensions: many parishioners were Welsh-speaking, while the established church operated in English.
