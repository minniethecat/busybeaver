# Sandy

The "vile Scotchman" (003-005) whose fishing at Sker provokes Evan Thomas's territorial jealousy — a figure of ethnic otherness and comic rivalry on the Welsh coast.

## Character Overview

Sandy appears primarily through the hostile reports of others. Evan Thomas complains of "one vile Scotchman" who fishes at Sker, and Davy has "no objection to his forbidding other people, and especially one vile Scotchman" (003-005). Sandy himself is glimpsed rather than developed — defined by his nationality and his perceived intrusion on local fishing rights.

## Role in the Novel

Sandy functions as a comic offstage presence and a marker of the ethnic tensions that characterized 18th-century British coastal communities. His Scottishness sets him apart from both the Welsh Evan Thomas and the Anglicized Davy, making him a triple outsider — and a convenient scapegoat for territorial disputes.

## Key Scenes

- **Ch. III**: Referred to as "one vile Scotchman" whom Evan Thomas wishes to exclude from Sker (003-005)

## Relationships

- [[evan-thomas]] — Evan's hostility to Sandy exemplifies his territorial possessiveness
- [[davy-llewellyn]] — Davy is content to let Evan exclude Sandy while Davy himself fishes at Sker

## Character Analysis

Sandy is barely a character — more a reference, a joke, an ethnic marker. But his presence in the novel is revealing. Blackmore registers the presence of Scottish migrants in Wales, the ethnic hierarchies of British coastal communities, and the way that shared prejudice can lubricate social relations — Davy and Evan find common ground in their shared antipathy to the Scotchman.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Scottish migration to England and Wales increased significantly in the 18th century, driven by economic opportunity and, after 1745, political integration. Scottish fishermen, traders, and laborers were a visible presence in many coastal communities, often meeting the kind of local suspicion that Blackmore registers through Sandy.
