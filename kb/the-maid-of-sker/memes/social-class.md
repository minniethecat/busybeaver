# Social Class

The hierarchical social order of Georgian Britain, which Blackmore depicts with an acute eye for the distances and intimacies between ranks — from fisherman to gentleman, from parish pauper to naval officer.

## Definition

Social class in *The Maid of Sker* is the omnipresent but often unspoken framework governing behavior, speech, marriage, and justice. Davy Llewellyn, a fisherman and former sailor, navigates a world in which rank determines everything from the credibility of testimony to the prospect of friendship. Blackmore portrays this hierarchy with a mixture of acceptance and subversion: Davy's plain speech and moral clarity often expose the pretensions of his social superiors.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Class consciousness permeates Davy's narration. He notes "gentlemanly actions done by the superior classes" (022-029), acknowledging a moral expectation attached to rank. Gentlemen are identified by their bearing and title: "a very nice gentleman indeed, the head of an ancient family" (034-024), or "that fine and pure old gentleman, the much-troubled Sir Philip" (062-016). Davy's own rise — from fisherman to man "in authority" (042-007) — traces a modest class mobility enabled by naval service and patronage.

> I am a man in authority now.
>
> (042-007)

Yet class is never merely observed; it is felt. Davy's awareness of his own rank conditions every interaction with his social superiors, and his narrative voice — direct, colloquial, unpretentious — constitutes a quiet argument for the dignity of the laboring man.

## Characters Associated

- [[davy-llewellyn]] — The fisherman narrator, whose class position is central to his identity

## Critical Context

The late 18th century was a period of considerable class anxiety in Britain, with the French Revolution casting long shadows over the social order. Blackmore, writing a century later, looks back with a Victorian's complex attitude toward Georgian hierarchy — nostalgic for its stability, critical of its injustice. The naval context is significant: the Royal Navy was one of the few institutions in which merit could enable social mobility, and Davy's trajectory reflects this possibility.
