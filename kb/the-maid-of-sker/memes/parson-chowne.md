# Parson Chowne

The corrupt clergyman whose machinations drive the novel's dark plot — a figure of clerical vice, calculated revenge, and ultimately self-destructive madness.

## Character Overview

Parson Chowne is introduced through reputation before appearance: "Passon Chowne, and Passon Jack, man alive now" (027-017) — the doubled name suggesting his divided nature. He is the supervisor of smugglers, a manipulator of legal process, and a man who "never hurried vengeance. He knew that he was sure to have it" (036-007). Blackmore draws him as a Gothic villain in clerical garb: intelligent, patient, and utterly without scruple.

## Role in the Novel

Chowne is the engine of the novel's darkest developments. His corruption of the parish system, his involvement with smuggling, and his campaign of revenge against those who cross him create the obstacles that Davy must overcome. His dog's death from "rabid madness" (068-010) and his own parallel demise literalize the moral contagion he represents. Chowne is the counterweight to the novel's vision of providential order — the force of chaos and malice that must be overcome for the plot to reach its comic resolution.

## Character Analysis

Chowne belongs to the tradition of the clerical villain — a stock figure of 18th- and 19th-century fiction reflecting genuine anxieties about the quality of rural clergy. Blackmore's treatment is notably Gothic: Chowne is not merely corrupt but demonic, "Satan's own chaplain" (049-003). His patience in revenge gives him a chilling competence that distinguishes him from more impulsive villains. His death — mirroring his dog's madness — is among the most violent and symbolic in Victorian fiction.

> This man never hurried vengeance. He knew that he was sure to have it.
>
> (036-007)

## Historical / Biographical Context

While Chowne is fictional, his character reflects historical realities of 18th-century Anglican clergy: some were sincere pastors, others political appointees with little religious vocation. The figure of the corrupt parson was sufficiently common to become a literary type, appearing in Fielding, Smollett, and the Gothic tradition that Blackmore inherits and extends.
