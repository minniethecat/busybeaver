# Identity and Disguise

The concealment and revelation of true identity, which drives the novel's central mystery — the unknown origins of the orphan Bardie — and shapes the moral and narrative architecture of the story.

## Definition

Identity in *The Maid of Sker* is never simply given; it is lost, concealed, mistaken, and finally revealed. The novel's plot turns on the question of who Bardie really is, but this central mystery radiates outward: nearly every major character has a relationship to truth and concealment. Blackmore treats identity as both a narrative puzzle and a moral condition — to be unknown is to be vulnerable; to be known is to be claimed.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The novel is structured around the gradual revelation of Bardie's origins. Davy's discovery of the child is framed in the language of mystery: her identity is concealed by the circumstances of shipwreck. The "very wealthy gentleman, whose name will appear in this story" (001-003) is a figure whose identity is tantalizingly withheld from the reader. Throughout, identity is tied to name, rank, and kinship — the coordinates by which a person is located in the social order.

> A very wealthy gentleman, whose name will appear in this story, has promised to pay all expenses, and £50 down.
>
> (001-003)

The "troublesome need of concealment" (019-012) affects characters across the social spectrum. Identity in this novel is not a fixed possession but something that must be established, defended, and — in Bardie's case — discovered.

## Critical Context

The hidden-identity plot is a staple of the Victorian novel, from *Oliver Twist* to *The Woman in White*. Blackmore adapts this convention to the coastal Gothic mode: identity is lost not in the city but at sea, and recovery depends not on lawyers but on the slow accumulation of local knowledge and moral witness. The novel's interest in identity reflects broader 19th-century anxieties about class mobility, family legitimacy, and the legibility of the self.
