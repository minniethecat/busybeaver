# Wrecking and Salvage

The practice of salvaging goods from shipwrecks — and the darker temptation of causing them — which Blackmore presents as the moral frontier of Welsh coastal life.

## Definition

Wrecking, in its criminal sense, was the deliberate luring of ships onto rocks to plunder their cargo; salvage was the legitimate recovery of goods from accidental wrecks. In *The Maid of Sker*, these practices exist on a moral spectrum, from the desperate scavenger to the outright wrecker. The narrator Davy navigates this boundary with a mixture of self-interest and conscience.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Shipwreck is a recurring presence. Davy immediately recognizes a derelict boat as "my chance of chances, worth perhaps ten times my catch of fish" (004-012) — salvage as economic opportunity. The great shipwreck that drives the early plot brings the community to the shore: "They are gone to meet a great shipwreck, worth all the rabbits all the way from Dunraven to Giant's Grave" (011-026). The dark figure of the wrecker appears as a moral warning: the "cruel wrecker" (011-007) who would make off with a boat.

> I saw at once, as an old sailor pretty well up in shipwrecks, that the boat was no more than a derelict; and feeling that here was my chance of chances, worth perhaps ten times my catch of fish, I set myself in earnest to the catching of that boat.
>
> (004-012)

Blackmore extends the metaphor of wrecking to the moral sphere: "so many of us are not the waifs and strays, and salvage only, but the dead shipwrecks of ourselves" (052-033), transforming a coastal practice into a spiritual meditation.

## Critical Context

The Welsh coast had a notorious reputation for wrecking in the 18th and early 19th centuries, though historians debate how much was deliberate luring versus opportunistic salvage. Blackmore engages with this controversy through character and metaphor: wrecking is both a social fact and a figure for moral ruin. The novel's treatment reflects Victorian fascination with coastal criminality while humanizing those who lived at the sea's margin.
