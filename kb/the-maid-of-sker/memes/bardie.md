# Bardie

The child discovered by Davy in a derelict boat off Sker — the eponymous "maid" whose identity, parentage, and fate constitute the novel's governing mystery.

## Character Overview

Bardie appears as if from the sea itself: a small child, perhaps three or four years old, alone in a beached boat, speaking a strange mixture of baby-English and Welsh-inflected phrases. "I'se Bardie. Didn't 'a know that?" (005-018) is her self-introduction. She is discovered wearing fine clothing that belies her circumstances — the first clue to her concealed identity. Throughout the novel, Bardie grows from mysterious foundling to young woman, her true origins revealed only at the climax.

## Role in the Novel

Bardie is both the novel's title character and its central enigma. Her presence catalyzes every major plot development: Davy's conflicts with Evan Thomas, his entanglement with Parson Chowne, and ultimately the revelation that ties her to the wealthy gentleman who has been the narrative's offstage patron. As a character, Bardie is less psychologically developed than Davy — she is more symbol than personality — but her silence and resilience make her a compelling figure in her own right.

## Character Analysis

Bardie represents the Victorian novel's fascination with the figure of the orphan — a blank slate upon which plots of inheritance, identity, and social order can be inscribed. Her near-muteness in the early chapters makes her an object of projection for other characters: for Davy she is providential good luck, for Evan Thomas she is a nuisance, for Parson Chowne she is a tool. Her eventual identification restores her to her proper social place, fulfilling the novel's conservative resolution of the identity plot.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Bardie is a fictional creation, but she draws on the Victorian literary type of the "mysterious orphan" — a figure that appears across the 19th-century novel from *Oliver Twist* to *The Secret Garden*. Blackmore's innovation is to locate this figure in the coastal Gothic mode, where the sea replaces the workhouse or the ancestral mansion as the site of lost identity.
