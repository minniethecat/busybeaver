# Death of Chowne

The novel's climactic event — the death of Parson Chowne from "rabid madness, under the clutch of his own mastiff" (068-010) — a scene in which Gothic horror and moral justice converge.

## Event Overview

In the novel's final chapter, Parson Chowne meets his end in a manner that literalizes the metaphor of moral contagion. His dog dies of rabies; Chowne himself succumbs to "canine madness" (068-002), dying in convulsions that mirror his dog's death. The scene is among the most violent and symbolic in Victorian fiction — justice rendered not by law but by the body's disintegration.

## Key Developments

- **The dog's death**: Chowne's mastiff dies of rabies, presaging his master's fate
- **Chowne's madness**: The parson exhibits symptoms of "burning madness" (056-032), his physical deterioration mirroring his moral corruption
- **The death**: Chowne dies "under the clutch of his own mastiff" — the instrument of his destruction is his own property, his own nature

## Significance

Chowne's death is the novel's most concentrated expression of its moral vision: evil is not merely punished but self-consuming. The canine imagery that has attended Chowne throughout the novel — his dog, his "canine" nature — reaches its horrific conclusion. The death is both Gothic spectacle and moral allegory.
