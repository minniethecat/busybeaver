# Rodney Bluett

A naval companion who appears in the novel's closing chapters — a figure of loyalty, competence, and the bonds of maritime comradeship.

## Character Overview

Rodney Bluett enters late in the narrative as a fellow sailor whose presence signals the novel's turn toward resolution. He brings Davy news from the wider world and assistance in the final confrontation. His name — Rodney — evokes the naval hero Admiral Rodney, marking him as a man of the service.

## Role in the Novel

Rodney functions as an ally in the novel's climactic sequences. His naval competence and loyalty to Davy provide practical support and moral reinforcement. As a minor character, he represents the network of naval comradeship that extends beyond the immediate setting.

## Character Analysis

Rodney is a functional late-novel ally — a type common in Victorian fiction where the protagonist's network of supporters must be mobilized for the final resolution. His naval identity reinforces the novel's investment in the values of service, loyalty, and competence.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Rodney is a fictional creation, but his name invokes Admiral George Brydges Rodney (1718–1792), a celebrated naval commander of the American War of Independence. The naming is characteristic of Blackmore's practice of embedding historical reference in fictional characters.
