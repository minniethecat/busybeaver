# Bunny

Davy Llewellyn's orphaned granddaughter, whose presence in his cottage — demanding, endearing, and perpetually hungry — provides the novel's domestic center and much of its humor.

## Character Overview

Bunny is introduced in the novel's second chapter as "my orphan grandchild 'Bunny,' daughter of my son just drowned at sea" (002-004). A child of around six or seven, she is characterized above all by her prodigious appetite: she has "eaten a barley-loaf, and two pig's feet, and a dogfish" (002-007) and is still hungry. Yet Bunny is no mere comic figure; she is the emotional anchor of Davy's household and the reason he continues to fish despite grief and poverty.

## Role in the Novel

Bunny serves as a foil to Bardie: where Bardie is mysterious and silent, Bunny is transparent and voluble. Her acceptance of Bardie as a foster sister models the domestic ideal that the novel endorses. While Bunny does not drive the plot, she grounds it — her presence reminds the reader (and Davy) of what is at stake in the struggle against Chowne and the forces of disorder.

## Key Scenes

- **Ch. II**: First appearance; her hunger and Davy's devotion established (002-004, 002-007)
- **Ch. VI–VIII**: Bonding with Bardie; the two children become companions
- **Ch. V**: Davy leaves Bunny in the care of Mrs. Jones while he goes fishing — the day he discovers Bardie (002-013)

## Relationships

- [[davy-llewellyn]] — Her grandfather and guardian; the central attachment of her life
- [[bardie]] — Her foster sister; childhood companion

## Character Analysis

Bunny exemplifies Blackmore's skill at rendering children without sentimentality. Her appetite is comic but never cruel; her affection for Davy is genuine without being cloying. In a novel dominated by Gothic menace and narrative mystery, Bunny provides the ordinary — meals, bedtime, childish questions — that makes the extraordinary events feel real. She is the domestic counterweight to the novel's Gothic machinery.

> My dear, and you shall have more. Only take your feet out of my pocket. Little heart have I for fishing, God knows; but a-fishing I will go this day.
>
> (002-009)

## Historical / Biographical Context

Bunny is a fictional creation, but her situation — an orphan cared for by a grandparent — reflects the demographic realities of 18th-century Britain, where high adult mortality meant that extended-family care arrangements were common. Blackmore's sympathetic portrayal of a working-class child was relatively unusual in Victorian fiction, which more often centered on middle- and upper-class childhoods.
