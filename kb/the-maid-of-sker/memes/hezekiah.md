# Hezekiah

The "well-known Brother Hezekiah" (012-013), a dissenting tradesman whose sanctimonious bargaining and physical cowardice provide the novel's most sustained comic portrait.

## Character Overview

Hezekiah is introduced as a fish-buyer who uses religious language to drive down prices: "Brother Hezekiah... in the hunger all people have to hear the Word, you have forgotten your own hunger, to break your fast" (012-018). His piety is transparently self-serving — a cover for sharp dealing. Later, his impressment into the navy — achieved by getting him "so entirely drunk" (053-027) — provides the novel's most farcical set piece.

## Role in the Novel

Hezekiah is a figure of comic relief and social satire. Blackmore uses him to mock the hypocrisy of those who deploy religious language for worldly gain — a theme that connects Hezekiah to the more sinister Parson Chowne. His impressment is both punishment and punchline: the man who cheated the fisherman ends up serving at sea.

## Key Scenes

- **Ch. XII**: Bargaining for fish; Davy sees through his pious bargaining (012-013, 012-018)
- **Ch. LIII**: Impressed into the navy through drink — a comic triumph for Davy (053-027)

## Character Analysis

Hezekiah belongs to the tradition of the religious hypocrite in English satire — a line running from Chaucer's Pardoner through Ben Jonson to Dickens. Blackmore's treatment is genial rather than savage: Hezekiah is ridiculous rather than evil, and his humiliation is comic comeuppance rather than moral destruction. His presence in the novel broadens its social range, bringing dissenting tradesmen into a narrative dominated by Anglicans and naval men.

> The well-known Brother Hezekiah... in the hunger all people have to hear the Word, you have forgotten your own hunger, to break your fast.
>
> (012-013)

## Historical / Biographical Context

Hezekiah represents the class of dissenting tradesmen — Baptists, Independents, Methodists — who were a significant presence in 18th-century Wales and the West Country. Blackmore, an Anglican clergyman's son, treats dissenters with the amused condescension characteristic of establishment literary culture, though Hezekiah's portrait is more comic than malicious.
