# Revenge

The pursuit of vengeance as a motivating force in the novel, operating on scales from personal grievance to cosmic retribution, and revealing character through its presence, absence, or transcendence.

## Definition

Revenge in *The Maid of Sker* is never simple. Blackmore presents it as a natural human impulse that the novel's moral framework simultaneously acknowledges and questions. Characters who act on revenge — swiftly, violently — are set against those who absorb injury without retaliation, and the narrative itself seems to enact a larger retributive logic through the workings of plot and providence.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The language of vengeance pervades the novel's climactic passages. "The deep revenge of the sea was rolling, not a hundred yards away" (010-018) — here revenge is attributed to nature itself, a cosmic force indifferent to human concerns. Human vengeance, by contrast, takes calculated form: "this man never hurried vengeance. He knew that he was sure to have it" (036-007). The novel traces the arc from grievance to retribution across multiple characters, testing whether revenge restores or destroys.

> It is out of my power to tell you how my heart went up to God, and all my spirit and soul was lifted into something purer... The fury of the earth and sky was all around and over them; the deep revenge of the sea was rolling, not a hundred yards away.
>
> (010-018)

## Critical Context

Revenge tragedy was a dominant mode of English drama, and the Victorian novel inherited its fascination with retribution while complicating its moral simplicity. Blackmore's treatment aligns with the Gothic tradition where revenge is both a narrative engine and a site of moral inquiry. The maritime setting adds a distinctive dimension: the sea's impersonal destructiveness sets human vengeance in scale, questioning its adequacy as a response to suffering.
