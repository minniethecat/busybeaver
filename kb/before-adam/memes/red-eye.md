## Overview

Red-Eye is the most feared member of the Folk and the novel's central antagonist. Physically larger and more aggressive than any of his peers, he operates as a predatory force within the horde — dominating through violence, claiming mates by force, and answering to no authority. The narrator explicitly identifies him as "an atavism" (005-086), a biological throwback to a fiercer evolutionary past who has no place in the emerging social order of cooperation and restraint.

## Role in the Narrative

Red-Eye serves as the primary obstacle to the narrator's survival and happiness. His "enmity" and the "love of the Swift One" form the central conflict of the narrator's prehistoric existence (003-039). Red-Eye covets the Swift One, the narrator's mate, and their rivalry drives much of the novel's action. In direct combat, the narrator cannot match Red-Eye's brute strength — survival requires cunning, alliance, and flight rather than confrontation (003-021).

## Relationships

Red-Eye's relationship to the Folk is purely one of dominance. He takes what he wants — food, mates, territory — without regard for the group's welfare. His presence creates a constant undercurrent of fear within the horde. Unlike other members who cooperate in the hee-hee councils, Red-Eye operates alone, a solitary predator within a social species.

## Traits and Characteristics

London describes Red-Eye as "hideous" in combat, a creature of "screaming incoherence and insane fury" (001-021). His defining physical trait — the red-rimmed eyes that give him his name — suggests both his ferocity and his otherness. He is larger than the other Folk: "Nor did I ever see one of the Fire People so large as he" (005-050). His size, combined with his aggression, makes him an apex threat within the tribe.
