## Overview

The drinking-place in _Before Adam_ is not merely a water source. It functions as the primary social gathering site of the horde — a place where the Folk congregate at day's end, where hierarchies are displayed, where social bonds and rivalries play out, and where predators and enemies stage ambushes. The narrator describes it as "the circus of the drinking-places at the end of the day," comparing the gathering of the Folk to the crowds pressing around a street performer (001-025). Run-ways — the steep earthen paths carved into the riverbank — provide the only safe passage between the cliff caves and the drinking-places (005-006).

## In the Novel

The drinking-places are integral to the geography and social life of the horde. The bluff where the Folk dwell contains multiple run-ways leading down to several different drinking-places strung along the river's edge (005-006, 005-016). Each drinking-place becomes a specific locale with its own associations — the "great drinking-place" where the horde once "wandered once or twice" and where the river spreads wide and quiet (013-171, 013-176).

The drinking-place doubles as a site of danger. When the Fire People attack, they strike at the drinking-places, knowing the Folk must descend to drink. After one attack, the horde "kept our run-ways and drinking-places to ourselves," suggesting territorial defense of these access points (006-097). Even ordinary trips carry risk: Red-Eye's swaggering dominance plays out along the run-ways and at the drinking-places, where his mere approach causes the narrator and Lop-Ear to flee (005-101).

The drinking-place also serves practical functions beyond hydration. The horde finds "ice on the surface of the quiet water in the eddy where was the drinking-place," a rare cold-weather event that causes bewilderment, with old Marrow-Bone examining the ice with "worried, plaintive" eyes (008-009, 008-010). It is from a drinking-place that Big-Tooth's sister, Lop-Ear's wife, flees up a run-way pursued by Red-Eye (014-070). And it is at a drinking-place that the horde first sees the Fire People's migration crossing the river — "splashing and floundering in a drinking-place" (013-063).

## Context and Significance

The drinking-place as social hub reflects a pattern observed across primate species: water sources function as gathering points where unrelated individuals interact, hierarchies are tested, and social information is exchanged. In early hominin evolution, the concentration of individuals at watering holes would have created both cooperative opportunities and predation risks — exactly the dynamic London portrays. The drinking-places represent the Folk's most exposed, communal spaces, in contrast with the individual safety of the caves.

## Evolutionary and Technological Significance

The drinking-place functions as more than a geographical feature — it is the social center of the Folk's world. All members of the horde, regardless of status or strength, must come to the drinking-places, making them sites of both community and conflict. The narrator's first encounter with the Folk occurs at a drinking-place, and the carrot-patch ambush takes place near one. These sites are where the boundaries between safety and danger, community and isolation, are most sharply drawn.

> The narrator's acquisition of this knowledge — at first unconscious, then gradually recognized — mirrors the evolutionary trajectory London traces throughout the novel: from instinct to awareness, from accident to intention. [005-006]
