## CHAPTER XV

[015-001]Lop-Ear got married. It was the second winter after our

[015-002]adventure-journey, and it was most unexpected. He gave me no warning.

[015-003]The first I knew was one twilight when I climbed the cliff to our cave.

[015-004]I squeezed into the entrance and there I stopped. There was no room for

[015-005]me. Lop-Ear and his mate were in possession, and she was none other

[015-006]than my sister, the daughter of my step-father, the Chatterer.

[015-007]I tried to force my way in. There was space only for two, and that

[015-008]space was already occupied. Also, they had me at a disadvantage, and,

[015-009]what of the scratching and hair-pulling I received, I was glad to

[015-010]retreat. I slept that night, and for many nights, in the connecting

[015-011]passage of the double-cave. From my experience it seemed reasonably

[015-012]safe. As the two Folk had dodged old Saber-Tooth, and as I had dodged

[015-013]Red-Eye, so it seemed to me that I could dodge the hunting animals by

[015-014]going back and forth between the two caves.

[015-015]I had forgotten the wild dogs. They were small enough to go through any

[015-016]passage that I could squeeze through. One night they nosed me out. Had

[015-017]they entered both caves at the same time they would have got me. As it

[015-018]was, followed by some of them through the passage, I dashed out the

[015-019]mouth of the other cave. Outside were the rest of the wild dogs. They

[015-020]sprang for me as I sprang for the cliff-wall and began to climb. One of

[015-021]them, a lean and hungry brute, caught me in mid-leap. His teeth sank

[015-022]into my thigh-muscles, and he nearly dragged me back. He held on, but I

[015-023]made no effort to dislodge him, devoting my whole effort to climbing

[015-024]out of reach of the rest of the brutes.

[015-025]Not until I was safe from them did I turn my attention to that live

[015-026]agony on my thigh. And then, a dozen feet above the snapping pack that

[015-027]leaped and scrambled against the wall and fell back, I got the dog by

[015-028]the throat and slowly throttled him. I was a long time doing it. He

[015-029]clawed and ripped my hair and hide with his hind-paws, and ever he

[015-030]jerked and lunged with his weight to drag me from the wall.

[015-031]At last his teeth opened and released my torn flesh. I carried his body

[015-032]up the cliff with me, and perched out the night in the entrance of my

[015-033]old cave, wherein were Lop-Ear and my sister. But first I had to endure

[015-034]a storm of abuse from the aroused horde for being the cause of the

[015-035]disturbance. I had my revenge. From time to time, as the noise of the

[015-036]pack below eased down, I dropped a rock and started it up again.

[015-037]Whereupon, from all around, the abuse of the exasperated Folk began

[015-038]afresh. In the morning I shared the dog with Lop-Ear and his wife, and

[015-039]for several days the three of us were neither vegetarians nor

[015-040]fruitarians.

[015-041]Lop-Ear’s marriage was not a happy one, and the consolation about it is

[015-042]that it did not last very long. Neither he nor I was happy during that

[015-043]period. I was lonely. I suffered the inconvenience of being cast out of

[015-044]my safe little cave, and somehow I did not make it up with any other of

[015-045]the young males. I suppose my long-continued chumming with Lop-Ear had

[015-046]become a habit.

[015-047]I might have married, it is true; and most likely I should have married

[015-048]had it not been for the dearth of females in the horde. This dearth, it

[015-049]is fair to assume, was caused by the exorbitance of Red-Eye, and it

[015-050]illustrates the menace he was to the existence of the horde. Then there

[015-051]was the Swift One, whom I had not forgotten.

[015-052]At any rate, during the period of Lop-Ear’s marriage I knocked about

[015-053]from pillar to post, in danger every night that I slept, and never

[015-054]comfortable. One of the Folk died, and his widow was taken into the

[015-055]cave of another one of the Folk. I took possession of the abandoned

[015-056]cave, but it was wide-mouthed, and after Red-Eye nearly trapped me in

[015-057]it one day, I returned to sleeping in the passage of the double-cave.

[015-058]During the summer, however, I used to stay away from the caves for

[015-059]weeks, sleeping in a tree-shelter I made near the mouth of the slough.

[015-060]I have said that Lop-Ear was not happy. My sister was the daughter of

[015-061]the Chatterer, and she made Lop-Ear’s life miserable for him. In no

[015-062]other cave was there so much squabbling and bickering. If Red-Eye was a

[015-063]Bluebeard, Lop-Ear was hen-pecked; and I imagine that Red-Eye was too

[015-064]shrewd ever to covet Lop-Ear’s wife.

[015-065]Fortunately for Lop-Ear, she died. An unusual thing happened that

[015-066]summer. Late, almost at the end of it, a second crop of the

[015-067]stringy-rooted carrots sprang up. These unexpected second-crop roots

[015-068]were young and juicy and tender, and for some time the carrot-patch was

[015-069]the favorite feeding-place of the horde. One morning, early, several

[015-070]score of us were there making our breakfast. On one side of me was the

[015-071]Hairless One. Beyond him were his father and son, old Marrow-Bone and

[015-072]Long-Lip. On the other side of me were my sister and Lop-Ear, she being

[015-073]next to me.

[015-074]There was no warning. On the sudden, both the Hairless One and my

[015-075]sister sprang and screamed. At the same instant I heard the thud of the

[015-076]arrows that transfixed them. The next instant they were down on the

[015-077]ground, floundering and gasping, and the rest of us were stampeding for

[015-078]the trees. An arrow drove past me and entered the ground, its feathered

[015-079]shaft vibrating and oscillating from the impact of its arrested flight.

[015-080]I remember clearly how I swerved as I ran, to go past it, and that I

[015-081]gave it a needlessly wide berth. I must have shied at it as a horse

[015-082]shies at an object it fears.

[015-083]Lop-Ear took a smashing fall as he ran beside me. An arrow had driven

[015-084]through the calf of his leg and tripped him. He tried to run, but was

[015-085]tripped and thrown by it a second time. He sat up, crouching, trembling

[015-086]with fear, and called to me pleadingly. I dashed back. He showed me the

[015-087]arrow. I caught hold of it to pull it out, but the consequent hurt made

[015-088]him seize my hand and stop me. A flying arrow passed between us.

[015-089]Another struck a rock, splintered, and fell to the ground. This was too

[015-090]much. I pulled, suddenly, with all my might. Lop-Ear screamed as the

[015-091]arrow came out, and struck at me angrily. But the next moment we were

[015-092]in full flight again.

[015-093]I looked back. Old Marrow-Bone, deserted and far behind, was tottering

[015-094]silently along in his handicapped race with death. Sometimes he almost

[015-095]fell, and once he did fall; but no more arrows were coming. He

[015-096]scrambled weakly to his feet. Age burdened him heavily, but he did not

[015-097]want to die. The three Fire-Men, who were now running forward from

[015-098]their forest ambush, could easily have got him, but they did not try.

[015-099]Perhaps he was too old and tough. But they did want the Hairless One

[015-100]and my sister, for as I looked back from the trees I could see the

[015-101]Fire-Men beating in their heads with rocks. One of the Fire-Men was the

[015-102]wizened old hunter who limped.

[015-103]We went on through the trees toward the caves—an excited and disorderly

[015-104]mob that drove before it to their holes all the small life of the

[015-105]forest, and that set the blue-jays screaming impudently. Now that there

[015-106]was no immediate danger, Long-Lip waited for his grand-father,

[015-107]Marrow-Bone; and with the gap of a generation between them, the old

[015-108]fellow and the youth brought up our rear.

[015-109]And so it was that Lop-Ear became a bachelor once more. That night I

[015-110]slept with him in the old cave, and our old life of chumming began

[015-111]again. The loss of his mate seemed to cause him no grief. At least he

[015-112]showed no signs of it, nor of need for her. It was the wound in his leg

[015-113]that seemed to bother him, and it was all of a week before he got back

[015-114]again to his old spryness.

[015-115]Marrow-Bone was the only old member in the horde. Sometimes, on looking

[015-116]back upon him, when the vision of him is most clear, I note a striking

[015-117]resemblance between him and the father of my father’s gardener. The

[015-118]gardener’s father was very old, very wrinkled and withered; and for all

[015-119]the world, when he peered through his tiny, bleary eyes and mumbled

[015-120]with his toothless gums, he looked and acted like old Marrow-Bone. This

[015-121]resemblance, as a child, used to frighten me. I always ran when I saw

[015-122]the old man tottering along on his two canes. Old Marrow-Bone even had

[015-123]a bit of sparse and straggly white beard that seemed identical with the

[015-124]whiskers of the old man.

[015-125]As I have said, Marrow-Bone was the only old member of the horde. He

[015-126]was an exception. The Folk never lived to old age. Middle age was

[015-127]fairly rare. Death by violence was the common way of death. They died

[015-128]as my father had died, as Broken-Tooth had died, as my sister and the

[015-129]Hairless One had just died—abruptly and brutally, in the full

[015-130]possession of their faculties, in the full swing and rush of life.

[015-131]Natural death? To die violently was the natural way of dying in those

[015-132]days.

[015-133]No one died of old age among the Folk. I never knew of a case. Even

[015-134]Marrow-Bone did not die that way, and he was the only one in my

[015-135]generation who had the chance. A bad crippling, any serious accidental

[015-136]or temporary impairment of the faculties, meant swift death. As a rule,

[015-137]these deaths were not witnessed.

[015-138]Members of the horde simply dropped out of sight. They left the caves

[015-139]in the morning, and they never came back. They disappeared—into the

[015-140]ravenous maws of the hunting creatures.

[015-141]This inroad of the Fire People on the carrot-patch was the beginning of

[015-142]the end, though we did not know it. The hunters of the Fire People

[015-143]began to appear more frequently as the time went by. They came in twos

[015-144]and threes, creeping silently through the forest, with their flying

[015-145]arrows able to annihilate distance and bring down prey from the top of

[015-146]the loftiest tree without themselves climbing into it. The bow and

[015-147]arrow was like an enormous extension of their leaping and striking

[015-148]muscles, so that, virtually, they could leap and kill at a hundred feet

[015-149]and more. This made them far more terrible than Saber-Tooth himself.

[015-150]And then they were very wise. They had speech that enabled them more

[015-151]effectively to reason, and in addition they understood cooperation.

[015-152]We Folk came to be very circumspect when we were in the forest. We were

[015-153]more alert and vigilant and timid. No longer were the trees a

[015-154]protection to be relied upon. No longer could we perch on a branch and

[015-155]laugh down at our carnivorous enemies on the ground. The Fire People

[015-156]were carnivorous, with claws and fangs a hundred feet long, the most

[015-157]terrible of all the hunting animals that ranged the primeval world.

[015-158]One morning, before the Folk had dispersed to the forest, there was a

[015-159]panic among the water-carriers and those who had gone down to the river

[015-160]to drink. The whole horde fled to the caves. It was our habit, at such

[015-161]times, to flee first and investigate afterward. We waited in the mouths

[015-162]of our caves and watched. After some time a Fire-Man stepped cautiously

[015-163]into the open space. It was the little wizened old hunter. He stood for

[015-164]a long time and watched us, looking our caves and the cliff-wall up and

[015-165]down. He descended one of the run-ways to a drinking-place, returning a

[015-166]few minutes later by another run-way. Again he stood and watched us

[015-167]carefully, for a long time. Then he turned on his heel and limped into

[015-168]the forest, leaving us calling querulously and plaintively to one

[015-169]another from the cave-mouths.
