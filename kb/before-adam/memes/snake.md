## Role in Ancestral Memory

Snakes feature prominently in the narrator's dream-memories as one of the most persistent and terrifying threats of the Younger World.
Unlike large mammalian predators such as [[lion|lions]] or [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] that could sometimes be avoided, snakes appeared everywhere — in the grass, on rocks, and even in the trees where [[the-folk|the Folk]] sought refuge.

The narrator describes the pervasiveness of this fear:

> Snakes? Long before I had heard of the existence of snakes, I was tormented by them in my sleep.
> They lurked for me in the forest glades; leaped up, striking, under my feet; squirmed off through the dry grass or across naked patches of rock; or pursued me into the tree-tops, encircling the trunks with their great shining bodies (001-073)

## Tree-Top Threat

The most distinctive element of the snake threat was that it could follow the Folk into what should have been their safest domain — the trees.
The image of snakes "encircling the trunks with their great shining bodies, driving me higher and higher" (001-077) captures the unique terror of an enemy that could negate the Folk's [[climbing]] advantage.

## Instinctive Fear

The narrator emphasizes that his fear of snakes predated any waking-life knowledge — he recognized them at his first circus before ever having seen one (001-082).
This is presented as evidence of racial memory: the fear of snakes was so evolutionarily ancient and survival-critical that it was stamped into the heredity of the species (002-072).

## Basking Behavior

In one daytime memory, the narrator encountered snakes basking on flat rocks in the sun (004-206). Though they did not pursue him, his inherited fear was so intense that he fled as though they were attacking.
