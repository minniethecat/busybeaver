## Overview

The Evolution of Migration is one of the central themes of Before Adam. The Folk's forced departure from the caves — triggered by the [[the-fire-migration|Fire People's expansion]] — was catastrophic in the short term: nearly the entire horde was exterminated, and the survivors fled into the unknown. But in evolutionary terms, the catastrophe was also a catalyst. The survivors who emerged from [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]] were not the same Folk who had cowered in the caves. They had been forced to innovate, to cooperate in new ways, and to imagine possibilities beyond the known.

The narrator reflects on this paradox explicitly: "the migration of the Fire People, and the consequent migration of the survivors of the Folk, set back our evolution for centuries" (011-133-011-135). But the very act of asserting that it "set back" evolution implies that migration is itself an evolutionary force — that movement across landscapes is also movement across developmental thresholds.

## Involuntary Progress

The migration was not chosen. [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] did not decide to explore the world beyond the river; they were thrown into the water by mischance and carried where the current willed. Their most consequential journey was involuntary, a surrender to forces larger than any individual could resist.

Yet within that surrender, they exercised agency: they learned to swim with the current rather than fight it, to choose which bank to land on, to recognize the salmon in the stream as a sign that the sea was near. This interplay of passive drift and active adaptation is London's model of evolutionary progress: organisms do not choose their environment, but those who adapt to its demands survive, and those who survive carry their adaptations forward into future generations.

## The Legacy

The narrator's modern self — a man living in civilization, tormented by ancestral dreams — is the product of the migration. "One thing only is certain, and that is that Big-Tooth did stamp into the cerebral constitution of one of his progeny all the impressions of his life, and stamped them in so indelibly that the hosts of intervening generations have failed to obliterate them" (018-108-018-111). The migration, in this sense, did not end when the survivors reached the sea. It continued across evolutionary time, encoded in the neural architecture of every descendant who inherited Big-Tooth's capacity to fear, to learn, and to adapt.
