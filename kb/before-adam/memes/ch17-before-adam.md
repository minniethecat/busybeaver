## CHAPTER XVII

[017-001]It was in the early fall of the following year that it happened. After

[017-002]his failure to get the Swift One, Red-Eye had taken another wife; and,

[017-003]strange to relate, she was still alive. Stranger still, they had a baby

[017-004]several months old—Red-Eye’s first child. His previous wives had never

[017-005]lived long enough to bear him children. The year had gone well for all

[017-006]of us. The weather had been exceptionally mild and food plentiful. I

[017-007]remember especially the turnips of that year. The nut crop was also

[017-008]very heavy, and the wild plums were larger and sweeter than usual.

[017-009]In short, it was a golden year. And then it happened. It was in the

[017-010]early morning, and we were surprised in our caves. In the chill gray

[017-011]light we awoke from sleep, most of us, to encounter death. The Swift

[017-012]One and I were aroused by a pandemonium of screeching and gibbering.

[017-013]Our cave was the highest of all on the cliff, and we crept to the mouth

[017-014]and peered down. The open space was filled with the Fire People. Their

[017-015]cries and yells were added to the clamor, but they had order and plan,

[017-016]while we Folk had none. Each one of us fought and acted for himself,

[017-017]and no one of us knew the extent of the calamity that was befalling us.

[017-018]By the time we got to stone-throwing, the Fire People had massed thick

[017-019]at the base of the cliff. Our first volley must have mashed some heads,

[017-020]for when they swerved back from the cliff three of their number were

[017-021]left upon the ground. These were struggling and floundering, and one

[017-022]was trying to crawl away. But we fixed them. By this time we males were

[017-023]roaring with rage, and we rained rocks upon the three men that were

[017-024]down. Several of the Fire-Men returned to drag them into safety, but

[017-025]our rocks drove the rescuers back.

[017-026]The Fire People became enraged. Also, they became cautious. In spite of

[017-027]their angry yells, they kept at a distance and sent flights of arrows

[017-028]against us. This put an end to the rock-throwing. By the time half a

[017-029]dozen of us had been killed and a score injured, the rest of us

[017-030]retreated inside our caves. I was not out of range in my lofty cave,

[017-031]but the distance was great enough to spoil effective shooting, and the

[017-032]Fire People did not waste many arrows on me. Furthermore, I was

[017-033]curious. I wanted to see. While the Swift One remained well inside the

[017-034]cave, trembling with fear and making low wailing sounds because I would

[017-035]not come in, I crouched at the entrance and watched.

[017-036]The fighting had now become intermittent. It was a sort of deadlock. We

[017-037]were in the caves, and the question with the Fire People was how to get

[017-038]us out. They did not dare come in after us, and in general we would not

[017-039]expose ourselves to their arrows. Occasionally, when one of them drew

[017-040]in close to the base of the cliff, one or another of the Folk would

[017-041]smash a rock down. In return, he would be transfixed by half a dozen

[017-042]arrows. This ruse worked well for some time, but finally the Folk no

[017-043]longer were inveigled into showing themselves. The deadlock was

[017-044]complete.

[017-045]Behind the Fire People I could see the little wizened old hunter

[017-046]directing it all. They obeyed him, and went here and there at his

[017-047]commands. Some of them went into the forest and returned with loads of

[017-048]dry wood, leaves, and grass. All the Fire People drew in closer. While

[017-049]most of them stood by with bows and arrows, ready to shoot any of the

[017-050]Folk that exposed themselves, several of the Fire-Men heaped the dry

[017-051]grass and wood at the mouths of the lower tier of caves. Out of these

[017-052]heaps they conjured the monster we feared—_FIRE_. At first, wisps of

[017-053]smoke arose and curled up the cliff. Then I could see the red-tongued

[017-054]flames darting in and out through the wood like tiny snakes. The smoke

[017-055]grew thicker and thicker, at times shrouding the whole face of the

[017-056]cliff. But I was high up and it did not bother me much, though it stung

[017-057]my eyes and I rubbed them with my knuckles.

[017-058]Old Marrow-Bone was the first to be smoked out. A light fan of air

[017-059]drifted the smoke away at the time so that I saw clearly. He broke out

[017-060]through the smoke, stepping on a burning coal and screaming with the

[017-061]sudden hurt of it, and essayed to climb up the cliff. The arrows

[017-062]showered about him. He came to a pause on a ledge, clutching a knob of

[017-063]rock for support, gasping and sneezing and shaking his head. He swayed

[017-064]back and forth. The feathered ends of a dozen arrows were sticking out

[017-065]of him. He was an old man, and he did not want to die. He swayed wider

[017-066]and wider, his knees giving under him, and as he swayed he wailed most

[017-067]plaintively. His hand released its grip and he lurched outward to the

[017-068]fall. His old bones must have been sadly broken. He groaned and strove

[017-069]feebly to rise, but a Fire-Man rushed in upon him and brained him with

[017-070]a club.

[017-071]And as it happened with Marrow-Bone, so it happened with many of the

[017-072]Folk. Unable to endure the smoke-suffocation, they rushed out to fall

[017-073]beneath the arrows. Some of the women and children remained in the

[017-074]caves to strangle to death, but the majority met death outside.

[017-075]When the Fire-Men had in this fashion cleared the first tier of caves,

[017-076]they began making arrangements to duplicate the operation on the second

[017-077]tier of caves. It was while they were climbing up with their grass and

[017-078]wood, that Red-Eye, followed by his wife, with the baby holding to her

[017-079]tightly, made a successful flight up the cliff. The Fire-Men must have

[017-080]concluded that in the interval between the smoking-out operations we

[017-081]would remain in our caves; so that they were unprepared, and their

[017-082]arrows did not begin to fly till Red-Eye and his wife were well up the

[017-083]wall. When he reached the top, he turned about and glared down at them,

[017-084]roaring and beating his chest. They arched their arrows at him, and

[017-085]though he was untouched he fled on.

[017-086]I watched a third tier smoked out, and a fourth. A few of the Folk

[017-087]escaped up the cliff, but most of them were shot off the face of it as

[017-088]they strove to climb. I remember Long-Lip. He got as far as my ledge,

[017-089]crying piteously, an arrow clear through his chest, the feathered shaft

[017-090]sticking out behind, the bone head sticking out before, shot through

[017-091]the back as he climbed. He sank down on my ledge bleeding profusely at

[017-092]the mouth.

[017-093]It was about this time that the upper tiers seemed to empty themselves

[017-094]spontaneously. Nearly all the Folk not yet smoked out stampeded up the

[017-095]cliff at the same time. This was the saving of many. The Fire People

[017-096]could not shoot arrows fast enough. They filled the air with arrows,

[017-097]and scores of the stricken Folk came tumbling down; but still there

[017-098]were a few who reached the top and got away.

[017-099]The impulse of flight was now stronger in me than curiosity. The arrows

[017-100]had ceased flying. The last of the Folk seemed gone, though there may

[017-101]have been a few still hiding in the upper caves. The Swift One and I

[017-102]started to make a scramble for the cliff-top. At sight of us a great

[017-103]cry went up from the Fire People. This was not caused by me, but by the

[017-104]Swift One. They were chattering excitedly and pointing her out to one

[017-105]another. They did not try to shoot her. Not an arrow was discharged.

[017-106]They began calling softly and coaxingly. I stopped and looked down. She

[017-107]was afraid, and whimpered and urged me on. So we went up over the top

[017-108]and plunged into the trees.

[017-109]This event has often caused me to wonder and speculate. If she were

[017-110]really of their kind, she must have been lost from them at a time when

[017-111]she was too young to remember, else would she not have been afraid of

[017-112]them. On the other hand, it may well have been that while she was their

[017-113]kind she had never been lost from them; that she had been born in the

[017-114]wild forest far from their haunts, her father maybe a renegade

[017-115]Fire-Man, her mother maybe one of my own kind, one of the Folk. But who

[017-116]shall say? These things are beyond me, and the Swift One knew no more

[017-117]about them than did I.

[017-118]We lived through a day of terror. Most of the survivors fled toward the

[017-119]blueberry swamp and took refuge in the forest in that neighborhood. And

[017-120]all day hunting parties of the Fire People ranged the forest, killing

[017-121]us wherever they found us. It must have been a deliberately executed

[017-122]plan. Increasing beyond the limits of their own territory, they had

[017-123]decided on making a conquest of ours. Sorry the conquest! We had no

[017-124]chance against them. It was slaughter, indiscriminate slaughter, for

[017-125]they spared none, killing old and young, effectively ridding the land

[017-126]of our presence.

[017-127]It was like the end of the world to us. We fled to the trees as a last

[017-128]refuge, only to be surrounded and killed, family by family. We saw much

[017-129]of this during that day, and besides, I wanted to see. The Swift One

[017-130]and I never remained long in one tree, and so escaped being surrounded.

[017-131]But there seemed no place to go. The Fire-Men were everywhere, bent on

[017-132]their task of extermination. Every way we turned we encountered them,

[017-133]and because of this we saw much of their handiwork.

[017-134]I did not see what became of my mother, but I did see the Chatterer

[017-135]shot down out of the old home-tree. And I am afraid that at the sight I

[017-136]did a bit of joyous teetering. Before I leave this portion of my

[017-137]narrative, I must tell of Red-Eye. He was caught with his wife in a

[017-138]tree down by the blueberry swamp. The Swift One and I stopped long

[017-139]enough in our flight to see. The Fire-Men were too intent upon their

[017-140]work to notice us, and, furthermore, we were well screened by the

[017-141]thicket in which we crouched.

[017-142]Fully a score of the hunters were under the tree, discharging arrows

[017-143]into it. They always picked up their arrows when they fell back to

[017-144]earth. I could not see Red-Eye, but I could hear him howling from

[017-145]somewhere in the tree.

[017-146]After a short interval his howling grew muffled. He must have crawled

[017-147]into a hollow in the trunk. But his wife did not win this shelter. An

[017-148]arrow brought her to the ground. She was severely hurt, for she made no

[017-149]effort to get away. She crouched in a sheltering way over her baby

[017-150](which clung tightly to her), and made pleading signs and sounds to the

[017-151]Fire-Men. They gathered about her and laughed at her—even as Lop-Ear

[017-152]and I had laughed at the old Tree-Man. And even as we had poked him

[017-153]with twigs and sticks, so did the Fire-Men with Red-Eye’s wife. They

[017-154]poked her with the ends of their bows, and prodded her in the ribs. But

[017-155]she was poor fun. She would not fight. Nor, for that matter, would she

[017-156]get angry. She continued to crouch over her baby and to plead. One of

[017-157]the Fire-Men stepped close to her. In his hand was a club. She saw and

[017-158]understood, but she made only the pleading sounds until the blow fell.

[017-159]Red-Eye, in the hollow of the trunk, was safe from their arrows. They

[017-160]stood together and debated for a while, then one of them climbed into

[017-161]the tree. What happened up there I could not tell, but I heard him yell

[017-162]and saw the excitement of those that remained beneath. After several

[017-163]minutes his body crashed down to the ground. He did not move. They

[017-164]looked at him and raised his head, but it fell back limply when they

[017-165]let go. Red-Eye had accounted for himself.

[017-166]They were very angry. There was an opening into the trunk close to the

[017-167]ground. They gathered wood and grass and built a fire. The Swift One

[017-168]and I, our arms around each other, waited and watched in the thicket.

[017-169]Sometimes they threw upon the fire green branches with many leaves,

[017-170]whereupon the smoke became very thick.

[017-171]We saw them suddenly swerve back from the tree. They were not quick

[017-172]enough. Red-Eye’s flying body landed in the midst of them.

[017-173]He was in a frightful rage, smashing about with his long arms right and

[017-174]left. He pulled the face off one of them, literally pulled it off with

[017-175]those gnarly fingers of his and those tremendous muscles. He bit

[017-176]another through the neck. The Fire-Men fell back with wild fierce

[017-177]yells, then rushed upon him. He managed to get hold of a club and began

[017-178]crushing heads like eggshells. He was too much for them, and they were

[017-179]compelled to fall back again. This was his chance, and he turned his

[017-180]back upon them and ran for it, still howling wrathfully. A few arrows

[017-181]sped after him, but he plunged into a thicket and was gone.

[017-182]The Swift One and I crept quietly away, only to run foul of another

[017-183]party of Fire-Men. They chased us into the blueberry swamp, but we knew

[017-184]the tree-paths across the farther morasses where they could not follow

[017-185]on the ground, and so we escaped. We came out on the other side into a

[017-186]narrow strip of forest that separated the blueberry swamp from the

[017-187]great swamp that extended westward. Here we met Lop-Ear. How he had

[017-188]escaped I cannot imagine, unless he had not slept the preceding night

[017-189]at the caves.

[017-190]Here, in the strip of forest, we might have built tree-shelters and

[017-191]settled down; but the Fire People were performing their work of

[017-192]extermination thoroughly. In the afternoon, Hair-Face and his wife fled

[017-193]out from among the trees to the east, passed us, and were gone. They

[017-194]fled silently and swiftly, with alarm in their faces. In the direction

[017-195]from which they had come we heard the cries and yells of the hunters,

[017-196]and the screeching of some one of the Folk. The Fire People had found

[017-197]their way across the swamp.

[017-198]The Swift One, Lop-Ear, and I followed on the heels of Hair-Face and

[017-199]his wife. When we came to the edge of the great swamp, we stopped. We

[017-200]did not know its paths. It was outside our territory, and it had been

[017-201]always avoided by the Folk. None had ever gone into it—at least, to

[017-202]return. In our minds it represented mystery and fear, the terrible

[017-203]unknown. As I say, we stopped at the edge of it. We were afraid. The

[017-204]cries of the Fire-Men were drawing nearer. We looked at one another.

[017-205]Hair-Face ran out on the quaking morass and gained the firmer footing

[017-206]of a grass-hummock a dozen yards away. His wife did not follow. She

[017-207]tried to, but shrank back from the treacherous surface and cowered

[017-208]down.

[017-209]The Swift One did not wait for me, nor did she pause till she had

[017-210]passed beyond Hair-Face a hundred yards and gained a much larger

[017-211]hummock. By the time Lop-Ear and I had caught up with her, the Fire-Men

[017-212]appeared among the trees. Hair-Face’s wife, driven by them into panic

[017-213]terror, dashed after us. But she ran blindly, without caution, and

[017-214]broke through the crust. We turned and watched, and saw them shoot her

[017-215]with arrows as she sank down in the mud. The arrows began falling about

[017-216]us. Hair-Face had now joined us, and the four of us plunged on, we knew

[017-217]not whither, deeper and deeper into the swamp.
