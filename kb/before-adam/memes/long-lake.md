## Overview

Long Lake was one of two mountain lakes — the other being [[Far Lake]] — that the Folk encountered during their arduous passage across [[the-mountain|the mountains]] between the [[the-river|River]] country and the lands beyond (012-010). It was a place of thin sustenance: [[the-mountain|the mountains]] offered little food, and the Folk were weakened by hunger when they reached its shore.

> Long Lake and Far Lake, and the calf we caught sleeping in the thicket. (012-010)

The lake was not a destination but a waypoint — a feature of the landscape that marked the Folk's progress through difficult country. What made it memorable was not the lake itself but what happened beside it: the discovery of a part-grown calf sleeping in a thicket on its upper shore, a rare and vital piece of game in the lean mountain passage.

## Geographic Context

Long Lake lay in the mountainous country between the territory of the [[the-cliffs|cliffs]] and the gentler lands beyond. The Folk reached the lake after leaving the [[the-river|River]] and working westward (012-014), then turning north and skirting the marshes (012-016). Upon arriving, they spent time around the upper end of the lake, where food was relatively plentiful (012-017).

> at what I have called Long Lake. We spent some time around its upper end, where we found food in plenty (012-017)

The lake was close enough to [[Far Lake]] that the two were spoken of together in [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s memory (012-010). Between them lay [[the-mountain|the mountains]] where the Folk endured some of the worst hunger of the migration — hunger that [[big-tooth|the narrator]] recalled vividly, tied to the memory of the two lakes (012-009).

The [[Tree People]] dwelt in the forest between Long Lake and [[the-mountain|the mountains]], and it was they who drove the Folk further into the high country (012-011-012-013).

## Role in the Narrative

Long Lake appears in a single chapter — the migration chapter (ch12) — and serves as a geographic marker in the Folk's long journey away from their ancestral territory. It is not itself a place of action, but a place named in passing as [[big-tooth|the narrator]] recounts the grim sequence of the mountain crossing.

The lake's narrative function is twofold: it anchors the migration route in real geography, and it provides the setting for the calf-hunting episode — one of the few moments of nourishment in an otherwise hungry stretch of the journey. The memory of hunger "on [[the-mountain|the mountains]] between Long Lake and [[far-lake|Far Lake]]" (012-009) is one [[big-tooth|the narrator]] singles out as particularly memorable.

## Inhabitants and Usage

Long Lake was not permanently inhabited by any group. The Folk passed through its vicinity during the migration, staying at the upper end where food was more abundant (012-017). The [[Tree People]] dwelt in the forest between the lake and [[the-mountain|the mountains]] but did not occupy the lakeshore itself (012-011). The lake's primary value to the Folk was as a hunting ground — [[the-thicket|the thickets]] on its shore harbored game at a time when [[the-mountain|the mountains]] offered little else.
