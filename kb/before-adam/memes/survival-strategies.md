## Overview

The Survival Strategies of the Folk constituted a toolkit of behaviors that compensated for their physical weakness in a world of much stronger predators. They could not outrun [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], could not outfight [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]], and could not outswim the [[the-river|river]]. What they could do was climb, throw stones, build shelters, and — crucially — learn from experience and from each other.

These strategies were not consciously designed or deliberately taught. They emerged from the interplay of instinct, imitation, and the selective pressure of the Pleistocene environment. Each strategy addressed a specific threat, and together they allowed the Folk to survive in a world that was, by any objective measure, trying to kill them.

## The Toolkit

The Folk's survival toolkit included five principal strategies. **[[climbing|Climbing]]** was the most fundamental — their bodies were adapted for arboreal life, and the ability to retreat upward into trees or cliffs was their primary defense against ground-based predators. **Stone-throwing** was their only ranged weapon, effective from height against attackers below. **Shelter-building** allowed them to survive nights away from the caves, constructing temporary platforms in tree branches. **Collective alarm-calling** warned the group of approaching danger, a proto-linguistic behavior that functioned as an early warning system. **Imitation** was their primary learning mechanism, allowing innovations like [[marrow-bone-invention|gourd water-storage]] to spread through the horde without formal instruction.

## The Limits

Each strategy had its limits. Climbing offered no protection against arboreal threats like the [[tree-people|Tree People]]. Stone-throwing was useless against [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s that could kill from a hundred paces. Shelter-building provided only temporary refuge. Collective alarm-calling could warn of danger but could not coordinate defense. And imitation, as the [[the-forest-fire|forest fire]] demonstrated, could spread catastrophe as easily as innovation.

The Folk's survival was never guaranteed. It was the product of constant vigilance, continuous adaptation, and — the narrator suggests — a measure of luck. The strategies that worked were preserved and transmitted; the strategies that failed died with those who employed them. This cruel culling was, in London's vision, the engine of evolutionary progress.
