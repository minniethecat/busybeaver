# Marrow-Bone

**Marrow-Bone** (also called **Old Marrow-Bone**) was the oldest member of the [[fire-people|Folk]] during Big-Tooth's generation. He was the only member of the [[fire-people|horde]] who reached old age — a notable exception in a world where "death by violence was the common way of death."[^1] Marrow-Bone is remembered chiefly as the inventor of gourd-based water storage, one of the Folk's earliest technological innovations.

[^1]: PN=017-128 — Marrow-Bone was the only old member of the horde; the Folk never lived to old age.

## Role in the Narrative

Marrow-Bone appears in the narrative as a figure of frailty and innovation. His great age, which made hunting and active foraging impossible, drove him to adapt. He responded to the "prod of some vague and inexpressible desire" that Big-Tooth identifies as the impulse behind early human invention — a worried, plaintive look that arose when the Folk encountered something beyond their understanding.[^2]

The first encounter with ice at the [[drinking-place|drinking-place]] is described through Marrow-Bone's eyes: he examined it with that characteristic worried, plaintive look shared by all the Folk when confronted with the unknown.[^2]

His death comes during the [[fire-discovery|Fire People]] attack on the caves. He was the first to be smoked out, breaking through the smoke and stepping on a burning coal. He attempted to climb the cliff but was brought down by the attackers' arrows.[^3]

> Old Marrow-Bone was the first to be smoked out. A light fan of air drifted the smoke away at the time so that I saw clearly. He broke out through the smoke, stepping on a burning coal and screaming with the sudden hurt of it, and essayed to climb up the cliff. The arrows showered about him.[^3]

## The Invention of Gourd Water-Storage

Marrow-Bone's most significant contribution to the Folk was the innovation of using [[gourd|gourds]] to store water:

> The honor was due to old Marrow-Bone, and it is fair to assume that it was the necessity of his great age that brought about the innovation.[^4]

He kept a supply of drinking water in his cave — which belonged to his son, the Hairless One, who permitted him to occupy a corner of it. The Folk, driven by their strong imitative instinct, adopted the practice one by one until gourd water-storage became universal.[^4] This practice later expanded: women began carrying berries, nuts, and roots in gourds, an evolutionary step that Big-Tooth traces directly back to Marrow-Bone's original innovation.[^5]

When Marrow-Bone had sick spells and could not leave the cave, his son the Hairless One filled his gourd, later delegating the task to Long-Lip. This arrangement — where the women and larger children carried water for the men — became institutionalized practice among the Folk.[^6]

## Relationships

Marrow-Bone is the patriarch of a notable lineage within the horde:
- His son is **the Hairless One**, who owned the cave where Marrow-Bone resided.
- His grandson is **Long-Lip**, who became his water-carrier.
- His granddaughter is **the Singing One**, whom [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] later attempted to claim.

During a confrontation, Red-Eye grabbed the old man by the neck. Marrow-Bone "shivered terribly, bowed his head and covered his face with his crossed arms" in a display of submission. Red-Eye slammed him face-down but spared his life — a rare act of mercy from the erratic tyrant.[^7]

## Traits and Characteristics

Marrow-Bone was physically frail — hobbling, tottering, shivering — the only member of the Folk to grow truly old. Big-Tooth draws a striking comparison between Marrow-Bone and a gardener's father in his modern life: "very old, very wrinkled and withered... he looked and acted like old Marrow-Bone."[^1] Yet within his frailty was the seed of innovation: necessity forced him to invent, and imitation spread his invention through the horde.

## Others' View of Marrow-Bone

Marrow-Bone occupied a unique position in the horde's social imagination: he was at once a figure of pity, a source of innovation, and a living anomaly. No one else among the Folk reached old age, and his mere existence — tottering and shivering, dependent on his son's cave and his grandson's water-carrying — was a standing challenge to the normal order of violent, early death. The horde tolerated and supported him, a fact that suggests an embryonic social contract: the elderly, if they could contribute something of value, would be cared for.[^1]

His invention of gourd water-storage earned him a different kind of regard. Big-Tooth explicitly credits Marrow-Bone with the "honor" of the innovation, and traces a direct line from the old man's gourd to the later practice of storing berries, nuts, and roots.[^4] The Folk's imitative instinct meant that Marrow-Bone's private necessity became public practice — and in this sense, he was the horde's first inventor, the prototype of the human capacity to turn individual need into collective advancement.

Yet Marrow-Bone was also seen as comically pitiable. His worried, plaintive look — the expression he wore when confronted with ice, or any other thing beyond his understanding — characterized not just him but the entire horde's response to the unknown.[^2] Big-Tooth's modern-life comparison of Marrow-Bone to a gardener's withered father draws the old man across the evolutionary divide and makes him recognizable: he is every frail elder, in every age, whose body has outlived its strength but whose mind continues to ask questions.

Red-Eye's treatment of Marrow-Bone reveals another layer of the horde's view. When Red-Eye seized the old man by the neck, Marrow-Bone's shivering submission was a display so abject that even Red-Eye — erratic, unpredictable, spiteful — released him unharmed. The horde witnessed this: the old man's frailty was a kind of armor, a weapon of the weak that even the strongest could not easily pierce.[^7]
