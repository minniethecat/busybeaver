## Overview

The Fire People Settlement was the peninsula stronghold of the [[fire-people|Fire People]] — a "splendid strategic selection" protected on three sides by the curving river (013-081-013-083). Its natural defenses made it nearly impregnable to ground-based predators, and its fertile territory supported population growth that would eventually drive the Fire People's expansion into Folk lands.

[[big-tooth|The narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] stumbled upon the settlement after the [[the-forest-fire|forest fire]] drove them westward. From a concealed vantage point, they observed the settlement's layout: caves opening onto an open space that sloped down to the river, a pattern that eerily mirrored their own home. But where the Folk's caves were crude and undecorated, the Fire People's showed signs of a more advanced material culture — organized fire-pits, evidence of tool-making, and the presence of domesticated dogs.

## Strategic Geography

The peninsula's geography was its greatest asset. The river curved around it on three sides, creating a natural moat that no ground predator could cross. The fourth side — the land approach — was narrow enough to be easily defended. Within this protected space, the Fire People had built what amounted to a fortress: a settlement that could sustain a growing population while remaining defensible against external threats.

The strategic genius of the location was not lost on the narrator, who recognized it as fundamentally superior to the Folk's own cave territory. The Folk's caves were defensible from above but vulnerable from below; the Fire People's peninsula was defensible from every direction.

## The Encounter

The narrator and Lop-Ear observed the settlement from hiding, watching the Fire People go about their daily lives: women and children moving between caves, men returning from hunting, dogs lounging near the fire-pits. This was the first time either of them had seen the Fire People in their domestic context rather than as raiders or scouts.

The encounter was pivotal. From this observation, the two Folk stole a catamaran — a floating platform of logs lashed together — that they used to escape down the river, laughing at the Fire People as the current carried them away (013-097-013-101). The theft was both a practical escape and a symbolic acquisition: the first Fire People technology that the Folk successfully appropriated and used to their own advantage.
