The impulse toward cooperation is one of the deepest tensions in the life of the Folk — a gregarious instinct that draws them together for united action, yet repeatedly fails in the absence of language. Throughout the narrative, the Folk are caught between their need for collective effort and their inability to articulate it, a gap that defines both their vulnerability and the early stirrings of art.

## The Gregarious Impulse

The drive toward cooperative action is described as an instinct — "the prod of gregarious instinct, the drawing together as though for united action, the impulse toward cooperation" [014-085]. This impulse is felt by the entire horde, a shared recognition that united action is necessary. Yet it remains inchoate: "in dim ways this need for united action was impressed upon us. But there was no way to achieve it because there was no way to express it" [014-085].

> We were vaguely thinking thoughts for which there were no thought-symbols. These thought-symbols were yet to be slowly and painfully invented.
> — [014-086]

## The Language Barrier

The central obstacle to cooperation is the absence of speech. When the Hairless One attempts to express the cooperative impulse that stirs within him, "his noises became gibberish" [014-087]. The narrator recognizes that the Folk "lacked a vocabulary" [014-085] — a direct link between linguistic development and social organization. Without words to coordinate, the collective rage against Red-Eye dissipates into individual noise.

This limitation has practical consequences. The Folk are described as "incapable of a cooperative effort strong enough to kill him or cast him out" [009-050] and "incapable of the collective action necessary to punish him" [009-050]. Red-Eye's tyranny persists not because the Folk accept it, but because they cannot coordinate resistance.

## The Hee-Hee Council as Proto-Cooperation

The hee-hee council represents the Folk's most successful form of collective action — not through planning or deliberation, but through rhythm. When the rage against Red-Eye peaks, someone seizes a stick and begins pounding a log: "In a moment he had struck a rhythm. Unconsciously, our yells and exclamations yielded to this rhythm" [014-088].

> These hee-hee councils splendidly illustrate the inconsecutiveness and inconsequentiality of the Folk. Here were we, drawn together by mutual rage and the impulse toward cooperation, led off into forgetfulness by the establishment of a rude rhythm.
> — [014-089]

The hee-hee council is a form of cooperation that works precisely because it bypasses language. Rhythm provides a shared structure that words cannot yet provide. The narrator describes it as "an adumbration of the councils of primitive man, and of the great national assemblies and international conventions of latter-day man" [014-090].

## Cooperation as an Evolutionary Marker

The ability to cooperate serves as a dividing line between groups. The Fire People are described as more advanced precisely because "they had speech that enabled them more effectively to reason, and in addition they understood cooperation" [018-117]. Their capacity for coordinated action — including warfare — makes them "far more terrible than Saber-Tooth himself" [018-117]. Cooperation, in this framing, is both a survival advantage and a source of existential threat.

## The Tension Between Individual and Group

The hee-hee council also reveals the limits of Folk cooperation. Between rhythms, "pandemonium reigned" [014-091]. Rival rhythms competed, "each rhythm backed by a group that strove ardently to drown out the other rhythms" [014-091]. In the intervals, each individual "chattered, cut up, hooted, screeched, and danced, himself sufficient unto himself" [014-092]. The pull toward collective action and the pull toward individual expression exist in constant tension — a tension that the Folk have not yet resolved.
