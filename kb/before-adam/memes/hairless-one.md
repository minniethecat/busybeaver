# Hairless One

The **Hairless One** is a member of the [[fire-people|Folk]], the son of [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]] and the father of [[long-lip|Long-Lip]] and [[singing-one|the Singing One]]. He owned the cave where his elderly father Marrow-Bone resided, and his family line represents a notable patriarchal lineage within the horde.

## Role in the Narrative

The Hairless One appears primarily in the context of filial duty and the emergence of specialized labor roles. When Marrow-Bone had sick spells and was unable to fetch water himself, the Hairless One filled his father's [[gourd]] and brought it to the cave.[^1] Later, he delegated this task to his son Long-Lip, establishing a pattern that became institutionalized among the Folk — a division of labor where water-carrying fell to women and older children.[^2]

This delegation chain — from Marrow-Bone to Hairless One to Long-Lip — is the first documented instance of task inheritance within the horde, marking an early form of family-based role transmission.

## The Red-Eye Confrontation

When [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] seized the elderly Marrow-Bone by the neck during one of his violent rampages, the Hairless One was present in the open space. Big-Tooth describes the scene:

> I saw the Hairless One, out in the open space, beating his chest and bristling, but afraid to come forward.[^3]

His impulse to defend his father warred with his fear of Red-Eye, and fear won — a moment that illustrates the paralyzing terror Red-Eye's tyranny held over the entire horde. In a rare act of mercy, Red-Eye released Marrow-Bone unharmed.

## The Council Against Red-Eye

The Hairless One participated in the gibbering council where the Folk attempted to coordinate action against Red-Eye. He "began to chatter loudly," expressing anger and the desire to harm Red-Eye — emotions the others could understand. But when he tried to express the cooperative impulse within him, "his noises became gibberish,"[^4] reflecting the severe limitations of the Folk's proto-language, which could convey emotion but not collaborative planning.

## Death

The Hairless One died abruptly in an ambush by the [[fire-people|Fire People]]. While making breakfast alongside his father Marrow-Bone, his son Long-Lip, Big-Tooth, and Big-Tooth's sister, arrows struck without warning:

> On the sudden, both the Hairless One and my sister sprang and screamed. At the same instant I heard the thud of the arrows that transfixed them.[^5]

His death exemplifies the violent mortality that defined life among the Folk — "abruptly and brutally, in the full possession of their faculties."[^6]

## Others' View of the Hairless One

The Hairless One was seen by his contemporaries as a responsible, dutiful son — a man who honored filial obligation even when it meant extra labor. His willingness to fill Marrow-Bone's gourd during sick spells, and his foresight in delegating the task to Long-Lip, marked him in the horde's collective memory as a transitional figure: part of the first generation to institutionalize specialized roles within a family line. Big-Tooth, the narrator, remembers him with a measure of respect, noting that his delegation chain from father to son established a precedent that outlived him.[^2]

Yet the Hairless One was also marked by his fear. During Red-Eye's attack on Marrow-Bone, the horde saw a man torn between filial courage and self-preservation — and self-preservation won. Big-Tooth's account records no censure for this; the terror Red-Eye inspired was understood by all. If anything, the Hairless One's visible conflict — beating his chest while afraid to advance — was a mirror in which the rest of the horde saw their own paralysis.[^3]

At the gibbering council, the Hairless One's attempt to articulate cooperation earned him a different kind of recognition. The others understood his anger; they could not grasp his strategic intent. His failure was linguistic, not moral — a reminder that the Folk's proto-language set hard limits on collective planning. In this failure, the Hairless One embodied the horde's central tragedy: the capacity for collective action existed as an impulse long before the means to coordinate it.[^4]

## Family Line

The Hairless One stands at the center of a three-generation lineage within the horde:
- Father: [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]], the oldest member of the Folk
- Son: [[long-lip|Long-Lip]], who inherited the water-carrying task
- Daughter: [[singing-one|the Singing One]], whom Red-Eye attempted to claim
