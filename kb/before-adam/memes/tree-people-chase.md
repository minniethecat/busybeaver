## Overview

The Tree People Chase was a hours-long pursuit of [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] by the [[tree-people|Tree People]] through the forest between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[the-mountain|the mountains]]. The chase was a direct consequence of the two Folk's casual cruelty toward an old Tree-Man — an episode that reveals as much about [[the-horde|the Folk]]'s own capacity for mindless aggression as about the Tree People's ferocity (012-032-012-094).

The pursuit drove the two travelers out of the forest and into the bleak, inhospitable mountains, where they endured a miserable night before continuing their westward journey. It marked a turning point in their travels — the moment when they learned that the world beyond the caves was not empty but populated by other peoples with their own territories, their own loyalties, and their own capacity for violence.

## The Provocation

The chase began not with aggression from the Tree People but with an act of cruelty by [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] themselves. They came upon a "little withered, dried-up old fellow, wrinkled-faced and bleary-eyed and tottery" sitting at the foot of his tree (012-032-012-034). Declaring him "legitimate prey" — "in our world there was no sympathy between the kinds" (012-034-012-035) — the two men rushed him.

What followed was an extended episode of casual sadism. The narrator and Lop-Ear pinched the old man, pulled his hair, tweaked his ears, and poked twigs into him, laughing until tears streamed down their faces (012-046-012-047). The old Tree-Man's "futile anger was most absurd" — he made "woeful faces in place of the ferocious ones he intended, grinding his worn teeth together, beating his meagre chest with feeble fists" (012-050-012-054). They kept him from climbing his tree, pulling him back again and again until he surrendered and simply sat weeping (012-058-012-060).

The old man's screams, however, were heard. From deeper in the forest came the answering call: "Goëk! Goëk!"

## The Tribal Response

The Tree People's response was immediate, coordinated, and relentless. From all directions in the forest came answering calls — "several of them, and from very far off we could hear a big, bass 'Goëk! Goëk! Goëk!'" (012-072-012-076). The "Whoo-whoo!" cry rose all around them, indicating a communication system capable of rallying the entire tribe across distance.

The chase that followed lasted for hours. The Tree People "raced us through the trees, the whole tribe of them, and nearly caught us" (012-079-012-080). In the trees, they were faster — their arboreal adaptation gave them a decisive advantage. "They were truly the Tree People, and while they out-climbed us we out-footed them on the ground" (012-060-012-061).

Forced to take to the ground, Big-Tooth and Lop-Ear ran through glades that "always ended in more thick forest" (012-065). More than once it was "nip and tuck" — a near miss that could have ended in capture or death (012-084-012-085).

## Into the Mountains

The chase drove them steadily northward, out of the forest and onto "the grassy flanks of the mountains" (012-091-012-092). Here, at the forest's edge, the Tree People finally gave up and returned to their trees (012-093-012-094). But they did not simply let their quarry go — they guarded the boundary.

Three times that afternoon, Big-Tooth and Lop-Ear tried to regain the woods. Each time, "the Tree People were lying in wait, and they drove us back" (012-097-012-098). This persistent territorial defense forced the two travelers to spend a miserable night in a dwarf tree "no larger than a bush" (012-099), cold and exposed in a landscape they had no experience navigating.

The chase marked the end of their forest journey and the beginning of their passage through the mountains — a direct chain of consequence from casual cruelty to territorial retaliation to forced migration.
