## Overview

The Chatterer's Abandonment was the event that ended [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]]'s childhood and thrust him into independent survival. Seizing the moment when Big-Tooth's mother was away at the [[blueberry-swamp|blueberry swamp]], the [[chatterer|Chatterer]] returned alone to the family nest, "roaring with self-induced rage," and deliberately drove the young boy out of the tree (004-128).

Big-Tooth fell from the branches but survived. The fall marked the end of his life as a dependent child and the beginning of his existence as a solitary forager, navigating the forest without the protection of a parent. The abandonment was, in the narrator's retrospective telling, both a trauma and a catalyst — the cruelty that forced him to become something more than a frightened child.

## The Attack

The [[chatterer|Chatterer]]'s attack was characteristically theatrical. He returned to the nest alone, knowing that Big-Tooth's mother — his only protector — was absent. He worked himself into a rage, "roaring" as he approached the tree, his anger as much performance as genuine emotion.

Big-Tooth, alone and terrified, climbed out onto the thin twigs and leaves at the end of a branch — the only place where the Chatterer's greater weight prevented pursuit. "Greater always than any anger he ever worked up was his caution" (004-076). The Chatterer, unwilling to risk a fall himself, celebrated from the safety of the nest as the boy lost his grip and tumbled through the branches to the ground.

## The Aftermath

The fall did not kill Big-Tooth — the forest floor was soft with accumulated leaves, and the branches had slowed his descent. But the abandonment was absolute. When Big-Tooth's mother returned, the Chatterer was still in possession of the nest, and the boy — bruised, frightened, and alone — had already begun his life in the trees below.

The abandonment was, in one sense, the making of Big-Tooth. Forced to fend for himself, he learned to find food, to build [[shelter|tree-shelters]], and to navigate the forest's dangers without adult protection. The skills that would later enable him to survive the migration, cross the river, and escape the massacre were all first developed in the aftermath of the Chatterer's cruelty. The stepfather who tried to kill him had, paradoxically, given him the gift of independence.
