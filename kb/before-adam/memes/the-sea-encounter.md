## Overview

The Sea Encounter was the end of the road. After the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People massacre]] at the caves, the survivors — no more than a handful — fled into [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]], a vast everglade of mud, snakes, and transient floods. For weeks they wandered through that "dank and soggy wilderness" (018-006), emerging at last where [[the-river|the river]] broke through a low range of hills. Beyond, toward the west, "the ocean boomed on the bar that lay across [[the-river|the river]]'s mouth" (018-010-018-011). Here, in sandstone caves, what remained of the Folk settled into their final abiding-place.

> Beyond, toward the west, the ocean boomed on the bar that lay across the river's mouth. And here, in the caves, we settled down in our abiding-place by the sea. (018-010-018-012)

The sea had been a distant presence in the narrative long before the survivors reached it. The narrator, looking back from his modern vantage, had already named it as the place where the Folk's story ended: "we, a branch of lower life budding toward the human, were nipped short off and perished down by the roaring surf where [[the-river|the river]] entered the sea" (011-143).
## Context and Causes

The arrival at the sea was not chosen — it was the only direction left. The [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People massacre]] had killed most of the Folk. The hunting parties that followed drove survivors into [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]], a territory the Folk had always avoided (018-013-018-014). The swamp was "outside our territory, and it had been always avoided by the Folk. None had ever gone into it — at least, to return" (018-012-018-014).

> In our minds it represented mystery and fear, the terrible unknown. (018-013)

Driven by the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] behind them, the survivors plunged deeper and deeper into the morass (017-046-017-047). The swamp crossing was a filtering — only about thirty of the Folk emerged alive, "more dead than alive, mere perambulating skeletons" (018-018-018-019). No children survived the journey (018-020).

## Narrative Account

### The Swamp Crossing

The weeks in [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]] were a protracted nightmare. The narrator's memories take "the form of nightmare" — endless wandering "through a dank and soggy wilderness, where poisonous snakes struck at us, and animals roared around us, and the mud quaked under us and sucked at our heels" (018-005-018-008). Storms and risings of water flooded the low-lying lands, keeping the survivors prisoner in the trees for days (018-010-018-011).

### Arrival

The survivors emerged where "a low range of hills ran down to the bank of [[the-river|the river]]" (018-017). The river, like themselves, had broken through [[the-great-swamp|the swamp]]. On the south bank, where [[the-river|the river]] had cut through the hills, they found sandstone caves (018-021). Thirty survivors gathered there — and then no more came from [[the-great-swamp|the swamp]] (018-018-018-020).

### Life by the Sea

The coastal settlement was a slow death. "It was not a happy abiding-place. The air was raw and chill, and we suffered continually from coughing and colds" (018-023-018-024). The radical dietary shift to seafood — mussels, abalones, clams, rock-oysters, ocean-crabs, and seaweed — caused stomach troubles. "None of us ever waxed fat. We were all lean and dyspeptic-looking" (018-028).

It was harvesting abalones that killed [[Lop-Ear]]. One closed upon his fingers at low tide, and the returning flood drowned him (018-029-018-030). His body was found the next day — a lesson none of the others forgot (018-030-018-031).

### The Fire People's Dugout

One day, the [[Fire People]] appeared again — this time not on foot but in a rude dugout paddled by three men, one of them the wizened old hunter (018-035-018-036). They landed on the beach, examined the caves, and departed. The [[Swift-One]] was badly scared (018-037-018-038). The next morning she took their child and, by sharp cries and gestures, started [[big-tooth|the narrator]] on their second flight — away from the sea, southward (018-039).

Eight of the Folk remained behind. "There was no hope for them" (018-040). The coastal climate was beyond the Folk's constitution.
## Consequences

The Sea Encounter marked the effective end of the Folk as a people. The narrator, [[Swift-One]], and their child fled south, crossing mountains and skirting [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]]. The eight who stayed behind "must soon have perished" (018-040). The sea was not a new beginning but a last stand — and it was not enough.
