## CHAPTER XI

[011-001]It must be remembered that the description I have just given of the

[011-002]Swift One is not the description that would have been given by

[011-003]Big-Tooth, my other self of my dreams, my prehistoric ancestor. It is

[011-004]by the medium of my dreams that I, the modern man, look through the

[011-005]eyes of Big-Tooth and see.

[011-006]And so it is with much that I narrate of the events of that far-off

[011-007]time. There is a duality about my impressions that is too confusing to

[011-008]inflict upon my readers. I shall merely pause here in my narrative to

[011-009]indicate this duality, this perplexing mixing of personality. It is I,

[011-010]the modern, who look back across the centuries and weigh and analyze

[011-011]the emotions and motives of Big-Tooth, my other self. He did not bother

[011-012]to weigh and analyze. He was simplicity itself. He just lived events,

[011-013]without ever pondering why he lived them in his particular and often

[011-014]erratic way.

[011-015]As I, my real self, grew older, I entered more and more into the

[011-016]substance of my dreams. One may dream, and even in the midst of the

[011-017]dream be aware that he is dreaming, and if the dream be bad, comfort

[011-018]himself with the thought that it is only a dream. This is a common

[011-019]experience with all of us. And so it was that I, the modern, often

[011-020]entered into my dreaming, and in the consequent strange dual

[011-021]personality was both actor and spectator. And right often have I, the

[011-022]modern, been perturbed and vexed by the foolishness, illogic,

[011-023]obtuseness, and general all-round stupendous stupidity of myself, the

[011-024]primitive.

[011-025]And one thing more, before I end this digression. Have you ever dreamed

[011-026]that you dreamed? Dogs dream, horses dream, all animals dream. In

[011-027]Big-Tooth’s day the half-men dreamed, and when the dreams were bad they

[011-028]howled in their sleep. Now I, the modern, have lain down with Big-Tooth

[011-029]and dreamed his dreams.

[011-030]This is getting almost beyond the grip of the intellect, I know; but I

[011-031]do know that I have done this thing. And let me tell you that the

[011-032]flying and crawling dreams of Big-Tooth were as vivid to him as the

[011-033]falling-through-space dream is to you.

[011-034]For Big-Tooth also had an other-self, and when he slept that other-self

[011-035]dreamed back into the past, back to the winged reptiles and the clash

[011-036]and the onset of dragons, and beyond that to the scurrying, rodent-like

[011-037]life of the tiny mammals, and far remoter still, to the shore-slime of

[011-038]the primeval sea. I cannot, I dare not, say more. It is all too vague

[011-039]and complicated and awful. I can only hint of those vast and terrific

[011-040]vistas through which I have peered hazily at the progression of life,

[011-041]not upward from the ape to man, but upward from the worm.

[011-042]And now to return to my tale. I, Big-Tooth, knew not the Swift One as a

[011-043]creature of finer facial and bodily symmetry, with long-lashed eyes and

[011-044]a bridge to her nose and down-opening nostrils that made toward beauty.

[011-045]I knew her only as the mild-eyed young female who made soft sounds and

[011-046]did not fight. I liked to play with her, I knew not why, to seek food

[011-047]in her company, and to go bird-nesting with her. And I must confess she

[011-048]taught me things about tree-climbing. She was very wise, very strong,

[011-049]and no clinging skirts impeded her movements.

[011-050]It was about this time that a slight defection arose on the part of

[011-051]Lop-Ear. He got into the habit of wandering off in the direction of the

[011-052]tree where my mother lived. He had taken a liking to my vicious sister,

[011-053]and the Chatterer had come to tolerate him. Also, there were several

[011-054]other young people, progeny of the monogamic couples that lived in the

[011-055]neighborhood, and Lop-Ear played with these young people.

[011-056]I could never get the Swift One to join with them. Whenever I visited

[011-057]them she dropped behind and disappeared. I remember once making a

[011-058]strong effort to persuade her. But she cast backward, anxious glances,

[011-059]then retreated, calling to me from a tree. So it was that I did not

[011-060]make a practice of accompanying Lop-Ear when he went to visit his new

[011-061]friends. The Swift One and I were good comrades, but, try as I would, I

[011-062]could never find her tree-shelter. Undoubtedly, had nothing happened,

[011-063]we would have soon mated, for our liking was mutual; but the something

[011-064]did happen.

[011-065]One morning, the Swift One not having put in an appearance, Lop-Ear and

[011-066]I were down at the mouth of the slough playing on the logs. We had

[011-067]scarcely got out on the water, when we were startled by a roar of rage.

[011-068]It was Red-Eye. He was crouching on the edge of the timber jam and

[011-069]glowering his hatred at us. We were badly frightened, for here was no

[011-070]narrow-mouthed cave for refuge. But the twenty feet of water that

[011-071]intervened gave us temporary safety, and we plucked up courage.

[011-072]Red-Eye stood up erect and began beating his hairy chest with his fist.

[011-073]Our two logs were side by side, and we sat on them and laughed at him.

[011-074]At first our laughter was half-hearted, tinged with fear, but as we

[011-075]became convinced of his impotence we waxed uproarious. He raged and

[011-076]raged at us, and ground his teeth in helpless fury. And in our fancied

[011-077]security we mocked and mocked him. We were ever short-sighted, we Folk.

[011-078]Red-Eye abruptly ceased his breast-beating and tooth-grinding, and ran

[011-079]across the timber-jam to the shore. And just as abruptly our merriment

[011-080]gave way to consternation. It was not Red-Eye’s way to forego revenge

[011-081]so easily. We waited in fear and trembling for whatever was to happen.

[011-082]It never struck us to paddle away. He came back with great leaps across

[011-083]the jam, one huge hand filled with round, water-washed pebbles. I am

[011-084]glad that he was unable to find larger missiles, say stones weighing

[011-085]two or three pounds, for we were no more than a score of feet away, and

[011-086]he surely would have killed us.

[011-087]As it was, we were in no small danger. Zip! A tiny pebble whirred past

[011-088]with the force almost of a bullet. Lop-Ear and I began paddling

[011-089]frantically. Whiz-zip-bang! Lop-Ear screamed with sudden anguish. The

[011-090]pebble had struck him between the shoulders. Then I got one and yelled.

[011-091]The only thing that saved us was the exhausting of Red-Eye’s

[011-092]ammunition. He dashed back to the gravel-bed for more, while Lop-Ear

[011-093]and I paddled away.

[011-094]Gradually we drew out of range, though Red-Eye continued making trips

[011-095]for more ammunition and the pebbles continued to whiz about us. Out in

[011-096]the centre of the slough there was a slight current, and in our

[011-097]excitement we failed to notice that it was drifting us into the river.

[011-098]We paddled, and Red-Eye kept as close as he could to us by following

[011-099]along the shore. Then he discovered larger rocks. Such ammunition

[011-100]increased his range. One fragment, fully five pounds in weight, crashed

[011-101]on the log alongside of me, and such was its impact that it drove a

[011-102]score of splinters, like fiery needles, into my leg. Had it struck me

[011-103]it would have killed me.

[011-104]And then the river current caught us. So wildly were we paddling that

[011-105]Red-Eye was the first to notice it, and our first warning was his yell

[011-106]of triumph. Where the edge of the current struck the slough-water was a

[011-107]series of eddies or small whirlpools. These caught our clumsy logs and

[011-108]whirled them end for end, back and forth and around. We quit paddling

[011-109]and devoted our whole energy to holding the logs together alongside

[011-110]each other. In the meanwhile Red-Eye continued to bombard us, the rock

[011-111]fragments falling about us, splashing water on us, and menacing our

[011-112]lives. At the same time he gloated over us, wildly and vociferously.

[011-113]It happened that there was a sharp turn in the river at the point where

[011-114]the slough entered, and the whole main current of the river was

[011-115]deflected to the other bank. And toward that bank, which was the north

[011-116]bank, we drifted rapidly, at the same time going down-stream. This

[011-117]quickly took us out of range of Red-Eye, and the last we saw of him was

[011-118]far out on a point of land, where he was jumping up and down and

[011-119]chanting a paean of victory.

[011-120]Beyond holding the two logs together, Lop-Ear and I did nothing. We

[011-121]were resigned to our fate, and we remained resigned until we aroused to

[011-122]the fact that we were drifting along the north shore not a hundred feet

[011-123]away. We began to paddle for it. Here the main force of the current was

[011-124]flung back toward the south shore, and the result of our paddling was

[011-125]that we crossed the current where it was swiftest and narrowest. Before

[011-126]we were aware, we were out of it and in a quiet eddy.

[011-127]Our logs drifted slowly and at last grounded gently on the bank.

[011-128]Lop-Ear and I crept ashore. The logs drifted on out of the eddy and

[011-129]swept away down the stream. We looked at each other, but we did not

[011-130]laugh. We were in a strange land, and it did not enter our minds that

[011-131]we could return to our own land in the same manner that we had come.

[011-132]We had learned how to cross a river, though we did not know it. And

[011-133]this was something that no one else of the Folk had ever done. We were

[011-134]the first of the Folk to set foot on the north bank of the river, and,

[011-135]for that matter, I believe the last. That they would have done so in

[011-136]the time to come is undoubted; but the migration of the Fire People,

[011-137]and the consequent migration of the survivors of the Folk, set back our

[011-138]evolution for centuries.

[011-139]Indeed, there is no telling how disastrous was to be the outcome of the

[011-140]Fire People’s migration. Personally, I am prone to believe that it

[011-141]brought about the destruction of the Folk; that we, a branch of lower

[011-142]life budding toward the human, were nipped short off and perished down

[011-143]by the roaring surf where the river entered the sea. Of course, in such

[011-144]an eventuality, I remain to be accounted for; but I outrun my story,

[011-145]and such accounting will be made before I am done.
