## Overview

The social structure of the Folk occupies a position midway between the dominance hierarchies of great apes and the kinship-based societies of early humans. They have the beginnings of collective decision-making, rudimentary pair-bonding customs, and a loose dominance order — but they lack formal leadership, coordinated action, and the conceptual vocabulary that would make true cooperation possible.

## The Dominance Hierarchy

The closest thing to authority among the Folk was physical dominance, embodied by [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] — "the most powerful member of the horde" (006-012). He took what he wanted through violence rather than consent: food, mates, space. His power was absolute within the limits of his physical reach, but it did not extend to command. He could not order the horde to act; he could only intimidate individuals into submission.

Below [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] in the hierarchy were the adult males of ordinary strength — [[big-tooth|the narrator]], [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]], [[hair-face|Hair-Face]] — who deferred to [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s superior violence while maintaining their own small spheres of autonomy. Below them were the females, the young, and the old, whose status depended on the protection of stronger individuals.

## Marriage and Pair-Bonding

Marriage among the Folk was fluid and unstable. "Marriage was as yet in a rude state, and couples had a way of quarrelling and separating" (008-018-008-019). There was no formal recognition of partnership, no ceremony, and no expectation of permanence. Males took females by force or by consent; females stayed or left as circumstances permitted.

Against this background, the relationship between [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] and the [[swift-one|Swift One]] was remarkable precisely because it was different: chosen rather than forced, sustained by affection rather than dominance, and productive of an enduring bond that would outlast the destruction of the horde itself.

## The Limits of Cooperation

The Folk's social limitations were most evident in crisis. When the [[fire-people|Fire People]] attacked, "each one of us fought and acted for himself, and no one of us knew the extent of the calamity that was befalling us" (017-008-017-009). The Folk could cooperate in small, familiar ways — sharing a tree-shelter, warning of approaching predators — but they could not coordinate collective defense or plan strategic action.

This limitation was not biological but cultural. The Folk possessed the raw materials of society — communication, pair-bonding, shared fear-responses — but lacked the concepts that would organize these materials into something more than the sum of their parts. Their social structure was, like everything else about them, in transition.
