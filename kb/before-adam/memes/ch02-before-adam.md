## CHAPTER II

[002-001]I have said that in my dreams I never saw a human being. Of this fact I

[002-002]became aware very early, and felt poignantly the lack of my own kind.

[002-003]As a very little child, even, I had a feeling, in the midst of the

[002-004]horror of my dreaming, that if I could find but one man, only one

[002-005]human, I should be saved from my dreaming, that I should be surrounded

[002-006]no more by haunting terrors. This thought obsessed me every night of my

[002-007]life for years—if only I could find that one human and be saved!

[002-008]I must iterate that I had this thought in the midst of my dreaming, and

[002-009]I take it as an evidence of the merging of my two personalities, as

[002-010]evidence of a point of contact between the two disassociated parts of

[002-011]me. My dream personality lived in the long ago, before ever man, as we

[002-012]know him, came to be; and my other and wake-a-day personality projected

[002-013]itself, to the extent of the knowledge of man’s existence, into the

[002-014]substance of my dreams.

[002-015]Perhaps the psychologists of the book will find fault with my way of

[002-016]using the phrase, “disassociation of personality.” I know their use of

[002-017]it, yet am compelled to use it in my own way in default of a better

[002-018]phrase. I take shelter behind the inadequacy of the English language.

[002-019]And now to the explanation of my use, or misuse, of the phrase.

[002-020]It was not till I was a young man, at college, that I got any clew to

[002-021]the significance of my dreams, and to the cause of them. Up to that

[002-022]time they had been meaningless and without apparent causation. But at

[002-023]college I discovered evolution and psychology, and learned the

[002-024]explanation of various strange mental states and experiences. For

[002-025]instance, there was the falling-through-space dream—the commonest dream

[002-026]experience, one practically known, by first-hand experience, to all

[002-027]men.

[002-028]This, my professor told me, was a racial memory. It dated back to our

[002-029]remote ancestors who lived in trees. With them, being tree-dwellers,

[002-030]the liability of falling was an ever-present menace. Many lost their

[002-031]lives that way; all of them experienced terrible falls, saving

[002-032]themselves by clutching branches as they fell toward the ground.

[002-033]Now a terrible fall, averted in such fashion, was productive of shock.

[002-034]Such shock was productive of molecular changes in the cerebral cells.

[002-035]These molecular changes were transmitted to the cerebral cells of

[002-036]progeny, became, in short, racial memories. Thus, when you and I,

[002-037]asleep or dozing off to sleep, fall through space and awake to

[002-038]sickening consciousness just before we strike, we are merely

[002-039]remembering what happened to our arboreal ancestors, and which has been

[002-040]stamped by cerebral changes into the heredity of the race.

[002-041]There is nothing strange in this, any more than there is anything

[002-042]strange in an instinct. An instinct is merely a habit that is stamped

[002-043]into the stuff of our heredity, that is all. It will be noted, in

[002-044]passing, that in this falling dream which is so familiar to you and me

[002-045]and all of us, we never strike bottom. To strike bottom would be

[002-046]destruction. Those of our arboreal ancestors who struck bottom died

[002-047]forthwith. True, the shock of their fall was communicated to the

[002-048]cerebral cells, but they died immediately, before they could have

[002-049]progeny. You and I are descended from those that did not strike bottom;

[002-050]that is why you and I, in our dreams, never strike bottom.

[002-051]And now we come to disassociation of personality. We never have this

[002-052]sense of falling when we are wide awake. Our wake-a-day personality has

[002-053]no experience of it. Then—and here the argument is irresistible—it must

[002-054]be another and distinct personality that falls when we are asleep, and

[002-055]that has had experience of such falling—that has, in short, a memory of

[002-056]past-day race experiences, just as our wake-a-day personality has a

[002-057]memory of our wake-a-day experiences.

[002-058]It was at this stage in my reasoning that I began to see the light. And

[002-059]quickly the light burst upon me with dazzling brightness, illuminating

[002-060]and explaining all that had been weird and uncanny and unnaturally

[002-061]impossible in my dream experiences. In my sleep it was not my

[002-062]wake-a-day personality that took charge of me; it was another and

[002-063]distinct personality, possessing a new and totally different fund of

[002-064]experiences, and, to the point of my dreaming, possessing memories of

[002-065]those totally different experiences.

[002-066]What was this personality? When had it itself lived a wake-a-day life

[002-067]on this planet in order to collect this fund of strange experiences?

[002-068]These were questions that my dreams themselves answered. He lived in

[002-069]the long ago, when the world was young, in that period that we call the

[002-070]Mid-Pleistocene. He fell from the trees but did not strike bottom. He

[002-071]gibbered with fear at the roaring of the lions. He was pursued by

[002-072]beasts of prey, struck at by deadly snakes. He chattered with his kind

[002-073]in council, and he received rough usage at the hands of the Fire People

[002-074]in the day that he fled before them.

[002-075]But, I hear you objecting, why is it that these racial memories are not

[002-076]ours as well, seeing that we have a vague other-personality that falls

[002-077]through space while we sleep?

[002-078]And I may answer with another question. Why is a two-headed calf? And

[002-079]my own answer to this is that it is a freak. And so I answer your

[002-080]question. I have this other-personality and these complete racial

[002-081]memories because I am a freak.

[002-082]But let me be more explicit.

[002-083]The commonest race memory we have is the falling-through-space dream.

[002-084]This other-personality is very vague. About the only memory it has is

[002-085]that of falling. But many of us have sharper, more distinct

[002-086]other-personalities. Many of us have the flying dream, the

[002-087]pursuing-monster dream, color dreams, suffocation dreams, and the

[002-088]reptile and vermin dreams. In short, while this other-personality is

[002-089]vestigial in all of us, in some of us it is almost obliterated, while

[002-090]in others of us it is more pronounced. Some of us have stronger and

[002-091]completer race memories than others.

[002-092]It is all a question of varying degree of possession of the

[002-093]other-personality. In myself, the degree of possession is enormous. My

[002-094]other-personality is almost equal in power with my own personality. And

[002-095]in this matter I am, as I said, a freak—a freak of heredity.

[002-096]I do believe that it is the possession of this other-personality—but

[002-097]not so strong a one as mine—that has in some few others given rise to

[002-098]belief in personal reincarnation experiences. It is very plausible to

[002-099]such people, a most convincing hypothesis. When they have visions of

[002-100]scenes they have never seen in the flesh, memories of acts and events

[002-101]dating back in time, the simplest explanation is that they have lived

[002-102]before.

[002-103]But they make the mistake of ignoring their own duality. They do not

[002-104]recognize their other-personality. They think it is their own

[002-105]personality, that they have only one personality; and from such a

[002-106]premise they can conclude only that they have lived previous lives.

[002-107]But they are wrong. It is not reincarnation. I have visions of myself

[002-108]roaming through the forests of the Younger World; and yet it is not

[002-109]myself that I see but one that is only remotely a part of me, as my

[002-110]father and my grandfather are parts of me less remote. This other-self

[002-111]of mine is an ancestor, a progenitor of my progenitors in the early

[002-112]line of my race, himself the progeny of a line that long before his

[002-113]time developed fingers and toes and climbed up into the trees.

[002-114]I must again, at the risk of boring, repeat that I am, in this one

[002-115]thing, to be considered a freak. Not alone do I possess racial memory

[002-116]to an enormous extent, but I possess the memories of one particular and

[002-117]far-removed progenitor. And yet, while this is most unusual, there is

[002-118]nothing over-remarkable about it.

[002-119]Follow my reasoning. An instinct is a racial memory. Very good. Then

[002-120]you and I and all of us receive these memories from our fathers and

[002-121]mothers, as they received them from their fathers and mothers.

[002-122]Therefore there must be a medium whereby these memories are transmitted

[002-123]from generation to generation. This medium is what Weismann terms the

[002-124]“germplasm.” It carries the memories of the whole evolution of the

[002-125]race. These memories are dim and confused, and many of them are lost.

[002-126]But some strains of germplasm carry an excessive freightage of

[002-127]memories—are, to be scientific, more atavistic than other strains; and

[002-128]such a strain is mine. I am a freak of heredity, an atavistic

[002-129]nightmare—call me what you will; but here I am, real and alive, eating

[002-130]three hearty meals a day, and what are you going to do about it?

[002-131]And now, before I take up my tale, I want to anticipate the doubting

[002-132]Thomases of psychology, who are prone to scoff, and who would otherwise

[002-133]surely say that the coherence of my dreams is due to overstudy and the

[002-134]subconscious projection of my knowledge of evolution into my dreams. In

[002-135]the first place, I have never been a zealous student. I graduated last

[002-136]of my class. I cared more for athletics, and—there is no reason I

[002-137]should not confess it—more for billiards.

[002-138]Further, I had no knowledge of evolution until I was at college,

[002-139]whereas in my childhood and youth I had already lived in my dreams all

[002-140]the details of that other, long-ago life. I will say, however, that

[002-141]these details were mixed and incoherent until I came to know the

[002-142]science of evolution. Evolution was the key. It gave the explanation,

[002-143]gave sanity to the pranks of this atavistic brain of mine that, modern

[002-144]and normal, harked back to a past so remote as to be contemporaneous

[002-145]with the raw beginnings of mankind.

[002-146]For in this past I know of, man, as we to-day know him, did not exist.

[002-147]It was in the period of his becoming that I must have lived and had my

[002-148]being.
