## Overview

The Fire People represent a stage of hominin development beyond that of [[the-horde|the Folk]]. They possess controlled fire — a [[technology]] [[the-horde|the Folk]] have not yet discovered — and their camps, social organization, and relationship to the environment differ markedly from the arboreal existence of [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s people. Contact with the Fire People proves transformative for [[the-horde|the Folk]], particularly in the transmission of fire-making knowledge, but also catastrophic: the ensuing [[conflict]] over [[territory]] reshapes the [[survival]] prospects of both groups.

## In the Novel

The narrator first learns of the Fire People through tribal memory and hearsay — mentions of "the Fire People and their squatting places" (001-134) circulate among [[the-horde|the Folk]] as a mixture of awe and fear. Direct encounters are tense and dangerous: [[the-horde|the Folk]] have been "roughly used at the hands of the Fire People" (002-073), suggesting territorial conflicts between the two groups.

The pivotal encounter occurs in Chapter 13, when [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and Big-Fat observe a [[fire|Fire]] Man's camp from hiding. They watch the [[fire|Fire]] Men tend their flames, feed them with wood, and sleep around the fire (013-043). When the [[fire|Fire]] Men depart, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] rushes to the dying embers and pokes at them with a stick — the first step toward acquiring fire himself (013-076).

## Context and Significance

The gulf between [[the-horde|the Folk]] and the Fire People reflects the uneven pace of [[technology|technological]] diffusion in human prehistory. Some groups mastered fire hundreds of thousands of years before others. The Fire People had also achieved the domestication of [[wild-dogs|dogs]] — a force multiplier in [[hunting]] and defense that the Folk could not match. The tension and eventual knowledge transfer between the two groups dramatizes how cultural contact — even under conditions of hostility — can drive [[evolution|evolutionary]] acceleration.
