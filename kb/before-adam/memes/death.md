> We were very near to death. (017-072)

## Overview

Death in *Before Adam* is not an occasional event but a constant presence. The narrator's world is one in which death can come at any moment — from the claws of [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], from the arrows of the [[fire-people|Fire People]], from the rage of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], from starvation, from a fall from [[the-cliffs|the cliffs]], from the treacherous surface of [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]]. The Folk live, as the narrator puts it, "very near to death" (017-072).

## Modes of Death

The novel catalogues many ways to die. Predators — [[saber-tooth|saber-toothed cats]] and [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]] — are the most ancient threat, the reason the Folk retreat to their caves at twilight. Violence within the [[the-horde|horde]] claims lives: [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] kills Crooked-Leg in a fit of rage, beating him to death before the assembled Folk (014-028-014-038). Inter-group violence is the most catastrophic: the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]] kills nearly the entire horde in a single morning, using fire and arrows in a systematic campaign of extermination.

Starvation and exposure are quieter killers. The narrator describes seasons of scarcity when the Folk "starved and died by scores" (010-045). The [[the-carrot-patch-ambush|carrot patch ambush]] exploits the Folk's need for food — the Fire People know where the Folk will go because hunger leaves them no choice.

## Death as Narrative Engine

Death drives the plot of *Before Adam*. The threat of death forces the Folk out of the caves and into [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]]. The near-extinction of the horde in the massacre makes possible everything that follows: the journey through the swamp, the encounter with new lands and peoples, and the eventual discovery of [[the-sea|the sea]]. London presents death not as an ending but as a reshuffling — the destruction of the old life is the precondition for the new one.

## The Meaning of Death

The Folk have no metaphysics of death — no afterlife, no ritual, no mourning beyond momentary grief. When [[marrow-bone|Old Marrow-Bone]] is shot with a dozen arrows and clubbed to death, the narrator reports it with stark directness (017-049-017-054). There is no eulogy, no reflection — only the brute fact of cessation. This absence of meaning-making around death is itself significant: the Folk are not yet human enough to ask what death means. They only know that it is, and that it must be avoided.
