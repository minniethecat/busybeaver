## Overview

The Predator Ecosystem of the Younger World was a layered hierarchy of carnivores, each occupying a distinct niche and presenting a different threat profile. For [[the-horde|the Folk]] — mid-level prey in this system — survival depended on understanding the distinct hunting patterns of each predator and navigating a landscape where fleeing one threat often meant exposure to another.

The ecosystem's complexity was both a danger and, paradoxically, a form of protection. The predators competed with each other as well as preying on the Folk, and their overlapping but non-identical hunting methods sometimes created opportunities for escape that would not have existed in a simpler system.

## The Hierarchy

At the apex stood [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], the solitary ambush predator whose sheer size and power made him the terror of the open space and the forest floor. The Folk could not fight Saber-Tooth — they could only flee upward into trees or cliffs, where the great cat's bulk became a liability.

Below Saber-Tooth in threat level but above him in tactical sophistication were the [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]], pack hunters whose coordination and persistence made them dangerous in a different way. Where Saber-Tooth relied on power, wild dogs relied on numbers — a single dog could be fought, but a pack could surround and exhaust even a strong Folk.

At the periphery were wolves and jackals — the former a threat in open terrain, the latter primarily scavengers who posed danger only to the very young, the very old, or the very unlucky.

## The Navigation Problem

The Folk's survival challenge was not simply to avoid each predator individually but to navigate between their overlapping territories. Fleeing [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] into narrow cave passages might lead directly into the jaws of waiting [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]], as the [[double-cave-attack|double-cave attack]] demonstrated. The [[the-river|river]] offered safety from ground-based predators but exposed the Folk to the danger of drowning.

This navigation problem required a cognitive sophistication that the Folk possessed without understanding: they could calculate risk across multiple dimensions, weigh the relative dangers of different escape routes, and choose actions that minimized exposure to the most immediate threat while accepting calculated risks from others. The ability to make these calculations — not physical strength or speed — was the Folk's most important survival adaptation.
