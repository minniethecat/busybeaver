## CHAPTER X

[010-001]After we had had out our laugh, Lop-Ear and I curved back in our flight

[010-002]and got breakfast in the blueberry swamp. It was the same swamp to

[010-003]which I had made my first journeys in the world, years before,

[010-004]accompanied by my mother. I had seen little of her in the intervening

[010-005]time. Usually, when she visited the horde at the caves, I was away in

[010-006]the forest. I had once or twice caught glimpses of the Chatterer in the

[010-007]open space, and had had the pleasure of making faces at him and

[010-008]angering him from the mouth of my cave. Beyond such amenities I had

[010-009]left my family severely alone. I was not much interested in it, and

[010-010]anyway I was doing very well by myself.

[010-011]After eating our fill of berries, with two nestfuls of partly hatched

[010-012]quail-eggs for dessert, Lop-Ear and I wandered circumspectly into the

[010-013]woods toward the river. Here was where stood my old home-tree, out of

[010-014]which I had been thrown by the Chatterer. It was still occupied. There

[010-015]had been increase in the family. Clinging tight to my mother was a

[010-016]little baby. Also, there was a girl, partly grown, who cautiously

[010-017]regarded us from one of the lower branches. She was evidently my

[010-018]sister, or half-sister, rather.

[010-019]My mother recognized me, but she warned me away when I started to climb

[010-020]into the tree. Lop-Ear, who was more cautious by far than I, beat a

[010-021]retreat, nor could I persuade him to return. Later in the day, however,

[010-022]my sister came down to the ground, and there and in neighboring trees

[010-023]we romped and played all afternoon. And then came trouble. She was my

[010-024]sister, but that did not prevent her from treating me abominably, for

[010-025]she had inherited all the viciousness of the Chatterer. She turned upon

[010-026]me suddenly, in a petty rage, and scratched me, tore my hair, and sank

[010-027]her sharp little teeth deep into my forearm. I lost my temper. I did

[010-028]not injure her, but it was undoubtedly the soundest spanking she had

[010-029]received up to that time.

[010-030]How she yelled and squalled. The Chatterer, who had been away all day

[010-031]and who was only then returning, heard the noise and rushed for the

[010-032]spot. My mother also rushed, but he got there first. Lop-Ear and I did

[010-033]not wait his coming. We were off and away, and the Chatterer gave us

[010-034]the chase of our lives through the trees.

[010-035]After the chase was over, and Lop-Ear and I had had out our laugh, we

[010-036]discovered that twilight was falling. Here was night with all its

[010-037]terrors upon us, and to return to the caves was out of the question.

[010-038]Red-Eye made that impossible. We took refuge in a tree that stood apart

[010-039]from other trees, and high up in a fork we passed the night. It was a

[010-040]miserable night. For the first few hours it rained heavily, then it

[010-041]turned cold and a chill wind blew upon us. Soaked through, with

[010-042]shivering bodies and chattering teeth, we huddled in each other’s arms.

[010-043]We missed the snug, dry cave that so quickly warmed with the heat of

[010-044]our bodies.

[010-045]Morning found us wretched and resolved. We would not spend another such

[010-046]night. Remembering the tree-shelters of our elders, we set to work to

[010-047]make one for ourselves. We built the framework of a rough nest, and on

[010-048]higher forks overhead even got in several ridge-poles for the roof.

[010-049]Then the sun came out, and under its benign influence we forgot the

[010-050]hardships of the night and went off in search of breakfast. After that,

[010-051]to show the inconsequentiality of life in those days, we fell to

[010-052]playing. It must have taken us all of a month, working intermittently,

[010-053]to make our tree-house; and then, when it was completed, we never used

[010-054]it again.

[010-055]But I run ahead of my story. When we fell to playing, after breakfast,

[010-056]on the second day away from the caves, Lop-Ear led me a chase through

[010-057]the trees and down to the river. We came out upon it where a large

[010-058]slough entered from the blueberry swamp. The mouth of this slough was

[010-059]wide, while the slough itself was practically without a current. In the

[010-060]dead water, just inside its mouth, lay a tangled mass of tree trunks.

[010-061]Some of these, what of the wear and tear of freshets and of being

[010-062]stranded long summers on sand-bars, were seasoned and dry and without

[010-063]branches. They floated high in the water, and bobbed up and down or

[010-064]rolled over when we put our weight upon them.

[010-065]Here and there between the trunks were water-cracks, and through them

[010-066]we could see schools of small fish, like minnows, darting back and

[010-067]forth. Lop-Ear and I became fishermen at once. Lying flat on the logs,

[010-068]keeping perfectly quiet, waiting till the minnows came close, we would

[010-069]make swift passes with our hands. Our prizes we ate on the spot,

[010-070]wriggling and moist. We did not notice the lack of salt.

[010-071]The mouth of the slough became our favorite playground. Here we spent

[010-072]many hours each day, catching fish and playing on the logs, and here,

[010-073]one day, we learned our first lessons in navigation. The log on which

[010-074]Lop-Ear was lying got adrift. He was curled up on his side, asleep. A

[010-075]light fan of air slowly drifted the log away from the shore, and when I

[010-076]noticed his predicament the distance was already too great for him to

[010-077]leap.

[010-078]At first the episode seemed merely funny to me. But when one of the

[010-079]vagrant impulses of fear, common in that age of perpetual insecurity,

[010-080]moved within me, I was struck with my own loneliness. I was made

[010-081]suddenly aware of Lop-Ear’s remoteness out there on that alien element

[010-082]a few feet away. I called loudly to him a warning cry. He awoke

[010-083]frightened, and shifted his weight rashly on the log. It turned over,

[010-084]sousing him under. Three times again it soused him under as he tried to

[010-085]climb out upon it. Then he succeeded, crouching upon it and chattering

[010-086]with fear.

[010-087]I could do nothing. Nor could he. Swimming was something of which we

[010-088]knew nothing. We were already too far removed from the lower life-forms

[010-089]to have the instinct for swimming, and we had not yet become

[010-090]sufficiently man-like to undertake it as the working out of a problem.

[010-091]I roamed disconsolately up and down the bank, keeping as close to him

[010-092]in his involuntary travels as I could, while he wailed and cried till

[010-093]it was a wonder that he did not bring down upon us every hunting animal

[010-094]within a mile.

[010-095]The hours passed. The sun climbed overhead and began its descent to the

[010-096]west. The light wind died down and left Lop-Ear on his log floating

[010-097]around a hundred feet away. And then, somehow, I know not how, Lop-Ear

[010-098]made the great discovery. He began paddling with his hands. At first

[010-099]his progress was slow and erratic. Then he straightened out and began

[010-100]laboriously to paddle nearer and nearer. I could not understand. I sat

[010-101]down and watched and waited until he gained the shore.

[010-102]But he had learned something, which was more than I had done. Later in

[010-103]the afternoon, he deliberately launched out from shore on the log.

[010-104]Still later he persuaded me to join him, and I, too, learned the trick

[010-105]of paddling. For the next several days we could not tear ourselves away

[010-106]from the slough. So absorbed were we in our new game that we almost

[010-107]neglected to eat. We even roosted in a nearby tree at night. And we

[010-108]forgot that Red-Eye existed.

[010-109]We were always trying new logs, and we learned that the smaller the log

[010-110]the faster we could make it go. Also, we learned that the smaller the

[010-111]log the more liable it was to roll over and give us a ducking. Still

[010-112]another thing about small logs we learned. One day we paddled our

[010-113]individual logs alongside each other. And then, quite by accident, in

[010-114]the course of play, we discovered that when each, with one hand and

[010-115]foot, held on to the other’s log, the logs were steadied and did not

[010-116]turn over. Lying side by side in this position, our outside hands and

[010-117]feet were left free for paddling. Our final discovery was that this

[010-118]arrangement enabled us to use still smaller logs and thereby gain

[010-119]greater speed. And there our discoveries ended. We had invented the

[010-120]most primitive catamaran, and we did not have sense enough to know it.

[010-121]It never entered our heads to lash the logs together with tough vines

[010-122]or stringy roots. We were content to hold the logs together with our

[010-123]hands and feet.

[010-124]It was not until we got over our first enthusiasm for navigation and

[010-125]had begun to return to our tree-shelter to sleep at night, that we

[010-126]found the Swift One. I saw her first, gathering young acorns from the

[010-127]branches of a large oak near our tree. She was very timid. At first,

[010-128]she kept very still; but when she saw that she was discovered she

[010-129]dropped to the ground and dashed wildly away. We caught occasional

[010-130]glimpses of her from day to day, and came to look for her when we

[010-131]travelled back and forth between our tree and the mouth of the slough.

[010-132]And then, one day, she did not run away. She waited our coming, and

[010-133]made soft peace-sounds. We could not get very near, however. When we

[010-134]seemed to approach too close, she darted suddenly away and from a safe

[010-135]distance uttered the soft sounds again. This continued for some days.

[010-136]It took a long while to get acquainted with her, but finally it was

[010-137]accomplished and she joined us sometimes in our play.

[010-138]I liked her from the first. She was of most pleasing appearance. She

[010-139]was very mild. Her eyes were the mildest I had ever seen. In this she

[010-140]was quite unlike the rest of the girls and women of the Folk, who were

[010-141]born viragos. She never made harsh, angry cries, and it seemed to be

[010-142]her nature to flee away from trouble rather than to remain and fight.

[010-143]The mildness I have mentioned seemed to emanate from her whole being.

[010-144]Her bodily as well as facial appearance was the cause of this. Her eyes

[010-145]were larger than most of her kind, and they were not so deep-set, while

[010-146]the lashes were longer and more regular. Nor was her nose so thick and

[010-147]squat. It had quite a bridge, and the nostrils opened downward. Her

[010-148]incisors were not large, nor was her upper lip long and down-hanging,

[010-149]nor her lower lip protruding. She was not very hairy, except on the

[010-150]outsides of arms and legs and across the shoulders; and while she was

[010-151]thin-hipped, her calves were not twisted and gnarly.

[010-152]I have often wondered, looking back upon her from the twentieth century

[010-153]through the medium of my dreams, and it has always occurred to me that

[010-154]possibly she may have been related to the Fire People. Her father, or

[010-155]mother, might well have come from that higher stock. While such things

[010-156]were not common, still they did occur, and I have seen the proof of

[010-157]them with my own eyes, even to the extent of members of the horde

[010-158]turning renegade and going to live with the Tree People.

[010-159]All of which is neither here nor there. The Swift One was radically

[010-160]different from any of the females of the horde, and I had a liking for

[010-161]her from the first. Her mildness and gentleness attracted me. She was

[010-162]never rough, and she never fought. She always ran away, and right here

[010-163]may be noted the significance of the naming of her. She was a better

[010-164]climber than Lop-Ear or I. When we played tag we could never catch her

[010-165]except by accident, while she could catch us at will. She was

[010-166]remarkably swift in all her movements, and she had a genius for judging

[010-167]distances that was equalled only by her daring. Excessively timid in

[010-168]all other matters, she was without fear when it came to climbing or

[010-169]running through the trees, and Lop-Ear and I were awkward and lumbering

[010-170]and cowardly in comparison.

[010-171]She was an orphan. We never saw her with any one, and there was no

[010-172]telling how long she had lived alone in the world. She must have

[010-173]learned early in her helpless childhood that safety lay only in flight.

[010-174]She was very wise and very discreet. It became a sort of game with

[010-175]Lop-Ear and me to try to find where she lived. It was certain that she

[010-176]had a tree-shelter somewhere, and not very far away; but trail her as

[010-177]we would, we could never find it. She was willing enough to join with

[010-178]us at play in the day-time, but the secret of her abiding-place she

[010-179]guarded jealously.
