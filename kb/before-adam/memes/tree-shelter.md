## Overview

Tree-shelters are the Folk's portable sleeping structures, built in the forest when they are away from the permanent cave dwellings. Unlike the simple tree-nest of Big-Tooth's infancy — a natural hollow used by his mother — tree-shelters are deliberately constructed with ridge-poles for a roof, representing a step forward in technological problem-solving (010-046). They fill an ecological niche between the safety of the caves and the exposure of open ground, allowing the Folk to extend their range deeper into the forest.

## In the Novel

Tree-shelters first appear when Lop-Ear and Big-Tooth, having ranged far from the cliffs during their adventure-journey, need a place to sleep. Remembering the techniques of their elders, "we set to work to make a shelter" with ridge-poles for the roof (010-046). The shelter becomes their base camp: "we had begun to return to our tree-shelter to sleep at night" (010-125).

The Swift One has her own secret tree-shelter, which Big-Tooth can never locate — "I could never find her tree-shelter" (011-062). This hidden refuge speaks to her independence and the defensive value of concealment. After Lop-Ear marries, Big-Tooth sleeps alone "in a tree-shelter I made near the mouth of the cave" (015-059), a middle ground between exile and community. He continues to use tree-shelters through his later adventures, and on the final migration, the narrator reflects that "we might have built tree-shelters" had they settled in a certain stretch of forest rather than pressing on (017-190).

## Context and Significance

Tree-shelters reflect a transitional stage in hominin technology. The shift from opportunistic use of natural shelters (tree hollows, rock overhangs) to deliberate construction marks a cognitive advance — planning, engineering, and the transmission of learned skills. The fact that Big-Tooth learns shelter-building from "remembering the tree-shelters of our elders" (010-046) highlights cultural transmission, not instinct, as the basis of the skill.

## Evolutionary and Technological Significance

The tree-shelter represents the transitional technology between arboreal life and terrestrial habitation. The Folk, having descended from the trees, retain the ability to construct rough shelters in branches when away from the caves. These shelters are crude — little more than platforms of broken branches — but they represent the persistence of ancestral knowledge and the adaptability that distinguishes the Folk from more specialized hominins like the Tree People. The tree-shelter is a technology of the in-between, used when the caves are too far and the ground too dangerous.

> The narrator's acquisition of this knowledge — at first unconscious, then gradually recognized — mirrors the evolutionary trajectory London traces throughout the novel: from instinct to awareness, from accident to intention. [003-005]
