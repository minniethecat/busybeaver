## Overview

Fire represents one of the most consequential technological thresholds in the world of _Before Adam_. At the time of the narrator's early life, his tribe — the Folk — had not yet learned to make or control fire. The discovery and eventual mastery of fire, mediated through encounters with the more advanced Fire People, marks a turning point in the tribe's evolution from prey-like arboreal existence toward greater security and capability (008-230).

## In the Novel

The Folk initially live without any knowledge of fire. They are "without weapons, without fire" (008-104), entirely vulnerable to the predators of their Pleistocene world. Their evolution toward cooking animals had not yet begun precisely because fire remained undiscovered (008-230).

The turning point comes through the Fire People — a more advanced group who possess fire-making skills. When the narrator and Big-Fat encounter a Fire Man's camp at night, they are drawn irresistibly to the unfamiliar glow (013-006). From hiding, they watch the Fire Men squat around the fire, feed it with wood, and sleep beside it (013-043, 013-046). The encounter is transformative: after the Fire Men depart, the narrator runs to the smoldering remnants and pokes the fire with a stick (013-076), the first tentative step toward learning to control flame himself.

## Context and Significance

In evolutionary anthropology, the control of fire is widely regarded as a watershed adaptation. It extended the working day into darkness, provided a barrier against nocturnal predators, and — crucially — enabled cooking, which dramatically increased the caloric availability of food and is believed to have supported the evolution of larger brains in the genus _Homo_. London's novel dramatizes this major transition through the eyes of a pre-fire hominin, capturing both the wonder and the practical cascading effects of the discovery.
