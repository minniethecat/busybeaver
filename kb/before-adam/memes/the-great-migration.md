## Overview

The Great Migration was the extended period of wandering that followed the dissolution of the horde at [[caves|caves]]. It began with the separation of [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[Lop-Ear]] from the rest of the Folk, was propelled by the accidental [[the-river-crossing|River Crossing]], and carried them through a chain of unfamiliar landscapes — the forest on the far side of [[the-river|the river]], the hungry mountains between [[Long Lake]] and [[Far Lake]], and finally to the roaring surf where the [[the-river|River]] met [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] (011-143).

The migration occupied three full chapters of the narrative (ch10–ch12) and was not a single deliberate journey but a series of forced movements — driven by hunger, chased by enemies, and pulled by the current of [[the-river|the river]]. At its end, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] had traveled farther than any of the Folk had ever gone, and the territory of the caves had become a distant memory.

## Context and Causes

The Folk's life at the caves was never stable. Raids by the [[Fire People]], internal conflicts with [[Red-Eye]], and the natural attrition of the horde had already weakened the group by the time [[big-tooth|the narrator]] reached adulthood. The immediate trigger of the migration was the separation of [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] from the rest of the horde — a separation that began as a temporary wandering (010-012) but became permanent when [[the-river|the river]] carried them beyond the possibility of return (011-130-011-131).

The narrator's mother had already left the horde for a [[shelter|tree-shelter]] in the forest (010-005), and the horde itself was fragmenting. The migration was thus not a planned exodus but the cumulative effect of individual movements away from the caves — movements that, for [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]], became irreversible.

## Narrative Account

### Leaving the Caves

The first stage of the migration was not a departure but a drift. The narrator and [[Lop-Ear]] wandered into the forest on what was meant to be a temporary excursion (010-012). They ate quail eggs, explored, and lost track of the days. By the time they attempted to return, the [[the-river-crossing|River Crossing]] had intervened, and return was no longer possible.

### The River Crossing

The crossing itself — an accident born of play, current, and [[Red-Eye]]'s rock bombardment (011-097-011-127) — was the event that made the migration irreversible. The narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] grounded on the north bank of [[the-river|the river]], in a strange land where they knew no landmarks and had no shelter (011-130).

### The Mountain Passage

Beyond [[the-river|the river]] lay forested country, then mountains. The Folk traveled westward (012-014), then north, skirting marshes (012-016), until they reached [[Long Lake]]. The mountains were a place of hunger — "the hunger we endured on [[the-mountain|the mountains]] between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[Far Lake]]" (012-009) — relieved only by chance kills of sleeping calves in lakeshore thickets (012-010)(012-101).

The [[Tree People]] dwelt in the forest between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[the-mountain|the mountains]] and chased the Folk into higher country (012-011-012-013).

### Arrival at the Sea

The stream that drained [[far-lake|Far Lake]] was packed with salmon that had come up from [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] to spawn (012-105-012-106) — a sign that the journey was nearing its end. Eventually, the River delivered them to [[the-sea-encounter|the ocean]]: "the roaring surf where [[the-river|the river]] entered [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]]" (011-143). Here, in the years that followed, they made their abiding-place (018-034).

## Consequences

The migration fundamentally changed the Folk. They learned to cross rivers (011-132), to navigate unfamiliar terrain, to survive on what the land offered rather than what they knew. They encountered new peoples — the [[Fire People]], the [[Tree People]] — and acquired new knowledge, including the use of fire. The migration was the making of them as a people, or at least of [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] as the surviving pair who carried forward what the Folk had been.

## Narrative Structure and Thematic Role

The Great Migration functions as the narrative spine of the novel's middle section, transforming what could have been a series of disconnected episodes into a coherent journey of transformation. Each stage of the migration — from the [[blueberry-swamp|slough]] to [[the-mountain|the mountains]], from [[long-lake|Long Lake]] to [[far-lake|Far Lake]], and finally to [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] — introduces new challenges, new discoveries, and incremental growth in the narrator's understanding of the world.

This structure mirrors the evolutionary paradigm London embraces: progress is not a single leap but an accumulation of small adaptations over time and distance. The migration is both literal (a journey across a landscape) and metaphorical (a journey across evolutionary thresholds). By the time the narrator reaches the sea, he is not the same being who left the caves — and London suggests that this transformation is the very mechanism by which humanity emerged from its pre-human ancestors.

> "We learned many things in those days" (018-045) — the narrator's understatement captures the cumulative nature of evolutionary change, each small discovery building upon the last.
