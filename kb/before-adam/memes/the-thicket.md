## Overview

The Thicket was a dense patch of tangled vegetation — brush, saplings, and undergrowth so thick that it provided natural concealment. Unlike the open, social space before the [[caves]] or the exposed sweep of the plain, the Thicket was a place of hiding: a refuge where the Folk could crouch unseen, watching for danger or waiting for prey.

> In the midst of the thicket was a [016-086]

The Thicket appears throughout the narrative as a recurring feature of the landscape — not a single named location, but a type of terrain that the Folk learned to use strategically. Game animals bedded down in thickets [012-010][012-101]; predators crashed through them [013-059]; and in moments of mortal danger, the Folk pressed themselves into the undergrowth and waited [017-141][017-168].

## Geographic Context

Thickets grew in patches across the territory of the Folk — along the shores of [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[far-lake|Far Lake]] [012-010][012-101], in the forests beyond the [[the-open-space|Open Space]], and throughout the gentler country on the far side of the [[the-mountain|mountains]] [016-046]. They were a feature of the lowlands and forests rather than the bare bluffs and caves, though dense brush could be found even near [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s.

The Thicket's value lay in its density: a grown animal or a crouching hominid could disappear within it, hidden from sight by the tangle of branches and leaves. This made it a favored bedding place for game — and therefore a favored hunting ground for the Folk [012-010].

## Role in the Narrative

The Thicket served the Folk primarily as a place of ambush and concealment. On the shores of a mountain lake, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and his companions found a part-grown calf sleeping in a thicket — a rare windfall during the hungry mountain wanderings [012-101]. In the forests beyond, the crashing of heavy bodies in the thickets signaled the presence of game or of danger [013-059].

During the conflict with the [[fire-people|Fire People]], the Thicket became a place of war. The narrator and his companion crouched in a thicket, arms around each other, watching and waiting as the enemy approached [017-141][017-168]. Later, in the final chapter, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] hid in a thicket to observe the [[Tree People]] [018-116] — a moment of reconnaissance that demonstrated how the instinct to use dense cover for concealment had become second nature.

Even [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] respected the Thicket's concealment: the great cat snarled and went away through the thicket [016-092], unable or unwilling to pursue prey into the tangled undergrowth.

## Inhabitants and Usage

No one lived in the Thicket — it was too dense for habitation. But game animals used it as a bedding ground, and the Folk learned to use it as a blind. The Thicket's thick cover offered protection that the open spaces and caves could not: where the [[the-open-space|Open Space]] exposed the Folk to the gaze of predators and rivals alike, the Thicket hid them.

The Thicket was also a place of intimacy in danger. During the siege by the [[fire-people|Fire People]], [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and his closest companion waited together in the thicket, "our arms around each other" [017-168] — a moment of human connection in the midst of war, sheltered by the dense green darkness of the brush.
