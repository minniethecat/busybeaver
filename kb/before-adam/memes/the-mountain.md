## Overview

The Mountains were a landscape of suffering — a seemingly endless succession of peaks and valleys that the Folk traversed during their long wanderings after leaving the [[the-river|river]] behind. Unlike the familiar [[the-cliffs|cliffs]] that had sheltered them, the Mountains offered no permanent caves, no berry swamps, and little game. They were a place to be crossed, not inhabited; a zone of hunger and cold that marked the darkest period of [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s journey.

> the mountains were bleak and inhospitable, and we suffered greatly from the cold [012-077]

The narrator's memories of the Mountains are notably fragmentary — "only misty memories" [012-085] — a narrative fog that mirrors the physical and psychological disorientation of the journey. What details survive are of hunger, of sleeping in whatever shelter could be found, and of the unceasing, range-upon-range topography that offered no end in sight.

## Geographic Context

The Mountains lay beyond the [[the-river|river]], rising after the Folk had crossed to the north bank [011-134] and been carried to the sea [011-143]. From the coast, they were driven inland — "like mariners wrecked upon a strange coast" [012-002] — and into the mountain fastness.

The terrain was dramatically different from the familiar landscape of the caves. Grassy flanks provided some grazing, and the Folk managed to catch occasional game — a half-grown calf sleeping in a thicket [012-010], another part-grown calf on the shores of a lake [012-101] — but these were rare windfalls. The dominant experience was of cold, exposure, and the endless labor of ascending one ridge only to see another beyond it [012-092].

A lake lay somewhere among the peaks, and it was from this lake that [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and his companions were chased further into the mountains by hostile tribes [012-012]. Rivers ran through the valleys, one of which the Folk followed down to a gentler country [016-046].

## Role in the Narrative

The Mountains occupy a transitional chapter in the narrative — between the familiar territory of the caves and the eventual encounter with the [[Fire People]]. They represent the lowest point of the Folk's fortunes: hungry, cold, and without a fixed home, they wandered for "weeks and months" [012-004] through terrain that offered no comfort.

Yet the Mountains also provided a kind of refuge. The pursuing tribes who had chased the Folk from the lake region did not follow them deep into the high country [012-012]. And on the far side of the mountains, coming down to the banks of a river, the Folk found a gentler land [016-046] — the territory that would eventually lead them to the [[fire-people|Fire People]] and the discovery that would change everything.

The narrator's sparse account of the mountain crossing — "I have only misty memories" [012-085] — is itself significant. Throughout the rest of the narrative, his recall is vivid and detailed, rich with sensory impressions and emotional texture. The Mountains, by contrast, are a blank: a period of such sustained hardship that memory itself seems to have shut down, retaining only fragments.

## Inhabitants and Usage

The Folk were transient in the Mountains — they passed through, they did not dwell. They slept where they could [012-092], ate what they could find, and kept moving because to stop was to freeze. The Mountains were not territory to be claimed or named; they were an obstacle to be survived.

Later, in the final chapter, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] mentions other mountains — "a range of mountains" that the survivors passed after emerging from the [[the-great-swamp|great swamp]], traveling east and south toward the coast [018-076][018-079]. These later mountains are mentioned only in passing, as though [[big-tooth|the narrator]], having once endured the unceasing mountains of his earlier wanderings, has no more words to spend on peaks and passes.
