## Role in the Younger World

The Lion was one of the apex predators of the Mid-Pleistocene landscape, alongside [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]].
In the ancestral memories of the narrator, the lion represents the ever-present threat of sudden, violent death that shaped the daily existence of [[the-folk|the Folk]].

In the first circus visit (001-101), the narrator's other-self recognizes the caged lion instantly:

> Ah, I knew him on the instant. The beast! The terrible one! (001-101)

This visceral recognition — before any waking-life knowledge — demonstrates the depth of the racial memory of predation.

## Hunting Behavior

The lion hunted by ambush, using tall grass for cover. The narrator's dreams preserve vivid images of predation:

> the midday sun shining on tall grass, the wild bull grazing quietly, the sudden parting of the grass before the swift rush of the tawny one, his leap to the bull's back, the crashing and the bellowing, and the crunch crunch of bones (001-104)

The lion also hunted at water-holes, attacking drinking animals such as wild horses (001-107), and was known to roar at twilight — a sound that triggered terror throughout the forest community.

## Place in the Food Chain

The lion occupied the same trophic level as [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], and both were threats that the Folk could not confront directly (008-133).
Lions hunted in pairs — the narrator recalls seeing a lion and lioness kill two fighting elk under moonlight (016-104).

## Cultural Memory

The lion's roar was one of the sounds that triggered the deepest [[fear]] in the Folk, alongside the hissing of [[snake|snakes]] (002-071). The predator's role in shaping hominid [[evolution]] is well-documented in modern paleoanthropology — constant predation pressure selected for vigilance, social cooperation, and eventually tool use.
