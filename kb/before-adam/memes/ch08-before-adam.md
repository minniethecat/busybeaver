## CHAPTER VIII

[008-001]Well do I remember that first winter after I left home. I have long

[008-002]dreams of sitting shivering in the cold. Lop-Ear and I sit close

[008-003]together, with our arms and legs about each other, blue-faced and with

[008-004]chattering teeth. It got particularly crisp along toward morning. In

[008-005]those chill early hours we slept little, huddling together in numb

[008-006]misery and waiting for the sunrise in order to get warm.

[008-007]When we went outside there was a crackle of frost under foot. One

[008-008]morning we discovered ice on the surface of the quiet water in the eddy

[008-009]where was the drinking-place, and there was a great How-do-you-do about

[008-010]it. Old Marrow-Bone was the oldest member of the horde, and he had

[008-011]never seen anything like it before. I remember the worried, plaintive

[008-012]look that came into his eyes as he examined the ice. (This plaintive

[008-013]look always came into our eyes when we did not understand a thing, or

[008-014]when we felt the prod of some vague and inexpressible desire.) Red-Eye,

[008-015]too, when he investigated the ice, looked bleak and plaintive, and

[008-016]stared across the river into the northeast, as though in some way he

[008-017]connected the Fire People with this latest happening.

[008-018]But we found ice only on that one morning, and that was the coldest

[008-019]winter we experienced. I have no memory of other winters when it was so

[008-020]cold. I have often thought that that cold winter was a fore-runner of

[008-021]the countless cold winters to come, as the ice-sheet from farther north

[008-022]crept down over the face of the land. But we never saw that ice-sheet.

[008-023]Many generations must have passed away before the descendants of the

[008-024]horde migrated south, or remained and adapted themselves to the changed

[008-025]conditions.

[008-026]Life was hit or miss and happy-go-lucky with us. Little was ever

[008-027]planned, and less was executed. We ate when we were hungry, drank when

[008-028]we were thirsty, avoided our carnivorous enemies, took shelter in the

[008-029]caves at night, and for the rest just sort of played along through

[008-030]life.

[008-031]We were very curious, easily amused, and full of tricks and pranks.

[008-032]There was no seriousness about us, except when we were in danger or

[008-033]were angry, in which cases the one was quickly forgotten and the other

[008-034]as quickly got over.

[008-035]We were inconsecutive, illogical, and inconsequential. We had no

[008-036]steadfastness of purpose, and it was here that the Fire People were

[008-037]ahead of us. They possessed all these things of which we possessed so

[008-038]little. Occasionally, however, especially in the realm of the emotions,

[008-039]we were capable of long-cherished purpose. The faithfulness of the

[008-040]monogamic couples I have referred to may be explained as a matter of

[008-041]habit; but my long desire for the Swift One cannot be so explained, any

[008-042]more than can be explained the undying enmity between me and Red-Eye.

[008-043]But it was our inconsequentiality and stupidity that especially

[008-044]distresses me when I look back upon that life in the long ago. Once I

[008-045]found a broken gourd which happened to lie right side up and which had

[008-046]been filled with the rain. The water was sweet, and I drank it. I even

[008-047]took the gourd down to the stream and filled it with more water, some

[008-048]of which I drank and some of which I poured over Lop-Ear. And then I

[008-049]threw the gourd away. It never entered my head to fill the gourd with

[008-050]water and carry it into my cave. Yet often I was thirsty at night,

[008-051]especially after eating wild onions and watercress, and no one ever

[008-052]dared leave the caves at night for a drink.

[008-053]Another time I found a dry gourd, inside of which the seeds rattled. I

[008-054]had fun with it for a while. But it was a plaything, nothing more. And

[008-055]yet, it was not long after this that the using of gourds for storing

[008-056]water became the general practice of the horde. But I was not the

[008-057]inventor. The honor was due to old Marrow-Bone, and it is fair to

[008-058]assume that it was the necessity of his great age that brought about

[008-059]the innovation.

[008-060]At any rate, the first member of the horde to use gourds was

[008-061]Marrow-Bone. He kept a supply of drinking-water in his cave, which cave

[008-062]belonged to his son, the Hairless One, who permitted him to occupy a

[008-063]corner of it. We used to see Marrow-Bone filling his gourd at the

[008-064]drinking-place and carrying it carefully up to his cave. Imitation was

[008-065]strong in the Folk, and first one, and then another and another,

[008-066]procured a gourd and used it in similar fashion, until it was a general

[008-067]practice with all of us so to store water.

[008-068]Sometimes old Marrow-Bone had sick spells and was unable to leave the

[008-069]cave. Then it was that the Hairless One filled the gourd for him. A

[008-070]little later, the Hairless One deputed the task to Long-Lip, his son.

[008-071]And after that, even when Marrow-Bone was well again, Long-Lip

[008-072]continued carrying water for him. By and by, except on unusual

[008-073]occasions, the men never carried any water at all, leaving the task to

[008-074]the women and larger children. Lop-Ear and I were independent. We

[008-075]carried water only for ourselves, and we often mocked the young

[008-076]water-carriers when they were called away from play to fill the gourds.

[008-077]Progress was slow with us. We played through life, even the adults,

[008-078]much in the same way that children play, and we played as none of the

[008-079]other animals played. What little we learned, was usually in the course

[008-080]of play, and was due to our curiosity and keenness of appreciation. For

[008-081]that matter, the one big invention of the horde, during the time I

[008-082]lived with it, was the use of gourds. At first we stored only water in

[008-083]the gourds—in imitation of old Marrow-Bone.

[008-084]But one day some one of the women—I do not know which one—filled a

[008-085]gourd with black-berries and carried it to her cave. In no time all the

[008-086]women were carrying berries and nuts and roots in the gourds. The idea,

[008-087]once started, had to go on. Another evolution of the

[008-088]carrying-receptacle was due to the women. Without doubt, some woman’s

[008-089]gourd was too small, or else she had forgotten her gourd; but be that

[008-090]as it may, she bent two great leaves together, pinning the seams with

[008-091]twigs, and carried home a bigger quantity of berries than could have

[008-092]been contained in the largest gourd.

[008-093]So far we got, and no farther, in the transportation of supplies during

[008-094]the years I lived with the Folk. It never entered anybody’s head to

[008-095]weave a basket out of willow-withes. Sometimes the men and women tied

[008-096]tough vines about the bundles of ferns and branches that they carried

[008-097]to the caves to sleep upon. Possibly in ten or twenty generations we

[008-098]might have worked up to the weaving of baskets. And of this, one thing

[008-099]is sure: if once we wove withes into baskets, the next and inevitable

[008-100]step would have been the weaving of cloth. Clothes would have followed,

[008-101]and with covering our nakedness would have come modesty.

[008-102]Thus was momentum gained in the Younger World. But we were without this

[008-103]momentum. We were just getting started, and we could not go far in a

[008-104]single generation. We were without weapons, without fire, and in the

[008-105]raw beginnings of speech. The device of writing lay so far in the

[008-106]future that I am appalled when I think of it.

[008-107]Even I was once on the verge of a great discovery. To show you how

[008-108]fortuitous was development in those days let me state that had it not

[008-109]been for the gluttony of Lop-Ear I might have brought about the

[008-110]domestication of the dog. And this was something that the Fire People

[008-111]who lived to the northeast had not yet achieved. They were without

[008-112]dogs; this I knew from observation. But let me tell you how Lop-Ear’s

[008-113]gluttony possibly set back our social development many generations.

[008-114]Well to the west of our caves was a great swamp, but to the south lay a

[008-115]stretch of low, rocky hills. These were little frequented for two

[008-116]reasons. First of all, there was no food there of the kind we ate; and

[008-117]next, those rocky hills were filled with the lairs of carnivorous

[008-118]beasts.

[008-119]But Lop-Ear and I strayed over to the hills one day. We would not have

[008-120]strayed had we not been teasing a tiger. Please do not laugh. It was

[008-121]old Saber-Tooth himself. We were perfectly safe. We chanced upon him in

[008-122]the forest, early in the morning, and from the safety of the branches

[008-123]overhead we chattered down at him our dislike and hatred. And from

[008-124]branch to branch, and from tree to tree, we followed overhead, making

[008-125]an infernal row and warning all the forest-dwellers that old

[008-126]Saber-Tooth was coming.

[008-127]We spoiled his hunting for him, anyway. And we made him good and angry.

[008-128]He snarled at us and lashed his tail, and sometimes he paused and

[008-129]stared up at us quietly for a long time, as if debating in his mind

[008-130]some way by which he could get hold of us. But we only laughed and

[008-131]pelted him with twigs and the ends of branches.

[008-132]This tiger-baiting was common sport among the folk. Sometimes half the

[008-133]horde would follow from overhead a tiger or lion that had ventured out

[008-134]in the daytime. It was our revenge; for more than one member of the

[008-135]horde, caught unexpectedly, had gone the way of the tiger’s belly or

[008-136]the lion’s. Also, by such ordeals of helplessness and shame, we taught

[008-137]the hunting animals to some extent to keep out of our territory. And

[008-138]then it was funny. It was a great game.

[008-139]And so Lop-Ear and I had chased Saber-Tooth across three miles of

[008-140]forest. Toward the last he put his tail between his legs and fled from

[008-141]our gibing like a beaten cur. We did our best to keep up with him; but

[008-142]when we reached the edge of the forest he was no more than a streak in

[008-143]the distance.

[008-144]I don’t know what prompted us, unless it was curiosity; but after

[008-145]playing around awhile, Lop-Ear and I ventured across the open ground to

[008-146]the edge of the rocky hills. We did not go far. Possibly at no time

[008-147]were we more than a hundred yards from the trees. Coming around a sharp

[008-148]corner of rock (we went very carefully, because we did not know what we

[008-149]might encounter), we came upon three puppies playing in the sun.

[008-150]They did not see us, and we watched them for some time. They were wild

[008-151]dogs. In the rock-wall was a horizontal fissure—evidently the lair

[008-152]where their mother had left them, and where they should have remained

[008-153]had they been obedient. But the growing life, that in Lop-Ear and me

[008-154]had impelled us to venture away from the forest, had driven the puppies

[008-155]out of the cave to frolic. I know how their mother would have punished

[008-156]them had she caught them.

[008-157]But it was Lop-Ear and I who caught them. He looked at me, and then we

[008-158]made a dash for it. The puppies knew no place to run except into the

[008-159]lair, and we headed them off. One rushed between my legs. I squatted

[008-160]and grabbed him. He sank his sharp little teeth into my arm, and I

[008-161]dropped him in the suddenness of the hurt and surprise. The next moment

[008-162]he had scurried inside.

[008-163]Lop-Ear, struggling with the second puppy, scowled at me and intimated

[008-164]by a variety of sounds the different kinds of a fool and a bungler that

[008-165]I was. This made me ashamed and spurred me to valor. I grabbed the

[008-166]remaining puppy by the tail. He got his teeth into me once, and then I

[008-167]got him by the nape of the neck. Lop-Ear and I sat down, and held the

[008-168]puppies up, and looked at them, and laughed.

[008-169]They were snarling and yelping and crying. Lop-Ear started suddenly. He

[008-170]thought he had heard something. We looked at each other in fear,

[008-171]realizing the danger of our position. The one thing that made animals

[008-172]raging demons was tampering with their young. And these puppies that

[008-173]made such a racket belonged to the wild dogs. Well we knew them,

[008-174]running in packs, the terror of the grass-eating animals. We had

[008-175]watched them following the herds of cattle and bison and dragging down

[008-176]the calves, the aged, and the sick. We had been chased by them

[008-177]ourselves, more than once. I had seen one of the Folk, a woman, run

[008-178]down by them and caught just as she reached the shelter of the woods.

[008-179]Had she not been tired out by the run, she might have made it into a

[008-180]tree. She tried, and slipped, and fell back. They made short work of

[008-181]her.

[008-182]We did not stare at each other longer than a moment. Keeping tight hold

[008-183]of our prizes, we ran for the woods. Once in the security of a tall

[008-184]tree, we held up the puppies and laughed again. You see, we had to have

[008-185]our laugh out, no matter what happened.

[008-186]And then began one of the hardest tasks I ever attempted. We started to

[008-187]carry the puppies to our cave. Instead of using our hands for climbing,

[008-188]most of the time they were occupied with holding our squirming

[008-189]captives. Once we tried to walk on the ground, but were treed by a

[008-190]miserable hyena, who followed along underneath. He was a wise hyena.

[008-191]Lop-Ear got an idea. He remembered how we tied up bundles of leaves to

[008-192]carry home for beds. Breaking off some tough vines, he tied his puppy’s

[008-193]legs together, and then, with another piece of vine passed around his

[008-194]neck, slung the puppy on his back. This left him with hands and feet

[008-195]free to climb. He was jubilant, and did not wait for me to finish tying

[008-196]my puppy’s legs, but started on. There was one difficulty, however. The

[008-197]puppy wouldn’t stay slung on Lop-Ear’s back. It swung around to the

[008-198]side and then on in front. Its teeth were not tied, and the next thing

[008-199]it did was to sink its teeth into Lop-Ear’s soft and unprotected

[008-200]stomach. He let out a scream, nearly fell, and clutched a branch

[008-201]violently with both hands to save himself. The vine around his neck

[008-202]broke, and the puppy, its four legs still tied, dropped to the ground.

[008-203]The hyena proceeded to dine.

[008-204]Lop-Ear was disgusted and angry. He abused the hyena, and then went off

[008-205]alone through the trees. I had no reason that I knew for wanting to

[008-206]carry the puppy to the cave, except that I _wanted_ to; and I stayed by

[008-207]my task. I made the work a great deal easier by elaborating on

[008-208]Lop-Ear’s idea. Not only did I tie the puppy’s legs, but I thrust a

[008-209]stick through his jaws and tied them together securely.

[008-210]At last I got the puppy home. I imagine I had more pertinacity than the

[008-211]average Folk, or else I should not have succeeded. They laughed at me

[008-212]when they saw me lugging the puppy up to my high little cave, but I did

[008-213]not mind. Success crowned my efforts, and there was the puppy. He was a

[008-214]plaything such as none of the Folk possessed. He learned rapidly. When

[008-215]I played with him and he bit me, I boxed his ears, and then he did not

[008-216]try again to bite for a long time.

[008-217]I was quite taken up with him. He was something new, and it was a

[008-218]characteristic of the Folk to like new things. When I saw that he

[008-219]refused fruits and vegetables, I caught birds for him and squirrels and

[008-220]young rabbits. (We Folk were meat-eaters, as well as vegetarians, and

[008-221]we were adept at catching small game.) The puppy ate the meat and

[008-222]thrived. As well as I can estimate, I must have had him over a week.

[008-223]And then, coming back to the cave one day with a nestful of

[008-224]young-hatched pheasants, I found Lop-Ear had killed the puppy and was

[008-225]just beginning to eat him. I sprang for Lop-Ear,—the cave was

[008-226]small,—and we went at it tooth and nail.

[008-227]And thus, in a fight, ended one of the earliest attempts to domesticate

[008-228]the dog. We pulled hair out in handfuls, and scratched and bit and

[008-229]gouged. Then we sulked and made up. After that we ate the puppy. Raw?

[008-230]Yes. We had not yet discovered fire. Our evolution into cooking animals

[008-231]lay in the tight-rolled scroll of the future.
