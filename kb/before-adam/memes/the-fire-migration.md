## Overview

The Fire Migration was not a single dramatic event but a slow, territorial expansion — the [[fire-people|Fire People]] outgrowing their peninsula stronghold and pressing outward into the lands of the [[the-folk|Folk]]. The narrator recognized it in hindsight as the force that destroyed his people: "the migration of the Fire People, and the consequent migration of the survivors of the Folk, set back our evolution for centuries" (011-133-011-135).

> Indeed, there is no telling how disastrous was to be the outcome of the Fire People's migration. Personally, I am prone to believe that it brought about the destruction of the Folk; that we, a branch of lower life budding toward the human, were nipped short off and perished down by the roaring surf where the river entered the sea. (011-135-011-143)

The migration was a population phenomenon. The [[fire-people|Fire People]]'s peninsula — "a splendid strategic selection" protected on three sides by the curving river (013-081-013-083) — had allowed them to prosper. But their very success became the Folk's doom. "The [[fire-people|Fire People]] must have increased in numbers until they pressed uncomfortably against the bounds of their habitat. They were expanding, and in the course of their expanding they drove the Folk before them, and settled down themselves in the caves and occupied the territory that we had occupied" (013-087-013-091).
## Context and Causes

The [[fire-people|Fire People]] were more advanced than the Folk in every dimension that mattered. They had [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s — weapons that "annihilate distance" and could kill from a hundred feet, "an enormous extension of their leaping and striking muscles" (015-036-015-038). They had speech that enabled reasoning and cooperation (015-039-015-040). They had fire. And they were organized: the wizened old hunter directed [[the-fire-people-massacre|the massacre at the caves]] with command authority, and the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] obeyed (017-014-017-015).

The fundamental cause was simple: the [[fire-people|Fire People]] needed more land. Their fertile peninsula had supported population growth until the pressure to expand became irresistible. The Folk's territory — with its caves, [[drinking-place|drinking-places]], blueberry swamp, and carrot patch — lay directly in the [[run-way|path]] of this expansion.

## Narrative Account

### Early Signs

The first sign was the ambush at the [[the-carrot-patch-ambush|carrot patch]]. A late-season second crop of carrots drew the horde to feed, and [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] — hidden in the forest — killed the [[hairless-one|Hairless One]] and [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s sister with [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s (015-013-015-019). The wizened old hunter was among them (015-038). This was not a raid for food but a deliberate thinning of the competing population.

> This inroad of the Fire People on the carrot-patch was the beginning of the end, though we did not know it. (015-036-015-037)

### Escalation

The [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] began appearing more frequently — "in twos and threes, creeping silently through the forest" (015-037-015-038). The Folk became circumspect, vigilant, and timid. "No longer were the trees a protection to be relied upon. No longer could we perch on a branch and laugh down at our carnivorous enemies on the ground" (015-041-015-042).

One morning the wizened old hunter walked alone into [[the-open-space|the open space before the caves]], examined [[the-cliffs|the cliff-wall]] and [[run-way|run-ways]], then limped back into the forest (015-048-015-053). He was reconnoitering.

### The Massacre

The migration culminated in the [[the-fire-people-massacre|massacre at the caves]]. One early morning, the [[fire-people|Fire People]] attacked in force — "they had order and plan, while we Folk had none" (017-006). They smoked out the caves tier by tier, shooting the Folk as they fled. "It was slaughter, indiscriminate slaughter, for they spared none, killing old and young, effectively ridding the land of our presence" (017-041-017-042).

> It was like the end of the world to us. (017-043)
## Consequences

The Fire Migration reshaped the entire landscape of the story. The Folk were reduced from a horde to a scattered handful. The survivors fled into [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]], and the remnant that emerged — thirty skeletons, no children among them — settled in the sandstone caves by [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] (018-017-018-020).

The [[fire-people|Fire People]], meanwhile, occupied the caves and the territory the Folk had held for generations. The narrator's assessment was blunt: the [[fire|Fire]] Migration may well have "brought about the destruction of the Folk" (011-136-011-137). The Folk were a branch "nipped short off" before they could develop the tools — speech, cooperation, weapons — that might have let them survive.
