## Overview

Far Lake was the second of the two mountain lakes — paired with [[Long Lake]] — that the Folk traversed during their hungry passage over [[the-mountain|the mountains]] (012-010). It lay beyond [[long-lake|Long Lake]] in the direction of the Folk's northward travel, and it was to Far Lake that they were driven by the hostility of the [[Tree People]] (012-013). What set Far Lake apart from its twin was what came after: the stream that drained it was packed with salmon that had ascended from the sea to spawn (012-105-012-106).

> Long Lake and Far Lake, and the calf we caught sleeping in the thicket. (012-010)

The lake itself was a place of thin reward — another calf sleeping in a thicket on its shore (012-101) — but the real prize lay downstream. The salmon run at Far Lake's outlet transformed the migration from a story of hunger into a story of abundance.

## Geographic Context

Far Lake lay north of [[Long Lake]] in the mountainous country beyond the familiar territory. The Folk reached it after being chased into [[the-mountain|the mountains]] by the [[Tree People]], who dwelt in the forest between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[the-mountain|the mountains]] (012-011-012-013).

> compelled us to travel on to Far Lake. (012-013)

It took two days to reach the lake itself, and the Folk arrived weak with hunger (012-100). On its shore, in a thicket, they found a part-grown calf (012-101) — a discovery parallel to the one at [[long-lake|Long Lake]]. But the more significant geographic feature was the stream that drained Far Lake, whose shore was "packed thick with salmon that had come up from the sea to spawn" (012-105-012-106).

The stream's outlet presumably connected Far Lake to [[the-river|the river]] system that led, ultimately, to the sea — the same sea that the Folk would later reach in their wanderings (011-143).

## Role in the Narrative

Like [[Long Lake]], Far Lake appears only in the migration chapter (ch12), but its role is more consequential. While [[long-lake|Long Lake]] gave the Folk a single calf, Far Lake gave them both a calf (012-101) and, critically, the salmon run (012-105-012-106). The salmon represented the end of the hunger that had dogged the mountain crossing — a transition from scarcity to plenty that [[big-tooth|the narrator]] marks with the burial of uneaten meat in the forest (012-104).

The two lakes together form a pair in [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s memory — "the hunger we endured on [[the-mountain|the mountains]] between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and Far Lake" (012-009) — binding them as twin landmarks of the hardest stretch of the migration.

## Inhabitants and Usage

Far Lake had no permanent inhabitants. The Folk passed through during the migration, staying long enough to hunt the lakeshore calf (012-101) and to gather at the salmon-rich stream (012-105). The buried meat that was left behind in the eastern forest (012-104) suggests that the abundance of salmon made further provisions unnecessary — the Folk ate what they needed and moved on, never returning to the hidden cache.

The [[Tree People]] dwelt in the forests to the south, between [[the-mountain|the mountains]] and [[long-lake|Long Lake]] (012-011), but did not range as far as Far Lake itself. The lake's isolation in the high country made it a brief stopping-place rather than contested territory.
