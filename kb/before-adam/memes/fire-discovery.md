## Overview

The discovery of fire by [[big-tooth|the narrator]], occurring in Chapter 13, is the single most consequential event in _Before Adam_. Prior to this encounter, the Folk lived without fire — "without weapons, without fire" (008-104). The narrator's chance observation of a [[fire|Fire]] Man's camp, and his subsequent experimentation with the dying embers, marks the beginning of a technological transition that will transform every aspect of Folk existence.

## In the Novel

The event unfolds in stages. First, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and Big-Fat are drawn to the glow of a fire in the darkness and creep closer to observe (013-006). From hiding, they watch the unfamiliar scene: the [[fire|Fire]] Men squatting around the flames, feeding wood to the fire, sleeping beside its warmth (013-043, 013-046).

After the [[fire|Fire]] Men depart at dawn, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] overcomes his fear and runs to the still-smoldering embers. He pokes at the fire with a stick — a simple gesture that represents the first instance of a Folk manipulating fire (013-076). He and Big-Fat experiment, adding first small sticks, then larger branches, until "we had a mighty fire" (013-083). Their excitement — dashing back and forth, laughing — betrays their inexperience; they fail to notice the surrounding dry grass catching fire (013-090). The resulting wildfire nearly kills them as the flames "ate around the circle and hemmed them in" (013-095).

## Context and Significance

This sequence captures the dual face of technological discovery: the exhilaration of new power and the danger born of inexperience. London presents the mastery of fire — historically one of the most significant thresholds in human evolution — as an accident stumbled upon rather than a planned invention, consistent with how many paleoanthropologists believe fire use emerged among early hominins through opportunistic encounters with natural fires.
