## Overview

The Cliffs were the towering rock face in which the caves of the Folk opened — a natural fortress that provided shelter, security, and a commanding view of the surrounding territory. The caves themselves were set into the cliff at varying heights, accessible only by climbing the near-vertical wall (015-020). This inaccessibility was the Folk's primary defense: no four-legged predator could scale the cliff face, and even two-legged enemies found the ascent perilous.

The Cliffs were more than geology; they were the fixed point around which all of the Folk's territorial understanding revolved. From their heights, one could survey the [[the-open-space|Open Space]] below, the [[the-river|river]] beyond, and the forested horizon in every direction. The opening memory of the novel is set here:

> the peace of the cool caves in the cliffs, the circus of the open space, and the shouting and laughter from the drinking-places — these were the memories that stayed (001-024)

## Geographic Context

The Cliffs rose sharply from the [[the-open-space|Open Space]], their base forming the eastern boundary of the gathering ground (005-003). At the foot of the cliff lay the place where bodies were sometimes found — those who had fallen or been thrown from above (009-035). The cliff face was irregular, with out-juts of rock that could provide momentary footholds or concealment (009-140).

To the north, beyond the immediate cliff face, the land stretched toward the [[the-great-swamp|great swamp]]; to the south lay the [[the-river|river]] and the forests. The cliff-top itself offered access to routes away from the caves — escape [[run-way|path]]s that the Folk would eventually use when the [[Fire People]] made the caves untenable (017-087)(017-102).

The narrator's own cave was among the highest on the cliff (017-013), a position that afforded safety during the siege but also made retreat more difficult.

## Defensive Architecture

The Cliffs functioned as a natural fortification that shaped the Folk's defensive tactics. Against four-legged predators like [[Saber-Tooth]], the cliff face was an absolute barrier: the Folk could scramble up to safety while the great cat clawed uselessly at the crumbling rock (006-041-006-043). The verticality reversed the normal predator-prey dynamic — the Folk, normally helpless against the larger carnivores, could rain stones down upon attackers from positions of complete safety, turning defense into counter-attack.

Against the [[Fire People]], however, this advantage eroded. The Fire People carried [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and arrows capable of reaching the cave entrances from below (017-019). Moreover, they brought fire — torches and burning brands that sent smoke curling up the cliff face, suffocating the defenders within their caves (017-053). The same inaccessibility that had protected the Folk became a trap: those who tried to climb out were shot down (017-087). The cliff that had been their sanctuary became their grave for all but the few who made the desperate scramble to the top (017-102).

## Role in the Narrative

The Cliffs appear at both the beginning and the end of the Folk's story. The narrative opens with the memory of the cool caves in the cliffs (001-024) — a memory of peace and belonging. Throughout the middle chapters, the daily rhythm of climbing up and down the cliff forms the background of life: returning at twilight (015-003), retreating from danger (014-049), or scrambling upward while an enemy snaps at one's heels (015-020).

The Cliffs become most prominent in the final act, during the siege by the [[fire-people|Fire People]]. The cliff face turned into a vertical battlefield: the Folk shot [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s down from their elevated caves while the Fire People, filling the Open Space with their numbers, attempted to climb up (017-019)(017-061). The smoke of the Fire People's torches curled up the cliff face (017-053), and many of the Folk were shot down as they tried to escape upward (017-087). The narrator and a handful of survivors made a desperate scramble for the cliff-top (017-102) — the last act of defiance before abandoning the caves forever.

The Cliffs also serve as a spatial metaphor for the Folk's evolutionary position: they live above the animals, protected by height and cleverness, but they are not yet masters of their environment. The vertical refuge is borrowed, not built — the Folk could not construct such a fortress themselves. When a more advanced hominid arrives with tools the Folk do not possess, the natural defense is breached, and the only option is flight.

## Inhabitants and Usage

The Cliffs housed the entire community of the Folk, with individual caves occupied by different members. [[Red-Eye]] occupied a cave that others dared not approach (014-038); [[big-tooth|the narrator]] shared a cave with [[the-Swift-One|the Swift One]] (015-003); and [[Lop-Ear]] had been [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s companion since their earliest days in the caves (001-024).

The verticality of the cliff dictated social hierarchy in a literal sense: the higher one's cave, the safer from ground-level threats, but the more isolated from the communal life of the [[the-open-space|Open Space]]. The narrator's high cave proved an advantage during the siege, allowing him to shoot [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s down upon the attackers while remaining above the reach of their return fire (017-013)(017-056).

After the final battle, the Cliffs were abandoned — the surviving Folk fled across the [[the-great-swamp|great swamp]] and never returned to the only permanent home they had ever known. The abandonment of the Cliffs marks the end of an era: the Folk's ancestral territory, defended for generations, is surrendered, and the community's history becomes a diaspora.
