## Overview

Territory and Identity were inseparable for [[the-horde|the Folk]]. Their sense of who they were — as individuals and as a group — was rooted in the specific places they inhabited: the [[the-cliffs|caves]] where they slept, the [[the-open-space|open space]] where they gathered, the [[drinking-place|drinking-places]] where they drank. These were not merely settings but carriers of meaning, each location encoding the experiences, losses, and triumphs of the collective.

The narrator's persistent returns to his "old home territory" after the [[adventure-journey]], searching the area between the [[blueberry-swamp|blueberry swamp]] and the slough (014-009-014-012), demonstrate this attachment. He was drawn back not by practical necessity but by the gravitational pull of the familiar — the need to be in a place that knew him as he knew it.

## The Rupture

The [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]] and the subsequent flight into [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]] represented more than a physical displacement. It was a rupture in the Folk's continuity — a severing from the place that had shaped them for generations. The caves had been their only permanent home; the river their only familiar boundary; the open space their only stage. To lose all of these at once was to lose the spatial framework within which they understood themselves.

The narrator's dream-memories return obsessively to these lost places, suggesting that the trauma of displacement was as deeply encoded in the Folk's collective consciousness as any physical danger. What was lost was not merely shelter but identity.

## Remaking Identity

The [[the-great-migration|Great Migration]] was not merely a journey through space but a passage from one identity to another. The Folk who reached [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] were no longer cave-dwellers; they had become coastal people, their identity remade by the territory they now inhabited. The new landscape — with its roaring surf, its unfamiliar vegetation, its different rhythms of tide and season — demanded new ways of being, and the survivors adapted.

This remaking of identity through territory is one of London's most subtle insights: that place does not merely reflect identity but produces it. The Folk did not bring their cave-dwelling identity to the sea; they became sea-dwellers, their sense of self reconfigured by the demands and possibilities of the new environment.
