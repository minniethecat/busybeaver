## CHAPTER XIV

[014-001]The months came and went. The drama and tragedy of the future were yet

[014-002]to come upon the stage, and in the meantime we pounded nuts and lived.

[014-003]It was a good year, I remember, for nuts. We used to fill gourds with

[014-004]nuts and carry them to the pounding-places. We placed them in

[014-005]depressions in the rock, and, with a piece of rock in our hands, we

[014-006]cracked them and ate them as we cracked.

[014-007]It was the fall of the year when Lop-Ear and I returned from our long

[014-008]adventure-journey, and the winter that followed was mild. I made

[014-009]frequent trips to the neighborhood of my old home-tree, and frequently

[014-010]I searched the whole territory that lay between the blueberry swamp and

[014-011]the mouth of the slough where Lop-Ear and I had learned navigation, but

[014-012]no clew could I get of the Swift One. She had disappeared. And I wanted

[014-013]her. I was impelled by that hunger which I have mentioned, and which

[014-014]was akin to physical hunger, albeit it came often upon me when my

[014-015]stomach was full. But all my search was vain.

[014-016]Life was not monotonous at the caves, however. There was Red-Eye to be

[014-017]considered. Lop-Ear and I never knew a moment’s peace except when we

[014-018]were in our own little cave. In spite of the enlargement of the

[014-019]entrance we had made, it was still a tight squeeze for us to get in.

[014-020]And though from time to time we continued to enlarge, it was still too

[014-021]small for Red-Eye’s monstrous body. But he never stormed our cave

[014-022]again. He had learned the lesson well, and he carried on his neck a

[014-023]bulging lump to show where I had hit him with the rock. This lump never

[014-024]went away, and it was prominent enough to be seen at a distance. I

[014-025]often took great delight in watching that evidence of my handiwork; and

[014-026]sometimes, when I was myself assuredly safe, the sight of it caused me

[014-027]to laugh.

[014-028]While the other Folk would not have come to our rescue had Red-Eye

[014-029]proceeded to tear Lop-Ear and me to pieces before their eyes,

[014-030]nevertheless they sympathized with us. Possibly it was not sympathy but

[014-031]the way they expressed their hatred for Red-Eye; at any rate they

[014-032]always warned us of his approach. Whether in the forest, at the

[014-033]drinking-places, or in the open space before the caves, they were

[014-034]always quick to warn us. Thus we had the advantage of many eyes in our

[014-035]feud with Red-Eye, the atavism.

[014-036]Once he nearly got me. It was early in the morning, and the Folk were

[014-037]not yet up. The surprise was complete. I was cut off from the way up

[014-038]the cliff to my cave. Before I knew it I had dashed into the

[014-039]double-cave,—the cave where Lop-Ear had first eluded me long years

[014-040]before, and where old Saber-Tooth had come to discomfiture when he

[014-041]pursued the two Folk. By the time I had got through the connecting

[014-042]passage between the two caves, I discovered that Red-Eye was not

[014-043]following me. The next moment he charged into the cave from the

[014-044]outside. I slipped back through the passage, and he charged out and

[014-045]around and in upon me again. I merely repeated my performance of

[014-046]slipping through the passage.

[014-047]He kept me there half a day before he gave up. After that, when Lop-Ear

[014-048]and I were reasonably sure of gaining the double-cave, we did not

[014-049]retreat up the cliff to our own cave when Red-Eye came upon the scene.

[014-050]All we did was to keep an eye on him and see that he did not cut across

[014-051]our line of retreat.

[014-052]It was during this winter that Red-Eye killed his latest wife with

[014-053]abuse and repeated beatings. I have called him an atavism, but in this

[014-054]he was worse than an atavism, for the males of the lower animals do not

[014-055]maltreat and murder their mates. In this I take it that Red-Eye, in

[014-056]spite of his tremendous atavistic tendencies, foreshadowed the coming

[014-057]of man, for it is the males of the human species only that murder their

[014-058]mates.

[014-059]As was to be expected, with the doing away of one wife Red-Eye

[014-060]proceeded to get another. He decided upon the Singing One. She was the

[014-061]granddaughter of old Marrow-Bone, and the daughter of the Hairless One.

[014-062]She was a young thing, greatly given to singing at the mouth of her

[014-063]cave in the twilight, and she had but recently mated with Crooked-Leg.

[014-064]He was a quiet individual, molesting no one and not given to bickering

[014-065]with his fellows. He was no fighter anyway. He was small and lean, and

[014-066]not so active on his legs as the rest of us.

[014-067]Red-Eye never committed a more outrageous deed. It was in the quiet at

[014-068]the end of the day, when we began to congregate in the open space

[014-069]before climbing into our caves. Suddenly the Singing One dashed up a

[014-070]run-way from a drinking-place, pursued by Red-Eye. She ran to her

[014-071]husband. Poor little Crooked-Leg was terribly scared. But he was a

[014-072]hero. He knew that death was upon him, yet he did not run away. He

[014-073]stood up, and chattered, bristled, and showed his teeth.

[014-074]Red-Eye roared with rage. It was an offence to him that any of the Folk

[014-075]should dare to withstand him. His hand shot out and clutched

[014-076]Crooked-Leg by the neck. The latter sank his teeth into Red-Eye’s arm;

[014-077]but the next moment, with a broken neck, Crooked-Leg was floundering

[014-078]and squirming on the ground. The Singing One screeched and gibbered.

[014-079]Red-Eye seized her by the hair of her head and dragged her toward his

[014-080]cave. He handled her roughly when the climb began, and he dragged and

[014-081]hauled her up into the cave.

[014-082]We were very angry, insanely, vociferously angry. Beating our chests,

[014-083]bristling, and gnashing our teeth, we gathered together in our rage. We

[014-084]felt the prod of gregarious instinct, the drawing together as though

[014-085]for united action, the impulse toward cooperation. In dim ways this

[014-086]need for united action was impressed upon us. But there was no way to

[014-087]achieve it because there was no way to express it. We did not turn to,

[014-088]all of us, and destroy Red-Eye, because we lacked a vocabulary. We were

[014-089]vaguely thinking thoughts for which there were no thought-symbols.

[014-090]These thought-symbols were yet to be slowly and painfully invented.

[014-091]We tried to freight sound with the vague thoughts that flitted like

[014-092]shadows through our consciousness. The Hairless One began to chatter

[014-093]loudly. By his noises he expressed anger against Red-Eye and desire to

[014-094]hurt Red-Eye. Thus far he got, and thus far we understood. But when he

[014-095]tried to express the cooperative impulse that stirred within him, his

[014-096]noises became gibberish. Then Big-Face, with brow-bristling and

[014-097]chest-pounding, began to chatter. One after another of us joined in the

[014-098]orgy of rage, until even old Marrow-Bone was mumbling and spluttering

[014-099]with his cracked voice and withered lips. Some one seized a stick and

[014-100]began pounding a log. In a moment he had struck a rhythm.

[014-101]Unconsciously, our yells and exclamations yielded to this rhythm. It

[014-102]had a soothing effect upon us; and before we knew it, our rage

[014-103]forgotten, we were in the full swing of a hee-hee council.

[014-104]These hee-hee councils splendidly illustrate the inconsecutiveness and

[014-105]inconsequentiality of the Folk. Here were we, drawn together by mutual

[014-106]rage and the impulse toward cooperation, led off into forgetfulness by

[014-107]the establishment of a rude rhythm. We were sociable and gregarious,

[014-108]and these singing and laughing councils satisfied us. In ways the

[014-109]hee-hee council was an adumbration of the councils of primitive man,

[014-110]and of the great national assemblies and international conventions of

[014-111]latter-day man. But we Folk of the Younger World lacked speech, and

[014-112]whenever we were so drawn together we precipitated babel, out of which

[014-113]arose a unanimity of rhythm that contained within itself the essentials

[014-114]of art yet to come. It was art nascent.

[014-115]There was nothing long-continued about these rhythms that we struck. A

[014-116]rhythm was soon lost, and pandemonium reigned until we could find the

[014-117]rhythm again or start a new one. Sometimes half a dozen rhythms would

[014-118]be swinging simultaneously, each rhythm backed by a group that strove

[014-119]ardently to drown out the other rhythms.

[014-120]In the intervals of pandemonium, each chattered, cut up, hooted,

[014-121]screeched, and danced, himself sufficient unto himself, filled with his

[014-122]own ideas and volitions to the exclusion of all others, a veritable

[014-123]centre of the universe, divorced for the time being from any unanimity

[014-124]with the other universe-centres leaping and yelling around him. Then

[014-125]would come the rhythm—a clapping of hands; the beating of a stick upon

[014-126]a log; the example of one that leaped with repetitions; or the chanting

[014-127]of one that uttered, explosively and regularly, with inflection that

[014-128]rose and fell, “A-bang, a-bang! A-bang, a-bang!” One after another of

[014-129]the self-centred Folk would yield to it, and soon all would be dancing

[014-130]or chanting in chorus. “Ha-ah, ha-ah, ha-ah-ha!” was one of our

[014-131]favorite choruses, and another was, “Eh-wah, eh-wah, eh-wah-hah!”

[014-132]And so, with mad antics, leaping, reeling, and over-balancing, we

[014-133]danced and sang in the sombre twilight of the primeval world, inducing

[014-134]forgetfulness, achieving unanimity, and working ourselves up into

[014-135]sensuous frenzy. And so it was that our rage against Red-Eye was

[014-136]soothed away by art, and we screamed the wild choruses of the hee-hee

[014-137]council until the night warned us of its terrors, and we crept away to

[014-138]our holes in the rocks, calling softly to one another, while the stars

[014-139]came out and darkness settled down.

[014-140]We were afraid only of the dark. We had no germs of religion, no

[014-141]conceptions of an unseen world. We knew only the real world, and the

[014-142]things we feared were the real things, the concrete dangers, the

[014-143]flesh-and-blood animals that preyed. It was they that made us afraid of

[014-144]the dark, for darkness was the time of the hunting animals. It was then

[014-145]that they came out of their lairs and pounced upon one from the dark

[014-146]wherein they lurked invisible.

[014-147]Possibly it was out of this fear of the real denizens of the dark that

[014-148]the fear of the unreal denizens was later to develop and to culminate

[014-149]in a whole and mighty unseen world. As imagination grew it is likely

[014-150]that the fear of death increased until the Folk that were to come

[014-151]projected this fear into the dark and peopled it with spirits. I think

[014-152]the Fire People had already begun to be afraid of the dark in this

[014-153]fashion; but the reasons we Folk had for breaking up our hee-hee

[014-154]councils and fleeing to our holes were old Saber-Tooth, the lions and

[014-155]the jackals, the wild dogs and the wolves, and all the hungry,

[014-156]meat-eating breeds.
