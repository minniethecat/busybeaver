# Singing One

The **Singing One** is a young woman of the [[fire-people|Folk]], notable for her family lineage — granddaughter of [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]], daughter of [[hairless-one|the Hairless One]], and sister of [[long-lip|Long-Lip]] — and for becoming one of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s most tragic victims.

## Character

Big-Tooth introduces the Singing One with affection:

> She was a young thing, greatly given to singing at the mouth of her cave in the twilight, and she had but recently mated with Crooked-Leg.[^1]

Her singing in the twilight is one of the few moments of beauty and gentleness recorded in the narrative of the Folk — a glimpse of aesthetic expression in a life otherwise defined by survival and fear. She had recently mated with [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]], a quiet and gentle man, and the two represented a peaceful pairing within the horde.

## Abduction by Red-Eye

Red-Eye, having disposed of a previous wife, decided to claim the Singing One as his next mate. He pursued her from the [[drinking-place|drinking-place]] as the horde gathered in the open space before climbing to their caves:

> Suddenly the Singing One dashed up a run-way from a drinking-place, pursued by Red-Eye. She ran to her husband.[^2]

Her instinct was to seek protection from Crooked-Leg, but Red-Eye killed him and dragged her by the hair to his cave, handling her "roughly when the climb began."[^3] The horde watched in collective rage — beating chests, bristling, gnashing teeth — but none dared intervene.

## Fate

The Singing One's ultimate fate was the same as Red-Eye's other wives. Big-Tooth notes: "She had gone the way of the rest" — a grim euphemism for death at Red-Eye's hands.[^4] Her fate was part of a pattern: Red-Eye went through wives rapidly, and after the Singing One, he moved on to a "little, soft, spiritless thing" and then to [[swift-one|the Swift One]].[^4]

## Family Line

The Singing One belonged to a notable three-generation lineage:
- Grandfather: [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]], the oldest member of the Folk
- Father: [[hairless-one|the Hairless One]]
- Brother: [[long-lip|Long-Lip]], the designated water-carrier

## Others' View of the Singing One

The Singing One was seen by the horde through a lens of tenderness and tragedy. Big-Tooth's description — "greatly given to singing at the mouth of her cave in the twilight" — is one of the novel's few moments of unguarded affection, a glimpse of a person valued not for her utility to the group but for the beauty she brought into their lives.[^1] In a world where aesthetic expression was rudimentary at best, her twilight singing represented something rare: the human impulse toward art, emerging even in the harshest conditions.

Her pairing with Crooked-Leg was, by the standards of the horde, an ideal match — two gentle souls in a world that demanded violence. The Folk recognized this. When Red-Eye pursued her from the drinking-place, her instinctive flight toward her husband was not merely a biological reflex but a testament to the bond the horde had witnessed them form.[^2]

Her abduction transformed the horde's view of her from affection to collective guilt. When Crooked-Leg was killed and the Singing One dragged by her hair to Red-Eye's cave, every member of the Folk who watched and did nothing became complicit in her fate. Big-Tooth records the scene with the precision of remembered shame: the chest-beating, the bristling, the gnashing of teeth — all the physical expressions of rage that the Folk were capable of, and none of the action that might have saved her.[^3]

After her death — "she had gone the way of the rest" — the Singing One became a symbol of Red-Eye's tyranny within the horde's collective memory, a name invoked alongside Crooked-Leg's whenever the Folk recalled the cost of their submission to the tyrant.[^4] The twilight singing that had once marked her as unique became, in hindsight, the sound of a gentleness that the Pleistocene world could not sustain.
