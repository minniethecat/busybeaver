## Overview

The Tree People are a hominin group encountered by [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[Lop-Ear]] during their journey westward after [[the-river-crossing|crossing the river]]. They dwell in the forest between [[Long Lake]] and [[the-mountain|the mountains]], and their ferocious territoriality — triggered by an act of casual cruelty — drives the two travelers into the bleak, inhospitable mountains and onward toward [[Far Lake]] (012-048-012-090).

> These creatures were ferocious apes, nothing more. And yet they were not so different from us. (012-015-012-016)

The Tree People represent an alternative evolutionary trajectory: they are fully arboreal, physically adapted to life in the trees, and organized in tribal bands with coordinated communication ("Goëk! Goëk!" calls). They are hostile to [[the-horde|the Folk]], but [[big-tooth|the narrator]] recognizes them as kin — "After all, the Tree People and [[the-horde|the Folk]] were not so unlike" (012-029) — making them one of the three related hominin kinds in the narrative world, alongside [[the-horde|the Folk]] and the [[Fire People]].

## Physical Characteristics

The narrator provides a detailed physical comparison between the Tree People and his own kind. They were "more hairy, it is true; their legs were a trifle more twisted and gnarly, their eyes a bit smaller, their necks a bit thicker and shorter, and their nostrils slightly more like orifices in a sunken surface" (012-017-012-023). Despite these differences, they shared important features with [[the-horde|the Folk]]: "they had no hair on their faces and on the palms of their hands and the soles of their feet, and they made sounds similar to ours with somewhat similar meanings" (012-023-012-027).

Their arboreal adaptation gave them a decisive advantage in the trees. During the chase, "they were truly the Tree People, and while they out-climbed us we out-footed them on the ground" (012-060-012-061). This physical specialization — superior climbing at the cost of ground speed — defines their ecological niche and their tactical limitations.

## First Encounter

The first encounter was initiated by [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[Lop-Ear]] themselves. They came upon a "little withered, dried-up old fellow, wrinkled-faced and bleary-eyed and tottery" sitting at the foot of his tree (012-032-012-034). Declaring him "legitimate prey" — "in our world there was no sym[[run-way|path]]y between the kinds" (012-034-012-035) — the two men rushed him.

What followed was an episode of casual cruelty that reveals as much about [[the-horde|the Folk]] as about the Tree People. The narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] pinched the old man, pulled his hair, tweaked his ears, and poked twigs into him, "and all the while we laughed with streaming eyes" (012-046-012-047). The old Tree-Man's "futile anger was most absurd" — he made "woeful faces in place of the ferocious ones he intended, grinding his worn teeth together, beating his meagre chest with feeble fists" (012-050-012-054).

> He had a cough, and he gasped and hacked and spluttered prodigiously. Every time he tried to climb the tree we pulled him back, until at last he surrendered to his weakness and did no more than sit and weep. (012-058-012-060)

This cruelty had consequences. The old man's screams summoned [[the-horde|the tribe]], and what had been a sport became a life-threatening chase.

## The Chase

The Tree People's response was immediate, coordinated, and relentless. From the forest came the cry "Goëk! Goëk!" — answered by others, "several of them, and from very far off we could hear a big, bass 'Goëk! Goëk! Goëk!'" (012-072-012-076). The "Whoo-whoo!" call rose all around them, indicating a communication system capable of rallying the entire tribe across distance.

The chase lasted for hours. The Tree People "raced us through the trees, the whole tribe of them, and nearly caught us" (012-079-012-080). The narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] were forced to take to the ground, where their running speed gave them an advantage. But the pursuit was relentless — "more than once it was nip and tuck" (012-084-012-085). The chase drove them steadily northward, out of the forest and onto "the grassy flanks of [[the-mountain|the mountains]]" (012-091-012-092), where the Tree People finally gave up and returned to their forest (012-093-012-094).

## Aftermath and Second Encounter

The Tree People did not simply let their quarry escape — they guarded the forest boundary. Three times that afternoon [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] tried to regain the woods, and each time "the Tree People were lying in wait, and they drove us back" (012-097-012-098). This persistent territorial defense forced the two travelers to spend a miserable night in a dwarf tree "no larger than a bush" (012-099) and to continue into [[the-mountain|the mountains]] the next morning.

A second encounter with a different tribe of Tree People occurs much later, after the survivors of the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]] have crossed [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]]. The narrator crouches in a thicket and watches them — this time as a cautious observer rather than an aggressor (018-075-018-080). This later encounter is more peaceful, suggesting that [[big-tooth|the narrator]] has learned from his earlier mistake.

## Relation to the Folk

The narrator's understanding of the relationship between the Tree People and his own kind evolves through the encounter. He begins by declaring the old Tree-Man "not [[the-horde|our kind]]" (012-035), but by the end of the retreat he recognizes that "we realized that we were not their kind, either, and that the bonds between us were anything but sym[[run-way|path]]etic" (012-085-012-087).

Later, reflecting on all three groups — Folk, [[fire-people|Fire People]], and Tree People — he reaches a more nuanced conclusion: "Certainly, all three kinds were related" (017-042). The Tree People are not mere "ferocious apes" (012-015) but an alternative branch of the same evolutionary tree, adapted to a different ecological niche. Their hostility is not mindless aggression but territorial defense — a behavior [[the-horde|the Folk]] themselves would understand.

## Ecological Niche and Evolutionary Role

The Tree People occupy a distinct ecological niche — the deep forest — and their physical specialization for arboreal life represents what evolutionary biologists would call adaptive radiation: a single ancestral stock diverging into forms adapted to different environments. The narrator intuits this relationship without the vocabulary to express it: he recognizes that the differences between Tree People and Folk are quantitative, not qualitative (012-017-012-027).

Their coordinated communication system — the "Goëk! Goëk!" alarm calls and "Whoo-whoo!" rally cries — suggests a level of social organization comparable to, or perhaps exceeding, that of the Folk. While the Folk communicate through gesture and simple sounds, the Tree People have developed a call-and-response system capable of coordinating tribal-level action across distances (012-072-012-076). This challenges the narrator's initial dismissal of them as "ferocious apes, nothing more" (012-015) and forces a recognition that intelligence and social complexity can take forms different from one's own kind.

> "After all, the Tree People and the Folk were not so unlike" (012-029) — the narrator's admission is both a moment of moral growth and an evolutionary insight: speciation does not imply fundamental difference, and the boundaries between kinds are permeable.
