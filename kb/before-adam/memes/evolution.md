**Evolution** is the intellectual foundation of _Before Adam_. Jack London, writing after Darwin and influenced by Ernst Haeckel and Herbert Spencer, frames the entire narrative as an exploration of how early hominids gradually developed the traits — physical, social, and cognitive — that would eventually produce modern humans. The novel is, in effect, a fictionalized account of human evolution.

## In the Novel

London embeds evolution throughout the narrative's structure. The narrator, a modern man, accesses his evolutionary past through dreams — "the memories of the whole evolution of the race" stored in what Weismann called the "germplasm" (002-124). He discovers evolution in college and recognizes that his dreams are not fantasy but "subconscious projection of my knowledge of evolution" (002-134) — though he eventually concludes the opposite: the dreams are real, inherited memories.

The novel portrays evolution as an ongoing process visible in the characters themselves. The Folk are "in the process of changing" from more bestial ancestors (005-088). Some individuals, like [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], represent atavistic throwbacks to an earlier stage (005-086-005-087). Others, like the [[fire-people|Fire People]], show more advanced traits — fire use, weapons, structured camps — representing a later evolutionary stage.

London tracks several evolutionary milestones: bipedalism and tree-climbing (004-010-004-014), the development of speech (004-029-004-042), tool use and invention (008-057-008-090), and the gradual emergence of cooking — though fire remained undiscovered by the Folk during the narrator's lifetime: "Our evolution into cooking animals lay in the tight-rolled scroll of the future" (008-230-008-231).

The social dimension of evolution is equally important. The shift from solitary or small-group existence toward larger cooperative tribes, the development of pair-bonding, and the emergence of proto-democratic councils all represent evolutionary advances toward human society.

> He was an atavism, and Red-Eye was an even greater atavism. (005-086)
