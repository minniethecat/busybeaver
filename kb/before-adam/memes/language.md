> We had no fixed names in the horde. We did not need them. When we wished to designate a particular person we did so by imitating his peculiarities, or by naming some incident in which he had figured. (002-020-002-024)

## Overview

The Folk do not have language as we understand it. They have no grammar, no fixed vocabulary, no capacity for abstract statement. What they do have is a rich system of proto-linguistic communication: cries, calls, gestures, imitations, and a small set of meaningful sounds that hover at the threshold of speech. The narrator describes this as "gibbering" — but it is a gibbering that carries real information.

## The Sound-World of the Folk

The Folk's communication includes several distinct registers. The alarm cry — a sharp, barked warning — spreads instantly through the [[the-horde|horde]] when danger appears. The "hee-hee" sound marks the [[council|councils]], the collective deliberation that is the horde's nearest approach to governance. Laughter exists — the narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] share moments of rough humor — but it is closer to the play-calls of animals than to human mirth.

Names are not fixed but descriptive. [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] is so called because of his prominent teeth; [[swift-one|the Swift One]] for her speed; [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] for his inflamed, baleful gaze. The narrator explains that to refer to someone, the Folk would imitate their distinctive trait or reenact a memorable incident involving them (002-020-002-024). This is communication through performance, not abstraction.

## The Emergence of Speech

London traces the boundary between animal communication and human language. The [[fire-people|Fire People]] — more advanced than the Folk — have developed further. They "chatter excitedly" and call "softly and coaxingly" to the [[swift-one|Swift One]], recognizing something in her that sets her apart (017-033-017-036). The narrator speculates that the Fire People may have actual words — a vocabulary that allows them to coordinate the systematic attack on the caves.

The Folk stand at an earlier stage. Their communication is adequate for immediate needs — warning of danger, coordinating a hunt, expressing rage or fear — but incapable of the abstraction needed for planning, storytelling, or collective decision-making. The [[red-eye-takes-singing-one|hee-hee council]] that follows Red-Eye's killing of Crooked-Leg demonstrates this limitation: the Folk want to act collectively against him but lack "the necessary vocabulary" for cooperative action (014-042-014-050).

## Language and Evolution

The development of language is, in London's vision, the critical threshold between animal and human. The Folk are "in the process of becoming men" (009-007-009-008), and their proto-language is the instrument of that becoming. Each new sound, each conventionalized gesture, each step toward fixed naming is a step toward the cognitive capacity that will eventually produce the narrator himself — a man who can write down the memories of his ancestors.
