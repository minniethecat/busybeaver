## Role in the Younger World

The Hyena appears in the narrator's ancestral memories as a persistent and opportunistic follower of [[the-folk|the Folk]].
Unlike the [[lion]] or [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] that hunted from ambush, the hyena was a scavenger-predator that shadowed the Folk, waiting to feed on what was left behind.

In one memory, the hyena follows [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] and the narrator down from a tree where they had been eating, and "proceeded to dine" on what they had discarded (008-203).

## Scavenger Behavior

The hyena's scavenging role is depicted matter-of-factly. After the narrator and Lop-Ear finish eating in a tree, the hyena below feeds on the remains:

> The hyena proceeded to dine. (008-203)

This relationship — the Folk taking the best parts, the hyena cleaning up — reflects the ecological niche partitioning of the Mid-Pleistocene.

## As Enemy

In Chapter XVI, the hyena is described as "my old enemy" (016-063). During the narrator's mad pursuit of the [[swift-one|Swift One]], the hyena followed for an hour, sensing that "something was going to happen" — an indication of its opportunistic nature and its long history of interaction with the Folk.

## Ecological Significance

The hyena, alongside [[wild-dogs|Wild Dogs]], [[lion|lions]], and [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], formed the guild of predators that shaped the [[survival]] pressures on the Folk.
Constant predation and scavenging pressure selected for vigilance, [[cooperation]], and arboreal [[shelter]] strategies.
