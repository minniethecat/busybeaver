## CHAPTER IX

[009-001]Red-Eye was an atavism. He was the great discordant element in our

[009-002]horde. He was more primitive than any of us. He did not belong with us,

[009-003]yet we were still so primitive ourselves that we were incapable of a

[009-004]cooperative effort strong enough to kill him or cast him out. Rude as

[009-005]was our social organization, he was, nevertheless, too rude to live in

[009-006]it. He tended always to destroy the horde by his unsocial acts. He was

[009-007]really a reversion to an earlier type, and his place was with the Tree

[009-008]People rather than with us who were in the process of becoming men.

[009-009]He was a monster of cruelty, which is saying a great deal in that day.

[009-010]He beat his wives—not that he ever had more than one wife at a time,

[009-011]but that he was married many times. It was impossible for any woman to

[009-012]live with him, and yet they did live with him, out of compulsion. There

[009-013]was no gainsaying him.

[009-014]No man was strong enough to stand against him.

[009-015]Often do I have visions of the quiet hour before the twilight. From

[009-016]drinking-place and carrot patch and berry swamp the Folk are trooping

[009-017]into the open space before the caves. They dare linger no later than

[009-018]this, for the dreadful darkness is approaching, in which the world is

[009-019]given over to the carnage of the hunting animals, while the

[009-020]fore-runners of man hide tremblingly in their holes.

[009-021]There yet remain to us a few minutes before we climb to our caves. We

[009-022]are tired from the play of the day, and the sounds we make are subdued.

[009-023]Even the cubs, still greedy for fun and antics, play with restraint.

[009-024]The wind from the sea has died down, and the shadows are lengthening

[009-025]with the last of the sun’s descent. And then, suddenly, from Red-Eye’s

[009-026]cave, breaks a wild screaming and the sound of blows. He is beating his

[009-027]wife.

[009-028]At first an awed silence comes upon us. But as the blows and screams

[009-029]continue we break out into an insane gibbering of helpless rage. It is

[009-030]plain that the men resent Red-Eye’s actions, but they are too afraid of

[009-031]him. The blows cease, and a low groaning dies away, while we chatter

[009-032]among ourselves and the sad twilight creeps upon us.

[009-033]We, to whom most happenings were jokes, never laughed during Red-Eye’s

[009-034]wife-beatings. We knew too well the tragedy of them. On more than one

[009-035]morning, at the base of the cliff, did we find the body of his latest

[009-036]wife. He had tossed her there, after she had died, from his cave-mouth.

[009-037]He never buried his dead. The task of carrying away the bodies, that

[009-038]else would have polluted our abiding-place, he left to the horde. We

[009-039]usually flung them into the river below the last drinking-place.

[009-040]Not alone did Red-Eye murder his wives, but he also murdered for his

[009-041]wives, in order to get them. When he wanted a new wife and selected the

[009-042]wife of another man, he promptly killed that man. Two of these murders

[009-043]I saw myself. The whole horde knew, but could do nothing. We had not

[009-044]yet developed any government, to speak of, inside the horde. We had

[009-045]certain customs and visited our wrath upon the unlucky ones who

[009-046]violated those customs. Thus, for example, the individual who defiled a

[009-047]drinking-place would be attacked by every onlooker, while one who

[009-048]deliberately gave a false alarm was the recipient of much rough usage

[009-049]at our hands. But Red-Eye walked rough-shod over all our customs, and

[009-050]we so feared him that we were incapable of the collective action

[009-051]necessary to punish him.

[009-052]It was during the sixth winter in our cave that Lop-Ear and I

[009-053]discovered that we were really growing up. From the first it had been a

[009-054]squeeze to get in through the entrance-crevice. This had had its

[009-055]advantages, however. It had prevented the larger Folk from taking our

[009-056]cave away from us. And it was a most desirable cave, the highest on the

[009-057]bluff, the safest, and in winter the smallest and warmest.

[009-058]To show the stage of the mental development of the Folk, I may state

[009-059]that it would have been a simple thing for some of them to have driven

[009-060]us out and enlarged the crevice-opening. But they never thought of it.

[009-061]Lop-Ear and I did not think of it either until our increasing size

[009-062]compelled us to make an enlargement. This occurred when summer was well

[009-063]along and we were fat with better forage. We worked at the crevice in

[009-064]spells, when the fancy struck us.

[009-065]At first we dug the crumbling rocks away with our fingers, until our

[009-066]nails got sore, when I accidentally stumbled upon the idea of using a

[009-067]piece of wood on the rock. This worked well. Also it worked woe. One

[009-068]morning early, we had scratched out of the wall quite a heap of

[009-069]fragments. I gave the heap a shove over the lip of the entrance. The

[009-070]next moment there came up from below a howl of rage. There was no need

[009-071]to look. We knew the voice only too well. The rubbish had descended

[009-072]upon Red-Eye.

[009-073]We crouched down in the cave in consternation. A minute later he was at

[009-074]the entrance, peering in at us with his inflamed eyes and raging like a

[009-075]demon. But he was too large. He could not get in to us. Suddenly he

[009-076]went away. This was suspicious. By all we knew of Folk nature he should

[009-077]have remained and had out his rage. I crept to the entrance and peeped

[009-078]down. I could see him just beginning to mount the bluff again. In one

[009-079]hand he carried a long stick. Before I could divine his plan, he was

[009-080]back at the entrance and savagely jabbing the stick in at us.

[009-081]His thrusts were prodigious. They could have disembowelled us. We

[009-082]shrank back against the side-walls, where we were almost out of range.

[009-083]But by industrious poking he got us now and again—cruel, scraping jabs

[009-084]with the end of the stick that raked off the hide and hair. When we

[009-085]screamed with the hurt, he roared his satisfaction and jabbed the

[009-086]harder.

[009-087]I began to grow angry. I had a temper of my own in those days, and

[009-088]pretty considerable courage, too, albeit it was largely the courage of

[009-089]the cornered rat. I caught hold of the stick with my hands, but such

[009-090]was his strength that he jerked me into the crevice. He reached for me

[009-091]with his long arm, and his nails tore my flesh as I leaped back from

[009-092]the clutch and gained the comparative safety of the side-wall.

[009-093]He began poking again, and caught me a painful blow on the shoulder.

[009-094]Beyond shivering with fright and yelling when he was hit, Lop-Ear did

[009-095]nothing. I looked for a stick with which to jab back, but found only

[009-096]the end of a branch, an inch through and a foot long. I threw this at

[009-097]Red-Eye. It did no damage, though he howled with a sudden increase of

[009-098]rage at my daring to strike back. He began jabbing furiously. I found a

[009-099]fragment of rock and threw it at him, striking him on the chest.

[009-100]This emboldened me, and, besides, I was now as angry as he, and had

[009-101]lost all fear. I ripped a fragment of rock from the wall. The piece

[009-102]must have weighed two or three pounds. With my strength I slammed it

[009-103]full into Red-Eye’s face. It nearly finished him. He staggered

[009-104]backward, dropping his stick, and almost fell off the cliff.

[009-105]He was a ferocious sight. His face was covered with blood, and he was

[009-106]snarling and gnashing his fangs like a wild boar. He wiped the blood

[009-107]from his eyes, caught sight of me, and roared with fury. His stick was

[009-108]gone, so he began ripping out chunks of crumbling rock and throwing

[009-109]them in at me. This supplied me with ammunition. I gave him as good as

[009-110]he sent, and better; for he presented a good target, while he caught

[009-111]only glimpses of me as I snuggled against the side-wall.

[009-112]Suddenly he disappeared again. From the lip of the cave I saw him

[009-113]descending. All the horde had gathered outside and in awed silence was

[009-114]looking on. As he descended, the more timid ones scurried for their

[009-115]caves. I could see old Marrow-Bone tottering along as fast as he could.

[009-116]Red-Eye sprang out from the wall and finished the last twenty feet

[009-117]through the air. He landed alongside a mother who was just beginning

[009-118]the ascent. She screamed with fear, and the two-year-old child that was

[009-119]clinging to her released its grip and rolled at Red-Eye’s feet. Both he

[009-120]and the mother reached for it, and he got it. The next moment the frail

[009-121]little body had whirled through the air and shattered against the wall.

[009-122]The mother ran to it, caught it up in her arms, and crouched over it

[009-123]crying.

[009-124]Red-Eye started over to pick up the stick. Old Marrow-Bone had tottered

[009-125]into his way. Red-Eye’s great hand shot out and clutched the old man by

[009-126]the back of the neck. I looked to see his neck broken. His body went

[009-127]limp as he surrendered himself to his fate. Red-Eye hesitated a moment,

[009-128]and Marrow-Bone, shivering terribly, bowed his head and covered his

[009-129]face with his crossed arms. Then Red-Eye slammed him face-downward to

[009-130]the ground. Old Marrow-Bone did not struggle. He lay there crying with

[009-131]the fear of death. I saw the Hairless One, out in the open space,

[009-132]beating his chest and bristling, but afraid to come forward. And then,

[009-133]in obedience to some whim of his erratic spirit, Red-Eye let the old

[009-134]man alone and passed on and recovered the stick.

[009-135]He returned to the wall and began to climb up. Lop-Ear, who was

[009-136]shivering and peeping alongside of me, scrambled back into the cave. It

[009-137]was plain that Red-Eye was bent upon murder. I was desperate and angry

[009-138]and fairly cool. Running back and forth along the neighboring ledges, I

[009-139]gathered a heap of rocks at the cave-entrance. Red-Eye was now several

[009-140]yards beneath me, concealed for the moment by an out-jut of the cliff.

[009-141]As he climbed, his head came into view, and I banged a rock down. It

[009-142]missed, striking the wall and shattering; but the flying dust and grit

[009-143]filled his eyes and he drew back out of view.

[009-144]A chuckling and chattering arose from the horde, that played the part

[009-145]of audience. At last there was one of the Folk who dared to face

[009-146]Red-Eye. As their approval and acclamation arose on the air, Red-Eye

[009-147]snarled down at them, and on the instant they were subdued to silence.

[009-148]Encouraged by this evidence of his power, he thrust his head into view,

[009-149]and by scowling and snarling and gnashing his fangs tried to intimidate

[009-150]me. He scowled horribly, contracting the scalp strongly over the brows

[009-151]and bringing the hair down from the top of the head until each hair

[009-152]stood apart and pointed straight forward.

[009-153]The sight chilled me, but I mastered my fear, and, with a stone poised

[009-154]in my hand, threatened him back. He still tried to advance. I drove the

[009-155]stone down at him and made a sheer miss. The next shot was a success.

[009-156]The stone struck him on the neck. He slipped back out of sight, but as

[009-157]he disappeared I could see him clutching for a grip on the wall with

[009-158]one hand, and with the other clutching at his throat. The stick fell

[009-159]clattering to the ground.

[009-160]I could not see him any more, though I could hear him choking and

[009-161]strangling and coughing. The audience kept a death-like silence. I

[009-162]crouched on the lip of the entrance and waited. The strangling and

[009-163]coughing died down, and I could hear him now and again clearing his

[009-164]throat. A little later he began to climb down. He went very quietly,

[009-165]pausing every moment or so to stretch his neck or to feel it with his

[009-166]hand.

[009-167]At the sight of him descending, the whole horde, with wild screams and

[009-168]yells, stampeded for the woods. Old Marrow-Bone, hobbling and

[009-169]tottering, followed behind. Red-Eye took no notice of the flight. When

[009-170]he reached the ground he skirted the base of the bluff and climbed up

[009-171]and into his own cave. He did not look around once.

[009-172]I stared at Lop-Ear, and he stared back. We understood each other.

[009-173]Immediately, and with great caution and quietness, we began climbing up

[009-174]the cliff. When we reached the top we looked back. The abiding-place

[009-175]was deserted, Red-Eye remained in his cave, and the horde had

[009-176]disappeared in the depths of the forest.

[009-177]We turned and ran. We dashed across the open spaces and down the slopes

[009-178]unmindful of possible snakes in the grass, until we reached the woods.

[009-179]Up into the trees we went, and on and on, swinging our arboreal flight

[009-180]until we had put miles between us and the caves. And then, and not till

[009-181]then, in the security of a great fork, we paused, looked at each other,

[009-182]and began to laugh. We held on to each other, arms and legs, our eyes

[009-183]streaming tears, our sides aching, and laughed and laughed and laughed.
