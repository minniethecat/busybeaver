## Overview

Crooked-Leg's Heroism is the novel's purest example of individual courage. [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] was, by the narrator's own description, "a quiet little fellow," thin and unaggressive, "no fighter anyway" (014-025-014-026). He had recently mated with the [[singing-one|Singing One]], the granddaughter of old [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]], and by all appearances he was the least likely candidate for heroism in the entire horde.

When [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] — the most powerful and feared member of the Folk — came to seize his mate, Crooked-Leg's response defied every expectation. "He knew that death was upon him, yet he did not run away" (014-030).

## The Stand

The scene unfolds with terrible clarity. It was dusk, and the Folk were gathering in [[the-open-space|the open space]] before the caves. The [[singing-one|Singing One]] burst up a run-way, pursued by [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], and fled to her husband.

[[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]], "terribly scared," did what no one expected: he stood up. He bristled. He chattered. He showed his teeth (014-029-014-031). It was a gesture of defiance that every member of the horde understood, and it was utterly, hopelessly futile. [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] killed him with a single hand — clutching his neck, breaking it — and dragged the [[singing-one|Singing One]] by her hair up to his cave (014-034-014-037).

## The Meaning of the Stand

Crooked-Leg's death achieved nothing in practical terms. The [[singing-one|Singing One]] was taken. [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] faced no consequences. The Folk, who felt collective rage at the act, could not translate that rage into collective action — their [[hee-hee-council|hee-hee council]] produced only noise.

Yet the narrator frames Crooked-Leg's stand as something more than futile. "Poor little Crooked-Leg was terribly scared. But he was a hero" (014-029-014-030). The assessment is stark and final: heroism is not victory but the choice to stand when standing means death.

In a world governed by brute force — where [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] takes what he wants and no one can stop him — Crooked-Leg's refusal to run represents the first glimmer of something beyond the survival calculus. He did not fight to win; he fought because some things were worth dying for. The concept would not have a name among the Folk for another hundred thousand years, but the impulse was already there.
