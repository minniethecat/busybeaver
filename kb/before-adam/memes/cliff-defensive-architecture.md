## Overview

The Cliff Defensive Architecture was the Folk's primary survival strategy — a natural fortification that transformed the vertical rock face into an asymmetrical battlefield. Against four-legged predators like [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], the cliff was an absolute barrier: the Folk could scramble up to safety while the great cat clawed uselessly at crumbling rock (006-041-006-043). From above, they could rain stones down upon attackers from positions of complete safety, turning passive defense into active counter-attack.

This defensive advantage was so fundamental to the Folk's existence that it shaped their entire way of life. They chose the caves specifically for their inaccessibility. They organized their community around the vertical dimension — the higher the cave, the safer the occupant. And they developed combat tactics (stone-throwing from height) that required precisely the terrain the cliffs provided.

## Against Predators

Against ground-based predators, the cliffs were effectively impregnable. [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] — the most feared carnivore of the Younger World — could do nothing but claw at the rock face while the Folk pelted him with stones from above. The verticality reversed the normal predator-prey relationship: the Folk, normally helpless against the larger carnivores, became untouchable tormentors.

This advantage extended to other ground-based threats as well. The Folk's [[arboreal-advantage|arboreal advantage]] in the trees had a parallel in the cliffs: in both cases, height provided safety that no four-legged predator could overcome.

## Against the Fire People

Against the [[fire-people|Fire People]], however, the defensive architecture collapsed. The reasons were cumulative:

First, [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and arrows could reach the cave entrances from below — the vertical distance that had protected the Folk from teeth and claws offered no protection against ranged weapons (017-019).

Second, the Fire People brought [[fire-as-weapon|fire as a weapon]] — torches and burning brands that sent smoke curling up the cliff face, suffocating the defenders within their caves (017-053). The same inaccessibility that had protected the Folk became a death trap.

Third, the Fire People had numbers and coordination. They filled the Open Space and executed a methodical, tier-by-tier extermination that left the Folk no avenue of escape except the desperate climb to the cliff-top (017-102).

## The Tragic Irony

The cliff that had been the Folk's sanctuary became their grave. Of the entire horde, only a handful reached the top and escaped into the forest beyond. The defensive architecture that had sustained the Folk for countless generations was rendered obsolete in a single morning by an enemy with better weapons and better tactics. The tragedy of the cliffs is the tragedy of all fixed defenses: they work until they don't, and when they fail, they fail absolutely.
