## Overview

Shelter for the Folk in _Before Adam_ means specifically an arboreal construct — a platform or nest built high in the branches of trees, accessible only by climbing. These tree-shelters represent the thin margin between survival and predation in a world roamed by saber-toothed cats and other carnivores. The construction and use of tree-shelters is a defining cultural practice that separates the Folk from the ground-dwelling Fire People.

## In the Novel

The narrator and his fellow Folk retreat to tree-shelters whenever danger threatens. After encounters with predators, they "took shelter in the trees" (008-028) — the phrase recurs like a ritual throughout the narrative. The shelters are not merely hiding places but constructed artifacts: the narrator recalls "the tree-shelters of our elders" as models to emulate (010-046). Over time, the habit of returning each night to a particular tree-shelter becomes routine, a proto-settlement pattern (010-125). When the narrator later explores independently, he builds his own tree-shelter near a cave mouth, demonstrating the transferable knowledge of shelter construction (015-059).

## Context and Significance

In paleoanthropology, evidence for constructed shelters among early hominins is sparse but significant. The oldest known structures date to roughly 400,000 years ago. London's depiction of tree-shelters aligns with the hypothesis that arboreal nesting — a behavior observed in chimpanzees and orangutans — formed the evolutionary precursor to ground-based dwellings. By emphasizing the Folk's arboreal shelter culture, London roots their survival in a credible, primatological reality.
