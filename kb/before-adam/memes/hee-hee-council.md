## Overview

The Hee-Hee Council was the closest approximation to governance that the Folk possessed — a gathering of the [[the-horde|horde]] in which members gibbered, gestured, and vociferated to reach a kind of consensus. The narrator describes these councils as "the beginning of government" (014-028), recognizing in them the earliest glimmer of deliberative process that would, across evolutionary time, develop into formal political institutions.

Yet the councils were profoundly limited. They lacked the conceptual vocabulary for true deliberation. When [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] killed [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] and seized the [[singing-one|Singing One]], the council's response was "a confusion of many voices" — noise without resolution (014-036-014-038). The council could express collective unease but could not produce collective action.

## Function and Limits

The council's primary function was mediation — a way for the horde to process conflicts and disturbances without resorting to the kind of unchecked violence that might destroy the group. When something happened that concerned the entire horde, members would gather in the open space and begin the "hee-hee" vocalizations that gave the institution its name.

But the council had no enforcement mechanism. It could neither punish [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] for his crimes nor compel him to act differently. Its authority was entirely moral — the weight of collective disapproval — and [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], who governed by physical dominance, was impervious to moral pressure.

## Evolutionary Significance

The Hee-Hee Council represents a crucial intermediate stage in the evolution of human social organization. It is not government — it cannot make binding decisions or enforce them. But it is also not mere chaos — it is a structured gathering in which the group processes information collectively, however crudely.

London's portrayal of the council is characteristically unsentimental. He does not present it as the noble precursor to democracy but as a noisy, ineffectual gathering that cannot contain the violence of individuals like [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]. Yet he also recognizes it as the seed from which more sophisticated forms of social coordination would grow. The council is the Folk's first halting step toward the principle that a group can be more than the sum of its strongest members.
