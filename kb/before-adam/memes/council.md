## Overview

The hee-hee council is one of the most distinctive cultural practices of the Folk in _Before Adam_. It is a communal gathering where members chant in rhythmic, musical patterns — producing what the narrator describes as a "gibbering" that serves social bonding and conflict-resolution functions (001-023). These councils represent an early stage in the evolution of collective deliberation and proto-linguistic communication.

## In the Novel

When the narrator first encounters a hee-hee council in full swing, he describes it as a spectacle of synchronized vocalization that "splendidly illustrates the incomplete development of the human mind" while simultaneously revealing its social sophistication (014-104). The council is not merely noise — it satisfies genuine social needs. The narrator notes that "these singing and laughing councils satisfied us" (014-108), functioning as a form of collective emotional regulation.

The hee-hee council is also described as "an adumbration of the councils of primitive man" (014-109) — London explicitly positions this behavior as an evolutionary precursor to more formal deliberative assemblies. At the council, the horde gathers to resolve disputes, share warnings about predators, and reinforce social bonds through shared vocal rhythm.

## Context and Significance

Ethnographic accounts of hunter-gatherer societies document similar communal vocal rituals — from the trance-dances of the San people to the song-duels of Inuit communities. These practices serve functions of group cohesion, conflict mediation, and information sharing that would later be taken over by formal language and political institutions. The hee-hee council represents London's imaginative reconstruction of how collective deliberation might have looked before full syntactic language existed.

## Key Passages
