## CHAPTER XII

[012-001]I have no idea how long Lop-Ear and I wandered in the land north of the

[012-002]river. We were like mariners wrecked on a desert isle, so far as

[012-003]concerned the likelihood of our getting home again. We turned our backs

[012-004]upon the river, and for weeks and months adventured in that wilderness

[012-005]where there were no Folk. It is very difficult for me to reconstruct

[012-006]our journeying, and impossible to do it from day to day. Most of it is

[012-007]hazy and indistinct, though here and there I have vivid recollections

[012-008]of things that happened.

[012-009]Especially do I remember the hunger we endured on the mountains between

[012-010]Long Lake and Far Lake, and the calf we caught sleeping in the thicket.

[012-011]Also, there are the Tree People who dwelt in the forest between Long

[012-012]Lake and the mountains. It was they who chased us into the mountains

[012-013]and compelled us to travel on to Far Lake.

[012-014]First, after we left the river, we worked toward the west till we came

[012-015]to a small stream that flowed through marshlands. Here we turned away

[012-016]toward the north, skirting the marshes and after several days arriving

[012-017]at what I have called Long Lake. We spent some time around its upper

[012-018]end, where we found food in plenty; and then, one day, in the forest,

[012-019]we ran foul of the Tree People. These creatures were ferocious apes,

[012-020]nothing more. And yet they were not so different from us. They were

[012-021]more hairy, it is true; their legs were a trifle more twisted and

[012-022]gnarly, their eyes a bit smaller, their necks a bit thicker and

[012-023]shorter, and their nostrils slightly more like orifices in a sunken

[012-024]surface; but they had no hair on their faces and on the palms of their

[012-025]hands and the soles of their feet, and they made sounds similar to ours

[012-026]with somewhat similar meanings. After all, the Tree People and the Folk

[012-027]were not so unlike.

[012-028]I found him first, a little withered, dried-up old fellow,

[012-029]wrinkled-faced and bleary-eyed and tottery. He was legitimate prey. In

[012-030]our world there was no sympathy between the kinds, and he was not our

[012-031]kind. He was a Tree-Man, and he was very old. He was sitting at the

[012-032]foot of a tree—evidently his tree, for we could see the tattered nest

[012-033]in the branches, in which he slept at night.

[012-034]I pointed him out to Lop-Ear, and we made a rush for him. He started to

[012-035]climb, but was too slow. I caught him by the leg and dragged him back.

[012-036]Then we had fun. We pinched him, pulled his hair, tweaked his ears, and

[012-037]poked twigs into him, and all the while we laughed with streaming eyes.

[012-038]His futile anger was most absurd. He was a comical sight, striving to

[012-039]fan into flame the cold ashes of his youth, to resurrect his strength

[012-040]dead and gone through the oozing of the years—making woeful faces in

[012-041]place of the ferocious ones he intended, grinding his worn teeth

[012-042]together, beating his meagre chest with feeble fists.

[012-043]Also, he had a cough, and he gasped and hacked and spluttered

[012-044]prodigiously. Every time he tried to climb the tree we pulled him back,

[012-045]until at last he surrendered to his weakness and did no more than sit

[012-046]and weep. And Lop-Ear and I sat with him, our arms around each other,

[012-047]and laughed at his wretchedness.

[012-048]From weeping he went to whining, and from whining to wailing, until at

[012-049]last he achieved a scream. This alarmed us, but the more we tried to

[012-050]make him cease, the louder he screamed. And then, from not far away in

[012-051]the forest, came a “Goëk! Goëk!” to our ears. To this there were

[012-052]answering cries, several of them, and from very far off we could hear a

[012-053]big, bass “Goëk! Goëk! Goëk!” Also, the “Whoo-whoo!” call was rising in

[012-054]the forest all around us.

[012-055]Then came the chase. It seemed it never would end. They raced us

[012-056]through the trees, the whole tribe of them, and nearly caught us. We

[012-057]were forced to take to the ground, and here we had the advantage, for

[012-058]they were truly the Tree People, and while they out-climbed us we

[012-059]out-footed them on the ground. We broke away toward the north, the

[012-060]tribe howling on our track. Across the open spaces we gained, and in

[012-061]the brush they caught up with us, and more than once it was nip and

[012-062]tuck. And as the chase continued, we realized that we were not their

[012-063]kind, either, and that the bonds between us were anything but

[012-064]sympathetic.

[012-065]They ran us for hours. The forest seemed interminable. We kept to the

[012-066]glades as much as possible, but they always ended in more thick forest.

[012-067]Sometimes we thought we had escaped, and sat down to rest; but always,

[012-068]before we could recover our breath, we would hear the hateful

[012-069]“Whoo-whoo!” cries and the terrible “Goëk! Goëk! Goëk!” This latter

[012-070]sometimes terminated in a savage “Ha ha ha ha haaaaa!!!”

[012-071]And in this fashion were we hunted through the forest by the

[012-072]exasperated Tree People. At last, by mid-afternoon, the slopes began

[012-073]rising higher and higher and the trees were becoming smaller. Then we

[012-074]came out on the grassy flanks of the mountains. Here was where we could

[012-075]make time, and here the Tree People gave up and returned to their

[012-076]forest.

[012-077]The mountains were bleak and inhospitable, and three times that

[012-078]afternoon we tried to regain the woods. But the Tree People were lying

[012-079]in wait, and they drove us back. Lop-Ear and I slept that night in a

[012-080]dwarf tree, no larger than a bush. Here was no security, and we would

[012-081]have been easy prey for any hunting animal that chanced along.

[012-082]In the morning, what of our new-gained respect for the Tree People, we

[012-083]faced into the mountains. That we had no definite plan, or even idea, I

[012-084]am confident. We were merely driven on by the danger we had escaped. Of

[012-085]our wanderings through the mountains I have only misty memories. We

[012-086]were in that bleak region many days, and we suffered much, especially

[012-087]from fear, it was all so new and strange. Also, we suffered from the

[012-088]cold, and later from hunger.

[012-089]It was a desolate land of rocks and foaming streams and clattering

[012-090]cataracts. We climbed and descended mighty canyons and gorges; and

[012-091]ever, from every view point, there spread out before us, in all

[012-092]directions, range upon range, the unceasing mountains. We slept at

[012-093]night in holes and crevices, and on one cold night we perched on top a

[012-094]slender pinnacle of rock that was almost like a tree.

[012-095]And then, at last, one hot midday, dizzy with hunger, we gained the

[012-096]divide. From this high backbone of earth, to the north, across the

[012-097]diminishing, down-falling ranges, we caught a glimpse of a far lake.

[012-098]The sun shone upon it, and about it were open, level grass-lands, while

[012-099]to the eastward we saw the dark line of a wide-stretching forest.

[012-100]We were two days in gaining the lake, and we were weak with hunger; but

[012-101]on its shore, sleeping snugly in a thicket, we found a part-grown calf.

[012-102]It gave us much trouble, for we knew no other way to kill than with our

[012-103]hands. When we had gorged our fill, we carried the remainder of the

[012-104]meat to the eastward forest and hid it in a tree. We never returned to

[012-105]that tree, for the shore of the stream that drained Far Lake was packed

[012-106]thick with salmon that had come up from the sea to spawn.

[012-107]Westward from the lake stretched the grass-lands, and here were

[012-108]multitudes of bison and wild cattle. Also were there many packs of wild

[012-109]dogs, and as there were no trees it was not a safe place for us. We

[012-110]followed north along the stream for days. Then, and for what reason I

[012-111]do not know, we abruptly left the stream and swung to the east, and

[012-112]then to the southeast, through a great forest. I shall not bore you

[012-113]with our journey. I but indicate it to show how we finally arrived at

[012-114]the Fire People’s country.

[012-115]We came out upon the river, but we did not know it for our river. We

[012-116]had been lost so long that we had come to accept the condition of being

[012-117]lost as habitual. As I look back I see clearly how our lives and

[012-118]destinies are shaped by the merest chance. We did not know it was our

[012-119]river—there was no way of telling; and if we had never crossed it we

[012-120]would most probably have never returned to the horde; and I, the

[012-121]modern, the thousand centuries yet to be born, would never have been

[012-122]born.

[012-123]And yet Lop-Ear and I wanted greatly to return. We had experienced

[012-124]homesickness on our journey, the yearning for our own kind and land;

[012-125]and often had I had recollections of the Swift One, the young female

[012-126]who made soft sounds, whom it was good to be with, and who lived by

[012-127]herself nobody knew where. My recollections of her were accompanied by

[012-128]sensations of hunger, and these I felt when I was not hungry and when I

[012-129]had just eaten.

[012-130]But to come back to the river. Food was plentiful, principally berries

[012-131]and succulent roots, and on the river bank we played and lingered for

[012-132]days. And then the idea came to Lop-Ear. It was a visible process, the

[012-133]coming of the idea. I saw it. The expression in his eyes became

[012-134]plaintive and querulous, and he was greatly perturbed. Then his eyes

[012-135]went muddy, as if he had lost his grip on the inchoate thought. This

[012-136]was followed by the plaintive, querulous expression as the idea

[012-137]persisted and he clutched it anew. He looked at me, and at the river

[012-138]and the far shore. He tried to speak, but had no sounds with which to

[012-139]express the idea. The result was a gibberish that made me laugh. This

[012-140]angered him, and he grabbed me suddenly and threw me on my back. Of

[012-141]course we fought, and in the end I chased him up a tree, where he

[012-142]secured a long branch and poked me every time I tried to get at him.

[012-143]And the idea had gone glimmering. I did not know, and he had forgotten.

[012-144]But the next morning it awoke in him again. Perhaps it was the homing

[012-145]instinct in him asserting itself that made the idea persist. At any

[012-146]rate it was there, and clearer than before. He led me down to the

[012-147]water, where a log had grounded in an eddy. I thought he was minded to

[012-148]play, as we had played in the mouth of the slough. Nor did I change my

[012-149]mind as I watched him tow up a second log from farther down the shore.

[012-150]It was not until we were on the logs, side by side and holding them

[012-151]together, and had paddled out into the current, that I learned his

[012-152]intention. He paused to point at the far shore, and resumed his

[012-153]paddling, at the same time uttering loud and encouraging cries. I

[012-154]understood, and we paddled energetically. The swift current caught us,

[012-155]flung us toward the south shore, but before we could make a landing

[012-156]flung us back toward the north shore.

[012-157]Here arose dissension. Seeing the north shore so near, I began to

[012-158]paddle for it. Lop-Ear tried to paddle for the south shore. The logs

[012-159]swung around in circles, and we got nowhere, and all the time the

[012-160]forest was flashing past as we drifted down the stream. We could not

[012-161]fight. We knew better than to let go the grips of hands and feet that

[012-162]held the logs together. But we chattered and abused each other with our

[012-163]tongues until the current flung us toward the south bank again. That

[012-164]was now the nearest goal, and together and amicably we paddled for it.

[012-165]We landed in an eddy, and climbed directly into the trees to

[012-166]reconnoitre.
