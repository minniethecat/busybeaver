## CHAPTER VI

[006-001]While the more courageous of the youngsters played in and out of the

[006-002]large-mouthed caves, I early learned that such caves were unoccupied.

[006-003]No one slept in them at night. Only the crevice-mouthed caves were

[006-004]used, the narrower the mouth the better. This was from fear of the

[006-005]preying animals that made life a burden to us in those days and nights.

[006-006]The first morning, after my night’s sleep with Lop-Ear, I learned the

[006-007]advantage of the narrow-mouthed caves. It was just daylight when old

[006-008]Saber-Tooth, the tiger, walked into the open space. Two of the Folk

[006-009]were already up. They made a rush for it. Whether they were

[006-010]panic-stricken, or whether he was too close on their heels for them to

[006-011]attempt to scramble up the bluff to the crevices, I do not know; but at

[006-012]any rate they dashed into the wide-mouthed cave wherein Lop-Ear and I

[006-013]had played the afternoon before.

[006-014]What happened inside there was no way of telling, but it is fair to

[006-015]conclude that the two Folk slipped through the connecting crevice into

[006-016]the other cave. This crevice was too small to allow for the passage of

[006-017]Saber-Tooth, and he came out the way he had gone in, unsatisfied and

[006-018]angry. It was evident that his night’s hunting had been unsuccessful

[006-019]and that he had expected to make a meal off of us. He caught sight of

[006-020]the two Folk at the other cave-mouth and sprang for them. Of course,

[006-021]they darted through the passageway into the first cave. He emerged

[006-022]angrier than ever and snarling.

[006-023]Pandemonium broke loose amongst the rest of us. All up and down the

[006-024]great bluff, we crowded the crevices and outside ledges, and we were

[006-025]all chattering and shrieking in a thousand keys. And we were all making

[006-026]faces—snarling faces; this was an instinct with us. We were as angry as

[006-027]Saber-Tooth, though our anger was allied with fear. I remember that I

[006-028]shrieked and made faces with the best of them. Not only did they set

[006-029]the example, but I felt the urge from within me to do the same things

[006-030]they were doing. My hair was bristling, and I was convulsed with a

[006-031]fierce, unreasoning rage.

[006-032]For some time old Saber-Tooth continued dashing in and out of first the

[006-033]one cave and then the other. But the two Folk merely slipped back and

[006-034]forth through the connecting crevice and eluded him. In the meantime

[006-035]the rest of us up the bluff had proceeded to action. Every time he

[006-036]appeared outside we pelted him with rocks. At first we merely dropped

[006-037]them on him, but we soon began to whiz them down with the added force

[006-038]of our muscles.

[006-039]This bombardment drew Saber-Tooth’s attention to us and made him

[006-040]angrier than ever. He abandoned his pursuit of the two Folk and sprang

[006-041]up the bluff toward the rest of us, clawing at the crumbling rock and

[006-042]snarling as he clawed his upward way. At this awful sight, the last one

[006-043]of us sought refuge inside our caves. I know this, because I peeped out

[006-044]and saw the whole bluff-side deserted, save for Saber-Tooth, who had

[006-045]lost his footing and was sliding and falling down.

[006-046]I called out the cry of encouragement, and again the bluff was covered

[006-047]by the screaming horde and the stones were falling faster than ever.

[006-048]Saber-Tooth was frantic with rage. Time and again he assaulted the

[006-049]bluff. Once he even gained the first crevice-entrances before he fell

[006-050]back, but was unable to force his way inside. With each upward rush he

[006-051]made, waves of fear surged over us. At first, at such times, most of us

[006-052]dashed inside; but some remained outside to hammer him with stones, and

[006-053]soon all of us remained outside and kept up the fusillade.

[006-054]Never was so masterly a creature so completely baffled. It hurt his

[006-055]pride terribly, thus to be outwitted by the small and tender Folk. He

[006-056]stood on the ground and looked up at us, snarling, lashing his tail,

[006-057]snapping at the stones that fell near to him. Once I whizzed down a

[006-058]stone, and just at the right moment he looked up. It caught him full on

[006-059]the end of his nose, and he went straight up in the air, all four feet

[006-060]of him, roaring and caterwauling, what of the hurt and surprise.

[006-061]He was beaten and he knew it. Recovering his dignity, he stalked out

[006-062]solemnly from under the rain of stones. He stopped in the middle of the

[006-063]open space and looked wistfully and hungrily back at us. He hated to

[006-064]forego the meal, and we were just so much meat, cornered but

[006-065]inaccessible. This sight of him started us to laughing. We laughed

[006-066]derisively and uproariously, all of us. Now animals do not like

[006-067]mockery. To be laughed at makes them angry. And in such fashion our

[006-068]laughter affected Saber-Tooth. He turned with a roar and charged the

[006-069]bluff again. This was what we wanted. The fight had become a game, and

[006-070]we took huge delight in pelting him.

[006-071]But this attack did not last long. He quickly recovered his common

[006-072]sense, and besides, our missiles were shrewd to hurt. Vividly do I

[006-073]recollect the vision of one bulging eye of his, swollen almost shut by

[006-074]one of the stones we had thrown. And vividly do I retain the picture of

[006-075]him as he stood on the edge of the forest whither he had finally

[006-076]retreated. He was looking back at us, his writhing lips lifted clear of

[006-077]the very roots of his huge fangs, his hair bristling and his tail

[006-078]lashing. He gave one last snarl and slid from view among the trees.

[006-079]And then such a chattering as went up. We swarmed out of our holes,

[006-080]examining the marks his claws had made on the crumbling rock of the

[006-081]bluff, all of us talking at once. One of the two Folk who had been

[006-082]caught in the double cave was part-grown, half child and half youth.

[006-083]They had come out proudly from their refuge, and we surrounded them in

[006-084]an admiring crowd. Then the young fellow’s mother broke through and

[006-085]fell upon him in a tremendous rage, boxing his ears, pulling his hair,

[006-086]and shrieking like a demon. She was a strapping big woman, very hairy,

[006-087]and the thrashing she gave him was a delight to the horde. We roared

[006-088]with laughter, holding on to one another or rolling on the ground in

[006-089]our glee.

[006-090]In spite of the reign of fear under which we lived, the Folk were

[006-091]always great laughers. We had the sense of humor. Our merriment was

[006-092]Gargantuan. It was never restrained. There was nothing half way about

[006-093]it. When a thing was funny we were convulsed with appreciation of it,

[006-094]and the simplest, crudest things were funny to us. Oh, we were great

[006-095]laughers, I can tell you.

[006-096]The way we had treated Saber-Tooth was the way we treated all animals

[006-097]that invaded the village. We kept our run-ways and drinking-places to

[006-098]ourselves by making life miserable for the animals that trespassed or

[006-099]strayed upon our immediate territory. Even the fiercest hunting animals

[006-100]we so bedevilled that they learned to leave our places alone. We were

[006-101]not fighters like them; we were cunning and cowardly, and it was

[006-102]because of our cunning and cowardice, and our inordinate capacity for

[006-103]fear, that we survived in that frightfully hostile environment of the

[006-104]Younger World.

[006-105]Lop-Ear, I figure, was a year older than I. What his past history was

[006-106]he had no way of telling me, but as I never saw anything of his mother

[006-107]I believed him to be an orphan. After all, fathers did not count in our

[006-108]horde. Marriage was as yet in a rude state, and couples had a way of

[006-109]quarrelling and separating. Modern man, what of his divorce

[006-110]institution, does the same thing legally. But we had no laws. Custom

[006-111]was all we went by, and our custom in this particular matter was rather

[006-112]promiscuous.

[006-113]Nevertheless, as this narrative will show later on, we betrayed

[006-114]glimmering adumbrations of the monogamy that was later to give power

[006-115]to, and make mighty, such tribes as embraced it. Furthermore, even at

[006-116]the time I was born, there were several faithful couples that lived in

[006-117]the trees in the neighborhood of my mother. Living in the thick of the

[006-118]horde did not conduce to monogamy. It was for this reason, undoubtedly,

[006-119]that the faithful couples went away and lived by themselves. Through

[006-120]many years these couples stayed together, though when the man or woman

[006-121]died or was eaten the survivor invariably found a new mate.

[006-122]There was one thing that greatly puzzled me during the first days of my

[006-123]residence in the horde. There was a nameless and incommunicable fear

[006-124]that rested upon all. At first it appeared to be connected wholly with

[006-125]direction. The horde feared the northeast. It lived in perpetual

[006-126]apprehension of that quarter of the compass. And every individual gazed

[006-127]more frequently and with greater alarm in that direction than in any

[006-128]other.

[006-129]When Lop-Ear and I went toward the north-east to eat the stringy-rooted

[006-130]carrots that at that season were at their best, he became unusually

[006-131]timid. He was content to eat the leavings, the big tough carrots and

[006-132]the little ropy ones, rather than to venture a short distance farther

[006-133]on to where the carrots were as yet untouched. When I so ventured, he

[006-134]scolded me and quarrelled with me. He gave me to understand that in

[006-135]that direction was some horrible danger, but just what the horrible

[006-136]danger was his paucity of language would not permit him to say.

[006-137]Many a good meal I got in this fashion, while he scolded and chattered

[006-138]vainly at me. I could not understand. I kept very alert, but I could

[006-139]see no danger. I calculated always the distance between myself and the

[006-140]nearest tree, and knew that to that haven of refuge I could out-foot

[006-141]the Tawny One, or old Saber-Tooth, did one or the other suddenly

[006-142]appear.

[006-143]One late afternoon, in the village, a great uproar arose. The horde was

[006-144]animated with a single emotion, that of fear. The bluff-side swarmed

[006-145]with the Folk, all gazing and pointing into the northeast. I did not

[006-146]know what it was, but I scrambled all the way up to the safety of my

[006-147]own high little cave before ever I turned around to see.

[006-148]And then, across the river, away into the northeast, I saw for the

[006-149]first time the mystery of smoke. It was the biggest animal I had ever

[006-150]seen. I thought it was a monster snake, up-ended, rearing its head high

[006-151]above the trees and swaying back and forth. And yet, somehow, I seemed

[006-152]to gather from the conduct of the Folk that the smoke itself was not

[006-153]the danger. They appeared to fear it as the token of something else.

[006-154]What this something else was I was unable to guess. Nor could they tell

[006-155]me. Yet I was soon to know, and I was to know it as a thing more

[006-156]terrible than the Tawny One, than old Saber-Tooth, than the snakes

[006-157]themselves, than which it seemed there could be no things more

[006-158]terrible.
