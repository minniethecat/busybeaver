## Overview

The blueberry swamp is a specific wetland territory east of the Folk's cliff caves, rich in food resources and serving as a waypoint in Big-Tooth's expanding mental map of his world [004-122]. First visited in early childhood under his mother's guidance, the swamp recurs at critical points in the novel — as a site of innovation and play in young adulthood [010-002][010-058], a boundary marker defining the known territory [014-010], and ultimately as part of the escape corridor during the Fire People's encroachment [017-119][017-183]. It spans the full narrative arc, from the dependent child to the fleeing refugee.

## In the Novel

Big-Tooth's first memory of the swamp comes from a childhood foraging trip with his mother. The journey marks his first significant venture away from the immediate cave territory, establishing the swamp as the eastern boundary of the known world [004-122]. Years later, during the adventure-journey with Lop-Ear, the swamp re-emerges as a site of discovery: it is in the "slough" of the blueberry swamp that the pair first experiment with floating on logs, the accidental invention that leads them toward the catamaran concept [010-058].

The swamp serves as a territorial reference point in the narrator's description of the adventure-journey's scope — a named place that demarcates how far they ranged [014-010][014-011]. After the Fire People's intensified attacks devastate the horde, the swamp becomes part of the escape geography: Big-Tooth and the Swift One retreat toward the swamp and beyond it [016-001][017-119]. The territory evolves from childhood playground to young adult's experimental ground to final refuge.

## Context and Significance

The swamp's multiple roles in the narrative — food source, boundary marker, experimental site, and refuge — illustrate how forager groups map their territories by resource patches. A swamp rich in berries, roots, and small game is not merely "nature"; it is a named, remembered place with layered personal and collective meanings [004-122][010-002]. The evolution of Big-Tooth's relationship with the swamp — from following his mother to independently navigating it — traces his own maturation as a forager and territorial being.

## Key Passages

The swamp is referenced throughout the novel as a known territory rather than described in a single extended passage. Its significance lies in its consistent recurrence as a landmark across the narrator's life stages: childhood [004-122], young adulthood [010-002][010-058], the adventure-journey's scope [014-010], and the final flight from the Fire People [016-001][017-119][017-183].
