## Overview

The Folk is the term Jack London uses throughout *Before Adam* to denote the collective hominid species — the narrator's own kind. They are a pre-human people living in the mid-Pleistocene, possessing rudimentary speech, social structures, and tool use, yet still governed largely by instinct and fear. The narrator consistently refers to his people as "the Folk," distinguishing them from other hominid groups such as the [[tree-people|Tree People]] and from the more brutish individuals like [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] within their own ranks.

## Characteristics

The Folk lived primarily in caves, though they also used trees for shelter and safety. They were omnivorous, eating "shell-fish, and berries, and the things that grew" (005-008), as well as meat from hunting. Their social organization was loose, with no formal leadership — instead, brute strength often determined dominance, as exemplified by Red-Eye's reign of terror over the group.

The Folk possessed rudimentary language: "we had no words for conversations" (004-025), communicating instead through gestures, simple sounds, and emotional expressions. Yet they were capable of cooperation, sharing food ("right heartily the Folk appreciated it" upon the narrator bringing back marrow, 005-108), and forming lasting bonds of friendship and partnership.

## Social Structure

The Folk had no formal governance. "We had no chief" — instead, the strongest individual often dominated through violence. The group gathered at a central open space in the cliffs, which served as a communal area where "the drinking-places of the Folk" and social interactions occurred (005-006). Despite the absence of formal organization, the Folk exhibited collective behaviors — fleeing together from danger, gathering to witness events, and collectively expressing emotions: "the Folk screeched, beat their" chests in excitement during a hunt (016-179).

## Relation to Other Groups

The Folk are distinct from the [[tree-people|Tree People]], a rival hominid species that inhabited the forest. Unlike the Tree People, the Folk were primarily cave-dwellers who had begun to venture into open spaces and near water sources. The narrator notes: "The Folk were not constituted for the coast-dwelling life" (018-073), indicating their adaptation to inland cave environments rather than coastal living. The Folk also encountered the [[fire-people|Fire People]] — a more advanced hominid group — and were eventually displaced or absorbed into the broader evolutionary stream.

## In the Narrative

The concept of the Folk serves as the backdrop for the narrator's personal journey. Through his dreams, the modern man experiences life among the Folk — their fears, their simple pleasures, and their gradual awakening to something beyond mere animal existence. The Folk represent humanity at its dawn: neither fully animal nor fully human, caught in the evolutionary transition that London dramatizes throughout the novel.

> "Perhaps, all told, the Folk in that day had" no words for what we would recognize as language, yet they were beginning to name things, to recognize individuals, and to form the first bonds of society (004-025).

> "right heartily the Folk appreciated it" (005-108) — the narrator describes the communal joy when food was shared, suggesting the embryonic social instincts that would eventually become human civilization.
