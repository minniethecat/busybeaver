## Overview

The Horde — known to themselves simply as "the Folk" — is the small hominin group into which [[big-tooth|the narrator]] is born and within which the entire narrative unfolds. They inhabit the caves above [[the-river|the river]], a defensive position that offers protection from predators like [[Saber-Tooth]] while providing access to [[drinking-place|drinking-places]] in the open space below (005-001-005-020). Numbering perhaps thirty to fifty individuals, they represent a stage of hominin social organization just above the family band: they have the beginnings of collective decision-making (the "[[council|hee-hee council]]s"), shared fear-responses, and a loose dominance hierarchy, but they lack formal leadership, coordinated defense, and any technology beyond simple stone-throwing (014-001-014-040).

> Perforce, because he was born in our horde he stayed with us; but in actuality he was a stranger among us. (006-019-006-020)

The Horde is simultaneously [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s home and his limitation. Throughout the narrative, the tension between belonging to the group and transcending its capacities — learning to cross rivers, acquiring fire, building catamarans — drives the evolutionary drama. The Horde's destruction in the [[fire-people|Fire People]] Massacre and the survivors' emergence into a new landscape marks the end of one evolutionary chapter and the beginning of another.
## Social Structure

The Horde has no formal leadership. The closest thing to authority is physical dominance, embodied by [[Red-Eye]] — "the most powerful member of the horde" (006-012) — who takes what he wants through violence rather than consent. But his power is not absolute: he cannot command the group, and his authority is limited to intimidation and physical force.

Beneath the dominance of individuals like [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], the Horde operates through rudimentary collective processes. The "[[council|hee-hee council]]" — a gathering where members gibber and gesture to reach a kind of consensus — represents the earliest glimmer of deliberative governance (014-028-014-038). But these councils lack the conceptual vocabulary for true cooperation. When [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] kills [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] and seizes the [[swift-one|Singing One]], the council's response is "a confusion of many voices" — noise without resolution (014-036-014-038).

Marriage customs are equally fluid: "Marriage was as yet in a rude state, and couples had a way of quarrelling and separating" (008-018-008-019). The Horde does not enforce monogamy, and the concept of exclusive partnership is still emerging.

## Territory and Habitat

The Horde's primary abiding-place is the caves in [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s above [[the-river|the river]] (005-001-005-020). The caves are organized in tiers: the lower caves are more accessible but more vulnerable, while the upper caves — where [[big-tooth|the narrator]] lives — offer greater safety. In front of the caves lies the open space, a cleared area used for social gatherings and stone-throwing defense.

The Horde's territory extends from the caves to the surrounding forest, the [[drinking-place|drinking-places]] along [[the-river|the river]], and the blueberry swamp to the northeast (008-001-008-020). They fear the northeast above all other directions: "It lived in perpetual dread of the northeast" (008-029-008-030). This fear is later vindicated when the [[Fire People]] attack from that direction during the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]].
## Customs and Knowledge

The Folk possess a limited but functional body of knowledge. They make rough shelters in trees when away from the caves (003-001-003-020). They recognize the danger of predators like [[Saber-Tooth]] and have developed collective defense responses — screaming and stone-throwing — that can drive even large predators away (007-001-007-030). They distinguish between types of trees and know which bark is edible and which berries are safe.

But their knowledge is bounded by their experience. They do not know how to cross rivers. They do not know how to make or control fire. They cannot communicate complex ideas, possessing "no fixed values" and an extremely limited vocabulary of perhaps a few dozen gesture-sounds (005-010-005-015). The narrator's acquisition of new knowledge — river-crossing, fire-making, navigation — is what gradually separates him from the Horde and ultimately makes him a different kind of being.
## Relation to Other Groups

The Horde exists in a landscape shared with other hominin groups, each at a different stage of development. The [[Fire People]] are technologically superior — they possess fire, [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s, and coordinated hunting tactics — and represent both a threat and a source of cultural transmission. The [[tree-people|Tree People]] are physically adapted to arboreal life and hostile to ground-dwellers, but are less advanced in other respects.

The narrator's understanding of the relationship between these groups evolves over the course of the story: "Certainly, all three kinds were related" (017-042), he concludes, recognizing that the differences between Folk, [[fire-people|Fire People]], and [[tree-people|Tree People]] are differences of degree, not kind.
## Fate

The Horde is effectively destroyed in the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]] (Chapter XVII), when the [[fire-people|Fire People]] launch a systematic extermination campaign against the caves. Of the entire group, only a handful escape: [[big-tooth|the narrator]], [[swift-one|the Swift One]], [[Lop-Ear]], [[Red-Eye]], [[Hair-Face]], and a few others (017-001-017-210). These survivors flee into the [[the-great-swamp|great swamp]], emerging weeks later into an unfamiliar landscape. The destruction of the Horde, though catastrophic, is the precondition for everything that follows — including [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s eventual encounter with new peoples, new technologies, and a new way of living by [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]].
## Evolutionary Significance

The Horde occupies a pivotal position in the evolutionary trajectory London dramatizes. They are neither brute animal nor fully human — they possess the raw materials of society (cooperation, communication, pair-bonding) without the conceptual framework to organize them. The narrator reflects on this liminal state: "we were very near the brute; but the brute is not all" (011-027).

What distinguishes the Horde from both their predecessors and their contemporaries is their capacity for change. Unlike the [[tree-people|Tree People]], who remain locked in arboreal specialization, the Folk are adaptable — they learn, however slowly, from experience and from contact with more advanced groups. The [[fire-people|Fire People]] massacre, while catastrophic, becomes the mechanism of evolutionary pressure: the survivors who cross [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]] are not the same Folk who cowered in the caves. They have been forced to innovate, to cooperate in new ways, and to imagine possibilities beyond the known.

> "We learned many things in those days" (018-045) — the narrator's understated summary of the Horde's transformation captures the essence of London's evolutionary vision: progress is earned through suffering and adaptation, not bestowed by nature.
