## Overview

It was late summer. An unusual second crop of carrots — "young and juicy and tender" — had sprung up in the carrot patch, and for some time it was the horde's favorite feeding place (015-014-015-015). One morning, "early, several score of us were there making our breakfast" (015-016). The narrator crouched between the [[hairless-one]] on one side and his sister ([[Lop-Ear]]'s wife) on the other. Beyond the [[hairless-one|Hairless One]] sat old [[Marrow-Bone]] and his grandson [[Long-Lip]].

There was no warning.

> On the sudden, both the Hairless One and my sister sprang and screamed. At the same instant I heard the thud of the arrows that transfixed them. The next instant they were down on the ground, floundering and gasping, and the rest of us were stampeding for the trees. (015-020-015-023)

An [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] drove past [[big-tooth|the narrator]] into the ground, its feathered shaft "vibrating and oscillating from the impact of its arrested flight" (015-023-015-024). [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] took an [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] through the calf of his leg and fell. The narrator dashed back, pulled the [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] out with all his might, and the two fled together while the [[hairless-one|Hairless One]] and [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s sister lay dying behind them.

## Context and Causes

The ambush was the opening engagement of the [[the-fire-migration|Fire Migration]]. The [[Fire People]], expanding from their peninsula stronghold, had begun pressing into Folk territory. They had reconnaissance: the wizened old hunter had already walked alone into [[the-open-space|the open space before the caves]], studying [[the-cliffs|the cliff-wall]] and [[run-way|run-ways]] (015-048-015-053).

The Folk, meanwhile, had no concept of organized warfare. They were gatherers scattered across a feeding ground — utterly vulnerable to enemies who could kill from a hundred feet with [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s, weapons that were "an enormous extension of their leaping and striking muscles" (015-037-015-038).

The immediate tactical cause was simple: the carrot patch was an exposed feeding ground with forest cover nearby — ideal for an ambush. Three [[fire-man|Fire-Men]], hidden in the trees, waited until the Folk were fully absorbed in eating, then loosed their [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s at close range.

## Narrative Account

### The Attack

The ambush was over in seconds. Two were killed instantly — the [[hairless-one|Hairless One]] and [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s sister — transfixed by [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s. The rest stampeded. "An [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] drove past me and entered the ground, its feathered shaft vibrating and oscillating" (015-023-015-024). The narrator gave it "a needlessly wide berth. I must have shied at it as a horse shies at an object it fears" (015-025).

[[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] fell — "an [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] had driven through the calf of his leg and tripped him. He tried to run, but was tripped and thrown by it a second time" (015-026-015-027). The narrator ran back to him. [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] showed him the [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]; when [[big-tooth|the narrator]] tried to pull it out, the pain made [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] seize his hand and stop him. A flying [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] passed between them (015-029-015-030). The narrator pulled with all his might — [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] screamed as the [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] came free — and they fled.

### The Aftermath

From the trees, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] looked back. [[marrow-bone|Old Marrow-Bone]], "deserted and far behind, was tottering silently along in his handicapped race with death" (015-031-015-032). The three [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] ran forward from their forest ambush — they could easily have caught the old man but did not try. "Perhaps he was too old and tough. But they did want the [[hairless-one|Hairless One]] and my sister, for as I looked back from the trees I could see the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] beating in their heads with rocks" (015-034-015-036). One of them was the wizened old hunter who limped (015-038).

### The Folk's Response

The horde fled to the caves — "an excited and disorderly mob that drove before it to their holes all the small life of the forest" (015-040). [[long-lip|Long-Lip]] waited for his grandfather [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]], and the old man and the youth brought up the rear together, "with the gap of a generation between them" (015-043).

That night [[big-tooth|the narrator]] slept again with [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] in their old cave — [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]]'s wife was dead, and the old life of chumming resumed. "The loss of his mate seemed to cause him no grief. At least he showed no signs of it, nor of need for her" (015-047-015-048).

## Consequences

The narrator understood the ambush's meaning only in retrospect: "This inroad of the [[fire-people|Fire People]] on the carrot-patch was the beginning of the end, though we did not know it" (015-036-015-037). The [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] began appearing more frequently. The Folk became circumspect, vigilant, timid — "no longer were the trees a protection to be relied upon" (015-041).

The psychological effect was profound. Before the ambush, the Folk's world — though dangerous — was understood. After it, a new kind of predator had entered: one that could kill from invisibility, at a distance, with weapons the Folk could not match. It was the start of a one-sided war that would end, within a single season, at the caves — in fire and massacre.

## The Cost of Asymmetric Warfare

The Carrot Patch Ambush illustrates the devastating asymmetry between the Folk and the Fire People. The Folk, foraging in the open, are defenseless against the Fire People's ranged weapons — arrows that kill from a distance, beyond the reach of teeth and fists. The deaths of Hairless One and the narrator's sister, and the wounding of Lop-Ear, are not heroic battlefield losses but sudden, inexplicable violence: the strange flying things are incomprehensible to the Folk, who have no framework for projectile weaponry.

This episode also marks a moral turning point for the narrator. The abandonment of old Marrow-Bone is a decision driven by survival necessity, but it haunts the narrative. The old man's fate represents the cost of evolutionary pressure: those who cannot adapt, who cannot run fast enough or think quickly enough, are left behind. London offers no comfort for this reality, only the narrator's quiet acknowledgment that survival sometimes requires abandoning the weak.

> "The strange things that flew through the air and stung" (015-040) — the narrator's inability to name arrows captures the cognitive gap that separates the Folk from the Fire People.
