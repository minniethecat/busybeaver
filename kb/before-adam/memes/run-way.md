## Overview

Run-ways are the paths that connect the Folk's cliff-dwelling territory to the river below. Formed by natural erosion but treated as named, recognized terrain by the horde, run-ways serve as the sole safe passage between the safety of the caves and the exposed drinking-places on the riverbank. The narrator describes them as forming part of the horde's known geography alongside the bluff, the open space, and the caves themselves (005-006, 005-016). Run-ways are sites of both daily life and life-threatening drama — the regular route for water, but also the narrow passage where a dominant male can block retreat, a predator can ambush, or an enemy can stage an attack.

## In the Novel

Run-ways first appear in the narrator's description of the horde's territorial layout: "the bluff, the open space, and the run-ways to the drinking-places" (005-016). Red-Eye uses the run-ways as a stage for dominance — his swaggering passage along a run-way forces the narrator and Lop-Ear to scramble out of his path, peering fearfully from a tree (005-101). After the Fire People's first attack, the horde "kept our run-ways and drinking-places to ourselves," treating these access routes as defended territory (006-097).

The run-ways figure in the horde's daily rhythm. At day's end, the Folk troop up the run-ways from the drinking-places to the caves, carrying water or food. But the paths also become scenes of tragedy: Big-Tooth's sister, fleeing Red-Eye's advances, runs "up a run-way from a drinking-place, pursued by Red-Eye" (014-070). A Fire People scout descends a run-way to survey the horde's territory, marking the beginning of the end for the Folk's occupation of the caves (015-165).

## Context and Significance

The run-ways function as what anthropologists call a "landscape of fear" — a terrain feature whose use is shaped by predation risk. The narrow, exposed paths concentrate movement and make the Folk predictable, exactly the condition that predators and hostile groups exploit. In hominin evolution, access routes between sleeping sites and water sources were a critical limitation on territorial choice, and London captures this constraint precisely.

## Evolutionary and Technological Significance

The run-way is the Folk's term for the network of paths and trails that connect the caves to the surrounding territory. These paths are not deliberately constructed but emerge from repeated use — they are 'run-ways' because they are used for running, for escape, for the constant flight from predators that defines the Folk's existence. The concept of the run-way encodes the Folk's spatial understanding of their world: a landscape organized not by geography but by danger and safety.

> The narrator's acquisition of this knowledge — at first unconscious, then gradually recognized — mirrors the evolutionary trajectory London traces throughout the novel: from instinct to awareness, from accident to intention. [005-010]
