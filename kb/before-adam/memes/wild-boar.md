## Role in the Younger World

The Wild Boar appears in the narrator's earliest memories as one of the first dangers encountered on the forest floor.
In Chapter III, a very young narrator is left alone when a wild boar approaches:

> It was a wild boar. He peered at me curiously. He grunted once or twice and shifted his weight from one foreleg to the other, at the same time moving his head from side to side and swaying the ferns. (003-057)

While not a dedicated predator like [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] or the [[lion]], the wild boar's tusks and aggressive disposition made it a serious threat — especially to the young and vulnerable.

## The Boar Attack

The narrator's earliest memory of danger involves a wild boar that advanced on him threateningly.
His instinct was to remain motionless — a survival response inherited from countless generations:

> It seemed that this movelessness and silence on my part was what was expected of me. I was not to cry out in the face of fear. It was a dictate of instinct. (003-062)

The narrator's mother arrived and snatched him into the trees just as the boar charged. The boar then called others, and "from every side wild hogs dashed into the open space — a score of them" (003-098). The family was trapped in the tree until the father arrived.

## Group Behavior

Wild boars moved in groups and could be called to join an attack (003-096). In a later encounter, a boar is described "gnashing his fangs like a wild boar" when a member of [[the-folk|the Folk]] fought over meat (009-106), suggesting the boar's aggressive display was well known and used as a behavioral reference point.

## Place in the Ecosystem

The wild boar occupied the forest floor alongside other large herbivores. Its omnivorous diet and aggressive defence made it an unpredictable encounter for the tree-dwelling Folk whenever they descended to the ground — a reminder that not only predators posed dangers in the [[younger-world|Younger World]].
