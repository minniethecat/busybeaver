## Overview

The Double-Cave Attack was a harrowing encounter between [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and a pack of [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]] at a geological formation with two entrances connected by a narrow passage. The narrator, sleeping in the connecting passage for its defensive advantage against larger predators, was discovered by a pack of wild dogs whose smaller bodies could navigate the same constriction he relied upon for safety (015-015-015-018).

The attack demonstrated a cruel irony of Pleistocene survival: every defensive adaptation created a corresponding vulnerability. The narrow passage that kept [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] out was exactly the right size to let wild dogs in.

## The Attack

The dogs entered one mouth of the cave, their snuffling and growling alerting the narrator in the darkness. He dashed out through the opposite mouth — but a dog caught him in mid-leap, sinking its teeth into the muscles of his thigh.

> They nosed me out. Had they entered both caves at the same time they would have got me. As it was, I dashed out the mouth of the other cave. But one dog caught me in mid-leap, his teeth sinking into my thigh. (015-015-015-018)

The tactical error of the pack — entering from only one direction rather than surrounding their prey — was the difference between the narrator's survival and his death. Wild dogs were coordinated hunters, but they were not strategists, and their failure to execute a pincer movement gave the narrator the narrow margin he needed.

## The Counter-Attack

With the dog still attached to his thigh, the narrator climbed the cliff wall to a height beyond the reach of the snapping pack below. There, suspended above the frustrated dogs, he throttled the animal to death — a kill born of pure desperation. The dog that had tried to eat him became, in that moment, his provision.

The following morning, the narrator shared the dog's flesh with [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] and Lop-Ear's wife, converting a near-fatal encounter into a communal meal (015-073). This reversal — predator becoming food, individual danger becoming group sustenance — is one of the small evolutionary victories that accumulate over the course of the narrative.

## Significance

The Double-Cave Attack illustrates the layered nature of Pleistocene predation. The narrator had chosen his sleeping spot for its defense against [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], only to discover that it exposed him to a different class of predator. Each layer of the ecosystem presented a distinct threat profile, and the Folk's survival depended on navigating a landscape where fleeing one danger often meant running toward another.

The encounter also prefigures the human capacity to transform predation into provision. The narrator does not merely survive the attack — he turns the attacker into food, and shares that food with his companions. This is not just survival but a specifically human response to danger: the conversion of threat into resource through the application of intelligence and physical courage.
