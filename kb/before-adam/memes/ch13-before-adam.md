## CHAPTER XIII

[013-001]It was not until the night of our first day on the south bank of the

[013-002]river that we discovered the Fire People. What must have been a band of

[013-003]wandering hunters went into camp not far from the tree in which Lop-Ear

[013-004]and I had elected to roost for the night. The voices of the Fire People

[013-005]at first alarmed us, but later, when darkness had come, we were

[013-006]attracted by the fire. We crept cautiously and silently from tree to

[013-007]tree till we got a good view of the scene.

[013-008]In an open space among the trees, near to the river, the fire was

[013-009]burning. About it were half a dozen Fire-Men. Lop-Ear clutched me

[013-010]suddenly, and I could feel him tremble. I looked more closely, and saw

[013-011]the wizened little old hunter who had shot Broken-Tooth out of the tree

[013-012]years before. When he got up and walked about, throwing fresh wood upon

[013-013]the fire, I saw that he limped with his crippled leg. Whatever it was,

[013-014]it was a permanent injury. He seemed more dried up and wizened than

[013-015]ever, and the hair on his face was quite gray.

[013-016]The other hunters were young men. I noted, lying near them on the

[013-017]ground, their bows and arrows, and I knew the weapons for what they

[013-018]were. The Fire-Men wore animal skins around their waists and across

[013-019]their shoulders. Their arms and legs, however, were bare, and they wore

[013-020]no footgear. As I have said before, they were not quite so hairy as we

[013-021]of the Folk. They did not have large heads, and between them and the

[013-022]Folk there was very little difference in the degree of the slant of the

[013-023]head back from the eyes.

[013-024]They were less stooped than we, less springy in their movements. Their

[013-025]backbones and hips and knee-joints seemed more rigid. Their arms were

[013-026]not so long as ours either, and I did not notice that they ever

[013-027]balanced themselves when they walked, by touching the ground on either

[013-028]side with their hands. Also, their muscles were more rounded and

[013-029]symmetrical than ours, and their faces were more pleasing. Their nose

[013-030]orifices opened downward; likewise the bridges of their noses were more

[013-031]developed, did not look so squat nor crushed as ours. Their lips were

[013-032]less flabby and pendent, and their eye-teeth did not look so much like

[013-033]fangs. However, they were quite as thin-hipped as we, and did not weigh

[013-034]much more. Take it all in all, they were less different from us than

[013-035]were we from the Tree People. Certainly, all three kinds were related,

[013-036]and not so remotely related at that.

[013-037]The fire around which they sat was especially attractive. Lop-Ear and I

[013-038]sat for hours, watching the flames and smoke. It was most fascinating

[013-039]when fresh fuel was thrown on and showers of sparks went flying upward.

[013-040]I wanted to come closer and look at the fire, but there was no way. We

[013-041]were crouching in the forks of a tree on the edge of the open space,

[013-042]and we did not dare run the risk of being discovered.

[013-043]The Fire-Men squatted around the fire and slept with their heads bowed

[013-044]forward on their knees. They did not sleep soundly. Their ears twitched

[013-045]in their sleep, and they were restless. Every little while one or

[013-046]another got up and threw more wood upon the fire. About the circle of

[013-047]light in the forest, in the darkness beyond, roamed hunting animals.

[013-048]Lop-Ear and I could tell them by their sounds. There were wild dogs and

[013-049]a hyena, and for a time there was a great yelping and snarling that

[013-050]awakened on the instant the whole circle of sleeping Fire-Men.

[013-051]Once a lion and a lioness stood beneath our tree and gazed out with

[013-052]bristling hair and blinking eyes. The lion licked his chops and was

[013-053]nervous with eagerness, as if he wanted to go forward and make a meal.

[013-054]But the lioness was more cautious. It was she that discovered us, and

[013-055]the pair stood and looked up at us, silently, with twitching, scenting

[013-056]nostrils. Then they growled, looked once again at the fire, and turned

[013-057]away into the forest.

[013-058]For a much longer time Lop-Ear and I remained and watched. Now and

[013-059]again we could hear the crashing of heavy bodies in the thickets and

[013-060]underbrush, and from the darkness of the other side, across the circle,

[013-061]we could see eyes gleaming in the firelight. In the distance we heard a

[013-062]lion roar, and from far off came the scream of some stricken animal,

[013-063]splashing and floundering in a drinking-place. Also, from the river,

[013-064]came a great grunting of rhinoceroses.

[013-065]In the morning, after having had our sleep, we crept back to the fire.

[013-066]It was still smouldering, and the Fire-Men were gone. We made a circle

[013-067]through the forest to make sure, and then we ran to the fire. I wanted

[013-068]to see what it was like, and between thumb and finger I picked up a

[013-069]glowing coal. My cry of pain and fear, as I dropped it, stampeded

[013-070]Lop-Ear into the trees, and his flight frightened me after him.

[013-071]The next time we came back more cautiously, and we avoided the glowing

[013-072]coals. We fell to imitating the Fire-Men. We squatted down by the fire,

[013-073]and with heads bent forward on our knees, made believe to sleep. Then

[013-074]we mimicked their speech, talking to each other in their fashion and

[013-075]making a great gibberish. I remembered seeing the wizened old hunter

[013-076]poke the fire with a stick. I poked the fire with a stick, turning up

[013-077]masses of live coals and clouds of white ashes. This was great sport,

[013-078]and soon we were coated white with the ashes.

[013-079]It was inevitable that we should imitate the Fire-Men in replenishing

[013-080]the fire. We tried it first with small pieces of wood. It was a

[013-081]success. The wood flamed up and crackled, and we danced and gibbered

[013-082]with delight. Then we began to throw on larger pieces of wood. We put

[013-083]on more and more, until we had a mighty fire. We dashed excitedly back

[013-084]and forth, dragging dead limbs and branches from out the forest. The

[013-085]flames soared higher and higher, and the smoke-column out-towered the

[013-086]trees. There was a tremendous snapping and crackling and roaring. It

[013-087]was the most monumental work we had ever effected with our hands, and

[013-088]we were proud of it. We, too, were Fire-Men, we thought, as we danced

[013-089]there, white gnomes in the conflagration.

[013-090]The dried grass and underbrush caught fire, but we did not notice it.

[013-091]Suddenly a great tree on the edge of the open space burst into flames.

[013-092]We looked at it with startled eyes. The heat of it drove us back.

[013-093]Another tree caught, and another, and then half a dozen. We were

[013-094]frightened. The monster had broken loose. We crouched down in fear,

[013-095]while the fire ate around the circle and hemmed us in. Into Lop-Ear’s

[013-096]eyes came the plaintive look that always accompanied incomprehension,

[013-097]and I know that in my eyes must have been the same look. We huddled,

[013-098]with our arms around each other, until the heat began to reach us and

[013-099]the odor of burning hair was in our nostrils. Then we made a dash of

[013-100]it, and fled away westward through the forest, looking back and

[013-101]laughing as we ran.

[013-102]By the middle of the day we came to a neck of land, made, as we

[013-103]afterward discovered, by a great curve of the river that almost

[013-104]completed a circle. Right across the neck lay bunched several low and

[013-105]partly wooded hills. Over these we climbed, looking backward at the

[013-106]forest which had become a sea of flame that swept eastward before a

[013-107]rising wind. We continued to the west, following the river bank, and

[013-108]before we knew it we were in the midst of the abiding-place of the Fire

[013-109]People.

[013-110]This abiding-place was a splendid strategic selection. It was a

[013-111]peninsula, protected on three sides by the curving river. On only one

[013-112]side was it accessible by land. This was the narrow neck of the

[013-113]peninsula, and here the several low hills were a natural obstacle.

[013-114]Practically isolated from the rest of the world, the Fire People must

[013-115]have here lived and prospered for a long time. In fact, I think it was

[013-116]their prosperity that was responsible for the subsequent migration that

[013-117]worked such calamity upon the Folk. The Fire People must have increased

[013-118]in numbers until they pressed uncomfortably against the bounds of their

[013-119]habitat. They were expanding, and in the course of their expanding they

[013-120]drove the Folk before them, and settled down themselves in the caves

[013-121]and occupied the territory that we had occupied.

[013-122]But Lop-Ear and I little dreamed of all this when we found ourselves in

[013-123]the Fire People’s stronghold. We had but one idea, and that was to get

[013-124]away, though we could not forbear humoring our curiosity by peeping out

[013-125]upon the village. For the first time we saw the women and children of

[013-126]the Fire People. The latter ran for the most part naked, though the

[013-127]former wore skins of wild animals.

[013-128]The Fire People, like ourselves, lived in caves. The open space in

[013-129]front of the caves sloped down to the river, and in the open space

[013-130]burned many small fires. But whether or not the Fire People cooked

[013-131]their food, I do not know. Lop-Ear and I did not see them cook. Yet it

[013-132]is my opinion that they surely must have performed some sort of rude

[013-133]cookery. Like us, they carried water in gourds from the river. There

[013-134]was much coming and going, and loud cries made by the women and

[013-135]children. The latter played about and cut up antics quite in the same

[013-136]way as did the children of the Folk, and they more nearly resembled the

[013-137]children of the Folk than did the grown Fire People resemble the grown

[013-138]Folk.

[013-139]Lop-Ear and I did not linger long. We saw some of the part-grown boys

[013-140]shooting with bow and arrow, and we sneaked back into the thicker

[013-141]forest and made our way to the river. And there we found a catamaran, a

[013-142]real catamaran, one evidently made by some Fire-Man. The two logs were

[013-143]small and straight, and were lashed together by means of tough roots

[013-144]and crosspieces of wood.

[013-145]This time the idea occurred simultaneously to us. We were trying to

[013-146]escape out of the Fire People’s territory. What better way than by

[013-147]crossing the river on these logs? We climbed on board and shoved off. A

[013-148]sudden something gripped the catamaran and flung it downstream

[013-149]violently against the bank. The abrupt stoppage almost whipped us off

[013-150]into the water. The catamaran was tied to a tree by a rope of twisted

[013-151]roots. This we untied before shoving off again.

[013-152]By the time we had paddled well out into the current, we had drifted so

[013-153]far downstream that we were in full view of the Fire People’s

[013-154]abiding-place. So occupied were we with our paddling, our eyes fixed

[013-155]upon the other bank, that we knew nothing until aroused by a yell from

[013-156]the shore. We looked around. There were the Fire People, many of them,

[013-157]looking at us and pointing at us, and more were crawling out of the

[013-158]caves. We sat up to watch, and forgot all about paddling. There was a

[013-159]great hullabaloo on the shore. Some of the Fire-Men discharged their

[013-160]bows at us, and a few of the arrows fell near us, but the range was too

[013-161]great.

[013-162]It was a great day for Lop-Ear and me. To the east the conflagration we

[013-163]had started was filling half the sky with smoke. And here we were,

[013-164]perfectly safe in the middle of the river, encircling the Fire People’s

[013-165]stronghold. We sat and laughed at them as we dashed by, swinging south,

[013-166]and southeast to east, and even to northeast, and then east again,

[013-167]southeast and south and on around to the west, a great double curve

[013-168]where the river nearly tied a knot in itself.

[013-169]As we swept on to the west, the Fire People far behind, a familiar

[013-170]scene flashed upon our eyes.

[013-171]It was the great drinking-place, where we had wandered once or twice to

[013-172]watch the circus of the animals when they came down to drink. Beyond

[013-173]it, we knew, was the carrot patch, and beyond that the caves and the

[013-174]abiding-place of the horde. We began to paddle for the bank that slid

[013-175]swiftly past, and before we knew it we were down upon the

[013-176]drinking-places used by the horde. There were the women and children,

[013-177]the water carriers, a number of them, filling their gourds. At sight of

[013-178]us they stampeded madly up the run-ways, leaving behind them a trail of

[013-179]gourds they had dropped.

[013-180]We landed, and of course we neglected to tie up the catamaran, which

[013-181]floated off down the river. Right cautiously we crept up a run-way. The

[013-182]Folk had all disappeared into their holes, though here and there we

[013-183]could see a face peering out at us. There was no sign of Red-Eye. We

[013-184]were home again. And that night we slept in our own little cave high up

[013-185]on the cliff, though first we had to evict a couple of pugnacious

[013-186]youngsters who had taken possession.
