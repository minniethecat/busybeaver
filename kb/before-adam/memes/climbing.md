## Overview

Climbing in _Before Adam_ is not a recreational activity but a fundamental survival adaptation. The Folk live, sleep, eat, and flee in the trees, and their anatomy reflects this arboreal specialization. Their hands, described as still adapted for gripping branches rather than manipulating tools, represent a stage of hominin evolution where arboreal locomotion remained the dominant mode of movement (008-187).

## In the Novel

The narrator's climbing ability exceeds anything possible for a modern human. He moves through the forest canopy with the confidence of a chimpanzee, "without fear when it came to climbing" (010-168). This skill transfers across generations — the Swift One teaches him techniques of tree-climbing that even he, with his ancestral memory, had not mastered (011-048). Climbing is the Folk's primary defense: when pursued by predators, the response is always upward, into the branches where saber-toothed cats and wolves cannot follow.

The physical description is precise — hands used "for climbing" rather than fine manipulation (008-187), feet capable of gripping, arms powerful enough to swing from branch to branch. In Chapter 14, the narrator describes climbing into caves above ground level, a transitional behavior bridging arboreal and terrestrial existence (014-069).

## Context and Significance

Paleoanthropological evidence suggests that _Australopithecus_ and early _Homo_ retained significant climbing adaptations even as bipedalism evolved. The curved finger bones and powerful shoulder girdles of early hominins indicate that arboreal locomotion remained important for millions of years after the split from the chimpanzee lineage. London's depiction captures this transitional anatomy — creatures who walk upright on the ground but still live primarily in the trees.
