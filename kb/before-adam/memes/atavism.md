## Overview

Atavism is the conceptual backbone of _Before Adam_. The narrator's entire experience — dreaming himself into the body of a Pleistocene ancestor — is framed as an atavistic phenomenon: the re-emergence of deeply buried ancestral traits in a modern human. London, influenced by Ernst Haeckel's recapitulation theory and Cesare Lombroso's criminal anthropology, uses atavism to argue that beneath civilization's veneer lies an earlier, more primal self (001-021).

## In the Novel

The term is most explicitly applied to Red-Eye, the brutish antagonist who terrorizes the Folk. London describes Red-Eye as "an atavism" — a throwback to an even earlier, more violent evolutionary stage than his contemporaries (005-086, 005-087). The narrator explains: "We were in the process of changing," evolving away from pure aggression toward greater social cooperation, but Red-Eye represented a reversion to an older type (005-088). His "place was elsewhere" — in a fiercer, more ancient past (005-093).

## Context and Significance

London's use of atavism reflects late-nineteenth-century scientific debates about heredity and criminality. Lombroso argued that "born criminals" were atavistic throwbacks to primitive humans, identifiable by physical stigmata. London extends this logic to his fictional hominins, making Red-Eye a living fossil — physically larger, more aggressive, and morally undeveloped compared to the Folk. The concept remains influential in popular understanding of evolution, though modern biology largely rejects the notion that individuals can "revert" to ancestral forms.

## Key Passages

> The lust and the atavism of Red-Eye. (001-021)

> Such an individual was an atavism, and Red-Eye was an even greater atavism. (005-086, 005-087)
