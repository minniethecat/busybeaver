## CHAPTER I

[001-001]Pictures! Pictures! Pictures! Often, before I learned, did I wonder

[001-002]whence came the multitudes of pictures that thronged my dreams; for

[001-003]they were pictures the like of which I had never seen in real

[001-004]wake-a-day life. They tormented my childhood, making of my dreams a

[001-005]procession of nightmares and a little later convincing me that I was

[001-006]different from my kind, a creature unnatural and accursed.

[001-007]In my days only did I attain any measure of happiness. My nights marked

[001-008]the reign of fear—and such fear! I make bold to state that no man of

[001-009]all the men who walk the earth with me ever suffer fear of like kind

[001-010]and degree. For my fear is the fear of long ago, the fear that was

[001-011]rampant in the Younger World, and in the youth of the Younger World. In

[001-012]short, the fear that reigned supreme in that period known as the

[001-013]Mid-Pleistocene.

[001-014]What do I mean? I see explanation is necessary before I can tell you of

[001-015]the substance of my dreams. Otherwise, little could you know of the

[001-016]meaning of the things I know so well. As I write this, all the beings

[001-017]and happenings of that other world rise up before me in vast

[001-018]phantasmagoria, and I know that to you they would be rhymeless and

[001-019]reasonless.

[001-020]What to you the friendship of Lop-Ear, the warm lure of the Swift One,

[001-021]the lust and the atavism of Red-Eye? A screaming incoherence and no

[001-022]more. And a screaming incoherence, likewise, the doings of the Fire

[001-023]People and the Tree People, and the gibbering councils of the horde.

[001-024]For you know not the peace of the cool caves in the cliffs, the circus

[001-025]of the drinking-places at the end of the day. You have never felt the

[001-026]bite of the morning wind in the tree-tops, nor is the taste of young

[001-027]bark sweet in your mouth.

[001-028]It would be better, I dare say, for you to make your approach, as I

[001-029]made mine, through my childhood. As a boy I was very like other boys—in

[001-030]my waking hours. It was in my sleep that I was different. From my

[001-031]earliest recollection my sleep was a period of terror. Rarely were my

[001-032]dreams tinctured with happiness. As a rule, they were stuffed with

[001-033]fear—and with a fear so strange and alien that it had no ponderable

[001-034]quality. No fear that I experienced in my waking life resembled the

[001-035]fear that possessed me in my sleep. It was of a quality and kind that

[001-036]transcended all my experiences.

[001-037]For instance, I was a city boy, a city child, rather, to whom the

[001-038]country was an unexplored domain. Yet I never dreamed of cities; nor

[001-039]did a house ever occur in any of my dreams. Nor, for that matter, did

[001-040]any of my human kind ever break through the wall of my sleep. I, who

[001-041]had seen trees only in parks and illustrated books, wandered in my

[001-042]sleep through interminable forests. And further, these dream trees were

[001-043]not a mere blur on my vision. They were sharp and distinct. I was on

[001-044]terms of practised intimacy with them. I saw every branch and twig; I

[001-045]saw and knew every different leaf.

[001-046]Well do I remember the first time in my waking life that I saw an oak

[001-047]tree. As I looked at the leaves and branches and gnarls, it came to me

[001-048]with distressing vividness that I had seen that same kind of tree many

[001-049]and countless times in my sleep. So I was not surprised, still later on

[001-050]in my life, to recognize instantly, the first time I saw them, trees

[001-051]such as the spruce, the yew, the birch, and the laurel. I had seen them

[001-052]all before, and was seeing them even then, every night, in my sleep.

[001-053]This, as you have already discerned, violates the first law of

[001-054]dreaming, namely, that in one’s dreams one sees only what he has seen

[001-055]in his waking life, or combinations of the things he has seen in his

[001-056]waking life. But all my dreams violated this law. In my dreams I never

[001-057]saw _anything_ of which I had knowledge in my waking life. My dream

[001-058]life and my waking life were lives apart, with not one thing in common

[001-059]save myself. I was the connecting link that somehow lived both lives.

[001-060]Early in my childhood I learned that nuts came from the grocer, berries

[001-061]from the fruit man; but before ever that knowledge was mine, in my

[001-062]dreams I picked nuts from trees, or gathered them and ate them from the

[001-063]ground underneath trees, and in the same way I ate berries from vines

[001-064]and bushes. This was beyond any experience of mine.

[001-065]I shall never forget the first time I saw blueberries served on the

[001-066]table. I had never seen blueberries before, and yet, at the sight of

[001-067]them, there leaped up in my mind memories of dreams wherein I had

[001-068]wandered through swampy land eating my fill of them. My mother set

[001-069]before me a dish of the berries. I filled my spoon, but before I raised

[001-070]it to my mouth I knew just how they would taste. Nor was I

[001-071]disappointed. It was the same tang that I had tasted a thousand times

[001-072]in my sleep.

[001-073]Snakes? Long before I had heard of the existence of snakes, I was

[001-074]tormented by them in my sleep. They lurked for me in the forest glades;

[001-075]leaped up, striking, under my feet; squirmed off through the dry grass

[001-076]or across naked patches of rock; or pursued me into the tree-tops,

[001-077]encircling the trunks with their great shining bodies, driving me

[001-078]higher and higher or farther and farther out on swaying and crackling

[001-079]branches, the ground a dizzy distance beneath me. Snakes!—with their

[001-080]forked tongues, their beady eyes and glittering scales, their hissing

[001-081]and their rattling—did I not already know them far too well on that day

[001-082]of my first circus when I saw the snake-charmer lift them up?

[001-083]They were old friends of mine, enemies rather, that peopled my nights

[001-084]with fear.

[001-085]Ah, those endless forests, and their horror-haunted gloom! For what

[001-086]eternities have I wandered through them, a timid, hunted creature,

[001-087]starting at the least sound, frightened of my own shadow, keyed-up,

[001-088]ever alert and vigilant, ready on the instant to dash away in mad

[001-089]flight for my life. For I was the prey of all manner of fierce life

[001-090]that dwelt in the forest, and it was in ecstasies of fear that I fled

[001-091]before the hunting monsters.

[001-092]When I was five years old I went to my first circus. I came home from

[001-093]it sick—but not from peanuts and pink lemonade. Let me tell you. As we

[001-094]entered the animal tent, a hoarse roaring shook the air. I tore my hand

[001-095]loose from my father’s and dashed wildly back through the entrance. I

[001-096]collided with people, fell down; and all the time I was screaming with

[001-097]terror. My father caught me and soothed me. He pointed to the crowd of

[001-098]people, all careless of the roaring, and cheered me with assurances of

[001-099]safety.

[001-100]Nevertheless, it was in fear and trembling, and with much encouragement

[001-101]on his part, that I at last approached the lion’s cage. Ah, I knew him

[001-102]on the instant. The beast! The terrible one! And on my inner vision

[001-103]flashed the memories of my dreams,—the midday sun shining on tall

[001-104]grass, the wild bull grazing quietly, the sudden parting of the grass

[001-105]before the swift rush of the tawny one, his leap to the bull’s back,

[001-106]the crashing and the bellowing, and the crunch crunch of bones; or

[001-107]again, the cool quiet of the water-hole, the wild horse up to his knees

[001-108]and drinking softly, and then the tawny one—always the tawny one!—the

[001-109]leap, the screaming and the splashing of the horse, and the crunch

[001-110]crunch of bones; and yet again, the sombre twilight and the sad silence

[001-111]of the end of day, and then the great full-throated roar, sudden, like

[001-112]a trump of doom, and swift upon it the insane shrieking and chattering

[001-113]among the trees, and I, too, am trembling with fear and am one of the

[001-114]many shrieking and chattering among the trees.

[001-115]At the sight of him, helpless, within the bars of his cage, I became

[001-116]enraged. I gritted my teeth at him, danced up and down, screaming an

[001-117]incoherent mockery and making antic faces. He responded, rushing

[001-118]against the bars and roaring back at me his impotent wrath. Ah, he knew

[001-119]me, too, and the sounds I made were the sounds of old time and

[001-120]intelligible to him.

[001-121]My parents were frightened. “The child is ill,” said my mother. “He is

[001-122]hysterical,” said my father. I never told them, and they never knew.

[001-123]Already had I developed reticence concerning this quality of mine, this

[001-124]semi-disassociation of personality as I think I am justified in calling

[001-125]it.

[001-126]I saw the snake-charmer, and no more of the circus did I see that

[001-127]night. I was taken home, nervous and overwrought, sick with the

[001-128]invasion of my real life by that other life of my dreams.

[001-129]I have mentioned my reticence. Only once did I confide the strangeness

[001-130]of it all to another. He was a boy—my chum; and we were eight years

[001-131]old. From my dreams I reconstructed for him pictures of that vanished

[001-132]world in which I do believe I once lived. I told him of the terrors of

[001-133]that early time, of Lop-Ear and the pranks we played, of the gibbering

[001-134]councils, and of the Fire People and their squatting places.

[001-135]He laughed at me, and jeered, and told me tales of ghosts and of the

[001-136]dead that walk at night. But mostly did he laugh at my feeble fancy. I

[001-137]told him more, and he laughed the harder. I swore in all earnestness

[001-138]that these things were so, and he began to look upon me queerly. Also,

[001-139]he gave amazing garblings of my tales to our playmates, until all began

[001-140]to look upon me queerly.

[001-141]It was a bitter experience, but I learned my lesson. I was different

[001-142]from my kind. I was abnormal with something they could not understand,

[001-143]and the telling of which would cause only misunderstanding. When the

[001-144]stories of ghosts and goblins went around, I kept quiet. I smiled

[001-145]grimly to myself. I thought of my nights of fear, and knew that mine

[001-146]were the real things—real as life itself, not attenuated vapors and

[001-147]surmised shadows.

[001-148]For me no terrors resided in the thought of bugaboos and wicked ogres.

[001-149]The fall through leafy branches and the dizzy heights; the snakes that

[001-150]struck at me as I dodged and leaped away in chattering flight; the wild

[001-151]dogs that hunted me across the open spaces to the timber—these were

[001-152]terrors concrete and actual, happenings and not imaginings, things of

[001-153]the living flesh and of sweat and blood. Ogres and bugaboos and I had

[001-154]been happy bed-fellows, compared with these terrors that made their bed

[001-155]with me throughout my childhood, and that still bed with me, now, as I

[001-156]write this, full of years.
