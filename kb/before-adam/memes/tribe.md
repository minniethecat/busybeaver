**Tribe** is the primary form of social organization in the world of _Before Adam_. The narrator lives among the Folk, a loosely organized horde (also called a tribe) that moves, hunts, and defends itself collectively. London portrays this tribal structure as the intermediate stage between solitary ape-like existence and fully human society — a critical step in hominid evolution.

## In the Novel

The Folk function as a tribe in every meaningful sense. They sleep together in the caves and trees (006-024-006-064), mob predators as a group (006-091-006-094), and migrate collectively when conditions demand it (012-006). The tribe provides the only safety net its members have — without it, an individual was prey.

London explicitly uses "tribe" to describe both the Folk and their rivals. The narrator encounters "a tribe of Tree People" during the [[the-great-migration|Great Migration]] (018-116), and the [[fire-people|Fire People]] also constitute a distinct tribe. The narrator reflects that cooperation was what made tribes "mighty" — "such tribes as embraced it" thrived, while those that did not perished (006-115).

The horde's structure was fluid. There was no formal chief, though dominant individuals like [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] exerted outsized influence through fear. Leadership emerged situationally — [[marrow-bone|Old Marrow-Bone]] led through wisdom and invention, while the stronger males led in combat. Fathers "did not count" in the social structure (006-107); kinship traced through mothers, and children separated from parents at an early age [004-023, 004-060].

> Living in the thick of the horde did not conduce to monogamy. (006-118)
