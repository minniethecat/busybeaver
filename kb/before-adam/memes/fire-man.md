# Fire-Man

The **Fire-Man** is a scout of the [[fire-people|Fire People]] encountered by [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] during their childhood explorations. He represents the first direct contact between the young members of the [[fire-people|Folk]] and the more technologically advanced [[fire-people|Fire People]], whose possession of the [[bow-and-arrow|bow and arrow]] and use of animal-skin clothing mark them as a more evolved human type.

## First Encounter

Big-Tooth's first sight of a Fire-Man came when he was perched in a tree. The scout was creeping along the ground, peering upward:

> I heard the crackle of a dry twig, and looking down saw my first Fire-Man. He was creeping stealthily along on the ground and peering up into the tree. At first I thought he was a wild animal, because he wore around his waist and over his shoulders a ragged piece of bearskin.[^1]

Big-Tooth noted the physical differences: the Fire-Man was less hairy than the Folk, and his feet were less hand-like — anatomical features that marked the evolutionary distance between the two hominid populations.[^1]

## Killing of Broken-Tooth

The Fire-Man's most significant act is the killing of [[broken-tooth|Broken-Tooth]]. Armed with a [[bow-and-arrow|bow and arrow]], the scout circled the tree where the three young Folk — Big-Tooth, Lop-Ear, and Broken-Tooth — were hiding. Lop-Ear, who had previous experience with the Fire People, kept the tree trunk between himself and the scout.[^2]

The Fire-Man's first arrow missed Lop-Ear, glancing off a limb. Big-Tooth, from his higher perch, viewed the exchange as a game. The scout then turned his attention to Broken-Tooth, who was "teetering slightly and involuntarily on the end of the branch." The first arrow struck upward; the second found its mark:

> The bow-string twanged, the arrow leaped upward, and Broken-Tooth, uttering a terrible scream, fell off the branch. I saw him as he went down, turning over and over, all arms and legs it seemed, the shaft of the arrow projecting from his chest and appearing and disappearing with each revolution of his body.[^3]

After Broken-Tooth fell, the Fire-Man "ran forward with a stone and hammered him on the head" — finishing the child off.[^4] This traumatic event recurs throughout Big-Tooth's dream-memories and is the source of his childhood nightmares.

## The Arrow Wound

During the flight that followed, Big-Tooth was struck by one of the Fire-Man's arrows. The shaft embedded in his right leg, "protruding head and shaft from either side," causing burning pain and hampering his movement through the trees. When he could no longer keep up, Lop-Ear returned to help him remove the arrow — a moment of loyalty that cemented their friendship.[^5]

## Later Appearances

The Fire People continued their encroachment into the Folk's territory. Big-Tooth and Lop-Ear later found a [[catamaran]] evidently built by a Fire-Man, which they used to cross the river and escape from Fire People territory.[^6] During the final attack on the caves, Fire-Men used fire and smoke to drive the Folk from their dwellings, raining arrows upon them as they emerged.[^7]

## Others' View of the Fire-Man

To the Folk, the Fire-Man was an object of terror and awe — a figure from a more advanced world whose technological superiority translated directly into lethal power. Big-Tooth's childhood encounter with the Fire-Man scout left an indelible mark on his dream-memories, recurring as nightmare imagery throughout his life. The scout's bearskin clothing and bow-and-arrow seemed almost supernatural to the Folk, who wore no clothing and fought with clubs and stones.[^1]

Lop-Ear, who had encountered Fire People before, regarded them with a wary respect founded on experience. His strategy of keeping the tree trunk between himself and the scout was not cowardice but hard-won survival knowledge — the kind of practical wisdom that separated those who lived from those who died in the contested borderlands between the two species.[^2] Lop-Ear understood what Big-Tooth, in his innocence, could not: the Fire-Man was not playing a game.

Big-Tooth's own view evolved over time. As a child, he initially perceived the arrow-exchange as entertainment — a detachment that shattered with Broken-Tooth's death. The killing transformed the Fire-Man from a curiosity into a mortal enemy in Big-Tooth's mental map. The arrow wound he received during the flight became a physical reminder of that transformation: the scar on his leg was also a scar on his understanding of the world.[^5]

The broader horde saw the Fire People collectively as an existential threat — a technologically superior group whose territorial expansion pushed relentlessly into Folk lands. When Fire-Men used smoke to drive the Folk from their caves and rained arrows upon the fleeing families, the horde's worst fears were confirmed: the Fire People were not merely competitors but exterminators.[^7] In the Folk's oral tradition — to the extent that a proto-linguistic horde had one — the Fire-Man became synonymous with death from afar, a killer who struck without ever coming within arm's reach.
