## CHAPTER XVI

[016-001]I found her down in the old neighborhood near the blueberry swamp,

[016-002]where my mother lived and where Lop-Ear and I had built our first

[016-003]tree-shelter. It was unexpected. As I came under the tree I heard the

[016-004]familiar soft sound and looked up. There she was, the Swift One,

[016-005]sitting on a limb and swinging her legs back and forth as she looked at

[016-006]me.

[016-007]I stood still for some time. The sight of her had made me very happy.

[016-008]And then an unrest and a pain began to creep in on this happiness. I

[016-009]started to climb the tree after her, and she retreated slowly out the

[016-010]limb. Just as I reached for her, she sprang through the air and landed

[016-011]in the branches of the next tree. From amid the rustling leaves she

[016-012]peeped out at me and made soft sounds. I leaped straight for her, and

[016-013]after an exciting chase the situation was duplicated, for there she

[016-014]was, making soft sounds and peeping out from the leaves of a third

[016-015]tree.

[016-016]It was borne in upon me that somehow it was different now from the old

[016-017]days before Lop-Ear and I had gone on our adventure-journey. I wanted

[016-018]her, and I knew that I wanted her. And she knew it, too. That was why

[016-019]she would not let me come near her. I forgot that she was truly the

[016-020]Swift One, and that in the art of climbing she had been my teacher. I

[016-021]pursued her from tree to tree, and ever she eluded me, peeping back at

[016-022]me with kindly eyes, making soft sounds, and dancing and leaping and

[016-023]teetering before me just out of reach. The more she eluded me, the more

[016-024]I wanted to catch her, and the lengthening shadows of the afternoon

[016-025]bore witness to the futility of my effort.

[016-026]As I pursued her, or sometimes rested in an adjoining tree and watched

[016-027]her, I noticed the change in her. She was larger, heavier, more

[016-028]grown-up. Her lines were rounder, her muscles fuller, and there was

[016-029]about her that indefinite something of maturity that was new to her and

[016-030]that incited me on. Three years she had been gone—three years at the

[016-031]very least, and the change in her was marked. I say three years; it is

[016-032]as near as I can measure the time. A fourth year may have elapsed,

[016-033]which I have confused with the happenings of the other three years. The

[016-034]more I think of it, the more confident I am that it must be four years

[016-035]that she was away.

[016-036]Where she went, why she went, and what happened to her during that

[016-037]time, I do not know. There was no way for her to tell me, any more than

[016-038]there was a way for Lop-Ear and me to tell the Folk what we had seen

[016-039]when we were away. Like us, the chance is she had gone off on an

[016-040]adventure-journey, and by herself. On the other hand, it is possible

[016-041]that Red-Eye may have been the cause of her going. It is quite certain

[016-042]that he must have come upon her from time to time, wandering in the

[016-043]woods; and if he had pursued her there is no question but that it would

[016-044]have been sufficient to drive her away. From subsequent events, I am

[016-045]led to believe that she must have travelled far to the south, across a

[016-046]range of mountains and down to the banks of a strange river, away from

[016-047]any of her kind. Many Tree People lived down there, and I think it must

[016-048]have been they who finally drove her back to the horde and to me. My

[016-049]reasons for this I shall explain later.

[016-050]The shadows grew longer, and I pursued more ardently than ever, and

[016-051]still I could not catch her. She made believe that she was trying

[016-052]desperately to escape me, and all the time she managed to keep just

[016-053]beyond reach. I forgot everything—time, the oncoming of night, and my

[016-054]meat-eating enemies. I was insane with love of her, and with—anger,

[016-055]too, because she would not let me come up with her. It was strange how

[016-056]this anger against her seemed to be part of my desire for her.

[016-057]As I have said, I forgot everything. In racing across an open space I

[016-058]ran full tilt upon a colony of snakes. They did not deter me. I was

[016-059]mad. They struck at me, but I ducked and dodged and ran on. Then there

[016-060]was a python that ordinarily would have sent me screeching to a

[016-061]tree-top. He did run me into a tree; but the Swift One was going out of

[016-062]sight, and I sprang back to the ground and went on. It was a close

[016-063]shave. Then there was my old enemy, the hyena. From my conduct he was

[016-064]sure something was going to happen, and he followed me for an hour.

[016-065]Once we exasperated a band of wild pigs, and they took after us. The

[016-066]Swift One dared a wide leap between trees that was too much for me. I

[016-067]had to take to the ground. There were the pigs. I didn’t care. I struck

[016-068]the earth within a yard of the nearest one. They flanked me as I ran,

[016-069]and chased me into two different trees out of the line of my pursuit of

[016-070]the Swift One. I ventured the ground again, doubled back, and crossed a

[016-071]wide open space, with the whole band grunting, bristling, and

[016-072]tusk-gnashing at my heels.

[016-073]If I had tripped or stumbled in that open space, there would have been

[016-074]no chance for me. But I didn’t. And I didn’t care whether I did or not.

[016-075]I was in such mood that I would have faced old Saber-Tooth himself, or

[016-076]a score of arrow-shooting Fire People. Such was the madness of

[016-077]love...with me. With the Swift One it was different. She was very wise.

[016-078]She did not take any real risks, and I remember, on looking back across

[016-079]the centuries to that wild love-chase, that when the pigs delayed me

[016-080]she did not run away very fast, but waited, rather, for me to take up

[016-081]the pursuit again. Also, she directed her retreat before me, going

[016-082]always in the direction she wanted to go.

[016-083]At last came the dark. She led me around the mossy shoulder of a canyon

[016-084]wall that out-jutted among the trees. After that we penetrated a dense

[016-085]mass of underbrush that scraped and ripped me in passing. But she never

[016-086]ruffled a hair. She knew the way. In the midst of the thicket was a

[016-087]large oak. I was very close to her when she climbed it; and in the

[016-088]forks, in the nest-shelter I had sought so long and vainly, I caught

[016-089]her.

[016-090]The hyena had taken our trail again, and he now sat down on the ground

[016-091]and made hungry noises. But we did not mind, and we laughed at him when

[016-092]he snarled and went away through the thicket. It was the spring-time,

[016-093]and the night noises were many and varied. As was the custom at that

[016-094]time of the year, there was much fighting among the animals. From the

[016-095]nest we could hear the squealing and neighing of wild horses, the

[016-096]trumpeting of elephants, and the roaring of lions. But the moon came

[016-097]out, and the air was warm, and we laughed and were unafraid.

[016-098]I remember, next morning, that we came upon two ruffled cock-birds that

[016-099]fought so ardently that I went right up to them and caught them by

[016-100]their necks. Thus did the Swift One and I get our wedding breakfast.

[016-101]They were delicious. It was easy to catch birds in the spring of the

[016-102]year. There was one night that year when two elk fought in the

[016-103]moonlight, while the Swift One and I watched from the trees; and we saw

[016-104]a lion and lioness crawl up to them unheeded, and kill them as they

[016-105]fought.

[016-106]There is no telling how long we might have lived in the Swift One’s

[016-107]tree-shelter. But one day, while we were away, the tree was struck by

[016-108]lightning. Great limbs were riven, and the nest was demolished. I

[016-109]started to rebuild, but the Swift One would have nothing to do with it.

[016-110]As I was to learn, she was greatly afraid of lightning, and I could not

[016-111]persuade her back into the tree. So it came about, our honeymoon over,

[016-112]that we went to the caves to live. As Lop-Ear had evicted me from the

[016-113]cave when he got married, I now evicted him; and the Swift One and I

[016-114]settled down in it, while he slept at night in the connecting passage

[016-115]of the double cave.

[016-116]And with our coming to live with the horde came trouble. Red-Eye had

[016-117]had I don’t know how many wives since the Singing One. She had gone the

[016-118]way of the rest. At present he had a little, soft, spiritless thing

[016-119]that whimpered and wept all the time, whether he beat her or not; and

[016-120]her passing was a question of very little time. Before she passed,

[016-121]even, Red-Eye set his eyes on the Swift One; and when she passed, the

[016-122]persecution of the Swift One began.

[016-123]Well for her that she was the Swift One, that she had that amazing

[016-124]aptitude for swift flight through the trees. She needed all her wisdom

[016-125]and daring in order to keep out of the clutches of Red-Eye. I could not

[016-126]help her. He was so powerful a monster that he could have torn me limb

[016-127]from limb. As it was, to my death I carried an injured shoulder that

[016-128]ached and went lame in rainy weather and that was a mark of his

[016-129]handiwork.

[016-130]The Swift One was sick at the time I received this injury. It must have

[016-131]been a touch of the malaria from which we sometimes suffered; but

[016-132]whatever it was, it made her dull and heavy. She did not have the

[016-133]accustomed spring to her muscles, and was indeed in poor shape for

[016-134]flight when Red-Eye cornered her near the lair of the wild dogs,

[016-135]several miles south from the caves. Usually, she would have circled

[016-136]around him, beaten him in the straight-away, and gained the protection

[016-137]of our small-mouthed cave. But she could not circle him. She was too

[016-138]dull and slow. Each time he headed her off, until she gave over the

[016-139]attempt and devoted her energies wholly to keeping out of his clutches.

[016-140]Had she not been sick it would have been child’s play for her to elude

[016-141]him; but as it was, it required all her caution and cunning. It was to

[016-142]her advantage that she could travel on thinner branches than he, and

[016-143]make wider leaps. Also, she was an unerring judge of distance, and she

[016-144]had an instinct for knowing the strength of twigs, branches, and rotten

[016-145]limbs.

[016-146]It was an interminable chase. Round and round and back and forth for

[016-147]long stretches through the forest they dashed. There was great

[016-148]excitement among the other Folk. They set up a wild chattering, that

[016-149]was loudest when Red-Eye was at a distance, and that hushed when the

[016-150]chase led him near. They were impotent onlookers. The females screeched

[016-151]and gibbered, and the males beat their chests in helpless rage. Big

[016-152]Face was especially angry, and though he hushed his racket when Red-Eye

[016-153]drew near, he did not hush it to the extent the others did.

[016-154]As for me, I played no brave part. I know I was anything but a hero.

[016-155]Besides, of what use would it have been for me to encounter Red-Eye? He

[016-156]was the mighty monster, the abysmal brute, and there was no hope for me

[016-157]in a conflict of strength. He would have killed me, and the situation

[016-158]would have remained unchanged. He would have caught the Swift One

[016-159]before she could have gained the cave. As it was, I could only look on

[016-160]in helpless fury, and dodge out of the way and cease my raging when he

[016-161]came too near.

[016-162]The hours passed. It was late afternoon. And still the chase went on.

[016-163]Red-Eye was bent upon exhausting the Swift One. He deliberately ran her

[016-164]down. After a long time she began to tire and could no longer maintain

[016-165]her headlong flight. Then it was that she began going far out on the

[016-166]thinnest branches, where he could not follow. Thus she might have got a

[016-167]breathing spell, but Red-Eye was fiendish. Unable to follow her, he

[016-168]dislodged her by shaking her off. With all his strength and weight, he

[016-169]would shake the branch back and forth until he snapped her off as one

[016-170]would snap a fly from a whip-lash. The first time, she saved herself by

[016-171]falling into branches lower down. Another time, though they did not

[016-172]save her from the ground, they broke her fall. Still another time, so

[016-173]fiercely did he snap her from the branch, she was flung clear across a

[016-174]gap into another tree. It was remarkable, the way she gripped and saved

[016-175]herself. Only when driven to it did she seek the temporary safety of

[016-176]the thin branches. But she was so tired that she could not otherwise

[016-177]avoid him, and time after time she was compelled to take to the thin

[016-178]branches.

[016-179]Still the chase went on, and still the Folk screeched, beat their

[016-180]chests, and gnashed their teeth. Then came the end. It was almost

[016-181]twilight. Trembling, panting, struggling for breath, the Swift One

[016-182]clung pitiably to a high thin branch. It was thirty feet to the ground,

[016-183]and nothing intervened. Red-Eye swung back and forth on the branch

[016-184]farther down. It became a pendulum, swinging wider and wider with every

[016-185]lunge of his weight. Then he reversed suddenly, just before the

[016-186]downward swing was completed. Her grips were torn loose, and,

[016-187]screaming, she was hurled toward the ground.

[016-188]But she righted herself in mid-air and descended feet first.

[016-189]Ordinarily, from such a height, the spring in her legs would have eased

[016-190]the shock of impact with the ground. But she was exhausted. She could

[016-191]not exercise this spring. Her legs gave under her, having only partly

[016-192]met the shock, and she crashed on over on her side. This, as it turned

[016-193]out, did not injure her, but it did knock the breath from her lungs.

[016-194]She lay helpless and struggling for air.

[016-195]Red-Eye rushed upon her and seized her. With his gnarly fingers twisted

[016-196]into the hair of her head, he stood up and roared in triumph and

[016-197]defiance at the awed Folk that watched from the trees. Then it was that

[016-198]I went mad. Caution was thrown to the winds; forgotten was the will to

[016-199]live of my flesh. Even as Red-Eye roared, from behind I dashed upon

[016-200]him. So unexpected was my charge that I knocked him off his feet. I

[016-201]twined my arms and legs around him and strove to hold him down. This

[016-202]would have been impossible to accomplish had he not held tightly with

[016-203]one hand to the Swift One’s hair.

[016-204]Encouraged by my conduct, Big-Face became a sudden ally. He charged in,

[016-205]sank his teeth in Red-Eye’s arm, and ripped and tore at his face. This

[016-206]was the time for the rest of the Folk to have joined in. It was the

[016-207]chance to do for Red-Eye for all time. But they remained afraid in the

[016-208]trees.

[016-209]It was inevitable that Red-Eye should win in the struggle against the

[016-210]two of us. The reason he did not finish us off immediately was that the

[016-211]Swift One clogged his movements. She had regained her breath and was

[016-212]beginning to resist. He would not release his clutch on her hair, and

[016-213]this handicapped him. He got a grip on my arm. It was the beginning of

[016-214]the end for me. He began to draw me toward him into a position where he

[016-215]could sink his teeth into my throat. His mouth was open, and he was

[016-216]grinning. And yet, though he had just begun to exert his strength, in

[016-217]that moment he wrenched my shoulder so that I suffered from it for the

[016-218]remainder of my life.

[016-219]And in that moment something happened. There was no warning. A great

[016-220]body smashed down upon the four of us locked together. We were driven

[016-221]violently apart and rolled over and over, and in the suddenness of

[016-222]surprise we released our holds on one another. At the moment of the

[016-223]shock, Big-Face screamed terribly. I did not know what had happened,

[016-224]though I smelled tiger and caught a glimpse of striped fur as I sprang

[016-225]for a tree.

[016-226]It was old Saber-Tooth. Aroused in his lair by the noise we had made,

[016-227]he had crept upon us unnoticed. The Swift One gained the next tree to

[016-228]mine, and I immediately joined her. I put my arms around her and held

[016-229]her close to me while she whimpered and cried softly. From the ground

[016-230]came a snarling, and crunching of bones. It was Saber-Tooth making his

[016-231]supper off of what had been Big-Face. From beyond, with inflamed rims

[016-232]and eyes, Red-Eye peered down. Here was a monster mightier than he. The

[016-233]Swift One and I turned and went away quietly through the trees toward

[016-234]the cave, while the Folk gathered overhead and showered down abuse and

[016-235]twigs and branches upon their ancient enemy. He lashed his tail and

[016-236]snarled, but went on eating.

[016-237]And in such fashion were we saved. It was a mere accident—the sheerest

[016-238]accident. Else would I have died, there in Red-Eye’s clutch, and there

[016-239]would have been no bridging of time to the tune of a thousand centuries

[016-240]down to a progeny that reads newspapers and rides on electric cars—ay,

[016-241]and that writes narratives of bygone happenings even as this is

[016-242]written.
