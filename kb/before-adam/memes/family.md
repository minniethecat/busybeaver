**Family** in the world of _Before Adam_ centers on the mother-child bond, the only recognized kinship tie in the horde. Fathers "did not count in our horde" (006-107) — the narrator has no memory of his father beyond a single, terrifying image (004-013). London portrays this as an early stage in human social evolution, before pair-bonding and paternal recognition became established.

## In the Novel

The narrator's earliest memories are of his mother. She carried him, fed him, and defended him — once being treed by a predator while holding him (004-008). He describes clinging to her as a child and being "warned away" when he later tried to approach her with a new infant (010-019). This separation was normal: children left their mothers early in life (004-060), joining the broader horde for survival.

Maternal recognition was powerful despite the absence of names. Mothers "uttered querulous, anxious cries, which were recognized by the young ones" (005-041-005-042). The narrator felt confident he would have recognized his mother's voice "amongst the voices of a thousand" (005-043).

Pair-bonding was limited and unstable. There were "several faithful couples" living near the narrator's mother (006-116), but "living in the thick of the horde did not conduce to monogamy" (006-118). The social structure was communitarian rather than nuclear.

Later in life, the narrator forms a lasting bond with the [[swift-one|Swift One]]. Together they manage to raise one child, a boy, through the harsh post-migration environment (018-058-018-060). This represents the novel's arc from primitive horde promiscuity toward something approaching the human family.
