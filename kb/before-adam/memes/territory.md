**Territory** is the spatial foundation of life for the [[the-folk|Folk]]. Their existence is defined by the lands they inhabit, the boundaries they defend, and the territories they are forced to abandon. From the limestone [[caves]] to the [[the-sea|sea]], territory maps their survival, their conflicts, and their evolution as a people.

## The Cave Territory

The Folk's primary territory centered on the limestone [[caves]] overlooking the [[the-river|river]] — a defensible position offering shelter, water access, and foraging grounds. The caves represented safety: their crevices and ledges provided refuge from ground predators like [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] (006-024). The surrounding territory included:

## Territorial Competition

Territory was never secure. The Folk competed with multiple groups for access to prime locations:

- The [[fire-people|Fire People]] encroached from beyond the river, their superior technology making them a persistent threat to Folk territory (005-001-005-010).

- The [[tree-people|Tree People]] inhabited the forests and represented a different mode of territorial adaptation — arboreal rather than cave-dwelling.

- Within the Folk's own range, seasonal availability of food (berries, nuts, game) dictated movement patterns within their territory.

## The Loss of Cave Territory

The [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People massacre]] marked the catastrophic loss of the cave territory. When Fire Men attacked with fire and arrows, they systematically smoked out each cave tier, killing most of the inhabitants (017-001-017-100). The surviving Folk had no choice but to abandon their ancestral home — a territory they had occupied for generations — and flee into an uncertain future.

## Territory and Memory

Territory for the Folk was not only a physical location but a repository of collective [[memory]]. The narrator's dream-memories repeatedly return him to specific places: the [[caves|cave]] tiers, the [[blueberry-swamp|blueberry swamp]], the [[the-open-space|open space]] before the cliff face. These are not merely settings but carriers of meaning — each location encodes the experiences, losses, and triumphs associated with it.

The narrator describes returning to his "old home territory" after the [[adventure-journey]], searching the area between the blueberry swamp and the slough (014-009-014-012). This act of return — of seeking out the familiar ground despite its dangers — reveals territory as a form of identity. To know a place is to know oneself; to lose a territory is to lose a part of the collective self.

After the [[the-fire-people-massacre|massacre]], the forced abandonment of the cave territory represented more than a practical setback. It was a rupture in the Folk's continuity — a severing from the place that had shaped them. The [[the-great-migration|Great Migration]] was not merely a journey through space but a passage from one identity to another. The Folk who reached the [[the-sea|sea]] were no longer cave-dwellers; they had become coastal people, their identity remade by the territory they now inhabited.

## Territory and Identity

For the Folk, territory was not merely geography but identity. The narrator's persistent returns to his "old home territory" after the [[adventure-journey]], searching the area between the blueberry swamp and the slough (014-009-014-012), demonstrate the deep attachment to place. Territory held memories, defined group membership, and provided the spatial framework within which the Folk understood themselves.

## The Sea as Final Territory

After the long migration through the mountains — suffering "the hunger we endured on the mountains" (012-009) — the surviving Folk reached the [[the-sea|sea]]. This became their final territory: a place where they "lived for years" (018-034), building new dwellings, adapting to a coastal existence, and becoming, in the narrator's retrospective view, a different people than the cave-dwellers they had been.
