## Overview

Memory in *Before Adam* operates at two levels. There is the ordinary memory of lived experience — Big-Tooth remembering the layout of [[the-cliffs|the cliffs]], the taste of carrots, the face of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]. And there is the extraordinary memory that bridges epochs: the modern narrator's ability to recall, in vivid detail, the life of a hominid who lived hundreds of thousands of years before him.

## Ancestral Memory

The narrator's relationship to Big-Tooth is not literary invention but inherited experience. "I know the things I know," he insists, because they come to him in [[sleep|dreams]] — dreams that are not fantasies but memories. The mechanism London invokes drew on contemporary scientific discourse about racial memory: the idea that experiences acquired by ancestors could be transmitted biologically to descendants.

This was a serious scientific hypothesis in London's time, associated with thinkers like Jean-Baptiste Lamarck and, later, Sigmund Freud's speculations about inherited trauma. London uses it as the narrative frame for the entire novel: the narrator does not imagine Big-Tooth's world — he *remembers* it.

## Memory and Identity

Big-Tooth's memories define his identity. He remembers his mother's care, [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]]'s friendship, the terror of the [[the-forest-fire|forest fire]], and the sweetness of the [[swift-one|Swift One]]'s presence. These memories make him who he is — a specific individual within the collective life of the [[the-horde|horde]]. When the horde is destroyed in the [[the-fire-people-massacre|massacre]], it is memory that preserves what was lost.

## Memory as Storytelling

The novel itself is an act of memory. The narrator writes down what he remembers from his dreams, creating a record that bridges the Pleistocene and the present. In this sense, *Before Adam* is about the origins of storytelling itself — the human impulse to preserve experience in words, to pass memory across not just generations but epochs.
