## Overview

Lop-Ear is the narrator [[Big-Tooth]]'s closest boyhood friend and constant companion through the early chapters of the story. Named for his single permanently limp ear — "some accident had injured the muscles and deprived him of the use of it" (005-121) — he is defined by a blend of playfulness and instinctive caution that makes him a reliable partner in the dangerous [[Mid-Pleistocene]] world. Along with Big-Tooth, Lop-Ear forms a small, self-sufficient unit within [[the-horde]], sharing a [[caves|cave]], hunting together, and navigating the daily perils of life among the Folk.

## Name and Physical Description

The name "Lop-Ear" was devised by Big-Tooth himself:

> I have so named him because he could prick up only one of his ears. The other ear always hung limp and without movement. Some accident had injured the muscles and deprived him of the use of it. (005-119)

Physically, Lop-Ear is a year older than the narrator but notably less aggressive. Big-Tooth describes their dynamic plainly: "Lop-Ear was a year older than I, but I was several times angrier than he" (005-131). What Lop-Ear lacks in temper he makes up for in local knowledge — he knows the river run-ways, the cave entrances, and the safest sleeping crevices.

## Friendship with Big-Tooth

Lop-Ear's relationship with the narrator begins with a fight — fittingly for the primitive world both inhabit. When the young Big-Tooth, newly arrived at the [[caves]] and surrounded by a circle of teasing children, singles out the ringleader, that ringleader proves to be Lop-Ear. The brawl ends in friendship, and the two quickly become inseparable:

> We were now good friends. When the other young ones gathered around to tease, he joined with me in attacking them; and so viciously did we behave that before long I was let alone. Lop-Ear made me acquainted with the village. (005-162)

It is Lop-Ear who introduces Big-Tooth to the village layout and the customs of [[the-horde]]. Though "there was little that he could tell me of conditions and customs — he had not the necessary vocabulary" (005-164), what he cannot explain in words he demonstrates through action, and the narrator learns by imitation.

## Encounter with the Fire-Man

One of the most dramatic episodes involving Lop-Ear occurs in CHAPTER VII, when the two friends venture beyond their usual range and encounter a member of the [[fire-people|Fire People]] — a being armed with a bow and arrow, a technology unknown to the Folk. While Big-Tooth sees only a curious stranger "throwing things," Lop-Ear immediately recognizes the mortal danger:

> But Lop-Ear knew. He had evidently seen the Fire People before and knew something of their ways. The Fire-Man peered up at him and circled around the tree. And around the main trunk above the fork Lop-Ear circled too, keeping always the trunk between himself and the Fire-Man. (007-076)

Lop-Ear dodges one arrow that "glanced against a limb" (007-083). The encounter results in the death of [[Broken-Tooth]] and a wound to Big-Tooth himself from an arrow in the leg.

## Loyalty Under Duress

When the wounded narrator can no longer keep up with the flight through the forest, Lop-Ear does not abandon him:

> Lop-Ear went right on. I called to him — most plaintively, I remember; and he stopped and looked back. Then he returned to me, climbing into the fork and examining the arrow. He tried to pull it out, but one way the flesh resisted the barbed head, and the other way it resisted the feathered shaft. Also, it hurt grievously, and I stopped him. (007-123)

This moment — Lop-Ear turning back despite his own fear — is one of the novel's clearest demonstrations of the bond between the two companions. They "wandered in the land north" together (012-001), surviving on their own far from the horde.

## Caution and Instinct

Lop-Ear's most prominent behavioral trait is his instinctive caution. He is the prudent counterweight to Big-Tooth's impulsiveness. When [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] approaches, Lop-Ear detects the danger before the narrator:

> The first I knew, Lop-Ear had shrunk away to one side and was crouching low against the bank. Naturally and involuntarily, I imitated him. Then it was that I looked to see the cause of his fear. It was Red-Eye, swaggering down the run-way. (005-173)

He leads the way to their sleeping place — a cleverly hidden crevice high in the bluff, "higher than all the other caves" and invisible from the ground (005-182), demonstrating an instinct for selecting secure [[shelter]].

## Life with the Horde

Lop-Ear functions as an essentially independent member of [[the-horde]]. As Big-Tooth notes, "Lop-Ear and I were independent. We did not bother with the rest of the tribe" (008-074). They hunt, forage, and sleep together as a self-contained unit. In the cold of the winter cave, "Lop-Ear and I sit close together, shivering" (008-002), sharing warmth against the Pleistocene cold.
