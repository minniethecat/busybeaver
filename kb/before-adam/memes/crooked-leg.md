# Crooked-Leg

**Crooked-Leg** is a member of the [[fire-people|Folk]], the husband of [[singing-one|the Singing One]] and one of the most tragic victims of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s tyranny. Though small, physically impaired, and by nature non-confrontational, he died in an act of extraordinary courage — standing up to the horde's most feared tyrant to protect his mate.

## Character

Crooked-Leg is introduced as a gentle figure, notably different from the aggressive norm of the Folk:

> He was a quiet individual, molesting no one and not given to bickering with his fellows. He was no fighter anyway. He was small and lean, and not so active on his legs as the rest of us.[^1]

His name, given by Big-Tooth, reflects his physical impairment — a detail consistent with the narrator's practice of naming the Folk by their most distinctive feature.

## The Heroic Stand

Crooked-Leg's defining moment comes when [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], having decided to claim the Singing One as his new mate, pursues her to the drinking-place. The Singing One — Crooked-Leg's recently mated wife — flees to her husband for protection:

> She ran to her husband. Poor little Crooked-Leg was terribly scared. But he was a hero. He knew that death was upon him, yet he did not run away. He stood up, and chattered, bristled, and showed his teeth.[^2]

Red-Eye, enraged that anyone would dare resist him, seized Crooked-Leg by the neck. In his final act, Crooked-Leg "sank his teeth into Red-Eye's arm" — the only wound inflicted on the tyrant during this encounter. The next moment, with a broken neck, Crooked-Leg was "floundering and squirming on the ground."[^3]

## Significance

Crooked-Leg's death is one of the most poignant moments in the narrative. His stand against Red-Eye — a fight he knew he could not win — embodies a quiet heroism that stands in stark contrast to the cowering passivity that Red-Eye's reign of terror normally inspired in the Folk. His sacrifice is noted by Big-Tooth as the marker of Red-Eye's most outrageous deed: the murder of a peaceful man and the forcible taking of his mate.
