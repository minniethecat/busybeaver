## Overview

The Mountain Passage was the bleakest period of the Folk's [[the-great-migration|great migration]] — a stretch of "weeks and months" (012-004) through the unceasing mountain ranges between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[far-lake|Far Lake]]. Unlike the familiar territory of the caves, the mountains offered no permanent shelter, no reliable food sources, and no landmarks to navigate by. They were "bleak and inhospitable," and the Folk "suffered greatly from the cold" (012-077).

The narrator's memories of this period are notably fragmentary. "I have only misty memories of the great climb of the mountains" (012-085). Throughout the rest of the narrative, his recall is vivid and detailed, rich with sensory impressions and emotional texture. The Mountains, by contrast, are a blank — a period of such sustained hardship that memory itself seems to have shut down, retaining only fragments of hunger, cold, and the unceasing labor of climbing one ridge only to see another beyond it.

## The Hunger

The defining experience of the mountain passage was hunger. The mountains offered little game and few edible plants. The Folk subsisted on chance: a half-grown calf sleeping in a thicket (012-010), another part-grown calf on the shores of a lake (012-101), salmon in a stream flowing from [[far-lake|Far Lake]] (012-105-012-106).

These windfalls were rare. Most days brought nothing. The narrator's description of "the hunger we endured on the mountains between Long Lake and Far Lake" (012-009) carries the weight of lived experience — not the abstract knowledge of deprivation but the physical memory of a body slowly consuming itself.

## The Memory Gap

The fragmentary nature of the narrator's mountain memories is itself significant. Throughout the rest of the novel, the dreams are intensely vivid — every leaf, every sound, every flicker of fear. But the mountains are remembered only in outline: cold, hunger, the endless ridges.

This memory gap suggests a psychological defense mechanism. The brain, overwhelmed by sustained deprivation, stopped recording the details and preserved only the essential: that it was terrible, that it lasted a long time, and that it ended. The mountains were not a place to be remembered but an ordeal to be survived — and survival, in this case, meant forgetting.
