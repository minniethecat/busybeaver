## CHAPTER III

[003-001]The commonest dream of my early childhood was something like this: It

[003-002]seemed that I was very small and that I lay curled up in a sort of nest

[003-003]of twigs and boughs. Sometimes I was lying on my back. In this position

[003-004]it seemed that I spent many hours, watching the play of sunlight on the

[003-005]foliage and the stirring of the leaves by the wind. Often the nest

[003-006]itself moved back and forth when the wind was strong.

[003-007]But always, while so lying in the nest, I was mastered as of tremendous

[003-008]space beneath me. I never saw it, I never peered over the edge of the

[003-009]nest to see; but I _knew_ and feared that space that lurked just

[003-010]beneath me and that ever threatened me like a maw of some all-devouring

[003-011]monster.

[003-012]This dream, in which I was quiescent and which was more like a

[003-013]condition than an experience of action, I dreamed very often in my

[003-014]early childhood. But suddenly, there would rush into the very midst of

[003-015]it strange forms and ferocious happenings, the thunder and crashing of

[003-016]storm, or unfamiliar landscapes such as in my wake-a-day life I had

[003-017]never seen. The result was confusion and nightmare. I could comprehend

[003-018]nothing of it. There was no logic of sequence.

[003-019]You see, I did not dream consecutively. One moment I was a wee babe of

[003-020]the Younger World lying in my tree nest; the next moment I was a grown

[003-021]man of the Younger World locked in combat with the hideous Red-Eye; and

[003-022]the next moment I was creeping carefully down to the water-hole in the

[003-023]heat of the day. Events, years apart in their occurrence in the Younger

[003-024]World, occurred with me within the space of several minutes, or

[003-025]seconds.

[003-026]It was all a jumble, but this jumble I shall not inflict upon you. It

[003-027]was not until I was a young man and had dreamed many thousand times,

[003-028]that everything straightened out and became clear and plain. Then it

[003-029]was that I got the clew of time, and was able to piece together events

[003-030]and actions in their proper order. Thus was I able to reconstruct the

[003-031]vanished Younger World as it was at the time I lived in it—or at the

[003-032]time my other-self lived in it. The distinction does not matter; for I,

[003-033]too, the modern man, have gone back and lived that early life in the

[003-034]company of my other-self.

[003-035]For your convenience, since this is to be no sociological screed, I

[003-036]shall frame together the different events into a comprehensive story.

[003-037]For there is a certain thread of continuity and happening that runs

[003-038]through all the dreams. There is my friendship with Lop-Ear, for

[003-039]instance. Also, there is the enmity of Red-Eye, and the love of the

[003-040]Swift One. Taking it all in all, a fairly coherent and interesting

[003-041]story I am sure you will agree.

[003-042]I do not remember much of my mother. Possibly the earliest recollection

[003-043]I have of her—and certainly the sharpest—is the following: It seemed I

[003-044]was lying on the ground. I was somewhat older than during the nest

[003-045]days, but still helpless. I rolled about in the dry leaves, playing

[003-046]with them and making crooning, rasping noises in my throat. The sun

[003-047]shone warmly and I was happy, and comfortable. I was in a little open

[003-048]space. Around me, on all sides, were bushes and fern-like growths, and

[003-049]overhead and all about were the trunks and branches of forest trees.

[003-050]Suddenly I heard a sound. I sat upright and listened. I made no

[003-051]movement. The little noises died down in my throat, and I sat as one

[003-052]petrified. The sound drew closer. It was like the grunt of a pig. Then

[003-053]I began to hear the sounds caused by the moving of a body through the

[003-054]brush. Next I saw the ferns agitated by the passage of the body. Then

[003-055]the ferns parted, and I saw gleaming eyes, a long snout, and white

[003-056]tusks.

[003-057]It was a wild boar. He peered at me curiously. He grunted once or twice

[003-058]and shifted his weight from one foreleg to the other, at the same time

[003-059]moving his head from side to side and swaying the ferns. Still I sat as

[003-060]one petrified, my eyes unblinking as I stared at him, fear eating at my

[003-061]heart.

[003-062]It seemed that this movelessness and silence on my part was what was

[003-063]expected of me. I was not to cry out in the face of fear. It was a

[003-064]dictate of instinct. And so I sat there and waited for I knew not what.

[003-065]The boar thrust the ferns aside and stepped into the open. The

[003-066]curiosity went out of his eyes, and they gleamed cruelly. He tossed his

[003-067]head at me threateningly and advanced a step. This he did again, and

[003-068]yet again.

[003-069]Then I screamed...or shrieked—I cannot describe it, but it was a shrill

[003-070]and terrible cry. And it seems that it, too, at this stage of the

[003-071]proceedings, was the thing expected of me. From not far away came an

[003-072]answering cry. My sounds seemed momentarily to disconcert the boar, and

[003-073]while he halted and shifted his weight with indecision, an apparition

[003-074]burst upon us.

[003-075]She was like a large orangutan, my mother, or like a chimpanzee, and

[003-076]yet, in sharp and definite ways, quite different. She was heavier of

[003-077]build than they, and had less hair. Her arms were not so long, and her

[003-078]legs were stouter. She wore no clothes—only her natural hair. And I can

[003-079]tell you she was a fury when she was excited.

[003-080]And like a fury she dashed upon the scene. She was gritting her teeth,

[003-081]making frightful grimaces, snarling, uttering sharp and continuous

[003-082]cries that sounded like “kh-ah! kh-ah!” So sudden and formidable was

[003-083]her appearance that the boar involuntarily bunched himself together on

[003-084]the defensive and bristled as she swerved toward him. Then she swerved

[003-085]toward me. She had quite taken the breath out of him. I knew just what

[003-086]to do in that moment of time she had gained. I leaped to meet her,

[003-087]catching her about the waist and holding on hand and foot—yes, by my

[003-088]feet; I could hold on by them as readily as by my hands. I could feel

[003-089]in my tense grip the pull of the hair as her skin and her muscles moved

[003-090]beneath with her efforts.

[003-091]As I say, I leaped to meet her, and on the instant she leaped straight

[003-092]up into the air, catching an overhanging branch with her hands. The

[003-093]next instant, with clashing tusks, the boar drove past underneath. He

[003-094]had recovered from his surprise and sprung forward, emitting a squeal

[003-095]that was almost a trumpeting. At any rate it was a call, for it was

[003-096]followed by the rushing of bodies through the ferns and brush from all

[003-097]directions.

[003-098]From every side wild hogs dashed into the open space—a score of them.

[003-099]But my mother swung over the top of a thick limb, a dozen feet from the

[003-100]ground, and, still holding on to her, we perched there in safety. She

[003-101]was very excited. She chattered and screamed, and scolded down at the

[003-102]bristling, tooth-gnashing circle that had gathered beneath. I, too,

[003-103]trembling, peered down at the angry beasts and did my best to imitate

[003-104]my mother’s cries.

[003-105]From the distance came similar cries, only pitched deeper, into a sort

[003-106]of roaring bass. These grew momentarily louder, and soon I saw him

[003-107]approaching, my father—at least, by all the evidence of the times, I am

[003-108]driven to conclude that he was my father.

[003-109]He was not an extremely prepossessing father, as fathers go. He seemed

[003-110]half man, and half ape, and yet not ape, and not yet man. I fail to

[003-111]describe him. There is nothing like him to-day on the earth, under the

[003-112]earth, nor in the earth. He was a large man in his day, and he must

[003-113]have weighed all of a hundred and thirty pounds. His face was broad and

[003-114]flat, and the eyebrows over-hung the eyes. The eyes themselves were

[003-115]small, deep-set, and close together. He had practically no nose at all.

[003-116]It was squat and broad, apparently without any bridge, while the

[003-117]nostrils were like two holes in the face, opening outward instead of

[003-118]down.

[003-119]The forehead slanted back from the eyes, and the hair began right at

[003-120]the eyes and ran up over the head. The head itself was preposterously

[003-121]small and was supported on an equally preposterous, thick, short neck.

[003-122]There was an elemental economy about his body—as was there about all

[003-123]our bodies. The chest was deep, it is true, cavernously deep; but there

[003-124]were no full-swelling muscles, no wide-spreading shoulders, no

[003-125]clean-limbed straightness, no generous symmetry of outline. It

[003-126]represented strength, that body of my father’s, strength without

[003-127]beauty; ferocious, primordial strength, made to clutch and gripe and

[003-128]rend and destroy.

[003-129]His hips were thin; and the legs, lean and hairy, were crooked and

[003-130]stringy-muscled. In fact, my father’s legs were more like arms. They

[003-131]were twisted and gnarly, and with scarcely the semblance of the full

[003-132]meaty calf such as graces your leg and mine. I remember he could not

[003-133]walk on the flat of his foot. This was because it was a prehensile

[003-134]foot, more like a hand than a foot. The great toe, instead of being in

[003-135]line with the other toes, opposed them, like a thumb, and its

[003-136]opposition to the other toes was what enabled him to get a grip with

[003-137]his foot. This was why he could not walk on the flat of his foot.

[003-138]But his appearance was no more unusual than the manner of his coming,

[003-139]there to my mother and me as we perched above the angry wild pigs. He

[003-140]came through the trees, leaping from limb to limb and from tree to

[003-141]tree; and he came swiftly. I can see him now, in my wake-a-day life, as

[003-142]I write this, swinging along through the trees, a four-handed, hairy

[003-143]creature, howling with rage, pausing now and again to beat his chest

[003-144]with his clenched fist, leaping ten-and-fifteen-foot gaps, catching a

[003-145]branch with one hand and swinging on across another gap to catch with

[003-146]his other hand and go on, never hesitating, never at a loss as to how

[003-147]to proceed on his arboreal way.

[003-148]And as I watched him I felt in my own being, in my very muscles

[003-149]themselves, the surge and thrill of desire to go leaping from bough to

[003-150]bough; and I felt also the guarantee of the latent power in that being

[003-151]and in those muscles of mine. And why not? Little boys watch their

[003-152]fathers swing axes and fell trees, and feel in themselves that some day

[003-153]they, too, will swing axes and fell trees. And so with me. The life

[003-154]that was in me was constituted to do what my father did, and it

[003-155]whispered to me secretly and ambitiously of aerial paths and forest

[003-156]flights.

[003-157]At last my father joined us. He was extremely angry. I remember the

[003-158]out-thrust of his protruding underlip as he glared down at the wild

[003-159]pigs. He snarled something like a dog, and I remember that his

[003-160]eye-teeth were large, like fangs, and that they impressed me

[003-161]tremendously.

[003-162]His conduct served only the more to infuriate the pigs. He broke off

[003-163]twigs and small branches and flung them down upon our enemies. He even

[003-164]hung by one hand, tantalizingly just beyond reach, and mocked them as

[003-165]they gnashed their tusks with impotent rage. Not content with this, he

[003-166]broke off a stout branch, and, holding on with one hand and foot,

[003-167]jabbed the infuriated beasts in the sides and whacked them across their

[003-168]noses. Needless to state, my mother and I enjoyed the sport.

[003-169]But one tires of all good things, and in the end, my father, chuckling

[003-170]maliciously the while, led the way across the trees. Now it was that my

[003-171]ambitions ebbed away, and I became timid, holding tightly to my mother

[003-172]as she climbed and swung through space. I remember when the branch

[003-173]broke with her weight. She had made a wide leap, and with the snap of

[003-174]the wood I was overwhelmed with the sickening consciousness of falling

[003-175]through space, the pair of us. The forest and the sunshine on the

[003-176]rustling leaves vanished from my eyes. I had a fading glimpse of my

[003-177]father abruptly arresting his progress to look, and then all was

[003-178]blackness.

[003-179]The next moment I was awake, in my sheeted bed, sweating, trembling,

[003-180]nauseated. The window was up, and a cool air was blowing through the

[003-181]room. The night-lamp was burning calmly. And because of this I take it

[003-182]that the wild pigs did not get us, that we never fetched bottom; else I

[003-183]should not be here now, a thousand centuries after, to remember the

[003-184]event.

[003-185]And now put yourself in my place for a moment. Walk with me a bit in my

[003-186]tender childhood, bed with me a night and imagine yourself dreaming

[003-187]such incomprehensible horrors. Remember I was an inexperienced child. I

[003-188]had never seen a wild boar in my life. For that matter I had never seen

[003-189]a domesticated pig. The nearest approach to one that I had seen was

[003-190]breakfast bacon sizzling in its fat. And yet here, real as life, wild

[003-191]boars dashed through my dreams, and I, with fantastic parents, swung

[003-192]through the lofty tree-spaces.

[003-193]Do you wonder that I was frightened and oppressed by my

[003-194]nightmare-ridden nights? I was accursed. And, worst of all, I was

[003-195]afraid to tell. I do not know why, except that I had a feeling of

[003-196]guilt, though I knew no better of what I was guilty. So it was, through

[003-197]long years, that I suffered in silence, until I came to man’s estate

[003-198]and learned the why and wherefore of my dreams.
