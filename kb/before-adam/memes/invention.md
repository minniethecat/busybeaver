**Invention** in _Before Adam_ is portrayed as the slow, painful, and largely unconscious process by which early hominids acquired the tools and techniques that would define humanity. London depicts invention not as flashes of individual genius but as collective, incremental discoveries driven by necessity and imitation — a fundamentally evolutionary process.

## In the Novel

The first major invention of the horde was the use of [[gourd|gourds]] as water-carrying vessels. The honor belonged to [[marrow-bone|Old Marrow-Bone]], whose "great age" and necessity "brought about the innovation" (008-057-008-059). The horde first stored only water, then a woman — "I do not know which one" — filled a gourd with berries, accidentally creating the first carrying-receptacle for food (008-082-008-084).

Invention was iterative and communal. The gourd led to folded-leaf containers — "some woman's gourd was too small, or else she had forgotten her gourd" — and she improvised by bending "two great leaves together, pinning the seams with thorns" (008-087-008-090). Each innovation built on the last, "once started, had to go on" (008-087).

The narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] accidentally invent the catamaran — the "most primitive catamaran" — by riding logs down the river. London notes wryly: "We had invented the most primitive catamaran, and we did not have sense enough to know it. It never entered our heads to lash the logs together" (010-119-010-122). This gap between discovery and understanding is a recurring theme.

Language itself was an invention in progress. The horde lacked "tools of speech not yet invented" (004-029), and names were "an invention we had not yet made" (005-040). "Thought-symbols were yet to be slowly and painfully invented" (014-090). London portrays language as the most fundamental human invention — the one that enables all others.

> The one big invention of the horde, during the time I lived with it, was the use of gourds. (008-081-008-082)
