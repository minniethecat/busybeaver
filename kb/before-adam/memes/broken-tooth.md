## Overview

Broken-Tooth is a minor but memorably tragic character in Jack London's *Before Adam*. He is a young member of [[the-horde|the Folk]], "another youngster who lived by himself" (007-001) with his mother on the outskirts of the horde's territory. His brief appearance in Chapter VII serves as the novel's most vivid illustration of the constant mortal danger of Pleistocene life — his playful childhood is abruptly ended by the arrows of the [[fire-people|Fire People]], a more technologically advanced hominid group. His death is one of the earliest traumatic memories that shape [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]]'s understanding of the world.

## Role in the Narrative

Broken-Tooth appears only in Chapter VII, where he functions as a narrative vehicle for introducing the existential threat posed by the Fire People to the Folk. His death by arrow is the first direct lethal encounter that Big-Tooth witnesses between the two groups, and it establishes the Fire People as a menacing presence for the remainder of the story. The narrator later recalls him alongside other lost members of the community: "as my father had died, as Broken-Tooth had died, as my sister" (015-128) — his death becomes part of the communal memory of loss that defines life for the Folk.

## Relationships

Broken-Tooth's primary relationship is with his **mother**, though the narrative mentions her only briefly. "Every time his mother left the cave he would cry" (007-005), suggesting a strong maternal bond typical of young hominid children. His relationship with **Big-Tooth** and **[[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]]** is that of a playmate — they engage in [[climbing|tree-climbing]] games together, with Broken-Tooth displaying "remarkable agility" (007-030) in a game of tag among the branches. The **Fire People**, particularly the "wizened little old hunter who had shot Broken-Tooth out of the tree" (013-011), represent the lethal other — a technologically superior tribe whose arrows spell death for the Folk.

## Traits and Characteristics

Broken-Tooth is characterized by his youth and playfulness. He is agile in the trees, capable of a tricky "teetering" game on branches that neither Big-Tooth nor Lop-Ear could replicate (007-039). His emotional state is vividly described: "like the eruption of a miniature volcano, Broken-Tooth would burst into tears" (007-011) when his mother left — a poignant reminder that even in the brutal world of the Pleistocene, children experienced attachment and separation anxiety much as modern humans do. His death, "uttering a terrible cry" (007-101) as the arrow struck, underscores the indiscriminate cruelty of survival in this era.

## Key Appearances

> "Broken-Tooth was another youngster who lived by himself" (007-001)

> "Broken-Tooth displayed remarkable agility in the game" (007-030)

> "Broken-Tooth, uttering a terrible cry, fell to the ground" (007-101)

Broken-Tooth appears exclusively in Chapter VII, in what amounts to a self-contained tragic episode. The chapter's closing lines: "Broken-Tooth and the tree of the tragedy are gone" (007-117), emphasize the ephemeral nature of individual existence in the prehistoric world — trees fall, children die, and only memory persists.

## Others' View of Broken-Tooth

Broken-Tooth was seen by his peers — Big-Tooth and Lop-Ear — as a companion in childhood, a fellow explorer of the trees whose remarkable agility made him a natural at their games. His "teetering" on branches, a trick neither Big-Tooth nor Lop-Ear could match, marked him in their eyes as gifted — a child whose physical talent made the world seem larger and more playful than it was.[^1] To the degree that the Folk's children formed emotional bonds before language could articulate them, Broken-Tooth was a friend.

His mother's view of him, though glimpsed only indirectly through his behavior, tells a story of its own. That Broken-Tooth "would burst into tears" every time his mother left the cave suggests a bond of unusual intensity — a child who felt his mother's absence as a wound.[^2] The narrative gives us no glimpse of the mother after the arrow struck, but the silence itself speaks: in a world where death came without warning, maternal grief was as common as it was unrecorded.

To Big-Tooth, the adult narrator looking back across the evolutionary divide, Broken-Tooth became a symbol — the first face of innocent death that he ever witnessed, and a memory that recurred alongside the deaths of his father and sister in the litany of loss.[^3] Broken-Tooth's name, carrying the physical mark of his mortality in its very syllables, suggests that even among the Folk — who had barely begun to name the world — the dead were remembered by what they had suffered.

The Fire-Man who killed him likely saw nothing more than a target — a small hominid shape in a tree, barely distinguishable from the branches. The casualness of the killing, followed by the stone to the head, underscores the brutal calculus of interspecies competition: Broken-Tooth was not an enemy combatant but a child in the wrong place at the wrong time.[^4] His death required no more justification than a predator's pounce — and this, more than anything, is what the Folk remembered.
