## Overview

The Plain was the stretch of open, level ground that lay between the edge of the forest and the base of the [[the-cliffs|cliffs]]. Unlike the [[the-open-space|Open Space]] immediately before the caves — a bustling social arena where the Folk gathered, played, and confronted danger together — the Plain was a zone of transit: a place to be crossed quickly, not a place to linger.

> Lop-Ear and I ventured across the open ground [008-145]

The narrator's language when describing movement across open terrain is consistently marked by caution and dread. The Plain represented exposure: with no trees to climb and no crevices to duck into, a hominid caught in the open was vulnerable to any predator watching from the forest's edge.

## Geographic Context

The Plain occupied the middle ground of the Folk's territory. On one side rose the forest — dense with timber, hiding [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] and other dangers. On the other side rose [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s, with their cool caves offering safety in the rock face. Between them stretched the Plain: a wide, gently sloping apron of grass and low scrub, broken here and there by patches of brush but offering no substantial cover.

The narrator recalls crossing open spaces "with dread, dashing from one patch of cover to the next" [004-198] — a description that captures the psychological geography of the Plain. It was not a place in itself so much as the dangerous gap between places that mattered: the feeding grounds of the forest and the sleeping safety of the [[caves]].

## Role in the Narrative

The Plain appears in the narrative primarily as a barrier — a space that must be crossed, and crossed quickly. When the Folk returned from foraging at twilight, they emerged from the forest and traversed the open ground to reach [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s [005-180]. When danger threatened from the forest, the Plain became a killing field: no cover, no escape, only the desperate sprint for [[the-cliffs|the cliff]] face [006-008].

The narrator and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] ventured across the open ground together [008-145], their partnership forged in part by the shared vulnerability of crossing such exposed terrain. Later, when the Folk were driven from their territory and wandered through unfamiliar country, they would encounter other plains — but none so freighted with daily danger as the original Plain between the forest and [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s.

## Inhabitants and Usage

No one lived on the Plain — it was too exposed. The Folk crossed it daily, moving between the forest (where they foraged and hunted) and [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]s (where they slept). Game animals sometimes ventured onto the Plain, but they too preferred the cover of the forest.

The Plain's emptiness was its defining characteristic. Where the [[the-open-space|Open Space]] before the caves was a theater of social life, the Plain was a blank stage — a stretch of ground whose only meaning lay in what waited on either side of it.
