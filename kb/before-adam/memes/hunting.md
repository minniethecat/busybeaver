> We were hunters. All we knew was the chase, and we chased what we ate. (006-015)

## Overview

Hunting is the central economic activity of the Folk. They do not cultivate, store, or trade — they hunt and they gather, and what they eat is what they can catch or find before someone else does. The narrator describes the Folk as "hunters" by nature: "All we knew was the chase, and we chased what we ate" (006-015). This is existence at its most direct — the gap between hunger and satisfaction is measured in speed, strength, and cunning.

## Hunting Methods

The Folk hunt with their hands, with stones, and — later — with clubs and [[bow-and-arrow|bows]]. Before the acquisition of fire and projectile weapons, hunting required close engagement with prey, which meant risk. A wounded animal could turn on its pursuer; a hunt that went too far into [[the-forest|the forest]] could attract [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]], who would happily take both prey and hunter.

The narrator describes cooperative hunting: the Folk working together to drive game toward waiting ambushers. But cooperation is rudimentary — "each one of us fought and acted for himself" (017-008-017-009) — and hunts as often dissolve into individual scrambles as they succeed through coordination.

## Competition with Predators

The Folk are not the top of the food chain. [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] is the apex predator, and the Folk fear him above all other beasts. [[wild-dogs|Wild dogs]] hunt in packs and can overwhelm a lone member of the horde. The Folk must hunt while simultaneously avoiding becoming prey — a calculation that shapes every expedition beyond [[the-cliffs|the cliffs]].

The narrator's description of being stalked while hunting captures this doubled consciousness: "I was hunting and being hunted. I was meat for the hunting animals even while I myself hunted meat" (003-028-003-030).

## Hunting and Social Structure

Hunting shapes the social organization of the [[the-horde|horde]]. Success in the hunt confers status; failure means hunger and diminished standing. [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] maintains his dominance partly through his hunting prowess — he can acquire meat that others cannot. The sharing of meat is one of the earliest forms of social obligation, and refusal to share is a declaration of enmity.

When the Folk acquire new hunting technology from the [[fire-people|Fire People]] — most importantly, the [[bow-and-arrow|bow and arrow]] — their relationship to hunting transforms. Projectile weapons reduce risk, increase success rates, and change the balance of power between the Folk and their competitors. Technology and subsistence evolve together.

## Hunting as Evolutionary Driver

London presents hunting as a primary engine of hominid evolution. The cognitive demands of tracking prey, predicting animal behavior, and coordinating group action selected for larger brains and more sophisticated social cognition. The transition from scavenger to hunter — from taking what predators left to actively pursuing and killing prey — marks a critical step toward humanity.
