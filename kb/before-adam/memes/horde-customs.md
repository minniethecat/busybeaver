## Overview

Horde Customs were the informal shared practices that constituted the Folk's cultural inheritance. They had no formal traditions, no oral history, and no explicit teaching — yet they maintained a consistent set of behaviors that spanned generations. These customs were transmitted entirely through imitation, the same instinct that led [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] to disastrously mimic the [[fire-people|Fire People]]'s fire-tending.

The narrator's description of these customs is one of the novel's most sustained anthropological observations. The Folk possessed, in embryonic form, behaviors that would develop into human cultural institutions — but they lacked the conceptual vocabulary to organize or transmit these behaviors deliberately.

## Body Disposal

The Folk disposed of their dead by flinging the bodies into the river below the last drinking-place (009-039). This was not a funeral ritual — there was no ceremony, no mourning period, no concept of an afterlife. It was a practical measure motivated by the smell and the attraction of scavengers. Yet it was also, in its consistency, a custom: the Folk always disposed of bodies in the same way, at the same place, suggesting that even purely practical behaviors can become culturally fixed.

## Shelter-Building

When away from the caves, the Folk constructed rough shelters in the branches of trees — platforms of interwoven branches that provided some protection from predators and the elements (003-001-003-020). These shelters were temporary, built for a single night and abandoned the next morning. But the technique for building them — which branches to use, how to interweave them — was consistent across the horde, transmitted from generation to generation through observation and imitation rather than instruction.

## Stone-Throwing Defense

The Folk's primary defensive tactic — raining stones down upon attackers from positions of height — was a custom that exploited the vertical advantage of the [[cliff-defensive-architecture|cliffs]]. Every member of the horde knew how to execute this defense, and it was effective enough to repel even [[saber-tooth|Saber-Tooth]] from the cliff face (006-041-006-043). The custom was not taught but learned through participation: young Folk saw their elders throw stones at threats and imitated the behavior.

## The Imitative Instinct

The mechanism by which all customs spread was imitation. The narrator describes the Folk as possessing a "strong imitative instinct" — the same instinct that, when [[marrow-bone|Marrow-Bone]] invented gourd water-storage, caused every member of the horde to adopt the practice (008-036). Imitation was the Folk's only pedagogy: they could not explain why they did what they did, but they could copy what they saw others doing. This instinct was both the Folk's greatest cultural asset and — as the [[the-forest-fire|forest fire]] demonstrated — a source of catastrophic risk when what was imitated was not understood.
