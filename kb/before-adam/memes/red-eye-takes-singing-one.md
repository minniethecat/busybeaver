## Overview

It was winter when [[Red-Eye]] killed his latest wife — "with abuse and repeated beatings" (014-020) — and immediately set his eyes on the [[singing-one]]. She was the granddaughter of old [[Marrow-Bone]], the daughter of the [[hairless-one]], a young female "greatly given to singing at the mouth of her cave in the twilight" (014-023). She had recently mated with [[Crooked-Leg]], a quiet, small, lean individual who was "no fighter anyway" (014-025-014-026).

What followed was the most brazen act of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s reign. In the quiet at the end of the day, as the Folk gathered in [[the-open-space|the open space before the caves]], the [[singing-one|Singing One]] dashed up a run-way from the drinking-place — pursued by [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]. She ran to her husband. [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]], though terribly scared, "did not run away. He stood up, and chattered, bristled, and showed his teeth" (014-030-014-031).

> He knew that death was upon him, yet he did not run away. (014-030)

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]] killed him with a single hand — clutching his neck, breaking it — and dragged the [[singing-one|Singing One]] by her hair up to his cave (014-034-014-037).
## Context and Causes

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]] was an atavism — a [[atavism|throwback]] to a fiercer evolutionary past — but also, [[big-tooth|the narrator]] notes, a foreshadowing of humanity's worst impulses: "the males of the human species only murder their mates" (014-022). He had already gone through multiple wives, each dying from abuse. The [[singing-one|Singing One]] was simply the next.

The Folk had long feared and hated [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]. He was "the mighty monster, the abysmal brute" (016-031), physically dominating — his body too large to enter the small-mouthed caves others used for safety. The narrator and [[Lop-Ear]] had survived his attacks only by enlarging their cave entrance to precisely the size that admitted them but not him (014-012-014-014). But survival was passive. No one had ever organized to stop him.

## Narrative Account

### The Killing

At dusk, as the Folk congregated, the [[singing-one|Singing One]] burst up a run-way with [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] behind her. She fled to [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]]. "Poor little [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] was terribly scared. But he was a hero" (014-029-014-030). He stood his ground — bristling, chattering, showing teeth.

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s response was instant. "His hand shot out and clutched [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] by the neck. The latter sank his teeth into [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s arm; but the next moment, with a broken neck, [[crooked-leg|Crooked-Leg]] was floundering and squirming on the ground" (014-034-014-035). The [[singing-one|Singing One]] screeched. [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] seized her hair and dragged her up [[the-cliffs|the cliff]], handling her roughly on the climb.

### The Hee-Hee Council

What followed was the Folk's moment of collective potential — and collective failure. "We were very angry, insanely, vociferously angry. Beating our chests, bristling, and gnashing our teeth, we gathered together in our rage" (014-039-014-040). They felt "the prod of gregarious instinct, the drawing together as though for united action, the impulse toward cooperation" (014-041).

But the impulse could not become action. "We did not turn to, all of us, and destroy [[red-eye|Red-Eye]], because we lacked a vocabulary. We were vaguely thinking thoughts for which there were no thought-symbols. These thought-symbols were yet to be slowly and painfully invented" (014-045-014-047).

The [[hairless-one]], father of the [[singing-one|Singing One]], tried to speak the cooperative impulse — his noises became gibberish. [[Big-Face]] tried. One after another, they all tried. "Some one seized a stick and began pounding a log. In a moment he had struck a rhythm. Unconsciously, our yells and exclamations yielded to this rhythm" (014-053-014-055).

And then the rage was gone. "Before we knew it, our rage forgotten, we were in the full swing of a [[council|hee-hee council]]" (014-056). The Folk who had gathered to destroy [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] were now dancing, chanting, and laughing — "soothed away by art" (014-065).

> These hee-hee councils splendidly illustrate the inconsecutiveness and inconsequentiality of the Folk. Here were we, drawn together by mutual rage and the impulse toward cooperation, led off into forgetfulness by the establishment of a rude rhythm. (014-058-014-059)
## Consequences

The [[singing-one|Singing One]] went the way of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s other wives — she did not survive long. But the event left a deeper mark. It was the clearest demonstration of the Folk's evolutionary bottleneck: they had the feelings that make cooperation possible, but not the language. Art — rhythm, chant, dance — could unite them momentarily, but only in forgetfulness, never in purposeful action.

The narrator, reflecting across a thousand centuries, saw the [[council|hee-hee council]] as "an adumbration of the councils of primitive man, and of the great national assemblies and international conventions of latter-day man" (014-062-014-063). The raw material of collective action was present. Only the word was missing.
