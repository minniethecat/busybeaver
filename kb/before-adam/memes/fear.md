> For my fear is the fear of long ago, the fear that was rampant in the Younger World, and in the youth of the Younger World. In short, the fear that reigned supreme in that period known as the Mid-Pleistocene. (001-010-001-013)

## Overview

Fear is not merely an emotion in *Before Adam* — it is the organizing principle of existence. [[big-tooth|The narrator]] opens his account by declaring that fear defined his childhood, his dreams, and his waking life. "My nights marked the reign of fear — and such fear!" (001-007-001-008). The fear he experiences is not personal but ancestral: inherited from [[big-tooth|Big-Tooth]] across the millennia, carried in the blood and the dreams.

## Fear in the Younger World

In the Pleistocene world of the Folk, fear was rational. Predators — [[saber-tooth|saber-toothed cats]], [[wild-dogs|wild dogs]], hunting beasts of every description — made the darkness a time of mortal danger. The Folk "hide tremblingly in their holes" as night falls (009-018-009-020). Fear of the dark, fear of the forest, fear of the unfamiliar — these were not neuroses but survival mechanisms.

The [[fire-people|Fire People]] introduced a new kind of fear: organized, technological terror. Fire, the weapon the Folk most feared, was wielded systematically against them. When the Fire People smoked out the caves, the Folk faced "the monster we feared — FIRE" (017-027-017-031).

## Fear as Evolutionary Pressure

Fear drives behavior and shapes evolution. The Folk's fear of predators keeps them in the caves at night; their fear of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] prevents collective action against him; their fear of [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]] — "mystery and fear, the terrible unknown" (017-184-017-186) — delays their escape from the pursuing Fire People. Each fear is a constraint, but also a pressure that selects for adaptation.

## Fear and the Narrator

The modern narrator inherits Big-Tooth's fear through his [[sleep|dreams]]. The "procession of nightmares" that tormented his childhood (001-004-001-006) is the literal inheritance of Pleistocene experience. London suggests that the fears we carry — of darkness, of predators, of strangers — are not learned but inherited, written into the nervous system by millennia of ancestral experience.
