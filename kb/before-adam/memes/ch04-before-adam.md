## CHAPTER IV

[004-001]There is one puzzling thing about these prehistoric memories of mine.

[004-002]It is the vagueness of the time element. I do not always know the order

[004-003]of events;—or can I tell, between some events, whether one, two, or

[004-004]four or five years have elapsed. I can only roughly tell the passage of

[004-005]time by judging the changes in the appearance and pursuits of my

[004-006]fellows.

[004-007]Also, I can apply the logic of events to the various happenings. For

[004-008]instance, there is no doubt whatever that my mother and I were treed by

[004-009]the wild pigs and fled and fell in the days before I made the

[004-010]acquaintance of Lop-Ear, who became what I may call my boyhood chum.

[004-011]And it is just as conclusive that between these two periods I must have

[004-012]left my mother.

[004-013]I have no memory of my father than the one I have given. Never, in the

[004-014]years that followed, did he reappear. And from my knowledge of the

[004-015]times, the only explanation possible lies in that he perished shortly

[004-016]after the adventure with the wild pigs. That it must have been an

[004-017]untimely end, there is no discussion. He was in full vigor, and only

[004-018]sudden and violent death could have taken him off. But I know not the

[004-019]manner of his going—whether he was drowned in the river, or was

[004-020]swallowed by a snake, or went into the stomach of old Saber-Tooth, the

[004-021]tiger, is beyond my knowledge.

[004-022]For know that I remember only the things I saw myself, with my own

[004-023]eyes, in those prehistoric days. If my mother knew my father’s end, she

[004-024]never told me. For that matter I doubt if she had a vocabulary adequate

[004-025]to convey such information. Perhaps, all told, the Folk in that day had

[004-026]a vocabulary of thirty or forty sounds.

[004-027]I call them _sounds_, rather than _words_, because sounds they were

[004-028]primarily. They had no fixed values, to be altered by adjectives and

[004-029]adverbs. These latter were tools of speech not yet invented. Instead of

[004-030]qualifying nouns or verbs by the use of adjectives and adverbs, we

[004-031]qualified sounds by intonation, by changes in quantity and pitch, by

[004-032]retarding and by accelerating. The length of time employed in the

[004-033]utterance of a particular sound shaded its meaning.

[004-034]We had no conjugation. One judged the tense by the context. We talked

[004-035]only concrete things because we thought only concrete things. Also, we

[004-036]depended largely on pantomime. The simplest abstraction was practically

[004-037]beyond our thinking; and when one did happen to think one, he was hard

[004-038]put to communicate it to his fellows. There were no sounds for it. He

[004-039]was pressing beyond the limits of his vocabulary. If he invented sounds

[004-040]for it, his fellows did not understand the sounds. Then it was that he

[004-041]fell back on pantomime, illustrating the thought wherever possible and

[004-042]at the same time repeating the new sound over and over again.

[004-043]Thus language grew. By the few sounds we possessed we were enabled to

[004-044]think a short distance beyond those sounds; then came the need for new

[004-045]sounds wherewith to express the new thought. Sometimes, however, we

[004-046]thought too long a distance in advance of our sounds, managed to

[004-047]achieve abstractions (dim ones I grant), which we failed utterly to

[004-048]make known to other folk. After all, language did not grow fast in that

[004-049]day.

[004-050]Oh, believe me, we were amazingly simple. But we did know a lot that is

[004-051]not known to-day. We could twitch our ears, prick them up and flatten

[004-052]them down at will. And we could scratch between our shoulders with

[004-053]ease. We could throw stones with our feet. I have done it many a time.

[004-054]And for that matter, I could keep my knees straight, bend forward from

[004-055]the hips, and touch, not the tips of my fingers, but the points of my

[004-056]elbows, to the ground. And as for bird-nesting—well, I only wish the

[004-057]twentieth-century boy could see us. But we made no collections of eggs.

[004-058]We ate them.

[004-059]I remember—but I out-run my story. First let me tell of Lop-Ear and our

[004-060]friendship. Very early in my life, I separated from my mother. Possibly

[004-061]this was because, after the death of my father, she took to herself a

[004-062]second husband. I have few recollections of him, and they are not of

[004-063]the best. He was a light fellow. There was no solidity to him. He was

[004-064]too voluble. His infernal chattering worries me even now as I think of

[004-065]it. His mind was too inconsequential to permit him to possess purpose.

[004-066]Monkeys in their cages always remind me of him. He was monkeyish. That

[004-067]is the best description I can give of him.

[004-068]He hated me from the first. And I quickly learned to be afraid of him

[004-069]and his malicious pranks. Whenever he came in sight I crept close to my

[004-070]mother and clung to her. But I was growing older all the time, and it

[004-071]was inevitable that I should from time to time stray from her, and

[004-072]stray farther and farther. And these were the opportunities that the

[004-073]Chatterer waited for. (I may as well explain that we bore no names in

[004-074]those days; were not known by any name. For the sake of convenience I

[004-075]have myself given names to the various Folk I was more closely in

[004-076]contact with, and the “Chatterer” is the most fitting description I can

[004-077]find for that precious stepfather of mine. As for me, I have named

[004-078]myself “Big-Tooth.” My eye-teeth were pronouncedly large.)

[004-079]But to return to the Chatterer. He persistently terrorized me. He was

[004-080]always pinching me and cuffing me, and on occasion he was not above

[004-081]biting me. Often my mother interfered, and the way she made his fur fly

[004-082]was a joy to see. But the result of all this was a beautiful and

[004-083]unending family quarrel, in which I was the bone of contention.

[004-084]No, my home-life was not happy. I smile to myself as I write the

[004-085]phrase. Home-life! Home! I had no home in the modern sense of the term.

[004-086]My home was an association, not a habitation. I lived in my mother’s

[004-087]care, not in a house. And my mother lived anywhere, so long as when

[004-088]night came she was above the ground.

[004-089]My mother was old-fashioned. She still clung to her trees. It is true,

[004-090]the more progressive members of our horde lived in the caves above the

[004-091]river. But my mother was suspicious and unprogressive. The trees were

[004-092]good enough for her. Of course, we had one particular tree in which we

[004-093]usually roosted, though we often roosted in other trees when nightfall

[004-094]caught us. In a convenient fork was a sort of rude platform of twigs

[004-095]and branches and creeping things. It was more like a huge bird-nest

[004-096]than anything else, though it was a thousand times cruder in the

[004-097]weaving than any bird-nest. But it had one feature that I have never

[004-098]seen attached to any bird-nest, namely, a roof.

[004-099]Oh, not a roof such as modern man makes! Nor a roof such as is made by

[004-100]the lowest aborigines of to-day. It was infinitely more clumsy than the

[004-101]clumsiest handiwork of man—of man as we know him. It was put together

[004-102]in a casual, helter-skelter sort of way. Above the fork of the tree

[004-103]whereon we rested was a pile of dead branches and brush. Four or five

[004-104]adjacent forks held what I may term the various ridge-poles. These were

[004-105]merely stout sticks an inch or so in diameter. On them rested the brush

[004-106]and branches. These seemed to have been tossed on almost aimlessly.

[004-107]There was no attempt at thatching. And I must confess that the roof

[004-108]leaked miserably in a heavy rain.

[004-109]But the Chatterer. He made home-life a burden for both my mother and

[004-110]me—and by home-life I mean, not the leaky nest in the tree, but the

[004-111]group-life of the three of us. He was most malicious in his persecution

[004-112]of me. That was the one purpose to which he held steadfastly for longer

[004-113]than five minutes. Also, as time went by, my mother was less eager in

[004-114]her defence of me. I think, what of the continuous rows raised by the

[004-115]Chatterer, that I must have become a nuisance to her. At any rate, the

[004-116]situation went from bad to worse so rapidly that I should soon, of my

[004-117]own volition, have left home. But the satisfaction of performing so

[004-118]independent an act was denied me. Before I was ready to go, I was

[004-119]thrown out. And I mean this literally.

[004-120]The opportunity came to the Chatterer one day when I was alone in the

[004-121]nest. My mother and the Chatterer had gone away together toward the

[004-122]blueberry swamp. He must have planned the whole thing, for I heard him

[004-123]returning alone through the forest, roaring with self-induced rage as

[004-124]he came. Like all the men of our horde, when they were angry or were

[004-125]trying to make themselves angry, he stopped now and again to hammer on

[004-126]his chest with his fist.

[004-127]I realized the helplessness of my situation, and crouched trembling in

[004-128]the nest. The Chatterer came directly to the tree—I remember it was an

[004-129]oak tree—and began to climb up. And he never ceased for a moment from

[004-130]his infernal row. As I have said, our language was extremely meagre,

[004-131]and he must have strained it by the variety of ways in which he

[004-132]informed me of his undying hatred of me and of his intention there and

[004-133]then to have it out with me.

[004-134]As he climbed to the fork, I fled out the great horizontal limb. He

[004-135]followed me, and out I went, farther and farther. At last I was out

[004-136]amongst the small twigs and leaves. The Chatterer was ever a coward,

[004-137]and greater always than any anger he ever worked up was his caution. He

[004-138]was afraid to follow me out amongst the leaves and twigs. For that

[004-139]matter, his greater weight would have crashed him through the foliage

[004-140]before he could have got to me.

[004-141]But it was not necessary for him to reach me, and well he knew it, the

[004-142]scoundrel! With a malevolent expression on his face, his beady eyes

[004-143]gleaming with cruel intelligence, he began teetering. Teetering!—and

[004-144]with me out on the very edge of the bough, clutching at the twigs that

[004-145]broke continually with my weight. Twenty feet beneath me was the earth.

[004-146]Wildly and more wildly he teetered, grinning at me his gloating hatred.

[004-147]Then came the end. All four holds broke at the same time, and I fell,

[004-148]back-downward, looking up at him, my hands and feet still clutching the

[004-149]broken twigs. Luckily, there were no wild pigs under me, and my fall

[004-150]was broken by the tough and springy bushes.

[004-151]Usually, my falls destroy my dreams, the nervous shock being sufficient

[004-152]to bridge the thousand centuries in an instant and hurl me wide awake

[004-153]into my little bed, where, perchance, I lie sweating and trembling and

[004-154]hear the cuckoo clock calling the hour in the hall. But this dream of

[004-155]my leaving home I have had many times, and never yet have I been

[004-156]awakened by it. Always do I crash, shrieking, down through the brush

[004-157]and fetch up with a bump on the ground.

[004-158]Scratched and bruised and whimpering, I lay where I had fallen. Peering

[004-159]up through the bushes, I could see the Chatterer. He had set up a

[004-160]demoniacal chant of joy and was keeping time to it with his teetering.

[004-161]I quickly hushed my whimpering. I was no longer in the safety of the

[004-162]trees, and I knew the danger I ran of bringing upon myself the hunting

[004-163]animals by too audible an expression of my grief.

[004-164]I remember, as my sobs died down, that I became interested in watching

[004-165]the strange light-effects produced by partially opening and closing my

[004-166]tear-wet eyelids. Then I began to investigate, and found that I was not

[004-167]so very badly damaged by my fall. I had lost some hair and hide, here

[004-168]and there; the sharp and jagged end of a broken branch had thrust fully

[004-169]an inch into my forearm; and my right hip, which had borne the brunt of

[004-170]my contact with the ground, was aching intolerably. But these, after

[004-171]all, were only petty hurts. No bones were broken, and in those days the

[004-172]flesh of man had finer healing qualities than it has to-day. Yet it was

[004-173]a severe fall, for I limped with my injured hip for fully a week

[004-174]afterward.

[004-175]Next, as I lay in the bushes, there came upon me a feeling of

[004-176]desolation, a consciousness that I was homeless. I made up my mind

[004-177]never to return to my mother and the Chatterer. I would go far away

[004-178]through the terrible forest, and find some tree for myself in which to

[004-179]roost. As for food, I knew where to find it. For the last year at least

[004-180]I had not been beholden to my mother for food. All she had furnished me

[004-181]was protection and guidance.

[004-182]I crawled softly out through the bushes. Once I looked back and saw the

[004-183]Chatterer still chanting and teetering. It was not a pleasant sight. I

[004-184]knew pretty well how to be cautious, and I was exceedingly careful on

[004-185]this my first journey in the world.

[004-186]I gave no thought as to where I was going. I had but one purpose, and

[004-187]that was to go away beyond the reach of the Chatterer. I climbed into

[004-188]the trees and wandered on amongst them for hours, passing from tree to

[004-189]tree and never touching the ground. But I did not go in any particular

[004-190]direction, nor did I travel steadily. It was my nature, as it was the

[004-191]nature of all my folk, to be inconsequential. Besides, I was a mere

[004-192]child, and I stopped a great deal to play by the way.

[004-193]The events that befell me on my leaving home are very vague in my mind.

[004-194]My dreams do not cover them. Much has my other-self forgotten, and

[004-195]particularly at this very period. Nor have I been able to frame up the

[004-196]various dreams so as to bridge the gap between my leaving the home-tree

[004-197]and my arrival at the caves.

[004-198]I remember that several times I came to open spaces. These I crossed in

[004-199]great trepidation, descending to the ground and running at the top of

[004-200]my speed. I remember that there were days of rain and days of sunshine,

[004-201]so that I must have wandered alone for quite a time. I especially dream

[004-202]of my misery in the rain, and of my sufferings from hunger and how I

[004-203]appeased it. One very strong impression is of hunting little lizards on

[004-204]the rocky top of an open knoll. They ran under the rocks, and most of

[004-205]them escaped; but occasionally I turned over a stone and caught one. I

[004-206]was frightened away from this knoll by snakes. They did not pursue me.

[004-207]They were merely basking on flat rocks in the sun. But such was my

[004-208]inherited fear of them that I fled as fast as if they had been after

[004-209]me.

[004-210]Then I gnawed bitter bark from young trees. I remember vaguely the

[004-211]eating of many green nuts, with soft shells and milky kernels. And I

[004-212]remember most distinctly suffering from a stomach-ache. It may have

[004-213]been caused by the green nuts, and maybe by the lizards. I do not know.

[004-214]But I do know that I was fortunate in not being devoured during the

[004-215]several hours I was knotted up on the ground with the colic.
