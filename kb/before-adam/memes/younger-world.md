## Overview

The "Younger World" is the narrator's name for the geological and experiential era in which his dream-self — Big-Tooth — lived. It is not the scientifically defined Pleistocene but a poetic, subjectively inhabited past. London uses the term to frame the entire narrative, invoking it as the first words that orient the reader: "in the youth of the Younger World, the scene of my dreams" (001-011). The phrase recurs across the novel as a temporal anchor, contrasting the raw vitality of the prehistoric past with the narrator's restrained modern existence.

## In the Novel

The Younger World is introduced in the opening chapter as the stage for the narrator's dreams: "rampant in the Younger World, and in the youth of the Younger World" (001-011). It reappears in the narrator's theoretical exposition in Chapter 2, where he explains that his racial memories come from "running through the forests of the Younger World" (002-108). The phrase recurs at key moments — Big-Tooth lying in his tree nest as an infant (003-020), the horde's conflicts (006-104), Lop-Ear's wounding (007-148), and the Folk's collective lack of momentum (008-102).

In Chapter 3, the narrator offers his most vivid evocation: he describes "the vanished Younger World as it was at the time I lived" (003-031), capturing both the world's reality and its irrevocable loss. By Chapter 14, the term has taken on an almost elegiac quality — "we Folk of the Younger World lacked speech" (014-111) — acknowledging the communicative poverty of this earlier age.

## Context and Significance

London's "Younger World" reflects the influence of late-nineteenth-century popular science, which treated the Pleistocene as a romantic epoch of giant mammals and primitive humans. The term carries both scientific pretension (borrowing the language of geology) and literary invention (coining a proper noun that sounds like it belongs in a creation myth). By naming it, London gives the prehistoric past a coherent identity, making it feel as real and specific to the reader as any named historical era.
