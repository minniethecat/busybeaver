## CHAPTER XVIII

[018-001]Of our wanderings in the great swamp I have no clear knowledge. When I

[018-002]strive to remember, I have a riot of unrelated impressions and a loss

[018-003]of time-value. I have no idea of how long we were in that vast

[018-004]everglade, but it must have been for weeks. My memories of what

[018-005]occurred invariably take the form of nightmare. For untold ages,

[018-006]oppressed by protean fear, I am aware of wandering, endlessly

[018-007]wandering, through a dank and soggy wilderness, where poisonous snakes

[018-008]struck at us, and animals roared around us, and the mud quaked under us

[018-009]and sucked at our heels.

[018-010]I know that we were turned from our course countless times by streams

[018-011]and lakes and slimy seas. Then there were storms and risings of the

[018-012]water over great areas of the low-lying lands; and there were periods

[018-013]of hunger and misery when we were kept prisoners in the trees for days

[018-014]and days by these transient floods.

[018-015]Very strong upon me is one picture. Large trees are about us, and from

[018-016]their branches hang gray filaments of moss, while great creepers, like

[018-017]monstrous serpents, curl around the trunks and writhe in tangles

[018-018]through the air. And all about is the mud, soft mud, that bubbles forth

[018-019]gases, and that heaves and sighs with internal agitations. And in the

[018-020]midst of all this are a dozen of us. We are lean and wretched, and our

[018-021]bones show through our tight-stretched skins. We do not sing and

[018-022]chatter and laugh. We play no pranks. For once our volatile and

[018-023]exuberant spirits are hopelessly subdued. We make plaintive, querulous

[018-024]noises, look at one another, and cluster close together. It is like the

[018-025]meeting of the handful of survivors after the day of the end of the

[018-026]world.

[018-027]This event is without connection with the other events in the swamp.

[018-028]How we ever managed to cross it, I do not know, but at last we came out

[018-029]where a low range of hills ran down to the bank of the river. It was

[018-030]our river emerging like ourselves from the great swamp. On the south

[018-031]bank, where the river had broken its way through the hills, we found

[018-032]many sand-stone caves. Beyond, toward the west, the ocean boomed on the

[018-033]bar that lay across the river’s mouth. And here, in the caves, we

[018-034]settled down in our abiding-place by the sea.

[018-035]There were not many of us. From time to time, as the days went by, more

[018-036]of the Folk appeared. They dragged themselves from the swamp singly,

[018-037]and in twos and threes, more dead than alive, mere perambulating

[018-038]skeletons, until at last there were thirty of us. Then no more came

[018-039]from the swamp, and Red-Eye was not among us. It was noticeable that no

[018-040]children had survived the frightful journey.

[018-041]I shall not tell in detail of the years we lived by the sea. It was not

[018-042]a happy abiding-place. The air was raw and chill, and we suffered

[018-043]continually from coughing and colds. We could not survive in such an

[018-044]environment. True, we had children; but they had little hold on life

[018-045]and died early, while we died faster than new ones were born. Our

[018-046]number steadily diminished.

[018-047]Then the radical change in our diet was not good for us. We got few

[018-048]vegetables and fruits, and became fish-eaters. There were mussels and

[018-049]abalones and clams and rock-oysters, and great ocean-crabs that were

[018-050]thrown upon the beaches in stormy weather. Also, we found several kinds

[018-051]of seaweed that were good to eat. But the change in diet caused us

[018-052]stomach troubles, and none of us ever waxed fat. We were all lean and

[018-053]dyspeptic-looking. It was in getting the big abalones that Lop-Ear was

[018-054]lost. One of them closed upon his fingers at low-tide, and then the

[018-055]flood-tide came in and drowned him. We found his body the next day, and

[018-056]it was a lesson to us. Not another one of us was ever caught in the

[018-057]closing shell of an abalone.

[018-058]The Swift One and I managed to bring up one child, a boy—at least we

[018-059]managed to bring him along for several years. But I am quite confident

[018-060]he could never have survived that terrible climate. And then, one day,

[018-061]the Fire People appeared again. They had come down the river, not on a

[018-062]catamaran, but in a rude dug-out. There were three of them that paddled

[018-063]in it, and one of them was the little wizened old hunter. They landed

[018-064]on our beach, and he limped across the sand and examined our caves.

[018-065]They went away in a few minutes, but the Swift One was badly scared. We

[018-066]were all frightened, but none of us to the extent that she was. She

[018-067]whimpered and cried and was restless all that night. In the morning she

[018-068]took the child in her arms, and by sharp cries, gestures, and example,

[018-069]started me on our second long flight. There were eight of the Folk (all

[018-070]that was left of the horde) that remained behind in the caves. There

[018-071]was no hope for them. Without doubt, even if the Fire People did not

[018-072]return, they must soon have perished. It was a bad climate down there

[018-073]by the sea. The Folk were not constituted for the coast-dwelling life.

[018-074]We travelled south, for days skirting the great swamp but never

[018-075]venturing into it. Once we broke back to the westward, crossing a range

[018-076]of mountains and coming down to the coast. But it was no place for us.

[018-077]There were no trees—only bleak headlands, a thundering surf, and strong

[018-078]winds that seemed never to cease from blowing. We turned back across

[018-079]the mountains, travelling east and south, until we came in touch with

[018-080]the great swamp again.

[018-081]Soon we gained the southern extremity of the swamp, and we continued

[018-082]our course south and east. It was a pleasant land. The air was warm,

[018-083]and we were again in the forest. Later on we crossed a low-lying range

[018-084]of hills and found ourselves in an even better forest country. The

[018-085]farther we penetrated from the coast the warmer we found it, and we

[018-086]went on and on until we came to a large river that seemed familiar to

[018-087]the Swift One. It was where she must have come during the four years’

[018-088]absence from the horde. This river we crossed on logs, landing on the

[018-089]other side at the base of a large bluff. High up on the bluff we found

[018-090]our new home most difficult of access and quite hidden from any eye

[018-091]beneath.

[018-092]There is little more of my tale to tell. Here the Swift One and I lived

[018-093]and reared our family. And here my memories end. We never made another

[018-094]migration. I never dream beyond our high, inaccessible cave. And here

[018-095]must have been born the child that inherited the stuff of my dreams,

[018-096]that had moulded into its being all the impressions of my life—or of

[018-097]the life of Big-Tooth, rather, who is my other-self, and not my real

[018-098]self, but who is so real to me that often I am unable to tell what age

[018-099]I am living in.

[018-100]I often wonder about this line of descent. I, the modern, am

[018-101]incontestably a man; yet I, Big-Tooth, the primitive, am not a man.

[018-102]Somewhere, and by straight line of descent, these two parties to my

[018-103]dual personality were connected. Were the Folk, before their

[018-104]destruction, in the process of becoming men? And did I and mine carry

[018-105]through this process? On the other hand, may not some descendant of

[018-106]mine have gone in to the Fire People and become one of them? I do not

[018-107]know. There is no way of learning. One thing only is certain, and that

[018-108]is that Big-Tooth did stamp into the cerebral constitution of one of

[018-109]his progeny all the impressions of his life, and stamped them in so

[018-110]indelibly that the hosts of intervening generations have failed to

[018-111]obliterate them.

[018-112]There is one other thing of which I must speak before I close. It is a

[018-113]dream that I dream often, and in point of time the real event must have

[018-114]occurred during the period of my living in the high, inaccessible cave.

[018-115]I remember that I wandered far in the forest toward the east. There I

[018-116]came upon a tribe of Tree People. I crouched in a thicket and watched

[018-117]them at play. They were holding a laughing council, jumping up and down

[018-118]and screeching rude choruses.

[018-119]Suddenly they hushed their noise and ceased their capering. They shrank

[018-120]down in fear, and quested anxiously about with their eyes for a way of

[018-121]retreat. Then Red-Eye walked in among them. They cowered away from him.

[018-122]All were frightened. But he made no attempt to hurt them. He was one of

[018-123]them. At his heels, on stringy bended legs, supporting herself with

[018-124]knuckles to the ground on either side, walked an old female of the Tree

[018-125]People, his latest wife. He sat down in the midst of the circle. I can

[018-126]see him now, as I write this, scowling, his eyes inflamed, as he peers

[018-127]about him at the circle of the Tree People. And as he peers he crooks

[018-128]one monstrous leg and with his gnarly toes scratches himself on the

[018-129]stomach. He is Red-Eye, the atavism.
