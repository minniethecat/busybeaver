The **Sea** is the vast ocean that the [[the-folk|Folk]] encountered at the end of their [[the-great-migration|great migration]]. It represents both the physical terminus of their journey and a new, challenging environment that proved ill-suited to their forest-dwelling nature.

## Geographic Context

The sea lay beyond the mountains and swamps that the Folk traversed during their migration. The [[the-river|river]] that ran past the [[caves]] eventually flowed into the sea, forming a surf where fresh water met salt water (011-143). The transitional area included a bar that stretched across the river's mouth, behind which the Folk eventually settled (018-033-018-034).

> (011-143) …by the roaring surf where the river entered the sea.

The coastal region was characterized by raw, chill air and frequent storms. Unlike the forested inland where the Folk had evolved, the seaside offered a fundamentally different ecosystem with shellfish, seaweed, and marine resources (018-050-018-051).

## The Folk by the Sea

After the great migration, the surviving Folk established their abiding-place in caves near the sea (018-034). The settlement period lasted several years, though the exact duration remains unclear (018-041). Life by the sea was difficult for the Folk, who had evolved for forest living rather than coastal dwelling.

> (018-041) I shall not tell in detail of the years we lived by the sea. It was not a happy abiding-place. The air was raw and chill, and we suffered…

The dietary shift to marine food sources caused significant health problems. The Folk discovered several kinds of seaweed that were edible, along with shellfish thrown onto the beaches during storms. However, the change in diet led to persistent stomach troubles, and none of the Folk grew fat — they remained lean and unhealthy throughout the coastal period (018-050-018-052).

## Climate and Adaptation

The narrator notes that the coastal climate was fundamentally unsuited to the Folk's constitution (018-073). The damp, cold conditions contrasted sharply with the temperate forest and cave environments they had previously inhabited. This environmental mismatch became one of the motivations for the Folk's eventual departure from the coast.

> (018-073) …by the sea. The Folk were not constituted for the coast-dwelling life.

The wind from the sea influenced the local weather patterns, and even inland at the [[caves]], the narrator sensed the sea's distant presence through changes in the atmosphere (009-024). Salmon that came up from the sea to spawn in the river provided a seasonal food source (012-106).

## Symbolic Significance

The sea served as an evolutionary boundary marker in the narrator's consciousness. In moments of ancestral memory, he glimpsed even more remote origins — the "shore-slime of the primeval sea" from which all life ultimately emerged (011-037-011-038). This places the sea at two critical junctures: the ancient evolutionary past and the Folk's immediate present.
