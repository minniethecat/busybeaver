## CHAPTER V

[005-001]My vision of the scene came abruptly, as I emerged from the forest. I

[005-002]found myself on the edge of a large clear space. On one side of this

[005-003]space rose up high bluffs. On the other side was the river. The earth

[005-004]bank ran steeply down to the water, but here and there, in several

[005-005]places, where at some time slides of earth had occurred, there were

[005-006]run-ways. These were the drinking-places of the Folk that lived in the

[005-007]caves.

[005-008]And this was the main abiding-place of the Folk that I had chanced

[005-009]upon. This was, I may say, by stretching the word, the village. My

[005-010]mother and the Chatterer and I, and a few other simple bodies, were

[005-011]what might be termed suburban residents. We were part of the horde,

[005-012]though we lived a distance away from it. It was only a short distance,

[005-013]though it had taken me, what of my wandering, all of a week to arrive.

[005-014]Had I come directly, I could have covered the trip in an hour.

[005-015]But to return. From the edge of the forest I saw the caves in the

[005-016]bluff, the open space, and the run-ways to the drinking-places. And in

[005-017]the open space I saw many of the Folk. I had been straying, alone and a

[005-018]child, for a week. During that time I had seen not one of my kind. I

[005-019]had lived in terror and desolation. And now, at the sight of my kind, I

[005-020]was overcome with gladness, and I ran wildly toward them.

[005-021]Then it was that a strange thing happened. Some one of the Folk saw me

[005-022]and uttered a warning cry. On the instant, crying out with fear and

[005-023]panic, the Folk fled away. Leaping and scrambling over the rocks, they

[005-024]plunged into the mouths of the caves and disappeared...all but one, a

[005-025]little baby, that had been dropped in the excitement close to the base

[005-026]of the bluff. He was wailing dolefully. His mother dashed out; he

[005-027]sprang to meet her and held on tightly as she scrambled back into the

[005-028]cave.

[005-029]I was all alone. The populous open space had of a sudden become

[005-030]deserted. I sat down forlornly and whimpered. I could not understand.

[005-031]Why had the Folk run away from me? In later time, when I came to know

[005-032]their ways, I was to learn. When they saw me dashing out of the forest

[005-033]at top speed they concluded that I was being pursued by some hunting

[005-034]animal. By my unceremonious approach I had stampeded them.

[005-035]As I sat and watched the cave-mouths I became aware that the Folk were

[005-036]watching me. Soon they were thrusting their heads out. A little later

[005-037]they were calling back and forth to one another. In the hurry and

[005-038]confusion it had happened that all had not gained their own caves. Some

[005-039]of the young ones had sought refuge in other caves. The mothers did not

[005-040]call for them by name, because that was an invention we had not yet

[005-041]made. All were nameless. The mothers uttered querulous, anxious cries,

[005-042]which were recognized by the young ones. Thus, had my mother been there

[005-043]calling to me, I should have recognized her voice amongst the voices of

[005-044]a thousand mothers, and in the same way would she have recognized mine

[005-045]amongst a thousand.

[005-046]This calling back and forth continued for some time, but they were too

[005-047]cautious to come out of their caves and descend to the ground. Finally

[005-048]one did come. He was destined to play a large part in my life, and for

[005-049]that matter he already played a large part in the lives of all the

[005-050]members of the horde. He it was whom I shall call Red-Eye in the pages

[005-051]of this history—so called because of his inflamed eyes, the lids being

[005-052]always red, and, by the peculiar effect they produced, seeming to

[005-053]advertise the terrible savagery of him. The color of his soul was red.

[005-054]He was a monster in all ways. Physically he was a giant. He must have

[005-055]weighed one hundred and seventy pounds. He was the largest one of our

[005-056]kind I ever saw. Nor did I ever see one of the Fire People so large as

[005-057]he, nor one of the Tree People. Sometimes, when in the newspapers I

[005-058]happen upon descriptions of our modern bruisers and prizefighters, I

[005-059]wonder what chance the best of them would have had against him.

[005-060]I am afraid not much of a chance. With one grip of his iron fingers and

[005-061]a pull, he could have plucked a muscle, say a biceps, by the roots,

[005-062]clear out of their bodies. A back-handed, loose blow of his fist could

[005-063]have smashed their skulls like egg-shells. With a sweep of his wicked

[005-064]feet (or hind-hands) he could have disembowelled them. A twist could

[005-065]have broken their necks, and I know that with a single crunch of his

[005-066]jaws he could have pierced, at the same moment, the great vein of the

[005-067]throat in front and the spinal marrow at the back.

[005-068]He could spring twenty feet horizontally from a sitting position. He

[005-069]was abominably hairy. It was a matter of pride with us to be not very

[005-070]hairy. But he was covered with hair all over, on the inside of the arms

[005-071]as well as the outside, and even the ears themselves. The only places

[005-072]on him where the hair did not grow were the soles of his hands and feet

[005-073]and beneath his eyes. He was frightfully ugly, his ferocious grinning

[005-074]mouth and huge down-hanging under-lip being but in harmony with his

[005-075]terrible eyes.

[005-076]This was Red-Eye. And right gingerly he crept out of his cave and

[005-077]descended to the ground. Ignoring me, he proceeded to reconnoitre. He

[005-078]bent forward from the hips as he walked; and so far forward did he

[005-079]bend, and so long were his arms, that with every step he touched the

[005-080]knuckles of his hands to the ground on either side of him. He was

[005-081]awkward in the semi-erect position of walking that he assumed, and he

[005-082]really touched his knuckles to the ground in order to balance himself.

[005-083]But oh, I tell you he could run on all-fours! Now this was something at

[005-084]which we were particularly awkward. Furthermore, it was a rare

[005-085]individual among us who balanced himself with his knuckles when

[005-086]walking. Such an individual was an atavism, and Red-Eye was an even

[005-087]greater atavism.

[005-088]That is what he was—an atavism. We were in the process of changing our

[005-089]tree-life to life on the ground. For many generations we had been going

[005-090]through this change, and our bodies and carriage had likewise changed.

[005-091]But Red-Eye had reverted to the more primitive tree-dwelling type.

[005-092]Perforce, because he was born in our horde he stayed with us; but in

[005-093]actuality he was an atavism and his place was elsewhere.

[005-094]Very circumspect and very alert, he moved here and there about the open

[005-095]space, peering through the vistas among the trees and trying to catch a

[005-096]glimpse of the hunting animal that all suspected had pursued me. And

[005-097]while he did this, taking no notice of me, the Folk crowded at the

[005-098]cave-mouths and watched.

[005-099]At last he evidently decided that there was no danger lurking about. He

[005-100]was returning from the head of the run-way, from where he had taken a

[005-101]peep down at the drinking-place. His course brought him near, but still

[005-102]he did not notice me. He proceeded casually on his way until abreast of

[005-103]me, and then, without warning and with incredible swiftness, he smote

[005-104]me a buffet on the head. I was knocked backward fully a dozen feet

[005-105]before I fetched up against the ground, and I remember, half-stunned,

[005-106]even as the blow was struck, hearing the wild uproar of clucking and

[005-107]shrieking laughter that arose from the caves. It was a great joke—at

[005-108]least in that day; and right heartily the Folk appreciated it.

[005-109]Thus was I received into the horde. Red-Eye paid no further attention

[005-110]to me, and I was at liberty to whimper and sob to my heart’s content.

[005-111]Several of the women gathered curiously about me, and I recognized

[005-112]them. I had encountered them the preceding year when my mother had

[005-113]taken me to the hazelnut canyons.

[005-114]But they quickly left me alone, being replaced by a dozen curious and

[005-115]teasing youngsters. They formed a circle around me, pointing their

[005-116]fingers, making faces, and poking and pinching me. I was frightened,

[005-117]and for a time I endured them, then anger got the best of me and I

[005-118]sprang tooth and nail upon the most audacious one of them—none other

[005-119]than Lop-Ear himself. I have so named him because he could prick up

[005-120]only one of his ears. The other ear always hung limp and without

[005-121]movement. Some accident had injured the muscles and deprived him of the

[005-122]use of it.

[005-123]He closed with me, and we went at it for all the world like a couple of

[005-124]small boys fighting. We scratched and bit, pulled hair, clinched, and

[005-125]threw each other down. I remember I succeeded in getting on him what in

[005-126]my college days I learned was called a half-Nelson. This hold gave me

[005-127]the decided advantage. But I did not enjoy it long. He twisted up one

[005-128]leg, and with the foot (or hind-hand) made so savage an onslaught upon

[005-129]my abdomen as to threaten to disembowel me. I had to release him in

[005-130]order to save myself, and then we went at it again.

[005-131]Lop-Ear was a year older than I, but I was several times angrier than

[005-132]he, and in the end he took to his heels. I chased him across the open

[005-133]and down a run-way to the river. But he was better acquainted with the

[005-134]locality and ran along the edge of the water and up another run-way. He

[005-135]cut diagonally across the open space and dashed into a wide-mouthed

[005-136]cave.

[005-137]Before I knew it, I had plunged after him into the darkness. The next

[005-138]moment I was badly frightened. I had never been in a cave before. I

[005-139]began to whimper and cry out. Lop-Ear chattered mockingly at me, and,

[005-140]springing upon me unseen, tumbled me over. He did not risk a second

[005-141]encounter, however, and took himself off. I was between him and the

[005-142]entrance, and he did not pass me; yet he seemed to have gone away. I

[005-143]listened, but could get no clew as to where he was. This puzzled me,

[005-144]and when I regained the outside I sat down to watch.

[005-145]He never came out of the entrance, of that I was certain; yet at the

[005-146]end of several minutes he chuckled at my elbow. Again I ran after him,

[005-147]and again he ran into the cave; but this time I stopped at the mouth. I

[005-148]dropped back a short distance and watched. He did not come out, yet, as

[005-149]before, he chuckled at my elbow and was chased by me a third time into

[005-150]the cave.

[005-151]This performance was repeated several times. Then I followed him into

[005-152]the cave, where I searched vainly for him. I was curious. I could not

[005-153]understand how he eluded me. Always he went into the cave, never did he

[005-154]come out of it, yet always did he arrive there at my elbow and mock me.

[005-155]Thus did our fight transform itself into a game of hide and seek.

[005-156]All afternoon, with occasional intervals, we kept it up, and a playful,

[005-157]friendly spirit arose between us. In the end, he did not run away from

[005-158]me, and we sat together with our arms around each other. A little later

[005-159]he disclosed the mystery of the wide-mouthed cave. Holding me by the

[005-160]hand he led me inside. It connected by a narrow crevice with another

[005-161]cave, and it was through this that we regained the open air.

[005-162]We were now good friends. When the other young ones gathered around to

[005-163]tease, he joined with me in attacking them; and so viciously did we

[005-164]behave that before long I was let alone. Lop-Ear made me acquainted

[005-165]with the village. There was little that he could tell me of conditions

[005-166]and customs—he had not the necessary vocabulary; but by observing his

[005-167]actions I learned much, and also he showed me places and things.

[005-168]He took me up the open space, between the caves and the river, and into

[005-169]the forest beyond, where, in a grassy place among the trees, we made a

[005-170]meal of stringy-rooted carrots. After that we had a good drink at the

[005-171]river and started up the run-way to the caves.

[005-172]It was in the run-way that we came upon Red-Eye again. The first I

[005-173]knew, Lop-Ear had shrunk away to one side and was crouching low against

[005-174]the bank. Naturally and involuntarily, I imitated him. Then it was that

[005-175]I looked to see the cause of his fear. It was Red-Eye, swaggering down

[005-176]the centre of the run-way and scowling fiercely with his inflamed eyes.

[005-177]I noticed that all the youngsters shrank away from him as we had done,

[005-178]while the grown-ups regarded him with wary eyes when he drew near, and

[005-179]stepped aside to give him the centre of the path.

[005-180]As twilight came on, the open space was deserted. The Folk were seeking

[005-181]the safety of the caves. Lop-Ear led the way to bed. High up the bluff

[005-182]we climbed, higher than all the other caves, to a tiny crevice that

[005-183]could not be seen from the ground. Into this Lop-Ear squeezed. I

[005-184]followed with difficulty, so narrow was the entrance, and found myself

[005-185]in a small rock-chamber. It was very low—not more than a couple of feet

[005-186]in height, and possibly three feet by four in width and length. Here,

[005-187]cuddled together in each other’s arms, we slept out the night.
