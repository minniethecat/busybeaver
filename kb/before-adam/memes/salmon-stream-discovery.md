## Overview

The Salmon Stream Discovery was a providential moment during the Folk's arduous passage through the mountains. After days of hunger — "the hunger we endured on the mountains between Long Lake and Far Lake" (012-009) — [[big-tooth|the narrator]] and [[lop-ear|Lop-Ear]] came upon a stream flowing out of [[far-lake|Far Lake]]. The stream was packed with salmon that had come up from [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] to spawn.

The discovery was significant on two levels. Practically, it provided food at a moment of desperate need — the salmon were so numerous and so easy to catch that the two travelers could eat their fill. Symbolically, it was the first sign that the journey was nearing its end: the salmon had come from the sea, and where the salmon went, the sea could not be far.

## The Hunger

The mountain passage between [[long-lake|Long Lake]] and [[far-lake|Far Lake]] was the hardest leg of the migration. The mountains were "a bleak and inhospitable region" where edible vegetation was scarce and game was sparse (012-009). The narrator describes the hunger of this period in terms that suggest starvation was a real and present danger — the kind of deprivation that, in a less fortunate individual, would have ended the journey before it reached the sea.

The only relief during this passage came from chance kills of sleeping calves in lakeshore thickets [012-010, 012-101] — rare, lucky finds that could not be relied upon. The salmon stream changed everything.

## The Signal

The salmon's presence was more than food — it was a signal. These were fish that had migrated up from [[the-sea-encounter|the sea]] to spawn in the freshwater streams, and their presence in such numbers meant that the sea was reachable. The narrator, though he could not have articulated the logic, understood the connection: follow the stream, find the sea.

The discovery marked the transition from the mountain passage to the coastal phase of the migration. From the salmon stream onward, the journey was no longer an aimless wandering but a directed movement toward the roar of the surf that could now be heard in the distance.
