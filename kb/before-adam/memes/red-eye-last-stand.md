## Overview

Red-Eye's Last Stand was the most dramatic individual combat of the [[the-fire-people-massacre|Fire People Massacre]]. Cornered by a score of hunters in a tree by the [[blueberry-swamp|blueberry swamp]], [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] — who had terrorized the Folk for years with his brute strength and violent temper — turned that same ferocity against their common enemies in a display of desperate heroism (017-120-017-168).

Unlike the massacre as a whole, which was a one-sided slaughter, Red-Eye's stand was a fight in which the Fire People tasted genuine fear. For a few violent minutes, one man of the Folk — the most feared among them — proved that even against superior technology, raw ferocity could still exact a price.

## The Ambush

After escaping over [[the-cliffs|the cliff]]-top with his wife and baby, [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] fled into the forest. But the [[fire-people|Fire People]] sent out hunting parties that ranged all day, "killing us wherever they found us" (017-066). [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] was eventually cornered with his family in a large tree near the [[blueberry-swamp|blueberry swamp]]. Fully a score of hunters — twenty [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] armed with [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and clubs — surrounded the tree and began shooting [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s upward (017-120-017-122).

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s wife was the first to fall. An [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]] struck her and she tumbled from the branches to the ground. Mortally wounded, she crouched over her baby and made pleading signs to the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] — signs that any human being should have understood. They laughed at her. They prodded her with the ends of their [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s, then clubbed her to death (017-125-017-139). [[big-tooth|The narrator]] and the [[swift-one|Swift One]] watched this from a distance, "helpless in our rage and grief" (017-140).

## The Hollow Tree

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]] had crawled into a hollow in the trunk of the tree — a natural cavity that gave him cover from the [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s. The [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] sent a climber up after him. Presently the man's body crashed to the ground, limp — [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] had killed him inside the hollow (017-142-017-146).

Unwilling to lose more men in close quarters, the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] changed tactics. Following the same strategy that had destroyed the caves — fire — they built a fire at the base of the tree and began feeding it green branches to make thick, choking smoke (017-147-017-150). They intended to smoke [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] out as they had smoked out [[marrow-bone|Old Marrow-Bone]].

## The Leap

But [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] did not wait to be suffocated. The [[fire-man|Fire-Men]], gathered close around the base of the tree, suddenly swerved back — too late.

> We saw them suddenly swerve back from the tree. They were not quick enough. Red-Eye's flying body landed in the midst of them. (017-154-017-155)

[[red-eye|Red-Eye]] had leaped from the tree directly into the cluster of [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] — a tactic of pure aggression that caught them completely off guard. What followed was perhaps the most violent single combat in the entire narrative.

## The Fight

In the seconds that followed his landing, [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] became a whirlwind of destruction. He tore the face off one [[fire-man|Fire-Man]] with his bare fingers — a wound so horrific that [[big-tooth|the narrator]], hardened by a life of violence, describes it with uncharacteristic vividness. He seized a second man and bit him through the neck, killing him on the spot (017-157-017-160).

Then [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] caught up a fallen club and began swinging. Skulls cracked "like eggshells" under his blows. The [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] — twenty of them, armed with [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and clubs — fell back before one enraged man. For perhaps the first time in their conquest, the [[fire-people|Fire People]] experienced the terror they had come to inflict (017-161-017-165).

## The Escape

When the [[fire-man|Fire-Men]] regrouped and began to draw their [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s, [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] turned and ran. A few [[bow-and-arrow|arrow]]s sped after him, but he plunged into a dense thicket and was gone (017-167-017-168). He was never seen again — neither by the Folk nor, as far as [[big-tooth|the narrator]] ever learned, by the [[fire-people|Fire People]].

Whether [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] survived the aftermath of the massacre is unknown. [[big-tooth|The narrator]] records no further sighting of him, and he does not appear among the survivors who gathered at [[the-great-swamp|the great swamp]]. He vanished into the forest as he had lived — alone, violent, and terrifying to the last.

## Significance

Red-Eye's Last Stand is remarkable in the narrative for several reasons. First, it is the only moment in the entire book where [[red-eye|Red-Eye]] fights on behalf of anyone other than himself — however inadvertently, his violence is directed outward against the common enemy rather than against his own people.

Second, the [[fire-people|Fire People]]'s treatment of [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s wife — their cruel mockery of a dying mother pleading for her baby — strips away any pretense that the massacre was a straightforward territorial dispute. It was, in [[big-tooth|the narrator]]'s words, "indiscriminate slaughter" (017-069), and the scene at the hollow tree shows its ugliest face.

Third, the stand demonstrates that technology — [[bow-and-arrow|bow]]s and fire — is not an absolute advantage. In close-quarters combat, [[red-eye|Red-Eye]]'s raw physical power and ferocity proved more than a match for a score of technologically superior opponents. The moment prefigures the broader arc of the novel: the Folk, though weaker, survive and eventually absorb the technologies that had nearly destroyed them.
