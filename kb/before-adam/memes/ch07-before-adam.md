## CHAPTER VII

[007-001]Broken-Tooth was another youngster who lived by himself. His mother

[007-002]lived in the caves, but two more children had come after him and he had

[007-003]been thrust out to shift for himself. We had witnessed the performance

[007-004]during the several preceding days, and it had given us no little glee.

[007-005]Broken-Tooth did not want to go, and every time his mother left the

[007-006]cave he sneaked back into it. When she returned and found him there her

[007-007]rages were delightful. Half the horde made a practice of watching for

[007-008]these moments. First, from within the cave, would come her scolding and

[007-009]shrieking. Then we could hear sounds of the thrashing and the yelling

[007-010]of Broken-Tooth. About this time the two younger children joined in.

[007-011]And finally, like the eruption of a miniature volcano, Broken-Tooth

[007-012]would come flying out.

[007-013]At the end of several days his leaving home was accomplished. He wailed

[007-014]his grief, unheeded, from the centre of the open space, for at least

[007-015]half an hour, and then came to live with Lop-Ear and me. Our cave was

[007-016]small, but with squeezing there was room for three. I have no

[007-017]recollection of Broken-Tooth spending more than one night with us, so

[007-018]the accident must have happened right away.

[007-019]It came in the middle of the day. In the morning we had eaten our fill

[007-020]of the carrots, and then, made heedless by play, we had ventured on to

[007-021]the big trees just beyond. I cannot understand how Lop-Ear got over his

[007-022]habitual caution, but it must have been the play. We were having a

[007-023]great time playing tree tag. And such tag! We leaped ten or

[007-024]fifteen-foot gaps as a matter of course. And a twenty or twenty-five

[007-025]foot deliberate drop clear down to the ground was nothing to us. In

[007-026]fact, I am almost afraid to say the great distances we dropped. As we

[007-027]grew older and heavier we found we had to be more cautious in dropping,

[007-028]but at that age our bodies were all strings and springs and we could do

[007-029]anything.

[007-030]Broken-Tooth displayed remarkable agility in the game. He was “It” less

[007-031]frequently than any of us, and in the course of the game he discovered

[007-032]one difficult “slip” that neither Lop-Ear nor I was able to accomplish.

[007-033]To be truthful, we were afraid to attempt it.

[007-034]When we were “It,” Broken-Tooth always ran out to the end of a lofty

[007-035]branch in a certain tree. From the end of the branch to the ground it

[007-036]must have been seventy feet, and nothing intervened to break a fall.

[007-037]But about twenty feet lower down, and fully fifteen feet out from the

[007-038]perpendicular, was the thick branch of another tree.

[007-039]As we ran out the limb, Broken-Tooth, facing us, would begin teetering.

[007-040]This naturally impeded our progress; but there was more in the

[007-041]teetering than that. He teetered with his back to the jump he was to

[007-042]make. Just as we nearly reached him he would let go. The teetering

[007-043]branch was like a spring-board. It threw him far out, backward, as he

[007-044]fell. And as he fell he turned around sidewise in the air so as to face

[007-045]the other branch into which he was falling. This branch bent far down

[007-046]under the impact, and sometimes there was an ominous crackling; but it

[007-047]never broke, and out of the leaves was always to be seen the face of

[007-048]Broken-Tooth grinning triumphantly up at us.

[007-049]I was “It” the last time Broken-Tooth tried this. He had gained the end

[007-050]of the branch and begun his teetering, and I was creeping out after

[007-051]him, when suddenly there came a low warning cry from Lop-Ear. I looked

[007-052]down and saw him in the main fork of the tree crouching close against

[007-053]the trunk. Instinctively I crouched down upon the thick limb.

[007-054]Broken-Tooth stopped teetering, but the branch would not stop, and his

[007-055]body continued bobbing up and down with the rustling leaves.

[007-056]I heard the crackle of a dry twig, and looking down saw my first

[007-057]Fire-Man. He was creeping stealthily along on the ground and peering up

[007-058]into the tree. At first I thought he was a wild animal, because he wore

[007-059]around his waist and over his shoulders a ragged piece of bearskin. And

[007-060]then I saw his hands and feet, and more clearly his features. He was

[007-061]very much like my kind, except that he was less hairy and that his feet

[007-062]were less like hands than ours. In fact, he and his people, as I was

[007-063]later to know, were far less hairy than we, though we, in turn, were

[007-064]equally less hairy than the Tree People.

[007-065]It came to me instantly, as I looked at him. This was the terror of the

[007-066]northeast, of which the mystery of smoke was a token. Yet I was

[007-067]puzzled. Certainly he was nothing of which to be afraid. Red-Eye or

[007-068]any of our strong men would have been more than a match for him. He was

[007-069]old, too, wizened with age, and the hair on his face was gray. Also, he

[007-070]limped badly with one leg. There was no doubt at all that we could

[007-071]out-run him and out-climb him. He could never catch us, that was

[007-072]certain.

[007-073]But he carried something in his hand that I had never seen before. It

[007-074]was a bow and arrow. But at that time a bow and arrow had no meaning

[007-075]for me. How was I to know that death lurked in that bent piece of wood?

[007-076]But Lop-Ear knew. He had evidently seen the Fire People before and knew

[007-077]something of their ways. The Fire-Man peered up at him and circled

[007-078]around the tree. And around the main trunk above the fork Lop-Ear

[007-079]circled too, keeping always the trunk between himself and the Fire-Man.

[007-080]The latter abruptly reversed his circling. Lop-Ear, caught unawares,

[007-081]also hastily reversed, but did not win the protection of the trunk

[007-082]until after the Fire-Man had twanged the bow.

[007-083]I saw the arrow leap up, miss Lop-Ear, glance against a limb, and fall

[007-084]back to the ground. I danced up and down on my lofty perch with

[007-085]delight. It was a game! The Fire-Man was throwing things at Lop-Ear as

[007-086]we sometimes threw things at one another.

[007-087]The game continued a little longer, but Lop-Ear did not expose himself

[007-088]a second time. Then the Fire-Man gave it up. I leaned far out over my

[007-089]horizontal limb and chattered down at him. I wanted to play. I wanted

[007-090]to have him try to hit me with the thing. He saw me, but ignored me,

[007-091]turning his attention to Broken-Tooth, who was still teetering slightly

[007-092]and involuntarily on the end of the branch.

[007-093]The first arrow leaped upward. Broken-Tooth yelled with fright and

[007-094]pain. It had reached its mark. This put a new complexion on the matter.

[007-095]I no longer cared to play, but crouched trembling close to my limb. A

[007-096]second arrow and a third soared up, missing Broken-Tooth, rustling the

[007-097]leaves as they passed through, arching in their flight and returning to

[007-098]earth.

[007-099]The Fire-Man stretched his bow again. He shifted his position, walking

[007-100]away several steps, then shifted it a second time. The bow-string

[007-101]twanged, the arrow leaped upward, and Broken-Tooth, uttering a terrible

[007-102]scream, fell off the branch. I saw him as he went down, turning over

[007-103]and over, all arms and legs it seemed, the shaft of the arrow

[007-104]projecting from his chest and appearing and disappearing with each

[007-105]revolution of his body.

[007-106]Sheer down, screaming, seventy feet he fell, smashing to the earth with

[007-107]an audible thud and crunch, his body rebounding slightly and settling

[007-108]down again. Still he lived, for he moved and squirmed, clawing with his

[007-109]hands and feet. I remember the Fire-Man running forward with a stone

[007-110]and hammering him on the head...and then I remember no more.

[007-111]Always, during my childhood, at this stage of the dream, did I wake up

[007-112]screaming with fright—to find, often, my mother or nurse, anxious and

[007-113]startled, by my bedside, passing soothing hands through my hair and

[007-114]telling me that they were there and that there was nothing to fear.

[007-115]My next dream, in the order of succession, begins always with the

[007-116]flight of Lop-Ear and myself through the forest. The Fire-Man and

[007-117]Broken-Tooth and the tree of the tragedy are gone. Lop-Ear and I, in a

[007-118]cautious panic, are fleeing through the trees. In my right leg is a

[007-119]burning pain; and from the flesh, protruding head and shaft from either

[007-120]side, is an arrow of the Fire-Man. Not only did the pull and strain of

[007-121]it pain me severely, but it bothered my movements and made it

[007-122]impossible for me to keep up with Lop-Ear.

[007-123]At last I gave up, crouching in the secure fork of a tree. Lop-Ear went

[007-124]right on. I called to him—most plaintively, I remember; and he stopped

[007-125]and looked back. Then he returned to me, climbing into the fork and

[007-126]examining the arrow. He tried to pull it out, but one way the flesh

[007-127]resisted the barbed head, and the other way it resisted the feathered

[007-128]shaft. Also, it hurt grievously, and I stopped him.

[007-129]For some time we crouched there, Lop-Ear nervous and anxious to be

[007-130]gone, perpetually and apprehensively peering this way and that, and

[007-131]myself whimpering softly and sobbing. Lop-Ear was plainly in a funk,

[007-132]and yet his conduct in remaining by me, in spite of his fear, I take as

[007-133]a foreshadowing of the altruism and comradeship that have helped make

[007-134]man the mightiest of the animals.

[007-135]Once again Lop-Ear tried to drag the arrow through the flesh, and I

[007-136]angrily stopped him. Then he bent down and began gnawing the shaft of

[007-137]the arrow with his teeth. As he did so he held the arrow firmly in both

[007-138]hands so that it would not play about in the wound, and at the same

[007-139]time I held on to him. I often meditate upon this scene—the two of us,

[007-140]half-grown cubs, in the childhood of the race, and the one mastering

[007-141]his fear, beating down his selfish impulse of flight, in order to stand

[007-142]by and succor the other. And there rises up before me all that was

[007-143]there foreshadowed, and I see visions of Damon and Pythias, of

[007-144]life-saving crews and Red Cross nurses, of martyrs and leaders of

[007-145]forlorn hopes, of Father Damien, and of the Christ himself, and of all

[007-146]the men of earth, mighty of stature, whose strength may trace back to

[007-147]the elemental loins of Lop-Ear and Big-Tooth and other dim denizens of

[007-148]the Younger World.

[007-149]When Lop-Ear had chewed off the head of the arrow, the shaft was

[007-150]withdrawn easily enough. I started to go on, but this time it was he

[007-151]that stopped me. My leg was bleeding profusely. Some of the smaller

[007-152]veins had doubtless been ruptured. Running out to the end of a branch,

[007-153]Lop-Ear gathered a handful of green leaves. These he stuffed into the

[007-154]wound. They accomplished the purpose, for the bleeding soon stopped.

[007-155]Then we went on together, back to the safety of the caves.
