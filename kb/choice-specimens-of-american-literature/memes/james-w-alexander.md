# James W. Alexander

## Biography

James Waddel Alexander (1804–1859) was an American Presbyterian minister and theologian. He was born in Louisa County, Virginia, the eldest son of the renowned theologian Archibald Alexander, who served as the first professor at Princeton Theological Seminary. James W. Alexander graduated from the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University) in 1820 and subsequently studied theology at Princeton Seminary under his father and Samuel Miller.

Alexander held several prominent pastorates, including the First Presbyterian Church of Trenton, New Jersey, and the Duane Street Presbyterian Church in New York City. In 1849 he was appointed professor of ecclesiastical history and church government at Princeton Theological Seminary, and in 1851 he succeeded his father as professor of pastoral theology, a position he held until his death. A man of wide cultivation, Alexander was also a skilled musician and an accomplished translator of French and German theological works.

## Literary Career

Alexander was a prolific writer whose works span homiletics, pastoral theology, and devotional literature. His most enduring book is *Discourses on Christian Faith and Practice* (published posthumously), a collection of sermons that display his characteristic blend of doctrinal depth and warm pastoral appeal. Other notable works include *The American Mechanic* (1838), published under the pseudonym "Charles Quill," *Thoughts on Family Worship* (1847), and *Consolation* (1852), a volume of letters to the bereaved.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, titled "The Church a Temple," is drawn from *Discourses on Christian Faith and Practice*. In it, Alexander employs an extended architectural metaphor to describe the building up of the church across the ages, with each prophet and apostle occupying a distinctive place in the edifice:

> In surveying the past, we observe a beautiful fitness and an enchanting variety in the materials which have been already built into that part of the edifice which has thus far been reared. (008-012)

Alexander's rhetorical gift is on display as he individuates the biblical figures:

> Isaiah with his evangelic clarion, Jeremiah with his pastoral reed of sorrows, and David with his many-voiced harp, sometimes loud in notes of triumph, and sometimes subdued to the voice of weeping, stand out with a marked individuality. (008-012)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Church a Temple* (008-012) — From *Discourses on Christian Faith and Practice*. An extended meditation on the church as a spiritual temple built from the diverse materials of prophets, apostles, martyrs, and confessors across the ages. Alexander draws vivid character sketches of Isaiah, Jeremiah, David, Paul, Peter, and John, each occupying a "salient angle of the great foundation," before extending the metaphor through the ages of persecution and reform to the present day.

## Critical Reception

Alexander was highly esteemed in his own day as both a preacher and a writer. His sermons were praised for their combination of theological precision and emotional warmth, avoiding the extremes of dry scholasticism on one side and unmoored enthusiasm on the other. New York's literary and religious circles valued him as a bridge figure — a conservative Presbyterian who engaged sympathetically with the broader currents of American letters.

His early death at fifty-five cut short what contemporaries expected would be a still more substantial literary legacy. His *Discourses* continued to be reprinted in devotional series through the late nineteenth century, though like many antebellum religious writers, his readership narrowed in the twentieth century to historians of American Presbyterianism and Princeton theology.
