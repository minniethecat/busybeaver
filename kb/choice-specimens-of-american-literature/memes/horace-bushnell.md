# Horace Bushnell

## Biography

Horace Bushnell (1804–1876) was an American Congregational minister and theologian, widely regarded as one of the most original religious thinkers in nineteenth-century America. Born in Litchfield, Connecticut, he graduated from Yale College in 1827 and, after a brief career in teaching and journalism, entered Yale Divinity School in 1831. He was ordained in 1833 and became pastor of the North Congregational Church in Hartford, Connecticut, a pulpit he would hold for over twenty years.

Bushnell's theology was shaped by a deep engagement with Romantic thought — particularly Coleridge and Schleiermacher — and by a lifelong struggle against the revivalistic Calvinism of his New England upbringing. His 1847 book *Christian Nurture* argued against the prevailing emphasis on sudden adult conversion, insisting that children raised in Christian homes should grow into faith as naturally as they learned language — not through crisis but through the organic influence of family and church. The idea was revolutionary in its time and helped reshape American Protestant religious education.

Bushnell's later years were marked by controversy. His views on the Trinity and the atonement were denounced as heterodox, and he narrowly avoided a heresy trial. Yet he remained in his Hartford pulpit, respected even by his critics for the depth of his piety and the beauty of his prose. His health declined after 1860, but he continued to write — on science and religion, on the meaning of sacrifice, and on the moral influence of Christ.

## Literary Career

Bushnell's major works include *Christian Nurture* (1847), *God in Christ* (1849) — the volume that provoked the heresy controversy — *Nature and the Supernatural* (1858), and *The Vicarious Sacrifice* (1866). His literary reputation, however, rests as much on his occasional essays and sermons as on his formal theological treatises. Bushnell was a master of the extended metaphor, capable of sustaining a single image — light, a lake, a vine — across pages of argument without losing either the thread of the logic or the emotional force of the image.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn from Bushnell's essay on unconscious influence, which argues that the most powerful force a Christian exerts is not what he says or does deliberately but what he is — the silent, steady emanation of character:

> So the Christian shines, it would say, not so much because he will, as because he is a luminous object. Not that the active influence of Christians is made of no account in the figure, but only that this symbol of light has its propriety in the fact that their unconscious influence is the chief influence. (010-011)

Earlier in the same essay, Bushnell develops a parallel metaphor — the reflection of the sky in a mountain lake, which becomes a figure for the clarity with which a loving heart perceives God:

> Behold on the borders of a mountain lake, the reflection of the scene above, received into the bosom of the lake below! (010-010)

When the wind rises and the lake grows turbulent, the image is lost — just as the passions of an unholy mind render God's character "all mingled in confusion":

> God and his truth are always clear, always the same, but the passions of men fill their own hearts with obscurity and turbulence. (010-010)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Unconscious Influence* (010-010, 010-011) — From *Sermons for the New Life* (1858). Bushnell develops one of his signature arguments: that the most potent human influence is not deliberate action but unconscious character — the steady, silent emanation of what a person is. He develops two extended metaphors: the mountain lake that perfectly reflects the sky above (the calm heart that mirrors God) until the wind disturbs it (the passions that obscure God), and the morning light that silently renews the world (the Christian whose unconscious influence reshapes those around him).

## Critical Reception

Bushnell was recognized in his lifetime as one of the most gifted prose stylists in the American pulpit, and his theological writings — despite the controversies they provoked — were widely admired for their imaginative power. His influence extended well beyond his own denomination; liberal theologians of the late nineteenth century claimed him as a forerunner, and even conservative critics acknowledged the beauty of his language.

Bushnell's reputation has endured better than that of most nineteenth-century American theologians. *Christian Nurture* is still read in seminaries, and his essays on language and metaphor anticipate themes in twentieth-century theology and literary criticism. Scholars regard him as a transitional figure between the Calvinist orthodoxy of the early republic and the liberal Protestantism that dominated the late nineteenth century — a theologian who preserved the emotional depth of evangelical piety while discarding much of its doctrinal apparatus.
