# Henry Lee

## Biography

Henry Lee III (1756–1818), known to history as "Light-Horse Harry" Lee, was an American cavalry officer, politician, and writer. Born at Leesylvania plantation in Prince William County, Virginia, he graduated from the College of New Jersey (Princeton) in 1773. When the Revolutionary War broke out, Lee joined the Continental Army and rose rapidly through the ranks, earning his famous sobriquet for his masterful command of a mixed legion of cavalry and infantry.

Lee's Legion conducted daring raids against British supply lines and fought with distinction in the southern campaign under General Nathanael Greene. After the war, Lee served in the Virginia House of Delegates, the Continental Congress (1786–1788), and as Governor of Virginia (1791–1794). He commanded the federal troops that suppressed the Whiskey Rebellion in 1794. Lee's later years were marked by financial misfortune; he suffered severe injuries defending a friend's newspaper office from a mob in Baltimore in 1812 and spent his final years in the West Indies, dying on his return voyage to Virginia. He is also remembered as the father of Confederate General Robert E. Lee and as the author of the famous epitaph for George Washington: "first in war, first in peace, and first in the hearts of his countrymen."

## Literary Career

Lee's major literary work is *Memoirs of the War in the Southern Department of the United States* (1812), a vivid first-hand account of the Revolutionary War's southern campaigns. The *Memoirs* combines military narrative with character sketches of the war's principal figures, written in a vigorous, sometimes ornate Federalist-era style. Lee also wrote political essays and delivered speeches as a Virginia statesman. His prose reflects the values of the Revolutionary generation — patriotism, honor, and a conviction that public virtue was essential to republican government.

## Selections in This Anthology

In "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Lee is represented by two selections: "Indian Services" and "Sketch of Penn." The latter offers a stirring tribute to William Penn, the founder of Pennsylvania, whom Lee presents as a model of peaceful statesmanship superior to the military lawgivers of antiquity:

> "WILLIAM PENN stands the first among the lawgivers whose names and deeds are recorded in history. Shall we compare him with Lycurgus, Solon, Romulus, those founders of military commonwealths, who organized their citizens in deadly array against the rest of their species, taught them to consider their fellow-men as barbarians, and themselves as alone worthy to rule over the earth?" (025-011)

> "But see William Penn, with weaponless hand, sitting down peaceably with his followers, in the midst of savage nations whose only occupation was shedding the blood of their fellow-men, disarming them by his justice, and teaching them, for the first time, to view a stranger without distrust. See them bury their tomahawks in his presence, so deep that man shall never be able to find them again." (025-011)

Lee's eulogy of Penn contrasts the Quaker founder's principled pacifism with the violence of classical republics — a striking argument coming from a career soldier. The passage reflects the Revolutionary generation's complex relationship with warfare and their hope that the American experiment might transcend the bloody patterns of European history.

## Critical Reception

Lee's *Memoirs of the War* was well received upon publication and remains a valuable source for historians of the American Revolution. His prose, though sometimes florid by modern standards, was admired by contemporaries for its energy and patriotic fervor. Lee's reputation as a writer has been somewhat overshadowed by his military fame and by his son's prominence in later American history, but his writings offer an important window into the mind of the Revolutionary officer class and their vision of the republic they had fought to create.
