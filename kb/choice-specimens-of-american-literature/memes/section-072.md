From \"Evangeline.\"

=\_369.\_= SONG OF THE MOCKING-BIRD, AT SUNSET.

[071-001]Softly the evening came. The sun, from the western horizon, Like a magician, extended his golden wand o\'er the landscape; Twinkling vapors arose; and sky and water and forest Seemed all on fire at the touch, and melted and mingled together. Hanging between two skies, a cloud with edges of silver, Floated the boat, with its dripping oars, on the motionless water.

[071-002]Filled was Evangeline\'s heart with inexpressible sweetness. Touched by the magic spell, the sacred fountains of feeling Glowed with the light of love, as the skies and waters around her.

[071-003]Then from a neighboring thicket the mocking-bird, wildest of singers, Swinging aloft on a willow spray that hung o\'er the water, Shook from his little throat such floods of delirious music, That the whole air and the woods and the waves seemed silent to listen.

[071-004]Plaintive at first were the tones and sad; then soaring to madness, Seemed they to follow or guide the revel of frenzied Bacchantes. Single notes were then heard, in sorrowful, low lamentation; Till, having gathered them all, he flung them abroad in derision, As when, after a storm, a gust of wind through the tree-tops Shakes down the rattling rain in a crystal shower on the branches.

[071-005]With such a prelude as this, and hearts that throbbed with emotion, Slowly they entered the Têche, where it flows through the green Opelousas, And through the amber air, above the crest of the woodland, Saw the column of smoke that arose from a neighboring dwelling;\-- Sounds of a horn they heard, and the distant lowing of cattle.

From \"The Song of Hiawatha.\"

=\_370.\_= HIAWATHA\'S DEPARTURE.

[071-006]On the shore stood Hiawatha, Turned and waved his hand at parting; On the clear and luminous water Launched his birch canoe for sailing,

From the pebbles of the margin

[071-007]Shoved it forth into the water; Whispered to it, \"Westward! westward!\"

[071-008]And with speed it darted forward.

[071-009]And the evening sun descending Set the clouds on fire with redness, Burned the broad sky, like a prairie, Left upon the level water One long track and trail of splendor, Down whose streams, as down a river, Westward, westward Hiawatha Sailed into the fiery sunset, Sailed into the purple vapors, Sailed into the dusk of evening.

[071-010]And the people from the margin Watched him floating, rising, sinking, Till the birch canoe seemed lifted High into that sea of splendor, Till it sank into the vapors Like the new moon slowly, slowly Sinking in the purple distance.

[071-011]And they said, \"Farewell for ever!\"

[071-012]Said, \"Farewell, O Hiawatha!\"

[071-013]And the forests, dark and lonely, Moved through all their depth of darkness, Sighed, \"Farewell, O Hiawatha!\"

[071-014]And the waves upon the margin Rising, rippling on the pebbles, Sobbed, \"Farewell, O Hiawatha!\"

[071-015]And the heron, the Shu-shuh-gah,

From her haunts among the fen-lands,

[071-016]Screamed, \"Farewell, O Hiawatha!\"

[071-017]Thus departed Hiawatha, Hiawatha the beloved, In the glory of the sunset, In the purple mists of evening, To the regions of the home-wind, Of the Northwest wind Keewaydin, To the islands of the Blessed, To the kingdom of Ponemah, To the land of the Hereafter!

=\_371.\_= THE LABORER.

[071-018]Stand up\--erect! Thou hast the form, And likeness of thy God!\--who more?

[071-019]A soul as dauntless mid the storm Of daily life, a heart as warm And pure, as breast e\'er bore.

[071-020]What then?\--Thou art as true a Man As moves the human mass among; As much a part of the Great plan That with creation\'s dawn began, As any of the throng.

[071-021]Who is thine enemy? the high In station, or in wealth the chief?

[071-022]The great, who coldly pass thee by, With proud step and averted eye?

[071-023]Nay! nurse not such belief.

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-024]No:\--uncurbed passions\--low desires\-- Absence of noble self-respect\-- Death, in the breast\'s consuming fires, To that high [[nature-emerson|Nature]] which aspires For ever, till thus checked:

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-025]True, wealth thou hast not: \'tis but dust!

[071-026]Nor place; uncertain as the wind!

[071-027]But that thou hast, which, with thy crust And water, may despise the lust Of both\--a noble mind.

[071-028]With this and passions under ban, True faith, and holy trust in God, Thou art the peer of any man.

[071-029]Look up, then\--that thy little span Of life, may be well trod!

=\_372.\_= WHAT THE VOICE SAID.

[071-030]Maddened by Earth\'s wrong and evil, \"Lord,\" I cried in sudden ire, \"From thy right hand, clothed with thunder, Shake the bolted fire!

[071-031]\"Love is lost, and Faith is dying; With the brute, the man is sold; And the dropping blood of labor Hardens into gold.\"

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-032]\"Thou, the patient Heaven upbraiding,\"

[071-033]Spake a solemn Voice within; \"Weary of our Lord\'s forbearance, Art thou free from sin?\"

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-034]\"Earnest words must needs be spoken When the warm heart bleeds or burns With its scorn of wrong, or pity For the wronged, by turns.

[071-035]\"But, by all thy nature\'s weakness, Hidden faults and follies known, Be thou, in rebuking evil, Conscious of thine own.

[071-036]\"Not the less shall stern-eyed Duty To thy lips her trumpet set, But with harsher blasts shall mingle Wailings of regret.\"

[071-037]Cease not, Voice of holy speaking, Teacher sent of God, be near, Whispering through the day\'s cool silence, Let my spirit hear!

[071-038]So, when thoughts of evil doers Waken scorn, or hatred move, Shall a mournful fellow-feeling Temper all with love.

From \"The Tent on the Beach.\"

=\_373.\_= THE ATLANTIC TELEGRAPH.

[071-039]O lonely bay of Trinity, O dreary shores, give ear!

[071-040]Lean down unto the white-lipped sea The voice of God to hear!

From world to world his couriers fly,

[071-041]Thought-winged, and shod with fire; The angel of his stormy sky Rides down the sunken wire.

[071-042]What saith the herald of the Lord?

[071-043]\"The world\'s long strife is done; Close wedded by that mystic cord, Its continents are one.

[071-044]\"And one in heart, as one in blood, Shall all her peoples be; The hands of human brotherhood Are clasped beneath the sea.

[071-045]\"Through Orient seas, o\'er Afric\'s plain And Asian mountains borne, The vigor of the Northern brain Shall nerve the world outworn.

[071-046]\"From clime to clime, from shore to shore, Shall thrill the magic thread; The new Prometheus steals once more The fire that wakes the dead.\"

[071-047]Throb on, strong pulse of thunder! beat

From answering beach to beach;

[071-048]Fuse nations in thy kindly heat, And melt the chains of each!

[071-049]Wild terror of the sky above, Glide tamed and dumb below!

[071-050]Bear gently, Ocean\'s carrier-dove, Thy errands to and fro.

[071-051]Weave on, swift shuttle of the Lord, Beneath the deep so far, The bridal robe of earth\'s accord, The funeral shroud of war!

[071-052]For lo! the fall of Ocean\'s wall, Space mocked, and time outrun; And round the world the thought of all Is as the thought of one!

[071-053]The poles unite, the zones agree, The tongues of striving cease; As on the sea of Galilee, The Christ is whispering, Peace!

From Snow-Bound.

=\_374.\_= DESCRIPTION OF A SNOW STORM.

[071-054]The sun that brief December day Rose cheerless over hills of gray, And, darkly circled, gave at noon A sadder light than waning moon, Slow tracing down the thickening sky Its mute and ominous prophecy, A portent seeming less than threat, It sank from sight before it set.

[071-055]A chill no coat, however stout, Of homespun stuff could quite shut out, A hard, dull bitterness of cold, That checked, mid-vein, the circling race Of life-blood in the sharpened face, The coming of the snow-storm told.

[071-056]The wind blew east: we heard the roar Of Ocean on his wintry shore, And felt the strong pulse throbbing there Beat with low rhythm our inland air.

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-057]Unwarmed by any sunset light The gray day darkened into night, A night made hoary with the swarm And whirl-dance of the blinding storm, A zigzag wavering to and fro Crossed and recrossed the wingéd snow: And ere the early bed-time came The white drift piled the window-frame, And, through the glass, the clothes-line posts Looked in like tall and sheeted ghosts.

[071-058]So all night long the storm rolled on: The morning broke without a sun; In tiny spherule traced with lines Of Nature\'s geometric signs, In starry flake and pellicle, All day the hoary meteor fell; And, when the second morning shone, We looked upon a world unknown, On nothing we could call our own.

[071-059]Around the glistening wonder bent The blue walls of the firmament, No cloud above, no earth below,\-- A universe of sky and snow!

From \"The Pennsylvania Pilgrim.\"

=\_375.\_= THE QUAKER\'S CREED.

\* \* \* \* \*

[071-060]Gathered from many sects, the Quaker brought His old beliefs, adjusting to the thought That moved his soul, the creed his fathers taught. One faith alone, so broad that all mankind Within themselves its secret witness find, The soul\'s communion with the Eternal Mind, The Spirit\'s [[justice|law]], the Inward Rule and Guide, Scholar and peasant, lord and serf, allied, The polished Penn, and Cromwell\'s Ironside.

[071-061]As still in Hemskerck\'s Quaker meeting, face By face, in Flemish detail, we may trace How loose-mouthed boor, and fine ancestral grace, Sat in close contrast,\--the clipt-headed churl, Broad market-dame, and simple serving-girl, By skirt of silk and periwig in curl!

[071-062]For soul touched soul; the spiritual treasure-trove Made all men equal, none could rise above, Nor sink below, that level of God\'s love.

[071-063]So, with his rustic neighbors sitting down, The homespun frock beside the scholar\'s gown, Pastorius, to the manners of the town Added the [[liberty|freedom]] of the woods, and sought The bookless wisdom by experience taught, And learned to love his new-found home, while not Forgetful of the old; the seasons went Their rounds, and somewhat to his spirit lent Of their own calm and measureless content.

[071-064]Glad even to tears, he heard the robin sing His song of welcome to the Western spring, And bluebird borrowing from the sky his wing.

[071-065]And when the miracle of autumn came, And all the woods with many-colored flame Of splendor, making summer\'s greenness tame, Burned unconsumed, a voice without a sound Spake to him from each kindled bush around And made the strange, new landscape holy ground.

From \"Lines on the Rocky Mountains.\"

=\_376.\_= THE EVERLASTING HILLS.

[071-066]The deep, transparent sky is full Of many thousand glittering lights\-- Unnumbered stars that calmly rule The dark dominions of the night.

[071-067]The mild, bright moon has upward risen, Out of the gray and boundless plain, And all around the white snows glisten, Where frost, and ice, and silence, reign,\-- While ages roll away, and they unchanged remain.

[071-068]These mountains, piercing the blue sky With their eternal cones of ice,\-- The torrents dashing from on high, O\'er rock, and crag, and precipice,\-- Change not, but still remain as ever, Unwasting, deathless, and sublime, And will remain while lightnings quiver, Or stars the hoary summits climb, Or rolls the thunder-chariot of eternal Time.

From her \"Poems.\"

=\_377.\_= THE DUMB CREATION.

[071-069]Deal kindly with those speechless ones, That throng our gladsome earth; Say not the bounteous gift of life Alone is nothing worth.

[071-070]What though with mournful memories They sigh not for the past?

[071-071]What though their ever joyous now No future overcast.

[071-072]No aspirations fill their breast With longings undefined; They live, they love, and they are blest For what they seek they find.

[071-073]They see no mystery in the stars, No wonder in the plain, And Life\'s enigma wakes in them, No questions dark and vain.

[071-074]To them earth is a final home, A bright and blest abode; Their lives unconsciously flow on In harmony with God.
