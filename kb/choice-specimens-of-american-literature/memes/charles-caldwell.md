# Charles Caldwell

## Biography

Charles Caldwell (1772–1853) was an American physician, educator, and prolific polemicist who played a significant role in the development of medical education in the trans-Appalachian West. Born in Caswell County, North Carolina, Caldwell studied medicine under Benjamin Rush at the University of Pennsylvania, where he received his M.D. in 1796. The biographical note in "Choice Specimens" identifies him concisely:

> A native of North Carolina; prominent as a physician and controversialist. (022-017, 022-018)

After practicing in Philadelphia for over two decades, Caldwell moved to Lexington, Kentucky, in 1819 at the invitation of Transylvania University. There he founded the institution's medical department, which under his leadership became one of the most respected medical schools west of the Alleghenies. His energetic — and often combative — personality made him a dominant figure in Kentucky's intellectual life. In 1837, after a falling-out with the Transylvania trustees, Caldwell moved to Louisville and helped establish the Louisville Medical Institute, which later became the University of Louisville School of Medicine.

Caldwell's intellectual range was extraordinarily broad. He wrote on yellow fever, phrenology, temperance, the philosophy of medicine, and the supposed inferiority of African races (a position for which he is now remembered with censure). His combative style earned him many enemies, but his energy and organizational skill left an enduring imprint on medical education in the American West.

## Literary Career

Caldwell was a voluminous author whose publications numbered more than two hundred titles. His prose is vigorous, often polemical, and animated by the same combative spirit that marked his professional life. He edited several medical journals, including the *Port Folio* and the *American Medical Review*, using them as platforms to advance his views and to attack his rivals.

His most distinctive contribution to American letters, paradoxically, is not his medical writing but his vivid personal reminiscences. The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" — an account of attending the introductory lecture of his mentor, Dr. Benjamin Rush, at the University of Pennsylvania — shows Caldwell at his best as a descriptive writer:

> At length, however, though the class of the winter, all told, amounted to less than a hundred, a sufficient number had arrived to induce the professors to commence their lectures; and the introductory of Dr. Rush was a performance of deep and touching interest, and never, I think, to be forgotten (while his memory endures), by any one who listened to it, and was susceptible of the impression it was calculated to make. (022-016)

The lecture Caldwell describes was Rush's response to the yellow fever epidemic that had devastated Philadelphia in 1793. Caldwell captures both the emotional power of the occasion and the substance of Rush's address:

> It consisted in a well-written and graphical description of the terrible sweep of the late pestilence; the wild dismay and temporary desolation it had produced; the scenes of family and individual suffering and woe he had witnessed during its ravages. (022-016)

Caldwell's judgment on the lecture is measured — he acknowledges its rhetorical excesses while affirming its genuine instructional value:

> The discourse, though highly colored, and marked by not a few figures of fancy and bursts of feeling, was, notwithstanding, sufficiently fraught with substantial matter to render it no less instructive than it was fascinating. (022-016)

## Selections in This Anthology

## Critical Reception

Caldwell's reputation has declined sharply since his death, in part because of his advocacy of racial theories now universally repudiated, and in part because his aggressive personality alienated many contemporaries. Yet historians of American medicine continue to study him as a representative figure in the professionalization of medicine and the westward expansion of medical education. The physician and medical historian Henry E. Sigerist noted that Caldwell "did more than any other single individual to establish medical education in the Mississippi Valley." His autobiography, published posthumously in 1855, remains a valuable and readable source for the social history of early American medicine.
