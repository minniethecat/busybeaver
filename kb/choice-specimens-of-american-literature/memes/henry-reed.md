# Henry Reed

## Biography

Henry Reed (1808–1854) was an American educator, literary scholar, and translator. Born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, he was the son of a distinguished family; his grandfather, Joseph Reed, had served as an aide-de-camp to George Washington and as President of Pennsylvania. Reed graduated from the University of Pennsylvania in 1825, studied law and was admitted to the bar, but soon abandoned legal practice in favor of literary scholarship and teaching.

In 1831, Reed was appointed professor of English literature at the University of Pennsylvania — one of the earliest professorships in English literature established at an American college. He held this position until his death, with a brief interruption in 1834–1835 when he served as vice-provost. Reed was also an active participant in Philadelphia's literary life, contributing to periodicals and maintaining a wide correspondence with writers and scholars in America and Britain.

Reed's life was cut short in 1854 when he perished in the wreck of the steamship *Arctic* while returning from a visit to England. He was forty-six. The ship collided with a French steamer off the coast of Newfoundland; Reed was among the more than three hundred passengers who lost their lives. His death was widely mourned in literary circles on both sides of the Atlantic.

## Literary Career

Reed's most significant contribution to American letters was his editorship of the first complete American edition of William Wordsworth's *Poetical Works* (1837), for which Wordsworth himself provided revisions and a new preface. Reed was a devoted Wordsworthian, and his editorial labors helped introduce Wordsworth to a wide American audience. The two men corresponded extensively, and Reed's visit to Wordsworth at Rydal Mount in 1854 was the occasion of his final, fatal voyage.

Reed's original works include *Lectures on English History* (1855), published posthumously, and *Lectures on the British Poets*, delivered at the University of Pennsylvania. He also produced translations and essays on Shakespeare, and his lecture style — combining careful scholarship with a warm, conversational manner — was praised by contemporaries for making literary history accessible without sacrificing depth.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn from *Lectures on English History*, and takes up the question of the legendary origins of Britain — the stories of Brutus the Trojan and the ancient British kings that medieval chroniclers recorded as fact:

> It would be a weary, and probably vain inquiry to consider minutely the claims which such historical materials have on our belief; and so little is there attractive in the legends of British history, that I need not attempt to dwell upon any of the alleged facts. (043-019)

Reed's treatment is notable for its historiographical sophistication. Rather than simply dismissing the legends as false, he examines the psychology of belief — "the curious tenacity" with which they were held — and traces the gradual triumph of a "more critical standard of historic belief" over "uninquiring and passive credulity." He illustrates this with a lively example from the reign of Edward I, whose claims to sovereignty over Scotland were derived from the supposed conquest of Britain by Brutus in the time of Eli and Samuel:

> No Philadelphia estate could be more methodically traced back to the proprietary title of William Penn, than was this claim to Scotland up to Brutus, the exile from Troy. (043-019)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Legendary Period of British History* (043-019) — From *Lectures on English History* (1855, posthumous). A historiographical inquiry into the legendary origins of Britain, examining the persistence of belief in the Brutus myth — the idea that the Britons were descended from Trojan exiles — from medieval chronicles down through the sixteenth century. Reed uses the story to illustrate the transition from credulous chronicle to critical history.

## Critical Reception

Reed's contemporaries esteemed him as a scholar and a teacher. His edition of Wordsworth was the standard American text for decades, and his *Lectures on English History* was widely adopted as a textbook. His death in the *Arctic* disaster drew tributes from across the English-speaking world; the *North American Review* eulogized him as "a scholar of fine taste and solid learning."

Reed's subsequent reputation has been modest. He left no major original work, and his editorial achievements — however important in their time — are the kind that fade as editions are superseded. Recent scholarly interest in the transatlantic reception of Wordsworth and the early history of English studies in America has, however, drawn attention to Reed's role as a cultural mediator between British Romanticism and American literary culture. His correspondence with Wordsworth remains an important resource for scholars of both figures.
