# Leonard Bacon

## Biography

Leonard Bacon (1802–1881) was an American Congregational clergyman, editor, and social reformer. Born in Detroit, Michigan Territory, he was the son of a missionary to the Native Americans. Bacon graduated from Yale College in 1820 and from Andover Theological Seminary in 1823. In 1825 he was ordained and installed as pastor of the Center Church in New Haven, Connecticut, a pulpit he would occupy for over forty years.

Bacon was a central figure in the religious and intellectual life of New England. He served as professor of systematic theology at Yale Divinity School from 1866 to 1871, and was a lecturer on church polity and history until his death. A moderate antislavery voice within the Congregational church, Bacon advocated for colonization as a gradualist approach to emancipation, though his views evolved over time. He founded and edited the *New Englander* (later the *Yale Review*), a quarterly journal that became a leading platform for Congregationalist thought on theology, politics, and social reform.

## Literary Career

Bacon's literary output was chiefly in the form of sermons, periodical essays, and polemical pamphlets. As editor of the *New Englander* for nearly three decades, he shaped the discourse of New England Congregationalism on questions ranging from slavery and temperance to foreign missions and higher education. His collected works include *Slavery Discussed in Occasional Essays* (1846), *The Genesis of the New England Churches* (1874), and numerous individual sermons published as pamphlets.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, titled "The Day Approaching," is drawn from a missionary sermon. It exemplifies the millennial optimism that characterized much of nineteenth-century American evangelical thought. In a passage of sustained anaphora, Bacon envisions the coming triumph of Christianity over every form of human corruption:

> The time is to come when the world will be filled with the knowledge, the fear, and the praise of God. Not always will war deluge the earth with fire and blood. Not always will idolatry offend the heavens with its abominations. (008-010)

The sermon closes with a vision of universal redemption, drawing on Isaiah's prophecy:

> The ransomed of the Lord shall return and come to Zion with songs, and with garlands of everlasting joy; and from the earth, no longer accursed for the sake of man, sorrow and sighing shall flee away. (008-010)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Day Approaching* (008-010) — From a Missionary Sermon. A millennial vision of the world filled with the knowledge and praise of God. Bacon catalogs the evils that will be vanquished — war, idolatry, despotism, superstition, infidelity, and fanaticism — before proclaiming the coming day when "the sun, in his daily journey round the renovated world, shall waken with his morning beam in every human dwelling the voice of joyful, thankful, spiritual worship."

## Critical Reception

Bacon was recognized among his contemporaries as one of the leading theological minds of New England Congregationalism. His pastorate at Center Church placed him at the heart of Yale and New Haven society, and his editorials in the *New Englander* reached a national audience of clergy and educated laity. His moderate antislavery stance — opposing immediate abolition while denouncing the extension of slavery — drew criticism from both radical abolitionists and pro-slavery apologists, but his moral seriousness and institutional loyalty were never in doubt.

Bacon's reputation has receded along with the broader eclipse of the New England theology. Modern scholars of American religion study him chiefly as a representative figure: the theologically orthodox, institutionally conservative, socially reformist New England pastor who struggled to reconcile biblical authority with the moral demands of a nation divided over slavery.
