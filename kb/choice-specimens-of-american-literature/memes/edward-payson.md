# Edward Payson

## Biography

Edward Payson (1783–1827) was an American Congregational minister whose intense piety, literary eloquence, and pastoral devotion earned him the reverential nickname "Payson of Portland." Born in Rindge, New Hampshire, he was the son of the Rev. Seth Payson, a Congregational minister. Edward graduated from Harvard College in 1803 and, after a period of teaching and studying theology, was ordained in 1807 as pastor of the Second Congregational Church of Portland, Maine — a position he held for the remaining twenty years of his life.

Payson's ministry was marked by extraordinary pastoral diligence and a deeply personal, almost mystical piety. He was known to spend hours in private prayer and was revered by his congregation for his evident holiness of life. His physical constitution was frail — he suffered from a spinal condition and chronic pulmonary illness — but his spiritual intensity and intellectual energy never flagged. His early death at the age of forty-four was widely mourned across New England; his posthumously published works became standards of American evangelical devotional literature, reprinted continuously through the nineteenth century.

## Literary Career

Payson's literary legacy rests primarily on his sermons and devotional writings, which were collected and published after his death. His most famous works include *Selections from the Conversations and Unpublished Writings of the Rev. Edward Payson* and published volumes of his sermons. His prose was characterized by a combination of logical rigor and emotional intensity — he argued with the precision of a trained theologian but appealed with the warmth of an earnest pastor.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," titled "Natural Religion," is taken from Payson's published *Selections* and represents a polemical engagement with one of the central intellectual questions of the Enlightenment: whether unaided human reason, apart from divine revelation, is sufficient to guide mankind to truth and happiness. Payson's answer is an emphatic and rhetorically sustained "no":

> I know that those who hate and despise the religion of Jesus because it condemns their evil deeds, have endeavored to deprive him of the honor of communicating to mankind the glad tidings of life and immortality. I know that they have dragged the mouldering carcass of paganism from the grave, animated her lifeless form with a spark stolen from the sacred altar, arrayed her in the spoils of Christianity, re-lighted her extinguished taper at the torch of revelation, dignified her with the name of natural religion, and exalted her in the temple of reason, as a goddess, able, without divine assistance, to guide mankind to truth and happiness. (007-006)

This opening is a masterful piece of polemical imagery. Payson personifies "natural religion" as a reanimated pagan corpse, dressed in borrowed Christian garments and falsely enthroned. The extended metaphor — dragging a carcass from the grave, animating it with stolen fire, arraying it in spoils — is extraordinarily vivid and deliberately grotesque. It reflects Payson's conviction that the Enlightenment's appeal to "natural religion" was not merely mistaken but fraudulent: a theft of Christian ideas repackaged as autonomous reason.

> But we also know, that all her boasted pretensions are vain, the offspring of ignorance, wickedness, and pride. We know that she is indebted to that revelation which she presumes to ridicule, and contemn, for every semblance of truth or energy which she displays. (007-006)

The balance of the passage consists of a rhetorical question — a long catalogue of ancient civilizations (Egyptians, Greeks, Romans, Phoenicians) who, despite possessing "the light of reason and natural religion, in its fullest extent," failed to arrive at moral truth, worshiping "leeks and onions," "sixty thousand gods," "deified murderers," and sacrificing infants to Saturn. The argument is historical and empirical rather than abstract: if natural religion is sufficient, why has it never, in any actual civilization, sufficed? Payson's rhetorical method here — accumulating examples to the point of absurdity — owes something to the satirical tradition but is deployed in the service of earnest theological argument.

## Critical Reception

Payson's posthumous reputation was immense within American evangelical circles. His biography, *Memoir, Select Thoughts, and Sermons of the Late Rev. Edward Payson* (compiled by Asa Cummings), became one of the most widely read religious biographies of the antebellum period, going through numerous editions on both sides of the Atlantic. His devotional writings were cited as formative by figures as diverse as Charles Spurgeon and the leaders of the Second Great Awakening.

Modern scholarship has recognized Payson as a representative figure of what historian Joseph Conforti has called "the New England evangelical tradition" — a fusion of Edwardsean theology, revivalist piety, and literary culture that shaped American Protestantism through the nineteenth century.
