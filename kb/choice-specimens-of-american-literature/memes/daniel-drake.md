# Daniel Drake

## Biography

Daniel Drake (1785–1852) was an American physician, medical educator, and author who became the preeminent figure in medicine and science in the early American West. Born in New Jersey and taken to Kentucky as a young child, Drake grew up on the frontier near present-day Cincinnati. Largely self-taught in his early years, he studied medicine under Dr. William Goforth and later at the University of Pennsylvania, where he earned his medical degree in 1815.

Drake returned to Cincinnati and became the driving force behind the city's emergence as a center of medical education in the West. He founded the Medical College of Ohio in 1819 and later established the Cincinnati Eye Infirmary and the Cincinnati College. His comprehensive study of his adopted city — *Picture of Cincinnati* (1815) — was one of the first scientific surveys of an American western settlement, combining medical geography, natural history, and social observation.

## Literary Career

Drake's most enduring literary achievement is his *Picture of Cincinnati*, a work that ranges from geological and climatic descriptions to social and economic analysis. It belongs to the tradition of the American "city biography" and reflects the Enlightenment-era conviction that scientific method could illuminate every aspect of civic life. Drake also authored *A Systematic Treatise, Historical, Etiological, and Practical, on the Principal Diseases of the Interior Valley of North America* (1850–1854), a landmark in American medical geography.

The selection included in this anthology is drawn from Drake's discussion of the ancient earthworks and mounds scattered across the Ohio Valley — a subject that fascinated both scientists and the public in the early nineteenth century:

> No objects in the State of Ohio seem to have more forcibly arrested the attention of travellers, nor employed a greater number of pens, than its antiquities. It is to be regretted, however, that so hastily and superficially have they been examined by strangers, and so generally neglected by ourselves, that the materials for a full description have not yet been collected. (054-001)

Drake approached the problem with the empiricism of a trained observer. He noted that the forests growing over these ruins provided evidence of their great antiquity:

> The forests over these remains exhibit no appearances of more recent growth than in other parts. Trees, several hundred years old, are in many places seen growing out of the ruins of others, which appear to have been of equal size. (054-002)

Most significantly, Drake distinguished between two classes of earthworks — the valley enclosures and the hill fortifications — and offered a functional interpretation that departed from earlier, more speculative theories:

> Those at Cincinnati, for example, exhibit so few of the characters of a defensive work, that General Wayne, upon attentively surveying them in 1794, was of opinion that they were not designed for that purpose. ... It would be precipitate to assert that the relics found in the valleys were for this purpose, and those of the uplands for defence. But while it is certain that the latter were military posts, it seems highly probable that the former were for ordinary abode in times of peace. They were towns and the seats of chiefs, whose perishable parts have crumbled into earth, and disappeared with the generations which formed them. (054-003)

This careful differentiation between domestic and military structures marks Drake as a contributor to the emerging scientific archaeology of North America, even as his primary identity remained that of a physician and medical educator.

## Critical Reception

Drake was widely respected by his contemporaries as the foremost medical figure of the early West. His *Picture of Cincinnati* was praised by reviewers for its scope and scholarly rigor. His *Principal Diseases of the Interior Valley* remains a foundational text in the history of American medical geography. Daniel J. Boorstin, in *The Americans: The National Experience*, cites Drake as an exemplar of the "booster-scientist" — the frontier intellectual who combined civic promotion with genuine scientific inquiry. While his literary reputation is modest, his works are valued by historians of American science, medicine, and the early Midwest as primary sources of enduring importance.
