# Richard H. Dana

## Biography

Richard Henry Dana (1787–1879) was an American poet, essayist, and literary critic. Born in Cambridge, Massachusetts, to a distinguished New England family, Dana was educated at Harvard College, where he developed a lasting interest in English literature and Romantic poetry. He was admitted to the bar but soon abandoned the practice of law to devote himself to literature. In 1815, Dana helped found the *North American Review*, one of the first important literary periodicals in the United States, and served as one of its early editors.

Dana spent much of his life in Boston and Cambridge, where he was a central figure in the emerging American literary scene. He was a friend and correspondent of Washington Allston, William Cullen Bryant, and other leading writers of the period. His son, Richard Henry Dana Jr. (1815–1882), would go on to achieve lasting fame as the author of *Two Years Before the Mast* (1840), the classic account of a sailor's life at sea.

## Literary Career

Danas literary reputation rests primarily on his poetry and critical essays. His verse, collected in *Poems and Prose Writings* (1833), anticipated the dark Romantic sensibility that would later characterize the work of Edgar Allan Poe and Nathaniel Hawthorne. Dana's poetry is marked by somber meditations on death, nature, and the supernatural, often set against the rugged New England coastline.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes Dana's narrative poem "The Buccaneer," a darkly atmospheric tale of guilt and supernatural retribution. The poem opens with an eerie, lyrical passage that evokes the haunted conscience of its protagonist:

> A sweet, low voice, in starry nights, / Chants to his ear a plaining song; / Its tones come winding up the heights, / Telling of woe and wrong; / And he must listen, till the stars grow dim, / The song that gentle voice doth sing to him. (065-001)

The poem's meditation on sin and memory draws on the Gothic tradition while remaining distinctly American in its coastal setting and moral seriousness. Dana reflects: "O, it is sad that aught so mild / Should bind the soul with bands of fear; / That strains to soothe a little child / The man should dread to hear! / But sin hath broke the world's sweet peace, unstrung / The harmonious chords to which the angels sung" (065-002).

## Critical Reception

Dana was a transitional figure in American literary history — one of the first American writers to break from the neoclassical tradition of the eighteenth century and embrace the emotional intensity and natural imagery of Romanticism. While his poetry never achieved the popularity of Longfellow or Bryant, Dana is recognized by literary historians as an important precursor to the darker strains of American Romanticism. His role in founding the *North American Review* gave him an influential platform for shaping literary taste in the early republic, and his critical essays helped establish standards for American literary criticism in its formative period.
