# William Byrd

## Biography

William Byrd II (1674–1744) was a Virginia planter, surveyor, public official, and writer whose "History of the Dividing Line between Virginia and North Carolina" ranks among the most accomplished literary works of colonial America. Born into one of Virginia's wealthiest families — his father, William Byrd I, had built the family estate at Westover on the James River — Byrd was educated in England at the Felsted School and later studied law at the Middle Temple in London. His extended stays in England, where he moved in fashionable literary and political circles, gave him a cosmopolitan perspective rare among American colonials.

Byrd returned permanently to Virginia in 1705 upon his father's death, inheriting the Westover plantation and assuming the responsibilities of a major landowner and public figure. He served as a member of the Virginia House of Burgesses, as receiver-general of the colony, and as a commissioner appointed to survey the boundary line between Virginia and North Carolina in 1728 — an expedition that became the subject of his most famous work. Byrd was also a fellow of the Royal Society of London, to which he contributed scientific observations, and the founder of Richmond, Virginia, on land he owned at the falls of the James River.

Byrd embodied the ideal of the Virginia gentleman-planter: wealthy, politically influential, intellectually curious, and deeply engaged with both American and European cultures. His library at Westover, one of the largest private collections in the colonies, numbered over four thousand volumes.

## Literary Career

Byrd's literary reputation rests primarily on three related works: *The History of the Dividing Line betwixt Virginia and North Carolina* (written c. 1738, published 1841), its more candid variant *The Secret History of the Line* (published 1929), and *A Journey to the Land of Eden* (published 1841). All three grew out of his 1728 surveying expedition, but they are far more than dry reports of boundary measurements. Byrd transforms the mundane task of surveying into a richly detailed, wryly humorous narrative of colonial life, frontier encounter, and natural observation.

Byrd's prose style is characterized by wit, irony, and an Augustan polish that reflects his long immersion in English literary culture. He was a master of the comic aside, the self-deprecating observation, and the vivid physical description. The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" — "The Ginseng and Snakeroot Plants" — displays his gift for blending natural history with urbane humor:

> Though practice will soon make a man of tolerable vigor an able footman, yet, as a help to bear fatigue, I used to chew a root of ginseng as I walked along. This kept up my spirits, and made me trip away as nimbly in my half jack-boots as younger men could in their shoes. (004-004)

Byrd's description of ginseng oscillates between the factual and the playful. He notes its international prestige — "The Emperor of China sends ten thousand men every year on purpose to gather it" — before offering a mock-lament that "Providence has planted it very thin in every country. Nor, indeed, is mankind worthy of so great a blessing, since health and long life are commonly abused to ill purposes." The humor deepens as he catalogs the plant's virtues:

> Its virtues are, that it gives an uncommon warmth and vigor to the blood, and frisks the spirits beyond any other cordial. It cheers the heart even of a man that has a bad wife, and makes him look down with great composure on the crosses of the world. (004-004)

The aside about the bad wife is pure Byrd — a flash of worldly irony that transforms a botanical description into social commentary. He continues in the same vein, claiming ginseng "promotes insensible perspiration, dissolves all phlegmatic and viscous humors that are apt to obstruct the narrow channels of the nerves. It helps the memory, and would quicken even Helvetian dullness." The reference to "Helvetian dullness" — a playful jab at the supposed slowness of the Swiss — is another example of Byrd's cosmopolitan wit.

The selection also includes Byrd's account of rattlesnake root (star-grass), which he describes with similar verve, noting its supposed power to neutralize snake venom and its observable effect of "putting the spirits into a great hurry." Throughout, Byrd writes as a gentleman-amateur of science — curious, observant, but always ready to leaven his observations with a turn of phrase or a knowing wink at his reader.

## Critical Reception

Byrd's writings remained largely unpublished during his lifetime and for nearly a century after his death. When the "History of the Dividing Line" was finally published in 1841, it was recognized as a significant contribution to early American literature. In the twentieth century, the publication of the "Secret History" (1929) and Byrd's surviving diaries — which offer a startlingly candid record of his daily life, including his reading, his business dealings, and his intimate habits — transformed scholarly understanding of colonial Virginia's elite culture.

Modern critics have ranked Byrd alongside Jonathan Edwards and Benjamin Franklin as one of the three most accomplished prose writers of colonial America. His combination of intellectual sophistication, urbane wit, and vivid observation of the American scene makes him a unique figure — a colonial writer who was at once deeply Virginian and securely at home in the transatlantic culture of the Enlightenment.
