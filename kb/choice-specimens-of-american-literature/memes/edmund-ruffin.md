## Biography

Edmund Ruffin (1794–1865) was an American agricultural reformer, writer, and political activist.
Born in Prince George County, Virginia, he inherited a family plantation on the James River.
Ruffin's early experience with the declining productivity of Virginia's tobacco-worn soils led him
to a lifelong study of agricultural chemistry.

Ruffin's practical experiments with marl (calcareous earth) as a soil amendment revolutionized
agriculture in the Upper South. He founded and edited the *Farmers' Register* (1833–1842),
one of the most influential agricultural periodicals in the antebellum South. His scientific
methodology — combining chemical analysis with field experimentation — anticipated the
professionalization of American agricultural science.

In his later years, Ruffin became an increasingly radical advocate for Southern secession.
He gained notoriety for firing one of the first shots at Fort Sumter in April 1861, at the age of
sixty-seven. Following the Confederate surrender in 1865, Ruffin took his own life at his
Virginia estate, leaving a note declaring his "unmitigated hatred" of the Union.

## Literary Career

Ruffin's major literary contribution was *An Essay on Calcareous Manures* (1832), a detailed
scientific study that demonstrated how the application of marl could neutralize soil acidity
and restore fertility to exhausted lands. The work combined empirical observation with chemical
theory and was widely cited by agricultural reformers on both sides of the Atlantic.

As editor of the *Farmers' Register*, Ruffin shaped a generation of Southern agricultural thought.
His writing was characterized by a direct, analytical style that translated complex scientific
concepts for a practical farming audience. Later in life, his pen turned increasingly to
political polemics, producing secessionist pamphlets and a posthumously published diary of the
Civil War years.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Ruffin:

- **"Improvement of Acid Soils"** (033-009): An excerpt from *An Essay on Calcareous Manures*
  in which Ruffin diagnoses soil acidity as the hidden cause of declining Southern agriculture:

> "Nearly all the woodland now remaining in lower Virginia, and also much of the land which has
> long been arable, is rendered unproductive by acidity; and successive generations have toiled
> on such land, almost without remuneration, and without suspecting that their worst virgin land
> was then richer than their manured lots appeared to be." (033-009)

Ruffin goes on to argue that acidic soils, paradoxically, have been "saved from exhaustion" by
their very infertility, and that with proper treatment they can be made highly productive.

## Critical Reception

Ruffin's agricultural writings were widely respected in his time for their practical utility
and scientific rigor. His contributions to soil chemistry have been recognized by historians
of American science as pioneering. However, his legacy is deeply complicated by his role as a
leading fire-eater and his symbolic connection to Southern secession. Modern scholarship has
tended to separate his scientific achievements from his political extremism while acknowledging
the inseparability of the two in Ruffin's own mind.
