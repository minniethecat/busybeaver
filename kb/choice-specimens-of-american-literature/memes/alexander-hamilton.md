# Alexander Hamilton

## Biography

Alexander Hamilton (1757–1804) was an American statesman, political philosopher, and Founding Father who served as the first Secretary of the Treasury of the United States (1789–1795). Born out of wedlock on the island of Nevis in the British West Indies, Hamilton overcame a childhood of hardship and obscurity through exceptional intelligence and ambition. After arriving in New York in 1772, he attended King's College (now Columbia University), where he wrote influential pamphlets supporting the revolutionary cause.

During the American Revolution, Hamilton served as an artillery captain and later as General George Washington's aide-de-camp, a position in which his administrative and writing talents proved indispensable. After the war, he established a successful legal practice in New York, served in the Congress of the Confederation, and was a delegate to the Constitutional Convention of 1787. As the principal author of the Federalist Papers — writing fifty-one of the eighty-five essays — Hamilton articulated the most comprehensive defense of the proposed Constitution and the principles of federal government.

As Secretary of the Treasury, Hamilton established the financial foundations of the new republic: the funding of the national debt, the assumption of state debts, the creation of the Bank of the United States, and a system of tariffs and excise taxes. His vision of a strong federal government, a commercial economy, and an active role for the United States in international affairs shaped the Federalist Party and provoked the formation of the Democratic-Republican opposition led by Thomas Jefferson and James Madison. Hamilton's life was cut short in 1804 when he was mortally wounded in a duel with Aaron Burr, the sitting Vice President of the United States.

## Literary Career

Hamilton's literary legacy rests primarily on his contributions to the Federalist Papers (1787–1788) and his state papers as Secretary of the Treasury. His writing is distinguished by its logical rigor, rhetorical force, and ability to translate complex financial concepts into accessible argument. Unlike many of his contemporaries, whose prose drew on classical models and leisurely periods, Hamilton wrote with the urgency of a man building institutions against the clock.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) includes two representative selections from Hamilton's public writings. The first, drawn from his "Vindication of the Funding System," addresses the widespread misrepresentation of the national debt. Hamilton methodically dismantles the charge that his funding system had created new burdens for the American people:

> A person who, unacquainted with the fact, should learn the history of our debt from the declamations with which certain newspapers are perpetually charged, would be led to suppose that it is the mere creature of the present government, for the purpose of burthening the people with taxes, and producing an artificial and corrupt influence over them; he would, at least, take it for granted that it had been contracted in the pursuit of some wanton or vain project of ambition or glory; he would scarcely be able to conceive that every part of it was the relict of a war which had given independence, and preserved liberty to the country. (015-003)

Against these misrepresentations, Hamilton lays out the simple arithmetic of the case: "Nothing more was done by that system, than to incorporate these two species of debt into the mass, and to make for the whole, one general, comprehensive provision. There is therefore, no arithmetic, no logic, by which it can be shown that the funding system has augmented the aggregate debt of the country. The sum total is manifestly the same; though the parts which were before divided are now united" (015-004). This passage exemplifies Hamilton's characteristic method — reducing a politically charged controversy to a matter of accounting that no honest observer could dispute.

Yet Hamilton was no mere technician. When addressing the moral dimension of public credit, his prose takes on a sharper, more indignant edge:

> When we hear the epithets, "vile matter," "corrupt mass," bestowed upon the public debt, and the owners of it indiscriminately maligned as the harpies and vultures of the community, there is ground to suspect that those who hold the language, though they may not dare to avow it, contemplate a more summary process for getting rid of debts than that of paying them. Charity itself cannot avoid concluding from the language and conduct of some men, (and some of them of no inconsiderable importance,) that in their vocabularies creditor and enemy are synonymous terms. (015-006)

The second selection, from "The French Revolution," reveals Hamilton the diplomat and foreign policy realist. Writing during the early phases of the French Revolution, he offers a prescient warning against revolutionary excess:

> I have seen, with a mixture of pleasure and apprehension, the progress of events which have lately taken place in your country. As a friend to mankind and to liberty, I rejoice in the efforts which you are making to establish it, while I fear much for the final success of the attempts, for the fate of those I esteem who are engaged in them, and for the danger in case of success, of innovations greater than will consist with the real felicity of your nation. (015-007)

Hamilton's fear was that the French Revolution would be captured by "philosophic politicians, who appear in the moment to have great influence, and who, being mere speculatists, may aim at more refinement than suits either with human nature, or the composition of your nation" (015-007). This skepticism toward abstract political theory, grounded in a hard-won understanding of human nature, is a defining feature of Hamilton's thought.

The selection on the Jay Treaty (1795) demonstrates Hamilton's conception of political courage. Confronting opposition to the treaty, he insists that the consequences of repudiation cannot be evaded by a mere legislative vote:

> It is worse than weakness to say, that as to public faith, our vote has already settled the question. Another tribunal than our own is already erected; the public opinion, not merely of our own country, but of the enlightened world, will pronounce a judgment that we cannot resist, that we dare not even affect to despise. (015-008)

He concludes with a remarkable confession of the limits of reason in questions of national honor: "This, sir, is a cause that would be dishonored and betrayed if I contented myself with appealing only to the understanding. It is too cold, and its processes are too slow, for the occasion. I desire to thank God that since he has given me an intellect so fallible, he has impressed upon me an instinct that is sure. On a question of shame and honor, reasoning is sometimes useless, and worse. I feel the decision in my pulse; if it throws no light upon the brain, it kindles a fire at the heart" (015-008).

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Alexander Hamilton's writings appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Character of the Debt* (015-003–015-006) — From "Vindication of the Funding System." A defense of Hamilton's financial program against the charge that the funding of the national debt had increased the burden on American citizens. Hamilton demonstrates through rigorous argument that the funding system consolidated existing obligations rather than creating new ones, and exposes the moral dishonesty of those who sought to repudiate public debts through rhetorical vilification of creditors.

- *The French Revolution* (015-007–015-008) — From Hamilton's diplomatic correspondence and public writings on the French Revolution and the Jay Treaty. Hamilton offers a nuanced assessment of revolutionary France — applauding the aspiration toward liberty while warning against the dangers of speculative politics and the excesses of popular passion. The selection also includes his powerful defense of treaty obligations as matters of national honor transcending ordinary legislative calculation.

## Critical Reception

Alexander Hamilton's reputation has experienced dramatic fluctuations over the course of American history. During his lifetime and for decades afterward, he was a polarizing figure — revered by Federalists as the architect of American prosperity, reviled by Jeffersonians as an apostle of monarchy and concentrated wealth. In the twentieth century, progressive historians portrayed him as the champion of an aristocratic capitalism at odds with American democratic ideals, while conservatives and proponents of a strong central government celebrated him as the indispensable nation-builder.

The late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries have witnessed a sustained revival of interest in Hamilton, spurred in part by Ron Chernow's monumental biography (2004) and the cultural phenomenon of Lin-Manuel Miranda's musical "Hamilton" (2015). Scholarly attention has increasingly focused on Hamilton's role in creating the institutional infrastructure of American governance — the Treasury Department, the Coast Guard, the Bank of the United States — and on his prescient vision of a diversified commercial economy. Hamilton's writings, particularly the Federalist Papers and the Reports on Public Credit and Manufactures, are now recognized as foundational texts of American political thought, rivaling in importance the contributions of Madison and Jefferson.
