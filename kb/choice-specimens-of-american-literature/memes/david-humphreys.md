## Biography

David Humphreys (1752–1818) was an American poet, diplomat, and military officer during the Revolutionary War.
Born in Derby, Connecticut, he graduated from Yale College in 1771 and was among the group of Yale-educated poets
known as the Hartford Wits or Connecticut Wits, alongside [[john-trumbull]], [[joel-barlow]],
and [[lemuel-hopkins]].

At the outbreak of the Revolutionary War, Humphreys enlisted in the Continental Army and rose rapidly through
the ranks. He served as an aide-de-camp to General [[george-washington]] from 1780 to 1783, a position
that brought him into the inner circle of the American high command. Washington held Humphreys in high personal
regard and entrusted him with confidential missions. After the war, Humphreys served in various diplomatic posts,
including as minister to Portugal (1791–1796) and minister to Spain (1796–1801), where he negotiated treaties
relating to American trade in the Mediterranean.

## Literary Career

Humphreys's literary output includes patriotic poems, military memoirs, and agricultural treatises.
His major poetic works include *The Happiness of America* (1786), a poem celebrating the promise of
the young republic, and *A Poem on the Industry of the United States of America* (1794).
His war recollections, excerpted in this anthology, depict his experiences with Washington and other
Revolutionary commanders in heroic couplets.

As a member of the Hartford Wits, Humphreys contributed to collaborative satirical works including
*The Anarchiad* (1786–1787) and *The Echo*, which used verse to comment on the political controversies
of the early republic. He also wrote a biography of Major General [[nathaniel-greene]], published in 1819.
During his years as a diplomat, Humphreys introduced Merino sheep to the United States from Spain,
significantly influencing American wool production, and wrote extensively on agricultural improvement.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Humphreys:

- **"Recollections of the War"** (064-037): An excerpt from *The Happiness of America* in which
  Humphreys recalls his military service under Washington in heroic couplets:

> "I too, perhaps, should Heaven prolong my date,
> The oft-repeated tale shall oft relate;
> Shall tell the feelings in the first alarms,
> Of some bold enterprise the unequalled charms;
> Shall tell from whom I learnt the martial art,
> With what high chiefs I played my early part —
> With Parsons first —
> ...
> Death-daring Putnam — then immortal Greene —
> Then how great Washington my youth approved,
> In rank preferred, and as a parent loved.
> With him what hours on warlike plains I spent,
> Beneath the shadow of th' imperial tent;" (064-037)

## Critical Reception

Humphreys was well regarded by his contemporaries, with Washington himself commending his literary
abilities. His poetry, while reflective of eighteenth-century neoclassical conventions, provides
valuable firsthand testimony of the Revolutionary War experience. Modern scholarship has recognized
Humphreys as an important transitional figure in American letters, bridging the Revolutionary
generation's literary aspirations with the more fully developed national literature of the early
nineteenth century.
