# George B. Cheever

## Biography

George Barrell Cheever (1807–1890) was an American clergyman, abolitionist, and author. Born in Hallowell, Maine, he graduated from Bowdoin College in 1825 — a classmate of Henry Wadsworth Longfellow and Nathaniel Hawthorne — and from Andover Theological Seminary in 1830. He was ordained as a Congregational minister and served pastorates in Salem, Massachusetts, and later at the Church of the Puritans in New York City, where he remained for over two decades.

Cheever was an outspoken and often controversial figure. In Salem, he was publicly whipped (an unusual punishment for a minister) for libel after publishing a satirical temperance allegory that lampooned a local distiller. Far from silencing him, the episode made Cheever a national celebrity and a hero of the temperance movement. He later became a prominent voice in the abolitionist cause, arguing from the pulpit and in print that slavery was a sin that demanded immediate repentance, not gradual reform. His church in New York was a center of antislavery activism.

Cheever's career was shaped by the conviction that the minister's duty extended beyond the care of souls to the moral reformation of society. He saw no distinction between personal piety and social justice, and his sermons and essays consistently applied the language of biblical prophecy to the political crises of his day — slavery, intemperance, and the corrosive effects of wealth.

## Literary Career

Cheever was an extraordinarily prolific writer. His works include *God's Hand in America* (1841), a providential interpretation of American history; *The Guilt of Slavery and the Crime of Slaveholding* (1860), a biblical indictment of the institution; and numerous collections of sermons and lectures. He also edited an edition of John Bunyan's *Pilgrim's Progress* with extensive annotations, and published travel writings from Europe and the Holy Land.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn from Cheever's sermon "Lead Us Not into Temptation," which interprets the Lord's Prayer through rich metaphorical language. Cheever describes the prayerful conscience as a miner's safety lamp:

> A conscience quick and sensitive, under the presence of the indwelling Spirit, is like the safety-lamp of the miner, a ready witness and a mysterious guardian against the deathful damps, that unseen, but fatal, cluster around our darkling way. (010-003)

The sermon builds to an arresting image of the soul without God — a traveler passing beneath the waters of Niagara, guided only by Satan:

> You are like a presumptuous and unskilful traveller, passing under the arch of the waters of Niagara. The falling cataract thundering above you; a slippery, slimy rock beneath your gliding feet; the smoking, roaring abyss yawning beside you. (010-004)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Sin Distorts the Judgment* (010-003, 010-004, 010-006) — From a sermon on the Lord's Prayer petition "Lead Us Not into Temptation." Cheever argues that an unguarded conscience is like a miner without a safety lamp — defenseless against the unseen spiritual dangers that surround it. The sermon combines vivid natural imagery (Niagara Falls, Mont Blanc, the Red Sea crossing) with a searching moral psychology, insisting that the neglect of prayer is itself a form of tempting God.

## Critical Reception

Cheever was one of the most widely read American religious writers of the mid-nineteenth century. His sermons were collected and reprinted in both America and Britain, and his abolitionist writings placed him in the front rank of clerical antislavery activists alongside Theodore Parker and Henry Ward Beecher. Contemporaries admired the rhetorical force of his prose — what one reviewer called its "picturesque energy" — even when they disagreed with his positions.

Cheever's reputation faded after his death, as the causes for which he fought — temperance, abolition — were either won or transformed, and as the style of American preaching moved away from the elaborate literary sermon toward simpler, more conversational forms. He is now remembered primarily by historians of American religion and the antislavery movement, for whom his career exemplifies the fusion of evangelical piety and social reform that characterized the most dynamic currents of antebellum Protestantism.
