# John W. Francis

## Biography

John Wakefield Francis (1789–1861) was an American physician and writer born in New York City. He studied medicine at Columbia College and became one of the most respected medical practitioners in the city, serving as president of the New York Academy of Medicine. Beyond his medical career, Francis was an active participant in the city's literary and historical societies, delivering orations and publishing essays on a wide range of subjects. He was a member of the New-York Historical Society and contributed significantly to preserving and chronicling the city's early history.

## Literary Career

Francis's literary output spanned medicine, history, and social commentary. His most distinctive voice emerged in his historical addresses and reminiscences, in which he reflected on the astonishing growth of New York City from a small colonial port to a metropolis. He also wrote on education and the medical profession, displaying a humane and philosophical temperament. His style combined the precision of a scientist with the warmth of a civic booster proud of his native city's achievements.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology features two selections from Francis's writings:

- "Profession of the Schoolmaster" (026-004–026-007) — A tribute to the teaching profession, arguing that the schoolmaster's "obscure and inglorious" daily occupation requires the same noble spirit that "inspired the most illustrious benefactors of mankind." He urges citizens not to be "content with barren honor to buried merit" but to "elevate the station, to enlarge the usefulness, and to raise the character of the schoolmaster amongst us."
- "Public Changes" (026-009–026-010) — A panoramic reflection on the transformation of New York City in a single lifetime, from "the cheap and humble dwellings" of the colonial era to the "costly and magnificent edifices which now beautify the metropolis." He celebrates "the triumphs of a self-governed people, accomplished within the limited memory of an ordinary life."

## Critical Reception

Francis was esteemed by his contemporaries as a cultured physician who bridged the worlds of medicine, letters, and civic life. His historical reminiscences remain valuable as vivid testimony to New York City's rapid growth in the early nineteenth century. Modern readers may find his prose ornate, but his sense of wonder at the transformations he witnessed — "the progress of an era the most remarkable in the history of his species" — captures the spirit of the antebellum American metropolis.
