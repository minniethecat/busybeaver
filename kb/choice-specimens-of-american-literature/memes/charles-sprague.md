# Charles Sprague

## Biography

Charles Sprague (1791–1875) was an American poet, known to his contemporaries as the "Banker Poet of Boston." Born in Boston, Massachusetts, he spent his professional life as a cashier at the Globe Bank while pursuing poetry as an avocation. His formal education was limited, but he acquired a broad knowledge of English literature through self-directed reading. Sprague was a prominent figure in Boston literary circles and was regarded as one of the city's leading versifiers in the decades before the Civil War.

## Literary Career

Sprague's poetry was characterized by its polished occasional verse, dignified sentiment, and mastery of the heroic couplet. He first gained wide recognition with his "Shakespeare Ode," delivered at a public celebration in 1823, and his "Curiosity," a longer philosophical poem, was well received. Sprague was not a prolific poet — his complete works fill a modest volume — but his finished craftsmanship and moral seriousness earned him the respect of readers and fellow writers alike. He was among the first American poets to win an audience abroad, and his work appeared in British anthologies.

In "The Newspaper," drawn from his longer poem "Curiosity," Sprague offered a satirical panorama of the daily press in heroic couplets:

> Turn to the Press — its teeming sheets survey,
> Big with the wonders of each passing day;
> Births, deaths, and weddings, forgeries, fires, and wrecks,
> Harangues and hailstorms, brawls and broken necks. (066-018)

The poem surveys the newspaper's diverse readership: "Trade hardly deems the busy day begun / Till his keen eye along the page has run; / The blooming daughter throws her needle by, / And reads her schoolmate's marriage with a sigh" (066-020). Even the clergy are not immune: "The preacher, too, his Sunday theme lays down / To know what last new folly fills the town" (066-021, 066-022). Sprague concluded with a comprehensive verdict on the newspaper's indiscriminate appetite:

> Lively or sad, life's meanest, mightiest things,
> The fate of fighting cocks, or fighting kings —
> Nought comes amiss; we take the nauseous stuff,
> Verjuice or oil, a libel or a puff. (066-023)

## Selections in This Anthology

Sprague is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) by "The Newspaper," a passage drawn from his longer poem "Curiosity" (066-018 to 066-023). The selection — twenty-six lines of polished heroic couplets — offers a gently satirical survey of the newspaper's role in American life, from the sensational to the mundane. The passage exemplifies Sprague's facility with the neoclassical couplet form and his characteristic blend of wit and moral observation.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Sprague was highly regarded. Edgar Allan Poe praised his technical skill, and his poems were widely anthologized. Rufus Wilmot Griswold included him prominently in *The Poets and Poetry of America*. His reputation declined in the later nineteenth century as literary taste shifted away from neoclassical formalism, but he remains a notable figure in the history of early American poetry — a transitional writer who brought polished craftsmanship to a still-developing national literature.
