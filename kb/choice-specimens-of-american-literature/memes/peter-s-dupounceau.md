# Peter S. Duponceau

Peter Stephen Duponceau (1760–1844) was a French-American jurist, linguist, and philologist who became one of Philadelphia's most distinguished citizens. Arriving in America during the Revolutionary War as a secretary to Baron von Steuben, he settled in Philadelphia and built a career spanning law, scholarship, and public life.

Duponceau is best remembered for his pioneering studies of Native American languages and his contributions to American philology. He served as president of the American Philosophical Society and was a foundational figure in the study of linguistics in the United States.

His essay "Character of William Penn," excerpted in the anthology *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, exemplifies his moral and philosophical prose, celebrating Penn's unique achievement as a lawgiver who founded a commonwealth on principles of peace and universal love.

## Biography

Peter Stephen Duponceau was born Pierre-Étienne Du Ponceau on June 3, 1760, in Saint-Martin-de-Ré, France. He displayed an early aptitude for languages and, at age seventeen, came to America in 1777 as secretary to Baron Friedrich Wilhelm von Steuben, the Prussian officer who trained the Continental Army at Valley Forge.

After the war, Duponceau settled in Philadelphia, where he studied law and was admitted to the bar. He became a prominent lawyer and a respected figure in the city's intellectual life. His linguistic gifts — he was said to have mastered more than twenty languages — drew him increasingly toward philological research.

## Literary Career

Duponceau's most significant scholarly contribution was his work on Native American languages. In 1819, he published a groundbreaking report to the American Philosophical Society arguing that the grammatical structures of Native American languages shared a distinctive "polysynthetic" character, a concept that influenced linguistic theory for generations.

He also wrote extensively on legal topics, international law, and the history of Pennsylvania. His essay on William Penn, from which the anthology selection is drawn, reflects both his legal training and his admiration for Penn's moral vision. Duponceau served as president of the American Philosophical Society from 1827 until his death, and was a corresponding member of learned societies across Europe.

## Selections in the Anthology

The anthology *Choice Specimens of American Literature* includes an extended passage from Duponceau's character sketch of William Penn. The selection presents Penn as a lawgiver who surpassed the founders of classical commonwealths by founding his state on peace rather than military power.

[025-011]Duponceau writes:

> William Penn stands the first among the lawgivers whose names and deeds are recorded in history. Shall we compare him with Lycurgus, Solon, Romulus, those founders of military commonwealths, who organized their citizens in deadly array against the rest of their species, taught them to consider their fellow-men as barbarians, and themselves as alone worthy to rule over the earth?... But see William Penn, with weaponless hand, sitting down peaceably with his followers, in the midst of savage nations whose only occupation was shedding the blood of their fellow-men, disarming them by his justice, and teaching them, for the first time, to view a stranger without distrust. See them bury their tomahawks in his presence, so deep that man shall never be able to find them again. See them, under the shade of the thick groves of Coaquannock, extend the bright chain of friendship, and solemnly promise to preserve it as long as the sun and moon shall endure. See him then, with his companions, establishing his commonwealth on the sole basis of religion, morality, and universal love, and adopting, as the fundamental maxim of his government, the rule handed down to us from heaven, "Glory to God, and good will towards men."

This passage, with its rhetorical crescendo of parallel "See him" clauses, exemplifies the oratorical prose style of the early Republic and its fusion of classical allusion with Enlightenment ideals.

## Critical Reception

Duponceau's scholarship earned him international recognition during his lifetime. His 1819 report on Native American languages was translated into several European languages and influenced the work of Wilhelm von Humboldt and other comparative linguists. His legal writings were cited by the U.S. Supreme Court, and his *Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States* contributed to antebellum constitutional discourse.

The anthology's inclusion of Duponceau alongside figures like [[daniel-webster]] and [[john-marshall]] signals his standing in the nineteenth-century American intellectual pantheon.
