# Francis Wayland

## Biography

Francis Wayland (1796–1865) was an American Baptist minister, moral philosopher, and educator who served as the fourth president of Brown University from 1827 to 1855. Born in New York City, he was the son of Francis Wayland Sr., a Baptist minister of English descent. Wayland entered Union College in 1811, studied medicine briefly, and then turned to theology, being ordained a Baptist minister in 1821.

Wayland served as pastor of the First Baptist Church in Boston from 1821 to 1826, where his preaching attracted widespread attention. In 1827, at the age of thirty-one, he was appointed president of Brown University. During his twenty-eight-year tenure, he transformed Brown from a small college into a significant institution, introducing elective courses, expanding the library, and advocating for a broader, more practical curriculum. His reforms were controversial but influential; his 1850 report to the Brown Corporation, advocating for major curricular revisions, became a landmark document in the history of American higher education.

Wayland was also an active participant in national debates over temperance, antislavery, and prison reform. Though a moderate on the slavery question, he opposed the annexation of Texas and argued, in his 1838 work *The Limitations of Human Responsibility*, that individuals and governments have limited moral obligations — a doctrine that blended evangelical piety with classical liberal thought.

## Literary Career

Wayland's most influential work was *The Elements of Moral Science* (1835), a systematic treatise on ethics that served as the standard college textbook on moral philosophy in the United States for more than half a century. The book sold over one hundred thousand copies and was translated into several languages. It combined natural-law reasoning with evangelical Protestant conviction, arguing that moral duties could be derived from both revelation and the structure of human nature.

His other major publications include *The Elements of Political Economy* (1837), which sold nearly as widely as his moral-science text and was praised for its clarity of argument; *The Limitations of Human Responsibility* (1838); and *The Life and Labors of the Rev. Adoniram Judson* (1853), a biography of the pioneer American Baptist missionary to Burma.

The single selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn from *The Limitations of Human Responsibility* and demonstrates Wayland's characteristic mode of argument. He begins with a broad observation about human nature — that appeals to moral sentiment have always been the most powerful force in human affairs — and proceeds, through a series of carefully reasoned steps, to show that "the consciousness of right is a source of power which belongs specially to the oppressed":

> It is a common remark, that, whenever it has been thought necessary to arouse the mind of man to enterprises of great pith and moment, the appeal has always been made to his moral sentiments. Hence, among the most ancient nations, it was the invariable custom to accompany the declaration of war with religious ceremonies. (033-011)

The passage builds through historical illustration to a meditation on martyrdom as the ultimate expression of the power of conscience:

> The highest glory of human nature is to love right better than life, and to obey the dictates of conscience at every conceivable hazard. Even falsehood, when sealed with blood, acquires not unfrequently, for a time, an irrepressible power. Truth, when uttered from the stake, or on the scaffold, becomes absolutely irresistible. (033-011)

> We admire Plato, surrounded by listening princes, and vieing with them in oriental magnificence; but we venerate Socrates in his dungeon, patiently suffering death for holding forth the truth; and the dictates of our own bosoms spontaneously assign to him the highest place among the uninspired teachers of wisdom. (033-011)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Superiority of the Moral Sentiments* (033-011) — From *The Limitations of Human Responsibility* (1838). A sustained argument that moral conviction — the sense of right — has been, across all of human history, the most powerful force for mobilizing human action. Wayland contrasts the power of conscience with that of physical force, arguing that the former "is efficient in proportion to the purity of the moral principle of a people."

## Critical Reception

Wayland's contemporaries regarded him as the leading moral philosopher of antebellum America. His textbooks dominated American college curricula from the 1830s through the 1880s, and several generations of American college students — including many who later became prominent in law, politics, and the clergy — were shaped by his ethical framework. The *North American Review* praised his "clear and manly style," and his influence extended abroad: *The Elements of Moral Science* was widely used in British dissenting academies and missionary colleges.

Later scholarly assessments have been more measured. Twentieth-century historians of philosophy have tended to see Wayland as an able synthesizer rather than an original thinker — a figure who transmitted the Scottish Common Sense tradition and Lockean liberalism to an American audience rather than breaking new philosophical ground. His political economy, with its unqualified endorsement of free trade and laissez-faire, has been criticized as doctrinaire. Nevertheless, recent scholarship on American higher education has restored Wayland's reputation as a significant institutional innovator whose curricular reforms at Brown anticipated elements of the modern university system.
