# Ralph Waldo Emerson

Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882) was the central figure of the [[nature-in-literature|Transcendentalist]] movement and the most influential American essayist of the nineteenth century.

From his home in Concord, Massachusetts, Emerson articulated a philosophy of [[nature-in-literature|nature]], [[self-reliance]], and spiritual independence.

His ideas shaped writers from [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] to [[walt-whitman|Whitman]]. His 1837 address "The American Scholar" was called America's "intellectual Declaration of Independence" by [[oliver-wendell-holmes]].

## Biography

Emerson was born on May 25, 1803, in Boston, Massachusetts.

His father, William Emerson, was a Unitarian minister who died when Ralph was eight. His mother managed to send him to Boston Latin School and then Harvard College, where he graduated in 1821.

After Harvard, Emerson taught school before entering Harvard Divinity School. He was ordained as a Unitarian minister in 1829, but doctrinal doubts soon troubled him. He resigned his pulpit in 1832, unable to administer the Lord's Supper in good conscience.

On a tour of Europe in 1832–33, Emerson met Carlyle, Wordsworth, and Coleridge. These encounters, combined with his reading in German idealism, crystallized his emerging philosophy.

He returned to America and settled in Concord, where he became the nucleus of a remarkable intellectual circle. His neighbors included [[henry-david-thoreau]], [[nathaniel-hawthorne]], and [[margaret-fuller]].

Emerson supported his family through lecturing, delivering over 1,500 lectures across the country. His essays were published in two series (1841, 1844). He died in Concord on April 27, 1882.

## Philosophy and Literary Style

Emerson's philosophy centered on individual intuition over tradition and institutional authority:

| Tenet | Key Work | Core Idea |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Self-Reliance | "Self-Reliance" (1841) | Trust your inner voice; society conspires against individuality |
| The Oversoul | "The Over-Soul" (1841) | A universal spirit unites all individual souls with nature |
| Compensation | "Compensation" (1841) | Moral law operates with the precision of natural law |
| Nature as Revelation | *Nature* (1836) | The natural world is a manifestation of the divine |
| The Poet-Prophet | "The Poet" (1844) | The true poet articulates hidden unity beneath surfaces |

His prose style was aphoristic and organic rather than systematic. Emerson composed in sentences that could stand alone. This quality made him quotable; his critics saw a lack of logical rigor.

## Major Works

Emerson's literary production spanned five decades:

- **"Nature" (1836)** — The foundational text of Transcendentalism.

Emerson argued that nature is the visible manifestation of spirit, accessible without priestly mediation.

- **"The American Scholar" (1837)** — Phi Beta Kappa address at Harvard.

A call for American intellectual independence from European tradition.

- **"The Divinity School Address" (1838)** — A controversial lecture.

Emerson was not invited back to Harvard for thirty years after this address.

- **Essays: First Series (1841)** — Including "Self-Reliance," "Compensation," and "The Over-Soul."

- **Essays: Second Series (1844)** — Including "The Poet," "Experience," and "Politics."

- **Poems (1847)** and **May-Day (1867)** — His verse included the "Concord Hymn."

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Ralph Waldo Emerson appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

**From "Essays," Second Series — "Influence of Nature"**

> [041-012] There are days which occur in this climate, at almost any season of the year, wherein the world reaches its perfection; when the air, the heavenly bodies, and the earth, make a harmony, as if Nature would indulge her offspring; when, in these bleak upper sides of the planet, nothing is to desire that we have heard of the happiest latitudes...

> To have lived through all its sunny hours seems longevity enough. The solitary places do not seem quite lonely. At the gates of the forest, the surprised man of the world is forced to leave his city estimates of great and small, wise and foolish. The knapsack of custom falls off his back with the first step he makes into these precincts.

**"Hymn Sung at the Completion of the Concord Monument" (1837)**

> [069-012] By the rude bridge that arched the flood, / Their flag to April's breeze unfurled, / Here once the embattled farmers stood, / And fired the shot heard round the world.

> [069-015] Spirit, that made those heroes dare / To die, or leave their children free, / Bid Time and Nature gently spare / The shaft we raise to them and thee.

These selections show Emerson at the intersection of philosophical prose and public poetry.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

Emerson's reputation in American letters is unparalleled for an essayist. Matthew Arnold judged his essays "the most important work done in prose" in nineteenth-century English.

In American literary history, Emerson stands as the founding figure of Transcendentalism. [[henry-david-thoreau]] was his most direct disciple. Emerson owned the land at Walden Pond where Thoreau built his cabin. [[walt-whitman]] declared, "I was simmering; Emerson brought me to a boil."

[[nathaniel-hawthorne]], though temperamentally opposed to Emerson's optimism, lived in Concord as his neighbor. He wrote *The Blithedale Romance* partly in response to Transcendentalist experiments like Brook Farm.

[[emily-dickinson]] read Emerson carefully. His call for distinctly American poetry echoes through her compressed, visionary verse. [[robert-frost]] later called Emerson his favorite American poet.

Twentieth-century critics complicated Emerson's legacy. Some saw in his individualism a proto-capitalist cheerleader. Others charged that his optimism ignored evil and tragedy. "Experience" (1844), written after his young son's death, answers this with hard-won wisdom about grief and limitation.
