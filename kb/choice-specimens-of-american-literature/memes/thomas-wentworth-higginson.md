Thomas Wentworth Higginson (1823–1911) was an American Unitarian minister, abolitionist, soldier, and man of letters whose career spanned the major reform movements and literary developments of the nineteenth century.

He was born in Cambridge, Massachusetts, into a family descended from the earliest Puritan settlers of New England. He commanded the first federally authorized African-American regiment during the Civil War and later became one of the most prolific essayists of the Gilded Age, publishing regularly in [[the-atlantic-monthly]]. He is best remembered today as the correspondent and literary mentor of [[emily-dickinson]], whose genius he recognized and whose posthumous publications he helped bring to press.

## Biography

Thomas Wentworth Higginson was born on December 22, 1823, in Cambridge, Massachusetts. His father, Stephen Higginson, was a Boston merchant and Harvard steward who died when Thomas was ten, leaving the family in straitened circumstances. His mother, Louisa Storrow Higginson, ensured he received a rigorous education. He entered Harvard College at thirteen and graduated in 1841, second in his class. He remained at Harvard for graduate study at the Divinity School, receiving his degree in 1847, and was ordained as a Unitarian minister that same year.

Higginson's pastoral career was shaped from the beginning by his fierce abolitionist convictions. He served congregations in Newburyport, Massachusetts, and later in Worcester, but his pulpit proved too radical for his parishioners — he preached against slavery with such vehemence that his Newburyport congregation dismissed him in 1850. Undeterred, he threw himself into the abolitionist movement. He was a member of the Boston Vigilance Committee, which aided fugitive slaves; he helped break down the door of the Boston courthouse in the failed attempt to rescue Anthony Burns, a fugitive slave, in 1854; and he was one of the "Secret Six" who helped fund [[john-brown]]'s raid on Harpers Ferry in 1859.

When the [[american-civil-war]] began, Higginson applied his abolitionist principles to military service. In November 1862, he was commissioned colonel of the 1st South Carolina Volunteers, the first federally authorized regiment composed of formerly enslaved African Americans. His memoir of this command, *Army Life in a Black Regiment* (1870), remains one of the most important first-person narratives of the Civil War and a landmark in African American military history. After the war, he settled in Newport, Rhode Island, and later Cambridge, devoting himself to literature, reform, and public lecturing. He died on May 9, 1911, at the age of eighty-seven.

## Literary Career

Higginson's literary output was prodigious and varied. Over a career spanning more than six decades, he published essays, criticism, biography, history, poetry, and a novel. He was a regular contributor to *The Atlantic Monthly* from its founding in 1857, writing on subjects ranging from nature and outdoor life to literary criticism and feminist reform. His essay collections — including *Atlantic Essays* (1871), *Outdoor Papers* (1863), and *Oldport Days* (1873) — showcase a prose style characterized by elegance, clarity, and a cosmopolitan breadth of reference. Higginson was deeply influenced by European literature, particularly French prose, which he admired for its discipline and grace — qualities he sought to cultivate in American letters.

As a literary critic, Higginson was generous but discerning. He championed the work of young American writers and was an early advocate for a distinctly national literature, yet he measured American achievements against the highest international standards. His critical essays on figures such as [[ralph-waldo-emerson]], [[henry-david-thoreau]], and [[walt-whitman]] helped shape the canon of American literature during its formative period. He also wrote a substantial biography of [[margaret-fuller-ossoli]] and a life of [[henry-wadsworth-longfellow]] for the "American Men of Letters" series. His *Cheerful Yesterdays* (1898) and *The Contemporaries* (1899) provide valuable retrospective portraits of the literary New England he knew firsthand.

Higginson's most enduring literary relationship, however, was with [[emily-dickinson]]. In April 1862, Dickinson sent him four poems with a letter asking whether her verse "breathed." Higginson responded with encouragement and criticism, beginning a correspondence that lasted until Dickinson's death in 1886. He visited her twice at the family homestead in Amherst, and after her death he co-edited the first two volumes of her poems (1890, 1891), introducing Dickinson's work to a national audience. Though his editorial interventions — regularizing her punctuation and smoothing unconventional syntax — have been criticized by later scholars, his role in recognizing and preserving Dickinson's poetry was indispensable.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selection from Thomas Wentworth Higginson appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin, under the title "Elegance of French Style" (No. 241). The passage is drawn from his collection *Atlantic Essays* (1871) and appears in [[section-051|Section 51]] of the anthology.

### "Elegance of French Style"

In this essay, Higginson argues that French literature stands alone among modern national traditions in treating writing as a deliberate art. He contrasts the disciplined grace of French prose — cultivated and refined by the [[french-academy]] — with what he regards as the excesses of English literary culture: the "brutality of [[thomas-carlyle]]" and the "shrill screams of [[john-ruskin]]." Higginson finds in French prose a "habitual grace and perfection, a clearness and directness, a light and pliable strength, and a fine shading of expression, such as no other tongue can even define."

Higginson extends this judgment to the French novel, which he praises for its "quiet power" and fidelity to "the level of real human life." He argues that French novelists, relying on "perfection of style" and "the most subtle analysis of human character," produce work of enduring seriousness — however painful in its honesty — rather than resorting to the sensational plot machinery that drives so much English fiction.

> In France alone among living nations is literature habitually pursued as an art; and in consequence of this, despite the seeds of decay which imperialism sowed, French prose-writing has no rival in contemporary literature. We cannot fully recognize this fact through translations, because only the most sensational French books appear to be translated. But as French painters and actors now habitually surpass all others even in what are claimed as the English qualities, — simplicity and truth, — so do French prose-writers excel. To be set against the brutality of Carlyle and the shrill screams of Ruskin, there is to be seen across the Channel the extraordinary fact of an actual organization of good writers, the French Academy, whose influence all nations feel.
> (050-010)

> In the average type of French novels, one feels the superiority to the English in quiet power, in the absence of the sensational and exaggerated, and in keeping close to the level of real human life. They rely for success upon perfection of style, and the most subtle analysis of human character; and therefore they are often painful, — just as Thackeray is painful, — because they look at artificial society, and paint what they see. Thus they dwell often on unhappy marriages, because such things grow naturally from the false social system in France. On the other hand, in France there is very little house-breaking, and bigamy is almost impossible, so that we hear delightfully little about them: whereas, if you subtract these from the current English novels, what is there left?
> (050-011)

Higginson's thesis — that French literary culture, through institutions like the Academy and the *Revue des Deux Mondes*, imposed standards of craft that elevated prose to the level of a fine art — reflects his own aspirations for American literature. He wanted American writers to develop a comparable discipline, a national tradition of literary excellence that would earn the respect of the world.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Thomas Wentworth Higginson was widely respected as a man of letters. His essays in *The Atlantic* reached a broad readership, and his literary opinions carried weight in the shaping of American taste. Contemporaries valued his combination of moral seriousness and stylistic polish. His memoir *Army Life in a Black Regiment* was praised for its vivid and sympathetic portrait of the soldiers under his command and has been recognized as an essential document of the Civil War.

Higginson's posthumous reputation has been somewhat eclipsed by the writers he championed. His name now survives most prominently in association with [[emily-dickinson]], whose letters to him and whose editorial fate at his hands have been the subject of extensive scholarly discussion. Feminist critics have also noted his consistent support for women's rights and women writers — he was a member of the American Woman Suffrage Association and published *Common Sense About Women* (1881). While he is no longer read as widely as the major figures of the American Renaissance, Higginson remains a significant figure in the cultural history of nineteenth-century America: a bridge between the reformist energies of the antebellum era and the literary professionalism of the Gilded Age.
