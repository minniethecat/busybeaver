# Henry Timrod

## Biography

Henry Timrod (1829–1867) was an American poet born in Charleston, South Carolina. The son of a bookbinder of German descent, Timrod studied at the University of Georgia but was unable to complete his degree due to financial constraints. He returned to Charleston, where he worked as a private tutor and began publishing poetry in regional periodicals, including the *Southern Literary Messenger* (002-028).

Timrod married Kate Goodwin in 1864, but the Civil War brought severe hardship. Charleston was under Union siege, and the privations of war exacerbated his lifelong struggle with tuberculosis. He died at the age of thirty-eight in Columbia, South Carolina, just two years after the war's end. His posthumous reputation grew steadily, and he is now recognized as one of the most accomplished Southern poets of the nineteenth century.

## Literary Career

Timrod's poetry is marked by lush natural imagery, classical allusion, and an elegiac tone. His early work celebrated the landscapes of the South Carolina Lowcountry; his later poems, written during and after the Civil War, turned toward themes of loss, memory, and reconciliation. His only volume published during his lifetime, *Poems* (1860), was well received but did not bring financial success.

During the war, Timrod wrote patriotic verse that earned him the unofficial title "Poet Laureate of the Confederacy." Poems such as "Ethnogenesis" and "The Cotton Boll" expressed Southern identity, though his finest work transcends sectional politics. His post-war poems — including "The Unknown Dead" (076-055) and "Ode Sung on the Occasion of Decorating the Graves of the Confederate Dead" — achieve a universal pathos that speaks to all who mourn the nameless fallen.

> The rain is plashing on my sill,
> But all the winds of Heaven are still;
> And so it falls with that dull sound
> Which thrills us in the church-yard ground,
> When the first spadeful drops like lead
> Upon the coffin of the dead. (076-056)

## Selections in This Anthology

- **"The Unknown Dead"** (076-055–076-057): A meditation on the nameless graves of soldiers, written in Timrod's characteristic elegiac mode. The poem uses the image of rain on a windowsill to evoke the sound of earth falling on coffins, drawing a parallel between the churchyard and the battlefield (076-056). Timrod himself noted that the poet "attained high repute, both in prose and verse" (076-055), though his own fame was largely posthumous.

## Critical Reception

Timrod's reputation declined in the decades immediately after his death, as Southern literature fell out of fashion in the postwar literary establishment. A revival began in the early twentieth century, led by scholars who recognized the craftsmanship of his verse. Contemporary critics praise his technical skill — particularly his mastery of the sonnet form — and the restrained emotion of his war poetry, which avoids the bombast common to patriotic verse of the period.
