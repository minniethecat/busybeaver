# John Greenleaf Whittier

## Biography

John Greenleaf Whittier (1807–1892) was an American poet, essayist, and abolitionist. Born on a farm in Haverhill, Massachusetts, to a Quaker family, Whittier received limited formal education but was an avid reader. His first published poem appeared in 1826 in the *Newburyport Free Press*, edited by William Lloyd Garrison, who became a lifelong friend and mentor.

Whittier's Quaker faith deeply informed his moral convictions. He became a committed abolitionist in the 1830s, serving as a delegate to the first meeting of the American Anti-Slavery Society and later editing abolitionist newspapers. His anti-slavery poems and pamphlets brought him both national recognition and considerable controversy. In his later years, after the Civil War, Whittier turned increasingly toward nostalgic, pastoral themes rooted in New England rural life, becoming one of the most beloved American poets of the nineteenth century.

## Literary Career

Whittier is counted among the Fireside Poets — a group of New England writers whose work was widely read in American households and schools, alongside Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Oliver Wendell Holmes, James Russell Lowell, and William Cullen Bryant. His poetry is characterized by simple, direct language, deep moral conviction, and vivid depictions of the New England landscape and its people.

His major works include *Snow-Bound* (1866), a long narrative poem depicting a rural family snowed in during a blizzard, which became an immediate bestseller and cemented his reputation; *Legends of New England* (1831), his first published book of poetry and prose sketches; and the anti-slavery collection *Voices of Freedom* (1846). His narrative poems frequently drew upon local legends, historical events, and the landscape of the Merrimack Valley.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from John G. Whittier's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *What the Voice Said* (070-001–070-003) — A meditation on faith and mortality, reflecting Whittier's Quaker spirituality
- *Inca-Pah-Cho's Haunted Water* (070-004–070-006) — A narrative poem set in the American wilderness, drawing upon Native American legend
- *The Maiden's Warning* (070-007–070-008) — A lyrical piece blending romantic sentiment with the supernatural atmosphere of the Southern landscape

## Critical Reception

Whittier was widely celebrated during his lifetime. *Snow-Bound* sold over 20,000 copies within months of publication, making him financially secure for the first time. His contemporaries praised his sincerity, moral clarity, and ability to capture the spirit of New England. Later critics have sometimes faulted his work for sentimentality, but his place as a foundational voice in American poetry — particularly in the tradition of social justice literature — remains secure.
