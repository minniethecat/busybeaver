# John M. Harney

## Biography

John Milton Harney (1789–1825) was an American poet and journalist. Born in Delaware, he later moved westward, becoming one of the earliest literary figures to establish himself in the Ohio Valley.

Harney studied medicine and practiced briefly as a physician, but his temperament inclined him toward literary pursuits. He settled in Kentucky, where he edited a newspaper and contributed poems to periodicals. His life was cut short at thirty-six.

## Literary Career

Harney's best-known work is "Crystallina: a Fairy Tale," a narrative poem in the Gothic-Romantic vein. The poem employs dark imagery of storm, witchcraft, and nocturnal dread characteristic of the era's Gothic literary fashion. Harney wrote in an ornate, ballad-like style with short stanzas and insistent rhythms.

The *Manual of American Literature* notes him as "one of the earliest poets of the West, but a native of Delaware" — a figure who bridged the Eastern literary tradition with the emerging culture of the trans-Appalachian frontier.

## Selections in This Anthology

Harney's contribution to "Choice Specimens" is an excerpt from "Crystallina: a Fairy Tale" (333), a poem rich in Gothic atmosphere:

> On the stormy heath a ring they form;
> They place therein the fearful maid,
> And round her dance in the howling storm.
> The winds beat hard on her lovely head:
> But she clasped her hands, and nothing said.
> O, 'twas, I ween, a ghastly sight
> To see their uncouth revelry.
> The lightning was the taper bright,
> The thunder was the melody,
> To which they danced with horrid glee.
> (066-010)

## Critical Reception

Harney's reputation remained modest, tied to his association with frontier literary culture. His poetry represents an early instance of the Gothic-Romantic mode in American letters, predating the more celebrated work of Edgar Allan Poe. Literary historians of the Ohio Valley have recognized Harney as a representative figure of early Western literary ambition.
