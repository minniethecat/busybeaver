# Henry Clay

## Biography

Henry Clay (1777–1852) was an American statesman, orator, and lawyer who represented Kentucky in both the United States Senate and House of Representatives. Born in Hanover County, Virginia, Clay studied law under George Wythe and was admitted to the Virginia bar in 1797 before relocating to Lexington, Kentucky, where he established a successful legal practice and entered politics. He served as Speaker of the House of Representatives (1811–1820, 1823–1825) and as Secretary of State under John Quincy Adams (1825–1829).

Clay was the principal architect of the "American System" — a program of protective tariffs, a national bank, and internal improvements designed to foster American economic development. Known as the "Great Compromiser," he played a decisive role in brokering the Missouri Compromise (1820) and the Compromise of 1850, measures that temporarily eased sectional tensions over slavery. Clay ran for president three times (1824, 1832, and 1844) but never attained the office, famously declaring, "I would rather be right than be president."

## Literary Career

Clay was one of the preeminent orators of the early republic, and his speeches were widely circulated in print, forming an important body of antebellum political literature. His oratory blended logical argument with emotional appeal, drawing on classical models and biblical cadences. The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes a passage from Clay's Senate speech of March 24, 1818, on the emancipation of the South American states from Spanish rule (017-001).

In this address, Clay articulated a vision of American foreign policy rooted in republican solidarity. He contrasted the American Revolution — directed "mainly against the mere theory of tyranny" — with the sufferings of Spanish America, which had been "doomed to the practical effects of an odious tyranny" (017-002). Clay declared: "I am no propagandist. I would not seek to force upon other nations our principles and our liberty if they did not want them. I would not disturb the repose even of a detestable despotism. But if an abused and oppressed people will their freedom; if they seek to establish it; if, in truth, they have established it, — we have a right, as a sovereign power, to recognize that fact" (017-003). This principle of recognizing de facto independent governments became a cornerstone of American diplomatic practice in the Western Hemisphere.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Henry Clay's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Emancipation of the South American States* (017-001–017-006) — From a speech in the United States Senate, March 24, 1818, in support of recognizing the newly independent republics of Spanish America. Clay argues that the American Revolution was justified against mere "theory of tyranny," while the Spanish American colonies had suffered centuries of actual oppression, making their cause for independence even more compelling.

## Critical Reception

Clay is remembered less as a systematic political thinker than as a master of legislative persuasion and a symbol of the Union. His speeches, once memorized by schoolchildren, have faded from the literary canon, but historians continue to study Clay as a pivotal figure whose compromises delayed — though could not ultimately prevent — the Civil War. Carl Schurz, writing in the late nineteenth century, praised Clay as a statesman whose "whole political life was devoted to the service of his country" and whose "name will always be held in grateful remembrance." Contemporary assessments continue to debate whether Clay's compromises were acts of statesmanship or moral evasion, but his influence on the development of American political institutions is beyond dispute.
