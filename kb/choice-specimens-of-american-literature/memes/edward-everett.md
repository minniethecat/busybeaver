# Edward Everett

## Biography

Edward Everett (1794–1865) was an American statesman, orator, and educator, widely regarded as the most accomplished public speaker of his generation. Born in Dorchester, Massachusetts, Everett graduated from Harvard College at the age of seventeen with the highest honors. After studying theology, he served as minister of the Brattle Street Church in Boston before being appointed, at twenty, as Harvard's first Eliot Professor of Greek Literature.

To prepare for his professorship, Everett spent four years in Europe, studying at the University of Göttingen — becoming one of the first Americans to earn a German doctorate — and traveling widely through England, France, and Italy. His exposure to European scholarship and culture gave him a cosmopolitan breadth unusual among American intellectuals of his time.

Everett's public career was extraordinarily varied. He served five terms in the U.S. House of Representatives, four years as Governor of Massachusetts, and four years as U.S. Minister to Great Britain. In 1846, he returned to Harvard as its fifteenth president. He later served as Secretary of State under President Millard Fillmore and, in 1860, was the vice-presidential nominee of the Constitutional Union Party. Throughout his career, Everett was in constant demand as an orator. His most famous address, delivered at the dedication of the Gettysburg National Cemetery in November 1863, was, at two hours, the main speech of the day — though it was eclipsed in posterity by the two-minute address that followed, delivered by Abraham Lincoln.

## Literary Career

Everett's literary reputation rests on his orations, which combine classical erudition with a cultivated, period-conscious prose style. His sentences are balanced, his diction elevated without being obscure, and his range of reference broad — extending from ancient Greek and Roman sources through modern European literature to the natural sciences. He was the model of what the nineteenth century called "eloquence," and his speeches were read as literature no less than heard as performance.

The two selections in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" are drawn from Everett's *Orations and Speeches* and demonstrate his gift for investing scientific and natural description with moral and spiritual resonance. The first, "Astronomy, For All Time," meditates on the changelessness of the heavens against the transience of human affairs:

> There is much by day to engage the attention of the observatory; the sun, his apparent motions, his dimensions, the spots on his disk, a solar eclipse, a transit of the interior planets, the mysteries of the spectrum — all phenomena of vast importance and interest. But night is the astronomer's accepted time: he goes to his delightful labors when the busy world goes to its rest. (040-004)

The passage builds to a declaration of cosmic permanence that serves, implicitly, as a rebuke to human pride:

> All has changed on earth; but the glorious heavens remain unchanged. The plough has passed over the remains of mighty cities, the homes of powerful nations are desolate, the languages they spoke are forgotten; but the stars that shone for them are shining for us; the same eclipses run their steady cycle; the same equinoxes call out the flowers of spring, and send the husbandman to the harvest. (040-004)

The second selection, "Description of the Sunrise," is a set-piece of Romantic nature description — Everett's account of a journey from Providence to Boston in the predawn darkness:

> Much, however, as we are indebted to our observatories for elevating our conceptions of the heavenly bodies, they present even to the unaided sight, scenes of glory which, words are too feeble to describe. I had occasion, a few weeks since, to take the early train from Providence to Boston; and for this purpose rose at two o'clock in the morning. Everything around was wrapt in darkness and hushed in silence, broken only by what seemed at that hour the unearthly clank and rush of the train. It was a mild, serene, midsummer's night, — the sky was without a cloud, — the winds were whist. (040-005)

As the train proceeds, the darkness gives way to the gradual approach of dawn in a passage of sustained visual beauty:

> Steadily the wondrous transfiguration went on. Hands of angels, hidden from mortal eyes, shifted the scenery of the heavens; the glories of night dissolved into the glories of the dawn. The blue sky now turned more softly gray; the great watch-stars shut up their holy eyes; the east began to kindle. Faint streaks of purple soon blushed along the sky; the whole celestial concave was filled with the inflowing tides of the morning light. (040-006)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Astronomy, For All Time* (040-004) — From *Orations and Speeches*. A meditation on the permanence of the heavens, contrasting the unchanging stars with the transience of human civilizations. Everett deploys his characteristic blend of scientific literacy and elevated prose to invest astronomy with moral significance.

- *Description of the Sunrise* (040-005–040-007) — From *Orations and Speeches*. A celebrated description of the passage from night to dawn, observed from an early-morning train traveling from Providence to Boston. The selection exemplifies the Romantic sensibility that Everett brought to American oratory, combining precise observation with a reverence for natural beauty.

## Critical Reception

Everett's contemporaries held him in the highest esteem. Edward Everett Hale called him "the most finished orator of his time," and Ralph Waldo Emerson, no uncritical admirer of the Harvard establishment, praised his "radiant beauty of person" and the "perfect elegance" of his delivery. His Gettysburg oration was received with widespread acclaim — though Lincoln's brief remarks have proven more enduring.

Later assessments have been less generous. Twentieth-century critics tended to see Everett as the embodiment of a florid oratorical tradition that the modern age had rendered obsolete. The comparison with Lincoln at Gettysburg — the polished orator versus the plain-spoken statesman — has become a staple of American cultural commentary. More recently, however, scholars have begun to recover Everett as a significant figure in his own right: a proponent of liberal education, a moderate antislavery voice, and an orator whose polished surface concealed genuine intellectual substance.
