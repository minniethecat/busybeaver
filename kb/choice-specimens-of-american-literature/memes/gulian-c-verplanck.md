## Biography

Gulian Crommelin Verplanck (1786–1870) was an American lawyer, politician, and man of letters. Born in New York City to a prominent Dutch-American family, he was educated at Columbia College, graduating in 1801. He studied law and was admitted to the bar, but his interests extended far beyond the legal profession to literature, education, and public affairs.

Verplanck served in the New York State Assembly and later in the United States House of Representatives (1825–1833), where he was a member of the Ways and Means Committee. He was also a regent of the University of the State of New York and a leading figure in the cultural and educational life of antebellum New York.

## Literary Career

Verplanck was one of the most prominent literary figures of his era in New York. He contributed to the *North American Review* and the *New York Review*, and was a central figure in the Knickerbocker Group — the circle of New York writers that included Washington Irving, James Fenimore Cooper, and William Cullen Bryant.

His most significant literary work, *Literary and Historical Discourses*, collected his essays and addresses on a wide range of subjects, from education and literary criticism to historical commemoration. He also published *Essays on the Nature and Uses of the Various Evidences of Revealed Religion* (1824) and a satirical political pamphlet, *The State Triumvirate* (1819).

## Selections in the Corpus

Verplanck appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* through an excerpt from his *Literary and Historical Discourses*, titled "The Schoolmaster" (selection 121). The passage is an eloquent defense of the teaching profession.

> The schoolmaster's occupation is laborious and ungrateful; its rewards are scanty and precarious. He may indeed be, and he ought to be animated by the consciousness of doing good, that best of all consolations, that noblest of all motives. But that too must be often clouded by doubt and uncertainty. (026-004)

> Obscure and inglorious as his daily occupation may appear to learned pride or worldly ambition, yet to be truly successful and happy he must be animated by the spirit of the same great principles which inspired the most illustrious benefactors of mankind. (026-004)

> He must feel deeply the reverence due to the youthful mind fraught with mighty though undeveloped energies and affections, and ready to burst forth into the exuberant activity of generous feeling or the wild misdirection of overpowering passion. Thence he must have learned to reverence himself and his profession, and to look upon its otherwise ill-requited toils as the most dignified and the most ennobling of all human pursuits. (026-005)

> Let us prove our gratitude to the dead by faithfully endeavoring to elevate the station, to enlarge the usefulness, and to raise the estimation of the teacher. Thus shall we best testify our gratitude to the teachers and guides of our own youth, thus best serve our country, and thus best honor the memories of the illustrious dead. (026-007)

The selection exemplifies Verplanck's elevated rhetorical style and his conviction that education was the foundation of republican citizenship.

## Style and Themes

Verplanck's prose reflects the formal elegance of early 19th-century American oratory. His sentences are carefully balanced, his diction elevated, and his arguments grounded in classical and Enlightenment ideals of civic virtue. The theme of education as a noble calling — worthy of society's highest respect — runs throughout his work, as does a concern for the moral and cultural development of the young republic.

## Reception and Legacy

Verplanck was highly regarded by his contemporaries as a man of letters and a public intellectual. His discourses on education were widely circulated and contributed to the growing movement for professionalizing teaching in the United States. In his later years he was a revered elder statesman of New York's literary culture. He died in 1870 at the age of eighty-three.
