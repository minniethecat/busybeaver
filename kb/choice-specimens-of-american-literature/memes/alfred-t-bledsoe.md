## Biography

Albert Taylor Bledsoe (about 1809–1877) was born in Frankfort, Kentucky. He graduated from West Point in 1830 and served briefly in the United States Army before resigning to study law and theology. He was ordained an Episcopal minister in 1835 but later returned to lay life and a career in education and letters.

Bledsoe taught mathematics and philosophy at several institutions, including the University of Mississippi, where he served as professor of mathematics, and the University of Virginia, where he held the chair of mathematics. During the Civil War, he served as Assistant Secretary of War for the Confederacy. After the war, he founded and edited the *Southern Review*, a quarterly periodical that became the leading intellectual organ of the postbellum South. He died in 1877 in Alexandria, Virginia.

## Literary Career

Bledsoe's most significant philosophical work was *The Theodicy; or, Vindication of the Divine Glory* (1853), an attempted reconciliation of the existence of moral evil with the holiness and omnipotence of God. The work engaged with the free-will defense — the argument that moral agency requires the genuine possibility of sin — and positioned itself within the broader tradition of philosophical theology associated with Leibniz and later analytic defenses of theism.

Bledsoe also authored *An Essay on Liberty and Slavery* (1856), a proslavery philosophical tract that attempted to ground the institution in natural law and scriptural argument. His editorial leadership of the *Southern Review* (1867–1877) shaped the intellectual discourse of the post-Reconstruction South.

## Selections

The corpus presents a single passage from *The Theodicy*, in which Bledsoe mounts a philosophical argument that moral evil is not incompatible with divine holiness because free moral agency necessarily entails the possibility of sin.

### Moral Evil Consistent with the Holiness of God (011-002)

Bledsoe's argument hinges on the concept of moral agency itself: a being incapable of choosing evil would not be a moral agent at all but a mere machine. Omnipotence, he contends, cannot create a contradiction — a moral agent who cannot sin is a logical impossibility. This passage represents mid-nineteenth-century American engagement with classical theism's problem of evil:

> [011-002] The argument of the atheist assumes, as we have seen, that a Being of infinite power could easily prevent sin, and cause holiness to exist. It assumes that it is possible, that it implies no contradiction, to create an intelligent moral agent, and place it beyond all liability to sin. But this is a mistake. Almighty power itself, we may say with the most profound reverence, cannot create such a being, and place it beyond the possibility of sinning. If it could not sin, there would be no merit, no virtue, in its obedience. That is to say, it would not be a moral agent at all, but a machine merely. The power to do wrong, as well as to do right, is included in the very idea of a moral and accountable agent, and no such agent can possibly exist without being invested with such a power. To suppose such an agent to be created, and placed beyond all liability to sin, is to suppose it to be what it is, and not what it is, at one and the same time; it is to suppose a creature to be endowed with a power to do wrong, and yet destitute of such a power, which is a plain contradiction. Hence Omnipotence cannot create such a being, and deny to it a power to do evil, or secure it against the possibility of sinning.

## Reception

Bledsoe's *Theodicy* was respectfully received by American religious thinkers of his era, valued for its clear exposition of free-will theodicy in accessible prose. However, Bledsoe's posthumous reputation has been complicated by his prominent role in Confederate intellectual life. Modern historians of American philosophy recognize him as an important transitional figure between antebellum moral philosophy and the professionalized academic philosophy of the later nineteenth century. His *Southern Review* remains a significant primary source for understanding post-Civil War Southern thought.
