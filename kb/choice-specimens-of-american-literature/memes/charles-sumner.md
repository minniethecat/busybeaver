# Charles Sumner

## Biography

Charles Sumner (1811–1874) was an American statesman, United States Senator from Massachusetts, and one of the most eloquent and uncompromising opponents of slavery in American history. Born in Boston, Sumner graduated from Harvard College in 1830 and Harvard Law School in 1833. He practiced law briefly and edited law reports before traveling to Europe from 1837 to 1840, where he attended lectures at the Sorbonne, studied at the Inns of Court, and formed friendships with leading European liberals and intellectuals.

Sumner returned to America a confirmed reformer. He was a vocal opponent of the Mexican-American War, a critic of capital punishment, and an early advocate for prison reform and public education. But it was the anti-slavery cause that became his consuming passion. In 1851, a coalition of Free-Soilers and Democrats elected him to the U.S. Senate, where he immediately established himself as one of the most radical and electrifying voices against the slave power.

In May 1856, two days after delivering his incendiary speech "The Crime Against Kansas," Sumner was attacked at his desk on the Senate floor by Representative Preston Brooks of South Carolina, who beat him repeatedly with a heavy cane until Sumner lay unconscious and bleeding. The caning of Charles Sumner became one of the most notorious events in antebellum American politics — a visceral symbol of the violence at the heart of the slavery debate. Sumner took three years to recover, but Massachusetts kept his seat vacant in defiance, and his empty chair became a rallying point for the emerging Republican Party.

Upon his return to the Senate in 1859, Sumner assumed leadership of the Radical Republican faction. During the Civil War he pressed President Lincoln to move faster on emancipation and afterward became a central architect of Reconstruction, insisting on full civil and political rights for freedmen. His final years were consumed by the battle for the Civil Rights Act of 1875, which he did not live to see enacted. Sumner died in Washington in 1874, having devoted his entire public career to the cause of human equality.

## Literary Career

Sumner was one of the most learned and classically trained orators in American political history. His speeches draw freely upon Greek and Roman history, European literature, and biblical allusion, woven into a prose style of formidable rhetorical power. Unlike many of his contemporaries, Sumner rarely employed humor or folksy anecdote; his characteristic mode was moral intensity elevated by historical breadth — a style that could veer from forensic analysis to prophetic denunciation in the space of a single paragraph.

The selections in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" are drawn from Sumner's speech on the Kansas-Nebraska Bill — the very measure that, by repealing the Missouri Compromise, opened the western territories to slavery and ignited the political crisis that led to civil war. Sumner begins with a paradoxical judgment that the bill is both the worst and the best ever enacted:

> Sir, the bill which you are now about to pass is at once the worst and the best bill on which Congress ever acted. Yes, sir, worst and best at the same time. (021-004)

It is the worst, he explains, because "it is a present victory of slavery. In a Christian land, and in an age of civilization, a time-honored statute of freedom is struck down" (021-005). Yet it is also the best, for it makes slavery's ultimate defeat inevitable:

> Sir, it is the best bill on which Congress ever acted; for it annuls all past compromises with slavery, and makes all future compromises impossible. Thus it puts freedom and slavery face to face, and bids them grapple. Who can doubt the result? (021-006)

Sumner's rhetorical genius lies in his refusal to accept political prudence as a sufficient standard. When critics counsel compromise in the choice of candidates, Sumner responds with an absolute moral standard that brooks no equivocation:

> But the question now before the country is of another character. This will not admit of compromise. It is not within the domain of expediency. *To be wrong on this is to be wholly wrong.* (021-008)

The speech builds to a visionary crescendo, as Sumner invokes the example of history's martyrs — Greek, Christian, and American — to encourage those who fear that their cause is hopeless:

> Did the martyrs fail, when with their precious blood they sowed the seed of the Church? Did the discomfited champions of Freedom fail, who have left those names in history which can never die? Did the three hundred Spartans fail, when, in the narrow pass, they did not fear to brave the innumerable Persian hosts, whose very arrows darkened the sun? (021-009)

He closes with an invocation of the Revolutionary generation — Adams, Franklin, Jefferson — and a call for union among the friends of freedom (021-010). The later passage on the Civil War, likely from a subsequent address, frames the conflict as a providential struggle: "That we may continue freemen there must be no slaves; so that our own security is linked with the redemption of a race" (021-011).

## Selections in This Anthology

- Speech on the Kansas-Nebraska Bill (021-004–021-010) — Sumner's paradoxical argument that the Kansas-Nebraska Act, though a triumph for slavery in the short term, was "the best bill" because it annulled all past compromises and made slavery's ultimate defeat inevitable.
- On the Civil War and Slavery (021-011) — From a later wartime address, framing emancipation as both a moral imperative and a necessity for national survival.

## Critical Reception

Sumner's oratory divided opinion sharply in his own time. His friends and admirers — among them Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Ralph Waldo Emerson, and John Greenleaf Whittier — regarded him as the conscience of the Senate, a man whose learning and moral courage placed him among the great figures of the age. His enemies saw him as arrogant, self-righteous, and politically obtuse, a judgment that even sympathetic historians have had to weigh against his genuine achievements.

Modern scholarship has generally affirmed Sumner's historical importance while maintaining a critical distance from his personality. David Donald's two-volume biography (1960, 1970) remains the standard work, portraying Sumner as a man of immense erudition and moral conviction whose rigidity often alienated potential allies. Recent historians have been more generous, emphasizing Sumner's prescience on civil rights and his role in articulating the moral foundations of the Reconstruction Amendments. His speeches remain studied as masterworks of American political oratory and as documents of the moral imagination that sustained the anti-slavery movement.
