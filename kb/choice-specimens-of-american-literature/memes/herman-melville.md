# Herman Melville

## Biography

Herman Melville (1819–1891) was an American novelist, short story writer, and poet, best known as the author of *Moby-Dick* (1851). Born in New York City to a merchant family that fell into financial difficulty after his father's death, Melville went to sea as a young man — experiences that furnished the material for his early novels.

His first books, *Typee* (1846) and *Omoo* (1847), were popular successes, drawing on his adventures in the South Seas. *Moby-Dick*, however, was a commercial and critical failure upon publication, and Melville's literary reputation declined sharply during his lifetime. He spent his later years working as a customs inspector in New York and writing poetry, including the long poem *Clarel* (1876).

At the time of his death, Melville was largely forgotten. The posthumous publication of *Billy Budd* in 1924 sparked a revival of interest that culminated in the "Melville Revival" of the 1920s and his recognition as one of the central figures of the American literary canon.

## Literary Career

Melville's fiction is distinguished by its philosophical depth, its immersion in the physical realities of life at sea, and its restless exploration of fate, evil, and the limits of human knowledge. His prose ranges from the documentary precision of his early travel narratives to the Shakespearean grandeur of *Moby-Dick*, a work that combines the adventure of the whaling voyage with metaphysical inquiry.

The selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) is drawn from the whaling chapters of *Moby-Dick*. The passage captures the visceral terror and exhilaration of the whale hunt, as the crew of the Pequod close in on their quarry:

> It was a sight full of quick wonder and awe! The vast swells of the omnipotent sea; the surging, hollow roar they made as they rolled along the eight gunwales, like gigantic bowls in a boundless bowling-green; the brief suspended agony of the boat, as it would tip for an instant on the knife-like edge of the sharper waves, that almost seemed threatening to cut it in two; the sudden profound dip into the watery glens and hollows; the keen spurrings and goadings to gain the top of the opposite hill; the headlong, sled-like slide down its other side; — all these, with the cries of the headsmen and harpooners, and the shuddering gasps of the oarsmen, with the wondrous sight of the ivory Pequod bearing down upon her boats, with outstretched sails, like a wild hen after her screaming brood; — all this was thrilling. (062-015)

Melville then turns from physical description to the inner experience of the novice oarsman, measuring the intensity of the moment against the most extreme human experiences:

> Not the raw recruit, marching from the bosom of his wife into the fever heat of his first battle; not the dead man's ghost, encountering the first unknown phantom in the other world; neither of these can feel stranger and stronger emotions than that man does, who for the first time finds himself pulling into the charmed, churned circle of the hunted sperm whale. (062-015)

Though represented by only a single selection in this anthology, the passage captures Melville's characteristic intensity — the fusion of precise physical observation with a reach toward experiences at the very edge of human comprehension.

## Critical Reception

Melville's contemporaries were baffled by *Moby-Dick*. Reviewers praised the vivid whaling passages but condemned the book's philosophical digressions and what they saw as its blasphemous undertones. By the time of his death, the New York Times noted his passing in a brief paragraph that identified him as the author of books "which attracted some attention many years ago."

The twentieth-century rediscovery of Melville transformed his reputation. Beginning with Raymond Weaver's biography (1921) and Carl Van Doren's essays, a generation of critics — including F. O. Matthiessen, whose *American Renaissance* (1941) placed Melville alongside Emerson, Thoreau, Hawthorne, and Whitman — established *Moby-Dick* as perhaps the single greatest American novel. Subsequent scholarship has explored Melville's engagement with race, imperialism, sexuality, and the crisis of religious faith, confirming his status as a writer whose work has only grown in relevance.
