# Maria J. McIntosh

## Biography

Maria Jane McIntosh (1803–1878) was an American novelist and essayist. Born in Sunbury, Georgia, she grew up in a prominent Southern family and received her education at an academy in Savannah. Following financial reverses after her father's death, she turned to writing as a means of support. Under the pseudonym "Aunt Kitty," she published a series of popular domestic novels and juvenile tales that found an appreciative audience in the antebellum United States.

## Literary Career

McIntosh's fiction, including *Two Pictures; or, What We Think of Ourselves, and What the World Thinks of Us* (1863) and *The Lofty and the Lowly* (1853), addressed themes of character, social responsibility, and sectional identity. Her work belonged to the tradition of American domestic fiction associated with writers such as Catharine Maria Sedgwick and Harriet Beecher Stowe, though McIntosh brought a distinctively Southern perspective to the genre.

In *Two Pictures*, McIntosh turned her attention to the national political stage, offering a dramatic portrait of the great Senate debates of the antebellum era. She described the commanding presence of the era's leading statesmen:

> Webster, Clay, Calhoun — the triumvirate to which, it is to be feared, we shall long have to look back as to our last, were still living. (062-008)

McIntosh captured the gravity of the proceedings: "On neither side was there any trickery, any bullying, any flimsy display of rhetorical power. All was grand as the subject for which they contended, solemn as the doom to which they seemed approaching" (062-009). Her account emphasized the personal magnetism of the figures involved, writing that her heroine "felt that she stood in the midst of her dream fulfilment" as she observed these giants of American political life (062-008).

## Selections in This Anthology

McIntosh is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) by a passage from *Two Pictures* titled "Debate between Webster and Hayne" (062-008, 062-009). The selection offers a fictionalized but historically grounded account of the Webster-Hayne debate, one of the defining oratorical contests of the antebellum Senate. McIntosh's narrator surveys the gallery with the keen eye of a social novelist, noting that "the deep tones of Webster found as ready an entrance to southern as to northern hearts" (062-009).

## Critical Reception

During her lifetime, McIntosh enjoyed considerable popularity as a writer of domestic fiction. Her work was praised for its moral seriousness and its nuanced treatment of sectional character. Modern scholarship has reassessed her as a significant voice in the Southern domestic tradition, one whose fiction engaged seriously with the political and social questions of her time even as it observed the conventions of the domestic novel.
