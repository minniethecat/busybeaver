# Ephraim C. Squier

**Ephraim C. Squier** (1821–1888) was an American archaeologist, diplomat, and ethnologist whose pioneering studies of prehistoric mound-builder earthworks laid the foundation for American archaeology. He co-authored the landmark *Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley* (1848), the first publication of the Smithsonian Institution.

## Biography

Ephraim C. Squier was born in 1821 in Bethlehem, New York. He began his career as a journalist and edited several newspapers before turning to archaeological research. In 1845, he began a systematic survey of prehistoric earthworks in the Mississippi Valley and the Great Lakes region, working in collaboration with Dr. Edwin H. Davis. Their work resulted in the first comprehensive archaeological survey published by the Smithsonian Institution. Squier later served as a diplomat in Central America, where he negotiated the Honduras Interoceanic Railway Treaty and conducted ethnographic research on indigenous cultures. He died in 1888 at the age of sixty-seven.

## Literary Career

Squier's most enduring contribution is *Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley* (1848), which he co-authored with Edwin H. Davis. This meticulously illustrated work documented hundreds of prehistoric mounds and earthworks, establishing the field of American archaeology on a scientific footing. Squier also published extensively on Central America, including *Nicaragua: Its People, Scenery, Monuments* (1852) and *The States of Central America* (1858). His travel writing combined ethnographic observation with vivid landscape description, capturing the frontier regions of the mid-nineteenth century.

## Selections

### The Ottawa Sugar-Makers' Voyage to Mackinaw

From Squier's observations of Great Lakes Indian life, this passage describes the annual pilgrimage of Ottawa families carrying their winter sugar harvest across the waters of Green Bay to the trading post at Mackinaw.

> [055-001] It was now about the middle of June, and some fifty birchen canoes have just been launched upon the waters of Green Bay. They are occupied by our Ottawa sugar-makers, who have started upon a pilgrimage to Mackinaw. The distance is near two hundred miles, and as the canoes are heavily laden not only with mocucks of sugar, but with furs collected by the hunters during the past winter, and the Indians are travelling at their leisure, the party will probably reach their desired haven in the course of ten days. Well content with their accumulated treasures, both the women and the men are in a particularly happy mood, and many a wild song is heard to echo over the placid lake.

The passage offers a panoramic view of seasonal Indian travel patterns in the Great Lakes region during the 1840s, just as traditional lifeways were being transformed by the expansion of American settlement and the fur trade economy.

## Critical Reception

Squier's archaeological work earned him wide recognition among American and European scholars. *Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley* was hailed as a landmark of American science. His later diplomatic and ethnographic writings on Central America were well received, though his archaeological reputation has proved the more enduring. Modern scholars recognize Squier as a foundational figure in American archaeology, even as some of his theoretical conclusions about the mound-builders have been revised by subsequent research.
