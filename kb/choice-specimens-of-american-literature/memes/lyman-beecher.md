# Lyman Beecher

## Biography

Lyman Beecher (1775–1863) was an American Presbyterian and Congregationalist clergyman, theologian, and reformer, and the patriarch of one of the most influential families in American intellectual life. Born in New Haven, Connecticut, Beecher graduated from Yale College in 1797 and studied theology under Timothy Dwight, the president of Yale and a leading figure of the Second Great Awakening. He was ordained in 1799 and served congregations in East Hampton, Long Island (1799–1810), and Litchfield, Connecticut (1810–1826), before moving to Boston's Hanover Street Church (1826–1832).

In 1832, Beecher became the first president of Lane Theological Seminary in Cincinnati, Ohio, a position he held until 1850. At Lane, he presided over the famous debates on slavery in 1834, which resulted in the mass departure of abolitionist students who found his gradualist approach insufficiently radical. Beecher was a leading figure in the temperance movement, a defender of orthodox Calvinism against Unitarianism, and the father of thirteen children, among them Henry Ward Beecher, Harriet Beecher Stowe, Catharine Beecher, and Edward Beecher — a dynasty of ministers, writers, and reformers whose collective influence on nineteenth-century American culture was immense.

## Literary Career

Lyman Beecher was first and foremost a preacher, and his published works retain the rhetorical force of the spoken word. His "Lectures on Political Atheism," from which the selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn, represent a sustained argument for the necessity of religious belief to the health of republican institutions. Writing in an era when the French Revolution had raised the specter of a godless republic, Beecher insisted that morality and liberty alike depended on the acknowledgment of a divine lawgiver.

The selection "The Being of a God" opens with a statement of desire and necessity rather than formal proof — a rhetorical strategy that places Beecher closer to the affective piety of the Great Awakening than to the rational theology of the Enlightenment:

> It is a thing eminently to be desired that there should be a supreme benevolent Intelligence, who is the creator and moral governor of the universe, whose subjects and kingdom shall endure for ever. Such a one the nature of man demands, and his whole soul pants after. (006-008)

Beecher's argument proceeds not from abstract reasoning but from the existential predicament of human finitude. He evokes the experience of contemplating a universe without God:

> We feel our littleness in presence of the majestic elements of nature, our weakness compared with their power, and our loneliness in the vast universe, unenlightened, unguided, and unblessed, by any intelligence superior to our own. We behold the flight of time, the passing fashion of the world, and the gulf of annihilation curtained with the darkness of an eternal night. (006-008)

The centerpiece of the selection is an extended apostrophe — a cry from the soul confronting the possibility of its own extinction:

> At the side of this vortex, which covers with deep oblivion the past, and impenetrable darkness the future, nature shudders and draws back, and the soul, with sinking heart, looks mournfully around upon this fair creation, and up to these beautiful heavens, and in plaintive accents demands, "Is there, then, no deliverance from this falling back into nothing? Must this conscious being cease — this reasoning, thinking power, and these warm affections, their delightful movements? Must this eye close in an endless night, and this heart fall back upon everlasting insensibility?" (006-009)

Beecher extends this cry outward to the cosmos itself:

> "O, thou cloudless sun, and ye far-distant stars, in all your journeyings in light, have ye discovered no blessed intelligence who called you into being, lit up your fires, marked your orbits, wheels you in your courses, around whom ye roll, and whose praises ye silently celebrate? Are ye empty worlds, and desolate, the sport of chance? or, like our sad earth, are ye peopled with inhabitants, waked up to a brief existence, and hurried reluctantly, from an almost untested being, back to nothing?" (006-009)

The peroration gathers these threads into a single, compressed utterance: "O that there were a God, who made you greater than ye all, whose being in yours we might see, whose intelligence we might admire, whose will we might obey, and whose goodness we might adore!" (006-009). Beecher concludes that "such, except where guilt seeks annihilation as the choice of evils, is the unperverted, universal longing after God and immortality" (006-009).

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selection from Lyman Beecher's works appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *The Being of a God* (006-008–006-010) — From "Lectures on Political Atheism." An impassioned argument for the existence of God grounded not in logical demonstration but in the soul's existential longing. Beecher evokes the terror of a universe without a divine intelligence and argues that the universal human desire for God is itself evidence of His being.

## Critical Reception

Lyman Beecher's reputation has been overshadowed by the literary achievements of his children, particularly Harriet Beecher Stowe and Henry Ward Beecher. His own prose, while less polished than that of his more famous descendants, exhibits a raw rhetorical power characteristic of the revivalist tradition from which he emerged. His "Lectures on Political Atheism" were influential in their time, articulating a widely held conviction that republican government required religious foundation.

Historians of the Second Great Awakening have valued Beecher as a representative figure — a bridge between the Edwardsian Calvinism of the eighteenth century and the evangelical activism of the nineteenth. His role as patriarch of the Beecher dynasty, perhaps more than his own writings, secures his place in American cultural history. The figure described by contemporary accounts as "a Congregational clergyman, prominent, in the early part of this century, for his zeal and piety, and for the eloquence and originality of his sermons: father of a numerous family distinguished in theology and literature" (006-011) remains the essential frame for understanding his legacy.
