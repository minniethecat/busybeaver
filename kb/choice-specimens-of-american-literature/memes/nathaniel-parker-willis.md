# Nathaniel Parker Willis

## Biography

Nathaniel Parker Willis (1807–1867) was an American author, poet, and editor who became one of the most widely read literary journalists of the antebellum period. Born in Portland, Maine, and raised in Boston, Willis graduated from Yale College in 1827. He founded the *American Monthly Magazine* and later served as editor of the *New-York Mirror*, where he established himself as a tastemaker in American letters.

Willis traveled extensively in Europe, and his letters from abroad — collected as *Pencillings by the Way* (1835) — made him one of America's first successful travel writers. His literary circle included many of the era's leading figures, and his home in the Hudson Valley, Idlewild, became a gathering place for writers and artists. He was the brother of composer Richard Storrs Willis and the brother-in-law of the English actress Fanny Kemble.

## Literary Career

Willis produced a varied body of work spanning poetry, travel sketches, literary criticism, and fiction. His early verse was collected in *Sketches* (1827) and showed the influence of the Romantic poets. However, his most significant contribution to American letters was as a journalist and editor who brought literary culture to a broad middle-class readership.

His travel writing is marked by acute social observation and a distinctive voice that blends cosmopolitan sophistication with American directness:

> It is a queer feeling to find oneself a *foreigner*. One can not realize long at a time how his face or his manners should have become peculiar; and after looking at a print for five minutes in a shop-window, or dipping into an English book, or in any manner throwing off the mental habit of the instant, the curious gaze of the passer-by, or the accent of a strange language, strikes one very singularly. (043-001)

Willis was also a perceptive literary critic. His memorial essay on the poet and playwright James Hillhouse demonstrates his gift for combining critical assessment with vivid personal portraiture:

> Such men as Hillhouse are not common, even in these days of universal authorship. In accomplishment of mind and person, he was probably second to no man. His poems show the first. They are fully conceived, nicely balanced, exquisitely finished — works for the highest taste to relish, and for the severest student in dramatic style to erect into a model. (043-003)

He continues with a memorable physical description:

> In no part of the world have I seen a man of more distinguished mien, or of a more inborn dignity and elegance of address. His person was very finely proportioned, his carriage chivalric and high-bred, and his countenance purely and brightly intellectual. Add to this a sweet voice, a stamp of high courtesy on everything he uttered, and singular simplicity and taste in dress, and you have the portrait of one who, in other days, would have been the mirror of chivalry, and the flower of nobles and troubadours. (043-003)

Willis could also strike a lighter, more playful tone. In "The Interrupted Legend," drawn from *Ephemera*, he dramatizes a flirtatious literary exchange:

> Flemming was reclining on the flowery turf, at the lady's feet, looking up with dreamy eyes into her sweet face, and then into the leaves of the linden-trees overhead. "Gentle Lady! Dost thou remember the linden trees of Bülach, — those tall and stately trees, with velvet down upon their shining leaves, and rustic benches underneath their overhanging eaves? A leafy dwelling, fit to be the home of elf or fairy, where first I told my love to thee, thou cold and stately Hermione!" (043-015–043-016)

The lady's interruption — "Why, it is in rhyme!" — and Flemming's protest that "you have already snapped asunder the gossamer threads of as sweet a dream as was ever spun from a poet's brain" (043-017–043-018) capture Willis's self-aware humor about the creative process.

## Critical Reception

In his lifetime, Willis was among the highest-paid magazine writers in America. His travel letters and society sketches commanded a wide audience, and his editorial influence helped shape the careers of many younger writers. However, his literary reputation declined after his death. Edgar Allan Poe, who both admired and resented Willis, described him as a writer of "taste rather than genius." Twentieth-century criticism has generally viewed Willis as a transitional figure — a skilled literary entrepreneur who helped professionalize American authorship, even if his own work has not endured at the level of his contemporaries Hawthorne and Melville. Recent scholarship has begun to reassess his contributions to the development of American magazine culture and travel writing.
