# Jesse Buel

## Biography

Jesse Buel (1778–1839) was born in Coventry, Connecticut, and raised on a farm — an early experience that shaped his lifelong commitment to agricultural improvement.

As a young man he entered the printing trade, moving from New York City to Albany, where he became an influential newspaper editor (001-017). He published the *Albany Argus*, a leading Democratic-Republican newspaper, and his political connections brought him into the orbit of Martin Van Buren.

In middle age, Buel turned from politics to farming with the zeal of a convert. He purchased a farm near Albany and applied scientific principles to its cultivation. In 1834 he founded *The Cultivator*, an agricultural journal that became the leading voice of agricultural reform in the Northeast.

## Literary Career

Buel's prose is clear, forceful, and aimed at practical improvement. *The Farmer's Instructor*, published posthumously, collected his writings on soil management, crop rotation, and agricultural education.

Buel wrote for the working farmer, emphasizing that scientific agriculture was essential for a nation whose "agricultural portion probably exceeds five-sixths" of the population (032-016).

> In no country does the agricultural class bear so great a proportion to the whole population as in this. ... And in no country does the agricultural population exercise such a controlling political power, contribute so much to the wealth, or tend so strongly to give an impress to the character of a nation as in the United States. (032-016)

Buel argued that agricultural education was the missing link in American prosperity, anticipating the land-grant university system created by the Morrill Act a generation later. "We have no place where they can learn the principles upon which the art depends," he wrote (032-016).

## Selections in This Anthology

- **"Extent and Defects of American Agriculture"** (032-016): From *The Farmer's Instructor*, this essay surveys the scope of American farming and identifies its central weakness: the absence of systematic agricultural education. Buel argues that the young "cannot learn as they ought to learn ... because they have no suitable school for their instruction" (032-016).

## Critical Reception

Buel's reputation rests on his success as an organizer and popularizer of agricultural knowledge. *The Cultivator* became a model for agricultural periodicals across the country. Modern historians recognize him as a transitional figure whose call for agricultural education helped create the intellectual groundwork for the land-grant university system.
