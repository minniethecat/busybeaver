# The Scarlet Letter

*The Scarlet Letter: A Romance* (1850) is Nathaniel Hawthorne's most celebrated novel and a cornerstone of American literature.

Set in seventeenth-century Puritan Boston, the novel follows [[hester-prynne|Hester Prynne]], a woman condemned to wear a scarlet letter "A" for adultery. Through her story, [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Hawthorne]] explores the hidden recesses of guilt, hypocrisy, and the possibility of redemption.

The novel was an immediate success upon publication. It sold out its first edition of 2,500 copies within ten days. [[henry-james]] later called it "the finest piece of imaginative writing yet put forth in the country."

## Plot Summary

[[hester-prynne|Hester Prynne]] emerges from the Boston prison holding her infant daughter Pearl, the scarlet letter "A" embroidered on her chest. She refuses to name her lover — the father of her child.

Her husband, presumed dead at sea, arrives in Boston and assumes the identity of Roger Chillingworth. He vows to discover and destroy the man who wronged him. The Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale, the town's beloved young minister, wastes away under the weight of concealed guilt — for he is Pearl's father.

Over seven years, Hester rebuilds her life through charity and skill with her needle. The scarlet letter, meant as a mark of shame, becomes a symbol of her strength. Dimmesdale, tormented by Chillingworth's psychological manipulation, ascends the scaffold at last. He confesses publicly and dies in Hester's arms.

Pearl, freed from the triangle of sin and secrecy, goes on to live a full life. Hester returns to Boston and resumes wearing the scarlet letter by her own choice — not as a punishment, but as part of her identity.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression in the Novel | Literary Significance |
|-------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Sin and Concealment | Dimmesdale's hidden guilt destroys him; Hester's open acknowledgment frees her | The psychological cost of hypocrisy |
| Individual vs. Society | Hester stands outside Puritan law, finding wisdom through suffering | Critique of institutional moral authority |
| Identity and Transformation | The letter "A" changes meaning: Adultery → Able → Angel | Symbols are socially constructed |
| Nature of Evil | Chillingworth's calculated revenge is more sinister than Hester's passion | Evil as deliberate violation of the human heart |
| Redemption | Hester earns respect through service; Dimmesdale through confession | Redemption requires truth-telling |

Hawthorne framed the novel as a "romance" rather than a novel, claiming for himself the freedom to explore psychological and moral truths rather than mere realistic detail.

## Literary Style

Hawthorne's prose in *The Scarlet Letter* is dense, allusive, and richly symbolic.

His long introductory chapter, "The Custom-House," blends autobiography with meditation on the relationship between the writer and his material. The main narrative moves with the formal gravity of tragedy, punctuated by moments of extraordinary psychological insight.

The novel's symbolic architecture — the scaffold scenes at beginning, middle, and end — gives it a structural unity rare in nineteenth-century fiction. The forest, the prison door, the rosebush, and of course the scarlet letter itself: Hawthorne makes every element of his fictional world bear moral and psychological weight.

## Selections in This Anthology

Hawthorne's prose style is represented in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) through selections from his shorter works and romances:

> [060-006] The whole portrait started so distinctly out of the background, that it had the effect of a person looking down from the wall at the astonished and awe-stricken spectators. The expression of the face, if any words can convey an idea of it, was that of a wretch detected in some hideous guilt...

> [060-008] ...it seemed as if we could see Nature shaping out a woman before our very eyes, and yet had only a more reverential sense of the mystery of a woman's soul and frame.

These selections, though not drawn directly from *The Scarlet Letter*, exemplify Hawthorne's characteristic fusion of psychological penetration with symbolic imagination.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

*The Scarlet Letter* has never gone out of print. It is among the most widely taught American novels in schools and universities worldwide.

[[d-h-lawrence|D.H. Lawrence]], in *Studies in Classic American Literature* (1923), read the novel as a fable of the American psyche's struggle with desire and repression. [[leslie-fiedler|Leslie Fiedler]] saw in it the archetype of the American romance — a narrative of forbidden love in a new world haunted by old sins.

Feminist critics have reclaimed Hester Prynne as a proto-feminist heroine. Her quiet defiance, economic independence, and moral growth outside patriarchal institutions make her one of the most complex female characters in nineteenth-century fiction.

The novel's influence extends beyond literature. It has been adapted for film, opera, and theater dozens of times. Its title phrase has entered the English language as shorthand for public shaming and the stigma of transgression.
