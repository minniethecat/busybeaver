# George Bancroft

## Biography

George Bancroft (1800–1891) was an American historian, statesman, and educator, best known for his monumental "History of the United States, from the Discovery of the American Continent" — a ten-volume work that established American historiography as a serious scholarly discipline. Born in Worcester, Massachusetts, Bancroft was a prodigy who entered Harvard College at the age of thirteen and graduated at seventeen. He then studied at the University of Göttingen in Germany, where he absorbed the methods of the new scientific historiography under the tutelage of Arnold Heeren and other leading German scholars. Bancroft was the first American to earn a Ph.D. from a German university.

Returning to the United States in 1822, Bancroft attempted a career as a clergyman and a teacher before turning to literature and politics. He served as Collector of the Port of Boston (1838–1841), Secretary of the Navy under President James K. Polk (1845–1846) — during which he founded the United States Naval Academy at Annapolis — and as American minister to Great Britain (1846–1849) and later to the German Empire (1867–1874). Throughout his public career, Bancroft continued to labor on his "History of the United States," the first volume of which appeared in 1834 and the tenth and final volume in 1874 — an extraordinary span of sustained scholarly production.

Bancroft's historical philosophy was shaped by his conviction that American democracy represented the culmination of human progress — the realization, in political institutions, of principles of liberty and equality that had been unfolding through history under providential guidance. This "Whig interpretation" of American history would dominate the field for generations before being challenged by later historians.

## Literary Career

George Bancroft was the preeminent American historian of the nineteenth century, and his "History of the United States" set the standard against which all subsequent American historiography was measured. His prose — expansive, luminous, and charged with moral conviction — belongs to the Romantic tradition of historical writing exemplified by Thomas Babington Macaulay and Jules Michelet. Bancroft wrote history not as a neutral chronicle of facts but as a grand narrative of the progress of liberty, in which the American experiment was the central drama.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) includes several passages from Bancroft's "History of the United States." The longest selection, "Virginia and its Inhabitants in Early Times," is a set-piece of descriptive historical writing that evokes the natural world encountered by the first English settlers:

> The genial climate and transparent atmosphere delighted those who had come from the denser air of England. Every object in nature was new and wonderful. The loud and frequent thunder-storms were phenomena that had been rarely witnessed in the colder summers of the north; the forests, majestic in their growth, and free from underwood, deserved admiration for their unrivalled magnificence; the purling streams and the frequent rivers, flowing between alluvial banks, quickened the ever-pregnant soil into an unwearied fertility; the strangest and the most delicate flowers grew familiarly in the fields; the woods were replenished with sweet barks and odors; the gardens matured the fruits of Europe, of which the growth was invigorated and the flavor improved by the activity of the virgin mould. (028-004)

Bancroft's catalogue of the New World's marvels proceeds with a naturalist's eye and a poet's delight: the mocking-bird "which carolled a thousand several tunes, imitating and excelling the notes of all its rivals," the humming-bird "so brilliant in its plumage, and so delicate in its form," the rattlesnake with "the terrors of its alarms," the opossum and the flying squirrel, the "myriads of pigeons, darkening the air with the immensity of their flocks" (028-004). This was the American Eden — fertile, abundant, and awaiting the arrival of English liberty.

A second selection, "Contrast of English and French Colonization in America," develops Bancroft's larger thesis about the providential character of American history. The triumph of England over France in the struggle for North America was, in his view, no accident of military fortune but the working out of deeper historical forces:

> In America, the Teutonic race, with its strong tendency to individuality and freedom, was become the master from the Gulf of Mexico to the Poles; and the English tongue, which but a century and a half before had for its entire world a part only of two narrow islands on the outer verge of Europe, was now to spread more widely than any that had ever given expression to human thought. (028-005)

The passage culminates in one of the most celebrated apostrophes in American historical writing — a prophetic invocation of the English language as the vehicle of liberty:

> Go forth, then, language of Milton and Hampden, language of my country, take possession of the North American continent! Gladden the waste places with every tone that has been rightly struck on the English lyre, with every English word that has been spoken well for liberty and for man! (028-006)

Bancroft's contrast between French and English colonization turns on the difference between absolutism and liberty. French America was "governed exclusively by the monarchy of its metropolis" and "shut against the intellectual daring of its philosophy, the liberality of its political economists, the movements of its industrial genius, its legal skill, and its infusion of Protestant freedom" (028-007). "Nothing representing the new activity of thought in modern France, went to America. Nothing had leave to go there but what was old and worn out" (028-007). By contrast, "The colonists from England brought over the forms of the government of the mother country, and the purpose of giving them a better development and a fairer career in the western world" (028-008). In this antithesis — French despotism versus English liberty — Bancroft found the explanation for why the continent spoke English rather than French.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from George Bancroft's writings appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Virginia and its Inhabitants in Early Times* (028-004) — From "History of the United States." A richly detailed description of the natural environment encountered by the first English settlers in Virginia: the climate, forests, rivers, flowers, birds, and wildlife of the New World. Bancroft's prose evokes the wonder of the first colonists at a landscape that seemed to them both Edenic and strange.

- *Contrast of English and French Colonization in America* (028-005–028-008) — From "History of the United States." Bancroft develops his thesis that the triumph of English civilization over French power in North America was not a contingency of war but a consequence of the superior liberty inherent in English institutions. Includes the famous apostrophe to the English language and an analysis of why French absolutism, however brilliant, could not compete with English self-government in colonizing the continent.

## Critical Reception

George Bancroft's reputation as a historian has followed the trajectory of American historiography itself. In his own century, he was celebrated as the nation's greatest historian — the man who gave Americans a usable past and a sense of providential destiny. Presidents sought his counsel, foreign governments honored him, and his "History" sold in editions that would be the envy of any modern academic historian.

In the twentieth century, the "scientific" historians of the Progressive school — Charles Beard, Carl Becker, Frederick Jackson Turner — repudiated Bancroft's providential framework as unscientific and his nationalism as uncritical. The Whig interpretation of history, of which Bancroft was the American exemplar, fell into disrepute among professional scholars. Yet Bancroft's literary gifts have never been denied, and recent scholarship has begun to reassess his achievement with greater sympathy — recognizing in his work not merely patriotic apologetics but a serious engagement with the philosophical problem of historical meaning.
