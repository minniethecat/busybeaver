# John M. Mason

## Biography

John Mitchell Mason (1770–1829) was an American Presbyterian clergyman, theologian, and educator. Born in New York City, the son of a prominent Presbyterian minister, Mason was educated at Columbia College and the University of Edinburgh, where he studied theology. Returning to America in 1792, he was ordained and installed as pastor of the Associate Reformed Church in New York, where his preaching quickly attracted large congregations and established his reputation as one of the foremost pulpit orators of his generation.

Mason served as president of Dickinson College from 1821 to 1824 and was instrumental in founding the American Bible Society in 1816, becoming one of its most energetic advocates. His address on behalf of the Society, from which the first selection in "Choice Specimens" is drawn, was delivered before a national audience and exemplifies the rhetorical power that made him — as the editors note — "a Presbyterian clergyman of great distinction, long settled in New York; rarely surpassed in controversial acuteness, and in religious eloquence."

## Literary Career

Mason's published works consist predominantly of sermons, addresses, and theological treatises produced in the course of his pastoral and institutional work. His prose was characterized by rhetorical intensity, logical precision, and a willingness to engage directly with skeptical or hostile audiences. He was a prolific contributor to periodicals, including the *Christian Magazine*, which he founded and edited, and he sustained an active correspondence with religious leaders in Britain and across the United States.

The first selection in "Choice Specimens," "Grandeur of the Enterprise" — taken from Mason's Address in behalf of the Bible Society — is a sustained exercise in rhetorical amplification, building momentum through a series of increasingly urgent appeals:

> If there be a single measure which can overrule objection, subdue opposition, and command exertion, this is the measure. That all our voices, all our affections, all our hands, should be joined in the grand design of promoting "peace on earth and good will toward man" — that they should resist the advance of misery — should carry the light of instruction into the dominions of ignorance, and the balm of joy to the soul of anguish; and all this by diffusing the oracles of God — addresses to the understanding an argument which cannot be encountered; and to the heart an appeal which its holiest emotions rise up to second. (005-008)

Mason's rhetoric then pivots to direct address, challenging his American audience by comparison with European efforts:

> People of the United States; Have you ever been invited to an enterprise of such grandeur and glory? Do you not value the Holy Scriptures? Value them as containing your sweetest hope; your most thrilling joy? Can you submit to the thought that *you* should be torpid in your endeavors to disperse them, while the rest of Christendom is awake and alert? (005-008)

The historical moment is read theologically: "The most heavenly charity treads close upon the march of conflict and blood! The world is at peace!" — the end of the Napoleonic Wars is interpreted as providential preparation for the global work of Bible distribution. "Crowned heads bow to the head which is to wear 'many crowns,' and, for the first time since the promulgation of Christianity, appear to act in unison."

The second selection, "The Right of the State to Educate," takes up a different register — philosophical rather than hortatory — arguing from the unknowability of children's futures to the public obligation to educate them:

> No sagacity can foretell what characters shall be developed, or what parts performed, by these boys and girls who throng our streets, and sport in our fields. In their tender breasts are concealed the germs, in their little hands are lodged the weapons, of a nation's overthrow — or of its glory and salvation. (005-012)

The argument proceeds from epistemic humility (we cannot know which children will become leaders or criminals) to civic obligation: the state has a right and a duty to educate all children because their future influence on the commonwealth cannot be predicted in advance. This coupling of evangelical conviction with republican political theory is characteristic of early American educational thought.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "Grandeur of the Enterprise" (005-008) — From the Address in behalf of the Bible Society. An impassioned appeal for universal Bible distribution, notable for its rhetorical architecture and its reading of post-Napoleonic peace as providential opportunity.
- "The Right of the State to Educate" (005-012) — From the same Address. An argument grounding public education in the unknowability of children's future roles and their potential impact on the commonwealth.

## Critical Reception

Mason was recognized by his contemporaries as one of the most formidable pulpit orators of the early republic. His sermons and addresses circulated widely in pamphlet form, and his role in founding the American Bible Society placed him at the center of the interdenominational evangelical network that shaped American Protestantism in the early nineteenth century. His presidency at Dickinson College was brief but consequential — he attempted to strengthen the college's Presbyterian identity, a move that generated controversy and contributed to his resignation.
