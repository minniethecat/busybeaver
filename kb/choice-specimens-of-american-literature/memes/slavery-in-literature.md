# Slavery in American Literature

## Definition

Slavery, in American literary tradition, appears both as a historical institution documented in colonial records and early national writing, and as a moral challenge that produced some of the most powerful rhetoric in American letters. From 17th-century observations of the practice through the abolitionist literature of the 19th century, the theme of slavery forced American writers to confront the contradiction between the ideal of liberty and the reality of human bondage.

## Historical Context

Colonial writers like William Stith recorded early protests against enslaving Christians, noting the principle that "liberty is the natural right of all men" (005-004). Timothy Dwight identified slavery as a moral stain incompatible with American ideals, writing of "prosperity not tainted by slavery, selfishness, or any wrong" (007-005). Patrick Henry, who famously demanded "liberty or death," acknowledged the moral challenge of a nation that proclaimed freedom while practicing slavery (013-012).

## Key Authors and Works

- **William Stith**, in his History of Virginia (1747), recorded colonial assertions that Christians should not be enslaved, establishing an early tension between the ideal of liberty and the practice of bondage (005-004).
- **Timothy Dwight**, in his poem "Greenfield Hill" (1794), envisioned an America purified of slavery, where prosperity would be untainted by oppression (007-005).
- **Patrick Henry**, despite owning slaves himself, acknowledged in his speeches the moral contradiction of fighting for liberty while denying it to others (013-012).
- **The Federalist authors**, in debates over the Constitution, grappled with the political and moral implications of slavery for the new Republic (016-002).

## Related Concepts

- [[liberty]] — The ideal whose denial to enslaved people created the central moral tension of American literature
- [[virtue]] — The civic and personal morality that abolitionists argued was undermined by the institution of slavery
- [[independence]] — The national sovereignty that, abolitionists contended, could never be secure while the nation practiced slavery

## Legacy

The literary engagement with slavery culminated in the mid-19th century with works like Harriet Beecher Stowe's "Uncle Tom's Cabin" (1852) and the slave narratives of Frederick Douglass. These works transformed American literature by giving voice to the enslaved and by forcing the nation to confront the moral reality of the institution. The tension between American ideals of liberty and the practice of slavery remains a central theme in American literary study.
