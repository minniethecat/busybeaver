# Albert Pike

## Biography

Albert Pike (1809–1891) was an American poet, lawyer, soldier, and Freemason. Born in Boston, Massachusetts, he migrated westward as a young man and settled in Arkansas, where he built a distinguished legal career. He served as a Confederate brigadier general during the Civil War and is perhaps best known for his extensive contributions to Freemason ritual and philosophy, particularly through his book *Morals and Dogma of the Ancient and Accepted Scottish Rite of Freemasonry* (1871). Pike was also an accomplished poet whose work appeared in anthologies of American literature, including the 1874 *Choice Specimens of American Literature* compiled by Benjamin N. Martin.

## Literary Career

Pike began writing poetry in the 1830s and contributed to numerous periodicals of the era. His poetry is characterized by a Romantic sensibility, with particular attention to the grandeur of the American landscape — a theme that aligned him with contemporaries such as [[william-cullen-bryant|William Cullen Bryant]] and the broader Romantic movement in American letters. His poem "Hymns to the Gods" and other verses were published in *Blackwood's Magazine*. Pike's literary output, while overshadowed by his legal and Masonic work, represents a genuine contribution to the tradition of nineteenth-century American nature poetry. His verse frequently contemplates the sublime scale of the American West, combining classical form with frontier subject matter.

## Selections in This Anthology

Martin's *Choice Specimens* includes one selection from Albert Pike: "The Everlasting Hills" (No. 376), excerpted from his longer poem "Lines on the Rocky Mountains." This selection appears in Section 72 of the anthology.

> "The deep, transparent sky is full / Of many thousand glittering lights — / Unnumbered stars that calmly rule / The dark dominions of the night."
> (071-066)

> "These mountains, piercing the blue sky / With their eternal cones of ice, — / The torrents dashing from on high, / O'er rock, and crag, and precipice, — / Change not, but still remain as ever, / Unwasting, deathless, and sublime, / And will remain while lightnings quiver, / Or stars the hoary summits climb, / Or rolls the thunder-chariot of eternal Time."
> (071-067, 071-068)

## Critical Reception

Pike's poetry received modest recognition during his lifetime, praised for its formal competence and descriptive power. However, his literary reputation has been largely eclipsed by his Masonic writings, particularly *Morals and Dogma*, which remains a central text in Scottish Rite Freemasonry. Modern critics have noted Transcendentalist undertones in his nature poetry, particularly in its treatment of landscape as a source of spiritual insight — as exemplified in "The Everlasting Hills."
