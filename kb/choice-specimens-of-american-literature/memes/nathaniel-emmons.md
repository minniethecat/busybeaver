# Nathaniel Emmons

## Biography

Nathaniel Emmons (1745–1840) was an American Congregational clergyman whose sixty-year pastorate in Franklin, Massachusetts, and prolific theological writing made him one of the most influential figures of the "New Divinity" movement — the school of Reformed theology that grew out of the teachings of Jonathan Edwards and Samuel Hopkins.

Born in East Haddam, Connecticut, Emmons graduated from Yale College in 1767 and studied theology under the Rev. John Smalley, a disciple of Hopkins. In 1773, he was ordained pastor of the Congregational church in Franklin, Massachusetts, a position he held continuously until his death in 1840 — a remarkable tenure of sixty-seven years. During this time, he trained nearly one hundred young men for the ministry, conducting what amounted to an informal theological seminary in his parsonage. His students, known as "Emmonsites," carried his distinctive theological views throughout New England and into the expanding frontier.

Emmons lived to the age of ninety-five, spanning the colonial period, the Revolution, the early republic, and the antebellum era. His longevity made him a living link between the Great Awakening and the Second Great Awakening, and he was one of the last surviving figures who had personally known the major theologians of the Edwardsean tradition.

## Literary Career

Emmons's literary output was vast — his collected *Works*, published in six volumes between 1842 and 1863, comprise sermons, theological treatises, and systematic expositions of doctrine. His prose style, as noted in the "Choice Specimens" editorial apparatus, was "eminently lucid" — a quality not always associated with the dense metaphysical theology of the Edwardsean school.

Emmons's central theological contribution was his development of "consistent Calvinism," a system that pressed Reformed doctrines to their logical conclusions. He was known for his "exercise scheme" — the view that all human actions are directly caused by God, and that moral character resides not in the will but in the exercises of the heart. These views made him a controversial figure even among fellow Calvinists, but his intellectual rigor and personal integrity earned widespread respect.

The sermon excerpted in "Choice Specimens" — "The Right of Private Judgment" — represents Emmons at his most accessible. He articulates a principle that would become foundational to American religious culture: that every individual has both the right and the duty to form religious opinions based on their own examination of the evidence:

> The right of private judgment involves the right of forming our opinions according to the best light we can obtain. After a man knows what others have said or written, and after he has thought and searched the Scriptures, upon any religious subject, he has a right to form his own judgment exactly according to evidence. (003-003)

Emmons's formulation is carefully balanced. The right of private judgment is not a license for prejudice or laziness; it is a responsibility that requires diligent inquiry:

> He has no right to exercise prejudice or partiality; but he has a right to exercise impartiality, in spite of all the world. After all the evidence is collected from every quarter, then it is the proper business of the understanding or judgment to compare and balance evidence, and to form a decisive opinion or belief, according to apparent truth. (003-003)

What makes Emmons's argument distinctive is its symmetry: the moral obligation to form judgments applies equally in both directions. One must not believe without evidence, but equally, one must not withhold belief when the evidence is sufficient:

> We have no more right to judge without evidence than we have to judge contrary to evidence; and we have no more right to doubt without, or contrary to, evidence, than we have to believe without, or contrary to, evidence. We have no right to keep ourselves in a state of doubt or uncertainty, when we have sufficient evidence to come to a decision. The command is, "Prove all things; hold fast that which is good." The meaning is, Examine all things; and after examination, decide what is right. (003-003)

This is Enlightenment rationalism in a Puritan pulpit — a fusion of scriptural authority with the conviction that the human understanding, properly exercised, is capable of arriving at truth.

## Critical Reception

Emmons's influence on American theology was profound but contested. His "consistent Calvinism" was regarded by some as a rigorous extension of Edwardsean principles and by others as a reduction to absurdity. The Princeton theologian Charles Hodge criticized Emmons's "exercise scheme" as undermining human moral responsibility, while the Andover seminary represented a moderate alternative to the Emmonsite position.

Yet Emmons's emphasis on the "right of private judgment" had an impact that extended beyond theological circles. His articulation of the duty of individual inquiry resonated with broader American cultural values — the same impulse that animated democratic politics, universal education, and the proliferation of voluntary associations. In this sense, Emmons the rigorous Calvinist paradoxically contributed to the intellectual underpinnings of American individualism.
