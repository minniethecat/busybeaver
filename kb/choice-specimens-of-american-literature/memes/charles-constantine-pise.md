# Charles Constantine Pise

## Biography

Charles Constantine Pise (1802–1866) was an American Catholic priest, poet, and essayist born in Annapolis, Maryland. Educated at Georgetown College (now Georgetown University), he entered the Society of Jesus briefly before becoming a diocesan priest. Pise served as chaplain to the United States Senate from 1832 to 1833, the first Catholic to hold that position. His literary career was largely conducted from Washington, D.C. and New York, where he contributed to Catholic periodicals including the *United States Catholic Miscellany* and the *Metropolitan*. A figure of the early Catholic literary revival in America, Pise sought to demonstrate that devout Catholic faith and refined literary taste were compatible — a position still contested in the Protestant-dominated literary culture of antebellum America. He died in Brooklyn, New York, in 1866.

## Literary Career

Pise's literary output spanned poetry, essays, and travel writing. His major works include *The Pleasures of Religion* (1835), a verse meditation on the consolations of faith, and *The Tourist* (1833), an account of his European travels. His poetry favored neoclassical forms — heroic couplets, blank verse, and polished lyric stanzas — through which he explored religious themes, natural beauty, and patriotic sentiment. Pise's verse is characterized by its formal decorum and its consistent argument that nature, properly read, points toward divine revelation. Unlike the more introspective Romantic poets of his era, Pise maintained an eighteenth-century sensibility in his poetic architecture, even as his subject matter engaged Romantic interests in the sublime and the picturesque. His essays, collected in *Letters to Ada* and other volumes, addressed literary, moral, and religious topics from a distinctly Catholic perspective. As one of the earliest American Catholic writers to gain a national readership, Pise occupies a unique position at the intersection of American religious history and literary culture.

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) includes two poems by Charles Constantine Pise drawn from two separate volumes:

1. **"The Rainbow"** (068-038–068-041), from *The Pleasures of Religion*. This poem opens by describing the visual splendor of a rainbow breaking through a clearing storm, then converts the natural image into a theological metaphor — the rainbow becomes an emblem of religious hope shining across "the wilds of life." Pise's closing couplet captures his central poetic conviction: nature's beauty is God's pedagogy.

2. **"View at Gibraltar"** (068-042–068-045), from *The Tourist*. This poem surveys the strategic panorama of Gibraltar from its heights, enumerating the flags of the nations gathered peacefully in the bay before singling out the American flag with patriotic fervor: "But mark, bold-towering o'er the conscious wave, / The starry banners of my country brave."

Martin prefaces the poems with a brief excerpt from Pise's descriptive prose (068-037), a passage on the sublimity of Mammoth Cave that praises the "thousand snow-white gems" of its chambers while concluding that the natural beauties of the upper world — stars, sunsets, flowers, birds, forests, mountains — are "far more beautiful and wild."

> Beautiful Are all the thousand snow-white gems that lie In these mysterious chambers, gleaming out Amid the melancholy gloom, and wild These rocky hills and cliffs, and gulfs, but far More beautiful and wild, the things that greet The wanderer in our world of light. (068-037)

> Mark, o'er yon wild, as melts the storm away, The rainbow tints their various hues display; Beauteous, though faint, though deeply shaded, bright, They span the clearing heavens, and charm the sight. Yes, as I gaze, methinks I view — the while, Hope's radiant form, and Mercy's genial smile. (068-038)

> And from this height, how beauteous to survey The neighboring shores, the bright cerulean bay: Myriads of sails are swelling on the deep, And oars, in myriads, through the waters sweep. Behold, in peace, all nations here unite, Their various pennons streaming to the sight. (068-042–068-043)

## Critical Reception

Pise was recognized in his lifetime as a pioneer of Catholic letters in America. The *Manual of American Literature* (which Martin cites at 068-037) acknowledged him among noteworthy American authors. However, modern scholarship has largely overlooked Pise in favor of the Protestant-dominated canon of nineteenth-century American literature. Recent studies in American Catholic history have begun to recover his significance as a cultural figure who bridged the gap between Catholic devotion and secular literary ambition. His poetry, while formally conservative, represents an early American engagement with the relationship between natural observation and religious experience that anticipates later Catholic literary figures.
