# Francis Parkman

## Biography

Francis Parkman (1823–1893) was an American historian and travel writer, best known for his multi-volume work *France and England in North America*. Born in Boston to a prominent New England family, he attended Harvard College, where he developed an early passion for the American wilderness.

Despite chronic ill health — severe headaches, partial blindness, and a nervous disorder — Parkman maintained a prodigious output. In 1846, he journeyed along the Oregon Trail, living among the Dakota Sioux. This produced *The Oregon Trail* (1849), a vivid first-person account that established his reputation.

His magnum opus, *France and England in North America*, appeared in seven volumes (1865–1892), covering colonial conflict from the earliest French settlements through the fall of Quebec. He composed under extreme conditions — dictating to assistants and writing with his eyes closed. He also served as Professor of Horticulture at Harvard, and died at his home in Jamaica Plain in 1893.

## Literary Career

Parkman fused rigorous archival research with the narrative drive of the novel. He treated the American wilderness not merely as setting but as a historical actor in its own right. His prose earned comparison to Sir Walter Scott and set the standard for American narrative history.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) presents two selections from Parkman's work. The first, from *The Conspiracy of Pontiac* (1851), portrays the hunters and trappers who ranged beyond the settled frontier:

> These rude and hardy men, hunters and traders, scouts and guides, who ranged the woods beyond the English borders, and formed a connecting link between barbarism and civilization, have been touched upon already. They were a distinct, peculiar class, marked with striking contrasts of good and evil. Many, though by no means all, were coarse, audacious, and unscrupulous; yet, even in the worst, one might often have found a vigorous growth of warlike virtues, an iron endurance, an undespairing courage, a wondrous sagacity, and singular fertility of resource. (031-009)

Parkman traces the frontiersman's lineage to the barbarian warriors of northern Europe:

> In them was renewed, with all its ancient energy, that wild and daring spirit, that force and hardihood of mind, which marked our barbarous ancestors of Germany and Norway. These sons of the wilderness still survive. We may find them to this day, not in the valley of the Ohio, nor on the shores of the lakes, but far westward on the desert range of the buffalo, and among the solitudes of Oregon. (031-009)

The passage culminates in an image of mythic grandeur, as the solitary trapper is elevated through a cascade of precise sensory detail:

> Even now, while I write, some lonely trapper is climbing the perilous defiles of the Rocky Mountains, his strong frame cased in time-worn buck-skin, his rifle griped in his sinewy hand. Keenly he peers from side to side, lest Blackfoot or Arapahoe should ambuscade his path. The rough earth is his bed, a morsel of dried meat and a draught of water are his food and drink, and death and danger his companions. No anchorite could fare worse, no hero could dare more; yet his wild, hard life has resistless charms; and while he can wield a rifle, he will never leave it. (031-009)

The second selection, from *The Discovery of the Great West* (1869), recounts the Joliet-Marquette expedition of 1673 crossing into the Mississippi watershed:

> The river twisted among the lakes and marshes choked with wild rice; and, but for their guides, they could scarcely have followed the perplexed and narrow channel. It brought them at last to the portage; where, after carrying their canoes a mile and a half over the prairie and through the marsh, they launched them on the Wisconsin, bade farewell to the waters that flowed to the St. Lawrence, and committed themselves to the current that was to bear them they knew not whither, — perhaps to the Gulf of Mexico, perhaps to the South Sea or the Gulf of California. (031-010)

The first sight of the great river is rendered with dignified economy:

> They had found what they sought, and "with a joy," writes Marquette, "which I cannot express," they steered forth their canoes on the eddies of the Mississippi. (031-011)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Hunters and Trappers* (031-009) — From *The Conspiracy of Pontiac* (1851). A celebrated portrait of the frontiersmen who inhabited the borderlands between colonial settlement and Native American territory. Parkman depicts them with moral realism and romantic admiration, tracing their lineage to the warriors of Germania.

- *Exploration of the Mississippi River* (031-010–031-016) — From *The Discovery of the Great West* (1869). An account of the Joliet-Marquette expedition of 1673, describing the passage from the Great Lakes to the Mississippi and the explorers' first encounter with the great river.

## Critical Reception

Parkman was acknowledged in his lifetime as the preeminent historian of colonial North America, praised by Henry Adams, Theodore Roosevelt, and John Fiske. Twentieth-century assessments emphasized his narrative artistry while questioning his biases — notably his hostility toward French Catholicism and his ambivalent portrayal of Native Americans.

Later scholars subjected him to more searching critique, noting how his narrative of Anglo-Saxon triumph served Manifest Destiny. Yet his critics acknowledged the power of his prose and the depth of his archival research. Modern assessments recognize him as a literary artist of the first rank whose work must be read critically in light of its cultural assumptions.
