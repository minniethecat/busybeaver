## Biography

Jane T.L. Worthington (1847--?) was an American writer active during the mid-nineteenth century. Very little biographical detail about her life has survived; the principal facts known are her birth year of 1847 and her authorship of a single published volume, *Love Sketches*. She was included in Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874), making her one of the youngest living writers anthologized in the collection -- she would have been in her late twenties at the time of compilation.

Martin identifies her in the index simply as "J.T.L. Worthington, 1847--," with the open-ended terminal date reflecting her still-active career at the time of the anthology's publication. Beyond this entry and her published book, no further records of her life -- education, family, residence, or subsequent literary activity -- have come down to modern scholarship.

## Literary Career

Worthington's known literary output consists of *Love Sketches*, a collection of short sentimental prose pieces published in the mid-nineteenth century. The work belongs to the tradition of American sentimental literature that flourished during the decades before the Civil War and continued into the post-war period, a tradition that includes writers such as [[Harriet Beecher Stowe]], [[Alice Cary]], and the many contributors to popular periodicals and gift books of the era.

"[[The Sisters]]," the selection chosen by Martin for inclusion in the *Choice Specimens* anthology, is representative of Worthington's literary approach. The piece focuses on a domestic scene of emotional intimacy -- two sisters sharing a quiet evening on the eve of the elder's wedding -- and uses this intimate moment as a springboard for broader meditation on memory, loss, and the passage of time. The prose style is characteristic of the sentimental mode: elevated in diction, attentive to atmospheric detail, and concerned with the emotional interiority of its subjects. Worthington's sentences move from concrete sensory observation -- the fragrance of early spring flowers, the brightening crescent moon, the stillness of the evening air -- into philosophical reflection on the nature of memory and the bittersweet quality of human experience.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selection from Jane T.L. Worthington's *Love Sketches* appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- **"The Sisters"** (049-007, 049-008) -- A domestic scene depicting two sisters together for the last time in their childhood home before the elder's wedding.

The piece opens with the two sisters seated before an open window as evening falls. The elder sister wears the orange-flower wreath of a bride, her complexion pale beneath it. The scene is suffused with the quiet of early spring, the two figures clasping hands in silence as they watch the stars emerge.

> The sisters were together, together for the last time in the happy home of their childhood. The window before them was thrown open, and the shadows of evening were slowly passing from each familiar outline on which the gazers looked. They were both young and fair; and one, the elder, wore that pale wreath the maiden wears but once. (049-007)

After describing this intimate tableau, the narrative broadens into a meditation on memory and the passage of time. Worthington captures the twilight hour as a moment when "reverie is deepest," when the quiet stillness invites the mind to dwell on those who have been lost.

> It was the hour when reverie is deepest, and fantasies have the earnestness of truth, when memory is melancholy in its vividness, and we feel, "almost like a reality," the presence of those who may bless our pathway no more. The loved, the lost -- "So many, yet how few!" -- gather around us, not as they are, chastened and troubled by battling with trials and disappointments, but as they used to be, in the glow of unwearied expectation. Old fears flit before us altered into pleasures, and old hopes return bathed in tears. (049-008)

The selection is notable for its seamless movement from the particular -- two sisters on a specific evening -- to the universal. Worthington does not linger on the wedding itself, or on the drama of separation; instead, she finds her subject in the suspended moment before change arrives, and in the way memory reshapes the past into something at once more beautiful and more painful than the original experience.

## Critical Reception

Jane T.L. Worthington remains a minor figure in American literary history. Her inclusion in Martin's 1874 anthology secured her a place in the historical record that might otherwise have been lost, but *Love Sketches* did not achieve wide circulation or lasting recognition. The work exemplifies the conventions of American sentimental prose without breaking new ground, and Worthington's apparent withdrawal from -- or failure to sustain -- a literary career after her early publication makes her a representative case of the many mid-century women writers who entered print briefly and then disappeared from view. Contemporary scholarship on sentimental literature has begun to recover figures like Worthington as part of a broader reassessment of the genre's cultural significance, but critical attention to her specific work remains minimal.
