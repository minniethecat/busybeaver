# Thomas C. Upham

## Biography

Thomas Cogswell Upham (1799–1873) was an American philosopher, psychologist, and poet born in Deerfield, New Hampshire. He graduated from Dartmouth College in 1818 and studied at Andover Theological Seminary before joining the faculty of Bowdoin College in 1825, where he served as professor of mental and moral philosophy until 1868. Upham was a prolific author whose major philosophical works — including *Elements of Mental Philosophy* (1831) and *A Treatise on the Will* (1834) — established him as one of the most widely read American philosophers of the antebellum period. His philosophical system, grounded in Scottish common-sense realism and evangelical Christianity, influenced a generation of American college students. Upham's personal life was marked by tragedy: the loss of his young son at sea, an event that inspired some of his most moving verse. He died in 1873 in Kennebunkport, Maine.

## Literary Career

While Upham's primary reputation rests on his philosophical and psychological writings, he was also a poet of considerable emotional power. His verse, collected in volumes such as *American Cottage Life* (1850) and various hymns, addressed domestic themes, religious devotion, and personal grief. Upham's poetry eschewed the ornate conventions of contemporaneous Romantic verse in favor of plain, direct expression that reflected his philosophical commitment to clarity and moral seriousness. His hymn writing contributed several pieces to American Protestant worship, and his poems appeared in literary magazines of the period. As a college professor who published across disciplinary boundaries — philosophy, psychology, theology, and poetry — Upham embodied the antebellum ideal of the Christian scholar, for whom intellectual inquiry and spiritual life were inseparable pursuits. His mental philosophy textbooks remained in use at American colleges through the 1880s, making him one of the most influential academic writers of his era.

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) includes one poem by Thomas C. Upham:

**"On a Son Lost at Sea"** (069-028–069-035). This elegy opens with the poet's vision of his drowned son returning to him in memory's eye — "Boy of my earlier days and hopes! Once more, / Dear child of memory, of love, of tears!" — before confronting the horror of the boy's oceanic grave: "Deep in the stormy ocean's hidden cave / Buried, and lost to human care and sight." Upham wrestles with the supernatural quality of the visitation, asking "What power hath interposed to rend thy grave? / What arm hath brought thee thus to life and light?" before concluding that even a brief, ghostly reunion offers grounds for Christian hope: "Yet shall this kindly visit's mystery / Give rise to hopes that shall not pass away."

> Boy of my earlier days and hopes! Once more, Dear child of memory, of love, of tears! I see thee, as I saw in days of yore, As in thy young, and in thy lovely, years. (069-029–069-030)

> Deep in the stormy ocean's hidden cave Buried, and lost to human care and sight, What power hath interposed to rend thy grave? What arm hath brought thee thus to life and light? (069-032–069-033)

The poem is notable for its direct, unadorned treatment of parental grief — a register rarely struck in the more rhetorical elegiac traditions of the period. Martin's inclusion of Upham alongside poets such as Longfellow and Whittier acknowledges the place of the philosopher-poet in the American literary landscape.

## Critical Reception

Upham's philosophical works were widely reviewed and admired in the antebellum period; *Elements of Mental Philosophy* went through numerous editions and was translated into several languages. His poetry, however, received comparatively little critical attention, overshadowed by his academic reputation. Modern scholarship on Upham has focused primarily on his contributions to the development of American psychology and his role in the Scottish common-sense tradition, with his literary output remaining a peripheral interest. The inclusion of "On a Son Lost at Sea" in Martin's 1874 anthology suggests that his verse was valued by contemporaries as sincere and affecting, if not innovative in form.
