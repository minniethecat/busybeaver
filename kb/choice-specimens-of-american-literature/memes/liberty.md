# Liberty

Liberty is the most persistent and contested theme in early American literature.

From the Puritan defense of religious conscience to the Revolutionary cry of "[[give-me-liberty-speech|Give me liberty, or give me death!]]" to the abolitionist demand for universal [[emancipation|freedom]], the ideal of liberty has shaped American literary expression across two centuries.

American writers did not merely celebrate liberty — they wrestled with its meanings, its limits, and the contradictions between the nation's ideals and its practices.

## Religious Liberty

The first great American debates over liberty concerned conscience.

[[john-winthrop]]'s "[[a-model-of-christian-charity|A Model of Christian Charity]]" (1630) envisioned a community bound by mutual obligation. [[roger-williams]] argued for the separation of church and state and full liberty of conscience. [[jonathan-edwards]] defended the freedom of the will within a Calvinist framework that emphasized divine sovereignty.

The tension between individual conscience and communal authority runs through American writing from the colonial period to the present. It is present in [[nathaniel-hawthorne]]'s portrayal of Puritan intolerance and in [[henry-david-thoreau]]'s defense of civil disobedience.

## Revolutionary Liberty

The American Revolution transformed liberty from a philosophical ideal into a political program.

[[patrick-henry]]'s "Give me liberty, or give me death!" speech (1775) distilled the Revolutionary spirit into a single, unforgettable phrase. The [[declaration-of-independence|Declaration of Independence]] (1776) asserted that liberty was an inalienable right grounded in "the laws of Nature and of Nature's God."

Revolutionary-era writing drew on multiple intellectual traditions:

| Tradition | Source | Key Idea |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| English Whig thought | Locke, Sidney | Natural rights, social contract, resistance to tyranny |
| Classical republicanism | Cicero, Plutarch | Civic virtue, public service, fear of corruption |
| Protestant dissent | Reformation theology | Liberty of conscience, priesthood of all believers |
| Enlightenment rationalism | Montesquieu, Voltaire | Universal rights, progress, reason |

These traditions converged in American Revolutionary rhetoric to create a powerful synthesis that resonated far beyond the political sphere.

## Liberty and Slavery

The greatest challenge to American ideals of liberty was the institution of [[slavery-in-literature|slavery]].

[[thomas-jefferson]], who wrote that "all men are created equal," was a slaveholder. This contradiction haunted American literature throughout the antebellum period. [[frederick-douglass]]'s *Narrative* (1845) exposed the hypocrisy of a nation that proclaimed liberty while denying it to millions.

[[harriet-beecher-stowe]]'s [[uncle-toms-cabin|Uncle Tom's Cabin]] (1852) turned the sentimental power of domestic fiction toward the abolitionist cause. The novel insisted that liberty was not an abstraction but a matter of families, bodies, and the most intimate human bonds.

The tension between American liberty and American slavery produced some of the nation's greatest writing. It also revealed the limits of a literary culture that could celebrate freedom in one register while tolerating its denial in another.

## Selections in This Anthology

The theme of liberty echoes throughout "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

> [013-013] ...but as for me, give me liberty, or give me death!

> [005-004] ...to make slaves of the Christians, liberty is the natural right of all men...

> [013-003] ...formed on the basis of equal liberty. Many cares, many labors, and many duties awaited those who undertook the task of establishing a new government on principles of freedom...

> [002-049] ...I affirm that all the liberty of conscience, that ever I pleaded for...

These passages trace the evolution of liberty from religious conscience through political revolution to the affirmation of natural rights.

## Legacy

The American literary engagement with liberty continues to resonate.

[[abraham-lincoln]]'s Gettysburg Address redefined the nation as "conceived in Liberty." [[martin-luther-king-jr|Martin Luther King Jr.]] drew on both the Declaration of Independence and the prophetic tradition to demand that America finally fulfill its founding promises.

In American literature, liberty remains what it has always been: not a settled doctrine but a living argument — contested, incomplete, and generative of the nation's most powerful writing.
