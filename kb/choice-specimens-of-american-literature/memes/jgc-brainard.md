## Biography

John G. C. Brainard (1796–1828) was an American poet and lawyer. Born in New London, Connecticut, he
studied at Yale College and practiced law in Middletown. Brainard published frequently in literary
periodicals and gained recognition for his sentimental and patriotic verse before his early death at
the age of thirty-two.

## Literary Career

Brainard is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* by an excerpt from "Lines to
the Connecticut River" (001-202). The poem blends patriotic sentiment with a touch of humor, as in
its memorable description of a British soldier's encounter with American music:

> He was mistaken, it was no such thing,
> 'Twas Yankee Doodle, played by Scudder's band;
> He muttered, as he lingered listening,
> Something of freedom and our happy land;
> Then sketched, as to his home he hurried fast,
> This sentimental song — his saddest and his last. (068-013)

## Selections in the Corpus

Brainard's selection in the corpus is drawn from his poem on the Connecticut River (001-202), one of
the earliest poetic celebrations of that New England waterway.

## Critical Reception

Brainard was one of several promising American poets of the 1820s whose careers were cut short by
early death. His verse, while modest in scale, displays a genuine feeling for American subjects and
a lightness of touch unusual in the serious poetry of the period.
