# Cotton Mather

## Biography

Cotton Mather (1663–1728) was an American Puritan minister, prolific author, and the most learned man in colonial New England. The grandson of Richard Mather and John Cotton — two of the founding figures of Massachusetts Bay — and the son of Increase Mather, Cotton Mather was born into what amounted to a dynasty of Puritan leadership. He entered Harvard College at the age of eleven, the youngest student ever admitted, and graduated in 1678. Ordained in 1685, he served for over forty years as minister of the Second (North) Church in Boston alongside his father.

Mather's life and reputation are marked by deep contradictions. He was among the most cosmopolitan intellectuals of his age — a Fellow of the Royal Society of London, a correspondent of European savants, an early advocate of smallpox inoculation in the face of violent popular opposition — yet he is remembered principally for his role in the Salem witch trials of 1692. Though Mather cautioned the judges against the use of spectral evidence, his writings on witchcraft, particularly *The Wonders of the Invisible World* (1693), provided intellectual and theological support for the proceedings. This association has shadowed his legacy ever since, obscuring the breadth and energy of his intellectual achievements.

Mather's magnum opus, the *Magnalia Christi Americana* (1702), is an ecclesiastical history of New England in seven books, subtitled "The Ecclesiastical History of New England, from Its First Planting in the Year 1620 unto the Year of Our Lord 1698." Written in a baroque, allusion-dense Latinized prose that attempts to match the grandeur of its subject, the *Magnalia* is at once a chronicle, a hagiography of Puritan founders, and a theological argument that New England's history is a chapter in the providential drama of redemption. Mather died in Boston in 1728, having published more than four hundred works — sermons, histories, biographies, scientific treatises, and polemics — an output unmatched by any American writer before the nineteenth century.

## Literary Career

Mather's prose is ornate, erudite, and self-consciously monumental. He wrote in what Perry Miller called the "cosmic" style of New England Puritanism — a mode in which every event, however mundane, carries typological significance within the divine plan. His sentences are long, densely subordinated, and freighted with classical and biblical references; his characteristic tone moves between exhortation, lamentation, and awed reverence before the works of God.

The selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn from the opening of the *Magnalia*, where Mather sets forth the purpose of his vast undertaking. He writes as a man alarmed by what he perceives as the decay of primitive piety — the "encroaching and ill-bodied degeneracies" that threaten to erode the founders' achievement. His response is to preserve in writing what he fears may be lost in practice:

> 'Tis now time for me to tell my reader, that in *our age*, there has been another essay made, not by French, but by English PROTESTANTS, to fill a certain country in America with *Reformed Churches*; nothing in *doctrine*, little in *discipline*, different from that of Geneva. (002-050)

This self-conscious framing — the comparison with earlier colonizing enterprises, the insistence on New England's doctrinal purity, the alignment with Geneva — establishes the *Magnalia* as a work of both history and apologetics. Mather presents himself not as a dispassionate chronicler but as a native son defending a patrimony:

> Mankind will pardon *me*, a native of that country, if smitten with a just fear of encroaching and ill-bodied *degeneracies*, I shall use my modest endeavors to prevent the *loss* of a country so signalized for the *profession* of the purest *Religion*, and for the *protection* of God upon it in that holy profession. (002-050)

The passage rises to a statement of Mather's governing conviction: that the meaning of New England lies in the principles of its founding, and that the loss of those principles would be tantamount to the loss of the country itself:

> I shall count my country *lost*, in the loss of the primitive *principles*, and the primitive *practices*, upon which it was at first established: but certainly one good way to save that *loss*, would be to do something, that the memory of *the great things done for us by our God*, may not be *lost*, and that the story of the circumstances attending the *foundation* and *formation* of this country, and of its *preservation* hitherto, may be impartially handed unto posterity. (002-050)

Mather closes the passage with a prayer that situates his scholarship as an act of devotion — "Grant me thy gracious assistances, O my God! that in this my undertaking I may be kept from every false way." The union of historical method with spiritual purpose is the defining characteristic of Mather's literary art.

## Critical Reception

Cotton Mather has been one of the most contested figures in American literary and intellectual history. To his contemporaries, he was the celebrity of New England letters — the author of the most ambitious work of history yet produced in British America, a correspondent of European men of science, the voice of Boston's most influential pulpit. To the generations that followed, shaped by Enlightenment rationalism and romantic individualism, Mather became a symbol of Puritan fanaticism and intellectual obscurantism. The Salem witch trials, in which his role was ambiguous but undeniable, sealed this reputation for more than two centuries.

The rehabilitation of Mather began in the twentieth century, led by the Harvard scholar Perry Miller, who argued that Mather's baroque prose, far from being mere pedantry, was a sophisticated instrument for rendering the cosmic drama of Puritan theology. Subsequent scholars — Sacvan Bercovitch, Kenneth Silverman, David Levin — have deepened this appreciation, presenting Mather as a transitional figure between medieval typology and modern historical consciousness, a writer whose immense learning and fierce piety produced some of the most extraordinary prose in early American literature. The *Magnalia*, once dismissed as unreadable antiquarianism, is now studied as a work of literary ambition comparable in its way to the Puritan epics of Milton.
