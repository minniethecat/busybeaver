# Robert C. Sands

## Biography

Robert Charles Sands (1799–1832) was an American poet, editor, and man of letters associated with the Knickerbocker Group — the loose circle of New York writers that included Washington Irving, James Kirke Paulding, and Fitz-Greene Halleck. Born in Flatbush, Long Island (now part of Brooklyn), Sands was the son of a merchant and the grandson of a Dutch Reformed minister.

Sands graduated from Columbia College in 1815 and was admitted to the bar, but he never practiced law. His interests inclined him toward literature and journalism from an early age. In 1824, he became editor of the *Atlantic Magazine*, a short-lived but distinguished New York periodical. He later co-edited *The Talisman*, an annual literary gift-book published with his friends Gulian C. Verplanck and William Cullen Bryant, which became one of the most admired American annuals of its era.

His literary career, though brief — he died of a stroke at the age of thirty-three — was energetic and productive. Sands was known to his contemporaries as a man of wide reading, lively wit, and generous literary friendship. His early death was mourned as a loss to American letters by figures including Bryant and Irving.

## Literary Career

Sands's poetry is distinguished by its topographical character: "Weehawken," his most famous poem, takes the Palisades of the Hudson River as its subject, turning the landscape of New York and New Jersey into a site of historical reminiscence and Romantic meditation. This mode — the American topographical poem — was a genre that would reach its fullest expression in Bryant's "The Prairies" and Walt Whitman's "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry."

Sands also wrote prose sketches, literary criticism, and a translation of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* (with Henry Rowley). His poem "Yamoyden" (1820), a narrative of King Philip's War written in collaboration with James Wallis Eastburn, was among the earliest American poems to treat Native American subjects with literary ambition.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *From "Weehawken"* (068-019, 349) — A topographical poem of the Hudson River Palisades, in which the cliffs and waters of New York harbor become a theater of national sentiment:

> Here, safe returned from every stormy sea,
> Waves the striped flag, the mantle of the free —
> That star-lit flag, by all the breezes curled
> Of yon vast deep whose waters grasp the world.
> (068-019)

The poem opens with a visionary panorama of American commerce and liberty — "Here cities rise, and sea-washed commerce hails / Thy shores and winds with all her flapping sails" (068-017) — before turning, in its closing movement, to the historical reminiscences that the landscape evokes:

> Yet should the stranger ask what lore
> Of by-gone days, this winding shore,
> Yon cliffs, and fir-clad steeps, could tell
> If vocal made by Fancy's spell,
> The varying legend might rehearse
> Fit themes for high romantic verse.
> (068-021)

Sands's Weehawken is doubly enchanted: by the natural beauty of the Palisades at sunset, and by the layers of human history — Native, Dutch, Revolutionary — that the landscape has absorbed.

## Critical Reception

Sands was well regarded by his fellow Knickerbocker writers and was memorialized after his death in Bryant's *Tales of the Glauber-Spa* and other contemporary tributes. His translation of Ovid, co-authored with Henry Rowley, was for many years the standard American edition.

His reputation, however, has suffered the fate of many early nineteenth-century American poets who wrote topographical and occasional verse: valued by their contemporaries, they fell out of the canon as American literary history came to be organized around a narrower set of major figures. "Weehawken" remains his most anthologized work, a polished example of the Hudson River school of poetry that paralleled the contemporaneous Hudson River school of painting.
