# Nathaniel W. Taylor

## Biography

Nathaniel William Taylor (1786–1858) was an American Congregational theologian who, as the first professor of didactic theology at the Yale Divinity School, became the most influential and controversial American theologian of the antebellum period. Born in New Milford, Connecticut, Taylor graduated from Yale College in 1807, where he studied under Timothy Dwight, the president of Yale and a grandson of Jonathan Edwards. After ordination and a brief pastorate at the First Congregational Church of New Haven (1812–1822), Taylor was appointed in 1822 to the newly established theological professorship at Yale — a position he held for the remaining thirty-six years of his life.

As described in the "Choice Specimens" editorial apparatus, Taylor was "an eminent Congregational divine, long professor of theology in Yale College, and distinguished by the vigor and originality of his thinking." (008-002) His teaching shaped two generations of Congregational ministers, many of whom carried his theological methods into pulpits across New England and the expanding western frontier.

Taylor was the principal architect of what became known as the "New Haven theology" or "Taylorism" — a revision of Edwardsean Calvinism that sought to reconcile Reformed orthodoxy with Enlightenment rationalism and the moral demands of revivalist piety. His central innovation was a strong emphasis on human moral agency: Taylor argued that sin is a matter of free choice, not inherited depravity, and that regeneration involves the sinner's voluntary turning toward God under the persuasive influence of divine truth. This position brought him into sustained controversy with more conservative Calvinists, particularly those associated with Princeton Theological Seminary, who regarded Taylorism as a fatal compromise with Pelagianism.

## Literary Career

Taylor's literary output was predominantly in the form of lectures delivered to his Yale students. His most important works — *Lectures on the Moral Government of God* (1859, posthumous), *Essays on the Means of Regeneration* (1829), and *Lectures on the Divine Decrees* — were systematic expositions of his theological system. His prose was distinguished by logical rigor, dialectical energy, and a persistent effort to ground theological claims in appeals to human moral experience.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," "Proof of Immortality from the Moral Nature of Man," is taken from Taylor's *Lectures on the Moral Government of God* and exemplifies his characteristic method of arguing from the structure of human moral consciousness to theological conclusions:

> The argument from *the moral nature* of man is made still more impressive by the superiority of its design and object. If there is no existence for man beyond the present state, what can we suppose to be the design of his Creator in forming him a moral being? What powers, what capacities are involved in his nature! What capacity to enjoy, and what power to impart happiness to others! (008-002)

Taylor's argument is a classic instance of the teleological argument for immortality — but with a distinctive moral emphasis. He does not appeal to the complexity of the eye or the orbits of the planets but to the internal experience of moral aspiration and frustration. The very structure of human moral consciousness, he argues, points beyond earthly existence:

> Who can reflect on the nature of such a creature, his intelligence, his susceptibility, his will, his conscience, the dignity, the excellence of which he is capable, the moral victories and triumphs he may win, his fitness to hold on his way with archangels, strong in advancing all that good which infinite wisdom could devise, and infinite benevolence could love, the graces with which he may be adorned, and the beatitudes with which he may be blessed, and not believe that he is made to be one with the God who has created him — a partaker of his blessedness, a companion of his eternity. (008-002)

The rhetoric builds through an extended catalogue — intelligence, susceptibility, will, conscience, dignity, excellence, victories, triumphs, fitness, graces, beatitudes — each noun adding another layer to the portrait of human moral capacity, until the conclusion ("a partaker of his blessedness, a companion of his eternity") feels not like a leap but an arrival. The argument then modulates into a deeply introspective passage that catalogues the failures and frustrations of the moral life — "how weak in resolution and feeble in heart," "how remote he is from the uniform and settled tranquility of perfect virtue," "what an Impatience and disgust with the littleness of all he finds" — and concludes that, unless there is a future state, human moral experience is a cruel absurdity, "an insignificance… imparted to all that can be called virtue here on earth, and also to man himself."

## Critical Reception

Taylor's influence on American theology was immense but polarizing. His students — who included Horace Bushnell, the most creative American theologian of the mid-nineteenth century — carried his emphasis on moral agency and the persuasion model of conversion into the mainstream of American Protestant thought. His critics, particularly the Princeton theologians Charles Hodge and Archibald Alexander, regarded Taylorism as a capitulation to Enlightenment rationalism and a betrayal of Reformation principles.

The "New Haven theology" was one of the defining theological movements of the antebellum period, and its influence extended beyond the seminary. Taylor's emphasis on human moral capacity resonated with the democratic ethos of Jacksonian America and informed the activism of the evangelical reform movements — temperance, abolition, missions — that characterized the period.
