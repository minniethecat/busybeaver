# Thomas H. Benton

## Biography

Thomas Hart Benton (1782–1858) was an American politician who served as a United States Senator from Missouri from 1821 to 1851 — a remarkable thirty-year tenure. Born in Hillsborough, North Carolina, Benton studied law at the University of North Carolina and was admitted to the bar in 1805. After a brief period in Tennessee, where he served in the state senate and fought a notorious duel with Andrew Jackson, Benton moved to the Missouri Territory in 1815.

In the Senate, Benton became one of the most powerful Democratic voices of the antebellum era. Nicknamed "Old Bullion" for his advocacy of hard-money currency (gold and silver coinage over paper money), he was a fierce opponent of the Bank of the United States and a leading proponent of westward expansion. Benton was instrumental in the passage of the Oregon Territory bill and fervently believed in Manifest Destiny. His opposition to the expansion of slavery into the western territories ultimately cost him his Senate seat in 1851. He served one term in the House of Representatives (1853–1855) before retiring to write his memoirs.

## Literary Career

Benton was a prolific writer. His major literary achievement is *Thirty Years' View; or, A History of the Working of the American Government for Thirty Years, from 1820 to 1850* (1854–1856), a two-volume memoir that remains an invaluable first-hand account of the antebellum Senate. He also wrote *An Examination of the Dred Scott Case* (1857), a powerful critique of the Supreme Court's decision. Benton's prose is vigorous, combative, and richly detailed, reflecting his personality as a political brawler who valued substance over stylistic refinement.

## Selections in This Anthology

In "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Benton is represented by "The Character of Macon," a tribute to Nathaniel Macon, the North Carolina statesman and Benton's early political mentor:

> "He was above the pursuit of wealth, but also above dependence and idleness, and, like an old Roman of the elder Cato's time, worked in the fields at the head of his slaves in the intervals of public duty, and did not cease this labor until advancing age rendered him unable to stand the hot sun of summer." (022-019)

> "He was not rich, but rich enough to dispense hospitality and charity, to receive all guests in his house, from the president to the day laborer — no other title being necessary to enter his house but that of an honest man; and above all, he was rich enough to pay as he went, and never to owe a dollar to any man." (022-019)

Benton's portrait of Macon embodies the Jeffersonian ideal of republican simplicity — the virtuous citizen-farmer who serves the public without seeking wealth or power. The sketch is both a personal tribute and a political manifesto, holding up Macon's life as a model for American statesmanship.

## Critical Reception

Benton's *Thirty Years' View* was widely read in its day and remains a standard source for historians of the Jacksonian era. Though his prose lacks the polish of more literary contemporaries, his writing is valued for its candor, immediacy, and insider perspective. Modern scholars regard Benton as a transitional figure: the last of the Jeffersonian agrarians in an America increasingly defined by industrialization and sectional conflict. His literary legacy is inseparable from his political career — both are monuments to an era of epic Senate debates and continental ambition.
