# Independence in American Literature

## Definition

Independence, in American literary tradition, operates on multiple levels: personal independence as economic and moral self-reliance; national independence as political sovereignty from foreign rule; and intellectual independence as the assertion of a distinctly American voice in letters. These dimensions are intertwined in the literature of the Revolutionary and early national periods.

## Historical Context

The concept of independence emerged most forcefully during the Revolutionary crisis, when colonial writers articulated the case for political separation from Britain. In the early Republic, independence became a defining national value—celebrated in oratory, debated in political writing, and embodied in literary works that asserted American cultural autonomy. By the 19th century, the ideal of "personal independence" had become central to the American conception of character (025-003).

## Key Authors and Works

- **Jonathan Edwards** described how the Revolutionary conflict "had given independence, and preserved liberty" to the country, linking national sovereignty to divine providence (015-003).
- **Timothy Dwight** celebrated "personal independence, and universal prosperity" as the fruits of American liberty, connecting individual autonomy to national flourishing (006-001).
- **Washington Irving** contributed to "the spirit of national independence" in literature, asserting that American letters should reflect American experience and character (019-002).
- **Daniel Webster** framed American history as divided between "settlement" and "independence," treating the latter as the nation's defining epoch that "should announce, should command, should impress" American identity (020-007).

## Legacy

The ideal of independence, once secured politically, evolved into a cultural imperative. Emerson's "Self-Reliance" (1841) transformed the concept from political sovereignty to intellectual and spiritual autonomy, arguing that true independence requires thinking one's own thoughts rather than deferring to European precedent. This literary declaration of independence became a touchstone for generations of American writers.
