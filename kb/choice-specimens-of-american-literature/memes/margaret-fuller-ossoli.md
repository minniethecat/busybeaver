Margaret Fuller Ossoli (1810–1850), known professionally as Margaret Fuller, was the foremost American female intellectual of the antebellum era and a central figure in the Transcendentalist movement. Born in Cambridgeport, Massachusetts, she received an extraordinarily rigorous education under her father's direction—learning Latin at six and reading [[ovid]] in the original by eight. Fuller served as the first editor of *The Dial*, the Transcendentalist quarterly, from 1840 to 1842, and as a literary critic for [[horace-greeley]]'s *New-York Tribune* from 1844 to 1846, becoming the first woman to hold a full-time position as a book reviewer for a major American newspaper. Her feminist treatise *Woman in the Nineteenth Century* (1845) remains a foundational text of American feminism. In 1846 she traveled to Europe as a foreign correspondent, witnessing the Roman Revolution of 1848–49, and died tragically in a shipwreck off Fire Island, New York, returning to America in 1850.

## Biography

Sarah Margaret Fuller was born on May 23, 1810, in Cambridgeport, Massachusetts, the first child of Timothy Fuller, a prominent lawyer and congressman, and Margaret Crane Fuller. Her father, disappointed to have a daughter rather than a son, resolved to educate her as if she were male—a decision that shaped her extraordinary intellectual development. By the age of thirty, Fuller had become the most learned woman in America, renowned for her "Conversations" (1839–1844), a series of discussion salons for educated Boston women that covered topics from Greek mythology to ethics. In 1844 she joined the *New-York Tribune*, where her reviews and social commentary reached a national audience. Her European sojourn (1846–1850) brought her into contact with [[giuseppe-mazzini]], [[adam-mickiewicz]], and the Italian revolutionary Giovanni Ossoli, whom she married. The couple had a son, Angelo, in 1848. Returning to America in 1850, all three perished when their ship, the *Elizabeth*, struck a sandbar near Fire Island. Fuller's unfinished manuscript on the Roman Revolution was lost with her.

## Literary Career

Fuller's literary output spans criticism, travel writing, translation, and polemical essay. Her first book, *Summer on the Lakes, in 1843* (1844), combined travel narrative with meditations on the condition of women, Native Americans, and the Western frontier. *Woman in the Nineteenth Century* (1845), originally published as "The Great Lawsuit" in *The Dial*, argued that women must be granted intellectual and spiritual self-reliance—an extension of [[ralph-waldo-emerson]]'s concept of self-reliance to the female sex. Her *Papers on Literature and Art* (1846) collected her best *Tribune* criticism, including insightful essays on [[goethe]], [[george-sand]], and American contemporaries like [[henry-wadsworth-longfellow]] and [[edgar-allan-poe]]. Her translation of [[goethe]]'s *Torquato Tasso*, published posthumously, reflected her lifelong engagement with German Romanticism. "Carlyle Characterized," from *At Home and Abroad* (1856), a posthumous collection of her European dispatches, demonstrates her gift for the penetrating character sketch—a form she raised to the level of literary art.

## Selections

### 044-003 — Character of Carlyle (from *At Home and Abroad*)

In this celebrated character sketch, Fuller assesses the Scottish essayist and historian [[thomas-carlyle]], whom she visited in London during her European journey. The selection demonstrates Fuller's distinctive critical voice—personal, incisive, and richly metaphorical:

[044-003]Accustomed to the infinite wit and exhuberant richness of his writings, his talk is still an amazement and a splendor scarcely to be faced with steady eyes. He does not converse,—only harangues. It is the usual misfortune of such marked men (happily not one invariable or inevitable) that they cannot allow other minds room to breathe and show themselves in their atmosphere, and thus miss the refreshment and instruction which the greatest never cease to need from the experience of the humblest. Carlyle allows no one a chance, but bears down all opposition, not only by his wit and onset of words, resistless in their sharpness as so many bayonets, but by actual physical superiority, raising his voice and rushing on his opponent with a torrent of sound.

Fuller's portrait balances admiration with critical detachment. The military metaphor of "bayonets" and the physical description of Carlyle "rushing on his opponent" convey his overpowering presence, while the parenthetical qualification ("happily not one invariable or inevitable") signals Fuller's feminist awareness that intellectual dominance is not inherent to greatness but a cultivated—and corrigible—habit. The closing observation that Carlyle "seemed to me quite isolated, lonely as the desert" hints at the cost of such intellectual imperialism: the very genius that conquered a century's ideas could not secure companionship.

## Critical Reception

Fuller's posthumous reputation has undergone a dramatic revaluation. For much of the nineteenth century she was remembered primarily through the sentimentalized lens of her tragic death and through the condescending memoirs of her male contemporaries, including [[ralph-waldo-emerson]], [[james-russell-lowell]], and [[oliver-wendell-holmes]]. [[nathaniel-hawthorne]]'s character Zenobia in *The Blithedale Romance* (1852) was partially based on Fuller and reinforced a caricature of her as proud and doomed. The feminist revival of the 1970s restored Fuller to her rightful place as a major American intellectual. Modern scholarship, benefiting from the complete edition of her letters (six volumes, 1983–1994), has established Fuller as a key theorist of gender, a pioneering cultural critic, and a transatlantic cosmopolitan whose engagement with the Italian Risorgimento anticipated later intersections of American literature and international revolution.
