## Biography

Horace Greeley (1811–1872) was an American newspaper editor, reformer, and political figure.
Born in Amherst, New Hampshire, he was largely self-educated and began his career as a printer's apprentice
in Vermont before moving to New York City in 1831.

In 1841, Greeley founded the *New-York Tribune*, which became the most influential newspaper
in the United States during the antebellum period. Through its weekly edition, distributed nationally,
Greeley's editorial voice reached hundreds of thousands of readers, shaping public opinion on
issues including [[abolitionism]], temperance, and land reform.

## Literary Career

Greeley's most famous editorial advocacy was for Western expansion, encapsulated in the phrase
often attributed to him: "Go West, young man, and grow up with the country" (001-025).
His editorials combined moral earnestness with practical argument, influencing the newly formed
[[republican-party]] and its anti-slavery platform.

Beyond journalism, Greeley authored several books, including *The American Conflict* (1864–1866),
a history of the [[american-civil-war|civil-war]], and *Recollections of a Busy Life* (1868), his autobiography.
His writing style was direct and forceful, eschewing ornament for clarity and conviction.

In 1872, he was the presidential nominee of the Liberal Republican Party against
[[ulysses-s-grant]], losing in a landslide and dying shortly after the election.

## Critical Reception

Greeley's contemporaries viewed him with a mixture of admiration and amused skepticism —
his eccentric personal appearance and passionate convictions made him a frequent subject
of caricature. Yet even his critics acknowledged the *Tribune*'s enormous influence on
American political discourse.

Modern scholarship recognizes Greeley as a foundational figure in American journalism,
whose editorials helped define the moral vocabulary of the anti-slavery movement and
whose commitment to reform journalism set a precedent for later crusading editors.
