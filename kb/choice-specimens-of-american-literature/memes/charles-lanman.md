# Charles Lanman

**Charles Lanman** (1819–1895) was an American essayist, travel writer, and naturalist whose writings celebrated the landscapes and wildlife of North America. His work bridged the romantic sensibility of mid-century American letters with careful natural observation.

## Biography

Charles Lanman was born in 1819 in Monroe, Michigan. He studied at the Monroe Academy before pursuing a career in journalism and literature. Lanman served as librarian for the War Department and later as secretary to Daniel Webster, which brought him into contact with the leading political and literary figures of the day. He was also an accomplished landscape painter, exhibiting at the National Academy of Design. Lanman died in 1895 at the age of seventy-six.

## Literary Career

Lanman's most notable early work, *Essays for Summer Hours* (1841), collected his nature essays and travel sketches. He went on to publish numerous volumes including travel narratives, biographical compendia such as the *Dictionary of the United States Congress*, and essays on the natural world. His writing was characterized by a painterly attention to detail and a deep appreciation for the American landscape. He contributed to major periodicals of the era and was recognized as a leading travel writer of his generation.

## Selections

### The Mocking-Bird's Song

From Lanman's natural history essays, this passage captures the extraordinary power of the mocking-bird's song to command the attention of other birds — a phenomenon he insists is not a fancy but observable fact.

> [054-016] This curious phenomenon I have witnessed many times since. Even in the morning choir, when every little throat seems strained in emulation, if the mocking-bird breathes forth in one of its mad, bewildered, and bewildering extravaganzas, the other birds pause almost invariably, and remain silent until his song is done. This, I assure you, is no figment of the imagination, or illusion of an excited fancy; it is just as substantial a fact as any other one in natural history. Whether the other birds stop from envy, as has been said, or from awe, cannot be so well ascertained, but I believe it is from the sentiment of awe, for as I certainly have felt it myself in listening to the mocking-bird, I do not know why these inferior creatures should not also.

Lanman's insistence that the phenomenon is "no figment of the imagination" reflects the tension in mid-nineteenth-century nature writing between romantic enthusiasm and the emerging standards of empirical observation — a tension that characterized much of American natural history prose in the decades before Darwin.

## Critical Reception

Lanman was well-regarded in his own time as a travel writer and nature essayist. His *Dictionary of the United States Congress* became a standard reference work. While his literary reputation did not endure into the twentieth century at the level of contemporaries such as [[Henry David Thoreau]] or [[John James Audubon]], his essays remain valued documents of the mid-nineteenth-century American landscape. His dual career as writer and painter placed him in the tradition of the American artist-naturalist.
