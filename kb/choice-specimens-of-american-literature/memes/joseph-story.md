# Joseph Story

## Biography

Joseph Story (1779–1845) was an American jurist who served as an Associate Justice of the United States Supreme Court from 1811 to 1845, and simultaneously as the Dane Professor of Law at Harvard University. Appointed to the Court by President James Madison at the age of thirty-two — the youngest justice ever confirmed to the Supreme Court — Story became one of the most influential legal minds in American history.

Born in Marblehead, Massachusetts, Story graduated from Harvard College in 1798 and was admitted to the bar in 1801. He served briefly in the Massachusetts legislature and in the U.S. House of Representatives before his appointment to the Supreme Court. On the bench, Story became Chief Justice John Marshall's most trusted ally and intellectual partner, writing opinions that extended federal power, protected contracts, and defined the limits of state authority. His opinion in *Martin v. Hunter's Lessee* (1816) established the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction over state court decisions involving federal questions — a pillar of American judicial federalism.

Story's simultaneous career at Harvard Law School, which he joined in 1829, transformed American legal education. His *Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States* (1833), *Commentaries on the Conflict of Laws* (1834), and numerous other treatises systematized vast areas of American law and were cited as authorities in courts throughout the English-speaking world. Story was also a founding donor and first vice president of Mount Auburn Cemetery, a president of the Massachusetts Historical Society, and a prolific contributor to the *North American Review*. He died in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1845.

## Literary Career

Story wrote with a clarity and moral purpose that elevated legal prose to literature. His opinions and treatises are marked by exhaustive learning worn lightly, a Ciceronian sense of rhetorical structure, and a conviction that law must serve the higher ends of justice and national unity. He was as comfortable weaving classical allusions into a Supreme Court opinion as he was distilling complex legal doctrines for the benefit of students.

The selections in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" are drawn from Story's "Miscellaneous Writings" and showcase two dimensions of his literary character. The first, "Chief Justice Marshall," is a biographical tribute that doubles as an anatomy of judicial greatness. Story begins by asking what combination of qualities could possibly account for the singular stature of his friend and colleague:

> When can we expect to be permitted to behold again so much moderation united with so much firmness, so much sagacity with so much modesty, so much learning with so much experience, so much solid wisdom with so much purity, so much of every thing to love and admire, with nothing — absolutely nothing, to regret? What, indeed, strikes us as the most remarkable in his whole character, even more than his splendid talents, is the entire consistency of his public life and principles. (019-006)

Story's portrait of Marshall rests not on abstract virtues but on concrete political steadiness — the rare capacity to stand firm against both the seductions of ambition and the pressures of partisan fury:

> Amid the extravagances of party spirit he has stood with a calm, and steady inflexibility, neither bending to the pressure of adversity, nor bounding with the elasticity of success. He has lived as such a man should live, (and yet, how few deserve the commendation!) by and with his principles. (019-006)

The passage culminates in a claim that would prove prescient: "The constitution, since its adoption, owes more to him than to any other single mind, for its true interpretation and vindication" (019-006). Story concludes by observing that Marshall's greatness, far from diminishing on closer inspection, only deepens — that "in the domestic circle he is exactly what a wife, a child, a brother, and a friend would most desire."

The second selection, "Dignity of American Jurisprudence," is a compact statement of the principle that Story himself helped establish — judicial review — and a reminder, written in an age when democracies were still experimental, of what made American government distinctive:

> The most delicate, and at the same time the proudest attribute of American jurisprudence, is the right of its judicial tribunals to decide questions of constitutional law. In other governments these questions cannot be entertained or decided by courts of justice; and, therefore, whatever may be the theory of the constitution, the legislative authority is practically omnipotent. (019-007)

Against the European model of legislative supremacy, Story sets the American innovation of constitutional adjudication — and attaches to it a democratic dignity: "the privilege of bringing every law to the test of the constitution belongs to the humblest citizen, who owes no obedience to any legislative act which transcends the constitutional limits" (019-007).

## Selections in This Anthology

- "Chief Justice Marshall" (019-006) — From the "Miscellaneous Writings." A biographical tribute to John Marshall, celebrating his judicial wisdom, moral consistency, and domestic character. Story finds the essence of Marshall's greatness in the union of firmness and moderation, learning and modesty.
- "Dignity of American Jurisprudence" (019-007) — From the "Miscellanies." A defense of judicial review as the defining attribute of American constitutional government, elevating the humblest citizen's right to challenge unconstitutional laws.

## Critical Reception

Story's reputation as a writer has always been intertwined with his stature as a jurist. His *Commentaries on the Constitution* were acknowledged by contemporaries on both sides of the Atlantic as a work of magisterial learning — "the most thorough and exhaustive treatise on the subject," in the words of the English jurist Sir Frederick Pollock. Story's ability to combine technical precision with moral vision made his legal writings accessible to a broad audience, and his opinions remain among the most frequently cited of any nineteenth-century justice.

Modern legal scholars have both celebrated and critiqued Story's legacy. His nationalism and his opinions favoring federal power over state prerogatives placed him on what would become the winning side of American constitutional development, but his views on slavery — he enforced the Fugitive Slave Act in *Prigg v. Pennsylvania* (1842) while personally abhorring the institution — have been the subject of sustained critical scrutiny. Yet Story's contributions to the intellectual architecture of American law — the doctrines of judicial review, federal common law, the conflict of laws, and the very idea of law as a learned discipline — remain foundational. He is remembered as the scholar of the Marshall Court, the jurist who gave the new republic's legal system its first comprehensive articulation.
