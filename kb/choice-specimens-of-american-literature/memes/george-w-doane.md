# George W. Doane

## Biography

George Washington Doane (1799–1859) was an American Episcopal bishop, educator, and hymn writer. Born in Trenton, New Jersey, he was named for the first president, who had died only weeks before his birth. Doane graduated from Union College in 1818, studied theology under Bishop John Henry Hobart, and was ordained a deacon in 1821 and a priest in 1823.

Doane's early ministry included service at Trinity Church, New York, where he assisted the rector and founded a Sunday school that became a model for the diocese. In 1828 he moved to Boston to become rector of Trinity Church, and in 1832 he was elected the second Bishop of New Jersey. He would hold this position for the remainder of his life.

As bishop, Doane was a vigorous institution-builder. He founded St. Mary's Hall (1837) in Burlington, New Jersey, one of the first schools for girls in the United States, and Burlington College (1846), an Episcopal college for men. Both institutions reflected his conviction that the church had a duty to educate the young in a Christian setting. Doane was a leader of the High Church or Oxford Movement wing of American Episcopalianism, and his theological convictions — emphasizing apostolic succession, the sacraments, and the dignity of the liturgy — generated controversy within his diocese but also gave his episcopate a distinctive character.

## Literary Career

Doane's literary contributions were primarily in the field of hymnody, where he is remembered for two hymns that have endured in Protestant worship. "Softly Now the Light of Day" — published in his 1824 collection *Songs by the Way* — is an evening hymn of great simplicity and devotion, sung to this day in churches around the world.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* reproduces the complete text of this hymn:

> Softly now the light of day / Fades upon my sight away; / Free from care, from labor free, / Lord, I would commune with thee. (068-028)

> Thou, whose all-pervading eye / Nought escapes, without, within, / Pardon each infirmity, / Open fault, and secret sin. (068-028)

> Soon for me the light of day / Shall forever pass away; / Then, from sin and sorrow free, / Take me, Lord, to dwell with thee! (068-028)

The hymn's structure — three quatrains moving from the fading of daylight, through confession of sin, to anticipation of death and eternal life — follows the classic pattern of the evening hymn, a genre perfected by the English hymnodist Thomas Ken in the seventeenth century. Doane's language is plain but polished, avoiding the elaborate classical machinery that characterizes much antebellum religious verse.

Doane also published *Songs by the Way* (1824), a collection of his early poems, and numerous episcopal charges, sermons, and addresses collected in several volumes over the course of his career.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Evening* (068-028) — From *Songs by the Way* (1824). The complete text of the hymn commonly known by its first line, "Softly Now the Light of Day." A short evening hymn in the tradition of Ken and Keble, progressing from the fading of natural light through an acknowledgment of human frailty to a quiet prayer for eternal rest.

## Critical Reception

Doane's hymns have proved his most enduring literary legacy. "Softly Now the Light of Day" has been set to music by numerous composers and appears in hymnals across denominational lines. His other well-known hymn, "Fling Out the Banner! Let It Float," was written in 1848 for a missionary occasion and became a staple of Protestant mission hymns.

As a prose writer, Doane's episcopal charges were admired in their time for their combination of pastoral warmth and theological learning, though they have been less read outside Episcopalian circles. His role in the Oxford Movement in America made him a significant, if controversial, figure in nineteenth-century church history; recent scholarship has emphasized his contributions to American education and the development of Episcopal identity in the early republic.
