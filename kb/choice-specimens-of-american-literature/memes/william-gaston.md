# William Gaston

## Biography

William Gaston (1778–1844) was an American jurist, politician, and writer. Born in New Bern, North Carolina, he was educated at Georgetown College (the first student to enroll there) and later at the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University). He studied law and was admitted to the bar in 1798.

Gaston served in the North Carolina General Assembly and in the United States House of Representatives (1813–1817). He later became a justice of the North Carolina Supreme Court, where he delivered opinions that shaped the state's jurisprudence for decades. A devout Catholic in a predominantly Protestant state, Gaston was also a prominent advocate for religious toleration. His personal integrity and eloquence earned him the respect of colleagues across political and sectarian lines.

## Literary Career

Gaston's literary reputation rests primarily on his orations and essays, collected posthumously as *Essays and Addresses*. Among these, "The Importance of Integrity" is the most frequently anthologized. The essay combines the moral seriousness of the eighteenth-century sermon tradition with the rhetorical polish of classical oratory.

Gaston was also known for his judicial opinions, which were praised for their clarity and moral gravity. His address to the students of the University of North Carolina, from which "The Importance of Integrity" is drawn, was widely reprinted and used as a moral primer for young men entering public life.

## Selections

In the *Choice Specimens* anthology, Gaston is represented by "The Importance of Integrity" (032-014), a passage that opens with an emphatic declaration:

> The first great maxim of human conduct — that which it is all-important to impress on the understandings of young men, and recommend to their hearty adoption — is, above all things, in all circumstances, and under every emergency, to preserve a clean heart and an honest purpose. (032-014)

Gaston distinguishes between the "vulgar virtue" of mere transactional honesty and the higher integrity that "however tempting the opportunity, or however secure against detection, no selfishness nor resentment, no lust of power, place, favor, profit, or pleasure, can cause to swerve from the strict rule of right" (032-014). This, he concludes, "is the perfection of man's moral nature."

The piece is preceded in the anthology by a passage from Joseph Dennie's "Reflections on the Seasons," which muses on the restorative power of spring — setting Gaston's moral gravity in relief against Dennie's lighter, more personal meditation.

## Critical Reception

Gaston's reputation as a writer has always been secondary to his stature as a jurist and public figure. His essays, however, represent a significant contribution to the early American tradition of moral and civic discourse. "The Importance of Integrity" remains his most enduring legacy as a writer, a compact statement of the civic-republican ideal of public virtue.
