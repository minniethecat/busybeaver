# George Ticknor

## Biography

George Ticknor (1791–1871) was an American literary scholar, Hispanist, and educator, best known for his *History of Spanish Literature* (1849), a work that established Spanish literary studies in the English-speaking world. Born in Boston, Massachusetts, Ticknor graduated from Dartmouth College in 1807 and practiced law briefly before turning to literary scholarship.

In 1815, Ticknor traveled to Europe, where he spent four years studying at the University of Göttingen — becoming, along with Edward Everett, one of the first Americans to pursue advanced study at a German university. His exposure to German philological methods profoundly shaped his approach to literary history. In 1819, he returned to Harvard as the first Smith Professor of the French and Spanish Languages and Literatures, a position he held until 1835.

Ticknor's *History of Spanish Literature*, based on lectures delivered at Harvard, was the first comprehensive survey of its subject in any language. Translated into Spanish, French, and German, it earned Ticknor an international reputation and remains a landmark of nineteenth-century literary scholarship. He was also instrumental in the founding of the Boston Public Library, serving on its board of trustees and helping to shape its collecting philosophy. Ticknor's home on Park Street in Boston became a center of literary society, visited by figures including Charles Dickens, William Makepeace Thackeray, and Washington Irving.

## Literary Career

Ticknor's prose is characterized by breadth of learning, clarity of judgment, and an urbane, balanced tone. He wrote literary history not as a catalog of names and dates but as a narrative of national character, tracing the expression of the Spanish spirit through its successive literary forms. His method, learned from the German universities, combined exhaustive bibliography with critical synthesis.

The selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn from Ticknor's analysis of Cervantes's *Don Quixote*. He begins by challenging the fashionable interpretation that would read the novel as a universal allegory of the conflict between idealism and reality:

> His purpose in writing the Don Quixote has sometimes been enlarged by the ingenuity of a refined criticism, until it has been made to embrace the whole of the endless contrast between the poetical and the prosaic in our natures, — between heroism and generosity on one side, as if they were mere illusions, and a cold selfishness on the other, as if it were the truth and reality of life. But this is a metaphysical conclusion drawn from views of the work at once imperfect and exaggerated; a conclusion contrary to the spirit of the age, which was not given to a satire so philosophical and generalizing, and contrary to the character of Cervantes himself. (039-009)

Against the metaphysical reading, Ticknor insists on the plain, practical intention that Cervantes himself declared:

> At the very beginning of the work, he announces it to be his sole purpose to break down the vogue and authority of books of chivalry, and at the end of the whole he declares anew in his own person, that "he had no other desire than to render abhorred of men the false and absurd stories contained in books of chivalry;" exulting in his success as an achievement of no small moment. (039-010)

Ticknor measures the magnitude of this achievement by the depth of the chivalric craze it overcame, noting that "the fanaticism for these romances was so great in Spain, during the sixteenth century, as to have become matter of alarm to the more judicious" (039-010). The success of Cervantes's undertaking was immediate and complete:

> No book of chivalry was written after the appearance of Don Quixote, in 1605; and from the same date, even those already enjoying the greatest favor ceased to be reprinted; so that, what is acknowledged to be one of the most remarkable and successful works of human genius suddenly crushed the whole class of literature out of which it had grown. (039-011)

The passage closes with an appreciation of Cervantes's method — the simple device of a country gentleman driven mad by reading romances:

> The general plan Cervantes adopted to accomplish this object, without, perhaps, foreseeing its whole course, and still less all its results, was simple and obvious: a country gentleman of La Mancha, full of genuine Castilian honor and enthusiasm, gentle and dignified in his whole nature, is represented as so completely crazed by reading the romances of chivalry that he believes them to be true. (039-012)

## Critical Reception

Ticknor's *History of Spanish Literature* was hailed on both sides of the Atlantic as a work of unprecedented scope and learning. William H. Prescott praised it as "the most comprehensive and philosophical view of Spanish literature that has been offered to the public," and Spanish scholars acknowledged it as superior to any work on the subject produced in Spain itself. Ticknor was elected a corresponding member of the Spanish Royal Academy and honored by learned societies throughout Europe.

Subsequent generations of Hispanists, while acknowledging their debt to Ticknor, have inevitably revised many of his judgments. His conception of Spanish literary history has been challenged by scholars attentive to the Arabic and Jewish contributions he tended to minimize, and his critical preferences — shaped by the canons of New England Unitarianism — have been subjected to ideological scrutiny. Yet the *History* remains an imposing monument of nineteenth-century American scholarship, and Ticknor's place as the founder of Hispanism in the United States is secure.
