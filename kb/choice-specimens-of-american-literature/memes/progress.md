# Progress in American Literature

## Definition

Progress, in American literary thought, denotes the conviction that human history tends toward improvement—in knowledge, morality, political institutions, and material welfare. This optimistic faith, rooted in Enlightenment philosophy and Christian providentialism, pervades American writing from the colonial period onward.

## Historical Context

The idea of progress drew on both secular Enlightenment thought and Christian millennialism. Jonathan Edwards fused these traditions, describing how "religion, liberty, and reason have been bending their course" through history toward a brighter future (015-001). The American Revolution was widely interpreted as evidence of progress—the establishment of a republic founded on reason and natural rights. In the 19th century, technological innovation, territorial expansion, and social reform movements all fed the narrative of American progress.

## Key Authors and Works

- **Jonathan Edwards**, in his reflections on the progress of religion, articulated a vision of history as the unfolding of divine purpose through human affairs, with America playing a providential role (015-001).
- **Joel Barlow**, in "The Vision of Columbus" and "The Columbiad," celebrated American progress in epic verse, treating the discovery and settlement of America as a decisive advance in human history.
- **The Revolutionary generation** interpreted the events of their time as evidence of progress, with writers rejoicing "in the efforts which you make to promote the cause of liberty" as advancing human welfare (015-007).

## Legacy

The faith in progress became a defining feature of 19th-century American thought, influencing everything from Manifest Destiny to technological utopianism. Writers like Emerson and Whitman celebrated American dynamism, while critics like Hawthorne and Melville questioned whether material progress truly advanced human happiness. This debate—between faith in progress and skepticism of its costs—remains central to American literary culture.
