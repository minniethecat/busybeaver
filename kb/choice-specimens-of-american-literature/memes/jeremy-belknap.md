## Biography

Jeremy Belknap (1744–1798) was an American clergyman and historian, best known as the author of *The History of New Hampshire* (1784–1792) and as the founder of the Massachusetts Historical Society in 1791. Born in Boston, Massachusetts, he was educated at Harvard College, graduating in 1762.

Belknap served as a Congregational minister in Dover, New Hampshire, and later at the Federal Street Church in Boston. His pastoral work brought him into close contact with the people and landscapes of New England, which became the subject of his historical and natural history writings.

## Literary Career

Belknap's *The History of New Hampshire*, published in three volumes between 1784 and 1792, was one of the earliest and most thorough state histories produced in the United States. It combined political narrative with detailed observations on geography, natural resources, and the customs of the inhabitants. The work was noted for its careful use of documentary sources and its balanced treatment of colonial and Revolutionary history.

He also compiled *American Biography* (1794–1798), a pioneering collection of biographical sketches of notable Americans. As the founder of the Massachusetts Historical Society — the first historical society in the United States — Belknap helped to establish the professional practice of history in the new republic.

## Selections in the Corpus

Belknap appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* through an excerpt from his *History of New Hampshire*, titled "The Mast Pine" (selection 113).

> Another thing worthy of observation is the aged and majestic appearance of the trees, of which the most noble is the mast pine. This tree often grows to the height of one hundred and fifty, and sometimes two hundred feet. It is straight as an arrow, and has no branches but very near the top. It is from twenty to forty inches in diameter at its base, and appears like a stately pillar, adorned with a verdant capital, in form of a cone. (024-015)

> When a mast tree is to be felled, much preparation is necessary. So tall a stick, without any limbs nearer the ground than eighty or a hundred feet, is in great danger of breaking in the fall. To prevent this the workmen have a contrivance which they call bedding the tree, which is thus executed. They know in what direction the tree will fall; and they cut down a number of smaller trees which grow in that direction... (024-016)

The selection illustrates Belknap's ability to combine precise natural observation with clear, accessible prose, reflecting the Enlightenment ideal of useful knowledge applied to the resources of the new nation.

## Style and Themes

Belknap wrote in the plain, factual style characteristic of 18th-century Enlightenment historiography. His prose was direct and unadorned, prioritizing accuracy and utility over rhetorical flourish. His work reflected a deep interest in the natural world and a conviction that knowledge of the land and its resources was essential to the cultivation of civic virtue in a republic.

## Reception and Legacy

*The History of New Hampshire* was widely praised by contemporaries, including George Washington, who commended Belknap's diligence. The Massachusetts Historical Society, which Belknap founded, became the model for state and local historical societies across the United States. Belknap's emphasis on primary sources and documentary evidence anticipated the professional standards of later American historiography.

Belknap died in 1798 at the age of fifty-four. His papers and correspondence, preserved by the Massachusetts Historical Society, remain an important source for the study of early American intellectual life.
