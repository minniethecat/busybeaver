# Virtue in American Literature

## Definition

Virtue, in American literary tradition, denotes the moral qualities deemed essential for both individual character and republican self-government. The concept bridges classical republican ideals—civic virtue as dedication to the common good—with Protestant ethics of personal piety, industry, and temperance.

## Historical Context

Puritan writers emphasized virtue as conformity to divine law, cultivated through self-examination and discipline (004-004). Revolutionary-era writers reframed virtue in republican terms: a free people must be a virtuous people, capable of self-restraint and public-spiritedness, or liberty would degenerate into license. This synthesis of classical and Christian virtue became a hallmark of early American political thought.

## Key Authors and Works

- **John Winthrop** articulated the Puritan ideal of virtue as communal righteousness, bound by covenant to God and one another (004-004).
- **Benjamin Franklin**, in his "Autobiography" and "Poor Richard's Almanack," reframed virtue in practical, bourgeois terms—industry, frugality, and temperance as the path to both personal success and public usefulness.
- **Timothy Dwight** warned that prosperity without virtue would corrupt the Republic, arguing that national happiness depends on "truth, virtue, and God" (005-012).
- **Jonathan Edwards** connected personal virtue to religious conversion, emphasizing the inward transformation of the affections rather than mere outward compliance.

## Related Concepts

- [[religious-liberty]] — The freedom of conscience seen by many Puritan writers as essential to genuine virtue
- [[civic-duty]] — The practical expression of virtue in public life and political participation
- [[democracy]] — The system of government that depends upon the virtue of its citizens for its survival

## Legacy

The ideal of republican virtue profoundly shaped American civic culture. The 19th century saw the concept challenged by commercial expansion, urbanization, and slavery—each testing whether virtue could survive prosperity and power. Writers from Emerson to Lincoln continued to wrestle with this tension, making virtue a perennial theme of American letters.
