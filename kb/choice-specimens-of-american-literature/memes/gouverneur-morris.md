# Gouverneur Morris

## Biography

Gouverneur Morris (1752–1816) was an American statesman, diplomat, and a Founding Father of the United States, best known for penning the Preamble to the Constitution and for serving as minister to France during the French Revolution. Born at Morrisania, his family's estate in what is now the Bronx, New York, he graduated from King's College (now Columbia University) in 1768, was admitted to the bar in 1771, and quickly entered public life.

Elected to the New York Provincial Congress in 1775, Morris helped draft the state's first constitution. He served in the Continental Congress from 1778 to 1779, where he was a signer of the Articles of Confederation. As assistant superintendent of finance under Robert Morris (no relation) from 1781 to 1785, he proposed the decimal coinage system that later became the basis of the U.S. dollar. In 1787, as a delegate from Pennsylvania to the Constitutional Convention, Morris gave more speeches than any other delegate — one hundred and seventy-three — and chaired the Committee of Style, which produced the final draft of the Constitution. The memorable opening phrase, "We the People of the United States," is attributed to his pen.

In 1792, President Washington appointed him minister plenipotentiary to France, where Morris witnessed the French Revolution at close range. His detailed diaries from this period are among the most vivid primary sources on revolutionary Paris. He was the only foreign diplomat to remain in the city during the Reign of Terror, and he later recorded his impressions with characteristic wit and skepticism. After returning to America, he served briefly in the U.S. Senate (1800–1803) and chaired the Erie Canal Commission, whose work would transform the economy of New York and the nation.

## Literary Career

Morris's literary reputation rests on his political oratory, his diplomatic correspondence, and his private journals. As an orator, he combined classical rhetorical training with a pungent, epigrammatic wit. His speeches at the Constitutional Convention were recorded in Madison's notes and reveal a thinker deeply engaged with the problem of balancing democratic energy against the need for stable institutions.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," drawn from a speech on the qualification of foreign ministers, addresses the nature of political duty with a deliberative gravity characteristic of the founding generation:

> A minister of foreign affairs should have a genius quick, lively, penetrating; should write on all occasions with clearness and perspicuity; be capable of expressing his sentiments with dignity, and conceiving his designs with boldness and ability. (015-010)

But it is his meditation on patriotism — probing the question "What is patriotism?" — that has proved most enduring. Morris refuses the parochial definition of patriotism as mere attachment to one's birthplace and insists upon a broader, more demanding ideal:

> What is patriotism? Is it a narrow affection for the spot where a man was born? Are the very clods where we tread entitled to this ardent preference, because they are greener? No, sir, this is not the character of the virtue, and it soars higher for its object. It is an extended self-love, mingling with all the enjoyments of life, and twisting itself with the minutest filaments of the heart. (015-009)

For Morris, patriotism is inseparable from the rule of law and the honor of one's country:

> Every good citizen makes that honor his own, and cherishes it not only as precious, but as sacred. He is willing to risk his life in its defence; and is conscious that he gains protection, while he gives it. For what rights of a citizen will be deemed inviolable, when a state renounces the principles that constitute their security? (015-009)

## Critical Reception

Morris's contemporaries regarded him as one of the most brilliant conversationalists of his age, though his aristocratic manner and acerbic tongue made him a polarizing figure. John Adams, no easy critic, called him "a man of genius." Modern scholarship has emphasized his role at the Constitutional Convention, where his speeches — though often at odds with democratic orthodoxy — forced delegates to confront the tensions between popular sovereignty and effective governance. His Paris diaries, published as *A Diary of the French Revolution*, remain indispensable to historians of the period. The diplomat and historian George Bancroft described Morris as "the most original and independent mind" among the Framers.
