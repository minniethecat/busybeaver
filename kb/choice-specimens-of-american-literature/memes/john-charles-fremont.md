# John Charles Frémont

**John Charles Frémont** (1813–1890) was an American explorer, military officer, and politician who led multiple expeditions into the American West, earning the nickname "The Pathfinder." He was the first Republican Party candidate for President of the United States and played a controversial role in the conquest of California during the Mexican-American War.

## Biography

John Charles Frémont was born in 1813 in Savannah, Georgia. He joined the United States Topographical Corps in 1838 and soon became involved in surveying expeditions. Under the patronage of Senator Thomas Hart Benton, whose daughter Jessie Benton he married, Frémont led three major expeditions to the Rocky Mountains and beyond between 1842 and 1846. His vividly written expedition reports, edited by his wife, became widely popular and helped fuel westward migration. During the Civil War, he served as a Union general. He died in 1890 at the age of seventy-seven.

## Literary Career

Frémont's literary reputation rests on his *Report of the Exploring Expedition to the Rocky Mountains* (1845) and subsequent expedition narratives. These reports combined scientific observation — documenting geography, botany, and geology — with dramatic accounts of adventure and discovery. The reports were edited by his wife Jessie Benton Frémont, whose literary skill transformed them from dry government documents into widely read narratives that captured the public imagination and inspired a generation of westward migrants.

## Selections

### Mystery of the Ancient Ruins

During one of his expeditions across the Rocky Mountain region, Frémont encountered the ruins of an ancient civilization and reflected on the unknown builders whose structures challenged the assumption that America had been "peopled by savages."

> [055-007] We sat down on the very edge of the wall, and strove in vain to penetrate the mystery by which we were surrounded. Who were the people that built this city? In the ruined cities of Egypt — even in the long lost Petra, the stranger knows the story of the people whose vestiges are around him. America, say historians, was peopled by savages; but savages never reared these structures, savages never carved these stones.

This passage captures the intellectual dilemma faced by nineteenth-century explorers whose discoveries of ancient ruins in the Americas posed a direct challenge to prevailing narratives about the continent's pre-Columbian inhabitants. Frémont's rhetorical question — "Who were the people that built this city?" — echoes a debate that would continue into the era of modern archaeology.

## Critical Reception

Frémont's expedition reports were widely celebrated in their day, contributing to his popularity as a public figure. While his military and political legacy has been subject to reassessment, his expedition narratives remain valuable historical documents of the American West at a pivotal moment of exploration and expansion. Modern readers recognize the collaborative literary role of Jessie Benton Frémont, whose editorial contributions significantly elevated the prose of the published reports.
