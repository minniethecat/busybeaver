# Alexander Wilson

Alexander Wilson (1766–1813) was a Scottish-American poet, naturalist, and ornithologist, celebrated as the father of American ornithology. His monumental *American Ornithology* (9 volumes, 1808–1814) documented and illustrated the birds of North America with a combination of scientific precision and literary grace that influenced generations of naturalist-writers.

Before turning to ornithology, Wilson was a weaver, peddler, and poet in Scotland, publishing poems in the Robert Burns tradition of vernacular verse. His migration to America in 1794 proved transformative: meeting the naturalist William Bartram near Philadelphia, he resolved to document every bird species in the United States.

## Biography

Wilson was born on July 6, 1766, in Paisley, Scotland. The son of a weaver, he worked in the textile trade while composing poetry in his spare time. His satirical poem "The Shark, or Lang Mills Detected" got him into trouble with authorities, and he emigrated to America in 1794.

Settling near Philadelphia, Wilson taught school and continued writing poetry. A decisive encounter with [[william-bartram]], the Philadelphia naturalist, at Bartram's botanical garden turned his interests toward ornithology. Bartram encouraged Wilson's ambition to create a comprehensive illustrated catalog of American birds.

## Literary Career

Wilson's *American Ornithology* was a landmark achievement. Between 1808 and 1814, he published nine volumes containing descriptions and hand-colored engravings of 268 species, 26 of which he was the first to describe. Wilson did much of the engraving and coloring himself, traveling thousands of miles on foot across the eastern United States to observe and collect specimens.

His prose in *American Ornithology* departs from the dry taxonomic style of earlier natural histories, offering vivid descriptions that convey the living bird in its habitat. His account of the bald eagle attacking a fish hawk (osprey) is among the most frequently anthologized passages of nineteenth-century American nature writing.

Unlike his successor [[john-j-audubon]], whose illustrations would surpass his in artistic ambition, Wilson came to ornithology from literature rather than art. His prose reveals a poet's eye for detail and a naturalist's precision.

## Selections in the Anthology

The anthology includes two extended passages from Wilson's *American Ornithology*.

[053-001]The first describes the wood ibis in a tone of solitary contemplation:

> This solitary bird does not associate in flocks, but is generally seen alone, commonly near the banks of great rivers, in vast marshes or meadows... He stands alone on the topmost limb of tall dead cypress trees, his neck contracted or drawn in upon his shoulders, and beak resting like a long scythe upon his breast: in this pensive posture and solitary situation, it looks extremely grave, sorrowful, and melancholy, as if in the deepest thought.

[053-003]The second and more famous passage depicts the bald eagle's aerial combat with the fish hawk:

> Elevated on the high dead limb of some gigantic tree that commands a wide view of the neighboring shore and ocean, he seems calmly to contemplate the motions of the various feathered tribes that pursue their busy avocations below... Down, rapid as an arrow from heaven, descends the distant object of his attention, the roar of its wings reaching the ear as it disappears in the deep, making the surges foam around... The unincumbered Eagle rapidly advances, and is just on the point of reaching his opponent, when, with a sudden scream, probably of despair and honest execration, the latter drops his fish; the Eagle, poising himself for a moment as if to take a more certain aim, descends like a whirlwind, snatches it in his grasp ere it reaches the water, and bears his ill-gotten booty silently away to the woods.

Wilson's eagle narrative blends close observation with dramatic pacing. The eagle is not romanticized but described with clinical precision, yet the prose achieves the suspense of epic contest.

## Critical Reception

Wilson's *American Ornithology* was warmly received on both sides of the Atlantic. It earned the praise of Thomas Jefferson, who corresponded with Wilson about its scientific merits. The work went through multiple editions in the United States and Britain.

Later ornithologists, including Audubon, acknowledged their debt to Wilson. While Audubon's *Birds of America* ultimately surpassed Wilson's in scope and artistic ambition, Wilson's prose descriptions remain unmatched for their combination of scientific detail and literary sensibility.
