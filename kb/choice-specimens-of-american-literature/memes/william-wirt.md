# William Wirt

## Biography

William Wirt (1772–1834) was an American author, lawyer, and statesman who served as the ninth Attorney General of the United States (1817–1829), the longest tenure in that office's history. Born in Bladensburg, Maryland, Wirt was orphaned at an early age and raised by relatives. He was admitted to the Virginia bar in 1792 and quickly established himself as one of the most accomplished attorneys in the country.

As a prosecutor in the treason trial of Aaron Burr (1807), Wirt delivered an address that cemented his reputation as an orator of the first rank. President James Monroe appointed him Attorney General in 1817, and he continued to serve under President John Quincy Adams. In 1832, Wirt was the Anti-Masonic Party's nominee for President of the United States, a campaign that reflected his lifelong commitment to principled, if sometimes unconventional, public service.

## Literary Career

Wirt's literary reputation rests principally on two works: *The Letters of the British Spy* (1803), a series of observational essays modeled on the Spectator tradition, and *Sketches of the Life and Character of Patrick Henry* (1817), the first full-length biography of the Virginia revolutionary. The *Letters* established Wirt as a master of the prose essay, blending wit, social observation, and moral reflection. The *Life of Patrick Henry* became the foundational text of the Henry legend — it was Wirt who shaped the national memory of Henry as the impassioned champion of liberty.

In the biographical sketch selected for "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Wirt reflects on Henry's unpromising youth:

> I cannot learn that he gave in his youth any evidence of that precocity which sometimes distinguishes uncommon genius. His companions recollect no instance of premature wit, no striking sentiment, no flash of fancy, no remarkable beauty or strength of expression, and no indication however slight, either of that impassioned love of liberty, or of that adventurous daring and intrepidity, which marked so strongly his future character. (037-003)

Wirt describes Henry's early life as marked by "coarse" person, "uncommonly awkward" manners, "slovenly" dress, and an "invincible" aversion to study (037-003). Henry "ran wild in the forest like one of the Aborigines of the country, and divided his life between the dissipation and uproar of the chase, and the languor of inaction" (037-003). Yet Wirt's purpose is not merely descriptive. Having established the unpromising beginning, he draws a pointed moral lesson:

> Let not the youthful reader, however, deduce from the example of Mr. Henry, an argument in favor of indolence, and the contempt of study. Let him remember that the powers which surmounted the disadvantage of those early habits, were such as very rarely appear upon this earth. Let him remember, too, how long the genius even of Mr. Henry was kept down, and hidden from the public view, by the sorcery of those pernicious habits; through what years of poverty and wretchedness they doomed him to struggle. (037-005)

This passage exemplifies Wirt's characteristic prose — lucid, balanced, and morally earnest. The biographer turns the apparent exception of Henry's genius into a reinforcement of conventional wisdom about the value of education and industry.

Wirt's "Eulogium on Adams and Jefferson" demonstrates a different facet of his literary gift: descriptive elegance. His sketch of Jefferson's Monticello offers a detailed portrait of the retired statesman's home as a museum of the Enlightenment:

> Approaching the house on the east, the visitor instinctively paused, to cast around one thrilling glance at this magnificent panorama, and then passed to the vestibule, where, if he had not been previously informed, he would immediately perceive that he was entering the house of no common man. In the spacious and lofty hall which opens before him, he marks no tawdry and unmeaning ornaments, but before, on the right, on the left, all around, the eye is struck and gratified with objects of science and taste, so classed and arranged as to produce their finest effect. (037-006)

Wirt inventories the collections: sculptures charting "the historical progress of that art, from the first rude attempts of the aborigines of our country up to that exquisite and finished bust of the great patriot himself, from the master-hand of Ceracchi"; Indian artifacts — "paintings, weapons, ornaments, and manufactures"; and "the fossil productions of our country, mineral and animal, the polished remains of those colossal monsters that once trod our forests" (037-006). The visitor, lost in contemplation, is then startled by the approach of:

> ...the tall, and animated, and stately figure of the patriot himself, his countenance beaming with intelligence and benignity, and his outstretched hand, with its strong and cordial pressure, confirming the courteous welcome of his lips; and then came that charm of manner and conversation that passes all description — so cheerful, so unassuming, so free, and easy, and frank, and kind, and gay, that even the young, and overawed, and embarrassed visitor at once forgot his fears, and felt himself by the side of an old and familiar friend. (037-008)

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from William Wirt's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Henry's Example No Argument for Indolence* (037-003–037-005) — From "Life of Patrick Henry." Wirt argues that Patrick Henry's unpromising youth — marked by indolence, aversion to study, and lack of early distinction — is no excuse for neglecting education, as Henry's genius was an exceedingly rare exception.
- *Jefferson's Seat at Monticello* (037-006–037-008) — From "Eulogium on Adams and Jefferson." A vivid description of Thomas Jefferson's home, its collections of art, science, and natural history, and the commanding yet genial presence of the retired statesman.

## Critical Reception

Wirt's *Life of Patrick Henry* was both a critical and commercial success, passing through numerous editions in the nineteenth century. While modern historians have questioned Wirt's reliance on oral tradition and his possible embellishment of Henry's speeches, the biography remains a landmark of American literary nationalism — an early and influential attempt to construct a pantheon of revolutionary heroes for the young republic.

Wirt's prose was praised by contemporaries for its elegance and moral clarity. Edgar Allan Poe, in his "Autography," noted Wirt's "fine taste and keen sense of the beautiful." Later critics have seen in Wirt a transitional figure between the Augustan prose of the eighteenth century and the romantic sensibility that would dominate American letters in the decades after his death. His influence on the art of American biography, particularly the shaping of Henry's legacy, remains a significant chapter in the literary history of the early republic.
