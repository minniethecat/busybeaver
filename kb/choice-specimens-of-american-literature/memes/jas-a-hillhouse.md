## Biography

James A. Hillhouse (1789–1841) was an American poet and dramatist who lived in New Haven, Connecticut.
According to a contemporary critic, "in accomplishment of mind and person, he was probably second to no man"
(043-003). Hillhouse was noted for his distinguished bearing and intellectual presence:
"In no part of the world have I seen a man of more distinguished mien, or of a more inborn dignity and elegance
of address" (043-003).

Hillhouse's life was divided between literary ambition and the practical demands of commerce. "Hillhouse had
fallen upon days of thrift, and many years of his life which he should have passed either in his study, or in the
councils of the nation, were enslaved to the drudgery of business" (043-004). Despite these constraints, he
eventually retired to his country estate, "the beautiful and poetic home of 'Sachem's Wood,'" where his friends
anticipated "the commencement of a ripe and long enduring career of letters" (043-004).

## Literary Career

Hillhouse's most celebrated work is the dramatic poem **Hadad**, published in 1825. The poem made a powerful
impression on contemporaries. One reader recalled that "to me it was the opening of a new heaven of imagination.
The leading characters possessed me for months, and the bright, clear, harmonious language was, for a long time,
constantly in my ears" (043-003).

His poems were described as "fully conceived, nicely balanced, exquisitely finished — works for the highest
taste to relish, and for the severest student in dramatic style to erect into a model" (043-003). Beyond poetry,
Hillhouse was also a noted orator: "Hillhouse was no less distinguished in oratory" (043-003).

At the time of his death, Hillhouse had been planning a new series of dramas, "different from all he had
attempted, upon which he designed to work with the first mood and leisure" (043-004). A contemporary lamented
his unfinished career: "his hand is cold, and his lips still, and his light, just rising to its meridian, is lost
now to the world. Love and honor to the memory of such a man" (043-004).

## Selections in the Corpus

Hillhouse is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* by an excerpt from *Hadad* (001-185).
The selection opens with the line:

> Hadad. Confide in me. (066-004)

## Critical Reception

Hillhouse's reputation among his contemporaries was substantial. The critical portrait in the corpus describes
him in almost chivalric terms: "His person was very finely proportioned, his carriage chivalric and high-bred,
and his countenance purely and brightly intellectual. Add to this a sweet voice, a stamp of high courtesy on
everything he uttered, and singular simplicity and taste in dress, and you have the portrait of one who, in other
days, would have been the mirror of chivalry, and the flower of nobles and troubadours" (043-003).

His premature death cut short what promised to be a major literary career. A friend wrote: "With his
scholarship, knowledge of life, taste, and genius, what might not have been expected from its fulfilment?"
(043-004).
