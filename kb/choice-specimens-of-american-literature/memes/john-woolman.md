# John Woolman

## Biography

John Woolman (1720–1772) was an American Quaker minister, tailor, and abolitionist whose "Journal" is widely regarded as one of the masterpieces of American spiritual autobiography. Born in Northampton, Burlington County, New Jersey, to a Quaker farming family, Woolman received a basic education and was apprenticed to a tailor — a trade he practiced throughout his life as a deliberate exercise in simplicity and self-discipline.

From an early age, Woolman experienced profound religious conviction and a growing unease with the institution of slavery, which he encountered directly when, as a young clerk, he was asked by his employer to write a bill of sale for an enslaved woman. The incident troubled his conscience deeply, and although he complied at the time, it marked the beginning of a lifelong commitment to the abolitionist cause. From the 1740s onward, Woolman traveled extensively through the American colonies — from New England to the Carolinas — visiting Quaker meetings and urging fellow Quakers to free their slaves and refuse any participation in the slave trade.

Woolman's abolitionism was not political in the modern sense but deeply spiritual. He believed that slaveholding corrupted the soul of the slaveholder and that the wealth produced by slave labor was tainted. He refused to wear dyed clothing because the dyes were often produced by enslaved labor, and he would not eat from silver vessels or use products he believed were produced through exploitation. His radical simplicity — walking rather than riding when traveling to meetings, refusing to use the postal service on the grounds that it exploited post-boys — was not asceticism for its own sake but a lived theology, an effort to make every act consistent with his principles.

Woolman died of smallpox in York, England, in 1772, while traveling among English Quakers to enlist their support for abolition. His was a life of extraordinary consistency and moral courage, and his influence on the abolitionist movement was profound: the Quaker community formally prohibited slaveholding among its members in 1776, four years after his death.

## Literary Career

Woolman's literary legacy rests primarily on his "Journal," first published posthumously in 1774 and never out of print since. The work traces his spiritual development and his travels among American Quakers, interweaving personal reflection, social critique, and theological meditation in a prose style of extraordinary simplicity and moral clarity. Charles Lamb called it "the only American book I know that deserves to be ranked with the great autobiographies of the world." William Ellery Channing said it was "beyond comparison the purest and sweetest of all autobiographies."

The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" — "Remarks on Slavery and Labor" — distills Woolman's economic and moral critique of slavery into a few paragraphs of luminous prose. He begins with a principle that was radical for its time and remains challenging: that free labor is not only more just but more productive and happier than slave labor:

> A people used to labor moderately for their living, training up their children in frugality and business, have a happier life than those who live on the labor of slaves. Freemen find satisfaction in improving and providing for their families; but negroes, laboring to support others who claim them as their property, and expecting nothing but slavery during life, have not the like inducement to be industrious. (005-004)

This is an economic argument against slavery avant la lettre — the insight that enslaved labor is inherently inefficient because it destroys the worker's motivation. But Woolman immediately moves from economics to universal principle:

> Men having power, too often misapply it: though we make slaves of the negroes, and the Turks make slaves of the Christians, liberty is the natural right of all men equally. (005-004)

The phrase "liberty is the natural right of all men equally" places Woolman squarely in the tradition of natural rights philosophy that would shape the American Revolution — yet he applies it more consistently than most of the Founders, extending it without exception to enslaved Africans. The symmetry of his moral reasoning — what Americans do to negroes, the Turks do to Christians — is a rhetorical strategy that asks his readers to recognize themselves in the position of the enslaved.

Woolman's prophetic voice emerges most clearly in his warning about the future: "The slaves look to me like a burdensome stone to such who burden themselves with them. The burden will grow heavier and heavier, till times change in a way disagreeable to us." Written in the 1760s or early 1770s, these words carry the weight of tragic foresight — a Quaker minister in colonial New Jersey seeing, with uncanny clarity, the national catastrophe that was gathering.

The passage ends on a note of characteristic Woolman introspection. Unable to find the words to speak during a meeting, he turns inward: "I was silent during the meeting for worship, and, when business came on, my mind was exercised concerning the poor slaves, but did not feel my way clear to speak. In this condition I was bowed in spirit before the Lord, and, with tears and inward supplication, besought him so to open my understanding that I might know his will concerning me; and at length my mind was settled in silence." (005-004) This is Woolman at his most distinctive: the activist who waits for divine guidance, the prophet who trusts silence as much as speech.

- "Remarks on Slavery and Labor" (005-004) — From his "Life and Travels" (the "Journal"). Woolman argues that free labor is both more just and more productive than slave labor, affirms that liberty is the natural right of all, and warns of the accumulating moral burden of slavery.

## Critical Reception

Woolman's "Journal" has attracted an extraordinary range of admirers across more than two centuries. In the nineteenth century, it was beloved by the Transcendentalists — Emerson praised it, and Thoreau's "Walden" echoes Woolman's ethic of simplicity in ways that suggest direct influence. The English essayist Charles Lamb and the poet Samuel Taylor Coleridge both ranked it among the greatest works of spiritual literature. In the twentieth century, Woolman was claimed by the civil rights movement, the peace movement, and the environmental movement — each finding in his writings a precedent for their own forms of conscientious witness.

Scholars have also attended to Woolman's literary art — the way his deceptively simple prose achieves its effects through understatement, repetition, and a rhythm drawn from the King James Bible. His "Journal" is now standard reading in courses on American literature, where it is studied alongside the writings of Jonathan Edwards and Benjamin Franklin as a foundational text of the American autobiographical tradition.
