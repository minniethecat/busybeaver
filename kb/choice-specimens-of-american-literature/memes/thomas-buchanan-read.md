# Thomas Buchanan Read

## Biography

Thomas Buchanan Read (1822–1872) was an American poet and painter born in Chester County, Pennsylvania. Largely self-educated, Read left home at an early age and worked as a grocer's clerk, a tailor's apprentice, and a cigar maker before finding his way to artistic circles in Cincinnati, where he began to study painting and publish poetry. He moved to Boston in the 1840s, then to Philadelphia, establishing himself as both a portrait painter and a poet. Read spent much of the 1850s in Italy, studying painting in Florence and Rome, and his work as a visual artist informed the pictorial quality of his verse. During the American Civil War, he returned to the United States and became famous for his patriotic poems — most notably "Sheridan's Ride," which celebrated General Philip Sheridan's dramatic ride to rally Union troops at the Battle of Cedar Creek (1864). Read's dual career as painter and poet made him a distinctive figure in mid-nineteenth-century American culture, a representative of the Romantic ideal of the artist equally fluent in word and image. He died in New York City in 1872.

## Literary Career

Read's poetry spanned pastoral idylls, patriotic ballads, and narrative verse. His major volumes include *Poems* (1847), *Lays and Ballads* (1848), *The New Pastoral* (1855), *The House by the Sea* (1856), and *Sylvia, or the Last Shepherd* (1857). Read's characteristic mode was the pastoral — poems set in idealized rural landscapes, often with classical overtones — but he also wrote forcefully about the social transformations of industrializing America. "The Mournful Mowers," his most ambitious poem in this vein, paints a darkly nostalgic picture of agricultural life disrupted by mechanization, where "steeds of steam" replace horses and the iron "mower's-car" sweeps across fields once worked by hand. The poem's refrain — "The world has lost its dewy prime; / Alas! the Golden age is dead, / And we are of the Iron time!" — channels a pervasive nineteenth-century anxiety about the social costs of industrial progress. Read's dual vocation as painter gave his poetry a strong visual sensibility; his landscapes are consistently rendered with a painter's attention to light, color, and spatial composition. "Sheridan's Ride," his most famous single poem, became one of the most recited pieces of the post-Civil War era and cemented Read's reputation as a patriotic poet.

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) includes a substantial selection of verse by Thomas Buchanan Read drawn from *Sylvia, or the Last Shepherd* and other volumes:

1. **"The Mournful Mowers"** (075-045–075-047). A pastoral elegy for a vanishing agricultural world, the poem's mowers lament the incursion of factories and steam-powered machinery into the countryside. The opening stanzas contrast the brook that "erewhile laughed abroad" with its present state, "like a felon, groaning trod / Its hundred treadmills night and day." The refrain — "The world has lost its dewy prime; / Alas! the Golden age is dead, / And we are of the Iron time!" — frames industrialization as a fall from pastoral innocence.

2. Additional selections (075-048–075-053) extend Read's pastoral meditation through landscape description, domestic scenes of the matron at the spinning wheel, and the elegiac note of mortality — "At last the thread was snapped; her head was bowed; / Life dropped the distaff through his hands serene."

3. A section of shorter lyrics (075-054–075-059) includes verses addressed to the poet's deceased sister, striking a personal and elegiac register.

4. The anthology closes Read's selection with stanzas drawn from his Civil War poems (075-060–075-065), including the sunset scene at the Rappahannock, where the sound of a bugle rises "amid the fervid air" with "a strain — now rich and tender" before the atmosphere settles into "a holy quiet."

> The brook that erewhile laughed abroad, And o'er one light wheel loved to play, Now, like a felon, groaning trod Its hundred treadmills night and day. (075-045)

> And still the mournful mowers said, "The world has lost its dewy prime; Alas! the Golden age is dead, And we are of the Iron time!" (075-047)

> When on the fervid air there came A strain — now rich and tender; The music seemed itself aflame With day's departing splendor. (075-064)

## Critical Reception

Read enjoyed considerable popularity during his lifetime, particularly for "Sheridan's Ride," which was widely reprinted and recited in schools and public gatherings. His pastoral verse was admired for its pictorial vividness, though some critics noted its dependence on established Romantic conventions. Edgar Allan Poe, in his *Literati* sketches, praised Read's poetic gifts while observing that his work sometimes lacked originality of conception. After Read's death, his reputation declined steadily as literary tastes shifted away from the pastoral and patriotic modes in which he excelled. Twentieth-century scholarship largely ignored Read, though recent interest in the cultural dimensions of the Civil War and in nineteenth-century American pastoralism has prompted modest reexamination of his work. His dual identity as painter and poet continues to attract scholarly attention as an example of the interdisciplinary creative practice valued in Romantic aesthetics.
