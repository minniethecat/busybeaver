# William Clifton

## Biography

William Clifton (1772–1799) was an American poet. Born in Philadelphia, he showed an early aptitude for verse and was regarded as a young writer of considerable promise. His life was cut short at the age of twenty-seven. (The *Manual* dates him 1772–1790, suggesting he may have died even younger, at eighteen.)

Clifton belonged to the generation of American poets who worked within the neoclassical conventions inherited from eighteenth-century English verse. His poems appeared in periodicals of the day, and a posthumous collection of his work was published shortly after his death.

## Literary Career

Clifton's best-known poem is "To Fancy," a lyric in which the speaker celebrates the power of imagination to transform the humblest surroundings into a palace. The poem belongs to the tradition of eighteenth-century meditative verse — Samuel Johnson's "The Vanity of Human Wishes" and Gray's "Elegy" are distant cousins — but Clifton's voice is lighter, more personal, and touched with a melancholic awareness of transience.

His shorter lyrics on nature and the passage of time show a sensibility attuned to the fleeting beauties of the American landscape. Though his output was small, the poems that survive demonstrate a craftsman's command of meter and a genuine lyrical gift.

## Selections

In the *Choice Specimens* anthology, Clifton is represented by "Pleasures of Imagination" (064-057), an extract from "To Fancy." The poem opens with a series of rhetorical questions:

> Is my lonely pittance past? / Fleeting good too light to last? / Lifts my friend the latch no more? / Fancy, thou canst all restore; / Thou canst, with thy airy shell, / To a palace raise my cell. (064-057–060)

The poem then shifts to a Gothic-Romantic register, the speaker imagining himself in "some old haunted nook to sleep, / Lulled by the dreary night-bird's scream, / That flits along the wizard stream" (064-061). Clifton moves fluidly between this darker mode and the serene beauty of dawn:

> Sweet's the dawn's ambiguous light, / Quiet pause 'tween day and night, / When afar the mellow horn / Chides the tardy gaited morn. (064-062)

The closing stanza returns to Fancy as the poet's constant companion, the one presence that transforms every scene — "But the morn, though sweet and fair; / Sweeter is when thou art there" (064-063).

## Critical Reception

Clifton's reputation has always been that of a minor poet of considerable charm — a "poet's poet" whose early death left unrealized potential. The *Manual of American Literature* notes him briefly among the early poets of the Republic. His verse, with its blend of neoclassical formality and Romantic sensibility, offers a window into the literary culture of Federal-era Philadelphia.
