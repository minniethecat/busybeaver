# Leaves of Grass

*Leaves of Grass* (1855) is a poetry collection by [[walt-whitman]]. It is widely regarded as the most influential volume of American poetry.

The book celebrated democracy, the body, and the self in an unprecedented free-verse form. Whitman broke decisively with European poetic conventions, creating a distinctively American voice. [[ralph-waldo-emerson]] greeted the first edition as "the most extraordinary piece of wit and wisdom that America has yet contributed."

## Publication History

Whitman published the first edition of *Leaves of Grass* on July 4, 1855. He paid for the printing himself and set some of the type. The edition contained only twelve poems, headed by the untitled work later known as "Song of Myself."

The book grew across Whitman's lifetime through six major editions:

| Edition | Year | Poems | Notable Additions |
|---------|------|-------|-------------------|
| 1st | 1855 | 12 | "Song of Myself" (untitled), Emerson's letter |
| 2nd | 1856 | 32 | "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry," "Song of the Broad-Axe" |
| 3rd | 1860 | 154 | "Calamus" poems, "Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking" |
| 4th | 1867 | 236 | "Drum-Taps" (Civil War poems), "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" |
| 5th | 1871 | 273 | "Passage to India" |
| Deathbed | 1891–92 | ~400 | Final arrangement, retrospective prefaces |

This continuous expansion reflects Whitman's poetic philosophy. The book was never "finished" but grew organically, absorbing the experiences of his life — including his service as a Civil War nurse — and the expanding nation he celebrated.

## Poetic Innovation

*Leaves of Grass* revolutionized American poetry through several formal innovations:

**Free Verse**: Whitman abandoned meter and rhyme for long, cadenced lines organized by parallel structure and anaphora. His line — modeled partly on biblical verse and partly on operatic aria — liberated American poetry from European formalism.

**Catalog Technique**: Whitman's famous catalogs (long lists of people, places, objects, and occupations) enact the democratic principle that every element of American life deserves poetic attention. The catalogs create a sense of overwhelming plenitude and national scope.

**The Democratic "I"**: The speaker of *Leaves of Grass* is simultaneously an individual self and a representative consciousness. Whitman's "I" expands to contain multitudes: "I am large, I contain multitudes."

**The Body Electric**: Whitman was the first major American poet to treat the body — including sexuality — as a sacred subject. His frankness about physical love scandalized nineteenth-century readers and led to the book being banned in Boston.

## Selections in the Anthology

The following passage from *Leaves of Grass* appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

> [075-001] Give me the splendid silent sun with all his beams full-dazzling. Give me juicy autumnal fruit ripe and red from the orchard. Give me a field where the unmowed grass grows. Give me an arbor, give me the trellised grape.
>
> — From "Give Me the Splendid Silent Sun" (selection 280)

This excerpt — from one of Whitman's later poems — shows the poet's characteristic fusion of sensory immediacy ("juicy autumnal fruit") with cosmic scale ("the splendid silent sun"). The anaphoric repetition ("Give me...Give me...") exemplifies Whitman's debt to biblical parallelism.

## Critical Significance

*Leaves of Grass* has been called the **American epic** — a democratic counterpart to Homer, Virgil, and Dante. Where the classical epic celebrated a single hero, Whitman's epic celebrated the common person.

The book's influence on subsequent American poetry is incalculable. Ezra Pound, who initially rebelled against Whitman's example, eventually wrote a poem of reconciliation ("A Pact"). Allen Ginsberg's *Howl* (1956) explicitly invoked Whitman as a forerunner. The free-verse tradition from William Carlos Williams to the present flows through *Leaves of Grass*.

Beyond poetry, Whitman's vision of American democracy — inclusive, embodied, and spiritual — has inspired writers, musicians, and political thinkers from Langston Hughes to Bruce Springsteen.
