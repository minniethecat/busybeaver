# Robert Treat Paine

Robert Treat Paine (1773–1811) was an American poet, editor, and Federalist political writer active in the early Republic period.

Born Thomas Paine, he changed his name to avoid confusion with the famous Revolutionary pamphleteer. His poem "The Ruling Passion" and his song "Adams and Liberty" earned him national recognition.

He edited the *Federal Orrery*, a Federalist newspaper in Boston, and contributed to periodicals of the era. Paine died at age thirty-eight after a period of declining health and financial difficulty.

## Biography

Paine was born on December 9, 1773, in Taunton, Massachusetts. His father, also named Robert Treat Paine, was a signer of the Declaration of Independence and a Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court justice. The younger Paine attended Harvard College, graduating in 1792.

He initially took the name Thomas Paine but legally changed it to Robert Treat Paine in 1801 to distinguish himself from the author of *Common Sense*. He worked as an editor for the *Federal Orrery* and later the *Boston Gazette*, using his platform to advocate Federalist political positions.

Paine married Elizabeth Baker in 1797, and they had several children. Financial troubles and failing health marked his final years; he died on November 13, 1811, in Boston at the age of thirty-eight.

## Literary Career

Paine's literary output was concentrated in the 1790s and early 1800s. His most famous work, "Adams and Liberty" (1798), was a patriotic song celebrating the presidency of John Adams. It became widely performed and earned Paine a national reputation.

His poem "The Ruling Passion," from which "The Miser" is taken, follows the neoclassical tradition of satirizing human vices through character portraits. The poem shows the influence of Alexander Pope and the Augustan satirists.

Paine also published *The Invention of Letters* (1795), a commencement poem delivered at Harvard, and various political essays and satirical pieces in Federalist periodicals. His work represents the intersection of early American political culture and neoclassical literary form.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Paine: "The Miser," taken from the longer poem "The Ruling Passion." This character sketch of a miser is in the tradition of eighteenth-century satirical portraiture.

> [065-001] Next comes the miser; palsied, jealous, lean, / He looks the very skeleton of Spleen! / 'Mid forests drear, he haunts, in spectred gloom, / Some desert abbey or some druid's tomb; / Where hearsed in earth, his occult riches lay, / Fleeced from the world, and buried from the day.

> [065-002] With crutch in hand, he points his mineral rod, / Limps to the spot, and turns the well-known sod. / While there, involved in night, he counts his store / By the soft tinklings of the golden ore, / He shakes with terror lest the moon should spy, / And the breeze whisper, where his treasures lie.

The selection exemplifies Paine's neoclassical style: heroic couplets in iambic pentameter, personified abstractions, and a moralizing tone that exposes human folly. (065-001)

## Critical Reception

In his own time, Paine was celebrated as a leading American poet. "Adams and Liberty" was immensely popular, sung at political gatherings and public events. Joseph Dennie's *Port Folio* praised his poetic craftsmanship.

Modern literary historians regard Paine as a minor figure of the early national period. His neoclassical style, rigidly formal by later standards, has limited his appeal to contemporary readers. However, his work remains valuable as a document of Federalist literary culture and early American political verse.

Critics note that Paine's career was cut short by his early death, and that his poetic gifts — while genuine — were constrained by the didactic conventions of his era.
