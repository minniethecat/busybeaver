# Matthew F. Maury

## Biography

Matthew Fontaine Maury (1806–1873) was an American oceanographer, naval officer, meteorologist, and author. He is widely regarded as the father of modern oceanography for his pioneering work in charting winds, currents, and other physical features of the ocean. Born in Spotsylvania County, Virginia, Maury entered the United States Navy as a midshipman in 1825, and his career at sea gave him firsthand experience of the maritime challenges that his later scientific work would address.

During a voyage to Rio de Janeiro in 1834, Maury began the systematic study of ocean navigation that would become his life's work. In 1839, a stagecoach accident left him permanently lame and ended his active sea service. Confined to shore duty, Maury threw himself into the study of ships' logs, seeking in the accumulated records of thousands of voyages the patterns that would make navigation safer and more efficient.

In 1842, he was appointed superintendent of the Navy's Depot of Charts and Instruments (later the U.S. Naval Observatory). From this position, he compiled the data that became *Wind and Current Charts*, issued to merchant and naval vessels, and *Explanations and Sailing Directions to Accompany the Wind and Current Charts*, which guided mariners for decades.

During the Civil War, Maury resigned his U.S. Navy commission and served the Confederacy, spending much of the war in England acquiring ships and naval technology. After the war, he lived in Mexico and England before returning to Virginia, where he accepted a professorship of physics at the Virginia Military Institute. He died in Lexington, Virginia, in 1873.

## Literary Career

Maury's literary reputation rests primarily on *The Physical Geography of the Sea* (1855), a work that transformed the emerging science of oceanography into a popular and poetic subject. The book was an international success, running through numerous editions and translations; it was one of the most widely read scientific works published in America before the Civil War.

Maury's prose is notable for its marriage of empirical rigor and religious wonder: he saw in the ocean's currents and winds the visible hand of Providence. This synthesis of science and faith was characteristic of antebellum American intellectual life, and Maury's ability to render the ocean intelligible — both to sailors seeking safe passage and to readers seeking edification — gave his work its lasting appeal.

The *Choice Specimens* anthology lists "Matthew F. Maury, 1806-1873" in the Table of Contents with the selection "The Mariner's Guide across the Deep" (001-106, 250).

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Mariner's Guide across the Deep* (052-007, 250) — From *The Physical Geography of the Sea*, a passage on the navigator's art:

> So to shape the course on voyages as to make the most of the winds and currents at sea, is the perfection of the navigator's art. How the winds blow, and the currents flow, along this route or that, is no longer matter of opinion or subject of speculation, but it is a matter of certainty determined by actual observation... The winds and the weather daily encountered by hundreds who have sailed...
> (052-007, 052-008)

Maury's selection exemplifies his method: the accumulation of data from countless ships' logs, transformed by the synthesizing eye of science into a comprehensive, usable picture of the world ocean. The editor places him alongside other noteworthy writers on scientific and natural subjects in the anthology.

## Critical Reception

Maury's reputation was immense in his lifetime. His *Wind and Current Charts* were credited with shortening the average sailing time from New York to San Francisco by thirty days, a practical result that earned him the gratitude of mariners and merchants worldwide. *The Physical Geography of the Sea* was praised by Alexander von Humboldt, and Maury was awarded gold medals by numerous European scientific societies.

His Civil War service complicated his posthumous reputation in the United States; some Northern institutions were slow to recognize his scientific achievements. However, his standing as a foundational figure in oceanography has only grown with time. The Maury River in Virginia, Maury Island in Puget Sound, and numerous naval vessels have been named in his honor.
