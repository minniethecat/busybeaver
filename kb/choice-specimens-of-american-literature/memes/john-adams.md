# John Adams

## Biography

John Adams (1735–1826) was an American statesman, attorney, diplomat, and political philosopher who served as the second President of the United States (1797–1801) and the first Vice President (1789–1797). Born in Braintree (now Quincy), Massachusetts, Adams graduated from Harvard College in 1755 and was admitted to the bar in 1758. He rose to prominence as a leading advocate for colonial rights, serving as a delegate to the Continental Congress and as a commissioner to France during the Revolutionary War.

Adams was a principal architect of American independence. He served on the committee that drafted the Declaration of Independence alongside Thomas Jefferson and Benjamin Franklin, and later served on the committee to design the Great Seal of the United States (022-001). His diplomatic missions to France, the Netherlands, and Great Britain were instrumental in securing foreign support and formal recognition for the new republic. As president, Adams navigated the quasi-war with France and signed the Alien and Sedition Acts — controversial measures that tested the young nation's commitment to civil liberties.

## Literary Career

Adams was a voluminous writer whose works span political philosophy, legal theory, history, and personal correspondence. His prose is characterized by intellectual vigor, moral conviction, and a lawyers precision. His *Dissertation on the Canon and Feudal Law* (1765) was an early articulation of American resistance to British authority, and his *Thoughts on Government* (1776) influenced the drafting of several state constitutions.

In his later years, Adams engaged in an extraordinary correspondence with Thomas Jefferson, a dialogue that spanned over a decade and covered philosophy, religion, history, and the meaning of the American experiment. The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes Adams's biographical sketch of the Revolutionary orator James Otis, drawn from his *Life and Works*. Adams wrote of Otis with evident admiration, describing him as a gentleman of "general science and extensive literature," well versed in "Greek and Roman history, philosophy, oratory, poetry, and mythology" (013-001). Adams's biographical writing reveals his conviction that the character of a nation depends on the character of its leaders.

The following selections from John Adams's writings appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Character of James Otis* (013-001–013-006) — From the "Life and Works of John Adams," a biographical portrait of the fiery Boston orator and lawyer who argued against the Writs of Assistance. Adams describes Otis's classical education, legal training under Mr. Gridley, and his maxim that "a lawyer ought never to be without a volume of natural or public law, or moral philosophy, on his table or in his pocket" (013-003).

## Critical Reception

Adams has often been overshadowed by the more celebrated figures of the founding era — Washington, Jefferson, Franklin — but recent scholarship, notably David McCullough's Pulitzer Prize-winning biography (2001), has restored Adams to his rightful place as one of the most consequential and intellectually formidable of the Founders. His political writings, though less elegant than Jefferson's, are marked by a candor and psychological insight that make them enduringly readable. Adams's insistence on the separation of powers and the dangers of unchecked democracy shaped the constitutional architecture of the United States, and his lifelong correspondence with Jefferson remains one of the great intellectual dialogues in American letters.
