## Biography

William Dean Howells (1837--1920) was an American novelist, literary critic, and editor who dominated the American literary scene in the late nineteenth century. Born in Martins Ferry, Ohio (then called Martinsville), he was the second of eight children in a family of modest means. His father, William Cooper Howells, was a printer and newspaper publisher whose peripatetic career -- moving from town to town across the Ohio frontier -- denied young William a stable formal education. Instead, the future author educated himself in his father's printing offices, setting type by day and reading voraciously by night, absorbing the works of Shakespeare, Cervantes, Goldsmith, and Heine. By his early teens, Howells was already contributing sketches and poems to his father's newspapers, and by the age of nineteen he was a working journalist in Columbus, Ohio.

The turning point in Howells's early career came in 1860, when he wrote a campaign biography of Abraham Lincoln. The book's commercial success and his political connections earned him the consulship to Venice, a post he held from 1861 to 1865 during the American Civil War. Those four years in Italy proved formative: Howells learned Italian, studied Renaissance art and architecture, and gathered the materials for his first book, *Venetian Life* (1866), a collection of travel sketches that established his reputation as a graceful observer of foreign manners. Upon his return to America, he settled into literary journalism, working for *The Nation* and *The Atlantic Monthly*, whose editorship he assumed in 1871 at the age of thirty-four. From that post, Howells shaped the literary taste of the nation for the next decade, championing the realist fiction of [[mark-twain|Mark Twain]], [[henry-james|Henry James]], [[stephen-crane|Stephen Crane]], and [[frank-norris|Frank Norris]], among many others. He would later be known as the "Dean of American Letters" -- a title that reflected both his longevity and his unrivaled influence over American literary culture from the end of the Civil War to the outbreak of the Great War.

## Literary Career

Howells's own fiction spans the full range of his realist commitments. His early novels, such as *Their Wedding Journey* (1872) and *A Chance Acquaintance* (1873), are gentle comedies of manners that apply the realist method to the everyday lives of the American middle class. His masterpiece, *The Rise of Silas Lapham* (1885), is a study of a self-made paint manufacturer whose moral integrity is tested in the corrupt world of Back Bay Boston society. The novel's famous dinner-party scene, in which Lapham drinks too much wine and embarrasses himself before the aristocratic Corey family, is among the most psychologically acute passages in nineteenth-century American fiction. *A Hazard of New Fortunes* (1890), his longest and most ambitious novel, moves the scene to New York City and broadens the canvas to include the class conflicts of industrial capitalism, the struggles of immigrants, and the ethical dilemmas of a literary editor -- in part a portrait of Howells himself. These two novels represent the summit of American literary realism before the coming of naturalism.

As an editor and critic, Howells was relentless in his advocacy of realism over what he called the "romanticistic" tradition of Scott, Dickens, and their American imitators. In his monthly "Editor's Study" columns for *Harper's Monthly* -- collected in *Criticism and Fiction* (1891) -- he laid out the case that fiction should concern itself with ordinary life, tell the truth as it is, and avoid the sensational, the melodramatic, and the falsely sentimental. His famous dictum -- that the novel should depict "the smiling aspects of life" -- was often quoted against him by later naturalists who insisted on the darker side of experience, but it was never the full story. Howells's later novels, including *A Modern Instance* (1882), which traces the dissolution of a marriage, and *Annie Kilburn* (1889), which examines the failure of philanthropic benevolence, demonstrate that his realism could accommodate tragedy when tragedy was truthful. His critical influence is attested by the writers he championed: he was among the first to recognize the genius of [[henry-james|Henry James]] and [[mark-twain|Mark Twain]], and he helped launch the careers of [[stephen-crane|Stephen Crane]], [[hamlin-garland|Hamlin Garland]], [[sarah-orne-jewett|Sarah Orne Jewett]], and [[paul-laurence-dunbar|Paul Laurence Dunbar]].

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) preserves a single passage from Howells's *Venetian Life* (1866): "Snow in Venice" (Selection No. 247). The piece is drawn from Howells's years as American consul in Venice and represents his early mastery of descriptive travel-writing, a genre in which he had few American rivals.

In this passage, Howells captures the transformation of St. Mark's Square under a heavy snowfall -- a rare event in Venice that renders the familiar scene unfamiliar and luminous:

> The lofty crest of the bell-tower was hidden in the folds of falling snow, and I could no longer see the golden angel upon its summit. But looked at across the Piazza, the beautiful outline of St. Mark's Church was perfectly penciled in the air, and the shifting threads of the snow-fall were woven into a spell of novel enchantment around a structure that always seemed to me too exquisite in its fantastic loveliness to be anything but the creation of magic. (051-015)

Howells's eye moves from the architecture to the moral effect of the snow on the aging building:

> The tender snow had compassionated the beautiful edifice for all the wrongs of time, and so hid the stains and ugliness of decay that it looked as if just from the hands of the builder -- or, better said, just from the brain of the architect. (051-015)

His prose then widens to encompass the whole square and the canals beyond, in a passage that descends from the architectural into the ethereal:

> The snow lay lightly on the golden globes that tremble like peacock-crests above the vast domes, and plumed them with softest white; it robed the saints in ermine; and it danced over all its work as if exulting in its beauty. (051-015)

The sketch closes with a quiet meditation on the city's famed silence, now deepened by the snowfall:

> The gondolas stole in and out of the opaque distance, more noiselessly and dreamily than ever; and a silence almost palpable, lay upon the mutest city in the world. (051-015)

Even in this early work, the voice that would become the "Dean of American Letters" is recognizable: patient in observation, painterly in detail, and attentive to the human meaning that attaches to places and things. The "tender snow" that compassionates the old building tells us as much about the sensibility of the observer as about the city observed.

## Critical Reception

Howells was, in his lifetime, the most powerful and widely respected man of letters in the United States. His editorial decisions determined which writers received national attention, and his critical pronouncements set the terms by which fiction was judged. The young [[f-scott-fitzgerald|F. Scott Fitzgerald]] would later look back on Howells as "the closest approach we have ever had to a representative American man of letters." As president of the American Academy of Arts and Letters, and as the recipient of honors from Harvard, Yale, and Oxford, Howells embodied the literary establishment.

That very establishment, however, made him a target for the generation that followed. The naturalists [[theodore-dreiser|Theodore Dreiser]] and [[frank-norris|Frank Norris]] faulted Howells for his supposed timidity -- for refusing to confront the brutal facts of sex, poverty, and violence with the unflinching directness they demanded. The modernists of the 1920s, with their taste for formal experiment and their rejection of the "genteel tradition," largely dismissed him. Yet a reassessment was underway by the mid-twentieth century. Critics such as [[edmund-wilson|Edmund Wilson]] and [[lionel-trilling|Lionel Trilling]] recovered *The Rise of Silas Lapham* and *A Hazard of New Fortunes* as serious works of social and moral inquiry, and later scholars have situated Howells as a pivotal figure in the transition from the romantic idealism of the mid-nineteenth century to the darker, more fragmented vision of the twentieth. His determination to ground American fiction in the texture of ordinary experience -- in the way people actually talk, work, compromise, and fail -- is now recognized as one of the defining achievements of American literary culture.
