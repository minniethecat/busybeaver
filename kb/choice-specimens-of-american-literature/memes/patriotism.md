# Patriotism in American Literature

## Definition
Patriotism, in American literary tradition, denotes love of country expressed not merely as sentimental attachment but as active devotion to national ideals—liberty, self-government, and the common good. American writers shaped patriotism as a moral obligation rather than mere chauvinism, rooting it in the defense of universal principles.

## Historical Context
Revolutionary-era oratory produced the most celebrated expressions of American patriotism. Patrick Henry's "give me liberty, or give me death" (013-013) distilled patriotic devotion into its purest rhetorical form. Jonathan Edwards and other clerical writers framed patriotism in providential terms, warning that those "deaf to all the calls of liberty, virtue, and religion" betrayed both country and God (015-002). After independence, patriotism became intertwined with the project of national consolidation—the effort to transform revolutionary fervor into enduring civic commitment.

## Key Authors and Works
- **Patrick Henry**, in his speech to the Virginia Convention (1775), gave American patriotism its most famous expression, framing the choice as one between liberty and death (013-013).
- **Jonathan Edwards** urged citizens to honor the sacrifices of the Revolutionary generation, who had "given independence, and preserved liberty to the country" (015-003).
- **Joel Barlow** and other early national poets celebrated American patriotism in verse, treating the Revolution as an epic worthy of Homeric treatment.

## Legacy
The patriotic tradition in American literature was both celebrated and critiqued in the 19th century. While orators at Fourth of July celebrations rehearsed Revolutionary themes, writers like Hawthorne and Melville explored the darker dimensions of patriotic fervor—its capacity for self-deception, its exclusion of those deemed un-American, and its vulnerability to demagogic manipulation.
