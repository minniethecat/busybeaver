# Francis Hopkinson

Francis Hopkinson (1737–1791) was an American Founding Father, signer of the Declaration of Independence, jurist, poet, and satirist. A versatile figure of the Revolutionary era, Hopkinson designed the American flag (or contributed to its early design), served as a federal judge, and wrote some of the most popular satirical verse of the American Revolution.

His comic ballad "The Battle of the Kegs," which ridicules the British panic over floating mines on the Delaware River, remains a classic of American Revolutionary humor and is included in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*.

## Biography

Hopkinson was born on October 2, 1737, in Philadelphia, to Thomas Hopkinson, a lawyer and friend of Benjamin Franklin. He was a member of the first class to graduate from the College of Philadelphia (now the University of Pennsylvania) in 1757. He studied law and was admitted to the bar, establishing a practice in Philadelphia and later in Bordentown, New Jersey.

Hopkinson served as a delegate from New Jersey to the Second Continental Congress and signed the Declaration of Independence in 1776. After the Revolution, he was appointed Judge of the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania by President George Washington, serving from 1789 until his death in 1791.

## Literary Career

A polymath of the American Enlightenment, Hopkinson wrote across multiple genres. His satirical essays, published under pseudonyms, targeted British policy and Loyalist sympathizers during the Revolution. He was also a composer of songs and hymns, some of which were published in his *Collection of Psalm Tunes* (1763).

His most famous work, "The Battle of the Kegs" (1778), was inspired by a real incident: American patriots floated kegs filled with gunpowder down the Delaware River toward British ships in Philadelphia. The kegs failed to detonate, but the British panicked and fired cannons at them for hours. Hopkinson's ballad, which describes the entire city of Philadelphia roused to arms against a "fleet" of kegs, became an instant sensation and was widely reprinted.

"The Battle of the Kegs" exemplifies the satiric spirit that pervaded Revolutionary-era American writing. Hopkinson's use of mock-heroic conventions — treating barrels as a naval threat — draws on the tradition of Dryden, Swift, and Pope, adapted to distinctly American circumstances.

## Selections in the Anthology

The anthology includes the opening stanzas of "The Battle of the Kegs," Hopkinson's best-known work:

[064-007]The ballad opens:

> Gallants, attend, and hear a friend / Trill forth harmonious ditty; / Strange things I'll tell, which late befell / In Philadelphia city.

[064-008]The scene is set:

> 'Twas early day, as poets say, / Just when the sun was rising, / A soldier stood on a log of wood, / And saw a thing surprising.

[064-009]The discovery:

> As in amaze he stood to gaze, — / The truth can't be denied, sir, — / He spied a score of kegs, or more, / Come floating down the tide, sir.

The ballad proceeds to describe the city-wide alarm — citizens running half-naked through the streets, cannons roaring from shore to shore, and finally the anti-climactic realization that the fearsome "fleet" consisted only of empty barrels.

## Critical Reception

Hopkinson was recognized by his contemporaries as one of the most talented literary figures of the Revolutionary generation. His friend [[benjamin-franklin]] praised his wit and versatility, while later critics have noted his role in establishing a distinctly American tradition of political satire.

The inclusion of "The Battle of the Kegs" in numerous nineteenth-century anthologies, including Martin's *Choice Specimens*, testifies to its enduring appeal as a piece of Revolutionary Americana.
