# John Pierpont

## Biography

John Pierpont (1785–1866) was an American poet, Unitarian minister, temperance reformer, and abolitionist. Born in Litchfield, Connecticut, he was the son of a farmer and descended from James Pierpont, a founder of Yale College. Pierpont graduated from Yale in 1804 and initially worked as a private tutor in South Carolina, where he also studied law.

After a brief legal career in Newburyport, Massachusetts, Pierpont abandoned law for commerce, then commerce for theology. He graduated from Harvard Divinity School in 1818 and was ordained as minister of the Hollis Street Church (Unitarian) in Boston, where he served from 1819 to 1845. His outspoken advocacy of temperance and abolition alienated conservative members of his congregation, leading to a protracted controversy that ended with his resignation in 1845.

During the Civil War, Pierpont — then in his late seventies — served as a chaplain in the Union Army, attached to the 22nd Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry. After the war, he worked as a clerk in the Treasury Department in Washington, D.C., where he died in 1866. His daughter, Juliet Pierpont, married Junius Spencer Morgan; their son, J. Pierpont Morgan, became one of the most powerful financiers in American history.

## Literary Career

Pierpont's literary reputation rests chiefly on his poetry. His most acclaimed long poem, *The Airs of Palestine* (1816), is a descriptive-meditative work inspired by the landscape and sacred history of the Holy Land. First published anonymously, the poem went through several editions and was praised by contemporaries for its musical versification and devotional warmth.

Pierpont was also a prolific writer of hymns and reform poetry. His temperance poems, notably "A Temperance Song," use biblical imagery and a gently satiric tone to argue for total abstinence — a position that was controversial in the 1830s and 1840s, when wine and spirits were still commonly served at clerical gatherings and church functions. The poem opens by locating the temperance ideal in Eden itself:

> In Eden's green retreats,
> A water-brook — that played
> Between soft, mossy seats,
> Beneath a plane tree's shade,
> Whose rustling leaves
> Danced o'er its brink —
> Was Adam's drink,
> And also Eve's. (065-032)

The poem's rhetorical climax turns on the image of Moses at the rock in the wilderness, deploying a humorous anachronism to drive home its point:

> Had Moses built a still,
> And dealt out to that host
> To every man his gill,
> And pledged him in a toast,
> Would cooler brains,
> Or stronger hands,
> Have braved the sands
> Of those hot plains? (065-035)

This deft turn — imagining the Exodus as a drunken brawl — is characteristic of Pierpont's satiric touch. The poem's argument that water is the drink of Eden, the desert, and heaven itself links temperance to a broader moral vision of innocence and spiritual purity.

"The Pilgrim Fathers" is Pierpont's most enduringly popular poem. A staple of school recitations and patriotic occasions throughout the nineteenth century, the poem memorializes the Mayflower settlers in elegiac stanzas that balance reverence with a haunting sense of loss:

> The Pilgrim Fathers, — where are they?
> The waves that brought them o'er
> Still roll in the bay, and throw their spray,
> As they break along the shore. (065-037)

The poem's power lies in its counterpoint between the permanence of the natural world and the transience of the human actors who once moved through it. The final stanza declares that the Pilgrim spirit survives not in monuments or institutions but as a moral presence watching over the nation:

> The Pilgrim Fathers are at rest.
> When summer's throned on high,
> And the world's warm breast is in verdure dress'd
> Go, stand on the hill where they lie. (065-044)

Pierpont's verse, while unfashionable by modernist standards, exemplifies the public and moral functions that nineteenth-century American readers expected of poetry: to instruct, to commemorate, and to elevate.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "A Temperance Song" (065-032–065-036) — A lyric temperance poem using biblical imagery (Eden, Moses in the wilderness) to argue for total abstinence. The poem's light satiric touch and firm moral purpose are characteristic of Pierpont's reform verse.
- "The Pilgrim Fathers" (065-037–065-048) — An elegy for the Mayflower settlers that became one of the most widely recited schoolroom poems in nineteenth-century America. Counterpoints the enduring power of nature with the transience of human life, concluding with the Pilgrim spirit as a living moral presence.

## Critical Reception

Pierpont's poetry was well regarded in his lifetime, and *The Airs of Palestine* earned him a place among the respected American poets of the early republic. "The Pilgrim Fathers" enjoyed a long afterlife as a widely anthologized recitation piece, and its opening line — "The Pilgrim Fathers, — where are they?" — became a recognized quotation in nineteenth-century American discourse.

Pierpont's literary reputation, however, was always secondary to his public role as a reformer and preacher. His reform activities — temperance, abolition, pacifism — consumed much of his energy and, at times, complicated his literary output. After his death, his poetry, like that of many antebellum American poets, was gradually eclipsed by the rise of post-Civil War realism and the modernist reaction against didactic verse.
