# Rufus Choate

## Biography

Rufus Choate (1799–1859) was an American lawyer, orator, and statesman from Massachusetts. Born in Ipswich, Massachusetts, he graduated from Dartmouth College in 1819 and studied law at Harvard Law School under Justice Joseph Story. Admitted to the bar in 1823, Choate quickly established himself as one of the foremost trial lawyers in New England, known for his eloquent courtroom style and prodigious memory.

Choate served in the United States House of Representatives (1831–1834) and later in the United States Senate (1841–1845), succeeding Daniel Webster. As a Whig, he was an ardent supporter of Webster's constitutional conservatism and opposed the annexation of Texas. After leaving the Senate, he devoted himself primarily to his law practice, becoming the leading figure of the Massachusetts bar. His courtroom arguments were legendary for their rhetorical brilliance and classical erudition.

## Literary Career

Choate's literary reputation rests primarily on his oratory. He delivered numerous public addresses on topics ranging from politics to literature, and his speeches were collected and published posthumously. His oration "The Conservative Principles in Politics," delivered at the dedication of a new hall at Dartmouth College, articulates a philosophy of constitutional conservatism rooted in the American founding. Choate's prose was characterized by its density, classical allusions, and sweeping periodic sentences — a Ciceronian style well suited to the grand jury trials and Senate debates of his era. His writings and speeches reflect the Whig conviction that law, tradition, and ordered liberty form the bedrock of republican government.

## Selections in This Anthology

In "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Choate is represented by his oration "Conservative Principles in Politics," which meditates on the role of the American Bar in preserving constitutional order and the importance of looking to the founding generation for guidance:

> "Is it not so that in its nature, in its functions, in the intellectual and practical habits which it forms, in the opinions to which it conducts, in all its tendencies and influences of speculation and action, it is, and ought to be, professionally and peculiarly such an element and such an agent, that it has been called the conservative principle to our polity?" (020-006)

> "I have said that I deemed it a great thing for a nation, in all the periods of its fortunes, to be able to look back to a race of founders, and a principle of institution, in which, it might seem to see the realized idea of true heroism. That felicity, that pride, that help, is ours." (020-007)

Choate argues that the legal profession serves as a stabilizing force in American democracy, tempering popular passion with constitutional principle and anchoring the present to the wisdom of the founders.

## Critical Reception

Choate was widely regarded by contemporaries as the most brilliant American lawyer of his generation. Justice Joseph Story called him "the first advocate in America," and Daniel Webster himself acknowledged Choate's superiority in certain courtroom skills. Later critics have noted that his ornate, classical style fell out of favor as American prose moved toward greater simplicity, but his speeches remain studied as exemplars of nineteenth-century forensic eloquence. Edward Everett remarked that Choate's oratory "had no equal at the American bar."
