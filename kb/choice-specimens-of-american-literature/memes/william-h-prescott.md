# William H. Prescott

## Biography

William Hickling Prescott (1796–1859) was an American historian, widely regarded as one of the first great narrative historians of the United States. Born in Salem, Massachusetts, to a prosperous family, Prescott graduated from Harvard College in 1814. While at Harvard, an accident left him nearly blind in one eye, and his remaining vision deteriorated steadily throughout his life. Undeterred, Prescott developed a system of research that relied on secretaries who read Spanish and other sources aloud to him, while he composed his narratives using a noctograph — a special writing frame for the visually impaired.

Prescott never traveled to Spain or Latin America, yet he produced vivid, sweeping accounts of the Spanish conquests that captivated readers on both sides of the Atlantic. He married Susan Amory in 1820 and lived a life of disciplined scholarship at his Beacon Hill home in Boston, where he was a central figure in the Brahmin literary circle.

## Literary Career

Prescott's major works include *History of the Reign of Ferdinand and Isabella the Catholic* (1837), *History of the Conquest of Mexico* (1843), and *History of the Conquest of Peru* (1847). His histories combined exhaustive archival research — he was among the first American historians to work extensively with Spanish primary sources — with the narrative flair and dramatic sensibility of the Romantic era. His accounts of the Aztec and Inca civilizations, and of the conquistadors Hernán Cortés and Francisco Pizarro, remain among the most widely read nineteenth-century American historical works.

Prescott was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and corresponded with leading European historians. His work influenced the development of American historical writing toward a more literary, accessible style while maintaining rigorous scholarly standards.

## Critical Reception

Prescott was highly esteemed in his lifetime on both sides of the Atlantic. His histories were translated into multiple European languages and earned him membership in learned societies across Europe. Washington Irving praised his work, and his narrative style was admired for making scholarly history accessible to a broad reading public. Modern historians have noted his biases — particularly his Romantic portrayal of the Spanish conquest — but his archival rigor and literary craftsmanship continue to be acknowledged.
