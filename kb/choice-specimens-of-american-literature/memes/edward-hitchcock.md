# Edward Hitchcock

## Biography

Edward Hitchcock (1793–1864) was an American geologist, Congregational minister, and educator who served as professor of chemistry and natural history at Amherst College from 1825 to 1845 and as the college's third president from 1845 to 1854. Born in Deerfield, Massachusetts, to a family of modest means, Hitchcock was largely self-educated in the sciences, developing an early interest in natural history while working as a farmer and schoolteacher. His systematic study of the geology of the Connecticut River Valley produced important contributions to ichnology — the study of trace fossils — including his identification and description of fossil footprints in the red sandstone of the valley, which he correctly attributed to ancient birds.

Hitchcock was ordained as a Congregational minister in 1821 and served briefly as pastor of the Congregational Church in Conway, Massachusetts, before illness — a chronic bronchial condition — forced him to leave the pulpit. His subsequent career at Amherst allowed him to combine his scientific interests with his religious convictions. He was one of the most prominent American voices arguing for the compatibility of geological science — particularly the evidence for an ancient earth and progressive creation — with evangelical Christian faith. His position represented a middle way between the biblical literalism of some clergy and the materialism of some scientists, and his writings on the subject were widely read on both sides of the Atlantic.

## Literary Career

Hitchcock was an extraordinarily prolific author. His major works include *The Religion of Geology and Its Connected Sciences* (1851), *Elementary Geology* (1840) — which became the standard American textbook on the subject and went through more than thirty editions — and *Ichnology of New England* (1858), his magnum opus on fossil footprints. He also published volumes of sermons, devotional poetry, and a memoir of his wife. His writing was notable for its clarity, its pedagogical effectiveness, and its persistent effort to demonstrate that scientific investigation, properly conducted, leads to reverence for the Creator.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," "Geological Proof of Divine Benevolence," is taken from *The Religion of Geology* and exemplifies Hitchcock's characteristic rhetorical strategy: taking a feature of the natural world that appears destructive or chaotic and rereading it as evidence of divine benevolence:

> My second argument in proof of the divine benevolence is derived from the disturbed, broken, and overturned condition of the earth's crust. To the casual observer the rocks have the appearance of being lifted up, shattered, and overturned; but it is only the geologist who knows the vast extent of this disturbance. (008-005)

The surface appearance is one of catastrophe — "disturbed, broken, and overturned" — but Hitchcock's geological knowledge allows him to reinterpret that apparent chaos as providential design. The key move in the argument is economic: if the strata had remained in their original horizontal position, the valuable minerals and ores buried deep underground would have been inaccessible to human industry. The disturbances that lifted, tilted, and exposed them were acts of divine preparation for human civilization:

> If these strata had remained horizontal, as they were originally deposited, it is obvious that all the valuable ores, minerals, and rocks, which man could not have discovered by direct excavation, must have remained forever unknown to him. (008-005)

Hitchcock calculates that human mining rarely penetrates more than half a mile below the surface, whereas geological disturbances have brought deposits from as deep as fifteen or twenty miles within reach. "He is indebted, then, to this disturbing agency for nearly all the useful metals, coal, rock salt, marble, gypsum, and other useful minerals; and when we consider how necessary these substances are to civilized society, who will doubt that it was a striking act of benevolence which thus introduced disturbance, dislocation, and apparent ruin into the earth's crust?" This is natural theology of a distinctively nineteenth-century, industrial kind: the geological processes that produced coal and iron are read as provisions for human progress. The earthquake and the mine shaft are two faces of the same divine economy.

## Critical Reception

Hitchcock's *Elementary Geology* was the most widely used geology textbook in the United States through the mid-nineteenth century, and his work on fossil footprints established ichnology as a significant field of American paleontology. His efforts to reconcile geology and Genesis — he advocated a "gap theory" interpretation that allowed for an ancient earth within a broadly biblical framework — were influential among moderate Protestants seeking to maintain both scientific credibility and religious orthodoxy.

Modern historians of science have recognized Hitchcock as a transitional figure: he helped establish geology as a professional scientific discipline in America while also representing the last generation for whom natural theology was an intellectually serious project. His geological museum at Amherst, which he built into one of the largest collections of fossil footprints in the world, remains a significant scientific resource.
