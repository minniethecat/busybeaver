# Nature

*Nature* (1836) is a philosophical essay by [[ralph-waldo-emerson]]. It is the foundational text of American Transcendentalism.

The essay argues that the natural world is a direct source of spiritual truth. Emerson contends that every individual can access divine wisdom through intuition and direct experience of nature, rejecting the mediation of church, scripture, or tradition.

## Publication and Structure

*Nature* was published anonymously in Boston by James Munroe and Company in September 1836. The first edition was small — only five hundred copies — and took over twelve years to sell out. Yet its impact far exceeded its initial readership.

The essay is structured in eight chapters:

| Chapter | Title | Theme |
|---------|-------|-------|
| I | Nature | The restorative power of the natural world |
| II | Commodity | Nature's material uses for human life |
| III | Beauty | Aesthetic pleasure derived from nature |
| IV | Language | Nature as the source of all language and symbolism |
| V | Discipline | Nature's moral and intellectual education |
| VI | Idealism | The philosophical argument that nature is a projection of mind |
| VII | Spirit | Nature as the visible expression of divine Spirit |
| VIII | Prospects | The future of the human relationship with nature |

The opening chapter, with its famous "transparent eyeball" passage, became the most quoted section. Emerson describes a moment of mystical unity with nature in which "all mean egotism vanishes" and he becomes "part or particle of God."

## Philosophical Context

*Nature* emerged from Emerson's break with Unitarian orthodoxy. After resigning his pastorate at Boston's Second Church in 1832, Emerson traveled to Europe and met Romantic thinkers including Coleridge, Wordsworth, and Carlyle.

The essay synthesizes several intellectual currents:

- **German Idealism**: spirit pervades the material world (via Coleridge and Carlyle)

- **Swedenborgian mysticism**: correspondences between natural and spiritual realms

- **New England Unitarianism**: rejection of Calvinist predestination

- **Romanticism**: nature as a source of sublime experience

*Nature* provided the philosophical foundation for the [[transcendentalism|Transcendentalist movement]] that would flourish in Concord, Massachusetts, in the 1830s and 1840s. [[henry-david-thoreau]] acknowledged it as the inspiration for his experiment at Walden Pond.

## Selections in the Anthology

The following passage from *Nature* appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

> [030-001] To go into solitude, a man needs to retire as much from his chamber as from society. I am not solitary whilst I read and write, though nobody is with me. But if a man would be alone, let him look at the stars. The rays that come from those heavenly worlds will separate between him and what he touches.
>
> — From *Nature*, Chapter I (selection 68)

This excerpt — the essay's opening meditation on solitude, stars, and the sublime — captures Emerson's characteristic movement from sensory observation to spiritual insight.

## Critical Significance

*Nature* transformed American intellectual life. It declared intellectual independence from Europe as emphatically as the Declaration of Independence had declared political independence. Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr. called it "our intellectual Declaration of Independence."

The essay's influence extended across multiple domains.

In **literature**, it inspired [[henry-david-thoreau]], [[walt-whitman]], and the American Renaissance of the 1850s. In **philosophy**, it established the first distinctively American school of thought. In **environmental thought**, it provided an early articulation of nature's spiritual value.

Critics have noted the essay's idealism sometimes obscures the material realities of nature — a critique articulated by later environmental thinkers. Yet *Nature* remains the indispensable starting point for understanding the American Transcendentalist tradition.
