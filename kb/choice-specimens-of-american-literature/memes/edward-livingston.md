# Edward Livingston

## Biography

Edward Livingston (1764–1836) was an American jurist, statesman, and legal reformer whose career spanned the formative decades of the United States. Born in Clermont, New York, into the prominent Livingston family — his father Robert R. Livingston was a judge, and his brother Robert R. Livingston served as Chancellor of New York and negotiated the Louisiana Purchase — Edward graduated from the College of New Jersey (Princeton) in 1781 and was admitted to the New York bar in 1785.

Livingston was elected to the U.S. House of Representatives in 1795, serving until 1801, when he was appointed U.S. Attorney for the District of New York. That same year, he was also elected mayor of New York City. In 1803, a financial scandal involving a subordinate's embezzlement forced Livingston to resign both positions and move to New Orleans, then recently acquired as part of the Louisiana Purchase. He surrendered his personal fortune to repay the missing funds, a gesture of integrity that earned widespread respect.

In Louisiana, Livingston rebuilt his career and reputation. He drafted a provisional legal code for the territory and later, at the request of the Louisiana legislature, produced a complete civil code adopted in 1825 — a landmark of American legal reform that blended French and Spanish civil law traditions with Anglo-American jurisprudence. According to the brief biographical note in "Choice Specimens":

> Was born in New York; eminent as a statesman, and as the author of a code of laws for Louisiana, his adopted state. (017-003, 017-004)

Livingston's national reputation was restored; he served again in Congress (1823–1829) and as a U.S. Senator from Louisiana (1829–1831). President Andrew Jackson appointed him Secretary of State in 1831, a position he held for two years before being named minister to France, where he served until his death in 1836.

## Literary Career

Livingston's literary legacy lies primarily in legal and political discourse. His most influential work, the *System of Penal Law for the State of Louisiana* (1826), though never adopted in full, was widely studied in Europe and America. Livingston envisioned a comprehensive criminal code organized on rational principles — an approach distinguished by its abolition of capital punishment and its emphasis on rehabilitation over retribution. The work was praised by Victor Hugo and other European reformers.

Livingston's prose is marked by clarity, precision, and a philosophical breadth rare in legal writing. His 1826 address on the federal judiciary, excerpted in "Choice Specimens," exhibits these qualities in its defense of state court jurisdiction and the decentralized character of American justice:

> The judicial power of the United States is necessarily limited to national objects. The vast field of the law of property, the very extensive head of equity jurisdiction, and the principal rights and duties which flow from our civil and domestic relations, fall within the control, and we might almost say the exclusive cognizance, of the state governments. We look essentially to the state courts for protection to all these momentous interests. They touch, in their operation, every chord of human sympathy, and control our best destinies. (017-001)

Livingston's meditation on the judge's role — condemning the practice of judges acting as prosecutors, and calling for impartiality — reflects his sustained concern with the architecture of justice:

> A good judge should have no wish that the guilty should escape, or that the innocent should suffer; no false pity, no undue severity, should bias the unshaken rectitude of his judgment; calm in deliberation, firm in resolve, patient in investigating the truth, tenacious of it when discovered, he should join urbanity of manners, to dignity of demeanor, and an integrity above suspicion, to learning sufficient to guide, and talents to adorn his office. (017-002)

His defense of the right of petition — delivered in the House of Representatives — shows a more rhetorical and impassioned mode:

> Petition is *supplication* — it is *entreaty* — it is *prayer!* And where is the degree of vice or immorality which shall deprive the citizen of the right to *supplicate* for a boon, or to *pray for mercy!* (017-005)

## Selections in This Anthology

- "The Proper Office of a Judge" (017-001, 017-002) — From his 1826 address on the federal judiciary. A systematic defense of state court jurisdiction and a philosophical treatise on judicial impartiality.
- "On the Right of Petition" (017-005) — Delivered in the House of Representatives. An eloquent argument for the inviolability of the right of petition, regardless of the petitioner's character or station.

## Critical Reception

Livingston's reputation has endured principally among legal historians, who recognize him as a founder of American penal reform and a key figure in the codification movement. His Louisiana Civil Code, substantially intact after two centuries, remains his most tangible monument. Sir Henry Maine, the British jurist, called him "the first legal genius of modern times." In American letters, however, Livingston has been overshadowed by contemporaries whose literary reputations eclipse his more specialized contributions — though his prose, as the excerpts in "Choice Specimens" demonstrate, rises at moments to genuine eloquence.
