Charles Gayarré (1805–1895) was a Louisiana historian, jurist, and politician whose multi-volume *History of Louisiana* (029-010) established him as the foremost chronicler of his native state. A descendant of Spanish colonial officials, Gayarré served as Louisiana's Secretary of State and in the state legislature, bringing firsthand knowledge of Creole society and political culture to his historical writing.

## Biography

Charles Étienne Arthur Gayarré was born in New Orleans in 1805 to a prominent Creole family of Spanish and French descent. He was educated at the Collège d'Orléans in France and later studied law in Philadelphia, returning to Louisiana to practice. His political career included service in the Louisiana House of Representatives (1830–1832) and as Louisiana Secretary of State (1845–1852). A supporter of the Confederacy during the Civil War, Gayarré's later years were marked by financial difficulties, though he continued writing until his death in 1895.

## Literary Career

Gayarré's literary reputation rests primarily on his historical works. His *Histoire de la Louisiane* (1846–1847), written in French, was followed by an expanded English-language *History of Louisiana* (1854–1866) covering the French, Spanish, and American periods. He also wrote *Fernando de Lemos: Truth and Fiction* (1872), a novel, and *Aubert Dubayet* (1882), a historical romance. His prose style is characterized by dramatic portraiture and a romantic sensibility that enlivens his treatment of historical figures.

## Selections

The anthology presents Gayarré's vivid character sketch of Andrew Jackson from his *History of Louisiana*, depicting the general on the eve of the Battle of New Orleans.

### General Jackson at New Orleans

> [029-010] His very physiognomy prognosticated what soul was encased within the spare but well-ribbed form which had that "lean and hungry look" described by England's greatest bard as bespeaking little sleep of nights, but much of ambition, self-reliance, and impatience of control. His lip and eye denoted the man of unyielding temper, and his very hair, slightly silvered, stood erect like quills round his wrinkled brow, as if they scorned to bend.

> [029-010] Such was the man who, with a handful of raw militia, was to stand in the way of the veteran troops of England, whose boast it was to have triumphed over one of the greatest captains known in history. But those who entertained such distrust had hardly come in contact with General Jackson, when they felt that they had to deal with a master-spirit. True, he was rough hewn from the rock, but rock he was, and of that kind of rock which Providence chooses to select as a fit material to use in its structures of human greatness.

> [029-010] His will was of such an extraordinary nature that, like Christian faith, it could almost have accomplished prodigies and removed mountains. It is impossible to study the life of General Jackson without being convinced that this is the most remarkable feature of his character. His will had, as it were, the force and the fixity of fate; that will carried him triumphantly through his military and civil career, and through the difficulties of private life.

> [029-010] This man, when he took the command at New Orleans, had made up his mind to beat the English; and, as that mind was so constituted that it was not susceptible of entertaining much doubt as to the results of any of its resolves, he went to work with an innate confidence which transfused itself into the population he had been sent to protect.

## Critical Reception

Gayarré's *History of Louisiana* was widely praised by contemporaries including [[George Bancroft]] for its thorough research and engaging narrative style. Modern scholars value his work as both a historical source and a literary artifact of the nineteenth-century Creole perspective. His romantic portraiture of figures like [[Andrew Jackson]] has been noted for its psychological penetration, though later historians have moderated his more dramatic claims.
