## Biography

James Jackson Jarves (1818–1888) was born in Boston, Massachusetts, into a prosperous family with deep New England roots. In 1837, at the age of nineteen, he left Boston for the Hawaiian Islands, where his entrepreneurial spirit and literary inclinations soon found an outlet. In 1840 he founded *The Polynesian*, the first newspaper in the Kingdom of Hawaii, which became the official government publication and an important vehicle for recording the political and cultural life of the islands during the mid-nineteenth century.

After more than a decade in Hawaii, Jarves turned his attention increasingly toward art. He traveled to Europe in the early 1850s and settled in Florence, Italy, where he became one of the earliest American collectors of Italian Renaissance painting. At a time when American museums and collectors were focused almost exclusively on contemporary academic art, Jarves assembled a remarkable collection of early Italian "primitives" — works by artists such as Giotto, Fra Angelico, and Botticelli — that introduced American audiences to a tradition then largely unknown in the United States. He married twice, first to Elizabeth Swain and later to Isabella Heyden. Jarves spent the latter part of his life in Europe, dying in Tarasp, Switzerland, in 1888. His collection of Italian paintings was eventually acquired by Yale University, where it forms a cornerstone of the Yale University Art Gallery's early Renaissance holdings.

## Literary Career

Jarves's most important literary work is *Art Hints: Architecture, Sculpture, and Painting* (1855), a wide-ranging volume that sought to educate American readers about the principles and history of European art. The book appeared at a crucial moment in American cultural development, when the nation was beginning to establish its own museums, academies, and public collections. *Art Hints* is distinguished by its democratic conviction that art is not a luxury for the wealthy but a public good — a position that placed Jarves at odds with the prevailing American suspicion of the fine arts as aristocratic and European.

In 1864, Jarves published *The Art-Idea: Sculpture, Painting, and Architecture in America*, a more focused and polemical work that surveyed the state of the arts in the United States and called for the creation of public museums, galleries, and art schools on the European model. Together, *Art Hints* and *The Art-Idea* established Jarves as one of the earliest and most articulate American art critics, a bridge between the European art world and the emerging American audience. His writings anticipated the museum-building movement that would transform American cultural life in the decades after the Civil War. While his prose can be earnest and didactic, it is animated by a genuine faith in the civilizing power of beauty, a conviction that art belongs to all people and not merely to connoisseurs.

## Selections in This Anthology

Martin's *Choice Specimens* includes one selection from James Jackson Jarves: "The Art Idea" (No. 235), drawn from *Art Hints* (1855). This passage appears in Section 50 of the anthology. Jarves argues that art is an essential element of civilization and that governments have a duty to make it freely accessible to all citizens. Taken as a whole, the selection distills Jarves's central democratic thesis: that beauty is not a private indulgence but a public necessity.

In the first paragraph, Jarves insists that art must be woven into the fabric of public life:

> "The first duty of art, as we have already intimated, is to make our public buildings and places, as instructive and enjoyable as possible. They should be pleasurable, full of attractive beauty and eloquent teachings. Picturesque groupings of natural objects, architectural surprises, sermons from the sculptor's chisel and the painter's palette, the ravishment of the soul by its superior senses, the refinement of mind and body by the sympathetic power of beauty, — these are a portion of the means which a due estimation of art, as an element of civilization, inspires the ruling will to provide freely for all."
> (049-004)

He then argues that art restricted to the wealthy breeds resentment, while art offered freely becomes a shared inheritance — a claim that anticipates the philosophy behind the great American public museums:

> "If art be kept a rare and tabooed thing, a specialty for the rich and powerful, it excites in the vulgar mind, envy and hate; but proffer it freely to the public, and the public soon learns to delight in and protect it as its rightful inheritance."
> (049-004)

One of the most striking passages in the selection invokes the history of the Italian city-states, where art flourished even amid civil strife:

> "During the civil strifes of Italy, art flourished and was respected. Indeed, to some extent, it operated as a sort of peace society, and was held sacred when nothing else was. Even rude soldiers, amid the perils and necessities of sieges, turned aside destruction from the walls that sheltered it."
> (049-004)

The second paragraph of the selection draws a direct link between the awakening of public taste and the institutional structures required to sustain it:

> "The desire for art being awakened, museums to illustrate its technical and historical progress, and galleries to exhibit its master-works, become indispensable. In the light of education, appropriations for such purposes are as much a duty of the government as for any other purpose connected with the true welfare of the people; for its responsibilities extend over the entire social system."
> (049-005)

This passage is remarkable for its time, asserting that government funding for museums and galleries is not an optional luxury but an educational obligation on par with any other public good. Jarves's argument that government "responsibilities extend over the entire social system" reflects a broad, holistic vision of what a civilized society owes its citizens — a vision that would bear fruit in the establishment of the Metropolitan Museum of Art (1870), the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston (1870), and the Art Institute of Chicago (1879) in the years following his writing.

## Critical Reception

Jarves's critical writings earned him a solid reputation among cultured Americans of the mid-nineteenth century. *Art Hints* was favorably reviewed upon publication and went through multiple editions, finding an audience among the educated classes who were beginning to travel to Europe and encounter its artistic heritage firsthand. His inclusion in Martin's *Choice Specimens* (1874) alongside figures such as [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]] and [[henry-david-thoreau|Henry David Thoreau]] testifies to the esteem in which his prose was held.

In the twentieth century, Jarves's literary reputation faded as his role as a critic was overshadowed by his significance as a collector. Art historians, however, have continued to recognize his importance: the Jarves Collection at Yale remains a subject of scholarly attention, and his pioneering advocacy for early Italian painting is acknowledged in histories of American taste and collecting. Though he is not widely read today outside specialist circles, Jarves occupies a significant place in the history of American art criticism — a writer who argued, with conviction and foresight, that a democracy could and should make beauty a public trust.
