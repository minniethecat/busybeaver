# Noah Porter

## Biography

Noah Porter (1811–1892) was an American Congregational clergyman, philosopher, and educator. He was born in Farmington, Connecticut, into a family with deep roots in New England's clerical and intellectual life. Porter graduated from Yale College in 1831 and, after teaching at a grammar school and studying theology at Yale Divinity School, was ordained as a Congregational minister in 1836. He served pastorates in New Milford, Connecticut, and Springfield, Massachusetts, where he gained a reputation as a thoughtful preacher and a rising intellectual figure.

In 1846 Porter was appointed professor of moral philosophy and metaphysics at Yale College, a position he held for the remainder of his career. Upon the retirement of Theodore Dwight Woolsey in 1871, Porter was elected the eleventh president of Yale, serving until 1886. His presidency was marked by steady institutional growth, a cautious resistance to the elective system then being championed at Harvard by Charles W. Eliot, and a firm commitment to the moral and religious ends of collegiate education. Porter also served as editor-in-chief of the 1864 and 1890 editions of *Webster's American Dictionary of the English Language*, a responsibility he undertook with characteristic thoroughness.

## Literary Career

Porter's most significant philosophical work, *The Human Intellect* (1868), was one of the most ambitious American contributions to psychology and epistemology in the nineteenth century. Drawing on the Scottish common-sense realism that dominated American collegiate philosophy, Porter also engaged seriously with the British empiricists and the emerging German psychological tradition. His other major works include *The Elements of Moral Science* (1885), a widely-used college textbook, and *Books and Reading* (1871), a treatise on liberal education.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, titled "Science Magnifies God," is drawn from his essay "The Science of Nature versus the Science of Man." Porter argues that the scientific study of nature, properly understood, does not diminish religious faith but enlarges it:

> We contend at present only for the position that we cannot have a science of nature which does not regard the spirit of man as a part of nature. But is this all? Do man and nature exhaust the possibilities of being? (012-007)

He develops the argument that nature's vastness and complexity point beyond themselves:

> The materials with which we have to do in their most brilliant scientific theories seem at first to overwhelm us with their vastness and complexity. The bulks are so enormous, the forces are so mighty, the laws are so wide-sweeping, and at times so pitiless, the distances are so over-mastering... that we bow in mute and almost abject submission to the incomprehensible all. (012-007)

Yet the very act of scientific comprehension testifies to a spiritual reality beyond the material:

> We forget what our overmastering thought has done in subjecting this universe to its interpretations. Its vast distances have been annihilated, for we have connected the distant with the near by the one pervading force which Newton divined. (012-007)

## Critical Reception

Porter's *The Human Intellect* was well-received on both sides of the Atlantic, with the British philosopher James Martineau praising its scope and judiciousness. As Yale's president, Porter was respected — if sometimes considered overly conservative — for his defense of the traditional liberal arts curriculum against the encroachments of specialization and vocationalism. His editions of *Webster's Dictionary* were the standard authority for American usage until the publication of the *Century Dictionary*.

Porter's philosophical reputation declined in the twentieth century as pragmatism and analytic philosophy displaced the Scottish realism he represented. Historians of American higher education continue to study him as a transitional figure — a college president who held the old classical curriculum together even as the forces that would create the modern research university were gathering strength.
