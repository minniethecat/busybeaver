# Benjamin Silliman

Benjamin Silliman (1779–1864) was an American chemist, geologist, and educator who played a foundational role in establishing the sciences in the United States. A professor at Yale College for over fifty years, he founded the *American Journal of Science* in 1818 — still published today as the oldest continuously published scientific journal in the country.

Silliman's *A Tour to Canada*, from which his anthology selection is drawn, represents the intersection of scientific observation and literary travel writing that characterized antebellum American letters.

## Biography

Silliman was born on August 8, 1779, in North Stratford (now Trumbull), Connecticut. He graduated from Yale College in 1796 and, though initially destined for law, was persuaded by Yale's president, Timothy Dwight, to study chemistry and natural history in preparation for a new professorship.

With no scientific training available in America, Silliman studied in Edinburgh and London from 1805 to 1806, attending lectures in chemistry, geology, and mineralogy. Upon his return, he became Yale's first professor of chemistry and natural history, a position he held until 1853.

## Literary Career

Silliman's literary reputation rests on his ability to communicate scientific ideas to a popular audience. His *American Journal of Science* (often called "Silliman's Journal") published not only technical papers but also articles of general interest, contributing to the diffusion of scientific knowledge in the early Republic.

His travel writings — *A Tour to Canada* (1819) and *A Visit to Europe* (1853) — combine geological and botanical observation with the broader cultural commentary typical of the genre. The selection from *A Tour to Canada*, describing the Montmorenci Falls near Quebec, exemplifies his style: precise empirical description elevated by aesthetic appreciation.

Beyond his own writings, Silliman was a significant mentor. His students at Yale included [[edward-hitchcock]], the geologist and college president, and numerous other figures who shaped American science in the nineteenth century.

## Selections in the Anthology

The anthology includes Silliman's description of the Montmorenci Falls from *A Tour to Canada*.

[055-003]Silliman describes the cascade:

> The Montmorenci, after a gentle previous declivity, which greatly increases its velocity, takes its stupendous leap of two hundred and forty feet, into a chasm among the rocks, where it boils and foams in a natural rocky basin, from which, after its force is in some measure exhausted in its own whirlpools and eddies, it flows away in a gentle stream towards the St. Lawrence. The fall is nearly perpendicular, and appears not to deviate more than three or four degrees from it. This deviation is caused by the ledges of rock below, and is just sufficient to break the water completely into foam and spray. The effect on the beholder is most delightful. The river, at some distance, seems suspended in a sheet of billowy foam, and contrasted as it is, with the black frowning abyss into which it falls, it is an object of the highest interest.

[055-005]He continues with a meditation on the rainbow formed in the spray:

> The sheet of foam which breaks over the ridge is more and more divided as it is dashed against the successive layers of rocks... The rainbow we saw in great perfection; bow within bow, and (what I never saw elsewhere so perfectly), as I advanced into the spray, the bow became complete, myself being a part of its circumference, and its transcendent glories encircling my own person.

This image — the observer standing at the center of a rainbow — captures the Romantic fusion of scientific observation and personal experience that distinguishes Silliman's travel writing from purely technical description.

## Critical Reception

Silliman enjoyed immense prestige in his lifetime. He was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, the American Philosophical Society, and numerous European scientific bodies. His *American Journal of Science* was recognized internationally as a serious scientific publication.

The inclusion of Silliman in Martin's *Choice Specimens* is noteworthy: it signals the anthology's ambition to represent scientific prose alongside belletristic writing, acknowledging that American literary culture encompassed natural philosophy as well as poetry and fiction.
