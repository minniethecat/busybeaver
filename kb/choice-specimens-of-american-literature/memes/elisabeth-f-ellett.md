Elisabeth Fries Ellett (1818–1877) was an American historian, poet, and biographer who pioneered the historical study of women's experiences in early America. At a time when the writing of history was almost exclusively a male enterprise, Ellett devoted her career to recovering and celebrating the lives of women who had been overlooked by traditional scholarship. Her two major works—*The Women of the American Revolution* (1848) and *Pioneer Women of the West* (1852)—established a genre of collective female biography that would not be fully recognized until the rise of women's history in the twentieth century. Benj. N. Martin included a passage from her work in this anthology, acknowledging her place among the prominent American writers of the mid-nineteenth century.

## Biography

Elisabeth Fries Ellett was born in 1818 in New York, where she received an education that was unusually thorough for a young woman of her era. She demonstrated literary talent early, publishing poetry in periodicals while still in her teens. In 1835, at the age of seventeen, she published her first volume of verse, *Poems, Translated and Original*, which included her translation of Silvio Pellico's tragedy *Euphemio of Messina*. Her early literary ambitions placed her in the circle of New York writers and editors who were shaping the nation's fledgling literary culture.

In the late 1830s, she married William Henry Ellett, a professor of chemistry at Columbia College (later the University of South Carolina). The marriage brought her into the intellectual life of the South, where she continued to write and publish. After the death of her husband, she supported herself through her literary work—a necessity that sharpened her professional identity at a time when few women could claim writing as a career. Her later years were devoted to historical research and writing; she traveled extensively to gather materials and interview descendants of the figures she chronicled. Elisabeth F. Ellett died in 1877, leaving behind a body of work that, while largely forgotten in the decades following her death, has since been recognized as foundational to the field of American women's history.

## Literary Career

Ellett's career began with poetry, but her lasting contribution lay in historical biography. Her breakthrough came with *The Women of the American Revolution* (1848), a two-volume work that assembled biographical sketches of more than one hundred and sixty women who had supported the revolutionary cause. Drawing on personal letters, diaries, and interviews with descendants, Ellett constructed a narrative of female patriotism that had no precedent in American historiography. The work was widely read and went through multiple editions, establishing her reputation as a serious researcher and writer.

Her most ambitious project, however, was *Pioneer Women of the West* (1852), a study of the women who accompanied and sustained the westward expansion of the American frontier. The book collected biographical accounts from Kentucky, Tennessee, Ohio, and the broader trans-Appalachian region, focusing on the domestic heroism of women who endured Indian attacks, wilderness privation, and the loss of husbands and children. Ellett's method was documentary rather than analytic—she allowed the voices of her subjects and their descendants to speak through letters and oral testimony—but her insistence that women's experience constituted a legitimate subject of historical inquiry was itself a radical act. She was one of the first American historians to argue, implicitly through the sheer weight of accumulated evidence, that the national story could not be told without accounting for the contributions of women.

In addition to her historical work, Ellett was a prolific contributor to periodicals including *Godey's Lady's Book*, *Graham's Magazine*, and the *Southern Literary Messenger*. She also wrote on art criticism and published translations from French, German, and Italian. Her range as a writer—from poetry to translation to documentary history—reflected both the versatility expected of a professional woman of letters in the mid-nineteenth century and the constraints of a literary marketplace that confined female writers to certain acceptable genres. By the end of her career, she had produced over a dozen books, making her one of the most industrious female authors of her generation.

## Selections in This Anthology

### 049-003 — Escape of Mary Bledsoe from the Indians (from *Pioneer Women of the West*)

This selection, drawn from *Pioneer Women of the West*, tells the story of Mary Bledsoe, a frontier settler whose life was marked by extraordinary courage and devastating loss. The episode centers on Spencer, a companion who risks his life to protect Mary during an Indian pursuit. The narrative exemplifies Ellett's characteristic method: she elevates the everyday heroism of frontier women to the level of patriotic legend, while recording the cost of that heroism in vividly personal terms.

> It was not consistent with Spencer's chivalrous character to attempt to save himself by leaving his companion to the mercy of the foe. Bidding her retreat as fast as possible, and encouraging her to keep her seat firmly, he protected her by following more slowly in her rear, with his trusty rifle in his hand. (049-003)

Spencer's "chivalrous character" is the hinge of the episode—not the romantic chivalry of medieval romance, but the practical courage of a man who understands that a single rifle shot would give away his position and seal both their fates. He does not fire; he threatens, and his reputation as a marksman is enough to keep the Indians at bay. The restraint Ellett describes here mirrors her own narrative restraint: she does not embellish, but lets the facts carry the moral weight.

> Bereaved of her husband, sons, and brother-in-law, by the murderous savages, Mrs. Bledsoe was obliged to undertake not only the charge of her husband's estate, but the care of the children, and their education and settlement in life. These duties were discharged with unwavering energy and Christian patience. (049-003)

The second half of the passage turns from the dramatic escape to the quieter, longer heroism of Mary Bledsoe's widowhood. Ellett's phrase "unwavering energy and Christian patience" captures the dual register in which she presents her subjects: they are at once practical managers of property and households, and moral exemplars whose suffering sanctifies the national project. The wider significance Ellett claims for these women—"their patriotic labors, their struggles with the surrounding savages... will never be forgotten"—is her central argument in miniature: the frontier was settled as much by the endurance of women as by the arms of men.

## Critical Reception

During her lifetime, Ellett enjoyed a reputation as a diligent and respected historical writer. *The Women of the American Revolution* was widely reviewed and praised for its thoroughness, and it became a standard reference in the decades following its publication. Her work was cited by subsequent historians, and she was regarded by contemporaries as one of the leading female literary figures of her day. Yet, like many women writers of the nineteenth century, Ellett's reputation faded in the early twentieth century as academic historiography professionalized and dismissed the "amateur" scholarship that had preceded it.

The rise of feminist historiography in the 1970s and 1980s brought a reassessment of Ellett's contributions. Scholars recognized that her work, for all its Victorian sentimentality and lack of modern scholarly apparatus, had preserved documentary evidence that would otherwise have been lost—letters, oral histories, and family traditions that constituted the only surviving record of countless women's lives. Her books are now understood as precocious examples of social history, anticipating by more than a century the academic field of women's history. Modern critics value her not only for the archive she created but for the argument implicit in her method: that narrative history, grounded in the particularity of individual experience, could serve as a vehicle for recovering voices that official history had silenced.
