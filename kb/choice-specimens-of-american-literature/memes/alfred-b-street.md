# Alfred B. Street

## Biography

Alfred Billings Street (1811–1881) was an American poet and librarian. Born in Poughkeepsie, New York, he spent most of his life in Albany, where he served as the New York State Librarian from 1848 until his death. Street was a devoted student of nature, and his poetry is distinguished by its precise, detailed observation of the American landscape, particularly the forests, mountains, and waterways of New York State. His work earned him the informal title "Poet of the Catskills."

## Literary Career

Street published several collections of poetry, including *The Burning of Schenectady, and Other Poems* (1842), *Drawings and Tintings* (1844), and *Frontenac: A Poem of the Iroquois* (1849). His verse is representative of the American Romantic tradition's engagement with the natural world, emphasizing the wild sublimity of untamed landscapes. Unlike the more philosophical nature poetry of [[william-cullen-bryant|William Cullen Bryant]] or [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]], Street's approach is primarily descriptive and pictorial — he sought to capture in words the visual splendor of the American wilderness. His poem "The Falls of the Mongaup" is among his most accomplished works, demonstrating his characteristic combination of vivid imagery and formal control.

## Selections in This Anthology

Martin's *Choice Specimens* includes two selections from Alfred B. Street: "An Autumn Landscape" (No. 385), from his *Poems*, and "The Falls of the Mongaup" (No. 386), from *Drawings and Tintings*. Both appear in Section 74 of the anthology.

From "An Autumn Landscape":

> "Overhead / There is a blending of cloud, haze, and sky; / A silvery sheet, with spaces of soft hue; / A trembling veil of gauze is stretched athwart / The shadowy hill-sides and dark forest-flanks; / A soothing quiet broods upon the air, / And the faint sunshine winks with drowsiness."
> (073-011)

> "Nature in her change / Varies each day, as in the world of man / She moulds the differing features. Yea, each leaf / Is variant from its fellow. Yet her works / Are blended in a glorious harmony, / For thus God made his earth."
> (073-013)

From "The Falls of the Mongaup":

> "Swift as an arrow from the bow; / Headlong the torrent leaps, / Then tumbling round, in dazzling snow / And dizzy whirls it sweeps; / Then, shooting through the narrow aisle / Of this sublime cathedral pile, / Amidst its vastness, dark and grim, / It peals its everlasting hymn."
> (073-015)

> "Myriads of man's time-measured race / Have vanished from the earth, / Nor left a memory of their trace, / Since first this scene had birth; / These waters, thundering now along, / Joined in Creation's matin-song; / And only by their dial-trees / Have known the lapse of centuries!"
> (073-021)

## Critical Reception

Street was widely read in his day and appreciated for the accuracy and beauty of his nature descriptions. His position as State Librarian gave him visibility in New York literary circles. While modern critics have sometimes faulted his work for lacking the philosophical depth of his more celebrated contemporaries, his poetry remains a valuable record of nineteenth-century American attitudes toward the natural landscape. His descriptive precision has been recognized as anticipating certain aspects of later American nature writing.
