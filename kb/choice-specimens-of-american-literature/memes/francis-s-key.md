## Biography

Francis Scott Key (1779–1843) was an American lawyer, poet, and author of the United States national anthem.
Born at his family's estate, Terra Rubra, in Frederick County, Maryland (now Carroll County), he studied at
St. John's College in Annapolis, graduating in 1796. Key established a successful legal practice in Georgetown,
Washington, D.C., and served as the United States Attorney for the District of Columbia from 1833 to 1841.

During the War of 1812, Key undertook a mission to negotiate the release of Dr. William Beanes, an American
physician captured by British forces. In September 1814, Key boarded a British ship in Chesapeake Bay under a
flag of truce. He secured Beanes's release, but both men were detained aboard the vessel during the night of
September 13–14 so they could not warn American forces of the impending bombardment of Fort McHenry.
Throughout the night, Key watched the British bombardment of the fort, and at dawn he saw the American flag
still flying over the ramparts — a sight that inspired him to write the verses of "The Star-Spangled Banner."

Key was a devout Episcopalian and was active in the American Colonization Society, which sought to resettle
free African Americans in Liberia. He argued numerous cases before the Supreme Court and served under
President [[andrew-jackson]] as U.S. Attorney.

## Literary Career

Key's literary output was modest, consisting primarily of occasional poems on religious and patriotic themes.
His collected poems were published posthumously as *Poems of the Late Francis S. Key, Esq.* (1857).

"The Star-Spangled Banner" was first published as a broadside under the title "Defence of Fort McHenry"
and was soon set to the tune of "To Anacreon in Heaven," a popular British drinking song. The song gained
rapid popularity and was adopted by the U.S. Navy for official occasions in 1889, by the Army in 1916,
and finally as the national anthem by congressional resolution in 1931. Despite its fame, the poem
was Key's only work to achieve lasting recognition.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes Key's most famous work in its entirety:

- **"The Star-Spangled Banner"** (065-019–065-022): The complete four-stanza poem, beginning with the
  famous opening lines:

> "O say, can you see, by the dawn's early light,
> What so proudly we hailed, at the twilight's last gleaming?
> Whose broad stripes and bright stars, through the perilous fight,
> O'er the ramparts we watched, were so gallantly streaming;
> And the rocket's red glare, the bombs bursting in air,
> Gave proof through the night that our flag was still there:" (065-019)

The final stanza (065-022) concludes with the patriotic affirmation:

> "And the Star-Spangled Banner in triumph shall wave
> O'er the land of the free and the home of the brave!" (065-022)

The editor, Benj. N. Martin, includes Key in the "Later Poets" section of the anthology,
noting his status as a Maryland native whose single celebrated poem achieved a unique
place in American culture.

## Critical Reception

"The Star-Spangled Banner" has been both celebrated and critiqued. Its defenders praise the poem's
stirring imagery and its association with a defining moment in American history. Critics note the
difficulty of its wide melodic range and the martial character of its lesser-known later stanzas.
In the literary history of the United States, Key occupies an unusual position: a minor poet who
produced one of the most recognized texts in American civic life. Recent scholarship has also
drawn attention to Key's complex legacy as a slaveholder and colonization advocate.
