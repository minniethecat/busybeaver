## Biography

Washington Allston (1779–1843) was an American painter and poet, born in Georgetown County, South Carolina.
A graduate of Harvard College (1800), he traveled to Europe in 1801 to study art,
spending extended periods in Rome, Paris, and London. In England, he formed a close friendship
with the poet [[samuel-taylor-coleridge]], whose aesthetic philosophy deeply influenced
Allston's own theories of art and imagination.

Upon returning to the United States in 1818, Allston settled in Cambridge, Massachusetts,
where he spent the remainder of his life producing large-scale historical and biblical paintings,
including *Belshazzar's Feast*, on which he worked for over twenty-five years without completing it.

## Literary Career

Allston's literary output, though limited in volume, was highly regarded by his contemporaries.
His novel *Monaldi* (1841) is a Gothic romance set in Italy, exploring themes of artistic jealousy
and madness. His poetry, collected in *The Sylphs of the Seasons and Other Poems* (1813),
was praised by critics for its refined sensibility and imaginative power.

> "He was a man of genius, and genius of a high order," wrote his friend [[richard-henry-dana]]
> (001-179), describing Allston's ability to infuse his paintings with narrative and his writing
> with pictorial vividness.

His *Lectures on Art*, published posthumously in 1850, articulated a transcendental theory
of artistic creation that influenced later American aesthetic thought.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Allston was celebrated as the foremost American painter of his generation.
[[ralph-waldo-emerson]] praised his "artistic conscience," while [[edgar-allan-poe]]
commended his poetry for its "exquisite purity of style."

Art historians now regard Allston as a crucial transitional figure between Neoclassicism
and Romanticism in American art, and as one of the first American artists to successfully
synthesize European training with a distinctly American sensibility.
