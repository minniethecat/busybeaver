# John Quincy Adams

## Biography

John Quincy Adams (1767–1848) was an American statesman, diplomat, and the sixth President of the United States (1825–1829). The eldest son of John Adams, the second President, John Quincy Adams received a diplomatic education unique in American history: he accompanied his father on missions to Europe, studied at the University of Leiden, and served as secretary to the American minister to Russia at the age of fourteen. He graduated from Harvard College in 1787 and was admitted to the bar in 1790.

Adams's diplomatic career was among the most distinguished in American history. He served as minister to the Netherlands, Portugal, Prussia, Russia, and Great Britain, and as the chief American negotiator of the Treaty of Ghent (1814), which ended the War of 1812. As Secretary of State under James Monroe (1817–1825), Adams was the principal architect of the Monroe Doctrine and negotiated the Adams-Onís Treaty, which acquired Florida and defined the western boundary of the Louisiana Purchase.

His presidency was marred by the "corrupt bargain" controversy surrounding his election by the House of Representatives in 1824 and by an unrelenting opposition from Jacksonian Democrats. Yet Adams's greatest public service came after his presidency, when he returned to Washington as a member of the House of Representatives (1831–1848). In Congress, "Old Man Eloquent" became the foremost antislavery voice in the federal government, waging a sustained battle against the "gag rule" that prohibited the discussion of abolition petitions. He died at his desk in the Capitol, collapsing after casting a vote against a resolution of thanks to the generals of the Mexican-American War.

## Literary Career

John Quincy Adams was one of the most prolific writers ever to occupy the American presidency. His diary — kept for nearly sixty-nine years — runs to fifty-one volumes and constitutes an unparalleled record of American political and intellectual life from the Washington administration through the eve of the Civil War. His published works include poetry, literary criticism, political pamphlets, and rhetorical studies. His "Lectures on Rhetoric and Oratory," delivered while he was Boylston Professor at Harvard, established him as a serious scholar of classical models.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) includes two selections from Adams's congressional career. The first, from his "Speech on the Right of Petition," is a sustained defense of the most fundamental of republican liberties against a House leadership that sought to silence antislavery petitioners:

> Sir, it is well known, that, from the time I entered this House, down to the present day, I have felt it a sacred duty to present any petition, couched in respectful language, from any citizen of the United States, be its object what it may; be the prayer of it that in which I could concur, or that to which I was utterly opposed. (017-005)

Adams's argument is relentlessly egalitarian. He refuses to accept any restriction on the right of petition based on the petitioner's character or station:

> Where is your law which says that the mean, and the low, and the degraded, shall be deprived of the right of petition, if their moral character is not good? Where, in the land of freemen, was the right of petition ever placed on the exclusive basis of morality and virtue? Petition is supplication — it is entreaty — it is prayer! And where is the degree of vice or immorality which shall deprive the citizen of the right to supplicate for a boon, or to pray for mercy! (017-005)

The rhetorical climax of the passage turns on Adams's insistence that the right belongs to all, not merely the respectable: "The right of petition belongs to all. And so far from refusing to present a petition because it might come from those low in the estimation of the world, it, would be an additional incentive, if such incentive were wanting" (017-005). For eight years, Adams fought the gag rule, and for eight years the rule was renewed — until 1844, when his persistence at last prevailed.

The second selection, from Adams's "Discourse on the Jubilee of the Constitution" (1839), offers a measured assessment of George Washington's presidency:

> When Solon, by the appointment of the people of Athens, had formed, and prevailed upon them to adopt a code of fundamental laws, the best that they would bear, he went into voluntary banishment for ten years, to save his system from the batteries of rival statesmen working upon popular passions and prejudices excited against his person. In eight years of a turbulent and tempestuous administration, Washington had settled upon firm foundations the practical execution of the Constitution of the United States. (017-006)

Adams enumerates Washington's achievements with the precision of a diplomat who understood what each item had cost: "he had subdued all opposition to the Constitution itself; had averted all dangers of European war; had redeemed the captive children of his country from Algiers; had reduced by chastisement, and conciliated by kindness, the most hostile of the Indian tribes; had restored the credit of the nation, and redeemed their reputation of fidelity to their engagements" (017-006). The verdict is unequivocal: "The administration of Washington fixed the character of the Constitution of the United States, as a practical system of government, which it retains to this day" (017-006).

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from John Quincy Adams's writings appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *The Right of Petition Universal* (017-005) — From Adams's "Speech on the Right of Petition" in the House of Representatives (1838). An impassioned defense of the constitutional right to petition Congress, delivered in the context of the congressional gag rule that prohibited the reception of antislavery petitions. Adams argues that the right of petition cannot be restricted on grounds of the petitioner's moral character or the content of the prayer.

- *The Administration of Washington* (017-006) — From a "Discourse on the Jubilee of the Constitution" (1839), delivered on the fiftieth anniversary of the government under the Constitution. Adams compares Washington to Solon, the Athenian lawgiver, and surveys the achievements of the first presidency — subduing constitutional opposition, averting foreign war, restoring national credit, and establishing the practical character of American government.

## Critical Reception

John Quincy Adams's historical reputation has undergone a significant reassessment in recent decades. Long overshadowed by the more charismatic presidencies of Andrew Jackson and Abraham Lincoln, Adams is now recognized as one of the most consequential American statesmen of the nineteenth century. His post-presidential career in Congress — particularly his battle against the gag rule, dramatized in the film "Amistad" (1997) — has attracted renewed scholarly and popular attention.

Adams's diary, which he began at the age of twelve and maintained until his death, is an incomparable literary and historical document. Historians have mined it for its candid portraits of contemporaries, its detailed accounts of political negotiations, and its record of Adams's inner life — including his struggles with ambition, depression, and religious faith. His published writings, while less read than those of Jefferson or Lincoln, reveal a mind of extraordinary range and discipline, equally at home in classical philology, constitutional law, and diplomatic statecraft.
