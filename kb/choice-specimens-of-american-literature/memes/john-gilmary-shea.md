## Biography

John Gilmary Shea (1824–1892) was born in New York City to Irish immigrant parents. Educated at Columbia College and later at St. John's College (now Fordham University), he briefly studied law before turning to historical research. A devout Catholic, Shea devoted his life to documenting the history of the Catholic Church in America — a subject that had been largely neglected by Protestant-dominated academic institutions of his era.

Shea never married and lived modestly, supporting himself through editorial work for Catholic periodicals while pursuing his historical research independently. He was a founder of the United States Catholic Historical Society and received an honorary doctorate from Georgetown University in recognition of his scholarly contributions. He died in 1892 in Elizabeth, New Jersey.

## Literary Career

Shea's magnum opus was the four-volume *History of the Catholic Church in the United States* (1886–1892), but his earlier work *History of the Catholic Missions among the Indian Tribes of the United States, 1529–1854* (1854) established his reputation. In this work, Shea documented the extensive missionary efforts of French, Spanish, and English-speaking Catholic orders among Native American nations.

Shea's scholarship was groundbreaking in its scope: he translated and published rare missionary narratives from French and Spanish sources, compiled previously scattered records of diocesan history, and offered one of the earliest comprehensive accounts of Catholic presence in colonial America. His work helped correct the historiographic bias that had minimized the Catholic contribution to American settlement and culture.

## Selections

The corpus presents a passage from Shea's *History of the Catholic Missions among the Indians*, in which he reflects on the providential significance of America's discovery and the formidable obstacles facing early missionaries.

### Difficulties of the Enterprise (031-013–031-016)

Shea opens with a theological frame — the discovery of America as a divine plan for spreading the gospel — before cataloguing the practical hardships of mission work. His description of the continent's pre-contact state emphasizes the vast diversity of Native tribes, the absence of European material culture, and the physical dangers confronting missionaries:

> [031-013] The discovery of America, like every other event in the history of the world, had, in the designs of God, the great object of the salvation of mankind. In that event, more clearly, perhaps, than it is often given to us here below, we can see and adore that Providence which thus gave to millions, long sundered from the rest of man by pathless oceans, the light of the gospel, and the proffered boon of redemption.... The field was one as yet unmatched for extent and difficulty. That region now studded with cities and towns, traversed in every direction by the panting steam-car or lightning telegraph, was then an almost unbroken forest....

> [031-014] The simplest arts of civilized life were unknown. In one little section of the Gila and Rio Grande, the people spun and wove a native cotton, manufactured a rude pottery, and lived in houses or castle-towns of unburnt bricks. Elsewhere the canoe or cabin of bark or hides, and the arabesque mat, denoted the highest point of social progress.

> [031-016] The country itself presented a thousand obstacles: there was danger from flood, danger from wild beasts, danger from the roving savage, danger from false friends, danger from the furious rapids on rivers, danger of loss of sight, of health, of use of motion and of limbs, in the new, strange life of an Indian wigwam....

## Reception

Shea was recognized during his lifetime as the foremost American Catholic historian and the preeminent scholar of early Catholic missions. His work was cited extensively by both Catholic and Protestant historians. Modern assessments acknowledge him as a pioneering figure in American religious historiography, though some scholars note that his devotional commitments sometimes shaped his interpretive framework. His translations of French and Spanish missionary documents remain an essential resource for colonial American historians.
