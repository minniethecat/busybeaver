# Brantz Mayer

## Biography

Brantz Mayer (1809–1879) was an American author, historian, and diplomat. Born in Baltimore, Maryland, to a prominent merchant family of German descent, Mayer was educated at St. Mary's College in Baltimore and later studied law. He was admitted to the bar in 1829 but soon turned from legal practice to literary and historical pursuits.

Mayer's civic involvement in Baltimore was substantial. He was one of the founders of the Maryland Historical Society in 1844 and served as its corresponding secretary and librarian for many years, building the society's collections into one of the most significant state historical archives in the country. His diplomatic career included service as secretary of the United States legation to Mexico in the 1840s, an experience that shaped his later historical writing.

Mayer traveled extensively in Mexico and Central America, and his observations of the region — its people, antiquities, and political upheavals — became the basis for several of his most important books. He was an early American voice in the field of Mesoamerican archaeology and ethnography, bringing the discoveries of Stephens and Catherwood to a broader audience and contributing his own researches into Aztec and Toltec culture.

## Literary Career

Mayer's major works include *Mexico As It Was and As It Is* (1844), an account of his diplomatic service and travels; *Mexico, Aztec, Spanish and Republican* (1852), a comprehensive historical survey; and *Observations on Mexican History and Archaeology* (1856), published by the Smithsonian Institution. His *Calvert and Penn* (1852) examined the founding of Maryland, while *Captain Canot; or, Twenty Years of an African Slaver* (1854) was a controversial and widely read ghostwritten narrative exposing the Atlantic slave trade.

Mayer also contributed to American Revolutionary War history with his biography of John Sevier, the Tennessee frontiersman and governor, and wrote on the Battle of New Orleans. The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* offers a character portrait of Andrew Jackson at New Orleans that emphasizes the General's indomitable will:

> True, he was rough hewn from the rock, but rock he was, and of that kind of rock which Providence chooses to select as a fit material to use in its structures of human greatness. (029-010)

Mayer's portrait of Jackson is less concerned with military tactics than with the moral and psychological dimensions of leadership — the force of will that, in Mayer's telling, functioned almost as a form of genius:

> In him it supplied the place of genius — or, rather, it was almost genius. (029-010)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Character of Andrew Jackson* (029-010) — From Mayer's history of the Mexican War and the Southwestern frontier. A portrait of Andrew Jackson on the eve of the Battle of New Orleans, celebrating Jackson's force of will, physical presence, and the "master-spirit" that inspired confidence in the raw militia confronting Britain's veteran troops. Mayer describes Jackson's "lean and hungry look," his unyielding temper, and the "lofty will" that "towered into sublimity."

## Critical Reception

Mayer was well regarded in his own time as a diligent historian and a graceful writer. His works on Mexico were among the most widely read American accounts of that country before the Civil War, and his editorship at the Maryland Historical Society established standards of archival scholarship that influenced a generation of local historians. Edgar Allan Poe reviewed Mayer's work favorably, and he moved comfortably in the literary circles of mid-century Baltimore and New York.

Mayer's subsequent reputation has been modest. He produced no single masterwork that secured a place in the American literary canon, and his histories — while competent — were superseded by later scholars with access to broader archives. His most enduring contribution may be institutional rather than literary: the Maryland Historical Society, which preserved the raw materials upon which much subsequent Maryland history was built.
