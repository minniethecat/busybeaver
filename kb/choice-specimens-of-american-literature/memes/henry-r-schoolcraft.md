# Henry R. Schoolcraft

## Biography

Henry Rowe Schoolcraft (1793–1864) was an American geographer, geologist, and ethnologist. Born in Guilderland, New York, he studied chemistry and mineralogy at Union College and Middlebury College before embarking on a career of exploration and scholarship. In 1820, he accompanied the Lewis Cass expedition to the upper Great Lakes region, and in 1832 he led an expedition that identified Lake Itasca as the true source of the Mississippi River.

Schoolcraft's most significant contribution to American letters was his extensive study of Native American cultures, particularly the Ojibwe (Chippewa) people of the Great Lakes region. In 1823, he married Jane Johnston, of Ojibwe and Irish ancestry, whose knowledge of Ojibwe language and oral tradition profoundly shaped his ethnographic work. He served as Superintendent of Indian Affairs for Michigan (1836–1841) and later compiled the monumental six-volume *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1851–1857).

## Literary Career

Schoolcraft's major literary achievement was his documentation of Ojibwe oral narratives and cultural practices, which he published in *Algic Researches* (1839) and other works. These collections of Native American legends and myths directly influenced Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, who drew extensively on Schoolcraft's material for *The Song of Hiawatha* (1855). Schoolcraft's prose combines the observational precision of the natural scientist with a genuine, if sometimes paternalistic, appreciation for the cultures he documented.

Beyond his ethnographic work, Schoolcraft wrote on geology, geography, and the mineral resources of the American frontier. His travel narratives and government reports provided some of the earliest detailed descriptions of the upper Mississippi Valley and Great Lakes region.

## Critical Reception

Schoolcraft's ethnographic work has undergone significant reassessment. Modern scholars acknowledge the unprecedented scope of his documentation while critiquing the colonial context and mediating lens through which he presented Native American cultures. His role as a source for Longfellow's *Hiawatha* ensures his enduring place in American literary history, and his geographical and geological contributions remain part of the historical record of American exploration.
