## Biography

Alexander Slidell Mackenzie (1803–1848) was an American naval officer and author. He entered the United States Navy as a midshipman in 1815 and rose through the ranks, eventually attaining the rank of commander. His naval career included service in the Mediterranean, the Pacific, and along the coast of South America. (023-006)

Mackenzie was the elder brother of John Slidell, a prominent Confederate diplomat. He adopted the surname Mackenzie to distinguish himself from other branches of the family.

## Literary Career

Mackenzie was best known for his biographical and travel writings. His most celebrated work, *Life of Commodore Decatur*, chronicled the exploits of Stephen Decatur, the naval hero of the Barbary Wars and the War of 1812. The biography was praised for its vivid narrative style and its intimate portrait of one of America's most daring naval commanders.

He also authored *A Year in Spain* (1829), a travelogue based on his experiences in the Iberian Peninsula, and *Life of John Paul Jones*, a biography of the Revolutionary War naval hero. His writings combined first-hand maritime knowledge with a flair for dramatic storytelling.

## Selections in the Corpus

Mackenzie appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* through an excerpt from his *Life of Commodore Decatur*, titled "Recapture and Burning of the Frigate 'Philadelphia' at Tripoli" (selection 106). The passage recounts one of the most celebrated exploits in American naval history.

> When all were safely assembled on the deck of the Intrepid, (for so admirably had the service been executed that not a man was missing, and only one slightly wounded,) Decatur gave the order to cut the fasts and shove off. The necessity for prompt obedience and exertion was urgent. The flames had now gained the lower rigging, and ascended to the tops; they darted furiously from the ports, flashing from the quarter gallery round the mizzen of the Intrepid, as her stern dropped clear of the ship. (023-004)

> With destruction thus encompassing them within and without, Decatur and his brave followers were unmoved. Calmly they put forth the necessary exertion, breasted the Intrepid off with spars, and pressing on their sweeps, caused her slowly to withdraw from the vicinity of the burning mass. (023-005)

The selection captures the daring of Decatur's 1804 raid on Tripoli harbor, where he and his men boarded and burned the captured USS *Philadelphia* to prevent its use by the Barbary pirates. Admiral Horatio Nelson reportedly called it "the most bold and daring act of the age."

## Style and Themes

Mackenzie's prose is characterized by its dramatic pacing and attention to tactical detail. He wrote with the authority of a serving naval officer who understood the technical aspects of seamanship and naval combat, while also possessing a romantic sensibility suited to the heroic subjects he chose. His *Life of Decatur* in particular helped to establish the genre of American naval biography.

## Reception and Legacy

*Life of Commodore Decatur* was well received upon publication and remained a standard reference on Decatur's life for much of the 19th century. Mackenzie's contribution to American literature lies in his role as a chronicler of the early U.S. Navy's heroic age, preserving the memory of its first generation of commanders for posterity.

Mackenzie died in 1848 at the age of forty-five. His name is also associated with the controversial "Somers Affair" of 1842, in which he, as captain of the brig USS *Somers*, ordered the execution of three crewmen suspected of mutiny — an incident that later influenced naval discipline procedures.
