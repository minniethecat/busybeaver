# Maria Brooks

## Biography

Maria Gowen Brooks (1795–1845) was an American poet known for the epic *Zophiël; or, The Bride of Seven*, a work praised by Robert Southey, who christened her "Maria del Occidente" — Mary of the West. Born in Medford, Massachusetts, she was the daughter of a prosperous merchant. Her early life was marked by both privilege and tragedy; she was orphaned at a young age and raised by a family friend.

In 1810, at fifteen, she married John Brooks, a Boston merchant, and settled in that city. The marriage was not a happy one, and following her husband's financial ruin and death, Maria turned to literature as a means of emotional and financial survival. She moved to Cuba in the 1820s, where she lived with a relative who owned a coffee plantation, and it was there that much of *Zophiël* was composed.

Her literary career took a decisive turn when Robert Southey, then Poet Laureate of England, read portions of the manuscript and gave it his enthusiastic endorsement. He arranged for its publication in London in 1833. Southey admired Brooks's fusion of Miltonic grandeur with a Romantic sensibility, and he saw in her work evidence that America could produce poetry of the highest order.

Brooks spent her final years in London and later on the Isle of Wight, where she died in 1845.

## Literary Career

Brooks's major work is *Zophiël; or, The Bride of Seven*, an epic poem in six cantos that retells the story of Sara, the daughter of Raguel, from the Book of Tobit. The poem's central conceit — that the heroine, beloved by the angel Zophiël, endures the death of seven bridegrooms before her true marriage can be consummated — allows Brooks to explore themes of divine love, earthly passion, and spiritual transcendence in richly figurative language.

Southey's approbation was the making of Brooks's reputation. He declared that no living English poet had written anything comparable, and he placed *Zophiël* alongside the works of Milton and Tasso. This was extravagant praise, but it ensured that Brooks was taken seriously on both sides of the Atlantic.

Her other works include the shorter poem "Marriage," from which the *Choice Specimens* selection is taken, and various lyrics published in American periodicals — though her literary output beyond *Zophiël* is modest.

Brooks is catalogued in the Table of Contents of *Choice Specimens* as "Maria Brooks, 1795-1845" with the poem "Marriage" (001-198, 344).

## Selections in This Anthology

- *"Marriage"* (067-039, 344) — An excerpt from *Zophiël*, Canto III, meditating on the Platonic ideal of the soul's destined mate:

> The bard has sung, God never formed a soul
> Without its own peculiar mate, to meet
> Its wandering half, when ripe to crown the whole
> Bright plan of bliss, most heavenly, most complete!
> But thousand evil things there are that hate
> To look on happiness: these hurt, impede,
> And, leagued with time, space, circumstance, and fate,
> Keep kindred heart from heart, to pine, and pant, and bleed.
> (067-039, 067-040)

The passage — a rendering of the Aristophanic myth of the divided soul from Plato's *Symposium* — is one of the anthology's most polished examples of Romantic Platonism. Brooks's Miltonic blank verse is evident in the stately, enjambed lines and the Latinate diction ("impede," "congenial," "unquaffed").

## Critical Reception

Brooks's reputation in her own day rested almost entirely on Southey's endorsement, and this proved a double-edged sword. While it secured her a respectful hearing, it also set expectations unreasonably high: subsequent readers, unable to find in *Zophiël* a second *Paradise Lost*, felt let down.

Nevertheless, Brooks holds a distinctive place in American literary history. She was the first American woman to publish a major epic poem in England, and her work anticipates — in its fusion of sensuous imagery and theological ambition — the poetry of later figures like Elizabeth Barrett Browning. Modern feminist critics have recovered *Zophiël* as a significant if flawed work that intervenes in the male-dominated epic tradition to center a female protagonist's spiritual and erotic experience.
