## Biography

Charles Wentworth Upham (1802–1875) was an American historian, Unitarian minister, and politician. Born in St. John, New Brunswick (then part of British North America), he was raised in Massachusetts and graduated from Harvard College in 1821. He subsequently studied at Harvard Divinity School and was ordained as a Unitarian minister.

Upham served as pastor of the First Church in Salem, Massachusetts, where he developed a deep interest in the history of the Salem witch trials of 1692. His knowledge of the town's history and his access to local records made him the foremost authority on the subject. He later served as a U.S. Representative from Massachusetts (1853–1855), as a member of the Whig Party.

## Literary Career

Upham's major literary achievement was his two-volume *History of Witchcraft and Salem Village* (1867), which remains one of the most detailed and influential studies of the Salem witch trials. Drawing on court records, personal correspondence, and town archives, Upham provided a comprehensive narrative of the events, their antecedents, and their aftermath. The work was notable for its psychological insight into the dynamics of mass hysteria and its careful attention to the social and political context of the trials.

He also wrote a *Life of Timothy Pickering* (1867–1873), a biography of the Revolutionary War colonel and Secretary of State, and numerous lectures and historical addresses.

## Selections in the Corpus

Upham appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* through an excerpt from his *History of Witchcraft and Salem Village*, titled "Defeat of the Indian King Philip" (selection 138). The passage recounts the decisive battle of King Philip's War (1675–1678), a turning point in early New England history.

> The Indians were carrying all before them. Philip was spreading conflagration, devastation, and slaughter around the borders, and striking sudden and deadly blows into the heart of the country. It was evident that he was consolidating the Indian power into irresistible strength... From other scouting parties it became evident that this opinion was correct, and that the Indians were collecting stores and assembling their warriors somewhere, to fall upon the colonies at the first opening of spring. (030-004)

> Further information made it certain that their place of gathering was in the Narragansett country, in the south-westerly part of the colony of Rhode Island. There was no alternative but, as a last effort, to strike the enemy at that point with the utmost available force... It was between one and two o'clock in the afternoon, and the short winter day was wearing away, Winslow saw the position at a glance, and, by the promptness of his decision, proved himself a great captain. He ordered an instant assault. (030-004)

> The Massachusetts troops were in the van, the Plymouth, with the commander-in-chief, in the center, the Connecticut in the rear. The Indians had erected a block-house near the entrance, filled with sharpshooters, who also lined the palisades. The men rushed on, although it was into the jaws of death, under an unerring fire. (030-004)

> Enough survived the terrible passage to bring the Indians to a hand-to-hand fight within the fort. After a desperate struggle of nearly three hours, the savages were driven from their stronghold, and with the setting of that sun their power was broken. Philip's fortunes had received a decided overthrow, and the colonies were saved. In all military history there is not a more daring exploit. Never, on any field, has more heroic prowess been displayed. (030-004)

The selection captures Upham's ability to combine meticulous historical narrative with dramatic pacing, transforming archival research into compelling storytelling.

## Style and Themes

Upham's prose combined the thoroughness of the archival historian with the narrative instincts of a storyteller. His *History of Witchcraft and Salem Village* was both a work of scholarship and a moral meditation on the dangers of popular delusion. The excerpt on King Philip's War demonstrates his talent for battlefield narrative — vivid, well-paced, and grounded in specific detail about troop movements and tactical decisions.

## Reception and Legacy

*History of Witchcraft and Salem Village* was recognized as a landmark study upon publication and has remained a foundational text for scholars of the Salem witch trials. Upham's meticulous documentation of primary sources established a standard for historical research in the field. The work also influenced literary treatments of Salem, including Nathaniel Hawthorne's fiction and later Arthur Miller's *The Crucible*.

Upham died in Salem in 1875. His house on Essex Street, where he wrote much of his history, still stands as a reminder of his long association with the town whose past he did so much to preserve.
