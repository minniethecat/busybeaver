# George Washington Greene

## Biography

George Washington Greene (1811–1883) was an American historian, biographer, and educator. Born in East Greenwich, Rhode Island, he was the grandson of Major General Nathanael Greene, one of George Washington's most trusted commanders during the Revolutionary War. Greene graduated from Bowdoin College and spent much of his early career in Europe, serving as United States consul in Rome from 1837 to 1845. His years in Italy brought him into contact with leading literary and political figures, and he developed a lasting interest in Italian history and culture.

Returning to the United States, Greene became a professor of modern languages at Brown University and later taught at Cornell University. He wrote extensively on historical subjects, producing works on the Revolutionary War, the Middle Ages, and Italian literature. His most significant publication was *The Life of Nathanael Greene* (1867–1871), a three-volume biography of his grandfather that drew upon family papers and original documents to provide the first comprehensive account of the general's military career.

## Literary Career

Greene's historical writing is characterized by meticulous research and a narrative style that brings to life the personalities and settings of the Revolutionary era. In *The Life of Nathanael Greene*, he devoted considerable attention not only to military operations but to the social world of the Continental Army — the officers, their families, and the foreign volunteers who flocked to the American cause.

The selection in this anthology portrays the circle of foreign officers who gathered in the parlor of Greene's wife, Catharine Greene, during the winter encampment of 1778–1779. It is a passage of vivid portraiture:

> There was often to be seen Lafayette, not yet turned of twenty-one, though a husband, a father, and a major-general; graver somewhat in his manners than strictly belonged either to his years or his country; and loved and trusted by all, by Washington and Greene especially. (023-012)

Greene sketches each figure — Steuben, De Kalb, Fleury, Pulaski, Kosciusko — with an eye for the telling detail: Steuben's "glittering star on his left breast" and his limited English; De Kalb's "cold, observant eye of the diplomatist"; the Marquis de la Rouerie "combatting an unsuccessful love by throwing himself headlong into the tumult of war" (023-012). The passage serves as both military history and social portraiture, capturing the cosmopolitan character of the Continental Army's officer corps and the bonds of affection and ambition that held it together.

## Critical Reception

Greene's *Life of Nathanael Greene* was well received upon publication and remained the standard biography of the general for decades. Contemporary reviewers praised Greene's access to family papers and his balanced treatment of controversial episodes in his grandfather's career. Later historians have noted that Greene's familial connection gave him unique access to sources but also introduced a natural bias in his grandfather's favor. His broader contribution lies in his attention to the human dimension of the Revolutionary War — the friendships, rivalries, and personal histories of the men who fought it — a dimension often neglected by the military and political histories that dominated nineteenth-century American historiography.
