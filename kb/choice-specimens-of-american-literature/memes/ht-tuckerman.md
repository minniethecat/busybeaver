# H.T. Tuckerman

## Biography

Henry Theodore Tuckerman (1813–1871) was an American essayist, art critic, poet, and travel writer. Born in Boston, Massachusetts, he was the son of a prosperous merchant. Tuckerman was educated at the Boston Latin School but, owing to delicate health, did not attend college. Instead, he traveled extensively in Europe, particularly in Italy, where he developed the passion for art and architecture that would become the foundation of his literary career.

Returning to the United States, Tuckerman settled in New York City and devoted himself to literature. He never married, living quietly among books and works of art. His circle included many of the leading literary figures of his day, including Washington Irving, William Cullen Bryant, and the younger Henry James, who later acknowledged Tuckerman's influence on his own early art criticism.

Tuckerman was a prolific contributor to periodicals, publishing essays and reviews in the *North American Review*, *Godey's Lady's Book*, and *The Knickerbocker*. He was one of the first Americans to treat art criticism as a serious literary pursuit rather than a mere appendage to journalism; his *Book of the Artists* (1867), a biographical dictionary of American painters and sculptors, remains a valuable source for historians of American art.

## Literary Career

Tuckerman's literary output was extensive and varied. *Isabel; or, Sicily: A Pilgrimage* (1839) is a travel narrative in the manner of Irving; *The Italian Sketch Book* (1835) and *Thoughts on the Poets* (1846) combine descriptive travel writing with literary criticism. His collection of essays *The Optimist* (1850) contains the essay "New England Philosophy," from which the *Choice Specimens* selection is drawn.

"The Heart Superior to the Intellect" is Tuckerman's most characteristic essay — a defense of sentiment, sensibility, and the affections against the narrow rationalism that he saw dominating New England culture:

> Constant supplies of knowledge to the intellect, and the exclusive cultivation of reason, may, indeed, make a pedant and a logician; but the probability is, these benefits — if such they are — will be gained at the expense of the soul. (047-002)

Tuckerman argues that "the tastes, affections, and sentiments are more absolutely the man than his talents or acquirements," and observes pointedly that "in the New Testament, allusions to the intellect are so rare, while the 'heart' and the 'spirit we are of' are ever appealed to." The essay concludes with a tribute to Shakespeare, whose "marvelous insight into the human soul" Tuckerman attributes not to book-learning but to "his profound sympathy with man":

> He might have conned whole libraries on the philosophy of the passions, he might have coldly observed facts for years, and never have conceived of jealousy like Othello's, — the remorse of Macbeth, or love like that of Juliet. (047-002)

The second Tuckerman selection in the anthology, "The Statue of Washington," is a poem inspired by the marble statue of George Washington by the American sculptor Horatio Greenough.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Heart Superior to the Intellect* (047-002) — From "New England Philosophy," an essay in *The Optimist* (1850). A sustained critique of intellectualism in New England culture, arguing that sentiment, sympathy, and the "heart" are truer guides to wisdom and character than mere intellect. Tuckerman draws on the New Testament and Shakespeare to support his defense of "sensibility to the beautiful."
- *The Statue of Washington* — From *Poems* (1851). A sonnet inspired by Horatio Greenough's celebrated marble statue of Washington.

## Critical Reception

Tuckerman was highly regarded by his contemporaries as a critic and essayist. Edgar Allan Poe, rarely generous to fellow American writers, praised the grace and learning of Tuckerman's prose. Henry James acknowledged him in his early essays on art, and Tuckerman's *Book of the Artists* was the standard reference work on American artists for decades after its publication.

Tuckerman's reputation, however, has followed the general fate of the nineteenth-century American essayist: once central to literary culture, now largely forgotten. His travel writing, like that of many of his contemporaries, has been superseded, and his art criticism was eclipsed by the rise of professional art history in the twentieth century. Nevertheless, his essays remain readable examples of the cultivated, urbane tradition of American letters that preceded the post-Civil War turn toward realism and naturalism.
