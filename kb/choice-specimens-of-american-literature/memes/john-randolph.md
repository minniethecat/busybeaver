# John Randolph

## Biography

John Randolph (1773–1833), known as John Randolph of Roanoke, was an American planter and politician from Virginia. Born at Cawsons, Virginia, into one of the colony's most distinguished families, he was a descendant of Pocahontas through his mother's line. Randolph briefly attended the College of New Jersey (Princeton) and studied law in Philadelphia, but his formal education was cut short by ill health and financial circumstances.

Elected to the United States House of Representatives in 1799 at the age of twenty-six, Randolph quickly established himself as one of the most brilliant and unpredictable orators in Congress. He was initially a fervent Jeffersonian and served as chairman of the Ways and Means Committee, but he later broke with Jefferson and Madison over what he perceived as betrayals of strict constructionist principles. Randolph opposed the War of 1812, the Missouri Compromise, and the expansion of federal power in all its forms. His eccentricities — his high-pitched voice, his theatrical gestures, his devotion to his hunting dogs — made him one of the most vivid figures in early American politics. He served in the Senate (1825–1827) and as Minister to Russia (1830) before returning to the House, where he died in office.

## Literary Career

Randolph's literary reputation rests on his oratory. His speeches were transcribed and widely circulated in newspapers, making him one of the best-known political speakers of his generation. His prose style was sharp, aphoristic, and laced with classical and historical references. Randolph's most quoted line — "change is not reform" — encapsulates his political philosophy: suspicion of innovation for its own sake and faith in established institutions. His speeches against the expansion of federal power influenced a generation of Southern states' rights advocates, though Randolph himself was too idiosyncratic to lead any faction.

## Selections in This Anthology

In "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Randolph is represented by his speech "Change is not Reform," a powerful attack on the idea that amending the Virginia Constitution represented progress:

> "Sir, I see no wisdom in making this provision for future changes. You must give Governments time to operate on the People, and give the People time to become gradually assimilated to their institutions. Almost any thing is better than this state of perpetual uncertainty." (016-013)

> "Sir, how often must I repeat, that *change* is not *reform*? I am willing that this new Constitution shall stand as long as it is possible for it to stand; and that, believe me, is a very short time." (016-013)

> "The four hundred men who went out with David were *in debt*. The fellow-laborers of Catiline were *in debt*. The partizans of Caesar were *in debt*. And I defy you to show me a desperately indebted People, anywhere, who can bear a regular, sober Government." (016-013)

Randolph's speech channels the classical republican fear of debt as a solvent of civic virtue — a conviction that financial dependence corrupts political independence. His critique of constitutional tinkering remains one of the most memorable expressions of conservative skepticism in the American political tradition.

## Critical Reception

Randolph was revered and reviled in equal measure. Contemporaries praised his wit and feared his tongue; John Quincy Adams called him "the most brilliant and the most eccentric" man in Congress. Later historians have seen in Randolph both a principled defender of limited government and a tragic figure whose rigid ideology left him isolated. His speeches are studied in histories of American conservatism and in accounts of the Old South's political culture. Randolph's literary significance lies in his mastery of the extemporaneous political speech as a form of public art.
