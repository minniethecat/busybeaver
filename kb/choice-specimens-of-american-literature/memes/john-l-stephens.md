# John L. Stephens

## Biography

John Lloyd Stephens (1805–1852) was an American explorer, travel writer, and diplomat. Born in Shrewsbury, New Jersey, he studied law at Columbia College and practiced briefly in New York City.

A chronic throat ailment led his physician to recommend travel abroad, launching Stephens on a career that would make him one of the most celebrated travel writers of his generation. In 1839, President Martin Van Buren appointed him Special Ambassador to the Federal Republic of Central America. There, accompanied by the English artist Frederick Catherwood, Stephens explored the ruins of the ancient Maya civilization, bringing them to international attention for the first time.

## Literary Career

Stephens's major works include *Incidents of Travel in Egypt, Arabia Petraea, and the Holy Land* (1837) and the landmark *Incidents of Travel in Central America, Chiapas, and Yucatan* (1841), followed by *Incidents of Travel in Yucatan* (1843).

His writing combined vivid description, historical scholarship, and narrative drive. Stephens argued convincingly that the ruined cities of Central America were built by the ancestors of the indigenous Maya, not by Egyptians or Israelites as previously proposed. His books, illustrated with Catherwood's meticulous engravings, became international bestsellers.

Stephens appears in "Choice Specimens" with "Discovery of a Ruined City in the Woods" (269), an excerpt from *Travels in Central America*. The anthology editor introduces him as a writer of travel and adventure (055-005). The selection captures the moment of encountering the ruins at Copán:

> The sight of this unexpected monument put at rest, at once and forever, in our minds, all uncertainty in regard to the character of American antiquities, and gave as the assurance that the objects we were in search of were interesting, not only as the remains of an unknown people, but as works of art, proving, like newly-discovered historical records, that the people who once occupied the continent of America were not savages.

## Critical Reception

Stephens's travel books were praised by contemporaries including Edgar Allan Poe, who called *Incidents of Travel in Central America* "perhaps the most interesting book of travel ever published." Modern archaeologists acknowledge Stephens and Catherwood as foundational figures in Maya studies, whose documentation of sites before later deterioration gives their work enduring scholarly importance.
