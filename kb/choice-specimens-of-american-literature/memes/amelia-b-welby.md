## Biography

Amelia B. Welby (1819–1852) was an American poet, born Amelia Ball Coppuck in Maryland.
After her marriage to George Welby, she lived in Louisville, Kentucky, where she became
a prominent figure in the city's literary circle. She published her poetry under the
simple pseudonym "Amelia," by which she was widely known to her readers.

## Literary Career

Welby's poetry appeared in the *Louisville Journal*, edited by [[george-d-prentice]],
who became her enthusiastic promoter. Her collected volume, *Poems by Amelia* (1845),
went through multiple editions and was reviewed favorably by [[edgar-allan-poe]],
who described her verse as possessing "imaginative power of no common order."

Her poems are characteristic of the sentimental lyric tradition of mid-nineteenth-century
America, treating themes of love, death, nature, and religious devotion. Her most famous poem,
"The Bereaved," exemplifies the elegiac mood that defined her poetic voice (002-015).

> "Her verse, though limited in range, possesses a sweetness and unaffected pathos
> that places her among the sincerest of our women poets" (075-003),
> noted the editor in the "Later Poets" section.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes two poems by Welby:

- **"The Bereaved"** (002-015): An elegiac poem on the loss of a child, noted for its
  tender pathos and direct emotional appeal.

- Additional selections in the "Later Poets" section (075-003), representing her
  contribution to the mid-century lyric tradition.

## Critical Reception

Welby's reputation, like that of many nineteenth-century American women poets, declined
sharply after her death as the sentimental tradition fell out of critical favor.
Twentieth-century reevaluations, however, have recognized her as a skilled practitioner
of the domestic lyric mode, whose work offers valuable insight into the emotional
culture of antebellum America.
