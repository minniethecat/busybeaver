# H.N. Hudson

## Biography

Henry Norman Hudson (1814–1886) was an American Shakespeare scholar, editor, and Episcopal clergyman. Born in Vermont, he graduated from Middlebury College and was ordained in the Episcopal Church. Hudson devoted much of his intellectual life to the study and teaching of Shakespeare, producing annotated editions that were widely used in American schools and homes during the nineteenth century. He served as a chaplain during the Civil War and later lectured on Shakespeare at Boston University.

## Literary Career

Hudson's principal contribution to American letters was his work as a Shakespeare editor and critic. He published *Lectures on Shakespeare* (1848) and later the *Harvard Edition of Shakespeare* (1880–1881), a comprehensive annotated edition in twenty volumes. His critical approach emphasized the moral and spiritual dimensions of Shakespeare's art, arguing that the plays offered profound instruction in human nature alongside their aesthetic power.

In his "Preface to the Works of Shakespeare," Hudson articulated his view that Shakespeare's genius lay in his ability to instruct while he moved the passions:

> It is true, he often lays on us burdens of passion that would not be borne in any other writer. But whether he wrings the heart with pity, or freezes the blood with terror, or fires the soul with indignation, the genial reader still rises from his pages refreshed. The reason of which is, instruction keeps pace with excitement: he strengthens the mind in proportion as he loads it. (047-004)

Hudson noted that even Shakespeare's darkest characters retain some trace of humanity: "He is not wont to exhibit either utterly worthless or utterly faultless monsters; persons too good, or too bad, to exist; even his worst characters ... have some slight fragrance of humanity about them" (047-004).

## Selections in This Anthology

Hudson appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) through a single passage drawn from his "Preface to the Works of Shakespeare" (047-004). The selection, titled "Shakespeare's Works Instructive," presents Hudson's argument that Shakespeare's greatness consists in his ability to balance passion with moral instruction — "with such consummate art does he manage the fiercest tempests of our being, that in a healthy mind the witnessing of them is always attended with an overbalance of pleasure" (047-004).

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Hudson was recognized as one of the leading American Shakespeare scholars. His editions brought Shakespeare to a broad American readership and helped establish the study of English literature as a serious academic discipline in the United States. His *Harvard Edition* remained in use for decades. Modern scholarship has largely superseded his textual work, but his role in popularizing Shakespeare in nineteenth-century America remains historically significant.
