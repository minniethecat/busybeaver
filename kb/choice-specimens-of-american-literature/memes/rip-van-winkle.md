# Rip Van Winkle

Rip Van Winkle is the title character of [[washington-irving]]'s most famous story, published in [[the-sketch-book|The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent.]] (1819–20).

A good-natured but shiftless Dutch-American villager, Rip escapes his scolding wife by wandering into the Catskill Mountains. There he encounters the ghosts of [[henry-hudson|Henry Hudson]]'s crew, drinks their liquor, and falls into a twenty-year sleep. He awakens to find the American Revolution has happened without him.

Rip Van Winkle is one of the first enduring characters created by an American writer. He has entered the folklore of the nation as the archetype of the man who sleeps through history.

## Role in the Story

Rip is introduced as a "simple, good-natured fellow" beloved by the village children and dogs but useless at managing his own farm. His wife, Dame Van Winkle, nags him constantly — a domestic tyranny from which the Catskills offer escape.

Irving's story operates on multiple levels simultaneously:

| Level | What Rip Represents |
|-------|-------------------|
| Comic | The henpecked husband whose escape is every lazy man's fantasy |
| Historical | The colonial subject who sleeps through the Revolution and wakes to a new nation |
| Mythic | The sleeper who returns — a motif found in folklore worldwide |
| National | America before and after independence — continuity and change |

When Rip returns to his village, nothing is familiar. The portrait of King George at the inn has been replaced by General Washington. The tranquil colonial village has become a bustling, contentious republic. Rip's confusion mirrors the disorientation of a generation that lived through revolutionary transformation.

## Literary Significance

"Rip Van Winkle" was among the first works of American fiction to achieve international recognition.

[[washington-irving]] created the story from a German folktale but transformed it into something distinctively American. The Hudson River Valley setting, the Dutch colonial inheritance, the presence of the American Revolution as historical backdrop — these elements rooted the tale in a specific American experience.

The story established the short story as a serious literary form in the United States. [[edgar-allan-poe]] later refined the form Irving pioneered. [[nathaniel-hawthorne]] learned from Irving's fusion of the actual and the fantastic.

Rip himself became an American archetype: the man who cannot keep up with change, the dreamer who misses the great events of his time, the innocent abroad in a world he no longer recognizes.

## Rip Van Winkle as American Myth

Few characters in American literature have so thoroughly entered the national imagination.

The phrase "a Rip Van Winkle" has become English-language shorthand for someone oblivious to change or asleep to their own time. Rip's twenty-year sleep has been adapted, alluded to, and reimagined in countless works of literature, film, and popular culture.

The story's resonance lies in its ambivalence. Is Rip's escape from domestic tyranny and revolutionary politics a liberation or a loss? Is he a comic figure or a tragic one? Irving leaves the question open — and this ambiguity has kept the character alive for two centuries.

## Selections in This Anthology

Washington Irving and the Rip Van Winkle story are represented in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

> [019-002] The following extract exhibits Irving's characteristic style — a style marked by grace, urbanity, and a quiet humor. It is from "The Sketch Book," a work that charmed readers on both sides of the Atlantic...

> [037-009] The author of the Sketch Book has invested the scenery of the Hudson with the charm of romantic association. His tales of Sleepy Hollow and Rip Van Winkle have become part of the common stock of American literature.

Irving's achievement, as the editor recognized, was to create characters and stories that felt both literary and folkloric — refined in style yet mythic in resonance.
