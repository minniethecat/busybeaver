## Biography

Ormsby M. Mitchell (1810–1862) was an American astronomer, engineer, and military officer. After
graduating from West Point, he turned to law and then to astronomy, founding the Cincinnati
Observatory in 1842 — one of the first major astronomical observatories in the United States. During
the Civil War, he served as a Union general, commanding troops in the Western Theater.

## Literary Career

Mitchell was one of the most effective popularizers of science in antebellum America. His lectures on
astronomy drew large audiences, and his writings translated complex scientific concepts into accessible
and inspiring prose. In *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, he is represented by a selection
from "The Great Unseen" (001-108), a scientific exposition on oceanography and astronomy:

> As a rule, the hottest water of the Gulf Stream is at, or near, the surface; and as the deep-sea
> thermometer is sent down, it shows that these waters, though still far warmer than the waters on
> either side at corresponding depths, gradually become less and less warm until the bottom of the
> current is reached. (052-012)

## Selections in the Corpus

Mitchell's selection in the corpus (001-108) exemplifies the inclusion of scientific prose alongside
literary works in *Choice Specimens*, reflecting the nineteenth-century view that American letters
encompassed both imaginative and scientific writing.

## Critical Reception

Mitchell's popular lectures made him nationally known. His ability to convey scientific wonder to
general audiences placed him in the tradition of American science communicators. His observatory in
Cincinnati became a model for public astronomical education.
