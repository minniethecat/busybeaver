# William Ellery Channing

## Biography

William Ellery Channing (1780–1842) was an American Unitarian minister, theologian, and essayist, widely regarded as the foremost spokesman of the liberal religious movement in the early republic. Born in Newport, Rhode Island, Channing graduated from Harvard College in 1798 and, after a period of private tutoring in Richmond, Virginia, returned to Harvard for theological study. He was ordained in 1803 as minister of the Federal Street Church (later the Arlington Street Church) in Boston, a pulpit he would occupy for the remainder of his life.

Channing's 1819 Baltimore sermon on "Unitarian Christianity" established him as the defining voice of American Unitarianism, articulating a theology that emphasized the moral perfectibility of human nature, the loving character of God, and the exercise of reason in religious inquiry. His influence extended well beyond the church: Channing's essays on social reform — addressing slavery, poverty, war, and education — made him one of the most widely read moralists of his era. Ralph Waldo Emerson, who admired Channing deeply, acknowledged him as a formative influence on the Transcendentalist movement, even as the younger generation moved beyond Channing's more restrained rationalism.

## Literary Career

Channing's literary output was substantial, encompassing sermons, theological treatises, and essays on literature, politics, and society. His prose style was characterized by moral earnestness, psychological penetration, and a remarkable power of concentrated characterization. The selection included in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" — his essay on Napoleon Bonaparte — demonstrates Channing's gift for the moral portrait.

The essay opens with a sweeping moral judgment that compresses Napoleon's entire career into a single devastating sentence:

> With powers which might have made him a glorious representative and minister of the beneficent Divinity, and with natural sensibilities which might have been exalted into sublime virtues, he chose to separate himself from his kind, to forego their love, esteem, and gratitude, that he might become their gaze, their fear, their wonder; and for this selfish, solitary good, parted with peace and imperishable renown. (006-012)

Channing's analysis traces the psychology of self-exaltation with relentless precision. He notes how Napoleon's "first success in Italy gave him the tone of a master, and he never laid it aside to his last hour" (006-012). The emperor spoke in "native language" rather than studied arrogance: "In his proudest claims, he speaks from his own mind... His style is swollen, but never strained, as if he were conscious of playing a part above his real claims" (006-012).

The essay traces the self-destructive logic of Napoleon's vanity:

> This spirit of self-exaggeration wrought its own misery... it diseased his fine intellect, gave imagination the ascendency over judgment, turned the inventiveness and fruitfulness of his mind into rash, impatient, restless energies, and thus precipitated him into projects, which, as the wisdom of his counsellors pronounced, were fraught with ruin. (006-012)

Channing's conclusion rises from the particular case of Napoleon to a universal meditation on the spectacle of despotism:

> To us there is something radically and increasingly shocking in the thought of one man's will becoming a law to his race; in the thought of multitudes, of vast communities, surrendering conscience, intellect, their affections, their rights, their interests, to the stern mandate of a fellow-creature. When we see one word of a frail man on the throne of France, tearing a hundred thousand sons from their homes, breaking asunder the sacred ties of domestic life, sentencing myriads of the young to make murder their calling, and rapacity their means of support, and extorting from nations their treasures to extend this ruinous sway, we are ready to ask ourselves, Is not this a dream? and, when the sad reality comes home to us, we blush for a race which can stoop to such an abject lot. (006-012)

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selection from William Ellery Channing's works appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Character of Napoleon* (006-012) — From the "Essay on Napoleon Bonaparte." A sustained moral critique of Napoleon's self-exaltation, tracing the psychological logic by which vanity corrupts intellect and leads to tyranny. Channing argues that Napoleon chose solitary grandeur over human connection, and that this choice carried its own punishment: the perversion of his extraordinary gifts.

## Critical Reception

Channing was among the most respected American writers of his era. His essays and sermons were widely reprinted on both sides of the Atlantic, and his influence on the New England literary renaissance was considerable. Emerson, in his 1880 lecture on Channing, praised him as "our first and best civilized man" — a figure whose moral seriousness and intellectual integrity set a standard for American letters.

Later critics have noted that Channing's rational Unitarianism occupies a middle ground between the Calvinist orthodoxy he helped dismantle and the Romantic individualism of the Transcendentalists who followed him. His essay on Napoleon, while perhaps overly reliant on moral categories, remains admired as a model of psychological portraiture and impassioned republican rhetoric.
