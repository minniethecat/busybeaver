# Abel Stevens

## Biography

Abel Stevens (1815–1897) was an American Methodist clergyman, editor, and historian. Born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, Stevens entered the ministry of the Methodist Episcopal Church at an early age and quickly distinguished himself as a preacher and writer. He served pastorates in New England and New York before devoting himself more fully to editorial and historical work.

Stevens's career bridged the worlds of the pulpit and the press. He edited several important Methodist periodicals, including *Zion's Herald* and the *Christian Advocate and Journal*, and his editorial leadership helped shape the public voice of American Methodism during its period of most rapid growth in the mid-nineteenth century. At a time when Methodism was transforming from a frontier revival movement into the nation's largest Protestant denomination, Stevens was among its most articulate chroniclers and defenders.

His masterpiece, the *History of the Methodist Episcopal Church in the United States of America* (four volumes, 1864–1867), was the first comprehensive account of the denomination's rise. It drew upon extensive original sources, including letters, journals, and conference minutes, and set a standard for American denominational historiography.

## Literary Career

Stevens's *History of Methodism* remains his defining work. Organized as a narrative of persons and events rather than an institutional chronicle, it gave vivid portraits of the early Methodist circuit riders — men like Francis Asbury and Freeborn Garrettson — whose prodigious travels and open-air preaching built the movement. Stevens described this generation with a warmth born of admiration:

> They were precisely the men whom the moral wants of the new world at the time demanded. (031-006)

His other works include *Memorials of the Introduction of Methodism into the Eastern States* (1848), *The Women of Methodism* (1866), and a biography of the Methodist missionary bishop Matthew Simpson. Stevens also traveled extensively in Europe, producing *A Compendious History of American Methodism* and contributing observations on European religious life to American periodicals.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn from Stevens's account of the early Methodist clergy in America. It captures the distinctive character of the circuit riders — their physical hardiness, their natural eloquence, and their complete devotion to the cause:

> They composed a class which, perhaps, will never be seen again. They were distinguished by native mental vigor, shrewdness, extraordinary knowledge of human nature, many of them by overwhelming natural eloquence, the effects of which on popular assemblies are scarcely paralleled in the history of ancient or modern oratory. (031-006)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Early Methodist Clergy in America* (031-005, 031-006) — From *The History of Methodism*. A portrait of the early Methodist circuit riders who carried the movement across the American frontier. Stevens describes the young Native American converts who embraced Methodism, as well as the itinerant preachers themselves — men of "rare physical abilities" whose feats of endurance, incessant preaching, and "overwhelming natural eloquence" made them uniquely suited to the moral needs of a new nation.

## Critical Reception

Stevens was recognized in his lifetime as the preeminent historian of American Methodism. His four-volume history was immediately adopted as the standard reference, and his shorter works circulated widely among Methodist readers in both America and Britain. Contemporaries praised his narrative gifts — the ability to make institutional history read like biography — and his judicious use of primary sources.

Stevens's reputation has persisted mainly within Methodist and religious-history circles. His *History* was superseded by later multi-volume projects in the early twentieth century, but modern scholars of American religion continue to cite him both as a source and as a historiographical landmark — the first to treat American Methodism not as an offshoot of the British movement but as a distinct national institution with its own character and significance.
