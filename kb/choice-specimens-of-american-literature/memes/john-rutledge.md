# John Rutledge

## Biography

John Rutledge (1739–1800) was an American statesman and jurist who played a prominent role in the founding of the United States. Born in Charleston, South Carolina, to a wealthy planter family, Rutledge studied law at the Middle Temple in London and returned to Charleston to build a successful legal practice. He served in the Stamp Act Congress (1765), the First and Second Continental Congresses (1774–1776), and as president (governor) of South Carolina during the Revolutionary War.

Rutledge was a delegate to the Constitutional Convention of 1787, where he served on the Committee of Detail and was instrumental in drafting key provisions of the Constitution. In 1789, President Washington appointed him one of the first Associate Justices of the Supreme Court, and in 1795 he served briefly as the second Chief Justice of the United States (by recess appointment, later rejected by the Senate). He died in Charleston in 1800.

## Literary Career

Rutledge's literary reputation rests on his oratory and legal writing rather than on published literary works. He was known as one of the most eloquent speakers of the Revolutionary generation — John Adams called him "one of the first orators in America." His surviving speeches, including those delivered at the Constitutional Convention and in the South Carolina legislature, display the formal, classical rhetoric characteristic of eighteenth-century American political discourse. His arguments on the judiciary establishment and the structure of the federal courts, represented in this anthology, reflect the legal sophistication of the founding era.

## Critical Reception

As a founding-era figure whose influence operated through spoken and legal channels rather than through published literary works, Rutledge has not received the level of literary study accorded to contemporaries like Jefferson or Franklin. His contributions are assessed primarily by legal and constitutional historians, who recognize his importance in shaping the federal judiciary and his role in the Constitutional Convention. His oratory, though widely praised by his contemporaries, survives only in fragmentary form.
