Hugh Swinton Legaré (1797–1843) was an American lawyer, jurist, and diplomat who served as United States Attorney General under President [[John Tyler]] and briefly as acting Secretary of State (040-008). A native of Charleston, South Carolina, Legaré combined a distinguished legal career with deep classical scholarship, contributing essays on law, literature, and public affairs that earned him a reputation as one of the most learned men of the antebellum South.

## Biography

Born in Charleston, South Carolina, in 1797, Legaré was a precocious scholar who entered the College of South Carolina at age fourteen. After graduating, he traveled to Europe and studied law, philosophy, and languages in Edinburgh. Returning to South Carolina, he practiced law and served in the state legislature before being appointed chargé d'affaires to Belgium in 1832. In 1841, President John Tyler appointed him Attorney General of the United States, a position he held until his sudden death in Boston in 1843 at the age of forty-six.

## Literary Career

Legaré's literary reputation rests on his essays, collected posthumously in *Writings of Hugh Swinton Legaré* (1845–1846), edited by his sister Mary S. Legaré. His intellectual range was unusually broad: he wrote with equal fluency on Roman law, Greek literature, political economy, and contemporary European affairs. His essay "The Study of the Classics" (040-010) remains one of the most eloquent American defenses of classical education in the nineteenth century, arguing that ancient languages provide "the golden keys which open all the recesses where the stored treasures of knowledge and taste have been laid up."

## Selections

The anthology includes two pieces by Legaré: a penetrating analysis of Irish immigration to America ("The Celtic Exodus") and his celebrated defense of classical learning ("The Study of the Classics").

### The Celtic Exodus

> [040-009] This "Celtic Exodus," as it has been aptly called, is to all the parties immediately connected with it one of the most important events of the day. To the emigrants themselves it may be regarded as a passing from death to life. It will benefit Ireland by reducing a surplus population, and restoring a sounder and juster relation of capital and labor. It will benefit the laboring classes in England, where wages have been kept down to the starvation-point by the struggle between native population and the inhabitants of the sister island, for that employment and food, of which there is not enough for both.

> [040-009] In addition to all this, the constant influx into America of stout and efficient hands supplies the greatest want in a new country, which is that of labor, gives value to land, and facilitates the execution of every species of private enterprise and public work. I am not insensible to the temporary inconveniences which are to be set off against these advantages, on both sides of the water. Much suffering attends the emigrant, there, on his passage, and after his arrival.

### The Study of the Classics

> [040-011] All that we ask, then, is, that a boy should be thoroughly taught the ancient languages from his eighth to his sixteenth year, or thereabouts, in which time he will have his curiosity fired for further acquisition, and find himself in possession of the golden keys which open all the recesses where the stored treasures of knowledge and taste have been laid up.

> [040-011] He will be let into that great communion of scholars throughout all ages and all nations — like that more awful communion of saints in the holy church universal — and feel a sympathy with the departed great, and with the wise and the gifted of all time, such as can never be awakened by any other discipline.

## Critical Reception

Legaré's premature death cut short a career of unusual intellectual promise. Contemporaries including [[Edward Everett]] and [[William H. Prescott]] praised his classical learning and his forceful prose style. Later scholars of Southern intellectual history have noted that Legaré represented a cosmopolitan, European-oriented tradition of Southern letters that has often been overshadowed by the region's political history. His essays on immigration offer a nuanced perspective on antebellum nativism, balancing economic benefits against social concerns.
