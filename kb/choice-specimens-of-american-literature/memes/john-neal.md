# John Neal

## Biography

John Neal (1793–1876) was born in Portland, Maine (then part of Massachusetts), to a Quaker family of modest means. After his father's death, Neal was largely self-educated, devouring books from the Portland Library and teaching himself languages, law, and literature with an intensity that became his hallmark. He worked variously as a dry-goods clerk, a schoolteacher, and a miniature-painter before turning to the law; he was admitted to the Maryland bar in 1819.

Neal's literary career began with a series of novels written in rapid succession: *Keep Cool* (1817), *Logan* (1822), *Seventy-Six* (1823), *Randolph* (1823), and *Errata* (1823). Dissatisfied with the American literary scene, Neal sailed for England in 1824, where he spent three productive years writing for *Blackwood's Magazine* and other British periodicals under the pseudonym "Carter Holmes." His vigorous, often combative essays on American literature were instrumental in introducing British readers — and through them, American readers — to the idea that the United States possessed a literary tradition worthy of serious attention.

Neal returned to Portland in 1827 and established *The Yankee*, one of the first periodicals in America devoted to literature and the arts. He continued to write novels, poems, essays, and reviews for the next five decades, his output as prodigious as his personality was contentious. Neal was an early advocate for women's rights, prison reform, and the abolition of slavery — causes that made him as many enemies as friends in nineteenth-century Portland. He died in 1876 at the age of eighty-three, having outlived most of his contemporaries and his reputation.

## Literary Career

Neal's literary style is energetic, opinionated, and often undisciplined — the product of a mind that moved faster than its own composition. His novels are marked by rapid shifts in tone, intrusive authorial commentary, and a willingness to experiment with narrative form that anticipated modernist techniques. Edgar Allan Poe, who admired Neal's critical writing, called him "the first who gave the first jog to the critical sensibilities of his country."

The selection listed in "Choice Specimens" is "The Nature of True Poetry" (001-143), an essay that typifies Neal's critical approach: assertive, broadly learned, and committed to the Romantic conviction that poetry is a form of inspired vision rather than a technical exercise. Neal argued that American poetry must break with European models and find its materials in the native landscape and experience — a position he shared with Emerson, though Neal came to it independently and a decade earlier.

Neal's fiction, particularly *Logan* and *Seventy-Six*, was among the first to treat American historical subjects with the seriousness and dramatic amplitude that Sir Walter Scott had brought to British history. His novels suffer from haste and structural looseness — Neal often wrote at breakneck speed, rarely revising — but they contain passages of remarkable psychological penetration and descriptive power. His influence on younger American writers, including John Greenleaf Whittier and Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, was acknowledged by both, though Neal's quarrelsomeness and self-promotion alienated many potential allies.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "The Nature of True Poetry" (001-143) — Neal's essay on the character and sources of poetic inspiration, arguing for a distinctively American poetic voice grounded in native experience rather than European tradition.

## Critical Reception

Neal's place in American literary history has always been uncertain. During the 1820s, he was among the most discussed American authors on both sides of the Atlantic; his essays in *Blackwood's* gave British readers their first serious exposure to the idea of an American national literature, and his novels were reviewed seriously by the major periodicals. Yet his reputation faded rapidly after 1830, displaced by the emergence of Irving, Cooper, and later Hawthorne and Melville as the canonical figures of the period.

Twentieth-century scholarship treated Neal largely as a curiosity — a transitional figure whose principal achievement was to clear the ground for more accomplished writers. More recently, critics have begun to re-evaluate Neal's contribution, seeing in his critical writings an early and sophisticated theory of American literary nationalism, and in his novels an experimental energy that anticipates later developments in American fiction. Benjamin Lease's biography *That Wild Fellow John Neal* (1972) and Donald A. Sears's critical study have helped restore Neal to visibility, though he remains among the least read of major antebellum American authors.
