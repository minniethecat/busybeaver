# William Pinkney

## Biography

William Pinkney (1764–1820) was an American lawyer, orator, and diplomat who served as Attorney General of the United States (1811–1814) and as minister to Russia (1816–1818). Born in Annapolis, Maryland, Pinkney studied law under Judge Samuel Chase and was admitted to the bar in 1786. He quickly established himself as one of the foremost advocates of his generation, arguing landmark Supreme Court cases and representing Maryland in the United States Senate.

Pinkney's diplomatic career included service as a commissioner to Great Britain (1796–1804) and as minister plenipotentiary to the Court of St. James (1807–1811). He was also a major in the Maryland militia during the War of 1812, seeing action at the Battle of Bladensburg. His last major diplomatic post was as minister to Russia, where he served until shortly before his death. Throughout his career, Pinkney was renowned as much for his personal elegance and refined manners as for the intellectual force of his arguments.

## Literary Career

Pinkney's oratory was celebrated for its classical balance and rhetorical range — he could move from precise legal reasoning to passages of impassioned eloquence without strain. As an advocate, his arguments before the Supreme Court in cases such as *The Nereide* (1815) set important precedents in admiralty and prize law. His speeches were studied by contemporaries as models of forensic style.

His 1798 speech before the Maryland Legislature on slavery reveals a striking moral dimension. Speaking at a time when the institution was firmly entrenched, Pinkney declared that Americans bore equal guilt with the slave traders of the past:

> But wherefore should we confine the edge of censure to our ancestors, or those from whom they purchased? Are not we equally guilty? *They* strewed around the seeds of slavery; *we* cherish and sustain the growth. (016-002)

His argument in the *Nereide* case, defending American belligerent rights on the high seas, drew upon both natural law and national pride, celebrating the "skill and valor of our infant navy" (016-007) in language that resonated with the post-War of 1812 mood of national confidence.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from William Pinkney's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Responsibility for Slavery* (016-001–016-004) — From a "Speech in the Maryland Legislature," 1798, condemning the perpetuation of slavery in a republic founded on liberty
- *War, and American Belligerent Rights* (016-005–016-007) — From the "Speech in the Nereide Case," championing the rights of American naval forces and the nation's maritime destiny

## Critical Reception

Pinkney was widely regarded as the greatest American advocate of his era. Chief Justice John Marshall reportedly said that Pinkney's arguments before the Supreme Court were among the finest he had ever heard. His blend of classical learning, rhetorical polish, and legal acumen set a standard for American appellate advocacy that would influence generations of lawyers. Modern assessments have noted the tension in Pinkney's legacy between his eloquent condemnation of slavery and his own position as a slaveholder, reflecting the moral contradictions of the early Republic.
