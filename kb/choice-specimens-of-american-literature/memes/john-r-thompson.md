# John R. Thompson

## Biography

John Reuben Thompson (1823–1873) was an American poet, editor, and journalist. Born in Richmond, Virginia, he graduated from the University of Virginia in 1842 and studied law, though he never practiced. Instead, Thompson turned to literature: in 1847, at the age of twenty-four, he purchased the *Southern Literary Messenger*, the influential Richmond-based periodical that had launched the career of Edgar Allan Poe a decade earlier.

As editor of the *Messenger* from 1847 to 1860, Thompson made the magazine the leading literary voice of the antebellum South. Under his stewardship it published work by many of the region's most prominent writers, including Philip Pendleton Cooke, John Esten Cooke, and the young Paul Hamilton Hayne. Thompson cultivated correspondences with prominent Northern and British literary figures, including Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Washington Irving, and William Makepeace Thackeray. His editorial policy sought to raise the standard of Southern letters beyond provincialism, advocating a broadly American literary culture while defending the intellectual dignity of the South.

During the Civil War, Thompson served — without enthusiasm — in the Confederate army, an experience that informed his most famous poem. After the war, he moved to New York City, where he became the literary editor of the *Evening Post* under William Cullen Bryant, a position he held until his death in 1873. Thompson's postwar career exemplified a reconciliationist impulse rare among former Confederates; he worked alongside Bryant, a prominent Unionist, and contributed to Northern periodicals without apology.

## Literary Career

Thompson's literary reputation rests almost entirely on a single poem: "Music in Camp" (1863). Set on the banks of the Rappahannock River in Virginia after a battle — likely the Battle of Fredericksburg (December 1862) — the poem depicts Union and Confederate soldiers camped on opposite hillsides, momentarily united by the sound of a band playing "Home, Sweet Home":

> Two armies covered hill and plain,
> Where Rappahannock's waters
> Ran deeply crimsoned with the stain
> Of battle's recent slaughters. (075-059)

The poem's setting — the "sad, slow stream," the "golden sunset," the cannons "grimly planted" — establishes a landscape of exhaustion and suspended violence. Into this silence, the music enters as an almost supernatural force:

> And now, where circling hills looked down,
> With cannon grimly planted,
> O'er listless camp and silent town
> The golden sunset slanted.
>
> When on the fervid air there came
> A strain — now rich and tender;
> The music seemed itself aflame
> With day's departing splendor.

The poem's central image — Northern and Southern soldiers listening together in silence — is handled without sentimentality. Thompson, who had served in the Confederate ranks, gives equal dignity to both sides:

> All silent now the Yankees stood,
> And silent stood the Rebels.
>
> No unresponsive soul had heard
> That plaintive note's appealing,
> So deeply "Home, Sweet Home" had stirred
> The hidden founts of feeling.

One of the poem's most poignant touches is its insistence that the soldiers' vision of home is identical, regardless of uniform: "Or Blue, or Gray, the soldier sees, / As by the wand of fairy, / The cottage 'neath the live-oak trees, / The cabin by the prairie." This democratic universalism — every soldier, Yankee or Rebel, dreams of the same humble home — was an unusual and generous sentiment in the rancorous atmosphere of 1863, when the poem was published.

Thompson published other poems and essays during his career, but none achieved the currency of "Music in Camp," which was widely reprinted in both Northern and Southern newspapers and anthologies. As an editor, his influence on Southern literature — through his decade-plus stewardship of the *Southern Literary Messenger* — was arguably greater than his own creative output.

## Critical Reception

"Music in Camp" was an immediate popular success when published in 1863, and its appeal crossed sectional lines. It was reprinted in both Union and Confederate newspapers, and after the war it became a staple of anthologies and school readers. Edmund Clarence Stedman included it in his influential *American Anthology* (1900), and it continued to appear in school recitation books well into the twentieth century.

Thompson's editorial work at the *Southern Literary Messenger* has been increasingly recognized by scholars of antebellum Southern literature. His cosmopolitanism — his cultivation of Northern and British literary contacts, his resistance to the more parochial tendencies of Southern literary nationalism — has been reassessed as a significant, if ultimately defeated, alternative to the sectional polarization that culminated in the Civil War.
