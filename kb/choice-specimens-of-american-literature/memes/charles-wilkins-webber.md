# Charles Wilkins Webber

**Charles Wilkins Webber** (1819–1856) was an American naturalist, explorer, and journalist whose writings documented the frontier landscapes and natural history of the early United States. He traveled extensively through the West, contributing observations on wildlife, geography, and Native American culture to periodicals of his day.

## Biography

Charles Wilkins Webber was born in 1819. He developed an early passion for the outdoors and natural science, which led him to travel widely across the western frontier. Webber served as a journalist for several periodicals, including the *Whig Review* and *Harper's Magazine*, bringing vivid accounts of frontier life to eastern readers. He later traveled to Central America, where he died in 1856 at the age of thirty-seven.

## Literary Career

Webber's literary reputation rests primarily on his nature writing and frontier narratives. His most notable work, *Wild Scenes and Song Birds* (1854), combined natural history with personal observation in a lyrical prose style that anticipated later American nature writing. He also contributed to periodicals with sketches of Western exploration, wildlife accounts, and descriptions of Native American cultures. His writing style blended scientific curiosity with a romantic sensibility, characteristic of mid-nineteenth-century American prose.

## Selections

### Ancient Earthworks in Wisconsin

Webber's account of the Wisconsin effigy mounds, from *Wild Scenes and Song Birds*, describes a unique class of prehistoric earthworks representing animal and human forms — structures that fascinated nineteenth-century archaeologists and naturalists alike.

> [054-014] There is a class of ancient earthworks in Wisconsin, not before found in any other country. Some have a resemblance to the buffalo, the eagle, or crane, or to the turtle or lizard. One, representing the human form, near the Blue Mounds, is, according to R.C. Taylor, Esq., one hundred and twenty feet in length: it lies in an east and west direction, the head towards the west, with the arms and legs extended. The body or trunk is thirty feet in breadth, the head twenty-five, and its elevation above the general surface of the prairie is about six feet. Its conformation is so distinct, that there can be no possibility of mistake in assigning it to the human figure.

Webber's description captures the wonder with which nineteenth-century observers regarded these monumental earthworks, whose builders remained a subject of intense speculation among archaeologists and historians of the period.

## Critical Reception

Webber's work received favorable notice from contemporaries who valued his firsthand knowledge of the frontier. His writings were appreciated for their vivid detail and energetic prose, though his literary reputation remained modest compared to more prominent nineteenth-century American authors. Modern scholars recognize him as a representative figure of mid-century American nature writing, contributing to the broader tradition that would later include figures such as [[John James Audubon]] and [[Henry David Thoreau]].
