# Daniel Webster

## Biography

Daniel Webster (1782–1852) was an American statesman, lawyer, and orator who represented Massachusetts in the United States Senate and served as Secretary of State under three presidents. Born in Salisbury, New Hampshire, Webster graduated from Dartmouth College in 1801 and was admitted to the bar in 1805.

Webster served in the House of Representatives (1813–1817, 1823–1827) and the Senate (1827–1841, 1845–1850), where he became the Senate's most formidable orator. As Secretary of State (1841–1843, 1850–1852), he negotiated the Webster-Ashburton Treaty (1842), which resolved boundary disputes with Great Britain.

His support for the Compromise of 1850, including the controversial Fugitive Slave Act, alienated many of his New England supporters and cast a shadow over his final years.

## Literary Career

Webster's literary reputation rests on his speeches, which were studied as models of American eloquence for generations after his death. His oratory combined legal precision with emotional grandeur, drawing on classical rhetoric and a deep well of patriotic sentiment.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes Webster's "Reply to Hayne" (1830), the most famous Senate debate in American history — a two-day exchange with Senator Robert Y. Hayne of South Carolina over the nature of the Union and the doctrine of nullification.

In this speech, Webster articulated a vision of national unity that would be echoed by Abraham Lincoln three decades later. He declared:

> I profess, sir, in my career hitherto, to have kept steadily in view the prosperity and honor of the whole country, and the preservation of our Federal Union. It is to that Union we owe our safety at home, and our consideration and dignity abroad. (018-010)

The peroration of Webster's reply is among the most celebrated passages in American oratory. He refused to contemplate disunion, declaring that he would not "hang over the precipice of disunion, to see whether, with my short sight, I can fathom the depth of the abyss below."

Instead, he invoked a vision of the flag preserved whole — "not a stripe erased or polluted, nor a single star obscured" — with the motto not "Liberty first, and Union afterwards," but "Liberty and Union, now and forever, one and inseparable" (018-010). These words, memorized by generations of American schoolchildren, cemented Webster's place in the national imagination.

## Critical Reception

Webster has long been regarded as the greatest orator in American political history. His speeches were published and republished throughout the nineteenth century, serving as the standard for American eloquence well into the twentieth. Literary historians have noted that Webster's oratory represents a distinctively American fusion of the classical tradition with the democratic ethos.

Yet Webster's legacy remains contested: his compromises on slavery, particularly his support for the Fugitive Slave Act, have led some historians to view him as a tragic figure who sacrificed moral principle for political expediency. His prose endures, however, as an exemplar of the unionist vision that Lincoln would later vindicate.
