=\_Theophilus H. Hill.\[101\] 1836-.\_=

From \"The Song of the Butterfly.\"

=\_426.\_=

[078-001]When the shades of evening fall, Like the foldings of a pall,\-- When the dew is on the flowers, And the mute, unconscious hours, Still pursue their noiseless flight Through the dreamy realms of night, In the shut or open rose Ah, how sweetly I repose!

\* \* \* \* \*

[078-002]And Diana\'s starry train, Sweetly scintillant again, Never sleep while I repose On the petals of the rose.

[078-003]Sweeter couch hath who than I?

[078-004]Quoth the brilliant Butterfly.

[078-005]Life is but a summer day, Gliding languidly away; Winter comes, alas! too soon,\-- Would it were forever June!

[078-006]Yet though brief my flight may be, Fun and frolic still for me!

[078-007]When the summer leaves and flowers, Now so beautiful and gay, In the cold autumnal showers, Droop and fade, and pine away, Who would not prefer to die?

[078-008]What were life to \_such as I\_?

[078-009]Quoth the flaunting Butterfly.

[078-010]\[Footnote 101: Born in North Carolina; in the intervals of his [[justice|law]]

[078-011]practice has published a volume of poems.\]

=\_Thomas Hailey Aldrich.\[102\] 1836-.\_=

From his \"Poems.\"

=\_427.\_= THE CRESCENT AND THE CROSS.

[078-012]Kind was my friend who, in the Eastern land, Remembered me with such a gracious hand, And sent this Moorish Crescent which has been Worn on the tawny bosom of a queen.

[078-013]No more it sinks and rises in unrest To the soft music of her heathen breast; No barbarous chief shall bow before it more, No turbaned slave shall envy and adore!

[078-014]I place beside this relic of the Sun A cross of Cedar brought from Lebanon, Once \'borne, perchance, by some pale monk who trod The desert to Jerusalem\--and his God!

[078-015]Here do they lie, two symbols of two creeds, Each meaning something to our human needs, Both stained with blood, and sacred made by faith, By tears, and prayers, and martyrdom, and death.

[078-016]That for the Moslem is, but this for me!

[078-017]The waning Crescent lacks divinity: It gives me dreams of battles, and the woes Of women shut in hushed seraglios.

[078-018]But when this Cross of simple wood I see, The Star of Bethlehem shines again for me, And glorious visions break upon my gloom\-- The patient Christ, and Mary at the Tomb!

[078-019]\[Footnote 102: Born in New Hampshire, but long connected with the press in

[078-020]New York. Has produced several volumes of poetry of unusual beauty and finish.\]

=\_Francis Bret Harte.\_=

From his \"Poems.\"

=\_428.\_= DICKENS IN CAMP.

[078-021]Above the pines the moon was slowly drifting, The river ran below; The dim Sierras, far beyond, uplifting Their minarets of snow.

[078-022]The roaring camp-fire, with rude humor, painted The ruddy tints of health, On haggard face, and form that drooped and fainted In the fierce race for wealth; Till one arose, and from his pack\'s scant treasure A hoarded volume drew, And cards were dropped from hands of listless leisure, To hear the tale anew; And then, while round them shadows gathered faster, And as the firelight fell, He read aloud the book wherein the Master Had writ of \"Little Nell.\"

[078-023]Perhaps \'twas boyish fancy,\--for the reader Was youngest of them all,\-- But, as he read, from clustering pine and cedar, A silence seemed to fall.

[078-024]The fir-trees, gathering closer in the shadows, Listened in every spray, While the whole camp, with \"Nell\" on English meadows, Wandered, and lost their way.

[078-025]And so in mountain solitudes\--o\'ertaken As by some spell divine\-- Their cares dropped from them like the needles shaken

From out the gusty pine.

[078-026]Lost is that camp I and wasted all its fire: And he who wrought that spell?\-- Ah, towering pine and stately Kentish spire, Ye have one tale to tell!

[078-027]Lost is that camp! but let its fragrant story Blend with the breath that thrills With hop-vines\' incense all the pensive glory That fills the Kentish hills.

[078-028]And on that grave where English oak and holly And laurel wreaths intwine, Deem it not all a too presumptuous folly,\-- This spray of Western pine!

From \"East and West Poems.\"

=\_429.\_= THE TWO SHIPS.

[078-029]As I stand by the cross on the lone mountain\'s crest, Looking over the ultimate sea, In the gloom of the mountain a ship lies at rest, And one sails away from the lea: One spreads its white wings on a far-reaching track, With pennant and sheet flowing free; One hides in the shadow with sails laid aback,\-- The ship that is waiting for me!

[078-030]But lo, in the distance the clouds break away!

[078-031]The Gate\'s glowing portals I see; And I hear from the outgoing ship in the bay The song of the sailors in glee: So I think of the luminous footprints that bore The comfort o\'er dark Galilee, And wait for the signal to go to the shore, To the ship that is waiting for me.

=\_Charles Dimitry,\[103\] 1838-.\_=

=\_430.\_= \"THE SERGEANT\'S STORY.\"

[078-032]Our army lay, At break of day, A full league from the foe away.

[078-033]At set of sun, The battle done, We cheered our triumph, dearly won.

\* \* \* \* \*

[078-034]All night before, We marked the roar Of hostile guns that on us bore; And \'here and there, The sudden blare Of fitful bugles smote the air.

[078-035]No idle word The quiet stirred Among us as the morning neared; And brows were bent, As silent went Unto its post each regiment.

[078-036]Blank broke the day, And wan and gray The drifting clouds went on their way.

[078-037]So sad the morn, Our colors torn, Upon the ramparts drooped forlorn!

[078-038]At early sun, The vapors dun Were lifted by a nearer gun; At stroke of nine, Auspicious sign The sun shone out along the line.

[078-039]Then loud and clear,

From cannoneer

[078-040]And rifleman arose a cheer; For as the gray Mists cleared away, We saw the charging foe\'s array.

[078-041]\[Footnote 103: Of a Louisiana family: is considered one of the most

[078-042]promising of the young writers of the South. The present is a favorable specimen of the poetry of the secession writers.\]

=\_John Hay.\_=\[104\]

From \"Pike County Ballads.\"

=\_431.\_= THE PRAIRIE.

[078-043]The skies are blue above my head, The prairie green below, And flickering o\'er the tufted grass The shifting shadows go, Vague-sailing, where the feathery clouds Fleck white the tranquil skies, Black javelins darting where aloft The whirring pheasant flies.

[078-044]A glimmering plain in drowsy trance The dim horizon bounds, Where all the air is resonant With sleepy summer sounds,\-- The life that sings among the flowers, The lisping of the breeze, The hot cicada\'s sultry cry, The murmurous dream of bees.

[078-045]The butterfly\--a flying flower\-- Wheels swift in flashing rings, And flutters round his quiet kin With brave flame-mottled wings.

[078-046]The wild Pinks burst in crimson fire, The Phlox\' bright clusters shine, And Prairie-cups are swinging free To spill their airy wine.

\* \* \* \* \*

[078-047]Far in the East, like low-hung clouds The waving woodlands lie; Far in the West, the glowing plain Melts warmly in the sky; No accent wounds the reverent air, No foot-print dints the sod,\-- Lone in the light the prairie lies, Rapt in a dream of God.

[078-048]\[Footnote 104: Born in Indiana. Gave up the practice of the law to become

[078-049]Secretary and Aide-de-camp to President Lincoln. Served briefly in the Rebellion war with the rank of Colonel, and was afterward Secretary of Legation at Paris and Madrid, and for some months, Chargé d\'Affaires at Vienna. Subsequently applied himself to literature and journalism.\]

=\_Joaquin Miller.\_=\[105\]

From \"Songs of the Sierras.\"

=\_432.\_= THE FUTURE OF CALIFORNIA.

[078-050]Dared I but say a prophecy, As sang the holy men of old, Of rock-built cities yet to be Along those shining shores of gold, Crowding athirst into the sea, What wondrous marvels might be told!

[078-051]Enough to know that empire here Shall burn her brightest, loftiest star; Here art and eloquence shall reign, As o\'er the wolf-reared realm of old; Here learn\'d and famous from afar, To pay their noble court, shall come, And shall not seek or see in vain, But look on all, with wonder dumb.

[078-052]Afar the bright Sierras lie, A swaying line of snowy white, A fringe of heaven hung in sight Against the blue base of the sky.

[078-053]I look along each gaping gorge, I near a thousand sounding strokes, Like giants rending giant oaks, Or brawny Vulcan at his forge; I see pick-axes flash and shine, And great wheels whirling in a mine.

[078-054]Here winds a thick and yellow thread, A moss\'d and silver stream instead; And trout that leap\'d its rippled tide Have turn\'d upon their sides and died.

[078-055]Lo! when the last pick in the mine Is rusting red with idleness, And rot yon cabins in the mould, And wheels no more croak in distress, And tall pines reassert command, Sweet bards along this sunset shore Their mellow melodies will pour; Will charm as charmers very wise, Will strike the harp with master-hand, Will sound unto the vaulted skies The valor of these men of old\-- The mighty men of \'Forty-nine; Will sweetly sing and proudly say, Long, long agone, there was a day When there were giants in the land.

[078-056]\[Footnote 105: Cincinnatus Heine Miller, commonly known by his assumed

[078-057]name of Joaquin Miller. Born in Indiana, but was taken when very young to Oregon. After a wild career in Oregon and California, he at length studied for the law. His poetry, like his life, is of an eccentric cast.\]

=\_Joel Chandler Harris,\[106\] 1846-.\_=

=\_433.\_= \"AGNES.\"

[078-058]She has a tender, winning way, And walks the earth with gentle grace, And roses with the lily play Amid the beauties of her face.

[078-059]When\'er she tunes her voice to sing, The song-birds list, with anxious looks, For it combines the notes of spring With all the music of the brooks.

[078-060]Her merry laughter, soft and low, Is as the chimes of silver bells,\-- That like sweet anthems float, and flow Through woodland groves and bosky dells, And when the violets see her eyes, They flush and glow\--with love and shame, They meekly droop with sad surprise, As though unworthy of the name.

[078-061]But still they bloom where\'er she throws Her dainty glance and smiles so sweet.

[078-062]And e\'en amid stern winter\'s snows The daisies spring beneath her feet.

[078-063]She wears a crown of Purity, Full set with woman\'s brightest gem,\-- A wreath of maiden modesty, And Virtue is the diadem.

[078-064]And when the pansies bloom again, And spring and summer intertwine.

[078-065]Great joys will fall on me like rain, For she will be for ever mine!

[078-066]\[Footnote 106: A native of Georgia; is deemed one of the best of the

[078-067]younger poets of the South.\]
