## Biography

Charles Pettit McIlvaine (1799–1873) was an American Episcopal bishop, theologian, and educator.
Born in Burlington, New Jersey, he graduated from the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University) in 1816
and was ordained as an Episcopal priest in 1821. He served as chaplain to the United States Senate from 1822 to 1823
and as rector of Christ Church in Georgetown, Washington, D.C.

In 1825, McIlvaine was appointed professor of ethics and chaplain at the United States Military Academy at West Point,
where he became a significant influence on the religious life of cadets. In 1832, he was elected the second president of
Kenyon College in Gambier, Ohio, succeeding Bishop Philander Chase. Later that same year, he was consecrated as the
Bishop of Ohio, a position he held until his death. During the Civil War, McIlvaine served as a trusted envoy for
President [[abraham-lincoln]], traveling to England to help prevent British recognition of the Confederacy.

## Literary Career

McIlvaine was a prolific writer of sermons and theological treatises. His most influential work,
*The Evidences of Christianity* (1832), was a widely circulated defense of Christian doctrine
that went through multiple editions in both America and England. His published sermons, including
those collected as *The Truth and Life* (1855), were noted for their clarity of argument and
earnestness of expression.

McIlvaine's theological perspective aligned with the evangelical wing of the Episcopal Church.
He was a leader in the revival movement within the denomination and maintained close ties with
leading evangelical figures of his time. His writings on the resurrection of Christ, represented
in this anthology, reflect the central place of doctrinal preaching in his ministry.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from McIlvaine:

- **"Attestations of the Resurrection"** (009-008): A passage from a sermon on the resurrection of Christ.
  McIlvaine argues that the resurrection was the great sign to which Christ referred throughout his ministry
  as the final confirmation of all his claims and doctrines:

> "He staked all on the promise that he would rise from death. The Jews asked of him a sign,
> that they might believe. He answered, 'There shall no sign be given, but the sign of the prophet Jonas.
> For as Jonas was three days and nights in the whale's belly, so shall the Son of Man be three days
> and three nights in the heart of the earth.' Thus on that single event, the resurrection of Christ,
> the whole of Christianity, as it all centres in, and depends on him, was made to hinge." (009-008)

## Critical Reception

McIlvaine was highly regarded as a preacher and theologian in his own time. His *Evidences of Christianity*
was used as a textbook in many American colleges and seminaries. As Bishop of Ohio, he oversaw significant
growth in the Episcopal Church in the American West. His diplomatic mission to England during the Civil War,
undertaken at President Lincoln's personal request, earned him recognition beyond ecclesiastical circles.
