# John P. Durbin

## Biography

John Price Durbin (1800–1875) was an American Methodist clergyman, educator, and missionary administrator. Born in Kentucky, he entered the ministry of the Methodist Episcopal Church at an early age and quickly acquired a reputation as one of the most eloquent preachers in the denomination. Durbin served as chaplain to the United States Senate, as editor of the *Christian Advocate and Journal*, and as president of Dickinson College in Carlisle, Pennsylvania, from 1834 to 1844. Under his leadership, Dickinson recovered from financial difficulties and expanded its curriculum and enrollment.

As described by the "Choice Specimens" editors, Durbin was "a native of Kentucky; is deemed one of the most eloquent divines in the Methodist church." His extensive travels in Europe and the Middle East, undertaken in part as a missionary representative, provided material for his most popular book, *Observations in the East* (1845), which went through multiple editions and established him as one of the earliest American writers of Holy Land travel narrative.

## Literary Career

Durbin's literary output bridged pastoral theology, missionary advocacy, and travel writing. His major publications include *Observations in the East* (1845), *Observations in Europe* (1844), and numerous published sermons and addresses. His travel narratives were distinguished by their descriptive immediacy and their persistent attention to the spiritual significance of biblical landscapes — a combination that made them enormously popular with American Protestant readers in the decades before the Civil War.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," "First Sight of Mount Sinai," is taken from *Observations in the East* and records the climactic moment of Durbin's approach to the traditional site of the giving of the Law:

> For two hours we ascended this wild, narrow pass, enclosed between stupendous granite cliffs, whose debris encumbered the defile, often rendering the passage difficult and dangerous. Escaping from the pass, we crossed the head of a basin-like plain, which declined to the south-west, and ascending gradually, gloomy, precipitous, mountain masses rose to view on either hand, with detached snow-beds lying in their clefts. The caravan moved slowly, and apparently with a more solemn, measured tread. The Bedouins became serious and silent, and looked steadily before them, as if to catch the first glimpse of some revered object. (008-007)

The landscape is rendered with a geologist's precision before the prose shifts register with the Bedouin guide's exclamation:

> The space before us gradually expanded, when suddenly Tualeb, pointing to a black, perpendicular cliff, whose two riven and rugged summits rose some twelve or fifteen hundred feet directly in front of us, exclaimed, *"Gebel Mousa!"* (008-007)

Durbin's rendering of his own response is carefully staged: "Not a word was spoken by Moslem or Christian, but slowly and silently we advanced into the still expanding plain, our eyes immovably fixed on the frowning precipices of the stern and desolate mountain." The moment of recognition is at once intensely personal and historically freighted:

> We were doubtless on the plain where Israel encamped at the giving of the law, and that grand and gloomy height before us was Sinai, on which God descended in fire, and the whole mountain was enveloped in smoke, and shook under the tread of the Almighty. (008-007)

The passage exemplifies a distinctively American Protestant mode of travel writing: physical geography is read as corroboration of scriptural history, and the traveler's personal emotion — silence, awe, fixity of gaze — becomes evidence of the continuing power of biblical place.

## Critical Reception

Durbin's *Observations in the East* was widely read and frequently reprinted in the mid-nineteenth century, establishing him as a significant American contributor to the genre of Holy Land travel narrative. His work was valued both for its descriptive vividness and for the authority conferred by his status as a church leader — readers trusted his theological framing of the biblical sites he described.

As president of Dickinson College, Durbin's administrative legacy was mixed: he succeeded in stabilizing the institution's finances and expanding its facilities, but his tenure was also marked by conflicts with faculty and trustees that led to his resignation in 1844. His subsequent work as a missionary secretary for the Methodist Episcopal Church extended his influence across the denomination's rapidly growing international missions program.
