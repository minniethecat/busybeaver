# Richard Fuller

## Biography

Richard Fuller (1808–1876) was an American Baptist clergyman and one of the most celebrated pulpit orators of the antebellum South. Born in Beaufort, South Carolina, he initially practiced law after graduating from Harvard College, achieving considerable success at the Charleston bar. A religious awakening in his mid-twenties led him to abandon the law for the ministry, and he was ordained as a Baptist preacher in 1835.

Fuller served as pastor of the Baptist Church in Beaufort before moving to the Seventh Baptist Church in Baltimore, Maryland, in 1847, where he would remain for the rest of his career. He was a central figure in the 1845 split of the national Baptist organizations over the issue of slavery, which resulted in the formation of the Southern Baptist Convention. Fuller served as the third president of the Southern Baptist Convention from 1851 to 1855. Despite his defense of slavery as a biblical institution, he was noted for the gentleness of his personal manner and his friendship with Northern Baptist leaders, including Francis Wayland of Brown University, with whom he conducted a widely-read published correspondence on the slavery question.

## Literary Career

Fuller's literary reputation rests almost entirely on his sermons and his published debate on slavery. His correspondence with Francis Wayland, *Domestic Slavery Considered as a Scriptural Institution* (1845), was one of the most important American texts on the biblical defense of slavery and circulated widely on both sides of the Mason-Dixon line. His collected sermons and occasional addresses, though never assembled into a comprehensive edition during his lifetime, were prized for their rhetorical power and emotional intensity.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, titled "The Desire of All Nations Shall Come," is drawn from a sermon on Haggai 2:7. In it, Fuller presents a sustained meditation on the crucifixion, the central theme of his preaching:

> Follow the adorable Jesus from scene to scene of ever deepening insult and sorrow, tracked everywhere by spies hunting for the precious blood. Behold his sacred face swollen with tears and stripes; and, last of all, ascend Mount Calvary, and view there the amazing spectacle. (011-005)

Fuller was known for his skill in classical allusion, as seen in his comparison of the Christian's relation to the cross with the Roman story of Manlius:

> That is an affecting passage in Roman history which records the death of Manlius. At night, and on the Capitol, fighting hand to hand, had he repelled the Gauls, and saved the city, when all seemed lost. (011-006)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Desire of All Nations Shall Come* (011-005, 011-006) — From a Sermon on Haggai 2:7. Fuller follows Christ from insult to sorrow to Calvary, then draws a striking parallel with the Roman story of Manlius, whose judges could not condemn him while the Capitol — the scene of his triumph — remained in view. So too, Fuller argues, the Christian cannot be faithless while the cross remains before the soul.

## Critical Reception

Fuller was widely regarded as one of the finest pulpit orators of the nineteenth-century South, and his reputation extended to the North through his published sermons and his debate with Wayland. Even those who disagreed with his position on slavery acknowledged his rhetorical gifts and the evident sincerity of his convictions. Baltimore's interdenominational community held him in particular esteem; his funeral in 1876 drew mourners from every Protestant church in the city.

Fuller's reputation today is complicated by his role in the pro-slavery schism of the Baptist church. Historians of American religion study him as a key figure in understanding how theologically conservative Southern Protestants reconciled evangelical piety with the institution of slavery, making him a subject of enduring historical interest if not theological admiration.
