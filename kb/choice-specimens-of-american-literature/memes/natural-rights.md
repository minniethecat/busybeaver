# Natural Rights in American Literature

## Definition

Natural rights, in American literary and political tradition, are those rights inherent in human nature and discoverable by reason, existing prior to and independent of government. The doctrine, derived from Enlightenment philosophy, became the intellectual foundation of the American Revolution and a recurring theme in American letters.

## Historical Context

The concept emerged from 17th-century natural law theory through Locke and the Scottish Enlightenment to become the philosophical framework of the Declaration of Independence. American writers articulated natural rights not merely as abstract theory but as actionable claims against tyranny. William Stith recorded early colonial assertions that "liberty is the natural right of all men" (005-004). Patrick Henry framed rights as "unalienable," arguing that to leave their defense to chance was to abandon them (013-014).

## Key Authors and Works

- **William Stith** documented early American appeals to natural rights as the basis for opposing enslavement of Christians (005-004).
- **Timothy Dwight** celebrated "a spirit of freedom, a recognition of the equal rights of men" as defining features of American society (007-004).
- **Patrick Henry**, in his revolutionary speeches, invoked natural rights as the moral foundation of the American cause, warning against any violation of "constitutional rights" (013-014, 014-001).
- **Alexander Hamilton** and other Federalist writers argued that the protection of natural rights required a properly structured constitutional government (014-001).

## Related Concepts

- [[liberty]] — The exercise of natural rights in civil society
- [[independence]] — The political sovereignty necessary to secure natural rights against foreign violation
- [[justice]] — The principle that government exists to protect natural rights impartially

## Legacy

The doctrine of natural rights, once revolutionary, became embedded in American constitutional law and political culture. The 19th century saw the language of natural rights extended to new claims: abolitionists demanded recognition of the natural rights of enslaved people; women's rights advocates applied the doctrine to the condition of women; and labor reformers argued for economic rights as natural and inalienable.
