# William Stith

## Biography

William Stith (1689–1755) was an American historian, Anglican clergyman, and educator. Born in Charles City County, Virginia, into a prominent family — his mother was a descendant of Pocahontas — Stith was educated at the College of William and Mary and at Oxford University. Upon returning to Virginia, he served as rector of Henrico Parish, as chaplain to the House of Burgesses, and in 1752 was appointed the third president of the College of William and Mary, a position he held until his death.

Stith's most important contribution to American letters was *The History of the First Discovery and Settlement of Virginia* (1747), the earliest substantial narrative history of the Virginia colony written by a native-born American. The work covered the period from the first attempted settlement under Sir Walter Raleigh through the dissolution of the Virginia Company in 1624, drawing extensively on colonial records and company documents.

## Literary Career

Stith's *History of Virginia* is notable for its documentary rigor and its narrative ambition. Unlike many earlier colonial chronicles, which were shaped primarily by promotional or polemical purposes, Stith made a deliberate effort to ground his narrative in official records, citing company proceedings, letters patent, and colonial statutes. His prose, while formal by modern standards, is animated by a genuine interest in the personalities of the early colonists — particularly Captain John Smith — and in the dramatic encounter between English settlers and the Powhatan Confederacy.

The first selection in "Choice Specimens," "The Rule of Powhatan," describes the political organization of the Powhatan chiefdom:

> Although both himself and people were very barbarous, and void of all letters and civility, yet was there such a government among them, that the magistrates for good command, and the people for due subjection, excelled many places that would be counted very civil. He had under him above thirty inferior Kings or Werowances, who had power of life and death, but were bound to govern according to the customs of their country. However, his will was in all cases, their supreme law, and must be obeyed. (003-009)

The passage is doubly revealing: it records Powhatan's absolutism while noting that the subordinate kings were "bound to govern according to the customs of their country" — a customary constitutionalism within what Stith calls barbarism. The tribute system is described in economic detail:

> They all knew their several lands, habitations, and limits, to fish, fowl, or hunt in. But they held all of their great Werowance, *Powhatan*; to whom they paid tribute of skins, beads, copper, pearl, deer, turkies, wild beasts, and corn. All his subjects reverenced him, not only as a King, but as half a God; and it was curious to behold, with what fear and adoration they obeyed him. For at his feet they presented whatever he commanded; and a frown of his brow would make their greatest Spirits tremble. (003-009)

The second selection, "Pocahontas in England," recounts the Virginia woman's reception at the court of James I:

> The whole court were charmed and surprised at the decency and grace of her deportment; and the king himself, and queen, were pleased honorably to receive and esteem her. The Lady Delawarr, and those other persons of quality, also waited on her to the masks, balls, plays, and other public entertainments, with which she was wonderfully pleased and delighted. And she would, doubtless, have well deserved, and fully returned, all this respect and kindness, had she lived to arrive in Virginia. (003-010)

Stith's Pocahontas is consistently portrayed as naturally noble — her "decency and grace" are presented as innate qualities that required only conversion to Christianity to be fully realized. The passage ends on the elegiac note that would become standard in Pocahontas narratives for the next two centuries.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "The Rule of Powhatan" (003-009) — From "The History of Virginia." An account of the political structure and authority of the Powhatan Confederacy, notable for its ethnographic detail and its ambivalent admiration of indigenous governance.

## Critical Reception

Stith's *History of Virginia* was the standard history of the colony for over a century, cited by Thomas Jefferson (who criticized Stith's style as "insipid") and relied upon by later historians such as George Bancroft. Modern scholars have recognized Stith's documentary thoroughness — he made extensive use of the records of the Virginia Company, many of which were subsequently lost — while acknowledging the limitations of his perspective as a colonial Anglican clergyman writing within the conventions of eighteenth-century providential historiography.
