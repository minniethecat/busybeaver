# James K. Paulding

## Biography

James Kirke Paulding (1779–1860) was born in Great Nine Partners, New York (now Dutchess County), into a family with deep Dutch roots in the Hudson Valley. His early life was shaped by the Revolutionary War: his father, a patriot merchant, suffered financial ruin during the conflict, and the family's circumstances were modest thereafter. Paulding's friendship with Washington Irving, formed in their New York youth, proved decisive — the two young men, along with Irving's brother William, collaborated on the *Salmagundi* papers (1807–1808), a series of satirical essays that established the Knickerbocker literary identity (001-136).

Paulding's public career was distinguished by loyal service in government. He served as Secretary of the Navy under President Martin Van Buren (1838–1841), and his literary and political lives were intertwined: his writings often defended American republican institutions against European, particularly British, cultural condescension.

## Literary Career

Paulding was a prolific and versatile writer whose career spanned more than four decades. After *Salmagundi*, he published a series of satirical works during the War of 1812 — *The Diverting History of John Bull and Brother Jonathan* (1812) and *The United States and England* (1815) — that used humor to argue for American cultural independence. His novels include *Koningsmarke, the Long Finne* (1823), *The Dutchman's Fireside* (1831), and *Westward Ho!* (1832), works that explored American history and regional character in the tradition of Irving and Cooper.

Paulding's prose style blends genial humor with sharp observation. An early critic of American impatience with permanence, he observed that "people seem to build everything extempore and pro tempore, as if they looked forward to a speedy removal or did not expect to want it long" (057-003). His championing of American subjects and settings helped establish the literary nationalism of the early republic.

> An original mind is rarely understood until it has been reflected from some half dozen congenial with it; so averse are men to admitting the true in an unusual form; whilst any novelty, however fantastic, however false, is greedily swallowed. (057-002)

## Selections in This Anthology

- **"Character of the Dutch and German Settlers"** (057-002–057-003): From *Letters from the South* (1817), this travel essay contrasts the solid, permanent building practices of Dutch and German settlers with the hasty, temporary construction Paulding saw elsewhere in the young republic (057-002). He wryly notes that even the trees Americans plant tend to be "Lombardy poplars, that spring up of a sudden, give no more shade than a broom stuck on end, and grow old with their planters" (057-003).

## Critical Reception

Paulding's reputation, like that of many Knickerbocker writers, was eclipsed by Irving's immense fame. Modern scholarship has recovered him as an important figure in the development of American literary nationalism and as a bridge between the Federalist-era literati and the Romantic generation. His satires, though of their time, represent an early and sophisticated example of American cultural criticism directed at European models.
