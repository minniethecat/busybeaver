# James Fenimore Cooper

## Biography

James Fenimore Cooper (1789–1851) was born in Burlington, New Jersey, the eleventh of twelve children. When he was one year old, his family moved to the frontier settlement of Cooperstown, New York, a village founded by his father, Judge William Cooper, on the shores of Otsego Lake. The frontier environment of his childhood — the dense forests, the nearby Iroquois reservations, the raw energy of a new settlement — would provide the imaginative landscape for his most enduring fiction.

Cooper attended Yale College at the age of thirteen but was dismissed after three years for misconduct, including an incident involving a prank with a donkey in a classroom. His father secured him a berth as a common sailor on a merchant vessel in 1806, and he later joined the United States Navy as a midshipman. These maritime experiences furnished the material for his sea novels and gave his writing a technical precision unusual among authors of his generation. In 1811, Cooper married Susan Augusta De Lancey, a member of a prominent New York family with Loyalist connections; he resigned his commission and settled into the life of a gentleman farmer in Westchester County.

Cooper's turn to fiction was, by his own account, accidental. In 1820, reading aloud to his wife from a popular English novel, he threw the book aside in frustration and declared that he could write a better one himself. His wife challenged him to make good on the boast, and *Precaution* (1820), a conventional domestic novel set in England, was the result. The book attracted little notice, but Cooper had discovered his vocation. He died at his home in Cooperstown in 1851, having published over thirty novels and numerous volumes of nonfiction.

## Literary Career

Cooper's literary achievement rests on two innovations: the creation of the American frontier romance and the establishment of the sea novel as a recognized genre. His second novel, *The Spy* (1821), a tale of the American Revolution set in Westchester County, was an immediate success on both sides of the Atlantic. *The Pioneers* (1823), the first of the five Leatherstocking Tales, introduced Natty Bumppo — also known as Leather-Stocking, the Pathfinder, and Hawkeye — the rugged frontiersman who moves ever westward ahead of the advance of civilization, embodying both the virtues and the contradictions of the American frontier experience.

Cooper's prose is characterized by precise descriptive detail and a measured, often digressive pace. His descriptive powers are evident in the passage selected for "Choice Specimens," which contrasts the quick, temporary building practices of most Americans with the durable, stone construction of the Dutch and German settlers:

> But you can always tell when you get among the Dutch and the Quakers, for there you perceive that something has been done for posterity. Their houses are of stone, and built for duration, not for show. If a German builds a house, its walls are twice as thick as others — if he puts down a gate-post, it is sure to be nearly as thick as it is long. Every thing about him, animate and inanimate, partakes of this character of solidity. (057-004)

The anthology also preserves a comic episode from *The Pioneers*, in which the speculator Zeno Paddock is lured to a "great city" in the wilderness, only to discover it is nothing but a forest:

> Truth obliges us to say, that on his arrival at the city of New Pekin, as it was called, he found it covered with a forest of trees, each of which would take a man half a day to walk round; and that on further inquiry he learned that the whole population of this great city consisted of a single family. Zeno set himself down, began to print his paper in a great hollow sycamore, and to live on anticipation, as many great speculators had done before him. (057-005)

This passage captures Cooper's satirical eye for the gap between American promise and American reality, a theme that runs through much of his work. The turkey-shooting contest that follows, featuring Natty Bumppo's marksmanship, showcases Cooper's gift for rendering action with clarity and suspense.

## Critical Reception

Cooper was the first American novelist to achieve a substantial international reputation. During his lifetime, his works were translated into every major European language, and he was read and admired by figures as diverse as Balzac, Goethe, and Thackeray. His depiction of the American wilderness and the Native American figured powerfully in the European Romantic imagination, shaping perceptions of the New World that persisted for generations.

At home, reception was more contentious. Cooper's prickly personality and his willingness to litigate against critics made him a controversial figure. His travel writings and social criticism, particularly *Notions of the Americans* (1828) and *The American Democrat* (1838), provoked resentment on both sides of the Atlantic. Mark Twain's famous essay "Fenimore Cooper's Literary Offenses" (1895) subjected Cooper's plotting and descriptive techniques to devastating satire, and for much of the twentieth century, Cooper's critical reputation suffered accordingly.

Modern scholarship has substantially revised this assessment. Critics including Leslie Fiedler, Richard Slotkin, and Wayne Franklin have argued for Cooper's centrality to the American literary tradition, seeing in the Leatherstocking Tales a profound meditation on the tensions between wilderness and civilization, individual freedom and social order, that define the American experience. The Leatherstocking myth of the lone frontiersman, at home in nature but doomed by the advance of settlement, remains one of the shaping narratives of American culture.
