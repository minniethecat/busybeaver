# Henry Ward Beecher

## Biography

Henry Ward Beecher (1813–1887) was an American Congregationalist clergyman, social reformer, and essayist who became one of the most celebrated preachers of the nineteenth century. Born in Litchfield, Connecticut, he was the eighth of thirteen children of the Reverend Lyman Beecher, a prominent evangelical minister, and the brother of Harriet Beecher Stowe, author of *Uncle Tom's Cabin*. Beecher graduated from Amherst College in 1834 and from Lane Theological Seminary in 1837.

Beecher served as pastor of the Second Presbyterian Church in Indianapolis (1837–1847) before being called to Plymouth Church in Brooklyn, New York, where he would minister for forty years (1847–1887). From this pulpit, Beecher became a national figure, renowned for his dramatic preaching style, his fervent abolitionism (he famously held mock slave auctions at Plymouth Church to dramatize the evil of slavery), and his advocacy of women's suffrage and temperance. His influence was amplified by his prolific writing, including contributions to the *New York Independent* and the publication of "The Star Papers," a collection of nature essays and personal reflections.

## Literary Career

Beecher's literary reputation rests on his essays, which blend theological reflection with vivid observation of the natural world and human life. His prose is characterized by warmth, sensory immediacy, and a Romantic sensibility that finds the divine in the ordinary. The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes four selections from Beecher's work, each exemplifying a different register of his literary voice.

"A Picture in a College at Oxford," from the "Star Papers," describes a moment of intense religious feeling before a painting of Christ:

> I was much affected by a head of Christ. Not that it met my ideal of that sacred front, but because it took me in a mood that clothed it with life and reality. For one blessed moment I was with the Lord. I know him. I loved him. My eyes I could not close for tears. (011-009)

Beecher describes the transformative power of this encounter in language that melds sensory description with theological reflection:

> ...there seemed to spring out upon me from my Master a certainty of love so great and noble as utterly to consume my unworth, and leave me shining bright, as if it were impossible for Christ to love a heart without making it pure and beautiful by the resting on it of that illuming affection, just as the sun bathes into beauty the homeliest object when he looks full upon it. (011-009)

"Frost on the Window" observes the fleeting beauty of winter ice formations, finding in them an instance of gratuitous divine artistry:

> Shall we not reverently and rejoicingly behold in these morning pictures, wrought without color, and kissed upon the window by the cold lips of Winter, another instance of that Divine Beneficence of beauty which suffuses the heavens? (011-010)

In "Nature, Designed for Our Enjoyment," Beecher develops a theology of earthly pleasure. He finds the natural world overflowing with beauty beyond any functional necessity:

> When I have journeyed through forests, where ten thousand shrubs and vines exist without apparent use; through prairies, whose undulations exhibit sheets of flowers innumerable, and absolutely dazzling the eye with their prodigality of beauty — beauty, not a tithe of which is ever seen by man — I have said, it is plain that God is himself passionately fond of beauty, and the earth is his garden, as an acre is man's. (011-011)

This conviction underwrites a moral argument against the pursuit of degraded pleasures: "Is it not enough that our Father's house is so full of dear delights, that we must wander prodigal to the swine-herd for husks, and to the slough for drink?" (011-012). Beecher's aim, as he states it, is "not to withdraw the young from pleasure, but from unworthy pleasures; not to lessen their enjoyments, but to increase them, by rejecting the counterfeit and the vile" (011-014).

The final passage broadens into a panoramic vision of the human drama:

> Upon the stage of life, the vastest tragedies are performing in every act; nations pitching headlong to their final catastrophe; others, raising their youthful forms to begin the drama of existence... Life mingles before us smiles and tears, sighs and laughter, joy and gloom, as the spring mingles the winter-storm and summer-sunshine. To this vast Theatre which God hath builded, where stranger plays are seen than ever author writ, man seldom cares to come. (011-014)

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Henry Ward Beecher's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *A Picture in a College at Oxford* (011-009) — From "The Star Papers." A meditation on a moment of spiritual transport before a painting of Christ, in which Beecher describes being overcome by a certainty of divine love.
- *Frost on the Window* (011-010) — From "The Star Papers." An observation of the transient beauty of frost patterns on glass, interpreted as evidence of the "Divine Beneficence of beauty."
- *Nature, Designed for Our Enjoyment* (011-011–011-014) — From "Lectures to Young Men." An extended argument that God created beauty for human enjoyment, urging the young to find their pleasures in nature and virtue rather than degraded substitutes.
- *Life in the Country* (not excerpted) — Also listed in the anthology's table of contents.
- *The Conception of Angels, Superhuman* (not excerpted) — Also listed in the anthology's table of contents.

## Critical Reception

Beecher's reputation has fluctuated dramatically. In his lifetime, he was among the most famous Americans, his sermons and essays read by hundreds of thousands. His oratorical style — emotional, dramatic, and conversational — influenced American preaching for generations. After his death, Beecher's literary reputation declined as tastes shifted toward more restrained prose, and his personal life became the subject of controversy (notably the 1875 adultery trial that captivated the nation).

Modern scholarship has begun to reassess Beecher's contributions to American letters, particularly his nature writing and his role in popularizing a Romantic theology of the natural world. His essays, especially those collected in the "Star Papers," are increasingly recognized as significant contributions to the American tradition of nature writing that extends from Jonathan Edwards through John Muir.
