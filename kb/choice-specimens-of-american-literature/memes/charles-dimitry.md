# Charles Dimitry

## Biography

Charles Dimitry (1838–1910) was an American poet, journalist, and educator from Louisiana. He was born into one of the most distinguished Creole families in New Orleans — his father, Alexander Dimitry, was a renowned linguist and the first person of color to serve as U.S. Minister to Costa Rica and Nicaragua under President John Tyler. The Dimitry family traced its lineage to French colonial Louisiana and had produced scholars, lawyers, and diplomats for generations. Charles grew up immersed in the multilingual, cosmopolitan culture of antebellum New Orleans.

During the Civil War, Dimitry served in the Confederate army, and his experience of military life — the long marches, the tense hours before battle, the camaraderie of the camps — became the defining material of his poetry. After the war he worked as a teacher in Louisiana and later in Mississippi, and he contributed poetry and prose to Southern periodicals. His later years were devoted to historical writing about Louisiana and his family's heritage.

## Literary Career

Dimitry's poetry is distinguished by its narrative compression — he tells stories in taut, ballad-like stanzas that convey the urgency and immediacy of wartime experience. "The Sergeant's Story," the selection anthologized in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, demonstrates this quality with remarkable economy. The poem opens at dawn, with the army poised on the brink of battle:

> Our army lay,
> At break of day,
> A full league from the foe away.
> At set of sun,
> The battle done,
> We cheered our triumph, dearly won. (078-031)

Dimitry's verse is notable for its lack of bombast. Unlike many poets of the Confederacy who indulged in rhetorical excess, Dimitry writes with a soldier's understatement. The poem's refrain-like four-line stanzas march forward with military precision, each stanza advancing the scene from the quiet of morning to the chaos of battle to the satisfaction of victory. The personal cost — "dearly won" — is stated simply, without elaboration.

Critics of his day recognized Dimitry as one of the most promising young poets of the South. The *Choice Specimens* anthology introduces him as "considered one of the most promising of the young writers of the South," noting that "The Sergeant's Story" is "a favorable specimen of the poetry of the secession writers." Though his output was small, Dimitry's disciplined and unsentimental voice distinguishes him from his more florid Confederate contemporaries. His later writings, mostly historical sketches of Louisiana and the Dimitry family, were published in local newspapers and periodicals.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "The Sergeant's Story" (078-031) — Dimitry's best-known poem. A soldier's ballad that moves from the tense quiet of dawn through the roar of approaching battle to the celebration of hard-won victory. The poem's terse, marching rhythm and absence of rhetorical excess distinguish it from much Civil War verse and mark Dimitry as a poet of genuine narrative control.

## Critical Reception

Dimitry was praised by Southern anthologists and critics as a voice of genuine promise. The *Choice Specimens* compiler noted that his work represented "a favorable specimen of the poetry of the secession writers" — a judgment that acknowledged both his skill and his historical position as a poet of the Confederate cause. In the postwar period, Dimitry's literary reputation faded along with the broader eclipse of Southern Confederate poetry. He continued to write, but his audience was largely local — his poetry appeared in New Orleans newspapers and literary journals rather than the national periodicals that had once recognized his talent.

Modern assessments are limited, as Dimitry is a minor figure even within the specialized field of Civil War literature. His work has been included in studies of Louisiana Creole literary culture and in surveys of Southern Civil War poetry, where he is recognized for the controlled narrative quality that sets him apart from the period's prevailing oratorical style.
