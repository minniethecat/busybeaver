# David Ramsay

## Biography

David Ramsay (1749–1815) was an American physician, historian, and statesman. Born in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, Ramsay was educated at the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University) and received his medical degree from the College of Philadelphia in 1773. He settled in Charleston, South Carolina, where he established a medical practice and became increasingly involved in the political life of the colony.

Ramsay served as a delegate from South Carolina to the Continental Congress (1782–1783, 1785–1786), was a member of the South Carolina convention that ratified the United States Constitution, and served in the state legislature. His medical career was equally distinguished: he was one of the earliest historians of American medicine and was among the first physicians in the United States to advocate for the systematic collection of public health data.

Ramsay's political loyalties were complex. A supporter of American independence who was briefly imprisoned by the British during the occupation of Charleston, he nevertheless maintained close intellectual ties with British correspondents. In 1815, while serving as a state senator, he was shot and mortally wounded on the streets of Charleston by a patient whom he had examined in a legal capacity — one of the most sensational episodes in the early history of American forensic medicine.

## Literary Career

Ramsay's literary output was prodigious and varied. His major works include *The History of the Revolution of South Carolina* (1785), *The History of the American Revolution* (1789), *The Life of George Washington* (1807), *The History of South Carolina* (1809), and *The History of the United States* (3 vols., 1816–1817, posthumous). His *History of the American Revolution* was the first comprehensive account of the war written by an American participant and was praised by George Washington himself.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," "Feeling of the Province Toward Great Britain," is taken from the *History of the Revolution in South Carolina* and offers an unusually measured retrospective portrait of colonial loyalty:

> In South Carolina, an enemy to the Hanoverian succession, or to the British constitution, was scarcely known. The inhabitants were fond of British manners even to excess. They for the most part, sent their children to Great Britain for education, and spoke of that country under the endearing appellation of Home. They were enthusiasts for that sacred plan of civil and religious happiness under which they had grown up and flourished.... Wealth poured in upon them from a thousand channels. The fertility of the soil generously repaid the labor of the husbandman, making the poor to sing, and industry to smile, through every corner of the land. None were indigent but the idle and unfortunate. (025-003)

This is a pastoral of colonial prosperity — the soil generous, the poor singing, industry smiling — and it is rendered with the deliberate calm of a historian who knows what catastrophe is about to unfold. The passage then pivots from the idyll to its dissolution, tracing how a people "completely satisfied with their government, and wish[ing] not for the smallest change in their political constitution" were "step by step drawn into an extensive war, which involved them in every species of difficulty, and finally dissevered them from the parent state."

Ramsay's explanation of this transformation is not primarily political but sociological. The colonists' independence of mind, he argues, was produced by their material circumstances:

> Every inhabitant was, or easily might be, a freeholder. Settled on lands of his own, he was both farmer and landlord. Having no superior to whom he was obliged to look up, and producing all the necessaries of life from his own grounds, he soon became independent. His mind was equally free from all the restraints of superstition. No ecclesiastical establishment invaded the rights of conscience, or fettered the free-born mind. At liberty to act and think as his inclination prompted, he disdained the ideas of dependence and subjection. (025-003)

This is the economic theory of American independence in its purest form: freehold tenure produces free minds; self-sufficient agriculture produces self-governing citizens. The Revolution is explained not as the work of ideology or leadership but as the natural political expression of a particular mode of material life. It is a thesis that would be elaborated and contested by American historians for the next two centuries.

## Critical Reception

Ramsay's *History of the American Revolution* was recognized immediately as an important work — Thomas Jefferson and John Adams both read and commented on it — and it went through multiple editions in the United States and Britain. Modern historians have acknowledged Ramsay's pioneering role in establishing American historical writing as a professional enterprise while noting the limitations of his perspective as a participant-historian writing in the immediate aftermath of events he had helped shape.

Ramsay's violent death in 1815 cut short what might have been an even more substantial literary career. His posthumously published *History of the United States* was the first attempt at a comprehensive national narrative from colonial origins to the ratification of the Constitution.
