# Nathaniel Hawthorne

Nathaniel Hawthorne (1804–1864) was the master of the American psychological romance.

His fiction explored the shadows of the Puritan past — sin, guilt, and the hidden recesses of the human heart. [[the-scarlet-letter|The Scarlet Letter]] (1850) remains one of the foundational novels of American literature, while his short stories in *Twice-Told Tales* (1837) established the form.

A neighbor of [[ralph-waldo-emerson]] in Concord and a friend of [[herman-melville]], Hawthorne stood apart from the Transcendentalist optimism of his contemporaries. His work insisted on the reality of evil and the inescapable weight of the past.

## Biography

Hawthorne was born on July 4, 1804, in Salem, Massachusetts.

His ancestor John Hathorne had been a judge in the Salem witch trials — a legacy that haunted Hawthorne throughout his life. He added the "w" to his surname to distance himself from that history. His father, a sea captain, died when Nathaniel was four years old.

Hawthorne attended Bowdoin College in Maine, where his classmates included [[henry-wadsworth-longfellow]] and future president Franklin Pierce. After graduation in 1825, he returned to Salem and spent twelve years in near-seclusion, reading widely and honing his craft.

His first collection, *Twice-Told Tales* (1837), brought him recognition. [[henry-wadsworth-longfellow]] praised its "weird and sombre beauty." To support himself, Hawthorne worked at the Boston Custom House (1839–41) and later lived at Brook Farm, the Transcendentalist utopian community — an experience he later satirized in [[the-blithedale-romance|The Blithedale Romance]] (1852).

He married Sophia Peabody in 1842 and moved to the Old Manse in Concord, where he lived near [[ralph-waldo-emerson]] and [[henry-david-thoreau]]. The years 1850–52 were his most productive: *The Scarlet Letter* (1850), *The House of the Seven Gables* (1851), and *The Blithedale Romance* (1852) appeared in rapid succession.

Hawthorne's final years were marked by declining health and creative struggles. He died on May 19, 1864, while traveling with Franklin Pierce.

## Literary Style and Themes

Hawthorne developed a distinctive mode he called the "romance" — distinct from the realistic novel:

| Characteristic | Description | Example |
|---------------|-------------|---------|
| Psychological depth | Interior states of guilt, obsession, and shame | Dimmesdale's torment in *The Scarlet Letter* |
| Historical imagination | Puritan New England as moral and psychological backdrop | *The Scarlet Letter*, "Young Goodman Brown" |
| Symbolism | Objects and settings charged with moral meaning | The scarlet A, the seven-gabled house |
| Ambiguity | Refusal of simple moral resolution | Multiple interpretations of the scarlet letter's meaning |
| Gothic atmosphere | Dark settings, ancestral curses, supernatural suggestion | The Maule curse, the haunted portrait |

[[herman-melville]], who dedicated *Moby-Dick* to Hawthorne, recognized in his friend "a great power of blackness." Hawthorne's darkness was not nihilistic but moral — a Calvinist sense of sin transformed into psychological art.

## Major Works

- *Twice-Told Tales* (1837, expanded 1842) — Short story collection establishing Hawthorne's reputation for allegorical and psychologically acute fiction.

- *Mosses from an Old Manse* (1846) — Stories including "Young Goodman Brown" and "The Birth-Mark," praised by [[herman-melville]] in a famous review.

- *The Scarlet Letter* (1850) — A novel of adultery, hypocrisy, and redemption in Puritan Boston. [[hester-prynne|Hester Prynne]] became one of literature's most complex heroines.

- *The House of the Seven Gables* (1851) — A family saga exploring inherited guilt across generations.

- *The Blithedale Romance* (1852) — A fictionalized account of the Brook Farm utopian experiment.

- *The Marble Faun* (1860) — Hawthorne's final completed novel, set among American artists in Rome.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Nathaniel Hawthorne appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874):

**From "Twice-Told Tales" — "Portrait of Edward Randolph"**

> [060-006] The whole portrait started so distinctly out of the background, that it had the effect of a person looking down from the wall at the astonished and awe-stricken spectators. The expression of the face, if any words can convey an idea of it, was that of a wretch detected in some hideous guilt, and exposed to the bitter hatred, and laughter, and withering scorn of a vast, surrounding multitude.

**From "The Blithedale Romance" — "A Picture of Girlhood"**

> [060-008] Priscilla had now grown to be a very pretty girl, and still kept budding and blossoming, and daily putting on some new charm, which you no sooner became sensible of than you thought it worth all she had previously possessed. So unformed, vague, and without substance, as she had come to us, it seemed as if we could see Nature shaping out a woman before our very eyes...

These selections display Hawthorne's mastery of psychological description and his characteristic blending of the actual and the allegorical.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

Hawthorne's contemporaries recognized his genius immediately. [[edgar-allan-poe]] praised *Twice-Told Tales* as the work of "one of the few men of indisputable genius to whom our country has as yet given birth." [[henry-james]] devoted an entire book to Hawthorne's achievement.

In the twentieth century, Hawthorne's reputation continued to grow. F.O. Matthiessen placed him at the center of the *American Renaissance*. T.S. Eliot praised his exploration of original sin, and D.H. Lawrence saw in his work the psychological complexity of the American soul.

Modern critics have expanded the reading of Hawthorne through feminist, psychoanalytic, and new historicist lenses. [[hester-prynne|Hester Prynne]] has been reclaimed as a proto-feminist heroine, and Hawthorne's engagement with antebellum politics has received renewed attention.

His influence extends to [[henry-james]], [[william-faulkner]], [[flannery-oconnor]], and [[toni-morrison]]. The American tradition of the psychological novel begins with Hawthorne.
