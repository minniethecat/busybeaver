# Laura M. H. Thurston

## Biography

Laura M. Hawley Thurston (1812–1842) was an American poet born in New York State. Little detailed biographical information about her brief life has survived; the *Manual of American Literature* (cited in Martin's anthology at 073-022) records her dates and her work, but contemporary records are sparse. She married and published poetry in periodicals of the 1830s before her death at the age of thirty. Her posthumous collection of verse, preserved in literary anthologies of the period, reveals a poet of genuine feeling for the American landscape and the emotional landscape of domestic affection. Thurston belongs to the generation of early American women poets — including Lucy Hooper, Margaret M. Davidson, and Amelia B. Welby — who found in the periodical press and in gift-book annuals a receptive audience for their lyric voice, even as their literary careers were constrained by the abbreviated lifespans and limited professional opportunities available to women writers in the antebellum period.

## Literary Career

Thurston's poetry as represented in her surviving work belongs to the Romantic tradition of landscape meditation and domestic sentiment. Her most characteristic poem, "Lines on Crossing the Alleghanies," exemplifies the American poet's negotiation between the wonders of the western frontier and the emotional pull of the settled East. Her verse is marked by its controlled lyricism, its attention to the affective dimensions of place, and its willingness to let doubt and melancholy coexist with patriotic optimism. Unlike the more declamatory male poets of her era who celebrated westward expansion as unambiguous progress, Thurston's speaker stands at the watershed of the continent — "Upon the lofty bound I stand, / That parts the East and West" — and experiences the tension between hope and homesickness, the bright promise of the future and the irrecoverable warmth of the past. Her poetry thus articulates a more complex emotional geography of American expansion than is found in much contemporaneous verse on similar subjects.

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) includes one poem by Laura M. H. Thurston:

**"Lines on Crossing the Alleghanies"** (073-022–073-029). Written from a mountain vantage point overlooking the western territories, the poem opens with an apostrophe to the "Valley of the West" and a declaration of faith in its destiny: "I hail thee, Valley of the West, / For what thou yet shalt be!" But the poem immediately pivots to acknowledge that for all the brightness of the western prospect, the poet's heart remains in the East — "For eyes beneath their radiant shrine / In kindlier glances answered mine: / Can these their light restore?" The key couplet at the poem's structural center — "Before me lies a fairy land; / Behind — *a home of rest!*" — crystallizes the tension between aspiration and attachment. This ambivalent vision of westward movement, in which the speaker's heart remains tethered to what she has left behind, voices a feminine counterpoint to the masculinist rhetoric of manifest destiny that dominated American public discourse in the decades before the Civil War.

> I hail thee, Valley of the West, For what thou yet shalt be! I hail thee for the hopes that rest Upon thy destiny! (073-022–073-023)

> Upon the lofty bound I stand, That parts the East and West; Before me lies a fairy land; Behind — *a home of rest!* (073-029)

The poem is followed in the anthology by several shorter pieces on parting and spiritual consolation (073-030–073-034, 073-035–073-040), which Martin groups under Thurston's name, though the attribution of some of these additional verses is uncertain from the anthology's layout.

## Critical Reception

Thurston's work, like that of many early American women poets who died young, has received minimal scholarly attention. She is occasionally mentioned in surveys of nineteenth-century American women's poetry, typically in the context of the gift-book and periodical culture that sustained women's literary production in the antebellum decades. Her inclusion in Martin's 1874 anthology — published over thirty years after her death — testifies to the endurance of her reputation among mid-century readers and editors. Recent feminist scholarship on American women's poetry has begun to recover figures like Thurston as contributors to a distinct tradition of women's landscape writing that complicates the dominant narrative of American literary nationalism.
