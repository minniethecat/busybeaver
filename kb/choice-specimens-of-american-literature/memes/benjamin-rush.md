# Benjamin Rush

## Biography

Benjamin Rush (1745–1813) was an American physician, educator, and statesman born near Philadelphia. He studied medicine at the University of Edinburgh and returned to Philadelphia to become one of the most influential medical practitioners and teachers in the young republic. A signer of the Declaration of Independence, Rush served as Surgeon General of the Continental Army and later as a professor at the University of Pennsylvania. He was a passionate advocate for social reforms including the abolition of slavery, prison reform, and public education. Rush died in Philadelphia at age sixty-seven.

## Literary Career

Rush was a prolific writer whose works spanned medicine, politics, education, and moral philosophy. His medical writings, particularly on yellow fever and mental illness, earned him the title "Father of American Psychiatry." His essays addressed the broadest questions of republican citizenship — he believed that education and moral improvement were essential to the success of the American experiment. Rush's prose combined Enlightenment rationality with a deeply religious sensibility, and his writings influenced generations of American physicians and reformers.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes two passages relating to Benjamin Rush:

- "Life of Edward Drinker" (022-005) — A biographical sketch that reflects on the life of one who "saw the same spot of earth which at one period of his life was covered with wood and bushes... afterwards become the seat of a city not only the first in wealth and arts in the new, but rivalling, in both, many of the first cities in the old world."
- "Introductory Lecture on the Yellow Fever" (022-016) — An account of Rush's experiences during the yellow fever epidemic in Philadelphia. His introductory lecture described "the terrible sweep of the late pestilence; the wild dismay and temporary desolation it had produced" and the joy he felt upon discovering "a successful mode of treating it." The discourse, though "highly colored," was "sufficiently fraught with substantial matter to render it no less instructive than it was fascinating."

## Critical Reception

Rush was celebrated in his lifetime as one of the most accomplished Americans of his generation. John Adams considered him a friend and intellectual equal, and his medical theories, though controversial, shaped American practice for decades. Modern historians recognize Rush as a complex figure whose medical methods (particularly his reliance on bloodletting) were sometimes misguided, but whose humanitarian vision and reformist energy were extraordinary. His writings remain essential documents of the American Enlightenment's aspirations for science, education, and social progress.
