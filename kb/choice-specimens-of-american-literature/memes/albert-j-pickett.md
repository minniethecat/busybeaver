## Biography

Albert James Pickett (1810–1858) was born in Anson County, North Carolina, and moved with his family to Alabama in early childhood. His father, a prosperous planter, sent him to study law, but Pickett's passion lay in the history of the region he called home. He abandoned legal practice to devote himself to research, traveling extensively through Alabama and neighboring states to interview surviving pioneers, Creek elders, and traders who had witnessed the transformation of the frontier.

Pickett married Sarah Smith Harris in 1832 and settled near Montgomery. His health declined in his forties, and he died in 1858 at the age of forty-eight, leaving behind a single major work that would define early Alabama historiography for generations.

## Literary Career

Pickett's *History of Alabama, and Incidentally of Georgia and Mississippi, from the Earliest Period* (1851) was the first comprehensive history of the state. Drawing on oral interviews with Creek Indians, "Indian country men" (white traders married into Native communities), and archival documents, Pickett produced a narrative that preserved first-hand accounts of frontier life, Native American customs, and the early territorial period that would otherwise have been lost.

The work was well received upon publication and remains a valuable primary source for historians of the early Southeast. Pickett's method — combining documentary research with oral history — was ahead of its time, anticipating the ethnographic approaches of later frontier historians.

## Selections

The corpus presents a single extended passage from Pickett's *History of Alabama*, in which he describes his youthful interactions with the Creek Indians and the sources that informed his historical method.

### The Indians and the Early Settlers of Alabama (030-001)

In this passage, Pickett recounts the personal experiences and oral informants that shaped his historical research. His detailed recollection of Creek life — the "green-corn dances, ball plays, war ceremonies" — establishes the ethnographic authority that grounds his *History*. Equally valuable is his portrait of the "Indian country men," white traders of Scotch descent who married Creek wives and served as cultural intermediaries between Native and settler communities:

> [030-001] During my youthful days, I was accustomed to be much with the Creek Indians, hundreds of whom came almost daily to the trading-house. For twenty years I frequently visited the Creek nation. Their green-corn dances, ball plays, war ceremonies, and manners and customs, are all fresh in my recollection. In my intercourse with them I was thrown into the company of many old white men called "Indian country men," who had for years conducted a commerce with them. Some of these men had come to the Creek nation before the Revolutionary War, and others, being tories, had fled to it during the war, and after it to escape from whig persecution. They were unquestionably the shrewdest and most interesting men with whom I ever conversed. Generally of Scotch descent, many of them were men of some education. All of them were married to Indian wives, and some of them had intelligent and handsome children.... I often conversed with the chiefs while they were seated in the shades of the spreading mulberry and walnut, upon the banks of the beautiful Tallapoosa. As they leisurely smoked their pipes, some of them related to me the traditions of their country. I occasionally saw Choctaw and Cherokee traders, and learned much from them. I had no particular object in view, at that time, except the gratification of a curiosity which led me, for my own satisfaction alone, to learn something of the early history of Alabama.

## Reception

Pickett's *History of Alabama* was well received in its time, praised for its detailed first-hand accounts and its preservation of the oral traditions of the Creek, Choctaw, and Cherokee peoples. Modern historians value it primarily as a primary source — a window into how the early nineteenth-century frontier remembered itself — while acknowledging its limitations as a work of professional history. Pickett's decision to foreground Native American perspectives, however mediated by white intermediaries, distinguishes his work from many contemporary histories of the period.
