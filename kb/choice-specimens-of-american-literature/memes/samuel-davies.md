# Samuel Davies

## Biography

Samuel Davies (1723–1761) was a Presbyterian minister, educator, and hymn writer who became one of the most celebrated preachers in colonial America. Born in New Castle County, Delaware, to Welsh-descended parents, Davies was educated at Samuel Blair's log college at Fagg's Manor, Pennsylvania — one of the "log colleges" that trained ministers during the Great Awakening before the establishment of formal seminaries.

Ordained as a Presbyterian evangelist in 1747, Davies was sent to Hanover County, Virginia, where he ministered to scattered congregations of dissenters in a colony where the Church of England was legally established. His eloquence and personal courage — he preached in a colony that had prosecuted Baptist and Presbyterian ministers under its toleration laws — drew large audiences and earned him the reputation as the foremost Presbyterian orator of the southern colonies. His success in Virginia helped secure legal recognition for dissenting congregations and laid groundwork for the disestablishment of the Anglican Church after the Revolution.

In 1759, Davies was elected the fourth president of the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University), succeeding Jonathan Edwards. His presidency was brief — he served only eighteen months before his death in 1761 at the age of thirty-seven — but he revitalized the institution, strengthened its library, and began raising funds for a new building. His early death was widely mourned; Patrick Henry, who had heard Davies preach as a young man in Virginia, later credited Davies's oratory as a formative influence on his own rhetorical style.

## Literary Career

Davies's literary output consists principally of his sermons, which were collected and published after his death in multiple volumes and went through numerous editions in both America and Britain. His preaching style combined the emotional fervor of the Great Awakening with a polished, literary quality that reflected his wide reading in English literature and classical rhetoric.

Davies was also a significant figure in American hymnody. He composed at least sixteen hymns, several of which — including "Great God of Wonders" and "Who Is a Pardoner Like Thee?" — remained in Protestant hymnals into the twentieth century. His hymns are characterized by a stately, meditative quality and a focus on divine mercy and human mortality, themes that also dominate his most famous sermon, excerpted in "Choice Specimens":

> So extensive have been the havoc and devastation which death has made in the world for near six thousand years, ever since it was first introduced by the sin of man, that this earth is now become one vast grave-yard or burying-place for her sons. (003-002)

The passage unfolds a grand, almost cosmic meditation on mortality. Davies surveys the entire span of human history — "from Adam to this day" — and sees the earth as a vast repository of the dead, a "huge carnage, a vast charnel-house, undermined and hollowed with the graves." His imagery is striking for its unflinching concreteness and its democratic sweep: princes and beggars, conquerors and slaves, "Heathens, Jews, Mahometans, and Christians, all lie equally low." The passage's power lies in its insistence that death is the one universal human experience, and that all human distinctions collapse before it:

> There the prince and the beggar, the conqueror and the slave, the giant and the infant, the scheming politician and the simple peasant, the wise and the fool, Heathens, Jews, Mahometans, and Christians, all lie equally low, and mingle their dust without distinction. (003-002)

Yet the sermon's ultimate direction is not toward despair but toward the gospel promise announced in its title: "Life and Immortality Revealed Through the Gospel." The very universality of death, for Davies, makes the Christian revelation of immortality all the more urgent and precious. His description of infants who "rise from nothing at the creative fiat of the Almighty, and take an immediate flight into the world of spirits" — "young immortals" who "immediately ripen in the world of spirits" — reflects a characteristic eighteenth-century synthesis of rational observation and evangelical piety.

## Critical Reception

Davies's reputation in his own lifetime was immense. The English hymn writer Philip Doddridge called him "the most eloquent preacher in America." In Virginia, where the established church viewed him with suspicion, his personal dignity and intellectual power won grudging respect even from opponents. The attorney general of Virginia, Peyton Randolph, after hearing Davies defend the legal rights of dissenters, reportedly remarked that Davies argued "with as much solicitude as if the fate of the British dominions depended upon his success."

Twentieth-century scholarship has recognized Davies as a pivotal figure in the development of American religious liberty, a transitional figure between the Puritan sermon tradition and the more polished, literary homiletics of the nineteenth century. His influence on Patrick Henry links the Great Awakening to the oratorical culture of the American Revolution — a connection that has drawn sustained attention from historians of American rhetoric.
