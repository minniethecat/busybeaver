# Thomas S. Grimke

Thomas Smith Grimké (1786–1834) was an American lawyer, social reformer, and essayist of the antebellum South. A member of the prominent Grimké family of South Carolina, he distinguished himself through his advocacy of educational reform, temperance, and the cause of peace.

Though his sisters [[sarah-grimke|Sarah]] and [[angelina-grimke|Angelina Grimké]] would become famous abolitionists, Thomas S. Grimké focused his reform energies on education, the law, and moral topics. His literary essays, collected as *Addresses, Scientific and Literary*, reflect the erudition and moral seriousness of the gentleman-reformer tradition in the early American republic.

His essay on "The Literary Excellence of the English Bible" is a notable early example of American literary appreciation of the King James Version as a work of English prose rather than merely a sacred text.

## Biography

Grimké was born on September 26, 1786, in Charleston, South Carolina, to John Faucheraud Grimké, a Revolutionary War officer and jurist, and Mary Smith Grimké. He was one of fourteen children in a family that would produce some of the most prominent reformers of the nineteenth century.

He graduated from Yale College in 1807, studied law, and was admitted to the South Carolina bar. He established a successful legal practice in Charleston and served in the state legislature.

Grimké became increasingly devoted to social reform causes in the 1820s and 1830s. He was a leading advocate for educational reform in South Carolina, arguing for a more practical, scientifically oriented curriculum. He also promoted temperance and was a prominent voice in the American peace movement. He died of cholera on October 12, 1834, in Columbus, Ohio, while traveling to deliver a lecture.

## Literary Career

Grimké's literary reputation rests primarily on his published addresses and essays. His *Addresses, Scientific and Literary* collected his lectures on topics ranging from legal reform to classical education.

His essay "The Literary Excellence of the English Bible" represents a significant moment in American literary criticism. Writing before the Bible was widely studied as literature, Grimké argued that the King James Version was a masterpiece of English prose, equal or superior to the classics. His six-point analysis of the translation's excellence — emphasizing its dignity, fidelity to thought over style, collaborative authorship, and freedom from sectarian bias — anticipates modern literary approaches to biblical texts.

Grimké's prose style is characteristic of the early nineteenth-century American orator: formal, periodic, and characterized by the balanced antitheses and enumerations favored by the neoclassical rhetorical tradition. His writing lacks the Romantic warmth of his Transcendentalist contemporaries, but it possesses clarity, conviction, and intellectual rigor.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Grimké: "Literary Excellence of the English Bible," from his *Addresses, Scientific and Literary*. The essay offers six reasons for the supreme literary quality of the King James translation.

> [033-003] The translation of the Bible, in the reign of James I., is the most remarkable and interesting event in the history of translations... The great excellence of the translation is due to six considerations. First, it was made under a very solemn sense of the important duty devolved on those who were thus selected. Hence arose that prevailing air of dignity, gravity, simplicity, which is so conspicuous.

> [033-004] Secondly, the translators came to the task looking to the thoughts, not to the style. Their object was not that of all other translators, to imitate and rival the beauty of style. Their sole object was to render faithfully, and in a plain, appropriate style, the thoughts of the sacred writers... Thirdly, the number of persons employed contributed very much to prevent any personal style from prevailing.

The essay exemplifies the neoclassical rhetorical mode at its most effective: orderly, analytical, and persuasive. (033-003)

## Critical Reception

Grimké was widely respected in his lifetime as an orator and reformer. His addresses were published and circulated in periodicals throughout the United States. His educational reform proposals, though not immediately adopted, influenced later curricular reforms in southern colleges.

In literary history, Grimké is a minor figure, remembered more for his family connections — particularly his sisters Sarah and Angelina Grimké, the pioneering abolitionists and feminists — than for his own writings. His essay on the Bible, however, has been noted by scholars of American biblical reception as an early and eloquent example of literary appreciation of the King James Version.

Grimké's life was cut short by cholera at age forty-eight, limiting his output and influence. He remains, nonetheless, a representative figure of the southern reform tradition: intellectually serious, morally earnest, and committed to the improvement of society through education and reasoned discourse.
