# John Bachman

## Biography

John Bachman (1790–1874) was an American Lutheran minister, naturalist, and social reformer. Born in Rhinebeck, New York, he moved to Charleston, South Carolina, in 1815 to serve as pastor of St. John's Lutheran Church.

He held this pastorate for nearly six decades while also pursuing an active career in natural science. Bachman became one of the most respected naturalists of the antebellum South and a central figure in Charleston's intellectual life.

Bachman studied theology in Philadelphia and was ordained before relocating southward. In Charleston he founded the Charleston Horticultural Society and corresponded with leading scientists. He advocated for the religious education of enslaved African Americans while remaining embedded in the culture of the antebellum South.

## Literary Career

Bachman's most significant literary contribution was his collaboration with [[john-j-audubon|John James Audubon]] on *The Quadrupeds of North America* (1845–1854). While Audubon provided the illustrations, Bachman wrote the majority of the scientific text, drawing on extensive field observations.

This work was a landmark in American mammalogy, providing detailed descriptions of North American mammals. Bachman also published theological works including *A Defence of Luther and the Reformation* (1853) and contributed to scientific journals. His prose combined empirical observation with a lucid, accessible style.

## Selections in This Anthology

Bachman appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" with his essay "The Opossum" (262), excerpted from *The Quadrupeds of North America*. The editor notes that Bachman "became distinguished as a medical professor and practitioner" (054-005).

The selection showcases his gift for combining scientific description with engaging narrative:

> We can imagine to ourselves the surprise with which the opossum was regarded by Europeans, when they first saw it. Scarcely anything was known of the marsupial animals, as New Holland had not as yet opened its unrivalled stores of singularities to astonish the world. Here was a strange animal, with the head and ears of the pig, sometimes hanging on the limb of a tree, and occasionally swinging like the monkey by the tail... The astonished traveller approaches this extraordinary compound of an animal, and touches it cautiously with a stick. Instantly it seems to be struck with some mortal disease: its eyes close, it falls to the ground, ceases to move, and appears to be dead.
> (054-006)

Bachman is listed in the Table of Contents as "John Bachman, 1790-1874" (001-118), among the writers of travel and adventure.

## Critical Reception

Bachman's scientific work received praise both in America and Europe for its thoroughness and readability. *The Quadrupeds of North America* was the most comprehensive treatment of its subject in its day.

Modern scholars have examined Bachman's complex legacy as a Southern clergyman-naturalist whose scientific pursuits coexisted with the institution of slavery, offering a window into antebellum intellectual culture.
