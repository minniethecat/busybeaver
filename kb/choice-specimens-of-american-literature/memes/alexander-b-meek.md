# Alexander B. Meek

Alexander Beaufort Meek (1814–1865) was an Alabama poet, historian, essayist, and public figure. He was among the few antebellum literary voices representing the Deep South, writing on historical subjects that ranged from the exiled French officers who settled in Alabama to the Creek Indian chief Weatherford.

His work, collected in volumes such as *Romantic Passages in Southwestern History*, blends historical research with literary sensibility, making him a distinctive representative of the antebellum Southern literary tradition.

## Biography

Meek was born on July 17, 1814, in Columbia, South Carolina. His family moved to Tuscaloosa, Alabama, when he was a child. He attended the University of Alabama, graduating in 1833, and studied law. He was admitted to the bar and practiced in Tuscaloosa.

Meek served in the Alabama legislature and was active in the state's political and cultural life. He edited several newspapers, including the *Southron* and the *Mobile Register*, and served as a judge. During his career, he combined his legal and political activities with literary pursuits.

## Literary Career

Meek's literary reputation rests primarily on his historical and descriptive writings about the South. His *Romantic Passages in Southwestern History* (1857) remains his best-known work, collecting essays on topics such as the French exiles who established the Vine and Olive Colony in Marengo County, Alabama, and the exploits of the Creek leader Weatherford.

He also published poetry, including *The Red Eagle* (1855), a verse narrative about the Creek War and the chief William Weatherford. His prose style combines the romantic sensibility of nineteenth-century historical writing with a journalist's attention to specific detail, as seen in his description of the colony of Napoleonic veterans at Marengo.

## Selections in the Anthology

The anthology *Choice Specimens of American Literature* includes two selections from Meek's work.

[031-001]The first, a meditation on republican responsibility, opens:

> The lessons of history and the fate of free states can never be sufficiently pondered by those upon whom so large and heavy a responsibility for the maintenance of rational human freedom rests.

[031-002]The second selection describes the Vine and Olive Colony, where French veterans of the Napoleonic Wars settled in Alabama:

> Upon the colony they bestowed the name of Marengo, which is still preserved in the county. Other relics of their nomenclature, drawn similarly from battles in which some of them had been distinguished, are to be found in the villages of Linden and Arcola... Who that would have looked upon Marshal Grouchy or General Lefebvre, as, dressed in their plain, rustic habiliments, — the straw hat, the homespun coat, the brogan shoes, — they drove the plough in the open field, or wielded the axe in the new-ground clearing, would, if unacquainted with their history, have dreamed that those farmer-looking men had sat in the councils of monarchs, and had headed mighty armies in the fields of the sternest strife the world has ever seen?

This passage crystallizes Meek's characteristic theme: the collision of world history with the American frontier. The image of Napoleon's generals — men who had commanded armies at Austerlitz and Waterloo — reduced to manual labor in the Alabama wilderness is both poignant and distinctively American.

[031-005]A third passage presents the young Creek chief Weatherford:

> The mind of the young Indian, though grasping with singular readiness the knowledge thus imparted, was subject to stronger tastes and propensities; and he indulged in all the wild pursuits and amusements of the youth of his nation. He became one of the most active, athletic, and swift-footed participants in their various games and dances, and was particularly expert and successful, as a hunter, in the use of the rifle and the bow.

## Critical Reception

The editor of the anthology, Benj. N. Martin, notes in a footnote that Meek was "one of the few writers of Alabama" and that his *Romantic Passages* "is a book of great interest" (031-003). This characterization reflects Meek's importance as a regional voice during a period when American literature was still overwhelmingly centered in the Northeast.
