# Education in American Literature

## Definition

Education, in the context of American literature, encompasses formal instruction, self-directed learning, and the broader cultivation of intellectual and moral character. American writers from the colonial period onward treated education as essential to both individual flourishing and republican citizenship.

## Historical Context

Colonial education reflected European traditions—particularly Dutch and English models—while adapting to American conditions. Cadwallader Colden observed how New Yorkers, "tinctured with a Dutch education," managed their affairs with distinctive practicality (003-011). The founding of colleges like Yale (where Jonathan Edwards studied) and the College of New Jersey (later Princeton) established institutional foundations for American intellectual life (004-006). By the early Republic, education was widely regarded as essential to the success of democratic self-government.

## Key Authors and Works

- **Cadwallader Colden** described the practical effects of colonial education on the character and manners of the people of New York (003-011).
- **Jonathan Edwards**, educated at Yale, embodied the synthesis of rigorous intellectual training with evangelical piety, becoming one of America's most formidable philosophical minds (004-006).
- **Benjamin Franklin** proposed plans for public education in Philadelphia and, through his own example as a self-taught polymath, demonstrated the American ideal of self-cultivation through reading and inquiry.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]] — The moral character that education was intended to cultivate in republican citizens
- [[independence]] — The intellectual self-reliance that American education sought to foster
- [[progress]] — The belief that education would drive material, moral, and political improvement

## Legacy

The American tradition of linking education to democratic citizenship—articulated by Franklin, Jefferson, and others—became a cornerstone of national identity. The 19th century saw the rise of the common school movement, the land-grant university system, and a vibrant tradition of self-education through lyceums, libraries, and literary societies, all of which drew on the educational ideals first articulated in the colonial and early national periods.
