# Timothy Flint

## Biography

Timothy Flint (1780–1840) was an American clergyman, missionary, and writer. Born in North Reading, Massachusetts, he graduated from Harvard College in 1800 and entered the Congregational ministry.

In 1815, Flint embarked on a missionary tour of the Mississippi Valley, traveling extensively through Ohio, Indiana, Missouri, and Louisiana. The experience transformed him into one of the earliest and most observant chroniclers of the American frontier. He settled for a time in Alexandria, Louisiana, and later in Cincinnati, Ohio, where he edited the *Western Monthly Review*. Flint brought a New Englander's literary sensibility to the raw material of the expanding West.

## Literary Career

Flint's most enduring work is *Recollections of the Last Ten Years* (1826), a vivid travelogue of his journeys through the Mississippi and Ohio Valleys. The book captures the frontier in transition — its boatmen, settlers, and the collision of wilderness with civilization. His writing combined the keen eye of a naturalist with the moral perspective of a clergyman, producing prose that was both descriptive and reflective.

Flint also wrote novels, including *Francis Berrian, or the Mexican Patriot* (1826) and *The Life and Adventures of Arthur Clenning* (1828), and a well-regarded biography of Daniel Boone (1833). His *Condensed Geography and History of the Western States* (1828) was among the first comprehensive surveys of the region.

## Selections

In the *Choice Specimens* anthology, Flint is represented by "The Western Boatman" (037-009), a celebrated sketch that depicts the seductive allure of river life along the Ohio. The passage captures the boatman's oscillation between "extremely indolent" stretches and moments of "hazardous beyond the Atlantic navigation" — a rhythm Flint knew from direct observation:

> The boats float by their dwellings on beautiful spring mornings, when the verdant forest, the mild and delicious temperature of the air, the delightful azure of the sky of this country, the fine bottom on the one hand, and the romantic bluff on the other, the broad, and smooth stream rolling calmly down through the forest, and floating the boat gently forward. (037-009)

Flint notes that "no wonder that to the young, who are reared in these remote regions, with that restless curiosity which is fostered by solitude and silence," the labor of agriculture "should become tasteless and irksome" in view of such scenes. The piece is a classic of early western American writing, blending landscape description with social observation.

## Critical Reception

Flint was among the first American writers to treat the Mississippi Valley not as exotic backdrop but as a lived-in cultural landscape. His *Recollections* influenced later regional writers and provided eastern readers with one of their earliest sustained portraits of frontier society. Literary historians regard him as a transitional figure between the travel-writing tradition of the eighteenth century and the regional realism that would emerge later in the nineteenth century.
