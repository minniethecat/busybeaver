# William Smith

## Biography

William Smith (1728–1793) was an American historian, jurist, and Anglican clergyman who served as Chief Justice of the Province of New York from 1780 to 1783. Born in New York City, the son of a prominent lawyer of the same name, Smith was educated at Yale College (graduating in 1745) and studied law under William Livingston. He was admitted to the bar in 1750 and rapidly established himself as one of the leading legal minds in the colony.

Smith's political career was shaped by the turbulence of the Revolutionary era. A moderate who sought reconciliation between the colonies and Great Britain, he ultimately sided with the Loyalist cause in 1778, serving on the Board of Associated Loyalists and later accepting appointment as Chief Justice of New York under British authority. Following the British evacuation, Smith removed to England and subsequently to Quebec, where he spent the final years of his life as Chief Justice of Lower Canada.

## Literary Career

Smith's principal literary achievement is *The History of the Province of New York, from the First Discovery to the Year 1732* (1757), published when he was only twenty-nine. The work was the first comprehensive history of the colony and drew on Smith's access to official records, family papers, and personal observation. It was republished in London and remained the standard authority on early New York for more than half a century. Smith wrote in an urbane, observant style, attentive to social customs, institutional arrangements, and the texture of daily life — qualities that distinguish his history from the drier chronicles of his contemporaries.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," "Manners of the Inhabitants," is taken from the *History of the Province of New York* and offers a vivid social portrait of colonial New York:

> New York is one of the most social places on the continent. The men collect themselves into weekly evening clubs. The ladies, in winter, are frequently entertained either at concerts of music or assemblies, and make a very good appearance. They are comely, and dress well, and scarce any of them have distorted shapes. Tinctured with a Dutch education, they manage their families with becoming parsimony, good providence, and singular neatness. (003-011)

Smith's catalogue of New York's virtues and vices is notable for its candor. He praises the women's domestic economy — "becoming parsimony, good providence, and singular neatness" — while lamenting their neglect of intellectual cultivation:

> There is nothing they so generally neglect as reading, and indeed all the arts for the improvement of the mind; in which, I confess, we have set them the example. They are modest, temperate, and charitable; naturally sprightly, sensible, and good-humored; and, by the helps of a more elevated education, would possess all the accomplishments desirable in the sex. (003-011)

Smith's criticism extends to the colony's educational and professional institutions. Of the schools he writes: "Our schools are in the lowest order: the instructors want instruction; and, through a long, shameful neglect of all the arts and sciences, our common speech is extremely corrupt." Of the practice of medicine, he observes: "Few physicians amongst us are eminent for their skill. Quacks abound like locusts in Egypt, and too many have recommended themselves to a full practice and profitable subsistence." He adds pointedly: "Any man, at his pleasure, sets up for physician, apothecary, and chirurgeon."

His observations on the clergy are measured but frank: "The clergy of this province are, in general, but indifferently supported, it is true they live easily, but few of them leave any thing to their children." He notes that compared to New England, "in matters of religion we are not so intelligent," but "both in this respect and good morals we certainly have the advantage of the Southern provinces." This comparative framework — New York positioned between the intellectual rigor of New England and the moral laxity of the South — is characteristic of Smith's self-conscious provincial perspective, at once proud and self-critical.

## Critical Reception

Smith's *History of the Province of New York* has been valued by modern historians for its documentary thoroughness and its sociological breadth. Unlike the more narrowly political and military chronicles of his contemporaries, Smith devoted sustained attention to the social institutions, professions, and domestic arrangements of colonial life. His Loyalist politics — which cost him his reputation in the immediate post-Revolutionary period — have been reassessed by scholars interested in the complexity of Revolutionary-era political allegiance.

Smith's subsequent career in Quebec, where he served as Chief Justice from 1786 to 1793, positioned him as a transitional figure in the British imperial legal system: an American-born jurist applying English common law in a French civil-law jurisdiction.
