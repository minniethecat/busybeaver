# John Pendleton Kennedy

John Pendleton Kennedy (1795–1870) was an American novelist, lawyer, and politician whose literary works helped establish the genre of Southern plantation fiction in the antebellum United States. A Baltimore native and lifelong Marylander, Kennedy served as Secretary of the Navy under President Millard Fillmore and was a prominent figure in both the literary and political life of the mid-Atlantic region. His best-known novel, [[swallow-barn|Swallow Barn]] (1832), offered a genial and picturesque portrait of Virginia plantation society, anticipating the [[plantation-tradition|plantation tradition]] in [[southern-literature|Southern literature]] that would later be transformed by writers such as [[thomas-nelson-page|Thomas Nelson Page]] and challenged by [[abolitionist-literature|abolitionist]] novelists including [[harriet-beecher-stowe|Harriet Beecher Stowe]].

## Biography

Kennedy was born in Baltimore, Maryland, on October 25, 1795, the son of a prosperous merchant of Irish descent. He attended Baltimore College and graduated in 1812, after which he studied law and was admitted to the bar in 1816. Kennedy established a successful legal practice in Baltimore, but his interests soon drew him toward both literature and public service. He was elected to the Maryland House of Delegates in 1821 and later served in the United States House of Representatives from 1838 to 1845, where he aligned himself with the Whig Party and advocated for protective tariffs and internal improvements.

In 1852, President Millard Fillmore appointed Kennedy as Secretary of the Navy, a position he held until the end of the Fillmore administration in 1853. During his brief tenure, Kennedy supported the modernization of the naval fleet and promoted [[matthew-f-maury|Matthew Fontaine Maury]]'s oceanographic research. As the sectional crisis deepened in the late 1850s, Kennedy remained a staunch Unionist, opposing secession while also advocating compromise. He supported the Union cause during the Civil War and lived to see the conflict's conclusion, dying at Newport, Rhode Island, on August 18, 1870.

## Literary Career

Kennedy's literary career began in the 1820s with contributions to Baltimore periodicals and the publication of a satirical magazine, *The Red Book* (1818–1819), written in collaboration with his friend Peter Hoffman Cruse. His first major novel, *Swallow Barn; or, A Sojourn in the Old Dominion* (1832), was originally conceived as a collaborative sketchbook and was published under the pseudonym Mark Littleton. The novel presents a series of sketches of life on a Virginia plantation, combining gentle satire of Southern manners with affectionate descriptions of the landscape and inhabitants. Unlike the Gothic or historical romances popular at the time, *Swallow Barn* adopted a realistic and humorous tone that distinguished Kennedy's approach to fiction.

Kennedy followed *Swallow Barn* with *Horse-Shoe Robinson* (1835), a historical novel set during the American Revolution in the Carolinas, and *Rob of the Bowl* (1838), a tale of colonial Maryland. Both novels were well received, and *Horse-Shoe Robinson* in particular was praised for its vivid depiction of backcountry characters and Revolutionary War settings. Kennedy also wrote a substantial biography, *Memoirs of the Life of William Wirt* (1849), a two-volume work on the [[william-wirt|Virginia jurist and author]] who had served as Attorney General of the United States.

Kennedy's literary philosophy emphasized a conception of poetry as a natural, spontaneous expression rather than a product of formal artifice. His editorial and critical writings consistently championed an American literary independence from European models, though his own fiction remained deeply influenced by British novelists such as Sir Walter Scott and the eighteenth-century essay tradition.

## Selections in the Anthology

The *Choice Specimens of American Literature* anthology includes excerpts from Kennedy's essay on poetry and from *Swallow Barn*, demonstrating both his critical principles and his narrative art. In his reflections on poetry (059-001), Kennedy articulates a Romantic conception of literary expression as organic and untutored:

> "Poetry is the naked expression of power and eloquence; but, for many hundred years, poetry has been confounded with false music, measure, and cadence, the soul with the body, the thought with the language, the manner of speaking with the mode of thinking.... What I call poetry, has nothing to do with art or learning. It is a natural music, the music of woods and waters, not that of the orchestra.... Poetry is a religion, as well as a music. Nay, it is eloquence. It is whatever affects, touches, or disturbs the animal or moral sense of man." (059-001)

This passage reflects Kennedy's alignment with Romantic theories of poetic inspiration and his resistance to neoclassical formalism — a perspective shared by many of his American contemporaries in the decades following the [[american-revolution|Revolution]].

The selection from *Swallow Barn* opens with a celebrated description of the titular plantation house (059-002–059-004):

> "Swallow Barn is an aristocratical old edifice, which sits, like a brooding hen, on the southern bank of the James River. It looks down upon a shady pocket, or nook, formed by an indentation of the shore, from a gentle acclivity, thinly sprinkled with oaks, whose magnificent branches afford habitation to sundry friendly colonies of squirrels and woodpeckers." (059-002)

The sketch continues with a gently comic depiction of the rambling architecture of the Hazard family mansion, whose additions "seem to have been built in defiance of all laws of congruity, just as convenience required" (059-003), and concludes with a description of the great barn that leans heavily over the landscape like a drowsy sentinel (059-004). This descriptive mode — precise, affectionate, and lightly humorous — became Kennedy's signature and influenced later writers of local-color fiction.

A political dialogue from the same novel (059-005–059-007) reveals Kennedy's ear for regional dialect and his ambivalent treatment of [[states-rights|states' rights]] politics. The character's complaint — "What's the use of states, if they are all to be cut up with canals, and railroads, and tariffs?" (059-005) — captures the provincial resistance to federal expansion that characterized Virginia politics in the Jacksonian era.

Finally, Kennedy's biographical portrait of William Wirt (059-008) displays his skill as a prose portraitist. He describes Wirt's transformation from a florid young orator into "one of the most philosophic and accomplished lawyers of his time," noting that "in logical precision of mind, clearness of statement, full investigation of complicated points, and close comparison of precedents, he had no superior at the bar of the Supreme Court." The passage exemplifies Kennedy's mature prose style: balanced, judicious, and enriched by firsthand observation.

## Critical Reception

Kennedy's contemporary reputation rested on both his literary and political achievements. *Swallow Barn* was praised by [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], who admired Kennedy's descriptive powers and commended the novel's fidelity to Southern life. [[washington-irving|Washington Irving]], to whom Kennedy was often compared, corresponded warmly with the Maryland author and offered encouragement. In the broader literary marketplace, Kennedy's fiction enjoyed a wide readership, though it never achieved the international fame of Irving's or [[james-fenimore-cooper|Cooper]]'s works.

Twentieth-century criticism has taken a more ambivalent view of Kennedy's legacy. While scholars recognize his importance in the development of Southern fiction and the local-color tradition, the plantation pastoral of *Swallow Barn* has been read as an apologetic for the slave system, its genial tone masking the realities of chattel slavery. More recent scholarship, however, has noted Kennedy's personal evolution on the slavery question — he eventually supported emancipation and opposed the Confederacy — and has sought to understand his fiction within the complex political currents of antebellum Maryland, a border state where Unionist sentiment and slaveholding coexisted uneasily.
