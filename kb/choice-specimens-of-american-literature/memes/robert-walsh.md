# Robert Walsh

Robert Walsh (1784–1859) was an American journalist, essayist, editor, and diplomat whose career bridged the early Republic and the antebellum period.

He founded and edited the *American Review of History and Politics*, the first American quarterly devoted to serious political and literary analysis. As a writer, Walsh advocated for American intellectual independence from European models and for the development of a distinct national literature.

From 1844 to 1851 he served as United States consul general in Paris, appointed by President [[john-tyler|John Tyler]]. His book *Didactics, Social, Literary, and Agricultural* (1836) collected his essays on social reform, literary culture, and practical subjects.

## Biography

Walsh was born on August 30, 1784, in Baltimore, Maryland, to an Irish immigrant father. He attended Georgetown College and later studied law, being admitted to the bar, though he never practiced extensively.

His true vocation was letters. In 1811 he launched the *American Review of History and Politics*, which ran until 1817 and established him as a leading man of letters. He later edited the *National Gazette and Literary Register* in Philadelphia and contributed to numerous periodicals.

In 1820 Walsh traveled to Europe, meeting leading literary figures and gathering material for his work. He married Anna Maria Moylan in Philadelphia. His diplomatic service in Paris from 1844 to 1851 capped a career devoted to transatlantic cultural exchange. Walsh died on February 9, 1859, in Philadelphia.

## Literary Career

Walsh's most significant contribution to American letters was his journalism and editorial work. The *American Review* published original essays on politics, history, and literature, providing a platform for American writers at a time when most periodicals relied heavily on reprinted British material.

His major book publications include *An Appeal from the Judgments of Great Britain Respecting the United States of America* (1819), a defense of American institutions against British criticism, and *Didactics, Social, Literary, and Agricultural* (1836), a two-volume collection of essays covering topics from criminal justice reform to agricultural improvement.

Walsh's prose style combined neoclassical clarity with moral earnestness. His essay "False Sympathy with Criminals" exemplifies this approach: tightly reasoned, socially engaged, and rhetorically forceful.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Walsh: "False Sympathy with Criminals," taken from his *Didactics*. The essay argues against sentimental leniency toward criminals, maintaining that genuine humanity requires firm moral judgment.

> [033-001] Our agriculture is greatly defective. Whatever the impulse to guilt, some suppression or aberration of the reason may ever be alleged and admitted. In this mode, however, sentimentalists might argue or whine away the whole body of crimes and punishments.

> [033-002] It is the duty of every true friend of humanity and order, to protest against perverted sensibilities or sophistical refinements, which find warrant or apology for depraved appetites, — for the worst distemperature of the mind, and the most fatal catastrophes, — in natural propension, and unrestrained feeling. Spurious sympathy is a more prolific evil than sanguinary rigor, useless and pernicious as the latter is, in our humble opinion.

The essay represents the reformist impulse in antebellum American thought, combining moral philosophy with practical social criticism. (033-001)

## Critical Reception

Walsh was respected in his lifetime as a judicious critic and a capable editor. Washington Irving praised his editorial work, and he maintained friendships with many leading American and European intellectuals.

His *Appeal from the Judgments of Great Britain* was well received on both sides of the Atlantic as a thoughtful rather than polemical defense of American institutions. His *Didactics* was less widely read but valued by educators and reformers.

Modern scholarship has given Walsh limited attention, though his role in the development of American periodical literature and his advocacy for cultural nationalism have been recognized by historians of the early Republic. He occupies a transitional position between the Federalist literary culture of the 1790s and the more democratic, expansive literary nationalism of the Jacksonian era.
