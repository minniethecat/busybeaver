# Justice in American Literature

## Definition
Justice, in American literary tradition, encompasses divine justice as God's righteous judgment, legal justice as the impartial administration of law, and social justice as the fair distribution of rights and opportunities. These dimensions are frequently intertwined in the moral and political literature of early America.

## Historical Context
Puritan writers like John Winthrop grounded justice in divine law, arguing that civil magistrates administer God's justice in the earthly commonwealth (004-001). Revolutionary-era writers reframed justice in constitutional terms—as the protection of rights and the restraint of arbitrary power. Timothy Dwight described national happiness as "consecrated to truth, to virtue, and to God" through the administration of justice (005-012).

## Key Authors and Works
- **John Winthrop** defined civil justice as the application of moral law to community life, distinguishing it from mere legalism (004-001).
- **Timothy Dwight** connected justice to national character, arguing that a just nation must be a virtuous nation (005-012, 007-004).
- **Patrick Henry** and other Revolutionary orators appealed to justice as the standard by which British rule was judged and found wanting (014-001).

## Legacy
The American literary engagement with justice evolved from Puritan theocracy through Revolutionary constitutionalism to the 19th-century movements for abolition, women's rights, and labor reform—each appealing to the ideal of justice as the measure of national character.
