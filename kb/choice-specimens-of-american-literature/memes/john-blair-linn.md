# John Blair Linn

## Biography

John Blair Linn (1777–1804) was an American poet and Presbyterian clergyman. Born in Shippensburg, Pennsylvania, he was the half-brother of the novelist Charles Brockden Brown, with whom he shared a close literary and intellectual bond.

Linn graduated from Columbia College in 1795 and subsequently studied theology, entering the Presbyterian ministry. He served as a pastor in Philadelphia, where he became part of the city's vibrant literary circle. His life was cut short by tuberculosis at the age of twenty-seven.

## Literary Career

Linn's major work was *The Powers of Genius*, an epic-didactic poem published in 1801. Written in heroic couplets after the manner of Pope and Akenside, the poem explored the nature of poetic creativity, the relationship between genius and morality, and the progress of the arts through history.

Linn also contributed to literary periodicals and published sermons. His poetry reflected neoclassical tastes while also showing interest in the American landscape. His work stands as a representative example of the literary aspirations of the early American Republic.

## Selections in This Anthology

Linn is represented in "Choice Specimens" by an excerpt from *The Powers of Genius*, titled "Wretchedness of Savage Life" (323). The passage contrasts the harshness of primitive existence with the comforts of civilization:

> The human fabric early from its birth,
> Feels some fond influence from its parent earth;
> In different regions different forms we trace,
> Here dwells a feeble, there an iron race;
> Here genius lives, and wakeful fancies play,
> Here noiseless stupor sleeps its life away.
> (065-008)

Linn is listed in the Table of Contents as "John Blair Linn, 1777-1804" (001-177), grouped among the poets of the early Republic.

## Critical Reception

Contemporaries praised Linn's precocity and the moral seriousness of his verse. His half-brother Charles Brockden Brown wrote admiringly of him in *The Literary Magazine*. Later literary historians have viewed Linn as a minor but representative figure of the neoclassical tradition in American poetry, his early death having limited his output.
