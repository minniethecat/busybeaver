# John Armstrong

## Biography

John Armstrong (1759–1843) was an American soldier and writer born in Carlisle, Pennsylvania. He served as an officer in the Continental Army during the Revolutionary War and later as a brigadier general in the War of 1812. Between the wars, Armstrong served briefly in the United States Senate from New York and as Minister to France. His military and diplomatic career gave him firsthand experience of the events he would later chronicle as a historian.

## Literary Career

Armstrong's principal literary contribution was his historical and biographical writing on military affairs. He authored *Notices of the War of 1812* and contributed to the early nineteenth-century discourse on the young republic's military history. His prose combined the precision of a soldier with the perspective of one who had witnessed the transformation of the American colonies into an independent nation. While not a prolific author by volume, Armstrong's works preserve important firsthand accounts of the Revolution's military campaigns and the subsequent war with Britain.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes passages from Armstrong's account of the Conquest of Canada during the French and Indian War:

- "The Conquest of Canada" (022-008–022-010) — Armstrong describes the campaign that ended French power in North America:
  > "That colossal power which France had been long erecting in America, with vast labor and expense; which had been the motive for one of the most extensive and desolating wars of modern times, was thus entirely overthrown." (022-009)
  > "The joy diffused throughout the British dominions by this splendid conquest, was mingled with a proud sense of superiority, which did not estimate with exact justice the relative means employed by the belligerents." (022-010)

He notes the particular significance for the American colonies: "In no part of those dominions was this joy felt in a higher degree, or with more reason, than in America."

## Critical Reception

Armstrong's historical writings were valued by contemporaries for their firsthand authority on military matters. His *Notices of the War of 1812* generated controversy for its candid assessments of American military leadership, but it has since been recognized as an important primary source. Armstrong remains a useful source for scholars of the early American military establishment.
