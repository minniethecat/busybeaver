# Edward C. Pinkney

Edward Coote Pinkney (1802–1828) was an American Romantic poet and naval officer whose small body of work, published in a single volume in 1825, earned him a posthumous reputation as one of the purest lyric voices of his generation. The son of the distinguished statesman and diplomat [[william-pinkney|William Pinkney]], Edward Pinkney served in the United States Navy and wrote poetry that combined the formal polish of the English Cavalier tradition with a distinctly American sensibility. His best-known poem, "A Health," is a toast to an idealized woman rendered in lines of such musical grace that [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]] later praised it as one of the finest lyrics in the language. Like his near-contemporary [[joseph-r-drake|Joseph Rodman Drake]], Pinkney died young — he was twenty-five — leaving behind the tantalizing suggestion of a major talent cut short.

## Biography

Pinkney was born in London on October 1, 1802, while his father, William Pinkney, was serving as American minister to the Court of St. James's. The elder Pinkney was one of the most celebrated lawyers and diplomats of the early Republic — a United States Attorney General, a senator from Maryland, and a minister to Russia and Great Britain — and his son grew up in an atmosphere of cosmopolitan privilege and high expectation. The family returned to Maryland when Edward was a child, and he was educated in Baltimore.

At the age of fourteen, Pinkney entered the United States Navy as a midshipman. He served in the Mediterranean and the West Indies, distinguishing himself in action against Caribbean pirates, and rose to the rank of lieutenant. The naval life, however, did not suit his literary temperament, and he resigned his commission in 1824 to pursue the practice of law in Maryland. In 1825 he published a single volume, *Poems*, in Baltimore, and was admitted to the bar. He married in the same year, but his health soon failed. Pinkney died in Baltimore on April 11, 1828, at the age of twenty-five, survived by his wife and a young daughter.

## Literary Career

Pinkney's *Poems* (1825) is a slender volume — fewer than thirty lyrics, songs, and occasional pieces — but its craftsmanship is of an unusually high order for an American poet of the 1820s. Pinkney's models were the English Cavalier poets of the seventeenth century — [[robert-herrick|Herrick]], [[richard-lovelace|Lovelace]], [[sir-john-suckling|Suckling]] — whose combination of formal elegance, erotic gallantry, and carpe diem melancholy he absorbed and reproduced with remarkable fidelity. His lines move with an ease and musicality that few American poets had achieved before him, and his best work achieves the quality that Poe, in his critical essays, identified as essential to lyric poetry: a unified effect in which sound, sense, and sentiment are indistinguishable.

"A Health," Pinkney's most celebrated poem, exemplifies his art at its purest. The poem takes the form of a toast — a ritual of conviviality — and transforms it into a meditation on ideal beauty and the consolations of memory. Other notable lyrics in the volume include "Serenade" ("Look out upon the stars, my love") and several songs that reflect his naval experience, but it is "A Health" that has secured his place in the American poetic tradition.

Pinkney's poetry shares with [[joseph-r-drake|Joseph Rodman Drake]]'s a commitment to the idea that American verse could attain the formal sophistication of its English models without sacrificing emotional authenticity. Both poets belonged to a generation that felt the influence of [[lord-byron|Byron]] and the English Romantics intensely, and both found in the lyric — brief, musical, emotionally concentrated — the form best suited to their talents. Their early deaths, occurring within eight years of each other, became a melancholy topos in American literary history, a double emblem of promise unfulfilled.

## Selection in the Anthology

The *Choice Specimens of American Literature* anthology presents Pinkney through his signature poem, "A Health" (069-010–069-011), which follows the author heading (069-009). The complete text is reproduced below:

> "I fill this cup to one made up of loveliness alone; / A woman, of her gentle sex the seeming paragon, / To whom the better elements and kindly stars have given / A form so fair, that, like the air, 'tis less of earth than heaven." (069-010)

The opening quatrain establishes the poem's central conceit — the raised glass as a gesture of homage to beauty that transcends the earthly — in lines whose rhythmic poise mirrors the ceremonial grace of the toast itself. Pinkney's diction owes something to the Shakespearean and Cavalier traditions ("paragon," "kindly stars"), but the voice is his own: direct, ardent, and unembarrassed by its own gallantry.

> "Her every tone is music's own, like those of morning birds; / And something more than melody dwells ever in her words. / The coinage of her heart are they, and from her lips each flows, / As one may see the burdened bee forth issue from the rose." (069-010)

The image of the "burdened bee" emerging from the rose is among the most admired similes in early American poetry. Its effectiveness derives not from novelty — the bee-and-flower figure is ancient — but from the precision with which Pinkney deploys it: the "burdened" bee carrying pollen as the woman's words carry "the coinage of her heart." The comparison is exact, graceful, and free of the straining after effect that mars much occasional verse of the period.

> "Of her bright face, one glance will trace a picture on the brain, / And of her voice, in echoing hearts a sound must long remain; / But memory such as mine of her, so very much, endears / When death is nigh, my latest sigh will not be life's, but hers." (069-010)

The final couplet (069-011) returns the poem to its opening gesture, closing the circle:

> "I fill this cup to one made up of loveliness alone, / A woman, of her gentle sex, the seeming paragon. / Her health! and would on earth there stood some more of such a frame, / That life might be all poetry, and weariness a name." (069-010–069-011)

The concluding wish — "that life might be all poetry, and weariness a name" — is Pinkney's most quoted line, and it distills the Romantic aspiration that animates his entire small oeuvre: the longing for a world in which beauty, and the art that celebrates it, might redeem the ordinary fatigue of living. That this wish was uttered by a poet who would be dead within three years of its publication lends it a poignancy that Pinkney could not have intended but that posterity has found impossible to ignore.

## Critical Reception

Pinkney's reputation rests substantially on the advocacy of [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], who, in his essay "The Poetic Principle" (1850), cited "A Health" as an exemplar of the lyric at its purest and declared that Pinkney "would have been better appreciated had he been born in England." Poe's praise was double-edged — it implied that American readers were too crude to recognize Pinkney's refinement — but it ensured that the poet's name would survive the obscurity into which his early death and small output might otherwise have consigned it.

Nineteenth-century anthologists, including [[rufus-wilmot-griswold|Rufus Wilmot Griswold]], regularly included Pinkney among the representative American poets of the early Romantic period. His work was admired for its metrical polish at a time when much American verse was still technically awkward, and "A Health" became a favorite of the parlor-recitation repertoire. In the twentieth century, Pinkney's reputation faded as the Cavalier lyric fell out of fashion, but recent scholarship on the early American Romantic lyric has begun to recover his work as evidence of the transatlantic currents that shaped American poetry before the emergence of a distinctly national tradition.
