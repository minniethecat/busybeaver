# John Trumbull

## Biography

John Trumbull (1750–1831) was an American satirical poet of the Revolutionary era and one of the Hartford Wits — the circle of Connecticut writers that included Joel Barlow, Timothy Dwight, and David Humphreys. Born in Watertown, Connecticut, Trumbull was a child prodigy: he passed the entrance examination for Yale College at the age of seven, though he did not actually enroll until he was thirteen. He graduated from Yale in 1767 and remained there as a tutor from 1770 to 1773, during which time he formed his literary friendships with Dwight and Barlow.

Trumbull studied law under John Adams in Boston, an experience that gave him direct exposure to the political ferment of the pre-Revolutionary years. He was admitted to the bar in 1774 and practiced law in New Haven and later in Hartford. Though he never held high political office, Trumbull served in the Connecticut legislature and later as a judge of the Superior Court of Connecticut from 1801 to 1819. His judicial career was distinguished by his interest in legal reform and his opposition to capital punishment.

Trumbull's later years were marked by declining literary output and increasing absorption in his legal work. He died in Detroit, Michigan, in 1831, at the home of a married daughter, having outlived all the other major Hartford Wits. His reputation as a poet, which had been formidable in the early republic, had by then been eclipsed by the younger generation of American writers.

## Literary Career

Trumbull's first major poem, *The Progress of Dulness* (1772–1773), was a three-part satire on contemporary education: the folly of forcing young men into the clergy, the pretensions of the fashionable education of women, and the empty conventions of collegiate learning. The poem established Trumbull's characteristic style — heroic couplets in the manner of Pope, applied to distinctly American subjects — and made his literary reputation.

His masterpiece, however, was *McFingal* (completed 1782), a mock-epic in four cantos modeled on Samuel Butler's *Hudibras*. The poem satirizes the Loyalists (American colonists who remained loyal to the British Crown) through the figure of Squire McFingal, a blustering Tory magistrate who holds forth in a town meeting only to be tarred and feathered by enraged patriots. The selection in *Choice Specimens* depicts McFingal's comically ineffectual martial contributions — fortifying Boston, building breastworks "to keep the patriots at a distance" — and deploys the poem's signature ironic style:

> Though this, not all his time was lost on,
> He fortified the town of Boston,
> Built breastworks that might lend assistance
> To keep the patriots at a distance;
> For, howsoe'er the rogues might scoff,
> He liked them best the farthest off. (064-017)

The poem's humor is sustained by Trumbull's deft handling of the mock-heroic register: the comparison of British General Gage to a fish that "was once" Peter's "chief cashier," and the observation that "Heaven can rain you at pleasure, / By Gage, as soon as by a Caesar." This technique — deflating British authority by ironically comparing it to biblical plagues — was a staple of Revolutionary-era propaganda, and Trumbull wielded it with exceptional skill.

*McFingal* was enormously popular in its time. The first two cantos, published in 1775, sold rapidly; the complete four-canto edition of 1782 went through over thirty editions in the United States and was widely pirated in Britain — an unusual distinction for an American poem. It was one of the most frequently quoted poems of the Revolutionary era, and its couplets entered the common currency of political discourse. Modern critics have noted that *McFingal*, though derivative in form, is one of the few American poems of its era that still yields pleasure as comic verse.

Trumbull also collaborated with Barlow, Dwight, and Humphreys on *The Anarchiad* (1786–1787), a series of satirical essays in verse published in the *New Haven Gazette*. The poem, which purported to be a found manuscript prophesying the collapse of republican government in America, was an intervention in contemporary debates over paper money and debtor relief, and it exemplified the Hartford Wits' Federalist politics and their belief in the civic function of satire.

## Critical Reception

Trumbull was widely regarded in his lifetime as the leading American satirist and one of the most accomplished poets of the early republic. His reputation was closely tied to Revolutionary politics, however, and as the passions of that era faded, so — gradually — did his readership. By the mid-nineteenth century, *McFingal* was more often cited by literary historians than read by the general public.

Modern scholarship has treated Trumbull with respect rather than enthusiasm: his historical importance as a pioneer of American literary satire is acknowledged, and *McFingal* is regularly studied in courses on Revolutionary-era literature. His skillful use of the Hudibrastic couplet and his ability to adapt English neoclassical forms to American political subjects remain his principal contributions to the American literary tradition.
