Charles Brockden Brown (1771–1810) was the first American to pursue a professional career as a novelist, and a foundational figure in the American Gothic tradition. Born in Philadelphia to a Quaker family, Brown abandoned a legal apprenticeship to devote himself entirely to literature. Influenced by the British Gothic novelist [[william-godwin]] and the radical philosophy of the French Enlightenment, Brown adapted the conventions of the European Gothic to distinctly American settings—replacing crumbling castles with the forests of Pennsylvania and the fever-ridden streets of Philadelphia. His novel *Wieland* (1798) remains the first major American Gothic novel, while *Ormond* (1799) and *Edgar Huntly* (1799) further established his reputation as an innovator in psychological horror.

## Biography

Charles Brockden Brown was born on January 17, 1771, in Philadelphia, the fifth of six children in a devout Quaker merchant family. His father, Elijah Brown, was a conveyancer and land speculator who had been imprisoned during the Revolution for his pacifist principles. Frail in health from childhood, Brown was a voracious reader who exhausted the resources of the Philadelphia Library Company before his teens. At sixteen, he entered the law office of Alexander Wilcocks, where he formed friendships with several young literary aspirants, including [[joseph-dennie]] and Elihu Hubbard Smith. Brown found legal practice distasteful, and by 1796 he had fully abandoned the profession for a literary life in New York, becoming part of the Friendly Club circle. His brief but intense literary career—essentially 1798 to 1801—produced six novels. After 1801, Brown returned to Philadelphia, married Elizabeth Linn (sister of the poet [[john-blair-linn]]), and turned to periodical editing and political pamphleteering. He died of tuberculosis on February 22, 1810, at the age of thirty-nine.

## Literary Career

Brown's first published work, the essay series *The Rhapsodist* (1789), appeared in the *Columbian Magazine* and showed the influence of [[jean-jacques-rousseau]]'s confessional style. But it was the novel that became his primary form. In a remarkable burst of productivity between 1798 and 1801, Brown wrote six novels: *Wieland, or The Transformation* (1798), *Ormond, or The Secret Witness* (1799), *Arthur Mervyn, or Memoirs of the Year 1793* (1799–1800), *Edgar Huntly, or Memoirs of a Sleep-Walker* (1799), *Clara Howard* (1801), and *Jane Talbot* (1801). These works are notable for their psychological penetration, their substitution of American landscapes for European Gothic settings, and their preoccupation with the limits of rational knowledge. Brown also edited *The Monthly Magazine* (1799–1800), *The Literary Magazine* (1803–1807), and wrote extensively on political topics, including a celebrated pamphlet on the Louisiana Purchase. Though his novels fell out of print after his death, his experiments with unreliable narration, doppelgängers, and the psychology of terror influenced [[edgar-allan-poe]], [[nathaniel-hawthorne]], and later American writers.

## Selections

### 056-011 — The Yellow Fever in Philadelphia (from *Ormond*)

In this passage from *Ormond* (1799), Brown's narrator Constantia Dudley ventures into plague-stricken Philadelphia during the yellow fever epidemic of 1793—an event Brown himself had witnessed. The selection, chosen for this anthology by the editor, illustrates Brown's power to render psychological terror through physical description:

[056-011]As she approached the house to which she was going, her reluctance to proceed increased. Frequently she paused to recollect the motives that had prescribed this task, and to re-enforce her purposes. At length she arrived at the house. Now, for the first time, her attention was excited by the silence and desolation that surrounded her. This evidence of fear and of danger struck upon her heart. All appeared to have fled from the presence of this unseen and terrible foe. The temerity of adventuring thus into the jaws of the pest, now appeared to her in glaring colors.

This passage captures the distinctive quality of Brown's fictional method: the external setting serves as a projection of internal dread. The "silence and desolation" of the emptied city mirrors Constantia's psychological isolation, while the phrase "the jaws of the pest" personifies the epidemic as a predatory creature—a Gothic transformation of an all-too-real Philadelphia disaster.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Brown enjoyed a considerable transatlantic reputation. *Wieland* was reviewed favorably in Britain, and [[william-godwin]] acknowledged Brown's influence on his own novel *Mandeville* (1817). [[john-keats]] is said to have read Brown, and [[percy-bysshe-shelley]] admired him. In America, however, Brown's reputation declined sharply after his death. [[washington-irving]] never mentioned him; [[james-fenimore-cooper]] drew on *Edgar Huntly* but rarely acknowledged the debt. The revival of Brown's reputation began in the twentieth century with studies by scholars of the American Gothic, who recognized him as the originator of a dark counter-tradition in American fiction—a line running from Brown through [[edgar-allan-poe]] and [[nathaniel-hawthorne]] to [[herman-melville]] and beyond. Contemporary criticism values Brown's formal experimentation, his treatment of the limits of Enlightenment reason, and his prescient engagement with themes of epidemic, political conspiracy, and psychological dissolution.
