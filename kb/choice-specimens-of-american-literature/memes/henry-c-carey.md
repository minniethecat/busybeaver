Henry C. Carey (1793–1879) was an American economist and publisher who became the leading advocate of the American School of economics, challenging the free-trade orthodoxy of British classical economics (033-008). As the son of Irish immigrant and publisher Mathew Carey, he inherited both a publishing business and a commitment to national economic development.

## Biography

Henry Charles Carey was born in Philadelphia in 1793, the son of Mathew Carey, a prominent publisher and economist. At age twenty-eight he succeeded his father as head of the publishing firm Carey & Lea, then the largest publishing house in the United States. He retired from business at forty-two to devote himself entirely to writing on political economy and sociology. Carey's work was influenced by his observations of American economic conditions and his belief that the policies of Adam Smith and David Ricardo did not fit the circumstances of a developing nation like the United States.

## Literary Career

Carey published his first major economic work, *Essay on the Rate of Wages*, in 1835, followed by his three-volume *Principles of Political Economy* (1837–1840). His most influential book, *The Harmony of Interests: Agricultural, Manufacturing, and Commercial* (1851), argued that protectionist policies fostered balanced national development by keeping manufacturing and agriculture in productive proximity. *The Past, the Present, and the Future* (1848) and *Principles of Social Science* (1858–1859) further developed his system. Carey's writing is direct and polemical, aimed at practical men rather than academic theorists.

## Selections

The anthology includes Carey's prescient argument for scientific agriculture as the foundation of national prosperity, drawn from his economic writings.

### Agriculture as a Science

> [033-008] That agriculture may become a science, it is indispensable that man always repay to the great bank from which he has drawn his food, the debt he thereby has contracted. The earth, as has been already said, gives nothing, but is ready to lend everything; and when the debts are punctually repaid, each successive loan is made on a larger scale; but when the debtor fails in punctuality, his credit declines, and the loans are gradually diminished, until at length he is turned out from house and home.

> [033-008] No truth in the whole range of science is more readily susceptible of proof than that the community which limits itself to the exportation of raw produce must end by the exportation of men, and those men the slaves of nature, even when not actually bought and sold by their fellow men.

> [033-009] With the growth of commerce, the necessity for moving commodities back, and forth steadily declines, with constant improvement in the machinery of transportation, and diminution in the risk of losses of the kind that are covered by insurance against dangers of the sea, or those of fire. The treasures of the earth then become developed, and stone and iron take the place of wood in all constructions, while the exchanges between the miner of coal and of iron — of the man who quarries the granite, and him who raises the food — rapidly increase in quantity, and diminish the necessity for resorting to the distant market.

## Critical Reception

Carey's ideas were highly influential in the United States and in continental Europe, particularly in Germany, where his work influenced the Historical School of economics. His theory of the harmony of interests offered an optimistic alternative to the "dismal science" of Malthus and Ricardo. Though eclipsed by the marginalist revolution in the late nineteenth century, Carey's emphasis on the interdependence of agriculture and manufacturing anticipated later work in development economics and economic geography.
