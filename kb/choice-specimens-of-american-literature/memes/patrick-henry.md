# Patrick Henry

Patrick Henry (1736–1799) was an American revolutionary orator and Founding Father.

He is best known for his "[[give-me-liberty-speech|Give me liberty, or give me death!]]" speech to the Virginia Convention in 1775. A defining voice of the [[independence|American independence]] movement, Henry fused classical rhetoric with evangelical conviction.

## Biography

Patrick Henry was born on May 29, 1736, in Hanover County, Virginia.

The son of a Scottish immigrant father and a mother from the Virginia gentry, Henry received little formal education. He was tutored by his father in Latin, Greek, and mathematics. After failing as a storekeeper and farmer, he turned to the law and was admitted to the Virginia bar in 1760.

Henry first gained public prominence in 1763 with the **Parson's Cause** case. He successfully argued against the Crown's authority to overturn a Virginia statute. His eloquence transformed a local legal dispute into a challenge to British sovereignty.

Elected to the Virginia House of Burgesses in 1765, Henry introduced the Virginia Resolves against the Stamp Act. He declared that only the colonial assembly had the right to tax Virginians. His fiery speech earned him the charge of treason — to which he reportedly replied, "If this be treason, make the most of it."

Henry served as the first Governor of independent Virginia (1776–1779, 1784–1786). He was a leading Anti-Federalist who opposed ratification of the U.S. Constitution without a Bill of Rights.

## Oratorical Style and Literary Significance

Patrick Henry was not a writer by profession. His reputation rests entirely on his oratory, transcribed by listeners including the jurist St. George Tucker and biographer William Wirt.

Henry's oratory drew on three traditions:

| Tradition | Source | Example in Henry's Work |
|-----------|--------|-------------------------|
| Classical rhetoric | Cicero, Demosthenes | Rhetorical questions, parallel structure, anaphora |
| Evangelical preaching | Great Awakening revivalism | Emotional crescendo, biblical allusion, direct address |
| English Whig ideology | Locke, Sidney, Commonwealthmen | Natural rights language, social contract theory |

His most famous speech was delivered on March 23, 1775, at St. John's Church in Richmond, Virginia. It responded to the conciliatory faction in the Convention who still hoped for reconciliation with Britain.

Henry's peroration — "Give me liberty, or give me death!" — became one of the most quoted phrases in American political history. It exemplifies the fusion of classical oratorical technique with revolutionary conviction.

>
> — From "Speech in the Convention of Virginia," March 23, 1775 (selection 58)

>
> — From "Life of Patrick Henry" by William Wirt (selection 175)

These selections represent the editor's judgment that Henry's contribution lies in the intersection of political argument and rhetorical art.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

Henry's reputation in American letters has undergone a complex evolution. The early nineteenth century celebrated him as the **Cicero of America**. William Wirt's *Sketches of the Life and Character of Patrick Henry* (1817) cemented the legend.

Scholars now recognize that many speech texts Wirt published were reconstructed from memory decades after delivery. In the twentieth century, critics debated the authenticity of the "liberty or death" text itself.

Unlike [[benjamin-franklin]] or [[jonathan-edwards]], Henry left few writings of his own. His legacy is preserved almost entirely in secondhand transcriptions. Yet as historian Bernard Bailyn observed, the very persistence of his words testifies to their literary power.

Modern scholarship situates Henry within the tradition of **American political oratory**. This lineage extends from the pulpit rhetoric of [[jonathan-edwards]] through the founding era to the later oratory of [[abraham-lincoln]] and [[frederick-douglass]].
