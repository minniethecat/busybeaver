# William H. Seward

## Biography

William Henry Seward (1801–1872) was an American statesman who served as Governor of New York, United States Senator, and Secretary of State under Presidents Abraham Lincoln and Andrew Johnson. Born in Florida, New York, Seward graduated from Union College in 1820 and was admitted to the bar in 1822. He entered politics as a Whig, serving in the New York State Senate before being elected governor in 1838. As governor, he championed internal improvements, prison reform, and the rights of free African Americans, refusing to surrender fugitive slaves to Southern claimants.

In 1849, Seward entered the U.S. Senate, where he established himself as one of the leading anti-slavery voices in the country. His "Higher Law" speech of 1850, opposing the Compromise of 1850, declared that there is "a higher law than the Constitution" — the law of God — that forbade the extension of slavery into the territories. This speech made Seward a hero to abolitionists and a target for defenders of the slave power. Seward joined the newly formed Republican Party and was widely expected to win its presidential nomination in 1860, but the convention chose Abraham Lincoln as a more moderate candidate. Seward accepted the position of Secretary of State and became Lincoln's most trusted cabinet adviser, helping to manage foreign relations during the Civil War and preventing European recognition of the Confederacy.

Seward's most enduring legacy is the Alaska Purchase of 1867, in which he negotiated the acquisition of Russian America for $7.2 million. Dismissed by contemporary critics as "Seward's Folly" and "Seward's Icebox," the purchase of nearly 600,000 square miles of territory proved to be one of the most important diplomatic achievements in American history. On the night of Lincoln's assassination in April 1865, Seward himself was attacked in a coordinated assassination attempt — stabbed repeatedly in his bed by Lewis Powell, a conspirator of John Wilkes Booth. Seward survived and continued to serve as Secretary of State under President Andrew Johnson until 1869.

## Literary Career

Seward's literary output belongs to the tradition of American political oratory and statecraft. His speeches, diplomatic dispatches, and public addresses are marked by moral seriousness, rhetorical elevation, and a conviction that American political life carried providential significance. His oratory drew upon classical and biblical models, blending legal argumentation with prophetic appeals to conscience.

The selection presented in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn from Seward's "Oration on Lafayette," delivered on July 16, 1834 — a tribute to the Marquis de Lafayette, the French aristocrat who served as a major general in the Continental Army during the American Revolution. Seward begins by recounting Lafayette's first acts of selfless devotion to the American cause, contrasting him with European volunteers who had purely professional motives:

> There were indeed other and heroic volunteers from European countries, but they were either exiles who had no homes, or they were soldiers by profession, who followed the sword wherever a harvest was to be reaped with it.... Lafayette's first act in America gave new evidence of disinterestedness and magnanimity. He found the small patriot army rent asunder by jealous feuds growing out of ambition for preferment. (020-010)

Seward highlights how Lafayette declined a paid commission, choosing instead to earn his rank through sacrifice on the battlefield:

> He declined the tender of a commission as major-general, with the emoluments, and stipulated, on the contrary, for leave to serve without reward, and even without a command, until he should have made a title to it by actual achievements. He won his commission by the blood he gave to his adopted country in the battle of Brandywine. (020-010)

Seward's Lafayette is not merely a military hero but a model of republican virtue — the rare figure who commands power yet remains indifferent to its trappings. Seward draws the moral lesson explicitly:

> Let those who think that preferment, power, and applause are always the chief objects of human ambition, look now at this illustrious and yet youthful personage, cheerfully resigning his command, and without one murmur of regret for the honors laid down, or one glance towards the honors gathering before him, taking affectionate leave of his companions in arms, and their great chief, and returning to his native land, to resume there the duties he owed as a subject and member of the State, in France. (020-011)

In this passage, Seward's characteristic themes converge: the subordination of personal ambition to civic duty, the ennobling power of republican institutions, and the conviction that American history offers moral exemplars for the world. The oration reflects Seward's lifelong effort to fuse moral conviction with political action — an impulse that would shape his career from the "Higher Law" speech to his tireless promotion of the Union cause.

## Critical Reception

Seward's reputation as a writer and orator has been inextricably bound to his political career. His contemporaries recognized him as one of the most eloquent and intellectually formidable figures in American public life. Charles Francis Adams called him "the most commanding figure in the Senate" of his era, and his diplomatic correspondence during the Civil War was praised by European statesmen for its clarity and force.

Modern historians have approached Seward with more critical nuance. While his anti-slavery credentials are unquestioned, scholars have noted the complexity of his political maneuvers — his willingness to compromise, his occasional rhetorical excesses, and the imperial dimensions of his vision for American expansion (of which the Alaska Purchase was one expression). Yet his legacy as a statesman of remarkable foresight and moral seriousness has grown in recent decades. His early advocacy for what he called "the irrepressible conflict" between slavery and freedom, his deft management of foreign relations during the nation's greatest crisis, and his prescient expansion of American territory in the Pacific all mark him as one of the most consequential figures of the nineteenth century.
