# John Lothrop Motley

## Biography

John Lothrop Motley (1814–1877) was born in Dorchester, Massachusetts (now part of Boston), the son of a prosperous merchant. He entered Harvard College at the age of thirteen and graduated in 1831, after which he studied at the universities of Göttingen and Berlin. At Göttingen, Motley formed a lifelong friendship with Otto von Bismarck, the future chancellor of Germany; the two young men shared rooms, dined together, and debated history and politics late into the night. This unlikely friendship would later prove diplomatically useful when Motley served as United States minister to Austria.

Motley returned to Boston in 1834, studied law briefly, and published two novels — *Morton's Hope* (1839) and *Merry Mount* (1849) — neither of which achieved success. It was during a visit to Europe in 1846 that he conceived the project that would define his career: a history of the Dutch revolt against Spain. Motley was drawn to the subject by its epic scale — the eighty-year struggle of a small, water-bound republic against the greatest empire of the age — and by its resonance with the democratic ideals he cherished as an American. For ten years he immersed himself in the archives of the Netherlands, Belgium, Germany, and Spain, reading original documents in Dutch, French, German, Spanish, and Latin.

*The Rise of the Dutch Republic* was published in 1856 to immediate and extraordinary acclaim on both sides of the Atlantic. Motley followed it with *The History of the United Netherlands* (1860–1867) and *The Life and Death of John of Barneveld* (1874). He served as United States minister to Austria (1861–1867) and later to Great Britain (1869–1870), though his tenure at the Court of St. James's ended controversially. Motley died in England in 1877, widely mourned as one of the great American historians of the nineteenth century.

## Literary Career

Motley's historical writing belongs to the romantic school of historiography associated with his contemporaries Francis Parkman and William H. Prescott. Like them, he conceived of history as a branch of literature — a narrative art that should combine rigorous archival research with dramatic structure, vivid characterization, and moral seriousness. The *Rise of the Dutch Republic* opens with a portrait of Philip II of Spain that reads like a novelistic character study, and its set pieces — the siege of Leyden, the assassination of William the Silent, the defense of Ostend — are rendered with the pacing and suspense of fiction.

The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" illustrates Motley's ability to sustain dramatic tension across long narrative arcs. It depicts the desperate assault on the Indian fortress in the Narragansett country during King Philip's War, led by General Josiah Winslow:

> It was between one and two o'clock in the afternoon, and the short winter day was wearing away. Winslow saw the position at a glance, and, by the promptness of his decision, proved himself a great commander. He ordered an instant assault. (030-004)

Motley's prose rises to a pitch of heroic exaltation as he describes the charge through the palisade entrance, where the leading officers are cut down:

> The men rushed on, although it was into the jaws of death, under an unerring fire. The block-house told them where the entrance was. The companies of Moseley and Davenport led the way. Moseley succeeded in passing through. Davenport fell beneath three fatal shots, just within the entrance. (030-004)

The conclusion of the passage — "In all military history there is not a more daring exploit. Never, on any field, has more heroic prowess been displayed" (030-004) — exemplifies Motley's characteristic rhetoric: unabashedly celebratory, morally engaged, committed to the view that history is driven by the courage and character of individuals.

Also included are Motley's portraits of the Duke of Alva and the siege of Ostend, displaying his command of European military history.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Motley was ranked alongside Prescott, Parkman, and George Bancroft as one of the four great American historians. His narrative gifts were widely acknowledged — William Makepeace Thackeray called the *Rise of the Dutch Republic* "as interesting as a novel" — and his archival diligence commanded the respect of European scholars. The Dutch government opened its archives to him, an exceptional gesture of confidence in a foreign historian.

Motley's reputation declined in the twentieth century as the romantic narrative tradition he represented gave way to more analytical and social-scientific approaches to history. His heroes-and-villains framework, his unabashed partisanship for the Protestant and republican cause, and his indifference to economic and institutional analysis came to seem dated. Recent scholarship, however, has begun to re-evaluate the romantic historians on their own terms, recognizing their achievement in creating a distinctively American historical literature — one that sought in the European past the foundations of American democratic identity. Motley's eloquence, his cosmopolitan erudition, and his commitment to the moral drama of history deserve a place in any account of nineteenth-century American letters.
