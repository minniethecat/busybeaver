# William R. Williams

## Biography

William R. Williams (1804–1885) was an American Baptist clergyman and theologian. Born in New York City, he was the son of a Baptist deacon and was educated at Columbia College, graduating in 1822. After studying law briefly, he turned to the ministry under the influence of the Baptist preacher William Collier. He was ordained in 1830 and installed as pastor of the Amity Street Baptist Church in New York City, where he served for the remainder of his long career.

Williams was among the most literate and scholarly Baptist ministers of his generation. A voracious reader in several languages, he built one of the finest private libraries in New York and was a mentor to younger ministers who sought a model of learned piety. He was a founding member of the American Bible Union and served on the board of several Baptist educational and missionary organizations. Though he declined offers of academic positions, his influence on American Baptist thought was exercised through the pulpit, the press, and extensive correspondence.

## Literary Career

Williams's most important work is *Religious Progress* (1850), a set of lectures originally delivered before the Young Men's Christian Association of New York. Tracing the historical development of religious ideas and institutions, the book sought to demonstrate the progressive character of revealed religion against secular narratives of inevitable human advancement. His other major publication, *Lectures on the Lord's Prayer* (1851), was a series of devotional and doctrinal expositions of the model prayer given by Christ.

The selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, titled "Lead Us Not into Temptation," is drawn from the *Lectures on the Lord's Prayer*. It expounds the sixth petition of the Lord's Prayer with a blend of pastoral concern and psychological insight. Williams distinguishes between temptation that is *God's sending* — providentially ordained trial — and temptation that results from the believer's own negligence:

> If we walk without care and without vigilance, if we acknowledge not God in our ways, and take counsel at Ekron, and not at Zion — leaving the Bible unread, and the closet unvisited — if the sanctuary and the Sabbath lose their ancient hold upon us, and we then go on frowardly in the way of our own eyes, and after the counsel of our own heart, we have reason to tremble. (010-003)

The passage is especially noted for its striking simile of conscience as a miner's safety lamp:

> A conscience quick and sensitive, under the presence of the indwelling Spirit, is like the safety-lamp of the miner, a ready witness and a mysterious guardian against the deathful damps, that unseen, but fatal, cluster around our darkling way. (010-003)

He extends the warning to the unconverted with equal vividness:

> With an evil nature, and a mortal body, and a brittle and brief tenure of earth, you are traversing perilous paths. (010-004)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Lead Us Not into Temptation* (010-003, 010-004) — From *Lectures on the Lord's Prayer*. An exposition of the sixth petition of the Lord's Prayer. Williams distinguishes providentially ordained trials from self-sought temptation, employs the memorable image of conscience as the miner's safety lamp guarding against unseen spiritual dangers, and concludes with a solemn warning to the unconverted about the perils of a mortal life lived without God.

## Critical Reception

Williams was regarded by contemporaries as one of the premier Baptist intellectuals of the mid-nineteenth century. His *Religious Progress* was favorably reviewed on both sides of the Atlantic and was seen as a significant Baptist contribution to the broader Protestant discourse on history and progress. Colleagues praised the range of his learning — he was at home in the church fathers, the Reformers, and the English Puritans — and the rhetorical finish of his prose.

Williams's reputation narrowed after his death, as is the case with many figures whose influence was exercised primarily through the spoken word and fugitive publications. His *Lectures on the Lord's Prayer* has been reprinted in devotional series into the modern period, appealing to readers who value theological depth expressed in accessible, image-rich prose.
