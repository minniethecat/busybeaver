# National Identity in American Literature

## Definition
National identity, as a theme in American literature, encompasses the ongoing effort to define what it means to be American—the collective character, values, and cultural mission of the United States. From colonial self-descriptions to 19th-century literary nationalism, American writers have persistently asked what distinguishes their nation from others.

## Historical Context
Colonial writers began the work of national self-definition by contrasting American conditions with European precedents. Cadwallader Colden recorded how the Iroquois Nations "think themselves by nature superior to the rest of mankind" (003-006)—a description that reflected both Iroquois and emerging American self-conception. Timothy Dwight celebrated the distinctive American "spirit of freedom" and "recognition of the equal rights of men" (007-004). By the early 19th century, Washington Irving and others consciously cultivated a national literature, contributing to "the spirit of national independence" in letters (019-002).

## Key Authors and Works
- **Cadwallader Colden** documented how colonial identities were formed through encounters with Native American nations, whose own strong sense of identity both challenged and shaped American self-understanding (003-006).
- **Timothy Dwight** articulated a vision of American national character rooted in "personal independence, and universal prosperity," linking national identity to republican virtue (006-001, 007-004).
- **Washington Irving** pioneered a distinctly American literary voice, demonstrating that American materials—the Hudson Valley, Dutch colonial history, the frontier—could produce literature equal to European models (019-002).

## Legacy
The literary construction of national identity remained a central preoccupation of American writers throughout the 19th century—from Emerson's "The American Scholar" (1837), which declared intellectual independence from Europe, to Whitman's "Leaves of Grass" (1855), which celebrated American democracy in a distinctively American poetic voice.
