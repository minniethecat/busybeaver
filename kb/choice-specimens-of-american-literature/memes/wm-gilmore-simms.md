## Biography

William Gilmore Simms (1806–1870) was born in Charleston, South Carolina. After his mother's death and his father's departure for the frontier, he was raised by his grandmother. Largely self-educated, Simms began publishing poetry in his teens and later studied law, though literary ambition soon overtook his legal career. He became the most prolific Southern writer of the antebellum period, producing novels, poems, histories, and literary criticism that shaped the region's cultural identity.

Simms married twice and raised a large family at his plantation "Woodlands" near Charleston. His life was marked by repeated tragedy — he lost his first wife, several children, and his home (burned during Sherman's March in 1865). Financially ruined after the Civil War, he continued writing until his death, producing over seventy volumes across multiple genres.

## Literary Career

Simms established his reputation with historical romances set in the colonial and Revolutionary South. His "Border Romances" — including *Guy Rivers* (1834), *The Yemassee* (1835), and *The Partisan* (1835) — drew on regional history and landscape to create a distinctly Southern literary tradition. He also authored biographies (notably *The Life of Francis Marion*), poetry collections, and edited several Southern periodicals.

His novel *Eutaw* (1856), a sequel to *The Foragers*, depicted the Revolutionary War battle in South Carolina. His poetry collection *The Cassique of Accabee* (1849) explored Native American and colonial themes in verse. Though his reputation declined after the Civil War as national literary taste shifted toward New England, Simms remains a foundational figure in Southern literature.

## Selections

The corpus of *Choice Specimens of American Literature* presents four selections from Simms's work, drawn from his fiction, biography, and poetry.

### The Battle of Eutaw (061-004–061-005)

From the novel *Eutaw, a Sequel to The Foragers*, this passage describes the tide-turning Battle of Eutaw Springs (1781) during the Revolutionary War. Simms dramatizes how the Americans, "flushed with success," scattered to loot the British camp, allowing the enemy to regroup and turn certain American victory into stalemate:

> [061-004] Up to this moment nothing had seemed more certain than the victory of the Americans. The consternation in the British camp was complete. Everything was given up for lost by a considerable portion of the army. The commissaries destroyed their stores, the loyalists and American deserters, dreading the rope, seizing every horse which they could command, fled incontinently for Charleston, whither they carried such an alarm that the stores along the road were destroyed, and the trees felled across it for the obstruction of the victorious Americans, who were supposed to be pressing down upon the city with all their might. Equally deceived were the conquerors. Flushed with success, the infantry scattered themselves about the British camp, which, as all the tents had been left standing, presented a thousand objects to tempt the appetites of a half-starved and half-naked soldiery. Insubordination followed disorder....

> [061-005] No more could be done. The laurels won in the first act of this exciting drama, were all withered in the second. Both parties claimed a victory. It belonged to neither. The British were beaten from the field at the point of the bayonet, sought shelter in a fortress, and repulsed their assailants from that fortress. It is to the shame and discredit of the Americans that they were repulsed. The victory was in their hands.

### Character and Services of General Marion (061-006–061-007)

From Simms's *Life of Francis Marion*, this biographical sketch portrays the Swamp Fox's leadership style. Simms emphasizes Marion's frugality with soldiers' lives and his skill in irregular warfare:

> [061-006] No commander had ever been more solicitous of the safety and comfort of his men. It was this which had rendered him so sure of their fidelity, which had enabled him to extract from them such admirable service. This simple entreaty stayed their quarrels; ... No duel took place among his officers during the whole of his command.

> [061-007] The province which was assigned to his control by Governor Rutledge, was the constant theatre of war. He was required to cover an immense extent of country. With a force constantly unequal and constantly fluctuating, he contrived to supply its deficiencies by the resources of his own vigilance and skill. His personal bravery was frequently shown, and the fact that he himself conducted an enterprise, was enough to convince his men that they were certain to be led to victory.... [H]e could advance and strike with an audacity, which in the ordinary relations of the leader with the soldier, might well be thought inexcusable rashness.... To him we owe that the fires of patriotism were never extinguished, even in the most disastrous hours, in the low country of South Carolina.

### Nature Inspires Sentiment (070-005–070-008)

From *The Cassique of Accabee*, this lyrical poem captures a moonlit boat excursion on the Ashley River near Charleston. The serene natural setting prompts reflections on love, legend, and the landscape's haunted history:

> [070-005] It was a night of calm. O'er Ashley's waters / Crept the sweet billows to their own soft tune, / While she, most bright of Keawah's fair daughters, / Whose voice might spell the footsteps of the moon, / As slow we swept along, / Poured forth her own sweet song — / A lay of rapture not forgotten soon.

> [070-006] Hushed was our breathing, stayed the lifted oar, / Our spirits rapt, our souls no longer free, / While the boat, drifting softly to the shore, / Brought us within the shades of Accabee.

## Reception

During his lifetime, Simms was widely read and respected both in the South and the North. Edgar Allan Poe praised his work highly. After the Civil War, however, his reputation suffered as the center of American literary culture shifted decisively toward New England. Twentieth-century scholarship has gradually restored attention to Simms, recognizing him as the most significant Southern man of letters before Faulkner and acknowledging the historical and ethnographic value embedded in his fiction.
