# John C. Calhoun

## Biography

John Caldwell Calhoun (1782–1850) was an American statesman, political theorist, and orator who served as the seventh Vice President of the United States (1825–1832) under Presidents John Quincy Adams and Andrew Jackson. Born in Abbeville District, South Carolina, Calhoun graduated from Yale College in 1804 and studied law at Tapping Reeve's law school in Litchfield, Connecticut. He was admitted to the South Carolina bar in 1807.

Calhoun served in the House of Representatives (1811–1817), as Secretary of War under James Monroe (1817–1825), as Vice President (1825–1832), as Secretary of State under John Tyler (1844–1845), and in the Senate (1832–1843, 1845–1850). His political career traced an arc from nationalist — he was an early advocate of internal improvements and a strong federal government — to the foremost champion of states' rights, nullification, and the defense of Southern institutions. His final Senate speech, delivered in March 1850 during the debates over the Compromise of 1850, was read by a colleague as Calhoun, mortally ill, sat silently in the chamber. He died in Washington, D.C., on March 31, 1850.

## Literary Career

Calhoun was among the most formidable political thinkers of the antebellum era. His speeches and writings, while composed in the service of specific legislative and constitutional debates, articulated a systematic political philosophy that influenced generations of American statesmen and theorists. The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes selections from three of his major addresses, each illustrating a distinct dimension of his thought.

The first selection, from Calhoun's "Speech on the Bill to Regulate the Power of Removal" (1835), warns against the concentration of executive patronage power:

> Let us not be deceived by names. The power in question is too great for the chief magistrate of a free state. It is in its nature an imperial power; and if he be permitted to exercise it, his authority must become as absolute as that of the autocrat of all the Russias. (018-002)

Calhoun argued that unchecked removal power would corrupt the republic by making officeholders dependent on executive favor. He observed, in language that resonates across American history:

> I have seen the spirit of independent men, holding public office, sink under the dread of this fearful power, too honest and too firm to become the instruments of the flatterers of power, yet too prudent, with all the consequences before them, to whisper disapprobation of what, in their hearts, they condemned. (018-003)

The second selection, from Calhoun's "Address on the Relation of the States to the General Government," articulates his theory of the federal system as a unique American achievement:

> So numerous and diversified are the interests of our country, that they could not be fairly represented in a single government... A plan was adopted better suited to our situation, but perfectly novel in its character. The powers of government were divided, not, as heretofore, in reference to classes, but geographically. (018-006)

Calhoun describes this distribution of power as "truly and emphatically American, without example or parallel" (018-006) — each sphere "perfectly adapted to its respective objects" (018-007), with the preservation of this balance being the central challenge of American constitutionalism.

The third selection presents Calhoun's doctrine of the concurrent majority, his most distinctive theoretical contribution. In a government of the concurrent majority:

> ...oppression is prevented, and the necessity of resorting to force superseded... and, hence, compromise, instead of force, becomes their conservative principle. (018-009)

He elevated this principle to almost theological heights:

> Traced to this source, the voice of a people, — uttered under the necessity of avoiding the greatest of calamities, through the organs of a government so constructed as to suppress the expression of all partial and selfish interests, and to give a full and faithful utterance to the sense of the whole community, in reference to its common welfare, — may without impiety, be called the voice of God. (018-010)

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from John C. Calhoun's works appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Dangers of an Unlimited Power of Removal from Office* (018-002–018-003) — From "Speech on the Bill to Regulate the Power of Removal" (1835). Calhoun warns that unchecked executive removal power would corrupt public officials and debase the American character.
- *Peculiar Merit of Our Political System* (018-005–018-007) — From "Address on the Relation of the States to the General Government." An exposition of federalism as a distinctively American innovation that protects diverse geographical interests from majority tyranny.
- *Concurrent Majorities Supersede Force* (018-008–018-010) — From Calhoun's "Works." The classic statement of the concurrent majority doctrine: by giving each interest a veto, compromise replaces force as the conservative principle of government.

## Critical Reception

Calhoun's reputation as a political thinker is inseparable from his defense of slavery and secession. His political theory, developed in the service of protecting Southern interests, has been described by some scholars as a profound analysis of the problem of majority rule in diverse societies, and by others as a sophisticated rationalization for minority veto power over democratic governance.

The literary qualities of Calhoun's prose have been widely acknowledged. His style is logical and rigorous, eschewing ornament for the tight construction of argument. Richard Hofstadter, in "The American Political Tradition," described Calhoun as "the Marx of the master class" — a thinker who provided the antebellum South with its most rigorous intellectual defense. Contemporary assessments continue to grapple with the tension between Calhoun's analytical power and the moral catastrophe of the cause he served.
