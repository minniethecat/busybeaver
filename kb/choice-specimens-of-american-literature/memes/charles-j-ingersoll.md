# Charles J. Ingersoll

## Biography

Charles Jared Ingersoll (1782–1862) was an American lawyer, congressman, and political writer whose career bridged the literary and political worlds of early nineteenth-century Philadelphia. Born into a prominent Pennsylvania family — his father, Jared Ingersoll, was a delegate to the Constitutional Convention and a signer of the Constitution — Charles was educated at the College of New Jersey (Princeton) and admitted to the bar in 1802. According to the brief note in "Choice Specimens":

> A native of Pennsylvania; long conspicuous in the law, literature, and political life. (025-015, 025-016)

Ingersoll served in the U.S. House of Representatives from 1813 to 1815 and again from 1841 to 1849. A Democrat in politics, he was an early and vocal supporter of Andrew Jackson. Between his two congressional terms, he devoted himself to literary pursuits, producing a substantial body of work that included legal treatises, political pamphlets, and the four-volume *Historical Sketch of the Second War Between the United States of America and Great Britain* (1845–1852), which remains his most significant publication.

A lifelong Philadelphian, Ingersoll moved in the city's intellectual circles and was a member of the American Philosophical Society. His literary tastes were broad — he wrote verse, translated French works, and compiled reminiscences of the eminent men he had known. He died in Philadelphia in 1862, having outlived most of his early nineteenth-century contemporaries.

## Literary Career

Ingersoll's prose style reflects his dual identity as lawyer and man of letters. He writes with the forensic precision of a courtroom advocate, yet his historical narratives are enlivened by a sharp eye for character and a willingness to render judgment. His four-volume history of the War of 1812, though partisan in its Jacksonian sympathies, remains a valuable contemporary account, particularly for its detailed treatment of the Niagara campaign.

The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" — a character sketch of John C. Calhoun — demonstrates Ingersoll's talent for concisely capturing personality and political character:

> John Caldwell Calhoun was the same slender, erect, and ardent logician, politician, and sectarian, in the House of Representatives in 1814 that he is in the Senate of 1847. Speaking with aggressive aspect, flashing eye, rapid action and enunciation, unadorned argument, eccentricity of judgment, unbounded love of rule, impatient, precipitate, kind temper, excellent in colloquial attractions, caressing the young, not courting rulers; conception, perception, and demonstration quick and clear, with logical precision arguing paradoxes, and carrying home conviction beyond rhetorical exposition. (025-014)

Ingersoll's history also contains careful military analysis, as in his assessment of the Battle of Chippewa (1814), which he recognized as a turning point in Anglo-American relations:

> The charm of British military invincibility was as effectually broken, by a single brigade, as that of naval supremacy was by a single frigate, as much as if a large army or fleet had been the agent. (025-017)

He perceived in this victory an epochal significance that went beyond the battlefield:

> The victory of Chippewa was the resurrection or birth of American arms, after their prostration by so long disuse, and when at length taken up again, by such continual and deplorable failures, that the martial and moral influence of the first decided victory opened and characterized an epoch in the annals and intercourse of the two kindred and rival nations. (025-017)

## Selections in This Anthology

- "Character of John C. Calhoun" (025-014) — From Ingersoll's *Historical Sketch of the Second War*. A vivid and psychologically acute portrait of the South Carolina statesman, notable for its concision and its balance of admiration with critique.
- "The Battle of Chippewa" (025-017) — From the same work. A tactical and cultural analysis of the 1814 battle, emphasizing its symbolic importance in establishing American military credibility.

## Critical Reception

Ingersoll's historical works were well received in his own time, particularly by Democratic reviewers who shared his political sympathies. The *North American Review* praised his "lively and vigorous style," while the *Southern Quarterly Review* found his War of 1812 history "the most complete and satisfactory account" of that conflict then available. Twentieth-century historians, while less reliant on his partisan narrative, have continued to consult his work for its eyewitness quality and its detailed documentation. His character sketches — especially of Calhoun, Clay, and Webster — are valued as primary sources by students of antebellum political culture.
