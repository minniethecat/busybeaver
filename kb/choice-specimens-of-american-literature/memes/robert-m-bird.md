# Robert M. Bird

## Biography

Robert Montgomery Bird (1803–1854) was an American novelist, playwright, and physician. Born in New Castle, Delaware, Bird graduated from the University of Pennsylvania with a medical degree in 1827. He practiced medicine only briefly before turning to literature, producing a series of successful plays and novels that established him as one of the most popular American writers of the 1830s.

Bird's theatrical career was closely tied to the actor Edwin Forrest, for whom he wrote several verse tragedies, including *The Gladiator* (1831), which became one of the most frequently performed American plays of the antebellum period. A financial dispute with Forrest led Bird to abandon the stage and concentrate on fiction. He married Mary Mayer in 1837 and later returned to medicine and newspaper editing in his final years, serving as editor of the *Philadelphia North American*.

## Literary Career

Bird's novels draw on American historical subjects and frontier settings, contributing to the development of a distinctly national literature. His most famous work, *Nick of the Woods; or, The Jibbenainosay: A Tale of Kentucky* (1837), is a frontier romance set during the Kentucky Indian wars. The novel features Nathan Slaughter, a pacifist Quaker who, by night, transforms into a fearsome avenger known as "the Jibbenainosay" — a figure who embodies the tension between civilized restraint and frontier violence.

The selection included in this anthology captures the novel's closing scene, in which Nathan — having revealed his secret identity — speaks with the Virginian Roland Forrester:

> "I have a thing to say to thee, which it concerns thee and the fair maid, thee cousin, to know. There was a will, friend, a true and lawful last will and testament of thee deceased uncle, in which theeself and thee cousin was made the sole heirs of the same. Truly, friend, I did take it from the breast of the villain that plotted thee ruin; but, truly, it was taken from me again, I know not how." (060-001)

Bird's rendering of Quaker plain speech, with its distinctive use of "thee" and its Biblical cadences, lends Nathan a quiet dignity. The character's refusal of reward and his plea for discretion about his true identity reveal the moral complexity beneath the surface of the frontier adventure:

> "I, friend!" exclaimed Nathan, with a melancholy shake of the head; "thee would not have me back in the Settlements, to scandalize them that is of my faith? No, friend; my lot is cast in the woods, and thee must not ask me again to leave them. And, friend, thee must not think I have served thee for the lucre of money or gain; for truly these things are now to me as nothing. The meat that feeds me, the skins that cover, the leaves that make my bed, are all in the forest around me, to be mine when I want them; and what more can I desire?" (060-002)

The emotional weight of the scene is concentrated in Nathan's final request — that his deeds should not "scandalize and disparage the faith to which I was born" (060-004):

> "Nay, friend," muttered Nathan, with both a warning and beseeching look, "all that I ask is, that thee shall say nothing of me that should scandalize and disparage the faith to which I was born." (060-004)

The chapter closes with Nathan withdrawing into the wilderness, "ashamed of the weakness which he could not resist," having "turned away to conceal his emotion" (060-005) — a gesture that preserves the character's enigmatic blend of ferocity and vulnerability.

## Critical Reception

In his own day, Bird was one of the few American authors who could support himself by his pen — a testament to his popularity. *Nick of the Woods* was widely read on both sides of the Atlantic and was adapted for the stage. His play *The Gladiator* was performed over a thousand times before the Civil War, making it one of the most successful American dramas of the century. However, Bird's literary reputation faded in the late nineteenth century as tastes shifted toward realism. Modern scholarship has reassessed his work as an important contribution to the frontier romance genre and to the development of American dramatic literature. The character of Nathan Slaughter, in particular, has been analyzed as a complex allegory of the ethical tensions inherent in westward expansion.
