# Henry David Thoreau

## Biography

Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862) was an American naturalist, essayist, poet, and philosopher. Born in Concord, Massachusetts, he graduated from Harvard College in 1837 and returned to Concord, where he formed a close friendship with Ralph Waldo Emerson and became a central figure in the Transcendentalist movement.

Thoreau is best known for his experiment in simple living at Walden Pond (1845–1847), chronicled in his masterpiece *Walden* (1854), and for his essay "Civil Disobedience" (1849), which influenced generations of nonviolent resistance movements worldwide. A committed abolitionist, he delivered passionate lectures against slavery and defended John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry. Thoreau worked variously as a surveyor, pencil-maker, and lecturer, living deliberately and frugally in his native Concord until his death from tuberculosis at age forty-four.

## Literary Career

Thoreau's writing blends acute natural observation with philosophical reflection and social critique. His major works include *A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers* (1849), *Walden; or, Life in the Woods* (1854), and the posthumously published *The Maine Woods* (1864), *Cape Cod* (1865), and extensive journals spanning over two million words. His prose is marked by its precision of natural detail, epigrammatic wit, and moral seriousness — a voice at once deeply local and universally resonant.

Though overshadowed during his lifetime by Emerson's greater fame, Thoreau's influence grew steadily after his death. His writings on nature, simplicity, and individual conscience have shaped American environmental thought and civil liberties discourse for over a century.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Thoreau's work received limited recognition. *Walden* sold modestly, and he was often regarded as a lesser imitator of Emerson. By the late nineteenth century, however, his reputation began to rise, and in the twentieth century he was recognized as one of the most important figures in American letters. His environmental philosophy anticipated modern ecology, and "Civil Disobedience" has been cited as an influence by figures from Mahatma Gandhi to Martin Luther King Jr.
