# Roger Williams

## Biography

Roger Williams (c. 1603–1683) was an English-born Puritan minister, theologian, and the founder of the Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations. He is widely regarded as one of the first and most articulate advocates for religious liberty and the separation of church and state in the Western tradition.

Born in London, Williams was educated at Pembroke College, Cambridge, where he distinguished himself in classical and biblical studies. He took holy orders in the Church of England but soon gravitated toward Puritan and eventually Separatist convictions. In 1630, fleeing the religious conformity demanded by Archbishop Laud, Williams sailed to the Massachusetts Bay Colony, where he was offered the position of minister in the Boston church. He refused, objecting that the Boston congregation had not formally separated from the Church of England — the first of many principled stands that would mark his career.

Banished from Massachusetts in 1635 for his "new and dangerous opinions" — chief among them the belief that civil authorities had no jurisdiction over matters of conscience — Williams fled into the winter wilderness and, with the help of Narragansett Indians whose language and culture he had studied, founded Providence in 1636. The settlement he established became the first place in the Western world where religious liberty was enshrined as a founding principle. Williams obtained a royal charter for Rhode Island in 1644, explicitly guaranteeing freedom of conscience. He served as the colony's president and continued to write and debate throughout his long life, engaging in pamphlet wars with John Cotton and the Quakers while maintaining his commitment to the principle that the state must not coerce belief.

## Literary Career

Williams wrote in a dense, allusive, often difficult prose shaped by Puritan sermon style and Renaissance learning. His major works — *The Bloody Tenent of Persecution* (1644), its sequel *The Bloody Tenent Yet More Bloody* (1652), and his linguistic study *A Key into the Language of America* (1643) — combine scriptural argument, political philosophy, and ethnographic observation in a voice at once passionate and relentlessly logical.

The selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn from Williams's "Memoirs" and presents his most vivid metaphor for religious toleration — the image of a ship at sea, carrying passengers of diverse beliefs. The ship represents the commonwealth; the passengers' diverse faiths, the consciences of citizens; and the ship's discipline, the legitimate sphere of civil government:

> There goes many a ship to sea, with many hundred souls in one ship, whose weal and woe is common, and is a true picture of a commonwealth, or a human combination or society. It hath fallen out, sometimes, that both Papists and Protestants, Jews and Turks, may be embarked into one ship. (002-049)

Williams's two "hinges" of religious liberty are simple and radical: no person shall be compelled to attend the established worship, and no person shall be prevented from practicing their own. Both are protections *from* the state, not permissions *granted* by it:

> Upon which supposal, I affirm that all the liberty of conscience, that ever I pleaded for, turns upon these two hinges; that none of the Papists, Protestants, Jews, or Turks, be forced to come to the ship's prayers, nor compelled from their own particular prayers or worship, if they practice any. (002-049)

Crucially, Williams's toleration is not unlimited. The ship's officers retain authority over conduct that threatens the common peace — a distinction between religious conscience and civil order that would prove foundational to American constitutional thought:

> If any refuse to obey the common laws or orders of the ship concerning their common peace or preservation; if any shall mutiny and rise up against their commanders and officers ... I say I never denied but in such cases, whatever is pretended, the commander or commanders may judge, resist, compel, and punish such transgressors, according to their deserts and merits. (002-049)

The passage, brief as it is, encapsulates a political philosophy centuries ahead of its time. Williams's image of a ship whose passengers include "Papists and Protestants, Jews and Turks" living peaceably under laws that protect all without favoring any creed, remains one of the most powerful statements of religious pluralism in the American tradition.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "Extent of Religious Freedom" (002-049) — From the "Memoirs." The famous ship-of-state metaphor: Williams argues that religious liberty rests on two principles — no forced worship and no prohibited worship — while affirming that civil authorities retain the right to enforce common peace and order.

## Critical Reception

Williams's reputation has undergone a dramatic rehabilitation over the centuries. In his own time, he was regarded by the Massachusetts establishment as a dangerous radical. Cotton Mather, while acknowledging Williams's learning, called him "the first rebel against the divine church-order in the wilderness." John Winthrop respected him personally but felt compelled to banish him.

By the nineteenth century, Williams had been rediscovered as a proto-Jeffersonian, a forerunner of the First Amendment. The historian George Bancroft called him "the first person in modern Christendom to assert in its plenitude the doctrine of liberty of conscience." In the twentieth century, Perry Miller and Edmund S. Morgan gave Williams serious intellectual treatment, recognizing his theological arguments as sophisticated contributions to political philosophy. More recently, scholars have emphasized Williams's relationships with Native Americans, his linguistic work, and the radical consistency of his thought — a man who took his Puritan convictions so seriously that he was willing to follow them all the way to religious freedom.
