# John Marshall

## Biography

John Marshall (1755–1835) was an American statesman, jurist, and historian who served as the fourth Chief Justice of the United States (1801–1835). Born in a log cabin on the Virginia frontier, in what is now Fauquier County, Marshall was the eldest of fifteen children. His formal education was limited to a single year at an academy and a brief course of legal lectures at the College of William and Mary under George Wythe, yet he became one of the most influential legal minds in American history.

During the American Revolution, Marshall served as an officer in the Continental Army, enduring the winter at Valley Forge and participating in the battles of Brandywine, Germantown, and Monmouth. The experience forged in him a lifelong commitment to national union — a conviction that the survival of republican liberty depended on a strong central government capable of commanding allegiance across state lines. After the war, he established a successful legal practice in Richmond, served in the Virginia legislature, and was a leading advocate for the ratification of the Constitution at the Virginia Ratifying Convention of 1788.

Marshall served as a diplomat (as one of the commissioners to France in the XYZ Affair), as a member of Congress, and briefly as Secretary of State under John Adams before his appointment as Chief Justice in 1801. Over thirty-four years on the Supreme Court, Marshall authored over five hundred opinions and established the foundations of American constitutional law: judicial review (Marbury v. Madison), the supremacy of federal law over state law (McCulloch v. Maryland), and the breadth of congressional power under the Commerce Clause (Gibbons v. Ogden). His "Life of Washington" (1804–1807), a five-volume biography, was for decades the standard work on the first president.

## Literary Career

Marshall's literary reputation rests principally on his judicial opinions — models of lucid reasoning and magisterial prose that transformed the Supreme Court from a weak third branch into a coequal department of government. Yet his "Life of Washington" and his earlier "History of the American Colonies" reveal a capable narrative historian whose prose, if less polished than that of a professional litterateur, possesses the virtues of clarity, accuracy, and steady moral purpose.

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874) includes a selection from Marshall's "History of the American Colonies," describing the British conquest of Canada during the French and Indian War. Marshall presents the fall of Montreal in 1760 with the economy of a military historian who understands that the decisive factor was not courage but logistics:

> During these transactions, General Amherst was taking measures for the annihilation of the remnant of French power in Canada. He determined to employ the immense force under his command for the accomplishment of this object, and made arrangements during the winter to bring the armies from Quebec, Lake Champlain, and Lake Ontario, to act against Montreal. The junction of these armies presenting before Montreal a force not to be resisted, the Governor offered to capitulate. (022-008)

The terms of surrender reflected the magnanimity that characterized British policy toward the conquered French Canadians: "The troops were to be transported to France, and the Canadians to be protected in their property, and the full enjoyment of their religion" (022-008). This was not mere generosity but strategic wisdom — a recognition that the allegiance of seventy thousand French Catholics could not be secured by coercion.

Marshall's assessment of the significance of the conquest rises to genuine historical vision. He understands that the elimination of French power from North America was an event of world-historical importance:

> That colossal power which France had been long erecting in America, with vast labor and expense; which had been the motive for one of the most extensive and desolating wars of modern times, was thus entirely overthrown. The causes of this interesting event are to be found in the superior wealth and population of the colonies of England, and in her immense naval strength; an advantage, in distant war, not to be counterbalanced by the numbers, the discipline, the courage, and the military talents, which may be combined in the armies of an inferior maritime power. (022-009)

The selection concludes with a somber reflection on the particular character of the colonial wars — a warfare that, unlike the comparatively restrained conflicts of Europe, fell with indiscriminate violence on civilians:

> In no part of those dominions was this joy felt in a higher degree, or with more reason, than in America. In that region, the wars between France and England had assumed a form, happily unknown to other parts of the civilized world. Not confined as in Europe to men in arms — women and children were its common victims. It had been carried by the savage to the fire-side of the peaceful peasant, where the tomahawk and the scalping-knife were applied indiscriminately to every age, and to either sex. The hope was now fondly indulged that these scenes, at least in the northern and middle colonies, were closed forever. (022-010)

This passage reveals Marshall the Virginian, for whom the frontier experience of warfare was not an abstract historical datum but a lived memory passed down by those who had witnessed it. The note of relief — "closed forever" — would prove premature, as the same frontier would become a theater of war again within a generation.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selection from John Marshall's writings appears in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *The Conquest of Canada* (022-008–022-010) — From "The History of the American Colonies." A narrative of the British capture of Montreal in 1760, the final episode in the destruction of French imperial power in North America. Marshall analyzes the strategic factors behind the British victory — superior wealth, population, and naval power — and reflects on the particular horror of colonial warfare, in which Native American allies brought the tomahawk and scalping-knife to the doorsteps of frontier families.

## Critical Reception

John Marshall's reputation as a jurist has long eclipsed his standing as a writer. Legal scholars and historians of the Supreme Court have exhaustively studied his opinions, and his tenure as Chief Justice is universally regarded as the formative period of American constitutional law. His "Life of Washington," though superseded by modern scholarship, remains an important primary source — Marshall wrote as a contemporary and admirer of Washington, and his biography preserves details and perspectives available to no later historian.

Recent scholarship has begun to reassess Marshall's historical writing on its own terms. His "History of the American Colonies," though never completed, demonstrates a narrative gift and a capacity for broad strategic analysis that anticipate the work of later American historians such as Francis Parkman and George Bancroft. Marshall wrote history as he wrote law: with clarity, authority, and an unwavering conviction that the American experiment in republican government was providentially guided.
