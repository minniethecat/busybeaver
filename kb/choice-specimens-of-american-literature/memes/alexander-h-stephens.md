# Alexander H. Stephens

## Biography

Alexander Hamilton Stephens (1812–1883) was an American politician who served as Vice President of the Confederate States of America (1861–1865) and as the 50th Governor of Georgia (1882–1883). Born near Crawfordville, Georgia, to a farming family, Stephens was orphaned at an early age and raised by relatives. He graduated from Franklin College (now the University of Georgia) in 1832, studied law, and was admitted to the bar in 1834.

Stephens entered politics as a Whig, serving in the Georgia House of Representatives (1836–1841) and the Georgia Senate (1842). He was elected to the United States House of Representatives in 1843, where he served for most of the next sixteen years. Though a defender of slavery and states' rights, Stephens initially opposed secession, arguing before the Georgia Secession Convention that the South's interests were better protected within the Union. After Georgia voted to secede, however, he accepted the vice presidency of the Confederacy under Jefferson Davis. Following the war, Stephens was imprisoned for several months, then returned to Georgia, where he was again elected to Congress (1873–1882) and, shortly before his death, to the governorship.

## Literary Career

Stephens's major literary work was *A Constitutional View of the Late War Between the States* (1868–1870), a two-volume defense of the Confederate position cast as a dialogue between three fictitious characters. Written with lawyerly precision, the work argued that the Civil War was fundamentally a contest over the constitutional doctrine of state sovereignty, rather than a struggle over slavery. Though this thesis has been thoroughly repudiated by modern historiography, the work remains a significant document of Lost Cause ideology and a window into the constitutional reasoning of the antebellum South.

The selection in this anthology, drawn from the appendix to *The Constitutional View*, addresses a less controversial subject: the origin of the American flag. Stephens traces the design of the stars and stripes to Thomas Jefferson's proposed seal for the United States, offering an engaging account of how the flag's elements evolved from heraldic imagery to national emblem:

> The stripes, or lines, which, on Mr. Jefferson's original plan, were to designate the six quarterings of the shield, as signs of the six countries from which our ancestors came, are now, I believe, considered as representations of the old thirteen states. (022-002)

This passage demonstrates the range of Stephens's interests beyond constitutional polemics and his capacity for historical narrative.

## Critical Reception

Stephens's legacy is inextricably bound to the Confederacy and the Lost Cause. His *Constitutional View* was influential in its day, widely read among southern readers as a vindication of the Confederate cause. Modern historians have been sharply critical of Stephens's historical method and constitutional reasoning, noting his systematic minimization of slavery as the war's central cause — a position starkly at odds with his own 1861 "Cornerstone Speech," in which he had declared that the Confederacy rested on "the great truth that the negro is not equal to the white man." This contradiction has come to define Stephens's historical reputation: a man of undeniable intellect who placed his considerable talents in the service of a fundamentally unjust cause.
