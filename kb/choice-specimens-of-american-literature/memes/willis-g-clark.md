# Willis G. Clark

## Biography

Willis Gaylord Clark (1810–1841) was an American poet and journalist born in Otisco, New York. The twin brother of Lewis Gaylord Clark, who would become the longtime editor of the *Knickerbocker Magazine*, Willis Clark grew up in a literary household. He moved to Philadelphia in the 1830s, where he became editor of the *Philadelphia Gazette* and established himself as a poet and critic. Though his career was brief — he died of tuberculosis at the age of thirty-one — Clark left behind a body of verse collected posthumously in *Literary Remains* (1844), edited by his brother Lewis. His poetry reflected the religious sensibility and moral seriousness characteristic of the antebellum literary culture of Philadelphia, a city that served as an important publishing center for American writers in the decades before the Civil War. Clark's early death cut short what contemporaries regarded as a promising literary career, and his work survives primarily through anthologies such as Martin's *Choice Specimens*.

## Literary Career

Clark's poetic output, though modest in volume, was diverse in subject matter — ranging from devotional verse to nature poetry to occasional pieces. His best-known poem, "An Invitation to Early Piety," exemplifies the religious lyric tradition in American poetry, urging youthful readers to embrace faith before the enchantments of the world fade: "Come, while the morning of thy life is glowing — / Ere the dim phantoms thou art chasing die." His verse employed conventional Romantic diction and meter, but his poems were distinguished by their sincerity of feeling and their lack of metaphysical pretension. Clark also wrote nature poetry, of which "Lines on a Shaded Brook" represents the reflective, meditative strain. As a journalist, he contributed editorials and literary criticism to the *Philadelphia Gazette*, helping to shape the literary taste of his adopted city. His twin brother Lewis Gaylord Clark's editorship of the *Knickerbocker* — one of the most influential literary magazines of the period — placed the Clark family at the center of American literary culture, though Willis himself remained in Philadelphia rather than New York.

## Selections in This Anthology

Benj. N. Martin's *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) includes two short poems by Willis G. Clark drawn from his *Literary Remains*:

1. **"An Invitation to Early Piety"** (072-024–072-025). A devotional lyric urging the reader to embrace religious faith in youth, before the illusions of worldly life fade. The poem's central imagery — morning, sunset, a "fadeless ray" of divine promise — draws on conventional Christian poetic tropes but deploys them with economy and grace. Clark's rhetorical question at the close — "Who would not *early* choose a lot like this?" — underscores his conviction that religious commitment, far from being a burden, is a liberation.

2. Two brief quatrains on nocturnal longing for the heavenly (072-027–072-028), meditating on tearful eyes and the "far-off spheres" of the afterlife.

> Come, while the morning of thy life is glowing — Ere the dim phantoms thou art chasing die; Ere the gay spell which earth is round thee throwing, Fade like the sunset of a summer sky. (072-024)

> Then will the crosses of this brief existence Seem airy nothings to thine ardent soul; And shining brightly in the forward distance, Will of thy patient race appear the goal. (072-025)

Clark's inclusion in Martin's anthology alongside much more prominent poets likely reflects both the genuine quality of his devotional verse and the editorial ties between the Clark brothers and the literary networks Martin drew upon.

## Critical Reception

Clark's posthumous reputation rested almost entirely on his brother Lewis's efforts to preserve and promote his work. The *Literary Remains* (1844) received respectful notices, and "An Invitation to Early Piety" was widely reprinted in religious anthologies and periodicals through the mid-nineteenth century. However, his early death and modest output prevented him from achieving the stature of contemporaries who lived to develop their craft over decades. Modern scholarship has largely overlooked Clark, though recent work on antebellum Philadelphia literary culture and the Clark brothers' role in American periodical publishing has begun to recover his context and significance.
