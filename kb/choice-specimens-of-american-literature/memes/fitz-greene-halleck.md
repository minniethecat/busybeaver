# Fitz-Greene Halleck

## Biography

Fitz-Greene Halleck (1790–1867) was an American poet and member of the Knickerbocker Group, a circle of New York-based writers that included Washington Irving and William Cullen Bryant. Born in Guilford, Connecticut, Halleck moved to New York City in 1811, where he worked as a clerk and later as a confidential secretary to the philanthropist John Jacob Astor — a position that provided him financial security and leisure for literary pursuits.

Halleck never married and lived a relatively quiet life. He traveled in Europe in the 1820s and maintained close friendships within the New York literary world, particularly with the poet Joseph Rodman Drake, with whom he collaborated on the satirical "Croaker Papers" published in the *New York Evening Post*. His later years were spent in Guilford, where he returned after Astor's death and lived in a house built for him by his former employer's estate.

## Literary Career

Halleck's most celebrated poem, "Marco Bozzaris" (1825), is a stirring tribute to the Greek revolutionary hero who fell in the War of Greek Independence. The poem became immensely popular in nineteenth-century America and was widely memorized by schoolchildren. Its opening lines — "Come to the bridal chamber, Death!" — were among the most quoted verses of the era.

His other major work, "Fanny" (1819), is a satirical poem in the manner of Byron's *Don Juan*, gently mocking the social pretensions of New York's fashionable elite. Halleck's verse is characterized by its polished technique, Byronic sensibility, and blend of heroic sentiment with worldly wit. Though his poetic output was modest in volume, his work was highly regarded by contemporaries including Edgar Allan Poe, who praised his "sweet and natural" versification.

## Critical Reception

During his lifetime, Halleck was considered one of America's foremost poets. "Marco Bozzaris" was a standard recitation piece in American schools throughout the nineteenth century. A statue of Halleck was erected in Central Park in 1877, making him one of the first American poets to be so honored. Later critical opinion has diminished his reputation, but his work remains an important example of the Byronic influence on early American poetry.
