# Richard Henry Wilde

## Biography

Richard Henry Wilde (1789–1847) was an American poet, lawyer, and politician. Born in Dublin, Ireland, he was brought to the United States as a child and raised in Augusta, Georgia. Largely self-educated, Wilde studied law and was admitted to the Georgia bar in 1809. He served as Attorney General of Georgia and was elected to the United States House of Representatives, where he served from 1815 to 1817 and again from 1827 to 1835.

Wilde's political career was interrupted by financial reversals, and he spent several years in Europe — notably in Italy, where he pursued scholarly research on the Renaissance poet Torquato Tasso. His study, *Conjectures and Researches Concerning the Love, Madness, and Imprisonment of Torquato Tasso* (1842), was a significant contribution to nineteenth-century Tasso scholarship. Wilde later served as a professor of law at the University of Louisiana (now Tulane) in New Orleans.

## Literary Career

Wilde's literary reputation rests primarily on a single lyric poem — "My Life is Like the Summer Rose" — which was widely anthologized throughout the nineteenth century. The poem belongs to the Romantic tradition of lyric melancholy, employing natural imagery to meditate on mortality, transience, and the yearning for sympathetic remembrance.

The poem's three stanzas each develop a simile — the summer rose, the autumn leaf, and footprints on a desert strand — to express the speaker's sense of isolation in the face of death:

> My life is like the summer rose
> That opens to the morning sky,
> But, ere the shades of evening close,
> Is scattered on the ground to die;
> Yet on that rose's humble bed
> The softest dews of night are shed,
> As if she wept such waste to see;
> But none shall drop a tear for me. (066-001)

The second stanza shifts the image from the garden to the forest, deepening the sense of inevitable decay:

> My life is like the autumn leaf
> That trembles in the moon's pale ray;
> Its hold is frail, its state is brief,
> Restless, and soon to pass away;
> But when that leaf shall fall and fade,
> The parent tree will mourn its shade,
> The winds bewail the leafless tree;
> But none shall breathe a sigh for me. (066-002)

The final stanza introduces a specific American landscape — "Tampa's desert strand" — grounding the poem's universal meditation in a recognizable place and giving it a distinctly national inflection:

> My life is like the print which feet
> Have left on Tampa's desert strand;
> Soon as the rising tide shall beat,
> Their track will vanish from the sand;
> Yet, as if grieving to efface
> All vestige of the human race,
> On that lone shore loud moans the sea;
> But none shall thus lament for me. (066-003)

The poem's refrain — the contrast between nature's solicitude for its own creations and the speaker's fear of being forgotten — gives it an emotional power that accounts for its enduring popularity in nineteenth-century anthologies. It is Wilde's most recognized work and was set to music by several composers.

Wilde also wrote a longer poem, *Hesperia*, and contributed to literary periodicals, but he published relatively little verse. His scholarly work on Tasso, while now largely superseded, was respected by contemporaries and reflects a mind drawn to the intersections of biography, literary creation, and the torments of the artistic temperament — themes that resonate with the melancholy of his own most famous lyric.

## Critical Reception

Wilde's "My Life is Like the Summer Rose" was among the most frequently anthologized American poems of the nineteenth century. Its musical quality and accessible melancholy won it a wide readership, and it was translated into several languages. However, Wilde's slender poetic output and the shift in taste away from Romantic lyricism meant that his reputation did not survive the nineteenth century. Rufus Wilmot Griswold included him in *The Poets and Poetry of America*, praising the "delicate sensibility" of his verse. Modern critics have noted the poem's place in the tradition of the American lyric and its subtle evocation of a specifically Southern coastal landscape. His Tasso scholarship has also attracted renewed attention from historians of Italian studies in America.
