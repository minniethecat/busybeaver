# John G. Saxe

## Biography

John Godfrey Saxe (1816–1887) was born in Highgate, Vermont, the son of a farmer and miller. He graduated from Middlebury College in 1839 and studied law, establishing a practice in St. Albans, Vermont, in 1843. Saxe was drawn early to local politics, serving as state's attorney for Franklin County and, in 1859, running unsuccessfully for governor of Vermont as a Democrat.

Saxe's true vocation, however, was literature. While still practicing law, he began contributing comic verse to *The Knickerbocker*, a New York literary magazine, where his witty, polished satires quickly attracted a national readership. His first collection, *Poems* (1849), was followed by several expanded editions, and by the 1850s Saxe was one of the most popular platform readers in America, sharing the lyceum circuit with the likes of Ralph Waldo Emerson and Oliver Wendell Holmes. His genial, self-deprecating manner and his gift for topical humor made him a favorite with audiences.

Personal tragedy and changing literary tastes dimmed Saxe's later years. A series of financial reverses, the death of several of his children, and a protracted public controversy over his political allegiances during the Civil War took their toll. By the time of his death in 1887, his brand of polished, good-natured satire had been largely displaced by the harder-edged humor of Mark Twain and the regional realists. Saxe died in Albany, New York, his reputation already fading into the half-light of minor literary celebrity.

## Literary Career

Saxe belongs to the tradition of light verse and social satire that flourished in mid-nineteenth-century America — a tradition that includes Holmes, James Russell Lowell, and the early Twain. His range extends from the purely comic ("The Proud Miss MacBride," "The Briefless Barrister") to the reflective ("The Blessings of Sleep"), and his technical facility — his command of rhyme, meter, and comic timing — is consistently impressive. Oliver Wendell Holmes praised Saxe as "one of the most dexterous and accomplished writers of verse in this country."

The selection from "Choice Specimens" identifies three Saxe compositions: "The Blessings of Sleep" (002-005), "Ye Tailyor man: a contemplative ballad" (002-006), and the frequently anthologized "Sonnet to a Clam" (002-009). This grouping illustrates the range of Saxe's comic art — from mock-heroic celebration of mundane subjects to the philosophical wit that characterized his most enduring work. "Sonnet to a Clam," with its mock-Petrarchan address to a shellfish, exemplifies Saxe's technique of applying elevated poetic diction to resolutely unelevated subjects, generating humor through the incongruity of form and content.

Saxe's verse was shaped by the lyceum circuit on which he performed it. His poems are designed for oral delivery — their rhythms are declamatory, their punch lines timed for live audience response, their diction clear and accessible. This performance orientation, which made Saxe enormously popular in his day, has contributed to his posthumous neglect; his poems, read silently on the page, can seem thin compared with the denser, more allusive verse of his Romantic contemporaries. Yet at their best, Saxe's comic poems achieve a lightness and precision that no American poet before the twentieth century surpassed.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "The Blessings of Sleep" (002-005) — A meditation in verse on the restorative powers of sleep.
- "Ye Tailyor man; a contemplative ballad" (002-006) — A comic ballad on the tailor's trade, written in dialect-tinged verse.
- "Sonnet to a Clam" (002-009) — Saxe's most famous comic sonnet, applying Petrarchan conventions to a mollusk with results at once absurd and oddly moving.
- "Ancient and Modern Ghosts contrasted" (002-007) — A satirical comparison of supernatural apparitions old and new.

## Critical Reception

Saxe's reputation declined more steeply after his death than that of almost any other mid-nineteenth-century American poet. The reasons are several: the eclipse of the comic verse tradition by realism and naturalism; the growing distinction, after the Civil War, between "serious" and "popular" literature; and the simple fact that light verse, however accomplished, rarely sustains the weight of academic canonization. By the early twentieth century, Saxe had been effectively erased from the standard literary histories.

Recent scholarship has begun to reconsider Saxe within the broader context of nineteenth-century print and performance culture. His work is now studied as an example of the ways in which poetry functioned as public entertainment in the antebellum United States — a mode of social performance rather than private meditation. The recovery of Saxe's verse, along with that of other platform poets, has enriched the understanding of a literary culture in which the boundaries between "serious" and "popular" were far more porous than later critics allowed.
