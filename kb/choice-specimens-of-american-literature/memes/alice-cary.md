## Biography

Alice Cary (1820–1871) was an American poet and writer, born near Cincinnati, Ohio.
She grew up on a farm in the Clovernook region, which later inspired her best-known prose sketches.
Together with her younger sister [[phoebe-cary]], she became a prominent figure in the New York literary scene,
hosting a celebrated Sunday evening salon attended by leading writers and editors of the day.

## Literary Career

Cary's literary career began with contributions to regional newspapers and journals.
Her first collection, *Poems of Alice and Phoebe Cary* (1850), was jointly published with her sister
and attracted favorable attention from Eastern critics including [[horace-greeley]] and [[rufus-w-griswold]].
She subsequently moved to New York City, where she published *Clovernook* (1852), a series of prose sketches
of rural Ohio life, and several volumes of poetry including *Lyrics and Hymns* (1866).

Her poetry is characterized by domestic and religious themes, with a lyrical quality influenced by
the sentimental tradition of mid-nineteenth-century American verse. Cary also contributed regularly
to periodicals including *The Atlantic Monthly* and *Harper's Magazine*.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes four selections from Cary:

- **"Clovernook"** (001-094): A prose sketch describing her childhood home and the natural surroundings of rural Ohio.
  The editor notes her skill in depicting the "quiet beauty" of pastoral life.

- **"Attractions of Rural Life"** (002-017): A prose passage on the pleasures of country living.

> The editor, Benj. N. Martin, includes Cary among the "Later Poets" section (section 49, 075-014),
> observing her "grace and sweetness of expression."

## Critical Reception

During her lifetime, Cary was widely read and respected. [[edgar-allan-poe]] praised her work
in his *Literati of New York* sketches. After her death, her literary reputation declined,
overshadowed by the rise of modernist poetics, but late twentieth-century scholarship on
nineteenth-century American women writers has revived interest in her contributions.

Cary's Sunday evening salon at her New York home on Twentieth Street became a noted gathering
place for literary figures including [[horace-greeley]], [[bayard-taylor]], and the
[[stoddard]] brothers.
