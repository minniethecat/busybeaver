# Lucy Hooper

## Biography

Lucy Hooper (1816–1841) was an American poet whose brief life and early death from tuberculosis made her a figure of romantic pathos in nineteenth-century literary memory. Born in Newburyport, Massachusetts, she was the daughter of a prosperous merchant. Hooper showed an early aptitude for writing, and her poems began appearing in periodicals while she was still in her teens.

Hooper's adult life was shaped and shortened by the illness — likely pulmonary tuberculosis — that had been diagnosed in her adolescence. She lived quietly in Newburyport and later in Brooklyn, New York, writing poetry during intervals of relative health. Her work reflects a sensibility marked equally by religious devotion and a keen, and at times melancholy, awareness of mortality — themes that her own circumstances made poignant rather than merely conventional.

She died in Brooklyn on August 1, 1841, at the age of twenty-five. Her poems were collected and published posthumously the following year under the title *The Complete Poetical Works of the Late Miss Lucy Hooper*, edited by John Keese, a New York publisher and anthologist. The volume went through several editions and preserved Hooper's name in the literary record for a generation.

## Literary Career

Hooper's literary output was modest in scale but consistent in quality. Her poems — mostly short lyrics, meditations, and occasional pieces — were published in periodicals including the *Knickerbocker* and *Godey's Lady's Book*, the leading literary magazines of her day. Her range was limited by her youth and illness, but within her chosen mode — the religious-meditative lyric — she achieved a distinctive voice.

"The Death-Summons" (sometimes titled "The Death-Bed") is Hooper's best-known poem and the selection anthologized in *Choice Specimens*. It is a first-person meditation on dying, sometimes read as a premonition of her own early death. The poem opens with the speaker hearing a "solemn voice" calling her to rest, and proceeds through a series of emotional movements — resistance, acceptance, and finally joyful anticipation of release:

> A voice is on mine ear — a solemn voice:
> I come, I come, it calls me to my rest;
> Faint not, my yearning heart; rejoice, rejoice;
> Soon shalt thou reach the gardens of the blest. (074-038)

The poem's structure mirrors the emotional arc of the dying speaker: from initial reluctance ("I would linger yet awhile, / Thinking o'er wasted hours, a weary train") to a transfigured understanding of death as liberation rather than loss. The final stanza arrives at an ecstatic note that is characteristic of nineteenth-century American deathbed piety:

> Sweet words — to die! O, pleasant, pleasant sounds,
> What bright revealings to my heart they bring;
> What melody, unheard in earth's dull rounds,
> And floating from the land of glorious Spring
> The eternal home!

The poem's emotional intensity, and the biographical fact of its author's youth and early death, gave "The Death-Summons" a pathos that ensured its place in anthologies and gift-books throughout the 1840s and 1850s. Hooper's work belongs to the tradition of American women's religious poetry — exemplified more substantially by Lydia Sigourney and the Cary sisters — in which piety, feeling, and literary ambition were understood to be mutually reinforcing rather than contradictory.

## Critical Reception

Hooper's posthumous collection was received warmly by contemporary reviewers, who praised the sincerity and refinement of her verse. The *Knickerbocker*, which had published many of her poems during her lifetime, noted that "she had a mind of a high order, and a heart of the purest and gentlest impulses." Her early death inevitably colored the reception of her work; she was often invoked as an exemplar of the fragile, sensitive poetess whose spiritual vision was clarified by suffering.

Hooper's reputation did not survive the nineteenth century. She is now remembered, if at all, as a footnote in the history of American women's poetry — one of many gifted writers of the antebellum period whose small bodies of work and early deaths prevented them from achieving lasting recognition. Her poems, though limited in range and ambition, remain competent and affecting examples of the religious lyric tradition in American literature.
