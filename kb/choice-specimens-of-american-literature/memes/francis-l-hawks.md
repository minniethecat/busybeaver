## Biography

Francis Lister Hawks (1798–1866) was an American Episcopal clergyman, historian, and educator.
Born in New Bern, North Carolina, he graduated from the University of North Carolina in 1815
and was admitted to the bar before turning to the ministry. Ordained as an Episcopal priest in 1827,
Hawks served as rector of several prominent parishes, including St. Thomas Church in New York City
and Christ Church in New Orleans.

In 1847, Hawks was elected the first president of the University of Louisiana (now Tulane University),
though his tenure was brief due to conflicts over the institution's direction. He returned to New York
and became a leading figure in the city's Episcopal community. Hawks was also deeply involved in the
church's missionary work and served as historiographer of the Protestant Episcopal Church.
His historical writings on the Anglican tradition in America were among the first scholarly treatments
of the subject.

## Literary Career

Hawks was one of the most industrious American editors and historians of his generation.
He compiled and edited the *Narrative of the Expedition of an American Squadron to the China Seas
and Japan* (1856), the official account of Commodore Matthew C. Perry's 1852–1854 expedition that
opened Japan to American trade. The work was based on Perry's journals and reports but shaped into
narrative form by Hawks, who never visited Japan himself.

Hawks also authored *The History of the Protestant Episcopal Church in the United States*
(2 vols., 1836–1839) and edited numerous periodicals. His writing combined meticulous research
with an engaging narrative style that appealed to both scholarly and popular audiences.
The Japan narrative, excerpted in this anthology, reflects Hawks's ability to synthesize
geographical, political, and cultural observations into a compelling account.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes one selection from Hawks:

- **"Japan Interesting in Many Aspects"** (041-001): The opening passage of the *Narrative of the
  United States Expedition to Japan*, in which Hawks surveys the various dimensions of Japan's
  significance to the Western observer:

> "Viewed in any of its aspects, the empire of Japan has long presented to the thoughtful mind
> an object of uncommon interest. And this interest has been greatly increased by the mystery
> with which, for the last two centuries, an exclusive policy has sought to surround the
> institutions of this remarkable country...." (041-001)

Hawks proceeds to enumerate the particular interests of the political inquirer, the student
of physical geography, and the commercial observer in opening Japan to Western contact.

## Critical Reception

Hawks's narrative of the Perry Expedition was widely read and translated into several European
languages, cementing the American public's fascination with Japan. His historical works on the
Episcopal Church were foundational texts that shaped denominational historiography for decades.
However, his controversial financial dealings and shifting ecclesiastical allegiances generated
criticism during his lifetime. Modern historians value his Japan narrative as both a literary
achievement and a document of mid-nineteenth-century American attitudes toward East Asia.
