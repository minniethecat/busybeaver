## Biography

Horace B. Wallace (1817–1852) was an American essayist, critic, and lawyer. A member of the
distinguished Wallace family of Philadelphia, he was educated at the University of Pennsylvania and
practiced law while contributing literary and art criticism to leading American periodicals. His death
at the age of thirty-five cut short a promising literary career.

## Literary Career

Wallace is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* by an essay titled "Art an
Emanation of the Affections" (001-087). The essay displays his wide-ranging intellect and engaging
prose style. A notable passage reflects on the desire for historical intimacy:

> What would we not give for a newspaper of the days of Homer, with personal recollections of the
> contractors and commanders in the siege of Troy; a reminiscence of Helen; the unedited fragments of
> Nestor; or a traditional saying of Ulysses, who may be supposed too wise to have published?
> (048-006)

## Selections in the Corpus

Wallace's essay appears in the corpus as an example of American critical prose (001-087). The selection
bridges classical literary reference with a distinctly modern sensibility about journalism and history.

## Critical Reception

Wallace was part of a circle of Philadelphia writers and critics who sought to elevate American literary
standards in the antebellum period. His work combines classical erudition with Romantic aesthetic
theory, reflecting the transatlantic intellectual currents of his time.
