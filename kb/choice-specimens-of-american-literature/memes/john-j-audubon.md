# John J. Audubon

## Biography

John James Audubon (1785–1851) was born Jean Rabin in Les Cayes, Saint-Domingue (now Haiti), the illegitimate son of a French naval captain and a Creole chambermaid who died shortly after his birth. Raised in Nantes, France, by his father's lawful wife, Audubon developed an early passion for the outdoors and for drawing birds — interests his father encouraged as a diversion from the military career planned for him. When conscription into Napoleon's army loomed, his father sent him to America in 1803 with a false passport, settling him on a farm at Mill Grove, near Philadelphia.

Mill Grove proved transformative. Audubon, already a skilled hunter and observer of nature, now found himself in a landscape teeming with birdlife unknown to European science. He conducted the first known bird-banding experiment in North America there, tying silver thread to the legs of Eastern Phoebes to determine whether they returned to the same nesting sites the following spring. In 1808, he married Lucy Bakewell, a neighbor's daughter who would become his indispensable partner — managing households, teaching, and supporting the family financially during his long absences in the field.

Audubon's life was one of restless wandering and repeated financial failure. A succession of business ventures in Kentucky and along the Ohio River frontier collapsed, culminating in bankruptcy and a brief imprisonment for debt in 1819. It was at this nadir that Audubon committed himself fully to his ornithological project: to paint every bird species in North America, life-size, in natural postures within their native habitats. For over a decade he traveled the continent — from the Florida Keys to Labrador, from the Atlantic coast to the Missouri River — supporting himself by painting portraits, giving drawing lessons, and occasionally dancing instruction. He died at his home on the Hudson River in 1851, his monumental work completed.

## Literary Career

Audubon's literary reputation rests on *The Birds of America* (1827–1838), published in four volumes of 435 hand-colored, life-size engravings, and the accompanying text, the *Ornithological Biography* (1831–1839), a five-volume prose work describing each species and recounting the adventures and observations of frontier life that attended its discovery. The *Ornithological Biography* is a literary achievement in its own right — a hybrid of scientific description, personal narrative, and frontier adventure that reads at times like a novel.

Audubon's prose is vivid, direct, and charged with the excitement of a man who saw the American wilderness before railroads and settlement transformed it. His accounts of westward emigration and frontier exploration similarly blend the naturalist's eye with the writer's sympathy for the human and animal lives being displaced.

## Critical Reception

Audubon occupies a unique position at the intersection of American art, science, and letters. During his lifetime, he was celebrated in Britain and Europe, where his engravings and his personal narrative of frontier adventure made him a romantic figure. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of London and the Royal Society of Edinburgh, and his *Birds of America* was hailed as one of the great publishing enterprises of the age.

In America, initial reception was cooler — partly owing to the rivalry of the Philadelphia scientific establishment, which favored the more systematic (if less artistic) ornithology of Alexander Wilson. Audubon's literary reputation was largely eclipsed in the century after his death, as scientific specialization separated the roles of naturalist and writer. However, the late twentieth century saw a revival of interest in Audubon as a literary figure, with scholars including Christoph Irmscher and Richard Rhodes emphasizing the narrative sophistication of the *Ornithological Biography* and the ecological prescience of his observations. His accounts of the passenger pigeon and the destruction of the American wilderness read today as prophetic documents of environmental consciousness.
