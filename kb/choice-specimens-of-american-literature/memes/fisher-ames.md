# Fisher Ames

## Biography

Fisher Ames (1758–1808) was an American politician and orator from Dedham, Massachusetts. A graduate of Harvard College in 1774, he served in the United States House of Representatives from 1789 to 1797 as one of the most articulate voices of the Federalist Party. Ames was renowned for his eloquence in defense of the Washington administration's policies, particularly on matters of public credit, treaty obligations, and foreign affairs. He retired from Congress due to ill health and spent his later years in Dedham, continuing to write political essays until his death at age fifty.

## Literary Career

Ames's reputation rests primarily on his oratory and political essays rather than published volumes. His speeches in Congress were widely admired for their rhetorical force, blending classical learning with passionate appeals to patriotism and national honor. His most celebrated addresses include his speech on the Jay Treaty (1796) and his reflections on patriotism and public faith. Ames's prose style is characterized by elaborate periodic sentences, classical allusions, and a conviction that republican government depends on the moral character of its citizens.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" includes several extracts from Ames's oratory and writings:

- "Obligation of Public Faith" (015-007–015-008) — From his speech on the Jay Treaty, arguing that treaty obligations are sacred and must be honored:
  > "I have seen, with a mixture of pleasure and apprehension, the progress of events which have lately taken place in your country. As a friend to mankind and to liberty, I rejoice in the efforts which you are making to establish it, while I fear much for the final success of the attempts..." (015-007)
  > "Before we resolve to leap into this abyss, so dark and so profound, it becomes us to pause, and reflect upon such of the dangers as are obvious and inevitable." (015-008)
- "What Is Patriotism?" (015-009) — A meditation on patriotism as "an extended self-love, mingling with all the enjoyments of life, and twisting itself with the minutest filaments of the heart."
- "Qualifications of a Minister of Foreign Affairs" (015-010) — A detailed portrait of the ideal diplomat: "He should know the human face and heart, and the connections between them; should be versed in the laws of nature and nations..."

## Critical Reception

Ames was celebrated by his Federalist contemporaries as one of the most gifted speakers of the early Republic. His speeches were studied as models of American eloquence well into the nineteenth century. Modern scholars, while acknowledging his rhetorical gifts, have noted the conservative cast of his thought — particularly his skepticism about popular democracy. His works remain valuable documents of Federalist political philosophy in the founding era.
