# John Henry Hobart

## Biography

John Henry Hobart (1775–1830) was an American Episcopal clergyman who served as the third bishop of the Diocese of New York from 1816 until his death. Born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, Hobart was educated at the College of Philadelphia (now the University of Pennsylvania) and the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University), graduating from the latter in 1793. He was ordained as a deacon in the Episcopal Church in 1798 and as a priest in 1800.

Hobart served successively as assistant minister of Trinity Church in New York, rector of Christ Church in New Brunswick, New Jersey, and assistant bishop of New York before becoming diocesan bishop in 1816. As described in the "Choice Specimens" editorial apparatus, he was "an eminent divine and bishop of the Episcopal church; a native of Pennsylvania." (006-004) His episcopate was marked by energetic efforts to expand the church: he founded the General Theological Seminary in New York City, established numerous new parishes, and was a tireless advocate for the distinctive doctrines and liturgy of the Episcopal tradition.

Hobart was a central figure in what became known as the "Hobartian" or high-church movement within American Episcopalianism. He emphasized apostolic succession, the authority of the Prayer Book, and the sacramental character of the church, while also championing personal piety and evangelical preaching. His theological writings and episcopal visitations helped define Episcopalian identity in the early American republic, at a time when the denomination was recovering from the disestablishment that followed the Revolution.

## Literary Career

Hobart's literary output was extensive and included sermons, theological treatises, devotional manuals, and controversial pamphlets. His published sermon collections — notably the two-volume *Sermons on the Principal Events and Truths of Redemption* (1824) — went through multiple editions and were widely read in both America and England. His prose style was characterized by rhetorical elevation, scriptural density, and a distinctive fusion of evangelical warmth with high-church dignity.

The selection in "Choice Specimens," titled "The Divine Glory in Redemption," is drawn from one of Hobart's sermons and exemplifies his characteristic mode of theological exposition. Hobart opens by invoking the cosmic rejoicing at creation — "the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy" — and argues that the work of redemption is an even greater display of divine glory:

> At the display of the divine power and glory that created the world, "the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy." Surely not less universal, not less ardent the exultation in those pure and perfect spirits that continually surround the Divine Majesty at the view of the infinite wisdom, love, and power which planned the redemption of a fallen world — which thus devised the mode by which pardon could be extended to the sinner without sanctioning his sin, and favor to the offending rebel against the divine government, without weakening its authority, impeaching its holiness, or subverting its justice. (006-004)

The central theological problem Hobart addresses — how God can pardon sin without compromising divine justice — is at the heart of Christian soteriology. His solution is characteristically Trinitarian: the "divine Persons" in council devise a plan in which "Holiness, and justice, and mercy are seen blending their sacred influences." The passage moves from the ineffable (the "inner glory of the divine nature" that "burns with a blaze… too intense, too radiant, for finite vision") to the accessible ("its manifestations, in its outer, its more distant rays… all is mildness, and softness, and peace"):

> God can be just, and yet justify the sinner. (006-004)

This aphoristic conclusion distills the entire theological argument into a single sentence. It captures a central paradox of Christian theology — the reconciliation of divine justice with divine mercy — and renders it with a clarity that reflects Hobart's gifts as both a systematic thinker and an effective communicator.

## Critical Reception

Hobart's influence on the American Episcopal Church was profound. His high-church principles, articulated in works such as *A Companion for the Altar* and *The Christian's Manual of Faith and Devotion*, shaped the theological trajectory of the diocese of New York for a generation and contributed to the emergence of a distinctively American expression of Anglican high-churchmanship. The General Theological Seminary, which he founded, became the flagship institution of Episcopalian theological education in the United States.

Hobart was also a controversial figure. His vigorous advocacy of episcopal authority and apostolic succession brought him into conflict with low-church and evangelical elements within his own denomination, and his pamphlet wars with Presbyterian and Congregational opponents were sharp. Yet even his adversaries acknowledged his personal piety, intellectual power, and pastoral dedication.
