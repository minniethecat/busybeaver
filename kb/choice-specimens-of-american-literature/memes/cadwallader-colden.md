# Cadwallader Colden

## Biography

Cadwallader Colden (1688–1776) was a Scottish-born physician, botanist, natural philosopher, historian, and colonial administrator who became one of the most intellectually versatile figures in eighteenth-century British America. Born in Ireland to Scottish parents, Colden was educated at the University of Edinburgh, where he studied medicine, mathematics, and natural philosophy. He emigrated to America in 1710, settling first in Philadelphia and then in New York, where he would spend the remainder of his long life in public service.

Colden's political career spanned more than half a century. He served as Surveyor General of New York, member of the Governor's Council, and, for many years, as Lieutenant Governor of the province — a position in which he repeatedly served as acting governor during interregnums. His tenure was marked by conflicts with the provincial assembly over issues of executive authority, and his Loyalist sympathies during the growing revolutionary crisis made him a controversial figure in his final years. He died in 1776 at the age of eighty-eight, just months before the British evacuated New York.

Beyond politics, Colden was a figure of genuine intellectual distinction. He corresponded with Benjamin Franklin, Carl Linnaeus, and other leading figures of the European Enlightenment. His botanical studies, particularly of the flora of New York, earned him international recognition; Linnaeus named the plant genus *Coldenia* in his honor. He was also among the first to propose a theory of gravitation that anticipated some aspects of Newtonian mechanics, though his scientific work in this area was ultimately superseded.

## Literary Career

Colden's most enduring literary work is *The History of the Five Indian Nations Depending on the Province of New-York in America* (1727, expanded 1747), the first systematic English-language account of the Iroquois Confederacy. Based on his direct experience with Iroquois diplomacy during negotiations at Albany and his careful study of indigenous political institutions, the work presented the Five Nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca) not as "savages" but as a sophisticated political confederation worthy of comparison to the ancient republics of Greece and Rome.

The passage selected for "Choice Specimens" — "Conviction of Their Superiority" — captures Colden's characteristic blend of ethnographic observation and political analysis. He describes the Iroquois's sense of their own preeminence with remarkable objectivity:

> The Five Nations think themselves by nature superior to the rest of mankind.... All the nations round them have, for many years, entirely submitted to them, and pay a yearly tribute to them in wampum; they dare neither make war nor peace without the consent of the Mohawks. (003-006)

Colden's description of Iroquois political authority is striking for its recognition of the informal but effective nature of indigenous governance:

> An old Mohawk Sachem, in a poor blanket and a dirty shirt, may be seen issuing his orders with as arbitrary an authority as a Roman dictator. (003-006)

But Colden's analysis goes beyond mere observation to draw a broader lesson about political culture. He recognizes that the Iroquois's power derives not from material resources but from deeply held values — particularly the thirst for military glory:

> It is not for the sake of tribute, however, that they make war, but from the notions of glory which they have ever most strongly imprinted on their minds; and the farther they go to seek an enemy, the greater glory they think they gain; there cannot, I think, be a greater or stronger instance than this, how much the sentiments impressed on a people's mind conduce to their grandeur. (003-006)

The passage concludes with a remarkable tribute that explicitly equates Iroquois martial virtue with that of classical antiquity:

> The Five Nations, in their love of liberty and of their country, in their bravery in battle, and their constancy in enduring torments, equal the fortitude of the most renowned Romans. (003-006)

This comparison of American Indians to the heroes of the Roman Republic was radical for its time and reflected Colden's Enlightenment conviction that political virtue was a universal human capacity, not the exclusive possession of any race or civilization. The passage had a significant afterlife in American letters; it was cited by later writers, including Benjamin Franklin, who drew on Colden's account of the Iroquois Confederacy in developing early proposals for colonial union.

## Critical Reception

Colden's *History of the Five Nations* was widely read in both America and Europe and went through multiple editions. Benjamin Franklin admired it and corresponded with Colden about its implications for colonial governance. In the twentieth century, scholars of Native American history recognized Colden as an important early ethnographer whose work, despite its limitations of perspective, preserved valuable information about Iroquois political institutions at a critical moment in their history.

Colden's broader reputation as a man of science has also been reassessed. While his gravitational theories did not prove influential, his botanical work was genuinely important, and his role as a colonial node in the transatlantic network of Enlightenment correspondence has drawn increasing scholarly attention. He represents the American Enlightenment at its most eclectic and intellectually ambitious.
