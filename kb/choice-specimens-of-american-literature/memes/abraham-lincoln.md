# Abraham Lincoln

## Biography

Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865) was the sixteenth President of the United States, serving from 1861 until his assassination in 1865. Born in a log cabin in Hardin County (now LaRue County), Kentucky, Lincoln was largely self-educated and worked as a rail-splitter, flatboatman, store clerk, and surveyor before studying law. He was admitted to the Illinois bar in 1836 and built a successful legal practice in Springfield.

Lincoln entered national politics as a Whig congressman from Illinois (1847–1849) and rose to prominence through his opposition to the Kansas-Nebraska Act and his debates with Senator Stephen A. Douglas in 1858. Elected president in 1860, he led the Union through the Civil War, issued the Emancipation Proclamation (1863), and delivered the Gettysburg Address (1863) — a brief speech that redefined the war as a struggle for human equality and national rebirth. He was assassinated by John Wilkes Booth on April 14, 1865, just days after the Confederate surrender at Appomattox.

## Literary Career

Lincoln's literary achievement rests on a relatively small body of work — speeches, letters, and official documents — that has exercised an influence on American prose unmatched by any other president. His writing is characterized by clarity, rhythmic power, and a deep engagement with the moral foundations of the American republic. The Gettysburg Address (272 words) and the Second Inaugural Address ("With malice toward none, with charity for all") are among the most analyzed and admired works in American literature.

Lincoln's prose style drew on the King James Bible, Shakespeare, and the legal rhetoric of his frontier practice. His ability to express complex ideas in language of lapidary simplicity and moral weight has been noted by writers from Walt Whitman to Edmund Wilson.

## Critical Reception

Lincoln's speeches and writings have been subjects of sustained literary study. His prose has been praised by critics including Edmund Wilson and Garry Wills for its concision, biblical cadences, and philosophical depth. The Gettysburg Address in particular has been described as a prose poem that re-founded the American republic on the principle of equality. Lincoln remains one of the most quoted and anthologized of all American writers.
