# Lydia H. Sigourney

Lydia Huntley Sigourney (1791–1865), widely known in her lifetime as "the Sweet Singer of Hartford," was the most commercially successful American woman poet of the antebellum period. Over a career spanning five decades, she published more than sixty books of poetry and prose, contributed regularly to leading periodicals, and became a household name whose verses on death, motherhood, nature, and domestic piety were read, recited, and memorized across the United States. Sigourney's work represents the fullest American expression of the [[sentimentalism|sentimental]] mode in poetry — a tradition that valued emotional sincerity, moral instruction, and the sanctity of the domestic sphere — and her career helped establish the model of the professional woman writer in a literary marketplace still dominated by men.

## Biography

Lydia Huntley was born in Norwich, Connecticut, on September 1, 1791, the only child of a gardener employed by the wealthy Lathrop family. Her father's employer, Mrs. Daniel Lathrop, recognized the girl's intelligence and took an interest in her education, providing her with access to books and tutors that would otherwise have been unavailable to a child of her station. This early patronage shaped Sigourney's lifelong conviction that education — particularly the education of women — was a moral imperative as well as a practical good.

In 1811, at the age of twenty, Huntley opened a school for young women in Norwich, and in 1814 she moved to Hartford, Connecticut, to establish a similar school there. Her first book, *Moral Pieces in Prose and Verse* (1815), was published with the encouragement of her Hartford patron, the poet and editor Daniel Wadsworth. In 1819 she married Charles Sigourney, a Hartford merchant, and at her husband's request initially curtailed her public literary activities, publishing anonymously or under pseudonyms. The financial pressures of the 1830s, however, led the family to rely on Sigourney's literary earnings, and from 1834 onward she published openly under her own name, becoming one of the most prolific and widely read authors in the United States.

Sigourney's fame extended beyond the literary sphere. She corresponded with leading political and cultural figures of her day, advocated for the education of the deaf (a cause she had embraced in her youth), and became a symbol of the cultivated American woman. She died in Hartford on June 10, 1865, having outlived most of her early literary contemporaries and having witnessed the transformation of American poetry from the neoclassical mode of her youth to the Romanticism of [[henry-wadsworth-longfellow|Longfellow]] and the [[fireside-poets|Fireside Poets]].

## Literary Career

Sigourney's literary output was staggering in its range and volume. She published collections of lyric and narrative poetry, including *Zinzendorff and Other Poems* (1836) and *Pocahontas and Other Poems* (1841); conduct books for young women, most notably *Letters to Young Ladies* (1833), which went through multiple editions and was widely used in female academies; travel sketches; temperance tracts; and a steady stream of elegiac verse occasioned by the deaths of public figures and private individuals alike. Her elegies, in particular, earned her a reputation as America's unofficial poet laureate of mourning — a role that reflected both the high mortality rates of the era and the cultural expectation that women were the natural custodians of grief and consolation.

Sigourney's poetry is characterized by a plain, accessible style, regular meter, and an unwavering commitment to moral and religious sentiment. Her subject matter — the death of children, the duties of wives and mothers, the beauty of the natural world as evidence of divine design, the virtues of the American republic — aligned with the dominant values of her middle-class readership. Critics of the twentieth century often dismissed her work as conventional and formulaic, but more recent scholarship has recovered Sigourney as a strategically astute literary professional who used the sentimental idiom to claim public authority for women's voices. Her poems on [[native-american-themes|Native American subjects]], including "Pocahontas" and "The Indian's Welcome to the Pilgrim Fathers," have attracted particular attention for their ambivalent treatment of conquest and assimilation.

## Selections in the Anthology

The *Choice Specimens of American Literature* anthology presents Sigourney through two contrasting selections: a satirical fragment on newspaper reading and the complete text of "Deal Gently with the Widow's Child," one of her most anthologized poems.

The satirical fragment (066-022–066-023) captures the voracious, undiscriminating appetite of the newspaper reader with a wit that is often overlooked in assessments of Sigourney's work:

> "Lively or sad, life's meanest, mightiest things, / The fate of fighting cocks, or fighting kings — / Nought comes amiss; we take the nauseous stuff, / Verjuice or oil, a libel or a puff." (066-023)

These lines, with their [[alexander-pope|Popean]] balance and their sharp eye for the absurdities of the print marketplace, reveal a side of Sigourney's talent — urbane, satirical, formally controlled — that the dominant image of the "Sweet Singer" has tended to obscure.

"Deal Gently with the Widow's Child" (066-024–066-030) is a poem of counsel addressed to a husband on the proper treatment of an adopted daughter — the "widow's child" of the title. The poem opens with a plea for gentleness that expands into a meditation on vulnerability, trust, and the sacred obligations of domestic life (066-024):

> "Deal gently, thou whose hand hath won / The young bird from its nest away, / Where, careless, 'neath a vernal sun, / She gayly carolled day by day; / The haunt is lone, the heart must grieve." (066-024)

Sigourney traces the arc of the girl's life within the adoptive home — her childhood devotion, her departure into the wider world, her marriage — and in each phase returns to the refrain of gentleness. The poem's emotional climax comes in its final stanza, where the speaker invokes the ultimate source of parental authority (066-030):

> "A mother yields her gem to thee, / On thy true breast to sparkle rare; / She places 'neath thy household tree / The idol of her fondest care; / And, by thy trust to be forgiven / When judgment wakes in terror wild, / By all thy treasured hopes of heaven, / Deal gently with the widow's child." (066-030)

The poem exemplifies Sigourney's characteristic strengths: a clear moral argument conducted through accessible imagery, an appeal to shared domestic experience, and a final turn toward eschatological seriousness that elevates the domestic counsel to the plane of spiritual obligation. The "widow's child" — doubly vulnerable, twice orphaned — becomes a figure for all who depend on the kindness of those who hold power over them.

## Critical Reception

In her lifetime, Sigourney enjoyed a readership that few American poets, male or female, could rival. Her books sold steadily, her poems were reprinted in newspapers across the country, and her name carried sufficient cultural weight that [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], in his "Literati of New York City" sketches, felt obliged to mention her among the notable authors of the day — albeit with characteristic ambivalence about the quality of her verse.

Twentieth-century criticism was largely dismissive. The rise of modernist aesthetics, with its suspicion of sentiment and its valorization of difficulty and indirection, made Sigourney's direct emotional appeals and conventional pieties seem embarrassingly naive. She was routinely omitted from anthologies and literary histories, or mentioned only as an example of the limitations of the "scribbling women" whom [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Nathaniel Hawthorne]] had famously disparaged.

The feminist recovery project of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries has substantially revised this judgment. Scholars including Nina Baym, Mary Loeffelholz, and Paula Bernat Bennett have argued that Sigourney's sentimentalism was not a failure of literary ambition but a deliberate rhetorical strategy — one that allowed a woman writer to intervene in public discourse on matters of national importance while remaining within the bounds of feminine propriety. Her poems on [[native-american-removal|Indian removal]], slavery, and the Civil War have been read as politically engaged interventions that used the language of domestic affection to critique state violence. This reappraisal has restored Sigourney to a central place in the history of [[american-womens-writing|American women's writing]] and the study of nineteenth-century [[print-culture|print culture]].
