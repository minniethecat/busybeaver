George Henry Calvert (1803–1889) was an American poet, essayist, and literary critic whose wide-ranging work encompassed nature writing, travel literature, and appreciations of European literary figures (041-009). A great-grandson of Lord Baltimore, the founder of Maryland, Calvert brought a transatlantic sensibility to his writing, dividing his time between his estate in Newport, Rhode Island, and extensive travels in Europe.

## Biography

Born in Mount Airy, Maryland, in 1803, Calvert was educated at Harvard College (class of 1823) and later at the University of Göttingen in Germany, where he absorbed the aesthetic philosophy of the German Romantics. He served as mayor of Newport, Rhode Island, and was active in New England literary circles, corresponding with figures such as [[Ralph Waldo Emerson]] and [[Henry Wadsworth Longfellow]]. His marriage to Elizabeth Steuart brought him into Maryland's landed gentry, but his intellectual allegiances were firmly with the New England literary renaissance.

## Literary Career

Calvert's literary output was substantial and diverse. His *Essays Aesthetical* (1875) collected his writings on art, literature, and taste, including his influential estimate of [[Samuel Taylor Coleridge]]. His travel books — *Scenes and Thoughts in Europe* (1846) and *First Years in Europe* (1866) — combined vivid descriptive passages with philosophical reflection. As a poet, he published several volumes including *Poems* (1847) and *The Nation's Birth and Other Poems* (1876), though his verse has been less enduring than his prose. His nature writing, grounded in close observation of the New England landscape, reveals the influence of both [[Washington Irving]] and the German Romantic aesthetic tradition.

## Selections

The anthology features two selections from Calvert: a lush description of the American forest landscape in autumn and a nuanced appreciation of Coleridge's intellectual legacy.

### The American Forest Landscape

> [041-009] The multitude of species, intermixed as they are in their spontaneous growth, gives the American forest landscape a variety of aspect not often seen in the woods of Europe; and the gorgeous tints which nature repeats from the dying dolphin to paint the falling leaf of the American maples, oaks, and ash trees, clothe the hill-sides and fringe the watercourses with a rainbow splendor of foliage, unsurpassed by the brightest groupings of the tropical flora.

### Estimate of Coleridge

> [041-010] That Coleridge with his mental pockets full of gold, and with a mine in fee wherefrom he not only replenished his daily purse but enriched his neighbors, should have taken what belonged to others, might be hard to believe, were not the fact undeniable. Let no man's greatness be a bar to full utterance; but let temperance and charity — duties peculiarly imperative when uttering derogatory truth — be scrupulously observed.

> [041-011] The writings of Coleridge — in tone high, refined, noble; in expression rich, choice, copious; in spirit as pure as the sun's light; intellectually one of the master-works of man — are fraught with pregnant thought and an insight which gives them the force of revelation. As for Coleridge himself, his is an immortal name; and as he walks through the ages his robes adjusting themselves with varying grace, in harmony with the changing aesthetic sense of successive generations, he will command the homage of the purest and the wisest.

## Critical Reception

Calvert was respected by his New England contemporaries as a cultivated man of letters, though his reputation never attained the heights of the major Transcendentalists. His study of Coleridge was one of the earliest serious American appreciations of the English Romantic poet-philosopher, and his essays on aesthetics contributed to the development of American art criticism. Modern scholars have noted Calvert's role as a transatlantic cultural mediator, introducing German aesthetic ideas to American readers while celebrating the distinctive character of the American landscape.
