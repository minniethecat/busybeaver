## Biography

John Heckewelder (1743–1823) was a Moravian missionary and ethnographer who spent much of his life among the Native American peoples of the Ohio Country and the Great Lakes region. Born in Bedford, England, to German Moravian parents, he emigrated to Pennsylvania in 1754. As a young man he accompanied Moravian missionaries to the frontier, learning the Delaware (Lenape) language and the customs of the tribes with whom the Moravian Brethren had established missions. (024-013)

Heckewelder's intimate knowledge of Native American languages and cultures made him one of the most respected authorities on the subject in the early American republic. He served as an intermediary between the U.S. government and Native American tribes and was consulted by scholars and statesmen, including Thomas Jefferson and the historian George Bancroft.

## Literary Career

Heckewelder's major literary contributions were his narratives of Moravian missionary work and his ethnographic studies of Native American peoples. His *Narrative of the Mission of the United Brethren among the Delaware and Mohegan Indians* (1819) recounted the history of the Moravian missions from their founding through the period of the American Revolution. (024-013)

His most influential work, *History, Manners, and Customs of the Indian Nations Who Once Inhabited Pennsylvania and the Neighbouring States* (1818), was a comprehensive ethnographic account that influenced James Fenimore Cooper's portrayal of Native Americans in the Leatherstocking Tales. Heckewelder's writings combined careful observation with genuine sympathy for the peoples he described.

## Selections in the Corpus

Heckewelder appears in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* through an excerpt from his *Narrative of the Moravian Missions*, titled "Settlements of the Christian Indians" (selection 112).

> Both these congregations, being supplied with missionaries and schoolmasters, were so prosperous that they became the admiration of visitors, some of whom thought it next to a miracle that, by the light of the gospel, a savage race should be brought to live together in peace and harmony, and above all devote themselves to religion. (024-011)

> In February of the year 1761, a white man, who had lost a child, came to Nain weeping, and begging that the Indian Brethren would assist him and his wife to search for his child, which had been missing since the day before. Several of the Indian Brethren immediately went to the house of the parents, and discovered the footsteps of the child, and tracing the same for the distance of two miles, found the child in the woods, wrapped up in its petticoat, and shivering with cold. (024-011)

> The war being over, the Indians who had been engaged in it freely confessed to their friends and relations, and to some white people they had heretofore been acquainted with, that "the Brethren's settlements had been as a stumbling-block to them; that had it not been for these, they would most assuredly have laid waste the whole country from the mountains to Philadelphia." (024-012)

The selection illustrates both the peaceful character of the Moravian missions and the respect that the Christianized Indians earned from neighboring white settlers — as well as the confessions of hostile Native Americans who acknowledged that the mission settlements acted as a buffer protecting the frontier.

## Style and Themes

Heckewelder wrote in a plain, unadorned style characteristic of missionary narratives, but his close observation and linguistic precision elevated his work above mere religious reportage. His ethnographic writings display a deep respect for Native American cultures and an effort to understand them on their own terms — a relatively unusual perspective for his era. His work is valuable both as a record of Moravian missionary activity and as an early contribution to American ethnography.

## Reception and Legacy

Heckewelder's writings were widely read in both America and Europe. *History, Manners, and Customs of the Indian Nations* went through multiple editions and was translated into German and French. His influence extended beyond ethnography into literature: James Fenimore Cooper drew on Heckewelder's work for his portrayal of Native American characters, and the historian Francis Parkman also consulted his writings, though sometimes critically.

Heckewelder died in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, in 1823. His papers are preserved in the archives of the Moravian Church and remain an important source for the study of Native American history and culture in the Ohio Country.
