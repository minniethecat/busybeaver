# John Dickinson

## Biography

John Dickinson (1732–1808) was an American lawyer, politician, and writer from Philadelphia. He studied law at the Middle Temple in London before returning to practice in Pennsylvania. Dickinson served as a delegate to the Continental Congress from both Pennsylvania and Delaware, and was a member of the Constitutional Convention of 1787. Though he opposed declaring independence in 1776 — believing reconciliation with Britain was still possible — he nevertheless served the revolutionary cause and later the new republic with distinction. He died at his home in Wilmington, Delaware.

## Literary Career

Dickinson earned the title "Penman of the Revolution" for his series of twelve essays, *Letters from a Farmer in Pennsylvania* (1767–1768), which argued against the Townshend Acts and became among the most widely read political tracts of the colonial period. His writing was marked by clarity, moderation, and a lawyerly precision that appealed to a broad readership. Dickinson also drafted several important state papers for the Continental Congress, including the *Olive Branch Petition* (1775). His later writings addressed the ratification of the Constitution and the proper relationship between church and state.

## Selections in This Anthology

The anthology includes Dickinson's address to the constituents of the Continental Congress, delivered during the Era of the Revolution:

- "Address to the Constituents" (013-003–013-006) — Dickinson exhorts fellow citizens to persevere in the struggle for liberty:
  > "To our constituents we submit the propriety and purity of our intentions, well knowing they will not forget that we lay no burdens upon them but those in which we participate with them — a happy sympathy, that pervades societies formed on the basis of equal liberty." (013-003)
  > "Persevere, and you insure peace, freedom, safety, glory, sovereignty, and felicity to yourselves, your children, and your children's children." (013-005)

The closing exhortation (013-006) calls upon citizens to "diligently promote piety, virtue, brotherly love, learning, frugality, and moderation."

## Critical Reception

Dickinson's *Letters from a Farmer in Pennsylvania* were celebrated by contemporaries as a masterful statement of American rights and grievances. Historians regard Dickinson as a moderate voice who sought to bridge the gap between radical patriots and conciliatory loyalists. His refusal to sign the Declaration of Independence — a principled stand based on timing rather than principle — has sometimes obscured his substantial contributions to the founding. Modern scholarship has restored his reputation as one of the most important political writers of the revolutionary era.
