# Civic Duty in American Literature

## Definition
Civic duty, in American literary tradition, encompasses the responsibilities of citizens in a republic: obedience to just law, participation in public deliberation, defense of liberty, and contribution to the common welfare. American writers treated civic duty not as optional virtue but as the necessary foundation of self-government.

## Historical Context
Puritan writers conceived of civic duty in covenantal terms—as mutual obligation binding members of a godly commonwealth. John Winthrop's "True Liberty Defined" (004-001) articulated the relationship between liberty and obligation: true freedom is not license but the liberty to fulfill one's duties to God and community. Revolutionary-era writers reframed civic duty in republican terms: a free people must be an engaged people, or liberty would perish. Jonathan Edwards warned that citizens "dead to all sense of honor and love of their country" threatened the survival of the Republic (015-002).

## Key Authors and Works
- **John Winthrop** defined civic duty as the willing submission to moral authority, distinguishing the liberty to do good from the license to do as one pleases (004-001).
- **Timothy Dwight** connected civic duty to national happiness, arguing that a nation "consecrated to truth, to virtue, and to happiness" required active citizen participation in public life (005-012).
- **Jonathan Edwards** called citizens to vigilance, warning that indifference to the "calls of liberty, virtue, and religion" endangered the providential experiment of American self-government (015-001, 015-002).

## Legacy
The tradition of civic duty in American letters continued through the 19th century in debates over slavery (where both sides invoked civic duty), the Civil War (which tested the limits of civic obligation), and the Reconstruction amendments (which redefined the scope of citizenship). Writers from Lincoln to Whitman reflected on what citizens owe their country and what their country owes them.
