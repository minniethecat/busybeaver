# William Ware

## Biography

William Ware (1797–1852) was an American Unitarian clergyman and novelist. Born in Hingham, Massachusetts, he graduated from Harvard College in 1816 and entered the Unitarian ministry. He served congregations in New York City and later in Cambridge, Massachusetts.

Ware's literary career grew out of his ministerial vocation. His novels were conceived as vehicles for religious instruction, dramatizing the early Christian experience for a nineteenth-century American readership. He wrote with a pastor's concern for moral edification and a scholar's interest in classical antiquity.

## Literary Career

Ware's first and most successful novel, *Zenobia, or the Fall of Palmyra* (1837), was originally published as a series of letters in the *Knickerbocker Magazine*. Set in third-century Syria, the novel tells the story of the conflict between the Palmyrene queen Zenobia and the Roman emperor Aurelian, with the emergence of Christianity as a backdrop. The book was praised for its vivid reconstruction of the ancient world and its dignified, if sometimes stilted, prose.

*Aurelian, or Rome in the Third Century* (1838) continued the story, following the persecution of Christians under the emperor Aurelian. Both novels found a wide readership in America and England and were translated into several European languages. Ware's third novel, *Julian, or Scenes in Judea* (1841), completed what amounted to a trilogy on the early Church.

## Selections

In the *Choice Specimens* anthology, Ware is represented by "The Christian Martyr" (059-010), a dramatic scene from *Aurelian*. The passage describes a Christian martyr facing a lion in the Roman arena:

> When now he had stood there not many minutes, one of the doors of the vivaria was suddenly thrown back, and bounding forth with a roar that seemed to shake the walls of the theatre, a lion of huge dimensions leaped upon the arena. (059-010)

Ware contrasts the lion's "gentle eye and noble carriage" with the "human monsters assembled to behold a solitary, unarmed man torn limb from limb" — a moral inversion typical of early Christian martyr narratives. The scene is preceded in the anthology by a passage describing the oratorical manner of William Wirt (059-009), whose "smooth, polished, scholar-like" eloquence provides an interesting counterpoint to Ware's dramatic prose.

## Critical Reception

Ware's novels were among the most popular American historical fictions of the antebellum period. Though they have fallen out of the literary canon, they represent an important chapter in the development of the American historical novel and the tradition of religious fiction. Modern scholars have noted Ware's skillful use of epistolary form and his nuanced treatment of the relationship between pagan and Christian cultures.
