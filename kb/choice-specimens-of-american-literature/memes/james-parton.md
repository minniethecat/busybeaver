# James Parton

## Biography

James Parton (1822–1891) was an American biographer and historian, widely regarded as one of the first professional biographers in the United States. Born in Canterbury, England, he was brought to America as a child and grew up in New York state. Parton was largely self-educated; his formal schooling ended early, but his voracious reading and facility with languages equipped him for a literary career.

Parton married the writer Sara Payson Willis, who under the pen name Fanny Fern became one of the most widely read newspaper columnists in America. Their marriage in 1856 united two of the most commercially successful writers of the period, though the union also exposed Parton to the gossip and notoriety that surrounded his famously outspoken wife.

As a biographer, Parton adhered to a principle of thorough documentation and impartial judgment. He believed that biography should neither flatter nor condemn, but understand — and he brought to this task an immense capacity for archival research and a style that combined narrative momentum with psychological penetration. His biography of Andrew Jackson (three volumes, 1859–1860) was a landmark in American historical writing, combining deep research in official records with vivid personal detail.

## Literary Career

Parton's most celebrated works are his multi-volume biographies: *Life of Andrew Jackson* (1859–1860), *Life and Times of Benjamin Franklin* (1864), *Life of Thomas Jefferson* (1874), and *Life of Voltaire* (1881). He also wrote *The Life and Times of Aaron Burr* (1858), from which the selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* is drawn.

Parton was a pioneer of the "life and times" approach — embedding his subjects so deeply in their historical context that the biography became, in effect, a history of the period. His research methods were unusually rigorous for a popular writer; he traveled to interview surviving witnesses, combed through newspaper archives and government records, and corresponded extensively with descendants and associates of his subjects.

The selection in this anthology offers Parton's assessment of Aaron Burr's failed political career. Rather than merely condemning Burr for his conspiracy trial and the Hamilton duel, Parton trains a compassionate eye on the arc of a wasted life:

> We all know that his life was an unhappy failure. He failed to gain the small honors at which he aimed; he failed to live a life worthy of his opportunities; he failed to achieve a character worthy of his powers. It was a great, great pity. (024-003)

Parton's diagnosis is succinct and devastating:

> The great error of his career, as before remarked, was his turning politician. He was too good for a politician, and not great enough for a statesman. (024-004)

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Career and Character of Aaron Burr* (024-003, 024-004) — From *The Life and Times of Aaron Burr* (1858). A moral and psychological portrait of Burr's failed career, in which Parton balances condemnation of Burr's treachery with compassion for a life of wasted talent. He traces Burr's failure to a single root cause — the decision to enter politics — and then broadens the reflection into a meditation on judgment itself: "And what occasion is there for judging him, or for judging any one?"

## Critical Reception

Parton was among the most successful nonfiction writers of his generation. His biographies sold widely and were reprinted throughout the nineteenth century; his *Life of Andrew Jackson* was considered the definitive account for decades, and his *Benjamin Franklin* was praised for its balanced portrayal of a figure too often treated hagiographically. The *North American Review* and *The Atlantic Monthly* reviewed his work favorably, and he was elected to the American Antiquarian Society.

Parton's reputation declined in the twentieth century as academic historians, with access to new archives and more rigorous methodologies, supplanted his once-definitive biographies. More recently, however, scholars have reassessed Parton as a transitional figure — the bridge between the amateur gentleman-historians of the early republic and the professional academic historians of the late nineteenth century. His insistence on documentary evidence and his psychological realism anticipated modern biographical practice.
