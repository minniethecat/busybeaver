# Thomas Starr King

## Biography

Thomas Starr King (1824–1864) was an American Unitarian minister and orator who became one of California's most influential voices during the Civil War. Born in New York City to a Universalist minister father, King was largely self-educated. He followed his father into the ministry and served congregations in Massachusetts before accepting a call to the First Unitarian Church of San Francisco in 1860.

In California, King emerged as a powerful pro-Union orator, crisscrossing the state to rally support for the Union cause. His efforts are credited with keeping California in the Union. He died of diphtheria at the age of thirty-nine. His collected writings, published posthumously as *Patriotism and Other Papers* (1864), preserve his gifts as a writer who fused moral conviction with a keen observation of the natural world.

## Literary Career

King's literary output reflects his dual calling as a preacher and a public intellectual. His essays and addresses draw on both Christian doctrine and the Romantic appreciation of nature — a synthesis characteristic of mid-nineteenth-century liberal Protestant thought. In "Great Principles and Small Duties," his most frequently anthologized essay, King argues that moral perfection is achieved not through grand, heroic deeds but through fidelity to everyday obligations.

His prose is marked by a distinctive use of natural analogy to illuminate moral truths:

> If we go to Nature for our morals, we shall learn the necessity of perfection in the smallest act. Infinite skill is not exhausted nor concentrated in the structure of a firmament, in drawing the orbit of a planet, in laying the strata of the earth, in rearing the mountain cone. The care for the bursting flower is as wise as the forces displayed in the rolling star; the smallest leaf that falls and dies unnoticed in the forest is wrought with a beauty as exquisite as the skill displayed in the sturdy oak. (037-001)

King extends this argument by invoking a classical allusion — the Platonic myth of souls choosing their next life — to suggest that wisdom naturally gravitates toward the humble and the ordinary rather than the conspicuous:

> The Greek philosopher, Plato, has left an instructive and beautiful poetic picture of the judgment of souls ... The dazzling and showy fortunes, the lives of kings and warriors and statesmen were soon exhausted; and the spirit of Ulysses, who had been the wisest prince among all the Greeks, came last to choose. He advanced with sorrow, fearing that his favorite condition had been selected by some more fortunate soul who had gone before him. But, to his surprise and pleasure, Ulysses found that the only life which had not been chosen was the lot of an obscure and private man, with its humble cares and quiet joys. (037-002)

The essay concludes by likening religious virtue to "the touch of Midas," which "converts the humblest call of conscience into spiritual gold" (037-001). This fusion of classical allusion, natural observation, and Christian moralism is the hallmark of King's literary style.

## Critical Reception

King was celebrated in his lifetime as one of the most eloquent speakers in America. His San Francisco orations drew immense crowds, and his death prompted public mourning across California. A mountain peak in Yosemite — Mount Starr King — and a giant sequoia were named in his honor. While his literary reputation has dimmed relative to his contemporaries, his essays continue to be studied as examples of the moral-aesthetic tradition in American letters, and his place in California history remains secure.
