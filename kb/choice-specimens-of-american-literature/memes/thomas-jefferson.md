# Thomas Jefferson

## Biography

Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826) was an American statesman, diplomat, philosopher, and architect who served as the third President of the United States (1801–1809). Born at Shadwell, Virginia, Jefferson was educated at the College of William & Mary and read law under George Wythe. He was a delegate to the Continental Congress, the principal author of the Declaration of Independence (1776), Governor of Virginia (1779–1781), minister to France (1785–1789), the first Secretary of State (1790–1793), and Vice President under John Adams (1797–1801).

As president, Jefferson oversaw the Louisiana Purchase (1803), which doubled the territory of the United States, and commissioned the Lewis and Clark Expedition. After his presidency, he retired to Monticello, his Virginia plantation, where he pursued his wide-ranging intellectual interests and founded the University of Virginia (1819). Jefferson died on July 4, 1826 — the fiftieth anniversary of the Declaration of Independence — on the same day as John Adams.

## Literary Career

Jefferson's literary legacy is vast and varied. The Declaration of Independence — drafted "from the fullness of his own mind" and submitted to Franklin and Adams for minor corrections (029-001) — remains one of the most influential political documents in world history. His prose style, characterized by clarity, balance, and moral seriousness, established a standard for American political writing.

Beyond his political writings, Jefferson was a man of Enlightenment breadth. His *Notes on the State of Virginia* (1785) is a comprehensive survey of the natural history, geography, economy, and society of his native state, written in response to queries from a French diplomat. The anthology "Choice Specimens of American Literature" draws from this work, including Jefferson's observations on the geographical limits of the elephant and the mammoth:

> From the thirtieth degree of south latitude to the thirtieth of north are nearly the limits which nature has fixed for the existence and multiplication of the elephant known to us. Proceeding thence northwardly to thirty-six and a half degrees, we enter those assigned to the mammoth. (014-003)

Jefferson's biographical sketch of George Washington — included in the anthology — offers a uniquely intimate portrait of the first president by a contemporary who knew him well. Jefferson wrote that Washington's "character was in its mass, perfect; in nothing, bad; in few points indifferent; and it may truly be said that never did nature and fortune combine more perfectly to make a man great" (014-006). Jefferson's correspondence, particularly his late-life exchange with John Adams, is regarded as one of the treasures of American letters.

## Selections in This Anthology

The following selections from Thomas Jeffersons writings appear in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874), edited by Benj. N. Martin:

- *Essential Principles of American Government* (014-001–014-002) — From Jeffersons "Notes on Virginia" and other writings, articulating his vision of limited government and constitutional liberty.
- *Geographical Limits of the Elephant and the Mammoth* (014-003–014-004) — From "Notes on the State of Virginia" (1781), an example of Jeffersons scientific curiosity and his contributions to early American natural history.
- *Character of Washington* (014-005–014-006) — From Jeffersons correspondence, a personal assessment of George Washingtons character and leadership during the Revolutionary War.

## Critical Reception

Jefferson's reputation as a writer has always been intertwined with his political legacy. The Declaration of Independence is universally celebrated for its eloquent articulation of natural rights; less widely read but equally significant are his *Notes on the State of Virginia*, his inaugural addresses, and his voluminous correspondence. Modern scholars have grappled with the profound contradiction between Jeffersons eloquent advocacy for liberty and his ownership of enslaved people — a tension that has fueled some of the most important debates in American historiography. As a stylist, Jefferson remains a model of Enlightenment prose: lucid, measured, and elevated without being pompous.
