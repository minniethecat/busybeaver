# Philip Freneau

## Biography

Philip Morin Freneau (1752–1832) was an American poet, journalist, and sea captain, widely known as the "Poet of the American Revolution." Born in New York City to a Huguenot family of moderate wealth, Freneau was educated at the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University), where he was a classmate and close friend of James Madison.

Freneau's career was shaped by the revolutionary ferment of his time. His earliest poems, written at Princeton, were satirical and political — exercises in the neoclassical mode that he would later turn to revolutionary purposes. During the War of Independence, he served as a privateer and was captured by the British, an experience that deepened his republican convictions and provided material for his most famous prison-ship poem, "The British Prison-Ship" (1781).

After the war, Freneau alternated between sea voyages as a ship captain and periods of intense journalistic activity. In 1791, Thomas Jefferson, then Secretary of State, brought Freneau to Philadelphia to edit the *National Gazette*, a newspaper that became the leading voice of the Democratic-Republican opposition to Alexander Hamilton and the Federalists. Freneau's editorial attacks on Hamilton and the Washington administration were so sharp that Washington is said to have referred to him as "that rascal Freneau."

He retired from journalism after 1800 and spent his later years at his New Jersey farm, where he continued to write poetry and died — according to legend, lost in a snowstorm — in 1832.

## Literary Career

Freneau's poetry falls into two broad categories: the political verse for which he is most famous, and the nature poetry and meditative lyrics that reveal a more private sensibility. His Revolutionary War poems — "The British Prison-Ship," "To the Memory of the Brave Americans," and numerous occasional satires — earned him the title "Poet of the American Revolution" and made him a household name among patriots.

After the war, Freneau's subjects broadened. Poems such as "The Wild Honey Suckle" (1786) and "The Indian Burying Ground" (1787) display a lyric grace and a sympathy for the American landscape and its indigenous inhabitants that anticipates the Romantic movement. "On the Emigration to America and Peopling the Western Country" is a vision of American expansion in Miltonic blank verse.

Freneau is listed in the *Choice Specimens* Table of Contents as "Philip Freneau, 1752-1832" with the selection "From 'An Indian Burying-ground'" (001-172, 318).

## Selections in This Anthology

- *From "An Indian Burying-ground"* (064-030, 318) — One of Freneau's most celebrated lyrics, meditating on Native American burial customs as evidence of the soul's immortality:

> In spite of all the learned have said,
> I still my old opinion keep;
> The posture that we give the dead,
> Points out the soul's eternal sleep.
> (064-031)

> Not so the ancients of these lands; —
> The Indian, when from life released,
> Again is seated with his friends,
> And shares again the joyous feast.
> (064-032)

> His imaged birds, and painted bowl,
> And venison, for a journey dressed,
> Bespeak the nature of the soul, —
> Activity, that wants no rest.
> (064-033)

The poem challenges European learning with what Freneau presents as the intuitive wisdom of Native American culture. The Indian, buried seated with his weapons and provisions, testifies not to materialism but to a vision of the afterlife in which the soul continues its earthly activities — "Activity, that wants no rest." This is one of the earliest American poems to treat Native American belief with philosophical seriousness rather than ethnographic condescension.

## Critical Reception

Freneau was a celebrity in his own revolutionary era — read, quoted, and attacked in the partisan press of the early republic. Madison, Jefferson, and other leading figures of the Democratic-Republican party regarded him as an essential voice in the struggle for American self-definition. His critics among the Federalists, by contrast, saw him as a reckless polemicist.

His posthumous reputation has been uneven. The Romantics of the next generation — particularly John Greenleaf Whittier and William Cullen Bryant — acknowledged him as a precursor, and Bryant's "Thanatopsis" owes a clear debt to Freneau's grave-meditations. In the twentieth century, Freneau was canonized as a significant early American poet; his nature lyrics, rather than his political satires, have proved his most enduring legacy.
