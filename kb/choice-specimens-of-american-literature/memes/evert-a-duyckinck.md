# Evert A. Duyckinck

## Biography

Evert Augustus Duyckinck (1816–1878) was an American editor, literary critic, biographer, and essayist — one of the most important literary tastemakers of mid-nineteenth-century America. Born in New York City, he was the son of Evert Duyckinck Sr., a prominent publisher and bookseller, and inherited both his father's business connections and his passion for literature. He was educated at Columbia College, graduating in 1835, and subsequently studied law, though he never practiced — literature claimed him entirely.

In partnership with his brother George L. Duyckinck, he edited the influential literary journal *Arcturus* (1840–1842), a magazine that championed American literary nationalism at the moment when it was first becoming self-conscious. The journal published work by Nathaniel Hawthorne, Edgar Allan Poe, James Russell Lowell, and Herman Melville — the last of whom became one of Duyckinck's closest literary friends. Duyckinck was Melville's primary New York literary connection during the composition of *Moby-Dick* and provided crucial editorial and intellectual encouragement.

After *Arcturus* ceased, Duyckinck became the literary editor of the *Literary World* (1847–1853), where he continued to shape the national conversation about American literature. His most ambitious project was the *Cyclopædia of American Literature* (1855), co-edited with his brother — a two-volume work that was the most comprehensive survey of American writing yet attempted, containing biographical sketches and selections from over five hundred authors.

## Literary Career

Duyckinck's gifts were critical rather than creative. His talent lay in recognizing literary quality, fostering talent, and articulating the emerging identity of American literature. His essay on "Newspapers" — the selection anthologized in *Choice Specimens* — is characteristic of his method: it takes a familiar, even mundane, cultural artifact and elevates it to philosophical significance:

> A newspaper is in some measure a picture of human life, and we can no more read its various paragraphs with pleasure, than we can look back upon the events of any single day with unmingled satisfaction. (048-002)

The essay argues that the newspaper is not merely a vehicle for information but a literary form in its own right — a daily epic of ordinary existence. With characteristic wit, Duyckinck dashes off an irreverent history of pre-journalistic literature, imagining the Roman poet hunting for an audience "when the wind was quiet" and Pythagoras claiming "a golden leg" to prove his divinity. In an age before newspapers, he suggests, the literary life was a precarious business:

> Doubtless it was a very satisfactory thing for a Roman poet, when the wind was quiet, to get an audience about him, under a portico, and unwind his well-written scroll for an hour or two; but there must have been a vast deal of secret machinery, and influence, and agitation, to keep up his name with the people. (048-002)

Duyckinck's broader contribution to American letters is difficult to overstate. As editor of *Arcturus* and the *Literary World*, he was among the first to argue that American literature was not merely an offshoot of English tradition but a distinct national enterprise. His *Cyclopædia* was a landmark of American literary historiography, and his personal library — one of the finest in New York — was a resource for the entire literary community. He was, in the best sense, a man of letters in an age when that title still meant something.

## Selections in This Anthology

- "Newspapers" (048-002) — From *Arcturus*. An essay that transforms the daily newspaper into a metaphor for human life itself — "a picture of motley human life; a poet's thought ... in one column, and the condemnation of a pickpocket in another." Duyckinck's prose is elegant without being precious and grounded in a democratic vision of literature that finds meaning in the commonplace.

## Critical Reception

Duyckinck was widely respected in his lifetime as a critic of sound judgment and wide learning. His salon — like that of Anne C. Lynch Botta — was a regular gathering place for New York's literary elite. Melville, in particular, valued Duyckinck's friendship and critical acumen, and their correspondence reveals a relationship of genuine intellectual intimacy. Hawthorne, Lowell, and other New England writers respected Duyckinck as a conduit to the New York literary market.

After his death, Duyckinck's reputation dimmed as the center of American literary criticism shifted from the periodical essay to the academic monograph. However, his *Cyclopædia* remains a vital source for scholars of early American literature, and his role in the formation of an American literary consciousness — particularly his friendship with and influence on Melville — has been the subject of sustained scholarly attention.
