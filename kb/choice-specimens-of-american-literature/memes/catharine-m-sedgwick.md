## Biography

Catharine Maria Sedgwick (1789–1867) was an American novelist, born in Stockbridge, Massachusetts,
into a prominent New England family. Her father, [[theodore-sedgwick]], was a Federalist
congressman and Chief Justice of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court.

Sedgwick never married, and her independent life as a professional author made her
one of the most successful American women writers of the early nineteenth century.
She converted to Unitarianism in the 1820s, and her religious convictions informed
the moral vision of her fiction.

## Literary Career

Sedgwick published her first novel, *A New-England Tale* (1822), anonymously.
It was followed by *Redwood* (1824), *Hope Leslie* (1827), and *Clarence* (1830),
establishing her as a leading figure in American fiction. *Hope Leslie*,
set in seventeenth-century Massachusetts, is her best-known work today, celebrated
for its sympathetic portrayal of Native American characters and its proto-feminist heroine.

> "Miss Sedgwick is among the earliest and most successful of our native novelists,"
> the editor notes in the anthology's biographical sketch (001-141).

Her fiction is characterized by vivid descriptions of New England life, moral seriousness,
and a concern for the position of women in American society. Sedgwick also wrote
instructional works for children, including *The Boy of Mount Rhigi* (1848).

## Critical Reception

During her lifetime, Sedgwick was ranked alongside [[james-fenimore-cooper]] and
[[washington-irving]] as a major figure in the development of an independent
American literature. Her works were translated into several European languages
and widely reviewed.

After falling into obscurity in the late nineteenth century, Sedgwick's work
was rediscovered by feminist literary scholars in the late twentieth century.
*Hope Leslie* is now a staple of American literature curricula, valued for
its complex treatment of gender, race, and national identity in the early Republic.
