# Providence in American Literature

## Definition
Providence, in American literary tradition, denotes the belief that a divine intelligence superintends human history, guiding events toward a purposeful end. This conviction shaped how American writers interpreted everything from personal misfortunes to national crises, investing historical events with theological significance.

## Historical Context
Puritan writers saw providence in every event—from the survival of a settlement to the outcome of a battle. John Winthrop described how "Providence has planted" virtue sparingly, as if to test human diligence (004-004). The Revolutionary generation interpreted American independence as providential, invoking "a superintending Providence in our favor" (005-001). Jonathan Edwards connected providence to the progress of "religion, liberty, and reason" through history (015-001).

## Key Authors and Works
- **John Winthrop** articulated the Puritan view that providence governs all events, from the cosmic to the personal, as part of God's redemptive plan (004-004, 004-007).
- **Jonathan Edwards** developed a sophisticated theology of providence, seeing the Great Awakening as a providential outpouring of divine grace (015-001).
- **Timothy Dwight** interpreted American national history as unfolding under divine guidance, with "instances of a superintending Providence" at every turn (005-001).

## Related Concepts

## Legacy
The providential interpretation of American history persisted through the 19th century, influencing Manifest Destiny, Civil War rhetoric (both sides claimed providence), and the idea of American exceptionalism. Even as secular thought gained ground, the providential framework remained a powerful undercurrent in American literary and political discourse.
