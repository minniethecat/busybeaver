# Nature in American Literature

Nature has been one of the defining themes of American literary expression.

From the Puritan encounter with the "howling wilderness" to the Transcendentalist celebration of nature as divine revelation, American writers have consistently turned to the natural world as a source of meaning, identity, and spiritual insight.

Unlike European traditions that often depicted nature as cultivated and human-scaled, American literature confronted a vast, untamed continent. The American [[wilderness]] was at once a threat, a promise, and a defining feature of national identity.

## Historical Development

The treatment of nature in American writing evolved through distinct phases:

| Period | View of Nature | Representative Writers |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Puritan (1620–1720) | Wilderness as spiritual testing ground | [[jonathan-edwards]], [[john-winthrop]] |
| Enlightenment (1720–1800) | Nature as orderly, rational, divinely designed | [[benjamin-franklin]], [[philip-freneau]] |
| Romantic / Transcendentalist (1830–1860) | Nature as divine presence, source of spiritual truth | [[ralph-waldo-emerson]], [[henry-david-thoreau]] |
| Later 19th Century (1860–1900) | Nature as both sublime and indifferent; realism | [[walt-whitman]], [[john-muir]] |

[[jonathan-edwards]] found in nature "images or shadows of divine things" — every spider's web, every flowing river a type of spiritual reality. This sacramental view of the natural world persisted through the Enlightenment and found its fullest expression in Transcendentalism.

## Transcendentalism and the Divine in Nature

[[ralph-waldo-emerson]]'s *Nature* (1836) was the foundational text of American Transcendentalism.

Emerson argued that nature is not merely matter but spirit made visible. "The world," he wrote, "emblemizes the mind." Every natural fact is a symbol of some spiritual fact. The transparent eyeball passage, in which Emerson describes becoming "part or parcel of God" while standing in the woods, is among the most famous in American prose.

[[henry-david-thoreau]] tested Emerson's philosophy through the experiment of living deliberately at Walden Pond. *Walden* (1854) is at once a naturalist's journal, a spiritual autobiography, and a critique of an industrializing America that was losing touch with the land.

Thoreau's method — meticulous observation of the particular combined with meditation on the universal — created a distinctive American nature writing tradition that extends through [[john-muir]], [[mary-austin]], [[aldo-leopold]], and [[rachel-carson]].

## Nature as the Sublime

A counter-current to the Transcendentalist celebration of nature's benevolence was the tradition of the sublime.

[[william-cullen-bryant]]'s "Thanatopsis" (1817) found in nature a consoling grandeur — but also the reminder of mortality. [[herman-melville]]'s white whale embodied a nature that was vast, indifferent, and potentially malevolent. [[edgar-allan-poe]]'s landscapes were projections of psychological states rather than observations of the actual world.

This darker vision acknowledged what Emerson's optimism sometimes overlooked: nature could be terrifying, alien, and utterly indifferent to human concerns.

## Selections in This Anthology

The theme of nature pervades "Choice Specimens of American Literature" (1874). The following passages are representative:

> [041-012] There are days which occur in this climate, at almost any season of the year, wherein the world reaches its perfection; when the air, the heavenly bodies, and the earth, make a harmony, as if Nature would indulge her offspring...

> [041-009] The multitude of species, intermixed as they are in their spontaneous growth, gives the American forest landscape a variety of aspect not often seen in the woods of Europe...

> [069-016] Not for a regiment's parade, Nor evil laws or rulers made, Blue Walden rolls its cannonade... That the bondage-days are told, And waters free as winds shall flow.

These passages illustrate the range of American literary responses to nature: as spiritual presence, as aesthetic spectacle, and as symbol of freedom.

## Influence and Legacy

The American literary engagement with nature has had consequences beyond literature.

The writings of Thoreau and Muir directly influenced the American conservation movement and the creation of the national park system. The wilderness ethic articulated in American literature shaped environmental policy and continues to inform debates about [[climate-change|climate]] and land use.

In literature, the nature writing tradition remains vital. [[wendell-berry]], [[barry-lopez]], [[annie-dillard]], and [[terry-tempest-williams]] are among the contemporary writers who carry forward a conversation that began with the Puritans' first encounter with the American wilderness.
