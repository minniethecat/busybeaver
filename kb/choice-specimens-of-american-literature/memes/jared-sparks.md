# Jared Sparks

## Biography

Jared Sparks (1794–1866) was an American historian, editor, and educator who served as President of Harvard College from 1849 to 1853. Born in Willington, Connecticut, Sparks worked as a carpenter and schoolteacher before attending Phillips Exeter Academy and Harvard College, from which he graduated in 1815. He subsequently studied theology and served briefly as a Unitarian minister in Baltimore, where he also edited the *Unitarian Miscellany*.

Sparks's greatest contribution to American letters was as an editor and collector of historical documents. He edited the *Diplomatic Correspondence of the American Revolution* (12 vols., 1829–1830), the *Writings of George Washington* (12 vols., 1834–1837), and the *Works of Benjamin Franklin* (10 vols., 1836–1840). His biographical writings include *The Life of Gouverneur Morris* (1832) and *The Life of General Stark*, from which the selection in this anthology is taken.

As an editor, Sparks was a controversial figure. His practice of silently "correcting" the grammar, style, and even substantive content of Washington's letters drew criticism from later historians. Nevertheless, his collections made the foundational documents of the American Revolution accessible to a broad readership and established documentary editing as a serious scholarly enterprise in the United States. He was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and served as McLean Professor of Ancient and Modern History at Harvard before becoming the university's president.

## Literary Career

Sparks's prose reflects the values of his era: clarity, dignity, and a conviction that the heroic episodes of the American past could instruct and inspire the present. He wrote history as a moral discipline, in which the deeds of great men furnished examples for the rising generation. His style is plain and declarative rather than rhetorical, suited to the documentary editor who saw his primary duty as presenting the facts.

The selection in "Choice Specimens of American Literature" is drawn from *The Life of General Stark*, Sparks's biography of the hero of the Battle of Bennington (1777). The passage describes General John Stark's tactical deployment against a combined force of German regulars and Tory militia during the Saratoga campaign:

> The German troops with their battery were advantageously posted upon a rising ground, at a bend in the Wollamsac (a tributary of the Hoosac), on its north bank. The ground fell off to the north and west, a circumstance of which Stark skilfully took advantage. Peters' corps of Tories were entrenched on the other side of the stream, in lower ground, and nearly in front of the German Battery. The little river, that meanders through the scene of the action, is fordable in all places. (026-012)

Stark's plan of attack exploited the topography with precision. He detached forces to strike the enemy's rear from both flanks, while advancing slowly in front to divert attention. When the action commenced, it was decisive:

> The action commenced at three o'clock in the afternoon, on the rear of the enemy's left, when Colonel Nichols, with great precision, carried into effect the dispositions of the commander. His example was followed by every other portion of the little army. General Stark himself moved forward slowly in front, till he heard the sound of the guns from Colonel Nichols' party, when he rushed upon the Tories, and in a few moments the action became general. (026-012)

Sparks lets Stark's own voice close the account with the memorable summary reported in his official dispatch:

> "It lasted," says Stark, in his official report, "two hours, and was the hottest I ever saw. It was like one continued clap of thunder." (026-012)

The passage exemplifies Sparks's method: a lucid narrative of military operations grounded in primary sources, with the general's own words providing the dramatic emphasis that the historian himself avoids.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *The Battle of Bennington* (026-012) — From *The Life of General Stark*. An account of the Battle of Bennington (August 16, 1777), a key engagement of the Saratoga campaign in which General John Stark's New Hampshire militia defeated a detachment of General Burgoyne's army. Sparks describes Stark's tactical skill in exploiting the terrain and the disciplined execution of his battle plan, culminating in Stark's own terse and memorable characterization of the engagement.

## Critical Reception

Sparks was widely respected in his own time as the foremost American historical editor. His documentary collections were regarded as monuments of national scholarship, and his presidency of Harvard, though brief, was seen as the capstone of a distinguished career. Later historians have been more critical. His editorial methods — particularly his habit of "improving" Washington's prose — are now regarded as a serious lapse of scholarly judgment, and his biographical writing has been faulted for its hagiographic tendency.

Yet Sparks's significance in the development of American historiography remains considerable. He was among the first to insist that American history must be built on a documentary foundation, and his collections preserved sources that might otherwise have been lost. Modern scholars, while reading his texts with appropriate caution, recognize him as a pioneer of the archival and editorial traditions that remain central to the discipline.
