# Theodore Dwight Woolsey

## Biography

Theodore Dwight Woolsey (1801–1889) was an American classicist, educator, and legal scholar. Born in New York City, he graduated from Yale College in 1820 and went on to study Greek and Latin philology in Europe. Upon his return, he was appointed professor of Greek at Yale, where he became one of the foremost classical scholars in America. In 1846, he was elected the tenth president of Yale College, a position he held for twenty-five years. Under his leadership, Yale expanded significantly, establishing new professional schools and strengthening its academic reputation. After retiring from the presidency, Woolsey turned his attention to international law, producing a treatise that became a standard text in American legal education.

## Literary Career

Woolsey's scholarly contributions span both classical philology and international law. His editions of Greek tragedies — particularly *The Alcestis of Euripides* (1834) and *The Antigone of Sophocles* (1835) — were widely used in American colleges. His most significant published work, however, was the *Introduction to the Study of International Law* (1860), which went through multiple editions and established him as a leading American authority on the subject. In this work, Woolsey argued for the importance of international law as both a practical guide for diplomats and a moral compass for nations. His prose is characterized by its lucidity and moral earnestness, reflecting his conviction that law and ethics were inseparable.

## Selections in This Anthology

Martin's *Choice Specimens* includes one selection from Theodore Dwight Woolsey: "Importance of the Study" (No. 161), from his *Introduction to the Study of International Law*. This selection appears in Section 35 of the anthology.

> "From all that has been said it has become apparent that the study of international law is important, as an index of civilization, and not to the student of law only, but to the student of history. In our land, especially, it is important, on more than one account, that this science should do its share in enlightening educated minds."
> (034-005)

> "Is it not desirable that the sense of justice, which seems fading out of the national mind before views of political expediency or destiny, should be deepened and made fast by that study which frowns on national crimes?"

> "I close this treatise here, hoping that it may be of some use to my native land, and to young men who may need a guide in the science of which it treats."

## Critical Reception

Woolsey's *Introduction to the Study of International Law* was well received by contemporaries and remained a standard text in American law schools for decades. It was praised for combining legal erudition with an accessible style suitable for students and general readers. His classical scholarship, while less innovative than his legal work, contributed to raising the standards of classical education in American colleges during a formative period in the development of American higher education.
