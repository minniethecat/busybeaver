# Geo. W. Bethune

## Biography

George Washington Bethune (1803–1862) was an American Reformed Dutch minister, poet, and editor. Born in New York City, he was the son of Divie Bethune, a prominent Scottish-born merchant and philanthropist. Bethune was educated at Columbia College and the theological seminary of the Reformed Dutch Church, being ordained in 1827.

Bethune served pastorates in Rhinebeck, New York (1827–1830), Utica (1830–1834), and Philadelphia (1834–1849), where his pulpit eloquence attracted large congregations and established him as one of the most admired preachers of his generation. In 1849 he moved to the Reformed Dutch Church on Brooklyn Heights, and in 1859 he returned to Philadelphia to serve the Third Reformed Dutch Church. He also traveled widely in Europe, publishing an account of his observations under the title *Expository Lectures on the Heidelberg Catechism* and a volume of travel sketches.

Bethune was offered several prominent positions during his career — including the chancellorship of New York University and a bishopric in the Protestant Episcopal Church — but declined them to remain in parish ministry. In 1861, at the outbreak of the Civil War, he traveled to Europe and died in Florence, Italy, in 1862.

## Literary Career

Bethune was a prolific author who published in multiple genres. His *Lays of Love and Faith, with Other Fugitive Poems* (1848) collected his devotional and occasional verse, much of which had first appeared in periodicals. He also edited *The British Female Poets* (1848), an anthology of English women's verse with critical introductions. His *Orations and Occasional Discourses* (1850) presented his most important sermons and public addresses, and his *Expository Lectures on the Heidelberg Catechism* (1864, posthumous) was a theological work of considerable learning.

Bethune's poetry is characterized by a dignified, elevated style, drawing on classical imagery while directing it toward Christian devotional ends. The single selection in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* — "Mythology Gives Place to Christianity" — exemplifies this blending of classical allusion and Christian conviction:

> Hushed is their song; from long-frequented grove, / Pale Memory, are thy bright-eyed daughters gone; / No more in strains of melody and love, / Gush forth thy sacred waters, Helicon. (069-050)

Bethune catalogs the fading of the classical gods — Apollo, the Pythian oracle, the priests at Delphi and Delos — to declare the triumph of Christianity over the pagan imagination:

> No more at Delos, or at Delphi now, / Or e'en at mighty Ammon's Libyan shrine, / The white-robed priests before the altar bow, / To slay the victim and to pour the wine, / While gifts of kingdoms round each pillar twine. (069-050)

The poem's rhetorical strategy is clear: the classical muses have fallen silent because a greater truth has spoken.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *Mythology Gives Place to Christianity* (069-050) — From "Invocation," printed in *Lays of Love and Faith* (1848). A Spenserian-stanza meditation on the displacement of classical mythology by Christian revelation. The poem surveys the ruined shrines of antiquity — Delos, Delphi, the shrine of Ammon — and finds in their desolation evidence that the pagan gods were never more than the projections of the human imagination.

## Critical Reception

Bethune's contemporaries esteemed him principally as a preacher; his printed sermons circulated widely, and his collected orations went through multiple editions. His poetry was less widely known but was admired by fellow clergymen and by readers of religious verse. Rufus Wilmot Griswold included Bethune in his *Poets and Poetry of America* (1842), describing him as "one of the most eloquent preachers of his church."

Bethune's reputation in American literary history has been modest. He belonged to a tradition — the learned clergyman-poet — that was central to early American letters but declined in importance by the later nineteenth century. His verse, carefully crafted and theologically earnest, has been largely forgotten by subsequent generations. However, his editorial work, particularly *The British Female Poets*, remains a notable early contribution to the recovery of women's literary history.
