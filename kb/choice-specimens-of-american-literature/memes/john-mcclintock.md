# John McClintock

## Biography

John McClintock (1814–1870) was an American Methodist clergyman, theologian, and educator. Born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, he graduated from the University of Pennsylvania before entering the Methodist ministry.

McClintock earned a reputation for eloquence in the pulpit and intellectual rigor in theological scholarship. He served as professor of ancient languages at Dickinson College and later as the first president of Drew Theological Seminary in Madison, New Jersey, founded in 1867. He also edited *The Methodist Quarterly Review*.

## Literary Career

McClintock's literary output included sermons, theological treatises, and editorial work. He co-edited the *Cyclopaedia of Biblical, Theological, and Ecclesiastical Literature*, a massive reference work that became a standard resource for Protestant clergy.

His sermon "The Ground of Man's Love to God," from which the anthology draws its selection, exemplifies his rhetorical method. McClintock's prose draws on Romantic conceptions of nature to convey theological truths, using the visible world as a lens through which to perceive the divine. The anthology editor, in a footnote, describes him as "distinguished among the Methodist clergy for eloquence and learning" (012-003).

## Selections in This Anthology

McClintock appears in "Choice Specimens" with a passage from his sermon "The Christian the Only True Lover of Nature" (52). The selection employs the metaphor of a cave illuminated by torchlight:

> It is not too much to say that the only *true* lover of nature, is he that loves God in Christ. It is as with one standing in one of those caves of unknown beauty of which travellers tell us. While it is dark, nothing can be seen but the abyss, or at most, a faint glimmer of ill-defined forms. But flash into it the light of a single torch, and myriad splendors crowd upon the gaze of the beholder.
> (012-004)

McClintock is listed in the Table of Contents preface as "John McClintock, 1814-1870" (P01-024), among the theologians of the collection.

## Critical Reception

McClintock was esteemed by his Methodist contemporaries as a preacher of exceptional power and a scholar of wide learning. Later historians of American religion have recognized him as a transitional figure in Methodist intellectual life, helping to move the denomination from its revivalist origins toward a more academically engaged faith.
