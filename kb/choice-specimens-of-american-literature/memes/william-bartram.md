# William Bartram

## Biography

William Bartram (1739–1823) was an American naturalist, botanist, and explorer. Born in Philadelphia, he was the son of John Bartram, the pioneering American botanist who founded the first botanical garden in the British colonies. William accompanied his father on collecting expeditions from an early age and inherited both his botanical knowledge and his passion for the American wilderness.

Between 1773 and 1777, Bartram undertook an epic solo journey through the southeastern colonies — the Carolinas, Georgia, and Florida — collecting plant specimens and recording his observations. His travels took him through largely uncharted territories inhabited by the Cherokee and Creek nations. The journals and drawings he produced during these years formed the basis of his literary reputation.

## Literary Career

Bartram's masterpiece, *Travels through North and South Carolina, Georgia, East and West Florida* (1791), was first published in Philadelphia and quickly found an enthusiastic audience in Europe, where it was translated into several languages. The book combines precise botanical observation with a rapturous, almost mystical appreciation of the American landscape. Bartram described the natural world not as a cataloguer but as a poet, investing his prose with a sense of wonder that anticipated the Romantic movement in literature.

The *Travels* influenced the English Romantic poets — Wordsworth and Coleridge both read it — and provided source material for Chateaubriand's American writings. In American literature, Bartram stands as a foundational figure in the tradition of nature writing that would later include Thoreau, Muir, and Carson.

## Selections

In the *Choice Specimens* anthology, Bartram is represented by "Scenes on the Upper Oconee" (052-017), an extract that displays his characteristic fusion of scientific precision and lyrical elevation:

> At this rural retirement were assembled a charming circle of mountain vegetable beauties. (052-017)

The prose moves from close botanical observation to a sublime description of an approaching thunderstorm:

> far distant thunder rolls over the trembling hills: the black clouds with august majesty and power move slowly forwards, shading regions of towering hills, and threatening all the destruction of a thunder-storm: all around is now still as death. (052-018)

Bartram's prose is distinguished by its personification of natural phenomena — clouds possess "august majesty," breezes "toss" flowers, and the earth itself seems a living, breathing presence. This animistic vision of nature was a significant departure from the utilitarian natural history of his father's generation.

## Critical Reception

Bartram's *Travels* is now recognized as a classic of American literature as well as a landmark of natural history. The literary critic N. Bryllion Fagin called it "the first genuinely American rhapsody of the wilderness." Modern scholars have also noted Bartram's unusually sympathetic portrayals of Native Americans and his proto-ecological vision of the natural world as an interconnected whole.
