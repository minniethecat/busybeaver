# Joseph Rodman Drake

Joseph Rodman Drake (1795–1820) was an American Romantic poet whose brief but influential career made him one of the most promising literary figures of early nineteenth-century New York. A central member of the [[knickerbocker-group|Knickerbocker Group]] — the circle of writers that included [[fitz-greene-halleck|Fitz-Greene Halleck]], [[washington-irving|Washington Irving]], and [[james-k-paulding|James Kirke Paulding]] — Drake is best remembered for *The Culprit Fay* (1816), an ambitious fairy poem set along the Hudson River that displayed a distinctly American imagination within a [[romanticism|Romantic]] framework inherited from British models. His death from consumption at the age of twenty-five cut short a career that contemporaries believed would have placed him among the foremost American poets.

## Biography

Drake was born in New York City on August 7, 1795. Orphaned at an early age, he was raised by relatives and received a classical education before studying medicine at the College of Physicians and Surgeons, where he earned his medical degree in 1816. That same year, at the age of twenty-one, Drake completed *The Culprit Fay*, a poem that would establish his literary reputation and become the work for which he is chiefly remembered.

While practicing as a physician, Drake continued to write poetry and became a leading figure in New York's literary circles. His closest literary friendship was with [[fitz-greene-halleck|Fitz-Greene Halleck]], with whom he collaborated on the satirical "Croaker" papers — a series of light verses published anonymously in the *New York Evening Post* in 1819 that gently mocked the city's social and literary pretensions. The two poets also co-authored "The American Flag," a patriotic lyric that enjoyed considerable popularity in the antebellum period.

Drake married Sarah Eckford, the daughter of a prominent shipbuilder, in 1816. In 1819, seeking a healthier climate for his worsening tuberculosis, he traveled to New Orleans, but the journey brought no improvement. He returned to New York and died on September 21, 1820, at the age of twenty-five. Halleck's elegy "On the Death of Joseph Rodman Drake" — beginning with the line "Green be the turf above thee" — became one of the most widely anthologized American poems of the nineteenth century and helped secure Drake's posthumous fame.

## Literary Career

Drake's literary output was small — constrained by his early death and the demands of his medical practice — but remarkably varied. His major work, *The Culprit Fay* (1816), is a narrative poem in rhymed octosyllabic couplets that tells the story of a fairy who falls in love with a mortal maiden and is punished by the fairy court. The poem is set in the Hudson Highlands, with precise topographical references to Crow-nest Mountain, the Dunderberg, and the waters of the Hudson River — a deliberate choice to locate fairy mythology in an American landscape. [[william-cullen-bryant|William Cullen Bryant]] later praised the poem as a pioneering effort to naturalize European Romantic conventions on American soil.

Drake's shorter lyrics include "Bronx," a meditative poem on the river of his native city, and several love poems marked by a characteristic mingling of longing and despair. The "Croaker" papers, written with Halleck, demonstrated a lighter side of his talent: satirical, topical, and urbane, they belong to the tradition of eighteenth-century periodical verse that had been popularized in America by [[joseph-dennie|Joseph Dennie]] and other early magazine writers. The collaboration also produced "The American Flag," a poem that married Drake's lyrical gift to Halleck's more declamatory style and became a staple of schoolroom recitation for generations.

## Selections in the Anthology

The *Choice Specimens of American Literature* anthology presents two facets of Drake's poetry: a passionate lyric on love and longing, and excerpts from *The Culprit Fay* that showcase his descriptive powers and his gift for Americanizing European fairy-lore.

The lyric (067-040–067-041) belongs to the tradition of the Platonic love-lament, in which the soul seeks its destined counterpart across a hostile world:

> "The bard has sung, God never formed a soul / Without its own peculiar mate, to meet / Its wandering half, when ripe to crown the whole / Bright plan of bliss, most heavenly, most complete!" (067-040)

The poem turns from this ideal to its frustration — "But thousand evil things there are that hate / To look on happiness" — and culminates in an image of spiritual exhaustion (067-041):

> "Weary, exhausted, longing, panting, sighing, / Lights sadly at the desert's bitter stream; / So, many a soul, o'er life's drear desert faring, / Love's pure, congenial spring unfound, unquaffed, / Suffers, recoils, then thirsty and despairing / Of what it would, descends, and sips the nearest draught." (067-041)

The psychological acuity of these lines — the soul settling for "the nearest draught" rather than the "pure spring" — reveals a maturity remarkable in a poet barely past his majority.

The selection from *The Culprit Fay* (067-042–067-046) opens with a moonlit nocturne of the Hudson River that demonstrates Drake's skill at atmospheric description:

> "The moon looks down on old Crow-nest, / She mellows the shades, on his shaggy breast, / And seems his huge grey form to throw / In a silver cone on the wave below." (067-042)

The stillness is rendered with precise auditory detail — "naught is heard on the lonely hill / But the cricket's chirp, and the answer shrill / Of the gauze-winged katy-did" (067-043) — before the fairy pageant disrupts the natural order. Drake's catalogue of creatures silenced by the Fay's passage (067-044) — the moth-fly "crept under the leaf," the katy-did "forgot its lay," the mosquito "checked his drone" — achieves a mock-heroic gravity that is at once playful and vividly imagined.

## Critical Reception

Drake's early death made him a figure of romantic legend, and his reputation in the decades following his death was sustained as much by Halleck's elegy as by his own verse. [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], in his critical writings of the 1840s, judged *The Culprit Fay* harshly, finding its fairy machinery derivative of British models and its versification uneven. Yet Poe acknowledged that Drake possessed "unusual poetical talent" and speculated that, had he lived longer, he would have outgrown his juvenile influences.

Later nineteenth-century critics were more generous. [[rufus-wilmot-griswold|Rufus Wilmot Griswold]] included Drake prominently in *The Poets and Poetry of America* (1842), and anthologists throughout the century preserved his place in the canon of early American Romanticism. Modern scholarship has tended to read Drake as a transitional figure — a poet whose work bridges the neoclassical verse of the [[hartford-wits|Hartford Wits]] and the fully realized Romanticism of [[william-cullen-bryant|Bryant]] and the [[transcendentalism|Transcendentalists]]. The topographical specificity of *The Culprit Fay*, with its insistence on the Hudson River as a fit setting for enchantment, has been recognized as an early instance of the literary nationalism that would become a defining feature of the [[american-renaissance|American Renaissance]].
