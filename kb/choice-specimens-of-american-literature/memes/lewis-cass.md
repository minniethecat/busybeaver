# Lewis Cass

## Biography

Lewis Cass (1782–1866) was an American military officer, politician, and diplomat. Born in Exeter, New Hampshire, he moved to the Ohio frontier as a young man and practiced law in Marietta. Cass served with distinction in the War of 1812, rising to the rank of brigadier general. In 1813, President James Madison appointed him Governor of the Michigan Territory, a position he held for eighteen years. During his tenure, he negotiated numerous treaties with Native American tribes and oversaw the territory's development into statehood.

Cass served as Secretary of War under President Andrew Jackson (1831–1836), where he oversaw the implementation of the Indian Removal Act. He later served as Minister to France (1836–1842), U.S. Senator from Michigan (1845–1857), and Secretary of State under President James Buchanan (1857–1860). In 1848, he was the Democratic Party's nominee for President, losing to Zachary Taylor. Cass was a leading proponent of popular sovereignty — the doctrine that territorial settlers should decide the slavery question for themselves.

## Literary Career

Cass wrote extensively on military affairs, Indian policy, and diplomacy. His published works include *Considerations on the Present State of the Indians and Their Removal to the West of the Mississippi* (1828) and *France: Its King, Court, and Government* (1840), a popular account of the July Monarchy. As a contributor to the *North American Review* and through his diplomatic dispatches, Cass helped shape American public opinion on territorial expansion and foreign policy. His prose was clear, direct, and characteristic of the Jacksonian era's plain-spoken political rhetoric.

## Selections in This Anthology

In "Choice Specimens of American Literature," Cass is represented by his address on the "Policy of Removing the Indians," which argues that the removal of Native American tribes to lands west of the Mississippi was both a practical necessity and a moral obligation:

> "The associations which bind the Indians to the land of their forefathers are strong and enduring; and these must be broken by their emigration. But they are also broken by our citizens, who every day encounter all the difficulties of similar changes in pursuit of the means of support." (020-002)

> "The great moral debt we owe to this unhappy race is universally felt and acknowledged. Diversities of opinion exist respecting the proper mode of discharging this obligation, but its validity is not denied." (020-003)

Cass presented removal as a humanitarian measure — a deeply contested view that reflects the dominant expansionist ideology of Jacksonian America. The selection provides a primary-source window into the rhetoric used to justify federal Indian policy in the 1830s.

## Critical Reception

Cass was respected by contemporaries as a capable administrator and a prolific political writer, though his literary reputation has not outlasted his political career. His writings on Indian affairs are now studied primarily as historical documents illustrating nineteenth-century American attitudes toward Native peoples. Modern scholars approach his work with critical scrutiny, recognizing both his influence on territorial policy and the devastating consequences of the removal policies he advocated.
