# Donald G. Mitchell

## Biography

Donald Grant Mitchell (1822–1908) was an American essayist, novelist, and agricultural writer. Born in Norwich, Connecticut, he graduated from Yale College in 1841 and briefly studied law before turning to literature. Mitchell traveled in Europe in the 1840s, and his early writings included travel sketches and contributions to New York periodicals.

Under the pen name "Ik Marvel," Mitchell achieved extraordinary popularity with *Reveries of a Bachelor, or A Book of the Heart* (1850) and its sequel *Dream Life* (1851). These sentimental, meditative works struck a deep chord with the antebellum reading public and made "Ik Marvel" one of the most widely read American authors of the 1850s. In his later years, Mitchell turned increasingly to agricultural and horticultural pursuits, serving as the first president of the Connecticut Board of Agriculture and writing extensively on rural life and landscape gardening. He lived out his long life at Edgewood, his country estate near New Haven.

## Literary Career

Mitchell's literary reputation rests primarily on *Reveries of a Bachelor*, a series of dream-like meditations on love, marriage, and domesticity that appealed powerfully to the sentimental tastes of mid-nineteenth-century America. The book went through numerous editions and was a staple of Victorian-era bookshelves. Its success was followed by *Dream Life*, a similar work structured around the stages of a boy's imaginative development into manhood.

Mitchell also wrote *My Farm of Edgewood* (1863) and *Wet Days at Edgewood* (1865), charming volumes of rural essays that prefigured the American nature-writing tradition. His prose is characterized by a warm, conversational tone, gentle humor, and an abiding affection for the rhythms of country life — qualities well represented in the selections included in this anthology.

## Critical Reception

Mitchell was one of the most popular American authors of the 1850s, with *Reveries of a Bachelor* selling over a million copies across multiple editions. His work was praised for its delicate sentiment and accessibility, though later critics have sometimes dismissed it as overly sentimental. His rural essays, however, have been reassessed as important precursors to the American nature-writing tradition that includes John Burroughs and later environmental writers.
