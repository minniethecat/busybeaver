# Margaret M. Davidson

## Biography

Margaret Miller Davidson (1823–1837) was an American poet whose brief life and precocious talent drew widespread attention in the antebellum literary world. Born at Plattsburgh, New York, on Lake Champlain, she was the youngest of the Davidson sisters, both of whom achieved posthumous fame as poets.

Margaret was barely an infant when her family moved to New York City, then to Ballston, and finally to a rural residence called "Ruremont" on the Hudson River. She was a delicate, sensitive child, of slender frame and extraordinary mental quickness. Her sister Lucretia Davidson (1808–1825), herself a poet of considerable reputation who died at seventeen, was Margaret's chief inspiration; Margaret is said to have begun composing verse almost as soon as she could write.

Her health was always fragile. In 1836, symptoms of tuberculosis appeared, and she was taken to Saratoga Springs in the hope of recovery. The effort was in vain: Margaret M. Davidson died on November 25, 1837, at the age of fourteen. Washington Irving wrote a biographical preface to her posthumous collected works, attesting to the high regard in which she was held by the leading literary figures of her time.

## Literary Career

Margaret Davidson began writing before the age of ten. Her earliest poems show a precocious mastery of meter and a Romantic sensibility — meditations on nature, mortality, and the passage of time that are startling in their maturity, given the author's youth.

Her poems were collected and published after her death by her mother, with Washington Irving contributing a biographical introduction. The volume, *Biography and Poetical Remains of the Late Margaret Miller Davidson* (1841), includes such pieces as "Lines in Memory of Her Sister Lucretia," "The Closing Scene," and various lyrics and hymns. Irving's endorsement lent her work a prestige that extended across the Atlantic, and she was read with sympathy by figures including Edgar Allan Poe and the Scottish poet Thomas Campbell.

In the preface to *Choice Specimens of American Literature*, editor Benj. N. Martin acknowledges James Wood Davidson's "Living Writers of the South" among the works that furnished material for the anthology (P01-001), placing the Davidson name within the larger literary culture of the mid-nineteenth century.

## Selections in This Anthology

- *From "Lines in Memory of Her Sister Lucretia"* (002-022, 409) — A poem of filial grief and artistic legacy, addressed to her deceased sister. Margaret took up the same poetic vocation that Lucretia had pursued, and the poem is an elegy not only for a sister but for a fellow artist cut down by illness.
- *From "The Closing Scene"* (075-053) — The closing poem of the "Later Poets" section. The poem presents an autumnal scene of a spinning woman at her wheel, her thread snapping as death arrives:

> Long, but not loud, the droning wheel went on,
> Like the low murmur of a hive at noon;
> Long, but not loud, the memory of the gone
> Breathed through her lips a sad and tremulous tune.
> At last the thread was snapped; her head was bowed;
> Life dropped the distaff through his hands serene;
> And loving neighbors smoothed her careful shroud,
> While death and winter closed the autumn scene.
> (075-053)

The imagery of the spinning wheel as the thread of life — familiar from classical mythology — is rendered with a delicate, elegiac grace that makes it one of the most affecting selections in the "Later Poets" section of the anthology.

## Critical Reception

Margaret Davidson was celebrated in her own brief lifetime and for decades afterward as a prodigy — the "American Sappho," as some enthusiasts styled her. Washington Irving's patronage was significant; his preface to the 1841 volume lent her work an imprimatur that it might not otherwise have attained.

Edgar Allan Poe, in his essay "The Literati of New York City," praised the Davidson sisters at some length, though he expressed the view that Lucretia was the greater poet. Modern critics have tended to view Margaret Davidson less as a major American poet and more as a case study in the nineteenth-century cult of the dying young female genius — a Romantic archetype that the Davidson sisters, taken together, exemplify almost perfectly.

Nevertheless, "The Closing Scene" and "Lines in Memory of Her Sister Lucretia" remain readable, emotionally vivid poems that reward attention. They are among the most widely anthologized works by an American poet under fifteen years of age.
