# Edwin P. Whipple

## Biography

Edwin Percy Whipple (1819–1886) was an American literary critic, essayist, and lecturer. Born in Gloucester, Massachusetts, he began his career as a clerk in a Boston bank while pursuing literary interests. Whipple established himself as one of the most prominent literary critics of his era, contributing essays and reviews to leading periodicals and delivering popular lecture series throughout the United States.

## Literary Career

Whipple's reputation rested on his essays on literature, culture, and public affairs. His collections *Essays and Reviews* (1848–1849) and *Character and Characteristic Men* (1866) were widely read. He championed American literary independence from European models and was an early and influential advocate for the recognition of American authors. His criticism was marked by a broad humanism and a conviction that literature should serve moral and social purposes.

In his essay "Wit and Humor in Literature," drawn from *Literature and Life*, Whipple celebrated the civilizing power of wit and mirth:

> Let us be thankful that we have in Wit a power before which the pride of wealth and the insolence of office are abased; which can transfix bigotry and tyranny with arrows of lightning; which can strike its object over thousands of miles of space, across thousands of years of time. (049-006)

Whipple surveyed the tradition of literary humor, noting that "five of the greatest humorists that ever made the world ring with laughter were priests — Rabelais, Scarron, Swift, Sterne, and Sydney Smith" (049-006). He argued that wit was not merely entertainment but a force for social reform: "Many of the most important reforms in England are directly traceable to him [Sydney Smith]. He really laughed his countrymen out of some of their most cherished stupidities of legislation" (049-006).

## Selections in This Anthology

Whipple is represented in *Choice Specimens of American Literature* (1874) by an excerpt from "Wit and Humor in Literature" (049-006). The passage celebrates the humanizing power of mirth amid life's sorrows, urging readers to welcome humor into daily life: "Let them scatter some humanities in the sullen marts of business; let them glide in through the open doors of the heart; let their glee lighten up the feast, and gladden the fireside of home" (049-006).

## Critical Reception

Whipple was among the most respected American literary critics of the mid-nineteenth century, alongside figures such as Evert Duyckinck and Rufus Griswold. His work helped shape the canon of American literature at a formative moment. Later criticism has tended to view him as a transitional figure between Romantic and Realist critical sensibilities, though his essays remain valuable as documents of nineteenth-century American cultural thought.
