# New York and Genesee Land Company

## Overview

The New York and Genesee Land Company was among the earliest and most aggressive of the land speculation enterprises that targeted Iroquois territory after the Revolutionary War. Hubbard records that the company was "called the New York and Genesee Land Company" (005-049) — an organization formed to capitalize on the Seneca's post-war vulnerability by acquiring title to their lands in the Genesee Valley.

## Structure and Governance

The company brought together investors from New York and Philadelphia, uniting capital from both cities in pursuit of Genesee lands. Oliver Phelps was a principal figure, and "after Mr. Phelps had secured an interest in the Genesee country" (011-162), he became one of the most active negotiators with the Seneca.

Robert Morris of Philadelphia, the Revolutionary War financier, purchased the pre-emption rights to Seneca territory from Massachusetts. His son Thomas Morris served as the company's agent at the pivotal Big Tree Treaty of 1797.

## Historical Role

The company's activities reshaped the landscape of western New York. Through the Phelps and Gorham Purchase (1788) and the Big Tree Treaty (1797), the company acquired millions of acres of Seneca land. Hubbard describes how the company, working alongside another organization "called the Niagara Genesee Company" (005-055), was formed in Canada "of those who were most in favor with the British."

The company's methods — leveraging political influence, exploiting divisions within the Seneca leadership, and presenting land acquisition as a fait accompli — established the pattern that all subsequent land companies would follow.

## Key Members

- **Oliver Phelps** — Principal agent and negotiator
- **Robert Morris** — Philadelphia financier, purchased pre-emption rights
- **Thomas Morris** — Son of Robert Morris, agent at Big Tree Treaty
- **Nathaniel Gorham** — Massachusetts investor
