# Anthony Wayne

## Biography

Anthony Wayne was an American officer of "untiring energy and vigilance" who was placed in command of the re-organized United States army raised to subdue the north-western Indian tribes. (009-202) A larger number of soldiers was called into the field and placed under a severe discipline, to inure them to the dangers and hardships of the campaign. (009-204)

Fearing the influence of an encampment near a town and wishing to inspire self-reliance in his soldiers, on 27 November 1792 Wayne marched his army twenty-two miles down the Ohio to a point he named Legionville, fortifying it and taking up his winter quarters there. (009-209) On 30 April 1793 he broke up the garrison at Legionville and led his army to Fort Washington, on the site of the present city of Cincinnati. (009-213)

## Role and Influence

Wayne's methodical advance earned him among the Indians a reputation for sleepless vigilance. His wariness gained for him the title of "Black Snake," and the Miami chief Little Turtle counselled his countrymen to weigh the American offers of peace before giving battle. (009-237) In Little Turtle's warning the danger was Wayne himself:

> "The Americans are now led by a chief who never sleeps. The night and the day are alike to him; and during all the time he has been marching on our villages, notwithstanding the watchfulness of our young men, we have never been able to surprise him." (009-243)

Marching in October toward the Miami, six miles beyond Fort Jefferson, Wayne fortified a post and named it Greenville. (009-222) On 23 December a detachment under Major Burbeck occupied the ground where General [[arthur-st-clair|St. Clair]]'s army had been defeated two years before, gathered up the bones of the fallen, and erected a fortress they appropriately named Fort Recovery. (009-230)

## Key Events

- **Battle of Fallen Timbers (20 August 1794)**: [[battle-of-fallen-timbers|Wayne's engagement]] with the tribes was fought near a British fort at the rapids of the Miami. His order to advance with trailed arms and rouse the Indians from their covert at the point of the bayonet was executed so promptly that the Indians were driven more than two miles in an hour and dispersed in dismay. (009-261) The victory, fought within reach of the British guns, decided the fate of the campaign. (009-264)

- **Destruction of the villages**: After the battle the army destroyed the property of the Indians in the vicinity, including the extensive possessions of Colonel McKee, an officer of the [[british-indian-department|British Indian Department]]. (009-272)

- **Treaty of Greenville (1795)**: Wayne closed his campaign the following year with a treaty with the north-western tribes entirely agreeable to the wishes of the United States, (009-283) the [[treaty-of-greenville|treaty concluded at Greenville]] in 1795, after which friendly relations followed. (016-010)
