# Buffalo Creek Reservation

## Geography

Buffalo Creek was located in western New York, near the present-day city of Buffalo, at the head of the Niagara River. Hubbard situates it "from Buffalo, at the head of Niagara river" (017-004), describing a location of strategic importance where Lake Erie drains into the Niagara River. The creek itself provided water, fishing, and a natural gathering place for the Seneca community that settled there.

The reservation was established as part of the Big Tree Treaty of 1797, which reserved specific tracts for the Seneca while ceding the vast majority of their western New York lands. Buffalo Creek became the most populous and politically significant of the Seneca reservations.

## Historical Significance

Buffalo Creek was the stage for much of the drama of Red Jacket's later life. Here he received visitors, addressed councils, debated missionaries, and ultimately died. The reservation was "the head-quarters of the Seneca nation," where the most important political decisions affecting the tribe were made. (007-043)

In April 1791, even before the reservation was formally established, a "great council fire was kindled at Buffalo Creek, on the 27th of April" — a gathering that brought together Seneca leaders, US commissioners, and representatives of other Iroquois nations. (007-053) Colonel Proctor and Cornplanter arrived to negotiate Seneca land concessions, and Red Jacket delivered one of his most significant early speeches on this occasion. (007-054)

The reservation also became a battleground in the cultural conflict between traditional Seneca religion and Christian missionaries. Buffalo Creek was where missionaries from various denominations established themselves in the early 19th century, leading to the religious controversies that would culminate in Red Jacket's trial and temporary deposition from chieftainship in the 1820s. (010-149)

## Key Events at This Location

The **Council of April 1791** at Buffalo Creek was a pivotal moment in Seneca-US relations. Red Jacket's speech on this occasion — his response to Colonel Proctor's visit to "the Indians at Buffalo Creek" — established his reputation as the foremost defender of Seneca sovereignty. (007-002) His address combined diplomatic politeness with an unwavering refusal to cede additional lands.

Throughout the 1810s and 1820s, Buffalo Creek hosted missionaries who sought to convert the Seneca. Hubbard depicts these encounters at length, with Red Jacket leading the opposition to Christian proselytizing while figures like the young chief "at a great council held at Buffalo Creek" challenged traditional Seneca practices. (014-056)

The reservation remained Red Jacket's home until his death. In his final years, Buffalo Creek symbolized both the persistence of Seneca community and the inexorable advance of white settlement that would eventually lead, through the Treaty of Buffalo Creek (1842), to the sale of the reservation and the removal of the Seneca to other lands.
