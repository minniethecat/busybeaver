# Iroquois Oratory

## Definition

Iroquois oratory was not merely a natural gift but a cultivated art central to the governance, diplomacy, and cultural life of the Six Nations. Orators held the highest distinction among their people, and their eloquence in council was the primary instrument of political persuasion — the means by which decisions were reached, treaties negotiated, and tribal history transmitted across generations. (001-003) Hubbard observes that oratory was "an art among the Iroquois," deliberately developed and refined over a lifetime of training in the council house. (001-141)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The orator occupied a position of extraordinary prestige in Iroquois society. The sachems — the civil chiefs who governed the Confederacy — were expected to be the most eloquent speakers of their nation, and their authority in council derived in significant measure from their rhetorical skill. (003-113) The great orators of the Iroquois were compared by contemporary observers to the finest speakers of classical antiquity: "the Iroquois, as their orators," drew comparisons to the achievements of Athenian democracy. (001-145)

The formal council speech followed established conventions: an invocation of the Great Spirit, a recapitulation of the history of relations between the parties, a point-by-point response to the matters at hand, and a closing invocation of mutual good faith. Wampum belts served as mnemonic records, each pattern of beads corresponding to a specific passage of the oration. The orator who could weave these elements together with force and dignity commanded the respect of both his own people and the European and American diplomats with whom he negotiated. (001-009)

## Role in the Narrative

Red Jacket was the supreme exemplar of Iroquois oratory, and his speeches form the dramatic center of Hubbard's biography. Hubbard notes the high distinction of orator among his people, and that Red Jacket "sought at the high distinction of orator among his people" from his youth. (001-044) Those who heard him — including Colonel William L. Stone, whose biography Hubbard draws upon — described him as a warm admirer of the orator's genius. (001-064)

Throughout Hubbard's account, the council speech is the pivot on which events turn. At Fort Stanwix, at Tioga Point, at Canandaigua, at Buffaloe Creek — in each of these settings, the orator's words determined whether peace would hold or conflict resume. Red Jacket's speeches were preserved, reprinted, and discussed across the United States and Europe, making him one of the most famous Native American public figures of his era. (001-056) Hubbard's insistence on recording these speeches in full reflects his conviction that "we did not give their orators the credit" they deserved. (001-106)
