# Seneca Nation

## Overview

The Seneca Nation — known among the Iroquois as the "Keepers of the Western Door" — occupied the westernmost territory of the Six Nations.

Their lands encompassed the Genesee Valley, the Finger Lakes region, and the lands stretching westward to Lake Erie and the Niagara River. Hubbard describes their territory as extending from "the western end of Lake Ontario" southward through the rich valleys that became the focus of land speculation after the Revolutionary War. (005-024)

Among the Six Nations, the Seneca were the most numerous. Hubbard records the traditional history of their rise to prominence within the Confederacy, noting their martial reputation and their role as the first line of defense against threats from the west.

## Structure and Governance

The Seneca, like all Iroquois nations, were organized into clans — matrilineal kinship groups that determined inheritance, political office, and social identity. Hubbard's account of the "clan system" among the Seneca describes how the clans "had their origin in the remote past" (020-197) and governed everything from the selection of chiefs to the protocols of hospitality.

The Seneca sachems — hereditary chiefs selected by clan mothers — represented the nation at the Grand Council of the Iroquois Confederacy. Red Jacket's own elevation to sachemship, which Hubbard describes in detail, illustrates the interplay between hereditary right, personal merit, and political circumstance in Seneca governance. (004-050)

## Historical Role

Throughout the period covered by Hubbard, the Seneca Nation was at the center of the most consequential events in Iroquois history.

During the Revolutionary War, the Seneca sided with Britain — a decision that brought the Sullivan-Clinton Campaign of 1779 down upon their towns and villages. The destruction of Seneca settlements in "the Genesee country in 1779" (014-151) was a catastrophe from which the nation never fully recovered.

In the post-Revolutionary period, the Seneca became the primary negotiating partner — and adversary — of the United States in western New York.

The treaties at Fort Stanwix (1784), Canandaigua (1794), and Big Tree (1797) progressively reduced Seneca territory. Red Jacket emerged as the nation's foremost defender, using his oratorical skill to resist land cessions and preserve what remained of Seneca sovereignty.

The religious controversy of the 1820s — pitting Christian converts against traditionalists — divided the Seneca Nation internally. Red Jacket's trial and deposition, followed by his restoration, revealed both the depth of these divisions and the enduring authority of traditional Seneca governance.

## Key Members

- **Red Jacket (Sa-Go-Ye-Wat-Ha)** — Principal sachem and orator of the Seneca Nation

- **Cornplanter (Gyantwachia)** — Seneca war chief and diplomat

- **Farmer's Brother** — Seneca leader who participated in major treaty councils

- **Young King** — Leader of the Christian Seneca faction

The Seneca were the most numerous and powerful of the Six Nations:

> The Senecas were the most numerous, and in many respects the most powerful of the Six Nations. Their location upon the western frontier brought them into more immediate contact with the western tribes, and they were regarded as the guardians of the Western Door of the Confederacy.
>
> (002-161)

Hubbard describes their martial reputation:

> The Senecas held the highest rank for prowess and skill in war. They were the first in the attack and the last in retreat. Their war-chiefs were renowned throughout the Confederacy, and their name carried terror to their enemies.
>
> (002-164)
