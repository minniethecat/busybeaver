# Council Fire

## Definition

The council fire was the symbolic and ceremonial center of Iroquois political life. It was both a physical gathering — chiefs and warriors assembled around an actual fire — and a powerful metaphor for the ongoing political existence of a nation. To "kindle" a council fire meant to convene a formal diplomatic or political assembly; to "cover" or "extinguish" it meant to conclude proceedings. The council fire was not merely a meeting but an institution — the visible manifestation of a people's collective political will. (001-098)

In Iroquois protocol, only the host who kindled a council fire had the authority to extinguish it. This principle was jealously guarded: when Red Jacket unilaterally "covered up the council fire" during negotiations with Thomas Morris in 1797, the Seneca leader Farmer's Brother immediately objected, declaring that "according to your custom, he only who kindles the council fire, has a right to cover it up." (011-309) Red Jacket's retort — "Then as I did not cover it up the council fire is still burning" — demonstrated both the diplomatic weight of the metaphor and his skill in turning protocol to his advantage. (012-028)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

For the Iroquois, the council fire was inseparable from their identity as a self-governing people. At the founding of the Confederacy, the Great Law of Peace established the central council fire at Onondaga, where fifty sachems would meet to deliberate on matters affecting all the nations. Each of the Five (later Six) Nations also maintained its own council fires for internal governance.

Before approaching the council fire, chiefs and warriors observed elaborate protocols of preparation. They halted at a distance from the meeting place, carefully decorated themselves with paint, feathers, and ornaments, and then marched in formal procession to the council ground. (010-120) The rituals that followed — smoking the peace pipe, exchanging wampum belts, and the ceremonial wiping away of tears — were all acts of preparing participants to speak truthfully and listen respectfully at the council fire. The phrase "wipe away their tears, brighten their faces, and clear their throats" captured the belief that only those unburdened by grief and undistracted by personal concerns could participate properly in council deliberations. (010-180)

## Role in the Narrative

Throughout Hubbard's account, the council fire serves as the stage on which Red Jacket's oratorical genius is displayed. Colonel Proctor and Cornplanter arrived at the council fire kindled at Buffalo Creek in April 1791 to negotiate Seneca land concessions. (007-053) When the Seneca addressed "the great council fire of the thirteen United States," they were acknowledging the American government's political legitimacy while simultaneously asserting their own — two sovereign peoples meeting at parallel council fires. (008-186)

The council fire metaphor appears most dramatically in the failed negotiations with Thomas Morris in 1797, where Red Jacket's tactical extinguishing of the fire almost derailed the proceedings. The incident reveals how thoroughly the council fire protocol governed Iroquois expectations of diplomatic conduct — and how deftly Red Jacket could manipulate those expectations. When a council fire was properly maintained, the parties were bound to continue their deliberations in good faith; when it was covered, all bets were off.

At the end of his life, Hubbard lamented that with Red Jacket's passing, "the glory of their council fire" had ceased. (001-027) This elegiac note reflects the broader historical reality: as the Seneca lost their lands and sovereignty, the council fire as a meaningful institution of self-governance was progressively extinguished.
