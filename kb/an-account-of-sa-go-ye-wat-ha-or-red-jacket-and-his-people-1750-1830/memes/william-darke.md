# William Darke

## Biography

William Darke was an American colonel of regulars who commanded a division at the disastrous defeat of General [[arthur-st-clair|St. Clair]] on the Miami, on the fourth of November, 1791. When the Indians charged with unexampled intrepidity upon the center of the two main divisions, those divisions were commanded by General Butler and Colonel Darke. (008-068)

## Role and Influence

Darke's regiment of regulars was St. Clair's instrument for meeting the concealed enemy. Seeing to what disadvantage his troops fought with an unseen foe, St. Clair ordered Colonel Darke, with his regiment of regulars, to rouse the Indians from their covert with the bayonet, and turn their left flank. (008-081)

The charge was made with spirit; the enemy were driven three or four hundred yards, but for want of cavalry or riflemen the pursuit slackened, and the troops were forced to give back in turn, the Indians coming on with a deadlier aim the moment pursuit was relinquished. (008-081)

When at last the ruin of the army made retreat unavoidable, Darke was again called upon.

A retreat was ordered, Colonel Darke being directed to charge the Indians that intercepted the way toward Fort Jefferson, while Major Clark with his battalion covered the rear; these movements were successfully made, and the most of the troops that remained collected in a body and departed, leaving their artillery and baggage. (008-096)

## Key Events

- **St. Clair's defeat (4 November 1791)**: Darke commanded one of the two main divisions upon which the Indians hurled their charge near the Miami villages. (008-068)

- **The bayonet charge**: Ordered to rouse the hidden enemy with the bayonet and turn their left flank, Darke's regulars drove the Indians three or four hundred yards before the pursuit failed for want of cavalry. (008-081)

- **Covering the retreat to Fort Jefferson**: In the final rout Darke was directed to charge the Indians blocking the road to Fort Jefferson, opening the way for the survivors' withdrawal. (008-096)

## Legacy and Historical Significance

In Hubbard's account William Darke stands as one of the few officers whose conduct is singled out amid the collapse of [[arthur-st-clair|St. Clair's]] expedition — the campaign that, following [[josiah-harmar|Harmar's]] failure of the year before, prepared the way for [[anthony-wayne|Wayne's]] later success at Fallen Timbers. (008-068)

His division's charge and his covering of the retreat are recorded as the two moments when order was briefly imposed on a day otherwise given over to slaughter. (008-081) (008-096)
