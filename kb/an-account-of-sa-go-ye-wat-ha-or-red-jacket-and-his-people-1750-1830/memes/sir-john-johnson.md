# Sir John Johnson

## Biography

Sir John Johnson was a son of Sir [[sir-william-johnson|William Johnson]], the crown's superintendent of Indian affairs, and after the outbreak of the Revolution he continued his father's work of attaching the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]] to the British interest. At a council of the Iroquois convened at [[oswego|Oswego]] by Sir John Johnson and other officers and friends of the crown, the assembled nations were informed that the king desired them to assist him in subduing the rebels, who had taken up arms against him. (003-177)

His portrait is among the plates that illustrate Hubbard's account of the period. (P05-004)

## Role and Influence

After the war Johnson, writing from Canada, laboured to keep the Indians firmly opposed to the United States. In a letter to Captain [[joseph-brant|Brant]] dated Quebec, 22 March 1787, he pressed the Mohawk leader not to sit still while the Americans attempted the western posts: (006-081)

> "*It is for your sakes chiefly, if not entirely, that we hold them.* If you become indifferent about them, they may perhaps be given up; what security would you then have? ... whereas, by supporting them, you encourage us to hold them, and encourage new settlements." (006-085)

The posts he had in mind — Oswegatchie, Oswego, [[fort-niagara|Niagara]], [[detroit|Detroit]] and Mackinaw — were those the British still held within territory acknowledged to belong to the United States. (006-084)

## Key Events

- **Council at Oswego**: Johnson helped convene the Iroquois at Oswego and carried the king's request that they take up arms against the colonists, drawing the Six Nations into the [[american-revolution-iroquois|Revolutionary War]] on the British side. (003-177)

- **Letter to Brant (1787)**: From Quebec, Johnson urged Brant and the Indians to support the retention of the western posts as their protection against American encroachment — a policy soon after confirmed by a further letter from Major Mathews. (006-095)
