# Eleazer Ripley

## Biography

Eleazer Ripley commanded one of the American brigades in the 1814 Niagara campaign of the war with Great Britain, in which warriors mostly from the [[seneca-nation|Seneca nation]] were in readiness to offer their services at the opening of the campaign, with Buffalo the appointed place of rendezvous. (017-025) (017-026) (017-027)

## Role and Influence

During the night of the second of July, General Ripley, with a part of his brigade, embarked in boats with a view of landing on the opposite shore one mile above Fort Erie at about daybreak the next morning, while General [[winfield-scott|Scott]] was to cross the Niagara river and make a simultaneous landing below the fort. (017-030) (017-031) (017-032) (017-034)

Though prompt in his departure, Ripley was delayed in reaching his position by a dense fog which misled his pilots. (017-042) (017-043) The two brigades nonetheless enclosed the fort, and at the demand of General [[jacob-brown|Brown]], without firing a gun or making any attempt at resistance, the fort and garrison were surrendered. (017-047) (017-048) (017-049)

## Key Events

- **The capture of Fort Erie**: Ripley's brigade formed one arm of the enveloping movement that took the fort and garrison at the opening of the July 1814 operations. (017-042) (017-049)

- **The advance to Chippewa**: On the evening of the day Fort Erie was captured, Scott proceeded down the river toward Chippewa, and in the evening he was joined by General Brown with General Ripley's brigade, which took post in the same field in rear of Scott. (017-062) (017-066) (017-067) (017-068)

- **The battle of Chippewa**: When the whole British army was discovered in the field, Ripley's brigade was awaited, having been ordered to make a detour through the woods and attack the enemy's flank; they soon came up in the same muddy plight as the volunteers and Indians who had traversed the same ground. (017-208) (017-209) (017-210) (017-211) (017-212)
