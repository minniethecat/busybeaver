# Witchcraft Accusations

## Definition

Witchcraft among the Iroquois was the belief that certain individuals possessed and exercised malevolent supernatural power to cause illness, death, or misfortune. Unlike European witchcraft — which theologically framed the witch as in league with the Devil — Iroquois witchcraft was understood as an innate capacity for harm, often wielded through ritual objects, spells, or simply through the witch's ill will. (019-044)

The Iroquois had well-established procedures for trying and executing suspected witches. Accusations were adjudicated by councils of chiefs, and those convicted could be put to death by the victim's relatives — a practice that persisted into the early nineteenth century, despite efforts by missionaries and American authorities to suppress it. Red Jacket himself was intimately familiar with witchcraft proceedings: he was formally charged with witchcraft by political rivals and was called as a witness in the notorious witchcraft trial of Tom Jemmy. (P04-089)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

In Iroquois society, witchcraft accusations served multiple functions. They explained otherwise inexplicable misfortunes — a sudden death, a hunting failure, a lingering illness — by attributing them to human malevolence rather than random chance. This explanatory function was psychologically powerful: it transformed the chaos of suffering into a comprehensible narrative with identifiable perpetrators and prescribed remedies. (019-044)

But witchcraft accusations were also instruments of political manipulation. A charge of witchcraft could be used to destroy a rival's reputation and influence, as happened when Cornplanter's brother, seeking to discredit his enemies, "conceived the idea of instituting against their enemies, the charge of witchcraft." (014-047) The charge was particularly effective because, as Hubbard notes, "the Indians generally believed" in witchcraft, making it a weapon that could be wielded against almost anyone. (014-047) Red Jacket himself was accused of witchcraft at a moment when his political influence was threatened — the charge was transparently political, as even Hubbard's narrative acknowledges.

## Role in the Narrative

Red Jacket appears in the witchcraft narrative in two distinct roles: as the accused and as a witness. The charge of witchcraft against him came at a moment of political vulnerability, and his defense — characteristically eloquent — turned the accusation back on his accusers by exposing their political motives. (014-002)

More dramatically, Red Jacket was called to testify in the trial of Tom Jemmy, a Seneca man accused of executing a woman convicted of witchcraft. The case raised fundamental questions about the conflict between Iroquois customary law and American criminal jurisdiction. Red Jacket's testimony, which Hubbard records at length, became a philippic against the hypocrisy of white observers who ridiculed Iroquois beliefs while ignoring the witch-burning history of their own ancestors. When he perceived that "the belief of the Indians in witchcraft, was made a subject of ridicule among the bystanders, as well as legal gentlemen present," he delivered a withering rebuttal that compared Iroquois practice favorably with the Salem witch trials and European inquisitions. (019-044)
