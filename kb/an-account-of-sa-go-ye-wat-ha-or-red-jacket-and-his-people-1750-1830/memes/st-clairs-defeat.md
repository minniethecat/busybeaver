# St. Clair's Defeat (1791)

## Background

In the years following the Revolutionary War, the Western Confederacy — an alliance of Shawnee, Miami, Delaware, and other nations — resisted American expansion into the Ohio Country. The United States, determined to assert control over the Northwest Territory, mounted a military expedition under General Arthur St. Clair, the governor of the Northwest Territory.

## Proceedings

St. Clair's army of approximately 1,400 men advanced into the Ohio wilderness in the fall of 1791. On November 4, near the headwaters of the Wabash River, the Western Confederacy launched a devastating surprise attack at dawn. The American camp was overrun; St. Clair's forces suffered over 600 killed — the worst defeat ever inflicted on an American army by Native forces.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The disaster underscored the military capability of Native confederacies and the vulnerability of the American frontier.

For the Iroquois, St. Clair's defeat was both encouraging and cautionary. It demonstrated that Native resistance could succeed against American armies, but it also ensured that the United States would redouble its efforts to secure Iroquois neutrality — a diplomatic priority that shaped the treaty-making of the 1790s.
