# Iroquois Tradition

## Definition

Iroquois tradition was the accumulated inheritance of generations — the customs, beliefs, ceremonies, and social practices that constituted Iroquois identity. It was transmitted orally, from elders to youth, through the recitation of the Great Law of Peace, the performance of seasonal ceremonies, and the daily practice of clan-based social life. (003-100) The "customs that prevailed in the wilds" were not static but living — adapted over centuries while maintaining essential continuity with the ancestral past.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

Tradition provided the framework within which all other aspects of Iroquois life found their meaning. The council of sachems deliberated according to traditional protocols. Orators drew their authority from their mastery of traditional rhetorical forms. Wampum belts preserved the memory of traditional treaties. The clan system, the Long House, the Thanksgiving Address — each was a strand in the fabric of tradition that wove Iroquois society together.

Hubbard's narrative repeatedly returns to the theme of tradition under pressure. He notes how the Iroquois were "accustomed" to their ways — a word that appears repeatedly in his account — and how those accustomed ways were challenged by war, land loss, and missionary activity. (001-056) (001-085) (001-152) The collision between Iroquois tradition and European-American modernity is, in many ways, the central dramatic tension of the book.

## Role in the Narrative

Red Jacket's career as an orator and diplomat was fundamentally dedicated to the defense of tradition. His famous speeches against missionary efforts — which Hubbard records at length — were arguments for the integrity of Iroquois tradition against the claim that it must be replaced by Christianity. Red Jacket insisted that the Great Spirit had made the white man and the Indian different, giving each their own customs, and that neither should impose their ways upon the other.

This defense of tradition was not reactionary nostalgia but a political argument for sovereignty: if Iroquois tradition was valid, then Iroquois self-governance was legitimate, and the United States had no right to dictate the terms of Iroquois cultural life.
