# Canada (British North America)

## Geography

In the context of Hubbard's narrative, "Canada" refers to British North America — the territories north of the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence River that remained under British control after American independence. This included the present-day provinces of Ontario and Quebec. The border between the United States and British Canada ran through the Great Lakes and along the St. Lawrence, placing key Iroquois territories directly athwart an international boundary.

Upper Canada (present-day Ontario), established in 1791 under Governor John Graves Simcoe, was the specific destination for most Iroquois loyalists who relocated northward. The Grand River, where Joseph Brant led his Mohawk followers, became the principal Iroquois settlement in Canadian territory.

## Historical Significance

Canada functioned as both a geographical reality and a political counterweight throughout the period covered by Hubbard's account. For Iroquois leaders dissatisfied with American treaty terms, the proximity of British Canada offered a credible alternative. The British Indian Department maintained posts and agents whose explicit purpose was to sustain influence over the Six Nations, ensuring that the United States could never take Iroquois compliance for granted.

Hubbard notes that "a strong party in Canada, whose control over the Indians was gained by bestowing upon them annually large amounts of goods" (005-157) actively worked to maintain Iroquois allegiance. The British superintendent of Indian affairs in Canada, "while he deprecated hostilities" (008-369), nevertheless pursued policies that kept the Iroquois in a state of dependency and potential military utility.

Governor Simcoe "had caused to be erected" a British fort on American soil (009-250), demonstrating how the Canada-based British presence routinely encroached on the territorial settlement. Land companies "formed in Canada, of those who were most in favor with the British" (005-056) competed with American speculators for influence and access.

## Key Events at This Location

Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea), the Mohawk war leader, embodied the Canadian connection. After the Revolutionary War, Brant "resided in Canada" (005-187) and from there pursued his "ambitious project" of a western Indian confederacy that would unite the Iroquois with the nations of the Ohio Valley and Great Lakes under British protection. (006-002) His Canadian base gave him access to British resources while insulating him from direct American pressure.

The British Indian agent in Canada, Colonel John Butler, maintained close ties with Seneca leaders even after the war. Seneca delegations regularly traveled to Canada to receive gifts and confer with British officials, a pattern that infuriated American negotiators who sought exclusive diplomatic control over the Six Nations.

The War of 1812 brought the Canadian dimension to a crisis point. British forces and their Native allies crossed from Canada into American territory, and Seneca warriors found their loyalties divided between the two powers. The war's conclusion, with the US-British border reaffirmed, permanently separated the Iroquois communities on either side of the line.
