# John Niles Hubbard

## Biography

John Niles Hubbard was born in 1815 and lived through much of the nineteenth century, dying in 1897 at the age of 82. He came of age in the decades following Red Jacket's death in 1830, when the story of the great Seneca orator was still within living memory. Hubbard gathered his material from published sources — including earlier biographies of Red Jacket, accounts of Iroquois diplomacy, and the published narratives of figures like Mary Jemison — as well as, it appears, from living informants who had known the orator or his contemporaries.

His *Account of Sa-Go-Ye-Wat-Ha, or Red Jacket, and His People, 1750–1830* was published in 1886, more than half a century after its subject's death. The book reflects the historical sensibilities of its era: it is admiring of Red Jacket's oratory, sympathetic to the Iroquois plight, but framed within the assumptions of nineteenth-century American historiography. Hubbard saw Red Jacket's life as a tragedy — the story of a great man whose people were destined to fade before the advance of "civilization." This framing, while dated, does not diminish the documentary value of his account, which preserves speeches, treaty proceedings, and anecdotes that would otherwise be lost.

## Role and Influence

Hubbard's role in this wiki is foundational: his book is the sole corpus from which all chapter pages, PN citations, and entity knowledge are derived. Every assertion on every page of this wiki ultimately traces back to Hubbard's text — either directly, through his own observations and transcriptions, or indirectly, through the sources he compiled and preserved. His book opens with the reflection that Red Jacket's name was "widely known" and that "interest naturally awakened in his history" — a framing that reveals Hubbard's own purpose as a biographer writing for an American audience curious about the vanishing frontier. (001-001)

As a biographer, Hubbard was neither a neutral observer nor a polemicist. He wrote with evident sympathy for Red Jacket and the Seneca, but his framework was that of a Christian American looking back on a "vanishing" Native world. Hubbard acknowledged his own religious perspective, describing himself as "a sincere believer in the Christian religion" — a confession that helps readers locate his narrative stance when he evaluates missionaries, pagan practices, and the conversion of Iroquois people. (001-132) Readers of the wiki should be aware that Hubbard's perspective shapes the selection and presentation of events — as any biographer's perspective does. The wiki attempts to surface this awareness through its citation system, which links every factual claim back to the specific passage in Hubbard's text. (001-001)
