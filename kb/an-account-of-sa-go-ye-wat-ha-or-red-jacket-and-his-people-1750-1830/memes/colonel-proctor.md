# Colonel Thomas Proctor

## Biography

Colonel Thomas Proctor served as a United States Indian agent and military officer in the years following the Revolutionary War. His most significant appearance in Hubbard's narrative is his arrival at the Buffalo Creek council fire in April 1791, accompanied by the Seneca war chief Cornplanter, to conduct negotiations with the assembled Iroquois nations. (007-053) (007-002) This mission was part of the federal government's broader effort, in the years after the Revolution, to establish peaceful relations with the Six Nations and to clarify the land and treaty boundaries that the war had thrown into confusion. (007-038)

Proctor's work as an Indian agent placed him in the difficult position of representing a government whose promises to Native nations were often honored in the breach. The annuities and land guarantees that agents like Proctor conveyed at council fires were subject to congressional appropriation, settler encroachment, and the shifting priorities of successive administrations — all of which undermined the credibility of the agents who delivered them.

## Role and Influence

Proctor's role at Buffalo Creek was to convey federal intentions and to gather intelligence about Iroquois dispositions. He arrived at a moment of tension: the Seneca were divided between those, like Cornplanter, who saw accommodation with the United States as the only viable path and those, like Red Jacket, who resisted any concession. Proctor's association with Cornplanter — who escorted him to the council fire — aligned him, in the eyes of many Seneca, with the accommodationist faction. During the council, "Col. Proctor explained fully the object of their coming," but ultimately "finding further negotiation hopeless," he "declared his purpose" to depart. (007-091) (007-107)

The council at Buffalo Creek in 1791 was one of many such gatherings in the post-Revolution period, as the United States and the Six Nations worked to define their new relationship. Proctor's mission, while less famous than Pickering's Canandaigua treaty three years later, was part of the same diplomatic process — the gradual, often contentious establishment of the federal-tribal framework that would govern Iroquois-American relations for the next two centuries.
