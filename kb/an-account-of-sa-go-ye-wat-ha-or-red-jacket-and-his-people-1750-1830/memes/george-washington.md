# George Washington

## Biography

George Washington, the first President of the United States, appears in Hubbard's narrative not as a direct participant in most Iroquois councils but as the ultimate authority whose word guaranteed — or failed to guarantee — the promises made by his agents. When federal commissioners like Timothy Pickering and Thomas Morris negotiated with the Seneca, they invoked Washington's name as the seal of American good faith. (008-186) The Iroquois leaders who received these assurances understood that Washington's word, not the agent's, was what mattered — and they judged the United States accordingly.

Washington's Indian policy, developed during his presidency (1789–1797), sought to balance two competing imperatives: securing peace on the frontier and acquiring Native lands for American settlement. The treaty system — negotiated at council fires, recorded in wampum and on paper, and backed by federal annuities — was his administration's mechanism for managing this tension. The Treaty of Canandaigua (1794), which remains in force today, was the most significant product of Washington's Iroquois policy. (018-029)

## Role and Influence

Washington's influence on the Iroquois operated through the officials he appointed and the policies he set. His choice of Timothy Pickering as commissioner to the Six Nations in 1794 reflected a preference for treaty-based accommodation over military confrontation — a preference that distinguished his administration from the more aggressive land-acquisition policies of some of his successors.

To the Seneca leaders who met with federal agents, "President Washington" was more than a name; he was the personification of the American government's credibility. When Washington declared, at the Canandaigua council, that the treaty would be "permanent," the Seneca recorded this promise in wampum and invoked it at subsequent councils for generations. (008-186) The gap between Washington's promises and the subsequent actions of the United States — as annuities were delayed, lands were encroached upon, and treaties were renegotiated under pressure — became a recurring theme in Seneca diplomacy.

## Key Events

- **Treaty of Canandaigua (1794)**: Washington's administration, through Pickering, negotiated the landmark treaty that established the federal-tribal relationship with the Six Nations. (008-186)
- **Washington's Indian Policy**: Developed the treaty-and-annuity framework that governed US-Iroquois relations during the critical post-Revolution decades.
