# George Hosmer

## Biography

George Hosmer was a resident of Avon, in the Genesee country of Western New York, whose personal acquaintance with [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] made him one of the sources later drawn upon for anecdotes of the chief. His home town lay near Canawangus, the Seneca settlement in the vicinity of the present town of Avon. (020-201)

His recollections passed into the record through Colonel Stone: the account cites "Mrs. George Hosmer to Col. Stone" (020-358) and again "Geo. Hosmer, Esq., to Col. Stone" as authorities for the chief's sayings. (020-383)

## Role and Influence

Hosmer's most fully told encounter belongs to a journey to a treaty. On one occasion when Mr. George Hosmer of Avon, and several others, were on their way to attend a certain treaty, the Indians one evening after the fatigues of the day were unusually mirthful. (020-301)

[[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], conceiving the idea that Hosmer, who was unacquainted with their language, might suppose he was the subject of their mirth, caused them to be silent, and through his interpreter Captain Parrish addressed him:

> "We have been made uncomfortable by the storm; we are now warm and comfortable, it has caused us to feel cheerful and merry. But I hope our friend who is traveling with us will not feel hurt at this merriment, or suppose that we are taking advantage of his ignorance of our language, to make him in any manner the subject of our mirth." (020-304)

To this Mr. Hosmer replied that, knowing himself to be in the company of brave and honorable men, he could not allow himself to entertain such an impression; after which they resumed their merriment, and Red Jacket his gravity. (020-313)

On another occasion, while Hosmer was accompanying Red Jacket to an Indian council, he inquired the chief's opinion of Hot Bread, the sachem of the Canawangus clan; Red Jacket answered that Hot Bread was a "Big man here," laying his hand on his abdomen, "but very small here," bringing his palm with emphasis to his forehead. (020-201)

## Key Events

- **The journey to the treaty**: Traveling with Red Jacket and his party to a treaty, Hosmer received the chief's courteous assurance that the Indians' merriment was not at his expense. (020-301) (020-304) (020-313)

- **The judgment on Hot Bread**: On the road to a council, Hosmer drew from Red Jacket his famously scornful estimate of the sachem Hot Bread. (020-201)

- **A source for the biographers**: Both George Hosmer and Mrs. Hosmer supplied Colonel Stone with material for the record of Red Jacket. (020-358) (020-383)

## Legacy and Historical Significance

In Hubbard's account George Hosmer belongs to the circle of Genesee-country residents whose firsthand recollections preserved the conversation and character of [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] for later writers. (020-383)

His remembered exchanges are valued less for public events than for the private glimpses they give of the orator — his courtesy toward a stranger and his cutting wit toward an unworthy chief. (020-304) (020-201)
