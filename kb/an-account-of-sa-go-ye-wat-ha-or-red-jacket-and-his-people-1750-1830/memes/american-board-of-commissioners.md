# American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions

## Overview

The American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions (ABCFM) was the first organized missionary society in the United States, established in 1810 by Congregationalist and Presbyterian leaders in Boston. The Board sent missionaries to numerous foreign and domestic fields, including to the Seneca Nation in western New York. The arrival of ABCFM missionaries among the Seneca was the direct catalyst for the religious controversies that divided the Seneca into "pagan" and "Christian" factions — a division that had profound political and social consequences through the 1820s. (015-131)

Hubbard's account describes how the arrival of a missionary from Massachusetts sparked intense debate within the Seneca community, with Red Jacket emerging as the leading voice of opposition. The missionary's efforts to convert the Seneca were seen by traditionalists as an extension of the cultural assault that accompanied land dispossession. (015-138)

## Structure and Governance

The American Board was governed by a board of commissioners based in Boston and supported by contributing congregations throughout New England and the Middle Atlantic states. The organization trained missionaries, funded their work, and directed their assignments. Missionaries were expected to learn native languages, translate religious texts, establish schools, and convert native peoples to Christianity. (015-131)

The Board's missionaries to the Seneca included Mr. Harris, who figures prominently in Hubbard's account of Red Jacket's final years. The missionaries worked from a mission house established on the Buffalo Creek Reservation, where they conducted services and provided instruction to those Seneca who were receptive to Christian teaching. They also served as intermediaries between the Seneca and white society, advocating for peaceful accommodation and agricultural settlement. (021-069)

## Historical Role

The American Board's mission to the Seneca began in earnest around 1811, when a missionary from Massachusetts arrived at Buffalo Creek. Hubbard notes that this was "the occasion of calling forth one of the most effective efforts of Red Jacket's life" — his famous speech rejecting the missionary's message and defending the traditional Seneca religion. (015-131) Red Jacket argued that the missionaries divided the people, undermined traditional authority, and sought to remake Seneca culture in the image of white society.

The religious division created by missionary activity became a critical political weapon. The Ogden Land Company exploited the pagan-Christian split to weaken Seneca resistance to land sales, as Christian Seneca were more inclined toward accommodation with white society. This conflict reached its apex in 1824 with the trial and temporary deposition of Red Jacket. Despite these controversies, Hubbard notes that the missionary enterprise persisted, and that Mr. Harris maintained a relationship with Red Jacket to the very end of his life. (021-069)

The ABCFM's work among the Seneca was part of a broader pattern of missionary activity among Native American nations in the early republic, which combined genuine religious conviction with a cultural assimilationist agenda that often aligned with the expansionist goals of the United States government. (019-229)
