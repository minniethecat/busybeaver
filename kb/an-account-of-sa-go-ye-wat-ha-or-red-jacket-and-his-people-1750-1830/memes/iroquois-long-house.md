# Long House

## Definition

The Long House (Ganonsyoni in the Iroquois language) was both a physical structure and a political symbol of the Iroquois Confederacy. As a dwelling, it was an extended bark-covered building — sometimes exceeding one hundred feet in length — housing multiple families of the same clan lineage under one roof. As a metaphor, the Confederacy itself was conceived as a single great Long House stretching across what is now upstate New York: "The eastern door was guarded by the Mohawks, who resided at one, and its western by the Senecas, who dwelt at the other extremity of this abode." (002-027) (002-028) The Onondagas, at the center, tended the council fire that gave light and warmth to all the nations dwelling under the common roof.

Hubbard's narrative records how the Iroquois were "accustomed to style their Long House" as the image of their collective nation. (002-021) It was, he writes, a "shadowy" image — one freighted with centuries of tradition and belonging.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The Long House represented the fundamental social unit of Iroquois life. Within its walls, multiple generations of a matrilineal clan lived together — grandmothers, mothers, daughters, and their husbands and children. The senior woman of the Long House exercised authority over domestic and agricultural life, and the clan mothers who nominated sachems emerged from this domestic sphere.

As a political symbol, the Long House embodied the principles of unity within diversity that defined the Confederacy. Each nation retained its autonomy within its own section of the house, but all were bound by the common roof and the common fire. This metaphor of shared dwelling — rather than conquest or hierarchy — was central to how the Iroquois understood their own political order.

## Role in the Narrative

The Long House metaphor underlies Hubbard's account of Iroquois political unity and its eventual fragmentation. The division of the Confederacy during the Revolutionary War — with some nations siding with the British and others attempting neutrality — represented a fire going out at the center of the house. Red Jacket's lifelong struggle to preserve Iroquois sovereignty can be understood as an effort to keep the Long House standing — to prevent its timbers from being dismantled by land cessions and political pressure.
