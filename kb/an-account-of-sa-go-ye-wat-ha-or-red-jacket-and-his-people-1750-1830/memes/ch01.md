[001-001]Name widely known—Interest naturally awakened in his history—His origin

[001-002]—Development of his genius—Opinion of Capt. Horatio Jones—Customs of

[001-003]his people—Their love of [[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]]—Distinguished orators among them—The

[001-004]inviting field opened.

[001-005]Among the aborigines of this country, few names have excited a deeper

[001-006]interest, or have been more widely and familiarly known than that of RED

[001-007]JACKET. The occasion of this notoriety was the rare fact that, though a

[001-008]rude and unlettered son of the forest, he was distinguished for the arts

[001-009]and accomplishments of the orator. His life marks an era in the history of

[001-010]his nation and his name like that of Demosthenes, is forever associated

[001-011]with eloquence.

[001-012]Other circumstances however, impart interest to his history. His was the

[001-013]last great name of a nation, and he is entitled to remembrance, on the

[001-014]soil which was once the home of his fathers. And though linked with a

[001-015]melancholy association, as connected with the waning history of a people

[001-016]that once laid a claim to greatness, but are now fast passing into

[001-017]obscurity, it is not on this account the less attractive, but [[treaty-annuities|presents]]

[001-018]another reason for our regard.

[001-019]Such was the name of SA-GO-YE-WAT-HA, or, as he has more commonly been

[001-020]called, [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]. Having risen, by the force of his eloquence, from an

[001-021]obscure station to the highest rank among his people, he became

[001-022]conspicuous in all of those great transactions, in which they gradually

[001-023]relinquished a title to their old hunting grounds, and gave place to the

[001-024]intrusive white man. And he lived to see his nation pass from the pride of

[001-025]their ancient dominion, to so humble an inheritance, that his last days

[001-026]were embittered with the thought, that the *red men* were destined to

[001-027]become extinct. With him has ceased the glory of their [[council-fire|council fire]], and

[001-028]of their name.

[001-029]His origin, as we have intimated, was obscure. He must be introduced, as

[001-030]he has come down to us, without rank or pedigree. His pedigree nature

[001-031]acknowledged, and gave him a right to become great among her sons. His

[001-032]birth is a matter of fact, its time and place, circumstances of

[001-033]conjecture. Some affirm that he was born at the Old [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] Castle, near

[001-034]the foot of Seneca lake, not far from 1750. [Footnote: Hist. of North

[001-035]American tribes by Thos. L. McKenney.]

[001-036]Another [[iroquois-tradition|tradition]] awards the honor of his birth to a place at, or near

[001-037]Canoga, on the banks of the [[cayuga-nation|Cayuga]] lake. [Footnote: Schoolcraft's Report.]

[001-038]Who were his parents? and what, his early history? As the wave casts upon

[001-039]the shore some treasured fragment, and then recedes to mingle with its

[001-040]parent waters, so their names, and much of his early history have been

[001-041]lost in the oblivion of the past.

[001-042]So likewise it is uncertain, as to the time when the wonderful powers of

[001-043]his genius began to be developed, or as to the steps by which he arrived

[001-044]at the high distinction of orator among his people.

[001-045]Whether by dint of study he gained the requisite discipline of mind, and

[001-046]acquired that elegance of diction for which he was distinguished; whether

[001-047]by repeated trial and failure, accompanied by a proud ambition, and an

[001-048]unyielding purpose, he reached, like Demosthenes, the summit of his

[001-049]aspirations; or, assisted more by nature than by art, emerged, like

[001-050]Patrick Henry at once, into the grand arena of mind, and by a single

[001-051]effort attained distinction and fame, is to be gathered more from

[001-052]circumstances than from facts.

[001-053]It is generally conceded, however, that the powers of his intellect were

[001-054]of the highest order. Captain Horatio Jones, the well known interpreter

[001-055]and agent among the Indians, and than whom no one was more intimately

[001-056]acquainted with this orator of the Seneca nation, was accustomed to speak

[001-057]of him as the greatest man that ever lived. "For," said he, "the great men

[001-058]of our own and of other times, have become so by education; but RED JACKET

[001-059]WAS AS NATURE MADE HIM. Had he enjoyed their advantages, he would have

[001-060]surpassed them, since it can hardly be supposed that they, without these,

[001-061]would have equalled him." [Footnote: Conversation of the author with Col.

[001-062]Wm. Jones, of [[big-tree|Geneseo]], Livingston Co., N. Y., son of Capt. Horatio Jones.]

[001-063]Some allowance should be made for this statement, perhaps, on the ground

[001-064]that Mr. Jones was a warm admirer of the orator's genius; yet his

[001-065]admiration sprang from an intimate knowledge of him, seen under

[001-066]circumstances, that afforded the best opportunity of forming a just

[001-067]opinion of his talents; and these, he maintained, "*were among the noblest

[001-068]that nature ever conferred upon man*."

[001-069]But genius, while it may have smoothed the way, may not have spared him

[001-070]the pains, by which ordinary minds ascend to greatness. For since it is so

[001-071]universally the fact, that the path to eminence, is rugged and steep, and

[001-072]the gifts of fame seldom bestowed but in answer to repeated toil;

[001-073]curiosity would inquire by what means one, who was reputed a barbarian,

[001-074]gained the highest distinction ever awarded to civilized man. It is not

[001-075]enough to reply simply, "*that nature made him so*," or to receive,

[001-076]without qualification, his own proud assertion, "I AM AN ORATOR, I WAS

[001-077]BORN AN ORATOR." The laws of mind are the same for peasants, and princes

[001-078]in intellect; great minds as well as small, must take measures to compass

[001-079]their object, or leave it unattained.

[001-080]It does not appear that his genius was sudden, or precocious in its

[001-081]development. It is said that his mind, naturally active and brilliant,

[001-082]gradually opened, until it reached its meridian splendor. Nor did his

[001-083]powers grow without any means to mature and perfect them. As the young oak

[001-084]is strengthened by warring with the storm, so the faculties of his mind

[001-085]gained force by entering freely into conflicts of opinion. Accustomed to

[001-086]canvass in private the questions which agitated the councils of his

[001-087]nation, he began to ascertain the reality of his own power, and by

[001-088]measuring his own with other minds, he gained the confidence that flows

[001-089]from superior wisdom. [Footnote: Conversation with Col. Wm. Jones.]

[001-090]The tastes and regulations of his own people favored very much, the

[001-091]promptings of his genius. They were lovers of eloquence, and their form of

[001-092]government fostered its cultivation. This though differing but little from

[001-093]the simplicity found in rude states of society, presented a feature

[001-094]peculiar among a people not far advanced in civilization, which served

[001-095]greatly to promote elevation of mind, and advance them far above a

[001-096]condition of barbarism. They were in the habit of meeting in public

[001-097]assemblies, to discuss those questions that pertained to the interests, or

[001-098]destiny of their nation. Around their council fires their chiefs and

[001-099]warriors gathered, and entered freely, so far as their dignity,

[001-100]consideration, or power of debate admitted, into a deliberation on public

[001-101]affairs. And here were manifested an ability and decorum which civilized

[001-102]nations even, have viewed with admiration and surprise. For though we

[001-103]might suppose their eloquence must have partaken of rant and rhapsody,

[001-104]presenting a mass of incoherent ideas, depending for their interest on the

[001-105]animation of gesture and voice, with which they were uttered, yet we would

[001-106]do injustice to their memory, if we did not give their orators the credit

[001-107]of speaking as much to the purpose, and of exhibiting as great a force of

[001-108]intellect, as many who would claim a higher place than they in the scale

[001-109]of intelligence and refinement.

[001-110]Many of their orators were distinguished for strength of mind, and in

[001-111]native power of genius, might compare favorably, with the men of any age

[001-112]or clime. The names of Garangula, Adario, [[hendrick|Hendrick]], Skenandoah, Logan and

[001-113]others, might be mentioned with pride by any people.

[001-114][Illustration: KING HENDRICK]

[001-115]GARANGULA, has been styled the very Nestor of his nation, whose powers of

[001-116]mind would not suffer in comparison with a Roman, or more modern Senator.

[001-117][Footnote: Drake.]

[001-118]ADARIO is said to have been a man of "great mind, the bravest of the

[001-119]brave," and possessing altogether the best qualities of any Indian known

[001-120]to the French in [[canada|Canada]]. [Footnote: Charlevoix.]

[001-121]It has been remarked of HENDRICK, that for capacity, bravery, vigor of

[001-122]mind, and immovable integrity united, he excelled all the aboriginal

[001-123]inhabitants of the United States, of whom any knowledge has come down to

[001-124]the present time. [Footnote: Dr. Dwight.]

[001-125]SKENANDOAH in his youth was a brave and intrepid warrior, and in his riper

[001-126]years one of the best of counsellors among the North American tribes. He

[001-127]possessed a most vigorous mind, and was alike active, sagacious, and

[001-128]persevering. He will long be remembered for a saying of his to one who

[001-129]visited him toward the close of life; "I am," said he, "an aged hemlock,

[001-130]the winds of an hundred winters have whistled through my branches. I am

[001-131]dead at the top. The generation to which I belonged has run away and left

[001-132]me." He was a sincere believer in the Christian religion, and added to the

[001-133]above "why I live the Great and Good Spirit only knows. Pray to my Jesus,

[001-134]that I may have patience to wait for my appointed time to die." [Footnote:

[001-135]Annals of Tryon County.]

[001-136]And Mr. Jefferson regarded the appeal of LOGAN to the white men, after the

[001-137]extirpation of his family, as without a parallel in the history of

[001-138]eloquence.

[001-139]These were men who have been revered by the civilized world, as worthy of

[001-140]a place with the distinguished and great among mankind.

[001-141]"Oratory was not alone a natural gift, but an art among the Iroquois. It

[001-142]enjoined painful study, unremitting practice, and sedulous observation of

[001-143]the style, and methods of the best masters. Red Jacket did not rely upon

[001-144]his native powers alone, but cultivated the art with the same assiduity

[001-145]that characterized the great Athenian orator. The Iroquois, as their

[001-146]earliest English historian observed, cultivated an Attic or classic

[001-147]elegance of speech, which entranced every ear, among their red auditory."

[001-148][Footnote: Mr. Bryant's speech.]

[001-149]Those public games, entertainments, religious ceremonies and dances,

[001-150]common among the Indian tribes, added interest to their council

[001-151]gatherings, and made them a scene of attraction for the entire nation.

[001-152]Thither the young and old of both sexes were accustomed to resort, and,

[001-153]assembled at their national forum, listened with profound attention and

[001-154]silence to each word spoken by their orators. "The unvarying courtesy,

[001-155]sobriety and dignity of their convocations led one of their learned Jesuit

[001-156]historians to liken them to the Roman Senate." [Footnote: W. C. Bryant's

[001-157]speech before the Buffalo Historical Society on the occasion of the reinterment of Red Jacket's remains.]

[001-158]"Their language was flexible and sonorous, the sense largely depending

[001-159]upon inflection, copious in vowel sounds, abounding in metaphor; affording

[001-160]constant opportunity for the ingenious combination and construction of

[001-161]words to image delicate, and varying shades of thought, and to express

[001-162]vehement manifestations of passion; admitting of greater and more sudden

[001-163]variations in pitch, than is permissable in English oratory, and

[001-164]encouraging pantomimic gesture, for greater force and effect. In other

[001-165]words it was not a cold, artificial, mechanical medium for the expression

[001-166]of thought or emotion, or the concealment of either, but was constructed,

[001-167]as we may fancy, much as was the tuneful tongue spoken by our first

[001-168]parents, who stood in even closer relations to nature." [Footnote: Ib.]

[001-169]Hence, though the Iroquois were a warlike people, and delighted in deeds

[001-170]of bravery, there was an inviting field opened to one, who could chain

[001-171]their attention by his eloquence, and sway their emotions at will.

[001-172]Such advantages being presented for the exercise of the powers of oratory,

[001-173]it can hardly be supposed that a mind endowed as richly, as was Red

[001-174]Jacket's, by the gifts of nature, would fail to perceive the path in which

[001-175]lay the true road to eminence among his people. And his subsequent career

[001-176]indicates but too clearly, the choice he made of the field in which to

[001-177]exercise his noble powers.
