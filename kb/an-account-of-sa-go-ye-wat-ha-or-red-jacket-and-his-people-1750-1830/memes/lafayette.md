# Lafayette

## Biography

The Marquis de Lafayette, the French general who had served the American cause in the Revolution, was present at the treaty of peace with the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]] at Fort [[fort-stanwix|Stanwix]] in 1784. (020-019) Four decades later, during his celebrated tour of the United States, he met again the Seneca orator who had spoken at that council. (020-016)

## Role and Influence

Hubbard offers the interview with Lafayette as evidence of [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s ease in the company of great men, for the orator never appeared to be intimidated or embarrassed at the thought of meeting them, but seemed to regard himself on an equality with them. (020-013) On being presented to the general in 1825, Red Jacket asked whether he recollected being present at the treaty at Fort Stanwix, and Lafayette replied that he remembered that great council very well. The general then inquired what had become of the young chief who had resisted the burying of the hatchet:

> "'And what,' said he, 'has become of the young chief, who resisted so strenuously and eloquently on that occasion, the idea of the Indians' burying the hatchet?' '*He is before you*,' was the instant reply." (020-020)

## Key Events

- **The wig anecdote**: When the general's abundant hair was noticed, it was explained that Lafayette was indebted for it to a wig; whereupon the orator playfully remarked, referring to the practice of his people in war, that he might supply the deficiency by scalping some of his neighbors. (020-035)

- **Levasseur's testimony**: M. Levasseur, the secretary of General Lafayette, recorded that the orator, though much worn down by time and intemperance, preserved yet in a surprising degree the exercise of all his faculties, and obstinately refused to speak any language but that of his own people. (020-036)

- **A gift of words**: A few Indian words that Lafayette had picked up during his earlier visit to the country, on being repeated to the orator, gratified him exceedingly and appeared to increase very much his regard for the general. (020-048)
