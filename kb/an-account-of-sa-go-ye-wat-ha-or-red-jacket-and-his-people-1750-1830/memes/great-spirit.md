# Great Spirit

## Definition

The Great Spirit — known to the Iroquois as Hawenneyu, the Master of Life — is the supreme and benevolent creator deity of Haudenosaunee traditional religion.


The Iroquois gave thanks to the Great Spirit "for having given them birth" and for the natural world that sustained their people. (002-175) The Great Spirit was understood not as an abstract philosophical principle but as an active presence who could send judgment, bestow favor, and preside over the most solemn human covenants.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

In Iroquois tradition, the Great Spirit was the proprietor of the land and the ultimate authority over human affairs.


Hubbard records how Iroquois speakers consistently invoked the Great Spirit as the witness to treaties — the force that guaranteed the permanence of agreements made between peoples. When Red Jacket and other sachems addressed councils, they spoke of themselves as standing "in the presence of the Great Spirit." (008-243)

The Great Spirit's role extended into daily life and seasonal ceremony. The Thanksgiving Address, still recited in Iroquois communities today, acknowledges the Great Spirit's gifts of earth, water, plants, animals, and celestial bodies. Hubbard notes the striking dignity of Iroquois invocation: "I observe that the Great Spirit might smile on one people, and frown upon another." (008-269)

## Role in the Narrative

The Great Spirit appears throughout Hubbard's account as both a rhetorical touchstone and a genuine object of reverence.


At the council of Tioga Point, the assembled chiefs "thank the Great Spirit" for the safe arrival of their American counterparts. (007-061) When adversity struck — such as the devastating sickness that swept through Iroquois villages — it was interpreted as "a judgment sent by the Great Spirit, to punish them" for failings in conduct. (004-063)


The Great Spirit also features in the religious controversies that marked Red Jacket's later life, as Christian missionaries sought to convert the Senecas. Red Jacket's famous defense of traditional Iroquois religion — that the Great Spirit had made the white man and the Indian different, giving each their own customs and beliefs — is one of the most quoted passages in Hubbard's biography.


(008-158) This argument, that the Great Spirit intentionally made nations distinct, became the philosophical cornerstone of Red Jacket's resistance to missionary efforts. (011-054)

Red Jacket invoked the Great Spirit in nearly every formal address:

> Brother: It was the will of the Great Spirit that we should meet together this day. He orders all things, and has given us a fine day for our council. He has taken his garment from before the sun, and caused it to shine with brightness upon us. Our eyes are opened that we see clearly; our ears are unstopped, that we have been able to hear distinctly the words you have spoken. For all these favors we thank the Great Spirit, and Him only.
>
> (008-142)

The Great Spirit was understood as a universal moral arbiter:

> I observe that the Great Spirit might smile on one people, and frown upon another. This is the reason we are here to-day. We wish to be at peace with you, and we desire that the Great Spirit may look down upon us both with an eye of favor.
>
> (008-269)
