[003-001]Name [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], how acquired—Indian name—Conferred name—Singular

[003-002][[iroquois-tradition|tradition]]—Red Jacket during the war of the Revolution—Neutrality of the

[003-003]Indians—Services sought by Great Britain—Sketch of [[sir-william-johnson|Sir William Johnson]]—

[003-004]Position of Red Jacket—Taunt of cowardice—Testimony of [[little-beard|Little Beard]]—

[003-005]Charge made by [[joseph-brant|Brant]]—Red Jacket's indifference—Anecdote—Early love of

[003-006][[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]]—Interesting reminiscences.

[003-007]The name Red Jacket, so familiar to the whites, was acquired during the

[003-008]war of the Revolution. He was distinguished at this time as well as

[003-009]afterward, for his fleetness on foot, his intelligence and activity.

[003-010]Having attracted the attention of a British officer by the vivacity of his

[003-011]manners, and the speedy execution of those errands with which he was

[003-012]intrusted, he received either in token of admiration, or for services

[003-013]rendered, or both, a beautifully ornamented jacket of a scarlet color.

[003-014]This he took pride in wearing, and when worn out, he was presented with

[003-015]another, and continued to wear this peculiar dress until it became a mark

[003-016]of distinction, and gave him the name by which he was afterward best

[003-017]known. At a treaty held at [[canandaigua|Canandaigua]] in 1794, Captain Parrish, who was

[003-018]for many years agent of the United States for the Indians, presented him

[003-019]with another *red jacket* to perpetuate a name of which he was

[003-020]particularly fond. [Footnote: McKenney's Indian Biography Politely favored

[003-021]by Alfred B. Street, Esq., and assistant Mr. J. H. Hickox, of the State

[003-022]Library, [[albany|Albany]], N. Y.]

[003-023]His original name was Oti-ti-ani, *always ready*. Sa-go-ye-wat-ha, the

[003-024]title conferred upon him at his election to the dignity of Sachem, has

[003-025]been rendered, "*The keeper awake, he keeps them awake, and the author, or

[003-026]cause of a wakeful spirit*." [Footnote: This latter translation was

[003-027]given to the author by the late Wm. Jones, a half-blood, son-in-law of Red

[003-028]Jacket and a chief of some note. This interpretation was given to some

[003-029]gentlemen from Buffalo who proposed to erect a monument at Red Jacket's

[003-030]grave. It was given in a full council of the chiefs of his tribe.]

[003-031]The name is connected with a curious superstition among his people, and

[003-032]will best be understood, by an acquaintance with the circumstances under

[003-033]which it is used.

[003-034]If during the still hours of night, an Indian's mind is taken up with

[003-035]thoughts that cause sleep to pass from him, preventing every effort of

[003-036]Morpheus to lock him in fond embrace, he ascribes it to a spirit, which he

[003-037]calls Sa-go-ye-wat-ha.

[003-038]The impressions made are regarded as ominous of some important event,

[003-039]joyful or otherwise, according to the feelings awakened. If his thoughts

[003-040]are of a pleasing nature, he is led to anticipate the occurrence of some

[003-041]joyful event. If they are of a melancholy turn, he regards it as

[003-042]foreboding evil.

[003-043]He may be led to dwell with interest on some absent friend; that friend he

[003-044]will expect to see the next day, or soon after. Yet should his thoughts be

[003-045]troubled or anxious, he would expect to hear soon of that friend's death,

[003-046]or that something evil had befallen him. [Footnote: Conversation with Wm.

[003-047]Jones, [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] chief.]

[003-048]Such was the spirit they called Sa-go-ye-wat-ha. He could arrest the

[003-049]current of their thought, bring before them visions of delight, or send

[003-050]upon them melancholy reflections, and fill their minds with anxiety and

[003-051]gloom.

[003-052]This title conferred on Red Jacket, while it indicated the cause of his

[003-053]elevation, presented the highest compliment that could be paid to his

[003-054]powers of oratory. By the magic spell of his words, he could control their

[003-055]minds, make their hearts beat quick with emotions of joy, or send over

[003-056]them at will the deep pulsations of grief.

[003-057]The incident referred to as giving rise to the name, Red Jacket,

[003-058]introduces him in connection with the war of the Revolution. As his

[003-059]conduct during this period has been the subject of frequent remark,

[003-060]severely criticised by some, and not very favorably viewed by others,

[003-061]justice to the orator's memory requires a brief statement of his reasons

[003-062]for the course he pursued.

[003-063]While thoughts of this contest were pending, the colonists took measures

[003-064]to secure the favorable disposition of the Iroquois, and these efforts at

[003-065]the time were successful.

[003-066]The general government advised them to remain neutral, during the

[003-067]anticipated conflict. This course met the approval of their most

[003-068]considerate [[sachem-chieftainship|sachems]]. For though inured to war, and apt to enter with

[003-069]avidity into the excitement of a conflict, their forces had been reduced

[003-070]by recent encounters with the Indians at the west, and south, and also

[003-071]with the French; and the few intervening years of peace served to convince

[003-072]them of its value, and caused them to receive with favor this proposition

[003-073]from our government.

[003-074]At a council held with the Iroquois at German Flats, in June, 1776, by

[003-075]Gen. Schuyler, who had been appointed for this purpose, these assurances

[003-076]of [[neutrality-policy|neutrality]] were renewed.

[003-077]Great Britain also was not indifferent about the course these Indian

[003-078]tribes would pursue. Wishing to prevent an alliance of the Indians with

[003-079]the colonists, willing to secure forces already on the ground, and with a

[003-080]view possibly, of striking terror into the minds of her rebellious

[003-081]subjects, her agents in this country spared no pains to enlist the

[003-082]sympathies of the Iroquois on her side.

[003-083]In this they were but too successful. Through their agents, Britain had

[003-084]been in correspondence with these tribes for more than a hundred years,

[003-085]had supplied them with implements of war, articles of clothing, and with

[003-086]many of the comforts and conveniences of life. The Indians had learned to

[003-087]be dependent upon her, and they called her king their "*great father*

[003-088]over the water." Her agents spent their lives among them. Through them

[003-089]their communications were made to the crown, and they regarded them as

[003-090]essential to their happiness. Hence they exerted a very great influence

[003-091]over them.

[003-092]This was especially true of Sir William Johnson, who died at Johnson Hall

[003-093]in the month of June, 1774.

[003-094]Mr. Johnson was a native of Ireland, of a good family and fitted by nature

[003-095]and education, to adorn the walks of civilized life. He came to this

[003-096]country not far from 1738, as land agent of his uncle, Sir Peter Warren,

[003-097]an admiral in the English navy, who had acquired a considerable tract of

[003-098]land upon the [[mohawk-nation|Mohawk]], in the present county of Montgomery.

[003-099]Possessing a romantic disposition, he readily adapted himself to the rude

[003-100]customs that prevailed in the wilds of America.

[003-101]The *Gentleman's Magazine* of London said of him in 1755,—"Besides

[003-102]his skill and experience as an officer, he is particularly happy in making

[003-103]himself beloved by all sorts of people, and can conform to all companies

[003-104]and to all conversations. He is very much of a gentleman in genteel

[003-105]company, but as the inhabitants next to him are mostly Dutch, he sits down

[003-106]with them and smokes his tobacco, drinks flip, and talks of improvements,

[003-107]bear and beaver skins. Being surrounded with Indians, he speaks several of

[003-108]their languages well, and has always some of them with him. He takes care

[003-109]of their wives, and old Indians, when they go out on parties; and even

[003-110]wears their dress. In short, by his honest dealings with them in trade,

[003-111]and his courage, which has often been successfully tried with them, he has

[003-112]so endeared himself to them, that they chose him as one of their chief

[003-113]sachems, or princes, and esteem him as their father."

[003-114]Not far from the year 1755, while the French and English were at war, he

[003-115]was made general of the colonial militia, and by virtue of a leadership

[003-116]that had been created by the Iroquois, he was head warrior of all the

[003-117]Indian tribes, who favored the English.

[003-118][Illustration: JOHNSON HALL]

[003-119]The gifts of his sovereign, and the opportunity he had of purchasing

[003-120]Indian lands, were the means of his securing great wealth. The ease with

[003-121]which he secured land of the Indians is illustrated by an amusing

[003-122]occurrence between him and a noted chief, [[hendrick|Hendrick]]. Soon after entering

[003-123]upon his duties as Superintendent of Indian Affairs in this country, he

[003-124]received from England some richly embroidered suits of clothes.

[003-125]Hendrick, a Mohawk chief, was present, when the package containing them

[003-126]was opened, and could not refrain from expressing his admiration of them.

[003-127]He went away very thoughtful, but soon after returned and said to Sir

[003-128]William, that he had dreamed a dream.

[003-129]"Ah! And what did you dream?" said Sir William.

[003-130]"I dreamed," said Hendrick, "that you gave me one of those new suits of

[003-131]uniform."

[003-132]Sir William could not refuse it, and one of the elegant suits was

[003-133]presented to Hendrick, who went away to show his gift to his countrymen

[003-134]and left Sir William to tell the joke to his friends. A while after the

[003-135]general met Hendrick and said—"Hendrick, *I have dreamed a dream*."

[003-136]Whether the Sachem mistrusted he was now to be taken in his own net or

[003-137]not, is not certain, but he also inquired,—"And what did you dream?"

[003-138]The general said he dreamed that Hendrick presented him with a certain

[003-139]piece of land which he described. It consisted of about five hundred

[003-140]acres, of the most valuable land in the Mohawk valley.

[003-141]Hendrick replied,—"It is yours;" but, shaking his head, said, "Sir

[003-142]William I will never dream with you again." [Footnote: Drake's Book of the

[003-143]Indians.]

[003-144]Sir William's large estate, the partiality of his countrymen, together

[003-145]with his military honors, and his great influence with the Indians,

[003-146]rendered him "as near a prince as anything the back-woods of America has

[003-147]witnessed." [Footnote: The expression of an English lady.—Turner.]

[003-148]He built two spacious and convenient residences on the Mohawk river, known

[003-149]as Johnson Castle and Johnson Hall. The Hall was his summer residence.

[003-150]Here he lived something like a sovereign, kept an excellent table for

[003-151]strangers and officers, whom the course of duty led into these wilds, and

[003-152]by confiding entirely in the Indians, and treating them with truth and

[003-153]justice, never yielding to solicitations once refused, they were taught to

[003-154]repose in him the utmost confidence.

[003-155]His personal popularity with the Indians, gave him an influence over them

[003-156]greater it is supposed, than any one of our own race has ever possessed.

[003-157]He was the first Englishman that contended successfully with French Indian

[003-158]diplomacy, as exercised by their governors, missionaries and traders.

[003-159][Footnote: Turner's Phelps and Gorham Purchase.]

[003-160]Had he lived until the war of the Revolution, it is supposed by some he

[003-161]might have remained neutral, and have kept the Indians from engaging in

[003-162]the conflict, though this is altogether uncertain. He lived to see the

[003-163]gathering of the storm that swept away most of his great possessions.

[003-164]On the death of Sir William, his son John Johnson succeeded to his titles

[003-165]and estate. The office of General Superintendent of the Indians, fell into

[003-166]the hands of Col. Guy Johnson, a son-in-law, who appointed Col. Claus,

[003-167]another son-in-law, as his deputy.

[003-168]Into their hands fell the property, and a large share of the influence

[003-169]over the Indians, possessed by Sir William Johnson. This influence was

[003-170]exerted in favor of Great Britain.

[003-171]When the Indians heard of the uprising in Boston, and of the battle of

[003-172]Lexington, they were told, that these out-breaks were the acts of

[003-173]disobedient children, against the great king, who had been kind to them,

[003-174]as he had to the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]]. That their "*great father over the water*,"

[003-175]was rich in money and men; that the colonists were poor, and their numbers

[003-176]small, and that they could easily be brought into subjection.

[003-177]At a council of the Iroquois convened at [[oswego|Oswego]], by Sir John Johnson and

[003-178]other officers and friends of the crown, they were informed that the king

[003-179]desired them to assist him in subduing the rebels, who had taken up arms

[003-180]against him, and were about to rob him of a part of his great possessions.

[003-181]But the chiefs one by one assured the British agents that they had the

[003-182]year before, in a council with General Schuyler, pledged themselves to

[003-183]neutrality, and could not without violating their promise, take up the

[003-184]hatchet.

[003-185]But they were assured that the rebels justly merited all the punishment

[003-186]that white men and Indians could inflict;—that they would be richly

[003-187]rewarded for their services, and *that the king's rum was as plenty as

[003-188]the waters of [[lake-ontario|Lake Ontario]]*.

[003-189]This appeal to their appetites, already vitiated, together with the

[003-190]promise of large rewards, at length prevailed; and a treaty was concluded,

[003-191]in which the Indians pledged themselves to take up arms against the

[003-192]rebels, and continue in service during the war. They were then presented

[003-193]each with a suit of clothes, a brass kettle, a gun, a tomahawk, a scalping

[003-194]knife, a quantity of powder and lead, and a piece of gold. [Footnote: Life

[003-195]of [[mary-jemison|Mary Jemison]].]

[003-196]The Senecas were among those who consented to join the royal standard. Of

[003-197]this action Red Jacket did not approve. He declared plainly and

[003-198]unhesitatingly to those who had determined to engage in the war,—"*This

[003-199]quarrel does not belong to us,—and it is test for us to take no part in

[003-200]it; we need not waste our blood to have it settled. If they fight us, we

[003-201]will fight them, but if they let us alone, we had better keep still*."

[003-202][Footnote: Testimony given to the author by Wm. Jones, Seneca chief, and

[003-203]confirmed by Col. Wm. Jones, son of the Indian interpreter, who affirms

[003-204]that prominent Indian chiefs had declared in his hearing that these were

[003-205]the sentiments of Red Jacket at this time.]

[003-206]Red Jacket at this time was not far from twenty-six years old. His

[003-207]forensic abilities had not been called forth, and his influence weighed

[003-208]but little in comparison with that of older men. But it may be observed

[003-209]that his conduct ever after this, will be found consistent with the

[003-210]sentiments he entertained, and was free to express. Though young, his

[003-211]perceptions were keen, he had a deep and penetrating mind and saw at a

[003-212]glance that in this contest his people were doomed to suffer, to be ground

[003-213]between the upper and nether mill stone.

[003-214]When, in the summer of 1777, his people received an invitation to join the

[003-215]forces that were preparing to march under the command of Col. St. Leger

[003-216]upon [[fort-stanwix|Fort Stanwix]], being assured that they would not be required to endure

[003-217]the fatigues and dangers of the battle, but might "sit down quietly and

[003-218]smoke their pipes, and see the sport;" Red Jacket endeavored, but in vain,

[003-219]to prevent his people from going. He said to them, "*it's a cheat; the

[003-220]design is to deceive you, and if you go you will find that you have been

[003-221]deluded*."

[003-222]They threw back the taunt,—"*You are a coward, you have the mind of a

[003-223]woman, and are not fit to go to war*."

[003-224]Red Jacket though not at this time a chief, was a young man of

[003-225]acknowledged talent and influence, and having a right to express his

[003-226]opinion, did not hesitate to give it in favor of peace. His opinion was

[003-227]well known among his people. Little Beard has frequently been seen to bury

[003-228]his face in his blanket, and give vent to his tears, in view of the havoc

[003-229]made among the Senecas by the war, at the same time declaring,—"*Red

[003-230]Jacket was opposed to the war*, HE WAS ALWAYS IN FAVOR OF PEACE, *and how

[003-231]much better it had been, had we listened to his advice*." [Footnote:

[003-232]Conversation of the author with Col. Jones.]

[003-233][Illustration: BARRY ST. LEGER]

[003-234]Red Jacket's prediction was too nearly verified. The Senecas suffered most

[003-235]severely in that campaign. They fell under the command of Thay-en-dan-egea or Brant, who went with a company of Tories, led by Col. Butler, to

[003-236]intercept General Herkimer, who was reported as coming to the relief of

[003-237]the garrison. At a certain point on the way, where they expected the

[003-238]general would pass, they formed an ambuscade, and though they selected

[003-239]their ground with wisdom, and acquitted themselves with great bravery,

[003-240]they were unable to stand before the invincible courage of the heroes of

[003-241]Oriskany.

[003-242]The Senecas claim to have lost in that engagement thirty-three of their

[003-243]chiefs, and their feelings in view of it are said to have been sad in the

[003-244]extreme. [Footnote: "The mourning was excessive, and was expressed by the

[003-245]most doleful yells, shrieks and howlings and by inimitable

[003-246]gesticulations."—Mrs. Jemison's Narrative.]

[003-247]The charge of cowardice applied by the young warriors to Red Jacket, upon

[003-248]their first starting out on this campaign, was one frequently made during

[003-249]the war. His views were at different times expressed in opposition to it,

[003-250]and his arguments as often repelled by the young braves, who could not

[003-251]endure his invectives. The reply was easily made, and hence in more

[003-252]frequent demand, than if it had imposed a greater tax upon their

[003-253]intellects. The epithet has often been applied to him since, and though

[003-254]his tastes did not lead him to seek the fame of a warrior, still it is

[003-255]believed he was not so devoid of courage, as has sometimes been

[003-256]represented.

[003-257]His views of the war, were not those of a partisan, hence his conduct was

[003-258]often censured by those who had entered heartily into the contest.

[003-259]Brant has charged him with being the occasion of trouble to him, in his

[003-260]efforts to arrest the march of Sullivan, and his army, into the Indian

[003-261]country. Particularly at Newtown, where considerable preparations had been

[003-262]made for defense. Says Col. Stone,—"Sa-go-ye-wat-ha was then twenty-nine

[003-263]years old, and though it does not appear that he had yet been created a

[003-264]chief, he nevertheless seems to have been already a man of influence. He

[003-265]was in the practice of holding private consultations with the young

[003-266]warriors, and some of the younger and less resolute chiefs, for the

[003-267]purpose of fomenting discontents, and persuading them to sue for what

[003-268]Brant considered, ignominious terms of peace.

[003-269]"On one occasion as Brant has alleged, Red Jacket had so far succeeded in

[003-270]his treachery, as to induce some of the disaffected chiefs to send a

[003-271]runner into Sullivan's camp, to make known dissensions he himself had

[003-272]awakened, and invite a flag of truce, *with propositions of peace to the

[003-273]Indians*."

[003-274]Though charged with acting criminally, it is here expressly asserted,

[003-275]*that it was to obtain peace*. Peace he most earnestly desired for his

[003-276]people, who were doomed to be wasted in a contest not their own.

[003-277]Nor, in view of his feelings respecting the war, is it surprising he

[003-278]should have incurred the displeasure of [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]], while endeavoring to

[003-279]bring his countrymen to make a stand against a portion of the invading

[003-280]army, on the beach of Canandaigua lake, where was an Indian village of

[003-281]some size. Not finding in Red Jacket an ardor for the undertaking which

[003-282]corresponded in any degree with his own, he turned to the young wife of

[003-283]the orator and exclaimed,—"*Leave that man, he is a coward; your children

[003-284]will disgrace you, they will all be cowards*." [Footnote: Col. Wm. Jones.]

[003-285]The epithet thus applied occasioned uneasiness to none less than to the

[003-286]orator himself. Whenever he chose to notice it, he would make a good

[003-287]return for what he had received.—In a war of words, he was on his own

[003-288]chosen ground. He was a match for their greatest champion, and in crossfiring, it could easily be seen that his missiles were directed by one who

[003-289]was perfect master of the art. He could handle at will the most cutting

[003-290]sarcasm, and while maintaining a good natured, playful mood, deal his

[003-291]blows with such power and effect, as to make the victim of his irony

[003-292]resort to some other means of defense, than the tongue. It is said that

[003-293]frequently by his cool, good natured railery, he has caused the victim of

[003-294]his sport to turn upon and strike him. He would answer it by a hearty

[003-295]laugh, unless the blow was of such a nature as to demand of him a

[003-296]different reception. [Footnote: Wm. Jones, Seneca chief.] He seemed to be

[003-297]armed at every point, as with a coat of mail, against the arrows of his

[003-298]assailants. Their most powerful weapons would be turned aside by his

[003-299]presence of mind, and matchless skill, and leave him apparently unharmed.

[003-300]A circumstance illustrating this point, once occurred between him and

[003-301]Little Billy, a chief of some note among the Senecas, who was frequently

[003-302]in the orator's company. This chief, with Red Jacket and one or two

[003-303]others, were once passing from their settlement on Canandaigua lake, to

[003-304]the old Seneca Castle, near the foot of Seneca lake. On their way they

[003-305]encountered a large grizzly bear. Little Billy and the others in the

[003-306]company, were frightened and began to run. Red Jacket who was

[003-307]distinguished as a hunter, and an excellent marksman, drew up his rifle,

[003-308]and brought the monster to the ground.

[003-309]It so happened, on one occasion sometime afterward, that Little Billy was

[003-310]very pertinacious in calling Red Jacket a coward. The orator did not

[003-311]appear to notice him at first; but finding that he persisted in the

[003-312]charge, he turned to him and coolly and sarcastically said,—"*Well, if I

[003-313]am coward I never run unless it's for something bigger than a bear*."

[003-314][Footnote: Conversation with Seneca chief, Wm. Jones.]

[003-315]It is hardly necessary to add, that nothing more was heard from Little

[003-316]Billy concerning his cowardice on that day.

[003-317]This charge of cowardice was owing in a great degree to the orator's

[003-318]position. He was not on the popular side. The majority of his people were

[003-319]against him. Had he acted in accordance with their wishes, it is a

[003-320]question whether anything would ever have been said about his deficiency

[003-321]in courage. And this supposition is strengthened by the fact, that at a

[003-322]subsequent period in his history, a little display of courage, when acting

[003-323]in accordance with the wishes of his people, gained for him a marked

[003-324]degree of approbation, and gave rise to the affirmation, "*the stain fixed

[003-325]upon his character, was thus wiped away by his good conduct in the

[003-326]field*." [Footnote: McKenney's Indian Biography.]

[003-327]In opposing the wishes of his people, when bent on a war of which he did

[003-328]not approve, he gained the epithet of *coward*. With less intelligence,

[003-329]and less moral courage, he might have seconded the views of his nation,

[003-330]and been ranked a brave.

[003-331]Hence, though we do not claim for Red Jacket the possession of qualities,

[003-332]adapted to make him conspicuous as a military chieftain, we are disposed

[003-333]to attribute to him the higher courage of acting in accordance with his

[003-334]own convictions of propriety and duty. "He was born an orator, and while

[003-335]morally brave, lacked the stolid insensibility to suffering and slaughter,

[003-336]which characterized the war-captains of his nation." [Footnote: Bryant's

[003-337]address.]

[003-338]We readily concede that Red Jacket was fitted by nature to excel in

[003-339]councils of peace, rather than in enterprises of war; to gain victories in

[003-340]a conflict of mind with mind rather than in physical strife, on the field

[003-341]of battle.

[003-342]And it may be questioned whether the qualities adapted to the highest

[003-343]achievements of oratory, would be congenial to the rough encounters of

[003-344]war. Especially when the mind is already preoccupied with inward

[003-345]thirstings after the glory of the rostrum; it will not be apt to sigh for

[003-346]the camp, or the noise and tinsel of mere military fame.

[003-347]It is related of him that when a boy, he was present at a great council

[003-348]held on the Shenandoah. Many nations were there represented by their wise

[003-349]men and orators. The greatest among them was Logan, who had removed from

[003-350]the territory of his tribe to Shamokin. He was the son of Shikellemus, a

[003-351]celebrated chief of the [[cayuga-nation|Cayuga]] nation, who, before the Revolution was a

[003-352]warm friend of the whites.

[003-353]On the occasion referred to, Red Jacket was so charmed with Logan's style,

[003-354]and manner of delivery, that he resolved to attain if possible the same

[003-355]high standard of eloquence; though he almost despaired of equalling his

[003-356]distinguished model.

[003-357]On his return to Cunadesaga, near the Seneca lake, which was at that time

[003-358]his home, he sometimes incurred the displeasure and reproof of his mother,

[003-359]by long absence from her cabin, without any ostensible cause. When hard

[003-360]pressed for an answer, he informed his mother, that "*he had been playing

[003-361]Logan*."

[003-362]"Thus in his mighty soul the fire of a generous emulation had been

[003-363]kindled, not to go out until his oratorical fame threw a refulgent glory

[003-364]on the declining fortunes of the once formidable Iroquois. In the deep and
