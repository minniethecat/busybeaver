# Canandaigua

## Geography

Canandaigua is located at the northern end of Canandaigua Lake, one of the Finger Lakes in western New York. The [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] occupied a village at this site, strategically positioned near "the head of Canandaigua lake" (002-128) and along a major trail connecting the [[genesee-river|Genesee]] country with the Mohawk Valley to the east.

The lake itself, approximately 15 miles long, provided fishing grounds and served as a natural transportation corridor. In the late 18th century, the settlement lay on the frontier of white expansion. (010-008)

Hubbard notes that reaching Canandaigua required a journey "from Long Island Sound to Canandaigua, with the exception of one or two short portages, entirely by water" — underscoring both its remoteness and its accessibility via the region's network of lakes and rivers. (010-008)

## Seneca Origin Site

The area held deep spiritual significance for the Seneca long before the treaty era. A hill near the head of Canandaigua Lake, called Ge-nun-de-wa, is venerated as the birthplace of the Seneca nation:

> The Senecas, the fifth tribe of the Iroquois, were directed in their original location, to occupy a hill near the head of Canandaigua lake. This hill, called Ge-nun-de-wa, is venerated as the birth place of their nation. It was surrounded anciently by a rude fortification which formed their dwelling in time of peace, and served for a shelter from any sudden attack of a hostile tribe.
>
> (002-127)

Hubbard records a Seneca tradition about this spot: the inhabitants of Ge-nun-de-wa awoke one morning to find themselves encircled by an immense serpent whose body coiled completely around the fort. The serpent was eventually slain, and from this victory the Seneca drew an omen of their nation's endurance. (002-131)

## Historical Significance

Canandaigua rose to prominence as an early center of white settlement and Indian diplomacy. By the 1790s, Hubbard remarks, "Canandaigua at an early day was the objective point for all who were seeking what was called the Genesee country." (010-005) The village served simultaneously as an outpost of American settlement and a meeting ground for diplomatic engagements with the Iroquois.

The settlement witnessed one of the most remarkable transformations of the era. Hubbard relates the experience of a visitor who, on revisiting Canandaigua in 1789, observed "a great change" — from a frontier outpost to a bustling settlement. (010-017) For many years thereafter, Canandaigua remained "head-quarters for the surveyors and land agents" engaged in acquiring Indian lands. (010-027)

| post-1794 | Repeated councils | Seneca protested broken treaty promises at Canandaigua (018-151) |
| 1790s–1810s | Land speculation hub | "Head-quarters for the surveyors and land agents" (010-027) |

At the close of the 1794 treaty, the Seneca "widened our path" — a metaphor for establishing a clear diplomatic channel between the two nations. (018-124) However, the Seneca later protested: "At the treaty of Canandaigua, we were promised that different nations should not be suffered to take our lands." (018-151) This grievance would echo through decades of subsequent negotiations.

Throughout the early 19th century, Canandaigua continued to serve as a focal point for Seneca-white relations, hosting numerous councils and negotiations. The town was also the residence of several important figures in the narrative, including Oliver Parrish and John Greig. (010-314)
