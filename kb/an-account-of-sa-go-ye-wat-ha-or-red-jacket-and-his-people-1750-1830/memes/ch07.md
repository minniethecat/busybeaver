[007-001]Expedition under Gen. Harmar—Its failure—High hopes of the Indians—Col.

[007-002][[colonel-proctor|Proctor]]'s visit to the Indians at [[buffalo-creek|Buffalo Creek]]—[[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s speech—

[007-003]Indian deputation refused—Interference of the matrons—Council at Painted

[007-004]Post—Chiefs invited to [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]].

[007-005]The efforts of our government to secure peace with the Indians, were but

[007-006]partially successful. As our settlements extended westward in

[007-007]Pennsylvania, and along the Ohio and Kentucky borders, Indian hostilities

[007-008]and depredations continued to multiply. From the year 1783 when peace was

[007-009]concluded with Great Britain, until October, 1790, when the United States

[007-010]commenced offensive operations against them, the Indians killed, wounded

[007-011]and took prisoners on the Ohio and the frontiers, about fifteen hundred

[007-012]men, women and children; besides taking away two thousand horses, and a

[007-013]large amount of other property.

[007-014]The Shawanese, Miamis and Wabash Indians were chiefly concerned in these

[007-015]bloody transactions; and our government finding protection for her

[007-016]citizens could not be secured by pacific means, resolved to proceed with

[007-017]vigorous offensive measures.

[007-018]General Harmar, a veteran of the Revolution, with a force of fourteen

[007-019]hundred and fifty men, three hundred and twenty from the regular army, and

[007-020]the balance made up of recruits from Kentucky and Pennsylvania, advanced

[007-021]toward the Indian country.

[007-022]The expedition left Fort [[george-washington|Washington]], the present site of Cincinnati, on

[007-023]the 30th of September, 1790.

[007-024]The Indians, who kept watch of his movements, burned before his coming,

[007-025]their principal village and retired. Seizing a favorable opportunity, they

[007-026]fell suddenly upon a detachment of the main army commanded by Colonel

[007-027]Harding, consisting of two hundred and ten men, thirty of whom were

[007-028]regulars.

[007-029]At the first onset the militia, the main part of the force, fled. The

[007-030]regulars stood their ground bravely for a time, but at a fearful odds;

[007-031]seven only escaped.

[007-032]Colonel Harding, desirous of retrieving the disgrace, the next day with

[007-033]three hundred militia and sixty regulars, gave battle to the Indians. They

[007-034]fought near the junction of St. Joseph and St. Mary rivers, and the

[007-035]struggle, though severe and bloody, ended with the defeat of the

[007-036]Americans.

[007-037]Success elated and emboldened the Indians, and rendered hopeless the

[007-038]negotiations for peace. Nevertheless the mission of Colonel Proctor, with

[007-039]a deputation from the Iroquois was not given up, and when spring opened he

[007-040]repaired to their country, to be joined by [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]], Red Jacket and

[007-041]others, and proceed on his visit to the hostile tribes of the West.

[007-042]Anticipating his arrival, a council of the Iroquois had been called at

[007-043]Buffalo Creek. Already messengers had been sent, earnestly soliciting them

[007-044]to join the warriors that were rising everywhere, from toward the setting

[007-045]sun. They had defeated the Americans, and nothing was wanting but the

[007-046]united action of all the Indian tribes, to secure the broad lands of the

[007-047]North-west, where they could spread their blankets in peace, and dwell

[007-048]securely forever. The [[seneca-nation|Senecas]], particularly, were urged to join in a war,

[007-049]that opened so many hopeful and glorious anticipations. The distinguished

[007-050]warrior [[joseph-brant|Brant]] was very solicitous on this point, and being encouraged by

[007-051]those at the head of British affairs in [[canada|Canada]], was sanguine of ultimate

[007-052]success.

[007-053]Colonel Proctor, accompanied by Cornplanter, arrived at the [[council-fire|council fire]]

[007-054]kindled at Buffalo Creek, on the 27th of April, 1791.

[007-055]Among the Indian chiefs present were [[young-king|Young King]], [[farmer-s-brother|Farmer's Brother]] and Red

[007-056]Jacket. The latter had now an acknowledged pre-eminence among his people,

[007-057]and took a leading part in the deliberations of this council. It was

[007-058]opened by a speech from Red Jacket, as follows:

[007-059]"Brother: Listen. As is our [[iroquois-tradition|custom]] we now address you, and we speak to you

[007-060]as to a brother that has been long absent. We all address you, and our

[007-061]chief warrior, Cornplanter; and we thank the [[great-spirit|Great Spirit]] for his and your

[007-062]safe arrival, coming as you do hand in hand from Honandaganius [Footnote:

[007-063]Name given to General Washington.] on important business.

[007-064]"You have traveled long with tearful eyes, from the roughness of the way,

[007-065]and the inclemency of the season. Besides the difficulties between the bad

[007-066]Indians and our brothers the white people, everything has been conspiring

[007-067]to prevent your coming, thwart your business, and cause you to lose your

[007-068]way. The great waters might have prevented your coming; the wars might

[007-069]have stopped you; sickness and death might have overtaken you, for we know

[007-070]not what is to happen till it comes upon us. Therefore we thank the Great

[007-071]Spirit, who has preserved you from dangers, that would have prevented our

[007-072]hearing the good news you have come to bring us. And when filled with good

[007-073]news, how is it possible that disasters should befall you on the way?

[007-074]"Wipe therefore from your eyes, the tears that have been occasioned by the

[007-075]dangers through which you have come. We now place you upon a seat where

[007-076]you can sit erect, a seat where you will be secure from the fear of your

[007-077]enemies, where you can look around upon all as your friends and brothers

[007-078]in peace.

[007-079]"You have come with your heart and lips firmly closed, lest you should

[007-080]lose anything you had to say. With a brotherly hand we now open your

[007-081]hearts, and we remove the seal from your lips, that you may open them and

[007-082]speak freely without obstruction. Your ears too have been closed, that

[007-083]they might hear nothing until saluted by our voice. Open your ears to hear

[007-084]our counsels when we shall have had messages from you.

[007-085]"We present therefore the compliments of the chiefs and head men of

[007-086]Buffalo Creek, to you and to our great warrior, the Cornplanter, hoping

[007-087]that you may each proceed safely with your business."

[007-088]To this Cornplanter replied briefly, in behalf of himself and Col.

[007-089]Procter, reciprocating the kindness manifested, in the welcome that had

[007-090]been given them.

[007-091]After which Col. Proctor explained fully the object of their coming, which

[007-092]was to obtain from them a deputation of peace, to visit with him the

[007-093]hostile Indian tribes at the West; and assured them of the liberal views,

[007-094]and friendly feelings of the chief of the thirteen fires toward them.

[007-095]Several days were thereupon consumed in devising expedients and raising

[007-096]objections, which terminated finally in the declaration that nothing could

[007-097]be done without consulting their *British friends at [[fort-niagara|Fort Niagara]]*.

[007-098]They desired the colonel to go with them there. His business not being

[007-099]with the British, but with them, he declined going. They then insisted

[007-100]upon having one of the officers of the fort to sit with them in council.

[007-101]This being allowed, Col. Butler afterward appeared among them, and after a

[007-102]little private consultation with him, they seemed to be utterly averse to

[007-103]sending the proposed delegation.

[007-104]Captain Brant, just before starting on a visit to western tribes, had been

[007-105]holding a consultation with these chiefs, and had no doubt been

[007-106]influential in causing them to be averse to joining this embassy.

[007-107]Col. Proctor, finding further negotiation hopeless, declared his purpose

[007-108]to return, and expressed his regret in having to carry back an unfavorable

[007-109]report to the government, on whose kind and pacific errand he had been

[007-110]sent forth.

[007-111]This announcement made a deep impression on their minds, and immediately a

[007-112]change took place in their proceedings, which revealed a peculiar feature

[007-113]of Indian diplomacy.

[007-114]The women, who had been carefully watching the proceedings of this

[007-115]council, began to express their unwillingness to send to General

[007-116]Washington an unfavorable reply. To them was conceded the right, in things

[007-117]pertaining to the safety of their homes, of reversing, if they thought

[007-118]proper, the decision of the men. They did so on this occasion, and

[007-119]employed Red Jacket to present their views on the following day.

[007-120]It was decided by them, in view of the threatening aspect of affairs, that

[007-121]Cornplanter, their most experienced warrior, should not leave them; but

[007-122]that a sufficient deputation, for which they had obtained volunteers,

[007-123]should accompany Col. Proctor, at the same time advising him of the

[007-124]danger, admonishing him to proceed with caution; "*to reach his neck over

[007-125]the land, and take in all the light he could, that would show him his

[007-126]danger*."

[007-127]The journey being regarded as too hazardous by land, and the Indians

[007-128]unwilling to perform it with their canoes, the case was decided by the

[007-129]British officers, who refused them a vessel for the undertaking.

[007-130]So great was the excitement among the Indians at this time, that before

[007-131]the result of Col. Proctor's mission was generally known, another council

[007-132]of the Iroquois was invited to meet at Painted Post, and was held during

[007-133]the month of June following. The British officers at Niagara, and runners

[007-134]from the western tribes, exerted their influence to prevent the Iroquois

[007-135]from coming into alliances of peace with the United States. But through

[007-136]the exertions of Col. Proctor, assisted by Cornplanter and the elderly

[007-137]matrons, the minds of the leading chiefs were turned from the proposed

[007-138]western alliance to [[timothy-pickering|Colonel Pickering]] and the treaty ground at Painted

[007-139]Post.

[007-140]Red Jacket, together with other leading chiefs was present, and took an

[007-141]active part in the deliberations of this council. It was well attended by

[007-142]the Indians, as also by several American gentlemen, and a number of

[007-143]speeches were interchanged, whose general drift was in the direction of

[007-144]peace.

[007-145]The result of this gathering was satisfactory to all parties. It served to

[007-146]bind more closely the friendship of the leading chiefs to the United

[007-147]States, and it served also to interest the minds of the young warriors,

[007-148]who had else from a love of adventure followed the war path, with the

[007-149]tribes at the West.

[007-150]At the close of this council, a large entertainment was prepared purely

[007-151]after the civilized style, and when it was about concluded, Colonel

[007-152]Pickering took occasion to place before them the blessings and advantages

[007-153]of a cultivated state of society; and the happy influences that would

[007-154]arise from the introduction among them of the arts of civilized life. He

[007-155]assured them of the kind interest felt by General Washington and others in

[007-156]their welfare, and promised to aid them in any efforts they were disposed

[007-157]to make, for the advancement of their people. Presents were then liberally

[007-158]distributed among them, and they were invited at a convenient time to

[007-159]visit General Washington, and confer with him more fully on the subject.

[007-160]The Indians were pleased with these suggestions, and promised to accept of

[007-161]the proffered invitation. Thus happily closed this council, gathered amid

[007-162]distracting influences, the Indians returning home better satisfied with

[007-163]their friendly attitude toward the government, and their feelings in

[007-164]striking contrast with those of their brethren at the West.
