# Albany

## Geography

Albany lies on the west bank of the Hudson River in eastern New York, approximately 150 miles north of New York City. The city occupies a strategic position near the confluence of the Mohawk and Hudson Rivers — the historic east-west corridor through Iroquois territory. In the late 18th century, Albany was the gateway between the settled Hudson Valley and the Iroquois country to the west.

The city's location on the Hudson made it accessible by water from New York City while placing it within close reach of the Mohawk Valley — the traditional territory of the Mohawk nation, the easternmost of the Six Nations.

## Historical Significance

Albany was the seat of New York State government throughout the period covered by Hubbard's narrative. As the state capital, it was the locus of legislative and executive decisions that directly affected Seneca lands and sovereignty. The state legislature in Albany authorized land grants, chartered the land companies that acquired Iroquois territory, and ratified the treaties that progressively diminished the Seneca estate.

A "council was appointed to be held at Albany" in December 1789 (005-104) — one of several state-level diplomatic gatherings where New York officials negotiated directly with Iroquois representatives. These Albany councils operated alongside the federal treaty system, creating a layered and often confusing jurisdictional landscape for the Six Nations.

## Key Events at This Location

The Albany council of 1789 was a significant state-level negotiation. While Hubbard provides only brief details, the council addressed land transactions and the terms under which the Seneca would cede territory to New York. The payment made at Albany "was for horses paid to the Indians" (005-132), suggesting that the council involved not only land but also the logistics of moving Native populations away from ceded territory.

Albany also appears in the narrative as a cultural reference point. Hubbard records that Red Jacket's father "was a resident in Albany" (014-111) — a biographical detail that connects the Seneca orator to the colonial world of the Hudson Valley before his birth in Seneca country. The Albany connection underscores the mobility and interconnectedness of the 18th-century frontier.
