# Big Tree

## Geography

Big Tree was located in the heart of the Genesee Valley, at the present site of Geneseo, New York. Hubbard describes it as a region "which at that time was the great center of the Seneca population." (011-213) The location lay on the west side of the Genesee River, near the great flats that the Seneca had cultivated for generations. The name derives from a large tree that stood at the site — likely a natural landmark and gathering place that had long served as a council ground for the Seneca.

The area was characterized by fertile bottomlands along the Genesee River, with hills rising on either side. It lay near the Canaseraga Creek confluence and was easily accessible by the Indian trails that crisscrossed the valley. (011-127)

## Historical Significance

The council at Big Tree in August 1797 represented the largest land transaction between the Seneca and American negotiators. Thomas Morris, son of the financier Robert Morris, arrived as the principal agent for the land companies that sought to acquire the Seneca's remaining Genesee lands. The stakes were enormous: the entire Seneca territory west of the Genesee River was on the table.

Red Jacket led the Seneca opposition to the sale. Hubbard records that "the Indians were mostly in the vicinity of Big Tree" at the time of the negotiations, and that "Red Jacket, or Big Tree, as principal chief was at the head of the opposition." (011-197) The treaty tested Red Jacket's diplomatic skills to their utmost — facing not only Morris's persistence but also divisions within the Seneca leadership.

After the council's conclusion, Hubbard notes, the Seneca "began to realize the magnitude of the transaction" and the full consequence of what they had surrendered. (014-008) The breaking up of the council at Big Tree marked the end of an era for the Seneca in the Genesee Valley.

## Key Events at This Location

The **Big Tree Treaty** (September 15, 1797) was the culminating event. Thomas Morris, through a combination of persuasion, pressure, and strategic manipulation, secured the cession of approximately 3.5 million acres of Seneca land. In exchange, the Seneca retained a handful of reservations, including Buffalo Creek.

The negotiation was contentious. Red Jacket "was opposed step by step" to Morris's designs, but the combined weight of American pressure, internal Seneca divisions, and what Hubbard depicts as the "unwearied and earnest" efforts of the land companies ultimately prevailed. (012-006) The treaty established a pattern of land transactions that would be replicated across Iroquois territory in the coming decades.

Earlier, in the years leading up to the council, representatives from Big Tree had visited Philadelphia, gaining firsthand exposure to American power and the futility of resisting the tide of settlement. (006-229) These visits may have softened Seneca resistance to the eventual cession.
