# Iroquois Delegation to Washington (1792)

## Background

In the spring of 1792, a delegation of Iroquois chiefs traveled to Philadelphia — then the temporary capital of the United States — to meet with President Washington and members of Congress. The journey was itself an education: traveling hundreds of miles through settled country, the Seneca leaders saw with their own eyes the scale and energy of the American republic. (008-126)

## Proceedings

The delegation was received with the formal diplomatic courtesies due to representatives of sovereign nations. Washington met with the chiefs, listened to their concerns, and responded with his characteristic gravity. The chiefs also met with members of Congress and with the officials of the War Department who managed Indian affairs.

While the chiefs were in Philadelphia, "a bill was passed by Congress" (009-008) relating to Indian affairs — giving them a direct view of how American legislative power operated. Red Jacket, reflecting on the experience later in life, remarked that his "only regret that when I was in Philadelphia I did not understand it" (020-292) — an acknowledgment of the cultural and linguistic barriers that complicated even the most sincere diplomatic encounters.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The delegation established personal relationships between Iroquois leaders and American officials that would prove crucial in subsequent negotiations. Timothy Pickering, who would negotiate the Treaty of Canandaigua two years later, interacted with the chiefs during this visit. The delegation also exposed Iroquois leaders to the sheer scale of American power — an experience that informed their strategic calculations.

Cornplanter, in particular, emerged from the Philadelphia visit as a more committed advocate of accommodation with the United States. Red Jacket drew different conclusions: the visit reinforced his determination to defend what remained of Seneca sovereignty through diplomatic skill rather than military resistance.

## Key Participants

- **Red Jacket** — Seneca sachem, delegation co-leader
- **Cornplanter** — Seneca war chief, delegation co-leader
- **George Washington** — President of the United States
- **Timothy Pickering** — Indian affairs official
