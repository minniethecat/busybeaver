# New York Missionary Society

## Overview

The New York Missionary Society was one of several Protestant missionary organizations active among the Iroquois in the early republic period. Along with the Boston Missionary Society (the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions), the New York society sent missionaries to the Seneca Nation in western New York. These missionary efforts were the direct catalyst for the religious controversies that divided the Seneca into pagan and Christian factions — a division with profound political and social consequences. (015-131)

The missionary enterprise among the Seneca was part of a broader movement of evangelical outreach to Native American nations in the years following the Revolution. Hubbard chronicles this movement, describing how the arrival of missionaries fundamentally altered the political landscape of the Buffalo Creek Reservation. (019-229)

## Structure and Governance

The Society was based in New York City and supported by Protestant congregations in the region. Like the American Board, it trained missionaries, funded their work, and directed their assignments to Native American communities. The missionaries were expected to learn native languages, establish schools, translate religious texts, and convert Native peoples to Christianity. (015-145)

## Historical Role

Missionary work among the Seneca was conducted by multiple organizations operating in western New York. Hubbard describes how the religious controversies at Buffalo Creek were directly tied to the missionary presence. (010-001) The missionaries' approach — emphasizing the superiority of Christian religion and the alleged falsehood of traditional Seneca beliefs — provoked a strong response from traditional leaders like Red Jacket, who saw the missionaries as agents of cultural destruction.

The arrival of "a missionary from Massachusetts" was the occasion that called forth one of the most effective efforts of Red Jacket's life — his famous speech rejecting the missionary's message. (015-131) The missionary was invited to address the council, and Red Jacket prepared a detailed reply that defended the traditional Seneca religion. (015-138)

The missionary enterprise persisted despite opposition. Hubbard notes that after various discouragements and reverses, "the missionary was at length" able to gain converts among the Seneca. (021-069) The true effect of the missionary effort became manifest over time, as the growing Christian faction among the Seneca fundamentally changed Seneca political dynamics. (019-248)
