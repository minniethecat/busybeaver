# US Office of Indian Affairs

## Overview

In the period covered by Hubbard's account, the federal government's relations with Native American nations were managed through the Office of Indian Affairs, a division of the War Department. The Secretary of War was the cabinet official responsible for Indian diplomacy, and under him served commissioners, agents, and superintendents who conducted treaty negotiations, distributed annuities, and managed the day-to-day administration of federal Indian policy. (005-079)

For the Seneca and other Iroquois nations, the Office of Indian Affairs was their primary counterpart in the American government. The commissioners who negotiated the major post-Revolutionary treaties — at Fort Stanwix, Canandaigua, and Big Tree — were appointed by and reported to the War Department. The promises of protection, annuity payments, and recognition of reservation boundaries that the Seneca received in these treaties were embodied in commitments from this office. (009-004)

## Structure and Governance

The Office of Indian Affairs operated under the authority of the Secretary of War from 1789 until 1849, when the Department of the Interior was created and Indian affairs were transferred to it. The Secretary of War appointed treaty commissioners on an ad hoc basis — individuals like Timothy Pickering, who served as commissioner for the Treaty of Canandaigua — to conduct specific negotiations. These commissioners reported back to the War Department on the terms obtained and the state of Indian relations. (009-018)

In addition to treaty commissioners, the office employed Indian agents stationed at frontier posts and within tribal territories. These agents served as the day-to-day representatives of the federal government, distributing annuity goods, mediating disputes, and reporting on conditions among the tribes. The office also maintained a system of licensed traders designed to regulate commerce with Indian nations. (005-079)

## Historical Role

The Office of Indian Affairs was central to the treaty-making process that progressively reduced Seneca landholdings. Hubbard describes how the federal government, through commissioners appointed by the President and accountable to the War Department, conducted a series of councils with the Seneca that resulted in major land cessions. The office's approach was shaped by the broader federal policy of acquiring Indian lands through negotiation rather than outright conquest — a policy that the Washington administration articulated and that persisted through the early republic. (009-016)

The Secretary of War occasionally intervened directly in Iroquois affairs. Hubbard recounts an incident in which a Seneca delegation visited the Secretary of War in Washington, seeking redress for grievances. The Secretary was reportedly "ashamed of the conduct of his subordinates" toward the Seneca, indicating both the office's acknowledged responsibilities and its frequent failures in execution. (019-406)

The office's role in Seneca history is particularly significant in relation to the Treaty of Canandaigua in 1794. Timothy Pickering, acting as commissioner on behalf of the United States, negotiated this landmark treaty that established a lasting peace between the Seneca and the United States and defined the boundaries of Seneca reservations. The treaty was ratified by the Senate and administered through the War Department's Indian affairs apparatus. (009-123)
