# Farmer's Brother

## Biography

Farmer's Brother was a Seneca chief active in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. His precise birth and death dates are uncertain — Hubbard places his active career in the decades surrounding the turn of the nineteenth century. He was a contemporary of Red Jacket and Cornplanter, and like them, his influence was exercised primarily at the council fires where the fate of Seneca lands and sovereignty was decided. (011-309)

His English name, Farmer's Brother, reflects the early American period's practice of assigning English names to Native leaders — a custom that both facilitated and obscured communication across cultures. Among his own people, he was known by his Seneca name.

## Role and Influence

Farmer's Brother appears most prominently in Hubbard's account of the 1797 Treaty of Big Tree. When Red Jacket dramatically declared "I now cover up the council fire" — a procedural move intended to derail Thomas Morris's land purchase negotiations — Farmer's Brother intervened with a crucial objection. He acknowledged that all Red Jacket had said was true, and he "regretted that the council fire had been extinguished so hastily." (012-028) But he also challenged Red Jacket's authority to perform the act: Red Jacket "assumed the right of covering up the fire. This did not belong to him. For according to your custom, he only who kindles the council fire, has a right to cover it up." (012-028)

This objection was diplomatically brilliant. By appealing to Iroquois protocol rather than to American interests, Farmer's Brother preserved his standing with both sides: he showed the Americans that the Seneca had internal checks on arbitrary action, and he showed his fellow Seneca that Red Jacket had violated the very traditions he claimed to defend. His intervention allowed the negotiations to resume, and Mr. Morris was informed "that their council fire was still burning, and that their business might proceed." (012-028)

The episode reveals Farmer's Brother as a leader who commanded sufficient respect to contradict the most famous orator of his nation — and to do so in a way that strengthened, rather than weakened, the Seneca negotiating position.
