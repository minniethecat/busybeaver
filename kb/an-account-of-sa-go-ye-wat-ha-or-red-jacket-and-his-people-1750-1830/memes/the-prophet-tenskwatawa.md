# The Prophet (Tenskwatawa)

## Biography

The Prophet was a Shawnee religious leader who gathered around him adherents from various tribes, encouraged pilgrimages to his camp, became conspicuous in all their general councils, and extended his influence to the various Indian towns in the vicinity of the northern lakes and on the plains watered by the Mississippi and its tributaries. (016-048) (016-049) (016-050) (016-051) Through this standing he could forward very effectively the ambitious views of his brother [[tecumseh|Tecumseh]]. (016-052) (016-053)

## Role and Influence

From the Prophet's town, established on the banks of the Wabash near the mouth of its tributary the Tippecanoe as early as 1808, a correspondence was kept up with the numerous tribes at the North and West. (016-054) (016-055) (016-056) Means were taken to extend the combination they were forming to the Cherokees and other nations of the South, and runners were sent as far even as the country of the [[seneca-nation|Senecas]], the Iroquois in New York and Canada being solicited to join the Great Western Confederacy. (016-057) (016-058) (016-059) (016-060)

The Prophet's retreat on the banks of the Wabash became not less noted for warlike exercises than for its religious harangues, and in time the report came that a thousand warriors awaited command in and about the Prophet's town. (016-112) (016-113) (016-119) (016-120) [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], though solicited, kept faith with the United States and warned the government of the warlike league that was forming. (016-100) (016-102) (016-103)

## Key Events

- **The battle of Tippecanoe**: When these threatening indications came to the knowledge of the government, General [[william-henry-harrison|Harrison]] was directed to go with an armed force to the Prophet's town, and his visit resulted in the battle of Tippecanoe, fought on the seventh of November, 1811. (016-137) (016-138) (016-139) (016-140)

- **The Prophet during the battle**: He was securely stationed on an adjoining eminence during the engagement; when a runner brought word that the American bullets were having a more powerful effect than had been anticipated, he was found singing piously and answered, "Go,—fight on: it will soon be as I have said," then resumed his singing more loudly. (016-150) (016-152) (016-153) (016-154) (016-155)

- **A broken assurance**: He had assured his followers that the Americans' bullets would not hurt the Indians, who would have light while their enemies would be in darkness. (016-156) (016-157) (016-158) Tecumseh himself was absent when the battle was fought. (016-160)
