# Buffalo Creek Council (1791)

## Background

Following the Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784), pressure on the Seneca to cede additional lands intensified. Colonel Thomas Proctor was dispatched by the federal government to negotiate with the Seneca at Buffalo Creek, their principal settlement. The Seneca had reason to be wary: the 1784 treaty had been a bitter experience, and further demands for land were anticipated.

## Proceedings

Colonel Proctor's visit "to the Indians at Buffalo Creek" (007-002) set the stage for a formal council. "Already messengers had been sent, earnestly soliciting them to refrain from making any further sales of their land." (007-043) The Seneca gathered from surrounding villages, and a "council fire was kindled at Buffalo Creek, on the 27th of April, 1791." (007-054)

Red Jacket delivered his response to Proctor — a speech that ranks among his most significant. Addressing "the great council fire of the thirteen United States" (008-186) through Proctor, Red Jacket combined diplomatic courtesy with an unwavering refusal. The speech was a masterpiece of Iroquois oratory: respectful in form, implacable in substance.

The council's proceedings demonstrated the fully developed Iroquois diplomatic protocol: formal addresses, the smoking of the calumet, the exchange of wampum belts, and carefully calibrated responses.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The council concluded without additional land cessions — a significant victory for Red Jacket and the Seneca traditionalists. "Red Jacket's speech at Buffalo Creek" became a touchstone in subsequent negotiations, cited by both Seneca and American officials as the definitive statement of the Seneca position.

The council also reinforced the alliance between Red Jacket and Cornplanter, who together represented the Seneca at Buffalo Creek. Their partnership — Red Jacket the orator, Cornplanter the war chief — defined Seneca diplomacy throughout the 1790s.

## Key Participants

- **Colonel Thomas Proctor** — US Commissioner sent to negotiate
- **Red Jacket** — Delivered the council's defining speech
- **Cornplanter** — Seneca war chief, co-leader of the delegation
