## Biography

Captain Horatio Jones was a native of Chester County, Pennsylvania. At the age of sixteen he enlisted as a volunteer in a company commanded by Captain John Boyd, during the period when Indians led by Butler, Brant, and Nellis were conducting raids against frontier settlements, "sparing neither age nor sex, from the tomahawk and scalping knife." (013-180)

Jones was captured by the Seneca and adopted into their families. As Red Jacket later recounted at a council: "We adopted them into our families, and made them our children. We loved them and nourished them. They lived with us many years." (013-092) This period of captivity gave Jones an intimate knowledge of the Seneca language and customs that would later define his career.

After hostilities ended and peace was restored, Jones — like other adopted captives — was given the opportunity to return to white society. He chose instead to maintain his connection with the Seneca people, settling in the Genesee region and raising a family. His son, William Jones, resided at Geneseo, Livingston County, New York. (001-062)

## Role and Influence

Horatio Jones became one of the most trusted interpreters between the Seneca Nation and the United States government. He was, along with [[jasper-parrish]], a "favorite interpreter of Red Jacket," and both men "passed no inconsiderable part of their lives among the Indians." (013-128)

Jones served as interpreter at critical treaty negotiations, including the [[canandaigua|Treaty of Canandaigua]] in 1794, where Red Jacket delivered some of his most memorable speeches. He also interpreted at councils concerning the [[ogden-land-company]]'s efforts to purchase Seneca reservations.

His deep familiarity with Red Jacket's oratory gave him a unique appreciation of the chief's genius. Hubbard records that Jones was a "warm admirer of the orator's genius" whose admiration "sprang from an intimate knowledge of him, seen under circumstances, that afforded the best opportunity of forming a just estimate of his powers." (001-054)

Jones was present alongside [[jasper-parrish]] at the council held at [[canandaigua]] where the [[ogden-land-company]] pressed for land cessions. (018-022)

## Key Events

- **Capture and Adoption** (circa 1779): Enlisted at sixteen and was captured during frontier warfare; adopted into a Seneca family and lived among them for many years. (013-180) (013-092)

- **Treaty of Canandaigua** (1794): Served as interpreter at this pivotal treaty where Red Jacket's oratory made a profound impression. His famous assessment of Red Jacket — "the greatest man that ever lived" — was based on observations made at such councils. (001-054)

- **Ogden Company Councils** (circa 1820s): Interpreted at councils where the Ogden Land Company sought to purchase remaining Seneca reservations, with Red Jacket leading the opposition. (018-022)
