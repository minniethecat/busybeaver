# Mary Jemison (Deh-he-wa-mis)

## Biography

Mary Jemison was born in 1743 to Scotch-Irish parents aboard a ship bound for America. Her family settled on the Pennsylvania frontier, and in 1758, during the French and Indian War, a Shawnee raiding party captured the fifteen-year-old Mary along with her family. Her parents and siblings were killed; Mary was taken to the Seneca and adopted into the tribe — a common Iroquois practice for replacing family members lost to war. (003-195)

Unlike many captives who sought to return to white society, Mary Jemison chose to remain with the Seneca. She married a Delaware man, then later a Seneca chief named Hiokatoo, and raised a family among her adopted people. Her knowledge of both white and Seneca ways made her a valuable intermediary, and she became a respected figure in the Genesee Valley — known to settlers as "the White Woman of the Genesee." She made her home at Gardeau, on the Genesee River, a location known throughout the region as "the residence of the 'White Woman.'" (011-204) (012-144)

She lived to the age of 90, dying in 1833 on the Buffalo Creek Reservation. Her narrative, recorded late in her life and published as *A Narrative of the Life of Mrs. Mary Jemison*, became one of the most widely read captivity narratives in American literature. (003-246)

## Role and Influence

Jemison's role in Hubbard's narrative is primarily as a witness and a symbol — she embodied the complex reality of cultural crossing on the Iroquois frontier. Her choice to remain Seneca was a tacit repudiation of the assumption, common among white observers, that Native life was inherently inferior and that any white person exposed to it would naturally wish to escape. She chose differently.

Her presence in the Genesee Valley also made her a figure of interest in the land negotiations of the 1790s. She received a tract of land known as the Gardeau Reservation, and her knowledge of both Seneca and American land practices gave her a unique perspective on the transactions — often fraudulent — that accompanied the transfer of Seneca territory.

## Key Events

- **Capture and Adoption (1758)**: Taken by a Shawnee raiding party at age 15, adopted into the Seneca, and chose to remain with her adopted people for the rest of her life.
- **Life in the Genesee Valley (1760s–1830s)**: Raised a family, managed lands, and served as an intermediary between Seneca and white communities in western New York.
