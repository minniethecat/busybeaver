# War of 1812 on the Niagara Frontier

## Background

When war broke out between the United States and Great Britain in June 1812, the Seneca found themselves in an impossible position. Their territory straddled the likely theater of conflict — the Niagara River, which formed the border between New York and Upper Canada. British agents from Canada, who had maintained ties with the Iroquois since the Revolution, expected Seneca support. The United States demanded Seneca loyalty. (009-073)

Red Jacket, consistent with his post-Revolutionary policy, counseled neutrality. But younger Seneca warriors, remembering old alliances and drawn by the prospect of military honors, were harder to restrain.

## Proceedings

The Niagara frontier saw intense military activity throughout the war. Hubbard details the campaign in vivid terms. British forces crossed the river and burned "the villages of Black Rock and Buffalo" (017-021), destroying American stores collected at both locations. (016-248) The "gallant defense made at Black Rock" (016-245) became a celebrated episode in the local memory of the war.

American forces retaliated with their own cross-river operations. General Peter B. Porter of Black Rock led troops across the Niagara, navigating the "difficult pass in the Black Rock Rapids" (017-035). In December 1813, American forces crossed "the Niagara at Black Rock during the night of the fourteenth" (017-070), establishing positions on the Canadian shore.

The campaign involved significant troop movements along the Niagara River, with General Jacob Brown's brigade "crossing the Niagara river" (017-034) in a coordinated assault. American forces "continued its march down the Niagara river, destroying some of the enemy's stores." (017-292)

## Outcome and Aftermath

The war ended with the Treaty of Ghent (1814), which restored the pre-war border. For the Seneca, the war's outcome was profoundly significant: the British-Iroquois military alliance that had shaped frontier politics for half a century was permanently ended. The international boundary between the United States and British Canada now definitively divided Iroquois territory — the Mohawk community at Grand River (Canada) and the Seneca reservations in New York were now subjects of different empires.

The war also solidified Red Jacket's relationship with the United States. Despite his earlier opposition to American expansion, his support for the American side during the war enhanced his standing with federal officials. This political capital would prove crucial less than a decade later, when he faced deposition by Christian Seneca at the Trial of Red Jacket and turned to Washington for support.
