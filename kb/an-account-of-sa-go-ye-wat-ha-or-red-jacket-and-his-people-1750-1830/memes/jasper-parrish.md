## Biography

The early life of Captain Jasper Parrish was marked by "scenes alike trying and eventful." (013-131) He was a native of Connecticut, from which state his family removed to the waters of the Delaware, in Pennsylvania.

In 1778, when Parrish was but eleven years old, he accompanied his father on an expedition to move a family of backwoodsmen to a less exposed part of the frontier settlement. On their way, they were attacked by a small party of Indians and made captives. (013-131) His father was taken to [[fort-niagara|Niagara]], and after a captivity of two years, was exchanged and enabled to return to his family.

The young Parrish was claimed by a war-chief, who treated him kindly, and after a time took him to the waters of the Chemung. On entering an Indian village, the war-party sounded the war-whoop, and it was answered by the inhabitants who came out to meet them. They pulled the young prisoner from the horse he was riding, scourged him with whips, and beat him with the handles of their tomahawks — one of the forms of their gauntlet — until his master humanely rescued him. (013-131)

He was afterwards sold to a family of Delawares and taken to reside with them on the Delaware River, where he "suffered much from want of proper clothing, and from scanty fare." To inure him to cold, the Indians compelled him almost daily "to strip and plunge into the icy waters of the river." (013-131)

Red Jacket later recalled the adoption of Parrish and Horatio Jones with affection: "We adopted them into our families, and made them our children. We loved them and nourished them. They lived with us many years." (013-091)

## Role and Influence

Jasper Parrish became, alongside [[horatio-jones]], one of the "favorite interpreters of Red Jacket," and both men "passed no inconsiderable part of their lives among the Indians." (013-128) Parrish settled at [[canandaigua]], where he served as a key intermediary between the Seneca Nation and the United States government.

Parrish interpreted at councils where Red Jacket's oratory was central. Hubbard notes that Parrish "has frequently been heard to speak" of Red Jacket's forensic efforts, echoing Jones's observation that translations could never fully capture the power of the chief's words. (004-271)

He was present alongside [[horatio-jones]] at the council held at [[canandaigua]] regarding the [[ogden-land-company]]'s land purchase efforts. (018-022)

## Key Events

- **Capture and Adoption** (1778): Captured at age eleven while accompanying his father on an expedition in Pennsylvania; suffered the gauntlet before being rescued by his master and sold to a Delaware family. (013-131)

- **Life Among the Delawares**: Endured harsh conditions on the Delaware River, including daily ice-water plunges to inure him to cold. (013-131)

- **Service as Interpreter**: Became one of Red Jacket's most trusted interpreters, based at Canandaigua, and served at treaty councils into the 1820s. (013-128) (018-022)
