# John Butler

## Biography

John Butler was a Loyalist colonel who, during the Revolutionary War, led Tory forces on the frontier in company with the Mohawk war leader [[joseph-brant|Brant]]. When the Senecas fell under Brant's command, they went with a company of Tories, led by Col. Butler, to intercept General Herkimer, who was reported as coming to the relief of the garrison. (003-235)

He is to be distinguished from General Richard Butler, an American officer of the same surname who fell at St. Clair's defeat; the account's "Col. Butler" of the Niagara frontier is the Loyalist. (008-068)

## Role and Influence

Butler's force joined the ambuscade laid for [[joseph-brant|Brant's]] warriors on the road by which General Herkimer was expected to pass; though they selected their ground with wisdom and fought with great bravery, they were unable to stand before the courage of the men at Oriskany. (003-235)

After the war, Butler's continuing interest in Iroquois lands was a matter of Indian anxiety. When the Onondagas sent an agent to Governor Clinton, they reported the warning circulating among them:

> "...that the governor of Quebec wanted their lands; that Sir John wanted them; Col. Butler wants the Cayugas' lands; and the commanding officer of Fort Niagara wants the Senecas' lands." (005-143)

Butler's influence was also felt in council. When Colonel Proctor sought to send a delegation of the Iroquois westward, the chiefs desired one of the officers of Fort Niagara to sit with them; this being allowed, Col. Butler afterward appeared among them, and after a little private consultation with him, they seemed to be utterly averse to sending the proposed delegation. (007-101)

## Key Events

- **The ambuscade against Herkimer (1777)**: Leading a company of Tories with [[joseph-brant|Brant]], Butler helped lay the ambush against General Herkimer's relief column near the beleaguered fort. (003-235)

- **Onondaga alarm over the Cayuga lands (1788)**: The Onondaga message to Governor Clinton listed "Col. Butler wants the Cayugas' lands" among the threats then felt by the [[cayuga-nation|Cayugas]] and [[onondaga-nation|Onondagas]]. (005-143)

- **Proctor's council at Buffalo Creek (1791)**: When Proctor pressed for a western delegation, Butler's private consultation with the chiefs left them averse to the plan. (007-101)

## Legacy and Historical Significance

In Hubbard's account John Butler is remembered as one of the frontier's most feared figures. An early passage, recalling the wartime raids, names the Indians as "led by the notorious Butler, Brant, and Nellis," committing their depredations and massacres among the settlers of the frontier. (013-182)

His recurrence across the narrative — from the field at Oriskany, to the anxieties over Iroquois land, to the council chamber at Fort Niagara — marks him as a persistent counter-weight to American designs among the Six Nations. (003-235) (007-101)
