# Treaty Annuities

## Definition

Treaty annuities were the recurring payments — in cash, goods, or services — that the United States government committed to provide to Native American nations as part of the consideration for land cessions. Unlike a one-time purchase price, annuities were structured as perpetual obligations, designed to create an ongoing economic relationship between the federal government and the tribes. For the Seneca, treaty annuities included annual cash payments (typically ranging from five hundred to several thousand dollars), distribution of clothing, blankets, and agricultural implements, and commitments to build infrastructure such as grist mills and saw mills on reservation lands. (005-095) (005-102)

The annuity system replaced the earlier British practice of distributing "presents" at treaty councils — a tradition the Iroquois regarded as an essential expression of diplomatic friendship. When the United States assumed the British role after the Revolution, it inherited both the expectation of gift-giving and its symbolic weight: to withhold presents or annuities was not merely to renege on a financial obligation but to repudiate the relationship itself. (010-162)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

For the Iroquois, the exchange of goods at diplomatic councils was never a purely commercial transaction. Wampum belts, presents, and later annuities were material embodiments of the covenant relationship — tangible evidence that the words spoken at the council fire were backed by genuine commitment. When the United States delayed or reduced annuity payments, as it frequently did, the Iroquois interpreted this not as bureaucratic inefficiency but as a breach of the diplomatic bond.

The annuity system also created new forms of dependency and new avenues of manipulation. Indian agents, who controlled the distribution of annuity goods and payments, acquired enormous influence over tribal affairs. They could reward allies and punish opponents through the timing and generosity of distributions. The transition from the British system of presents — distributed liberally at councils as expressions of mutual regard — to the American system of annuities — stipulated in treaty articles and administered by federal agents — reflected the broader shift in the balance of power between the Iroquois and the United States. (010-162)

## Role in the Narrative

In Hubbard's account, annuities appear at critical junctures in Red Jacket's diplomatic career. The Treaty of Canandaigua (1794) established an annuity of four thousand five hundred dollars for the Seneca — a substantial sum that the federal agent Thomas Morris later sought to use as leverage in land negotiations. (008-186) Morris brought ten thousand dollars in presents to the 1797 treaty council at Big Tree, distributing them strategically to Seneca leaders who were inclined to negotiate. (011-309)

Red Jacket understood the political economy of annuities perfectly. He recognized that the federal government used annuity payments as instruments of control — and that the Seneca's growing dependence on these payments eroded their bargaining power. His opposition to land sales was in part an opposition to the annuity trap: each cession brought a new annuity commitment, which deepened the Seneca's economic entanglement with the United States and made future resistance more costly.

Hubbard records the Onondagas' experience with the first annuity payment under the Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784) as a cautionary tale: "when the time drew near for paying the first annuity, the Onondagas sent an agent" — only to discover that the federal machinery of payment was slow, unreliable, and subject to political manipulation. (005-139)
