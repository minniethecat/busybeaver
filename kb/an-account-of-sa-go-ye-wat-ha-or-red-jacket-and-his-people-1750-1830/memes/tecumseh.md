# Tecumseh

## Biography

Tecumseh possessed rare mental endowments together with physical qualifications that extended his renown as an intrepid brave far beyond the boundaries of his own tribe, so that his name became a rallying word for the warriors even among the remote wilds. (016-018) He entertained the ambitious project, at various times a favorite design with the Indian, of uniting all the tribes at the West and South-west in one strenuous endeavor to resist the further advance of the whites into their country. (016-020)

In this design he sought to form a confederacy similar to that which had existed among the [[iroquois-confederacy|Iroquois]]. (016-023) He was greatly assisted and strengthened in these views by the influence and efforts of his brother, [[the-prophet-tenskwatawa|Elskawata]], the prophet who assumed a sacred office among the Shawnees. (016-025)

## Role and Influence

From the Prophet's town, established on the banks of the Wabash near the mouth of the Tippecanoe, a correspondence was kept up with the numerous tribes at the North and West. (016-054) Runners were sent as far even as the country of the Senecas, and the Iroquois in New York and Canada were solicited to join the Great Western Confederacy. (016-060)

Connected with this movement was the holding of Indian councils in the West. A very large council was held at or near Detroit, to which a strong deputation was sent from the Senecas with [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] at its head. (016-064)

## Key Events

- **Meeting at Vincennes (1810)**: Tecumseh appeared at Vincennes in the summer of 1810 with three hundred well armed warriors, his haughty and insulting bearing toward Governor Harrison indicating clearly the hostile spirit that was rife among the tribes. (016-116)

- **Absence at Tippecanoe (1811)**: Tecumseh was absent when the battle of Tippecanoe was fought, being engaged in forwarding his designs among the Indian tribes at the South, and he was disappointed and grieved with the result, regarding the battle as premature. (016-160)

- **Alliance with Britain in the War of 1812**: In the war the western tribes proved efficient allies of the British, increasing their force by an addition of nearly eighteen hundred, commanded by the renowned Tecumseh, who had been called the "Indian Bonaparte." (016-232) His preeminence among them was now widely acknowledged, and he swayed by his influence a greater number of warriors than any Indian of his time. (016-233)

- **Death at the Thames (1813)**: Before engaging in the Battle of the Thames he seemed to have a presentiment of his death. (016-235) He said to the chiefs about him, that they were about to enter into an engagement from which he should never come out. (016-236) His prediction was verified, and his ashes marked the field of strife where the Americans were victorious. (016-240)
