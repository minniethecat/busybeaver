# Pagan-Christian Divide

## Definition

The Pagan-Christian divide was the factional split that emerged within the Seneca Nation in the early decades of the nineteenth century, pitting those who adhered to traditional Iroquois religious practices (the "Pagan party," led by Red Jacket) against those who had converted to Christianity (the "Christian party," led by Young King and Captain Pollard). (021-103) The divide was never purely theological: it was simultaneously a contest over political power, cultural identity, and the direction of Seneca adaptation to American dominance. Red Jacket's opposition, Hubbard emphasizes, was political rather than doctrinal — he "cared very little for any religion except as it might affect the temporal interests of his people." (019-217)

The labels "Pagan" and "Christian" were themselves artifacts of the missionary encounter. The traditional Seneca religion had no name for itself as a distinct "-ism"; it was simply the way of life. The category "Pagan" was imposed from outside and, once internalized, forced Seneca people to define themselves in relation to Christianity — either as converts or as resisters.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

For the Iroquois, religion was not a separable domain of private belief; it was woven through governance, clan organization, healing, warfare, and the annual cycle of ceremonies. To abandon the traditional religion for Christianity was therefore not merely a change of theology but a repudiation of the entire Iroquois way of life. The missionaries understood this, which is why they coupled evangelism with programs of cultural transformation: English literacy, individual farming, European dress, and allegiance to the United States.

The pagan party's resistance was accordingly holistic: defending the traditional religion meant defending the traditional political order, in which sachems like Red Jacket derived their authority from clan mothers and the condolence council, not from Christian congregations or American-appointed "chiefs." When the Christian party sought to depose Red Jacket, they were not merely punishing him for his opposition to missions — they were asserting a new basis of political legitimacy, one grounded in Christian morality and alignment with American interests rather than in the hereditary institutions of the Confederacy.

## Role in the Narrative

The pagan-Christian divide is the central drama of Red Jacket's final years in Hubbard's account. The orator had spent decades warning that missionary activity would divide his people, and in the 1820s his prediction was fulfilled with devastating precision. His own wife's conversion to Christianity and departure from their home was a personal wound that mirrored the national schism. (021-078)

In 1827, the Christian party, now more numerous and emboldened by missionary support, formally deposed Red Jacket from his chiefly office. (021-157) The deposition was not merely a political coup; it was an act of cultural repudiation — a declaration that the old ways, represented by Red Jacket, no longer commanded the loyalty of a majority of Senecas. Red Jacket's journey to Washington to appeal his deposition was his last major political act. He succeeded in securing reinstatement, but he returned to a nation that remained deeply divided.

Hubbard's portrait of the pagan funeral — a ten-day affair with nightly fires kindled at the grave and feasting around it — suggests the vitality of traditional practice even at the end of Red Jacket's life. (021-311) But Red Jacket himself chose neither pagan nor Christian rites for his burial, a final acknowledgment that the divide he had so long navigated could not be resolved, only transcended. (021-281)
