# Jacob Brown

## Biography

Jacob Brown was the general who commanded the American forces on the Niagara frontier during the campaign of 1814. (017-028) Buffalo was the appointed place of rendezvous, and on the first of July, regarding his forces as sufficient to warrant the commencement of the plan of operations, he began by reconnoitering Fort Erie. (017-027)

## Role and Influence

Brown directed the opening operations of the campaign, coordinating the brigades of [[winfield-scott|General Scott]] and General Ripley in the movement against Fort Erie, and afterward advancing against the British position at Chippewa. (017-030) His command included the Seneca and volunteer forces brought over the Niagara by [[peter-b-porter|General Porter]], among whom [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] served. (017-085)

## Key Events

- **Capture of Fort Erie (July 1814)**: As the sun rose the British commandant discovered how completely he was invested. (017-047) At the demand of General Brown, without firing a gun or making any attempt at resistance, the fort and garrison were surrendered. (017-048) The fort and garrison were surrendered. (017-049)

- **Battle of Chippewa (July 1814)**: On the evening of the capture of Fort Erie, Scott's brigade advanced toward Chippewa, and was joined by General Brown with Ripley's brigade, which took post in the same field in rear of Scott. (017-067) In the action Red Jacket found Brown in the midst of the fighting. (017-202) Brown had assured the Indians that none of the British regulars would be found south of the Chippewa on that day. (017-085)

- **Withdrawal of the allied Indians**: With the consent and approval of General Brown, a deputation of two brave and influential chiefs was sent to the Indians who had fought with the British, and they were successful in persuading them to retire from the war. (017-283)

- **Siege of Fort Erie (August 1814)**: After the hard fought but not very decisive battle at Lundy's Lane, the American army fell back to Fort Erie, where a British force of five thousand advanced and laid siege to the fort. (017-296) The besiegers were repulsed with a loss of a thousand men, (017-298) and later General Brown issued from the fort and gave them so stunning a blow as caused them to relinquish the siege. (017-300)
