# Society of Friends (Quakers)

## Overview

The Society of Friends, commonly known as Quakers, was a Christian religious denomination distinguished by its commitment to pacifism, simplicity, and the belief in the "Inner Light" — the direct experience of God available to every person. Unlike other missionary organizations active among the Iroquois, the Quakers did not seek aggressive conversion. Their approach was characterized by moral witness, practical assistance, and advocacy for the fair treatment of Native peoples. (010-214)

During the councils at Buffalo Creek and elsewhere, Quaker deputations were present as observers and advocates. Hubbard records that a deputation from the Quakers attended the council at which Red Jacket delivered one of his most important speeches, representing a more benevolent form of white engagement with the Seneca. (010-259)

## Structure and Governance

The Society of Friends was organized around local "meetings" rather than a formal hierarchy. Decision-making was based on consensus, mirroring in some respects the governance practices of the Iroquois themselves. The Quaker deputations that attended Seneca councils were sent by regional meetings in New York and Pennsylvania, acting on the Friends' long-standing commitment to peaceful relations with Native peoples. (009-121)

## Historical Role

The Quakers played a distinctive role among the white organizations that engaged with the Seneca. While land companies sought to acquire Seneca territory and missionary societies sought to convert Seneca souls, the Quakers primarily sought to witness and advocate. Hubbard notes that a Quaker was specifically requested to attend a council, indicating that the Seneca themselves recognized the Friends as trustworthy observers. (009-121)

The Quaker presence at Seneca councils served as a counterweight to the more aggressive land speculators and missionaries. By inviting the Quakers to their councils, the Seneca secured sympathetic witnesses who could attest to the fairness or unfairness of the proceedings. (010-259)
