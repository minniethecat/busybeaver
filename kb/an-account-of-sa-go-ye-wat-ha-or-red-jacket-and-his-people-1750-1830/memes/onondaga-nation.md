# Onondaga Nation

## Overview

Central nation of the Iroquois Confederacy and the 'Keepers of the Council Fire' — the Onondaga hosted the Grand Council at their capital, which served as the political and spiritual center of the Six Nations.

## Structure and Governance

Like all Iroquois nations, the Onondaga Nation were organized into matrilineal clans that determined inheritance, political office, and social identity. Chiefs were selected by clan mothers and represented the nation at the Grand Council of the Iroquois Confederacy, where each nation held a specified number of hereditary sachemships.

## Historical Role

The Onondaga occupied the geographic center of Iroquois territory. (002-039) Their lands were subject to New York's acquisition. (005-094) Hubbard describes them, with pointed characterization, as 'the most profligate of the Iroquois.' (014-040)

The nation's experience during and after the Revolutionary War reflected the broader history of the Iroquois Confederacy — the devastation of war, the loss of territory through successive treaties, and the challenge of maintaining cultural identity under the pressures of American expansion and missionary activity.
