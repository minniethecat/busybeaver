# Arthur St. Clair

## Biography

Arthur St. Clair was an officer of the United States who, in the years after the Revolution, held the post of governor of the territory west of the Ohio. When the young republic resolved to mount a fresh expedition against the western Indian tribes, St. Clair was appointed commander-in-chief of the forces to be employed. (008-011)

Before this appointment he had already acted for the United States in a diplomatic capacity. In January 1789 he held a treaty with the Six Nations at Fort Harmar on the Muskingum, with a view to renew and confirm the engagements made at the [[treaty-of-fort-stanwix]] in 1784. (006-114)

It is as a soldier, however, that Hubbard's narrative chiefly remembers him, for the catastrophe that overtook his army in the autumn of 1791.

## Role and Influence

President [[george-washington]] had been deeply pained by the earlier disasters of General Harmar's expedition to the Wabash. In taking leave of his old military comrade he wished him success while putting him on his guard. (008-015) Washington's parting charge fixed on a single danger:

> "I will add but one word—Beware of a surprise! You know how the Indians fight. I repeat it—*Beware of a surprise*!" (008-018)

With these warning words sounding in his ear, St. Clair departed. (008-020)

On the seventh of September, 1791, he set out for the Indian country at the head of some two thousand soldiers. He designed to destroy the Indian villages on the Miami, expel the tribes from the region, and by a line of posts to the Ohio prevent them from returning. (008-022)

On the march his men constructed two forts, Hamilton and Jefferson, but advanced slowly and were thinned by desertion. Washington's warning proved prophetic, for St. Clair's dawn encampment near the Miami was overwhelmed by a surprise attack from concealed warriors. (008-022)

## Key Events

- **Treaty of Fort Harmar (1789)**: St. Clair held a treaty with the Six Nations at Fort Harmar in January 1789, seeking to renew and confirm the terms of Fort Stanwix. (006-114)

- **St. Clair's Defeat (November 1791)**: Near the Indian villages on the Miami, St. Clair, unable to mount his horse, was borne about on a litter, and in the midst of peril gave his orders with coolness and judgment. (008-078)

- **Aftermath**: The rout was one of the heaviest ever inflicted on a United States army by the western tribes. Hubbard notes that the leading spirit who directed the onslaught was afterward induced to visit the seat of government. (009-076)
