# Washington's Address to the Iroquois

## Background

In the early 1790s, Iroquois delegations regularly visited Philadelphia — then the seat of the federal government — to confer with President Washington and Congress. These visits were occasions for formal addresses in which both sides articulated their understanding of the relationship between the United States and the Six Nations.

## Proceedings

Washington's address at the parting of an Iroquois delegation was a carefully crafted statement of American policy. Hubbard records the speech (P04-039) (P04-042) and also notes a speech addressed to General Washington (004-293), presenting Washington as speaking with gravity and diplomatic precision. The address acknowledged the sovereignty of the Iroquois nations while simultaneously urging them toward the American model of "civilization" — agriculture, literacy, and Christianity.

Red Jacket's reply — "Washington — Red Jacket's reply — Cause of Iroquois decline" (P04-039) — was characteristically direct. Rather than accept Washington's civilizing prescription, Red Jacket offered a counter-narrative: the Iroquois were in decline because of the pressures of American expansion, not because of any deficiency in their own civilization.

## Outcome and Aftermath

Washington's address and Red Jacket's reply together encapsulated the fundamental tension that defined Iroquois-American relations: the United States offered "civilization" on American terms, while the Iroquois sought to maintain their own cultural and political autonomy. This tension persisted throughout the treaty period and beyond.

Hubbard preserves Thayendanegea's (Brant's) own assessment of "Washington's address at parting" (P04-042), suggesting that the speech was a topic of extensive discussion and analysis within Iroquois political circles.
