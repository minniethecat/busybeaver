# Pulteney Association

## Overview

The Pulteney Association was a British land syndicate that acquired extensive holdings in the Genesee region of western New York during the early period of white settlement. The association was formed by Sir William Pulteney and other British investors who purchased vast tracts of land in the Genesee Valley, becoming one of the major landholding entities in western New York alongside the Holland Land Company and the New York and Genesee Land Company. (005-049)

The association's agent, Charles Williamson, played a particularly energetic role in promoting settlement and developing the acquired lands. Hubbard notes that Williamson was an "enterprising young Scotchman" who worked to attract settlers to the Genesee country. (005-213)

## Structure and Governance

The Pulteney Association was organized as a British syndicate with capital raised from investors in England and Scotland. Its operations in New York were conducted through agents — notably Charles Williamson and his assistants John Johnstone and Charles Cameron — who managed the day-to-day affairs of land sales, settlement promotion, and relations with both the Seneca and incoming settlers. (005-219)

The Association's activities were part of a broader pattern of British investment in American lands following the Revolution. The association acquired its holdings through the same network of pre-emptive rights, state grants, and secondary purchases that characterized the operations of the Holland Land Company and the Ogden Land Company. (011-112)

## Historical Role

The Pulteney Association contributed to the rapid transformation of the Genesee Valley from Seneca territory to settled agricultural land. The valley of the Genesee was "a favorite resort of the Indians," prized for its rich soil and abundant resources. (011-005) The association's agents actively recruited settlers from the eastern states and from Europe, accelerating the demographic changes that increasingly marginalized the Seneca presence.

The Genesee country was "prized by the Indians," and the pressure exerted by the Pulteney Association and similar land companies contributed to the broader process of dispossession that Red Jacket and other Seneca leaders resisted through treaty negotiations and diplomatic appeals. (011-071)
