# Timothy Alden

## Biography

Timothy Alden, president of Allegheny College, is remembered in the account as the observer who spoke with delight of a visit he made to the old chief [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]]. (014-214) He found the chief on the banks of the Allegheny, on a piece of first-rate bottom land a little within the state's limits, where Cornplanter was the owner of thirteen hundred acres, on part of which stood his village. (014-215) (014-216) (014-217) (014-218)

## Role and Influence

Alden's significance for the account lies in the firsthand description his visit preserved. He found it pleasant to behold the agricultural habits of the place, which appeared from the numerous enclosures of buckwheat, corn and oats; he also noted a number of oxen, cows and horses, and many logs designed for the saw mill and the Pittsburgh market. (014-220) (014-221) (014-222) (014-223)

He recorded that Cornplanter had for some time been very much in favor of the Christian religion, and hailed with joy such as professed it. (014-223) (014-224) (014-225)

## Key Events

- **The visit to Cornplanter's village**: When apprised of Mr. Alden's arrival, Cornplanter hastened to welcome him to his village and to wait upon him. (014-225) (014-226)

- **The chief's hospitality**: Notwithstanding his high station as a chief, having many men under his command, Cornplanter chose rather, in the ancient patriarchal style, to serve his visitors himself; he took care of their horses, and went into the field and cut and brought oats for them. (014-227) (014-228) (014-229) (014-230)
