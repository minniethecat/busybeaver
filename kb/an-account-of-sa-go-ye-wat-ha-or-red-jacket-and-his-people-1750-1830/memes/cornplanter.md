# Cornplanter (John Abeel)

## Biography

Cornplanter was born around 1732 at Canawagus, a Seneca village on the [[genesee-river|Genesee River]]. He was the son of a Seneca mother of the Wolf clan and a Dutch trader named John Abeel (or O'Bail), from whom he took his English name. (014-003)

Unlike [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], whose reputation rested on oratory, Cornplanter earned his standing as a war chief — leading Seneca warriors in the campaigns that shaped the Revolutionary War frontier. (014-003)

After the Revolution, Cornplanter became the [[seneca-nation|Seneca]]'s principal diplomat to the new United States. He traveled repeatedly to Philadelphia and Washington, met with President Washington, and negotiated treaties that he believed would secure a viable future for his people. (014-047)

His willingness to sell Seneca lands as part of these negotiations — including the 1797 [[big-tree-treaty|Treaty of Big Tree]] — brought him into direct conflict with Red Jacket, who regarded any land cession as an existential threat. (011-309)

Cornplanter lived to be approximately 104 years old, dying in 1836 on the Cornplanter Tract in Pennsylvania — land granted to him personally. His extraordinary longevity meant his life spanned from the pre-contact era of Iroquois power, through the Revolution, to the era of reservations and Christian missions. (014-050)

## Role and Influence

As a war chief, Cornplanter led Seneca warriors alongside British forces during the Revolution. Hubbard records his participation in the defeat of General Braddock's expedition in 1755 and his prominence in the border warfare that devastated the New York and Pennsylvania frontier. (014-010)

After the war, he pivoted to diplomacy, recognizing that the Seneca's survival now depended on negotiation rather than armed resistance. (014-010)

His influence rested on his relationships with American officials. He was the Seneca leader most trusted by the Washington administration — a trust he earned by advocating for accommodation and peace. But this made him controversial. His rivals, including Red Jacket, accused him of selling Seneca land for personal gain. (014-047)

The conflict between Cornplanter and Red Jacket was more than personal rivalry; it represented a fundamental strategic disagreement. Cornplanter believed that limited accommodation was the only realistic path. Red Jacket believed that every concession weakened the Seneca's position and that resistance preserved their dignity and identity. (019-226)

Hubbard's tribute to Cornplanter captures the stature he held among his people:

> Cornplanter was a noble specimen of the Indian race. He had all the sagacity for which his people were distinguished, and was equally active, eloquent and brave. He was well qualified by his talents to engage in the legislative councils of his nation, and was unsurpassed by any, for prowess and daring in the bloody field of strife. No chief, Thayendanegea not excepted, had gained higher laurels for personal valor, and none commanded more fully the confidence and esteem of his nation. His people looked up to him as a tower of strength, and when he spake, his words fell upon them with the weight of great authority.
>
> (014-060)

At the [[treaty-of-canandaigua|Treaty of Canandaigua]] in 1794, Cornplanter stood alongside Pickering, helping to bring the council to a successful conclusion. A witness recorded his presence:

> Col. Proctor, accompanied by Cornplanter, arrived at the council fire. The chief warrior was received with demonstrations of great respect. His presence gave assurance that the negotiations would now move forward.
>
> (007-053)

## Legacy and Historical Significance

Cornplanter's legacy is inseparable from the question that haunted Iroquois leadership after the Revolution: was accommodation surrender, or the only path to survival? On this question, he and Red Jacket took opposing stands, and history has not rendered a simple verdict. (019-226)

Red Jacket's defiance preserved Seneca traditions but left the nation territorially diminished. Cornplanter's diplomacy secured temporary guarantees but set precedents for land cessions that successors exploited. (019-226)

The Cornplanter Tract — a grant of Pennsylvania land to Cornplanter personally — symbolized his unique standing with the United States. It was a reward for service, but also a mark of separation from the Seneca collective. (014-050)

The tract survived until the 1960s, when the Kinzua Dam flooded most of it — a final erasure that some Seneca saw as a delayed consequence of the accommodationist path Cornplanter had chosen. (014-050)

Cornplanter's 104-year lifespan made him a living link between eras. Born before the French and Indian War, he witnessed Braddock's defeat as a young warrior, led men in the Revolution, and negotiated with Washington and Pickering. (014-003)

He lived to see the full implementation of the reservation system. No other Iroquois leader's biography so completely mirrors the arc of Seneca history from independence to dependence. (014-003)
