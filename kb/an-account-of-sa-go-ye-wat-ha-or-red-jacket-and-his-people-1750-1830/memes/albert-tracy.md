# Albert H. Tracy

## Biography

The Hon. Albert H. Tracy belongs to the account not as an actor in the Seneca story but as one of its witnesses. Hubbard draws on him repeatedly for the record of [[red-jacket]]'s later years, noting that the account of one contested council was given by Hon. Albert Tracy, along with various utterances of the orator on that occasion. (018-004)

Tracy's value to the historical record was that of a firsthand observer who committed his impressions to paper. Colonel [[william-l-stone]] refers to the Hon. Albert H. Tracy as having furnished the notes of the council under consideration, the raw material from which later biographers reconstructed Red Jacket's speeches. (018-331)

## Role and Influence

Tracy witnessed Red Jacket in the decline of his life and left one of the most quoted appraisals of his oratory. Speaking of the eloquence of Red Jacket, he confessed the limits of any translation:

> "It is evident that the best translations of Indian speeches, must fail to express the beauty and sublimity of the originals; especially of such an original as Red Jacket." (018-334)

He added that his good fortune to hear the orator came only in later years, when Red Jacket's powers were enfeebled by age and still more by intemperance. (018-337) Even so, the impression of the first council never left him; through an illiterate interpreter he still saw the *disjecta membra* of a regular and splendid oration. (018-346)

Through Stone's biography, Tracy's testimony also fixed the record of specific occasions. On his authority, Stone mentions a treaty held for the purpose of obtaining Seneca lands in 1822 or 1823, in which Red Jacket replied to speeches by the commissioner and by Governor Ogden. (018-242)

## Key Events

- **Council testimony**: Tracy furnished eyewitness notes of a council at which Red Jacket rejected the [[ogden-land-company]]'s renewed demands, preserving utterances of the orator that would otherwise have been lost. (018-004) (018-331)

- **Record of the 1822–1823 treaty**: On Tracy's authority, Stone recorded a treaty of 1822 or 1823 at which Red Jacket answered the commissioner and Governor Ogden with a history of Indian-white dealings. (018-242)
