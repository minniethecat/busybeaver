# Philip Schuyler

## Biography

Philip Schuyler was an American general of the Revolutionary period through whom the new government first sought to secure the neutrality of the Iroquois. At a council held with the Iroquois at German Flats, in June, 1776, by Gen. Schuyler, who had been appointed for this purpose, the assurances of neutrality were renewed. (003-075)

He is to be distinguished from Peter Schuyler, a separate figure named among the later New York commissioners for Indian affairs. (005-083)

## Role and Influence

Schuyler's German Flats council became the standard to which the Iroquois chiefs afterward appealed when British agents pressed them to take up arms.

When those agents urged that the king desired their help in subduing the rebels, the chiefs one by one assured them that they had, the year before, in a council with General Schuyler, pledged themselves to neutrality, and could not without violating their promise, take up the hatchet. (003-182)

> "...in a council with General Schuyler, pledged themselves to neutrality, and could not without violating their promise, take up the hatchet." (003-182)

That pledge was in the end overcome, for the British agents pressed their appeal until a treaty of alliance was concluded, and the neutrality Schuyler had obtained gave way. (003-182)

After the Revolution, Philip Schuyler appears again in the account among the men employed to acquire Indian lands for the State of New York. He was associated with the commissioners — Abraham Cuyler, Peter Schuyler, and Henry Glen — who, with other associates, entered upon negotiations to purchase those Indian lands within the State not covered by the pre-emptive right of Massachusetts. (005-084)

A council of the Iroquois for this purpose was appointed at Fort Schuyler, on the first of September, 1788. (005-088)

## Key Events

- **The German Flats council (June 1776)**: Appointed by the government for the purpose, Schuyler renewed with the Iroquois their assurances of neutrality at the opening of the Revolutionary War. (003-075)

- **The neutrality pledge cited (1777)**: When British agents afterward sought to enlist the [[iroquois-confederacy|Iroquois]], the chiefs recalled their pledge to Schuyler as the reason they could not take up the hatchet. (003-182)

- **New York land negotiations (1788)**: Schuyler joined the New York commissioners purchasing Indian lands outside the Massachusetts pre-emption, a council for which was appointed at Fort Schuyler on the first of September, 1788. (005-084) (005-088)

## Legacy and Historical Significance

In Hubbard's account Philip Schuyler stands chiefly as the American officer through whom the United States first approached the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]] for neutrality, and later as one of the New York commissioners who pressed the purchase of Indian lands.

His 1776 pledge is treated as a fixed point in the narrative, remembered and quoted by the chiefs the following year. (003-182) The account is careful to separate him from Peter Schuyler, with whom his name might be confused among the commissioners of the period. (005-083)
