# Philadelphia

## Geography

Philadelphia lies on the Delaware River in southeastern Pennsylvania, far from the Iroquois territories of western New York. For the Seneca, a journey to Philadelphia meant traveling hundreds of miles through settled country — a vivid demonstration of the reach and power of the United States. The city's location on the eastern seaboard placed it at the center of American political and economic life during the 1790s, when it served as the temporary capital of the United States.

## Historical Significance

Philadelphia was, for the Seneca leaders who traveled there, the face of American power. When Seneca chiefs visited the city, they encountered a world utterly different from their own — a bustling commercial port, the seat of Congress, and the headquarters of the land companies that sought to acquire their territory. Hubbard records that representatives from "Big Tree visited Philadelphia, which was at that time the seat of government" (006-229), emphasizing the political significance of these journeys.

Robert Morris, the Philadelphia financier whose land speculation drove much of the pressure on Seneca lands, operated from this city. His purchase of pre-emption rights to Seneca territory and his subsequent lobbying of Congress made Philadelphia the command center of the land acquisition machine that was progressively dispossessing the Iroquois. (006-150)

The "Congress at Philadelphia" (011-159) was the legislative body that enacted the Indian Trade and Intercourse Acts and ratified treaties that shaped the legal framework within which the Seneca were compelled to negotiate.

## Key Events at This Location

Seneca delegations repeatedly traveled to Philadelphia to meet with the President and Congress. Red Jacket himself visited Philadelphia early in 1792, where "a bill was passed by Congress" during his visit (009-008), giving him a direct view of the legislative processes that affected his people.

Cornplanter led a particularly significant delegation to Philadelphia, where he addressed President Washington and members of Congress. "We kindled a council fire" in Philadelphia, Cornplanter later recounted, describing a diplomatic encounter that attempted to replicate Iroquois protocols in the heart of the American capital. (018-102) A treaty was subsequently concluded — the Treaty of Canandaigua (1794) — which, though negotiated in Iroquois territory, was shaped by the congressional deliberations in Philadelphia. (018-114)

Red Jacket, reflecting on his visits, later expressed "the only regret that when I was in Philadelphia I did not understand it" (020-292), acknowledging the cultural and linguistic barriers that complicated even the most earnest diplomatic encounters.
