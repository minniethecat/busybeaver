# Revolutionary War and the Iroquois

## Background

When the Revolutionary War began in 1775, the Iroquois Confederacy faced an unprecedented crisis. For centuries, the Great Law of Peace had kept the Six Nations united. But the war between Britain and the American colonies presented a choice that the Confederacy's traditional decision-making structures were not designed to handle.

Initially, the Confederacy declared neutrality. But neutrality proved unsustainable. Both British and American agents actively courted Iroquois allegiance, and the pressures of war — the flow of arms, the movement of armies, the presence of Loyalist refugees — made abstention impossible. At a general council at Oswego, Sir John Johnson urged the Iroquois to take up arms for the Crown. (003-177)

## Proceedings

The Confederacy fractured along national lines. The Mohawk, under Joseph Brant, were the most committed British allies. The Seneca, Cayuga, and Onondaga also sided predominantly with Britain, swayed by long-standing trade relationships and the British promise to protect Iroquois lands from American settlement. The Oneida and Tuscarora, influenced by American missionaries and proximity to rebel settlements, supported the Revolution.

The war on the frontier was characterized by raids and counter-raids. Iroquois war parties, often operating with Loyalist rangers, struck American settlements in the Mohawk Valley and along the Pennsylvania frontier. American forces retaliated with the Sullivan-Clinton Campaign of 1779, which systematically destroyed Iroquois towns and crops.

Red Jacket, then a young warrior, participated in the war. Hubbard notes that he was present at several engagements but that his temperament was better suited to the council fire than the battlefield. The experience of the war — the devastation of Seneca communities, the burning of their towns, the flight to British posts — profoundly shaped his subsequent political career.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The Treaty of Paris (1783) ended the war, but Britain made no provision for its Iroquois allies. The treaty ceded all territory south of the Great Lakes to the United States without consulting or protecting the Six Nations. Abandoned by their British patrons and devastated by years of warfare, the Iroquois faced the victorious Americans alone.

The war shattered the unity of the Iroquois Confederacy — a fracture from which it never fully recovered. Brant led many Mohawk to the Grand River in Canada, while the Seneca retreated to their remaining villages in western New York. The diplomatic pattern of the next half-century — treaties, land cessions, and the progressive diminishment of Iroquois sovereignty — was set in motion by the war's outcome.

## Key Participants

- **Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea)** — Mohawk war leader, British ally
- **Red Jacket** — Young Seneca warrior, later the foremost defender of Seneca sovereignty
- **Sir John Johnson** — British Indian superintendent
- **John Butler** — Loyalist ranger commander
