# Detroit

## Geography

Detroit occupies a commanding position on the Detroit River, the narrow waterway connecting Lake Erie to Lake Huron. The river served as the critical chokepoint in the Great Lakes transportation network — all traffic between the upper and lower lakes passed through this corridor. Hubbard references the "mouth of Detroit river" (006-063) as a significant geographical marker, noting its relationship to an Indian village near the river's entry into Lake Erie.

The broader Detroit region was a borderland where the territories of multiple Native nations intersected — the Iroquois to the east, the Algonquian peoples of the upper lakes, and the expanding American settlements to the south.

## Historical Significance

Detroit was the western anchor of Britain's post-Revolutionary military presence on the Great Lakes. Along with Oswegatchie, Oswego, Niagara, and Mackinaw, it formed a chain of posts from the St. Lawrence to the upper lakes that effectively surrounded the new American republic on its northwestern frontier. (006-084) Britain's retention of these posts was the most tangible evidence that the Treaty of Paris had not resolved the military balance in the Great Lakes region.

For the western Iroquois and their neighbors, Detroit offered an alternative source of trade goods, diplomatic recognition, and military support. British agents at Detroit distributed gifts to visiting Native delegations and encouraged resistance to American expansion. The post's distance from the main Iroquois settlements in New York meant its influence was felt more among the western nations, but its existence affected the strategic calculations of all Native peoples in the region.

An Indian village near "the mouth of Detroit River" (006-063) indicates that Native settlement was concentrated around this strategic waterway well before European fortification, making Detroit a site of long-standing indigenous presence and inter-tribal interaction.

## Key Events at This Location

Detroit figured in the broader diplomacy of the post-Revolutionary period. Hubbard mentions a council that "embraced in it deputations from the remote parts of Canada, Mackinaw, Grand River, and Detroit" (016-063), indicating that Detroit served as a hub for Native political coordination across vast distances.

During the War of 1812, Detroit was the scene of a major American setback — General William Hull's surrender of the fort to British and Native forces "attended by the surrender of Detroit" (016-221). This event had repercussions for the entire Great Lakes frontier, including the Iroquois communities in New York who watched the military balance shift.

The British evacuation of Detroit under the Jay Treaty in 1796, like the evacuation of the other posts, curtailed Britain's ability to directly influence the Native nations south of the Great Lakes and marked a turning point in the consolidation of American control over the Old Northwest.
