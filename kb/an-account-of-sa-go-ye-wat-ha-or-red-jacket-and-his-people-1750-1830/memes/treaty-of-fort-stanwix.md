# Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784)

## Background

The Revolutionary War had devastated the Iroquois Confederacy. The Sullivan-Clinton Campaign of 1779 had burned Seneca towns and crops, forcing survivors to flee to British posts for sustenance.

When the Treaty of Paris (1783) ended the war, Britain made no provision for its Iroquois allies — ceding all territory south of the Great Lakes to the United States without consulting or protecting the Six Nations.

The United States, having won the war, approached the Iroquois from a position of strength. The government adopted a policy "of purchasing Indian lands at a fair price, rather than by war and conquest." (004-045) The first test of this policy would be the council convened at Fort Stanwix.

## Proceedings

A "general council of the Iroquois was convened at Fort Stanwix, in the fall of 1784." (004-046) Oliver Wolcott, Richard Butler, and Arthur Lee served as commissioners for the United States. All tribes of the Iroquois were represented. (004-047)

For Red Jacket, recently elevated to the office of Sachem, "the council at Fort Stanwix was the first occasion in which he appeared before the public." (004-082) Hubbard captures the gravity of
the moment: "With an anxious heart the Indian left his home and wended his way, through his native forests, to the place where he was to meet in council, the chiefs of the thirteen fires." (004-083)

The negotiations were tense. The Iroquois, defeated in war and abandoned by their British allies, faced commissioners who held all the advantages. Red Jacket, though newly elevated, "took an active part in its deliberations." (004-050)

## Outcome and Aftermath

The treaty imposed harsh terms. The Iroquois were compelled to cede substantial territory, including claims to the Ohio Country, and to deliver hostages as guarantees of compliance. The goods distributed — "amounting to three thousand dollars" (006-116) — were a meager compensation for the lands surrendered.

The treaty generated lasting bitterness. Hubbard notes that "they were dissatisfied with the treaty at Fort Stanwix. They grew out of this dissatisfaction" (006-235) a series of subsequent disputes. Red Jacket, observing the proceedings, "projected the plan" (006-006) that would define his lifelong political strategy — opposition to all further land cessions.

The commissioners' conduct at Fort Stanwix set a precedent for the pattern of treaty-making that would follow: diplomatic formalities masking a fundamentally coercive transaction.

## Participants and Roles

| Party | Key Representatives | Demands / Terms |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| United States | Oliver Wolcott, Richard Butler, Arthur Lee | Cession of Iroquois claims to Ohio Country |
| Seneca | Red Jacket, Cornplanter | Hostages taken to guarantee performance |
| Mohawk | Joseph Brant | Represented British-allied faction |
| Other Six Nations | Onondaga, Cayuga, Oneida, Tuscarora delegates | Forced to accept dictated terms |

The treaty proceedings were conducted under the shadow of recent war. Hubbard records the atmosphere:

> The Americans met the Indians as conquerors, and dictated terms as to a vanquished people. The Iroquois were required to deliver up hostages as guarantees for the performance of the stipulations agreed upon. It was an ungracious beginning for the new relations between the United States and the Six Nations.
>
> (006-005)

Red Jacket's first public appearance as sachem came at this council. Though not yet the dominant voice he would become, his participation marked the beginning of his public career:

> At this treaty Red Jacket appeared for the first time in the character of a sachem. The office had been conferred upon him in recognition of his talents, and he came forward now to take his place among the recognized leaders of his nation.
>
> (004-002)
