# William Henry Harrison

## Biography

William Henry Harrison was the Governor of the western territory and the general who marched at the head of his troops against the Prophet's town. (016-005)

The visit of [[tecumseh|Tecumseh]] at Vincennes in the summer of 1810, with three hundred well armed warriors, and his haughty and insulting bearing toward Governor Harrison, indicated clearly the hostile spirit that was rife among the tribes. (016-117)

## Role and Influence

The Indians were greatly encouraged in their warlike feeling by the intercourse they constantly maintained with the [[british-indian-department|British Indian Department]]. (016-128) These threatening indications coming to the knowledge of the government, General Harrison was directed to go with an armed force to the Prophet's town. (016-138)

His visit resulted in the battle of Tippecanoe, fought on the seventh of November, 1811. (016-139)

## Key Events

- **Battle of Tippecanoe (1811)**: Harrison made a careful survey of the country and, selecting an advantageous position, encamped for the night. (016-144) At an early hour the troops were furiously assailed by the Indians, who had crept up unobserved. (016-147) The engagement was bloody and for a time doubtful, but the Indians were at length repulsed and a decisive victory gained. (016-149)

- **The Prophet during the battle**: The Prophet was securely stationed on an adjoining eminence during the battle, (016-150) engaged in singing one of his old war songs. (016-153) When told what was taking place, he answered and resumed his singing more loudly:

  > "Go,—fight on: it will soon be as I have said." (016-154)

- **Command in the War of 1812**: New troops were raised and brought to the field under the wise conduct of General Harrison. (016-225) With Commodore Perry's naval victory on Lake Erie, September 10, 1813, and the decisive battle on the river Thames that October, the prospect of the American arms was materially improved. (016-226)

- **Peace on the frontier**: After this battle the Western Indians were disposed to entertain propositions of peace. (016-229)
