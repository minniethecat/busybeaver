# Treaty of Canandaigua (1794)

## Background

After the contentious Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784), relations between the United States and the Iroquois Confederacy remained unsettled. The retention of British posts at Niagara, Oswego, and Detroit provided the Iroquois with ongoing access to British diplomatic support, while American settlers continued to encroach on Native lands. The defeat of the Western Confederacy at the Battle of Fallen Timbers (1794) shifted the military balance, making a comprehensive diplomatic settlement both possible and urgent.

President Washington appointed Colonel Timothy Pickering to negotiate a definitive treaty with the Six Nations. Pickering, who had already established relationships with Iroquois leaders at the Council of Tioga Point (1790), was well-positioned to conduct the negotiations. (006-158)

## Proceedings

The council convened at Canandaigua in the fall of 1794. Captain Parrish, a long-time resident of the area who was present at the treaty, served as interpreter. (003-017) The negotiations addressed the central issues that had festered since Fort Stanwix: land boundaries, the status of British-allied Iroquois, and the terms under which peaceful coexistence would be maintained.

Red Jacket played a prominent role in the deliberations. The treaty text was subsequently "copied by Drake and published" (004-174), providing a documentary record of the agreements reached. The Seneca, through their speeches, sought guarantees that their remaining lands would be protected from further encroachment — guarantees that Pickering, constrained by the realities of American expansion, could only partially provide.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The treaty's terms were presented as a mutual accommodation: the United States acknowledged Iroquois sovereignty over their reserved lands, and the Six Nations acknowledged the peace. "We widened our path," the Seneca later recounted — a metaphor for establishing a clear diplomatic channel. (018-124)

However, the promises made at Canandaigua were not always honored. At a subsequent council, the Seneca protested: "At the treaty of Canandaigua, we were promised that different nations should not be suffered to take our lands." (018-151) The gap between treaty promises and their implementation became a recurring grievance in Iroquois-American relations.

Despite its limitations, the Treaty of Canandaigua remains significant as one of the few early treaties that explicitly recognized Native sovereignty rather than merely recording land cessions. Captain Parrish of Canandaigua, who witnessed the proceedings, "has frequently been heard to speak" of the treaty's importance in later years. (004-275)

## Key Participants

- **Timothy Pickering** — US Commissioner, later Secretary of State
- **Red Jacket** — Seneca sachem, key orator for the Iroquois delegation
- **Cornplanter** — Seneca war chief, advocate for accommodation
- **Captain Parrish** — Canandaigua resident, interpreter
- **Horatio Jones** — Interpreter and intermediary
