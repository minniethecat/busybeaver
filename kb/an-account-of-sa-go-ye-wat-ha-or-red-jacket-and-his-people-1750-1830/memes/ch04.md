[004-001]Early struggles—[[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s opportunity for trial—Council at Fort

[004-002][[fort-stanwix|Stanwix]]—Red Jacket's office of Sachem—Red Jacket's opposition to the

[004-003]proposed treaty—Excitement created by his speech—Allayed by [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]]

[004-004]—His influence in deciding the treaty—How it affected him.

[004-005]How long and toilsome the way, ere the ambitious aspirant passes from the

[004-006]low grounds of obscurity, to the dazzling heights of fame! How many hours

[004-007]of anxious toil, through wearisome days and nights, protracted through

[004-008]months and years, are passed, before the arena even is entered, where the

[004-009]race commences in earnest! How many struggling emotions between hope and

[004-010]fear, encouragement and doubt, promise and despair, mark the experience,

[004-011]and clothe it with the sublimity and interest that belong to action in its

[004-012]highest forms!

[004-013]Did this child of nature cherishing the bright dream from early life,

[004-014]never suffer from these contending emotions, ere he awoke finally to the

[004-015]consciousness of the reality, where he could exclaim, I am an orator, yes,

[004-016]I AM AN ORATOR!

[004-017]This idea Red Jacket began now to cherish. He had practiced in his native

[004-018]wilds, the forest depths had echoed back those strains of [[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]], that

[004-019]had struggled for utterance in his impassioned bosom, and their force

[004-020]being expended here, served but to awaken a still stronger desire to try

[004-021]his powers, where he could have the answering sympathy of human hearts.

[004-022]His fame and greatness were yet to be achieved. With the inward

[004-023]consciousness of strength that would secure for him the eminence he

[004-024]desired, he awaited eagerly the opportunity for its exercise. This

[004-025]opportunity came.

[004-026]When the storm of war had rolled by, the hour came for deliberation, and

[004-027]council. England and America had concluded peace, and the jurisdiction of

[004-028]the country of the Iroquois had been surrendered to the United States.

[004-029]Still no provision had been made by the crown for those tribes that had

[004-030]freely fought in her defence. They were left to make their own peace, or

[004-031]prosecute the war on their own account. Their attitude was yet hostile. No

[004-032]expedition of importance was undertaken, but the border men were

[004-033]constantly annoyed by Indians, who drove away their horses and cattle, and

[004-034]committed other acts of depredation. And the inhabitants of the frontier

[004-035]had suffered so severely from the Indian tribes during the war, that these

[004-036]acts served to awaken still deeper feelings of hostility toward them, and

[004-037]led some openly to recommend that the Indians be driven from their lands,

[004-038]and that these be forfeited to the State.

[004-039]These councils were strenuously resisted by the general government. The

[004-040]humane and considerate [[george-washington|Washington]] thought it wiser to try and conciliate

[004-041]them, and if possible win their confidence and esteem, claiming that their

[004-042]lands, when needed, could be obtained at a cheaper rate by negotiation and

[004-043]purchase, than by war and conquest.

[004-044]This course, the excellence of which experience has fully demonstrated,

[004-045]was finally adopted, and in pursuance of this design, a general council of

[004-046]the Iroquois was convened at Fort Stanwix, in the fall of 1784. It was

[004-047]attended by Oliver Wolcott, Richard Butler, and Arthur Lee, who were

[004-048]appointed commissioners on the part of the United States. The different

[004-049]tribes of the Iroquois were represented, and Red Jacket was present, and

[004-050]took an active part in its deliberations. He had now been elected to the

[004-051]office of Sachem; at what time precisely, is not known, but probably not

[004-052]far from the close of the war of the Revolution.

[004-053]The manner in which he gained this office has been ascribed by some to

[004-054]artifice as well as the force of his eloquence. Col. Stone says, that

[004-055]"aspiring to the rank of chief, he not only wrought upon the minds of his

[004-056]people, by the exertion of that faculty which was ever with them a high

[004-057]standard of merit, but he succeeded in availing himself of the

[004-058]superstitious constitution of his race, to effect his purpose. His first

[004-059]essay was to dream that he was, or should be a chief, and that the Great

[004-060]Spirit was angry that his nation had not advanced him to that dignity.

[004-061]This dream, with the necessary variations, he repeated until, fortunately

[004-062]for him, the small pox broke out among the [[seneca-nation|Senecas]]. He then proclaimed the

[004-063]loathsome infliction a judgment sent by the [[great-spirit|Great Spirit]], to punish them

[004-064]for their ingratitude to him. The consequence ultimately was, that by

[004-065]administering flattery to some, working upon the superstitious fears of

[004-066]others, and by awakening the admiration of all by his eloquence, he

[004-067]reached the goal of his ambition." [Footnote: Col. Stone's Life and Times

[004-068]of [[joseph-brant|Thayendanegea]] and Life and Times of Red Jacket. This statement has been

[004-069]denied by some, who affirm that his eloquence was the sole cause of his

[004-070]elevation. If this representation came from Brant, it may be recollected

[004-071]that between Red Jacket and Brant there did not exist a very strong

[004-072]attachment, and statements made by one concerning the other, would not be

[004-073]likely to bear the coloring of a very warm friendship.]

[004-074]However this may have been, it is certain this course was not necessary to

[004-075]establish Red Jacket's position among his people. The circumstances of

[004-076]their history created a necessity for his transcendent abilities, and the

[004-077]light of his genius, though it may have been obscured for a time, must

[004-078]eventually have shone forth, in its original beauty and splendor.

[004-079]Red Jacket was now called upon to assist in the deliberations of his

[004-080]people, and from this time to the day of his death, we find him connected

[004-081]with, and bearing an important part in all of their public transactions.

[004-082]The council at Fort Stanwix was the first occasion in which he appeared

[004-083]before the public. It was a meeting of no small moment. With an anxious

[004-084]heart the Indian left his home and wended his way, through his native

[004-085]forests, to the place where he was to meet in council, the chiefs of the

[004-086]thirteen fires. His own tribes had been wasted, by a long and bloody war.

[004-087]The nation they had so long clung to, and by whose artifice they had been

[004-088]led to engage in the strife, stood confessedly vanquished. A new power had

[004-089]arisen in the land, what bearing would it have on their future fortunes?

[004-090]With the importance of this gathering none were more deeply impressed than

[004-091]Red Jacket.—Yonder he stands, alone;—his knit brow, and searching glance

[004-092]indicate a process of thought, which stirs deeply the emotions of the

[004-093]inner man.—Tread lightly, lest you disturb the silent evolutions of that

[004-094]airy battalion, that is wheeling into rank and file, thoughts that

[004-095]discharged in words, reach the mark and do execution.—Now he wears a look

[004-096]of indignation, which presently turns to one of proud defiance, as he

[004-097]contemplates the encroaching disposition of the white race.—Now you may

[004-098]detect an air of scorn, and his eye flashes fire, as he regards them at

[004-099]first a feeble colony, which might easily have been crushed by the strong

[004-100]arm of the Iroquois.—A feeling of deep concern directly overspreads his

[004-101]features, as he thinks of their advancing power, and of the prospect of

[004-102]their surpassing even the glory of his own ancestry.—A still deeper shade

[004-103]steals over him as he thinks of the waning fortunes of his people.—

[004-104]Presently his countenance is lighted up;—his feelings are all aglow,—a

[004-105]bright thought, has entered his mind.—He conceives the idea of the union

[004-106]of the entire race of red men, to resist the encroachments of the whites.

[004-107]—Are they not yet strong? And united, would they not yet be, a

[004-108]formidable power?

[004-109]With anxious and matured thoughts, Red Jacket comes to this council

[004-110]gathering. Its bearing on his nation and race, he deeply scans, and

[004-111]treasures up those burning thoughts, with which he is to electrify, and

[004-112]set on fire the bosoms of his countrymen.

[004-113]Of the proceedings of this council, little is known aside from the bare

[004-114]treaty itself. By this treaty perpetual peace and amity were agreed upon

[004-115]between the United States, and the Iroquois, and the latter ceded to the

[004-116]United States, all their lands lying west of a line commencing at the

[004-117]mouth of a creek four miles east of [[fort-niagara|Niagara]], at a place on [[lake-ontario|Lake Ontario]]

[004-118]called [[sir-william-johnson|Johnson]]'s Landing; thence south, in a direction always four miles

[004-119]east of the portage, or carrying-path, between Lakes Erie and Ontario, to

[004-120]the mouth of Buffalo creek, on Lake Erie; thence due south to the north

[004-121]boundary of the state of Pennsylvania; thence west to the end of said

[004-122]boundary; thence south along the west boundary of the state of

[004-123]Pennsylvania to the Ohio river.

[004-124]In consideration of this surrender to the United States of their claim to

[004-125]western lands, the Iroquois were to be secure in the peaceful possession

[004-126]of the lands they inhabited in the state of New York.

[004-127]This treaty Red Jacket strenuously resisted. He regarded the proposed

[004-128]cession of lands as exorbitant and unjust, and summoned all the resources

[004-129]of his eloquence to defend his position. The course of his argument and

[004-130]the various means he took to enforce it, we have no means of adequately

[004-131]presenting. A few hints respecting it, and the testimony of those present

[004-132]as to the effect produced, is all we have to guide us in forming any

[004-133]estimate of its merits.

[004-134]After giving a vivid representation of the encroachments already made upon

[004-135]them by the whites, and of the advances they were making in numbers and

[004-136]power, as well as extent of territory, he reminded his hearers of the

[004-137]ancient glory of the Iroquois, and contrasted it with their present wasted

[004-138]and feeble condition. They had been passing through a mighty convulsion,

[004-139]the hurricane had swept over their dwellings, their homes were laid waste,

[004-140]their country made desolate.

[004-141]He directed them to the extensive dominion they had exercised. Their

[004-142]empire was wide, on the north, and east, and south, and west, there were

[004-143]none to stay their hand, or limit their power. A broad continent was open

[004-144]to them on every side, and their seats were large. But now they were met

[004-145]by a people to whom they had surrendered a large portion of their lands,

[004-146]and "they are driving us on toward the setting sun. They would shut us in,

[004-147]they would close up the path to our brethren at the west. We demand an

[004-148]open way."

[004-149]They had no right, he affirmed, to part with their western lands. Their

[004-150]laws, their ancient usages forbade it. They ought never to decide a

[004-151]question so momentous as this, without giving all the parties a hearing,

[004-152]who have any interest in its decision. They should be present and join in

[004-153]their deliberations. Their brethren at the west had a right to be

[004-154]consulted in this matter.—It would be unworthy of the name, and exalted

[004-155]fame of the Iroquois, to decide the question without reference to them.—

[004-156]It was a question that affected deeply the interests of the entire race of

[004-157]red men on this continent. He declared finally that rather than yield to

[004-158]the exorbitant demands of the treaty, they should take up their arms, and

[004-159]prosecute the war on their own account.

[004-160]Such is the scanty outline of a speech that made a wonderful impression on

[004-161]the minds of all his people who were present. During the progress of his

[004-162]speech, their emotions were wrought up to a pitch, that seemed to betoken

[004-163]a rising storm, and at times it seemed as though it needed but a spark to

[004-164]set on fire a flame that was ready to burst out with consuming force.

[004-165]Those present, who did not understand the language of the orator, were

[004-166]deeply interested in his voice, his manner of elocution, and his perfect

[004-167]and inimitable action. They caught fire from his eye, and felt the

[004-168]inspiration, which was kindled in the minds of all who listened to him

[004-169]understandingly. When he sat down his work was accomplished. There was but

[004-170]one heart among his people. From this time on, he was the peerless orator

[004-171]of his nation.

[004-172]A very interesting sketch of Red Jacket as an orator, refers, for the

[004-173]existence of the facts which form the basis of its statements, to a treaty

[004-174]held at [[canandaigua|Canandaigua]] in 1794. It has been copied by Drake, and published in

[004-175]almost every sketch of the orator's life. Mr. Stone questions its

[004-176]truthfulness on the ground that there is no notice of it in any notes of

[004-177]this council taken at the time, and because also there was evidently an

[004-178]absence of the peculiar circumstances, which the speech referred to, seems

[004-179]to demand. Still he introduces it under the supposition that if delivered

[004-180]there at all, it might have been during the excitement produced among the

[004-181]Indians, by the rejection from the council, by Col. [[timothy-pickering|Pickering]], of one

[004-182]Johnson, a messenger from Brant, who had been invited to be present at

[004-183]that council. Yet this is by no means probable, as Red Jacket would have

[004-184]been far from rising into eloquence on an occasion, which from his known

[004-185]relations to the proud [[mohawk-nation|Mohawk]], he would naturally view with satisfaction,

[004-186]instead of resentment. The more probable supposition is, that the writer

[004-187]caught up this as a traditionary statement, which, owing to the lapse of

[004-188]time and the uncertainty of memory, had been changed in one or two of its

[004-189]items, and receiving it as correct, penned it in good faith, as having

[004-190]transpired at that treaty. It is a correct presentation of some of the

[004-191]points in the orator's speech on this occasion, and is as follows:

[004-192][Footnote: Mr. Stone justly supposes this speech might have been made at

[004-193]the treaty of Fort Stanwix in 1784.]

[004-194]"… The witnesses of the scene will never forget the powers of native

[004-195]oratory. Two days had passed away in negotiation with the Indians for a

[004-196]cession of their lands. The contract was supposed to be nearly completed,

[004-197]when Red Jacket arose. With the grace and dignity of a Roman Senator, he

[004-198]drew his blanket around him, and with a piercing eye surveyed the

[004-199]multitude. All was hushed. Nothing interposed to break the silence, but

[004-200]the rustling of the leaves. After a long and solemn, but not unmeaning

[004-201]pause, he commenced in a low voice, and sententious style. Rising

[004-202]gradually with the subject, he depicted the primitive simplicity and

[004-203]happiness of his nation, and the wrongs they had sustained from the

[004-204]usurpations of white men, with such a bold and faithful pencil, that every

[004-205]auditor was soon roused to vengeance, or melted to tears. The effect was

[004-206]inexpressible. But ere the emotions of admiration and sympathy had

[004-207]subsided, the white men became alarmed. They were in the heart of an

[004-208]Indian country, surrounded by ten times their number, who were inflamed by

[004-209]a remembrance of their injuries, and excited to indignation by the

[004-210]eloquence of a favorite chief. Appalled and terrified, the white men cast

[004-211]a cheerless gaze on the hordes around them. A nod from the chiefs might be

[004-212]the onset of destruction. At this portentious moment, [[farmer-s-brother|Farmer's Brother]]

[004-213]interposed. He replied not to his brother chief, but with a sagacity truly

[004-214]aboriginal, he caused the cessation of the council, introduced good cheer,

[004-215]commended the eloquence of Red Jacket, and before the meeting had

[004-216]reassembled, with the aid of other prudent chiefs, he had moderated the

[004-217]fury of his nation to a more salutary view of the question before them."

[004-218]The commissioners replied, but without making much headway on account of

[004-219]the agitation and excitement, produced by the orator's speech; that by the

[004-220]common usages of war they might lay claim to a much larger extent of

[004-221]territory; that their demand was characterized by great moderation, and

[004-222]insisted on their yielding to the terms proposed.

[004-223]There was little disposition among them to yield the point, yet the treaty

[004-224]was finally brought to a successful issue, by the influence of

[004-225]Cornplanter.

[004-226]Cornplanter was a noble specimen of the Indian race. He had all the

[004-227]sagacity for which his people were distinguished, and was equally active,

[004-228]eloquent and brave. He was well qualified by his talents to engage in the

[004-229]legislative councils of his nation, and was unsurpassed by any, for

[004-230]prowess and daring in the bloody field of strife. No chief, Thayendanegea

[004-231]not excepted, had gained higher laurels for personal valor, and none

[004-232]commanded more fully the confidence and esteem of his nation. His people

[004-233]looked up to him as a tower of strength, and when he spake, his words fell

[004-234]upon them with the weight of great authority. Better acquainted than his

[004-235]junior associate with the details of war, and understanding likewise the

[004-236]wasted and feeble condition of his people, and having learned in the late

[004-237]conflict something of the power of the enemy they would have to encounter,

[004-238]he regarded the idea of their resistance as wholly impracticable, and

[004-239]advised a compliance with the terms of the treaty. Though he regretted the

[004-240]loss of any more territory, he wisely concluded it was better to lose a

[004-241]part, than to be deprived of all. And by throwing his influence decidedly

[004-242]in favor, he succeeded finally in quieting the minds of his people, and in

[004-243]persuading them to accede to the proposals made.

[004-244]It is a matter of regret that so few traces are left, of Red Jacket's

[004-245]speech on this occasion. Yet had his speech been reported, we might have

[004-246]been as much at a loss as at present, to derive from it a just estimation

[004-247]of his talents. His speeches as reported are tame when compared with the

[004-248]effect produced.

[004-249]The Indian was an unwritten language. The most distinguished orators of

[004-250]the Iroquois confederacy, matured their thoughts in solitude without the

[004-251]aid of the pen, and when uttered in the hearing of the people, they passed

[004-252]forever into oblivion, only as a striking passage may hare been retained

[004-253]in memory. And with them the want of a written language was thus in a

[004-254]measure compensated. They made an increased effort to treasure up their

[004-255]thoughts. Yet how much must necessarily have been lost! and how liable to

[004-256]waste away, that which remained.

[004-257]Trusting to them how imperfect must have been a reported speech! And

[004-258]relying on those who transferred their speeches to a different language,

[004-259]we have little assurance of any thing better than mutilated transcripts of

[004-260]the original. Need we be surprised then, to find in Red Jacket's published

[004-261]speeches, a tameness unworthy of his fame? Red Jacket was esteemed by the

[004-262]men of his time as an orator, surpassingly eloquent.

[004-263]In his speeches as reported, this does not appear. Hence, his reported

[004-264]speeches fail to do him justice, or the men of his time very much

[004-265]overrated his talents.

[004-266]Taking the latter horn of the dilemma we impeach the judgment and good

[004-267]sense of those who have gone before us. Assuming the former, we present an

[004-268]admitted and proclaimed fact. His contemporaries, while they conceded to

[004-269]him the highest attributes and accomplishments of eloquence, unite in

[004-270]affirming that his reported speeches come far short of the original.

[004-271]*Captain Horatio Jones*, a favorite interpreter, has frequently

[004-272]declared,—"*it is impossible to do Red Jacket justice*." The peculiar

[004-273]shade given to the idea, its beauty in its own native idiom, was often

[004-274]entirely lost in the transfer. In much the same way, Captain Jasper

[004-275]Parrish, of Canandaigua, has frequently been heard to speak, when

[004-276]referring to the forensic efforts of the orator.

[004-277]And besides, those passages that were most deeply fraught with eloquence,

[004-278]were often lost entirely, from the fact that the way having been prepared

[004-279]by a recital of those details that are reported, the reporter himself has

[004-280]been carried away by the very flood that surrounded, uplifted, and carried

[004-281]away the mass of those who heard him speak. So that the only note that

[004-282]would be made, of a passage of considerable length, is given in one or two

[004-283]short sentences. [Footnote: Conversation of the author with Col. Wm.

[004-284]Jones.]

[004-285]By the generality of the Iroquois, the terms of the treaty at Fort Stanwix

[004-286]were regarded as severe; and though the services of the renowned

[004-287]Cornplanter were engaged by the commissioners, in an effort to persuade

[004-288]the disaffected into a reconciliation with it, the attempt was but

[004-289]partially successful, and was made at the expense of his own high standing

[004-290]among his people. They were not easily reconciled, and were so much

[004-291]displeased with his conduct on this, and one or two subsequent occasions,

[004-292]that they even threatened his life. A circumstance he touchingly refers to

[004-293]in a speech addressed to General Washington.

[004-294]"Father," said he, "we will not conceal from you that the great God and

[004-295]not man, has preserved Cornplanter, from the hands of his own nation. For

[004-296]they ask continually—where is the land which our children, and their

[004-297]children after them are to lie down upon? When the Sun goes down he opens

[004-298]his heart before God, and earlier than the sun appears upon the hills, he

[004-299]gives thanks for his protection during the night; for he feels that among

[004-300]men become desperate by their danger, it is God only that can preserve

[004-301]him."
