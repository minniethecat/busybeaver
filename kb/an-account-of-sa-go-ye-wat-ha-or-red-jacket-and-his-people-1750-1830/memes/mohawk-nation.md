# Mohawk Nation

## Overview

The Mohawk Nation was the easternmost of the Six Nations of the Iroquois Confederacy, traditionally occupying the territory along the Mohawk River valley in present-day upstate New York. As the "Keepers of the Eastern Door," the Mohawks guarded the Confederacy's eastern frontier against incursions from the Hudson Valley and New England. The eastern door was guarded by the Mohawks, who resided at one end of the symbolic long house of the Confederacy. (002-027)

The Mohawks were the Iroquois nation most closely and consistently allied with the British Crown throughout the colonial period. This alliance, forged through the Covenant Chain and cemented by Sir William Johnson's personal influence, positioned the Mohawks as key intermediaries between the British and other Native nations. The Mohawk River valley, now one of the most valuable lands, was their ancestral home. (003-098)

## Structure and Governance

The Mohawk Nation held nine sachem titles in the Iroquois Grand Council and were one of the "Elder Brothers" in the Confederacy's deliberative structure. The Mohawks were organized into three principal clans: the Turtle, Bear, and Wolf. Like all Iroquois nations, Mohawk society was matrilineal, with clan mothers exercising the authority to nominate and depose chiefs. The information derived from the Mohawks was often crucial to understanding Iroquois history. (002-284)

Following the American Revolution, many Mohawks relocated to Canada under the leadership of Joseph Brant, establishing new communities on the Grand River in Ontario. Hubbard records that a Seneca was "sold to an Indian of the Mohawk tribe" and resided at the Mohawk settlement near the present site of Brantford. (013-164)

## Historical Role

The Mohawks played a central role in colonial New York history. Their alliance with the British was critically important during the French and Indian War, when Mohawk leaders like Hendrick fought alongside British forces. Sir William Johnson, the British Superintendent of Indian Affairs, was deeply integrated into Mohawk society — he was adopted by the Mohawks, married Molly Brant, and was said to have been made a war chief. (003-001)

During the American Revolution, the Mohawks under Joseph Brant fought almost exclusively on the British side. This loyalty led to the devastation of Mohawk villages and the eventual relocation of most Mohawks to Canada. Brant pressed the claims of "the loyal Mohawks upon the crown" for compensation after the war. (006-036) The Mohawks continued to maintain their British alliance, and during the War of 1812, the Mohawks and other Indians in Canada participated in the conflict. (016-176)

In Hubbard's narrative, the Mohawks serve as a point of comparison with the Seneca. Red Jacket's "relations to the proud Mohawk he would naturally view with suspicion," reflecting the complex dynamic between the two largest Iroquois nations. (004-185)
