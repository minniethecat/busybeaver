# Ogden Land Company

## Overview

The Ogden Land Company, also known as Ogden and Company, was a land speculation enterprise that pursued the acquisition of the remaining Seneca reservation lands in western New York during the early nineteenth century.


Led by Colonel Aaron Ogden, the company obtained pre-emptive rights to Seneca lands from the Holland Land Company and other prior claimants, then pressed the Seneca for decades to sell their remaining territories. Hubbard devotes an entire chapter to the company's efforts, chronicling the central role of Red Jacket in opposing their designs. (018-001)

## Structure and Governance

The Ogden Land Company operated by purchasing pre-emptive rights — the right of first purchase — to Seneca lands from the State of Massachusetts and from the Holland Land Company, which had previously acquired such rights.


Along with Governor Ogden, the company included agents like Morris S. Miller of Oneida and Captain Parrish of Canandaigua, who attended councils on its behalf and pressed the Seneca to sell. (018-012) These agents used a combination of monetary incentives, diplomatic pressure, and exploitation of internal Seneca divisions to advance the company's goals. (018-019)

## Historical Role

The Ogden Land Company played a central role in the dispossession of the Seneca from their remaining lands. The company's efforts began with the acquisition of pre-emptive rights and progressed through a series of councils in which company agents attempted to persuade the Seneca to sell.


Hubbard provides a detailed account of one such council, where Governor Ogden addressed the Seneca and Red Jacket delivered a powerful reply that unfolded a long array of arguments in opposition. (018-061)

Despite Red Jacket's eloquence, the Ogden Company was persistent. Hubbard notes that though defeated time after time, the company continued its efforts, finding new avenues of persuasion.


(018-348) Red Jacket warned his people against the company, declaring that "all the right that Ogden and his company have to your lands" was merely a right of pre-emption — that the Seneca were free to refuse. (018-317)


The company's ultimate success was facilitated by the trial and temporary deposition of Red Jacket in 1824, which weakened the traditionalist opposition to land sales. The company also exploited the pagan-Christian divide within the Seneca Nation. (018-238)

The Ogden Company's methods were persistent and, in the eyes of Red Jacket's faction, predatory:

> The Ogden Company pressed their claims with unwearied perseverance. Agents were constantly employed to negotiate with the Indians, and every art was used to overcome their reluctance to sell. The chiefs were plied with presents, and division was sown among them, so that the Christian and Pagan parties became more bitterly opposed than ever.
>
> (018-019)

The company's agents exploited Seneca debts and vulnerabilities:

> The Senecas were already in debt to the traders, and money was advanced to them by the Ogden Company on the security of their lands. Once in debt, it became increasingly difficult for them to resist the demands for a sale, and each new negotiation brought fresh pressures upon a people already worn down by years of conflict with the advancing white settlements.
>
> (018-062)
