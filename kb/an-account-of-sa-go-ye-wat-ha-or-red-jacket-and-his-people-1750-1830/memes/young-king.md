# Young King

## Biography

Young King was a Seneca chief whose influence rose as Red Jacket's waned. He emerged as the leader of the Christian party among the Senecas — the faction that embraced the missionaries Red Jacket had spent decades opposing. Hubbard records the moment of division: "There was a Christian, and a Pagan party. The former led by Young King, Captain Pollard, and others; the latter recognized Red Jacket as its head." (021-103)

Young King's leadership represented a generational and ideological shift within the Seneca Nation. Where Red Jacket opposed all missionary activity and land sales, Young King and the Christian party accepted — even welcomed — the missionaries' presence. They saw in Christianity and its associated "civilization" programs (schools, agriculture, literacy) a path toward Seneca survival in a world where military resistance was no longer possible.

The conflict between Young King and Red Jacket was personal as well as political. When the Christian party grew numerous enough to act, Young King led the effort to depose Red Jacket from his chieftainship — an unprecedented act that stunned the Seneca world. (021-157) Red Jacket's subsequent journey to Washington and his restoration did not heal the breach; Young King's Christian party remained a powerful force within the nation until the orator's death.

## Role and Influence

Young King's influence was institutional rather than oratorical. Unlike Red Jacket, whose power rested on his individual genius for speech, Young King built his following through the structures the missionaries had created: congregations, schools, and networks of Christian Seneca families. His authority was that of a community organizer — mobilizing the Christian population to assert political power within the nation's traditional institutions.

His role in deposing Red Jacket revealed both the strength and the limits of his position. The deposition demonstrated that the Christian party had the numbers to prevail in a council vote. But Red Jacket's successful appeal to Washington showed that the old orator still commanded respect beyond the Seneca nation — and that the final authority over Seneca chieftainships was increasingly contested between traditional councils and the federal government.

## Key Events

- **Rise of the Christian Party (1800s–1820s)**: Led the growing faction of Christian Seneca, building a power base through missionary institutions.
- **Deposition of Red Jacket (1827)**: Led the council that formally removed Red Jacket from his chieftaincy — a political earthquake in Seneca society. (021-157)
