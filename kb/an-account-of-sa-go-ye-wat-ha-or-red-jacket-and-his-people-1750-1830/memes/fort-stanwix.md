# Fort Stanwix

## Geography

Fort Stanwix was situated in the Mohawk Valley, at a strategic carrying place between the Mohawk River and Wood Creek — the crucial portage in the water route connecting the Hudson River to Lake Ontario. This location, at present-day Rome, New York, made the fort a vital military and commercial chokepoint throughout the colonial period. The fort commanded the principal east-west corridor through Iroquois territory, a fact that gave it outsized importance in the diplomacy of the Revolutionary War era.

## Historical Significance

In the fall of 1784, Fort Stanwix became the setting for a "general council of the Iroquois" — the first treaty between the newly independent United States and the Six Nations in the aftermath of the Revolutionary War. (004-046) The council was attended by Oliver Wolcott, Richard Butler, and Arthur Lee, who were appointed commissioners on the part of the United States. All tribes of the Iroquois were represented. (004-047)

For Red Jacket personally, Fort Stanwix was the threshold of his public career. Hubbard records that "The council at Fort Stanwix was the first occasion in which he appeared before the public." (004-082) He had recently been elected to the office of Sachem, and this council provided the stage on which his oratorical talents would first be displayed to representatives of the United States.

The significance of the moment was not lost on the Seneca. Hubbard describes the somber mood: "With an anxious heart the Indian left his home and wended his way, through his native forests, to the place where he was to meet in council, the chiefs of the thirteen fires." (004-083) The Revolutionary War had devastated Iroquois communities, and the treaty would determine the terms under which they would coexist with the victorious American republic.

## Key Events at This Location

The **Treaty of Fort Stanwix** (October 22, 1784) imposed harsh terms on the Iroquois. The Six Nations were compelled to acknowledge the United States' sovereignty and to cede significant tracts of land as reparation for their alliance with Britain during the war. Hostages were delivered to the Americans as guarantees of compliance. (004-285)

Red Jacket, though newly elevated to sachemship, took an active part in the deliberations. It was here, Hubbard speculates, that Red Jacket "projected the plan" that would define his lifelong political strategy — staunch opposition to land cessions and defense of Seneca sovereignty. (006-006)

The treaty generated lasting resentment among the Iroquois. Hubbard notes that many were "dissatisfied with the treaty at Fort Stanwix," and "the terms of the treaty at Fort Stanwix were the source of much trouble." (006-235) The goods distributed by the commissioners — amounting to three thousand dollars — did little to assuage the deeper grievance of coerced land cession. (006-116)

The commissioners' conduct at Fort Stanwix set a precedent for future negotiations, establishing a pattern of treaty-making that combined diplomatic formality with substantial pressure on Native nations to cede territory.
