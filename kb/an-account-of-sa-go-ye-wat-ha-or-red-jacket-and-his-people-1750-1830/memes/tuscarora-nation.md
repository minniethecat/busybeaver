# Tuscarora Nation

## Overview

The sixth and last nation to join the Iroquois Confederacy — the Tuscarora migrated from North Carolina in the early 18th century, were adopted into the Confederacy around 1722, and settled in western New York.

## Structure and Governance

Like all Iroquois nations, the Tuscarora Nation were organized into matrilineal clans that determined inheritance, political office, and social identity. Chiefs were selected by clan mothers and represented the nation at the Grand Council of the Iroquois Confederacy, where each nation held a specified number of hereditary sachemships.

## Historical Role

Hubbard records the Tuscarora origin story: their migration 'from the south' and their traditions. (002-002) The Confederacy became 'six, by the addition of the Tuscaroras.' (002-039) A Tuscarora village existed in the Genesee region. (011-199)

The nation's experience during and after the Revolutionary War reflected the broader history of the Iroquois Confederacy — the devastation of war, the loss of territory through successive treaties, and the challenge of maintaining cultural identity under the pressures of American expansion and missionary activity.
