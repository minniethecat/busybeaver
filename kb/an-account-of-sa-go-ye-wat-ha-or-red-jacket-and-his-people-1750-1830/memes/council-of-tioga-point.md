# Council at Tioga Point (1790)

## Background

Six years after the Treaty of Fort Stanwix, relations between the Iroquois and the United States remained tense. The retention of British posts created a parallel diplomatic channel that American officials sought to close. Timothy Pickering, who had been appointed to manage Indian affairs, convened a council at Tioga Point — a strategic river confluence on the Pennsylvania-New York border — to bring representatives of the Six Nations together.

## Proceedings

The council convened on November 16 and remained in session until November 23, 1790. (006-132) Hubbard describes elaborate "Indian ceremonies" at the opening of the council — rituals of condolence, the smoking of the peace pipe, and the exchange of wampum belts that marked formal Iroquois diplomatic proceedings. (006-003)

The chiefs who attended "took an active part" in the deliberations, with Red Jacket and Cornplanter among the prominent Seneca voices. (006-134) Brant (Thayendanegea) was also present, and the council brought together the leading figures who would shape Iroquois-American relations for the next two decades.

The discussions addressed the full range of issues dividing the parties: land disputes, the British posts, and the terms under which the Iroquois would maintain peaceful relations with the United States.

## Outcome and Aftermath

"Very soon after the council at Tioga Point," Hubbard notes, the political dynamics among the Iroquois shifted perceptibly. (006-230) Red Jacket's growing influence was acknowledged by both his own people and by American officials who recognized his oratorical skill and political acumen.

The council did not produce a formal treaty, but its diplomatic consequences were significant. The relationships established at Tioga Point enabled Pickering to return four years later and negotiate the more comprehensive Treaty of Canandaigua. (006-158) The council established a pattern: personal relationships between American commissioners and Iroquois leaders, built through face-to-face deliberation, were the essential prerequisite for formal treaty-making.

## Key Participants

- **Timothy Pickering** — US Commissioner, later negotiator of the Canandaigua Treaty
- **Red Jacket** — Seneca sachem, demonstrating growing diplomatic authority
- **Joseph Brant** — Mohawk leader, representing the British-allied faction
- **Cornplanter** — Seneca war chief, mediating between factions
