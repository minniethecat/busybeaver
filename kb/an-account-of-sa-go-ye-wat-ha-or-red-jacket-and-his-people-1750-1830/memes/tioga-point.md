# Tioga Point

## Geography

Tioga Point is located at the confluence of the Chemung River and the Susquehanna River in present-day northern Pennsylvania, near the New York border. This strategically positioned river junction made it a natural gathering place for Native nations traveling by water — accessible from the Iroquois heartland to the north via the Chemung and from the broader Susquehanna watershed to the south.

The location lay on the southern frontier of Iroquois territory, close to the boundary between Six Nations lands and the expanding settlements of Pennsylvania. Its position made it suitable for councils that brought together parties from both sides of that frontier.

## Historical Significance

The Council at Tioga Point, held in 1790, was one of the important diplomatic gatherings that followed the Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784). Colonel Timothy Pickering, who would later negotiate the Treaty of Canandaigua, met with representatives of the Iroquois nations to address ongoing disputes and to reaffirm the peaceful relationship established at Fort Stanwix. (006-131)

For Red Jacket, Tioga Point was a significant milestone in his ascent as a diplomat. Hubbard notes that "very soon after the council at Tioga Point," Red Jacket's influence among his people continued to grow. (006-230) The council reinforced a pattern: Red Jacket's participation in high-level diplomacy was becoming indispensable to the Seneca.

## Key Events at This Location

The council was marked by elaborate Indian ceremonies — rituals of condolence, the exchange of wampum belts, and formal speeches — all conducted according to the protocols of Iroquois diplomacy. (006-003) These ceremonies were not mere formalities; they served the essential political function of "wiping away tears" for those lost in the recent conflict and "clearing the path" for peaceful relations.

Colonel Pickering's conference with the Iroquois at Tioga Point laid groundwork for the more famous Treaty of Canandaigua four years later. (006-158) The relationships and trust built at Tioga Point shaped the diplomatic architecture that Pickering would later formalize at Canandaigua.

The council also saw Red Jacket and Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea) appearing together in a diplomatic setting. Brant, the Mohawk leader who had led Iroquois forces during the Revolutionary War, represented a more accommodating stance toward the Americans, while Red Jacket increasingly positioned himself as the defender of Seneca autonomy. The tension between these approaches would define Iroquois politics for decades.
