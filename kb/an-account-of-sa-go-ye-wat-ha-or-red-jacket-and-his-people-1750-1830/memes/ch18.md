[018-001]Pre-emptive right to the Indian Reservations sold to Ogden and Company—

[018-002]Council to obtain the lands—Mr. Ogden's speech—[[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s reply—

[018-003]Indians refuse to sell—Another Council called—Account of it by Hon.

[018-004]Albert Tracy—Various utterances of the orator on that occasion—Indians'

[018-005]appeal to the governments of the United States and New York—Noble

[018-006]response of Governor De Witt Clinton of New York—Final success of the

[018-007][[ogden-land-company|Ogden Company]].

[018-008]Though the Indian lands within the state of New York, had now been

[018-009]narrowed down to a comparatively small compass, there were not wanting

[018-010]those who would take from them, the remaining portion of their ancient

[018-011]inheritance. The preemptive right to their reservations was sold by the

[018-012][[holland-land-company|Holland Land Company]], to Colonel Aaron Ogden and others, who were known as

[018-013]the Ogden Company. The efforts of these gentlemen to induce the Indians to

[018-014]dispose of their reservations, resulted in calling several Indian

[018-015]councils, at which Red Jacket was the prominent speaker, and in which the

[018-016]entire force of his great powers was summoned, to withstand and thwart

[018-017]their endeavors.

[018-018]A council for this purpose was convened at their village near Buffalo,

[018-019]during the summer of 1819. The Hon. [[thomas-morris|Morris]] S. Miller of [[oneida-nation|Oneida]], was

[018-020]present as a commissioner on the part of the United States; and the Hon.

[018-021]Nathaniel Gorham of [[canandaigua|Canandaigua]], represented the interests of the state of

[018-022]Massachusetts. Captain Parrish of Canandaigua, and Captain Horatio Jones

[018-023]of [[genesee-river|Genesee]], were present as interpreters.

[018-024]As it was known Red Jacket was to speak in opposition to the interests of

[018-025]the Land Company, the occasion drew together a large concourse of people;

[018-026]pale faces as well as red, who were interested in the result of the

[018-027]negotiations contemplated, as also by a desire to hear the speech of the

[018-028]distinguished orator of the [[seneca-nation|Senecas]]. Of this Colonel Stone remarks: "No

[018-029]subsequent assemblage of the Indians within the state of New York, has

[018-030]presented so numerous and imposing an array, nor is it likely that so many

[018-031]of them will ever again meet, on the soil of their fathers."

[018-032]A gentleman who was present at this treaty by the invitation of a friend,

[018-033]speaks of it, in the following terms:—"My friend and myself having

[018-034]arrived on the ground at an early hour; we saw at a little distance from

[018-035]the wigwams, a group of Indians, under the shade of a cluster of plum

[018-036]trees, lying on the ground. Among these were a number of chiefs, of whom

[018-037]in a conspicuous place, was Red Jacket, apparently in deep thought, with a

[018-038]pile of little small sticks, two or three inches long, before him.

[018-039]"I inquired of a gentleman who was conversant with Indian proceedings,

[018-040]what Red Jacket was doing? He replied that he was studying his speech, and

[018-041]advised us to retire, as he perceived it disturbed him. [[About]] this time

[018-042]the commissioners, Governor Ogden, his friends, and the two celebrated

[018-043]Indian interpreters, Parrish and Jones, and a large concourse of people,

[018-044]gentlemen and ladies, began to assemble under another cluster of trees,

[018-045]where benches had been prepared in two parallel lines, with a wide space

[018-046]between, and seats across the upper end, for the commissioners. The long

[018-047]seats were occupied on the right by Messrs. Ogden, their officers, and

[018-048]other gentlemen and ladies; the left by Red Jacket, a large number of

[018-049]chiefs, and other Indians.

[018-050]"There was order, dignity, and perfect silence. The contest soon

[018-051]commenced. Governor Ogden, a dignified, fine looking man, rose and opened

[018-052]the case. Mr. Parrish, a man of large stature, stood up at the same time,

[018-053]and interpreted it to the Indians, sentence by sentence." [Footnote:

[018-054]Author's Scrap Book.]

[018-055]The object was to buy the Indian title, as they had already brought the

[018-056]pre-emptive right. Governor Ogden told them it was the wish of their great

[018-057]Father, the President of the United States, that they should sell these

[018-058]lands, and go down to a reservation on the Allegany river, where they

[018-059]could live in peace, and have a good foothold forever; and used various

[018-060]arguments in favor of such a course.

[018-061]After Governor Ogden had finished his speech, Red Jacket rose with a great

[018-062]deal of composure, and adjusting his belt of handsome wampum, and looking

[018-063]to the sky for a moment spoke. Mr. Parrish interpreted: "Red Jacket says

[018-064]he thanks the [[great-spirit|Great Spirit]] that we are all alive and here this pleasant

[018-065]day." He then addressed the commissioners, answering all the statements

[018-066]and arguments of Governor Ogden in their order, unfolding a long roll of

[018-067]parchments attached together, of treaties that had been made at different

[018-068]times by the United States, with the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]]. They had been preserved

[018-069]in good order. He pointed to the dates, and to the substance of the

[018-070]treaties from time to time, with great accuracy, as appeared from the

[018-071]interpretation, answering Governor Ogden with the most forcible arguments,

[018-072]interspersed with wit and humor. His speech on this occasion, as quoted by

[018-073]Col. Stone, is as follows:

[018-074]"Brother: We understand that you have been appointed by our great Father,

[018-075]the President, to make these communications to us. We thank the Great

[018-076]Spirit for this pleasant day given us for our reply, and we beg you to

[018-077]listen.

[018-078]"BROTHER: Previous to your arrival at this [[council-fire|council fire]], we were told that

[018-079]our great Father had appointed a commissioner to meet us. You have

[018-080]produced your commission, and it has been read and explained to us. You

[018-081]have also explained the object of your mission, and the wishes of the

[018-082]President in sending you to the council fire of the Six Nations. We do not

[018-083]doubt that the sealed document you produced, contained the words of the

[018-084]President, our great Father. When first informed of your appointment, we

[018-085]supposed that you were coming to meet us on a very different subject.

[018-086]Since the war of the Revolution, we have held various councils with our

[018-087]white brothers, and in this same manner. We have made various speeches,

[018-088]and entered into several treaties, and these things are well known to our

[018-089]great Father; they are lodged with him. We, too, perfectly understand them

[018-090]all. The same interpreters were then present as now. In consequence of

[018-091]what took place during the late war, we made it known to our great Father,

[018-092]through our interpreter, that we wished to have a talk. Our application

[018-093]was not complied with. We sent a messenger to brighten the chain of

[018-094]friendship with our great Father, but he would not meet around the council

[018-095]fire, and we were disappointed. We had supposed that the commissioner he

[018-096]has now sent, came forward to brighten the chain of friendship, to renew

[018-097]former engagements. When we made a treaty at Canandaigua with Colonel

[018-098][[timothy-pickering|Pickering]] in 1794, we were told, and thought that it was to be permanent,

[018-099]and to be lasting, between us and the United States forever. After several

[018-100]treaties had been entered into under our great Father, [[george-washington|General Washington]],

[018-101]large delegations from the Six Nations were invited to meet him. We went

[018-102]and met him in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]. We kindled a council fire. A treaty was then

[018-103]made, and General Washington then declared that it should be permanent

[018-104]between the red and white brothers; that it should be spread out on the

[018-105]largest and strongest rocks, that nothing could undermine or break; that

[018-106]it should be exposed to the view of all.

[018-107]"BROTHER: We shall now see what has been done by the United States. After

[018-108]this treaty had been formed I then said that I did not doubt, but that the

[018-109]United States would faithfully perform their engagements. But I told our

[018-110]white brothers at that time, that I feared eventually they would wish to

[018-111]disturb those contracts. You white brothers have the faculty to burst the

[018-112]stoutest rocks. On our part we would not have disturbed those treaties.

[018-113]Shortly after our interview with our great Father, General Washington, at

[018-114]Philadelphia, a treaty was made at Canandaigua, by which we widened our

[018-115]former engagements with our white brothers, and made some new ones. The

[018-116]commissioner, Colonel Pickering, then told us that this treaty should be

[018-117]binding and should last, without alteration for two lives. We wished to

[018-118]make it extend much farther, and the Six Nations then wished to establish

[018-119]a lasting chain of friendship. On our part, we wished the treaty to last

[018-120]as long as trees grow, and waters run. Our Brother told us that he would

[018-121]agree to it.

[018-122]"BROTHER: I have reminded you of what had taken place between our

[018-123]confederates, the Six Nations, and our white brothers, down to the treaty

[018-124]of Canandaigua. At the close of that treaty it was agreed, it being as

[018-125]strong and binding, as by my former comparisons I have explained, that if

[018-126]any difficulty should occur, if any monster should cross the chain of

[018-127]friendship, that we would unite to remove those difficulties, to drive

[018-128]away the monster; that we would go hand in hand and prolong the chain. So

[018-129]it was agreed.

[018-130]"BROTHER: Many years ago we discovered a cloud rising that darkened the

[018-131]prospect of our peace and happiness. We heard eventful things from

[018-132]different quarters, from different persons, and at different times, and

[018-133]foresaw that the period was not very distant, when this threatening cloud

[018-134]would burst upon us.

[018-135]"BROTHER: During the late war we intended to take no part. Yet residing

[018-136]within the limits of the United States, and with the advice of General

[018-137]Porter, we agreed around our council fire, that it was right, and we took

[018-138]a part. We thought it would help to promote our friendship with our white

[018-139]brothers, to aid the arms of the United States, and to make our present

[018-140]seats still stronger. These were our reasons. What were the results? We

[018-141]lost many of our warriors. We spilt our blood in a cause between you, and

[018-142]a people not of our color.

[018-143]"BROTHER: These things may be new to you, but they are not new to your

[018-144]government. Records of these things are with our great Father, the

[018-145]President. You have come, therefore, for a very different purpose from the

[018-146]one we expected. You come to tell us of our situation, of our

[018-147]reservations, of the opinion of the President that we must change our old

[018-148]customs for new ones; that we must concentrate in order to enjoy the fair

[018-149]means you offer of civilization, and improvement in the arts of

[018-150]agriculture.

[018-151]"BROTHER: At the treaty of Canandaigua, we were promised that different

[018-152]kinds of mechanics, blacksmiths, and carpenters, should be sent among us;

[018-153]and farmers with their families, that our women might learn to spin. We

[018-154]agreed to receive them. We even applied for these benefits. We were told

[018-155]that our children were too young to be taught. Neither farmers or

[018-156]mechanics were sent.

[018-157]"BROTHER: We had thought that the promises made by one President, were

[018-158]handed down to the next. We do not change our chiefs as you do. Since

[018-159]these treaties were made, you have had several Presidents. We do not

[018-160]understand why the treaty made by one, is not binding on the other. On our

[018-161]part we expect to comply with our engagements.

[018-162]"BROTHER: You told us when the country was surrounded by whites, and in

[018-163]possession of Indians, that it was unproductive, not being liable to

[018-164]taxes, nor to make roads nor improvements, it was time to change. As for

[018-165]the taxing of Indians, this is extra-ordinary; and was never heard of,

[018-166]since the settlement of America. The land is ours, by the gift of the

[018-167]Great Spirit. How can you tax it? We can make such roads as we want, and

[018-168]did so when the land was all ours. We are improving our condition. See

[018-169]these large stocks of cattle, and those fences. We are surrounded by the

[018-170]whites, from whom we can procure cattle, and whatever is necessary for our

[018-171]improvement. Now that we are confined to narrow limits, we can easily make

[018-172]our roads, and improve our lands.

<!-- Source: 2386145434587187443_7339-5.txt.html -->

[018-173]"Look back to the first settlement by the whites, and then look at our

[018-174]present condition. Formerly we continued to grow in numbers, and in

[018-175]strength. What has become of the Indians, who extended to the salt water?

[018-176]They have been driven back and become few, while you have been growing

[018-177]numerous, and powerful. This lands is ours, from the God of Heaven. It was

[018-178]given to us. We cannot make land. Driven back and reduced as we are, you

[018-179]wish to cramp us more and more. You tell us of a pre-emptive right. Such

[018-180]men you say own one reservation, and such another. But they are all ours,

[018-181]ours from the top to the bottom. If Mr. Ogden had come from heaven, with

[018-182]flesh on his bones, as I we now see him, and said that the Heavenly Father

[018-183]had given him a title, we might then believe him.

[018-184]"BROTHER: You say that the President has sent us word that it is for our

[018-185]interest to dispose of our lands. You tell us that there is a good tract

[018-186]of land at Allegany. This too is very extraordinary. Our feet have covered

[018-187]every inch of that reservation. A communication like this has never been

[018-188]made to us, at any of our councils. The President must have been

[018-189]disordered in mind, when he offered to lead us off by the arms, to the

[018-190]Allegany reservation. I have told you of the treaty we made with the

[018-191]United States. Here is the belt of wampum, that confirmed that treaty.

[018-192]Here too is the parchment. You know its contents. I will not open it. Now

[018-193]the tree of friendship is decaying; its limbs are fast falling off. You

[018-194]are at fault.

[018-195]"Formerly we called the British brothers. Now we call the President, our

[018-196]Father. Probably among you, are persons with families of children. We

[018-197]consider ourselves the children of the President. What would be your

[018-198]feelings, were you told that your children were to be cast upon a naked

[018-199]rock, there to protect themselves? The different claims you tell us of, on

[018-200]our lands, I cannot understand. We are placed here by the Great Spirit,

[018-201]for purposes known to him. You have no right to interfere. You told us

[018-202]that we had large and unproductive tracts of land. We do not view it so.

[018-203]Our seats, we consider small; and if we are left here long, by the Great

[018-204]Spirit, we shall stand in need of them. We shall be in want of timber.

[018-205]Land after many years' use wears out; our fields must be renewed, and new

[018-206]ones improved, so that we have no more land in our reservations than we

[018-207]want. Look at the white people around us, and back. You are not cramped

[018-208]for lands. They are large. Look at that man. [Footnote: Mr. Ellicott,

[018-209]agent of the Holland Land Company.] If you want to buy, apply to him. He

[018-210]has lands enough to sell. We have none to part with. You laugh, but do not

[018-211]think I trifle. I am sincere. Do not think we are hasty in making up our

[018-212]minds. We have had many councils, and thought for a long time upon this

[018-213]subject. We will not part with any, not with one of our reservations.

[018-214]"We recollect that Mr. Ogden addressed his speech to you, therefore I have

[018-215]spoken to you. Now I will speak to Mr. Ogden.

[018-216]"BROTHER: You recollect when you first came to this ground, that you told

[018-217]us you had bought the pre-emptive right. A right to purchase given you by

[018-218]the government. Remember my reply. I told you, you had been unfortunate in

[018-219]buying. You said you would not disturb us. I then told you as long as I

[018-220]lived, you must not come forward to explain that right. You have come. See

[018-221]me before you. You have heard our reply to the commissioner sent by the

[018-222]President. I again repeat that, one and all, chiefs and warriors, we are

[018-223]of the same mind. We will not part with any of our reservations. Do not

[018-224]make your application anew, nor in any other shape. Let us hear no more of

[018-225]it. Let us part as we met, in friendship."

[018-226]Col. Stone refers to the kindness of Major Joseph Delafield, for the

[018-227]speeches made at this council, as given in his work, and the most

[018-228]important of which is presented here; they were taken down at the time

[018-229]from the lips of the interpreter, who stated that "he could not translate

[018-230]some of Red Jacket's figurative flights, they were too wild and difficult

[018-231]to be rendered in English, and he did not attempt it." Much doubtless that

[018-232]served to give point and zest to his speech, was either omitted, or lost

[018-233]its force, in being transferred to our language. The writer of the sketch

[018-234]previously alluded to, among several points in this speech which were

[018-235]impressed on his memory, mentions one not found in the above. "The

[018-236]gentleman says, that our great Father says, we can go Allegany, and have a

[018-237]good foothold forever; *yes, a good foothold, for it is all rock*."

[018-238]Though the efforts of the Ogden Company to obtain the consent of the

[018-239]Indians to sell their remaining lands, were at this time unsuccessful,

[018-240]they were nevertheless repeated. The demand of Red Jacket, "do not make

[018-241]your application anew, nor in any other shape," was unheeded.

[018-242]Col. Stone, on the authority of the Hon. Albert Tracy, mentions a treaty

[018-243]held for this same purpose in 1822 or 1823, in which Red Jacket replied to

[018-244]a speech made by the commissioner, and also by Governor Ogden, entering,

[018-245]as in the preceding speech, upon a regular and connected history of the

[018-246]transactions of the Indians with the whites, up to that time, and in the

[018-247]course of his speech, used the language very happily alluded to by Mr.

[018-248]Bryant, in his memorial address.

[018-249]At the close of the speech that has been quoted almost entire, some of his

[018-250]people desired him to apologize for one or two utterances he had made,

[018-251]regarding them as rude, and adapted to awaken unpleasant reflections. He

[018-252]refused, saying, "NO, IT HAS GONE FORTH, LET IT STAND." A circumstance

[018-253]doubtless alluded to, in the words which immediately follow: "Often the

[018-254]fierceness of his temper, the righteous indignation that swelled his

[018-255]bosom, impelled him to hurl defiance at his foes, and to use language, the

[018-256]possible consequences of which, caused the more timid and abject of his

[018-257]followers, to tremble with apprehension. But Red Jacket would retract not

[018-258]a single word, although a majority of the chiefs, would sometimes secretly

[018-259]deprecate the severity of his utterances."

[018-260]"Again on other occasions, sorely beset and almost despairing, he would

[018-261]essay to melt the hearts of the pitiless pursuers of his people, and give

[018-262]utterance to such touching words as these:

[018-263]"We first knew you a feeble plant, which wanted a little earth whereon to

[018-264]grow. We gave it to you, and afterward, when we could have trod you under

[018-265]our feet, we watered and protected you; and now you have grown to be a

[018-266]mighty tree, whose top reaches the clouds, and whose branches overspread

[018-267]the whole land; whilst we, who were then the tall pine of the forest, have

[018-268]become the feeble plant and need your protection."

[018-269]"Again assuming the pleading tones of a supplicant, he said, 'when you

[018-270]first came here, you clung around our knee, and called us FATHER. We took

[018-271]you by the hand and called you BROTHERS. You have grown greater than we,

[018-272]so that we can no longer reach up to your hand. But we wish to cling

[018-273]around your knee, and be called YOUR CHILDREN.'"

[018-274]In this same speech, referring to their services during the late war with

[018-275]England, he said:

[018-276]"Not long ago you raised the war-club against him, who was once our great

[018-277]Father over the waters. You asked us to go with you to the war. It was not

[018-278]our quarrel. We knew not that you were right. We asked not; we cared not;

[018-279]it was enough for us, that you were our brothers. We went with you to the

[018-280]battle. We fought and bled for you; and now," his eye kindling with

[018-281]emotion, and the deepest feeling indicated in his utterance, as he pointed

[018-282]to some Indians present, that had been wounded in that contest; "and now,

[018-283]dare you pretend to us, that our Father the President, while he sees our

[018-284]blood running, yet fresh from the wounds received, while fighting his

[018-285]battles, has sent you with a message to persuade us to relinquish the poor

[018-286]remains of our once boundless possessions; to sell the birth place of our

[018-287]children, and the graves of our fathers! No! Sooner than believe that he

[018-288]gave you this message, we will believe that *you have stolen your

[018-289]commission, and are a cheat and a liar*."

[018-290]Once more, speaking of the pre-emptive right and the assurance given them

[018-291]that their lands were desired only in return for a fair equivalent of

[018-292]their value, he called their attention to the great cessions the Indians

[018-293]had already made, together with the solemn declarations that they should

[018-294]not be importuned to relinquish their remaining reservations, he said:

[018-295]"You tell us of your claim to our land, and that you have purchased it

[018-296]from your State. We know nothing of your claim, and we care nothing for

[018-297]it. Even the whites have a law by which they cannot sell what they do not

[018-298]own. How then has your State, which never owned our land, sold it to you?

[018-299]We have a title to it, and we know that our title is good; for it came

[018-300]direct from the Great Spirit, who gave it to us, his red children. When

[018-301]you can ascend where he is," pointing toward the skies, "and will get his

[018-302]deed, and show it to us, then, and never till then, will we acknowledge

[018-303]your title. You say you came not to cheat us of our lands, but to buy

[018-304]them. Who told you that we have lands to sell? You never heard it from

[018-305]us."

[018-306]Then rising up and giving Mr. Ogden a look of deep earnestness, if not of

[018-307]indignation, he said:

[018-308]"Did I not tell you the last time we met, that whilst Red Jacket lived,

[018-309]you would get no more lands of the Indians? How then, while you see him

[018-310]alive and strong," striking his hand violently on his breast, "*do you

[018-311]think to make him a liar?*"

[018-312]The persistence with which the Senecas were importuned to sell their

[018-313]lands, led them to make an appeal to the president, and afterward to the

[018-314]governor of New York.

[018-315]The latter, Governor De Witt Clinton, sent them a reply worthy of his name

[018-316]and office. It is as follows:

[018-317]"All the right that Ogden and his company have to your reservations, is

[018-318]the right of purchasing them when you think it expedient to sell them,

[018-319]that is, they can buy your lands, but no other person can. You may retain

[018-320]them as long as you please, and you may sell them to Ogden as soon as you

[018-321]please. You are the owners of these lands in the same way that your

[018-322]brethren the Oneidas, are of their reservations. They are all that is left

[018-323]of what the Great Spirit gave to your ancestors. No man shall deprive you

[018-324]of them without your consent. The State will protect you in the full

[018-325]enjoyment of your property. We are strong and willing to shield you from

[018-326]oppression. The Great Spirit looks down on the conduct of mankind, and

[018-327]will punish us if we permit the remnant of the Indian nations which is

[018-328]with us to be injured. We feel for you, brethren; we shall watch over your

[018-329]interests. We know that in a future world we shall be called upon to

[018-330]answer for our conduct to our fellow creatures."

[018-331]Col. Stone refers to the Hon. Albert H. Tracy, as having furnished the

[018-332]notes of the council we have just been considering. The same authority

[018-333]speaking of the [[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]] of Red Jacket, says: "It is evident that the

[018-334]best translations of Indian speeches, must fail to express the beauty and

[018-335]sublimity of the originals; especially of such an original as Red Jacket.

[018-336]It has been my good fortune to hear him a few times, but only of late

[018-337]years, and when his powers were enfeebled by age, and still more, by

[018-338]intemperance. But I shall never forget the impression made on me, the

[018-339]first time I saw him in council:

[018-340]"Deep on his front engraven,

[018-341]"Deliberation sate, and public care,

[018-342]"And princely counsel in his face yet shone,

[018-343]"Majestic, though in ruin.

[018-344]"I can give no idea of the strong impression it made on my mind, though

[018-345]conveyed to it through the medium of an illiterate interpreter, Even in

[018-346]this mangled form, I saw the *disjecta membra* of a regular and

[018-347]splendid oration." [Footnote: Col. Stone's Life and Times of Red Jacket.]

[018-348]The Ogden Company though defeated time and again by the watchfulness, and

[018-349]powerful influence of Red Jacket, continued to ply their endeavors, until

[018-350]by degrees, the remaining portion of their once proud inheritance, was

[018-351]wrested from them, and the orator was left in the decline of life to

[018-352]survey, as he often did in a spirit of dejection, the haunts of his youth,

[018-353]which had nearly all passed into other hands, through the craft and

[018-354]avarice of the white man.
