# Josiah Harmar

## Biography

Josiah Harmar was a veteran of the Revolution who commanded the first federal expedition sent against the north-western tribes. (007-018) His force numbered fourteen hundred and fifty men, three hundred and twenty of them from the regular army, and the balance recruits from Kentucky and Pennsylvania. (007-019)

With this army he advanced toward the Indian country, leaving Fort Washington, the present site of Cincinnati, on the 30th of September, 1790. (007-022)

The account introduces the campaign under a chapter heading that fixes its outcome in advance: "Expedition under Gen. Harmar—Its failure—High hopes of the Indians." (007-001)

## Role and Influence

The Indians kept watch of Harmar's movements, and before his coming they burned their principal village and retired. (007-024) Seizing a favorable opportunity, they fell suddenly upon a detachment of the main army commanded by Colonel Harding, consisting of two hundred and ten men, thirty of whom were regulars. (007-026)

At the first onset the militia, the main part of the force, fled; the regulars stood their ground bravely for a time, but at a fearful odds. (007-030) The defeat carried consequences far beyond the field:

> "President Washington had been deeply pained by the disasters of General Harmar's expedition to the Wabash, resulting from Indian ambushes." (008-013)

A new expedition was therefore projected, with [[arthur-st-clair|General St. Clair]] appointed commander-in-chief of the forces to be employed. (008-010)

## Key Events

- **Harmar's expedition (1790)**: The campaign, marching from Fort Washington on 30 September 1790, ended in the failure the account announces in its own chapter heading. (007-022)

- **Defeat of Harding's detachment**: A detachment of two hundred and ten men under Colonel Harding was surprised; the militia fled and the regulars were overwhelmed at fearful odds. (007-027)

- **St. Clair's expedition follows**: Pained by the disaster, [[george-washington|Washington]] saw a new campaign projected under [[arthur-st-clair|St. Clair]], which would end in [[st-clairs-defeat|a still greater disaster]] in 1791. (008-011)

- **Treaty at Fort Harmar (1789)**: A treaty was held with the Six Nations at Fort Harmar, on the Muskingum, (006-113) where the terms of [[treaty-of-fort-stanwix|Fort Stanwix]] were confirmed in 1789. (006-241)
