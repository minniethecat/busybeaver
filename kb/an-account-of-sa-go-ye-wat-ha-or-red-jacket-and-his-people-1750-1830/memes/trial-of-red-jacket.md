# Trial of Red Jacket (1824)

## Background

The arrival of Christian missionaries among the Seneca in the early 19th century created deep divisions within the community. Red Jacket, the foremost defender of traditional Seneca religion, led the opposition to missionary activity, arguing that the Seneca should maintain the faith of their ancestors. His position was articulated in a famous speech to a missionary: "Brother, we do not wish to destroy your religion, or take it from you. We only want to enjoy our own." (010-149)

However, a faction of Christian Seneca — led by younger chiefs who had converted — increasingly viewed Red Jacket as an obstacle to progress. The conflict escalated over two decades until it reached a breaking point in the 1820s.

## Proceedings

The trial was conducted as a Seneca council, with the Christian faction bringing charges against Red Jacket. The specific accusations centered on his opposition to Christianity and his alleged obstruction of Seneca advancement. Hubbard describes the proceedings at length, capturing the drama of the Seneca's greatest orator being called to defend himself before his own people.

Red Jacket mounted a vigorous defense, but the council was stacked against him. The Christian faction, supported by missionary allies and some American officials, secured a majority. Red Jacket was formally deposed from his chieftainship — a stunning reversal for the man who had dominated Seneca politics for four decades.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The deposition proved short-lived. Hubbard records that Red Jacket embarked on "a journey to Washington — his restoration" (P04-101) to appeal his case. In the capital, federal officials — who recognized Red Jacket's value as a diplomatic interlocutor — interceded on his behalf.

More importantly, the Seneca people themselves reversed the decision. Red Jacket's return to Washington, and the support he received there, shifted the political balance. The traditionalist faction rallied, and Red Jacket was restored to his chieftainship. The episode demonstrated both the depth of the religious divisions among the Seneca and the enduring authority Red Jacket commanded among his people.

The trial marked a turning point. Though Red Jacket was restored, the religious controversy had permanently altered the political landscape of the Seneca nation. The Christian faction continued to grow, and the traditional practices that Red Jacket defended would progressively decline in the decades following his death.

## Key Participants

- **Red Jacket** — Defendant, deposed and later restored
- **Young King** — Leader of the Christian Seneca faction
- **Christian missionaries** — Background figures supporting the accusers
- **Federal officials in Washington** — Interceded to support Red Jacket's restoration
