# Fort Oswego

## Geography

Fort Oswego occupied a strategic position at the mouth of the Oswego River on the southeastern shore of Lake Ontario in present-day New York. The location commanded one of the principal water routes from the Great Lakes into the interior of Iroquois territory — the Oswego River provided access southward toward the Finger Lakes region, making the fort a gateway between the Great Lakes and the Iroquois heartland.

The Iroquois traced their origin, according to Hubbard, "to a point near Oswego Falls" (002-115), underscoring the deep cultural and mythological significance of this region to the Six Nations.

## Historical Significance

After the Revolutionary War, Fort Oswego was one of the five British posts — along with Oswegatchie, Niagara, Detroit, and Mackinaw — that Britain retained in violation of the Treaty of Paris (1783). (006-084) These posts served as tangible evidence that Britain had not truly accepted American sovereignty over the Great Lakes frontier.

For the Iroquois, particularly the Seneca and Onondaga, the continued British presence at Oswego provided diplomatic leverage and a fallback option in their negotiations with the United States. British officers at these posts distributed gifts, provisions, and ammunition to visiting Iroquois delegations, sustaining the military alliance that had characterized the Revolutionary War period.

Sir John Johnson, the British superintendent of Indian affairs, convened a "general council of the Iroquois at Oswego" during the war (003-177), reinforcing the settlement's role as a center of British-Iroquois coordination. The post remained a node in Britain's network of influence until its evacuation under the Jay Treaty in 1796.

## Key Events at This Location

The **British council at Oswego** during the Revolutionary War was a critical moment in Iroquois history. Sir John Johnson and other British agents used the location to rally Iroquois support for the Crown, urging the Six Nations to take up arms against the American rebels. This council helped cement the Iroquois-British alliance that would have devastating consequences for the Confederacy when the war ended in American victory. (003-177)

The eventual British evacuation of Oswego under the Jay Treaty removed a key pillar of British influence on Lake Ontario. With the Union Jack lowered at Oswego, the Iroquois lost their nearest British ally on the lake, accelerating their transition to exclusive diplomatic engagement with the United States.
