[021-001]Views at the close of life—Incident—His life work—Unfavorable

[021-002]influences—Advance of the [[pagan-christian-divide|Christian party]]—Conversion of [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s

[021-003]wife—He leaves her—His return—Red Jacket deposed—Journey to [[george-washington|Washington]]

[021-004]—His restoration—Rapid decline—Regards his end as near—Talks with his

[021-005]people—Endeavors to unite them.

[021-006]With the views entertained by Red Jacket, the objects that met him on

[021-007]every side, as he drew near the close of life, were far from pleasant.

[021-008]Yonder hillside, exposed to the gaze of the world, its huge rocks laid

[021-009]bare; those fields, stretching further than eye could reach, bounded not

[021-010]by woodland, lake, or river, but by the white man's fence; ten thousand

[021-011]dwellings, smiling with the abundance and thrift of the husbandman, city

[021-012]and village, bustling with tumult, and the noise of busy hammers, and

[021-013]rattling wheels, and roaring engines; all of these however gratifying to

[021-014]the white man, as marks of improvement, afforded him no pleasure. He saw

[021-015]in them the sepulcher of his people's pride and glory.

[021-016]The hillside opened to the sunlight, for the innocent lamb to sport upon,

[021-017]or to make the stable ox a home, he would have loved better, as when

[021-018]sheltered once by the sturdy oak or stately pine, its rocks jutting out

[021-019]from behind the ivy, and its bosom threaded by the path of the deer. The

[021-020]fields might have appeared inviting and green, but the white man's barrier

[021-021]would have warned him away, the road he would have looked upon as a

[021-022]prisoned path, and he would have taken to the woods, as a place more

[021-023]congenial to his spirit.

[021-024]It is said of him "that in the days of his youth he was wont to join the

[021-025]hunters in the beautiful valley of the [[genesee-river|Genesee]], with great enthusiasm.

[021-026]Game was then plenty, and they were the finest hunting grounds, he could

[021-027]traverse. Toward the close of his life he went thither to indulge once

[021-028]more, in the pleasures of the chase, where a forest apparently of

[021-029]considerable extent, yet remained. He entered it, recognizing some of his

[021-030]ancient friends among the more venerable of the trees, and hoping yet to

[021-031]find abundant game. But he had not proceeded far before he approached an

[021-032]opening; and his course was presently impeded by a fence, within the

[021-033]enclosure of which, one of the pale faces was guiding the plow. With a

[021-034]heavy heart he turned in another direction, the forest seeming yet to be

[021-035]deep, and where he hoped to find a deer, as in the days when he was young.

[021-036]But he had not traveled long, before another opening broke upon his view,

[021-037]another fence impeded his course, and another cultivated field appeared

[021-038]within. He sat down and wept." [Footnote: Circumstances related to Col.

[021-039]Stone by a [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] chief.]

[021-040]It has been well observed: "The whole life of the Seneca chief was spent

[021-041]in vain endeavors to preserve the independence of his tribe, and in active

[021-042]opposition as well to the plans of civilization proposed by the

[021-043]benevolent, as to the attempts at encroachment on the part of the

[021-044]mercenary…. He yielded nothing to persuasion, to bribery, or to menace,

[021-045]and never to his last hour remitted his exertions, in what he regarded the

[021-046]noblest purpose of his life." [Footnote: McKenney's Biography.]

[021-047]But at the close of life, Red Jacket began to realize more than ever the

[021-048]power of those forces bearing down upon him, to resist which he had

[021-049]summoned all the energies he could command. His people, notwithstanding

[021-050]his efforts, were constantly brought by the encroachments of the whites,

[021-051]into a narrower compass, and the religion and customs of the whites

[021-052]continued to gain ground, and threatened to supercede the time honored

[021-053]usages of his fathers.

[021-054]Intoxicating drinks also, the bane of the Indian race, wrought sad havoc

[021-055]among his people, and had well nigh ruined himself. His influence was thus

[021-056]effectually crippled, and his opposition to Christianity, and the efforts

[021-057]of the whites to obtain their land, carried much less weight, than at an

[021-058]earlier period of his life. He saw and felt this, and in view of it, was

[021-059]much cast down.

[021-060]His opposition to Christianity, is said to have been much encouraged by

[021-061]wicked and designing men among the whites, who feared that the presence of

[021-062]missionaries among the Indians, would interfere with their unworthy and

[021-063]base designs.

[021-064]But his decision when formed, as already intimated, was consistently and

[021-065]perseveringly maintained. He narrowly watched every proceeding, gathered

[021-066]around him such as would be controlled by his influence, or example, and

[021-067]inculcated in them those sentiments of steadfastness, in the religion of

[021-068]their fathers, so strikingly manifested in his own conduct.

[021-069]After various discouragements and reverses, the missionary was at length

[021-070]established among his people, and the adherents of Red Jacket, which at

[021-071]first were the most numerous, by degrees diminished, until finally those

[021-072]friendly to Christianity, outnumbered the others. Red Jacket's people one

[021-073]by one, became interested in the religion the missionary had come to

[021-074]teach. The schools established began to be well attended, several chiefs

[021-075]embraced the new religion; some of them were men of influence and carried

[021-076]with them many others. Finally in 1826, Red Jacket's wife became

[021-077]interested on the subject of religion, attended the meetings of the

[021-078]Christians, was led to abandon the pagan worship, she formerly attended,

[021-079]altogether, and giving evidences of piety, proposed to unite with the

[021-080]mission church, under the care of the Rev. Mr. Harris.

[021-081]Before uniting, she laid the subject before Red Jacket and desired his

[021-082]consent. This he utterly refused, and threatened in case she did so, to

[021-083]leave her and never visit her again. Her trial in view of this refusal,

[021-084]she referred to Mr. Harris, who kindly endeavored to show her what the law

[021-085]of Christianity demanded, that it required her to obey God, rather than

[021-086]man; that though her course might subject her to trial, she had the

[021-087]promise of the grace of Christ to help her, and that in the end it might

[021-088]promote her good. Still he committed the matter wholly to her own

[021-089]conscience, advising her to pursue the course that might thus be

[021-090]indicated, and leave the event in the hands of God.

[021-091]After deliberating for a time she united with the church, and Red Jacket,

[021-092]true to his threat, left her and went to another reservation. She bore his

[021-093]displeasure with a meek and Christian spirit, remained at home with her

[021-094]family, and conducted discreetly, pursuing as before the duties of her

[021-095]household.

[021-096]Red Jacket after a few months' absence returned, desiring to be welcomed

[021-097]again by his wife, who received him on condition of his not interfering

[021-098]with her, in her religious views, or attendance on the meetings of the

[021-099]mission. To this he gave his assent, and was ever afterward faithful in

[021-100]observing his pledge; not opposing, but aiding her in performing,

[021-101]according to her desire, her religious obligations.

[021-102]A division was now apparent among the Senecas, in regard to religion.

[021-103]There was a Christian, and a Pagan party. The former led by [[young-king|Young King]],

[021-104][[captain-pollard|Captain Pollard]], and others; the latter recognized Red Jacket as its

[021-105]ruling spirit.

[021-106]The opposition he had so long exerted, began to be regarded with

[021-107]impatience. As the Christian party advanced and became more numerous, they

[021-108]were unwilling to submit to the dictation of the orator. They began to

[021-109]feel that in his opposition to the education and improvement of his

[021-110]people, he was acting the part of an enemy, and not a friend.

[021-111]His habits of intemperance also, having greatly lessened their esteem,

[021-112]they became unwilling he should longer hold the commanding position he had

[021-113]enjoyed, and so well adorned, in the earlier part of his life. At a

[021-114]council held in September, 1827, a paper was drawn up, containing charges

[021-115]against the orator, which were assigned as a reason for the extraordinary

[021-116]course they pursued, closing with the declaration, that they renounced him

[021-117]as their chief, and forbade him to act as one, affirming that he should

[021-118]thereafter be regarded as a private man.

[021-119]This proceeding stung the orator to the quick, and aroused him to action,

[021-120]He could not endure the thought of the humiliation thus brought upon him,

[021-121]at the close of life. The thought too, that it had been effected by those

[021-122]who differed from him, in their religious sentiments, and would be

[021-123]regarded as a triumph over him, touching the views he had long

[021-124]entertained, as to what would best promote the welfare of his people,

[021-125]affected him in a point so near his heart, as to forbid his resting under

[021-126]it.

[021-127]"It shall not be said," thought he, "that Sa-go-ye-wat-ha, lived in

[021-128]insignificance, and died in disgrace. Am I not yet strong? Have I not yet

[021-129]power to withstand my enemies?"

[021-130]He set out for Washington, to spread his griefs before his great Father.

[021-131]On arriving there he visited Colonel McKenney, who had charge of Indian

[021-132]affairs. That officer had been informed, through the Indian agent, of all

[021-133]that had transpired among the Senecas, and of the cause of their

[021-134]displeasing Red Jacket.

[021-135]When the customary salutations were over, Red Jacket remarked through his

[021-136]interpreter, "I have a talk for my Father." "Tell him," said Colonel

[021-137]McKenney, "I have one for him. I will make it, and will then listen to

[021-138]him." The colonel then proceeded to give a minute history of all that had

[021-139]recently transpired, and dwelt upon the various causes that had operated

[021-140]in producing the rupture, that had taken place. He pointed out to him the

[021-141]course he ought to have pursued, that he should have manifested a spirit

[021-142]of forbearance, and allowed the Christian party the same liberty in the

[021-143]exercise of their sentiments, which he demanded for himself; and that this

[021-144]course would have saved him the mortification he now experienced, in being

[021-145]expelled from office and power.

[021-146]During this conversation Red Jacket never took his keen and searching eye

[021-147]from the speaker, but at its close turned to the interpreter, and pointing

[021-148]in the direction of his home and people, said, "*Our Father has got a

[021-149]long eye.*"

[021-150]He then proceeded to vindicate himself and his cause, not forgetting to

[021-151]pour upon the Black coats plentiful effusions of wrath. The colonel

[021-152]advised him to return to his people, convene a council and come to a

[021-153]better understanding with them, by allowing those among them who desired

[021-154]to do so, to become Christians, while himself and those who thought like

[021-155]him, might claim the privilege of following unmolested, the faith of their

[021-156]fathers. [Footnote: Col. McKenney's Indian Biography.]

[021-157][[About]] one month had passed since Red Jacket's deposition. In the mean time

[021-158]Red Jacket had been very active in going from one reservation to another,

[021-159]and sparing no pains, in gathering a Great Council, from those belonging

[021-160]to the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]].

[021-161]Another council was convened, much larger than the former, composed of

[021-162]members from other reservations, belonging to the Iroquois confederacy. It

[021-163]assembled at the upper council-house of the Seneca village near Buffalo.

[021-164]At the opening of the council, the paper declaring the orator's deposition

[021-165]was read. Half Town, a Seneca chief of the Cattaraugus reservation then

[021-166]arose, and said there was but one voice in his nation, and that was of

[021-167]general indignation at the contumely cast on so great a man as Red Jacket.

[021-168]The council was then addressed by several other chiefs very much to the

[021-169]same effect. After which the condemned orator arose slowly, as if grieved

[021-170]and humiliated, but yet with his ancient air of command.

[021-171]"My Brothers:" said he, after a solemn pause, "You have this day been

[021-172]correctly informed of an attempt to make me sit down, and throw off the

[021-173]authority of a chief, by twenty-six misguided chiefs of my nation. You

[021-174]have heard the statements of my associates in council, and their

[021-175]explanations of the foolish charges brought against me. I have taken the

[021-176]legal and proper way to meet these charges. It is the only way in which I

[021-177]could notice them. Charges which I despise, and which nothing would induce

[021-178]me to notice, but the concern which many respected chiefs of my nation,

[021-179]feel in the character of their aged comrade. Were it otherwise I should

[021-180]not be before you. I would fold my arms, and sit quietly under these

[021-181]ridiculous slanders.

[021-182]"The Christian party have not even proceeded legally to put me down." He

[021-183]then made some artful observations on the origin of the attack made upon

[021-184]him. He laid open its history step by step. He dwelt upon the various

[021-185]circumstances connected with the introduction of Christianity among them.

[021-186]He alluded to the course taken by the Christians as ruinous and

[021-187]disgraceful, especially in their abandonment of the religion of their

[021-188]fathers, and their sacrifices, and of the lands given them by the Great

[021-189]Spirit, for paltry considerations. As for the *Black coats*, Mr.

[021-190]Calhoun had told him at Washington four years before, that the Indians

[021-191]must treat with them as they thought proper; the government would not

[021-192]interfere. "I will not consent," said he, sagaciously identifying his

[021-193]disgrace with his opposition to the Christians, "I will not consent

[021-194]silently to be trampled under foot. As long as I can raise my voice, I

[021-195]will oppose such measures. As long as I can stand in my moccasins, I will

[021-196]do all I can for my nation. Ah! it grieves my heart, when I look around me

[021-197]and see the situation of my people, in old times united and powerful, now

[021-198]divided and feeble. I feel sorry for my nation. Many years have I guided

[021-199]my people. When I am gone to the other world, when the [[great-spirit|Great Spirit]] calls

[021-200]me away, who among them can take my place?" [Footnote: Thatcher's Indian

[021-201]Biography.]

[021-202]No adequate account of this speech has been preserved. It is said he spoke

[021-203]three hours in his own defense; that it was a masterly effort, and equal

[021-204]to the speeches he used to make in his palmiest days. [Footnote:

[021-205]Conversation of the author with Wm. Jones, Seneca chief.]

[021-206]Though greatly dilapidated in his powers by intemperance, he was

[021-207]thoroughly aroused on this occasion, and the [[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]], pathos, and fire

[021-208]of a former day, shed around him the luster of a superior mind, and his

[021-209]people for the time, forgot and forgave his delinquencies, and by

[021-210]unanimous consent, reinstated him in office and power.

[021-211]Thus by means of one more great exertion of this wonderful faculty, by

[021-212]which he controlled the minds of his people, they were led to reverse the

[021-213]decision that had been made against him, and though he stood among them

[021-214]but the blasted trunk of that tree, which, in its full and luxuriant

[021-215]prime, cast a deep and mellowing shade over their closing history, and

[021-216]invested it still with the appearance of strength; they resolved he should

[021-217]yet wear the title, that better befitted him in other days, though it

[021-218]served but slightly to hide the deformity, wrought in his noble nature, by

[021-219]the demon of intemperance.

[021-220]With this speech the public career of Red Jacket is closed. The effort he

[021-221]made on this occasion, added to his exertions previous to the gathering of

[021-222]the council, was too great for his aged and enfeebled condition. After

[021-223]this he declined very rapidly, and seemed to realize that his end was

[021-224]drawing near. He often adverted to this event, but always in language of

[021-225]philosophic calmness.

[021-226]In view of it he visited successively all of his most intimate friends, at

[021-227]their cabins, and talked with them in the most impressive and affecting

[021-228]manner. He told them that he was passing away, and his counsels would soon

[021-229]be heard no more. He ran over the history of his people, from the most

[021-230]remote period to which his knowledge extended, and pointed out as few

[021-231]could, the wrongs, the privations, and the loss of character, which almost

[021-232]of themselves constituted that history. "I am about to leave you," said

[021-233]he, "and when I am gone, and my warnings shall be no longer heard, or

[021-234]regarded, the craft and avarice of the white man will prevail. Many

[021-235]winters have I breasted the storm, but I am an aged tree, I can stand no

[021-236]longer. My leaves are fallen, my branches are withered, and I am shaken by

[021-237]every breeze. Soon my aged trunk will be prostrate, and the foot of the

[021-238]exulting foe of the Indian, may be placed upon it in safety; for I leave

[021-239]none who will be able to avenge such an indignity. Think not I mourn for

[021-240]myself. I go to join the spirits of my fathers, where age cannot come; but

[021-241]my heart fails, when I think of my people, who are soon to be scattered

[021-242]and forgotten."

[021-243]Many noticed that his feelings at this time were greatly modified and

[021-244]mellowed, with respect to the stand he had taken against Christianity. His

[021-245]wife's example, who was a woman of humble, consistent piety, exerted a

[021-246]salutary, and happy influence upon him. It led him to regard Christianity

[021-247]more favorably, and to recede very much from the hostile position he had

[021-248]previously maintained. He talked of peace, and sought to bring about a

[021-249]reconciliation between the two parties. He convened a council with this in

[021-250]view. He made special preparations to attend it, dressing himself with

[021-251]more than ordinary care, with all his gay apparel and ornaments. He went

[021-252]with the intention of making what would have been his farewell speech, and

[021-253]giving them his last counsel.

[021-254]He was taken suddenly ill at the Council-house, of cholera morbus and

[021-255]returned home, saying to his wife, "I am sick; I could not stay at the

[021-256]council, I shall never recover."

[021-257]He then took off his rich costume, and laid it carefully away, reclined

[021-258]upon his couch, and did not rise again till morning. His wife prepared him

[021-259]medicine, which he took, but said, "it will do no good. I shall die."

[021-260]The next day he called his wife and the little girl he loved so much,

[021-261]requested them to sit beside him and listen to his parting words.

[021-262]Addressing his wife, he said: "I am going to die, I shall never again

[021-263]leave this house alive. I wish to thank you for your kindness to me. You

[021-264]have loved me. You have always prepared my food, and taken care of my

[021-265]clothes, and been patient with me. I am sorry I ever treated you unkindly.

[021-266]I am sorry I left you, because of your new religion. I am convinced it is

[021-267]a good religion, and has made you a better woman, and wish you to

[021-268]persevere in it. I should like to live longer for your sake. I meant to

[021-269]build you a new house, and make you more comfortable, but it is now too

[021-270]late."

[021-271]Addressing his daughter, he said; "I hope my daughter will remember what I

[021-272]have so often told her, not to go in the streets with strangers, or

[021-273]associate with improper persons. She must stay with her mother, and grow

[021-274]up a respectable woman."

[021-275]He said again: "When I am dead, it will be noised abroad through all the

[021-276]world, they will hear of it across the great waters, and say, Red Jacket

[021-277]the great orator is dead. And white men will come and ask you for my body.

[021-278]They will wish to bury me. But do not let them take me. Clothe me in my

[021-279]simplest dress, put on my leggins and my moccasins, and hang the cross I

[021-280]have worn so long, around my neck, and let it lie upon my bosom. Then bury

[021-281]me among my people. Neither do I wish to be buried with Pagan rites. I

[021-282]wish the ceremonies to be as you like, according to the customs of your

[021-283]new religion, if you choose. Your minister says the dead will rise.

[021-284]Perhaps they will. If they do, I wish to rise with my old comrades. I do

[021-285]not wish to rise among pale faces. I wish to be surrounded by red men. Do

[021-286]not make a feast according to the customs of the Indians. Whenever my

[021-287]friends chose, they could come and feast with me, when I was well, and I

[021-288]do not wish those who have never eaten with me in my cabin, to surfeit at

[021-289]my funeral feast."

[021-290]When he had finished he laid down on his couch and did not rise again. He

[021-291]lived several days but was most of the time in a stupor, or else

[021-292]delirious. He often asked for Mr. Harris, the missionary, and would

[021-293]afterward unconsciously mutter: "I do not hate him. He thinks I hate him,

[021-294]but I do not, I would not hurt him." The missionary was sent for

[021-295]repeatedly, but was from home at the time, and did not return till after

[021-296]the chief's death.

[021-297]When the messenger told him Mr. Harris had not come, he replied: "Very

[021-298]well, the Great Spirit will order it as he sees best, whether I shall

[021-299]speak with him or not." [Footnote: Conversation of the author with Wm.

[021-300]Jones, Seneca chief, and sketch of Red Jacket in "The Iroquois." The

[021-301]account of the orator's closing hours given in this work, is more full,

[021-302]but in perfect accord with the statements made to the author by Mr.

[021-303]Jones.] Again he would murmur: "He accused me of being a snake, and trying

[021-304]to bite somebody. This was true, and I wish to make satisfaction."

[021-305]The cross he wore was a very rich one of stones set in gold, and large; by

[021-306]whom it was given, his friends never knew. This is all the ornament he

[021-307]requested to have buried with him.

[021-308]It was customary among the Indians to make funeral feasts. No family was

[021-309]so poor as not thus to honor the dead. If all they possessed was a cow, it

[021-310]was slaughtered for the occasion. Red Jacket desired nothing of this kind.

[021-311]A pagan funeral for a distinguished person is a pompous affair, and lasts

[021-312]for ten days. Every night a fire is kindled at the grave, and around it

[021-313]the mourners gather, and utter piteous wails.

[021-314]The wife and daughter were the only ones to whom he spoke parting words,

[021-315]or gave a parting blessing. As his last hour drew nigh, his family all

[021-316]gathered around him, but the children were not his own, they were stepchildren, his own were all sleeping in the churchyard, where he was soon

[021-317]to be laid.

[021-318]His step-children he always loved and cherished, their mother had taught

[021-319]them to love and honor him. The wife sat by his pillow and rested her hand

[021-320]on his head. At his feet stood the two sons, now aged and Christian men,

[021-321]and by his side the little girl, whose hand rested on his withered and

[021-322]trembling palm. His last words were still, "Where is the missionary?" He

[021-323]then clasped the child to his bosom, while she was sobbing in anguish, her

[021-324]ears caught his hurried breathing, his arms relaxed their hold, she looked

[021-325]up, he was gone.

[021-326]There was mourning in the household, there was great mourning among the

[021-327]people. The orator, the man of matchless gifts, of surpassing eloquence

[021-328]was no more; and there were none to fill his place.

[021-329]Red Jacket desired after his death, a vial of cold water might be placed

[021-330]in his hand. His reason for this his friends did not understand. Red

[021-331]Jacket felt that intemperance had been the bane of his life. Possibly from

[021-332]this conviction he may have desired to be accompanied in his journey to

[021-333]the spirit-land, by the beverage of which his better judgment most

[021-334]approved.

[021-335]The arrangements of his funeral Red Jacket committed to his wife's son-inlaw Wm. Jones. His friends, who belonged mostly to the Christian party,

[021-336]chose to have at his funeral the simple and appropriate services of that

[021-337]religion. It was largely attended by his own race, and by the whites

[021-338]living in that vicinity. He was buried in the mission burying ground,

[021-339]where were reposing many of his race, the aged and young, warrior, sachem,

[021-340]child.

[021-341]His death was at his residence near the church and mission-house at Seneca

[021-342]village on the 20th of January, 1830.
