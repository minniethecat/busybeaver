# John Breckenridge

## Biography

John Breckenridge was, at the time of his encounter with Red Jacket, a young candidate for the clerical office who afterward became an eminent divine. (019-073) (019-074) (019-075) A remarkable conversation took place between him and [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] not far from the period the account was then considering, and the author, having permission, gave it entire because it served to illustrate the orator's character and views. (019-072) (019-075) (019-076)

## Role and Influence

The first opportunity Breckenridge ever enjoyed of seeing the celebrated Indian chief was in the year 1821, at the residence of General [[peter-b-porter|Peter B. Porter]] at [[black-rock|Black Rock]], New York, while he was on a visit to the general and his family. (019-078) (019-079) (019-080) It seemed a peculiarly fit occasion to become acquainted with the great Seneca orator, whose tribe resided within a few miles of Black Rock. (019-081) (019-082)

His access to the chiefs was secured by family connection: General Porter's lamented lady, whose kindness had won the rugged hearts of all their leading men, was Breckenridge's sister, so that their united influence and his near relationship to them opened the way to him at once. (019-085) (019-088) (019-089) (019-090)

## Key Events

- **The 1821 interview at Black Rock**: In the recorded conversation Breckenridge said little to Red Jacket of religion, but urged him to prepare to meet the Great Spirit and recommended him to go to Jesus for all he needed; Red Jacket took it kindly, said he should see him no more, and was going to his people to die. (019-415) (019-416) (019-417) (019-418) Not long after, the orator was called to his last account. (019-418) (019-419)

- **A source for the account**: Stone represented the testimony of Dr. Breckenridge as corresponding with that of hundreds of others who confessed their inability to do the orator justice. (019-420) (019-421) Breckenridge himself lamented his inability to make even an approach to justice as to the language and figures in which Red Jacket clothed his thoughts, and by which he illustrated and enforced them. (019-422) (019-423) (019-424)
