## Biography

De Witt Clinton served as Governor of New York during a period when the remaining Seneca reservations were under intense pressure from land speculators. The [[ogden-land-company]], having acquired the preemptive right to Seneca lands from the [[holland-land-company]], sought to induce the Indians to sell their reservations.

Clinton's most significant interaction with the Seneca Nation came when the Indians appealed to the governments of the United States and New York for protection against the Ogden Company's efforts. Hubbard records the "noble response of Governor De Witt Clinton of New York" to their appeal. (018-006)

## Role and Influence

Clinton's reply to the Seneca appeal stands as a notable defense of Indian land rights during an era when such defenses were rare from government officials. He affirmed unequivocally that the Seneca were "the owners of these lands in the same way that your brethren the Oneidas, are of their reservations," and declared that "no man shall deprive you of them without your consent." (018-315)

Clinton distinguished between the right of preemption — the exclusive right to purchase — and the right of ownership, clarifying that "all the right that Ogden and his company have to your reservations, is the right of purchasing them when you think it expedient to sell them." (018-315) He assured the Seneca that the State would protect them in the full enjoyment of their lands.

> All the right that Ogden and his company have to your reservations, is the right of purchasing them when you think it expedient to sell them, that is, they can buy your lands, but no other person can. You may retain them as long as you please, and you may sell them to Ogden as soon as you please. You are the owners of these lands in the same way that your brethren the Oneidas, are of their reservations. They are all that is left of what the Great Spirit gave to your ancestors. No man shall deprive you of them without your consent. The State will protect you in the full enjoyment of your lands.
> — Governor De Witt Clinton's reply to the Seneca appeal (018-315)

This response, Hubbard notes, was "worthy of his name and office." (018-315)
