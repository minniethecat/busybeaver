# Sachem (Chieftainship)

## Definition

A sachem was a hereditary civil chieftain of the Iroquois Confederacy, holding a specific title tied to a particular clan within one of the constituent nations. Unlike war-chiefs, who earned their position through demonstrated courage in battle, sachems held authority in times of peace: they deliberated in council, negotiated treaties, managed relations between clans, and conducted the condolence ceremonies that restored social order after death or disruption. (009-040)

Hubbard records that Red Jacket "was not a war-chief but a sachem, the sachems being" the civil magistrates of the confederacy — a distinction that is essential to understanding his career. (009-049)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The sachem system was one of the defining institutions of the Iroquois Confederacy. Each of the fifty original sachemships was created by the Great Peacemaker at the founding of the league centuries before European contact. Clan mothers held the authority to nominate sachems; if a sachem proved unworthy, the clan mother could remove him — a process known as "dehorning."

Hubbard's narrative reveals the tension inherent in the sachem's dual role. Sachems were peace-time leaders by definition, yet the crises of the Revolutionary War era blurred this boundary: "it nevertheless, occurred so... that the sachems also went to war." (009-050) The sachems were also expected to be the most eloquent speakers of their nation — an expectation Red Jacket fulfilled to an extraordinary degree. (003-113)

## Role in the Narrative

Red Jacket's identity as a sachem — not a war-chief — is essential to understanding his position in Hubbard's narrative. While war-chiefs like Cornplanter and Brant earned fame on the battlefield, Red Jacket earned his standing through eloquence in council, and it was this civil authority that gave weight to his persistent advocacy for Iroquois sovereignty and land rights. (010-221)

The contrast between civil and military authority animates many of the diplomatic encounters Hubbard describes. When Red Jacket "requested the sachems and chiefs" to consider a course of action, he drew on the full moral authority of the Confederacy's traditional governance. (010-226) That authority was increasingly tested as the United States government sought to bypass the council and negotiate directly with individual chiefs — a strategy that struck at the heart of the sachem system.
