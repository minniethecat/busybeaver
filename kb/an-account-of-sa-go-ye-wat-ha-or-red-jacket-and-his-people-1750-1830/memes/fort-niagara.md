# Fort Niagara

## Geography

Fort Niagara stands at the mouth of the Niagara River where it empties into Lake Ontario. The Niagara River, connecting Lake Erie to Lake Ontario, forms a natural boundary between western New York and present-day Ontario. At its northern terminus, Fort Niagara controlled access to the upper Great Lakes via the critical portage route around Niagara Falls.

Hubbard describes the falls themselves in passing, noting that "a greater water wonder than the falls of Niagara" had scarcely been seen (020-071), suggesting the awe the region inspired in travelers.

## Historical Significance

During the Revolutionary War, Fort Niagara functioned as the principal British military headquarters on the Great Lakes frontier. From this base, British officers coordinated with their Iroquois allies, particularly the [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] and [[mohawk-nation|Mohawk]]. (006-084)

The fort was one of five key British posts — along with Oswegatchie, [[oswego|Oswego]], [[detroit|Detroit]], and Mackinaw — that the British retained after the war. (006-084)

For the Seneca, Fort Niagara was a source of both sustenance and manipulation. Hubbard describes how "the British officers at Niagara, and runners" continued to influence Iroquois politics after the Revolution. (007-133)

Seneca leaders frequently visited the fort to receive provisions and gifts, maintaining a relationship that complicated American efforts to establish exclusive ties with the Six Nations. (007-133)

> The British continued to hold possession of these posts, though it was a violation of the treaty of peace, to do so. They were urged by the Americans to give them up, but refused, on the ground, as they alleged, that the Americans had not complied with the stipulation, that the Tories should be paid for their losses during the war.
>
> (006-084)

British retention of Fort Niagara was a persistent factor in frontier instability. The British presence provided a tangible alternative for Iroquois leaders dissatisfied with American treaty terms, giving them diplomatic leverage but also perpetuating their dependency.

During the War of 1812, the American army moved "down the Niagara river, destroying some of the enemy's stores" (017-292) and crossed at [[black-rock|Black Rock]]. (017-070) Fort Niagara also served as a gathering place for Seneca families displaced by warfare and land cessions. Hubbard records that refugees "were taken to Niagara" in times of crisis.
