[005-001]Claim of the United States to Indian lands—Conflicting claims of

[005-002]different States—Difficulty settled—Attempt to acquire the land by a

[005-003]lease—Purchase by Phelps and Gorham—Further purchase by Robert [[thomas-morris|Morris]].

[005-004]At the close of the war of the Revolution, the territory ceded by Great

[005-005]Britain to the United States, included large tracts of country occupied by

[005-006]the Indians. In ceding these lands, she ceded only the right claimed by

[005-007]herself, on the ground of original discovery, which was simply a priority

[005-008]of right to purchase of the original occupants of the soil. The Indians

[005-009]were allowed to dwell upon these lands, and were considered in a certain

[005-010]sense the owners, but were required in case of a sale, to dispose of them

[005-011]to the government. [Footnote: Kent's Commentary.]

[005-012]As each State claimed to be sovereign in every interest not ceded to the

[005-013]general government, each State claimed the territory covered by its

[005-014]original charter. These charters, owing to great ignorance of geographical

[005-015]limits, created claims that conflicted with each other. From this source

[005-016]originated difficult questions about land titles and jurisdiction, between

[005-017]the States of Connecticut and Pennsylvania,—Massachusetts and New York.

[005-018]These difficulties which existed before, the greater question of the

[005-019]Revolutionary war suspended for a time, but when peace was concluded, they

[005-020]came up again for a consideration and settlement.

[005-021]The way was in a measure prepared for this, by the relinquishment to the

[005-022]general government, on the part of New York in 1781, and of Massachusetts

[005-023]in 1785, of all their right to territory west of a meridian line drawn

[005-024]south, from the western end of [[lake-ontario|Lake Ontario]].

[005-025]In the adjustment of these difficulties, Connecticut relinquished her

[005-026]claim to a tract of land on the Susquehanna in Pennsylvania, called the

[005-027]Gore, and acquired that part of the State of Ohio called New Connecticut,

[005-028]or Western Reserve. And Pennsylvania obtained a tract of land lying

[005-029]immediately beyond the western boundary of the State of New York, and

[005-030]north-east of her own, embracing the harbor of Presque Isle, on Lake Erie,

[005-031]familiarly known as the Triangle, thus giving her access to the waters of

[005-032]this Lake.

[005-033]The question in controversy between the States of New York and

[005-034]Massachusetts was more serious, owing to the large amount of territory

[005-035]claimed by the latter in western New York. It was brought to an amicable

[005-036]settlement, by Massachusetts surrendering to New York the right of

[005-037]jurisdiction, over all the land west of the present eastern boundary of

[005-038]the State; and by New York giving to Massachusetts the pre-emptive right,

[005-039]or right of purchasing of the Indians, all of the lands lying west of a

[005-040]meridian line drawn through [[seneca-nation|Seneca]] Lake, from a certain point on the

[005-041]northern boundary of Pennsylvania, reserving however, a strip of land one

[005-042]mile in width, along the eastern shore of the [[fort-niagara|Niagara]] river. Thus New

[005-043]York, while she retained the sovereignty, lost the fee of about six

[005-044]millions of acres of land, in one of the finest regions of country in the

[005-045]new world. [Footnote: For a more full account, see "Turner's History of

[005-046]the Phelps and Gorham Purchase."]

[005-047]While these difficulties were being adjusted, a magnificent speculation

[005-048]was in progress, which bid fair to meet the expectations of its earnest

[005-049]projectors. A company was organized, called the New York and [[genesee-river|Genesee]] Land

[005-050]Company, with a view to obtain the entire tract of Indian lands within the

[005-051]State. To evade the law forbidding the sale of these lands to any party

[005-052]not authorized by the State, it was proposed to obtain them by a lease,

[005-053]that should extend nine hundred and ninety-nine years. A lease extending

[005-054]so long, was regarded as equivalent to a sale.

[005-055]With a view to further its designs another company, the Niagara Genesee

[005-056]Company, was also formed in [[canada|Canada]], of those who were most in

[005-057]correspondence with the Indians, and who would be influential in securing

[005-058]from them a decision in favor of their object.

[005-059]These organizations, especially the New York Land Company, were large, and

[005-060]included men of wealth and prominence, both in New York and Canada. With

[005-061]such appliances as they were enabled to bring to bear upon the Indians,

[005-062]they secured, in November, 1787, a *lease for nine hundred and ninetynine years*, of all the lands of the Iroquois in the State of New York,

[005-063]except some small reservations, and the privilege of hunting and fishing,

[005-064]for an annual rent of two thousand dollars, and a promised gift of twenty

[005-065]thousand dollars.

[005-066]The formidable character of these associations created a just alarm, and

[005-067]measures were immediately undertaken to circumvent their influence. An act

[005-068]was passed by the Legislature of New York, in March, 1788, authorizing the

[005-069]governor to disregard all contracts made with the Indians, and not

[005-070]sanctioned by the State; and to cause those who had entered upon Indian

[005-071]lands under such contracts, to be driven off, and their houses destroyed.

[005-072]The sheriff of the county was directed to dispossess intruders and burn

[005-073]their dwellings, and a military force was called out, that strictly

[005-074]enforced these orders.

[005-075]Thus by the energetic action of Governor Clinton of New York, the designs

[005-076]of these organizations were overruled.

[005-077]As early as 1784, the Legislature of New York had passed an act,

[005-078]appointing the governor, and a [[american-board-of-commissioners|Board of Commissioners]], the Superintendents

[005-079]of Indian affairs, and as there were other Indian lands within the State,

[005-080]not covered by the pre-emptive right of Massachusetts, these commissioners

[005-081]with the governor at their head, entered upon negotiations with a view of

[005-082]purchasing them, and securing a title to them for the State. [Footnote:

[005-083]The commissioners designated were: Abraham Cuyler, Peter Schuyler and

[005-084]Henry Glen, who associated with them Philip Schuyler, Robert Yates,

[005-085]Abraham Ten Broeck, A. Yates, Jr., P. W. Yates, John J. Beekman, Mathew

[005-086]Vischer, and Gen. Gansevoort.]

[005-087]A council of the Iroquois was appointed for this purpose, at Fort

[005-088]Schuyler, on the first of September, 1788.

[005-089]The Leasees disappointed and angered by the bold and decisive measures

[005-090]taken against them, exerted their influence to prevent the Indians from

[005-091]assembling. But by measures equally energetic in its favor, a

[005-092]representation of the different tribes was obtained, and a treaty was

[005-093]concluded on the 12th, in which was conveyed to the State the land of the

[005-094][[onondaga-nation|Onondagas]]; some reservations excepted, in consideration of one thousand

[005-095]dollars, in hand paid and an [[treaty-annuities|annuity]] of five hundred dollars forever.

[005-096]Then followed negotiations with the [[oneida-nation|Oneidas]]. Speeches were interchanged,

[005-097]propositions made and rejected, until finally an agreement was made, and a

[005-098]deed of cession executed by the chiefs, conveying all their lands,

[005-099]excepting certain reservations, in consideration of two thousand dollars

[005-100]in money, two thousand dollars in clothing and other goods, one thousand

[005-101]dollars in provisions, five hundred dollars for the erection of a saw and

[005-102]grist mill on their reservation, and an annuity of six hundred dollars

[005-103]forever.

[005-104]The commissioners next appointed a council to be held at [[albany|Albany]], December

[005-105]15, 1788. Great difficulty was experienced in getting the Indians

[005-106]together, the Leasees it is said, "kept the Indians so continually

[005-107]intoxicated, it was impossible to do anything with them." [Footnote:

[005-108]Turner's History.]

[005-109]It was not until the eleventh of the February following, that a sufficient

[005-110]number were brought together, to proceed with the negotiations; and on the

[005-111]twenty-fifth, the preliminaries having been settled, the [[cayuga-nation|Cayugas]] ceded to

[005-112]the State all of their lands, excepting a large reservation of one hundred

[005-113]square miles. It was in consideration of five hundred dollars in hand,

[005-114]sixteen hundred and twenty-eight dollars in June following, and an annuity

[005-115]of five hundred dollars forever.

[005-116]Mr. Turner in alluding to these negotiations very properly observes, "it

[005-117]was only after a hard struggle of much perplexity and embarrassment, that

[005-118]the object was accomplished. For the honor of our country, it could be

[005-119]wished that all Indian negotiations and treaties, had been attended with

[005-120]as little wrong, had been conducted as fairly as were those under the

[005-121]auspices and general direction of George Clinton. No where has the veteran

[005-122]warrior and statesman left a better proof of his sterling integrity and

[005-123]ability, than is furnished by the records of these treaties. In no case

[005-124]did he allow the Indians to be deceived, but stated to them from time to

[005-125]time, with unwearied patience, the true conditions of the bargains they

[005-126]were consummating."

[005-127]He says further, "the treaties for lands found the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]] in a

[005-128]miserable condition. They had warred on the side of a losing party; for

[005-129]long years the field and the chase had been neglected; they were suffering

[005-130]for food and raiment. Half-famished they flocked to the treaties and were

[005-131]fed and clothed. One item of expense charged in the accounts of the treaty

[005-132]at Albany in 1789, was for horses paid for, that the Indians had killed

[005-133]and eaten on their way down. For several years in addition to the amount

[005-134]of provisions distributed to them at the treaties, boatloads of corn were

[005-135]distributed among them by the State."

[005-136]It does not appear that [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]], [[joseph-brant|Brant]], or other of the

[005-137]more noted chiefs among the Iroquois, were present to take a part in these

[005-138]negotiations. Hence exception was taken to these proceedings. When the

[005-139]time drew near for paying the first annuity, the Onondagas sent an agent

[005-140]to Governor Clinton, saying they had received four strings of wampum from

[005-141]the Senecas, forbidding them to go to [[fort-stanwix|Fort Stanwix]] to receive the money,

[005-142]and declaring also "that the governor of Quebec wanted their lands; that

[005-143]Sir John wanted them; Col. Butler wants the Cayugas' lands; and the

[005-144]commanding officer of Fort Niagara wants the Senecas' lands."

[005-145]They were assured in reply that they might "make their minds easy," the

[005-146]governor would protect them; that the Leasees were the cause of their

[005-147]trouble.

[005-148]The Cayugas also sent a message to the governor, saying they were

[005-149]"threatened with destruction, even total extermination. The voice comes

[005-150]from the west; *its sound is terrible, our brothers the Cayugas and

[005-151]Onondagas are to share the same fate*."

[005-152]The complaint was, they had sold their lands without consulting the

[005-153]*western tribes*.

[005-154]The decided position of the Executive in giving them assurance of

[005-155]protection, was the means of dissipating their alarm.

[005-156]Historical evidence renders it apparent, that at this early period, the

[005-157]design was entertained by those in Canada, whose control over the Indians

[005-158]was well nigh supreme, to gain through them possession of Western New

[005-159]York, and without compromising the government of Great Britain, sever it

[005-160]from the United States, connect it with the territory of the North-west,

[005-161]and hold it by Indian possession, in a sort of quasi allegiance, to the

[005-162]crown of England.

[005-163]Their design with respect to Western New York was defeated by the

[005-164]energetic measures of its chief executive, but further on we will see they

[005-165]did not relinquish the idea of holding from the United States, the

[005-166]territory of the North-west.

[005-167]Next in the race of competition for the broad and fertile lands of the

[005-168]Genesee, appear the names of Oliver Phelps and Nathaniel Gorham. They were

[005-169]the acknowledged representatives of a considerable body of men, who were

[005-170]ambitious of securing an interest in what was regarded as the most

[005-171]desirable region in this country.

[005-172]From the advent of Gen. Sullivan's army into the Indian country in 1779,

[005-173]their route being through the very finest portion of Western New York, and

[005-174]at a season of the year when vegetation was in its highest perfection; the

[005-175]beauty and fertility of these lands became the theme of praise, on the

[005-176]part of every soldier that beheld them. Their fame was thus carried to

[005-177]almost every village and hamlet in Pennsylvania and New England. Hence

[005-178]great eagerness was manifested in regard to the title, and settlement of

[005-179]these lands.

[005-180]The company of which Messrs. Phelps and Gorham were the leading spirits,

[005-181]having purchased the pre-emptive right of Massachusetts, in the spring of

[005-182]1788, Mr. Phelps went on to the ground, and was successful in convening a

[005-183]council of the Indians for the sale of their lands, at Buffalo creek,

[005-184]during the month of July of the same year. [Footnote: His success in

[005-185]obtaining this council, and securing a sale, was owing in a large degree,

[005-186]to his policy in paying court to the powerful faction of the Leasees

[005-187]residing in Canada, and giving them an interest in the purchase.]

[005-188]The Indians at this treaty strenuously resisted the sale of any of their

[005-189]land west of the Genesee river; yet with a view of furnishing "*a piece

[005-190]of ground for a mill yard*" at the Genesee Falls, were finally persuaded

[005-191]to give their assent to a boundary line, that included a tract twelve

[005-192]miles square, west of that river. The eastern boundary of the lands sold,

[005-193]was the Massachusetts pre-emptive line; the western, was a line "beginning

[005-194]in the northern line of Pennsylvania, due south of the corner or point of

[005-195]land made by the confluence of the Genesee river, and the Canaseraga

[005-196]creek, thence north on said meridian line to the corner or point, at the

[005-197]confluence aforesaid; thence northwardly along the waters of the Genesee

[005-198]river, to a point two miles north of Canawangus village, thence running

[005-199]due west 12 miles; thence running northwardly so as to be twelve miles

[005-200]distant from the western bounds of said river, to the shores of Lake

[005-201]Ontario." The lands thus ceded, are what has been called "*The Phelps and

[005-202]Gorham Purchase*." It contained by estimation two million and six hundred

[005-203]thousand acres, for which they agreed to pay the Indians five thousand

[005-204]dollars, and an annuity of five hundred dollars forever.

[005-205]Robert Morris, the distinguished financier of the Revolution, afterward

[005-206]became owner of the greater part of this purchase, as well as of the preemptive right of Massachusetts to the remaining part of Western New York.

[005-207]Through his agent in London, Wm. Temple Franklin, grandson of Doctor

[005-208]Franklin, these lands were again sold to an association of gentlemen,

[005-209]consisting of Sir William Pultney, John Hornby, and Patrick Colquhoun, and

[005-210]the farther settlement of this region, auspiciously commenced under its

[005-211]original proprietors, was conducted principally under their

[005-212]administration.

[005-213]An intelligent and enterprising young Scotchman, Charles Williamson, who

[005-214]had previously devoted his time while detained as a prisoner in this

[005-215]country, during the war of the Revolution, to investigations respecting

[005-216]its geographical resources and limits, and who from his disposition and

[005-217]business capacity, was well qualified for the station, was appointed their

[005-218]agent, and emigrating hither with his family, and two other young

[005-219]Scotchmen as his assistants, John Johnstone, and Charles Cameron, he

[005-220]became identified with the early history and progress of the extensive and

[005-221]important part of the Indian territory, that as we have seen, had just

[005-222]been opened, and was inviting a new race, to take possession of its virgin

[005-223]soil.
