# Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea)

## Biography

Joseph Brant was born in 1743 in the Ohio Country, a member of the Mohawk nation — the easternmost of the Six Nations, known as the "keepers of the eastern door" of the Iroquois Long House. His Mohawk name, Thayendanegea, meant "He Places Two Bets" — an apt name for a man who would spend his life navigating between two civilizations.

Brant's trajectory was shaped by his connection to Sir William Johnson, the British Superintendent of Indian Affairs, who married Brant's sister Molly. Through Johnson's patronage, Brant received an English education at Eleazar Wheelock's Moor's Indian Charity School in Connecticut (the institution that later became Dartmouth College). He became literate in English, converted to Anglicanism, and translated portions of the Bible and the Book of Common Prayer into Mohawk — a rare combination of traditional Iroquois leadership and European learning.

During the American Revolution, Brant led Mohawk and Loyalist forces in devastating raids along the New York and Pennsylvania frontiers, earning a reputation among American settlers as "Monster Brant" — a characterization that Hubbard's more measured account partially corrects. The settlers who suffered from these campaigns "fell under the command of Thayendanegea or Brant, who went with a company of Tories" in the border warfare that marked the Revolution's western theater. (003-235) Hubbard records that "prominent above all others were the exertions of Thayendanegea, or Brant, the famous war-chief, from whose leadership the inhabitants of our frontier endured such severe trials." (006-023)

After the British defeat, Brant led a contingent of Iroquois loyalists to the Grand River in Ontario (present-day Six Nations of the Grand River), where he spent his remaining years attempting to secure British recognition of Iroquois land rights and sovereignty. His "ambitious project" of securing a permanent homeland for the loyalist Iroquois in Canada consumed his later years. (006-002)

## Role and Influence

Brant's influence operated on both sides of the colonial divide. To the British, he was an indispensable ally whose command of English and familiarity with European customs made him an effective intermediary. To the Iroquois, he was a war chief whose military successes during the Revolution briefly restored the Confederacy's fading power. Hubbard's narrative reflects the complex position Brant occupied: too English for some Iroquois traditionalists, and too Iroquois for English officers who expected unquestioning deference.

After the war, Brant became an advocate for Iroquois land rights within the British Empire. He lobbied the colonial government in Quebec and the imperial government in London for clear title to the Grand River lands, arguing that the Iroquois had earned these lands through their wartime service to the Crown. His efforts met with mixed success — the British granted the land but subsequently permitted white settlement that eroded the reserve — and the frustration of this advocacy consumed his final years.

Brant's relationship with the American Iroquois — particularly the Seneca leaders Cornplanter and Red Jacket — was complicated. They shared a common heritage and faced similar pressures, but Brant had chosen permanent exile in Canada, while the Seneca remained within the United States. Hubbard observes that "between Red Jacket and Brant there did not exist a very strong" bond of friendship — an understatement that reflects the deep strategic and personal divisions between the two leaders. (004-071) Red Jacket, for his part, was alleged by Brant to have undermined his authority among the Iroquois during the war, and Brant "charged him with being the occasion of trouble to him." (003-259) The post-Revolution division of the Iroquois into American and Canadian branches was one of Brant's most enduring legacies.

## Key Events

- **Education at Moor's School (1761–1763)**: Learned English and Christianity at Eleazar Wheelock's school, laying the foundation for his role as a cross-cultural intermediary.
- **Revolutionary War (1775–1783)**: Led Mohawk and Loyalist forces in the Mohawk Valley campaigns. His raids at Cherry Valley and elsewhere made him a feared figure on the American frontier.
- **Post-War Migration (1784)**: Led Iroquois loyalists to the Grand River, establishing the Six Nations reserve in what is now Ontario, Canada.
- **Advocacy in London and Quebec (1780s–1790s)**: Traveled to London and Quebec City repeatedly to petition for secure land titles for the Grand River Iroquois, achieving partial success.
