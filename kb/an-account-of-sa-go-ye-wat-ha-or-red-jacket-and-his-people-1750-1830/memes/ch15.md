[015-001]Change in [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]'s views—How caused—His opposition to Christianity—

[015-002]Visit of a Missionary—Missionary's speech—Red Jacket's reply—Unpleasant

[015-003]termination of the Council.

[015-004]As time advanced, the mind of Red Jacket gradually receded from the

[015-005]favorable opinion he had entertained, with respect to the introduction

[015-006]among his people, of the customs of civilized life. Before this he

[015-007]regarded with favor the philanthropic designs of [[george-washington|Washington]] and others,

[015-008]which contemplated such a change. But henceforth his influence and

[015-009]energies were uniformly exerted, in resisting any innovation, upon the

[015-010]anciently established usages of the Iroquois. Several causes seemed to

[015-011]influence such a result.

[015-012]First of all was the condition of his people, as affected by the whites.

[015-013]They had been wasted and greatly enfeebled by the wars carried on between

[015-014]the whites, taking sides, as in the Revolution, against each other. And in

[015-015]their own conflicts, though in some instances successful, they had been so

[015-016]effectually overcome, that no hope now remained to them of resistance by

[015-017]war; no matter what combinations they might be able to effect among

[015-018]themselves.

[015-019]A still deeper source of regret, was the loss of so large a portion of

[015-020]their wide and beautiful country. Since parting with it, swarms of

[015-021]settlers had been flocking to the more favored portions, and were

[015-022]irresistibly advancing to full and entire possession. The idea that they

[015-023]could have their country to hunt in, as well after it was sold as before,

[015-024]was rapidly dissipated by the busy sounds, all through the forest, of the

[015-025]woodman's axe, and by the roar of the stately trees, as they fell down

[015-026]before the enterprising pioneer. The Indian brooded over this in silence,

[015-027]while all of these sounds, delightful to the emigrant, were as a knell of

[015-028]death to his ear. The [[iroquois-oratory|eloquence]] of Red Jacket had been exerted in vain, to

[015-029]arrest the progress of the white men. Onward they swept, bidding defiance

[015-030]to all the obstacles in their way. They were in possession of the ancient

[015-031]seats of the Iroquois. The red man's inheritance, was but a beggarly

[015-032]portion, when compared with his former princely domain. The thought of

[015-033]this weighed heavily upon Red Jacket's lofty spirit, and affected

[015-034]materially the disposition with which he regarded the white man.

[015-035]He had observed also that the Indian had not been improved, but rather

[015-036]made worse by intercourse with the white man. He more readily acquired his

[015-037]vices, than his virtues.

[015-038]The schools likewise that had been established among the Indians, had not

[015-039]been attended with very salutary results. And some of the Indian boys that

[015-040]had been sent to the schools of the whites, had failed to be qualified for

[015-041]usefulness among white men, and were unfitted in their tastes and habits

[015-042]for a life among the Indians. As was observed by Red Jacket: "they have

[015-043]returned to their kindred and color, neither white men nor Indians. The

[015-044]arts they have learned are incompatible with the chase, and ill adapted to

[015-045]our customs. They have been taught that which is useless to us. They have

[015-046]been made to feel artificial wants, which never entered the minds of their

[015-047]brothers. They have imbibed, in your great towns, the seeds of vices,

[015-048]which were unknown in the forest. They become discouraged and dissipated,

[015-049]—despised by the Indians, neglected by the whites, and without value to

[015-050]either,—less honest than the former, and perhaps more knavish than the

[015-051]latter." [Footnote: Washington had always been earnest in his desire to

[015-052]civilize the savages, but had little faith in the expedient which had been

[015-053]pursued, of sending their young men to our colleges; the true means he

[015-054]thought, was to introduce the arts and habits of husbandry among them.—

[015-055]Irving's Life of Washington.] Red Jacket was not alone in this opinion.

[015-056]One of [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]]'s sons, Henry O'Bail, had been educated in

[015-057][[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]; but on returning to his people, became a drunkard, and was

[015-058]discarded by his father. He had other sons, but resolved that no more of

[015-059]them should be educated among the whites, for said he, "it entirely spoils

[015-060]Indian."

[015-061]"What have we here?" exclaimed Red Jacket on one occasion addressing one

[015-062]of them. "What have we here? *You are neither a white man, or an Indian;

[015-063]for heaven's sake tell us, what are you?*"

[015-064]But further than this, Red Jacket had witnessed among the whites so many

[015-065]evidences of deceit and fraud; he had so often seen the Indians

[015-066]circumvented by their avarice and craft, that he looked with suspicion

[015-067]even on their attempts to do the Indians good. The language of the Trojan

[015-068]patriot concerning the Greeks—represents very nearly the feelings he

[015-069]entertained toward the whites.

[015-070]"Timeo Danaos et dona ferrentes.

[015-071]"The Greeks I fear, e'en in the gifts they bear."

[015-072]Hence Red Jacket began to look unfavorably on the attempts that had been

[015-073]made to civilize the Indian. He scorned to use the white man's axe, or

[015-074]hoe, or any implement of husbandry. He would not even use his language.

[015-075]Understanding well what was said to him in English, he spurned the idea of

[015-076]holding any communication with a white man, save through an interpreter.

[015-077]The Indian he looked upon as the rightful lord of this part of creation,

[015-078]the white man, as an intruder. The white man's ways were good for the

[015-079]white man; but in his view they would spoil the Indian. He believed that

[015-080]the peculiar characteristics of the Indian, were conferred on him by the

[015-081][[great-spirit|Great Spirit]] for a wise purpose, and for his good, they needed to be

[015-082]maintained. Hence all the ancient habits of his people, he earnestly

[015-083]strove to preserve, and had it been in his power, he would have built a

[015-084]wall like the Chinese, to keep his people from meeting with, and being

[015-085]contaminated by the whites. He would frown contempt on the Indian, who

[015-086]used a stool or chair in his cabin, and no king in his palace, ever sat

[015-087]more proudly, or with greater dignity on his throne, than did Red Jacket

[015-088]on his bear-skin in his humble dwelling.

[015-089]We can but admire in this, his independence of character; and when we

[015-090]reflect upon his conduct as influenced by the conviction, that such a

[015-091]course was essential for the good of his people, we may view it as

[015-092]meriting the praise of philanthrophy. Had he been as firm in resisting

[015-093]every enticement of the whites, he would have maintained a greater

[015-094]consistency, and himself attained a higher degree of excellence.

[015-095]Red Jacket was equally opposed to the introduction of Christianity among

[015-096]his people. He looked upon the religion of the white man, with the same

[015-097]feeling of suspicion and distrust as everything else coming from that

[015-098]source. He had no evidence from experience, of the benefits that would

[015-099]arise to them from its introduction among them. On the contrary his

[015-100]convictions, arising from observation, were against it; because he saw his

[015-101]people were made worse, by associating with the whites. When asked on one

[015-102]occasion, why he was opposed to the coming of missionaries among his

[015-103]people, he replied,—"Because they do us no good. If they are not useful

[015-104]among the white people, why do they send them among the Indians?—If they

[015-105]are useful to the white people, why do they not keep them at home? They

[015-106]are surely bad enough, to need the labor of every one, who can make them

[015-107]better."

[015-108]The Indians made no distinction between those who professed religion and

[015-109]those who made no profession. Their own religion was national. There was

[015-110]no division between the religious and irreligious. All were religious. In

[015-111]other words, they were all educated in the same faith, all united in

[015-112]observing the same religious rites, and all entertained the same religious

[015-113]belief, as had been handed down to them from their forefathers. This was

[015-114]salutary in promoting among them many virtues, worthy of commendation.

[015-115]They very properly estimated the value of religion, by the practical

[015-116]influence it exerted on those who received it. And they judged of the

[015-117]Christian religion, by the conduct and character of the nation that

[015-118]received and cherished it; who were nominally Christian.

[015-119]Unfortunately for the success of Christianity among them, they had

[015-120]witnessed so much deceit and fraud, there were so many among the whites,

[015-121]who were ready to take advantage of them,—to make them drunk, and then

[015-122]cheat them, they were unable to perceive in what way the religion of the

[015-123]whites, from whom they had received such treatment, could be better or as

[015-124]good, even as their own. They had not learned to regard those only as

[015-125]Christians, who reduced the principles of Christianity to practice, and

[015-126]were not aware that as a system, it enforced only what was right, and

[015-127]tolerated no conduct that was wrong.

[015-128]Hence in the efforts made to introduce Christianity among the [[seneca-nation|Senecas]], we

[015-129]find Red Jacket summoning the entire force of his influence, and eloquence

[015-130]in opposition to the measure.

[015-131]The arrival among them of a missionary from Massachusetts, was the

[015-132]occasion of a forensic effort, which defines very clearly his position,

[015-133]and though it may have suffered, as did most of his speeches, from coming

[015-134]through an interpreter, it displays nevertheless, indications of deep

[015-135]thought, and of a high order of talent. It was regarded at the time as an

[015-136]effort of great ability, and is perhaps as fair a specimen of his oratory,

[015-137]as has come down to us from the past.

[015-138]A council having been called to consider the matter, the missionary was

[015-139]introduced, who spoke as follows: [Footnote: The speech of the missionary

[015-140]is quoted from Col. Stone; the reply of Red Jacket from Drake, who is Col.

[015-141]Stone's authority for the same speech.]

[015-142]"My [[society-of-friends-seneca|Friends]]: I am thankful for the opportunity afforded us of meeting

[015-143]together at this time. I had a great desire to see you, and inquire into

[015-144]your state and welfare. For this purpose I have traveled a great distance,

[015-145]being sent by your old friends, the [[american-board-of-commissioners|Boston Missionary Society]]. You will

[015-146]recollect they formerly sent missionaries among you, to instruct you in

[015-147]religion, and labor for your good. Although they have not heard from you

[015-148]for a long time, yet they have not forgotten their brothers, the Six

[015-149]Nations, and are still anxious to do you good.

[015-150]"Brothers: I have not come to get your lands, or your money, but to

[015-151]enlighten your minds, and instruct you how to worship the Great Spirit,

[015-152]agreeably to his mind and will, and to preach to you the gospel of his

[015-153]Son, Jesus Christ. There is but one religion, and but one way to serve

[015-154]God, and if you do not embrace the right way, you can not be happy

[015-155]hereafter. You have never worshipped the Great Spirit, in a manner

[015-156]acceptable to him, but have all your lives, been in great errors and

[015-157]darkness. To endeavor to remove these errors, and open your eyes, so that

[015-158]you might see clearly, is my business with you.

[015-159]"Brothers: I wish to talk with you as one friend talks with another; and

[015-160]if you have any objections to receive the religion which I preach, I wish

[015-161]you to state them; and I will endeavor to satisfy your minds, and remove

[015-162]the objections.

[015-163]"Brothers: I want you to speak your minds freely; for I wish to reason

[015-164]with you on the subject, and if possible remove all doubts, if there be

[015-165]any on your minds. The subject is an important one, and it is of

[015-166]consequence, that you give it an early attention, while the offer is made

[015-167]you. Your friends, the Boston Missionary Society, will continue to send

[015-168]you good and faithful ministers, to instruct and strengthen you in

[015-169]religion, if on your part you are willing to receive them.

[015-170]"Brothers: Since I have been in this part of the country, I have visited

[015-171]some of your small villages, and talked with your people. They appear

[015-172]willing to receive instruction, but as they look up to you, as their elder

[015-173]brothers in council, they want first to know your opinion on the subject.

[015-174]You have now heard what I have to propose at present. I hope you will take

[015-175]it into consideration, and give me an answer before we part."

[015-176]The chiefs were in consultation for about two hours, when Red Jacket arose

[015-177]and spoke as follows:

[015-178]"Friend and Brother: It was the will of the Great Spirit that we should

[015-179]meet together this day. He orders all things, and has given us a fine day

[015-180]for our council. He has taken his garment from before the sun, and caused

[015-181]it to shine with brightness upon us. Our eyes are opened that we see

[015-182]clearly; our ears are unstopped, that we have been able to hear distinctly

[015-183]the words you have spoken. For all these favors we thank the Great Spirit,

[015-184]and Him only.

[015-185]"Brother: This [[council-fire|council fire]] was kindled by you. It was at your request

[015-186]that we came together at this time. We have listened with attention to

[015-187]what you have said. You requested us to speak our minds freely. This gives

[015-188]us great joy: for now we consider that we stand upright before you, and

[015-189]can speak what we think. All have heard your voice, and all speak to you

[015-190]now as one man. Our minds are agreed.

[015-191]"Brother: You say you want an answer to your talk before you leave this

[015-192]place. It is right you should have one, as you are a great distance from

[015-193]home, and we do not wish to detain you. But we will first look back a

[015-194]little, and tell you what our fathers have told us, and what we have heard

[015-195]from the white people.

[015-196]"Brother: Listen to what we say.—There was a time when our fathers owned

[015-197]this *great island*. [Footnote: The term used by the Indians when

[015-198]speaking of this continent.] Their seats extended from the rising to the

[015-199]setting sun. The Great Spirit had made it for the Indians. He had created

[015-200]the buffalo, the deer, and other animals for food. He had made the bear,

[015-201]and the beaver. Their skins served us for clothing. He had scattered them

[015-202]over the country, and taught us how to take them. He had caused the earth

[015-203]to produce corn for bread. All this he had done for his red children

[015-204]because he loved them. If we had some disputes about our hunting ground,

[015-205]they were generally settled without the shedding of much blood. But an

[015-206]evil day came upon us. Your forefathers crossed the great water, and

[015-207]landed on this island. Their numbers were small. They found friends and

[015-208]not enemies. They told us they had fled from their own country for fear of

[015-209]wicked men, and had come here to enjoy their religion. They asked for a

[015-210]small seat. We took pity on them, granted their request, and they sat down

[015-211]amongst us. We gave them corn and meat; they gave us poison in return.

[015-212][Footnote: Spirituous liquors.]

[015-213]"The white people had now found our country. Tidings were carried back,

[015-214]and more came amongst us. Yet we did not fear them. They called us

[015-215]brothers. We believed them and gave them a larger seat. At length their

[015-216]numbers had greatly increased. They wanted more land;—they wanted our

[015-217]country. Our eyes were opened, and our minds became uneasy.

[015-218]"Wars took place. Indians were hired to fight against Indians, and many of

[015-219]our people were destroyed. They also brought strong liquors among us;—

[015-220]they were strong and powerful, and have slain thousands.

[015-221]"Brother: Our seats were once large, and yours were very small. You have

[015-222]now become a great people, and we have scarcely a place left to spread our

[015-223]blankets. You have got our country, but are not satisfied;—you want to

[015-224]force your religion upon us.

[015-225]"Brother: Continue to listen. You say that you are sent to instruct us how

[015-226]to worship the Great Spirit agreeably to his mind, and if we do not take

[015-227]hold of this religion which you white people teach, we shall be unhappy

[015-228]hereafter. You say that you are right, that we are lost. How do we know

[015-229]this to be true? We understand that your religion is written in a book. If

[015-230]it was intended for us as well as you, why has not the Great Spirit given

[015-231]it to us, and not only to us, but why did he not give to our forefathers

[015-232]the knowledge of that book, with the means of understanding it rightly? We

[015-233]only know what you tell us about it. How shall we know when to believe,

[015-234]being so often deceived by the white people?

[015-235]"BROTHER: You say there is but one way to worship and serve the Great

[015-236]Spirit. If there is but one religion why do you white people differ so

[015-237]much about it? Why are you not all agreed,—as you can all read the book?

[015-238]"BROTHER: We do not understand these things. We are told that your

[015-239]religion was given to your forefathers, and has been handed down from

[015-240]father to son. We also have a religion which was given to our forefathers

[015-241]and has been handed down to us their children. We worship in that way. It

[015-242]teaches us to be thankful for all the favors we receive; to love each

[015-243]other, and to be united. We never quarrel about religion.

[015-244]"BROTHER: The Great Spirit has made us all, but he has made a great

[015-245]difference between his white and red children. He has given us different

[015-246]complexions, and different customs. To you He has given the arts. To these

[015-247]He has not opened our eyes. We know these things to be true. Since He has

[015-248]made so great a difference between us in other things, why may we not

[015-249]conclude that He has given us a different religion according to our

[015-250]understanding? The Great Spirit does right. He knows what is best for is

[015-251]children; we are satisfied.

[015-252]"BROTHER: We do not wish to destroy your religion, or take it from you. We

[015-253]only want to enjoy our own.

[015-254]"BROTHER: You say you have not come to get our land or our money, but to

[015-255]enlighten our minds. I will now tell you that I have been at your

[015-256]meetings, and saw you collect money from the meeting. I cannot tell what

[015-257]this money was intended for, but suppose it was for your minister, and if

[015-258]we should conform to your way of thinking, perhaps you may want some from

[015-259]us.

[015-260]"BROTHER: We are told that you have been preaching to the white people in

[015-261]this place. These people are our neighbors. We are acquainted with them.

[015-262]We will wait a little while, and see what effect your preaching has on

[015-263]them. If we find it does them good, makes them honest, and less disposed

[015-264]to cheat Indians, we will then consider again of what you have said.

[015-265]"BROTHER: You have now heard our answer to your talk, and this is all we

[015-266]have to say at present. As we are going to part, we will come and take you

[015-267]by the hand, and hope the Great Spirit will protect you on your journey,

[015-268]and return you in safety to your friends."

[015-269]Suiting the action to the word, he then drew near the missionary with the

[015-270]other chiefs, to take him by the hand; but he would not receive them, and

[015-271]rising hastily from his seat, said, there was no fellowship between the

[015-272]religion of God, and the works of the devil, and he could not therefore

[015-273]join hands with them.

[015-274]When this was interpreted to the Indians, they smiled and retired from the

[015-275]interview, without saying anything further.

[015-276]Such is the account given of this transaction. The termination is painful.

[015-277]It is a sad thought, that when the Indians had been invited to make known

[015-278]their objections, if they had any; and when they had been offered in good

[015-279]faith by this unlettered son of the forest, he should have been answered

[015-280]with so little patience or kindness. We hesitate not to say that the red

[015-281]man in this, manifested the better spirit.

[015-282]Mr. Crane afterward regretted the course he had taken, saying, "he

[015-283]supposed by shaking hands with them, they would regard it as signifying

[015-284]his approval of what they had said."
