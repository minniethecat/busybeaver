[002-001]Glance at the early history of the Iroquois—The territory occupied—

[002-002][[tuscarora-nation|Tuscaroras]]—Original strength—Traditions—Probable course of migration—

[002-003]The [[seneca-nation|Senecas]]—Story of their origin—Singular romance.

[002-004]Rising up from the obscurity of the past, we find a people, singular in

[002-005]their habits and character, whose history has been strangely, and in some

[002-006]respects sadly interwoven with our own. They were the original occupants

[002-007]of the soil, claiming to have lived here always, and to have grown out of

[002-008]the soil like the trees of the forest. Scattered over this continent were

[002-009]various Indian tribes, resembling each other in their general features and

[002-010]habits, but in some instances exhibiting stronger and more interesting

[002-011]traits of character than the others. Among these were the Iroquois, and if

[002-012][[red-jacket|Red Jacket]] was distinguished among his own people, his own people were not

[002-013]less conspicuous among the North American Indians.

[002-014]He sprang from the Senecas, and was accustomed to speak of his origin with

[002-015]feelings of conscious pride. For the Senecas were the most numerous and

[002-016]powerful of the six nations, of whom they were a part. Such was the title

[002-017]given to that celebrated Indian confederacy which, for a length of time

[002-018]unknown to us, inhabited the territory embraced by the State of New York.

[002-019]Here they lived in a line of settlements extending from one end to the

[002-020]other, through the middle of the State, and their domain as thus occupied,

[002-021]they were accustomed to style their *[[iroquois-long-house|Long House]]*. It was a shadowy

[002-022]dome, of generous amplitude, covered by the azure expanse above, garnished

[002-023]with hills, lakes, and laughing streams, and well stored with provisions,

[002-024]in the elk and deer that bounded freely through its forest halls, the

[002-025]moose that was mirrored in its waters, and the trout, those luscious

[002-026]speckled beauties, that nestled cosily in its crystal chambers.

[002-027]The eastern door was guarded by the [[mohawk-nation|Mohawks]], who resided at one, and its

[002-028]western by the Senecas, who dwelt at the other extremity of this abode.

[002-029]When ever a messenger from another nation came to them on business, or

[002-030]knocked, as it was termed, at the eastern or western door of their *long

[002-031]house*, it was the duty of the nation to which he came, to give him

[002-032]entertainment, and examine into the nature of his embassy. If it was of

[002-033]small importance, it was decided by their own council; but if it was such

[002-034]as to demand the united wisdom of the tribes, a runner was sent with a

[002-035]belt of wampum to the nearest nation, which would take the belt and send a

[002-036]runner with it to the next, and so on, and thus with but little delay, a

[002-037]general meeting was summoned of all the tribes.

[002-038]This confederacy at one time consisted of five nations, but afterward

[002-039]embraced six, by the addition of the Tuscaroras, a tribe that once

[002-040]occupied the territory of North Carolina.

[002-041]This tribe is said to have belonged at an early day to the Iroquois

[002-042]family, and to have inherited the enterprising and warlike character of

[002-043]the parent stock. They fought successfully with the Catawbas, Cowetas, and

[002-044]the Cherokees, and thought to exterminate by one decisive blow, all of the

[002-045]white inhabitants within their borders. Unsuccessful in the attempt,

[002-046]pressed sorely by the whites, who resisted the attack, and unwilling

[002-047]themselves to submit, they removed to the north, and through sympathy,

[002-048]similarity of taste, manners, or language, or from the stronger motives of

[002-049]consanguinity, became incorporated with the confederated tribes of the

[002-050]Iroquois. [Footnote: Schoolcraft's Report. Mr. Schoolcraft prefers, and

[002-051]quite justly the name Iroquois, as descriptive of this confederacy,

[002-052]instead of [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]], since the term is well known, and applicable to

[002-053]them in every part of their history. Whereas the other is appropriate only

[002-054]during the time when they were numerically six.]

[002-055]Thus constituted they presented the most formidable power, of which we

[002-056]have any knowledge in the annals of the Indian race. By their united

[002-057]strength they were able to repel invasion, from any of the surrounding

[002-058]nations, and by the force of their arms and their prowess in war, gained

[002-059]control over an extent of territory much greater than they occupied.

[002-060]They sent their war parties in every direction. The tribes north, east,

[002-061]south, and west of them were made to feel the power of their arms, and

[002-062]yield successively to their dexterity and valor. Now they were launching

[002-063]their war-canoes upon the lakes and rivers of the west, now engaged in

[002-064]bloody conflicts with the Catawbas and Cherokees of the south, now

[002-065]traversing regions of snow in pursuit of the Algonquins of the north, and

[002-066]anon spreading consternation and dread among the tribes at the remotest

[002-067]east. Their energy and warlike prowess made them a terror to their foes,

[002-068]and distant nations pronounced their name with awe.

[002-069]By what means these several tribes had been brought to unite themselves

[002-070]under one government, how long they had existed in this relation, and what

[002-071]was the origin of each one, or of all, are questions which will never

[002-072]perhaps be fully determined. There being no written records among them,

[002-073]all that can be ascertained of their history previous to their becoming

[002-074]known to the whites, must be gathered from the dim light of [[iroquois-tradition|tradition]],

[002-075]from their symbolic representations, from antique remains of their art,

[002-076]and from their legends and myths. These present in an obscure and shadowy

[002-077]form, a few materials of history, whose value is to be measured by the

[002-078]consideration, that they are all we have to tell the story of a noble and

[002-079]interesting race of men.

[002-080]Their traditions speak of the creation of the world, the formation of man,

[002-081]and the destruction of the world by a deluge. They suppose the existence

[002-082]originally of two worlds, an upper and lower. The upper completed and

[002-083]filled with an intelligent order of beings, the lower unformed and

[002-084]chaotic, whose surface was covered with water, in which huge monsters

[002-085]careered, uncontrolled and wild. From the upper there descended to the

[002-086]lower a creating spirit, in the form of a beautiful woman. She alighted on

[002-087]the back of a huge tortoise, gave birth to a pair of male twins and

[002-088]expired. Thereupon the shell of the tortoise began to enlarge, and grew

[002-089]until it became a "*big island*" and formed this continent.

[002-090]These two infant sons became, one the author of *good*, the other of

[002-091]*evil*. The creator of *good* formed whatever was praiseworthy and useful.

[002-092]From the head of his deceased mother he made the sun, from the remaining

[002-093]parts of her body, the moon and stars. When these were created the watermonsters were terrified by the light, and fled and hid themselves in the

[002-094]depths of the ocean. He diversified the earth by making rivers, seas

[002-095]and plains, covered it with animals, and filled it with productions

[002-096]beneficial to mankind. He then formed man and woman, put life into them,

[002-097]and called them Ong-we Hon-we *a real people*. [Footnote: This term is

[002-098]significant of true manhood. It implies that there was nothing of sham in

[002-099]their make up.]

[002-100]The creator of *evil* was active in making mountains, precipices,

[002-101]waterfalls, reptiles, morasses, apes, and whatever was injurious to, or in

[002-102]mockery of mankind. He put the works of the *good* out of order, hid

[002-103]his animals in the earth, and destroyed things necessary for the

[002-104]sustenance of man. His conduct so awakened the displeasure of the *good*,

[002-105]as to bring them into personal conflict. Their time of combat, and arms

[002-106]were chosen, one selecting flag-roots, the other the horns of a deer. Two

[002-107]whole days they were engaged in unearthly combat; but finally the *Maker

[002-108]of Good*, who had chosen the horns of a deer, prevailed, and retired to

[002-109]the world above. The *Maker of Evil* sank below to a region of darkness,

[002-110]and became the *Evil Spirit*, or Kluneolux of the world of despair.

[002-111][Footnote: Schoolcraft's Indian Cosmogony.]

[002-112]Many of their accounts appear to be purely fabulous, but not more so

[002-113]perhaps than similar traditions, to be found in the history of almost

[002-114]every nation.

[002-115]The Iroquois refer their origin to a point near [[oswego|Oswego Falls]]. They boldly

[002-116]affirm that their people were here taken from a subterranean vault, by the

[002-117]Divine Being, and conducted eastward along the river Ye-no-na-nat-che,

[002-118]*going around a mountain*, now the Mohawk, until they came to where it

[002-119]discharges into a great river running toward the mid-day sun, the Hudson,

[002-120]and went down this river and touched the bank of a *great water*, while

[002-121]the main body returned by the way they came, and as they proceeded

[002-122]westward, originated the different tribes composing their nation; and to

[002-123]each tribe was assigned the territory they occupied, when first discovered

[002-124]by the whites. [Footnote: Account by David Cusick, as contained in

[002-125]Schoolcraft's report. Mr. S. regards this account correct as indicating

[002-126]the probable course of their migrations.]

[002-127]The Senecas, the fifth tribe of the Iroquois, were directed in their

[002-128]original location, to occupy a hill near the head of [[canandaigua|Canandaigua]] lake.

[002-129]This hill, called Ge-nun-de-wa, is venerated as the birth place of their

[002-130]nation. It was surrounded anciently by a rude fortification which formed

[002-131]their dwelling in time of peace, and served for a shelter from any sudden

[002-132]attack of a hostile tribe. Tradition hallows this spot on account of the

[002-133]following very remarkable occurrence.

[002-134]Far back in the past, the inhabitants of the hill Genundewa, were

[002-135]surprised on awaking one morning, to behold themselves surrounded by an

[002-136]immense serpent. His dimensions were so vast as to enable him to coil

[002-137]himself completely around the fort. His head and tail came together at its

[002-138]gate. There he lay writhing and hissing, presenting a most menacing and

[002-139]hideous aspect. His jaws were widely extended, and he hissed so terribly

[002-140]no one ventured to approach near.

[002-141]The inhabitants were thus effectually blockaded. Some endeavored, but in

[002-142]vain, to kill this savage monster. Others tried to escape, but his

[002-143]watchful eyes prevented their endeavors. Others again sought to climb over

[002-144]his body, but were unable; while others still attempted to pass by his

[002-145]head, but fell into his extended jaws. Their confinement grew every day

[002-146]more and more painful, and was rendered doubly annoying by the serpent's

[002-147]breath, which was very offensive.

[002-148]Their situation drove them at length to an extremity not to be endured.

[002-149]They armed themselves with hatchets, and clubs, and whatever implements of

[002-150]war they could find, and made a vigorous sally upon their dreadful foe,

[002-151]but, alas! were all engulfed in his terrific jaws.

[002-152]It so happened that two orphan children remained, after the destruction

[002-153]which befell the rest. They were directed by an oracle to make a bow of a

[002-154]certain kind of willow, and an arrow of the same, the point of which they

[002-155]were to dip in poison, and then shoot the monster, aiming so as to hit him

[002-156]under his scales.

[002-157]In doing this, they encountered their adversary with entire success. For

[002-158]no sooner had the arrow penetrated his skin, than he presently began to

[002-159]grow sick, exhibiting signs of the deepest distress. He threw himself into

[002-160]every imaginable shape, and with wonderful contortions and agonizing

[002-161]pains, rolled his ponderous body down along the declivity of the mountain,

[002-162]uttering horrid noises as he went, prostrating trees in his course, and

[002-163]falling finally into the lake below.

[002-164]Here he slaked his thirst, and showed signs of great distress, by dashing

[002-165]about furiously in the water. Soon he vomited up the heads of those whom

[002-166]he had swallowed, and immediately after expired and sank to rise no more.

[002-167][Footnote: As related to the author by Col. Wm. Jones.]

[002-168]From these two children, as thus preserved, the Seneca nation are said to

[002-169]have sprung.

[002-170]So implicitly has this tradition been received by the Senecas, that it has

[002-171]been incorporated into the solemnities of their worship, and its

[002-172]remembrance continued from one generation to another by the aid of

[002-173]religious rites. Here they were formerly in the habit of assembling in

[002-174]council, and here their prayers and thanksgivings were offered to the

[002-175][[great-spirit|Great Spirit]], for having given them birth, and for rescuing their nation

[002-176]from entire destruction.

[002-177]In speaking of this to the whites, they point to the barren hillside, as

[002-178]evincing the truth of the story, affirming that one day the forest trees

[002-179]stood thick upon it, but was stripped of them by the great serpent as he

[002-180]rolled down its declivity. The round stones found there in great

[002-181]abundance, resembling in size and shape the human head, are taken as

[002-182]additional proof, for they affirm that these are the heads disgorged by

[002-183]the serpent, and have been petrified by the waters of the lake. [Footnote:

[002-184]The author remembers well that in conversation with a Seneca Indian on

[002-185]this point, he seemed to take it as quite an affront that doubts should be

[002-186]expressed by the white people as to the reality of this occurrence.]

[002-187]If nearness of locality will justify a glance of the eye for a moment, to

[002-188]an object not directly in the line of our pursuit, we might survey in

[002-189]passing a bold projecting height, not far from the hill Genundewa, marked

[002-190]by a legend which draws a tear from the eye of the dusky warrior, or sends

[002-191]him away in a thoughtful mood, with a shade of sadness upon his usually

[002-192]placid brow. The story is not of the same character and is of a more

[002-193]recent date than that of the serpent, but is said to be of great

[002-194]antiquity. It has been written with great beauty by Col. Stone, and as we

[002-195]are authorized, we present it in his own language.

[002-196]"During the wars of the Senecas and Algonquins of the north, a chief of

[002-197]the latter was captured and carried to Genundewa, whereon a fortification,

[002-198]consisting of a square without bastions, and surrounded by palisades, was

[002-199]situated. The captive though young in years, was famed for his prowess in

[002-200]the forest conflict, and nature had been bountiful to his person in those

[002-201]gifts of strength and symmetry, which awaken savage admiration. After a

[002-202]short debate he was condemned to die on the following day, by the slow

[002-203]torture of empalement. While he was thus lying in the cabin of death, a

[002-204]lodge devoted to condemned prisoners, the daughter of the sachem brought

[002-205]him food, and struck with his manly form and heroic bearing, resolved to

[002-206]save him or share his fate. Her bold enterprise was favored by the

[002-207]uncertain light of the gray dawn, while the solitary sentinel, weary of

[002-208]his night-watch, and forgetful of his duty, was slumbering. Stealing with

[002-209]noiseless tread to the side of the young captive, she cut the thongs

[002-210]wherewith his limbs were bound, and besought him in breathless accents to

[002-211]follow her.

[002-212]"The fugitives descended the hill by a wooded path conducting to the lake;

[002-213]but ere they reached the water, an alarm whoop, wild and shrill, was heard

[002-214]issuing from the waking guard. They tarried not, though thorny vines and

[002-215]fallen timber obstructed their way. At length they reached the smooth

[002-216]beach, and leaping into a canoe previously provided by the considerate

[002-217]damsel, they plied the paddle vigorously, steering for the opposite shore.

[002-218]Vain were their efforts. On the wind came cries of rage, and the quick

[002-219]tramp of savage warriors, bounding over rock and glen in fierce pursuit.

[002-220]The Algonquin with the reckless daring of a young brave, sent back a yell

[002-221]of defiance, and soon after the splash of oars was heard, and a dozen war

[002-222]canoes were cutting the billows in their rear. The unfortunate lovers on

[002-223]landing, took a trail leading in a western direction over the hills. The

[002-224]Algonquin, weakened by unhealed wounds, followed his active guide up the

[002-225]aclivity, with panting heart and flagging pace; while his enemies, with

[002-226]the grim old sachem at their head, drew nearer and nearer. At length

[002-227]finding further attempts at flight useless, she diverged from the trail,

[002-228]and conducted her lover to a table-crested rock that projected over a

[002-229]ravine or gulf, one hundred and fifty feet in depth, the bottom of which

[002-230]was strewed with misshapen rocks, scattered in rude confusion. With hearts

[002-231]nerved to a high resolve, the hapless pair awaited the arrival of their

[002-232]yelling pursuers. Conspicuous by his eagle plume, towering form and

[002-233]scowling brow, the daughter soon descried her inexorable sire, leaping

[002-234]from crag to crag below her. He paused abruptly when his fiery eye rested

[002-235]on the objects of his pursuit. Notching an arrow on the string of his

[002-236]tried and unerring bow, he raised his sinewy arms—but ere the missile was

[002-237]sent, Wun-nut-hay, *the Beautiful*, interposed her form between her father

[002-238]and his victim. In wild appealing tones she entreated her sire to spare

[002-239]the young chieftain, assuring him that they would leap together from the

[002-240]precipice rather than be separated. The stern old man, deaf to her

[002-241]supplication, and disregarding her menace, ordered his followers to seize

[002-242]the fugitive. Warrior after warrior darted up the rock, but on reaching

[002-243]the platform, at the moment when they were grasping to clutch the young

[002-244]brave, the lovers, locked in fond embrace, flung themselves

[002-245]'From the steep rock, and perished.'

[002-246]"The mangled bodies were buried in the bottom of the glen, beneath the

[002-247]shade of everlasting rocks; and two small hollows, resembling sunken

[002-248]graves, are to this day pointed out to the curious traveler, as the burial

[002-249]place of the lovers." It is a sweet, wild haunt, the sunbeams fall there

[002-250]with softened radiance, and a brook near by gives out a complaining

[002-251]murmur, as if mourning for the dead. [Footnote: Mr. Stone adds in a note—

[002-252]"This interesting legend was derived many years ago from a Seneca chief of

[002-253]some note, named Chequered Cap, and was communicated to me by W. H. C.

[002-254]Hosmer, Esq., of [[avon|Avon]]. On the top of Genundewa the remains of an Indian

[002-255]orchard are visible, a few moss-grown and wind-bowed apple trees still

[002-256]linger, sad, but fitting emblems of the wasted race by whom they were

[002-257]planted."]

[002-258]Let us return to the inquiry we were pursuing. Of the origin of the

[002-259]Iroquois confederacy, some traditionary accounts have been given, which

[002-260]represent the different tribes as dwelling for a time, in the separate

[002-261]locations assigned them, independent of each other. Here they increased in

[002-262]valor, skill and knowledge, suited to their forest home. At length

[002-263]becoming numerous, rival interests arose among them, which did not exist

[002-264]when they were small and feeble. They fell into contention, and wasted and

[002-265]destroyed each other. Each tribe fortified his own position, and dwelt in

[002-266]constant fear of being surprised and overcome by his neighboring foe.

[002-267]At length one of their [[sachem-chieftainship|sachems]], distinguished for his wisdom and address,

[002-268]proposed that they should cease from a strife, which was only destroying

[002-269]themselves, and unite their energies against the Alleghans, the

[002-270]Adirondacks, the Eries, and other ancient and warlike tribes, who were

[002-271]their superiors in their isolated and divided condition. Already weary of

[002-272]their unprofitable conflicts, the proposal was received with favor, and

[002-273]Ato-tar-ho, an Onandaga chieftain, unequalled in valor, and the fame of

[002-274]whose skill and daring was known among all the tribes, became the leading

[002-275]spirit of this confederacy, and by common consent was placed at its head.

[002-276]So fully did experience demonstrate the wisdom of this arrangement, that

[002-277]they used every means to strengthen the bands of their union, and by the

[002-278]most solemn engagements of fidelity to each other, they became the Ko-noshi-o-ni, or United people. [Footnote: Schoolcraft's Report.]

[002-279]How long this confederacy had existed before their discovery by the

[002-280]whites, is unknown. There is a tradition which places it one age, or the

[002-281]length of a man's life, before the white people came to this country.

[002-282][Footnote: Pyrlaus, a missionary at the ancient site of Dionderoga, or

[002-283]Fort Hunter, writing between 1742 and 1748, gives this as the best

[002-284]conjecture he could form, from information derived from the Mohawks. It is

[002-285]thought however that this time is too short, to account for the degree of

[002-286]development attained by the Iroquois, in their united capacity, at the

[002-287]time of their first discovery by the whites.]

[002-288]The union of these several tribes was the means of securing their preeminence over the other Indians in this country. Their individual traits

[002-289]are thus very fittingly represented;—"in their firm physical type, and in

[002-290]their energy of character, and love of independence, no people among the

[002-291]aboriginal race have ever exceeded, if any has equalled the Iroquois."

[002-292][Footnote: Schoolcraft.] They occupied a region surpassed by no other on

[002-293]the continent, for grandeur and beauty united, and inherited from this or

[002-294]some other source, a mental constitution of noble structure, which placed

[002-295]them in the fore-front of their race, and when united, no tribe on this

[002-296]continent could stand before them. This has served to render their

[002-297]history, a matter of earnest and interesting inquiry.
