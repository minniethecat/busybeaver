# Holland Land Company

## Overview

The Holland Land Company was a syndicate of Dutch investors who acquired vast tracts of land in western New York through pre-emptive rights during the late eighteenth century. Hubbard refers to them as "a company of gentlemen in Holland" who held interests in the Genesee country, and their agents were active participants in the land speculation that progressively dispossessed the Seneca of their territories. (011-112)

The company was one of several major land speculation enterprises — alongside the New York and Genesee Land Company, the Pulteney Association, and later the Ogden Land Company — that collectively acquired the vast majority of Seneca territory in the Genesee Valley. Their agents attended treaty councils, negotiated land cessions, and promoted white settlement of former Seneca lands. (005-049)

## Structure and Governance

The Holland Land Company was organized as a business syndicate with capital raised from investors in the Netherlands. Its American operations were managed by agents stationed in New York, including William Bayard of New York who represented the company's interests at the Big Tree treaty negotiations. (011-229) The company's agent worked alongside Thomas Morris at these councils, representing the interests of the Holland investors. (011-230)

Like other land companies of the period, the Holland Land Company operated through the system of pre-emptive rights — purchasing the right of first purchase from states and earlier grantees, then pressing the Seneca to agree to land sales. The company's pre-emptive rights later passed to the Ogden Land Company, creating a chain of speculative interests that ultimately led to the sale of the Buffalo Creek Reservation. (018-012)

The company's agents also included Colonel Aaron Burr, who became associated with the company during his time in western New York. (018-012)

## Historical Role

The Holland Land Company played a significant role in the acquisition of Seneca lands. Its agent was present at the negotiations for the Big Tree Treaty of 1797, representing Dutch investor interests alongside the Phelps and Gorham interests and the State of Massachusetts. The company accumulated pre-emptive rights to large portions of the Genesee Valley. (011-292)

The company's operations were part of the broader process by which Seneca territory passed into white ownership. The "Genesee country was prized by the Indians," and the pressure from land companies like the Holland syndicate contributed to the progressive erosion of Seneca landholdings. (011-071) The company's pre-emptive rights were eventually sold to the Ogden Land Company, which continued the pursuit of Seneca lands into the 1830s and 1840s. (018-012)
