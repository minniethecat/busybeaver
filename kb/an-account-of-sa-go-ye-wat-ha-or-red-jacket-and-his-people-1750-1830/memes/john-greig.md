# John Greig

## Biography

John Greig was a resident of [[canandaigua|Canandaigua]] who acted for the [[ogden-land-company|Ogden Company]] in its purchases of Seneca land. (012-318)

When Mr. Greig was holding a council with a view to purchase some of the smaller Indian reservations lying along the [[genesee-river|Genesee river]], he was opposed step by step by the persistent efforts of [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]. (012-320)

## Role and Influence

Notwithstanding the opposition, Mr. Greig was successful in securing the extinguishment of their title to about eighty thousand acres of their land. (012-322)

When the time came for signing the deed, Greig told the orator that, as he had been opposed to the sale, he need not attach his name to it:

> "As you have been opposed to the sale of the land, you need not have your name attached to the deed." (012-324)

But Red Jacket would hear to nothing of the kind, and insisted upon signing it, seeming to take pride in having his name appended to the paper. (012-326)

## Key Events

- **Purchase of the Genesee reservations**: Over Red Jacket's step-by-step resistance, Greig secured the extinguishment of Seneca title to about eighty thousand acres of their land. (012-322)

- **A source for the account**: The author drew on his conversation with the Hon. John Greig of Canandaigua. (012-327) A story in the papers had represented Greig as arguing a case in opposition to Red Jacket, (012-330) but its principal statements proved fictitious, and Greig never argued the case as represented. (012-333)

- **"A ready writer"**: Greig took down a speech from the interpreter and read it to the orator, who was much pleased with its correctness and bestowed on him an Indian name signifying "a ready writer." (012-336)
