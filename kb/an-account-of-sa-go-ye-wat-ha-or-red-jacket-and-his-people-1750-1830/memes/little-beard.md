# Little Beard

## Biography

Little Beard was a Seneca chief active during the Revolutionary War and early federal periods. Hubbard's account places him among the Seneca leaders who participated in the border warfare that devastated the New York and Pennsylvania frontiers during the Revolution. His English name, "Little Beard," was one of many such names assigned to Native leaders by American settlers — names that often obscured the individuals' Seneca identities while making them recognizable figures in frontier accounts. (003-004)

Little Beard's role in Hubbard's narrative is more notable for what it indicates about Seneca politics than for his individual actions. He appears in connection with charges brought by Joseph Brant, suggesting his involvement in the complex web of alliances and rivalries that characterized Iroquois politics during and after the Revolution. (P04-014) He was a familiar presence at treaty councils, recorded alongside "Little Billy, Little Beard, and Red Jacket, taking part in the discussion" of crucial land negotiations. (011-258)

## Role and Influence

As a Seneca chief during the Revolutionary era, Little Beard was one of many leaders who had to navigate the impossible choices presented by the war. The Iroquois Confederacy fractured under the pressure of choosing sides — the Mohawks and Senecas largely allied with the British, the Oneidas and Tuscaroras with the Americans — and chiefs like Little Beard found themselves leading warriors in a conflict that, regardless of its outcome, would diminish Iroquois power.

## Key Events

- **Revolutionary War (1775–1783)**: Participated in the Seneca war effort alongside British forces, during the border campaigns that shaped postwar Iroquois-American relations.
- **Post-War Adjustment**: Like many Seneca leaders, navigated the transition from war to treaty diplomacy in the 1780s–1790s.
