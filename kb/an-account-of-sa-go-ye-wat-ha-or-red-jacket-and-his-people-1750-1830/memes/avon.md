# Avon

## Geography

Avon is situated in the Genesee Valley, in present-day Livingston County, New York. The settlement lies west of the Genesee River, near the confluence of several smaller streams. Hubbard describes "the present site of West Avon" (011-200) as a location where an Indian village once stood, indicating that the area was inhabited by the Seneca before and during the early period of white settlement.

The Avon Springs — natural mineral springs in the vicinity — were a notable local feature. Hubbard mentions "the hotels at Avon Springs" (013-071), suggesting that by the time of his writing, Avon had become a destination for visitors drawn by the purported health benefits of the waters.

## Historical Significance

Avon's significance in Hubbard's narrative derives primarily from the Indian villages that preceded white settlement and from the early residents who recorded Seneca traditions. The area near West Avon was the site of "another Indian village" (011-200) within the band of settlements that lined the Genesee Valley — part of the dense Seneca occupation of the region before the land cessions of the 1790s.

The locality of Ca-na-wau-gus, "near West Avon" (013-004), was a Seneca settlement of particular importance. It was here that some of the events of the narrative unfolded, and where Seneca leaders continued to gather even as white settlement encroached on their territory.

## Key Events at This Location

George Hosmer, Esquire, of Avon was a key informant for Hubbard's history. He provided details about the Genundewa fortress and other Seneca sites, and his recollections of the Seneca chief who was "the chief of the clan" at Avon (020-199) preserved details that would otherwise have been lost. Hosmer's presence in Avon, and his willingness to share his knowledge with Hubbard, made the settlement a node in the preservation of Seneca history.

Mr. Parrish, another Avon-area resident, pointed out to early travelers the Indian remains and shared his own recollections of the Seneca period. (013-071) These resident-historians, living at the interface between the Seneca past and the American present, were essential to Hubbard's project of recording the history of Red Jacket's people before it passed from living memory.
