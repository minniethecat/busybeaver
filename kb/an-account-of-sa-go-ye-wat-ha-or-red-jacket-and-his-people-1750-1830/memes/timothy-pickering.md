# Timothy Pickering

## Biography

Timothy Pickering was born in Salem, Massachusetts, in 1745. A Harvard graduate and veteran of the Revolutionary War, he rose through the ranks of the new federal government to become Postmaster General, Secretary of War, and ultimately Secretary of State under Presidents Washington and Adams. But in Iroquois history, Pickering is remembered primarily for a single assignment: his work as federal commissioner to negotiate a comprehensive treaty with the Six Nations in 1794. (008-186)

The Treaty of Canandaigua, signed on November 11, 1794, was the most significant diplomatic agreement between the United States and the Iroquois Confederacy. It established a permanent annuity of four thousand five hundred dollars for the Seneca, recognized Iroquois land rights within defined boundaries, and created the framework for the federal-tribal relationship that would govern subsequent interactions. Pickering's personal credibility and his willingness to treat the Iroquois leaders as sovereign equals earned him the respect of figures like Red Jacket and Cornplanter — a rare achievement for an American official in that era.

After his service as Secretary of State (1795–1800), Pickering returned to Massachusetts, where he served in Congress and became a prominent Federalist critic of the Jefferson and Madison administrations. He died in 1829 at age 83.

## Role and Influence

Pickering's role at Canandaigua was as a representative of the federal government, but his approach differed markedly from the land-speculator diplomacy that would characterize Thomas Morris's mission three years later. Pickering came not to purchase land but to establish peace — to formalize the relationship between the United States and the Six Nations after the chaos of the Revolutionary War and the subsequent treaty confusion.

The Canandaigua council brought together "large delegations from the Six Nations," as Pickering himself described it, around a council fire kindled specifically for the purpose. (018-029) Pickering's address to the assembled chiefs, which Hubbard records, emphasized the sincerity of the United States' intentions and the permanence of the peace being established. The wampum belts exchanged at Canandaigua — including the "Great Belt" that Red Jacket later displayed — served as the Iroquois record of these commitments, more durable and trustworthy, in their view, than the written treaty text.

The annuity Pickering established — four thousand five hundred dollars annually — became the economic foundation of Seneca-American relations for decades. It was a sum that later agents like Thomas Morris would attempt to increase as leverage for additional land cessions, but the essential framework Pickering created proved remarkably durable: the Treaty of Canandaigua remains legally in force today, and the annuity cloth (a symbolic distribution of calico fabric) is still delivered annually to the Seneca Nation.

## Key Events

- **Treaty of Canandaigua (1794)**: Pickering's signal achievement — a comprehensive peace and land-rights treaty between the United States and the Six Nations. The treaty established the Seneca annuity, formalized boundaries, and created the federal-tribal relationship that would structure all subsequent interactions. (008-186)
- **Service as Secretary of State (1795–1800)**: Oversaw American foreign policy during the Quasi-War with France and the early years of the republic's international relations.
