# Nathaniel Gorham

## Biography

Nathaniel Gorham enters Hubbard's account as one of the two men whose names became fixed to a vast land speculation in the Genesee country. Next in the race of competition for those broad and fertile lands appear the names of Oliver Phelps and Nathaniel Gorham. (005-168)

They were the acknowledged representatives of a considerable body of men who were ambitious of securing an interest in what was regarded as one of the most valuable regions of the new world. (005-169)

The company of which Phelps and Gorham were the leading spirits purchased the pre-emptive right of Massachusetts in the spring of 1788. Mr. Phelps then went to the ground, where he succeeded in convening a council of the Indians for the sale of their lands at Buffalo Creek in July of the same year. (005-181)

## Role and Influence

The tract secured through this negotiation became known as the Phelps and Gorham Purchase. It contained by estimation two million and six hundred thousand acres, in one of the finest regions of country in the new world. (005-202)

The Seneca at the treaty strenuously resisted parting with any land west of the [[genesee-river]]. Yet, with a view of furnishing "a piece of ground for a mill yard" at the Genesee Falls, they were finally persuaded to assent to a boundary that included a tract twelve miles square west of that river. (005-190)

Gorham's speculation did not remain intact for long. [[robert-morris]], the distinguished financier of the Revolution, afterward became owner of the greater part of this purchase, as well as of the pre-emptive right to the remaining part of western New York. (005-205)

The Purchase thus formed one link in the chain of transactions by which the Seneca domain passed, tract by tract, into the hands of white proprietors during the lifetime of [[red-jacket]].

## Key Events

- **Phelps and Gorham Purchase (1788)**: With Oliver Phelps, Gorham led the company that bought the pre-emptive right of Massachusetts and, at the Buffalo Creek council of July 1788, obtained a cession of a tract twelve miles square west of the Genesee. (005-181) (005-190)

- **Buffalo Creek Council (1819)**: Hubbard records that at a later council near Buffalo, in the summer of 1819, the Hon. Nathaniel Gorham of Canandaigua represented the interests of the state of Massachusetts. (018-021)
