# Neutrality Policy

## Definition

The policy of neutrality was a strategic doctrine of the Iroquois Confederacy, developed over generations of contact with competing European colonial powers. Rather than permanently aligning with any single European power, the Confederacy sought to maintain a position that preserved its sovereignty, leveraged competition among the colonial powers, and minimized direct Iroquois involvement in European conflicts. (003-064)

Neutrality was not passivity — it was an active diplomatic posture requiring constant negotiation, careful management of relationships, and the willingness to shift alliances when circumstances demanded. The Iroquois understood that their strategic position between rival European empires was both their greatest vulnerability and their greatest source of leverage.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The neutrality policy drew on deep traditions of Iroquois diplomacy. The Condolence Ceremony, the Covenant Chain, and the council system all embodied the principle that disputes should be resolved through deliberation and mutual accommodation rather than violence. Neutrality extended this principle from the internal relations of the Confederacy to its external relations with the colonial powers. (016-091)

The policy was tested to its breaking point by the American Revolution. Hubbard records how the Iroquois initially attempted to remain neutral, but the pressures of war — the presence of British forts on their territory, the proximity of American settlements, and divisions within the Confederacy itself — made neutrality unsustainable. The "favorable disposition of the Iroquois" was courted by both sides. (003-064) (003-082)

## Role in the Narrative

The failure of Iroquois neutrality is one of the tragic threads running through Hubbard's account. The Confederacy's division — with some nations siding with the British and others attempting neutrality — shattered the political unity that had been the foundation of Iroquois power. The post-Revolutionary treaties, particularly the Treaty of Fort Stanwix, were the price paid for this failure: a defeated Confederacy, its neutrality abandoned, forced to cede territory to the victorious Americans.

Red Jacket's post-war career as a diplomat can be seen as an effort to reconstruct a viable neutrality policy under new conditions — to find a middle path between assimilation and resistance that would preserve what remained of Iroquois sovereignty.
