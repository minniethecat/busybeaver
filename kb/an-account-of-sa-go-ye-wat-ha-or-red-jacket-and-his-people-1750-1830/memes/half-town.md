# Half Town

## Biography

Half Town was a Seneca chief of [[cornplanter|Cornplanter]]'s following, remembered in Hubbard's account chiefly for the appeal he and his fellow chiefs carried to the federal government over the lands of his people. He led a clan settled on a portion of land south of Lake Erie, bordering upon Pennsylvania. (006-233)

With a view to obtaining some concession in their favor, Cornplanter, Half Town, and Big Tree visited [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], then the seat of the general government, very soon after the [[council-of-tioga-point|council at Tioga Point]]. (006-228)

## Role and Influence

The chiefs were especially anxious to obtain the restoration of the land occupied by Half Town and his clan, which they represented as the ground on which Half Town and all his people lived — chiefs who had always been, and still were, dissatisfied with the [[treaty-of-fort-stanwix|treaty at Fort Stanwix]]. (006-235) Their appeal was simple and touching:

> "They grew out of this land, and their fathers grew out of it, and they cannot be persuaded to part with it. We therefore entreat you to restore to us this little piece." (006-235)

President [[george-washington|Washington]] responded with kindness. He reminded them that the treaty at Fort Stanwix had been fully confirmed at Fort Harmar in 1789, and that it was not within his province to annul it, yet he assured them that Half Town and his people should not be disturbed in the peaceful occupancy of the land in question. (006-244)

## Key Events

- **Delegation to Philadelphia**: Received in a friendly manner and given generous gifts, the chiefs returned home satisfied that "the ruler of the thirteen fires" would do them no injustice. (006-248) Before leaving they were engaged to go in company with [[colonel-proctor|Colonel Proctor]]. (006-250)

- **War of 1812 on the Niagara frontier**: Captain Half Town was numbered among the principal chiefs — with [[farmer-s-brother|Farmer's Brother]], [[red-jacket|Red Jacket]], [[little-billy|Little Billy]], and [[captain-pollard|Pollard]] — who led the Seneca warriors in the fighting on the Niagara. (016-268) In a council held beforehand they covenanted not to scalp or murder, and afterward treated their prisoners with humanity, committing no wanton cruelties on the dead. (016-270)
