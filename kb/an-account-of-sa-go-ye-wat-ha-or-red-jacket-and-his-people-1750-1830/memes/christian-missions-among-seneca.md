# Christian Missions Among the Seneca

## Definition

Christian missions among the Seneca were organized efforts by Protestant missionaries — primarily from New England and New York — to convert the Seneca people to Christianity, establish schools, and introduce European-style agriculture.


These missions began in earnest in the 1790s and intensified after the establishment of the Buffalo Creek Reservation, where missionaries from the Quaker, Congregationalist, and other denominations worked among the relocated Seneca population. (019-217)

The missionary presence was not merely religious: it was intertwined with the broader American policy of "civilizing" Native peoples — teaching them to farm individually rather than hunt communally, to adopt English, and to accept the authority of the United States government.


The missionaries were often the most visible agents of this assimilationist project, and Red Jacket recognized them as such, opposing them not on theological grounds but on political ones — he saw their work as an existential threat to Seneca sovereignty and identity.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The arrival of Christian missionaries challenged the foundations of Seneca religious life. Traditional Seneca spirituality centered on the Great Spirit, the annual cycle of thanksgiving ceremonies, dream interpretation, and the medicine societies.


These practices were embedded in every dimension of Seneca existence — governance, healing, warfare, and social organization. To abandon them for Christianity was not simply to change one's private beliefs but to repudiate the entire cultural framework that had sustained the Seneca for centuries.

The missionaries' approach often involved appealing to individual conscience — a method that was itself corrosive to the communitarian values of Iroquois society.


By converting individuals and forming congregations, the missions created alternative communities within the nation that competed with traditional kinship and clan structures for loyalty and resources. The resulting tension was neither purely religious nor purely political: it was a contest over the very definition of what it meant to be Seneca.

## Role in the Narrative

In Hubbard's narrative, the missionary question is the thread that runs through Red Jacket's later years. The orator's famous speech to the Boston missionary society in 1805 was not his first word on the subject nor his last — it was the most polished expression of a position he had maintained for decades.


When missionaries asked permission to work among his people, Red Jacket replied that their predecessors had come with the Bible in one hand and, in effect, the instruments of dispossession in the other.


(015-102) He reminded his listeners that the French missionaries who had earlier worked among the Iroquois had done so while their countrymen seized Iroquois lands and hunted Iroquois game. (015-146)


The conflict reached its climax in the late 1820s. Red Jacket's own wife converted to Christianity and left him. (021-078) Young King, once his ally, became the leader of the Christian party. (021-103) In 1827, the Christian faction succeeded in deposing Red Jacket from his chiefly office — an unprecedented act that stunned the Seneca world.


Red Jacket traveled to Washington to appeal for reinstatement, and after a council of chiefs reviewed the matter, he was restored to his position.


(021-157) But the schism was never fully healed, and at his death in 1830, Red Jacket's funeral arrangements reflected the unresolved tension: he asked to be buried neither with pagan rites nor with Christian ones, but according to whatever customs his family preferred. (021-281)

Red Jacket's opposition to missionaries was political, not merely personal:

> Red Jacket asked the missionary why, if his religion was so good, it had not made the white men better in their dealings with the Indians. He recounted a long history of broken treaties, stolen lands, and corrupt traders, and asked whether the God who tolerated such conduct among his professed followers could truly be the Great Spirit whom the Seneca had always known.
>
> (015-124)

The missionary presence split the Seneca into opposing factions:

> The nation became divided into two great parties: the Christian party, led by Young King and others who had embraced the new faith, and the Pagan party, under Red Jacket, who clung to the traditions of their fathers. The breach between them grew wider with each passing year, until every council became a scene of bitter contention.
>
> (019-217)
