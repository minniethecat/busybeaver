# Clan System

## Definition

The Iroquois clan system was a matrilineal social organization that structured every aspect of traditional life — from political representation to marriage regulation, from inheritance to hospitality. Descent was traced through the mother's line, and each Iroquois nation was divided into named clans, each bearing the emblem of a totemic animal: Wolf, Bear, Turtle, Beaver, Deer, Snipe, Heron, Hawk, and Eel. (006-030)

A person's clan identity determined their place within the larger Confederacy. Council seats (sachemships) belonged to specific clans within specific nations, and a sachem could be nominated only by the clan mother of the clan that held the title. (010-226)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The clan provided the fundamental building block of Iroquois society. It functioned as an extended family that transcended national boundaries: a Wolf clansman of the Seneca nation recognized a Wolf clanswoman of the Mohawk nation as kin. This cross-cutting network of clan loyalties helped bind the Five Nations together and served as a check against internecine conflict. (011-054)

Clan mothers — the senior women of each clan — held authority that was remarkable for its time. They nominated the sachems who represented their clan in council and could remove (dehorn) a sachem who proved unworthy. Hubbard's account, while focused primarily on male political actors, records the influence of clan-based authority in the decisions of the council. (010-221) The clan system also governed land use, with territory held collectively by the women of the clan.

## Role in the Narrative

The clan system forms the unspoken architecture of much of Hubbard's narrative. When Red Jacket addresses the council, he speaks not as an individual but as a representative of his clan and nation. The persistence of clan-based governance through the crises of war, land loss, and missionary pressure is one of the undercurrents of the book. (020-146)

The clan system also explains the stakes of the missionary controversy that dominates Hubbard's middle chapters. The missionaries sought not just individual conversions but the dismantling of the traditional social order — including the authority of clan mothers — which they viewed as pagan. Red Jacket's defense of tradition was, in part, a defense of the clan-based social fabric that had sustained the Iroquois for centuries.
