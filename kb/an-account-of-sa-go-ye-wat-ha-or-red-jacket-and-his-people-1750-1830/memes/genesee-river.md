# Genesee River

## Geography

The Genesee River flows northward from its source in northern Pennsylvania through the western portion of New York State, emptying into Lake Ontario near present-day Rochester. Its most notable feature is the Genesee Falls (now within Letchworth State Park), a series of dramatic cascades that Hubbard describes as "scenery rarely surpassed in sublimity and grandeur." (011-037)

The river's valley, beginning a few miles south of Rochester and extending to the village of Mount Morris, is described as "almost of uniform width" with broad, rich flats "bordered on either side by hills that rose gradually to their summit." (011-013) Above Mount Morris, the terrain becomes increasingly rugged: deep ravines, overhanging cliffs, and mountain elevations create a landscape that Hubbard considered among the most romantic in the region. At the confluence of the Genesee and Canaseraga Creek, the flats spread into extensive table lands before the valley narrows again into wild gorges. (011-020)

## Historical Significance

The valley of the Genesee was, in Hubbard's words, "a favorite resort of the Indian." (011-005) The river's banks were lined with Indian trails, connecting villages and the headquarters of chiefs at short intervals. The Seneca had occupied this territory since their expansion westward during the Beaver Wars of the 17th century, and the valley was among their most cherished lands.

For land speculators and settlers in the post-Revolutionary period, the Genesee country held an irresistible allure. The region became a focal point of competing claims and intense negotiation throughout the 1790s. Hubbard describes the white settlers' perspective: "If the Genesee country was prized by the Indian, it was regarded with a jealous eye by the white man." (011-071) The New York and Genesee Land Company and the Niagara Genesee Company were formed to acquire these lands from the Seneca. (005-049)

## Key Events at This Location

The pivotal event in the Genesee's history was the **Council at Big Tree** (August 1797), held at the present site of Geneseo. Here, Thomas Morris negotiated with the Seneca for the sale of their remaining lands west of the Genesee River. Red Jacket played a central role in these negotiations, opposing the sale. Hubbard describes the dramatic confrontation: "They were soon deeply engaged in discussing the Genesee question." (011-171)

Earlier, during the Sullivan-Clinton Campaign of 1779, American forces destroyed numerous Seneca towns in the Genesee country, devastating crops and forcing the inhabitants to flee. (014-151) This military action fundamentally altered the demographic landscape of the valley.

Throughout the 1820s, additional pressures mounted on the Seneca to cede their Genesee lands, culminating in the removal of many Seneca from the valley by 1825. (012-241)
