[009-001]Indian appropriation—Embassy sent West—Instructions—Medal presented to

[009-002][[red-jacket|Red Jacket]]—Military suits—Close of conference—[[george-washington|Washington]]'s parting

[009-003]words—Visit of [[joseph-brant|Thayendanegea]]—Council at Au Glaize—Result—Another

[009-004]commission—Indian diplomacy—Washington's letter to Mr. Jay—Commission

[009-005]goes West—Various interviews—Result of council—Re-organization under

[009-006]General Wayne—Ready for action—Advice of Little Turtle—Wayne's battle

[009-007]and victory—Treaty of peace.

[009-008]While these Indian chiefs were at [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], a bill was passed by

[009-009]Congress, and ratified by the president, appropriating fifteen hundred

[009-010]dollars annually, for the benefit of the Iroquois, in purchasing for them

[009-011]clothing, domestic animals, implements of husbandry, and for encouraging

[009-012]useful artificers to reside in their villages.

[009-013]They were engaged also to go on a pacific embassy to the hostile Indians

[009-014]of the West, and assure them of the friendly disposition of the United

[009-015]States toward them;—that they want nothing which belongs to the hostile

[009-016]Indians;—that they appointed commissioners to treat with them for their

[009-017]lands, and give them a large quantity of goods;—that a number of chiefs

[009-018]signed the deeds, and from the reports of the commissioners, it was

[009-019]supposed the lands had been fairly obtained;—that under this supposition

[009-020]large tracts had been sold, and hence difficult to restore again; but as

[009-021]the United States desire only what is just, they will attentively hear the

[009-022]complaints of the western Indians;—they will re-examine the treaties, and

[009-023]inquire into the manner in which they were conducted;—and if the

[009-024]complaints of the western Indians, appear to be well founded, the United

[009-025]States will make them ample compensation for their lands. They will do

[009-026]more;—so far from desiring to injure, they would do them good; they would

[009-027]cheerfully impart to them that knowledge, and those arts, by which they

[009-028]propose to increase the happiness, and promote the welfare of the Six

[009-029]Nations.

[009-030]It was during this visit that President Washington, in token of his

[009-031]friendship and esteem, gave Red Jacket a large silver medal bearing his

[009-032]likeness, which he ever after preserved with much care, and took great

[009-033]pride in wearing.

[009-034][Illustration: GEORGE WASHINGTON PRESIDENT. 1792.]

[009-035]General Knox, the secretary of war, directed also that a military suit of

[009-036]clothes be given to each member of the deputation, including a cocked hat,

[009-037]as worn by the officers of the United States army. When Red Jacket's suit

[009-038]was presented to him he eyed it carefully, and rather admiringly, but

[009-039]requested the bearer to inform General Knox that the suit would hardly

[009-040]become him, as he was not a war-chief but a sachem, the [[sachem-chieftainship|sachems]] being

[009-041]civil, rather than military officers. He desired therefore that another

[009-042]suit be prepared, which would accord better with the relation he sustained

[009-043]to his people; at the same time declaring the one sent very good, and

[009-044]manifesting a disposition to retain it, until the other was prepared. A

[009-045]plain suit was accordingly prepared and brought to him, and with this he

[009-046]seemed to be highly pleased. The bearer tarrying a little, and manifesting

[009-047]a readiness to carry back the other suit, Red Jacket coolly and rather

[009-048]playfully remarked, that though the present suit was more in keeping with

[009-049]his character as sachem, it nevertheless, occurred sometimes, in cases of

[009-050]emergency, that the sachems also went to war, and as it would then be very

[009-051]becoming and proper for him to wear it, he was happy to have one in case a

[009-052]circumstance of this kind should occur.

[009-053]These Indian chiefs were all highly gratified with the attention shown

[009-054]them, during this visit to the general government. They were especially

[009-055]pleased with the interest that had been taken in the improvement of their

[009-056]people, and the pledges they had received of aid in carrying out the

[009-057]benevolent designs entertained toward them. And they all, Red Jacket with

[009-058]the rest, were favorably impressed with the views of Washington, in

[009-059]desiring to introduce among them the improvements of civilized life.

[009-060]These conferences were brought to a close on the thirtieth of April, and

[009-061]President Washington in a concluding speech, said to them,—"When you

[009-062]return to your country, tell your nation that it is my desire to promote

[009-063]their prosperity, by teaching them the use of domestic animals, and the

[009-064]manner that the white people plough and raise so much corn, and if upon

[009-065]consideration, it would be agreeable to the nation at large, to learn

[009-066]those arts, I will find some means of teaching them at some places within

[009-067]their country, as shall be agreed upon." [Footnote: Irving's Life of

[009-068]Washington.]

[009-069]The government had taken special pains also to secure the attendance of

[009-070]the celebrated Thayendanegea or Brant, with this deputation of friendly

[009-071]chiefs. The invitation, though a pressing one, was declined, and not

[009-072]without reason. For besides the powerful influence exerted over him by the

[009-073]officers of the British government in [[canada|Canada]], who strenuously opposed his

[009-074]coming, it has since been ascertained that he was the leading spirit who

[009-075]directed with so much success to the Indians, the onslaught upon General

[009-076]St. Clair's army, the preceding fall. Hence his own feelings could not

[009-077]have been of the most friendly character. He was, nevertheless, induced to

[009-078]visit the seat of government during the month of June following, and

[009-079]pledged himself to exert his influence in an effort to secure peace for

[009-080]the United States, with the Indians at the West.

[009-081]A very large Indian council, composed of delegates from many and some of

[009-082]them very distant nations, was held at Au Glaize, on the Miami of Lake

[009-083]Erie, in the autumn of 1792. A large delegation from the [[iroquois-confederacy|Six Nations]],

[009-084]friendly to the United States, was present and took part in the

[009-085]deliberations. Red Jacket was the principal speaker, and strenuously

[009-086]advocated the settlement of their difficulties, by peaceful negotiations

[009-087]instead of war.

[009-088]The Shawanese as strenuously advocated the continuance of hostilities.

[009-089]They taunted the Six Nations with having induced them to form a great

[009-090]confederacy, a few years before, and of having come to the council now,

[009-091]"with the voice of the United States folded under their arm;"—referring

[009-092]to the belt which was significant of their embassy.

[009-093]The Shawanese, Miamis and Kickapoos were addicted to horse-stealing, and

[009-094]while hostilities were continued, they reaped from this source, their

[009-095]greatest harvests.

[009-096]Captain Brant on account of sickness was unable to be present, and it may

[009-097]be noticed that from this time on, his efforts to form a North-western

[009-098]Indian Confederacy, were very sensibly remitted. He no doubt found there

[009-099]were so many conflicting interests and national jealousies in the way, as

[009-100]to render the project comparatively hopeless. But more than all, he had

[009-101]depended upon the following of the entire body, composed of the Six

[009-102]Nations, and when he saw them coming largely under the influence of the

[009-103]United States, he could realize that the strength and permanence of his

[009-104]contemplated position, were so seriously affected, as to render its

[009-105]attainment extremely doubtful. The addition of the entire Iroquois family,

[009-106]to the proposed confederation, would have brought into it an element of

[009-107]intellectual superiority, and their long established polity of acting in

[009-108]concert, would have been of essential service among forces that were wild

[009-109]and chaotic. And we are not surprised that the diversion effected among

[009-110]them, should have changed somewhat the views of the distinguished

[009-111]Thayendanegea.

[009-112]No decisive action was reached at this council, but an agreement was made

[009-113]to suspend hostilities during the winter, provided the United States would

[009-114]withdraw their troops from the west side of the Ohio; and another council

[009-115]was appointed to meet at the Miami Rapids during the following spring.

[009-116]The [[iroquois-delegation-to-washington|Iroquois delegation]] forwarded to our government a report of the

[009-117]service they had rendered, the action taken by the council, and the

[009-118]agreement to meet in the spring, and requested that agents might be sent,

[009-119]"who were men of honesty, not land-jobbers, but men who love and desire

[009-120]peace. We also desire that they may be accompanied by some Friend, or

[009-121]Quaker, to attend the council."

[009-122]On the 19th of February, 1793, General Benjamin Lincoln, Beverly Randolph

[009-123]and [[timothy-pickering|Colonel Pickering]] were commissioned by the president to attend the

[009-124]great Indian council at Miami Rapids, in the ensuing spring.

[009-125]Meanwhile the Indians, dissatisfied with the views of the president, as

[009-126]transmitted by the Six Nations, held another council at Au Glaize in

[009-127]February, and framed a very explicit address to the Six Nations, affirming

[009-128]they would listen to no proposition from the United States, that did not

[009-129]concede the Ohio river, as the boundary line between them, and the Indian

[009-130]country. They desired the United States to be fully apprised of this,

[009-131]before sending their delegation; and they notified the Six Nations of a

[009-132]private council at Miami Rapids, before meeting the American

[009-133]commissioners, to adjust their opinions, so as to speak but one language

[009-134]at the council; they further declared their intention not to meet the

[009-135]commissioners at all, until assured they had authority to conclude a

[009-136]treaty on this basis.

[009-137]In this determination they were encouraged, and sustained by the British

[009-138][[british-indian-department|Indian Department of Canada]]. President Washington, in a letter to Mr. Jay,

[009-139]our minister in London, writing in 1794, very clearly sets forth the work

[009-140]thus accomplished.—He says:—"There does not remain a doubt, in the mind

[009-141]of any well informed person in this country, not shut against conviction,

[009-142]that all the difficulties we encounter with the Indians, their

[009-143]hostilities, the murder of helpless women and children, along our

[009-144]frontiers, result from the conduct of agents of Great Britain in this

[009-145]country. In vain is it then for its administration in Britain to disavow

[009-146]having given orders which will warrant such conduct, whilst their agents

[009-147]go unpunished; while we have a thousand corroborating circumstances, and

[009-148]indeed as many evidences, some of which cannot be brought forward, to

[009-149]prove that they are seducing from our alliances, and endeavoring to remove

[009-150]over the line, tribes that have hitherto been kept in peace and friendship

[009-151]at great expense, and who have no causes of complaint, except pretended

[009-152]ones of their creating; whilst they keep in a state of irritation the

[009-153]tribes that are hostile to us, and are instigating those who know little

[009-154]of us or we of them, to unite in the war against us; and whilst it is an

[009-155]undeniable fact, that they are furnishing the *whole with arms,

[009-156]ammunition, clothing, and even provisions to carry on the war*, I might go

[009-157]farther, and if they are not much belied, add, *men in disguise*."

[009-158][Footnote: Marshall's Washington.]

[009-159]The commissioners of the United States appointed to confer with the Indian

[009-160]tribes at the West, proceeded on their way, arriving at [[fort-niagara|Niagara]] the latter

[009-161]part of May, 1793. Here they were very kindly entertained by Governor

[009-162]Simcoe until the council was ready to receive them.

[009-163]While here they were visited by a large deputation from the council at

[009-164]Miami Rapids, who desired an explicit answer to the inquiry whether they

[009-165]were authorized to run and establish a new boundary? Which they answered

[009-166]in the affirmative, at the same time reminding the Indians that in almost

[009-167]all disputes there were wrongs on both sides, and that, at the approaching

[009-168]council, both parties must expect to make some concessions.

[009-169]This reply was well received and sanguine hopes were entertained of a

[009-170]favorable termination of their mission.

[009-171]The Indians returned again to their council at Miami, and the

[009-172]commissioners supposing they would now be prepared to receive them,

[009-173]proceeded on their voyage westward. Arriving at the mouth of [[detroit|Detroit]] river

[009-174]they were obliged to land, being forbidden by the British authorities to

[009-175]proceed any farther toward the place of meeting.

[009-176]They were met here by another Indian deputation, bringing a paper with a

[009-177]written statement of their determination, to make the Ohio the boundary

[009-178]line between the Indian country and the United States, and requiring the

[009-179]latter, if sincere in their desires for peace, to remove their settlements

[009-180]to the south side of that river. To this the commissioners were desired to

[009-181]give an explicit written answer.

[009-182]They replied, referring to the understanding from their conference at

[009-183]Niagara, that some concessions were to be made on both sides, and giving a

[009-184]brief history of the treaties by which a title had been acquired to land

[009-185]north of the Ohio, on the faith of which, settlements had been formed

[009-186]which could not be removed; hence they answered explicitly.—"*The Ohio

[009-187]river cannot be designated as the boundary line.*"

[009-188]They expressed the hope that negotiations might proceed on the basis of

[009-189]these treaties, closing with some concessions, and liberal offers for some

[009-190]lands still held by the Indians.

[009-191]The debate at this council, it is said, ran high. Thayendanegea, and

[009-192]others of the Six Nations were strenuous in their advocacy of peace. The

[009-193]offer of the commissioners to establish a boundary line that would include

[009-194]the settlements already made north of the Ohio, they regarded as

[009-195]reasonable, and that farther concessions ought not to be required. Quite a

[009-196]number of tribes were influenced to adopt this view, which at one time it

[009-197]was thought would prevail. But there were certain ruling spirits present

[009-198]determined to make no concession, and the council broke up without

[009-199]allowing the commissioners, or any other white person, not in sympathy

[009-200]with Britain, to be present.

[009-201]Previous to the holding of this council, the army had been re-organized

[009-202]under the command of General Anthony Wayne, an officer of untiring energy

[009-203]and vigilance; a larger number of soldiers had been called into the field,

[009-204]and as they were placed under a severe discipline, to inure them to the

[009-205]dangers and hardships of the campaign, it was undertaken with flattering

[009-206]prospects of success.

[009-207]Pittsburgh had been made the place of rendezvous; but fearing the

[009-208]influence of an encampment near a town, and wishing to inspire in his

[009-209]soldiers a feeling of self reliance, General Wayne, on the 27th of

[009-210]November, 1792, marched his army to a point twenty-two miles distant on

[009-211]the Ohio, which he called Legionville, fortifying it and taking up his

[009-212]quarters there for the winter.

[009-213]On the 30th of April, 1793, as spring had opened, he broke up his garrison

[009-214]at Legionville, and led his army down the river, to Fort Washington, its

[009-215]site being that of the present beautiful and flourishing city of

[009-216]Cincinnati.

[009-217]Here he remained while the negotiations were going on with the Indians at

[009-218]the West. As soon as they were ended and the result known, he took a more

[009-219]advanced position, marching in October in the direction pursued by,

[009-220]General St. Clair, to a point on the south-west branch of the Miami, six

[009-221]miles beyond Fort Jefferson, and eighty from Fort Washington, which he

[009-222]fortified and called Greenville.

[009-223]On the 23d of December, a detachment of the army commanded by Major

[009-224]Burbeck took possession of the ground where the army of General St. Clair,

[009-225]two years before on the 4th of the preceding November, had sustained a

[009-226]terrible defeat. Here they gathered up sadly and sacredly the bones that

[009-227]marked this as a place of human slaughter, put in order the field-pieces

[009-228]that were still upon the ground, served them with a round of three times

[009-229]three, over the remains of their fallen comrades, and erected a fortress,

[009-230]appropriately naming it Fort Recovery.

[009-231]The army at different points had skirmishes with the enemy that were not

[009-232]serious, but they served to create confidence and inspire courage in the

[009-233]minds of the soldiers.

[009-234]It was not until the 20th of August, 1794, that General Wayne had a

[009-235]regular engagement with the Indians. Yet like a true gladiator he had been

[009-236]preparing for the struggle, and his wariness, which had gained for him the

[009-237]title of "*Black Snake*" may be gathered from the speech of Little Turtle,

[009-238]chief of the Miamis, and one of the most active and brave warriors of his

[009-239]time. He counselled his countrymen to think favorably of the proposals of

[009-240]peace offered by General Wayne before giving them battle; saying,—"We

[009-241]have beaten the enemy twice under separate commanders. We cannot expect

[009-242]the same good fortune always to attend us. The Americans are now led by a

[009-243]chief who never sleeps. The night and the day are alike to him; and during

[009-244]all the time he has been marching on our villages, notwithstanding the

[009-245]watchfulness of our young men, we have never been able to surprise him.

[009-246]There is something that whispers to me,—*it would be prudent to listen to

[009-247]his offers of peace*."

[009-248]But this counsel was rejected by the Indians, who determined to give

[009-249]battle to the Americans the next day. They fought in the vicinity of a

[009-250]British fort, which Governor Simcoe of Canada had caused to be erected at

[009-251]the foot of the rapids of the Miami emptying into the lakes, far within

[009-252]the acknowledged territory of the United States.

[009-253]The ground occupied by the Indians was well chosen, being a thick wood,

[009-254]where were old fallen trees that marked the track of some ancient

[009-255]hurricane, where the use of cavalry would be impracticable, a place suited

[009-256]to afford them shelter and well adapted to their peculiar mode of warfare.

[009-257]But the order of General Wayne to advance with trailed arms, and rouse the

[009-258]Indians from their covert at the point of the bayonet, and when up deliver

[009-259]a close and well directed fire on their backs, followed by a brisk charge,

[009-260]so as not to give them time to load again; was executed so promptly, and

[009-261]with so much effect that the Indians were driven in one hour more than two

[009-262]miles, and soon dispersed in terror and dismay, leaving the ground in full

[009-263]and quiet possession of the victorious army.

[009-264]This battle, which terminated within reach of the British guns, decided

[009-265]the fate of the campaign. The Indians after this were dispirited and

[009-266]unable to make a general rally. The distrust awakened by the coolness of

[009-267]their supposed friends, the gates of whose fort remained unopened while

[009-268]they were fleeing thither for a covert, served not less than the victory

[009-269]to dishearten them, and incline their thoughts toward peace.

[009-270]The few days spent by the army on the battle ground after its victory,

[009-271]were occupied in destroying the property of the Indians in that vicinity,

[009-272]including also the extensive possessions of Colonel McKee, an officer of

[009-273]the British Indian Department, whose influence had been exerted in

[009-274]promoting these hostilities, whose effects were now being experienced. The

[009-275]fort itself was poised in the General's mind, as was also the torch of the

[009-276]gunner, who was only restrained by his commanding officer from firing upon

[009-277]Wayne, who, as he thought came too near, in making his observations on one

[009-278]of His Majesty's forts. Prudence prevailed. The fighting was confined to a

[009-279]war of words in a spirited correspondence between General Wayne, and the

[009-280]officer in command of the fort.

[009-281]General Wayne after laying waste their principal towns in this region,

[009-282]continued in the Indian country during the following year, bringing his

[009-283]campaign to a close by a treaty with the North-western tribes, which was

[009-284]entirely agreeable to the wishes of the United States.
