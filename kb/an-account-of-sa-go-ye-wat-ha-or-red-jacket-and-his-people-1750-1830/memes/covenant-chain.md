# Covenant Chain

## Definition

The Covenant Chain was the central metaphor and institutional framework of Iroquois-British diplomacy from the late seventeenth through the eighteenth centuries. It described an alliance in which the Iroquois Confederacy and the British Crown were bound together like links in a silver chain — each agreement, treaty renewal, and exchange of wampum adding a new link and brightening those that preceded it. (016-091)

The Covenant Chain was not a single treaty but a continuous diplomatic relationship, regularly renewed through council meetings and the exchange of gifts and wampum belts. When the chain needed maintenance — when grievances accumulated or misunderstandings threatened — the parties would meet to "polish the chain," restoring its original brilliance through renewed pledges of friendship and mutual defense.

## Context in Iroquois Culture

For the Iroquois, the Covenant Chain embodied a relationship of sovereign equals — two peoples bound by mutual obligation, not by subordination. This understanding of alliance as covenant, not conquest, was deeply rooted in Iroquois diplomatic tradition. The wampum belts that recorded each renewal of the chain were stored and periodically "read" to recall the terms of the agreement, serving as a constitutional memory of the alliance.

Hubbard's narrative reflects how the Covenant Chain shaped Iroquois expectations of all diplomatic relationships. When the Iroquois later negotiated with the United States, they understood those negotiations through the framework that the Covenant Chain had established — as agreements between sovereign peoples, not submissions to a conquering power.

## Role in the Narrative

The Covenant Chain background informs the Revolutionary War chapters of Hubbard's account. When the Iroquois Confederacy divided in its sympathies — with some nations siding with the British and others attempting neutrality — the division was fundamentally about how to honor the obligations of the Covenant Chain. (003-082) The collapse of the British-Iroquois alliance after the Revolution left the Confederacy without its traditional diplomatic partner and forced it to negotiate an entirely new relationship with the United States.

Red Jacket's diplomacy in the post-Revolutionary period can be understood as an attempt to establish a new covenant with the United States that preserved Iroquois sovereignty — an attempt to forge an American Covenant Chain that would protect his people as the British chain once had.
