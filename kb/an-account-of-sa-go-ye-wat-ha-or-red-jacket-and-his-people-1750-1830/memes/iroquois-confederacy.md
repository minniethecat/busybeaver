# Iroquois Confederacy

## Definition

The Iroquois Confederacy, also known as the Haudenosaunee or the League of the Iroquois, was a political and cultural union of originally five Native American nations — the Mohawks, Oneidas, Onondagas, Cayugas, and Senecas — later joined by the Tuscaroras. The Senecas, of whom Red Jacket was a member, were the fifth tribe to join the confederacy. (P04-006)

Hubbard describes the name "Iroquois" as "quite justly" applied to this confederacy. (002-051) The confederacy was governed by a council of sachems whose authority was rooted in consensus, with its traditions handed down orally across generations. (002-259)

## Context in Iroquois Culture

The Iroquois held a distinctive position among the Native American tribes of the eastern woodlands. They occupied a broad territory, and their confederated system gave them unusual military and diplomatic strength. (002-011) Hubbard recounts that in oratory, the Iroquois developed a level of skill that drew comparisons to the great Athenian orator. (001-145)

The Senecas, as the westernmost nation of the confederacy, acted as the "keepers of the western door." Their role included territorial defense against incursions from the west. Within the confederacy, the Seneca were directed in their migrations and settlements according to traditional protocols. (002-127)

Though the Iroquois were a warlike people, Hubbard emphasizes that their political sophistication — including a functioning representative council system — was remarkable for its time. (001-169) The confederacy's development, in its "united capacity," drew praise from European observers who questioned whether any contemporary European institution had equalled it. (002-286) (002-291)

## Role in the Narrative

The Iroquois Confederacy provides the essential political framework for understanding Red Jacket's career. The confederacy's councils — convened at places like Oswego (003-177), Fort Stanwix (004-046), and Buffaloe Creek — are the stages on which the book's key diplomatic encounters unfold. Red Jacket rose to prominence as an orator within these councils, and his struggle to preserve Iroquois sovereignty unfolds in the context of the confederacy's dealings with both the British and the newly independent United States.

At the outbreak of the Revolutionary War, the confederacy found itself divided in its sympathies, with factions inclining toward both the British and the American causes. (003-082) The war would prove devastating: "the arm of the Iroquois" was broken, and a feeling of deep concern pervaded their councils. (004-100) Following the war, the confederacy's land was surrendered under duress, and the once-formidable confederacy entered a period of decline and loss. (003-364)

Hubbard's narrative traces the confederacy's repeated diplomatic encounters with the United States, from the council at German Flats through the pivotal Treaty of Fort Stanwix and the council at Tioga Point, where the "ancient glory of the Iroquois" was contrasted with their diminished present. (003-074) (004-137)
