# British Indian Department

## Overview

The British Indian Department was the administrative body responsible for managing Great Britain's relations with the Native nations of North America. From its headquarters in Canada and its network of frontier posts — Fort Niagara, Detroit, Oswego, and others — the Department conducted diplomacy, distributed gifts, and coordinated military alliances with the Iroquois and other Native peoples. (006-084)

Throughout the period of Hubbard's narrative, the British Indian Department functioned as both a diplomatic channel and a source of political manipulation. Its officers — superintendents, agents, and interpreters — wielded significant influence over Iroquois politics, often working at cross-purposes with American treaty commissioners.

## Structure and Governance

The Department was headed by the Superintendent General of Indian Affairs, a position held during the Revolutionary War period by Sir John Johnson and later by others operating from Canada. Subordinate officers — including deputy superintendents, interpreters, and storekeepers — staffed the frontier posts.

The Department's primary instrument of influence was the annual distribution of goods — blankets, arms, ammunition, clothing, and provisions — to Native communities. Hubbard notes that "a strong party in Canada, whose control over the Indians was gained by bestowing upon them annually large amounts of goods" (005-157) actively worked to maintain Iroquois allegiance to Britain.

## Historical Role

During the Revolutionary War, the British Indian Department coordinated the military alliance between the Crown and the Iroquois Confederacy. Sir John Johnson convened a "general council of the Iroquois at Oswego" (003-177) to secure their support. The Department's officers accompanied Iroquois war parties and supplied the posts from which raids were launched against American settlements.

After the war, the Department continued to operate from British posts retained in violation of the Treaty of Paris — a source of persistent friction with the United States. Hubbard describes how "British officers at Niagara, and runners" (007-133) maintained communication with Seneca leaders, distributing gifts and encouraging resistance to American demands for land cessions.

The British Indian Department in Canada maintained ambitions that extended well beyond the war's end. Hubbard records that its officials, "while they deprecated hostilities" (008-369), continued to pursue policies designed to keep the Iroquois dependent on British support. Governor John Graves Simcoe of Upper Canada erected a fort on American soil (009-250), demonstrating the Department's willingness to challenge American territorial claims.

The War of 1812 brought the Department's influence to a climax — and then to an end. After the war, the US-British border was permanently affirmed, and the British Indian Department's ability to intervene in Iroquois affairs south of the Great Lakes was permanently curtailed.
