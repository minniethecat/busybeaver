# Lake Ontario

## Geography

Lake Ontario is the easternmost and smallest of the five Great Lakes, lying between present-day New York State to the south and the Canadian province of Ontario to the north. Hubbard describes the Iroquois territory as extending "from the western end of Lake Ontario" southward (005-024), indicating the lake's role as a natural northern boundary. The lake is connected to Lake Erie by the Niagara River, and its outlet — the St. Lawrence River — provides the water route to the Atlantic.

The lake's southern shore was the locus of significant Iroquois settlement, particularly the Seneca villages and the strategic British posts at Niagara and Oswego. The lake's waters carried canoes bearing diplomats, warriors, refugees, and traders throughout the period of Hubbard's narrative.

## Historical Significance

For the Iroquois, Lake Ontario was both a boundary and a highway. The lake's southern shore marked the northern limit of their territory, but its waters connected them to the wider world of Great Lakes diplomacy and trade. The British posts situated on Lake Ontario — Oswego to the east, Niagara to the west — ensured that British influence was never more than a day's paddle from Iroquois villages.

During the Revolutionary War, the lake served as a British supply corridor, with vessels moving troops and provisions between the posts. After the war, the lake continued to function as a line of communication between British Canada and the Iroquois nations, complicating American efforts to assert exclusive sovereignty over the region.

## Key Events at This Location

The Sullivan-Clinton Campaign of 1779, which devastated Seneca towns in the Genesee Valley, concluded with American forces retiring "to the head of Lake Ontario" (017-228) — a distance of thirty miles from the Genesee River. This retirement underscores the lake's function as a strategic reference point and fallback position throughout the military campaigns in Iroquois territory.

During the War of 1812, Lake Ontario was a contested naval theater. British forces "retired to Lake Ontario" (017-291) after being driven from their positions, using the lake as both a defensive barrier and a base for renewed operations. The lake's presence shaped the military geography of the entire Niagara frontier.

The lake's shore was also a place of refuge. Hubbard mentions an Indian village situated "at a place on Lake Ontario" (004-117) four miles east of the Niagara River, one of many settlements that dotted the shoreline and drew sustenance from the lake's fisheries.
