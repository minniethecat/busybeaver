# Sullivan-Clinton Campaign (1779)

## Background

The Revolutionary War on the frontier was a brutal conflict of raids and counter-raids. In 1778, Loyalist and Iroquois forces under John Butler and Joseph Brant attacked settlements in the Wyoming Valley and Cherry Valley, killing and capturing hundreds of settlers. These attacks, combined with the Iroquois alliance with Britain, convinced General Washington that the Iroquois had to be neutralized.

Washington ordered a two-pronged invasion of Iroquois territory. General John Sullivan would advance from Easton, Pennsylvania, while General James Clinton moved down the Susquehanna from the Mohawk Valley. Their objective was "the total destruction and devastation of their settlements."

## Proceedings

The campaign was ruthlessly efficient. Sullivan's force of over 4,000 men — the largest American army assembled to that point — moved through the Finger Lakes region, encountering limited resistance. The Iroquois, forewarned, evacuated their villages rather than engage in a pitched battle against superior numbers.

Hubbard records that the Americans destroyed towns throughout "the Genesee country in 1779" (014-151), burning longhouses, cutting down orchards, and destroying crops in the field. The Seneca heartland — the beautiful Genesee Valley described with such admiration by Hubbard — was transformed into a wasteland. Canandaigua, the principal Seneca settlement, was among the towns burned.

## Outcome and Aftermath

The campaign achieved its military objective: the Iroquois Confederacy's capacity to wage war was crippled. Thousands of refugees fled to British posts, particularly Fort Niagara, where they spent a devastating winter dependent on British provisions. Hubbard describes how refugees "were taken to Niagara" (013-137) in the aftermath of the destruction.

The psychological impact was equally profound. The Iroquois, who had dominated their region for centuries, could no longer defend their homeland. The campaign permanently altered the demographic and political landscape of western New York, opening the way for the land cessions that would accelerate in the 1780s and 1790s.

The Sullivan Campaign cast a long shadow over subsequent treaty negotiations. The Iroquois who sat at the council fire at Fort Stanwix in 1784 were the survivors of a nation whose homes had been burned, whose crops had been destroyed, and whose confidence in their ability to resist American expansion had been shattered.
