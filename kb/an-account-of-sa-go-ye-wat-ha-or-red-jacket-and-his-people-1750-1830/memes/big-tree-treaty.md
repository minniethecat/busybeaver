# Big Tree Treaty (1797)

## Background

By 1797, pressure on the Seneca to sell their remaining lands in the Genesee Valley had become overwhelming.

Robert Morris of Philadelphia had purchased the pre-emption rights to Seneca territory from Massachusetts, and his son Thomas Morris was dispatched to secure the actual cession. The land companies — the New York and Genesee Land Company and the Niagara Genesee Company — stood ready to profit from the transaction. (005-049)

The Seneca were deeply divided. Some leaders, recognizing the impossibility of resisting American expansion, saw accommodation as the only viable path. Red Jacket led the opposition, determined to preserve what remained of the Seneca estate.

## Proceedings

The council convened at Big Tree — the present site of Geneseo — in August 1797. "The Indians were mostly in the vicinity of Big Tree, a region which at that time was the great center of the Seneca population." (011-213) Thomas Morris, described as "the agent for the land companies," (011-174) pressed the case for cession with persistence and strategic acumen.

Red Jacket led the opposition. "Red Jacket, or Big Tree, as principal chief was at the head of the opposition," Hubbard records.

(011-197) The negotiations were protracted and contentious. "It was not long before they were deeply engaged in discussing the Genesee question." (011-171) Red Jacket resisted step by step, but the combined weight of Morris's persuasion, internal Seneca divisions, and the implicit threat of forcible dispossession proved decisive.

The most dramatic moment came when Red Jacket, frustrated with the proceedings, declared "I now cover up the council fire" — a unilateral attempt to end the negotiations. Farmer's Brother immediately objected, citing the protocol that "he only who kindles the council fire, has a right to cover it up." (011-309)

## Outcome and Aftermath

The treaty, signed on September 15, 1797, transferred approximately 3.5 million acres to the land companies. The Seneca retained eleven reservations, including Buffalo Creek, which became the center of Seneca political life. Hubbard captures the mood after "the breaking up of the council at Big Tree" (012-006) as one of somber resignation.

In the aftermath, the Seneca "began to realize the magnitude of the transaction" (014-008) they had been compelled to make. Red Jacket's opposition to the treaty, though unsuccessful, cemented his reputation as the foremost defender of Seneca sovereignty.

## Key Participants

- **Thomas Morris** — Agent for the land companies, principal negotiator

- **Red Jacket** — Leader of the Seneca opposition

- **Farmer's Brother** — Seneca leader who challenged Red Jacket's council fire maneuver

- **Cornplanter** — Seneca war chief, more conciliatory toward the Americans


## Participants and Roles

| Party | Representative | Position |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| United States / Land Companies | Thomas Morris | Purchase of Seneca lands west of Genesee River |
| Seneca Opposition | Red Jacket, Farmer's Brother | Against any land cession |
| Seneca Accommodationists | Cornplanter | Supported negotiated sale with guarantees |
| Interpreters | Horatio Jones, Captain Parrish | Facilitated communication |

Thomas Morris, the young American agent, faced fierce Seneca opposition. Red Jacket led the resistance:

> Red Jacket strenuously resisted this treaty. He regarded it as an invasion of the rights of his people — an attempt to wrest from them, by artful means, the last remnants of their ancient domain. His eloquence at this council fired the hearts of his countrymen, and the negotiations were more than once on the point of being broken off.
>
> (011-309)

The treaty was eventually signed, but not before a dramatic confrontation between Morris and the Seneca leaders:

> The discussion between Red Jacket and Thomas Morris was long and animated. The orator employed all his powers of persuasion to defeat the measure, but Morris, backed by the influence of the land companies and the weight of official authority, prevailed in the end.
>
> (011-317)
