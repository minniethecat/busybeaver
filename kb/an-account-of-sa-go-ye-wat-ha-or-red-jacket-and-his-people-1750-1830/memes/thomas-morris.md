# Thomas Morris

## Biography

Thomas Morris was born in 1771, the son of Robert Morris — the "Financier of the Revolution" and one of the wealthiest men in the new United States. The elder Morris had acquired vast tracts of land in western New York through speculative purchases, and in 1797 he dispatched his twenty-six-year-old son Thomas to negotiate the purchase of Seneca lands at the Genesee River. What followed became one of the most dramatic episodes in Seneca-American diplomacy: the Treaty of Big Tree. (011-309)

Morris arrived at Buffalo Creek in the summer of 1797, bearing presents and annuity commitments intended to persuade the Seneca to sell. He found himself facing an adversary of formidable skill: Red Jacket, who understood that Morris's eagerness to acquire land was his greatest weakness. The negotiations were protracted and contentious, with Morris distributing gifts strategically to Seneca leaders more inclined to sell — Cornplanter among them — while Red Jacket worked to hold the line against any cession.

After the Big Tree negotiations, Morris continued his career as a public figure in New York. But his reputation in the historical record is inextricably tied to the land transaction that bore his father's ambition and his own diplomatic persistence.

## Role and Influence

Morris's role at Big Tree was that of a buyer representing immense economic interests — the Holland Land Company, backed by his father's fortune, sought to open western New York to white settlement. The Seneca land in question encompassed much of the Genesee Valley, some of the most fertile territory remaining under Iroquois control after the Revolutionary War land cessions. (011-309)

Morris's tactics blended generosity and pressure. He brought ten thousand dollars in presents, distributed liberally among the Seneca leaders gathered at the council. (011-309) He appealed to those chiefs, like Cornplanter, who believed that selling land in exchange for guaranteed annuities was the only viable path forward. And he pressed the negotiations forward even when Red Jacket's procedural maneuvers — including the dramatic extinguishing of the council fire — threatened to collapse them entirely.

The treaty Morris secured was controversial then and remains so in retrospect. It transferred the bulk of Seneca territory west of the Genesee to American ownership, leaving the Seneca a fraction of their former domain. Hubbard's account reflects the deep misgivings that many contemporaries — and not only Native voices — felt about the transaction.

## Key Events

- **Treaty of Big Tree (1797)**: At age 26, Morris negotiated the purchase of most remaining Seneca lands west of the Genesee River. The treaty was signed at the Buffalo Creek council ground after weeks of tense negotiation. Red Jacket's opposition and the procedural drama of the "covered council fire" made this one of the most memorable treaty councils in Iroquois history. (011-309)
