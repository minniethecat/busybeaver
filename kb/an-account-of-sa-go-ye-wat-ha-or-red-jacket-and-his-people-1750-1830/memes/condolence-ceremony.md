# Iroquois Condolence Ceremony

## Background

At the heart of Iroquois diplomatic protocol lay the condolence ceremony — a ritual practice so fundamental that no council fire could be properly kindled without it. When a chief died, when a community suffered loss, or when delegates gathered for diplomatic deliberation, the condolence ceremony was the essential first act. Its purpose was profound: to "wipe away the tears" of the bereaved, to "open their ears" so they could hear clearly, and to "clear their throats" so they could speak truthfully. Only those freed from the distorting effects of grief — those whose tears had been "wiped away" (010-179) and whose blood had been "wiped out" (006-146) — could participate properly in the deliberations of the council fire.

## Proceedings

The ceremony followed a precise protocol. The condoling party approached the bereaved community or the assembled delegates bearing wampum belts — each belt corresponding to a specific symbolic act of condolence. The first belt "wiped away the tears," restoring clear vision. Subsequent belts addressed the ears and throat, and finally the heart — removing the "obstruction" that grief had placed there.

The ceremony was accompanied by formal speeches (010-192) in which the condoling chiefs invoked the Great Law of Peace and the example of Deganawida, the legendary founder of the Iroquois Confederacy, who had first instituted the condolence ritual to console Hiawatha after the loss of his daughters.

## Outcome and Aftermath

Hubbard records numerous instances where the condolence ceremony preceded important diplomatic events. Before the councils at Tioga Point, Canandaigua, and elsewhere, the "wiping away of tears" prepared the participants for the serious work of negotiation. The ceremony was not a mere formality — it established the spiritual and psychological conditions for honest deliberation.

The ceremony's endurance throughout the period of Hubbard's narrative — including its practice at Tioga Point (006-003) and Buffalo Creek (007-054) — of Hubbard's narrative testifies to the resilience of Iroquois cultural practices even as their political autonomy was progressively eroded. While land was ceded and sovereignty diminished, the condolence ceremony persisted — a living link to the traditions that had sustained the Confederacy for centuries. Hubbard records the shedding of tears and the ritual response at numerous councils: "tears from the eyes of the mourners" (010-192) and the chiefs' own tears at solemn moments. (013-058)
