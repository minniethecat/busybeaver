# Battle of Fallen Timbers (1794)

## Background

After St. Clair's disaster in 1791 (P04-034), President Washington appointed General Anthony Wayne to rebuild the American army on the frontier. Wayne spent two years training a professional force — the Legion of the United States — before advancing into the Ohio Country in 1794. The Western Confederacy, still formidable but diminished by the strain of prolonged warfare, prepared to meet him.

## Proceedings

On August 20, 1794, Wayne's forces engaged the Western Confederacy near a stand of trees felled by a tornado — the "fallen timbers" that gave the battle its name. Wayne's disciplined infantry broke the Native lines in a bayonet charge. The defenders fell back toward nearby Fort Miami, a British post, expecting their allies to provide cover. The British commander, unwilling to provoke a war with the United States, closed the gates.

The British refusal to assist their Native allies was a devastating betrayal that reverberated through Native communities across the frontier.

## Outcome and Aftermath

Wayne's victory broke the Western Confederacy. The subsequent Treaty of Greenville (1795) ceded most of present-day Ohio to the United States. For the Iroquois, Fallen Timbers demonstrated that British support could no longer be relied upon — a lesson that shaped the negotiations at Canandaigua in November 1794.

The battle also underscored a pattern that Hubbard documents throughout his narrative: British agents urged Native resistance but abandoned their allies when confrontation with the United States threatened.
