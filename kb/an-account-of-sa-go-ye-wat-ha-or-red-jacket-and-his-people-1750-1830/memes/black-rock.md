# Black Rock

## Geography

Black Rock was located on the eastern shore of the Niagara River, approximately three miles downstream from Buffalo and just north of the present-day Peace Bridge. Hubbard describes it as situated "about three miles below Black Rock, beginning at the mouth of a creek" (013-105), placing it within the broader Niagara frontier corridor. The location took its name from a distinctive dark rock formation along the riverbank.

The Black Rock Rapids — a section of turbulent water in the Niagara River — presented both a hazard and a strategic challenge. Military operations required navigating this "difficult pass" (017-035), which constrained the movement of troops and supplies across the river.

## Historical Significance

Black Rock gained military prominence during the War of 1812 as a contested crossing point between the United States and British Canada. The location was used to concentrate American stores and as a staging area for cross-river operations. Hubbard records that the British "burned the American stores, collected at Black Rock and at Buffalo" during a raid (016-248), demonstrating the location's logistical importance.

The "gallant defense made at Black Rock" (016-245) became a point of pride in the local memory of the war. American forces under General Peter B. Porter, a resident of Black Rock, conducted operations from this location, and Porter's presence ensured that the settlement remained a hub of military activity along the Niagara frontier.

## Key Events at This Location

During the War of 1812, Black Rock was the scene of significant military action. British forces crossed the Niagara River and attacked the American positions, burning the villages of "Black Rock and Buffalo" (017-021) in a devastating raid. The American army later used Black Rock as a crossing point for its own operations into Canada.

In December 1813, American forces crossed "the Niagara at Black Rock during the night" (017-070) as part of a broader campaign. The crossing was hazardous — the Black Rock Rapids required careful navigation — but successful crossings at this point allowed American forces to establish positions on the Canadian shore.

After the war, Black Rock remained an important location in Seneca-American relations. General Porter, who maintained his residence there, hosted Seneca delegations and was involved in treaty negotiations. The launch of a schooner bearing Red Jacket's name at Black Rock (020-090) testified to the enduring connections between the Seneca leader and the Niagara frontier community.
