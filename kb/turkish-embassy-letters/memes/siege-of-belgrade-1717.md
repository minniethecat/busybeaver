# Siege of Belgrade 1717

## Overview

The Siege of Belgrade (June–August 1717) was a decisive engagement of the Austro-Turkish War (1716–1718) in which Habsburg forces under Prince Eugene of Savoy captured the Ottoman fortress of Belgrade. It occurred during [[Lady Mary Wortley Montagu]]'s embassy to [[Constantinople]] and forms the military backdrop to her letters.

Montagu had passed through [[Belgrade]] months before the siege, on the [[Embassy Journey 1716–1717]], and her correspondence registers the shifting balance between Habsburg and Ottoman power — and the strain it placed on the [[janissaries]] and the sultan.

## Background

Belgrade was the most important Ottoman fortress on the Danube, controlling the frontier between the Habsburg and Ottoman empires. It had been under Ottoman control since 1521, with brief Habsburg occupations. Prince Eugene's victory at Peterwardein (1716) opened the way to Belgrade, and the siege that followed was among the largest operations in 18th-century southeastern Europe.

Key elements of the 1717 campaign:

- **Commander**: Prince Eugene of Savoy, fresh from victory at Peterwardein
- **Objective**: the Danube fortress of Belgrade, gateway to the Ottoman Balkans
- **Outcome**: Habsburg capture, formalised by the Treaty of Passarowitz (1718)

Travelling through the region in early 1717, Montagu saw first-hand the devastation the frontier war had already worked on Ottoman towns:

> We passed through Adam and Todowar, both considerable towns, when in the hands of the Turks, but now quite ruined. (023-003)

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

The siege and its outcome shaped Montagu's diplomatic world. Ottoman defeats weakened the sultan relative to the Janissaries and the European powers, altering the dynamics of embassy life. The Treaty of Passarowitz (1718) formalised Habsburg gains, including Belgrade, and marked a lasting shift in the Ottoman-Habsburg balance.

Montagu does not narrate the siege directly — she was in Constantinople by then — but she conveys the danger of the frontier she had crossed, a passage possible only under diplomatic protection:

> The journey we have made from Belgrade hither, cannot possibly be passed by any out of a public character. (027-002)

The war's effects remain visible in her observations of Ottoman political instability and the Janissaries' hold over the throne.
