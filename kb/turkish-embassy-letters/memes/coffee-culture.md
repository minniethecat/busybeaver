# Coffee Culture in the Ottoman Empire

## Overview

Coffee drinking was a ubiquitous social practice in the 18th-century Ottoman Empire, with dedicated coffee houses serving as centers of male sociability and the women's bath (*bagnio*) functioning as an analogous space where coffee and sherbet were consumed during social gatherings. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's letters capture both the material culture of coffee service — the elaborate silver coffee pots, the rituals of serving — and the social world built around the coffee house.

## Description

Coffee houses in the Ottoman Empire were far more than places to drink coffee. They were public gathering spaces where men conducted business, exchanged news, and socialized. Montagu encountered coffee houses throughout her journey, from Sophia to Adrianople to Constantinople.

The material culture of coffee service was notably elaborate among the elite. A grandee's procession might include a servant carrying "his silver coffee-pot, on a staff" alongside other displays of wealth (028-008). Coffee was served alongside sherbet (a sweet fruit drink) as part of the ritual of Ottoman hospitality.

Montagu observed that coffee houses were also spaces of surveillance. In one passage, she noted that if a man's "conduct be dropt in a coffee-house (for they have spies every where)" it would be reported — suggesting that coffee houses functioned as nodes in an informal information network (028-004).

The women's equivalent of the coffee house was the *bagnio*, or Turkish bath. Montagu explicitly described the bath as "'tis the women's coffee-house, where all the news of the town is told, scandal invented, &c." (026-003). Women would spend four to five hours at the bath, "some working, others drinking coffee or sherbet, and many negligently lying on their cushions" while their slaves attended to their hair (026-003).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu first encountered Ottoman coffee culture early in her journey, noting at Adrianople that there were public spaces "where people go to drink coffee, tea, &c. and enjoy one of the most beautiful prospects in the world" (003-002). The combination of coffee drinking with scenic views and gardens was a characteristic feature of Ottoman leisure that she returned to in multiple letters.

Her description of the women's bath as the "women's coffee-house" is one of her most analytically acute observations. By drawing this parallel, she recognized that Ottoman women — despite their exclusion from male coffee houses and many public spaces — had created their own equivalent institution where the same functions of news-sharing, social networking, and leisure were performed (026-003).

In Letter XXX, Montagu described the grand vizier's court, where attendants "generally attended by a great number of servants, who bring them coffee, sherbet, and perfumes" — indicating that coffee service was integral to Ottoman court ceremony as well as everyday sociability (030-007).

## Cultural Context

Coffee had been introduced to the Ottoman Empire in the 16th century and by Montagu's time was thoroughly integrated into Ottoman daily life. Coffee houses served as a "third place" between home and work — a function they would later serve in Europe as coffee culture spread westward.

The gender segregation of coffee houses (male spaces) and the bath (female space) reflects the broader organization of Ottoman public life. Montagu's recognition that women had created a parallel institution in the bath demonstrates her attentiveness to the ways Ottoman women exercised agency within their social constraints.

The Ottoman coffee house would later influence European coffee house culture, particularly in Vienna and Paris, though the European versions developed their own distinctive character as centers of intellectual and political discourse.
