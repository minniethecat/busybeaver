# Lady Mary Wortley Montagu

## Overview

Lady Mary Wortley Montagu (born Mary Pierrepont, 1689–1762) was an English aristocrat, writer, and poet whose Turkish Embassy Letters constitute the most significant English-language account of Ottoman society in the early 18th century.

As the wife of the British ambassador to the Ottoman Empire, she undertook the [[Embassy Journey 1716–1717]] overland to [[Constantinople]], recording Ottoman culture, medicine, and social life with unprecedented ethnographic precision.

She gained rare access to the [[Ottoman Seraglio]] and to the female spaces closed to male travellers. Her account of [[Smallpox Inoculation]] — which she introduced to Britain — marks her as a significant figure in the history of medicine.

## Biography

Mary Pierrepont was born in 1689 to Evelyn Pierrepont, later 1st Duke of Kingston-upon-Hull, and Lady Mary Feilding. She taught herself Latin in her father's library and began writing poetry at an early age. In 1712, she eloped with Edward Wortley Montagu, a Whig politician, beginning a marriage that was intellectually companionable but emotionally strained.

In 1716, Edward Wortley Montagu was appointed British ambassador to the Ottoman Empire. Lady Mary accompanied him overland through Europe to Constantinople, recording her observations in the series of letters that would become the Turkish Embassy Letters. She remained in the Ottoman Empire until 1718, when the couple returned to England.

After her return, Montagu became a prominent figure in London literary and court circles. She was a friend and correspondent of [[Alexander Pope]] (before their later falling-out), Joseph Addison, and William Congreve. Her later years were spent in self-imposed exile in Italy, and she died in London in 1762.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu is both the author and the central consciousness of the Turkish Embassy Letters. Her perspective — that of an educated English woman observing Ottoman society — is unique in 18th-century travel literature. Unlike male travelers, she gained entry to the bath (*bagnio*), the seraglio, and elite women's homes.

She was acutely conscious that her position as ambassador's wife gave her an authority no ordinary traveller could claim, and she framed her letters as the product of methodical enquiry rather than second-hand report:

> The desire I have to oblige you to the utmost of my power, has made me very diligent in my enquiries and observations. (027-002)

Writing from the banks of the Hebrus near [[Adrianople]], she reflected on the discipline of truthful observation that governed her correspondence — a self-conscious preference for fact over poetic fancy:

> 'Tis more than an ordinary discretion that can resist the wicked suggestions of poetry, in a place where truth, for once, furnishes all the ideas of pastoral. (030-007)

Her letters are addressed to correspondents partly disguised by initials. The most frequent — the "Countess of ——" who received 14 letters — is generally identified as her sister, [[Lady Mar]]. Others include [[Alexander Pope]], to whom five letters are addressed, and the Abbé Antonio Conti.

The summary of the letters' contents records the correspondence as running from her departure in 1716 to her return, "by Lady Mary Wortley Montague" (062-060). Pope contributed verses "to Lady Mary Wortley Montague" (062-061), testifying to her literary reputation among her contemporaries.
