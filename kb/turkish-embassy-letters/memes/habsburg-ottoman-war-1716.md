# Habsburg-Ottoman War 1716–1718

## Overview

The Habsburg-Ottoman War of 1716–1718 — also known as the Austro-Turkish War — was the military conflict that formed the geopolitical backdrop of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's embassy to Constantinople. Begun shortly before Montagu's departure from England and concluded by the Treaty of Passarowitz just as she was preparing to return, the war shaped every aspect of her journey: the militarized landscape she traveled through, the diplomatic calculations of the embassy community, and the political instability she observed within the Ottoman state.

## Major Engagements

The war was dominated by the brilliant campaign of Prince Eugene of Savoy, the Habsburg commander who had earlier defeated the Ottomans at Zenta (1697) and who now led the imperial army to a series of decisive victories:

- **Battle of Peterwardein** (August 1716): Prince Eugene's army destroyed a large Ottoman force, killing the grand vizier and opening the way for an advance on the Danube fortresses (021-005).
- **Siege of Temesvár** (October 1716): The last Ottoman stronghold in Hungary fell to Habsburg forces.
- **Siege of Belgrade** (June–August 1717): The decisive engagement of the war, in which Habsburg forces captured the most important Ottoman fortress on the Danube.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu traveled through the war zone in early 1717, and her letters register the visible traces of recent conflict — the village "plundered by the Turks" near Peterwardein, the unburied dead on the battlefield, the militarized landscape of the frontier (021-003). The war also affected the diplomatic world she inhabited: Ottoman military defeats weakened the sultan's position, emboldened the Janissaries, and altered the calculations of European ambassadors in Constantinople.

The Treaty of Passarowitz (July 1718), which concluded the war, was signed as Montagu prepared to depart Constantinople. The treaty formalized Habsburg gains — including Belgrade and the Banat of Temesvár — and marked a significant shift in the Ottoman-Habsburg balance of power.
