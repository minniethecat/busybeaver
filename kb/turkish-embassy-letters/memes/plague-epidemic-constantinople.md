# Plague Epidemic in Constantinople

## Overview

During Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's residence in Constantinople (1717–1718), the city experienced recurring plague outbreaks — a seasonal reality of Ottoman urban life that Montagu observed with the same ethnographic attention she brought to court ceremony and women's baths. Her descriptions of Ottoman responses to plague, and her comparison of Ottoman and European approaches to epidemic disease, constitute one of the most medically significant aspects of the Turkish Embassy Letters. Montagu's observation that plague was managed differently — and in some respects more effectively — in the Ottoman Empire challenged European assumptions about medical superiority.

## Ottoman Plague Management

Montagu was struck by the fatalism with which Ottomans approached plague. Unlike Europeans, who attempted to flee infected areas or quarantine the sick, Ottomans generally accepted the risk and continued their daily routines — an approach Montagu described with a mixture of admiration and anthropological distance (031-003). She noted that "the plague is very seldom known in any of our countries" compared to Constantinople, where it was an almost annual occurrence (035-005).

The Ottoman approach, Montagu observed, was not simply fatalism but reflected a different understanding of disease transmission. Ottoman practice did not emphasize contagion in the European sense, and this conceptual difference had practical consequences for how communities responded to outbreaks. Montagu's comparison of these different disease epistemologies anticipated later debates about contagion theory.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's accounts of plague appear primarily in Letters XXXI and XXXV, where she describes both the epidemiological facts of the disease and the social response to it. Her observation that "those dreadful stories you have heard of the plague have very little foundation in truth" was directed at European fears that she saw as exaggerated (035-005).

The plague discussions are significant not only for their medical content but for their rhetorical strategy: Montagu used disease — the quintessential marker of Ottoman "backwardness" in European discourse — to demonstrate the limits of European knowledge. By presenting Ottoman plague management as a coherent, if different, response to a universal problem, she challenged the hierarchy that placed European medicine above Ottoman practice.
