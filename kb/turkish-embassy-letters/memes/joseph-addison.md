# Joseph Addison

## Overview

Joseph Addison (1672–1719) was an English essayist, poet, and Whig politician, best known as the co-founder of *The Spectator* and author of the tragedy *Cato*. He appears in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's Turkish Embassy Letters in connection with his appointment as secretary of state — a political event that Montagu received news of while in Constantinople. Addison was part of the literary-political circle that included Montagu, Pope, and Congreve, and his presence in the correspondence reflects the integration of literary and political networks in Augustan England.

## Biography

Born in 1672 in Wiltshire, Addison was educated at Charterhouse and Queen's College, Oxford, where he established himself as a classical scholar and Latin poet. His literary career took off after the publication of *The Campaign* (1704), a poem celebrating the Duke of Marlborough's victory at Blenheim, which earned him government patronage.

With Richard Steele, Addison founded *The Tatler* (1709) and *The Spectator* (1711–1712), periodicals that defined the essay as a literary form and shaped 18th-century English taste and manners. His play *Cato* (1713) was a political and theatrical sensation, admired by Whigs and Tories alike for its valorization of republican virtue.

Addison entered Parliament and rose through Whig political ranks, serving as secretary of state briefly in 1717–1718. His health declined rapidly thereafter, and he died in 1719 at the age of 47.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu received news of Addison's appointment as secretary of state while in Constantinople, recording it in Letter LV: "I received the news of Mr Addison's being declared secretary of state" (055-004). The event is noted alongside her anticipation of returning to England and seeing her literary friends — "that I may see you and Mr Congreve" — situating Addison within the web of personal and political relationships that connected Montagu's Ottoman world to London.

The summary of the letters' contents notes "observations on Mr Addison being appointed secretary of state" alongside mentions of Pope and Congreve, indicating that Addison's political elevation was considered significant enough to merit inclusion in the published summary (062-055).
