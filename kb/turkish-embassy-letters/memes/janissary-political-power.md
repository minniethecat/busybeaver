# Janissary Political Power

## Overview

The Janissaries — the elite infantry corps of the Ottoman Empire — were far more than a military force in the early eighteenth century: they were a political institution whose power rivaled and often exceeded that of the sultan himself. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's observations of Janissary political power in the Turkish Embassy Letters provide a valuable contemporary account of a corps that European observers often reduced to orientalist stereotypes. Montagu recognized the Janissaries as political actors whose influence shaped Ottoman governance, diplomacy, and daily life.

## The Janissary Corps

The Janissaries were a standing army, originally recruited through the *devşirme* system (the levy of Christian boys from the empire's European provinces), but by the eighteenth century they had evolved into a hereditary caste with significant economic and political interests. They controlled key urban institutions, dominated certain trades, and could make or unmake sultans through their collective action (033-005).

Montagu observed the Janissaries primarily in their ceremonial and political roles — escorting the sultan's Friday procession, guarding the embassies, and intervening in court politics. Her description of the Janissaries' role in the deposition of sultans — "the Janissaries are the masters of the sultan" — reflects her understanding that Ottoman sovereignty was constrained by military power in ways that European absolutism was not (035-006).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's interest in Janissary power was partly practical: the security of the British embassy and the success of her husband's diplomatic mission depended on navigating the complex relationship between the sultan, the Janissaries, and the European powers. Her letters register the political instability created by Janissary interventions — the sudden changes in Ottoman policy, the vulnerability of foreign residents to shifts in the military-political balance, and the limits of the sultan's authority.

Her observation that the Janissaries could depose sultans "at pleasure" was not an exaggeration: between 1618 and 1730, several sultans were deposed by Janissary-led revolts. Montagu's recognition of this political reality — so different from the divine-right monarchy she knew in England — informed her understanding of Ottoman governance as a system of negotiated power rather than despotic caprice.
