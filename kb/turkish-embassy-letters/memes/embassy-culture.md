# Embassy Culture in the Ottoman Empire

## Overview

The European embassy system in the Ottoman Empire was a distinctive diplomatic culture shaped by Ottoman protocols, the capitulations (trade privileges granted to European powers), and the social networks that developed around ambassador's households. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's position as wife of the British ambassador gave her unique access to this world — and her letters provide the most detailed English-language account of embassy life in 18th-century Constantinople.

## Description

European embassies in Constantinople operated under conditions markedly different from those of intra-European diplomacy. The Ottoman Empire did not maintain permanent embassies in European capitals until the late 18th century, and European ambassadors in Constantinople were, in Ottoman eyes, supplicants rather than equals. The elaborate protocols of Ottoman court ceremony — including the requirement that ambassadors be formally presented to the sultan — reinforced this hierarchical relationship.

Despite these formal constraints, ambassadors enjoyed significant practical privileges under the capitulations system. They held extraterritorial jurisdiction over their nationals, could extend protection (*berat*) to Ottoman subjects who served as dragomans (interpreters) or agents, and controlled access to European trade networks. The British embassy, where Montagu resided, was a center of both diplomatic business and social life for the European community in Constantinople.

The ambassador's household was a substantial establishment including secretaries, chaplains, physicians, dragomans, janissary guards, and domestic servants. Montagu observed that "the great number of Greeks, French, English, and Italians that are under our protection, make their court to me from morning till night" — describing a social network that extended well beyond formal diplomatic circles (031-002).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's account of embassy life is particularly valuable because she experienced it from a position of both privilege and marginality. As the ambassador's wife, she had access to spaces and people — particularly Ottoman women — that male diplomats could not reach. Yet as a woman, she was excluded from the formal negotiations and political discussions that constituted the official business of the embassy.

This dual position gave her observations a distinctive perspective. She noted, for example, that Christian residents of the Ottoman Empire depended on embassy protection: "there is no possibility for a Christian to live easily under this government, but by the protection of an ambassador — and the richer they are, the greater is their danger" (031-002). This observation reveals both the practical importance of the embassy system and the precarity of non-Muslim subjects in the Ottoman state.

Montagu also recorded encounters with other European diplomats. She met with the Portuguese ambassador, who "thinks himself very happy" in his posting — a comment that hints at the competitive social world of the diplomatic corps (017-003). The French ambassador, she noted, "says pleasantly, that they take the small-pox here by way of diversion" — illustrating how diplomatic circles served as conduits for cultural observation and wit (031-004).

## Cultural Context

The capitulations that governed European embassies in the Ottoman Empire originated as unilateral grants by Ottoman sultans — acts of imperial generosity rather than negotiated treaties. Over time, however, they hardened into customary rights that European powers defended vigorously. The embassy system was thus poised between Ottoman sovereignty and European commercial interests.

The dragoman (interpreter) was the crucial intermediary in this system. Dragomans were typically Ottoman Christians or Levantines who spoke both Turkish and European languages. Their role extended far beyond translation: they advised ambassadors on Ottoman protocol, managed relationships with Ottoman officials, and often exercised significant independent influence.
