# François de La Rochefoucauld

## Overview

François VI, duc de La Rochefoucauld (1613–1680) was a French moralist whose *Maximes* (1665) — a collection of sharp, epigrammatic reflections on human nature — made him one of the most quoted French writers of the 17th and 18th centuries. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu invokes La Rochefoucauld in the appendix to her Turkish Embassy Letters, citing his maxim on marriage as a starting point for her own reflections on the institution. The reference demonstrates Montagu's engagement with French moral philosophy and her habit of testing continental maxims against her own observations of Ottoman society.

## Biography

La Rochefoucauld was born into the highest French aristocracy in 1613. His early life was shaped by the political turmoil of the Fronde (1648–1653), the aristocratic rebellion against Cardinal Mazarin, in which La Rochefoucauld played an active and ultimately unsuccessful role. Wounded in battle and disillusioned with politics, he retired from public life and devoted himself to the literary salon of Madame de Sablé, where the *Maximes* were refined through collective discussion.

The *Maximes* — over 500 epigrammatic reflections on self-interest, vanity, and the deceits of human motivation — established a new literary genre and influenced writers across Europe. La Rochefoucauld died in Paris in 1680.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu references La Rochefoucauld in the appended materials to the Turkish Embassy Letters, specifically in connection with his maxim on marriage: "Monsieur de la ROCHEFOUCAULT'S Maxim—*That marriage is sometimes convenient, but never delightful*" (059-001). Montagu uses the maxim as a foil, developing her own more nuanced reflections on marriage that draw on her observations of both English and Ottoman customs.

The citation of La Rochefoucauld alongside other French literary references (Boileau, La Fontaine) reveals Montagu's deep engagement with French moralist literature and her strategy of testing European maxims against the evidence of Ottoman society.
