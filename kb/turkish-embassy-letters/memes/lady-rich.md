# Lady Rich

## Overview

Lady Rich (likely Elizabeth Griffith, Lady Rich, c. 1692–1773) was an English aristocrat and member of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's social circle who received three letters during Montagu's Ottoman embassy. Addressed to "Lady R——" in the published edition, these letters cover Ottoman court life, social customs, and Montagu's observations of elite Ottoman women — topics likely chosen for a female correspondent with court connections.

## Biography

Elizabeth Griffith married Sir Robert Rich, a prominent Whig politician and military officer, becoming Lady Rich. The Rich family was well-connected in Whig political circles — the same circles in which Edward Wortley Montagu moved — and Lady Rich was part of the London social world that Montagu left behind for Constantinople.

Lady Rich was known in London society for her intelligence and court connections. She moved in the same literary-political circles as Montagu and would have been particularly interested in Montagu's accounts of Ottoman court ceremony and elite women's lives — subjects on which her own experience at the English court gave her a comparative framework.

She died in 1773, surviving Montagu by over a decade.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

The three letters addressed to "Lady R——" focus primarily on Ottoman court life and elite social customs. Montagu's choice of topics for Lady Rich reflects both her addressee's interests and her own assessment of what would most interest a woman with court experience.

The letters to Lady Rich contain some of the most detailed accounts of Ottoman court ceremony and elite women's dress and adornment. Montagu describes imperial weddings, court protocol, and the elaborate costumes of the Ottoman elite in terms that a woman familiar with the English court would appreciate. The comparison between Ottoman and English court culture — implicit in the choice of detail — runs through these letters.

Lady Rich's position in London society also made her a useful conduit for Montagu's observations. Letters to Lady Rich were likely circulated among Montagu's social circle, serving a semi-public function that Montagu was surely aware of when composing them.
