# Ottoman Marriage Customs

## Overview

Ottoman marriage customs, as observed by Lady Mary Wortley Montagu during her embassy travels (1716–1718), encompassed a range of practices that differed markedly from 18th-century English norms. Montagu documented arranged marriages, the institution of *sub-marriage* (temporary or secondary unions), women's divorce rights, and the elaborate wedding ceremonies of the Ottoman elite. Her accounts reveal a marital system that was simultaneously more restrictive and, in certain respects, more legally protective of women than contemporary European arrangements.

## Description

Marriage in Ottoman society operated on several distinct tiers. Elite marriages among the ruling class involved elaborate ceremonies, significant dowries, and political considerations. At the imperial level, marriages connected powerful families and could involve ceremonial processions of extraordinary opulence, as when Montagu witnessed a wedding where "the decorations and habits cost the emperor thirty thousand pounds" (007-002).

A distinctive feature Montagu noted was the institution she termed "sub-marriage" — an arrangement in which a woman could have both a legal husband (who provided her status and household) and a companionate partner chosen for affection. These secondary unions, she reported, "generally last twenty years together, and the lady is then looked upon as a kind of saint" (010-003). The system reflected a pragmatic accommodation between the economic functions of marriage and the desire for romantic companionship.

Ottoman law granted women divorce rights that were not available to English women of the period. Montagu described a formal divorce procedure: a woman could be "divorced publicly, in such a manner" as to free her entirely from her husband (015-003). Moreover, a divorced woman could remarry her former husband only after an intervening marriage to another man — a provision that prevented hasty or coerced divorces.

Some marriages Montagu described as closer to contracts than to the English understanding of marriage: "This is rather to be called a contract than a marriage, since she never has lived with him" (028-003). These contractual arrangements could serve economic or social purposes without cohabitation.

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's accounts of Ottoman marriage appear across multiple letters, revealing her sustained interest in the institution. In Letter X, she described the "sub-marriage" system with ethnographic detachment, neither condemning nor romanticizing it, but noting its longevity and social acceptance (010-003).

Her most detailed account of a wedding ceremony appears in Letter VII, where she described an imperial wedding procession with "fifty dishes of meat all served in silver" and decorations costing "thirty thousand pounds" — a figure that conveyed both the grandeur of the event and the economic scale of elite Ottoman marriages (007-002).

In Letter XV, Montagu described the formal divorce procedure, noting that a woman who obtained a public divorce was "entirely" freed from the marriage — a legal outcome that contrasted sharply with English law, where divorce was virtually impossible for women without parliamentary intervention (015-003).

Montagu also recorded the social pressure toward early marriage and fertility. Ottoman women, she observed, had "a notion, that when a woman is past the age of fifteen, 'tis with us to be fruitful before marriage" — suggesting that Ottoman norms around marriage age and fertility differed significantly from English expectations (038-002). She wryly commented on "ridiculous marriages" in both cultures, noting that neither society was immune to ill-conceived matches (037-003).

## Cultural Context

The Ottoman marriage system must be understood within the broader framework of Islamic family law, which provided women with specific property rights and legal protections. A married woman retained control over her dower and personal property — rights that English women would not fully secure until the Married Women's Property Act of 1882.

Montagu's relatively non-judgmental reporting of Ottoman marriage practices is notable. While she occasionally remarked on the exotic or unusual, she consistently contextualized Ottoman customs within their own social logic rather than measuring them against an assumed European standard. Her observation that "ridiculous marriages" occurred in both cultures reflects this comparative, rather than hierarchical, approach.

The role of the bath (*bagnio*) in marriage introductions — where "several others go, out of curiosity" and families of the newly allied would meet — highlights the interconnection between Ottoman social institutions (042-002).

| Aspect | Ottoman Practice | English Practice (18th c.) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Women's divorce rights | Legally available, public procedure | Virtually impossible without Parliament |
| Property rights | Dower and personal property retained by wife | Property transferred to husband upon marriage |
| Polygamy | Permitted but uncommon among elites | Illegal |
| Companionate arrangements | "Sub-marriage" acknowledged | Informal, socially condemned |
| Marriage age | Often very young (early teens) | Similar or slightly older |
