# Sophia

## Overview

Sophia (modern Sofia, Bulgaria) was an Ottoman provincial city in the Balkans that Lady Mary Wortley Montagu passed through in 1717 on her journey from Belgrade to Adrianople. It was here that she visited the women's bath — the *bagnio* — and produced what is arguably the most celebrated description in the entire Turkish Embassy Letters. The Sophia bath scene, with its two hundred naked women, its classical allusions, and its explicit comparison of Ottoman and European social behavior, has been central to critical discussions of Montagu's work for nearly three centuries.

## Geography and the Bath

Montagu described Sophia as "situated in a large beautiful plain" surrounded by mountains, with "hot baths, very famous for their medicinal virtues" (025-003). The city's thermal springs had been exploited since Roman times and remained a defining feature of the urban landscape.

The bath Montagu visited was a substantial architectural complex: "It is built of stone, in the shape of a dome, with no windows but in the roof, which gives light enough. There were five of these domes joined together" (026-003). The five-dome structure allowed for graduated temperatures — from the cold entrance hall to the steam-filled hot rooms — in a sophisticated thermal engineering that impressed Montagu.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's account of the Sophia bath (Letter XXVI) is the most extensively quoted passage in the Turkish Embassy Letters. She arrived at ten o'clock in a hired Turkish coach, traveling incognito, and found the bath "already full of women" — some two hundred bathers in various states of leisure and grooming. Her description moves between ethnographic precision (the architectural details, the social protocols) and aesthetic appreciation (the bathers compared to figures by Guido or Titian, their hair "braided either with pearl or ribbon").

The passage includes Montagu's most explicit comparison of Ottoman and European social behavior: "I know no European court, where the ladies would have behaved themselves in so polite a manner to such a stranger." The absence of "disdainful smiles, and satirical whispers" that "never fail in our assemblies" is presented not as an absence of civilization but as evidence of a different, in some respects superior, form of civility.
