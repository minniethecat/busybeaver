# Smallpox Inoculation

## Overview

Smallpox inoculation — referred to as *ingrafting* in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's letters — was an Ottoman medical practice in which material from a mild smallpox case was introduced into a healthy person's vein to induce a controlled, survivable infection and confer lifelong immunity. Montagu encountered this practice in Adrianople in 1717 and became the first English writer to describe it in detail, later introducing the technique to Britain and contributing to the eventual development of modern vaccination.

## Description

The Ottoman method of inoculation was a folk medical practice performed by elderly women practitioners. Unlike European variolation techniques that later developed, the Ottoman approach was notable for its social organization — families would form "parties" to undergo the procedure together — and its remarkably low mortality rate.

The procedure involved four key elements:

- **Practitioners**: Elderly women who specialized in the operation, performing it each autumn when the summer heat subsided
- **Technique**: A large needle was used to open a vein, into which matter from a mild smallpox case was inserted; the wound was then bound with a hollow shell
- **Sites**: Typically four or five veins were opened — commonly on the legs or concealed parts of the arm; some Greek practitioners made incisions in the shape of a cross on the forehead, arms, and breast
- **Recovery**: Patients remained active for eight days, then experienced two to three days of mild fever, with very few pustules (rarely more than twenty or thirty) and no permanent scarring

The procedure was so well-established that, as the French ambassador in Constantinople observed, Ottomans "took the small-pox here by way of diversion, as they take the waters in other countries" (031-004).

## Montagu's Observations

Lady Montagu's account of Ottoman inoculation in Letter XXXI is the most detailed English-language description of the practice prior to its introduction to Britain. Writing to Mrs. S. C. from Adrianople in April 1717, she expressed astonishment that an affliction "so fatal and so general" in Europe was "entirely harmless" in the Ottoman Empire (031-004).

Montagu described the procedure with the precision of an ethnographer, noting the social ritual of families assembling in groups of fifteen or sixteen, the technique of the elderly women practitioners, the Greek custom of making incisions in the sign of the cross, and the eight-day progression from operation to recovery.

She concluded her account with two significant personal commitments. First, she declared her intention to have the procedure performed on her young son — a decision she subsequently carried out in 1718, making him one of the first Europeans to be inoculated. Second, she expressed a patriotic determination to "bring this useful invention into fashion in England," despite anticipating resistance from British physicians whose income depended on treating smallpox patients (031-004).

The summary of the letters' contents later noted the significance of this passage, recording simply: "Turkish method of inoculating the small-pox" (062-031).

## Cultural Context

The Ottoman inoculation practice existed within a broader medical culture that Montagu found both familiar and foreign. Ottoman physicians, she noted, were unacquainted with the "variety" of European diseases, just as Europeans were ignorant of the Ottoman approach to smallpox.

The fact that the procedure was performed by elderly women rather than formally trained physicians reflects the gendered division of medical labor in Ottoman society. Women served as primary healthcare providers for other women and children, a role that gave them access to empirical knowledge passed down through generations.

Montagu's willingness to trust this folk practice — contrary to the skepticism of European-trained physicians — demonstrates the value of her observational approach. Where a male traveler might have dismissed the procedure as superstition, Montagu assessed it on its empirical merits: thousands underwent it annually, none had died of it, and the results were visibly superior to the devastation smallpox wrought in Europe.

## Comparative Notes

The Ottoman inoculation method differed significantly from subsequent European variolation:

| Aspect | Ottoman Practice | European Variolation |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Practitioners | Elderly women specialists | Physicians and surgeons |
| Incision sites | Arms, legs (concealed areas) | Often face and visible areas |
| Social organization | Group "parties" | Individual procedures |
| Scarring | Minimal to none | Often significant |
| Mortality | Near zero (no deaths recorded by Montagu) | 2–3% in 18th-century England |

Montagu's advocacy of the Ottoman method, combined with her social position as an aristocrat and the dramatic evidence of her own inoculated son, helped persuade members of the British royal family to undergo the procedure in 1722 — a crucial step toward the later development of Edward Jenner's smallpox vaccine in 1796.
