# Inoculation of Edward Wortley Montagu Jr

## Overview

In March 1718, Lady Mary Wortley Montagu had her four-year-old son Edward inoculated against smallpox in Constantinople, using the Ottoman method she had described in Letter XXXI. This event — the first recorded inoculation of an English person — was a deliberate act of medical advocacy by Montagu, who believed the procedure could save countless lives in England. Edward's successful recovery provided the empirical evidence Montagu needed to campaign for the introduction of inoculation to Britain upon her return.

## The Procedure

The inoculation was performed by an Ottoman practitioner — likely one of the elderly women specialists Montagu had described — using the method detailed in Letter XXXI: matter from a mild smallpox case was introduced into the child's veins through small incisions, the wounds bound with hollow shells. The procedure was accompanied by the social rituals Montagu had observed in Adrianople.

Montagu's willingness to subject her own child to an unfamiliar medical procedure, administered by a practitioner from a culture many Europeans dismissed as backward, was an act of extraordinary intellectual courage. "You may believe I am well satisfied of the safety of this experiment, since I intend to try it on my dear little son," she had written — and she followed through (031-004).

## Historical Significance

Edward Wortley Montagu Jr survived the inoculation without complication, confirming Montagu's confidence in the procedure. Upon her return to England, she used her son's case as evidence in her campaign to introduce inoculation. In 1722, members of the British royal family — including the daughters of the Prince of Wales — were inoculated, following Montagu's advocacy and example.

The event represents a rare instance of direct medical knowledge transfer from Ottoman to European practice, mediated by an aristocratic woman rather than by physicians or scientists. Montagu's role in introducing inoculation to Britain has been increasingly recognized by historians of medicine.
