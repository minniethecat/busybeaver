# Hungary

## Overview

Hungary was the kingdom through which Lady Mary Wortley Montagu traveled on her overland journey from Vienna to the Ottoman frontier in 1717. At the time of her passage, Hungary was recovering from more than a century of warfare between the Habsburg and Ottoman empires — a conflict that had ended with the Habsburg reconquest of most of the historic kingdom. Montagu's observations of the Hungarian landscape register both the devastation of recent wars and the beginnings of reconstruction, making her letters a valuable record of the frontier zone between Christian Europe and the Ottoman Empire.

## Geography and Historical Context

The Kingdom of Hungary occupied the Carpathian Basin, a region that had been contested between Habsburg and Ottoman forces since the Battle of Mohács in 1526. The Habsburg reconquest, completed by the Treaty of Karlowitz (1699), had returned most of the kingdom to Habsburg control, but the scars of war were everywhere visible when Montagu traveled through in 1717.

The landscape Montagu described was one of abandoned villages, overgrown fields, and the ruins of fortifications — evidence of the demographic collapse that had accompanied the Ottoman-Habsburg wars. Yet she also noted the beginnings of recovery: new settlements, repaired roads, and the return of cultivation to lands that had been depopulated. Her observation that "the country is very fruitful, but ill-peopled" captures the paradox of a fertile landscape marked by the absence of its former inhabitants (016-004).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Hungary appears in Montagu's letters as a transitional space — neither fully European nor Ottoman, marked by the recent violence that had defined its history. Her descriptions of the Hungarian landscape emphasize its difference from the cultivated order of the German states: the roads were rougher, the accommodations more primitive, and the visible signs of recent conflict more pronounced.

Montagu's passage through Hungary also registered the ethnic and religious diversity of the region. She noted the presence of "Rascians" (Serbs), Hungarians, Germans, and Ottomans, each with their own customs, languages, and loyalties — a human geography that reflected the region's history as a borderland between empires (017-003). Her observations anticipate the ethnographic interest that would characterize her Ottoman letters.
