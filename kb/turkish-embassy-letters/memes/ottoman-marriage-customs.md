# Ottoman Marriage Customs (Observed)

## Overview

Ottoman marriage customs were among the social institutions Lady Mary Wortley Montagu observed most carefully during her embassy, and her descriptions of Ottoman marriage practices in the Turkish Embassy Letters constitute one of the earliest detailed English-language accounts of the subject. Montagu was particularly interested in the legal and social position of married women — a topic on which she found Ottoman practices, in several respects, superior to English ones.

## Marriage Practices

Montagu described a range of marriage-related customs: the negotiation of marriages by families, the financial settlements that protected women's property, the wedding ceremonies themselves, and the social life of married women within the harem system. Her informants were elite Ottoman women — the wives and relatives of officials — who explained the workings of their society to Montagu with what she described as remarkable candor (028-003).

She was struck by the legal protections Ottoman married women enjoyed — particularly their control over their own property, which was guaranteed by Islamic law in ways that English common law did not provide. "The Turks are not so unpolished as we represent them," she wrote, noting that Ottoman wives "'tis true, are locked up, but their money makes them amends for their confinement" (030-005). Her observation that Ottoman women's property rights compensated for their physical seclusion offered English readers an unfamiliar perspective on the relationship between wealth, gender, and freedom.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's accounts of marriage appear across multiple letters, reflecting her sustained interest in comparative social institutions. She described wedding processions through the streets of Adrianople, the negotiation of marriage contracts, and the daily life of married women within the elite households she visited. Her comparisons consistently positioned Ottoman practices not as exotic deviations from European norms but as alternative social arrangements with their own internal logic and, in some respects, their own advantages.

The theme of marriage also connected to Montagu's broader observations about gender and power in Ottoman society. Her interest in the political influence of the sultan's mother (*valide sultan*) and the sultan's favorites reflected her recognition that Ottoman women exercised significant power, albeit through different institutional channels than European women.
