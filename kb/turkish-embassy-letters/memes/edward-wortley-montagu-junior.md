# Edward Wortley Montagu (junior)

## Overview

Edward Wortley Montagu the younger (1713–1776) was the son of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu and Edward Wortley Montagu. Aged approximately four during his mother's Ottoman embassy, he is historically significant as the first English person known to have received smallpox inoculation using the Ottoman method — a procedure his mother observed in Adrianople and had performed on him in Constantinople in 1718. Lady Mary's decision to inoculate her own son was perhaps the strongest testimony to her confidence in the Ottoman practice she championed.

## Biography

Born in 1713, Edward Wortley Montagu the younger accompanied his parents on the Ottoman embassy as a small child. After the family's return to England in 1718, he was educated at Westminster School, but his relationship with his parents — particularly his father — was strained. He ran away from school multiple times and developed a reputation for wildness and instability unusual even by the standards of 18th-century aristocratic youth.

As an adult, Wortley Montagu led an eccentric and peripatetic life. He traveled extensively in the Middle East, converted to Islam at some point (though the sincerity of his conversion was disputed), and adopted Ottoman dress and customs. His later years were spent in Italy, where he died in 1776, having outlived both his parents.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

The younger Wortley Montagu appears in the Turkish Embassy Letters at a crucial moment. In Letter XXXI, after describing the Ottoman inoculation procedure in detail, Montagu declares: "you may believe I am well satisfied of the safety of this experiment, since I intend to try it on my dear little son" (031-004). This sentence — combining maternal affection with scientific conviction — is one of the most powerful moments in the correspondence.

The inoculation was subsequently performed in Constantinople in 1718, and the boy's successful recovery provided Montagu with the evidence she needed to advocate for the procedure upon her return to England. Members of the British royal family had their children inoculated in 1722, following Montagu's example.
