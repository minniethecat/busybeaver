# Genoa

## Overview

Genoa was an independent maritime republic and one of the great commercial cities of the Mediterranean in the eighteenth century. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu visited Genoa during her travels and her observations of the city reflect her interest in the distinctive political and social arrangements of the Italian states. Unlike the monarchical courts of Vienna and Dresden, Genoa represented an older model of urban republicanism — a city-state governed by a merchant aristocracy whose wealth came from maritime trade rather than territorial dominion.

## Geography and Significance

Genoa occupied a dramatic site on the Ligurian coast, its palaces and churches rising from the harbor in a terraced amphitheater facing the sea. The city's wealth, accumulated through centuries of Mediterranean trade and banking, was visible in its architecture — the Strade Nuove lined with the palaces of the great Genoese families were among the most impressive urban ensembles in Europe.

In the eighteenth century, Genoa's political independence was increasingly constrained by the great powers, but the city remained a significant commercial and financial center. Its position on the maritime routes between Western Europe and the Levant made it a natural point of contact between European and Ottoman commercial networks — a role that would have interested Montagu as she traveled eastward (007-003).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's account of Genoa is brief but characteristically sharp. She noted the city's distinctive social arrangements — the absence of a royal court, the dominance of the merchant aristocracy, the visible wealth of the urban fabric. Her comparison of Genoese palaces to those of other European cities reflects her ongoing interest in the relationship between political systems and architectural expression (007-004).

Genoa's position as a maritime republic also provided Montagu with a counterpoint to the Ottoman Empire. Both were Mediterranean powers, but their political structures — one a compact urban oligarchy, the other a vast imperial autocracy — represented opposite poles of political organization. Montagu's implicit comparison of the two registers her Enlightenment interest in comparative government.
