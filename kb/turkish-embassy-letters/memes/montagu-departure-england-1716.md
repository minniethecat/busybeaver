# Montagu's Departure from England 1716

## Overview

Lady Mary Wortley Montagu departed from England in early August 1716, accompanying her husband Edward Wortley Montagu on his appointment as British ambassador to the Ottoman Empire. The departure marked the beginning of a journey that would transform Montagu from a minor literary figure — known primarily for her court connections and her friendship with Alexander Pope — into one of the most important travel writers of the eighteenth century. The Turkish Embassy Letters begin with this departure, and Montagu's first letters from the road establish the voice — curious, ironic, culturally self-aware — that would define the collection.

## The Embassy Appointment

Edward Wortley Montagu's appointment as ambassador to the Ottoman Porte was the result of complex Whig political maneuvering following the accession of George I. The embassy was a significant diplomatic posting, charged with representing British commercial and political interests at the Ottoman court, and the appointment reflected Wortley Montagu's standing within the Whig establishment.

For Lady Mary, the embassy represented an opportunity to escape the constraints of London society and court politics. She had suffered a serious bout of smallpox the previous year, which had scarred her face and affected her confidence in the social circles where she had previously excelled. The journey eastward offered not only adventure but a form of liberation — a removal from the world of "disdainful smiles, and satirical whispers" that she would later contrast so pointedly with Ottoman civility (026-003).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

The departure letters are brief but significant: they establish Montagu's epistolary relationships with her various correspondents, introduce the conceit of the traveler writing from the road, and set the tone of ironic observation that would characterize the collection. Montagu's first letter from Rotterdam (Letter I) and her subsequent letters from the German courts demonstrate her ability to transform the practical details of travel — coaches, roads, accommodations — into material for cultural commentary.

The departure also marked a personal transition. Montagu was leaving behind her young son Edward (who would later join her in Constantinople) and her London social world. The letters register this ambivalence — excitement at the journey ahead, sadness at separation from friends and family — without indulging in the emotional excess that eighteenth-century epistolary convention permitted.
