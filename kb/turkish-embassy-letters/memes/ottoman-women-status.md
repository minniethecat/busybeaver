# Status of Women in Ottoman Society

## Overview

[[Lady Mary Wortley Montagu]]'s assessment of Ottoman women's status was one of the most controversial arguments in her Turkish Embassy Letters. Against the prevailing European view of Ottoman women as oppressed and secluded, she argued that Turkish women enjoyed greater practical liberty and stronger property rights than English women of equivalent rank.

Her analysis spans legal protection, economic independence, and social freedom, drawing on spaces no male traveller could enter — the [[turkish-baths|bath]], the visiting circuit, and the [[Ottoman Seraglio]]. This made women's status one of the most multi-dimensional concepts in her correspondence.

## Description

Ottoman women's status varied considerably by class, religion, and location. Elite urban women — those Montagu primarily encountered — operated within a system of gender segregation that was simultaneously restrictive and protective.

The veil and the harem, which European observers read solely as instruments of oppression, also functioned as markers of status and as mechanisms preserving a separate female economic and social sphere. Key dimensions of that status included:

- **Property rights**: married women retained control of their dower and personal property
- **Divorce**: formal legal procedures for dissolving a marriage existed and were used (035-008)
- **Economic activity**: women owned and managed property and controlled their own finances
- **Social networks**: the bath, visiting circuits, and family networks sustained a robust female community

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's most provocative claim was that the veil, far from subordinating women, granted them freedom of movement through anonymity. The "perpetual masquerade" of Ottoman dress, she argued, made women freer than their European counterparts:

> This perpetual masquerade gives them entire liberty of following their inclinations, without danger of discovery. (029-005)

The bath was central to her argument about women's social world. There, women commanded a space entirely their own — a female public sphere with no direct English equivalent:

> 'Tis the women's coffee-house, where all the news of the town is told, scandal invented, &c. (026-005)

She also noted that elite Ottoman women aged with dignity, reflecting that the empire was "such a paradise for old women" (010-002) — a comment on the respect accorded to elder women.

## Cultural Context

Montagu's defence of Ottoman women's status was shaped by her own position in English society, where a married woman's legal personality was subsumed into her husband's under the doctrine of coverture. Her argument that Ottoman women were in some respects freer was both genuine ethnography and an implicit critique of English gender relations.

Modern scholarship has both confirmed and complicated her account. Ottoman court records do show women exercising property rights and initiating divorces, but class and regional variation was significant, and the rural poor faced constraints Montagu — who moved in elite circles — did not observe.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Women (Elite) | English Women (18th c.) |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Property rights | Retained after marriage | Lost to husband (coverture) |
| Divorce | Legally available | Virtually unobtainable |
| Public mobility | Veil enabled anonymous movement | Restricted by social convention |
| Social sphere | Female-only spaces (bath, visits) | Mixed-gender but male-dominated |
| Economic activity | Trade and property management common | Limited to certain trades |
