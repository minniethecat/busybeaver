# Sultan's Friday Procession

## Overview

The sultan's Friday procession (*selamlik*) was a weekly imperial ceremony that Lady Mary Wortley Montagu observed during her residence in Constantinople. Each Friday, Sultan Ahmed III rode in public procession from the seraglio to a designated mosque for the noon prayer, accompanied by an elaborate military escort — the Janissaries, mounted guards, and court officials whose presence transformed the sultan's passage into a display of Ottoman imperial power. Montagu's description of this ceremony is one of the most vivid set pieces in the Turkish Embassy Letters.

## The Ceremony

Montagu described the procession from a window near the route, a vantage that gave her a clear view of the sultan and his retinue. Sultan Ahmed III rode a fine horse adorned in gold-embroidered trappings, attended by "a great number of janissaries" who cleared the streets and ensured the sultan's passage was unimpeded (032-003).

The procession was both a religious observance and a political performance. The sultan's public visibility on Fridays was one of the few occasions when ordinary subjects could present petitions directly to the sovereign — a tradition Montagu noted with interest, comparing it implicitly to the more restricted access of English subjects to their monarch. "He went to the mosque of Sancta Sophia," she wrote, describing the route that took the sultan through the heart of his capital, past the monuments of Byzantine and Ottoman power, under the gaze of his subjects.

## Political Significance

For Montagu, the Friday procession was evidence of the Ottoman sultan's dual role as religious leader and political sovereign. The ceremony dramatized the relationship between the sultan and his subjects in ways that differed from European court ritual — more public, less mediated by aristocrats, and directly tied to the weekly rhythm of Islamic observance.

Montagu's observation that the sultan appeared "extremely gracious" and that "one of his chiefs" informed her of the numbers and functions of each element of the procession reflects her access to informed Ottoman interpreters — members of the court who could explain the ceremony's meaning and significance (033-004).
