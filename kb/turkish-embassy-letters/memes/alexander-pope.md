# Alexander Pope

## Overview

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the preeminent English poet of the early 18th century and a significant figure in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's correspondence. Five of the Turkish Embassy Letters are addressed to Pope, and he contributed commendatory verses to the published edition of her letters. Their relationship — initially one of mutual admiration — later deteriorated into one of the most famous literary feuds of the Augustan age. Montagu's letters to Pope reveal the intellectual companionship between two of the era's sharpest literary minds before their eventual estrangement.

## Biography

Alexander Pope was born in London in 1688 to Catholic parents. Barred by his religion from attending university or holding public office, he dedicated himself to literary achievement with extraordinary discipline. By his early twenties, he had established himself as a major poet with *An Essay on Criticism* (1711) and *The Rape of the Lock* (1712). His translations of Homer's *Iliad* (1715–1720) and *Odyssey* (1725–1726) secured his financial independence — a rare achievement for a professional writer of the period.

Pope's physical stature — he stood about four and a half feet tall and suffered from a tubercular condition of the spine — made him a target of ridicule throughout his life. His sharp wit and capacity for literary warfare, culminating in *The Dunciad* (1728), were in part a response to the attacks he endured.

His relationship with Lady Mary Wortley Montagu began in the mid-1710s, when both moved in the same London literary circles. Their correspondence during her Ottoman embassy (1716–1718) represents the high point of their friendship. After her return, the relationship soured — the exact reasons remain disputed — and Pope subsequently attacked Montagu in his satires.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Pope is the addressee of five letters in the Turkish Embassy Letters, making him one of Montagu's most frequent named correspondents. The letters addressed to him tend to be more literary and intellectually ambitious than those sent to family members, reflecting Montagu's recognition of Pope as an intellectual equal.

The surviving letters to Pope cover her journey and early impressions of Ottoman society. Montagu writes to him as a fellow writer, offering observations designed to interest a literary mind. She is "firmly sensible of yours and Mr Congreve's" friendship and expresses her desire "to live in your memory" — acknowledgments of the importance she placed on maintaining literary connections during her time abroad (036-006).

After Montagu's return to England, Pope contributed verses "to Lady Mary Wortley Montague" that were included in the published edition of her letters (062-061). The summary of the letters' contents also notes "Mr Addison, Mr Pope, and Mr Congreve" together with "observations on Mr Addison being appointed secretary of state" — situating Pope within the network of literary-political figures who populated Montagu's correspondence (062-055).
