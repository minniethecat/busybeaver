# Vienna

## Overview

Vienna was the imperial capital of the Habsburg Empire and an important stop on Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's journey to Constantinople in 1716–1717. Her letters from Vienna, addressed primarily to her sister the Countess of Mar, contain some of the most vivid social satire in the Turkish Embassy Letters — detailed accounts of Viennese court fashion, the etiquette of imperial audiences, and the absurdities of aristocratic dress that Montagu described as "more monstrous, and contrary to all common sense and reason, than 'tis possible for you to imagine" (009-002).

## Geography and Imperial Status

Vienna occupied a strategic position on the Danube, serving as the eastern bulwark of Christian Europe against the Ottoman Empire. At the time of Montagu's visit, the city had recently emerged from the Ottoman siege of 1683 and was at the height of its Baroque rebuilding program. The Hofburg palace, the summer palace of Schönbrunn, and the city's new aristocratic palaces reflected Habsburg power and wealth.

Montagu approached Vienna from the west, traveling through Hanover and Dresden, and her first impressions were of a city that combined imperial grandeur with provincial manners. The Viennese court, under Emperor Charles VI, maintained elaborate ceremonial protocols that Montagu found both impressive and faintly ridiculous — a perspective shaped by her experiences at the English court and her Enlightenment skepticism toward rigid hierarchy.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's Viennese letters (Letters VIII through XIV) form a sustained study of Habsburg court culture. Her description of her audience with the Empress is carefully observed: the "squeezed up" gown, the gorget, and the "certain fabrics of gauze on their heads, about a yard high" that constituted court dress (009-002). The detailed description of the Bourle — the elaborate headdress framework — is characteristic of Montagu's ethnographic eye, treating Viennese fashion with the same analytical attention she would later bring to Ottoman dress.

Her comparison of the Viennese court to the English court is consistently satirical. She notes that Viennese women "build certain fabrics of gauze on their heads" and that their whale-bone petticoats "cover some acres of ground" — hyperbole that serves her larger argument about the relativity of fashion and the absurdity of taking any culture's conventions as natural (009-002). This comparative method, honed in Vienna, became the signature technique of her later Ottoman letters.
