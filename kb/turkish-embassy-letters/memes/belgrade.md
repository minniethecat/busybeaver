# Belgrade

## Overview

Belgrade was a strategic Ottoman fortress city on the Danube River that Lady Mary Wortley Montagu passed through in early 1717 on her journey to Constantinople. The city had recently been the site of a major battle between Ottoman and Habsburg forces, and Montagu's description captures Belgrade in a moment of military tension — a fortified frontier city whose strategic importance was visible in its walls, its garrison, and the war-scarred landscape surrounding it.

## Geography and Strategic Importance

Belgrade occupied a commanding position at the confluence of the Danube and Sava rivers, making it one of the most strategically significant fortresses in southeastern Europe. The city had changed hands between Ottoman and Habsburg forces multiple times, and at the time of Montagu's visit it was under Ottoman control — though a major Habsburg siege would occur later in 1717, shortly after her passage.

Montagu traveled from Belgrade with "letters to the bassa of Belgrade" — the Ottoman governor of the city — indicating the diplomatic formalities that structured travel through Ottoman territory (023-003). The city served as the gateway between Habsburg and Ottoman domains, and crossing from Belgrade into Ottoman territory marked a significant transition in Montagu's journey.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's account of Belgrade appears in Letter XXIV, addressed to Alexander Pope and dated "Belgrade, Feb. 12. O. S. 1717." Writing from the frontier between two empires, she reflects on the journey thus far and the Ottoman world she is beginning to enter. The letter to Pope — the first in the collection addressed to the poet — marks both a geographic and an intellectual transition.

She noted the practical arrangements of Ottoman travel: "They carry a number of waggons for our baggage from Belgrade hither for a certain hire" (025-003). The infrastructure of Ottoman travel — the wagon system, the post stations, the diplomatic escorts — becomes visible in her account of the journey from Belgrade onward.
