# Treaty of Passarowitz 1718

## Overview

The Treaty of Passarowitz (signed July 21, 1718) concluded the Habsburg-Ottoman War of 1716–1718 and marked a significant territorial and diplomatic shift in southeastern Europe. The treaty was signed just as Lady Mary Wortley Montagu was preparing to return from Constantinople, and its terms — which formalized Habsburg control over Belgrade, the Banat of Temesvár, and parts of Wallachia — reshaped the political geography she had traversed. For Montagu, the treaty represented the formal end of the military conflict that had formed the backdrop of her entire embassy.

## Terms and Significance

Under the treaty's terms, the Ottoman Empire ceded:
- The Banat of Temesvár (the last Ottoman territory in Hungary)
- Belgrade and northern Serbia
- Parts of Wallachia

The treaty confirmed Habsburg military superiority on the Danube frontier and established a new balance of power in southeastern Europe. For the Ottoman Empire, the territorial losses — following the earlier losses of the Treaty of Karlowitz (1699) — represented a continued erosion of the empire's European domains that would have long-term consequences for Ottoman politics and military organization (043-005).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu does not describe the treaty negotiations directly — she was in Constantinople, not Passarowitz — but its effects are visible in her later letters. The political instability she observed in Constantinople, the power of the Janissaries over the sultan, and the shifting calculations of the European diplomatic community were all shaped by the military defeats that the treaty formalized.

The treaty also marked the end of the wartime context in which Montagu had conducted her embassy. Her journey had begun in a Europe at war; it ended in a Europe at peace. The transition from war to peace — and the diplomatic realignments it entailed — framed Montagu's departure from Constantinople and her return to England.
