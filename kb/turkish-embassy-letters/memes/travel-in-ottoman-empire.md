# Travel in the Ottoman Empire

## Overview

Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's journey from Vienna to Constantinople in 1716–1717 was one of the most extensively documented overland travels by an English woman in the Ottoman Empire. Her letters describe the roads, transport modes, accommodations, and travel culture of the Ottoman Balkans — from the "covered waggons" that conveyed women incognito to the caravanserais and private houses that sheltered travelers. Her account provides a rare ground-level view of 18th-century Ottoman transport infrastructure.

## Description

Ottoman travel infrastructure in the early 18th century combined imperial roads inherited from Rome and Byzantium, caravanserais (roadside inns) established by pious foundations, and a variety of transport modes adapted to different terrains and seasons.

Key elements of Ottoman travel included:

- **Turkish coaches** (*araba*): Covered wagons with painted and gilded wooden lattices, lined with silk and "richly embroidered and fringed." Unlike European glass-windowed coaches, these used lattice screens that "entirely hides the persons in them, but may be thrown back at pleasure, and thus permits the ladies to peep through" (026-002). They held four people on cushions, seated rather than elevated on benches.

- **Roads**: The quality varied enormously. Montagu found the roads approaching Constantinople "well paved, shaded on all sides with large trees" — a landscape that made "the whole country appears a large garden" (002-002). Elsewhere, roads were rougher, and Montagu recorded being "so fatigued" by coach travel that she could not sleep (015-002).

- **Accommodations**: Travelers stayed in caravanserais (public inns for merchant caravans), private houses where hospitality was extended to travelers, or tents when no building was available. Montagu noted that Ottoman hosts provided for travelers generously.

- **River travel**: On the Danube, Montagu traveled by water, describing a "journey perfectly agreeable, down the river" — a welcome alternative to overland routes (007-002).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's travel writing combines practical detail with aesthetic appreciation. Her description of the Turkish coach is the most detailed English account of this vehicle: she notes its construction "in the manner of the Dutch stage-coaches," its painted baskets and nosegays of flowers, its "poetical mottos" decorating the interior, and its scarlet cloth covering lined with silk (026-002).

The purpose of the covered coach's design was not merely decorative. The lattice and covering "entirely hides the persons in them" — a feature that allowed Ottoman women to travel without being seen, preserving the gender segregation that structured Ottoman public life while enabling mobility.

Montagu's observation that she was "very well pleased with my journey" and that "we take care to make such short journeys" reflects the deliberate pacing of elite Ottoman travel (002-002, 003-002). Unlike European travelers who often prided themselves on speed and endurance, Ottoman travel culture prioritized comfort and enjoyment.

## Cultural Context

The Ottoman Empire maintained an extensive network of imperial roads and caravanserais that facilitated both military movement and commercial traffic. The caravanserai system, funded by *waqf* (pious endowments), provided free or low-cost accommodation to travelers regardless of religion — a public infrastructure that had no direct parallel in 18th-century Europe.

Montagu's journey was itself made possible by the Ottoman travel infrastructure she describes. The ability of an ambassador's wife to travel from Vienna to Constantinople with relative comfort and safety testified to the effectiveness of Ottoman roads, the security provided by Janissary escorts, and the hospitality networks that supported elite travel.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Travel | European Travel (18th c.) |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Coach design | Covered lattice (privacy) | Glass windows (visibility) |
| Accommodation | Caravanserais (free/low cost) | Inns (commercial) |
| Road quality | Variable; good near cities | Variable; turnpikes expanding |
| Women's mobility | Covered coach enabled anonymous travel | Restricted by social convention |
| Pacing | Comfort and enjoyment prioritized | Speed often valued |
