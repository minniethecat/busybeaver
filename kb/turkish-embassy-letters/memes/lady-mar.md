# Lady Mar

## Overview

Lady Mar (born Frances Pierrepont, c. 1690–1761) was the sister of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu and the most frequent addressee of the Turkish Embassy Letters, receiving 14 of the collection's letters. Anonymized in the published edition as the "Countess of ——," her identity was an open secret among contemporary readers — and her relationship with Montagu provides the emotional and familial anchor of the correspondence. The letters to Lady Mar are more intimate, candid, and personally revealing than those addressed to literary figures like Pope or intellectual correspondents like Abbé Conti.

## Biography

Frances Pierrepont was born around 1690, the daughter of Evelyn Pierrepont, later 1st Duke of Kingston-upon-Hull. Like her sister Mary, she received an unusual education for a woman of her class, developing literary interests that made her a receptive audience for Montagu's detailed travel accounts.

She married John Erskine, 6th Earl of Mar, a Scottish nobleman whose political career ended in disgrace after he led the Jacobite rising of 1715. Following the rising's failure, Lord Mar fled to the Continent, leaving Lady Mar in London. Her husband's attainder and exile placed her in a difficult social and financial position — circumstances that Montagu referenced obliquely in her letters.

Lady Mar eventually followed her husband into exile on the Continent. She died in 1761, a year before Montagu's own death.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

The 14 letters addressed to the "Countess of ——" form the backbone of the Turkish Embassy Letters. They are addressed to Montagu's closest confidante and cover the full range of her Ottoman experience: court ceremonies, domestic life, medical observations, and personal reflections.

The letters to Lady Mar are notably more frank than those to literary correspondents. Montagu shares her candid opinions of Ottoman customs, her frustrations with diplomatic life, and her observations about the relative freedom of Ottoman women compared to English women of equivalent rank. The intimacy of the address allows Montagu to develop arguments — such as her controversial claim that Turkish women enjoyed greater liberty than their English counterparts — that she might not have risked in letters intended for wider circulation.

Lady Mar's own troubled marriage — to an exiled Jacobite — provides an unspoken subtext to Montagu's observations about Ottoman marriage and divorce customs. When Montagu notes that Ottoman women can obtain divorce through formal legal procedures, she is implicitly contrasting this with her sister's situation: married to a man she could neither live with (due to his exile) nor divorce.
