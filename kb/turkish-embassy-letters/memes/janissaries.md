# Janissaries

## Overview

The Janissaries (*yenicheri*, "new soldiers") were the elite infantry corps of the Ottoman Empire, originally recruited through the *devshirme* system of conscripting Christian boys who were converted to Islam and trained for military and administrative service. By the time of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's embassy (1716–1718), the Janissaries had evolved from a disciplined military force into a powerful political constituency capable of deposing sultans. Montagu's observations capture the Janissaries at the height of their domestic political influence.

## Description

The Janissary corps constituted the standing army of the Ottoman Empire and served as the sultan's household troops. Their organization, discipline, and distinctive uniforms — including the tall *börk* headdress — made them immediately recognizable throughout Ottoman territories.

By the 18th century, the Janissaries had become deeply embedded in Ottoman urban life. Many had married, entered trade, and established family networks that blurred the line between military and civilian society. This integration amplified their political power: the corps could mobilize not only military force but also economic and social pressure.

Montagu observed that even the sultan "trembles at a janizary's frown" — a striking characterization of the power inversion that had occurred within the Ottoman political system (028-004). The Janissaries had deposed Sultan Mustafa, and their capacity to make or unmake rulers was a defining feature of 18th-century Ottoman politics (027-003).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's encounter with the Janissaries was personal as well as political. Her own Janissary guard — assigned to protect the British embassy — demonstrated both the corps' protective function and its potential for independent action. In one incident, her Janissary "in the height of his zeal" for her safety attempted to secure her passage through a crowd, though he "could get none" — illustrating both the Janissary's authority and its limits (028-008).

Her most acute political observation concerned the Janissaries' role in the deposition of Sultan Mustafa, whom "they deposed" — the pronoun "they" attributing agency to the corps rather than to any broader political movement (027-003). This recognition of institutional rather than personal power was characteristic of Montagu's analytical approach to Ottoman politics.

In a later letter, Montagu recorded a Janissary who had distinguished himself in battle against the Imperialists, noting "a janizary, who, in nine days, and yet went on foot, came from Belgrade to Constantinople" — a testament to the physical endurance that underpinned Janissary military prowess (055-002).

## Cultural Context

The Janissary corps originated in the 14th century under Sultan Murad I and reached its peak effectiveness in the 15th–16th centuries. By Montagu's time, the devshirme system had largely fallen into disuse — most Janissaries were now free-born Muslims who inherited their positions — and the corps' military effectiveness had declined as its political power increased.

The Janissaries' role in sultanic depositions was part of a broader pattern of 18th-century Ottoman political instability. Between 1618 and 1730, six sultans were deposed, several with Janissary involvement. Montagu's observation that the sultan "trembles at a janizary's frown" was not hyperbole but an accurate characterization of the balance of power.
