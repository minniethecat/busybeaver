# London

## Overview

London was Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's home city and the implicit audience for her Turkish Embassy Letters. Throughout her travels, Montagu consistently used London as a benchmark against which she measured foreign cities, customs, and institutions. Her comparisons — Pera to Westminster, Ottoman baths to English assemblies, Turkish poetry to English verse — assumed a London reader who would recognize the reference points. London appears less as a described place than as the constant, unspoken presence shaping Montagu's comparative method.

## Geography and Significance

In the early eighteenth century, London was the largest city in Western Europe, with a population approaching 600,000. The city had been rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1666, and its new architecture — St. Paul's Cathedral, the Wren churches, the expanding West End squares — reflected England's growing commercial and imperial power. Montagu's social circle included the literary and political elite centered on the Kit-Cat Club, the court of George I, and the publications of Addison and Steele.

London was also the center of English print culture, and Montagu's letters — though circulated privately during her lifetime — were shaped by the expectations of a London literary audience. Her references to English poetry, her comparisons of Ottoman and English social conventions, and her discussions of medical practices all assumed a London reader familiar with current debates.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

London functions in the Turkish Embassy Letters as the normative center against which difference is measured. Montagu's comparison of Pera to Westminster — "no more a suburb of Constantinople than Westminster is a suburb of London" — is typical of her method, using a London landmark to make Ottoman geography legible to English readers (035-006).

The comparison also works in reverse: Montagu uses her Ottoman observations to critique London customs. When she describes the civility of the Sophia bath, she contrasts it with "the disdainful smiles, and satirical whispers" that "never fail in our assemblies" — an implicit critique of London social behavior that uses distance to create critical perspective (026-004). Similarly, her discussions of Ottoman medical practices — particularly smallpox inoculation — were framed as interventions in London medical debates, with Montagu positioning herself as an advocate for the adoption of Ottoman techniques in England.
