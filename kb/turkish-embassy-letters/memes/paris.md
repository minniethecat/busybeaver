# Paris

## Overview

Paris was the cultural capital of eighteenth-century Europe and a recurring point of reference in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's Turkish Embassy Letters. Montagu had visited Paris and knew French literature and fashion intimately. Her comparisons between French, English, and Ottoman customs reveal a sophisticated understanding of cultural relativity — Paris represented neither a norm nor an exotic other, but one position within a spectrum of possible social arrangements that included London, Vienna, and Constantinople.

## Geography and Significance

In the early eighteenth century, Paris was the center of European fashion, philosophy, and court culture. The court of Louis XIV (until 1715) and the Regency that followed set the standard for aristocratic behavior across Europe. French was the language of diplomacy and polite society — a fact that shaped Montagu's own experience, as she noted the prevalence of French among Ottoman elites and the diplomatic community in Pera.

Parisian fashion, in particular, exercised a cultural dominance that Montagu both acknowledged and criticized. Her satirical descriptions of Viennese court dress implicitly compare it to Parisian norms, and her observations of Ottoman women's dress — which she found more graceful than European fashion — were framed against the backdrop of Parisian sartorial authority.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Paris appears in the Turkish Embassy Letters primarily as a comparative reference point. When Montagu describes Ottoman court culture, she measures it against both London and Paris, using the triangulation to establish her authority as a cultural critic. Her observation that French fashions had reached even the Ottoman court — "the French dress is very well known here" — reveals the global reach of Parisian cultural influence while also suggesting its limitations (045-004).

Montagu's later letters, written after her return from Constantinople and during her subsequent residence in France, reflect her continued engagement with Parisian intellectual life. Her comparisons of Ottoman poetry to French verse, and her discussions of literary reputation in both cultures, position Paris as a node in a transnational republic of letters that included London, Constantinople, and beyond.
