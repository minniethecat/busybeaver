# Edward Wortley Montagu

## Overview

Edward Wortley Montagu (1678–1761) was a British Whig politician and diplomat who served as ambassador to the Ottoman Empire from 1716 to 1718. His appointment to the Constantinople embassy provided the occasion for his wife Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's celebrated Turkish Embassy Letters. Though Wortley Montagu himself is a peripheral figure in his wife's correspondence, his diplomatic position was the enabling condition for her observations and the embassy household she described.

## Biography

Born in 1678, Edward Wortley Montagu was the son of a wealthy Yorkshire family. He entered Parliament as a Whig and established himself in London political circles. In 1712, he eloped with Lady Mary Pierrepont — a match opposed by her father, the Duke of Kingston, who disapproved of Wortley Montagu's political ambitions and financial arrangements.

Wortley Montagu's appointment as ambassador to the Ottoman Empire in 1716 was part of the Whig government's diplomatic strategy. The embassy's mission was primarily commercial — to negotiate trade privileges and manage relations with Ottoman authorities — but it also served as an listening post for British interests in the eastern Mediterranean.

After returning from Constantinople in 1718, Wortley Montagu resumed his parliamentary career but never held major office again. His marriage to Lady Mary became increasingly distant, particularly after she left England for an extended stay in Italy in 1739. He died in 1761, a year before his wife's return to England and death.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Edward Wortley Montagu appears in his wife's letters primarily as a structuring absence rather than a described presence. As ambassador, he was the reason for the embassy; as husband, he was the companion she rarely mentions directly. Montagu's letters focus on her own observations and experiences rather than her husband's diplomatic work.

The embassy household Montagu described — with its Greek, French, English, and Italian visitors making "their court to me from morning till night" — was Wortley Montagu's establishment. The Janissary guards, the interpreters, the social access that allowed Montagu to visit the seraglio and the baths: all were products of her husband's diplomatic position.
