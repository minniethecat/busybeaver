# Montagu–Pope Correspondence

## Overview

The correspondence between Lady Mary Wortley Montagu and Alexander Pope during her embassy to Constantinople represents one of the most significant literary exchanges of early eighteenth-century England. Several of the Turkish Embassy Letters were addressed to Pope, who was then at the height of his fame as the author of *The Rape of the Lock* and the translator of Homer. Montagu's letters to Pope — blending travel narrative, cultural observation, and literary allusion — were crafted for a reader she regarded as her intellectual equal. The friendship later collapsed in one of the most notorious literary quarrels of the century.

## The Letters

Montagu addressed Letters XXIV, XXXVI, and several others to Pope, writing from Belgrade, Adrianople, and Constantinople. These letters differ in tone from those addressed to her sister and other correspondents: they are more densely allusive, more self-consciously literary, and more playful in their engagement with the classical tradition that Pope was translating (024-002).

Montagu knew that Pope would appreciate her classical references — the comparisons of Ottoman women to figures from Guido and Titian, the allusions to Milton and Homer, the witty deployment of epic conventions to describe the quotidian details of Ottoman travel. Writing to Pope allowed Montagu to demonstrate her literary credentials while also asserting her independence from the Augustan literary establishment in which Pope was a central figure.

## The Rupture

The friendship between Montagu and Pope ended in a bitter quarrel in the 1720s, the exact causes of which remain disputed. Pope subsequently attacked Montagu in his satires — most viciously in the *Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot* — portraying her as sexually dissolute and intellectually pretentious. Montagu's responses were more restrained, but the rupture colored the later reception of her work: Pope's satirical portrait influenced how Montagu was remembered by literary history.
