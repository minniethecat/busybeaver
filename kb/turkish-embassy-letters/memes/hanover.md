# Hanover

## Overview

Hanover was the capital of the Electorate of Brunswick-Lüneburg (commonly called Hanover) and the ancestral home of the Hanoverian dynasty that ruled Great Britain from 1714. For Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, Hanover was significant as the departure point of her embassy journey — the last German court she visited before traveling eastward. Her husband, Edward Wortley Montagu, had diplomatic connections to the Hanoverian court, which had become politically central after the accession of George I to the British throne.

## Geography and Significance

Hanover in the early eighteenth century was a medium-sized German princely capital, modest in comparison to Dresden or Vienna but politically significant due to its dynastic connection to Great Britain. The court of George I maintained residences in Hanover, and the city served as a way station for British diplomats and travelers moving between London and the German states.

Montagu's brief stay in Hanover occurred during a period of intense political interest in the Hanoverian succession. As the wife of a Whig MP with connections to the new dynasty, Montagu would have been sensitive to the political symbolism of Hanover — the German principality that had, through the accident of succession, become the arbiter of British politics.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's references to Hanover appear in the early letters of her journey (Letters V and VI), where she describes the German courts with her characteristic mixture of observation and irony. She was received at the Hanoverian court and noted the social dynamics of a principality that had suddenly become the subject of intense English interest.

Her observations from Hanover register the cultural differences between German and English court life — the more formal ceremonies, the different fashions, the distinctive social protocols — while also acknowledging the political ties that bound Hanover to London. The experience of navigating a German court that was also, in a sense, an English one prepared Montagu for the cultural negotiations she would later perform in the Ottoman Empire (006-002).
