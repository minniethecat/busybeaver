# Ottoman Food and Hospitality

## Overview

Ottoman hospitality, with its elaborate culinary rituals, coffee and sherbet service, and generous multi-course meals, was a defining feature of elite social life that Lady Mary Wortley Montagu encountered repeatedly during her travels. Her letters record not only the material abundance of Ottoman dining — "fifty dishes of meat all served in silver" — but also the social protocols governing hospitality, which she found more courteous and less competitive than European entertaining.

## Description

Ottoman elite dining was characterized by several distinctive features:

- **Abundance**: Meals commonly featured dozens of dishes, served in silver vessels, reflecting the host's wealth and generosity
- **Sequential service**: Unlike European service where all dishes appeared simultaneously, Ottoman meals unfolded in courses
- **Beverage rituals**: Coffee and sherbet (a sweet fruit drink) were integral to hospitality, served before, during, and after meals
- **Seating**: Guests reclined on cushions rather than sitting at tables, with slaves or servants attending to their needs
- **Gender separation**: Men and women dined separately on formal occasions, though Montagu experienced both male and female dining settings

The material culture of Ottoman dining was notably rich. Montagu described meals where "fifty dishes of meat" appeared "all served in silver, and well dressed" (007-002). Sherbet was "cried about as oranges are in the streets of London" — a ubiquitous refreshment rather than an exotic luxury (034-002).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu was invited to dine "by several of the first people" and found Ottoman hospitality notably gracious (007-002). Unlike European dinner parties, which she characterized as competitive displays, Ottoman hosting emphasized the guest's comfort and the host's generosity.

She recorded a specific hospitality protocol: it was customary that a man who "invited a woman of quality to dinner" was also expected to provide her with a gift — typically a jewel or fabric — transforming the meal into an exchange of mutual obligation rather than a simple social call (010-003).

Coffee and sherbet service were ubiquitous markers of Ottoman hospitality. At the grand vizier's court, attendants "generally attended by a great number of servants, who bring them coffee, sherbet, and perfumes" — the combination of beverage service with perfuming indicating the multi-sensory nature of Ottoman hospitality rituals (030-007).

In more modest settings, Montagu observed women "drinking their coffee" at home, noting that "neither are they ignorant of how to employ their time" — a defense of Ottoman domesticity against European assumptions of idleness (032-004).

## Cultural Context

Ottoman hospitality was rooted in both Islamic values of generosity toward guests and the social competition of elite households. The scale of entertaining — the number of dishes, the value of serving vessels, the quality of coffee and sherbet — communicated a household's status within the Ottoman social hierarchy.

The practice of giving gifts to dinner guests inverted European norms: in England, the guest brought a gift; in the Ottoman Empire, the host provided one. This difference reflects broader cultural attitudes about hospitality as an expression of power and magnanimity.
