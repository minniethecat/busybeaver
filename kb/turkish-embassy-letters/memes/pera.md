# Pera

## Overview

Pera (modern Beyoğlu, Istanbul) was the European diplomatic quarter of Constantinople, situated across the Golden Horn from the old city. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu resided in Pera throughout her embassy stay, and her letters from this address — dated "Pera of Constantinople" — provide a detailed picture of diplomatic life on the margins of Ottoman power. Montagu's comparison of Pera to Westminster established a frame of reference her English readers would immediately understand.

## Geography and Character

Pera occupied the heights north of the Golden Horn, looking across the water toward the seraglio and the domes and minarets of old Constantinople. Montagu described it as "no more a suburb of Constantinople than Westminster is a suburb of London" — a comparison that captured both Pera's proximity to and distinctness from the imperial capital (035-006).

The quarter housed the embassies of European powers — British, French, Venetian, Dutch — each a self-contained diplomatic household with its own staff, guards, and social world. The British embassy palace, where Montagu lived, was a substantial establishment that received a stream of visitors: "the great number of Greeks, French, English, and Italians that are under our protection, make their court to me from morning till night" (031-002).

The European character of Pera coexisted with Ottoman institutions. Mosques stood alongside embassy chapels, and the streets mixed European and Ottoman dress, languages, and customs. This cultural hybridity made Pera a distinctive space — neither fully Ottoman nor fully European.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Pera was Montagu's home base and the location from which she wrote most of her letters. From her residence in the diplomatic quarter, she ventured into Ottoman spaces — the seraglio, the baths, elite women's homes — and returned to Pera to write about what she had seen. The physical distance between Pera and the old city mirrored the cultural distance Montagu navigated daily.

The letters from Pera span the full range of Montagu's interests: Ottoman court life, medical practices, social customs, religious observances, and the personal relationships of the diplomatic community. Pera itself appears as both setting and subject — the bridge between European and Ottoman worlds that Montagu inhabited.
