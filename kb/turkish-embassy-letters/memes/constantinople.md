# Constantinople

## Overview

Constantinople (modern Istanbul) was the imperial capital of the Ottoman Empire and the centre of [[Lady Mary Wortley Montagu]]'s embassy from 1717 to 1718. Her descriptions of the city — its architecture, its multi-confessional population, and its geography straddling Europe and Asia — form the geographic anchor of the Turkish Embassy Letters.

From her residence in Pera she observed both the public face of Ottoman power — the [[Sultan Ahmed III|sultan]]'s court and ceremony — and the private world of the [[Ottoman Seraglio]] and its women. The city was the destination of the [[Embassy Journey 1716–1717]] that carried her east.

## Geography and Architecture

Constantinople occupied a strategic peninsula on the Bosphorus, the strait separating Europe from Asia. Montagu described a skyline dominated by imperial mosques whose free-stone construction marked the sultan's patronage:

> Their mosques are all of free-stone, and the public hanns, or inns, extremely magnificent. (032-004)

The city's architecture reflected its layered history. Hagia Sophia (Sancta Sophia), the great Byzantine church converted to a mosque, remained its most remarkable structure, though restricted access made it "very difficult to see" for non-Muslims (041-002). The juxtaposition of Byzantine and Ottoman monuments created a cityscape unlike anything in Europe.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Constantinople serves as both setting and subject. From Pera, Montagu observed the sultan's public ceremonies, received visitors from the city's diverse communities, and corresponded with her English contacts. Its mixed population — Greeks, Armenians, Jews, and "Franks" — supplied constant material for cultural comparison.

Montagu situated her own diplomatic quarter within a geography her English readers would immediately understand:

> Pera ... which is no more a suburb of Constantinople, than Westminster is a suburb to London. (035-006)

The city was, for Montagu, both a diplomatic posting and an ethnographic laboratory.
