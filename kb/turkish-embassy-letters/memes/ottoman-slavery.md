# Slavery in the Ottoman Empire

## Overview

Slavery in the Ottoman Empire was a complex institution that differed fundamentally from the racialized chattel slavery of the Atlantic world. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu encountered enslaved people throughout her travels — as domestic servants, harem attendants, military recruits, and imperial administrators — and her letters offer glimpses of a system in which enslaved status did not preclude wealth, power, or social mobility. Her observations, while limited by her elite vantage point, reveal a slave system that was deeply embedded in Ottoman social and political structures.

## Description

Ottoman slavery operated on several distinct registers:

- **Domestic slavery**: Enslaved women and girls, often from the Caucasus or sub-Saharan Africa, served in elite households as attendants, companions, and concubines. Montagu observed that "their slaves (generally pretty girls of seventeen or eighteen) were employed in braiding their hair in several pretty fancies" at the bath (026-005). These domestic slaves were integrated into the household rather than segregated from it.

- **Military-administrative slavery**: The *devshirme* system and its successor practices brought enslaved or conscripted boys into the Janissary corps and imperial administration, where they could rise to the highest offices of state. This system produced grand viziers and provincial governors who were technically slaves of the sultan.

- **Court slavery**: The imperial harem was staffed by enslaved women who, if they became the sultan's favorites or the mothers of his children, could exercise significant political influence. The *valide sultan* (queen mother) was often herself of slave origin.

- **Eunuch slavery**: Black eunuchs, typically from East Africa, served as guardians of the harem. Montagu recorded being met "at the door by two black eunuchs, who led me through a long gallery" when visiting an elite Ottoman woman (033-004).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's perspective on Ottoman slavery was shaped by her experience as an elite woman whose own society practiced forms of servitude (indentured labor, apprenticeship, the subordinate legal status of wives). She did not write a systematic analysis of Ottoman slavery, but her incidental observations reveal its pervasiveness in elite Ottoman life.

At the women's bath, she noted that slaves attended their mistresses "behind them, but without any distinction of rank by their dress, all being in the state of nature" — an observation that highlights both the presence of slaves and the temporary suspension of status distinctions within the bath (026-003). The phrase "without any distinction of rank" is significant: in the bath, nakedness equalized, and slave and mistress were momentarily indistinguishable.

Montagu's own encounters with enslaved people included the "black eunuch" who helped her from her coach and the slaves who served coffee and sherbet at elite gatherings. She recorded these encounters matter-of-factly, without the moral outrage that later abolitionist writers would bring to the subject — a reflection of 18th-century elite attitudes rather than personal indifference.

In Letter XXXIX, she noted that a woman of thirty-six "has no black eunuchs for her guard, her husband being content with that" — suggesting that the number of slaves and eunuchs a household maintained was a marker of status and wealth (039-003).

## Cultural Context

Islamic law regulated slavery in ways that differed from the Roman law tradition inherited by Europe. Enslaved people had certain legal protections, including the right to petition for manumission. Children born to a free father and an enslaved mother who was his concubine were free — a rule that prevented the development of a hereditary slave caste. Manumission was encouraged as a pious act, and many slaves were freed after a term of service.

These legal features distinguished Ottoman slavery from Atlantic chattel slavery, where enslaved status was permanent, hereditary, and racialized. However, the Ottoman system was still a system of involuntary servitude, and the experience of enslaved people — particularly those in agricultural or galley labor — could be brutal.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Slavery | Atlantic Slavery (18th c.) |
|--------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Legal basis | Islamic law; protections and manumission paths | Roman law; chattel property |
| Heredity | Children of free fathers were free | Hereditary through mother |
| Racial basis | Multi-ethnic; not strictly racialized | Racialized (African) |
| Social mobility | Possible (military/administrative elite) | Virtually impossible |
| Manumission | Encouraged as piety | Rare and restricted |
