# Nicolas Boileau-Despréaux

## Overview

Nicolas Boileau-Despréaux (1636–1711) was the foremost French literary critic and poet of the 17th century, whose *Art poétique* (1674) codified the principles of French Neoclassicism. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu references Boileau in Letter XXX, quoting his observation on love and jealousy to support her own argument about the passions. The reference reveals Montagu's familiarity with French literary culture and her habit of anchoring personal observations in literary authority.

## Biography

Boileau was born in Paris in 1636 and initially studied law before dedicating himself to letters. His *Satires* (1666–1668) established his reputation as a sharp critic of contemporary literary fashion, and his *Art poétique* became the definitive statement of French Neoclassical doctrine, influencing English Augustan poets including Pope and Dryden.

As historiographer royal to Louis XIV, Boileau occupied a central position in French literary life. His friendship with Racine, Molière, and La Fontaine placed him at the heart of the Grand Siècle's literary achievement. He died in 1711, five years before Montagu's Ottoman embassy.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu references Boileau in Letter XXX: "Monsieur Boileau has very justly observed, that love makes a man look upon the defects of his mistress as perfections" (030-026). The citation serves a rhetorical purpose — Montagu is making an argument about the passions, and she invokes Boileau as an authority whose observation confirms her own.

The reference demonstrates Montagu's intellectual range. Writing from Constantinople to an English correspondent, she draws on a French literary authority — a gesture that positions her within the cosmopolitan literary culture of the European Enlightenment rather than within any narrowly English tradition.
