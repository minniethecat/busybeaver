# Jean de La Fontaine

## Overview

Jean de La Fontaine (1621–1695) was the great French fabulist whose *Fables* (1668–1694) are among the most enduring works of French literature. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu references La Fontaine in Letter XLIV, alluding to one of his tales — "the adventure, which Monsieur de la Fontaine has told so agreeably" — to illustrate a point about human credulity and the persistence of custom. The reference demonstrates Montagu's easy familiarity with French literary culture.

## Biography

La Fontaine was born in Château-Thierry in 1621 and spent much of his career under the patronage of French aristocrats, including Nicolas Fouquet and Madame de La Sablière. His *Fables*, adapting Aesop and other classical sources into elegant French verse, made him a central figure of the Grand Siècle. His *Contes et nouvelles en vers* (1665–1666), more ribald in content, also enjoyed wide circulation.

La Fontaine was elected to the Académie Française in 1684 and was part of the literary circle that included Molière, Racine, and Boileau. He died in 1695.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu references La Fontaine in Letter XLIV, noting that despite the "adventure, which Monsieur de la Fontaine has told so agreeably," the practice she is describing continues unabated — "abolished by a papal decree" but not by common sense (044-010). The reference uses La Fontaine's tale as a foil: even the French fabulist's witty treatment of human folly has not succeeded in changing the custom Montagu is criticizing.

The allusion reveals Montagu's literary sophistication. She expects her correspondent to recognize a La Fontaine reference without citation — evidence of the shared cultural framework that connected literate Europeans across national boundaries.
