# Adrianople

## Overview

Adrianople (modern Edirne, Turkey) was a major Ottoman city in Thrace and, during the reign of [[Sultan Ahmed III]], a favoured imperial residence. [[Lady Mary Wortley Montagu]] spent several months here in 1717, on the last stage of the [[Embassy Journey 1716–1717]].

From Adrianople she wrote some of the most important letters in the collection — her celebrated account of [[Smallpox Inoculation]] (Letter XXXI) and her observations of court life and landscape. The city gave her the most sustained exposure to Ottoman urban life outside the capital.

## Geography and Significance

Adrianople lay in Thrace, roughly 150 miles northwest of [[Constantinople]], on the overland route from Belgrade. Montagu described the approach in glowing terms:

> The country from hence to Adrianople, is the finest in the world. (025-003)

The cultivated landscape — gardens, orchards, and well-kept roads — impressed her as evidence of Ottoman agricultural achievement, and confirmed her sense that the region had been badly misrepresented by earlier travellers.

The city served as a secondary imperial capital. The sultan and his court frequently resided there, and its architecture and gardens reflected that status. Writing from a house on the banks of the Hebrus, Montagu found the surrounding country laid out almost entirely for pleasure and cultivation.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Adrianople was the setting for several of Montagu's most important letters. Letter XXXI contains both her demystification of the plague and her detailed account of inoculation — the observation with the most lasting historical impact of any in her correspondence.

She also recorded the city's culture of open-air leisure, a public sociability organised around gardens, rivers, and coffee:

> For some miles round Adrianople, the whole ground is laid out in gardens ... a set party of them chuse out a green spot, where the shade is very thick, and there they spread a carpet, on which they sit drinking their coffee. (030-007)

This taste for landscape and public leisure suggested to Montagu a refinement that predated the European coffee house by decades.
