# Abbé Antonio Conti

## Overview

Antonio Schinella Conti (1677–1749), known as the Abbé Conti, was an Italian scholar, poet, and polymath who lived in London during the period of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's Ottoman embassy. He received six letters from Montagu — addressed to the "Abbot ——" or "Abbot of ——" in the published edition — that contain some of her most intellectually ambitious discussions of Ottoman culture, philosophy, and literature. Conti represented Montagu's connection to the European Republic of Letters during her time in Constantinople.

## Biography

Born in Padua in 1677 to a noble family, Antonio Conti entered the religious life without taking full vows, adopting the title *abbé* (abbot). He pursued studies in philosophy, mathematics, and literature across Europe, spending significant periods in Paris and London. A Newtonian in science and a polymath in letters, Conti translated works between Italian, French, and English and served as a cultural intermediary between Italian, French, and English intellectual circles.

Conti lived in London from approximately 1715 to 1718, overlapping with the period of Montagu's embassy. He moved in the same literary circles — he knew Pope, Addison, and other figures in Montagu's network — and his intellectual interests made him an ideal correspondent for a traveler observing an unfamiliar culture.

After leaving England, Conti returned to Italy, where he continued his scholarly work and maintained his European intellectual connections until his death in 1749.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

The letters to Conti (as the "Abbot ——" or "Abbot of ——") are among the most intellectually substantial in the Turkish Embassy Letters. Unlike the more personal letters to Lady Mar, the letters to Conti emphasize philosophical observation, literary comparison, and cultural analysis.

Montagu writes to Conti as an intellectual equal, offering the kind of comparative cultural analysis that would interest a scholar of European letters. The letters likely include discussions of Ottoman poetry (Conti was a poet himself), religious practices (Conti had theological training), and the philosophical implications of Ottoman social organization.

Conti's presence as a correspondent reveals an important dimension of Montagu's letters: they were not merely personal correspondence but contributions to a broader European conversation about culture, religion, and society. By addressing an Italian scholar living in London, Montagu positioned her observations within the cosmopolitan discourse of the Republic of Letters.
