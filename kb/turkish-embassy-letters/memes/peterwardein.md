# Peterwardein

## Overview

Peterwardein (modern Petrovaradin, Serbia) was a major Habsburg fortress on the Danube River that Lady Mary Wortley Montagu passed during her embassy journey in 1717. The fortress had been the site of a decisive battle just months earlier — the Battle of Peterwardein (August 1716), in which Prince Eugene of Savoy defeated a large Ottoman army — and Montagu's account registers the recent violence that had made the fortress a byword for Habsburg military power.

## Geography and Strategic Significance

Peterwardein occupied a commanding position on the right bank of the Danube, its fortifications designed by the French military engineer Vauban to control the river crossing and the land approaches to the Habsburg heartland. The fortress complex was one of the largest in Europe, with extensive outworks, bastions, and underground passages that made it a formidable defensive position.

The Battle of Peterwardein had been fought while Montagu was preparing for her journey, and news of Prince Eugene's victory had reached Western Europe with significant political impact. The battle confirmed Habsburg military superiority on the Danube frontier and paved the way for the subsequent capture of Belgrade — events that shaped the geopolitical context of Montagu's embassy (021-005).

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

Montagu's description of Peterwardein reflects her dual interest in military affairs and human experience. She noted the scale of the fortifications — "one of the strongest fortifications in the world" — and the visible traces of the recent battle: "the field of battle is still strewed with sculls and carcasses of unburied men, horses, and camels" (021-002).

The juxtaposition of military engineering and human remains is characteristic of Montagu's method: she registers the abstract strategic logic of the fortress while also attending to the embodied reality of war. Her observation that the unburied dead were "enough to strike one with horror" but produced only indifference in the local population — "they were accustomed to the sight" — reflects her Enlightenment interest in the relationship between environment and sensibility (021-003).
