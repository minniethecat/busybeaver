# Ottoman Coffee Culture

## Overview

Coffee was one of the defining features of Ottoman social life in the early eighteenth century, and Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's observations of Ottoman coffee-drinking practices in the Turkish Embassy Letters provide a valuable contemporary account of a cultural institution that fascinated European observers. Montagu encountered coffee everywhere in the Ottoman world — in the women's bath at Sophia (which she characterized as "the women's coffee-house"), at the public promenades of Adrianople, and in the private homes of elite Ottoman women — and her descriptions link coffee to larger themes of sociability, gender, and cultural difference.

## Coffee in Ottoman Social Life

Montagu's most famous reference to Ottoman coffee culture appears in her description of the Sophia bath, where women drank coffee while reclining on marble sofas in the steam-filled chambers — an image that condensed the association between coffee, leisure, and female sociability (026-003). The bath as "coffee-house" inverted European expectations: in London, coffee houses were masculine spaces of political debate and commercial transaction; in the Ottoman Empire, Montagu suggested, coffee culture belonged to women as much as to men.

At Adrianople, Montagu described the public walks where people gathered "to drink coffee, tea, &c. and enjoy one of the most beautiful prospects in the world" (003-002). The scenic coffee setting — a cultivated landscape with views across the plain — represented a form of public leisure that predated and perhaps exceeded contemporary European practice.

## Cultural Significance

Coffee was more than a beverage in Ottoman society — it was a social ritual, a marker of hospitality, and a vehicle for the kind of leisurely sociability that Montagu found so impressive in Ottoman women's culture. When elite Ottoman women received Montagu in their homes, coffee was invariably served, accompanied by sherbet, perfumes, and conversation (028-005). The ritual of coffee service — the tiny porcelain cups, the precise sequence of offering and acceptance — communicated social meanings that Montagu was careful to decode for her English readers.
