# Montagu's Return Journey 1718

## Overview

Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's return journey from Constantinople to England in the summer and autumn of 1718 differed significantly from her overland outward journey. Rather than retracing the Danube route through the Balkans, she sailed through the Mediterranean — a voyage that took her through the Greek Archipelago, past classical sites she recognized from her reading, and into the world of Mediterranean commerce and naval power. The return journey letters reflect Montagu's deepened understanding of the Ottoman world after more than a year's residence in Constantinople, her observations now shaped by sustained experience rather than first impressions.

## The Voyage

The return voyage proceeded through the Dardanelles into the Aegean Sea, through the Greek Archipelago — where Montagu noted the classical associations of islands she knew from Homer and Virgil — and onward to the Italian coast. From there, the route continued overland through Italy and France to the English Channel and home (044-003).

Montagu's return letters show a writer now fully confident in her comparative method. She moved easily between Ottoman, classical, and contemporary European frames of reference, using each to illuminate the others. Her observations of the Greek islands, in particular, blend antiquarian interest with ethnographic observation — the ruins of ancient temples viewed alongside the contemporary Greek communities that lived among them.

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

The return journey letters form a distinct section of the collection — less frequently discussed than the Constantinople letters but important for understanding Montagu's development as a traveler and writer. The Mediterranean voyage gave her a different perspective on the Ottoman Empire: viewed from the sea rather than from within, the empire appeared less as a centered autocracy and more as a coastal network of ports, islands, and trading communities.

Montagu's return letters also register the melancholy of departure — the sense that she was leaving behind a world she had come to understand and appreciate in ways her European correspondents could not fully share. Her last letters from Constantinople and her first from the voyage home are colored by this awareness of impending loss (044-005).
