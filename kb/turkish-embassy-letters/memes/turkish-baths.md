# Turkish Baths

## Overview

The Turkish bath — called a *bagnio* in Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's letters — was a central social institution of the Ottoman Empire, functioning as a space for hygiene, relaxation, and community gathering. Montagu's account of the women's bath in Sophia (Sofia) is one of the most celebrated passages in her Turkish Embassy Letters, remarkable for its ethnographic precision and its challenge to European assumptions about Ottoman gender relations.

## Description

Turkish baths were architecturally sophisticated structures built of stone, typically consisting of multiple domed chambers of progressively increasing temperature. Montagu described the bath at Sophia as having five interconnected domes:

- **The entrance hall**: Where a portress collected fees (typically a crown or ten shillings from ladies of quality)
- **The cold room**: A large marble-paved chamber with raised marble sofas and fountains of cold water, where bathers undressed and socialized
- **The warm room**: Heated by steam from the adjacent hot baths, too hot to remain clothed
- **The hot baths**: Two domed chambers with temperature-controlled water via mixing cocks

The architectural sophistication extended to private baths as well. Wealthy Ottoman houses each had their own bagnio, consisting of two or three rooms with marble pavements and facilities for hot or cold baths (032-003). The imperial baths, as Montagu observed in Constantinople, featured gilded roofs, white marble throughout, and paired chambers "exactly answering to one another" (043-002).

Water management was notably advanced: cold water from fountains ran through marble channels in the floor, carrying water between chambers — a system that both cooled and cleaned the public spaces.

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's description of visiting the women's bath at Sophia in Letter XXVI is the most extensive eyewitness account of an Ottoman hamam by an English writer. Traveling incognito in a hired Turkish coach, she arrived at ten o'clock to find the bath "already full of women" — some two hundred in total (026-003).

Her observations combine architectural precision with cultural commentary. She noted the complete absence of rank distinction — all women being "in the state of nature, that is, in plain English, stark naked, without any beauty or defect concealed" — and was struck by the dignified behavior of the bathers. Despite her own clothed appearance being "very extraordinary to them," the Ottoman women showed "not the least surprise or impertinent curiosity" (026-003).

Montagu explicitly contrasted this behavior with European social gatherings: "I know no European court, where the ladies would have behaved themselves in so polite a manner to such a stranger." She observed that the bagnio functioned as "the women's coffee-house, where all the news of the town is told, scandal invented, &c." (026-003).

The aesthetic dimension of Montagu's account is equally significant. She compared the bathers to figures drawn by Guido or Titian, their hair "braided either with pearl or ribbon, perfectly representing the figures of the Graces" — a description that deliberately invokes classical beauty ideals to challenge European assumptions about Ottoman culture.

In Letter XLII, she returned to the social function of baths, noting that they were among the few public spaces available to women: "they have no places but the bagnios, and these can only be seen by their own sex" (042-001). Newly allied families would meet at the bath, and many attended out of curiosity, suggesting the bagnio served as both a social network hub and a marriage market (042-002).

## Cultural Context

The Turkish bath was a gendered institution in Ottoman society. While men had their own bathing facilities, the women's bath served as a uniquely female public sphere — a space for socializing, news-sharing, and community-building that operated outside male surveillance. Montagu's recognition of this function anticipates modern scholarly analyses of the hamam as a site of women's agency.

The bath also served medical purposes. The hot baths at Sophia were "very famous for their medicinal virtues" (025-003), and Montagu noted that the temperature-controlled water system allowed bathers to select their preferred degree of warmth — a sophistication not common in European spas of the period.

Montagu's account is notable for what it does NOT do: unlike many European travel narratives, she did not sexualize or exoticize the naked bathers. Instead, she emphasized their "majestic grace" and "obliging civility," positioning the bath as a space of respectable female sociability rather than an orientalist fantasy.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Bagnio | European Spa (18th c.) |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Social function | Women's community hub, news exchange | Mixed-gender socializing, gambling |
| Architecture | Domed stone, marble, graduated heat chambers | Often open pools, limited temperature control |
| Social mixing | All classes together, no rank distinction | Strict class segregation |
| Cost | Low (crown or ten shillings tip to portress) | Variable, often expensive |
| Gender | Strictly separated by sex | Mixed bathing in some regions |
