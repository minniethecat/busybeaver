# Ottoman Gardens and Landscape

## Overview

The garden was a central element of Ottoman aesthetic culture, appearing in forms ranging from the imperial pleasure gardens of the seraglio to public promenades, courtyard plantings, and the agricultural landscapes that surrounded Ottoman cities. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu encountered Ottoman gardens throughout her journey and repeatedly employed garden imagery to characterize the Ottoman landscape — most memorably in her claim that approaching Constantinople, "the whole country appears a large garden" (002-002).

## Description

Ottoman garden culture drew on Persian, Byzantine, and Islamic traditions, emphasizing water features, shade trees, flowers, and pavilions arranged for leisure and contemplation. Unlike the formal geometric gardens of contemporary France, Ottoman gardens prioritized sensory experience — the sound of water, the scent of flowers, the shade of plane trees — over geometric display.

Key forms of Ottoman garden included:

- **Imperial gardens**: The seraglio grounds in Constantinople featured extensive gardens with pavilions, fountains, and orchards that served as sites of informal political negotiation and imperial leisure
- **Public promenades**: Urban green spaces where both men and women gathered to walk, drink coffee, and enjoy "one of the most beautiful prospects in the world" (003-002)
- **Courtyard gardens**: Domestic gardens within the walled compounds of elite houses, providing private green space for women who could not access public promenades freely
- **Riverside gardens**: Along the shores of the Bosphorus and the Danube, where "shops for wafers and lemonade" served visitors enjoying the waterfront (002-002)

Water was essential to Ottoman garden design. Fountains, channels, and pools provided both irrigation and the sensory pleasure of moving water — a feature Montagu noted with appreciation.

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's observation that "the whole country appears a large garden" near Constantinople was not merely a traveler's hyperbole (002-002). The Ottoman landscape she described — with its well-paved roads "shaded on all sides with large trees" and set with "thick large branches of the jessamine and the honeysuckles" — was a deliberately cultivated environment that blended agriculture, horticulture, and aesthetics.

In Adrianople, she noted public walks where people gathered to drink coffee and tea while enjoying scenic views — a form of landscaped public leisure that predated the European public park movement (003-002). She acknowledged that the walks had "no great beauty but the thick shade of the trees," yet the combination of shade, refreshment, and prospect made them popular gathering places.

The interior gardens of the seraglio, which Montagu accessed through her diplomatic connections, represented the highest expression of Ottoman garden art. These were spaces where the sultan's favorites walked among flowers and fountains — a controlled natural environment that extended the architecture of the palace into the landscape.

## Cultural Context

The Ottoman garden was both a physical space and a cultural ideal. In Ottoman poetry and miniature painting, the garden (*bağ*) symbolized paradise, love, and the cultivated refinement of courtly life. Montagu's observation that the Ottoman countryside "appears a large garden" reflects not only the reality of the landscape but also the aesthetic lens through which Ottomans viewed their environment.

The Ottoman garden tradition influenced European garden design, particularly through the Turkish tent and kiosk (*köşk*) that became fashionable in 18th-century European landscape gardens. Montagu's descriptions of Ottoman gardens contributed to this cultural transfer, providing English readers with images of an alternative garden aesthetic.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Garden | European Formal Garden (18th c.) |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| Design principle | Sensory (water, scent, shade) | Geometric (symmetry, perspective) |
| Water features | Integral (fountains, channels) | Decorative (canals, basins) |
| Public access | Public promenades common | Private estates dominate |
| Symbolism | Paradise, courtly refinement | Power, order, mastery of nature |
| Planting | Trees, flowers, fruit | Parterres, topiary, exotics |
