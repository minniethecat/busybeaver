# Embassy Journey 1716–1717

## Overview

[[Lady Mary Wortley Montagu]]'s overland journey from Vienna to [[Constantinople]] (August 1716 – April 1717) was the formative experience of her Turkish Embassy Letters. Travelling with her husband's diplomatic party through Habsburg and Ottoman territory, she recorded the landscape, architecture, and customs of southeastern Europe in unprecedented detail.

The journey letters established the observational method and comparative framework that would define the [[Constantinople]] letters, and carried her through the war-torn frontier of the [[Siege of Belgrade 1717]] to the imperial residence at [[Adrianople]].

## Route and Stages

The journey proceeded through several distinct stages:

- **Vienna to Belgrade** (late 1716 – early 1717): through Habsburg territory into Ottoman domains
- **Belgrade to Sophia**: through the Balkan mountains and the ruined frontier towns of Serbia
- **Sophia to Adrianople**: including the celebrated incognito visit to the women's bath
- **Adrianople to Constantinople**: the final stage, arriving in the imperial capital

At each stage, Montagu recorded her impressions. She judged the Ottoman heartland to surpass anything in Europe:

> The country from hence to Adrianople, is the finest in the world. (025-003)

## In the Turkish Embassy Letters

The journey letters open the collection and introduce readers to the Ottoman world gradually, as Montagu herself encountered it. Early letters focus on landscape and infrastructure; later ones delve into social custom as her access and understanding increased.

The Turkish coach became the iconic vehicle of the journey — a covered waggon that let Ottoman women travel unseen, and which Montagu adopted for her own incognito excursions:

> In one of these covered waggons, I went to the bagnio about ten o'clock. (026-003)

The journey was both a physical transit and an intellectual transformation: Montagu began as an English traveller and ended as an informed observer of Ottoman society.
