# Countess of Bristol

## Overview

The Countess of Bristol (probably Elizabeth Felton, Countess of Bristol, c. 1676–1741) was an English aristocrat and a correspondent of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu during her Ottoman embassy. Addressed as the "Countess of B——" in the published edition of the Turkish Embassy Letters, she received three letters focusing on Ottoman court life, social customs, and Montagu's observations of elite society — topics selected for a woman of high rank with courtly interests.

## Biography

Elizabeth Felton was the daughter of Sir Thomas Felton and married John Hervey, 1st Earl of Bristol, becoming Countess of Bristol. The Hervey family was prominent in Whig political circles — the same circles in which Edward Wortley Montagu moved — and the Countess was part of the sophisticated London society that Montagu left behind for Constantinople.

The Countess of Bristol was known as a woman of intelligence and cultivation, maintaining literary and political connections. Her son, John Hervey (later Baron Hervey of Ickworth), would become a significant political figure and memoirist — and one of Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's most bitter literary enemies in later years.

## Role in the Turkish Embassy Letters

Three letters in the Turkish Embassy Letters are addressed to the "Countess of B——." These letters share characteristics with those addressed to Lady Rich and the Countess of —— (Lady Mar): they are personal in tone but substantial in content, combining social observation with cultural analysis.

The letters to the Countess of Bristol likely cover Ottoman court ceremonies, elite women's lives, and the diplomatic social world of Constantinople — subjects that would interest a woman of her rank and political connections. Montagu's choice to include her among the named (if anonymized) addressees indicates the Countess's significance within her social network.
