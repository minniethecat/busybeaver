# Montagu's Visit to the Sophia Bath

## Overview

In March 1717, during her overland journey to Constantinople, Lady Mary Wortley Montagu visited the women's public bath (*bagnio*) in Sophia, traveling incognito in a hired Turkish coach. Her account of this visit — Letter XXVI of the Turkish Embassy Letters — has become the most celebrated passage in the collection, admired for its ethnographic precision, its classical allusions, and its subversive comparison of Ottoman and European social behavior. The visit lasted four to five hours, during which Montagu observed approximately two hundred women bathing, socializing, and grooming in an all-female space that she memorably characterized as "the women's coffee-house."

## The Visit

Montagu arrived at the bath at ten o'clock in the morning, traveling in a Turkish coach with painted lattice windows and scarlet cloth covering — a vehicle that "entirely hides the persons in them" and allowed her to move through Ottoman public space incognito (026-003). She was received by a portress at the door, to whom she paid the customary fee of a crown or ten shillings.

Inside the five-domed stone building, Montagu encountered a scene that defied European expectations. Approximately two hundred women — "stark naked, without any beauty or defect concealed" — occupied the marble sofas and heated chambers. Some were drinking coffee or sherbet, others having their hair braided by slaves, still others engaged in conversation. Despite the nudity, Montagu observed "not the least wanton smile or immodest gesture" — a marked contrast with the sexualized European imagination of the harem.

The Ottoman women received Montagu — fully clothed in her riding dress and therefore "very extraordinary to them" — with "all the obliging civility possible." They invited her to undress and join them; when she demurred, they "were at last forced" to accept her excuse after she opened her shirt to show them her stays — European undergarments that satisfied their curiosity about her strange appearance.

## Significance

The Sophia bath visit provided Montagu with her most powerful argument for Ottoman civility. By explicitly comparing the bath to European assemblies — where "disdainful smiles, and satirical whispers, that never fail in our assemblies, when any body appears that is not dressed exactly in the fashion" — she used the Ottoman example to critique English social behavior. The bath became, in her account, evidence that civility was not the monopoly of Europe.
