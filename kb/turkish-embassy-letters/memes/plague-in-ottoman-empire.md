# Plague in the Ottoman Empire

## Overview

The plague was a recurring presence in 18th-century Ottoman life, yet Lady Mary Wortley Montagu observed that Ottoman attitudes toward the disease differed markedly from the terror it inspired in Europe. Where Europeans fled or quarantined at the mere rumor of plague, Ottomans had developed a more pragmatic accommodation — one that Montagu found, upon close observation, to be grounded in empirical reality rather than fatalistic resignation. Her analysis of plague management in the Ottoman Empire forms a significant component of her broader medical observations, alongside her more famous account of smallpox inoculation.

## Description

Montagu's key insight about plague in the Ottoman Empire was that its severity had been exaggerated in European accounts. "Those dreadful stories you have heard of the plague, have very little foundation in truth," she wrote to Mrs. S. C. from Adrianople. After observing several outbreaks firsthand, she concluded there was "little more in it, than in a fever" (031-003).

Several features characterized the Ottoman approach to plague:

- **Low mortality**: Montagu observed that "many that escape it" and that the disease did "so little mischief, they are not very solicitous about it"
- **No air transmission**: She was "persuaded, that it would be as easy a matter to root it out here, as out of Italy and France" — contradicting the European belief that plague was airborne and inescapable
- **Pragmatic fatalism**: Ottomans accepted plague as one distemper among many, rather than the uniquely terrifying affliction it was in European imagination

Montagu herself inadvertently experienced the reality of Ottoman plague management when she passed through "two or three towns most violently infected" without knowing it. Her hosts had concealed the outbreaks to avoid alarming her — and, crucially, no one in her party contracted the disease, despite sleeping in a house where "two persons died of it" (031-003).

## Montagu's Observations

Montagu's account of plague in Letter XXXI is deliberately structured to challenge European preconceptions. She begins by acknowledging her own fear — "I have much ado to reconcile myself to the sound of a word, which has always given me such terrible ideas" — before systematically dismantling the basis for that fear through empirical observation.

Her most striking claim was that the Ottoman Empire suffered from plague in place of the "variety" of diseases familiar in Europe — and that on balance, the Ottoman situation might be preferable, since plague was understood and manageable whereas the European "variety" offered no such predictability (031-003).

In Letter XXXVIII, Montagu recorded a darker aspect of plague: the fatalism it could produce. When she asked some people about their apparent lack of concern, "They answered, That the plague will certainly kill half of them" — a response that might be read as either stoic acceptance or a morbid recognition of statistical reality (038-002).

The cumulative effect of Montagu's plague observations was to demystify the disease. By treating plague as a medical and social phenomenon rather than a divine scourge, she anticipated the more empirical approaches to epidemiology that would develop later in the 18th century.

## Cultural Context

The Ottoman response to plague must be understood within the framework of Islamic attitudes toward disease and predestination. Some Muslims regarded plague as a martyrdom — a death sent by God that should be accepted rather than fled. This religious framing coexisted with practical public health measures, including the isolation of infected households.

Montagu's observation that Ottomans were "not very solicitous" about plague — that they accepted it as one disease among many rather than a uniquely terrifying affliction — reflects both this religious framework and the empirical reality that plague in the Ottoman context had a lower case fatality rate than the European imagination credited.

Her comparison between plague and the European "variety" of diseases is significant: it suggests that disease burden was roughly equivalent between the two societies, distributed differently rather than being objectively worse in one or the other.

## Comparative Notes

| Aspect | Ottoman Empire | Europe (18th c.) |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Perception of plague | Manageable distemper, one among many | Uniquely terrifying, divine punishment |
| Mortality rate (perceived) | Low — "many that escape it" | Very high in European imagination |
| Public response | Pragmatic acceptance, isolation of cases | Mass flight, quarantine, trade disruption |
| Disease variety | Low — plague as primary epidemic | High — "variety" of epidemic diseases |
| Religious framing | Martyrdom or divine will | Divine punishment |
