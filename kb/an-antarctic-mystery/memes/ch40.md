## Section 40

[040-001]None of these ships, then, were in those waters; so that, when our nutshell _[[the-paracuta|Paracuta]]_ was "alone on a lone, lone sea" beyond the [[iceberg|ice-barrier]], we were bound to believe that it was no longer possible we could be saved.

[040-002]We were fifteen hundred miles away from the nearest land, and winter was a month old!

[040-003]Hurliguerly himself was obliged to acknowledge the last fortunate chance upon which he had counted had failed us.

[040-004]On the 6th of April we were at the end of our resources; the sea began to threaten, the boat seemed likely to be swallowed up in the angry waves.

[040-005]"A ship!" cried the boatswain, and on the instant we made out a vessel about four miles to the north-east, beneath the mist which had suddenly risen.

[040-006]Signals were made, signals were perceived; the ship lowered her largest boat and sent it to our rescue.

[040-007]This ship was the _Tasman_, an American three-master, from

[040-008]Charlestown, where we were received with eager welcome and cordiality. The captain treated my companions as though they had been his own countrymen.

[040-009]The _Tasman_ had come from the [[falkland-islands|Falkland Islands]] where the captain had learned that seven months previously the American schooner _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_ had gone to the southern seas in search of the shipwrecked people of the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_. But as the season advanced, the schooner not having reappeared, she was given up for lost in the Antarctic regions.

[040-010]Fifteen days after our rescue the _Tasman_ disembarked the survivors of the crew of the two schooners at Melbourne, and it was there that our men were paid the sums they had so hardly earned, and so well deserved.

[040-011]We then learned from maps that the _Paracuta_ had debouched into the

[040-012]Pacific from the land called Clarie by Dumont d'Urville, and the land called Fabricia, which was discovered in 1838 by Bellenny.

[040-013]Thus terminated this adventurous and extraordinary expedition, which cost, alas, too many victims. Our final word is that although the chances and the necessities of our voyage carried us farther towards the south pole than those who preceded us, although we actually did pass beyond the axial point of the terrestrial globe, discoveries of great value still remain to be made in those waters!

[040-014][[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]], the hero whom Edgar Poe has made so famous, has shown the way. It is for others to follow him, and to wrest the last

[040-015]Antarctic Mystery from the Sphinx of the Ice-realm.

[040-016]THE END.
