## Overview

James Weddell (1787–1834) was a British navigator, sealer, and Antarctic explorer who served in the Royal Navy. In 1822, commanding the brig *Jane*, Weddell reached 74°15′S — the furthest southern latitude attained by any vessel up to that time. His achievement established a benchmark that Verne uses throughout the novel to measure the audacity and success of the *[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]* expedition.

## Role in the Novel

Weddell's southern record provides the historical baseline against which the novel's fictional Antarctic exploration is measured. Captain Len Guy enumerates the achievements of previous explorers in a famous passage:

> Captain Len Guy, in the Antarctic seas, had not gone beyond — Kemp, the sixty-sixth parallel; Ballerry, the sixty-seventh; Biscoe, the sixty-eighth; Bellinghausen and Morrell, the seventieth; Cook, the seventy-first; Weddell, the seventy-fourth. [011-015]

This enumeration positions Weddell as the furthest south of all historical navigators, making the *[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]*'s goal — to reach the eighty-third parallel, nearly five hundred and fifty miles beyond — appear all the more formidable.

## The Weddell Route

Throughout the novel, the Weddell Route is invoked as the path that both Weddell and [[william-guy|William Guy]] had followed into the Antarctic seas. The narrator [[jeorling|Jeorling]] frequently refers to Weddell's reports and the hope that the *[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]* might find the same passage open:

> This route it was that Weddell and then William Guy had followed, and, provided the schooner did not deflect either to the east or the west, she must inevitably come to Tsalal Island. [014-021]

The Weddell Route becomes almost mythologized — [[jeorling|Jeorling]] describes it wryly as though it were "a macadamized road, well kept, with mile-stones" [016-051], acknowledging the absurdity of treating such dangerous and uncharted waters as predictable.

## Verne's Use of Historical Fact

Verne's method in *An Antarctic Mystery* relies heavily on incorporating real historical figures like Weddell alongside fictional characters. This blending of fact and fiction is characteristic of Verne's *Voyages Extraordinaires*. By citing Weddell's actual 1822 achievement, Verne anchors the speculative elements of the narrative — the open polar sea, the magnetic phenomena, the Sphinx of the Ice — in authentic exploration history.

Weddell is mentioned alongside other real explorers including [[captain-cook|Captain James Cook]], Biscoe, Kemp, Balleny, Bellinghausen, and Morrell, forming a historical framework of Antarctic discovery that gives credibility to the fictional expedition of the *[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]*.
