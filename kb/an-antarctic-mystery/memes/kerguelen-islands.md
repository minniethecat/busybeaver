# Kerguelen Islands

The **Kerguelen Islands** (also known as the Desolation Islands) are a remote sub-Antarctic archipelago in the southern Indian Ocean, serving as the starting point of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_. Discovered in 1772 by the French navigator Baron Yves-Joseph de Kerguelen, the islands were "generally adopted for the centre of the southern fisheries" and formed the nucleus of a small whaling and sealing settlement at the time of the novel's opening (001-061) (001-062).

The narrator [[jeorling|Jeorling]] arrives at the Kerguelens as a tourist and mineralogist, lodging at the inn of the innkeeper [[atkins|Atkins]] at [[christmas-harbour|Christmas Harbour]] (001-077). It is here that he first encounters [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] and the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]], and where the Antarctic expedition is conceived.

## Geography and Setting

The Kerguelens possess "hundreds of other fjords" beyond Christmas Harbour, their shores "cut sharply, like the margin of a rock-bound coast" (001-067). The landscape is treeless, barren, and swept by incessant winds — a fitting gateway to the Antarctic wilderness beyond.

The islands lie at approximately 49°S latitude, in the path of the prevailing westerlies known to sailors as the "roaring forties." Their isolation is profound: the nearest human settlement of any size is thousands of miles distant.

## Role in the Novel

Christmas Harbour in the Kerguelens is the point of departure for the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] and the point of no return for [[jeorling|Jeorling]]. It is here that the narrator, initially a detached observer, commits to the expedition: "The idea of leaving the Kerguelens on the _Halbrane_ had full possession of me" (003-026).

The inn of [[atkins|Atkins]] serves as the novel's first setting — a microcosm of the whaling world where sailors from many nations congregate, and where the mysterious figure of [[hunt|Hunt]] (later revealed as [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]]) is first glimpsed (013-002).

The _Halbrane_ "positively leaves the Kerguelens in four days," setting a tone of urgency that carries through the entire narrative (002-059). From this moment, the expedition is committed, and there is no turning back.

## Real-World Context

The Kerguelen Islands remain a French overseas territory to this day, hosting a permanent scientific research station at Port-aux-Français. Verne's depiction of the islands as a whaling and sealing outpost accurately reflects their 19th-century role in the southern fisheries. The islands' nickname — "the Desolation Islands" — captures the bleakness that Verne evokes throughout the novel's opening chapters.
