# Pym's Disappearance

**Pym's Disappearance** is the central enigma of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_. After returning from the Antarctic with [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] vanished from recorded history — his ultimate fate unknown, his published narrative ending in an ambiguous vision of a "shrouded human figure" whose skin was "of the perfect whiteness of the snow." The question of what became of Pym is the engine that drives [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s obsessive quest and the mystery that the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition sets out to resolve.

## The Known Facts

What was known of Pym's disappearance came from fragmentary and contested sources. Edgar Allan Poe had published _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym_ — an account that most readers, including [[jeorling|Jeorling]], dismissed as fiction. The narrative ended with Pym and Peters drifting beyond the eighty-third parallel in a small boat, encountering a cataract of mist and the enigmatic white figure.

After their return, Pym and Peters separated. Peters — under the assumed name [[hunt|Hunt]] — lived in obscurity, first in Illinois, then at the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]]. Pym, however, simply vanished. His fate remained unknown for eleven years.

## The Resolution

The resolution, when it came, was both more scientific and more terrible than Poe's mystical ending. Pym had not vanished into a supernatural abyss. He had been carried by Antarctic currents beyond the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]], into the zone of the colossal magnetic sphinx. There, "while his boat was swept along on the northern current, he was seized by the magnetic fluid before he could get rid of the gun which was slung over his shoulder, and hurled against the fatal loadstone Sphinx of the Ice-realm" (039-024).

Pym's body, preserved by the Antarctic cold, remained at the foot of the sphinx for eleven years — "the head bent, a white beard hung down to the waist, the nails of the feet and hands like claws" (039-021). He had died not in mystical transcendence but in the grip of inexorable natural forces — a Vernean correction to Poe's supernaturalism.

## The Irony

The deepest irony of Pym's disappearance is that the one piece of evidence that might have led to his discovery — the iron musket slung across his body — was itself the instrument of his death. The magnetic sphinx drew every iron object to itself with irresistible force. Pym, unable to discard his weapon in time, was pinned to the loadstone and died there.
