# The Discovery of Patterson's Body

**The Discovery of Patterson's Body** is a pivotal event in Chapter 9 of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_.

During the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane's]] voyage from the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] toward [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan da Cunha]], the crew encounters a floating iceberg bearing the frozen corpse of [[patterson|Patterson]] — the mate of the lost [[the-jane|Jane]] and a shipmate of [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]] (009-026) (009-046).

The discovery transforms the expedition: it converts [[jeorling|Jeorling's]] scepticism into belief and provides the first material evidence that Edgar Allan Poe's _The Narrative of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]]_ was not fiction but fact.

> Captain Len Guy, who knew Patterson, had recognized him in this frozen corpse! It was indeed he who accompanied the captain of the Jane when he had interred that bottle, containing the letter which I had refused to believe authentic. [009-046]

## The Discovery

### The Iceberg Sighting

The encounter begins when [[james-west|James West]], scanning the horizon through his glasses, spots an unusual floating mass to starboard [008-026]. The object is initially ambiguous — some crewmen suggest it might be "the carcase of some deserted ship" — but as the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] draws closer, the shape resolves into a human figure encased in ice [008-027] [008-028].

A boat is lowered, and [[jeorling|Jeorling]], the boatswain, and several crewmen approach the iceberg. What they find is extraordinary: the perfectly preserved body of a man, frozen solid, with a notebook clutched in his rigid fingers [009-025] [009-026].

> So then for nearly three months Patterson's body had lain on the surface of this ice-waif which we had met on our way from the Kerguelens to Tristan d'Acunha! [009-046]

### Recognition

When [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] examines the corpse, his reaction is immediate and visceral. He lifts the hair preserved by the cold, gazes upon the scaled eyelids, and utters the name with a sob: "[[patterson|Patterson]]! [[patterson|Patterson]]!" [009-029]. The name resonates with [[jeorling|Jeorling]], who recalls that [[patterson|Patterson]] was the mate of the [[the-jane|Jane]] — the very ship that had carried [[william-guy|William Guy]]'s expedition to [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] eleven years earlier.

> Captain Len Guy lifted up the hair, which had been preserved by the cold, raised the head, gazed upon the scaled eyelids, and finally said with a sort of sob, — "Patterson! Patterson!" [009-029]

### The Notebook

In [[patterson|Patterson]]'s frozen hand is a fragmentary journal — the mate's final record of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s disastrous voyage. The notebook confirms key details of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s published narrative: the attack by natives on [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], the survival of some crew members who escaped to neighbouring islands, and [[patterson|Patterson]]'s own desperate drift northward on the ice before succumbing to starvation [009-043].

The notebook entry for June 16th reads: "exhausted... to-day... 16th of June... I am going to die" [009-045]. [[patterson|Patterson]] had survived for nearly three months on the ice before death claimed him.

## Narrative Significance

### The Sceptic's Conversion

The discovery of [[patterson|Patterson]]'s body marks the novel's most important turning point. Before this moment, [[jeorling|Jeorling]] — and through him the reader — regards [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s quest as a monomaniacal delusion. The idea that Poe's lurid adventure story could be factual seems absurd. "Like certain monomaniacs he had come to believe in his own inventions," [[jeorling|Jeorling]] had thought of the captain [005-059].

The frozen corpse changes everything. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] records the moment of his conversion: "My last doubts were buried in the depths of the ocean with the body of [[patterson|Patterson]]" [009-047]. The epistemological structure of the novel pivots: from scepticism to belief, from fiction to fact.

### Validation of the Quest

For [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]], the discovery is simultaneously devastating and vindicating. Devastating because it confirms the death of a man he knew and respected. Vindicating because it proves — at last — that his brother's expedition was real, that the [[the-jane|Jane]] had reached [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]], and that survivors might yet be found.

The discovery transforms [[captain-len-guy|Len Guy]] from an object of pity into a figure of tragic authority. His quiet, obsessive conviction, which had seemed like madness, is revealed as prescience.

### A Vernean Epistemological Hinge

The [[patterson|Patterson]] discovery is one of Verne's most effective narrative devices. By placing the proof of Poe's fiction within a corpse preserved in ice — an image at once Gothic and scientific — Verne achieves a double effect: he validates the fantastic elements of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s story while simultaneously grounding that validation in physical evidence. The frozen body is a forensic artifact, a document written in ice rather than ink.

## Aftermath

After [[jeorling|Jeorling]] reads [[patterson|Patterson]]'s journal, the body is committed to the deep. The crew gathers on deck as the ice-bound corpse slides into the Southern Ocean — the mate of the [[the-jane|Jane]] receiving, at last, the burial he had been denied for three months [009-047].

[[the-halbrane|The Halbrane]] alters course. With the proof of [[patterson|Patterson]]'s journal, [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s authority is restored and the expedition's purpose is no longer questioned. From this point forward, [[the-halbrane|the schooner]] sails south with the full conviction of its crew — toward the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]], the ice barrier, and the ultimate discovery of the Sphinx.
