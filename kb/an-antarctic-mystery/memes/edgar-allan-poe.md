# Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe (1809–1849) is the American poet and writer whose 1838 novel _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym_ provides the entire narrative premise of _An Antarctic Mystery_. Jules Verne constructs his novel as a direct sequel to Poe's work, with the characters treating Pym's account not as fiction but as a factual record that demands investigation and completion. Throughout the novel, Poe's name is invoked in debates about the boundary between imagination and reality, and his presence hovers over every major discovery.

## Poe's Romance as Narrative Foundation

The fifth chapter of the novel is explicitly titled "Edgar Poe's Romance," marking Poe's work as the essential backstory. Jeorling, the narrator, initially regards Poe's tale as pure invention:

> The hero of Edgar Poe's romance? [...] Edgar Poe's romance was nothing but a
> fiction, a work of imagination by the most brilliant of our American writers.
> [005-019]

Captain Len Guy, however, insists that Pym's narrative is factual, revealing that he personally investigated the matter in 1838:

> When Edgar Poe's narrative appeared in 1838, I was at New York. I immediately
> started for Baltimore, where the writer's family resided. [...] I inquired
> into certain matters relating to Edgar Poe. His abode was pointed out to me
> and I called at the house. [005-040]

## The Question of Truth

The central dramatic tension involving Poe is whether his published account was fiction or a disguised true story. Captain Guy presents the key evidence: Dirk Peters, the half-breed companion from Pym's narrative, is alive and serving on the Halbrane:

> Edgar Poe stated explicitly that Dirk Peters would be able to furnish
> information respecting the details of the voyage, and that he might be found
> at some future day. [005-048]

This claim transforms Poe from a distant literary figure into a direct participant in the novel's reality. The reader is forced to reconsider whether Poe was a novelist or, in effect, an early chronicler of Antarctic exploration.

## Poe's Philosophical Presence

Beyond the plot, Poe's philosophical observations are woven into the narrative texture. Jeorling quotes Poe's maxim about chance and fate:

> It is wise, as Edgar Poe has said, always "to reckon with the unforeseen, the
> unexpected, the inconceivable, which have a very large share in the
> accomplishment of the commonest events." [001-107]

Poe's insight into the nature of dreaming is also cited when Jeorling experiences a moment of unreality:

> Now — this is an observation made by Edgar Poe — when one suspects that one
> is dreaming, the waking comes almost instantly. [003-026]

## Verne's Treatment

Verne's engagement with Poe represents a remarkable act of literary homage. Rather than merely alluding to Poe, Verne builds an entire novel as a continuation of Poe's unfinished narrative. This intertextual strategy was unusual for its time and demonstrates Verne's deep respect for the American writer. Where Poe's _Pym_ ends in mystery — a white figure emerging from the Antarctic haze — Verne's novel attempts to supply the explanation, grounding Poe's supernatural overtones in natural phenomena and human agency.

The relationship between the two works raises enduring questions about authorship, truth, and the permeable boundary between fiction and exploration literature in the nineteenth century.
