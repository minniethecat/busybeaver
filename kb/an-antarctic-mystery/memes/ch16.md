## Section 16

[016-001]Was this a second accident? No! it was a voluntary action, a deed of self-sacrifice. Having finished his task, [[hunt|Hunt]] had thrown himself into the sea, that he might save [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]].

[016-002]"Two men overboard!"

[016-003]Yes, two--one to save the other. And were they not about to perish together?

[016-004]The two heads rose to the foaming surface of the water. [[dirk-peters|Hunt]] was swimming vigorously, cutting through the waves, and was nearing

[016-005]Martin Holt.

[016-006][Illustration: Hunt to the rescue.]

[016-007]"They are lost! both lost!" exclaimed the captain. "The boat,

[016-008]West, the boat!"

[016-009]"If you give the order to lower it," answered West, "I will be the first to get into it, although at the risk of my life. But I must have the order."

[016-010]In unspeakable suspense the ship's crew and myself had witnessed this scene. None thought of the position of the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_, which was sufficiently dangerous; all eyes were fixed upon the terrible waves.

[016-011]Now fresh cries, the frantic cheers of the crew, rose above the roar of the elements. Hunt had reached the drowning man just as he sank out of sight, had seized hold of him, and was supporting him with his left arm, while Holt, incapable of movement, swayed helplessly about like a weed. With the other arm Hunt was swimming bravely and making way towards the schooner.

[016-012]A minute, which seemed endless, passed. The two men, the one dragging the other, were hardly to be distinguished in the midst of the surging waves.

[016-013]At last Hunt reached the schooner, and caught one of the lines hanging over the side.

[016-014]In a minute Hunt and Martin Holt were hoisted on board; the latter was laid down at the foot of the fore-mast, and the former was quite ready to go to his work. Holt was speedily restored by the aid of vigorous rubbing; his senses came back, and he opened his eyes.

[016-015]"Martin Holt," said [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]], who was leaning over him,

[016-016]"you have been brought back from very far--"

[016-017]"Yes, yes, captain," answered Holt, as he looked about him with a searching gaze, "but who saved me?"

[016-018]"Hunt," cried the boatswain, "Hunt risked his life for you."

[016-019]As the latter was hanging back, Hurliguerly pushed him towards

[016-020]Martin Holt, whose eyes expressed the liveliest gratitude.

[016-021]"Hunt," said he, "you have saved me. But for you I should have been lost. I thank you."

[016-022]Hunt made no reply.

[016-023]"Hunt," resumed Captain Len Guy, "don't you hear?"

[016-024]The man seemed not to have heard.

[016-025]"Hunt," said Martin Holt again, "come near to me. I thank you.

[016-026]I want to shake hands with you."

[016-027]And he held out his right hand. Hunt stepped back a few paces, shaking his head with the air of a man who did not want so many compliments for a thing so simple, and quietly walked forward to join his shipmates, who were working vigorously under the orders of

[016-028]West.

[016-029]Decidedly, this man was a hero in courage and self-devotion; but equally decidedly he was a being impervious to impressions, and not on that day either was the boatswain destined to know "the colour of his words!"

[016-030]For three whole days, the 6th, 7th, and 8th of December, the tempest raged in these waters, accompanied by snow storms which perceptibly lowered the temperature. It is needless to say that Captain Len Guy proved himself a true seaman, that [[james-west|James West]] had an eye to everything, that the crew seconded them loyally, and that Hunt was always foremost when there was work to be done or danger to be incurred.

[016-031]In truth, I do not know how to give an idea of this man! What a difference there was between him and most of the sailors recruited at the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]], and especially between him and [[hearne|Hearne]], the sealing-master! They obeyed, no doubt, for such a master as James

[016-032]West gets himself obeyed, whether with good or ill will. But behind backs what complaints were made, what recriminations were exchanged!

[016-033]All this, I feared, was of evil presage for the future.

[016-034]Martin Holt had been able to resume his duties very soon, and he fulfilled them with hearty good-will. He knew the business of a sailor right well, and was the only man on board who could compete with Hunt in handiness and zeal.

[016-035]"Well, Holt," said I to him one day when he was talking with the boatswain, "what terms are you on with that queer fellow Hunt now?

[016-036]Since the salvage affair, is he a little more communicative?"

[016-037]"No, [[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]], and I think he even tries to avoid me."

[016-038]"To avoid you?"

[016-039]"Well, he did so before, for that matter."

[016-040]"Yes, indeed, that is true," added Hurliguerly; "I have made the same remark more than once."

[016-041]"Then he keeps aloof from you, Holt, as from the others?"

[016-042]"From me more than from the others."

[016-043]"What is the meaning of that?"

[016-044]"I don't know, Mr. Jeorling."

[016-045]I was surprised at what the two men had said, but a little observation convinced me that Hunt actually did avoid every occasion of coming in contact with Martin Holt. Did he not think that he had a right to Holt's gratitude although the latter owed his life to him? This man's conduct was certainly very strange.

[016-046]In the early morning of the 9th the wind showed a tendency to change in the direction of the east, which would mean more manageable weather for us. And, in fact, although the sea still remained rough, at about two in the morning it became feasible to put on more sail without risk, and thus the _Halbrane_ regained the course from which she had been driven by the prolonged tempest.

[016-047]In that portion of the Antarctic sea the ice-packs were more numerous, and there was reason to believe that the tempest, by hastening the smash-up, had broken the barrier of the [[iceberg|iceberg wall]] towards the east.

[016-048]CHAPTER XIII.

[016-049]ALONG THE FRONT OF THE ICEBERGS.

[016-050]Although the seas beyond the Polar Circle were wildly tumultuous, it is but just to acknowledge that our navigation had been accomplished so far under exceptional conditions. And what good luck it would be if the _Halbrane_, in this first fortnight of December, were to find the Weddell route open!

[016-051]There! I am talking of the Weddell route as though it were a macadamized road, well kept, with mile-stones and "This way to the

[016-052][[the-south-pole|South Pole]]" on a signpost!

[016-053]The numerous wandering masses of ice gave our men no trouble; they were easily avoided. It seemed likely that no real difficulties would arise until the schooner should have to try to make a passage for herself through the icebergs.

[016-054]Besides, there was no surprise to be feared. The presence of ice was indicated by a yellowish tint in the atmosphere, which the whalers called "blink." This is a phenomenon peculiar to the glacial zones which never deceives the observer.

[016-055]For five successive days the _Halbrane_ sailed without sustaining any damage, without having, even for a moment, had to fear a collision.

[016-056]It is true that in proportion as she advanced towards the south the number of ice-packs increased and the channels became narrower. On the 14th an observation gave us 72° 37' for latitude, our longitude remaining the same, between the forty-second and the forty-third meridian. This was already a point beyond the Antarctic

[016-057]Circle that few navigators had been able to reach. We were at only two degrees lower than Weddell.

[016-058]The navigation of the schooner naturally became a more delicate matter in the midst of those dim, wan masses soiled with the excreta of birds. Many of them had a leprous look: compared with their already considerable volume, how small our little ship, over whose mast some of the icebergs already towered, must have appeared!

[016-059]Captain Len Guy admirably combined boldness and prudence in his command of his ship. He never passed to leeward of an iceberg, if the distance did not guarantee the success of any manoeuvre whatsoever that might suddenly become necessary. He was familiar with all the contingencies of ice-navigation, and was not afraid to venture into the midst of these flotillas of drifts and packs. That day he said to me,--

[016-060]"Mr. Jeorling, this is not the first time that I have tried to penetrate into the Polar Sea, and without success. Well, if I made the attempt to do this when I had nothing but presumption as to the fate of the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_ to go upon, what shall I not do now that presumption is changed into certainty?"

[016-061]"I understand that, captain, and of course your experience of navigation in these waters must increase our chances of success."

[016-062]"Undoubtedly. Nevertheless, all that lies beyond the fixed icebergs is still the Unknown for me, as it is for other navigators."

[016-063]"The Unknown! No, not absolutely, captain, since we possess the important reports of Weddell, and, I must add, of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] also."

[016-064]"Yes, I know; they have spoken of the open sea."

[016-065]"Do you not believe that such a sea exists?"

[016-066]"Yes, I do believe that it exists, and for valid reasons. In fact, it is perfectly manifest that these masses, called icebergs and ice-fields, could not be formed in the ocean itself. It is the tremendous and irresistible action of the surge which detaches them from the continents or islands of the high latitudes. Then the currents carry them into less cold waters, where their edges are worn by the waves, while the temperature disintegrates their bases and their sides, which are subjected to thermometric influences."

[016-067]"That seems very plain," I replied. "Then these masses have come from the icebergs.(1) They clash with them in drifting, sometimes break into the main body, and clear their passage through.

[016-068]Again, we must not judge the southern by the northern zone. The conditions are not identical. Cook has recorded that he never met the equivalent of the Antarctic ice mountains in the Greenland seas, even at a higher latitude."

[016-069]"What is the reason?" I asked.

[016-070]"No doubt that the influence of the south winds is predominant in the northern regions. Now, those winds do not reach the northern regions until they have been heated in their passage over America,

[016-071]Asia, and Europe, and they contribute to raise the temperature of the atmosphere. The nearest land, ending in the points of the Cape of Good Hope, Patagonia, and Tasmania, does not modify the atmospheric currents."

[016-072]"That is an important observation, captain, and it justifies your opinion with regard to an open sea."

[016-073]"Yes, open--at least, for ten degrees behind the icebergs. Let us then only get through that obstacle, and our greatest difficulty will have been conquered. You were right in saying that the existence of that open sea has been formally recognized by

[016-074]Weddell."

[016-075]"And by Arthur Pym, captain."

[016-076]"And by Arthur Pym."

[016-077]From the 15th of December the difficulties of navigation increased with the number of the drifting masses. The wind, however, continued to be uniformly favourable, showing no tendency to veer to the south. The breeze freshened now and then, and we had to take in sail. When this occurred we saw the sea foaming along the sides of the ice packs, covering them with spray like the rocks on the coast of a floating island, but without hindering their onward march.

[016-078]Our crew could not fail to be impressed by the sight of the schooner making her way through these moving masses; the new men among them, at least, for the old hands had seen such manoeuvres before. But they soon became accustomed to it, and took it all for granted.

[016-079]It was necessary to organize the look-out ahead with the greatest care. West had a cask fixed at the head of the foremast--what is called a crow's-nest--and from thence an unremitting watch was kept.

[016-080]The 16th was a day of excessive fatigue to the men. The packs and drifts were so close that only very narrow and winding passage-way between them was to be found, so that the working of the ship was more than commonly laborious.
