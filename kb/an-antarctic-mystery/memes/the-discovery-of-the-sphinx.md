# The Discovery of the Sphinx

The **Discovery of the Sphinx** is the climactic event of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, occurring in Chapters 38 and 39 when the surviving crew of the _[[the-paracuta|Paracuta]]_ encounter the colossal [[iceberg|magnetic mountain]] at the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]] (038-021).

The formation — a natural structure shaped like an enormous sphinx — finally resolves the central mystery of the novel. This event reveals the ultimate fate of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]], brings about the death of the loyal half-breed [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], and gives name to the "Land of the Sphinx" — the last landmass visited before the survivors begin their desperate return journey northward (039-026).

The discovery transforms what had been an [[antarctic-exploration|Antarctic exploration]] mission into a confrontation with the sublime forces of nature — a colossal loadstone magnet whose inexorable attraction had destroyed [[the-halbrane|the Halbrane's]] stolen boat, killed the mutineer [[hearne|Hearne]] and his accomplices, and ultimately drew [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]] himself to his final resting place (038-056) (039-024).

## Context: The Approach to the Magnetic Pole

### The Voyage of the Paracuta

By early March, the survivors of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition — now sailing northward in their small iron-free boat, the _[[the-paracuta|Paracuta]]_ — had already passed beyond the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]] itself. Their position on 12 March placed them at 75 degrees 17 minutes south latitude, 118 degrees 3 minutes east longitude, approximately four hundred miles from the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]] [038-014] [038-016].

The journey was perilous in the extreme. The Antarctic summer was ending, and the sun hovered on the verge of the horizon, soon to disappear for the entire polar winter. The crew witnessed extraordinary electrical phenomena — sparks shooting from their fingers, hair, and beards in an electrical snowstorm — foreshadowing the magnetic marvel they were about to encounter [038-008].

> For three or four hours, sparks, accompanied by a sharp noise, shot out of our fingers' ends, our hair, and our beards. There was an electric snowstorm, with great flakes falling loosely, and the contact produced this strange luminosity. (038-008)

### The Mysterious Acceleration

On the night of 12-13 March, a thick fog descended and the _[[the-paracuta|Paracuta]]_ began to accelerate inexplicably — faster than the current could carry her and despite the diminishing wind. This unnatural increase in speed, occurring in dense fog that limited visibility to a few cable-lengths, was the first sign that the boat was being drawn towards something powerful and unseen [038-017] [038-019].

## The Encounter

### First Glimpse of the Sphinx

When the mist cleared at about ten o'clock on the morning of 13 March, the crew beheld a staggering sight. No more than a quarter of a mile away stood a huge mound rising three hundred feet above the plain, with a circumference of two to three hundred feet. Its form was unmistakable [038-021]:

> In its strange form this great mound resembled an enormous sphinx; the body upright, the paws stretched out, crouching in the attitude of the winged monster which Grecian Mythology has placed upon the way to Thebes. (038-021)

The shape was not merely suggestive — it was uncannily precise. A dusky-hued sphinx, its surface apparently oxidized by the inclemency of the polar climate, dominated the desolate landscape [038-051].

### The Magnetic Revelation

The nature of the sphinx was soon revealed with terrifying clarity. As the boat approached the shore, every iron and steel article on board — cooking utensils, arms, [[endicott|Endicott]]'s stove, knives torn from pockets — suddenly flew through the air towards the mound. The boat itself, quickening its course irresistibly, brought up against the beach [038-032].

> "The Antarctic Sphinx was simply a colossal magnet." (038-056)

The scientific explanation followed: the continuous electrical currents at [[the-south-pole|the pole]]s, which bewilder compasses, possessed an extraordinary influence. A block of iron subjected to their action would be changed into a magnet of power proportioned to the intensity of the current. The bulk of the sphinx — calculated by thousands of cubic yards — represented an inexhaustible accumulator of magnetic force, a gigantic calamite set up at the confines of the world [038-066] [038-068].

### Evidence of Prior Destruction

Upon landing, the explorers discovered the wrecked remains of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s stolen boat — the one taken by [[hearne|Hearne]] and his fellow mutineers. All its ironwork had been torn away, down to the hinges of the rudder. Nearby lay three corpses: [[hearne|Hearne]] himself, [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]], and one of the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]] men. Of the thirteen who had mutinied, only these three had reached the sphinx, and they had been dead for some days [038-036] [038-041].

The magnetic force respected nothing made of iron. The explorers watched as Hurliguerly's knife was snatched from his hand, the grapnel rope broke, and the implement shot like a projectile towards the sphinx, adhering high up on its side [038-031].

## The Fate of Arthur Gordon Pym

### The Discovery

As the party prepared to depart — West having reminded them of the imprudence of lingering on what he named the "Land of the Sphinx" — the voice of [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] rang out once more [039-016]:

> "There--yes--there!" (039-005)

Following [[dirk-peters|the half-breed]] around to the back of the sphinx's right paw, the explorers found him on his knees before an almost naked corpse, preserved intact by the Antarctic cold and as rigid as iron. The head was bent, a white beard hung down to the waist, the nails of the feet and hands were like claws. Across the body, held in place by its cross-belt, lay the twisted barrel of a musket, half-eaten by rust [039-019] [039-021].

It was [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]].

### The Explanation

The story was easy to reconstruct. After his separation from [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s boat had carried him through the Antarctic regions. Like the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] survivors, once he had passed beyond the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]] he came into the zone of the magnetic monster [039-024]:

> "And there, while his boat was swept along on the northern current, he was seized by the magnetic fluid before he could get rid of the gun which was slung over his shoulder, and hurled against the fatal loadstone Sphinx of the Ice-realm." (039-024)

[[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]] had been pinned to the sphinx by the iron musket he carried — magnetically fused to the colossal loadstone, his body preserved for eleven years in the polar cold, a frozen sentinel at the southernmost point of the world.

### The Death of Dirk Peters

The discovery was too much for the faithful half-breed. He tried to rise, to approach and kiss the ossified corpse of his beloved companion. But his knees bent under him. A strangled sob seemed to rend his throat. With a terrible spasm, his faithful heart broke, and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] fell back — dead [039-022] [039-023].

> "Pym--my poor Pym!" groaned Dirk Peters. (039-022)

The loyal companion who had shared every peril of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s adventures — from the wreck of the _[[the-grampus|Grampus]]_ to the horrors of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] — died at the side of his "poor [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]," the hero whose strange adventures had found a chronicler no less strange in Edgar Allan Poe [039-026].

## Significance

### Resolution of the Central Mystery

The discovery of the sphinx resolves the novel's two central questions. First, it confirms that [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] was a real historical figure whose adventures were genuine — vindicating [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s faith and [[jeorling|Jeorling]]'s growing conviction. Second, it explains [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s ultimate fate: he did not vanish into a milky white abyss as Poe's narrative had suggested, but met his end at the magnetic pole, a victim of the same natural forces that Verne's explorers themselves had barely survived.

### The Magnetic Pole as Narrative Device

Verne's sphinx is a masterful fusion of scientific speculation and mythological symbolism. The ancient Egyptian sphinx was the guardian of mysteries and the poser of riddles — and Verne's Antarctic Sphinx is precisely that, the keeper of the novel's central secret. By making the sphinx a natural phenomenon — a colossal loadstone magnet — Verne characteristically reconciles the supernatural atmosphere of Poe's original narrative with the scientific rationalism of his own age.

### The Human Cost

The discovery also completes the novel's accounting of human loss. Of the seventy souls who embarked on the two schooners — the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_ with thirty-two and the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_ with thirty-eight — only twelve survived to depart from the Land of the Sphinx [039-030]. [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], the last living link to [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s original expedition, joined his captain in death at the foot of the ice sphinx that had claimed them both.

## Aftermath

That same afternoon, the _[[the-paracuta|Paracuta]]_ departed from the coast of the Land of the Sphinx, which had lain to the west of the survivors since 21 February [039-029]. A few days later, the sun set behind the western horizon to reappear no more for the whole winter, and the party navigated northward through the semi-darkness of the austral night, illuminated only by the capricious splendour of the southern polar lights [039-033].

The Land of the Sphinx — a name retained, as West had insisted — entered the geography of the Antarctic as the farthest south any human being had ever reached, and the site where the greatest mystery of the southern seas found its final and tragic resolution.
