# The Jane Expedition

The **Jane Expedition** was the original Antarctic voyage of the schooner [[the-jane|Jane]] under the command of [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]], a Liverpool shipowner who sailed south in search of new sealing grounds and instead discovered [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], encountered its hostile natives, and vanished into the Antarctic unknown. The expedition — and the fate of its survivors — is the central mystery that drives the narrative of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, setting in motion the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s rescue voyage eleven years later.

## Origins and Purpose

The [[the-jane|Jane]] was an English schooner out of Liverpool, of which [[william-guy|William Guy]] was part-owner as well as captain (007-056). Her primary purpose was commercial — sealing and trading in the southern waters — but [[william-guy|William Guy]] was also an explorer by temperament, driven by the ambition to penetrate farther south than any vessel had gone before.

On board the _Jane_ as a passenger was [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]], the young adventurer from [[nantucket|Nantucket]] whose earlier experiences aboard the [[the-grampus|Grampus]] had already marked him as one of the most extraordinary figures in the annals of American seafaring. With him was his inseparable companion, the half-breed [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]].

## The Voyage South

### Departure and Early Stages

The [[the-jane|Jane]] departed from Liverpool and made her way south through the Atlantic, calling at [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan da Cunha]] where the innkeeper [[glass|Glass]] met the captain and his passengers (010-008). From Tristan, the _Jane_ "headed for the bearings of the southern lands" (010-065), following the route that would take her past the Falklands and into the Antarctic waters.

After leaving Tristan da Cunha, the _Jane_ "headed towards the Antarctic seas" on the 12th of December (007-051). The timing was deliberate: the austral summer offered the narrow window of favourable weather and open water that made southern exploration possible.

### Discoveries and Landfalls

The [[the-jane|Jane]] sighted and passed [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] on the 13th of October, then the [[crozet-islands|Crozet Islands]], and afterwards the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] (007-045). This southeasterly route through the Indian Ocean positioned the vessel for her final run toward the Antarctic Circle.

The crowning achievement of the expedition was the discovery of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], a large and previously unknown land in the high southern latitudes. The island appeared fertile and inhabited, and the crew of the _Jane_ established relations with its black-skinned native population. At first, the islanders seemed friendly; they traded with the Europeans and appeared to welcome their presence.

## The Catastrophe at Tsalal

### The Landslip

The apparent friendship of the Tsalal natives was a deception. The islanders had "cunningly contrived" a massive landslip — an artificial avalanche designed to bury the crew of the [[the-jane|Jane]] (008-025). The attack was devastating: "Captain William Guy and his twenty-eight companions" were caught in the trap (008-027).

> The landslip contrived by the natives. (011-072)

The natives had not given the strangers any cause for suspicion. The landslip was an "infernal device" (005-069) — a premeditated act of mass murder that demonstrated a sophistication and ruthlessness the Europeans had not imagined.

### The Explosion

In the aftermath of the landslip, a catastrophic explosion tore through the native settlement. Whether this was an accident — the detonation of the powder store — or a deliberate act of destruction is never fully resolved. The explosion killed "a thousand natives and mutilating as many more" (008-028), scattering the survivors of both sides.

> The native store — killing a thousand natives and mutilating as many more. (008-028)

The [[the-jane|Jane]] herself was destroyed in the chaos. [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] managed to escape, seizing a native boat and fleeing the island with three [[galapagos|galapagos tortoises]] for provisions. The fate of [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]] and the remaining crew members was unknown.

## The Fate of the Survivors

### Pym and Peters

[[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] survived the destruction of the [[the-jane|Jane]] and escaped from [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]]. Their subsequent journey carried them farther south than any human beings had ever been — into the region of the [[the-sphinx-of-the-ice|magnetic sphinx]], where Pym met his [[the-death-of-pym|death]]. Peters alone returned to America, bearing the knowledge that would, eleven years later, launch the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition.

### William Guy and the Five

The discovery of [[william-patterson|Patterson]]'s body on a drifting ice floe provided the first evidence that some of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew had survived the initial catastrophe. Patterson's dying words indicated that [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]] and "five of the men of the _Jane_" were still alive on an island in the austral regions (009-043). This testimony — the "last link" in the chain of evidence — transformed the [[the-jane|Jane]] expedition from a closed tragedy into an open mystery.

> Five of the men of the _Jane_ — the piece of ice I am on is — (009-043)

### The Return of the Jane's News

[[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] returned to America — "how I do not know," as [[jeorling|Jeorling]] acknowledges (010-079) — and his account of the voyage was published by [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]]. Pym's narrative, with its mixture of verified fact and incredible claim, became the foundation upon which [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] would build his case for a rescue expedition. "Unfortunately, the _Jane_ did not return," Jeorling notes, but the knowledge of what had happened to her survived through Pym's account and through the fragmentary testimony of those, like [[william-patterson|Patterson]], whose bodies the currents delivered into the path of the rescue voyage (010-079).

## Legacy

The [[the-jane|Jane]] expedition stands as one of the founding events of Verne's Antarctic mythology. It is the original penetration of the polar unknown — the voyage that breached the [[the-ice-barrier|ice barrier]], discovered [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]], and established the line of evidence that would, more than a decade later, draw the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] south on its mission of rescue. The expedition's tragedy — the treacherous attack, the explosion, the scattering of survivors — created the mystery that gives Verne's novel its name and its narrative momentum.
