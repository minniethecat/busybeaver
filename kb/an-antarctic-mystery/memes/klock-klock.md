# Klock-Klock

**Klock-Klock** is the principal native village on [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], situated in a valley of the same name. It was here that the [[the-jane|Jane]] expedition established contact with the black-skinned islanders, here that the catastrophic landslip was "cunningly contrived" (008-025) to destroy the European visitors, and here that the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition, arriving eleven years later, found only silence and abandonment.

## Location and Layout

Klock-Klock lies in a valley on Tsalal Island, approached from the sea through a bay crossed by a reef-lined channel. The village was substantial enough to accommodate a large native population — "thousands of natives on Tsalal Island" (012-041) — and was situated between hills that provided the raw material for the landslip that would destroy the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew.

> The valley of Klock-Klock extended no farther. (019-058)

The village was surrounded by "labyrinths" of gorges and ravines — a complex topography that provided hiding places for survivors of the catastrophe and which [[jeorling|Jeorling]] and his companions would later explore in search of evidence of the lost expedition (036-054).

## The Landslip

### The Engineered Catastrophe

The most significant event associated with Klock-Klock is the landslip — the massive and deliberately engineered collapse of a hillside that buried a large portion of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew. The hill of Klock-Klock was "cunningly contrived" by the natives to collapse upon the Europeans (008-025), a trap that demonstrated a level of planning and engineering skill that the visitors had not anticipated.

> The landslip of the hill at Klock-Klock. (011-069)

The landslip was the pivot on which the entire narrative turned. Before it, the [[the-jane|Jane]] expedition was a voyage of discovery and commerce; after it, it became a tragedy whose survivors were scattered across the Antarctic wilderness. The question of who escaped the landslip — and how — drives much of the novel's mystery.

### Survivors of the Landslip

Some of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew "escaped the landslip of the Klock-Klock hill, and had come to hide" in the surrounding gorges (019-067). The labyrinthine terrain around the village, which had facilitated the natives' ambush, now served as refuge for the Europeans who had survived it. In these gorges, the survivors found the means to sustain themselves — feeding on the roots and shellfish that the island provided.

## The Abandoned Village

### The Halbrane's Discovery

When the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition reached Tsalal Island eleven years later, they made their way "towards the hill of Klock-Klock" expecting to find either the surviving crew of the [[the-jane|Jane]] or, at the very least, the native population (019-073). What they found instead was desolation. "Nothing remained of the" village they had expected (019-073) — no natives, no Europeans, no trace of the bustling settlement that [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] had described.

> We took our way towards the hill of Klock-Klock. Nothing remained of the... (019-073)

The valley that had been "swarming with natives" (008-026) was empty. The village whose inhabitants had numbered in the thousands was deserted. The disappearance was total — as though Klock-Klock had never existed.

### Evidence of the Dead

In and around Klock-Klock, the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition discovered human remains — "the bones we had seen in or near Klock-Klock were those of the poor" souls who had perished there (036-068). These bones, bleached by the Antarctic elements, were the only physical evidence of the catastrophe that had engulfed the original expedition. They confirmed that death had visited Klock-Klock on a massive scale — but whether they were the bones of Europeans, natives, or both, the novel leaves deliberately ambiguous.

### The Gorges

The "labyrinths of Klock-Klock" (036-054) yielded further evidence. In these ravines, survivors had "fed on the roots and shellfish" that were abundant there, sustaining themselves in the aftermath of the catastrophe. The gorges had sheltered the living and, eventually, entombed the dead — a natural cemetery for those who had escaped the landslip only to perish of exposure, starvation, or the lingering effects of their injuries.

## Klock-Klock and the Tiger

The novel's final revelations about Klock-Klock involve the monstrous creature known as the Tiger — a beast that had "returned to Klock-Klock, to spread terror among" the surviving natives (036-065). This creature, whose existence straddles the boundary between natural and supernatural, adds a final layer of mystery to the abandoned village. Klock-Klock was not merely deserted; it was haunted — by memories, by bones, and by a predator whose reality [[jeorling|Jeorling]] can neither fully confirm nor entirely dismiss.
