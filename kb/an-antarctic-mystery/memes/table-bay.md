# Table Bay

**Table Bay** is the natural harbour of Cape Town, at the southern tip of Africa. In the geography of _An Antarctic Mystery_, it represents one of the established waypoints on the maritime route into the Southern Ocean — a gateway between the civilized ports of the Atlantic and the uncharted waters of the Antarctic.

The bay takes its name from the flat-topped Table Mountain that dominates its shoreline. For nineteenth-century mariners, Table Bay was the last major harbour where ships could be fully reprovisioned before entering the southern latitudes — a function it shares in the novel's maritime geography with ports such as [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan da Cunha]] and the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]].

## Role in Antarctic Exploration

Table Bay served as a critical resupply point for Antarctic expeditions throughout the nineteenth century. The bay offered:

- Fresh water and provisions from the Cape Colony
- Ship repairs and naval stores
- The last reliable anchorage before the Southern Ocean's unpredictable weather

In the wider context of Verne's Antarctic geography — which maps real-world exploration history onto fictional narrative — Table Bay anchors the southern route in historical reality, connecting the novel's speculative geography to the actual ports that sustained the age of polar exploration.
