## Section 29

[029-001]Needless to say, we had set up a camp on the plateau of which I have spoken, not far from the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_. Sufficient shelter against the inclemency of the weather, not unfrequent at this time of the year, was to be found under tents, constructed of sails placed on spars and fastened down by pegs. The glass remained set fair; the wind was nor'-east, the temperature having risen to 46 degrees (2° 78' C.).

[029-002][[endicott|Endicott]]'s kitchen was fitted up at the end of the plain, near a steep projection by which we could climb to the very top of the berg.

[029-003]It is only fair to state that during these three days of hard work no fault was to be found with [[hearne|Hearne]]. The sealing-master knew he was being closely watched, and he was well aware that [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] would not spare him if he tried to get up insubordination amongst his comrades. It was a pity that his bad instincts had induced him to play such a part, for his strength, skill, and cleverness made him a very valuable man, and he had never proved more useful than under these circumstances.

[029-004]Was he changed for the better? Did he understand that general good feeling was necessary for the safety of all? I know not, but I had no confidence in him, neither had Hurliguerly!

[029-005]I need not dwell on the ardour with which [[dirk-peters|the half-breed]] did the rough work, always first to begin and the last to leave off, doing as much as four men, and scarcely sleeping, only resting during meals, which he took apart from the others. He had hardly spoken to me at all since the schooner had met with this terrible accident.

[029-006]What indeed could he say to me? Did I not know as well as he that it would be necessary to renounce every hope of pursuing our intended voyage?

[029-007]Now and again I noticed [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]] and the half-breed near each other while some difficult piece of work was in progress. Our sailing-master did not miss a chance of getting near Dirk Peters, who always tried his best to escape from him, for reasons well known to me. And whenever I thought of the secret of the fate of the so-called Parker, Martin Holt's brother, which had been entrusted to me, that dreadful scene of the _[[the-grampus|Grampus]]_ filled me with horror. I was certain that if this secret were made known the half-breed would become an object of terror. He would no longer be looked upon as the rescuer of the sailing-master; and the latter, learning that his brother-- Luckily, Dirk Peters and myself were the only two acquainted with the fact.

[029-008]While the _Halbrane_ was being unloaded, Captain Len Guy and the mate were considering how the vessel might be launched. They had to allow for a drop of one hundred feet between the cavity in which the ship lay and the sea; this to be effected by means of an inclined bed hollowed in an oblique line along the west side of the iceberg, and to measure two or three hundred perches in length. So, while the first lot of men, commanded by the boatswain, was unloading the schooner, a second batch under West's orders began to cut the trench between the blocks which covered the side of the floating mountain.

[029-009]Floating? I know not why I use this expression, for the iceberg no longer floated, but remained as motionless as an island. There was nothing to indicate that it would ever move again. Other [[iceberg|icebergs]] drifted along and passed us, going south-east, whilst ours, to use

[029-010]Dirk Peters' expression, was "lying to." Would its base be sufficiently undermined to allow it to detach itself? Perhaps some heavy mass of ice might strike it and set it free by the shock. No one could predict such an event, and we had only the _Halbrane_ to rely upon for getting us out of these regions.

[029-011]We were engaged in these various tasks until the 24th of January.

[029-012]The atmosphere was clear, the temperature was even, and the thermometer had indeed gone up to two or three degrees above freezing-point. The number of icebergs coming from the nor'-west was therefore increasing; there were now a hundred of them, and a collision with any of these might have a most disastrous result.

[029-013][[hardy|Hardy]], the caulker, hastened first of all to mend the hull; pegs had to be changed, bits of planking to be replaced, seams to be caulked.

[029-014]We had everything that was necessary for this work, and we might rest assured that it would be performed in the best possible manner.

[029-015]In the midst of the silence of these solitudes, the noise of the hammers striking nails into the side, and the sound of the mallet stuffing tow into the seams, had a startling effect. Sea-gulls, wild duck, albatross, and petrels flew in a circle round the top of the berg with a shrill screaming, and made a terrible uproar.

[029-016]When I found myself with West and the captain, our conversation naturally turned on our situation and how to get out of it, and upon our chances of pulling through. The mate had good hopes that if no accident occurred the launching would be successfully accomplished.

[029-017]The captain was more reserved on the subject, but at the thought that he would have to renounce all hope of finding the survivors of the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_, his heart was ready to break.

[029-018]When the _Halbrane_ should again be ready for the sea, and when West should inquire what course he was to steer, would Captain Len Guy dare to reply, "To the south"? No! for he would not be followed either by the new hands, or by the greater portion of the older members of the crew. To continue our search in this direction, to go [[the-journey-beyond-the-pole|beyond the pole]], without being certain of reaching the Indian Ocean instead of the Atlantic, would have been rashness of which no navigator would be guilty. If a continent bound the sea on this side, the schooner would run the danger of being crushed by the mass of ice before it could escape the southern winter.

[029-019]Under such circumstances, to attempt to persuade Captain Len Guy to pursue the voyage would only be to court a certain refusal. It could not even be proposed, now that necessity obliged us to return northwards, and not to delay a single day in this portion of the

[029-020]Antarctic regions. At any rate, though I resolved not again to speak of the matter to the captain, I lost no opportunity of sounding the boatswain. Often when he had finished his work, Hurliguerly would come and join me; we would chat, and we would compare our recollections of travel.

[029-021]One day as we were seated on the summit of the iceberg, gazing fixedly on the deceptive horizon, he exclaimed,--

[029-022]"Who could ever have imagined, [[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]], when the _Halbrane_ left [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelen]], that six and a half months afterwards she would be stuck on the side of an ice-mountain?"

[029-023]"A fact much more to be regretted," I replied, "because only for that accident we should have attained our object, and we should have begun our return journey."

[029-024]"I don't mean to contradict," replied the boatswain, "but you say we should have attained our object. Do you mean by that, that we should have found our countrymen?"

[029-025]"Perhaps."

[029-026]"I can scarcely believe such would have been the case, Mr.

[029-027]Jeorling, although this was the principal and perhaps even the only object of our navigation in the polar seas."

[029-028]"The only one--yes--at the start," I insinuated. "But since the half-breed's revelations about [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]--"

[029-029]"Ah! You are always harking back on that subject, like brave Dirk

[029-030]Peters."

[029-031]"Always, Hurliguerly; and only that a deplorable and unforeseen accident made us run aground--"

[029-032]"I leave you to your delusions, Mr. Jeorling, since you believe you have run aground--"

[029-033]"Why? Is not this the case?"

[029-034]"In any case it is a wonderful running aground," replied the boatswain. "Instead of a good solid bottom, we have run aground in the air."

[029-035]"Then I am right, Hurliguerly, in saying it is an unfortunate adventure."

[029-036]"Unfortunate, truly, but in my opinion we should take warning by it."

[029-037]"What warning?"

[029-038]"That it is not permitted to us to venture so far in these latitudes, and I believe that the Creator forbids His creatures to climb to the summit of the poles."

[029-039]"Notwithstanding that the summit of one pole is only sixty miles away from us now."

[029-040]"Granted, Mr. Jeorling, but these sixty miles are equal to thousands when we have no means of making them! And if the launch of the schooner is not successful, here are we condemned to winter quarters which the polar bears themselves would hardly relish!"

[029-041]I replied only by a shake of my head, which Hurliguerly could not fail to understand.

[029-042]"Do you know, Mr. Jeorling, of what I think oftenest?"

[029-043]"What do you think of, boatswain?"

[029-044]"Of the Kerguelens, whither we are certainly not travelling.

[029-045]Truly, in a bad season it was cold enough there! There is not much difference between this archipelago and the islands situated on the edge of the Antarctic Sea! But there one is not far from the Cape, and if we want to warm our shins, no iceberg bars the way. Whereas here it is the devil to weigh anchor, and one never knows if one shall find a clear course."

[029-046]"I repeat it, boatswain. If this last accident had not occurred, everything would have been over by this time, one way or another. We should still have had more than six weeks to get out of these southern seas. It is seldom that a ship is so roughly treated as ours has been, and I consider it real bad luck, after our having profited by such fortunate circumstances--"

[029-047]"These circumstances are all over, Mr. Jeorling," exclaimed

[029-048]Hurliguerly, "and I fear indeed--"

[029-049]"What--you also, boatswain--you whom I believed to be so confident!"

[029-050]"Confidence, Mr. Jeorling, wears out like the ends of one's trousers. What would you have me do? When I compare my lot to old

[029-051][[atkins|Atkins]], installed in his cosy inn; when I think of the Green

[029-052]Cormorant, of the big parlours downstairs with the little tables round which friends sip whisky and gin, discussing the news of the day, while the stove makes more noise than the weathercock on the roof--oh, then the comparison is not in our favour, and in my opinion Mr. Atkins enjoys life better than I do."

[029-053]"You shall see them all again, boatswain--Atkins, the Green

[029-054]Cormorant, and Kerguelen! For God's sake do not let yourself grow downhearted! And if you, a sensible and courageous man, despair already--"

[029-055]"Oh, if I were the only one it would not be half so bad as it is!"

[029-056]"The whole crew does not despair, surely?"

[029-057]"Yes--and no," replied Hurliguerly, "for I know some who are not at all satisfied!"

[029-058]"Has Hearne begun his mischief again? Is he exciting his companions?"

[029-059]"Not openly at least, Mr. Jeorling, and since I have kept him under my eye I have neither seen nor heard anything. Besides, he knows what awaits him if he budges. I believe I am not mistaken, the sly dog has changed his tactics. But what does not astonish me in him, astonishes me in Martin Holt."

[029-060]"What do you mean, boatswain?"

[029-061]"That they seem to be on good terms with each other. See how

[029-062]Hearne seeks out Martin Holt, talks to him frequently, and Holt does not treat his overtures unfavourably.''

[029-063]"Martin Holt is not one of those who would listen to Hearne's advice, or follow it if he tried to provoke rebellion amongst the crew."

[029-064]"No doubt, Mr. Jeorling. However, I don't fancy seeing them so much together. Hearne is a dangerous and unscrupulous individual, and most likely Martin Holt does not distrust him sufficiently."

[029-065]"He is wrong, boatswain."
