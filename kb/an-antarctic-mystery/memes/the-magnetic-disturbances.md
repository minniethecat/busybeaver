# The Magnetic Disturbances

**The Magnetic Disturbances** are a series of anomalous magnetic and compass phenomena encountered by the crew of the _Halbrane_ and later the _Paracuta_ as they approached the Antarctic magnetic pole. These disturbances become increasingly severe as the expedition presses southward, ultimately confronting the voyagers with forces beyond their scientific understanding.

## Early Compass Stability

Throughout the early stages of the voyage south, Captain Len Guy had always been able to rely upon the indications of the compass. The magnetic pole, still hundreds of miles distant, exerted no influence upon the needle, which remained steady and could be trusted for navigation (026-035). This period of normalcy made the subsequent anomalies all the more disorienting.

## Fog and the Compass Question

As the expedition penetrated deeper into Antarctic waters, dense fog enveloped the vessel. The half-condensed mist became so compact that "it was difficult to walk through it," and the composition of the air seemed to change, "as though it were passing into a solid state." Under these conditions, the crew questioned whether the fog had any effect upon the compass. Meteorological study had suggested that the needle is not affected by atmospheric conditions, yet the uncertainty gnawed at the navigators (033-045).

## The Magnetic Pole's Influence

The most dramatic magnetic phenomena occurred as the expedition approached the vicinity of the magnetic pole itself. An occult force irresistibly attracted everything made of iron on the _Paracuta_, and the narrator reflected that "the boat itself would have shared the fate of the _Halbrane_'s boat had a single bit of that metal been employed in its construction" (038-057). The question arose: was it "the proximity of the magnetic pole that produced such effects?" (038-057).

At the location where the magnetic meridians cross, the magnetic needle assumes a vertical position — a phenomenon already proved by observations. The narrator speculated that the vast ice formation they encountered "ought to be in the magnetic axis, as a sort of gigantic calamite, from whence the imponderable fluid whose currents made an inexhaustible accumulator set up at the confines of the world should issue" (038-068). The compass needle "staggered about, helpless and useless," confirming that they were in the presence of forces beyond normal navigation (038-068).

## The Sphinx of the Ice-Realm

The ultimate expression of the magnetic disturbances was the monstrous ice formation that Jeorling described as the "Sphinx of the Ice-realm." As they neared it, the voyagers "seemed to be drawn towards it by the force of its magnetic attraction" (039-010). The fate of Arthur Pym was explained by this phenomenon: after passing beyond the south pole, Pym was "seized by the magnetic fluid before he could get rid of the gun which was slung over his shoulder, and hurled against the fatal loadstone Sphinx of the Ice-realm" (039-024).
