# The Halbrane

The sturdy English schooner commanded by [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] that carries the Antarctic rescue expedition — a vessel crewed by ten men under the iron discipline of Lieutenant [[james-west|James West]], ultimately destroyed by [[iceberg|ice]] but memorialized forever in the names of [[halbrane-land|Halbrane Land]] on the Antarctic continent.

## Overview

The *Halbrane* is the central vessel of Jules Verne's *An Antarctic Mystery*, the ship that transports narrator [[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]] from the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelen Islands]] to the very edge of the known world. She is a schooner of modest size but exceptional seaworthiness, built for the rigours of the Southern Ocean. As the boatswain Hurliguerly later reflected with fondness, she became a character in her own right: "Poor, dear *Halbrane*. She will remain in these seas, like the *[[the-jane|Jane]]*, her elder sister!" [031-024].

The ship was "evidently well commanded, her crew well kept in hand, discipline strictly maintained" [004-009]. Her interior matched her exterior: "Nothing could exceed the perfect order, the Dutch cleanliness of the vessel" [004-041].

The eight sailors were [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]] (sailing-master), [[hardy|Hardy]], Rogers, Drap, [[francis|Francis]], Gratian, Burg, and Stern — "all Englishmen, well trained, and remarkably well disciplined by a hand of iron" [004-042]. The ship's cook was [[endicott|Endicott]], an African-born negro who had served eight years in that post [004-050]. The boatswain, Hurliguerly, was a loyal and cheerful presence who often mediated between captain and crew.

## Command Structure

The true power aboard the *Halbrane* rested not solely with [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] but with his second officer. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] observed that "the exceptionally able man whom they all obeyed at a word, a gesture, was not the captain of the *Halbrane*; that man was the second officer, [[james-west|James West]], who was then thirty-two years of age" [004-044]. West had served ten years as second in command and was described in almost mystical terms:

> "He seemed to be one of the essential organs of his ship, and if the *Halbrane* had a heart it was in James West's breast that it beat." [004-049]

## The Voyage

The *Halbrane* sailed from [[christmas-harbour|Christmas Harbour]] in the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelen]]s on 15 August, bound first for [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan d'Acunha]], then the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]], and finally into the Antarctic unknown.

Her voyage was inspired by the discovery of [[patterson|Patterson]]'s frozen body on an ice-waif — a discovery that proved the *[[the-jane|Jane]]*'s expedition was real and that survivors might still be found at [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]]. "He would take the *Halbrane* to [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], as marked upon [[patterson|Patterson]]'s note-book" [009-057].

The ship weathered fierce storms — "for five successive days the *Halbrane* sailed without sustaining any damage" [016-055] — and navigated through fields of [[iceberg|icebergs]] under West's vigilant eye [026-034]. She surpassed the achievements of all previous explorers: "the *Halbrane* made progress toward higher waters, thirteen degrees in advance upon Weddell's ship and two degrees upon the *[[the-jane|Jane]]*" [023-045].

## Destruction and Legacy

The *Halbrane* met her end not in battle but through the inexorable forces of the polar environment. After the catastrophic [[the-iceberg-collision|iceberg collision]], the ship was hauled onto an iceberg for repairs. When that iceberg overturned, the schooner was crushed. [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] salvaged what he could: "the cargo of the *Halbrane*, furniture, bedding, sails, clothing, instruments, and utensils" [034-070] was recovered and stored in caverns.

In a moment of profound symbolism, [[captain-len-guy|Len Guy]] staked his authority on the ship's surviving boat:

> "This boat, belonging to the *Halbrane*, is now the *Halbrane* herself! I am the captain of it, and let him who disobeys me, beware!" [031-009]

The ship's memory endures in geographical nomenclature. When the expedition discovered new lands in the Antarctic, they named the coastal region [[halbrane-land|Halbrane Land]] "in memory of our schooner" [035-073].
