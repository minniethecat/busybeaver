# Galapagos Tortoises

The **Galapagos tortoises** (also called antarctic turtles or simply galapagos) are giant marine turtles that inhabit the shores of the Antarctic islands in Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_. They serve as a crucial food source for shipwrecked sailors and stranded explorers, providing both nourishing flesh and, remarkably, a store of fresh water — a gift of nature in a region where potable water is otherwise locked in ice.

## Description

The galapagos are huge, slow-moving creatures, described in considerable detail by Verne through the observations of [[jeorling|Jeorling]] and the earlier account of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]. They have "thin necks two feet long, triangular snake-like heads, and can go without food for very long periods" (035-026). Their movement is "slow, heavy, and waddling" (035-026), making them easy prey for castaways in need of sustenance.

> The beach was the home of numbers of galapagos — a kind of turtle so called from an archipelago in the equinoctial sea, where also they abound, and mentioned by Arthur Pym as supplying food to the islanders. (035-023)

The name "galapagos" itself derives from the Galapagos archipelago in the equatorial Pacific, where similar giant tortoises are found. Verne's use of the term reflects the nineteenth-century practice of applying familiar names to newly encountered species in remote regions.

## Size and Weight

Pym's narrative, which Verne cites with his characteristic blend of credulity and caution, describes some of these turtles as weighing "from twelve to fifteen hundred pounds" (035-027). Those found on [[halbrane-land|Halbrane Land]] were somewhat smaller — "did not go beyond seven or eight hundred pounds, but their flesh was none the less savoury" (035-028). Even at this reduced size, a single turtle could provision a small crew for days.

> If Pym is to be believed, some of the great turtles weigh from twelve to fifteen hundred pounds. Those of Halbrane Land did not go beyond seven or eight hundred pounds, but their flesh was none the less savoury. (035-027)

## The Fresh-Water Reservoir

The most remarkable feature of the galapagos is their capacity to store fresh water. [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] compared them to dromedaries "because, like those ruminants, they have a pouch just where the neck begins, which contains from two to three gallons of cold fresh water" (035-026). This biological adaptation — whether factual or fanciful — makes the turtles doubly valuable to castaways: they provide both food and drink in a single animal.

> Arthur Pym has compared the antarctic turtles to dromedaries, because, like those ruminants, they have a pouch just where the neck begins, which contains from two to three gallons of cold fresh water. (035-026)

[[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] confirms this from his own experience, recalling the three turtles carried in the native boat during the escape from [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]]: "These animals contain a store of fresh water — and their flesh is sweet, even raw. Oh, raw flesh, sir!" (022-009). The horror in Peters's recollection — the memory of eating raw turtle flesh during the desperate flight — underscores the life-saving role these creatures played.

> You know — the three turtles that were in the boat. These animals contain a store of fresh water — and their flesh is sweet, even raw. (022-009)

## Role in Survival

### The Grampus Shipwreck

The galapagos are first mentioned in the context of Pym's earlier adventures aboard the [[the-grampus|Grampus]]. Pym relates that "but for one of these turtles the shipwrecked crew of the _Grampus_ must have died of hunger and thirst" (035-026). This episode — which Verne's characters treat as historical fact — establishes the turtles as providential agents of survival in the Antarctic economy.

### Provisions for the Halbrane Expedition

When the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition finds itself stranded on [[halbrane-land|Halbrane Land]], the presence of hundreds of galapagos on the beach becomes a critical factor in their survival calculations. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] notes that "there were hundreds of other animals to secure our little company from hunger, and even from thirst, at need" (035-023). The turtles, along with seals and other marine mammals, would sustain the party through the long Antarctic winter.

### Food for the Jane's Crew

The [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew under [[william-guy|William Guy]] also relied on these turtles. When provisions ran low on [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], the islanders supplied three turtles in their native boat — a resource that proved essential during the escape and the subsequent drift toward the southern lands.

## The Carved Tortoise at Bennet Islet

The galapagos appear symbolically as well as literally in the novel. At [[bennet-islet|Bennet Islet]], [[jeorling|Jeorling]] and [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] discover a piece of wood with traces of carving that "appeared to have belonged to the bow of a ship" (018-041). Captain Len Guy recalls: "Among the carving my brother fancied he could trace the design of a tortoise" (018-042). This fragment of the [[the-jane|Jane]] — bearing the emblem of the creature that had sustained its crew — becomes a piece of physical evidence linking the lost expedition to the rescue voyage.

> Among the carving my brother fancied he could trace the design of a tortoise. (018-042)
