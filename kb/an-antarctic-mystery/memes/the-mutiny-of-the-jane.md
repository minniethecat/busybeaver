# The Mutiny of the Jane

The **Mutiny of the Jane** refers to the catastrophic native uprising on [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] that resulted in the destruction of the schooner [[the-jane|Jane]] and the massacre of most of her crew. This event — initially described in [[the-pym-narrative|Poe's narrative]] and treated as historical fact throughout _An Antarctic Mystery_ — is the originating catastrophe that sets the entire novel's plot in motion.

The mutiny occurred in February 1828, when the native inhabitants of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]] — who had initially appeared friendly and welcoming to the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s crew — launched a coordinated attack on the vessel and its men. The schooner was destroyed by an explosion, and of her entire crew, only [[captain-william-guy|Captain William Guy]], his first mate [[patterson|Patterson]], and five of their companions survived — buried alive in the earthworks of a hill on the island [037-007].

## Events of the Mutiny

The sequence of events, as reconstructed by Verne from Poe's narrative and the testimony of survivors:

**Initial welcome**: When the [[the-jane|Jane]] first arrived at [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]], the natives — led by their chief Too-Wit — received the crew with apparent friendliness. The sailors were permitted to explore the island, to witness native ceremonies, and to establish a camp ashore [007-009].

**The trigger**: According to [[the-pym-narrative|Poe's account]], the natives' hostility was triggered by the Europeans' inadvertent display of the colour white — a colour that held some deep and terrible significance in Tsalal culture. The natives' word for white, "Tekeli-li," was uttered with horror whenever white objects appeared [007-010].

**The attack**: On the day of the ambush, the natives invited twenty-eight of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s men to visit the village of [[klock-klock|Klock-Klock]]. At a prearranged signal, the natives attacked, overwhelming the sailors before they could organize resistance. Simultaneously, the [[the-jane|Jane]] herself was attacked and destroyed by explosion [008-018].

**The aftermath**: [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]], [[patterson|Patterson]], and five others who had remained in a different part of the island were not immediately killed. Instead, the natives buried them alive in a ravine — an earth-choked prison from which they would not be freed for eleven years [037-007] [037-058].

## Survivors

The mutiny's survivors fell into two groups:

**Those who escaped**: [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] — who had been separated from the main party — escaped in a small boat and eventually returned to civilization. Pym's account of these events became the basis of [[the-pym-narrative|Poe's 1838 narrative]].

**Those who remained buried**: [[captain-william-guy|Captain William Guy]], [[patterson|Patterson]], and five other crew members — imprisoned beneath the earth of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]] for eleven years, surviving on the provisions the natives periodically supplied, until their discovery by the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition in 1840 [037-007].

The fate of the remaining crew — those killed in the initial ambush — is never detailed. They are the anonymous dead whose loss haunts the narrative, their number known but their names forgotten.

## Significance in the Novel

The Mutiny of the Jane serves as the novel's originating trauma — the event that explains why [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] is obsessed with the Antarctic, why [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] cannot rest, and why [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]] has become a place of such charged significance.

The mutiny also establishes the novel's central thematic tension: the encounter between European civilization and the unknown cultures of the Antarctic. The natives of Tsalal — whose motivations remain opaque, whose terror of whiteness suggests some ancient trauma or taboo — represent the ultimate unknowability of the southern continent. The mutiny is not merely a historical event but a parable about the limits of understanding between peoples separated by an abyss of geography and culture.

## Contrast with the Halbrane Mutiny

The novel contains two mutinies, mirroring each other across the gulf of eleven years:

| Aspect | Jane Mutiny (1828) | Halbrane Mutiny (1839) |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Perpetrators** | Natives of Tsalal | European crew members |
| **Motive** | Unknown (possibly cultural/religious) | Fear and self-preservation |
| **Outcome** | Massacre; schooner destroyed | Averted by Dirk Peters |
| **Knowledge** | Already past when novel begins | Occurs during the novel's action |

The juxtaposition suggests that mutiny — the breakdown of social order in extreme conditions — is a universal phenomenon, not confined to any one culture. The natives' uprising and [[the-mutiny-aboard-the-halbrane|Hearne's mutiny aboard the Halbrane]] are different expressions of the same human capacity for violence when pushed beyond the limits of normal restraint.
