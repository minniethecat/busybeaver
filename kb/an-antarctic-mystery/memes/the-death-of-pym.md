# The Death of Pym

The death of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] at the Antarctic magnetic sphinx is the climactic event that concluded Edgar Allan Poe's narrative and became the central mystery driving the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s rescue expedition eleven years later.

Unlike the general [[pyms-disappearance|disappearance of Pym]], which refers to the broader mystery of his vanishing, the death at the sphinx represents the specific and definitive end of the young adventurer. This end is reconstructed through the testimony of [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] and the physical evidence discovered at the magnetic mound.

## Prior Knowledge

Before the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition, the circumstances of Pym's death were unknown even to those closest to the mystery. [[jeorling|Jeorling]], recounting his conversation with the innkeeper Glass at [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan da Cunha]], states plainly: "Arthur Pym died, but under what circumstances I am ignorant" (007-045). The crew of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] believed Pym was "drowned, or frozen, or crushed — killed, anyhow, one way or another, eleven years ago" (023-043).

> Arthur Pym died, but under what circumstances I am ignorant. (007-045)

This uncertainty created the narrative space for Verne's sequel: Poe had left Pym's ultimate fate ambiguous, and it fell to Verne to provide the resolution.

## The Magnetic Sphinx

### Discovery

As the [[the-paracuta|Paracuta]] approached the southern shore of [[halbrane-land|Halbrane Land]], the expedition encountered a massive mound that resolved itself out of the polar mist. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] describes it: "Ah! that sphinx! I remembered, at sight of it, that on the night when the iceberg was overturned and the _Halbrane_ was carried away, I had dreamed of a fabulous animal of this kind, seated at the pole of the world, and from whom Edgar Poe could only wrest its secrets" (038-025).

The mound, "almost that of a sphinx, a dusky-hued sphinx, as though the matter which composed it had been oxidized by the inclemency of the polar climate" (038-051), proved to be a colossal natural magnet — the Antarctic Sphinx.

> I have said, almost that of a sphinx, a dusky-hued sphinx, as though the matter which composed it had been oxidized by the inclemency of the polar climate. (038-051)

### The Magnetic Phenomenon

The sphinx was, in fact, "simply a colossal magnet" (038-066). Its magnetic attraction was so powerful that it tore every piece of iron and steel from the boats that approached it — the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s stolen boat was found smashed to pieces on the shore, "all the ironwork of the boat had disappeared, down to the hinges of the rudder" (038-066). Even the grapnel, cooking utensils, knives, and firearms were wrenched from the expedition's own boat and projected toward the sphinx.

> The Antarctic Sphinx was simply a colossal magnet. Under the influence of that magnet the iron bands of the _Halbrane's_ boat had been torn out and projected as though by the action of a catapult. (038-066)

The scientific explanation offered by [[jeorling|Jeorling]] invokes the electric currents that flow perpetually at the poles: "The Trade-winds bring a constant succession of clouds or mists in which immense quantities of electricity not completely exhausted by storms, are stored. Hence there exists a formidable accumulation of electric fluid at the poles" (038-068). This electric current, acting upon an enormous block of iron over geological time, had created a magnet of "prodigious intensity" — the Antarctic Sphinx.

## The Fate of the Mutineers

Before the full truth of Pym's death is revealed, the expedition discovers the fate of [[hearne|Hearne]] and his fellow mutineers. Three corpses — those of Hearne, [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]], and one of the Falklands men — are found on the stony beach near the sphinx (038-053). The ten other mutineers had vanished entirely. Their boat had been ripped apart by the magnetic force, hurled against the rocks, and stripped of every piece of metal.

This discovery serves as a grim prefiguration of Pym's own end: the magnetic sphinx is not merely a geographical curiosity but a deadly force that destroys any vessel carrying iron that comes within its zone of attraction.

## Pym's End

### Dirk Peters's Account

The definitive account of Pym's death comes from [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], the half-breed who had accompanied Pym throughout his Antarctic adventures and had been his sole companion during the return from [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]]. Peters, who had been "seized by the magnetic fluid before he could get rid of his iron" (039-024), witnessed Pym's final moments.

No iron-bound boat could approach the sphinx safely, and Pym — drawn by the same irresistible curiosity that had driven him throughout his life — approached too close. The magnetic force that had stripped the metal from Hearne's boat claimed Pym as well. Peters, who had survived the horrors of the [[the-grampus|Grampus]] and the terrors of Tsalal, was forced to watch as his companion perished at the foot of the mysterious sphinx.

### The Body at the Sphinx

The culmination of the discovery comes when the expedition finds Pym's body at the base of the sphinx — "his poor Pym who was drowned, or frozen, or crushed — killed, anyhow, one way or another" (023-043). The body, preserved by the intense polar cold, rests against the magnetic mound that had drawn him to his death. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] reflects that Pym's life "had ended under sudden and deplorable" circumstances — a tragic end for the bold pioneer who had penetrated farther into the Antarctic than any man before him (005-046).

## Significance

The death of Pym at the sphinx serves multiple functions in Verne's narrative. It provides closure to Poe's deliberately unfinished tale. It completes the arc of Pym's character — the impetuous adventurer whose recklessness carried him to the ends of the earth and ultimately destroyed him. And it transforms the sphinx from a geological curiosity into a site of sacrifice, where the Antarctic claims its most famous victim.

For [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]], whose brother [[william-guy|William Guy]] had sailed with Pym, the discovery of Pym's body confirms both the truth of the original expedition and the futility of any hope that his brother might have survived. The sphinx that had appeared in [[jeorling|Jeorling]]'s dream — "a fabulous animal of this kind, seated at the pole of the world" — had claimed its victim, and "Edgar Poe could only wrest its secrets" through the pages of his narrative (038-025).
