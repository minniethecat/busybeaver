# Verne as Continuator of Poe

> **Thesis**: Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_ is not merely a sequel to Edgar Allan Poe's _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym_ — it is a radical act of literary appropriation that transforms Poe's fiction into Verne's fact, creating a metafictional framework in which the reality of one novel becomes the premise of another.

## The Prequel: Poe's Unfinished Narrative

Edgar Allan Poe's _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket_ was published in 1838, when Verne was ten years old. The novel recounts the adventures of Arthur Gordon Pym, a young man from Nantucket who stows away aboard a whaling brig, survives mutiny and shipwreck, and ultimately journeys into the Antarctic interior, where he encounters a colossal white figure at the southern pole — a vision that the narrative, breaking off mid-sentence, leaves unexplained (006-014).

Poe presented the work with deliberate ambiguity, publishing the first portion in the _Southern Literary Messenger_ "under the cloak of fiction" and later issuing it in book form with a preface attributing the text to Pym himself (006-017). The novel ends with an editorial note claiming that the final chapters were lost through the "accidental death" of the author — a characteristic Poe gesture that blurs the boundary between reportage and imagination.

## Verne's Appropriation: Fiction Becomes Foundation

Verne's radical move, executed across the opening chapters of _An Antarctic Mystery_, is to treat Poe's fiction as documentary truth. When Captain Len Guy first reveals his conviction that Poe's narrative is "exactly true" (011-066), the narrator Jeorling — a man of science — responds with incredulity:

> "Captain! Why, that story is due to the powerful imagination of our great poet. It is a pure invention." (005-027)

This is the novel's central epistemological pivot. Jeorling's skepticism mirrors that of every reader who encountered Poe's narrative as fiction. But as evidence accumulates — the existence of Dirk Peters (005-048), the discovery of Tsalal Island exactly as Poe described it, the recovery of Pym's journal — Jeorling is forced to revise his position:

> So then it was all true? Edgar Poe's work was that of an historian, not a writer of romance? Arthur Gordon Pym's journal had actually been confided to him! Direct relations had been established between them! (009-037)

The transformation is complete: "There is now no question of a romance signed 'Edgar Poe,' but of a veracious narrative signed 'Patterson'" (011-078). Poe, in Verne's universe, was not a novelist but a chronicler — the first historian of the Antarctic.

## The Metafictional Architecture

Verne constructs a three-layer architecture of belief:

1. **Poe's fiction** — the base layer, presented to Verne's readers as a work of imagination 2. **The characters' belief** — Captain Len Guy's conviction, Jeorling's gradual conversion, Dirk Peters's living testimony 3. **Verne's fiction** — the framing layer that makes Poe's fiction its own factual premise, transforming the reader's relationship to both texts

This architecture achieves something remarkable: it makes the reader complicit in Jeorling's journey from skepticism to belief. When Jeorling protests that Poe's story is "a pure invention," the reader shares his confidence. When he concedes its truth, the reader is invited to concede alongside him. The novel's greatest metafictional trick is to make Poe's imaginary Antarctic feel, by the final chapters, more real than Verne's own.

## Evidence: The Chain of Verification

Verne does not simply assert Poe's truth — he constructs a meticulous chain of verification:

**Captain Len Guy's Testimony.** Guy had personally sought out Dirk Peters in Illinois — at Springfield, then Vandalia — years before the Halbrane expedition, confirming that Pym's companion was a living man rather than a fictional construct (005-048) (005-049).

**Dirk Peters's Presence.** The half-breed's existence aboard the Halbrane, his detailed knowledge of the Grampus disaster, and his unmistakable physical presence transform Poe's character into Verne's witness (013-021).

**The Journal.** Pym's own manuscript, which Poe published as fiction, is treated by Verne's characters as the expedition's most reliable navigational guide: "recourse was had to the surest guide within our reach, that veracious narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym, which I read and re-read with intense attention" (017-050).

**The Geography.** Tsalal Island, Bennet Islet, Christmas Harbour — every location described in Poe's narrative is found by Verne's characters exactly where Pym placed them.

## The Irony of the Enterprise

Verne's homage to Poe is suffused with a deep and productive irony. Poe, the "marvellous poet" who "had exhausted the resources of his imagination" (008-041), is celebrated precisely because Verne has decided that his imagination was not imagination at all. The "greatest poet of the New World" (008-041) is praised for having written the truth — a compliment that works only because Verne has rewritten the rules of what counts as truth.

This irony is not lost on Verne. The novel ends with Jeorling describing Pym's body frozen beside the Sphinx of the Ice, "that hero whose strange adventures found a chronicler no less strange in the great American poet" (039-026). The word "chronicler" — not novelist, not poet, but chronicler — is Verne's final, definitive reclassification of Poe. The _Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym_ is not a romance; it is a record.

## Conclusion: What Verne Achieved

By transforming Poe's fiction into the factual substrate of his own novel, Verne achieved three things that neither author could have achieved alone:

1. **Narrative density:** Poe's novel, read through Verne's lens, acquires a weight and solidity it never possessed on its own. Every detail of _Pym_ becomes a clue, a prophecy, a truth waiting to be confirmed.

2. **Epistemological sophistication:** The question "Is Poe's story true?" becomes indistinguishable from the question "What makes any story true?" — a philosophical depth rare in adventure fiction.

3. **Literary continuity:** Verne positioned himself not as Poe's imitator but as his continuator — the historian who completed what the chronicler had begun. In doing so, he created a diptych whose two halves illuminate each other: Poe's obscurity gives meaning to Verne's clarity, and Verne's clarity makes Poe's obscurity mysterious rather than merely incomplete.

The Verne-Poe relationship, as embodied in _An Antarctic Mystery_, is one of the nineteenth century's most audacious literary experiments — a novel that dares to treat another novel as the truth, and in so doing, makes us wonder whether the distinction matters at all.
