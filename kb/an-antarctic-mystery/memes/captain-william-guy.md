# Captain William Guy

**Captain William Guy** is the commander of the schooner _Jane_ and the elder brother of Captain Len Guy of the _Halbrane_. He led the original expedition into Antarctic waters that resulted in the discovery of Tsalal Island and the catastrophic events that followed. A capable and prudent seaman, William Guy is the central figure whose fate drives the entire narrative of the _Halbrane_'s rescue mission.

## The Original Expedition

Captain William Guy commanded the _Jane_ on its voyage into the southern polar seas, following the route that would later be taken by the _Halbrane_. His expedition reached Tsalal Island, where he and his companions encountered a native population at the village of Klock-Klock, estimated by Arthur Pym at ten thousand souls, "men, women, and children" (008-014).

As a prudent commander, Guy took precautions, leaving six men on board the _Jane_ with "the guns charged, the bulwark nettings in their place, the anchor hanging at the forepeak" in readiness to oppose any hostile approach by the natives (008-018).


## The Massacre at Tsalal

Despite Guy's precautions, the expedition met with disaster. An artificial landslip, "cunningly contrived by the natives," took place at Klock-Klock. Captain William Guy and his twenty-eight companions disappeared, presumed "crushed beneath more than a million tons of earth and stones" (008-025). However, this proved not to be the full story.

## Survival

As later revealed through the discovery of Patterson's note-book and a drifting ice-block bearing a message, Captain William Guy did not perish in the landslip. Patterson's final message, written on an ice-floe, stated that "Captain William Guy and five of the men of the _Jane_" were still alive on Tsalal Island as of June, seven months after the catastrophe (009-043). This revelation transformed the _Halbrane_'s voyage from a commercial venture to a rescue mission.

William Guy "escaped the doom of the _Jane_ and the most of her crew" (010-080), surviving alongside five companions including his second officer Patterson.


## Relationship with Len Guy

The fraternal bond between the two Guy brothers is the emotional core of the novel. Captain Len Guy's determination to sail into Antarctic waters is driven entirely by his belief that his brother might still be alive. As the narrator Jeorling discovered, Len Guy was not the unsympathetic eccentric he first appeared, but a man consumed by devotion to his lost brother. The revelation that "Captain William Guy of the _Jane_, and Captain Len Guy of the _Halbrane_" are brothers (009-049) reshapes the reader's understanding of the entire expedition.

## Role in Arthur Pym's Account

William Guy figures prominently in Edgar Allan Poe's _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym_, and Verne's novel treats Poe's fiction as historical fact. Arthur Pym acknowledged "his responsibility for the sad and tragic events which were the results of his advice," having "over-persuaded Captain William Guy" to press further south (018-062). The question of whether Pym "separated himself, with the half-breed and one of the sailors, from his companions while Captain William Guy had gone to the village of Klock-Klock" is a matter of investigation during the _Halbrane_'s voyage (020-080).
