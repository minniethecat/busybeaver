# The Jane

The Liverpool schooner captained by [[william-guy|William Guy]] that carried [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] on his fateful Antarctic voyage — the missing ship whose disappearance eleven years earlier drives the entire [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition and whose survivors are the object of [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s desperate rescue mission.

## Overview

The *Jane* (formally the *Jane of Liverpool*) is the ghost at the centre of Jules Verne's *An Antarctic Mystery*. She is never seen in Verne's narrative — she was destroyed eleven years before the action begins — yet her fate animates every page. She is the ship that rescued [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] from the wreck of the [[the-grampus|Grampus]], carried them south into the Antarctic, and touched the shores of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] — the last land seen by civilised men before the unknown.

As [[jeorling|Jeorling]] learned from the testimony of the innkeeper Glass at [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan d'Acunha]], the *Jane* was a real vessel known in southern ports: Glass "perfectly remembers to have seen the *Jane*, eleven years ago, when she had put in at [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan d'Acunha]]" [011-043]. The captain of the *Jane*, [[william-guy|William Guy]], was the brother of [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] — a blood connection that transformed a routine sealing voyage into a personal crusade.

The schooner anchored at [[bennet-islet|Bennet Islet]] and [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] in the positions precisely recorded in [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s narrative. Eleven years later, the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] "cast anchor on the 6th of September at Ansiedling, in Falmouth Bay, precisely in the place indicated in [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]'s narrative as the moorings of the *Jane*" [010-006].

## The Native Attack and Destruction

The *Jane*'s fate was sealed by the natives of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]]. The schooner was "attacked by natives and blown up in the midst of those waters" [009-036]. [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]] and five of his sailors survived the initial destruction, remaining alive on the island as captives or fugitives. The second officer, [[patterson|Patterson]], escaped separately — drifting on an ice-floe for months before perishing.

His frozen body, with its note-book, was found by the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s crew, providing the first solid evidence that the *Jane*'s expedition was not fiction.

> "Patterson was the second officer of the *Jane*, the mate of that schooner which had picked up Arthur Pym and Dirk Peters on the wreck of the *Grampus*, the *Jane* having reached Tsalal Island; the *Jane* which was attacked by natives and blown up in the midst of those waters." [009-036]

## Physical Evidence

The most poignant testimony to the *Jane*'s fate was discovered on [[bennet-islet|Bennet Islet]] during the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s southward voyage. [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] found a wooden plank bearing the ship's name:

> "The *Jane of Liverpool*! ... It was a fragment of the *Jane*! I did not utter a word until the captain's emotion had subsided. As for Hunt, I had never seen such a lightning glance from his brilliant hawk-like eyes as he now cast towards the southern horizon." [018-050–054]

[[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] "had taken the plank in his hands, and now he pressed his lips to it, while tears fell from his eyes" [018-053]. This discovery, combined with [[patterson|Patterson]]'s note-book, confirmed beyond all doubt that the *Jane* and her crew were real — and that survivors might yet be found.

## Legacy

The *Jane*'s name endures in the geography of the Antarctic. When the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition discovered a strait dividing the polar continent, they "called the strait that separated the two parts of the polar continent the Jane Sound" [035-073]. This was a tribute to the vessel whose mysterious fate had drawn them all to the ends of the earth.

[[the-halbrane|The Halbrane]] surpassed the *Jane*'s achievement by two degrees of latitude [023-045], but in a deeper sense, the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s entire voyage was made possible by the *Jane*. It was the *Jane* that first braved the Antarctic, first encountered the people of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]], and whose lost crew — [[william-guy|William Guy]] and his five sailors — gave the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition its moral purpose.

In the novel's emotional climax, when the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] was herself destroyed by ice, the boatswain Hurliguerly drew the parallel explicitly: "Poor, dear *[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]*. She will remain in these seas, like the *Jane*, her elder sister!" [031-024].
