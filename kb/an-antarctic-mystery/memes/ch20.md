## Section 20

[020-001]We had only to regain our ship, returning by the east side of the coast. [[hunt|Hunt]] brought us through the space where sheds had been erected for the preparation of the _bêche-de mer_, and we saw the remains of them. On all sides silence and abandonment reigned.

[020-002]We made a brief pause at the place where [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] seized upon the boat which bore them towards higher latitudes, even to that horizon of dark vapour whose rents permitted them to discern the huge human figure, the white giant.

[020-003]Hunt stood with crossed arms, his eyes devouring the vast extent of the sea.

[020-004]"Well, Hunt?" said I, tentatively.

[020-005]Hunt did not appear to hear me; he did not turn his head in my direction.

[020-006]"What are we doing here?" I asked him, and touched him on the shoulder.

[020-007]He started, and cast a glance upon me which went to my heart.

[020-008]"Come along, Hunt," cried Hurliguerly. "Are you going to take root on this rock? Don't you see the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_ waiting for us at her moorings? Come along. We shall be off to-morrow. There is nothing more to do here."

[020-009]It seemed to me that Hunt's trembling lips repeated the word

[020-010]"nothing," while his whole bearing protested against what the boatswain said.

[020-011]The boat brought us back to the ship. [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] had not left his cabin. West, having received no orders, was pacing the deck aft.

[020-012]I seated myself at the foot of the mainmast, observing the sea which lay open and free before us.

[020-013]At this moment the captain came on deck; he was very pale, and his features looked pinched and weary.

[020-014]"[[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]]," said he, "I can affirm conscientiously that I have done all it was possible to do. Can I hope henceforth that my brother William and his companions-- No! No! We must go away--before winter--"

[020-015]He drew himself up, and cast a last glance towards [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]].

[020-016]"To-morrow, Jim," he said to West, "to-morrow we will make sail as early as possible."

[020-017]At this moment a rough voice uttered the words:

[020-018]"And Pym--poor Pym!"

[020-019]I recognized this voice.

[020-020]It was the voice I had heard in my dream.

[020-021]CHAPTER XVII.

[020-022]AND PYM?

[020-023]"And Pym--poor Pym?"

[020-024]I turned round quickly.

[020-025]Hunt had spoken. This strange person was standing motionless at a little distance, gazing fixedly at the horizon.

[020-026]It was so unusual to hear Hunt's voice on board the schooner, that the men, whom the unaccustomed sound reached, drew near, moved by curiosity. Did not his unexpected intervention point to--I had a presentiment that it did--some wonderful revelation?

[020-027]A movement of West's hand sent the men forward, leaving only the mate, the boatswain, [[martin-holt|Martin Holt]], the sailing-master, and [[hardy|Hardy]], with the captain and myself in the vicinity of Hunt. The captain approached and addressed him:

[020-028]"What did you say?"

[020-029]"I said, ‘And Pym--poor Pym.'"

[020-030]"Well, then, what do you mean by repeating the name of the man whose pernicious advice led my brother to the island on which the

[020-031]_[[the-jane|Jane]]_ was lost, the greater part of her crew was massacred, and where we have not found even one left of those who were still here seven months ago?"

[020-032]Hunt did not speak.

[020-033]"Answer, I say--answer!" cried the captain.

[020-034]Hunt hesitated, not because he did not know what to say, but from a certain difficulty in expressing his ideas. The latter were quite clear, but his speech was confused, his words were unconnected. He had a certain language of his own which sometimes was picturesque, and his pronunciation was strongly marked by the hoarse accent of the Indians of the Far West.

[020-035]"You see," he said, "I do not know how to tell things. My tongue stops. Understand me, I spoke of Pym, poor Pym, did I not?"

[020-036]"Yes," answered West, sternly; "and what have you to say about

[020-037]Arthur Pym?"

[020-038]"I have to say that he must not be abandoned."

[020-039]"Abandoned!" I exclaimed.

[020-040]"No, never! It would be cruel--too cruel. We must go to seek him."

[020-041]"To seek him?" repeated Captain Len Guy.

[020-042]"Understand me; it is for this that I have embarked on the

[020-043]_Halbrane_--yes, to find poor Pym!"

[020-044]"And where is he," I asked, "if not deep in a grave, in the cemetery of his natal city?"

[020-045]"No, he is in the place where he remained, alone, all alone,"

[020-046]continued Hunt, pointing towards the south; "and since then the sun has risen on that horizon seven times."

[020-047]It was evident that Hunt intended to designate the Antarctic regions, but what did he mean by this?

[020-048]"Do you not know that Arthur Pym is dead?" said the captain.

[020-049]"Dead!" replied Hunt, emphasizing the word with an expressive gesture. "No! listen to me: I know things; understand me, he is not dead."

[020-050]"Come now, Hunt," said I, "remember what you do know. In the last chapter of the adventures of Arthur Pym, does not Edgar Poe relate his sudden and deplorable end?"

[020-051]"Explain yourself, Hunt," said the captain, in a tone of command. "Reflect, take your time, and say plainly whatever you have to say."

[020-052]And, while Hunt passed his hand over his brow, as though to collect his memory of far-off things, I observed to Captain Len Guy,--

[020-053]"There is something very singular in the intervention of this man, if indeed he be not mad."

[020-054]At my words the boatswain shook his head, for he did not believe

[020-055]Hunt to be in his right mind.

[020-056]The latter understood this shake of the boatswain's head, and cried out in a harsh tone,--

[020-057]"No, not mad. And madmen are respected on the prairies, even if they are not believed. And I--I must be believed. No, no, no! Pym is not dead!"

[020-058]"Edgar Poe asserts that he is," I replied.

[020-059]"Yes, I know, Edgar Poe of Baltimore. But--he never saw poor Pym, never, never."

[020-060]"What!" exclaimed Captain Len Guy; "the two men were not acquainted?"

[020-061]"No!"

[020-062]"And it was not Arthur Pym himself who related his adventures to

[020-063]Edgar Poe?"

[020-064]"No, captain, no! He, below there, at Baltimore, had only the notes written by Pym from the day when he hid himself on board the

[020-065]_[[the-grampus|Grampus]]_ to the very last hour--the last--understand me the last."

[020-066]"Who, then, brought back that journal?" asked Captain Len Guy, as he seized Hunt's hand.

[020-067]"It was Pym's companion, he who loved him, his poor Pym, like a son. It was Dirk Peters, the half-breed, who came back alone from there--beyond."

[020-068]"The half-breed, Dirk Peters!" I exclaimed.

[020-069]"Yes."

[020-070]"Alone?"

[020-071]"Alone."

[020-072]"And Arthur Pym may be--"

[020-073]"There," answered Hunt, in a loud voice, bending towards the southern line, from which he had not diverted his gaze for a moment.

[020-074]Could such an assertion prevail against the general incredulity? No, assuredly not! Martin Holt nudged Hurliguerly with his elbow, and both regarded Hunt with pity, while West observed him without speaking. Captain Len Guy made me a sign, meaning that nothing serious was to be got out of this poor fellow, whose mental faculties must have been out of gear for a long time.

[020-075]And nevertheless, when I looked keenly at Hunt, it seemed to me that a sort of radiance of truth shone out of his eyes.

[020-076]Then I set to work to interrogate the man, putting to him precise and pressing questions which he tried to answer categorically, as we shall see, and not once did he contradict himself.

[020-077]"Tell me," I asked, "did Arthur Pym really come to Tsalal

[020-078]Island on board the _Grampus_?"

[020-079]"Yes."

[020-080]"Did Arthur Pym separate himself, with the half-breed and one of the sailors, from his companions while [[william-guy|Captain William Guy]] had gone to the village of Klock-Klock?"

[020-081]"Yes. The sailor was one Allen, and he was almost immediately stifled under the stones."

[020-082]"Then the two others saw the attack, and the destruction of the schooner, from the top of the hill?"

[020-083]"Yes."

[020-084]"Then, some time later, the two left the island, after they had got possession of one of the boats which the natives could not take from them?"

[020-085]"Yes."

[020-086]"And, after twenty days, having reached the front of the curtain of vapour, they were both carried down into the gulf of the cataract?"

[020-087]This time Hunt did not reply in the affirmative; he hesitated, he stammered out some vague words; he seemed to be trying to rekindle the half-extinguished flame of his memory. At length, looking at me and shaking his head, he answered,--

[020-088]"No, not both. Understand me--Dirk never told me--"

[020-089]"Dirk Peters," interposed Captain Len Guy, quickly. "You knew

[020-090]Dirk Peters?"

[020-091]"Yes."

[020-092]"Where?"

[020-093]"At Vandalia, State of Illinois."

[020-094]"And it is from him that you have all this information concerning the voyage?"

[020-095]"From him."

[020-096]"And he came back alone--alone--from that voyage, having left

[020-097]Arthur Pym."

[020-098]"Alone!"

[020-099]"Speak, man--do speak!" I cried, impatiently. Then, in broken, but intelligible sentences, Hunt spoke,--

[020-100]"Yes--there--a curtain of vapour--so the half-breed often said--understand me. The two, Arthur Pym and he, were in the Tsalal boat. Then an enormous block of ice came full upon them. At the shock Dirk Peters was thrown into the sea, but he clung to the ice block, and--understand me, he saw the boat drift with the current, far, very far, too far! In vain did Pym try to rejoin his companion, he could not; the boat drifted on and on, and Pym, that poor dear

[020-101]Pym, was carried away. It is he who has never come back, and he is there, still there!"

[020-102]If Hunt had been the half-breed in person he could not have spoken with more heartfelt emotion of "poor Pym."

[020-103]It was then, in front of the "curtain of vapour," that Arthur

[020-104]Pym and the half-breed had been separated from each other. Dirk

[020-105]Peters had succeeded in returning from the ice-world to America, whither he had conveyed the notes that were communicated to Edgar

[020-106]Poe.

[020-107]Hunt was minutely questioned upon all these points and he replied, conformably, he declared, to what the half-breed had told him many times. According to this statement, Dirk Peters had Arthur Pym's note-book in his pocket at the moment when the ice-block struck them, and thus the journal which the half-breed placed at the disposal of the American romance-writer was saved.

[020-108]"Understand me," Hunt repeated, "for I tell you things as I have them from Dirk Peters. While the drift was carrying him away, he cried out with all his strength. Pym, poor Pym, had already disappeared in the midst of the vapour. The half-breed, feeding upon raw fish, which he contrived to catch, was carried back by a cross current to Tsalal Island, where he landed half dead from hunger."

[020-109]"To Tsalal Island!" exclaimed Captain Len Guy. "And how long was it since they had left it?"

[020-110]"Three weeks--yes, three weeks at the farthest, so Dirk Peters told me."

[020-111]"Then he must have found all that remained of the crew of the

[020-112]_Jane_--my brother William and those who had survived with him?"

[020-113]"No," replied Hunt; "and Dirk Peters always believed that they had perished--yes, to the very last man. There was no one upon the island."

[020-114]"No one?"

[020-115]"Not a living soul."

[020-116]"But the population?"

[020-117]"No one! No one, I tell you. The island was a desert--yes, a desert!"

[020-118]This statement contradicted certain facts of which we were absolutely certain. After all, though, it was possible that when Dirk

[020-119]Peters returned to Tsalal Island, the population, seized by who can tell what terror, had already taken refuge upon the south-western group, and that William Guy and his companions were still hidden in the gorges of Klock-Klock. That would explain why the half-breed had not come across them, and also why the survivors of the _Jane_ had had nothing to fear during the eleven years of their sojourn in the island.

[020-120]On the other hand, since [[patterson|Patterson]] had left them there seven months previously, if we did not find them, that must have been because they had been obliged to leave Tsalal, the place being rendered uninhabitable by the earthquake.
