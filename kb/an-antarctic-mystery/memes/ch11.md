## Section 11

[011-001]On the table lay also a volume which had been read and re-read a hundred times. Most of its pages were dogs'-eared and their margins were filled with pencilled notes. And on the cover shone the title in brightly gilded letters:

[011-002]THE ADVENTURES OF ARTHUR GORDON PYM.

[011-003]CHAPTER VIII.

[011-004]BOUND FOR THE FALKLANDS.

[011-005]On the 8th of September, in the evening, I had taken leave of His

[011-006]Excellency the Governor-General of the Archipelago of [[tristan-da-cunha|Tristan d'Acunha]]--for such is the official title bestowed upon himself by that excellent fellow, Glass, ex-corporal of artillery in the

[011-007]British Army. On the following day, before dawn, the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_ sailed.

[011-008]After we had rounded Herald Point, the few houses of Ansiedlung disappeared behind the extremity of Falmouth Bay. A fine breeze from the east carried us along gaily.

[011-009]During the morning we left behind us in succession Elephant Bay,

[011-010][[hardy|Hardy]] Rock, West Point, Cotton Bay, and Daly's Promontory; but it took the entire day to lose sight of the volcano of Tristan d'Acunha, which is eight thousand feet high; its snow-clad bulk was at last veiled by the shades of evening.

[011-011]During that week our voyage proceeded under the most favourable conditions; if these were maintained, the end of the month of

[011-012]September ought to bring us within sight of the first peaks of the

[011-013]Falkland Group; and so, very sensibly towards the south; the schooner having descended from the thirty-eighth parallel to the fifty-fifth degree of latitude.

[011-014]The most daring, or, perhaps I ought to say, the most lucky of those discoverers who had preceded the _Halbrane_, under the command of

[011-015][[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]], in the Antarctic seas, had not gone beyond--Kemp, the sixty-sixth parallel; Ballerry, the sixty-seventh; Biscoe, the sixty-eighth; Bellinghausen and Morrell, the seventieth; Cook, the seventy-first; Weddell, the seventy-fourth. And it was beyond the eighty-third, nearly five hundred and fifty miles farther, that we must go to the succour of the survivors of the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_!

[011-016]I confess that for a practical man of unimaginative temperament, I felt strangely excited; a nervous restlessness had taken possession of me. I was haunted by the figures of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] and his companions, lost in Antarctic ice-deserts. I began to feel a desire to take part in the proposed undertaking of Captain Len Guy. I thought about it incessantly. As a fact there was nothing to recall me to America. It is true that whether I should get the consent of the commander of the _Halbrane_ remained to be seen; but, after all, why should he refuse to keep me as a passenger? Would it not be a very "human" satisfaction to him to give me material proof that he was in the right, by taking me to the very scene of a catastrophe that I had regarded as fictitious, showing me the remains of the

[011-017]_Jane_ at [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]], and landing me on that selfsame island which I had declared to be a myth?

[011-018]Nevertheless, I resolved to wait, before I came to any definite determination, until an opportunity of speaking to the captain should arise.

[011-019][Illustration: Cook's route was effectually barred by ice floes.]

[011-020]After an interval of unfavourable weather, during which the _Halbrane_ made but slow progress, on the 4th of October, in the morning, the aspect of the sky and the sea underwent a marked change. The wind became calm, the waves abated, and the next day the breeze veered to the north-west. This was very favourable to us, and in ten days, with a continuance of such fortunate conditions, we might hope to reach the [[falkland-islands|Falklands]].

[011-021]It was on the 11th that the opportunity of an explanation with

[011-022]Captain Len Guy was presented to me, and by himself, for he came out of his cabin, advanced to the side of the ship where I was seated, and took his place at my side.

[011-023]Evidently he wished to talk to me, and of what, if not the subject which entirely absorbed him? He began by saying:

[011-024]"I have not yet had the pleasure of a chat with you, [[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]], since our departure from Tristan d'Acunha!"

[011-025]"To my regret, captain," I replied, but with reserve, for I wanted him to make the running.

[011-026]"I beg you to excuse me," he resumed, "I have so many things to occupy me and make me anxious. A plan of campaign to organize, in which nothing must be unforeseen or unprovided for. I beg you not to be displeased with me--"

[011-027]"I am not, I assure you."

[011-028]"That is all right, Mr. Jeorling; and now that I know you, that I am able to appreciate you, I congratulate myself upon having you for a passenger until our arrival at the Falklands."

[011-029]"I am very grateful, captain, for what you have done for me, and I feel encouraged to--"

[011-030]The moment seemed propitious to my making my proposal, when Captain

[011-031]Len Guy interrupted me.

[011-032]"Well, Mr. Jeorling," he asked, "are you now convinced of the reality of the voyage of the _Jane_, or do you still regard Edgar

[011-033]Poe's book as a work of pure imagination?"

[011-034]"I do not so regard it, captain."

[011-035]"You no longer doubt that Arthur Pym and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] have really existed, or that my brother [[william-guy|William Guy]] and five of his companions are living?"

[011-036]"I should be the most incredulous of men, captain, to doubt either fact, and my earnest desire is that the favour of Heaven may attend you and secure the safety of the shipwrecked mariners of the _Jane_."

[011-037]"I will do all in my power, Mr. Jeorling, and by the blessing of

[011-038]God I shall succeed."

[011-039]"I hope so, captain. Indeed, I am certain it will be so, and if you consent--"

[011-040]"Is it not the case that you talked of this matter with one Glass, an English ex-corporal, who sets up to be Governor of Tristan d'Acunha?" inquired the captain, without allowing me to finish my sentence.

[011-041]"That is so," I replied, "and what I learned from Glass has contributed not a little to change my doubts into certainty."

[011-042]"Ah! he has satisfied you?"

[011-043]"Yes. He perfectly remembers to have seen the _Jane_, eleven years ago, when she had put in at Tristan d'Acunha."

[011-044]"The _Jane_--and my brother?"

[011-045]"He told me that he had personal dealings with Captain William

[011-046]Guy."

[011-047]"And he traded with the _Jane_?"

[011-048]"Yes, as he has just been trading with the _Halbrane_."

[011-049]"She was moored in this bay?"

[011-050]"In the same place as your schooner."

[011-051]"And--Arthur Pym--Dirk Peters?"

[011-052]"He was with them frequently."

[011-053]"Did he ask what had become of them?"

[011-054]"Oh yes, and I informed him of the death of Arthur Pym, whom he regarded as a foolhardy adventurer, capable of any daring folly."

[011-055]"Say a madman, and a dangerous madman, Mr. Jeorling. Was it not he who led my unfortunate brother into that fatal enterprise?"

[011-056]"There is, indeed, reason to believe so from his narrative."

[011-057]"And never to forget it!" added the captain in a tone of agitation.

[011-058]"This man, Glass," I resumed, "also knew [[patterson|Patterson]], the mate of the _Jane_."

[011-059]"He was a fine, brave, faithful fellow, Mr. Jeorling, and devoted, body and soul, to my brother."

[011-060]"As West is to you, captain."

[011-061]"Does Glass know where the shipwrecked men from the _Jane_ are now?"

[011-062]"I told him, captain, and also all that you have resolved to do to save them."

[011-063]I did not think proper to add that Glass had been much surprised at

[011-064]Captain Guy's abstaining from visiting him, as, in his absurd vanity, he held the commander of the _Halbrane_ bound to do, nor that he did not consider the Governor of Tristan d'Acunha bound to take the initiative.

[011-065]"I wish to ask you, Mr. Jeorling, whether you think everything in

[011-066]Arthur Pym's journal, which has been published by Edgar Poe, is exactly true?"

[011-067]"I think there is some need for doubt," I answered "the singular character of the hero of those adventures being taken into consideration--at least concerning the phenomena of the island of

[011-068]Tsalal. And we know that Arthur Pym was mistaken in asserting that

[011-069]Captain William Guy and several of his companions perished in the landslip of the hill at Klock-Klock."

[011-070]"Ah! but he does not assert this, Mr. Jeorling! He says only that, when he and Dirk Peters had reached the opening through which they could discern the surrounding country, the seat of the artificial earthquake was revealed to them. Now, as the whole face of the hill was rushing into the ravine, the fate of my brother and twenty-nine of his men could not be doubtful to his mind. He was, most naturally, led to believe that Dirk Peters and himself were the only white men remaining alive on the island. He said nothing but this--nothing more. These were only suppositions--very reasonable, are they not?"

[011-071]"I admit that, fully, captain."

[011-072]"But now, thanks to Patterson's note-book, we are certain that my brother and five of his companions escaped from the landslip contrived by the natives."

[011-073]"That is quite clear, captain. But, as to what became of the survivors of the _Jane_, whether they were taken by the natives of

[011-074]Tsalal and kept in captivity, or remained free, Patterson's note-book says nothing, nor does it relate under what circumstances he himself was carried far away from them."

[011-075]"All that we shall learn, Mr. Jeorling. Yes, we shall know all.

[011-076]The main point is that we are quite sure my brother and five of his sailors were living less than four months ago on some part of Tsalal

[011-077]Island. There is now no question of a romance signed ‘Edgar

[011-078]Poe,' but of a veracious narrative signed ‘Patterson.'"

[011-079]"Captain," said I, "will you let me be one of your company until the end of the campaign of the _Halbrane_ in the Antarctic seas?"

[011-080]Captain Len Guy looked at me with a glance as penetrating as a keen blade. Otherwise he did not appear surprised by the proposal I had made; perhaps he had been expecting it--and he uttered only the single word:

[011-081]"Willingly."

[011-082]CHAPTER IX.

[011-083]FITTING OUT THE _HALBRANE_.

[011-084]On the 15th of October, our schooner cast anchor in [[port-egmont|Port Egmont]], on the north of West Falkland. The group is composed of two islands, one the above-named, the other Soledad or East Falkland. Captain Len

[011-085]Guy gave twelve hours' leave to the whole crew. The next day the proceedings were to begin by a careful and minute inspection of the vessel's hull and keel, in view of the contemplated prolonged navigation of the Antarctic seas. That day Captain Len Guy went ashore, to confer with the Governor of the group on the subject of the immediate re-victualling of the schooner. He did not intend to make expense a consideration, because the whole adventure might be wrecked by an unwise economy. Besides I was ready to aid with my purse, as I told him, and I intended that we should be partners in tile cost of this expedition.

[011-086][[james-west|James West]] remained on board all day, according to his custom in the absence of the captain, and was engaged until evening in the inspection of the hold. I did not wish to go ashore until the next day. I should have ample time while we remained in port to explore

[011-087]Port Egmont and its surroundings, and to study the geology and mineralogy of the island. Hurliguerly regarded the opportunity as highly favourable for the renewal of talk with me, and availed himself of it accordingly. He accosted me as follows:

[011-088]"Accept my sincere compliments, Mr. Jeorling."

[011-089]"And wherefore, boatswain?"

[011-090]"On account of what I have just heard--that you are to come with us to the far end of the Antarctic seas."

[011-091]"Oh! not so far, I imagine, and if it is not a matter of going beyond the eighty-fourth parallel--"

[011-092]"Who can tell," replied the boatswain, "at all events the

[011-093]_Halbrane_ will make more degrees of latitude than any other ship before her."

[011-094]"We shall see."

[011-095]"And does that not alarm you, Mr. Jeorling?"
