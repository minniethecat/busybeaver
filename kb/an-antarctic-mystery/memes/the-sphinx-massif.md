# The Sphinx Massif

The **Sphinx Massif** (also called the Antarctic Sphinx, the Ice Sphinx, or the Land of the Sphinx) is the colossal magnetic mountain discovered by the crew of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] on the Antarctic continent. Its shape — that of an enormous crouching sphinx — and its extraordinary magnetic properties make it the novel's central geographical mystery, giving Jules Verne's work its original French title: _Le Sphinx des Glaces_ (The Sphinx of the Ice).

Verne describes the massif as "an enormous sphinx; the body upright, the paws stretched out, crouching in the attitude of the winged monster which Grecian Mythology has placed upon the way to Thebes" [038-021]. Its dusky hue suggests that the material composing it has been "oxidized by the inclemency of the polar climate" [038-051].

## The Magnetic Phenomenon

The sphinx massif is revealed to be a colossal natural magnet — a discovery that explains the strange magnetic disturbances that have plagued the expedition throughout its journey. Verne's scientific explanation is characteristically precise:

> The Antarctic Sphinx was simply a colossal magnet. [038-056]

The massif's magnetic force is powerful enough to snatch iron objects from men's hands and to attract a human being carrying a firearm. When [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] approaches the massif, "his body was stretched out in the direction of the sphinx, as though it had been turned to iron and was attracted by the magnet" [039-008].

This magnetic force is ultimately revealed to be the cause of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym's]] death: Pym, carrying an iron gun slung over his shoulder, was seized by the magnetic fluid and hurled against the fatal loadstone before he could free himself of the weapon [039-024].

## Physical Description

The sphinx massif is described as an enormous free-standing mound on the Antarctic continent, rather than a peak of a mountain range. Its shape is so perfectly sphinx-like that it appears almost artificial — "a dusky-hued sphinx" whose bulk "might be calculated by thousands of cubic yards" [038-066].

The massif's composition is a naturally magnetized iron ore — an "artificial loadstone" of immense scale — that interacts with metallic lodes and mineral deposits in the surrounding clouds to produce the extraordinary atmospheric and navigational effects that characterize the region [038-068].

## Role in the Narrative

The sphinx massif serves multiple narrative functions in _An Antarctic Mystery_:

**As geographical climax**: The discovery of the massif is the culmination of the expedition's southward journey. After months of navigating through ice-fields, enduring mutiny, and surviving the overturning of their iceberg refuge, the crew's arrival at the Land of the Sphinx represents the geographical heart of the mystery they have pursued.

**As scientific explanation**: The massif's magnetic properties provide Verne's rational explanation for the supernatural effects described in Poe's original narrative — the "grey vapour shot with luminous and moving rays," the strange behaviour of compasses, and the ultimate disappearance of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]].

**As symbolic center**: The sphinx — the mythological creature that poses riddles and devours those who cannot answer — is the perfect symbol for the Antarctic mystery itself. The continent that has claimed so many lives, that has hidden its secrets behind walls of ice, is personified in this crouching stone figure "upon this outer edge of the southern lands" [038-066].

**As elegy**: The massif becomes Pym's unintended tomb — the magnetic force that draws iron to itself is the mechanism of his death, making the Sphinx not merely a geographical feature but a monument to the novel's lost hero.

## Connection to the Novel's Title

The original French title — _Le Sphinx des Glaces_ — directly references this geographical feature. The English title _An Antarctic Mystery_ was chosen by the translator Mrs. Cashel Hoey, though the alternative title _The Sphinx of the Ice Fields_ is also recorded [001-054].

The sphinx metaphor operates at multiple levels: it is the literal shape of the mountain, the figurative riddle that the Antarctic poses to explorers, and the symbolic embodiment of the mystery that drives the narrative forward. As [[jeorling|Jeorling]] reflects early in the voyage: "who could tell but that the sphinx of the Antarctic regions would speak for the first time to human ears!" [013-007].

## The Land of the Sphinx

The surrounding region is named "the Land of the Sphinx" by [[james-west|James West]], who notes the prudence of not prolonging their stay upon it [039-016]. The _Paracuta_ departs from this coast on the 21st of February, and a few days after their departure, "the sun set behind the western horizon to reappear no more for the whole winter" [039-029] [039-033] — marking the onset of the Antarctic night and the expedition's race against the season.
