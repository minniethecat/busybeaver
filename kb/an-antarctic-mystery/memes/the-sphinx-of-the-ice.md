# The Sphinx of the Ice

A massive sphinx-shaped rock formation at the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]] composed of magnetized iron, functioning as a colossal natural magnet that exerts irresistible attraction on all metallic objects. It is the central mystery of Verne's novel, serving as both a physical destination and a symbolic threshold between the known and unknown polar world.

## Definition

The Sphinx of the Ice is a natural magnetic formation located at the southern polar axis. As described in the novel, it is a mound approximately three hundred feet high and two to three hundred feet in circumference, shaped like the winged sphinx of Greek mythology — body upright, paws outstretched, crouching. Its dark, oxidized surface has been weathered by the polar climate, giving it a "dusky hue." (038-021)

Verne presents the formation as an enormous block of magnetized iron situated at the confluence of the magnetic meridians. According to the scientific explanation offered in the narrative, the block has been transformed into a permanent magnet of extraordinary power by the continuous electric currents at [[the-south-pole|the pole]]s — an "inexhaustible accumulator set up at the confines of the world." (038-066) (038-068)

## Role in the Novel

The Sphinx of the Ice appears as the climactic revelation of the expedition's journey beyond the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]]. It is first foreshadowed, then glimpsed in a dream, and finally encountered as a physical reality at the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]]. Its discovery forms the resolution of the novel's central mystery and provides the explanation for the fate of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]].

### Foreshadowing

The sphinx is first mentioned early in the voyage when the narrator, [[jeorling|Jeorling]], reflects on the nature of their quest. He describes himself as "under a spell which drew me towards the unknown" and wonders whether "the sphinx of the Antarctic regions would speak for the first time to human ears." (013-007) This early reference establishes the sphinx as the symbolic custodian of polar secrets.

### The Dream

During the catastrophe of the [[the-overturning-of-the-iceberg|iceberg overturning]] and the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] being carried away, [[jeorling|Jeorling]] experiences a vivid dream of "a fabulous animal of this kind, seated at [[the-south-pole|the pole]] of the world, and from whom Edgar Poe could only wrest its secrets." (038-025) This dream proves prophetic, as the sphinx is later found precisely at the polar axis.

### The Vision

As the survivors drift in their boat, one of them — [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] — experiences a vision that links the sphinx directly to [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]'s fate. In the vision, [[dirk-peters|Peters]] sees "a sort of sphinx surmounting the southern peak — the sea-sphinx" that reveals the polar mysteries, culminating in a vision of "[[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]], fierce guardian of the south pole." (026-053) This premonition ties the sphinx to [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s destiny and establishes [[dirk-peters|Peters]] as the character most connected to the unfolding mystery.

### The Discovery

The physical encounter with the sphinx occurs in the final chapters of the novel. The formation's magnetic power is first demonstrated when a metal grapnel is torn from a sailor's hand and shoots towards the sphinx "like a projectile." (038-031) As the party approaches, they feel themselves "drawn towards it by the force of its magnetic attraction." (039-010) The irresistible pull affects all iron objects, including the gun slung over [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]'s shoulder.

### The Revelation

The sphinx ultimately provides the explanation for [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]'s death. After [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Peters]] were separated, [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]]'s boat drifted into the zone of the sphinx's magnetic influence. Before he could divest himself of the iron gun he carried, [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]] was "seized by the magnetic fluid" and "hurled against the fatal loadstone Sphinx of the Ice-realm." (039-024) His body, preserved by the polar cold, remains frozen against the sphinx — an eternal guardian of the southern pole.

## Verne's Treatment

Verne employs the Sphinx of the Ice as a masterful fusion of several literary and scientific elements:

**Scientific Exposition.** The novel provides a detailed pseudo-scientific explanation of the formation's magnetic properties, invoking the convergence of magnetic meridians, electric helix theory, and the physics of electromagnetic induction. This reflects Verne's characteristic method of embedding real 19th-century scientific concepts within his fiction. (038-057) (038-060) (038-066) (038-068)

**Gothic Atmosphere.** Despite the scientific framing, the sphinx is described in language that evokes awe and terror. It is "mythological," "monstrous," and produces "a sense of awe" in all who behold it. (039-010) The imagery deliberately recalls Greek mythology and the riddle of the Sphinx — here transposed to the polar wasteland.

**Narrative Convergence.** The sphinx serves as the point where all narrative threads converge: the pursuit of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]], the mystery of the magnetic pole, the survival of the expedition, and the thematic exploration of the limits of human knowledge. It is simultaneously a physical object, a scientific phenomenon, and a symbol of Nature's ultimate secrets.

**Homage to Poe.** Verne explicitly connects the sphinx to Edgar Allan Poe, whose Narrative of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] is the precursor text. The dream reference — "from whom Edgar Poe could only wrest its secrets" (038-025) — positions Verne's novel as the completion of Poe's unfinished mystery, with the sphinx as the missing answer.

## Real-World Context

The concept of a magnetic mountain at [[the-south-pole|the pole]] draws on several 19th-century scientific ideas:

**[[terrestrial-magnetism|Terrestrial Magnetism]].** The location and nature of the Earth's magnetic poles were subjects of active scientific investigation throughout the 19th century. Verne was well-read in contemporary geomagnetic theory and correctly describes the vertical orientation of a magnetic needle at the magnetic pole. (038-060)

**Electromagnetic Theory.** The explanation of the sphinx as a natural electromagnet — iron magnetized by polar electric currents — reflects the era's fascination with the recently discovered relationship between electricity and magnetism, formalized by Orsted, Ampere, and Faraday in the decades before the novel's 1897 publication.

**[[antarctic-exploration|Polar Exploration]].** The novel was written during the Heroic Age of [[antarctic-exploration|Antarctic Exploration]], when the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]] remained one of the last uncharted territories on Earth. Verne's sphinx embodies the era's mixture of scientific ambition and imaginative speculation about what might be found at [[the-south-pole|the pole]].
