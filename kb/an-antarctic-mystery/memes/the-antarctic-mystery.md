# The Antarctic Mystery

The Antarctic Mystery is the central narrative enigma of Jules Verne's novel _An Antarctic Mystery_ (originally _Le Sphinx des Glaces_, 1897). It refers to the unexplained fate of the crew of the schooner [[the-jane|Jane]], who vanished on [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]] in the aftermath of the events recounted in Edgar Allan Poe's _The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket_.

## Origins of the Mystery

The mystery originates with the disappearance of the Jane's crew, including Captain William Guy, from Tsalal Island. The sole survivor, [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]], returns to America carrying fragments of the story that prompt [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] — brother of the Jane's captain — to mount a rescue expedition aboard the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]].

The novel is presented as a first-person account by [[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]], an American naturalist who joins the Halbrane expedition. The redactor's note frames the entire narrative as a factual continuation of Poe's fictional work:

This deliberate blurring of fiction and reality is central to Verne's literary technique, treating Poe's work as historical fact and building his own narrative upon it.

## The Sphinx of the Ice Fields

The novel's original French title, _Le Sphinx des Glaces_ (The Sphinx of the Ice Fields), evokes the image of a frozen, silent guardian withholding its secrets. The Antarctic landscape itself becomes a character — vast, indifferent, and concealing the answers beneath its ice.

> Antarctic Mystery from the Sphinx of the Ice-re… ([040-015])

The "sphinx" metaphor runs through the novel: the ice holds its secrets as stubbornly as the mythical creature posed its riddles. What happened to the Jane's crew? What did Arthur Gordon Pym discover in his final moments? These questions drive the narrative forward.

## Resolution

The Halbrane expedition eventually reaches Tsalal Island, where the crew discovers evidence of the Jane's presence and the catastrophic events that befell its crew. The mystery is partially resolved through the recovery of Pym's journal and the testimony of surviving witnesses, though some elements remain deliberately ambiguous.

> … of the facts narrated in "An Antarctic Mystery." The public is free to belie… ([001-057])

Verne ends his novel with the same gesture toward ambiguity that Poe used — the mystery is resolved enough to satisfy the narrative while preserving the sense of the unknown that gives the Antarctic its power.

## Literary Significance

The novel is notable as both a sequel to Poe's only novel and as Verne's tribute to the American writer he admired. It extends Poe's fragmentary ending into a full narrative while maintaining the gothic atmosphere and epistemological uncertainty that characterized the source material.
