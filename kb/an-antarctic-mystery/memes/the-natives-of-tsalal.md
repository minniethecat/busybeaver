# The Natives of Tsalal

The **natives of Tsalal** are the black-skinned inhabitants of [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]], a numerous and hostile people whose treacherous attack on the crew of the [[the-jane|Jane]] forms one of the central mysteries of Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_. They are the antagonists of the original expedition's story — and, in their sudden and complete disappearance, the antagonists of the rescue voyage as well.

## Numbers and Appearance

The natives of Tsalal are described as being present in overwhelming numbers. The plain behind the village of Klock-Klock was "swarming with natives who had come, no doubt, from the neighbouring islands" (008-026). Their multitude made any resistance by the small crew of the [[the-jane|Jane]] impossible.

> The plain was swarming with natives who had come, no doubt, from the neighbouring islands. (008-026)

The islanders are consistently described as having black skins and black hair — a physical characteristic that sets them apart from other indigenous peoples encountered in the narrative and that [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]'s account emphasises repeatedly (019-037).

## The Attack on the Jane

### The Landslip Device

The natives' attack on the [[the-jane|Jane]] was not a simple assault but a carefully engineered act of treachery. The islanders had contrived a landslip — an artificial avalanche of earth and rock — that buried a large portion of the ship's crew. This "infernal device of the natives of Tsalal" (005-069) demonstrated a cunning that the Europeans had not anticipated from a people they regarded as primitive.

> The landslip contrived by the natives. (011-072)

The landslip was a "cunningly contrived" trap (008-025). [[captain-william-guy|Captain William Guy]] and his men had been lulled into complacency by the apparent friendliness of the islanders, who had initially appeared cooperative and eager to trade. The attack, when it came, was devastating in its suddenness and effectiveness.

### The Explosion

The destruction of the [[the-jane|Jane]] was followed by an event that scattered the survivors and the islanders alike. An explosion — whether accidental or deliberate is never fully resolved — tore through the settlement, "killing a thousand natives and mutilating as many more" (008-028). The "infernal device" that had been meant for the Europeans appears to have claimed its creators as well.

> The native store — killing a thousand natives and mutilating as many more. (008-028)

This explosion, whatever its cause, marked the end of the [[the-jane|Jane]] expedition as an organised venture. The surviving crew members scattered, some fleeing into the interior of the island, others attempting to reach the remaining boats. [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] and [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] were among those who escaped, using a native boat to flee Tsalal and begin the journey that would ultimately lead Pym to the [[the-death-of-pym|magnetic sphinx]].

## The Disappearance

### The Emptied Island

When the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition reached [[tsalal-island|Tsalal]] eleven years later, they expected to find either the surviving crew of the [[the-jane|Jane]] or, at the very least, the native population that had attacked them. Instead, they found nothing. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] records the bewilderment: "Where were the multitude of natives, with black skins, black hair?" (019-037). The island was deserted — its villages abandoned, its population vanished as completely as if they had never existed.

> Where were the multitude of natives, with black skins, black hair? (019-037)

The disappearance of the Tsalal natives is one of the novel's deepest mysteries. Had they fled after the explosion? Had some catastrophe — natural or supernatural — wiped them out? Or had they, as some aboard the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] began to suspect, never existed in the numbers that Pym's narrative described?

### Implications for the Mystery

For [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]], the absence of the natives is both a relief and a frustration. It means that his brother [[william-guy|William Guy]] and the other survivors were not killed by the islanders — but it also means that the trail has gone cold. If the natives had taken prisoners, those prisoners might have been traced. If the natives had died in some calamity, the prisoners might have perished with them.

[[jeorling|Jeorling]] speculates: "Evidently, Mr. Jeorling; and the natives, if there were any, would have — because we do not see any natives" (034-021). The double negative captures the uncertainty that pervades the expedition's investigation: the natives are not seen, but whether they ever existed in the numbers claimed, or have simply departed for some unknown destination, remains unclear.

## Verne's Racial Imagery

The portrayal of the Tsalal natives reflects the racial attitudes of Verne's era. The islanders are depicted as treacherous and savage — the dark-skinned Other whose apparent friendliness masks murderous intent. Yet Verne complicates this simple binary: the natives are also victims of their own device (the explosion that kills a thousand of them), and their sudden disappearance suggests that they, like the Europeans, are subject to forces beyond their comprehension.

The contrast between the black-skinned Tsalal natives and the white figures that [[arthur-gordon-pym|Pym]] encounters at the end of his journey — the shrouded white figure at the magnetic sphinx — creates a symbolic opposition that Verne inherits from Poe and develops throughout his narrative.
