# New South Orkneys

The **New South Orkneys** (also called the South Orkneys) are a sub-Antarctic archipelago lying between the forty-fourth and forty-seventh meridians, south of the [[south-sandwich-islands|South Sandwich Islands]] [011-007]. In Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, they represent the last charted land the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] passes before entering the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]] and confronting the ice barrier — the final waypoint between known geography and the polar unknown [011-007] [013-010].

[[the-halbrane|The Halbrane]] reaches the group in six days from the Sandwich Islands, "singularly favoured by the weather" [011-007]. From this point forward, [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s expedition leaves behind even the remote margins of the navigated world.

> The Halbrane, singularly favoured by the weather, sighted the New South Orkneys group in six days after she had sailed from the Sandwich Islands. [011-007]

## Discovery

The archipelago was discovered jointly by the American sealer Nathaniel Palmer and the Englishman Bothwell during the 1821–22 Antarctic season — the same expedition that charted the [[south-sandwich-islands|South Sandwich Islands]] [011-007]. The islands lie between the forty-fourth and forty-seventh meridians west, crossed by the sixty-first parallel south.

Verne's narrative gives the group relatively little descriptive attention, treating it as a landmark rather than a destination. This reflects the expedition's focus: the Orkneys are not a target of exploration but a navigational reference point confirming that the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] is on the correct course.

## Geography and Ecology

### Landscape

The islands present "contorted masses and steep cliffs" when approached from the north [011-007]. The terrain is described as volcanic in origin, consistent with the geology of the Scotia Arc — the chain of islands (including [[new-georgia|South Georgia]], the [[south-sandwich-islands|South Sandwich Islands]], and the South Orkneys) that follows the boundary between the South American and Antarctic tectonic plates.

### Flora

The vegetable kingdom in the South Orkneys is extremely limited. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] records finding "grey lichen and some scanty seaweeds" — the last traces of terrestrial plant life the expedition would encounter before entering the truly barren Antarctic latitudes [013-008].

> As for the vegetable kingdom in the New South Orkneys, it is represented only by grey lichen and some scanty seaweeds. [013-008]

### Fauna

Mussels are found "in great abundance all along the rocks" [013-008]. The surrounding waters teem with the same marine life that populates the entire Scotia Arc: seals, whales, and seabirds. Bird life includes species described as having "red-rimmed eyelids" that "can be knocked over with little difficulty" — a detail that speaks to the tameness of creatures unaccustomed to human presence [013-007].

## Role in the Expedition

### The Last Charted Land

For the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition, the South Orkneys mark a psychological threshold. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] observes: "The voyage from the South Orkneys to the Polar Circle would be a short one. Beyond, as I knew, we should have to force the gate of the thick barrier of [[iceberg|icebergs]], or to discover a breach in that ice-fortress" [013-010].

After departing the South Orkneys, [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] maintains a steady meridian — a deliberate navigational choice to keep [[the-halbrane|the schooner]] on the same longitude as the route the [[the-jane|Jane]] had followed under [[william-guy|William Guy]]'s command [016-001]. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] records that "[[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] was made anxious by this alteration in the wind" as weather conditions began to deteriorate south of the Orkneys, foreshadowing the challenges the expedition would face at higher latitudes [016-001].

### Navigational Significance

The South Orkneys serve a crucial navigational function: they confirm the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]'s position before [[the-halbrane|the schooner]] enters waters where celestial navigation becomes unreliable due to fog, cloud cover, and the distortion of polar atmospheric conditions. After leaving the group, the expedition's progress becomes increasingly dependent on dead reckoning and [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s intimate knowledge of the [[the-jane|Jane]]'s original route.

## Real-World Counterpart

The South Orkney Islands exist today as part of the British Antarctic Territory (claimed also by Argentina). They lie at approximately 60°35′S, 45°30′W, and consist of four main islands — Coronation, Laurie, Powell, and Signy — along with numerous smaller islets and rocks. The British Antarctic Survey maintains a research station (Signy Research Station) on Signy Island, established in 1947.

The islands' role in _An Antarctic Mystery_ reflects Verne's characteristic method: using real, documented geographic features as stepping stones toward the speculative. By routing the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] through the South Orkneys — a real archipelago charted only eighteen years before the novel's events — Verne anchors his fantastic tale in the era's expanding geographic knowledge.
