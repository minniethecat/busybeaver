# The Antarctic Circle

The **Antarctic Circle** is the parallel of latitude at approximately 66°33' South, marking the northern limit of the Antarctic region. In Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, it serves as a crucial geographical and psychological threshold — the line beyond which the expedition enters a world of perpetual summer daylight, magnetic anomalies, and ultimately, the polar unknown.

## Geographical Significance

The Antarctic Circle defines the region where, for at least one day each year, the sun does not set (the midnight sun of the austral summer) and, conversely, does not rise (the polar night of the austral winter). The [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] crossed this line on its southward voyage, entering a realm where the normal rhythms of day and night began to dissolve.

[[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] was acutely aware of the circle's significance. The expedition had only a narrow window of the Antarctic summer in which to operate: "Between the south pole and antarctic circle lay twenty degrees, and those would have to be cleared in a month or six weeks at the most; if not, the iceberg barrier would be re-formed and closed-up. As for wintering in that part of the antarctic circle, not a man of us could have survived it" (033-008).

## The Psychological Threshold

For [[jeorling|Jeorling]], the narrator, the Antarctic Circle is more than a line on a chart — it is the boundary of human experience. No navigator had ever ventured significantly beyond it, and those who had approached it — Cook, Weddell, Biscoe — had returned with tales of impenetrable ice and unendurable cold.

The crossing of the circle marks the point at which [[jeorling|Jeorling]]'s rational scepticism begins to erode. North of the circle, he was a man of science, a mineralogist observing the world with professional detachment. South of it, he becomes something else: a man "under the dominion of a sort of hallucination" (033-001), increasingly unable to distinguish between the empirical reality of the voyage and the deranged perceptions of [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]].

## Historical Context

In Verne's time, the Antarctic Circle was the effective limit of human exploration. James Cook had crossed it in 1773, becoming the first navigator to do so, and had reached as far south as 71°10' S before being turned back by the ice. James Weddell had reached 74°15' S in 1823. But no one had approached the pole itself, and the region within the circle remained — as [[jeorling|Jeorling]] repeatedly notes — the "Unknown."
