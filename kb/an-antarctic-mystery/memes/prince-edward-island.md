# Prince Edward Island

**Prince Edward Island** is a small sub-Antarctic island in the southern Indian Ocean, situated at approximately 46° 55' south latitude and 37° 46' east longitude (006-009). In Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, it serves as an intermediate geographical waypoint on the voyage from the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelen Isles]] southward toward the Antarctic — a lonely speck of land that marks the transition from the temperate Indian Ocean into the cold southern waters.

## Geography and Position

Prince Edward Island lies in the sub-Antarctic Indian Ocean, roughly midway between the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] and the Antarctic Convergence. Verne gives its coordinates precisely — south latitude 46° 55', east longitude 37° 46' — and notes that the island was visible from the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] for some twelve hours as she sailed past (006-009).

The island is part of a small archipelago that also includes the smaller Marion Island, though Verne mentions only the principal landmass. Its position places it squarely in the path of vessels sailing from the Cape of Good Hope or the Kerguelens toward the polar seas, making it a natural landmark for navigators.

## Role in the Novel

### The Halbrane's Passage

On the 22nd of August, the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] sighted the outline of Prince Edward Island as she sailed south from the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] (006-009). The sighting confirmed the vessel's position and served as a waypoint on the long passage toward the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]]. The chapter title "From the Kerguelen Isles to Prince Edward Island" signals the island's importance as a geographical milestone — it marks the first stage of the voyage after departing the last inhabited outpost.

### The Jane's Earlier Passage

The island had also been sighted years earlier by the [[the-jane|Jane]] under [[william-guy|William Guy]]'s command. After recovering from the effects of their earlier sufferings, the crew of the _Jane_ "sighted Prince Edward's Island on the 13th of October, then the Crozet Islands, and afterwards the Kerguelens" (007-045). This sequence — Prince Edward Island, then the Crozets, then the Kerguelens — represents the standard navigation route for vessels working south and east through the Indian Ocean.

> On the 22nd of August the outline of Prince Edward's Island was sighted, south latitude 46° 55', and 37° 46' east longitude. We were in sight of the island for twelve hours, and then it was lost to view. (006-009)

### A Link Between Expeditions

The fact that both the _Jane_ and the _Halbrane_ sighted Prince Edward Island creates a geographical link between the two expeditions separated by eleven years. For [[jeorling|Jeorling]], the narrator, these shared landmarks lend credibility to [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]'s conviction that he is faithfully retracing his brother's path toward the Antarctic unknown.

## Real-World Counterpart

Prince Edward Island is a real sub-Antarctic territory, part of the Prince Edward Islands group that belongs to South Africa. The archipelago consists of the larger Marion Island and the smaller Prince Edward Island proper. Discovered in 1663 by the Dutch navigator Barent Barentszoon Lam, the islands lie approximately 1,770 kilometres southeast of Port Elizabeth, South Africa. In Verne's time, the islands were known primarily to sealers and whalers who operated in southern waters.
