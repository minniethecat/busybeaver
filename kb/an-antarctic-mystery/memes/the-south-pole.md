# The South Pole

**The South Pole** is the ultimate geographical objective of the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] expedition in Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_. Unlike the North Pole, which by Verne's time had been the target of numerous expeditions, the South Pole remained entirely unconquered — a region of mystery, magnetic anomaly, and sublime terror that no human being had ever approached.

## Approaching the Pole

The expedition's passage beyond the South Pole is one of the novel's most remarkable episodes. After the catastrophic [[the-iceberg-collision|iceberg collision]], the surviving crew drift southward on an ice fragment, carried by currents beyond the eighty-third parallel — and then beyond the pole itself.

[[jeorling|Jeorling]] records their extraordinary position: the party had passed to a latitude where "the sun was on the verge of the horizon" during the brief Antarctic summer, and where the magnetic compass became utterly useless (038-008).

> "It was now beyond a doubt that we were moving between the two halves of a continent, one on the east, the other on the west, which formed the vast antarctic region." (033-012)

## The Magnetic Pole

The magnetic south pole — distinct from the geographic pole — is the source of the novel's most extraordinary phenomena. As the expedition approaches this region, compasses become "wild," their needles "staggering about, helpless" (038-068). The magnetic force is so powerful that it animates the colossal [[the-discovery-of-the-sphinx|Antarctic Sphinx]], a natural loadstone magnet capable of tearing iron objects from human hands.

> "The Antarctic Sphinx was simply a colossal magnet." (038-056)

The magnetic pole represents the intersection of Verne's scientific imagination and his gift for the sublime. It is a real physical phenomenon — magnetic poles were well understood in the 19th century — amplified to mythic proportions.

## Beyond the Pole

The expedition's passage beyond the South Pole places them in a geographic limbo: between the pole and the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]], "lay twenty degrees, and those would have to be cleared in a month or six weeks at the most; if not, the iceberg barrier would be re-formed and closed-up" (033-008). The threat of being trapped in perpetual Antarctic winter adds urgency to every decision.

At the pole, the crew witnesses the [[the-polar-aurora|polar aurora]] in its full splendour — "broad and brilliant rays" illuminating the southern sky as the sun dips below the horizon for the long polar night (039-033).
