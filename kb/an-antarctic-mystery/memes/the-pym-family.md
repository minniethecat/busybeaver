# The Pym Family

The **Pym family** of [[nantucket|Nantucket Island]] is the New England merchant family into which [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Gordon Pym]] was born — a family whose very existence becomes a contested question in Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, as characters debate whether Pym was a real person whose adventures were documented by [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Poe]], or a fictional creation whose family never existed at all.

## The Family in Nantucket

Arthur Gordon Pym was born at [[nantucket|Nantucket]], the island off the coast of Massachusetts that was the centre of American whaling in the early nineteenth century (006-018). His father was "one of the principal merchants" of the island — a man of substance and reputation whose position in Nantucket society gave his son access to the maritime world that would ultimately consume him (005-025).

> Arthur Gordon Pym was born at Nantucket, where he attended the school of Mr. Ronalds. (006-018)

The Pym family belonged to the merchant class of Nantucket — families whose wealth derived from the whaling and sealing industries that sent ships into all the oceans of the world. It was natural that a son of such a family would be drawn to the sea, and the "association of the ship and the sea fired the imagination of Arthur Pym" from an early age (006-020).

## The Question of Existence

### Fiction or Reality?

The existence of the Pym family is one of the central meta-fictional questions of Verne's novel. [[jeorling|Jeorling]], the narrator, is from [[nantucket|Connecticut]] and has visited Nantucket — yet he claims no knowledge of the Pym family. When [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] presses him on the point, Jeorling must admit: "It is possible that you did not know the Pym family, that you have never met them either at Providence or at Nantucket" (005-034).

> Since you have visited Nantucket Island, you might have known the Pym family who lived in Nantucket Island. (005-014)

Captain Len Guy, however, insists on the family's reality: "You belong to Connecticut, since you have visited Nantucket Island, that you might have known the Pym family who lived in Nantucket Island" (005-014). For the captain, the existence of the Pym family is a necessary premise — without it, his brother's story and his own mission collapse into fiction.

### Verne's Narrative Strategy

Verne's treatment of the Pym family is a brilliant piece of narrative engineering. By having his characters debate the reality of Pym and his family, Verne creates a framework in which Poe's fiction can be treated as fact without the reader being required to accept this premise naively. The controversy over the Pym family's existence mirrors the reader's own uncertainty about the boundary between Verne's novel and Poe's.

[[jeorling|Jeorling]] acknowledges having read Poe's narrative but initially regards it as fiction — "the fictions of Edgar Poe's romance" (004-053). His gradual acceptance of the Pym family's reality parallels the reader's immersion in Verne's fictional world, where the conventions of realist fiction allow Poe's fantastic tale to be treated as documented history.

## Arthur Pym's Father

Arthur Pym's father, though never directly characterised, casts a long shadow over the narrative. As a principal merchant of Nantucket, he represents the world of commercial respectability that Arthur Pym fled — and to which he never returned. The mention of "Arthur Pam's father" (a variant spelling in the text) as "one of the principal merchants" of Nantucket establishes the social position from which the young adventurer descended into the Antarctic unknown (005-025).

## Connecticut and Nantucket

The geography of the Pym family's world — Connecticut, Providence, Nantucket — maps onto the New England maritime economy of the early nineteenth century. [[jeorling|Jeorling]] is from Connecticut and intends to return there; the Pym family is rooted in Nantucket and has connections in Providence. This tight geographical focus contrasts with the vast reaches of the Southern Ocean into which [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]] ventured, creating a tension between the domestic world of New England and the alien world of the Antarctic that runs throughout the novel.

## After Arthur Pym

After Arthur Pym's death at the [[the-death-of-pym|magnetic sphinx]], the Pym family fades from the narrative. Their son's fate — and the publication of Poe's account of his adventures — becomes their legacy.

The novel offers no glimpse of the family's reception of the news of Arthur's end, nor of their response to the confirmation, eleven years later, that his adventures were real. This silence is itself significant: the domestic world of Nantucket cannot assimilate the Antarctic experience, and the Pym family's story ends not with resolution but with the unbridgeable gap between the Nantucket counting-house and the magnetic sphinx at the bottom of the world.
