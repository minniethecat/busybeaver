## Section 22

[022-001]"Ah! if it depended only on me! if I were sure of the assent and the help of them all!"

[022-002]Then [[hearne|Hearne]] spoke roughly:

[022-003]"Captain," said he, "it's two months since we left the

[022-004][[falkland-islands|Falklands]]. Now, my companions were engaged for a voyage which was not to take them farther beyond the [[iceberg|icebergs]] than [[tsalal-island|Tsalal Island]]."

[022-005]"That is not so," exclaimed [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]]. "No! That is not so. I recruited you all for an enterprise which I have a right to pursue, so far as I please."

[022-006]"Beg pardon," said Hearne, coolly, "but we have come to a point which no navigator has ever yet reached, in a sea, no ship except the _[[the-jane|Jane]]_ has ever ventured into before us, and therefore my comrades and I mean to return to the Falklands before the bad season. From there _you_ can return to Tsalal Island, and even go on to the Pole, if you so please."

[022-007]A murmur of approbation greeted his words; no doubt the sealing-master justly interpreted the sentiments of the majority, composed of the new recruits. To go against their opinion, to exact the obedience of these ill-disposed men, and under such conditions to risk the unknown Antarctic waters, would have been an act of temerity--or, rather, an act of madness--that would have brought about some catastrophe.

[022-008]Nevertheless, West, advancing upon Hearne, said to him in a threatening tone, "Who gave you leave to speak?"

[022-009]"The captain questioned us," replied Hearne. "I had a right to reply."

[022-010]The man uttered these words with such insolence that West, who was generally so self-restrained, was about to give free vent to his wrath, when Captain Len Guy, stopping him by a motion of his hand, said quietly,--

[022-011]"Be calm, Jem. Nothing can be done unless we are all agreed. What is your opinion, Hurliguerly?"

[022-012]"It is very clear, captain," replied the boatswain. "I will obey your orders, whatever they may be! It is our duty not to forsake [[william-guy|William Guy]] and the others so long as any chance of saving them remains."

[022-013]The boatswain paused for a moment, while several of the sailors gave unequivocal signs of approbation.

[022-014]"As for what concerns [[arthur-gordon-pym|Arthur Pym]]--"

[022-015]"There is no question of Arthur Pym," struck in the captain,

[022-016]"but only of my brother William and his companions."

[022-017]I saw at this moment that [[dirk-peters|Dirk Peters]] was about to protest, and caught hold of his arm. He shook with anger, but kept silence.

[022-018]The captain continued his questioning of the men, desiring to know by name all those upon whom he might reckon. The old crew to a man acquiesced in his proposals, and pledged themselves to obey his orders implicitly and follow him whithersoever he chose to go.

[022-019]Three only of the recruits joined those faithful seamen; these were

[022-020]English sailors. The others were of Hearne's opinion, holding that for them the campaign was ended at Tsalal Island. They therefore refused to go beyond that point, and formally demanded that the ship should be steered northward so as to clear the icebergs at the most favourable period of the season.

[022-021]Twenty men were on their side, and to constrain them to lend a hand to the working of the ship if she were to be diverted to the south would have been to provoke them to rebel. There was but one resource: to arouse their covetousness, to strike the chord of self-interest.

[022-022]I intervened, therefore, and addressed them in a tone which placed the seriousness of my proposal beyond a doubt.

[022-023]"Men of the _[[the-halbrane|Halbrane]]_, listen to me! Just as various States have done for voyages of discovery in the Polar Regions, I offer a reward to the crew of this schooner. Two thousand dollars shall be shared among you for every degree we make beyond the eighty-fourth parallel."

[022-024]Nearly seventy dollars to each man; this was a strong temptation.

[022-025]I felt that I had hit the mark.

[022-026]"I will sign an agreement to that effect," I continued, "with

[022-027]Captain Len Guy as your representative, and the sums gained shall be handed to you on your return, no matter under what conditions that return be accomplished."

[022-028]I waited for the effect of this promise, and, to tell the truth, I had not to wait long.

[022-029]"Hurrah!" cried the boatswain, acting as fugleman to his comrades, who almost unanimously added their cheers to his. Hearne offered no farther opposition; it would always be in his power to put in his word when the circumstances should be more propitious.

[022-030]Thus the bargain was made, and, to gain my ends, I have made a heavier sacrifice.

[022-031]It is true we were within seven degrees of the [[the-south-pole|South Pole]], and, if the _Halbrane_ should indeed reach that spot, it would never cost me more than fourteen thousand dollars.

[022-032]Early in the morning of the 27th of December the _Halbrane_ put out to sea, heading south-west.

[022-033]After the scene of the preceding evening Captain Len Guy had taken a few hours' rest. I met him next day on deck while West was going about fore and aft, and he called us both to him.

[022-034]"[[jeorling|Mr. Jeorling]]," he said, "it was with a terrible pang that I came to the resolution to bring our schooner back to the north! I felt I had not done all I ought to do for our unhappy fellow-countrymen: but I knew that the majority of the crew would be against me if I insisted on going beyond Tsalal Island."

[022-035]"That is true, captain; there was a beginning of indiscipline on board, and perhaps it might have ended in a revolt."

[022-036]"A revolt we should have speedily put down," said West, coolly,

[022-037]"were it only by knocking Hearne, who is always exciting the mutinous men, on the head."

[022-038]"And you would have done well, Jem," said the captain. "Only, justice being satisfied, what would have become of the agreement together, which we must have in order to do anything?"

[022-039]"Of course, captain, it is better that things passed off without violence! But for the future Hearne will have to look out for himself."

[022-040]"His companions," observed the captain, "are now greedy for the prizes that have been promised them. The greed of gain will make them more willing and persevering. The generosity of Mr. Jeorling has succeeded where our entreaties would undoubtedly have failed. I thank him for it."

[022-041]Captain Len Guy held out a hand to me, which I grasped cordially.

[022-042]After some general conversation relating to our purpose, the ship's course, and the proposed verification of the bearings of the group of islands on the west of Tsalal which is described by

[022-043]Arthur Pym, the captain said,--

[022-044]"As it is possible that the ravages of the earthquake did not extend to this group, and that it may still be inhabited, we must be on our guard in approaching the bearings."

[022-045]"Which cannot be very far off," I added. "And then, captain, who knows but that your brother and his sailors might have taken refuge on one of these islands!"

[022-046]This was admissible, but not a consoling eventuality, for in that case the poor fellows would have fallen into the hands of those savages of whom they were rid while they remained at Tsalal.

[022-047]"Jem," resumed Captain Len Guy, "we are making good way, and no doubt land will be signalled in a few hours. Give orders for the watch to be careful."

[022-048]"It's done, captain."

[022-049]"There is a man in the crow's-nest?"

[022-050]"Dirk Peters himself, at his own request."

[022-051]"All right, Jem; we may trust his vigilance."

[022-052]"And also his eyes," I added, "for he is gifted with amazing sight."

[022-053]For two hours of very quick sailing not the smallest indication of the group of eight islands was visible.

[022-054]"It is incomprehensible that we have not come in sight of them,"

[022-055]said the captain. "I reckon that the _Halbrane_ has made sixty miles since this morning, and the islands in question are tolerably close together."

[022-056]"Then, captain, we must conclude--and it is not unlikely--that the group to which Tsalal belonged has entirely disappeared in the earthquake."

[022-057]"Land ahead!" cried Dirk Peters.

[022-058]We looked, but could discern nothing on the sea, nor was it until a quarter of an hour had elapsed that our glasses enabled us to recognize the tops of a few scattered islets shining in the oblique rays of the sun, two or three miles to the westward.

[022-059]What a change! How had it come about? Arthur Pym described spacious islands, but only a small number of tiny islets, half a dozen at most, protruded from the waters.

[022-060]At this moment the half-breed came sliding down from his lofty perch and jumped to the deck.

[022-061]"Well, Dirk Peters! Have you recognized the group?" asked the captain.

[022-062]"The group?" replied the half-breed, shaking his head. "No, I have only seen the tops of five or six islets. There is nothing but stone heaps there--not a single island!"

[022-063]As the schooner approached we easily recognized these fragments of the group, which had been almost entirely destroyed on its western side. The scattered remains formed dangerous reefs which might seriously injure the keel or the sides of the _Halbrane_, and there was no intention of risking the ship's safety among them. We accordingly cast anchor at a safe distance, and a boat was lowered for the reception of Captain Len Guy, the boatswain, Dirk Peters,

[022-064][[martin-holt|Holt]], two men and myself. The still, transparent water, as Peters steered us skilfully between the projecting edges of the little reefs, allowed us to see, not a bed of sand strewn with shells, but blackish heaps which were overgrown by land vegetation, tufts of plants not belonging to the marine flora that floated on the surface of the sea. Presently we landed on one of the larger islets which rose to about thirty feet above the sea.

[022-065]"Do the tides rise sometimes to that height?" I inquired of the captain.

[022-066][Illustration: The half-breed in the crow's-nest.]

[022-067]"Never," he replied, "and perhaps we shall discover some remains of the vegetable kingdom, of habitations, or of an encampment."

[022-068]"The best thing we can do," said the boatswain, "is to follow

[022-069]Dirk Peters, who has already distanced us. The half-breed's lynx eyes will see what we can't."

[022-070]Peters had indeed scaled the eminence in a moment, and we presently joined him on the top.

[022-071]The islet was strewn with remains (probably of those domestic animals mentioned in Arthur Pym's journal), but these bones differed from the bones on Tsalal Island by the fact that the heaps dated from a few months only. This then agreed with the recent period at which we placed the earthquake. Besides, plants and tufts of flowers were growing here and there.

[022-072]"And these are this year's," I cried, "no southern winter has passed over them."

[022-073]These facts having been ascertained, no doubt could remain respecting the date of the cataclysm after the departure of

[022-074][[patterson|Patterson]]. The destruction of the population of Tsalal whose bones lay about the village was not attributable to that catastrophe.

[022-075]William Guy and the five sailors of the _Jane_ had been able to fly in time, since no bones that could be theirs had been found on the island.

[022-076]Where had they taken refuge? This was the ever-pressing question.

[022-077]What answer were we to obtain? Must we conclude that having reached one of these islets they had perished in the swallowing-up of the archipelago? We debated this point, as may be supposed, at a length and with detail which I can only indicate here. Suffice it to say that a decision was arrived at to the following effect. Our sole chance of discovering the unfortunate castaways was to continue our voyage for two or three parallels farther; the goal was there, and which of us would not sacrifice even his life to attain it?

[022-078]"God is guiding us, Mr. Jeorling," said Captain Len Guy.

[022-079]CHAPTER XVIII.

[022-080]A REVELATION.

[022-081]The following day, the 29th of December, at six in the morning, the schooner set sail with a north-east wind, and this time her course was due south. The two succeeding days passed wholly without incident; neither land nor any sign of land was observed. The men on the _Halbrane_ took great hauls of fish, to their own satisfaction and ours. It was New Year's Day, 1840, four months and seventeen days since I had left the Kerguelens and two months and five days since the _Halbrane_ had sailed from the Falklands. The half-breed, between whom and myself an odd kind of tacit understanding subsisted, approached the bench on which I was sitting--the captain was in his cabin, and West was not in sight--with a plain intention of conversing with me. The subject may easily be guessed.
