# Crozet Islands

The **Crozet Islands** are a remote sub-Antarctic archipelago in the southern Indian Ocean, situated between the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] and [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]]. In Jules Verne's _An Antarctic Mystery_, they appear as a geographical reference point — one of the scattered island groups that punctuate the long voyage from temperate latitudes into the Antarctic unknown.

## Geography

The Crozet group is described as a mountainous archipelago, visible from a considerable distance at sea. When the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] passed within sight, the look-out "discerned the mountains of the Crozet group" (004-053), confirming the vessel's position on the route south. The mountainous character of these islands distinguishes them from the lower-lying lands of the surrounding ocean and makes them conspicuous landmarks for navigators.

## Role in the Novel

### The Halbrane's Passage

The [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] sighted the mountains of the Crozet group during her passage south from the [[kerguelen-islands|Kerguelens]] (004-053). The sighting was brief — a confirmation of longitude rather than a port of call — but significant as a waypoint on the route toward the [[the-antarctic-circle|Antarctic Circle]].

### The Jane's Earlier Voyage

The Crozets had also been sighted by the [[the-jane|Jane]] eleven years earlier. After departing [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] on the 13th of October, the _Jane_ passed "the Crozet Islands, and afterwards the Kerguelens" (007-045). This sequence — Prince Edward Island, the Crozets, the Kerguelens — traces the standard sailing route through the southern Indian Ocean, a route that both expeditions followed.

> After fine and bad weather the _Jane_ sighted Prince Edward's Island on the 13th of October, then the Crozet Islands, and afterwards the Kerguelens. (007-045)

### A Shared Geography

Like [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]], the Crozets function as a shared geographical reference that links the two expeditions separated by more than a decade. The fact that both the [[the-jane|Jane]] and the [[the-halbrane|Halbrane]] passed these islands reinforces the sense that [[captain-len-guy|Captain Len Guy]] is faithfully retracing his brother [[william-guy|William Guy]]'s route — that the geography of the southern Indian Ocean is the fixed stage upon which the two voyages, eleven years apart, play out their parallel dramas.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Crozet Islands are a real French sub-Antarctic archipelago, discovered in 1772 by the French explorer Marc-Joseph Marion du Fresne, who named them after his second-in-command, Jules Crozet. The archipelago consists of five main island groups — Île de la Possession, Île de l'Est, Île aux Cochons, Île des Pingouins, and Îlots des Apôtres — scattered across roughly 500 square kilometres of ocean.

In Verne's time, the Crozets were frequented by sealers and whalers, and the waters around them were known for their abundance of marine life — a detail consistent with the novel's references to the rich Antarctic ecosystem. Today, the islands are a French National Nature Reserve and are uninhabited except for a small rotating research station on Île de la Possession.
