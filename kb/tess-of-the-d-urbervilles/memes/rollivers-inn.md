# Rolliver's Inn

## Location

Rolliver's Inn is "the single alehouse at this end of the long and broken village" of [[marlott|Marlott]] (004-001). It sits at the lower end of the village, distinguished from The Pure Drop Inn at the other end. The inn is owned and operated by Mrs Rolliver, whose "great woollen shawl lately discarded by the landlady" (004-012) serves as a makeshift bed for the children who accompany their mothers to the alehouse.

The inn's physical character is modest: a low-ceilinged room where farmers and laborers gather to drink and talk. Its isolation at one end of [[marlott|Marlott]]'s "long and broken" street marks it as a place apart — a space where the usual restraints of village life loosen and where news, whether true or false, circulates freely.

## Description

Rolliver's is the quintessential rural alehouse of Hardy's Wessex: unpretentious, convivial, and slightly disreputable. The interior is described as a place of warmth and sociability, where the men of [[marlott|Marlott]] "drink with Rolliver in a corner of the housetop" (004-019) — the "housetop" likely referring to an upper room or loft where drinkers gather away from the main bar.

The presence of women and children at the inn is a notable detail. Mrs Durbeyfield goes "to discover him at Rolliver's, to sit there for an hour or two" (003-145), bringing her children with her, who sleep under Mrs Rolliver's discarded shawl (004-012). This blurring of domestic and public space is characteristic of the rural working-class world Hardy depicts — the alehouse is an extension of the home, not a separate male sphere.

The inn's landlady, Mrs Rolliver, is a background figure whose discarded shawl becomes a memorable detail. Her name attaches to the inn, suggesting a long-established presence in the village; she is the keeper of its most important social institution.

## Significance

Rolliver's Inn is the novel's ground zero for the disaster of ancestry. It is at Rolliver's that [[parson-tringham|Parson Tringham]]'s revelation about the Durbeyfield lineage is first discussed: "Your father learnt it on his way hwome from [[shaston|Shaston]], and the pa'son's news — that he went up to Rolliver's half an hour ago to tell it" (003-120). The inn is where private knowledge becomes public gossip, where [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s delusions of grandeur are fed by an audience of fellow drinkers.

The inn represents the communal life of the rural working class — the warmth, the shared hardship, and the collective vulnerability to fate. [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s drinking at Rolliver's is both a symptom of his weakness and a perfectly understandable response to a life of rural poverty. The alehouse is where the burdens of agricultural labor are temporarily lifted, but it is also where the seeds of the family's destruction are watered.

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]]'s trips to retrieve her husband from Rolliver's are a recurring domestic ritual: "This going to hunt up her shiftless husband at the inn was one of Mrs Durbeyfield's still extant enjoyments" (003-143). The phrase captures the ambiguity of the inn's role — it is simultaneously a place of irresponsibility and a source of the only pleasures available to the rural poor.

## Analysis

### The Alehouse as Fate's Engine

Rolliver's Inn is where the novel's plot acquires its momentum. The parson's casual revelation is transformed, in the alehouse atmosphere, from a genealogical curiosity into a life-changing claim. The alcohol that lubricates the telling also lubricates the believing: [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s drunken embrace of his ancestry at Rolliver's is the first domino in the chain that leads [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to [[trantridge|Trantridge]]. The inn is fate's amplifier — it takes the parson's mild historical observation and converts it into a family obsession.

### Women, Children, and the Public House

Hardy's depiction of women and children at Rolliver's is notably unsentimental. Mrs Durbeyfield brings her children to the alehouse because there is nowhere else to leave them; the children sleep under a discarded shawl because the alternative is a cold cottage. This detail captures the casual neglect that poverty imposes: the children are not abused, but they are exposed to adult vices because the structures that would protect them — nurseries, schools, attentive parenting — are luxuries the family cannot afford. The alehouse is honest about what rural life is actually like.

### The Two Inns of Marlott

That [[marlott|Marlott]] has two inns — Rolliver's at one end and The Pure Drop at the other — is a structural detail that Hardy uses to map the village's social geography. The Pure Drop Inn is mentioned in connection with the May-Day dance (002-085) and the more respectable end of village life. Rolliver's, by contrast, is the more disreputable establishment — the place where [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] drinks away what little money the family has. The two inns mark the moral poles of [[marlott|Marlott]]: celebration and dissolution, community and escapism.
