# Sorrow's Baptism and Death

## Summary

In the harvest fields near [[marlott|Marlott]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] nurses her illegitimate infant — a child she regards with deeply ambivalent emotions, veering between cold distance and passionate kissing (014-131). The baby, born of her violation by [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]], falls suddenly ill. When it becomes clear the child is dying, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is plunged into spiritual terror: her baby has not been baptized. (014-217)

With her father refusing to allow a parson into the house — he has returned from his weekly drinking at [[rollivers-inn|Rolliver's Inn]] and barred the door (014-226–014-229) — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] performs the baptism herself. Gathering her younger siblings around her, she stands in her white nightgown, dips her hand into a basin, and christens the infant "SORROW" in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost. (014-286)

The baby dies shortly after. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], now calm, seeks out the Vicar to confirm that her improvised baptism was sufficient for a Christian burial. After a tense exchange, the Vicar reluctantly concedes it was "just the same." (014-367) [[sorrow|Sorrow]] is buried in the corner of the churchyard reserved for unbaptized infants, drunkards, and suicides — "God's allotment where He lets the nettles grow" (014-376). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] marks the grave with a makeshift cross of flowers.

## Context

The baptism occurs in the aftermath of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s return from [[trantridge|Trantridge]], where she was violated by [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] under the cover of the Chase woods. She has returned to [[marlott|Marlott]] pregnant and shamed, a fallen woman by Victorian standards — though Hardy's narrative insists on her purity.

The social context is one of absolute condemnation. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s baby is "a nameless child" (014-178), an offence against society merely by existing. Her fellow field-workers regard her as a cautionary tale: "she's fond of that there child, though she mid pretend to hate en, and say she wishes the baby and her too were in the churchyard" (014-134).

The religious context is one of doctrinal terror. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], having studied the Scriptures during her confinement, has absorbed a vivid eschatology in which unbaptized infants are consigned to "the nethermost corner of hell" (014-239). Her fear for her child's soul drives her to take on the priestly role herself.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — The young mother, not yet twenty, who performs the baptism herself when the institutional Church is closed to her. Her action is simultaneously heretical (a woman baptizing) and profoundly faithful (she believes baptism matters absolutely). The scene marks [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s first direct confrontation with institutional religion — a confrontation that will recur throughout the novel.

**[[sorrow|Sorrow]]** — The infant, "that intrusive creature, that bastard gift of shameless Nature" (014-323). The name [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] gives her child — [[sorrow|Sorrow]] — is both a confession of her own feeling and an indictment of the world that condemns the innocent. [[sorrow|Sorrow]] exists in the novel only to die, and that death serves to illuminate both [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s moral courage and the cruelty of the social order that declares her child damned.

## Narrative Impact

The baptism scene is crucial to the novel's moral architecture. It establishes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as someone who, when institutions fail, acts anyway — a pattern that will recur in her later life. The Vicar's concession that her baptism was valid is an admission that institutional religion is not the only path to grace, a theological position that Hardy develops throughout the novel.

The scene also deepens the novel's critique of sexual morality. [[sorrow|Sorrow]] dies because society has no place for illegitimate children; [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s terror for her baby's soul reflects a theology that punishes the innocent for the circumstances of their birth. "So passed away [[sorrow|Sorrow the Undesired]]" (014-323) — the epitaph is Hardy's own, and its coldness is deliberate. Nature, indifferent to human morality, produces life and takes it away; only human society calls some lives "bastard."

## Key Passages

**The Ambivalence of Motherhood**:
> Tess, with a curiously stealthy yet courageous movement, and with a still rising colour, unfastened her frock and began suckling the child... then all of a sudden she fell to violently kissing it some dozens of times, as if she could never leave off, the child crying at the violence of the kisses. (014-121–014-132)

**The Terror of Damnation**:
> Her baby had not been baptized. ... She thought of the child consigned to the nethermost corner of hell, as to the double doom of lack of baptism and illegitimacy; saw the arch-fiend tossing it with his three-pronged fork. (014-217–014-241)

**The Baptism**:
> "SORROW, I baptize thee in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost." ... Here she dipped her hand into the basin, and fervently drew an immense cross upon the baby with her forefinger. (014-286–014-295)

**The Death**:
> Poor Sorrow's campaign against sin, the world, and the devil was doomed to be of but short duration. ... In silence, the infant breathed his last. (014-312–014-315)

**The Vicar's Concession**:
> "Don't for God's sake speak as saint to sinner, but as you yourself to me myself — poor me! ... will it be just the same for him as if you had baptized him?" ... The Vicar felt himself cornered. (014-367–014-369)

**The Burial**:
> So passed away Sorrow the Undesired — that intrusive creature, that bastard gift of shameless Nature who respects not the social law. ... So the baby was carried in a small deal box ... to that shabby corner of God's allotment where He lets the nettles grow, and where all unbaptized infants, notorious drunkards, suicides, and others of the conjecturally damned are laid. (014-323–014-377)

## Analysis

### The Sacramental Woman

Hardy presents [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s baptism as genuine sacramental action. She does not merely simulate the priestly role — she fulfills it. The Vicar's admission that it was "just the same" is theologically radical: it implies that the validity of a sacrament depends on the faith and intention of the officiant, not on institutional authorization. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], the fallen woman, becomes the channel of grace.

### Nature vs. Society

[[sorrow|Sorrow]] is called "that bastard gift of shameless Nature who respects not the social law" (014-323). The phrasing is double-edged: "shameless Nature" produces life without regard for human conventions, and the epithet condemns not Nature but the society that names some children "bastard." [[sorrow|Sorrow]] dies in a world that has no category for his existence.

### The Children as Congregation

The baptism is performed before an assembly of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s younger siblings, who hold the Prayer-Book, recite the responses, and at the conclusion "raised their voices to clerk's pitch" (014-299). This makeshift congregation — children of the same impoverished household — becomes, for a moment, a true church. The scene suggests that community, not institution, is the ground of authentic religious life.
