[034-001]They drove by the level road along the valley to a distance of a few miles,

[034-002]and, reaching Wellbridge, turned away from the village to the left, and over

[034-003]the great Elizabethan bridge which gives the place half its name. Immediately

[034-004]behind it stood the house wherein they had engaged lodgings, whose exterior

[034-005]features are so well known to all travellers through the Froom Valley; once

[034-006]portion of a fine manorial residence, and the property and seat of a

[034-007]d’Urberville, but since its partial demolition a farmhouse.

[034-008]“Welcome to one of your ancestral mansions!” said Clare as he

[034-009]handed her down. But he regretted the pleasantry; it was too near a satire.

[034-010]On entering they found that, though they had only engaged a couple of rooms,

[034-011]the farmer had taken advantage of their proposed presence during the coming

[034-012]days to pay a New Year’s visit to some friends, leaving a woman from a

[034-013]neighbouring cottage to minister to their few wants. The absoluteness of

[034-014]possession pleased them, and they realized it as the first moment of their

[034-015]experience under their own exclusive roof-tree.

[034-017]the carriage was gone they ascended the stairs to wash their hands, the

[034-018]charwoman showing the way. On the landing Tess stopped and started.

[034-020]“Those horrid women!” she answered with a smile. “How they

[034-021]frightened me.”

[034-026]smirk of the one, so suggestive of merciless treachery; the bill-hook nose,

[034-027]large teeth, and bold eye of the other suggesting arrogance to the point of

[034-030]“I have been told by old folk that they were ladies of the

[034-031]d’Urberville family, the ancient lords of this manor,” she said,

[034-032]“Owing to their being builded into the wall they can’t be moved

[034-033]away.”

[034-035]Tess, her fine features were unquestionably traceable in these exaggerated

[034-041]“They are very much mixed.”

[034-044]such an occasion; it was what every sensible woman would show: but Tess knew

[034-121]bracelets, and ear-rings; and also some other small ornaments.

[034-124]“Are they mine?” she asked incredulously.

[034-125]“They are, certainly,” said he.

[034-126]He looked into the fire. He remembered how, when he was a lad of fifteen, his

[034-127]godmother, the Squire’s wife—the only rich person with whom he had

[034-128]ever come in contact—had pinned her faith to his success; had prophesied

[034-132]“Yet why?” he asked himself. It was but a question of vanity

[034-133]throughout; and if that were admitted into one side of the equation it should

[034-134]be admitted into the other. His wife was a d’Urberville: whom could they

[034-135]become better than her?

[034-136]Suddenly he said with enthusiasm—

[034-137]“Tess, put them on—put them on!” And he turned from the fire

[034-138]to help her.

[034-139]But as if by magic she had already donned them—necklace, ear-rings,

[034-140]bracelets, and all.

[034-141]“But the gown isn’t right, Tess,” said Clare. “It ought

[034-142]to be a low one for a set of brilliants like that.”

[034-143]“Ought it?” said Tess.

[034-144]“Yes,” said he.

[034-157]“If you were only to appear in a ball-room!” he said. “But

[034-158]no—no, dearest; I think I love you best in the wing-bonnet and

[034-161]Tess’s sense of her striking appearance had given her a flush of

[034-163]“I’ll take them off,” she said, “in case Jonathan

[034-164]should see me. They are not fit for me, are they? They must be sold, I

[034-165]suppose?”
