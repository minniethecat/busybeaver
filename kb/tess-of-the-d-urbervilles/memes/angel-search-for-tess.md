# Angel's Search for Tess

## Summary

[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] returns from Brazil to [[emminster|Emminster Vicarage]], so physically reduced that his own mother declares, "it is not [[angel-clare|Angel]] — not my son — the [[angel-clare|Angel]] who went away!" (053-035–053-036). He reads [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s final desperate letter and immediately sets out across Wessex to find her. His search takes him to [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] (where she was last known to be), to [[marlott|Marlott]] (where he discovers [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] is dead), eastward to [[kingsbere|Kingsbere]] (following the Durbeyfield family's trail), and ultimately to [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]] — a fashionable seaside resort where Mrs Durbeyfield directs him. The journey retraces [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s own earlier travels in reverse, with [[angel-clare|Angel]] now the supplicant.

## Context

[[angel-clare|Angel]] left [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]] after the wedding-night confession, departing for Brazil within days. His Brazilian venture proved disastrous: illness reduced him to "the skeleton behind the man, and almost the ghost behind the skeleton" (053-040–053-041). His farming enterprise failed.

Before returning, he received [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s impassioned letter — the one beginning "O why have you treated me so monstrously, [[angel-clare|Angel]]!" — but only by chance after considerable postal delay (053-051–053-053). Another letter, written more recently in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s "hurried scrawl," awaited him at [[emminster|Emminster]]. Its contents disturbed him deeply (053-058–053-060).

His parents, who had opposed the marriage, now extend unconditional welcome. Mrs Clare's maternal love overrides theological scruples: "What woman, indeed, among the most faithful adherents of the truth… would not throw her theology to the wind if weighed against their happiness?" (053-031–053-033). [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]], too, has softened. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s parents would now welcome [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — but [[angel-clare|Angel]] must find her first.

## Key Participants

**[[Angel Clare]]** — The returned husband. His Brazilian year has devastated him physically and matured him morally. The rigid idealism that caused him to abandon [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has been dissolved by suffering. He now understands what he lost, and his search across Wessex is both a physical journey and an act of penance. He repeatedly confronts evidence of what [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] endured alone: the hostile farmer at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], the degraded family at [[marlott|Marlott]], his father's gravestone for [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] that memorializes ancestral pride alongside present poverty.

His physical deterioration is extreme — "He matched Crivelli's dead Christus" (053-041) — and Hardy emphasizes the toll of guilt: "the mockery of events at home" (053-039–053-040) had warped him as much as the Brazilian climate.

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — Absent throughout the search, yet everywhere present. [[angel-clare|Angel]] encounters her traces: the [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] farmer's dismissive memory of her Christian name, the empty [[marlott|Marlott]] cottage where another family now lives oblivious to her history, the makeshift grave at [[kingsbere|Kingsbere]]. Each location documents another layer of her suffering during his absence. Her final letter, which [[angel-clare|Angel]] reads at [[emminster|Emminster]], sets the search in motion.

## Narrative Impact

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s search reverses the novel's spatial trajectory. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s earlier journeys — from [[marlott|Marlott]] to [[trantridge|Trantridge]], from home to [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]], from [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]] back to [[marlott|Marlott]] — were flights from one form of suffering toward uncertain hope. [[angel-clare|Angel]] now follows her path, experiencing belatedly the geography of her ordeal. At [[cross-in-hand|Cross-in-Hand]], he passes the "unholy stone" where Alec made [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] swear her strange oath (054-012–054-014). At [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], he learns she never used the name "Mrs Clare" — "her dignified sense of their total severance" (054-025–054-026). At the [[marlott|Marlott]] cottage, new residents live "as if the homestead had never passed its primal time in conjunction with the histories of others" (054-047–054-048). At the churchyard, he reads the ironically magnificent epitaph on [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s unpaid headstone (054-066–054-077).

Each discovery compounds [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s guilt. The search transforms him from judge to penitent. By the time he reaches [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]], he has absorbed the moral education his Brazilian year could not provide — the knowledge of what his fastidiousness cost the woman he loves.

The search also completes the Clare family's arc. Reverend and Mrs Clare, who once dismissed [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as an unsuitable match, now wait anxiously for news of her. Their transformation mirrors [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s: experience has eroded dogma.

## Key Passages

[[mrs-clare|Angel's mother]] reacts to his ravaged appearance:

> "O, it is not Angel — not my son — the Angel who went away!" she cried in all the irony of sorrow. (053-035–053-036)

Hardy describes the depth of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s physical ruin:

> You could see the skeleton behind the man, and almost the ghost behind the skeleton. He matched Crivelli's dead Christus. (053-040–053-041)

At [[cross-in-hand|Cross-in-Hand]], [[angel-clare|Angel]] passes the stone of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s compelled oath:

> He ascended to the untoward solitude of Cross-in-Hand, the unholy stone whereon Tess had been compelled by Alec d'Urberville, in his whim of reformation, to swear the strange oath that she would never wilfully tempt him again. (054-012–054-014)

At [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], he learns [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] never used his name:

> His name she had obviously never used during their separation, and her dignified sense of their total severance was shown not much less by this abstention than by the hardships she had chosen to undergo (of which he now learnt for the first time) rather than apply to his father for more funds. (054-025–054-028)

At the [[marlott|Marlott]] cottage, Hardy reflects on how the new residents erase [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s history:

> They walked about the garden paths with thoughts of their own concerns entirely uppermost, bringing their actions at every moment in jarring collision with the dim ghosts behind them. (054-050–054-052)

[[angel-clare|Angel]] reads [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s gravestone with its grandiose inscription:

> In memory of John Durbeyfield, rightly d'Urberville, of the once powerful family of that Name, and Direct Descendant through an illustrious Line from Sir Pagan d'Urberville, one of the Knights of the Conqueror. Died March 10th, 18— How are the Mighty Fallen. (054-066–054-070)

The sexton reveals the headstone is unpaid for — "for all the flourish wrote upon en, is not paid for" (054-077).

## Analysis

### The Journey as Penance

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s search is structured as pilgrimage. He moves from site to site of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s suffering, each stop providing painful instruction. His parents' home at [[emminster|Emminster]] — the origin of the narrow theology that shaped his moral fastidiousness — is the starting point. [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] labored under [[farmer-groby|Farmer Groby]]'s tyranny, is his first destination. [[marlott|Marlott]], the scene of her childhood and the place from which she was driven by poverty, is next. [[kingsbere|Kingsbere]], the burial ground of the d'Urbervilles, marks the final degradation of her family.

Each stage of the journey strips away a layer of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s former self-assurance. By the time he reaches [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]], the confident young man who lectured [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on "the pure woman" has been wholly dismantled.

### Ghosts and Traces

The [[marlott|Marlott]] cottage scene is among the novel's most direct meditations on historical erasure. The new residents take "as much interest in their own doings as if the homestead had never passed its primal time in conjunction with the histories of others, beside which the histories of these were but as a tale told by an idiot" (054-047–054-049). [[angel-clare|Angel]] perceives [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ghost everywhere — in the field where he first saw her dance, in the churchyard where her father's unpaid stone mocks ancestral pretension — but she herself has vanished.

### Ironies of Return

The journey is dense with ironies. [[cross-in-hand|Cross-in-Hand]], where Alec — then a preacher — compelled [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to swear, is now an empty landmark [[angel-clare|Angel]] passes without stopping. The farmer who tormented [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is "smooth-tongued to Clare" (054-035), treating the gentleman husband with a deference he denied the working woman. [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s headstone — with its comically grandiose claim to Norman lineage — is revealed to be unpaid for, the epitaph a lie in more senses than one.

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s search reconstitutes the geography of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s suffering as the geography of his own education. But the education comes too late: when he finally traces her to [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]], he will learn that she has already been "won back" by Alec.
