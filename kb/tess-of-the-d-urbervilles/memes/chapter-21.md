[021-001]There was a great stir in the milk-house just after breakfast. The churn

[021-002]revolved as usual, but the butter would not come. Whenever this happened the

[021-003]dairy was paralyzed. Squish, squash echoed the milk in the great cylinder, but

[021-004]never arose the sound they waited for.

[021-005]Dairyman Crick and his wife, the milkmaids Tess, Marian, Retty Priddle, Izz

[021-006]Huett, and the married ones from the cottages; also Mr Clare, Jonathan Kail,

[021-007]old Deborah, and the rest, stood gazing hopelessly at the churn; and the boy

[021-008]who kept the horse going outside put on moon-like eyes to show his sense of the

[021-009]situation. Even the melancholy horse himself seemed to look in at the window in

[021-010]inquiring despair at each walk round.

[021-011]“’Tis years since I went to Conjuror Trendle’s son in

[021-012]Egdon—years!” said the dairyman bitterly. “And he was nothing

[021-013]to what his father had been. I have said fifty times, if I have said once, that

[021-014]I *don’t* believe in en; though ’a do cast folks’ waters

[021-015]very true. But I shall have to go to ’n if he’s alive. O yes, I

[021-016]shall have to go to ’n, if this sort of thing continnys!”

[021-017]Even Mr Clare began to feel tragical at the dairyman’s desperation.

[021-018]“Conjuror Fall, t’other side of Casterbridge, that they used to

[021-019]call ‘Wide-O’, was a very good man when I was a boy,” said

[021-020]Jonathan Kail. “But he’s rotten as touchwood by now.”

[021-021]“My grandfather used to go to Conjuror Mynterne, out at Owlscombe, and a

[021-022]clever man a’ were, so I’ve heard grandf’er say,”

[021-023]continued Mr Crick. “But there’s no such genuine folk about

[021-024]nowadays!”

[021-025]Mrs Crick’s mind kept nearer to the matter in hand.

[021-026]“Perhaps somebody in the house is in love,” she said tentatively.

[021-027]“I’ve heard tell in my younger days that that will cause it. Why,

[021-028]Crick—that maid we had years ago, do ye mind, and how the butter

[021-029]didn’t come then—”

[021-030]“Ah yes, yes!—but that isn’t the rights o’t. It had

[021-031]nothing to do with the love-making. I can mind all about it—’twas

[021-032]the damage to the churn.”

[021-033]He turned to Clare.

[021-034]“Jack Dollop, a ’hore’s-bird of a fellow we had here as

[021-035]milker at one time, sir, courted a young woman over at Mellstock, and deceived

[021-036]her as he had deceived many afore. But he had another sort o’ woman to

[021-037]reckon wi’ this time, and it was not the girl herself. One Holy Thursday

[021-038]of all days in the almanack, we was here as we mid be now, only there was no

[021-039]churning in hand, when we zid the girl’s mother coming up to the door,

[021-040]wi’ a great brass-mounted umbrella in her hand that would ha’

[021-041]felled an ox, and saying ‘Do Jack Dollop work here?—because I want

[021-042]him! I have a big bone to pick with he, I can assure ’n!’ And some

[021-043]way behind her mother walked Jack’s young woman, crying bitterly into her

[021-044]handkercher. ‘O Lard, here’s a time!’ said Jack, looking out

[021-045]o’ winder at ’em. ‘She’ll murder me! Where shall I

[021-046]get—where shall I—? Don’t tell her where I be!’ And

[021-047]with that he scrambled into the churn through the trap-door, and shut himself

[021-048]inside, just as the young woman’s mother busted into the milk-house.

[021-049]‘The villain—where is he?’ says she. ‘I’ll claw

[021-050]his face for’n, let me only catch him!’ Well, she hunted about

[021-051]everywhere, ballyragging Jack by side and by seam, Jack lying a’most

[021-052]stifled inside the churn, and the poor maid—or young woman

[021-053]rather—standing at the door crying her eyes out. I shall never forget it,

[021-054]never! ’Twould have melted a marble stone! But she couldn’t find

[021-055]him nowhere at all.”

[021-056]The dairyman paused, and one or two words of comment came from the listeners.

[021-057]Dairyman Crick’s stories often seemed to be ended when they were not

[021-058]really so, and strangers were betrayed into premature interjections of

[021-059]finality; though old friends knew better. The narrator went on—

[021-060]“Well, how the old woman should have had the wit to guess it I could

[021-061]never tell, but she found out that he was inside that there churn. Without

[021-062]saying a word she took hold of the winch (it was turned by handpower then), and

[021-063]round she swung him, and Jack began to flop about inside. ‘O Lard! stop

[021-064]the churn! let me out!’ says he, popping out his head. ‘I shall be

[021-065]churned into a pummy!’ (He was a cowardly chap in his heart, as such men

[021-066]mostly be). ‘Not till ye make amends for ravaging her virgin

[021-067]innocence!’ says the old woman. ‘Stop the churn you old

[021-068]witch!’ screams he. ‘You call me old witch, do ye, you

[021-069]deceiver!’ says she, ‘when ye ought to ha’ been calling me

[021-070]mother-law these last five months!’ And on went the churn, and

[021-071]Jack’s bones rattled round again. Well, none of us ventured to interfere;

[021-072]and at last ’a promised to make it right wi’ her.

[021-073]‘Yes—I’ll be as good as my word!’ he said. And so it

[021-074]ended that day.”

[021-075]While the listeners were smiling their comments there was a quick movement

[021-076]behind their backs, and they looked round. Tess, pale-faced, had gone to the

[021-077]door.

[021-078]“How warm ’tis to-day!” she said, almost inaudibly.

[021-079]It was warm, and none of them connected her withdrawal with the reminiscences

[021-080]of the dairyman. He went forward and opened the door for her, saying with

[021-081]tender raillery—

[021-082]“Why, maidy” (he frequently, with unconscious irony, gave her this

[021-083]pet name), “the prettiest milker I’ve got in my dairy; you

[021-084]mustn’t get so fagged as this at the first breath of summer weather, or

[021-085]we shall be finely put to for want of ’ee by dog-days, shan’t we,

[021-086]Mr Clare?”

[021-087]“I was faint—and—I think I am better out o’

[021-088]doors,” she said mechanically; and disappeared outside.

[021-089]Fortunately for her the milk in the revolving churn at that moment changed its

[021-090]squashing for a decided flick-flack.

[021-091]“’Tis coming!” cried Mrs Crick, and the attention of all was

[021-092]called off from Tess.

[021-093]That fair sufferer soon recovered herself externally; but she remained much

[021-094]depressed all the afternoon. When the evening milking was done she did not care

[021-095]to be with the rest of them, and went out of doors, wandering along she knew

[021-096]not whither. She was wretched—O so wretched—at the perception that

[021-097]to her companions the dairyman’s story had been rather a humorous

[021-098]narration than otherwise; none of them but herself seemed to see the sorrow of

[021-099]it; to a certainty, not one knew how cruelly it touched the tender place in her

[021-100]experience. The evening sun was now ugly to her, like a great inflamed wound in

[021-101]the sky. Only a solitary cracked-voice reed-sparrow greeted her from the bushes

[021-102]by the river, in a sad, machine-made tone, resembling that of a past friend

[021-103]whose friendship she had outworn.

[021-104]In these long June days the milkmaids, and, indeed, most of the household, went

[021-105]to bed at sunset or sooner, the morning work before milking being so early and

[021-106]heavy at a time of full pails. Tess usually accompanied her fellows upstairs.

[021-107]To-night, however, she was the first to go to their common chamber; and she had

[021-108]dozed when the other girls came in. She saw them undressing in the orange light

[021-109]of the vanished sun, which flushed their forms with its colour; she dozed

[021-110]again, but she was reawakened by their voices, and quietly turned her eyes

[021-111]towards them.

[021-112]Neither of her three chamber-companions had got into bed. They were standing in

[021-113]a group, in their nightgowns, barefooted, at the window, the last red rays of

[021-114]the west still warming their faces and necks and the walls around them. All

[021-115]were watching somebody in the garden with deep interest, their three faces

[021-116]close together: a jovial and round one, a pale one with dark hair, and a fair

[021-117]one whose tresses were auburn.

[021-118]“Don’t push! You can see as well as I,” said Retty, the

[021-119]auburn-haired and youngest girl, without removing her eyes from the window.

[021-120]“’Tis no use for you to be in love with him any more than me, Retty

[021-121]Priddle,” said jolly-faced Marian, the eldest, slily. “His thoughts

[021-122]be of other cheeks than thine!”

[021-123]Retty Priddle still looked, and the others looked again.

[021-124]“There he is again!” cried Izz Huett, the pale girl with dark damp

[021-125]hair and keenly cut lips.

[021-126]“You needn’t say anything, Izz,” answered Retty. “For I

[021-127]zid you kissing his shade.”

[021-128]*”What* did you see her doing?” asked Marian.

[021-129]“Why—he was standing over the whey-tub to let off the whey, and the

[021-130]shade of his face came upon the wall behind, close to Izz, who was standing

[021-131]there filling a vat. She put her mouth against the wall and kissed the shade of

[021-132]his mouth; I zid her, though he didn’t.”

[021-133]“O Izz Huett!” said Marian.

[021-134]A rosy spot came into the middle of Izz Huett’s cheek.

[021-135]“Well, there was no harm in it,” she declared, with attempted

[021-136]coolness. “And if I be in love wi’en, so is Retty, too; and so be

[021-137]you, Marian, come to that.”

[021-138]Marian’s full face could not blush past its chronic pinkness.

[021-139]“I!” she said. “What a tale! Ah, there he is again! Dear

[021-140]eyes—dear face—dear Mr Clare!”

[021-141]“There—you’ve owned it!”

[021-142]“So have you—so have we all,” said Marian, with the dry

[021-143]frankness of complete indifference to opinion. “It is silly to pretend

[021-144]otherwise amongst ourselves, though we need not own it to other folks. I would

[021-145]just marry ’n to-morrow!”

[021-146]“So would I—and more,” murmured Izz Huett.

[021-147]“And I too,” whispered the more timid Retty.

[021-148]The listener grew warm.

[021-149]“We can’t all marry him,” said Izz.

[021-150]“We shan’t, either of us; which is worse still,” said the

[021-151]eldest. “There he is again!”

[021-152]They all three blew him a silent kiss.

[021-153]“Why?” asked Retty quickly.

[021-154]“Because he likes Tess Durbeyfield best,” said Marian, lowering her

[021-155]voice. “I have watched him every day, and have found it out.”

[021-156]There was a reflective silence.

[021-157]“But she don’t care anything for ’n?” at length

[021-158]breathed Retty.

[021-159]“Well—I sometimes think that too.”

[021-160]“But how silly all this is!” said Izz Huett impatiently. “Of

[021-161]course he won’t marry any one of us, or Tess either—a

[021-162]gentleman’s son, who’s going to be a great landowner and farmer

[021-163]abroad! More likely to ask us to come wi’en as farm-hands at so much a

[021-164]year!”

[021-165]One sighed, and another sighed, and Marian’s plump figure sighed biggest

[021-166]of all. Somebody in bed hard by sighed too. Tears came into the eyes of Retty

[021-167]Priddle, the pretty red-haired youngest—the last bud of the Paridelles,

[021-168]so important in the county annals. They watched silently a little longer, their

[021-169]three faces still close together as before, and the triple hues of their hair

[021-170]mingling. But the unconscious Mr Clare had gone indoors, and they saw him no

[021-171]more; and, the shades beginning to deepen, they crept into their beds. In a few

[021-172]minutes they heard him ascend the ladder to his own room. Marian was soon

[021-173]snoring, but Izz did not drop into forgetfulness for a long time. Retty Priddle

[021-174]cried herself to sleep.

[021-175]The deeper-passioned Tess was very far from sleeping even then. This

[021-176]conversation was another of the bitter pills she had been obliged to swallow

[021-177]that day. Scarce the least feeling of jealousy arose in her breast. For that

[021-178]matter she knew herself to have the preference. Being more finely formed,

[021-179]better educated, and, though the youngest except Retty, more woman than either,

[021-180]she perceived that only the slightest ordinary care was necessary for holding

[021-181]her own in Angel Clare’s heart against these her candid friends. But the

[021-182]grave question was, ought she to do this? There was, to be sure, hardly a ghost

[021-183]of a chance for either of them, in a serious sense; but there was, or had been,

[021-184]a chance of one or the other inspiring him with a passing fancy for her, and

[021-185]enjoying the pleasure of his attentions while he stayed here. Such unequal

[021-186]attachments had led to marriage; and she had heard from Mrs Crick that Mr Clare

[021-187]had one day asked, in a laughing way, what would be the use of his marrying a

[021-188]fine lady, and all the while ten thousand acres of Colonial pasture to feed,

[021-189]and cattle to rear, and corn to reap. A farm-woman would be the only sensible

[021-190]kind of wife for him. But whether Mr Clare had spoken seriously or not, why

[021-191]should she, who could never conscientiously allow any man to marry her now, and

[021-192]who had religiously determined that she never would be tempted to do so, draw

[021-193]off Mr Clare’s attention from other women, for the brief happiness of

[021-194]sunning herself in his eyes while he remained at Talbothays?
