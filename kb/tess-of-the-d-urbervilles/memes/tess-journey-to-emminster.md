# Tess's Journey to Emminster

## Summary

Driven by [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s prolonged silence and stung by [[Izz Huett]]'s revelation that [[angel-clare|Angel]] had asked [[izz-huett|Izz]] to accompany him to Brazil, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] undertakes a fifteen-mile winter walk on a Sunday from [[Flintcomb-Ash]] to [[Emminster Vicarage]], hoping to ask [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s parents for news of her husband. She rises at four in the morning, dressed in her best clothes (044-031–044-044). But the pilgrimage ends in humiliation: arriving during church service, she encounters [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s brothers [[Cuthbert Clare]] and [[Felix Clare]] on the road, overhears them disparaging [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s "ill-considered marriage" to "a dairymaid, or whatever she may be" (044-169–044-170), and watches them retrieve her walking boots from the hedge where she had hidden them, mistaking them for a tramp's discards (044-186–044-193). She abandons her mission, her courage broken. On the return journey, she stops at [[evershead|Evershead]] and discovers that the fiery preacher in the barn is none other than [[Alec d'Urberville]], now converted to an antinomian form of [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]].

## Context

A year has passed since [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s marriage. [[angel-clare|Angel]] has sent no word — no address, no letter — despite having "distinctly implied that he would at least let her know of the locality to which he had journeyed" (044-016–044-018). [[izz-huett|Izz]]'s recent confession that [[angel-clare|Angel]], in his despair immediately after the separation, had impulsively asked her to go to Brazil with him (an offer he almost immediately withdrew) has shaken [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s confidence that her husband still thinks of her.

The Vicarage represents [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s last legitimate channel to [[angel-clare|Angel]]. He had told her she could write to him through his parents, and to write to them directly if in difficulty (044-003–044-004). But [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s "independent character of desiring nothing by way of favour or pity to which she was not entitled on a fair consideration of her deserts" (044-009–044-010) has kept her from reaching out. Now, desperation overcomes pride.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] embarks on this journey with a mixture of hope and dread. She dresses carefully — a "soft gray woollen gown, with white crape quilling against the pink skin of her face and neck, and a black velvet jacket and hat" (044-042–044-044) — preserving "sufficient draperies from the wreck of her then full wardrobe" (044-040–044-041). Her companions [[Marian]] and [[izz-huett|Izz]] urge her to look her best, but [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] knows the Clares' "austere and Calvinistic tenets" (044-038–044-039) may make appearance irrelevant or even suspect.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s collapse of courage at the critical moment — "the greatest misfortune of her life was this feminine loss of courage at the last and critical moment through her estimating her father-in-law by his sons" (044-218–044-220) — is identified by Hardy's narrator as the decisive failure. The narrator makes clear that old Mr and Mrs Clare would have received her warmly: "Their hearts went out of them at a bound towards extreme cases" (044-221–044-222).

**[[Cuthbert Clare]] and [[Felix Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s brothers appear only briefly, but their effect is devastating. Their conversation reveals them as priggish, class-conscious, and entirely unaware that the woman they are overtaking on the road is their sister-in-law. Cuthbert's remark — "I never see that nice girl without more and more regretting his precipitancy in throwing himself away upon a dairymaid, or whatever she may be" (044-168–044-170) — is the novel's sharpest illustration of the class prejudice that structures even "enlightened" Victorian society.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec's reappearance as a converted preacher is the chapter's stunning reversal. The man who seduced [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] now stands on sacks of corn, preaching "justification by faith, as expounded in the theology of St Paul" (044-261–044-262). His conversion has been brought about, he claims, by the influence of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s own father — "a certain clergyman, whom he had at first grossly insulted; but whose parting words had sunk into his heart" (044-276–044-278). The irony is layered: [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s father has saved the soul of the man who destroyed [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s wife.

## Narrative Impact

This chapter is one of the novel's most tightly structured set-pieces, weaving together the novel's central themes — class, gender, religion, and fate — in a single day's journey. The walk from [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] to [[emminster|Emminster]] is a pilgrimage in the medieval sense: a journey undertaken in hope of grace. But the grace [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] seeks — news of her husband, acknowledgment from his family — is denied, and what she finds instead is humiliation and the return of her persecutor in the guise of a savior.

The chapter's structure is symmetrical: the outbound journey is hopeful ([[izz-huett|Izz]] and [[marian|Marian]] dress her like a bride); the destination crushes her; the return journey brings Alec's re-entry. The boots hidden in the hedge — practical footwear [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] removes before approaching the Vicarage, which [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s brothers then retrieve for [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]] as an act of charity — become the chapter's central symbol of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s invisibility. She is literally under their feet, and they do not see her.

## Key Passages

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s departure at dawn, dressed by her companions:

> With a final tug and touch here, and a slight brush there, they let her go; and she was absorbed into the pearly air of the fore-dawn. They heard her footsteps tap along the hard road as she stepped out to her full pace. (044-054–044-056)

The landscape of the journey, contrasting upland and valley:

> Instead of the colourless air of the uplands, the atmosphere down there was a deep blue. Instead of the great enclosures of a hundred acres in which she was now accustomed to toil, there were little fields below her of less than half-a-dozen acres... Yet it was in that vale that her sorrow had taken shape, and she did not love it as formerly. Beauty to her, as to all who have felt, lay not in the thing, but in what the thing symbolized. (044-069–044-076)

The brothers' conversation, overheard by the woman they discuss:

> "Ah! poor Angel, poor Angel! I never see that nice girl without more and more regretting his precipitancy in throwing himself away upon a dairymaid, or whatever she may be. It is a queer business, apparently." (044-168–044-170)

The brothers finding [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s hidden boots:

> "Here's a pair of old boots," he said. "Thrown away, I suppose, by some tramp or other."
> "Some imposter who wished to come into the town barefoot, perhaps, and so excite our sympathies," said Miss Chant. (044-186–044-189)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s collapse:

> Tears, blinding tears, were running down her face. She knew that it was all sentiment, all baseless impressibility, which had caused her to read the scene as her own condemnation; nevertheless she could not get over it. (044-197–044-200)

The narrator's judgment on [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s failure:

> She went her way without knowing that the greatest misfortune of her life was this feminine loss of courage at the last and critical moment through her estimating her father-in-law by his sons. (044-217–044-220)

Alec's voice, recognized:

> But more startling to Tess than the doctrine had been the voice, which, impossible as it seemed, was precisely that of Alec d'Urberville. (044-280–044-282)

## Analysis

### Pilgrimage Inverted

The walk to [[emminster|Emminster]] follows the formal pattern of a pilgrimage: early rising, careful dress, a long journey on foot, a sacred destination. But every element is inverted. The sacred destination — the Vicarage — offers no sanctuary. The clergyman's sons, who should embody Christian charity, dispense casual cruelty. The sought-for grace — acknowledgment, connection — is withheld. Hardy subverts the pilgrimage form to expose the gap between Christian ideals and Christian practice.

### The Boots as Symbol

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s boots — practical, worn, hidden in a hedge — become the chapter's most eloquent symbol. She removes them to approach the Vicarage in her "pretty thin ones of patent leather" (044-101–044-102), performing respectability at the cost of comfort. The brothers find the boots and give them to [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]] for "some poor person" (044-191), performing charity while blind to the real poverty before them. The boots are [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] herself: useful, discarded, appropriated by those who cannot recognize her.

### Alec's Conversion

Alec's reappearance as a preacher is the chapter's most shocking twist. His conversion is apparently genuine — brought about by the "parting words" of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s father (044-277–044-278) — but Hardy's presentation is deeply ambiguous. The text that Alec preaches, Galatians 3:1 ("O foolish Galatians, who hath bewitched you"), is a warning against abandoning true faith for legalism. That Alec should be the vehicle for this message, while [[angel-clare|Angel]] — the genuine moral seeker — languishes in Brazil, is an irony that questions the very foundations of moral judgment. As the chapter closes, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has lost her husband to conventional morality and found her seducer in the pulpit.

### The Failure of Courage

Hardy's narrator identifies [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s "feminine loss of courage" as "the greatest misfortune of her life" (044-218–044-219). This is a complex judgment. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s retreat is motivated by her accurate perception of the brothers' hostility — but it is also a failure to trust that the father might differ from the sons. The narrator insists that old [[reverend-clare|Mr Clare]]'s "hearts went out of them at a bound towards extreme cases" (044-221–044-222). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s tragedy, in this reading, is not inevitable but contingent — a failure of nerve at the decisive instant, born of experiences that have taught her to expect rejection.
