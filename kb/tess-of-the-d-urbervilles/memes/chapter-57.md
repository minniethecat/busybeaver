[057-001]Meanwhile Angel Clare had walked automatically along the way by which he had

[057-002]come, and, entering his hotel, sat down over the breakfast, staring at

[057-003]nothingness. He went on eating and drinking unconsciously till on a sudden he

[057-004]demanded his bill; having paid which, he took his dressing-bag in his hand, the

[057-005]only luggage he had brought with him, and went out.

[057-006]At the moment of his departure a telegram was handed to him—a few words

[057-007]from his mother, stating that they were glad to know his address, and informing

[057-008]him that his brother Cuthbert had proposed to and been accepted by Mercy Chant.

[057-009]Clare crumpled up the paper and followed the route to the station; reaching it,

[057-010]he found that there would be no train leaving for an hour and more. He sat down

[057-011]to wait, and having waited a quarter of an hour felt that he could wait there

[057-012]no longer. Broken in heart and numbed, he had nothing to hurry for; but he

[057-013]wished to get out of a town which had been the scene of such an experience, and

[057-014]turned to walk to the first station onward, and let the train pick him up

[057-015]there.

[057-016]The highway that he followed was open, and at a little distance dipped into a

[057-017]valley, across which it could be seen running from edge to edge. He had

[057-018]traversed the greater part of this depression, and was climbing the western

[057-019]acclivity when, pausing for breath, he unconsciously looked back. Why he did so

[057-020]he could not say, but something seemed to impel him to the act. The tape-like

[057-021]surface of the road diminished in his rear as far as he could see, and as he

[057-022]gazed a moving spot intruded on the white vacuity of its perspective.

[057-023]It was a human figure running. Clare waited, with a dim sense that somebody was

[057-024]trying to overtake him.

[057-025]The form descending the incline was a woman’s, yet so entirely was his

[057-026]mind blinded to the idea of his wife’s following him that even when she

[057-027]came nearer he did not recognize her under the totally changed attire in which

[057-028]he now beheld her. It was not till she was quite close that he could believe

[057-029]her to be Tess.

[057-030]“I saw you—turn away from the station—just before I got

[057-031]there—and I have been following you all this way!”

[057-032]She was so pale, so breathless, so quivering in every muscle, that he did not

[057-033]ask her a single question, but seizing her hand, and pulling it within his arm,

[057-034]he led her along. To avoid meeting any possible wayfarers he left the high road

[057-035]and took a footpath under some fir-trees. When they were deep among the moaning

[057-036]boughs he stopped and looked at her inquiringly.

[057-037]“Angel,” she said, as if waiting for this, “do you know what

[057-038]I have been running after you for? To tell you that I have killed him!” A

[057-039]pitiful white smile lit her face as she spoke.

[057-040]“What!” said he, thinking from the strangeness of her manner that

[057-041]she was in some delirium.

[057-042]“I have done it—I don’t know how,” she continued.

[057-043]“Still, I owed it to you, and to myself, Angel. I feared long ago, when I

[057-044]struck him on the mouth with my glove, that I might do it some day for the trap

[057-045]he set for me in my simple youth, and his wrong to you through me. He has come

[057-046]between us and ruined us, and now he can never do it any more. I never loved

[057-047]him at all, Angel, as I loved you. You know it, don’t you? You believe

[057-048]it? You didn’t come back to me, and I was obliged to go back to him. Why

[057-049]did you go away—why did you—when I loved you so? I can’t

[057-050]think why you did it. But I don’t blame you; only, Angel, will you

[057-051]forgive me my sin against you, now I have killed him? I thought as I ran along

[057-052]that you would be sure to forgive me now I have done that. It came to me as a

[057-053]shining light that I should get you back that way. I could not bear the loss of

[057-054]you any longer—you don’t know how entirely I was unable to bear

[057-055]your not loving me! Say you do now, dear, dear husband; say you do, now I have

[057-056]killed him!”

[057-057]“I do love you, Tess—O, I do—it is all come back!” he

[057-058]said, tightening his arms round her with fervid pressure. “But how do you

[057-059]mean—you have killed him?”

[057-060]“I mean that I have,” she murmured in a reverie.

[057-061]“What, bodily? Is he dead?”

[057-062]“Yes. He heard me crying about you, and he bitterly taunted me; and

[057-063]called you by a foul name; and then I did it. My heart could not bear it. He

[057-064]had nagged me about you before. And then I dressed myself and came away to find

[057-065]you.”

[057-066]By degrees he was inclined to believe that she had faintly attempted, at least,

[057-067]what she said she had done; and his horror at her impulse was mixed with

[057-068]amazement at the strength of her affection for himself, and at the strangeness

[057-069]of its quality, which had apparently extinguished her moral sense altogether.

[057-070]Unable to realize the gravity of her conduct, she seemed at last content; and

[057-071]he looked at her as she lay upon his shoulder, weeping with happiness, and

[057-072]wondered what obscure strain in the d’Urberville blood had led to this

[057-073]aberration—if it were an aberration. There momentarily flashed through

[057-074]his mind that the family tradition of the coach and murder might have arisen

[057-075]because the d’Urbervilles had been known to do these things. As well as

[057-076]his confused and excited ideas could reason, he supposed that in the moment of

[057-077]mad grief of which she spoke, her mind had lost its balance, and plunged her

[057-078]into this abyss.

[057-079]It was very terrible if true; if a temporary hallucination, sad. But, anyhow,

[057-080]here was this deserted wife of his, this passionately-fond woman, clinging to

[057-081]him without a suspicion that he would be anything to her but a protector. He

[057-082]saw that for him to be otherwise was not, in her mind, within the region of the

[057-083]possible. Tenderness was absolutely dominant in Clare at last. He kissed her

[057-084]endlessly with his white lips, and held her hand, and said—

[057-085]“I will not desert you! I will protect you by every means in my power,

[057-086]dearest love, whatever you may have done or not have done!”

[057-087]They then walked on under the trees, Tess turning her head every now and then

[057-088]to look at him. Worn and unhandsome as he had become, it was plain that she did

[057-089]not discern the least fault in his appearance. To her he was, as of old, all

[057-090]that was perfection, personally and mentally. He was still her Antinous, her

[057-091]Apollo even; his sickly face was beautiful as the morning to her affectionate

[057-092]regard on this day no less than when she first beheld him; for was it not the

[057-093]face of the one man on earth who had loved her purely, and who had believed in

[057-094]her as pure!

[057-095]With an instinct as to possibilities, he did not now, as he had intended, make

[057-096]for the first station beyond the town, but plunged still farther under the

[057-097]firs, which here abounded for miles. Each clasping the other round the waist

[057-098]they promenaded over the dry bed of fir-needles, thrown into a vague

[057-099]intoxicating atmosphere at the consciousness of being together at last, with no

[057-100]living soul between them; ignoring that there was a corpse. Thus they proceeded

[057-101]for several miles till Tess, arousing herself, looked about her, and said,

[057-102]timidly—

[057-103]“Are we going anywhere in particular?”

[057-104]“I don’t know, dearest. Why?”

[057-105]“I don’t know.”

[057-106]“Well, we might walk a few miles further, and when it is evening find

[057-107]lodgings somewhere or other—in a lonely cottage, perhaps. Can you walk

[057-108]well, Tessy?”

[057-109]“O yes! I could walk for ever and ever with your arm round me!”

[057-110]Upon the whole it seemed a good thing to do. Thereupon they quickened their

[057-111]pace, avoiding high roads, and following obscure paths tending more or less

[057-112]northward. But there was an unpractical vagueness in their movements throughout

[057-113]the day; neither one of them seemed to consider any question of effectual

[057-114]escape, disguise, or long concealment. Their every idea was temporary and

[057-115]unforefending, like the plans of two children.

[057-116]At mid-day they drew near to a roadside inn, and Tess would have entered it

[057-117]with him to get something to eat, but he persuaded her to remain among the

[057-118]trees and bushes of this half-woodland, half-moorland part of the country till

[057-119]he should come back. Her clothes were of recent fashion; even the ivory-handled

[057-120]parasol that she carried was of a shape unknown in the retired spot to which

[057-121]they had now wandered; and the cut of such articles would have attracted

[057-122]attention in the settle of a tavern. He soon returned, with food enough for

[057-123]half-a-dozen people and two bottles of wine—enough to last them for a day

[057-124]or more, should any emergency arise.

[057-125]They sat down upon some dead boughs and shared their meal. Between one and two

[057-126]o’clock they packed up the remainder and went on again.

[057-127]“I feel strong enough to walk any distance,” said she.

[057-128]“I think we may as well steer in a general way towards the interior of

[057-129]the country, where we can hide for a time, and are less likely to be looked for

[057-130]than anywhere near the coast,” Clare remarked. “Later on, when they

[057-131]have forgotten us, we can make for some port.”

[057-132]She made no reply to this beyond that of grasping him more tightly, and

[057-133]straight inland they went. Though the season was an English May, the weather

[057-134]was serenely bright, and during the afternoon it was quite warm. Through the

[057-135]latter miles of their walk their footpath had taken them into the depths of the

[057-136]New Forest, and towards evening, turning the corner of a lane, they perceived

[057-137]behind a brook and bridge a large board on which was painted in white letters,

[057-138]“This desirable Mansion to be Let Furnished”; particulars

[057-139]following, with directions to apply to some London agents. Passing through the

[057-140]gate they could see the house, an old brick building of regular design and

[057-141]large accommodation.

[057-142]“I know it,” said Clare. “It is Bramshurst Court. You can see

[057-143]that it is shut up, and grass is growing on the drive.”

[057-144]“Some of the windows are open,” said Tess.

[057-145]“Just to air the rooms, I suppose.”

[057-146]“All these rooms empty, and we without a roof to our heads!”

[057-147]“You are getting tired, my Tess!” he said. “We’ll stop

[057-148]soon.” And kissing her sad mouth, he again led her onwards.

[057-149]He was growing weary likewise, for they had wandered a dozen or fifteen miles,

[057-150]and it became necessary to consider what they should do for rest. They looked

[057-151]from afar at isolated cottages and little inns, and were inclined to approach

[057-152]one of the latter, when their hearts failed them, and they sheered off. At

[057-153]length their gait dragged, and they stood still.

[057-154]“Could we sleep under the trees?” she asked.

[057-155]He thought the season insufficiently advanced.

[057-156]“I have been thinking of that empty mansion we passed,” he said.

[057-157]“Let us go back towards it again.”

[057-158]They retraced their steps, but it was half an hour before they stood without

[057-159]the entrance-gate as earlier. He then requested her to stay where she was,

[057-160]whilst he went to see who was within.

[057-161]She sat down among the bushes within the gate, and Clare crept towards the

[057-162]house. His absence lasted some considerable time, and when he returned Tess was

[057-163]wildly anxious, not for herself, but for him. He had found out from a boy that

[057-164]there was only an old woman in charge as caretaker, and she only came there on

[057-165]fine days, from the hamlet near, to open and shut the windows. She would come

[057-166]to shut them at sunset. “Now, we can get in through one of the lower

[057-167]windows, and rest there,” said he.

[057-168]Under his escort she went tardily forward to the main front, whose shuttered

[057-169]windows, like sightless eyeballs, excluded the possibility of watchers. The

[057-170]door was reached a few steps further, and one of the windows beside it was

[057-171]open. Clare clambered in, and pulled Tess in after him.

[057-172]Except the hall, the rooms were all in darkness, and they ascended the

[057-173]staircase. Up here also the shutters were tightly closed, the ventilation being

[057-174]perfunctorily done, for this day at least, by opening the hall-window in front

[057-175]and an upper window behind. Clare unlatched the door of a large chamber, felt

[057-176]his way across it, and parted the shutters to the width of two or three inches.

[057-177]A shaft of dazzling sunlight glanced into the room, revealing heavy,

[057-178]old-fashioned furniture, crimson damask hangings, and an enormous four-post

[057-179]bedstead, along the head of which were carved running figures, apparently

[057-180]Atalanta’s race.

[057-181]“Rest at last!” said he, setting down his bag and the parcel of

[057-182]viands.

[057-183]They remained in great quietness till the caretaker should have come to shut

[057-184]the windows: as a precaution, putting themselves in total darkness by barring

[057-185]the shutters as before, lest the woman should open the door of their chamber

[057-186]for any casual reason. Between six and seven o’clock she came, but did

[057-187]not approach the wing they were in. They heard her close the windows, fasten

[057-188]them, lock the door, and go away. Then Clare again stole a chink of light from

[057-189]the window, and they shared another meal, till by-and-by they were enveloped in

[057-190]the shades of night which they had no candle to disperse.
