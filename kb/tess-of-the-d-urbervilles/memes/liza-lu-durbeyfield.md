# Liza-Lu Durbeyfield

## Role in the Novel

Liza-Lu Durbeyfield, whose full name is Eliza-Louisa, is [[Tess Durbeyfield]]'s younger sister. (003-172) She appears in the early domestic scenes in [[Marlott]] and returns significantly at the novel's conclusion, when [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], facing execution, asks [[Angel Clare]] to marry Liza-Lu as a final act of atonement and continuity.

Liza-Lu is portrayed as a gentler, less worldly version of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — "a spiritualized image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]" — representing what [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] might have been in a kinder world.

## Biography

Liza-Lu is the second eldest daughter in the Durbeyfield household, twelve and a half years old at the novel's opening. (003-172) She grows up in the shadow of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s tragedy, the domestic anchor left behind as [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] ventures into the world.

At the novel's conclusion, Liza-Lu accompanies [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] to watch the execution of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] from a nearby hill. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s final request — that [[angel-clare|Angel]] care for and marry Liza-Lu — is communicated through this younger sister. (049-211)

## Key Relationships

- **[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — her elder sister and protector
- **[[Angel Clare]]** — the man [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] asks to marry Liza-Lu after her death
- **[[John Durbeyfield]]** — her father
- **[[Joan Durbeyfield]]** — her mother
- **[[Abraham Durbeyfield]]** — her brother

## Key Scenes

- **[[marlott|Marlott]] Domestic Scene** (003-172–003-207): Liza-Lu appears as part of the Durbeyfield household, helping put the younger children to bed.
- **Reunion** (049-196): [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] encounters Liza-Lu and asks her to come in and sit down. (049-210)
- **The Final Wish**: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] entrusts her dying wish to Liza-Lu — that [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] marry her younger sister as recompense for what was taken from [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]].

## Analysis

Liza-Lu functions as [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s symbolic replacement — a purer, younger version of the protagonist who can continue where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] leaves off. Hardy uses her to suggest the possibility of renewal after tragedy, though this resolution is subdued and elegiac rather than triumphant.
