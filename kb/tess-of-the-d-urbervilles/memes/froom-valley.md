# Froom Valley

## Location

The Froom Valley (also called the Valley of the Var or the Vale of the Great Dairies) lies in the heart of Wessex, "well known to all travellers through the Froom Valley" (034-005). It is traversed by the River Froom — also called the Var — whose waters are "clear as the pure River of Life shown to the Evangelist" (016-068). The valley is situated at some distance from [[marlott|Marlott]] and the [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor Vale]], requiring [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to journey by hired trap through [[stourcastle|Stourcastle]] to reach it (016-005).

The valley's location is defined by the river that runs through it, "the Var or Froom" (016-049), and by the surrounding hills. [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]], approaching from the uplands, observes the valley "down there" (044-073) from a height — the rich green pastures forming a distinct contrast with the brown upland country above.

## Description

The Froom Valley is Hardy's vision of pastoral abundance — a landscape of "oozing fatness and warm ferments" (024-001) at the height of summer. Unlike the enclosed, secluded [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor Vale]], the Froom Valley is open and fertile, its meadows producing the rich grazing that sustains the great dairy herds of [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]].

The river Froom defines the character of the valley. Its waters "were clear as the pure River of Life shown to the Evangelist" (016-068), rapid and crystalline in contrast with the "slow, silent, often turbid" streams of [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor]] (016-066). This hydrological difference is emblematic: the Froom represents clarity, vitality, and purity — precisely the qualities associated with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s relationship with [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] during the [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] period.

The valley floor presents a patchwork of water-meadows and rich pasture, while the surrounding slopes carry the "humidity" of the vale (027-004). [[dairyman-crick|Dairyman Crick]]'s herds require abundant moisture, and the Froom Valley's lush climate provides it — a landscape of moisture and growth that Hardy describes in sensuous terms in Chapter XXIV.

## Significance

The Froom Valley is the setting for the novel's central pastoral idyll — the period between [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s departure from [[trantridge|Trantridge]] and the revelation that destroys her marriage. Here, in the meadows and milking-sheds of [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] experiences the only sustained happiness of her adult life. The valley itself becomes a character in this portion of the novel: its fertility, its seasonal rhythms, and its sensuous richness shape the love that grows between [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]].

Hardy constructs the Froom Valley as a deliberate counterpoint to two other landscapes. Against the enclosed, "engirdled" [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor Vale]] (002-002) — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s childhood world of folk superstition and inherited poverty — the Froom represents freedom and possibility. Against the desolate upland farm of [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] later labours through the winter, the Froom represents warmth, community, and human connection.

The valley's significance extends beyond the pastoral. It is here that [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] constructs his idealized image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a "fresh and virginal daughter of Nature" (025-059), an illusion that the valley itself seems to authorise. When this idealisation shatters on the wedding night, the Froom Valley's beauty is retrospectively tinged with irony — the landscape that nurtured their love was, all along, enabling a fatal misunderstanding.

## Analysis

### The Pastoral as Moral Landscape

The Froom Valley represents Hardy's most sustained use of pastoral convention — and his most thorough subversion of it. The valley's beauty and fecundity are real, but they also enable a dangerous sentimentalism. [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] falls in love with a [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] who seems to him an extension of the valley itself — natural, pure, untouched by history. The landscape colludes in this misreading, its "oozing fatness" suggesting a world of innocent abundance that has never known loss.

### Water as Purity and Danger

The River Froom, with its crystalline waters, becomes a metaphor for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] herself during the [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] period. The water is "clear as the pure River of Life" — the language of Revelation suggesting Edenic innocence. Yet the same river that symbolizes purity also carries the potential for dissolution: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s past cannot be washed away, however clear the waters of the Froom may run.
