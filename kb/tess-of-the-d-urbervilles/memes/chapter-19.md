[019-001]In general the cows were milked as they presented themselves, without fancy or

[019-002]choice. But certain cows will show a fondness for a particular pair of hands,

[019-003]sometimes carrying this predilection so far as to refuse to stand at all except

[019-004]to their favourite, the pail of a stranger being unceremoniously kicked over.

[019-005]It was Dairyman Crick’s rule to insist on breaking down these

[019-006]partialities and aversions by constant interchange, since otherwise, in the

[019-007]event of a milkman or maid going away from the dairy, he was placed in a

[019-008]difficulty. The maids’ private aims, however, were the reverse of the

[019-009]dairyman’s rule, the daily selection by each damsel of the eight or ten

[019-010]cows to which she had grown accustomed rendering the operation on their willing

[019-011]udders surprisingly easy and effortless.

[019-012]Tess, like her compeers, soon discovered which of the cows had a preference for

[019-013]her style of manipulation, and her fingers having become delicate from the long

[019-014]domiciliary imprisonments to which she had subjected herself at intervals

[019-015]during the last two or three years, she would have been glad to meet the

[019-016]milchers’ views in this respect. Out of the whole ninety-five there were

[019-017]eight in particular—Dumpling, Fancy, Lofty, Mist, Old Pretty, Young

[019-018]Pretty, Tidy, and Loud—who, though the teats of one or two were as hard

[019-019]as carrots, gave down to her with a readiness that made her work on them a mere

[019-020]touch of the fingers. Knowing, however, the dairyman’s wish, she

[019-021]endeavoured conscientiously to take the animals just as they came, excepting

[019-022]the very hard yielders which she could not yet manage.

[019-023]But she soon found a curious correspondence between the ostensibly chance

[019-024]position of the cows and her wishes in this matter, till she felt that their

[019-025]order could not be the result of accident. The dairyman’s pupil had lent

[019-026]a hand in getting the cows together of late, and at the fifth or sixth time she

[019-027]turned her eyes, as she rested against the cow, full of sly inquiry upon him.

[019-028]“Mr Clare, you have ranged the cows!” she said, blushing; and in

[019-029]making the accusation, symptoms of a smile gently lifted her upper lip in spite

[019-030]of her, so as to show the tips of her teeth, the lower lip remaining severely

[019-031]still.

[019-032]“Well, it makes no difference,” said he. “You will always be

[019-033]here to milk them.”

[019-034]“Do you think so? I *hope* I shall! But I don’t *know* .”

[019-035]She was angry with herself afterwards, thinking that he, unaware of her grave

[019-036]reasons for liking this seclusion, might have mistaken her meaning. She had

[019-037]spoken so earnestly to him, as if his presence were somehow a factor in her

[019-038]wish. Her misgiving was such that at dusk, when the milking was over, she

[019-039]walked in the garden alone, to continue her regrets that she had disclosed to

[019-040]him her discovery of his considerateness.

[019-041]It was a typical summer evening in June, the atmosphere being in such delicate

[019-042]equilibrium and so transmissive that inanimate objects seemed endowed with two

[019-043]or three senses, if not five. There was no distinction between the near and the

[019-044]far, and an auditor felt close to everything within the horizon. The

[019-045]soundlessness impressed her as a positive entity rather than as the mere

[019-046]negation of noise. It was broken by the strumming of strings.

[019-047]Tess had heard those notes in the attic above her head. Dim, flattened,

[019-048]constrained by their confinement, they had never appealed to her as now, when

[019-049]they wandered in the still air with a stark quality like that of nudity. To

[019-050]speak absolutely, both instrument and execution were poor; but the relative is

[019-051]all, and as she listened Tess, like a fascinated bird, could not leave the

[019-052]spot. Far from leaving she drew up towards the performer, keeping behind the

[019-053]hedge that he might not guess her presence.

[019-054]The outskirt of the garden in which Tess found herself had been left

[019-055]uncultivated for some years, and was now damp and rank with juicy grass which

[019-056]sent up mists of pollen at a touch; and with tall blooming weeds emitting

[019-057]offensive smells—weeds whose red and yellow and purple hues formed a

[019-059]cat through this profusion of growth, gathering cuckoo-spittle on her skirts,

[019-062]snow-white on the apple-tree trunks, made madder stains on her skin; thus she

[019-063]drew quite near to Clare, still unobserved of him.

[019-064]Tess was conscious of neither time nor space. The exaltation which she had

[019-065]described as being producible at will by gazing at a star came now without any

[019-066]determination of hers; she undulated upon the thin notes of the second-hand

[019-067]harp, and their harmonies passed like breezes through her, bringing tears into

[019-068]her eyes. The floating pollen seemed to be his notes made visible, and the

[019-069]dampness of the garden the weeping of the garden’s sensibility. Though

[019-070]near nightfall, the rank-smelling weed-flowers glowed as if they would not

[019-071]close for intentness, and the waves of colour mixed with the waves of sound.

[019-072]The light which still shone was derived mainly from a large hole in the western

[019-073]bank of cloud; it was like a piece of day left behind by accident, dusk having

[019-074]closed in elsewhere. He concluded his plaintive melody, a very simple

[019-075]performance, demanding no great skill; and she waited, thinking another might

[019-076]be begun. But, tired of playing, he had desultorily come round the fence, and

[019-077]was rambling up behind her. Tess, her cheeks on fire, moved away furtively, as

[019-079]Angel, however, saw her light summer gown, and he spoke; his low tones reaching

[019-080]her, though he was some distance off.

[019-081]“What makes you draw off in that way, Tess?” said he. “Are

[019-082]you afraid?”

[019-083]“Oh no, sir—not of outdoor things; especially just now when the

[019-084]apple-blooth is falling, and everything is so green.”

[019-085]“But you have your indoor fears—eh?”

[019-086]“Well—yes, sir.”

[019-087]“What of?”

[019-088]“I couldn’t quite say.”

[019-089]“The milk turning sour?”

[019-090]“No.”

[019-091]“Life in general?”

[019-092]“Yes, sir.”

[019-093]“Ah—so have I, very often. This hobble of being alive is rather

[019-094]serious, don’t you think so?”

[019-095]“It is—now you put it that way.”

[019-096]“All the same, I shouldn’t have expected a young girl like you to

[019-097]see it so just yet. How is it you do?”

[019-098]She maintained a hesitating silence.

[019-099]“Come, Tess, tell me in confidence.”

[019-100]She thought that he meant what were the aspects of things to her, and replied

[019-101]shyly—

[019-102]“The trees have inquisitive eyes, haven’t they?—that is, seem

[019-103]as if they had. And the river says,—‘Why do ye trouble me with your

[019-104]looks?’ And you seem to see numbers of to-morrows just all in a line, the

[019-105]first of them the biggest and clearest, the others getting smaller and smaller

[019-107]they said, ‘I’m coming! Beware of me! Beware of me!’ ...

[019-108]But *you* , sir, can raise up dreams with your music, and drive all such

[019-111]just that touch of rarity about her which might make her the envied of her

[019-112]housemates—shaping such sad imaginings. She was expressing in her own

[019-117]latest fashion in definition—a more accurate expression, by words in *logy* and *ism* , of sensations which men and women have vaguely

[019-122]not as to duration. Tess’s passing corporeal blight had been her mental

[019-123]harvest.

[019-124]Tess, on her part, could not understand why a man of clerical family and good

[019-125]education, and above physical want, should look upon it as a mishap to be

[019-127]could this admirable and poetic man ever have descended into the Valley of

[019-128]Humiliation, have felt with the man of Uz—as she herself had felt two or

[019-129]three years ago—“My soul chooseth strangling and death rather than

[019-130]my life. I loathe it; I would not live alway.”

[019-134]cows, but because he was learning to be a rich and prosperous dairyman,

[019-135]landowner, agriculturist, and breeder of cattle. He would become an American or

[019-141]Thus, neither having the clue to the other’s secret, they were

[019-142]respectively puzzled at what each revealed, and awaited new knowledge of each

[019-143]other’s character and mood without attempting to pry into each

[019-144]other’s history.

[019-145]Every day, every hour, brought to him one more little stroke of her nature, and

[019-146]to her one more of his. Tess was trying to lead a repressed life, but she

[019-147]little divined the strength of her own vitality.
