[013-001]The event of Tess Durbeyfield’s return from the manor of her bogus

[013-002]kinsfolk was rumoured abroad, if rumour be not too large a word for a space of

[013-003]a square mile. In the afternoon several young girls of Marlott, former

[013-004]schoolfellows and acquaintances of Tess, called to see her, arriving dressed in

[013-005]their best starched and ironed, as became visitors to a person who had made a

[013-006]transcendent conquest (as they supposed), and sat round the room looking at her

[013-007]with great curiosity. For the fact that it was this said thirty-first cousin,

[013-008]Mr d’Urberville, who had fallen in love with her, a gentleman not

[013-009]altogether local, whose reputation as a reckless gallant and heartbreaker was

[013-010]beginning to spread beyond the immediate boundaries of Trantridge, lent

[013-011]Tess’s supposed position, by its fearsomeness, a far higher fascination

[013-012]than it would have exercised if unhazardous.

[013-013]Their interest was so deep that the younger ones whispered when her back was

[013-014]turned—

[013-015]“How pretty she is; and how that best frock do set her off! I believe it

[013-016]cost an immense deal, and that it was a gift from him.”

[013-017]Tess, who was reaching up to get the tea-things from the corner-cupboard, did

[013-018]not hear these commentaries. If she had heard them, she might soon have set her

[013-019]friends right on the matter. But her mother heard, and Joan’s simple

[013-020]vanity, having been denied the hope of a dashing marriage, fed itself as well

[013-021]as it could upon the sensation of a dashing flirtation. Upon the whole she felt

[013-022]gratified, even though such a limited and evanescent triumph should involve her

[013-023]daughter’s reputation; it might end in marriage yet, and in the warmth of

[013-024]her responsiveness to their admiration she invited her visitors to stay to tea.

[013-025]Their chatter, their laughter, their good-humoured innuendoes, above all, their

[013-026]flashes and flickerings of envy, revived Tess’s spirits also; and, as the

[013-027]evening wore on, she caught the infection of their excitement, and grew almost

[013-028]gay. The marble hardness left her face, she moved with something of her old

[013-029]bounding step, and flushed in all her young beauty.

[013-030]At moments, in spite of thought, she would reply to their inquiries with a

[013-031]manner of superiority, as if recognizing that her experiences in the field of

[013-032]courtship had, indeed, been slightly enviable. But so far was she from being,

[013-033]in the words of Robert South, “in love with her own ruin,” that the

[013-034]illusion was transient as lightning; cold reason came back to mock her

[013-035]spasmodic weakness; the ghastliness of her momentary pride would convict her,

[013-036]and recall her to reserved listlessness again.

[013-037]And the despondency of the next morning’s dawn, when it was no longer

[013-038]Sunday, but Monday; and no best clothes; and the laughing visitors were gone,

[013-039]and she awoke alone in her old bed, the innocent younger children breathing

[013-040]softly around her. In place of the excitement of her return, and the interest

[013-041]it had inspired, she saw before her a long and stony highway which she had to

[013-042]tread, without aid, and with little sympathy. Her depression was then terrible,

[013-043]and she could have hidden herself in a tomb.

[013-044]In the course of a few weeks Tess revived sufficiently to show herself so far

[013-045]as was necessary to get to church one Sunday morning. She liked to hear the

[013-046]chanting—such as it was—and the old Psalms, and to join in the

[013-047]Morning Hymn. That innate love of melody, which she had inherited from her

[013-048]ballad-singing mother, gave the simplest music a power over her which could

[013-049]well-nigh drag her heart out of her bosom at times.

[013-050]To be as much out of observation as possible for reasons of her own, and to

[013-051]escape the gallantries of the young men, she set out before the chiming began,

[013-052]and took a back seat under the gallery, close to the lumber, where only old men

[013-053]and women came, and where the bier stood on end among the churchyard tools.

[013-054]Parishioners dropped in by twos and threes, deposited themselves in rows before

[013-055]her, rested three-quarters of a minute on their foreheads as if they were

[013-056]praying, though they were not; then sat up, and looked around. When the chants

[013-057]came on, one of her favourites happened to be chosen among the rest—the

[013-058]old double chant “Langdon”—but she did not know what it was

[013-059]called, though she would much have liked to know. She thought, without exactly

[013-060]wording the thought, how strange and god-like was a composer’s power, who

[013-061]from the grave could lead through sequences of emotion, which he alone had felt

[013-062]at first, a girl like her who had never heard of his name, and never would have

[013-063]a clue to his personality.

[013-064]The people who had turned their heads turned them again as the service

[013-065]proceeded; and at last observing her, they whispered to each other. She knew

[013-066]what their whispers were about, grew sick at heart, and felt that she could

[013-067]come to church no more.

[013-068]The bedroom which she shared with some of the children formed her retreat more

[013-069]continually than ever. Here, under her few square yards of thatch, she watched

[013-070]winds, and snows, and rains, gorgeous sunsets, and successive moons at their

[013-071]full. So close kept she that at length almost everybody thought she had gone

[013-072]away.

[013-073]The only exercise that Tess took at this time was after dark; and it was then,

[013-074]when out in the woods, that she seemed least solitary. She knew how to hit to a

[013-075]hair’s-breadth that moment of evening when the light and the darkness are

[013-076]so evenly balanced that the constraint of day and the suspense of night

[013-077]neutralize each other, leaving absolute mental liberty. It is then that the

[013-078]plight of being alive becomes attenuated to its least possible dimensions. She

[013-079]had no fear of the shadows; her sole idea seemed to be to shun mankind—or

[013-080]rather that cold accretion called the world, which, so terrible in the mass, is

[013-081]so unformidable, even pitiable, in its units.

[013-082]On these lonely hills and dales her quiescent glide was of a piece with the

[013-083]element she moved in. Her flexuous and stealthy figure became an integral part

[013-084]of the scene. At times her whimsical fancy would intensify natural processes

[013-085]around her till they seemed a part of her own story. Rather they became a part

[013-086]of it; for the world is only a psychological phenomenon, and what they seemed

[013-087]they were. The midnight airs and gusts, moaning amongst the tightly-wrapped

[013-088]buds and bark of the winter twigs, were formulae of bitter reproach. A wet day

[013-089]was the expression of irremediable grief at her weakness in the mind of some

[013-090]vague ethical being whom she could not class definitely as the God of her

[013-091]childhood, and could not comprehend as any other.

[013-092]But this encompassment of her own characterization, based on shreds of

[013-093]convention, peopled by phantoms and voices antipathetic to her, was a sorry and

[013-094]mistaken creation of Tess’s fancy—a cloud of moral hobgoblins by

[013-095]which she was terrified without reason. It was they that were out of harmony

[013-096]with the actual world, not she. Walking among the sleeping birds in the hedges,

[013-097]watching the skipping rabbits on a moonlit warren, or standing under a

[013-098]pheasant-laden bough, she looked upon herself as a figure of Guilt intruding

[013-099]into the haunts of Innocence. But all the while she was making a distinction

[013-100]where there was no difference. Feeling herself in antagonism, she was quite in

[013-101]accord. She had been made to break an accepted social law, but no law known to

[013-102]the environment in which she fancied herself such an anomaly.
