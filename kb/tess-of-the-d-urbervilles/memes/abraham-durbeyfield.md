# Abraham Durbeyfield

## Role in the Novel

Abraham Durbeyfield is the younger brother of [[Tess Durbeyfield]], a bright and inquisitive boy who appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel. (003-172) He shares his father [[John Durbeyfield]]'s fascination with the family's supposed noble lineage, dreaming of claiming kinship with the wealthy d'Urbervilles. (004-081)

Abraham's most significant contribution to the plot occurs when he accompanies [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on the night journey to deliver beehives to market. During this journey, their horse [[Prince]] is killed in a collision, an event that propels [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] toward the d'Urberville estate and her tragic fate. (004-135)

## Biography

Abraham is one of the younger children in the Durbeyfield household in [[Marlott]]. He is described as bright-eyed and impressionable, easily caught up in his parents' excitement over the family's noble ancestry. (003-204)

He accompanies [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on the ill-fated journey to deliver beehives, during which their horse Prince is killed. (004-135) After the accident, Abraham returns home while [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] bears the guilt and its consequences. His later appearances in the novel are minimal, as the narrative focus shifts to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s journey.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — his eldest sister, with whom he shares the fateful night journey
- **[[John Durbeyfield]]** — his father, whose vanity about the family lineage he inherits
- **[[Joan Durbeyfield]]** — his mother
- **[[Liza-Lu Durbeyfield]]** — his sister

## Key Scenes

- **The Revelation** (003-172–003-204): Abraham appears as part of the Durbeyfield household, fascinated by the family's discovery of their lineage. (003-199)
- **The Journey and Accident** (004-081–004-175): Abraham travels with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to deliver beehives. The journey ends in disaster when their horse is killed, an event that changes the course of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s life. (004-135)

## Analysis

Abraham functions as a narrative device in the early chapters — his childish enthusiasm for the d'Urberville name mirrors his father's vanity and underscores the theme of inherited delusion.

His presence during the fatal accident with Prince makes him a witness to the pivotal event that sets [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s tragedy in motion. Though a minor character, Abraham represents the domestic world [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] must leave behind as she enters the wider world of [[trantridge|Trantridge]] and beyond.
