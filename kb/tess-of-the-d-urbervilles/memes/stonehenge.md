# Stonehenge

## Location

Stonehenge stands on the open downs of Salisbury Plain, in the southern reaches of Wessex. In the novel, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] come upon it by chance during their flight across the plain, having left their refuge in a deserted mansion in the New Forest. The monument is described as "a forest of monoliths grouped upon the grassy expanse of the plain" (058-131), rising out of the empty landscape without hedge or fence — "all around was open loneliness and black solitude" (058-106).

The plain itself is vast and featureless, offering no concealment. Hardy notes that "the whole plain was empty of human habitation, the only sign of man's presence being the grey ribs of the prehistoric temple" (058-128). This isolation makes Stonehenge both a sanctuary and a trap — a place where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] can hide for a few hours, but where they can be seen from miles away.

## Description

Hardy's description of Stonehenge emphasizes its antiquity and its alien quality. The stones are "monoliths" — upright pillars of unknown origin from "a stratum unknown in any local quarry" (045-199). They form a circle against the sky, grey and weather-worn, relics of a religion that predates [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]] and even written history. The monument is not beautiful in any conventional sense; it is stark, severe, and strangely inhuman.

The setting of the arrest scene (058-118–058-170) uses the stone circle as architecture: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] lies down to sleep on "a stone altar" (058-152), a slab within the circle that may once have been used for sacrifice. The caretaker and constables arrive through the morning mist, their figures moving among the stones like figures in a ritual. The scene transforms the prehistoric temple into a stage, with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at its centre.

The atmosphere Hardy creates is one of exhausted peace followed by inexorable doom. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] arrive "in the small hours" (058-118), and she falls asleep on the altar stone as dawn breaks. The caretaker's arrival — "a figure came out of the darkness and approached the stone whereon she lay" (058-162) — is the intrusion of the modern world, the law, into this ancient sanctuary.

## Significance

Stonehenge is the novel's final and most symbolically charged setting. It is the place where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is arrested for the murder of [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]], and it serves as the threshold between her brief stolen happiness with [[angel-clare|Angel]] and her execution at [[wintoncester|Wintoncester]]. The choice of Stonehenge is precise: it is a heathen temple, not a Christian church, and Hardy uses this distinction to frame [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s fate in terms older and more terrible than Christian morality.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] herself makes the connection explicit. When [[angel-clare|Angel]] identifies the place — "It is Stonehenge!" — she replies, "The heathen temple, you mean?" (058-135). And later, lying on the altar stone: "You used to say at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] that I was a heathen. So now I am at home" (058-148). This line is one of the novel's most devastating ironies: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who has been judged and condemned by Christian society, finds her final resting place in a pre-Christian temple. The implication is that her suffering belongs to an order of tragedy older than the moral categories that have been used to condemn her.

The arrest at Stonehenge also completes the novel's pattern of sacrifice imagery. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] lies on "a stone altar" (058-152) — the word is not incidental. From Prince's death to the pheasants in the wood to her own life, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has been associated with the shedding of innocent blood. Now she lies on the sacrificial slab itself, waiting for the agents of the law to claim her.

## Analysis

### The Heathen Temple and Christian Judgment

Hardy's decision to stage [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s arrest at Stonehenge rather than in a church or courtroom is one of the novel's boldest symbolic gestures. The entire moral apparatus of Victorian society — the law, the church, the marriage vow — is Christian. By placing [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s final scene in a heathen temple, Hardy removes her from the jurisdiction of Christian morality and places her in a larger, older, and more ambiguous moral universe.

The heathen gods, Hardy implies, demanded sacrifice, but they did not pretend to be just. The Christian God claims to be both loving and just, yet [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s fate is indistinguishable from that of a sacrificial victim. The heathen temple is, in this reading, more honest than the church: it makes no promises it cannot keep.

### The Altar and the Victim

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s choice to lie on "a stone altar" (058-152) is the culminating moment of the novel's sacrificial imagery. Throughout the narrative, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has been associated with innocent creatures that are killed: Prince the horse, the pheasants in the wood, even the songbirds in the cage that made her cry as a child (058-043). Now she takes her place on the altar herself, completing the transformation from witness to victim.

But there is agency in this gesture. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] does not resist arrest; she has "had enough" of flight and concealment. Lying on the altar stone, she is choosing to stop running, to accept the consequences of her actions. The altar is not just a place of sacrifice but a place of rest — "I am ready," she says, and falls asleep.

### The Empty Plain

The landscape around Stonehenge — "open loneliness and black solitude" (058-106) — reflects the existential emptiness at the novel's core. There are no hedges to hide behind, no churches to appeal to, no houses to shelter in. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] are exposed on the plain as they are exposed to fate. The prehistoric monoliths, raised by unknown hands for unknown purposes, are the only witnesses to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s final hours of freedom.
