# The Execution of Tess

## Summary

On a bright July morning in [[wintoncester|Wintoncester]], [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] emerge from the wicket-gate of the prison and climb the long incline of West Hill. They reach the first milestone and turn to face the city. Below them, against the Gothic irregularity of [[wintoncester|Wintoncester]]'s cathedral and towers, rises an "ugly flat-topped octagonal tower" — the prison. At eight o'clock, as the city clocks strike, a black flag rises slowly on the staff atop the tower. Hardy's narrator delivers the novel's final sentence: "'[[justice-and-law|Justice]]' was done, and the President of the Immortals, in Æschylean phrase, had ended his sport with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]" (059-055–059-056).

## Context

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has stabbed [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] to death at [[the-herons|The Herons]] in [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]]. She and [[angel-clare|Angel]] flee together, spending a brief idyll of hiding — five days in an empty mansion at Bramshurst, then the final night at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]], where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] sleeps on the altar stone at dawn. The police arrive; [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] wakes, declares she is "ready," and is taken into custody.

The execution follows the trial, which is not narrated directly. Hardy's choice to skip the legal proceedings and cut directly to the morning of execution is deliberate: the novel's concern is not with judicial process but with the existential framing of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s death. The brief chapter — barely two pages — functions as an epilogue rendered in visual tableau.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — Absent from the scene yet its sole subject. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is inside the prison as the black flag rises. Her absence from the narrative's final moments mirrors her absence from the legal and social structures that have determined her fate — she is acted upon, not present. Her last spoken words in the novel were at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]]: "I am ready."

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]] walks hand in hand with '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]], "never spoke a word, the drooping of their heads being that of Giotto's 'Two Apostles'" (059-023–059-024). He has been spiritually hollowed by the events of the preceding days. His role in the final scene is that of witness: he watches the black flag rise, and when the pair have strength, they "arose, joined hands again, and went on" (059-060). The novel ends not with [[angel-clare|Angel]] alone but with [[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] together, a pairing charged with ambiguous suggestion.

**[[Liza-Lu|'Liza-Lu]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s younger sister, described as "a spiritualized image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], slighter than she, but with the same beautiful eyes" (059-020–059-021). Her presence at [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s side in the final scene is significant. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]], had asked [[angel-clare|Angel]] to marry '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] after her death: "She is so good and simple and pure... soon to be out of your life, and in mine" (058-124–058-127). The novel's closing image of [[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] walking onward together encodes the possibility — never stated — that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s wish may be fulfilled.

## Narrative Impact

The execution scene is the novel's shortest major event — barely sixty paragraphs, most of them descriptive. Its economy is functional: by compressing the ending into a single visual image, Hardy avoids melodrama and sentiment. The black flag replaces any deathbed speech, any courtroom drama, any prison farewell. It is the visual distillation of the novel's tragic logic.

The scene's famous final sentence — "'[[justice-and-law|Justice]]' was done, and the President of the Immortals, in Æschylean phrase, had ended his sport with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]" — places quotation marks around "[[justice-and-law|Justice]]," indicating Hardy's ironic distance from the concept. The Æschylean reference invokes the Greek tragic tradition in which mortals are playthings of indifferent or hostile gods. The "President of the Immortals" is Hardy's sardonic name for whatever cosmic force presides over human suffering — a force that treats [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s life and death as sport, ending only when it chooses.

The d'Urberville knights and dames "slept on in their tombs unknowing" (059-056–059-057). The ancestral lineage that propelled the novel's plot — [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s discovery, the fatal visit to [[the-slopes|The Slopes]] — is indifferent even to the death that completes its logic. The tombs at [[kingsbere|Kingsbere]], the object of [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s drunken pride, contain ancestors who neither know nor care that their name has been extinguished.

## Key Passages

The chapter opens with the description of [[wintoncester|Wintoncester]]:

> The city of Wintoncester, that fine old city, aforetime capital of Wessex, lay amidst its convex and concave downlands in all the brightness and warmth of a July morning. (059-001–059-003)

[[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] emerge from the prison:

> They had emerged upon this road through a narrow, barred wicket in a high wall a little lower down. They seemed anxious to get out of the sight of the houses and of their kind. (059-014–059-016)

They reach the milestone and turn to face the prison:

> They entered upon the turf, and, impelled by a force that seemed to overrule their will, suddenly stood still, turned, and waited in paralyzed suspense beside the stone. (059-029–059-031)

The prison is described as a visual blot on the ancient city:

> From the middle of the building an ugly flat-topped octagonal tower ascended against the east horizon, and viewed from this spot, on its shady side and against the light, it seemed the one blot on the city's beauty. Yet it was with this blot, and not with the beauty, that the two gazers were concerned. (059-048–059-051)

The black flag rises:

> Upon the cornice of the tower a tall staff was fixed. Their eyes were riveted on it. A few minutes after the hour had struck something moved slowly up the staff, and extended itself upon the breeze. It was a black flag. (059-052–059-054)

The novel's final sentence:

> "Justice" was done, and the President of the Immortals, in Æschylean phrase, had ended his sport with Tess. And the d'Urberville knights and dames slept on in their tombs unknowing. (059-055–059-057)

[[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] in the final image:

> The two speechless gazers bent themselves down to the earth, as if in prayer, and remained thus a long time, absolutely motionless: the flag continued to wave silently. As soon as they had strength, they arose, joined hands again, and went on. (059-058–059-060)

## Analysis

### The Visual Ending

Hardy's decision to render the novel's conclusion as a visual tableau — two figures on a hill, a distant tower, a rising flag — is a radical narrative choice. There is no interior monologue, no theological meditation, no moral explicit. The black flag performs all the work of conclusion. Its silent ascent replaces the sound of a death, the speech of a judge, the tears of mourners. Everything is reduced to an image, and the image is unmistakable.

The structure echoes cinematic technique before cinema existed: long shot of the city, tracking shot up the hill, medium shot of the two figures, then the fixed gaze on the tower as the flag rises. The effect is to make the reader a fellow watcher, sharing [[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]]'s paralyzed gaze.

### "Justice" in Quotation Marks

The quotation marks around "[[justice-and-law|Justice]]" are the novel's most concentrated act of moral commentary. They indicate that what has occurred is not justice but its institutional simulacrum. The legal system has executed a woman for killing the man who seduced, abandoned, and exploited her — after the social system failed to protect her, after the religious system extended charity to her seducer but not to her, after the marriage system enabled her husband to abandon her with legal impunity.

The Æschylean reference frames [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s death as a cosmic injustice rather than a legal resolution. In Aeschylus's Prometheus Bound, Zeus is the "President of the Immortals" who tortures Prometheus for the crime of benefiting humanity. Hardy's appropriation of the phrase suggests that the forces governing [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s world are similarly capricious. Her death is sport, not justice.

### The Unknowing Ancestors

The reference to the d'Urberville ancestors "sleeping on in their tombs unknowing" (059-056–059-057) closes the novel's genealogical theme. The entire plot has been set in motion by [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s discovery of his lineage. The d'Urberville name has determined [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s fate — bringing her to Alec, to [[the-slopes|The Slopes]], to the identity she claims at the end as "Mrs d'Urberville." Yet the ancestors whose name has carried such destructive force are utterly indifferent. The tombs at [[kingsbere|Kingsbere]] contain bones and dust, not guardians of family honor.

This final irony completes the novel's critique of ancestry and class. The Norman blood that [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] considered his greatest asset has been the instrument of his daughter's destruction. And the ancestors, for whose honor [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has been sacrificed, do not stir.

### 'Liza-Lu and the Future

The closing image of [[angel-clare|Angel]] and '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] walking onward together encodes the novel's one gesture toward continuation. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s death is final, but the pairing of [[angel-clare|Angel]] with her younger, purer sister — a "spiritualized image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]" (059-020) — suggests the cycle may repeat, or redeem. Critics have debated whether this gesture is hopeful or bitterly ironic: does '[[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] represent [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s renewal, or merely the replacement of one woman with another more conveniently "pure"?

Hardy offers no resolution, only the image of two figures "going on" (059-060). The ambiguity is intentional. The novel ends not with closure but with motion — away from the black flag, toward an uncertain horizon.
