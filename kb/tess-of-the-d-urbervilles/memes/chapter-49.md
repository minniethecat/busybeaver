[049-001]The appeal duly found its way to the breakfast-table of the quiet Vicarage to

[049-002]the westward, in that valley where the air is so soft and the soil so rich that

[049-003]the effort of growth requires but superficial aid by comparison with the

[049-004]tillage at Flintcomb-Ash, and where to Tess the human world seemed so different

[049-005](though it was much the same). It was purely for security that she had been

[049-006]requested by Angel to send her communications through his father, whom he kept

[049-007]pretty well informed of his changing addresses in the country he had gone to

[049-008]exploit for himself with a heavy heart.

[049-009]“Now,” said old Mr Clare to his wife, when he had read the

[049-010]envelope, “if Angel proposes leaving Rio for a visit home at the end of

[049-011]next month, as he told us that he hoped to do, I think this may hasten his

[049-012]plans; for I believe it to be from his wife.” He breathed deeply at the

[049-013]thought of her; and the letter was redirected to be promptly sent on to Angel.

[049-014]“Dear fellow, I hope he will get home safely,” murmured Mrs Clare.

[049-015]“To my dying day I shall feel that he has been ill-used. You should have

[049-016]sent him to Cambridge in spite of his want of faith and given him the same

[049-017]chance as the other boys had. He would have grown out of it under proper

[049-018]influence, and perhaps would have taken Orders after all. Church or no Church,

[049-019]it would have been fairer to him.”

[049-020]This was the only wail with which Mrs Clare ever disturbed her husband’s

[049-021]peace in respect to their sons. And she did not vent this often; for she was as

[049-022]considerate as she was devout, and knew that his mind too was troubled by

[049-023]doubts as to his justice in this matter. Only too often had she heard him lying

[049-024]awake at night, stifling sighs for Angel with prayers. But the uncompromising

[049-025]Evangelical did not even now hold that he would have been justified in giving

[049-026]his son, an unbeliever, the same academic advantages that he had given to the

[049-027]two others, when it was possible, if not probable, that those very advantages

[049-028]might have been used to decry the doctrines which he had made it his

[049-029]life’s mission and desire to propagate, and the mission of his ordained

[049-030]sons likewise. To put with one hand a pedestal under the feet of the two

[049-031]faithful ones, and with the other to exalt the unfaithful by the same

[049-032]artificial means, he deemed to be alike inconsistent with his convictions, his

[049-033]position, and his hopes. Nevertheless, he loved his misnamed Angel, and in

[049-034]secret mourned over this treatment of him as Abraham might have mourned over

[049-035]the doomed Isaac while they went up the hill together. His silent

[049-036]self-generated regrets were far bitterer than the reproaches which his wife

[049-037]rendered audible.

[049-038]They blamed themselves for this unlucky marriage. If Angel had never been

[049-039]destined for a farmer he would never have been thrown with agricultural girls.

[049-040]They did not distinctly know what had separated him and his wife, nor the date

[049-041]on which the separation had taken place. At first they had supposed it must be

[049-042]something of the nature of a serious aversion. But in his later letters he

[049-043]occasionally alluded to the intention of coming home to fetch her; from which

[049-044]expressions they hoped the division might not owe its origin to anything so

[049-045]hopelessly permanent as that. He had told them that she was with her relatives,

[049-046]and in their doubts they had decided not to intrude into a situation which they

[049-047]knew no way of bettering.

[049-048]The eyes for which Tess’s letter was intended were gazing at this time on

[049-049]a limitless expanse of country from the back of a mule which was bearing him

[049-050]from the interior of the South-American Continent towards the coast. His

[049-051]experiences of this strange land had been sad. The severe illness from which he

[049-052]had suffered shortly after his arrival had never wholly left him, and he had by

[049-053]degrees almost decided to relinquish his hope of farming here, though, as long

[049-054]as the bare possibility existed of his remaining, he kept this change of view a

[049-055]secret from his parents.

[049-056]The crowds of agricultural labourers who had come out to the country in his

[049-057]wake, dazzled by representations of easy independence, had suffered, died, and

[049-058]wasted away. He would see mothers from English farms trudging along with their

[049-059]infants in their arms, when the child would be stricken with fever and would

[049-060]die; the mother would pause to dig a hole in the loose earth with her bare

[049-061]hands, would bury the babe therein with the same natural grave-tools, shed one

[049-062]tear, and again trudge on.

[049-063]Angel’s original intention had not been emigration to Brazil but a

[049-064]northern or eastern farm in his own country. He had come to this place in a fit

[049-065]of desperation, the Brazil movement among the English agriculturists having by

[049-066]chance coincided with his desire to escape from his past existence.

[049-067]During this time of absence he had mentally aged a dozen years. What arrested

[049-068]him now as of value in life was less its beauty than its pathos. Having long

[049-069]discredited the old systems of mysticism, he now began to discredit the old

[049-070]appraisements of morality. He thought they wanted readjusting. Who was the

[049-071]moral man? Still more pertinently, who was the moral woman? The beauty or

[049-072]ugliness of a character lay not only in its achievements, but in its aims and

[049-073]impulses; its true history lay, not among things done, but among things willed.

[049-074]How, then, about Tess?

[049-075]Viewing her in these lights, a regret for his hasty judgement began to oppress

[049-076]him. Did he reject her eternally, or did he not? He could no longer say that he

[049-077]would always reject her, and not to say that was in spirit to accept her now.

[049-078]This growing fondness for her memory coincided in point of time with her

[049-079]residence at Flintcomb-Ash, but it was before she had felt herself at liberty

[049-080]to trouble him with a word about her circumstances or her feelings. He was

[049-081]greatly perplexed; and in his perplexity as to her motives in withholding

[049-082]intelligence, he did not inquire. Thus her silence of docility was

[049-083]misinterpreted. How much it really said if he had understood!—that she

[049-084]adhered with literal exactness to orders which he had given and forgotten; that

[049-085]despite her natural fearlessness she asserted no rights, admitted his judgement

[049-086]to be in every respect the true one, and bent her head dumbly thereto.

[049-087]In the before-mentioned journey by mules through the interior of the country,

[049-088]another man rode beside him. Angel’s companion was also an Englishman,

[049-089]bent on the same errand, though he came from another part of the island. They

[049-090]were both in a state of mental depression, and they spoke of home affairs.

[049-091]Confidence begat confidence. With that curious tendency evinced by men, more

[049-092]especially when in distant lands, to entrust to strangers details of their

[049-093]lives which they would on no account mention to friends, Angel admitted to this

[049-094]man as they rode along the sorrowful facts of his marriage.

[049-095]The stranger had sojourned in many more lands and among many more peoples than

[049-096]Angel; to his cosmopolitan mind such deviations from the social norm, so

[049-097]immense to domesticity, were no more than are the irregularities of vale and

[049-098]mountain-chain to the whole terrestrial curve. He viewed the matter in quite a

[049-099]different light from Angel; thought that what Tess had been was of no

[049-100]importance beside what she would be, and plainly told Clare that he was wrong

[049-101]in coming away from her.

[049-102]The next day they were drenched in a thunder-storm. Angel’s companion was

[049-103]struck down with fever, and died by the week’s end. Clare waited a few

[049-104]hours to bury him, and then went on his way.

[049-105]The cursory remarks of the large-minded stranger, of whom he knew absolutely

[049-106]nothing beyond a commonplace name, were sublimed by his death, and influenced

[049-107]Clare more than all the reasoned ethics of the philosophers. His own

[049-108]parochialism made him ashamed by its contrast. His inconsistencies rushed upon

[049-109]him in a flood. He had persistently elevated Hellenic Paganism at the expense

[049-110]of Christianity; yet in that civilization an illegal surrender was not certain

[049-111]disesteem. Surely then he might have regarded that abhorrence of the un-intact

[049-112]state, which he had inherited with the creed of mysticism, as at least open to

[049-113]correction when the result was due to treachery. A remorse struck into him. The

[049-114]words of Izz Huett, never quite stilled in his memory, came back to him. He had

[049-115]asked Izz if she loved him, and she had replied in the affirmative. Did she

[049-116]love him more than Tess did? No, she had replied; Tess would lay down her life

[049-117]for him, and she herself could do no more.

[049-118]He thought of Tess as she had appeared on the day of the wedding. How her eyes

[049-119]had lingered upon him; how she had hung upon his words as if they were a

[049-120]god’s! And during the terrible evening over the hearth, when her simple

[049-121]soul uncovered itself to his, how pitiful her face had looked by the rays of

[049-122]the fire, in her inability to realize that his love and protection could

[049-123]possibly be withdrawn.

[049-124]Thus from being her critic he grew to be her advocate. Cynical things he had

[049-125]uttered to himself about her; but no man can be always a cynic and live; and he

[049-126]withdrew them. The mistake of expressing them had arisen from his allowing

[049-127]himself to be influenced by general principles to the disregard of the

[049-128]particular instance.

[049-129]But the reasoning is somewhat musty; lovers and husbands have gone over the

[049-130]ground before to-day. Clare had been harsh towards her; there is no doubt of

[049-131]it. Men are too often harsh with women they love or have loved; women with men.

[049-132]And yet these harshnesses are tenderness itself when compared with the

[049-133]universal harshness out of which they grow; the harshness of the position

[049-134]towards the temperament, of the means towards the aims, of to-day towards

[049-135]yesterday, of hereafter towards to-day.

[049-136]The historic interest of her family—that masterful line of

[049-137]d’Urbervilles—whom he had despised as a spent force, touched his

[049-138]sentiments now. Why had he not known the difference between the political value

[049-139]and the imaginative value of these things? In the latter aspect her

[049-140]d’Urberville descent was a fact of great dimensions; worthless to

[049-141]economics, it was a most useful ingredient to the dreamer, to the moralizer on

[049-142]declines and falls. It was a fact that would soon be forgotten—that bit

[049-143]of distinction in poor Tess’s blood and name, and oblivion would fall

[049-144]upon her hereditary link with the marble monuments and leaded skeletons at

[049-145]Kingsbere. So does Time ruthlessly destroy his own romances. In recalling her

[049-146]face again and again, he thought now that he could see therein a flash of the

[049-147]dignity which must have graced her grand-dames; and the vision sent that *aura* through his veins which he had formerly felt, and which left behind

[049-148]it a sense of sickness.

[049-149]Despite her not inviolate past, what still abode in such a woman as Tess

[049-150]outvalued the freshness of her fellows. Was not the gleaning of the grapes of

[049-151]Ephraim better than the vintage of Abi-ezer?

[049-152]So spoke love renascent, preparing the way for Tess’s devoted outpouring,

[049-153]which was then just being forwarded to him by his father; though owing to his

[049-154]distance inland it was to be a long time in reaching him.

[049-155]Meanwhile the writer’s expectation that Angel would come in response to

[049-156]the entreaty was alternately great and small. What lessened it was that the

[049-157]facts of her life which had led to the parting had not changed—could

[049-158]never change; and that, if her presence had not attenuated them, her absence

[049-159]could not. Nevertheless she addressed her mind to the tender question of what

[049-160]she could do to please him best if he should arrive. Sighs were expended on the

[049-161]wish that she had taken more notice of the tunes he played on his harp, that

[049-162]she had inquired more curiously of him which were his favourite ballads among

[049-163]those the country-girls sang. She indirectly inquired of Amby Seedling, who had

[049-164]followed Izz from Talbothays, and by chance Amby remembered that, amongst the

[049-165]snatches of melody in which they had indulged at the dairyman’s, to

[049-166]induce the cows to let down their milk, Clare had seemed to like

[049-167]“Cupid’s Gardens”, “I have parks, I have hounds”,

[049-168]and “The break o’ the day”; and had seemed not to care for

[049-169]“The Tailor’s Breeches” and “Such a beauty I did

[049-170]grow”, excellent ditties as they were.

[049-171]To perfect the ballads was now her whimsical desire. She practised them

[049-172]privately at odd moments, especially “The break o’ the day”:

[049-173]Arise, arise, arise!

[049-174]And pick your love a posy,

[049-175]All o’ the sweetest flowers

[049-176]That in the garden grow.

[049-177]The turtle doves and sma’ birds

[049-178]In every bough a-building,

[049-179]So early in the May-time

[049-180]At the break o’ the day!

[049-181]It would have melted the heart of a stone to hear her singing these ditties

[049-182]whenever she worked apart from the rest of the girls in this cold dry time; the

[049-183]tears running down her cheeks all the while at the thought that perhaps he

[049-184]would not, after all, come to hear her, and the simple silly words of the songs

[049-185]resounding in painful mockery of the aching heart of the singer.

[049-186]Tess was so wrapt up in this fanciful dream that she seemed not to know how the

[049-187]season was advancing; that the days had lengthened, that Lady-Day was at hand,

[049-188]and would soon be followed by Old Lady-Day, the end of her term here.

[049-189]But before the quarter-day had quite come, something happened which made Tess

[049-190]think of far different matters. She was at her lodging as usual one evening,

[049-191]sitting in the downstairs room with the rest of the family, when somebody

[049-192]knocked at the door and inquired for Tess. Through the doorway she saw against

[049-193]the declining light a figure with the height of a woman and the breadth of a

[049-194]child, a tall, thin, girlish creature whom she did not recognize in the

[049-195]twilight till the girl said “Tess!”

[049-196]“What—is it ’Liza-Lu?” asked Tess, in startled accents.

[049-197]Her sister, whom a little over a year ago she had left at home as a child, had

[049-198]sprung up by a sudden shoot to a form of this presentation, of which as yet Lu

[049-199]seemed herself scarce able to understand the meaning. Her thin legs, visible

[049-200]below her once-long frock, now short by her growing, and her uncomfortable

[049-201]hands and arms revealed her youth and inexperience.

[049-202]“Yes, I have been traipsing about all day, Tess,” said Lu, with

[049-203]unemotional gravity, “a-trying to find ’ee; and I’m very

[049-204]tired.”

[049-205]“What is the matter at home?”

[049-206]“Mother is took very bad, and the doctor says she’s dying, and as

[049-207]father is not very well neither, and says ’tis wrong for a man of such a

[049-208]high family as his to slave and drave at common labouring work, we don’t

[049-209]know what to do.”

[049-210]Tess stood in reverie a long time before she thought of asking ’Liza-Lu

[049-211]to come in and sit down. When she had done so, and ’Liza-Lu was having

[049-212]some tea, she came to a decision. It was imperative that she should go home.

[049-213]Her agreement did not end till Old Lady-Day, the sixth of April, but as the

[049-214]interval thereto was not a long one she resolved to run the risk of starting at

[049-215]once.

[049-216]To go that night would be a gain of twelve-hours; but her sister was too tired

[049-217]to undertake such a distance till the morrow. Tess ran down to where Marian and

[049-218]Izz lived, informed them of what had happened, and begged them to make the best

[049-219]of her case to the farmer. Returning, she got Lu a supper, and after that,

[049-220]having tucked the younger into her own bed, packed up as many of her belongings

[049-221]as would go into a withy basket, and started, directing Lu to follow her next

[049-222]morning.
