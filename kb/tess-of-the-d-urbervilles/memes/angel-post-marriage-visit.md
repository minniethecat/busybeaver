# Angel's Post-Marriage Visit to His Parents

## Summary

Three weeks after his marriage to [[Tess Durbeyfield]], [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] returns alone to his parents' vicarage at [[emminster|Emminster]]. He arrives "like a ghost" (039-005), a man hollowed out by the moral crisis that has destroyed his marriage within days of its beginning. He announces his plan to emigrate to Brazil, offering a thin excuse for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s absence — she is "at her mother's — temporarily" (039-060). The visit unfolds as a sustained exercise in painful irony: his mother presses him with questions about [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s purity and virtue; his father reads Proverbs 31 in praise of the virtuous wife; and [[angel-clare|Angel]], who has abandoned the very woman who embodies those virtues, must sit through it all in silence, telling "that lie" (039-170) to preserve his parents' illusions.

## Context

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s mental state has deteriorated in the three weeks since the wedding. He has attempted "mechanically... to pursue his agricultural plans as though nothing unusual had happened" (039-013–039-014) and found the Stoic and Christian consolations equally hollow. His mood has "transmuted itself into a dogged indifference" (039-024). The Brazil plan offers a geographical solution to a moral problem — "perhaps in that country of contrasting scenes and notions and habits the conventions would not be so operative which made life with her seem impracticable to him here" (039-043–039-044).

The visit to [[emminster|Emminster]] is structurally a mirror of his earlier visit in Chapter 26, when he announced his intention to marry [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. That visit was filled with hope and defiance; this one is steeped in concealment and despair. The symmetry exposes how completely his marriage has failed within weeks.

## Key Participants

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]] is at his most self-divided. He loves [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — "he perceived how intimately the notion of having [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a dear possession was mixed up with all his schemes and words and ways" (039-035–039-037) — yet cannot overcome the "conventions" that make life with her "impracticable." His lie to his mother — "She is spotless!" (039-169) — is the chapter's moral crux: he knows [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is innocent in spirit but cannot reconcile this knowledge with his inherited code. Hardy's narrator delivers a devastating verdict: "With all his attempted independence of judgement this advanced and well-meaning young man... was yet the slave to custom and conventionality when surprised back into his early teachings" (039-193–039-195).

**Mrs Clare** — [[mrs-clare|Angel's mother]] embodies well-meaning ignorance. Her questions probe exactly the wound [[angel-clare|Angel]] is hiding: "And that she is pure and virtuous goes without question?" (039-104). Her image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a "simple, rosy-mouthed, robust" farm girl (039-116–039-117) is both accurate and catastrophically incomplete. Her parting observation — "Since she is pure and chaste, she would have been refined enough for me" (039-149–039-150) — is the irony that breaks [[angel-clare|Angel]]: "His eyes were full of tears, which seemed like drops of molten lead" (039-151–039-152).

**[[reverend-clare|Mr Clare]]** — The Vicar's choice to read Proverbs 31 — the praise of the virtuous wife — is an unintended indictment of [[angel-clare|Angel]]. The description of the ideal wife as a working woman who "riseth while it is yet night" and "girdeth her loins with strength" (039-135–039-137) describes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] far more accurately than [[mrs-clare|Angel's mother]] realizes.

## Narrative Impact

This chapter is the novel's most concentrated dramatization of the gap between public piety and private moral failure. The Vicarage, with its Bible readings and evening prayers, is a space of genuine goodness — the Clares are "sincere and simple souls whom he loved so well; who knew neither the world, the flesh, nor the devil in their own hearts" (039-152–039-154) — yet this very innocence makes them instruments of cruelty. They praise a woman they have never met for virtues they cannot verify, while the woman herself has been cast off by their son.

The chapter's final paragraph delivers one of Hardy's most explicit authorial judgments, declaring that [[angel-clare|Angel]] is "the slave to custom and conventionality" (039-194) and that "in considering what [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] was not, he overlooked what she was, and forgot that the defective can be more than the entire" (039-201–039-203). This is the novel's thesis statement on the moral inadequacy of conventional judgment.

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s arrival, ghostlike and diminished:

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s recognition of his continuing love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]:

> As the hours dropped past, as the motive of each act in the long series of bygone days presented itself to his view, he perceived how intimately the notion of having Tess as a dear possession was mixed up with all his schemes and words and ways. (039-034–039-037)

Mrs Clare's probing questions:

> "And that she is pure and virtuous goes without question?"
> "Pure and virtuous, of course, she is." (039-104–039-105)

The reading of Proverbs 31, the chapter's central irony:

> "Who can find a virtuous woman? for her price is far above rubies. She riseth while it is yet night, and giveth meat to her household... Her children arise up and call her blessed; her husband also, and he praiseth her." (039-135–039-141)

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s lie and its cost:

> "She is spotless!" he replied; and felt that if it had sent him to eternal hell there and then he would have told that lie. (039-169–039-170)

The narrator's verdict on [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s limitation:

> With all his attempted independence of judgement this advanced and well-meaning young man, a sample product of the last five-and-twenty years, was yet the slave to custom and conventionality when surprised back into his early teachings. (039-193–039-195)

The chapter's thesis:

> In considering what Tess was not, he overlooked what she was, and forgot that the defective can be more than the entire. (039-201–039-203)

## Analysis

### The Insufficiency of Goodness

The Clares are good people — genuinely charitable, sincerely devout. Yet their goodness is structured in ways that exclude [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. Mrs Clare can imagine a "pure" country girl as a suitable wife for her son, but she cannot imagine that purity might coexist with a sexual past. [[reverend-clare|Mr Clare]] reads Proverbs 31 with genuine feeling, never suspecting that every verse describes the woman his son has abandoned. The chapter argues that conventional goodness, unaccompanied by moral imagination, is not merely inadequate but actively harmful.

### The Brazil Escape

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s plan to emigrate to Brazil is a spatial solution to a spiritual problem. He hopes that "in that country of contrasting scenes and notions and habits the conventions would not be so operative" (039-043–039-044). The logic is revealing: [[angel-clare|Angel]] believes the problem is in the conventions, not in himself. Brazil becomes a fantasy of moral relocation — the idea that changing geography can change character. Hardy undercuts this fantasy by the chapter's end: the problem is not where [[angel-clare|Angel]] lives but what he is.

### The Limits of Angel's Progressivism

[[angel-clare|Angel]] prides himself on independence of judgment — he has rejected a career in the church, chosen agricultural life, married beneath his class. But the chapter exposes how shallow this independence runs. "No prophet had told him, and he was not prophet enough to tell himself, that essentially this young wife of his was as deserving of the praise of King Lemuel as any other woman endowed with the same dislike of evil" (039-196–039-198). [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s progressivism is aesthetic, not ethical — it changes what he does, not what he values.
