[048-001]In the afternoon the farmer made it known that the rick was to be finished that

[048-002]night, since there was a moon by which they could see to work, and the man with

[048-003]the engine was engaged for another farm on the morrow. Hence the twanging and

[048-004]humming and rustling proceeded with even less intermission than usual.

[048-005]It was not till “nammet”-time, about three o’clock, that Tess

[048-006]raised her eyes and gave a momentary glance round. She felt but little surprise

[048-007]at seeing that Alec d’Urberville had come back, and was standing under

[048-008]the hedge by the gate. He had seen her lift her eyes, and waved his hand

[048-009]urbanely to her, while he blew her a kiss. It meant that their quarrel was

[048-010]over. Tess looked down again, and carefully abstained from gazing in that

[048-011]direction.

[048-012]Thus the afternoon dragged on. The wheat-rick shrank lower, and the straw-rick

[048-013]grew higher, and the corn-sacks were carted away. At six o’clock the

[048-014]wheat-rick was about shoulder-high from the ground. But the unthreshed sheaves

[048-015]remaining untouched seemed countless still, notwithstanding the enormous

[048-016]numbers that had been gulped down by the insatiable swallower, fed by the man

[048-017]and Tess, through whose two young hands the greater part of them had passed.

[048-018]And the immense stack of straw where in the morning there had been nothing,

[048-019]appeared as the *faeces* of the same buzzing red glutton. From the west

[048-020]sky a wrathful shine—all that wild March could afford in the way of

[048-021]sunset—had burst forth after the cloudy day, flooding the tired and

[048-022]sticky faces of the threshers, and dyeing them with a coppery light, as also

[048-023]the flapping garments of the women, which clung to them like dull flames.

[048-024]A panting ache ran through the rick. The man who fed was weary, and Tess could

[048-025]see that the red nape of his neck was encrusted with dirt and husks. She still

[048-026]stood at her post, her flushed and perspiring face coated with the corndust,

[048-027]and her white bonnet embrowned by it. She was the only woman whose place was

[048-028]upon the machine so as to be shaken bodily by its spinning, and the decrease of

[048-029]the stack now separated her from Marian and Izz, and prevented their changing

[048-030]duties with her as they had done. The incessant quivering, in which every fibre

[048-031]of her frame participated, had thrown her into a stupefied reverie in which her

[048-032]arms worked on independently of her consciousness. She hardly knew where she

[048-033]was, and did not hear Izz Huett tell her from below that her hair was tumbling

[048-034]down.

[048-035]By degrees the freshest among them began to grow cadaverous and saucer-eyed.

[048-036]Whenever Tess lifted her head she beheld always the great upgrown straw-stack,

[048-037]with the men in shirt-sleeves upon it, against the gray north sky; in front of

[048-038]it the long red elevator like a Jacob’s ladder, on which a perpetual

[048-039]stream of threshed straw ascended, a yellow river running uphill, and spouting

[048-040]out on the top of the rick.

[048-041]She knew that Alec d’Urberville was still on the scene, observing her

[048-042]from some point or other, though she could not say where. There was an excuse

[048-043]for his remaining, for when the threshed rick drew near its final sheaves a

[048-044]little ratting was always done, and men unconnected with the threshing

[048-045]sometimes dropped in for that performance—sporting characters of all

[048-046]descriptions, gents with terriers and facetious pipes, roughs with sticks and

[048-047]stones.

[048-048]But there was another hour’s work before the layer of live rats at the

[048-049]base of the stack would be reached; and as the evening light in the direction

[048-050]of the Giant’s Hill by Abbot’s-Cernel dissolved away, the

[048-051]white-faced moon of the season arose from the horizon that lay towards

[048-052]Middleton Abbey and Shottsford on the other side. For the last hour or two

[048-053]Marian had felt uneasy about Tess, whom she could not get near enough to speak

[048-054]to, the other women having kept up their strength by drinking ale, and Tess

[048-055]having done without it through traditionary dread, owing to its results at her

[048-056]home in childhood. But Tess still kept going: if she could not fill her part

[048-057]she would have to leave; and this contingency, which she would have regarded

[048-058]with equanimity and even with relief a month or two earlier, had become a

[048-059]terror since d’Urberville had begun to hover round her.

[048-060]The sheaf-pitchers and feeders had now worked the rick so low that people on

[048-061]the ground could talk to them. To Tess’s surprise Farmer Groby came up on

[048-062]the machine to her, and said that if she desired to join her friend he did not

[048-063]wish her to keep on any longer, and would send somebody else to take her place.

[048-064]The “friend” was d’Urberville, she knew, and also that this

[048-065]concession had been granted in obedience to the request of that friend, or

[048-066]enemy. She shook her head and toiled on.

[048-067]The time for the rat-catching arrived at last, and the hunt began. The

[048-068]creatures had crept downwards with the subsidence of the rick till they were

[048-069]all together at the bottom, and being now uncovered from their last refuge,

[048-070]they ran across the open ground in all directions, a loud shriek from the

[048-071]by-this-time half-tipsy Marian informing her companions that one of the rats

[048-072]had invaded her person—a terror which the rest of the women had guarded

[048-073]against by various schemes of skirt-tucking and self-elevation. The rat was at

[048-074]last dislodged, and, amid the barking of dogs, masculine shouts, feminine

[048-075]screams, oaths, stampings, and confusion as of Pandemonium, Tess untied her

[048-076]last sheaf; the drum slowed, the whizzing ceased, and she stepped from the

[048-077]machine to the ground.

[048-078]Her lover, who had only looked on at the rat-catching, was promptly at her

[048-079]side.

[048-080]“What—after all—my insulting slap, too!” said she in an

[048-081]underbreath. She was so utterly exhausted that she had not strength to speak

[048-082]louder.

[048-083]“I should indeed be foolish to feel offended at anything you say or

[048-084]do,” he answered, in the seductive voice of the Trantridge time.

[048-085]“How the little limbs tremble! You are as weak as a bled calf, you know

[048-086]you are; and yet you need have done nothing since I arrived. How could you be

[048-087]so obstinate? However, I have told the farmer that he has no right to employ

[048-088]women at steam-threshing. It is not proper work for them; and on all the better

[048-089]class of farms it has been given up, as he knows very well. I will walk with

[048-090]you as far as your home.”

[048-091]“O yes,” she answered with a jaded gait. “Walk wi’ me

[048-092]if you will! I do bear in mind that you came to marry me before you knew

[048-093]o’ my state. Perhaps—perhaps you are a little better and kinder

[048-094]than I have been thinking you were. Whatever is meant as kindness I am grateful

[048-095]for; whatever is meant in any other way I am angered at. I cannot sense your

[048-096]meaning sometimes.”

[048-097]“If I cannot legitimize our former relations at least I can assist you.

[048-098]And I will do it with much more regard for your feelings than I formerly

[048-099]showed. My religious mania, or whatever it was, is over. But I retain a little

[048-100]good nature; I hope I do. Now, Tess, by all that’s tender and strong

[048-101]between man and woman, trust me! I have enough and more than enough to put you

[048-102]out of anxiety, both for yourself and your parents and sisters. I can make them

[048-103]all comfortable if you will only show confidence in me.”

[048-104]“Have you seen ’em lately?” she quickly inquired.

[048-105]“Yes. They didn’t know where you were. It was only by chance that I

[048-106]found you here.”

[048-107]The cold moon looked aslant upon Tess’s fagged face between the twigs of

[048-108]the garden-hedge as she paused outside the cottage which was her temporary

[048-109]home, d’Urberville pausing beside her.

[048-110]“Don’t mention my little brothers and sisters—don’t

[048-111]make me break down quite!” she said. “If you want to help

[048-112]them—God knows they need it—do it without telling me. But no,

[048-113]no!” she cried. “I will take nothing from you, either for them or

[048-114]for me!”

[048-115]He did not accompany her further, since, as she lived with the household, all

[048-116]was public indoors. No sooner had she herself entered, laved herself in a

[048-117]washing-tub, and shared supper with the family than she fell into thought, and

[048-118]withdrawing to the table under the wall, by the light of her own little lamp

[048-119]wrote in a passionate mood—

[048-120]My own Husband,

[048-121]Let me call you so—I must—even if it makes you angry to think

[048-122]of such an unworthy wife as I. I must cry to you in my trouble—I have no

[048-123]one else! I am so exposed to temptation, Angel. I fear to say who it is, and I

[048-124]do not like to write about it at all. But I cling to you in a way you cannot

[048-125]think! Can you not come to me now, at once, before anything terrible happens?

[048-126]O, I know you cannot, because you are so far away! I think I must die if you do

[048-127]not come soon, or tell me to come to you. The punishment you have measured out

[048-128]to me is deserved—I do know that—well deserved—and you are

[048-129]right and just to be angry with me. But, Angel, please, please, not to be

[048-130]just—only a little kind to me, even if I do not deserve it, and come to

[048-131]me! If you would come, I could die in your arms! I would be well content to do

[048-132]that if so be you had forgiven me!

[048-133]Angel, I live entirely for you. I love you too much to blame you for going

[048-134]away, and I know it was necessary you should find a farm. Do not think I shall

[048-135]say a word of sting or bitterness. Only come back to me. I am desolate without

[048-136]you, my darling, O, so desolate! I do not mind having to work: but if you will

[048-137]send me one little line, and say, “ *I am coming soon* ”, I will

[048-138]bide on, Angel—O, so cheerfully!

[048-139]It has been so much my religion ever since we were married to be faithful

[048-140]to you in every thought and look, that even when a man speaks a compliment to

[048-141]me before I am aware, it seems wronging you. Have you never felt one little bit

[048-142]of what you used to feel when we were at the dairy? If you have, how can you

[048-143]keep away from me? I am the same woman, Angel, as you fell in love with; yes,

[048-144]the very same!—not the one you disliked but never saw. What was the past

[048-145]to me as soon as I met you? It was a dead thing altogether. I became another

[048-146]woman, filled full of new life from you. How could I be the early one? Why do

[048-147]you not see this? Dear, if you would only be a little more conceited, and

[048-148]believe in yourself so far as to see that you were strong enough to work this

[048-149]change in me, you would perhaps be in a mind to come to me, your poor wife.

[048-150]How silly I was in my happiness when I thought I could trust you always to

[048-151]love me! I ought to have known that such as that was not for poor me. But I am

[048-152]sick at heart, not only for old times, but for the present. Think—think

[048-153]how it do hurt my heart not to see you ever—ever! Ah, if I could only

[048-154]make your dear heart ache one little minute of each day as mine does every day

[048-155]and all day long, it might lead you to show pity to your poor lonely one.

[048-156]People still say that I am rather pretty, Angel (handsome is the word they

[048-157]use, since I wish to be truthful). Perhaps I am what they say. But I do not

[048-158]value my good looks; I only like to have them because they belong to you, my

[048-159]dear, and that there may be at least one thing about me worth your having. So

[048-160]much have I felt this, that when I met with annoyance on account of the same, I

[048-161]tied up my face in a bandage as long as people would believe in it. O Angel, I

[048-162]tell you all this not from vanity—you will certainly know I do

[048-163]not—but only that you may come to me!

[048-164]If you really cannot come to me, will you let me come to you? I am, as I

[048-165]say, worried, pressed to do what I will not do. It cannot be that I shall yield

[048-166]one inch, yet I am in terror as to what an accident might lead to, and I so

[048-167]defenceless on account of my first error. I cannot say more about this—it

[048-168]makes me too miserable. But if I break down by falling into some fearful snare,

[048-169]my last state will be worse than my first. O God, I cannot think of it! Let me

[048-170]come at once, or at once come to me!

[048-171]I would be content, ay, glad, to live with you as your servant, if I may

[048-172]not as your wife; so that I could only be near you, and get glimpses of you,

[048-173]and think of you as mine.

[048-174]The daylight has nothing to show me, since you are not here, and I

[048-175]don’t like to see the rooks and starlings in the field, because I grieve

[048-176]and grieve to miss you who used to see them with me. I long for only one thing

[048-177]in heaven or earth or under the earth, to meet you, my own dear! Come to

[048-178]me—come to me, and save me from what threatens me!—

[048-179]Your faithful heartbroken

[048-180]Tess
