[001-001]On an evening in the latter part of May a middle-aged man was walking homeward

[001-002]from Shaston to the village of Marlott, in the adjoining Vale of Blakemore, or

[001-003]Blackmoor. The pair of legs that carried him were rickety, and there was a bias

[001-004]in his gait which inclined him somewhat to the left of a straight line. He

[001-005]occasionally gave a smart nod, as if in confirmation of some opinion, though he

[001-006]was not thinking of anything in particular. An empty egg-basket was slung upon

[001-007]his arm, the nap of his hat was ruffled, a patch being quite worn away at its

[001-008]brim where his thumb came in taking it off. Presently he was met by an elderly

[001-009]parson astride on a gray mare, who, as he rode, hummed a wandering tune.

[001-010]“Good night t’ee,” said the man with the basket.

[001-011]“Good night, Sir John,” said the parson.

[001-012]The pedestrian, after another pace or two, halted, and turned round.

[001-013]“Now, sir, begging your pardon; we met last market-day on this road about

[001-014]this time, and I said ‘Good night,’ and you made reply

[001-015]‘ *Good night, Sir John* ,’ as now.”

[001-016]“I did,” said the parson.

[001-017]“And once before that—near a month ago.”

[001-018]“I may have.”

[001-019]“Then what might your meaning be in calling me ‘Sir John’

[001-020]these different times, when I be plain Jack Durbeyfield, the haggler?”

[001-021]The parson rode a step or two nearer.

[001-022]“It was only my whim,” he said; and, after a moment’s

[001-023]hesitation: “It was on account of a discovery I made some little time

[001-024]ago, whilst I was hunting up pedigrees for the new county history. I am Parson

[001-025]Tringham, the antiquary, of Stagfoot Lane. Don’t you really know,

[001-026]Durbeyfield, that you are the lineal representative of the ancient and knightly

[001-027]family of the d’Urbervilles, who derive their descent from Sir Pagan

[001-028]d’Urberville, that renowned knight who came from Normandy with William

[001-029]the Conqueror, as appears by Battle Abbey Roll?”

[001-030]“Never heard it before, sir!”

[001-031]“Well it’s true. Throw up your chin a moment, so that I may catch

[001-032]the profile of your face better. Yes, that’s the d’Urberville nose

[001-033]and chin—a little debased. Your ancestor was one of the twelve knights

[001-034]who assisted the Lord of Estremavilla in Normandy in his conquest of

[001-035]Glamorganshire. Branches of your family held manors over all this part of

[001-036]England; their names appear in the Pipe Rolls in the time of King Stephen. In

[001-037]the reign of King John one of them was rich enough to give a manor to the

[001-038]Knights Hospitallers; and in Edward the Second’s time your forefather

[001-039]Brian was summoned to Westminster to attend the great Council there. You

[001-040]declined a little in Oliver Cromwell’s time, but to no serious extent,

[001-041]and in Charles the Second’s reign you were made Knights of the Royal Oak

[001-042]for your loyalty. Aye, there have been generations of Sir Johns among you, and

[001-043]if knighthood were hereditary, like a baronetcy, as it practically was in old

[001-044]times, when men were knighted from father to son, you would be Sir John

[001-045]now.”

[001-046]“Ye don’t say so!”

[001-047]“In short,” concluded the parson, decisively smacking his leg with

[001-048]his switch, “there’s hardly such another family in England.”

[001-049]“Daze my eyes, and isn’t there?” said Durbeyfield. “And

[001-050]here have I been knocking about, year after year, from pillar to post, as if I

[001-051]was no more than the commonest feller in the parish.... And how long hev

[001-052]this news about me been knowed, Pa’son Tringham?”

[001-053]The clergyman explained that, as far as he was aware, it had quite died out of

[001-054]knowledge, and could hardly be said to be known at all. His own investigations

[001-055]had begun on a day in the preceding spring when, having been engaged in tracing

[001-056]the vicissitudes of the d’Urberville family, he had observed

[001-057]Durbeyfield’s name on his waggon, and had thereupon been led to make

[001-058]inquiries about his father and grandfather till he had no doubt on the subject.

[001-059]“At first I resolved not to disturb you with such a useless piece of

[001-060]information,” said he. “However, our impulses are too strong for

[001-061]our judgement sometimes. I thought you might perhaps know something of it all

[001-062]the while.”

[001-063]“Well, I have heard once or twice, ’tis true, that my family had

[001-064]seen better days afore they came to Blackmoor. But I took no notice o’t,

[001-065]thinking it to mean that we had once kept two horses where we now keep only

[001-066]one. I’ve got a wold silver spoon, and a wold graven seal at home, too;

[001-067]but, Lord, what’s a spoon and seal?... And to think that I and these

[001-068]noble d’Urbervilles were one flesh all the time. ’Twas said that my

[001-069]gr’t-granfer had secrets, and didn’t care to talk of where he came

[001-070]from.... And where do we raise our smoke, now, parson, if I may make so

[001-071]bold; I mean, where do we d’Urbervilles live?”

[001-072]“You don’t live anywhere. You are extinct—as a county

[001-073]family.”

[001-074]“That’s bad.”

[001-075]“Yes—what the mendacious family chronicles call extinct in the male

[001-076]line—that is, gone down—gone under.”

[001-077]“Then where do we lie?”

[001-078]“At Kingsbere-sub-Greenhill: rows and rows of you in your vaults, with

[001-079]your effigies under Purbeck-marble canopies.”

[001-080]“And where be our family mansions and estates?”

[001-081]“You haven’t any.”

[001-082]“Oh? No lands neither?”

[001-083]“None; though you once had ’em in abundance, as I said, for your

[001-084]family consisted of numerous branches. In this county there was a seat of yours

[001-085]at Kingsbere, and another at Sherton, and another in Millpond, and another at

[001-086]Lullstead, and another at Wellbridge.”

[001-087]“And shall we ever come into our own again?”

[001-088]“Ah—that I can’t tell!”

[001-089]“And what had I better do about it, sir?” asked Durbeyfield, after

[001-090]a pause.

[001-091]“Oh—nothing, nothing; except chasten yourself with the thought of

[001-092]‘how are the mighty fallen.’ It is a fact of some interest to the

[001-093]local historian and genealogist, nothing more. There are several families among

[001-094]the cottagers of this county of almost equal lustre. Good night.”

[001-095]“But you’ll turn back and have a quart of beer wi’ me on the

[001-096]strength o’t, Pa’son Tringham? There’s a very pretty brew in

[001-097]tap at The Pure Drop—though, to be sure, not so good as at

[001-098]Rolliver’s.”

[001-099]“No, thank you—not this evening, Durbeyfield. You’ve had

[001-100]enough already.” Concluding thus, the parson rode on his way, with doubts

[001-101]as to his discretion in retailing this curious bit of lore.

[001-102]When he was gone, Durbeyfield walked a few steps in a profound reverie, and

[001-103]then sat down upon the grassy bank by the roadside, depositing his basket

[001-104]before him. In a few minutes a youth appeared in the distance, walking in the

[001-105]same direction as that which had been pursued by Durbeyfield. The latter, on

[001-106]seeing him, held up his hand, and the lad quickened his pace and came near.

[001-107]“Boy, take up that basket! I want ’ee to go on an errand for

[001-108]me.”

[001-109]The lath-like stripling frowned. “Who be you, then, John Durbeyfield, to

[001-110]order me about and call me ‘boy’? You know my name as well as I

[001-111]know yours!”

[001-112]“Do you, do you? That’s the secret—that’s the secret!

[001-113]Now obey my orders, and take the message I’m going to charge ’ee

[001-114]wi’... Well, Fred, I don’t mind telling you that the secret is

[001-115]that I’m one of a noble race—it has been just found out by me this

[001-116]present afternoon, p.m.” And as he made the announcement, Durbeyfield,

[001-117]declining from his sitting position, luxuriously stretched himself out upon the

[001-118]bank among the daisies.

[001-119]The lad stood before Durbeyfield, and contemplated his length from crown to

[001-120]toe.

[001-121]“Sir John d’Urberville—that’s who I am,”

[001-122]continued the prostrate man. “That is if knights were

[001-123]baronets—which they be. ’Tis recorded in history all about me. Dost

[001-124]know of such a place, lad, as Kingsbere-sub-Greenhill?”

[001-125]“Ees. I’ve been there to Greenhill Fair.”

[001-126]“Well, under the church of that city there lie—”

[001-127]“’Tisn’t a city, the place I mean; leastwise

[001-128]’twaddn’ when I was there—’twas a little one-eyed,

[001-129]blinking sort o’ place.”

[001-130]“Never you mind the place, boy, that’s not the question before us.

[001-131]Under the church of that there parish lie my ancestors—hundreds of

[001-132]’em—in coats of mail and jewels, in gr’t lead coffins

[001-133]weighing tons and tons. There’s not a man in the county o’

[001-134]South-Wessex that’s got grander and nobler skillentons in his family than

[001-135]I.”

[001-136]“Oh?”

[001-137]“Now take up that basket, and goo on to Marlott, and when you’ve

[001-138]come to The Pure Drop Inn, tell ’em to send a horse and carriage to me

[001-139]immed’ately, to carry me hwome. And in the bottom o’ the carriage

[001-140]they be to put a noggin o’ rum in a small bottle, and chalk it up to my

[001-141]account. And when you’ve done that goo on to my house with the basket,

[001-142]and tell my wife to put away that washing, because she needn’t finish it,

[001-143]and wait till I come hwome, as I’ve news to tell her.”

[001-144]As the lad stood in a dubious attitude, Durbeyfield put his hand in his pocket,

[001-145]and produced a shilling, one of the chronically few that he possessed.

[001-146]“Here’s for your labour, lad.”

[001-147]This made a difference in the young man’s estimate of the position.

[001-148]“Yes, Sir John. Thank ’ee. Anything else I can do for ’ee,

[001-149]Sir John?”

[001-150]“Tell ’em at hwome that I should like for supper,—well,

[001-151]lamb’s fry if they can get it; and if they can’t, black-pot; and if

[001-152]they can’t get that, well chitterlings will do.”

[001-153]“Yes, Sir John.”

[001-154]The boy took up the basket, and as he set out the notes of a brass band were

[001-155]heard from the direction of the village.

[001-156]“What’s that?” said Durbeyfield. “Not on account

[001-157]o’ I?”

[001-158]“’Tis the women’s club-walking, Sir John. Why, your

[001-159]da’ter is one o’ the members.”

[001-160]“To be sure—I’d quite forgot it in my thoughts of greater

[001-161]things! Well, vamp on to Marlott, will ye, and order that carriage, and maybe

[001-162]I’ll drive round and inspect the club.”

[001-163]The lad departed, and Durbeyfield lay waiting on the grass and daisies in the

[001-164]evening sun. Not a soul passed that way for a long while, and the faint notes

[001-165]of the band were the only human sounds audible within the rim of blue hills.
