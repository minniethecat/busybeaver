[022-001]They came downstairs yawning next morning; but skimming and milking were

[022-002]proceeded with as usual, and they went indoors to breakfast. Dairyman Crick was

[022-003]discovered stamping about the house. He had received a letter, in which a

[022-004]customer had complained that the butter had a twang.

[022-005]“And begad, so ’t have!” said the dairyman, who held in his

[022-006]left hand a wooden slice on which a lump of butter was stuck.

[022-007]“Yes—taste for yourself!”

[022-008]Several of them gathered round him; and Mr Clare tasted, Tess tasted, also the

[022-009]other indoor milkmaids, one or two of the milking-men, and last of all Mrs

[022-010]Crick, who came out from the waiting breakfast-table. There certainly was a

[022-011]twang.

[022-012]The dairyman, who had thrown himself into abstraction to better realize the

[022-013]taste, and so divine the particular species of noxious weed to which it

[022-014]appertained, suddenly exclaimed—

[022-015]“’Tis garlic! and I thought there wasn’t a blade left in that

[022-016]mead!”

[022-017]Then all the old hands remembered that a certain dry mead, into which a few of

[022-018]the cows had been admitted of late, had, in years gone by, spoilt the butter in

[022-019]the same way. The dairyman had not recognized the taste at that time, and

[022-020]thought the butter bewitched.

[022-021]“We must overhaul that mead,” he resumed; “this mustn’t

[022-022]continny!”

[022-023]All having armed themselves with old pointed knives, they went out together. As

[022-024]the inimical plant could only be present in very microscopic dimensions to have

[022-025]escaped ordinary observation, to find it seemed rather a hopeless attempt in

[022-026]the stretch of rich grass before them. However, they formed themselves into

[022-027]line, all assisting, owing to the importance of the search; the dairyman at the

[022-028]upper end with Mr Clare, who had volunteered to help; then Tess, Marian, Izz

[022-029]Huett, and Retty; then Bill Lewell, Jonathan, and the married

[022-030]dairywomen—Beck Knibbs, with her wooly black hair and rolling eyes; and

[022-031]flaxen Frances, consumptive from the winter damps of the water-meads—who

[022-032]lived in their respective cottages.

[022-033]With eyes fixed upon the ground they crept slowly across a strip of the field,

[022-034]returning a little further down in such a manner that, when they should have

[022-035]finished, not a single inch of the pasture but would have fallen under the eye

[022-036]of some one of them. It was a most tedious business, not more than half a dozen

[022-037]shoots of garlic being discoverable in the whole field; yet such was the

[022-038]herb’s pungency that probably one bite of it by one cow had been

[022-039]sufficient to season the whole dairy’s produce for the day.

[022-040]Differing one from another in natures and moods so greatly as they did, they

[022-041]yet formed, bending, a curiously uniform row—automatic, noiseless; and an

[022-042]alien observer passing down the neighbouring lane might well have been excused

[022-043]for massing them as “Hodge”. As they crept along, stooping low to

[022-044]discern the plant, a soft yellow gleam was reflected from the buttercups into

[022-045]their shaded faces, giving them an elfish, moonlit aspect, though the sun was

[022-046]pouring upon their backs in all the strength of noon.

[022-047]Angel Clare, who communistically stuck to his rule of taking part with the rest

[022-048]in everything, glanced up now and then. It was not, of course, by accident that

[022-049]he walked next to Tess.

[022-050]“Well, how are you?” he murmured.

[022-051]“Very well, thank you, sir,” she replied demurely.

[022-052]As they had been discussing a score of personal matters only half-an-hour

[022-053]before, the introductory style seemed a little superfluous. But they got no

[022-054]further in speech just then. They crept and crept, the hem of her petticoat

[022-055]just touching his gaiter, and his elbow sometimes brushing hers. At last the

[022-056]dairyman, who came next, could stand it no longer.

[022-057]“Upon my soul and body, this here stooping do fairly make my back open

[022-058]and shut!” he exclaimed, straightening himself slowly with an excruciated

[022-059]look till quite upright. “And you, maidy Tess, you wasn’t well a

[022-060]day or two ago—this will make your head ache finely! Don’t do any

[022-061]more, if you feel fainty; leave the rest to finish it.”

[022-062]Dairyman Crick withdrew, and Tess dropped behind. Mr Clare also stepped out of

[022-063]line, and began privateering about for the weed. When she found him near her,

[022-064]her very tension at what she had heard the night before made her the first to

[022-065]speak.

[022-066]“Don’t they look pretty?” she said.

[022-067]“Who?”

[022-068]“Izzy Huett and Retty.”

[022-069]Tess had moodily decided that either of these maidens would make a good

[022-070]farmer’s wife, and that she ought to recommend them, and obscure her own

[022-071]wretched charms.

[022-072]“Pretty? Well, yes—they are pretty girls—fresh looking. I

[022-073]have often thought so.”

[022-074]“Though, poor dears, prettiness won’t last long!”

[022-075]“O no, unfortunately.”

[022-076]“They are excellent dairywomen.”

[022-077]“Yes: though not better than you.”

[022-078]“They skim better than I.”

[022-079]“Do they?”

[022-080]Clare remained observing them—not without their observing him.

[022-081]“She is colouring up,” continued Tess heroically.

[022-082]“Who?”

[022-083]“Retty Priddle.”

[022-084]“Oh! Why it that?”

[022-085]“Because you are looking at her.”

[022-086]Self-sacrificing as her mood might be, Tess could not well go further and cry,

[022-087]“Marry one of them, if you really do want a dairywoman and not a lady;

[022-088]and don’t think of marrying me!” She followed Dairyman Crick, and

[022-089]had the mournful satisfaction of seeing that Clare remained behind.

[022-090]From this day she forced herself to take pains to avoid him—never

[022-091]allowing herself, as formerly, to remain long in his company, even if their

[022-092]juxtaposition were purely accidental. She gave the other three every chance.

[022-093]Tess was woman enough to realize from their avowals to herself that Angel Clare

[022-094]had the honour of all the dairymaids in his keeping, and her perception of his

[022-095]care to avoid compromising the happiness of either in the least degree bred a

[022-096]tender respect in Tess for what she deemed, rightly or wrongly, the

[022-097]self-controlling sense of duty shown by him, a quality which she had never

[022-098]expected to find in one of the opposite sex, and in the absence of which more

[022-099]than one of the simple hearts who were his house-mates might have gone weeping

[022-100]on her pilgrimage.
