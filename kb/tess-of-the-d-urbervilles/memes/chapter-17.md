[017-001]The dairymaids and men had flocked down from their cottages and out of the

[017-002]dairy-house with the arrival of the cows from the meads; the maids walking in

[017-003]pattens, not on account of the weather, but to keep their shoes above the mulch

[017-004]of the barton. Each girl sat down on her three-legged stool, her face sideways,

[017-005]her right cheek resting against the cow, and looked musingly along the

[017-006]animal’s flank at Tess as she approached. The male milkers, with

[017-007]hat-brims turned down, resting flat on their foreheads and gazing on the

[017-008]ground, did not observe her.

[017-009]One of these was a sturdy middle-aged man—whose long white

[017-010]“pinner” was somewhat finer and cleaner than the wraps of the

[017-011]others, and whose jacket underneath had a presentable marketing

[017-012]aspect—the master-dairyman, of whom she was in quest, his double

[017-013]character as a working milker and butter maker here during six days, and on the

[017-014]seventh as a man in shining broad-cloth in his family pew at church, being so

[017-015]marked as to have inspired a rhyme:

[017-016]Dairyman Dick

[017-017]All the week:

[017-018]On Sundays Mister Richard Crick.

[017-019]Seeing Tess standing at gaze he went across to her.

[017-020]The majority of dairymen have a cross manner at milking time, but it happened

[017-021]that Mr Crick was glad to get a new hand—for the days were busy ones

[017-022]now—and he received her warmly; inquiring for her mother and the rest of

[017-023]the family—(though this as a matter of form merely, for in reality he had

[017-024]not been aware of Mrs Durbeyfield’s existence till apprised of the fact

[017-025]by a brief business-letter about Tess).

[017-026]“Oh—ay, as a lad I knowed your part o’ the country very

[017-027]well,” he said terminatively. “Though I’ve never been there

[017-028]since. And a aged woman of ninety that use to live nigh here, but is dead and

[017-029]gone long ago, told me that a family of some such name as yours in Blackmoor

[017-030]Vale came originally from these parts, and that ’twere a old ancient race

[017-031]that had all but perished off the earth—though the new generations

[017-032]didn’t know it. But, Lord, I took no notice of the old woman’s

[017-033]ramblings, not I.”

[017-034]“Oh no—it is nothing,” said Tess.

[017-035]Then the talk was of business only.

[017-036]“You can milk ’em clean, my maidy? I don’t want my cows going

[017-037]azew at this time o’ year.”

[017-038]She reassured him on that point, and he surveyed her up and down. She had been

[017-039]staying indoors a good deal, and her complexion had grown delicate.

[017-040]“Quite sure you can stand it? ’Tis comfortable enough here for

[017-041]rough folk; but we don’t live in a cowcumber frame.”

[017-042]She declared that she could stand it, and her zest and willingness seemed to

[017-043]win him over.

[017-044]“Well, I suppose you’ll want a dish o’ tay, or victuals of

[017-045]some sort, hey? Not yet? Well, do as ye like about it. But faith, if

[017-046]’twas I, I should be as dry as a kex wi’ travelling so far.”

[017-047]“I’ll begin milking now, to get my hand in,” said Tess.

[017-048]She drank a little milk as temporary refreshment—to the

[017-049]surprise—indeed, slight contempt—of Dairyman Crick, to whose mind

[017-050]it had apparently never occurred that milk was good as a beverage.

[017-051]“Oh, if ye can swaller that, be it so,” he said indifferently,

[017-052]while holding up the pail that she sipped from. “’Tis what I

[017-053]hain’t touched for years—not I. Rot the stuff; it would lie in my

[017-054]innerds like lead. You can try your hand upon she,” he pursued, nodding

[017-055]to the nearest cow. “Not but what she do milk rather hard. We’ve

[017-056]hard ones and we’ve easy ones, like other folks. However, you’ll

[017-057]find out that soon enough.”

[017-058]When Tess had changed her bonnet for a hood, and was really on her stool under

[017-059]the cow, and the milk was squirting from her fists into the pail, she appeared

[017-060]to feel that she really had laid a new foundation for her future. The

[017-061]conviction bred serenity, her pulse slowed, and she was able to look about her.

[017-062]The milkers formed quite a little battalion of men and maids, the men operating

[017-063]on the hard-teated animals, the maids on the kindlier natures. It was a large

[017-064]dairy. There were nearly a hundred milchers under Crick’s management, all

[017-065]told; and of the herd the master-dairyman milked six or eight with his own

[017-066]hands, unless away from home. These were the cows that milked hardest of all;

[017-067]for his journey-milkmen being more or less casually hired, he would not entrust

[017-068]this half-dozen to their treatment, lest, from indifference, they should not

[017-069]milk them fully; nor to the maids, lest they should fail in the same way for

[017-070]lack of finger-grip; with the result that in course of time the cows would

[017-071]“go azew”—that is, dry up. It was not the loss for the moment

[017-072]that made slack milking so serious, but that with the decline of demand there

[017-073]came decline, and ultimately cessation, of supply.

[017-074]After Tess had settled down to her cow there was for a time no talk in the

[017-075]barton, and not a sound interfered with the purr of the milk-jets into the

[017-076]numerous pails, except a momentary exclamation to one or other of the beasts

[017-077]requesting her to turn round or stand still. The only movements were those of

[017-078]the milkers’ hands up and down, and the swing of the cows’ tails.

[017-079]Thus they all worked on, encompassed by the vast flat mead which extended to

[017-080]either slope of the valley—a level landscape compounded of old landscapes

[017-081]long forgotten, and, no doubt, differing in character very greatly from the

[017-082]landscape they composed now.

[017-083]“To my thinking,” said the dairyman, rising suddenly from a cow he

[017-084]had just finished off, snatching up his three-legged stool in one hand and the

[017-085]pail in the other, and moving on to the next hard-yielder in his vicinity,

[017-086]“to my thinking, the cows don’t gie down their milk to-day as

[017-087]usual. Upon my life, if Winker do begin keeping back like this, she’ll

[017-088]not be worth going under by midsummer.”

[017-089]“’Tis because there’s a new hand come among us,” said

[017-090]Jonathan Kail. “I’ve noticed such things afore.”

[017-091]“To be sure. It may be so. I didn’t think o’t.”

[017-092]“I’ve been told that it goes up into their horns at such

[017-093]times,” said a dairymaid.

[017-094]“Well, as to going up into their horns,” replied Dairyman Crick

[017-095]dubiously, as though even witchcraft might be limited by anatomical

[017-096]possibilities, “I couldn’t say; I certainly could not. But as nott

[017-097]cows will keep it back as well as the horned ones, I don’t quite agree to

[017-098]it. Do ye know that riddle about the nott cows, Jonathan? Why do nott cows give

[017-099]less milk in a year than horned?”

[017-100]“I don’t!” interposed the milkmaid, “Why do

[017-101]they?”

[017-102]“Because there bain’t so many of ’em,” said the

[017-103]dairyman. “Howsomever, these gam’sters do certainly keep back their

[017-104]milk to-day. Folks, we must lift up a stave or two—that’s the only

[017-105]cure for’t.”

[017-106]Songs were often resorted to in dairies hereabout as an enticement to the cows

[017-107]when they showed signs of withholding their usual yield; and the band of

[017-108]milkers at this request burst into melody—in purely business-like tones,

[017-109]it is true, and with no great spontaneity; the result, according to their own

[017-110]belief, being a decided improvement during the song’s continuance. When

[017-111]they had gone through fourteen or fifteen verses of a cheerful ballad about a

[017-112]murderer who was afraid to go to bed in the dark because he saw certain

[017-113]brimstone flames around him, one of the male milkers said—

[017-114]“I wish singing on the stoop didn’t use up so much of a man’s

[017-115]wind! You should get your harp, sir; not but what a fiddle is best.”

[017-116]Tess, who had given ear to this, thought the words were addressed to the

[017-117]dairyman, but she was wrong. A reply, in the shape of “Why?” came

[017-118]as it were out of the belly of a dun cow in the stalls; it had been spoken by a

[017-119]milker behind the animal, whom she had not hitherto perceived.

[017-120]“Oh yes; there’s nothing like a fiddle,” said the dairyman.

[017-121]“Though I do think that bulls are more moved by a tune than cows—at

[017-122]least that’s my experience. Once there was an old aged man over at

[017-123]Mellstock—William Dewy by name—one of the family that used to do a

[017-124]good deal of business as tranters over there—Jonathan, do ye

[017-125]mind?—I knowed the man by sight as well as I know my own brother, in a

[017-126]manner of speaking. Well, this man was a coming home along from a wedding,

[017-127]where he had been playing his fiddle, one fine moonlight night, and for

[017-128]shortness’ sake he took a cut across Forty-acres, a field lying that way,

[017-129]where a bull was out to grass. The bull seed William, and took after him, horns

[017-130]aground, begad; and though William runned his best, and hadn’t *much* drink in him (considering ’twas a wedding, and the folks well

[017-131]off), he found he’d never reach the fence and get over in time to save

[017-132]himself. Well, as a last thought, he pulled out his fiddle as he runned, and

[017-133]struck up a jig, turning to the bull, and backing towards the corner. The bull

[017-134]softened down, and stood still, looking hard at William Dewy, who fiddled on

[017-135]and on; till a sort of a smile stole over the bull’s face. But no sooner

[017-136]did William stop his playing and turn to get over hedge than the bull would

[017-137]stop his smiling and lower his horns towards the seat of William’s

[017-138]breeches. Well, William had to turn about and play on, willy-nilly; and

[017-139]’twas only three o’clock in the world, and ’a knowed that

[017-140]nobody would come that way for hours, and he so leery and tired that ’a

[017-141]didn’t know what to do. When he had scraped till about four o’clock

[017-142]he felt that he verily would have to give over soon, and he said to himself,

[017-143]‘There’s only this last tune between me and eternal welfare! Heaven

[017-144]save me, or I’m a done man.’ Well, then he called to mind how

[017-145]he’d seen the cattle kneel o’ Christmas Eves in the dead o’

[017-146]night. It was not Christmas Eve then, but it came into his head to play a trick

[017-147]upon the bull. So he broke into the ’Tivity Hymm, just as at Christmas

[017-148]carol-singing; when, lo and behold, down went the bull on his bended knees, in

[017-149]his ignorance, just as if ’twere the true ’Tivity night and hour.

[017-150]As soon as his horned friend were down, William turned, clinked off like a

[017-151]long-dog, and jumped safe over hedge, before the praying bull had got on his

[017-152]feet again to take after him. William used to say that he’d seen a man

[017-153]look a fool a good many times, but never such a fool as that bull looked when

[017-154]he found his pious feelings had been played upon, and ’twas not Christmas

[017-155]Eve. Yes, William Dewy, that was the man’s name; and I can tell you to a

[017-156]foot where’s he a-lying in Mellstock Churchyard at this very

[017-157]moment—just between the second yew-tree and the north aisle.”

[017-158]“It’s a curious story; it carries us back to medieval times, when

[017-159]faith was a living thing!”

[017-160]The remark, singular for a dairy-yard, was murmured by the voice behind the dun

[017-161]cow; but as nobody understood the reference, no notice was taken, except that

[017-162]the narrator seemed to think it might imply scepticism as to his tale.

[017-163]“Well, ’tis quite true, sir, whether or no. I knowed the man

[017-164]well.”

[017-165]“Oh yes; I have no doubt of it,” said the person behind the dun

[017-166]cow.

[017-167]Tess’s attention was thus attracted to the dairyman’s interlocutor,

[017-168]of whom she could see but the merest patch, owing to his burying his head so

[017-169]persistently in the flank of the milcher. She could not understand why he

[017-170]should be addressed as “sir” even by the dairyman himself. But no

[017-171]explanation was discernible; he remained under the cow long enough to have

[017-172]milked three, uttering a private ejaculation now and then, as if he could not

[017-173]get on.

[017-174]“Take it gentle, sir; take it gentle,” said the dairyman.

[017-175]“’Tis knack, not strength, that does it.”

[017-176]“So I find,” said the other, standing up at last and stretching his

[017-177]arms. “I think I have finished her, however, though she made my fingers

[017-178]ache.”

[017-179]Tess could then see him at full length. He wore the ordinary white pinner and

[017-180]leather leggings of a dairy-farmer when milking, and his boots were clogged

[017-181]with the mulch of the yard; but this was all his local livery. Beneath it was

[017-182]something educated, reserved, subtle, sad, differing.

[017-183]But the details of his aspect were temporarily thrust aside by the discovery

[017-184]that he was one whom she had seen before. Such vicissitudes had Tess passed

[017-185]through since that time that for a moment she could not remember where she had

[017-186]met him; and then it flashed upon her that he was the pedestrian who had joined

[017-187]in the club-dance at Marlott—the passing stranger who had come she knew

[017-188]not whence, had danced with others but not with her, and slightingly left her,

[017-189]and gone on his way with his friends.

[017-190]The flood of memories brought back by this revival of an incident anterior to

[017-191]her troubles produced a momentary dismay lest, recognizing her also, he should

[017-192]by some means discover her story. But it passed away when she found no sign of

[017-193]remembrance in him. She saw by degrees that since their first and only

[017-194]encounter his mobile face had grown more thoughtful, and had acquired a young

[017-195]man’s shapely moustache and beard—the latter of the palest straw

[017-196]colour where it began upon his cheeks, and deepening to a warm brown farther

[017-197]from its root. Under his linen milking-pinner he wore a dark velveteen jacket,

[017-198]cord breeches and gaiters, and a starched white shirt. Without the milking-gear

[017-199]nobody could have guessed what he was. He might with equal probability have

[017-200]been an eccentric landowner or a gentlemanly ploughman. That he was but a

[017-201]novice at dairy work she had realized in a moment, from the time he had spent

[017-202]upon the milking of one cow.

[017-203]Meanwhile many of the milkmaids had said to one another of the newcomer,

[017-204]“How pretty she is!” with something of real generosity and

[017-205]admiration, though with a half hope that the auditors would qualify the

[017-206]assertion—which, strictly speaking, they might have done, prettiness

[017-207]being an inexact definition of what struck the eye in Tess. When the milking

[017-208]was finished for the evening they straggled indoors, where Mrs Crick, the

[017-209]dairyman’s wife—who was too respectable to go out milking herself,

[017-210]and wore a hot stuff gown in warm weather because the dairymaids wore

[017-211]prints—was giving an eye to the leads and things.

[017-212]Only two or three of the maids, Tess learnt, slept in the dairy-house besides

[017-213]herself, most of the helpers going to their homes. She saw nothing at

[017-214]supper-time of the superior milker who had commented on the story, and asked no

[017-215]questions about him, the remainder of the evening being occupied in arranging

[017-216]her place in the bed-chamber. It was a large room over the milk-house, some

[017-217]thirty feet long; the sleeping-cots of the other three indoor milkmaids being

[017-218]in the same apartment. They were blooming young women, and, except one, rather

[017-219]older than herself. By bedtime Tess was thoroughly tired, and fell asleep

[017-220]immediately.

[017-221]But one of the girls, who occupied an adjoining bed, was more wakeful than

[017-222]Tess, and would insist upon relating to the latter various particulars of the

[017-223]homestead into which she had just entered. The girl’s whispered words

[017-224]mingled with the shades, and, to Tess’s drowsy mind, they seemed to be

[017-225]generated by the darkness in which they floated.

[017-226]“Mr Angel Clare—he that is learning milking, and that plays the

[017-227]harp—never says much to us. He is a pa’son’s son, and is too

[017-228]much taken up wi’ his own thoughts to notice girls. He is the

[017-229]dairyman’s pupil—learning farming in all its branches. He has

[017-230]learnt sheep-farming at another place, and he’s now mastering

[017-231]dairy-work.... Yes, he is quite the gentleman-born. His father is the Reverend

[017-232]Mr Clare at Emminster—a good many miles from here.”

[017-233]“Oh—I have heard of him,” said her companion, now awake.

[017-234]“A very earnest clergyman, is he not?”

[017-235]“Yes—that he is—the earnestest man in all Wessex, they

[017-236]say—the last of the old Low Church sort, they tell me—for all about

[017-237]here be what they call High. All his sons, except our Mr Clare, be made

[017-238]pa’sons too.”

[017-239]Tess had not at this hour the curiosity to ask why the present Mr Clare was not

[017-240]made a parson like his brethren, and gradually fell asleep again, the words of

[017-241]her informant coming to her along with the smell of the cheeses in the

[017-242]adjoining cheeseloft, and the measured dripping of the whey from the wrings

[017-243]downstairs.
