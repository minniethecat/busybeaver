[028-001]Her refusal, though unexpected, did not permanently daunt Clare. His experience

[028-002]of women was great enough for him to be aware that the negative often meant

[028-003]nothing more than the preface to the affirmative; and it was little enough for

[028-004]him not to know that in the manner of the present negative there lay a great

[028-005]exception to the dallyings of coyness. That she had already permitted him to

[028-006]make love to her he read as an additional assurance, not fully trowing that in

[028-007]the fields and pastures to “sigh gratis” is by no means deemed

[028-008]waste; love-making being here more often accepted inconsiderately and for its

[028-009]own sweet sake than in the carking, anxious homes of the ambitious, where a

[028-010]girl’s craving for an establishment paralyzes her healthy thought of a

[028-011]passion as an end.

[028-012]“Tess, why did you say ‘no’ in such a positive way?” he

[028-013]asked her in the course of a few days.

[028-014]She started.

[028-015]“Don’t ask me. I told you why—partly. I am not good

[028-016]enough—not worthy enough.”

[028-017]“How? Not fine lady enough?”

[028-018]“Yes—something like that,” murmured she. “Your friends

[028-019]would scorn me.”

[028-020]“Indeed, you mistake them—my father and mother. As for my brothers,

[028-021]I don’t care—” He clasped his fingers behind her back to keep

[028-022]her from slipping away. “Now—you did not mean it, sweet?—I am

[028-023]sure you did not! You have made me so restless that I cannot read, or play, or

[028-024]do anything. I am in no hurry, Tess, but I want to know—to hear from your

[028-025]own warm lips—that you will some day be mine—any time you may

[028-026]choose; but some day?”

[028-027]She could only shake her head and look away from him.

[028-028]Clare regarded her attentively, conned the characters of her face as if they

[028-029]had been hieroglyphics. The denial seemed real.

[028-030]“Then I ought not to hold you in this way—ought I? I have no right

[028-031]to you—no right to seek out where you are, or walk with you! Honestly,

[028-032]Tess, do you love any other man?”

[028-033]“How can you ask?” she said, with continued self-suppression.

[028-034]“I almost know that you do not. But then, why do you repulse me?”

[028-035]“I don’t repulse you. I like you to—tell me you love me; and

[028-036]you may always tell me so as you go about with me—and never offend

[028-037]me.”

[028-038]“But you will not accept me as a husband?”

[028-039]“Ah—that’s different—it is for your good, indeed, my

[028-040]dearest! O, believe me, it is only for your sake! I don’t like to give

[028-041]myself the great happiness o’ promising to be yours in that

[028-042]way—because—because I am *sure* I ought not to do it.”

[028-043]“But you will make me happy!”

[028-044]“Ah—you think so, but you don’t know!”

[028-045]At such times as this, apprehending the grounds of her refusal to be her modest

[028-046]sense of incompetence in matters social and polite, he would say that she was

[028-047]wonderfully well-informed and versatile—which was certainly true, her

[028-048]natural quickness and her admiration for him having led her to pick up his

[028-049]vocabulary, his accent, and fragments of his knowledge, to a surprising extent.

[028-050]After these tender contests and her victory she would go away by herself under

[028-051]the remotest cow, if at milking-time, or into the sedge or into her room, if at

[028-052]a leisure interval, and mourn silently, not a minute after an apparently

[028-053]phlegmatic negative.

[028-054]The struggle was so fearful; her own heart was so strongly on the side of

[028-055]his—two ardent hearts against one poor little conscience—that she

[028-056]tried to fortify her resolution by every means in her power. She had come to

[028-057]Talbothays with a made-up mind. On no account could she agree to a step which

[028-058]might afterwards cause bitter rueing to her husband for his blindness in

[028-059]wedding her. And she held that what her conscience had decided for her when her

[028-060]mind was unbiassed ought not to be overruled now.

[028-061]“Why don’t somebody tell him all about me?” she said.

[028-062]“It was only forty miles off—why hasn’t it reached here?

[028-063]Somebody must know!”

[028-064]Yet nobody seemed to know; nobody told him.

[028-065]For two or three days no more was said. She guessed from the sad countenances

[028-066]of her chamber companions that they regarded her not only as the favourite, but

[028-067]as the chosen; but they could see for themselves that she did not put herself

[028-068]in his way.

[028-069]Tess had never before known a time in which the thread of her life was so

[028-070]distinctly twisted of two strands, positive pleasure and positive pain. At the

[028-071]next cheese-making the pair were again left alone together. The dairyman

[028-072]himself had been lending a hand; but Mr Crick, as well as his wife, seemed

[028-073]latterly to have acquired a suspicion of mutual interest between these two;

[028-074]though they walked so circumspectly that suspicion was but of the faintest.

[028-075]Anyhow, the dairyman left them to themselves.

[028-076]They were breaking up the masses of curd before putting them into the vats. The

[028-077]operation resembled the act of crumbling bread on a large scale; and amid the

[028-078]immaculate whiteness of the curds Tess Durbeyfield’s hands showed

[028-079]themselves of the pinkness of the rose. Angel, who was filling the vats with

[028-080]his handful, suddenly ceased, and laid his hands flat upon hers. Her sleeves

[028-081]were rolled far above the elbow, and bending lower he kissed the inside vein of

[028-082]her soft arm.

[028-083]Although the early September weather was sultry, her arm, from her dabbling in

[028-084]the curds, was as cold and damp to his mouth as a new-gathered mushroom, and

[028-085]tasted of the whey. But she was such a sheaf of susceptibilities that her pulse

[028-086]was accelerated by the touch, her blood driven to her finger-ends, and the cool

[028-087]arms flushed hot. Then, as though her heart had said, “Is coyness longer

[028-088]necessary? Truth is truth between man and woman, as between man and man,”

[028-089]she lifted her eyes and they beamed devotedly into his, as her lip rose in a

[028-090]tender half-smile.

[028-091]“Do you know why I did that, Tess?” he said.

[028-092]“Because you love me very much!”

[028-093]“Yes, and as a preliminary to a new entreaty.”

[028-094]“Not *again* !”

[028-095]She looked a sudden fear that her resistance might break down under her own

[028-096]desire.

[028-097]“O, Tessy!” he went on, “I *cannot* think why you are so

[028-098]tantalizing. Why do you disappoint me so? You seem almost like a coquette, upon

[028-099]my life you do—a coquette of the first urban water! They blow hot and

[028-100]blow cold, just as you do, and it is the very last sort of thing to expect to

[028-101]find in a retreat like Talbothays.... And yet, dearest,” he quickly

[028-102]added, observing how the remark had cut her, “I know you to be the most

[028-103]honest, spotless creature that ever lived. So how can I suppose you a flirt?

[028-104]Tess, why don’t you like the idea of being my wife, if you love me as you

[028-105]seem to do?”

[028-106]“I have never said I don’t like the idea, and I never could say it;

[028-107]because—it isn’t true!”

[028-108]The stress now getting beyond endurance, her lip quivered, and she was obliged

[028-109]to go away. Clare was so pained and perplexed that he ran after and caught her

[028-110]in the passage.

[028-111]“Tell me, tell me!” he said, passionately clasping her, in

[028-112]forgetfulness of his curdy hands: “do tell me that you won’t belong

[028-113]to anybody but me!”

[028-114]“I will, I will tell you!” she exclaimed. “And I will give

[028-115]you a complete answer, if you will let me go now. I will tell you my

[028-116]experiences—all about myself—all!”

[028-117]“Your experiences, dear; yes, certainly; any number.” He expressed

[028-118]assent in loving satire, looking into her face. “My Tess, no doubt,

[028-119]almost as many experiences as that wild convolvulus out there on the garden

[028-120]hedge, that opened itself this morning for the first time. Tell me anything,

[028-121]but don’t use that wretched expression any more about not being worthy of

[028-122]me.”

[028-123]“I will try—not! And I’ll give you my reasons

[028-124]to-morrow—next week.”

[028-125]“Say on Sunday?”

[028-126]“Yes, on Sunday.”

[028-127]At last she got away, and did not stop in her retreat till she was in the

[028-128]thicket of pollard willows at the lower side of the barton, where she could be

[028-129]quite unseen. Here Tess flung herself down upon the rustling undergrowth of

[028-130]spear-grass, as upon a bed, and remained crouching in palpitating misery broken

[028-131]by momentary shoots of joy, which her fears about the ending could not

[028-132]altogether suppress.

[028-133]In reality, she was drifting into acquiescence. Every see-saw of her breath,

[028-134]every wave of her blood, every pulse singing in her ears, was a voice that

[028-135]joined with nature in revolt against her scrupulousness. Reckless,

[028-136]inconsiderate acceptance of him; to close with him at the altar, revealing

[028-137]nothing, and chancing discovery; to snatch ripe pleasure before the iron teeth

[028-138]of pain could have time to shut upon her: that was what love counselled; and in

[028-139]almost a terror of ecstasy Tess divined that, despite her many months of lonely

[028-140]self-chastisement, wrestlings, communings, schemes to lead a future of austere

[028-141]isolation, love’s counsel would prevail.

[028-142]The afternoon advanced, and still she remained among the willows. She heard the

[028-143]rattle of taking down the pails from the forked stands; the

[028-144]“waow-waow!” which accompanied the getting together of the cows.

[028-145]But she did not go to the milking. They would see her agitation; and the

[028-146]dairyman, thinking the cause to be love alone, would good-naturedly tease her;

[028-147]and that harassment could not be borne.

[028-148]Her lover must have guessed her overwrought state, and invented some excuse for

[028-149]her non-appearance, for no inquiries were made or calls given. At half-past six

[028-150]the sun settled down upon the levels with the aspect of a great forge in the

[028-151]heavens; and presently a monstrous pumpkin-like moon arose on the other hand.

[028-152]The pollard willows, tortured out of their natural shape by incessant

[028-153]choppings, became spiny-haired monsters as they stood up against it. She went

[028-154]in and upstairs without a light.

[028-155]It was now Wednesday. Thursday came, and Angel looked thoughtfully at her from

[028-156]a distance, but intruded in no way upon her. The indoor milkmaids, Marian and

[028-157]the rest, seemed to guess that something definite was afoot, for they did not

[028-158]force any remarks upon her in the bedchamber. Friday passed; Saturday.

[028-159]To-morrow was the day.

[028-160]“I shall give way—I shall say yes—I shall let myself marry

[028-161]him—I cannot help it!” she jealously panted, with her hot face to

[028-162]the pillow that night, on hearing one of the other girls sigh his name in her

[028-163]sleep. “I can’t bear to let anybody have him but me! Yet it is a

[028-164]wrong to him, and may kill him when he knows! O my

[028-165]heart—O—O—O!”
