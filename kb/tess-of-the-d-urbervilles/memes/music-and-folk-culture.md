# Music and Folk Culture

## Overview

Music pervades the world of _Tess of the d'Urbervilles_ — from the psalm-singing at [[marlott|Marlott]] church and the ballads of the harvest field to [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s harp at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] and the raucous songs at Chaseborough. Hardy presents folk music as both the living expression of rural culture and a fragile inheritance threatened by the encroachments of industrial modernity.

## Angel's Harp

Angel Clare's harp-playing at Talbothays functions as the novel's central musical symbol. His playing draws [[Tess Durbeyfield]] to him across the garden: "the floating, ethereal notes of a harp" create a space of enchantment in which class distinctions and social anxieties momentarily dissolve. Music here represents a realm of pure feeling — unmediated, transcendent — that the social world cannot sustain. Hardy describes how "the harmonies passed like breezes" (019-067) through the garden air, Tess listening "like a fascinated bird" to Angel's harping. It is one of the novel's most potent evocations of love's promise and its fragility.

The scene is elaborately synesthetic: "the floating pollen seemed to be his notes made visible" (019-068), dissolving the boundary between sound and sight, the natural and the aesthetic. Tess first heard "those notes in the attic above her head" (019-047) — "dim, flattened" — before following them into the garden where Angel played. Her nighttime listening to his harp becomes a ritual of longing and wonder, the music promising a world beyond the dairy's daily labour. As she tells him: "you, sir, can raise up dreams with your music, and drive all such horrid fancies away" (019-108). The harp stands as both instrument of courtship and symbol of an elevated, cultivated sensibility that Angel brings into the rural world, its refinements at once attractive and alien to Tess.

## Ballad, Dance, and Communal Song

The novel is rich with communal musical forms: the May-Day dance at Marlott, the harvest-home songs, the ballads sung at the dairy. (002-001–002-040) [[dairyman-crick|Dairyman Crick]]'s stories — themselves a form of oral performance — are interspersed with musical fragments. The ballads that Tess and the other dairymaids sing serve as a collective emotional language, expressing longings and sorrows that individual speech cannot. The fiddle-playing at the Marlott gatherings and the tavern songs at Chaseborough create spaces of communal pleasure that stand in defiant contrast to the repressive moral order. (010-047–010-066)

The May-Day dance in Chapter 2 serves as the novel's opening tableau of communal music and dance. "The May-Day dance" (002-038) brings the young women of Marlott together in white frocks, walking in procession and dancing on the green. The encounter is freighted with significance when [[Angel Clare]] and his brothers pass by: Angel dances briefly with some of the girls but misses Tess, and "as he fell out of the dance his eyes lighted on Tess Durbeyfield, whose own large orbs wore a rounder, juicier look as she lingered, looking back" (002-202). This missed dance becomes a recurring motif of regret — Angel later recalls at Talbothays: "you danced on the green… O, why didn't I stay and speak to you?" (031-157).

At Chaseborough, the dance becomes a site of dangerous intoxication. Tess approaches the hay-trussers and hears "the fiddled notes of a reel" (010-047), where "the stamping drowned the music" (010-050). Hardy renders the scene in elemental, almost botanical terms: "the muted fiddles feebly pushed their notes, in marked contrast to the passion of the dancers… they danced, and laughed as they coughed" (010-068, 010-070). The dance here marks a threshold between Marlott's innocent May-Day and the sexual menace that awaits Tess at Trantridge.

## The Fiddle and the Rural Musician

Hardy populates his Wessex with musicians: the village fiddler at Marlott, the singers at the dairy, the ballad-singers at the fair. These figures represent a tradition of amateur, communal music-making that was already vanishing in Hardy's time. The fiddle — portable, adaptable, folk — is the instrument of this tradition. When Angel plays his harp, he imports a more cultivated musical tradition into the dairy; the tension between his "classical" music and the folk music of the labourers is one expression of the wider class dynamics at Talbothays. (019-060–019-070)

The rural musician is a figure of continuity and conviviality. Joan Durbeyfield "sung songs at The Pure Drop till past eleven" (038-019), her voice carrying the same untutored vitality as the village fiddlers. Even at the moment of her deepest disgrace, Tess hears "her mother singing by the back door" (038-039) — music persisting through poverty and shame as a stubborn assertion of life.

## Tess's Ballads and Musical Memory

Music becomes for Tess a language of longing and memory after Angel's departure. In his absence, she practises the ballads he loved. Hardy records this with devastating precision: "She wished that she had taken more notice of the tunes he played on his harp, that she had inquired more curiously of him which were his favourite ballads" (049-161, 049-162). "To perfect the ballads was now her whimsical desire" (049-171) — a doomed act of devotion to a husband who has abandoned her.

The ballads become a measure of her isolation. She sings "her foolish little songs with scarce now a hope that Clare would ever hear them" (050-117). When she finally does sing them, Hardy writes: "It would have melted the heart of a stone to hear her singing these ditties" (049-181). Yet "the simple silly words of the songs" keep "resounding in painful mockery of the aching heart of the singer" (049-185). The folk ballad — communal in origin, shared in performance — becomes a solitary lament, its traditional function inverted by Tess's social isolation.

Earlier, on her journey to Talbothays, Tess had already discovered the limits of music as consolation: "She tried several ballads, but found them inadequate" (016-091). The ballads could not express what she carried. This pattern — turning to song, finding it insufficient, yet returning to it — runs through Tess's entire story, a testament to the centrality of music in her emotional life and its ultimate inability to alter the forces arrayed against her.

## Music as Memory and Loss

For Angel, the memory of Tess's voice — "the voice of a woman who was to him like the voice of the April breeze" — becomes a torment after their separation. He recalls "the tunes he played on his harp" as an emblem of what is forever lost. Music in the novel is inseparable from memory and loss: it marks what has been and cannot be recovered. The folk culture that Hardy lovingly documents is, by the time of the novel's writing, already vanishing under the pressure of modernization. The novel itself becomes an act of musical preservation — a written record of songs and dances that would otherwise be lost to silence.
