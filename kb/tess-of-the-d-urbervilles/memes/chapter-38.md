[038-001]As she drove on through Blackmoor Vale, and the landscape of her youth began to

[038-002]open around her, Tess aroused herself from her stupor. Her first thought was

[038-003]how would she be able to face her parents?

[038-004]She reached a turnpike-gate which stood upon the highway to the village. It was

[038-005]thrown open by a stranger, not by the old man who had kept it for many years,

[038-006]and to whom she had been known; he had probably left on New Year’s Day,

[038-007]the date when such changes were made. Having received no intelligence lately

[038-008]from her home, she asked the turnpike-keeper for news.

[038-009]“Oh—nothing, miss,” he answered. “Marlott is Marlott

[038-010]still. Folks have died and that. John Durbeyfield, too, hev had a daughter

[038-011]married this week to a gentleman-farmer; not from John’s own house, you

[038-012]know; they was married elsewhere; the gentleman being of that high standing

[038-013]that John’s own folk was not considered well-be-doing enough to have any

[038-014]part in it, the bridegroom seeming not to know how’t have been discovered

[038-015]that John is a old and ancient nobleman himself by blood, with family

[038-016]skillentons in their own vaults to this day, but done out of his property in

[038-017]the time o’ the Romans. However, Sir John, as we call ’n now, kept

[038-018]up the wedding-day as well as he could, and stood treat to everybody in the

[038-019]parish; and John’s wife sung songs at The Pure Drop till past eleven

[038-020]o’clock.”

[038-021]Hearing this, Tess felt so sick at heart that she could not decide to go home

[038-022]publicly in the fly with her luggage and belongings. She asked the

[038-023]turnpike-keeper if she might deposit her things at his house for a while, and,

[038-024]on his offering no objection, she dismissed her carriage, and went on to the

[038-025]village alone by a back lane.

[038-026]At sight of her father’s chimney she asked herself how she could possibly

[038-027]enter the house? Inside that cottage her relations were calmly supposing her

[038-028]far away on a wedding-tour with a comparatively rich man, who was to conduct

[038-029]her to bouncing prosperity; while here she was, friendless, creeping up to the

[038-030]old door quite by herself, with no better place to go to in the world.

[038-031]She did not reach the house unobserved. Just by the garden-hedge she was met by

[038-032]a girl who knew her—one of the two or three with whom she had been

[038-033]intimate at school. After making a few inquiries as to how Tess came there, her

[038-034]friend, unheeding her tragic look, interrupted with—

[038-035]“But where’s thy gentleman, Tess?”

[038-036]Tess hastily explained that he had been called away on business, and, leaving

[038-037]her interlocutor, clambered over the garden-hedge, and thus made her way to the

[038-038]house.

[038-039]As she went up the garden-path she heard her mother singing by the back door,

[038-040]coming in sight of which she perceived Mrs Durbeyfield on the doorstep in the

[038-041]act of wringing a sheet. Having performed this without observing Tess, she went

[038-042]indoors, and her daughter followed her.

[038-043]The washing-tub stood in the same old place on the same old quarter-hogshead,

[038-044]and her mother, having thrown the sheet aside, was about to plunge her arms in

[038-045]anew.

[038-046]“Why—Tess!—my chil’—I thought you was

[038-047]married!—married really and truly this time—we sent the

[038-048]cider—”

[038-049]“Yes, mother; so I am.”

[038-050]“Going to be?”

[038-051]“No—I am married.”

[038-052]“Married! Then where’s thy husband?”

[038-053]“Oh, he’s gone away for a time.”

[038-054]“Gone away! When was you married, then? The day you said?”

[038-055]“Yes, Tuesday, mother.”

[038-056]“And now ’tis on’y Saturday, and he gone away?”

[038-057]“Yes, he’s gone.”

[038-058]“What’s the meaning o’ that? ’Nation seize such

[038-059]husbands as you seem to get, say I!”

[038-060]“Mother!” Tess went across to Joan Durbeyfield, laid her face upon

[038-061]the matron’s bosom, and burst into sobs. “I don’t know how to

[038-062]tell ’ee, mother! You said to me, and wrote to me, that I was not to tell

[038-063]him. But I did tell him—I couldn’t help it—and he went

[038-064]away!”

[038-065]“O you little fool—you little fool!” burst out Mrs

[038-066]Durbeyfield, splashing Tess and herself in her agitation. “My good God!

[038-067]that ever I should ha’ lived to say it, but I say it again, you little

[038-068]fool!”

[038-069]Tess was convulsed with weeping, the tension of so many days having relaxed at

[038-070]last.

[038-071]“I know it—I know—I know!” she gasped through her sobs.

[038-072]“But, O my mother, I could not help it! He was so good—and I felt

[038-073]the wickedness of trying to blind him as to what had happened!

[038-074]If—if—it were to be done again—I should do the same. I could

[038-075]not—I dared not—so sin—against him!”

[038-076]“But you sinned enough to marry him first!”

[038-077]“Yes, yes; that’s where my misery do lie! But I thought he could

[038-078]get rid o’ me by law if he were determined not to overlook it. And O, if

[038-079]you knew—if you could only half know how I loved him—how anxious I

[038-080]was to have him—and how wrung I was between caring so much for him and my

[038-081]wish to be fair to him!”

[038-082]Tess was so shaken that she could get no further, and sank, a helpless thing,

[038-083]into a chair.

[038-084]“Well, well; what’s done can’t be undone! I’m sure I

[038-085]don’t know why children o’ my bringing forth should all be bigger

[038-086]simpletons than other people’s—not to know better than to blab such

[038-087]a thing as that, when he couldn’t ha’ found it out till too

[038-088]late!” Here Mrs Durbeyfield began shedding tears on her own account as a

[038-089]mother to be pitied. “What your father will say I don’t

[038-090]know,” she continued; “for he’s been talking about the

[038-091]wedding up at Rolliver’s and The Pure Drop every day since, and about his

[038-092]family getting back to their rightful position through you—poor silly

[038-093]man!—and now you’ve made this mess of it! The Lord-a-Lord!”

[038-094]As if to bring matters to a focus, Tess’s father was heard approaching at

[038-095]that moment. He did not, however, enter immediately, and Mrs Durbeyfield said

[038-096]that she would break the bad news to him herself, Tess keeping out of sight for

[038-097]the present. After her first burst of disappointment Joan began to take the

[038-098]mishap as she had taken Tess’s original trouble, as she would have taken

[038-099]a wet holiday or failure in the potato-crop; as a thing which had come upon

[038-100]them irrespective of desert or folly; a chance external impingement to be borne

[038-101]with; not a lesson.

[038-102]Tess retreated upstairs and beheld casually that the beds had been shifted, and

[038-103]new arrangements made. Her old bed had been adapted for two younger children.

[038-104]There was no place here for her now.

[038-105]The room below being unceiled she could hear most of what went on there.

[038-106]Presently her father entered, apparently carrying in a live hen. He was a

[038-107]foot-haggler now, having been obliged to sell his second horse, and he

[038-108]travelled with his basket on his arm. The hen had been carried about this

[038-109]morning as it was often carried, to show people that he was in his work, though

[038-110]it had lain, with its legs tied, under the table at Rolliver’s for more

[038-111]than an hour.

[038-112]“We’ve just had up a story about—” Durbeyfield began,

[038-113]and thereupon related in detail to his wife a discussion which had arisen at

[038-114]the inn about the clergy, originated by the fact of his daughter having married

[038-115]into a clerical family. “They was formerly styled ‘sir’, like

[038-116]my own ancestry,” he said, “though nowadays their true style,

[038-117]strictly speaking, is ‘clerk’ only.” As Tess had wished that

[038-118]no great publicity should be given to the event, he had mentioned no

[038-119]particulars. He hoped she would remove that prohibition soon. He proposed that

[038-120]the couple should take Tess’s own name, d’Urberville, as

[038-121]uncorrupted. It was better than her husbands’s. He asked if any letter

[038-122]had come from her that day.

[038-123]Then Mrs Durbeyfield informed him that no letter had come, but Tess

[038-124]unfortunately had come herself.

[038-125]When at length the collapse was explained to him, a sullen mortification, not

[038-126]usual with Durbeyfield, overpowered the influence of the cheering glass. Yet

[038-127]the intrinsic quality of the event moved his touchy sensitiveness less than its

[038-128]conjectured effect upon the minds of others.

[038-129]“To think, now, that this was to be the end o’t!” said Sir

[038-130]John. “And I with a family vault under that there church of Kingsbere as

[038-131]big as Squire Jollard’s ale-cellar, and my folk lying there in sixes and

[038-132]sevens, as genuine county bones and marrow as any recorded in history. And now

[038-133]to be sure what they fellers at Rolliver’s and The Pure Drop will say to

[038-134]me! How they’ll squint and glane, and say, ‘This is yer mighty

[038-135]match is it; this is yer getting back to the true level of yer forefathers in

[038-136]King Norman’s time!’ I feel this is too much, Joan; I shall put an

[038-137]end to myself, title and all—I can bear it no longer!... But she can

[038-138]make him keep her if he’s married her?”

[038-139]“Why, yes. But she won’t think o’ doing that.”

[038-140]“D’ye think he really have married her?—or is it like the

[038-141]first—”

[038-142]Poor Tess, who had heard as far as this, could not bear to hear more. The

[038-143]perception that her word could be doubted even here, in her own parental house,

[038-144]set her mind against the spot as nothing else could have done. How unexpected

[038-145]were the attacks of destiny! And if her father doubted her a little, would not

[038-146]neighbours and acquaintance doubt her much? O, she could not live long at home!

[038-147]A few days, accordingly, were all that she allowed herself here, at the end of

[038-148]which time she received a short note from Clare, informing her that he had gone

[038-149]to the North of England to look at a farm. In her craving for the lustre of her

[038-150]true position as his wife, and to hide from her parents the vast extent of the

[038-151]division between them, she made use of this letter as her reason for again

[038-152]departing, leaving them under the impression that she was setting out to join

[038-153]him. Still further to screen her husband from any imputation of unkindness to

[038-154]her, she took twenty-five of the fifty pounds Clare had given her, and handed

[038-155]the sum over to her mother, as if the wife of a man like Angel Clare could well

[038-156]afford it, saying that it was a slight return for the trouble and humiliation

[038-157]she had brought upon them in years past. With this assertion of her dignity she

[038-158]bade them farewell; and after that there were lively doings in the Durbeyfield

[038-159]household for some time on the strength of Tess’s bounty, her mother

[038-160]saying, and, indeed, believing, that the rupture which had arisen between the

[038-161]young husband and wife had adjusted itself under their strong feeling that they

[038-162]could not live apart from each other.
