[025-001]Clare, restless, went out into the dusk when evening drew on, she who had won

[025-002]him having retired to her chamber.

[025-003]The night was as sultry as the day. There was no coolness after dark unless on

[025-004]the grass. Roads, garden-paths, the house-fronts, the barton-walls were warm as

[025-005]hearths, and reflected the noontime temperature into the noctambulist’s

[025-006]face.

[025-007]He sat on the east gate of the dairy-yard, and knew not what to think of

[025-008]himself. Feeling had indeed smothered judgement that day.

[025-009]Since the sudden embrace, three hours before, the twain had kept apart. She

[025-010]seemed stilled, almost alarmed, at what had occurred, while the novelty,

[025-011]unpremeditation, mastery of circumstance disquieted him—palpitating,

[025-012]contemplative being that he was. He could hardly realize their true relations

[025-013]to each other as yet, and what their mutual bearing should be before third

[025-014]parties thenceforward.

[025-015]Angel had come as pupil to this dairy in the idea that his temporary existence

[025-016]here was to be the merest episode in his life, soon passed through and early

[025-017]forgotten; he had come as to a place from which as from a screened alcove he

[025-018]could calmly view the absorbing world without, and, apostrophizing it with Walt

[025-019]Whitman—

[025-020]Crowds of men and women attired in the usual costumes,

[025-021]How curious you are to me!—

[025-022]resolve upon a plan for plunging into that world anew. But behold, the

[025-023]absorbing scene had been imported hither. What had been the engrossing world

[025-024]had dissolved into an uninteresting outer dumb-show; while here, in this

[025-025]apparently dim and unimpassioned place, novelty had volcanically started up, as

[025-026]it had never, for him, started up elsewhere.

[025-027]Every window of the house being open, Clare could hear across the yard each

[025-028]trivial sound of the retiring household. The dairy-house, so humble, so

[025-029]insignificant, so purely to him a place of constrained sojourn that he had

[025-030]never hitherto deemed it of sufficient importance to be reconnoitred as an

[025-031]object of any quality whatever in the landscape; what was it now? The aged and

[025-032]lichened brick gables breathed forth “Stay!” The windows smiled,

[025-033]the door coaxed and beckoned, the creeper blushed confederacy. A personality

[025-034]within it was so far-reaching in her influence as to spread into and make the

[025-035]bricks, mortar, and whole overhanging sky throb with a burning sensibility.

[025-036]Whose was this mighty personality? A milkmaid’s.

[025-037]It was amazing, indeed, to find how great a matter the life of the obscure

[025-038]dairy had become to him. And though new love was to be held partly responsible

[025-039]for this, it was not solely so. Many besides Angel have learnt that the

[025-040]magnitude of lives is not as to their external displacements, but as to their

[025-041]subjective experiences. The impressionable peasant leads a larger, fuller, more

[025-042]dramatic life than the pachydermatous king. Looking at it thus, he found that

[025-043]life was to be seen of the same magnitude here as elsewhere.

[025-044]Despite his heterodoxy, faults, and weaknesses, Clare was a man with a

[025-045]conscience. Tess was no insignificant creature to toy with and dismiss; but a

[025-046]woman living her precious life—a life which, to herself who endured or

[025-047]enjoyed it, possessed as great a dimension as the life of the mightiest to

[025-048]himself. Upon her sensations the whole world depended to Tess; through her

[025-049]existence all her fellow-creatures existed, to her. The universe itself only

[025-050]came into being for Tess on the particular day in the particular year in which

[025-051]she was born.

[025-052]This consciousness upon which he had intruded was the single opportunity of

[025-053]existence ever vouchsafed to Tess by an unsympathetic First Cause—her

[025-054]all; her every and only chance. How then should he look upon her as of less

[025-055]consequence than himself; as a pretty trifle to caress and grow weary of; and

[025-056]not deal in the greatest seriousness with the affection which he knew that he

[025-057]had awakened in her—so fervid and so impressionable as she was under her

[025-058]reserve—in order that it might not agonize and wreck her?

[025-059]To encounter her daily in the accustomed manner would be to develop what had

[025-060]begun. Living in such close relations, to meet meant to fall into endearment;

[025-061]flesh and blood could not resist it; and, having arrived at no conclusion as to

[025-062]the issue of such a tendency, he decided to hold aloof for the present from

[025-063]occupations in which they would be mutually engaged. As yet the harm done was

[025-064]small.

[025-065]But it was not easy to carry out the resolution never to approach her. He was

[025-066]driven towards her by every heave of his pulse.

[025-067]He thought he would go and see his friends. It might be possible to sound them

[025-068]upon this. In less than five months his term here would have ended, and after a

[025-069]few additional months spent upon other farms he would be fully equipped in

[025-070]agricultural knowledge and in a position to start on his own account. Would not

[025-071]a farmer want a wife, and should a farmer’s wife be a drawing-room

[025-072]wax-figure, or a woman who understood farming? Notwithstanding the pleasing

[025-073]answer returned to him by the silence, he resolved to go his journey.

[025-074]One morning when they sat down to breakfast at Talbothays Dairy some maid

[025-075]observed that she had not seen anything of Mr Clare that day.

[025-076]“O no,” said Dairyman Crick. “Mr Clare has gone hwome to

[025-077]Emminster to spend a few days wi’ his kinsfolk.”

[025-078]For four impassioned ones around that table the sunshine of the morning went

[025-079]out at a stroke, and the birds muffled their song. But neither girl by word or

[025-080]gesture revealed her blankness. “He’s getting on towards the end of

[025-081]his time wi’ me,” added the dairyman, with a phlegm which

[025-082]unconsciously was brutal; “and so I suppose he is beginning to see about

[025-083]his plans elsewhere.”

[025-084]“How much longer is he to bide here?” asked Izz Huett, the only one

[025-085]of the gloom-stricken bevy who could trust her voice with the question.

[025-086]The others waited for the dairyman’s answer as if their lives hung upon

[025-087]it; Retty, with parted lips, gazing on the tablecloth, Marian with heat added

[025-088]to her redness, Tess throbbing and looking out at the meads.

[025-089]“Well, I can’t mind the exact day without looking at my

[025-090]memorandum-book,” replied Crick, with the same intolerable unconcern.

[025-091]“And even that may be altered a bit. He’ll bide to get a little

[025-092]practice in the calving out at the straw-yard, for certain. He’ll hang on

[025-093]till the end of the year I should say.”

[025-094]Four months or so of torturing ecstasy in his society—of “pleasure

[025-095]girdled about with pain”. After that the blackness of unutterable night.

[025-096]At this moment of the morning Angel Clare was riding along a narrow lane ten

[025-097]miles distant from the breakfasters, in the direction of his father’s

[025-098]Vicarage at Emminster, carrying, as well as he could, a little basket which

[025-099]contained some black-puddings and a bottle of mead, sent by Mrs Crick, with her

[025-100]kind respects, to his parents. The white lane stretched before him, and his

[025-101]eyes were upon it; but they were staring into next year, and not at the lane.

[025-102]He loved her; ought he to marry her? Dared he to marry her? What would his

[025-103]mother and his brothers say? What would he himself say a couple of years after

[025-104]the event? That would depend upon whether the germs of staunch comradeship

[025-105]underlay the temporary emotion, or whether it were a sensuous joy in her form

[025-106]only, with no substratum of everlastingness.

[025-107]His father’s hill-surrounded little town, the Tudor church-tower of red

[025-108]stone, the clump of trees near the Vicarage, came at last into view beneath

[025-109]him, and he rode down towards the well-known gate. Casting a glance in the

[025-110]direction of the church before entering his home, he beheld standing by the

[025-111]vestry-door a group of girls, of ages between twelve and sixteen, apparently

[025-112]awaiting the arrival of some other one, who in a moment became visible; a

[025-113]figure somewhat older than the school-girls, wearing a broad-brimmed hat and

[025-114]highly-starched cambric morning-gown, with a couple of books in her hand.

[025-115]Clare knew her well. He could not be sure that she observed him; he hoped she

[025-116]did not, so as to render it unnecessary that he should go and speak to her,

[025-117]blameless creature that she was. An overpowering reluctance to greet her made

[025-118]him decide that she had not seen him. The young lady was Miss Mercy Chant, the

[025-119]only daughter of his father’s neighbour and friend, whom it was his

[025-120]parents’ quiet hope that he might wed some day. She was great at

[025-121]Antinomianism and Bible-classes, and was plainly going to hold a class now.

[025-122]Clare’s mind flew to the impassioned, summer-steeped heathens in the Var

[025-123]Vale, their rosy faces court-patched with cow-droppings; and to one the most

[025-124]impassioned of them all.

[025-125]It was on the impulse of the moment that he had resolved to trot over to

[025-126]Emminster, and hence had not written to apprise his mother and father, aiming,

[025-127]however, to arrive about the breakfast hour, before they should have gone out

[025-128]to their parish duties. He was a little late, and they had already sat down to

[025-129]the morning meal. The group at the table jumped up to welcome him as soon as he

[025-130]entered. They were his father and mother, his brother the Reverend

[025-131]Felix—curate at a town in the adjoining county, home for the inside of a

[025-132]fortnight—and his other brother, the Reverend Cuthbert, the classical

[025-133]scholar, and Fellow and Dean of his College, down from Cambridge for the long

[025-134]vacation. His mother appeared in a cap and silver spectacles, and his father

[025-135]looked what in fact he was—an earnest, God-fearing man, somewhat gaunt,

[025-136]in years about sixty-five, his pale face lined with thought and purpose. Over

[025-137]their heads hung the picture of Angel’s sister, the eldest of the family,

[025-138]sixteen years his senior, who had married a missionary and gone out to Africa.

[025-139]Old Mr Clare was a clergyman of a type which, within the last twenty years, has

[025-140]well nigh dropped out of contemporary life. A spiritual descendant in the

[025-141]direct line from Wycliff, Huss, Luther, Calvin; an Evangelical of the

[025-142]Evangelicals, a Conversionist, a man of Apostolic simplicity in life and

[025-143]thought, he had in his raw youth made up his mind once for all in the deeper

[025-144]questions of existence, and admitted no further reasoning on them

[025-145]thenceforward. He was regarded even by those of his own date and school of

[025-146]thinking as extreme; while, on the other hand, those totally opposed to him

[025-147]were unwillingly won to admiration for his thoroughness, and for the remarkable

[025-148]power he showed in dismissing all question as to principles in his energy for

[025-149]applying them. He loved Paul of Tarsus, liked St John, hated St James as much

[025-150]as he dared, and regarded with mixed feelings Timothy, Titus, and Philemon. The

[025-151]New Testament was less a Christiad then a Pauliad to his

[025-152]intelligence—less an argument than an intoxication. His creed of

[025-153]determinism was such that it almost amounted to a vice, and quite amounted, on

[025-154]its negative side, to a renunciative philosophy which had cousinship with that

[025-155]of Schopenhauer and Leopardi. He despised the Canons and Rubric, swore by the

[025-156]Articles, and deemed himself consistent through the whole category—which

[025-157]in a way he might have been. One thing he certainly was—sincere.

[025-158]To the aesthetic, sensuous, pagan pleasure in natural life and lush womanhood

[025-159]which his son Angel had lately been experiencing in Var Vale, his temper would

[025-160]have been antipathetic in a high degree, had he either by inquiry or

[025-161]imagination been able to apprehend it. Once upon a time Angel had been so

[025-162]unlucky as to say to his father, in a moment of irritation, that it might have

[025-163]resulted far better for mankind if Greece had been the source of the religion

[025-164]of modern civilization, and not Palestine; and his father’s grief was of

[025-165]that blank description which could not realize that there might lurk a

[025-166]thousandth part of a truth, much less a half truth or a whole truth, in such a

[025-167]proposition. He had simply preached austerely at Angel for some time after. But

[025-168]the kindness of his heart was such that he never resented anything for long,

[025-169]and welcomed his son to-day with a smile which was as candidly sweet as a

[025-170]child’s.

[025-171]Angel sat down, and the place felt like home; yet he did not so much as

[025-172]formerly feel himself one of the family gathered there. Every time that he

[025-173]returned hither he was conscious of this divergence, and since he had last

[025-174]shared in the Vicarage life it had grown even more distinctly foreign to his

[025-175]own than usual. Its transcendental aspirations—still unconsciously based

[025-176]on the geocentric view of things, a zenithal paradise, a nadiral

[025-177]hell—were as foreign to his own as if they had been the dreams of people

[025-178]on another planet. Latterly he had seen only Life, felt only the great

[025-179]passionate pulse of existence, unwarped, uncontorted, untrammelled by those

[025-180]creeds which futilely attempt to check what wisdom would be content to

[025-181]regulate.

[025-182]On their part they saw a great difference in him, a growing divergence from the

[025-183]Angel Clare of former times. It was chiefly a difference in his manner that

[025-184]they noticed just now, particularly his brothers. He was getting to behave like

[025-185]a farmer; he flung his legs about; the muscles of his face had grown more

[025-186]expressive; his eyes looked as much information as his tongue spoke, and more.

[025-187]The manner of the scholar had nearly disappeared; still more the manner of the

[025-188]drawing-room young man. A prig would have said that he had lost culture, and a

[025-189]prude that he had become coarse. Such was the contagion of domiciliary

[025-190]fellowship with the Talbothays nymphs and swains.

[025-192]well-educated, hall-marked young men, correct to their remotest fibre, such

[025-196]it was the custom to wear a double glass they wore a double glass; when it was

[025-197]the custom to wear spectacles they wore spectacles straightway, all without

[025-199]Wordsworth was enthroned they carried pocket copies; and when Shelley was

[025-200]belittled they allowed him to grow dusty on their shelves. When

[025-201]Correggio’s Holy Families were admired, they admired Correggio’s

[025-202]Holy Families; when he was decried in favour of Velasquez, they sedulously

[025-203]followed suit without any personal objection.

[025-212]their parents. Felix, though an offshoot from a far more recent point in the

[025-219]him—that whatever their advantages by comparison with himself, neither
