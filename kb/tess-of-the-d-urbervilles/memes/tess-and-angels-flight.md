# Tess and Angel's Flight

## Summary

Moments after [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] leaves [[the-herons|The Herons]] boarding house, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] catches up to him on the road out of [[sandbourne|Sandbourne]] and confesses: "I have killed him!" The couple, reunited in extremity, flee together. They travel on foot through the New Forest, evading the search parties they know will come. At Bramshurst Court, an empty country mansion, they find five days of desperate, blissful seclusion — a brief paradise before the inevitable reckoning. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], fully aware that capture means death, accepts her fate with calm: "What must come will come."

## Context

The flight occurs in the novel's final chapters, between the murder of [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s arrest at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]]. It is the briefest phase of the narrative — five days — but contains the novel's most concentrated expression of its central theme: happiness is possible only outside society. In the empty mansion, cut off from the world, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] finally experience the marriage they were denied in civilization.

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s transformation is complete. The man who could not forgive [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on their wedding night now accepts her as a murderer without hesitation. Hardy suggests that it took the extremity of violence to break through [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s moral rigidity — a devastating irony at the heart of the novel.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — After the murder, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is paradoxically liberated. The burden of secrecy and shame that has weighed on her since [[the-chase|The Chase]] is lifted. She tells [[angel-clare|Angel]] everything and accepts whatever consequences follow. Her calm in the face of death is the novel's final portrait of her character: a woman who has been broken by the world but has found, at the last, a way to act.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s response to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s confession of murder reveals how far he has traveled from the judgmental husband of the wedding night. He does not recoil. He takes her hand, pulls her into the shelter of the woods, and becomes her protector. His love, which failed [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] when she needed forgiveness, finally proves adequate when she needs complicity.

## Key Passages

**[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s Confession of Murder**:
> "Angel," she said, as if waiting for this, "do you know what I have been running after you for? To tell you that I have killed him!" A pitiful white smile lit her face as she spoke. (057-037)

**Five Days of Seclusion**:
> five days had slipped by in absolute seclusion, not a sight or sound of a human being disturbing their peacefulness, such as it was. The changes of the weather were their only events, the birds of the New Forest their only company. (058-020)

**[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s Wish to Die Before [[love-and-desire|Love]] Fades**:
> "I fear that what you think of me now may not last. I do not wish to outlive your present feeling for me. I would rather not. I would rather be dead and buried when the time comes for you to despise me." (058-036)

**Inside and Outside**:
> "All is trouble outside there; inside here content." He peeped out also. It was quite true; within was affection, union, error forgiven: outside was the inexorable. (058-031)

## Narrative Impact

The flight is the novel's emotional climax, not of action but of feeling. For five days, the dream that has eluded [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] throughout the novel — honest love, mutual acceptance, freedom from social judgment — becomes real, but only in hiding. The empty mansion is the antithesis of the social world: a place without neighbors, without church, without law. It is the only place where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love can exist.

The flight also completes [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s character arc. His acceptance of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] the murderer contrasts devastatingly with his rejection of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] the victim. Hardy leaves the reader to ponder: what kind of moral code forgives murder but not sexual violation? The answer is implicit — a code that values male possession of female purity above female life itself.
