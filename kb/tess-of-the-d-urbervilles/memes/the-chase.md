# The Chase

## Location

The Chase is a vast ancient forest situated on the borders of [[trantridge|Trantridge parish]] in Wessex. It lies in "the direction of the district known as The Chase, on the borders of which, as she had been informed, Mrs d'Urberville's seat, [[the-slopes|The Slopes]], would be found" (005-090). The forest is visible as a panoramic backdrop from [[the-slopes|The Slopes]] itself: "stretched the soft azure landscape of The Chase — a truly venerable tract of forest land, one of the few remaining woodlands in England" (005-106).

The Chase is geographically proximate to both [[trantridge|Trantridge]] and [[chaseborough|Chaseborough]], forming a dark boundary between the cultivated estate lands and the wild. Its name gives rise to the nearby market-town of [[chaseborough|Chaseborough]], which lies on its periphery.

## Description

Hardy describes The Chase as "the oldest wood in England" (011-073), emphasising its primeval character. The forest is dense and disorienting, composed of ancient yews and oaks. In the darkness, it becomes nearly impassable: "the moon had quite gone down, and partly on account of the fog The Chase was wrapped in thick darkness" (011-148). [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] is "obliged to advance with outstretched hands to avoid contact with the boughs" (011-150).

By daybreak, however, the forest reveals its gentler aspect: "yews and oaks of The Chase, in which there poised gentle roosting birds in their last nap; and about them stole the hopping rabbits and hares" (011-164). This duality — beauty by day, menace by night — is central to The Chase's symbolic function in the novel.

## Significance

The Chase is the setting of the novel's pivotal violation scene (Chapter XI). After the fair at [[chaseborough|Chaseborough]], [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] deliberately leads [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] into the depths of the forest, having "ridden quite at random for over an hour, taking any turning that came to hand in order to prolong companionship with her" (011-141). When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] finally realises their situation, she cries out: "How could you be so treacherous!" (011-075).

The forest is both a literal location and a symbolic space — the territory where the ancient, indifferent natural world becomes complicit in human cruelty. Hardy's narrator asks the pointed question: "But, might some say, where was [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s guardian angel? where was the providence of..." (011-166) — a question cut short, as if even the narrator cannot bear to complete it.

The Chase reappears in the novel's symbolic register: the dates of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s baby's birth and death are set against "the background of The Chase" (015-021), linking the forest permanently to the consequences of that night. Later, [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s suspicions are raised when he learns a man lived "in the neighbourhood of The Chase" (030-171), recognising the place name as a marker of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s history.

## Analysis

### The Oldest Wood as Oldest Crime

The Chase is repeatedly described as ancient — "the oldest wood in England" (011-073). This antiquity is not incidental. Hardy positions the forest as a space outside modern morality, governed by older, darker laws. What happens to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in The Chase is not merely a Victorian crime but an ancient one, repeated across centuries in the same dark woods. The forest's age makes it complicit: it has seen this before.

### Nature's Indifference

The famous passage of the "gentle roosting birds" and "hopping rabbits and hares" (011-164) that immediately precedes the narrator's question about [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s guardian angel (011-166) is one of the novel's most devastating juxtapositions. Nature is beautiful, peaceful, and utterly indifferent to human suffering. The Chase, for all its ancient grandeur, offers [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] no protection — only witnesses.

### The Fog of Moral Ambiguity

The fog that descends on The Chase (011-148) functions as both physical description and moral metaphor. Alec's actions take place in a fog of justifications — his claim that the detour is innocent, the darkness that conceals his intentions. The Chase's natural features (fog, darkness, dense undergrowth) become instruments of deception, turning the landscape itself into an accomplice.
