[005-001]The haggling business, which had mainly depended on the horse, became

[005-002]disorganized forthwith. Distress, if not penury, loomed in the distance.

[005-003]Durbeyfield was what was locally called a slack-twisted fellow; he had good

[005-004]strength to work at times; but the times could not be relied on to coincide

[005-005]with the hours of requirement; and, having been unaccustomed to the regular

[005-006]toil of the day-labourer, he was not particularly persistent when they did so

[005-007]coincide.

[005-008]Tess, meanwhile, as the one who had dragged her parents into this quagmire, was

[005-009]silently wondering what she could do to help them out of it; and then her

[005-010]mother broached her scheme.

[005-011]“We must take the ups wi’ the downs, Tess,” said she;

[005-012]“and never could your high blood have been found out at a more called-for

[005-013]moment. You must try your friends. Do ye know that there is a very rich Mrs

[005-014]d’Urberville living on the outskirts o’ The Chase, who must be our

[005-015]relation? You must go to her and claim kin, and ask for some help in our

[005-016]trouble.”

[005-017]“I shouldn’t care to do that,” says Tess. “If there is

[005-018]such a lady, ’twould be enough for us if she were friendly—not to

[005-019]expect her to give us help.”

[005-020]“You could win her round to do anything, my dear. Besides, perhaps

[005-021]there’s more in it than you know of. I’ve heard what I’ve

[005-022]heard, good-now.”

[005-023]The oppressive sense of the harm she had done led Tess to be more deferential

[005-024]than she might otherwise have been to the maternal wish; but she could not

[005-025]understand why her mother should find such satisfaction in contemplating an

[005-026]enterprise of, to her, such doubtful profit. Her mother might have made

[005-027]inquiries, and have discovered that this Mrs d’Urberville was a lady of

[005-028]unequalled virtues and charity. But Tess’s pride made the part of poor

[005-029]relation one of particular distaste to her.

[005-031]“Durbeyfield, you can settle it,” said his wife, turning to where

[005-032]he sat in the background. “If you say she ought to go, she will

[005-033]go.”

[005-035]strange kin,” murmured he. “I’m the head of the noblest

[005-036]branch o’ the family, and I ought to live up to it.”

[005-037]His reasons for staying away were worse to Tess than her own objections to

[005-038]going. “Well, as I killed the horse, mother,” she said mournfully,

[005-039]“I suppose I ought to do something. I don’t mind going and seeing

[005-040]her, but you must leave it to me about asking for help. And don’t go

[005-041]thinking about her making a match for me—it is silly.”

[005-042]“Very well said, Tess!” observed her father sententiously.

[005-043]“Who said I had such a thought?” asked Joan.

[005-044]“I fancy it is in your mind, mother. But I’ll go.”

[005-045]Rising early next day she walked to the hill-town called Shaston, and there

[005-046]took advantage of a van which twice in the week ran from Shaston eastward to

[005-047]Chaseborough, passing near Trantridge, the parish in which the vague and

[005-048]mysterious Mrs d’Urberville had her residence.

[005-049]Tess Durbeyfield’s route on this memorable morning lay amid the

[005-050]north-eastern undulations of the Vale in which she had been born, and in which

[005-052]inhabitants the races thereof. From the gates and stiles of Marlott she had

[005-053]looked down its length in the wondering days of infancy, and what had been

[005-056]all, the town of Shaston standing majestically on its height; its windows

[005-057]shining like lamps in the evening sun. She had hardly ever visited the place,

[005-058]only a small tract even of the Vale and its environs being known to her by

[005-059]close inspection. Much less had she been far outside the valley. Every contour

[005-060]of the surrounding hills was as personal to her as that of her relatives’

[005-061]faces; but for what lay beyond, her judgment was dependent on the teaching of

[005-062]the village school, where she had held a leading place at the time of her

[005-063]leaving, a year or two before this date.

[005-064]In those early days she had been much loved by others of her own sex and age,

[005-065]and had used to be seen about the village as one of three—all nearly of

[005-066]the same year—walking home from school side by side; Tess the middle

[005-075]Malthusian towards her mother for thoughtlessly giving her so many little

[005-076]sisters and brothers, when it was such a trouble to nurse and provide for them.

[005-077]Her mother’s intelligence was that of a happy child: Joan Durbeyfield was

[005-078]simply an additional one, and that not the eldest, to her own long family of

[005-079]waiters on Providence.

[005-080]However, Tess became humanely beneficent towards the small ones, and to help

[005-081]them as much as possible she used, as soon as she left school, to lend a hand

[005-084]cows; and being deft-fingered it was a kind of work in which she excelled.

[005-085]Every day seemed to throw upon her young shoulders more of the family burdens,

[005-086]and that Tess should be the representative of the Durbeyfields at the

[005-087]d’Urberville mansion came as a thing of course. In this instance it must

[005-088]be admitted that the Durbeyfields were putting their fairest side outward.

[005-092]be found. It was not a manorial home in the ordinary sense, with fields, and

[005-095]country-house built for enjoyment pure and simple, with not an acre of

[005-096]troublesome land attached to it beyond what was required for residential

[005-097]purposes, and for a little fancy farm kept in hand by the owner, and tended by

[005-107]venerable tract of forest land, one of the few remaining woodlands in England

[005-110]they had grown when they were pollarded for bows. All this sylvan antiquity,

[005-113]Everything on this snug property was bright, thriving, and well kept; acres of

[005-114]glass-houses stretched down the inclines to the copses at their feet.

[005-115]Everything looked like money—like the last coin issued from the Mint. The

[005-116]stables, partly screened by Austrian pines and evergreen oaks, and fitted with

[005-117]every late appliance, were as dignified as Chapels-of-Ease. On the extensive

[005-118]lawn stood an ornamental tent, its door being towards her.

[005-119]Simple Tess Durbeyfield stood at gaze, in a half-alarmed attitude, on the edge

[005-120]of the gravel sweep. Her feet had brought her onward to this point before she

[005-121]had quite realized where she was; and now all was contrary to her expectation.

[005-122]“I thought we were an old family; but this is all new!” she said,

[005-123]in her artlessness. She wished that she had not fallen in so readily with her

[005-124]mother’s plans for “claiming kin,” and had endeavoured to

[005-125]gain assistance nearer home.

[005-126]The d’Urbervilles—or Stoke-d’Urbervilles, as they at first

[005-127]called themselves—who owned all this, were a somewhat unusual family to

[005-128]find in such an old-fashioned part of the country. Parson Tringham had spoken

[005-129]truly when he said that our shambling John Durbeyfield was the only really

[005-130]lineal representative of the old d’Urberville family existing in the

[005-131]county, or near it; he might have added, what he knew very well, that the

[005-134]good stock whereon to regraft a name which sadly wanted such renovation.

[005-135]When old Mr Simon Stoke, latterly deceased, had made his fortune as an honest

[005-136]merchant (some said money-lender) in the North, he decided to settle as a

[005-137]county man in the South of England, out of hail of his business district; and
