[040-001]At breakfast Brazil was the topic, and all endeavoured to take a hopeful view

[040-002]of Clare’s proposed experiment with that country’s soil,

[040-003]notwithstanding the discouraging reports of some farm-labourers who had

[040-004]emigrated thither and returned home within the twelve months. After breakfast

[040-005]Clare went into the little town to wind up such trifling matters as he was

[040-006]concerned with there, and to get from the local bank all the money he

[040-007]possessed. On his way back he encountered Miss Mercy Chant by the church, from

[040-008]whose walls she seemed to be a sort of emanation. She was carrying an armful of

[040-009]Bibles for her class, and such was her view of life that events which produced

[040-010]heartache in others wrought beatific smiles upon her—an enviable result,

[040-011]although, in the opinion of Angel, it was obtained by a curiously unnatural

[040-012]sacrifice of humanity to mysticism.

[040-013]She had learnt that he was about to leave England, and observed what an

[040-014]excellent and promising scheme it seemed to be.

[040-015]“Yes; it is a likely scheme enough in a commercial sense, no

[040-016]doubt,” he replied. “But, my dear Mercy, it snaps the continuity of

[040-017]existence. Perhaps a cloister would be preferable.”

[040-018]“A cloister! O, Angel Clare!”

[040-019]“Well?”

[040-020]“Why, you wicked man, a cloister implies a monk, and a monk Roman

[040-021]Catholicism.”

[040-022]“And Roman Catholicism sin, and sin damnation. Thou art in a parlous

[040-023]state, Angel Clare.”

[040-024]“ *I* glory in my Protestantism!” she said severely.

[040-025]Then Clare, thrown by sheer misery into one of the demoniacal moods in which a

[040-026]man does despite to his true principles, called her close to him, and

[040-027]fiendishly whispered in her ear the most heterodox ideas he could think of. His

[040-028]momentary laughter at the horror which appeared on her fair face ceased when it

[040-029]merged in pain and anxiety for his welfare.

[040-030]“Dear Mercy,” he said, “you must forgive me. I think I am

[040-031]going crazy!”

[040-032]She thought that he was; and thus the interview ended, and Clare re-entered the

[040-033]Vicarage. With the local banker he deposited the jewels till happier days

[040-034]should arise. He also paid into the bank thirty pounds—to be sent to Tess

[040-035]in a few months, as she might require; and wrote to her at her parents’

[040-036]home in Blackmoor Vale to inform her of what he had done. This amount, with the

[040-037]sum he had already placed in her hands—about fifty pounds—he hoped

[040-038]would be amply sufficient for her wants just at present, particularly as in an

[040-039]emergency she had been directed to apply to his father.

[040-040]He deemed it best not to put his parents into communication with her by

[040-041]informing them of her address; and, being unaware of what had really happened

[040-042]to estrange the two, neither his father nor his mother suggested that he should

[040-043]do so. During the day he left the parsonage, for what he had to complete he

[040-044]wished to get done quickly.

[040-045]As the last duty before leaving this part of England it was necessary for him

[040-046]to call at the Wellbridge farmhouse, in which he had spent with Tess the first

[040-047]three days of their marriage, the trifle of rent having to be paid, the key

[040-048]given up of the rooms they had occupied, and two or three small articles

[040-049]fetched away that they had left behind. It was under this roof that the deepest

[040-050]shadow ever thrown upon his life had stretched its gloom over him. Yet when he

[040-051]had unlocked the door of the sitting-room and looked into it, the memory which

[040-052]returned first upon him was that of their happy arrival on a similar afternoon,

[040-053]the first fresh sense of sharing a habitation conjointly, the first meal

[040-054]together, the chatting by the fire with joined hands.

[040-055]The farmer and his wife were in the field at the moment of his visit, and Clare

[040-056]was in the rooms alone for some time. Inwardly swollen with a renewal of

[040-057]sentiment that he had not quite reckoned with, he went upstairs to her chamber,

[040-058]which had never been his. The bed was smooth as she had made it with her own

[040-059]hands on the morning of leaving. The mistletoe hung under the tester just as he

[040-060]had placed it. Having been there three or four weeks it was turning colour, and

[040-061]the leaves and berries were wrinkled. Angel took it down and crushed it into

[040-062]the grate. Standing there, he for the first time doubted whether his course in

[040-063]this conjecture had been a wise, much less a generous, one. But had he not been

[040-064]cruelly blinded? In the incoherent multitude of his emotions he knelt down at

[040-065]the bedside wet-eyed. “O Tess! If you had only told me sooner, I would

[040-066]have forgiven you!” he mourned.

[040-067]Hearing a footstep below, he rose and went to the top of the stairs. At the

[040-068]bottom of the flight he saw a woman standing, and on her turning up her face

[040-069]recognized the pale, dark-eyed Izz Huett.

[040-070]“Mr Clare,” she said, “I’ve called to see you and Mrs

[040-071]Clare, and to inquire if ye be well. I thought you might be back here

[040-072]again.”

[040-073]This was a girl whose secret he had guessed, but who had not yet guessed his;

[040-074]an honest girl who loved him—one who would have made as good, or nearly

[040-075]as good, a practical farmer’s wife as Tess.

[040-076]“I am here alone,” he said; “we are not living here

[040-077]now.” Explaining why he had come, he asked, “Which way are you

[040-078]going home, Izz?”

[040-079]“I have no home at Talbothays Dairy now, sir,” she said.

[040-080]“Why is that?”

[040-081]Izz looked down.

[040-082]“It was so dismal there that I left! I am staying out this way.”

[040-083]She pointed in a contrary direction, the direction in which he was journeying.

[040-084]“Well—are you going there now? I can take you if you wish for a

[040-085]lift.”

[040-086]Her olive complexion grew richer in hue.

[040-087]“Thank ’ee, Mr Clare,” she said.

[040-088]He soon found the farmer, and settled the account for his rent and the few

[040-089]other items which had to be considered by reason of the sudden abandonment of

[040-090]the lodgings. On Clare’s return to his horse and gig, Izz jumped up

[040-091]beside him.

[040-092]“I am going to leave England, Izz,” he said, as they drove on.

[040-093]“Going to Brazil.”

[040-094]“And do Mrs Clare like the notion of such a journey?” she asked.

[040-095]“She is not going at present—say for a year or so. I am going out

[040-096]to reconnoitre—to see what life there is like.”

[040-097]They sped along eastward for some considerable distance, Izz making no

[040-098]observation.

[040-099]“How are the others?” he inquired. “How is Retty?”

[040-100]“She was in a sort of nervous state when I zid her last; and so thin and

[040-101]hollow-cheeked that ’a do seem in a decline. Nobody will ever fall in

[040-102]love wi’ her any more,” said Izz absently.

[040-103]“And Marian?”

[040-104]Izz lowered her voice.

[040-105]“Marian drinks.”

[040-106]“Indeed!”

[040-107]“Yes. The dairyman has got rid of her.”

[040-108]“And you!”

[040-109]“I don’t drink, and I bain’t in a decline. But—I am no

[040-110]great things at singing afore breakfast now!”

[040-111]“How is that? Do you remember how neatly you used to turn

[040-112]‘’Twas down in Cupid’s Gardens’ and ‘The

[040-113]Tailor’s Breeches’ at morning milking?”

[040-114]“Ah, yes! When you first came, sir, that was. Not when you had been there

[040-115]a bit.”

[040-116]“Why was that falling-off?”

[040-117]Her black eyes flashed up to his face for one moment by way of answer.

[040-118]“Izz!—how weak of you—for such as I!” he said, and fell

[040-119]into reverie. “Then—suppose I had asked *you* to marry

[040-120]me?”

[040-121]“If you had I should have said ‘Yes’, and you would have

[040-122]married a woman who loved ’ee!”

[040-123]“Really!”

[040-124]“Down to the ground!” she whispered vehemently. “O my God!

[040-125]did you never guess it till now!”

[040-126]By-and-by they reached a branch road to a village.

[040-127]“I must get down. I live out there,” said Izz abruptly, never

[040-128]having spoken since her avowal.

[040-129]Clare slowed the horse. He was incensed against his fate, bitterly disposed

[040-130]towards social ordinances; for they had cooped him up in a corner, out of which

[040-131]there was no legitimate pathway. Why not be revenged on society by shaping his

[040-132]future domesticities loosely, instead of kissing the pedagogic rod of

[040-133]convention in this ensnaring manner?

[040-134]“I am going to Brazil alone, Izz,” said he. “I have separated

[040-135]from my wife for personal, not voyaging, reasons. I may never live with her

[040-136]again. I may not be able to love you; but—will you go with me instead of

[040-137]her?”

[040-138]“You truly wish me to go?”

[040-139]“I do. I have been badly used enough to wish for relief. And you at least

[040-140]love me disinterestedly.”

[040-141]“Yes—I will go,” said Izz, after a pause.

[040-142]“You will? You know what it means, Izz?”

[040-143]“It means that I shall live with you for the time you are over

[040-144]there—that’s good enough for me.”

[040-145]“Remember, you are not to trust me in morals now. But I ought to remind

[040-146]you that it will be wrong-doing in the eyes of civilization—Western

[040-147]civilization, that is to say.”

[040-148]“I don’t mind that; no woman do when it comes to agony-point, and

[040-149]there’s no other way!”

[040-150]“Then don’t get down, but sit where you are.”

[040-151]He drove past the cross-roads, one mile, two miles, without showing any signs

[040-152]of affection.

[040-153]“You love me very, very much, Izz?” he suddenly asked.

[040-154]“I do—I have said I do! I loved you all the time we was at the

[040-155]dairy together!”

[040-156]“More than Tess?”

[040-157]She shook her head.

[040-158]“No,” she murmured, “not more than she.”

[040-159]“How’s that?”

[040-160]“Because nobody could love ’ee more than Tess did!... She

[040-161]would have laid down her life for ’ee. I could do no more.”

[040-162]Like the prophet on the top of Peor, Izz Huett would fain have spoken

[040-163]perversely at such a moment, but the fascination exercised over her rougher

[040-164]nature by Tess’s character compelled her to grace.

[040-165]Clare was silent; his heart had risen at these straightforward words from such

[040-166]an unexpected unimpeachable quarter. In his throat was something as if a sob

[040-167]had solidified there. His ears repeated, “ *She would have laid down her

[040-168]life for ’ee. I could do no more!* ”

[040-169]“Forget our idle talk, Izz,” he said, turning the horse’s

[040-170]head suddenly. “I don’t know what I’ve been saying! I will

[040-171]now drive you back to where your lane branches off.”

[040-172]“So much for honesty towards ’ee! O—how can I bear

[040-173]it—how can I—how can I!”

[040-174]Izz Huett burst into wild tears, and beat her forehead as she saw what she had

[040-175]done.

[040-176]“Do you regret that poor little act of justice to an absent one? O, Izz,

[040-177]don’t spoil it by regret!”

[040-178]She stilled herself by degrees.

[040-179]“Very well, sir. Perhaps I didn’t know what I was saying, either,

[040-180]wh—when I agreed to go! I wish—what cannot be!”

[040-181]“Because I have a loving wife already.”

[040-182]“Yes, yes! You have!”

[040-183]They reached the corner of the lane which they had passed half an hour earlier,

[040-184]and she hopped down.

[040-185]“Izz—please, please forget my momentary levity!” he cried.

[040-186]“It was so ill-considered, so ill-advised!”

[040-187]“Forget it? Never, never! O, it was no levity to me!”

[040-188]He felt how richly he deserved the reproach that the wounded cry conveyed, and,

[040-189]in a sorrow that was inexpressible, leapt down and took her hand.

[040-190]“Well, but, Izz, we’ll part friends, anyhow? You don’t know

[040-191]what I’ve had to bear!”

[040-192]She was a really generous girl, and allowed no further bitterness to mar their

[040-193]adieux.

[040-194]“I forgive ’ee, sir!” she said.

[040-195]“Now, Izz,” he said, while she stood beside him there, forcing

[040-196]himself to the mentor’s part he was far from feeling; “I want you

[040-197]to tell Marian when you see her that she is to be a good woman, and not to give

[040-200]the words—wisely and well—for my sake. I send this message to them

[040-202]you have saved me by your honest words about my wife from an incredible impulse

[040-205]and sincere girl you have hitherto been; and think of me as a worthless lover,

[040-206]but a faithful friend. Promise.”

[040-207]She gave the promise.

[040-208]“Heaven bless and keep you, sir. Goodbye!”

[040-209]He drove on; but no sooner had Izz turned into the lane, and Clare was out of

[040-213]intervened between Angel Clare’s parting from her and her arrival home.

[040-216]feather-weight’s turn of abandoning his road to the nearest station, and

[040-218]his Tess’s home. It was neither a contempt for her nature, nor the

[040-219]probable state of her heart, which deterred him.

[040-220]No; it was a sense that, despite her love, as corroborated by Izz’s

[040-221]admission, the facts had not changed. If he was right at first, he was right

[040-222]now. And the momentum of the course on which he had embarked tended to keep him

[040-223]going in it, unless diverted by a stronger, more sustained force than had

[040-224]played upon him this afternoon. He could soon come back to her. He took the

[040-225]train that night for London, and five days after shook hands in farewell of his

[040-226]brothers at the port of embarkation.
