[056-001]Mrs Brooks, the lady who was the householder at The Herons and owner of all the

[056-002]handsome furniture, was not a person of an unusually curious turn of mind. She

[056-003]was too deeply materialized, poor woman, by her long and enforced bondage to

[056-004]that arithmetical demon Profit-and-Loss, to retain much curiousity for its own

[056-005]sake, and apart from possible lodgers’ pockets. Nevertheless, the visit

[056-006]of Angel Clare to her well-paying tenants, Mr and Mrs d’Urberville, as

[056-007]she deemed them, was sufficiently exceptional in point of time and manner to

[056-008]reinvigorate the feminine proclivity which had been stifled down as useless

[056-009]save in its bearings to the letting trade.

[056-010]Tess had spoken to her husband from the doorway, without entering the

[056-011]dining-room, and Mrs Brooks, who stood within the partly-closed door of her own

[056-012]sitting-room at the back of the passage, could hear fragments of the

[056-013]conversation—if conversation it could be called—between those two

[056-014]wretched souls. She heard Tess re-ascend the stairs to the first floor, and the

[056-015]departure of Clare, and the closing of the front door behind him. Then the door

[056-016]of the room above was shut, and Mrs Brooks knew that Tess had re-entered her

[056-017]apartment. As the young lady was not fully dressed, Mrs Brooks knew that she

[056-018]would not emerge again for some time.

[056-019]She accordingly ascended the stairs softly, and stood at the door of the front

[056-020]room—a drawing-room, connected with the room immediately behind it (which

[056-021]was a bedroom) by folding-doors in the common manner. This first floor,

[056-022]containing Mrs Brooks’s best apartments, had been taken by the week by

[056-023]the d’Urbervilles. The back room was now in silence; but from the

[056-024]drawing-room there came sounds.

[056-025]All that she could at first distinguish of them was one syllable, continually

[056-026]repeated in a low note of moaning, as if it came from a soul bound to some

[056-027]Ixionian wheel—

[056-028]“O—O—O!”

[056-029]Then a silence, then a heavy sigh, and again—

[056-030]“O—O—O!”

[056-031]The landlady looked through the keyhole. Only a small space of the room inside

[056-032]was visible, but within that space came a corner of the breakfast table, which

[056-033]was already spread for the meal, and also a chair beside. Over the seat of the

[056-034]chair Tess’s face was bowed, her posture being a kneeling one in front of

[056-035]it; her hands were clasped over her head, the skirts of her dressing-gown and

[056-036]the embroidery of her night-gown flowed upon the floor behind her, and her

[056-037]stockingless feet, from which the slippers had fallen, protruded upon the

[056-038]carpet. It was from her lips that came the murmur of unspeakable despair.

[056-039]Then a man’s voice from the adjoining bedroom—

[056-040]“What’s the matter?”

[056-041]She did not answer, but went on, in a tone which was a soliloquy rather than an

[056-042]exclamation, and a dirge rather than a soliloquy. Mrs Brooks could only catch a

[056-043]portion:

[056-044]“And then my dear, dear husband came home to me ... and I did not

[056-045]know it!... And you had used your cruel persuasion upon me ... you did

[056-046]not stop using it—no—you did not stop! My little sisters and

[056-047]brothers and my mother’s needs—they were the things you moved me by

[056-048]... and you said my husband would never come back—never; and you

[056-049]taunted me, and said what a simpleton I was to expect him!... And at last

[056-050]I believed you and gave way!... And then he came back! Now he is gone.

[056-051]Gone a second time, and I have lost him now for ever ... and he will not

[056-052]love me the littlest bit ever any more—only hate me!... O yes, I

[056-053]have lost him now—again because of—you!” In writhing, with

[056-054]her head on the chair, she turned her face towards the door, and Mrs Brooks

[056-055]could see the pain upon it, and that her lips were bleeding from the clench of

[056-056]her teeth upon them, and that the long lashes of her closed eyes stuck in wet

[056-057]tags to her cheeks. She continued: “And he is dying—he looks as if

[056-058]he is dying!... And my sin will kill him and not kill me!... O, you

[056-059]have torn my life all to pieces ... made me be what I prayed you in pity

[056-060]not to make me be again!... My own true husband will never, never—O

[056-061]God—I can’t bear this!—I cannot!”

[056-062]There were more and sharper words from the man; then a sudden rustle; she had

[056-063]sprung to her feet. Mrs Brooks, thinking that the speaker was coming to rush

[056-064]out of the door, hastily retreated down the stairs.

[056-065]She need not have done so, however, for the door of the sitting-room was not

[056-066]opened. But Mrs Brooks felt it unsafe to watch on the landing again, and

[056-067]entered her own parlour below.

[056-068]She could hear nothing through the floor, although she listened intently, and

[056-069]thereupon went to the kitchen to finish her interrupted breakfast. Coming up

[056-070]presently to the front room on the ground floor she took up some sewing,

[056-071]waiting for her lodgers to ring that she might take away the breakfast, which

[056-072]she meant to do herself, to discover what was the matter if possible. Overhead,

[056-073]as she sat, she could now hear the floorboards slightly creak, as if some one

[056-074]were walking about, and presently the movement was explained by the rustle of

[056-075]garments against the banisters, the opening and the closing of the front door,

[056-076]and the form of Tess passing to the gate on her way into the street. She was

[056-077]fully dressed now in the walking costume of a well-to-do young lady in which

[056-078]she had arrived, with the sole addition that over her hat and black feathers a

[056-079]veil was drawn.

[056-080]Mrs Brooks had not been able to catch any word of farewell, temporary or

[056-081]otherwise, between her tenants at the door above. They might have quarrelled,

[056-082]or Mr d’Urberville might still be asleep, for he was not an early riser.

[056-083]She went into the back room, which was more especially her own apartment, and

[056-084]continued her sewing there. The lady lodger did not return, nor did the

[056-085]gentleman ring his bell. Mrs Brooks pondered on the delay, and on what probable

[056-086]relation the visitor who had called so early bore to the couple upstairs. In

[056-087]reflecting she leant back in her chair.

[056-088]As she did so her eyes glanced casually over the ceiling till they were

[056-089]arrested by a spot in the middle of its white surface which she had never

[056-090]noticed there before. It was about the size of a wafer when she first observed

[056-091]it, but it speedily grew as large as the palm of her hand, and then she could

[056-092]perceive that it was red. The oblong white ceiling, with this scarlet blot in

[056-093]the midst, had the appearance of a gigantic ace of hearts.

[056-094]Mrs Brooks had strange qualms of misgiving. She got upon the table, and touched

[056-095]the spot in the ceiling with her fingers. It was damp, and she fancied that it

[056-096]was a blood stain.

[056-097]Descending from the table, she left the parlour, and went upstairs, intending

[056-098]to enter the room overhead, which was the bedchamber at the back of the

[056-099]drawing-room. But, nerveless woman as she had now become, she could not bring

[056-100]herself to attempt the handle. She listened. The dead silence within was broken

[056-101]only by a regular beat.

[056-102]Drip, drip, drip.

[056-103]Mrs Brooks hastened downstairs, opened the front door, and ran into the street.

[056-104]A man she knew, one of the workmen employed at an adjoining villa, was passing

[056-105]by, and she begged him to come in and go upstairs with her; she feared

[056-106]something had happened to one of her lodgers. The workman assented, and

[056-107]followed her to the landing.

[056-108]She opened the door of the drawing-room, and stood back for him to pass in,

[056-109]entering herself behind him. The room was empty; the breakfast—a

[056-110]substantial repast of coffee, eggs, and a cold ham—lay spread upon the

[056-111]table untouched, as when she had taken it up, excepting that the carving-knife

[056-112]was missing. She asked the man to go through the folding-doors into the

[056-113]adjoining room.

[056-114]He opened the doors, entered a step or two, and came back almost instantly with

[056-115]a rigid face. “My good God, the gentleman in bed is dead! I think he has

[056-116]been hurt with a knife—a lot of blood has run down upon the floor!”

[056-117]The alarm was soon given, and the house which had lately been so quiet

[056-118]resounded with the tramp of many footsteps, a surgeon among the rest. The wound

[056-119]was small, but the point of the blade had touched the heart of the victim, who

[056-120]lay on his back, pale, fixed, dead, as if he had scarcely moved after the

[056-121]infliction of the blow. In a quarter of an hour the news that a gentleman who

[056-122]was a temporary visitor to the town had been stabbed in his bed, spread through

[056-123]every street and villa of the popular watering-place.
