[010-001]Every village has its idiosyncrasy, its constitution, often its own code of

[010-002]morality. The levity of some of the younger women in and about Trantridge was

[010-003]marked, and was perhaps symptomatic of the choice spirit who ruled The Slopes

[010-004]in that vicinity. The place had also a more abiding defect; it drank hard. The

[010-005]staple conversation on the farms around was on the uselessness of saving money;

[010-006]and smock-frocked arithmeticians, leaning on their ploughs or hoes, would enter

[010-007]into calculations of great nicety to prove that parish relief was a fuller

[010-008]provision for a man in his old age than any which could result from savings out

[010-009]of their wages during a whole lifetime.

[010-010]The chief pleasure of these philosophers lay in going every Saturday night,

[010-011]when work was done, to Chaseborough, a decayed market-town two or three miles

[010-012]distant; and, returning in the small hours of the next morning, to spend Sunday

[010-013]in sleeping off the dyspeptic effects of the curious compounds sold to them as

[010-014]beer by the monopolizers of the once-independent inns.

[010-015]For a long time Tess did not join in the weekly pilgrimages. But under pressure

[010-016]from matrons not much older than herself—for a field-man’s wages

[010-017]being as high at twenty-one as at forty, marriage was early here—Tess at

[010-018]length consented to go. Her first experience of the journey afforded her more

[010-019]enjoyment than she had expected, the hilariousness of the others being quite

[010-020]contagious after her monotonous attention to the poultry-farm all the week. She

[010-021]went again and again. Being graceful and interesting, standing moreover on the

[010-022]momentary threshold of womanhood, her appearance drew down upon her some sly

[010-023]regards from loungers in the streets of Chaseborough; hence, though sometimes

[010-024]her journey to the town was made independently, she always searched for her

[010-025]fellows at nightfall, to have the protection of their companionship homeward.

[010-026]This had gone on for a month or two when there came a Saturday in September, on

[010-027]which a fair and a market coincided; and the pilgrims from Trantridge sought

[010-028]double delights at the inns on that account. Tess’s occupations made her

[010-029]late in setting out, so that her comrades reached the town long before her. It

[010-030]was a fine September evening, just before sunset, when yellow lights struggle

[010-031]with blue shades in hairlike lines, and the atmosphere itself forms a prospect

[010-032]without aid from more solid objects, except the innumerable winged insects that

[010-033]dance in it. Through this low-lit mistiness Tess walked leisurely along.

[010-034]She did not discover the coincidence of the market with the fair till she had

[010-035]reached the place, by which time it was close upon dusk. Her limited marketing

[010-036]was soon completed; and then as usual she began to look about for some of the

[010-037]Trantridge cottagers.

[010-038]At first she could not find them, and she was informed that most of them had

[010-039]gone to what they called a private little jig at the house of a hay-trusser and

[010-040]peat-dealer who had transactions with their farm. He lived in an out-of-the-way

[010-041]nook of the townlet, and in trying to find her course thither her eyes fell

[010-042]upon Mr d’Urberville standing at a street corner.

[010-043]“What—my Beauty? You here so late?” he said.

[010-044]She told him that she was simply waiting for company homeward.

[010-045]“I’ll see you again,” said he over her shoulder as she went

[010-046]on down the back lane.

[010-047]Approaching the hay-trussers, she could hear the fiddled notes of a reel

[010-048]proceeding from some building in the rear; but no sound of dancing was

[010-049]audible—an exceptional state of things for these parts, where as a rule

[010-050]the stamping drowned the music. The front door being open she could see

[010-051]straight through the house into the garden at the back as far as the shades of

[010-052]night would allow; and nobody appearing to her knock, she traversed the

[010-053]dwelling and went up the path to the outhouse whence the sound had attracted

[010-054]her.

[010-055]It was a windowless erection used for storage, and from the open door there

[010-056]floated into the obscurity a mist of yellow radiance, which at first Tess

[010-057]thought to be illuminated smoke. But on drawing nearer she perceived that it

[010-058]was a cloud of dust, lit by candles within the outhouse, whose beams upon the

[010-059]haze carried forward the outline of the doorway into the wide night of the

[010-060]garden.

[010-061]When she came close and looked in she beheld indistinct forms racing up and

[010-062]down to the figure of the dance, the silence of their footfalls arising from

[010-063]their being overshoe in “scroff”—that is to say, the powdery

[010-064]residuum from the storage of peat and other products, the stirring of which by

[010-065]their turbulent feet created the nebulosity that involved the scene. Through

[010-066]this floating, fusty *débris* of peat and hay, mixed with the

[010-067]perspirations and warmth of the dancers, and forming together a sort of

[010-068]vegeto-human pollen, the muted fiddles feebly pushed their notes, in marked

[010-069]contrast to the spirit with which the measure was trodden out. They coughed as

[010-070]they danced, and laughed as they coughed. Of the rushing couples there could

[010-071]barely be discerned more than the high lights—the indistinctness shaping

[010-072]them to satyrs clasping nymphs—a multiplicity of Pans whirling a

[010-073]multiplicity of Syrinxes; Lotis attempting to elude Priapus, and always

[010-074]failing.

[010-075]At intervals a couple would approach the doorway for air, and the haze no

[010-076]longer veiling their features, the demigods resolved themselves into the homely

[010-077]personalities of her own next-door neighbours. Could Trantridge in two or three

[010-078]short hours have metamorphosed itself thus madly!

[010-079]Some Sileni of the throng sat on benches and hay-trusses by the wall; and one

[010-080]of them recognized her.

[010-081]“The maids don’t think it respectable to dance at The

[010-082]Flower-de-Luce,” he explained. “They don’t like to let

[010-083]everybody see which be their fancy-men. Besides, the house sometimes shuts up

[010-084]just when their jints begin to get greased. So we come here and send out for

[010-085]liquor.”

[010-086]“But when be any of you going home?” asked Tess with some anxiety.

[010-087]“Now—a’most directly. This is all but the last jig.”

[010-088]She waited. The reel drew to a close, and some of the party were in the mind of

[010-089]starting. But others would not, and another dance was formed. This surely would

[010-090]end it, thought Tess. But it merged in yet another. She became restless and

[010-091]uneasy; yet, having waited so long, it was necessary to wait longer; on account

[010-092]of the fair the roads were dotted with roving characters of possibly ill

[010-093]intent; and, though not fearful of measurable dangers, she feared the unknown.

[010-094]Had she been near Marlott she would have had less dread.

[010-095]“Don’t ye be nervous, my dear good soul,” expostulated,

[010-096]between his coughs, a young man with a wet face and his straw hat so far back

[010-097]upon his head that the brim encircled it like the nimbus of a saint.

[010-098]“What’s yer hurry? To-morrow is Sunday, thank God, and we can sleep

[010-099]it off in church-time. Now, have a turn with me?”

[010-100]She did not abhor dancing, but she was not going to dance here. The movement

[010-101]grew more passionate: the fiddlers behind the luminous pillar of cloud now and

[010-102]then varied the air by playing on the wrong side of the bridge or with the back

[010-103]of the bow. But it did not matter; the panting shapes spun onwards.

[010-104]They did not vary their partners if their inclination were to stick to previous

[010-105]ones. Changing partners simply meant that a satisfactory choice had not as yet

[010-106]been arrived at by one or other of the pair, and by this time every couple had

[010-107]been suitably matched. It was then that the ecstasy and the dream began, in

[010-108]which emotion was the matter of the universe, and matter but an adventitious

[010-109]intrusion likely to hinder you from spinning where you wanted to spin.

[010-110]Suddenly there was a dull thump on the ground: a couple had fallen, and lay in

[010-111]a mixed heap. The next couple, unable to check its progress, came toppling over

[010-112]the obstacle. An inner cloud of dust rose around the prostrate figures amid the

[010-113]general one of the room, in which a twitching entanglement of arms and legs was

[010-114]discernible.

[010-115]“You shall catch it for this, my gentleman, when you get home!”

[010-116]burst in female accents from the human heap—those of the unhappy partner

[010-117]of the man whose clumsiness had caused the mishap; she happened also to be his

[010-118]recently married wife, in which assortment there was nothing unusual at

[010-119]Trantridge as long as any affection remained between wedded couples; and,

[010-120]indeed, it was not uncustomary in their later lives, to avoid making odd lots

[010-121]of the single people between whom there might be a warm understanding.

[010-122]A loud laugh from behind Tess’s back, in the shade of the garden, united

[010-123]with the titter within the room. She looked round, and saw the red coal of a

[010-124]cigar: Alec d’Urberville was standing there alone. He beckoned to her,

[010-125]and she reluctantly retreated towards him.

[010-126]“Well, my Beauty, what are you doing here?”

[010-127]She was so tired after her long day and her walk that she confided her trouble

[010-128]to him—that she had been waiting ever since he saw her to have their

[010-129]company home, because the road at night was strange to her. “But it seems

[010-130]they will never leave off, and I really think I will wait no longer.”

[010-131]“Certainly do not. I have only a saddle-horse here to-day; but come to

[010-132]The Flower-de-Luce, and I’ll hire a trap, and drive you home with

[010-133]me.”

[010-134]Tess, though flattered, had never quite got over her original mistrust of him,

[010-135]and, despite their tardiness, she preferred to walk home with the work-folk. So

[010-136]she answered that she was much obliged to him, but would not trouble him.

[010-137]“I have said that I will wait for ’em, and they will expect me to

[010-138]now.”

[010-139]“Very well, Miss Independence. Please yourself... Then I shall not

[010-140]hurry... My good Lord, what a kick-up they are having there!”

[010-141]He had not put himself forward into the light, but some of them had perceived

[010-142]him, and his presence led to a slight pause and a consideration of how the time

[010-143]was flying. As soon as he had re-lit a cigar and walked away the Trantridge

[010-144]people began to collect themselves from amid those who had come in from other

[010-145]farms, and prepared to leave in a body. Their bundles and baskets were gathered

[010-146]up, and half an hour later, when the clock-chime sounded a quarter past eleven,

[010-147]they were straggling along the lane which led up the hill towards their homes.

[010-148]It was a three-mile walk, along a dry white road, made whiter to-night by the

[010-149]light of the moon.

[010-150]Tess soon perceived as she walked in the flock, sometimes with this one,

[010-151]sometimes with that, that the fresh night air was producing staggerings and

[010-152]serpentine courses among the men who had partaken too freely; some of the more

[010-153]careless women also were wandering in their gait—to wit, a dark virago,

[010-154]Car Darch, dubbed Queen of Spades, till lately a favourite of

[010-155]d’Urberville’s; Nancy, her sister, nicknamed the Queen of Diamonds;

[010-156]and the young married woman who had already tumbled down. Yet however

[010-157]terrestrial and lumpy their appearance just now to the mean unglamoured eye, to

[010-158]themselves the case was different. They followed the road with a sensation that

[010-159]they were soaring along in a supporting medium, possessed of original and

[010-160]profound thoughts, themselves and surrounding nature forming an organism of

[010-161]which all the parts harmoniously and joyously interpenetrated each other. They

[010-162]were as sublime as the moon and stars above them, and the moon and stars were

[010-163]as ardent as they.

[010-164]Tess, however, had undergone such painful experiences of this kind in her

[010-165]father’s house that the discovery of their condition spoilt the pleasure

[010-166]she was beginning to feel in the moonlight journey. Yet she stuck to the party,

[010-167]for reasons above given.

[010-168]In the open highway they had progressed in scattered order; but now their route

[010-169]was through a field-gate, and the foremost finding a difficulty in opening it,

[010-170]they closed up together.

[010-171]This leading pedestrian was Car the Queen of Spades, who carried a

[010-172]wicker-basket containing her mother’s groceries, her own draperies, and

[010-173]other purchases for the week. The basket being large and heavy, Car had placed

[010-174]it for convenience of porterage on the top of her head, where it rode on in

[010-175]jeopardized balance as she walked with arms akimbo.

[010-176]“Well—whatever is that a-creeping down thy back, Car Darch?”

[010-177]said one of the group suddenly.

[010-178]All looked at Car. Her gown was a light cotton print, and from the back of her

[010-179]head a kind of rope could be seen descending to some distance below her waist,

[010-180]like a Chinaman’s queue.

[010-181]“’Tis her hair falling down,” said another.

[010-182]No; it was not her hair: it was a black stream of something oozing from her

[010-183]basket, and it glistened like a slimy snake in the cold still rays of the moon.

[010-184]“’Tis treacle,” said an observant matron.

[010-185]Treacle it was. Car’s poor old grandmother had a weakness for the sweet

[010-186]stuff. Honey she had in plenty out of her own hives, but treacle was what her

[010-187]soul desired, and Car had been about to give her a treat of surprise. Hastily

[010-188]lowering the basket the dark girl found that the vessel containing the syrup

[010-189]had been smashed within.

[010-190]By this time there had arisen a shout of laughter at the extraordinary

[010-191]appearance of Car’s back, which irritated the dark queen into getting rid

[010-192]of the disfigurement by the first sudden means available, and independently of

[010-193]the help of the scoffers. She rushed excitedly into the field they were about

[010-194]to cross, and flinging herself flat on her back upon the grass, began to wipe

[010-195]her gown as well as she could by spinning horizontally on the herbage and

[010-196]dragging herself over it upon her elbows.

[010-197]The laughter rang louder; they clung to the gate, to the posts, rested on their

[010-198]staves, in the weakness engendered by their convulsions at the spectacle of

[010-199]Car. Our heroine, who had hitherto held her peace, at this wild moment could

[010-200]not help joining in with the rest.

[010-201]It was a misfortune—in more ways than one. No sooner did the dark queen

[010-202]hear the soberer richer note of Tess among those of the other work-people than

[010-203]a long-smouldering sense of rivalry inflamed her to madness. She sprang to her

[010-204]feet and closely faced the object of her dislike.

[010-205]“How darest th’ laugh at me, hussy!” she cried.

[010-206]“I couldn’t really help it when t’others did,”

[010-207]apologized Tess, still tittering.

[010-208]“Ah, th’st think th’ beest everybody, dostn’t, because

[010-209]th’ beest first favourite with He just now! But stop a bit, my lady, stop

[010-210]a bit! I’m as good as two of such! Look here—here’s at

[010-211]’ee!”

[010-212]To Tess’s horror the dark queen began stripping off the bodice of her

[010-213]gown—which for the added reason of its ridiculed condition she was only

[010-214]too glad to be free of—till she had bared her plump neck, shoulders, and

[010-215]arms to the moonshine, under which they looked as luminous and beautiful as

[010-216]some Praxitelean creation, in their possession of the faultless rotundities of

[010-217]a lusty country-girl. She closed her fists and squared up at Tess.

[010-218]“Indeed, then, I shall not fight!” said the latter majestically;

[010-219]“and if I had known you was of that sort, I wouldn’t have so let

[010-220]myself down as to come with such a whorage as this is!”

[010-221]The rather too inclusive speech brought down a torrent of vituperation from

[010-222]other quarters upon fair Tess’s unlucky head, particularly from the Queen

[010-223]of Diamonds, who having stood in the relations to d’Urberville that Car

[010-224]had also been suspected of, united with the latter against the common enemy.

[010-225]Several other women also chimed in, with an animus which none of them would

[010-226]have been so fatuous as to show but for the rollicking evening they had passed.

[010-227]Thereupon, finding Tess unfairly browbeaten, the husbands and lovers tried to

[010-228]make peace by defending her; but the result of that attempt was directly to

[010-229]increase the war.

[010-230]Tess was indignant and ashamed. She no longer minded the loneliness of the way

[010-231]and the lateness of the hour; her one object was to get away from the whole

[010-232]crew as soon as possible. She knew well enough that the better among them would

[010-233]repent of their passion next day. They were all now inside the field, and she

[010-234]was edging back to rush off alone when a horseman emerged almost silently from

[010-235]the corner of the hedge that screened the road, and Alec d’Urberville

[010-236]looked round upon them.

[010-237]“What the devil is all this row about, work-folk?” he asked.

[010-238]The explanation was not readily forthcoming; and, in truth, he did not require

[010-239]any. Having heard their voices while yet some way off he had ridden creepingly

[010-240]forward, and learnt enough to satisfy himself.

[010-241]Tess was standing apart from the rest, near the gate. He bent over towards her.

[010-242]“Jump up behind me,” he whispered, “and we’ll get shot

[010-243]of the screaming cats in a jiffy!”

[010-244]She felt almost ready to faint, so vivid was her sense of the crisis. At almost

[010-245]any other moment of her life she would have refused such proffered aid and

[010-246]company, as she had refused them several times before; and now the loneliness

[010-247]would not of itself have forced her to do otherwise. But coming as the

[010-248]invitation did at the particular juncture when fear and indignation at these

[010-251]his instep, and scrambled into the saddle behind him. The pair were speeding

[010-253]aware of what had happened.

[010-254]The Queen of Spades forgot the stain on her bodice, and stood beside the Queen

[010-255]of Diamonds and the new-married, staggering young woman—all with a gaze

[010-256]of fixity in the direction in which the horse’s tramp was diminishing

[010-257]into silence on the road.

[010-258]“What be ye looking at?” asked a man who had not observed the

[010-259]incident.

[010-260]“Ho-ho-ho!” laughed dark Car.

[010-261]“Hee-hee-hee!” laughed the tippling bride, as she steadied herself

[010-262]on the arm of her fond husband.

[010-263]“Heu-heu-heu!” laughed dark Car’s mother, stroking her

[010-264]moustache as she explained laconically: “Out of the frying-pan into the

[010-265]fire!”

[010-266]Then these children of the open air, whom even excess of alcohol could scarce

[010-267]injure permanently, betook themselves to the field-path; and as they went there

[010-268]moved onward with them, around the shadow of each one’s head, a circle of

[010-269]opalized light, formed by the moon’s rays upon the glistening sheet of

[010-270]dew. Each pedestrian could see no halo but his or her own, which never deserted

[010-271]the head-shadow, whatever its vulgar unsteadiness might be; but adhered to it,

[010-274]night’s mist; and the spirit of the scene, and of the moonlight, and of

[010-275]Nature, seemed harmoniously to mingle with the spirit of wine.
