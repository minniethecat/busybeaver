# Love and Desire

## Definition

Love and desire in *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles* operate as distinct and often opposing forces. Hardy draws a sharp distinction between spiritual love — the selfless devotion [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] feels for [[angel-clare|Angel]] — and physical desire — the possessive, consuming passion Alec feels for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. The tragedy of the novel arises in part from the failure of these two forces to coincide in the right person at the right time.

## Manifestations

### Tess's Love for Angel

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s feeling for [[Angel Clare]] is portrayed as genuine, self-abnegating love. She consistently puts [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s welfare before her own: she accepts his abandonment without protest, endures hardship in silence, and in her final letter declares that his happiness is all she desires. Her love is characterised by purity of intention, even when circumstances force her into actions that contradict that purity.

### Alec's Possessive Desire

[[Alec d'Urberville]]'s pursuit of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is presented as sexual desire masquerading as affection. His offer of marriage after his "conversion" (045-046) is an attempt to legitimise his continued possession of her. Hardy's narrator makes clear that Alec's passion is fundamentally selfish: he pursues [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] not for her sake but for his own gratification.

### Angel's Idealised Love

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is genuine but flawed. He falls in love with an idealised image — a "fresh and virginal daughter of Nature" (018-037) — rather than the real woman with a past. When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] confesses, this ideal shatters, and [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love proves unable to survive contact with reality. His abandonment of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the cruellest act in the novel, precisely because it is performed by someone who genuinely loved her.

### The Fateful Mismatch

Hardy's narrator offers a philosophical meditation on the tragedy of mismatched love:

> Thus the thing began. Had she perceived this meeting's import she might have asked why she was doomed to be seen and coveted that day by the wrong man, and not by some other man, the right and desired one in all respects — as nearly as humanity can supply the right and desired; yet to him who amongst her acquaintance might have approximated to this kind, she was but a transient impression, half forgotten. (005-275–005-280)

> In the ill-judged execution of the well-judged plan of things the call seldom produces the comer, the man to love rarely coincides with the hour for loving. (005-281–005-282)

## Key Passages

> "I suppose—because I don't love you." (011-012)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s simple, honest reply to Alec's demand for an explanation of her coldness. This line establishes the fundamental asymmetry of their relationship: his desire versus her indifference.

> She was a passionate lover of tune. No ditty floated into Blackmoor Vale from the outer world but Tess's mother caught up the air and sang it. (003-060)

The hereditary nature of passion is established early. [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]]'s "passionate" nature foreshadows [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s own capacity for deep feeling — a capacity that becomes both her glory and her undoing.

> Her soul's desire was to continue that offence by which she had been wronged. (014-212)

After the birth and death of [[Sorrow (Tess's Child)|Sorrow]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s attachment to the child — conceived through violation — illustrates how love can grow even from the soil of trauma, complicating any simple moral judgment.

## Related Characters

- [[Tess Durbeyfield]] — the novel's central figure of selfless love
- [[Angel Clare]] — idealistic lover who fails the test of reality
- [[Alec d'Urberville]] — embodies desire as possession and control
- [[Izz Huett]] — a fellow dairymaid whose love for [[angel-clare|Angel]] mirrors [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s devotion

## Analysis

Hardy structures the novel around three competing models of love: Alec's raw desire, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s spiritualised ideal, and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s self-sacrificing devotion. None of these models proves adequate to sustain a human relationship. Alec's desire destroys its object; [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s ideal cannot survive contact with reality; [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s devotion receives no reciprocation.

The narrator's famous meditation — "the man to love rarely coincides with the hour for loving" (005-282) — frames love not as a moral choice but as a matter of cosmic timing, subject to the same indifferent fate that governs the rest of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s existence. This is a deeply pessimistic vision: love is real, but it is helpless against the machinery of circumstance.

## Historical Context

Victorian society sharply distinguished between respectable love (leading to marriage) and disreputable desire (leading to ruin). The "fallen woman" who experienced desire outside marriage was permanently stigmatised, while the man who desired her faced no equivalent social penalty. Hardy's novel challenges this double standard by making [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — the "fallen woman" — the moral centre of the story, and by showing that her capacity for love far exceeds that of the "respectable" characters who judge her.
