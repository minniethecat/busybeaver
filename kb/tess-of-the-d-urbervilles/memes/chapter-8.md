[008-001]Having mounted beside her, Alec d’Urberville drove rapidly along the

[008-002]crest of the first hill, chatting compliments to Tess as they went, the cart

[008-003]with her box being left far behind. Rising still, an immense landscape

[008-004]stretched around them on every side; behind, the green valley of her birth,

[008-005]before, a gray country of which she knew nothing except from her first brief

[008-006]visit to Trantridge. Thus they reached the verge of an incline down which the

[008-007]road stretched in a long straight descent of nearly a mile.

[008-008]Ever since the accident with her father’s horse Tess Durbeyfield,

[008-009]courageous as she naturally was, had been exceedingly timid on wheels; the

[008-010]least irregularity of motion startled her. She began to get uneasy at a certain

[008-011]recklessness in her conductor’s driving.

[008-012]“You will go down slow, sir, I suppose?” she said with attempted

[008-013]unconcern.

[008-014]D’Urberville looked round upon her, nipped his cigar with the tips of his

[008-015]large white centre-teeth, and allowed his lips to smile slowly of themselves.

[008-016]“Why, Tess,” he answered, after another whiff or two, “it

[008-017]isn’t a brave bouncing girl like you who asks that? Why, I always go down

[008-018]at full gallop. There’s nothing like it for raising your spirits.”

[008-019]“But perhaps you need not now?”

[008-020]“Ah,” he said, shaking his head, “there are two to be

[008-021]reckoned with. It is not me alone. Tib has to be considered, and she has a very

[008-022]queer temper.”

[008-023]“Who?”

[008-024]“Why, this mare. I fancy she looked round at me in a very grim way just

[008-025]then. Didn’t you notice it?”

[008-026]“Don’t try to frighten me, sir,” said Tess stiffly.

[008-027]“Well, I don’t. If any living man can manage this horse I can: I

[008-028]won’t say any living man can do it—but if such has the power, I am

[008-029]he.”

[008-030]“Why do you have such a horse?”

[008-031]“Ah, well may you ask it! It was my fate, I suppose. Tib has killed one

[008-032]chap; and just after I bought her she nearly killed me. And then, take my word

[008-033]for it, I nearly killed her. But she’s touchy still, very touchy; and

[008-034]one’s life is hardly safe behind her sometimes.”

[008-035]They were just beginning to descend; and it was evident that the horse, whether

[008-036]of her own will or of his (the latter being the more likely), knew so well the

[008-037]reckless performance expected of her that she hardly required a hint from

[008-038]behind.

[008-039]Down, down, they sped, the wheels humming like a top, the dog-cart rocking

[008-040]right and left, its axis acquiring a slightly oblique set in relation to the

[008-041]line of progress; the figure of the horse rising and falling in undulations

[008-042]before them. Sometimes a wheel was off the ground, it seemed, for many yards;

[008-043]sometimes a stone was sent spinning over the hedge, and flinty sparks from the

[008-044]horse’s hoofs outshone the daylight. The aspect of the straight road

[008-045]enlarged with their advance, the two banks dividing like a splitting stick; one

[008-046]rushing past at each shoulder.

[008-047]The wind blew through Tess’s white muslin to her very skin, and her

[008-048]washed hair flew out behind. She was determined to show no open fear, but she

[008-049]clutched d’Urberville’s rein-arm.

[008-050]“Don’t touch my arm! We shall be thrown out if you do! Hold on

[008-051]round my waist!”

[008-052]She grasped his waist, and so they reached the bottom.

[008-053]“Safe, thank God, in spite of your fooling!” said she, her face on

[008-054]fire.

[008-055]“Tess—fie! that’s temper!” said d’Urberville.

[008-056]“’Tis truth.”

[008-057]“Well, you need not let go your hold of me so thanklessly the moment you

[008-058]feel yourself out of danger.”

[008-059]She had not considered what she had been doing; whether he were man or woman,

[008-060]stick or stone, in her involuntary hold on him. Recovering her reserve, she sat

[008-061]without replying, and thus they reached the summit of another declivity.

[008-062]“Now then, again!” said d’Urberville.

[008-063]“No, no!” said Tess. “Show more sense, do, please.”

[008-064]“But when people find themselves on one of the highest points in the

[008-065]county, they must get down again,” he retorted.

[008-066]He loosened rein, and away they went a second time. D’Urberville turned

[008-067]his face to her as they rocked, and said, in playful raillery: “Now then,

[008-068]put your arms round my waist again, as you did before, my Beauty.”

[008-069]“Never!” said Tess independently, holding on as well as she could

[008-070]without touching him.

[008-071]“Let me put one little kiss on those holmberry lips, Tess, or even on

[008-072]that warmed cheek, and I’ll stop—on my honour, I will!”

[008-073]Tess, surprised beyond measure, slid farther back still on her seat, at which

[008-074]he urged the horse anew, and rocked her the more.

[008-075]“Will nothing else do?” she cried at length, in desperation, her

[008-076]large eyes staring at him like those of a wild animal. This dressing her up so

[008-077]prettily by her mother had apparently been to lamentable purpose.

[008-078]“Nothing, dear Tess,” he replied.

[008-079]“Oh, I don’t know—very well; I don’t mind!” she

[008-080]panted miserably.

[008-081]He drew rein, and as they slowed he was on the point of imprinting the desired

[008-082]salute, when, as if hardly yet aware of her own modesty, she dodged aside. His

[008-083]arms being occupied with the reins there was left him no power to prevent her

[008-084]manœuvre.

[008-085]“Now, damn it—I’ll break both our necks!” swore her

[008-086]capriciously passionate companion. “So you can go from your word like

[008-087]that, you young witch, can you?”

[008-088]“Very well,” said Tess, “I’ll not move since you be so

[008-089]determined! But I—thought you would be kind to me, and protect me, as my

[008-090]kinsman!”

[008-091]“Kinsman be hanged! Now!”

[008-092]“But I don’t want anybody to kiss me, sir!” she implored, a

[008-093]big tear beginning to roll down her face, and the corners of her mouth

[008-094]trembling in her attempts not to cry. “And I wouldn’t ha’

[008-095]come if I had known!”

[008-096]He was inexorable, and she sat still, and d’Urberville gave her the kiss

[008-097]of mastery. No sooner had he done so than she flushed with shame, took out her

[008-098]handkerchief, and wiped the spot on her cheek that had been touched by his

[008-099]lips. His ardour was nettled at the sight, for the act on her part had been

[008-100]unconsciously done.

[008-101]“You are mighty sensitive for a cottage girl!” said the young man.

[008-102]Tess made no reply to this remark, of which, indeed, she did not quite

[008-103]comprehend the drift, unheeding the snub she had administered by her

[008-104]instinctive rub upon her cheek. She had, in fact, undone the kiss, as far as

[008-105]such a thing was physically possible. With a dim sense that he was vexed she

[008-106]looked steadily ahead as they trotted on near Melbury Down and Wingreen, till

[008-107]she saw, to her consternation, that there was yet another descent to be

[008-108]undergone.

[008-109]“You shall be made sorry for that!” he resumed, his injured tone

[008-110]still remaining, as he flourished the whip anew. “Unless, that is, you

[008-111]agree willingly to let me do it again, and no handkerchief.”

[008-112]She sighed. “Very well, sir!” she said. “Oh—let me get

[008-113]my hat!”

[008-114]At the moment of speaking her hat had blown off into the road, their present

[008-115]speed on the upland being by no means slow. D’Urberville pulled up, and

[008-116]said he would get it for her, but Tess was down on the other side.

[008-117]She turned back and picked up the article.

[008-118]“You look prettier with it off, upon my soul, if that’s

[008-119]possible,” he said, contemplating her over the back of the vehicle.

[008-120]“Now then, up again! What’s the matter?”

[008-121]The hat was in place and tied, but Tess had not stepped forward.

[008-122]“No, sir,” she said, revealing the red and ivory of her mouth as

[008-123]her eye lit in defiant triumph; “not again, if I know it!”

[008-124]“What—you won’t get up beside me?”

[008-125]“No; I shall walk.”

[008-126]“’Tis five or six miles yet to Trantridge.”

[008-127]“I don’t care if ’tis dozens. Besides, the cart is

[008-128]behind.”

[008-129]“You artful hussy! Now, tell me—didn’t you make that hat blow

[008-130]off on purpose? I’ll swear you did!”

[008-131]Her strategic silence confirmed his suspicion.

[008-132]Then d’Urberville cursed and swore at her, and called her everything he

[008-133]could think of for the trick. Turning the horse suddenly he tried to drive back

[008-134]upon her, and so hem her in between the gig and the hedge. But he could not do

[008-135]this short of injuring her.

[008-136]“You ought to be ashamed of yourself for using such wicked words!”

[008-137]cried Tess with spirit, from the top of the hedge into which she had scrambled.

[008-138]“I don’t like ’ee at all! I hate and detest you! I’ll

[008-139]go back to mother, I will!”

[008-140]D’Urberville’s bad temper cleared up at sight of hers; and he

[008-141]laughed heartily.

[008-142]“Well, I like you all the better,” he said. “Come, let there

[008-143]be peace. I’ll never do it any more against your will. My life upon it

[008-144]now!”

[008-145]Still Tess could not be induced to remount. She did not, however, object to his

[008-146]keeping his gig alongside her; and in this manner, at a slow pace, they

[008-147]advanced towards the village of Trantridge. From time to time

[008-148]d’Urberville exhibited a sort of fierce distress at the sight of the

[008-149]tramping he had driven her to undertake by his misdemeanour. She might in truth

[008-150]have safely trusted him now; but he had forfeited her confidence for the time,

[008-151]and she kept on the ground progressing thoughtfully, as if wondering whether it

[008-152]would be wiser to return home. Her resolve, however, had been taken, and it

[008-153]seemed vacillating even to childishness to abandon it now, unless for graver

[008-154]reasons. How could she face her parents, get back her box, and disconcert the

[008-155]whole scheme for the rehabilitation of her family on such sentimental grounds?

[008-156]A few minutes later the chimneys of The Slopes appeared in view, and in a snug

[008-157]nook to the right the poultry-farm and cottage of Tess’s destination.
