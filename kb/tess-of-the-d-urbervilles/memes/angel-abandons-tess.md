# Angel Abandons Tess

## Summary

On the night of their wedding at [[wellbridge|Wellbridge farmhouse]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] exchange confessions. [[angel-clare|Angel]] admits to a youthful "dissipation" in London — an affair he has long since put behind him. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], relieved by his honesty and believing her own confession will be equally forgiven, tells him of her history with [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] and the child she bore. (034-258–035-120)

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s response is devastating. The woman he married — the pure, innocent milkmaid of [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] — dissolves before his eyes. "[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]!" he cries, "I cannot forgive you!" (035-125). He cannot reconcile the "real" [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] with the idealized image he has constructed. The morning after, Clare rises in "a dawn that was ashy and furtive, as though associated with crime" (036-001–036-002). Within days he arranges passage to Brazil, leaving [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to return to her family in disgrace — a married woman with no husband.

## Context

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s abandonment occurs immediately after the wedding, in the very farmhouse that once belonged to the d'Urberville ancestors. The irony is deliberate: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], the true heir of the d'Urbervilles, is rejected in a house built by her own forebears. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s hypocrisy — he expects forgiveness for his own sexual past while refusing it to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — is the novel's sharpest critique of Victorian sexual double standards.

[[angel-clare|Angel]] has spent months at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] constructing an idealized [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]: a pure daughter of nature, unsullied by the world. His love is for an abstraction, not a woman. When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] reveals her history, it is not the act itself that [[angel-clare|Angel]] cannot forgive — it is the gap between his fantasy and her reality.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s confession is an act of honesty and courage. Having been forgiven by [[angel-clare|Angel]] for his past, she believes herself owed the same grace. Her devastation when he refuses is compounded by her genuine love for him: she has, in her own words, "forgiven him everything" and cannot understand why he will not do the same.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s rejection reveals the limits of his progressive ideals. The freethinking agnostic who has rejected his father's evangelical [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]] proves incapable of rejecting its sexual morality. His abandonment of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the act of a coward: rather than confront the complexity of the woman he married, he flees to another continent.

## Narrative Impact

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s abandonment is the second great catastrophe of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s life, following Alec's violation. Where the first destroyed her body, the second destroys her hope. The pattern is cruelly symmetrical: Alec takes her physically; [[angel-clare|Angel]] takes her spiritually. Between them, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is granted no space to exist as a complete person.

The abandonment also sets in motion the novel's final movement: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s return to [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], her re-encounter with Alec, her ultimate surrender to him, and the murder that leads to her execution. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s departure for Brazil is not an ending but a deferral — his return will come too late.

## Analysis

### The Double Standard

[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] embodies the Victorian sexual double standard in its most insidious form: it is not that he is a hypocrite in any simple sense (he confesses his own past freely) but that he applies different standards to male and female sexuality. His own "dissipation" is a youthful peccadillo; [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s violation is a permanent stain. Hardy's critique is structural: [[angel-clare|Angel]] cannot see his own inconsistency because the culture that formed him renders it invisible.

### The Idealized Woman

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has always been love for an idea. At [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] he called her "Artemis" and "Demeter" — goddess-names that deny her humanity. When the real [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] emerges — a woman with a history, a body that has been used, a child who died — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s goddess shatters. The tragedy is not that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] concealed her past but that [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love was too weak to survive the truth.
