# Alec's Conversion

## Summary

On her return journey from [[emminster|Emminster]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] hears a fiery Methodist preacher in a barn at [[evershead|Evershead]]. The voice belongs to [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]], who has undergone a dramatic religious conversion under the influence of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s father, [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]]. When Alec recognizes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] among the congregation, his eloquence collapses.

He abandons his calling, pursues [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], and renounces his faith entirely, declaring her the innocent cause of his backsliding. His conversion and its collapse expose the superficial, theatrical quality of his religious transformation.

## Context

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is working at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], abandoned by [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] for nearly a year. Stung by [[izz-huett|Izz Huett]]'s revelation that [[angel-clare|Angel]] had invited her to Brazil, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] resolves to seek help from [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s parents at [[emminster|Emminster Vicarage]].

She walks fifteen miles on a Sunday morning (044-027), but loses courage after being inadvertently humiliated by [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s brothers and [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]], who discover her discarded boots and mock her as a tramp (044-186–044-193).

On her despondent return journey, she stops at [[evershead|Evershead]] and hears preaching in a barn. The sermon is of "the extremest antinomian type" (044-261), expounding justification by faith.

The preacher describes his own conversion from "the greatest of sinners" through the influence of a clergyman whose parting words "had sunk into his heart" (044-274–044-278). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] recognizes the voice with a shock of disbelief — it belongs to [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] (044-280–044-281).

Hardy notes that Alec's transformation was catalyzed by both [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]] and personal loss: "the loss of my mother hit me most; and by degrees I was brought to see daylight" (045-129). The convert now preaches throughout the district, painting Scripture texts on gates and stiles (045-188–045-193).

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is at her most vulnerable: abandoned, impoverished, and returning from a failed attempt to seek help. Her unexpected appearance disrupts Alec's religious performance.

The sight of her drains his eloquence instantly (045-044–045-045). She rejects his proposal of marriage, defends her absent husband, and inadvertently supplies the intellectual arguments that complete his de-conversion.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — The seducer turned preacher. Alec's conversion was catalyzed by [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]], who visited [[trantridge|Trantridge]] several years earlier and told him "those who came to scoff sometimes remained to pray" (045-127–045-128).

Recognition of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] undoes his faith within moments. His pursuit reveals that religious enthusiasm and sensual desire are continuous in him — not opposed forces. The narrator later confirms that "Reason had had nothing to do with his whimsical conversion" (046-380).

## Narrative Impact

The conversion brings Alec back into [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s life after four years, reactivating the central triangle of the novel. His abandonment of preaching for passion mirrors the pattern of impulsive self-indulgence established at [[the-slopes|The Slopes]]. The episode demonstrates that his transformations are performances — changes of costume rather than character.

The event connects the Clare family directly to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s tragedy. [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]], in winning Alec's soul, inadvertently creates the instrument of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s second fall.

The irony is compounded when [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] deploys [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s skeptical arguments against Alec's faith, unwittingly demolishing the religious restraint that might have kept him at a distance. Alec later realizes that [[angel-clare|Angel]] "might be paving my way back to her" (046-386–046-387).

The conversion episode also establishes the psychological dynamic for the later threshing-machine scene and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s eventual return to Alec. Once his religious costume is discarded, Alec's pursuit becomes relentless, and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s material vulnerability at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] leaves her with diminishing options for resistance.

## Key Passages

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] hears the sermon text repeated as she stands outside the barn:

> "O foolish Galatians, who hath bewitched you, that ye should not obey the truth, before whose eyes Jesus Christ hath been evidently set forth, crucified among you?" (044-267)

She recognizes the preacher's voice:

> "But more startling to Tess than the doctrine had been the voice, which, impossible as it seemed, was precisely that of Alec d'Urberville." (044-280–044-281)

Hardy describes the nature of Alec's transformation:

> "It was less a reform than a transfiguration. The former curves of sensuousness were now modulated to lines of devotional passion." (045-019)

Alec renounces his preaching to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]:

> "I do believe I ought to preach it, but, like the devils, I believe and tremble, for I suddenly leave off preaching it, and give way to my passion for you." (046-313–046-314)

Alec declares [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] the cause of his spiritual collapse:

> "I thought I worshipped on the mountains, but I find I still serve in the groves!" (046-334)

## Analysis

### Performance vs. Transformation

Hardy frames Alec's conversion as theatrical rather than genuine. The same features that once expressed sensuality now display religious passion: "animalism had become fanaticism; Paganism, Paulinism" (045-023). This is not a change of heart but a redirection of the same energies. Hardy's term "transfiguration" (045-019) is ironic — it suggests surface alteration, not inner renewal.

The speed of Alec's collapse confirms this reading. When he recognizes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], "his fire, the tumultuous ring of his eloquence, seemed to go out of him" (045-045). The conversion that took months to develop dissolves in seconds.

The narrator later states plainly that it was "the mere freak of a careless man in search of a new sensation, and temporarily impressed by his mother's death" (046-381–046-382).

### The Irony of Reverend Clare

[[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]]'s evangelical success produces catastrophic results. His words at [[trantridge|Trantridge]] — "those who came to scoff sometimes remained to pray" (045-127–045-128) — set in motion the chain of events that returns Alec to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. The elder Clare's Christian charity extends to the seducer, yet never reaches the victim.

This irony is structural. [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]]'s theology of saving "Publicans and Sinners" (044-224) makes Alec a prime candidate for conversion. But the same framework cannot accommodate [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], whose suffering falls into the invisible category of "the worries of Scribes and Pharisees" (044-225). The Clares' compassion bypasses the person who most urgently needs it.

### Religion as Costume

Alec's conversion manifests as outward alteration. He wears "half-clerical" dress, has grown "old-fashioned whiskers," and his moustache is gone (045-010–045-011). When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] appears, the costume drops. When [[farmer-groby|Farmer Groby]] threatens [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in the field, Alec's face blackens with "something that was not [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]]" (046-178).

Hardy suggests religious enthusiasm and sexual passion are continuous rather than opposed. Alec's own confession collapses the distinction: "I thought I worshipped on the mountains, but I find I still serve in the groves!" (046-334).

Biblical devotion and pagan sensuality are revealed as two registers of the same impulse. The novel treats this not as Alec's unique failing but as a truth about the psychology of conversion itself.
