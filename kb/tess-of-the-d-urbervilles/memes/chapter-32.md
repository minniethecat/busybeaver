[032-001]This penitential mood kept her from naming the wedding-day. The beginning of

[032-002]November found its date still in abeyance, though he asked her at the most

[032-003]tempting times. But Tess’s desire seemed to be for a perpetual betrothal

[032-004]in which everything should remain as it was then.

[032-005]The meads were changing now; but it was still warm enough in early afternoons

[032-006]before milking to idle there awhile, and the state of dairy-work at this time

[032-007]of year allowed a spare hour for idling. Looking over the damp sod in the

[032-008]direction of the sun, a glistening ripple of gossamer webs was visible to their

[032-009]eyes under the luminary, like the track of moonlight on the sea. Gnats, knowing

[032-010]nothing of their brief glorification, wandered across the shimmer of this

[032-011]pathway, irradiated as if they bore fire within them, then passed out of its

[032-012]line, and were quite extinct. In the presence of these things he would remind

[032-013]her that the date was still the question.

[032-014]Or he would ask her at night, when he accompanied her on some mission invented

[032-015]by Mrs Crick to give him the opportunity. This was mostly a journey to the

[032-016]farmhouse on the slopes above the vale, to inquire how the advanced cows were

[032-017]getting on in the straw-barton to which they were relegated. For it was a time

[032-018]of the year that brought great changes to the world of kine. Batches of the

[032-019]animals were sent away daily to this lying-in hospital, where they lived on

[032-020]straw till their calves were born, after which event, and as soon as the calf

[032-021]could walk, mother and offspring were driven back to the dairy. In the interval

[032-022]which elapsed before the calves were sold there was, of course, little milking

[032-023]to be done, but as soon as the calf had been taken away the milkmaids would

[032-024]have to set to work as usual.

[032-025]Returning from one of these dark walks they reached a great gravel-cliff

[032-026]immediately over the levels, where they stood still and listened. The water was

[032-027]now high in the streams, squirting through the weirs, and tinkling under

[032-028]culverts; the smallest gullies were all full; there was no taking short cuts

[032-029]anywhere, and foot-passengers were compelled to follow the permanent ways. From

[032-030]the whole extent of the invisible vale came a multitudinous intonation; it

[032-031]forced upon their fancy that a great city lay below them, and that the murmur

[032-032]was the vociferation of its populace.

[032-033]“It seems like tens of thousands of them,” said Tess;

[032-034]“holding public-meetings in their market-places, arguing, preaching,

[032-035]quarrelling, sobbing, groaning, praying, and cursing.”

[032-036]Clare was not particularly heeding.

[032-037]“Did Crick speak to you to-day, dear, about his not wanting much

[032-038]assistance during the winter months?”

[032-039]“No.”

[032-040]“The cows are going dry rapidly.”

[032-041]“Yes. Six or seven went to the straw-barton yesterday, and three the day

[032-042]before, making nearly twenty in the straw already. Ah—is it that the

[032-043]farmer don’t want my help for the calving? O, I am not wanted here any

[032-044]more! And I have tried so hard to—”

[032-045]“Crick didn’t exactly say that he would no longer require you. But,

[032-046]knowing what our relations were, he said in the most good-natured and

[032-047]respectful manner possible that he supposed on my leaving at Christmas I should

[032-048]take you with me, and on my asking what he would do without you he merely

[032-049]observed that, as a matter of fact, it was a time of year when he could do with

[032-050]a very little female help. I am afraid I was sinner enough to feel rather glad

[032-051]that he was in this way forcing your hand.”

[032-052]“I don’t think you ought to have felt glad, Angel. Because

[032-053]’tis always mournful not to be wanted, even if at the same time

[032-054]’tis convenient.”

[032-055]“Well, it is convenient—you have admitted that.” He put his

[032-056]finger upon her cheek. “Ah!” he said.

[032-057]“What?”

[032-058]“I feel the red rising up at her having been caught! But why should I

[032-059]trifle so! We will not trifle—life is too serious.”

[032-060]“It is. Perhaps I saw that before you did.”

[032-061]She was seeing it then. To decline to marry him after all—in obedience to

[032-062]her emotion of last night—and leave the dairy, meant to go to some

[032-063]strange place, not a dairy; for milkmaids were not in request now calving-time

[032-064]was coming on; to go to some arable farm where no divine being like Angel Clare

[032-065]was. She hated the thought, and she hated more the thought of going home.

[032-066]“So that, seriously, dearest Tess,” he continued, “since you

[032-067]will probably have to leave at Christmas, it is in every way desirable and

[032-068]convenient that I should carry you off then as my property. Besides, if you

[032-069]were not the most uncalculating girl in the world you would know that we could

[032-070]not go on like this for ever.”

[032-071]“I wish we could. That it would always be summer and autumn, and you

[032-072]always courting me, and always thinking as much of me as you have done through

[032-073]the past summer-time!”

[032-074]“I always shall.”

[032-075]“O, I know you will!” she cried, with a sudden fervour of faith in

[032-076]him. “Angel, I will fix the day when I will become yours for

[032-077]always!”

[032-078]Thus at last it was arranged between them, during that dark walk home, amid the

[032-079]myriads of liquid voices on the right and left.

[032-080]When they reached the dairy Mr and Mrs Crick were promptly told—with

[032-081]injunctions of secrecy; for each of the lovers was desirous that the marriage

[032-082]should be kept as private as possible. The dairyman, though he had thought of

[032-083]dismissing her soon, now made a great concern about losing her. What should he

[032-084]do about his skimming? Who would make the ornamental butter-pats for the

[032-085]Anglebury and Sandbourne ladies? Mrs Crick congratulated Tess on the

[032-086]shilly-shallying having at last come to an end, and said that directly she set

[032-087]eyes on Tess she divined that she was to be the chosen one of somebody who was

[032-088]no common outdoor man; Tess had looked so superior as she walked across the

[032-089]barton on that afternoon of her arrival; that she was of a good family she

[032-090]could have sworn. In point of fact Mrs Crick did remember thinking that Tess

[032-091]was graceful and good-looking as she approached; but the superiority might have

[032-092]been a growth of the imagination aided by subsequent knowledge.

[032-093]Tess was now carried along upon the wings of the hours, without the sense of a

[032-094]will. The word had been given; the number of the day written down. Her

[032-095]naturally bright intelligence had begun to admit the fatalistic convictions

[032-096]common to field-folk and those who associate more extensively with natural

[032-097]phenomena than with their fellow-creatures; and she accordingly drifted into

[032-098]that passive responsiveness to all things her lover suggested, characteristic

[032-099]of the frame of mind.

[032-100]But she wrote anew to her mother, ostensibly to notify the wedding-day; really

[032-101]to again implore her advice. It was a gentleman who had chosen her, which

[032-102]perhaps her mother had not sufficiently considered. A post-nuptial explanation,

[032-103]which might be accepted with a light heart by a rougher man, might not be

[032-104]received with the same feeling by him. But this communication brought no reply

[032-105]from Mrs Durbeyfield.

[032-106]Despite Angel Clare’s plausible representation to himself and to Tess of

[032-107]the practical need for their immediate marriage, there was in truth an element

[032-108]of precipitancy in the step, as became apparent at a later date. He loved her

[032-109]dearly, though perhaps rather ideally and fancifully than with the impassioned

[032-110]thoroughness of her feeling for him. He had entertained no notion, when doomed

[032-111]as he had thought to an unintellectual bucolic life, that such charms as he

[032-112]beheld in this idyllic creature would be found behind the scenes.

[032-113]Unsophistication was a thing to talk of; but he had not known how it really

[032-114]struck one until he came here. Yet he was very far from seeing his future track

[032-115]clearly, and it might be a year or two before he would be able to consider

[032-116]himself fairly started in life. The secret lay in the tinge of recklessness

[032-117]imparted to his career and character by the sense that he had been made to miss

[032-118]his true destiny through the prejudices of his family.

[032-119]“Don’t you think ’twould have been better for us to wait till

[032-120]you were quite settled in your midland farm?” she once asked timidly. (A

[032-121]midland farm was the idea just then.)

[032-122]“To tell the truth, my Tess, I don’t like you to be left anywhere

[032-123]away from my protection and sympathy.”

[032-124]The reason was a good one, so far as it went. His influence over her had been

[032-125]so marked that she had caught his manner and habits, his speech and phrases,

[032-126]his likings and his aversions. And to leave her in farmland would be to let her

[032-127]slip back again out of accord with him. He wished to have her under his charge

[032-128]for another reason. His parents had naturally desired to see her once at least

[032-129]before he carried her off to a distant settlement, English or colonial; and as

[032-130]no opinion of theirs was to be allowed to change his intention, he judged that

[032-131]a couple of months’ life with him in lodgings whilst seeking for an

[032-132]advantageous opening would be of some social assistance to her at what she

[032-133]might feel to be a trying ordeal—her presentation to his mother at the

[032-134]Vicarage.

[032-135]Next, he wished to see a little of the working of a flour-mill, having an idea

[032-136]that he might combine the use of one with corn-growing. The proprietor of a

[032-137]large old water-mill at Wellbridge—once the mill of an Abbey—had

[032-138]offered him the inspection of his time-honoured mode of procedure, and a hand

[032-139]in the operations for a few days, whenever he should choose to come. Clare paid

[032-140]a visit to the place, some few miles distant, one day at this time, to inquire

[032-141]particulars, and returned to Talbothays in the evening. She found him

[032-142]determined to spend a short time at the Wellbridge flour-mills. And what had

[032-143]determined him? Less the opportunity of an insight into grinding and bolting

[032-144]than the casual fact that lodgings were to be obtained in that very farmhouse

[032-145]which, before its mutilation, had been the mansion of a branch of the

[032-146]d’Urberville family. This was always how Clare settled practical

[032-147]questions; by a sentiment which had nothing to do with them. They decided to go

[032-148]immediately after the wedding, and remain for a fortnight, instead of

[032-149]journeying to towns and inns.

[032-150]“Then we will start off to examine some farms on the other side of London

[032-151]that I have heard of,” he said, “and by March or April we will pay

[032-152]a visit to my father and mother.”

[032-153]Questions of procedure such as these arose and passed, and the day, the

[032-154]incredible day, on which she was to become his, loomed large in the near

[032-155]future. The thirty-first of December, New Year’s Eve, was the date. His

[032-156]wife, she said to herself. Could it ever be? Their two selves together, nothing

[032-157]to divide them, every incident shared by them; why not? And yet why?

[032-158]One Sunday morning Izz Huett returned from church, and spoke privately to Tess.

[032-159]“You was not called home this morning.”

[032-160]“What?”

[032-161]“It should ha’ been the first time of asking to-day,” she

[032-162]answered, looking quietly at Tess. “You meant to be married New

[032-163]Year’s Eve, deary?”

[032-164]The other returned a quick affirmative.

[032-165]“And there must be three times of asking. And now there be only two

[032-166]Sundays left between.”

[032-167]Tess felt her cheek paling; Izz was right; of course there must be three.

[032-168]Perhaps he had forgotten! If so, there must be a week’s postponement, and

[032-169]that was unlucky. How could she remind her lover? She who had been so backward

[032-170]was suddenly fired with impatience and alarm lest she should lose her dear

[032-171]prize.

[032-172]A natural incident relieved her anxiety. Izz mentioned the omission of the

[032-173]banns to Mrs Crick, and Mrs Crick assumed a matron’s privilege of

[032-174]speaking to Angel on the point.

[032-175]“Have ye forgot ’em, Mr Clare? The banns, I mean.”

[032-176]“No, I have not forgot ’em,” says Clare.

[032-177]As soon as he caught Tess alone he assured her:

[032-178]“Don’t let them tease you about the banns. A licence will be

[032-179]quieter for us, and I have decided on a licence without consulting you. So if

[032-180]you go to church on Sunday morning you will not hear your own name, if you

[032-181]wished to.”

[032-182]“I didn’t wish to hear it, dearest,” she said proudly.

[032-183]But to know that things were in train was an immense relief to Tess

[032-184]notwithstanding, who had well-nigh feared that somebody would stand up and

[032-185]forbid the banns on the ground of her history. How events were favouring her!

[032-186]“I don’t quite feel easy,” she said to herself. “All

[032-187]this good fortune may be scourged out of me afterwards by a lot of ill.

[032-188]That’s how Heaven mostly does. I wish I could have had common

[032-189]banns!”

[032-190]But everything went smoothly. She wondered whether he would like her to be

[032-191]married in her present best white frock, or if she ought to buy a new one. The

[032-192]question was set at rest by his forethought, disclosed by the arrival of some

[032-193]large packages addressed to her. Inside them she found a whole stock of

[032-194]clothing, from bonnet to shoes, including a perfect morning costume, such as

[032-195]would well suit the simple wedding they planned. He entered the house shortly

[032-196]after the arrival of the packages, and heard her upstairs undoing them.

[032-197]A minute later she came down with a flush on her face and tears in her eyes.

[032-198]“How thoughtful you’ve been!” she murmured, her cheek upon

[032-199]his shoulder. “Even to the gloves and handkerchief! My own love—how

[032-200]good, how kind!”

[032-201]“No, no, Tess; just an order to a tradeswoman in London—nothing

[032-202]more.”

[032-203]And to divert her from thinking too highly of him, he told her to go upstairs,

[032-204]and take her time, and see if it all fitted; and, if not, to get the village

[032-205]sempstress to make a few alterations.

[032-206]She did return upstairs, and put on the gown. Alone, she stood for a moment

[032-207]before the glass looking at the effect of her silk attire; and then there came

[032-208]into her head her mother’s ballad of the mystic robe—

[032-209]That never would become that wife

[032-210]That had once done amiss,

[032-211]which Mrs Durbeyfield had used to sing to her as a child, so blithely and so

[032-212]archly, her foot on the cradle, which she rocked to the tune. Suppose this robe

[032-213]should betray her by changing colour, as her robe had betrayed Queen Guinevere.

[032-214]Since she had been at the dairy she had not once thought of the lines till now.
