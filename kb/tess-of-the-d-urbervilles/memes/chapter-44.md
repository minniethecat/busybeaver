[044-001]By the disclosure in the barn her thoughts were led anew in the direction which

[044-002]they had taken more than once of late—to the distant Emminster Vicarage.

[044-003]It was through her husband’s parents that she had been charged to send a

[044-004]letter to Clare if she desired; and to write to them direct if in difficulty.

[044-005]But that sense of her having morally no claim upon him had always led Tess to

[044-006]suspend her impulse to send these notes; and to the family at the Vicarage,

[044-007]therefore, as to her own parents since her marriage, she was virtually

[044-008]non-existent. This self-effacement in both directions had been quite in

[044-009]consonance with her independent character of desiring nothing by way of favour

[044-010]or pity to which she was not entitled on a fair consideration of her deserts.

[044-011]She had set herself to stand or fall by her qualities, and to waive such merely

[044-012]technical claims upon a strange family as had been established for her by the

[044-013]flimsy fact of a member of that family, in a season of impulse, writing his

[044-014]name in a church-book beside hers.

[044-015]But now that she was stung to a fever by Izz’s tale, there was a limit to

[044-016]her powers of renunciation. Why had her husband not written to her? He had

[044-017]distinctly implied that he would at least let her know of the locality to which

[044-018]he had journeyed; but he had not sent a line to notify his address. Was he

[044-019]really indifferent? But was he ill? Was it for her to make some advance? Surely

[044-020]she might summon the courage of solicitude, call at the Vicarage for

[044-021]intelligence, and express her grief at his silence. If Angel’s father

[044-022]were the good man she had heard him represented to be, he would be able to

[044-023]enter into her heart-starved situation. Her social hardships she could conceal.

[044-024]To leave the farm on a week-day was not in her power; Sunday was the only

[044-025]possible opportunity. Flintcomb-Ash being in the middle of the cretaceous

[044-026]tableland over which no railway had climbed as yet, it would be necessary to

[044-027]walk. And the distance being fifteen miles each way she would have to allow

[044-028]herself a long day for the undertaking by rising early.

[044-029]A fortnight later, when the snow had gone, and had been followed by a hard

[044-030]black frost, she took advantage of the state of the roads to try the

[044-031]experiment. At four o’clock that Sunday morning she came downstairs and

[044-032]stepped out into the starlight. The weather was still favourable, the ground

[044-033]ringing under her feet like an anvil.

[044-034]Marian and Izz were much interested in her excursion, knowing that the journey

[044-035]concerned her husband. Their lodgings were in a cottage a little further along

[044-036]the lane, but they came and assisted Tess in her departure, and argued that she

[044-037]should dress up in her very prettiest guise to captivate the hearts of her

[044-038]parents-in-law; though she, knowing of the austere and Calvinistic tenets of

[044-039]old Mr Clare, was indifferent, and even doubtful. A year had now elapsed since

[044-040]her sad marriage, but she had preserved sufficient draperies from the wreck of

[044-041]her then full wardrobe to clothe her very charmingly as a simple country girl

[044-042]with no pretensions to recent fashion; a soft gray woollen gown, with white

[044-043]crape quilling against the pink skin of her face and neck, and a black velvet

[044-044]jacket and hat.

[044-045]“’Tis a thousand pities your husband can’t see ’ee

[044-046]now—you do look a real beauty!” said Izz Huett, regarding Tess as

[044-047]she stood on the threshold between the steely starlight without and the yellow

[044-048]candlelight within. Izz spoke with a magnanimous abandonment of herself to the

[044-049]situation; she could not be—no woman with a heart bigger than a hazel-nut

[044-050]could be—antagonistic to Tess in her presence, the influence which she

[044-051]exercised over those of her own sex being of a warmth and strength quite

[044-052]unusual, curiously overpowering the less worthy feminine feelings of spite and

[044-053]rivalry.

[044-054]With a final tug and touch here, and a slight brush there, they let her go; and

[044-055]she was absorbed into the pearly air of the fore-dawn. They heard her footsteps

[044-056]tap along the hard road as she stepped out to her full pace. Even Izz hoped she

[044-057]would win, and, though without any particular respect for her own virtue, felt

[044-058]glad that she had been prevented wronging her friend when momentarily tempted

[044-059]by Clare.

[044-060]It was a year ago, all but a day, that Clare had married Tess, and only a few

[044-061]days less than a year that he had been absent from her. Still, to start on a

[044-062]brisk walk, and on such an errand as hers, on a dry clear wintry morning,

[044-063]through the rarefied air of these chalky hogs’-backs, was not depressing;

[044-064]and there is no doubt that her dream at starting was to win the heart of her

[044-065]mother-in-law, tell her whole history to that lady, enlist her on her side, and

[044-066]so gain back the truant.

[044-067]In time she reached the edge of the vast escarpment below which stretched the

[044-068]loamy Vale of Blackmoor, now lying misty and still in the dawn. Instead of the

[044-069]colourless air of the uplands, the atmosphere down there was a deep blue.

[044-070]Instead of the great enclosures of a hundred acres in which she was now

[044-071]accustomed to toil, there were little fields below her of less than

[044-072]half-a-dozen acres, so numerous that they looked from this height like the

[044-073]meshes of a net. Here the landscape was whitey-brown; down there, as in Froom

[044-074]Valley, it was always green. Yet it was in that vale that her sorrow had taken

[044-075]shape, and she did not love it as formerly. Beauty to her, as to all who have

[044-076]felt, lay not in the thing, but in what the thing symbolized.

[044-077]Keeping the Vale on her right, she steered steadily westward; passing above the

[044-078]Hintocks, crossing at right-angles the high-road from Sherton-Abbas to

[044-079]Casterbridge, and skirting Dogbury Hill and High-Stoy, with the dell between

[044-080]them called “The Devil’s Kitchen”. Still following the

[044-081]elevated way she reached Cross-in-Hand, where the stone pillar stands desolate

[044-082]and silent, to mark the site of a miracle, or murder, or both. Three miles

[044-083]further she cut across the straight and deserted Roman road called Long-Ash

[044-084]Lane; leaving which as soon as she reached it she dipped down a hill by a

[044-085]transverse lane into the small town or village of Evershead, being now about

[044-086]halfway over the distance. She made a halt here, and breakfasted a second time,

[044-087]heartily enough—not at the Sow-and-Acorn, for she avoided inns, but at a

[044-088]cottage by the church.

[044-089]The second half of her journey was through a more gentle country, by way of

[044-090]Benvill Lane. But as the mileage lessened between her and the spot of her

[044-091]pilgrimage, so did Tess’s confidence decrease, and her enterprise loom

[044-092]out more formidably. She saw her purpose in such staring lines, and the

[044-093]landscape so faintly, that she was sometimes in danger of losing her way.

[044-094]However, about noon she paused by a gate on the edge of the basin in which

[044-095]Emminster and its Vicarage lay.

[044-096]The square tower, beneath which she knew that at that moment the Vicar and his

[044-097]congregation were gathered, had a severe look in her eyes. She wished that she

[044-098]had somehow contrived to come on a week-day. Such a good man might be

[044-099]prejudiced against a woman who had chosen Sunday, never realizing the

[044-100]necessities of her case. But it was incumbent upon her to go on now. She took

[044-101]off the thick boots in which she had walked thus far, put on her pretty thin

[044-102]ones of patent leather, and, stuffing the former into the hedge by the gatepost

[044-103]where she might readily find them again, descended the hill; the freshness of

[044-104]colour she had derived from the keen air thinning away in spite of her as she

[044-105]drew near the parsonage.

[044-106]Tess hoped for some accident that might favour her, but nothing favoured her.

[044-107]The shrubs on the Vicarage lawn rustled uncomfortably in the frosty breeze; she

[044-108]could not feel by any stretch of imagination, dressed to her highest as she

[044-109]was, that the house was the residence of near relations; and yet nothing

[044-110]essential, in nature or emotion, divided her from them: in pains, pleasures,

[044-111]thoughts, birth, death, and after-death, they were the same.

[044-112]She nerved herself by an effort, entered the swing-gate, and rang the

[044-113]door-bell. The thing was done; there could be no retreat. No; the thing was not

[044-114]done. Nobody answered to her ringing. The effort had to be risen to and made

[044-115]again. She rang a second time, and the agitation of the act, coupled with her

[044-116]weariness after the fifteen miles’ walk, led her to support herself while

[044-117]she waited by resting her hand on her hip and her elbow against the wall of the

[044-118]porch. The wind was so nipping that the ivy-leaves had become wizened and gray,

[044-119]each tapping incessantly upon its neighbour with a disquieting stir of her

[044-120]nerves. A piece of blood-stained paper, caught up from some meat-buyer’s

[044-121]dust-heap, beat up and down the road without the gate; too flimsy to rest, too

[044-122]heavy to fly away; and a few straws kept it company.

[044-123]The second peal had been louder, and still nobody came. Then she walked out of

[044-124]the porch, opened the gate, and passed through. And though she looked dubiously

[044-125]at the house-front as if inclined to return, it was with a breath of relief

[044-126]that she closed the gate. A feeling haunted her that she might have been

[044-127]recognized (though how she could not tell), and orders been given not to admit

[044-128]her.

[044-129]Tess went as far as the corner. She had done all she could do; but determined

[044-130]not to escape present trepidation at the expense of future distress, she walked

[044-131]back again quite past the house, looking up at all the windows.

[044-132]Ah—the explanation was that they were all at church, every one. She

[044-133]remembered her husband saying that his father always insisted upon the

[044-134]household, servants included, going to morning-service, and, as a consequence,

[044-135]eating cold food when they came home. It was, therefore, only necessary to wait

[044-136]till the service was over. She would not make herself conspicuous by waiting on

[044-137]the spot, and she started to get past the church into the lane. But as she

[044-138]reached the churchyard-gate the people began pouring out, and Tess found

[044-139]herself in the midst of them.

[044-140]The Emminster congregation looked at her as only a congregation of small

[044-141]country-townsfolk walking home at its leisure can look at a woman out of the

[044-142]common whom it perceives to be a stranger. She quickened her pace, and ascended

[044-143]the road by which she had come, to find a retreat between its hedges till the

[044-144]Vicar’s family should have lunched, and it might be convenient for them

[044-145]to receive her. She soon distanced the churchgoers, except two youngish men,

[044-146]who, linked arm-in-arm, were beating up behind her at a quick step.

[044-147]As they drew nearer she could hear their voices engaged in earnest discourse,

[044-148]and, with the natural quickness of a woman in her situation, did not fail to

[044-149]recognize in those noises the quality of her husband’s tones. The

[044-150]pedestrians were his two brothers. Forgetting all her plans, Tess’s one

[044-151]dread was lest they should overtake her now, in her disorganized condition,

[044-152]before she was prepared to confront them; for though she felt that they could

[044-153]not identify her, she instinctively dreaded their scrutiny. The more briskly

[044-154]they walked, the more briskly walked she. They were plainly bent upon taking a

[044-155]short quick stroll before going indoors to lunch or dinner, to restore warmth

[044-156]to limbs chilled with sitting through a long service.

[044-157]Only one person had preceded Tess up the hill—a ladylike young woman,

[044-158]somewhat interesting, though, perhaps, a trifle *guindée* and prudish.

[044-159]Tess had nearly overtaken her when the speed of her brothers-in-law brought

[044-160]them so nearly behind her back that she could hear every word of their

[044-161]conversation. They said nothing, however, which particularly interested her

[044-162]till, observing the young lady still further in front, one of them remarked,

[044-163]“There is Mercy Chant. Let us overtake her.”

[044-164]Tess knew the name. It was the woman who had been destined for Angel’s

[044-165]life-companion by his and her parents, and whom he probably would have married

[044-166]but for her intrusive self. She would have known as much without previous

[044-167]information if she had waited a moment, for one of the brothers proceeded to

[044-168]say: “Ah! poor Angel, poor Angel! I never see that nice girl without more

[044-169]and more regretting his precipitancy in throwing himself away upon a dairymaid,

[044-170]or whatever she may be. It is a queer business, apparently. Whether she has

[044-171]joined him yet or not I don’t know; but she had not done so some months

[044-172]ago when I heard from him.”

[044-173]“I can’t say. He never tells me anything nowadays. His

[044-174]ill-considered marriage seems to have completed that estrangement from me which

[044-175]was begun by his extraordinary opinions.”

[044-176]Tess beat up the long hill still faster; but she could not outwalk them without

[044-177]exciting notice. At last they outsped her altogether, and passed her by. The

[044-178]young lady still further ahead heard their footsteps and turned. Then there was

[044-179]a greeting and a shaking of hands, and the three went on together.

[044-180]They soon reached the summit of the hill, and, evidently intending this point

[044-181]to be the limit of their promenade, slackened pace and turned all three aside

[044-182]to the gate whereat Tess had paused an hour before that time to reconnoitre the

[044-183]town before descending into it. During their discourse one of the clerical

[044-184]brothers probed the hedge carefully with his umbrella, and dragged something to

[044-185]light.

[044-186]“Here’s a pair of old boots,” he said. “Thrown away, I

[044-187]suppose, by some tramp or other.”

[044-188]“Some imposter who wished to come into the town barefoot, perhaps, and so

[044-189]excite our sympathies,” said Miss Chant. “Yes, it must have been,

[044-190]for they are excellent walking-boots—by no means worn out. What a wicked

[044-191]thing to do! I’ll carry them home for some poor person.”

[044-192]Cuthbert Clare, who had been the one to find them, picked them up for her with

[044-193]the crook of his stick; and Tess’s boots were appropriated.

[044-194]She, who had heard this, walked past under the screen of her woollen veil till,

[044-195]presently looking back, she perceived that the church party had left the gate

[044-196]with her boots and retreated down the hill.

[044-197]Thereupon our heroine resumed her walk. Tears, blinding tears, were running

[044-198]down her face. She knew that it was all sentiment, all baseless impressibility,

[044-199]which had caused her to read the scene as her own condemnation; nevertheless

[044-200]she could not get over it; she could not contravene in her own defenceless

[044-201]person all those untoward omens. It was impossible to think of returning to the

[044-202]Vicarage. Angel’s wife felt almost as if she had been hounded up that

[044-203]hill like a scorned thing by those—to her—superfine clerics.

[044-204]Innocently as the slight had been inflicted, it was somewhat unfortunate that

[044-205]she had encountered the sons and not the father, who, despite his narrowness,

[044-206]was far less starched and ironed than they, and had to the full the gift of

[044-207]charity. As she again thought of her dusty boots she almost pitied those

[044-208]habiliments for the quizzing to which they had been subjected, and felt how

[044-209]hopeless life was for their owner.

[044-210]“Ah!” she said, still sighing in pity of herself,

[044-211]“ *they* didn’t know that I wore those over the roughest part

[044-212]of the road to save these pretty ones *he* bought for

[044-213]me—no—they did not know it! And they didn’t think that *he* chose the colour o’ my pretty frock—no—how could

[044-214]they? If they had known perhaps they would not have cared, for they don’t

[044-215]care much for him, poor thing!”

[044-216]Then she grieved for the beloved man whose conventional standard of judgement

[044-217]had caused her all these latter sorrows; and she went her way without knowing

[044-218]that the greatest misfortune of her life was this feminine loss of courage at

[044-219]the last and critical moment through her estimating her father-in-law by his

[044-220]sons. Her present condition was precisely one which would have enlisted the

[044-221]sympathies of old Mr and Mrs Clare. Their hearts went out of them at a bound

[044-222]towards extreme cases, when the subtle mental troubles of the less desperate

[044-223]among mankind failed to win their interest or regard. In jumping at Publicans

[044-224]and Sinners they would forget that a word might be said for the worries of

[044-225]Scribes and Pharisees; and this defect or limitation might have recommended

[044-226]their own daughter-in-law to them at this moment as a fairly choice sort of

[044-227]lost person for their love.

[044-228]Thereupon she began to plod back along the road by which she had come not

[044-229]altogether full of hope, but full of a conviction that a crisis in her life was

[044-230]approaching. No crisis, apparently, had supervened; and there was nothing left

[044-232]summon courage to face the Vicarage. She did, indeed, take sufficient interest

[044-233]in herself to throw up her veil on this return journey, as if to let the world

[044-234]see that she could at least exhibit a face such as Mercy Chant could not show.

[044-235]But it was done with a sorry shake of the head. “It is nothing—it

[044-236]is nothing!” she said. “Nobody loves it; nobody sees it. Who cares

[044-237]about the looks of a castaway like me!”

[044-238]Her journey back was rather a meander than a march. It had no sprightliness, no

[044-239]purpose; only a tendency. Along the tedious length of Benvill Lane she began to

[044-240]grow tired, and she leant upon gates and paused by milestones.

[044-241]She did not enter any house till, at the seventh or eighth mile, she descended

[044-242]the steep long hill below which lay the village or townlet of Evershead, where

[044-243]in the morning she had breakfasted with such contrasting expectations. The

[044-244]cottage by the church, in which she again sat down, was almost the first at

[044-245]that end of the village, and while the woman fetched her some milk from the

[044-246]pantry, Tess, looking down the street, perceived that the place seemed quite

[044-247]deserted.

[044-248]“The people are gone to afternoon service, I suppose?” she said.

[044-249]“No, my dear,” said the old woman. “’Tis too soon for

[044-250]that; the bells hain’t strook out yet. They be all gone to hear the

[044-251]preaching in yonder barn. A ranter preaches there between the services—an

[044-252]excellent, fiery, Christian man, they say. But, Lord, I don’t go to

[044-253]hear’n! What comes in the regular way over the pulpit is hot enough for

[044-254]I.”

[044-255]Tess soon went onward into the village, her footsteps echoing against the

[044-256]houses as though it were a place of the dead. Nearing the central part, her

[044-257]echoes were intruded on by other sounds; and seeing the barn not far off the

[044-258]road, she guessed these to be the utterances of the preacher.

[044-263]rhapsodist was delivered with animated enthusiasm, in a manner entirely

[044-264]declamatory, for he had plainly no skill as a dialectician. Although Tess had

[044-265]not heard the beginning of the address, she learnt what the text had been from

[044-266]its constant iteration—

[044-267]“O foolish Galatians, who hath bewitched you, that ye should not obey the

[044-268]truth, before whose eyes Jesus Christ hath been evidently set forth, crucified

[044-269]among you?”
