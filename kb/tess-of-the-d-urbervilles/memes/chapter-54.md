[054-001]In a quarter of an hour Clare was leaving the house, whence his mother watched

[054-002]his thin figure as it disappeared into the street. He had declined to borrow

[054-003]his father’s old mare, well knowing of its necessity to the household. He

[054-004]went to the inn, where he hired a trap, and could hardly wait during the

[054-005]harnessing. In a very few minutes after, he was driving up the hill out of the

[054-006]town which, three or four months earlier in the year, Tess had descended with

[054-007]such hopes and ascended with such shattered purposes.

[054-008]Benvill Lane soon stretched before him, its hedges and trees purple with buds;

[054-009]but he was looking at other things, and only recalled himself to the scene

[054-010]sufficiently to enable him to keep the way. In something less than an

[054-011]hour-and-a-half he had skirted the south of the King’s Hintock estates

[054-012]and ascended to the untoward solitude of Cross-in-Hand, the unholy stone

[054-013]whereon Tess had been compelled by Alec d’Urberville, in his whim of

[054-014]reformation, to swear the strange oath that she would never wilfully tempt him

[054-015]again. The pale and blasted nettle-stems of the preceding year even now

[054-016]lingered nakedly in the banks, young green nettles of the present spring

[054-017]growing from their roots.

[054-018]Thence he went along the verge of the upland overhanging the other Hintocks,

[054-019]and, turning to the right, plunged into the bracing calcareous region of

[054-020]Flintcomb-Ash, the address from which she had written to him in one of the

[054-021]letters, and which he supposed to be the place of sojourn referred to by her

[054-022]mother. Here, of course, he did not find her; and what added to his depression

[054-023]was the discovery that no “Mrs Clare” had ever been heard of by the

[054-024]cottagers or by the farmer himself, though Tess was remembered well enough by

[054-025]her Christian name. His name she had obviously never used during their

[054-026]separation, and her dignified sense of their total severance was shown not much

[054-027]less by this abstention than by the hardships she had chosen to undergo (of

[054-028]which he now learnt for the first time) rather than apply to his father for

[054-029]more funds.

[054-030]From this place they told him Tess Durbeyfield had gone, without due notice, to

[054-031]the home of her parents on the other side of Blackmoor, and it therefore became

[054-032]necessary to find Mrs Durbeyfield. She had told him she was not now at Marlott,

[054-033]but had been curiously reticent as to her actual address, and the only course

[054-034]was to go to Marlott and inquire for it. The farmer who had been so churlish

[054-035]with Tess was quite smooth-tongued to Clare, and lent him a horse and man to

[054-036]drive him towards Marlott, the gig he had arrived in being sent back to

[054-037]Emminster; for the limit of a day’s journey with that horse was reached.

[054-038]Clare would not accept the loan of the farmer’s vehicle for a further

[054-039]distance than to the outskirts of the Vale, and, sending it back with the man

[054-040]who had driven him, he put up at an inn, and next day entered on foot the

[054-041]region wherein was the spot of his dear Tess’s birth. It was as yet too

[054-042]early in the year for much colour to appear in the gardens and foliage; the

[054-043]so-called spring was but winter overlaid with a thin coat of greenness, and it

[054-044]was of a parcel with his expectations.

[054-045]The house in which Tess had passed the years of her childhood was now inhabited

[054-046]by another family who had never known her. The new residents were in the

[054-047]garden, taking as much interest in their own doings as if the homestead had

[054-048]never passed its primal time in conjunction with the histories of others,

[054-049]beside which the histories of these were but as a tale told by an idiot. They

[054-050]walked about the garden paths with thoughts of their own concerns entirely

[054-051]uppermost, bringing their actions at every moment in jarring collision with the

[054-052]dim ghosts behind them, talking as though the time when Tess lived there were

[054-053]not one whit intenser in story than now. Even the spring birds sang over their

[054-054]heads as if they thought there was nobody missing in particular.

[054-055]On inquiry of these precious innocents, to whom even the name of their

[054-056]predecessors was a failing memory, Clare learned that John Durbeyfield was

[054-057]dead; that his widow and children had left Marlott, declaring that they were

[054-058]going to live at Kingsbere, but instead of doing so had gone on to another

[054-059]place they mentioned. By this time Clare abhorred the house for ceasing to

[054-060]contain Tess, and hastened away from its hated presence without once looking

[054-061]back.

[054-062]His way was by the field in which he had first beheld her at the dance. It was

[054-063]as bad as the house—even worse. He passed on through the churchyard,

[054-064]where, amongst the new headstones, he saw one of a somewhat superior design to

[054-065]the rest. The inscription ran thus:

[054-066]In memory of John Durbeyfield, rightly d’Urberville, of the once powerful

[054-067]family of that Name, and Direct Descendant through an illustrious Line from Sir

[054-068]Pagan d’Urberville, one of the Knights of the Conqueror. Died March 10th,

[054-069]18—

[054-070]How are the Mighty Fallen.

[054-071]Some man, apparently the sexton, had observed Clare standing there, and drew

[054-072]nigh. “Ah, sir, now that’s a man who didn’t want to lie here,

[054-073]but wished to be carried to Kingsbere, where his ancestors be.”

[054-074]“And why didn’t they respect his wish?”

[054-075]“Oh—no money. Bless your soul, sir, why—there, I

[054-076]wouldn’t wish to say it everywhere, but—even this headstone, for

[054-077]all the flourish wrote upon en, is not paid for.”

[054-078]“Ah, who put it up?”

[054-079]The man told the name of a mason in the village, and, on leaving the

[054-080]churchyard, Clare called at the mason’s house. He found that the

[054-081]statement was true, and paid the bill. This done, he turned in the direction of

[054-082]the migrants.

[054-083]The distance was too long for a walk, but Clare felt such a strong desire for

[054-084]isolation that at first he would neither hire a conveyance nor go to a

[054-085]circuitous line of railway by which he might eventually reach the place. At

[054-086]Shaston, however, he found he must hire; but the way was such that he did not

[054-087]enter Joan’s place till about seven o’clock in the evening, having

[054-088]traversed a distance of over twenty miles since leaving Marlott.

[054-089]The village being small he had little difficulty in finding Mrs

[054-090]Durbeyfield’s tenement, which was a house in a walled garden, remote from

[054-091]the main road, where she had stowed away her clumsy old furniture as best she

[054-092]could. It was plain that for some reason or other she had not wished him to

[054-093]visit her, and he felt his call to be somewhat of an intrusion. She came to the

[054-094]door herself, and the light from the evening sky fell upon her face.

[054-095]This was the first time that Clare had ever met her, but he was too preoccupied

[054-096]to observe more than that she was still a handsome woman, in the garb of a

[054-097]respectable widow. He was obliged to explain that he was Tess’s husband,

[054-098]and his object in coming there, and he did it awkwardly enough. “I want

[054-099]to see her at once,” he added. “You said you would write to me

[054-100]again, but you have not done so.”

[054-101]“Because she’ve not come home,” said Joan.

[054-102]“Do you know if she is well?”

[054-103]“I don’t. But you ought to, sir,” said she.

[054-104]“I admit it. Where is she staying?”

[054-105]From the beginning of the interview Joan had disclosed her embarrassment by

[054-106]keeping her hand to the side of her cheek.

[054-107]“I—don’t know exactly where she is staying,” she

[054-108]answered. “She was—but—”

[054-109]“Where was she?”

[054-110]“Well, she is not there now.”

[054-111]In her evasiveness she paused again, and the younger children had by this time

[054-112]crept to the door, where, pulling at his mother’s skirts, the youngest

[054-113]murmured—

[054-114]“Is this the gentleman who is going to marry Tess?”

[054-115]“He has married her,” Joan whispered. “Go inside.”

[054-116]Clare saw her efforts for reticence, and asked—

[054-117]“Do you think Tess would wish me to try and find her? If not, of

[054-118]course—”

[054-119]“I don’t think she would.”

[054-120]“Are you sure?”

[054-121]“I am sure she wouldn’t.”

[054-122]He was turning away; and then he thought of Tess’s tender letter.

[054-123]“I am sure she would!” he retorted passionately. “I know her

[054-124]better than you do.”

[054-125]“That’s very likely, sir; for I have never really known her.”

[054-126]“Please tell me her address, Mrs Durbeyfield, in kindness to a lonely

[054-127]wretched man!” Tess’s mother again restlessly swept her cheek with

[054-128]her vertical hand, and seeing that he suffered, she at last said, is a low

[054-129]voice—

[054-130]“She is at Sandbourne.”

[054-131]“Ah—where there? Sandbourne has become a large place, they

[054-132]say.”

[054-133]“I don’t know more particularly than I have said—Sandbourne.

[054-134]For myself, I was never there.”

[054-135]It was apparent that Joan spoke the truth in this, and he pressed her no

[054-136]further.

[054-137]“Are you in want of anything?” he said gently.

[054-138]“No, sir,” she replied. “We are fairly well provided

[054-139]for.”

[054-140]Without entering the house Clare turned away. There was a station three miles

[054-141]ahead, and paying off his coachman, he walked thither. The last train to

[054-142]Sandbourne left shortly after, and it bore Clare on its wheels.
