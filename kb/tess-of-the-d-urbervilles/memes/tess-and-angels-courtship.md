# Tess and Angel's Courtship

## Summary

[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] arrives at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays Dairy]] as a gentleman-pupil learning farming after abandoning his intended career in the Church. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]], seeking a new beginning after the death of her child, arrives at the same dairy to work as a milkmaid. Their meeting is quietly charged: "[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] rises out of the past not altogether as a distinct figure, but as an appreciative voice" (018-001).

The courtship unfolds across the summer and autumn months in the fertile Valley of the Great Dairies. [[angel-clare|Angel]] is drawn to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] by her beauty, her "unsophisticated open-air existence" (018-148), and what he perceives as her purity.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], for her part, regards [[angel-clare|Angel]] as "an intelligence rather than as a man" (019-148) — a being of higher perception whose interest in her is both thrilling and terrifying.

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s proposal is repeatedly delayed by [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s sense of unworthiness. She believes her past disqualifies her from marriage to a gentleman, and she defers answering him through the autumn and winter. When she finally accepts, it is with a mixture of joy and dread — joy that he loves her, dread that he will discover what she has concealed.

## Context

The courtship occupies Chapters 18 through 31 of the novel — the entire span of Phase the Third (The Rally) and Phase the Fourth (The Consequence). These chapters form the novel's pastoral heart, set in the lush dairy country of the [[froom-valley|Froom Valley]], a landscape of abundance and fertility that stands in stark contrast to the barren uplands of [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] will later suffer.

[[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays Dairy]] is portrayed as an Edenic community. [[dairyman-crick|Dairyman Crick]] presides over a household in which class distinctions are softened: [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]], the gentleman, dines at the same table as the milkmaids. "Sitting down as a level member of the dairyman's household seemed at the outset an undignified proceeding — but the influences of the place worked on him" (018-140).

The courtship is shadowed throughout by [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s secret. [[angel-clare|Angel]] does not know of her past with [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]], and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — despite resolving several times to tell him — cannot bring herself to speak. This concealment is not presented as deceit but as the natural consequence of a moral standard that would destroy her for a violation she did not commit.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — The pursued and pursuer. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is simultaneously drawn to and frightened by [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love. Her repeated attempts to confess her past are thwarted by circumstance, by her mother's injunction of secrecy (032-081), and by her own terror of losing him.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — The suitor. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is genuine but idealizing — he loves the pure milkmaid of his imagination rather than the real woman with a history. His intellectual liberalism, which leads him to reject his father's evangelical [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]], coexists with a deep emotional conservatism about female purity that will prove fatal on the wedding night.

## Narrative Impact

The courtship at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] is the longest sustained section of happiness in the novel, and its function is structural: the more beautiful and convincing the pastoral idyll, the more devastating its destruction. Hardy lavishes descriptive attention on the dairy, the landscape, and the rituals of rural life precisely to make the reader feel the value of what will be lost.

The courtship also develops the novel's central theme of concealment and confession. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s inability to speak her past is not portrayed as moral failure but as a rational response to a world that punishes honesty in women. When she finally does confess — on her wedding night — the very quality [[angel-clare|Angel]] claimed to love in her (her honesty) becomes the instrument of her destruction.

## Key Passages

**[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s Arrival at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]]**:
> Angel Clare rises out of the past not altogether as a distinct figure, but as an appreciative voice. ... So we find Angel Clare at six-and-twenty here at Talbothays as a student of the dairy. (018-001–018-115)

**[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s First Impression**:
> At first Tess seemed to regard Angel Clare as an intelligence rather than as a man. (019-148)

**The Dairyman's Assessment**:
> "Mr Clare," said the dairyman emphatically, "is one of the most rebellest rozums you ever knowed — not a bit of a countryman." (019-214)

**[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s Idealization**:
> She was no longer the milkmaid, but a visionary essence of woman — a whole sex condensed into one typical form. He called her Artemis, Demeter. (020-044)

**[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s Reluctance**:
> "I cannot be your wife, Mr Clare — I cannot be!" (028-091)

**The Acceptance**:
> Tess's heart ached. There was no help for it. She loved him so well that she could bear anything, even this misgiving of his, rather than lose him. (023-156)

**The Ominous Joy**:
> She was no longer the milkmaid of Talbothays but the bride of Angel Clare — and yet the old sorrows, keeping back the gloomy spectres that waited like wolves, seemed to close upon her. (031-133)

## Analysis

### The Pastoral Trap

[[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] is a deliberately heightened pastoral world — Hardy's version of Eden. Yet even within this paradise, the serpent is present. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] cannot fully inhabit her happiness because she knows her past disqualifies her from it.

The pastoral setting is not a refuge from the novel's moral universe but its most vivid expression: in a world where female purity is the supreme value, no woman with a history can be at home.

### The Idealizing Lover

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s courtship of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is a study in the dangers of idealization. He loves not [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] but his image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — Artemis, Demeter, the pure daughter of the soil. When she reveals herself to be a real woman with a sexual history, his ideal collapses, and with it his love. The courtship chapters contain, in embryo, the tragedy of the wedding night.
