# Tess's Wandering After Abandonment

## Summary

Following [[Angel Clare]]'s departure for Brazil and her failed return to [[marlott|Marlott]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] undertakes a winter journey across the Wessex uplands in search of agricultural work. She walks from farm to farm, her hopes diminishing with each rejection, until she reaches the barren chalk plateau of [[Flintcomb-Ash]]. To protect herself from male attention, she disfigures her appearance — cutting off her eyebrows and binding her face with a handkerchief (042-013–042-019). The journey strips [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of every external marker of her identity as [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s wife: her wedding ring is hidden on a ribbon around her neck (042-112–042-113), and she reunites with [[Marian]], now coarsened by drink and hardship. The chapter ends with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] signing on for the roughest field labour at a "starve-acre place" (042-129), having found "a shelter for the winter at any rate" (042-161).

## Context

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is now entirely alone. [[angel-clare|Angel]] has sailed for Brazil without sending his address. Her parents' cottage offered no refuge — her bed had been given to her younger siblings, and even her father doubted the validity of her marriage. She has taken refuge in the fiction that she is joining her husband, preserving his reputation at the cost of her own isolation.

The economic realities of her situation are stark. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has only half the money [[angel-clare|Angel]] gave her — she left the other half with her mother. She must find work that will sustain her through the winter. But the rural economy of 19th-century Wessex, with its seasonal cycles and limited opportunities for solitary women, offers little. Each farm she approaches represents a diminishing hope.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — This chapter presents [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at her most radically self-effacing. She deliberately destroys her own beauty: "with her little scissors, by the aid of a pocket looking-glass, she mercilessly nipped her eyebrows off" (042-018–042-019). The self-mutilation is both practical strategy and psychological surrender — she is erasing the face [[angel-clare|Angel]] loved, inscribing her abandonment on her body. Yet her resilience persists: "But I don't care!... I'll always be ugly now, because [[angel-clare|Angel]] is not here, and I have nobody to take care of me" (042-024–042-026).

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s wedding ring, worn on a ribbon around her neck rather than on her finger (042-112–042-113), is the chapter's central symbol. The ring is still there — her marriage is real to her — but hidden, because the world will not honor it and would only complicate her search for work.

**[[Marian]]** — [[marian|Marian]]'s reappearance is a measure of how far both women have fallen since [[Talbothays Dairy]]. Then [[marian|Marian]] was a fellow dairymaid; now she is "even stouter and redder in the face than formerly, and decidedly shabbier in attire" (042-093–042-094), and confesses she has "got out o' that since I took to drink" (042-132). Yet [[marian|Marian]] retains a rough loyalty — she promises to keep [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s secret and introduces her to the farm.

## Narrative Impact

This wandering chapter is the novel's most sustained portrait of rural female poverty. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s journey from farm to farm, "beginning with the dairy and poultry tendance that she liked best" and ending with "the heavy and course pursuits which she liked least — work on arable land" (042-052–042-055), traces a downward economic spiral that mirrors her psychological trajectory. The landscape itself grows harsher as she ascends from the fertile valleys to the chalk uplands — "There were few trees, or none, those that would have grown in the hedges being mercilessly plashed down" (042-061–042-063).

The chapter's emotional register oscillates between defiance and despair. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s cry of "I don't care!" (042-024) is assertion and surrender in equal measure. Her arrival at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] — "There seemed to be no help for it; hither she was doomed to come" (042-073) — closes the chapter on a note of exhausted acceptance that foreshadows the suffering to come.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s self-disfigurement:

> Then with her little scissors, by the aid of a pocket looking-glass, she mercilessly nipped her eyebrows off, and thus insured against aggressive admiration, she went on her uneven way. (042-018–042-020)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s defiant self-address:

> "But I don't care!" she said. "O no — I don't care! I'll always be ugly now, because Angel is not here, and I have nobody to take care of me. My husband that was is gone away, and never will love me any more; but I love him just the same, and hate all other men, and like to make 'em think scornfully of me!" (042-024–042-028)

The landscape portrait of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as fieldwoman:

> Thus Tess walks on; a figure which is part of the landscape; a fieldwoman pure and simple, in winter guise... Inside this exterior, over which the eye might have roved as over a thing scarcely percipient, almost inorganic, there was the record of a pulsing life which had learnt too well, for its years, of the dust and ashes of things, of the cruelty of lust and the fragility of love. (042-029–042-042)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on the upland plateau, between the valley of her birth and the valley of her love:

> Towards the second evening she reached the irregular chalk table-land or plateau, bosomed with semi-globular tumuli — as if Cybele the Many-breasted were supinely extended there — which stretched between the valley of her birth and the valley of her love. (042-056–042-059)

The arrival at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]]:

> Before her, in a slight depression, were the remains of a village. She had, in fact, reached Flintcomb-Ash, the place of Marian's sojourn. There seemed to be no help for it; hither she was doomed to come. (042-071–042-073)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s moment of bitter self-recognition:

> "Who would think I was Mrs Angel Clare!" she said. (042-079)

The wedding ring hidden:

> "Yes, I do; but not in public. I wear it round my neck on a ribbon. I don't wish people to think who I am by marriage, or that I am married at all; it would be so awkward while I lead my present life." (042-112–042-114)

## Analysis

### The Body as Site of Erasure

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s self-disfigurement is among the most painful scenes in the novel. She cuts off her eyebrows — one of the features [[angel-clare|Angel]] loved — and binds her face. The act is a practical defense against the "aggressive admiration" (042-019) that has dogged her since girlhood, but it is also a self-punishment. By making herself ugly, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] aligns her exterior with her interior sense of worthlessness. The body becomes the text on which her abandonment is written.

### The Hidden Ring

The wedding ring worn on a ribbon around the neck is a complex symbol. It preserves the marriage in secret — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is still [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s wife, even if the world cannot know it. But it also marks the marriage as something that must be concealed, a private truth that has no public reality. The ring, meant to be a public sign of commitment, becomes another secret [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] carries alone.

### The Landscape of Desolation

Hardy's description of the chalk plateau — "bosomed with semi-globular tumuli — as if Cybele the Many-breasted were supinely extended there" (042-057–042-058) — connects [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s journey to ancient, elemental forces. The tumuli (burial mounds) suggest death; Cybele, the mother goddess, suggests life. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] walks between the valley of her birth ([[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor]]) and the valley of her love (Froom), suspended in a middle landscape that offers neither nurture nor love, only survival.

### Economic Descent

The chapter traces a precise economic trajectory: from dairy work (skilled, indoor, relatively respected) to poultry tending, to "the heavy and course pursuits which she liked least — work on arable land" (042-054–042-055). Each step downward reduces [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s autonomy and visibility. At [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], she will be a field hand among field hands, her identity as "Mrs [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]" entirely submerged.
