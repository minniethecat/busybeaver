# Tess's Return to Marlott

## Summary

On a Sunday morning in late October, some weeks after the night in [[the-chase|The Chase]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] walks home from [[trantridge|Trantridge]] to [[marlott|Marlott]] carrying a heavy basket and bundle. She pauses at the edge of the escarpment overlooking [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor Vale]] — the familiar green world of her childhood — now "terribly beautiful" to eyes that have learned "that the serpent hisses where the sweet birds sing" (012-021–012-025). She is pregnant with [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]]'s child.

Alec, having discovered her departure, drives after her in a cart. He confronts her with upbraiding breathlessness: "Why did you slip away by stealth like this?" (012-034–012-036). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], sitting like a puppet, replies in monosyllables. When [[marlott|Marlott]] comes into view, tears trickle down her face. "I wish I had never been born — there or anywhere else!" (012-059) She refuses Alec's money and jewelry and walks on alone into the village, crossing the threshold from the world of [[trantridge|Trantridge]] back into the world of her girlhood — a world to which she can never truly return.

## Context

The return to [[marlott|Marlott]] marks the transition between Phase the First (The Maiden) and Phase the Second (Maiden No More). Hardy's chapter title itself announces the irreversible change: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is no longer a maiden. The walk home is an ordeal of physical endurance and psychological reckoning — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] carries not only her basket but the knowledge of what has been done to her and the child growing within her.

The landscape functions as witness and mirror. The escarpment she climbs is the same one down which Alec had driven her "so wildly on that day in June" (012-019–012-020). The geography of Wessex becomes a map of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s moral journey: each hill and vale marks a stage in her transformation from girl to woman, from innocence to experience.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — Walking alone with her burden, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is both the same girl who left [[marlott|Marlott]] and a different person. Hardy's language emphasizes the transformation: "Verily another girl than the simple one she had been at home was she who, bowed by thought, stood still here" (012-025–012-027). Her silence during Alec's pursuit is not passivity but a form of resistance — she has nothing left to say to him.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec's pursuit of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in his cart is characteristic: he drives "like the deuce" to catch her, then reproaches her for leaving without his permission. His inability to understand why she might wish to leave — "if you didn't wish to come to [[trantridge|Trantridge]] why did you come?" (012-060–012-061) — reveals the depth of his moral blindness.

## Narrative Impact

The return to [[marlott|Marlott]] establishes the pattern of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s subsequent life: she will repeatedly walk long distances alone, carrying burdens both physical and emotional. The journey also introduces the novel's geography of shame: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] cannot "bear to look forward into the Vale" (012-027–012-028) because the landscape of home has become the landscape of judgment.

The encounter with Alec on the road is their last before the novel's final sections. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s refusal of his money — she will not be paid for her violation — is a moment of moral clarity that prefigures her later refusals of his offers of help.

## Analysis

### The Serpent and the Birds

Hardy's image of the serpent hissing "where the sweet birds sing" (012-024) is a Genesis-inflected figure for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s loss of innocence. The knowledge she has gained — of male sexuality, of her own vulnerability, of the gap between appearances and realities — is knowledge that cannot be un-known. The "sweet birds" of her girlhood still sing, but she can now hear the serpent beneath them. This is the novel's version of the Fall: not a moral failing but a forced awakening.

### Walking as Fate

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s long walk home is one of the novel's characteristic images of solitary endurance. Like her later walks to [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], to [[emminster|Emminster]], and ultimately to [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]], this journey is undertaken alone and on foot — the mode of the poor, the female, the powerless. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] never rides toward her destiny; she walks, and the walking itself becomes a form of penance for crimes she did not commit.
