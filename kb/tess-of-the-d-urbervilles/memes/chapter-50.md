[050-001]She plunged into the chilly equinoctial darkness as the clock struck ten, for

[050-002]her fifteen miles’ walk under the steely stars. In lonely districts night

[050-003]is a protection rather than a danger to a noiseless pedestrian, and knowing

[050-004]this, Tess pursued the nearest course along by-lanes that she would almost have

[050-005]feared in the day-time; but marauders were wanting now, and spectral fears were

[050-006]driven out of her mind by thoughts of her mother. Thus she proceeded mile after

[050-007]mile, ascending and descending till she came to Bulbarrow, and about midnight

[050-008]looked from that height into the abyss of chaotic shade which was all that

[050-009]revealed itself of the vale on whose further side she was born. Having already

[050-010]traversed about five miles on the upland, she had now some ten or eleven in the

[050-011]lowland before her journey would be finished. The winding road downwards became

[050-012]just visible to her under the wan starlight as she followed it, and soon she

[050-013]paced a soil so contrasting with that above it that the difference was

[050-014]perceptible to the tread and to the smell. It was the heavy clay land of

[050-015]Blackmoor Vale, and a part of the Vale to which turnpike-roads had never

[050-016]penetrated. Superstitions linger longest on these heavy soils. Having once been

[050-017]forest, at this shadowy time it seemed to assert something of its old

[050-018]character, the far and the near being blended, and every tree and tall hedge

[050-019]making the most of its presence. The harts that had been hunted here, the

[050-020]witches that had been pricked and ducked, the green-spangled fairies that

[050-021]“whickered” at you as you passed;—the place teemed with

[050-022]beliefs in them still, and they formed an impish multitude now.

[050-023]At Nuttlebury she passed the village inn, whose sign creaked in response to the

[050-024]greeting of her footsteps, which not a human soul heard but herself. Under the

[050-025]thatched roofs her mind’s eye beheld relaxed tendons and flaccid muscles,

[050-026]spread out in the darkness beneath coverlets made of little purple patchwork

[050-027]squares, and undergoing a bracing process at the hands of sleep for renewed

[050-028]labour on the morrow, as soon as a hint of pink nebulosity appeared on

[050-029]Hambledon Hill.

[050-030]At three she turned the last corner of the maze of lanes she had threaded, and

[050-031]entered Marlott, passing the field in which as a club-girl she had first seen

[050-032]Angel Clare, when he had not danced with her; the sense of disappointment

[050-033]remained with her yet. In the direction of her mother’s house she saw a

[050-034]light. It came from the bedroom window, and a branch waved in front of it and

[050-035]made it wink at her. As soon as she could discern the outline of the

[050-036]house—newly thatched with her money—it had all its old effect upon

[050-037]Tess’s imagination. Part of her body and life it ever seemed to be; the

[050-038]slope of its dormers, the finish of its gables, the broken courses of brick

[050-039]which topped the chimney, all had something in common with her personal

[050-040]character. A stupefaction had come into these features, to her regard; it meant

[050-041]the illness of her mother.

[050-042]She opened the door so softly as to disturb nobody; the lower room was vacant,

[050-043]but the neighbour who was sitting up with her mother came to the top of the

[050-044]stairs, and whispered that Mrs Durbeyfield was no better, though she was

[050-045]sleeping just then. Tess prepared herself a breakfast, and then took her place

[050-046]as nurse in her mother’s chamber.

[050-047]In the morning, when she contemplated the children, they had all a curiously

[050-048]elongated look; although she had been away little more than a year, their

[050-049]growth was astounding; and the necessity of applying herself heart and soul to

[050-050]their needs took her out of her own cares.

[050-051]Her father’s ill-health was the same indefinite kind, and he sat in his

[050-052]chair as usual. But the day after her arrival he was unusually bright. He had a

[050-053]rational scheme for living, and Tess asked him what it was.

[050-054]“I’m thinking of sending round to all the old antiqueerians in this

[050-055]part of England,” he said, “asking them to subscribe to a fund to

[050-056]maintain me. I’m sure they’d see it as a romantical, artistical,

[050-057]and proper thing to do. They spend lots o’ money in keeping up old ruins,

[050-058]and finding the bones o’ things, and such like; and living remains must

[050-059]be more interesting to ’em still, if they only knowed of me. Would that

[050-060]somebody would go round and tell ’em what there is living among

[050-061]’em, and they thinking nothing of him! If Pa’son Tringham, who

[050-062]discovered me, had lived, he’d ha’ done it, I’m sure.”

[050-064]pressing matters in hand, which seemed little improved by her remittances. When

[050-065]indoor necessities had been eased, she turned her attention to external things.

[050-066]It was now the season for planting and sowing; many gardens and allotments of

[050-067]the villagers had already received their spring tillage; but the garden and the

[050-070]lapse of the improvident. At the earliest moment she obtained what others she

[050-073]allotment-plot which they rented in a field a couple of hundred yards out of

[050-074]the village.

[050-075]She liked doing it after the confinement of the sick chamber, where she was not

[050-076]now required by reason of her mother’s improvement. Violent motion

[050-077]relieved thought. The plot of ground was in a high, dry, open enclosure, where

[050-078]there were forty or fifty such pieces, and where labour was at its briskest

[050-080]o’clock and extended indefinitely into the dusk or moonlight. Just now

[050-084]the last rays of the sun smote flat upon the white pegs that divided the plots.

[050-085]As soon as twilight succeeded to sunset the flare of the couch-grass and

[050-091]wall by day and a light by night, could be understood.

[050-103]the gloom, except when at moments they caught a flash from the flames.

[050-104]Westward, the wiry boughs of the bare thorn hedge which formed the boundary of

[050-106]hung like a full-blown jonquil, so bright as almost to throw a shade. A few

[050-107]small nondescript stars were appearing elsewhere. In the distance a dog barked,

[050-114]the warmth of summer as a lover, came as a tranquillizer on this March day.

[050-145]Beyond a row of myrtles....

[050-147]My conduct, I can bring thee thither soon.’
