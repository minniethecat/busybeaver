[030-001]In the diminishing daylight they went along the level roadway through the

[030-002]meads, which stretched away into gray miles, and were backed in the extreme

[030-003]edge of distance by the swarthy and abrupt slopes of Egdon Heath. On its summit

[030-004]stood clumps and stretches of fir-trees, whose notched tips appeared like

[030-005]battlemented towers crowning black-fronted castles of enchantment.

[030-006]They were so absorbed in the sense of being close to each other that they did

[030-007]not begin talking for a long while, the silence being broken only by the

[030-008]clucking of the milk in the tall cans behind them. The lane they followed was

[030-009]so solitary that the hazel nuts had remained on the boughs till they slipped

[030-010]from their shells, and the blackberries hung in heavy clusters. Every now and

[030-011]then Angel would fling the lash of his whip round one of these, pluck it off,

[030-012]and give it to his companion.

[030-013]The dull sky soon began to tell its meaning by sending down herald-drops of

[030-014]rain, and the stagnant air of the day changed into a fitful breeze which played

[030-015]about their faces. The quick-silvery glaze on the rivers and pools vanished;

[030-016]from broad mirrors of light they changed to lustreless sheets of lead, with a

[030-017]surface like a rasp. But that spectacle did not affect her preoccupation. Her

[030-018]countenance, a natural carnation slightly embrowned by the season, had deepened

[030-019]its tinge with the beating of the rain-drops; and her hair, which the pressure

[030-020]of the cows’ flanks had, as usual, caused to tumble down from its

[030-021]fastenings and stray beyond the curtain of her calico bonnet, was made clammy

[030-022]by the moisture till it hardly was better than seaweed.

[030-023]“I ought not to have come, I suppose,” she murmured, looking at the

[030-024]sky.

[030-025]“I am sorry for the rain,” said he. “But how glad I am to

[030-026]have you here!”

[030-027]Remote Egdon disappeared by degree behind the liquid gauze. The evening grew

[030-028]darker, and the roads being crossed by gates, it was not safe to drive faster

[030-029]than at a walking pace. The air was rather chill.

[030-030]“I am so afraid you will get cold, with nothing upon your arms and

[030-031]shoulders,” he said. “Creep close to me, and perhaps the drizzle

[030-032]won’t hurt you much. I should be sorrier still if I did not think that

[030-033]the rain might be helping me.”

[030-034]She imperceptibly crept closer, and he wrapped round them both a large piece of

[030-035]sail-cloth, which was sometimes used to keep the sun off the milk-cans. Tess

[030-036]held it from slipping off him as well as herself, Clare’s hands being

[030-037]occupied.

[030-038]“Now we are all right again. Ah—no we are not! It runs down into my

[030-039]neck a little, and it must still more into yours. That’s better. Your

[030-040]arms are like wet marble, Tess. Wipe them in the cloth. Now, if you stay quiet,

[030-041]you will not get another drop. Well, dear—about that question of

[030-042]mine—that long-standing question?”

[030-043]The only reply that he could hear for a little while was the smack of the

[030-044]horse’s hoofs on the moistening road, and the cluck of the milk in the

[030-045]cans behind them.

[030-046]“Do you remember what you said?”

[030-047]“I do,” she replied.

[030-048]“Before we get home, mind.”

[030-049]“I’ll try.”

[030-050]He said no more then. As they drove on, the fragment of an old manor house of

[030-051]Caroline date rose against the sky, and was in due course passed and left

[030-052]behind.

[030-053]“That,” he observed, to entertain her, “is an interesting old

[030-054]place—one of the several seats which belonged to an ancient Norman family

[030-055]formerly of great influence in this county, the d’Urbervilles. I never

[030-056]pass one of their residences without thinking of them. There is something very

[030-057]sad in the extinction of a family of renown, even if it was fierce,

[030-058]domineering, feudal renown.”

[030-059]“Yes,” said Tess.

[030-060]They crept along towards a point in the expanse of shade just at hand at which

[030-061]a feeble light was beginning to assert its presence, a spot where, by day, a

[030-062]fitful white streak of steam at intervals upon the dark green background

[030-063]denoted intermittent moments of contact between their secluded world and modern

[030-064]life. Modern life stretched out its steam feeler to this point three or four

[030-065]times a day, touched the native existences, and quickly withdrew its feeler

[030-066]again, as if what it touched had been uncongenial.

[030-067]They reached the feeble light, which came from the smoky lamp of a little

[030-068]railway station; a poor enough terrestrial star, yet in one sense of more

[030-069]importance to Talbothays Dairy and mankind than the celestial ones to which it

[030-070]stood in such humiliating contrast. The cans of new milk were unladen in the

[030-071]rain, Tess getting a little shelter from a neighbouring holly tree.

[030-072]Then there was the hissing of a train, which drew up almost silently upon the

[030-073]wet rails, and the milk was rapidly swung can by can into the truck. The light

[030-074]of the engine flashed for a second upon Tess Durbeyfield’s figure,

[030-075]motionless under the great holly tree. No object could have looked more foreign

[030-076]to the gleaming cranks and wheels than this unsophisticated girl, with the

[030-077]round bare arms, the rainy face and hair, the suspended attitude of a friendly

[030-078]leopard at pause, the print gown of no date or fashion, and the cotton bonnet

[030-079]drooping on her brow.

[030-080]She mounted again beside her lover, with a mute obedience characteristic of

[030-081]impassioned natures at times, and when they had wrapped themselves up over head

[030-082]and ears in the sailcloth again, they plunged back into the now thick night.

[030-083]Tess was so receptive that the few minutes of contact with the whirl of

[030-084]material progress lingered in her thought.

[030-085]“Londoners will drink it at their breakfasts to-morrow, won’t

[030-086]they?” she asked. “Strange people that we have never seen.”

[030-087]“Yes—I suppose they will. Though not as we send it. When its

[030-088]strength has been lowered, so that it may not get up into their heads.”

[030-089]“Noble men and noble women, ambassadors and centurions, ladies and

[030-090]tradeswomen, and babies who have never seen a cow.”

[030-091]“Well, yes; perhaps; particularly centurions.”

[030-092]“Who don’t know anything of us, and where it comes from; or think

[030-093]how we two drove miles across the moor to-night in the rain that it might reach

[030-094]’em in time?”

[030-095]“We did not drive entirely on account of these precious Londoners; we

[030-096]drove a little on our own—on account of that anxious matter which you

[030-097]will, I am sure, set at rest, dear Tess. Now, permit me to put it in this way.

[030-098]You belong to me already, you know; your heart, I mean. Does it not?”

[030-099]“You know as well as I. O yes—yes!”

[030-100]“Then, if your heart does, why not your hand?”

[030-101]“My only reason was on account of you—on account of a question. I

[030-102]have something to tell you—”

[030-103]“But suppose it to be entirely for my happiness, and my worldly

[030-104]convenience also?”

[030-105]“O yes; if it is for your happiness and worldly convenience. But my life

[030-106]before I came here—I want—”

[030-107]“Well, it is for my convenience as well as my happiness. If I have a very

[030-108]large farm, either English or colonial, you will be invaluable as a wife to me;

[030-109]better than a woman out of the largest mansion in the country. So

[030-110]please—please, dear Tessy, disabuse your mind of the feeling that you

[030-111]will stand in my way.”

[030-112]“But my history. I want you to know it—you must let me tell

[030-113]you—you will not like me so well!”

[030-114]“Tell it if you wish to, dearest. This precious history then. Yes, I was

[030-115]born at so and so, Anno Domini—”

[030-116]“I was born at Marlott,” she said, catching at his words as a help,

[030-117]lightly as they were spoken. “And I grew up there. And I was in the Sixth

[030-118]Standard when I left school, and they said I had great aptness, and should make

[030-119]a good teacher, so it was settled that I should be one. But there was trouble

[030-120]in my family; father was not very industrious, and he drank a little.”

[030-121]“Yes, yes. Poor child! Nothing new.” He pressed her more closely to

[030-122]his side.

[030-123]“And then—there is something very unusual about it—about me.

[030-124]I—I was—”

[030-125]Tess’s breath quickened.

[030-126]“Yes, dearest. Never mind.”

[030-127]“I—I—am not a Durbeyfield, but a d’Urberville—a

[030-128]descendant of the same family as those that owned the old house we passed.

[030-129]And—we are all gone to nothing!”

[030-130]“A d’Urberville!—Indeed! And is that all the trouble, dear

[030-131]Tess?”

[030-132]“Yes,” she answered faintly.

[030-133]“Well—why should I love you less after knowing this?”

[030-134]“I was told by the dairyman that you hated old families.”

[030-135]He laughed.

[030-136]“Well, it is true, in one sense. I do hate the aristocratic principle of

[030-137]blood before everything, and do think that as reasoners the only pedigrees we

[030-138]ought to respect are those spiritual ones of the wise and virtuous, without

[030-139]regard to corporal paternity. But I am extremely interested in this

[030-140]news—you can have no idea how interested I am! Are you not interested

[030-141]yourself in being one of that well-known line?”

[030-142]“No. I have thought it sad—especially since coming here, and

[030-143]knowing that many of the hills and fields I see once belonged to my

[030-144]father’s people. But other hills and fields belonged to Retty’s

[030-145]people, and perhaps others to Marian’s, so that I don’t value it

[030-146]particularly.”

[030-147]“Yes—it is surprising how many of the present tillers of the soil

[030-148]were once owners of it, and I sometimes wonder that a certain school of

[030-149]politicians don’t make capital of the circumstance; but they don’t

[030-150]seem to know it... I wonder that I did not see the resemblance of your name

[030-151]to d’Urberville, and trace the manifest corruption. And this was the

[030-152]carking secret!”

[030-153]She had not told. At the last moment her courage had failed her; she feared his

[030-154]blame for not telling him sooner; and her instinct of self-preservation was

[030-155]stronger than her candour.

[030-156]“Of course,” continued the unwitting Clare, “I should have

[030-157]been glad to know you to be descended exclusively from the long-suffering,

[030-158]dumb, unrecorded rank and file of the English nation, and not from the

[030-159]self-seeking few who made themselves powerful at the expense of the rest. But I

[030-160]am corrupted away from that by my affection for you, Tess (he laughed as he

[030-161]spoke), and made selfish likewise. For your own sake I rejoice in your descent.

[030-162]Society is hopelessly snobbish, and this fact of your extraction may make an

[030-163]appreciable difference to its acceptance of you as my wife, after I have made

[030-164]you the well-read woman that I mean to make you. My mother too, poor soul, will

[030-165]think so much better of you on account of it. Tess, you must spell your name

[030-166]correctly—d’Urberville—from this very day.”

[030-167]“I like the other way rather best.”

[030-168]“But you *must* , dearest! Good heavens, why dozens of mushroom

[030-169]millionaires would jump at such a possession! By the bye, there’s one of

[030-170]that kidney who has taken the name—where have I heard of him?—Up in

[030-171]the neighbourhood of The Chase, I think. Why, he is the very man who had that

[030-172]rumpus with my father I told you of. What an odd coincidence!”

[030-173]“Angel, I think I would rather not take the name! It is unlucky,

[030-174]perhaps!”

[030-175]She was agitated.

[030-176]“Now then, Mistress Teresa d’Urberville, I have you. Take my name,

[030-177]and so you will escape yours! The secret is out, so why should you any longer

[030-178]refuse me?”

[030-179]“If it is *sure* to make you happy to have me as your wife, and you

[030-180]feel that you do wish to marry me, *very, very* much—”

[030-181]“I do, dearest, of course!”

[030-182]“I mean, that it is only your wanting me very much, and being hardly able

[030-183]to keep alive without me, whatever my offences, that would make me feel I ought

[030-184]to say I will.”

[030-185]“You will—you do say it, I know! You will be mine for ever and

[030-186]ever.”

[030-187]He clasped her close and kissed her.

[030-188]“Yes!”

[030-189]She had no sooner said it than she burst into a dry hard sobbing, so violent

[030-190]that it seemed to rend her. Tess was not a hysterical girl by any means, and he

[030-191]was surprised.

[030-192]“Why do you cry, dearest?”

[030-193]“I can’t tell—quite!—I am so glad to think—of

[030-194]being yours, and making you happy!”

[030-195]“But this does not seem very much like gladness, my Tessy!”

[030-196]“I mean—I cry because I have broken down in my vow! I said I would

[030-197]die unmarried!”

[030-198]“But, if you love me you would like me to be your husband?”

[030-199]“Yes, yes, yes! But O, I sometimes wish I had never been born!”

[030-200]“Now, my dear Tess, if I did not know that you are very much excited, and

[030-201]very inexperienced, I should say that remark was not very complimentary. How

[030-202]came you to wish that if you care for me? Do you care for me? I wish you would

[030-203]prove it in some way.”

[030-204]“How can I prove it more than I have done?” she cried, in a

[030-205]distraction of tenderness. “Will this prove it more?”

[030-206]She clasped his neck, and for the first time Clare learnt what an impassioned

[030-207]woman’s kisses were like upon the lips of one whom she loved with all her

[030-208]heart and soul, as Tess loved him.

[030-209]“There—now do you believe?” she asked, flushed, and wiping

[030-210]her eyes.

[030-211]“Yes. I never really doubted—never, never!”

[030-212]So they drove on through the gloom, forming one bundle inside the sail-cloth,

[030-213]the horse going as he would, and the rain driving against them. She had

[030-214]consented. She might as well have agreed at first. The “appetite for

[030-215]joy” which pervades all creation, that tremendous force which sways

[030-216]humanity to its purpose, as the tide sways the helpless weed, was not to be

[030-217]controlled by vague lucubrations over the social rubric.

[030-218]“I must write to my mother,” she said. “You don’t mind

[030-219]my doing that?”

[030-220]“Of course not, dear child. You are a child to me, Tess, not to know how

[030-221]very proper it is to write to your mother at such a time, and how wrong it

[030-222]would be in me to object. Where does she live?”

[030-223]“At the same place—Marlott. On the further side of Blackmoor

[030-224]Vale.”

[030-225]“Ah, then I *have* seen you before this summer—”

[030-226]“Yes; at that dance on the green; but you would not dance with me. O, I

[030-227]hope that is of no ill-omen for us now!”
