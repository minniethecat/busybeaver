[003-001]As for Tess Durbeyfield, she did not so easily dislodge the incident from her

[003-002]consideration. She had no spirit to dance again for a long time, though she

[003-003]might have had plenty of partners; but ah! they did not speak so nicely as the

[003-004]strange young man had done. It was not till the rays of the sun had absorbed

[003-005]the young stranger’s retreating figure on the hill that she shook off her

[003-006]temporary sadness and answered her would-be partner in the affirmative.

[003-007]She remained with her comrades till dusk, and participated with a certain zest

[003-008]in the dancing; though, being heart-whole as yet, she enjoyed treading a

[003-009]measure purely for its own sake; little divining when she saw “the soft

[003-010]torments, the bitter sweets, the pleasing pains, and the agreeable

[003-011]distresses” of those girls who had been wooed and won, what she herself

[003-012]was capable of in that kind. The struggles and wrangles of the lads for her

[003-013]hand in a jig were an amusement to her—no more; and when they became

[003-014]fierce she rebuked them.

[003-015]She might have stayed even later, but the incident of her father’s odd

[003-016]appearance and manner returned upon the girl’s mind to make her anxious,

[003-017]and wondering what had become of him she dropped away from the dancers and bent

[003-018]her steps towards the end of the village at which the parental cottage lay.

[003-019]While yet many score yards off, other rhythmic sounds than those she had

[003-020]quitted became audible to her; sounds that she knew well—so well. They

[003-021]were a regular series of thumpings from the interior of the house, occasioned

[003-022]by the violent rocking of a cradle upon a stone floor, to which movement a

[003-023]feminine voice kept time by singing, in a vigorous gallopade, the favourite

[003-024]ditty of “The Spotted Cow”—

[003-025]I saw her lie do′-own in yon′-der green gro′-ove;

[003-026]Come, love!′ and I'll tell′ you where!′

[003-027]The cradle-rocking and the song would cease simultaneously for a moment, and an

[003-028]exclamation at highest vocal pitch would take the place of the melody.

[003-029]“God bless thy diment eyes! And thy waxen cheeks! And thy cherry mouth!

[003-030]And thy Cubit’s thighs! And every bit o’ thy blessed body!”

[003-031]After this invocation the rocking and the singing would recommence, and the

[003-032]“Spotted Cow” proceed as before. So matters stood when Tess opened

[003-033]the door and paused upon the mat within it, surveying the scene.

[003-034]The interior, in spite of the melody, struck upon the girl’s senses with

[003-035]an unspeakable dreariness. From the holiday gaieties of the field—the

[003-036]white gowns, the nosegays, the willow-wands, the whirling movements on the

[003-037]green, the flash of gentle sentiment towards the stranger—to the yellow

[003-038]melancholy of this one-candled spectacle, what a step! Besides the jar of

[003-039]contrast there came to her a chill self-reproach that she had not returned

[003-040]sooner, to help her mother in these domesticities, instead of indulging herself

[003-041]out-of-doors.

[003-042]There stood her mother amid the group of children, as Tess had left her,

[003-043]hanging over the Monday washing-tub, which had now, as always, lingered on to

[003-044]the end of the week. Out of that tub had come the day before—Tess felt it

[003-045]with a dreadful sting of remorse—the very white frock upon her back which

[003-046]she had so carelessly greened about the skirt on the damping grass—which

[003-047]had been wrung up and ironed by her mother’s own hands.

[003-048]As usual, Mrs Durbeyfield was balanced on one foot beside the tub, the other

[003-049]being engaged in the aforesaid business of rocking her youngest child. The

[003-050]cradle-rockers had done hard duty for so many years, under the weight of so

[003-051]many children, on that flagstone floor, that they were worn nearly flat, in

[003-052]consequence of which a huge jerk accompanied each swing of the cot, flinging

[003-053]the baby from side to side like a weaver’s shuttle, as Mrs Durbeyfield,

[003-054]excited by her song, trod the rocker with all the spring that was left in her

[003-055]after a long day’s seething in the suds.

[003-056]Nick-knock, nick-knock, went the cradle; the candle-flame stretched itself

[003-057]tall, and began jigging up and down; the water dribbled from the matron’s

[003-058]elbows, and the song galloped on to the end of the verse, Mrs Durbeyfield

[003-059]regarding her daughter the while. Even now, when burdened with a young family,

[003-060]Joan Durbeyfield was a passionate lover of tune. No ditty floated into

[003-061]Blackmoor Vale from the outer world but Tess’s mother caught up its

[003-062]notation in a week.

[003-063]There still faintly beamed from the woman’s features something of the

[003-064]freshness, and even the prettiness, of her youth; rendering it probable that

[003-065]the personal charms which Tess could boast of were in main part her

[003-066]mother’s gift, and therefore unknightly, unhistorical.

[003-067]“I’ll rock the cradle for ’ee, mother,” said the

[003-068]daughter gently. “Or I’ll take off my best frock and help you wring

[003-069]up? I thought you had finished long ago.”

[003-070]Her mother bore Tess no ill-will for leaving the housework to her single-handed

[003-071]efforts for so long; indeed, Joan seldom upbraided her thereon at any time,

[003-072]feeling but slightly the lack of Tess’s assistance whilst her instinctive

[003-073]plan for relieving herself of her labours lay in postponing them. To-night,

[003-074]however, she was even in a blither mood than usual. There was a dreaminess, a

[003-075]pre-occupation, an exaltation, in the maternal look which the girl could not

[003-076]understand.

[003-077]“Well, I’m glad you’ve come,” her mother said, as soon

[003-078]as the last note had passed out of her. “I want to go and fetch your

[003-079]father; but what’s more’n that, I want to tell ’ee what have

[003-080]happened. Y’ll be fess enough, my poppet, when th’st know!”

[003-081](Mrs Durbeyfield habitually spoke the dialect; her daughter, who had passed the

[003-082]Sixth Standard in the National School under a London-trained mistress, spoke

[003-083]two languages: the dialect at home, more or less; ordinary English abroad and

[003-084]to persons of quality.)

[003-085]“Since I’ve been away?” Tess asked.

[003-086]“Ay!”

[003-087]“Had it anything to do with father’s making such a mommet of

[003-088]himself in thik carriage this afternoon? Why did ’er? I felt inclined to

[003-089]sink into the ground with shame!”

[003-090]“That wer all a part of the larry! We’ve been found to be the

[003-091]greatest gentlefolk in the whole county—reaching all back long before

[003-092]Oliver Grumble’s time—to the days of the Pagan Turks—with

[003-093]monuments, and vaults, and crests, and ’scutcheons, and the Lord knows

[003-094]what all. In Saint Charles’s days we was made Knights o’ the Royal

[003-095]Oak, our real name being d’Urberville!... Don’t that make your

[003-096]bosom plim? ’Twas on this account that your father rode home in the vlee;

[003-097]not because he’d been drinking, as people supposed.”

[003-098]“I’m glad of that. Will it do us any good, mother?”

[003-099]“O yes! ’Tis thoughted that great things may come o’t. No

[003-100]doubt a mampus of volk of our own rank will be down here in their carriages as

[003-101]soon as ’tis known. Your father learnt it on his way hwome from Shaston,

[003-102]and he has been telling me the whole pedigree of the matter.”

[003-103]“Where is father now?” asked Tess suddenly.

[003-104]Her mother gave irrelevant information by way of answer: “He called to

[003-105]see the doctor to-day in Shaston. It is not consumption at all, it seems. It is

[003-106]fat round his heart, ’a says. There, it is like this.” Joan

[003-107]Durbeyfield, as she spoke, curved a sodden thumb and forefinger to the shape of

[003-108]the letter C, and used the other forefinger as a pointer. “‘At the

[003-109]present moment,’ he says to your father, ‘your heart is enclosed

[003-110]all round there, and all round there; this space is still open,’ ’a

[003-111]says. ‘As soon as it do meet, so,’”—Mrs Durbeyfield

[003-112]closed her fingers into a circle complete—“‘off you will go

[003-113]like a shadder, Mr Durbeyfield,’ ’a says. ‘You mid last ten

[003-114]years; you mid go off in ten months, or ten days.’”

[003-115]Tess looked alarmed. Her father possibly to go behind the eternal cloud so

[003-116]soon, notwithstanding this sudden greatness!

[003-117]“But where *is* father?” she asked again.

[003-118]Her mother put on a deprecating look. “Now don’t you be bursting

[003-119]out angry! The poor man—he felt so rafted after his uplifting by the

[003-120]pa’son’s news—that he went up to Rolliver’s half an

[003-121]hour ago. He do want to get up his strength for his journey to-morrow with that

[003-122]load of beehives, which must be delivered, family or no. He’ll have to

[003-123]start shortly after twelve to-night, as the distance is so long.”

[003-124]“Get up his strength!” said Tess impetuously, the tears welling to

[003-125]her eyes. “O my God! Go to a public-house to get up his strength! And you

[003-126]as well agreed as he, mother!”

[003-127]Her rebuke and her mood seemed to fill the whole room, and to impart a cowed

[003-128]look to the furniture, and candle, and children playing about, and to her

[003-129]mother’s face.

[003-130]“No,” said the latter touchily, “I be not agreed. I have been

[003-131]waiting for ’ee to bide and keep house while I go fetch him.”

[003-132]“I’ll go.”

[003-133]“O no, Tess. You see, it would be no use.”

[003-134]Tess did not expostulate. She knew what her mother’s objection meant. Mrs

[003-135]Durbeyfield’s jacket and bonnet were already hanging slily upon a chair

[003-136]by her side, in readiness for this contemplated jaunt, the reason for which the

[003-137]matron deplored more than its necessity.

[003-138]“And take the *Compleat Fortune-Teller* to the outhouse,” Joan

[003-139]continued, rapidly wiping her hands, and donning the garments.

[003-140]The *Compleat Fortune-Teller* was an old thick volume, which lay on a

[003-141]table at her elbow, so worn by pocketing that the margins had reached the edge

[003-142]of the type. Tess took it up, and her mother started.

[003-143]This going to hunt up her shiftless husband at the inn was one of Mrs

[003-144]Durbeyfield’s still extant enjoyments in the muck and muddle of rearing

[003-145]children. To discover him at Rolliver’s, to sit there for an hour or two

[003-146]by his side and dismiss all thought and care of the children during the

[003-147]interval, made her happy. A sort of halo, an occidental glow, came over life

[003-148]then. Troubles and other realities took on themselves a metaphysical

[003-149]impalpability, sinking to mere mental phenomena for serene contemplation, and

[003-150]no longer stood as pressing concretions which chafed body and soul. The

[003-151]youngsters, not immediately within sight, seemed rather bright and desirable

[003-152]appurtenances than otherwise; the incidents of daily life were not without

[003-153]humorousness and jollity in their aspect there. She felt a little as she had

[003-154]used to feel when she sat by her now wedded husband in the same spot during his

[003-155]wooing, shutting her eyes to his defects of character, and regarding him only

[003-156]in his ideal presentation as lover.

[003-157]Tess, being left alone with the younger children, went first to the outhouse

[003-158]with the fortune-telling book, and stuffed it into the thatch. A curious

[003-159]fetishistic fear of this grimy volume on the part of her mother prevented her

[003-160]ever allowing it to stay in the house all night, and hither it was brought back

[003-161]whenever it had been consulted. Between the mother, with her fast-perishing

[003-162]lumber of superstitions, folk-lore, dialect, and orally transmitted ballads,

[003-163]and the daughter, with her trained National teachings and Standard knowledge

[003-164]under an infinitely Revised Code, there was a gap of two hundred years as

[003-165]ordinarily understood. When they were together the Jacobean and the Victorian

[003-166]ages were juxtaposed.

[003-167]Returning along the garden path Tess mused on what the mother could have wished

[003-168]to ascertain from the book on this particular day. She guessed the recent

[003-169]ancestral discovery to bear upon it, but did not divine that it solely

[003-170]concerned herself. Dismissing this, however, she busied herself with sprinkling

[003-171]the linen dried during the day-time, in company with her nine-year-old brother

[003-172]Abraham, and her sister Eliza-Louisa of twelve and a half, called

[003-173]“’Liza-Lu,” the youngest ones being put to bed. There was an

[003-174]interval of four years and more between Tess and the next of the family, the

[003-175]two who had filled the gap having died in their infancy, and this lent her a

[003-176]deputy-maternal attitude when she was alone with her juniors. Next in

[003-177]juvenility to Abraham came two more girls, Hope and Modesty; then a boy of

[003-178]three, and then the baby, who had just completed his first year.

[003-179]All these young souls were passengers in the Durbeyfield ship—entirely

[003-180]dependent on the judgement of the two Durbeyfield adults for their pleasures,

[003-181]their necessities, their health, even their existence. If the heads of the

[003-182]Durbeyfield household chose to sail into difficulty, disaster, starvation,

[003-183]disease, degradation, death, thither were these half-dozen little captives

[003-184]under hatches compelled to sail with them—six helpless creatures, who had

[003-185]never been asked if they wished for life on any terms, much less if they wished

[003-186]for it on such hard conditions as were involved in being of the shiftless house

[003-187]of Durbeyfield. Some people would like to know whence the poet whose philosophy

[003-188]is in these days deemed as profound and trustworthy as his song is breezy and

[003-189]pure, gets his authority for speaking of “Nature’s holy

[003-190]plan.”

[003-191]It grew later, and neither father nor mother reappeared. Tess looked out of the

[003-192]door, and took a mental journey through Marlott. The village was shutting its

[003-193]eyes. Candles and lamps were being put out everywhere: she could inwardly

[003-194]behold the extinguisher and the extended hand.

[003-195]Her mother’s fetching simply meant one more to fetch. Tess began to

[003-196]perceive that a man in indifferent health, who proposed to start on a journey

[003-197]before one in the morning, ought not to be at an inn at this late hour

[003-198]celebrating his ancient blood.

[003-199]“Abraham,” she said to her little brother, “do you put on

[003-200]your hat—you bain’t afraid?—and go up to Rolliver’s,

[003-201]and see what has gone wi’ father and mother.”

[003-202]The boy jumped promptly from his seat, and opened the door, and the night

[003-203]swallowed him up. Half an hour passed yet again; neither man, woman, nor child

[003-204]returned. Abraham, like his parents, seemed to have been limed and caught by

[003-205]the ensnaring inn.

[003-206]“I must go myself,” she said.

[003-207]’Liza-Lu then went to bed, and Tess, locking them all in, started on her

[003-208]way up the dark and crooked lane or street not made for hasty progress; a

[003-209]street laid out before inches of land had value, and when one-handed clocks

[003-210]sufficiently subdivided the day.
