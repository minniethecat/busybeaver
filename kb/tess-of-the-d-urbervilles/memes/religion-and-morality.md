# Religion and Morality

## Definition

In *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles*, religion functions not as a source of moral truth but as a social system that enforces sexual double standards and perpetuates injustice. Hardy draws a sharp distinction between institutional Christianity — embodied in clergymen, creeds, and moral codes — and a deeper, pre-Christian ethical order associated with nature, intuition, and the cycles of the earth. The novel's subtitle, "A Pure Woman Faithfully Presented," is itself a theological provocation: Hardy insists on [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s purity against the verdict of Christian morality, relocating moral authority from the church to the natural world.

## Manifestations

### Angel Clare's Intellectual Christianity

[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s religious journey represents the novel's most sustained engagement with Victorian faith. The son of an evangelical clergyman, [[angel-clare|Angel]] rejects his father's dogmatic Christianity in favour of a rational, humanistic morality — or so he believes. But when [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] confesses her past, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s intellectual liberalism collapses into emotional conservatism. His judgment of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] reveals that he has internalized the very Christian-purity ethic he claims to have rejected. "Different societies, different manners," he tells [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] (035-176), invoking cultural relativism to justify a moral absolutism he cannot escape.

### Alec d'Urberville's Conversion

Alec's transformation into a Methodist preacher is Hardy's most satirical treatment of religion. The conversion is presented as real but shallow — a new channel for the same aggressive energy that drove his libertinism. When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] re-enters his life, Alec quickly sheds his preaching for pursuit, becoming "the late Evangelist" (047-130). His religious phase serves only to demonstrate that evangelical conversion, in Hardy's world, is no match for the deeper forces of desire and self-interest.

### The Baptism of Sorrow

The baptism of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s dying infant — which she performs herself after her father refuses to let the parson enter — is the novel's most powerful religious scene. "She had no knowledge of the proper form; she only knew that baptism washed away sin" (014-225). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s instinctive, unauthorized baptism contrasts the institutional church (which bars the door) with a purer, maternal spirituality. The child, named [[sorrow|Sorrow]], is given a Christian burial only after the vicar is moved by [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s anguish — a concession that reveals the gap between dogma and compassion.

### The Stonehenge Scene

The novel's final religious image is pagan, not Christian. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] sleeps on the altar stone at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] as the sun rises and the authorities approach. The scene superimposes pre-Christian sacrificial ritual onto [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s arrest, casting her as a victim of a morality that has forgotten its own origins. "What is the place?" [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] asks. "Do they sacrifice to God here?" (058-030). The question, asked of a Neolithic monument, reframes the entire novel's moral logic.

## Key Passages

**The Subtitle's Challenge**:
The novel's full title — *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles: A Pure Woman Faithfully Presented* — is itself the text's first and most important theological statement. Hardy asserts [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s purity against the moral consensus of his culture, placing the author in the position of moral authority that the church claims.

**The Baptism**:
> "She had no knowledge of the proper form; she only knew what she had read in the Bible, that baptism washed away sin, and she was determined that her child should not die uncared for." (014-225)

**[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s Moral Failure**:
> "O Tess, forgiveness does not apply to the case! You were one person; now you are another." (035-043)

**The Late Evangelist**:
> "The newcomer was, indeed, Alec d'Urberville, the late Evangelist, despite his changed attire." (047-130)

**[[stonehenge|Stonehenge]]**:
> "What is the place?... Do they sacrifice to God here?" (058-030)

## Related Characters

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the novel's moral centre, consistently portrayed as pure despite society's judgment. Her instinctive baptism of [[sorrow|Sorrow]], her forgiveness of [[angel-clare|Angel]], and her final peace at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] all associate her with a spirituality beyond Christian orthodoxy.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]] embodies the failure of intellectual liberalism to overcome cultural conditioning. His inability to forgive [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] exposes the Christian-purity ethic at the core of his apparently secular worldview.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec's trajectory from libertine to preacher and back again is Hardy's satire of evangelical conversion as a costume change rather than a moral transformation.

**[[Parson Tringham]]** — The clergyman whose casual revelation of [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s ancestry sets the plot in motion, representing the institutional church's unwitting complicity in tragedy.

## Analysis

### The Failure of Christian Forgiveness

The novel systematically demonstrates the failure of Christian forgiveness. [[angel-clare|Angel]] cannot forgive [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]; Alec's "conversion" produces no lasting moral change; the church refuses to baptize [[sorrow|Sorrow]]. In each case, the institutional mechanism of grace is revealed as hollow. Hardy's alternative is not a rival theology but a naturalistic ethic rooted in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s instinctive compassion and the cycles of the earth. The novel's moral logic is pagan, not Christian — and it is no accident that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s final scene takes place at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]].

### Victorian Religious Controversy

The novel was published into a culture in crisis over faith. Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859) had destabilized biblical literalism; the Oxford Movement was fighting a rearguard action for Anglican orthodoxy; evangelical revivalism was sweeping the working classes. Hardy engages all of these currents: [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s rationalism, Alec's Methodism, the parson's gentle orthodoxy. The novel's religious geography maps a culture in which faith is everywhere contested and nowhere secure.

### The Double Standard as Theology

Hardy traces the sexual double standard to its theological roots. The Christian equation of female purity with moral worth is the ideological engine of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s destruction. She is condemned not for what she did but for what was done to her — and the church provides the vocabulary of "sin" and "forgiveness" that makes this inversion possible. By insisting on [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s purity, Hardy attacks not just Victorian morality but the theological framework that sustains it.

### Nature as Moral Authority

Against Christian morality, Hardy posits nature as an alternative moral order. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is repeatedly associated with natural imagery — the May-Day dance, the [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] garden, the [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] altar — that suggests a pre-Christian ethical framework in which she is blameless. This nature-worship is not naive: Hardy's nature is often cruel and indifferent. But it is honest in a way that Christian morality, in his view, is not.

## Historical Context

The novel is set in the 1870s, a period of intense religious ferment in England. The Evangelical revival had transformed the Church of England, emphasizing personal conversion and strict moral conduct. The publication of *Essays and Reviews* (1860) had brought German higher criticism to English readers, challenging biblical authority. Darwin's work had made natural selection the dominant intellectual paradigm. Hardy, who had considered a career in the church before turning to literature, was deeply formed by these debates. *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]* is in part a novel of religious disillusionment — a record of a faith that could not survive the nineteenth century's intellectual revolutions.
