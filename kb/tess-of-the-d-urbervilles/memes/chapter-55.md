[055-001]At eleven o’clock that night, having secured a bed at one of the hotels

[055-002]and telegraphed his address to his father immediately on his arrival, he walked

[055-003]out into the streets of Sandbourne. It was too late to call on or inquire for

[055-004]any one, and he reluctantly postponed his purpose till the morning. But he

[055-005]could not retire to rest just yet.

[055-006]This fashionable watering-place, with its eastern and its western stations, its

[055-007]piers, its groves of pines, its promenades, and its covered gardens, was, to

[055-008]Angel Clare, like a fairy place suddenly created by the stroke of a wand, and

[055-009]allowed to get a little dusty. An outlying eastern tract of the enormous Egdon

[055-010]Waste was close at hand, yet on the very verge of that tawny piece of antiquity

[055-011]such a glittering novelty as this pleasure city had chosen to spring up. Within

[055-012]the space of a mile from its outskirts every irregularity of the soil was

[055-013]prehistoric, every channel an undisturbed British trackway; not a sod having

[055-014]been turned there since the days of the Cæsars. Yet the exotic had grown here,

[055-015]suddenly as the prophet’s gourd; and had drawn hither Tess.

[055-016]By the midnight lamps he went up and down the winding way of this new world in

[055-017]an old one, and could discern between the trees and against the stars the lofty

[055-018]roofs, chimneys, gazebos, and towers of the numerous fanciful residences of

[055-019]which the place was composed. It was a city of detached mansions; a

[055-020]Mediterranean lounging-place on the English Channel; and as seen now by night

[055-021]it seemed even more imposing than it was.

[055-022]The sea was near at hand, but not intrusive; it murmured, and he thought it was

[055-023]the pines; the pines murmured in precisely the same tones, and he thought they

[055-024]were the sea.

[055-025]Where could Tess possibly be, a cottage-girl, his young wife, amidst all this

[055-026]wealth and fashion? The more he pondered, the more was he puzzled. Were there

[055-027]any cows to milk here? There certainly were no fields to till. She was most

[055-028]probably engaged to do something in one of these large houses; and he sauntered

[055-029]along, looking at the chamber-windows and their lights going out one by one,

[055-030]and wondered which of them might be hers.

[055-031]Conjecture was useless, and just after twelve o’clock he entered and went

[055-032]to bed. Before putting out his light he re-read Tess’s impassioned

[055-033]letter. Sleep, however, he could not—so near her, yet so far from

[055-034]her—and he continually lifted the window-blind and regarded the backs of

[055-035]the opposite houses, and wondered behind which of the sashes she reposed at

[055-036]that moment.

[055-037]He might almost as well have sat up all night. In the morning he arose at

[055-038]seven, and shortly after went out, taking the direction of the chief

[055-039]post-office. At the door he met an intelligent postman coming out with letters

[055-040]for the morning delivery.

[055-041]“Do you know the address of a Mrs Clare?” asked Angel. The postman

[055-042]shook his head.

[055-043]Then, remembering that she would have been likely to continue the use of her

[055-044]maiden name, Clare said—

[055-045]“Of a Miss Durbeyfield?”

[055-046]“Durbeyfield?”

[055-047]This also was strange to the postman addressed.

[055-048]“There’s visitors coming and going every day, as you know,

[055-049]sir,” he said; “and without the name of the house ’tis

[055-050]impossible to find ’em.”

[055-051]One of his comrades hastening out at that moment, the name was repeated to him.

[055-052]“I know no name of Durbeyfield; but there is the name of

[055-053]d’Urberville at The Herons,” said the second.

[055-054]“That’s it!” cried Clare, pleased to think that she had

[055-055]reverted to the real pronunciation. “What place is The Herons?”

[055-056]“A stylish lodging-house. ’Tis all lodging-houses here, bless

[055-057]’ee.”

[055-058]Clare received directions how to find the house, and hastened thither, arriving

[055-059]with the milkman. The Herons, though an ordinary villa, stood in its own

[055-060]grounds, and was certainly the last place in which one would have expected to

[055-061]find lodgings, so private was its appearance. If poor Tess was a servant here,

[055-062]as he feared, she would go to the back-door to that milkman, and he was

[055-063]inclined to go thither also. However, in his doubts he turned to the front, and

[055-064]rang.

[055-065]The hour being early, the landlady herself opened the door. Clare inquired for

[055-066]Teresa d’Urberville or Durbeyfield.

[055-067]“Mrs d’Urberville?”

[055-068]“Yes.”

[055-069]Tess, then, passed as a married woman, and he felt glad, even though she had

[055-070]not adopted his name.

[055-071]“Will you kindly tell her that a relative is anxious to see her?”

[055-072]“It is rather early. What name shall I give, sir?”

[055-073]“Angel.”

[055-074]“Mr Angel?”

[055-075]“No; Angel. It is my Christian name. She’ll understand.”

[055-076]“I’ll see if she is awake.”

[055-077]He was shown into the front room—the dining-room—and looked out

[055-078]through the spring curtains at the little lawn, and the rhododendrons and other

[055-079]shrubs upon it. Obviously her position was by no means so bad as he had feared,

[055-080]and it crossed his mind that she must somehow have claimed and sold the jewels

[055-081]to attain it. He did not blame her for one moment. Soon his sharpened ear

[055-082]detected footsteps upon the stairs, at which his heart thumped so painfully

[055-083]that he could hardly stand firm. “Dear me! what will she think of me, so

[055-084]altered as I am!” he said to himself; and the door opened.

[055-085]Tess appeared on the threshold—not at all as he had expected to see

[055-086]her—bewilderingly otherwise, indeed. Her great natural beauty was, if not

[055-087]heightened, rendered more obvious by her attire. She was loosely wrapped in a

[055-088]cashmere dressing-gown of gray-white, embroidered in half-mourning tints, and

[055-089]she wore slippers of the same hue. Her neck rose out of a frill of down, and

[055-090]her well-remembered cable of dark-brown hair was partially coiled up in a mass

[055-091]at the back of her head and partly hanging on her shoulder—the evident

[055-092]result of haste.

[055-093]He had held out his arms, but they had fallen again to his side; for she had

[055-094]not come forward, remaining still in the opening of the doorway. Mere yellow

[055-095]skeleton that he was now, he felt the contrast between them, and thought his

[055-096]appearance distasteful to her.

[055-097]“Tess!” he said huskily, “can you forgive me for going away?

[055-098]Can’t you—come to me? How do you get to be—like this?”

[055-099]“It is too late,” said she, her voice sounding hard through the

[055-100]room, her eyes shining unnaturally.

[055-101]“I did not think rightly of you—I did not see you as you

[055-102]were!” he continued to plead. “I have learnt to since, dearest

[055-103]Tessy mine!”

[055-104]“Too late, too late!” she said, waving her hand in the impatience

[055-105]of a person whose tortures cause every instant to seem an hour.

[055-106]“Don’t come close to me, Angel! No—you must not. Keep

[055-107]away.”

[055-108]“But don’t you love me, my dear wife, because I have been so pulled

[055-109]down by illness? You are not so fickle—I am come on purpose for

[055-110]you—my mother and father will welcome you now!”

[055-111]“Yes—O, yes, yes! But I say, I say it is too late.”

[055-112]She seemed to feel like a fugitive in a dream, who tries to move away, but

[055-113]cannot. “Don’t you know all—don’t you know it? Yet how

[055-114]do you come here if you do not know?”

[055-115]“I inquired here and there, and I found the way.”

[055-116]“I waited and waited for you,” she went on, her tones suddenly

[055-117]resuming their old fluty pathos. “But you did not come! And I wrote to

[055-118]you, and you did not come! He kept on saying you would never come any more, and

[055-119]that I was a foolish woman. He was very kind to me, and to mother, and to all

[055-120]of us after father’s death. He—”

[055-121]“I don’t understand.”

[055-122]“He has won me back to him.”

[055-123]Clare looked at her keenly, then, gathering her meaning, flagged like one

[055-124]plague-stricken, and his glance sank; it fell on her hands, which, once rosy,

[055-125]were now white and more delicate.

[055-126]She continued—

[055-127]“He is upstairs. I hate him now, because he told me a lie—that you

[055-128]would not come again; and you *have* come! These clothes are what

[055-129]he’s put upon me: I didn’t care what he did wi’ me!

[055-130]But—will you go away, Angel, please, and never come any more?”

[055-131]They stood fixed, their baffled hearts looking out of their eyes with a

[055-132]joylessness pitiful to see. Both seemed to implore something to shelter them

[055-133]from reality.

[055-134]“Ah—it is my fault!” said Clare.

[055-135]But he could not get on. Speech was as inexpressive as silence. But he had a

[055-136]vague consciousness of one thing, though it was not clear to him till later;

[055-137]that his original Tess had spiritually ceased to recognize the body before him

[055-138]as hers—allowing it to drift, like a corpse upon the current, in a

[055-139]direction dissociated from its living will.

[055-140]A few instants passed, and he found that Tess was gone. His face grew colder

[055-141]and more shrunken as he stood concentrated on the moment, and a minute or two

[055-142]after, he found himself in the street, walking along he did not know whither.
