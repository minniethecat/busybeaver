# Tess's Journey to Trantridge

## Summary

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]]'s journey from [[marlott|Marlott]] to [[trantridge|Trantridge]] is the novel's first major transitional event. Following the death of Prince, the family horse, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] overcomes her reluctance and agrees to seek work at the d'Urberville poultry farm. On the appointed morning, her mother [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]] dresses her in her finest clothes, and she bids farewell to her family at the foot of the hill. At the summit, [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] arrives not in the humble spring-cart expected but in a polished gig, and personally escorts [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] away — a moment of misgiving that foreshadows the tragedy to come.

## Context

The journey is directly precipitated by the death of Prince (004-012), which has deprived the Durbeyfield family of their sole means of livelihood. [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s drinking and delusions of aristocratic ancestry have already strained the household, and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], consumed by guilt over the horse's death, feels responsible for restoring the family's fortunes.

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]] has been the driving force behind the plan. After learning of the "rich lady" at [[trantridge|Trantridge]] who shares their surname (004-065), she sent [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to make contact. [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]]'s subsequent visit to [[marlott|Marlott]] and his mother's letter offering [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] employment at the poultry farm (006-055) seal the arrangement. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s misgivings about Alec are overcome by her sense of duty — "I killed the old horse, and I suppose I ought to do something to get ye a new one" (006-123).

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — The protagonist, making her first journey away from home. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is dressed by her mother in a white frock with a pink ribbon, intended to enhance her attractiveness to the d'Urbervilles. Her reluctance is evident throughout — she would have preferred the humble cart to Alec's gig, and her instinctive misgiving proves prophetic.

**[[Joan Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mother, who orchestrates the departure with a mixture of maternal ambition and simple-minded vanity. She washes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s hair, dresses her elaborately, and surveys her "like a painter from his easel" (007-030). Her later admission — "perhaps it would ha' been better if [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] had not gone" (007-133) — is one of the novel's many retrospective ironies.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Appears at the hilltop not in the expected workman's cart but in a polished gig, smoking a cigar, wearing dandy attire (007-104–007-107). His interception of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at the moment of arrival is the first overt sign of his predatory intentions.

**[[John Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s father, who offers to sell the family title to Alec for "twenty pound" (007-076), a grotesque display of the Durbeyfield pride that has set the whole scheme in motion.

## Narrative Impact

The journey to [[trantridge|Trantridge]] marks [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s passage from the protected world of [[marlott|Marlott]] into the wider, more dangerous world beyond the [[vale-of-blackmoor|Vale of Blackmoor]]. It is the narrative hinge between the domestic tragedy of Prince's death and the sexual tragedy at [[the-slopes|The Slopes]]. Hardy structures the departure as a ritual: the washing, the dressing, the procession up the hill, the appearance of Alec's gig at the crest — each detail weighted with dramatic irony.

The event also establishes the novel's economic determinism. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] does not go to [[trantridge|Trantridge]] out of ambition or desire but out of economic necessity, guilt, and family pressure. She is an actor in a drama she did not choose, and her "consent" is extracted by circumstances rather than freely given.

## Key Passages

**The Mother's Artifice**:
> "I declare there's a hole in my stocking-heel!" said Tess. "Never mind holes in your stockings — they don't speak!" (007-026–007-029)

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s insistence that appearance outweighs substance encapsulates the Durbeyfield family's tragic flaw.

**The Farewell**:
> Tess's eyes were too full and her voice too choked to utter the sentiments that were in her. She turned quickly, and went out. (007-078–007-079)

**Alec's Arrival**:
> The driver was a young man of three- or four-and-twenty, with a cigar between his teeth; wearing a dandy cap, drab jacket, breeches of the same hue, white neckcloth, stick-up collar, and brown driving-gloves. (007-104–007-107)

**The Departure**:
> She suddenly stepped up; he mounted beside her, and immediately whipped on the horse. In a moment they had passed the slow cart with the box, and disappeared behind the shoulder of the hill. (007-119–007-121)

## Analysis

The journey is dense with Hardy's characteristic symbolism. The rose thorn that pricks [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s chin on the van (006-015) is the "first ill omen" of the day, anticipating all the harm to come. [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s use of the window-pane as a mirror (007-035) reflects the distortion of reality that characterizes the Durbeyfields' aspirations. The children's tears at [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s departure — "I wish poor, poor [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] wasn't gone away to be a lady!" (007-124) — speak a truth that the adults cannot or will not see.

The substitution of Alec's gig for the expected spring-cart is a masterstroke of dramatic structure. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] expects humble work; she receives instead the attentions of a predatory gentleman. The moment at the hilltop, where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] looks back at her family and then steps into Alec's vehicle, is the novel's first point of no return.
