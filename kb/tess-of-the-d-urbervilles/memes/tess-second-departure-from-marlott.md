# Tess's Second Departure from Marlott

## Summary

After a winter of seclusion following the death of her infant [[sorrow|Sorrow]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] resolves to leave [[marlott|Marlott]] and seek a new life. In early May, a letter arrives offering a milkmaid position at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] dairy, many miles to the south. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] departs determined to escape the shadow of her past — "to escape the past and all that appertained thereto was to annihilate it" (015-050–015-051) — and to be "the dairymaid [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], and nothing more" (015-068–015-069). This departure marks the structural midpoint of the novel, a southward movement into a landscape of hope and regeneration that deliberately contrasts with her first journey to [[trantridge|Trantridge]].

## Context

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has spent the winter at her father's house, "plucking fowls, or cramming turkeys and geese, or making clothes for her sisters and brothers out of some finery which d'Urberville had given her" (015-014–015-017). The tragic arc of Phase the First — the seduction at [[The Chase]], the pregnancy, the baby's birth and death — has concluded. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has retreated from the world, her "trouble, never generally known, was nearly forgotten in [[marlott|Marlott]]" (015-042–015-043). Yet she cannot remain: "it became evident to her that she could never be really comfortable again in a place which had seen the collapse of her family's attempt to 'claim kin'" (015-044–015-046).

The psychological transformation is profound. Hardy marks this moment as the end of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s innocence: "Almost at a leap [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] thus changed from simple girl to complex woman. Symbols of reflectiveness passed into her face, and a note of tragedy at times into her voice" (015-035–015-037). Yet the movement is not solely toward despair; she feels "the pulse of hopeful life still warm within her" (015-048–015-049).

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — At this moment [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is neither the innocent girl of the [[May-Day Dance]] nor the tragic figure she will become. She is in transition — "her soul that of a woman whom the turbulent experiences of the last year or two had quite failed to demoralize" (015-039–015-040). She chooses agency: "Apply to him she would not" (015-017), refusing to seek help from [[Alec d'Urberville]]. Her resolution to be "the dairymaid [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], and nothing more" (015-069) is both a survival strategy and an assertion of identity beyond imposed categories.

The [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] position reaches her through a former friend of [[Joan Durbeyfield]]'s — "a person whom she had never seen" (015-060) — emphasizing how [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s escape is enabled by impersonal fortune, not privileged connections.

## Narrative Impact

This departure is the novel's pivotal transitional event. It closes Phase the Second ("Maiden No More") and opens Phase the Third ("The Rally"). The southward trajectory reverses her first departure to [[trantridge|Trantridge]] — she now moves toward a landscape associated with her d'Urberville ancestry and the possibility of renewal.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mental state reflects the tension between intellect and circumstance. She asks: "Was once lost always lost really true of chastity? she would ask herself. She might prove it false if she could veil bygones" (015-052–015-053). This is [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] thinking philosophically — testing received wisdom against experience, refusing to accept that her past defines her future. Hardy reinforces this with organic metaphor: "The recuperative power which pervaded organic nature was surely not denied to maidenhood alone" (015-053–015-054).

## Key Passages

Hardy opens the chapter with an epigraph from Roger Ascham that encapsulates [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s condition:

> "By experience," says Roger Ascham, "we find out a short way by a long wandering." Not seldom that long wandering unfits us for further travel, and of what use is our experience to us then? Tess Durbeyfield's experience was of this incapacitating kind. At last she had learned what to do; but who would now accept her doing? (015-001–015-005)

The spring that stirs [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to action:

> A particularly fine spring came round, and the stir of germination was almost audible in the buds; it moved her, as it moved the wild animals, and made her passionate to go. (015-056–015-058)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s resolution to reinvent herself:

> On one point she was resolved: there should be no more d'Urberville air-castles in the dreams and deeds of her new life. She would be the dairymaid Tess, and nothing more. (015-067–015-069)

The closing passage connects her journey to ancestral memory and the instinct for renewal:

> All the while she wondered if any strange good thing might come of her being in her ancestral land; and some spirit within her rose automatically as the sap in the twigs. It was unexpected youth, surging up anew after its temporary check, and bringing with it hope, and the invincible instinct towards self-delight. (015-080–015-084)

## Analysis

### The Geography of Renewal

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s southward journey to the [[froom-valley|Froom Valley]] is a symbolic descent into fertility. [[Talbothays Dairy]] lies "not remotely from some of the former estates of the d'Urbervilles, near the great family vaults of her granddames and their powerful husbands" (015-075–015-077). The ancestral connection is ironic — she goes to the land of her fallen forefathers seeking humble work — but also regenerative. The proximity to her ancestral roots stirs something dormant: "some spirit within her rose automatically as the sap in the twigs" (015-081–015-082).

### The Power of Secrecy and Reinvention

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s strategy is not confession but erasure. She will "veil bygones" (015-053), not deny them. Unlike the confession that will later destroy her marriage, this departure is premised on the possibility that the past can be rendered powerless by distance and anonymity. "To escape the past and all that appertained thereto was to annihilate it" (015-050–015-051) is [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s credo: geography as absolution.

### Structure and Symmetry

This is the second of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s three major departures from [[marlott|Marlott]] — the first being [[Tess's Journey to Trantridge]] and the third [[Tess's Return Home in Disgrace]]. Each traces a different stage: innocence deceived, hope renewed, and hope extinguished. The structural symmetry underscores Hardy's deterministic vision — the same motion repeated under diminished circumstances.
