[007-001]On the morning appointed for her departure Tess was awake before dawn—at

[007-002]the marginal minute of the dark when the grove is still mute, save for one

[007-003]prophetic bird who sings with a clear-voiced conviction that he at least knows

[007-004]the correct time of day, the rest preserving silence as if equally convinced

[007-005]that he is mistaken. She remained upstairs packing till breakfast-time, and

[007-006]then came down in her ordinary week-day clothes, her Sunday apparel being

[007-007]carefully folded in her box.

[007-008]Her mother expostulated. “You will never set out to see your folks

[007-009]without dressing up more the dand than that?”

[007-010]“But I am going to work!” said Tess.

[007-011]“Well, yes,” said Mrs Durbeyfield; and in a private tone, “at

[007-012]first there mid be a little pretence o’t.... But I think it will be

[007-013]wiser of ’ee to put your best side outward,” she added.

[007-014]“Very well; I suppose you know best,” replied Tess with calm

[007-015]abandonment.

[007-016]And to please her parent the girl put herself quite in Joan’s hands,

[007-017]saying serenely—“Do what you like with me, mother.”

[007-018]Mrs Durbeyfield was only too delighted at this tractability. First she fetched

[007-019]a great basin, and washed Tess’s hair with such thoroughness that when

[007-020]dried and brushed it looked twice as much as at other times. She tied it with a

[007-021]broader pink ribbon than usual. Then she put upon her the white frock that Tess

[007-022]had worn at the club-walking, the airy fulness of which, supplementing her

[007-023]enlarged *coiffure* , imparted to her developing figure an amplitude which

[007-024]belied her age, and might cause her to be estimated as a woman when she was not

[007-025]much more than a child.

[007-026]“I declare there’s a hole in my stocking-heel!” said Tess.

[007-027]“Never mind holes in your stockings—they don’t speak! When I

[007-028]was a maid, so long as I had a pretty bonnet the devil might ha’ found me

[007-029]in heels.”

[007-030]Her mother’s pride in the girl’s appearance led her to step back,

[007-031]like a painter from his easel, and survey her work as a whole.

[007-032]“You must zee yourself!” she cried. “It is much better than

[007-033]you was t’other day.”

[007-034]As the looking-glass was only large enough to reflect a very small portion of

[007-035]Tess’s person at one time, Mrs Durbeyfield hung a black cloak outside the

[007-036]casement, and so made a large reflector of the panes, as it is the wont of

[007-037]bedecking cottagers to do. After this she went downstairs to her husband, who

[007-038]was sitting in the lower room.

[007-039]“I’ll tell ’ee what ’tis, Durbeyfield,” said she

[007-040]exultingly; “he’ll never have the heart not to love her. But

[007-041]whatever you do, don’t zay too much to Tess of his fancy for her, and

[007-042]this chance she has got. She is such an odd maid that it mid zet her against

[007-043]him, or against going there, even now. If all goes well, I shall certainly be

[007-044]for making some return to pa’son at Stagfoot Lane for telling

[007-045]us—dear, good man!”

[007-046]However, as the moment for the girl’s setting out drew nigh, when the

[007-047]first excitement of the dressing had passed off, a slight misgiving found place

[007-048]in Joan Durbeyfield’s mind. It prompted the matron to say that she would

[007-049]walk a little way—as far as to the point where the acclivity from the

[007-050]valley began its first steep ascent to the outer world. At the top Tess was

[007-051]going to be met with the spring-cart sent by the Stoke-d’Urbervilles, and

[007-052]her box had already been wheeled ahead towards this summit by a lad with

[007-053]trucks, to be in readiness.

[007-054]Seeing their mother put on her bonnet, the younger children clamoured to go

[007-055]with her.

[007-056]“I do want to walk a little-ways wi’ Sissy, now she’s going

[007-057]to marry our gentleman-cousin, and wear fine cloze!”

[007-058]“Now,” said Tess, flushing and turning quickly, “I’ll

[007-059]hear no more o’ that! Mother, how could you ever put such stuff into

[007-060]their heads?”

[007-061]“Going to work, my dears, for our rich relation, and help get enough

[007-062]money for a new horse,” said Mrs Durbeyfield pacifically.

[007-063]“Goodbye, father,” said Tess, with a lumpy throat.

[007-064]“Goodbye, my maid,” said Sir John, raising his head from his breast

[007-065]as he suspended his nap, induced by a slight excess this morning in honour of

[007-066]the occasion. “Well, I hope my young friend will like such a comely

[007-067]sample of his own blood. And tell’n, Tess, that being sunk, quite, from

[007-068]our former grandeur, I’ll sell him the title—yes, sell it—and

[007-069]at no onreasonable figure.”

[007-070]“Not for less than a thousand pound!” cried Lady Durbeyfield.

[007-071]“Tell’n—I’ll take a thousand pound. Well, I’ll

[007-072]take less, when I come to think o’t. He’ll adorn it better than a

[007-073]poor lammicken feller like myself can. Tell’n he shall hae it for a

[007-074]hundred. But I won’t stand upon trifles—tell’n he shall hae

[007-075]it for fifty—for twenty pound! Yes, twenty pound—that’s the

[007-076]lowest. Dammy, family honour is family honour, and I won’t take a penny

[007-077]less!”

[007-078]Tess’s eyes were too full and her voice too choked to utter the

[007-079]sentiments that were in her. She turned quickly, and went out.

[007-080]So the girls and their mother all walked together, a child on each side of

[007-081]Tess, holding her hand and looking at her meditatively from time to time, as at

[007-082]one who was about to do great things; her mother just behind with the smallest;

[007-083]the group forming a picture of honest beauty flanked by innocence, and backed

[007-084]by simple-souled vanity. They followed the way till they reached the beginning

[007-085]of the ascent, on the crest of which the vehicle from Trantridge was to receive

[007-086]her, this limit having been fixed to save the horse the labour of the last

[007-087]slope. Far away behind the first hills the cliff-like dwellings of Shaston

[007-088]broke the line of the ridge. Nobody was visible in the elevated road which

[007-089]skirted the ascent save the lad whom they had sent on before them, sitting on

[007-090]the handle of the barrow that contained all Tess’s worldly possessions.

[007-091]“Bide here a bit, and the cart will soon come, no doubt,” said Mrs

[007-092]Durbeyfield. “Yes, I see it yonder!”

[007-093]It had come—appearing suddenly from behind the forehead of the nearest

[007-094]upland, and stopping beside the boy with the barrow. Her mother and the

[007-095]children thereupon decided to go no farther, and bidding them a hasty goodbye,

[007-096]Tess bent her steps up the hill.

[007-097]They saw her white shape draw near to the spring-cart, on which her box was

[007-098]already placed. But before she had quite reached it another vehicle shot out

[007-099]from a clump of trees on the summit, came round the bend of the road there,

[007-100]passed the luggage-cart, and halted beside Tess, who looked up as if in great

[007-101]surprise.

[007-102]Her mother perceived, for the first time, that the second vehicle was not a

[007-103]humble conveyance like the first, but a spick-and-span gig or dog-cart, highly

[007-104]varnished and equipped. The driver was a young man of three- or

[007-105]four-and-twenty, with a cigar between his teeth; wearing a dandy cap, drab

[007-106]jacket, breeches of the same hue, white neckcloth, stick-up collar, and brown

[007-107]driving-gloves—in short, he was the handsome, horsey young buck who had

[007-108]visited Joan a week or two before to get her answer about Tess.

[007-109]Mrs Durbeyfield clapped her hands like a child. Then she looked down, then

[007-110]stared again. Could she be deceived as to the meaning of this?

[007-111]“Is dat the gentleman-kinsman who’ll make Sissy a lady?”

[007-112]asked the youngest child.

[007-113]Meanwhile the muslined form of Tess could be seen standing still, undecided,

[007-114]beside this turn-out, whose owner was talking to her. Her seeming indecision

[007-115]was, in fact, more than indecision: it was misgiving. She would have preferred

[007-116]the humble cart. The young man dismounted, and appeared to urge her to ascend.

[007-117]She turned her face down the hill to her relatives, and regarded the little

[007-118]group. Something seemed to quicken her to a determination; possibly the thought

[007-119]that she had killed Prince. She suddenly stepped up; he mounted beside her, and

[007-120]immediately whipped on the horse. In a moment they had passed the slow cart

[007-121]with the box, and disappeared behind the shoulder of the hill.

[007-122]Directly Tess was out of sight, and the interest of the matter as a drama was

[007-123]at an end, the little ones’ eyes filled with tears. The youngest child

[007-124]said, “I wish poor, poor Tess wasn’t gone away to be a lady!”

[007-125]and, lowering the corners of his lips, burst out crying. The new point of view

[007-126]was infectious, and the next child did likewise, and then the next, till the

[007-127]whole three of them wailed loud.

[007-128]There were tears also in Joan Durbeyfield’s eyes as she turned to go

[007-129]home. But by the time she had got back to the village she was passively

[007-130]trusting to the favour of accident. However, in bed that night she sighed, and

[007-131]her husband asked her what was the matter.

[007-132]“Oh, I don’t know exactly,” she said. “I was thinking

[007-133]that perhaps it would ha’ been better if Tess had not gone.”

[007-134]“Oughtn’t ye to have thought of that before?”

[007-135]“Well, ’tis a chance for the maid—Still, if ’twere the

[007-136]doing again, I wouldn’t let her go till I had found out whether the

[007-137]gentleman is really a good-hearted young man and choice over her as his

[007-138]kinswoman.”

[007-139]“Yes, you ought, perhaps, to ha’ done that,” snored Sir John.

[007-140]Joan Durbeyfield always managed to find consolation somewhere: “Well, as

[007-141]one of the genuine stock, she ought to make her way with ’en, if she

[007-142]plays her trump card aright. And if he don’t marry her afore he will

[007-143]after. For that he’s all afire wi’ love for her any eye can

[007-144]see.”

[007-145]“What’s her trump card? Her d’Urberville blood, you

[007-146]mean?”

[007-147]“No, stupid; her face—as ’twas mine.”
