[016-001]On a thyme-scented, bird-hatching morning in May, between two and three years

[016-002]after the return from Trantridge—silent, reconstructive years for Tess

[016-003]Durbeyfield—she left her home for the second time.

[016-004]Having packed up her luggage so that it could be sent to her later, she started

[016-005]in a hired trap for the little town of Stourcastle, through which it was

[016-006]necessary to pass on her journey, now in a direction almost opposite to that of

[016-007]her first adventuring. On the curve of the nearest hill she looked back

[016-008]regretfully at Marlott and her father’s house, although she had been so

[016-009]anxious to get away.

[016-010]Her kindred dwelling there would probably continue their daily lives as

[016-011]heretofore, with no great diminution of pleasure in their consciousness,

[016-012]although she would be far off, and they deprived of her smile. In a few days

[016-013]the children would engage in their games as merrily as ever, without the sense

[016-014]of any gap left by her departure. This leaving of the younger children she had

[016-015]decided to be for the best; were she to remain they would probably gain less

[016-016]good by her precepts than harm by her example.

[016-017]She went through Stourcastle without pausing and onward to a junction of

[016-018]highways, where she could await a carrier’s van that ran to the

[016-019]south-west; for the railways which engirdled this interior tract of country had

[016-020]never yet struck across it. While waiting, however, there came along a farmer

[016-021]in his spring cart, driving approximately in the direction that she wished to

[016-022]pursue. Though he was a stranger to her she accepted his offer of a seat beside

[016-023]him, ignoring that its motive was a mere tribute to her countenance. He was

[016-024]going to Weatherbury, and by accompanying him thither she could walk the

[016-025]remainder of the distance instead of travelling in the van by way of

[016-026]Casterbridge.

[016-027]Tess did not stop at Weatherbury, after this long drive, further than to make a

[016-028]slight nondescript meal at noon at a cottage to which the farmer recommended

[016-029]her. Thence she started on foot, basket in hand, to reach the wide upland of

[016-030]heath dividing this district from the low-lying meads of a further valley in

[016-031]which the dairy stood that was the aim and end of her day’s pilgrimage.

[016-032]Tess had never before visited this part of the country, and yet she felt akin

[016-033]to the landscape. Not so very far to the left of her she could discern a dark

[016-034]patch in the scenery, which inquiry confirmed her in supposing to be trees

[016-035]marking the environs of Kingsbere—in the church of which parish the bones

[016-036]of her ancestors—her useless ancestors—lay entombed.

[016-037]She had no admiration for them now; she almost hated them for the dance they

[016-038]had led her; not a thing of all that had been theirs did she retain but the old

[016-039]seal and spoon. “Pooh—I have as much of mother as father in

[016-040]me!” she said. “All my prettiness comes from her, and she was only

[016-041]a dairymaid.”

[016-042]The journey over the intervening uplands and lowlands of Egdon, when she

[016-043]reached them, was a more troublesome walk than she had anticipated, the

[016-044]distance being actually but a few miles. It was two hours, owing to sundry

[016-045]wrong turnings, ere she found herself on a summit commanding the

[016-046]long-sought-for vale, the Valley of the Great Dairies, the valley in which milk

[016-047]and butter grew to rankness, and were produced more profusely, if less

[016-048]delicately, than at her home—the verdant plain so well watered by the

[016-049]river Var or Froom.

[016-050]It was intrinsically different from the Vale of Little Dairies, Blackmoor Vale,

[016-051]which, save during her disastrous sojourn at Trantridge, she had exclusively

[016-052]known till now. The world was drawn to a larger pattern here. The enclosures

[016-053]numbered fifty acres instead of ten, the farmsteads were more extended, the

[016-054]groups of cattle formed tribes hereabout; there only families. These myriads of

[016-055]cows stretching under her eyes from the far east to the far west outnumbered

[016-056]any she had ever seen at one glance before. The green lea was speckled as

[016-057]thickly with them as a canvas by Van Alsloot or Sallaert with burghers. The

[016-058]ripe hue of the red and dun kine absorbed the evening sunlight, which the

[016-059]white-coated animals returned to the eye in rays almost dazzling, even at the

[016-060]distant elevation on which she stood.

[016-061]The bird’s-eye perspective before her was not so luxuriantly beautiful,

[016-062]perhaps, as that other one which she knew so well; yet it was more cheering. It

[016-063]lacked the intensely blue atmosphere of the rival vale, and its heavy soils and

[016-064]scents; the new air was clear, bracing, ethereal. The river itself, which

[016-065]nourished the grass and cows of these renowned dairies, flowed not like the

[016-066]streams in Blackmoor. Those were slow, silent, often turbid; flowing over beds

[016-067]of mud into which the incautious wader might sink and vanish unawares. The

[016-068]Froom waters were clear as the pure River of Life shown to the Evangelist,

[016-069]rapid as the shadow of a cloud, with pebbly shallows that prattled to the sky

[016-070]all day long. There the water-flower was the lily; the crow-foot here.

[016-071]Either the change in the quality of the air from heavy to light, or the sense

[016-072]of being amid new scenes where there were no invidious eyes upon her, sent up

[016-073]her spirits wonderfully. Her hopes mingled with the sunshine in an ideal

[016-074]photosphere which surrounded her as she bounded along against the soft south

[016-075]wind. She heard a pleasant voice in every breeze, and in every bird’s

[016-076]note seemed to lurk a joy.

[016-077]Her face had latterly changed with changing states of mind, continually

[016-078]fluctuating between beauty and ordinariness, according as the thoughts were gay

[016-079]or grave. One day she was pink and flawless; another pale and tragical. When

[016-080]she was pink she was feeling less than when pale; her more perfect beauty

[016-081]accorded with her less elevated mood; her more intense mood with her less

[016-082]perfect beauty. It was her best face physically that was now set against the

[016-083]south wind.

[016-084]The irresistible, universal, automatic tendency to find sweet pleasure

[016-085]somewhere, which pervades all life, from the meanest to the highest, had at

[016-086]length mastered Tess. Being even now only a young woman of twenty, one who

[016-087]mentally and sentimentally had not finished growing, it was impossible that any

[016-088]event should have left upon her an impression that was not in time capable of

[016-089]transmutation.

[016-090]And thus her spirits, and her thankfulness, and her hopes, rose higher and

[016-091]higher. She tried several ballads, but found them inadequate; till,

[016-092]recollecting the psalter that her eyes had so often wandered over of a Sunday

[016-093]morning before she had eaten of the tree of knowledge, she chanted: “O ye

[016-094]Sun and Moon ... O ye Stars ... ye Green Things upon the Earth ...

[016-095]ye Fowls of the Air ... Beasts and Cattle ... Children of Men ...

[016-096]bless ye the Lord, praise Him and magnify Him forever!”

[016-097]She suddenly stopped and murmured: “But perhaps I don’t quite know

[016-098]the Lord as yet.”

[016-099]And probably the half-unconscious rhapsody was a Fetishistic utterance in a

[016-100]Monotheistic setting; women whose chief companions are the forms and forces of

[016-101]outdoor Nature retain in their souls far more of the Pagan fantasy of their

[016-102]remote forefathers than of the systematized religion taught their race at later

[016-103]date. However, Tess found at least approximate expression for her feelings in

[016-104]the old *Benedicite* that she had lisped from infancy; and it was enough.

[016-105]Such high contentment with such a slight initial performance as that of having

[016-106]started towards a means of independent living was a part of the Durbeyfield

[016-107]temperament. Tess really wished to walk uprightly, while her father did nothing

[016-108]of the kind; but she resembled him in being content with immediate and small

[016-109]achievements, and in having no mind for laborious effort towards such petty

[016-110]social advancement as could alone be effected by a family so heavily

[016-111]handicapped as the once powerful d’Urbervilles were now.

[016-112]There was, it might be said, the energy of her mother’s unexpended

[016-113]family, as well as the natural energy of Tess’s years, rekindled after

[016-114]the experience which had so overwhelmed her for the time. Let the truth be

[016-115]told—women do as a rule live through such humiliations, and regain their

[016-116]spirits, and again look about them with an interested eye. While there’s

[016-117]life there’s hope is a conviction not so entirely unknown to the

[016-118]“betrayed” as some amiable theorists would have us believe.

[016-119]Tess Durbeyfield, then, in good heart, and full of zest for life, descended the

[016-120]Egdon slopes lower and lower towards the dairy of her pilgrimage.

[016-121]The marked difference, in the final particular, between the rival vales now

[016-122]showed itself. The secret of Blackmoor was best discovered from the heights

[016-123]around; to read aright the valley before her it was necessary to descend into

[016-124]its midst. When Tess had accomplished this feat she found herself to be

[016-125]standing on a carpeted level, which stretched to the east and west as far as

[016-126]the eye could reach.

[016-127]The river had stolen from the higher tracts and brought in particles to the

[016-128]vale all this horizontal land; and now, exhausted, aged, and attenuated, lay

[016-129]serpentining along through the midst of its former spoils.

[016-130]Not quite sure of her direction, Tess stood still upon the hemmed expanse of

[016-131]verdant flatness, like a fly on a billiard-table of indefinite length, and of

[016-132]no more consequence to the surroundings than that fly. The sole effect of her

[016-133]presence upon the placid valley so far had been to excite the mind of a

[016-134]solitary heron, which, after descending to the ground not far from her path,

[016-135]stood with neck erect, looking at her.

[016-136]Suddenly there arose from all parts of the lowland a prolonged and repeated

[016-137]call—“Waow! waow! waow!”

[016-138]From the furthest east to the furthest west the cries spread as if by

[016-139]contagion, accompanied in some cases by the barking of a dog. It was not the

[016-140]expression of the valley’s consciousness that beautiful Tess had arrived,

[016-141]but the ordinary announcement of milking-time—half-past four

[016-142]o’clock, when the dairymen set about getting in the cows.

[016-143]The red and white herd nearest at hand, which had been phlegmatically waiting

[016-144]for the call, now trooped towards the steading in the background, their great

[016-145]bags of milk swinging under them as they walked. Tess followed slowly in their

[016-146]rear, and entered the barton by the open gate through which they had entered

[016-147]before her. Long thatched sheds stretched round the enclosure, their slopes

[016-148]encrusted with vivid green moss, and their eaves supported by wooden posts

[016-149]rubbed to a glossy smoothness by the flanks of infinite cows and calves of

[016-150]bygone years, now passed to an oblivion almost inconceivable in its profundity.

[016-151]Between the post were ranged the milchers, each exhibiting herself at the

[016-152]present moment to a whimsical eye in the rear as a circle on two stalks, down

[016-153]the centre of which a switch moved pendulum-wise; while the sun, lowering

[016-154]itself behind this patient row, threw their shadows accurately inwards upon the

[016-155]wall. Thus it threw shadows of these obscure and homely figures every evening

[016-156]with as much care over each contour as if it had been the profile of a court

[016-157]beauty on a palace wall; copied them as diligently as it had copied Olympian

[016-158]shapes on marble *façades* long ago, or the outline of Alexander,

[016-159]Caesar, and the Pharaohs.

[016-160]They were the less restful cows that were stalled. Those that would stand still

[016-161]of their own will were milked in the middle of the yard, where many of such

[016-162]better behaved ones stood waiting now—all prime milchers, such as were

[016-163]seldom seen out of this valley, and not always within it; nourished by the

[016-164]succulent feed which the water-meads supplied at this prime season of the year.

[016-165]Those of them that were spotted with white reflected the sunshine in dazzling

[016-166]brilliancy, and the polished brass knobs of their horns glittered with

[016-167]something of military display. Their large-veined udders hung ponderous as

[016-168]sandbags, the teats sticking out like the legs of a gipsy’s crock; and as

[016-169]each animal lingered for her turn to arrive the milk oozed forth and fell in

[016-170]drops to the ground.
