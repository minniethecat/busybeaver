## Character Overview

Sorrow is the infant son of **[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** and **[[Alec d'Urberville]]**, conceived during [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s stay at [[trantridge|Trantridge]]. His brief life — born in secret, named in desperation, and buried without church sanction — encapsulates the novel's central themes of illicit sexuality, religious hypocrisy, and the crushing weight of social judgment on women. Hardy gives the child his own chapter-length treatment, making his baptism and death one of the most emotionally devastating sequences in the novel.

## Birth and Naming

Sorrow is born during the harvest season at [[marlott|Marlott]], while [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] works in the fields. Her physical suffering during pregnancy is compounded by social isolation: "her confidence her sorrow lay" (012-047). The child's birth is never directly depicted; Hardy focuses instead on its moral and emotional consequences.

The naming scene in Chapter XIV forms the emotional core of Sorrow's story. When the infant falls mortally ill and the local parson refuses to baptize a child of sin, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] performs the rite herself before her sleeping younger siblings:

> "SORROW, I baptize thee in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost." (014-286)

This improvised ceremony — a milk jug serving as font, her siblings as unwitting congregation — represents one of the novel's most powerful assertions of spiritual dignity against institutional religion.

## Death and Burial

Sorrow dies shortly after [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s baptism, and the established church refuses him Christian burial because he was illegitimate and privately baptized. Hardy's epitaph for the child is devastating:

> "So passed away Sorrow the Undesired — that intrusive creature, that bastard gift of shameless Nature who respects not the social law" (014-323)

The inability to give Sorrow a proper churchyard burial forces [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to bury him in a corner of the [[marlott|Marlott]] graveyard where suicides and unbaptized infants are interred. This denial of ritual closure becomes a wound that never fully heals.

## Thematic Significance

Sorrow functions as the novel's most potent symbol of the double standard imposed on women. The child is evidence of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s "fall," but the social punishment falls entirely on her and the innocent infant, while [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]] escapes consequence. The baptism scene challenges organized religion's monopoly on grace — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s improvised ritual carries more spiritual authenticity than the parson's rigid adherence to doctrine.

The name "Sorrow" itself is a Hardy-esque touch: allegorical, almost medieval in its directness, yet devastatingly effective. It transforms the child from a plot element into a living emblem of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s condition.
