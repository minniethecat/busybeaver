# Angel Clare

## Role in the Novel

Angel Clare serves as [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]]'s love interest and, despite his progressive ideals, becomes an agent of her destruction. As the novel's embodiment of intellectual and spiritual aspiration, Angel represents both the promise and the failure of enlightened Victorian manhood.

His inability to transcend the sexual double standard he claims to reject makes him a tragic figure in his own right.

## Biography

Angel is the third son of [[Reverend Clare|Reverend James Clare]], a devout clergyman in [[Emminster]]. Rejecting his father's wish that he enter the church, Angel pursues agricultural education, believing that farming offers a more honest and useful life (017-005).

He arrives at [[Talbothays Dairy]] in the Valley of the Great Dairies to learn practical dairy farming (017-008), where he encounters [[Tess Durbeyfield]]. Their courtship unfolds amid the pastoral beauty of the dairy, and Angel idealises [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a pure "daughter of Nature" (020-012).

Despite his family's reservations about [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s social standing, Angel marries her. When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] confesses her relationship with [[Alec d'Urberville]] on their wedding night, Angel's idealism shatters — he cannot reconcile the "pure" [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of his imagination with the woman before him (034-025).

He abandons [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] for Brazil, where he suffers illness and hardship (036-008). After a transformative experience abroad, Angel returns to England seeking [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], only to find she has become Alec's mistress (050-015). The novel ends with Angel and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s sister [[Liza-Lu Durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]] watching the black flag rise over [[Wintoncester]] prison (059-015).

## Key Relationships

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — Angel's love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is genuine but fatally flawed by his idealisation. He sees [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a symbol of natural purity rather than as a complex human being, and his disillusionment when she fails to match this ideal destroys their marriage (034-025).

**[[Reverend Clare]]** — Angel's father, whose rigid evangelical [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]] Angel rejects. Their philosophical differences define Angel's character, yet Angel shares his father's moral absolutism in ways he does not recognise.

**[[Mercy Chant]]** — The pious young woman Angel's parents hope he will marry, representing the conventional path Angel rejects in favour of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]].

## Analysis

Angel Clare is Hardy's most complex male character — neither villain nor hero. His name suggests both "angel" and "clarity," yet he is the character most blinded by his own illusions. Angel's intellectual rebellion against his father's [[religion-and-morality|Christianity]] masks a deeper moral conservatism. He rejects doctrine but retains the Puritan equation of female virtue with sexual purity.

> So we find Angel Clare at six-and-twenty here at Talbothays... (018-115)

The narrator's introduction of Angel situates him within the pastoral world while hinting at his incomplete liberation from the values he claims to have left behind.

The tragedy of Angel is that he recognises his error too late. His Brazilian ordeal strips away his intellectual pride, and he returns capable of the compassion [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] needed on their wedding night. But by then, material circumstances have driven [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] back to Alec, and Angel's redemption can only take the form of accepting [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s sister as a surrogate.

Angel represents the limitations of reform without genuine transformation. He can question the church but cannot question the sexual ideology that underpins it. In this, Hardy suggests that the Victorian crisis of faith was insufficient — what was needed was a crisis of morality itself.
