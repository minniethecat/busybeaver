[045-001]Till this moment she had never seen or heard from d’Urberville since her

[045-002]departure from Trantridge.

[045-003]The rencounter came at a heavy moment, one of all moments calculated to permit

[045-004]its impact with the least emotional shock. But such was unreasoning memory

[045-005]that, though he stood there openly and palpably a converted man, who was

[045-006]sorrowing for his past irregularities, a fear overcame her, paralyzing her

[045-007]movement so that she neither retreated nor advanced.

[045-008]To think of what emanated from that countenance when she saw it last, and to

[045-009]behold it now!... There was the same handsome unpleasantness of mien, but

[045-010]now he wore neatly trimmed, old-fashioned whiskers, the sable moustache having

[045-011]disappeared; and his dress was half-clerical, a modification which had changed

[045-012]his expression sufficiently to abstract the dandyism from his features, and to

[045-013]hinder for a second her belief in his identity.

[045-014]To Tess’s sense there was, just at first, a ghastly *bizarrerie* , a

[045-015]grim incongruity, in the march of these solemn words of Scripture out of such a

[045-016]mouth. This too familiar intonation, less than four years earlier, had brought

[045-017]to her ears expressions of such divergent purpose that her heart became quite

[045-018]sick at the irony of the contrast.

[045-019]It was less a reform than a transfiguration. The former curves of sensuousness

[045-020]were now modulated to lines of devotional passion. The lip-shapes that had

[045-021]meant seductiveness were now made to express supplication; the glow on the

[045-022]cheek that yesterday could be translated as riotousness was evangelized to-day

[045-023]into the splendour of pious rhetoric; animalism had become fanaticism;

[045-024]Paganism, Paulinism; the bold rolling eye that had flashed upon her form in the

[045-025]old time with such mastery now beamed with the rude energy of a theolatry that

[045-026]was almost ferocious. Those black angularities which his face had used to put

[045-027]on when his wishes were thwarted now did duty in picturing the incorrigible

[045-028]backslider who would insist upon turning again to his wallowing in the mire.

[045-029]The lineaments, as such, seemed to complain. They had been diverted from their

[045-030]hereditary connotation to signify impressions for which Nature did not intend

[045-031]them. Strange that their very elevation was a misapplication, that to raise

[045-032]seemed to falsify.

[045-033]Yet could it be so? She would admit the ungenerous sentiment no longer.

[045-034]D’Urberville was not the first wicked man who had turned away from his

[045-035]wickedness to save his soul alive, and why should she deem it unnatural in him?

[045-036]It was but the usage of thought which had been jarred in her at hearing good

[045-037]new words in bad old notes. The greater the sinner, the greater the saint; it

[045-038]was not necessary to dive far into Christian history to discover that.

[045-039]Such impressions as these moved her vaguely, and without strict definiteness.

[045-040]As soon as the nerveless pause of her surprise would allow her to stir, her

[045-041]impulse was to pass on out of his sight. He had obviously not discerned her yet

[045-042]in her position against the sun.

[045-043]But the moment that she moved again he recognized her. The effect upon her old

[045-044]lover was electric, far stronger than the effect of his presence upon her. His

[045-045]fire, the tumultuous ring of his eloquence, seemed to go out of him. His lip

[045-046]struggled and trembled under the words that lay upon it; but deliver them it

[045-047]could not as long as she faced him. His eyes, after their first glance upon her

[045-048]face, hung confusedly in every other direction but hers, but came back in a

[045-049]desperate leap every few seconds. This paralysis lasted, however, but a short

[045-050]time; for Tess’s energies returned with the atrophy of his, and she

[045-051]walked as fast as she was able past the barn and onward.

[045-052]As soon as she could reflect, it appalled her, this change in their relative

[045-053]platforms. He who had wrought her undoing was now on the side of the Spirit,

[045-054]while she remained unregenerate. And, as in the legend, it had resulted that

[045-055]her Cyprian image had suddenly appeared upon his altar, whereby the fire of the

[045-056]priest had been well nigh extinguished.

[045-057]She went on without turning her head. Her back seemed to be endowed with a

[045-058]sensitiveness to ocular beams—even her clothing—so alive was she to

[045-059]a fancied gaze which might be resting upon her from the outside of that barn.

[045-060]All the way along to this point her heart had been heavy with an inactive

[045-061]sorrow; now there was a change in the quality of its trouble. That hunger for

[045-062]affection too long withheld was for the time displaced by an almost physical

[045-063]sense of an implacable past which still engirdled her. It intensified her

[045-064]consciousness of error to a practical despair; the break of continuity between

[045-065]her earlier and present existence, which she had hoped for, had not, after all,

[045-066]taken place. Bygones would never be complete bygones till she was a bygone

[045-067]herself.

[045-077]There was not much time, however, for thought or elusion, and she yielded as

[045-080]“Tess!” he said.

[045-081]She slackened speed without looking round.

[045-082]“Tess!” he repeated. “It is I—Alec

[045-083]d’Urberville.”

[045-084]She then looked back at him, and he came up.

[045-086]“Well—is that all? Yet I deserve no more! Of course,” he
