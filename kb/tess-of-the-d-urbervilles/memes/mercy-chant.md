# Mercy Chant

## Role in the Novel

Mercy Chant is a young woman from a respected [[emminster|Emminster]] family whom [[Reverend Clare]] considers an ideal match for his son [[Angel Clare]]. (025-118) She represents the conventional, socially approved path that [[angel-clare|Angel]] rejects in favor of his love for [[Tess Durbeyfield]].

As a character, Mercy Chant functions primarily as a foil to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — she embodies the respectable, church-going woman of good family that Victorian society would deem appropriate for a clergyman's son. (026-067)

## Biography

Mercy Chant lives in [[emminster|Emminster]] and is described as a young lady of good family and devout character. She is acquainted with the Clare family through church connections and is viewed favorably by [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]] as a prospective wife for [[angel-clare|Angel]]. (026-067)

She appears briefly at church, where [[angel-clare|Angel]] encounters her on his way back from his father's house. (040-007) Later in the novel, it is reported that [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s brother Cuthbert has proposed to and been accepted by Mercy Chant. (057-008)

## Key Relationships

- **[[Angel Clare]]** — the man [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]] wishes her to marry; [[angel-clare|Angel]] rejects this match
- **[[Reverend Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s father, who favors her as a daughter-in-law
- **[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — her unwitting rival for [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s affections

## Key Scenes

- **First Appearance** (025-118): Mercy Chant is introduced as the young lady whom [[angel-clare|Angel]] observes at church.
- **The Approved Match** (026-067): [[reverend-clare|Reverend Clare]] expresses his approval of Mercy Chant as a match for his son.
- **Church Encounter** (040-007): [[angel-clare|Angel]] encounters Mercy Chant by the church on his way back from home.
- **Resolution** (057-008): It is revealed that Cuthbert Clare has proposed to Mercy Chant.

## Analysis

Mercy Chant embodies the path not taken — the life of conventional respectability that [[angel-clare|Angel]] rejects. Her name itself is symbolic: "Mercy" suggests the compassion and forgiveness that the Clare family, in their rigid morality, struggle to extend to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. Her eventual engagement to Cuthbert rather than [[angel-clare|Angel]] reinforces the novel's theme of misaligned relationships.
