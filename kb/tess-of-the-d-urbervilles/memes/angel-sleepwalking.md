# Angel's Sleepwalking

## Summary

On the night before their separation at [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]], a sleepwalking [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] enters [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s bedroom, wraps her in a sheet as a shroud, and carries her across the Froom on a narrow plank bridge to the ruined Abbey-church. There he lays her in the abbot's stone coffin, kisses her lips, and falls into exhausted sleep. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who has not resisted, leads the still-sleeping [[angel-clare|Angel]] back to the house and covers him on the sofa. The scene is the last moment of physical intimacy before [[angel-clare|Angel]] departs for Brazil, and its burial imagery foreshadows [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ultimate fate.

## Context

The confession of their wedding night has shattered the marriage. [[angel-clare|Angel]] cannot forgive [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] for the past she revealed, and he has resolved to separate from her. They are staying at the former d'Urberville mansion in [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]] — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ancestral home, now a converted farmhouse — a setting steeped in the irony of her family's decay.

The psychological strain of the preceding days has exhausted [[angel-clare|Angel]]. His shattered idealism and the irreconcilable conflict between his love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and his rigid moral code have driven him into somnambulism — a state [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] recognizes from an earlier episode before their marriage (037-013–037-018). He plans to leave the next morning. For [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who faces returning to her mother's house in disgrace, this is the final night they will spend under the same roof.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] lies awake when [[angel-clare|Angel]] enters. Her trust in him is absolute: "awake or asleep, he inspired her with no sort of personal fear" (037-019–037-022). She chooses not to wake him, surrendering to whatever he might do — even drowning — as preferable to the separation awaiting them at dawn.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — In his sleep, [[angel-clare|Angel]] expresses the love and tenderness his waking self has repressed since the confession. He murmurs endearments "withheld so severely in his waking hours" (037-031–037-032), revealing the conflict between his ideological convictions and his emotional truth.

## Narrative Impact

The sleepwalking scene is the novel's most concentrated dramatization of the split between [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s conscious ideology and his unconscious desire. By day he rejects [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as morally impure; by night he carries her like a bride, calling her "so sweet, so good, so true" (037-029–037-030). The scene exposes the artificiality of his moral judgment — his body and heart know what his mind refuses to accept.

For [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], the episode offers a bittersweet reprieve. She experiences the tenderness her waking husband denies her, even as she contemplates her own death. Her willingness to be drowned — "it would be better than parting to-morrow to lead severed lives" (037-083–037-084) — reveals how thoroughly the confession has destroyed her hope. Yet she also protects [[angel-clare|Angel]]: she does not wake him, and the next morning she keeps the episode secret, knowing the shame it would cause him to learn that his body had betrayed his principles (037-153–037-158).

The burial imagery is prophetic. [[angel-clare|Angel]] wraps [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in a sheet "as in a shroud" (037-026–037-027), lays her in a stone coffin (037-100–037-103), and murmurs "Dead! dead! dead!" (037-012) throughout. These actions prefigure the death that [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s rejection will set in motion. The scene lingers in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s memory after their parting: "the revelation of his tenderness by the incident of the night raised dreams of a possible future with him" (037-165–037-166).

## Key Passages

[[angel-clare|Angel]] enters the bedroom in a trance and speaks the refrain that defines the scene:

> He reached the middle of the room he stood still and murmured in tones of indescribable sadness — "Dead! dead! dead!" (037-010–037-012)

He wraps [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in the sheet and carries her, speaking the endearments his waking self has withheld:

> "My poor, poor Tess — my dearest, darling Tess! So sweet, so good, so true!" (037-029–037-030)

On the staircase, before descending into the night, [[angel-clare|Angel]] pauses to kiss her:

> He did not let her fall, but took advantage of the support of the handrail to imprint a kiss upon her lips — lips in the day-time scorned. (037-044–037-045)

At the ruined Abbey, [[angel-clare|Angel]] completes his somnambulistic ritual:

> Against the north wall was the empty stone coffin of an abbot... In this Clare carefully laid Tess. Having kissed her lips a second time he breathed deeply, as if a greatly desired end were attained. (037-100–037-103)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], now cold and awake in the coffin, leads the sleeping [[angel-clare|Angel]] back across the stone bridge:

> She whispered in his ear... "Let us walk on, darling"... her words had apparently thrown him back into his dream, which thenceforward seemed to enter on a new phase, wherein he fancied she had risen as a spirit, and was leading him to Heaven. (037-119–037-126)

## Analysis

### The Unconscious Truth

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s waking self condemns [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]; his sleeping self cherishes her. The somnambulism is not random — it is the eruption of everything his conscious mind has suppressed. His murmured endearments are the truth his ideology cannot permit in daylight. Hardy uses the sleepwalking to demonstrate that [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s rejection of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is not natural or inevitable but learned, a product of the "fastidiousness" he cannot shed even when it destroys him.

The next morning, [[angel-clare|Angel]] retains no clear memory of the episode, and his resolve to separate stands "in its bones; nothing but a skeleton, but none the less there" (037-149–037-150). The metaphor is precise: his conscious morality is skeletal, dead structure, while his sleeping self was alive with feeling.

### Death and Rebirth Imagery

The death symbolism is pervasive and layered. [[angel-clare|Angel]] wraps [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in a sheet "as in a shroud" (037-026–037-027). He repeats "Dead! dead! dead!" (037-012) like an incantation. The river crossing on a narrow plank without a handrail — a giddy passage between life and death (037-074–037-080) — echoes the mythological crossing into the underworld. The stone coffin of the abbot (037-100–037-101) is the literal destination: [[angel-clare|Angel]] enacts a burial, laying his wife to rest in a tomb not meant for her.

Yet the scene also contains the seeds of survival. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] rises from the coffin (037-113), and she leads [[angel-clare|Angel]] back across the stone bridge (037-125–037-126) — he dreaming she is a spirit guiding him to Heaven. The movement from entombment to return journey suggests that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], not [[angel-clare|Angel]], is the agent of survival. She will rise from this symbolic death; he will not wake from his moral slumber.

### Tess's Agency

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s stillness during the episode is not simple passivity. Her trust is genuine — "her loyal confidence in him lay so deep down in her heart" (037-019) — and she does not fear him. But her surrender also carries an edge of despair: "If they could only fall together, and both be dashed to pieces, how fit, how desirable" (037-042–037-043). On the river, the impulse to let them both fall into the current stirs in her, but she restrains it: "his — she had no right to tamper with" (037-095–037-096).

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] makes deliberate choices throughout the night. She chooses not to wake him during the crossing. She chooses to lead him safely back. She chooses the next morning to keep the episode secret, knowing it would "stultify him, to know that he had instinctively manifested a fondness for her of which his common-sense did not approve" (037-154–037-155). Her silence protects [[angel-clare|Angel]], but it also preserves the memory of his tenderness as a private consolation for the ordeal ahead.
