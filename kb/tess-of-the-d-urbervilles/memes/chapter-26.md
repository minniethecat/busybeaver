[026-001]It was not till the evening, after family prayers, that Angel found opportunity

[026-002]of broaching to his father one or two subjects near his heart. He had strung

[026-003]himself up to the purpose while kneeling behind his brothers on the carpet,

[026-004]studying the little nails in the heels of their walking boots. When the service

[026-005]was over they went out of the room with their mother, and Mr Clare and himself

[026-006]were left alone.

[026-007]The young man first discussed with the elder his plans for the attainment of

[026-008]his position as a farmer on an extensive scale—either in England or in

[026-009]the Colonies. His father then told him that, as he had not been put to the

[026-010]expense of sending Angel up to Cambridge, he had felt it his duty to set by a

[026-011]sum of money every year towards the purchase or lease of land for him some day,

[026-012]that he might not feel himself unduly slighted.

[026-013]“As far as worldly wealth goes,” continued his father, “you

[026-014]will no doubt stand far superior to your brothers in a few years.”

[026-015]This considerateness on old Mr Clare’s part led Angel onward to the other

[026-016]and dearer subject. He observed to his father that he was then six-and-twenty,

[026-017]and that when he should start in the farming business he would require eyes in

[026-018]the back of his head to see to all matters—some one would be necessary to

[026-019]superintend the domestic labours of his establishment whilst he was afield.

[026-020]Would it not be well, therefore, for him to marry?

[026-021]His father seemed to think this idea not unreasonable; and then Angel put the

[026-022]question—

[026-023]“What kind of wife do you think would be best for me as a thrifty

[026-024]hard-working farmer?”

[026-025]“A truly Christian woman, who will be a help and a comfort to you in your

[026-026]goings-out and your comings-in. Beyond that, it really matters little. Such an

[026-027]one can be found; indeed, my earnest-minded friend and neighbour, Dr

[026-028]Chant—”

[026-029]“But ought she not primarily to be able to milk cows, churn good butter,

[026-030]make immense cheeses; know how to sit hens and turkeys and rear chickens, to

[026-031]direct a field of labourers in an emergency, and estimate the value of sheep

[026-032]and calves?”

[026-033]“Yes; a farmer’s wife; yes, certainly. It would be

[026-034]desirable.” Mr Clare, the elder, had plainly never thought of these

[026-035]points before. “I was going to add,” he said, “that for a

[026-036]pure and saintly woman you will not find one more to your true advantage, and

[026-037]certainly not more to your mother’s mind and my own, than your friend

[026-038]Mercy, whom you used to show a certain interest in. It is true that my

[026-039]neighbour Chant’s daughter had lately caught up the fashion of the

[026-040]younger clergy round about us for decorating the Communion-table—alter,

[026-041]as I was shocked to hear her call it one day—with flowers and other stuff

[026-042]on festival occasions. But her father, who is quite as opposed to such flummery

[026-043]as I, says that can be cured. It is a mere girlish outbreak which, I am sure,

[026-044]will not be permanent.”

[026-045]“Yes, yes; Mercy is good and devout, I know. But, father, don’t you

[026-046]think that a young woman equally pure and virtuous as Miss Chant, but one who,

[026-047]in place of that lady’s ecclesiastical accomplishments, understands the

[026-048]duties of farm life as well as a farmer himself, would suit me infinitely

[026-049]better?”

[026-050]His father persisted in his conviction that a knowledge of a farmer’s

[026-051]wife’s duties came second to a Pauline view of humanity; and the

[026-052]impulsive Angel, wishing to honour his father’s feelings and to advance

[026-053]the cause of his heart at the same time, grew specious. He said that fate or

[026-054]Providence had thrown in his way a woman who possessed every qualification to

[026-055]be the helpmate of an agriculturist, and was decidedly of a serious turn of

[026-056]mind. He would not say whether or not she had attached herself to the sound Low

[026-057]Church School of his father; but she would probably be open to conviction on

[026-058]that point; she was a regular church-goer of simple faith; honest-hearted,

[026-059]receptive, intelligent, graceful to a degree, chaste as a vestal, and, in

[026-060]personal appearance, exceptionally beautiful.

[026-061]“Is she of a family such as you would care to marry into—a lady, in

[026-062]short?” asked his startled mother, who had come softly into the study

[026-063]during the conversation.

[026-064]“She is not what in common parlance is called a lady,” said Angel,

[026-065]unflinchingly, “for she is a cottager’s daughter, as I am proud to

[026-066]say. But she *is* a lady, nevertheless—in feeling and nature.”

[026-067]“Mercy Chant is of a very good family.”

[026-068]“Pooh!—what’s the advantage of that, mother?” said

[026-069]Angel quickly. “How is family to avail the wife of a man who has to rough

[026-070]it as I have, and shall have to do?”

[026-071]“Mercy is accomplished. And accomplishments have their charm,”

[026-072]returned his mother, looking at him through her silver spectacles.

[026-073]“As to external accomplishments, what will be the use of them in the life

[026-074]I am going to lead?—while as to her reading, I can take that in hand.

[026-075]She’ll be apt pupil enough, as you would say if you knew her. She’s

[026-076]brim full of poetry—actualized poetry, if I may use the expression. She *lives* what paper-poets only write... And she is an unimpeachable

[026-077]Christian, I am sure; perhaps of the very tribe, genus, and species you desire

[026-078]to propagate.”

[026-079]“O Angel, you are mocking!”

[026-080]“Mother, I beg pardon. But as she really does attend Church almost every

[026-081]Sunday morning, and is a good Christian girl, I am sure you will tolerate any

[026-082]social shortcomings for the sake of that quality, and feel that I may do worse

[026-083]than choose her.” Angel waxed quite earnest on that rather automatic

[026-084]orthodoxy in his beloved Tess which (never dreaming that it might stand him in

[026-085]such good stead) he had been prone to slight when observing it practised by her

[026-086]and the other milkmaids, because of its obvious unreality amid beliefs

[026-087]essentially naturalistic.

[026-088]In their sad doubts as to whether their son had himself any right whatever to

[026-089]the title he claimed for the unknown young woman, Mr and Mrs Clare began to

[026-090]feel it as an advantage not to be overlooked that she at least was sound in her

[026-091]views; especially as the conjunction of the pair must have arisen by an act of

[026-092]Providence; for Angel never would have made orthodoxy a condition of his

[026-093]choice. They said finally that it was better not to act in a hurry, but that

[026-094]they would not object to see her.

[026-095]Angel therefore refrained from declaring more particulars now. He felt that,

[026-096]single-minded and self-sacrificing as his parents were, there yet existed

[026-097]certain latent prejudices of theirs, as middle-class people, which it would

[026-098]require some tact to overcome. For though legally at liberty to do as he chose,

[026-099]and though their daughter-in-law’s qualifications could make no practical

[026-100]difference to their lives, in the probability of her living far away from them,

[026-101]he wished for affection’s sake not to wound their sentiment in the most

[026-102]important decision of his life.

[026-103]He observed his own inconsistencies in dwelling upon accidents in Tess’s

[026-104]life as if they were vital features. It was for herself that he loved Tess; her

[026-105]soul, her heart, her substance—not for her skill in the dairy, her

[026-106]aptness as his scholar, and certainly not for her simple formal

[026-107]faith-professions. Her unsophisticated open-air existence required no varnish

[026-108]of conventionality to make it palatable to him. He held that education had as

[026-109]yet but little affected the beats of emotion and impulse on which domestic

[026-110]happiness depends. It was probable that, in the lapse of ages, improved systems

[026-111]of moral and intellectual training would appreciably, perhaps considerably,

[026-112]elevate the involuntary and even the unconscious instincts of human nature; but

[026-113]up to the present day, culture, as far as he could see, might be said to have

[026-114]affected only the mental epiderm of those lives which had been brought under

[026-115]its influence. This belief was confirmed by his experience of women, which,

[026-116]having latterly been extended from the cultivated middle-class into the rural

[026-117]community, had taught him how much less was the intrinsic difference between

[026-118]the good and wise woman of one social stratum and the good and wise woman of

[026-119]another social stratum, than between the good and bad, the wise and the

[026-120]foolish, of the same stratum or class.

[026-121]It was the morning of his departure. His brothers had already left the Vicarage

[026-122]to proceed on a walking tour in the north, whence one was to return to his

[026-123]college, and the other to his curacy. Angel might have accompanied them, but

[026-124]preferred to rejoin his sweetheart at Talbothays. He would have been an awkward

[026-125]member of the party; for, though the most appreciative humanist, the most ideal

[026-126]religionist, even the best-versed Christologist of the three, there was

[026-127]alienation in the standing consciousness that his squareness would not fit the

[026-128]round hole that had been prepared for him. To neither Felix nor Cuthbert had he

[026-129]ventured to mention Tess.

[026-130]His mother made him sandwiches, and his father accompanied him, on his own

[026-131]mare, a little way along the road. Having fairly well advanced his own affairs,

[026-132]Angel listened in a willing silence, as they jogged on together through the

[026-133]shady lanes, to his father’s account of his parish difficulties, and the

[026-134]coldness of brother clergymen whom he loved, because of his strict

[026-135]interpretations of the New Testament by the light of what they deemed a

[026-136]pernicious Calvinistic doctrine.

[026-137]“Pernicious!” said Mr Clare, with genial scorn; and he proceeded to

[026-138]recount experiences which would show the absurdity of that idea. He told of

[026-139]wondrous conversions of evil livers of which he had been the instrument, not

[026-140]only amongst the poor, but amongst the rich and well-to-do; and he also

[026-141]candidly admitted many failures.

[026-142]As an instance of the latter, he mentioned the case of a young upstart squire

[026-143]named d’Urberville, living some forty miles off, in the neighbourhood of

[026-144]Trantridge.

[026-145]“Not one of the ancient d’Urbervilles of Kingsbere and other

[026-146]places?” asked his son. “That curiously historic worn-out family

[026-147]with its ghostly legend of the coach-and-four?”

[026-148]“O no. The original d’Urbervilles decayed and disappeared sixty or

[026-149]eighty years ago—at least, I believe so. This seems to be a new family

[026-150]which had taken the name; for the credit of the former knightly line I hope

[026-151]they are spurious, I’m sure. But it is odd to hear you express interest

[026-152]in old families. I thought you set less store by them even than I.”

[026-153]“You misapprehend me, father; you often do,” said Angel with a

[026-154]little impatience. “Politically I am sceptical as to the virtue of their

[026-155]being old. Some of the wise even among themselves ‘exclaim against their

[026-156]own succession,’ as Hamlet puts it; but lyrically, dramatically, and even

[026-157]historically, I am tenderly attached to them.”

[026-158]This distinction, though by no means a subtle one, was yet too subtle for Mr

[026-159]Clare the elder, and he went on with the story he had been about to relate;

[026-160]which was that after the death of the senior so-called d’Urberville, the

[026-161]young man developed the most culpable passions, though he had a blind mother,

[026-162]whose condition should have made him know better. A knowledge of his career

[026-163]having come to the ears of Mr Clare, when he was in that part of the country

[026-164]preaching missionary sermons, he boldly took occasion to speak to the

[026-165]delinquent on his spiritual state. Though he was a stranger, occupying

[026-166]another’s pulpit, he had felt this to be his duty, and took for his text

[026-167]the words from St Luke: “Thou fool, this night thy soul shall be required

[026-168]of thee!” The young man much resented this directness of attack, and in

[026-169]the war of words which followed when they met he did not scruple publicly to

[026-170]insult Mr Clare, without respect for his gray hairs.

[026-171]Angel flushed with distress.

[026-172]“Dear father,” he said sadly, “I wish you would not expose

[026-173]yourself to such gratuitous pain from scoundrels!”

[026-174]“Pain?” said his father, his rugged face shining in the ardour of

[026-175]self-abnegation. “The only pain to me was pain on his account, poor,

[026-176]foolish young man. Do you suppose his incensed words could give me any pain, or

[026-177]even his blows? ‘Being reviled we bless; being persecuted we suffer it;

[026-178]being defamed we entreat; we are made as the filth of the world, and as the

[026-179]offscouring of all things unto this day.’ Those ancient and noble words

[026-180]to the Corinthians are strictly true at this present hour.”

[026-181]“Not blows, father? He did not proceed to blows?”

[026-182]“No, he did not. Though I have borne blows from men in a mad state of

[026-183]intoxication.”

[026-184]“No!”

[026-185]“A dozen times, my boy. What then? I have saved them from the guilt of

[026-186]murdering their own flesh and blood thereby; and they have lived to thank me,

[026-187]and praise God.”

[026-188]“May this young man do the same!” said Angel fervently. “But

[026-189]I fear otherwise, from what you say.”

[026-190]“We’ll hope, nevertheless,” said Mr Clare. “And I

[026-191]continue to pray for him, though on this side of the grave we shall probably

[026-192]never meet again. But, after all, one of those poor words of mine may spring up

[026-193]in his heart as a good seed some day.”

[026-194]Now, as always, Clare’s father was sanguine as a child; and though the

[026-195]younger could not accept his parent’s narrow dogma, he revered his

[026-196]practice and recognized the hero under the pietist. Perhaps he revered his

[026-197]father’s practice even more now than ever, seeing that, in the question

[026-198]of making Tessy his wife, his father had not once thought of inquiring whether

[026-199]she were well provided or penniless. The same unworldliness was what had

[026-200]necessitated Angel’s getting a living as a farmer, and would probably

[026-201]keep his brothers in the position of poor parsons for the term of their

[026-202]activities; yet Angel admired it none the less. Indeed, despite his own

[026-203]heterodoxy, Angel often felt that he was nearer to his father on the human side

[026-204]than was either of his brethren.
