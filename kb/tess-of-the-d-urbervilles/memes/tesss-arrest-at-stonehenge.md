# Tess's Arrest at Stonehenge

## Summary

After five days of hiding in the New Forest, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] and [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] walk north under cover of darkness and reach the ancient stone circle of [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] on Salisbury Plain. Exhausted, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] lies down on a stone altar — once used, perhaps, for human sacrifice — and falls asleep. As dawn breaks, [[angel-clare|Angel]] sees figures approaching through the mist: the police have found them. [[angel-clare|Angel]] asks them to let [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] sleep until she wakes naturally. When she opens her eyes, she sees the officers and says calmly: "I am ready." The novel's final dramatic scene fuses pagan ritual sacrifice, Christian martyrdom, and Hardy's vision of cosmic indifference into one indelible image.

## Context

The arrest at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] is the novel's concluding dramatic scene, followed only by the brief epilogue describing [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s execution and [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s departure with [[liza-lu-durbeyfield|Liza-Lu]]. The choice of [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] — a prehistoric monument whose original purpose remains unknown — is freighted with symbolic meaning.

Hardy had visited [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] and was fascinated by its ambiguous history. By placing [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s arrest on a stone that may have been an altar, he invites multiple interpretations: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as pagan sacrificial victim, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as Christian martyr, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as representative of all women destroyed by the double standard of sexual morality. The ambiguity is deliberate — [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] predates all the moral codes that have condemned [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]].

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — In her final moments of freedom, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] achieves a serenity unprecedented in the novel. She has accepted her fate entirely: "I am ready." Her calm is not resignation but completion. She has loved [[angel-clare|Angel]], she has destroyed Alec, and she has had her five days of happiness. Death holds no terror for her.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s final act is one of tenderness: he asks the officers to let [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] sleep. It is a small, futile gesture — the law will not be delayed — but it represents the love that [[angel-clare|Angel]] could not offer [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in life. His watch over her sleeping body is an echo of the sleepwalking scene, when his unconscious self carried [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to safety while his conscious self could not.

## Narrative Impact

The arrest at [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] is one of the most famous final scenes in English literature. Its power lies in its fusion of three registers: the realistic (a wanted murderer is captured), the symbolic (a sacrificial victim lies on an ancient altar), and the cosmic (the indifferent stones of [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] predate all human moral codes).

Hardy refuses to resolve the tension between these registers. Is [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] a victim of Alec's predation, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s hypocrisy, Victorian sexual morality, or the indifferent universe itself? The stones offer no answer. They have stood for millennia, witnessing countless human tragedies, and they will stand for millennia more. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s death is, in the novel's final devastating line, simply the end: "[[justice-and-law|Justice]] was done, and the President of the Immortals (in Aeschylean phrase) had ended his sport with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]."

The scene also completes the novel's recurring motif of sacrifice. From the death of Prince the horse to the sacrificial imagery surrounding [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s violation at [[the-chase|The Chase]], Hardy has prepared the reader for this final altar. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] becomes, at the last, the offering demanded by a society that values female purity above female life.
