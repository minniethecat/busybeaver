[015-001]“By experience,” says Roger Ascham, “we find out a short way

[015-002]by a long wandering.” Not seldom that long wandering unfits us for

[015-003]further travel, and of what use is our experience to us then? Tess

[015-004]Durbeyfield’s experience was of this incapacitating kind. At last she had

[015-005]learned what to do; but who would now accept her doing?

[015-006]If before going to the d’Urbervilles’ she had vigorously moved

[015-007]under the guidance of sundry gnomic texts and phrases known to her and to the

[015-008]world in general, no doubt she would never have been imposed on. But it had not

[015-009]been in Tess’s power—nor is it in anybody’s power—to

[015-010]feel the whole truth of golden opinions while it is possible to profit by them.

[015-011]She—and how many more—might have ironically said to God with Saint

[015-012]Augustine: “Thou hast counselled a better course than Thou hast

[015-013]permitted.”

[015-014]She remained at her father’s house during the winter months, plucking

[015-015]fowls, or cramming turkeys and geese, or making clothes for her sisters and

[015-016]brothers out of some finery which d’Urberville had given her, and she had

[015-017]put by with contempt. Apply to him she would not. But she would often clasp her

[015-018]hands behind her head and muse when she was supposed to be working hard.

[015-019]She philosophically noted dates as they came past in the revolution of the

[015-020]year; the disastrous night of her undoing at Trantridge with its dark

[015-021]background of The Chase; also the dates of the baby’s birth and death;

[015-022]also her own birthday; and every other day individualized by incidents in which

[015-023]she had taken some share. She suddenly thought one afternoon, when looking in

[015-024]the glass at her fairness, that there was yet another date, of greater

[015-025]importance to her than those; that of her own death, when all these charms

[015-026]would have disappeared; a day which lay sly and unseen among all the other days

[015-027]of the year, giving no sign or sound when she annually passed over it; but not

[015-028]the less surely there. When was it? Why did she not feel the chill of each

[015-029]yearly encounter with such a cold relation? She had Jeremy Taylor’s

[015-030]thought that some time in the future those who had known her would say:

[015-031]“It is the ——th, the day that poor Tess Durbeyfield

[015-032]died”; and there would be nothing singular to their minds in the

[015-033]statement. Of that day, doomed to be her terminus in time through all the ages,

[015-034]she did not know the place in month, week, season or year.

[015-035]Almost at a leap Tess thus changed from simple girl to complex woman. Symbols

[015-036]of reflectiveness passed into her face, and a note of tragedy at times into her

[015-037]voice. Her eyes grew larger and more eloquent. She became what would have been

[015-038]called a fine creature; her aspect was fair and arresting; her soul that of a

[015-039]woman whom the turbulent experiences of the last year or two had quite failed

[015-040]to demoralize. But for the world’s opinion those experiences would have

[015-041]been simply a liberal education.

[015-042]She had held so aloof of late that her trouble, never generally known, was

[015-043]nearly forgotten in Marlott. But it became evident to her that she could never

[015-044]be really comfortable again in a place which had seen the collapse of her

[015-045]family’s attempt to “claim kin”—and, through her, even

[015-046]closer union—with the rich d’Urbervilles. At least she could not be

[015-047]comfortable there till long years should have obliterated her keen

[015-048]consciousness of it. Yet even now Tess felt the pulse of hopeful life still

[015-049]warm within her; she might be happy in some nook which had no memories. To

[015-050]escape the past and all that appertained thereto was to annihilate it, and to

[015-051]do that she would have to get away.

[015-052]Was once lost always lost really true of chastity? she would ask herself. She

[015-053]might prove it false if she could veil bygones. The recuperative power which

[015-054]pervaded organic nature was surely not denied to maidenhood alone.

[015-055]She waited a long time without finding opportunity for a new departure. A

[015-056]particularly fine spring came round, and the stir of germination was almost

[015-057]audible in the buds; it moved her, as it moved the wild animals, and made her

[015-058]passionate to go. At last, one day in early May, a letter reached her from a

[015-059]former friend of her mother’s, to whom she had addressed inquiries long

[015-060]before—a person whom she had never seen—that a skilful milkmaid was

[015-061]required at a dairy-house many miles to the southward, and that the dairyman

[015-062]would be glad to have her for the summer months.

[015-063]It was not quite so far off as could have been wished; but it was probably far

[015-064]enough, her radius of movement and repute having been so small. To persons of

[015-065]limited spheres, miles are as geographical degrees, parishes as counties,

[015-066]counties as provinces and kingdoms.

[015-067]On one point she was resolved: there should be no more d’Urberville

[015-068]air-castles in the dreams and deeds of her new life. She would be the dairymaid

[015-069]Tess, and nothing more. Her mother knew Tess’s feeling on this point so

[015-070]well, though no words had passed between them on the subject, that she never

[015-071]alluded to the knightly ancestry now.

[015-072]Yet such is human inconsistency that one of the interests of the new place to

[015-073]her was the accidental virtues of its lying near her forefathers’ country

[015-074](for they were not Blakemore men, though her mother was Blakemore to the bone).

[015-075]The dairy called Talbothays, for which she was bound, stood not remotely from

[015-076]some of the former estates of the d’Urbervilles, near the great family

[015-077]vaults of her granddames and their powerful husbands. She would be able to look

[015-078]at them, and think not only that d’Urberville, like Babylon, had fallen,

[015-079]but that the individual innocence of a humble descendant could lapse as

[015-080]silently. All the while she wondered if any strange good thing might come of

[015-081]her being in her ancestral land; and some spirit within her rose automatically

[015-082]as the sap in the twigs. It was unexpected youth, surging up anew after its

[015-083]temporary check, and bringing with it hope, and the invincible instinct towards

[015-084]self-delight.
