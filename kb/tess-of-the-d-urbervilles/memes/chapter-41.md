[041-001]From the foregoing events of the winter-time let us press on to an October day,

[041-002]more than eight months subsequent to the parting of Clare and Tess. We discover

[041-003]the latter in changed conditions; instead of a bride with boxes and trunks

[041-004]which others bore, we see her a lonely woman with a basket and a bundle in her

[041-005]own porterage, as at an earlier time when she was no bride; instead of the

[041-006]ample means that were projected by her husband for her comfort through this

[041-007]probationary period, she can produce only a flattened purse.

[041-008]After again leaving Marlott, her home, she had got through the spring and

[041-009]summer without any great stress upon her physical powers, the time being mainly

[041-010]spent in rendering light irregular service at dairy-work near Port-Bredy to the

[041-011]west of the Blackmoor Valley, equally remote from her native place and from

[041-012]Talbothays. She preferred this to living on his allowance. Mentally she

[041-013]remained in utter stagnation, a condition which the mechanical occupation

[041-014]rather fostered than checked. Her consciousness was at that other dairy, at

[041-015]that other season, in the presence of the tender lover who had confronted her

[041-016]there—he who, the moment she had grasped him to keep for her own, had

[041-017]disappeared like a shape in a vision.

[041-018]The dairy-work lasted only till the milk began to lessen, for she had not met

[041-019]with a second regular engagement as at Talbothays, but had done duty as a

[041-020]supernumerary only. However, as harvest was now beginning, she had simply to

[041-021]remove from the pasture to the stubble to find plenty of further occupation,

[041-022]and this continued till harvest was done.

[041-023]Of the five-and-twenty pounds which had remained to her of Clare’s

[041-024]allowance, after deducting the other half of the fifty as a contribution to her

[041-025]parents for the trouble and expense to which she had put them, she had as yet

[041-026]spent but little. But there now followed an unfortunate interval of wet

[041-027]weather, during which she was obliged to fall back upon her sovereigns.

[041-028]She could not bear to let them go. Angel had put them into her hand, had

[041-029]obtained them bright and new from his bank for her; his touch had consecrated

[041-030]them to souvenirs of himself—they appeared to have had as yet no other

[041-031]history than such as was created by his and her own experiences—and to

[041-032]disperse them was like giving away relics. But she had to do it, and one by one

[041-033]they left her hands.

[041-034]She had been compelled to send her mother her address from time to time, but

[041-035]she concealed her circumstances. When her money had almost gone a letter from

[041-036]her mother reached her. Joan stated that they were in dreadful difficulty; the

[041-037]autumn rains had gone through the thatch of the house, which required entire

[041-038]renewal; but this could not be done because the previous thatching had never

[041-039]been paid for. New rafters and a new ceiling upstairs also were required,

[041-040]which, with the previous bill, would amount to a sum of twenty pounds. As her

[041-041]husband was a man of means, and had doubtless returned by this time, could she

[041-042]not send them the money?

[041-043]Tess had thirty pounds coming to her almost immediately from Angel’s

[041-044]bankers, and, the case being so deplorable, as soon as the sum was received she

[041-045]sent the twenty as requested. Part of the remainder she was obliged to expend

[041-046]in winter clothing, leaving only a nominal sum for the whole inclement season

[041-047]at hand. When the last pound had gone, a remark of Angel’s that whenever

[041-048]she required further resources she was to apply to his father, remained to be

[041-049]considered.

[041-050]But the more Tess thought of the step, the more reluctant was she to take it.

[041-051]The same delicacy, pride, false shame, whatever it may be called, on

[041-052]Clare’s account, which had led her to hide from her own parents the

[041-053]prolongation of the estrangement, hindered her owning to his that she was in

[041-054]want after the fair allowance he had left her. They probably despised her

[041-055]already; how much more they would despise her in the character of a mendicant!

[041-056]The consequence was that by no effort could the parson’s daughter-in-law

[041-057]bring herself to let him know her state.

[041-058]Her reluctance to communicate with her husband’s parents might, she

[041-059]thought, lessen with the lapse of time; but with her own the reverse obtained.

[041-060]On her leaving their house after the short visit subsequent to her marriage

[041-061]they were under the impression that she was ultimately going to join her

[041-062]husband; and from that time to the present she had done nothing to disturb

[041-063]their belief that she was awaiting his return in comfort, hoping against hope

[041-064]that his journey to Brazil would result in a short stay only, after which he

[041-065]would come to fetch her, or that he would write for her to join him; in any

[041-066]case that they would soon present a united front to their families and the

[041-067]world. This hope she still fostered. To let her parents know that she was a

[041-068]deserted wife, dependent, now that she had relieved their necessities, on her

[041-069]own hands for a living, after the *éclat* of a marriage which was to

[041-070]nullify the collapse of the first attempt, would be too much indeed.

[041-071]The set of brilliants returned to her mind. Where Clare had deposited them she

[041-072]did not know, and it mattered little, if it were true that she could only use

[041-073]and not sell them. Even were they absolutely hers it would be passing mean to

[041-074]enrich herself by a legal title to them which was not essentially hers at all.

[041-075]Meanwhile her husband’s days had been by no means free from trial. At

[041-076]this moment he was lying ill of fever in the clay lands near Curitiba in

[041-077]Brazil, having been drenched with thunder-storms and persecuted by other

[041-078]hardships, in common with all the English farmers and farm-labourers who, just

[041-079]at this time, were deluded into going thither by the promises of the Brazilian

[041-080]Government, and by the baseless assumption that those frames which, ploughing

[041-081]and sowing on English uplands, had resisted all the weathers to whose moods

[041-082]they had been born, could resist equally well all the weathers by which they

[041-083]were surprised on Brazilian plains.

[041-084]To return. Thus it happened that when the last of Tess’s sovereigns had

[041-085]been spent she was unprovided with others to take their place, while on account

[041-086]of the season she found it increasingly difficult to get employment. Not being

[041-087]aware of the rarity of intelligence, energy, health, and willingness in any

[041-088]sphere of life, she refrained from seeking an indoor occupation; fearing towns,

[041-089]large houses, people of means and social sophistication, and of manners other

[041-090]than rural. From that direction of gentility Black Care had come. Society might

[041-091]be better than she supposed from her slight experience of it. But she had no

[041-092]proof of this, and her instinct in the circumstances was to avoid its purlieus.

[041-093]The small dairies to the west, beyond Port-Bredy, in which she had served as

[041-094]supernumerary milkmaid during the spring and summer required no further aid.

[041-095]Room would probably have been made for her at Talbothays, if only out of sheer

[041-096]compassion; but comfortable as her life had been there, she could not go back.

[041-097]The anti-climax would be too intolerable; and her return might bring reproach

[041-098]upon her idolized husband. She could not have borne their pity, and their

[041-099]whispered remarks to one another upon her strange situation; though she would

[041-100]almost have faced a knowledge of her circumstances by every individual there,

[041-101]so long as her story had remained isolated in the mind of each. It was the

[041-102]interchange of ideas about her that made her sensitiveness wince. Tess could

[041-103]not account for this distinction; she simply knew that she felt it.

[041-104]She was now on her way to an upland farm in the centre of the county, to which

[041-105]she had been recommended by a wandering letter which had reached her from

[041-106]Marian. Marian had somehow heard that Tess was separated from her

[041-107]husband—probably through Izz Huett—and the good-natured and now

[041-108]tippling girl, deeming Tess in trouble, had hastened to notify to her former

[041-109]friend that she herself had gone to this upland spot after leaving the dairy,

[041-110]and would like to see her there, where there was room for other hands, if it

[041-111]was really true that she worked again as of old.

[041-112]With the shortening of the days all hope of obtaining her husband’s

[041-113]forgiveness began to leave her; and there was something of the habitude of the

[041-114]wild animal in the unreflecting instinct with which she rambled

[041-115]on—disconnecting herself by littles from her eventful past at every step,

[041-116]obliterating her identity, giving no thought to accidents or contingencies

[041-117]which might make a quick discovery of her whereabouts by others of importance

[041-118]to her own happiness, if not to theirs.

[041-119]Among the difficulties of her lonely position not the least was the attention

[041-120]she excited by her appearance, a certain bearing of distinction, which she had

[041-121]caught from Clare, being superadded to her natural attractiveness. Whilst the

[041-122]clothes lasted which had been prepared for her marriage, these casual glances

[041-123]of interest caused her no inconvenience, but as soon as she was compelled to

[041-124]don the wrapper of a fieldwoman, rude words were addressed to her more than

[041-125]once; but nothing occurred to cause her bodily fear till a particular November

[041-126]afternoon.

[041-127]She had preferred the country west of the River Brit to the upland farm for

[041-128]which she was now bound, because, for one thing, it was nearer to the home of

[041-129]her husband’s father; and to hover about that region unrecognized, with

[041-130]the notion that she might decide to call at the Vicarage some day, gave her

[041-131]pleasure. But having once decided to try the higher and drier levels, she

[041-132]pressed back eastward, marching afoot towards the village of Chalk-Newton,

[041-133]where she meant to pass the night.

[041-134]The lane was long and unvaried, and, owing to the rapid shortening of the days,

[041-135]dusk came upon her before she was aware. She had reached the top of a hill down

[041-136]which the lane stretched its serpentine length in glimpses, when she heard

[041-137]footsteps behind her back, and in a few moments she was overtaken by a man. He

[041-138]stepped up alongside Tess and said—

[041-139]“Good night, my pretty maid”: to which she civilly replied.

[041-140]The light still remaining in the sky lit up her face, though the landscape was

[041-141]nearly dark. The man turned and stared hard at her.

[041-142]“Why, surely, it is the young wench who was at Trantridge

[041-143]awhile—young Squire d’Urberville’s friend? I was there at

[041-144]that time, though I don’t live there now.”

[041-145]She recognized in him the well-to-do boor whom Angel had knocked down at the

[041-146]inn for addressing her coarsely. A spasm of anguish shot through her, and she

[041-147]returned him no answer.

[041-148]“Be honest enough to own it, and that what I said in the town was true,

[041-149]though your fancy-man was so up about it—hey, my sly one? You ought to

[041-150]beg my pardon for that blow of his, considering.”

[041-151]Still no answer came from Tess. There seemed only one escape for her hunted

[041-152]soul. She suddenly took to her heels with the speed of the wind, and, without

[041-153]looking behind her, ran along the road till she came to a gate which opened

[041-154]directly into a plantation. Into this she plunged, and did not pause till she

[041-155]was deep enough in its shade to be safe against any possibility of discovery.

[041-156]Under foot the leaves were dry, and the foliage of some holly bushes which grew

[041-157]among the deciduous trees was dense enough to keep off draughts. She scraped

[041-158]together the dead leaves till she had formed them into a large heap, making a

[041-159]sort of nest in the middle. Into this Tess crept.

[041-160]Such sleep as she got was naturally fitful; she fancied she heard strange

[041-161]noises, but persuaded herself that they were caused by the breeze. She thought

[041-162]of her husband in some vague warm clime on the other side of the globe, while

[041-163]she was here in the cold. Was there another such a wretched being as she in the

[041-164]world? Tess asked herself; and, thinking of her wasted life, said, “All

[041-165]is vanity.” She repeated the words mechanically, till she reflected that

[041-166]this was a most inadequate thought for modern days. Solomon had thought as far

[041-167]as that more than two thousand years ago; she herself, though not in the van of

[041-168]thinkers, had got much further. If all were only vanity, who would mind it? All

[041-169]was, alas, worse than vanity—injustice, punishment, exaction, death. The

[041-170]wife of Angel Clare put her hand to her brow, and felt its curve, and the edges

[041-171]of her eye-sockets perceptible under the soft skin, and thought as she did so

[041-172]that a time would come when that bone would be bare. “I wish it were

[041-173]now,” she said.

[041-174]In the midst of these whimsical fancies she heard a new strange sound among the

[041-175]leaves. It might be the wind; yet there was scarcely any wind. Sometimes it was

[041-176]a palpitation, sometimes a flutter; sometimes it was a sort of gasp or gurgle.

[041-177]Soon she was certain that the noises came from wild creatures of some kind, the

[041-178]more so when, originating in the boughs overhead, they were followed by the

[041-179]fall of a heavy body upon the ground. Had she been ensconced here under other

[041-180]and more pleasant conditions she would have become alarmed; but, outside

[041-181]humanity, she had at present no fear.

[041-182]Day at length broke in the sky. When it had been day aloft for some little

[041-183]while it became day in the wood.

[041-184]Directly the assuring and prosaic light of the world’s active hours had

[041-185]grown strong, she crept from under her hillock of leaves, and looked around

[041-186]boldly. Then she perceived what had been going on to disturb her. The

[041-187]plantation wherein she had taken shelter ran down at this spot into a peak,

[041-188]which ended it hitherward, outside the hedge being arable ground. Under the

[041-189]trees several pheasants lay about, their rich plumage dabbled with blood; some

[041-190]were dead, some feebly twitching a wing, some staring up at the sky, some

[041-191]pulsating quickly, some contorted, some stretched out—all of them

[041-192]writhing in agony, except the fortunate ones whose tortures had ended during

[041-193]the night by the inability of nature to bear more.

[041-194]Tess guessed at once the meaning of this. The birds had been driven down into

[041-195]this corner the day before by some shooting-party; and while those that had

[041-196]dropped dead under the shot, or had died before nightfall, had been searched

[041-197]for and carried off, many badly wounded birds had escaped and hidden themselves

[041-198]away, or risen among the thick boughs, where they had maintained their position

[041-199]till they grew weaker with loss of blood in the night-time, when they had

[041-200]fallen one by one as she had heard them.

[041-201]She had occasionally caught glimpses of these men in girlhood, looking over

[041-202]hedges, or peeping through bushes, and pointing their guns, strangely

[041-203]accoutred, a bloodthirsty light in their eyes. She had been told that, rough

[041-204]and brutal as they seemed just then, they were not like this all the year

[041-205]round, but were, in fact, quite civil persons save during certain weeks of

[041-206]autumn and winter, when, like the inhabitants of the Malay Peninsula, they ran

[041-207]amuck, and made it their purpose to destroy life—in this case harmless

[041-208]feathered creatures, brought into being by artificial means solely to gratify

[041-209]these propensities—at once so unmannerly and so unchivalrous towards

[041-210]their weaker fellows in Nature’s teeming family.

[041-211]With the impulse of a soul who could feel for kindred sufferers as much as for

[041-212]herself, Tess’s first thought was to put the still living birds out of

[041-213]their torture, and to this end with her own hands she broke the necks of as

[041-214]many as she could find, leaving them to lie where she had found them till the

[041-215]game-keepers should come—as they probably would come—to look for

[041-216]them a second time.

[041-217]“Poor darlings—to suppose myself the most miserable being on earth

[041-218]in the sight o’ such misery as yours!” she exclaimed, her tears

[041-219]running down as she killed the birds tenderly. “And not a twinge of

[041-220]bodily pain about me! I be not mangled, and I be not bleeding, and I have two

[041-221]hands to feed and clothe me.” She was ashamed of herself for her gloom of

[041-222]the night, based on nothing more tangible than a sense of condemnation under an

[041-223]arbitrary law of society which had no foundation in Nature.
