# Reverend Clare

## Character Overview

Reverend James Clare is the father of [[Angel Clare]] and a devout clergyman at [[emminster|Emminster]]. He represents the traditional Victorian religious establishment against which [[angel-clare|Angel]] defines his intellectual independence. Though a man of genuine principle, his rigid worldview creates distance between himself and his son, and his assumptions about class and morality complicate Angel's relationship with [[Tess Durbeyfield]].

## Character and Theology

Hardy introduces Reverend Clare through one of the novel's most sustained character portraits, situating him as a representative of a vanishing evangelical tradition. "Old Mr Clare was a clergyman of a type which, within the last twenty years, has well nigh dropped out of contemporary life" (025-139, 025-140). Hardy traces his spiritual lineage: "A spiritual descendant in the direct line from Wycliff, Huss, Luther, Calvin; an Evangelical of the Evangelicals, a Conversionist, a man of Apostolic simplicity in life and thought" (025-140–025-142). This theological genealogy — Reformation, Puritan, Evangelical — places the reverend at the end of a tradition whose intellectual and spiritual energy has been exhausted.

The reverend's intellectual closure is presented with wry precision: "he had in his raw youth made up his mind once for all in the deeper questions of existence, and admitted no further reasoning on them thenceforward" (025-143, 025-144). This finality of conviction, while a source of moral strength, is also a form of intellectual arrest. Hardy notes that "he was regarded even by those of his own date and school of thinking as extreme" (025-145) — yet even his opponents "were unwillingly won to admiration for his thoroughness, and for the remarkable power he showed in dismissing all question as to principles in his energy for applying them" (025-147, 025-148).

Hardy's theological satire deepens: "He loved Paul of Tarsus, liked St John, hated St James as much as he dared, and regarded with mixed feelings Timothy, Titus, and Philemon. The New Testament was less a Christiad than a Pauliad to his intelligence — less an argument than an intoxication" (025-149–025-152). The reverend's faith is Pauline rather than Christ-like — doctrinal, combative, systematic — a faith of propositions rather than parables. The characterization is affectionate but devastating: a good man whose goodness is inseparable from the narrowness of its expression.

## Role in the Novel

Reverend Clare serves as vicar at [[emminster|Emminster]], where he lives with his wife. He has three sons: [[cuthbert-clare|Cuthbert]] and [[felix-clare|Felix]], who follow conventional paths, and [[angel-clare|Angel]], the youngest, whose rejection of organized religion and embrace of agricultural life disappoints his father.

Despite their differences, Reverend Clare's moral seriousness has shaped Angel's character. The reverend favors a match between Angel and [[Mercy Chant]], a young woman from a respected local family. (026-067) His preference for [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]] over [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] reflects the class prejudices of his world.

## Key Scenes

### The Emminster Visit: Angel's Confession (Ch 25–26)

Angel Clare's visit to his father's vicarage — "his father's hill-surrounded little town, the Tudor church-tower of red stone, the clump of trees near the Vicarage" (025-107, 025-108) — is undertaken to seek parental approval for his marriage. Angel "had strung himself up to the determination of broaching to his father one or two subjects near his heart" (026-002). After dinner, "Mr Clare and himself were left" (026-005) — the masculine conversation that follows revealing both the reverend's generosity and his limits.

The conversation turns on marriage. The reverend's ideal daughter-in-law is "a truly Christian woman, who will be a help and a comfort to you in your parish work" (026-025). When Angel asks whether his bride must necessarily be a lady, "Mr Clare, the elder, had plainly never thought of these things as desirable" (026-034). His preference for Mercy Chant — "Mercy is good and devout" (026-045) — is grounded in the assumption that piety and social standing are naturally allied. Angel, pressing for recognition of Tess's worth outside those categories, encounters a paternal refusal that is gentle but absolute.

### The Post-Marriage Visit and Proverbs 31 (Ch 39)

Three weeks after the marriage, Angel returns to Emminster alone, his arrival stirring "the atmosphere of the Vicarage as the dive of the kingfisher stirs a quiet pool" (039-054). His father and mother are there, but neither of his brothers. Over supper, Angel makes "further exposition of his plans" (039-095) — the Brazil emigration scheme by which he hopes to escape the consequences of his broken marriage.

The visit's most charged moment comes at evening prayers. The reverend, with unerring instinct for the text that will wound his son most deeply, selects Proverbs 31: "Your father has decided to read us the chapter in Proverbs in praise of a virtuous wife" (039-128). Mrs Clare, with guileless cruelty, observes after the reading: "I could not help thinking how very aptly that chapter your dear father read applied, in some of its particulars, to the woman you have chosen" (039-143). The praise of the virtuous woman — "Who can find a virtuous woman?" (039-135) — spoken over a son who has just abandoned his wife for not being virtuous enough, achieves an irony that neither parent intends. The reverend reads Scripture in good faith; the text's application to Angel's situation is left for the reader to supply.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Angel Clare]]** — his youngest son, whose intellectual independence creates filial tension
- **[[Mercy Chant]]** — the young woman he favors as a match for Angel
- **[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — his daughter-in-law, whose past scandalizes his conventional sensibilities

## Analysis

Reverend Clare serves as a foil to his son Angel — embodying the established religious orthodoxy that Angel rejects while paradoxically shaping his son's intense moral seriousness. His conventional piety, though sincere, blinds him to the deeper moral complexities represented by Tess. Yet Hardy's portrait is notably generous: the reverend is no villain, no hypocrite. He is a man of "Apostolic simplicity" whose faith has genuinely animated a life of pastoral care and whose love for his son, however constrained by his categories, is real. His tragedy — a minor-key echo of his son's — is that his goodness cannot stretch to comprehend a goodness that exceeds his definitions.
