[052-001]During the small hours of the next morning, while it was still dark, dwellers

[052-002]near the highways were conscious of a disturbance of their night’s rest

[052-003]by rumbling noises, intermittently continuing till daylight—noises as

[052-004]certain to recur in this particular first week of the month as the voice of the

[052-005]cuckoo in the third week of the same. They were the preliminaries of the

[052-006]general removal, the passing of the empty waggons and teams to fetch the goods

[052-007]of the migrating families; for it was always by the vehicle of the farmer who

[052-008]required his services that the hired man was conveyed to his destination. That

[052-009]this might be accomplished within the day was the explanation of the

[052-010]reverberation occurring so soon after midnight, the aim of the carters being to

[052-011]reach the door of the outgoing households by six o’clock, when the

[052-012]loading of their movables at once began.

[052-013]But to Tess and her mother’s household no such anxious farmer sent his

[052-014]team. They were only women; they were not regular labourers; they were not

[052-015]particularly required anywhere; hence they had to hire a waggon at their own

[052-016]expense, and got nothing sent gratuitously.

[052-017]It was a relief to Tess, when she looked out of the window that morning, to

[052-018]find that though the weather was windy and louring, it did not rain, and that

[052-019]the waggon had come. A wet Lady-Day was a spectre which removing families never

[052-020]forgot; damp furniture, damp bedding, damp clothing accompanied it, and left a

[052-021]train of ills.

[052-022]Her mother, ’Liza-Lu, and Abraham were also awake, but the younger

[052-023]children were let sleep on. The four breakfasted by the thin light, and the

[052-024]“house-ridding” was taken in hand.

[052-025]It proceeded with some cheerfulness, a friendly neighbour or two assisting.

[052-026]When the large articles of furniture had been packed in position, a circular

[052-027]nest was made of the beds and bedding, in which Joan Durbeyfield and the young

[052-028]children were to sit through the journey. After loading there was a long delay

[052-029]before the horses were brought, these having been unharnessed during the

[052-030]ridding; but at length, about two o’clock, the whole was under way, the

[052-031]cooking-pot swinging from the axle of the waggon, Mrs Durbeyfield and family at

[052-032]the top, the matron having in her lap, to prevent injury to its works, the head

[052-033]of the clock, which, at any exceptional lurch of the waggon, struck one, or

[052-034]one-and-a-half, in hurt tones. Tess and the next eldest girl walked alongside

[052-035]till they were out of the village.

[052-036]They had called on a few neighbours that morning and the previous evening, and

[052-037]some came to see them off, all wishing them well, though, in their secret

[052-038]hearts, hardly expecting welfare possible to such a family, harmless as the

[052-039]Durbeyfields were to all except themselves. Soon the equipage began to ascend

[052-040]to higher ground, and the wind grew keener with the change of level and soil.

[052-041]The day being the sixth of April, the Durbeyfield waggon met many other waggons

[052-042]with families on the summit of the load, which was built on a wellnigh

[052-043]unvarying principle, as peculiar, probably, to the rural labourer as the

[052-044]hexagon to the bee. The groundwork of the arrangement was the family dresser,

[052-045]which, with its shining handles, and finger-marks, and domestic evidences thick

[052-046]upon it, stood importantly in front, over the tails of the shaft-horses, in its

[052-047]erect and natural position, like some Ark of the Covenant that they were bound

[052-048]to carry reverently.

[052-049]Some of the households were lively, some mournful; some were stopping at the

[052-050]doors of wayside inns; where, in due time, the Durbeyfield menagerie also drew

[052-051]up to bait horses and refresh the travellers.

[052-052]During the halt Tess’s eyes fell upon a three-pint blue mug, which was

[052-053]ascending and descending through the air to and from the feminine section of a

[052-054]household, sitting on the summit of a load that had also drawn up at a little

[052-055]distance from the same inn. She followed one of the mug’s journeys

[052-056]upward, and perceived it to be clasped by hands whose owner she well knew. Tess

[052-057]went towards the waggon.

[052-058]“Marian and Izz!” she cried to the girls, for it was they, sitting

[052-059]with the moving family at whose house they had lodged. “Are you

[052-060]house-ridding to-day, like everybody else?”

[052-061]They were, they said. It had been too rough a life for them at Flintcomb-Ash,

[052-062]and they had come away, almost without notice, leaving Groby to prosecute them

[052-063]if he chose. They told Tess their destination, and Tess told them hers.

[052-064]Marian leant over the load, and lowered her voice. “Do you know that the

[052-065]gentleman who follows ’ee—you’ll guess who I mean—came

[052-066]to ask for ’ee at Flintcomb after you had gone? We didn’t

[052-067]tell’n where you was, knowing you wouldn’t wish to see him.”

[052-068]“Ah—but I did see him!” Tess murmured. “He found

[052-069]me.”

[052-070]“And do he know where you be going?”

[052-071]“I think so.”

[052-072]“Husband come back?”

[052-073]“No.”

[052-074]She bade her acquaintance goodbye—for the respective carters had now come

[052-075]out from the inn—and the two waggons resumed their journey in opposite

[052-076]directions; the vehicle whereon sat Marian, Izz, and the ploughman’s

[052-077]family with whom they had thrown in their lot, being brightly painted, and

[052-078]drawn by three powerful horses with shining brass ornaments on their harness;

[052-079]while the waggon on which Mrs Durbeyfield and her family rode was a creaking

[052-080]erection that would scarcely bear the weight of the superincumbent load; one

[052-081]which had known no paint since it was made, and drawn by two horses only. The

[052-082]contrast well marked the difference between being fetched by a thriving farmer

[052-083]and conveying oneself whither no hirer waited one’s coming.

[052-084]The distance was great—too great for a day’s journey—and it

[052-085]was with the utmost difficulty that the horses performed it. Though they had

[052-086]started so early, it was quite late in the afternoon when they turned the flank

[052-087]of an eminence which formed part of the upland called Greenhill. While the

[052-088]horses stood to stale and breathe themselves Tess looked around. Under the

[052-089]hill, and just ahead of them, was the half-dead townlet of their pilgrimage,

[052-090]Kingsbere, where lay those ancestors of whom her father had spoken and sung to

[052-091]painfulness: Kingsbere, the spot of all spots in the world which could be

[052-092]considered the d’Urbervilles’ home, since they had resided there

[052-093]for full five hundred years.

[052-094]A man could be seen advancing from the outskirts towards them, and when he

[052-095]beheld the nature of their waggon-load he quickened his steps.

[052-096]“You be the woman they call Mrs Durbeyfield, I reckon?” he said to

[052-097]Tess’s mother, who had descended to walk the remainder of the way.

[052-098]She nodded. “Though widow of the late Sir John d’Urberville, poor

[052-099]nobleman, if I cared for my rights; and returning to the domain of his

[052-100]forefathers.”

[052-101]“Oh? Well, I know nothing about that; but if you be Mrs Durbeyfield, I am

[052-102]sent to tell ’ee that the rooms you wanted be let. We didn’t know

[052-103]that you was coming till we got your letter this morning—when ’twas

[052-104]too late. But no doubt you can get other lodgings somewhere.”

[052-105]The man had noticed the face of Tess, which had become ash-pale at his

[052-106]intelligence. Her mother looked hopelessly at fault. “What shall we do

[052-107]now, Tess?” she said bitterly. “Here’s a welcome to your

[052-108]ancestors’ lands! However, let’s try further.”

[052-109]They moved on into the town, and tried with all their might, Tess remaining

[052-110]with the waggon to take care of the children whilst her mother and

[052-111]’Liza-Lu made inquiries. At the last return of Joan to the vehicle, an

[052-112]hour later, when her search for accommodation had still been fruitless, the

[052-113]driver of the waggon said the goods must be unloaded, as the horses were

[052-114]half-dead, and he was bound to return part of the way at least that night.

[052-115]“Very well—unload it here,” said Joan recklessly.

[052-116]“I’ll get shelter somewhere.”

[052-117]The waggon had drawn up under the churchyard wall, in a spot screened from

[052-118]view, and the driver, nothing loth, soon hauled down the poor heap of household

[052-119]goods. This done, she paid him, reducing herself to almost her last shilling

[052-120]thereby, and he moved off and left them, only too glad to get out of further

[052-121]dealings with such a family. It was a dry night, and he guessed that they would

[052-122]come to no harm.

[052-123]Tess gazed desperately at the pile of furniture. The cold sunlight of this

[052-124]spring evening peered invidiously upon the crocks and kettles, upon the bunches

[052-125]of dried herbs shivering in the breeze, upon the brass handles of the dresser,

[052-126]upon the wicker-cradle they had all been rocked in, and upon the well-rubbed

[052-127]clock-case, all of which gave out the reproachful gleam of indoor articles

[052-128]abandoned to the vicissitudes of a roofless exposure for which they were never

[052-129]made. Round about were deparked hills and slopes—now cut up into little

[052-130]paddocks—and the green foundations that showed where the

[052-131]d’Urberville mansion once had stood; also an outlying stretch of Egdon

[052-132]Heath that had always belonged to the estate. Hard by, the aisle of the church

[052-133]called the d’Urberville Aisle looked on imperturbably.

[052-134]“Isn’t your family vault your own freehold?” said

[052-135]Tess’s mother, as she returned from a reconnoitre of the church and

[052-136]graveyard. “Why, of course ’tis, and that’s where we will

[052-137]camp, girls, till the place of your ancestors finds us a roof! Now, Tess and

[052-138]’Liza and Abraham, you help me. We’ll make a nest for these

[052-139]children, and then we’ll have another look round.”

[052-140]Tess listlessly lent a hand, and in a quarter of an hour the old four-post

[052-141]bedstead was dissociated from the heap of goods, and erected under the south

[052-142]wall of the church, the part of the building known as the d’Urberville

[052-143]Aisle, beneath which the huge vaults lay. Over the tester of the bedstead was a

[052-144]beautiful traceried window, of many lights, its date being the fifteenth

[052-145]century. It was called the d’Urberville Window, and in the upper part

[052-146]could be discerned heraldic emblems like those on Durbeyfield’s old seal

[052-147]and spoon.

[052-148]Joan drew the curtains round the bed so as to make an excellent tent of it, and

[052-149]put the smaller children inside. “If it comes to the worst we can sleep

[052-150]there too, for one night,” she said. “But let us try further on,

[052-151]and get something for the dears to eat! O, Tess, what’s the use of your

[052-152]playing at marrying gentlemen, if it leaves us like this!”

[052-153]Accompanied by ’Liza-Lu and the boy, she again ascended the little lane

[052-154]which secluded the church from the townlet. As soon as they got into the street

[052-155]they beheld a man on horseback gazing up and down. “Ah—I’m

[052-156]looking for you!” he said, riding up to them. “This is indeed a

[052-157]family gathering on the historic spot!”

[052-158]It was Alec d’Urberville. “Where is Tess?” he asked.

[052-159]Personally Joan had no liking for Alec. She cursorily signified the direction

[052-160]of the church, and went on, d’Urberville saying that he would see them

[052-161]again, in case they should be still unsuccessful in their search for shelter,

[052-162]of which he had just heard. When they had gone, d’Urberville rode to the

[052-163]inn, and shortly after came out on foot.

[052-164]In the interim Tess, left with the children inside the bedstead, remained

[052-165]talking with them awhile, till, seeing that no more could be done to make them

[052-166]comfortable just then, she walked about the churchyard, now beginning to be

[052-167]embrowned by the shades of nightfall. The door of the church was unfastened,

[052-168]and she entered it for the first time in her life.

[052-169]Within the window under which the bedstead stood were the tombs of the family,

[052-170]covering in their dates several centuries. They were canopied, altar-shaped,

[052-171]and plain; their carvings being defaced and broken; their brasses torn from the

[052-172]matrices, the rivet-holes remaining like martin-holes in a sandcliff. Of all

[052-173]the reminders that she had ever received that her people were socially extinct,

[052-174]there was none so forcible as this spoliation.

[052-175]She drew near to a dark stone on which was inscribed:

[052-176]**OSTIUM SEPULCHRI ANTIQUAE FAMILIAE D’URBERVILLE**

[052-177]Tess did not read Church-Latin like a Cardinal, but she knew that this was the

[052-178]door of her ancestral sepulchre, and that the tall knights of whom her father

[052-179]had chanted in his cups lay inside.

[052-180]She musingly turned to withdraw, passing near an altar-tomb, the oldest of them

[052-181]all, on which was a recumbent figure. In the dusk she had not noticed it

[052-182]before, and would hardly have noticed it now but for an odd fancy that the

[052-183]effigy moved. As soon as she drew close to it she discovered all in a moment

[052-184]that the figure was a living person; and the shock to her sense of not having

[052-185]been alone was so violent that she was quite overcome, and sank down nigh to

[052-186]fainting, not, however, till she had recognized Alec d’Urberville in the

[052-187]form.

[052-188]He leapt off the slab and supported her.

[052-189]“I saw you come in,” he said smiling, “and got up there not

[052-190]to interrupt your meditations. A family gathering, is it not, with these old

[052-191]fellows under us here? Listen.”

[052-192]He stamped with his heel heavily on the floor; whereupon there arose a hollow

[052-193]echo from below.

[052-194]“That shook them a bit, I’ll warrant!” he continued.

[052-195]“And you thought I was the mere stone reproduction of one of them. But

[052-196]no. The old order changeth. The little finger of the sham d’Urberville

[052-197]can do more for you than the whole dynasty of the real underneath.... Now

[052-198]command me. What shall I do?”

[052-199]“Go away!” she murmured.

[052-200]“I will—I’ll look for your mother,” said he blandly.

[052-201]But in passing her he whispered: “Mind this; you’ll be civil

[052-202]yet!”

[052-203]When he was gone she bent down upon the entrance to the vaults, and said—

[052-204]“Why am I on the wrong side of this door!”

[052-205]In the meantime Marian and Izz Huett had journeyed onward with the chattels of

[052-206]the ploughman in the direction of their land of Canaan—the Egypt of some

[052-207]other family who had left it only that morning. But the girls did not for a

[052-208]long time think of where they were going. Their talk was of Angel Clare and

[052-209]Tess, and Tess’s persistent lover, whose connection with her previous

[052-210]history they had partly heard and partly guessed ere this.

[052-211]“’Tisn’t as though she had never known him afore,” said

[052-212]Marian. “His having won her once makes all the difference in the world.

[052-213]’Twould be a thousand pities if he were to tole her away again. Mr Clare

[052-214]can never be anything to us, Izz; and why should we grudge him to her, and not

[052-215]try to mend this quarrel? If he could on’y know what straits she’s

[052-216]put to, and what’s hovering round, he might come to take care of his

[052-217]own.”

[052-218]“Could we let him know?”

[052-219]They thought of this all the way to their destination; but the bustle of

[052-220]re-establishment in their new place took up all their attention then. But when

[052-221]they were settled, a month later, they heard of Clare’s approaching

[052-222]return, though they had learnt nothing more of Tess. Upon that, agitated anew

[052-223]by their attachment to him, yet honourably disposed to her, Marian uncorked the

[052-224]penny ink-bottle they shared, and a few lines were concocted between the two

[052-225]girls.

[052-226]Honour’d Sir,

[052-227]Look to your Wife if you do love her as much as she do love you. For she is

[052-228]sore put to by an Enemy in the shape of a Friend. Sir, there is one near her

[052-229]who ought to be Away. A woman should not be try’d beyond her Strength,

[052-230]and continual dropping will wear away a Stone—ay, more—a Diamond.

[052-231]From Two Well-Wishers

[052-232]This was addressed to Angel Clare at the only place they had ever heard him to

[052-233]be connected with, Emminster Vicarage; after which they continued in a mood of

[052-234]emotional exaltation at their own generosity, which made them sing in

[052-235]hysterical snatches and weep at the same time.
