[053-001]It was evening at Emminster Vicarage. The two customary candles were burning

[053-002]under their green shades in the Vicar’s study, but he had not been

[053-003]sitting there. Occasionally he came in, stirred the small fire which sufficed

[053-004]for the increasing mildness of the spring, and went out again; sometimes

[053-005]pausing at the front door, going on to the drawing-room, then returning again

[053-006]to the front door.

[053-007]It faced westward, and though gloom prevailed inside, there was still light

[053-008]enough without to see with distinctness. Mrs Clare, who had been sitting in the

[053-009]drawing-room, followed him hither.

[053-010]“Plenty of time yet,” said the Vicar. “He doesn’t reach

[053-011]Chalk-Newton till six, even if the train should be punctual, and ten miles of

[053-012]country-road, five of them in Crimmercrock Lane, are not jogged over in a hurry

[053-013]by our old horse.”

[053-014]“But he has done it in an hour with us, my dear.”

[053-015]“Years ago.”

[053-016]Thus they passed the minutes, each well knowing that this was only waste of

[053-017]breath, the one essential being simply to wait.

[053-018]At length there was a slight noise in the lane, and the old pony-chaise

[053-019]appeared indeed outside the railings. They saw alight therefrom a form which

[053-020]they affected to recognize, but would actually have passed by in the street

[053-021]without identifying had he not got out of their carriage at the particular

[053-022]moment when a particular person was due.

[053-023]Mrs Clare rushed through the dark passage to the door, and her husband came

[053-024]more slowly after her.

[053-025]The new arrival, who was just about to enter, saw their anxious faces in the

[053-026]doorway and the gleam of the west in their spectacles because they confronted

[053-027]the last rays of day; but they could only see his shape against the light.

[053-028]“O, my boy, my boy—home again at last!” cried Mrs Clare, who

[053-029]cared no more at that moment for the stains of heterodoxy which had caused all

[053-030]this separation than for the dust upon his clothes. What woman, indeed, among

[053-031]the most faithful adherents of the truth, believes the promises and threats of

[053-032]the Word in the sense in which she believes in her own children, or would not

[053-033]throw her theology to the wind if weighed against their happiness? As soon as

[053-034]they reached the room where the candles were lighted she looked at his face.

[053-035]“O, it is not Angel—not my son—the Angel who went

[053-036]away!” she cried in all the irony of sorrow, as she turned herself aside.

[053-037]His father, too, was shocked to see him, so reduced was that figure from its

[053-038]former contours by worry and the bad season that Clare had experienced, in the

[053-039]climate to which he had so rashly hurried in his first aversion to the mockery

[053-040]of events at home. You could see the skeleton behind the man, and almost the

[053-041]ghost behind the skeleton. He matched Crivelli’s dead *Christus* .

[053-042]His sunken eye-pits were of morbid hue, and the light in his eyes had waned.

[053-043]The angular hollows and lines of his aged ancestors had succeeded to their

[053-044]reign in his face twenty years before their time.

[053-045]“I was ill over there, you know,” he said. “I am all right

[053-046]now.”

[053-047]As if, however, to falsify this assertion, his legs seemed to give way, and he

[053-048]suddenly sat down to save himself from falling. It was only a slight attack of

[053-049]faintness, resulting from the tedious day’s journey, and the excitement

[053-050]of arrival.

[053-051]“Has any letter come for me lately?” he asked. “I received

[053-052]the last you sent on by the merest chance, and after considerable delay through

[053-053]being inland; or I might have come sooner.”

[053-054]“It was from your wife, we supposed?”

[053-055]“It was.”

[053-056]Only one other had recently come. They had not sent it on to him, knowing he

[053-057]would start for home so soon.

[053-058]He hastily opened the letter produced, and was much disturbed to read in

[053-059]Tess’s handwriting the sentiments expressed in her last hurried scrawl to

[053-060]him.

[053-061]O why have you treated me so monstrously, Angel! I do not deserve it. I have

[053-062]thought it all over carefully, and I can never, never forgive you! You know

[053-063]that I did not intend to wrong you—why have you so wronged me? You are

[053-064]cruel, cruel indeed! I will try to forget you. It is all injustice I have

[053-065]received at your hands!

[053-066]T.

[053-067]“It is quite true!” said Angel, throwing down the letter.

[053-068]“Perhaps she will never be reconciled to me!”

[053-069]“Don’t, Angel, be so anxious about a mere child of the soil!”

[053-070]said his mother.

[053-071]“Child of the soil! Well, we all are children of the soil. I wish she

[053-072]were so in the sense you mean; but let me now explain to you what I have never

[053-073]explained before, that her father is a descendant in the male line of one of

[053-074]the oldest Norman houses, like a good many others who lead obscure agricultural

[053-075]lives in our villages, and are dubbed ‘sons of the soil.’”

[053-076]He soon retired to bed; and the next morning, feeling exceedingly unwell, he

[053-077]remained in his room pondering. The circumstances amid which he had left Tess

[053-078]were such that though, while on the south of the Equator and just in receipt of

[053-079]her loving epistle, it had seemed the easiest thing in the world to rush back

[053-080]into her arms the moment he chose to forgive her, now that he had arrived it

[053-081]was not so easy as it had seemed. She was passionate, and her present letter,

[053-082]showing that her estimate of him had changed under his delay—too justly

[053-083]changed, he sadly owned,—made him ask himself if it would be wise to

[053-084]confront her unannounced in the presence of her parents. Supposing that her

[053-085]love had indeed turned to dislike during the last weeks of separation, a sudden

[053-086]meeting might lead to bitter words.

[053-087]Clare therefore thought it would be best to prepare Tess and her family by

[053-088]sending a line to Marlott announcing his return, and his hope that she was

[053-089]still living with them there, as he had arranged for her to do when he left

[053-090]England. He despatched the inquiry that very day, and before the week was out

[053-091]there came a short reply from Mrs Durbeyfield which did not remove his

[053-092]embarrassment, for it bore no address, though to his surprise it was not

[053-093]written from Marlott.

[053-094]Sir,

[053-095]J write these few lines to say that my Daughter is away from me at present,

[053-096]and J am not sure when she will return, but J will let you know as Soon as she

[053-097]do. J do not feel at liberty to tell you Where she is temperly biding. J should

[053-098]say that me and my Family have left Marlott for some Time.—

[053-099]Yours,

[053-100]J. Durbeyfield

[053-101]It was such a relief to Clare to learn that Tess was at least apparently well

[053-102]that her mother’s stiff reticence as to her whereabouts did not long

[053-103]distress him. They were all angry with him, evidently. He would wait till Mrs

[053-104]Durbeyfield could inform him of Tess’s return, which her letter implied

[053-105]to be soon. He deserved no more. His had been a love “which alters when

[053-106]it alteration finds”. He had undergone some strange experiences in his

[053-107]absence; he had seen the virtual Faustina in the literal Cornelia, a spiritual

[053-108]Lucretia in a corporeal Phryne; he had thought of the woman taken and set in

[053-109]the midst as one deserving to be stoned, and of the wife of Uriah being made a

[053-110]queen; and he had asked himself why he had not judged Tess constructively

[053-111]rather than biographically, by the will rather than by the deed?

[053-112]A day or two passed while he waited at his father’s house for the

[053-113]promised second note from Joan Durbeyfield, and indirectly to recover a little

[053-114]more strength. The strength showed signs of coming back, but there was no sign

[053-115]of Joan’s letter. Then he hunted up the old letter sent on to him in

[053-116]Brazil, which Tess had written from Flintcomb-Ash, and re-read it. The

[053-117]sentences touched him now as much as when he had first perused them.

[053-118]...I must cry to you in my trouble—I have no one else!... I think I must

[053-119]die if you do not come soon, or tell me to come to you... please, please, not

[053-120]to be just—only a little kind to me.... If you would come, I could die in

[053-121]your arms! I would be well content to do that if so be you had forgiven me!...

[053-122]if you will send me one little line, and say, *I am coming soon* , I will

[053-123]bide on, Angel—O, so cheerfully!... think how it do hurt my heart not to

[053-124]see you ever—ever! Ah, if I could only make your dear heart ache one

[053-125]little minute of each day as mine does every day and all day long, it might

[053-126]lead you to show pity to your poor lonely one.... I would be content, ay,

[053-127]glad, to live with you as your servant, if I may not as your wife; so that I

[053-128]could only be near you, and get glimpses of you, and think of you as mine....

[053-129]I long for only one thing in heaven or earth or under the earth, to meet you,

[053-130]my own dear! Come to me—come to me, and save me from what threatens me!

[053-131]Clare determined that he would no longer believe in her more recent and severer

[053-132]regard of him, but would go and find her immediately. He asked his father if

[053-133]she had applied for any money during his absence. His father returned a

[053-134]negative, and then for the first time it occurred to Angel that her pride had

[053-135]stood in her way, and that she had suffered privation. From his remarks his

[053-136]parents now gathered the real reason of the separation; and their Christianity

[053-137]was such that, reprobates being their especial care, the tenderness towards

[053-138]Tess which her blood, her simplicity, even her poverty, had not engendered, was

[053-139]instantly excited by her sin.

[053-140]Whilst he was hastily packing together a few articles for his journey he

[053-141]glanced over a poor plain missive also lately come to hand—the one from

[053-142]Marian and Izz Huett, beginning—

[053-143]“Honour’d Sir, Look to your Wife if you do love her as much as she

[053-144]do love you,” and signed, “From Two Well-Wishers.”
