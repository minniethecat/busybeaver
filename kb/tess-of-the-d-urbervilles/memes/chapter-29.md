[029-001]“Now, who mid ye think I’ve heard news o’ this

[029-002]morning?” said Dairyman Crick, as he sat down to breakfast next day, with

[029-003]a riddling gaze round upon the munching men and maids. “Now, just who mid

[029-004]ye think?”

[029-005]One guessed, and another guessed. Mrs Crick did not guess, because she knew

[029-006]already.

[029-007]“Well,” said the dairyman, “’tis that slack-twisted

[029-008]’hore’s-bird of a feller, Jack Dollop. He’s lately got

[029-009]married to a widow-woman.”

[029-010]“Not Jack Dollop? A villain—to think o’ that!” said a

[029-011]milker.

[029-012]The name entered quickly into Tess Durbeyfield’s consciousness, for it

[029-013]was the name of the lover who had wronged his sweetheart, and had afterwards

[029-014]been so roughly used by the young woman’s mother in the butter-churn.

[029-015]“And had he married the valiant matron’s daughter, as he

[029-016]promised?” asked Angel Clare absently, as he turned over the newspaper he

[029-017]was reading at the little table to which he was always banished by Mrs Crick,

[029-018]in her sense of his gentility.

[029-019]“Not he, sir. Never meant to,” replied the dairyman. “As I

[029-020]say, ’tis a widow-woman, and she had money, it seems—fifty

[029-021]poun’ a year or so; and that was all he was after. They were married in a

[029-022]great hurry; and then she told him that by marrying she had lost her fifty

[029-023]poun’ a year. Just fancy the state o’ my gentleman’s mind at

[029-024]that news! Never such a cat-and-dog life as they’ve been leading ever

[029-025]since! Serves him well beright. But onluckily the poor woman gets the worst

[029-026]o’t.”

[029-027]“Well, the silly body should have told en sooner that the ghost of her

[029-028]first man would trouble him,” said Mrs Crick.

[029-029]“Ay, ay,” responded the dairyman indecisively. “Still, you

[029-030]can see exactly how ’twas. She wanted a home, and didn’t like to

[029-031]run the risk of losing him. Don’t ye think that was something like it,

[029-032]maidens?”

[029-033]He glanced towards the row of girls.

[029-034]“She ought to ha’ told him just before they went to church, when he

[029-035]could hardly have backed out,” exclaimed Marian.

[029-036]“Yes, she ought,” agreed Izz.

[029-037]“She must have seen what he was after, and should ha’ refused

[029-038]him,” cried Retty spasmodically.

[029-039]“And what do you say, my dear?” asked the dairyman of Tess.

[029-040]“I think she ought—to have told him the true state of

[029-041]things—or else refused him—I don’t know,” replied Tess,

[029-042]the bread-and-butter choking her.

[029-043]“Be cust if I’d have done either o’t,” said Beck

[029-044]Knibbs, a married helper from one of the cottages. “All’s fair in

[029-045]love and war. I’d ha’ married en just as she did, and if he’d

[029-046]said two words to me about not telling him beforehand anything whatsomdever

[029-047]about my first chap that I hadn’t chose to tell, I’d ha’

[029-048]knocked him down wi’ the rolling-pin—a scram little feller like he!

[029-049]Any woman could do it.”

[029-050]The laughter which followed this sally was supplemented only by a sorry smile,

[029-051]for form’s sake, from Tess. What was comedy to them was tragedy to her;

[029-052]and she could hardly bear their mirth. She soon rose from table, and, with an

[029-053]impression that Clare would soon follow her, went along a little wriggling

[029-054]path, now stepping to one side of the irrigating channels, and now to the

[029-055]other, till she stood by the main stream of the Var. Men had been cutting the

[029-056]water-weeds higher up the river, and masses of them were floating past

[029-057]her—moving islands of green crow-foot, whereon she might almost have

[029-058]ridden; long locks of which weed had lodged against the piles driven to keep

[029-059]the cows from crossing.

[029-060]Yes, there was the pain of it. This question of a woman telling her

[029-061]story—the heaviest of crosses to herself—seemed but amusement to

[029-062]others. It was as if people should laugh at martyrdom.

[029-063]“Tessy!” came from behind her, and Clare sprang across the gully,

[029-064]alighting beside her feet. “My wife—soon!”

[029-065]“No, no; I cannot. For your sake, O Mr Clare; for your sake, I say

[029-066]no!”

[029-067]“Tess!”

[029-068]“Still I say no!” she repeated.

[029-069]Not expecting this, he had put his arm lightly round her waist the moment after

[029-070]speaking, beneath her hanging tail of hair. (The younger dairymaids, including

[029-071]Tess, breakfasted with their hair loose on Sunday mornings before building it

[029-072]up extra high for attending church, a style they could not adopt when milking

[029-073]with their heads against the cows.) If she had said “Yes” instead

[029-074]of “No” he would have kissed her; it had evidently been his

[029-075]intention; but her determined negative deterred his scrupulous heart. Their

[029-076]condition of domiciliary comradeship put her, as the woman, to such

[029-077]disadvantage by its enforced intercourse, that he felt it unfair to her to

[029-078]exercise any pressure of blandishment which he might have honestly employed had

[029-079]she been better able to avoid him. He released her momentarily-imprisoned

[029-080]waist, and withheld the kiss.

[029-081]It all turned on that release. What had given her strength to refuse him this

[029-082]time was solely the tale of the widow told by the dairyman; and that would have

[029-083]been overcome in another moment. But Angel said no more; his face was

[029-084]perplexed; he went away.

[029-085]Day after day they met—somewhat less constantly than before; and thus two

[029-086]or three weeks went by. The end of September drew near, and she could see in

[029-087]his eye that he might ask her again.

[029-088]His plan of procedure was different now—as though he had made up his mind

[029-089]that her negatives were, after all, only coyness and youth startled by the

[029-090]novelty of the proposal. The fitful evasiveness of her manner when the subject

[029-091]was under discussion countenanced the idea. So he played a more coaxing game;

[029-092]and while never going beyond words, or attempting the renewal of caresses, he

[029-093]did his utmost orally.

[029-094]In this way Clare persistently wooed her in undertones like that of the purling

[029-095]milk—at the cow’s side, at skimmings, at butter-makings, at

[029-096]cheese-makings, among broody poultry, and among farrowing pigs—as no

[029-097]milkmaid was ever wooed before by such a man.

[029-098]Tess knew that she must break down. Neither a religious sense of a certain

[029-099]moral validity in the previous union nor a conscientious wish for candour could

[029-100]hold out against it much longer. She loved him so passionately, and he was so

[029-101]godlike in her eyes; and being, though untrained, instinctively refined, her

[029-102]nature cried for his tutelary guidance. And thus, though Tess kept repeating to

[029-103]herself, “I can never be his wife,” the words were vain. A proof of

[029-104]her weakness lay in the very utterance of what calm strength would not have

[029-105]taken the trouble to formulate. Every sound of his voice beginning on the old

[029-106]subject stirred her with a terrifying bliss, and she coveted the recantation

[029-107]she feared.

[029-108]His manner was—what man’s is not?—so much that of one who

[029-109]would love and cherish and defend her under any conditions, changes, charges,

[029-110]or revelations, that her gloom lessened as she basked in it. The season

[029-111]meanwhile was drawing onward to the equinox, and though it was still fine, the

[029-112]days were much shorter. The dairy had again worked by morning candlelight for a

[029-113]long time; and a fresh renewal of Clare’s pleading occurred one morning

[029-114]between three and four.

[029-115]She had run up in her bedgown to his door to call him as usual; then had gone

[029-116]back to dress and call the others; and in ten minutes was walking to the head

[029-117]of the stairs with the candle in her hand. At the same moment he came down his

[029-118]steps from above in his shirt-sleeves and put his arm across the stairway.

[029-119]“Now, Miss Flirt, before you go down,” he said peremptorily.

[029-120]“It is a fortnight since I spoke, and this won’t do any longer. You *must* tell me what you mean, or I shall have to leave this house. My door

[029-121]was ajar just now, and I saw you. For your own safety I must go. You

[029-122]don’t know. Well? Is it to be yes at last?”

[029-123]“I am only just up, Mr Clare, and it is too early to take me to

[029-124]task!” she pouted. “You need not call me Flirt. ’Tis cruel

[029-125]and untrue. Wait till by and by. Please wait till by and by! I will really

[029-126]think seriously about it between now and then. Let me go downstairs!”

[029-127]She looked a little like what he said she was as, holding the candle sideways,

[029-128]she tried to smile away the seriousness of her words.

[029-129]“Call me Angel, then, and not Mr Clare.”

[029-130]“Angel.”

[029-131]“Angel dearest—why not?”

[029-132]“’Twould mean that I agree, wouldn’t it?”

[029-133]“It would only mean that you love me, even if you cannot marry me; and

[029-134]you were so good as to own that long ago.”

[029-135]“Very well, then, ‘Angel dearest’, if I *must* ,”

[029-136]she murmured, looking at her candle, a roguish curl coming upon her mouth,

[029-137]notwithstanding her suspense.

[029-138]Clare had resolved never to kiss her until he had obtained her promise; but

[029-139]somehow, as Tess stood there in her prettily tucked-up milking gown, her hair

[029-140]carelessly heaped upon her head till there should be leisure to arrange it when

[029-141]skimming and milking were done, he broke his resolve, and brought his lips to

[029-142]her cheek for one moment. She passed downstairs very quickly, never looking

[029-143]back at him or saying another word. The other maids were already down, and the

[029-144]subject was not pursued. Except Marian, they all looked wistfully and

[029-145]suspiciously at the pair, in the sad yellow rays which the morning candles

[029-146]emitted in contrast with the first cold signals of the dawn without.

[029-147]When skimming was done—which, as the milk diminished with the approach of

[029-148]autumn, was a lessening process day by day—Retty and the rest went out.

[029-149]The lovers followed them.

[029-150]“Our tremulous lives are so different from theirs, are they not?”

[029-151]he musingly observed to her, as he regarded the three figures tripping before

[029-152]him through the frigid pallor of opening day.

[029-153]“Not so very different, I think,” she said.

[029-154]“Why do you think that?”

[029-155]“There are very few women’s lives that are

[029-156]not—tremulous,” Tess replied, pausing over the new word as if it

[029-157]impressed her. “There’s more in those three than you think.”

[029-158]“What is in them?”

[029-159]“Almost either of ’em,” she began, “would

[029-160]make—perhaps would make—a properer wife than I. And perhaps they

[029-161]love you as well as I—almost.”

[029-162]“O, Tessy!”

[029-163]There were signs that it was an exquisite relief to her to hear the impatient

[029-164]exclamation, though she had resolved so intrepidly to let generosity make one

[029-165]bid against herself. That was now done, and she had not the power to attempt

[029-166]self-immolation a second time then. They were joined by a milker from one of

[029-167]the cottages, and no more was said on that which concerned them so deeply. But

[029-168]Tess knew that this day would decide it.

[029-169]In the afternoon several of the dairyman’s household and assistants went

[029-170]down to the meads as usual, a long way from the dairy, where many of the cows

[029-171]were milked without being driven home. The supply was getting less as the

[029-172]animals advanced in calf, and the supernumerary milkers of the lush green

[029-173]season had been dismissed.

[029-174]The work progressed leisurely. Each pailful was poured into tall cans that

[029-175]stood in a large spring-waggon which had been brought upon the scene; and when

[029-176]they were milked, the cows trailed away. Dairyman Crick, who was there with the

[029-177]rest, his wrapper gleaming miraculously white against a leaden evening sky,

[029-178]suddenly looked at his heavy watch.

[029-179]“Why, ’tis later than I thought,” he said. “Begad! We

[029-180]shan’t be soon enough with this milk at the station, if we don’t

[029-181]mind. There’s no time to-day to take it home and mix it with the bulk

[029-182]afore sending off. It must go to station straight from here. Who’ll drive

[029-183]it across?”

[029-184]Mr Clare volunteered to do so, though it was none of his business, asking Tess

[029-185]to accompany him. The evening, though sunless, had been warm and muggy for the

[029-186]season, and Tess had come out with her milking-hood only, naked-armed and

[029-187]jacketless; certainly not dressed for a drive. She therefore replied by

[029-188]glancing over her scant habiliments; but Clare gently urged her. She assented

[029-189]by relinquishing her pail and stool to the dairyman to take home, and mounted

[029-190]the spring-waggon beside Clare.
