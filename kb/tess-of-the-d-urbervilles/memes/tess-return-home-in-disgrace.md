# Tess's Return Home in Disgrace

## Summary

After her husband [[Angel Clare]] abandons her following the wedding-night confession, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] returns alone to her parents' cottage in [[marlott|Marlott]]. The homecoming is a study in humiliation: the turnpike-keeper unknowingly reports the village gossip about her own wedding (038-009–038-020); her mother [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]] greets her with incredulous fury — "O you little fool—you little fool!" (038-065–038-068); and her father John mourns not her suffering but the blow to his social pretensions. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] discovers her old bed has been given to the younger children: "There was no place here for her now" (038-104). After a few days, she departs again, leaving half of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s money with her mother and fabricating a story that she is joining her husband.

## Context

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] were married only days before at [[Wellbridge]], the former d'Urberville mansion. On their wedding night, after [[angel-clare|Angel]] confessed a past transgression and was forgiven, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] confessed her own history with [[Alec d'Urberville]]. [[angel-clare|Angel]], unable to reconcile his idealized image of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] with the reality of her past, has resolved to separate. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] faces the choice of returning to her parents or wandering alone.

The home she returns to is unchanged in its poverty but transformed in its relation to her. Her father has been "talking about the wedding up at [[rollivers-inn|Rolliver's]] and The Pure Drop every day since, and about his family getting back to their rightful position through you" (038-091–038-093). Her parents have invested their social aspirations in her marriage, and her return as an abandoned wife shatters those fantasies.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] arrives exhausted and heartsick, yet she protects [[angel-clare|Angel]] even in her despair. When her mother berates her for confessing, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s reply reveals her moral core: "He was so good — and I felt the wickedness of trying to blind him as to what had happened! If — if — it were to be done again — I should do the same. I could not — I dared not — so sin — against him!" (038-072–038-075). Even in ruin, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] stands by her ethical choice.

**[[Joan Durbeyfield]]** — [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s reaction is practical rather than compassionate. She treats the catastrophe "as she had taken [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s original trouble, as she would have taken a wet holiday or failure in the potato-crop; as a thing which had come upon them irrespective of desert or folly; a chance external impingement to be borne with; not a lesson" (038-097–038-101). Her pragmatism is both a comfort and a moral vacuum.

**[[John Durbeyfield]]** — John's response exposes the vanity at the heart of the Durbeyfield pretensions. His mortification is not for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] but for himself: "How they'll squint and glane, and say, 'This is yer mighty match is it; this is yer getting back to the true level of yer forefathers in King Norman's time!'" (038-133–038-136). Even his threat of suicide is theatrical self-pity.

## Narrative Impact

This event is one of the novel's most concentrated studies of disgrace and its asymmetrical burden. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] returns to the community that saw her grow up, now bearing the double stigma of her earlier "trouble" and her current abandonment. The scene exposes how female suffering is absorbed into the machinery of family economics and male vanity — her mother sees a wasted investment, her father sees a damaged reputation, but neither sees [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] herself.

The chapter's devastating conclusion — "There was no place here for her now" (038-104) — is both literal (her bed has been reassigned) and existential. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has become homeless in the only home she has known. Her departure after a few days, leaving money and a reassuring lie behind, inaugurates the wandering that will define the remainder of Phase the Fifth.

## Key Passages

The turnpike-keeper's unwitting cruelty, reporting the wedding gossip:

> "John Durbeyfield, too, hev had a daughter married this week to a gentleman-farmer; not from John's own house, you know; they was married elsewhere; the gentleman being of that high standing that John's own folk was not considered well-be-doing enough to have any part in it." (038-010–038-014)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s self-perception as she approaches the cottage:

> Inside that cottage her relations were calmly supposing her far away on a wedding-tour with a comparatively rich man, who was to conduct her to bouncing prosperity; while here she was, friendless, creeping up to the old door quite by herself, with no better place to go to in the world. (038-027–038-030)

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s furious response to the confession:

> "O you little fool—you little fool!" burst out Mrs Durbeyfield, splashing Tess and herself in her agitation. "My good God! that ever I should ha' lived to say it, but I say it again, you little fool!" (038-065–038-068)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s defense of her choice:

> "I know it—I know—I know!" she gasped through her sobs. "But, O my mother, I could not help it! He was so good—and I felt the wickedness of trying to blind him as to what had happened!" (038-071–038-075)

The moment [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] realizes her displacement:

> Tess retreated upstairs and beheld casually that the beds had been shifted, and new arrangements made. Her old bed had been adapted for two younger children. There was no place here for her now. (038-102–038-104)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] overhearing her father doubt the marriage's validity:

> Poor Tess, who had heard as far as this, could not bear to hear more. The perception that her word could be doubted even here, in her own parental house, set her mind against the spot as nothing else could have done. How unexpected were the attacks of destiny! (038-142–038-145)

## Analysis

### The Failed Homecoming

The return to the parental home is a narrative archetype — the prodigal's return, the refuge sought in childhood. Hardy systematically dismantles every consolation the archetype promises. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mother is more concerned with the wasted opportunity than with her daughter's suffering. Her father's first thought is for his damaged reputation at the alehouse. Her bed has been given away. The house itself offers no sanctuary; the only warmth comes from [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s own moral resolve.

### The Lie That Protects

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s behavior in the chapter's final paragraphs is characteristic: she protects others at her own expense. She takes only half of [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s fifty pounds, giving the rest to her mother "as if the wife of a man like [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] could well afford it" (038-155–038-156). She fabricates a story that she is joining her husband, "still further to screen her husband from any imputation of unkindness to her" (038-153–038-154). These small deceptions are acts of dignity — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] refusing to let the world see either her husband's cruelty or her own desperation.

### Joan's Fatalism

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]]'s response to catastrophe — "a thing which had come upon them irrespective of desert or folly... not a lesson" (038-099–038-101) — is a philosophy of passivity that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] both inherits and struggles against. [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s refusal to extract meaning from suffering is a form of survival, but it also perpetuates the cycle. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], by contrast, cannot help but ask why — and her insistence on moral accountability, both for herself and for [[angel-clare|Angel]], is what elevates her tragedy above her mother's mere misfortune.
