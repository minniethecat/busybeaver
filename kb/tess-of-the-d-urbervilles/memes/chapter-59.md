[059-001]The city of Wintoncester, that fine old city, aforetime capital of Wessex, lay

[059-002]amidst its convex and concave downlands in all the brightness and warmth of a

[059-003]July morning. The gabled brick, tile, and freestone houses had almost dried off

[059-004]for the season their integument of lichen, the streams in the meadows were low,

[059-005]and in the sloping High Street, from the West Gateway to the mediæval

[059-006]cross, and from the mediæval cross to the bridge, that leisurely dusting

[059-007]and sweeping was in progress which usually ushers in an old-fashioned

[059-008]market-day.

[059-009]From the western gate aforesaid the highway, as every Wintoncestrian knows,

[059-010]ascends a long and regular incline of the exact length of a measured mile,

[059-011]leaving the houses gradually behind. Up this road from the precincts of the

[059-012]city two persons were walking rapidly, as if unconscious of the trying

[059-013]ascent—unconscious through preoccupation and not through buoyancy. They

[059-014]had emerged upon this road through a narrow, barred wicket in a high wall a

[059-015]little lower down. They seemed anxious to get out of the sight of the houses

[059-016]and of their kind, and this road appeared to offer the quickest means of doing

[059-017]so. Though they were young, they walked with bowed heads, which gait of grief

[059-018]the sun’s rays smiled on pitilessly.

[059-019]One of the pair was Angel Clare, the other a tall budding creature—half

[059-020]girl, half woman—a spiritualized image of Tess, slighter than she, but

[059-021]with the same beautiful eyes—Clare’s sister-in-law, ’Liza-Lu.

[059-022]Their pale faces seemed to have shrunk to half their natural size. They moved

[059-023]on hand in hand, and never spoke a word, the drooping of their heads being that

[059-024]of Giotto’s “Two Apostles”.

[059-025]When they had nearly reached the top of the great West Hill the clocks in the

[059-026]town struck eight. Each gave a start at the notes, and, walking onward yet a

[059-027]few steps, they reached the first milestone, standing whitely on the green

[059-028]margin of the grass, and backed by the down, which here was open to the road.

[059-029]They entered upon the turf, and, impelled by a force that seemed to overrule

[059-030]their will, suddenly stood still, turned, and waited in paralyzed suspense

[059-031]beside the stone.

[059-032]The prospect from this summit was almost unlimited. In the valley beneath lay

[059-033]the city they had just left, its more prominent buildings showing as in an

[059-034]isometric drawing—among them the broad cathedral tower, with its Norman

[059-035]windows and immense length of aisle and nave, the spires of St Thomas’s,

[059-036]the pinnacled tower of the College, and, more to the right, the tower and

[059-037]gables of the ancient hospice, where to this day the pilgrim may receive his

[059-038]dole of bread and ale. Behind the city swept the rotund upland of St

[059-039]Catherine’s Hill; further off, landscape beyond landscape, till the

[059-040]horizon was lost in the radiance of the sun hanging above it.

[059-041]Against these far stretches of country rose, in front of the other city

[059-042]edifices, a large red-brick building, with level gray roofs, and rows of short

[059-043]barred windows bespeaking captivity, the whole contrasting greatly by its

[059-044]formalism with the quaint irregularities of the Gothic erections. It was

[059-045]somewhat disguised from the road in passing it by yews and evergreen oaks, but

[059-046]it was visible enough up here. The wicket from which the pair had lately

[059-047]emerged was in the wall of this structure. From the middle of the building an

[059-048]ugly flat-topped octagonal tower ascended against the east horizon, and viewed

[059-049]from this spot, on its shady side and against the light, it seemed the one blot

[059-050]on the city’s beauty. Yet it was with this blot, and not with the beauty,

[059-051]that the two gazers were concerned.

[059-052]Upon the cornice of the tower a tall staff was fixed. Their eyes were riveted

[059-053]on it. A few minutes after the hour had struck something moved slowly up the

[059-054]staff, and extended itself upon the breeze. It was a black flag.

[059-055]“Justice” was done, and the President of the Immortals, in

[059-056]Æschylean phrase, had ended his sport with Tess. And the d’Urberville

[059-057]knights and dames slept on in their tombs unknowing. The two speechless gazers

[059-058]bent themselves down to the earth, as if in prayer, and remained thus a long

[059-059]time, absolutely motionless: the flag continued to wave silently. As soon as

[059-060]they had strength, they arose, joined hands again, and went on.
