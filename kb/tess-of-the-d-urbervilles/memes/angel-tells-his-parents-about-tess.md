# Angel Tells His Parents About Tess

## Summary

After family prayers at the [[emminster|Emminster]] vicarage, [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] broaches two subjects with his father: his plan to become a farmer in the Colonies, and his desire to marry. When his father suggests [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]], the devout daughter of a neighbouring clergyman, [[angel-clare|Angel]] counters that what a farmer needs is not ecclesiastical accomplishments but practical competence — a wife who can "milk cows, churn good butter, make immense cheeses" (026-029). He then describes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in terms calculated to appeal to his parents' religious sensibilities: "chaste as a vestal" (026-060), a regular churchgoer, "an unimpeachable Christian" (026-077). His parents, though uneasy about her social station as a "cottager's daughter" (026-065), agree not to object to seeing her. The scene reveals both [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s genuine love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and his strategic manipulation of his parents' values.

## Context

[[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] is the youngest son of Reverend [[reverend-clare|James Clare]], an Evangelical clergyman whose rigid theology [[angel-clare|Angel]] has intellectually rejected but whose personal integrity he deeply respects. His two brothers, Cuthbert and Felix, have followed their father into the church and into Cambridge — paths closed to [[angel-clare|Angel]] by his own unconventional choices. The family's middle-class propriety and Evangelical piety form the background against which [[angel-clare|Angel]] must present his unconventional choice of bride.

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s visit to [[emminster|Emminster]] occurs during the idyllic courtship period at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]]. He has not yet proposed to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], but he is preparing the ground. The conversation with his parents reveals his awareness of the social obstacles his marriage will face and his determination — not yet fully tested — to overcome them.

## Key Participants

**[[Angel Clare]]** — At twenty-six, [[angel-clare|Angel]] is the family's prodigal: the son who refused Cambridge, chose practical farming over the church, and holds religious views his parents consider dangerous. His presentation of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to his parents is a study in rhetoric: he emphasizes her piety and practical skills while omitting her social class until pressed, and when confronted, he defiantly declares, "she is a cottager's daughter, as I am proud to say" (026-065).

**[[Reverend James Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s father, a man of genuine Evangelical devotion whose narrow theology is leavened by personal kindness. He has set aside money for [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s future despite the son's theological heterodoxy (026-010). His suggestion of [[mercy-chant|Mercy Chant]] as a suitable wife (026-036) represents the conventional path [[angel-clare|Angel]] is rejecting.

**[[Mrs Clare]]** — [[mrs-clare|Angel's mother]], who enters the conversation "softly" (026-062) but whose questions about family and social standing cut to the heart of the matter. Her observation that "Mercy is accomplished. And accomplishments have their charm" (026-071) articulates the middle-class values against which [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] will be measured.

## Narrative Impact

The [[emminster|Emminster]] interview is a crucial prelude to the marriage plot. It establishes the Clare family's values — Evangelical piety, middle-class respectability, social aspiration — that will later become the invisible jury judging [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s defence of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is passionate and principled, but it is also a performance: he presents [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as more conventionally religious than she really is, and he withholds the truth of her past entirely. This strategic omission foreshadows the disaster of the wedding night, when [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s inability to accept [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s history reveals the limits of his rebellion against his upbringing.

The scene also develops the novel's critique of class. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s insistence that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is "a lady, nevertheless — in feeling and nature" (026-066) is both a genuine tribute and an evasion of the social realities that will ultimately destroy her.

## Key Passages

**[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s Practical Wife**:
> "But ought she not primarily to be able to milk cows, churn good butter, make immense cheeses; know how to sit hens and turkeys and rear chickens, to direct a field of labourers in an emergency, and estimate the value of sheep and calves?" (026-029–026-032)

**The Cottager's Daughter**:
> "She is not what in common parlance is called a lady," said Angel, unflinchingly, "for she is a cottager's daughter, as I am proud to say. But she *is* a lady, nevertheless — in feeling and nature." (026-064–026-066)

**Actualized Poetry**:
> "She's brim full of poetry — actualized poetry, if I may use the expression. She *lives* what paper-poets only write." (026-076)

**Latent Prejudices**:
> He felt that, single-minded and self-sacrificing as his parents were, there yet existed certain latent prejudices of theirs, as middle-class people, which it would require some tact to overcome. (026-096–026-098)

## Analysis

The [[emminster|Emminster]] interview dramatizes the gap between [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s intellectual convictions and his social instincts. He can argue, with genuine conviction, that culture has "affected only the mental epiderm" (026-114) and that the difference between good women of different classes is negligible compared to the difference between good and bad. Yet his presentation of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to his parents relies on exactly the conventional measures — piety, chastity, churchgoing — that his philosophy dismisses.

This inconsistency is not hypocrisy but the novel's diagnosis of how deeply social conditioning penetrates. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is real; his rebellion against his parents' world is sincere. But when that world's values are tested — as they will be on the wedding night — it is the parents' morality, not [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s philosophy, that proves decisive.

The scene also marks [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s first entry, however indirect, into the Clare family's consciousness. She is introduced as an abstraction — "a woman who possessed every qualification to be the helpmate of an agriculturist" (026-054) — and the gap between this idealized image and the real [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], with her history of suffering, is the space in which the tragedy will unfold.
