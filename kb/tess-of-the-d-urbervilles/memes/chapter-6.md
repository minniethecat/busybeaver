[006-001]Tess went down the hill to Trantridge Cross, and inattentively waited to take

[006-002]her seat in the van returning from Chaseborough to Shaston. She did not know

[006-003]what the other occupants said to her as she entered, though she answered them;

[006-004]and when they had started anew she rode along with an inward and not an outward

[006-005]eye.

[006-006]One among her fellow-travellers addressed her more pointedly than any had

[006-007]spoken before: “Why, you be quite a posy! And such roses in early

[006-008]June!”

[006-009]Then she became aware of the spectacle she presented to their surprised vision:

[006-010]roses at her breasts; roses in her hat; roses and strawberries in her basket to

[006-011]the brim. She blushed, and said confusedly that the flowers had been given to

[006-012]her. When the passengers were not looking she stealthily removed the more

[006-013]prominent blooms from her hat and placed them in the basket, where she covered

[006-014]them with her handkerchief. Then she fell to reflecting again, and in looking

[006-015]downwards a thorn of the rose remaining in her breast accidentally pricked her

[006-016]chin. Like all the cottagers in Blackmoor Vale, Tess was steeped in fancies and

[006-017]prefigurative superstitions; she thought this an ill omen—the first she

[006-018]had noticed that day.

[006-019]The van travelled only so far as Shaston, and there were several miles of

[006-020]pedestrian descent from that mountain-town into the vale to Marlott. Her mother

[006-021]had advised her to stay here for the night, at the house of a cottage-woman

[006-022]they knew, if she should feel too tired to come on; and this Tess did, not

[006-023]descending to her home till the following afternoon.

[006-024]When she entered the house she perceived in a moment from her mother’s

[006-025]triumphant manner that something had occurred in the interim.

[006-026]“Oh yes; I know all about it! I told ’ee it would be all right, and

[006-027]now ’tis proved!”

[006-028]“Since I’ve been away? What has?” said Tess rather wearily.

[006-029]Her mother surveyed the girl up and down with arch approval, and went on

[006-030]banteringly: “So you’ve brought ’em round!”

[006-031]“How do you know, mother?”

[006-032]“I’ve had a letter.”

[006-033]Tess then remembered that there would have been time for this.

[006-034]“They say—Mrs d’Urberville says—that she wants you to

[006-035]look after a little fowl-farm which is her hobby. But this is only her artful

[006-036]way of getting ’ee there without raising your hopes. She’s going to

[006-037]own ’ee as kin—that’s the meaning o’t.”

[006-038]“But I didn’t see her.”

[006-039]“You zid somebody, I suppose?”

[006-040]“I saw her son.”

[006-041]“And did he own ’ee?”

[006-042]“Well—he called me Coz.”

[006-043]“An’ I knew it! Jacky—he called her Coz!” cried Joan to

[006-044]her husband. “Well, he spoke to his mother, of course, and she do want

[006-045]’ee there.”

[006-046]“But I don’t know that I am apt at tending fowls,” said the

[006-047]dubious Tess.

[006-048]“Then I don’t know who is apt. You’ve be’n born in the

[006-049]business, and brought up in it. They that be born in a business always know

[006-050]more about it than any ’prentice. Besides, that’s only just a show

[006-051]of something for you to do, that you midn’t feel beholden.”

[006-052]“I don’t altogether think I ought to go,” said Tess

[006-053]thoughtfully. “Who wrote the letter? Will you let me look at it?”

[006-054]“Mrs d’Urberville wrote it. Here it is.”

[006-055]The letter was in the third person, and briefly informed Mrs Durbeyfield that

[006-056]her daughter’s services would be useful to that lady in the management of

[006-057]her poultry-farm, that a comfortable room would be provided for her if she

[006-058]could come, and that the wages would be on a liberal scale if they liked her.

[006-059]“Oh—that’s all!” said Tess.

[006-060]“You couldn’t expect her to throw her arms round ’ee,

[006-061]an’ to kiss and to coll ’ee all at once.”

[006-062]Tess looked out of the window.

[006-063]“I would rather stay here with father and you,” she said.

[006-064]“But why?”

[006-065]“I’d rather not tell you why, mother; indeed, I don’t quite

[006-066]know why.”

[006-067]A week afterwards she came in one evening from an unavailing search for some

[006-068]light occupation in the immediate neighbourhood. Her idea had been to get

[006-069]together sufficient money during the summer to purchase another horse. Hardly

[006-070]had she crossed the threshold before one of the children danced across the

[006-071]room, saying, “The gentleman’s been here!”

[006-072]Her mother hastened to explain, smiles breaking from every inch of her person.

[006-073]Mrs d’Urberville’s son had called on horseback, having been riding

[006-074]by chance in the direction of Marlott. He had wished to know, finally, in the

[006-075]name of his mother, if Tess could really come to manage the old lady’s

[006-076]fowl-farm or not; the lad who had hitherto superintended the birds having

[006-077]proved untrustworthy. “Mr d’Urberville says you must be a good girl

[006-078]if you are at all as you appear; he knows you must be worth your weight in

[006-079]gold. He is very much interested in ’ee—truth to tell.”

[006-080]Tess seemed for the moment really pleased to hear that she had won such high

[006-081]opinion from a stranger when, in her own esteem, she had sunk so low.

[006-082]“It is very good of him to think that,” she murmured; “and if

[006-083]I was quite sure how it would be living there, I would go any-when.”

[006-084]“He is a mighty handsome man!”

[006-085]“I don’t think so,” said Tess coldly.

[006-086]“Well, there’s your chance, whether or no; and I’m sure he

[006-087]wears a beautiful diamond ring!”

[006-088]“Yes,” said little Abraham, brightly, from the window-bench;

[006-089]“and I seed it! and it did twinkle when he put his hand up to his

[006-090]mistarshers. Mother, why did our grand relation keep on putting his hand up to

[006-091]his mistarshers?”

[006-092]“Hark at that child!” cried Mrs Durbeyfield, with parenthetic

[006-093]admiration.

[006-094]“Perhaps to show his diamond ring,” murmured Sir John, dreamily,

[006-095]from his chair.

[006-096]“I’ll think it over,” said Tess, leaving the room.

[006-097]“Well, she’s made a conquest o’ the younger branch of us,

[006-098]straight off,” continued the matron to her husband, “and

[006-099]she’s a fool if she don’t follow it up.”

[006-100]“I don’t quite like my children going away from home,” said

[006-101]the haggler. “As the head of the family, the rest ought to come to

[006-102]me.”

[006-103]“But do let her go, Jacky,” coaxed his poor witless wife.

[006-104]“He’s struck wi’ her—you can see that. He called her

[006-105]Coz! He’ll marry her, most likely, and make a lady of her; and then

[006-106]she’ll be what her forefathers was.”

[006-107]John Durbeyfield had more conceit than energy or health, and this supposition

[006-108]was pleasant to him.

[006-109]“Well, perhaps that’s what young Mr d’Urberville

[006-110]means,” he admitted; “and sure enough he mid have serious thoughts

[006-111]about improving his blood by linking on to the old line. Tess, the little

[006-112]rogue! And have she really paid ’em a visit to such an end as

[006-113]this?”

[006-114]Meanwhile Tess was walking thoughtfully among the gooseberry-bushes in the

[006-115]garden, and over Prince’s grave. When she came in her mother pursued her

[006-116]advantage.

[006-117]“Well, what be you going to do?” she asked.

[006-118]“I wish I had seen Mrs d’Urberville,” said Tess.

[006-119]“I think you mid as well settle it. Then you’ll see her soon

[006-120]enough.”

[006-121]Her father coughed in his chair.

[006-122]“I don’t know what to say!” answered the girl restlessly.

[006-123]“It is for you to decide. I killed the old horse, and I suppose I ought

[006-124]to do something to get ye a new one. But—but—I don’t quite

[006-125]like Mr d’Urberville being there!”

[006-126]The children, who had made use of this idea of Tess being taken up by their

[006-127]wealthy kinsfolk (which they imagined the other family to be) as a species of

[006-128]dolorifuge after the death of the horse, began to cry at Tess’s

[006-129]reluctance, and teased and reproached her for hesitating.

[006-130]“Tess won’t go-o-o and be made a la-a-dy of!—no, she says she

[006-131]wo-o-on’t!” they wailed, with square mouths. “And we

[006-132]shan’t have a nice new horse, and lots o’ golden money to buy

[006-133]fairlings! And Tess won’t look pretty in her best cloze no

[006-134]mo-o-ore!”

[006-135]Her mother chimed in to the same tune: a certain way she had of making her

[006-136]labours in the house seem heavier than they were by prolonging them

[006-137]indefinitely, also weighed in the argument. Her father alone preserved an

[006-138]attitude of neutrality.

[006-139]“I will go,” said Tess at last.

[006-140]Her mother could not repress her consciousness of the nuptial vision conjured

[006-141]up by the girl’s consent.

[006-142]“That’s right! For such a pretty maid as ’tis, this is a fine

[006-143]chance!”

[006-144]Tess smiled crossly.

[006-145]“I hope it is a chance for earning money. It is no other kind of chance.

[006-146]You had better say nothing of that silly sort about parish.”

[006-147]Mrs Durbeyfield did not promise. She was not quite sure that she did not feel

[006-148]proud enough, after the visitor’s remarks, to say a good deal.

[006-149]Thus it was arranged; and the young girl wrote, agreeing to be ready to set out

[006-150]on any day on which she might be required. She was duly informed that Mrs

[006-151]d’Urberville was glad of her decision, and that a spring-cart should be

[006-152]sent to meet her and her luggage at the top of the Vale on the day after the

[006-153]morrow, when she must hold herself prepared to start. Mrs

[006-154]d’Urberville’s handwriting seemed rather masculine.

[006-155]“A cart?” murmured Joan Durbeyfield doubtingly. “It might

[006-156]have been a carriage for her own kin!”

[006-157]Having at last taken her course Tess was less restless and abstracted, going

[006-158]about her business with some self-assurance in the thought of acquiring another

[006-159]horse for her father by an occupation which would not be onerous. She had hoped

[006-160]to be a teacher at the school, but the fates seemed to decide otherwise. Being

[006-161]mentally older than her mother she did not regard Mrs Durbeyfield’s

[006-162]matrimonial hopes for her in a serious aspect for a moment. The light-minded

[006-163]woman had been discovering good matches for her daughter almost from the year

[006-164]of her birth.
