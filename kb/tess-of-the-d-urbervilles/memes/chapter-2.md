[002-001]The village of Marlott lay amid the north-eastern undulations of the beautiful

[002-002]Vale of Blakemore, or Blackmoor, aforesaid, an engirdled and secluded region,

[002-003]for the most part untrodden as yet by tourist or landscape-painter, though

[002-004]within a four hours’ journey from London.

[002-005]It is a vale whose acquaintance is best made by viewing it from the summits of

[002-006]the hills that surround it—except perhaps during the droughts of summer.

[002-007]An unguided ramble into its recesses in bad weather is apt to engender

[002-008]dissatisfaction with its narrow, tortuous, and miry ways.

[002-009]This fertile and sheltered tract of country, in which the fields are never

[002-010]brown and the springs never dry, is bounded on the south by the bold chalk

[002-011]ridge that embraces the prominences of Hambledon Hill, Bulbarrow,

[002-012]Nettlecombe-Tout, Dogbury, High Stoy, and Bubb Down. The traveller from the

[002-013]coast, who, after plodding northward for a score of miles over calcareous downs

[002-014]and corn-lands, suddenly reaches the verge of one of these escarpments, is

[002-015]surprised and delighted to behold, extended like a map beneath him, a country

[002-016]differing absolutely from that which he has passed through. Behind him the

[002-017]hills are open, the sun blazes down upon fields so large as to give an

[002-018]unenclosed character to the landscape, the lanes are white, the hedges low and

[002-019]plashed, the atmosphere colourless. Here, in the valley, the world seems to be

[002-020]constructed upon a smaller and more delicate scale; the fields are mere

[002-021]paddocks, so reduced that from this height their hedgerows appear a network of

[002-022]dark green threads overspreading the paler green of the grass. The atmosphere

[002-023]beneath is languorous, and is so tinged with azure that what artists call the

[002-024]middle distance partakes also of that hue, while the horizon beyond is of the

[002-025]deepest ultramarine. Arable lands are few and limited; with but slight

[002-026]exceptions the prospect is a broad rich mass of grass and trees, mantling minor

[002-027]hills and dales within the major. Such is the Vale of Blackmoor.

[002-028]The district is of historic, no less than of topographical interest. The Vale

[002-029]was known in former times as the Forest of White Hart, from a curious legend of

[002-030]King Henry III’s reign, in which the killing by a certain Thomas de la

[002-031]Lynd of a beautiful white hart which the king had run down and spared, was made

[002-032]the occasion of a heavy fine. In those days, and till comparatively recent

[002-033]times, the country was densely wooded. Even now, traces of its earlier

[002-034]condition are to be found in the old oak copses and irregular belts of timber

[002-035]that yet survive upon its slopes, and the hollow-trunked trees that shade so

[002-036]many of its pastures.

[002-037]The forests have departed, but some old customs of their shades remain. Many,

[002-038]however, linger only in a metamorphosed or disguised form. The May-Day dance,

[002-039]for instance, was to be discerned on the afternoon under notice, in the guise

[002-040]of the club revel, or “club-walking,” as it was there called.

[002-041]It was an interesting event to the younger inhabitants of Marlott, though its

[002-042]real interest was not observed by the participators in the ceremony. Its

[002-043]singularity lay less in the retention of a custom of walking in procession and

[002-044]dancing on each anniversary than in the members being solely women. In

[002-045]men’s clubs such celebrations were, though expiring, less uncommon; but

[002-046]either the natural shyness of the softer sex, or a sarcastic attitude on the

[002-047]part of male relatives, had denuded such women’s clubs as remained (if

[002-048]any other did) of this their glory and consummation. The club of Marlott alone

[002-049]lived to uphold the local Cerealia. It had walked for hundreds of years, if not

[002-050]as benefit-club, as votive sisterhood of some sort; and it walked still.

[002-051]The banded ones were all dressed in white gowns—a gay survival from Old

[002-052]Style days, when cheerfulness and May-time were synonyms—days before the

[002-053]habit of taking long views had reduced emotions to a monotonous average. Their

[002-054]first exhibition of themselves was in a processional march of two and two round

[002-055]the parish. Ideal and real clashed slightly as the sun lit up their figures

[002-056]against the green hedges and creeper-laced house-fronts; for, though the whole

[002-057]troop wore white garments, no two whites were alike among them. Some approached

[002-058]pure blanching; some had a bluish pallor; some worn by the older characters

[002-059](which had possibly lain by folded for many a year) inclined to a cadaverous

[002-060]tint, and to a Georgian style.

[002-061]In addition to the distinction of a white frock, every woman and girl carried

[002-062]in her right hand a peeled willow wand, and in her left a bunch of white

[002-063]flowers. The peeling of the former, and the selection of the latter, had been

[002-064]an operation of personal care.

[002-065]There were a few middle-aged and even elderly women in the train, their

[002-066]silver-wiry hair and wrinkled faces, scourged by time and trouble, having

[002-067]almost a grotesque, certainly a pathetic, appearance in such a jaunty

[002-068]situation. In a true view, perhaps, there was more to be gathered and told of

[002-069]each anxious and experienced one, to whom the years were drawing nigh when she

[002-070]should say, “I have no pleasure in them,” than of her juvenile

[002-071]comrades. But let the elder be passed over here for those under whose bodices

[002-072]the life throbbed quick and warm.

[002-073]The young girls formed, indeed, the majority of the band, and their heads of

[002-074]luxuriant hair reflected in the sunshine every tone of gold, and black, and

[002-075]brown. Some had beautiful eyes, others a beautiful nose, others a beautiful

[002-076]mouth and figure: few, if any, had all. A difficulty of arranging their lips in

[002-077]this crude exposure to public scrutiny, an inability to balance their heads,

[002-078]and to dissociate self-consciousness from their features, was apparent in them,

[002-079]and showed that they were genuine country girls, unaccustomed to many eyes.

[002-080]And as each and all of them were warmed without by the sun, so each had a

[002-081]private little sun for her soul to bask in; some dream, some affection, some

[002-082]hobby, at least some remote and distant hope which, though perhaps starving to

[002-083]nothing, still lived on, as hopes will. They were all cheerful, and many of

[002-084]them merry.

[002-085]They came round by The Pure Drop Inn, and were turning out of the high road to

[002-086]pass through a wicket-gate into the meadows, when one of the women said—

[002-087]“The Load-a-Lord! Why, Tess Durbeyfield, if there isn’t thy father

[002-088]riding hwome in a carriage!”

[002-089]A young member of the band turned her head at the exclamation. She was a fine

[002-090]and handsome girl—not handsomer than some others, possibly—but her

[002-091]mobile peony mouth and large innocent eyes added eloquence to colour and shape.

[002-092]She wore a red ribbon in her hair, and was the only one of the white company

[002-093]who could boast of such a pronounced adornment. As she looked round Durbeyfield

[002-094]was seen moving along the road in a chaise belonging to The Pure Drop, driven

[002-095]by a frizzle-headed brawny damsel with her gown-sleeves rolled above her

[002-096]elbows. This was the cheerful servant of that establishment, who, in her part

[002-097]of factotum, turned groom and ostler at times. Durbeyfield, leaning back, and

[002-098]with his eyes closed luxuriously, was waving his hand above his head, and

[002-099]singing in a slow recitative—

[002-100]“I’ve-got-a-gr’t-family-vault-at-Kingsbere—and

[002-101]knighted-forefathers-in-lead-coffins-there!”

[002-102]The clubbists tittered, except the girl called Tess—in whom a slow heat

[002-103]seemed to rise at the sense that her father was making himself foolish in their

[002-104]eyes.

[002-105]“He’s tired, that’s all,” she said hastily, “and

[002-106]he has got a lift home, because our own horse has to rest to-day.”

[002-107]“Bless thy simplicity, Tess,” said her companions.

[002-108]“He’s got his market-nitch. Haw-haw!”

[002-109]“Look here; I won’t walk another inch with you, if you say any

[002-110]jokes about him!” Tess cried, and the colour upon her cheeks spread over

[002-111]her face and neck. In a moment her eyes grew moist, and her glance drooped to

[002-112]the ground. Perceiving that they had really pained her they said no more, and

[002-113]order again prevailed. Tess’s pride would not allow her to turn her head

[002-114]again, to learn what her father’s meaning was, if he had any; and thus

[002-115]she moved on with the whole body to the enclosure where there was to be dancing

[002-116]on the green. By the time the spot was reached she had recovered her

[002-117]equanimity, and tapped her neighbour with her wand and talked as usual.

[002-118]Tess Durbeyfield at this time of her life was a mere vessel of emotion

[002-119]untinctured by experience. The dialect was on her tongue to some extent,

[002-120]despite the village school: the characteristic intonation of that dialect for

[002-121]this district being the voicing approximately rendered by the syllable UR,

[002-122]probably as rich an utterance as any to be found in human speech. The pouted-up

[002-123]deep red mouth to which this syllable was native had hardly as yet settled into

[002-124]its definite shape, and her lower lip had a way of thrusting the middle of her

[002-125]top one upward, when they closed together after a word.

[002-126]Phases of her childhood lurked in her aspect still. As she walked along to-day,

[002-127]for all her bouncing handsome womanliness, you could sometimes see her twelfth

[002-128]year in her cheeks, or her ninth sparkling from her eyes; and even her fifth

[002-129]would flit over the curves of her mouth now and then.

[002-130]Yet few knew, and still fewer considered this. A small minority, mainly

[002-131]strangers, would look long at her in casually passing by, and grow momentarily

[002-132]fascinated by her freshness, and wonder if they would ever see her again: but

[002-133]to almost everybody she was a fine and picturesque country girl, and no more.

[002-134]Nothing was seen or heard further of Durbeyfield in his triumphal chariot under

[002-135]the conduct of the ostleress, and the club having entered the allotted space,

[002-136]dancing began. As there were no men in the company, the girls danced at first

[002-137]with each other, but when the hour for the close of labour drew on, the

[002-138]masculine inhabitants of the village, together with other idlers and

[002-139]pedestrians, gathered round the spot, and appeared inclined to negotiate for a

[002-140]partner.

[002-141]Among these on-lookers were three young men of a superior class, carrying small

[002-142]knapsacks strapped to their shoulders, and stout sticks in their hands. Their

[002-143]general likeness to each other, and their consecutive ages, would almost have

[002-144]suggested that they might be, what in fact they were, brothers. The eldest wore

[002-145]the white tie, high waistcoat, and thin-brimmed hat of the regulation curate;

[002-146]the second was the normal undergraduate; the appearance of the third and

[002-147]youngest would hardly have been sufficient to characterize him; there was an

[002-148]uncribbed, uncabined aspect in his eyes and attire, implying that he had hardly

[002-149]as yet found the entrance to his professional groove. That he was a desultory

[002-150]tentative student of something and everything might only have been predicted of

[002-151]him.

[002-152]These three brethren told casual acquaintance that they were spending their

[002-153]Whitsun holidays in a walking tour through the Vale of Blackmoor, their course

[002-154]being south-westerly from the town of Shaston on the north-east.

[002-155]They leant over the gate by the highway, and inquired as to the meaning of the

[002-156]dance and the white-frocked maids. The two elder of the brothers were plainly

[002-157]not intending to linger more than a moment, but the spectacle of a bevy of

[002-158]girls dancing without male partners seemed to amuse the third, and make him in

[002-159]no hurry to move on. He unstrapped his knapsack, put it, with his stick, on the

[002-160]hedge-bank, and opened the gate.

[002-161]“What are you going to do, Angel?” asked the eldest.

[002-162]“I am inclined to go and have a fling with them. Why not all of

[002-163]us—just for a minute or two—it will not detain us long?”

[002-164]“No—no; nonsense!” said the first. “Dancing in public

[002-165]with a troop of country hoydens—suppose we should be seen! Come along, or

[002-166]it will be dark before we get to Stourcastle, and there’s no place we can

[002-167]sleep at nearer than that; besides, we must get through another chapter of *A

[002-168]Counterblast to Agnosticism* before we turn in, now I have taken the trouble

[002-169]to bring the book.”

[002-170]“All right—I’ll overtake you and Cuthbert in five minutes;

[002-171]don’t stop; I give my word that I will, Felix.”

[002-172]The two elder reluctantly left him and walked on, taking their brother’s

[002-173]knapsack to relieve him in following, and the youngest entered the field.

[002-174]“This is a thousand pities,” he said gallantly, to two or three of

[002-175]the girls nearest him, as soon as there was a pause in the dance. “Where

[002-176]are your partners, my dears?”

[002-177]“They’ve not left off work yet,” answered one of the boldest.

[002-178]“They’ll be here by and by. Till then, will you be one, sir?”

[002-179]“Certainly. But what’s one among so many!”

[002-180]“Better than none. ’Tis melancholy work facing and footing it to

[002-181]one of your own sort, and no clipsing and colling at all. Now, pick and

[002-182]choose.”

[002-183]“’Ssh—don’t be so for’ard!” said a shyer

[002-184]girl.

[002-185]The young man, thus invited, glanced them over, and attempted some

[002-186]discrimination; but, as the group were all so new to him, he could not very

[002-187]well exercise it. He took almost the first that came to hand, which was not the

[002-188]speaker, as she had expected; nor did it happen to be Tess Durbeyfield.

[002-189]Pedigree, ancestral skeletons, monumental record, the d’Urberville

[002-190]lineaments, did not help Tess in her life’s battle as yet, even to the

[002-191]extent of attracting to her a dancing-partner over the heads of the commonest

[002-192]peasantry. So much for Norman blood unaided by Victorian lucre.

[002-193]The name of the eclipsing girl, whatever it was, has not been handed down; but

[002-194]she was envied by all as the first who enjoyed the luxury of a masculine

[002-195]partner that evening. Yet such was the force of example that the village young

[002-196]men, who had not hastened to enter the gate while no intruder was in the way,

[002-197]now dropped in quickly, and soon the couples became leavened with rustic youth

[002-198]to a marked extent, till at length the plainest woman in the club was no longer

[002-199]compelled to foot it on the masculine side of the figure.

[002-200]The church clock struck, when suddenly the student said that he must

[002-201]leave—he had been forgetting himself—he had to join his companions.

[002-202]As he fell out of the dance his eyes lighted on Tess Durbeyfield, whose own

[002-203]large orbs wore, to tell the truth, the faintest aspect of reproach that he had

[002-204]not chosen her. He, too, was sorry then that, owing to her backwardness, he had

[002-205]not observed her; and with that in his mind he left the pasture.

[002-206]On account of his long delay he started in a flying-run down the lane westward,

[002-207]and had soon passed the hollow and mounted the next rise. He had not yet

[002-208]overtaken his brothers, but he paused to get breath, and looked back. He could

[002-209]see the white figures of the girls in the green enclosure whirling about as

[002-210]they had whirled when he was among them. They seemed to have quite forgotten

[002-211]him already.

[002-212]All of them, except, perhaps, one. This white shape stood apart by the hedge

[002-213]alone. From her position he knew it to be the pretty maiden with whom he had

[002-214]not danced. Trifling as the matter was, he yet instinctively felt that she was

[002-215]hurt by his oversight. He wished that he had asked her; he wished that he had

[002-216]inquired her name. She was so modest, so expressive, she had looked so soft in

[002-217]her thin white gown that he felt he had acted stupidly.

[002-218]However, it could not be helped, and turning, and bending himself to a rapid

[002-219]walk, he dismissed the subject from his mind.
