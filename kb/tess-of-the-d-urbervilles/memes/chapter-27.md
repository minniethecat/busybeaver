[027-001]An up-hill and down-hill ride of twenty-odd miles through a garish mid-day

[027-002]atmosphere brought him in the afternoon to a detached knoll a mile or two west

[027-003]of Talbothays, whence he again looked into that green trough of sappiness and

[027-004]humidity, the valley of the Var or Froom. Immediately he began to descend from

[027-005]the upland to the fat alluvial soil below, the atmosphere grew heavier; the

[027-006]languid perfume of the summer fruits, the mists, the hay, the flowers, formed

[027-007]therein a vast pool of odour which at this hour seemed to make the animals, the

[027-008]very bees and butterflies drowsy. Clare was now so familiar with the spot that

[027-009]he knew the individual cows by their names when, a long distance off, he saw

[027-010]them dotted about the meads. It was with a sense of luxury that he recognized

[027-011]his power of viewing life here from its inner side, in a way that had been

[027-012]quite foreign to him in his student-days; and, much as he loved his parents, he

[027-013]could not help being aware that to come here, as now, after an experience of

[027-014]home-life, affected him like throwing off splints and bandages; even the one

[027-015]customary curb on the humours of English rural societies being absent in this

[027-016]place, Talbothays having no resident landlord.

[027-017]Not a human being was out of doors at the dairy. The denizens were all enjoying

[027-018]the usual afternoon nap of an hour or so which the exceedingly early hours kept

[027-019]in summer-time rendered a necessity. At the door the wood-hooped pails, sodden

[027-020]and bleached by infinite scrubbings, hung like hats on a stand upon the forked

[027-021]and peeled limb of an oak fixed there for that purpose; all of them ready and

[027-022]dry for the evening milking. Angel entered, and went through the silent

[027-023]passages of the house to the back quarters, where he listened for a moment.

[027-024]Sustained snores came from the cart-house, where some of the men were lying

[027-025]down; the grunt and squeal of sweltering pigs arose from the still further

[027-026]distance. The large-leaved rhubarb and cabbage plants slept too, their broad

[027-027]limp surfaces hanging in the sun like half-closed umbrellas.

[027-028]He unbridled and fed his horse, and as he re-entered the house the clock struck

[027-029]three. Three was the afternoon skimming-hour; and, with the stroke, Clare heard

[027-030]the creaking of the floor-boards above, and then the touch of a descending foot

[027-031]on the stairs. It was Tess’s, who in another moment came down before his

[027-032]eyes.

[027-033]She had not heard him enter, and hardly realized his presence there. She was

[027-034]yawning, and he saw the red interior of her mouth as if it had been a

[027-035]snake’s. She had stretched one arm so high above her coiled-up cable of

[027-036]hair that he could see its satin delicacy above the sunburn; her face was

[027-037]flushed with sleep, and her eyelids hung heavy over their pupils. The

[027-038]brim-fulness of her nature breathed from her. It was a moment when a

[027-039]woman’s soul is more incarnate than at any other time; when the most

[027-040]spiritual beauty bespeaks itself flesh; and sex takes the outside place in the

[027-041]presentation.

[027-042]Then those eyes flashed brightly through their filmy heaviness, before the

[027-043]remainder of her face was well awake. With an oddly compounded look of

[027-044]gladness, shyness, and surprise, she exclaimed—“O Mr Clare! How you

[027-045]frightened me—I—”

[027-046]There had not at first been time for her to think of the changed relations

[027-047]which his declaration had introduced; but the full sense of the matter rose up

[027-048]in her face when she encountered Clare’s tender look as he stepped

[027-049]forward to the bottom stair.

[027-050]“Dear, darling Tessy!” he whispered, putting his arm round her, and

[027-051]his face to her flushed cheek. “Don’t, for Heaven’s sake,

[027-052]Mister me any more. I have hastened back so soon because of you!”

[027-053]Tess’s excitable heart beat against his by way of reply; and there they

[027-054]stood upon the red-brick floor of the entry, the sun slanting in by the window

[027-055]upon his back, as he held her tightly to his breast; upon her inclining face,

[027-056]upon the blue veins of her temple, upon her naked arm, and her neck, and into

[027-057]the depths of her hair. Having been lying down in her clothes she was warm as a

[027-058]sunned cat. At first she would not look straight up at him, but her eyes soon

[027-059]lifted, and his plumbed the deepness of the ever-varying pupils, with their

[027-060]radiating fibrils of blue, and black, and gray, and violet, while she regarded

[027-061]him as Eve at her second waking might have regarded Adam.

[027-062]“I’ve got to go a-skimming,” she pleaded, “and I have

[027-063]on’y old Deb to help me to-day. Mrs Crick is gone to market with Mr

[027-064]Crick, and Retty is not well, and the others are gone out somewhere, and

[027-065]won’t be home till milking.”

[027-066]As they retreated to the milk-house Deborah Fyander appeared on the stairs.

[027-067]“I have come back, Deborah,” said Mr Clare, upwards. “So I

[027-068]can help Tess with the skimming; and, as you are very tired, I am sure, you

[027-069]needn’t come down till milking-time.”

[027-070]Possibly the Talbothays milk was not very thoroughly skimmed that afternoon.

[027-071]Tess was in a dream wherein familiar objects appeared as having light and shade

[027-072]and position, but no particular outline. Every time she held the skimmer under

[027-073]the pump to cool it for the work her hand trembled, the ardour of his affection

[027-074]being so palpable that she seemed to flinch under it like a plant in too

[027-075]burning a sun.

[027-076]Then he pressed her again to his side, and when she had done running her

[027-077]forefinger round the leads to cut off the cream-edge, he cleaned it in

[027-078]nature’s way; for the unconstrained manners of Talbothays dairy came

[027-079]convenient now.

[027-080]“I may as well say it now as later, dearest,” he resumed gently.

[027-081]“I wish to ask you something of a very practical nature, which I have

[027-082]been thinking of ever since that day last week in the meads. I shall soon want

[027-083]to marry, and, being a farmer, you see I shall require for my wife a woman who

[027-084]knows all about the management of farms. Will you be that woman, Tessy?”

[027-085]He put it that way that she might not think he had yielded to an impulse of

[027-086]which his head would disapprove.

[027-087]She turned quite careworn. She had bowed to the inevitable result of proximity,

[027-088]the necessity of loving him; but she had not calculated upon this sudden

[027-089]corollary, which, indeed, Clare had put before her without quite meaning

[027-090]himself to do it so soon. With pain that was like the bitterness of dissolution

[027-091]she murmured the words of her indispensable and sworn answer as an honourable

[027-092]woman.

[027-093]“O Mr Clare—I cannot be your wife—I cannot be!”

[027-094]The sound of her own decision seemed to break Tess’s very heart, and she

[027-095]bowed her face in her grief.

[027-096]“But, Tess!” he said, amazed at her reply, and holding her still

[027-097]more greedily close. “Do you say no? Surely you love me?”

[027-098]“O yes, yes! And I would rather be yours than anybody’s in the

[027-099]world,” returned the sweet and honest voice of the distressed girl.

[027-100]“But I *cannot* marry you!”

[027-101]“Tess,” he said, holding her at arm’s length, “you are

[027-102]engaged to marry some one else!”

[027-103]“No, no!”

[027-104]“Then why do you refuse me?”

[027-105]“I don’t want to marry! I have not thought of doing it. I cannot! I

[027-106]only want to love you.”

[027-107]“But why?”

[027-108]Driven to subterfuge, she stammered—

[027-109]“Your father is a parson, and your mother wouldn’ like you to marry

[027-110]such as me. She will want you to marry a lady.”

[027-111]“Nonsense—I have spoken to them both. That was partly why I went

[027-112]home.”

[027-113]“I feel I cannot—never, never!” she echoed.

[027-114]“Is it too sudden to be asked thus, my Pretty?”

[027-115]“Yes—I did not expect it.”

[027-116]“If you will let it pass, please, Tessy, I will give you time,” he

[027-117]said. “It was very abrupt to come home and speak to you all at once.

[027-118]I’ll not allude to it again for a while.”

[027-119]She again took up the shining skimmer, held it beneath the pump, and began

[027-120]anew. But she could not, as at other times, hit the exact under-surface of the

[027-121]cream with the delicate dexterity required, try as she might; sometimes she was

[027-122]cutting down into the milk, sometimes in the air. She could hardly see, her

[027-123]eyes having filled with two blurring tears drawn forth by a grief which, to

[027-124]this her best friend and dear advocate, she could never explain.

[027-125]“I can’t skim—I can’t!” she said, turning away

[027-126]from him.

[027-127]Not to agitate and hinder her longer, the considerate Clare began talking in a

[027-128]more general way:

[027-129]“You quite misapprehend my parents. They are the most simple-mannered

[027-130]people alive, and quite unambitious. They are two of the few remaining

[027-131]Evangelical school. Tessy, are you an Evangelical?”

[027-132]“I don’t know.”

[027-133]“You go to church very regularly, and our parson here is not very High,

[027-134]they tell me.”

[027-135]Tess’s ideas on the views of the parish clergyman, whom she heard every

[027-136]week, seemed to be rather more vague than Clare’s, who had never heard

[027-137]him at all.

[027-138]“I wish I could fix my mind on what I hear there more firmly than I

[027-139]do,” she remarked as a safe generality. “It is often a great sorrow

[027-140]to me.”

[027-141]She spoke so unaffectedly that Angel was sure in his heart that his father

[027-142]could not object to her on religious grounds, even though she did not know

[027-143]whether her principles were High, Low or Broad. He himself knew that, in

[027-144]reality, the confused beliefs which she held, apparently imbibed in childhood,

[027-145]were, if anything, Tractarian as to phraseology, and Pantheistic as to essence.

[027-146]Confused or otherwise, to disturb them was his last desire:

[027-147]Leave thou thy sister, when she prays,

[027-148]Her early Heaven, her happy views;

[027-149]Nor thou with shadow’d hint confuse

[027-150]A life that leads melodious days.

[027-151]He had occasionally thought the counsel less honest than musical; but he gladly

[027-152]conformed to it now.

[027-153]He spoke further of the incidents of his visit, of his father’s mode of

[027-154]life, of his zeal for his principles; she grew serener, and the undulations

[027-155]disappeared from her skimming; as she finished one lead after another he

[027-156]followed her, and drew the plugs for letting down the milk.

[027-157]“I fancied you looked a little downcast when you came in,” she

[027-158]ventured to observe, anxious to keep away from the subject of herself.

[027-159]“Yes—well, my father had been talking a good deal to me of his

[027-160]troubles and difficulties, and the subject always tends to depress me. He is so

[027-161]zealous that he gets many snubs and buffetings from people of a different way

[027-162]of thinking from himself, and I don’t like to hear of such humiliations

[027-163]to a man of his age, the more particularly as I don’t think earnestness

[027-164]does any good when carried so far. He has been telling me of a very unpleasant

[027-165]scene in which he took part quite recently. He went as the deputy of some

[027-166]missionary society to preach in the neighbourhood of Trantridge, a place forty

[027-167]miles from here, and made it his business to expostulate with a lax young cynic

[027-168]he met with somewhere about there—son of some landowner up that

[027-169]way—and who has a mother afflicted with blindness. My father addressed

[027-170]himself to the gentleman point-blank, and there was quite a disturbance. It was

[027-171]very foolish of my father, I must say, to intrude his conversation upon a

[027-172]stranger when the probabilities were so obvious that it would be useless. But

[027-173]whatever he thinks to be his duty, that he’ll do, in season or out of

[027-174]season; and, of course, he makes many enemies, not only among the absolutely

[027-175]vicious, but among the easy-going, who hate being bothered. He says he glories

[027-176]in what happened, and that good may be done indirectly; but I wish he would not

[027-177]wear himself out now he is getting old, and would leave such pigs to their

[027-178]wallowing.”

[027-179]Tess’s look had grown hard and worn, and her ripe mouth tragical; but she

[027-180]no longer showed any tremulousness. Clare’s revived thoughts of his

[027-181]father prevented his noticing her particularly; and so they went on down the

[027-182]white row of liquid rectangles till they had finished and drained them off,

[027-183]when the other maids returned, and took their pails, and Deb came to scald out

[027-184]the leads for the new milk. As Tess withdrew to go afield to the cows he said

[027-185]to her softly—

[027-186]“And my question, Tessy?”

[027-187]“O no—no!” replied she with grave hopelessness, as one who

[027-188]had heard anew the turmoil of her own past in the allusion to Alec

[027-189]d’Urberville. “It *can’t* be!”

[027-190]She went out towards the mead, joining the other milkmaids with a bound, as if

[027-191]trying to make the open air drive away her sad constraint. All the girls drew

[027-192]onward to the spot where the cows were grazing in the farther mead, the bevy

[027-193]advancing with the bold grace of wild animals—the reckless, unchastened

[027-194]motion of women accustomed to unlimited space—in which they abandoned

[027-195]themselves to the air as a swimmer to the wave. It seemed natural enough to him

[027-196]now that Tess was again in sight to choose a mate from unconstrained Nature,

[027-197]and not from the abodes of Art.
