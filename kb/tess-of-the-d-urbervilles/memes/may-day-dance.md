# The May-Day Dance

## Summary

On a May afternoon in the [[vale-of-blackmoor|Vale of Blackmoor]], the women of [[marlott|Marlott]] observe their ancient club-walking festival — a pagan survival in which the village maidens, all dressed in white, process through the lanes and then dance on the village green. Among them is [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]], distinguished by the red ribbon in her hair — the only spot of colour in the white company. Three young brothers pass through on a walking tour: [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] and his two elder siblings. [[angel-clare|Angel]] joins the dance briefly, partnering with several girls, but he does not dance with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who stands apart. Their eyes meet, and he regrets the missed connection, but the moment passes.

## Context

The club-walking is described as a survival of the ancient Cerealia — the Roman festival of Ceres, goddess of agriculture — preserved in rural Wessex for "hundreds of years, if not as benefit-club, as votive sisterhood of some sort" (002-050). By the Victorian era, such women's clubs had nearly vanished, denuded of male participants. [[marlott|Marlott]]'s club alone "lived to uphold the local Cerealia" (002-049).

The scene occurs before the novel's central plot begins. [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] has just learned of his d'Urberville ancestry in the preceding pages, and the brass band heard in the distance (001-154) connects the two opening strands: the Durbeyfield family pride and the village festival are both relics of a vanishing rural past.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] appears for the first time among the white-clad dancers. She is "a fine and handsome girl — not handsomer than some others, possibly" but distinguished by "the red ribbon in her hair," the only one of the white company so adorned (002-092). When a frizzle-headed damsel makes a crude joke about her father's new title, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] flushes with embarrassment (002-102). She dances with the other girls until [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s arrival, then stands apart, wounded by his failure to choose her.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s first appearance in the novel. He is the youngest of three brothers, walking through the [[vale-of-blackmoor|Vale of Blackmoor]] during the Whitsun holidays. He is distinguished from his brothers by his willingness to join the village dance — an impulse toward the rural and the spontaneous that will later define his romance with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. But he dances with the "plainest woman in the club" and several others, never with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], and leaves without speaking to her. His regret — "he felt he had acted stupidly" (002-217) — is the novel's first note of missed opportunity.

## Narrative Impact

The May-Day Dance is a structural prologue to the entire novel. Its significance operates through four key narrative functions:

**1. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s First Appearance.** The opening description of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] establishes her physical beauty, her rural innocence, and her difference from her peers — the red ribbon, her embarrassment at her father's pretensions, her sensitivity. Every subsequent development in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s character is foreshadowed in this first glimpse.

**2. The First Missed Connection.** [[angel-clare|Angel]] passes through and does not dance with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]. He even notices her — "he knew it to be the pretty maiden with whom he had not danced" (002-213) — but the moment passes. This miniature failure of connection prefigures the larger failure that will destroy their marriage: [[angel-clare|Angel]] sees [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], desires her, and walks away.

**3. Pastoral Elegy.** The scene establishes the novel's elegiac mode. The club-walking is presented as a dying custom, a "gay survival from Old Style days" (002-051). Hardy positions the entire narrative as a lament for a vanishing rural world, and the May-Day Dance is its emblem.

**4. The Symbolism of White.** All the women wear white, but "no two whites were alike among them" (002-057) — a detail that encapsulates the novel's vision of virtue as varied and individual rather than absolute. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the only one with a red ribbon, marking her for both distinction and danger.

## Key Passages

**The White Company**:
> "The banded ones were all dressed in white gowns — a gay survival from Old Style days, when cheerfulness and May-time were synonyms — days before the habit of taking long views had reduced emotions to a monotonous average." (002-051)

This passage encapsulates Hardy's pastoral nostalgia and his critique of Victorian emotional repression. The "long views" of utilitarianism and evangelical morality have "reduced emotions to a monotonous average" — a diagnosis that applies directly to [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s later failure of feeling.

**[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s First Description**:
> "She was a fine and handsome girl — not handsomer than some others, possibly — but her mobile peony mouth and large innocent eyes added eloquence to colour and shape. She wore a red ribbon in her hair, and was the only one of the white company who could boast of such a pronounced adornment." (002-089–002-092)

**The Missed Meeting**:
> "As he fell out of the dance his eyes lighted on Tess Durbeyfield, whose own large orbs wore, to tell the truth, the faintest aspect of reproach that he had not chosen her. He, too, was sorry then that, owing to her backwardness, he had not observed her... he wished that he had asked her; he wished that he had inquired her name. She was so modest, so expressive, she had looked so soft in her thin white gown that he felt he had acted stupidly." (002-202–002-217)

**The Vanishing Figure**:
> "All of them, except, perhaps, one. This white shape stood apart by the hedge alone. From her position he knew it to be the pretty maiden with whom he had not danced." (002-212–002-213)

## Analysis

### The Pagan Survival

Hardy explicitly frames the club-walking as a pre-Christian ritual preserved into the Victorian present. The reference to Cerealia — the Roman festival honouring Ceres — connects [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] to classical mythology and the cycles of nature. Throughout the novel, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is associated with pagan and natural imagery (the [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] garden, the [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] altar) rather than Christian morality. The May-Day Dance is her introduction to this symbolic register.

### The Red Ribbon

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s red ribbon is a brilliant piece of visual symbolism. In a field of white — the colour of purity and maidenhood — the red ribbon marks her as vivid, passionate, and marked for tragedy. The colour red recurs throughout the novel: Alec's blood on the ceiling, the "scarlet oozing stain" of Prince's wound, the red interior of the d'Urberville coach. The ribbon in the first scene is the novel's first chromatic warning.

### The Dance as Microcosm

The dance enacts in miniature the entire structure of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s relationship with [[angel-clare|Angel]]: she is present, he notices her, he chooses others, he regrets, he leaves. All of this happens in a few pages, and the rest of the novel — nearly five hundred pages — is the working-out of what this first scene compressed. [[angel-clare|Angel]] will spend the rest of the novel leaving [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], returning, and leaving again.

### Missed Connections

The May-Day Dance establishes the novel's tragic rhythm: opportunities glimpsed and lost. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] exist in the same space, are aware of each other, and fail to connect. The moment is small — "trifling as the matter was" (002-214) — but the novel's philosophy of fate insists that small moments are the material of destiny.
