[058-001]The night was strangely solemn and still. In the small hours she whispered to

[058-002]him the whole story of how he had walked in his sleep with her in his arms

[058-003]across the Froom stream, at the imminent risk of both their lives, and laid her

[058-004]down in the stone coffin at the ruined abbey. He had never known of that till

[058-005]now.

[058-006]“Why didn’t you tell me next day?” he said. “It might

[058-007]have prevented much misunderstanding and woe.”

[058-008]“Don’t think of what’s past!” said she. “I am not

[058-009]going to think outside of now. Why should we! Who knows what to-morrow has in

[058-010]store?”

[058-011]But it apparently had no sorrow. The morning was wet and foggy, and Clare,

[058-012]rightly informed that the caretaker only opened the windows on fine days,

[058-013]ventured to creep out of their chamber and explore the house, leaving Tess

[058-014]asleep. There was no food on the premises, but there was water, and he took

[058-015]advantage of the fog to emerge from the mansion and fetch tea, bread, and

[058-016]butter from a shop in a little place two miles beyond, as also a small tin

[058-017]kettle and spirit-lamp, that they might get fire without smoke. His re-entry

[058-018]awoke her; and they breakfasted on what he had brought.

[058-019]They were indisposed to stir abroad, and the day passed, and the night

[058-020]following, and the next, and next; till, almost without their being aware, five

[058-021]days had slipped by in absolute seclusion, not a sight or sound of a human

[058-022]being disturbing their peacefulness, such as it was. The changes of the weather

[058-023]were their only events, the birds of the New Forest their only company. By

[058-024]tacit consent they hardly once spoke of any incident of the past subsequent to

[058-025]their wedding-day. The gloomy intervening time seemed to sink into chaos, over

[058-026]which the present and prior times closed as if it never had been. Whenever he

[058-027]suggested that they should leave their shelter, and go forwards towards

[058-028]Southampton or London, she showed a strange unwillingness to move.

[058-029]“Why should we put an end to all that’s sweet and lovely!”

[058-030]she deprecated. “What must come will come.” And, looking through

[058-031]the shutter-chink: “All is trouble outside there; inside here

[058-032]content.”

[058-033]He peeped out also. It was quite true; within was affection, union, error

[058-034]forgiven: outside was the inexorable.

[058-035]“And—and,” she said, pressing her cheek against his, “I

[058-036]fear that what you think of me now may not last. I do not wish to outlive your

[058-037]present feeling for me. I would rather not. I would rather be dead and buried

[058-038]when the time comes for you to despise me, so that it may never be known to me

[058-039]that you despised me.”

[058-040]“I cannot ever despise you.”

[058-041]“I also hope that. But considering what my life has been, I cannot see

[058-042]why any man should, sooner or later, be able to help despising me.... How

[058-043]wickedly mad I was! Yet formerly I never could bear to hurt a fly or a worm,

[058-044]and the sight of a bird in a cage used often to make me cry.”

[058-046]result was that the old caretaker at the cottage awoke early. The brilliant

[058-047]sunrise made her unusually brisk; she decided to open the contiguous mansion

[058-048]immediately, and to air it thoroughly on such a day. Thus it occurred that,

[058-049]having arrived and opened the lower rooms before six o’clock, she

[058-050]ascended to the bedchambers, and was about to turn the handle of the one

[058-051]wherein they lay. At that moment she fancied she could hear the breathing of

[058-052]persons within. Her slippers and her antiquity had rendered her progress a

[058-053]noiseless one so far, and she made for instant retreat; then, deeming that her

[058-054]hearing might have deceived her, she turned anew to the door and softly tried

[058-055]the handle. The lock was out of order, but a piece of furniture had been moved

[058-056]forward on the inside, which prevented her opening the door more than an inch

[058-057]or two. A stream of morning light through the shutter-chink fell upon the faces

[058-063]at the effrontery of tramps and vagabonds gave way to a momentary

[058-065]and withdrew as softly as she had come, to go and consult with her neighbours

[058-066]on the odd discovery.

[058-067]Not more than a minute had elapsed after her withdrawal when Tess woke, and

[058-068]then Clare. Both had a sense that something had disturbed them, though they

[058-069]could not say what; and the uneasy feeling which it engendered grew stronger.

[058-070]As soon as he was dressed he narrowly scanned the lawn through the two or three

[058-071]inches of shutter-chink.

[058-072]“I think we will leave at once,” said he. “It is a fine day.

[058-073]And I cannot help fancying somebody is about the house. At any rate, the woman

[058-074]will be sure to come to-day.”

[058-075]She passively assented, and putting the room in order, they took up the few

[058-076]articles that belonged to them, and departed noiselessly. When they had got

[058-078]“Ah, happy house—goodbye!” she said. “My life can only

[058-079]be a question of a few weeks. Why should we not have stayed there?”

[058-080]“Don’t say it, Tess! We shall soon get out of this district

[058-081]altogether. We’ll continue our course as we’ve begun it, and keep

[058-082]straight north. Nobody will think of looking for us there. We shall be looked

[058-084]will get to a port and away.”

[058-085]Having thus persuaded her, the plan was pursued, and they kept a bee-line

[058-086]northward. Their long repose at the manor-house lent them walking power now;

[058-092]To walk across country without much regard to roads was not new to Tess, and

[058-093]she showed her old agility in the performance. The intercepting city, ancient

[058-097]lamps, keeping off the pavement that it might not echo their footsteps. The

[058-102]moon had hitherto helped them a little. But the moon had now sunk, the clouds

[058-103]seemed to settle almost on their heads, and the night grew as dark as a cave.

[058-104]However, they found their way along, keeping as much on the turf as possible

[058-108]They had proceeded thus gropingly two or three miles further when on a sudden

[058-109]Clare became conscious of some vast erection close in his front, rising sheer

[058-111]“What monstrous place is this?” said Angel.

[058-112]“It hums,” said she. “Hearken!”

[058-117]Carrying his fingers onward he found that what he had come in contact with was

[058-118]a colossal rectangular pillar; by stretching out his left hand he could feel a

[058-119]similar one adjoining. At an indefinite height overhead something made the

[058-120]black sky blacker, which had the semblance of a vast architrave uniting the

[058-123]was roofless. Tess drew her breath fearfully, and Angel, perplexed, said—

[058-124]“What can it be?”

[058-125]Feeling sideways they encountered another tower-like pillar, square and

[058-126]uncompromising as the first; beyond it another and another. The place was all

[058-127]doors and pillars, some connected above by continuous architraves.

[058-128]“A very Temple of the Winds,” he said.

[058-134]“It is Stonehenge!” said Clare.

[058-135]“The heathen temple, you mean?”

[058-136]“Yes. Older than the centuries; older than the d’Urbervilles! Well,

[058-145]“I fear not. This spot is visible for miles by day, although it does not

[058-151]“Sleepy are you, dear? I think you are lying on an altar.”

[058-152]“I like very much to be here,” she murmured. “It is so solemn

[058-161]“I will.”

[058-162]“She is so good and simple and pure. O, Angel—I wish you would
