# Mrs d'Urberville

## Character Overview

Mrs Stoke-d'Urberville is the blind, white-haired mistress of [[the-slopes|The Slopes]], the country estate near [[trantridge|Trantridge]] where [[Tess Durbeyfield]] briefly works as a poultry-keeper. She is the widowed mother of [[Alec d'Urberville]], and her relationship with her son — part resentful, part fond — provides an early glimpse into the moral atmosphere of the household Tess is entering.

## The Mistress of The Slopes

Tess first encounters Mrs d'Urberville when summoned to the manor house to present the fowls for the old lady's inspection. The house itself, once a fine manorial residence, has been "indifferently turned into a fowl-house by Mrs Stoke-d'Urberville as soon as the property fell into hand" (009-006), a detail that signals both the family's reduced circumstances and the old lady's eccentric devotion to her birds.

Mrs d'Urberville is a "white-haired woman of not more than sixty, or even less, wearing a large cap" (009-008). Hardy notes the particular quality of her face — "the mobile face frequent in those whose sight has decayed by stages, has been laboriously striven after, and reluctantly let go" (009-008) — distinguishing her from those born blind. Her gradual loss of sight and the effort with which she once struggled to retain it suggest a life of will and attachment.

## The Fowl Ceremony

The daily ritual of presenting the birds to Mrs d'Urberville is rendered as an almost ecclesiastical ceremony. Hardy writes: "It reminded Tess of a Confirmation, in which Mrs d'Urberville was the bishop, the fowls the young people presented, and herself and the maid-servant the parson and curate of the parish" (009-014). The blind woman's touch is extraordinarily perceptive; she can recognize each bird by feel, "examining their beaks, their combs, the manes of the cocks, their wings, and their claws" (009-010), and know at once if a single feather were "crippled or draggled" (009-010).

This sensory acuity, developed in compensation for her lost sight, marks Mrs d'Urberville as a figure of paradoxical insight — she sees through touch what others cannot, yet remains blind to the true nature of her son's designs upon Tess.

## Relationship with Alec

The emotional dynamic between mother and son is one of the novel's revealing early details. When the maid [[Elizabeth]] mentions that Alec had whistled to the bullfinches that morning, the old lady's face "creased into furrows of repugnance, and she made no further reply" (009-018). Yet Hardy immediately undercuts any simple reading of this reaction: "Mrs d'Urberville was not the first mother compelled to love her offspring resentfully, and to be bitterly fond" (009-019).

This compressed psychological portrait of "bitter fondness" illuminates Alec's own character. His mother's resentment suggests a son who has disappointed, who takes liberties, who whistles to her birds in her absence — small transgressions that gesture toward larger ones.

## Significance

Mrs d'Urberville functions as a threshold figure in Tess's story. Her blindness literalizes the moral blindness of the d'Urberville household — she cannot see Tess, cannot see Alec's intentions, cannot see the danger. Yet Hardy's treatment of her is characteristically complex: she is neither villain nor fool, but a woman trapped in her own dependency, "compelled to love her offspring resentfully" (009-019). Her brief appearance at the novel's outset establishes the compromised domestic world into which Tess is being drawn under the guise of claiming kinship.
