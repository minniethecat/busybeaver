[018-001]Angel Clare rises out of the past not altogether as a distinct figure, but as

[018-002]an appreciative voice, a long regard of fixed, abstracted eyes, and a mobility

[018-003]of mouth somewhat too small and delicately lined for a man’s, though with

[018-004]an unexpectedly firm close of the lower lip now and then; enough to do away

[018-005]with any inference of indecision. Nevertheless, something nebulous,

[018-006]preoccupied, vague, in his bearing and regard, marked him as one who probably

[018-007]had no very definite aim or concern about his material future. Yet as a lad

[018-008]people had said of him that he was one who might do anything if he tried.

[018-009]He was the youngest son of his father, a poor parson at the other end of the

[018-010]county, and had arrived at Talbothays Dairy as a six months’ pupil, after

[018-011]going the round of some other farms, his object being to acquire a practical

[018-012]skill in the various processes of farming, with a view either to the Colonies

[018-013]or the tenure of a home-farm, as circumstances might decide.

[018-014]His entry into the ranks of the agriculturists and breeders was a step in the

[018-015]young man’s career which had been anticipated neither by himself nor by

[018-016]others.

[018-017]Mr Clare the elder, whose first wife had died and left him a daughter, married

[018-018]a second late in life. This lady had somewhat unexpectedly brought him three

[018-019]sons, so that between Angel, the youngest, and his father the Vicar there

[018-020]seemed to be almost a missing generation. Of these boys the aforesaid Angel,

[018-021]the child of his old age, was the only son who had not taken a University

[018-022]degree, though he was the single one of them whose early promise might have

[018-023]done full justice to an academical training.

[018-024]Some two or three years before Angel’s appearance at the Marlott dance,

[018-025]on a day when he had left school and was pursuing his studies at home, a parcel

[018-026]came to the Vicarage from the local bookseller’s, directed to the

[018-027]Reverend James Clare. The Vicar having opened it and found it to contain a

[018-028]book, read a few pages; whereupon he jumped up from his seat and went straight

[018-029]to the shop with the book under his arm.

[018-030]“Why has this been sent to my house?” he asked peremptorily,

[018-031]holding up the volume.

[018-032]“It was ordered, sir.”

[018-033]“Not by me, or any one belonging to me, I am happy to say.”

[018-034]The shopkeeper looked into his order-book.

[018-035]“Oh, it has been misdirected, sir,” he said. “It was ordered

[018-036]by Mr Angel Clare, and should have been sent to him.”

[018-037]Mr Clare winced as if he had been struck. He went home pale and dejected, and

[018-038]called Angel into his study.

[018-039]“Look into this book, my boy,” he said. “What do you know

[018-040]about it?”

[018-041]“I ordered it,” said Angel simply.

[018-042]“What for?”

[018-043]“To read.”

[018-044]“How can you think of reading it?”

[018-045]“How can I? Why—it is a system of philosophy. There is no more

[018-046]moral, or even religious, work published.”

[018-047]“Yes—moral enough; I don’t deny that. But

[018-048]religious!—and for *you* , who intend to be a minister of the

[018-049]Gospel!”

[018-050]“Since you have alluded to the matter, father,” said the son, with

[018-051]anxious thought upon his face, “I should like to say, once for all, that

[018-052]I should prefer not to take Orders. I fear I could not conscientiously do so. I

[018-053]love the Church as one loves a parent. I shall always have the warmest

[018-054]affection for her. There is no institution for whose history I have a deeper

[018-055]admiration; but I cannot honestly be ordained her minister, as my brothers are,

[018-056]while she refuses to liberate her mind from an untenable redemptive

[018-057]theolatry.”

[018-058]It had never occurred to the straightforward and simple-minded Vicar that one

[018-059]of his own flesh and blood could come to this! He was stultified, shocked,

[018-060]paralysed. And if Angel were not going to enter the Church, what was the use of

[018-061]sending him to Cambridge? The University as a step to anything but ordination

[018-062]seemed, to this man of fixed ideas, a preface without a volume. He was a man

[018-063]not merely religious, but devout; a firm believer—not as the phrase is

[018-064]now elusively construed by theological thimble-riggers in the Church and out of

[018-065]it, but in the old and ardent sense of the Evangelical school: one who could

[018-066]Indeed opine

[018-067]That the Eternal and Divine

[018-068]Did, eighteen centuries ago

[018-069]In very truth...

[018-070]Angel’s father tried argument, persuasion, entreaty.

[018-071]“No, father; I cannot underwrite Article Four (leave alone the rest),

[018-072]taking it ‘in the literal and grammatical sense’ as required by the

[018-073]Declaration; and, therefore, I can’t be a parson in the present state of

[018-074]affairs,” said Angel. “My whole instinct in matters of religion is

[018-075]towards reconstruction; to quote your favorite Epistle to the Hebrews,

[018-076]‘the removing of those things that are shaken, as of things that are

[018-077]made, that those things which cannot be shaken may remain.’”

[018-078]His father grieved so deeply that it made Angel quite ill to see him.

[018-079]“What is the good of your mother and me economizing and stinting

[018-080]ourselves to give you a University education, if it is not to be used for the

[018-081]honour and glory of God?” his father repeated.

[018-082]“Why, that it may be used for the honour and glory of man, father.”

[018-083]Perhaps if Angel had persevered he might have gone to Cambridge like his

[018-084]brothers. But the Vicar’s view of that seat of learning as a

[018-085]stepping-stone to Orders alone was quite a family tradition; and so rooted was

[018-086]the idea in his mind that perseverance began to appear to the sensitive son

[018-087]akin to an intent to misappropriate a trust, and wrong the pious heads of the

[018-088]household, who had been and were, as his father had hinted, compelled to

[018-089]exercise much thrift to carry out this uniform plan of education for the three

[018-090]young men.

[018-091]“I will do without Cambridge,” said Angel at last. “I feel

[018-092]that I have no right to go there in the circumstances.”

[018-093]The effects of this decisive debate were not long in showing themselves. He

[018-094]spent years and years in desultory studies, undertakings, and meditations; he

[018-095]began to evince considerable indifference to social forms and observances. The

[018-096]material distinctions of rank and wealth he increasingly despised. Even the

[018-097]“good old family” (to use a favourite phrase of a late local

[018-098]worthy) had no aroma for him unless there were good new resolutions in its

[018-099]representatives. As a balance to these austerities, when he went to live in

[018-100]London to see what the world was like, and with a view to practising a

[018-101]profession or business there, he was carried off his head, and nearly entrapped

[018-102]by a woman much older than himself, though luckily he escaped not greatly the

[018-103]worse for the experience.

[018-104]Early association with country solitudes had bred in him an unconquerable, and

[018-105]almost unreasonable, aversion to modern town life, and shut him out from such

[018-106]success as he might have aspired to by following a mundane calling in the

[018-107]impracticability of the spiritual one. But something had to be done; he had

[018-108]wasted many valuable years; and having an acquaintance who was starting on a

[018-109]thriving life as a Colonial farmer, it occurred to Angel that this might be a

[018-110]lead in the right direction. Farming, either in the Colonies, America, or at

[018-111]home—farming, at any rate, after becoming well qualified for the business

[018-112]by a careful apprenticeship—that was a vocation which would probably

[018-113]afford an independence without the sacrifice of what he valued even more than a

[018-114]competency—intellectual liberty.

[018-115]So we find Angel Clare at six-and-twenty here at Talbothays as a student of

[018-116]kine, and, as there were no houses near at hand in which he could get a

[018-117]comfortable lodging, a boarder at the dairyman’s.

[018-118]His room was an immense attic which ran the whole length of the dairy-house. It

[018-119]could only be reached by a ladder from the cheese-loft, and had been closed up

[018-120]for a long time till he arrived and selected it as his retreat. Here Clare had

[018-121]plenty of space, and could often be heard by the dairy-folk pacing up and down

[018-122]when the household had gone to rest. A portion was divided off at one end by a

[018-123]curtain, behind which was his bed, the outer part being furnished as a homely

[018-124]sitting-room.

[018-125]At first he lived up above entirely, reading a good deal, and strumming upon an

[018-126]old harp which he had bought at a sale, saying when in a bitter humour that he

[018-127]might have to get his living by it in the streets some day. But he soon

[018-128]preferred to read human nature by taking his meals downstairs in the general

[018-129]dining-kitchen, with the dairyman and his wife, and the maids and men, who all

[018-130]together formed a lively assembly; for though but few milking hands slept in

[018-131]the house, several joined the family at meals. The longer Clare resided here

[018-132]the less objection had he to his company, and the more did he like to share

[018-133]quarters with them in common.

[018-134]Much to his surprise he took, indeed, a real delight in their companionship.

[018-135]The conventional farm-folk of his imagination—personified in the

[018-136]newspaper-press by the pitiable dummy known as Hodge—were obliterated

[018-137]after a few days’ residence. At close quarters no Hodge was to be seen.

[018-138]At first, it is true, when Clare’s intelligence was fresh from a

[018-139]contrasting society, these friends with whom he now hobnobbed seemed a little

[018-140]strange. Sitting down as a level member of the dairyman’s household

[018-141]seemed at the outset an undignified proceeding. The ideas, the modes, the

[018-142]surroundings, appeared retrogressive and unmeaning. But with living on there,

[018-143]day after day, the acute sojourner became conscious of a new aspect in the

[018-144]spectacle. Without any objective change whatever, variety had taken the place

[018-145]of monotonousness. His host and his host’s household, his men and his

[018-146]maids, as they became intimately known to Clare, began to differentiate

[018-147]themselves as in a chemical process. The thought of Pascal’s was brought

[018-148]home to him: “ *A mesure qu’on a plus d’esprit, on trouve

[018-149]qu’il y a plus d’hommes originaux. Les gens du commun ne trouvent

[018-150]pas de différence entre les hommes* .” The typical and unvarying Hodge

[018-151]ceased to exist. He had been disintegrated into a number of varied

[018-152]fellow-creatures—beings of many minds, beings infinite in difference;

[018-153]some happy, many serene, a few depressed, one here and there bright even to

[018-154]genius, some stupid, others wanton, others austere; some mutely Miltonic, some

[018-155]potentially Cromwellian—into men who had private views of each other, as

[018-156]he had of his friends; who could applaud or condemn each other, amuse or sadden

[018-157]themselves by the contemplation of each other’s foibles or vices; men

[018-158]every one of whom walked in his own individual way the road to dusty death.

[018-159]Unexpectedly he began to like the outdoor life for its own sake, and for what

[018-160]it brought, apart from its bearing on his own proposed career. Considering his

[018-161]position he became wonderfully free from the chronic melancholy which is taking

[018-162]hold of the civilized races with the decline of belief in a beneficent Power.

[018-163]For the first time of late years he could read as his musings inclined him,

[018-164]without any eye to cramming for a profession, since the few farming handbooks

[018-165]which he deemed it desirable to master occupied him but little time.

[018-166]He grew away from old associations, and saw something new in life and humanity.

[018-167]Secondarily, he made close acquaintance with phenomena which he had before

[018-168]known but darkly—the seasons in their moods, morning and evening, night

[018-169]and noon, winds in their different tempers, trees, waters and mists, shades and

[018-170]silences, and the voices of inanimate things.

[018-171]The early mornings were still sufficiently cool to render a fire acceptable in

[018-172]the large room wherein they breakfasted; and, by Mrs Crick’s orders, who

[018-173]held that he was too genteel to mess at their table, it was Angel Clare’s

[018-174]custom to sit in the yawning chimney-corner during the meal, his cup-and-saucer

[018-175]and plate being placed on a hinged flap at his elbow. The light from the long,

[018-176]wide, mullioned window opposite shone in upon his nook, and, assisted by a

[018-177]secondary light of cold blue quality which shone down the chimney, enabled him

[018-178]to read there easily whenever disposed to do so. Between Clare and the window

[018-179]was the table at which his companions sat, their munching profiles rising sharp

[018-180]against the panes; while to the side was the milk-house door, through which

[018-181]were visible the rectangular leads in rows, full to the brim with the

[018-182]morning’s milk. At the further end the great churn could be seen

[018-183]revolving, and its slip-slopping heard—the moving power being discernible

[018-184]through the window in the form of a spiritless horse walking in a circle and

[018-185]driven by a boy.

[018-186]For several days after Tess’s arrival Clare, sitting abstractedly reading

[018-187]from some book, periodical, or piece of music just come by post, hardly noticed

[018-188]that she was present at table. She talked so little, and the other maids talked

[018-189]so much, that the babble did not strike him as possessing a new note, and he

[018-190]was ever in the habit of neglecting the particulars of an outward scene for the

[018-191]general impression. One day, however, when he had been conning one of his

[018-192]music-scores, and by force of imagination was hearing the tune in his head, he

[018-193]lapsed into listlessness, and the music-sheet rolled to the hearth. He looked

[018-194]at the fire of logs, with its one flame pirouetting on the top in a dying dance

[018-195]after the breakfast-cooking and boiling, and it seemed to jig to his inward

[018-196]tune; also at the two chimney crooks dangling down from the cotterel, or

[018-197]cross-bar, plumed with soot, which quivered to the same melody; also at the

[018-198]half-empty kettle whining an accompaniment. The conversation at the table mixed

[018-199]in with his phantasmal orchestra till he thought: “What a fluty voice one

[018-200]of those milkmaids has! I suppose it is the new one.”

[018-201]Clare looked round upon her, seated with the others.

[018-202]She was not looking towards him. Indeed, owing to his long silence, his

[018-203]presence in the room was almost forgotten.

[018-204]“I don’t know about ghosts,” she was saying; “but I do

[018-205]know that our souls can be made to go outside our bodies when we are

[018-206]alive.”

[018-207]The dairyman turned to her with his mouth full, his eyes charged with serious

[018-208]inquiry, and his great knife and fork (breakfasts were breakfasts here) planted

[018-209]erect on the table, like the beginning of a gallows.

[018-210]“What—really now? And is it so, maidy?” he said.

[018-211]“A very easy way to feel ’em go,” continued Tess, “is

[018-212]to lie on the grass at night and look straight up at some big bright star; and,

[018-213]by fixing your mind upon it, you will soon find that you are hundreds and

[018-214]hundreds o’ miles away from your body, which you don’t seem to want

[018-215]at all.”

[018-216]The dairyman removed his hard gaze from Tess, and fixed it on his wife.

[018-217]“Now that’s a rum thing, Christianer—hey? To think o’

[018-218]the miles I’ve vamped o’ starlight nights these last thirty year,

[018-219]courting, or trading, or for doctor, or for nurse, and yet never had the least

[018-220]notion o’ that till now, or feeled my soul rise so much as an inch above

[018-221]my shirt-collar.”

[018-222]The general attention being drawn to her, including that of the

[018-223]dairyman’s pupil, Tess flushed, and remarking evasively that it was only

[018-224]a fancy, resumed her breakfast.

[018-225]Clare continued to observe her. She soon finished her eating, and having a

[018-226]consciousness that Clare was regarding her, began to trace imaginary patterns

[018-227]on the tablecloth with her forefinger with the constraint of a domestic animal

[018-228]that perceives itself to be watched.

[018-229]“What a fresh and virginal daughter of Nature that milkmaid is!” he

[018-230]said to himself.

[018-231]And then he seemed to discern in her something that was familiar, something

[018-232]which carried him back into a joyous and unforeseeing past, before the

[018-233]necessity of taking thought had made the heavens gray. He concluded that he had

[018-234]beheld her before; where he could not tell. A casual encounter during some

[018-235]country ramble it certainly had been, and he was not greatly curious about it.

[018-236]But the circumstance was sufficient to lead him to select Tess in preference to

[018-237]the other pretty milkmaids when he wished to contemplate contiguous womankind.
