# Purity and Virtue

## Definition

The concept of purity in "[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles" is the novel's primary moral and philosophical battleground. Hardy's subtitle — "A Pure Woman Faithfully Presented" — was deliberately provocative in an era when "purity" for women was defined by sexual inexperience.

The novel argues that moral purity is a quality of character rather than a fact of biography, and that society's conflation of virtue with virginity is both logically incoherent and morally destructive. Through [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]]'s story, Hardy mounts a sustained critique of the Victorian ideology of female purity.

## Manifestations

**The Subtitle as Argument** — "A Pure Woman Faithfully Presented" is not a neutral description but a thesis statement. Hardy knew the subtitle would provoke controversy, and it did. Victorian reviewers attacked the novel for "moral filth."

The subtitle stakes Hardy's claim: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s moral worth is unaffected by the sexual violation she suffers, and her subsequent actions — including the murder of [[Alec d'Urberville|Alec]] — do not negate her essential purity.

**The Double Standard** — The novel systematically exposes the asymmetry of Victorian sexual morality. [[Angel Clare]]'s premarital affair is a brief confession, easily forgiven by [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] (034-018).

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s comparable experience — in which she was the victim, not the agent — destroys her marriage. The narrator's ironic commentary drives the point home: "In considering what [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] was not, he overlooked what she was" (035-010).

**Nature as Moral Order** — The novel presents the natural world as a realm beyond Victorian moral categories. The ancient woodland of [[The Chase]] (011-018), the fertile Froom valley (016-008), the cosmic indifference of [[Stonehenge]] (058-008) — these settings suggest a world in which purity is not defined by sexual history.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s repeated association with natural imagery aligns her with a pre-Christian or extra-Christian moral order.

**[[sorrows-baptism-and-death|The Baptism of Sorrow]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s self-administered baptism of her dying infant (014-015) is the novel's most concentrated exploration of purity. Unable to call a parson because of her father's shame, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] performs the rite herself, asserting a spiritual authority that challenges clerical monopoly. The scene argues that purity of intention is the true measure of a sacrament.

## Key Passages

> The trees have inquisitive eyes, haven't they? — that is, seem as if they had. (011-019)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] in [[The Chase]], attributing witness to the natural world in a scene where human moral categories fail.

> Her baby had not been baptized. (014-217)

The stark factual statement that sets up the novel's most radical scene of spiritual self-determination — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] baptizing her dying infant [[sorrow|Sorrow]] herself.

> She baptised the child, and the name she gave him was Sorrow. (014-015)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s act of spiritual self-authorisation, challenging the clerical monopoly on sacraments.

## Related Characters

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the "pure woman" of the subtitle. Her purity is established not through sexual innocence but through honesty, self-sacrifice, and her refusal to instrumentalise others — even when she has been instrumentalised herself.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]] is the novel's primary vehicle for the critique of the sexual double standard. His inability to extend to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] the forgiveness she freely granted him exposes the hypocrisy at the heart of Victorian ideals of female purity.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec represents the sexual predator who is nevertheless socially respectable because he is male and wealthy. His later religious conversion (045-012) satirises the Protestant emphasis on faith over works — Alec changes his theology without changing his behaviour.

## Analysis

Hardy's treatment of purity engages with the most heated moral debates of late Victorian Britain. The Contagious Diseases Acts of the 1860s, which subjected women suspected of prostitution to forced medical examination while ignoring male clients, had galvanised feminist opposition. The "Maiden Tribute of Modern Babylon" exposé by W.T.

Stead (1885) had revealed the traffic in child prostitution, challenging the official narrative of Victorian moral progress. The "New Woman" fiction of the 1890s was contesting the sexual double standard in literature and life.

"[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles" (1891) entered this charged atmosphere with a radical thesis: that female virtue is a matter of the soul, not the body. The novel's argument is not merely that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is innocent — she is also, at key moments, guilty by any legal standard (she does murder Alec).

Rather, Hardy argues that the entire framework of "purity" as a sexual category is a male invention designed to control women. By insisting on [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s purity even as she commits a capital crime, Hardy exposes the absurdity of a moral system that would condemn an exploited milkmaid as impure while excusing the men who exploit her.

## Historical Context

The Victorian cult of female purity was rooted in the doctrine of "separate spheres" — the ideology that men and women occupied distinct moral domains. Women were assigned the role of moral guardians, their purity serving as a bulwark against male vice. This ideology elevated women spiritually while subordinating them socially and legally.

A woman's "ruin" — the loss of virginity outside marriage — was treated as an irreversible fall, a social death from which there was no recovery.

Hardy's novel systematically dismantles this ideology. By making his pure woman a murderess, a mistress, and an unmarried mother, he forces the question: if this woman is pure, then what does "purity" actually mean?

The answer the novel suggests — that purity inheres in character, not in sexual history — was too radical for many Victorian readers but has proven central to the novel's enduring moral authority.
