# Marriage and Courtship

## Definition

Marriage in *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles* functions simultaneously as a social institution, a spiritual sacrament, and a narrative fulcrum. The novel traces two contrasting models: the transactional marriage envisioned by [[Joan Durbeyfield]] — a vehicle for social ascent — and the companionate marriage [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] hopes to forge with [[Angel Clare]]. The failure of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s marriage is not a failure of the institution itself but of the sexual double standard that makes honesty fatal for women.

## Manifestations

### Parental Ambition

[[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]] sees marriage as the family's only escape from poverty. Upon learning of the d'Urberville lineage, her first thought is of its matrimonial possibilities: she believes [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]] will "marry a gentleman" (004-089) and thereby elevate the whole family. This instrumental view of marriage as social and economic strategy drives the plot's initial movement and sets [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] on the path to [[The Slopes]].

### Angel's Courtship

[[angel-clare|Angel]]'s courtship of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at [[Talbothays Dairy]] is portrayed as an idyllic pastoral romance — milk-skimming in the summer dawn, walks through the water-meadows, a growing intimacy conducted largely in silence and shared labour. Yet beneath this pastoral surface, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s pursuit is marked by an idealisation that ultimately proves destructive. He loves an abstraction, not the woman before him.

> The reality of marriage was startling when it loomed so near. (031-206)

This line captures the moment when theoretical love must confront institutional reality — and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who knows she carries a secret, feels the weight of that confrontation most acutely.

### The Wedding and Confession

The wedding itself is filled with omens: the cock crowing in the afternoon, the cancelled wedding date on New Year's Eve. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] marry in a hurried ceremony at [[Wellbridge]], and on their wedding night, after [[angel-clare|Angel]] confesses a youthful indiscretion, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] reciprocates with her own confession about [[Alec d'Urberville]]. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s reaction — "I should not have let it go on to marriage with you if I had" (036-074) — reveals the fundamental inequity at the heart of Victorian marriage: what is forgivable in the husband is unforgivable in the wife.

### The Failed Union

After [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s confession, [[angel-clare|Angel]] does not seek an annulment but simply abandons her — a legal and moral limbo that leaves [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] neither married nor free. This state of suspension mirrors her social condition: marked by a sexual past, she can belong fully to neither the world of marriage nor the world of independent womanhood. The marriage, never consummated in mutual trust, becomes a ghost that haunts the remainder of the novel.

## Key Passages

> Each of the lovers was desirous that the marriage should be kept secret. (032-081)

[[angel-clare|Angel]] and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] agree to a quiet wedding, a decision that isolates their union from community recognition and contributes to its fragility.

> "Could we put off our wedding till a little later?" Tess asked in a faltering voice. (033-049)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s instinctive hesitation, born of her unconfessed past, marks the moment where tragedy might have been averted — but [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s eagerness overrides her doubt.

> Having been denied the hope of a dashing marriage, fed itself as well as it could upon the small triumphs of the dairy. (013-020)

Hardy's narrator describes the romantic hopes of the dairymaids at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]], framing courtship as the central drama of rural women's lives — the one narrative avenue through which they might achieve a change of circumstance.

## Related Characters

- [[Tess Durbeyfield]] — the bride whose honesty destroys her marriage
- [[Angel Clare]] — the groom whose idealism cannot survive reality
- [[Joan Durbeyfield]] — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mother, who sees marriage as social strategy
- [[Alec d'Urberville]] — offers [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] marriage as a form of possession after his "conversion"
- [[Marian (Tess)|Marian]], [[Izz Huett]], [[Retty Priddle]] — the dairymaids whose romantic hopes centre on [[angel-clare|Angel]]

## Analysis

Hardy uses marriage to expose the fundamental hypocrisy of Victorian sexual morality. [[angel-clare|Angel]] expects to marry a virgin, yet he himself is not one; he demands honesty from [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], yet abandons her when she provides it. The marriage ceremony, meant to unite, becomes the instrument that shatters the couple's relationship — because it creates the obligation of confession, and confession proves destructive when the listener is not prepared to forgive.

The novel also suggests that marriage as an institution is inadequate to the complexities of human experience. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s first sexual encounter with Alec — a rape — is treated by society as a moral stain on her, not on him. Her marriage to [[angel-clare|Angel]] — a sacrament — is destroyed by that same stain. Between these two poles, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is crushed: she can neither erase her past nor integrate it into a respectable present.

## Historical Context

The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had reformed divorce law, but divorce remained expensive, scandalous, and practically unavailable to the poor. A woman separated from her husband had almost no legal rights. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s abandonment by [[angel-clare|Angel]] — legally married but socially and economically abandoned — reflects the precarious position of women in a legal system designed by and for men. The novel's tragic ending — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] killing Alec to rejoin [[angel-clare|Angel]] — can be read as a desperate rejection of the legal and social structures that have trapped her.
