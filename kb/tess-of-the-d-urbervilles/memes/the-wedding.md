# The Wedding

## Summary

After months of courtship at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays Dairy]], [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] finally accepts [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s proposal of marriage. The wedding is arranged with deliberate simplicity: no guests, a licence rather than banns, and the ceremony at a distant country church. [[angel-clare|Angel]] has grown impatient with delays: "This penitential mood kept her from naming the wedding-day" (032-001).

The day is shadowed by omens. As [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and [[angel-clare|Angel]] travel by carriage to the church, a cock crows in the afternoon — a traditional portent of ill fortune: "I don't remember hearing it before. Is it a bad omen?" (033-245). The wedding party — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], [[angel-clare|Angel]], and two reluctant witnesses from the dairy — proceeds to the church by a roundabout route [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] does not recognize.

In the church, "there were only a dozen or so of people" (033-201). The simplicity of the ceremony contrasts with the weight of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s unspoken secret. She has tried to confess her past to [[angel-clare|Angel]] before the wedding — writing a letter that slipped under his carpet undelivered — but each attempt has been forestalled.

After the ceremony, the couple travel to [[wellbridge|Wellbridge Mill]], an ancient d'Urberville mansion where they will spend their wedding night. The house is hung with portraits of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ancestors — "two life-size portraits on panels built into the masonry" (034-030) — whose features are "unquestionably traceable" in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s own face (034-035). The ancestral setting transforms the wedding night into a reckoning with the past.

## Context

The wedding occupies Chapters 32 through 34, bridging Phase the Fourth (The Consequence) and Phase the Fifth (The Woman Pays). It is the structural pivot of the novel — the point at which [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s hoped-for happiness and her concealed past converge with devastating force.

The practical circumstances of the wedding are modest. [[angel-clare|Angel]], estranged from his clerical family for marrying beneath him, has arranged everything himself. The marriage is by licence rather than banns, a choice that spares [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] the public proclamation of her name but also removes the traditional opportunity for objections to be raised — a symbol of the secrecy that pervades the union.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — The bride. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] approaches the wedding with trembling hope and buried dread. Her multiple attempts to confess her past before the wedding — the undelivered letter, the half-spoken words — reveal her moral seriousness and her terror of deception, even as circumstances conspire to keep her silent.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — The groom. [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s impatience to marry and his insistence on simplicity reflect both his genuine love for [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and his failure to see her as she really is. He is marrying the milkmaid of his pastoral ideal, not the woman with a history.

## Narrative Impact

The wedding is the hinge on which the novel turns. Before it lie the pastoral idyll of [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]] and the concealment of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s past; after it lie the confession, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s rejection, and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s long descent into suffering. The wedding chapter itself is masterfully suspenseful — Hardy piles omen upon omen, each one ratcheting the tension toward the inevitable revelation.

The ancestral setting at [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]] is crucial. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] spends her wedding night in a house hung with the portraits of her d'Urberville ancestors — the very lineage whose discovery set the tragedy in motion. The d'Urberville past, which [[john-durbeyfield|Jack Durbeyfield]] celebrated as a windfall in Chapter 1, becomes in Chapter 34 a judgment: the living heir is condemned in the house of her forebears.

## Analysis

### The Ominous Wedding

Hardy's use of omens in the wedding chapters has divided critics: some see them as heavy-handed foreshadowing, others as an expression of the folk-belief that pervades rural Wessex. The crowing cock belongs to the latter tradition — a genuine piece of English rural folklore that Hardy embeds without authorial endorsement. The omens register in [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s consciousness without determining the outcome; they are atmospheric rather than deterministic.

### Ancestry as Fate

The [[wellbridge|Wellbridge]] mansion literalizes the novel's central metaphor: the past is not past. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ancestors literally surround her on her wedding night, their painted eyes watching as she moves toward the confession that will destroy her marriage. The d'Urberville inheritance, which promised elevation in Chapter 1, delivers only ruin in Chapter 34.
