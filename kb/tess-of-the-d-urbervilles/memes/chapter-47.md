[047-001]It is the threshing of the last wheat-rick at Flintcomb-Ash farm. The dawn of

[047-002]the March morning is singularly inexpressive, and there is nothing to show

[047-003]where the eastern horizon lies. Against the twilight rises the trapezoidal top

[047-004]of the stack, which has stood forlornly here through the washing and bleaching

[047-005]of the wintry weather.

[047-006]When Izz Huett and Tess arrived at the scene of operations only a rustling

[047-007]denoted that others had preceded them; to which, as the light increased, there

[047-008]were presently added the silhouettes of two men on the summit. They were busily

[047-009]“unhaling” the rick, that is, stripping off the thatch before

[047-010]beginning to throw down the sheaves; and while this was in progress Izz and

[047-011]Tess, with the other women-workers, in their whitey-brown pinners, stood

[047-012]waiting and shivering, Farmer Groby having insisted upon their being on the

[047-013]spot thus early to get the job over if possible by the end of the day. Close

[047-014]under the eaves of the stack, and as yet barely visible, was the red tyrant

[047-015]that the women had come to serve—a timber-framed construction, with

[047-016]straps and wheels appertaining—the threshing-machine which, whilst it was

[047-017]going, kept up a despotic demand upon the endurance of their muscles and

[047-018]nerves.

[047-019]A little way off there was another indistinct figure; this one black, with a

[047-020]sustained hiss that spoke of strength very much in reserve. The long chimney

[047-021]running up beside an ash-tree, and the warmth which radiated from the spot,

[047-022]explained without the necessity of much daylight that here was the engine which

[047-023]was to act as the *primum mobile* of this little world. By the engine

[047-024]stood a dark, motionless being, a sooty and grimy embodiment of tallness, in a

[047-025]sort of trance, with a heap of coals by his side: it was the engine-man. The

[047-026]isolation of his manner and colour lent him the appearance of a creature from

[047-027]Tophet, who had strayed into the pellucid smokelessness of this region of

[047-028]yellow grain and pale soil, with which he had nothing in common, to amaze and

[047-029]to discompose its aborigines.

[047-030]What he looked he felt. He was in the agricultural world, but not of it. He

[047-031]served fire and smoke; these denizens of the fields served vegetation, weather,

[047-032]frost, and sun. He travelled with his engine from farm to farm, from county to

[047-033]county, for as yet the steam threshing-machine was itinerant in this part of

[047-034]Wessex. He spoke in a strange northern accent; his thoughts being turned

[047-035]inwards upon himself, his eye on his iron charge, hardly perceiving the scenes

[047-036]around him, and caring for them not at all: holding only strictly necessary

[047-037]intercourse with the natives, as if some ancient doom compelled him to wander

[047-038]here against his will in the service of his Plutonic master. The long strap

[047-039]which ran from the driving-wheel of his engine to the red thresher under the

[047-040]rick was the sole tie-line between agriculture and him.

[047-041]While they uncovered the sheaves he stood apathetic beside his portable

[047-042]repository of force, round whose hot blackness the morning air quivered. He had

[047-043]nothing to do with preparatory labour. His fire was waiting incandescent, his

[047-044]steam was at high pressure, in a few seconds he could make the long strap move

[047-045]at an invisible velocity. Beyond its extent the environment might be corn,

[047-046]straw, or chaos; it was all the same to him. If any of the autochthonous idlers

[047-047]asked him what he called himself, he replied shortly, “an

[047-048]engineer.”

[047-049]The rick was unhaled by full daylight; the men then took their places, the

[047-050]women mounted, and the work began. Farmer Groby—or, as they called him,

[047-051]“he”—had arrived ere this, and by his orders Tess was placed

[047-052]on the platform of the machine, close to the man who fed it, her business being

[047-053]to untie every sheaf of corn handed on to her by Izz Huett, who stood next, but

[047-054]on the rick; so that the feeder could seize it and spread it over the revolving

[047-055]drum, which whisked out every grain in one moment.

[047-056]They were soon in full progress, after a preparatory hitch or two, which

[047-057]rejoiced the hearts of those who hated machinery. The work sped on till

[047-058]breakfast time, when the thresher was stopped for half an hour; and on starting

[047-059]again after the meal the whole supplementary strength of the farm was thrown

[047-060]into the labour of constructing the straw-rick, which began to grow beside the

[047-061]stack of corn. A hasty lunch was eaten as they stood, without leaving their

[047-062]positions, and then another couple of hours brought them near to dinner-time;

[047-063]the inexorable wheel continuing to spin, and the penetrating hum of the

[047-064]thresher to thrill to the very marrow all who were near the revolving

[047-065]wire-cage.

[047-066]The old men on the rising straw-rick talked of the past days when they had been

[047-067]accustomed to thresh with flails on the oaken barn-floor; when everything, even

[047-068]to winnowing, was effected by hand-labour, which, to their thinking, though

[047-069]slow, produced better results. Those, too, on the corn-rick talked a little;

[047-070]but the perspiring ones at the machine, including Tess, could not lighten their

[047-071]duties by the exchange of many words. It was the ceaselessness of the work

[047-072]which tried her so severely, and began to make her wish that she had never come

[047-073]to Flintcomb-Ash. The women on the corn-rick—Marian, who was one of them,

[047-074]in particular—could stop to drink ale or cold tea from the flagon now and

[047-075]then, or to exchange a few gossiping remarks while they wiped their faces or

[047-076]cleared the fragments of straw and husk from their clothing; but for Tess there

[047-077]was no respite; for, as the drum never stopped, the man who fed it could not

[047-078]stop, and she, who had to supply the man with untied sheaves, could not stop

[047-079]either, unless Marian changed places with her, which she sometimes did for half

[047-080]an hour in spite of Groby’s objections that she was too slow-handed for a

[047-081]feeder.

[047-082]For some probably economical reason it was usually a woman who was chosen for

[047-083]this particular duty, and Groby gave as his motive in selecting Tess that she

[047-084]was one of those who best combined strength with quickness in untying, and both

[047-085]with staying power, and this may have been true. The hum of the thresher, which

[047-086]prevented speech, increased to a raving whenever the supply of corn fell short

[047-087]of the regular quantity. As Tess and the man who fed could never turn their

[047-088]heads she did not know that just before the dinner-hour a person had come

[047-089]silently into the field by the gate, and had been standing under a second rick

[047-090]watching the scene and Tess in particular. He was dressed in a tweed suit of

[047-091]fashionable pattern, and he twirled a gay walking-cane.

[047-092]“Who is that?” said Izz Huett to Marian. She had at first addressed

[047-093]the inquiry to Tess, but the latter could not hear it.

[047-094]“Somebody’s fancy-man, I s’pose,” said Marian

[047-095]laconically.

[047-096]“I’ll lay a guinea he’s after Tess.”

[047-097]“O no. ’Tis a ranter pa’son who’s been sniffing after

[047-098]her lately; not a dandy like this.”

[047-099]“Well—this is the same man.”

[047-100]“The same man as the preacher? But he’s quite different!”

[047-101]“He hev left off his black coat and white neckercher, and hev cut off his

[047-102]whiskers; but he’s the same man for all that.”

[047-103]“D’ye really think so? Then I’ll tell her,” said

[047-104]Marian.

[047-105]“Don’t. She’ll see him soon enough, good-now.”

[047-106]“Well, I don’t think it at all right for him to join his preaching

[047-107]to courting a married woman, even though her husband mid be abroad, and she, in

[047-108]a sense, a widow.”

[047-109]“Oh—he can do her no harm,” said Izz drily. “Her mind

[047-110]can no more be heaved from that one place where it do bide than a stooded

[047-111]waggon from the hole he’s in. Lord love ’ee, neither court-paying,

[047-112]nor preaching, nor the seven thunders themselves, can wean a woman when

[047-113]’twould be better for her that she should be weaned.”

[047-114]Dinner-time came, and the whirling ceased; whereupon Tess left her post, her

[047-115]knees trembling so wretchedly with the shaking of the machine that she could

[047-116]scarcely walk.

[047-117]“You ought to het a quart o’ drink into ’ee, as I’ve

[047-118]done,” said Marian. “You wouldn’t look so white then. Why,

[047-119]souls above us, your face is as if you’d been hagrode!”

[047-120]It occurred to the good-natured Marian that, as Tess was so tired, her

[047-121]discovery of her visitor’s presence might have the bad effect of taking

[047-122]away her appetite; and Marian was thinking of inducing Tess to descend by a

[047-123]ladder on the further side of the stack when the gentleman came forward and

[047-124]looked up.

[047-125]Tess uttered a short little “Oh!” And a moment after she said,

[047-126]quickly, “I shall eat my dinner here—right on the rick.”

[047-127]Sometimes, when they were so far from their cottages, they all did this; but as

[047-128]there was rather a keen wind going to-day, Marian and the rest descended, and

[047-129]sat under the straw-stack.

[047-130]The newcomer was, indeed, Alec d’Urberville, the late Evangelist, despite

[047-131]his changed attire and aspect. It was obvious at a glance that the original *Weltlust* had come back; that he had restored himself, as nearly as a man

[047-132]could do who had grown three or four years older, to the old jaunty, slapdash

[047-133]guise under which Tess had first known her admirer, and cousin so-called.

[047-134]Having decided to remain where she was, Tess sat down among the bundles, out of

[047-135]sight of the ground, and began her meal; till, by-and-by, she heard footsteps

[047-136]on the ladder, and immediately after Alec appeared upon the stack—now an

[047-137]oblong and level platform of sheaves. He strode across them, and sat down

[047-138]opposite of her without a word.

[047-139]Tess continued to eat her modest dinner, a slice of thick pancake which she had

[047-140]brought with her. The other workfolk were by this time all gathered under the

[047-141]rick, where the loose straw formed a comfortable retreat.

[047-142]“I am here again, as you see,” said d’Urberville.

[047-143]“Why do you trouble me so!” she cried, reproach flashing from her

[047-144]very finger-ends.

[047-145]“ *I* trouble *you* ? I think I may ask, why do you trouble

[047-146]me?”

[047-147]“Sure, I don’t trouble you any-when!”

[047-148]“You say you don’t? But you do! You haunt me. Those very eyes that

[047-149]you turned upon me with such a bitter flash a moment ago, they come to me just

[047-150]as you showed them then, in the night and in the day! Tess, ever since you told

[047-151]me of that child of ours, it is just as if my feelings, which have been flowing

[047-152]in a strong puritanical stream, had suddenly found a way open in the direction

[047-153]of you, and had all at once gushed through. The religious channel is left dry

[047-154]forthwith; and it is you who have done it!”

[047-155]She gazed in silence.

[047-156]“What—you have given up your preaching entirely?” she asked.

[047-157]She had gathered from Angel sufficient of the incredulity of modern thought to

[047-158]despise flash enthusiasm; but, as a woman, she was somewhat appalled.

[047-159]In affected severity d’Urberville continued—

[047-160]“Entirely. I have broken every engagement since that afternoon I was to

[047-161]address the drunkards at Casterbridge Fair. The deuce only knows what I am

[047-162]thought of by the brethren. Ah-ha! The brethren! No doubt they pray for

[047-163]me—weep for me; for they are kind people in their way. But what do I

[047-164]care? How could I go on with the thing when I had lost my faith in it?—it

[047-165]would have been hypocrisy of the basest kind! Among them I should have stood

[047-166]like Hymenaeus and Alexander, who were delivered over to Satan that they might

[047-167]learn not to blaspheme. What a grand revenge you have taken! I saw you

[047-168]innocent, and I deceived you. Four years after, you find me a Christian

[047-169]enthusiast; you then work upon me, perhaps to my complete perdition! But Tess,

[047-170]my coz, as I used to call you, this is only my way of talking, and you must not

[047-174]field-girls should never wear those bonnets if you wish to keep out of

[047-175]danger.” He regarded her silently for a few moments, and with a short

[047-176]cynical laugh resumed: “I believe that if the bachelor-apostle, whose

[047-177]deputy I thought I was, had been tempted by such a pretty face, he would have

[047-178]let go the plough for her sake as I do!”

[047-179]Tess attempted to expostulate, but at this juncture all her fluency failed her,

[047-180]and without heeding he added:

[047-181]“Well, this paradise that you supply is perhaps as good as any other,

[047-182]after all. But to speak seriously, Tess.” D’Urberville rose and

[047-183]came nearer, reclining sideways amid the sheaves, and resting upon his elbow.

[047-184]“Since I last saw you, I have been thinking of what you said that *he* said. I have come to the conclusion that there does seem rather a

[047-185]want of common-sense in these threadbare old propositions; how I could have

[047-186]been so fired by poor Parson Clare’s enthusiasm, and have gone so madly

[047-189]intelligence—whose name you have never told me—about having what

[047-190]they call an ethical system without any dogma, I don’t see my way to that

[047-191]at all.”

[047-192]“Why, you can have the religion of loving-kindness and purity at least,

[047-194]“O no! I’m a different sort of fellow from that! If there’s

[047-195]nobody to say, ‘Do this, and it will be a good thing for you after you

[047-207]“Not as then—never as then—’tis different!” she

[047-208]entreated. “And there was never warmth with me! O why didn’t you
