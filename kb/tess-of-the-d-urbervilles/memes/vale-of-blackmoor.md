# Vale of Blackmoor

## Location

The Vale of Blackmoor (also called Blackmoor Vale or the Vale of Blakemore) is a fertile, enclosed valley in the heart of Wessex. It is "an engirdled and secluded region" (002-002), bounded by hills that separate it from the surrounding countryside. Within this valley lies the village of [[marlott|Marlott]], where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] is born and raised.

The valley's geographical character is defined by enclosure. Unlike the open, traversable [[froom-valley|Froom Valley]] to the north, Blackmoor is a world unto itself — "the Vale of Blackmoor was to her the world" (005-051). Its streams are "slow, silent, often turbid" (016-066), in marked contrast with the rapid, crystalline waters of the River Froom. This hydrological difference encodes a psychological truth: Blackmoor is a landscape of depth, stillness, and hidden currents.

The valley is situated "on the further side of Blackmoor" (030-223) from the [[froom-valley|Froom Valley]], requiring a journey through uplands and intervening country to reach. It lies west of the Blackmoor Valley, "equally remote from her native" (041-011) place — a geographical remoteness that mirrors [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s increasing distance from her origins.

## Description

Hardy describes the Vale of Blackmoor with the intimate knowledge of a native. The valley is "loamy" (044-068), its soil rich and dark, supporting the mixed farming and orchard cultivation that sustains the cottagers of [[marlott|Marlott]]. The landscape is one of small fields, ancient hedgerows, and winding lanes — a countryside shaped by centuries of human occupation rather than the grand gestures of nature.

The valley's atmosphere changes with the seasons. In spring, the orchards bloom and the May-Day dance fills the meadows (002-001). In autumn, the "loamy Vale of Blackmoor" lies "misty and still in the dawn" (044-068), its fertility giving way to melancholy. Winter brings isolation: the hills that enclose the valley become barriers, and [[marlott|Marlott]]'s cottagers draw inward.

The "secret of Blackmoor was best discovered from the heights" (016-122) — a characteristic Hardy observation that suggests the valley yields its meaning only to those who can see it whole, from above. Those who live within it, like [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a child, know it intimately but cannot grasp its shape.

## Significance

The Vale of Blackmoor is the novel's Ur-landscape — the place from which everything flows and to which everything returns. It is where [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]] learns of his ancestry (001-003), where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] grows up steeped in "fancies and prefigurative superstitions" (006-016), where the death of Prince sets the plot in motion (004-156), and where [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] returns repeatedly — after [[trantridge|Trantridge]], after the death of her child, after [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s abandonment.

Hardy uses the valley to explore the relationship between landscape and identity. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is a product of Blackmoor — its folkways, its superstitions, its rhythms of agricultural life. "Like all the cottagers in Blackmoor Vale, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] was steeped in fancies and prefigurative superstitions" (006-016). When she leaves the valley, she carries its imprint with her; when she returns, she is both native and stranger.

The valley also represents a vanishing world. The novel, published in 1891, looks back on the rural Wessex of Hardy's childhood — a world of oral tradition, agricultural custom, and ancient connection to the land. The Vale of Blackmoor is not just [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s home; it is the repository of a way of life that Hardy saw passing away. Its "engirdled and secluded" character (002-002) is both its protection and its doom.

## Analysis

### The Engirdled World

Hardy's word "engirdled" (002-002) is the key to understanding the Vale of Blackmoor. To be girdled is to be enclosed, bounded, held within limits. The valley is a world within the world, its horizons defined by the surrounding hills. For [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] as a child, this enclosure is protective — the valley is all she knows, and its limits are the limits of reality. But as she grows and leaves, the same enclosure becomes a kind of prison, a place she can neither fully escape nor fully return to.

### Landscape as Fate

The novel's determinism is encoded in its geography. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is born in the Vale of Blackmoor, and the valley shapes her as surely as her ancestry does. Its streams are "slow, silent, often turbid" (016-066) — and so, in a sense, is [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]: a person of hidden depths, still surfaces, and currents that run dark. The valley's enclosure becomes her enclosure; its fertility becomes her vulnerability. Hardy does not present this as metaphor so much as identity: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] *is* the Vale of Blackmoor in human form, carrying its landscape within her wherever she goes.
