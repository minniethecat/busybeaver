# Marlott

## Location

Marlott lies in the [[vale-of-blackmoor|Vale of Blakemore]] (or [[vale-of-blackmoor|Blackmoor]]), an agricultural region in Thomas Hardy's fictional Wessex. The vale is described as "an engirdled and secluded region, for the most part untrodden as yet by tourist or landscape-painter, though within a four hours' journey from London" (002-003).

It is surrounded by hills that give it a sense of enclosure and separation from the modern world.

> ...for the most part untrodden as yet by tourist or landscape-painter, though within a four hours' journey from London. (002-003)

Marlott itself is a small village of thatched cottages, fields, and hedgerows, typical of the southwestern English countryside of the mid-nineteenth century (002-005). The Vale of Blakemore maps onto the real Vale of Blackmore in Dorset, Hardy's native county.

## Description

Hardy depicts Marlott through a series of carefully observed rural scenes. The village is entered through "a long and broken" lane bordered by hedges (002-007). The landscape includes fields partitioned by hedgerows, old manor houses that speak of a feudal past, and the distinctive figure of the "Blakemore vale, in which the haymakers are at work" (002-012).

The novel opens with the May-Day club-walking, a folk tradition observed by the village maidens dressed in white (001-005). This opening tableau establishes Marlott as a place where ancient customs survive.

The rhythms of agricultural life remain tied to seasonal cycles rather than industrial time. The [[John Durbeyfield|Durbeyfield]] cottage — small, cramped, and economically precarious — represents the poverty that coexists with this pastoral beauty (003-005).

## Significance

Marlott is the novel's symbolic heart — the place of [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]]'s innocence and the home she can never truly return to. Every subsequent setting in the novel is measured against Marlott's pastoral baseline:

## Key Scenes

- **[[may-day-dance|The May-Day Dance]] (001-005)** — The novel opens with [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]] among the white-clad maidens of Marlott, celebrating an ancient fertility rite on the village green.

- **[[the-durbeyfield-discovery|Parson Tringham's Revelation]] (001-003)** — [[John Durbeyfield]] learns of his noble ancestry on the road outside Marlott, setting the entire plot in motion.

- **The Morning After the Dance (002-010)** — [[Angel Clare]] passes through Marlott but does not dance with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — the first missed connection between them occurs in this village setting.

- **[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s Return After [[the-chase|The Chase]] (012-005)** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] walks back to Marlott along the familiar lanes, now irrevocably changed.

- **The Baptism and Burial of [[sorrow|Sorrow]] (014-015)** — The most charged religious scene in the novel occurs in the Durbeyfield cottage in Marlott.

## Analysis

Marlott represents the lost world of rural England that Hardy's novels memorialise. The village is not idealised — its poverty, gossip, and limitations are honestly rendered — but it possesses a coherence and rootedness that the novel's other settings lack.

When [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] leaves Marlott, she enters a world of displacement, moving from dairy to farm to lodging house, never again finding a true home.

The Vale of Blakemore functions as a kind of Eden from which [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is expelled. The novel's tragic structure maps onto a geography of increasing distance from Marlott: [[Trantridge]] (the fall), [[Talbothays Dairy|Talbothays]] (brief paradise regained), [[Flintcomb-Ash]] (the hell of agricultural labour), [[Sandbourne]] (the final corruption).

That [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is arrested at [[Stonehenge]] and executed at [[Wintoncester]] — neither of them Marlott — completes the geography of exile.
