[009-001]The community of fowls to which Tess had been appointed as supervisor,

[009-002]purveyor, nurse, surgeon, and friend made its headquarters in an old thatched

[009-003]cottage standing in an enclosure that had once been a garden, but was now a

[009-004]trampled and sanded square. The house was overrun with ivy, its chimney being

[009-005]enlarged by the boughs of the parasite to the aspect of a ruined tower. The

[009-006]lower rooms were entirely given over to the birds, who walked about them with a

[009-007]proprietary air, as though the place had been built by themselves, and not by

[009-008]certain dusty copyholders who now lay east and west in the churchyard. The

[009-009]descendants of these bygone owners felt it almost as a slight to their family

[009-010]when the house which had so much of their affection, had cost so much of their

[009-011]forefathers’ money, and had been in their possession for several

[009-012]generations before the d’Urbervilles came and built here, was

[009-013]indifferently turned into a fowl-house by Mrs Stoke-d’Urberville as soon

[009-014]as the property fell into hand according to law. “’Twas good enough

[009-015]for Christians in grandfather’s time,” they said.

[009-016]The rooms wherein dozens of infants had wailed at their nursing now resounded

[009-017]with the tapping of nascent chicks. Distracted hens in coops occupied spots

[009-018]where formerly stood chairs supporting sedate agriculturists. The

[009-019]chimney-corner and once-blazing hearth was now filled with inverted beehives,

[009-020]in which the hens laid their eggs; while out of doors the plots that each

[009-021]succeeding householder had carefully shaped with his spade were torn by the

[009-022]cocks in wildest fashion.

[009-023]The garden in which the cottage stood was surrounded by a wall, and could only

[009-024]be entered through a door.

[009-025]When Tess had occupied herself about an hour the next morning in altering and

[009-026]improving the arrangements, according to her skilled ideas as the daughter of a

[009-027]professed poulterer, the door in the wall opened and a servant in white cap and

[009-028]apron entered. She had come from the manor-house.

[009-029]“Mrs d’Urberville wants the fowls as usual,” she said; but

[009-030]perceiving that Tess did not quite understand, she explained,

[009-031]“Mis’ess is a old lady, and blind.”

[009-032]“Blind!” said Tess.

[009-033]Almost before her misgiving at the news could find time to shape itself she

[009-034]took, under her companion’s direction, two of the most beautiful of the

[009-035]Hamburghs in her arms, and followed the maid-servant, who had likewise taken

[009-036]two, to the adjacent mansion, which, though ornate and imposing, showed traces

[009-037]everywhere on this side that some occupant of its chambers could bend to the

[009-038]love of dumb creatures—feathers floating within view of the front, and

[009-039]hen-coops standing on the grass.

[009-040]In a sitting-room on the ground-floor, ensconced in an armchair with her back

[009-041]to the light, was the owner and mistress of the estate, a white-haired woman of

[009-042]not more than sixty, or even less, wearing a large cap. She had the mobile face

[009-043]frequent in those whose sight has decayed by stages, has been laboriously

[009-044]striven after, and reluctantly let go, rather than the stagnant mien apparent

[009-045]in persons long sightless or born blind. Tess walked up to this lady with her

[009-046]feathered charges—one sitting on each arm.

[009-047]“Ah, you are the young woman come to look after my birds?” said Mrs

[009-048]d’Urberville, recognizing a new footstep. “I hope you will be kind

[009-049]to them. My bailiff tells me you are quite the proper person. Well, where are

[009-050]they? Ah, this is Strut! But he is hardly so lively to-day, is he? He is

[009-051]alarmed at being handled by a stranger, I suppose. And Phena too—yes,

[009-052]they are a little frightened—aren’t you, dears? But they will soon

[009-053]get used to you.”

[009-054]While the old lady had been speaking Tess and the other maid, in obedience to

[009-055]her gestures, had placed the fowls severally in her lap, and she had felt them

[009-056]over from head to tail, examining their beaks, their combs, the manes of the

[009-057]cocks, their wings, and their claws. Her touch enabled her to recognize them in

[009-058]a moment, and to discover if a single feather were crippled or draggled. She

[009-059]handled their crops, and knew what they had eaten, and if too little or too

[009-060]much; her face enacting a vivid pantomime of the criticisms passing in her

[009-061]mind.

[009-062]The birds that the two girls had brought in were duly returned to the yard, and

[009-063]the process was repeated till all the pet cocks and hens had been submitted to

[009-064]the old woman—Hamburghs, Bantams, Cochins, Brahmas, Dorkings, and such

[009-065]other sorts as were in fashion just then—her perception of each visitor

[009-066]being seldom at fault as she received the bird upon her knees.

[009-067]It reminded Tess of a Confirmation, in which Mrs d’Urberville was the

[009-068]bishop, the fowls the young people presented, and herself and the maid-servant

[009-069]the parson and curate of the parish bringing them up. At the end of the

[009-070]ceremony Mrs d’Urberville abruptly asked Tess, wrinkling and twitching

[009-071]her face into undulations, “Can you whistle?”

[009-072]“Whistle, Ma’am?”

[009-073]“Yes, whistle tunes.”

[009-074]Tess could whistle like most other country-girls, though the accomplishment was

[009-075]one which she did not care to profess in genteel company. However, she blandly

[009-076]admitted that such was the fact.

[009-077]“Then you will have to practise it every day. I had a lad who did it very

[009-078]well, but he has left. I want you to whistle to my bullfinches; as I cannot see

[009-079]them, I like to hear them, and we teach ’em airs that way. Tell her where

[009-080]the cages are, Elizabeth. You must begin to-morrow, or they will go back in

[009-081]their piping. They have been neglected these several days.”

[009-082]“Mr d’Urberville whistled to ’em this morning,

[009-083]ma’am,” said Elizabeth.

[009-084]“He! Pooh!”

[009-085]The old lady’s face creased into furrows of repugnance, and she made no

[009-086]further reply.

[009-087]Thus the reception of Tess by her fancied kinswoman terminated, and the birds

[009-088]were taken back to their quarters. The girl’s surprise at Mrs

[009-089]d’Urberville’s manner was not great; for since seeing the size of

[009-090]the house she had expected no more. But she was far from being aware that the

[009-091]old lady had never heard a word of the so-called kinship. She gathered that no

[009-092]great affection flowed between the blind woman and her son. But in that, too,

[009-093]she was mistaken. Mrs d’Urberville was not the first mother compelled to

[009-094]love her offspring resentfully, and to be bitterly fond.

[009-095]In spite of the unpleasant initiation of the day before, Tess inclined to the

[009-096]freedom and novelty of her new position in the morning when the sun shone, now

[009-097]that she was once installed there; and she was curious to test her powers in

[009-098]the unexpected direction asked of her, so as to ascertain her chance of

[009-099]retaining her post. As soon as she was alone within the walled garden she sat

[009-100]herself down on a coop, and seriously screwed up her mouth for the

[009-101]long-neglected practice. She found her former ability to have degenerated to

[009-102]the production of a hollow rush of wind through the lips, and no clear note at

[009-103]all.

[009-104]She remained fruitlessly blowing and blowing, wondering how she could have so

[009-105]grown out of the art which had come by nature, till she became aware of a

[009-106]movement among the ivy-boughs which cloaked the garden-wall no less then the

[009-107]cottage. Looking that way she beheld a form springing from the coping to the

[009-108]plot. It was Alec d’Urberville, whom she had not set eyes on since he had

[009-109]conducted her the day before to the door of the gardener’s cottage where

[009-110]she had lodgings.

[009-111]“Upon my honour!” cried he, “there was never before such a

[009-112]beautiful thing in Nature or Art as you look, ‘Cousin’ Tess

[009-113](‘Cousin’ had a faint ring of mockery). I have been watching you

[009-114]from over the wall—sitting like *Im* -patience on a monument, and

[009-115]pouting up that pretty red mouth to whistling shape, and whooing and whooing,

[009-116]and privately swearing, and never being able to produce a note. Why, you are

[009-117]quite cross because you can’t do it.”

[009-118]“I may be cross, but I didn’t swear.”

[009-119]“Ah! I understand why you are trying—those bullies! My mother wants

[009-120]you to carry on their musical education. How selfish of her! As if attending to

[009-121]these curst cocks and hens here were not enough work for any girl. I would

[009-122]flatly refuse, if I were you.”

[009-123]“But she wants me particularly to do it, and to be ready by to-morrow

[009-124]morning.”

[009-125]“Does she? Well then—I’ll give you a lesson or two.”

[009-126]“Oh no, you won’t!” said Tess, withdrawing towards the door.

[009-127]“Nonsense; I don’t want to touch you. See—I’ll stand on

[009-128]this side of the wire-netting, and you can keep on the other; so you may feel

[009-129]quite safe. Now, look here; you screw up your lips too harshly. There

[009-130]’tis—so.”

[009-131]He suited the action to the word, and whistled a line of “Take, O take

[009-132]those lips away.” But the allusion was lost upon Tess.

[009-133]“Now try,” said d’Urberville.

[009-134]She attempted to look reserved; her face put on a sculptural severity. But he

[009-135]persisted in his demand, and at last, to get rid of him, she did put up her

[009-136]lips as directed for producing a clear note; laughing distressfully, however,

[009-137]and then blushing with vexation that she had laughed.

[009-138]He encouraged her with “Try again!”

[009-139]Tess was quite serious, painfully serious by this time; and she

[009-140]tried—ultimately and unexpectedly emitting a real round sound. The

[009-141]momentary pleasure of success got the better of her; her eyes enlarged, and she

[009-142]involuntarily smiled in his face.

[009-143]“That’s it! Now I have started you—you’ll go on

[009-144]beautifully. There—I said I would not come near you; and, in spite of

[009-145]such temptation as never before fell to mortal man, I’ll keep my word....

[009-146]Tess, do you think my mother a queer old soul?”

[009-147]“I don’t know much of her yet, sir.”

[009-148]“You’ll find her so; she must be, to make you learn to whistle to

[009-149]her bullfinches. I am rather out of her books just now, but you will be quite

[009-150]in favour if you treat her live-stock well. Good morning. If you meet with any

[009-151]difficulties and want help here, don’t go to the bailiff, come to

[009-152]me.”

[009-153]It was in the economy of this *régime* that Tess Durbeyfield had

[009-154]undertaken to fill a place. Her first day’s experiences were fairly

[009-155]typical of those which followed through many succeeding days. A familiarity

[009-156]with Alec d’Urberville’s presence—which that young man

[009-157]carefully cultivated in her by playful dialogue, and by jestingly calling her

[009-158]his cousin when they were alone—removed much of her original shyness of

[009-159]him, without, however, implanting any feeling which could engender shyness of a

[009-160]new and tenderer kind. But she was more pliable under his hands than a mere

[009-161]companionship would have made her, owing to her unavoidable dependence upon his

[009-162]mother, and, through that lady’s comparative helplessness, upon him.

[009-163]She soon found that whistling to the bullfinches in Mrs

[009-164]d’Urberville’s room was no such onerous business when she had

[009-165]regained the art, for she had caught from her musical mother numerous airs that

[009-166]suited those songsters admirably. A far more satisfactory time than when she

[009-167]practised in the garden was this whistling by the cages each morning.

[009-168]Unrestrained by the young man’s presence she threw up her mouth, put her

[009-169]lips near the bars, and piped away in easeful grace to the attentive listeners.

[009-170]Mrs d’Urberville slept in a large four-post bedstead hung with heavy

[009-171]damask curtains, and the bullfinches occupied the same apartment, where they

[009-172]flitted about freely at certain hours, and made little white spots on the

[009-173]furniture and upholstery. Once while Tess was at the window where the cages

[009-174]were ranged, giving her lesson as usual, she thought she heard a rustling

[009-175]behind the bed. The old lady was not present, and turning round the girl had an

[009-176]impression that the toes of a pair of boots were visible below the fringe of

[009-177]the curtains. Thereupon her whistling became so disjointed that the listener,

[009-178]if such there were, must have discovered her suspicion of his presence. She

[009-179]searched the curtains every morning after that, but never found anybody within

[009-180]them. Alec d’Urberville had evidently thought better of his freak to

[009-181]terrify her by an ambush of that kind.
