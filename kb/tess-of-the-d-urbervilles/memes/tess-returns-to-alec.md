# Tess Returns to Alec

## Summary

After [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]]'s departure to Brazil, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess Durbeyfield]] endures crushing poverty as a field worker at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]]. There she encounters [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]], who has undergone a startling transformation into a Methodist preacher. Alec's religious conversion proves temporary: upon seeing [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] again, his old obsession rekindles. He abandons his ministry to pursue her relentlessly, while [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s family — made homeless by her father's death — faces eviction. With nowhere to turn and her mother and siblings destitute, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] ultimately yields to Alec's persistence, returning to a man she despises.

## Context

The event unfolds in the novel's seventh phase, "Fulfilment." [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] has been working at [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]] under the brutal [[farmer-groby|Farmer Groby]], enduring physical hardship and emotional isolation. [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]] has not written; his family has rejected her plea for help. The Durbeyfield family, already impoverished, faces complete ruin after [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s death.

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s meeting with Alec occurs at a moment of maximum vulnerability. She has walked to [[emminster|Emminster]] seeking aid from [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s parents, only to turn back unseen. On her return journey, she hears Alec's voice — now preaching the very Scripture he once mocked.

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — Worn down by field labor and [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s silence, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] initially resists Alec with fierce determination. She writes an angry letter to [[angel-clare|Angel]], declaring she will "try to forget" him. But her family's eviction leaves her with no viable alternatives, and her mother's pressure — combined with Alec's promises of security — wears down her resistance.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec's conversion to Methodism is dramatic but shallow. The sight of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] unravels his religious fervor with startling speed. He blames [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] for his relapse, telling her, "You have been the cause of my backsliding." His pursuit shifts from spiritual argument to material temptation, offering her family housing and comfort. Hardy portrays Alec's transformation as a chilling cycle: seducer to preacher and back to seducer, each role performed with equal conviction.

## Narrative Impact

Alec's return and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s eventual capitulation mark the novel's darkest turn. The event completes the tragic arc: [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s abandonment, [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s poverty, her father's death, and Alec's relentless pressure combine into an inescapable trap. Hardy presents [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s surrender not as moral failure but as economic coercion — she sacrifices herself to save her family from homelessness.

The transformation of Alec from preacher to predator exposes the hollowness of his conversion. Hardy's critique is double-edged: Alec's religious fervor is genuine but shallow, while the social structures that would have protected [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] — family, church, community — are all absent when she needs them most.
