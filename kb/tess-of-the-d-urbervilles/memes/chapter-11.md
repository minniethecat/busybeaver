[011-001]The twain cantered along for some time without speech, Tess as she clung to him

[011-002]still panting in her triumph, yet in other respects dubious. She had perceived

[011-003]that the horse was not the spirited one he sometimes rose, and felt no alarm on

[011-004]that score, though her seat was precarious enough despite her tight hold of

[011-005]him. She begged him to slow the animal to a walk, which Alec accordingly did.

[011-006]“Neatly done, was it not, dear Tess?” he said by and by.

[011-007]“Yes!” said she. “I am sure I ought to be much obliged to

[011-008]you.”

[011-009]“And are you?”

[011-010]She did not reply.

[011-011]“Tess, why do you always dislike my kissing you?”

[011-012]“I suppose—because I don’t love you.”

[011-013]“You are quite sure?”

[011-014]“I am angry with you sometimes!”

[011-015]“Ah, I half feared as much.” Nevertheless, Alec did not object to

[011-016]that confession. He knew that anything was better then frigidity. “Why

[011-017]haven’t you told me when I have made you angry?”

[011-018]“You know very well why. Because I cannot help myself here.”

[011-019]“I haven’t offended you often by love-making?”

[011-020]“You have sometimes.”

[011-021]“How many times?”

[011-022]“You know as well as I—too many times.”

[011-023]“Every time I have tried?”

[011-024]She was silent, and the horse ambled along for a considerable distance, till a

[011-025]faint luminous fog, which had hung in the hollows all the evening, became

[011-026]general and enveloped them. It seemed to hold the moonlight in suspension,

[011-027]rendering it more pervasive than in clear air. Whether on this account, or from

[011-028]absent-mindedness, or from sleepiness, she did not perceive that they had long

[011-029]ago passed the point at which the lane to Trantridge branched from the highway,

[011-030]and that her conductor had not taken the Trantridge track.

[011-031]She was inexpressibly weary. She had risen at five o’clock every morning

[011-032]of that week, had been on foot the whole of each day, and on this evening had

[011-033]in addition walked the three miles to Chaseborough, waited three hours for her

[011-034]neighbours without eating or drinking, her impatience to start them preventing

[011-035]either; she had then walked a mile of the way home, and had undergone the

[011-036]excitement of the quarrel, till, with the slow progress of their steed, it was

[011-037]now nearly one o’clock. Only once, however, was she overcome by actual

[011-038]drowsiness. In that moment of oblivion her head sank gently against him.

[011-039]D’Urberville stopped the horse, withdrew his feet from the stirrups,

[011-040]turned sideways on the saddle, and enclosed her waist with his arm to support

[011-041]her.

[011-042]This immediately put her on the defensive, and with one of those sudden

[011-043]impulses of reprisal to which she was liable she gave him a little push from

[011-044]her. In his ticklish position he nearly lost his balance and only just avoided

[011-045]rolling over into the road, the horse, though a powerful one, being fortunately

[011-046]the quietest he rode.

[011-047]“That is devilish unkind!” he said. “I mean no

[011-048]harm—only to keep you from falling.”

[011-049]She pondered suspiciously, till, thinking that this might after all be true,

[011-050]she relented, and said quite humbly, “I beg your pardon, sir.”

[011-051]“I won’t pardon you unless you show some confidence in me. Good

[011-052]God!” he burst out, “what am I, to be repulsed so by a mere chit

[011-053]like you? For near three mortal months have you trifled with my feelings,

[011-054]eluded me, and snubbed me; and I won’t stand it!”

[011-055]“I’ll leave you to-morrow, sir.”

[011-056]“No, you will not leave me to-morrow! Will you, I ask once more, show

[011-057]your belief in me by letting me clasp you with my arm? Come, between us two and

[011-058]nobody else, now. We know each other well; and you know that I love you, and

[011-059]think you the prettiest girl in the world, which you are. Mayn’t I treat

[011-060]you as a lover?”

[011-061]She drew a quick pettish breath of objection, writhing uneasily on her seat,

[011-062]looked far ahead, and murmured, “I don’t know—I

[011-063]wish—how can I say yes or no when—”

[011-064]He settled the matter by clasping his arm round her as he desired, and Tess

[011-065]expressed no further negative. Thus they sidled slowly onward till it struck

[011-066]her they had been advancing for an unconscionable time—far longer than

[011-067]was usually occupied by the short journey from Chaseborough, even at this

[011-068]walking pace, and that they were no longer on hard road, but in a mere

[011-069]trackway.

[011-070]“Why, where be we?” she exclaimed.

[011-071]“Passing by a wood.”

[011-072]“A wood—what wood? Surely we are quite out of the road?”

[011-073]“A bit of The Chase—the oldest wood in England. It is a lovely

[011-074]night, and why should we not prolong our ride a little?”

[011-075]“How could you be so treacherous!” said Tess, between archness and

[011-076]real dismay, and getting rid of his arm by pulling open his fingers one by one,

[011-077]though at the risk of slipping off herself. “Just when I’ve been

[011-078]putting such trust in you, and obliging you to please you, because I thought I

[011-079]had wronged you by that push! Please set me down, and let me walk home.”

[011-080]“You cannot walk home, darling, even if the air were clear. We are miles

[011-081]away from Trantridge, if I must tell you, and in this growing fog you might

[011-082]wander for hours among these trees.”

[011-083]“Never mind that,” she coaxed. “Put me down, I beg you. I

[011-084]don’t mind where it is; only let me get down, sir, please!”

[011-085]“Very well, then, I will—on one condition. Having brought you here

[011-086]to this out-of-the-way place, I feel myself responsible for your safe-conduct

[011-087]home, whatever you may yourself feel about it. As to your getting to Trantridge

[011-088]without assistance, it is quite impossible; for, to tell the truth, dear, owing

[011-089]to this fog, which so disguises everything, I don’t quite know where we

[011-090]are myself. Now, if you will promise to wait beside the horse while I walk

[011-091]through the bushes till I come to some road or house, and ascertain exactly our

[011-092]whereabouts, I’ll deposit you here willingly. When I come back I’ll

[011-093]give you full directions, and if you insist upon walking you may; or you may

[011-094]ride—at your pleasure.”

[011-095]She accepted these terms, and slid off on the near side, though not till he had

[011-096]stolen a cursory kiss. He sprang down on the other side.

[011-097]“I suppose I must hold the horse?” said she.

[011-098]“Oh no; it’s not necessary,” replied Alec, patting the

[011-099]panting creature. “He’s had enough of it for to-night.”

[011-100]He turned the horse’s head into the bushes, hitched him on to a bough,

[011-101]and made a sort of couch or nest for her in the deep mass of dead leaves.

[011-102]“Now, you sit there,” he said. “The leaves have not got damp

[011-103]as yet. Just give an eye to the horse—it will be quite sufficient.”

[011-104]He took a few steps away from her, but, returning, said, “By the bye,

[011-105]Tess, your father has a new cob to-day. Somebody gave it to him.”

[011-106]“Somebody? You!”

[011-107]D’Urberville nodded.

[011-108]“O how very good of you that is!” she exclaimed, with a painful

[011-109]sense of the awkwardness of having to thank him just then.

[011-110]“And the children have some toys.”

[011-111]“I didn’t know—you ever sent them anything!” she

[011-112]murmured, much moved. “I almost wish you had not—yes, I almost wish

[011-113]it!”

[011-114]“Why, dear?”

[011-115]“It—hampers me so.”

[011-116]“Tessy—don’t you love me ever so little now?”

[011-117]“I’m grateful,” she reluctantly admitted. “But I fear I

[011-118]do not—” The sudden vision of his passion for herself as a factor

[011-119]in this result so distressed her that, beginning with one slow tear, and then

[011-120]following with another, she wept outright.

[011-121]“Don’t cry, dear, dear one! Now sit down here, and wait till I

[011-122]come.” She passively sat down amid the leaves he had heaped, and shivered

[011-123]slightly. “Are you cold?” he asked.

[011-124]“Not very—a little.”

[011-125]He touched her with his fingers, which sank into her as into down. “You

[011-126]have only that puffy muslin dress on—how’s that?”

[011-127]“It’s my best summer one. ’Twas very warm when I started, and

[011-128]I didn’t know I was going to ride, and that it would be night.”

[011-129]“Nights grow chilly in September. Let me see.” He pulled off a

[011-130]light overcoat that he had worn, and put it round her tenderly.

[011-131]“That’s it—now you’ll feel warmer,” he continued.

[011-132]“Now, my pretty, rest there; I shall soon be back again.”

[011-133]Having buttoned the overcoat round her shoulders he plunged into the webs of

[011-134]vapour which by this time formed veils between the trees. She could hear the

[011-135]rustling of the branches as he ascended the adjoining slope, till his movements

[011-136]were no louder than the hopping of a bird, and finally died away. With the

[011-137]setting of the moon the pale light lessened, and Tess became invisible as she

[011-138]fell into reverie upon the leaves where he had left her.

[011-139]In the meantime Alec d’Urberville had pushed on up the slope to clear his

[011-140]genuine doubt as to the quarter of The Chase they were in. He had, in fact,

[011-141]ridden quite at random for over an hour, taking any turning that came to hand

[011-142]in order to prolong companionship with her, and giving far more attention to

[011-143]Tess’s moonlit person than to any wayside object. A little rest for the

[011-144]jaded animal being desirable, he did not hasten his search for landmarks. A

[011-145]clamber over the hill into the adjoining vale brought him to the fence of a

[011-146]highway whose contours he recognized, which settled the question of their

[011-147]whereabouts. D’Urberville thereupon turned back; but by this time the

[011-148]moon had quite gone down, and partly on account of the fog The Chase was

[011-149]wrapped in thick darkness, although morning was not far off. He was obliged to

[011-150]advance with outstretched hands to avoid contact with the boughs, and

[011-151]discovered that to hit the exact spot from which he had started was at first

[011-152]entirely beyond him. Roaming up and down, round and round, he at length heard a

[011-153]slight movement of the horse close at hand; and the sleeve of his overcoat

[011-154]unexpectedly caught his foot.

[011-155]“Tess!” said d’Urberville.

[011-156]There was no answer. The obscurity was now so great that he could see

[011-157]absolutely nothing but a pale nebulousness at his feet, which represented the

[011-158]white muslin figure he had left upon the dead leaves. Everything else was

[011-159]blackness alike. D’Urberville stooped; and heard a gentle regular

[011-160]breathing. He knelt and bent lower, till her breath warmed his face, and in a

[011-161]moment his cheek was in contact with hers. She was sleeping soundly, and upon

[011-162]her eyelashes there lingered tears.

[011-163]Darkness and silence ruled everywhere around. Above them rose the primaeval

[011-164]yews and oaks of The Chase, in which there poised gentle roosting birds in

[011-165]their last nap; and about them stole the hopping rabbits and hares. But, might

[011-166]some say, where was Tess’s guardian angel? where was the providence of

[011-167]her simple faith? Perhaps, like that other god of whom the ironical Tishbite

[011-168]spoke, he was talking, or he was pursuing, or he was in a journey, or he was

[011-169]sleeping and not to be awaked.

[011-170]Why it was that upon this beautiful feminine tissue, sensitive as gossamer, and

[011-171]practically blank as snow as yet, there should have been traced such a coarse

[011-172]pattern as it was doomed to receive; why so often the coarse appropriates the

[011-173]finer thus, the wrong man the woman, the wrong woman the man, many thousand

[011-174]years of analytical philosophy have failed to explain to our sense of order.

[011-175]One may, indeed, admit the possibility of a retribution lurking in the present

[011-176]catastrophe. Doubtless some of Tess d’Urberville’s mailed ancestors

[011-177]rollicking home from a fray had dealt the same measure even more ruthlessly

[011-178]towards peasant girls of their time. But though to visit the sins of the

[011-179]fathers upon the children may be a morality good enough for divinities, it is

[011-180]scorned by average human nature; and it therefore does not mend the matter.

[011-181]As Tess’s own people down in those retreats are never tired of saying

[011-182]among each other in their fatalistic way: “It was to be.” There lay

[011-183]the pity of it. An immeasurable social chasm was to divide our heroine’s

[011-184]personality thereafter from that previous self of hers who stepped from her

[011-185]mother’s door to try her fortune at Trantridge poultry-farm.
