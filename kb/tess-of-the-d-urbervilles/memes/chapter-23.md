[023-001]The hot weather of July had crept upon them unawares, and the atmosphere of the

[023-002]flat vale hung heavy as an opiate over the dairy-folk, the cows, and the trees.

[023-003]Hot steaming rains fell frequently, making the grass where the cows fed yet

[023-004]more rank, and hindering the late hay-making in the other meads.

[023-005]It was Sunday morning; the milking was done; the outdoor milkers had gone home.

[023-006]Tess and the other three were dressing themselves rapidly, the whole bevy

[023-007]having agreed to go together to Mellstock Church, which lay some three or four

[023-008]miles distant from the dairy-house. She had now been two months at Talbothays,

[023-009]and this was her first excursion.

[023-010]All the preceding afternoon and night heavy thunderstorms had hissed down upon

[023-011]the meads, and washed some of the hay into the river; but this morning the sun

[023-012]shone out all the more brilliantly for the deluge, and the air was balmy and

[023-013]clear.

[023-014]The crooked lane leading from their own parish to Mellstock ran along the

[023-015]lowest levels in a portion of its length, and when the girls reached the most

[023-016]depressed spot they found that the result of the rain had been to flood the

[023-017]lane over-shoe to a distance of some fifty yards. This would have been no

[023-018]serious hindrance on a week-day; they would have clicked through it in their

[023-019]high pattens and boots quite unconcerned; but on this day of vanity, this

[023-020]Sun’s-day, when flesh went forth to coquet with flesh while

[023-021]hypocritically affecting business with spiritual things; on this occasion for

[023-022]wearing their white stockings and thin shoes, and their pink, white, and lilac

[023-023]gowns, on which every mud spot would be visible, the pool was an awkward

[023-024]impediment. They could hear the church-bell calling—as yet nearly a mile

[023-025]off.

[023-026]“Who would have expected such a rise in the river in summer-time!”

[023-027]said Marian, from the top of the roadside bank on which they had climbed, and

[023-028]were maintaining a precarious footing in the hope of creeping along its slope

[023-029]till they were past the pool.

[023-030]“We can’t get there anyhow, without walking right through it, or

[023-031]else going round the Turnpike way; and that would make us so very late!”

[023-032]said Retty, pausing hopelessly.

[023-033]“And I do colour up so hot, walking into church late, and all the people

[023-034]staring round,” said Marian, “that I hardly cool down again till we

[023-035]get into the That-it-may-please-Thees.”

[023-036]While they stood clinging to the bank they heard a splashing round the bend of

[023-037]the road, and presently appeared Angel Clare, advancing along the lane towards

[023-038]them through the water.

[023-039]Four hearts gave a big throb simultaneously.

[023-040]His aspect was probably as un-Sabbatarian a one as a dogmatic parson’s

[023-041]son often presented; his attire being his dairy clothes, long wading boots, a

[023-042]cabbage-leaf inside his hat to keep his head cool, with a thistle-spud to

[023-043]finish him off. “He’s not going to church,” said Marian.

[023-044]“No—I wish he was!” murmured Tess.

[023-045]Angel, in fact, rightly or wrongly (to adopt the safe phrase of evasive

[023-046]controversialists), preferred sermons in stones to sermons in churches and

[023-047]chapels on fine summer days. This morning, moreover, he had gone out to see if

[023-048]the damage to the hay by the flood was considerable or not. On his walk he

[023-049]observed the girls from a long distance, though they had been so occupied with

[023-050]their difficulties of passage as not to notice him. He knew that the water had

[023-051]risen at that spot, and that it would quite check their progress. So he had

[023-052]hastened on, with a dim idea of how he could help them—one of them in

[023-053]particular.

[023-054]The rosy-cheeked, bright-eyed quartet looked so charming in their light summer

[023-055]attire, clinging to the roadside bank like pigeons on a roof-slope, that he

[023-056]stopped a moment to regard them before coming close. Their gauzy skirts had

[023-057]brushed up from the grass innumerable flies and butterflies which, unable to

[023-058]escape, remained caged in the transparent tissue as in an aviary. Angel’s

[023-059]eye at last fell upon Tess, the hindmost of the four; she, being full of

[023-060]suppressed laughter at their dilemma, could not help meeting his glance

[023-061]radiantly.

[023-062]He came beneath them in the water, which did not rise over his long boots; and

[023-063]stood looking at the entrapped flies and butterflies.

[023-064]“Are you trying to get to church?” he said to Marian, who was in

[023-065]front, including the next two in his remark, but avoiding Tess.

[023-066]“Yes, sir; and ’tis getting late; and my colour do come up

[023-067]so—”

[023-068]“I’ll carry you through the pool—every Jill of you.”

[023-069]The whole four flushed as if one heart beat through them.

[023-070]“I think you can’t, sir,” said Marian.

[023-071]“It is the only way for you to get past. Stand still. Nonsense—you

[023-072]are not too heavy! I’d carry you all four together. Now, Marian,

[023-073]attend,” he continued, “and put your arms round my shoulders, so.

[023-074]Now! Hold on. That’s well done.”

[023-075]Marian had lowered herself upon his arm and shoulder as directed, and Angel

[023-076]strode off with her, his slim figure, as viewed from behind, looking like the

[023-077]mere stem to the great nosegay suggested by hers. They disappeared round the

[023-078]curve of the road, and only his sousing footsteps and the top ribbon of

[023-079]Marian’s bonnet told where they were. In a few minutes he reappeared. Izz

[023-080]Huett was the next in order upon the bank.

[023-081]“Here he comes,” she murmured, and they could hear that her lips

[023-082]were dry with emotion. “And I have to put my arms round his neck and look

[023-083]into his face as Marian did.”

[023-084]“There’s nothing in that,” said Tess quickly.

[023-085]“There’s a time for everything,” continued Izz, unheeding.

[023-086]“A time to embrace, and a time to refrain from embracing; the first is

[023-087]now going to be mine.”

[023-088]“Fie—it is Scripture, Izz!”

[023-089]“Yes,” said Izz, “I’ve always a’ ear at church

[023-090]for pretty verses.”

[023-091]Angel Clare, to whom three-quarters of this performance was a commonplace act

[023-092]of kindness, now approached Izz. She quietly and dreamily lowered herself into

[023-093]his arms, and Angel methodically marched off with her. When he was heard

[023-094]returning for the third time Retty’s throbbing heart could be almost seen

[023-095]to shake her. He went up to the red-haired girl, and while he was seizing her

[023-096]he glanced at Tess. His lips could not have pronounced more plainly, “It

[023-097]will soon be you and I.” Her comprehension appeared in her face; she

[023-098]could not help it. There was an understanding between them.

[023-099]Poor little Retty, though by far the lightest weight, was the most troublesome

[023-100]of Clare’s burdens. Marian had been like a sack of meal, a dead weight of

[023-101]plumpness under which he has literally staggered. Izz had ridden sensibly and

[023-102]calmly. Retty was a bunch of hysterics.

[023-103]However, he got through with the disquieted creature, deposited her, and

[023-104]returned. Tess could see over the hedge the distant three in a group, standing

[023-105]as he had placed them on the next rising ground. It was now her turn. She was

[023-106]embarrassed to discover that excitement at the proximity of Mr Clare’s

[023-107]breath and eyes, which she had contemned in her companions, was intensified in

[023-108]herself; and as if fearful of betraying her secret, she paltered with him at

[023-109]the last moment.

[023-110]“I may be able to clim’ along the bank perhaps—I can

[023-111]clim’ better than they. You must be so tired, Mr Clare!”

[023-112]“No, no, Tess,” said he quickly. And almost before she was aware,

[023-113]she was seated in his arms and resting against his shoulder.

[023-114]“Three Leahs to get one Rachel,” he whispered.

[023-115]“They are better women than I,” she replied, magnanimously sticking

[023-116]to her resolve.

[023-117]“Not to me,” said Angel.

[023-118]He saw her grow warm at this; and they went some steps in silence.

[023-119]“I hope I am not too heavy?” she said timidly.

[023-120]“O no. You should lift Marian! Such a lump. You are like an undulating

[023-121]billow warmed by the sun. And all this fluff of muslin about you is the

[023-122]froth.”

[023-123]“It is very pretty—if I seem like that to you.”

[023-124]“Do you know that I have undergone three-quarters of this labour entirely

[023-125]for the sake of the fourth quarter?”

[023-126]“No.”

[023-127]“I did not expect such an event to-day.”

[023-128]“Nor I... The water came up so sudden.”

[023-129]That the rise in the water was what she understood him to refer to, the state

[023-130]of breathing belied. Clare stood still and inclinced his face towards hers.

[023-131]“O Tessy!” he exclaimed.

[023-132]The girl’s cheeks burned to the breeze, and she could not look into his

[023-133]eyes for her emotion. It reminded Angel that he was somewhat unfairly taking

[023-134]advantage of an accidental position; and he went no further with it. No

[023-135]definite words of love had crossed their lips as yet, and suspension at this

[023-136]point was desirable now. However, he walked slowly, to make the remainder of

[023-137]the distance as long as possible; but at last they came to the bend, and the

[023-138]rest of their progress was in full view of the other three. The dry land was

[023-139]reached, and he set her down.

[023-140]Her friends were looking with round thoughtful eyes at her and him, and she

[023-141]could see that they had been talking of her. He hastily bade them farewell, and

[023-142]splashed back along the stretch of submerged road.

[023-143]The four moved on together as before, till Marian broke the silence by

[023-144]saying—

[023-145]“No—in all truth; we have no chance against her!” She looked

[023-146]joylessly at Tess.

[023-147]“What do you mean?” asked the latter.

[023-148]“He likes ’ee best—the very best! We could see it as he

[023-149]brought ’ee. He would have kissed ’ee, if you had encouraged him to

[023-150]do it, ever so little.”

[023-151]“No, no,” said she.

[023-152]The gaiety with which they had set out had somehow vanished; and yet there was

[023-153]no enmity or malice between them. They were generous young souls; they had been

[023-154]reared in the lonely country nooks where fatalism is a strong sentiment, and

[023-155]they did not blame her. Such supplanting was to be.

[023-156]Tess’s heart ached. There was no concealing from herself the fact that

[023-157]she loved Angel Clare, perhaps all the more passionately from knowing that the

[023-158]others had also lost their hearts to him. There is contagion in this sentiment,

[023-159]especially among women. And yet that same hungry nature had fought against

[023-160]this, but too feebly, and the natural result had followed.

[023-161]“I will never stand in your way, nor in the way of either of you!”

[023-162]she declared to Retty that night in the bedroom (her tears running down).

[023-163]“I can’t help this, my dear! I don’t think marrying is in his

[023-164]mind at all; but if he were ever to ask me I should refuse him, as I should

[023-165]refuse any man.”

[023-166]“Oh! would you? Why?” said wondering Retty.

[023-167]“It cannot be! But I will be plain. Putting myself quite on one side, I

[023-168]don’t think he will choose either of you.”

[023-169]“I have never expected it—thought of it!” moaned Retty.

[023-170]“But O! I wish I was dead!”

[023-171]The poor child, torn by a feeling which she hardly understood, turned to the

[023-172]other two girls who came upstairs just then.

[023-173]“We be friends with her again,” she said to them. “She thinks

[023-174]no more of his choosing her than we do.”

[023-175]So the reserve went off, and they were confiding and warm.

[023-176]“I don’t seem to care what I do now,” said Marian, whose mood

[023-177]was turned to its lowest bass. “I was going to marry a dairyman at

[023-178]Stickleford, who’s asked me twice; but—my soul—I would put an

[023-179]end to myself rather’n be his wife now! Why don’t ye speak,

[023-180]Izz?”

[023-181]“To confess, then,” murmured Izz, “I made sure to-day that he

[023-182]was going to kiss me as he held me; and I lay still against his breast, hoping

[023-183]and hoping, and never moved at all. But he did not. I don’t like biding

[023-184]here at Talbothays any longer! I shall go hwome.”

[023-185]The air of the sleeping-chamber seemed to palpitate with the hopeless passion

[023-186]of the girls. They writhed feverishly under the oppressiveness of an emotion

[023-187]thrust on them by cruel Nature’s law—an emotion which they had

[023-188]neither expected nor desired. The incident of the day had fanned the flame that

[023-189]was burning the inside of their hearts out, and the torture was almost more

[023-190]than they could endure. The differences which distinguished them as individuals

[023-191]were abstracted by this passion, and each was but portion of one organism

[023-192]called sex. There was so much frankness and so little jealousy because there

[023-193]was no hope. Each one was a girl of fair common sense, and she did not delude

[023-194]herself with any vain conceits, or deny her love, or give herself airs, in the

[023-195]idea of outshining the others. The full recognition of the futility of their

[023-196]infatuation, from a social point of view; its purposeless beginning; its

[023-197]self-bounded outlook; its lack of everything to justify its existence in the

[023-198]eye of civilization (while lacking nothing in the eye of Nature); the one fact

[023-199]that it did exist, ecstasizing them to a killing joy—all this imparted to

[023-200]them a resignation, a dignity, which a practical and sordid expectation of

[023-201]winning him as a husband would have destroyed.

[023-202]They tossed and turned on their little beds, and the cheese-wring dripped

[023-203]monotonously downstairs.

[023-204]“B’ you awake, Tess?” whispered one, half-an-hour later.

[023-205]It was Izz Huett’s voice.

[023-206]Tess replied in the affirmative, whereupon also Retty and Marian suddenly flung

[023-207]the bedclothes off them, and sighed—

[023-208]“So be we!”

[023-209]“I wonder what she is like—the lady they say his family have looked

[023-210]out for him!”

[023-211]“I wonder,” said Izz.

[023-212]“Some lady looked out for him?” gasped Tess, starting. “I

[023-213]have never heard o’ that!”

[023-214]“O yes—’tis whispered; a young lady of his own rank, chosen

[023-215]by his family; a Doctor of Divinity’s daughter near his father’s

[023-216]parish of Emminster; he don’t much care for her, they say. But he is sure

[023-217]to marry her.”

[023-218]They had heard so very little of this; yet it was enough to build up wretched

[023-219]dolorous dreams upon, there in the shade of the night. They pictured all the

[023-220]details of his being won round to consent, of the wedding preparations, of the

[023-221]bride’s happiness, of her dress and veil, of her blissful home with him,

[023-222]when oblivion would have fallen upon themselves as far as he and their love

[023-223]were concerned. Thus they talked, and ached, and wept till sleep charmed their

[023-224]sorrow away.

[023-225]After this disclosure Tess nourished no further foolish thought that there

[023-226]lurked any grave and deliberate import in Clare’s attentions to her. It

[023-227]was a passing summer love of her face, for love’s own temporary

[023-228]sake—nothing more. And the thorny crown of this sad conception was that

[023-229]she whom he really did prefer in a cursory way to the rest, she who knew

[023-230]herself to be more impassioned in nature, cleverer, more beautiful than they,

[023-231]was in the eyes of propriety far less worthy of him than the homelier ones whom

[023-232]he ignored.
