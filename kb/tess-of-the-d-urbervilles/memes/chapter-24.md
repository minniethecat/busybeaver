[024-001]Amid the oozing fatness and warm ferments of the Froom Vale, at a season when

[024-002]the rush of juices could almost be heard below the hiss of fertilization, it

[024-003]was impossible that the most fanciful love should not grow passionate. The

[024-004]ready bosoms existing there were impregnated by their surroundings.

[024-005]July passed over their heads, and the Thermidorean weather which came in its

[024-006]wake seemed an effort on the part of Nature to match the state of hearts at

[024-007]Talbothays Dairy. The air of the place, so fresh in the spring and early

[024-008]summer, was stagnant and enervating now. Its heavy scents weighed upon them,

[024-009]and at mid-day the landscape seemed lying in a swoon. Ethiopic scorchings

[024-010]browned the upper slopes of the pastures, but there was still bright green

[024-011]herbage here where the watercourses purled. And as Clare was oppressed by the

[024-012]outward heats, so was he burdened inwardly by waxing fervour of passion for the

[024-013]soft and silent Tess.

[024-014]The rains having passed, the uplands were dry. The wheels of the

[024-015]dairyman’s spring-cart, as he sped home from market, licked up the

[024-016]pulverized surface of the highway, and were followed by white ribands of dust,

[024-017]as if they had set a thin powder-train on fire. The cows jumped wildly over the

[024-018]five-barred barton-gate, maddened by the gad-fly; Dairyman Crick kept his

[024-019]shirt-sleeves permanently rolled up from Monday to Saturday; open windows had

[024-020]no effect in ventilation without open doors, and in the dairy-garden the

[024-021]blackbirds and thrushes crept about under the currant-bushes, rather in the

[024-022]manner of quadrupeds than of winged creatures. The flies in the kitchen were

[024-023]lazy, teasing, and familiar, crawling about in the unwonted places, on the

[024-024]floors, into drawers, and over the backs of the milkmaids’ hands.

[024-025]Conversations were concerning sunstroke; while butter-making, and still more

[024-026]butter-keeping, was a despair.

[024-027]They milked entirely in the meads for coolness and convenience, without driving

[024-028]in the cows. During the day the animals obsequiously followed the shadow of the

[024-029]smallest tree as it moved round the stem with the diurnal roll; and when the

[024-030]milkers came they could hardly stand still for the flies.

[024-031]On one of these afternoons four or five unmilked cows chanced to stand apart

[024-032]from the general herd, behind the corner of a hedge, among them being Dumpling

[024-033]and Old Pretty, who loved Tess’s hands above those of any other maid.

[024-034]When she rose from her stool under a finished cow, Angel Clare, who had been

[024-035]observing her for some time, asked her if she would take the aforesaid

[024-036]creatures next. She silently assented, and with her stool at arm’s

[024-037]length, and the pail against her knee, went round to where they stood. Soon the

[024-038]sound of Old Pretty’s milk fizzing into the pail came through the hedge,

[024-039]and then Angel felt inclined to go round the corner also, to finish off a

[024-040]hard-yielding milcher who had strayed there, he being now as capable of this as

[024-041]the dairyman himself.

[024-042]All the men, and some of the women, when milking, dug their foreheads into the

[024-043]cows and gazed into the pail. But a few—mainly the younger

[024-044]ones—rested their heads sideways. This was Tess Durbeyfield’s

[024-045]habit, her temple pressing the milcher’s flank, her eyes fixed on the far

[024-046]end of the meadow with the quiet of one lost in meditation. She was milking Old

[024-047]Pretty thus, and the sun chancing to be on the milking-side, it shone flat upon

[024-048]her pink-gowned form and her white curtain-bonnet, and upon her profile,

[024-049]rendering it keen as a cameo cut from the dun background of the cow.

[024-050]She did not know that Clare had followed her round, and that he sat under his

[024-051]cow watching her. The stillness of her head and features was remarkable: she

[024-052]might have been in a trance, her eyes open, yet unseeing. Nothing in the

[024-053]picture moved but Old Pretty’s tail and Tess’s pink hands, the

[024-054]latter so gently as to be a rhythmic pulsation only, as if they were obeying a

[024-055]reflex stimulus, like a beating heart.

[024-056]How very lovable her face was to him. Yet there was nothing ethereal about it;

[024-057]all was real vitality, real warmth, real incarnation. And it was in her mouth

[024-058]that this culminated. Eyes almost as deep and speaking he had seen before, and

[024-059]cheeks perhaps as fair; brows as arched, a chin and throat almost as shapely;

[024-060]her mouth he had seen nothing to equal on the face of the earth. To a young man

[024-061]with the least fire in him that little upward lift in the middle of her red top

[024-062]lip was distracting, infatuating, maddening. He had never before seen a

[024-063]woman’s lips and teeth which forced upon his mind with such persistent

[024-064]iteration the old Elizabethan simile of roses filled with snow. Perfect, he, as

[024-065]a lover, might have called them off-hand. But no—they were not perfect.

[024-066]And it was the touch of the imperfect upon the would-be perfect that gave the

[024-067]sweetness, because it was that which gave the humanity.

[024-068]Clare had studied the curves of those lips so many times that he could

[024-069]reproduce them mentally with ease: and now, as they again confronted him,

[024-070]clothed with colour and life, they sent an *aura* over his flesh, a breeze

[024-071]through his nerves, which well nigh produced a qualm; and actually produced, by

[024-072]some mysterious physiological process, a prosaic sneeze.

[024-073]She then became conscious that he was observing her; but she would not show it

[024-074]by any change of position, though the curious dream-like fixity disappeared,

[024-075]and a close eye might easily have discerned that the rosiness of her face

[024-076]deepened, and then faded till only a tinge of it was left.

[024-077]The influence that had passed into Clare like an excitation from the sky did

[024-078]not die down. Resolutions, reticences, prudences, fears, fell back like a

[024-079]defeated battalion. He jumped up from his seat, and, leaving his pail to be

[024-080]kicked over if the milcher had such a mind, went quickly towards the desire of

[024-081]his eyes, and, kneeling down beside her, clasped her in his arms.

[024-082]Tess was taken completely by surprise, and she yielded to his embrace with

[024-083]unreflecting inevitableness. Having seen that it was really her lover who had

[024-084]advanced, and no one else, her lips parted, and she sank upon him in her

[024-085]momentary joy, with something very like an ecstatic cry.

[024-086]He had been on the point of kissing that too tempting mouth, but he checked

[024-087]himself, for tender conscience’ sake.

[024-088]“Forgive me, Tess dear!” he whispered. “I ought to have

[024-089]asked. I—did not know what I was doing. I do not mean it as a liberty. I

[024-090]am devoted to you, Tessy, dearest, in all sincerity!”

[024-091]Old Pretty by this time had looked round, puzzled; and seeing two people

[024-092]crouching under her where, by immemorial custom, there should have been only

[024-093]one, lifted her hind leg crossly.

[024-094]“She is angry—she doesn’t know what we

[024-095]mean—she’ll kick over the milk!” exclaimed Tess, gently

[024-096]striving to free herself, her eyes concerned with the quadruped’s

[024-097]actions, her heart more deeply concerned with herself and Clare.

[024-098]She slipped up from her seat, and they stood together, his arm still encircling

[024-099]her. Tess’s eyes, fixed on distance, began to fill.

[024-100]“Why do you cry, my darling?” he said.

[024-101]“O—I don’t know!” she murmured.

[024-102]As she saw and felt more clearly the position she was in she became agitated

[024-103]and tried to withdraw.

[024-104]“Well, I have betrayed my feeling, Tess, at last,” said he, with a

[024-105]curious sigh of desperation, signifying unconsciously that his heart had outrun

[024-106]his judgement. “That I—love you dearly and truly I need not say.

[024-107]But I—it shall go no further now—it distresses you—I am as

[024-108]surprised as you are. You will not think I have presumed upon your

[024-109]defencelessness—been too quick and unreflecting, will you?”

[024-110]“N’—I can’t tell.”

[024-111]He had allowed her to free herself; and in a minute or two the milking of each

[024-112]was resumed. Nobody had beheld the gravitation of the two into one; and when

[024-113]the dairyman came round by that screened nook a few minutes later, there was

[024-114]not a sign to reveal that the markedly sundered pair were more to each other

[024-115]than mere acquaintance. Yet in the interval since Crick’s last view of

[024-116]them something had occurred which changed the pivot of the universe for their

[024-117]two natures; something which, had he known its quality, the dairyman would have

[024-118]despised, as a practical man; yet which was based upon a more stubborn and

[024-119]resistless tendency than a whole heap of so-called practicalities. A veil had

[024-120]been whisked aside; the tract of each one’s outlook was to have a new

[024-121]horizon thenceforward—for a short time or for a long.
