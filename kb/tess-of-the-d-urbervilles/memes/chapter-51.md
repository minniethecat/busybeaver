[051-001]At length it was the eve of Old Lady-Day, and the agricultural world was in a

[051-002]fever of mobility such as only occurs at that particular date of the year. It

[051-003]is a day of fulfilment; agreements for outdoor service during the ensuing year,

[051-004]entered into at Candlemas, are to be now carried out. The labourers—or

[051-005]“work-folk”, as they used to call themselves immemorially till the

[051-006]other word was introduced from without—who wish to remain no longer in

[051-007]old places are removing to the new farms.

[051-008]These annual migrations from farm to farm were on the increase here. When

[051-009]Tess’s mother was a child the majority of the field-folk about Marlott

[051-010]had remained all their lives on one farm, which had been the home also of their

[051-011]fathers and grandfathers; but latterly the desire for yearly removal had risen

[051-012]to a high pitch. With the younger families it was a pleasant excitement which

[051-013]might possibly be an advantage. The Egypt of one family was the Land of Promise

[051-014]to the family who saw it from a distance, till by residence there it became in

[051-015]turn their Egypt also; and so they changed and changed.

[051-016]However, all the mutations so increasingly discernible in village life did not

[051-017]originate entirely in the agricultural unrest. A depopulation was also going

[051-018]on. The village had formerly contained, side by side with the argicultural

[051-019]labourers, an interesting and better-informed class, ranking distinctly above

[051-020]the former—the class to which Tess’s father and mother had

[051-021]belonged—and including the carpenter, the smith, the shoemaker, the

[051-022]huckster, together with nondescript workers other than farm-labourers; a set of

[051-023]people who owed a certain stability of aim and conduct to the fact of their

[051-024]being lifeholders like Tess’s father, or copyholders, or occasionally,

[051-025]small freeholders. But as the long holdings fell in, they were seldom again let

[051-026]to similar tenants, and were mostly pulled down, if not absolutely required by

[051-027]the farmer for his hands. Cottagers who were not directly employed on the land

[051-028]were looked upon with disfavour, and the banishment of some starved the trade

[051-029]of others, who were thus obliged to follow. These families, who had formed the

[051-030]backbone of the village life in the past, who were the depositaries of the

[051-031]village traditions, had to seek refuge in the large centres; the process,

[051-032]humorously designated by statisticians as “the tendency of the rural

[051-033]population towards the large towns”, being really the tendency of water

[051-034]to flow uphill when forced by machinery.

[051-035]The cottage accommodation at Marlott having been in this manner considerably

[051-036]curtailed by demolitions, every house which remained standing was required by

[051-037]the agriculturist for his work-people. Ever since the occurrence of the event

[051-038]which had cast such a shadow over Tess’s life, the Durbeyfield family

[051-039](whose descent was not credited) had been tacitly looked on as one which would

[051-040]have to go when their lease ended, if only in the interests of morality. It

[051-041]was, indeed, quite true that the household had not been shining examples either

[051-042]of temperance, soberness, or chastity. The father, and even the mother, had got

[051-043]drunk at times, the younger children seldom had gone to church, and the eldest

[051-044]daughter had made queer unions. By some means the village had to be kept pure.

[051-045]So on this, the first Lady-Day on which the Durbeyfields were expellable, the

[051-046]house, being roomy, was required for a carter with a large family; and Widow

[051-047]Joan, her daughters Tess and ’Liza-Lu, the boy Abraham, and the younger

[051-048]children had to go elsewhere.

[051-049]On the evening preceding their removal it was getting dark betimes by reason of

[051-050]a drizzling rain which blurred the sky. As it was the last night they would

[051-051]spend in the village which had been their home and birthplace, Mrs Durbeyfield,

[051-052]’Liza-Lu, and Abraham had gone out to bid some friends goodbye, and Tess

[051-053]was keeping house till they should return.

[051-054]She was kneeling in the window-bench, her face close to the casement, where an

[051-055]outer pane of rain-water was sliding down the inner pane of glass. Her eyes

[051-056]rested on the web of a spider, probably starved long ago, which had been

[051-057]mistakenly placed in a corner where no flies ever came, and shivered in the

[051-058]slight draught through the casement. Tess was reflecting on the position of the

[051-059]household, in which she perceived her own evil influence. Had she not come

[051-060]home, her mother and the children might probably have been allowed to stay on

[051-061]as weekly tenants. But she had been observed almost immediately on her return

[051-062]by some people of scrupulous character and great influence: they had seen her

[051-063]idling in the churchyard, restoring as well as she could with a little trowel a

[051-064]baby’s obliterated grave. By this means they had found that she was

[051-065]living here again; her mother was scolded for “harbouring” her;

[051-066]sharp retorts had ensued from Joan, who had independently offered to leave at

[051-067]once; she had been taken at her word; and here was the result.

[051-068]“I ought never to have come home,” said Tess to herself, bitterly.

[051-069]She was so intent upon these thoughts that she hardly at first took note of a

[051-070]man in a white mackintosh whom she saw riding down the street. Possibly it was

[051-071]owing to her face being near to the pane that he saw her so quickly, and

[051-072]directed his horse so close to the cottage-front that his hoofs were almost

[051-073]upon the narrow border for plants growing under the wall. It was not till he

[051-074]touched the window with his riding-crop that she observed him. The rain had

[051-075]nearly ceased, and she opened the casement in obedience to his gesture.

[051-076]“Didn’t you see me?” asked d’Urberville.

[051-077]“I was not attending,” she said. “I heard you, I believe,

[051-078]though I fancied it was a carriage and horses. I was in a sort of dream.”

[051-079]“Ah! you heard the d’Urberville Coach, perhaps. You know the

[051-080]legend, I suppose?”

[051-081]“No. My—somebody was going to tell it me once, but

[051-082]didn’t.”

[051-083]“If you are a genuine d’Urberville I ought not to tell you either,

[051-084]I suppose. As for me, I’m a sham one, so it doesn’t matter. It is

[051-085]rather dismal. It is that this sound of a non-existent coach can only be heard

[051-086]by one of d’Urberville blood, and it is held to be of ill-omen to the one

[051-087]who hears it. It has to do with a murder, committed by one of the family,

[051-088]centuries ago.”

[051-089]“Now you have begun it, finish it.”

[051-090]“Very well. One of the family is said to have abducted some beautiful

[051-091]woman, who tried to escape from the coach in which he was carrying her off, and

[051-092]in the struggle he killed her—or she killed him—I forget which.

[051-093]Such is one version of the tale... I see that your tubs and buckets are

[051-094]packed. Going away, aren’t you?”

[051-095]“Yes, to-morrow—Old Lady Day.”

[051-096]“I heard you were, but could hardly believe it; it seems so sudden. Why

[051-097]is it?”

[051-098]“Father’s was the last life on the property, and when that dropped

[051-099]we had no further right to stay. Though we might, perhaps, have stayed as

[051-100]weekly tenants—if it had not been for me.”

[051-101]“What about you?”

[051-102]“I am not a—proper woman.”

[051-103]D’Urberville’s face flushed.

[051-104]“What a blasted shame! Miserable snobs! May their dirty souls be burnt to

[051-105]cinders!” he exclaimed in tones of ironic resentment. “That’s

[051-106]why you are going, is it? Turned out?”

[051-107]“We are not turned out exactly; but as they said we should have to go

[051-108]soon, it was best to go now everybody was moving, because there are better

[051-109]chances.”

[051-110]“Where are you going to?”

[051-111]“Kingsbere. We have taken rooms there. Mother is so foolish about

[051-112]father’s people that she will go there.”

[051-113]“But your mother’s family are not fit for lodgings, and in a little

[051-114]hole of a town like that. Now why not come to my garden-house at Trantridge?

[051-115]There are hardly any poultry now, since my mother’s death; but

[051-116]there’s the house, as you know it, and the garden. It can be whitewashed

[051-117]in a day, and your mother can live there quite comfortably; and I will put the

[051-118]children to a good school. Really I ought to do something for you!”

[051-119]“But we have already taken the rooms at Kingsbere!” she declared.

[051-120]“And we can wait there—”

[051-121]“Wait—what for? For that nice husband, no doubt. Now look here,

[051-122]Tess, I know what men are, and, bearing in mind the *grounds* of your

[051-123]separation, I am quite positive he will never make it up with you. Now, though

[051-124]I have been your enemy, I am your friend, even if you won’t believe it.

[051-125]Come to this cottage of mine. We’ll get up a regular colony of fowls, and

[051-126]your mother can attend to them excellently; and the children can go to

[051-127]school.”

[051-128]Tess breathed more and more quickly, and at length she said—

[051-129]“How do I know that you would do all this? Your views may

[051-130]change—and then—we should be—my mother would

[051-131]be—homeless again.”

[051-132]“O no—no. I would guarantee you against such as that in writing, if

[051-133]necessary. Think it over.”

[051-134]Tess shook her head. But d’Urberville persisted; she had seldom seen him

[051-135]so determined; he would not take a negative.

[051-136]“Please just tell your mother,” he said, in emphatic tones.

[051-137]“It is her business to judge—not yours. I shall get the house swept

[051-138]out and whitened to-morrow morning, and fires lit; and it will be dry by the

[051-139]evening, so that you can come straight there. Now mind, I shall expect

[051-140]you.”

[051-141]Tess again shook her head, her throat swelling with complicated emotion. She

[051-142]could not look up at d’Urberville.

[051-143]“I owe you something for the past, you know,” he resumed.

[051-144]“And you cured me, too, of that craze; so I am glad—”

[051-145]“I would rather you had kept the craze, so that you had kept the practice

[051-146]which went with it!”

[051-147]“I am glad of this opportunity of repaying you a little. To-morrow I

[051-148]shall expect to hear your mother’s goods unloading... Give me your

[051-149]hand on it now—dear, beautiful Tess!”

[051-150]With the last sentence he had dropped his voice to a murmur, and put his hand

[051-151]in at the half-open casement. With stormy eyes she pulled the stay-bar quickly,

[051-152]and, in doing so, caught his arm between the casement and the stone mullion.

[051-153]“Damnation—you are very cruel!” he said, snatching out his

[051-154]arm. “No, no!—I know you didn’t do it on purpose. Well, I

[051-155]shall expect you, or your mother and children at least.”

[051-156]“I shall not come—I have plenty of money!” she cried.

[051-157]“Where?”

[051-158]“At my father-in-law’s, if I ask for it.”

[051-159]“ *If* you ask for it. But you won’t, Tess; I know you;

[051-160]you’ll never ask for it—you’ll starve first!”

[051-161]With these words he rode off. Just at the corner of the street he met the man

[051-162]with the paint-pot, who asked him if he had deserted the brethren.

[051-163]“You go to the devil!” said d’Urberville.

[051-164]Tess remained where she was a long while, till a sudden rebellious sense of

[051-165]injustice caused the region of her eyes to swell with the rush of hot tears

[051-166]thither. Her husband, Angel Clare himself, had, like others, dealt out hard

[051-167]measure to her; surely he had! She had never before admitted such a thought;

[051-168]but he had surely! Never in her life—she could swear it from the bottom

[051-169]of her soul—had she ever intended to do wrong; yet these hard judgements

[051-170]had come. Whatever her sins, they were not sins of intention, but of

[051-171]inadvertence, and why should she have been punished so persistently?

[051-172]She passionately seized the first piece of paper that came to hand, and

[051-173]scribbled the following lines:

[051-174]O why have you treated me so monstrously, Angel! I do not deserve it. I have

[051-175]thought it all over carefully, and I can never, never forgive you! You know

[051-176]that I did not intend to wrong you—why have you so wronged me? You are

[051-177]cruel, cruel indeed! I will try to forget you. It is all injustice I have

[051-178]received at your hands!

[051-179]T.

[051-180]She watched till the postman passed by, ran out to him with her epistle, and

[051-181]then again took her listless place inside the window-panes.

[051-182]It was just as well to write like that as to write tenderly. How could he give

[051-183]way to entreaty? The facts had not changed: there was no new event to alter his

[051-184]opinion.

[051-185]It grew darker, the fire-light shining over the room. The two biggest of the

[051-186]younger children had gone out with their mother; the four smallest, their ages

[051-187]ranging from three-and-a-half years to eleven, all in black frocks, were

[051-188]gathered round the hearth babbling their own little subjects. Tess at length

[051-189]joined them, without lighting a candle.

[051-190]“This is the last night that we shall sleep here, dears, in the house

[051-191]where we were born,” she said quickly. “We ought to think of it,

[051-192]oughtn’t we?”

[051-193]They all became silent; with the impressibility of their age they were ready to

[051-194]burst into tears at the picture of finality she had conjured up, though all the

[051-195]day hitherto they had been rejoicing in the idea of a new place. Tess changed

[051-196]the subject.

[051-197]“Sing to me, dears,” she said.

[051-198]“What shall we sing?”

[051-199]“Anything you know; I don’t mind.”

[051-200]There was a momentary pause; it was broken, first, in one little tentative

[051-201]note; then a second voice strengthened it, and a third and a fourth chimed in

[051-202]unison, with words they had learnt at the Sunday-school—

[051-203]Here we suffer grief and pain,

[051-204]Here we meet to part again;

[051-205]In Heaven we part no more.

[051-206]The four sang on with the phlegmatic passivity of persons who had long ago

[051-207]settled the question, and there being no mistake about it, felt that further

[051-208]thought was not required. With features strained hard to enunciate the

[051-209]syllables they continued to regard the centre of the flickering fire, the notes

[051-210]of the youngest straying over into the pauses of the rest.

[051-211]Tess turned from them, and went to the window again. Darkness had now fallen

[051-212]without, but she put her face to the pane as though to peer into the gloom. It

[051-213]was really to hide her tears. If she could only believe what the children were

[051-214]singing; if she were only sure, how different all would now be; how confidently

[051-215]she would leave them to Providence and their future kingdom! But, in default of

[051-216]that, it behoved her to do something; to be their Providence; for to Tess, as

[051-217]to not a few millions of others, there was ghastly satire in the poet’s

[051-218]lines—

[051-219]Not in utter nakedness

[051-220]But trailing clouds of glory do we come.

[051-221]To her and her like, birth itself was an ordeal of degrading personal

[051-222]compulsion, whose gratuitousness nothing in the result seemed to justify, and

[051-223]at best could only palliate.

[051-224]In the shades of the wet road she soon discerned her mother with tall

[051-225]’Liza-Lu and Abraham. Mrs Durbeyfield’s pattens clicked up to the

[051-226]door, and Tess opened it.

[051-227]“I see the tracks of a horse outside the window,” said Joan.

[051-228]“Hev somebody called?”

[051-229]“No,” said Tess.

[051-230]The children by the fire looked gravely at her, and one murmured—

[051-231]“Why, Tess, the gentleman a-horseback!”

[051-232]“He didn’t call,” said Tess. “He spoke to me in

[051-233]passing.”

[051-234]“Who was the gentleman?” asked the mother. “Your

[051-235]husband?”

[051-236]“No. He’ll never, never come,” answered Tess in stony

[051-237]hopelessness.

[051-238]“Then who was it?”

[051-239]“Oh, you needn’t ask. You’ve seen him before, and so have

[051-240]I.”

[051-241]“Ah! What did he say?” said Joan curiously.

[051-242]“I will tell you when we are settled in our lodging at Kingsbere

[051-243]to-morrow—every word.”

[051-244]It was not her husband, she had said. Yet a consciousness that in a physical

[051-245]sense this man alone was her husband seemed to weigh on her more and more.
