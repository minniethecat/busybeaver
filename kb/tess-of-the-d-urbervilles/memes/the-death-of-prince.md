# The Death of Prince

## Summary

The death of Prince, the Durbeyfield family's horse, is the inciting incident of the novel. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] and her younger brother [[abraham-durbeyfield|Abraham]] set out at night to deliver beehives to market. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] falls asleep at the reins, and the cart collides with the morning mail-cart. Prince is fatally impaled on the shaft of the mail-cart and dies on the road.

> The pointed shaft of the cart had entered the breast of the unhappy Prince like a sword, and from the wound his life's blood was spouting in a stream, and falling with a hiss into the road. (004-278)

The family loses its sole means of livelihood, and [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], consumed by guilt, agrees to visit the d'Urbervilles at [[trantridge|Trantridge]] to claim kinship.

> Prince lay alongside, still and stark; his eyes half open, the hole in his chest looking scarcely large enough to have let out all that had animated him. "'Tis all my doing—all mine!" the girl cried, regarding herself in the light of a murderess. (004-298)

## Context

The Durbeyfield family depends on Prince for their modest trade as higglers — buying and selling goods around [[Marlott]]. [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s drinking and his delusions of aristocratic ancestry have already strained the family's resources.

The night journey to deliver beehives is necessitated by John's inability to go himself after a day of celebrating his "noble" lineage (004-005). [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], despite being exhausted from the previous night's journey to [[chaseborough|Chaseborough]], takes her father's place alongside her brother [[abraham-durbeyfield|Abraham]] (004-007). The scene establishes [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s sense of responsibility and the family's precarious economic position.

During the drive, [[abraham-durbeyfield|Abraham]] asks the question that introduces the novel's cosmic dimension:

> "Did you say the stars were worlds, Tess?" "All like ours?" "I don't know; but I think so. They sometimes seem to be like the apples on our stubbard-tree. Most of 'em splendid and sound—a few blighted." (004-229)

## Key Participants

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] shoulders the burden of the night drive despite her exhaustion. Her falling asleep at the reins, an entirely human failing, becomes the source of overwhelming guilt that drives her to [[trantridge|Trantridge]]. This guilt makes her psychologically vulnerable to [[Alec d'Urberville]]'s subsequent manipulation.

**[[Abraham Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s younger brother accompanies her on the drive. His childish questions about the stars and whether they live on a "blighted" world introduce the novel's cosmic pessimism. [[abraham-durbeyfield|Abraham]] falls asleep during the drive, leaving [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] alone at the reins.

## Narrative Impact

The death of Prince is the novel's first turning point. It transforms [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] from a young woman content in her village life into an economic actor forced to confront the world beyond [[Marlott]]. The accident demonstrates Hardy's characteristic fatalism: a moment of human weakness — falling asleep — triggers catastrophic consequences that ripple through the entire narrative.

The event also introduces the theme of economic determinism. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] does not go to the d'Urbervilles out of ambition or desire but out of economic necessity. The family's poverty, not [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s character, sets her on the path to ruin. The mail-cart that kills Prince represents the modern world — punctual, mechanised, indifferent — crashing into the slow rhythms of rural life.

## Key Passages

- The starlit sky during the journey, with [[abraham-durbeyfield|Abraham]]'s meditation on the "blighted" world, establishes the novel's cosmic frame (004-229).
- The moment of the collision: the pointed shaft entering Prince's breast like a sword (004-278).
- [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s reaction: regarding herself "in the light of a murderess" as she looks upon the dead horse (004-298).

## Analysis

The death of Prince functions on multiple symbolic levels. Prince the horse bears the name of the Durbeyfield family's lost nobility — Sir Pagan d'Urberville was known as "[[john-durbeyfield|Sir John]]" in his day, and Prince carries that aristocratic echo. The horse's death thus symbolises the final extinction of any genuine claim to the d'Urberville legacy.

The scene also establishes the novel's moral framework. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is objectively innocent — her "crime" is human fatigue — yet she is consumed by guilt. This pattern — innocent woman punished by circumstances — recurs throughout the novel.

The death of Prince demonstrates that in Hardy's world, moral responsibility and actual culpability are tragically disconnected. This disconnect forms the basis of Hardy's critique of conventional morality.
