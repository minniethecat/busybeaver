# Fate and Destiny

## Definition

Fate and destiny in "[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles" operate on multiple levels. At the cosmic level, Thomas Hardy presents a universe governed not by divine providence but by blind chance or malevolent indifference.

At the social level, fate takes the form of inherited circumstances — class, gender, ancestry — that circumscribe individual agency. At the psychological level, fate manifests as the consequences of character: [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]]'s pride, her sense of guilt, her unwillingness to manipulate [[Angel Clare|Angel]].

The novel refuses to resolve these levels into a single explanation, maintaining a productive ambiguity between determinism and moral responsibility.

## Manifestations

**Ancestry as Fate** — The Durbeyfield family's noble lineage, discovered by chance and [[John Durbeyfield]]'s vanity, becomes the engine of [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s destruction. The irony is pointed: the family's "glorious" past as d'Urbervilles leads directly to [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s ruin (001-003).

**Chance and Coincidence** — The novel is structured around moments of fatal coincidence: the mail-cart that kills [[The Death of Prince|Prince]] (004-011), the letter that slips under [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s carpet (033-010), [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s failure to dance with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] at the [[May-Day Dance]] (002-010). These events suggest a universe in which meaning is retrospective — events become "fate" only in hindsight.

**The President of the Immortals** — The novel's final sentence invokes "the President of the Immortals" who had "ended his sport with [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]" (059-015). This classical reference to Aeschylus frames [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s suffering as cosmic sport, the plaything of indifferent gods. The ironic quotation marks around "President" and "sport" signal Hardy's distance from any literal belief in divine agency.

**Social Determinism** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s fate is also shaped by forces that are entirely human: the class system that makes her vulnerable to [[Alec d'Urberville|Alec]], the sexual double standard that condemns her for being a victim, the economic pressures that drive her back to her seducer. These are "fate" only in the sense that they operate beyond any individual's control.

## Key Passages

> The pair of legs that carried him were rickety, and there was a bias in his gait which inclined him somewhat to the left of a straight line. (001-001)

The novel's opening sentence describes [[John Durbeyfield]]'s walk, immediately linking physical imperfection to the theme of deviation from a destined path.

> But, might some say, where was Tess's guardian angel? Where was the providence of her simple faith? (011-024)

The narrator's direct address during [[The Chase Incident]], raising the problem of theodicy.

> "Justice" was done, and the President of the Immortals (in Aeschylean phrase) had ended his sport with Tess. (059-015)

The novel's devastating final sentence.

## Related Characters

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] is the primary vessel through which Hardy explores fate. Her repeated question — why me? — is the novel's central unanswered question.

**[[Angel Clare]]** — [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s intellectual rejection of Christian providence coexists with a practical fatalism. His belief that [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s past determines her moral worth reveals fate operating as ideology.

**[[Alec d'Urberville]]** — Alec embodies the role of the "villain" in the melodramatic tradition, but Hardy complicates this by showing Alec as himself shaped by class and circumstance.

## Analysis

Hardy's treatment of fate reflects his intellectual formation in the Victorian crisis of faith. Trained in biblical criticism and influenced by Darwin, Hardy could no longer believe in a benevolent providence, but he retained the narrative structures of theodicy — the attempt to justify suffering within a cosmic frame.

The result is a novel that asks theological questions (Is there justice? Does suffering have meaning?) while providing only negative answers (No. No.).

The novel's form embodies its fatalism. The subtitle — "A Pure Woman Faithfully Presented" — announces the conclusion before the narrative begins, as if the story could only end one way. The chapter titles, the narrative prolepsis, the authorial commentary all work to create a sense of inevitability.

Yet Hardy's realism complicates this determinism: [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s choices matter, her character matters, and the social conditions that destroy her are contingent and could be otherwise.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period saw intense debate about free will and determinism, fueled by advances in geology, biology, and biblical criticism. Darwin's theory of natural selection challenged the idea of divine design, while the Higher Criticism of the Bible undermined scriptural authority.

Hardy, who had considered a career in the church before turning to literature, was deeply engaged with these debates. His novels register the intellectual vertigo of a generation that had lost its faith in providence but could find no secular alternative that made suffering bearable.

The "President of the Immortals" reference to Aeschylus is significant. Greek tragedy offered Hardy a model of cosmic injustice — suffering without redemption — that he found more honest than Christian theodicy. But Hardy's irony (the quotation marks around "President") signals his awareness that even this classical framework is a literary construct, not a genuine explanation.
