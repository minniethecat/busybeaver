# The Family Eviction

## Summary

After [[john-durbeyfield|John Durbeyfield]]'s death, his family loses their lifehold cottage in [[marlott|Marlott]]. On Old Lady-Day — the traditional moving day for agricultural laborers — [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]] and her children load their furniture and belongings onto a hired wagon and join the annual migration of displaced rural families. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]], who has returned home from [[flintcomb-ash|Flintcomb-Ash]], walks alongside the wagon as the family departs the village that has been their home for generations. The eviction strips [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the last physical anchor connecting her to [[angel-clare|Angel Clare]], pushing her further toward dependence on [[alec-durberville|Alec d'Urberville]].

## Context

The eviction occurs against the backdrop of a broader social transformation. Hardy describes the "annual migrations from farm to farm" that have replaced the stable village life of earlier generations (051-008). The Durbeyfields are not alone — they are part of a systemic displacement of rural cottagers, whose traditional lifehold tenancies are being replaced by a more precarious labor market.

The family's vulnerability is compounded by their social status. Since [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s "fall," the Durbeyfields have been "tacitly looked on as one which would have to go when their lease ended, if only in the interests of morality" (051-038). The village enforces its moral code through economic exclusion.

## Key Participants

**[[Joan Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mother organizes the move with characteristic resilience, loading the clock — the family's prized possession — onto her lap to cushion its works from the wagon's jolts. [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan]]'s practicality masks the tragedy: a family that traces its lineage to the Norman conquerors is being carted away like refuse.

**[[Tess Durbeyfield]]** — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] walks beside the wagon with her younger sister, unable to sit inside with the family. Her position is symbolic: she belongs neither to the old life in [[marlott|Marlott]] nor to the uncertain future ahead. The eviction hardens her desperation, making Alec's offers of comfort increasingly difficult to refuse.

## Key Passages

**The Annual Migration**:
> These annual migrations from farm to farm were on the increase here. When Tess's mother was a child the majority of the field-folk about Marlott had remained all their lives on one farm, which had been the home also of their fathers and grandfathers. (051-008)

**Moral Exclusion**:
> Ever since the occurrence of the event which had cast such a shadow over Tess's life, the Durbeyfield family (whose descent was not credited) had been tacitly looked on as one which would have to go when their lease ended, if only in the interests of morality. (051-038)

**The Burden of the Clock**:
> the matron having in her lap, to prevent injury to its works, the head of the clock, which, at any exceptional lurch of the waggon, struck one, or one-and-a-half, in hurt tones. (052-032)

**The Departure**:
> Tess and the next eldest girl walked alongside till they were out of the village. (052-034)

## Narrative Impact

The family eviction represents the culmination of the Durbeyfield decline. The family that dreamed of noble ancestry at the novel's opening is now homeless, joining the anonymous stream of displaced rural laborers. The eviction strips [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of her last independent refuge. Without a home in [[marlott|Marlott]], she has no place to wait for [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s return — and Alec's offers of material security become correspondingly harder to resist.

Hardy uses the eviction to expose the economic forces reshaping rural England. The Durbeyfields' displacement is not a personal tragedy alone but a symptom of a society in which traditional rights of residence are being replaced by market logic. The family's Norman ancestry — the source of so much pride at the novel's opening — means nothing against the arithmetic of profit and loss.
