# Social Class

## Definition

Social class in *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] of the d'Urbervilles* refers to the stratified hierarchy of Victorian English society that governs every character's access to power, marriage, education, and moral standing. Hardy portrays class not merely as an economic reality but as an almost metaphysical force — an inheritance that, like the d'Urberville name itself, is simultaneously a source of pride and a curse.

## Manifestations

### The d'Urberville Name and False Nobility

The plot is set in motion by [[parson-tringham|Parson Tringham]]'s revelation that [[John Durbeyfield]] is descended from the ancient knightly family of d'Urberville. This discovery sparks [[joan-durbeyfield|Joan Durbeyfield]]'s ambition to send [[Tess Durbeyfield|Tess]] to "claim kin" with the wealthy Mrs d'Urberville — who is in fact [[Alec d'Urberville|Alec]]'s mother, bearing a purchased name with no actual connection to the ancient line. Hardy uses this coincidence to underline the emptiness of hereditary claims in a modern world where names can be bought.

### Middle-Class Morality and the Clare Family

[[Angel Clare]]'s family embodies the cultivated middle class. His father, [[Reverend James Clare]], is a clergyman; his brothers Cuthbert and Felix are university-educated. [[angel-clare|Angel]] himself initially rebels against this world, choosing to learn farming at [[Talbothays Dairy]] rather than enter the church. Yet when [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] confesses her past, [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s reaction reveals that he has not escaped middle-class sexual morality: "I should not have let it go on to marriage with you if I had" (036-074). Hardy shows that class prejudices are internalised — they persist even in those who consciously reject them.

### Agricultural Labourers

The Durbeyfield family represents the rural labouring class, for whom a single misfortune — the death of [[Death of Prince|Prince]] the horse — can mean destitution. Hardy describes them as "an interesting and better-informed class, ranking distinctly above" the purely manual labourer (051-019), yet their precarious position is constantly emphasised. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]] moves between classes throughout the novel: from labourer's daughter to "cousin" at [[The Slopes]], to dairymaid at [[talbothays-dairy|Talbothays]], to field worker at [[Flintcomb-Ash]].

## Key Passages

> Having latterly been extended from the cultivated middle-class into the rural community, had taught him how much less was the intrinsic difference between the good and wise woman of one social stratum and the good and wise woman of another social stratum, than between the good and bad, the wise and the foolish, of the same stratum or class. (026-116–026-120)

> Among these on-lookers were three young men of a superior class, carrying small knapsacks strapped to their shoulders... (002-141)

> "Well, it is true, in one sense. I do hate the aristocratic principle of blood before everything." (030-136)

[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s father, on learning of his lineage: the discovery that he is "the lineal representative of the ancient and knightly family of the d'Urbervilles" (001-026) transforms his sense of self-worth overnight, illustrating how class identity in Victorian England was tied to ancestry rather than achievement.

## Related Characters

- [[Tess Durbeyfield]] — moves across class boundaries throughout the novel
- [[Angel Clare]] — middle-class rebel who cannot escape his upbringing
- [[Alec d'Urberville]] — embodies the predatory aristocracy, though his name is purchased
- [[John Durbeyfield]] — the rural labourer intoxicated by the dream of noble lineage
- [[Joan Durbeyfield]] — [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s mother, who sees marriage to a gentleman as the only route to social ascent
- [[Reverend James Clare]] — represents respectable middle-class religious values

## Analysis

Hardy's treatment of social class is fundamentally pessimistic — he shows class boundaries to be permeable only in the downward direction. [[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]'s attempt to rise through the d'Urberville connection leads to her ruin; [[angel-clare|Angel]]'s attempt to marry below his class leads to tragedy. The novel suggests that class, like fate, is an inheritance that cannot be escaped.

The d'Urberville name functions as a symbol for the emptiness of hereditary class pride: the ancient line has decayed, and the name has been purchased by a nouveau riche manufacturer from the north of England. This double fraud — the false lineage and the false name — mirrors the novel's larger critique of a society that judges worth by birth rather than character.

## Historical Context

Victorian England was a society in transition. The old feudal order was giving way to industrial capitalism, and class boundaries were simultaneously rigid and newly unstable. The 1870 Education Act had created new possibilities for social mobility, but the moral codes of the middle class — particularly around female sexuality — were more strictly enforced than ever. Hardy wrote *[[tess-durbeyfield|Tess]]* against this backdrop, and the novel's indictment of "the President of the Immortals" (059-010) is also an indictment of a class system that destroys the innocent.
