[042-001]It was now broad day, and she started again, emerging cautiously upon the

[042-002]highway. But there was no need for caution; not a soul was at hand, and Tess

[042-003]went onward with fortitude, her recollection of the birds’ silent

[042-004]endurance of their night of agony impressing upon her the relativity of sorrows

[042-005]and the tolerable nature of her own, if she could once rise high enough to

[042-006]despise opinion. But that she could not do so long as it was held by Clare.

[042-007]She reached Chalk-Newton, and breakfasted at an inn, where several young men

[042-008]were troublesomely complimentary to her good looks. Somehow she felt hopeful,

[042-009]for was it not possible that her husband also might say these same things to

[042-010]her even yet? She was bound to take care of herself on the chance of it, and

[042-011]keep off these casual lovers. To this end Tess resolved to run no further risks

[042-012]from her appearance. As soon as she got out of the village she entered a

[042-013]thicket and took from her basket one of the oldest field-gowns, which she had

[042-014]never put on even at the dairy—never since she had worked among the

[042-015]stubble at Marlott. She also, by a felicitous thought, took a handkerchief from

[042-016]her bundle and tied it round her face under her bonnet, covering her chin and

[042-017]half her cheeks and temples, as if she were suffering from toothache. Then with

[042-018]her little scissors, by the aid of a pocket looking-glass, she mercilessly

[042-019]nipped her eyebrows off, and thus insured against aggressive admiration, she

[042-020]went on her uneven way.

[042-021]“What a mommet of a maid!” said the next man who met her to a

[042-022]companion.

[042-023]Tears came into her eyes for very pity of herself as she heard him.

[042-024]“But I don’t care!” she said. “O no—I don’t

[042-025]care! I’ll always be ugly now, because Angel is not here, and I have

[042-026]nobody to take care of me. My husband that was is gone away, and never will

[042-027]love me any more; but I love him just the same, and hate all other men, and

[042-028]like to make ’em think scornfully of me!”

[042-029]Thus Tess walks on; a figure which is part of the landscape; a fieldwoman pure

[042-030]and simple, in winter guise; a gray serge cape, a red woollen cravat, a stuff

[042-031]skirt covered by a whitey-brown rough wrapper, and buff-leather gloves. Every

[042-032]thread of that old attire has become faded and thin under the stroke of

[042-033]raindrops, the burn of sunbeams, and the stress of winds. There is no sign of

[042-034]young passion in her now—

[042-035]The maiden’s mouth is cold

[042-039]Inside this exterior, over which the eye might have roved as over a thing

[042-040]scarcely percipient, almost inorganic, there was the record of a pulsing life

[042-041]which had learnt too well, for its years, of the dust and ashes of things, of

[042-042]the cruelty of lust and the fragility of love.

[042-043]Next day the weather was bad, but she trudged on, the honesty, directness, and

[042-044]impartiality of elemental enmity disconcerting her but little. Her object being

[042-045]a winter’s occupation and a winter’s home, there was no time to

[042-046]lose. Her experience of short hirings had been such that she was determined to

[042-047]accept no more.

[042-048]Thus she went forward from farm to farm in the direction of the place whence

[042-049]Marian had written to her, which she determined to make use of as a last shift

[042-050]only, its rumoured stringencies being the reverse of tempting. First she

[042-051]inquired for the lighter kinds of employment, and, as acceptance in any variety

[042-052]of these grew hopeless, applied next for the less light, till, beginning with

[042-053]the dairy and poultry tendance that she liked best, she ended with the heavy

[042-054]and course pursuits which she liked least—work on arable land: work of

[042-055]such roughness, indeed, as she would never have deliberately voluteered for.

[042-056]Towards the second evening she reached the irregular chalk table-land or

[042-057]plateau, bosomed with semi-globular tumuli—as if Cybele the Many-breasted

[042-058]were supinely extended there—which stretched between the valley of her

[042-059]birth and the valley of her love.

[042-060]Here the air was dry and cold, and the long cart-roads were blown white and

[042-061]dusty within a few hours after rain. There were few trees, or none, those that

[042-062]would have grown in the hedges being mercilessly plashed down with the quickset

[042-063]by the tenant-farmers, the natural enemies of tree, bush, and brake. In the

[042-064]middle distance ahead of her she could see the summits of Bulbarrow and of

[042-065]Nettlecombe Tout, and they seemed friendly. They had a low and unassuming

[042-066]aspect from this upland, though as approached on the other side from Blackmoor

[042-067]in her childhood they were as lofty bastions against the sky. Southerly, at

[042-068]many miles’ distance, and over the hills and ridges coastward, she could

[042-069]discern a surface like polished steel: it was the English Channel at a point

[042-070]far out towards France.

[042-071]Before her, in a slight depression, were the remains of a village. She had, in

[042-072]fact, reached Flintcomb-Ash, the place of Marian’s sojourn. There seemed

[042-073]to be no help for it; hither she was doomed to come. The stubborn soil around

[042-074]her showed plainly enough that the kind of labour in demand here was of the

[042-075]roughest kind; but it was time to rest from searching, and she resolved to

[042-076]stay, particularly as it began to rain. At the entrance to the village was a

[042-077]cottage whose gable jutted into the road, and before applying for a lodging she

[042-078]stood under its shelter, and watched the evening close in.

[042-079]“Who would think I was Mrs Angel Clare!” she said.

[042-080]The wall felt warm to her back and shoulders, and she found that immediately

[042-081]within the gable was the cottage fireplace, the heat of which came through the

[042-082]bricks. She warmed her hands upon them, and also put her cheek—red and

[042-083]moist with the drizzle—against their comforting surface. The wall seemed

[042-084]to be the only friend she had. She had so little wish to leave it that she

[042-085]could have stayed there all night.

[042-086]Tess could hear the occupants of the cottage—gathered together after

[042-087]their day’s labour—talking to each other within, and the rattle of

[042-088]their supper-plates was also audible. But in the village-street she had seen no

[042-089]soul as yet. The solitude was at last broken by the approach of one feminine

[042-090]figure, who, though the evening was cold, wore the print gown and the

[042-091]tilt-bonnet of summer time. Tess instinctively thought it might be Marian, and

[042-092]when she came near enough to be distinguishable in the gloom, surely enough it

[042-093]was she. Marian was even stouter and redder in the face than formerly, and

[042-094]decidedly shabbier in attire. At any previous period of her existence Tess

[042-095]would hardly have cared to renew the acquaintance in such conditions; but her

[042-096]loneliness was excessive, and she responded readily to Marian’s greeting.

[042-097]Marian was quite respectful in her inquiries, but seemed much moved by the fact

[042-098]that Tess should still continue in no better condition than at first; though

[042-099]she had dimly heard of the separation.

[042-100]“Tess—Mrs Clare—the dear wife of dear he! And is it really so

[042-101]bad as this, my child? Why is your cwomely face tied up in such a way? Anybody

[042-102]been beating ’ee? Not *he* ?”

[042-103]“No, no, no! I merely did it not to be clipsed or colled, Marian.”

[042-104]She pulled off in disgust a bandage which could suggest such wild thoughts.

[042-105]“And you’ve got no collar on” (Tess had been accustomed to

[042-106]wear a little white collar at the dairy).

[042-107]“I know it, Marian.”

[042-108]“You’ve lost it travelling.”

[042-109]“I’ve not lost it. The truth is, I don’t care anything about

[042-110]my looks; and so I didn’t put it on.”

[042-111]“And you don’t wear your wedding-ring?”

[042-112]“Yes, I do; but not in public. I wear it round my neck on a ribbon. I

[042-113]don’t wish people to think who I am by marriage, or that I am married at

[042-114]all; it would be so awkward while I lead my present life.”

[042-115]Marian paused.

[042-116]“But you *be* a gentleman’s wife; and it seems hardly fair

[042-117]that you should live like this!”

[042-118]“O yes it is, quite fair; though I am very unhappy.”

[042-119]“Well, well. *He* married you—and you can be unhappy!”

[042-120]“Wives are unhappy sometimes; from no fault of their husbands—from

[042-121]their own.”

[042-122]“You’ve no faults, deary; that I’m sure of. And he’s

[042-123]none. So it must be something outside ye both.”

[042-124]“Marian, dear Marian, will you do me a good turn without asking

[042-125]questions? My husband has gone abroad, and somehow I have overrun my allowance,

[042-126]so that I have to fall back upon my old work for a time. Do not call me Mrs

[042-127]Clare, but Tess, as before. Do they want a hand here?”

[042-128]“O yes; they’ll take one always, because few care to come.

[042-129]’Tis a starve-acre place. Corn and swedes are all they grow. Though I be

[042-130]here myself, I feel ’tis a pity for such as you to come.”

[042-131]“But you used to be as good a dairywoman as I.”

[042-132]“Yes; but I’ve got out o’ that since I took to drink. Lord,

[042-133]that’s the only comfort I’ve got now! If you engage, you’ll

[042-134]be set swede-hacking. That’s what I be doing; but you won’t like

[042-135]it.”

[042-136]“O—anything! Will you speak for me?”

[042-137]“You will do better by speaking for yourself.”

[042-138]“Very well. Now, Marian, remember—nothing about *him* if I get

[042-139]the place. I don’t wish to bring his name down to the dirt.”

[042-140]Marian, who was really a trustworthy girl though of coarser grain than Tess,

[042-141]promised anything she asked.

[042-142]“This is pay-night,” she said, “and if you were to come with

[042-143]me you would know at once. I be real sorry that you are not happy; but

[042-144]’tis because he’s away, I know. You couldn’t be unhappy if he

[042-145]were here, even if he gie’d ye no money—even if he used you like a

[042-146]drudge.”

[042-147]“That’s true; I could not!”

[042-148]They walked on together and soon reached the farmhouse, which was almost

[042-149]sublime in its dreariness. There was not a tree within sight; there was not, at

[042-150]this season, a green pasture—nothing but fallow and turnips everywhere,

[042-151]in large fields divided by hedges plashed to unrelieved levels.

[042-152]Tess waited outside the door of the farmhouse till the group of workfolk had

[042-153]received their wages, and then Marian introduced her. The farmer himself, it

[042-154]appeared, was not at home, but his wife, who represented him this evening, made

[042-155]no objection to hiring Tess, on her agreeing to remain till Old Lady-Day.

[042-156]Female field-labour was seldom offered now, and its cheapness made it

[042-157]profitable for tasks which women could perform as readily as men.

[042-158]Having signed the agreement, there was nothing more for Tess to do at present

[042-159]than to get a lodging, and she found one in the house at whose gable-wall she

[042-160]had warmed herself. It was a poor subsistence that she had ensured, but it

[042-161]would afford a shelter for the winter at any rate.

[042-162]That night she wrote to inform her parents of her new address, in case a letter

[042-163]should arrive at Marlott from her husband. But she did not tell them of the

[042-164]sorriness of her situation: it might have brought reproach upon him.
