[039-001]It was three weeks after the marriage that Clare found himself descending the

[039-002]hill which led to the well-known parsonage of his father. With his downward

[039-003]course the tower of the church rose into the evening sky in a manner of inquiry

[039-004]as to why he had come; and no living person in the twilighted town seemed to

[039-005]notice him, still less to expect him. He was arriving like a ghost, and the

[039-006]sound of his own footsteps was almost an encumbrance to be got rid of.

[039-007]The picture of life had changed for him. Before this time he had known it but

[039-008]speculatively; now he thought he knew it as a practical man; though perhaps he

[039-009]did not, even yet. Nevertheless humanity stood before him no longer in the

[039-010]pensive sweetness of Italian art, but in the staring and ghastly attitudes of a

[039-011]Wiertz Museum, and with the leer of a study by Van Beers.

[039-012]His conduct during these first weeks had been desultory beyond description.

[039-013]After mechanically attempting to pursue his agricultural plans as though

[039-014]nothing unusual had happened, in the manner recommended by the great and wise

[039-015]men of all ages, he concluded that very few of those great and wise men had

[039-016]ever gone so far outside themselves as to test the feasibility of their

[039-017]counsel. “This is the chief thing: be not perturbed,” said the

[039-018]Pagan moralist. That was just Clare’s own opinion. But he was perturbed.

[039-019]“Let not your heart be troubled, neither let it be afraid,” said

[039-020]the Nazarene. Clare chimed in cordially; but his heart was troubled all the

[039-021]same. How he would have liked to confront those two great thinkers, and

[039-022]earnestly appeal to them as fellow-man to fellow-men, and ask them to tell him

[039-023]their method!

[039-024]His mood transmuted itself into a dogged indifference till at length he fancied

[039-025]he was looking on his own existence with the passive interest of an outsider.

[039-026]He was embittered by the conviction that all this desolation had been brought

[039-027]about by the accident of her being a d’Urberville. When he found that

[039-028]Tess came of that exhausted ancient line, and was not of the new tribes from

[039-029]below, as he had fondly dreamed, why had he not stoically abandoned her in

[039-030]fidelity to his principles? This was what he had got by apostasy, and his

[039-031]punishment was deserved.

[039-032]Then he became weary and anxious, and his anxiety increased. He wondered if he

[039-033]had treated her unfairly. He ate without knowing that he ate, and drank without

[039-034]tasting. As the hours dropped past, as the motive of each act in the long

[039-035]series of bygone days presented itself to his view, he perceived how intimately

[039-036]the notion of having Tess as a dear possession was mixed up with all his

[039-037]schemes and words and ways.

[039-038]In going hither and thither he observed in the outskirts of a small town a

[039-039]red-and-blue placard setting forth the great advantages of the Empire of Brazil

[039-040]as a field for the emigrating agriculturist. Land was offered there on

[039-041]exceptionally advantageous terms. Brazil somewhat attracted him as a new idea.

[039-042]Tess could eventually join him there, and perhaps in that country of

[039-043]contrasting scenes and notions and habits the conventions would not be so

[039-044]operative which made life with her seem impracticable to him here. In brief he

[039-045]was strongly inclined to try Brazil, especially as the season for going thither

[039-046]was just at hand.

[039-047]With this view he was returning to Emminster to disclose his plan to his

[039-048]parents, and to make the best explanation he could make of arriving without

[039-049]Tess, short of revealing what had actually separated them. As he reached the

[039-050]door the new moon shone upon his face, just as the old one had done in the

[039-051]small hours of that morning when he had carried his wife in his arms across the

[039-052]river to the graveyard of the monks; but his face was thinner now.

[039-053]Clare had given his parents no warning of his visit, and his arrival stirred

[039-054]the atmosphere of the Vicarage as the dive of the kingfisher stirs a quiet

[039-055]pool. His father and mother were both in the drawing-room, but neither of his

[039-056]brothers was now at home. Angel entered, and closed the door quietly behind

[039-057]him.

[039-058]“But—where’s your wife, dear Angel?” cried his mother.

[039-059]“How you surprise us!”

[039-060]“She is at her mother’s—temporarily. I have come home rather

[039-061]in a hurry because I’ve decided to go to Brazil.”

[039-062]“Brazil! Why they are all Roman Catholics there surely!”

[039-063]“Are they? I hadn’t thought of that.”

[039-064]But even the novelty and painfulness of his going to a Papistical land could

[039-065]not displace for long Mr and Mrs Clare’s natural interest in their

[039-066]son’s marriage.

[039-067]“We had your brief note three weeks ago announcing that it had taken

[039-068]place,” said Mrs Clare, “and your father sent your

[039-069]godmother’s gift to her, as you know. Of course it was best that none of

[039-070]us should be present, especially as you preferred to marry her from the dairy,

[039-071]and not at her home, wherever that may be. It would have embarrassed you, and

[039-072]given us no pleasure. Your bothers felt that very strongly. Now it is done we

[039-073]do not complain, particularly if she suits you for the business you have chosen

[039-074]to follow instead of the ministry of the Gospel.... Yet I wish I could

[039-075]have seen her first, Angel, or have known a little more about her. We sent her

[039-076]no present of our own, not knowing what would best give her pleasure, but you

[039-077]must suppose it only delayed. Angel, there is no irritation in my mind or your

[039-078]father’s against you for this marriage; but we have thought it much

[039-079]better to reserve our liking for your wife till we could see her. And now you

[039-080]have not brought her. It seems strange. What has happened?”

[039-081]He replied that it had been thought best by them that she should go to her

[039-082]parents’ home for the present, whilst he came there.

[039-083]“I don’t mind telling you, dear mother,” he said, “that

[039-084]I always meant to keep her away from this house till I should feel she could

[039-085]come with credit to you. But this idea of Brazil is quite a recent one. If I do

[039-086]go it will be unadvisable for me to take her on this my first journey. She will

[039-087]remain at her mother’s till I come back.”

[039-088]“And I shall not see her before you start?”

[039-089]He was afraid they would not. His original plan had been, as he had said, to

[039-090]refrain from bringing her there for some little while—not to wound their

[039-091]prejudices—feelings—in any way; and for other reasons he had

[039-092]adhered to it. He would have to visit home in the course of a year, if he went

[039-093]out at once; and it would be possible for them to see her before he started a

[039-094]second time—with her.

[039-095]A hastily prepared supper was brought in, and Clare made further exposition of

[039-096]his plans. His mother’s disappointment at not seeing the bride still

[039-097]remained with her. Clare’s late enthusiasm for Tess had infected her

[039-098]through her maternal sympathies, till she had almost fancied that a good thing

[039-099]could come out of Nazareth—a charming woman out of Talbothays Dairy. She

[039-100]watched her son as he ate.

[039-101]“Cannot you describe her? I am sure she is very pretty, Angel.”

[039-102]“Of that there can be no question!” he said, with a zest which

[039-103]covered its bitterness.

[039-104]“And that she is pure and virtuous goes without question?”

[039-105]“Pure and virtuous, of course, she is.”

[039-106]“I can see her quite distinctly. You said the other day that she was fine

[039-107]in figure; roundly built; had deep red lips like Cupid’s bow; dark

[039-108]eyelashes and brows, an immense rope of hair like a ship’s cable; and

[039-109]large eyes violety-bluey-blackish.”

[039-110]“I did, mother.”

[039-111]“I quite see her. And living in such seclusion she naturally had scarce

[039-112]ever seen any young man from the world without till she saw you.”

[039-113]“Scarcely.”

[039-114]“You were her first love?”

[039-115]“Of course.”

[039-116]“There are worse wives than these simple, rosy-mouthed, robust girls of

[039-117]the farm. Certainly I could have wished—well, since my son is to be an

[039-118]agriculturist, it is perhaps but proper that his wife should have been

[039-119]accustomed to an outdoor life.”

[039-120]His father was less inquisitive; but when the time came for the chapter from

[039-121]the Bible which was always read before evening prayers, the Vicar observed to

[039-122]Mrs Clare—

[039-123]“I think, since Angel has come, that it will be more appropriate to read

[039-124]the thirty-first of Proverbs than the chapter which we should have had in the

[039-125]usual course of our reading?”

[039-126]“Yes, certainly,” said Mrs Clare. “The words of King

[039-127]Lemuel” (she could cite chapter and verse as well as her husband).

[039-128]“My dear son, your father has decided to read us the chapter in Proverbs

[039-129]in praise of a virtuous wife. We shall not need to be reminded to apply the

[039-130]words to the absent one. May Heaven shield her in all her ways!”

[039-131]A lump rose in Clare’s throat. The portable lectern was taken out from

[039-132]the corner and set in the middle of the fireplace, the two old servants came

[039-133]in, and Angel’s father began to read at the tenth verse of the aforesaid

[039-134]chapter—

[039-135]“Who can find a virtuous woman? for her price is far above rubies. She

[039-136]riseth while it is yet night, and giveth meat to her household. She girdeth her

[039-137]loins with strength and strengtheneth her arms. She perceiveth that her

[039-138]merchandise is good; her candle goeth not out by night. She looketh well to the

[039-139]ways of her household, and eateth not the bread of idleness. Her children arise

[039-140]up and call her blessed; her husband also, and he praiseth her. Many daughters

[039-141]have done virtuously, but thou excellest them all.”

[039-142]When prayers were over, his mother said—

[039-143]“I could not help thinking how very aptly that chapter your dear father

[039-144]read applied, in some of its particulars, to the woman you have chosen. The

[039-145]perfect woman, you see, was a working woman; not an idler; not a fine lady; but

[039-146]one who used her hands and her head and her heart for the good of others.

[039-147]‘Her children arise up and call her blessed; her husband also, and he

[039-148]praiseth her. Many daughters have done virtuously, but she excelleth them

[039-149]all.’ Well, I wish I could have seen her, Angel. Since she is pure and

[039-150]chaste, she would have been refined enough for me.”

[039-151]Clare could bear this no longer. His eyes were full of tears, which seemed like

[039-152]drops of molten lead. He bade a quick good night to these sincere and simple

[039-153]souls whom he loved so well; who knew neither the world, the flesh, nor the

[039-154]devil in their own hearts, only as something vague and external to themselves.

[039-155]He went to his own chamber.

[039-156]His mother followed him, and tapped at his door. Clare opened it to discover

[039-157]her standing without, with anxious eyes.

[039-158]“Angel,” she asked, “is there something wrong that you go

[039-159]away so soon? I am quite sure you are not yourself.”

[039-160]“I am not, quite, mother,” said he.

[039-161]“About her? Now, my son, I know it is that—I know it is about her!

[039-162]Have you quarrelled in these three weeks?”

[039-163]“We have not exactly quarrelled,” he said. “But we have had a

[039-164]difference—”

[039-165]“Angel—is she a young woman whose history will bear

[039-166]investigation?”

[039-167]With a mother’s instinct Mrs Clare had put her finger on the kind of

[039-168]trouble that would cause such a disquiet as seemed to agitate her son.

[039-169]“She is spotless!” he replied; and felt that if it had sent him to

[039-170]eternal hell there and then he would have told that lie.

[039-171]“Then never mind the rest. After all, there are few purer things in

[039-172]nature then an unsullied country maid. Any crudeness of manner which may offend

[039-173]your more educated sense at first, will, I am sure, disappear under the

[039-174]influence or your companionship and tuition.”

[039-175]Such terrible sarcasm of blind magnanimity brought home to Clare the secondary

[039-176]perception that he had utterly wrecked his career by this marriage, which had

[039-177]not been among his early thoughts after the disclosure. True, on his own

[039-178]account he cared very little about his career; but he had wished to make it at

[039-179]least a respectable one on account of his parents and brothers. And now as he

[039-180]looked into the candle its flame dumbly expressed to him that it was made to

[039-181]shine on sensible people, and that it abhorred lighting the face of a dupe and

[039-182]a failure.

[039-183]When his agitation had cooled he would be at moments incensed with his poor

[039-184]wife for causing a situation in which he was obliged to practise deception on

[039-185]his parents. He almost talked to her in his anger, as if she had been in the

[039-186]room. And then her cooing voice, plaintive in expostulation, disturbed the

[039-187]darkness, the velvet touch of her lips passed over his brow, and he could

[039-188]distinguish in the air the warmth of her breath.

[039-189]This night the woman of his belittling deprecations was thinking how great and

[039-190]good her husband was. But over them both there hung a deeper shade than the

[039-191]shade which Angel Clare perceived, namely, the shade of his own limitations.

[039-192]With all his attempted independence of judgement this advanced and well-meaning

[039-193]young man, a sample product of the last five-and-twenty years, was yet the

[039-194]slave to custom and conventionality when surprised back into his early

[039-195]teachings. No prophet had told him, and he was not prophet enough to tell

[039-196]himself, that essentially this young wife of his was as deserving of the praise

[039-197]of King Lemuel as any other woman endowed with the same dislike of evil, her

[039-198]moral value having to be reckoned not by achievement but by tendency. Moreover,

[039-199]the figure near at hand suffers on such occasion, because it shows up its

[039-200]sorriness without shade; while vague figures afar off are honoured, in that

[039-201]their distance makes artistic virtues of their stains. In considering what Tess

[039-202]was not, he overlooked what she was, and forgot that the defective can be more

[039-203]than the entire.
