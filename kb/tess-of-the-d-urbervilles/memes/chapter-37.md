[037-001]Midnight came and passed silently, for there was nothing to announce it in the

[037-002]Valley of the Froom.

[037-003]Not long after one o’clock there was a slight creak in the darkened

[037-004]farmhouse once the mansion of the d’Urbervilles. Tess, who used the upper

[037-005]chamber, heard it and awoke. It had come from the corner step of the staircase,

[037-006]which, as usual, was loosely nailed. She saw the door of her bedroom open, and

[037-007]the figure of her husband crossed the stream of moonlight with a curiously

[037-008]careful tread. He was in his shirt and trousers only, and her first flush of

[037-009]joy died when she perceived that his eyes were fixed in an unnatural stare on

[037-010]vacancy. When he reached the middle of the room he stood still and murmured in

[037-011]tones of indescribable sadness—

[037-012]“Dead! dead! dead!”

[037-013]Under the influence of any strongly-disturbing force, Clare would occasionally

[037-014]walk in his sleep, and even perform strange feats, such as he had done on the

[037-015]night of their return from market just before their marriage, when he

[037-016]re-enacted in his bedroom his combat with the man who had insulted her. Tess

[037-017]saw that continued mental distress had wrought him into that somnambulistic

[037-018]state now.

[037-019]Her loyal confidence in him lay so deep down in her heart, that, awake or

[037-020]asleep, he inspired her with no sort of personal fear. If he had entered with a

[037-021]pistol in his hand he would scarcely have disturbed her trust in his

[037-022]protectiveness.

[037-023]Clare came close, and bent over her. “Dead, dead, dead!” he

[037-024]murmured.

[037-025]After fixedly regarding her for some moments with the same gaze of unmeasurable

[037-026]woe, he bent lower, enclosed her in his arms, and rolled her in the sheet as in

[037-027]a shroud. Then lifting her from the bed with as much respect as one would show

[037-028]to a dead body, he carried her across the room, murmuring—

[037-029]“My poor, poor Tess—my dearest, darling Tess! So sweet, so good, so

[037-030]true!”

[037-031]The words of endearment, withheld so severely in his waking hours, were

[037-032]inexpressibly sweet to her forlorn and hungry heart. If it had been to save her

[037-033]weary life she would not, by moving or struggling, have put an end to the

[037-034]position she found herself in. Thus she lay in absolute stillness, scarcely

[037-035]venturing to breathe, and, wondering what he was going to do with her, suffered

[037-036]herself to be borne out upon the landing.

[037-037]“My wife—dead, dead!” he said.

[037-038]He paused in his labours for a moment to lean with her against the banister.

[037-039]Was he going to throw her down? Self-solicitude was near extinction in her, and

[037-040]in the knowledge that he had planned to depart on the morrow, possibly for

[037-041]always, she lay in his arms in this precarious position with a sense rather of

[037-042]luxury than of terror. If they could only fall together, and both be dashed to

[037-043]pieces, how fit, how desirable.

[037-044]However, he did not let her fall, but took advantage of the support of the

[037-045]handrail to imprint a kiss upon her lips—lips in the day-time scorned.

[037-046]Then he clasped her with a renewed firmness of hold, and descended the

[037-047]staircase. The creak of the loose stair did not awaken him, and they reached

[037-048]the ground-floor safely. Freeing one of his hands from his grasp of her for a

[037-049]moment, he slid back the door-bar and passed out, slightly striking his

[037-050]stockinged toe against the edge of the door. But this he seemed not to mind,

[037-051]and, having room for extension in the open air, he lifted her against his

[037-052]shoulder, so that he could carry her with ease, the absence of clothes taking

[037-053]much from his burden. Thus he bore her off the premises in the direction of the

[037-054]river a few yards distant.

[037-055]His ultimate intention, if he had any, she had not yet divined; and she found

[037-056]herself conjecturing on the matter as a third person might have done. So

[037-057]easefully had she delivered her whole being up to him that it pleased her to

[037-058]think he was regarding her as his absolute possession, to dispose of as he

[037-059]should choose. It was consoling, under the hovering terror of to-morrow’s

[037-060]separation, to feel that he really recognized her now as his wife Tess, and did

[037-061]not cast her off, even if in that recognition he went so far as to arrogate to

[037-062]himself the right of harming her.

[037-063]Ah! now she knew what he was dreaming of—that Sunday morning when he had

[037-064]borne her along through the water with the other dairymaids, who had loved him

[037-065]nearly as much as she, if that were possible, which Tess could hardly admit.

[037-066]Clare did not cross the bridge with her, but proceeding several paces on the

[037-067]same side towards the adjoining mill, at length stood still on the brink of the

[037-068]river.

[037-069]Its waters, in creeping down these miles of meadowland, frequently divided,

[037-070]serpentining in purposeless curves, looping themselves around little islands

[037-071]that had no name, returning and re-embodying themselves as a broad main stream

[037-072]further on. Opposite the spot to which he had brought her was such a general

[037-073]confluence, and the river was proportionately voluminous and deep. Across it

[037-074]was a narrow foot-bridge; but now the autumn flood had washed the handrail

[037-075]away, leaving the bare plank only, which, lying a few inches above the speeding

[037-076]current, formed a giddy pathway for even steady heads; and Tess had noticed

[037-077]from the window of the house in the day-time young men walking across upon it

[037-078]as a feat in balancing. Her husband had possibly observed the same performance;

[037-079]anyhow, he now mounted the plank, and, sliding one foot forward, advanced along

[037-080]it.

[037-081]Was he going to drown her? Probably he was. The spot was lonely, the river deep

[037-082]and wide enough to make such a purpose easy of accomplishment. He might drown

[037-083]her if he would; it would be better than parting to-morrow to lead severed

[037-084]lives.

[037-085]The swift stream raced and gyrated under them, tossing, distorting, and

[037-086]splitting the moon’s reflected face. Spots of froth travelled past, and

[037-087]intercepted weeds waved behind the piles. If they could both fall together into

[037-088]the current now, their arms would be so tightly clasped together that they

[037-089]could not be saved; they would go out of the world almost painlessly, and there

[037-090]would be no more reproach to her, or to him for marrying her. His last

[037-091]half-hour with her would have been a loving one, while if they lived till he

[037-092]awoke, his day-time aversion would return, and this hour would remain to be

[037-093]contemplated only as a transient dream.

[037-094]The impulse stirred in her, yet she dared not indulge it, to make a movement

[037-095]that would have precipitated them both into the gulf. How she valued her own

[037-096]life had been proved; but his—she had no right to tamper with it. He

[037-097]reached the other side with her in safety.

[037-098]Here they were within a plantation which formed the Abbey grounds, and taking a

[037-099]new hold of her he went onward a few steps till they reached the ruined choir

[037-100]of the Abbey-church. Against the north wall was the empty stone coffin of an

[037-101]abbot, in which every tourist with a turn for grim humour was accustomed to

[037-102]stretch himself. In this Clare carefully laid Tess. Having kissed her lips a

[037-103]second time he breathed deeply, as if a greatly desired end were attained.

[037-104]Clare then lay down on the ground alongside, when he immediately fell into the

[037-105]deep dead slumber of exhaustion, and remained motionless as a log. The spurt of

[037-106]mental excitement which had produced the effort was now over.

[037-107]Tess sat up in the coffin. The night, though dry and mild for the season, was

[037-108]more than sufficiently cold to make it dangerous for him to remain here long,

[037-109]in his half-clothed state. If he were left to himself he would in all

[037-110]probability stay there till the morning, and be chilled to certain death. She

[037-111]had heard of such deaths after sleep-walking. But how could she dare to awaken

[037-112]him, and let him know what he had been doing, when it would mortify him to

[037-113]discover his folly in respect of her? Tess, however, stepping out of her stone

[037-114]confine, shook him slightly, but was unable to arouse him without being

[037-115]violent. It was indispensable to do something, for she was beginning to shiver,

[037-116]the sheet being but a poor protection. Her excitement had in a measure kept her

[037-117]warm during the few minutes’ adventure; but that beatific interval was

[037-118]over.

[037-119]It suddenly occurred to her to try persuasion; and accordingly she whispered in

[037-120]his ear, with as much firmness and decision as she could summon—

[037-121]“Let us walk on, darling,” at the same time taking him suggestively

[037-122]by the arm. To her relief, he unresistingly acquiesced; her words had

[037-123]apparently thrown him back into his dream, which thenceforward seemed to enter

[037-124]on a new phase, wherein he fancied she had risen as a spirit, and was leading

[037-125]him to Heaven. Thus she conducted him by the arm to the stone bridge in front

[037-126]of their residence, crossing which they stood at the manor-house door.

[037-127]Tess’s feet were quite bare, and the stones hurt her, and chilled her to

[037-128]the bone; but Clare was in his woollen stockings and appeared to feel no

[037-129]discomfort.

[037-130]There was no further difficulty. She induced him to lie down on his own sofa

[037-131]bed, and covered him up warmly, lighting a temporary fire of wood, to dry any

[037-132]dampness out of him. The noise of these attentions she thought might awaken

[037-133]him, and secretly wished that they might. But the exhaustion of his mind and

[037-134]body was such that he remained undisturbed.

[037-135]As soon as they met the next morning Tess divined that Angel knew little or

[037-136]nothing of how far she had been concerned in the night’s excursion,

[037-137]though, as regarded himself, he may have been aware that he had not lain still.

[037-138]In truth, he had awakened that morning from a sleep deep as annihilation; and

[037-139]during those first few moments in which the brain, like a Samson shaking

[037-140]himself, is trying its strength, he had some dim notion of an unusual nocturnal

[037-141]proceeding. But the realities of his situation soon displaced conjecture on the

[037-142]other subject.

[037-143]He waited in expectancy to discern some mental pointing; he knew that if any

[037-144]intention of his, concluded over-night, did not vanish in the light of morning,

[037-145]it stood on a basis approximating to one of pure reason, even if initiated by

[037-146]impulse of feeling; that it was so far, therefore, to be trusted. He thus

[037-147]beheld in the pale morning light the resolve to separate from her; not as a hot

[037-148]and indignant instinct, but denuded of the passionateness which had made it

[037-149]scorch and burn; standing in its bones; nothing but a skeleton, but none the

[037-150]less there. Clare no longer hesitated.

[037-151]At breakfast, and while they were packing the few remaining articles, he showed

[037-152]his weariness from the night’s effort so unmistakeably that Tess was on

[037-153]the point of revealing all that had happened; but the reflection that it would

[037-154]anger him, grieve him, stultify him, to know that he had instinctively

[037-155]manifested a fondness for her of which his common-sense did not approve, that

[037-156]his inclination had compromised his dignity when reason slept, again deterred

[037-157]her. It was too much like laughing at a man when sober for his erratic deeds

[037-158]during intoxication.

[037-159]It just crossed her mind, too, that he might have a faint recollection of his

[037-160]tender vagary, and was disinclined to allude to it from a conviction that she

[037-161]would take amatory advantage of the opportunity it gave her of appealing to him

[037-162]anew not to go.

[037-163]He had ordered by letter a vehicle from the nearest town, and soon after

[037-164]breakfast it arrived. She saw in it the beginning of the end—the

[037-165]temporary end, at least, for the revelation of his tenderness by the incident

[037-166]of the night raised dreams of a possible future with him. The luggage was put

[037-167]on the top, and the man drove them off, the miller and the old waiting-woman

[037-168]expressing some surprise at their precipitate departure, which Clare attributed

[037-169]to his discovery that the mill-work was not of the modern kind which he wished

[037-170]to investigate, a statement that was true so far as it went. Beyond this there

[037-171]was nothing in the manner of their leaving to suggest a *fiasco* , or that

[037-172]they were not going together to visit friends.

[037-173]Their route lay near the dairy from which they had started with such solemn joy

[037-174]in each other a few days back, and as Clare wished to wind up his business with

[037-175]Mr Crick, Tess could hardly avoid paying Mrs Crick a call at the same time,

[037-176]unless she would excite suspicion of their unhappy state.

[037-177]To make the call as unobtrusive as possible, they left the carriage by the

[037-178]wicket leading down from the high road to the dairy-house, and descended the

[037-179]track on foot, side by side. The withy-bed had been cut, and they could see

[037-183]their first embrace. The gold of the summer picture was now gray, the colours

[037-184]mean, the rich soil mud, and the river cold.

[037-185]Over the barton-gate the dairyman saw them, and came forward, throwing into his

[037-186]face the kind of jocularity deemed appropriate in Talbothays and its vicinity

[037-187]on the re-appearance of the newly-married. Then Mrs Crick emerged from the

[037-188]house, and several others of their old acquaintance, though Marian and Retty

[037-189]did not seem to be there.

[037-190]Tess valiantly bore their sly attacks and friendly humours, which affected her

[037-191]far otherwise than they supposed. In the tacit agreement of husband and wife to

[037-192]keep their estrangement a secret they behaved as would have been ordinary. And

[037-195]home to her father’s, and Marian had left to look for employment

[037-196]elsewhere. They feared she would come to no good.

[037-208]“How onnatural the brightness of her eyes did seem, and how they stood

[037-210]’ee that ’twas so? Tess had always sommat strange in her, and

[037-211]she’s not now quite like the proud young bride of a well-be-doing

[037-221]asked her to accompany him for a few steps on foot along one of the branch

[037-223]away.

[037-224]“Now, let us understand each other,” he said gently. “There

[037-225]is no anger between us, though there is that which I cannot endure at present.

[037-226]I will try to bring myself to endure it. I will let you know where I go to as

[037-227]soon as I know myself. And if I can bring myself to bear it—if it is

[037-228]desirable, possible—I will come to you. But until I come to you it will

[037-231]clearly enough; he could regard her in no other light than that of one who had

[037-232]practised gross deceit upon him. Yet could a woman who had done even what she

[037-233]had done deserve all this? But she could contest the point with him no further.

[037-234]She simply repeated after him his own words.

[037-235]“Until you come to me I must not try to come to you?”

[037-236]“Just so.”

[037-237]“May I write to you?”

[037-238]“O yes—if you are ill, or want anything at all. I hope that will

[037-239]not be the case; so that it may happen that I write first to you.”

[037-240]“I agree to the conditions, Angel; because you know best what my

[037-241]punishment ought to be; only—only—don’t make it more than I

[037-244]scene, fainted, wept hysterically, in that lonely lane, notwithstanding the
