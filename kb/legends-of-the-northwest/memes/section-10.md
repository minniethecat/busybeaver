[009-001] \[a\] My Sister.

[009-002] \"I pity Winona,\" he said,

[009-003] \"but my path is a pathway of danger,

[009-004] And long is the trail for the maid

[009-005] to the far-away land of the sunrise;

[009-006] And few are the braves of my band,

[009-007] and the braves of Tamdóka are many;

[009-008] But soon I return to the land,

[009-009] and a cloud of my hunters will follow.

[009-010] When the cold winds of winter return,

[009-011] and toss the white robes of the prairies,

[009-012] The fire of the White Chief will burn

[009-013] in his lodge at the Meeting-of-Waters; \[a\]

[009-014] And when from the Sunrise again

[009-015] comes the chief of the suns of the Morning,

[009-016] Many moons will his hunters remain

[009-017] in the land of the friendly [[dakota|Dakotas]].

[009-018] The son of Chief Wazí-kuté

[009-019] guides the White Chief afar on his journey;

[009-020] Nor long on the Tonka Medé \[b\]

[009-021] \--on the breast of the blue, bounding billows\--

[009-022] Shall the bark of the Frenchman delay,

[009-023] but his pathway shall kindle behind him.\"

[009-024] \[a\] Mendota, properly _Mdó-tè_\--meaning the outlet of lake or river into

[009-025] another,

[009-026] commonly applied to the region about Fort Snelling.

[009-027] \[b\] Tonka Mede\--Great Lake, i.e. [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]]. The Dakotas seem to have

[009-028] had no other name for it. They generally referred to it as

[009-029] _Mini-ya-ta\--There at the water._

[009-030] She was pale, and her hurried voice swelled

[009-031] with alarm as she questioned replying

[009-032] \"Tamdóka thy guide?

[009-033] \--I beheld thy death in his face at the races!

[009-034] He covers his heart with a smile,

[009-035] but revenge never sleeps in his bosom;

[009-036] His tongue\--it is soft to beguile;

[009-037] but beware of the pur of the panther!

[009-038] For death, like a shadow,

[009-039] will walk by thy side in the midst of the forest,

[009-040] Or follow thy path like a hawk

[009-041] on the trail of a wounded Mastinca. \[a\]

[009-042] A son of Unktéhee is he,

[009-043] \--the Chief of the crafty magicians;

[009-044] They have plotted thy death; I foresee,

[009-045] and thy trail, it is red in the forest;

[009-046] Beware of Tamdóka,\--beware.

[009-047] Slumber not like the grouse of the woodlands,

[009-048] With head under wing,

[009-049] for the glare of the eyes that sleep not are upon thee.\"

[009-050] \[a\] The rabbit. The Dakotas called the Crees \"Mastincapi\"\--Rabbits.

[009-051] \"Winona, fear not,\" said Duluth,

[009-052] \"for I carry the fire of Wakínyan, \[a\]

[009-053] And strong is the arm of my youth,

[009-054] and stout are the hearts of my warriors;

[009-055] But Winona has spoken the truth,

[009-056] and the heart of the White Chief is thankful.

[009-057] Hide this in thy bosom, dear maid,

[009-058] \--\'tis the crucified Christ of the white men. \[b\]

[009-059] Lift thy voice to his spirit in need,

[009-060] and his spirit will hear thee and answer;

[009-061] For often he comes to my aid;

[009-062] he is stronger than all the Dakotas;

[009-063] And the Spirits of evil, afraid,

[009-064] hide away when he looks from the heavens.\"

[009-065] In her swelling brown bosom

[009-066] she hid the crucified Jesus in silver;

[009-067] \"Niwástè,\" \[c\] she sadly replied;

[009-068] in her low voice the rising tears trembled;

[009-069] Her dewy eyes turned she aside,

[009-070] and she slowly returned to the teepees.

[009-071] But still on the swift river\'s strand,

[009-072] admiring the graceful Winona,

[009-073] As she gathered, with brown, dimpled hand,

[009-074] her hair from the wind, stood the Frenchman.

[009-075] \[a\] i.e. a fire arm which the Dakotas compare to the roar of the wings of

[009-076] the Thunder-bird and the fiery arrows he shoots.

[009-077] \[b\] Duluth was a devout Catholic.

[009-078] \[c\] Nee-wahshtay\--Thou art good.

[009-079] To bid the brave White Chief adieu,

[009-080] on the shady shore gathered the warriors;

[009-081] His glad boatmen manned the canoe,

[009-082] and the oars in their hands were impatient.

[009-083] Spake the Chief of Isántees,

[009-084] \--\"A feast will await the return of my brother

[009-085] In peace rose the sun in the East,

[009-086] in peace in the West he descended.

[009-087] May the feet of my brother be swift,

[009-088] till they bring him again to our teepees;

[009-089] The red pipe he takes as a gift,

[009-090] may he smoke that red pipe many winters.

[009-091] At my lodge-fire his pipe shall be lit,

[009-092] when the White Chief returns to Kathága;

[009-093] On the robes of my tee shall he sit,

[009-094] he shall smoke with the chiefs of my people.

[009-095] The brave love the brave;

[009-096] and his son sends the Chief as a guide for his brother,

[009-097] By the way of the Wákpa Wakán \[a\]

[009-098] to the Chief at the Lake of the Spirits.

[009-099] \[a\] [[spirit|Spirit]] River, now called _Rum_ River.

[009-100] As light as the foot-steps of dawn

[009-101] are the feet of the stealthy Tamdóka,

[009-102] And he fears not the Máza Wakán; \[a\]

[009-103] he is sly as the fox of the forest.

[009-104] When he [[dance|dances]] the dance of red war

[009-105] all the hungry wolves howl by the Big Sea, \[b\]

[009-106] For they scent on the south-wind

[009-107] afar their feast on the bones of Ojibways.\"

[009-108] Thrice the Chief puffed the red pipe of peace,

[009-109] ere it passed to the lips of the Frenchman.

[009-110] Spake DuLuth,\--\"May the [[wakan|Great Spirit]]

[009-111] bless with abundance the Chief and his people;

[009-112] May their sons and their daughters increase,

[009-113] and the fire ever burn in their teepees.\"

[009-114] Then he waved with a flag his adieu

[009-115] to the Chief and the warriors assembled;

[009-116] And away shot Tamdóka\'s canoe

[009-117] to the strokes of ten sinewy hunters;

[009-118] And a white path he clove up the blue,

[009-119] bubbling stream of the swift Mississippi;

[009-120] And away on his foaming trail flew,

[009-121] like a Sea-Gull the bark of the Frenchman.

[009-122] Then merrily rose the blithe song

[009-123] of the _voyageurs_ homeward returning,

[009-124] And thus, as they glided along,

[009-125] sang the bugle-voiced boatmen in chorus:

SONG

[009-126] Home again! home again! bend to the oar!

[009-127] Merry is the life of the gay _[[voyageurs|voyageur]]_

[009-128] He rides on the river with his paddle in his hand,

[009-129] And his boat is his shelter on the water and the land.

[009-130] The clam in his shell and the water turtle too,

[009-131] And the brave boatman\'s shell is his birch bark canoe.

[009-132] So pull away, boatmen, bend to the oar;

[009-133] Merry is the life of the gay _voyageur_.

[009-135] Merry is the life of the gay _voyageur_.

[009-136] His couch is as downy as a couch can be,

[009-137] For he sleeps on the feathers of the green fir-tree.

[009-138] He dines on the fat of the pemmican-sack,

[009-139] And his _eau de vie_ is the _eau de lac_.

[009-145] When he meets at the portage a red, forest maid,

[009-146] A Huron, or a Cree, or a blooming Chippeway;

[009-147] And he marks his trail with the _bois brulès_.

[009-152] \[a\] Fire arm\--spirit metal.

[009-153] \[b\] Lake Superior\--at that time the home of the Ojibways. (Chippewas)

[009-154] In the reeds of the meadow the stag

[009-155] lifts his branchy head stately and listens,

[009-156] And the bobolink, perched on the flag,

[009-157] her ear sidelong bends to the chorus.

[009-158] From the brow of the Beautiful Isle, \[a\]

[009-159] half hid in the midst of the maples,

[009-160] The sad-faced Winona, the while,

[009-161] watched the boat growing less in the distance.

[009-162] Till away in the bend of the stream,

[009-163] where it turned and was lost in the lindens,

[009-164] She saw the last dip and the gleam

[009-165] of the oars ere they vanished forever.

[009-166] Still afar on the waters the song,

[009-167] like bridal bells distantly chiming,

[009-168] The stout, jolly boatmen prolong,

[009-169] beating time with the stroke of their paddles;

[009-170] And Winona\'s ear, turned to the breeze,

[009-171] lists the air falling fainter and fainter

[009-172] Till it dies like the murmur of bees

[009-173] when the sun is aslant on the meadows.

[009-174] Blow, breezes,\--blow softly

[009-175] and sing in the dark, flowing hair of the maiden;

[009-176] But never again shall you bring

[009-177] the voice that she loves to Winona.

[009-178] \[a\] Wista Waste\--Nicollet Island.

[009-179] Now a light, rustling wind from the South

[009-180] shakes his wings o\'er the wide, wimpling waters;

[009-181] Up the dark winding river

[009-182] DuLuth follows fast in the wake of Tamdóka.

[009-183] On the slopes of the emerald shores

[009-184] leafy woodlands and prairies alternate;

[009-185] On the vine-tangled islands

[009-186] the flowers peep timidly out at the white men;

[009-187] In the dark-winding eddy the loon sits warily,

[009-188] watching and voiceless,

[009-189] And the wild goose, in reedy lagoon,

[009-190] stills the prattle and play of her children.

[009-191] The does and their sleek, dappled fawns

[009-192] prick their ears and peer out from the thickets,

[009-193] And the bison-calves play on the lawns,

[009-194] and gambol like colts in the clover.

[009-195] Up the still flowing Wákpa Wakán\'s winding path

[009-196] through the groves and the meadows.

[009-197] Now DuLuth\'s brawny boatmen

[009-198] pursue the swift gliding bark of Tamdóka;

[009-199] And hardly the red braves out-do

[009-200] the stout, steady oars of the white men.

[009-201] Now they bend to their oars in the race

[009-202] \--the ten tawny braves of Tamdóka;

[009-203] And hard on their heels in the chase

[009-204] ply the six stalwart oars of the Frenchmen.

[009-205] In the stern of his boat sits DuLuth,

[009-206] in the stern of his boat stands Tamdóka;

[009-207] And warily, cheerily,

[009-208] both urge the oars of their men to the utmost.

[009-209] Far-stretching away to the eyes,

[009-210] winding blue in the midst of the meadows,

[009-211] As a necklet of sapphires

[009-212] that lies unclaspt in the lap of a virgin,

[009-213] Here asleep in the lap of the plain

[009-214] lies the reed-bordered, beautiful river.

[009-215] Like two flying coursers that strain,

[009-216] on the track, neck and neck, on the home-stretch,

[009-217] With nostrils distended, and mane froth-flecked,

[009-218] and the neck and the shoulders,

[009-219] Each urged to his best by the cry

[009-220] and the whip and the rein of his rider,

[009-221] Now they skim o\'er the waters and fly,

[009-222] side by side, neck and neck, through the meadows.

[009-223] The blue heron flaps from the reeds,

[009-224] and away wings her course up the river;

[009-225] Straight and swift is her flight o\'er the meads,

[009-226] but she hardly outstrips the canoemen.

[009-227] See! the _voyageurs_ bend to their oars

[009-228] till the blue veins swell out on their foreheads;

[009-229] And the sweat from their brawny breasts pours;

[009-230] but in vain their Herculean labor;

[009-231] For the oars of Tamdôka are ten,

[009-232] and but six are the oars of the Frenchmen,

[009-233] And the red warriors\' burden of men

[009-234] is matched by the _voyageur\'s_ luggage.

[009-235] Side by side, neck and neck, for a mile,

[009-236] still they strain their strong arms to the utmost,

[009-237] Till rounding a willowy isle, now ahead creeps the boat of Tamdóka,

[009-238] And the neighboring forests profound,

[009-239] and the far-stretching plain of the meadows

[009-240] To the whoop of the victors resound,

[009-241] while the panting French rest on their paddles.

[009-242] With sable wings wide o\'er the land,

[009-243] night sprinkles the dew of the heavens;

[009-244] And hard by the dark river\'s strand,

[009-245] in the midst of a tall, somber forest,

[009-246] Two camp-fires are lighted, and beam

[009-247] on the trunks and the arms of the pine-trees.

[009-248] In the fitful light darkle and gleam

[009-249] the swarthy-hued faces around them.

[009-250] And one is the camp of DuLuth,

[009-251] and the other the camp of Tamdóka,

[009-252] But few are the jests and uncouth

[009-253] of the _voyageurs_ over their supper,

[009-254] While moody and silent the braves

[009-255] round their fire in a circle sit crouching;

[009-256] And low is the whisper of leaves

[009-257] and the sough of the wind in the branches;

[009-258] And low is the long-winding howl

[009-259] of the lone wolf afar in the forest;

[009-260] But shrill is the hoot of the owl,

[009-261] like a bugle blast blown in the pine-tops,

[009-262] And the half-startled _voyageurs_

[009-263] scowl at the sudden and saucy intruder.

[009-264] Like the eyes of the wolves are the eyes

[009-265] of the watchful and silent Dakotas;

[009-266] Like the face of the moon in the skies,

[009-267] when the clouds chase each other across it.

[009-268] Is Tamdóka\'s dark face in the light

[009-269] of the flickering flames of the camp fire.

[009-270] They have plotted red murder by night,

[009-271] and securely contemplate their victims.

[009-272] But wary and armed to the teeth

[009-273] are the resolute Frenchmen and ready,

[009-274] If need be, to grapple with death,

[009-275] and to die hand to hand in the desert.

[009-276] Yet skilled in the arts and the wiles

[009-277] of the cunning and crafty Algonkins,

[009-278] They cover their hearts with their smiles,

[009-279] and hide their suspicions of evil.

[009-280] Round their low, smouldering fire,

[009-281] feigning sleep, lie the watchful and wily Dakotas;

[009-282] But DuLuth and his _voyageurs_ heap their fire

[009-283] that shall blaze till the morning,

[009-284] Ere they lay themselves snugly to rest,

[009-285] with their guns by their side on the blankets,

[009-286] As if there were none to molest

[009-287] but the ravening beasts of the forest.
