# Ojibway Nation

The **Ojibway Nation** (also *Chippewa* in English, *Ojibwa* in earlier transliteration) is the [[algonkin|Algonkin]]-speaking people of the [[Lake Superior|Great Lakes]] region. They are the rivals of the [[Dakota]] in [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]*, and their conflict with the [[dakota|Dakota]] drives much of the narrative tension in the legend cycles.

## Identity and Language

The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] are an [[Algonkin]] ([[algonkin|Algonquian]]) people, linguistically and culturally distinct from the Siouan-speaking [[dakota|Dakota]]. Their language belongs to the [[algonkin|Algonquian]] family, and they share cultural affinities with the Ottawa and Potawatomi peoples.

Gordon's footnotes acknowledge the dual naming:

> Lake Superior — at that time the home of the Ojibways. (Chippewas)
> (009-153)

## Homeland

The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] homeland centered on [[Lake Superior]] and extended westward toward the [[Mississippi River]]:

> The Ojibways call the St. Louis River...
> (010-119)

Their territory included the [[St. Louis River]] watershed, the [[Pictured Rocks]] region, and the islands of the great lake.

## Conflict with the Dakotas

The [[Dakota-Ojibway War|rivalry between Dakota and Ojibway]] is a central theme of Gordon's [[legend|legends]]. The two nations contested the same hunting grounds and trade routes:

> And the lance of a crouching Ojibway shone,
> (004-196)

The threat of [[ojibway|Ojibway]] attack was ever-present in [[dakota|Dakota]] life:

> To die by the cruel Ojibway's hand?
> (005-109)

## In Gordon's Legends

### The Winona Cycle

In the [[Winona]] cycle, an [[ojibway|Ojibway]] chief seeks [[winona|Winona]]'s hand in marriage, creating the central romantic and political conflict. The [[dakota|Dakota]] braves' hostility toward the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] suitor reflects the deep intertribal tensions:

> ...their feast on the bones of Ojibways.
> (009-107)

### The Sea-Gull

*The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]* legend is set entirely within [[ojibway|Ojibway]] territory on [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]]. The story centers on an [[ojibway|Ojibway]] maiden rather than a [[dakota|Dakota]] protagonist, giving voice to the other side of the cultural divide.

### Names and Meanings

Gordon records several [[ojibway|Ojibway]] place names that reveal their deep connection to the landscape:

| Name | Meaning | Reference |
|------|---------|-----------|
| *[[lake-superior|Gitchee Gumee]]* | Great Sea/Lake (Superior) | 010-118 |
| *Michee seebee* | Big River (Mississippi) | 010-116 |
| *Waub-ó-nong* | Sunrise / the East | 016-080 |

## Warfare and Signals

The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] used fire signals to communicate across distances — a tactic noted by their [[dakota|Dakota]] enemies:

> ...signal by their enemies, the Ojibways.
> (017-022)

In battle, the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] were described by the [[dakota|Dakota]] as "crafty and creeping" — a characterization that reflects the guerrilla tactics of woodland warfare:

> ...of the crafty and creeping Ojibways?
> (012-128)

## Historical Documentation

The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] were documented by early explorers, missionaries, and scholars. [[Henry H. Sibley]] and [[Jonathan Carver]] both recorded [[ojibway|Ojibway]] [[tradition|customs]]. Schoolcraft later collected [[ojibway|Ojibway]] [[legend|legends]], including versions of stories that appear in Gordon's collection (018-153).
