Mary Henderson Eastman (Mrs. Eastman) was an American author and historian. Her book "Dacotah; or, Life and [[legend|Legend]]s of the [[dakota|Sioux]] around [[fort-snelling|Fort Snelling]]" (1849) served as a primary ethnographic source for [[hanford-gordon|Hanford Lennox Gordon]]. He references her work extensively throughout his footnotes in "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest."

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: European-American
- **Role**: Author, ethnographer, historian of [[dakota|Dakota]] culture
- **Family**: Married to Seth Eastman, U.S. Army officer and illustrator at [[fort-snelling|Fort Snelling]]
- **Active Period**: Mid-19th century (1840s–1850s)

## 在书中的角色

### 学术来源

Gordon acknowledges Mrs. Eastman's work in his preface (P01-069). Her book "Dacotah" is the most frequently cited ethnographic reference in his footnotes.

### 内容贡献

Eastman's "Dacotah" provided Gordon with descriptions of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|customs]] across multiple topics.

Gordon cites Eastman on war [[tradition|customs]] and ceremonies (016-085), documenting [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior practices. Her accounts of [[soul|spirit]] beliefs describe forest-dwelling [[spirit|spirits]] in [[dakota|Dakota]] tradition (016-117). She also recorded geographic features and landmarks significant to the [[dakota|Dakotas]] (016-125).

Additional topics covered include the role and status of [[dakota|Dakota]] chiefs (017-006), [[dakota|Dakota]] concepts of evil [[spirit|spirits]] and cosmology (017-014), and religious beliefs referenced alongside Riggs' "Tah-koo Wah-kân" (017-081).

## 历史/文化背景

Mary Eastman lived at [[fort-snelling|Fort Snelling]] in present-day [[minnesota|Minnesota]] during the 1840s. Her husband Seth Eastman served as the fort's commanding officer and made sketches of [[dakota|Dakota]] life. Her first-hand observations and interviews of [[dakota|Dakota]] people made "Dacotah" one of the earliest ethnographic records of [[dakota|Dakota]] culture by a woman author.

Her work stands alongside that of [[stephen-riggs|Stephen R. Riggs]] and other early [[dakota|Dakota]] ethnographers. Together they formed the scholarly foundation for Gordon's poetical retellings.
