# Wigwam

The wigwam is the traditional dome-shaped dwelling of the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[chippewa|Chippewa]]) people, appearing prominently in Gordon's later [[legend|legends]] as the domestic center of [[ojibway|Ojibway]] life along [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]].

## Definition / Identity

A wigwam is a traditional dwelling built by the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] and related [[algonkin|Algonquian]] peoples, typically constructed with a frame of bent saplings covered with birch bark, mats, or hides:

- **Origin**: Indigenous to the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]]) and [[algonkin|Algonquian]] peoples of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s region
- **Language**: [[ojibway|Ojibway]] / [[algonkin|Algonquian]] (from *wigwâm*)
- **Domain**: dwelling

While "wigwam" and "lodge" are sometimes used interchangeably in English, Gordon consistently associates the wigwam with [[ojibway|Ojibway]] characters. He describes "a cluster of [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] wigwams" along the shore of [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]] (010-153), locating the wigwam within a specifically [[ojibway|Ojibway]] cultural landscape.

## In the Book

The wigwam appears most frequently in the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] legend (Sections 13–14) and the [[red-fox|Red Fox]] legend (Section 14), where it serves as the primary dwelling of [[ojibway|Ojibway]] characters.

### Role in the Legends

In the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] legend, the wigwam is the home from which the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] maiden departs and to which she returns. When the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] "sat...in the wigwam" (013-150), awaiting her lover's return from the hunt, the wigwam frames her domestic role. The phrase "had she ruled the humble wigwam" (013-240, 013-294) emphasizes her authority within the domestic sphere.

The wigwam also features in the [[red-fox|Red Fox]] story where [[kewaydin|Kewáydin]] calls from his wigwam (014-078) and the [[red-fox|Red Fox]] sits within the wigwam as the narrative reaches its climax (014-248). Gordon describes the wigwam as a place of waiting, watching, and domestic ritual.

Gordon's physical description — seeing "the smoke of the wigwams up-curling" (010-161) — evokes the sensory landscape of an [[ojibway|Ojibway]] village along [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]].

### Representative Passages

> "a cluster of Chippewa wigwams" (010-153)

> "Sat the Sea-Gull in the wigwam" (013-150)

> "Had she ruled the humble wigwam" (013-240)

> "In the wigwam sat the Red Fox" (014-248)
