# Chaské

A brave [[dakota|Dakota]] hunter and warrior, the devoted suitor of [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] in Gordon's "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" legend (Chapters II–V). Chaské's steadfast love and honorable courtship win him the hand of the fairest [[dakota|Dakota]] maiden, triumphing over rival suitors who rely on trickery and supernatural intimidation.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Hunter, warrior, suitor, later chief
- **Family**: Wife: [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] (005-205); younger brother: unnamed chief (004-132); the name Chaské is traditionally given to the first-born son among the [[dakota|Dakotas]] (016-003)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Chaské is the hero of "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" legend (Chapters II–V). He is introduced as a brave and honorable hunter whose genuine love for [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] contrasts with the schemes of rival suitors. Ultimately he proves himself worthy and wins [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]]'s hand.

### 关键事件

Chaské first appears in [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]]'s thoughts — she dreams "of your hunter — the brave Chaskè" (002-169). Her affection is deep: "My beautiful hunter — my brave Chaskè" (002-210). The refrain "my brave Chaskè" echoes throughout the legend, marking him as the beloved hero (003-011, 003-015, 003-021).

Chaské's reputation spreads far: "the nations spoke of the brave Chaskè" (003-050). A chief welcomes him to his lodge: "the chief to his lodge led the brave Chaskè" (003-053). He tarries through the seasons in his courtship: "nights he tarried — the brave Chaskè" (003-116).

The [[soul|spirit]] [[wakawa|Wakâwa]] promises aid to Chaské's suit: "For [[wakawa|Wakâwa]] promised the brave Chaskè" (003-173). Time passes — "the May-days pass and the brave Chaskè" (003-204) — and he rises in stature: "the brave young brother — the chief Chaskè" (004-132).

A dark period follows when no tidings come "nor the brave Chaskè" (004-260). [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]]'s father asks, "Where is the lover — the brave Chaskè?" (004-265). But Chaské returns, and [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] longs for "the sheltering lodge of my brave Chaskè" (005-059).

The reunion is triumphant: "Sat the manly form of her own Chaskè!" (005-135). [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] formally requests union with "the brave Chaskè" (005-152), and she is celebrated as "the beautiful bride of the brave Chaskè" (005-205). The legend closes with the image of [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] riding "with her proud Chaskè" (005-220), remembered "ever her beautiful, brave Chaskè" (005-236).

## 历史/文化背景

Chaské is a legendary figure whose name carries cultural significance: among the [[dakota|Dakotas]], "the first born son is called Chaskè" (016-003). Gordon's use of this name for the hero suggests Chaské represents the archetypal first-born son — brave, honorable, and destined for leadership. His quiet, steadfast courtship contrasts with the aggressive rivalry of [[tamdoka|Tamdóka]] in the parallel "[[winona|Winona]]" legend, presenting an alternative model of [[dakota|Dakota]] masculinity rooted in patience and genuine devotion.
