# Mille Lacs

**Mille Lacs** — *Mdé Waksân* in [[Dakota]], meaning "Spirit Lake" — is one of the principal sacred sites and settlements of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people, recorded in [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]* and in early French accounts of the upper Mississippi.

## Names

| People | Name | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| [[Dakota]] | *Mdé Waksân* | Spirit Lake (017-029) |
| French | *Lac Buade* | Named by DuLuth after Governor Frontenac (017-027) |
| French | *Mille Lacs* | Thousand Lakes |

The lake was discovered by [[Daniel Duluth|DuLuth]] and by him named *Lac Buade*, in honor of Governor Frontenac of Canada, whose family name was Buade (017-026–028).

## A Principal Dakota Settlement

Mille Lacs was one of the most important permanent [[dakota|Dakota]] settlements:

> There were certain places, like Mille Lacs, the Falls of St. Anthony, Kapóza (near St. Paul), Remnica, (where the city of Red Wing now stands), and Keúxa (or Keóza) on the site of the city of Winona, so frequently occupied by several of the bands as to be considered their chief villages respectively.
> (018-139–143)

## Early European Encounters

### Father Hennepin at Mille Lacs

When Father [[Louis Hennepin]] visited Mille Lacs in 1679–80, the head chief of the Isantee [[dakota|Dakota]] band was [[Wazi-kuté]] (*Wah-ze-koo-tay* — Pine-shooter):

> When Father Hennepin was at Mille Lacs in 1679-80, Wazi-kuté was the head Chief (Itâncan) of the band of Isantees.
> (017-161–162)

Hennepin was subjected to the vapour-bath (*inipi* / [[sweat-lodge|sweat lodge]] ceremony) at Mille Lacs by Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin:

> Father Hennepin was subjected to the vapour-bath at Mille Lacs by Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin, two hundred years ago. After describing the method Hennepin says: "When he had made me sweat thus three times in a week, I felt as strong as ever."
> (018-062–064)

### DuLuth's Exploration

[[Daniel Duluth|DuLuth]]'s discovery of the lake in 1679 placed Mille Lacs on the map of French North America. His naming of it *Lac Buade* honored the Governor of New France, establishing a French claim to the region (017-026–027).

## Cultural and Spiritual Significance

The [[dakota|Dakota]] considered certain places sacred, and Mille Lacs — *Mdé Waksân*, Spirit Lake — held a special place in their [[soul|spirit]]ual geography. The lake was the site of [[sweat-lodge|sweat lodge]] ceremonies and other [[ritual|rituals]], as recorded by Hennepin and later by [[Stephen R. Riggs]] in *Tah-Koo [[wakan|Wah-Kan]]* (018-060–062).
