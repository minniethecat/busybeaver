## 身份与命名

Hârpstinà (pronounced Hârps-te-nâh) is the Dakota birth-order name given to a **third-born daughter**. The Dakota system of child-naming assigns specific names by birth order: the first-born daughter is called Winona; the second, Hârpen; the third, Hârpstinà; the fourth, Wâska; and the fifth, Wehârka. These names are retained until replaced by names earned through deeds or distinctive traits. (015-205)

## 在传说中的角色

Hârpstinà appears as the principal antagonist in "The Feast of the Virgins," a Dakota legend rendered in verse by H. L. Gordon. She is introduced as the leader of the opposing team in a ceremonial ball game, where she faces Wiwâstè, the fair daughter of Little Crow.

### 球赛之争

The ball game is played on the frozen river, with a robe of black fox lined with beaver offered as prize. Hârpstinà leads one band of fifty maidens while Wiwâstè leads the other. Gordon describes Hârpstinà as "the dusky, impetuous Hârpstinà, the queenly cousin of Wapasa." (001-105) When Wiwâstè wins the contest, Hârpstinà reacts with fury:

> Dark was the visage of Hârpstinà
> When the robe was laid at her rival's feet,
> And merry maidens and warriors saw
> Her flashing eyes and her look of hate.
>
> (001-131)

She publicly accuses Wiwâstè of cheating, claiming she was struck on the head as she snatched the ball. Her accusation is dismissed with laughter, for "her lips were false and her charge untrue." (001-133)

### 爱情与嫉妒

Hârpstinà loves Mahpíya (Red Cloud), but his heart belongs to Wiwâstè. (001-186) Consumed by jealousy, she spies on her rival, crouching in the darkness outside Wiwâstè's teepee while the maiden dreams of her distant lover, the Hóhé hunter Chaskè. (002-177) In Wiwâstè's prophetic dream, the "hateful visage of Hârpstinà" appears among dark omens — a falling star, a wailing spirit, and the mysterious Kan-ó-ti-dan. (002-200)

### 处女节与复仇

At the Feast of the Virgins, Hârpstinà sits "aside with the mothers" — she dares not enter the Sacred Ring reserved for virgins. (004-012) Her exclusion from this sacred ceremony deepens her resentment. She turns to Red Cloud and "kindled revenge in his savage soul," (005-001) manipulating his pride and twisting his thwarted love into bitter hatred:

> I turned his love to a bitter hate;
> I nourished revenge, and I pricked his pride;
> Till the Feast of the Virgins I bade him wait.
>
> (005-002)

Red Cloud acts on her instigation but pays with his life. His death drives Hârpstinà into madness and fatal remorse.

### 悔恨与死亡

Racked by guilt and fever, Hârpstinà raves alone in her lodge, crying:

> "He is dead! He is dead! ... And the blame is mine, — it was I, — it was I!
> I hated Wiwâstè, for she was fair,
> And my brave was caught in her net of hair."
>
> (005-003)

She believes Red Cloud's angry spirit has entered her body — a Dakota belief in spirit-possession as the cause of illness. (015-221) She dies alone, wailing to the night winds, a victim of her own jealousy and the revenge she set in motion. (005-004)

## 性格特征

Hârpstinà embodies several destructive passions in the Dakota moral universe: **jealousy** (envy of Wiwâstè's beauty and victory), **pride** (her haughty bearing as cousin of Wapasa), **deceit** (spying and false accusation), and **vengeance** (manipulating Red Cloud to murderous action). Gordon consistently characterizes her with dark imagery — "dusky," "hateful visage," "venomous," "serpent" — casting her as a foil to the virtuous Wiwâstè. Yet her deathbed confession and remorse lend her the dimension of a tragic figure, destroyed by passions she could not control. (005-001)

## 文化意义

The character of Hârpstinà illustrates several Dakota cultural values through negative example: the sacredness of the Virgins' Feast and the consequences of violating its requirements; the destructive power of jealousy and revenge within a community; and the belief that a vengeful spirit can enter the body of the living to cause sickness and death. Her story forms the emotional core of "The Feast of the Virgins," driving the legend's central conflict and its tragic resolution. (015-205)(015-221)
