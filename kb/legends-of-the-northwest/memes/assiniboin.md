## 名称与起源

The **Assiniboins** are known to the Dakotas as the **Hó-hé** (or Hó-hây), meaning "Stone-roasters." They speak the Dakota language and are originally a band of the Dakota nation. Their separation from the parent tribe is attributed in Dakota tradition to a legendary quarrel — a Dakota "Helen" whose disputed beauty or loyalty sparked a bloody feud that lasted for many years. (016-040)

Their home territory lies in the region of the **Assiniboin River** in what is now British America (western Canada), north of the Dakota lands in present-day Minnesota. (016-041)

## 得名由来：石烤人

The name "Stone-roasters" derives from the distinctive cooking method traditionally used by the Hó-hé. Until recent times, they employed **wa-ta-pe kettles** — vessels made of the fibrous roots of the white cedar, interlaced and tightly woven. When soaked, these vessels became watertight. To cook their food, the Hó-hé heated stones in a fire and dropped them into the water-filled kettles, thus boiling water and cooking without the use of metal or clay pots. (016-041)

This method of stone-boiling was shared with other northern peoples; the Dakota themselves used similar techniques before the introduction of European trade goods.

## 与达科他人的关系

Despite their common language and origin, the Hó-hé and the Dakotas became hereditary enemies after their separation. The Dakota term for them carries a tone of contempt, and they appear in the narrative poems as adversaries. In the Feast of the Virgins, Wiwâstè dreams of her lover Chaskè — "my beautiful hunter — my brave Chaskè" — who resides "in the happy land of the tall Hóhé." (007-298) This reference, while romantic, underscores the separation between the related peoples.

The Assiniboins are identified with the **Knisteneaux** (Cree) by the explorer Alexander Mackenzie in his Travels (London, 1801). Their geographic position between the Dakota Sioux and the Cree placed them at a crossroads of intertribal relations on the northern plains. (016-041)

## 在传说中的出现

In H. L. Gordon's "Legends of the Northwest," the Hó-hé appear in the Feast of the Virgins as the distant people among whom the Hóhé hunter Chaskè dwells, separated from his beloved Wiwâstè. The mention is brief but significant — it situates the Hó-hé within the Dakota geographical imagination as a related but separate people dwelling in the northern lands. (007-298)
