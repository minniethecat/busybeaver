# Mendota

**Mendota** — properly *Mdó-tè* in [[Dakota]] — means "the outlet of lake or river into another" (009-024). It refers to the confluence of the [[Mississippi River|Mississippi]] and [[Minnesota|Minnesota]] Rivers, the heart of the [[dakota|Dakota]] homeland and the site later occupied by [[Fort Snelling]].

## The Name

The French rendering *Mendota* — translated as "meeting of the waters" — describes the dramatic confluence:

> Mendota — (meeting of the waters) at the confluence of the Mississippi and Minnesota rivers.
> (015-218–219)

The true [[dakota|Dakota]] word is *Mdó tè*, applied to the mouth of a river flowing into another, and also to the outlet of a lake (015-220–221). The name was:

> commonly applied to the region about Fort Snelling.
> (009-026)

## Historical Significance

### The English Trader

The earliest recorded event at Mendota marks a decisive moment in [[dakota|Dakota]] history. More than a century before [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s time:

> an English trader was killed at Mendota, because he persisted, after repeated warnings by the chiefs, in dealing out *mini-wakan* (Devil-water) to the Dakota braves.
> (P01-028–030)

This episode illustrates the [[dakota|Dakota]] leadership's resistance to the introduction of alcohol, which they recognized as a destructive force.

### The Fur Trade Era

Mendota's strategic location at the confluence made it a natural center for the [[fur trade]]. [[voyageurs|French voyageurs]] and traders used the meeting of the two rivers as a waypoint connecting the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s to the Mississippi watershed.

## Geography

The confluence at Mendota joins two of the region's greatest waterways. Near the source of the [[Minnesota|Minnesota River]] is "Thunder-Tracks," where the footprints of the Thunder-bird are visible on the rocks (016-123–125). The granite boulders scattered over the prairies of [[minnesota|Minnesota]] and [[dakota|Dakota]] were said to have been hurled in battle by [[Wa-zí-ya]], the Winter-maker, from his home in the North (015-213–216).

## In Gordon's Work

An illustration captioned "View of the Mississippi at [[fort-snelling|Fort Snelling]]" appears among the front matter of Gordon's *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]* (P01-028), depicting the view from the Mendota bluffs.
