Kapóza was the principal village of the Light-foot band of [[dakota|Dakotas]] (015-235), originally situated on the east bank of the Mississippi River below the mouth of the Minnesota, serving as a major center of [[dakota|Dakota]] political and social life.

## Location

Kapóza was located on the east bank of the [[mississippi-river|Mississippi]], below the confluence with the [[minnesota]] River (015-235). The village occupied a strategic position that facilitated trade, diplomacy, and military movements across the region. The settlement was one of several important [[dakota|Dakota]] villages in the area, including [[ke-uk-sa]], [[mendota]], and villages near [[mille-lacs|Mille Lacs]].

- **Location**: East bank of the Mississippi River, below the mouth of the Minnesota River
- **Region**: Present-day Ramsey County / Dakota County, Minnesota
- **Significance**: Principal village of the Kapóza band; political and trade center

## In the Book

Kapóza appears throughout Gordon's poems as a central reference point in the [[dakota|Dakota]] world, anchoring the geography of the legends.

### Role in the Legends

Gordon opens his Prelude with a hunting scene featuring "Kapóza's chief and his tawny hunters" (001-107), establishing the village as the seat of [[dakota|Dakota]] leadership. In "[[winona|Winona]]," the distance between lovers is measured by journeys from Kapóza — "His feet from Kapóza so long delay" (004-126) and "From the tees of Kapóza a maiden, lo" (005-149). The village serves as the home base of the "tall chief" who becomes [[winona|Winona]]'s betrothed (007-246).

In the ethnographic footnotes, Gordon records that the "Crows" were successively Chiefs of the Light-foot or Kapóza band (015-234), and that Kapóza was the principal village, originally located on the east bank of the [[mississippi-river|Mississippi]] (015-235). The footnote also mentions a notable event: a killing near Kapóza by the son of Cetan-Wakawa-mâni (016-135).
