# Wanâta

A tall young [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior who appears in the opening of Gordon's "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XII–XIII). Wanâta is chosen as the first Itâncan (chief) of his warrior band, and his relationship with Apè-dúta sets in motion events that lead to the legend's central narrative.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Warrior, Itâncan (chief of a warrior band)
- **Family**: Partner: Apè-dúta (012-249, 013-012)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Wanâta appears in the opening passages of "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XII–XIII). His story forms a prelude to the main [[panther|Panther]]–[[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] narrative, establishing the themes of love, rivalry, and fate that resonate throughout the legend.

### 关键事件

Wanâta is introduced with heroic stature: "Tall was young Wanâta, stronger than [[heyoka|Heyóka]]'s giant form" (012-219). His bravery is exceptional — "All but brave Wanâta wondered, and the gods with fear implored" (012-222). The warriors select him as their leader: "For the warriors chose Wanâta first Itâncan of the band" (012-239).

But Wanâta's honor carries a fatal edge. His selection as Itâncan brings "the fatal honor that her brave Wanâta won" (012-243). He becomes ensnared by Apè-dúta: "In her net of hair she caught him — caught Wanâta with her wiles" (012-249). Apè-dúta gains control: "Apè-dúta ruled the teepee — all Wanâta's smiles were hers" (012-251).

Wanâta leads his band on a journey: "Camped Wanâta, on the highlands, just above" (013-011). His band includes many warriors: "Many braves were with Wanâta; Apè-dúta, too, was there" (013-012). In a moment of action, "shot Wanâta's birch canoe" (013-017) as they navigate treacherous waters.

The legend's tone darkens as Wanâta's fate unfolds: "in the gloaming, all in vain Wanâta calls" (013-057). The image of Wanâta calling in vain into the twilight marks the transition from his personal story to the broader legend of [[panther|Panther]] and the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]].

## 历史/文化背景

Wanâta is a legendary figure whose story follows a pattern common in [[dakota|Dakota]] oral tradition — the rise and fall of a promising young leader. The title Itâncan, meaning chief or headman of a warrior band, was a significant position in traditional [[dakota|Dakota]] society, carrying both honor and obligation. Gordon's portrayal of Wanâta emphasizes the tension between personal desire and leadership responsibility, a theme that prefigures [[panther|Panther]]'s own domestic struggles in the legend that follows.
