# Dance

**Ceremonial dance** was a sacred communal practice in [[Dakota]] culture — a form of [[soul|spirit]]ual expression, war preparation, courtship, and celebration that features at key dramatic moments throughout [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]*.

## The Sacred Dance

Dance held explicit religious significance for the [[dakota|Dakota]]:

> The sacred dance to the giant great.
> (001-198)

The transition from song to dance marked the completion of ritual phases in the [[Feast of the Virgins]]:

> They have finished the song and the sacred dance,
> (004-018)

## The Virgins' Dance

At the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]], the [[dakota|Dakota]] maidens performed a ceremonial dance in their finest attire:

> The maidens dance in their gay attire.
> (004-015)

The maidens danced in a circle, hand in hand, around the feast ground:

> Hand in hand round the feast they dance,
> (003-245)

> In the merry dance or the merry tune.
> (003-248)

Gordon's footnote gives the [[dakota|Dakota]] name: *[[wiwaste|Wiwâstè]]* — Virgins Dance (or Feast) (015-177).

## The War Dance

Before battle, [[dakota|Dakota]] warriors raised the war dance — a powerful ritual of psychological and [[soul|spirit]]ual preparation:

> They dance to the tune of their wild "Ha-Ha!"
> (001-216)

The war dance was accompanied by the [[Song|war song]], with drum rhythms driving the warriors' movements:

> They raised the song of the red war dance.
> (004-178)

This combination of music and movement prepared warriors for the [[soul|spirit]]ual and physical demands of combat.

## Dance and the Spirit World

Dance connected the [[dakota|Dakota]] to the [[Spirit|spirit world]]. The rhythm of drums and the circular movement of dancers were understood to invoke [[soul|spirit]]ual presences.

The "witches dance at the midnight hour" (005-045) represents the darker dimension of dance — movements associated with malevolent [[spirit|spirits]] and supernatural forces.

## In Gordon's Poetic Structure

Gordon uses dance imagery to mark transitions in his narrative. The opening of gatherings, the height of ceremony, and the approach of war are all signaled by dance scenes. His verse captures the kinetic energy:

> And here to the feast and the dance,
> (006-037)

> they danced to the god of the waters,
> (006-042)

> And danced on the undulant green,
> (006-088)
