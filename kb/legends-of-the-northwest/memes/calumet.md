# Calumet

The calumet is the ceremonial pipe used by the [[dakota|Dakota]] and neighboring tribes, documented by early French explorers and cited by Gordon as a principal element of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|custom]] — employed at feasts, diplomatic encounters, and sacred rituals (018-110).

## Definition / Identity

A calumet is a highly ornamented ceremonial pipe, often with a long decorated stem and a bowl carved from catlinite (pipestone). Among the [[dakota|Dakota]] and other tribes of the Upper Mississippi, the calumet served both sacred and diplomatic functions — smoking it sealed treaties, welcomed strangers, and opened important councils.

- **Origin**: French term (*chalumet*) adopted from Norman dialect, applied to Native American ceremonial pipes encountered in the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s region
- **Language**: French → English; used across [[algonkin|Algonquian]] and Siouan cultures
- **Domain**: custom / ceremony / material-culture

The calumet was not merely a smoking implement but a ritual instrument of profound social significance. Gordon's source, Father [[jacques-marquette|Jacques Marquette]], observed the [[dakota|Dakota]] in the 17th century and recorded their [[tradition|customs]], noting that they "principally use the calumet" (018-110) — placing it at the center of their ceremonial life.

## In the Book

### Role in Gordon's Sources

Gordon's footnote (018-110) preserves Marquette's early ethnographic observation of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people. Marquette, writing in the 1670s, provided one of the earliest European accounts of [[dakota|Dakota]] life. His detail about the calumet appears alongside other observations: the [[dakota|Dakota]]'s many villages, their "extraordinary [[tradition|customs]]," and their protocol of not speaking at great feasts (018-108–018-111).

The calumet reference is brief but significant: it situates the [[dakota|Dakota]] within a broader Native American ceremonial tradition while distinguishing their specific [[tradition|customs]]. Gordon's decision to retain this detail in his footnotes reflects his commitment to ethnographic documentation alongside poetic narrative.

### Context in Dakota Life

The calumet ceremony structured relations between groups. Smoking the pipe together established kinship obligations, ratified agreements, and opened communication between strangers. Among the [[dakota|Dakota]], the calumet was often used in conjunction with the feast — the principal social institution through which alliances were maintained and [[spirit|spiritual]] power was exchanged.

### Representative Passage

> "They principally use the calumet. They do not speak at great feasts, and when a stranger arrives give him to eat of a wooden fork, as we would a child." (018-110–018-111)
