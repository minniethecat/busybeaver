# Winona

**Winona** — *Wiwaste*, the fairest [[dakota|Dakota]] maiden (001-151) — is the central heroine of [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s seven-part legend cycle in *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]*.

Her story, spanning Chapters 6–12 of the collection, embodies the conflict between the [[Dakota]] and [[Ojibway]] nations. Her tragic refusal to marry outside her people is the emotional climax of the entire work.

## Origins and Identity

Winona was born to [[ta-te-psin|Ta-té-psin]], a chief of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people. Gordon introduces her lineage in the opening of *Winona*:

> Of Ta-té-psin [a] was born Winona
> (006-082)

Her beauty was legendary among her people, celebrated throughout the [[dakota|Dakota]] camps:

> ...'er the hills was the face of Winona;
> (006-085)

Gordon labored over her story for years, as he records in his preface:

> In Winona I have "tried my hand" on the legends of the Dakotas.
> (P01-073)

## The Winona Legend Cycle

Winona's story unfolds across seven chapters in Gordon's collection, the longest sustained narrative in *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest*:

- **Sections 6–7**: Birth, early life, the love of a [[dakota|Dakota]] brave, rivalry and courtship
- **Sections 8–9**: DuLuth's arrival, diplomatic feast and council, Winona's refusal
- **Sections 10–12**: The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] chief's offer, the leap, Winona's tragic end and legacy

## Relationships

### Ta-té-psin (Father)

Winona's father was a respected [[dakota|Dakota]] chief. He appears in the early chapters as a figure of authority within the [[dakota|Dakota]] camp.

### The Dakota Brave

A young [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior loved Winona deeply:

> ...in the heart of the dark eyed Winona.
> (006-230)

The rivalry between this [[dakota|Dakota]] brave and an [[ojibway|Ojibway]] suitor forms the central romantic conflict.

### DuLuth

The French explorer [[Daniel DuLuth]] appears as a diplomatic figure who mediates between the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]]:

> "Winona, fear not," said Duluth,
> (009-051)

> said DuLuth to the blushing Winona.
> (008-294)

### The Ojibway Chief

An [[ojibway|Ojibway]] chief sought Winona's hand in marriage, offering peace between the nations. His proposal placed Winona in an impossible position between duty to her people and loyalty to her own heart.

## The Tragic Leap

Winona's climactic act — leaping to her death rather than marrying the [[Ojibway Nation|Ojibway]] chief — is the defining moment of her legend (see [[Winona's Leap]]):

> "Let Winona depart with the chief,
> (008-287)

> But the voice of Winona replied
> (008-315)

This act of defiance became the event known as [[Winona's Leap]], commemorated in [[dakota|Dakota]] oral tradition and at a site on the [[Mississippi River]].

## Cultural Significance

Winona represents the intersection of multiple themes in Gordon's *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest*:

- **Cultural Conflict**: Her story embodies the [[Dakota-Ojibway War|tensions between Dakota and Ojibway peoples]]

- **Female Agency**: Winona's choice, though tragic, asserts her autonomy in a world of political marriage alliances

- **Landscape and [[legend|Legend]]**: Her story is inseparable from the geography of the upper [[Mississippi River|Mississippi]], the [[Falls of St. Anthony]], the prairies

- **Oral [[tradition|Tradition]]**: Gordon's literary version preserves [[dakota|Dakota]] oral narratives of the Winona figure in English verse

## Gordon's Portrayal

Gordon describes Winona in elevated, poetic language throughout the cycle:

> ...the queenly and peerless Winona,
> (007-118)

> the sad-faced Winona, the while,
> (009-160)

He presents her as both a flesh-and-blood character and a symbolic figure. His preface reveals the personal investment he had in her story:

> ...for the last four years, Winona is at last done, if not
> (P01-157)
