Henry Wadsworth Longfellow (1807–1882) was one of the most celebrated American poets of the 19th century. His narrative poems "Evangeline" (1847) and "The [[song|Song]] of Hiawatha" (1855) were key literary influences on [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest." Gordon both admired Longfellow's craft and positioned his own work in explicit relation to — and at times in distinction from — Longfellow's.

## 在书中的角色

### 诗歌影响与对照

Gordon opens his preface by invoking Longfellow as the standard of narrative verse against which his own work must be measured. He praises the beauty of Longfellow's hexameter while noting its technical difficulties:

> Beautiful as is the Evangeline of Longfellow, his Hexameter lines are sometimes hard to scan, and often grate harshly on the ear. (P01-098)

This is not a dismissal but a craftsman's critique — Gordon is explaining his own choice of meter by analyzing where Longfellow's approach, however beautiful, sometimes strained.

### 共享的素材来源

Both Longfellow and Gordon drew from the same ethnographic source for their [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] [[legend|legends]]:

> This beautiful allegory has been "done into verse" by Longfellow in Hiawatha. I took my version from the lips of an old Chippewa Chief. (019-005)

Gordon makes a point of noting his direct oral source — an old [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] chief — distinguishing his method from Longfellow's literary adaptation:

> I have compared it with Schoolcraft's version, from which Mr. Longfellow evidently took his. (019-007)

This passage reveals Gordon's dual stance: he acknowledges Longfellow's precedence while asserting the greater authenticity of his own source material.

## 历史/文化背景

Longfellow was the most popular American poet of his era, and "The [[song|Song]] of Hiawatha" (1855) was a landmark in bringing Native American [[legend|legends]] to a broad American readership. The poem, written in trochaic tetrameter inspired by the Finnish Kalevala, drew heavily on [[henry-schoolcraft|Henry Schoolcraft]]'s ethnographic collections. Longfellow's "Evangeline" (1847), set during the Acadian expulsion, established the dactylic hexameter in American narrative poetry.

Gordon, writing in the 1880s, was a second-generation inheritor of Longfellow's achievement. He shared Longfellow's project of rendering Native American oral [[tradition|traditions]] in English verse but distinguished his approach by emphasizing direct contact with Native sources and a closer adherence to the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] originals.
