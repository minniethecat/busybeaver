# Red River

The Red River of the North, called the **Bloody River** in Gordon's [[legend|legends]], appears in the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]] narrative as a landmark on the heroine [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]]'s journey. The river earned its blood-stained name from the numerous battles fought on its banks between the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] nations (016-174–177).

## 地理位置

The Red River of the North flows northward along the border between present-day [[minnesota|Minnesota]] and North [[dakota|Dakota]], continuing into Manitoba, Canada. In Gordon's poem, the river is referenced as a landmark on the vast prairie landscape that [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] traverses alone during her escape from the [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] war party (005-089–094).

- **[[dakota|Dakota]]/[[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] Name**: Called "The Bloody River" by both nations (016-174)
- **Modern Name**: Red River of the North
- **Region**: [[minnesota|Minnesota]]–North [[dakota|Dakota]] border region, flowing north to Lake Winnipeg

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传说

The Bloody River appears in the **[[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]** narrative:

During the great feast, a storyteller recounts how [[wakawa|Wakâwa]]'s rival [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] journeyed alone across the frozen plains, braving "hurricanes that swept the snow from the frozen plains and piled the banks of the Bloody River" (003-131–132).

After receiving divine aid, [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] "reached at last" the Bloody River and "boldly walked in the light of day on the level plain of the valley vast" (005-089–091). At the river, she unknowingly entered the "treacherous Bloody Land" — the contested territory between [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] (005-094).

### 叙事功能

The Bloody River serves as a geographic waypoint marking [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]]'s progress on her arduous journey (005-089–091). It also functions as a narrative boundary: crossing the river means entering the contested "Bloody Land" where [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] war parties roam (005-094).

## 历史与文化意义

According to Gordon's footnote, the Red River was "so-called on account of the numerous Indian battles that have been fought on its banks." The [[ojibway-nation|Chippewas]] ([[ojibway|Ojibway]]) "say that its waters were colored red by the blood of many warriors slain on its banks in the fierce wars between themselves and the [[dakota|Dakotas]]" (016-174–177).

The Red River Valley was historically a contested frontier between the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]]) peoples, its waters "colored red by the blood of many warriors" in the fierce intertribal wars that gave the river its name (016-174–177).
