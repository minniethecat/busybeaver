[008-002] \[b\] A favorite boast of the [[dakota|Dakota]] braves.

[008-005] Then forth from the side of the chief

[008-006] stepped DuLuth and he looked on the boaster;

[008-007] \"The words of a warrior are brief,

[008-008] \--I will run with the brave,\" said the Frenchman;

[008-009] \"But the feet of Tamdóka are tired;

[008-010] abide till the cool of the sunset.\"

[008-011] All the hunters and maidens admired,

[008-012] for strong were the limbs of the stranger.

[008-013] \"Hiwó! Ho!\" \[a\] they shouted

[008-014] and loud rose the cheers of the multitude mingled;

[008-015] And there in the midst of the crowd

[008-016] stood the glad-eyed and blushing Winona.

[008-017] \[a\] Hurra there!

[008-018] Now afar o\'er the plains of the west

[008-019] walked the sun at the end of his journey,

[008-020] And forth came the brave and the guest,

[008-021] at the tap of the drum, for the trial.

[008-022] Like a forest of larches the hordes

[008-023] were gathered to witness the contest;

[008-024] As loud is the drums were their words

[008-025] and they roared like the roar of the Ha-ha.

[008-026] For some for Tamdóka contend,

[008-027] and some for the fair, bearded stranger,

[008-028] And the betting runs high to the end,

[008-029] with the skins of the bison and beaver.

[008-030] A wife of tall Wazi-kuté

[008-031] \--the mother of boastful Tamdóka\--

[008-032] Brought her handsomest robe from the tee,

[008-033] with a vaunting and loud proclamation:

[008-034] She would stake her last robe on her son who,

[008-035] she boasted, was fleet as the Cábri \[80\]

[008-036] And the tall, tawny chieftain looked on,

[008-037] approving the boast of the mother.

[008-038] Then fleet as the feet of a fawn to her lodge

[008-039] ran the dark eyed Winona,

[008-040] She brought and she staked on the lawn,

[008-041] by the side of the robe of the boaster,

[008-042] The lily-red mantle Duluth, with his own hands,

[008-043] had laid on her shoulders.

[008-044] \"Tamdóka is swift, but forsooth,

[008-045] the tongue of his mother is swifter,\"

[008-046] She said, and her face was aflame

[008-047] with the red of the rose and the lily,

[008-048] And loud was the roar of acclaim;

[008-049] but dark was the face of Tamdóka.

[008-050] They strip for the race and prepare,

[008-051] \--DuLuth in his breeches and leggins;

[008-052] And the brown, curling locks of his hair

[008-053] downward droop to his bare, brawny shoulders,

[008-054] And his face wears a smile debonair,

[008-055] as he tightens his red sash around him;

[008-056] But stripped to the moccasins bare,

[008-057] save the belt and the breech-clout of buckskin,

[008-058] Stands the haughty Tamdóka aware

[008-059] that the eyes of the warriors admire him;

[008-060] For his arms are the arms of a bear

[008-061] and his legs are the legs of a panther.

[008-062] The drum beats,\--the chief waves the flag,

[008-063] and away on the course speed the runners,

[008-064] And away leads the brave like a stag,

[008-065] \--like a hound on his track flies the Frenchman;

[008-066] And away haste the hunters, once more,

[008-067] to the hills for a view to the lake-side,

[008-068] And the dark-swarming hill-tops,

[008-069] they roar with the storm of loud voices commingled.

[008-070] Far away o\'er the prairie they fly,

[008-071] and still in the lead is Tamdóka,

[008-072] But the feet of his rival are nigh,

[008-073] and slowly he gains on the hunter.

[008-074] Now they turn on the post at the lake,

[008-075] \--now they run full abreast on the home-stretch;

[008-076] Side by side they contend for the stake,

[008-077] for a long mile or more on the prairie.

[008-078] They strain like a stag and a hound,

[008-079] when the swift river gleams through the thicket,

[008-080] And the horns of the rulers resound,

[008-081] winding shrill through the depths of the forest.

[008-082] But behold!\--at full length on the ground

[008-083] falls the fleet-footed Frenchman abruptly.

[008-084] And away with a whoop and a bound,

[008-085] springs the eager, exulting Tamdóka.

[008-086] Long and loud on the hills

[008-087] is the shout of his swarthy admirers and backers;

[008-088] \"But the race is not won till it\'s out,\"

[008-089] said DuLuth, to himself as he gathered,

[008-090] With a frown on his face,

[008-091] for the foot of the wily Tamdóka had tripped him.

[008-092] Far ahead ran the brave on the route,

[008-093] and turning he boasted exultant.

[008-094] Like spurs to the steed to DuLuth

[008-095] were the jeers and the taunts of the boaster;

[008-096] Indignant was he and red wroth,

[008-097] at the trick of the runner dishonest;

[008-098] And away like a whirlwind he speeds

[008-099] \--like a hurricane mad from the mountains;

[008-100] He gains on Tamdóka,\--he leads!

[008-101] \--and behold, with the spring of a panther,

[008-102] He leaps to the goal and succeeds,

[008-103] \'mid the roar of the mad acclamation.

[008-104] Then glad as the robin in May

[008-105] was the voice of Winona exulting;

[008-106] And the crest-fallen brave turned away,

[008-107] and lonely he walked by the river;

[008-108] He glowered as he went

[008-109] and the fire of revenge in his bosom was kindled,

[008-110] But he strove to dissemble his ire,

[008-111] and he whistled alone by the Ha-ha.

[008-112] THE \"WAKAN WACEPEE,\" OR SACRED DANCE. \[81\]

[008-113] Lo the lights in the \"Teepee Wakán!\"

[008-114] \'tis the night of the Wakán-Wacépee.

[008-115] Round and round walks the chief of the clan,

[008-116] as he rattles the sacred Ta-shá-kay; \[81\]

[008-117] Long and loud on the Chân-che-ga \[81\]

[008-118] beat the drummers with magical drumsticks,

[008-119] And the notes of the Chô-tánka \[81\] greet,

[008-120] like the murmur of winds on the waters.

[008-121] By the friction of white-cedar wood

[008-122] for the feast was a Virgin-fire \[20\] kindled.

[008-123] They that enter the firm brotherhood

[008-124] first must fast and be cleansed by E-neé-pee; \[81\]

[008-125] And from foot-sole to crown of the head

[008-126] must they paint with the favorite colors;

[008-127] For Unktéhee likes bands of blood-red,

[008-128] with the stripings of blue intermingled.

[008-129] In the hollow earth, dark and profound,

[008-130] Unktéhee and fiery Wakín-yan

[008-131] Long fought and the terrible sound

[008-132] of the battle was louder than thunder;

[008-133] The mountains were heaved and around

[008-134] were scattered the hills and the boulders,

[008-135] And the vast solid plains of the ground

[008-136] rose and fell like the waves of the ocean.

[008-137] But the god of the waters prevailed.

[008-138] Wakín-yan escaped from the cavern,

[008-139] And long on the mountains he wailed,

[008-140] and his hatred endureth forever.

[008-141] When Unktéhee had finished the earth,

[008-142] and the beasts and the birds and the fishes,

[008-143] And men at his bidding came forth

[008-144] from the heart of the huge hollow mountains \[69\]

[008-145] A band chose the god from the hordes,

[008-146] and he said \"Ye are sons of Unktéhee;

[008-147] Ye are lords of the beasts and the birds,

[008-148] and the fishes that swim in the waters.

[008-149] But hearken ye now to my words,

[008-150] \--let them sound in your bosoms forever.

[008-151] Ye shall honor Unktéhee and hate Wakínyan,

[008-152] the [[spirit|Spirit]] of Thunder,

[008-153] For the power of Unktéhee is great,

[008-154] and he laughs at the darts of Wakínyan.

[008-155] Ye shall honor the Earth and the Sun,

[008-156] \--for they are your father and mother. \[70\]

[008-157] Let your prayer to the Sun be

[008-158] \--_Wakán, Até: on-si-má-da oheé-neé_ \[a\]

[008-159] And remember the Táku Wakán, \[73\]

[008-160] all pervading in earth and in ether\--

[008-161] Invisible ever to man,

[008-162] but he dwells in the midst of all matter;

[008-163] Yea, he dwells in the heart of the stone

[008-164] \--in the hard granite heart of the boulder;

[008-165] Ye shall call him forever Tunkán

[008-166] \--grandfather of all the Dakotas.

[008-167] Ye are men that I choose for my own;

[008-168] ye shall be as a strong band of brothers,

[008-169] Now I give you the magical bone

[008-170] and the magical pouch of the spirits. \[b\]

[008-171] And these are the laws ye shall heed:

[008-172] Ye shall honor the pouch and the giver.

[008-173] Ye shall walk as twin-brothers;

[008-174] in need, one shall forfeit his life for another.

[008-175] Listen not to the voice of the crow. \[c\]

[008-176] Hold as sacred the wife of a brother.

[008-177] Strike, and fear not the shaft of the foe,

[008-178] for the soul of the brave is immortal.

[008-179] Slay the warrior in battle,

[008-180] but spare the innocent babe and the mother.

[008-181] Remember a promise;\--beware,

[008-182] \--let the word of a warrior be sacred.

[008-183] When a stranger arrives at the tee

[008-184] \--be he friend of the band or a foeman,

[008-185] Give him food; let your bounty be free;

[008-186] lay a robe for the guest by the lodge-fire;

[008-187] Let him go to his kindred in peace,

[008-188] if the peace-pipe he smoke in the teepee;

[008-189] And so shall your children increase,

[008-190] and your lodges shall laugh with abundance.

[008-191] And long shall ye live in the land,

[008-192] and the spirits of earth and the waters

[008-193] Shall come to your aid, at command,

[008-194] with the power of invisible magic.

[008-195] And at last, when you journey afar

[008-196] \--o\'er the shining \"_Wanágee Ta-chán-ku_,\" \[70\]

[008-197] You shall walk as a red, shining star, \[18\]

[008-198] in the land of perpetual summer.\"

[008-199] \[a\] \"Sacred Spirit, Father have pity on me always\"

[008-200] \[b\] Riggs\' Tahkoo [[wakan|Wakan]], p. 90.

[008-201] \[c\] Slander.

[008-202] All the night in the teepee they sang,

[008-203] and they danced to the mighty Unktéhee,

[008-204] While the loud-braying Chán-che-ga rang

[008-205] and the shrill-piping flute and the rattle,

[008-206] Till Anpétuwee \[70\] rose in the east

[008-207] \--from the couch of the blushing Han-nân-na.

[008-208] And then at the dance and the feast

[008-209] sang the song of Unktéhee in chorus:

[008-210] \"Wa-dú-ta o-hna mi-ká-ge!

[008-211] Wa-dú-ta o-hná mi-ká-ge!

[008-212] Mini-yâta ité wakândè makù,

[008-213] Atè wakán\--Tunkánsidán,

[008-214] Tunkânsidán pejihúta wakán

[008-215] Micâgè\--he Wicâgè!

[008-216] Miniyáta ité wakándé makú.

[008-217] Taukánsidán ite, nápè dú-win-ta woo,

[008-218] Wahutôpa wan yúha, nápè dú-win-ta too.\"

[008-264] Admiring he gazed\--she was fair

[008-266] With her golden locks loose on the air,

[008-267] like the gleam of the sun through the olives,

[008-272] Said Winona,\--\"The wail of the sprite

[008-274] Is heard in the midst of the night,
