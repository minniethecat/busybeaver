# Sweat Lodge

The sweat lodge (*E-né-pee* in [[dakota|Dakota]]) is a vapor bath used as a purification ceremony preparatory to sacred feasts (018-050). A domed frame of bent poles is covered with robes and blankets. Heated stones are rolled inside, and water poured over them creates steam within the enclosed space. The participant sits inside the "steam-tight dome, sweating profusely at every pore" (018-051).

Father [[Louis Hennepin]] was subjected to the vapor bath at [[mille-lacs|Mille Lacs]] by Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin two hundred years ago. After three sessions in a week, Hennepin reported: "I felt as strong as ever" (018-051).

## Historical Accounts

Father [[Louis Hennepin]] was subjected to the vapor-bath at [[Mille Lacs]] by Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin two hundred years ago (018-062). The construction of the sweat lodge is described in Riggs' "Tâhkoo Wakan" (p. 83):

> A number of poles the size of hoop-poles or less are taken, and their larger ends being set in the ground in a circle, the flexible tops are bent over and tied in the centre. This frame work is then covered with robes and blankets, a small hole being left on one side for an entrance. Before the door a fire is built, and round stones about the size of a man's head are heated in it.
>
> (018-052–056)

When hot, the stones are rolled within and the door closed. Water poured over them creates steam. The devotee, stripped to the skin, sits within this steam-tight dome, sweating profusely at every pore, until he is nearly suffocated (018-057–059).

After three sessions in a week, Hennepin reported: "I felt as strong as ever" (018-064). The vapor bath was not merely physical cleansing — it was a purification preparatory to the sacred feasts (018-050).
