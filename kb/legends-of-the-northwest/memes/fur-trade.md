# Fur Trade

The fur trade in the upper Mississippi region was conducted by French traders and [[voyageurs]] (coureurs des bois) beginning in the 17th century. These were the first Europeans to make contact with the [[dakota|Dakota]] people, whom they named "Sioux" (P01-010).

The [[fur-trade-company|Fur Trade Company]] employed voyageurs who traveled the rivers by canoe, trading European goods for furs. Key figures included [[daniel-duluth|Daniel Duluth]] and [[Louis Hennepin]].

Hudson Bay and the [[St. Lawrence River]] served as export routes for pelts bound for Europe.

## The Voyageurs

French voyageurs were the mobile workforce of the fur trade, traveling the Mississippi and its tributaries by canoe.

Their distinctive paddling songs became part of regional lore. In Gordon's "Winona," the refrain "Merry is the life of the gay voyageur" echoes across several stanzas (009-127–009-151).

These early French traders gave the Dakota the name "Sioux," a French shortening of the Ojibway term Nadouessioux (P01-010).

## Trade Goods and Routes

The trade centered on furs — beaver, otter, mink, and buffalo robes — exchanged for European goods including blankets, firearms, and metal tools.

A "blanket of beaver" served as winter clothing and a mark of status among Dakota warriors (006-237).

Mendota, at the confluence of the Minnesota and Mississippi rivers, was a key trading post. Its Dakota name Mdó-tè means "meeting of the waters" (015-218).

Disputes could turn violent. An English trader was killed at Mendota after persisting in behavior that violated local customs (P01-028).
