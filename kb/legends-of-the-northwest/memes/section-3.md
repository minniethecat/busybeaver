[002-001] Then tall Wakâwa arose and said:

[002-002] \"Brave warriors, listen, and give due heed.

[002-003] Great is Heyóka, the magical god;

[002-004] He can walk on the air; he can float on the flood.

[002-005] He\'s a worker of magic and wonderful wise;

[002-006] He cries when he laughs and he laughs when he cries;

[002-007] He sweats when he\'s cold, and he shivers when hot,

[002-008] And the water is cold in his boiling pot.

[002-009] He hides in the earth and he walks in disguise,

[002-010] But he loves the brave and their sacrifice.

[002-011] We are sons of Heyóka. The Giant commands

[002-012] In the boiling water to thrust our hands;

[002-013] And the warrior that scorneth the foe and fire

[002-014] Heyóka will crown with his hearts desire.\"

[002-015] They thrust their hands in the boiling pot;

[002-016] They swallow the bison meat steaming hot,

[002-017] Not a wince on their stoical faces bold.

[002-018] For the meat and the water, they say, are cold,

[002-019] And great is Heyóka and wonderful wise;

[002-020] He floats on the flood and he walks in the skies,

[002-021] And ever appears in a strange disguise;

[002-022] But he loves the brave and their sacrifice;

[002-023] And the warrior that scorneth the foe and fire

[002-024] Heyóka will crown with his heart\'s desire.

[002-025] Proud was the chief of his warriors proud,

[002-026] The sinewy sons of the Giant\'s race;

[002-027] But the bravest of all was the tall Red Cloud;

[002-028] The eyes of the panther were set in his face;

[002-029] He strode like a stag and he stood like a pine;

[002-030] Ten feathers he wore at the great Wanmdeé; \[13\]

[002-031] With crimsoned quills of the porcupine

[002-032] His leggins were worked to his brawny knee.

[002-033] Blood-red were the stripes on his swarthy cheek,

[002-034] And the necklace that girdled his brawny neck

[002-035] Was the polished claws of the great Mató \[14\]

[002-036] He grappled and slew in the northern snow.

[002-037] Proud Red Cloud turned to the braves and said,

[002-038] As he shook the plumes on his haughty head:

[002-039] \"Ho! the warrior that scorneth the foe and fire

[002-040] Heyóka will crown with his heart\'s desire!\"

[002-041] He snatched from the embers a red-hot brand,

[002-042] And held it aloft in his naked hand.

[002-043] He stood like a statue in bronze or stone,\--

[002-044] Not a muscle moved, and the braves looked on.

[002-045] He turned to the chieftain,\--\"I scorn the fire,\--

[002-046] Ten feathers I wear of the great Wanmdeé;

[002-047] Then grant me, Wakâwa, my heart\'s desire;

[002-048] Let the sunlight shine in my lonely tee. \[19\]

[002-049] I laugh at red death and I laugh at red fire;

[002-050] Brave Red Cloud is only afraid of fear;

[002-051] But Wiwâstè is fair to his heart and dear;

[002-052] Then grant him, Wakâwa, his heart\'s desire.\"

[002-053] The warriors applauded with loud \"Ho! Ho!\" \[24\]

[002-054] And he flung the brand to the drifting snow.

[002-055] Three times Wakâwa puffed forth the smoke

[002-056] From his silent lips; then he slowly spoke:

[002-057] \"Mâhpíya is strong as the stout-armed oak

[002-058] That stands on the bluff by the windy plain,

[002-059] And laughs at the roar of the hurricane.

[002-060] He has slain the foe and the great Mató

[002-061] With his hissing arrow and deadly stroke.

[002-062] My heart is swift but my tongue is slow.

[002-063] Let the warrior come to my lodge and smoke;

[002-064] He may bring the gifts; \[25\] but the timid doe

[002-065] May fly from the hunter and say him no.\"

[002-066] Wiwâstè sat late in the lodge alone,

[002-067] Her dark eyes bent on the glowing fire.

[002-068] She heard not the wild winds shrill and moan;

[002-069] She heard not the tall elms toss and groan;

[002-070] Her face was lit like the harvest moon;

[002-071] For her thoughts flew far to her heart\'s desire.

[002-072] Far away in the land of the Hóhé \[15\] dwelt

[002-073] The warrior she held in her secret heart;

[002-074] But little he dreamed of the pain she felt,

[002-075] For she hid her love with a maiden\'s art.

[002-076] Not a tear she shed, not a word she said,

[002-077] When the fair young chief from the lodge departed;

[002-078] But she sat on the mound when the day was dead,

[002-079] And gazed at the full moon mellow hearted.

[002-080] Fair was the chief as the morning-star;

[002-081] His eyes were mild and his words were low,

[002-082] But his heart was stouter than lance or bow;

[002-083] And her young heart flew to her love afar

[002-084] O\'er his trail long covered with drifted snow.

[002-085] But she heard a warrior\'s stealthy tread,

[002-086] And the tall Wakâwa appeared, and said\--

[002-087] \"Is Wiwâstè afraid of the spirit dread

[002-088] That fires the sky in the fatal north? \[26\]

[002-089] Behold the mysterious lights. Come forth

[002-090] Some evil threatens,\--some danger nears,

[002-091] For the skies are pierced with the burning spears.\"

[002-092] The warriors rally beneath the moon;

[002-093] They shoot their shafts at the evil spirit.

[002-094] The spirit is slain and the flame is gone,

[002-095] And his blood lies red on the snow fields near it.

[002-096] But again from the dead will the spirit rise,

[002-097] And flash his spears in the northern skies.

[002-098] Then the chief and the queenly Wiwâstè stood

[002-099] Alone in the moon-lit solitude,

[002-100] And she was silent and he was grave.

[002-101] \"And fears not my daughter the evil spirit?

[002-102] The strongest warriors and bravest fear it

[002-103] The burning spears are an evil omen;

[002-104] They threaten the wrath of a wicked woman,

[002-105] Or a treacherous foe; but my warriors brave,

[002-106] When danger nears, or the foe appears,

[002-107] Are a cloud of arrows,\--a grove of spears.\"

[002-108] \"My Father,\" she said, and her words were low,

[002-109] \"Why should I fear? for I soon will go

[002-110] To the broad, blue lodge in the [[spirit|Spirit]] land,

[002-111] Where my dark eyed mother went long ago,

[002-112] And my dear twin sisters walk hand in hand.

[002-113] My Father, listen,\--my words are true,\"

[002-114] And sad was her voice as the whippowil

[002-115] When she mourns her mate by the moon-lit rill,

[002-116] \"Wiwâstè lingers alone with you,

[002-117] The rest are sleeping on yonder hill,\--

[002-118] Save one\--and he an undutiful son,\--

[002-119] And you, my Father, will sit alone

[002-120] When Sisóka \[27\] sings and the snow is gone.

[002-121] I sat, when the maple leaves were red,

[002-122] By the foaming falls of the haunted river;

[002-123] The night sun was walking above my head,

[002-124] And the arrows shone in his burnished quiver;

[002-125] And the winds were hushed and the hour was dread

[002-126] With the walking ghosts of the silent dead.

[002-127] I heard the voice of the Water-Fairy; \[28\]

[002-128] I saw her form in the moon-lit mist,

[002-129] As she sat on a stone with her burden weary,

[002-130] By the foaming eddies of amethyst.

[002-131] And robed in her mantle of mist the sprite

[002-132] Her low wail poured on the silent night.

[002-133] Then the spirit spake, and the floods were still\--

[002-134] They hushed and listened to what she said,

[002-135] And hushed was the plaint of the whippowil

[002-136] In the silver-birches above her head:

[002-137] \'Wiwâstè,\--the prairies are green and fair,

[002-138] When the robin sings and the whippowil;

[002-139] But the land of the Spirits is fairer still,

[002-140] For the winds of winter blow never there;

[002-141] And forever the [[song|songs]] of the whippowils

[002-142] And the robins are heard on the leafy hills.

[002-143] Thy mother looks from her lodge above,\--

[002-144] Her fair face shines in the sky afar,

[002-145] And the eyes of thy sisters are bright with love,

[002-146] As they peep from the tee of the mother-star.

[002-147] To her happy lodge in the spirit-land

[002-148] She beckons Wiwâstè with shining hand.\'

[002-149] \"My Father,\--my Father, her words were true;

[002-150] And the death of Wiwâstè will rest on you.

[002-151] You have pledged me as wife to the tall Red Cloud;

[002-152] You will take the gifts of the warrior proud;

[002-153] But I, Wakâwa,\--I answer\--never!

[002-154] I will stain your knife in my heart\'s red blood,

[002-155] I will plunge and sink in the sullen river,

[002-156] Ere I will be wife to the fierce Red Cloud!\"

[002-157] \"Wiwâstè,\" he said, and his voice was low,

[002-158] \"Let it be as you will, for Wakâwa\'s tongue

[002-159] Has spoken no promise;\--his lips are slow,

[002-160] And the love of a father is deep and strong.

[002-161] Be happy, Micúnksee \[29\], the flames are gone,\--

[002-162] They flash no more in the Northern sky.

[002-163] See the smile on the face of the watching moon;

[002-164] No more will the fatal red arrows fly;

[002-165] For the singing shafts of my warriors sped

[002-166] To the bad spirit\'s bosom and laid him dead,

[002-167] And his blood on the snow of the North lies red.

[002-168] Go,\--sleep in the robe that you won to-day,

[002-169] And dream of your hunter\--the brave Chaskè.\"

[002-170] Light was her heart as she turned away;

[002-171] It sang like the lark in the skies of May.

[002-172] The round moon laughed, but a lone red star, \[30\]

[002-173] As she turned to the teepee and entered in,

[002-174] Fell flashing and swift in the sky afar,

[002-175] Like the polished point of a javelin.

[002-176] Nor chief nor daughter the shadow saw

[002-177] Of the crouching listener\--Hârpstinà.

[002-178] Wiwâstè, wrapped in her robe and sleep

[002-179] Heard not the storm-sprites wail and weep,

[002-180] As they rode on the winds in the frosty air;

[002-181] But she heard the voice of her hunter fair;

[002-182] For a shadowy spirit with fairy fingers

[002-183] The curtains drew from the land of dreams;

[002-184] And lo in her teepee her lover lingers;

[002-185] The light of love in his dark eye beams,

[002-186] And his voice is the music of mountain streams.

[002-187] And then with her round, brown arms she pressed

[002-188] His phantom form to her throbbing breast,

[002-189] And whispered the name, in her happy sleep,

[002-190] Of her Hóhé hunter so fair and far.

[002-191] And then she saw in her dreams the deep

[002-192] Where the spirit wailed, and a falling star;

[002-193] Then stealthily crouching under the trees,

[002-194] By the light of the moon, the Kan-ó-ti-dan, \[31\]

[002-195] The little, wizened, mysterious man,

[002-196] With his long locks tossed by the moaning breeze.

[002-197] Then a flap of wings, like a thunder-bird, \[32\]

[002-198] And a wailing spirit the sleeper heard;

[002-199] And lo, through the mists of the moon, she saw

[002-200] The hateful visage of Hârpstinà.

[002-201] But waking she murmured\--\"And what are these\--

[002-202] The flap of wings and the falling star,

[002-203] The wailing spirit that\'s never at ease,

[002-204] The little man crouching under the trees,

[002-205] And the hateful visage of Hârpstinà?

[002-206] My dreams are like feathers that float on the breeze,

[002-207] And none can tell what the omens are\--

[002-208] Save the beautiful dream of my love afar

[002-209] In the happy land of the tall Hóhé \[15\]\--

[002-210] My beautiful hunter\--my brave Chaskè.\"

[002-211] \"Ta-tânka! Ta-tânka!\" \[33\] the hunters cried,

[002-212] With a joyous shout at the break of dawn;

[002-213] And darkly lined on the white hill-side,

[002-214] A herd of bison went marching on

[002-215] Through the drifted snow like a caravan.

[002-216] Swift to their ponies the hunters sped,

[002-217] And dashed away on the hurried chase.

[002-218] The wild steeds scented the game ahead,

[002-219] And sprang like hounds to the eager race.

[002-220] But the brawny bulls in the swarthy van

[002-221] Turned their polished horns to the charging foes,

[002-222] And reckless rider and fleet foot-man

[002-223] Were held at bay in the drifted snows,

[002-224] While the bellowing herd o\'er the hill-tops ran,

[002-225] Like the frightened beasts of a caravan

[002-226] On the Sahara\'s sands when the simoon blows.

[002-227] Sharp were the twangs of the hunters\' bows,

[002-228] And swift and humming the arrows sped,

[002-229] Till ten huge bulls on the bloody snows

[002-230] Lay pierced with arrows and dumb and dead.

[002-231] But the chief with the flankers had gained the rear,

[002-232] And flew on the trail of the flying herd.

[002-233] The shouts of the riders rang loud and clear,

[002-234] As their frothing steeds to the chase they spurred.

[002-235] And now like the roar of an avalanche

[002-236] Rolls the sullen wrath of the maddened bulls.

[002-237] They charge on the riders and runners stanch,

[002-238] And a dying steed in the snow-drift rolls,

[002-239] While the rider, flung to the frozen ground

[002-240] Escapes the horns by a panther\'s bound.

[002-241] But the raging monsters are held at bay,

[002-242] While the flankers dash on the swarthy rout.

[002-243] With lance and arrow they slay and slay;

[002-244] And the welkin rings to the gladsome shout\--

[002-245] To the loud Inâs and the wild Ihós, \[34\]\--

[002-246] And dark and dead, on the bloody snows,

[002-247] Lie the swarthy heaps of the buffaloes.
