# Panther

A [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior and hunter, central figure in Gordon's "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XIII–XV). Panther's domestic life — his marriage to the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]], his infidelity with the [[red-fox|Red Fox]], and his eventual redemption — drives the narrative of the legend.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Warrior, hunter, husband, father
- **Family**: First wife: the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]; son: [[raven|Kâk-kâh-gè]] (the [[raven|Raven]]) (013-122, 013-123); second wife: the [[red-fox|Red Fox]] (014-013)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Panther is the protagonist of "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XIII–XV). The legend traces the arc of his marriage to the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]], his betrayal, and the supernatural consequences that explain the origin of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] of St. Anthony.

### 关键事件

Panther first appears as a joyful newlywed: "When the Panther wed the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]], Joyful was the heart of Panther" (013-093, 013-104). He is a skilled provider — "Happy hunter was the Panther" (013-124) — and a proud warrior: "Brave among the brave is Panther" (013-211).

The marriage sours as Panther grows distant: "Cold and sullen grew the Panther" (013-141). He becomes listless and withdrawn — "Listless, dreaming walked the Panther" (013-227). The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] tries to understand: "Tell me, O my silent Panther" (013-200), but receives only gruff replies (013-210).

Panther eventually weds the [[red-fox|Red Fox]]: "So the Panther wed the [[red-fox|Red Fox]]" (014-013). The [[red-fox|Red Fox]] receives the best of everything — "Choicest bits of all for [[red-fox|Red Fox]]" (014-025) — and the rivalry between the two wives becomes central. The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] and the [[red-fox|Red Fox]] sit together in tension (014-098).

Panther and his son [[raven|Raven]] undertake a journey: "Forth went Panther and the [[raven|Raven]]" (014-257). The [[raven|Raven]] speaks to his father about the situation (014-266), and Panther responds (014-279). Eventually, Panther sees "an evil [[soul|spirit]]" (015-019) and springs from hiding (015-037), leading to his redemption. The legend concludes with domestic peace: "Long and happy with the Panther" (015-089), "Walked the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] and the Panther" (015-095).

## 历史/文化背景

Panther is a legendary figure without historical counterpart. His story follows a universal narrative pattern — the loyal wife, the unfaithful husband, the other woman — rendered through the [[dakota|Dakota]] legend tradition. Gordon uses the Panther's personal drama to explain the natural wonder of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]], embedding moral causality in the landscape. Panther's eventual redemption distinguishes the tale from simpler cautionary stories.
