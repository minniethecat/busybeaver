[014-001] Ah, the tail and tawny Panther!

[014-002] Ah, the brave and brawny Panther!

[014-003] Son of Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior!

[014-004] With a slender hair she led him,

[014-005] With a slender hair he drew him,

[014-006] Drew him often to her wigwam;

[014-007] There she bound him, there she held him

[014-008] Tangled in her raven tresses,

[014-009] Tied and tangled in her tresses.

[014-010] Ah, the best of men are tangled\--

[014-011] Sometime tangled in the tresses

[014-012] Of a fair and crafty woman.

[014-013] So the Panther wed the Red Fox,

[014-014] And she followed to his wigwam.

[014-015] Young again he seemed and gladsome,

[014-016] Glad as Raven when the father

[014-017] Made his first bow from the elm-tree,

[014-018] From the ash tree made his arrows,

[014-019] Taught him how to aim his arrows,

[014-020] How to shoot Wabóse\--the rabbit.

[014-021] Then again the brawny hunter

[014-022] Brought the black bear and the beaver,

[014-023] Brought the haunch of elk and red-deer,

[014-024] Brought the rabbit and the pheasant\--

[014-025] Choicest bits of all for Red Fox.

[014-026] For her robes he brought the sable,

[014-027] Brought the otter and the ermine,

[014-028] Brought the black-fox tipped with silver.

[014-029] But the Sea-Gull murmured never,

[014-030] Not a word she spoke in anger,

[014-031] Went about her work as ever,

[014-032] Tanned the skins of bear and beaver,

[014-033] Tanned the hides of moose and red deer,

[014-034] Gathered fagots for the lodge-fire,

[014-035] Gathered rushes from the marches;

[014-036] Deftly into mats she wove them;

[014-037] Kept the lodge as bright as ever.

[014-038] Only to herself she murmured,

[014-039] All alone with Waub-omeé-meé,

[014-040] On the tall and toppling highland,

[014-041] O\'er the wilderness of waters;

[014-042] Murmured to the murmuring waters,

[014-043] Murmured to the Nébe-nâw-baigs\--

[014-044] To the [[spirit|spirits]] of the waters;

[014-045] On the wild waves poured her sorrow,

[014-046] Save the infant on her bosom

[014-047] With her dark eyes wide with wonder,

[014-048] None to hear her but the spirits,

[014-049] And the murmuring pines above her.

[014-050] Thus she cast away her burdens,

[014-051] Cast her burdens on the waters;

[014-052] Thus unto the Mighty Spirit,

[014-053] Made her lowly lamentation:

[014-054] \"Wahonówin!\--Wahonówin!\" \[1013\]

[014-055] Gitchee Mânito, benâ nin!

[014-056] Nah, Ba-bâ, showâin neméshin!

[014-057] \"Wahonówin!\--Wahonówin!\"

[014-058] Ka-be-bón-ík-ka, the mighty, \[109\]

[014-059] He that sends the cruel winter,

[014-060] From the distant Thunder-mountain,

[014-061] On the shore of Gitchee Gumee\--

[014-062] On the rugged northern limit,

[014-063] Sent his solemn, final warning,

[014-064] Sent the white wolves of the Nor\'land \[1014\]

[014-065] Like the dust of stars in ether\--

[014-066] In the Pathway of the Spirits. \[1015\]

[014-067] Like the sparkling dust of diamonds,

[014-068] Fell the frost upon the forest,

[014-069] On the mountains and the meadows,

[014-070] On the wilderness of woodland.

[014-071] On the wilderness of waters.

[014-072] All the lingering fowls departed\--

[014-073] All that seek the South in winter,

[014-074] All but Shingebís, the diver. \[1016\]

[014-075] He defies the Winter-maker,

[014-076] Sits and laughs at Winter-maker.

[014-077] Ka-be-bón-ík-ka, the mighty,

[014-078] From his wigwam called Kewáydin,\--

[014-079] From his home among the ice-bergs,

[014-080] From the sea of frozen waters,

[014-081] Called the swift and hungry North-wind.

[014-082] Then he spread his mighty pinions

[014-083] Over all the land and shook them,

[014-084] Like the white down of Wâubésè \[1017\]

[014-085] Fell the feathery snow and covered,

[014-086] All the marshes and the meadows,

[014-087] All the hill-tops and the highlands.

[014-088] Then old Péboân\--the winter\--\[1018\]

[014-089] Laughed along the stormy waters,

[014-090] Danced upon the windy headlands,

[014-091] On the storm his white hair streaming,\--

[014-092] And his steaming breath, ascending,

[014-093] On the pine-tops and the cedars

[014-094] Fell in frosty mists refulgent,

[014-095] Sprinkling somber shades with silver,

[014-096] Sprinkling all the woods with silver.

[014-097] By the lodge-fire all the winter

[014-098] Sat the Sea-Gull and the Red Fox,

[014-099] Sat and kindly spoke and chatted,

[014-100] Till the twain seemed friends together.

[014-101] Friends they seemed in word and action,

[014-102] But within the breast of either

[014-103] Smouldered still the baneful embers\--

[014-104] Fires of jealousy and hatred,\--

[014-105] Like a camp-fire in the forest

[014-106] Left by hunters and deserted;

[014-107] Only seems a bed of ashes,

[014-108] But the East-wind, Wâbun noódin,

[014-109] Scatters through the woods the ashes,

[014-110] Fans to flame the sleeping embers,

[014-111] And the wild-fire roars and rages,

[014-112] Roars and rages through the forest.

[014-113] So the baneful embers smouldered,

[014-114] Smouldered in the breast of either.

[014-115] From the far-off Sunny Islands,

[014-116] From the pleasant land of Summer,

[014-117] Where the spirits of the blessèd

[014-118] Feel no more the fangs of hunger,

[014-119] Or the cold breath of Kewâydin,

[014-120] Came a stately youth and handsome,

[014-121] Came Según the foe of Winter. \[1019\]

[014-122] Like the rising sun his face was,

[014-123] Like the shining stars his eyes were,

[014-124] Light his footsteps as the Morning\'s.

[014-125] In his hand were buds and blossoms,

[014-126] On his brow a blooming garland.

[014-127] Straightway to the icy wigwam

[014-128] Of old Péboân, the Winter,

[014-129] Strode Según and quickly entered.

[014-130] There old Péboân sat and shivered,

[014-131] Shivered o\'er his dying lodge-fire.

[014-132] \"Ah, my son, I bid you welcome;

[014-133] Sit and tell me your adventures;

[014-134] I will tell you of my power;

[014-135] We will pass the night together.\"

[014-136] Thus spake Péboân\--the Winter;

[014-137] Then he filled his pipe and lighted;

[014-138] Then by sacred custom raised it

[014-139] To the spirits in the ether;

[014-140] To the spirits in the caverns

[014-141] Of the hollow earth he lowered it.

[014-142] Thus he passed it to the spirits,

[014-143] And the unseen spirits puffed it.

[014-144] Next himself old Péboân honored;

[014-145] Thrice he puffed his pipe and passed it,

[014-146] Passed it to the handsome stranger.

[014-147] \"Lo I blow my breath,\" said Winter,

[014-148] \"And the laughing brooks are silent;

[014-149] Hard as flint become the waters,

[014-150] And the rabbit runs upon them.\"

[014-151] Then Según, the fair youth, answered:

[014-152] \"Lo I breathe upon the hill-sides,

[014-153] On the valleys and the meadows,

[014-154] And behold, as if by magic\--

[014-155] By the magic of the Spirits,

[014-156] Spring the flowers and tender grasses.\"

[014-157] Then old Péboân replying:

[014-158] \"Nah! \[1020\] I breathe upon the forests,

[014-159] And the leaves fall sere and yellow;

[014-160] Then I shake my locks and snow falls,

[014-161] Covering all the naked landscape.\"

[014-162] Then Según arose and answered:

[014-163] \"Nashké! \[1020\]\--see!\--I shake my ringlets;

[014-164] On the earth the warm rain falleth,

[014-165] And the flowers look up like children

[014-166] Glad-eyed from their mother\'s bosom.

[014-167] Lo my voice recalls the robin,

[014-168] Brings the bobolink and blue-bird,

[014-169] And the woods are full of music.

[014-170] With my breath I melt their fetters,

[014-171] And the brooks leap laughing onward.\"

[014-172] Then old Péboân looked upon him,

[014-173] Looked and knew Según, the Summer,

[014-174] From his eyes the big tears started

[014-175] And his boastful tongue was silent.

[014-176] Now Keezís \[1021\]\--the great life-giver,

[014-177] From his wigwam in Waubú-nong \[1021\]

[014-178] Rose and wrapped his shining blanket

[014-179] Round his giant form and started;

[014-180] Westward started on his journey,

[014-181] Striding on from hill to hill-top.

[014-182] Upward then he climbed the ether\--

[014-183] On the Bridge of Stars \[1022\] he traveled,

[014-184] Westward traveled on his journey

[014-185] To the far-off Sunset Mountains\--

[014-186] To the gloomy land of shadows.

[014-187] On the lodge-poles sang the robin,\--

[014-188] And the brooks began to murmur.

[014-189] On the South wind floated fragrance

[014-190] Of the early buds and blossoms.

[014-191] From old Péboân\'s eyes the teardrops

[014-192] Down his pale face ran in streamlets;

[014-193] Less and less he grew in stature

[014-194] Till he melted doun to nothing;

[014-195] And behold, from out the ashes,

[014-196] From the ashes of his lodge-fire,

[014-197] Sprang the Miscodeed \[1023\] and, blushing,

[014-198] Welcomed Según to the North-land.

[014-199] So from Sunny Isles returning,

[014-200] From the Summer-Land of spirits,

[014-201] On the poles of Panther\'s wigwam

[014-202] Sang Opeé-chee\--sang the robin.

[014-203] In the maples cooed the pigeons\--

[014-204] Cooed and wooed like silly lovers.

[014-205] \"Hah!\--hah!\" laughed the crow derisive,

[014-206] In the pine-top, at their folly,\--

[014-207] Laughed and jeered the silly lovers.

[014-208] Blind with love were they, and saw not;

[014-209] Deaf to all but love, and heard not;

[014-210] So they cooed and wooed unheeding,

[014-211] Till the gray hawk pounced upon them,

[014-212] And the old crow shook with laughter.

[014-213] On the tall cliff by the sea-shore

[014-214] Red Fox made a swing. She fastened

[014-215] Thongs of moose-hide to the pine-tree,

[014-216] To the strong arm of the pine-tree.

[014-217] like a hawk, above the waters,

[014-218] There she swung herself and fluttered,

[014-219] Laughing at the thought of danger,

[014-220] Swung and fluttered o\'er the waters.

[014-221] Then she bantered Sea-Gull, saying,

[014-222] \"See!\--I swing above the billows!

[014-223] Dare you swing above the billows,\--

[014-224] Swing like me above the billows?\"

[014-225] To herself said Sea-Gull\--\"Surely

[014-226] I will dare whatever danger

[014-227] Dares the Red Fox\--dares my rival;

[014-228] She shall never call me coward.\"

[014-229] So she swung above the waters\--

[014-230] Dizzy height above the waters,

[014-231] Pushed and aided by her rival,

[014-232] To and fro with reckless daring,

[014-233] Till the strong tree rocked and trembled,

[014-234] Rocked and trembled with its burden.

[014-235] As above the yawning billows

[014-236] Flew the Sea-Gull like a whirlwind,

[014-237] Red Fox, swifter than red lightning,

[014-238] Cut the cords, and headlong downward,

[014-239] Like an osprey from the ether,

[014-240] Like a wild-goose pierced with arrows,

[014-241] Fluttering fell the frantic woman,

[014-242] Fluttering fell into the waters\--

[014-243] Plunged and sank beneath the waters!

[014-244] Hark!\--the wailing of the West-wind!

[014-245] Hark!\--the wailing of the waters,

[014-246] And the beating of the billows!

[014-247] But no more the voice of Sea-Gull.

[014-248] In the wigwam sat the Red Fox,

[014-249] Hushed the wail of Waub-omeé-meé,

[014-250] Weeping for her absent mother.

[014-251] With the twinkling stars the hunter

[014-252] From the forest came and Raven.

[014-253] \"Sea-Gull wanders late\" said Red Fox,

[014-254] \"Late she wanders by the sea-shore,

[014-255] And some evil may befall her.\"

[014-256] In the misty morning twilight

[014-257] Forth went Panther and the Raven,

[014-258] Searched the forest and the marshes,

[014-259] Searched for leagues along the lake-shore,

[014-260] Searched the islands and the highlands;

[014-261] But they found no trace or tidings,

[014-262] Found no track in marsh or meadow,

[014-263] Found no trail in fen or forest,

[014-264] On the shore sand found no foot-prints.

[014-265] Many days they sought and found not.

[014-266] Then to Panther spoke the Raven:

[014-267] \"She is in the Land of Spirits\--

[014-268] Surely in the Land of Spirits.

[014-269] High at midnight I beheld her\--

[014-270] Like a flying star beheld her\--

[014-271] To the waves of Gitchee Gumee,

[014-272] Downward flashing through the ether.

[014-273] Thus she flashed that I might see her,

[014-274] See and know my mother\'s spirit;

[014-275] Thus she pointed to the waters,

[014-276] And beneath them lies her body,

[014-277] In the wigwam of the spirits\--

[014-278] In the lodge of Nébe-nâw-baigs.\" \[1024\]

[014-279] Then spoke Panther to the Raven:

[014-280] \"On the tall cliff by the waters

[014-281] Wait and watch with Waub-omeé-meé.

[014-282] If the Sea-Gull hear the wailing

[014-283] Of her infant she will answer.\"

[014-284] On the tall cliff by the waters

[014-285] So the Raven watched and waited;

[014-286] All the day he watched and waited,

[014-287] But the hungry infant slumbered,

[014-288] Slumbered by the side of Raven,

[014-289] Till the pines\' gigantic shadows

[014-290] Stretched and pointed to Waubú-Nong\--\[1021\]

[014-291] To the far off land of Sunrise;

[014-292] Then the wee one woke and famished,

[014-293] Made a long and piteous wailing.

[014-294] From afar where sky and waters

[014-295] Meet in misty haze and mingle,

[014-296] Straight toward the rocky highland,

[014-297] Straight as flies die feathered arrow,

[014-298] Straight to Raven and the infant

[014-299] Swiftly flew a snow white sea-gull.\--

[014-300] Flew and touched the earth a woman.

[014-301] And behold, the long-lost mother

[014-302] Caught her wailing child and nursed her,

[014-303] Sang a lullaby and nursed her.

[014-304] Thrice was wound a chain of silver

[014-305] Round her waist and strongly fastened.

[014-306] Far away into the waters\--

[014-307] To the wigwam of the spirits,\--

[014-308] To the lodge of Nébe-nâw-baigs,\--

[014-309] Stretched the magic chain of silver.

[014-310] Spoke the mother to the Raven:

[014-311] \"O my son\--my brave young hunter,

[014-312] Feed my tender little orphan;

[014-313] Be a father to my orphan;

[014-314] Be a mother to my orphan,\--

[014-315] For the Crafty Red Fox robbed us,\--

[014-316] Robbed the Sea-Gull of her husband,

[014-317] Robbed the infant of her mother.

[014-318] From this cliff the treacherous woman

[014-319] Headlong into Gitchee Gumee

[014-320] Plunged the mother of my orphan.

[014-321] Then a Nébe-nâw-baig caught me,\--

[014-322] Chief of all the Nébe-nâw-baigs\--

[014-323] Took me to his shining wigwam,

[014-324] In the cavern of the waters,

[014-325] Deep beneath the might waters.

[014-326] All below is burnished copper,

[014-327] All above is burnished silver

[014-328] Gemmed with amethyst and agates.

[014-329] As his wife the Spirit holds me;

[014-330] By this silver chain he holds me.

[014-331] When my little one is famished,

[014-332] When with long and piteous wailing

[014-333] Cries the orphan for her mother,

[014-334] Hither bring her, O my Raven;

[014-335] I will hear her,\--I will answer.

[014-336] Now the Nébe-nâw-baig calls me,\--

[014-337] Pulls the chain,\--I must obey him.\"

[014-338] Thus she spoke and in the twinkling

[014-339] Of a star the spirit-woman

[014-340] Changed into a snow-white sea-gull,

[014-341] Spread her wings and o\'er the waters

[014-342] Swiftly flew and swiftly vanished.
