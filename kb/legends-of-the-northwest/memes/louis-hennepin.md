# Louis Hennepin

Father Louis Hennepin — Franciscan missionary and early European explorer of the upper Mississippi.

Hennepin was among the earliest Europeans to document the upper Mississippi region and its Native peoples. His accounts of the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] of St. Anthony provided early source material for later writers including Gordon.

## In Gordon's Legends

Gordon's *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest* preserves Louis Hennepin's role through English verse, informed by his study of [[dakota|Dakota]] history (P01-007) and the work of scholars like Riggs (P01-063).


Hennepin's accounts of the upper Mississippi and the [[dakota|Dakota]] people were among the earliest European records. His descriptions of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] of St. Anthony (which Gordon renders in *The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]*) provided source material for later writers including Carver and Gordon himself (P01-007).

## At Mille Lacs

In 1679–80, Father Hennepin visited the Dakota at [[Mille Lacs]], where [[wazi-kute|Wazí-kuté]] was the head chief (*Itâncan*) of the Isantee band (017-161–162). Hennepin recorded the chief's name as Ouasicoudé, translating it as the "Pierced Pine" (017-163–164).

## Ethnographic Observations

Hennepin was the first European to describe many Dakota customs in detail. His account of the plum-stone game captures the intensity of Dakota gambling:

> They are placed in a dish and thrown up like dice; indeed the game is virtually a game of dice. Hennepin says: "There are some so given to this game that they will gamble away even their great coat. Those who conduct the game cry at the top of their voices when they rattle the platter and they strike their shoulders so hard as to leave them all black with the blows."
>
> (017-193–197)

Hennepin also documented the [[sweat-lodge|vapor bath]] ritual at Mille Lacs, where Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin subjected him to three sessions in a week (018-061–064).

## Geographic Names

Hennepin's nomenclature shaped later cartography. He called [[lake-pepin|Lake Pepin]] the "Lake of Tears" (012-087), and his *Description de la Louisiane* (Paris, 1683) provided the first detailed European account of the upper Mississippi region (017-187–188).
