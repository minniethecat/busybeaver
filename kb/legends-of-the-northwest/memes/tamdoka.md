# Tamdóka

A [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior, son of the tall [[wazi-kute|Wazí-kuté]], and a prominent antagonist in the "[[winona|Winona]]" legend cycle. Tamdóka is portrayed as handsome, fleet-footed, and proud — a skilled hunter and runner whose rivalry for [[winona|Winona]]'s hand drives much of the legend's conflict.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Warrior, hunter, suitor of [[winona|Winona]]
- **Family**: Son of [[wazi-kute|Wazí-kuté]] (008-238); mother referenced as "mother of boastful Tamdóka" (008-031)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Tamdóka appears throughout the "[[winona|Winona]]" legend cycle (Chapters VI–XII) as a central rival figure. He competes for the hand of [[winona|Winona]], the compassionate daughter of [[ta-te-psin|Ta-té-psin]], and his persistent pursuit of her creates the central tension leading to [[winona|Winona]]'s tragic leap.

### 关键事件

Tamdóka first appears as a rejected suitor — [[winona|Winona]] "rejected the handsome Tamdóka" (007-036). He proves himself the fastest runner among the [[dakota|Dakota]] braves: "the leader of all is Tamdóka" and he "first reaches the post" in the foot race (007-277, 007-282).

During the race, Tamdóka is overtaken by another warrior, and his pride is wounded — "dark was the face of Tamdóka" (008-049). He resorts to tripping his rival: "the foot of the wily Tamdóka had tripped him" (008-091).

Tamdóka's pursuit of [[winona|Winona]] becomes increasingly aggressive. He brings gifts to her lodge (011-107), but [[winona|Winona]] declares "The crafty Tamdóka I hate" (011-006). [[ta-te-psin|Ta-té-psin]] warns his daughter: "Beware of Tamdóka, — beware" (009-046).

Tamdóka leads a canoe chase after [[winona|Winona]] with ten warriors: "the ten tawny braves of Tamdóka" (009-202). The chase culminates in violence — Tamdóka's signal of death (010-044) and his involvement in the killing at Kathága. DuLuth later notes "a snake in the bush is Tamdóka" (010-096).

## 历史/文化背景

Tamdóka is a legendary rather than historical figure, representing the archetype of the proud warrior-suitor in [[dakota|Dakota]] oral tradition. His characterization — handsome but crafty, swift but treacherous — embodies the tension between outward martial virtue and inner moral failing. Gordon's portrayal draws on familiar narrative patterns of the rejected suitor turned antagonist, rendered through the lens of [[dakota|Dakota]] legend.
