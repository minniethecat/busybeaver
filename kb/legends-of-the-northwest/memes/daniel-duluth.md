# Daniel DuLuth

Daniel Greysolon, Sieur du Lhut — known in Gordon's *Legends of the Northwest* as **DuLuth** or **Duluth** — was a French explorer and soldier who ventured into the upper Mississippi River region in the late 17th century. The city of Duluth, Minnesota, bears his name. He was among the first Europeans to explore the Dakota territories around Lake Superior and the headwaters of the Mississippi.

Gordon describes him as "a bold and adventuresome man" who was "dauntless in danger." DuLuth was a French captain who explored the upper Mississippi region and held council with the [[dakota|Dakotas]] (009-110).

## Role in the Winona Narrative

In the *Winona* section of *Legends of the Northwest*, DuLuth appears as a central figure — a French trader and suitor who competes for the affection of [[Winona]], the Dakota maiden. He arrives at the Dakota village of [[kapoza|Kathága]] with his voyageur companions and is received by Chief [[wazi-kute|Wázi-kuté]].

DuLuth and his men were welcomed into the Lodge of the Stranger, where robes of beaver and bison were spread and the Peace pipe was smoked with the Frenchman. The scene depicts the ceremonial hospitality extended to the European visitor by the [[isantee-band|Isantee]] Dakotas.

## Character and Faith

Gordon notes that Duluth was a **devout Catholic**, a detail grounding the poetic character in the historical figure. His faith is depicted as a source of inner strength, contrasting with the Dakota spiritual world that surrounds him.

When Winona's tragic end approaches, Duluth offers comfort: "Winona, fear not," said Duluth. At the Falls of St. Anthony ([[falls-of-st-anthony|Ha-Ha]]), he stands alone contemplating the events:

> Duluth stood alone by the roar of the Ha-ha.

## The Expedition's Route

After the events at Kathága, DuLuth and his voyageurs followed an ancient canoe route from the Wild Rice River to Lake Superior — a portage trod by the feet of half-breeds and voyageurs for more than two centuries, and by the Indians for perhaps a thousand years.

The journey carried them through Chippewa territory at the western end of Lake Superior. Gordon reflects on the passage of time since DuLuth's arrival on that wild, somber shore in the unbroken forest primeval — two hundred white winters and more before the poet's own time.

## In Gordon's Legends

Gordon's *[[legend|Legends]] of the Northwest* preserves Daniel DuLuth's role through English verse, informed by his study of [[dakota|Dakota]] history (P01-007) and the work of scholars like [[stephen-riggs|Riggs]] (P01-063). In the *[[winona|Winona]]* legend, he appears as a diplomatic figure addressing the assembled [[dakota|Dakota]] braves.
