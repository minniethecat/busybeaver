Hóhé is the [[dakota|Dakota]] name for the [[assiniboin]] people (001-185), a Siouan-speaking group that had split from the [[dakota|Dakotas]] and were allied with the [[ojibway|Ojibways]], known for their tall stature and skilled hunters.

## Definition / Identity

In [[dakota|Dakota]] usage, "Hóhé" refers specifically to the Assiniboin people — the "tall Hóhé" (002-209, 003-054, 005-068) and "brave Hóhé" (001-185, 003-042, 003-066) as they appear throughout Gordon's poems. They are also known as "Stone-roasters" in English, a name derived from their distinctive method of cooking with heated stones.

- **Origin**: [[dakota|Dakota]] language; the English term "Assiniboin" comes from the Ojibway "Assin-i-bo-in" meaning "Stone-roasters"
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]] / English / Ojibway
- **Domain**: ethnography / terminology / social-structure

The Hóhé were originally part of the [[dakota|Dakota]] Confederation but separated and became allies of the [[ojibway|Ojibways]], traditional enemies of the [[dakota|Dakotas]]. In Gordon's preface, he notes they were among the principal tribes of the region.

## In the Book

Gordon uses "Hóhé" extensively throughout his narrative poems, particularly in "[[winona|Winona]]" where a Hóhé warrior becomes a central romantic figure.

### Role in the Legends

In "[[winona|Winona]]," a tall Hóhé chief becomes the lover of [[winona]] (001-185). He appears as a noble warrior — "a brave Hóhé loved she better than all" (001-185). The narrative follows his presence from "the land of the Hóhé" (002-072) through the courtship and tragic events. The "tall Hóhé" is a recurring epithet, emphasizing their physical distinction (002-209, 003-010, 003-054).

### Representative Passages

> "And a brave Hóhé loved she better than all" (001-185)

> "Far away in the land of the Hóhé dwelt" (002-072)

> "In the happy land of the tall Hóhé" (002-209, 003-010)

> "And Wiwâstè smiled on the tall Hóhé" (003-054)

> "And laugh with her lover — the brave Hóhé" (003-066)
