[012-018] Still hope, like a star in the night

[012-019] gleaming oft through the broken clouds somber,

[012-020] Cheered the heart of Winona, and bright,

[012-021] on her dreams, beamed the face of the Frenchman.

[012-022] As the thought of a loved one and lost,

[012-023] sad and sweet were her thoughts of the White Chief;

[012-024] In the moon\'s mellow light, like a [[spirit|ghost]],

[012-025] walked Winona alone by the Ha-ha,

[012-026] Ever wrapped in a dream. Far away

[012-027] \--to the land of the sunrise\--she wandered;

[012-028] On the blue rolling Tánka Medé, \[a\]

[012-029] in the midst of her dreams, she beheld him\--

[012-030] In his white-winged canoe, like a bird,

[012-031] to the land of [[dakota|Dakotas]] returning;

[012-032] And often in fancy she heard

[012-033] the dip of his oars on the river.

[012-034] On the dark waters glimmered the moon,

[012-035] but she saw not the boat of the Frenchman;

[012-036] On the somber night bugled the loon,

[012-037] but she heard not the song of the boatmen.

[012-038] The moon waxed and waned, but the star

[012-039] of her hope never waned to the setting;

[012-040] Through her tears she beheld it afar,

[012-041] like a torch on the eastern horizon.

[012-042] \"He will come,\--he is coming,\" she said;

[012-043] \"he will come, for my White Eagle promised,\"

[012-044] And low to the bare earth the maid

[012-045] bent her ear for the sound of his footsteps.

[012-046] \"He is gone, but his voice in my ear

[012-047] still remains like the voice of the robin;

[012-048] He is far, but his footsteps I hear;

[012-049] he is coming; my White Chief is coming!\"

[012-067] Down the broad Gitchee Seebee \[a\]

[012-068] the band took their way to the Games at Keóza.

[012-069] While the swift-footed hunters by land

[012-070] ran the shores for the elk and the bison.

[012-071] Like magás \[b\] ride the birchen canoes

[012-072] on the breast of the dark Gitchee Seebee;

[012-073] By the willow-fringed islands they cruise

[012-074] by the grassy hills green to their summits;

[012-075] By the lofty bluffs hooded with oaks

[012-076] that darken the deep with their shadows;

[012-077] And bright in the sun gleam the strokes

[012-078] of the oars in the hands of the women.

[012-079] With the band went Winona.

[012-080] The oar plied the maid with the skill of a hunter.

[012-081] They loitered and camped on the shore of Remníca

[012-082] \--the Lake of the Mountains. \[c\]

[012-083] There the fleet hunters followed the deer,

[012-084] and the thorny _pahin_ \[d\] for the women.

[012-086] \[b\] Wild Geese

[012-087] \[c\] Lake Pepin; by Hennepin called Lake of Tears\--Called by the Dakotas

[012-088] Remnee-chah-Mday\--Lake of the Mountains.

[012-089] \[d\] Pah hin\--the porcupine\--the quill of which are greatly prized for

[012-091] From the tees rose the smoke of good cheer,

[012-092] curling blue through the tops of the maples,

[012-093] Near the foot of a cliff that arose,

[012-094] like the battle-scarred walls of a castle.

[012-095] Up-towering, in rugged repose,

[012-096] to a dizzy height over the waters.

[012-097] But the man-wolf still followed his prey,

[012-098] and the step-mother ruled in the tepee;

[012-099] Her will must Winona obey,

[012-100] by the custom and law of Dakotas.

[012-101] The gifts to the teepee were brought

[012-102] \--the blankets, and beads of the White men,

[012-103] And Winona, the orphaned, was bought

[012-104] by the crafty relentless Tamdóka.

[012-105] In the Spring-time of life,

[012-106] in the flush of the gladsome mid-May days of Summer,

[012-107] When the bobolink sang and the thrush,

[012-108] and the red robin chirped in the branches,

[012-109] To the tent of the brave must she go;

[012-110] she must kindle the fire in his tepee;

[012-111] She must sit in the lodge of her foe,

[012-112] as a slave at the feet of her master.

[012-113] Alas for her waiting!

[012-114] the wings of the East-wind have brought her no tidings;

[012-115] On the meadow the meadow-lark sings

[012-116] but sad is her song to Winona,

[012-117] For the glad warblers melody brings

[012-118] but the memory of voices departed.

[012-119] The Day-Spirit walked in the west

[012-120] to his lodge in the land of the shadows;

[012-121] His shining face gleamed on the crest

[012-122] of the oak-hooded hills and the mountains,

[012-123] And the meadow-lark hied to her nest,

[012-124] and the mottled owl peeped from her cover.

[012-125] But hark! from the teepees a cry!

[012-126] Hear the shouts of the hurrying warriors!

[012-127] Are the steps of the enemy nigh,

[012-128] \--of the crafty and creeping Ojibways?

[012-129] Nay; look on the dizzy cliff high!

[012-130] \--on the brink of the cliff stands Winona!

[012-131] Her sad face up-turned to the sky. Hark!

[012-132] I hear the wild [[song|chant]] of her [[death-song]]:

[012-133] My Father\'s Spirit, look down, look down\--

[012-134] From your hunting-grounds in the shining skies;

[012-135] Behold, for the light of my soul is gone,\--

[012-139] I harked for his footsteps in vain; afar

[012-140] His bark sailed over the Sunrise-sea.

[012-141] Long have I watched till my heart is cold;

[012-142] In my breast it is heavy and cold as stone.

[012-143] No more shall Winona his face behold,

[012-144] And the robin that sang in her heart is gone.

[012-145] Shall I sit at the feet of the treacherous brave?

[012-146] On his hateful couch shall Winona lie?

[012-147] Shall she kindle his fire like a coward slave?

[012-148] No!\--a warrior\'s daughter can bravely die.

[012-149] My Father\'s Spirit, look down, look down\--

[012-150] From your hunting-grounds in the shining skies;

[012-151] Behold, for the light of my soul is gone,\--

[012-152] The light is gone and Winona dies.

[012-153] Swift the strong hunters clomb as she sang,

[012-154] and the foremost of all was Tamdóka;

[012-155] From crag to crag upward he sprang;

[012-156] like a panther he leaped to the summit.

[012-157] Too late! on the brave as he crept

[012-158] turned the maid in her scorn and defiance;

[012-159] Then swift from the dizzy height leaped.

[012-160] Like a brant arrow-pierced in mid-heaven.

[012-161] Down-whirling and fluttering she fell,

[012-163] Forever she sank mid the wail,

[012-164] and the wild lamentation of women.

[012-165] Her lone spirit evermore dwells

[012-166] in the depths of the Lake of the Mountains,

[012-167] And the lofty cliff evermore tells

[012-169] In the silence of sorrow the night

[012-170] o\'er the earth spread her wide, sable pinions;

[012-171] And the stars \[18\] hid their faces,

[012-172] and light on the lake fell the tears of the spirits.

[012-180] from the Falls to the Games at Keóza.

THE LEGEND OF THE FALLS.

[012-187] Note: An-pe-tu Sa-pa\--Clouded Day\--was the name of the Dakota mother who

[012-188] committed suicide, as related in this legend, by plunging over the Falls

[012-189] of St. Anthony. Schoolcraft calls her \"_Ampata_ Sapa.\" _Ampata_ is not

[012-190] Dakota. There are several versions of this legend, all agreeing in the

[012-191] main points.

[012-192] \[Read at the celebration of the Old Settlers of Hennepin County, at the

[012-193] Academy of Music, Minneapolis, July 4, 1879.\]

[012-194] (The numerals refer to notes in the Appendix.)

[012-195] On the Spirit-Island \[a\] sitting under midnight\'s misty moon,

[012-196] Lo I see the spirits flitting o\'er the waters one by one!

[012-197] Slumber wraps the silent city, and the droning mills are dumb;

[012-198] One lone whippowil\'s shrill ditty calls her mate that ne\'er will come.

[012-199] Sadly moans the mighty river, foaming down the fettered falls,

[012-200] Where of old he thundered ever o\'er abrupt and lofty walls.

[012-201] Great Unktèhee \[69\]\--god of waters\--lifts no more his mighty head;\--

[012-202] Fled he with the timid otters?\--lies he in the cavern dead?

[012-203] \[a\] The small island of rock a few rods below the Falls, was called by the

[012-204] Dakotas Wanagee We-ta----Spirit-Island. They say the spirit of Anpetu Sapa

[012-205] sits upon that island at night and pours forth her sorrow in song. They

[012-206] also say that from time out of mind, war-eagles nested on that island,

[012-207] until the advent of white men frightened them away. This seems to be true.

[012-208] Carver\'s Travels. London. 1778, p. 71.

[012-209] Hark!\--the waters hush their sighing, and the whippowil her call,

[012-210] Through the moon-lit mists are flying dusky shadows silent all.

[012-211] Lo from out the waters foaming\--from the cavern deep and dread\--

[012-212] Through the glamour and the gloaming, comes a spirit of the dead.

[012-213] Sad she seems, her tresses raven on her tawny shoulders rest;

[012-214] Sorrow on her brow is graven, in her arms a babe is pressed.

[012-215] Hark!\--she chants the solemn story,\--sings the legend sad and old,

[012-216] And the river wrapt in glory listens while the tale is told.

[012-217] Would you hear the legend olden, hearken while I tell the tale\--

[012-218] Shorn, alas, of many a golden, weird Dakota chant and wail.

THE LEGEND.

[012-219] Tall was young Wanâta, stronger than Heyóka\'s \[16\] giant form.

[012-220] Laughed at flood and fire and hunger, faced the fiercest winter storm.

[012-221] When Wakínyan \[32\] flashed and thundered, when Unktéhee raved and roared,

[012-222] All but brave Wanâta wondered, and the gods with fear implored.

[012-223] When the war-whoop wild resounded, calling friends to meet the foe,

[012-224] From the teepee swift he bounded, armed with polished lance and bow.

[012-225] In the battle\'s din and clangor fast his fatal arrows flew,

[012-226] Flashed his fiery eyes with anger,\--many a haughty foe he slew.

[012-227] Hunter, swift was he and cunning, caught the beaver, slew the bear,

[012-228] Overtook the roebuck running, dragged the panther from his lair.

[012-229] Loved was he by many a maiden; many a dark eye glanced in vain;

[012-230] Many a heart with sighs was laden for the love it might not gain.

[012-231] So they called the brave \"Ska Câpa\"; \[a\] but the fairest of the band\--

[012-232] Moon-faced, meek Anpétu-Sâpa\--won the hunter\'s heart and hand.

[012-233] \[a\] Or Capa Ska\--White beaver. White beavers are very rare, very cunning

[012-234] and hard to catch.

[012-235] From the wars with triumph burning, from the chase of bison fleet,

[012-236] To his lodge the brave returning, spread his trophies at her feet.

[012-237] Love and joy sat in the tepee; him a black-eyed boy she bore;

[012-238] But alas, she lived to weep a love she lost forevermore.

[012-239] For the warriors chose Wanâta first Itâncan \[a\] of the band.

[012-240] At the council-fire he sat a leader loved a chieftain grand.

[012-241] Proud was fair Anpétu-Sâpa, and her eyes were glad with joy;

[012-242] Proud was she and very happy, with her chieftain and her boy.

[012-243] But alas, the fatal honor that her brave Wanâta won,

[012-244] Brought a bitter woe upon her,\--hid with clouds the summer sun

[012-245] For among the brave Dakotas, wives bring honor to the chief.

[012-246] On the vine-clad Minnesota\'s banks he met the Scarlet Leaf.

[012-247] Young and fair was Apè-dúta \[b\]\--full of craft and very fair;

[012-248] Proud she walked a queen of beauty with her wondrous flowing hair.

[012-249] In her net of hair she caught him\--caught Wanâta with her wiles;

[012-250] All in vain his wife besought him\--begged in vain his wonted smiles.

[012-251] Apè-dúta ruled the teepee\--all Wanâta\'s smiles were hers;

[012-252] When the lodge was wrapped in sleep a star \[c\] beheld the mother\'s tears.

[012-253] Long she strove to do her duty for the black-eyed babe she bore;

[012-254] But the proud, imperious beauty made her sad forevermore.

[012-255] Still she dressed the skins of beaver, bore the burdens, spread the fare;

[012-256] Patient ever, murmuring never, while her cheeks were creased with care.
