[010-001] \'Tis midnight. The rising moon gleams,

[010-002] weird and still o\'er the dusky horizon;

[010-003] Through the hushed, somber forest she beams,

[010-004] and fitfully gloams on the meadows;

[010-005] And a dim, glimmering pathway she paves,

[010-006] at times, on the dark stretch of river.

[010-007] The winds are asleep in the caves

[010-008] \--in the heart of the far-away mountains;

[010-009] And here on the meadows and there,

[010-010] the lazy mists gather and hover;

[010-011] And the lights of the Fen-Spirits \[72\] flare

[010-012] and dance on the low-lying marshes,

[010-013] As still as the footsteps of death

[010-014] by the bed of the babe and its mother;

[010-015] And hushed are the pines, and beneath

[010-016] lie the weary limbed boatmen in slumber.

[010-017] Walk softly,\--walk softly, O Moon,

[010-018] through the gray, broken clouds in thy pathway,

[010-019] For the earth lies asleep, and the boon

[010-020] of repose is bestowed on the weary.

[010-021] Toiling hands have forgotten their care;

[010-022] e\'en the brooks have forgotten to murmur;

[010-023] But hark!\--there\'s a sound on the air!

[010-024] \--\'tis the light-rustling robes of the Spirits.

[010-025] Like the breath of the night in the leaves,

[010-026] or the murmur of reeds on the river,

[010-027] In the cool of the mid-summer eves,

[010-028] when the blaze of the day has descended.

[010-029] Low-crouching and shadowy forms,

[010-030] as still as the gray morning\'s footsteps,

[010-031] Creep sly as the serpent that charms,

[010-032] on her nest in the meadow, the plover;

[010-033] In the shadows of pine-trunks they creep,

[010-034] but their panther-eyes gleam in the fire-light,

[010-035] As they peer on the white men asleep,

[010-036] in the glow of the fire, on their blankets.

[010-037] Lo, in each swarthy right hand a knife,

[010-038] in the left hand, the bow and the arrows!

[010-039] Brave Frenchmen! awake to the strife!

[010-040] \--or you sleep in the forest forever.

[010-041] Nay, nearer and nearer they glide,

[010-042] like ghosts on the fields of their battles,

[010-043] Till close on the sleepers, they bide

[010-044] but the signal of death from Tamdóka.

[010-045] Still the sleepers sleep on.

[010-046] Not a breath stirs the leaves of the awe-stricken forest;

[010-047] The hushed air is heavy with death;

[010-048] like the footsteps of death are the moments.

[010-049] \"_Arise_!\"\--At the word, with a bound,

[010-050] to their feet spring the vigilant Frenchmen;

[010-051] And the dark, dismal forests resound

[010-052] to the crack and the roar of their rifles;

[010-053] And seven writhing forms on the ground

[010-054] clutch the earth. From the pine-tops the screech owl

[010-055] Screams and flaps his wide wings in affright,

[010-056] and plunges away through the shadows;

[010-057] And swift on the wings of the night

[010-058] flee the dim, phantom forms of the spirit.

[010-059] Like cabris \[80\] when white wolves pursue,

[010-060] fled the four yet remaining [[dakota|Dakotas]];

[010-061] Through forest and fen-land they flew,

[010-062] and wild terror howled on their footsteps.

[010-063] And one was Tamdóka. DuLuth through the night

[010-064] sent his voice like a trumpet;

[010-065] \"Ye are Sons of Unktéhee, forsooth!

[010-066] Return to your mothers, ye cowards!\"

[010-067] His shrill voice they heard as they fled,

[010-068] but only the echoes made answer.

[010-069] At the feet of the brave Frenchmen, dead,

[010-070] lay seven swarthy Sons of Unktéhee;

[010-071] And there, in the midst of the slain,

[010-072] they found, as it gleamed in the fire light,

[010-073] The horn-handled knife from the Seine,

[010-074] where it fell from the hand of Tamdóka.

\[Illustration: THE RIVER WAKPA WAKAN OR SPIRIT RIVER\]

[010-075] In the gray of the morn,

[010-076] ere the sun peeped over the dewy horizon,

[010-077] Their journey again was begun,

[010-078] and they toiled up the swift, winding river;

[010-079] And many a shallow they passed

[010-080] on their way to the Lake of the Spirits;

[010-081] But dauntless they reached it at last,

[010-082] and found Akee-pá-kee-tin\'s village, \[a\]

[010-083] On an isle in the midst of the lake;

[010-084] and a day in his teepee they tarried.

[010-085] \[a\] see Hennepin\'s account of Aqui-pa-que-tin and his village.

[010-086] Shea\'s Hennepin 227.

[010-087] Of the deed in the wilderness spake,

[010-088] to the brave Chief, the frank-hearted Frenchman.

[010-089] A generous man was the Chief

[010-090] and a friend of the fearless explorer;

[010-091] And dark was his visage with grief

[010-092] at the treacherous act of the warriors.

[010-093] \"Brave Wazi-Kuté is a man,

[010-094] and his heart is as clear as the sun-light;

[010-095] But the head of a treacherous clan,

[010-096] and a snake in the bush is Tamdóka,\"

[010-097] Said the chief; and he promised Duluth,

[010-098] on the word of a friend and a warrior,

[010-099] To carry the pipe and the truth

[010-100] to his cousin, the chief at Kathága;

[010-101] For thrice at the Tânka Medé

[010-102] had he smoked in the lodge of the Frenchman;

[010-103] And thrice had he carried away

[010-104] the bountiful gifts of the trader.

[010-105] When the chief could no longer prevail

[010-106] on the white men to rest in his teepee,

[010-107] He guided their feet on the trail

[010-108] to the lakes of the winding Rice-River. \[a\]

[010-109] Now on speeds the light bark canoe,

[010-110] through the lakes to the broad Gitchee Seebee; \[b\]

[010-111] And up the great river they row,

[010-112] \--up the Big Sandy Lake and Savanna;

[010-113] And down through the meadows they go

[010-114] to the river of broad Gitchee Gumee. \[c\]

[010-115] \[a\] Now called \"Mud River\"\--it empties into the Mississippi at Aitkin.

[010-116] \[b\] _Gitchee seebee_\--Big River\--the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] name for the Mississippi,

[010-117] which is a corruption of Gitchee Seebee\--as Michigan is a corruption of

[010-118] _Gitchee Gumee_\--Great Lake, the Ojibway name of [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]].

[010-119] \[c\] The Ojibways call the [[st-louis-river|St. Louis River]]

[010-120] _Gitchee-Gumee See-bee\--Great-lake River_, i.e. the river of the Great Lake

[010-121] (Lake Superior).

\[Illustration: DALLES OF THE ST. LOUIS\]

[010-122] Still onward they speed to the Dalles

[010-123] \--to the roar of the white-rolling rapids,

[010-124] Where the dark river tumbles and falls

[010-125] down the ragged ravine of the mountains,

[010-126] And singing his wild jubilee

[010-127] to the low-moaning pines and the cedars,

[010-128] Rushes on to the unsalted sea

[010-129] o\'er the ledges upheaved by volcanoes.

[010-130] Their luggage the _voyageurs_ bore

[010-131] down the long, winding path of the portage, \[a\]

[010-132] While they mingled their song

[010-133] with the roar of the turbid and turbulent waters.

[010-134] Down-wimpling and murmuring there,

[010-135] twixt two dewy hills winds a streamlet,

[010-136] Like a long, flaxen ringlet of hair

[010-137] on the breast of a maid in her slumber.

[010-138] \[a\] The route of Duluth above described\--from the mouth of the Wild Rice

[010-139] Mud River to Lake Superior\--was for centuries and still is, the Indians\'

[010-140] canoe route. I have walked over the old portage from the foot of the

[010-141] Dalles to the St. Louis above\--trod by the feet of half-breeds and

[010-142] _voyageurs_ for more than two centuries, and by the Indians for,

[010-143] perhaps, a thousand years.

[010-144] All safe at the foot of the trail,

[010-145] where they left it, they found their felucca,

[010-146] And soon to the wind spread the sail,

[010-147] and glided at ease through the waters,

[010-148] Through the meadows and lakelets and forth,

[010-149] round the point stretching south like a finger,

[010-150] From the mist-wreathen hill on the north,

[010-151] sloping down to the bay and the lake-side

[010-152] And behold, at the foot of the hill,

[010-153] a cluster of Chippewa wigwams,

[010-154] And the busy wives plying with skill

[010-155] their nets in the emerald waters.

[010-156] Two hundred white winters and more

[010-157] have fled from the face of the Summer

[010-158] Since DuLuth, on that wild, somber shore,

[010-159] in the unbroken forest primeval,

[010-160] From the midst of the spruce and the pines,

[010-161] saw the smoke of the wigwams up-curling,

[010-162] Like the fumes from the temples and shrines

[010-163] of the Druids of old in their forests.

[010-164] Ah, little he dreamed then, forsooth,

[010-165] that a city would stand on that hill-side,

[010-166] And bear the proud name of Duluth,

[010-167] the untiring and dauntless explorer.

[010-168] A refuge for ships from the storms,

[010-169] and for men from the bee-hives of Europe.

[010-170] Out-stretching her long, iron arms

[010-171] o\'er an empire of Saxons and Normans.

[010-172] The swift west-wind sang in the sails,

[010-173] and on flew the boat like a Sea-Gull,

[010-174] By the green, templed hills and the dales,

[010-175] and the dark rugged rocks of the North Shore;

[010-176] For the course of the brave Frenchman

[010-177] lay to his fort at the Gáh-mah-na-ték-wáhk, \[83\]

[010-178] By the shore of the grand Thunder Bay,

[010-179] where the gray rocks loom up into mountains;

[010-180] Where the Stone Giant sleeps on the Cape,

[010-181] and the god of the storms makes the thunder, \[83\]

[010-182] And the Makinak \[83\] lifts his huge shape

[010-183] from the breast of the blue-rolling waters,

[010-184] And thence to the south-westward led his course

[010-185] to the Holy Ghost Mission. \[84\]

[010-186] Where the Black Robes, the brave shepherds,

[010-187] fed their wild sheep on the isle Wau-ga-bá-mé. \[84\]

\[Illustration: SUNSET BAY, LAKE SUPERIOR.\]

[010-188] In the enchanting Cha-quam-e-gon Bay,

[010-189] defended by all the Apostles; \[a\]

[010-190] And thence by the Ké-we-naw,

[010-191] lay his course to the Mission Sainte Marie. \[b\]

[010-192] Now the waves drop their myriad hands,

[010-193] and streams the white hair of the surges;

[010-194] DuLuth at the steady helm stands,

[010-195] and he hums as he bounds o\'er the billows:

[010-196] O sweet is the carol of bird,

[010-197] And sweet is the murmur of streams,

[010-198] But sweeter the voice that I heard\--

[010-199] In the night\--in the midst of my dreams.

[010-200] \[a\] The Apostle Islands.

[010-201] \[b\] At the Saut St. Marie.

[010-202] \'Tis the moon of the sere, falling leaves.

[010-203] From the heads of the maples the west-wind

[010-204] Plucks the red-and-gold plumage

[010-205] and grieves on the meads for the rose and the lily;

[010-206] Their brown leaves the moaning oaks strew,

[010-207] and the breezes that roam on the prairies,

[010-208] Low-whistling and wanton pursue

[010-209] the down of the silk weed and thistle.

[010-210] All sere are the prairies and brown,

[010-211] in the glimmer and haze of the Autumn;

[010-212] From the far northern marshes flock down,

[010-213] by thousands, the geese and the mallards.

[010-214] From the meadows and wide-prairied plains,

[010-215] for their long southward journey preparing,

[010-216] In croaking flocks gather the cranes,

[010-217] and choose with loud clamor their leaders.

[010-218] The breath of the evening is cold,

[010-219] and lurid along the horizon

[010-220] The flames of the prairies are rolled,

[010-221] on the somber skies flashing their torches.

[010-222] At noontide a shimmer of gold,

[010-223] through the haze, pours the sun from his pathway.

[010-224] The wild-rice is gathered and ripe,

[010-225] on the moors, lie the scarlet po-pán-ka; \[a\]

[010-226] [[michabo|Michabo]] \[85\] is smoking his pipe,

[010-227] \--\'tis the soft, dreamy Indian Summer,

[010-228] When the god of the South as he flies

[010-229] from Wazíya, the god of the Winter,

[010-230] For a time turns his beautiful eyes,

[010-231] and backward looks over his shoulder.

[010-232] \[a\] Cranberries.

[010-233] It is noon. From his path in the skies

[010-234] the red sun looks down on Kathága,

[010-235] Asleep in the valley it lies,

[010-236] for the swift hunters follow the bison.

[010-237] Ta-té-psin, the aged brave, bends

[010-238] as he walks by the side of Winona;

[010-239] Her arm to his left hand she lends,

[010-240] and he feels with his staff for the pathway;

[010-241] On his slow, feeble footsteps attends

[010-242] his gray dog, the watchful Wicháka; \[a\]

[010-243] For blind in his years is the chief

[010-244] of a fever that followed the Summer,

[010-245] And the days of Ta-té-psin are brief.

[010-246] Once more by the dark-rolling river

[010-247] Sits the Chief in the warm, dreamy haze

[010-248] of the beautiful Summer in Autumn;

[010-249] And the faithful dog lovingly lays his head

[010-250] at the feet of his master.

[010-251] On a dead, withered branch sits a crow,

[010-252] down-peering askance at the old man;

[010-253] On the marge of the river below

[010-254] romp the nut-brown and merry-voiced children,

[010-255] And the dark waters silently flow,

[010-256] broad and deep, to the plunge of the Ha-ha.

[010-257] \[a\] Wee-chah kah\--literally \"Faithful\".

[010-258] By his side sat Winona.

[010-259] He laid his thin, shriveled hand on her tresses,

[010-260] \"Winona my daughter,\" he said,

[010-261] \"no longer thy father beholds thee;

[010-262] But he feels the long locks of thy hair,

[010-263] and the days that are gone are remembered,

[010-264] When Sisóka \[a\] sat faithful and fair

[010-265] in the lodge of swift footed Ta-té-psin.

[010-266] The white years have broken my spear;

[010-267] from my bow they have taken the bow-string;

[010-268] But once on the trail of the deer,

[010-269] like a gray wolf from sunrise till sunset,

[010-270] By woodland and meadow and mere,

[010-271] ran the feet of Ta-té-psin untiring.

[010-272] But dim are the days that are gone,

[010-273] and darkly around me they wander,

[010-274] Like the pale, misty face of the moon

[010-275] when she walks through the storm of the winter;

[010-276] And sadly they speak in my ear.

[010-277] I have looked on the graves of my kindred.

[010-278] The Land of the Spirits is near.

[010-279] Death walks by my side like a shadow.

[010-280] Now open thine ear to my voice,

[010-281] and thy heart to the wish of thy father,

[010-282] And long will Winona rejoice

[010-283] that she heeded the words of Ta-té-psin.

[010-284] The cold, cruel winter is near,

[010-285] and famine will sit in the teepee.

[010-286] What hunter will bring me the deer,

[010-287] or the flesh of the bear or the bison?

[010-288] For my kinsmen before me have gone;

[010-289] they hunt in the land of the shadows.

[010-290] In my old age forsaken, alone,

[010-291] must I die in my teepee of hunger?

[010-292] Winona, Tamdóka can make my empty lodge

[010-293] laugh with abundance;

[010-294] For thine aged and blind father\'s sake,

[010-295] to the son of the Chief speak the promise.

[010-296] For gladly again to my tee

[010-297] will the bridal gifts come for my daughter.

[010-298] A fleet-footed hunter is he,

[010-299] and the good [[spirit|spirits]] feather his arrows;

[010-300] And the cold, cruel winter

[010-301] will be a feast-time instead of a famine.\"
