WINONA.

\[Illustration: FALLS OF ST. ANTHONY. FACSIMILE OF THE CUT IN CARVER\'S

[006-002] TRAVELS, PUBLISHED AT LONDON, IN 1778, FROM A SURVEY AND SKETCH MADE

[006-003] BY CAPT. J. CARVER, NOV. 17, 1766. PERPENDICULAR FALL, 30 FEET; BREADTH

[006-004] NEAR 600 FEET.\]

[006-005] _When the meadow-lark trilled o\'er the leas

[006-006] and the oriole piped in the maples,

[006-007] From my hammock, all under the trees,

[006-008] by the sweet scented field of red-clover,

[006-009] I harked to the hum of the bees,

[006-010] as they gathered the mead of the blossoms,

[006-011] And caught from their low melodies

[006-012] the rhythm of the song of Winona_.

[006-013] (In pronouncing [[dakota|Dakota]] words give \"a\" the sound of \"ah,\"\--\"e\" the sound

[006-014] of \"a,\"\--\"i\" the sound of \"e\" and \"u\" the sound of \"oo.\" Sound \"ee\"

[006-015] the same as in English. The numerals 1-2 etc. refer to notes in the

[006-016] appendix).

[006-017] Two hundred white Winters and more

[006-018] have fled from the face of the Summer,

[006-019] Since here on the oak shaded shore

[006-020] of the dark winding swift Mississippi,

[006-021] Where his foaming floods tumble and roar,

[006-022] on the falls and white rolling rapids,

[006-023] In the fair, fabled center of Earth,

[006-024] sat the Indian town of Ka-thá-ga. \[86\]

[006-025] Far rolling away to the north, and the south,

[006-026] lay the emerald prairies,

[006-027] Alternate with woodlands and lakes,

[006-028] and above them the blue vast of ether.

[006-029] And here where the dark river breaks into spray

[006-030] and the roar of the Ha-Ha, \[76\]

[006-031] Were gathered the bison-skin tees

[006-032] of the chief tawny tribe of Dakotas;

[006-033] For here, in the blast and the breeze,

[006-034] flew the flag of the chief of Isantees, \[86\]

[006-035] Up-raised on the stem of a lance

[006-036] \--the feathery flag of the eagle.

[006-037] And here to the feast and the dance,

[006-038] from the prairies remote and the forests,

[006-039] Oft gathered the out-lying bands,

[006-040] and honored the gods of the nation.

[006-041] On the islands and murmuring strands

[006-042] they danced to the god of the waters,

[006-043] Unktéhee, \[69\] who dwelt in the caves

[006-044] deep under the flood of the Ha-Ha; \[76\]

[006-045] And high o\'er the eddies and waves

[006-046] hung their offerings of fur and tobacco. \[a\]

[006-047] And here to the Master of life

[006-048] \--Anpé-tu-wee, \[70\] god of the heavens,

[006-049] Chief, warrior, and maiden, and wife,

[006-050] burned the sacred green sprigs of the cedar.

[006-051] And here to the Searcher-of-hearts

[006-052] \--fierce Tá-ku Skan-skán, \[51\] the avenger,

[006-053] Who dwells in the uttermost parts

[006-054] \--in the earth and the blue, starry ether,

[006-055] Ever watching, with all-seeing eyes,

[006-056] the deeds of the wives and the warriors,

[006-057] As an osprey afar in the skies,

[006-058] sees the fish as they swim in the waters,

[006-059] Oft spread they the bison-tongue feast,

[006-060] and singing preferred their petitions,

[006-061] Till the Day-[[spirit|Spirit]] \[70\] rose in the East

[006-062] \--in the red, rosy robes of the morning,

[006-063] To sail o\'er the sea of the skies,

[006-064] to his lodge in the land of the shadows,

[006-065] Where the black winged tornadoes \[b\] arise

[006-066] \--rushing loud from the mouths of their caverns.

[006-067] And here with a shudder they heard,

[006-068] flying far from his tee in the mountains,

[006-069] Wa-kin-yan, \[32\] the huge Thunder-Bird,

[006-070] \--with the arrows of fire in his talons.

[006-071] \[a\] See Hennepin\'s Description of Louisiana by Shea pp 243 and

[006-072] 256\. Parkman\'s Discovery p. 246\--and Carver\'s Travels, p. 67

[006-073] \[b\] The Dakotas like the ancient Romans and Greeks think the home

[006-074] of the winds is in the caverns of the mountains, and their great Thunder

[006-075] bird resembles in many respects the Jupiter of the Romans and the Zeus

[006-076] of the Greeks. The resemblance of the Dakota mythology to that of the

[006-077] older Greeks and Romans is striking.

[006-078] Two hundred white Winters and more

[006-079] have fled from the face of the Summer,

[006-080] Since here by the cataract\'s roar,

[006-081] in the moon of the red blooming lilies, \[71\]

[006-082] In the tee of Ta-té-psin \[a\] was born Winona

[006-083] \--wild-rose of the prairies.

[006-084] Like the summer sun peeping, at morn,

[006-085] o\'er the hills was the face of Winona;

[006-086] And here she grew up like a queen

[006-087] \--a romping and lily-lipped laughter,

[006-088] And danced on the undulant green,

[006-089] and played in the frolicsome waters,

[006-090] Where the foaming tide tumbles and twirls

[006-091] o\'er the murmuring rocks in the rapids;

[006-092] And whiter than foam were the pearls

[006-093] that gleamed in the midst of her laughter.

[006-094] Long and dark was her flowing hair flung,

[006-095] like the robe of the night to the breezes;

[006-096] And gay as the robin she sung,

[006-097] or the gold-breasted lark of the meadows.

[006-098] Like the wings of the wind were her feet,

[006-099] and as sure as the feet of Ta-tó-ka; \[b\]

[006-100] And oft like an antelope fleet

[006-101] o\'er the hills and the prairies she bounded,

[006-102] Lightly laughing in sport as she ran,

[006-103] and looking back over her shoulder,

[006-104] At the fleet footed maiden or man,

[006-105] that vainly her flying steps followed.

[006-106] The belle of the village was she,

[006-107] and the pride of the aged Ta-té-psin,

[006-108] Like a sunbeam she lighted his tee,

[006-109] and gladdened the heart of her father.

[006-110] \[a\] Ta te\--Wind, Psin\--Wild Rice,\--wild rice wind.

[006-111] \[b\] The Mountain Antelope.

[006-112] In the golden hued Wázu-pe-weé

[006-113] \--the moon when the wild rice is gathered;

[006-114] When the leaves on the tall sugar-tree

[006-115] are as red as the breast of the robin,

[006-116] And the red-oaks that border the lea

[006-117] are aflame with the fire of the sunset,

[006-118] From the wide waving fields of wild-rice

[006-119] \--from the meadows of Psin-ta-wak-pá-dan, \[a\]

[006-120] Where the geese and the mallards rejoice,

[006-121] and grow fat on the bountiful harvest,

[006-122] Came the hunters with saddles of moose

[006-123] and the flesh of the bear and the bison,

[006-124] And the women in birchen canoes

[006-125] well laden with rice from the meadows,

[006-126] With the tall, dusky hunters, behold,

[006-127] came a marvelous man or a spirit,

[006-128] White-faced and so wrinkled and old,

[006-129] and clad in the robe of the raven.

[006-130] Unsteady his steps were and slow,

[006-131] and he walked with a staff in his right hand,

[006-132] And white as the first-falling snow

[006-133] were the thin locks that lay on his shoulders.

[006-134] Like rime-covered moss hung his beard,

[006-135] flowing down from his face to his girdle;

[006-136] And wan was his aspect and weird;

[006-137] and often he chanted and mumbled

[006-138] In a strange and mysterious tongue,

[006-139] as he bent o\'er his book in devotion.

[006-140] Or lifted his dim eyes and sung,

[006-141] in a low voice, the solemn \"_Te Deum_.\"

[006-142] Or Latin, or Hebrew, or Greek

[006-143] \--all the same were his words to the warriors,\--

[006-144] All the same to the maids

[006-145] and the meek, wide-wondering-eyed, hazel-brown children.

[006-146] \[a\] Little Rice River. It bears the name of Rice Creek to-day

[006-147] and empties into the Mississippi from the east, a few miles above

[006-148] Minneapolis.

[006-149] Father Renè Menard \[a\]\--it was he,

[006-150] long lost to his Jesuit brothers,

[006-151] Sent forth by an holy decree

[006-152] to carry the Cross to the heathen.

[006-153] In his old age abandoned to die,

[006-154] in the swamps, by his timid companions,

[006-155] He prayed to the Virgin on high,

[006-156] and she led him forth from the forest;

[006-157] For angels she sent him as men

[006-158] \--in the forms of the tawny Dakotas,

[006-159] And they led his feet from the fen,

[006-160] \--from the slough of despond and the desert.

[006-161] Half-dead in a dismal morass,

[006-162] as they followed the red-deer they found him,

[006-163] In the midst of the mire and the grass,

[006-164] and mumbling \"_Te Deum laudamus_.\"

[006-165] \"Unktómee \[72\]\--Ho!\" muttered the braves,

[006-166] for they deemed him the black Spider-Spirit

[006-167] That dwells in the drearisome caves,

[006-168] and walks on the marshes at midnight,

[006-169] With a flickering torch in his hand,

[006-170] to decoy to his den the unwary.

[006-171] His tongue could they not understand,

[006-172] but his torn hands all shriveled with famine,

[006-173] He stretched to the hunters and said:

[006-174] \"He feedeth his chosen with manna;

[006-175] And ye are the angels of God,

[006-176] sent to save me from death in the desert.\"

[006-177] His famished and woe-begone face,

[006-178] and his tones touched the hearts of the hunters;

[006-179] They fed the poor father apace,

[006-180] and they led him away to Ka-thá-ga.

[006-181] \[a\] See the account of Father Menard, his mission and disappearance in the

[006-182] wilderness, etc. [[edward-neill|Neill]]\'s Hist. Minnesota, pp 104 to 107 inc.

[006-183] There little by little he learned

[006-184] the tongue of the tawny Dakotas;

[006-185] And the heart of the good father yearned

[006-186] to lead them away from their idols\--

[006-187] Their giants \[16\] and dread Thunder-birds

[006-188] \--their worship of stones \[73\] and the devil.

[006-189] \"Wakán-de!\" \[a\] they answered his words,

[006-190] for he read from his book in the Latin,

[006-191] Lest the Nazarene\'s holy commands

[006-192] by his tongue should be marred in translation;

[006-193] And oft with his beads in his hands,

[006-194] or the cross and the crucified Jesus,

[006-195] He knelt by himself on the sands,

[006-196] and his dim eyes uplifted to heaven.

[006-197] But the braves bade him look to the East

[006-198] \--to the silvery lodge of Han-nán-na; \[b\]

[006-199] And to dance with the chiefs at the feast

[006-200] \--at the feast of the Giant Heyó-ka. \[16\]

[006-201] They frowned when the good father

[006-202] spurned the flesh of the dog in the kettle,

[006-203] And laughed when his fingers were burned

[006-204] in the hot, boiling pot of the giant.

[006-205] \"The Blackrobe\" they called the poor priest,

[006-206] from the hue of his robe and his girdle;

[006-207] And never a game or a feast

[006-208] but the father must grace with his presence.

[006-209] His prayer book the hunters revered,

[006-210] \--they deemed it a marvelous spirit;

[006-211] It spoke and the white father heard,

[006-212] \--it interpreted visions and omens.

[006-213] And often they bade him

[006-214] to pray this marvelous spirit to answer,

[006-215] And tell where the sly Chippeway might be ambushed

[006-216] and slain in his forests.

[006-217] For Menard was the first in the land,

[006-218] proclaiming, like John in the desert\--

[006-219] \"The Kingdom of Heaven is at hand;

[006-220] repent ye, and turn from your idols.\"\--

[006-221] The first of the brave brotherhood that,

[006-222] threading the fens and the forest,

[006-223] Stood afar by the turbulent flood

[006-224] at the falls of the Father of Waters.

[006-225] \[a\] It is wonderful.

[006-226] \[b\] The morning.

[006-227] In the lodge of the Stranger \[a\]

[006-228] he sat awaiting the crown of a martyr;

[006-229] His sad face compassion begat

[006-230] in the heart of the dark eyed Winona.

[006-231] Oft she came to the teepee and spoke;

[006-232] she brought him the tongue of the bison,

[006-233] Sweet nuts from the hazel and oak,

[006-234] and flesh of the fawn and the mallard.

[006-235] Soft hánpa \[b\] she made for his feet

[006-236] and leggins of velvety fawn-skin,\--

[006-237] A blanket of beaver complete,

[006-238] and a hood of the hide of the otter.

[006-239] And oft at his feet on the mat,

[006-240] deftly braiding the flags and the rushes,

[006-241] Till the sun sought his teepee she sat,

[006-242] enchanted with what he related

[006-243] Of the white winged ships on the sea

[006-244] and the teepees far over the ocean,

[006-245] Of the love and the sweet charity of the Christ

[006-246] and the beautiful Virgin.

[006-247] \[a\] A lodge set apart for guests of the village.

[006-248] \[b\] Moccasins.

[006-249] She listened like one in a trance

[006-250] when he spoke of the brave, bearded Frenchmen,

[006-251] From the green sun-lit valleys of France

[006-252] to the wild Hochelága \[a\] transplanted,

[006-253] Oft trailing the deserts of snow

[006-254] in the heart of the dense Huron forests,

[006-255] Or steering the dauntless canoe

[006-256] through the waves of the fresh water ocean.

[006-257] \"Yea, stronger and braver are they,\"

[006-258] said the aged Menard to Winona,

[006-259] \"Than the head-chief, tall Wazi-kuté, \[74\]

[006-260] but their words are as soft as a maiden\'s;

[006-261] Their eyes are the eyes of the swan,

[006-262] but their hearts are the hearts of the eagles;

[006-263] And the terrible Máza Wakán \[b\] ever walks,

[006-264] by their side like a spirit.

[006-265] Like a Thunder-bird, roaring in wrath,

[006-266] flinging fire from his terrible talons,

[006-267] It sends to their enemies death,

[006-268] in the flash of the fatal Wakándee.\" \[c\]

[006-269] \[a\] The Ottawa name for the region of the St. Lawrence River.

[006-270] \[b\] \"Mysterious metal\"\--or metal having a spirit in it. This is the

[006-271] common name applied by the Dakotas to all fire arms.

[006-272] \[c\] Lightning.

[006-273] The Autumn was past and the snow

[006-274] lay drifted and deep on the prairies;

[006-275] From his teepee of ice came the foe

[006-276] \--came the storm-breathing god of the winter.

[006-277] Then roared in the groves,\--on the plains,

[006-278] \--on the ice-covered lakes and the river\--

[006-279] The blasts of the fierce hurricanes

[006-280] blown abroad from the breast of Wazíya. \[3\]

[006-281] The bear cuddled down in his den,

[006-282] and the elk fled away to the forest;

[006-283] The pheasant and gray prairie-hen

[006-284] made their beds in the heart of the snow-drift;

[006-285] The bison-herds huddled and stood

[006-286] in the hollows and under the hill-sides;

[006-287] Or rooted the snow for their food

[006-288] in the lee of the bluffs and the timber;

[006-289] And the mad winds that howled from the north,

[006-290] from the ice-covered seas of Wazíya,

[006-291] Chased the gray wolf and red fox and swarth

[006-292] to their dens in the hills of the forest.
