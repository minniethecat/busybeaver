Father Jacques Marquette (1637–1675) was a French Jesuit missionary and explorer. In 1669 he succeeded [[Claude Allouez|Father Claude Allouez]] at La Pointe, and his letters from that post contain some of the earliest European descriptions of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people — a passage that [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]] preserved in the footnotes to "The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]."

## 在书中的角色

### 接替 Allouez 在 La Pointe

Marquette appears in Gordon's footnotes as the successor to [[claude-allouez|Claude Allouez]] at the Mission of the Holy Ghost:

> The famous Father Marquette, who took Allouëz's place, Sept. 13. 1669, (018-104)

### 对 Dakota 人的早期记述

Gordon preserves Marquette's description of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people — written to his Superior shortly after arriving at La Pointe — as an important historical witness:

> The Nadouessi are the Iroquois of this country, beyond La Pointe, *but less faithless, and never attack till attacked.* Their language is entirely different from the Huron and Algonquin. They have many villages, but are widely scattered. (018-105)

Marquette's observations, including details about [[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|customs]] such as their use of the calumet, wild rice harvesting, and distinctive protocols for greeting strangers, form one of the earliest written records of [[dakota|Dakota]] life (018-109).

## 历史/文化背景

Jacques Marquette is best known for his 1673 expedition with Louis Jolliet, during which they explored and mapped the upper [[mississippi-river|Mississippi River]] — the first Europeans to do so. Before this famous journey, however, Marquette spent several years serving at missions in the western [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s, including La Pointe, where his linguistic skills and diplomatic approach earned him respect among the tribes.

Marquette died young, at age 37, on Lake Michigan's eastern shore in 1675. His journals and letters, collected in *The Jesuit Relations*, remain a cornerstone source for the ethnohistory of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s and Upper Mississippi region.

The [[dakota|Dakotas]] called Jesuit priests "Black Robes" (013-048), a name that reflected both their distinctive attire and their role as cultural intermediaries.
