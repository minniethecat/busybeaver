Big Thunder (Wakínyan Tânka, "Great Thunderbird") appears in Gordon's "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" in a dual role: as a historical [[dakota|Dakota]] chief of the Kapóza band and as the [[legend|myth]]ological thunder-being of [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmology. He was the son of the first [[little-crow|Little Crow]] and the father of [[little-crow|Ta-ó-ya-te-dú-ta]] ([[little-crow|Little Crow]]), the last chief of that name.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]]), Kapóza band
- **Role**: Chief of the Light-foot band; [[legend|myth]]ological thunder deity
- **Family**: Son of the first [[little-crow|Little Crow]]; father of [[little-crow|Ta-ó-ya-te-dú-ta]] ([[little-crow|Little Crow]])
- **Mythological Aspect**: Wakínyan Tânka is the great thunder-bird whose teepee sits on a lofty mountain in the far West

## 在书中的角色

### Winona 传奇

In the [[winona|Winona]] narrative, Big Thunder is invoked as a figure of authority. The line "And Wakínyan Tânka, his son, is chief" (004-221) establishes the lineage of chieftainship. However, Gordon also portrays tension: "Wakínyan Tanka—undutiful son!" (004-126) hints at strained family dynamics within the chief's household.

### 神话维度

Gordon's footnotes elaborate on Big Thunder's [[legend|myth]]ological significance (016-127). Wakínyan Tânka has his teepee on a lofty mountain in the far West, where he controls the thunder and lightning. This dual identity—historical chief and cosmic being—reflects the [[dakota|Dakota]] understanding of leadership as [[soul|spirit]]ually invested.

## 历史/文化背景

The historical Big Thunder succeeded his father (the first [[little-crow|Little Crow]]) as chief of the Kapóza band. Gordon notes (015-231–015-241) that Big Thunder was killed by the accidental discharge of his own gun, after which his son [[little-crow|Ta-ó-ya-te-dú-ta]] became chief. Big Thunder was buried with his kindred near the "[[wakan-teepee-site|Wakan Teepee]]" (Carver's Cave).

The name Wakínyan Tânka represents the thunder-bird concept in [[dakota|Dakota]] religion—a powerful sky [[soul|spirit]] associated with storms, lightning, and divine authority. Gordon's use of this name for a human chief illustrates the [[dakota|Dakota]] practice of giving children names drawn from the [[soul|spirit]] world.
