The Black Robes were Jesuit missionary priests who came to the Lake Superior region in the mid-17th century. The Dakotas gave them the name "Black Robes" from the color of their priestly vestments (013-048). They established the first Christian missions among the Native peoples of what is now northern Wisconsin and Minnesota.

## Mission of the Holy Ghost

The principal Jesuit mission was the Mission of the Holy Ghost at La Pointe, on the island of Wau-ga-bá-mé (winding view) in Cha-quam-egon Bay (018-099). The mission was founded by the Jesuits about the year 1660 (018-101).

Father René Menard was the first priest stationed at this outpost (018-101). After Menard disappeared in the wilderness, Father Claude Allouëz permanently established the mission in 1665 (018-103). The celebrated Father Jacques Marquette succeeded Allouëz on September 13, 1669 (018-103).

## Relationship with the Dakotas

Gordon's poem records that the maiden Winona brought Father Menard's "robe and the missal" and related the tale of the "Black Robe" to visitors (007-128, 007-130).

She spoke of the "sacred regard" he inspired in the hearts of the Dakotas (007-131). After his death, Winona buried his bones with her kin in the mound by the Cave of the Council (007-133).

She kept his robe and prayer-book "treasured and wrapt in the skin of the red-deer" until his Jesuit brothers could come from the East (007-135, 007-141).

## In Gordon's Poem

In "The Legend of the Falls," Gordon describes the Black Robes as "the brave shepherds" who "fed their wild sheep on the isle Wau-ga-bá-mé" (010-186).

The footnote in "Winona" explains the etymology: "The Dakotas called the Jesuit priests 'Black Robes' from the color of their vestments" (013-048). The poem also mentions the "Black Robe's sacred shadow" falling across the narrative landscape (013-006).
