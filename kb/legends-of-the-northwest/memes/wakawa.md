# Wakâwa

A tall [[dakota|Dakota]] chief who presides over the central events of Gordon's "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" (Chapters I–V). Wakâwa is both a father protective of his daughter and a tribal authority figure whose decisions shape the courtship rivalry between the legend's suitors.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Chief, father, arbiter of courtship
- **Family**: Daughter: courted by rival suitors (004-037, 004-083)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Wakâwa is a central authority figure in "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" legend (Chapters I–V). He appears at key ceremonial moments — presiding over games, hearing suitors' petitions, and rendering judgments that drive the plot. His presence anchors the legend's social world, grounding the romantic narrative in the structures of [[dakota|Dakota]] leadership.

### 关键事件

Wakâwa first appears at the ceremonial game: "Wakâwa, the chief, and the loud acclaim" (001-128). [[wiwaste|Wiwásté]] turns to him as the authority: "As she turned to Wakâwa, the chief, and said" (001-135). He presides over rituals honoring [[heyoka|Heyóka]]: "To honor [[heyoka|Heyóka]], Wakâwa lifts" (001-210).

As the central authority, Wakâwa opens proceedings: "Then tall Wakâwa arose and said" (002-001). Suitors petition him directly: "Then grant me, Wakâwa, my heart's desire" (002-047), and advocates plead: "Then grant him, Wakâwa, his heart's desire" (002-052). Wakâwa performs the ceremonial pipe ritual: "Three times Wakâwa puffed forth the smoke" (002-055).

Wakâwa appears with authority: "And the tall Wakâwa appeared, and said" (002-086). He issues a stark refusal to a scheming suitor: "But I, Wakâwa — I answer — never!" (002-153). His word is binding: "Let it be as you will, for Wakâwa's tongue" (002-158).

Wakâwa welcomes [[chaske|Chaské]] warmly: "Right glad is Wakâwa his friend to hail" (003-024). But questions arise about his promises — "That Wakâwa's promise is hollow wind" (003-073) — which is rebutted: "Let it be as you will, for Wakâwa's tongue" (003-083). Wakâwa is observant and perceptive: "Wakâwa marked, and the lilies fair" (003-141).

Wakâwa makes a crucial promise to [[chaske|Chaské]]: "For Wakâwa promised the brave [[chaske|Chaskè]]" (003-173). He appears at the climactic gathering: "The chief Wakâwa himself is there" (003-220). His daughter accuses him: "'Wakâwa — my Father; he lies! — he lies!'" (004-037), and Wakâwa reflects on his daughter's welfare: "Wakâwa thought of his daughter's woes" (004-083). He acknowledges his guilt in the conflict: "'Wakâwa,' he muttered, 'the guilt is mine'" (004-112).

## 历史/文化背景

Wakâwa is a legendary figure whose name may derive from the [[dakota|Dakota]] word for "sacred" or "[[soul|spirit]]ual power" (wakân). As the chief who presides over courtship rituals, he represents the institutional authority through which personal relationships must be negotiated in traditional [[dakota|Dakota]] society. Gordon portrays Wakâwa as neither flawless nor villainous — a father whose decisions carry unintended consequences, making him a more complex authority figure than the stereotypical wise chief.
