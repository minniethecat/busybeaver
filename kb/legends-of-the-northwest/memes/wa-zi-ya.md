# Wa-zí-ya

Wa-zí-ya is the [[dakota|Dakota]] god of Winter — the "Winter-maker" who dwells in the frozen North at a place called I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta (015-215–216). He is the personification of the North Wind, blowing from his icy throne (001-082), and his star — Wazíya's Star — is the North Star (015-216 fn; 006-section, verse 62a).

## In Dakota Cosmology

Wa-zí-ya advances southward each winter, locked in struggle with the warm southern winds. The [[dakota|Dakota]] believed the granite boulders across the [[minnesota|Minnesota]] and [[dakota|Dakota]] prairies were hurled in battle by Wa-zí-ya from his home in the North (015-213–216). In spring, the god of the South drives him back to his northern domain (015-209–212).

The Ojibways know this same spirit as *Ka-be-bón-ík-ka* — the "Winter-maker" (015-217). The North Wind is also called Kewaydin or Kewaytin by the Ojibways (018-169 fn).

## The Legend of Shingebís

In the legend of [[section-15|The Legend of the Falls]], the hell-diver [[shingebis|Shingebís]] alone defies Wa-zí-ya. While all other birds flee south for winter, Shingebís stays behind, sitting and laughing at the Winter-maker (014-075–076).

When Wa-zí-ya descends from the North — "from his land of perpetual winter" — the [[dakota|Dakota]] Magic Men dance in their teepees to appease his wrath, but famine and fatal disease still follow (015-section).

## In Gordon's Footnotes

Gordon preserved Dakota cosmology in his extensive notes. On the seasonal battle between the Winter-maker and the South Wind, he recorded:

> In winter Wa-zí-ya advances southward and drives "I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta" before him to the Summer-Islands. But in Spring the god of the South, having renewed his youth and strength, in the "Happy Hunting Grounds," is able to drive Wa-zí-ya back again to his icy wigwam in the North. Some Dakotas say that the numerous granite boulders, scattered over the prairies of Minnesota and Dakota, were hurled in battle by Wa-zí-ya from his home in the North.
>
> (015-209–216)

The Wa-zí-ya of the Dakotas is substantially the same as "Ka-be-bón-ík-ka" — the "Winter-maker" of the [[ojibway|Ojibways]] (015-217).
