Francis Parkman (1823–1893) was an American historian, best known for his multi-volume work *France and England in North America*. His book *Discovery of the Great West* (also published as *La Salle and the Discovery of the Great West*) is cited by [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]] in the footnotes to "[[winona|Winona]]" as a scholarly source on [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology, specifically concerning the [[thunder-bird|Thunder-Bird]].

## 在书中的角色

### Gordon 的学术来源

Parkman appears in Gordon's annotation on the [[dakota|Dakota]] [[thunder-bird|Thunder-Bird]] legend in "[[winona|Winona]]." In a passage describing Wa-kin-yan, the great [[thunder-bird|Thunder-Bird]] of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology, Gordon cites Parkman alongside other early sources:

> See Hennepin's Description of Louisiana by Shea pp 243 and 256. Parkman's Discovery p. 246 — and Carver's Travels, p. 67 (006-072)

This footnote places Parkman within Gordon's network of scholarly references, alongside [[Jonathan Carver]] and [[louis-hennepin|Louis Hennepin]], used to authenticate and contextualize [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ological references in the poem.

### Thunder-Bird 研究与 Dakota 宗教

The citation occurs in the context of Gordon's description of the [[thunder-bird|Thunder-Bird]] (006-069), a central figure in [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmology. Parkman's *Discovery of the Great West* provided early English-language documentation of Native American belief systems, and Gordon drew on such sources to ground his poetic treatment of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|legends]] in historical scholarship.

## 历史/文化背景

Francis Parkman was one of the most significant American historians of the nineteenth century. Despite chronic health problems (including near-blindness), he produced a monumental history of the French and English colonial contest for North America. His first book, *The Oregon Trail* (1849), was based on his own travels in the West and included firsthand observations of Native American life.

Parkman's *Discovery of the Great West*, first published in 1869 as part of his larger series, documented French exploration of the Mississippi Valley — a region central to Gordon's "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest." Parkman's work reflected the racial attitudes of his era but remains a valuable record of primary sources and early American frontier history.
