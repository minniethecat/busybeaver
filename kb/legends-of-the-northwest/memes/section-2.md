PRELUDE.

THE MISSISSIPPI.

[001-001] Onward rolls the Royal River, proudly sweeping to the sea,

[001-002] Dark and deep and grand, forever wrapt in [[legend|myth]] and mystery.

[001-003] Lo he laughs along the highlands, leaping o\'er the granite walls:

[001-004] Lo he sleeps among the islands, where the loon her lover calls.

[001-005] Still like some huge monster winding downward through the prairie plains,

[001-006] Seeking rest but never finding, till the tropic gulf he gains.

[001-007] In his mighty arms he claspeth now an empire broad and grand;

[001-008] In his left hand lo he graspeth leagues of fen and forest land;

[001-009] In his right the mighty mountains, hoary with eternal snow.

[001-010] Where a thousand foaming fountains singing seek the plains below.

[001-011] Fields of corn and feet of cities lo the mighty river laves,

[001-012] Where the Saxon sings his ditties o\'er the swarthy warriors\' graves.

[001-013] Aye, before the birth, of Moses\--ere the Pyramids were piled\--

[001-014] All his banks were red with roses from the sea to nor\'lands wild,

[001-015] And from forest, fen and meadows, in the deserts of the north,

[001-016] Elk and bison stalked like shadows, and the tawny tribes came forth;

[001-017] Deeds of death and deeds of daring on his leafy banks were done\--

[001-018] Women loved and men went warring\--ere the siege of Troy begun.

[001-019] Where his wayward waters thundered, roaring o\'er the rocky walls,

[001-020] Dusky hunters sat and wondered, listening to the [[spirit|spirits]]\' calls.

[001-021] \"Ha-ha!\" \[76\] cried the warrior greeting from afar the cataract\'s roar;

[001-022] \"Ha-ha!\" rolled the answer, beating down the rock-ribbed leagues of shore.

[001-023] Now, alas, the bow and quiver and the dusky braves have fled,

[001-024] And the sullen, shackled river drives the droning mills instead.

[001-025] Where the war whoop rose, and, after, women wailed their warriors slain,

[001-026] List the Saxon\'s silvery laughter, and his humming hives of gain.

[001-027] Swiftly sped the tawny runner o\'er the pathless prairies then,

[001-028] Now the iron-reindeer sooner carries weal or woe to men.

[001-029] On thy bosom, Royal River, silent sped the birch canoe,

[001-030] Bearing brave with bow and quiver, on his way to war or woo;

[001-031] Now with flaunting flags and streamers\--mighty monsters of the deep\--

[001-032] Lo the puffing, panting steamers, through thy foaming waters sweep;

[001-033] And behold the grain-fields golden, where the bison grazed of eld;

[001-034] See the fanes of forests olden by the ruthless Saxon felled,\--

[001-035] Pluméd pines that spread their shadows ere Columbus spread his sails.

[001-036] Firs that fringed the mossy meadows ere the Mayflower braved the gales,

[001-037] Iron oaks that nourished bruin while the Vikings roamed the main,

[001-038] Crashing fall in broken ruin for the greedy marts of gain.

[001-039] Still forever and forever rolls the restless river on,

[001-040] Slumbering oft but ceasing never, while the circling centuries run.

[001-041] In his palm the lakelet lingers, in his hair the brooklets hide,

[001-042] Grasped within his thousand fingers lies a continent fair and wide,\--

[001-043] Yea, a mighty empire swarming with its millions like the bees,

[001-044] Delving, drudging, striving, storming, all their lives, for golden ease.

[001-045] Still, methinks, the dusky shadows of the days that are no more

[001-046] Stalk around the lakes and meadows, haunting oft the wonted shore,\--

[001-047] Hunters from the land of spirits seek the bison and the deer,

[001-048] Where the Saxon now inherits golden field and silver mere;

[001-049] And beside the mound where burried lies the dark-eyed maid he loves,

[001-050] Some tall warrior, wan and wearied, in the misty moonlight moves.

[001-051] See\--he stands erect and lingers\--stoic still, but loth to go\--

[001-052] Clutching in his tawny fingers feathered shaft and polished bow.

[001-053] Never wail or moan he utters and no tear is on his face,

[001-054] But a warrior\'s curse he mutters on the crafty Saxon race.

[001-055] O thou dark, mysterious River, speak and tell thy tales to me;

[001-056] Seal not up thy lips forever\--veiled in mist and mystery.

[001-057] I will sit and lowly listen at the phantom-haunted falls,

[001-058] Where thy waters foam and glisten o\'er the rugged, rocky walls.

[001-059] Till some spirit of the olden, mystic, weird, romantic days

[001-060] Shall emerge and pour her golden tales and legends through my lays.

[001-061] Then again the elk and bison on thy grassy banks shall feed,

[001-062] And along the low horizon shall the pluméd hunter speed;

[001-063] Then again on lake and river shall the silent birch canoe

[001-064] Bear the brave with bow and quiver on his way to war or woo:

[001-065] Then the beaver on the meadow shall rebuild his broken wall,

[001-066] And the wolf shall chase his shadow and his mate the panther call.

[001-067] From the prairies and the regions where the pine-plumed forest grows

[001-068] Shall arise the tawny legions with their lances and their bows;

[001-069] And again the shouts of battle shall resound along the plain,

[001-070] Bows shall twang and quivers rattle, women wail their warriors slain.

[001-076] THE GAME OF BALL. \[2\]

[001-077] Clear was the sky as a silver shield;

[001-078] The bright sun blazed on the frozen field.

[001-079] On icebound river and white robed prairie

[001-080] The diamonds gleamed in the flame of noon;

[001-081] But cold and keen were the breezes airy

[001-082] Wa-zi-ya \[3\] blew from his icy throne.

[001-083] On the solid ice of the silent river

[001-084] The bounds are marked, and a splendid prize,

[001-085] A robe of black fox lined with beaver\--

[001-086] Is hung in view of the eager eyes;

[001-087] And fifty merry Dakota maidens,

[001-088] The fairest moulded of woman kind,

[001-089] Are gathered in groups on the level ice.

[001-090] They look on the robe and its beauty gladdens,

[001-091] And maddens their hearts for the splendid prize.

[001-092] Lo the rounded ankles and raven hair

[001-093] That floats at will on the wanton wind,

[001-094] And the round brown arms to the breezes bare,

[001-095] And breasts like the mounds where the waters meet, \[4\]

[001-096] And feet as fleet as the red deer\'s feet,

[001-097] And faces that glow like the full, round moon

[001-098] When she laughs in the luminous skies of June.

[001-099] The leaders are chosen and swiftly divide

[001-100] The opposing parties on either side.

[001-101] Wiwâstè \[5\] is chief of a nimble band.

[001-102] The star-eyed daughter of Little Crow; \[6\]

[001-103] And the leader chosen to hold command

[001-104] Of the band adverse is a haughty foe\--

[001-105] The dusky, impetuous Hârpstinà, \[7\]

[001-106] The queenly cousin of Wapasa. \[8\]

[001-107] Kapóza\'s chief and his tawny hunters

[001-108] Are gathered to witness the queenly game.

[001-109] The ball is thrown and a bat encounters,

[001-110] And away it flies with a loud acclaim.

[001-111] Swift are the maidens that follow after,

[001-112] And swiftly it flies for the farther bound:

[001-113] And long and loud are the peals of laughter,

[001-114] As some fair runner is flung to ground;

[001-115] While backward and forward, and to and fro,

[001-116] The maidens contend on the trampled snow.

[001-117] With loud \"Ihó!\--Itó!\--Ihó!\" \[9\]

[001-118] And waving the beautiful prize anon,

[001-119] The dusky warriors cheer them on.

[001-120] And often the limits are almost passed,

[001-121] As the swift ball flies and returns. At last

[001-122] It leaps the line at a single bound

[001-123] From the fair Wiwâstè\'s sturdy stroke,

[001-124] Like a fawn that flies from the baying hound.

[001-125] Wild were the shouts, and they rolled and broke

[001-126] On the beetling bluffs and the hills profound,

[001-127] An echoing, jubilant sea of sound.

[001-128] Wakâwa, the chief, and the loud acclaim

[001-129] Announced the end of the well-fought game,

[001-130] And the fair Wiwâstè was victor crowned.

[001-131] Dark was the visage of Hârpstinà

[001-132] When the robe was laid at her rival\'s feet,

[001-133] And merry maidens and warriors saw

[001-134] Her flashing eyes and her look of hate,

[001-135] As she turned to Wakâwa, the chief, and said:\--

[001-136] \"The game was mine were it fairly played.

[001-137] I was stunned by a blow on my bended head,

[001-138] As I snatched the ball from slippery ground

[001-139] Not half a fling from Wiwâstè\'s bound.

[001-140] And the cheat\--behold her! for there she stands

[001-141] With the prize that is mine in her treacherous hands.

[001-142] The fawn may fly, but the wolf is fleet;

[001-143] The fox creeps sly on Magâ\'s \[10\] retreat;

[001-144] And a woman\'s revenge\--it is swift and sweet.\"

[001-145] She turned to her lodge, but a roar of laughter

[001-146] And merry mockery followed after.

[001-147] Little they heeded the words she said,

[001-148] Little they cared for her haughty tread,

[001-149] For maidens and warriors and chieftain knew

[001-150] That her lips were false and her charge untrue.

[001-151] Wiwâstè, the fairest Dakota maiden,

[001-152] The sweet-faced daughter of Little Crow,

[001-153] To her teepee \[11\] turned with her trophy laden\--

[001-154] The black robe trailing the virgin snow.

[001-155] Beloved was she by her princely father,

[001-156] Beloved was she by the young and old,

[001-157] By merry maidens and many a mother,

[001-158] And many a warrior bronzed and bold.

[001-159] For her face was as fair as a beautiful dream,

[001-160] And her voice like the song of the mountain stream;

[001-161] And her eyes like the stars when they glow and gleam.

[001-162] Through the somber pines of the nor\'land wold,

[001-163] When the winds of winter are keen and cold.

[001-164] Mah-pí-ya Dú-ta \[12\] the tall Red Cloud,

[001-165] A hunter swift and a warrior proud,

[001-166] With many a scar and many a feather,

[001-167] Was a suitor bold and a lover fond.

[001-168] Long had he courted Wiwâstè\'s father,

[001-169] Long had he sued for the maiden\'s hand.

[001-170] Aye, brave and proud was the tall Red Cloud,

[001-171] A peerless son of a giant race,

[001-172] And the eyes of the panther were set in his face.

[001-173] He strode like a stag, and he stood like a pine:

[001-174] Ten feathers he wore of the great Wanmdeè; \[13\]

[001-175] With crimsoned quills of the porcupine

[001-176] His leggins were worked to his brawny knee.

[001-177] The bow he bent was a giant\'s bow;

[001-178] The swift red elk could he overtake,

[001-179] And the necklace that girdled his brawny neck

[001-180] Was the polished claws of the great Mató \[14\]

[001-181] He grappled and slew in the northern snow.

[001-182] Wiwâstè looked on the warrior tall;

[001-183] She saw he was brawny and brave and great,

[001-184] But the eyes of the panther she could but hate,

[001-185] And a brave Hóhé \[15\] loved she better than all.

[001-186] Loved was Mahpíya by Hârpstinà,

[001-187] But the warrior she never could charm or draw;

[001-188] And bitter indeed was her secret hate

[001-189] For the maiden she reckoned so fortunate.

[001-190] HEYÓKA WACÍPEE \[16\]\--THE GIANT\'S DANCE.

[001-191] The night-sun \[17\] sails in his gold canoe,

[001-192] The spirits \[18\] walk in the realms of air

[001-193] With their glowing faces and flaming hair,

[001-194] And the shrill, chill winds o\'er the prairies blow.

[001-195] In the Tee \[19\] of the Council the Virgins light

[001-196] The Virgin-fire \[20\] for the feast to-night;

[001-197] For the Sons of Heyóka will celebrate

[001-198] The sacred dance to the giant great.

[001-199] The kettle boils on the blazing fire,

[001-200] And the flesh is done to the chief\'s desire.

[001-201] With his stoic face to sacred East, \[21\]

[001-202] He takes his seat at the Giant\'s Feast.

[001-203] For the feast of Heyóka \[22\] the braves are dressed

[001-204] With crowns from the bark of the white-birch trees,

[001-205] And new skin leggins that reach the knees;

[001-206] With robes of the bison and swarthy bear,

[001-207] And eagle-plumes in their coal-black hair,

[001-208] And marvelous rings in their tawny ears,

[001-209] Which were pierced with the points of their shining spears.

[001-210] To honor Heyóka, Wakâwa lifts

[001-211] His fuming pipe from the Red-stone Quarry. \[23\]

[001-212] The warriors follow. The white cloud drifts

[001-213] From the Council-lodge to the welkin starry,

[001-214] Like a fog at morn on the fir-clad hill,

[001-215] When the meadows are damp and the winds are still.

[001-216] They dance to the tune of their wild \"Ha-ha!\"

[001-217] A warrior\'s shout and a raven\'s caw\--

[001-218] Circling the pot and the blaming fire

[001-219] To the tom-tom\'s bray and the rude bassoon;

[001-220] Round and round to their heart\'s desire,

[001-221] And ever the same wild [[song|chant]] and tune\--

[001-222] A warrior\'s shout and a raven\'s caw\--

[001-223] \"Ha-ha,\--ha-ha,\--ha-ha,\--ha!\"

[001-224] They crouch, they leap, and their burning eyes

[001-225] Flash fierce in the light of the flaming fire,

[001-226] As fiercer and fiercer and higher and higher

[001-227] The rude, wild notes of their chant arise.

[001-228] They cease, they sit, and the curling smoke

[001-229] Ascends again from their polished pipes,

[001-230] And upward curls from their swarthy lips

[001-231] To the God whose favor their hearts invoke.
