# Dakota

The Dakota are a confederation of Native American bands sharing a common origin and language, who historically inhabited the upper Mississippi region and are central to the [[legend|legends]] collected in H. L. Gordon's *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest*.

## Definition

The term "Dakota" signifies *alliance* or *confederation*. Many separate bands, all having a common origin and speaking a common tongue, were united under this name (P01-009).

- **Dakota Term**: Dakota (Da-ko'-ta) — "alliance" or "confederation" (P01-010)
- **English**: Allied / Confederated peoples
- **Domain**: ethnography / language / social-structure

## In the Book's Context

### Role in *Legends of the Northwest*

Gordon devoted several years of his leisure hours to the study of the language, history, [[tradition|traditions]], [[tradition|customs]], and [[superstition|superstitions]] of the Dakotas (P01-007). The [[legend|legends]] he collected — *The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]*, *[[winona|Winona]]*, and *The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]* — are explicitly subtitled "A [[legend|Legend]] of the Dakotas" (P01-004).

The author acknowledges his debt to the Reverend S. R. Riggs, author of the *Grammar and Dictionary of the Dakota Language* and *Tah-Koo [[wakan|Wah-Kan]]* (P01-063), who spent years as a missionary among the Dakotas (P01-064).

### Pronunciation

In pronouncing Dakota words, "a" has the sound of "ah," "e" the sound of "a" as in "fate," and "i" the sound of "e" as in "me" (001-072).

## Historical Note

Gordon notes that "the day of the Dakotas is done" and that "the Dakotas of old are no more" (P01-046, P01-049). The brilliant heroes, sages, and orators that once led them have passed away, and the degenerate remnant now sleeps by the [[saskatchewan|Saskatchewan]] (P01-049). This elegiac tone pervades the entire collection.
