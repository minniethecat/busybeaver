## 名称与位置

Ke-úk-sa (also spelled Ke-ó-sa) was the principal village of the hereditary line of **Wapasa** chiefs — one of the most illustrious chiefly lineages among the Dakotas. The village occupied the site where the city of **Winona, Minnesota** now stands, on the west bank of the Mississippi River. (016-015)

## 名称含义：违法者之村

The name Ke-úk-sa carries an unusual story. It signifies "**the village of law-breakers**" — so called because this particular band broke a fundamental Dakota custom that **forbade marriage between blood relatives of any degree**. This violation of tribal law set them apart from other Dakota bands and gave their settlement its distinctive and somewhat notorious name. (016-016)

The information on the name's origin comes from the Reverend **Stephen R. Riggs**, author of the Dakota Grammar and Dictionary and the missionary tract "Takoo Wakan," who spent four decades studying Dakota language and customs. (016-016)

## 与瓦帕萨酋长家族的关系

Wapasa (often corrupted to "Wabashaw" in English sources) was the hereditary name of a long and illustrious line of Dakota chiefs. The name derives from "Wâ-pa-hâ-sa," referring to the standard or pole used in Dakota ceremonial dances, upon which colored feathers are tied — not from "Wâ-pa" (leaf), as some writers have erroneously assumed. Ke-úk-sa served as the seat of this chiefly dynasty. (016-015)

The grandfather of the last chief Wapasa was a contemporary of **Cetan-Wa-kâ-wa-mâni** (the grandfather of Little Crow) and was noted as a friend of the British during the American War of the Revolution. (016-016)

## 今日的遗址

The site of Ke-úk-sa is now occupied by the modern city of **Winona, Minnesota**, a prosperous river town on the Mississippi. No visible traces of the Dakota village remain, but the location preserves a connection to one of the most significant chiefly centers of the pre-settlement Dakota world. (016-015)
