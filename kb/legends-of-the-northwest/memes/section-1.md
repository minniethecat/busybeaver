PREFACE.

[P01-007] I have for several years devoted many of my leisure hours to the study

[P01-008] of the language, history, [[tradition|traditions]], customs and [[superstition|superstitions]] of the

[P01-009] [[dakota|Dakotas]]. These Indians are now commonly called the \"Sioux\"\--a name given

[P01-010] them by the early [[voyageurs|French traders]] and _voyageurs_. \"Dakota\" signifies

[P01-011] _alliance_ or _confederation_. Many separate bands, all having a common

[P01-012] origin and speaking a common tongue, were united under this name. See

[P01-013] \"_Tah-Koo [[wakan|Wah-Kan]]_,\" or \"_The Gospel Among the Dakotas_,\" by Stephen R.

[P01-014] Riggs, pp. 1 to 6 inc.

[P01-015] They were, but yesterday, the occupants and owners of the fair forests

[P01-016] and fertile prairies of Minnesota\--a brave, hospitable and generous

[P01-017] people,\--barbarians, indeed, but noble in their barbarism. They may be

[P01-018] fitly called the Iroquois of the West. In form and features, in language

[P01-019] and traditions, they are distinct from all other Indian tribes. When

[P01-020] first visited by white men, and for many years afterwards, the Falls of

[P01-021] St. Anthony (by them called the Ha-Ha) was the center of their country.

[P01-022] They cultivated tobacco, and hunted the elk, the beaver and the bison.

[P01-023] They were open-hearted, truthful and brave. In their wars with other

[P01-024] tribes they seldom slew women or children, and rarely sacrificed the

[P01-025] lives of their prisoners.

[P01-026] For many years their chiefs and head men successfully resisted the

[P01-027] attempts to introduce spirituous liquors among them. More than a century

[P01-028] ago an English trader was killed at Mendota, because he persisted,

[P01-029] after repeated warnings by the chiefs, in dealing out _mini-wakan_

[P01-030] (Devil-water) to the Dakota braves.

[P01-031] With open arms and generous hospitality they welcomed the first white

[P01-032] men to their land; and were ever faithful in their friendship,

[P01-033] till years of wrong and robbery, and want and insult, drove them to

[P01-034] desperation and to war. They were barbarians, and their warfare was

[P01-035] barbarous, but not more barbarous than the warfare of our Saxon and

[P01-036] Celtic ancestors. They were ignorant and superstitious, but their

[P01-037] condition closely resembled the condition of our British forefathers

[P01-038] at the beginning of the Christian era. Macaulay says of Britain, \"Her

[P01-039] inhabitants, when first they became known to the Tyrian mariners, were

[P01-040] little superior to the natives of the Sandwich Islands.\" And again,

[P01-041] \"While the German princes who reigned at Paris, Toledo, Arles and

[P01-042] Ravenna listened with reverence to the instructions of Bishops, adored

[P01-043] the relics of martyrs, and took part eagerly in disputes touching the

[P01-044] Nicene theology, the rulers of Wessex and Mercia were still performing

[P01-045] savage rites in the temples of Thor and Woden.\"

[P01-046] The day of the Dakotas is done. The degenerate remnants of that once

[P01-047] powerful and warlike people still linger around the forts and agencies

[P01-048] of the Northwest, or chase the caribou and the bison on the banks of the

[P01-049] Sascatchewan, but the Dakotas of old are no more. The brilliant defeat

[P01-050] of Custer, by Sitting Bull and his braves, was their last grand rally

[P01-051] against the resistless march of the sons of the Saxons and the Celts.

[P01-052] The plow-shares of a superior race are fast leveling the sacred mounds

[P01-053] of their dead. But yesterday, the shores of our lakes, and our rivers,

[P01-054] were dotted with their tepees. Their light canoes glided over our

[P01-055] waters, and their hunters chased the deer and the buffalo on the sites

[P01-056] of our cities. To-day, they are not. Let us do justice to their memory,

[P01-057] for there was much that was noble in their natures. In the following

[P01-058] Dakota Legends I have endeavored to faithfully represent many of the

[P01-059] customs and superstitions, and some of the traditions, of that people.

[P01-060] I have taken very little \"poetic license\" with their traditions; none,

[P01-061] whatever, with their customs and superstitions. In my studies for these

[P01-062] Legends I have been greatly aided by Rev. S. R. Riggs, author of the

[P01-063] Grammar and Dictionary of the Dakota language, \"Tah-Koo Wah-Kan,\" &c.,

[P01-064] and for many years a missionary among the Dakotas. He has patiently

[P01-065] answered my numerous inquiries and given me valuable information. I am

[P01-066] also indebted to Gen. H. H. Sibley, one of the earliest American traders

[P01-067] among them, and to Rev. S. W. Pond, of Shakopee, one of the first

[P01-068] Protestant missionaries to these people, and himself the author of

[P01-069] poetical versions of some of their principal [[legend|legends]]; to Mrs. Eastman\'s

[P01-070] \"Dacotah.\" and last, but not least, to the Rev. E. D. [[edward-neill|Neill]], whose

[P01-071] admirable \"History of Minnesota\" so fully and faithfully presents almost

[P01-072] all that is known of the history, traditions, customs, manners and

[P01-073] superstitions of the Dakotas. In _Winona_ I have \"tried my hand\" on

[P01-074] Hexameter verse. With what success, I leave to those who are better able

[P01-075] to judge than I. If I have failed, I have but added another failure to

[P01-076] the numerous vain attempts to naturalize Hexameter verse in the English

[P01-077] language.

[P01-078] The Earl of Derby, in the preface to his translation of the Iliad, calls

[P01-079] it \"That pestilent heresy of the so-called English Hexameter; a metre

[P01-080] wholly repugnant to the genius of our language; which can only be

[P01-081] pressed into the service by a violation of every rule of prosody.\" Lord

[P01-082] Kames, in his \"Elements of Criticism.\" says, \"Many attempts have been

[P01-083] made to introduce Hexameter verse into the living languages, but

[P01-084] without success. The English language, I am inclined to think, is

[P01-085] not susceptible of this melody, and my reasons are these: First, the

[P01-086] polysyllables in Latin and Greek are finely diversified by long and

[P01-087] short syllables, a circumstance that qualifies them for the melody of

[P01-088] Hexameter verse: ours are extremely ill qualified for that service,

[P01-089] because they super-abound in short syllables. Secondly, the bulk of our

[P01-090] monosyllables are arbitrary with regard to length, which is an unlucky

[P01-091] circumstance in Hexameter. * * * In Latin and Greek Hexameter invariable

[P01-092] sounds direct and ascertain the melody. English Hexameter would

[P01-093] be destitute of melody, unless by artful pronunciation; because of

[P01-094] necessity the bulk of its sounds must be arbitrary. The pronunciation

[P01-095] is easy in a simple movement of alternate long and short syllables;

[P01-096] but would be perplexing and unpleasant in the diversified movement of

[P01-097] Hexameter verse.\"

[P01-098] Beautiful as is the _Evangeline_ of Longfellow, his Hexameter lines

[P01-099] are sometimes hard to scan, and often grate harshly on the ear. He is

[P01-100] frequently forced to divide a word by the central or pivotal pause of

[P01-101] the line, and sometimes to make a pause in the sense where the rhythm

[P01-102] forbids it. Take for example some of the opening lines of _Evangeline_:

[P01-103] \"This is the forest prime\|val. The murmuring pines and the hemlocks,

[P01-104] Bearded with moss, and in gar\|ments green, indistinct in the twilight.

[P01-105] Loud from its rocky cav\|erns, the deep-voiced neighboring ocean

[P01-106] Speaks, and in accents discon\|solate answers the wail of the forest.

[P01-107] Lay in the fruitful val\|ley. Vast meadows stretched to the eastward.\"

[P01-108] Again, in order to comply with the Greek and Latin rule of beginning

[P01-109] each line with a _long_ syllable, he is compelled to emphasize words

[P01-110] contrary to the sense. Examples:

[P01-111] _In_ the Acadian land, on the shores of the Basin of Minas

[P01-112] _Some_what apart from the village, and nearer the Basin of Minas.

[P01-113] _But_ a celestial bright\|ness\--a more etherial beauty.

[P01-114] _And_ the retreating sun the sign of the scorpion enters.

[P01-115] _In_-doors, warmed by the wide-\|mouthed fireplace idly the farmer,

[P01-116] _Four_ times the sun had ris\|en and set; and now on the fifth day,

[P01-117] \"Greek and Latin Hexameter lines, as to time, are all of the same

[P01-118] length, being equivalent to the time taken in pronouncing twelve long

[P01-119] syllables, or twenty-four short ones. An Hexameter line may consist of

[P01-120] seventeen syllables, and when regular and not Spondiac, it never has

[P01-121] fewer than thirteen: whence it follows that where the syllables are

[P01-122] many, the plurality must be short; where few, the plurality must be

[P01-123] long. This line is susceptible of much variety as to the succession of

[P01-124] long and short syllables. It is however subject to laws that confine its

[P01-125] variety within certain limits. * * *

[P01-126] 1st. The line must always commence with a _long_ syllable, and close

[P01-127] with two long preceded by two short.

[P01-128] 2d. More than two short syllables can never be found together, nor

[P01-129] _fewer_ than two.

[P01-130] 3d. Two long syllables which have been preceded by two short can not

[P01-131] also be followed by two short.

[P01-132] These few rules fulfill all the conditions of an Hexameter line

[P01-133] with relation to order of arrangement.\"\--_Lord Kames, \"Elements of

[P01-134] Criticism.\"_ One who attempts to write English Hexameter, under the

[P01-135] Greek and Latin rules, will speedily be made aware that the English

[P01-136] language \"super-abounds in short syllables.\" Why then should we rigidly

[P01-137] adhere to rules repugnant to the genius of our language, if they can be

[P01-138] modified so as to adapt the sonorous Hexameter to the structure of our

[P01-139] mother-tongue? Can they be so modified? I have attempted it. I venture

[P01-140] to change them as follows:

[P01-141] 1st. By beginning each line with a _short_ syllable instead of a long

[P01-142] one. And it will be seen that I often begin a line with two short

[P01-143] syllables.

[P01-144] 2d. By often using one short syllable unaccompanied by another.

[P01-145] 3d. I have increased the average number of syllables in the line to

[P01-146] better adapt it to our super-abundance of short syllables.

[P01-147] 4th. In _Winona_ I have introduced a rhyme at the pivotal pause of

[P01-148] the line, not because my Hexameter requires it, but because I think it

[P01-149] increases the melody, and more emphatically marks the central pause.

[P01-150] I am not quite sure that, in a long poem, the rhyme is not detrimental.

[P01-151] That depends greatly, however, upon the skill with which it is handled.

[P01-152] Surely the same Hexameter can be written as smoothly and more vigorously

[P01-153] without rhyme. Rhyme adds greatly to the labor of composition; it rarely

[P01-154] assists, but often hinders, the expression of the sense which the author

[P01-155] would convey. At times I have been on the point of abandoning it

[P01-156] in despair, but after having been under the hammer and the file, at

[P01-157] intervals for the last four years, _Winona_ is at last _done_, if not

[P01-158] finished.

[P01-159] It will be observed that I have slightly changed the length and the

[P01-160] rhythm of the old Hexameter line, but it is still Hexameter, and, I

[P01-161] think, improved. I am not afraid of intelligent criticism. I invoke it,

[P01-162] and will endeavor to profit by it in the future as in the past.

[P01-163] The reception of my _Pauline_ at home and abroad has been so flattering

[P01-164] that I have been encouraged to attempt something better. That was my

[P01-165] first real effort and full of crudities but if the Legends are received

[P01-166] by our best critics as well as _Pauline_ was received I shall be well

[P01-167] pleased with my efforts.

[P01-168] After much thought I have decided to publish the first edition of my

[P01-169] _Legends_ here at home:

[P01-170] 1st Because they pertain particularly to the lakes and rivers to the

[P01-171] fair forests and fertile fields of our own Minnesota and ought to be

[P01-172] appreciated here if anywhere.

[P01-173] 2d Because many of our people are competent to judge whether my

[P01-174] representations of Dakota customs, life, traditions, and superstitions

[P01-175] are correct or not and at the same time the reading public of the North

[P01-176] west is as intelligent and discriminating as that of any other portion

[P01-177] of our country. If these _Legends_ be appreciated and approved by our

[P01-178] own people who are familiar with the scenery described and more or less,

[P01-179] with the customs, traditions and superstitions of the Dakotas, and if

[P01-180] beyond that these poems shall stand the test of candid criticism I may

[P01-181] give them a wider publication.

H. L. GORDON.

[P01-182] MINNEAPOLIS. June 1, 1881.
