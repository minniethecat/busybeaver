George Catlin (1796–1872) was an American painter, author, and traveler who dedicated much of his career to documenting Native American life through portraits, landscapes, and written accounts of tribal [[tradition|customs]]. Gordon cites Catlin's work in his footnotes to "The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]" as an ethnographic source on the [[dakota|Dakota]] Sun-[[dance|Dance]] ceremony.

## 在书中的角色

### Gordon 关于 Sun-Dance 的学术引用

Catlin appears in Gordon's annotation on the severe nature of [[dakota|Dakota]] worship practices. In describing the [[dakota|Dakota]] belief that the sun ([[anpetu-wee|Anpétuwee]]) is the father of life — beneficent and kind, yet worshipped through the Sun-[[dance|Dance]] in an extreme manner — Gordon directs readers to Catlin for further detail:

> See Riggs' "Tah-koo Wah-kân," pp. 81-2, and Catlin's (017-092)

This citation follows Gordon's reference to [[Stephen Return Riggs|Stephen Riggs]]' account of the O-kee-pa ceremony, indicating that Catlin's documentation of the Mandan O-kee-pa ritual (a related Plains ceremony) was considered relevant context for understanding [[dakota|Dakota]] religious practice.

### 作为视觉记录者

Catlin's significance to Gordon's scholarly apparatus lies in his role as a visual and descriptive recorder of ceremonies that were already threatened by cultural disruption in the mid-nineteenth century. His paintings and writings captured details of Native American rituals, including the piercing and endurance elements of the Sun-[[dance|Dance]] and O-kee-pa, that might otherwise have been lost.

## 历史/文化背景

George Catlin made five trips to the American West between 1830 and 1836, visiting over 50 tribes and producing more than 500 paintings. His *Indian Gallery*, which toured major American and European cities, introduced many audiences to Native American cultures for the first time. His principal published works include *Letters and Notes on the Manners, Customs, and Condition of the North American Indians* (1841) and *O-kee-pa: A Religious Ceremony of the Mandans* (1867).

While Catlin's work was shaped by the romantic and sensational conventions of his era, his paintings and descriptions preserve irreplaceable ethnographic data, particularly for the Mandan people, whose population was devastated by smallpox shortly after his visits. Gordon's citation of Catlin alongside Riggs places "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" within a nineteenth-century tradition of attempting to document Native American cultures before their perceived disappearance.
