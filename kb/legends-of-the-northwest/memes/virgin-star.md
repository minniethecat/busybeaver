The Virgin Star is the [[dakota|Dakota]] name for the Evening Star (the planet Venus), believed to be the [[soul|spirit]] of a virgin maiden wronged at the great ceremonial feast (017-024, 017-025). In Gordon's [[legend|legends]], it is identified with Wiwástè, whose beautiful face is said to shine from the heavens.

## Definition / Identity

The [[dakota|Dakotas]] call the Evening Star the "Virgin Star" (017-024) and hold that it embodies the [[soul|spirit]] of a virgin who was wronged at the feast (017-025). This celestial identification transforms a personal tragedy into a permanent cosmic presence — the wronged maiden becomes a visible, nightly reminder in the western sky.

- **Origin**: [[dakota|Dakota]] astronomical-[[soul|spirit]]ual tradition; linked to the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]] narrative
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]] / English
- **Domain**: [[legend|myth]]ology / [[soul|spirit]]uality / celestial

The Virgin Star fuses two realms of [[dakota|Dakota]] belief: astronomical observation (the planet Venus as Evening Star) and [[soul|spirit]]ual narrative (the transmigrated [[soul|spirit]] of a mortal maiden). This is characteristic of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology, where natural phenomena are given personal, moral histories (017-107).

## In the Book

The Virgin Star appears in "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" as both a celestial object and a character in the narrative. After Wiwástè is wronged, a grieving figure looks to the Evening Star and names it the "Virgin Star" (004-111), declaring it to be Wiwástè's face looking down from her celestial lodge upon the fading [[dakota|Dakota]] race (004-106, 004-107).

### Role in the Legends

The Virgin Star serves as the supernatural resolution to Wiwástè's tragedy. Rather than simply dying, she is transfigured into the Evening Star, giving her a permanent, sacred presence.

Gordon uses the star as a recurring image in the closing sections of "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]": beneath its glow the remaining ceremonies unfold (004-177), and her "beautiful face" is identified with the star itself (004-255). The footnote (017-024, 017-025) confirms this is authentic [[dakota|Dakota]] belief, not Gordon's poetic invention.

### Representative Passages

> "And he named the planet the _Virgin Star_." (004-111)

> "Beneath the glow of the Virgin Star" (004-177)

> "That her beautiful face is the Virgin Star" (004-255)
