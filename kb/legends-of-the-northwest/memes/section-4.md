[003-001] All snug in the teepee Wiwâstè lay,

[003-002] All wrapped in her robe, at the dawn of day,\--

[003-003] All snug and warm from the wind and snow,

[003-004] While the hunters followed the buffalo.

[003-005] Her dreams and her slumber their wild shouts broke;

[003-006] The chase was afoot when the maid awoke;

[003-007] She heard the twangs of the hunter\'s bows,

[003-008] And the bellowing bulls and the loud Ihós,

[003-009] And she murmured\--\"My hunter is far away

[003-010] In the happy land of the tall Hóhé\--

[003-011] My beautiful hunter, my brave Chaskè;

[003-012] But the robins will come and my warrior too,

[003-013] And Wiwâstè will find her a way to woo.\"

[003-014] And long she lay in a reverie,

[003-015] And dreamed, wide-awake, of her brave Chaskè,

[003-016] Till a trampling of feet on the crispy snow

[003-017] She heard, and the murmur of voices low;\--

[003-018] Then the hunters\' greeting\--Ihó! Ihó!

[003-019] And behold, in the blaze of the risen day,

[003-020] With the hunters that followed the buffalo,\--

[003-021] Came her beautiful hunter\--her brave Chaskè.

[003-022] Far south has he followed the bison-trail

[003-023] With his band of warriors so brave and true.

[003-024] Right glad is Wakâwa his friend to hail,

[003-025] And Wiwâstè will find her a way to woo.

[003-026] Tall and straight as the larch tree stood

[003-027] The manly form of the brave young chief,

[003-028] And fair as the larch in its vernal leaf,

[003-029] When the red fawn bleats in the feathering wood.

[003-030] Mild was his face as the morning skies,

[003-031] And friendship shone in his laughing eyes;

[003-032] But swift were his feet o\'er the drifted snow

[003-033] On the trail of the elk or the buffalo;

[003-034] And his heart was stouter than lance or bow,

[003-035] When he heard the whoop of his enemies.

[003-036] Five feathers he wore of the great Wanmdeè,

[003-037] And each for the scalp of a warrior slain,

[003-038] When down on his camp from the northern plain,

[003-039] With their murder cries rode the bloody Cree. \[35\]

[003-040] But never the stain of an infant slain,

[003-041] Or the blood of a mother that plead in vain,

[003-042] Soiled the honored plumes of the brave Hóhé.

[003-043] A mountain bear to his enemies,

[003-044] To his friends like the red fawn\'s dappled form;

[003-045] In peace, like the breeze from the summer seas;

[003-046] In war, like the roar of the mountain storm.

[003-047] His fame in the voice of the winds went forth

[003-048] From his hunting grounds in the happy north,

[003-049] And far as the shores of the Great Medè \[36\]

[003-050] The nations spoke of the brave Chaskè.

[003-051] Dark was the visage of grim Red Cloud,

[003-052] Fierce were the eyes of the warrior proud,

[003-053] When the chief to his lodge led the brave Chaskè,

[003-054] And Wiwâstè smiled on the tall Hóhé.

[003-055] Away he strode with a sullen frown,

[003-056] And alone in his teepee he sat him down.

[003-057] From the gladsome greeting of braves he stole,

[003-058] And wrapped himself in his gloomy soul.

[003-059] But the eagle eyes of the Hârpstinà

[003-060] The clouded face of the warrior saw.

[003-061] Softly she spoke to the sullen brave:

[003-062] \"Mah-pí-ya Dúta,\--his face is sad.

[003-063] And why is the warrior so glum and grave?

[003-064] For the fair Wiwâstè is gay and glad.

[003-065] She will sit in the teepee the live-long day,

[003-066] And laugh with her lover\--the brave Hóhé.

[003-067] Does the tall Red Cloud for the false one sigh?

[003-068] There are fairer maidens than she, and proud

[003-069] Were their hearts to be loved by the brave Red Cloud.

[003-070] And trust not the chief with the smiling eyes;

[003-071] His tongue is swift, but his words are lies;

[003-072] And the proud Mah-pí-ya will surely find

[003-073] That Wakâwa\'s promise is hollow wind.

[003-074] Last night I stood by his lodge, and lo

[003-075] I heard the voice of the Little Crow;

[003-076] But the fox is sly and his words were low.

[003-077] But I heard her answer her father\--\"Never!

[003-078] I will stain your knife in my heart\'s red blood,

[003-079] I will plunge and sink in the sullen river,

[003-080] Ere I will be wife to the fierce Red Cloud!\"

[003-081] Then he spake again, and his voice was low,

[003-082] But I heard the answer of Little Crow:

[003-083] \"Let it be as you will, for Wakâwa\'s tongue

[003-084] Has spoken no promise,\--his lips are slow,

[003-085] And the love of a father is deep and strong.\"

[003-086] Mâh-pí-ya Dúta, they scorn your love,

[003-087] But the false chief covets the warrior\'s gifts.

[003-088] False to his promise the fox will prove,

[003-089] And fickle as snow in Wo-kâ-da-weè, \[37\]

[003-090] That slips into brooks when the gray cloud lifts,

[003-091] Or the red sun looks through the ragged rifts.

[003-092] Mah-pí-ya Dúta will listen to me

[003-093] There are fairer birds in the bush than she,

[003-094] And the fairest would gladly be Red Cloud\'s wife.

[003-095] Will the warrior sit like a girl bereft,

[003-096] When fairer and truer than she are left

[003-097] That love Red Cloud as they love their life?

[003-098] Mah-pí-ya Dúta will listen to me

[003-099] I love him well,\--I have loved him long:

[003-100] A woman is weak, but a warrior is strong,

[003-101] And a lovelorn brave is a scorn to see.

[003-102] Mah-pí-ya Dúta, O listen to me!

[003-103] Revenge is swift and revenge is strong,

[003-104] And sweet as the hive in the hollow tree.

[003-105] The proud Red Cloud will revenge his wrong

[003-106] Let the brave be patient, it is not long

[003-107] Till the leaves be green on the maple tree,

[003-108] And the Feast of the Virgins is then to be;\--

[003-109] The Feast of the Virgins is then to be!\"

[003-110] Proudly she turned from the silent brave,

[003-111] And went her way; but the warrior\'s eyes\--

[003-112] They flashed with the flame of a sudden fire,

[003-113] Like the lights that gleam in the Sacred Cave, \[38\]

[003-114] When the black night covers the autumn skies,

[003-115] And the stars from their welkin watch retire.

[003-116] Three nights he tarried\--the brave Chaskè;

[003-117] Winged were the hours and they flitted away;

[003-118] On the wings of Wakândee \[39\] they silently flew,

[003-119] For Wiwâstè had found her a way to woo.

[003-120] Ah, little he cared for the bison-chase;

[003-121] For the red lilies bloomed on the fair maid\'s face;

[003-122] Ah, little he cared for the winds that blew,

[003-123] For Wiwâstè had found her a way to woo.

[003-124] Brown-bosomed she sat on her fox-robe dark,

[003-125] Her ear to the tales of the brave inclined,

[003-126] Or tripped from the tee like the song of a lark,

[003-127] And gathered her hair from the wanton wind.

[003-128] Ah, little he thought of the leagues of snow

[003-129] He trode on the trail of the buffalo;

[003-130] And little he recked of the hurricanes

[003-131] That swept the snow from the frozen plains

[003-132] And piled the banks of the Bloody River. \[40\]

[003-133] His bow unstrung and forgotten hung

[003-134] With his beaver hood and his otter quiver;

[003-135] He sat spell-bound by the artless grace

[003-136] Of her star-lit eyes and her moon-lit face.

[003-137] Ah, little he cared for the storms that blew,

[003-138] For Wiwâstè had found her a way to woo.

[003-139] When he spoke with Wakâwa her sidelong eyes

[003-140] Sought the handsome chief in his hunter-guise.

[003-141] Wakâwa marked, and the lilies fair

[003-142] On her round cheeks spread to her raven hair.

[003-143] They feasted on rib of the bison fat,

[003-144] On the tongue of the Ta \[41\] that the hunters prize,

[003-145] On the savory flesh of the red Hogân, \[42\]

[003-146] On sweet tipsânna \[43\] and pemmican,

[003-147] And the dun-brown cakes of the golden maize;

[003-148] And hour after hour the young chief sat,

[003-149] And feasted his soul on the maiden\'s eyes.

[003-150] The sweeter the moments the swifter they fly;

[003-151] Love takes no account of the fleeting hours;

[003-152] He walks in a dream mid the blooming of flowers,

[003-153] And never awakes till the blossoms die.

[003-154] Ah, lovers are lovers the wide world over\--

[003-155] In the hunter\'s lodge and the royal palace.

[003-156] Sweet are the lips of his love to the lover,\--

[003-157] Sweet as new wine in a golden chalice,

[003-158] From the Tajo\'s \[44\] slopes or the hills beyond;

[003-159] And blindly he sips from his loved one\'s lips,

[003-160] In lodge or palace the wide world over,

[003-161] The maddening honey of Trebizond. \[45\]

[003-162] O, what are leagues to the loving hunter,

[003-163] Or the blinding drift of the hurricane,

[003-164] When it raves and roars o\'er the frozen plain!

[003-165] He would face the storm,\--he would death encounter

[003-166] The darling prize of his heart to gain.

[003-167] But his hunters chafed at the long delay,

[003-168] For the swarthy bison were far away,

[003-169] And the brave young chief from the lodge departed.

[003-170] He promised to come with the robin in May,

[003-171] With the bridal gifts for the bridal day;

[003-172] And the fair Wiwâstè was happy-hearted,

[003-173] For Wakâwa promised the brave Chaskè.

[003-174] Birds of a feather will flock together.

[003-175] The robin sings to his ruddy mate,

[003-176] And the chattering jays, in the winter weather,

[003-177] To prate and gossip will congregate;

[003-178] And the cawing crows on the autumn heather,

[003-179] Like evil omens, will flock together,

[003-180] In extra-session, for high debate;

[003-181] And the lass will slip from a doting mother

[003-182] To hang with her lad on the garden gate.

[003-183] Birds of a feather will flock together,\--

[003-184] \'Tis an adage old,\--it is nature\'s law,

[003-185] And sure as the pole will the needle draw,

[003-186] The fierce Red Cloud with the flaunting feather,

[003-187] Will follow the finger of Hârpstinà.

[003-188] The winter wanes and the south-wind blows

[003-189] From the Summer Islands legendary.

[003-190] The skéskas \[46\] fly and the melted snows

[003-191] In lakelets lie on the dimpled prairie.

[003-192] The frost-flowers \[47\] peep from their winter sleep

[003-193] Under the snow-drifts cold and deep.

[003-194] To the April sun and the April showers,

[003-195] In field and forest, the baby flowers

[003-196] Lift their golden faces and azure eyes;

[003-197] And wet with the tears of the winter-fairies,

[003-198] Soon bloom and blossom the emerald prairies,

[003-199] Like the fabled Garden of Paradise.

[003-200] The plum-trees, white with their bloom in May,

[003-201] Their sweet perfume on the vernal breeze

[003-202] Wide strew like the isles of the tropic seas,

[003-203] Where the paroquet chatters the livelong day.

[003-204] But the May-days pass and the brave Chaskè\--

[003-205] O, why does the lover so long delay?

[003-206] Wiwâstè waits in the lonely tee,

[003-207] Has her fair face fled from his memory?

[003-208] For the robin cherups his mate to please,

[003-209] The blue bird pipes in the poplar trees,

[003-210] The meadow lark warbles his jubilees,

[003-211] Shrilling his song in the azure seas,

[003-212] Till the welkin throbs to his melodies;

[003-213] And low is the hum of the humble bees,

[003-214] And the Feast of the Virgins is now to be.

THE FEAST OF THE VIRGINS.

[003-215] The sun sails high in his azure realms;

[003-216] Beneath the arch of the breezy elms

[003-217] The feast is spread by the murmuring river.

[003-218] With his battle spear and his bow and quiver,

[003-219] And eagle plumes in his ebon hair,

[003-220] The chief Wakâwa himself is there;

[003-221] And round the feast in the Sacred Ring, \[48\]

[003-222] Sit his weaponed warriors witnessing.

[003-223] Not a morsel of food have the Virgins tasted

[003-224] For three long days ere the holy feast;

[003-225] They sat in their teepee alone and fasted,

[003-226] Their faces turned to the Sacred East. \[21\]

[003-227] In the polished bowls lies the golden maize

[003-228] And the flesh of fawn on the polished trays.

[003-229] For the Virgins the bloom of the prairies wide\--

[003-230] The blushing pink and the meek blue-bell,

[003-231] The purple plumes of the prairie\'s pride, \[49\]

[003-232] The wild, uncultured asphodel,

[003-233] And the beautiful, blue-eyed violet

[003-234] That the Virgins call \"Let-me-not-forget,\"

[003-235] In gay festoons and garlands twine

[003-236] With the cedar sprigs \[50\] and the wildwood vine.

[003-237] So gaily the Virgins are decked and dressed,

[003-238] And none but a virgin may enter there;

[003-239] And clad is each in a scarlet vest,

[003-240] And a fawn skin frock to the brown calves bare.

[003-241] Wild rosebuds peep from their flowing hair,

[003-242] And a rose half-blown on the budding breast;

[003-243] And bright with the quills of the porcupine

[003-244] The moccasined feet of the maidens shine.

[003-245] Hand in hand round the feast they dance,

[003-246] And sing to the notes of a rude bassoon,

[003-247] And never a pause or a dissonance

[003-248] In the merry dance or the merry tune.

[003-249] Brown-bosomed and fair as the rising moon,

[003-250] When she peeps o\'er the hills of the dewy east,

[003-251] Wiwâstè sings at the Virgins Feast;

[003-252] And bright is the light in her luminous eyes;

[003-253] They glow like the stars in the winter skies;

[003-254] And the lilies that bloom in her virgin heart

[003-255] Their golden blush to her cheeks impart\--

[003-256] Her cheeks half hid in her midnight hair.

[003-257] Fair is her form\--as the red fawn\'s fair,

[003-258] And long is the flow of her raven hair;

[003-259] It falls to her knees, and it streams on the breeze

[003-260] Like the path of a storm on the swelling seas.
