# Superstition

The superstitions of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people were a primary subject of H. L. Gordon's study and a central element of the [[legend|legends]] he collected in *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest*.

## Gordon's Study

In his preface, Gordon states that he "devoted many of his leisure hours to the study of the language, history, [[tradition|traditions]], [[tradition|customs]] and superstitions of the [[dakota|Dakotas]]" (P01-007). This five-part study — language, history, [[tradition|traditions]], [[tradition|customs]], and superstitions — provided the foundation for the poetic [[legend|legends]] that follow.

## Scope

The [[dakota|Dakota]] superstitions encompassed:

- Belief in [[spirit|spirits]] that walk "in the realms of air" (001-192)
- The concept of *[[wakan|Wakan]]* — sacred or mysterious power
- The role of the *Wicasta [[wakan|Wakan]]* (medicine man) in [[soul|spirit]]ual healing (017-044)
- The sacred nature of certain locations like the [[wakan-teepee-site|Wakan Teepee]] (015-242)

## In the Book's Introduction

Gordon acknowledges his debt to the Reverend S. R. Riggs, whose *Tah-Koo [[wakan|Wah-Kan]]* ("The Gospel Among the [[dakota|Dakotas]]") documented Dakota beliefs from a missionary perspective (P01-013, P01-063). He also notes that his poetic representations of "[[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|customs]], life, [[tradition|traditions]], and superstitions" aim to be faithful to his sources (P01-174).
