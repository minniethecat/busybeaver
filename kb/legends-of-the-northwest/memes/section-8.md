[007-001] Poor Father Menard,\--he was ill;

[007-002] in his breast burned the fire of the fever;

[007-003] All in vain was the magical skill

[007-004] of Wicásta Wakán \[61\] with his rattle;

[007-005] Into soft child-like slumber he fell,

[007-006] and awoke in the land of the blessèd\--

[007-007] To the holy applause of \"Well done!\"

[007-008] and the harps in the hands of the angels.

[007-009] Long he carried the cross,

[007-010] and he won the coveted crown of a martyr.

[007-011] In the land of the heathen he died,

[007-012] meekly following the voice of his Master,

[007-013] One mourner alone by his side

[007-014] \--Ta-té-psin\'s compassionate daughter.

[007-015] She wailed the dead father with tears,

[007-016] and his bones by her kindred she buried.

[007-017] Then winter followed winter.

[007-018] The years sprinkled frost on the head of her father;

[007-019] And three weary winters she dreamed

[007-020] of the fearless and fair-bearded Frenchmen;

[007-021] In her sweet sleep their swift paddles gleamed

[007-022] on the breast of the broad Mississippi,

[007-023] And the eyes of the brave strangers beamed

[007-024] on the maid in the midst of her slumber.

[007-025] She lacked not admirers;

[007-026] the light of the lover oft burned in her teepee\--

[007-027] At her couch in the midst of the night,

[007-028] \--but she never extinguished the flambeau.

[007-029] The son of Chief Wazi-kuté

[007-030] \--a fearless and eagle plumed warrior\--

[007-031] Long sighed for Winona, and he

[007-032] \--was the pride of the band of Isántees.

[007-033] Three times, in the night, at her bed,

[007-034] had the brave held the torch of the lover, \[75\]And thrice had she

[007-035] covered her head

[007-036] and rejected the handsome Tamdóka. \[a\]

[007-037] \[a\] Tah-mdo-kah\--literally the buck deer.

[007-038] \'Twas Summer. The merry voiced birds

[007-039] trilled and warbled in woodland and meadow;

[007-040] And abroad on the prairies the herds

[007-041] cropped the grass in the land of the lilies,\--

[007-042] And sweet was the odor of rose

[007-043] wide-wafted from hillside and heather;

[007-044] In the leaf-shaded lap of repose

[007-045] lay the bright, blue eyed babes of the summer;

[007-046] And low was the murmur of brooks

[007-047] and low was the laugh of the Ha-Ha; \[76\]

[007-048] And asleep in the eddies and nooks

[007-049] lay the broods of magá \[60\] and the mallard.

[007-050] \'Twas the moon of Wasúnpa. \[71\]

[007-051] The band lay at rest in the tees at Ka-thá-ga,

[007-052] And abroad o\'er the beautiful land

[007-053] walked the [[spirit|spirits]] of Peace and of Plenty\--

[007-054] Twin sisters, with bountiful hand,

[007-055] wide scatt\'ring wild rice and the lilies.

[007-056] An-pé-tu-wee \[70\] walked in the west

[007-057] \--to his lodge in the midst of the mountains,

[007-058] And the war eagle flew to her nest

[007-059] in the oak on the Isle of the Spirit. \[a\]

[007-060] And now at the end of the day,

[007-061] by the shore of the Beautiful Island, \[b\]

[007-062] A score of fair maidens and gay

[007-063] made joy in the midst of the waters.

[007-064] Half-robed in their dark, flowing hair,

[007-065] and limbed like the fair Aphroditè,

[007-066] They played in the waters,

[007-067] and there they dived and they swam like the beavers,\--

[007-068] Loud-laughing like loons on the lake

[007-069] when the moon is a round shield of silver,

[007-070] And the [[song|songs]] of the whippowils

[007-071] wake on the shore in the midst of the maples.

[007-072] \[a\] The [[dakota|Dakotas]] say that for many years in olden times a war-eagle

[007-073] made her nest in an oak tree on Spirit island\--Wanagi-wita just below the

[007-074] Falls till frightened away by the advent of white men.

[007-075] \[b\] The Dakotas called Nicollet Island \"Wi-ta Waste\"\--the Beautiful Island.

[007-076] But hark!\--on the river a song,

[007-077] \--strange voices commingled in chorus;

[007-078] On the current a boat swept along

[007-079] with DuLuth and his hardy companions;

[007-080] To the stroke of their paddles they sung,

[007-081] and this the refrain that they chanted:

[007-082] \"Dans mon chemin j\'ai recontré

[007-083] Deux cavaliers bien monteés.

[007-084] Lon, lon, laridon daine,

[007-085] Lon, lon, laridon dai.\"

[007-086] \"Deux cavaliers bien monteés;

[007-087] L\'un a cheval, et l\'autre a pied.

[007-088] Lon, lon, laridon daine,

[007-089] Lon, lon, laridon dai.\" \[a\]

[007-090] Like the red, dappled deer in the glade,

[007-091] alarmed by the footsteps of hunters,

[007-092] Discovered, disordered, dismayed,

[007-093] the nude nymphs fled forth from the waters,

[007-094] And scampered away to the shade,

[007-095] and peered from the screen of the lindens.

[007-096] \[a\] A part of one of the favorite songs of the French _voyageurs_.

[007-097] A bold and and adventuresome man was DuLuth,

[007-098] and a dauntless in danger,

[007-099] And straight to Kathága he ran,

[007-100] and boldly advanced to the warriors,

[007-101] Now gathering, a cloud, on the strand,

[007-102] and gazing amazed on the strangers;

[007-103] And straightway he offered his hand

[007-104] unto Wázi-kuté, the Itáncan.

[007-105] To the Lodge of the Stranger were led

[007-106] DuLuth and his hardy companions;

[007-107] Robes of beaver and bison were spread,

[007-108] and the Peace pipe \[23\] was smoked with the Frenchman.

[007-109] There was dancing and feasting at night,

[007-110] and joy at the presents he lavished.

[007-111] All the maidens were wild with delight

[007-112] with the flaming red robes and the ribbons,

[007-113] With the beads and the trinkets untold,

[007-114] and the fair, bearded face of the giver;

[007-115] And glad were they all to behold the friends

[007-116] from the Land of the Sunrise.

[007-117] But one stood apart from the rest

[007-118] \--the queenly and peerless Winona,

[007-119] Intently regarding the guest

[007-120] \--hardly heeding the robes and the ribbons,

[007-121] Whom the White Chief beholding admired,

[007-122] and straightway he spread on her shoulders

[007-123] A lily-red robe and attired,

[007-124] with necklet and ribbons, the maiden.

[007-125] The red lilies bloomed in her face,

[007-126] and her glad eyes gave thanks to the giver,

[007-127] And forth from her teepee apace

[007-128] she brought him the robe and the missal

[007-129] Of the father\--poor Renè Menard;

[007-130] and related the tale of the \"Black Robe.\"

[007-131] She spoke of the sacred regard

[007-132] he inspired in the hearts of Dakotas;

[007-133] That she buried his bones with her kin,

[007-134] in the mound by the Cave of the Council;

[007-135] That she treasured and wrapt

[007-136] in the skin of the red-deer his robe and his prayer-book\--

[007-137] \"Till his brothers should come from the East

[007-138] \--from the land of the far Hochelága,

[007-139] To smoke with the braves at the feast,

[007-140] on the shores of the Loud-laughing Waters. \[76\]

[007-141] For the \"Black Robe\" spake much of his youth

[007-142] and his friends in the Land of the Sunrise;

[007-143] It was then as a dream, now in truth,

[007-144] I behold them, and not in a vision.\"

[007-145] But more spake her blushes, I ween,

[007-146] and her eyes full of language unspoken,

[007-147] As she turned with the grace of a queen,

[007-148] and carried her gifts to the teepee.

[007-149] Far away from his beautiful France

[007-150] \--from his home in the city of Lyons,

[007-151] A noble youth full of romance,

[007-152] with a Norman heart big with adventure,

[007-153] In the new world a wanderer, by chance,

[007-154] DuLuth sought the wild Huron forests.

[007-155] But afar by the vale of the Rhone,

[007-156] the winding and musical river,

[007-157] And the vine-covered hills of the Saône,

[007-158] the heart of the wanderer lingered,\--

[007-159] \'Mid the vineyards and mulberry trees,

[007-160] and the fair fields of corn and of clover

[007-161] That rippled and waved in the breeze,

[007-162] while the honey-bees hummed in the blossoms

[007-163] For there, where the impetuous Rhone,

[007-164] leaping down from the Switzerland mountains,

[007-165] And the silver-lipped soft flowing Saône,

[007-166] meeting, kiss and commingle together,

[007-167] Down-winding by vineyards and leas,

[007-168] by the orchards of fig trees and olives,

[007-169] To the island-gemmed, sapphire-blue seas

[007-170] of the glorious Greeks and the Romans;

[007-171] Aye, there, on the vine covered shore,

[007-172] \'mid the mulberry trees and the olives,

[007-173] Dwelt his blue-eyed and beautiful Flore,

[007-174] with her hair like a wheat field at harvest,

[007-175] All rippled and tossed by the breeze,

[007-176] and her cheeks like the glow of the morning,

[007-177] Far away o\'er the emerald seas,

[007-178] ere the sun lifts his brow from the billows,

[007-179] Or the red-clover fields when the bees,

[007-180] singing sip the sweet cups of the blossoms.

[007-181] Wherever he wandered

[007-182] \--alone in the heart of the wild Huron forests,

[007-183] Or cruising the rivers unknown

[007-184] to the land of the Crees or Dakotas\--

[007-185] His heart lingered still on the Rhone,

[007-186] \'mid the mulberry-trees and the vineyards,

[007-187] Fast-fettered and bound by the zone

[007-188] that girdled the robes of his darling.

[007-189] Till the red Harvest Moon \[71\]

[007-190] he remained in the vale of the swift Mississippi.

[007-191] The esteem of the warriors he gained,

[007-192] and the love of the dark eyed Winona.

[007-193] He joined in the sports and the chase;

[007-194] with the hunters he followed the bison,

[007-195] And swift were his feet in the race

[007-196] when the red elk they ran on the prairies.

[007-197] At the Game of the Plum-stones \[77\] he played

[007-198] and he won from the skillfulest players;

[007-199] A feast to Wa\'tánka \[78\] he made,

[007-200] and he danced at the feast of Heyóka. \[16\]

[007-201] With the flash and the roar of his gun

[007-202] he astonished the fearless Dakotas;

[007-203] They called it the \"Máza Wakán\"

[007-204] \--the mighty, mysterious metal.

[007-205] \"\'Tis a brother,\" they said,

[007-206] \"of the fire in the talons of dreadful Wakínyan, \[32\]

[007-207] When he flaps his huge wings in his ire,

[007-208] and shoots his red shafts at Unktéhee.\" \[69\]

[007-209] The Itancan, \[74\] tall Wazí-kuté,

[007-210] appointed a day for the races.

[007-211] From the red stake that stood by his tee,

[007-212] on the southerly side of the Ha-ha

[007-213] To a stake at the Lake of the Loons \[79\]

[007-214] \--a league and return\--was the distance.

[007-215] On the crest of the hills red batons

[007-216] marked the course for the feet of the runners.

[007-217] They gathered from near and afar,

[007-218] to the races and dancing and feasting.

[007-219] Five hundred tall warriors were there

[007-220] from Kapóza \[6\] and far off Keóza; \[8\]

[007-221] Remnica, \[a\] too, furnished a share

[007-222] of the legions that thronged to the races,

[007-223] And a bountiful feast was prepared

[007-224] by the diligent hands of the women,

[007-225] And gaily the multitudes fared

[007-226] in the generous tees of Kathága.

[007-227] The chief of the mystical clan

[007-228] appointed a feast to Unktéhee\--

[007-229] The mystic \"Wacípee Wakán\" \[b\]\--

[007-230] at the end of the day and the races.

[007-231] A band of sworn brothers are they,

[007-232] and the secrets of each one are sacred.

[007-233] And death to the lips that betray

[007-234] is the doom of the swarthy avengers,

[007-235] And the son of tall Wazí-kuté

[007-236] was the chief of the mystical order.

[007-237] \[a\] Pronounced Ray mne chah\--the village of the Mountains situate where

[007-238] Red Wing now stands.

[007-239] \[b\] Sacred [[dance|Dance]]\--The Medicine dance\--See description infra.

[007-240] On an arm of an oak hangs the prize

[007-241] for the swiftest and strongest of runners\--

[007-242] A blanket as red as the skies,

[007-243] when the flames sweep the plains in October.

[007-244] And beside it a strong, polished bow,

[007-245] and a quiver of iron tipped arrows,

[007-246] Which Kapóza\'s tall chief will bestow

[007-247] on the fleet-footed second that follows.

[007-248] A score of swift-runners are there

[007-249] from the several bands of the nation;

[007-250] And now for the race they prepare,

[007-251] and among them fleet-footed Tamdóka.

[007-252] With the oil of the buck and the bear

[007-253] their sinewy limbs are anointed,

[007-254] For fleet are the feet of the deer

[007-255] and strong are the limbs of the bruin,

[007-256] And long is the course and severe

[007-257] for the swiftest and strongest of runners.

[007-258] Hark!\--the shouts and the braying of drums,

[007-259] and the Babel of tongues and confusion!

[007-260] From his teepee the tall chieftain comes,

[007-261] and Duluth brings a prize for the runners\--

[007-262] A keen hunting-knife from the Seine,

[007-263] horn-handled and mounted with silver.

[007-264] The runners are ranged on the plain,

[007-265] and the Chief waves a flag as a signal,

[007-266] And away like the gray wolves they fly

[007-267] \--like the wolves on the trail of the red deer;

[007-268] O\'er the hills and the prairie they vie,

[007-269] and strain their strong limbs to the utmost,

[007-270] While high on the hills hangs a cloud

[007-271] of warriors and maidens and mothers,

[007-272] To behold the swift runners,

[007-273] and loud are the cheers and the shouts of the warriors.

[007-274] Now swift from the lake they return,

[007-275] o\'er the emerald hills and the heather;

[007-276] Like grey-hounds they pant and they yearn,

[007-277] and the leader of all is Tamdóka.

[007-278] At his heels flies Hu-pá-hu, \[a\] the fleet

[007-279] \--the pride of the band of Kaóza,

[007-280] A warrior with eagle-winged feet,

[007-281] but his prize is the bow and the quiver.

[007-282] Tamdóka first reaches the post,

[007-283] and his are the knife and the blanket,

[007-284] By the mighty acclaim of the host

[007-285] and award of the chief and the judges.

[007-286] Then proud was the tall warrior\'s stride,

[007-287] and haughty his look and demeanor;

[007-288] He boasted aloud in his pride,

[007-289] and he scoffed at the rest of the runners.

[007-290] \"Behold me, for I am a man! \[b\]

[007-291] my feet are as swift as the West wind.

[007-292] With the coons and the beavers I ran;

[007-293] but where is the elk or the cabri? \[80\]

[007-294] Come!\--where is the hunter will dare

[007-295] match his feet with the feet of Tamdóka?

[007-296] Let him think of Taté \[c\] and beware,

[007-297] ere he stake his last robe on the trial.\"

[007-298] \"Ohó! Ho! Hó-héca!\" \[d\] they jeered,

[007-299] for they liked not the boast of the boaster;

[007-300] But to match him no warrior appeared,

[007-301] for his feet wore the wings of the west-wind.
