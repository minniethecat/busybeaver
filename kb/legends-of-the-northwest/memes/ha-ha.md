# Ha-Ha

Ha-Ha (I-ha-ha) is the [[dakota|Dakota]] name for the [[falls-of-st-anthony|Falls of St. Anthony]], an onomatopoeic term imitating the sound of laughing water — "lightly laughing" (017-180). Gordon weaves the phrase into his poetic refrain throughout the Prelude.

## Definition / Identity

The term "Ha-Ha" is the [[dakota|Dakota]] rendering of the sound of water rushing over [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] — a linguistic fossil of how the [[dakota|Dakota]] people perceived the great cataract not as a roar of destruction but as laughter.

- **Origin**: [[dakota|Dakota]] onomatopoeia — the sound of falling water
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]] (*I-ha-ha*, pronounced "E-rhah-rhah — lightly laughing" per [[samuel-pond|Rev. S. W. Pond]]) (017-180)
- **Domain**: linguistic / geographical

Gordon adopts the [[dakota|Dakota]] term directly into his English verse as a rhythmic refrain, preserving the indigenous acoustic perception of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] within the poem's sonic texture.

## In the Book

Gordon opens the Prelude with the voice of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] itself — the "Ha-ha" refrain recurs as the cataract's own speech, personifying the Mississippi at its most dramatic geographical feature.

### Role in the Legends

The "Ha-ha" refrain appears in the Prelude as the laughing voice of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]: "Ha-ha, — ha-ha, — ha-ha, — ha!" (001-223). This framing device establishes [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] as a living presence before any human character appears, and the [[dakota|Dakota]] name embeds an indigenous worldview directly into the poem's opening.

Gordon's footnote explains the term's pronunciation and meaning: "I-ha-ha pronounced E-rhah-rhah — lightly laughing" (017-180), crediting the [[samuel-pond|Rev. S. W. Pond]], a missionary linguist who documented the [[dakota|Dakota]] language.

### Representative Passages

> "I-ha-ha pronounced E-rhah-rhah — lightly laughing." — Rev. S. W. Pond, cited in Gordon's Notes (017-180)
