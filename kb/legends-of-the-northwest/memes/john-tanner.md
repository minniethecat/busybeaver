John Tanner (c. 1780–1846) was an American frontiersman who was captured as a child by Ojibwe warriors and spent most of his life among the Ojibwe and Ottawa peoples of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s region. His published account — "A Narrative of the Captivity and Adventures of John Tanner" (1830) — became a widely cited ethnographic source on [[algonkin|Algonkin]] [[tradition|customs]], language, and beliefs. Gordon draws on Tanner's narrative multiple times in the footnotes and appendix of "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest," treating it as an authoritative reference for [[algonkin|Algonkin]] terminology and social organization.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: American (European descent), adopted into Ojibwe/Ottawa society
- **Role**: Frontiersman, captive-adoptee, author
- **Family**: Adopted by [[net-no-kwa|Net-nó-kwa]], an Ottawa female chief ([[net-no-kwa|Oge-mâ-kwa]])

## 在书中的角色

### 作为民族志来源

Gordon cites Tanner's narrative in several footnotes and appendix entries as an authoritative source on [[algonkin|Algonkin]] cultural and linguistic knowledge. Unlike other historical figures who appear in the poetry itself, Tanner's presence in "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" is entirely scholarly — his eyewitness account of Ojibwe and Ottawa life provides corroboration for Gordon's descriptions of [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[algonkin|Algonkin]] [[tradition|customs]].

### Net-nó-kwa 的收养关系

In a footnote discussing female leadership among [[algonkin|Algonkin]] tribes, Gordon records that Tanner was adopted by a prominent Ottawa woman:

> Among the Algonkin tribes women are sometimes made chiefs. Net-nó-kwa, who adopted Tanner as her son, was Oge-mâ-kwa of a band of Ottawas. (019-022)

This brief reference situates Tanner within the matrilineal kinship networks that Gordon's broader ethnographic notes describe. The adoption of white captives into Native families was a recognized practice in the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s frontier, and Tanner's narrative provides one of the most detailed accounts of this experience.

## 历史/文化背景

John Tanner was born around 1780 on the Kentucky frontier. At approximately nine years old, he was captured by a Shawnee war party and later sold to an Ottawa family. He was adopted by [[net-no-kwa|Net-nó-kwa]] (known in English as "[[net-no-kwa|The Otter Woman]]"), who was an [[net-no-kwa|Oge-mâ-kwa]] — a female chief — of a band of Ottawas in the [[red-river|Red River]] region. Tanner lived among the Ojibwe and Ottawa for nearly thirty years, learning their languages, [[tradition|customs]], and hunting techniques, before attempting to reintegrate into American society in the 1820s.

His narrative, dictated to Dr. Edwin James and published in 1830, provides detailed descriptions of Ojibwe hunting practices, [[soul|spirit]]ual beliefs, kinship systems, and intertribal warfare. The work was immediately recognized as a significant ethnographic document and was cited by later writers including [[henry-schoolcraft|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]] and [[hanford-gordon|Hanford Lennox Gordon]]. Tanner's life ended tragically — he disappeared from Sault Ste. Marie in 1846 under suspicious circumstances, possibly murdered after being falsely accused of a crime.

## 书中引文

Gordon references Tanner's work in multiple scholarly contexts:

On [[algonkin|Algonkin]] cosmology:
> The Algonkins call the earth "Me-suk-kum-mik-o-kwa" — the great-grandmother of all. Narrative of John Tanner, p. 193. (017-109)

On [[algonkin|Algonkin]] zoological terminology:
> It is the Pish-tah-te-koosh of the Algonkin tribes, "reckoned the fleetest animal in the prairie country about the Assinneboin." Captivity and Adventures of John Tanner, p. 301. (018-020)

On [[net-no-kwa|Net-nó-kwa]], Tanner's adoptive mother:
> Among the Algonkin tribes women are sometimes made chiefs. Net-nó-kwa, who adopted Tanner as her son, was Oge-mâ-kwa of a band of Ottawas. See John Tanner's Narrative, p. 36. (019-022), (019-023)
