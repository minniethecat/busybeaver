# Fort Snelling

**Fort Snelling** — the U.S. military fortification at the confluence of the [[Mississippi River|Mississippi]] and [[Minnesota|Minnesota]] Rivers — stands at a site the [[Dakota]] knew as [[Mendota]] (*[[mendota|Mdó-tè]]*), meaning "the outlet of lake or river into another" (009-024–026).

## Location

The fort occupies the bluff overlooking the meeting of the two great rivers. The [[dakota|Dakota]] name *[[mendota|Mdó-tè]]* was:

> commonly applied to the region about Fort Snelling.
> (009-026)

An illustration captioned "View of the Mississippi at Fort Snelling" appears in Gordon's original volume, underscoring the site's scenic and strategic importance (P01-028).

## The Name Mendota

The area around Fort Snelling was called *[[mendota|Mendota]]* by the [[dakota|Dakota]]:

> Mendota, properly *Mdó-tè* — meaning the outlet of lake or river into another.
> (009-024–025)

The French name *[[mendota|Mendota]]* — "meeting of the waters" — describes the confluence of the [[Mississippi River|Mississippi]] and [[Minnesota|Minnesota]] Rivers (015-218–219).

## Historical Significance

### Early Contact

More than a century before Gordon's time, an English trader was killed at [[mendota|Mendota]] for persisting in selling alcohol (*mini-wakan*, "devil-water") to the [[dakota|Dakota]] braves, despite repeated warnings by the chiefs (P01-028–030).

### Military and Trade Role

Fort Snelling became a key node in the upper Mississippi region, overseeing both military operations and the [[fur trade]] that connected [[voyageurs|French voyageurs]], the [[Dakota]], and the [[Ojibway Nation|Ojibway]].

## In Gordon's Sources

Gordon drew upon Snelling's *Tales of the Northwest* as a source for his own *[[Legend|Legends]]*, citing it for details of [[dakota|Dakota]] life and natural history (016-050, 018-016).
