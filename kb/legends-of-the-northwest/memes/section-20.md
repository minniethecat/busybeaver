[019-001] \[Footnote 1017: Waub-ésè\--the white swan.\]

[019-002] \[Footnote 1018: Pé-boân, Winter, is represented as an old man with long

[019-003] white hair and beard.\]

[019-004] \[Footnote 1019: Se-gún is Spring or Summer. This beautiful allegory has

[019-005] been \"done into verse\" by Longfellow in _Hiawatha_. I took my version

[019-006] from the lips of an old [[ojibway|Chippewa]] Chief. I have compared it with

[019-007] Schoolcraft\'s version, from which Mr. Longfellow evidently took his.\]

[019-008] \[Footnote 1020: Nah\--look, see. Nashké\--behold.\]

[019-009] \[Footnote 1021: Kee-zis\--the sun,\--the father of life. Waubúnong\--or

[019-010] Waub-ó-nong\--is the White Land or Land of Light,\--the Sun-rise, the

[019-011] East.\]

[019-012] \[Footnote 1022: The Bridge of Stars spans the vast sea of the skies, and

[019-013] the sun and moon walk over on it.\]

[019-014] \[Footnote 1023: The Miscodeed is a small white flower with a pink

[019-015] border. It is the earliestblooming wild-flower on the shores of Lake

[019-016] Superior, and belongs to the crocus family.\]

[019-017] \[Footnote 1024: The Ne-be-naw-baigs, are Water-[[spirit|spirits]]; they dwell in

[019-018] caverns in the depths of the lake, and in some respects resemble the

[019-019] Unktéhees of the [[dakota|Dakotas]].\]

[019-020] \[Footnote 1025: Ogema, Chief,\--Ogema-kwa\--female Chief. Among the

[019-021] [[algonkin|Algonkin]] tribes women are sometimes made chiefs. Net-nó-kwa, who

[019-022] adopted Tanner as her son, was Oge-mâ-kwa of a band of Ottawas. See John

[019-023] Tanner\'s Narrative, p. 36.\]

[019-024] \[Footnote 1026: The \"Bridge of Souls\" leads from the earth over dark and

[019-025] stormy waters to the Spirit-land. The \"Dark River\" seems to have been a

[019-026] part of the superstition of all nations.\]

[019-027] \[Footnote 1027: The Jossakeeds of the Ojibways are sooth-sayers who are

[019-028] able, by the aid of spirits, to read the past as well as the future.\]
