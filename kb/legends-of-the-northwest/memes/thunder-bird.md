The Thunder-Bird (Wakínyan in [[dakota|Dakota]]) is an immense supernatural bird whose wing-flapping produces thunder (016-122). It is both a powerful [[soul|spirit]]-being in [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmology and, as [[big-thunder|Wakínyan Tânka]] ([[big-thunder|Big Thunder]]), a hereditary chiefly title among the [[dakota|Dakota]] people.

## Definition / Identity

The [[dakota|Dakotas]] believe thunder is produced by the flapping wings of an immense bird called Wakínyan — the Thunder-Bird (016-122). The "father of all the Thunder-birds" is [[big-thunder|Wakínyan Tânka]] — [[big-thunder|Big Thunder]] (016-126), the supreme manifestation of this being.

In [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology, Wakínyan is the antithesis of [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] (the water [[soul|spirit]]): "Ye shall honor [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] and hate Wakínyan" (008-151), establishing a cosmic opposition between sky-thunder and water-depth.

- **Origin**: [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmology; the term Wakínyan is the indigenous [[dakota|Dakota]] name
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]]
- **Domain**: [[legend|myth]]ology / [[soul|spirit]]uality / social-structure

Wakínyan is credited with creative acts: it "created wild-rice, the bow and..." (016-132). The Thunder-Bird's fire — its lightning — is a feared and respected power (009-052, 011-055). Men also bore the name [[big-thunder|Wakínyan Tânka]] as a chiefly title, claiming descent from the Thunder-Bird (015-231, 016-136).

## In the Book

Gordon uses Thunder-Bird imagery throughout the [[legend|legends]] as a symbol of fearsome power. In "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]," a flap of wings is "like a thunder-bird" (002-197), and the fiery dart of Wakínyan represents unstoppable force (004-055).

In "[[winona|Winona]]," the Thunder-Bird flashes and thunders in cosmic battle with [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] (012-221). The historical notes identify [[big-thunder|Wakínyan Tânka]] as a chiefly lineage: the son of [[red-cloud|Red Cloud]] who succeeded as chief (015-231, 015-233).

### Role in the Legends

The Thunder-Bird serves a dual role. In the poetic narrative, it is a [[legend|myth]]ological force — swift, fiery, and terrible (004-055, 007-206). In the scholarly footnotes, it becomes historical: [[dakota|Dakota]] men bore the name [[big-thunder|Wakínyan Tânka]] as a chiefly title (016-136).

Gordon's young warrior protagonist carries "the fire of Wakínyan" (009-052, 011-055), marking him as both [[soul|spirit]]ually potent and of chiefly lineage. The Thunder-Bird thus bridges the [[legend|myth]]ological and the historical in Gordon's treatment.

### Representative Passages
