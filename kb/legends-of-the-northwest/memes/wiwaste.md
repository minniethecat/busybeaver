# Wiwásté

The fairest [[dakota|Dakota]] maiden, daughter of a [[dakota|Dakota]] chief, and the central romantic figure in Gordon's "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" (Chapters II–V). Wiwásté is pursued by multiple suitors, and her courtship by the brave [[chaske|Chaské]] drives the narrative of the legend.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Sioux]])
- **Role**: Maiden, daughter of a chief
- **Family**: Father: a [[dakota|Dakota]] chief (001-168); husband: [[chaske|Chaské]] (005-205, 005-220)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Wiwásté is the heroine of "The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]" legend (Chapters II–V). She is introduced as the jewel of the [[dakota|Dakota]] village — beautiful, sought-after, and the object of the warriors' athletic competitions. Her courtship and eventual union with [[chaske|Chaské]] form the legend's emotional core.

### 关键事件

Wiwásté first appears as a vision of beauty and skill: "Wiwâstè is chief of a nimble band" (001-101). Her prowess is matched by her grace: "From the fair Wiwâstè's sturdy stroke" (001-123), and she is crowned victor in the game: "And the fair Wiwâstè was victor crowned" (001-130).

Her beauty is celebrated: "Wiwâstè, the fairest [[dakota|Dakota]] maiden" (001-151). A warrior has long courted her father for permission: "Long had he courted Wiwâstè's father" (001-168). Wiwásté observes the warriors who compete for her favor: "Wiwâstè looked on the warrior tall" (001-182).

The courtship intensifies as Wiwásté waits alone at night: "Wiwâstè sat late in the lodge alone" (002-066). A rival suitor threatens her with supernatural terror — "Is Wiwâstè afraid of the [[soul|spirit]] dread" (002-087) — but Wiwásté remains steadfast. The chief and "the queenly Wiwâstè stood" together (002-098).

The brave [[chaske|Chaské]] declares his love: "'Wiwâstè,' he said, and his voice was low" (002-157). Wiwásté waits through the seasons: "All snug in the teepee Wiwâstè lay" (003-001). The rival continues scheming — "And Wiwâstè will find her a way to woo" (003-013, 003-025) — but ultimately Wiwásté chooses [[chaske|Chaské]].

The legend concludes with their union: "Wiwâstè requests that the brave [[chaske|Chaské]]" (005-152), and Wiwásté is called "the beautiful bride of the brave [[chaske|Chaské]]" (005-205). She rides triumphantly with her husband: "Wiwâstè rode with her proud [[chaske|Chaské]]" (005-220), and is remembered "ever her beautiful, brave [[chaske|Chaské]]" (005-236).

## 历史/文化背景

Wiwásté is a legendary figure, representing the [[dakota|Dakota]] ideal of feminine beauty, grace, and fidelity. Her story follows a courtship narrative pattern common in oral tradition — the desirable maiden whose choice among rival suitors drives the plot. Gordon portrays Wiwásté as more than a passive prize; her skill in the game (001-123) and her steadfastness against supernatural intimidation (002-087) give her agency within the traditional framework.
