Claude Allouez (also recorded as Glaude Allouëz) was a French Jesuit missionary who in 1665 permanently established the Mission of the Holy Ghost at La Pointe (Cha-quam-egon Bay, present-day Wisconsin). He succeeded [[René Menard|Father René Menard]] and preceded [[Jacques Marquette|Father Jacques Marquette]] at this early outpost, which became an important site of French–[[dakota|Dakota]] contact.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: French (European)
- **Role**: Jesuit missionary, priest
- **Family**: —

## 在书中的角色

### La Pointe 传教站的开创者

Allouez appears in Gordon's footnotes to "The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]" as part of the historical chain of Jesuit missionaries at La Pointe:

> Father Glaude Allouëz permanently established the mission in 1665. (018-103)

The Mission of the Holy Ghost at La Pointe was founded by the Jesuits around 1660, with [[rene-menard|Father René Menard]] as the first priest. After Menard was lost in the wilderness, Allouez arrived and made the mission a permanent establishment.

### Marquette 的前任

Gordon notes the transition between missionaries:

> The famous Father Marquette, who took Allouëz's place, Sept. 13. 1669, (018-104)

This handover marked a significant moment — Marquette would go on to become one of the most celebrated explorers of the [[mississippi-river|Mississippi River]], and his letters from La Pointe (written just after replacing Allouez) contain some of the earliest European descriptions of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people.

## 历史/文化背景

Claude Allouez (1622–1689) was among the most active Jesuit missionaries in New France. After establishing the La Pointe mission in 1665, he spent years traveling among the tribes of the western [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s. The [[dakota|Dakotas]] knew the Jesuit priests as "Black Robes" (013-048), a term that appears throughout Gordon's poetry in reference to these early European visitors.

Allouez's letters and mission reports (included in the multi-volume *Jesuit Relations*) are significant primary sources for ethnohistorical study of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s tribes in the seventeenth century. His description of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people — recorded through his successor Marquette's letters — provides a snapshot of the [[dakota|Dakota]] at the moment of early European contact.
