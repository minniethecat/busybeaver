# Shingebís

Shingebís is the diver — the only water-fowl that remains about [[Lake Superior]] all winter (018-187 fn). In [[section-15|The Legend of the Falls]], while all other birds seek the South in winter, Shingebís alone stays behind and defies the Winter-maker (014-074).

## The Legend

When [[Ka-be-bón-ík-ka]], the mighty Winter-maker, comes from his wigwam called Kewáydin among the ice-bergs, all creatures flee south — all but Shingebís, the diver. The hell-diver sits and laughs at the Winter-maker, refusing to yield to the cold (014-075–076).

The Shingebís legend is noted in Schoolcraft's *Hiawatha Legends* (p. 113) and also appears in Longfellow's *The Song of Hiawatha*, where Shingebis defies Kabibonokka, the North Wind.

## In Literature

The Shingebís legend appears in both Henry Rowe Schoolcraft's ethnographic collections and [[henry-longfellow|Henry Wadsworth Longfellow]]'s literary adaptation. Schoolcraft recorded the tale in his *Hiawatha Legends* (p. 113), noting that Shingebis is the only water-fowl that remains about [[Lake Superior]] all winter (018-187–188).

Longfellow incorporated the Shingebis story into *The Song of Hiawatha*, where the hell-diver defies Kabibonokka, the North Wind — the Ojibway counterpart to the Dakota [[wa-zi-ya|Wa-zí-ya]]. This literary transmission from Dakota oral tradition through Schoolcraft's ethnography to Longfellow's epic poem follows the same path as many legends in Gordon's collection.
