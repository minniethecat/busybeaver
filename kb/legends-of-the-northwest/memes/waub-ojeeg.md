# Waub-Ojeeg

A famous [[ojibway|Ojibway]] warrior and chief, father of the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] ([[sea-gull|Waub-omeé-meé]]) in Gordon's "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XIII–XV). Waub-Ojeeg is invoked repeatedly as the standard of martial excellence — his name a byword for warrior pride and prowess.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]])
- **Role**: Warrior, chief, father
- **Family**: Daughter: the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]], also called [[sea-gull|Waub-omeé-meé]] (013-084); son-in-law: [[panther|Panther]] (013-093)

## 在书中的角色

### 相关传奇

Waub-Ojeeg appears in "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]" (Chapters XIII–XV). He is introduced as the father of the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]], and his name — meaning "White Fisher" — carries the weight of his warrior reputation. Throughout the legend, Waub-Ojeeg serves as the benchmark against which other warriors are measured.

### 关键事件

Waub-Ojeeg is first named when the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] is introduced: "Son of Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior, Famous Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior" (013-084, 013-085). The double invocation establishes his renown — he is not merely a warrior but a famous one, his name known across nations.

[[panther|Panther]]'s pride is measured by comparison: "Proud as Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior" (013-186). To be compared to Waub-Ojeeg is the highest praise a [[dakota|Dakota]] warrior can receive. The refrain returns when [[panther|Panther]]'s martial qualities are extolled: "Son of Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior" (013-212), associating the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]'s lineage with power.

The final invocation comes as the legend's action intensifies: "Son of Waub-Ojeeg, the warrior!" (014-003), the exclamation mark underscoring the awe his name commands. Though Waub-Ojeeg never appears directly in the narrative action, his presence is felt through the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]'s dignified endurance — she is, after all, the daughter of the most famous warrior.

## 历史/文化背景

The name Waub-Ojeeg (White Fisher) appears in historical accounts of [[ojibway|Ojibway]] leadership. [[henry-schoolcraft|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]] recorded [[tradition|traditions]] of a noted [[ojibway|Ojibway]] chief named Waub-Ojeeg who lived at La Pointe on [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]] in the 18th century. Gordon draws on this historical figure to lend weight to the legendary narrative, connecting the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]'s lineage to documented [[ojibway|Ojibway]] leadership. In the legend, Waub-Ojeeg represents the ideal of [[ojibway|Ojibway]] warrior virtue — courage, fame, and the pride of honorable lineage — against which the domestic drama of his daughter's marriage unfolds.
