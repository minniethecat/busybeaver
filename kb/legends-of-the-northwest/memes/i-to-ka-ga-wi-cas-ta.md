## 名称与身份

I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta (pronounced roughly "ee-TOH-kah-gah wee-CHAH-sh-tah") translates literally as "the **South Man**" in the Dakota language. He is the spirit or god of the South, embodying warmth, spring, and the renewal of life. His domain is the warm southern region and the Summer-Islands, where he retreats when overcome by his adversary. (015-207)

## 永恒之战：达科他人的季节神话

The central myth of I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta is his eternal war with **Wa-zí-ya**, the god of the North or Winter. The two spirits are "inveterate enemies, and always on the war-path against each other." (015-208) Their cyclical battle explains the changing of the seasons:

In **winter**, Wa-zí-ya advances southward and drives I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta before him to the Summer-Islands. In **spring**, the god of the South renews his youth and strength in the Happy Hunting Grounds, then returns to drive Wa-zí-ya back to his icy wigwam in the frozen North. (015-210)

This seasonal narrative is briefly referenced in Gordon's verse during a description of Indian Summer:

> When the god of the South as he flies
> from Wazíya, the god of the Winter,
> For a time turns his beautiful eyes,
> and backward looks over his shoulder.
>
> (010-228)

The image captures the fleeting warmth of autumn — the South Wind pausing in his retreat to cast one last warm glance upon the land before winter's conquest is complete.

## 巨石的由来

Some Dakotas explain the numerous granite boulders scattered across the prairies of Minnesota and Dakota as physical evidence of the cosmic war. These stones, they say, were **hurled in battle by Wa-zí-ya** from his northern home at I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta — frozen missiles thrown across the sky in the endless conflict between winter and summer. (015-215)

## 在达科他信仰体系中的位置

I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta is part of a broader Dakota cosmological dualism. Just as the sun and moon are twin brothers (Anpé-tu-wee and Han-yé-tu-wee), and the Thunder-bird wars eternally with Unktéhee, the North and South spirits embody the fundamental opposition that structures the Dakota universe. (015-207)(015-219)

Unlike Wa-zí-ya, who is described in more fearsome terms — dwelling in a great teepee of ice and snow, blowing cold blasts from his mouth and nostrils — I-tó-ka-ga Wi-câs-ta is associated with renewal, beauty, and the return of life. He is a beneficent force whose annual victory brings the growing season back to the Dakota people. (015-207)
