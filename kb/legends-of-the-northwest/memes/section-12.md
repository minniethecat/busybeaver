[011-001] \[a\] The Robin\--the name of Winona\'s Mother.

[011-002] \"My father,\" she said, and her voice

[011-003] was filial and full of compassion,

[011-004] \"Would the heart of Ta-té-psin rejoice

[011-005] at the death of Winona, his daughter?

[011-006] The crafty Tamdóka I hate.

[011-007] Must I die in his teepee of sorrow?

[011-008] For I love the White Chief,

[011-009] and I wait his return to the land of [[dakota|Dakotas]].

[011-010] When the cold winds of winter return,

[011-011] and toss the white robes of the prairies,

[011-012] The fire of the White Chief will burn,

[011-013] in his lodge, at the Meeting-of-Waters.

[011-014] Winona\'s heart followed his feet

[011-015] far away to the land of the morning,

[011-016] And she hears in her slumber

[011-017] his sweet, kindly voice call the name of thy daughter.

[011-018] My father, abide, I entreat,

[011-019] the return of the brave to Kathága.

[011-020] The wild-rice is gathered,

[011-021] the meat of the bison is stored in the teepee;

[011-022] Till the Coon-Moon \[71\] enough and to spare;

[011-023] and if then the white warrior return not,

[011-024] Winona will follow the bear, and the coon,

[011-025] to their dens in the forest.

[011-026] She is strong; she can handle the spear;

[011-027] she can bend the stout bow of the hunter;

[011-028] And swift on the trail of the deer

[011-029] will she run o\'er the snow on her snow-shoes.

[011-030] Let the step-mother sit in the tee,

[011-031] and kindle the fire for my father;

[011-032] And the cold, cruel winter shall be

[011-033] a feast-time instead of a famine.\"

[011-034] \"The White Chief will never return,\"

[011-035] half angrily muttered Ta-té-psin;

[011-036] \"His camp-fire will nevermore burn

[011-037] in the land of the warriors he slaughtered.

[011-038] I grieve, for my daughter has said

[011-039] that she loves the false friend of her kindred;

[011-040] For the hands of the White Chief are red

[011-041] with the blood of the trustful Dakotas.\"

[011-042] Then warmly Winona replied,

[011-043] \"Tamdóka himself is the traitor,

[011-044] And the white-hearted stranger had died

[011-045] by his treacherous hand in the forest,

[011-046] But thy daughter\'s voice bade him beware

[011-047] of the sly death that followed his footsteps.

[011-048] The words of Tamdóka are fair,

[011-049] but his heart is the den of the serpents.

[011-050] When the braves told their tale,

[011-051] like a bird sang the heart of Winona rejoicing,

[011-052] But gladlier still had she heard

[011-053] of the death of the crafty Tamdóka.

[011-054] The Chief will return, he is bold,

[011-055] and he carries the fire of Wakínyan;

[011-056] To our people the truth will be told,

[011-057] and Tamdóka will hide like a coward.\"

[011-058] His thin locks the aged brave shook;

[011-059] to himself half inaudibly muttered;

[011-060] To Winona no answer he spoke

[011-061] \--only moaned he \"Micunksee! Micunksee! \[a\]

[011-062] In my old age forsaken and blind!

[011-063] Yun! He he! Micúnksee! Micúnksee!\" \[b\]

[011-064] And Wicháka, the pitying dog, whined,

[011-065] as he looked on the face of his master.

[011-066] \[a\] My Daughter! My Daughter!

[011-067] \[b\] Alas! O My Daughter,\--My Daughter!

[011-068] Wazíya came down from the North

[011-069] \--from his land of perpetual winter.

[011-070] From his frost-covered beard issued forth

[011-071] the sharp-biting, shrill-whistling North-wind;

[011-072] At the touch of his breath the wide earth turned to stone,

[011-073] and the lakes and the rivers;

[011-074] From his nostrils the white vapors rose,

[011-075] and they covered the sky like a blanket.

[011-076] Like the down of Magá \[a\] fell the snows,

[011-077] tossed and whirled into heaps by the North-wind.

[011-078] Then the blinding storms roared on the plains,

[011-079] like the simoons on sandy Sahara;

[011-080] From the fangs of the fierce hurricanes

[011-081] fled the elk and the deer and the bison.

[011-082] Ever colder and colder it grew,

[011-083] till the frozen earth cracked and split open;

[011-084] And harder and harder it blew,

[011-085] till the prairies were bare as the boulders.

[011-086] To the southward the buffaloes fled,

[011-087] and the white rabbits hid in their burrows;

[011-088] On the bare sacred mounds of the dead

[011-089] howled the gaunt, hungry wolves in the night-time.

[011-090] The strong hunters crouched in their tees;

[011-091] by the lodge-fires the little ones shivered;

[011-092] And the Magic Men \[b\] danced to appease,

[011-093] in their teepee, the wrath of Wazíya;

[011-094] But famine and fatal disease,

[011-095] like phantoms, crept into the village.

[011-096] The Hard Moon \[c\] was past, but the moon

[011-097] when the coons make their trails in the forest \[d\]

[011-098] Grew colder and colder. The coon or the bear,

[011-099] ventured not from his cover;

[011-100] For the cold, cruel Arctic Simoon swept the earth

[011-101] like the breath of a furnace.

[011-102] In the tee of Ta-té-psin the store of wild-rice

[011-103] and dried meat was exhausted;

[011-104] And Famine crept in at the door,

[011-105] and sat crouching and gaunt by the lodge-fire.

[011-106] But now with the saddle of deer,

[011-107] and the gifts, came the crafty Tamdóka;

[011-108] And he said, \"Lo I bring you good cheer,

[011-109] for I love the blind Chief and his daughter.

[011-110] Take the gifts of Tamdóka,

[011-111] for dear to his heart is the dark-eyed Winona.\"

[011-112] The aged chief opened his ears;

[011-113] in his heart he already consented;

[011-114] But the moans of his child and her tears

[011-115] touched the age-softened heart of the father,

[011-116] And he said, \"I am burdened with years,

[011-117] \--I am bent by the snows of my winters;

[011-118] Ta-té-psin will die in his tee;

[011-119] let him pass to the Land of the Spirits;

[011-120] But Winona is young; she is free,

[011-121] and her own heart shall choose her a husband.\"

[011-122] The dark warrior strode from the tee;

[011-123] low-muttering and grim he departed.

[011-124] \"Let him die in his lodge,\" muttered he,

[011-125] \"but Winona shall kindle my lodge-fire.\"

[011-126] \[a\] Wild goose.

[011-127] \[b\] Medicine men.

[011-128] \[c\] January.

[011-129] \[d\] February.

[011-130] Then forth went Winona. The bow of Ta-té-psin

[011-131] she took and his arrows,

[011-132] And afar o\'er the deep, drifted snow,

[011-133] through the forest, she sped on her snow-shoes.

[011-134] Over meadow and ice-covered mere,

[011-135] through the thickets of red oak and hazel,

[011-136] She followed the tracks of the deer,

[011-137] but like phantoms they fled from her vision.

[011-138] From sunrise till sunset she sped;

[011-139] half-famished she camped in the thicket;

[011-140] In the cold snow she made her lone bed;

[011-141] on the buds of the birch \[a\] made her supper.

[011-142] To the dim moon the gray owl preferred,

[011-143] from the tree top, his shrill lamentation,

[011-144] And around her at midnight she heard

[011-145] the dread famine-cries of the gray wolves.

[011-146] In the gloam of the morning again

[011-147] on the trail of the red-deer she followed\--

[011-148] All day long through the thickets in vain,

[011-149] for the gray wolves were chasing the roebucks;

[011-150] And the cold, hungry winds from the plain

[011-151] chased the wolves and the deer and Winona.

[011-152] \[a\] The pheasant feeds on birch-buds in winter. Indians eat them when very

[011-153] hungry.

[011-154] In the twilight of sundown she sat,

[011-155] in the forest, all weak and despairing;

[011-156] Ta-té-psin\'s bow lay at her feet,

[011-157] and his otter skin quiver of arrows.

[011-158] \"He promised,\--he promised,\" she said

[011-159] \--half-dreamily uttered and mournful,\--

[011-160] \"And why comes he not? Is he dead?

[011-161] Was he slain by the crafty Tamdóka?

[011-162] Must Winona, alas, make her choice

[011-163] \--make her choice between death and Tamdóka?

[011-164] She will die but her soul will rejoice

[011-165] in the far Summer-land of the [[spirit|spirits]].

[011-166] Hark! I hear his low, musical voice!

[011-167] He is coming! My White Chief is coming!

[011-168] Ah, no; I am half in a dream!

[011-169] \--\'twas the mem\'ry of days long departed;

[011-170] But the birds of the green Summer

[011-171] seem to be singing above in the branches.\"

[011-172] Then forth from her bosom she drew

[011-173] the crucified Jesus in silver.

[011-174] In her dark hair the cold north wind blew,

[011-175] as meekly she bent o\'er the image.

[011-176] \"O Christ of the White man,\" she prayed,

[011-177] \"lead the feet of my brave to Kathága;

[011-178] Send a good spirit down to my aid,

[011-179] or the friend of the White Chief will perish.\"

[011-180] Then a smile on her wan features played,

[011-181] and she lifted her pale face and chanted:

[011-182] \"E-ye-he-ktá! E-ye-he-ktá!

[011-183] Hé-kta-cè; é-ye-ce-quón.

[011-184] Mí-Wamdee-ská, he-he-ktá;

[011-185] He-kta-cè; é-ye-ce-quón,

[011-186] Mí-Wamdee-ská.\"

[011-187] \[TRANSLATION.\]

[011-188] He will come; he will come;

[011-189] He will come, for he promised.

[011-190] My White Eagle, he will come;

[011-191] He will come, for he promised,\--

[011-192] My White Eagle.

[011-193] Thus sadly she chanted, and lo

[011-194] \--allured by her sorrowful accents\--

[011-195] From the dark covert crept a red doe

[011-196] and wondrously gazed on Winona.

[011-197] Then swift caught the huntress her bow;

[011-198] from her trembling hand hummed the keen arrow.

[011-199] Up-leaped the red gazer and fled,

[011-200] but the white snow was sprinkled with scarlet,

[011-201] And she fell in the oak thicket dead.

[011-202] On the trail ran the eager Winona.

[011-203] Half-famished the raw flesh she ate.

[011-204] To the hungry maid sweet was her supper.

[011-205] Then swift through the night ran her feet,

[011-206] and she trailed the sleek red-deer behind her.

[011-207] And the guide of her steps was a star

[011-208] \--the cold-glinting star of Wazíya\--\[a\]

[011-209] Over meadow and hilltop afar,

[011-210] on the way to the lodge of her father.

[011-211] But hark! on the keen frosty air

[011-212] wind the shrill hunger-howls of the gray wolves!

[011-213] And nearer,\--still nearer!

[011-214] \--the blood of the doe have they scented and follow;

[011-215] Through the thicket, the meadow,

[011-216] the wood, dash the pack on the trail of Winona.

[011-217] Swift she speeds with her burden,

[011-218] but swift on her track fly the minions of famine;

[011-219] Now they yell on the view from the drift,

[011-220] in the reeds at the marge of the meadow;

[011-221] Red gleam their wild, ravenous eyes;

[011-222] for they see on the hill-side their supper;

[011-223] The dark forest echoes their cries;

[011-224] but her heart is the heart of a warrior.

[011-225] From its sheath snatched Winona her knife,

[011-226] and a leg from the red doe she severed;

[011-227] With the carcass she ran for her life,

[011-228] \--to a low-branching oak ran the maiden;

[011-229] Round the deer\'s neck her head-strap \[b\] was tied;

[011-230] swiftly she sprang to the arms of the oak-tree;

[011-231] Quick her burden she drew to her side,

[011-232] and higher she clomb on the branches,

[011-233] While the maddened wolves battled and bled,

[011-234] dealing death o\'er the leg to each other;

[011-235] Their keen fangs devouring the dead,

[011-236] \--yea, devouring the flesh of the living,

[011-237] They raved and they gnashed and they growled,

[011-238] like the fiends in the regions infernal;

[011-239] The wide night re-echoing howled,

[011-240] and the hoarse North wind laughed o\'er the slaughter.

[011-241] But their ravenous maws unappeased

[011-242] by the blood and the flesh of their fellows,

[011-243] To the cold wind their muzzles they raised,

[011-244] and the trail to the oak-tree they followed.

[011-245] Round and round it they howled for the prey,

[011-246] madly leaping and snarling and snapping;

[011-247] But the brave maiden\'s keen arrows slay,

[011-248] till the dead number more than the living.

[011-249] All the long, dreary night-time, at bay,

[011-250] in the oak sat the shivering Winona;

[011-251] But the sun gleamed at last, and away

[011-252] skulked the gray cowards \[c\] down through the forest.

[011-253] Then down dropped the doe and the maid.

[011-254] Ere the sun reached the midst of his journey,

[011-255] Her red, welcome burden she laid

[011-256] at the feet of her famishing father.

[011-257] \[a\] Wazíya\'s Star is the North Star.

[011-258] \[b\] A strap used in carrying burdens.

[011-259] \[c\] Wolves sometimes attack people at night but rarely if ever in the day

[011-260] time. If they have followed a hunter all night, or \"treed\" him they will

[011-261] skulk away as soon as the sun rises.

[011-262] Wazíya\'s wild wrath was appeased,

[011-263] and homeward he turned to his teepee, \[3\]

[011-264] O\'er the plains and the forest-land breezed,

[011-265] from the Islands of Summer, the South wind.

[011-266] From their dens came the coon and the bear;

[011-267] o\'er the snow through the woodlands they wandered;

[011-268] On her snow shoes with stout bow and spear

[011-269] on their trails ran the huntress Winona.

[011-270] The coon to his den in the tree,

[011-271] and the bear to his burrow she followed;

[011-272] A brave, skillful hunter was she,

[011-273] and Ta-té-psin\'s lodge laughed with abundance.

[011-274] The long winter wanes. On the wings

[011-275] of the spring come the geese and the mallards;

[011-276] On the bare oak the red-robin sings,

[011-277] and the crocuses peep on the prairies,

[011-278] And the bobolink pipes, but he brings,

[011-279] of the blue-eyed, brave White Chief, no tidings.

[011-280] With the waning of winter, alas,

[011-281] waned the life of the aged Tatépsin;

[011-282] Ere the blue pansies peeped from the grass,

[011-283] to the Land of the Spirits he journeyed;

[011-284] Like a babe in its slumber he passed,

[011-285] or the snow from the hill tops in April;

[011-286] And the dark-eyed Winona, at last,

[011-287] stood alone by the graves of her kindred.

[011-288] When their myriad mouths opened the trees

[011-289] to the sweet dew of heaven and the rain drops,

[011-290] And the April showers fell on the leas,

[011-291] on his mound fell the tears of Winona.

[011-292] Round her drooping form gathered the years

[011-293] and the spirits unseen of her kindred,

[011-294] As low, in the midst of her tears,

[011-295] at the grave of her father she chanted:
