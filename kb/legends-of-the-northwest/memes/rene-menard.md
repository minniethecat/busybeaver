Father René Menard was a Jesuit missionary and the first Catholic priest in the [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]] region, appearing in Gordon's "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" as the aged "Black Robe" who ministers to [[winona|Winona]] and the [[dakota|Dakota]] people. He was based at the Mission of Sainte Marie, founded about 1660.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: French / European
- **Role**: Jesuit missionary, first priest in the [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]] region
- **Family**: Jesuit order (no blood relations in the narrative)
- **Mission**: Sainte Marie, founded circa 1660

## 在书中的角色

### Winona 传奇

In the [[winona|Winona]] tale, Father Menard serves as a [[soul|spirit]]ual counselor and witness to the unfolding tragedy. Gordon introduces him with reverence: "Father Renè Menard—it was he, long lost to his Jesuit brothers" (006-149–006-150), setting him as a solitary figure of faith in the wilderness.

Menard baptizes [[winona|Winona]] and speaks to her of the Christian faith. The line "For Menard was the first in the land, proclaiming, like John in the desert" (006-217–006-218) casts him as a prophetic figure, bringing Christianity to the [[dakota|Dakota]] country for the first time.

### 最后的岁月

The narrative acknowledges Menard's physical frailty: "Poor Father Menard—he was ill; in his breast burned the fire of the fever" (007-001–007-002). The aged missionary addresses [[winona|Winona]] directly in the poem: "said the aged Menard to [[winona|Winona]]" (006-258), and his eventual fate is mourned: "Of the father—poor Renè Menard" (007-129).

## 历史/文化背景

Father René Menard was a real historical figure. According to Gordon's footnotes (006-181), detailed accounts of Menard's mission and disappearance in the wilderness can be found in [[edward-neill|Neill]]'s "History of [[minnesota|Minnesota]]" (pp. 104–107). The Mission of Sainte Marie was established by the Jesuits around 1660, and Menard was its first priest (018-101).

After Menard was lost in the wilderness, the mission was permanently established in 1665 by Father Claude Allouëz, who was succeeded in 1669 by the famous Father [[jacques-marquette|Jacques Marquette]].
