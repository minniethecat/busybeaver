[018-001] \[Footnote 78: Wa\'tanka\--contraction of Wa-kan Tanka\--[[wakan|Great Spirit]]. The

[018-002] [[dakota|Dakotas]] had no Wakan Tanka\--or Wakan-péta\--fire spirit\--till whitemen

[018-003] imported them. There being no name for the Supreme Being in the Dakota

[018-004] tongue (except Tá-ku Wakan\--See note 73)\--and all their gods and

[018-005] [[spirit|spirits]] being Wakan\--the missionaries named God in Dakota\--\"_Wakan

[018-006] Tanka_\"\--which means _Big Spirit_, or _The Big Mysterious_.\]

[018-007] \[Footnote 79: The Dakotas called Lake Calhoun\--Mdé-mdó-za\--Loon Lake.

[018-008] They also called it\--_Re-ya-ta-mde_\--the lake back from the river.

[018-009] They called Lake Harriet\--Mdé-únma\--the other lake\--or (perhaps) Mdé

[018-010] úma---Hazel-nut Lake. The lake nearest Calhoun on the north\--Lake of the

[018-011] Isles\--they called Wí-ta Mdé\--Island-Lake. Lake Minnetonka they called

[018-012] Me-me-a-tán-ka\--_Broad Water_.\]

[018-013] \[Footnote 80: The animal called by the French _voyageurs_ the _cabri_

[018-014] (the kid) is found only on the prairies. It is of the goat kind, smaller

[018-015] than a deer, and so swift that neither horse nor dog can overtake

[018-016] it. (Snelling\'s) \"Tales of the Northwest,\" p. 286. note 15. It is the

[018-017] gazelle, or prairie antelope, called by the Dakotas Tato-ka-dan\--little

[018-018] antelope. It is the _Pish-tah-te-koosh_ of the [[algonkin|Algonkin]] tribes,

[018-019] \"reckoned the fleetest animal in the prairie country about the

[018-020] Assinneboin.\" Captivity and Adventures of John Tanner, p. 301.\]

[018-021] \[Footnote 81: The Wicâstâpi Wakânpi (literally, _men supernatural_) are

[018-022] the \"Medicine-men\" or Magicians of the Dakotas. They call themselves the

[018-023] sons, or disciples of Unktéhee. In their rites, ceremonies, tricks and

[018-024] pretensions they closely resemble the Dactyli, Idæ and Curetes of the

[018-025] ancient Greeks and Romans, the Magi of the Persians, and the Druids of

[018-026] Britain. Their pretended intercourse with spirits, their powers of magic

[018-027] and divination, and their rites are substantially the same, and point

[018-028] unmistakably to a common origin. The Dakota \"Medicine-Man\" can do the

[018-029] \"rope-trick\" of the Hindoo magician to perfection. The teepee used for

[018-030] the _Wakan Wacipee_\--or Sacred [[dance|Dance]]\--is called the _[[wakan-teepee-site|Wakan Teepee]]_\--the

[018-031] Sacred Teepee. Carver\'s Cave at St. Paul was also called Wakan Teepee,

[018-032] because the Medicine-men or magicians often held their dances and

[018-033] feasts in it. For a full account of the rites, etc., see Riggs\' \"Tâhkoo

[018-034] Wahkan\", Chapter VI. The _Ta-sha-ke_\--literally, \"Deer-hoofs\"\--is a

[018-035] rattle made by hanging the hard segments of deer-hoofs to a wooden rod a

[018-036] foot long\--about an inch in diameter at the handle end, and tapering to

[018-037] a point at the other. The clashing of these horny bits makes a sharp,

[018-038] shrill sound something like distant sleigh-bells. In their incantations

[018-039] over the sick they sometimes use the gourd-shell rattle.\]

[018-040] The Chân-che-ga\--is a drum or \"Wooden Kettle.\" The hoop of the drum is

[018-041] from a foot to eighteen inches in diameter, and from three to ten inches

[018-042] deep. The skin covering is stretched over one end making a drum with one

[018-043] end only. The magical drum sticks are ornamented with down, and heads of

[018-044] birds or animals are carved on them. This makes them Wakan.\]

[018-045] The flute called _Cho-tanka_ (big pith) is of two varieties\--one made of

[018-046] sumac, the pith of which is punched out, etc. The second variety is made

[018-047] of the long bone of the wing or thigh of the swan or crane. They call

[018-048] the first the _bubbling chotanka_ from the tremulous note it gives when

[018-049] blown with all the holes stopped. Riggs\' Tahkoo Wahkan, p. 476, et seq.\]

[018-050] E-né-pee\--vapor bath is used as a purification preparatory to the sacred

[018-051] feasts. The vapor bath is taken in this way: \"A number of poles the size

[018-052] of hoop-poles or less are taken, and their larger ends being set in

[018-053] the ground in a circle, the flexible tops are bent over and tied in the

[018-054] centre. This frame work is then covered with robes and blankets, a small

[018-055] hole being left on one side for an entrance. Before the door a fire is

[018-056] built, and round stones about the size of a man\'s head are heated in it.

[018-057] When hot, they are rolled within, and the door being closed, steam is

[018-058] made by pouring water on them. The devotee, stripped to the skin, sits

[018-059] within this steam-tight dome, sweating profusely at every pore, until he

[018-060] is nearly suffocated. Sometimes a number engage in it together and unite

[018-061] their prayers and [[song|songs]].\" \"Tâhkoo Wakan,\" p. 83. Father Hennepin was

[018-062] subjected to the vapour-bath at Mille Lacs by Chief Aqui-pa-que-tin, two

[018-063] hundred years ago. After describing the method Hennepin says: \"When he

[018-064] had made me sweat thus three times in a week, I felt as strong as ever.\"

[018-065] Shea\'s Hennepin, p. 228. For a very full and accurate account of the

[018-066] Medicine men of the Dakotas, and their rites etc., see Chap. II, [[edward-neill|Neill]]\'s

[018-067] Hist. Minnesota.\]

[018-068] \[Footnote 82: The sacred _O-zu-ha_\--or Medicine-sack must be made of

[018-069] the skin of the otter, the coon, the weasel, the squirrel, the loon,

[018-070] a certain kind of fish or the skins of serpents. It must contain four

[018-071] kinds of medicine (or magic) representing birds, beasts, herbs and

[018-072] trees, viz: The down of the female swan colored red, the roots of

[018-073] certain grasses, bark from the roots of cedar trees, and hair of the

[018-074] buffalo. \"From this combination proceeds a Wakân influence so powerful

[018-075] that no human being unassisted can resist it.\" Wonderful indeed must be

[018-076] the magic power of these Dakota Druids to lead such a man aa the Rev.

[018-077] S. R. Riggs to say of them: \"By great shrewdness, untiring industry,

[018-078] and more or less of _actual demoniacal possession_, they convince great

[018-079] numbers of their fellows, and in the process are convinced _themselves_,

[018-080] of their sacred character and office.\" Tahkoo Wakân, pp. 88-9: \]

[018-081] \[Footnote 83: Gâh-ma-na-tek-wâhk\--_the river of many falls_\--is the

[018-082] [[ojibway|Ojibway]] name of the river commonly called Kaministiguia, near the mouth

[018-083] of which is situate Fort William, on the site of DuLuth\'s old fort. The

[018-084] view on Thunder-Bay is one of the grandest in America. Thunder-Cap,

[018-085] with its sleeping stone-giant, looms up into the heavens. Here

[018-086] _Ka-be-bon-ikka_\--the Ojibway\'s god of storms, flaps his huge wings and

[018-087] makes the Thunder. From this mountain he sends forth the rain, the snow,

[018-088] the hail, the lightning and the tempest. A vast giant, turned to stone

[018-089] by his magic, lies asleep at his feet. The island called by the Ojibways

[018-090] the _Mak-i-nak_ (the turtle) from its tortoise-like shape, lifts

[018-091] its huge form in the distance. Some \"down-east\" Yankee, called it

[018-092] \"Pie-Island,\" from its (to his hungry imagination) fancied resemblance

[018-093] to a pumpkin pie, and the name, like all bad names, _sticks_. McKay\'s

[018-094] Mountain on the main-land, a perpendicular rock more than a thousand

[018-095] feet high, up-heaved by the throes of some vast volcano, and numerous

[018-096] other bold and precipitous head lands, and rock-built islands, around

[018-097] which roll the sapphire-blue waters of the fathomless bay, present some

[018-098] of the most magnificent views to be found on either continent.\]

[018-099] \[Footnote 84: The Mission of the Holy Ghost\--at La Pointe on the isle

[018-100] Waug-a-bá-me\--(winding view) in the beautiful bay of Cha-quam-egon---was

[018-101] founded by the Jesuits about the year 1660, and Father Renè Menard was

[018-102] the first priest at this point. After he was lost in the wilderness,

[018-103] Father Glaude Allouëz permanently established ihe mission in 1665.

[018-104] The famous Father Marquette, who took Allouëz\'s place, Sept. 13. 1669,

[018-105] writing to his Superior, thus describes the Dakotas: \"The Nadouessi are

[018-106] the Iroquois of this country, beyond La Pointe, _but less faithless, and

[018-107] never attack till attacked._ Their language is entirely different

[018-108] from the Huron and Algonquin. They have many villages, but are widely

[018-109] scattered. They have very extraordinary [[tradition|customs]]. They principally use

[018-110] the calumet. They do not speak at great feasts, and when a stranger

[018-111] arrives give him to eat of a wooden fork, as we would a child. All the

[018-112] lake tribes make war on them, but with small success. They have false

[018-113] oats, (wild rice) use little canoes, _and keep their word strictly_.\"

[018-114] Neill\'s Hist. Minn., p. 111.\]

[018-115] \[Footnote 85: Michâbo\--the Good, Great Spirit of the Algonkins. In

[018-116] Autumn, in the moon of the falling leaf, ere he composes himself to his

[018-117] winter\'s sleep, he fills his great pipe and takes a god-like smoke. The

[018-118] balmy clouds from his pipe float over the hills and woodland, filling

[018-119] the air with the haze of \"Indian Summer.\" Brinton\'s Myths of the New

[018-120] World, p. 163.\]

[018-121] \[Footnote 86: Pronounced _Kah-thah-gah_\--literally, _the place of waves

[018-122] and foam_. This was the principal village of the Isantee band of Dakotas

[018-123] two hundred years ago, and was located at the [[falls-of-st-anthony|Falls of St. Anthony]],

[018-124] which the Dakotas called the _Ha-ha_\--pronounced _Rhah-rhah_\--the _loud,

[018-125] laughing waters_. The Dakotas believed that the Falls were in the centre

[018-126] of the earth. Here dwelt the Great Unktéhee, the creator of the earth

[018-127] and man; and from this place a path led to the Spirit-land. DuLuth

[018-128] undoubtedly visited Kathága in the year 1679. In his \"Memoir\" (Archives

[018-129] of the Ministry of the Marine) addressed to Seignelay, 1685, he says:

[018-130] \"On the 2nd of July, 1679, I had the honor to plant his Majesty\'s arms

[018-131] in the great village of the Nadouecioux called Izatys, where never had

[018-132] a Frenchman been, etc.\" _Izatys_ is here used not as the name of the

[018-133] village, but as the name of the band\--the Isantees. _Nadouecioux_ was

[018-134] a name given the Dakotas generally by the early [[voyageurs|French traders]] and the

[018-135] Ojibways. See Shea\'s Hennepin\'s Description of Louisiana pp. 203: and

[018-136] 375\. The villages of the Dakotas were not permanent towns. They were

[018-137] hardly more than camping grounds, occupied at intervals and for longer

[018-138] or shorter periods, as suited the convenience of the hunters: yet there

[018-139] were certain places, like Mille Lacs, the Falls of St. Anthony, Kapóza

[018-140] (near St. Paul), Remnica, (where the city of Red Wing now stands),

[018-141] and Keúxa (or Keóza) on the site of the city of Winona, so frequently

[018-142] occupied by several of the bands as to be considered their chief

[018-143] villages respectively.\]
