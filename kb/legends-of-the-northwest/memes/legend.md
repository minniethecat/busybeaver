# Legend

The legend — as a literary form and cultural practice — is central to H. L. Gordon's *Legends of the Northwest*. The book's title announces its genre: these are not original poems but English verse renderings of indigenous oral legends.

## The Dakota Legend Tradition

[[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] legends served as vehicles for preserving history, teaching moral lessons, and explaining natural phenomena. They were transmitted orally across generations, with skilled storytellers maintaining the narrative [[tradition|traditions]].

Gordon collected these legends during his years of study among the [[dakota|Dakota]] people (P01-007). His preface explains that his work aims to represent "[[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|customs]], life, [[tradition|traditions]], and [[superstition|superstitions]]" faithfully through the medium of English poetry (P01-174).

## In the Book

*Legends of the Northwest* contains four main legend cycles:

- *The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]]* — A Legend of the [[dakota|Dakotas]] (003-108)
- *[[winona|Winona]]* — A Legend of the [[dakota|Dakotas]]
- *The Legend of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]* — A Legend of the [[dakota|Dakotas]]
- *The [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]]* — The [[ojibway-nation|Ojibwa]] Legend of the [[pictured-rocks|Pictured Rocks]]

The *Prelude — The Mississippi* sets the legendary tone, invoking the [[spirit|spirits]] of the ancient past (001-020, 001-059).
