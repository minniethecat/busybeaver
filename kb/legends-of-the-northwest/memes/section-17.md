[016-001] \[Footnote 7: Hârps-te-nâh. The first-born _daughter_ of a [[dakota|Dakota]] is

[016-002] called Winona; the second, Hârpen; the third, Hârpstinâ; the fourth.

[016-003] Wâska; the fifth, Wehârka. The first born _son_ is called Chaskè; the

[016-004] second, Hârpam; the third, Hapéda; the fourth, Châtun; the fifth, Hârka.

[016-005] They retain these names till others are given them on account of some

[016-006] action, peculiarity, etc. The females often retain their child-names

[016-007] through life.\]

[016-008] \[Footnote 8: Wah-pah-sâh was the hereditary name of a long and

[016-009] illustrious lineof Dakota Chiefs. Wabashaw is a corrupt pronounciation.

[016-010] The name is a contraction of \"Wâ-pa-hâ-sa,\" which is from \"Wâ-ha-pa,\"

[016-011] the standard or pole used in the Dakota [[dance|dances]], and upon which feathers

[016-012] of various colors are tied, and not from \"Wâ-pa\"\--leaf or leaves, as has

[016-013] been generally supposed. Therefore Wâpasa means the Standard\--and not

[016-014] the \"Leaf-Shaker,\" as many writers have it. The principal village of

[016-015] these hereditary Chiefs was Ke-úk-sa, or Ke-ó-sa,\--where now stands the

[016-016] fair city of Winona. Ke-úk-sa signifies\--The village of law-breakers;

[016-017] so-called because this band broke the law or custom of the Dakotas

[016-018] against marrying blood relatives of any degree. I get this information

[016-019] from Rev. [[stephen-riggs|Stephen R. Riggs]], author of the Dakota Grammar and Dictionary,

[016-020] \"_Takoo [[wakan|Wakan]]_,\" etc. Wapasa, grandfather of the last Chief of that

[016-021] name, and a contemporary of Cetan-Wa-kâ-wa-mâni, was a noted Chief,

[016-022] and a friend of the British in the war of the Revolution. [[edward-neill|Neill]]\'s Hist.

[016-023] Minn., pp. 225-9.\]

[016-024] \[Footnote 9: E-hó, E-tó\--Exclamations of surprise and delight.\]

[016-025] \[Footnote 10: Mah-gâh\--The wild-goose.\]

[016-026] \[Footnote 11: Teé-peé\--A lodge or wigwam, often contracted to \"tee.\"\]

[016-027] \[Footnote 12: Pronounced Mahr-peé-yah-doó-tah\--literally, Cloud Red.\]

[016-028] \[Footnote 13: Pronounced Wahnmdeé\--The War-Eagle. Each feather worn by a

[016-029] warrior represents an enemy slain or captured\--man, woman or child; but

[016-030] the Dakotas, before they became desperate under the cruel warfare of

[016-031] their enemies, generally spared the lives of their captives, and never

[016-032] killed women or infants, except in rare instances, under the _lex

[016-033] talionis_. Neill\'s Hist. Minn., p. 112.\]

[016-034] \[Footnote 14: Mah-tó\--The polar bear\--_ursus maritimus_. The Dakotas say

[016-035] that, in olden times, white bears were often found about Rainy Lake

[016-036] and the Lake of the Woods, in winter, and sometimes as far south as the

[016-037] mouth of the Minnesota. They say one was once killed at White Bear Lake

[016-038] (but a few miles from St. Paul and Minneapolis), and they therefore

[016-039] named the lake Medé Mató\--White Bear Lake.\]

[016-040] \[Footnote 15: The Hó-hé (Hó-hây) are the Assiniboins or

[016-041] \"Stone-roasters.\" Their home is the region of the Assiniboin river in

[016-042] British America. They speak the Dakota tongue, and originally were a

[016-043] band of that nation. [[tradition|Tradition]] says a Dakota \"Helen\" was the cause of

[016-044] the separation and a bloody feud that lasted for many years. The Hohés

[016-045] are called \"Stone roasters,\" because, until recently at least, they used

[016-046] \"Wa-ta-pe\" kettles and vessels made of birch bark in which they cooked

[016-047] their food. They boiled water in these vessels by heating stones and

[016-048] putting them in the water. The \"wa-ta-pe\" kettle is made of the fibrous

[016-049] roots of the white cedar, interlaced and tightly woven. When the vessel

[016-050] is soaked it becomes watertight. (Footnote Snelling\'s) Tales of the

[016-051] North west, p 21. Mackenzie\'s Travels.\]

[016-052] \[Footnote 16: Hey-ó-ka is one of the principal Dakota deities. He is a

[016-053] Giant, but can change himself into a buffalo, a bear, a fish or a bird.

[016-054] He is called the Anti-natural God or [[spirit|Spirit]]. In summer he shivers with

[016-055] cold, in winter he suffers from heat; he cries when he laughs and he

[016-056] laughs when he cries, &c. He is the reverse of nature in all things.

[016-057] Heyóka is universally feared and reverenced by the Dakotas, but so

[016-058] severe is the ordeal that the Heyóka Wacípee (the dance to Heyóka) is

[016-059] now rarely celebrated. It is said that the \"Medicine-men\" use a secret

[016-060] preparation which enables them to handle fire and dip their hands in

[016-061] boiling water without injury, and thereby gain great _eclat_ from the

[016-062] uninitiated. The chiefs and the leading warriors usually belong to the

[016-063] secret order of \"Medicine-men,\" or \"Sons of Unktéhee\"\--the Spirit of the

[016-064] Waters.\]

[016-065] \[Footnote 17: The Dakota name for the moon is Han-yé-tu-wee\--literally,

[016-066] Night-Sun. He is the twin brother of An-pé-tu-wee\--the Day Sun. See note

[016-067] 70.\]

[016-068] \[Footnote 18: The Dakotas believe that the stars are the spirits of

[016-069] their departed friends.\]

[016-070] \[Footnote 19: Tee\--Contracted from teepee, lodge or wigwam, and means

[016-071] the same.\]

[016-072] \[Footnote 20: For all their sacred feasts the Dakotas kindle a new

[016-073] fire called \"The Virgin Fire.\" This is done with flint and steel, or by

[016-074] rubbing together pieces of wood till friction produces fire. It must be

[016-075] done by a virgin, nor must any woman, except a virgin, ever touch the

[016-076] \"sacred armor\" of a Dakota warrior. White cedar is \"Wakân\"\--sacred. See

[016-077] note 50. Riggs\' \"Tahkoo Wakân,\" p. 84.\]

[016-078] \[Footnote 21: All Northern Indians consider the East a mysterious

[016-079] and sacred land whence comes the sun. The Dakota name for the East is

[016-080] Wee-yo-heé-yan-pa\--the sunrise. The Ojibways call it Waub-ó-nong\--the

[016-081] white land or land of light, and they have many myths, [[legend|legends]] and

[016-082] traditions relating thereto. Barbarous peoples of all times have

[016-083] regarded the East with superstitious reverence, simply because the sun

[016-084] rises in that quarter.\]

[016-085] \[Footnote 22: See Mrs. Eastman\'s Dacotah, pp. 225-8, describing the

[016-086] feast to Heyóka.\]

[016-087] \[Footnote 23: This stone from which the Dakotas have made their pipes

[016-088] for ages, is esteemed \"wakân\"\--sacred. They call it I-yân-ska, probably

[016-089] from \"íya,\" to speak, and \"ska,\" white, truthful, peaceful,\--hence,

[016-090] peace-pipe, herald of peace, pledge of truth, etc. In the cabinet at

[016-091] Albany, N.Y., there is a very ancient pipe of this material which the

[016-092] Iroquois obtained from the Dakotas. Charlevoix speaks of this pipe-stone

[016-093] in his History of New France. LeSueur refers to the Yanktons as the

[016-094] village of the Dakotas at the Red-Stone Quarry, See Neill\'s Hist. Minn.,

[016-095] p\. 514.\]

[016-096] \[Footnote 24: \"Ho\" is an exclamation of approval----yea, yes, bravo.\]

[016-097] \[Footnote 25: Buying is the honorable way of taking a wife among the

[016-098] Dakotas. The proposed husband usually gives a horse or its, value

[016-099] in other articles to the father or natural guardian of the woman

[016-100] selected\--sometimes against her will. See note 75.\]

[016-101] \[Footnote 26: The Dakotas believe that the _Aurora Borealis_ is an

[016-102] evil omen and the threatening of an evil spirit, (perhaps Wazíya, the

[016-103] Winter-god\--some say a witch, or a very ugly old woman). When the lights

[016-104] appear, danger threatens, and the warriors shoot at, and often slay, the

[016-105] evil spirit, but it rises from the dead again.\]

[016-111] \[Footnote 30: The Dakotas call the meteor, \"Wakân-denda\" (sacred fire)

[016-112] and Wakân-wohlpa (sacred gift.) Meteors are messengers from the Land

[016-113] of Spirits, warning of impending danger. It is a curious fact that the

[016-114] \"sacred stone\" of the Mohammedans, in the Kaaba at Mecca, is a meteoric

[016-115] stone, and obtains its sacred character from the fact that it fell from

[016-116] heaven. 31: Kah-nó-te-dahn\--The little, mysterious dweller in the woods.

[016-117] This spirit lives in the forest in hollow trees. Mrs. Eastman\'s Dacotah,

[016-118] Pre. Rem. xxxi. \"The Dakota god of the woods\--an unknown animal said to

[016-119] resemble a man, which the Dakotas worship; perhaps, the monkey.\" Riggs\'

[016-120] Dakota Dic. Tit\--_Canotidan_.\]

[016-121] \[Footnote 32: The Dakotas believe that thunder is produced by the

[016-122] flapping of the wings of an immense bird which they call Wakínyan\--the

[016-123] Thunder-bird. Near the source of the Minnesota River is a place called

[016-124] \"Thunder-Tracks\" where the foot-prints of a \"Thunder-bird\" are seen on

[016-125] the rocks twenty-five miles apart. Mrs. Eastman\'s Dacotah, p. 71. There

[016-126] are many Thunder-birds. The father of all the Thunder-birds\--\"Wakínyan

[016-127] Tanka\"\--or \"Big Thunder,\" has his teepee on a lofty mountain in the far

[016-128] West. His teepee has four openings, at each of which is a sentinel; at

[016-129] the east, a butterfly; at the west, a bear; at the south, a red deer;

[016-130] at the north, a caribou. He has a bitter enmity against Unktéhee (god

[016-131] of waters) and often shoots his fiery arrows at him, and hits the earth,

[016-132] trees, rocks, and sometimes men. Wakínyan created wild-rice, the bow and

[016-133] arrow, the [[tomahawk]] and the spear. He is a great war-spirit, and Wanmdée

[016-134] (the war-eagle) is his messenger. A Thunder-bird (say the Dakotas) was

[016-135] once killed near Kapóza by the son of Cetan-Wakawa-mâni, and he there

[016-136] upon took the name of \"Wakínyan Tanka\"\--\"Big Thunder.\"\]

[016-147] \[Footnote 38: Carver\'s Cave at St. Paul was called by the Dakotas \"Wakân

[016-148] Teepee\"\--sacred lodge. In the days that are no more, they lighted their

[016-149] Council-fires in this cave, and buried their dead near it. See Neill\'s

[016-150] Hist. Minn., p. 207. Capt. Carver in his _Travels_, London, 1778, p. 63,

[016-151] et seq., describes this cave as follows: \"It is a remarkable cave of an

[016-152] amazing depth. The Indians term it Wakon-teebe, that is, the Dwelling

[016-153] of the Great Spirit. The entrance into it is about ten feet wide, the

[016-154] height of it five feet, the arch within is near fifteen feet high and

[016-155] about thirty feet broad. The bottom of it consists of fine clear sand.

[016-156] [[About]] twenty feet from the entrance begins a lake, the water of which is

[016-157] transparent, and extends to an unsearchable distance; for the darkness

[016-158] of the cave prevents all attempts to acquire a knowledge of it. I threw

[016-159] a small pebble towards the interior parts of it with my utmost strength.

[016-160] I could hear that it fell into the water, and notwithstanding it was

[016-161] of so small a size, it caused an astonishing and horrible noise that

[016-162] reverberated through all those gloomy regions. I found in this cave many

[016-163] Indian hieroglyphics, which appeared very ancient, for time had nearly

[016-164] covered them with moss, so that it was with difficulty I could trace

[016-165] them. They were cut in a rude manner upon the inside of the walls,

[016-166] which were composed of a stone so extremely soft that it might be

[016-167] easily penetrated with a knife: a stone everywhere to be found near the

[016-168] Mississippi. This cave is only accessible by ascending a narrow, steep

[016-169] passage that lies near the brink of the river. At a little distance

[016-170] from this dreary cavern is the burying-place of several bands of the

[016-171] Naudowessie (Dakota) Indians.\" Many years ago the roof fell in, but the

[016-172] cave has been partially restored and is now used as a beer cellar.\]
