[017-001] \[Footnote 48: The \"Sacred Ring\" around the feast of the Virgins is

[017-002] formed by armed warriors sitting, and none but a virgin must enter this

[017-003] ring. The warrior who knows is bound on honor, and by old and sacred

[017-004] custom, to expose and publicly denounce any tarnished maiden who dares

[017-005] to enter this ring, and his word cannot be questioned\--even by the

[017-006] chief. See Mrs Eastman\'s Dacotah, p. 64.\]

[017-007] \[Footnote 49: [[prairie|Prairie]]\'s Pride.\--This annual shrub, which abounds on many

[017-008] of the sandy prairies in Minnesota, is sometimes called \"tea-plant,\"

[017-009] \"sage-plant,\" and \"red-root willow.\" I doubt if it has any botanic

[017-010] name. Its long plumes of purple and gold are truly the \"pride of the

[017-011] prairies.\"\]

[017-012] \[Footnote 50: The [[dakota|Dakotas]] consider white cedar \"Wakân,\" (sacred). They

[017-013] use sprigs of it at their feasts, and often burn it to destroy the power

[017-014] of evil [[spirit|spirits]]. Mrs Eastman\'s Dacotah, p. 210.\]

[017-015] \[Footnote 51: Tâhkoo-skahng-skang.\--This deity is supposed to be

[017-016] invisible, yet everywhere present; he is an avenger and a searcher of

[017-017] hearts. ([[edward-neill|Neill]]\'s Hist. Minn., p. 57.) I suspect he was the chief spirit

[017-018] of the Dakotas before the missionaries imported \"[[wakan|Wakan]] Tânka\"\--(Great

[017-019] Spirit).\]

[017-020] \[Footnote 52: The Dakotas believe in \"were-wolves\" as firmly as did our

[017-021] Saxon ancestors, and for similar reasons\--the howl of the wolf being

[017-022] often imitated as a decoy or signal by their enemies, the Ojibways.\]

[017-023] \[Footnote 53: Shee-shó-kah\--The Robin.\]

[017-024] \[Footnote 54: The Dakotas cail the Evening Star the \"_Virgin Star_,\" and

[017-029] Lake.\]

[017-030] \[Footnote 56: The Ojibways imitate the hoot of the owl and the howl of

[017-031] the wolf to perfection, and often use these cries as signals to each

[017-033] \[Footnote 57: The Dakotas called the Ojibways the \"Snakes of the

[017-037] \[Footnote 60: Mahgâh\--The Wild-goose. _Fox-pups_. I could never see the

[017-038] propriety of calling the young of foxes _kits_ or _kittens_, which mean

[017-039] _little cats_. The fox belongs to the _canis_, or dog family and not the

[017-040] _felis_, or cat family. If it is proper to call the young of dogs and

[017-041] wolves _pups_, it is equally proper to so call the young of foxes.\]

[017-042] \[Footnote 61: When a Dakota is sick, he thinks the spirit of an enemy or

[017-044] \"medicine man\"\--_Wicasta Wakan_\--is to cast out the \"unclean spirit,\"

[017-045] with incantations and charms. See Neill\'s Hist. Minn., pp. 66\--8. The

[017-046] Jews entertained a similar belief in the days of Jesus of Nazareth.\]

[017-057] \[Footnote 68: The _Via Lactea_ or Milky Way. The Dakotas call it

[017-058] _Wanagee-Tach-anku_\--The path-way of the spirits and believe that over

[017-059] this path the spirits of the dead pass to the Spirit-land. See Riggs\'

[017-061] \[Footnote 69: Oonk-táy-hee\--There are many Unktéhees, children of the

[017-062] Great Unktéhee, who created the earth and man and who formerly dwelt

[017-063] in a vast cavern under the [[falls-of-st-anthony|Falls of St. Anthony]]. The Unktéhee sometimes

[017-064] reveals himself in the form of a huge buffalo-bull. From him proceed

[017-065] invisible influences. The Great Unktéhee created the earth. \"Assembling

[017-066] in grand conclave all the aquatic tribes he ordered them to bring up

[017-067] dirt from beneath the waters, and proclaimed death to the disobedient.

[017-068] The beaver and otter forfeited their lives. At last the muskrat went

[017-069] beneath the waters, and, after a long time appeared at the surface,

[017-070] nearly exhausted, with some dirt. From this, Unktéhee fashioned the

[017-071] earth into a large circular plain. The earth being finished, he took a

[017-072] deity, one of his own offspring, and grinding him to powder, sprinkled

[017-073] it upon the earth, and this produced many worms. The worms were then

[017-074] collected and scattered again. They matured into infants and these were

[017-075] then collected and scattered and became full-grown Dakotas. The bones

[017-076] of the mastodon, the Dakotas think, are the bones of Unktéhees, and they

[017-077] preserve the with the greatest care in the medicine bag.\" Neill\'s Hist.

[017-078] Minn., p. 55. The Unktéhees and the Thunder-birds are perpetually it

[017-079] war. There are various accounts of the creation of man. Some say that

[017-080] at the bidding of the Great Unktéhee, men sprang full grown from the

[017-081] caverns of the earth. See Riggs\' \"Tah-koo Wah-kân,\" and Mrs Eastman\'s

[017-082] Dacotah. The Great Unktéhee and the Great Thunder-bird had a terrible

[017-083] battle in the bowels of the earth to determine which should be the ruler

[017-084] of the world. See description in [[legend|Legend]] of Winona.\]

[017-085] \[Footnote 70: Prononced Ahng-pay-too-wee\--The Sun; literally the Day

[017-086] Sun, thus distinguishing him from Han-yé-tuwee (Hahng-yay-too-wee) the

[017-087] night sun, (the moon). They are twin brothers but Anpétuwee is the more

[017-088] powerful Han-yé-tuwee receives his power from his brother and obeys him.

[017-089] He watches over the earth while the Sun sleeps. The Dakotas believe the

[017-090] sun is the father of life. Unlike the most of their other gods, he is

[017-091] beneficent and kind; yet they worship him (in the sun-dance) in the most

[017-092] dreadful manner. See Riggs\' \"Tah-koo Wah-kân,\" pp. 81-2, and Catlin\'s

[017-093] Riggs\' \"Okee-pa.\" The moon is worshipped as the representative of the

[017-094] sun; and in the great Sun-dance, which is usually held in the full of

[017-095] the moon, when the moon rises the dancers turn their eyes on her (or

[017-096] him). Anpétuwee issues every morning from the lodge of Han-nan-na (the

[017-097] Morning) and begins his journey over the sky to his lodge in the land

[017-098] of shadows. Sometimes he walks over on the Bridge (or path) of the

[017-099] Spirits\--Wanâgee Ta-chan-ku,\--and sometimes he sails over the sea of

[017-100] the skies in his shining canoe; but _somehow_, and the Dakotas do not

[017-101] explain how, he gets back again to the lodge of Hannanna in time to take

[017-102] a nap and eat his breakfast before starting anew on his journey. The

[017-103] Dakotas swear by the sun. \"_As Anpé-tu-wee hears me, this is true_!\"

[017-104] They call him Father and pray to him \--\"_Wakan! Ate, on-she-ma-da._\"

[017-105] \"Sacred Spirit,\--Father, have mercy on me.\" As the Sun is the father,

[017-106] so they believe the Earth is the mother, of life. Truly there is

[017-107] much philosophy in the Dakota mythology. The Algonkins call the earth

[017-108] \"_Me-suk-kum-mik-o-kwa_\"\--the great-grandmother of all. Narrative of

[017-109] John Tanner, p. 193.\]

[017-110] \[Footnote 71: The Dakotas reckon their months by _moon_. They name their

[017-111] moons from natural circumstances. They correspond very nearly with our

[017-112] months, as follows:

[017-113] January\--Wee-té-rhee\--The Hard Moon, i.e.\--the cold moon.

[017-114] February\--Wee-câ-ta-wee\--The Coon Moon.

[017-115] March\--Istâ-wee-ca-ya-zang-wee\--the sore eyes moon (from snow

[017-116] blindness.)

[017-117] April\--Magâ-okâ-da-wee\--the moon when the geese lay eggs; also called

[017-118] Wokâda-wee\--egg-moon, and sometimes Wató-papee-wee, the canoe moon, or

[017-119] moon when the streams become free from ice.

[017-120] May\--Wó-zu-pee-wee\--the planting moon.

[017-121] June\--Wazú-ste-ca-sa-wee\--the strawberry moon.

[017-122] July\--Wa-sun-pa-wee\--moon when the geese shed their feathers,

[017-123] also called Chang-pâ-sapa-wee\--Choke-Cherry moon, and

[017-124] sometimes\--Mna-rchâ-rhca-wee\--\"The moon of the red blooming lilies\",

[017-125] literally, the red-lily moon.

[017-126] August\--Wasú-ton-wee\--the ripe moon, i.e. Harvest Moon.

[017-127] September\--Psin-na-ké-tu-wee\--the ripe rice moon.

[017-128] October\--Wà-zu-pee-wee or Wee-wa-zú-pee\--the moon when wild rice is

[017-129] gathered and laid up for winter.

[017-130] November\--Ta-kee-yu-hrâ-wee\--the deer-rutting moon.

[017-131] December\--Ta-hé-cha-psung-wee\--the moon when deer shed their horns.\]

[017-132] \[Footnote 72: Oonk-tó-mee\--is a \"bad spirit\" in the form of a monstrous

[017-133] black spider. He inhabits fens and marshes and lies in wait for his

[017-134] prey. At night he often lights a torch (evidently the _ignis fatuus_ or

[017-135] Jack-a-lantern) and swings it on the marshes to decoy the unwary into

[017-136] his toils.\]

[017-137] \[Footnote 73: The Dakotas have their stone idol, or god, called

[017-138] Toon-kan\--or In-yan. This god dwells in stone or rocks and is they

[017-139] say, the _oldest god of all_\--he is grandfather of all living things. I

[017-140] think, however that the stone is merely the symbol of the everlasting,

[017-141] all pervading, invisible _Ta-ku Wa-kan_\--the essence of all

[017-142] life,\--pervading all nature, animate and inanimate. The Rev. S. R.

[017-143] Riggs who, for forty years, has been a student of Dakota [[tradition|customs]],

[017-144] [[superstition|superstitions]] etc., says, \"Tâhkoo Wahkan,\" p. 55: et seq. \"The religious

[017-145] faith of the Dakota is not in his gods as such. It is in an intangible,

[017-146] mysterious something of which they are only the embodiment, and that in

[017-147] such measure and degree as may accord with the individual fancy of the

[017-148] worshipper. Each one will worship some of these divinities, and neglect

[017-149] or despise others, but the great object of all their worship, whatever

[017-150] its chosen medium, is the _Ta-koo Wa-kan_, which is the _supernatural_

[017-151] and _mysterious_. No one term can express the full meaning of the

[017-152] Dakotas _Wakan_. It comprehends all mystery, secret power and divinity.

[017-153] Awe and reverence are its due, and it is as unlimited in manifestation

[017-154] as it is in idea. All life is _Wakan_; so also is everything which

[017-155] exhibits power, whether in action as the winds and drifting clouds;

[017-156] or in passive endurance, as the boulder by the wayside. For even the

[017-157] commonest sticks and stones have a spiritual essence which must be

[017-158] reverenced as a manifestation of the all-pervading mysterious power that

[017-159] fills the the universe.\"\]

[017-160] \[Footnote 74: Wazi-kuté\--Wah-ze-koo-tay; literally\--Pine-shooter\--he

[017-161] that shoots among the pines. When Father Hennepin was at Mille Lacs in

[017-162] 1679-80, Wazi-kuté was the head Chief (Itâncan) of the band of Isantees.

[017-163] Hennepin writes his name--- Ouasicoudé and translates it\--the \"Pierced

[017-164] Pine.\" See Shea\'s Hennepin p. 234, Minn. Hist. Coll. vol. I. p. 316.\]

[017-165] \[Footnote 75: When a Dakota brave wishes to \"propose\" to a \"dusky maid\",

[017-166] he visits her teepee at night after she has retired, or rather, laid

[017-167] down in her robe to sleep. He lights a splinter of wood and holds it to

[017-168] her face. If she blows out the light, he is accepted; if she covers her

[017-169] head and leaves it burning, he is rejected. The rejection however is not

[017-170] considered final till it has been thrice repeated. Even then the maiden

[017-171] is often bought of her parents or guardian, and forced to become the

[017-172] wife of the rejected suitor. If she accepts the proposal, still the

[017-173] suitor must buy her of her parents with suitable gifts.\]

[017-174] \[Footnote 76: The Dakotas called the Falls of St. Anthony the Ha-Ha\--the

[017-175] _loud laughing_, or _roaring_. The Mississippi River they called

[017-176] Ha-Ha Wá-kpa\--River of the Falls. The [[ojibway|Ojibway]] name for the Falls

[017-177] is Ka-ká-bih-kúng. Minnehaha is a combination of two Dakota

[017-178] words\--Mini\--water and Ha-Ha\--Falls; but it is not the name by which the

[017-179] Dakotas designated that cataract. Some authorities say they called it

[017-180] I-ha-ha pronounced E-rhah-rhah\--lightly laughing. Rev. S. W. Pond,

[017-181] whose long residence as a missionary among the Dakotas in this immediate

[017-182] vicinity makes him an authority that can hardly be questioned, says

[017-183] \"they called the Falls of Minnehaha \"Mini-i-hrpâ-ya dan,\" and it had no

[017-184] other name in Dakota. It means Little Falls and nothing else.\" Letter to

[017-185] the author.\]

[017-186] \[Footnote 77: The game of the Plum-stones is one of the favorite games

[017-187] of the Dakotas. Hennepin was the first to describe this game in his

[017-188] \"Description de la Louisiane,\" Paris, 1683, and he describes it very

[017-189] accurately. See Shea\'s translation p. 301. The Dakotas call this game

[017-190] _Kan-soo Koo tay-pe_\--shooting plum-stones. Each stone is painted black

[017-191] on one side and red on the other; on one side they grave certain figures

[017-192] which make the stones \"Wakan.\" They are placed in a dish and thrown up

[017-193] like dice; indeed the game is virtually a game of dice. Hennepin says:

[017-194] \"There are some so given to this game that they will gamble away even

[017-195] their great coat. Those who conduct the game cry at the top of their

[017-196] voices when they rattle the platter and they strike their shoulders so

[017-197] hard as to leave them all black with the blows.\"\]
