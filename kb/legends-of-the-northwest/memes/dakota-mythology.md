# Dakota Mythology

[[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology is the body of [[dakota|Dakota]] traditional narratives and beliefs documented in [[hanford-lennox-gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s ethnographic footnotes and woven into the poetic narratives of *[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest*. Gordon notes its resemblance to the [[legend|myth]]ology of the Greeks (006-076) and finds "much philosophy" within it (017-107).

## Definition / Identity

The [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ological system recorded by Gordon centers on a creation narrative involving the Great [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] ([[unktéhee|Water Spirit]]) and a cosmic opposition between subterranean water powers and sky-thunder powers. Gordon's footnotes preserve ethnographic data from missionary-linguists including [[samuel-pond|Rev. S. W. Pond]] and Rev. G. H. Pond.

- **Origin**: [[dakota|Dakota]] oral tradition, transcribed by 19th-century missionary-ethnographers
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]] (terms: *[[unktéhee|Unktéhee]]*, *[[thunder-bird|Wakínyan]]*, *[[wakan|Wakan]]*, *[[heyoka|Heyóka]]*); English (Gordon's commentary)
- **Domain**: [[legend|myth]]ology / religion / cosmology

Gordon explicitly compares [[dakota|Dakota]] and Greek [[legend|myth]]ology: "The resemblance of the [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology to that of the Greeks" (006-076), and later asserts "there is much philosophy in the [[dakota|Dakota]] [[legend|myth]]ology" (017-107), defending indigenous belief systems against contemporary dismissal.

## In the Book

Gordon's [[legend|myth]]ological material appears primarily in the scholarly apparatus — footnotes and the Appendix (Notes) — which together form a substantial ethnographic record of [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmology, [[soul|spirit]] taxonomy, and ritual practice.

### Role in the Legends

Within the poetic narratives, [[legend|myth]]ological beings drive the plot: [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] creates [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] in the [[legend-of-the-falls|Legend of the Falls]], [[thunder-bird|Wakínyan]] opposes the water powers, and [[heyoka|Heyóka]]'s contrary nature shapes the Medicine-Men's rites. The poems themselves become vehicles for preserving and transmitting [[dakota|Dakota]] cosmological concepts.

The creation account describes [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] forming the earth from mud brought from primordial waters, after which the [[thunder-bird|Thunder-Bird]]'s wings dried the land (017-070). This origin establishes the enduring conflict between water and sky powers that structures [[dakota|Dakota]] religious practice.

### Representative Passages

> "The resemblance of the Dakota mythology to that of the Greeks" (006-076)

> "there is much philosophy in the Dakota mythology. The Algonkins call the earth…" (017-107)

## The Dakota Pantheon in Gordon

Gordon's footnotes document a layered [[soul|spirit]] taxonomy:

| Being | Domain | Role |
|-------|--------|------|
| [[Unktéhee]] | Water-depth | Creator of earth and man |
| [[Thunder-Bird]] | Sky-storm | Cosmic adversary of [[unktéhee|Unktéhee]] |
| [[Anpétuwee]] | Sun | Father of life, worshipped through Sun-[[dance|Dance]] |
| [[Heyóka]] | Contrary | Anti-natural [[soul|spirit]], patron of Medicine-Men |
| [[Wanagee-Tach-anku]] | [[spirit|Spirit]]-path | [[wanagee-tach-anku|Milky Way]] — path of departed [[spirit|spirits]] |
| [[Wakan]] | Sacred | Pervasive sacred power in all things |
