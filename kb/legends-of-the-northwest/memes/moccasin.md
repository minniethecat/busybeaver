# Moccasin

Moccasins are the traditional soft-soled footwear of the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] peoples, mentioned in Gordon's [[legend|legends]] as both practical garments and items of domestic care and affection.

## Definition / Identity

Moccasins are traditional Native American footwear made from soft leather (typically deerskin), often decorated with beadwork or quillwork:

- **Origin**: Indigenous to the Native peoples of North America; the word derives from [[algonkin|Algonquian]] *makasin*
- **Language**: [[algonkin|Algonquian]] → English
- **Domain**: clothing / domestic / ceremonial

In Gordon's poetry, moccasins appear both in ceremonial dance contexts — "the moccasined feet of the maidens shine" at the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]] (003-244) — and in intimate domestic scenes where a mother or wife makes moccasins for a loved one.

## In the Book

The moccasin carries both practical and emotional significance in Gordon's narratives, appearing in domestic and ritual settings.

### Role in the Legends

In the [[sea-gull|Sea-Gull]] legend, the maiden makes "little moccasins" for her child as an act of maternal care (013-111). This domestic detail humanizes the legendary figure, grounding the supernatural tale in everyday [[ojibway|Ojibway]] life. The repetition — "Made him moccasins and leggings" (013-246, 013-299) — reinforces the moccasin as an emblem of domestic provision.

At the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]], the maidens' "moccasined feet" shine as they dance (003-244), the moccasins becoming part of the ceremonial beauty of the occasion. In [[winona|Winona]]'s story, her lover stands "stripped to the moccasins bare" (008-056) in a moment of vulnerability before battle.

### Representative Passages

> "The moccasined feet of the maidens shine" (003-244)

> "But stripped to the moccasins bare" (008-056)

> "Little moccasins she made him" (013-111)

> "Made him moccasins and leggings" (013-246)
