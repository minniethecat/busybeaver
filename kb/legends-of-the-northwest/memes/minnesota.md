# Minnesota

**Minnesota** — the land at the heart of [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s *[[Legend|Legends of the Northwest]]* — was the homeland of the [[Dakota]] people before European settlement. Gordon described it as the "fair forests and fertile fields of our own Minnesota" (P01-171).

## The Dakota Homeland

At the time of first European contact, the [[Dakota]] occupied the forests and prairies of Minnesota:

> They were, but yesterday, the occupants and owners of the fair forests and fertile prairies of Minnesota — a brave, hospitable and generous people — barbarians, indeed, but noble in their barbarism.
> (P01-015–017)

The [[Falls of St. Anthony]], called *[[ha-ha|Ha-Ha]]* by the [[dakota|Dakotas]], was the center of their country (P01-020–021).

## Geography

Minnesota is defined by its two great rivers: the [[Mississippi River|Mississippi]] and the Minnesota. The confluence of these two rivers at [[Mendota]] — *[[mendota|Mdó-tè]]* in [[dakota|Dakota]], meaning "meeting of the waters" — was a site of great significance (015-218–219).

Near the source of the Minnesota River is a place called "Thunder-Tracks," where the footprints of the Thunder-bird are said to be visible on the rocks (016-123–125).

## Historical Events

### The Dakota War of 1862

The "terrible outbreak in Minnesota in 1862-3" (015-227) — the [[Dakota War of 1862|U.S.–Dakota War]] — marked a catastrophic turning point. [[little-crow|Ta-ó-ya-te-dú-ta]], the last of the [[little-crow|Little Crow]]s, was killed near Hutchinson, Minnesota, on July 3, 1863 (015-244).

### The Fur Trade Era

The sandy prairies of Minnesota were traversed by [[voyageurs|French voyageurs]] as part of the [[fur trade]] network connecting the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s to the Mississippi watershed (017-008).

## In Gordon's Work

Gordon chose to publish his *[[legend|Legend]]s* in Minnesota because:

> They pertain particularly to the lakes and rivers … of our own Minnesota and ought to be appreciated here if anywhere.
> (P01-170–172)

He drew on [[edward-neill|Neill]]'s *History of Minnesota* (P01-071, 006-182, 015-248, 018-067) as a key historical source.

## Natural Landscape

The Minnesota prairies were noted for their seasonal beauty:

> I have seen the prairies in Minnesota spangled with these beautiful flowers in various colors before the ground was entirely free from frost.
> (016-194–196)

The landscape also held the dramatic legend of [[Wa-zí-ya]] (the Winter-maker), who was said to have hurled granite boulders scattered over the prairies of Minnesota and [[dakota|Dakota]] from his home in the North (015-213–216).
