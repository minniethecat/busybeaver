[005-001] But what of the venomous Hârpstinà\--

[005-002] The serpent that tempted the proud Red Cloud,

[005-003] And kindled revenge in his savage soul?

[005-004] He paid for his crime with his false heart\'s blood,

[005-005] But his angry spirit has brought her dole; \[61\]

[005-006] It has entered her breast and her burning head,

[005-007] And she raves and burns on her fevered bed.

[005-008] \"He is dead! He is dead!\" is her wailing cry.

[005-009] \"And the blame is mine,\--it was I,\--it was I!

[005-010] I hated Wiwâstè, for she was fair,

[005-011] And my brave was caught in her net of hair.

[005-012] I turned his love to a bitter hate;

[005-013] I nourished revenge, and I pricked his pride;

[005-014] Till the Feast of the Virgins I bade him wait.

[005-015] He had his revenge, but he died,\--he died!

[005-016] And the blame is mine,\--it was I,\--it was I!

[005-017] And his spirit burns me, I die,\--I die!\"

[005-018] Thus, alone in her lodge and her agonies,

[005-019] She wails to the winds of the night, and dies.

[005-020] But where is Wiwâstè? Her swift feet flew

[005-021] To the somber shades of the tangled thicket.

[005-022] She hid in the copse like a wary cricket,

[005-023] And the fleetest hunters in vain pursue.

[005-024] Seeing unseen from her hiding place,

[005-025] She sees them fly on the hurried chase;

[005-026] She sees their fierce eyes glance and dart,

[005-027] As they pass and peer for a track or trace,

[005-028] And she trembles with fear in the copse apart.

[005-029] Lest her nest be betrayed by her throbbing heart.

[005-030] \[Illustration\]

[005-031] Weary the hours; but the sun at last

[005-032] Went down to his lodge in the west, and fast

[005-033] The wings of the [[spirit|spirits]] of night were spread

[005-034] O\'er the darkling woods and Wiwâstè\'s head.

[005-035] Then, slyly she slipped from her snug retreat,

[005-036] And guiding her course by Wazíya\'s star, \[62\]

[005-037] That shone through the shadowy forms afar,

[005-038] She northward hurried with silent feet;

[005-039] And long ere the sky was aflame in the east,

[005-040] She was leagues from the place of the fatal feast.

[005-041] \'Twas the hoot of the owl that the hunters heard,

[005-042] And the scattering drops of the threat\'ning shower,

[005-043] And the far wolf\'s cry to the moon preferred.

[005-044] Their ears were their fancies,\--the scene was weird,

[005-045] And the witches \[63\] dance at the midnight hour.

[005-046] She leaped the brook and she swam the river;

[005-047] Her course through the forest Wiwâstè wist

[005-048] By the star that gleamed through the glimmering mist

[005-049] That fell from the dim moon\'s downy quiver.

[005-050] In her heart she spoke to her spirit-mother:

[005-051] \"Look down from your teepee, O starry spirit.

[005-052] The cry of Wiwâstè, O mother, hear it;

[005-053] And touch the heart of my cruel father.

[005-054] He hearkened not to a virgin\'s words;

[005-055] He listened not to a daughter\'s wail.

[005-056] O give me the wings of the thunder-birds,

[005-057] For his were-wolves \[52\] follow Wiwâstè\'s trail;

[005-058] O, guide my flight to the far Hóhé\--

[005-059] The sheltering lodge of my brave Chaskè.\"

[005-060] The shadows paled in the hazy east,

[005-061] And the light of the kindling morn increased.

[005-062] The pale-faced stars fled one by one,

[005-063] And hid in the vast from the rising sun.

[005-064] From woods and waters and welkin soon

[005-065] Fled the hovering mists of the vanished moon.

[005-066] The young robins chirped in their feathery beds,

[005-067] The loon\'s song shrilled like a winding horn,

[005-068] And the green hills lifted their dewy heads

[005-069] To greet the god of the rising morn.

[005-070] She reached the rim of the rolling prairie\--

[005-071] The boundless ocean of solitude;

[005-072] She hid in the feathery hazel wood,

[005-073] For her heart was sick and her feet were weary;

[005-074] She fain would rest, and she needed food.

[005-075] Alone by the billowy, boundless prairies,

[005-076] She plucked the cones of the scarlet berries;

[005-077] In feathering copse and the grassy field

[005-078] She found the bulbs of the young Tipsânna, \[43\]

[005-079] And the sweet medó \[64\] that the meadows yield.

[005-080] With the precious gift of his priceless manna

[005-081] God fed his fainting and famished child.

[005-082] At night again to the northward far

[005-083] She followed the torch of Wazíya\'s star.

[005-084] For leagues away o\'er the prairies green,

[005-085] On the billowy vast, may a man be seen,

[005-086] When the sun is high and the stars are low;

[005-087] And the sable breast of the strutting crow

[005-088] Looms up like the form of the buffalo.

[005-089] The Bloody River \[40\] she reached at last,

[005-090] And boldly walked in the light of day,

[005-091] On the level plain of the valley vast;

[005-092] Nor thought of the terrible Chippeway.

[005-093] She was safe from the wolves of her father\'s band,

[005-094] But she trode on the treacherous \"Bloody Land.\"

[005-095] And lo\--from afar o\'er the level plain\--

[005-096] As far as the sails of a ship at sea

[005-097] May be seen as they lift from the rolling main\--

[005-098] A band of warriors rode rapidly.

[005-099] She shadowed her eyes with her sun browned hand;

[005-100] All backward streamed on the wind her hair,

[005-101] And terror spread o\'er her visage fair,

[005-102] As she bent her brow to the far off band.

[005-103] For she thought of the terrible Chippeway\--

[005-104] The fiends that the babe and the mother slay;

[005-105] And yonder they came in their war-array!

[005-106] She hid like a grouse in the meadow-grass,

[005-107] And moaned\--\"I am lost!\--I am lost! alas;

[005-108] And why did I fly my native land

[005-109] To die by the cruel [[ojibway|Ojibway]]\'s hand?\"

[005-110] And on rode the braves. She could hear the steeds

[005-111] Come galloping on o\'er the level meads;

[005-112] And lowly she crouched in the waving grass,

[005-113] And hoped against hope that the braves would pass.

[005-114] They have passed, she is safe,\--she is safe! Ah, no,

[005-115] They have struck her trail and the hunters halt.

[005-116] Like wolves on the track of the bleeding doe,

[005-117] That grappled breaks from the dread assault,

[005-118] Dash the warriors wild on Wiwâstè\'s trail.

[005-119] She flies,\--but what can her flight avail?

[005-120] Her feet are fleet, but the flying feet

[005-121] Of the steeds of the prairie are fleeter still;

[005-122] And where can she fly for a safe retreat?

[005-123] But hark to the shouting:\--\"Ihó!\--Ihó!\" \[9\]

[005-124] Rings over the wide plain sharp and shrill.

[005-125] She halts, and the hunters come riding on;

[005-126] But the horrible fear from her heart is gone,

[005-127] For it is not the shout of the dreaded foe;

[005-128] \'Tis the welcome shout of her native land!

[005-129] Up galloped the chief of the band, and lo\--

[005-130] The clutched knife dropped from her trembling hand;

[005-131] She uttered a cry and she swooned away;

[005-132] For there; on his steed in the blaze of day,

[005-133] On the boundless prairie, so far away,

[005-134] With his burnished lance and his feathers gay,

[005-135] Sat the manly form of her own Chaskè!

[005-136] There\'s a mote in my eye or a blot on the page,

[005-137] And I cannot tell of the joyful greeting;

[005-138] You may take it for granted and I will engage,

[005-139] There were kisses and tears at the strange, glad meeting;

[005-140] For aye since the birth of the swift-winged years,

[005-141] In the desert drear, in the field of clover,

[005-142] In the cot, and the palace, and all the world over,\--

[005-143] Yea, away on the stars to the ultimate spheres,

[005-144] The language of love to the long sought lover,\--

[005-145] Is tears and kisses and kisses and tears.

[005-146] But why did the lover so long delay?

[005-147] And whitherward rideth the chief to-day?

[005-148] As he followed the trail of the buffalo,

[005-149] From the tees of Kapóza a maiden, lo,

[005-150] Came running in haste o\'er the drifted snow.

[005-151] She spoke to the chief of the tall Hóhé:

[005-152] \"Wiwâstè requests that the brave Chaskè

[005-153] Will abide with his band and his coming delay

[005-154] \'Till the moon when the strawberries are ripe and red,

[005-155] And then will the chief and Wiwâstè wed\--

[005-156] When the Feast of the Virgins is past,\" she said.

[005-157] Wiwâstè\'s wish was her lover\'s law;

[005-158] And so his coming the chief delayed

[005-159] Till the mid-May blossoms should bloom and fade,\--

[005-160] But the lying runner was Hârpstinà.

[005-161] And now with the gifts for the bridal day

[005-162] And his chosen warriors he took his way,

[005-163] And followed his heart to his moon-faced maid,

[005-164] And thus was the lover so long delayed;

[005-165] And so as he rode with his warriors gay,

[005-166] On that bright and beautiful summer day,

[005-167] His bride he met on the trail mid-way,

[005-168] By the haunts of the treacherous Chippeway.

[005-169] God arms the innocent. He is there\--

[005-170] In the desert vast, in the wilderness,

[005-171] On the bellowing sea, in the lion\'s lair,

[005-172] In the midst of battle, and everywhere.

[005-173] In his hand he holds with a father\'s care

[005-174] The tender hearts of the motherless;

[005-175] The maid and the mother in sore distress

[005-176] He shields with his love and his tenderness;

[005-177] He comforts the widowed\--the comfortless,

[005-178] And sweetens her chalice of bitterness;

[005-179] He clothes the naked\--the numberless,\--

[005-180] His charity covers their nakedness,\--

[005-181] And he feeds the famished and fatherless

[005-182] With the hand that feedeth the birds of air.

[005-183] Let the myriad tongues of the earth confess

[005-184] His infinite love and his holiness;

[005-185] For his pity pities the pitiless,

[005-186] His wayward children his bounties bless,

[005-187] And his mercy flows to the merciless;

[005-188] And the countless worlds in the realms above,

[005-189] Revolve in the light of his boundless love.

[005-190] And what of the lovers? you ask, I trow.

[005-191] She told him all ere the sun was low,\--

[005-192] Why she fled from the Feast to a safe retreat.

[005-193] She laid her heart at her lover\'s feet,

[005-194] And her words were tears and her lips were slow.

[005-195] As she sadly related the bitter tale

[005-196] His face was aflame and anon grew pale,

[005-197] And his dark eyes flashed with a brave desire,

[005-198] Like the midnight gleam of the sacred fire. \[65\]

[005-199] \"Mitâwin,\" \[66\] he said, and his voice was low,

[005-200] \"Thy father no more is the false Little Crow;

[005-201] But the fairest plume shall Wiwâstè wear

[005-202] Of the great Wanmdeè \[13\] in her midnight hair.

[005-203] In my lodge, in the land of the tall Hóhé,

[005-204] The robins will sing all the long summer day

[005-205] To the beautiful bride of the brave Chaskè.\"

[005-206] Aye, love is tested by stress and trial

[005-207] Since the finger of time on the endless dial

[005-208] Began its rounds, and the orbs to move

[005-209] In the boundless vast, and the sunbeams clove

[005-210] The chaos; but only by fate\'s denial

[005-211] Are fathomed the fathomless depths of love.

[005-212] Man is the rugged and wrinkled oak,

[005-213] And woman the trusting and tender vine\--

[005-214] That clasps and climbs till its arms entwine

[005-215] The brawny arms of the sturdy stoke. \[67\]

[005-216] The dimpled babes are the flowers divine

[005-217] That the blessing of God on the vine and oak

[005-218] With their cooing and blossoming lips invoke.

[005-219] To the pleasant land of the brave Hóhé

[005-220] Wiwâstè rode with her proud Chaskè.

[005-221] She ruled like a queen in his bountiful tee,

[005-222] And the life of the twain was a jubilee.

[005-223] Their wee ones climbed on the father\'s knee,

[005-224] And played with his plumes of the great Wanmdeè.

[005-225] The silken threads of the happy years

[005-226] They wove into beautiful robes of love

[005-227] That the spirits wear in the lodge above;

[005-228] And time from the reel of the rolling spheres

[005-229] His silver threads with the raven wove;

[005-230] But never the stain of a mother\'s tears

[005-231] Soiled the shining web of their happy years.

[005-232] When the wrinkled mask of the years they wore,

[005-233] And the raven hair of their youth was gray,

[005-234] Their love grew deeper, and more and more;

[005-235] For he was a lover for aye and aye,

[005-236] And ever her beautiful, brave Chaskè.

[005-237] Through the wrinkled mask of the hoary years

[005-238] To the loving eyes of the lover aye

[005-239] The blossom of beautiful youth appears.

[005-240] At last, when their locks were as white as snow,

[005-241] Beloved and honored by all the band,

[005-242] They silently slipped from their lodge below,

[005-243] And walked together, and hand in hand,

[005-244] O\'er the Shining Path \[68\] to the Spirit-land;

[005-245] Where the hills and the meadows for aye and aye

[005-246] Are clad with the verdure and flowers of May,

[005-247] And the unsown prairies of Paradise

[005-248] Yield the golden maize and the sweet wild rice.

[005-249] There ever ripe in the groves and prairies

[005-250] Hang the purple plums and the luscious berries.

[005-251] And the swarthy herds of bison feed

[005-252] On the sun-lit slope and the waving mead;

[005-253] The dappled fawns from their coverts peep,

[005-254] And countless flocks on the waters sleep;

[005-255] And the silent years with their fingers trace

[005-256] No furrows for aye on the hunter\'s face.
