# Ceremonial Feast

The ceremonial feast tradition of the [[dakota|Dakota]] people, marking major communal events.

The feast was central to [[dakota|Dakota]] social life, marking occasions from marriages to war councils. The [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]] is the most elaborate example preserved in Gordon's work (003-108). [[dakota|Dakota]] maidens played a key role in these ceremonial gatherings (003-109).

## In Gordon's Legends

Gordon preserves these [[dakota|Dakota]] events through English verse, informed by his years of study and the work of missionaries like Riggs (P01-063). His representations aim to be faithful to the original [[tradition|traditions]] (P01-174).

## Feast Customs of the Dakota

The most elaborate ceremonial feast preserved in Gordon's work is the [[feast-of-the-virgins|Feast of the Virgins]], a four-day rite involving the Sacred Ring — a circle of armed warriors within which only maidens of proven virtue may enter (017-001–006; 003-224). The ceremonial feast was preceded by purification in the [[sweat-lodge|sweat lodge]], or *E-né-pee* (018-050).

Feasts were not merely social gatherings; they were sacred events governed by strict ritual protocols. At great feasts, the Dakota observed formal silence — they did not speak at them (018-110). The calumet, or [[calumet|peace-pipe]], played a central role in feast ceremonial, and the distribution of food followed prescribed order reflecting social rank and spiritual significance.
