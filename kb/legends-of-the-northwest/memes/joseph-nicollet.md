Joseph Nicollet (Joseph Nicolas Nicollet) was a French explorer, mathematician, and cartographer who conducted extensive surveys of the Upper [[mississippi-river|Mississippi River]] region in the 1830s. [[nicollet-island|Nicollet Island]], the picturesque island in the Mississippi just above [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]] of St. Anthony, bears his name, and it features prominently in Gordon's "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest."

## 在书中的角色

### 地名遗存

[[nicollet-island|Nicollet Island]] appears in Gordon's [[winona|Winona]] narrative as "[[nicollet-island|Fairy Island]]" — a place of beauty, love, and dancing. Gordon provides the [[dakota|Dakota]] name for the island in his footnotes:

> The Dakotas called Nicollet Island "Wi-ta Waste" — the Beautiful Island. (007-075)

In the poem's action, the island serves as a gathering place for the [[dakota|Dakota]] people, where [[wanata|Wanâta]] and his warriors assemble after their buffalo hunt. The island's [[dakota|Dakota]] name is referenced again as the scene shifts:

> Wee tah Wah-stay — Beautiful Island, — the Dakota name for Nicollet Island just above the Falls. (013-050)

### 作为地理锚点

[[nicollet-island|Nicollet Island]] appears in Gordon's annotations as a geographical reference point, anchoring the legendary landscape in real-world topography. The island's dual identity — "[[nicollet-island|Fairy Island]]" in poetry, [[nicollet-island|Nicollet Island]] in geography — reflects Gordon's method of weaving historical place names into legendary narrative.

## 历史/文化背景

Joseph N. Nicollet (1786–1843) led three expeditions exploring the Upper Mississippi region between 1836 and 1839, producing the first accurate map of the area. His work was commissioned by the United States government and later completed by John C. Frémont after Nicollet's death. The island near [[st-anthony-falls|St. Anthony Falls]] was named in his honor, and Gordon's annotations preserve the [[dakota|Dakota]] perspective on this same landscape, calling it [[nicollet-island|Wi-ta Waste]] ([[nicollet-island|Beautiful Island]]) and also referring to it as [[nicollet-island|Fairy Island]] (013-070).

Nicollet's maps and journals remain important historical documents for understanding the Upper Midwest before widespread European settlement.
