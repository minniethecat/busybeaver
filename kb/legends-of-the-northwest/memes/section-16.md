[015-001] Then in secret to the Panther

[015-002] Raven told his tale of wonder.

[015-003] Sad and sullen was the hunter;

[015-004] Sorrow gnawed his heart like hunger;

[015-005] All the old love came upon him,

[015-006] And the new love was a hatred.

[015-007] Hateful to his heart was Red Fox,

[015-008] But he kept from her the secret\--

[015-009] Kept his knowledge of the murder.

[015-010] Vain was she and very haughty\-\--

[015-011] Oge-mâ-kwa \[1025\] of the wigwam.

[015-012] All in vain her fond caresses

[015-013] On the Panther now she lavished;

[015-014] When she smiled his face was sullen,

[015-015] When she laughed he frowned upon her;

[015-016] In her net of raven tresses

[015-017] Now no more she held him tangled.

[015-018] Now through all her fair disguises

[015-019] Panther saw an evil spirit,

[015-020] Saw the false heart of the woman.

[015-021] On the tall cliff o\'er the waters

[015-022] Raven sat with Waub-omeé-meé,

[015-023] Sat and watched again and waited,

[015-024] Till the wee one faint and famished,

[015-025] Made a long and piteous wailing.

[015-026] Then again the snow-white Sea-Gull

[015-027] From afar where sky and waters

[015-028] Meet in misty haze and mingle,

[015-029] Straight toward the rocky highland,

[015-030] Straight as flies the feathered arrow,

[015-031] Straight to Raven and the infant,

[015-032] With the silver chain around her,

[015-033] Flew and touched the earth a woman.

[015-034] In her arms she caught her infant\--

[015-035] Caught the wailing Waub-omeé-meé,

[015-036] Sang a lullaby and nursed her.

[015-037] Sprang the Panther from the thicket\--

[015-038] Sprang and broke the chain of silver!

[015-039] With his [[tomahawk]] he broke it.

[015-040] Thus he freed the willing Sea-Gull\--

[015-041] From the Water-[[spirit|Spirit]] freed her,

[015-042] From the Chief of Nébe-nâw-baigs.

[015-043] Very angry was the Spirit;

[015-044] When he drew the chain of silver,

[015-045] Drew and found that it was broken,

[015-046] Found that he had lost the woman,

[015-047] Very angry was the Spirit.

[015-048] Then he raged beneath the waters,

[015-049] Raged and smote the mighty waters,

[015-050] Till the big sea boiled and bubbled,

[015-051] Till the white-haired, bounding billows

[015-052] Roared around the rocky head-lands,

[015-053] Roared and plashed upon the shingle.

[015-054] To the wigwam happy Panther,

[015-055] As when first he wooed and won her,

[015-056] Led his wife\--as young and handsome.

[015-057] For the waves of Gitchee Gumee

[015-058] Washed away the frost and wrinkles,

[015-059] And the Spirits by their magic

[015-060] Made her young and fair forever.

[015-061] In the wigwam sat the Red Fox,

[015-062] Sat and sang a song of triumph,

[015-063] For she little dreamed of danger,

[015-064] Till the haughty hunter entered,

[015-065] Followed by the happy mother,

[015-066] Holding in her arms her infant.

[015-067] Then the Red Fox saw the Sea-Gull\--

[015-068] Saw the dead a living woman,

[015-069] One wild cry she gave despairing,

[015-070] One wild cry as of a demon.

[015-071] Up she sprang and from the wigwam

[015-072] To the tall cliff flew in terror;

[015-073] Frantic sprang upon the margin,

[015-074] Frantic plunged into the water,

[015-075] Headlong plunged into the waters.

[015-076] Dead she tossed upon the billows;

[015-077] For the Nébe-nâw-baigs knew her,

[015-078] Knew the crafty, wicked woman,

[015-079] And they cast her from the waters,

[015-080] Spurned her from their shining wigwams;

[015-081] Far away upon the shingle

[015-082] With the roaring waves they cast her.

[015-083] There upon her bloated body

[015-084] Fed the cawing crows and ravens,

[015-085] Fed the hungry wolves and foxes.

[015-086] On the shore of Gitchee Gumee,

[015-087] Ever young and ever handsome,

[015-088] Long and happy lived the Sea-Gull,

[015-089] Long and happy with the Panther.

[015-090] Evermore the happy hunter

[015-091] Loved the mother of his children.

[015-092] Like a red star many winters

[015-093] Blazed their lodge-fire on the sea-shore.

[015-094] O\'er the Bridge of Souls together \[1026\]

[015-095] Walked the Sea-Gull and the Panther.

[015-096] To the far-off Sunny Islands\--

[015-097] To the Summer-Land of Spirits,

[015-098] Where no more the happy hunter

[015-099] Feels the fangs of frost or famine,

[015-100] Or the keen blasts of Kewâydin.

[015-101] Where no pain or sorrow enters,

[015-102] And no crafty, wicked woman,

[015-103] Sea-Gull journeyed with her husband.

[015-104] There she rules his lodge forever,

[015-105] And the twain are very happy,

[015-106] On the far-off Sunny Islands,

[015-107] In the Summer-Land of Spirits.

[015-108] On the rocks of Gitchee Gumee\--

[015-109] On the [[pictured-rocks|Pictured Rocks]]\--the [[legend|Legend]]

[015-110] Long ago was traced and written,

[015-111] Pictured by the Water Spirits;

[015-112] But the storms of many winters

[015-113] Have bedimmed the pictured story,

[015-114] So that none can read the legend

[015-115] But the Jossakeeds, the prophets. \[1027\]

\[Illustration: CRYSTAL BAY, LAKE MINNETONKA.\]

MINNETONKA

[015-116] Note: The [[dakota|Dakota]] name for this beautiful lake is _Me-ne-a-tan-ka_\--Broad

[015-117] Water. By dropping the a before tánka, we have changed the name to _Big

[015-118] Water_.

[015-119] I sit once more on breezy shore, at sunset in this glorious June.

[015-120] I hear the dip of gleaming oar. I list the singer\'s merry tune.

[015-121] Beneath my feet the waters beat and ripple on the polished stones.

[015-122] The squirrel chatters from his seat: the bag-pipe beetle hums and drones.

[015-123] The pink and gold in blooming wold,\--the green hills mirrored in the lake!

[015-124] The deep, blue waters, zephyr-rolled, along the murmuring pebbles break.

[015-125] The maples screen the ferns, and lean the leafy lindens o\'er the deep;

[015-126] The sapphire, set in emerald green, lies like an Orient gem asleep.

[015-127] The crimsoned west glows like the breast of _Rhuddin_ \[a\]

[015-128] when he pipes in May,

[015-129] As downward droops the sun to rest, and shadows gather on the bay.

[015-130] \[a\] The Welsh name for the robin.

[015-131] In amber sky the swallows fly, and sail and circle o\'er the deep;

[015-132] The light-winged night-hawks whir and cry; the silver pike and salmon leap.

[015-133] The rising moon, the woods aboon, looks laughing down on lake and lea;

[015-134] Weird o\'er the waters shrills the loon; the high stars twinkle in the sea.

[015-135] From bank and hill the whippowil sends piping forth his flute-like notes,

[015-136] And clear and shrill the answers trill from leafy isles and silver throats.

[015-137] The twinkling light on cape and height; the hum of voices on the shores;

[015-138] The merry laughter on the night; the dip and plash of frolic oars,\--

[015-139] These tell the tale. On hill and dale the cities pour their gay and fair;

[015-140] Along the sapphire lake they sail, and quaff like wine the balmy air.

[015-141] \'Tis well. Of yore from isle and shore

[015-142] the smoke of Indian teepees \[a\] rose;

[015-143] The hunter plied the silent oar; the forest lay in still repose.

[015-144] The moon-faced maid, in leafy glade, her warrior waited from the chase;

[015-145] The nut-brown, naked children played, and chased the gopher on the grass.

[015-146] The dappled fawn, on wooded lawn, peeped out upon the birch canoe,

[015-147] Swift-gliding in the gray of dawn along the silent waters blue.

[015-148] In yonder tree the great _Wanm-dee_ \[b\] securely built her spacious nest;

[015-149] The blast that swept the land-locked sea \[c\]

[015-150] but rocked her clamorous babes to rest.

[015-151] By grassy mere the elk and deer gazed on the hunter as he came;

[015-152] Nor fled with fear from bow or spear;\--\"so wild were they that they were

[015-153] tame.\"

[015-154] \[a\] Lodges.

[015-155] \[b\] Wanm-dee\--the war-eagle of the Dakotas.

[015-156] \[c\] [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]].

[015-157] Ah, birch canoe, and hunter, too, have long forsaken lake and shore:

[015-158] He bade his father\'s bones adieu and turned away forevermore.

[015-159] But still, methinks, on dusky brinks the spirit of the warrior moves;

[015-160] At crystal springs the hunter drinks, and nightly haunts the spot he loves.

[015-161] For oft at night I see the light of lodge-fires on the shadowy shores,

[015-162] And hear the wail some maiden\'s sprite above her slaughtered warrior pours.

[015-163] I hear the sob on Spirit Knob \[a\] of Indian mother o\'er her child;

[015-164] And on the midnight waters throb her low _yun-he-he\'s_ \[b\] weird and wild.

[015-165] And sometimes, too, the light canoe glides like a shadow o\'er the deep

[015-166] At midnight, when the moon is low, and all the shores are hushed in sleep.

[015-167] \[a\] Spirit Knob is a small hill up on a point in the lake in full view

[015-168] from Wayzata. The spirit of a Dakota mother whose only child was drowned

[015-169] in the lake during a storm, many, many years ago often wails at midnight

[015-170] (so the Dakotas say), on this hill. So they called it _Wa-na-gee

[015-171] Pa-ze-dan_\--Spirit Knob. (Literally\--little hill of the spirit.)

[015-172] \[b\] Pronounced _Yoon-hay-hay_\--the exclamation used by Dakota women

[015-173] in their lament for the dead, and equivalent to \"woe is me.\"

[015-174] Alas\--Alas!\--for all things pass; and we shall vanish, too, as they;

[015-175] We build our monuments of brass, and granite, but they waste away.

[015-178] \[Footnote 2: One of the favorite and most exciting games of the Dakotas

[015-179] is ball-playing. A smooth place on the prairie, or in winter, on a

[015-180] frozen lake or river, is chosen. Each player has a sort of bat, called

[015-181] \"Tâ-kée-cha-psé-cha,\" about thirty two inches long with a hoop at the

[015-182] lower end four or five inches in diameter, interlaced with thongs of

[015-183] deer-skin, forming a sort of pocket. With these bats they catch and

[015-184] throw the ball. Stakes are set as bounds at a considerable distance from

[015-185] the centre on either side. Two parties are then formed, and each chooses

[015-186] a leader or chief. The ball (Tâ-pa) is then thrown up half way between

[015-187] the bounds, and the game begins, the contestants contending with their

[015-188] bats for the ball as it falls. When one succeeds in getting it fairly in

[015-189] the pocket of his bat he swings it aloft and throws it as far as he can

[015-190] towards the bound to which his party is working, taking care to send it,

[015-191] if possible, where some of his own side will take it up. Thus the ball

[015-192] is thrown and contended for till one party succeeds in casting it

[015-193] beyond the bound of the opposite party. A hundred players on a side are

[015-194] sometimes engaged in this exciting game. Betting on the result often

[015-195] runs high. Moccasins, pipes, knives, hatchets, blankets, robes and guns

[015-196] are hung on the prize-pole. Not unfrequently horses are staked on the

[015-197] issue, and sometimes even women. Old men and mothers are among the

[015-198] spectators praising their swift-footed sons, and young wives and maidens

[015-199] are there to stimulate their husbands and lovers. This game is not

[015-200] confined to the warriors, but is also a favorite amusement of the Dakota

[015-201] maidens who generally play for prizes offered by the chief or warriors.
