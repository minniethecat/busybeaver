Net-nó-kwa, known in English as "The Otter Woman," was an Ottawa (Odawa) female chief — an Oge-mâ-kwa — of the [[red-river|Red River]] region in the late 18th and early 19th centuries. She is remembered primarily as the adoptive mother of [[john-tanner|John Tanner]], the American frontiersman whose captivity narrative became a widely cited ethnographic source on [[algonkin|Algonkin]] life. Gordon references Net-nó-kwa in the Notes and Appendix of "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" as an example of the [[algonkin|Algonkin]] practice of female chieftainship.

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: Ottawa (Odawa), an [[algonkin|Algonkin]] people of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s region
- **Role**: Female chief (Oge-mâ-kwa), adoptive mother
- **Family**: Adoptive mother of [[John Tanner]], the American captivity-narrative author

## 在书中的角色

### Gordon 笔下的女性酋长典范

Gordon cites Net-nó-kwa in the scholarly apparatus of "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" as evidence that [[algonkin|Algonkin]] tribes recognized female political authority — a fact relevant to his broader argument about the sophistication of Native American governance structures:

> Among the Algonkin tribes women are sometimes made chiefs. Net-nó-kwa, who adopted Tanner as her son, was Oge-mâ-kwa of a band of Ottawas. See John Tanner's Narrative, p. 36. (019-022), (019-023)

The footnote preceding this passage establishes the linguistic basis for her title:

> Ogema, Chief, — Ogema-kwa — female Chief. (019-020)

### 与 John Tanner 的收养关系

Net-nó-kwa adopted [[john-tanner|John Tanner]] after he had been captured by a Shawnee war party and sold to an Ottawa family. As Tanner's adoptive mother, she raised him within Ottawa society, teaching him the language, [[tradition|customs]], and survival skills of her people. Tanner lived among the Ojibwe and Ottawa for nearly thirty years — including under Net-nó-kwa's care in the [[red-river|Red River]] region — before attempting to reintegrate into American society in the 1820s (019-021).

Tanner's narrative, published in 1830 as "A Narrative of the Captivity and Adventures of [[john-tanner|John Tanner]]," contains detailed descriptions of his life with Net-nó-kwa and the Ottawa band she led. This firsthand account provides one of the most complete portraits of an [[algonkin|Algonkin]] female chief in early American ethnographic literature.

## 历史/文化背景

Among the [[algonkin|Algonkin]] peoples — including the Ojibwe ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]]), Ottawa (Odawa), and Potawatomi — women occasionally held formal leadership positions. The title *Ogema* (chief) had a feminine form, *Oge-mâ-kwa*, indicating that the role was linguistically and socially recognized for both genders. Female chiefs were not merely ceremonial figures; they exercised political authority, led bands, and made decisions about migration, trade, and warfare (019-020).

Net-nó-kwa's leadership of an Ottawa band in the [[red-river|Red River]] region (present-day [[minnesota|Minnesota]]–North [[dakota|Dakota]] border area) places her within the complex intercultural landscape of the early 19th-century fur trade. The [[red-river|Red River]] was a major transportation corridor and the site of intense interaction between Native peoples, European traders, and American settlers. Female chiefs like Net-nó-kwa navigated these cross-cultural pressures while maintaining the cohesion of their communities.

The practice of captive adoption — by which war captives like [[john-tanner|John Tanner]] were incorporated into Native families — was a recognized institution in [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s societies. Adoption served both to replace family members lost to warfare or disease and to integrate outsiders into kinship networks. Net-nó-kwa's adoption of Tanner followed this established pattern, and her status as a chief meant that Tanner was raised within a family of considerable political influence.
