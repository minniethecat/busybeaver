John Caldwell Calhoun (1782–1850) was a prominent American statesman who served as Vice President, Secretary of War, Secretary of State, and U.S. Senator from South Carolina. Lake Calhoun in Minneapolis — one of the city's chain of lakes — was named in his honor during his tenure as Secretary of War (1817–1825), when the U.S. Army established fortifications and surveyed the Upper Mississippi region. The lake appears in Gordon's geographical annotations to "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest."

## 在书中的角色

### 作为地名来源

Calhoun appears in Gordon's "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest" only indirectly — through the lake that once bore his name. In a detailed footnote on [[dakota|Dakota]] place names, Gordon records:

> The Dakotas called Lake Calhoun — Mdé-mdó-za — Loon Lake. They also called it Re-ya-ta-mde — the lake back from the river. (018-007)

Gordon further notes the [[dakota|Dakota]] names for the surrounding lakes, placing Lake Calhoun within a network of indigenous geographical knowledge:

> The lake nearest Calhoun on the north — Lake of the Isles — they called Wí-ta Mdé — Island-Lake. (018-010)

### 双重命名

Gordon's footnote exemplifies the dual-naming pattern throughout his work: the official American name (Lake Calhoun, honoring a distant political figure) sits alongside the descriptive [[dakota|Dakota]] names (Mdé-mdó-za, meaning Loon Lake, and Re-ya-ta-mde, the lake back from the river). This layering of names reflects the broader cultural encounter that Gordon's poetry documents.

## 历史/文化背景

John C. Calhoun was one of the most influential American politicians of the antebellum period. As Secretary of War under President James Monroe (1817–1825), he oversaw the westward expansion of U.S. military infrastructure, including the establishment of [[fort-snelling|Fort Snelling]] (completed 1825) at the confluence of the Mississippi and [[minnesota|Minnesota]] rivers. It was during this period of Upper Mississippi exploration and fortification that his name was attached to the lake.

Lake Calhoun was officially renamed Bde Maka Ska in 2018, restoring a [[dakota|Dakota]] name meaning "White Earth Lake." Gordon's footnote preserves a third [[dakota|Dakota]] name: Mdé-mdó-za (Loon Lake), illustrating the rich and varied [[dakota|Dakota]] toponymy that predated American settlement.
