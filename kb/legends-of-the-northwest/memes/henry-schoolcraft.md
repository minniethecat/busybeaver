Henry Rowe Schoolcraft (1793–1864) was an American geographer, geologist, and ethnologist, noted for his pioneering studies of Native American cultures, particularly the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]]) people of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s region. His published collections of Native American [[legend|legends]] served as primary source material for both [[Henry Wadsworth Longfellow|Henry Longfellow]]'s "Hiawatha" and [[Hanford Lennox Gordon|H. L. Gordon]]'s "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest."

## 身份与背景

- **Tribe / Nation**: American (European descent)
- **Role**: Ethnologist, geologist, Indian agent, author
- **Family**: Married a descendant of [[waub-ojeeg|Waub-Ojeeg]], a noted [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] chief (018-153)

## 在书中的角色

### 作为 Gordon 的学术来源

Gordon relied on Schoolcraft's ethnographic collections for several [[legend|legends]] included in "[[legend|Legend]]s of the Northwest." In his notes to the [[segun|Según]] legend, Gordon acknowledges that both he and Longfellow drew from the same source:

> I have compared it with Schoolcraft's version, from which Mr. Longfellow evidently took his. (019-007)

### 记录 Ampata Sapa 传说

In Gordon's notes to "The [[legend|Legend]] of [[falls-of-st-anthony|the Falls]]," Schoolcraft is cited as recording a different version of the [[dakota|Dakota]] mother's name:

> Schoolcraft calls her "Ampata Sapa." Ampata is not Dakota. (012-189)

This footnote reveals Gordon's critical engagement with Schoolcraft's work — he notes linguistic inaccuracies while still relying on Schoolcraft's collected materials.

### 与 Waub-Ojeeg 家族的联姻

Schoolcraft's marriage into the family of [[waub-ojeeg|Waub-Ojeeg]], a noted [[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]] chief, gave him privileged access to [[ojibway|Ojibway]] oral [[tradition|traditions]]:

> Schoolcraft married one of his descendants. (018-153)

## 历史/文化背景

Henry Schoolcraft served as a U.S. Indian agent in the Michigan Territory and later as Superintendent of Indian Affairs. His major works include *Algic Researches* (1839) and the six-volume *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1851–1857). His collection of Native American [[legend|legends]], published as *Algic Researches* and later expanded, directly inspired Longfellow's "The [[song|Song]] of Hiawatha" (1855).

Schoolcraft's work is both celebrated for preserving Native oral [[tradition|traditions]] and critiqued for his editorial modifications of the source material — a tension that Gordon himself implicitly acknowledges in his footnotes.
