# Lodge

The lodge is the traditional dwelling of the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] peoples, serving as both a domestic home and a site for ceremonial gatherings in Gordon's [[legend|legends]].

## Definition / Identity

A lodge is a traditional Native American dwelling structure, typically constructed of poles covered with bark, hides, or mats. In the context of Gordon's [[legend|legends]], the lodge appears in multiple forms:

- **Origin**: Indigenous to the [[dakota|Dakota]] and [[ojibway|Ojibway]] peoples of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s and Upper Mississippi regions
- **Language**: [[dakota|Dakota]] / [[ojibway|Ojibway]]
- **Domain**: dwelling / ceremonial / [[soul|spirit]]ual

Gordon uses "lodge" broadly — it can be a family home, a council gathering place, or a metaphorical dwelling in the [[soul|spirit]] world. The "Council-lodge" appears as a political meeting place (001-213), while the "broad, blue lodge in the [[spirit|Spirit]] land" represents the afterlife dwelling of the deceased (002-110).

## In the Book

The lodge is one of the most frequently mentioned structures in Gordon's poems, appearing across multiple [[legend|legends]] as a setting for key narrative moments.

### Role in the Legends

The lodge serves as a domestic center where characters gather, depart, and return. When [[wiwaste|Wiwâstè]] sits "late in the lodge alone" awaiting her lover (002-066), the lodge becomes a space of longing and anticipation. When [[chaske|Chaskè]] brings his bride to his lodge (003-053), it marks the transition to married life.

The lodge also carries [[soul|spirit]]ual significance. Gordon describes the "broad, blue lodge in the [[spirit|Spirit]] land" where the dead reside (002-110), and a mother who "looks from her lodge above" watching over her son (002-143). The soul of the deceased travels "to her happy lodge in the [[spirit|spirit-land]]" (002-147), merging the domestic and the sacred.

In moments of grief, the lodge becomes a place of solitude — a chief "in his lodge he sat in his silent grief" (004-075), looking out afar at the world he can no longer join.

### Representative Passages

> "From the Council-lodge to the welkin starry" (001-213)

> "To the broad, blue lodge in the Spirit land" (002-110)

> "Thy mother looks from her lodge above" (002-143)

> "In his lodge he sat in his silent grief" (004-075)
