# Mississippi Flood

The seasonal flooding of the [[mississippi-river|Mississippi River]], a natural event with [[soul|spirit]]ual significance.

The Mississippi — Michesee Seebee in the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] tongue (010-116) — was both a life-giving waterway and a force of nature. The river's moods feature in the Prelude, where [[spirit|spirits]]' calls echo across its waters (001-020).

## In Gordon's Legends

Gordon's [[legend|legends]] capture these events within the broader narrative of [[dakota|Dakota]] life. His preface notes his study of [[dakota|Dakota]] [[tradition|customs]] (P01-007) and his debt to Riggs' ethnographic work (P01-063).

## In Poetry and Legend

The Mississippi's flood waters feature prominently in the Dakota legends Gordon preserves. In the Prelude, the river appears as a living presence whose moods echo the actions of spirit-beings (001-020). The mighty Heyóka, the anti-natural god, floats on the flood and walks in the skies, ever appearing in strange disguise (002-004–005; 002-020–021).

At the [[falls-of-st-anthony|Falls of St. Anthony]], the river's power is most dramatically rendered:

> Where his foaming floods tumble and roar, on the falls and white rolling rapids —
>
> (006-021–022)

The turbulent flood at the "Father of Waters" forms the backdrop against which Winona's tragedy unfolds (006-223–224). When the spirit speaks in the legend, even the floods grow still and listen (002-133–134).
