# Algonkin

Algonkin (also Algonquian) refers to the language family spoken by the [[ojibway|Ojibway]] ([[ojibway-nation|Chippewa]]) and related peoples of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s and upper Mississippi regions — distinct from the Siouan [[dakota|Dakota]] language.

## Linguistic Identity

Gordon distinguishes between the two major linguistic and cultural groups that appear in his [[legend|legends]]:

- **[[dakota|Dakota]]**: A Siouan-speaking confederation of the upper Mississippi plains
- **[[ojibway|Ojibway]] / Algonkin**: An Algonquian-speaking people of the [[lake-superior|Great Lake]]s forests

The [[ojibway-nation|Ojibways]] are explicitly identified as Algonkin speakers in the text (009-153). Gordon's footnotes reference [[michabo|Michabo]] — "the Good, [[wakan|Great Spirit]] of the Algonkins" (018-115), highlighting the distinct [[soul|spirit]]ual [[tradition|traditions]] of the Algonkin-speaking peoples.

## In the Book

### Place Names

The [[ojibway|Ojibway]], as Algonkin speakers, gave their own names to the region's major geographical features. These names appear throughout Gordon's text:

- *Michesee Seebee* — "Big River," the Mississippi (010-116)
- *[[lake-superior|Michee Gumee]]* — "[[lake-superior|Great Lake]]," [[lake-superior|Lake Superior]] (010-118)
- The [[st-louis-river|St. Louis River]] also bears an [[ojibway|Ojibway]] name (010-119)

### Cultural Context

At the time period depicted in the [[legend|legends]], the [[dakota|Dakota]] and Algonkin-speaking [[ojibway|Ojibway]] were rivals competing for territory and resources in the upper Mississippi region. This inter-tribal conflict — between the prairie-dwelling [[dakota|Dakotas]] and the forest-dwelling Algonkin peoples — forms the dramatic backdrop for several of the [[legend|legends]], particularly *[[winona|Winona]]*.
