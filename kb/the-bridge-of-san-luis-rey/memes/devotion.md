# Devotion

## Definition

Devotion in *The Bridge of San Luis Rey* occupies the complex territory between [[love]] and duty. It is love institutionalized—love that persists when feeling falters. The [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess]] is devoted to her mission long after it ceases to bring her joy; [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] is devoted to the Perichole long after she scorns his guidance; [[pepita|Pepita]] is devoted to a Marquesa who barely notices her. Devotion is the novel's answer to the fragility of passion: it is what remains when love is no longer spontaneous but chosen.

## In the Novel

**The Abbess Madre María del Pilar** embodies devotion as vocation. Her loyalty to "her mother in the Lord" and her sense of duty (002-053) sustain decades of thankless work on behalf of Lima's forgotten women. The Abbess's devotion is not warm or sentimental—she is described as having "fallen in love with an idea several centuries before its appointed appearance" (002-050)—but it is inexhaustible.

**Pepita's devotion** is the novel's purest form. A child raised in the Abbess's care, she serves the difficult [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]] with "a sense of duty and her loyalty" (002-053) that the Marquesa never fully recognizes. Pepita's devotion is invisible until after her death, when the Abbess's grief reveals how much that quiet loyalty meant.

**Uncle Pio's devotion** to [[camila-perichole|Camila]] survives everything: her ingratitude, her cruelty to him, her decline. "She was sturdily loyal. They loved one another deeply but without communication" (004-014). His devotion is the novel's most extravagant—a life given to another's art—and its most tragic, ending on the bridge with her son.

**Camila after the bridge** learns what devotion costs. After her beauty is gone, she realizes "she need look for no more devotion now that her beauty had passed" (004-081). The devotion she once commanded as tribute now appears as what it always was: contingent on her power to please. Only the Abbess's devotion remains unconditional.

The novel raises the question: is devotion noble or pathological? The Marquesa's devotion to her daughter is indistinguishable from obsession. Uncle Pio's devotion is his greatness and his destruction. The Abbess's devotion is the closest the novel comes to sainthood.
