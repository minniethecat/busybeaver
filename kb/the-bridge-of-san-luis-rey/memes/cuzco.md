# Cuzco

## Overview

Cuzco is the ancient Inca capital, located at the eastern end of the high road from [[lima|Lima]] across the Peruvian Andes. The [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]] stood on this road, and "hundreds of persons passed over it every day" (001-002) — travellers, merchants, officials, and pilgrims moving between the viceregal capital and the old imperial city. Cuzco serves as an important offstage presence in the novel: it is a place of scholarship, political sensitivity, and refuge, drawing several of the novel's central characters toward it in moments of crisis or purpose.

## In the Narrative

### The High Road

The Bridge of San Luis Rey was "on the high road between Lima and Cuzco" (001-002), and the bridge itself had been "woven of osier by the Incas more than a century before" (001-002) — a detail that links the engineering of the road and bridge to Cuzco's Inca heritage. Visitors to the city were "always led out to see it" (001-002), suggesting that the bridge was regarded as one of the marvels on the approach to the ancient capital.

### Esteban's Refuge

After the death of his twin brother [[manuel|Manuel]], [[esteban|Esteban]] wandered the Peruvian interior in grief — "strayed with eyes like coals along the dried-up beds of rivers or through the great ruins of the old race" (003-105). Eventually he found work in Cuzco, "doing some copying for the University" (003-105). It was there that [[captain-alvarado|Captain Alvarado]], sent by the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess Madre María del Pilar]], went to find him and persuade him to embark on a voyage around the world (003-105). When Alvarado located Esteban, he told him: "You know Lima and Cuzco and the road. You have nothing more to know about them. You see it's the ocean you want" (003-160) — urging him to leave the familiar circuit of colonial Peru behind.

### Uncle Pio's Travels

[[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]], the adventurer and impresario who moved through every stratum of Peruvian society, also travelled to Cuzco. [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]], recalling their shared past, asked him: "You remember the night you came back from Cuzco?" (004-033), suggesting that his return from the old Inca capital marked a moment of significance in their long and complicated relationship.

### A Politically Charged City

Cuzco also carried political weight in the colonial imagination. In a letter from Spain, the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]]'s daughter [[dona-clara|Doña Clara]] reproached her mother for indiscreet remarks: "Nothing could be more ill-judged than your remarks on the you-know-what-I-mean at Cuzco. Such remarks are not funny ... and they might get us into a great deal of trouble with Certain Persons here in Spain" (002-011). The warning reveals that Cuzco was a subject of political sensitivity — perhaps linked to indigenous unrest or the tensions between colonial administrators and the old Inca nobility — and that loose words about it could carry consequences across the Atlantic.

## Historical Context

Cuzco (also spelled Cusco, from the Quechua *Qosqo*) was the historic capital of the Inca Empire, a city of monumental stone architecture and ceremonial plazas that served as the administrative and spiritual center of the largest empire in pre-Columbian America. After the Spanish conquest in the 16th century, Cuzco declined in political importance relative to Lima — the new viceregal capital on the coast — but it retained its cultural prestige and its university, the University of San Antonio Abad (founded 1692), where Esteban found work as a copyist. Wilder's novel captures this historical moment: Cuzco as a city of the old order, still resonant with Inca memory, now a provincial outpost of the Spanish Empire where a broken man could disappear into copying work and where a sharp remark could still alarm the authorities in Madrid.
