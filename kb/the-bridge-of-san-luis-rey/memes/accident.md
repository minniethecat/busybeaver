# Accident

## Definition

The [[collapse-of-the-bridge|collapse of the Bridge of San Luis Rey]] on July 20, 1714 is both the novel's inciting event and its central philosophical problem. "Either we live by accident and die by accident, or we live by plan and die by plan" (001-006)—this is the question that [[brother-juniper|Brother Juniper]]'s investigation seeks to answer, and that Wilder leaves fundamentally open. The concept of accident in the novel stands in productive tension with Providence: the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|bridge]]'s failure could be sheer random catastrophe, or it could be the visible edge of a divine pattern. The novel refuses to resolve this tension, instead insisting on "the bridge of love, the only bridge that survives" (005-018).

## The Accident/Design Spectrum

| Position | Character | Key Claim |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Divine design | Brother Juniper | Each life was "a perfect whole" (001-010); the accident was God's precision |
| Ambiguous faith | The Abbess | "There is a land of the living and a land of the dead, and the bridge is love, the only survival, the only meaning" (005-050)) |
| Stoic acceptance | Doña Clara | Refuses to investigate; lives on in quiet dignity |
| Penitential transformation | Camila Perichole | The accident as personal judgment; dedicates life to service |
| Empirical doubt | The narrator | "We shall never know" — the novel's final word |

## In the Novel

The novel opens with the accident itself: "On Friday noon, July the twentieth, 1714, the finest bridge in all Peru broke and precipitated five travellers into the gulf below" (001-002). This moment implicates five apparently unrelated lives—the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]], [[pepita|Pepita]], [[esteban|Esteban]], [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]], and [[don-jaime|Don Jaime]]—uniting them in death while leaving their survivors to grapple with meaning.

Brother Juniper, who happened to witness the accident, finds in it a theological laboratory: "It seemed to Brother Juniper that it was high time for theology to take its place among the exact sciences, and he had long intended putting it there. What he had lacked hitherto was a laboratory" (001-007). His six-year investigation seeks to prove that "each of the five lost lives was a perfect whole" (001-010)—that the accident was in fact a moment of divine precision.

The novel's structure embodies the accident/design tension: each Part examines a life that was cut off, revealing the intricate patterns of [[love]], obsession, and transformation that preceded the collapse. Yet the collision of these lives on the bridge remains fundamentally unexplained. The bridge was "a mere ladder of thin slats swung out over the gorge" (001-002)—a fragile thing whose failure could be purely material, purely divine, or somewhere in between.

The novel's conclusion at the Convent of Santa María Rosa de las Rosas returns to the question obliquely. The Abbess's meditation—"There is a land of the living and a land of the dead, and the bridge is love, the only survival, the only meaning" (005-050))—suggests that whether we live by accident or design matters less than whether we love.
