## Overview

Peru is the primary geographic setting of *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*. In 1714, when the novel opens, Peru was a Spanish viceroyalty. The story unfolds across its landscape — Lima, the capital; the high road to Cuzco; and the gorge spanned by the bridge of San Luis Rey.

## Description

The novel opens with Peru's most famous catastrophe: "On Friday noon, July the twentieth, 1714, the finest bridge in all Peru broke and precipitated five travellers into the gulf below" (001-002). This bridge "was on the high road between Lima and Cuzco and hundreds of persons passed over it every day" (001-002).

Peru's colonial society was hierarchical and complex. "All the wealth of Peru would have been insufficient to maintain her in the grand manner" (002-005) — referring to the Marquesa's ambitions. The land was home to "the sons and daughters of Adam from Cathay to Peru" (002-010), reflecting its diverse population.

Lima, the capital, is the center of social life: the Marquesa, "left alone in Lima... grew more and more inward" (002-004). The city contained the Viceroy's palace, the Comedia theatre, the Convent of Santa María Rosa de las Rosas, and the bridge that led travelers toward Cuzco.

## In the Narrative

Brother Juniper, a Franciscan from Italy, witnesses the bridge's collapse. "It prompted him to busy himself for six years, knocking at all the doors in Lima" (001-010), collecting testimony about the five victims. The bridge connects two worlds — Lima's colonial society and the ancient Inca capital of Cuzco.

The Peruvian setting shapes the novel's themes. The bridge itself was "woven of osier by the Incas more than a century before" (001-002), a remnant of the pre-colonial world still serving the colonial one. The social fabric, like the bridge, is revealed to be fragile.

## Inhabitants

Peru's diverse population includes Spanish aristocrats like the Marquesa de Montemayor; celebrated performers like the Perichole; orphaned twins Esteban and Manuel; the wise Abbess Madre María del Pilar; and indigenous servants and laborers. "Everyone drank chicha in Peru and there was no particular disgrace in being found unconscious on a feast day" (002-023).
