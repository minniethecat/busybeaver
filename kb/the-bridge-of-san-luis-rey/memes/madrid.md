# Madrid

## Overview

Madrid is the far shore of the novel's emotional geography — the city where [[condesa-dona-clara|Doña Clara]] lives at court, where the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]]'s letters arrive, and from which Doña Clara's brief, cool replies return to [[lima|Lima]]. It is less a particular place than the idea of a place: the metropole against which colonial Peru measures itself, the "there" that makes Lima feel like "here." When [[captain-alvarado|Captain Alvarado]] says "All the world is alike, Madrid or Lima" (004-072), he collapses the distance the novel has spent pages establishing — and in doing so, exposes how much that distance has mattered.

## In the Narrative

Madrid enters the novel primarily through Doña Clara. After leaving Lima, she settles at the Spanish court, where "all the wealth of Peru would not have bought her the atmosphere which her presence gave to the palace" (002-005). The court is a world of refinement and intelligence that the Marquesa can only imagine, and her letters are in part an attempt to prove herself worthy of it.

The Marquesa visits Madrid once: "On both sides the visit was anticipated with resolutions well nourished on self-reproach: the one to be patient, the other to be undemonstrative. Both failed" (002-006). Madrid becomes the scene of a failed reunion — the place where mother and daughter discover that proximity cannot heal what distance has broken.

[[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] also carries Madrid within him. He "ran away to Madrid from his father's hacienda" at age ten (004-009), and the city shaped his cosmopolitanism. He brought to Lima a knowledge of Spanish theatre, literature, and court intrigue that was rooted in his years in the capital.

## Madrid and Lima

The novel's two cities — Madrid and Lima — form a pair of cultural poles. Madrid is the old world: courtly, literary, sophisticated, and distant. Lima is the new world: raw, religious, vital, and immediate. The Marquesa's letters cross between them, and in the crossing they become art. Captain Alvarado's dismissal — "All the world is alike, Madrid or Lima" — is both true (human nature does not change with longitude) and false (the novel's entire plot depends on the distance between them).
