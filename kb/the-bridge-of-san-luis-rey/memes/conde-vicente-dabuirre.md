# Conde Vicente d'Abuirre

## Overview

The Conde Vicente d'Abuirre is the Spanish nobleman who married Doña Clara, the daughter of the Marquesa de Montemayor. Though he never appears directly in the novel, his presence is felt throughout the Correspondence: he is the unintended patron of Spanish literature, for "it is to the son-in-law that we owe their preservation" (002-007). A man of wit and political influence, he delighted in the Marquesa's letters without fully grasping their depth—"missing (as most readers do) the whole purport of literature, which is the notation of the heart" (002-008).

## Role in the Novel

The Conde serves three crucial functions in the narrative. First, he is the political counterweight that shields the Marquesa from the Inquisition: "It is not impossible that she might have been burned had her son-in-law been less influential in Spain" (002-004). Second, his preservation of the letters transforms Doña María's desperate attempt to reach her daughter into a monument of Spanish literature—letters that within a century of her death "had become one of the monuments of Spanish literature" (002-002). Third, his misunderstanding of those letters embodies one of the novel's central themes: the gap between style and substance, between the vessel and "the bitter liquid" it contains (002-008).

## Relationship with the Marquesa

The Marquesa's letters frequently address Don Vicente with warmth and anxious concern. In Letter XXII she pleads: "Don Vicente, I implore you to make my child see reason" (002-013), revealing her dependence on him as an intermediary between herself and her distant daughter. She receives his "affectionate and jocose note" (002-061) alongside her daughter's wounding letters, suggesting a warmer relationship with her son-in-law than with Doña Clara herself.

## Political Significance

By the time of the novel's events, Don Vicente had become "an increasingly important personage in Spain, laden with possibilities of injury to the Viceroy, nay with the possibility of supplanting him" (002-021). This political weight explains the Viceroy's insistence that the Perichole apologize to the Marquesa after the theatre incident—"The Conde Vicente d'Abuirre must not be vexed, even through his half-wit mother-in-law" (002-021).

## The Preservation of the Letters

The novel's narrator notes that Doña Clara "barely glanced at the letters" (002-007). It was the Conde who recognized their literary value—if only for their style, not their soul. "The Conde delighted in her letters, but he thought that when he had enjoyed the style he had extracted all their richness and intention" (002-008). His act of preservation, though motivated by aesthetic rather than emotional appreciation, ensured the survival of what became textbooks for Spanish schoolboys and the delight of grammarians.

## Epilogue

After the bridge collapse, Doña Clara—now the Condesa d'Abuirre (005-040)—returns from Spain to visit the Abbess Madre María del Pilar. In this final chapter, she makes "a long passionate defense of Doña María" (005-046), suggesting that through her husband's preservation of the letters, she has finally come to understand the mother she neglected in life. The Conde's unwitting gift to literature becomes, in the end, a bridge between mother and daughter that neither could build in life.
