# Scribe

## Definition

In *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, the **scribe** is a professional copyist and letter-writer in 18th-century colonial Peru, a society with "few printing presses in the New World" [003-002]. The profession is embodied by the twin brothers Manuel and Esteban, who make their living "transcribing comedies for the theatre, ballads for the crowds, and advertisements for the merchants" — and above all, serving as "the copyists of the choirmasters" [003-002]. The scribe's work sits at the intersection of literacy, commerce, and intimacy in the novel.

## In the Novel

The profession of scribe defines the social position and narrative arc of the twin brothers in Part Three. Found as infants at the [[convent-of-santa-maria-rosa-org]], Manuel and Esteban grow up in the shadow of the Church but "showed no desire for the clerical life" [003-002]. Instead, they drift into scribal work — a trade that places them at the margins of Lima's cultural life: close enough to copy the great motets of Morales and Vittoria, close enough to transcribe comedies for the [[theatre]], but never participants in the world of letters.

The scribe's craft becomes a narrative hinge when Manuel is hired by the [[camila-perichole]] to write her secret love letters [003-048]. This act of professional writing transfigures into personal catastrophe: Manuel falls in love with the actress through the intimacy of taking dictation, which drives a wedge between the inseparable brothers. Esteban's jealousy and Manuel's shame cascade toward Manuel's infection and death, and Esteban's subsequent wandering.

After Esteban's grief-driven wanderings across Peru, the Abbess learns that he "was doing some copying for the University" in Cuzco [003-105] — the scribe's trade becoming his last known anchor before he returns to Lima and crosses the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey]].

The scribe thus functions in the novel as a figure of mediation: between speech and writing, between the powerful and the literate, between brothers, and ultimately between life and the record left behind.

## Key Passages

> They gradually assumed the profession of the scribe. There were few printing presses in the New World, and the boys soon made a fair living transcribing comedies for the theatre, ballads for the crowds, and advertisements for the merchants. Above all they were the copyists of the choirmasters and made endless parts of the motets of Morales and Vittoria. [003-002]

> They had returned to the city and resumed the copying of parts for the theatre. One night the manager, foreseeing a thinning house, gave them a free admission. [003-007]

> Esteban assumed that the call was for copyist's work and was therefore utterly unprepared for a visit that they received. [003-048]
