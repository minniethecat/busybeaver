# University of San Martín

## Overview

The University of San Martín is a colonial university in [[lima|Lima]], appearing in the novel's frame narrative (Part I) and in its philosophical resolution (Part V). It serves two crucial functions in the architecture of Wilder's story: it is the final resting place of [[brother-juniper|Brother Juniper]]'s secret manuscript, and it is the home of the master whose skeptical worldview gave Brother Juniper "the hint for his procedure" after the [[collapse-of-the-bridge|collapse of the Bridge of San Luis Rey]] (005-014). Through the university and its master, Wilder introduces the novel's central philosophical tension — between the belief in a divinely guided world and the suspicion that "all was wrong" (005-003).

## In the Narrative

### The Secret Manuscript

After Brother Juniper spent six years investigating the lives of the five victims of the bridge collapse, he produced "an enormous book" cataloguing "thousands of little facts and anecdotes and testimonies" and concluding with "a dignified passage describing why God had settled upon that person and upon that day for His demonstration of wisdom" (001-011). The book was publicly burned in the great square of Lima — condemned as heretical. But a secret copy survived, and "after a great many years and without much notice it found its way to the library of the University of San Martín," where it lies "between two great wooden covers collecting dust in a cupboard" (001-011). The narrator notes wryly that "for all his diligence, Brother Juniper never knew the central passion of Doña María's life; nor of Uncle Pio's, not even of Esteban's" (001-011) — and then turns the question back upon himself, and upon the reader: "And I, who claim to know so much more, isn't it possible that even I have missed the very spring within the spring?" (001-011).

### The Master of San Martín

Brother Juniper "would never have arrived at his method had it not been for his friendship with a certain master in the University of San Martín" (005-003). This master — whose wife had abandoned him for a soldier, leaving him with two infant daughters — "was possessed of all the bitterness that Brother Juniper lacked, and derived a sort of joy from the conviction that all was wrong in the world" (005-003). He would whisper into the Franciscan's ear "such thoughts and anecdotes as belied the notion of a guided world" (005-003), testing Brother Juniper's faith with stories of unanswered prayers and undeserved suffering. Each time, Brother Juniper would patiently explain why "such stories held no difficulty for a believer" (005-003).

It was another story of this same master — "not so subversive, this one" — that "probably gave to Brother Juniper the hint for his procedure after the fall of the bridge of San Luis Rey" (005-014). The story tells of the master walking through the [[cathedral-of-lima|Cathedral of Lima]], pausing to read a woman's epitaph that praised her as "the centre and joy of her home" and "the delight of her friends" (005-015). In a rage born of his own disappointments, the master spoke aloud: "The shame of it, the persecution of it! Everyone knows that in the world we do nothing but feed our wills. Why perpetuate this legend of selflessness? Why keep this thing alive, this rumour of disinterestedness?" (005-015). This radical skepticism — the doubt that any human life is truly virtuous — became the intellectual spark for Brother Juniper's doomed attempt to prove, scientifically, that the bridge collapse was an act of divine justice.

## Historical Context

The novel's University of San Martín is likely a fictional institution — Wilder does not name an actual colonial university. In historical Lima, the principal institution of higher learning was the University of San Marcos (Universidad Nacional Mayor de San Marcos), founded in 1551 and the oldest continuously operating university in the Americas. Wilder may have deliberately renamed the institution to give himself latitude in creating the character of the skeptical master — or the fictional university may suggest that the questions the novel raises about divine providence belong to no single institution or creed.
