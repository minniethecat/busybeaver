# Don Jaime

## Overview

Don Jaime is the novel's briefest life—[[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]]'s seven-year-old son, taken by [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] in a desperate act of devotion and killed with him on the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]]. "Don Jaime, at seven years, was a rachitic little body who seemed to have inherited not only his mother's forehead and eyes, but the stoop of Uncle Pio" (004-052). He is the novel's most innocent victim, a child caught in the machinery of adult passions who barely exists as a character before he ceases to exist as a person.

## Biography

Born to Camila and the [[viceroy-don-andres|Viceroy]] (or possibly Uncle Pio), Don Jaime is raised on his mother's country estate. Uncle Pio, banished from Camila's presence, transfers his devotion to the boy—seeing in him the potential for the cultivation he once gave Camila. His abduction of Jaime is an act of love that destroys what it intends to save.

## Relationships

- **[[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]]** — his mother; her grief for him drives her final descent
- **[[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]]** — his abductor and devotee; dies with him on the bridge

## Themes

Don Jaime represents innocence cut short—a life that has barely begun, extinguished before it can take shape. His death is the novel's starkest argument against the idea that every death occurs at its appointed time.
