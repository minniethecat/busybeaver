# Marquesa de Montemayor

## Overview

Doña María, Marquesa de Montemayor, is the novel's deepest character study and the subject of its longest chapter (Part II). An ugly, stuttering daughter of a cloth-merchant who married into nobility, she pours her thwarted capacity for [[love]] into an obsessive devotion to her cold and intellectual daughter [[dona-clara|Clara]]—producing, in the process, letters that "within a century of her death had become one of the monuments of Spanish literature" (002-002). She is the novel's central illustration of love's power to create and destroy, and her transformation on the night before her death is the narrative's most redemptive moment.

## Biography

Born the daughter of a cloth-merchant in [[lima|Lima]], Doña María's childhood was "unhappy; she was ugly; she stuttered; her mother persecuted her with sarcasms" (002-003). At twenty-six, cornered into marriage with a "supercilious and ruined nobleman," she transferred all her emotional energy to her daughter Clara—an "idolatrous love" that Clara, cold and intellectual, returned with "astonishment and repulsion."

When Clara chose a marriage requiring removal to [[spain|Spain]], the Marquesa's life "grew more and more inward" (002-004). Her letters became her lifeline—works of art produced from desperation. "She forced herself to go out into society in order to cull its ridicules; she taught her eye to observe; she read the masterpieces of her language to discover its effects" (002-007). The knowledge that her daughter barely read them did not stop her.

On the night before her death, after reading a letter from the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess]], the Marquesa confronts her own selfishness. Acknowledging that she "had never brought courage to either life or love" (002-083), she writes her famous Letter LVI, known as her "Second Corinthians" for its immortal paragraph about love. "Let me live now," she whispers. "Let me begin again." The next morning she crosses the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]] with [[pepita|Pepita]].

## Relationships

- **[[dona-clara|Doña Clara]]** — her daughter; the object of her obsessive love and the audience for her letters
- **[[pepita|Pepita]]** — the orphan girl who accompanies her; a silent witness she barely notices
- **[[abbess-madre-maria|The Abbess Madre María del Pilar]]** — her spiritual advisor; the author of the letter that prompts her transformation

## Themes

The Marquesa embodies love's duality: her devotion is both her greatness and her pathology. "She loved her daughter not for her daughter's sake, but for her own" (002-010)—this recognition is the beginning of wisdom. Her story asks whether a lifetime of wrong love can be redeemed by a single act of right love, and whether that redemption is sufficient if death follows immediately.

## Key Passages

> "She lived alone and she thought alone." (002-003)

> "She longed to be back in this simplicity of love, to throw off the burden of pride and vanity that hers had always carried." (002-066)

> "Let me live now. Let me begin again." (002-083)
