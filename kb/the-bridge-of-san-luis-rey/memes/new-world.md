# New World

The "New World" — the Spanish colonial empire in the Americas — is the broad geographic and political context within which the events of *The Bridge of San Luis Rey* unfold. [[peru|Peru]] in the early 18th century is a viceroyalty of [[spain|Spain]], and its society is shaped by the complex hierarchies of colonial rule: peninsulares (Spanish-born), criollos (American-born Spaniards), mestizos, indigenous peoples, and enslaved Africans. The novel registers these hierarchies without making them its explicit subject.

## In the Novel

The colonial world of the novel is stratified and hierarchical. At the top sits the [[viceroy-don-andres|Viceroy]], the King's representative in Lima, whose [[palace-of-the-viceroy|Palace]] is the center of worldly power. The [[archbishop-of-lima|Archbishop]] represents the ecclesiastical arm of empire. The [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa de Montemayor]] occupies the aristocracy, though her wealth is inherited from her father's cloth trade rather than ancient lineage. Below them, the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess Madre María del Pilar]] runs her convent as a refuge for women and orphans, while figures like [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] and the twin brothers [[esteban|Esteban]] and [[manuel|Manuel]] navigate the edges of respectability.

Wilder depicts this world with an anthropologist's detachment. The Viceroy's supper table includes the Archbishop, the Perichole, and Uncle Pio — a mixing of ranks that scandalizes Lima society. The bridge itself spans the road between Lima and [[cuzco|Cuzco]], connecting the colonial capital to the old Inca heartland.
