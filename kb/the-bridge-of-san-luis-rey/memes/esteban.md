# Esteban

## Overview

Esteban is one half of the twin brothers at the emotional center of [[part-iii|Part III]]. Found as infants in the foundlings' basket at the [[convent-of-santa-maria-rosa|Convent of Santa María Rosa de las Rosas]], he and his brother [[manuel|Manuel]] grow up in a silence that is both their bond and their armor. When Manuel falls in love with [[camila-perichole|the Perichole]] and then dies of an infected wound, Esteban discovers "that secret from which one never quite recovers, that even in the most perfect love one person loves less profoundly than the other" (003-010). His [[grief]] drives him toward self-destruction and ultimately to the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]], where he dies in the [[collapse-of-the-bridge|collapse]] on July 20, 1714.

## Biography

Esteban and Manuel are discovered as twin infants and raised by the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess Madre María del Pilar]]. "Because they had no family, because they were twins, and because they were brought up by women, they were silent" (003-003). As young men they work as scribes, then as laborers, always together, always silent. "Their sombre faces took on from these labors a male and gypsy cast" (003-005).

The arrival of the Perichole in Manuel's imagination shatters their unity. While Manuel is consumed by romantic obsession—"that crazy loss of one's self, that neglect of everything but one's dramatic thoughts about the beloved" (003-009)—Esteban is left watching his brother slip away. He discovers the asymmetry of love: "even in the most perfect love one person loves less profoundly than the other" (003-010).

When Manuel dies of an infected wound, Esteban's grief becomes self-destructive. He attempts suicide, is saved by [[captain-alvarado|Captain Alvarado]], and is given a position on a ship—a new life he cannot accept. "He was silent because there was nothing left to say" (003-019). He crosses the Bridge of San Luis Rey on the day it falls.

## Themes

Esteban represents the impossibility of surviving the loss of one's other self. His silence is not chosen but innate—a way of being that makes articulation of grief impossible, and therefore makes survival after loss impossible. His story is the novel's starkest illustration of how [[love]] and death are bound together.
