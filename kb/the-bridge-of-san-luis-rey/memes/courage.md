# Courage

## Definition

Courage in *The Bridge of San Luis Rey* is not physical bravery but moral courage—the courage to love without guarantee of return, to speak the truth when silence is easier, to begin again after a lifetime of wrong choices. "She had never brought courage to either life or love" (002-083)—this is the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]]'s self-indictment, and her Letter LVI is the novel's central act of courage: a first genuine attempt at generous love, written by a woman who has spent decades loving possessively.

## In the Novel

**The Marquesa de Montemayor** discovers courage at the end of her life. After reading the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess]]'s letter, she confronts the reality that she "had never brought courage to either life or love" (002-083). Her response is to write Letter LVI—her "first stumbling misspelled letter in courage"—a letter that asks for nothing, demands nothing, and simply loves. The courage is in the vulnerability: after a lifetime of armoring herself in wit and sarcasm, she opens herself to the possibility of being ignored.

**The Captain Alvarado** understands courage as a form of presence. After [[esteban|Esteban]]'s suicide attempt, the [[captain-alvarado|Captain]] reflects that "sometimes when it requires a high courage to speak the banal" (003-161), the ordinary words are the hardest to say. His courage is to offer companionship without demanding confession—a quiet gift of presence that Esteban ultimately cannot accept but that the novel honors nonetheless.

**Camila Perichole's courage** comes late and incompletely. After smallpox destroys her beauty, "she had been meaning to go forth each year and just once offer her courage to [[don-jaime|Jaime]] in his sufferings" (005-024). Her courage is the courage to stop performing, to inhabit her disfigured life without the armor of beauty and wit—a courage she achieves only fitfully.

**Brother Juniper** shows a different kind of courage: intellectual courage. His willingness to pursue his investigation to its logical conclusion, even when it leads to heresy and death, is a form of bravery the novel treats with complex respect. His courage is admirable in its integrity and tragic in its blindness.
