# Passion

Passion, in Thornton Wilder's *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, is the consuming force that drives human beings beyond the boundaries of reason, propriety, and self-interest. It manifests in many forms throughout the novel: the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa de Montemayor]]'s obsessive love for her daughter; [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]]'s lifelong devotion to cultivating [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]]'s talent; [[esteban|Esteban]]'s grief for his twin brother [[manuel|Manuel]]; and [[brother-juniper|Brother Juniper]]'s fervent desire to prove divine order through scientific inquiry. Passion in Wilder's vision is rarely rewarded — it is more often the engine of suffering and, paradoxically, the source of whatever meaning human life can attain.

## In the Novel

### The Marquesa's Devouring Love

The [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]]'s passion is the novel's most thoroughly documented case. Her love for her daughter [[dona-clara|Doña Clara]] is so overwhelming that it drives Clara away to [[spain|Spain]], where she marries and distances herself as thoroughly as possible. The Marquesa's famous letters — "the text-book of schoolboys and the ant-hill of the grammarians" (002-007) — are the product of a passion so intense it transforms into literature. She writes "at the galloping speed of the diccionario de la Real Academia" (002-018), pouring into her letters all the love her daughter refuses to receive in person.

Wilder's judgment on this passion is ambiguous. The letters are masterpieces, and they survive the bridge collapse to become monuments of Spanish literature. But the Marquesa's passion is also possessive and blind — she loves an image of her daughter rather than the real woman, and her letters, for all their brilliance, never succeed in bridging the distance between them. It is only in her final letter, written just before she crosses the bridge, that the Marquesa achieves a breakthrough: she accepts that her love need not be returned, and that loving is itself the gift.

### Uncle Pio's Cultivation of Beauty

[[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]]'s passion is less fraught but no less consuming. He discovers [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]] as a twelve-year-old singing in a café, and he devotes his entire life to shaping her into the greatest actress of her generation. "He divided the inhabitants of this world into two groups," Wilder tells us, "into those who had loved and those who had not" (004-050). Uncle Pio's love is not romantic but aesthetic — he loves beauty, and he sees in Camila the raw material for a masterpiece.

### Esteban's Grief

[[esteban|Esteban]]'s passion takes the form of grief. After [[manuel|Manuel]] dies from an infected wound, Esteban is consumed by a sorrow so absolute that he can barely eat, sleep, or speak. He takes Manuel's name and tries to live as his brother, as if by becoming Manuel he can undo the separation. The [[captain-alvarado|Captain Alvarado]] offers him a way out — a voyage to [[peru|Peru]] and beyond — but Esteban's grief is such that he seems almost relieved to die on the bridge.
