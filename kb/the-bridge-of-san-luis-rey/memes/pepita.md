# Pepita

## Overview

Pepita is the novel's invisible witness—the orphan girl raised by the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess Madre Maria del Pilar]], sent to serve the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa de Montemayor]], and killed on the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]]. She speaks almost no lines, leaves no letters, and is barely noticed by the Marquesa she serves. Yet her silent presence drives the Abbess's grief and becomes, in the Abbess's final vision, a symbol of all those "out in the dark" whom the world forgets. She is the novel's argument that the smallest lives matter as much as the grandest.

## Biography

Raised by the Abbess at the [[convent-of-santa-maria-rosa-org|Convent of Santa Maria Rosa de las Rosas]], Pepita is sent to the Marquesa's palace as a companion. "The beginnings of hope and affection that Pepita had so lately come to feel" (002-053) are barely established before she accompanies the Marquesa on the journey that ends at the bridge. She endures the Marquesa's neglect with "silent, bewildered" (002-053) loyalty, and she is present on the night the Marquesa writes her transformative Letter LVI—a witness to transformation she herself will not survive.

## Relationships

- **The [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess Madre Maria del Pilar]]** — her guardian; the one who grieves her
- **The [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa de Montemayor]]** — the woman she serves, who barely sees her

## Themes

Pepita represents the unnoticed—those whose lives are full of potential that death cuts short before it can be realized. "Almost no one remembers [[esteban|Esteban]] and Pepita," the Abbess thinks at the novel's close, "but myself" (005-050). Her death is the novel's argument that every life is worth remembering.
