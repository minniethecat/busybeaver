# Uncle Pio

## Overview

Uncle Pio is the novel's philosopher of [[love]] and [[beauty]]—a man who "divided the inhabitants of this world into two groups, into those who had loved and those who had not" (004-050). He discovers [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]] as a twelve-year-old café singer in [[lima|Lima]] and shapes her into the greatest actress of her generation, serving as her manager, teacher, and devoted shadow. His genius is not for creation but for cultivation: he recognizes dormant talent and dedicates himself to its flowering. In his final act, he takes [[don-jaime|Don Jaime]] from Camila's country retreat, intending to educate him as he once educated her. They die together on the [[bridge-of-san-luis-rey|Bridge of San Luis Rey]] in the [[collapse-of-the-bridge|collapse]] of July 20, 1714.

## Biography

Uncle Pio's origins are obscure. "He was born of good stock in Castile and fell into low company. He passed through the service of a dozen princes" (004-006). By the time he arrives in [[peru|Peru]], he has been a soldier, a spy, a trader, a forger, and a theatrical manager. His "reverence for beauty and charm was there for anyone to see" (004-011), and he cultivates it wherever he finds it—in actresses, viceroys, dogs, and birds.

His greatest project is Camila Perichole. He discovers her singing in a café, "set her endless exercises in acting and mimicry" (004-014), and guides her to fame. Their relationship is the novel's deepest study in [[devotion]]: "She was sturdily loyal. They loved one another deeply but without communication" (004-014). When she tires of him and dismisses him, he transfers his devotion to her son Don Jaime.

In his final act, he takes Jaime from Camila's country retreat, intending to educate him as he once educated her. Camila, in a fury, pursues them to Lima. Uncle Pio and Jaime cross the Bridge of San Luis Rey together.

## Themes

Uncle Pio's "own definition of love that was like no other" (004-050) treats love as "a sort of cruel malady through which the elect are required to pass in their late youth and from which they emerge, pale and wrung, but ready for the business of living." His personal taxonomy—"those who had loved and those who had not"—is the novel's starkest moral criterion. He is both its wisest character and its most deluded, a connoisseur who mistakes cultivation for connection.
