## Definition

Resignation, in the context of the novel, refers to a spiritual and emotional posture of acceptance — a yielding to circumstances, fate, or divine will. It is both a symptom of despair and a potential path toward grace, depending on the character and context.

## In the Novel

Resignation appears as a recurring motif across multiple characters. The Marquesa de Montemayor, after years of obsessive letter-writing and emotional turmoil, "at last resigned all pretense" (002-023), surrendering the social performances that had defined her life. Later, in her final moments: "she was listening to the new tide of resignation that was rising within her" (002-060).

For the twin brothers, resignation takes a different form. After Manuel's death, Esteban's language of grief is described in terms where "resignation was a word insufficient to describe the spiritual change that came over him" (003-004). The inadequacy of the word itself becomes significant — what Esteban experiences transcends mere resignation.

In Part Four, the Abbess counsels: "You must be resigned" (004-078), offering resignation as a spiritual discipline. But the novel complicates this simple prescription through its exploration of whether human suffering admits of any satisfying resignation.

The novel's conclusion offers a different vision. Brother Juniper, having spent six years trying to prove divine order in the bridge's collapse, "tore up his findings and cast them into the waves" (005-013) — an act that might be read as resignation, or as something more profound: an acceptance beyond resignation.

## Related Characters
