# Providence

## Definition

In *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, providence is the question that the novel refuses to settle: whether the events of a human life -- and a human death -- are governed by divine intention or by mere accident. The novel's two framing titles announce the ambiguity: "Perhaps an Accident" (001-001) opens the story, and "Perhaps an Intention" (005-001) closes it. Between these two "perhapses" lies the entire narrative, and Wilder never resolves the tension. Providence is what [[brother-juniper|Brother Juniper]] tries to prove scientifically, what the [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]] struggles to believe in her letters, and what [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]] ultimately rejects in favor of a simpler [[love]]. The novel's final sentence -- "There is a land of the living and a land of the dead and the bridge is love, the only survival, the only meaning" (005-050) -- suggests that the search for providence may be less important than the act of loving attention that the search inspires.

## In the Novel

### Brother Juniper's Experiment

Brother Juniper, a Franciscan friar from Northern Italy, witnesses the bridge collapse and immediately asks: "Why did this happen to *those five*?" (001-006). His reasoning is that "if there were any plan in the universe at all, if there were any pattern in a human life, surely it could be discovered mysteriously latent in those lives so suddenly cut off" (001-006). He resolves to make theology "take its place among the exact sciences" (001-007), spending six years interviewing everyone who knew the five victims and compiling an "enormous book" that catalogues "thousands of little facts and anecdotes and testimonies" (001-011). The narrator dryly observes that "coming from anyone but Brother Juniper this plan would be the flower of a perfect skepticism" (001-008) -- it resembles the Tower of Babel in its presumption. The book concludes that "the wicked visited by destruction and the good called early to Heaven" (005-019), but it is declared heretical and burned publicly with its author. Brother Juniper dies believing "the devil had made use of him to effect a brilliant campaign in Peru" (005-020).

### The Master of San Martin's Skepticism

Brother Juniper's method was shaped by his friendship with "a certain master in the [[university-of-san-martin|University of San Martin]]" (005-003), a bitter man whose wife had abandoned him. This master "derived a sort of joy from the conviction that all was wrong in the world" and whispered into the Franciscan's ear "such thoughts and anecdotes as belied the notion of a guided world" (005-003). It was another story of this master -- his rage against a woman's flattering epitaph in the [[cathedral-of-lima|Cathedral of Lima]] -- that "probably gave to Brother Juniper the hint for his procedure after the fall of the bridge" (005-014). The irony is profound: the method designed to prove divine providence was inspired by a radical skeptic who believed that "in the world we do nothing but feed our wills" (005-015).

### Camila's Awakening

For [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]], the bridge collapse is the third of three divine signs: "Her smallpox, Jaime's illness, and now the fall of the bridge -- oh, these were not accidents" (005-024). She interprets her suffering as a divine reproach for her vanity and worldliness, and she retreats to the [[convent-of-santa-maria-rosa|Convent of Santa Maria Rosa de las Rosas]] to serve the sick under the [[abbess-madre-maria|Abbess]]'s guidance. But the Abbess gently redirects her: "Even now, she thought, almost no one remembers Esteban and Pepita but myself. Camila alone remembers her Uncle Pio and her son... But soon we shall die and all memory of those five will have left the earth" (005-050). The Abbess's answer to the question of providence is not theological but human -- to remember, and to love.

### The Narrator's Verdict

The narrator, looking back across the centuries, admits that Brother Juniper's book missed "the central passion of Dona Maria's life; nor of Uncle Pio's, not even of Esteban's" (001-011). He then turns the question on himself: "And I, who claim to know so much more, isn't it possible that even I have missed the very spring within the spring?" (001-011). This recursive doubt is the novel's final word on providence: the search for divine meaning may be impossible, but the attempt reveals the depth and mystery of human lives.
