# Viceroy Don Andres de Ribera

## Overview

Don Andres de Ribera, Viceroy of [[peru|Peru]], is "the remnant of a delightful man, broken by the table, the alcove, a grandeeship and ten years of exile" (004-043). He is [[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]]'s patron and lover, [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]]'s sometime employer, and a figure whose institutional power masks personal emptiness. "From the grandeeship a pride so vast and puerile that he seldom heard anything that was said to him" (004-043)—he is the novel's study of how power hollows out the powerful.

## Biography

As a youth, Don Andres "had accompanied embassies to Versailles and Rome; he had fought in the wars in Austria; he had been to Jerusalem" (004-043). Exiled to Peru as Viceroy—a form of banishment disguised as promotion—he becomes a collector of "coins a little, wines, actresses, orders and maps." His relationship with Camila is his last genuine [[passion|passion]], but even that devolves into routine.

## Relationships

- **[[camila-perichole|Camila Perichole]]** — his mistress; the one person who might have reached him
- **[[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]]** — a fellow connoisseur; an employee he respects without understanding

## Themes

The Viceroy represents the tragedy of privilege without purpose. His life is full of everything but meaning—a cautionary figure whose emptiness the novel treats with more pity than judgment.
