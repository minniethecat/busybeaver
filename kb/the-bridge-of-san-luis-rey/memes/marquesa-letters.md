# The Marquesa's Letters

## Overview

The letters of the Marquesa de Montemayor are one of the great achievements of Spanish literature—and one of its great accidents. Written not for posterity but for the distant daughter who "barely glanced at the letters" (002-007), they were preserved by the son-in-law who admired their style without understanding their soul. Within a century of her death, they "had become one of the monuments of Spanish literature" (002-002), "the textbooks of schoolboys and the anthill of the grammarians" (002-007).

## Origin

The letters began as a substitute for presence. After a disastrous visit to Spain—"Each tortured the other and was on the point of losing her mind" (002-006)—Doña María returned to Peru, and "henceforth letter-writing had to take the place of all the affection that could not be lived" (002-006). She "forced herself to go out into society in order to cull its ridicules; she taught her eye to observe; she read the masterpieces of her language to discover its effects" (002-007), all to capture the attention of Doña Clara. "Night after night in her baroque palace she wrote and rewrote the incredible pages, forcing from her despairing mind those miracles of wit and grace" (002-007).

## Style and Substance

The letters are celebrated for their style, but the narrator insists this is to miss their point: "Style is but the faintly contemptible vessel in which the bitter liquid is recommended to the world" (002-008). The Marquesa "would even have been astonished to learn that her letters were very good, for such authors live always in the noble weather of their own minds, and those productions which seem remarkable to us are little better than a day's routine to them" (002-008).

## Notable Letters

### Letter XXII
The longest letter excerpted in the novel (002-011–002-018). Written after a visit to the theatre where the Perichole publicly humiliated her, it contains the famous passage about "walking into" a Velasquez painting to obtain a gold chain for the King of Spain—a surreal tour de force of imagination and flattery.

### Letter XLII
Written in response to Doña Clara's announcement of her pregnancy (002-055). This letter triggered the Marquesa's descent into superstition and religious mania: "She refused to allow a knot in the house" and practiced "a degrading system of taboos" (002-057), culminating in the pilgrimage to Santa María de Cluxambuqua.

### Letter LVI — "Second Corinthians"
Written on the night of the Marquesa's transformation at Cluxambuqua, after reading Pepita's unsent letter to the Abbess. Known to "the Encyclopedists as her Second Corinthians because of its immortal paragraph about love: 'Of the thousands of persons we meet in a lifetime, my child…'" (002-083). This was her "first letter, her first stumbling misspelled letter in courage" (002-083)—the letter in which she finally wrote not to possess her daughter but to free her.

### The Last Letter
After the bridge collapse, Doña Clara shows the Abbess "Doña María's last letter" (005-047). The Abbess "dared not say aloud how great her astonishment was that such words (words that since then the whole world has murmured over with joy) could spring in the heart of Pepita's mistress" (005-047).

## Preservation and Legacy

The irony of the letters' survival is central to the novel's vision. Doña Clara "barely glanced at the letters and...it is to the son-in-law that we owe their preservation" (002-007). The [[conde-vicente-dabuirre|Conde Vicente d'Abuirre]]—the very man who "thought that when he had enjoyed the style he had extracted all their richness and intention" (002-008)—became the unwitting patron of Spanish literature. The letters outlived the mother, the daughter, the bridge, and the very love they were written to sustain.

## Thematic Significance

The letters embody the novel's deepest paradox: that the most desperate, private, and failed act of love became a public monument to love itself. Doña María wrote to hold her daughter; instead she held the attention of generations. "Any Spanish schoolboy is required to know today more about Doña María, Marquesa de Montemayor, than Brother Juniper was to discover in years of research" (002-002)—yet neither the schoolboy nor Brother Juniper ever grasped what Pepita's unsent letter revealed: that courage, not style, is the true measure of a letter.
