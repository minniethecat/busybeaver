## Definition

**Chance**, in the philosophical inquiry of *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, represents the possibility that the bridge's collapse was a meaningless accident — random, unplanned, carrying no theological significance. It is the null hypothesis against which Brother Juniper's entire investigation strains. If chance governs the fall of bridges, then the five lives were cut short for no reason, and the universe is indifferent to human merit.

## In the Novel

The novel opens by establishing the bridge's collapse as a fact, then immediately pivots to the question of meaning. "Either we live by accident and die by accident, or we live by plan and die by plan" — this is the dichotomy the novel inherits from its source material and relentlessly interrogates.

Brother Juniper's project is a direct assault on chance. He gathers every scrap of evidence about the five victims' lives, believing that "if there were any pattern in the universe at all, any plan in a human life, surely it could be discovered in the lives of those five people." His method assumes that sufficient data will reveal design, rendering chance impossible.

But the novel's structure quietly subverts this confidence. The parallel biographies of the five victims do not converge on a single theological meaning; they diverge into irreducible particularity. The Abbess's final reflection — "There is a land of the living and a land of the dead and the bridge is love" — bypasses the chance-versus-design binary entirely, proposing a third term.

## Key Passages

> "Some say that to the gods we are like flies that boys idly kill on a summer's day; others say, on the contrary, that the very sparrow cannot fall to the ground without the will of Heaven." [001-001]

The novel's framing of its central question uses the language of chance ("idly kill") versus providence ("without the will of Heaven"). This opening gesture establishes chance as the novel's antagonist — the possibility it must contend with.

> "If there were any pattern in the universe at all, any plan in a human life, surely it could be discovered in the lives of those five people." [001-008]

Brother Juniper's wager is that design is demonstrable and chance is falsifiable. His failure — the Church burns his book — suggests the opposite: that the question exceeds human method.
