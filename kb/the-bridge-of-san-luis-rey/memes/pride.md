# Pride

## Definition

Pride in *The Bridge of San Luis Rey* operates as a double-edged force: it isolates characters from love and grace, yet it also preserves their dignity in a world that offers little kindness. The [[marquesa-de-montemayor|Marquesa]] must "throw off the burden of pride and vanity" (002-066) to write her first genuine letter; [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] is "proud in his loneliness" (004-014) as a defense against disappointment; the [[viceroy-don-andres|Viceroy]]'s pride is "so vast and puerile that he seldom heard anything that was said to him" (004-043). Wilder treats pride as both sin and survival strategy—a barrier to human connection that is also, sometimes, the only thing holding a person upright.

## In the Novel

**The Marquesa de Montemayor** wages her central battle against pride. Her letter-writing genius is inseparable from her vanity—she "forced herself to go out into society" and "insinuated herself into the company of those who were celebrated for their conversation" (002-007) to impress her distant daughter. But her Letter LVI, her "Second Corinthians," represents the moment she overcomes pride to write with "courage" rather than with need. "Let me live now," she whispers. "Let me begin again" (002-083).

**Uncle Pio's pride** is the pride of the connoisseur. "Proud in his loneliness, as though there resided a certain superiority in such a solitude" (004-014)—he constructs an identity around his aesthetic discernment, elevating himself above the world he serves. His pride enables his devotion to [[beauty]] but also distances him from ordinary human connection. He "divided the inhabitants of this world into two groups, into those who had loved and those who had not" (004-050)—a taxonomy born of pride as much as wisdom.

**The Viceroy, Don Andrés de Ribera**, embodies institutional pride. "From the grandeeship a pride so vast and puerile that he seldom heard anything that was said to him" (004-043). His pride is hollow—a performance of rank that masks a "remnant of a delightful man" worn down by power and exile.

**Doña Clara** exemplifies pride inherited rather than chosen. Her coldness toward her mother, her intellectual superiority, her deliberate choice of a marriage requiring removal to [[spain]]—all are expressions of pride that mirror and punish her mother's own.
