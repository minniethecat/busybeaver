# Auto-da-fé of Brother Juniper

## Overview

The auto-da-fé—the public burning of Brother Juniper and his enormous book—is the final event of the novel's historical frame and the moment where faith, reason, and human love converge in flame. "The book being done fell under the eyes of some judges and was suddenly pronounced heretical. It was ordered to be burned in the Square with its author" (005-020). On "a beautiful Spring morning in the great square" (001-011), the little red-haired Franciscan who had spent six years trying to prove God's wisdom was consumed by fire—smiling.

## Background

Brother Juniper's book, the product of six years of "knocking at all the doors in Lima, asking thousands of questions, filling scores of notebooks" (001-010), attempted to demonstrate mathematically that each of the five bridge victims was a "perfect whole" whose death manifested divine intention. The judges of the Inquisition pronounced it heretical. Brother Juniper "submitted to the decision that the devil had made use of him to effect a brilliant campaign in Peru" (005-020).

## The Night Before

In his cell on the last night, Brother Juniper tried "to seek in his own life the pattern that had escaped him in five others" (005-020). He was not rebellious—"He was willing to lay down his life for the purity of the church"—but "he longed for one voice somewhere to testify for him that his intention, at least, had been for faith; he thought there was no one in the world who believed him" (005-020).

## The Execution

The next morning revealed the depth of his error about being unloved. "In all that crowd and sunlight there were many who believed, for he was much loved" (005-020). A delegation came from the village of Puerto—including Nina ("Goodness 2, Piety 5, Usefulness 10")—who "stood with drawn puzzled faces while their little friar was given to the congenial flames" (005-021). In his final moment, Brother Juniper "not daring to call upon a greater name, since he seemed so open to error in these matters...called twice upon St. Francis and leaning upon a flame he smiled and died" (005-021).

## Aftermath

The burning did not destroy the book entirely. A secret copy survived and "after a great many years and without much notice it found its way to the library of the University of San Martín" (001-011). There "it lies between two great wooden covers collecting dust in a cupboard" (001-011)—waiting, the novel implies, for the narrator who would write the story we are reading.

## Significance

The auto-da-fé is the novel's most concentrated image of its central paradox: that the search for divine meaning in human suffering leads not to proof but to mystery, not to knowledge but to love. Brother Juniper's method failed—he never found the pattern—but his faith, tested to destruction, was vindicated not by his book but by the crowd who loved him. The Church burned the heretic; the novel canonizes him.
