# Spain

## Overview

Spain is the absent center of colonial [[lima|Lima]]'s cultural and emotional geography — a distant metropole whose influence reaches across the Atlantic to shape everything from the theatre to the mail. In *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, Spain is less a physical place than a gravitational field: it pulls [[condesa-dona-clara|Doña Clara]] away from her mother, draws the Marquesa's letters across the ocean, and provides the literary tradition — Calderón, Moreto, seventeenth-century Spanish drama — that fills the [[comedia|Comedia]] theatre and gives the [[camila-perichole|Perichole]] her repertoire. It is the "there" against which Peru's "here" is measured, and the novel's characters navigate the space between them with longing, letters, and resignation.

## In the Novel

Spain enters the novel through three channels: the court, the letters, and the theatre.

**The Court.** Doña Clara departs Lima for the Spanish court, where she becomes "the outstanding woman of intelligence" (002-005). The court is the apex of the social world the Marquesa can only imagine — a place of refinement, wit, and status that her daughter inhabits and she cannot reach. The Marquesa travels to Spain once, a visit that ends in mutual torture: "On both sides the visit was anticipated with resolutions well nourished on self-reproach: the one to be patient, the other to be undemonstrative. Both failed" (002-006).

**The Letters.** Spain is the destination of the Marquesa's famous letters. "Doña María had arranged that any letters arriving from Spain should be brought to her at once by a special messenger" (002-061). The letters cross the Atlantic in both directions — the Marquesa's elaborate missives going east, Doña Clara's curt replies coming west. Within a century of the Marquesa's death, these letters become "one of the monuments of Spanish literature" (002-002), studied by schoolboys throughout Spain.

**The Theatre.** Lima's theatrical life is built on Spanish drama. "The manager had the whole flowering of the seventeenth-century Spanish drama to draw upon, including many of the plays of Calderón, Moreto, and Lope de Vega" (004-008). [[uncle-pio|Uncle Pio]] is drawn to Lima precisely because he "wanted to be near those that loved Spanish literature and its masterpieces, especially in the theatre" (004-012). Yet the Perichole grows contemptuous of acting, due in part to "the poverty of interest in women's roles throughout Spanish classical drama" (004-040) — Spain's cultural gift to Peru is also its limitation.

## Spain and Peru

The novel sets up a quiet opposition between Spain and Peru. Spain is the old world — courtly, literary, refined, and distant. Peru is the new world — raw, religious, physical, and immediate. When [[captain-alvarado|the Captain]] says "I shall never see Spain. All the world is alike, Madrid or Lima" (004-072), he voices the novel's skeptical cosmopolitanism: from a sufficient distance, metropole and colony are the same.

Yet the novel does not dismiss the difference. The Marquesa's letters are great precisely because of the distance they must cross — between Lima and Madrid, between mother and daughter, between love and indifference. Spain is the necessary other pole, the far shore that gives the crossing its meaning.
