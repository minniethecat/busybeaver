## Definition

The **Peruvian Inquisition** (formally the Tribunal of the Holy Office of the Inquisition in Lima) was the colonial extension of the Spanish Inquisition, established in 1570 to enforce Catholic orthodoxy in the Viceroyalty of Peru. In *The Bridge of San Luis Rey*, the Inquisition appears as an institution of surveillance and suppression — it reads thoughts, suppresses books, and threatens the unconventional with punishment. It represents the institutional face of the religion that Brother Juniper's personal faith attempts to serve.

## In the Novel

Wilder introduces the Inquisition in the novel's opening pages as part of the intellectual atmosphere of eighteenth-century Peru: "even in countries where the Inquisition can read your very thoughts in your eyes" [001-008]. This phrase frames the Inquisition as a power of psychological penetration — a rival to Brother Juniper's own project of reading meaning from lives.

The Inquisition touches several characters' lives directly. Doña María "had been denounced three times before the Inquisition" [002-004], and the narrator notes that "it is not impossible that she might have been burned had her son-in-law been less influential." Her survival depends on social connections rather than innocence — a subtle critique of the institution's conflation of political power with spiritual authority.

Uncle Pio, in his restless career, "had been reduced for a time to making investigations for the Inquisition" [004-010], but abandoned the work "when he had seen several of his victims led off in hoods." This detail humanizes Pio — even his flexible conscience drew a line at the Inquisition's brutality — while underscoring the institution's role as an engine of suffering.

Most tellingly, the Marquesa's son-in-law had written a "dense treatise on the laws of hydraulics" that was "suppressed by the Inquisition as being too exacting" [002-005]. The adjective is devastating: the Inquisition suppresses not heresy but intellectual rigor, threatened by any claim of knowledge that competes with its own authority.

## Key Passages

> "in countries where the Inquisition can read your very thoughts in your eyes" [001-008]

This phrase establishes the Inquisition as the novel's emblem of institutional surveillance — an earthly power that claims the divine prerogative of reading hearts.

> "She had been denounced three times before the Inquisition. It is not impossible that she might have been burned had her son-in-law been less influential" [002-004]

The Marquesa's survival through social connection rather than innocence exposes the Inquisition's justice as political rather than spiritual.

> "whose dense treatise on the laws of hydraulics was suppressed by the Inquisition as being too exacting" [002-005]

Wilder's irony is at its sharpest here: the Inquisition suppresses a book not for heresy but for intellectual rigor, suggesting that the institution fears knowledge more than unbelief.
