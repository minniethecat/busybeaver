[012-001]She became thoughtful, and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] turned to George. “You tried to swing

[012-002]underneath me and break the fall for me when we went over,” she said.

[012-003]“I knew you were doing that, and—it was nice of you.”

[012-004]“Wasn’t any fall to speak of,” he returned brusquely. “Couldn’t have

[012-005]hurt either of us.”

[012-006]“Still it was friendly of you—and awfully quick, too. I’ll not—I’ll not

[012-007]forget it!”

[012-008]Her voice had a sound of genuineness, very pleasant; and George

[012-009]began to forget his annoyance with her father. This annoyance of

[012-010]his had not been alleviated by the circumstance that neither of the

[012-011]seats of the old sewing-machine was designed for three people, but

[012-012]when his neighbour spoke thus gratefully, he no longer minded the

[012-013]crowding—in fact, it pleased him so much that he began to wish the old

[012-014]sewing-machine would go even slower. And she had spoken no word of

[012-015]blame for his letting that darned horse get the cutter into the ditch.

[012-016]George presently addressed her hurriedly, almost tremulously, speaking

[012-017]close to her ear:

[012-018]“I forgot to tell you something: you’re pretty nice! I thought so the

[012-019]first second I saw you last night. I’ll come for you tonight and take

[012-020]you to the Assembly at [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Hotel. You’re going, aren’t you?”

[012-021]“Yes, but I’m going with papa and the Sharons. I’ll see you there.”

[012-022]“Looks to me as if you were awfully conventional,” George grumbled; and

[012-023]his disappointment was deeper than he was willing to let her see—though

[012-024]she probably did see. “Well, we’ll dance the cotillion together,

[012-025]anyhow.”

[012-026]“I’m afraid not. I promised Mr. Kinney.”

[012-027]“What!” George’s tone was shocked, as at incredible news. “Well, you

[012-028]could break _that_ engagement, I guess, if you wanted to! Girls always

[012-029]can get out of things when they want to. Won’t you?”

[012-030]“I don’t think so.”

[012-031]“Why not?”

[012-032]“Because I promised him. Several days ago.”

[012-033]George gulped, and lowered his pride, “I don’t—oh, look here! I only

[012-034]want to go to that thing tonight to get to see something of you; and if

[012-035]you don’t dance the cotillion with me, how can I? I’ll only be here two

[012-036]weeks, and the others have got all the rest of your visit to see you.

[012-037]_Won’t_ you do it, please?”

[012-038]“I couldn’t.”

[012-039]“See here!” said the stricken George. “If you’re going to decline to

[012-040]dance that cotillion with me simply because you’ve promised a—a—a

[012-041]miserable red-headed outsider like Fred Kinney, why we might as well

[012-042]quit!”

[012-043]“Quit what?”

[012-044]“You know perfectly well what I mean,” he said huskily.

[012-045]“I don’t.”

[012-046]“Well, you ought to!”

[012-047]“But I don’t at all!”

[012-048]George, thoroughly hurt, and not a little embittered, expressed himself

[012-049]in a short outburst of laughter: “Well, I ought to have seen it!”

[012-050]“Seen what?”

[012-051]“That you might turn out to be a girl who’d like a fellow of the

[012-052]red-headed Kinney sort. I ought to have seen it from the first!”

[012-053]Lucy bore her disgrace lightly. “Oh, dancing a cotillion with a person

[012-054]doesn’t mean that you like him—but I don’t see anything in particular

[012-055]the matter with Mr. Kinney. What is?”

[012-056]“If you don’t see anything the matter with him for yourself,” George

[012-057]responded, icily, “I don’t think pointing it out would help you. You

[012-058]probably wouldn’t understand.”

[012-059]“You might try,” she suggested. “Of course I’m a stranger here, and

[012-060]if people have done anything wrong or have something unpleasant about

[012-061]them, I wouldn’t have any way of knowing it, just at first. If poor Mr.

[012-062]Kinney—”

[012-063]“I prefer not to discuss it,” said George curtly. “He’s an enemy of

[012-064]mine.”

[012-065]“Why?”

[012-066]“I prefer not to discuss it.”

[012-067]“Well, but—”

[012-068]“I prefer not to discuss it!”

[012-069]“Very well.” She began to hum the air of the song which Mr. George

[012-070]Amberson was now discoursing, “O moon of my delight that knows no

[012-071]wane”—and there was no further conversation on the back seat.

[012-072]They had entered [[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]], and the moon of [[george-amberson|Mr. Amberson]]’s

[012-073]delight was overlaid by a slender Gothic filagree; the branches that

[012-074]sprang from the shade trees lining the street. Through the windows of

[012-075]many of the houses rosy lights were flickering; and silver tinsel and

[012-076]evergreen wreaths and brilliant little glass globes of silver and wine

[012-077]colour could be seen, and glimpses were caught of Christmas trees, with

[012-078]people decking them by firelight—reminders that this was Christmas Eve.

[012-079]The ride-stealers had disappeared from the highway, though now and

[012-080]then, over the gasping and howling of the [[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons|horseless carriage]], there

[012-081]came a shrill jeer from some young passer-by upon the sidewalk:

[012-082]“Mister, fer heaven’s sake go an’ git a hoss! Git a hoss! _Git_ a hoss!”

[012-083]The contrivance stopped with a heart-shaking jerk before Isabel’s

[012-084]house. The gentlemen jumped down, helping Isabel and [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] to descend;

[012-085]there were friendly leavetakings—and one that was not precisely

[012-086]friendly.

[012-087]“It’s ‘au revoir,’ till to-night, isn’t it?” Lucy asked, laughing.

[012-088]“Good afternoon!” said George, and he did not wait, as his relatives

[012-089]did, to see the old sewing machine start briskly down the street,

[012-090]toward the Sharons’; its lighter load consisting now of only Mr. Morgan

[012-091]and his daughter. George went into the house at once.

[012-092]He found his father reading the evening paper in the library. “Where

[012-093]are your mother and your Aunt Fanny?” [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]] inquired, not looking

[012-094]up.

[012-095]“They’re coming,” said his son; and, casting himself heavily into a

[012-096]chair, stared at the fire.

[012-097]His prediction was verified a few moments later; the two ladies came in

[012-098]cheerfully, unfastening their fur cloaks. “It’s all right, Georgie,”

[012-099]said Isabel. “Your Uncle George called to us that Pendennis got home

[012-100]safely. Put your shoes close to the fire, dear, or else go and change

[012-101]them.” She went to her husband and patted him lightly on the shoulder,

[012-102]an action which George watched with sombre moodiness. “You might dress

[012-103]before long,” she suggested. “We’re all going to the Assembly, after

[012-104]dinner, aren’t we? Brother George said he’d go with us.”

[012-105]“Look here,” said George abruptly. “How about this man Morgan and his

[012-106]old sewing-machine? Doesn’t he want to get grandfather to put [[wealth-and-fortune|money]]

[012-107]into it? Isn’t he trying to work Uncle George for that? Isn’t that what

[012-108]he’s up to?”

[012-109]It was Miss Fanny who responded. “You little silly!” she cried, with

[012-110]surprising sharpness. “What on earth are you talking about? [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]

[012-111]Morgan’s perfectly able to finance his own inventions these days.”

[012-112]“I’ll bet he borrows money of Uncle George,” the nephew insisted.

[012-113]Isabel looked at him in grave perplexity. “Why do you say such a thing,

[012-114]George?” she asked.

[012-115]“He strikes me as that sort of man,” he answered doggedly. “Isn’t he,

[012-116]father?”

[012-117]Minafer set down his paper for the moment. “He was a fairly wild young

[012-118]fellow twenty years ago,” he said, glancing at his wife absently. “He

[012-119]was like you in one thing, Georgie; he spent too much money—only he

[012-120]didn’t have any mother to get money out of a grandfather for him, so he

[012-121]was usually in debt. But I believe I’ve heard he’s done fairly well of

[012-122]late years. No, I can’t say I think he’s a swindler, and I doubt if he

[012-123]needs anybody else’s money to back his horseless carriage.”

[012-124]“Well, what’s he brought the old thing here for, then? People that

[012-125]own elephants don’t take them elephants around with ’em when they go

[012-126]visiting. What’s he got it here for?”

[012-127]“I’m sure I don’t know,” said Mr. Minafer, resuming his paper. “You

[012-128]might ask him.”

[012-129]Isabel laughed, and patted her husband’s shoulder again. “Aren’t you

[012-130]going to dress? Aren’t we all going to the dance?”

[012-131]He groaned faintly. “Aren’t your brother and Georgie escorts enough for

[012-132]you and Fanny?”

[012-133]“Wouldn’t you enjoy it at all?”

[012-134]“You know I don’t.”

[012-135]Isabel let her hand remain upon his shoulder a moment longer; she stood

[012-136]behind him, looking into the fire, and George, watching her broodingly,

[012-137]thought there was more colour in her face than the reflection of the

[012-138]flames accounted for. “Well, then,” she said indulgently, “stay at home

[012-139]and be happy. We won’t urge you if you’d really rather not.”

[012-140]“I really wouldn’t,” he said contentedly.

[012-141]Half an hour later, George was passing through the upper hall, in a

[012-142]bath-robe stage of preparation for the evening’s gaieties, when he

[012-143]encountered his Aunt Fanny. He stopped her. “Look here!” he said.

[012-144]“What in the world is the matter with you?” she demanded, regarding

[012-145]him with little amiability. “You look as if you were rehearsing for a

[012-146]villain in a play. Do change your expression!”

[012-147]His expression gave no sign of yielding to the request; on the

[012-148]contrary, its somberness deepened. “I suppose you don’t know why father

[012-149]doesn’t want to go tonight,” he said solemnly. “You’re his only sister,

[012-150]and yet you don’t know!”

[012-151]“He never wants to go anywhere that I ever heard of,” said Fanny. “What

[012-152]is the matter with you?”

[012-153]“He doesn’t want to go because he doesn’t like this man Morgan.”

[012-154]“Good gracious!” Fanny cried impatiently. “Eugene Morgan isn’t in your

[012-155]father’s thoughts at all, one way or the other. Why should he be?”

[012-156]George hesitated. “Well—it strikes me—Look here, what makes you and—and

[012-157]everybody—so excited over him?”

[012-158]“Excited!” she jeered. “Can’t people be glad to see an old friend

[012-159]without silly children like you having to make a to-do about it? I’ve

[012-160]just been in your mother’s room suggesting that she might give a little

[012-161]dinner for them—”

[012-162]“For who?”

[012-163]“For _whom_, Georgie! For Mr. Morgan and his daughter.”

[012-164]“Look here!” George said quickly. “Don’t do that! Mother mustn’t do

[012-165]that. It wouldn’t look well.”

[012-166]“Wouldn’t look well!” Fanny mocked him; and her suppressed vehemence

[012-167]betrayed a surprising acerbity. “See here, [[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]], I suggest

[012-168]that you just march straight on into your room and finish your

[012-169]dressing! Sometimes you say things that show you have a pretty mean

[012-170]little mind!”

[012-171]George was so astounded by this outburst that his indignation was

[012-172]delayed by his curiosity. “Why, what upsets you this way?” he inquired.

[012-173]“I know what you mean,” she said, her voice still lowered, but not

[012-174]decreasing in sharpness. “You’re trying to insinuate that I’d get

[012-175]your mother to invite Eugene Morgan here on my account because he’s a

[012-176]widower!”

[012-177]“I am?” George gasped, nonplussed. “I’m trying to insinuate that you’re

[012-178]setting your cap at him and getting mother to help you? Is that what

[012-179]you mean?”

[012-180]Beyond a doubt that was what Miss Fanny meant. She gave him a white-hot

[012-181]look. “You attend to your own affairs!” she whispered fiercely, and

[012-182]swept away.

[012-183]George, dumfounded, returned to his room for meditation.

[012-184]He had lived for years in the same house with his Aunt Fanny, and it

[012-185]now appeared that during all those years he had been thus intimately

[012-186]associating with a total stranger. Never before had he met the

[012-187]passionate lady with whom he had just held a conversation in the hall.

[012-188]So she wanted to get married! And wanted George’s mother to help her

[012-189]with this horseless-carriage widower!

[012-190]“Well, I _will_ be shot!” he muttered aloud. “I will—I certainly will

[012-191]be shot!” And he began to laugh. “Lord ’lmighty!”

[012-192]But presently, at the thought of the horseless-carriage widower’s

[012-193]daughter, his grimness returned, and he resolved upon a line of conduct

[012-194]for the evening. He would nod to her carelessly when he first saw her;

[012-195]and, after that, he would notice her no more: he would not dance with

[012-196]her; he would not favour her in the cotillion—he would not go near her!

[012-197]He descended to dinner upon the third urgent summons of a coloured

[012-198]butler, having spent two hours dressing—and rehearsing.

Chapter IX

[012-199]The Honourable George Amberson was a congressman who led cotillions—the

[012-200]sort of congressman an Amberson would be. He did it negligently,

[012-201]tonight, yet with infallible dexterity, now and then glancing

[012-202]humorously at the spectators, people of his own age. They were seated

[012-203]in a tropical grove at one end of the room whither they had retired at

[012-204]the beginning of the cotillion, which they surrendered entirely to the

[012-205]twenties and the late ’teens. And here, grouped with that stately pair,

[012-206][[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] and [[amelia-amberson|Amelia Amberson]], sat Isabel with Fanny, while Eugene Morgan

[012-207]appeared to bestow an amiable devotion impartially upon the three

[012-208]sisters-in-law. Fanny watched his face eagerly, laughing at everything

[012-209]he said; Amelia smiled blandly, but rather because of graciousness

[012-210]than because of interest; while Isabel, looking out at the dancers,

[012-211]rhythmically moved a great fan of blue ostrich feathers, listened to

[012-212]Eugene thoughtfully, yet all the while kept her shining eyes on Georgie.
