[038-001]Amberson went over, the next summer, and joined his sister and nephew

[038-002]in Paris, where they were living. “Isabel does want to come home,”

[038-003]he told [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] gravely, on the day of his return, in October. “She’s

[038-004]wanted to for a long while—and she ought to come while she can stand

[038-005]the journey—” And he amplified this statement, leaving Fanny looking

[038-006]startled and solemn when [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] came by to drive him out to dinner at the

[038-007]new house [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] had just completed.

[038-008]This was no white-and-blue cottage, but a great Georgian picture in

[038-009]brick, five miles north of [[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]], with four acres of its

[038-010]own hedged land between it and its next neighbour; and Amberson laughed

[038-011]wistfully as they turned in between the stone and brick gate pillars,

[038-012]and rolled up the crushed stone driveway. “I wonder, Lucy, if history’s

[038-013]going on forever repeating itself,” he said. “I wonder if this town’s

[038-014]going on building up things and rolling over them, as poor father once

[038-015]said it was rolling over his poor old heart. It looks like it: here’s

[038-016][[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Mansion again, only it’s Georgian instead of nondescript

[038-017]Romanesque; but it’s just the same [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]] that my father

[038-018]built long before you were born. The only difference is that it’s your

[038-019]father who’s built this one now. It’s all the same, in the long run.”

[038-020]Lucy did not quite understand, but she laughed as a friend should, and,

[038-021]taking his arm, showed him through vast rooms where ivory-panelled

[038-022]walls and trim window hangings were reflected dimly in dark, rugless

[038-023]floors, and the sparse furniture showed that Lucy had been “collecting”

[038-024]with a long purse. “By Jove!” he said. “You have been going it! Fanny

[038-025]tells me you had a great ‘house-warming’ dance, and you keep right on

[038-026]being the belle of the ball, not any softer-hearted than you used to

[038-027]be. Fred Kinney’s father says you’ve refused Fred so often that he got

[038-028]engaged to Janie Sharon just to prove that someone would have him in

[038-029]spite of his hair. Well, the material world do move, and you’ve got the

[038-030]new kind of house it moves into nowadays—if it has the new price! And

[038-031]even the grand old expanses of plate glass we used to be so proud of at

[038-032]the other Amberson Mansion—they’ve gone, too, with the crowded heavy

[038-033]gold and red stuff. Curious! We’ve still got the plate glass windows,

[038-034]though all we can see out of ’em is the smoke and the old Johnson

[038-035]house, which is a counter-jumper’s boardinghouse now, while you’ve got

[038-036]a view, and you cut it all up into little panes. Well, you’re pretty

[038-037]refreshingly out of the smoke up here.”

[038-038]“Yes, for a while,” Lucy laughed. “Until it comes and we have to move

[038-039]out farther.”

[038-040]“No, you’ll stay here,” he assured her. “It will be somebody else

[038-041]who’ll move out farther.”

[038-042]He continued to talk of the house after Eugene arrived, and gave them

[038-043]no account of his journey until they had retired from the dinner table

[038-044]to Eugene’s library, a gray and shadowy room, where their coffee was

[038-045]brought. Then, equipped with a cigar, which seemed to occupy his

[038-046]attention, Amberson spoke in a casual tone of his sister and her son.

[038-047]“I found Isabel as well as usual,” he said, “only I’m afraid ‘as usual’

[038-048]isn’t particularly well. [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] and Amelia had been up to Paris in the

[038-049]spring, but she hadn’t seen them. Somebody told her they were there, it

[038-050]seems. They’d left Florence and were living in Rome; Amelia’s become

[038-051]a Catholic and is said to give great sums to charity and to go about

[038-052]with the gentry in consequence, but Sydney’s ailing and lives in a

[038-053]wheel-chair most of the time. It struck me Isabel ought to be doing the

[038-054]same thing.”

[038-055]He paused, bestowing minute care upon the removal of the little band

[038-056]from his cigar; and as he seemed to have concluded his narrative,

[038-057]Eugene spoke out of the shadow beyond a heavily shaded lamp: “What do

[038-058]you mean by that?” he asked quietly.

[038-059]“Oh, she’s cheerful enough,” said Amberson, still not looking at either

[038-060]his young hostess or her father. “At least,” he added, “she manages to

[038-061]seem so. I’m afraid she hasn’t been really well for several years. She

[038-062]isn’t stout you know—she hasn’t changed in looks much—and she seems

[038-063]rather alarmingly short of breath for a slender person. Father’s been

[038-064]that way for years, of course; but never nearly so much as Isabel is

[038-065]now. Of course she makes nothing of it, but it seemed rather serious

[038-066]to me when I noticed she had to stop and rest twice to get up the one

[038-067]short flight of stairs in their two-floor apartment. I told her I

[038-068]thought she ought to make George let her come home.”

[038-069]“Let her?” Eugene repeated, in a low voice. “Does she want to?”

[038-070]“She doesn’t urge it. George seems to like the life there—in his grand,

[038-071]gloomy, and peculiar way; and of course she’ll never change about

[038-072]being proud of him and all that—he’s quite a swell. But in spite of

[038-073]anything she said, rather than because, I know she does indeed want to

[038-074]come. She’d like to be with father, of course; and I think she’s—well,

[038-075]she intimated one day that she feared it might even happen that she

[038-076]wouldn’t get to see him again. At the time I thought she referred to

[038-077]his age and feebleness, but on the boat, coming home, I remembered the

[038-078]little look of wistfulness, yet of resignation, with which she said

[038-079]it, and it struck me all at once that I’d been mistaken: I saw she was

[038-080]really thinking of her own state of health.”

[038-081]“I see,” Eugene said, his voice even lower than it had been before.

[038-082]“And you say he won’t ‘let’ her come home?”

[038-083]Amberson laughed, but still continued to be interested in his cigar.

[038-084]“Oh, I don’t think he uses force! He’s very gentle with her. I doubt

[038-085]if the subject is mentioned between them, and yet—and yet, knowing my

[038-086]interesting nephew as you do, wouldn’t you think that was about the way

[038-087]to put it?”

[038-088]“Knowing him as I do—yes,” said Eugene slowly. “Yes, I should think

[038-089]that was about the way to put it.”

[038-090]A murmur out of the shadows beyond him—a faint sound, musical and

[038-091]feminine, yet expressive of a notable intensity—seemed to indicate that

[038-092]Lucy was of the same opinion.

Chapter XXIX

[038-093]“Let her” was correct; but the time came—and it came in the spring of

[038-094]the next year when it was no longer a question of George’s letting his

[038-095]mother come home. He had to bring her, and to bring her quickly if she

[038-096]was to see her father again; and Amberson had been right: her danger of

[038-097]never seeing him again lay not in the Major’s feebleness of heart but

[038-098]in her own. As it was, George telegraphed his uncle to have a wheeled

[038-099]chair at the station, for the journey had been disastrous, and to this

[038-100]hybrid vehicle, placed close to the platform, her son carried her in

[038-101]his arms when she arrived. She was unable to speak, but patted her

[038-102]brother’s and Fanny’s hands and looked “very sweet,” Fanny found the

[038-103]desperate courage to tell her. She was lifted from the chair into a

[038-104]carriage, and seemed a little stronger as they drove home; for once she

[038-105]took her hand from George’s, and waved it feebly toward the carriage

[038-106]window.

[038-107]“Changed,” she whispered. “So changed.”

[038-108]“You mean the town,” Amberson said. “You mean the old place is changed,

[038-109]don’t you, dear?”

[038-110]She smiled and moved her lips: “Yes.”

[038-111]“It’ll change to a happier place, old dear,” he said, “now that you’re

[038-112]back in it, and going to get well again.”

[038-113]But she only looked at him wistfully, her eyes a little frightened.

[038-114]When the carriage stopped, her son carried her into the house, and up

[038-115]the stairs to her own room, where a nurse was waiting; and he came

[038-116]out a moment later, as the doctor went in. At the end of the hall a

[038-117]stricken group was clustered: Amberson, and Fanny, and the Major.

[038-118]George, deathly pale and speechless, took his grandfather’s hand, but

[038-119]the old gentleman did not seem to notice his action.

[038-120]“When are they going to let me see my daughter?” he asked querulously.

[038-121]“They told me to keep out of the way while they carried her in,

[038-122]because it might upset her. I wish they’d let me go in and speak to my

[038-123]daughter. I think she wants to see me.”

[038-124]He was right—presently the doctor came out and beckoned to him; and

[038-125]the Major shuffled forward, leaning on a shaking cane; his figure,

[038-126]after all its years of proud soldierliness, had grown stooping at last,

[038-127]and his untrimmed white hair straggled over the back of his collar.

[038-128]He looked old—old and divested of the world—as he crept toward his

[038-129]daughter’s room. Her voice was stronger, for the waiting group heard

[038-130]a low cry of tenderness and welcome as the old man reached the open

[038-131]doorway. Then the door was closed.

[038-132]Fanny touched her nephew’s arm. “George, you must need something to

[038-133]eat—I know she’d want you to. I’ve had things ready: I knew she’d want

[038-134]me to. You’d better go down to the dining room: there’s plenty on the

[038-135]table, waiting for you. She’d want you to eat something.”

[038-136]He turned a ghastly face to her, it was so panic-stricken. “I don’t

[038-137]want anything to eat!” he said savagely. And he began to pace the

[038-138]floor, taking care not to go near Isabel’s door, and that his footsteps

[038-139]were muffled by the long, thick hall rug. After a while he went to

[038-140]where Amberson, with folded arms and bowed head, had seated himself

[038-141]near the front window. “[[george-amberson|Uncle George]],” he said hoarsely. “I didn’t—”

[038-142]“Well?”

[038-143]“Oh, my God, I didn’t think this thing the matter with her could ever

[038-144]be serious! I—” He gasped. “When that doctor I had meet us at the

[038-145]boat—” He could not go on.

[038-146]Amberson only nodded his head, and did not otherwise change his

[038-147]attitude.

[038-148]Isabel lived through the night. At eleven o’clock Fanny came timidly to

[038-149]George in his room. “Eugene is here,” she whispered. “He’s downstairs.

[038-150]He wants—” She gulped. “He wants to know if he can’t see her. I didn’t

[038-151]know what to say. I said I’d see. I didn’t know—the doctor said—”

[038-152]“The doctor said we ‘must keep her peaceful,’” George said sharply.

[038-153]“Do you think that man’s coming would be very soothing? My God! if it

[038-154]hadn’t been for him this mightn’t have happened: we could have gone on

[038-155]living here quietly, and—why, it would be like taking a stranger into

[038-156]her room! She hasn’t even spoken of him more than twice in all the time

[038-157]we’ve been away. Doesn’t he know how sick she is? You tell him the

[038-158]doctor said she had to be quiet and peaceful. That’s what he did say,

[038-159]isn’t it?”

[038-160]Fanny acquiesced tearfully. “I’ll tell him. I’ll tell him the doctor

[038-161]said she was to be kept very quiet. I—I didn’t know—” And she pottered

[038-162]out.

[038-163]An hour later the nurse appeared in George’s doorway; she came

[038-164]noiselessly, and his back was toward her; but he jumped as if he had

[038-165]been shot, and his jaw fell, he so feared what she was going to say.

[038-166]“She wants to see you.”

[038-167]The terrified mouth shut with a click; and he nodded and followed her;

[038-168]but she remained outside his mother’s room while he went in.

[038-169]Isabel’s eyes were closed, and she did not open them or move her head,

[038-170]but she smiled and edged her hand toward him as he sat on a stool

[038-171]beside the bed. He took that slender, cold hand, and put it to his

[038-172]cheek.

[038-173]“Darling, did you—get something to eat?” She could only whisper, slowly

[038-174]and with difficulty. It was as if Isabel herself were far away, and

[038-175]only able to signal what she wanted to say.
