[033-001]“Oh, my Lord!” he groaned. “I’m doing these things for her, not against

[033-002]her!”

[033-003]A mildness had come upon [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]], and she had controlled her weeping. She

[033-004]shook her head gently. “No, I’d let her alone if I were you. I don’t

[033-005]think she’s very well, George.”

[033-006]“She! I never saw a healthier person in my life.”

[033-007]“No. She doesn’t let anybody know, but she goes to the doctor

[033-008]regularly.”

[033-009]“Women are always going to doctors regularly.”

[033-010]“No. He told her to.”

[033-011]George was not impressed. “It’s nothing at all; she spoke of it to me

[033-012]years ago—some kind of family failing. She said grandfather had it,

[033-013]too; and look at him! Hasn’t proved very serious with him! You act as

[033-014]if I’d done something wrong in sending that man about his business, and

[033-015]as if I were going to persecute my mother, instead of protecting her.

[033-016]By Jove, it’s sickening! You told me how all the riffraff in town were

[033-017]busy with her name, and then the minute I lift my hand to protect her,

[033-018]you begin to attack me and—”

[033-019]“Sh!” Fanny checked him, laying her hand on his arm. “Your uncle is

[033-020]going.”

[033-021]The library doors were heard opening, and a moment later there came the

[033-022]sound of the front door closing.

[033-023]George moved toward the head of the stairs, then stood listening; but

[033-024]the house was silent.

[033-025]Fanny made a slight noise with her lips to attract his attention, and,

[033-026]when he glanced toward her, shook her head at him urgently. “Let her

[033-027]alone,” she whispered. “She’s down there by herself. Don’t go down. Let

[033-028]her alone.”

[033-029]She moved a few steps toward him and halted, her face pallid and

[033-030]awestruck, and then both stood listening for anything that might

[033-031]break the silence downstairs. No sound came to them; that poignant

[033-032]silence was continued throughout long, long minutes, while the two

[033-033]listeners stood there under its mysterious spell; and in its plaintive

[033-034]eloquence—speaking, as it did, of the figure alone in the big,

[033-035]dark library, where dead Wilbur’s new silver frame gleamed in the

[033-036]dimness—there was something that checked even George.

[033-037]Above the aunt and nephew, as they kept this strange vigil, there was

[033-038]a triple window of stained glass, to illumine the landing and upper

[033-039]reaches of the stairway. Figures in blue and amber garments posed

[033-040]gracefully in panels, conceived by some craftsman of the Eighties to

[033-041]represent Love and Purity and Beauty, and these figures, leaded to

[033-042]unalterable attitudes, were little more motionless than the two human

[033-043]beings upon whom fell the mottled faint light of the window. The

[033-044]colours were growing dull; evening was coming on.

[033-045]Fanny Minafer broke the long silence with a sound from her throat, a

[033-046]stilled gasp; and with that great companion of hers, her handkerchief,

[033-047]retired softly to the loneliness of her own chamber. After she had

[033-048]gone George looked about him bleakly, then on tiptoe crossed the hall

[033-049]and went into his own room, which was filled with twilight. Still

[033-050]tiptoeing, though he could not have said why, he went across the room

[033-051]and sat down heavily in a chair facing the window. Outside there was

[033-052]nothing but the darkening air and the wall of the nearest of the

[033-053]new houses. He had not slept at all, the night before, and he had

[033-054]eaten nothing since the preceding day at lunch, but he felt neither

[033-055]drowsiness nor hunger. His set determination filled him, kept him but

[033-056]too wide awake, and his gaze at the grayness beyond the window was

[033-057]wide-eyed and bitter.

[033-058]Darkness had closed in when there was a step in the room behind him.

[033-059]Then someone knelt beside the chair, two arms went round him with

[033-060]infinite compassion, a gentle head rested against his shoulder, and

[033-061]there came the faint scent as of apple-blossoms far away.

[033-062]“You mustn’t be troubled, darling,” his mother whispered.

[033-063]George choked. For an instant he was on the point of breaking down, but

[033-064]he commanded himself, bravely dismissing the self-pity roused by her

[033-065]compassion. “How can I help but be?” he said.

[033-066]“No, no.” She soothed him. “You mustn’t. You mustn’t be troubled, no

[033-067]matter what happens.”

[033-068]“That’s easy enough to say!” he protested; and he moved as if to rise.

[033-069]“Just let’s stay like this a little while, dear. Just a minute or

[033-070]two. I want to tell you: brother George has been here, and he told

[033-071]me everything about—about how unhappy you’d been—and how you went so

[033-072]gallantly to that old woman with the operaglasses.” Isabel gave a sad

[033-073]little laugh. “What a terrible old woman she is! What a really terrible

[033-074]thing a vulgar old woman can be!”

[033-075]“Mother, I—” And again he moved to rise.

[033-076]“Must you? It seemed to me such a comfortable way to talk. Well—” She

[033-077]yielded; he rose, helped her to her feet, and pressed the light into

[033-078]being.

[033-079]As the room took life from the sudden lines of fire within the

[033-080]bulbs Isabel made a deprecatory gesture, and, with a faint laugh of

[033-081]apologetic protest, turned quickly away from George. What she meant

[033-082]was: “You mustn’t see my face until I’ve made it nicer for you.” Then

[033-083]she turned again to him, her eyes downcast, but no sign of tears in

[033-084]them, and she contrived to show him that there was the semblance of

[033-085]a smile upon her lips. She still wore her hat, and in her unsteady

[033-086]fingers she held a white envelope, somewhat crumpled.

[033-087]“Now, mother—”

[033-088]“Wait, dearest,” she said; and though he stood stone cold, she lifted

[033-089]her arms, put them round him again, and pressed her cheek lightly to

[033-090]his. “Oh, you do look so troubled, poor dear! One thing you couldn’t

[033-091]doubt, beloved boy: you know I could never care for anything in the

[033-092]world as I care for you—never, never!”

[033-093]“Now, mother—”

[033-094]She released him, and stepped back. “Just a moment more, dearest. I

[033-095]want you to read this first. We can get at things better.” She pressed

[033-096]into his hand the envelope she had brought with her, and as he opened

[033-097]it, and began to read the long enclosure, she walked slowly to the

[033-098]other end of the room; then stood there, with her back to him, and her

[033-099]head drooping a little, until he had finished.

[033-100]The sheets of paper were covered with [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s handwriting.

[033-101][[george-amberson|George Amberson]] will bring you this, dear Isabel. He is waiting

[033-102]while I write. He and I have talked things over, and before he

[033-103]gives this to you he will tell you what has happened. Of course I’m

[033-104]rather confused, and haven’t had time to think matters out very

[033-105]definitely, and yet I believe I should have been better prepared

[033-106]for what took place to-day—I ought to have known it was coming,

[033-107]because I have understood for quite a long time that young George

[033-108]was getting to dislike me more and more. Somehow, I’ve never been

[033-109]able to get his friendship; he’s always had a latent distrust of

[033-110]me—or something like distrust—and perhaps that’s made me sometimes

[033-111]a little awkward and diffident with him. I think it may be he

[033-112]felt from the first that I cared a great deal about you, and he

[033-113]naturally resented it. I think perhaps he felt this even during

[033-114]all the time when I was so careful—at least I thought I was—not to

[033-115]show, even to you, how immensely I did care. And he may have feared

[033-116]that you were thinking too much about me—even when you weren’t

[033-117]and only liked me as an old friend. It’s perfectly comprehensible

[033-118]to me, also, that at his age one gets excited about gossip. Dear

[033-119]Isabel, what I’m trying to get at, in my confused way, is that you

[033-120]and I don’t care about this nonsensical gossip, ourselves, at all.

[033-121]Yesterday I thought the time had come when I could ask you to marry

[033-122]me, and you were dear enough to tell me “sometime it might come to

[033-123]that.” Well, you and I, left to ourselves, and knowing what we have

[033-124]been and what we are, we’d pay as much attention to “talk” as we

[033-125]would to any other kind of old cats’ mewing! We’d not be very apt

[033-126]to let such things keep us from the plenty of life we have left to

[033-127]us for making up to ourselves for old unhappinesses and mistakes.

[033-128]But now we’re faced with—not the slander and not our own fear of

[033-129]it, because we haven’t any, but someone else’s fear of it—your

[033-130]son’s. And, oh, dearest woman in the world, I know what your son is

[033-131]to you, and it frightens me! Let me explain a little: I don’t think

[033-132]he’ll change—at twenty-one or twenty-two so many things appear

[033-133]solid and permanent and terrible which forty sees are nothing but

[033-134]disappearing miasma. Forty can’t tell twenty about this; that’s the

[033-135]pity of it! Twenty can find out only by getting to be forty. And

[033-136]so we come to this, dear: Will you live your own life your way,

[033-137]or George’s way? I’m going a little further, because it would be

[033-138]fatal not to be wholly frank now. George will act toward you only

[033-139]as your long worship of him, your sacrifices—all the unseen little

[033-140]ones every day since he was born—will make him act. Dear, it breaks

[033-141]my heart for you, but what you have to oppose now is the history

[033-142]of your own selfless and perfect motherhood. I remember saying

[033-143]once that what you worshipped in your son was the angel you saw

[033-144]in him—and I still believe that is true of every mother. But in a

[033-145]mother’s worship she may not see that the Will in her son should

[033-146]not always be offered incense along with the angel. I grow sick

[033-147]with fear for you—for both you and me—when I think how the Will

[033-148]against us two has grown strong through the love you have given the

[033-149]angel—and how long your own sweet Will has served that other. Are

[033-150]you strong enough, Isabel? Can you make the fight? I promise you

[033-151]that if you will take heart for it, you will find so quickly that

[033-152]it has all amounted to nothing. You shall have happiness, and, in

[033-153]a little while, only happiness. You need only to write me a line—I

[033-154]can’t come to your house—and tell me where you will meet me. We

[033-155]will come back in a month, and the angel in your son will bring him

[033-156]to you; I promise it. What is good in him will grow so fine, once

[033-157]you have beaten the turbulent Will—but it must be beaten!

[033-158]Your brother, that good friend, is waiting with such patience; I

[033-159]should not keep him longer—and I am saying too much for wisdom, I

[033-160]fear. But, oh, my dear, won’t you be strong—such a little short

[033-161]strength it would need! Don’t strike my life down twice, dear—this

[033-162]time I’ve not deserved it.

[033-163]Eugene.

[033-164]Concluding this missive, George tossed it abruptly from him so that one

[033-165]sheet fell upon his bed and the others upon the floor; and at the faint

[033-166]noise of their falling Isabel came, and, kneeling, began to gather them

[033-167]up.

[033-168]“Did you read it, dear?”

[033-169]George’s face was pale no longer, but pink with fury. “Yes, I did.”

[033-170]“All of it?” she asked gently, as she rose.

[033-171]“Certainly!”

[033-172]She did not look at him, but kept her eyes downcast upon the letter in

[033-173]her hands, tremulously rearranging the sheets in order as she spoke—and

[033-174]though she smiled, her smile was as tremulous as her hands. Nervousness

[033-175]and an irresistible timidity possessed her. “I—I wanted to say,

[033-176]George,” she faltered. “I felt that if—if some day it should happen—I

[033-177]mean, if you came to feel differently about it, and Eugene and I—that

[033-178]is if we found that it seemed the most sensible thing to do—I was

[033-179]afraid you might think it would be a little queer about—[[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], I mean

[033-180]if—if she were your step-sister. Of course, she’d not be even legally

[033-181]related to you, and if you—if you cared for her—”
