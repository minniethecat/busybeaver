## Description

[[National Avenue]] is the grandest street in the [[Downtown Midland|Midland town]], the stage upon which the town's social drama unfolds. In the novel's opening, Tarkington establishes its significance immediately: "Trotters were out, in the winter afternoons, racing light sleighs on National Avenue and [[Tennessee Street]]; everybody recognized both the trotters and the drivers" (001-014). The Avenue was where the town's elite displayed themselves and where social status was publicly performed and confirmed.

[[Major Amberson]]'s purchase of "two hundred acres of land at the end of National Avenue" (002-063) established the family's territorial dominance. The creation of [[Amberson Boulevard]] as "an oblique continuation of National Avenue" (002-079) extended this influence, literally reshaping the town's geography to accommodate Amberson magnificence.

## Role in the Story

National Avenue serves as the geographic spine of the novel's social commentary. At the height of Amberson power, it is the exclusive domain of the town's gentry: "the best young men in town were out, most of them on National Avenue" (010-024). The Avenue is where [[George Amberson Minafer|George]] drives his horse, where the Amberson sleigh cuts through winter snow, and where family status is most visibly asserted.

As the novel progresses, National Avenue becomes a measure of [[The Decline of the Ambersons|decline]]. The boarding-houses begin "marching up National Avenue" (026-010), encroaching on the once-exclusive territory of the gentry. George, walking "up National Avenue on his homeward way" (late chapters), is no longer a commanding figure but merely another pedestrian on a street that has outgrown him.

The Avenue culminates at the stone pillars marking the entrance to [[Amberson Addition]] (011-069), a threshold that once separated the Amberson domain from the common world. By the novel's end, this boundary has become meaningless — the forces of change have "come into every house on National Avenue and Amberson Boulevard" (030-160).

## Associated Characters

- **[[Major Amberson]]**: Created the Avenue's significance through his land purchases and the construction of the [[Amberson Mansion]] at its terminus.
- **[[George Amberson Minafer]]**: The Avenue is his natural habitat during his years of glory; his later walks along it mark his diminished status.
- **[[Eugene Morgan]]**: His [[Morgan's Automobile Factory]] represents the industrial forces that will transform the Avenue's character.
- The townspeople: The Avenue belongs as much to the collective social memory of Midland as to any individual.

## Symbolism

National Avenue represents the public face of Amberson power — the space where private [[Wealth and Fortune|wealth]] becomes social status. Its transformation from exclusive promenade to mixed-use thoroughfare mirrors the democratization of American life that Tarkington both chronicles and, with characteristic ambivalence, laments. The Avenue endures, but its meaning changes; what was once a stage for aristocratic display becomes simply a street, serving a city too large and diverse for any single family to dominate. This trajectory parallels the fate of the [[Amberson Mansion]] itself.
