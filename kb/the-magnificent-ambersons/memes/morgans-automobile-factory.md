Morgan's Automobile Factory, the enterprise established by [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]] upon his return to Midland, stands at the thematic center of Booth Tarkington's *The Magnificent Ambersons* (1918) as the physical embodiment of the [[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons|new industrial age]] that overtakes the old aristocratic order. Initially a modest collection of "small machine-shops built in a western outskirt of the town" (014-127), the factory expands over the course of the novel into a thriving manufacturing plant — a transformation that mirrors and accelerates the [[decline-of-the-ambersons|decline of the Amberson family]] itself. Located beyond the terminus of [[national-avenue|National Avenue]], the factory occupies the geographic frontier of Midland's westward growth, a position that underscores its role as the vanguard of change. Where the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]] once defined the town's social summit, Morgan's factory comes to define its economic future.

## Description

Eugene Morgan's automobile factory begins as a deliberately modest venture. When George Amberson offered to invest, "Mr. Morgan laughingly declined his offer, as Mr. M. was fully able to finance this venture, though not starting in a very large way" (014-030). The factory's physical footprint, too, was initially small — Isabel Minafer describes it to George as "so small I understand it's taken hardly anything" (016-004). The plant was situated in a sparsely developed area:

> Lucy and her father were living at the Amberson Hotel, while Morgan got his small machine-shops built in a western outskirt of the town; and George grumbled about the shabbiness and the old-fashioned look of the hotel, though it was "still the best in the place, of course." (014-127–129)

Yet the factory did not remain modest for long. By the time George visits during his summer vacation, the operation has expanded dramatically. The foreman of the paint shop informs the visitors that the factory is "turning out a car and a quarter a day" (023-077). The scale of the enterprise and its industrial vitality are described through Isabel's delighted eyes:

> What makes me laugh so much at nothing is Eugene's factory. Wouldn't anybody be delighted to see an old friend take an idea out of the air like that — an idea that most people laughed at him for — wouldn't any old friend of his be happy to see how he'd made his idea into such a splendid, humming thing as that factory — all shiny steel, clicking and buzzing away, and with all those workmen, such muscled looking men and yet so intelligent looking? (023-101–107)

The factory's architecture was utilitarian and modern — a sharp contrast to the ornate, decaying grandeur of the Amberson Mansion. Tarkington presents the factory not as a place of grime and exploitation but as a temple of productive energy, "all shiny steel," a humming organism of purposeful labor. The workmen themselves are described with admiration — "muscled looking men and yet so intelligent looking" — suggesting that the new industrial order, unlike the old social order, rewards capability rather than lineage.

The factory complex included specialized departments. The visit mentions a "paint shop" with its own foreman (023-076–077), indicating a division of labor and a level of organizational sophistication. Eugene was actively involved in technical improvements: he had moved "the engine's in front — Eugene thinks that's a great improvement" (016-076), and the cars featured "rubber tires blown up with air — pneumatic!" (016-074). The factory was not merely assembling vehicles; it was engaged in ongoing innovation and refinement of the automobile itself.

By the novel's middle chapters, the factory has achieved commercial success. Isabel reports that "this spring they've finished eight automobiles and sold them all, and they've got twelve more almost finished, and they're sold already!" (016-069). The news reaches George at college through his uncle: "Uncle George told me he heard that the automobile factory had been doing quite well — won a race, too!" (021-193–194). A racing victory in this era served as both technical validation and marketing triumph — proof that Morgan's machines were not merely novelties but competitive, reliable vehicles.

As the novel progresses, the factory becomes part of a larger industrial landscape. By its later chapters, "new factory districts were thundering into life" across Midland (037-038), and Morgan's enterprise had grown so significant that the factory itself had become a benchmark of success. Workmen at other plants dreamed: "If Eugene Morgan had hold of this place you'd see things hum!" (047-106–107). The "Morgan Place" — Eugene's grand new house — had replaced the Amberson Mansion as the town's symbol of wealth, and the factory that made it all possible had transformed from a speculative machine-shop into a pillar of Midland's economy.

## Role in the Story

The factory serves multiple narrative functions across the novel's arc, each corresponding to a different stage of the Amberson family's trajectory.

### Catalyst for Eugene's Return

The decision to build the factory brings Eugene Morgan permanently back to Midland, setting the entire plot in motion. Fanny Minafer's letter to George at college contains the first mention: "He will soon begin to build his factory here for the manufacture of _automobiles_, which he says is a term he prefers to 'horseless carriages'" (014-022–024). This homecoming is not merely geographical; it reopens Eugene's long-dormant courtship of the now-widowed [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]] and reintroduces Lucy Morgan into George Amberson Minafer's life. Without the factory, the novel's central romantic entanglements could not occur.

### Measure of Industrial Progress

The factory's growth provides a running timeline of the new economy's advance. Tarkington carefully documents its evolution:

| Period | Factory Status | Novel Context |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Early (George at college) | "Will soon begin to build" (014-022) | Eugene has just announced his return |
| First year | "Rather a dubious time," "experiments turned out badly" (016-067–068) | Early struggles with the technology |
| Second year | "Finished eight automobiles and sold them all" (016-069) | First commercial success |
| Third year (George's visit) | "Turning out a car and a quarter a day" (023-077) | Factory tour with Isabel and Fanny |
| Fourth year | "Doing quite well — won a race, too!" (021-193–194) | Market recognition and racing prestige |
| Later years | Morgan's wealth becomes proverbial (047-104–107) | New Amberson Mansion replaced by "the Morgan Place" |

### Site of Social Confrontation

The factory becomes a stage where class tensions play out explicitly. During the factory tour, George — the novel's embodiment of aristocratic pride — is "excessively bored" (023-078), while his mother Isabel is enchanted. Lucy does "most of the explaining, while her father looked on and laughed at the mistakes she made" (023-080–081). Fanny Minafer "remained in the background with George, exhibiting a bleakness that overmatched his boredom" (023-081–082). This tableau economically dramatizes the novel's central conflict: those who embrace the future (Isabel, Lucy, Eugene) versus those who cannot adapt to it (George, Fanny).

The lunch that follows the factory visit becomes "almost a festive one" (023-088), with Isabel declaring: "It's beautiful to see such a thing. It makes us all happy, dear old Eugene!" (023-110–111). Eugene is moved nearly to tears; Isabel calls it "making a factory visit into such a kind celebration" (023-138). Yet George and Lucy, the younger generation, laugh at their parents' sentimentality — "People that age always are. They get sentimental over anything at all. Factories or restaurants, it doesn't matter what!" (023-140–141) — a moment of youthful detachment that proves bitterly ironic given what the factory will come to mean for George's own destiny.

### Instrument of Rejection

The factory also functions as a point of class hostility. At the Major's dinner, George attacks automobiles directly:

> "Automobiles are a useless nuisance," George answered, repeating not only the words but the tone in which he had uttered them. And he added, "They'll never amount to anything but a nuisance. They had no business to be invented." (026-037–040)

The Major is horrified at this rudeness to a guest: "Of course you forget that Mr. Morgan makes them, and also did his share in inventing them" (026-041–043). George's dismissal of the automobile is simultaneously a dismissal of Eugene Morgan as a person — a refusal to grant legitimacy to the world the factory represents.

Eugene, with characteristic generosity, deflects the insult philosophically:

> "I'm not sure he's wrong about automobiles," he said. "With all their speed forward they may be a step backward in civilization — that is, in spiritual civilization. It may be that they will not add to the beauty of the world, nor to the life of men's souls. I am not sure. But automobiles have come, and they bring a greater change in our life than most of us suspect. They are here, and almost all outward things are going to be different because of what they bring." (026-048–054)

The factory's proprietor understands what its detractor cannot: the change the factory heralds is not a matter of opinion but of historical force.

### Final Irony

In the novel's closing movement, the factory becomes the place that George — now fallen, working in a nitroglycerin plant — cannot enter. Eugene's friend Kinney suggests "that a position might be made for George in the Morgan factory" (047-055), but Eugene refuses and knows George would refuse: "he knew full well the proud devil wouldn't have taken it from him" (047-065). The factory that George once disdained has become a position he is too proud — and too fallen — to accept. The instrument of the new age that George scorned has become the salvation he cannot bring himself to receive.

## Associated Characters

### Eugene Morgan

The factory is Eugene Morgan's creation and the instrument of his transformation from a disgraced former suitor into Midland's most successful citizen. Where Major Amberson had "made a fortune" in 1873, Eugene makes his in the first decade of the twentieth century — through manufacturing rather than speculation, through creation rather than acquisition. The factory manifests Eugene's character: patient (he endures early failures), innovative (he continuously improves his designs), and generous (he declines investment offers to keep control of his vision).

### George Amberson Minafer

[[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]'s relationship to the factory is defined by contempt that becomes a kind of self-fulfilling prophecy. He calls automobiles a "useless nuisance" (026-037), dismisses the profession of "engine driver" as beneath a gentleman (023-055–058), and is "excessively bored" by the factory tour (023-078). Years later, when he might benefit from the factory's prosperity, his own pride — and Eugene's lingering bitterness — foreclose the possibility. The factory that George ridiculed becomes the measure of how far he has fallen.

### Isabel Amberson

Isabel's visit to the factory produces the novel's most sustained description of the plant and reveals her essential character: generous, forward-looking, and emotionally honest. Unlike her son, she sees the factory for what it is — "a splendid, humming thing" (023-105) — and her open delight transforms a routine tour into a moment of genuine celebration. Her happiness for Eugene's success is inseparable from her unspoken love for him, making the factory tour one of the novel's most poignant set-pieces.

### Lucy Morgan

Lucy serves as the factory's unofficial ambassador to the Amberson family, guiding them through the machinery and handling her father's amusement at "the mistakes she made" in her explanations (023-080–081). Her competence and ease in the industrial setting contrast with George's studied boredom, underscoring the gap between the two young people that will ultimately prove unbridgeable.

### Fanny Minafer

Fanny's response to the factory is one of "bleakness that overmatched his boredom" (023-082) — a hostility rooted not in aristocratic principle, as George's is, but in personal anxiety. She fears what the factory represents: Eugene's rising prosperity and the corresponding decline of her own family's fortunes. Her bleak gaze during the tour foreshadows her later financial ruin.

## Symbolism

Morgan's Automobile Factory is the novel's most concentrated symbol of the forces that Tarkington saw transforming American life in the early twentieth century. Its symbolic dimensions operate on multiple levels.

### The Triumph of Making Over Being

The novel's central philosophical tension is articulated by one of George's college friends: "Don't you think that being things is rather better than doing things?" (021-099–100). George adopts this creed wholesale, spending four years at college "being" a gentleman rather than learning to "do" anything. The factory represents the opposite principle: it is a place of pure doing, of making, of transforming raw materials and human ingenuity into tangible products. Eugene Morgan, who "did his share in inventing" the automobile (026-042), embodies the "doing" that George and his class disdain. The factory's triumph — and the Ambersons' collapse — is Tarkington's verdict on which principle prevails in the modern world.

### The Irreversibility of Change

The factory is the physical evidence of a transformation that cannot be undone. Eugene tells the Major at dinner: "Automobiles have come, and they bring a greater change in our life than most of us suspect. They are here, and almost all outward things are going to be different because of what they bring" (026-052–054). The factory — "all shiny steel, clicking and buzzing away" (023-105) — is the engine room of that change, operating continuously, indifferent to the preferences of those who would prefer the world to stay as it was. Tarkington's description of Midland's later industrial growth makes the point explicitly: "new factory districts were thundering into life" (037-038), and the city itself "came to be like the body of a great dirty man, skinned, to show his busy works" (037-039–040).

### The Democratic Alternative to Aristocracy

Where the Amberson Mansion is exclusive, hierarchical, and closed, the factory is — at least in its idealized depiction — open, meritocratic, and dynamic. Its workmen are not deferential servants but "muscled looking men and yet so intelligent looking" (023-106–107). Its proprietor earned his position through talent and persistence, not inheritance. When George says that being "a sort of engine driver" doesn't seem to him "precisely the thing a gentleman ought to do" — "Too much overalls and monkey-wrenches and grease!" (023-055–058) — he is rejecting not merely an occupation but an entire social vision in which dignity derives from work rather than from status. The factory, by flourishing, proves that vision correct.

### The Engine of Amberson Decline

| Aspect | Amberson Mansion | Morgan's Factory |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Source of wealth** | Inheritance, land speculation | Invention, manufacturing |
| **Relationship to time** | Preserves the past ("gas drop-light lamp," refusal to wire for electricity) | Creates the future (continuous improvements, racing triumphs) |
| **Social organization** | Hierarchical (servants, family precedence) | Meritocratic (skilled workmen, innovation rewarded) |
| **Physical state** | Decaying (boarding-houses encroaching, taxes lowered) | Growing ("turning out a car and a quarter a day") |
| **Sound** | Silence, creaking rocking-chairs | "Clicking and buzzing," "humming" |
| **Light** | Gas lamps, shadows, dusk | "Shiny steel," daylight industry |
| **Geographic orientation** | East (the old town, National Avenue) | West (the outskirts, the direction of expansion) |

The factory does not merely coexist with the Ambersons' decline; it actively causes it. Eugene's manufacturing prosperity draws capital, labor, and prestige away from the old landed wealth. The automobile itself — the factory's product — reshapes the city, making "farther out" accessible and draining value from Amberson Addition. As George Amberson (the uncle) ruefully notes, the Major's property taxes were lowered because "the property has gone down in value" (037-098), while prices "farther out" — where the factory's economic pull extends — were "magnificent" (037-101).

### The Two-Sided Nature of Progress

Tarkington does not sentimentalize the factory or the industrial order it represents. Even as the novel celebrates Eugene's success, it acknowledges the costs. By the later chapters, Midland has become blighted by smoke and grime:

> They had one supreme theory: that the perfect beauty and happiness of cities and of human life was to be brought about by more factories; they had a mania for factories; there was nothing they would not do to cajole a factory away from another city; and they were never more piteously embittered than when another city cajoled one away from them. (037-016–020)

The boosters' creed — "The more dirt, the more prosperity" (037-031) — has turned the city foul:

> In truth, the city came to be like the body of a great dirty man, skinned, to show his busy works, yet wearing a few barbaric ornaments; and such a figure carved, coloured, and discoloured, and set up in the market-place, would have done well enough as the god of the new people. (037-039–044)

Lucy has had to "give up her blue-and-white curtains and her white walls" and repaint her house "the dark green nearest to black" (037-057–059). The factory that Isabel found so beautiful has become one among many, and the cumulative effect of industrialization has been environmental degradation that spares no one — not even the Morgans.

This ambivalence is captured in Eugene's own musing about whether automobiles "may be a step backward in civilization — that is, in spiritual civilization" (026-049–050). The factory's creator understands that the world he is building may not, in every respect, be better than the one he is helping to dismantle.
