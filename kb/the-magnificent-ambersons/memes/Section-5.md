[005-001]“Welcome, Friend of the Ace.”

[005-002]“Welcome, Friend of the Ace,” Georgie responded, and all of the other

[005-003]boys repeated the words, “Welcome, Friend of the Ace.”

[005-004]“Take your seat in the secret semicircle,” said the presiding officer.

[005-005]“We will now proceed to—”

[005-006]But Georgie was disposed to be informal. He interrupted, turning to

[005-007]the boy who had admitted him: “Look here, Charlie Johnson, what’s Fred

[005-008]Kinney doing in the president’s chair? That’s my place, isn’t it? What

[005-009]you men been up to here, anyhow? Didn’t you all agree I was to be

[005-010]president just the same, even if I was away at school?”

[005-011]“Well—” said Charlie Johnson uneasily. “Listen! I didn’t have much to

[005-012]do with it. Some of the other members thought that long as you weren’t

[005-013]in town or anything, and Fred gave the sideboard, why—”

[005-014]Mr. Kinney, presiding, held in his hand, in lieu of a gavel, and

[005-015]considered much more impressive, a Civil War relic known as a

[005-016]“horse-pistol.” He rapped loudly for order. “All Friends of the Ace

[005-017]will take their seats!” he said sharply. “I’m president of the F. O.

[005-018]T. A. now, [[george-amberson-minafer|George Minafer]], and don’t you forget it! You and Charlie

[005-019]Johnson sit down, because I was elected perfectly fair, and we’re goin’

[005-020]to hold a meeting here.”

[005-021]“Oh, you are, are you?” said George skeptically.

[005-022]Charlie Johnson thought to mollify him. “Well, didn’t we call this

[005-023]meeting just especially because you told us to? You said yourself we

[005-024]ought to have a kind of celebration because you’ve got back to town,

[005-025]George, and that’s what we’re here for now, and everything. What do you

[005-026]care about being president? All it amounts to is just calling the roll

[005-027]and—”

[005-028]The president _de facto_ hammered the table. “This meeting will now

[005-029]proceed to—”

[005-030]“No, it won’t,” said George, and he advanced to the desk, laughing

[005-031]contemptuously. “Get off that platform.”

[005-032]“This meeting will come to order!” Mr. Kinney commanded fiercely.

[005-033]“You put down that gavel,” said George. “Whose is it, I’d like to know?

[005-034]It belongs to my grandfather, and you quit hammering it that way or

[005-035]you’ll break it, and I’ll have to knock your head off.”

[005-036]“This meeting will come to order! I was legally elected here, and I’m

[005-037]not going to be bulldozed!”

[005-038]“All right,” said Georgie. “You’re president. Now we’ll hold another

[005-039]election.”

[005-040]“We will not!” Fred Kinney shouted. “We’ll have our reg’lar meeting,

[005-041]and then we’ll play euchre & nickel a corner, what we’re here for. This

[005-042]meeting will now come to ord—”

[005-043]Georgie addressed the members. “I’d like to know who got up this thing

[005-044]in the first place,” he said. “Who’s the founder of the F.O.T.A., if

[005-045]you please? Who got this room rent free? Who got the janitor to let us

[005-046]have most of this furniture? You suppose you could keep this clubroom

[005-047]a minute if I told my grandfather I didn’t want it for a literary club

[005-048]any more? I’d like to say a word on how you members been acting, too!

[005-049]When I went away I said I didn’t care if you had a _vice_-president or

[005-050]something while I was gone, but here I hardly turned my back and you

[005-051]had to go and elect Fred Kinney president! Well, if that’s what you

[005-052]want, you can have it. I was going to have a little celebration down

[005-053]here some night pretty soon, and bring some port wine, like we drink

[005-054]at school in our crowd there, and I was going to get my grandfather to

[005-055]give the club an extra room across the hall, and prob’ly I could get my

[005-056][[george-amberson|Uncle George]] to give us his old billiard table, because he’s got a new

[005-057]one, and the club could put it in the other room. Well, you got a new

[005-058]president now!” Here Georgie moved toward the door and his tone became

[005-059]plaintive, though undeniably there was disdain beneath his sorrow. “I

[005-060]guess all I better do is—resign!”

[005-061]And he opened the door, apparently intending to withdraw.

[005-062]“All in favour of having a new election,” Charlie Johnson shouted

[005-063]hastily, “say, ‘Aye’!”

[005-064]“Aye” was said by everyone present except Mr. Kinney, who began a hot

[005-065]protest, but it was immediately smothered.

[005-066]“All in favour of me being president instead of Fred Kinney,” shouted

[005-067]Georgie, “say ‘Aye.’ The ‘Ayes’ have it!” “I resign,” said the

[005-068]red-headed boy, gulping as he descended from the platform. “I resign

[005-069]from the club!”

[005-070]Hot-eyed, he found his hat and departed, jeers echoing after him as he

[005-071]plunged down the corridor. Georgie stepped upon the platform, and took

[005-072]up the emblem of office.

[005-073]“Ole red-head Fred’ll be around next week,” said the new chairman.

[005-074]“He’ll be around boot-lickin’ to get us to take him back in again, but

[005-075]I guess we don’t want him: that fellow always was a trouble-maker. We

[005-076]will now proceed with our meeting. Well, fellows, I suppose you want

[005-077]to hear from your president. I don’t know that I have much to say, as

[005-078]I have already seen most of you a few times since I got back. I had a

[005-079]good time at the old school, back East, but had a little trouble with

[005-080]the faculty and came on home. My family stood by me as well as I could

[005-081]ask, and I expect to stay right here in the old town until whenever I

[005-082]decide to enter college. Now, I don’t suppose there’s any more business

[005-083]before the meeting. I guess we might as well play cards. Anybody that’s

[005-084]game for a little quarter-limit poker or any limit they say, why I’d

[005-085]like to have ’em sit at the president’s card-table.”

[005-086]When the diversions of the Friends of the Ace were concluded for that

[005-087]afternoon, Georgie invited his chief supporter, Mr. Charlie Johnson, to

[005-088]drive home with him to dinner, and as they jingled up [[national-avenue|National Avenue]]

[005-089]in the dog-cart, Charlie asked:

[005-090]“What sort of men did you run up against at that school, George?”

[005-091]“Best crowd there: finest set of men I ever met.”

[005-092]“How’d you get in with ’em?”

[005-093]Georgie laughed. “I let them get in with me, Charlie,” he said in a

[005-094]tone of gentle explanation. “It’s vulgar to do any other way. Did I

[005-095]tell you the nickname they gave me—‘King’? That was what they called me

[005-096]at that school, ‘King Minafer.”

[005-097]“How’d they happen to do that?” his friend asked innocently.

[005-098]“Oh, different things,” George answered lightly. “Of course, any of ’em

[005-099]that came from anywhere out in this part the country knew about the

[005-100]family and all that, and so I suppose it was a good deal on account

[005-101]of—oh, on account of the family and the way I do things, most likely.”

Chapter IV

[005-102]When Mr. George Amberson Minafer came home for the holidays at

[005-103]Christmastide, in his sophomore year, probably no great change had

[005-104]taken place inside him, but his exterior was visibly altered. Nothing

[005-105]about him encouraged any hope that he had received his come-upance; on

[005-106]the contrary, the yearners for that stroke of justice must yearn even

[005-107]more itchingly: the gilded youth’s manner had become polite, but his

[005-108]politeness was of a kind which democratic people found hard to bear.

[005-109]In a word, M. le Due had returned from the gay life of the capital

[005-110]to show himself for a week among the loyal peasants belonging to the

[005-111]old chateau, and their quaint habits and costumes afforded him a mild

[005-112]amusement.

[005-113]Cards were out for a ball in his honour, and this pageant of the

[005-114]tenantry was held in the ballroom of [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Mansion the night

[005-115]after his arrival. It was, as Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster said of

[005-116]Isabel’s wedding, “a big Amberson-style thing,” though that wise Mrs.

[005-117]Henry Franklin Foster had long ago gone the way of all wisdom, having

[005-118]stepped out of the [[downtown-midland|Midland town]], unquestionably into heaven—a long

[005-119]step, but not beyond her powers. She had successors, but no successor;

[005-120]the town having grown too large to confess that it was intellectually

[005-121]led and literarily authoritated by one person; and some of these

[005-122]successors were not invited to the ball, for dimensions were now so

[005-123]metropolitan that intellectual leaders and literary authorities loomed

[005-124]in outlying regions unfamiliar to the Ambersons. However, all “old

[005-125]citizens” recognizable as gentry received cards, and of course so did

[005-126]their dancing descendants.

[005-127]The orchestra and the caterer were brought from away, in the Amberson

[005-128]manner, though this was really a gesture—perhaps one more of habit

[005-129]than of ostentation—for servitors of gaiety as proficient as these

[005-130]importations were nowadays to be found in the town. Even flowers and

[005-131]plants and roped vines were brought from afar—not, however, until

[005-132]the stock of the local florists proved insufficient to obliterate

[005-133]the interior structure of the big house, in the Amberson way. It was

[005-134]the last of the great, long remembered dances that “everybody talked

[005-135]about”—there were getting to be so many people in town that no later

[005-136]than the next year there were too many for “everybody” to hear of even

[005-137]such a ball as the Ambersons’.

[005-138]George, white-gloved, with a gardenia in his buttonhole, stood with his

[005-139]mother and the Major, embowered in the big red and gold drawing room

[005-140]downstairs, to “receive” the guests; and, standing thus together, the

[005-141]trio offered a picturesque example of good looks persistent through

[005-142]three generations. [[major-amberson|The Major]], his daughter, and his grandson were of

[005-143]a type all Amberson: tall, straight, and regular, with dark eyes,

[005-144]short noses, good chins; and the grandfather’s expression, no less

[005-145]than the grandson’s, was one of faintly amused condescension. There

[005-146]was a difference, however. The grandson’s unlined young face had

[005-147]nothing to offer except this condescension; the grandfather’s had other

[005-148]things to say. It was a handsome, worldly old face, conscious of its

[005-149]importance, but persuasive rather than arrogant, and not without tokens

[005-150]of sufferings withstood. The Major’s short white hair was parted in the

[005-151]middle, like his grandson’s, and in all he stood as briskly equipped to

[005-152]the fashion as exquisite young George.

[005-153]Isabel, standing between her father and her son caused a vague

[005-154]amazement in the mind of the latter. Her age, just under forty, was

[005-155]for George a thought of something as remote as the moons of Jupiter:

[005-156]he could not possibly have conceived such an age ever coming to be

[005-157]his own: five years was the limit of his thinking in time. Five years

[005-158]ago he had been a child not yet fourteen; and those five years were

[005-159]an abyss. Five years hence he would be almost twenty-four; what the

[005-160]girls he knew called “one of the older men.” He could imagine himself

[005-161]at twenty-four, but beyond that, his powers staggered and refused

[005-162]the task. He saw little essential difference between thirty-eight

[005-163]and eighty-eight, and his mother was to him not a woman but wholly

[005-164]a mother. He had no perception of her other than as an adjunct to

[005-165]himself, his mother; nor could he imagine her thinking or doing

[005-166]anything—falling in love, walking with a friend, or reading a book—as

[005-167]a woman, and not as his mother. The woman, Isabel, was a stranger to

[005-168]her son; as completely a stranger as if he had never in his life seen

[005-169]her or heard her voice. And it was to-night, while he stood with her,

[005-170]“receiving,” that he caught a disquieting glimpse of this stranger whom

[005-171]he thus fleetingly encountered for the first time.

[005-172]Youth cannot imagine romance apart from youth. That is why the rôles of

[005-173]the heroes and heroines of plays are given by the managers to the most

[005-174]youthful actors they can find among the competent. Both middle-aged

[005-175]people and young people enjoy a play about young lovers; but only

[005-176]middle-aged people will tolerate a play about middle-aged lovers; young

[005-177]people will not come to see such a play, because, for them, middle-aged

[005-178]lovers are a joke—not a very funny one. Therefore, to bring both the

[005-179]middle-aged people and the young people into his house, the manager

[005-180]makes his romance as young as he can. Youth will indeed be served, and

[005-181]its profound instinct is to be not only scornfully amused but vaguely

[005-182]angered by middle-age romance. So, standing beside his mother, George

[005-183]was disturbed by a sudden impression, coming upon him out of nowhere,

[005-184]so far as he could detect, that her eyes were brilliant, that she was

[005-185]graceful and youthful—in a word, that she was romantically lovely.
