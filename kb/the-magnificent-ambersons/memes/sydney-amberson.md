# Sydney Amberson

> "Sydney was an Amberson exaggerated, and Rex Corliss used to say that Uncle George had got the diplomatic position Sydney should have had." (006-175–006-181)

Sydney Amberson is the novel's most vivid example of Amberson magnificence as pure performance. Where his brother George Amberson earned a diplomatic post through competence, Sydney merely looks the part — "an Amberson exaggerated," complete with "an Edward the Seventh beard" (006-177) and the manner of a man who believes the world owes him a grand stage. He is, in Tarkington's economical characterization, everything that is both impressive and hollow about the family name.

## Role in the Novel

Sydney functions as a satirical mirror held up to the Amberson family's self-regard. His principal appearance occurs in Chapter 6, where he and his wife Amelia arrive for a family gathering. The narrator describes him with an irony that is unmistakable: Sydney embodies the Amberson ideal so completely that he tips over into self-parody. His Edward the Seventh beard, his "ambassadorial" bearing (despite never having been an ambassador), his conviction that the world should arrange itself around his importance — all of these mark him as a specimen of a type that the novel simultaneously celebrates and critiques.

The contrast with his brother George is carefully drawn. "Uncle George had got the diplomatic position Sydney should have had" (006-181) — the word "should" carries the full weight of Sydney's self-conception. In his own mind, his Amberson birth entitles him to diplomatic status; the fact that he never achieved it is, the novel implies, both a judgment on his character and a commentary on the gap between Amberson self-image and Amberson reality.

## Relationships

**Amelia Amberson (wife)**: Sydney's marriage to Amelia mirrors the dynastic pattern of the Amberson family — she is "an Amberson born, herself" (006-181), making their union one of consolidated family identity rather than external alliance. The narrator describes Amelia as "beautifully upholstered" (006-181), a phrase that captures both the luxury and the inertness of their shared existence. Together, Sydney and Amelia represent the Amberson tendency toward self-enclosure — marrying within the family circle, living within its assumptions, never questioning its premises.

**Major Amberson (father)**: As the Major's son, Sydney is a direct inheritor of the family fortune and status. Unlike his father, however, Sydney appears to have done nothing to augment or even maintain what he inherited. He is a pure consumer of the Amberson legacy — a living demonstration of what happens when magnificence is passed down without the discipline that created it.

## Themes

Sydney Amberson is the novel's sharpest illustration of the risks inherent in inherited magnificence. He has all the outward markers of Amberson status — the bearing, the beard, the expectation of deference — without any of the substance that originally justified that status. He is what happens when the Amberson name becomes a costume rather than a credential: impressive at a distance, hollow up close.

The narrator's judgment is delivered through humor rather than condemnation, but it is judgment nonetheless. In a novel that traces the decline of a family, Sydney is less a cause of decline than a symptom — proof that the rot was already present in the generation before George Amberson Minafer accelerated it to its catastrophic conclusion.
