[029-001]“You told me,” said George, fiercely, “that mother never saw him except

[029-002]when she was chaperoning you.”

[029-003]“They weren’t much alone together, then,” [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] returned. “Hardly

[029-004]ever, before Wilbur died. But you don’t suppose that stops people from

[029-005]talking, do you? Your father never went anywhere, and people saw [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]

[029-006]with her everywhere she went—and though I was with them people just

[029-007]thought”—she choked—“they just thought I didn’t count! ‘Only old Fanny

[029-008]Minafer,’ I suppose they’d say! Besides, everybody knew that he’d been

[029-009]engaged to her—”

[029-010]“What’s that?” George cried.

[029-011]“Everybody knows it. Don’t you remember your grandfather speaking of it

[029-012]at the Sunday dinner one night?”

[029-013]“He didn’t say they were engaged or—”

[029-014]“Well, they were! Everybody knows it; and she broke it off on account

[029-015]of that serenade when Eugene didn’t know what he was doing. He drank

[029-016]when he was a young man, and she wouldn’t stand it, but everybody in

[029-017]this town knows that Isabel has never really cared for any other man in

[029-018]her life! Poor Wilbur! He was the only soul alive that didn’t know it!”

[029-019]Nightmare had descended upon the unfortunate George; he leaned back

[029-020]against the foot-board of his bed, gazing wildly at his aunt. “I

[029-021]believe I’m going crazy,” he said. “You mean when you told me there

[029-022]wasn’t any talk, you told me a falsehood?”

[029-023]“No!” Fanny gasped.

[029-024]“You did!”

[029-025]“I tell you I didn’t know how much talk there was, and it wouldn’t have

[029-026]amounted to much if Wilbur had lived.” And Fanny completed this with a

[029-027]fatal admission: “I didn’t want you to interfere.”

[029-028]George overlooked the admission; his mind was not now occupied with

[029-029]analysis. “What do you mean,” he asked, “when you say that if father

[029-030]had lived, the talk wouldn’t have amounted to anything?”

[029-031]“Things might have been—they might have been different.”

[029-032]“You mean Morgan might have married you?”

[029-033]Fanny gulped. “No. Because I don’t know that I’d have accepted him.”

[029-034]She had ceased to weep, and now she sat up stiffly. “I certainly didn’t

[029-035]care enough about him to marry him; I wouldn’t have let myself care

[029-036]that much until he showed that he wished to marry me. I’m not that sort

[029-037]of person!” The poor lady paid her vanity this piteous little tribute.

[029-038]“What I mean is, if Wilbur hadn’t died, people wouldn’t have had it

[029-039]proved before their very eyes that what they’d been talking about was

[029-040]true!”

[029-041]“You say—you say that people believe—” George shuddered, then forced

[029-042]himself to continue, in a sick voice: “They believe my mother is—is in

[029-043]love with that man?”

[029-044]“Of course!”

[029-045]“And because he comes here—and they see her with him driving—and all

[029-046]that—they think they were right when they said she was in—in love with

[029-047]him before—before my father died?”

[029-048]She looked at him gravely with her eyes now dry between their reddened

[029-049]lids. “Why, George,” she said, gently, “don’t you know that’s what they

[029-050]say? You must know that everybody in town thinks they’re going to be

[029-051]married very soon.”

[029-052]George uttered an incoherent cry; and sections of him appeared to

[029-053]writhe. He was upon the verge of actual nausea.

[029-054]“You know it!” Fanny cried, getting up. “You don’t think I’d have

[029-055]spoken of it to you unless I was sure you knew it?” Her voice was

[029-056]wholly genuine, as it had been throughout the wretched interview:

[029-057]Fanny’s sincerity was unquestionable. “George, I wouldn’t have told

[029-058]you, if you didn’t know. What other reason could you have for treating

[029-059]Eugene as you did, or for refusing to speak to them like that a while

[029-060]ago in the yard? Somebody must have told you?”

[029-061]“Who told you?” he said.

[029-062]“What?”

[029-063]“Who told you there was talk? Where is this talk? Where does it come

[029-064]from? Who does it?”

[029-065]“Why, I suppose pretty much everybody,” she said. “I know it must be

[029-066]pretty general.”

[029-067]“Who said so?”

[029-068]“What?”

[029-069]George stepped close to her. “You say people don’t speak to a person of

[029-070]gossip about that person’s family. Well, how did you hear it, then? How

[029-071]did you get hold of it? Answer me!”

[029-072]Fanny looked thoughtful. “Well, of course nobody not one’s most

[029-073]intimate friends would speak to them about such things, and then only

[029-074]in the kindest, most considerate way.”

[029-075]“Who’s spoken of it to you in any way at all?” George demanded.

[029-076]“Why—” Fanny hesitated.

[029-077]“You answer me!”

[029-078]“I hardly think it would be fair to give names.”

[029-079]“Look here,” said George. “One of your most intimate friends is that

[029-080]mother of Charlie Johnson’s, for instance. Has she ever mentioned this

[029-081]to you? You say everybody is talking. Is she one?”

[029-082]“Oh, she may have intimated—”

[029-083]“I’m asking you: Has she ever spoken of it to you?”

[029-084]“She’s a very kind, discreet woman, George; but she may have intimated—”

[029-085]George had a sudden intuition, as there flickered into his mind the

[029-086]picture of a street-crossing and two absorbed ladies almost run down

[029-087]by a fast horse. “You and she have been talking about it to-day!” he

[029-088]cried. “You were talking about it with her not two hours ago. Do you

[029-089]deny it?”

[029-090]“I—”

[029-091]“Do you deny it?”

[029-092]“No!”

[029-093]“All right,” said George. “That’s enough!”

[029-094]She caught at his arm as he turned away. “What are you going to do,

[029-095]George?”

[029-096]“I’ll not talk about it, now,” he said heavily. “I think you’ve done a

[029-097]good deal for one day, Aunt Fanny!”

[029-098]And Fanny, seeing the passion in his face, began to be alarmed. She

[029-099]tried to retain possession of the black velvet sleeve which her fingers

[029-100]had clutched, and he suffered her to do so, but used this leverage to

[029-101]urge her to the door. “George, you know I’m sorry for you, whether you

[029-102]care or not,” she whimpered. “I never in the world would have spoken of

[029-103]it, if I hadn’t thought you knew all about it. I wouldn’t have—”

[029-104]But he had opened the door with his free hand. “Never mind!” he said,

[029-105]and she was obliged to pass out into the hall, the door closing quickly

[029-106]behind her.

Chapter XXII

[029-107]George took off his dressing-gown and put on a collar and a tie, his

[029-108]fingers shaking so that the tie was not his usual success; then he

[029-109]picked up his coat and waistcoat, and left the room while still in

[029-110]process of donning them, fastening the buttons, as he ran down the

[029-111]front stairs to the door. It was not until he reached the middle of the

[029-112]street that he realized that he had forgotten his hat; and he paused

[029-113]for an irresolute moment, during which his eye wandered, for no reason,

[029-114]to the Fountain of Neptune. This castiron replica of too elaborate

[029-115]sculpture stood at the next corner, where the Major had placed it when

[029-116][[amberson-addition|the Addition]] was laid out so long ago. The street corners had been

[029-117]shaped to conform with the great octagonal basin, which was no great

[029-118]inconvenience for horse-drawn vehicles, but a nuisance to speeding

[029-119]automobiles; and, even as George looked, one of the latter, coming

[029-120]too fast, saved itself only by a dangerous skid as it rounded the

[029-121]fountain. This skid was to George’s liking, though he would have been

[029-122]more pleased to see the car go over, for he was wishing grief and

[029-123]destruction, just then, upon all the automobiles in the world.

[029-124]His eyes rested a second or two longer upon the Fountain of Neptune,

[029-125]not an enlivening sight even in the shielding haze of autumn twilight.

[029-126]For more than a year no water had run in the fountain: the connections

[029-127]had been broken, and the Major was evasive about restorations, even

[029-128]when reminded by his grandson that a dry fountain is as gay as a dry

[029-129]fish. Soot streaks and a thousand pits gave Neptune the distinction,

[029-130]at least, of leprosy, which the mermaids associated with him had been

[029-131]consistent in catching; and his trident had been so deeply affected as

[029-132]to drop its prongs. Altogether, this heavy work of heavy art, smoked

[029-133]dry, hugely scabbed, cracked, and crumbling, was a dismal sight to the

[029-134]distracted eye of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]], and its present condition

[029-135]of craziness may have added a mite to his own. His own was sufficient,

[029-136]with no additions, however, as he stood looking at the Johnsons’ house

[029-137]and those houses on both sides of it—that row of riffraff dwellings he

[029-138]had thought so damnable, the day when he stood in his grandfather’s

[029-139]yard, staring at them, after hearing what his [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]] said of the

[029-140]“talk” about his mother.

[029-141]He decided that he needed no hat for the sort of call he intended to

[029-142]make, and went forward hurriedly. Mrs. Johnson was at home, the Irish

[029-143]girl who came to the door informed him, and he was left to await the

[029-144]lady, in a room like an elegant well—the Johnsons’ “reception room”:

[029-145]floor space, nothing to mention; walls, blue calcimined; ceiling,

[029-146]twelve feet from the floor; inside shutters and gray lace curtains;

[029-147]five gilt chairs, a brocaded sofa, soiled, and an inlaid walnut table,

[029-148]supporting two tall alabaster vases; a palm, with two leaves, dying in

[029-149]a corner.

[029-150]Mrs. Johnson came in, breathing noticeably; and her round head,

[029-151]smoothly but economically decorated with the hair of an honest woman,

[029-152]seemed to be lingering far in the background of the Alpine bosom which

[029-153]took precedence of the rest of her everywhere; but when she was all

[029-154]in the room, it was to be seen that her breathing was the result of

[029-155]hospitable haste to greet the visitor, and her hand, not so dry as

[029-156]Neptune’s Fountain, suggested that she had paused for only the briefest

[029-157]ablutions. George accepted this cold, damp lump mechanically.

[029-158]“[[george-amberson|Mr. Amberson]]—I mean [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]]!” she exclaimed. “I’m really

[029-159]delighted: I understood you asked for me. Mr. Johnson’s out of the

[029-160]city, but Charlie’s downtown and I’m looking for him at any minute,

[029-161]now, and he’ll be so pleased that you—”

[029-162]“I didn’t want to see Charlie,” George said. “I want—”

[029-163]“Do sit down,” the hospitable lady urged him, seating herself upon the

[029-164]sofa. “Do sit down.”

[029-165]“No, I thank you. I wish—”

[029-166]“Surely you’re not going to run away again, when you’ve just come. Do

[029-167]sit down, Mr. Minafer. I hope you’re all well at your house and at the

[029-168]dear old Major’s, too. He’s looking—”

[029-169]“Mrs. Johnson” George said, in a strained loud voice which arrested her

[029-170]attention immediately, so that she was abruptly silent, leaving her

[029-171]surprised mouth open. She had already been concealing some astonishment

[029-172]at this unexampled visit, however, and the condition of George’s

[029-173]ordinarily smooth hair (for he had overlooked more than his hat) had

[029-174]not alleviated her perplexity. “Mrs. Johnson,” he said, “I have come

[029-175]to ask you a few questions which I would like you to answer, if you

[029-176]please.”

[029-177]She became grave at once. “Certainly, Mr. Minafer. Anything I can—”

[029-178]He interrupted sternly, yet his voice shook in spite of its sternness.

[029-179]“You were talking with my Aunt Fanny about my mother this afternoon.”

[029-180]At this Mrs. Johnson uttered an involuntary gasp, but she recovered

[029-181]herself. “Then I’m sure our conversation was a very pleasant one, if we

[029-182]were talking of your mother, because—”

[029-183]Again he interrupted. “My aunt has told me what the conversation

[029-184]virtually was, and I don’t mean to waste any time, Mrs. Johnson.

[029-185]You were talking about a—” George’s shoulders suddenly heaved

[029-186]uncontrollably; but he went fiercely on: “You were discussing a [[gossip-and-reputation|scandal]]

[029-187]that involved my mother’s name.”

[029-188]“Mr. Minafer!”

[029-189]“Isn’t that the truth?”

[029-190]“I don’t feel called upon to answer, Mr. Minafer,” she said with

[029-191]visible agitation. “I do not consider that you have any right—”

[029-192]“My aunt told me you repeated this scandal to her.”

[029-193]“I don’t think your aunt can have said that,” Mrs. Johnson returned

[029-194]sharply. “I did not repeat a scandal of any kind to your aunt and I

[029-195]think you are mistaken in saying she told you I did. We may have

[029-196]discussed some matters that have been a topic of comment about town—”
