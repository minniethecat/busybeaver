[015-001]“Papa, I think they were shocking. Weren’t they _awful!_”

[015-002]“Just—just boys!” he moaned, wiping his eyes. But [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] could not smile

[015-003]at all; she was beginning to look indignant. “I can forgive that poor

[015-004]Fred Kinney,” she said. “He’s just blundering—but George—oh, George

[015-005]behaved outrageously!”

[015-006]“It’s a difficult age,” her father observed, his calmness somewhat

[015-007]restored. “Girls don’t seem to have to pass through it quite as boys

[015-008]do, or their _savoir faire_ is instinctive—or something!” And he gave

[015-009]away to a return of his convulsion.

[015-010]She came and sat upon the arm of his chair. “Papa, _why_ should George

[015-011]behave like that?”

[015-012]“He’s sensitive.”

[015-013]“Rather! But why is he? He does anything he likes to, without any

[015-014]regard for what people think. Then why should he mind so furiously when

[015-015]the least little thing reflects upon him, or on anything or anybody

[015-016]connected with him?”

[015-017][[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] patted her hand. “That’s one of the greatest puzzles of human

[015-018]vanity, dear; and I don’t pretend to know the answer. In all my life,

[015-019]the most arrogant people that I’ve known have been the most sensitive.

[015-020]The people who have done the most in contempt of other people’s

[015-021]opinion, and who consider themselves the highest above it, have been

[015-022]the most furious if it went against them. Arrogant and domineering

[015-023]people can’t stand the least, lightest, faintest breath of criticism.

[015-024]It just kills them.”

[015-025]“Papa, do you think George is _terribly_ arrogant and domineering?”

[015-026]“Oh, he’s still only a boy,” said Eugene consolingly. “There’s plenty

[015-027]of fine stuff in him—can’t help but be, because he’s [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]]’s

[015-028]son.”

[015-029]Lucy stroked his hair, which was still almost as dark as her own. “You

[015-030]liked her pretty well once, I guess, papa.”

[015-031]“I do still,” he said quietly.

[015-032]“She’s lovely—lovely! Papa—” she paused, then continued—“I wonder

[015-033]sometimes—”

[015-034]“What?”

[015-035]“I wonder just how she happened to marry [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]].”

[015-036]“Oh, Minafer’s all right,” said Eugene. “He’s a quiet sort of man, but

[015-037]he’s a good man and a kind man. He always was, and those things count.”

[015-038]“But in a way—well, I’ve heard people say there wasn’t anything to him

[015-039]at all except business and saving [[wealth-and-fortune|money]]. [[fanny-minafer|Miss Fanny Minafer]] herself

[015-040]told me that everything George and his mother have of their own—that

[015-041]is, just to spend as they like—she says it has always come from Major

[015-042]Amberson.”

[015-043]“Thrift, Horatio!” said Eugene lightly. “Thrift’s an inheritance, and

[015-044]a common enough one here. The people who settled the country _had_ to

[015-045]save, so making and saving were taught as virtues, and the people, to

[015-046]the third generation, haven’t found out that making and saving are

[015-047]only means to an end. Minafer doesn’t believe in money being spent. He

[015-048]believes God made it to be invested and saved.”

[015-049]“But George isn’t saving. He’s reckless, and even if he is arrogant and

[015-050]conceited and bad-tempered, he’s awfully generous.”

[015-051]“Oh, he’s an Amberson,” said her father. “The Ambersons aren’t saving.

[015-052]They’re too much the other way, most of them.”

[015-053]“I don’t think I should have called George bad-tempered,” Lucy said

[015-054]thoughtfully. “No. I don’t think he is.”

[015-055]“Only when he’s cross about something?” Morgan suggested, with a

[015-056]semblance of sympathetic gravity.

[015-057]“Yes,” she said brightly, not perceiving that his intention was

[015-058]humorous. “All the rest of the time he’s really very amiable. Of

[015-059]course, he’s much more a perfect _child_, the whole time, than he

[015-060]realizes! He certainly behaved awfully to-night.” She jumped up, her

[015-061]indignation returning. “He did, indeed, and it won’t do to encourage

[015-062]him in it. I think he’ll find me pretty cool—for a week or so!”

[015-063]Whereupon her father suffered a renewal of his attack of uproarious

[015-064]laughter.

Chapter XI

[015-065]In the matter of coolness, George met Lucy upon her own predetermined

[015-066]ground; in fact, he was there first, and, at their next encounter,

[015-067]proved loftier and more formal than she did. Their estrangement lasted

[015-068]three weeks, and then disappeared without any preliminary treaty: it

[015-069]had worn itself out, and they forgot it.

[015-070]At times, however, George found other disturbances to the friendship.

[015-071]Lucy was “too much the village belle,” he complained; and took a

[015-072]satiric attitude toward his competitors, referring to them as her

[015-073]“local swains and bumpkins,” sulking for an afternoon when she reminded

[015-074]him that he, too, was at least “local.” She was a belle with older

[015-075]people as well; Isabel and Fanny were continually taking her driving,

[015-076]bringing her home with them to lunch or dinner, and making a hundred

[015-077]little engagements with her, and the Major had taken a great fancy

[015-078]to her, insisting upon her presence and her father’s at [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]]

[015-079]family dinner at the Mansion every Sunday evening. She knew how to

[015-080]flirt with old people, he said, as she sat next him at the table on

[015-081]one of these Sunday occasions; and he had always liked her father,

[015-082]even when Eugene was a “terror” long ago. “Oh, yes, he was!” the Major

[015-083]laughed, when she remonstrated. “He came up here with my son George and

[015-084]some others for a serenade one night, and Eugene stepped into a bass

[015-085]fiddle, and the poor musicians just gave up! I had a pretty half-hour

[015-086]getting my son George upstairs. I remember! It was the last time

[015-087]Eugene ever touched a drop—but he’d touched plenty before that, young

[015-088]lady, and he daren’t deny it! Well, well; there’s another thing that’s

[015-089]changed: hardly anybody drinks nowadays. Perhaps it’s just as well, but

[015-090]things used to be livelier. That serenade was just before Isabel was

[015-091]married—and don’t you fret, Miss Lucy: your father remembers it well

[015-092]enough!” The old gentleman burst into laughter, and shook his finger at

[015-093]Eugene across the table. “The fact is,” the Major went on hilariously,

[015-094]“I believe if Eugene hadn’t broken that bass fiddle and given himself

[015-095]away, Isabel would never have taken Wilbur! I shouldn’t be surprised if

[015-096]that was about all the reason that Wilbur got her! What do you think,

[015-097]Wilbur?”

[015-098]“I shouldn’t be surprised,” said Wilbur placidly. “If your notion is

[015-099]right, I’m glad ’Gene broke the fiddle. He was giving me a hard run!”

[015-100][[major-amberson|The Major]] always drank three glasses of champagne at his Sunday dinner,

[015-101]and he was finishing the third. “What do you say about it, Isabel? By

[015-102]Jove!” he cried, pounding the table. “She’s blushing!”

[015-103]Isabel did blush, but she laughed. “Who wouldn’t blush!” she cried, and

[015-104]her sister-in-law came to her assistance.

[015-105]“The important thing,” said Fanny jovially, “is that Wilbur did get

[015-106]her, and not only got her, but kept her!”

[015-107]Eugene was as pink as Isabel, but he laughed without any sign of

[015-108]embarrassment other than his heightened colour. “There’s another

[015-109]important thing—that is, for me,” he said. “It’s the only thing that

[015-110]makes me forgive that bass viol for getting in my way.”

[015-111]“What is it?” the Major asked.

[015-112]“Lucy,” said Morgan gently.

[015-113]Isabel gave him a quick glance, all warm approval, and there was a

[015-114]murmur of friendliness round the table.

[015-115]George was not one of those who joined in this applause. He considered

[015-116]his grandfather’s nonsense indelicate, even for second childhood, and

[015-117]he thought that the sooner the subject was dropped the better. However,

[015-118]he had only a slight recurrence of the resentment which had assailed

[015-119]him during the winter at every sign of his mother’s interest in Morgan;

[015-120]though he was still ashamed of his aunt sometimes, when it seemed to

[015-121]him that Fanny was almost publicly throwing herself at the widower’s

[015-122]head. Fanny and he had one or two arguments in which her fierceness

[015-123]again astonished and amused him.

[015-124]“You drop your criticisms of your relatives,” she bade him, hotly, one

[015-125]day, “and begin thinking a little about your own behaviour! You say

[015-126]people will ‘talk’ about my—about my merely being pleasant to an old

[015-127]friend! What do I care how they talk? I guess if people are talking

[015-128]about anybody in this family they’re talking about the impertinent

[015-129]little snippet that hasn’t any respect for anything, and doesn’t even

[015-130]know enough to attend to his own affairs!”

[015-131]“‘Snippet,’ Aunt Fanny!” George laughed. “How elegant! And ‘little

[015-132]snippet’—when I’m over five-feet-eleven?”

[015-133]“I said it!” she snapped, departing. “I don’t see how Lucy can stand

[015-134]you!”

[015-135]“You’d make an amiable stepmother-in-law!” he called after her. “I’ll

[015-136]be careful about proposing to Lucy!”

[015-137]These were but roughish spots in a summer that glided by evenly and

[015-138]quickly enough, for the most part, and, at the end, seemed to fly. On

[015-139]the last night before George went back to be a Junior, his mother asked

[015-140]him confidently if it had not been a happy summer.

[015-141]He hadn’t thought about it, he answered. “Oh, I suppose so. Why?”

[015-142]“I just thought it would be nice to hear you say so,” she said,

[015-143]smiling. “I mean, it’s pleasant for people of my age to know that

[015-144]people of your age realize that they’re happy.”

[015-145]“People of your age!” he repeated. “You know you don’t look precisely

[015-146]like an old woman, mother. Not precisely!”

[015-147]“No,” she said. “And I suppose I feel about as young as you do, inside,

[015-148]but it won’t be many years before I must begin to look old. It does

[015-149]come!” She sighed, still smiling. “It’s seemed to me that, it must have

[015-150]been a happy summer for you—a real ‘summer of roses and wine’—without

[015-151]the wine, perhaps. ‘Gather ye roses while ye may’—or was it primroses?

[015-152]Time does really fly, or perhaps it’s more like the sky—and smoke—”

[015-153]George was puzzled. “What do you mean: time being like the sky and

[015-154]smoke?”

[015-155]“I mean the things that we have and that we think are so solid—they’re

[015-156]like smoke, and time is like the sky that the smoke disappears into.

[015-157]You know how a wreath of smoke goes up from a chimney, and seems all

[015-158]thick and black and busy against the sky, as if it were going to do

[015-159]such important things and last forever, and you see it getting thinner

[015-160]and thinner—and then, in such a little while, it isn’t there at all;

[015-161]nothing is left but the sky, and the sky keeps on being just the same

[015-162]forever.”

[015-163]“It strikes me you’re getting mixed up,” said George cheerfully. “I

[015-164]don’t see much resemblance between time and the sky, or between things

[015-165]and smoke-wreaths; but I do see one reason you like Lucy Morgan so

[015-166]much. She talks that same kind of wistful, moony way sometimes—I don’t

[015-167]mean to say I mind it in either of you, because I rather like to listen

[015-168]to it, and you’ve got a very good voice, mother. It’s nice to listen

[015-169]to, no matter how much smoke and sky, and so on, you talk. So’s Lucy’s

[015-170]for that matter; and I see why you’re congenial. She talks that way to

[015-171]her father, too; and he’s right there with the same kind of guff. Well,

[015-172]it’s all right with me!” He laughed, teasingly, and allowed her to

[015-173]retain his hand, which she had fondly seized. “I’ve got plenty to think

[015-174]about when people drool along!”

[015-175]She pressed his hand to her cheek, and a tear made a tiny warm streak

[015-176]across one of his knuckles.

[015-177]“For heaven’s sake!” he said. “What’s the matter? Isn’t everything all

[015-178]right?”

[015-179]“You’re going away!”

[015-180]“Well, I’m coming back, don’t you suppose? Is that all that worries

[015-181]you?”

[015-182]She cheered up, and smiled again, but shook her head. “I never can bear

[015-183]to see you go—that’s the most of it. I’m a little bothered about your

[015-184]father, too.”

[015-185]“Why?”

[015-186]“It seems to me he looks so badly. Everybody thinks so.”

[015-187]“What nonsense!” George laughed. “He’s been looking that way all

[015-188]summer. He isn’t much different from the way he’s looked all his life,

[015-189]that I can see. What’s the matter with him?”

[015-190]“He never talks much about his business to me but I think he’s been

[015-191]worrying about some investments he made last year. I think his worry

[015-192]has affected his health.”
