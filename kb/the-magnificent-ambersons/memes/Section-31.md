[031-001]“It would be monstrous!” George shouted. “Monstrous even if this

[031-002]horrible thing hadn’t happened, but now in the face of this—oh, that

[031-003]you can sit there and even speak of it! Your own sister! O God! Oh—” He

[031-004]became incoherent, swinging away from Amberson and making for the door,

[031-005]wildly gesturing.

[031-006]“For heaven’s sake, don’t be so theatrical!” said his uncle, and then,

[031-007]seeing that George was leaving the room: “Come back here. You mustn’t

[031-008]speak to your mother of this!”

[031-009]“Don’t ’tend to,” George said indistinctly; and he plunged out into

[031-010]the big dimly lit hall. He passed his grandfather’s room on the way to

[031-011]the stairs; and the Major was visible within, his white head brightly

[031-012]illumined by a lamp, as he bent low over a ledger upon his roll-top

[031-013]desk. He did not look up, and his grandson strode by the door, not

[031-014]really conscious of the old figure stooping at its tremulous work with

[031-015]long additions and subtractions that refused to balance as they used

[031-016]to. George went home and got a hat and overcoat without seeing either

[031-017]his mother or [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]]. Then he left word that he would be out for dinner,

[031-018]and hurried away from the house.

[031-019]He walked the dark streets of [[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]] for an hour, then went

[031-020]downtown and got coffee at a restaurant. After that he walked through

[031-021]the lighted parts of the town until ten o’clock, when he turned north

[031-022]and came back to the purlieus of the Addition. He strode through the

[031-023]length and breadth of it again, his hat pulled down over his forehead,

[031-024]his overcoat collar turned up behind. He walked fiercely, though his

[031-025]feet ached, but by and by he turned homeward, and, when he reached the

[031-026]Major’s, went in and sat upon the steps of the huge stone veranda in

[031-027]front—an obscure figure in that lonely and repellent place. All lights

[031-028]were out at the Major’s, and finally, after twelve, he saw his mother’s

[031-029]window darken at home.

[031-030]He waited half an hour longer, then crossed the front yards of the

[031-031]new houses and let himself noiselessly in the front door. The light

[031-032]in the hall had been left burning, and another in his own room, as he

[031-033]discovered when he got there. He locked the door quickly and without

[031-034]noise, but his fingers were still upon the key when there was a quick

[031-035]footfall in the hall outside.

[031-036]“Georgie, dear?”

[031-037]He went to the other end of the room before replying.

[031-038]“Yes?”

[031-039]“I’d been wondering where you were, dear.”

[031-040]“Had you?”

[031-041]There was a pause; then she said timidly: “Wherever it was, I hope you

[031-042]had a pleasant evening.”

[031-043]After a silence, “Thank you,” he said, without expression.

[031-044]Another silence followed before she spoke again.

[031-045]“You wouldn’t care to be kissed good-night, I suppose?” And with a

[031-046]little flurry of placative laughter, she added: “At your age, of

[031-047]course!”

[031-048]“I’m going to bed, now,” he said. “Goodnight.”

[031-049]Another silence seemed blanker than those which had preceded it, and

[031-050]finally her voice came—it was blank, too.

[031-051]“Good-night.”

[031-052]After he was in bed his thoughts became more tumultuous than ever;

[031-053]while among all the inchoate and fragmentary sketches of this dreadful

[031-054]day, now rising before him, the clearest was of his uncle collapsed

[031-055]in a big chair with a white tie dangling from his hand; and one

[031-056]conviction, following upon that picture, became definite in George’s

[031-057]mind: that his [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] Amberson was a hopeless dreamer from

[031-058]whom no help need be expected, an amiable imbecile lacking in normal

[031-059]impulses, and wholly useless in a struggle which required honour to be

[031-060]defended by a man of action.

[031-061]Then would return a vision of Mrs. Johnson’s furious round head, set

[031-062]behind her great bosom like the sun far sunk on the horizon of a

[031-063]mountain plateau—and her crackling, asthmatic voice... “Without sharing

[031-064]in other people’s disposition to put an evil interpretation on what may

[031-065]be nothing more than unfortunate appearances.”... “Other people may be

[031-066]less considerate in not confining their discussion of it, as I have, to

[031-067]charitable views.”... “you’ll know something pretty quick! You’ll know

[031-068]you’re out in the street.”... And then George would get up again—and

[031-069]again—and pace the floor in his bare feet.

[031-070]That was what the tormented young man was doing when daylight came

[031-071]gauntly in at his window—pacing the floor, rubbing his head in his

[031-072]hands, and muttering:

[031-073]“It can’t be true: this can’t be happening to me!”

Chapter XXIV

[031-074]Breakfast was brought to him in his room, as usual; but he did not

[031-075]make his normal healthy raid upon the dainty tray: the food remained

[031-076]untouched, and he sustained himself upon coffee—four cups of it, which

[031-077]left nothing of value inside the glistening little percolator. During

[031-078]this process he heard his mother being summoned to the telephone in

[031-079]the hall, not far from his door, and then her voice responding: “Yes?

[031-080]Oh, it’s you! Indeed I should!... Of course... Then I’ll expect you

[031-081]about three... Yes. Good-bye till then.” A few minutes later he heard

[031-082]her speaking to someone beneath his window and, looking out, saw her

[031-083]directing the removal of plants from a small garden bed to the Major’s

[031-084]conservatory for the winter. There was an air of briskness about

[031-085]her; as she turned away to go into the house, she laughed gaily with

[031-086]the Major’s gardener over something he said, and this unconcerned

[031-087]cheerfulness of her was terrible to her son.

[031-088]He went to his desk, and, searching the jumbled contents of a drawer,

[031-089]brought forth a large, unframed photograph of his father, upon which he

[031-090]gazed long and piteously, till at last hot tears stood in his eyes. It

[031-091]was strange how the inconsequent face of Wilbur seemed to increase in

[031-092]high significance during this belated interview between father and son;

[031-093]and how it seemed to take on a reproachful nobility—and yet, under the

[031-094]circumstances, nothing could have been more natural than that George,

[031-095]having paid but the slightest attention to his father in life, should

[031-096]begin to deify him, now that he was dead. “Poor, poor father!” the son

[031-097]whispered brokenly. “Poor man, I’m glad you didn’t know!”

[031-098]He wrapped the picture in a sheet of newspaper, put it under his arm,

[031-099]and, leaving the house hurriedly and stealthily, went downtown to the

[031-100]shop of a silversmith, where he spent sixty dollars on a resplendently

[031-101]festooned silver frame for the picture. Having lunched upon more

[031-102]coffee, he returned to the house at two o’clock, carrying the framed

[031-103]photograph with him, and placed it upon the centre-table in the

[031-104]library, the room most used by Isabel and Fanny and himself. Then he

[031-105]went to a front window of the long “reception room,” and sat looking

[031-106]out through the lace curtains.

[031-107]The house was quiet, though once or twice he heard his mother and Fanny

[031-108]moving about upstairs, and a ripple of song in the voice of Isabel—a

[031-109]fragment from the romantic ballad of Lord Bateman.

[031-110]“Lord Bateman was a noble lord,

[031-111]A noble lord of high degree;

[031-112]And he sailed West and he sailed East,

[031-113]Far countries for to see....”

[031-114]The words became indistinct; the air was hummed absently; the humming

[031-115]shifted to a whistle, then drifted out of hearing, and the place was

[031-116]still again.

[031-117]George looked often at his watch, but his vigil did not last an hour.

[031-118]At ten minutes of three, peering through the curtain, he saw an

[031-119]automobile stop in front of the house and [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]] jump lightly

[031-120]down from it. The car was of a new pattern, low and long, with an ample

[031-121]seat in the tonneau, facing forward; and a professional driver sat at

[031-122]the wheel, a strange figure in leather, goggled out of all personality

[031-123]and seemingly part of the mechanism.

[031-124]Eugene himself, as he came up the cement path to the house, was a

[031-125]figure of the new era which was in time to be so disastrous to stiff

[031-126]hats and skirted coats; and his appearance afforded a debonair contrast

[031-127]to that of the queer-looking duck capering at [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Ball in an

[031-128]old dress coat, and chugging up [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] through the snow in

[031-129]his nightmare of a sewing-machine. Eugene, this afternoon, was richly

[031-130]in the new outdoor mode: motoring coat was soft gray fur; his cap and

[031-131]gloves were of gray suede; and though [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]’s hand may have shown itself

[031-132]in the selection of these garnitures, he wore them easily, even with

[031-133]becoming hint of jauntiness. Some change might be seen in his face,

[031-134]too, for a successful man is seldom to be mistaken, especially if his

[031-135]temper be genial. Eugene had begun to look like a millionaire.

[031-136]But above everything else, what was most evident about him, as he came

[031-137]up the path, was confidence in the happiness promised by his errand;

[031-138]the anticipation in his eyes could have been read by a stranger. His

[031-139]look at the door of Isabel’s house was the look of a man who is quite

[031-140]certain that the next moment will reveal something ineffably charming,

[031-141]inexpressibly dear.

[031-142]When the bell rang, George waited at the entrance of the “reception

[031-143]room” until a housemaid came through the hall on her way to answer the

[031-144]summons.

[031-145]“You needn’t mind, Mary,” he told her. “I’ll see who it is and what

[031-146]they want. Probably it’s only a pedlar.”

[031-147]“Thank you, sir, Mister George,” said Mary; and returned to the rear of

[031-148]the house.

[031-149]George went slowly to the front door, and halted, regarding the misty

[031-150]silhouette of the caller upon the ornamental frosted glass. After a

[031-151]minute of waiting, this silhouette changed outline so that an arm

[031-152]could be distinguished—an arm outstretched toward the bell, as if the

[031-153]gentleman outside doubted whether or not it had sounded, and were

[031-154]minded to try again. But before the gesture was completed George

[031-155]abruptly threw open the door, and stepped squarely upon the middle of

[031-156]the threshold.

[031-157]A slight change shadowed the face of Eugene; his look of happy

[031-158]anticipation gave way to something formal and polite. “How do you

[031-159]do, George,” he said. “Mrs. Minafer expects to go driving with me, I

[031-160]believe—if you’ll be so kind as to send her word that I’m here.”

[031-161]George made not the slightest movement.

[031-162]“No,” he said.

[031-163]Eugene was incredulous, even when his second glance revealed how hot of

[031-164]eye was the haggard young man before him. “I beg your pardon. I said—”

[031-165]“I heard you,” said George. “You said you had an engagement with my

[031-166]mother, and I told you, No!”

[031-167]Eugene gave him a steady look, and then he asked quietly: “What is

[031-168]the—the difficulty?”

[031-169]George kept his own voice quiet enough, but that did not mitigate the

[031-170]vibrant fury of it. “My mother will have no interest in knowing that

[031-171]you came for her to-day,” he said. “Or any other day!”

[031-172]Eugene continued to look at him with a scrutiny in which began to gleam

[031-173]a profound anger, none the less powerful because it was so quiet. “I am

[031-174]afraid I do not understand you.”

[031-175]“I doubt if I could make it much plainer,” George said, raising his

[031-176]voice slightly, “but I’ll try. You’re not wanted in this house, Mr.

[031-177]Morgan, now or at any other time. Perhaps you’ll understand—this!”

[031-178]And with the last word he closed the door in Eugene’s face.

[031-179]Then, not moving away, he stood just inside door, and noted that the

[031-180]misty silhouette remained upon the frosted glass for several moments,

[031-181]as if the forbidden gentleman debated in his mind what course to

[031-182]pursue. “Let him ring again!” George thought grimly. “Or try the side

[031-183]door—or the kitchen!”

[031-184]But Eugene made no further attempt; the silhouette disappeared;

[031-185]footsteps could be heard withdrawing across the floor of the veranda;

[031-186]and George, returning to the window in the “reception room,” was

[031-187]rewarded by the sight of an automobile manufacturer in baffled retreat,

[031-188]with all his wooing furs and fineries mocking him. Eugene got into his

[031-189]car slowly, not looking back at the house which had just taught him

[031-190]such a lesson; and it was easily visible—even from a window seventy

[031-191]feet distant—that he was not the same light suitor who had jumped

[031-192]so gallantly from the car only a few minutes earlier. Observing the

[031-193]heaviness of his movements as he climbed into the tonneau, George

[031-194]indulged in a sickish throat rumble which bore a distant cousinship to

[031-195]mirth.
