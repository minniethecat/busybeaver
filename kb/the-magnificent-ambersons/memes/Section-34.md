[034-001]Thus far she got stumblingly with what she wanted to say, while

[034-002]George watched her with a gaze that grew harder and hotter; but here

[034-003]he cut her off. “I have already given up all idea of [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]],” he said.

[034-004]“Naturally, I couldn’t have treated her father as I deliberately did

[034-005]treat him—I could hardly have done that and expected his daughter ever

[034-006]to speak to me again.”

[034-007]Isabel gave a quick cry of compassion, but he allowed her no

[034-008]opportunity to speak. “You needn’t think I’m making any particular

[034-009]sacrifice,” he said sharply, “though I would, quickly enough, if I

[034-010]thought it necessary in a matter of honour like this. I was interested

[034-011]in her, and I could even say I did care for her; but she proved pretty

[034-012]satisfactorily that she cared little enough about me! She went away

[034-013]right in the midst of a—of a difference of opinion we were having; she

[034-014]didn’t even let me know she was going, and never wrote a line to me,

[034-015]and then came back telling everybody she’d had ‘a perfectly gorgeous

[034-016]time!’ That’s quite enough for me. I’m not precisely the sort to

[034-017]arrange for that kind of thing to be done to me more than once! The

[034-018]truth is, we’re not congenial and we’d found that much out, at least,

[034-019]before she left. We should never have been happy; she was ‘superior’

[034-020]all the time, and critical of me—not very pleasant, that! I was

[034-021]disappointed in her, and I might as well say it. I don’t think she has

[034-022]the very deepest nature in the world, and—”

[034-023]But Isabel put her hand timidly on his arm. “Georgie, dear, this is

[034-024]only a quarrel: all young people have them before they get adjusted,

[034-025]and you mustn’t let—”

[034-026]“If you please!” he said emphatically, moving back from her. “This

[034-027]isn’t that kind. It’s all over, and I don’t care to speak of it again.

[034-028]It’s settled. Don’t you understand?”

[034-029]“But, dear—”

[034-030]“No. I want to talk to you about this letter of her father’s.”

[034-031]“Yes, dear, that’s why—”

[034-032]“It’s simply the most offensive piece of writing that I’ve ever held in

[034-033]my hands!”

[034-034]She stepped back from him, startled. “But, dear, I thought—”

[034-035]“I can’t understand your even showing me such a thing!” he cried. “How

[034-036]did you happen to bring it to me?”

[034-037]“Your uncle thought I’d better. He thought it was the simplest thing

[034-038]to do, and he said that he’d suggested it to [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]], and Eugene had

[034-039]agreed. They thought—”

[034-040]“Yes!” George said bitterly. “I should like to hear what they thought!”

[034-041]“They thought it would be the most straightforward thing.”

[034-042]George drew a long breath. “Well, what do you think, mother?”

[034-043]“I thought it would be the simplest and most straightforward thing; I

[034-044]thought they were right.”

[034-045]“Very well! We’ll agree it was simple and straightforward. Now, what do

[034-046]you think of that letter itself?”

[034-047]She hesitated, looking away. “I—of course I don’t agree with him in

[034-048]the way he speaks of you, dear—except about the angel! I don’t agree

[034-049]with some of the things he implies. You’ve always been unselfish—nobody

[034-050]knows that better than your mother. When [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] was left with nothing,

[034-051]you were so quick and generous to give up what really should have come

[034-052]to you, and—”

[034-053]“And yet,” George broke in, “you see what he implies about me. Don’t

[034-054]you think, really, that this was a pretty insulting letter for that man

[034-055]to be asking you to hand your son?”

[034-056]“Oh, no!” she cried. “You can see how fair he means to be, and he

[034-057]didn’t ask for me to give it to you. It was brother George who—”

[034-058]“Never mind that, now! You say he tries to be fair, and yet do you

[034-059]suppose it ever occurs to him that I’m doing my simple duty? That I’m

[034-060]doing what my father would do if he were alive? That I’m doing what

[034-061]my father would ask me to do if he could speak from his grave out

[034-062]yonder? Do you suppose it ever occurs to that man for one minute that

[034-063]I’m protecting my mother?” George raised his voice, advancing upon the

[034-064]helpless lady fiercely; and she could only bend her head before him.

[034-065]“He talks about my ‘Will’—how it must be beaten down; yes, and he asks

[034-066]my mother to do that little thing to please him! What for? Why does he

[034-067]want me ‘beaten’ by my mother? Because I’m trying to protect her name!

[034-068]He’s got my mother’s name bandied up and down the streets of this town

[034-069]till I can’t step in those streets without wondering what every soul I

[034-070]meet is thinking of me and of my family, and now he wants you to marry

[034-071]him so that every gossip in town will say ‘There! What did I tell you?

[034-072]I guess that proves it’s true!’ You can’t get away from it; that’s

[034-073]exactly what they’d say, and this man pretends he cares for you, and

[034-074]yet asks you to marry him and give them the right to say it. He says

[034-075]he and you don’t care what they say, but I know better! He may not

[034-076]care—probably he’s that kind—but you do. There never was an Amberson

[034-077]yet that would let [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] name go trailing in the dust like

[034-078]that! It’s the proudest name in this town and it’s going to stay the

[034-079]proudest; and I tell you that’s the deepest thing in my nature—not that

[034-080]I’d expect Eugene Morgan to understand—the very deepest thing in my

[034-081]nature is to protect that name, and to fight for it to the last breath

[034-082]when danger threatens it, as it does now—through my mother!” He turned

[034-083]from her, striding up and down and tossing his arms about, in a tumult

[034-084]of gesture. “I can’t believe it of you, that you’d think of such a

[034-085]sacrilege! That’s what it would be—sacrilege! When he talks about your

[034-086]unselfishness toward me, he’s right—you have been unselfish and you

[034-087]have been a perfect mother. But what about him? Is it unselfish of him

[034-088]to want you to throw away your good name just to please him? That’s all

[034-089]he asks of you—and to quit being my mother! Do you think I can believe

[034-090]you really care for him? I don’t! You are my mother and you’re an

[034-091]Amberson—and I believe you’re too proud! You’re too proud to care for a

[034-092]man who could write such a letter as that!” He stopped, faced her, and

[034-093]spoke with more self-control: “Well, what are you going to do about it,

[034-094]mother?”

[034-095]George was right about his mother’s being proud. And even when she

[034-096]laughed with a negro gardener, or even those few times in her life

[034-097]when people saw her weep, Isabel had a proud look—something that was

[034-098]independent and graceful and strong. But she did not have it now: she

[034-099]leaned against the wall, beside his dressing-table, and seemed beset

[034-100]with humility and with weakness. Her head drooped.

[034-101]“What answer are you going to make to such a letter?” George demanded,

[034-102]like a judge on the bench.

[034-103]“I—I don’t quite know, dear,” she murmured.

[034-104]“You don’t?” he cried. “You—”

[034-105]“Wait,” she begged him. “I’m so—confused.”

[034-106]“I want to know what you’re going to write him. Do you think if you did

[034-107]what he wants you to I could bear to stay another day in this town,

[034-108]mother? Do you think I could ever bear even to see you again if you

[034-109]married him? I’d want to, but you surely know I just—couldn’t!”

[034-110]She made a futile gesture, and seemed to breathe with difficulty. “I—I

[034-111]wasn’t—quite sure,” she faltered, “about—about it’s being wise for us

[034-112]to be married—even before knowing how you feel about it. I wasn’t even

[034-113]sure it was quite fair to—to Eugene. I have—I seem to have that family

[034-114]trouble—like father’s—that I spoke to you about once.” She managed

[034-115]a deprecatory little dry laugh. “Not that it amounts to much, but I

[034-116]wasn’t at all sure that it would be fair to him. Marrying doesn’t mean

[034-117]so much, after all—not at my age. It’s enough to know that—that people

[034-118]think of you—and to see them. I thought we were all—oh, pretty happy

[034-119]the way things were, and I don’t think it would mean giving up a great

[034-120]deal for him or me, either, if we just went on as we have been. I—I see

[034-121]him almost every day, and—”

[034-122]“Mother!” George’s voice was loud and stern. “Do you think you could go

[034-123]on seeing him after this!”

[034-124]She had been talking helplessly enough before; her tone was little more

[034-125]broken now. “Not—not even—see him?”

[034-126]“How could you?” George cried. “Mother, it seems to me that if he ever

[034-127]set foot in this house again—oh! I can’t speak of it! Could you see

[034-128]him, knowing what talk it makes every time he turns into this street,

[034-129]and knowing what that means to me? Oh, I don’t understand all this—I

[034-130]don’t! If you’d told me, a year ago, that such things were going to

[034-131]happen, I’d have thought you were insane—and now I believe I am!”

[034-132]Then, after a preliminary gesture of despair, as though he meant harm

[034-133]to the ceiling, he flung himself heavily, face downward, upon the bed.

[034-134]His anguish was none the less real for its vehemence; and the stricken

[034-135]lady came to him instantly and bent over him, once more enfolding him

[034-136]in her arms. She said nothing, but suddenly her tears fell upon his

[034-137]head; she saw them, and seemed to be startled.

[034-138]“Oh, this won’t do!” she said. “I’ve never let you see me cry before,

[034-139]except when your father died. I mustn’t!”

[034-140]And she ran from the room.

[034-141]...A little while after she had gone, George rose and began solemnly

[034-142]to dress for dinner. At one stage of these conscientious proceedings

[034-143]he put on, temporarily, his long black velvet dressing-gown, and,

[034-144]happening to catch sight in his pier glass of the picturesque and

[034-145]medieval figure thus presented, he paused to regard it; and something

[034-146]profoundly theatrical in his nature came to the surface.

[034-147]His lips moved; he whispered, half-aloud, some famous fragments:

[034-148]“’Tis not alone my inky cloak, good mother,

[034-149]Nor customary suits of solemn black...”

[034-150]For, in truth, the mirrored princely image, with hair dishevelled on

[034-151]the white brow, and the long tragic fall of black velvet from the

[034-152]shoulders, had brought about (in his thought at least) some comparisons

[034-153]of his own times, so out of joint, with those of that other gentle

[034-154]prince and heir whose widowed mother was minded to marry again.

[034-155]“But I have that within which passeth show;

[034-156]These but the trappings and the suits of Woe.”

[034-157]Not less like Hamlet did he feel and look as he sat gauntly at the

[034-158]dinner table with Fanny to partake of a meal throughout which neither

[034-159]spoke. Isabel had sent word “not to wait” for her, an injunction it was

[034-160]as well they obeyed, for she did not come at all. But with the renewal

[034-161]of sustenance furnished to his system, some relaxation must have

[034-162]occurred within the high-strung George. Dinner was not quite finished

[034-163]when, without warning, sleep hit him hard. His burning eyes could no

[034-164]longer restrain the lids above them; his head sagged beyond control;

[034-165]and he got to his feet, and went lurching upstairs, yawning with

[034-166]exhaustion. From the door of his room, which he closed mechanically,

[034-167]with his eyes shut, he went blindly to his bed, fell upon it soddenly,

[034-168]and slept—with his face full upturned to the light.

[034-169]It was after midnight when he woke, and the room was dark. He had not

[034-170]dreamed, but he woke with the sense that somebody or something had been

[034-171]with him while he slept—somebody or something infinitely compassionate;

[034-172]somebody or something infinitely protective, that would let him come to

[034-173]no harm and to no grief.

[034-174]He got up, and pressed the light on. Pinned to the cover of his

[034-175]dressing-table was a square envelope, with the words, “For you, dear,”

[034-176]written in pencil upon it. But the message inside was in ink, a little

[034-177]smudged here and there.
