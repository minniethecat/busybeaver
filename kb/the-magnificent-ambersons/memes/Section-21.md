[021-001]“Well, Mister Bones, of these three things which are the good ones and

[021-002]which are the bad ones?”

[021-003]“All of them,” said [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]].

[021-004]It happened that just then they came in sight of the subject of their

[021-005]discourse. George was walking under the elms with [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], swinging a

[021-006]stick and pointing out to her various objects and localities which had

[021-007]attained historical value during the last four years. The two older men

[021-008]marked his gestures, careless and graceful; they observed his attitude,

[021-009]unconsciously noble, his easy proprietorship of the ground beneath his

[021-010]feet and round about, of the branches overhead, of the old buildings

[021-011]beyond, and of Lucy.

[021-012]“I don’t know,” Eugene said, smiling whimsically. “I don’t know. When I

[021-013]spoke of his being a human being—I don’t know. Perhaps it’s more like

[021-014]deity.”

[021-015]“I wonder if I was like that!” Amberson groaned. “You don’t suppose

[021-016]every Amberson has had to go through it, do you?”

[021-017]“Don’t worry! At least half of it is a combination of youth, good

[021-018]looks, and college; and even the noblest Ambersons get over their

[021-019]nobility and come to be people in time. It takes more than time,

[021-020]though.”

[021-021]“I should say it did take more than time!” his friend agreed, shaking a

[021-022]rueful head.

[021-023]Then they walked over to join the loveliest Amberson, whom neither time

[021-024]nor trouble seemed to have touched. She stood alone, thoughtful under

[021-025]the great trees, chaperoning George and Lucy at a distance; but, seeing

[021-026]the two friends approaching, she came to meet them.

[021-027]“It’s charming, isn’t it!” she said, moving her black-gloved hand to

[021-028]indicate the summery dressed crowd strolling about them, or clustering

[021-029]in groups, each with its own hero. “They seem so eager and so

[021-030]confident, all these boys—it’s touching. But of course youth doesn’t

[021-031]know it’s touching.”

[021-032]Amberson coughed. “No, it doesn’t seem to take itself as pathetic,

[021-033]precisely! Eugene and I were just speaking of something like that. Do

[021-034]you know what I think whenever I see these smooth, triumphal young

[021-035]faces? I always think: ‘Oh, how you’re going to catch it’!”

[021-036]“George!”

[021-037]“Oh, yes,” he said. “Life’s most ingenious: it’s got a special

[021-038]walloping for every mother’s son of ’em!”

[021-039]“Maybe,” said Isabel, troubled—“maybe some of the mothers can take the

[021-040]walloping for them.”

[021-041]“Not one!” her brother assured her, with emphasis. “Not any more than

[021-042]she can take on her own face the lines that are bound to come on her

[021-043]son’s. I suppose you know that all these young faces have got to get

[021-044]lines on ’em?”

[021-045]“Maybe they won’t,” she said, smiling wistfully. “Maybe times will

[021-046]change, and nobody will have to wear lines.”

[021-047]“Times have changed like that for only one person that I know,” Eugene

[021-048]said. And as Isabel looked inquiring, he laughed, and she saw that she

[021-049]was the “only one person.” His implication was justified, moreover, and

[021-050]she knew it. She blushed charmingly.

[021-051]“Which is it puts the lines on the faces?” Amberson asked. “Is it age

[021-052]or trouble? Of course we can’t decide that wisdom does it—we must be

[021-053]polite to Isabel.”

[021-054]“I’ll tell you what puts the lines there,” Eugene said. “Age puts some,

[021-055]and trouble puts some, and work puts some, but the deepest are carved

[021-056]by lack of faith. The serenest brow is the one that believes the most.”

[021-057]“In what?” Isabel asked gently.

[021-058]“In everything!”

[021-059]She looked at him inquiringly, and he laughed as he had a moment

[021-060]before, when she looked at him that way. “Oh, yes, you do!” he said.

[021-061]She continued to look at him inquiringly a moment or two longer, and

[021-062]there was an unconscious earnestness in her glance, something trustful

[021-063]as well as inquiring, as if she knew that whatever he meant it was all

[021-064]right. Then her eyes drooped thoughtfully, and she seemed to address

[021-065]some inquiries to herself. She looked up suddenly. “Why, I believe,”

[021-066]she said, in a tone of surprise, “I believe I do!”

[021-067]And at that both men laughed. “Isabel!” her brother exclaimed. “You’re

[021-068]a foolish person! There are times when you look exactly fourteen years

[021-069]old!”

[021-070]But this reminded her of her real affair in that part of the world.

[021-071]“Good gracious!” she said. “Where have the children got to? We must

[021-072]take Lucy pretty soon, so that George can go and sit with the Class. We

[021-073]must catch up with them.”

[021-074]She took her brother’s arm, and the three moved on, looking about them

[021-075]in the crowd.

[021-076]“Curious,” Amberson remarked, as they did not immediately discover the

[021-077]young people they sought. “Even in such a concourse one would think we

[021-078]couldn’t fail to see the proprietor.”

[021-079]“Several hundred proprietors today,” Eugene suggested.

[021-080]“No; they’re only proprietors of the university,” said George’s uncle.

[021-081]“We’re looking for the proprietor of the universe.”

[021-082]“There he is!” cried Isabel fondly, not minding this satire at all.

[021-083]“And doesn’t he look it!”

[021-084]Her escorts were still laughing at her when they joined the proprietor

[021-085]of the universe and his pretty friend, and though both Amberson and

[021-086]Eugene declined to explain the cause of their mirth, even upon Lucy’s

[021-087]urgent request, the portents of the day were amiable, and the five made

[021-088]a happy party—that is to say, four of them made a happy audience for

[021-089]the fifth, and the mood of this fifth was gracious and cheerful.

[021-090]George took no conspicuous part in either the academic or the social

[021-091]celebrations of his class; he seemed to regard both sets of exercises

[021-092]with a tolerant amusement, his own “crowd” “not going in much for

[021-093]either of those sorts of things,” as he explained to Lucy. What his

[021-094]crowd had gone in for remained ambiguous; some negligent testimony

[021-095]indicating that, except for an astonishing reliability which they all

[021-096]seemed to have attained in matters relating to musical comedy, they had

[021-097]not gone in for anything. Certainly the question one of them put to

[021-098]Lucy, in response to investigations of hers, seemed to point that way:

[021-099]“Don’t you think,” he said, “really, don’t you think that being things

[021-100]is rather better than doing things?”

[021-101]He said “rahthuh bettuh” for “rather better,” and seemed to do it

[021-102]deliberately, with perfect knowledge of what he was doing. Later, Lucy

[021-103]mocked him to George, and George refused to smile: he somewhat inclined

[021-104]to such pronunciations, himself. This inclination was one of the things

[021-105]that he had acquired in the four years.

[021-106]What else he had acquired, it might have puzzled him to state, had

[021-107]anybody asked him and required a direct reply within a reasonable

[021-108]space of time. He had learned how to pass examinations by “cramming”;

[021-109]that is, in three or four days and nights he could get into his head

[021-110]enough of a selected fragment of some scientific or philosophical or

[021-111]literary or linguistic subject to reply plausibly to six questions out

[021-112]of ten. He could retain the information necessary for such a feat just

[021-113]long enough to give a successful performance; then it would evaporate

[021-114]utterly from his brain, and leave him undisturbed. George, like his

[021-115]“crowd,” not only preferred “being things” to “doing things,” but had

[021-116]contented himself with four years of “being things” as a preparation

[021-117]for going on “being things.” And when Lucy rather shyly pressed him for

[021-118]his friend’s probable definition of the “things” it seemed so superior

[021-119]and beautiful to be, George raised his eyebrows slightly, meaning that

[021-120]she should have understood without explanation; but he did explain:

[021-121]“Oh, family and all that—being a gentleman, I suppose.”

[021-122]Lucy gave the horizon a long look, but offered no comment.

Chapter XVI

[021-123]“[[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]] doesn’t look much better,” George said to his mother, a few

[021-124]minutes after their arrival, on the night they got home. He stood with

[021-125]a towel in her doorway, concluding some sketchy ablutions before going

[021-126]downstairs to a supper which Fanny was hastily preparing for them.

[021-127]Isabel had not telegraphed; Fanny was taken by surprise when they drove

[021-128]up in a station cab at eleven o’clock; and George instantly demanded “a

[021-129]little decent food.” (Some criticisms of his had publicly disturbed the

[021-130]composure of the dining-car steward four hours previously.) “I never

[021-131]saw anybody take things so hard as she seems to,” he observed, his

[021-132]voice muffled by the towel. “Doesn’t she get over it at all? I thought

[021-133]she’d feel better when we turned over the insurance to her—gave it to

[021-134]her absolutely, without any strings to it. She looks about a thousand

[021-135]years old!”

[021-136]“She looks quite girlish, sometimes, though,” his mother said.

[021-137]“Has she looked that way much since father—”

[021-138]“Not so much,” Isabel said thoughtfully. “But she will, as times goes

[021-139]on.”

[021-140]“Time’ll have to hurry, then, it seems to me,” George observed,

[021-141]returning to his own room.

[021-142]When they went down to the dining room, he pronounced acceptable the

[021-143]salmon salad, cold beef, cheese, and cake which Fanny made ready

[021-144]for them without disturbing the servants. The journey had fatigued

[021-145]Isabel, she ate nothing, but sat to observe with tired pleasure

[021-146]the manifestations of her son’s appetite, meanwhile giving her

[021-147]sister-in-law a brief summary of the events of commencement. But

[021-148]presently she kissed them both good-night—taking care to kiss George

[021-149]lightly upon the side of his head, so as not to disturb his eating—and

[021-150]left aunt and nephew alone together.

[021-151]“It never was becoming to her to look pale,” Fanny said absently, a few

[021-152]moments after Isabel’s departure.

[021-153]“Wha’d you say, Aunt Fanny?”

[021-154]“Nothing. I suppose your mother’s been being pretty gay? Going a lot?”

[021-155]“How could she?” George asked cheerfully. “In mourning, of course all

[021-156]she could do was just sit around and look on. That’s all Lucy could do

[021-157]either, for the matter of that.”

[021-158]“I suppose so,” his aunt assented. “How did Lucy get home?”

[021-159]George regarded her with astonishment. “Why, on the train with the rest

[021-160]of us, of course.”

[021-161]“I didn’t mean that,” Fanny explained. “I meant from the station. Did

[021-162]you drive out to their house with her before you came here?”

[021-163]“No. She drove home with her father, of course.”

[021-164]“Oh, I see. So Eugene came to the station to meet you.”

[021-165]“To meet us?” George echoed, renewing his attack upon the salmon salad.

[021-166]“How could he?”

[021-167]“I don’t know what you mean,” Fanny said drearily, in the desolate

[021-168]voice that had become her habit. “I haven’t seen him while your

[021-169]mother’s been away.”

[021-170]“Naturally,” said George. “He’s been East himself.”

[021-171]At this Fanny’s drooping eyelids opened wide.

[021-172]“Did you see him?”

[021-173]“Well, naturally, since he made the trip home with us!”

[021-174]“He did?” she said sharply. “He’s been with you all the time?”

[021-175]“No; only on the train and the last three days before we left. Uncle

[021-176]George got him to come.”

[021-177]Fanny’s eyelids drooped again, and she sat silent until George pushed

[021-178]back his chair and lit a cigarette, declaring his satisfaction

[021-179]with what she had provided. “You’re a fine housekeeper,” he said

[021-180]benevolently. “You know how to make things look dainty as well as taste

[021-181]the right way. I don’t believe you’d stay single very long if some of

[021-182]the bachelors and widowers around town could just once see—”

[021-183]She did not hear him. “It’s a little odd,” she said.

[021-184]“What’s odd?”

[021-185]“Your mother’s not mentioning that Mr. Morgan had been with you.”

[021-186]“Didn’t think of it, I suppose,” said George carelessly; and, his

[021-187]benevolent mood increasing, he conceived the idea that a little

[021-188]harmless rallying might serve to elevate his aunt’s drooping spirits.

[021-189]“I’ll tell you something, in confidence,” he said solemnly.

[021-190]She looked up, startled. “What?”

[021-191]“Well, it struck me that Mr. Morgan was looking pretty absent-minded,

[021-192]most of the time; and he certainly is dressing better than he used to.

[021-193][[george-amberson|Uncle George]] told me he heard that the automobile factory had been

[021-194]doing quite well—won a race, too! I shouldn’t be a bit surprised if all

[021-195]the young fellow had been waiting for was to know he had an assured

[021-196]income before he proposed.”
