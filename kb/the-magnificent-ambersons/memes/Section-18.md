[018-001]“He seemed rather confused and pained about it,” Isabel said. “I think

[018-002]they oughtn’t to urge it. George says that the estate won’t stand

[018-003]taking out the third that [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] wants, and that Sydney and Amelia are

[018-004]behaving like a couple of pigs.” She laughed, continuing, “Of course I

[018-005]don’t know whether they are or not: I never have understood any more

[018-006]about business myself than a little pig would! But I’m on George’s

[018-007]side, whether he’s right or wrong; I always was from the time we were

[018-008]children: and Sydney and Amelia are hurt with me about it, I’m afraid.

[018-009]They’ve stopped speaking to George entirely. Poor father. Family rows

[018-010]at his time of life.”

[018-011]George became thoughtful. If Sydney and Amelia were behaving like pigs,

[018-012]things might not be so simple as at first they seemed to be. Uncle

[018-013]Sydney and [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]] might live an awful long while, he thought; and

[018-014]besides, people didn’t always leave their fortunes to relatives. Sydney

[018-015]might die first, leaving everything to his widow, and some curly-haired

[018-016]Italian adventurer might get round her, over there in Florence; she

[018-017]might be fool enough to marry again—or even adopt somebody!

[018-018]He became more and more thoughtful, forgetting entirely a plan he had

[018-019]formed for the continued teasing of his [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]]; and, an hour after

[018-020]lunch, he strolled over to his grandfather’s, intending to apply for

[018-021]further information, as a party rightfully interested.

[018-022]He did not carry out this intention, however. Going into the big house

[018-023]by a side entrance, he was informed that the Major was upstairs in his

[018-024]bedroom, that his sons Sydney and George were both with him, and that

[018-025]a serious argument was in progress. “You kin stan’ right in de middle

[018-026]dat big, sta’y-way,” said Old Sam, the ancient negro, who was his

[018-027]informant, “an’ you kin heah all you a-mind to wivout goin’ on up no

[018-028]fudda. Mist’ Sydney an’ Mist’ Jawge talkin’ louduh’n I evuh heah nobody

[018-029]ca’y on in nish heah house! Quollin’, honey, big quollin’!”

[018-030]“All right,” said George shortly. “You go on back to your own part of

[018-031]the house, and don’t make any talk. Hear me?”

[018-032]“Yessuh, yessuh,” Sam chuckled, as he shuffled away. “Plenty talkin’

[018-033]wivout Sam! Yessuh!”

[018-034]George went to the foot of the great stairway. He could hear angry

[018-035]voices overhead—those of his two uncles—and a plaintive murmur, as

[018-036]if the Major tried to keep the peace. Such sounds were far from

[018-037]encouraging to callers, and George decided not to go upstairs

[018-038]until this interview was over. His decision was the result of no

[018-039]timidity, nor of a too sensitive delicacy. What he felt was, that

[018-040]if he interrupted the scene in his grandfather’s room, just at this

[018-041]time, one of the three gentlemen engaging in it might speak to him

[018-042]in a peremptory manner (in the heat of the moment) and George saw

[018-043]no reason for exposing his dignity to such mischances. Therefore he

[018-044]turned from the stairway, and going quietly into the library, picked

[018-045]up a magazine—but he did not open it, for his attention was instantly

[018-046]arrested by his Aunt Amelia’s voice, speaking in the next room. The

[018-047]door was open and George heard her distinctly.

[018-048]“Isabel does? Isabel!” she exclaimed, her tone high and shrewish. “You

[018-049]needn’t tell me anything about [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Minafer]], I guess, my dear old

[018-050]Frank Bronson! I know her a little better than you do, don’t you think?”

[018-051]George heard the voice of Mr. Bronson replying—a voice familiar to him

[018-052]as that of his grandfather’s attorney-in-chief and chief intimate as

[018-053]well. He was a contemporary of the Major’s, being over seventy, and

[018-054]they had been through three years of the War in the same regiment.

[018-055]Amelia addressed him now, with an effect of angry mockery, as “my dear

[018-056]old Frank Bronson”; but that (without the mockery) was how [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]]

[018-057]family almost always spoke of him: “dear old Frank Bronson.” He was a

[018-058]hale, thin old man, six feet three inches tall, and without a stoop.

[018-059]“I doubt your knowing Isabel,” he said stiffly. “You speak of her as

[018-060]you do because she sides with her brother George, instead of with you

[018-061]and Sydney.”

[018-062]“Pooh!” Aunt Amelia was evidently in a passion. “You know what’s been

[018-063]going on over there, well enough, Frank Bronson!”

[018-064]“I don’t even know what you’re talking about.”

[018-065]“Oh, you don’t? You don’t know that Isabel takes George’s side simply

[018-066]because he’s [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]]’s best friend?”

[018-067]“It seems to me you’re talking pure nonsense,” said Bronson sharply.

[018-068]“Not impure nonsense, I hope!”

[018-069]Amelia became shrill. “I thought you were a man of the world: don’t

[018-070]tell me you’re blind! For nearly two years Isabel’s been pretending

[018-071]to chaperone Fanny Minafer with Eugene, and all the time she’s been

[018-072]dragging that poor fool Fanny around to chaperone her and Eugene!

[018-073]Under the circumstances, she knows people will get to thinking Fanny’s

[018-074]a pretty slim kind of chaperone, and Isabel wants to please George

[018-075]because she thinks there’ll be less talk if she can keep her own

[018-076]brother around, seeming to approve. ‘Talk!’ She’d better look out! The

[018-077]whole town will be talking, the first thing she knows! She—”

[018-078]Amelia stopped, and stared at the doorway in a panic, for her nephew

[018-079]stood there.

[018-080]She kept her eyes upon his white face for a few strained moments, then,

[018-081]regaining her nerve, looked away and shrugged her shoulders.

[018-082]“You weren’t intended to hear what I’ve been saying, George,” she said

[018-083]quietly. “But since you seem to—”

[018-084]“Yes, I did.”

[018-085]“So!” She shrugged her shoulders again. “After all, I don’t know but

[018-086]it’s just as well, in the long run.”

[018-087]He walked up to where she sat. “You—you—” he said thickly. “It seems—it

[018-088]seems to me you’re—you’re pretty common!”

[018-089]Amelia tried to give the impression of an unconcerned person laughing

[018-090]with complete indifference, but the sounds she produced were disjointed

[018-091]and uneasy. She fanned herself, looking out of the open window near

[018-092]her. “Of course, if you want to make more trouble in the family than

[018-093]we’ve already got, George, with your eavesdropping, you can go and

[018-094]repeat—”

[018-095]Old Bronson had risen from his chair in great distress. “Your aunt was

[018-096]talking nonsense because she’s piqued over a business matter, George,”

[018-097]he said. “She doesn’t mean what she said, and neither she nor any one

[018-098]else gives the slightest credit to such foolishness—no one in the

[018-099]world!”

[018-100]George gulped, and wet lines shone suddenly along his lower eyelids.

[018-101]“They—they’d better not!” he said, then stalked out of the room, and

[018-102]out of the house. He stamped fiercely across the stone slabs of the

[018-103]front porch, descended the steps, and halted abruptly, blinking in the

[018-104]strong sunshine.

[018-105]In front of his own gate, beyond the Major’s broad lawn, his mother

[018-106]was just getting into her victoria, where sat already his Aunt Fanny

[018-107]and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]]. It was a summer fashion-picture: the three ladies

[018-108]charmingly dressed, delicate parasols aloft; the lines of the victoria

[018-109]graceful as those of a violin; the trim pair of bays in glistening

[018-110]harness picked out with silver, and the serious black driver whom

[018-111]Isabel, being an Amberson, dared even in that town to put into a black

[018-112]livery coat, boots, white breeches, and cockaded hat. They jingled

[018-113]smartly away, and, seeing George standing on the Major’s lawn, Lucy

[018-114]waved, and Isabel threw him a kiss.

[018-115]But George shuddered, pretending not to see them, and stooped as if

[018-116]searching for something lost in the grass, protracting that posture

[018-117]until the victoria was out of hearing. And ten minutes later, George

[018-118]Amberson, somewhat in the semblance of an angry person plunging out of

[018-119]the Mansion, found a pale nephew waiting to accost him.

[018-120]“I haven’t time to talk, Georgie.”

[018-121]“Yes, you have. You’d better!”

[018-122]“What’s the matter, then?”

[018-123]His namesake drew him away from the vicinity of the house. “I want to

[018-124]tell you something I just heard Aunt Amelia say, in there.”

[018-125]“I don’t want to hear it,” said Amberson. “I’ve been hearing entirely

[018-126]too much of what ‘Aunt Amelia’ says, lately.”

[018-127]“She says my mother’s on your side about this division of the property

[018-128]because you’re Eugene Morgan’s best friend.”

[018-129]“What in the name of heaven has that got to do with your mother’s being

[018-130]on my side?”

[018-131]“She said—” George paused to swallow. “She said—” He faltered.

[018-132]“You look sick,” said his uncle; and laughed shortly. “If it’s because

[018-133]of anything Amelia’s been saying, I don’t blame you! What else did she

[018-134]say?”

[018-135]George swallowed again, as with nausea, but under his uncle’s

[018-136]encouragement he was able to be explicit. “She said my mother wanted

[018-137]you to be friendly to her about Eugene Morgan. She said my mother had

[018-138]been using Aunt Fanny as a chaperone.”

[018-139]Amberson emitted a laugh of disgust. “It’s wonderful what tommy-rot a

[018-140]woman in a state of spite can think of! I suppose you don’t doubt that

[018-141]Amelia Amberson created this specimen of tommy-rot herself?”

[018-142]“I know she did.”

[018-143]“Then what’s the matter?”

[018-144]“She said—” George faltered again. “She said—she implied people

[018-145]were—were talking about it.”

[018-146]“Of all the damn nonsense!” his uncle exclaimed.

[018-147]George looked at him haggardly. “You’re sure they’re not?”

[018-148]“Rubbish! Your mother’s on my side about this division because she

[018-149]knows Sydney’s a pig and always has been a pig, and so has his spiteful

[018-150]wife. I’m trying to keep them from getting the better of your mother as

[018-151]well as from getting the better of me, don’t you suppose? Well, they’re

[018-152]in a rage because Sydney always could do what he liked with father

[018-153]unless your mother interfered, and they know I got Isabel to ask him

[018-154]not to do what they wanted. They’re keeping up the fight and they’re

[018-155]sore—and Amelia’s a woman who always says any damn thing that comes

[018-156]into her head! That’s all there is to it.”

[018-157]“But she said,” George persisted wretchedly; “she said there was talk.

[018-158]She said—”

[018-159]“Look here, young fellow!” Amberson laughed good-naturedly. “There

[018-160]probably is some harmless talk about the way your Aunt Fanny goes after

[018-161]poor Eugene, and I’ve no doubt I’ve abetted it myself. People can’t

[018-162]help being amused by a thing like that. Fanny was always languishing at

[018-163]him, twenty-odd years ago, before he left here. Well, we can’t blame

[018-164]the poor thing if she’s got her hopes up again, and I don’t know that I

[018-165]blame her, myself, for using your mother the way she does.”

[018-166]“How do you mean?”

[018-167]Amberson put his hand on George’s shoulder. “You like to tease Fanny,”

[018-168]he said, “but I wouldn’t tease her about this, if I were you. Fanny

[018-169]hasn’t got much in her life. You know, Georgie, just being an aunt

[018-170]isn’t really the great career it may sometimes appear to you! In fact,

[018-171]I don’t know of anything much that Fanny has got, except her feeling

[018-172]about Eugene. She’s always had it—and what’s funny to us is pretty much

[018-173]life-and-death to her, I suspect. Now, I’ll not deny that Eugene Morgan

[018-174]is attracted to your mother. He is; and that’s another case of ‘always

[018-175]was’; but I know him, and he’s a knight, George—a crazy one, perhaps,

[018-176]if you’ve read ‘Don Quixote.’ And I think your mother likes him better

[018-177]than she likes any man outside her own family, and that he interests

[018-178]her more than anybody else—and ‘always has.’ And that’s all there is to

[018-179]it, except—”

[018-180]“Except what?” George asked quickly, as he paused.

[018-181]“Except that I suspect—” Amberson chuckled, and began over: “I’ll tell

[018-182]you in confidence. I think Fanny’s a fairly tricky customer, for such

[018-183]an innocent old girl! There isn’t any real harm in her, but she’s a

[018-184]great diplomatist—lots of cards up her lace sleeves, Georgie! By the

[018-185]way, did you ever notice how proud she is of her arms? Always flashing

[018-186]’em at poor Eugene!” And he stopped to laugh again.

[018-187]“I don’t see anything confidential about that,” George complained. “I

[018-188]thought—”
