# Fred Kinney Sr.

Fred Kinney Sr. was an old friend of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]] in Midland, and served as a confidant and expositor during the period of Morgan's return to the town. He was the father of Fred Kinney Jr., who became a recurring figure in [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]'s social circle. Kinney Sr. appears principally in a long conversation with Eugene Morgan that reveals much about the latter's feelings regarding his return to Midland and his unresolved past with [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]].

## A Friend's Return

When Eugene Morgan returned to Midland with his daughter [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], Fred Kinney Sr. was among the first to welcome him back. The two men shared a long conversation in which Kinney, with the privilege of old friendship, gently prodded Eugene about what had changed in his feelings toward the town — and toward Isabel (009-071). Kinney's role in this scene is that of the trusted old friend who can say things others cannot, and his observations draw out Eugene's private reflections about his youthful love and the passage of time.

## An Expositor of the Past

Kinney served as a bridge figure between the present and the town's lost era of courtship and splendor. In his conversation with Eugene, he remarked sympathetically, "Well, life's odd enough, I expect," acknowledging the strange turns of fate that had brought Eugene back to a town he had once left under a cloud (009-079). When Eugene spoke of seeing Isabel again, Kinney was the sounding board for his friend's emotions, noting the way Isabel still looked at Eugene "as though you were a something on a stick" — the old flame still burning (009-096).

## Between Two Generations

Kinney Sr. occupied an unusual position, connected both to Eugene's generation and, through his son Fred Kinney Jr., to the younger set that included George Amberson Minafer. His son was a member of [[friends-of-the-ace|the Friends of the Ace]] and an early romantic rival of George's, giving the elder Kinney a perspective that spanned both the old Midland and the new. When Kinney heard Eugene reflect on George's character, he listened with the knowing sympathy of a man who had seen the Amberson pride at work across two generations (009-133).
