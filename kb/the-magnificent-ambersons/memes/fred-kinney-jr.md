# Fred Kinney Jr.

Fred Kinney Jr. was the son of [[fred-kinney-sr|Fred Kinney Sr.]] and a member of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]'s social circle in Midland. Described unflatteringly by George as a "miserable red-headed outsider" (012-041), Fred Kinney Jr. served as both a rival and a foil to the young Amberson heir, his modest circumstances and good-natured persistence highlighting George's own arrogance and insecurity.

## Role in the Novel

Fred Kinney Jr. functions primarily as a dramatic irritant in George Amberson Minafer's life. His first significant appearance occurs in the clubroom of [[friends-of-the-ace|the Friends of the Ace]], where during George's absence at school the members elected Fred president. Upon his return, George stormed into a meeting and promptly overthrew him:

> "We will not!" Fred Kinney shouted. "We'll have our reg'lar meeting, and then we'll play euchre & nickel a corner, what we're here for."

> "All in favour of me being president instead of Fred Kinney," shouted Georgie, "say 'Aye.'" The "Ayes" have it! (005-066)

The presidency dispute established Fred Kinney Jr. as the one boy in George's circle willing to challenge him openly, even if briefly.

Fred reappears later in the novel as a source of romantic friction. Fanny Minafer maliciously spreads a rumor that [[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]] and Fred Kinney are engaged (016-123), provoking George to confront Lucy directly: "Your conduct with Fred Kinney!" George shouted (017-041). The rumor was false; Isabel Amberson dismisses Fred as beneath notice — "Fred Kinney's only a clerk in his uncle's hardware place: he couldn't marry for ages" (016-146) — yet George's jealousy reveals how deeply even a minor rival like Kinney could unsettle him.

By the novel's later chapters, Fred Kinney Jr. has moved on from the Amberson orbit entirely. He ultimately becomes engaged to Janie Sharon, a match that Fred Kinney Sr. dryly notes resulted from Lucy having "refused Fred so often that he got engaged to Janie Sharon just to prove that someone would have him" (038-027). The remark, delivered to Lucy herself, underscores Fred Kinney Jr.'s role as a persistent but ultimately unthreatening suitor whose path never quite intersected with the Amberson grand design except as an irritant to George.

## Relationships

- **Fred Kinney Sr. (father)**: His father was an old friend of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]] and served as a confidant to the older generation, giving Fred Jr. a familial connection to both the Amberson and Morgan circles.

- **George Amberson Minafer**: Fred's relationship with George was defined by competition and resentment. George viewed Fred as a "red-headed outsider" (012-041) and reacted with bitterness whenever Fred's name arose (013-034). The F.O.T.A. presidency dispute early in the novel set the pattern: Fred could temporarily occupy a position George considered his own, but George would always reclaim it by force of personality and social position.

- **Lucy Morgan**: Fred was an unsuccessful suitor to Lucy Morgan. The false engagement rumor caused a confrontation between George and Lucy, and Fred's repeated proposals — all refused — became a mild running joke in Midland society.

## Key Scenes

- **F.O.T.A. Presidency Dispute (Section 5)**: Elected president in George's absence, Fred attempted to conduct a regular meeting before George reclaimed control by acclamation.

- **Lewis Home Visit (Section 12)**: George referred to Fred as a "miserable red-headed outsider," revealing the class and personal disdain with which he viewed his rival.

- **The False Engagement Rumor (Section 16)**: Fanny Minafer's gossip about an engagement between Fred Kinney and Lucy Morgan successfully goaded George into a jealous reaction.

- **Confrontation with Lucy (Section 17)**: George shouted at Lucy about her "conduct with Fred Kinney," a confrontation that Lucy met with delighted laughter.

- **Moonlit Porch (Section 25)**: Fred appears in a tableau of Midland youth, "sitting on the white steps of her own front porch in the moonlight, with red-headed Fred Kinney and silly Charlie Johnson" (025-133), part of the ordinary social world that George's pride would eventually cost him.
