## Overview

Fashion in *The Magnificent Ambersons* is never merely decorative. Booth Tarkington employs clothing, dress, and personal adornment as one of the novel's most sophisticated systems of social signification — a visual language through which class position, economic fortune, and moral character are simultaneously declared and contested. From the opening chapter's miniature history of Midwestern sartorial codes to the final image of George Amberson Minafer retaining his magnificence even amid ruin, what people wear and how they wear it maps the trajectory of an entire social order in transition.

The novel's treatment of fashion operates on three interlocking levels. First, Tarkington offers a direct historical chronicle of changing dress codes in the American Midwest between the 1870s and the 1910s — the shift from an "aristocracy of texture" (001-031) to a regime of rapidly shifting shapes, from broadcloth and silk to ready-made suits and the "Derby" hat. Second, clothing functions as a precise class marker: the Ambersons' sartorial codes distinguish insiders from outsiders, the established gentry from the newly wealthy industrial class, and the dignified from the vulgar. Third, and most significantly, changes in fashion register deeper transformations in the economic and social structure that will ultimately destroy the Amberson family. The new mode of dress that Eugene Morgan embodies — the soft gray fur motoring coat, the suede cap and gloves of the automobile age — is not simply a different style but a different world.

Throughout the novel, Tarkington trains a satirical yet elegiac eye on the costumes of his characters, understanding implicitly that fashion is the theater in which social power performs itself. The [[the-last-ball|Last Ball]] is as much a fashion event as a social one; the [[amberson-mansion]] is not merely a house but a stage for the display of sartorial magnificence; and the [[decline-of-the-ambersons]] is written just as legibly in the withering of dress codes as in the dwindling of bank balances.

## In the Novel

### The Aristocracy of Texture

Tarkington establishes his fashion thesis in the novel's very first chapter with an extended historical treatment of Midwestern dress. The opening social order is defined by what might be called an aristocracy of material:

> "During the earlier years of this period, elegance of personal appearance was believed to rest more upon the texture of garments than upon their shaping. A silk dress needed no remodelling when it was a year or so old; it remained distinguished by merely remaining silk." (001-022)

This passage reveals the foundation of the Amberson-era class system: status inhered in substance, not surface. Silk, velvet, broadcloth, and sealskin were not merely expensive fabrics but visible certificates of membership in a stable hierarchy. The key word is "distinguished" — the silk dress did not merely look elegant; it performed the work of social distinction, separating its wearer from those who could afford only cotton or wool. In this world, a woman who possessed a sealskin coat was a public event: "sick people were got to windows to see it go by" (001-012). Fashion was a form of civic spectacle, and the Ambersons were its principal performers.

The social map of the town was, in Tarkington's telling, a map of textiles: "all the women who wore silk or velvet knew all the other women who wore silk or velvet" (001-010). The verb "knew" here does double duty: it signals both social acquaintance and social recognition. To wear silk was to be known; to be known was to matter. This equation of visible fabric with invisible social substance is the founding principle of the Amberson world.

### Shifting Fashions and the Threat of Novelty

The stability of this texture-based order could not last. Tarkington's language grows almost martial in describing the assault of the new:

> "Shifting fashions of shape replaced aristocracy of texture: dressmakers, shoemakers, hatmakers, and tailors, increasing in cunning and in power, found means to make new clothes old." (001-031)

The phrase "increasing in cunning and in power" casts the makers of fashion as a rising class of their own — artisans and merchants whose economic interests lay precisely in destabilizing the old order of durable distinction. Where silk stayed distinguished "by merely remaining silk," the new fashion system required constant turnover. The "Derby" hat arrived; trousers acquired and lost creases; the "dude" was invented and then discarded. Each seasonal shift eroded the connection between material substance and social permanence. In a world where "ready-made" trousers with a crease were "considered plebeian" because the crease "proved that the garment had lain upon a shelf, and hence was 'ready-made'" (001-040), the very concept of authentic gentility was under siege by a democratizing marketplace.

This transformation in fashion prefigures the larger transformation that will destroy the Ambersons. Just as "shifting fashions of shape" make old clothes unwearable, the economic forces of industrialization — represented by Eugene Morgan's automobile factory — make old fortunes unsustainable. The novel's fashion history is, in miniature, the novel's plot.

### George Amberson Minafer: The Sartorial Prince

No character is more precisely costumed than George Amberson Minafer, and his clothing traces the arc of his family's fortunes with diagrammatic clarity. Tarkington introduces George's childhood through his mother's disastrous dress choices: dressed in "a silk sash, and silk stockings, and a broad lace collar, with his little black velvet suit" and "long brown curls" (003-108), the boy is a walking contradiction — a Little Lord Fauntleroy exterior concealing a brawler's temperament. The velvet suit is his mother Isabel's fantasy of aristocratic childhood, imposed upon a boy who would rather be fighting in the alleys. The dissonance between the clothing and the character beneath it is Tarkington's first hint that the Amberson surface is not quite what it seems.

At the height of his glory — the Last Ball — George achieves a perfection of appearance that the novel presents with equal parts admiration and irony. He stands "white-gloved, with a gardenia in his buttonhole" (005-138), "briskly equipped to the fashion as exquisite young George" (005-152). The word "exquisite" returns repeatedly to describe George at his peak, and it is the perfect word: it suggests both beauty and fragility, something finely wrought that will not bear rough handling. George's sartorial magnificence is real — no one denies his elegance — but it is the magnificence of a surface with nothing behind it, a costume worn by a man who has never produced anything.

Lucy Morgan, the novel's clearest-eyed observer, sees through the costume from the first. At the ball, when George dismisses Eugene Morgan as a "queer-looking duck," Lucy corrects him: "I mean the aristocratic duck" (006-153), referring to George's own impeccably dressed Uncle George. Her remark subtly suggests that the difference between aristocratic and queer-looking is largely a matter of tailoring — and that she knows it.

### Eugene Morgan: The New Man's New Clothes

If George Amberson Minafer represents the old regime's sartorial perfection, Eugene Morgan represents the rising order's sartorial disruption. Eugene's first appearance at the Amberson ball is a study in sartorial failure: George judges him by "his unfashionable hair, his eyebrows, his preoccupied tie and his old coat" (006-050) and "set him down as a queer-looking duck" (006-052). The judgment is devastating precisely because it is correct by the Amberson code. Eugene's coat is "no product of this year, or of last year either" (006-045). In a world where clothing is status, a man in an unfashionable coat is a man without status.

But Tarkington's great insight is that Eugene's sartorial deficiencies are the inverse of his economic dynamism. He is not keeping up with fashion because he is too busy building the future. When he returns years later, economically triumphant, the contrast is absolute:

> "Eugene, this afternoon, was richly in the new outdoor mode: motoring coat was soft gray fur; his cap and gloves were of gray suede; and though Lucy's hand may have shown itself in the selection of these garnitures, he wore them easily, even with a becoming hint of jauntiness. Some change might be seen in his face, too, for a successful man is seldom to be mistaken, especially if his temper be genial. Eugene had begun to look like a millionaire." (031-130)

The transformation is total. The "queer-looking duck capering at the Amberson Ball in an old dress coat" (031-127) has become "a figure of the new era" (031-125). His gray suede and soft fur are the livery of the automobile age, as distinct from the Ambersons' stiff collars and skirted coats as his factory is from their inherited rent-rolls. Tarkington even notes that "his appearance afforded a debonair contrast to that of the queer-looking duck capering at the Amberson Ball in an old dress coat, and chugging up National Avenue through the snow in his nightmare of a sewing-machine" (031-126). The word "debonair" is telling: it is a French borrowing that connotes both style and ease, and it suggests that the new industrial wealth has developed an aesthetic of its own — one that makes the old Amberson formality look stiff and dated by comparison.

### Dress, Decline, and the Performance of Class

The novel meticulously tracks the relationship between economic decline and sartorial panic. When the family fortunes begin to falter, George becomes acutely sensitive to appearances. He "grumbled about the shabbiness and the old-fashioned look of the hotel" (014-128) where the Morgans are staying, and more broadly frets that "the whole Amberson Estate would be getting 'run-down and out-at-heel, if things weren't taken in hand pretty soon'" (014-131). The phrase "out-at-heel" is a sartorial metaphor for economic decay, and it captures the Amberson terror: that the family's material decline will become visible.

George's own maintenance of dress becomes almost pathological in the novel's later sections. Even in straitened circumstances, living in a boarding house with his Aunt Fanny, "she got George's 'dinner clothes' out for him — he maintained this habit — and she changed her own dress" (044-150). The persistence of formal dressing — the dinner clothes, the evening change of dress — when there is no dinner party to attend and no society to impress, is both touching and futile. It is the sartorial equivalent of whistling in the dark: a ritual performance of a class identity that has lost its material foundation.

Fanny Minafer's appearance follows a parallel trajectory of decline. When she visits George to deliver desperate financial news, she appears "in a street dress and a black hat, with a black umbrella in her black-gloved hand — for Fanny's dress, now that George was in trouble, was never cheerful" (028-046). The relentless black of Fanny's costume — dress, hat, umbrella, gloves — literalizes her emotional state while also signaling the family's economic mourning. The Ambersons who once dressed in silk and velvet now dress in mourning for themselves.

### The Summer Fashion-Picture and Its Meaning

One of the novel's most painterly passages depicts Isabel, Fanny, and Lucy departing the Amberson Mansion in a victoria:

> "It was a summer fashion-picture: the three ladies charmingly dressed, delicate parasols aloft; the lines of the victoria graceful as those of a violin; the trim pair of bays in glistening harness picked out with silver, and the serious black driver whom Isabel, being an Amberson, dared even in that town to put into a black livery coat, boots, white breeches, and cockaded hat." (018-107)

This "summer fashion-picture" is a tableau of the Amberson world at its most beautiful — and its most endangered. Every detail is a signifier of class position: the delicate parasols, the violin-graceful victoria, the silver-picked harness, and most of all the "serious black driver" in full livery. The aside "being an Amberson, dared even in that town" is crucial: in a Midwestern city whose ethos was democratic and commercial, the livery of a European-style coachman was an act of social daring, a deliberate assertion of aristocratic difference. But the passage's beauty is elegiac. It is a "fashion-picture" — a composed image, a work of art — and like all pictures, it cannot last. The insistence on describing it as a "picture" suggests its unreality, its status as an aesthetic object rather than a living social fact.

### The Persistence of Magnificence

The novel's final passages offer a complex coda to the theme of fashion and status. After everything has been lost — the mansion sold, the fortune gone, the family name buried under the city's growth — George Amberson Minafer walks the streets of what once were his family's domains. The building that was the Amberson Mansion has become a boarding house; the old Minafer house bears a sign for "Mutual Benev't Order Cavaliers and Dames of Purity" (042-012). But George himself "held his head up, and except for his gravity of countenance and the prison pallor he had acquired by too constantly remaining indoors, there was little to warn an acquaintance that he was not precisely the same George Amberson Minafer known aforetime. He was still so magnificent, indeed, that there came to his ears a waft of comment from a passing automobile" (042-017).

The word "magnificent" returns at the end, but now it describes something internal — a bearing, a demeanor, a refusal to be diminished — rather than the external trappings of silk, velvet, and gardenias. The "waft of comment from a passing automobile" is the final irony: the passing stranger in the automobile (the instrument of the Ambersons' destruction!) still recognizes George's magnificence. The surface still holds, even when the substance is gone. This is, in one sense, a tribute to the Amberson spirit. But it is also Tarkington's final observation on the nature of fashion and class: that the performance can outlast the reality, that the costume can survive the play.

Tarkington opens the novel with a direct analysis of the fashion-class nexus:

> In that town, in those days, all the women who wore silk or velvet knew all the other women who wore silk or velvet, and when there was a new purchase of sealskin, sick people were got to windows to see it go by. (001-010)

The novel's historical thesis on fashion and social change:

> Shifting fashions of shape replaced aristocracy of texture: dressmakers, shoemakers, hatmakers, and tailors, increasing in cunning and in power, found means to make new clothes old. (001-031)

George's childhood costume as the fantasy of aristocratic boyhood:

> Not only did he wear a silk sash, and silk stockings, and a broad lace collar, with his little black velvet suit: he had long brown curls, and often came home with burrs in them. (003-108)

George at his sartorial zenith, the Last Ball:

> George, white-gloved, with a gardenia in his buttonhole, stood with his mother and the Major, embowered in the big red and gold drawing room downstairs, to "receive" the guests. (005-138)

The judgment of Eugene Morgan by Amberson dress codes:

> Nevertheless, observing only his unfashionable hair, his eyebrows, his preoccupied tie and his old coat, the olympic George set him down as a queer-looking duck, and having thus completed his portrait, took no interest in him. (006-050)

Old John Minafer's defiance of formal dress codes and what it reveals about class boundaries within the family:

> Members of his family had exerted their influence uselessly — at eighty-nine conservative people seldom form radical new habits, and old John wore his "Sunday suit" of black broadcloth to the Amberson ball. The coat was square, with skirts to the knees; old John called it a "Prince Albert" and was well enough pleased with it, but his great-nephew considered it the next thing to an insult. (006-061)

Amelia Amberson, a walking display of inherited wealth made material:

> Amelia, likewise full-bodied, showed glittering blond hair exuberantly dressed; a pink, fat face cold under a white-hot tiara; a solid, cold bosom under a white-hot necklace; great, cold, gloved arms, and the rest of her beautifully upholstered. (006-178)

The summer fashion-picture as elegy for a vanishing world:

> It was a summer fashion-picture: the three ladies charmingly dressed, delicate parasols aloft; the lines of the victoria graceful as those of a violin; the trim pair of bays in glistening harness picked out with silver, and the serious black driver whom Isabel, being an Amberson, dared even in that town to put into a black livery coat, boots, white breeches, and cockaded hat. Eugene Morgan's transformation from shabby inventor to sartorial figure of the new era:

Isabel's dresses, the ghostly remnants of a vanished social life:

> her own special chairs were, and her own special books, and the two great walnut wardrobes filled with her dresses and wraps (040-033)

The persistence of formal dressing amid poverty:

## Historical Context

### Fashion and Class in Gilded Age America

The novel's fashion history tracks the evolution of American dress from the post-Civil War period through the Progressive Era. Tarkington published *The Magnificent Ambersons* in 1918, but his fictional chronology spans roughly 1873 to 1910 — the period during which the United States transformed from a predominantly agricultural nation of small towns into an industrial power of growing cities. Fashion, during these decades, was undergoing a parallel transformation that Tarkington captures with remarkable precision.

In the 1870s, the American upper class still looked to European models of aristocratic dress. The "broadcloth" and "doeskin" that "old men and governors wore" (001-026) were English imports, signifying a transatlantic class identity. The silk dress that "remained distinguished by merely remaining silk" (001-025) reflected an economy of scarcity in which fine textiles were genuinely rare and genuinely expensive. A woman's wardrobe was an inventory of capital, meant to last years and to be passed down. The "two great walnut wardrobes filled with her dresses and wraps" (040-033) in Isabel's room represent not merely vanity but a form of stored wealth, a textile treasury.

By the 1890s and 1900s, however, the rise of American manufacturing — particularly the ready-made clothing industry centered in cities like New York and Chicago — had democratized fashion. The "hand-me-downs" (001-042) that the Ambersons disdained were the product of a revolution in clothing production: standardized sizing, factory cutting, and mass distribution made stylish dress available to the middle and even working classes. The "shifting fashions of shape" that Tarkington describes (001-031) were in part a commercial strategy: manufacturers and retailers needed customers to buy new clothes each season, and they engineered a culture of rapid stylistic obsolescence to ensure they did.

### The Automobile and the New Sartorial Order

Eugene Morgan's transformation from the shabby figure in an "old dress coat" (031-128) to the polished man in "soft gray fur" and "gray suede" (031-130) reflects a genuine cultural shift that accompanied the rise of the automobile. Motoring required new kinds of clothing — dust coats, goggles, leather gauntlets, fur-lined driving coats — that blurred the old distinctions between formal and informal dress. The automobile democratized mobility, and with it, appearance: a man in motoring clothes could be a millionaire or a mechanic. The Ambersons' code of silk, broadcloth, and formal dress could not survive in a world where the most powerful men dressed for the road rather than the drawing room.

Tarkington was writing at a moment when the old American aristocracy of family and inherited wealth was visibly giving way to a new plutocracy of industry. The novel's fashion history is, in this sense, a precise allegory for the larger economic history: the "aristocracy of texture" was replaced by the "shifting fashions of shape," just as the aristocracy of inherited position was replaced by the shifting fortunes of industrial capitalism.

### The Midwest as Fashion's Borderland

The Midwestern setting of the novel adds a specific inflection to its treatment of fashion. Midland is neither the cosmopolitan East (where George briefly attends college) nor the provincial frontier. It is a "town spread and darken into a city" (001-007), and its fashion consciousness is both aspirational and anxious. The Ambersons import their fashions — orchestra and caterer "brought from away" for the ball (005-114) — because Midland cannot quite supply the standard they wish to maintain. The town's own dressmakers, shoemakers, and hatmakers, "increasing in cunning and in power" (001-032), are local agents of a national transformation.

Isabel's daring to put her black driver into "black livery coat, boots, white breeches, and cockaded hat" (018-112) is specifically an Amberson provocation within a Midwestern context — a deliberate importation of European aristocratic style into a town whose ethos was democratic and commercial. The gesture is magnificent, but it is also fragile. It depends entirely on the Amberson fortune to sustain it, and when that fortune fails, the livery, the victoria, the delicate parasols, and the summer fashion-picture all vanish together.
