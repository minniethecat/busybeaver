[037-001]A new spirit of citizenship had already sharply defined itself. It was

[037-002]idealistic, and its ideals were expressed in the new kind of young

[037-003]men in business downtown. They were optimists—optimists to the point

[037-004]of belligerence—their motto being “Boost! Don’t Knock!” And they were

[037-005]hustlers, believing in hustling and in honesty because both paid. They

[037-006]loved their city and worked for it with a plutonic energy which was

[037-007]always ardently vocal. They were viciously governed, but they sometimes

[037-008]went so far to struggle for better government on account of the helpful

[037-009]effect of good government on the price of real estate and “betterment”

[037-010]generally; the politicians could not go too far with them, and knew it.

[037-011]The idealists planned and strove and shouted that their city should

[037-012]become a better, better, and better city—and what they meant, when

[037-013]they used the word “better,” was “more prosperous,” and the core of

[037-014]their idealism was this: “The more prosperous my beloved city, the more

[037-015]prosperous beloved I!” They had one supreme theory: that the perfect

[037-016]beauty and happiness of cities and of human life was to be brought

[037-017]about by more factories; they had a mania for factories; there was

[037-018]nothing they would not do to cajole a factory away from another city;

[037-019]and they were never more piteously embittered than when another city

[037-020]cajoled one away from them.

[037-021]What they meant by Prosperity was credit at the bank; but in exchange

[037-022]for this credit they got nothing that was not dirty, and, therefore,

[037-023]to a sane mind, valueless; since whatever was cleaned was dirty again

[037-024]before the cleaning was half done. For, as the town grew, it grew dirty

[037-025]with an incredible completeness. The idealists put up magnificent

[037-026]business buildings and boasted of them, but the buildings were begrimed

[037-027]before they were finished. They boasted of their libraries, of their

[037-028]monuments and statues; and poured soot on them. They boasted of their

[037-029]schools, but the schools were dirty, like the children within them.

[037-030]This was not the fault of the children or their mothers. It was the

[037-031]fault of the idealists, who said: “The more dirt, the more prosperity.”

[037-032]They drew patriotic, optimistic breaths of the flying powdered filth

[037-033]of the streets, and took the foul and heavy smoke with gusto into the

[037-034]profundities of their lungs. “Boost! Don’t knock!” they said. And

[037-035]every year or so they boomed a great Clean-up Week, when everybody was

[037-036]supposed to get rid of the tin cans in his backyard.

[037-037]They were happiest when the tearing down and building up were most

[037-038]riotous, and when new factory districts were thundering into life.

[037-039]In truth, the city came to be like the body of a great dirty man,

[037-040]skinned, to show his busy works, yet wearing a few barbaric ornaments;

[037-041]and such a figure carved, coloured, and discoloured, and set up in

[037-042]the market-place, would have done well enough as the god of the new

[037-043]people. Such a god they had indeed made in their own image, as all

[037-044]peoples make the god they truly serve; though of course certain of

[037-045]the idealists went to church on Sunday, and there knelt to Another,

[037-046]considered to be impractical in business. But while the Growing went

[037-047]on, this god of their market-place was their true god, their familiar

[037-048]and spirit-control. They did not know that they were his helplessly

[037-049]obedient slaves, nor could they ever hope to realize their serfdom (as

[037-050]the first step toward becoming free men) until they should make the

[037-051]strange and hard discovery that matter should serve man’s spirit.

[037-052]“Prosperity” meant good credit at the bank, black lungs, and

[037-053]housewives’ Purgatory. The women fought the dirt all they could; but if

[037-054]they let the air into their houses they let in the dirt. It shortened

[037-055]their lives, and kept them from the happiness of ever seeing anything

[037-056]white. And thus, as the city grew, the time came when [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], after a

[037-057]hard struggle, had to give up her blue-and-white curtains and her

[037-058]white walls. Indoors, she put everything into dull gray and brown, and

[037-059]outside had the little house painted the dark green nearest to black.

[037-060]Then she knew, of course, that everything was as dirty as ever, but was

[037-061]a little less distressed because it no longer looked so dirty as it was.

[037-062]These were bad times for [[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]]. This quarter, already

[037-063]old, lay within a mile of the centre of the town, but business moved

[037-064]in other directions; and the Addition’s share of Prosperity was only

[037-065]the smoke and dirt, with the bank credit left out. The owners of

[037-066]the original big houses sold them, or rented them to boarding-house

[037-067]keepers, and the tenants of the multitude of small houses moved

[037-068]“farther out” (where the smoke was thinner) or into apartment houses,

[037-069]which were built by dozens now. Cheaper tenants took their places,

[037-070]and the rents were lower and lower, and the houses shabbier and

[037-071]shabbier—for all these shabby houses, burning soft coal, did their best

[037-072]to help in the destruction of their own value. They helped to make

[037-073]the quarter so dingy and the air so foul to breathe that no one would

[037-074]live there who had [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] enough to get “farther out” where there were

[037-075]glimpses of ungrayed sky and breaths of cleaner winds. And with the

[037-076]coming of the new speed, “farther out” was now as close to business

[037-077]as the Addition had been in the days of its prosperity. Distances had

[037-078]ceased to matter.

[037-079]The five new houses, built so closely where had been the fine lawn of

[037-080][[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Mansion, did not look new. When they were a year old they

[037-081]looked as old as they would ever look; and two of them were vacant,

[037-082]having never been rented, for the Major’s mistake about apartment

[037-083]houses had been a disastrous one. “He guessed wrong,” [[george-amberson|George Amberson]]

[037-084]said. “He guessed wrong at just the wrong time! Housekeeping in a house

[037-085]is harder than in an apartment; and where the smoke and dirt are as

[037-086]thick as they are in the Addition, women can’t stand it. People were

[037-087]crazy for apartments—too bad he couldn’t have seen it in time. Poor

[037-088]man! he digs away at his ledgers by his old gas drop-light lamp almost

[037-089]every night—he still refuses to let the Mansion be torn up for wiring,

[037-090]you know. But he had one painful satisfaction this spring: he got his

[037-091]taxes lowered!”

[037-092]Amberson laughed ruefully, and [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]] asked how the Major could

[037-093]have managed such an economy. They were sitting upon the veranda at

[037-094]Isabel’s one evening during the third summer of the absence of their

[037-095]nephew and his mother; and the conversation had turned toward Amberson

[037-096]finances.

[037-097]“I said it was a ‘painful satisfaction,’ Fanny,” he explained. “The

[037-098]property has gone down in value, and they assessed it lower than they

[037-099]did fifteen years ago.”

[037-100]“But farther out—”

[037-101]“Oh, yes, ‘farther out!’ Prices are magnificent ‘farther out,’ and

[037-102]farther in, too! We just happen to be the wrong spot, that’s all. Not

[037-103]that I don’t think something could be done if father would let me have

[037-104]a hand; but he won’t. He can’t, I suppose I ought to say. He’s ‘always

[037-105]done his own figuring,’ he says; and it’s his lifelong habit to keep

[037-106]his affairs: and even his books, to himself, and just hand us out the

[037-107]money. Heaven knows he’s done enough of that!”

[037-108]He sighed; and both were silent, looking out at the long flares of the

[037-109]constantly passing automobile headlights, shifting in vast geometric

[037-110]demonstrations against the darkness. Now and then a bicycle wound its

[037-111]nervous way among these portents, or, at long intervals, a surrey or

[037-112]buggy plodded forlornly by.

[037-113]“There seem to be so many ways of making money nowadays,” Fanny said

[037-114]thoughtfully. “Every day I hear of a new fortune some person has got

[037-115]hold of, one way or another—nearly always it’s somebody you never heard

[037-116]of. It doesn’t seem all to be in just making motor cars; I hear there’s

[037-117]a great deal in manufacturing these things that motor cars use—new

[037-118]inventions particularly. I met dear old Frank Bronson the other day,

[037-119]and he told me—”

[037-120]“Oh, yes, even dear old Frank’s got the fever,” Amberson laughed. “He’s

[037-121]as wild as any of them. He told me about this invention he’s gone into,

[037-122]too. ‘Millions in it!’ Some new electric headlight better than anything

[037-123]yet—‘every car in America can’t help but have ’em,’ and all that. He’s

[037-124]putting half he’s laid by into it, and the fact is, he almost talked me

[037-125]into getting father to ‘finance me’ enough for me to go into it. Poor

[037-126]father! he’s financed me before! I suppose he would again if I had the

[037-127]heart to ask him; and this seems to be a good thing, though probably

[037-128]old Frank is a little too sanguine. At any rate, I’ve been thinking it

[037-129]over.”

[037-130]“So have I,” Fanny admitted. “He seemed to be certain it would pay

[037-131]twenty-five per cent. the first year, and enormously more after that;

[037-132]and I’m only getting four on my little principal. People are making

[037-133]such enormous fortunes out of everything to do with motor cars, it does

[037-134]seem as if—” She paused. “Well, I told him I’d think it over seriously.”

[037-135]“We may turn out to be partners and millionaires then,” Amberson

[037-136]laughed. “I thought I’d ask [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s advice.”

[037-137]“I wish you would,” said Fanny. “He probably knows exactly how much

[037-138]profit there would be in this.”

[037-139]Eugene’s advice was to “go slow”: he thought electric lights for

[037-140]automobiles were “coming—someday but probably not until certain

[037-141]difficulties could be overcome.” Altogether, he was discouraging, but

[037-142]by this time his two friends “had the fever” as thoroughly as old Frank

[037-143]Bronson himself had it; for they had been with Bronson to see the light

[037-144]working beautifully in a machine shop. They were already enthusiastic,

[037-145]and after asking Eugene’s opinion they argued with him, telling him how

[037-146]they had seen with their own eyes that the difficulties he mentioned

[037-147]had been overcome. “Perfectly!” Fanny cried. “And if it worked in the

[037-148]shop it’s bound to work any place else, isn’t it?”

[037-149]He would not agree that it was “bound to”—yet, being pressed, was

[037-150]driven to admit that “it might,” and, retiring from what was developing

[037-151]into an oratorical contest, repeated a warning about not “putting too

[037-152]much into it.”

[037-153]George Amberson also laid stress on this caution later, though the

[037-154]Major had “financed him” again, and he was “going in.” “You must be

[037-155]careful to leave yourself a ‘margin of safety,’ Fanny,” he said. “I’m

[037-156]confident that is a pretty conservative investment of its kind, and

[037-157]all the chances are with us, but you must be careful to leave yourself

[037-158]enough to fall back on, in case anything should go wrong.”

[037-159]Fanny deceived him. In the impossible event of “anything going wrong”

[037-160]she would have enough left to “live on,” she declared, and laughed

[037-161]excitedly, for she was having the best time that had come to her since

[037-162]Wilbur’s death. Like so many women for whom money has always been

[037-163]provided without their understanding how, she was prepared to be a

[037-164]thorough and irresponsible plunger.

[037-165]Amberson, in his wearier way, shared her excitement, and in the winter,

[037-166]when [[amberson-motor-company|the exploiting company]] had been formed, and he brought Fanny her

[037-167]importantly engraved shares of stock, he reverted to his prediction of

[037-168]possibilities, made when they first spoke of the new light.

[037-169]“We seem to be partners, all right,” he laughed. “Now let’s go ahead

[037-170]and be millionaires before Isabel and young George come home.”

[037-171]“When they come home!” she echoed sorrowfully—and it was a phrase which

[037-172]found an evasive echo in Isabel’s letters. In these letters Isabel was

[037-173]always planning pleasant things that she and Fanny and the Major and

[037-174]George and “brother George” would do—when she and her son came home.

[037-175]“They’ll find things pretty changed, I’m afraid,” Fanny said. “If they

[037-176]ever do come home!”
