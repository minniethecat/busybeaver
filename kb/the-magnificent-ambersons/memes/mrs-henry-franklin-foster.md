# Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster

Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster was the acknowledged intellectual leader of the Midland community during the era of Amberson splendor. She was closely connected to the social world of [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]], whose beauty and romantic history made her a perennial subject of discussion in the town. Mrs. Foster also had a well-known opinion of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]], Isabel's imperious young son, which she expressed in her characteristic manner.

## The Foremost Literary Authority

Both daily newspapers in Midland described Mrs. Foster as the foremost literary authority in the community (002-166). Her pronouncements on art, letters, and the drama were accepted more as law than as opinion. This extraordinary standing derived from the organization she founded: the [[womens-tennyson-club|Women's Tennyson Club]], a cultural institution that cemented her position as the town's arbiter of taste (002-168). When a new play such as "Hazel Kirke" arrived in town, many citizens waited to hear what Mrs. Foster thought of it before feeling warranted in expressing any estimate themselves (002-172).

## The Observer of Miss Amberson

Mrs. Foster was a pointed observer of the town's most beautiful woman. Like other ineligible young men of the town, she was unable to content herself with a distant view; she and others spent their time struggling to keep Miss Amberson's face turned toward them, though in Mrs. Foster's case this was less romantic pursuit than critical observation (003-008). Her remark about Isabel's appearance became one of the town's frequently quoted pronouncements.

## Prophet of the Amberson Fate

Mrs. Foster was noted for a particular observation about the Amberson family and its direction. In her role as social commentator, she said of the family what many others came to believe only much later (005-115). Her pronouncement foretold the decline that would eventually overtake the once-magnificent Ambersons. Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster herself had long ago gone the way of all wisdom, having died before the full sweep of the decline she had glimpsed (005-117).
