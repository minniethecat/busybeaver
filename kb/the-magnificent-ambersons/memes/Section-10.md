[010-001]“We’re going pretty fast, [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]]!”

[010-002]“Well, you see, I’m only here for two weeks.”

[010-003]“I mean the sleigh!” she explained. “We’re not the only people on the

[010-004]street, you know.”

[010-005]“Oh, _they’ll_ keep out of the way.”

[010-006]“That’s very patrician charioteering, but it seems to me a horse like

[010-007]this needs guidance. I’m sure he’s going almost twenty miles an hour.”

[010-008]“That’s nothing,” said George; but he consented to look forward again.

[010-009]“He can trot under three minutes, all right.” He laughed. “I suppose

[010-010]your father thinks he can build a [[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons|horseless carriage]] to go that fast!”

[010-011]“They go that fast already, sometimes.”

[010-012]“Yes,” said George; “they do—for about a hundred feet! Then they give a

[010-013]yell and burn up.”

[010-014]Evidently she decided not to defend her father’s faith in horseless

[010-015]carriages, for she laughed, and said nothing. The cold air was

[010-016]polka-dotted with snowflakes, and trembled to the loud, continuous

[010-017]jingling of sleighbells. Boys and girls, all aglow and panting jets of

[010-018]vapour, darted at the passing sleighs to ride on the runners, or sought

[010-019]to rope their sleds to any vehicle whatever, but the fleetest no more

[010-020]than just touched the flying cutter, though a hundred soggy mittens

[010-021]grasped for it, then reeled and whirled till sometimes the wearers

[010-022]of those daring mittens plunged flat in the snow and lay a-sprawl,

[010-023]reflecting. For this was the holiday time, and all the boys and girls

[010-024]in town were out, most of them on [[national-avenue|National Avenue]].

[010-025]But there came panting and chugging up that flat thoroughfare a thing

[010-026]which some day was to spoil all their sleigh-time merriment—save

[010-027]for the rashest and most disobedient. It was vaguely like a topless

[010-028]surry, but cumbrous with unwholesome excrescences fore and aft, while

[010-029]underneath were spinning leather belts and something that whirred and

[010-030]howled and seemed to stagger. The ride-stealers made no attempt to

[010-031]fasten their sleds to a contrivance so nonsensical and yet so fearsome.

[010-032]Instead, they gave over their sport and concentrated all their energies

[010-033]in their lungs, so that up and down the street the one cry shrilled

[010-034]increasingly: “Git a hoss! Git a hoss! Git a hoss! Mister, why don’t

[010-035]you git a hoss?” But the mahout in charge, sitting solitary on the

[010-036]front seat, was unconcerned—he laughed, and now and then ducked a

[010-037]snowball without losing any of his good-nature. It was Mr. [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]

[010-038]Morgan who exhibited so cheerful a countenance between the forward

[010-039]visor of a deer-stalker cap and the collar of a fuzzy gray ulster. “Git

[010-040]a hoss!” the children shrieked, and gruffer voices joined them. “Git a

[010-041]hoss! Git a hoss! Git a _hoss!_”

[010-042][[george-amberson-minafer|George Minafer]] was correct thus far: the twelve miles an hour of such

[010-043]a machine would never over-take George’s trotter. The cutter was

[010-044]already scurrying between the stone pillars at the entrance to Amberson

[010-045]Addition.

[010-046]“That’s my grandfather’s,” said George, nodding toward [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]]

[010-047]Mansion.

[010-048]“I ought to know that!” [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] exclaimed. “We stayed there late enough

[010-049]last night: papa and I were almost the last to go. He and your mother

[010-050]and [[fanny-minafer|Miss Fanny Minafer]] got the musicians to play another waltz when

[010-051]everybody else had gone downstairs and the fiddles were being put away

[010-052]in their cases. Papa danced part of it with Miss Minafer and the rest

[010-053]with your mother. Miss Minafer’s your aunt, isn’t she?”

[010-054]“Yes; she lives with us. I tease her a good deal.”

[010-055]“What about?”

[010-056]“Oh, anything handy—whatever’s easy to tease an old maid about.”

[010-057]“Doesn’t she mind?”

[010-058]“She usually has sort of a grouch on me,” laughed George. “Nothing

[010-059]much. That’s our house just beyond grandfather’s.” He waved a sealskin

[010-060]gauntlet to indicate the house [[major-amberson|Major Amberson]] had built for Isabel as

[010-061]a wedding gift. “It’s almost the same as grandfather’s, only not as

[010-062]large and hasn’t got a regular ballroom. We gave the dance, last night,

[010-063]at grandfather’s on account of the ballroom, and because I’m the only

[010-064]grandchild, you know. Of course, some day that’ll be my house, though I

[010-065]expect my mother will most likely go on living where she does now, with

[010-066]father and Aunt Fanny. I suppose I’ll probably build a country house,

[010-067]too—somewhere East, I guess.” He stopped speaking, and frowned as they

[010-068]passed a closed carriage and pair. The body of this comfortable vehicle

[010-069]sagged slightly to one side; the paint was old and seamed with hundreds

[010-070]of minute cracks like little rivers on a black map; the coachman, a fat

[010-071]and elderly darky, seemed to drowse upon the box; but the open window

[010-072]afforded the occupants of the cutter a glimpse of a tired, fine old

[010-073]face, a silk hat, a pearl tie, and an astrachan collar, evidently out

[010-074]to take the air.

[010-075]“There’s your grandfather now,” said Lucy. “Isn’t it?”

[010-076]George’s frown was not relaxed. “Yes, it is; and he ought to give

[010-077]that rat-trap away and sell those old horses. They’re a disgrace, all

[010-078]shaggy—not even clipped. I suppose he doesn’t notice it—people get

[010-079]awful funny when they get old; they seem to lose their self-respect,

[010-080]sort of.”

[010-081]“He seemed a real Brummell to me,” she said.

[010-082]“Oh, he keeps up about what he wears, well enough, but—well, look

[010-083]at that!” He pointed to a statue of Minerva, one of the cast-iron

[010-084]sculptures Major Amberson had set up in opening [[amberson-addition|the Addition]]

[010-085]years before. Minerva was intact, but a blackish streak descended

[010-086]unpleasantly from her forehead to the point of her straight nose, and a

[010-087]few other streaks were sketched in a repellent dinge upon the folds of

[010-088]her drapery.

[010-089]“That must be from soot,” said Lucy. “There are so many houses around

[010-090]here.”

[010-091]“Anyhow, somebody ought to see that these statues are kept clean. My

[010-092]grandfather owns a good many of these houses, I guess, for renting.

[010-093]Of course, he sold most of the lots—there aren’t any vacant ones, and

[010-094]there used to be heaps of ’em when I was a boy. Another thing I don’t

[010-095]think he ought to allow: a good many of these people bought big lots

[010-096]and they built houses on ’em; then the price of the land kept getting

[010-097]higher, and they’d sell part of their yards and let the people that

[010-098]bought it build houses on it to live in, till they haven’t hardly any

[010-099]of ’em got big, open yards any more, and it’s getting all too much

[010-100]built up. The way it used to be, it was like a gentleman’s country

[010-101]estate, and that’s the way my grandfather ought to keep it. He lets

[010-102]these people take too many liberties: they do anything they want to.”

[010-103]“But how could he stop them?” Lucy asked, surely with reason. “If he

[010-104]sold them the land, it’s theirs, isn’t it?”

[010-105]George remained serene in the face of this apparently difficult

[010-106]question. “He ought to have all the trades-people boycott the families

[010-107]that sell part of their yards that way. All he’d have to do would be

[010-108]to tell the trades-people they wouldn’t get any more orders from the

[010-109]family if they didn’t do it.”

[010-110]“From ‘the family’? What family?”

[010-111]“Our family,” said George, unperturbed. “The Ambersons.”

[010-112]“I see!” she murmured, and evidently she did see something that he did

[010-113]not, for, as she lifted her muff to her face, he asked:

[010-114]“What are you laughing at now?”

[010-115]“Why?”

[010-116]“You always seem to have some little secret of your own to get happy

[010-117]over!”

[010-118]“Always!” she exclaimed. “What a big word when we only met last night!”

[010-119]“That’s another case of it,” he said, with obvious sincerity. “One of

[010-120]the reasons I don’t like you—much!—is you’ve got that way of seeming

[010-121]quietly superior to everybody else.”

[010-122]“I!” she cried. “I have?”

[010-123]“Oh, you think you keep it sort of confidential to yourself, but it’s

[010-124]plain enough! I don’t believe in that kind of thing.”

[010-125]“You don’t?”

[010-126]“No,” said George emphatically. “Not with me! I think the world’s like

[010-127]this: there’s a few people that their birth and position, and so on,

[010-128]puts them at the top, and they ought to treat each other entirely as

[010-129]equals.” His voice betrayed a little emotion as he added, “I wouldn’t

[010-130]speak like this to everybody.”

[010-131]“You mean you’re confiding your deepest creed—or code, whatever it

[010-132]is—to me?”

[010-133]“Go on, make fun of it, then!” George said bitterly. “You do think

[010-134]you’re terribly clever! It makes me tired!”

[010-135]“Well, as you don’t like my seeming ‘quietly superior,’ after this I’ll

[010-136]be noisily superior,” she returned cheerfully. “We aim to please!”

[010-137]“I had a notion before I came for you today that we were going to

[010-138]quarrel,” he said.

[010-139]“No, we won’t; it takes two!” She laughed and waved her muff toward a

[010-140]new house, not quite completed, standing in a field upon their right.

[010-141]They had passed beyond Amberson Addition, and were leaving the northern

[010-142]fringes of the town for the open country. “Isn’t that a beautiful

[010-143]house!” she exclaimed. “Papa and I call it our Beautiful House.”

[010-144]George was not pleased. “Does it belong to you?”

[010-145]“Of course not! Papa brought me out here the other day, driving in

[010-146]his machine, and we both loved it. It’s so spacious and dignified and

[010-147]plain.”

[010-148]“Yes, it’s plain enough!” George grunted.

[010-149]“Yet it’s lovely; the gray-green roof and shutters give just enough

[010-150]colour, with the trees, for the long white walls. It seems to me the

[010-151]finest house I’ve seen in this part of the country.”

[010-152]George was outraged by an enthusiasm so ignorant—not ten minutes ago

[010-153]they had passed the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]]. “Is that a sample of your taste

[010-154]in architecture?” he asked.

[010-155]“Yes. Why?”

[010-156]“Because it strikes me you better go somewhere and study the subject a

[010-157]little!”

[010-158]Lucy looked puzzled. “What makes you have so much feeling about it?

[010-159]Have I offended you?”

[010-160]“‘Offended’ nothing!” George returned brusquely. “Girls usually

[010-161]think they know it all as soon as they’ve learned to dance and dress

[010-162]and flirt a little. They never know anything about things like

[010-163]architecture, for instance. That house is about as bum a house as any

[010-164]house I ever saw!”

[010-165]“Why?”

[010-166]“Why?” George repeated. “Did you ask me why?”

[010-167]“Yes.”

[010-168]“Well, for one thing—” he paused—“for one thing—well, just look at it!

[010-169]I shouldn’t think you’d have to do any more than look at it if you’d

[010-170]ever given any attention to architecture.”

[010-171]“What is the matter with its architecture, Mr. Minafer?”

[010-172]“Well, it’s this way,” said George. “It’s like this. Well, for

[010-173]instance, that house—well, it was built like a town house.” He spoke of

[010-174]it in the past tense, because they had now left it far behind them—a

[010-175]human habit of curious significance. “It was like a house meant for a

[010-176]street in the city. What kind of a house was that for people of any

[010-177]taste to build out here in the country?”

[010-178]“But papa says it’s built that way on purpose. There are a lot of other

[010-179]houses being built in this direction, and papa says the city’s coming

[010-180]out this way; and in a year or two that house will be right in town.”

[010-181]“It was a bum house, anyhow,” said George crossly. “I don’t even know

[010-182]the people that are building it. They say a lot of riffraff come to

[010-183]town every year nowadays and there’s other riffraff that have always

[010-184]lived here, and have made a little [[wealth-and-fortune|money]], and act as if they owned the

[010-185]place. Uncle [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] was talking about it yesterday: he says he and some

[010-186]of his friends are organizing a country club, and already some of these

[010-187]riffraff are worming into it—people he never heard of at all! Anyhow, I

[010-188]guess it’s pretty clear you don’t know a great deal about architecture.”

[010-189]She demonstrated the completeness of her amiability by laughing. “I’ll

[010-190]know something about the North Pole before long,” she said, “if we keep

[010-191]going much farther in this direction!”

[010-192]At this he was remorseful. “All right, we’ll turn, and drive south

[010-193]awhile till you get warmed up again. I expect we have been going

[010-194]against the wind about long enough. Indeed, I’m sorry!”
