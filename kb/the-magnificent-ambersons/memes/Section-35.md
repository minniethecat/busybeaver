[035-001]I have been out to the mail-box, darling, with a letter I’ve

[035-002]written to [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]], and he’ll have it in the morning. It would be

[035-003]unfair not to let him know at once, and my decision could not

[035-004]change if I waited. It would always be the same. I think it is a

[035-005]little better for me to write to you, like this, instead of waiting

[035-006]till you wake up and then telling you, because I’m foolish and

[035-007]might cry again, and I took a vow once, long ago, that you should

[035-008]never see me cry. Not that I’ll feel like crying when we talk

[035-009]things over tomorrow. I’ll be “all right and fine” (as you say so

[035-010]often) by that time—don’t fear. I think what makes me most ready to

[035-011]cry now is the thought of the terrible suffering in your poor face,

[035-012]and the unhappy knowledge that it is I, your mother, who put it

[035-013]there. It shall never come again! I love you better than anything

[035-014]and everything else on earth. God gave you to me—and oh! how

[035-015]thankful I have been every day of my life for that sacred gift—and

[035-016]nothing can ever come between me and God’s gift. I cannot hurt

[035-017]you, and I cannot let you stay hurt as you have been—not another

[035-018]instant after you wake up, my darling boy! It is beyond my power.

[035-019]And Eugene was right—I know you couldn’t change about this. Your

[035-020]suffering shows how deep-seated the feeling is within you. So I’ve

[035-021]written him just about what I think you would like me to—though I

[035-022]told him I would always be fond of him and always his best friend,

[035-023]and I hoped his dearest friend. He’ll understand about not seeing

[035-024]him. He’ll understand that, though I didn’t say it in so many

[035-025]words. You mustn’t trouble about that—he’ll understand. Good-night,

[035-026]my darling, my beloved, my beloved! You mustn’t be troubled. I

[035-027]think I shouldn’t mind anything very much so long as I have you

[035-028]“all to myself”—as people say—to make up for your long years away

[035-029]from me at college. We’ll talk of what’s best to do in the morning,

[035-030]shan’t we? And for all this pain you’ll forgive your loving and

[035-031]devoted mother.

[035-032]Isabel.

Chapter XXVII

[035-033]Having finished some errands downtown, the next afternoon, George

[035-034]Amberson Minafer was walking up [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] on his homeward way

[035-035]when he saw in the distance, coming toward him, upon the same side

[035-036]of the street, the figure of a young lady—a figure just under the

[035-037]middle height, comely indeed, and to be mistaken for none other in the

[035-038]world—even at two hundred yards. To his sharp discomfiture his heart

[035-039]immediately forced upon him the consciousness of its acceleration; a

[035-040]sudden warmth about his neck made him aware that he had turned red,

[035-041]and then, departing, left him pale. For a panicky moment he thought

[035-042]of facing about in actual flight; he had little doubt that [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] would

[035-043]meet him with no token of recognition, and all at once this probability

[035-044]struck him as unendurable. And if she did not speak, was it the

[035-045]proper part of chivalry to lift his hat and take the cut bareheaded?

[035-046]Or should the finer gentleman acquiesce in the lady’s desire for no

[035-047]further acquaintance, and pass her with stony mien and eyes constrained

[035-048]forward? George was a young man badly flustered.

[035-049]But the girl approaching him was unaware of his trepidation, being

[035-050]perhaps somewhat preoccupied with her own. She saw only that he was

[035-051]pale, and that his eyes were darkly circled. But here he was advantaged

[035-052]with her, for the finest touch to his good looks was given by this

[035-053]toning down; neither pallor nor dark circles detracting from them, but

[035-054]rather adding to them a melancholy favour of distinction. George had

[035-055]retained his mourning, a tribute completed down to the final details of

[035-056]black gloves and a polished ebony cane (which he would have been pained

[035-057]to name otherwise than as a “walking-stick”) and in the aura of this

[035-058]sombre elegance his straight figure and drawn face were not without a

[035-059]tristful and appealing dignity.

[035-060]In everything outward he was cause enough for a girl’s cheek to flush,

[035-061]her heart to beat faster, and her eyes to warm with the soft light that

[035-062]came into Lucy’s now, whether she would or no. If his spirit had been

[035-063]what his looks proclaimed it, she would have rejoiced to let the light

[035-064]glow forth which now shone in spite of her. For a long time, thinking

[035-065]of that spirit of his, and what she felt it should be, she had a

[035-066]persistent sense: “It must be there!” but she had determined to believe

[035-067]this folly no longer. Nevertheless, when she met him at the Sharons’,

[035-068]she had been far less calm than she seemed.

[035-069]People speaking casually of Lucy were apt to define her as “a little

[035-070]beauty,” a definition short of the mark. She was “a little beauty,”

[035-071]but an independent, masterful, sell-reliant little American, of whom

[035-072]her father’s earlier gipsyings and her own sturdiness had made a woman

[035-073]ever since she was fifteen. But though she was the mistress of her own

[035-074]ways and no slave to any lamp save that of her own conscience, she

[035-075]had a weakness: she had fallen in love with [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]

[035-076]at first sight, and no matter how she disciplined herself, she had

[035-077]never been able to climb out. The thing had happened to her; that was

[035-078]all. George had looked just the way she had always wanted someone to

[035-079]look—the riskiest of all the moonshine ambushes wherein tricky romance

[035-080]snares credulous young love. But what was fatal to Lucy was that this

[035-081]thing having happened to her, she could not change it. No matter what

[035-082]she discovered in George’s nature she was unable to take away what she

[035-083]had given him; and though she could think differently about him, she

[035-084]could not feel differently about him, for she was one of those too

[035-085]faithful victims of glamour. When she managed to keep the picture of

[035-086]George away from her mind’s eye, she did well enough; but when she let

[035-087]him become visible, she could not choose but love what she disdained.

[035-088]She was a little angel who had fallen in love with high-handed Lucifer;

[035-089]quite an experience, and not apt to be soon succeeded by any falling in

[035-090]love with a tamer party—and the unhappy truth was that George did make

[035-091]better men seem tame. But though she was a victim, she was a heroic

[035-092]one, anything but helpless.

[035-093]As they drew nearer, George tried to prepare himself to meet her with

[035-094]some remnants of aplomb. He decided that he would keep on looking

[035-095]straight ahead, and lift his hand toward his hat at the very last

[035-096]moment when it would be possible for her to see him out of the corner

[035-097]of her eye: then when she thought it over later, she would not be sure

[035-098]whether he had saluted her or merely rubbed his forehead. And there

[035-099]was the added benefit that any third person who might chance to look

[035-100]from a window, or from a passing carriage, would not think that he

[035-101]was receiving a snub, because he did not intend to lift his hat, but,

[035-102]timing the gesture properly, would in fact actually rub his forehead.

[035-103]These were the hasty plans which occupied his thoughts until he was

[035-104]within about fifty feet of her—when he ceased to have either plans or

[035-105]thoughts. He had kept his eyes from looking full at her until then, and

[035-106]as he saw her, thus close at hand, and coming nearer, a regret that was

[035-107]dumfounding took possession of him. For the first time he had the sense

[035-108]of having lost something of overwhelming importance.

[035-109]Lucy did not keep to the right, but came straight to meet him, smiling,

[035-110]and with her hand offered to him.

[035-111]“Why—you—” he stammered, as he took it. “Haven’t you—” What he meant to

[035-112]say was, “Haven’t you heard?”

[035-113]“Haven’t I what?” she asked; and he saw that Eugene had not yet told

[035-114]her.

[035-115]“Nothing!” he gasped. “May I—may I turn and walk with you a little way?”

[035-116]“Yes, indeed!” she said cordially.

[035-117]He would not have altered what had been done: he was satisfied with all

[035-118]that—satisfied that it was right, and that his own course was right.

[035-119]But he began to perceive a striking inaccuracy in some remarks he had

[035-120]made to his mother. Now when he had put matters in such shape that

[035-121]even by the relinquishment of his “ideals of life” he could not have

[035-122]Lucy, knew that he could never have her, and knew that when Eugene

[035-123]told her the history of yesterday he could not have a glance or word

[035-124]even friendly from her—now when he must in good truth “give up all

[035-125]idea of Lucy,” he was amazed that he could have used such words as “no

[035-126]particular sacrifice,” and believed them when he said them! She had

[035-127]looked never in his life so bewitchingly pretty as she did today; and

[035-128]as he walked beside her he was sure that she was the most exquisite

[035-129]thing in the world.

[035-130]“Lucy,” he said huskily, “I want to tell you something. Something that

[035-131]matters.”

[035-132]“I hope it’s a lively something then,” she said; and laughed. “Papa’s

[035-133]been so glum to-day he’s scarcely spoken to me. Your [[george-amberson|Uncle George]]

[035-134]Amberson came to see him an hour ago and they shut themselves up in the

[035-135]library, and your uncle looked as glum as papa. I’d be glad if you’ll

[035-136]tell me a funny story, George.”

[035-137]“Well, it may seem one to you,” he said bitterly. “Just to begin with:

[035-138]when you went away you didn’t let me know; not even a word—not a line—”

[035-139]Her manner persisted in being inconsequent. “Why, no,” she said. “I

[035-140]just trotted off for some visits.”

[035-141]“Well, at least you might have—”

[035-142]“Why, no,” she said again briskly. “Don’t you remember, George? We’d

[035-143]had a grand quarrel, and didn’t speak to each other all the way home

[035-144]from a long, long drive! So, as we couldn’t play together like good

[035-145]children, of course it was plain that we oughtn’t to play at all.”

[035-146]“Play!” he cried.

[035-147]“Yes. What I mean is that we’d come to the point where it was time to

[035-148]quit playing—well, what we were playing.”

[035-149]“At being lovers, you mean, don’t you?”

[035-150]“Something like that,” she said lightly. “For us two, playing at being

[035-151]lovers was just the same as playing at cross-purposes. I had all the

[035-152]purposes, and that gave you all the crossness: things weren’t getting

[035-153]along at all. It was absurd!”

[035-154]“Well, have it your own way,” he said. “It needn’t have been absurd.”

[035-155]“No, it couldn’t help but be!” she informed him cheerfully. “The way I

[035-156]am and the way you are, it couldn’t ever be anything else. So what was

[035-157]the use?”

[035-158]“I don’t know,” he sighed, and his sigh was abysmal. “But what I wanted

[035-159]to tell you is this: when you went away, you didn’t let me know and

[035-160]didn’t care how or when I heard it, but I’m not like that with you.

[035-161]This time, I’m going away. That’s what I wanted to tell you. I’m going

[035-162]away tomorrow night—indefinitely.”

[035-163]She nodded sunnily. “That’s nice for you. I hope you’ll have ever so

[035-164]jolly a time, George.”

[035-165]“I don’t expect to have a particularly jolly time.”

[035-166]“Well, then,” she laughed, “if I were you I don’t think I’d go.”

[035-167]It seemed impossible to impress this distracting creature, to make her

[035-168]serious. “Lucy,” he said desperately, “this is our last walk together.”

[035-169]“Evidently!” she said. “If you’re going away tomorrow night.”

[035-170]“Lucy—this may be the last time I’ll see you—ever—ever in my life.”

[035-171]At that she looked at him quickly, across her shoulder, but she smiled

[035-172]as brightly as before, and with the same cordial inconsequence: “Oh, I

[035-173]can hardly think that!” she said. “And of course I’d be awfully sorry

[035-174]to think it. You’re not moving away, are you, to live?”
