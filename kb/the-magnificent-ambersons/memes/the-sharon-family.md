# The Sharon Family

Janie Sharon and Mary Sharon, invariably referred to in the novel as "the Sharon girls," are cousins of [[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]] and relatives of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene Morgan]]. As members of an established Midland family, they occupy a distinctive position in the novel's social architecture.

They are the bridge through which the outsider Morgans gain entry to the town's inner circle. They host the dances and gatherings at which the novel's central relationships develop.

Though never explored as individuals in depth, the Sharon sisters appear at crucial moments throughout the narrative. Their presence threads the story from the first [[the-last-ball|Amberson ball]] through to the epilogue.

## Role in the Novel

The Sharon family serves a dual structural function in *The Magnificent Ambersons*. Socially, they are the facilitators: it is through them that [[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]] enters Midland society, and it is at their house on National Avenue that many of the novel's pivotal social encounters occur.

Their home is the setting for the dance where Fanny Minafer observes the developing relationship between [[george-amberson-minafer|George]] and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] (013-133), for George's disastrously awkward reunion with Lucy after her return from abroad (027-075), and for the childhood parties where George first knew Mary Sharon (041-178).

Narratively, the Sharons function as witnesses and sometimes unwitting agents of George Amberson Minafer's humiliation. When George, attempting to project "icy indifference" toward [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], instead dissolves into stammering incoherence at their house, Janie and Mary Sharon are the Greek chorus of his embarrassment.

They lean forward, “staring at him with strained eyes, their lips tightly compressed” (027-100). Their eventual breakdown into laughter — “the outburst of wild, irrepressible emotion which his performance had inspired” (027-119) — marks one of the novel’s most vivid comic humiliations, and one that George, in his Amberson pride, can neither forgive nor forget.

The Sharons also represent the changing face of Midland itself. By the time of Eugene Morgan's return to the town, they have sold their house on National Avenue and are building in the country. As [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] tells Major Amberson:

> "My relatives, the Sharons, have sold their house and are building in the country — at least, they call it 'the country.' It will be city in two or three years." (026-012)

This detail aligns the Sharons with the outward movement and transformation that defines the new Midland. Their departure from National Avenue anticipates the end of the old residential order, a world in which the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]] is becoming an anomaly amidst boarding-houses and commercial encroachment.

## Janie Sharon

Janie Sharon is the more prominent of the two sisters in the novel's action. At the Assembly cotillion, she is one of the two girls seen "wandering, not finding a predetermined object in sight" — the other being her cousin [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] (013-049).

Her role as confidante is established at the Sharons' house, where "Janie Sharon had just begun to tell him that she heard Lucy was expected home soon, after having 'a perfectly gorgeous time'" (027-076). She is the conveyor of social intelligence — the person through whom George learns of [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]'s movements.

Janie's romantic destiny links the Sharon family to the Kinney family. After repeatedly refusing Fred Kinney's proposals, [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] is told that Kinney "got engaged to Janie Sharon just to prove that someone would have him in spite of his hair" (038-028).

By the novel's final chapters, the marriage is a settled fact: the "red-haired Kinney, now married to Janie Sharon" (042-071) is among the few figures from George's past who remain within his diminished social cognizance.

## Mary Sharon

Mary Sharon figures primarily in the novel's backward glances. She appears most notably as the object of George Amberson Minafer's earliest romantic aggression.

In a memory triggered by the decaying house that once belonged to the Sharons, the narrative reveals that George, as a boy, "had danced there often, and fallen in love with Mary Sharon, and kissed her, apparently by force, under the stairs in the hall" (041-178).

This childhood episode — a miniature of the coercive entitlement that will define George's adult relationships — is rendered with characteristic Tarkington irony. The boy who would grow up to be George Amberson Minafer was already, in the Sharon house, exercising the Amberson prerogative of taking what he wanted.

In the novel's present action, Mary Sharon appears alongside her sister as a participant in the social life of Midland's younger set. She is present at the Sharons' house for George's mortifying reunion with [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], and she joins Janie in the barely-suppressed laughter that sends George fleeing into the night.

> "George Amberson Minafer to look a figure of fun. And while he stood there, undeniably such a figure, with Janie and Mary Sharon threatening to burst at any moment, if laughter were longer denied them, Lucy sat looking at him with her eyebrows delicately lifted in casual, polite inquiry." (027-112)

## The Sharon House

The Sharon residence on National Avenue serves as a significant setting in the novel. It is the site of a dance where [[george-amberson-minafer|George]] and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]'s relationship develops under the observation of Fanny Minafer (013-133).

It is to the Sharons' house that George goes "in the secret hope that he might hear something about her" after [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]'s return (027-075). And it is from this house that George flees in humiliation, having made himself ridiculous before the three young women who should have been his audience for a display of indifference.

By the later chapters, however, the Sharon house has been sold, its fate emblematic of the decline that overtakes National Avenue (026-012). The Sharons, unlike the Ambersons, adapt — they move outward, anticipating the suburban expansion that will transform the Midland town.
