## Summary

In the novel's climactic event, [[George Amberson Minafer]] — now reduced from Amberson heir to "an employee of the Akers Chemical Co." — is struck by an automobile at the corner of Tennessee and Main. The newspaper reports the incident with brutal concision:

> G. A. Minafer, an employee of the Akers Chemical Co., was run down by an automobile yesterday at the corner of Tennessee and Main and had both legs broken. Minafer was to blame for the accident according to patrolman F. A. Kax, who witnessed the affair. The automobile was a small one driven by Herbert Cottleman of 9173 Noble Avenue who stated that he was making less than 4 miles an hour. (047-144)

The newspaper adds a devastating coda: "Minafer is said to belong to a family formerly of considerable prominence in the city" (047-146). The word "formerly" encapsulates the entire arc of [[The Decline of the Ambersons|Amberson decline]].

George is taken to the City Hospital, suffering from "internal injuries besides the fracture of his legs" (047-148). The accident is at once a literal physical catastrophe and the symbolic fulfillment of the novel's central theme: [[The Automobile in The Magnificent Ambersons|the automobile age]] George scorned has literally crushed him.

## Context

The accident occurs after the complete collapse of the Amberson fortune. The [[Amberson Mansion]] has been sold, the family [[Wealth and Fortune|wealth]] dissipated through bad investments and neglect. George, who once declared "I guess my grandpa owns it now, you bet!" (003-089), now works as a low-level employee. His walk home along the streets he once commanded as a princely figure is interrupted by the very technology he dismissed as a "useless nuisance."

The accident is witnessed by patrolman F. A. Kax, who assigns blame to George — a detail that strips the former Amberson heir of any special status. In the democratic world of the automobile age, a pedestrian is a pedestrian, regardless of family name.

## Consequences

The accident serves as the instrument of the novel's final moral reckoning. [[Eugene Morgan]] reads the newspaper account and, despite years of ill-treatment from George, feels "human pity for Georgie" (047-148). Morgan's response — compassion toward the man who destroyed his happiness with [[Isabel Amberson]] — provides the novel's clearest statement of its moral values.

The accident also literalizes the novel's theme of technological retribution. George, who spent his life opposing everything the automobile represented, is physically broken by one. The "less than 4 miles an hour" detail is painfully ironic: the force that destroys him is not even operating at full speed — it is simply an ordinary manifestation of the new world passing through the old. This connects the event thematically to [[Morgan's Automobile Factory]] and the industrial transformation of Midland.
