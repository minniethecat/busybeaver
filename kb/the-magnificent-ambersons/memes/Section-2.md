[002-001]“Keeping Open House” was a merry custom; it has gone, like the all-day

[002-002]picnic in the woods, and like that prettiest of all vanished customs,

[002-003]the serenade. When a lively girl visited the town she did not long

[002-004]go unserenaded, though a visitor was not indeed needed to excuse a

[002-005]serenade. Of a summer night, young men would bring an orchestra under

[002-006]a pretty girl’s window—or, it might be, her father’s, or that of an

[002-007]ailing maiden aunt—and flute, harp, fiddle, cello, cornet, and bass

[002-008]viol would presently release to the dulcet stars such melodies as

[002-009]sing through “You’ll Remember Me,” “I Dreamt That I Dwelt in Marble

[002-010]Halls,” “Silver Threads Among the Gold,” “Kathleen Mavourneen,” or “The

[002-011]Soldier’s Farewell.”

[002-012]They had other music to offer, too, for these were the happy days

[002-013]of “Olivette” and “The Macotte” and “The Chimes of Normandy” and

[002-014]“Girofle-Girofla” and “Fra Diavola.” Better than that, these were the

[002-015]days of “Pinafore” and “The Pirates of Penzance” and of “Patience.”

[002-016]This last was needed in the [[downtown-midland|Midland town]], as elsewhere, for the

[002-017]“aesthetic movement” had reached thus far from London, and terrible

[002-018]things were being done to honest old furniture. Maidens sawed

[002-019]what-nots in two, and gilded the remains. They took the rockers from

[002-020]rocking-chairs and gilded the inadequate legs; they gilded the easels

[002-021]that supported the crayon portraits of their deceased uncles. In the

[002-022]new spirit of art they sold old clocks for new, and threw wax flowers

[002-023]and wax fruit, and the protecting glass domes, out upon the trash-heap.

[002-024]They filled vases with peacock feathers, or cattails, or sumac, or

[002-025]sunflowers, and set the vases upon mantelpieces and marble-topped

[002-026]tables. They embroidered daisies (which they called “marguerites”) and

[002-027]sunflowers and sumac and cat-tails and owls and peacock feathers upon

[002-028]plush screens and upon heavy cushions, then strewed these cushions upon

[002-029]floors where fathers fell over them in the dark. In the teeth of sinful

[002-030]oratory, the daughters went on embroidering: they embroidered daisies

[002-031]and sunflowers and sumac and cat-tails and owls and peacock feathers

[002-032]upon “throws” which they had the courage to drape upon horsehair sofas;

[002-033]they painted owls and daisies and sunflowers and sumac and cat-tails

[002-034]and peacock feathers upon tambourines. They hung Chinese umbrellas of

[002-035]paper to the chandeliers; they nailed paper fans to the walls. They

[002-036]“studied” painting on china, these girls; they sang Tosti’s new songs;

[002-037]they sometimes still practiced the old, genteel habit of lady-fainting,

[002-038]and were most charming of all when they drove forth, three or four in a

[002-039]basket phaeton, on a spring morning.

[002-040]Croquet and the mildest archery ever known were the sports of people

[002-041]still young and active enough for so much exertion; middle-age played

[002-042]euchre. There was a theatre, next door to [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Hotel, and when

[002-043]Edwin Booth came for a night, everybody who could afford to buy a

[002-044]ticket was there, and all the “hacks” in town were hired. “The Black

[002-045]Crook” also filled the theatre, but the audience then was almost

[002-046]entirely of men who looked uneasy as they left for home when the final

[002-047]curtain fell upon the shocking girls dressed as fairies. But the

[002-048]theatre did not often do so well; the people of the town were still too

[002-049]thrifty.

[002-050]They were thrifty because they were the sons or grandsons of the “early

[002-051]settlers,” who had opened the wilderness and had reached it from the

[002-052]East and the South with wagons and axes and guns, but with no [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] at

[002-053]all. The pioneers were thrifty or they would have perished: they had to

[002-054]store away food for the winter, or goods to trade for food, and they

[002-055]often feared they had not stored enough—they left traces of that fear

[002-056]in their sons and grandsons. In the minds of most of these, indeed,

[002-057]their thrift was next to their religion: to save, even for the sake

[002-058]of saving, was their earliest lesson and discipline. No matter how

[002-059]prosperous they were, they could not spend money either upon “art,” or

[002-060]upon mere luxury and entertainment, without a sense of sin.

[002-061]Against so homespun a background the magnificence of the Ambersons was

[002-062]as conspicuous as a brass band at a funeral. [[major-amberson|Major Amberson]] bought

[002-063]two hundred acres of land at the end of [[national-avenue|National Avenue]]; and through

[002-064]this tract he built broad streets and cross-streets; paved them with

[002-065]cedar block, and curbed them with stone. He set up fountains, here and

[002-066]there, where the streets intersected, and at symmetrical intervals

[002-067]placed cast-iron statues, painted white, with their titles clear upon

[002-068]the pedestals: Minerva, Mercury, Hercules, Venus, Gladiator, Emperor

[002-069]Augustus, Fisher Boy, Stag-hound, Mastiff, Greyhound, Fawn, Antelope,

[002-070]Wounded Doe, and Wounded Lion. Most of the forest trees had been left

[002-071]to flourish still, and, at some distance, or by moonlight, the place

[002-072]was in truth beautiful; but the ardent citizen, loving to see his

[002-073]city grow, wanted neither distance nor moonlight. He had not seen

[002-074]Versailles, but, standing before the Fountain of Neptune in Amberson

[002-075]Addition, at bright noon, and quoting the favourite comparison of the

[002-076]local newspapers, he declared Versailles outdone. All this Art showed a

[002-077]profit from the start, for the lots sold well and there was something

[002-078]like a rush to build in the new Addition. Its main thoroughfare, an

[002-079]oblique continuation of National Avenue, was called [[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]],

[002-080]and here, at the juncture of the new Boulevard and the Avenue, Major

[002-081]Amberson reserved four acres for himself, and built his new house—the

[002-082][[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]], of course.

[002-083]This house was the pride of the town. Faced with stone as far back

[002-084]as the dining-room windows, it was a house of arches and turrets and

[002-085]girdling stone porches: it had the first porte-cochere seen in that

[002-086]town. There was a central “front hall” with a great black walnut

[002-087]stairway, and open to a green glass skylight called the “dome,” three

[002-088]stories above the ground floor. A ballroom occupied most of the third

[002-089]story; and at one end of it was a carved walnut gallery for the

[002-090]musicians. Citizens told strangers that the cost of all this black

[002-091]walnut and wood-carving was sixty thousand dollars. “Sixty thousand

[002-092]dollars for the wood-work _alone!_ Yes, sir, and hardwood floors all

[002-093]over the house! Turkish rugs and no carpets at all, except a Brussels

[002-094]carpet in the front parlour—I hear they call it the ‘reception-room.’

[002-095]Hot and cold water upstairs and down, and stationary washstands in

[002-096]every last bedroom in the place! Their sideboard’s built right into the

[002-097]house and goes all the way across one end of the dining room. It isn’t

[002-098]walnut, it’s solid mahogany! Not veneering—solid mahogany! Well, sir, I

[002-099]presume the President of the United States would be tickled to swap the

[002-100]White House for the new Amberson Mansion, if the Major’d give him the

[002-101]chance—but by the Almighty Dollar, you bet your sweet life the Major

[002-102]wouldn’t!”

[002-103]The visitor to the town was certain to receive further enlightenment,

[002-104]for there was one form of entertainment never omitted: he was always

[002-105]patriotically taken for “a little drive around our city,” even if

[002-106]his host had to hire a hack, and the climax of the display was the

[002-107]Amberson Mansion. “Look at that greenhouse they’ve put up there in

[002-108]the side yard,” the escort would continue. “And look at that brick

[002-109]stable! Most folks would think that stable plenty big enough and good

[002-110]enough to live in; it’s got running water and four rooms upstairs for

[002-111]two hired men and one of ’em’s family to live in. They keep one hired

[002-112]man loafin’ in the house, and they got a married hired man out in the

[002-113]stable, and his wife does the washing. They got box-stalls for four

[002-114]horses, and they keep a coupay, and some new kinds of fancy rigs you

[002-115]never saw the beat of! ‘Carts’ they call two of ’em—‘way up in the air

[002-116]they are—too high for me! I guess they got every new kind of fancy rig

[002-117]in there that’s been invented. And harness—well, everybody in town can

[002-118]tell when Ambersons are out driving after dark, by the jingle. This

[002-119]town never did see so much style as Ambersons are putting on, these

[002-120]days; and I guess it’s going to be expensive, because a lot of other

[002-121]folks’ll try to keep up with ’em. The Major’s wife and the daughter’s

[002-122]been to Europe, and my wife tells me since they got back they make tea

[002-123]there every afternoon about five o’clock, and drink it. Seems to me it

[002-124]would go against a person’s stomach, just before supper like that, and

[002-125]anyway tea isn’t fit for much—not unless you’re sick or something. My

[002-126]wife says Ambersons don’t make lettuce salad the way other people do;

[002-127]they don’t chop it up with sugar and vinegar at all. They pour olive

[002-128]oil on it with their vinegar, and they have it separate—not along with

[002-129]the rest of the meal. And they _eat_ these olives, too: green things

[002-130]they are, something like a hard plum, but a friend of mine told me they

[002-131]tasted a good deal like a bad hickory-nut. My wife says she’s going to

[002-132]buy some; you got to eat nine and then you get to like ’em, she says.

[002-133]Well, I wouldn’t eat nine bad hickory-nuts to get to like _them_, and

[002-134]I’m going to let these olives alone. Kind of a woman’s dish, anyway,

[002-135]I suspect, but most everybody’ll be makin’ a stagger to worm through

[002-136]nine of ’em, now Ambersons brought ’em to town. Yes, sir, the rest’ll

[002-137]eat ’em, whether they get sick or not! Looks to me like some people

[002-138]in this city’d be willing to go crazy if they thought that would help

[002-139]’em to be as high-toned as Ambersons. Old Aleck Minafer—he’s about the

[002-140]closest old codger we got—he come in my office the other day, and he

[002-141]pretty near had a stroke tellin’ me about his daughter [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]]. Seems

[002-142][[isabel-amberson|Miss Isabel Amberson]]’s got some kind of a dog—they call it a Saint

[002-143]Bernard—and Fanny was bound to have one, too. Well, old Aleck told her

[002-144]he didn’t like dogs except rat-terriers, because a rat-terrier cleans

[002-145]up the mice, but she kept on at him, and finally he said all right she

[002-146]could have one. Then, by George! she says Ambersons _bought_ their

[002-147]dog, and you can’t get one without paying for it: they cost from fifty

[002-148]to a hundred dollars up! Old Aleck wanted to know if I ever heard of

[002-149]anybody buyin’ a dog before, because, of course, even a Newfoundland or

[002-150]a setter you can usually get somebody to give you one. He says he saw

[002-151]some sense in payin’ a nigger a dime, or even a quarter, to _drown_ a

[002-152]dog for you, but to pay out fifty dollars and maybe more—well, sir, he

[002-153]like to choked himself to death, right there in my office! Of course

[002-154]everybody realizes that Major Amberson is a fine business man, but what

[002-155]with throwin’ money around for dogs, and every which and what, some

[002-156]think all this style’s bound to break him up, if his family don’t quit!”

[002-157]One citizen, having thus discoursed to a visitor, came to a thoughtful

[002-158]pause, and then added, “Does seem pretty much like squandering, yet

[002-159]when you see that dog out walking with this Miss Isabel, he seems worth

[002-160]the money.”

[002-161]“What’s she look like?”

[002-162]“Well, sir,” said the citizen, “she’s not more than just about eighteen

[002-163]or maybe nineteen years old, and I don’t know as I know just how to put

[002-164]it—but she’s kind of a _delightful_ lookin’ young lady!”

Chapter II

[002-165]Another citizen said an eloquent thing about Miss Isabel Amberson’s

[002-166]looks. This was Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster, the foremost literary

[002-167]authority and intellectual leader of the community—-for both the daily

[002-168]newspapers thus described Mrs. Foster when she founded the Women’s

[002-169]Tennyson Club; and her word upon art, letters, and the drama was

[002-170]accepted more as law than as opinion. Naturally, when “Hazel Kirke”

[002-171]finally reached the town, after its long triumph in larger places,

[002-172]many people waited to hear what Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster thought of

[002-173]it before they felt warranted in expressing any estimate of the play.

[002-174]In fact, some of them waited in the lobby of the theatre, as they came

[002-175]out, and formed an inquiring group about her.
