[008-001]She laughed again. “I don’t think I’ve often had so large a compliment

[008-002]as that,” she said, “especially on such short notice—and yet, I don’t

[008-003]think I’ll go with you.

[008-004]“You be ready at ten minutes after two.”

[008-005]“No, I won’t.”

[008-006]“Yes, you will!”

[008-007]“Yes,” she said, “I will!” And her partner for the next dance arrived,

[008-008]breathless with searching.

[008-009]“Don’t forget I’ve got the third from now,” George called after her.

[008-010]“I won’t.”

[008-011]“And every third one after that.”

[008-012]“I know!” she called, over her partner’s shoulder, and her voice was

[008-013]amused—but meek.

[008-014]When “the third from now” came, George presented himself before her

[008-015]without any greeting, like a brother, or a mannerless old friend.

[008-016]Neither did she greet him, but moved away with him, concluding, as

[008-017]she went, an exchange of badinage with the preceding partner: she had

[008-018]been talkative enough with him, it appeared. In fact, both George

[008-019]and Miss Morgan talked much more to every one else that evening,

[008-020]than to each other; and they said nothing at all at this time. Both

[008-021]looked preoccupied, as they began to dance, and preserved a gravity

[008-022]of expression to the end of the number. And when “the third one

[008-023]after that” came, they did not dance, but went back to the gallery

[008-024]stairway, seeming to have reached an understanding without any verbal

[008-025]consultation, that this suburb was again the place for them.

[008-026]“Well,” said George, coolly, when they were seated, “what did you say

[008-027]your name was?”

[008-028]“Morgan.”

[008-029]“Funny name!”

[008-030]“Everybody else’s name always is.”

[008-031]“I didn’t mean it was really funny,” George explained. “That’s just one

[008-032]of my crowd’s bits of horsing at college. We always say ‘funny name’

[008-033]no matter what it is. I guess we’re pretty fresh sometimes; but I knew

[008-034]your name was Morgan because my mother said so downstairs. I meant:

[008-035]what’s the rest of it?”

[008-036]“[[lucy-morgan|Lucy]].”

[008-037]He was silent.

[008-038]“Is ‘Lucy’ a funny name, too?” she inquired.

[008-039]“No. Lucy’s very much all right!” he said, and he went so far as to

[008-040]smile. Even his [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]] admitted that when George smiled “in a

[008-041]certain way” he was charming.

[008-042]“Thanks about letting my name be Lucy,” she said.

[008-043]“How old are you?” George asked.

[008-044]“I don’t really know, myself.”

[008-045]“What do you mean: you don’t really know yourself?”

[008-046]“I mean I only know what they tell me. I believe them, of course, but

[008-047]believing isn’t really knowing. You believe some certain day is your

[008-048]birthday—at least, I suppose you do—but you don’t really know it is

[008-049]because you can’t remember.”

[008-050]“Look here!” said George. “Do you always talk like this?”

[008-051]Miss Lucy Morgan laughed forgivingly, put her young head on one side,

[008-052]like a bird, and responded cheerfully: “I’m willing to learn wisdom.

[008-053]What are you studying in school?”

[008-054]“College!”

[008-055]“At the university! Yes. What are you studying there?”

[008-056]George laughed. “Lot o’ useless guff!”

[008-057]“Then why don’t you study some useful guff?”

[008-058]“What do you mean: ‘useful’?”

[008-059]“Something you’d use later, in your business or profession?”

[008-060]George waved his hand impatiently. “I don’t expect to go into any

[008-061]‘business or profession’.”

[008-062]“No?”

[008-063]“Certainly not!” George was emphatic, being sincerely annoyed by a

[008-064]suggestion which showed how utterly she failed to comprehend the kind

[008-065]of person he was.

[008-066]“Why not?” she asked mildly.

[008-067]“Just look at ’em!” he said, almost with bitterness, and he made a

[008-068]gesture presumably intended to indicate the business and professional

[008-069]men now dancing within range of vision. “That’s a fine career for a

[008-070]man, isn’t it! Lawyers, bankers, politicians! What do they get out of

[008-071]life, I’d like to know! What do they ever know about _real_ things?

[008-072]Where do they ever _get?_”

[008-073]He was so earnest that she was surprised and impressed. Evidently he

[008-074]had deep-seated ambitions, for he seemed to speak with actual emotion

[008-075]of these despised things which were so far beneath his planning for the

[008-076]future. She had a vague, momentary vision of Pitt, at twenty-one, prime

[008-077]minister of England; and she spoke, involuntarily in a lowered voice,

[008-078]with deference:

[008-079]“What do you want to be?” she asked.

[008-080]George answered promptly.

[008-081]“A yachtsman,” he said.

Chapter VI

[008-082]Having thus, in a word, revealed his ambition for a career above

[008-083]courts, marts, and polling booths, George breathed more deeply than

[008-084]usual, and, turning his face from the lovely companion whom he had

[008-085]just made his confidant, gazed out at the dancers with an expression

[008-086]in which there was both sternness and a contempt for the squalid lives

[008-087]of the unyachted Midlanders before him. However, among them, he marked

[008-088]his mother; and his sombre grandeur relaxed momentarily; a more genial

[008-089]light came into his eyes.

[008-090]Isabel was dancing with the queer-looking duck; and it was to be noted

[008-091]that the lively gentleman’s gait was more sedate than it had been with

[008-092]Miss Fanny Minafer, but not less dexterous and authoritative. He was

[008-093]talking to Isabel as gaily as he had talked to Miss Fanny, though with

[008-094]less laughter, and Isabel listened and answered eagerly: her colour

[008-095]was high and her eyes had a look of delight. She saw George and the

[008-096]beautiful Lucy on the stairway, and nodded to them. George waved his

[008-097]hand vaguely: he had a momentary return of that inexplicable uneasiness

[008-098]and resentment which had troubled him downstairs.

[008-099]“How lovely your mother is!” Lucy said

[008-100]“I think she is,” he agreed gently.

[008-101]“She’s the gracefulest woman in that ballroom. She dances like a girl

[008-102]of sixteen.”

[008-103]“Most girls of sixteen,” said George, “are bum dancers. Anyhow, I

[008-104]wouldn’t dance with one unless I had to.”

[008-105]“Well, you’d better dance with your mother! I never saw anybody

[008-106]lovelier. How wonderfully they dance together!”

[008-107]“Who?”

[008-108]“Your mother and—and the queer-looking duck,” said Lucy. “I’m going to

[008-109]dance with him pretty soon.”

[008-110]“I don’t care—so long as you don’t give him one of the numbers that

[008-111]belong to me.”

[008-112]“I’ll try to remember,” she said, and thoughtfully lifted to her face

[008-113]the bouquet of violets and lilies, a gesture which George noted without

[008-114]approval.

[008-115]“Look here! Who sent you those flowers you keep makin’ such a fuss

[008-116]over?”

[008-117]“He did.”

[008-118]“Who’s ‘he’?”

[008-119]“The queer-looking duck.”

[008-120]George feared no such rival; he laughed loudly. “I s’pose he’s some old

[008-121]widower!” he said, the object thus described seeming ignominious enough

[008-122]to a person of eighteen, without additional characterization. “Some old

[008-123]widower!”

[008-124]Lucy became serious at once. “Yes, he is a widower,” she said. “I ought

[008-125]to have told you before; he’s my father.”

[008-126]George stopped laughing abruptly. “Well, that’s a horse on me. If I’d

[008-127]known he was your father, of course I wouldn’t have made fun of him.

[008-128]I’m sorry.”

[008-129]“Nobody could make fun of him,” she said quietly.

[008-130]“Why couldn’t they?”

[008-131]“It wouldn’t make him funny: it would only make themselves silly.”

[008-132]Upon this, George had a gleam of intelligence. “Well, I’m not going to

[008-133]make myself silly any more, then; I don’t want to take chances like

[008-134]that with _you_. But I thought he was the Sharon girls’ uncle. He came

[008-135]with them—”

[008-136]“Yes,” she said, “I’m always late to everything: I wouldn’t let them

[008-137]wait for me. We’re visiting the Sharons.”

[008-138]“[[About]] time I knew that! You forget my being so fresh about your

[008-139]father, will you? Of course he’s a distinguished looking man, in a way.”

[008-140]Lucy was still serious. “‘In a way?’” she repeated. “You mean, not in

[008-141]your way, don’t you?”

[008-142]George was perplexed. “How do you mean: not in my way?”

[008-143]“People pretty often say ‘in a way’ and ‘rather distinguished looking,’

[008-144]or ‘rather’ so-and-so, or ‘rather’ anything, to show that _they’re_

[008-145]superior, don’t they? In New York last month I overheard a climber sort

[008-146]of woman speaking of me as ‘little Miss Morgan,’ but she didn’t mean

[008-147]my height; she meant that she was important. Her husband spoke of a

[008-148]friend of mine as ‘little Mr. Pembroke’ and ‘little Mr. Pembroke’

[008-149]is six-feet-three. This husband and wife were really so terribly

[008-150]unimportant that the only way they knew to pretend to be important was

[008-151]calling people ‘little’ Miss or Mister so-and-so. It’s a kind of snob

[008-152]slang, I think. Of course people don’t always say ‘rather’ or ‘in a

[008-153]way’ to be superior.”

[008-154]“I should say not! I use both of ’em a great deal myself,” said

[008-155]George. “One thing I don’t see though: What’s the use of a man being

[008-156]six-feet-three? Men that size can’t handle themselves as well as a

[008-157]man about five-feet-eleven and a half can. Those long, gangling men,

[008-158]they’re nearly always too kind of wormy to be any good in athletics,

[008-159]and they’re so awkward they keep falling over chairs or—”

[008-160]“Mr. Pembroke is in the army,” said Lucy primly. “He’s extraordinarily

[008-161]graceful.”

[008-162]“In the army? Oh, I suppose he’s some old friend of your father’s.”

[008-163]“They got on very well,” she said, “after I introduced them.”

[008-164]George was a straightforward soul, at least. “See here!” he said. “Are

[008-165]you engaged to anybody?”

[008-166]“No.”

[008-167]Not wholly mollified, he shrugged his shoulders. “You seem to know a

[008-168]good many people! Do you live in New York?”

[008-169]“No. We don’t live anywhere.”

[008-170]“What you mean: you don’t live anywhere?”

[008-171]“We’ve lived all over,” she answered. “Papa used to live here in this

[008-172]town, but that was before I was born.”

[008-173]“What do you keep moving around so for? Is he a promoter?”

[008-174]“No. He’s an inventor.”

[008-175]“What’s he invented?”

[008-176]“Just lately,” said Lucy, “he’s been working on a new kind of horseless

[008-177]carriage.”

[008-178]“Well, I’m sorry for him,” George said, in no unkindly spirit. “Those

[008-179]things are never going to amount to anything. People aren’t going to

[008-180]spend their lives lying on their backs in the road and letting grease

[008-181]drip in their faces. Horseless carriages are pretty much a failure, and

[008-182]your father better not waste his time on ’em.”

[008-183]“Papa’d be so grateful,” she returned, “if he could have your advice.”

[008-184]Instantly George’s face became flushed. “I don’t know that I’ve done

[008-185]anything to be insulted for!” he said. “I don’t see that what I said

[008-186]was particularly fresh.”

[008-187]“No, indeed!”

[008-188]“Then what do you—”

[008-189]She laughed gaily. “I don’t! And I don’t mind your being such a lofty

[008-190]person at all. I think it’s ever so interesting—but papa’s a great man!”

[008-191]“Is he?” George decided to be good-natured. “Well, let us hope so. I

[008-192]hope so, I’m sure.”

[008-193]Looking at him keenly, she saw that the magnificent youth was

[008-194]incredibly sincere in this bit of graciousness. He spoke as a tolerant,

[008-195]elderly statesman might speak of a promising young politician; and with

[008-196]her eyes still upon him, Lucy shook her head in gentle wonder. “I’m

[008-197]just beginning to understand,” she said.

[008-198]“Understand what?”

[008-199]“What it means to be a real Amberson in this town. Papa told me

[008-200]something about it before we came, but I see he didn’t say half enough!”

[008-201]George superbly took this all for tribute. “Did your father say he knew

[008-202]the family before he left here?”

[008-203]“Yes. I believe he was particularly a friend of your [[george-amberson|Uncle George]]; and

[008-204]he didn’t say so, but I imagine he must have known your mother very

[008-205]well, too. He wasn’t an inventor then; he was a young lawyer. The town

[008-206]was smaller in those days, and I believe he was quite well known.”

[008-207]“I dare say. I’ve no doubt the family are all very glad to see him

[008-208]back, especially if they used to have him at the house a good deal, as

[008-209]he told you.”

[008-210]“I don’t think he meant to boast of it,” she said: “He spoke of it

[008-211]quite calmly.”

[008-212]George stared at her for a moment in perplexity, then perceiving that

[008-213]her intention was satirical, “Girls really ought to go to a man’s

[008-214]college,” he said—“just a month or two, anyhow. It’d take _some_ of the

[008-215]freshness out of ’em!”

[008-216]“I can’t believe it,” she retorted, as her partner for the next

[008-217]dance arrived. “It would only make them a little politer on the

[008-218]surface—they’d be really just as awful as ever, after you got to know

[008-219]them a few minutes.”
