[044-001]“I’m sure we can manage it,” she interrupted quickly. “There really

[044-002]isn’t a cheaper place in town that we could actually live in and be—”

[044-003]Here she interrupted herself. “Oh! There’s one great economy I forgot

[044-004]to tell you, and it’s especially an economy for you, because you’re

[044-005]always too generous about such things: they don’t allow any tipping.

[044-006]They have signs that prohibit it.”

[044-007]“That’s good,” he said grimly. “But the rent is thirty-six dollars a

[044-008]month; the dinner is twenty-two and a half for each of us, and we’ve

[044-009]got to have some provision for other food. We won’t need any clothes

[044-010]for a year, perhaps—”

[044-011]“Oh, longer!” she exclaimed. “So you see—”

[044-012]“I see that forty-five and thirty-six make eighty-one,” he said. “At

[044-013]the lowest, we need a hundred dollars a month—and I’m going to make

[044-014]thirty-two.”

[044-015]“I thought of that, George,” she said confidently, “and I’m sure it

[044-016]will be all right. You’ll be earning a great deal more than that very

[044-017]soon.”

[044-018]“I don’t see any prospect of it—not till I’m admitted to the bar, and

[044-019]that will be two years at the earliest.”

[044-020][[fanny-minafer|Fanny]]’s confidence was not shaken. “I know you’ll be getting on faster

[044-021]than—”

[044-022]“Faster?” George echoed gravely. “We’ve got to have more than that to

[044-023]start with.”

[044-024]“Well, there’s the six hundred dollars from the sale. Six hundred and

[044-025]twelve dollars it was.”

[044-026]“It isn’t six hundred and twelve now,” said George. “It’s about one

[044-027]hundred and sixty.”

[044-028]Fanny showed a momentary dismay. “Why, how—”

[044-029]“I lent [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] two hundred; I gave fifty apiece to old Sam and

[044-030]those two other old darkies that worked for grandfather so long, and

[044-031]ten to each of the servants here—”

[044-032]“And you gave me thirty-six,” she said thoughtfully, “for the first

[044-033]month’s rent, in advance.”

[044-034]“Did I? I’d forgotten. Well, with about a hundred and sixty in bank and

[044-035]our expenses a hundred a month, it doesn’t seem as if this new place—”

[044-036]“Still,” she interrupted, “we have paid the first month’s rent in

[044-037]advance, and it does seem to be the most practical—”

[044-038]George rose. “See here, Aunt Fanny,” he said decisively. “You stay here

[044-039]and look after the moving. Old Frank doesn’t expect me until afternoon,

[044-040]this first day, but I’ll go and see him now.”

[044-041]It was early, and old Frank, just established at his big, flat-topped

[044-042]desk, was surprised when his prospective assistant and pupil walked

[044-043]in. He was pleased, as well as surprised, however, and rose, offering

[044-044]a cordial old hand. “The real flare!” he said. “The real flare for the

[044-045]law. That’s right! Couldn’t wait till afternoon to begin! I’m delighted

[044-046]that you—”

[044-047]“I wanted to say—” George began, but his patron cut him off.

[044-048]“Wait just a minute, my boy. I’ve prepared a little speech of welcome,

[044-049]and even though you’re five hours ahead of time, I mean to deliver

[044-050]it. First of all, your grandfather was my old war-comrade and my best

[044-051]client; for years I prospered through my connection with his business,

[044-052]and his grandson is welcome in my office and to my best efforts in

[044-053]his behalf. But I want to confess, Georgie, that during your earlier

[044-054]youth I may have had some slight feeling of—well, prejudice, not

[044-055]altogether in your favour; but whatever slight feeling it was, it

[044-056]began to vanish on that afternoon, a good while ago, when you stood up

[044-057]to your [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]] Amberson as you did in the Major’s library, and

[044-058]talked to her as a man and a gentleman should. I saw then what good

[044-059]stuff was in you—and I always wanted to mention it. If my prejudice

[044-060]hadn’t altogether vanished after that, the last vestiges disappeared

[044-061]during these trying times that have come upon you this past year, when

[044-062]I have been a witness to the depth of feeling you’ve shown and your

[044-063]quiet consideration for your grandfather and for everyone else around

[044-064]you. I just want to add that I think you’ll find an honest pleasure

[044-065]now in industry and frugality that wouldn’t have come to you in a more

[044-066]frivolous career. The law is a jealous mistress and a stern mistress,

[044-067]but a—”

[044-068]George had stood before him in great and increasing embarrassment; and

[044-069]he was unable to allow the address to proceed to its conclusion.

[044-070]“I can’t do it!” he burst out. “I can’t take her for my mistress.”

[044-071]“What?”

[044-072]“I’ve come to tell you, I’ve got to find something that’s quicker. I

[044-073]can’t—”

[044-074]Old Frank got a little red. “Let’s sit down,” he said. “What’s the

[044-075]trouble?”

[044-076]George told him.

[044-077]The old gentleman listened sympathetically, only murmuring: “Well,

[044-078]well!” from time to time, and nodding acquiescence.

[044-079]“You see she’s set her mind on this apartment,” George explained.

[044-080]“She’s got some old cronies there, and I guess she’s been looking

[044-081]forward to the games of bridge and the kind of harmless gossip that

[044-082]goes on in such places. Really, it’s a life she’d like better than

[044-083]anything else—better than that she’s lived at home, I really believe.

[044-084]It struck me she’s just about got to have it, and after all she could

[044-085]hardly have anything less.”

[044-086]“This comes pretty heavily upon me, you know,” said old Frank. “I got

[044-087]her into that headlight company, and she fooled me about her resources

[044-088]as much as she did your Uncle George. I was never your father’s

[044-089]adviser, if you remember, and when the insurance was turned over to

[044-090]her some other lawyer arranged it—probably your father’s. But it comes

[044-091]pretty heavily on me, and I feel a certain responsibility.”

[044-092]“Not at all. I’m taking the responsibility.” And George smiled with one

[044-093]corner of his mouth. “She’s not your aunt, you know, sir.”

[044-094]“Well, I’m unable to see, even if she’s yours, that a young man is

[044-095]morally called upon to give up a career at the law to provide his aunt

[044-096]with a favourable opportunity to play bridge whist!”

[044-097]“No,” George agreed. “But I haven’t begun my ‘career at the law’ so it

[044-098]can’t be said I’m making any considerable sacrifice. I’ll tell you how

[044-099]it is, sir.” He flushed, and, looking out of the streaked and smoky

[044-100]window beside which he was sitting, spoke with difficulty. “I feel

[044-101]as if—as if perhaps I had one or two pretty important things in my

[044-102]life to make up for. Well, I can’t. I can’t make them up to—to whom I

[044-103]would. It’s struck me that, as I couldn’t, I might be a little decent

[044-104]to somebody else, perhaps—if I could manage it! I never have been

[044-105]particularly decent to poor old Aunt Fanny.”

[044-106]“Oh, I don’t know: I shouldn’t say that. A little youthful teasing—I

[044-107]doubt if she’s minded so much. She felt your father’s death

[044-108]terrifically, of course, but it seems to me she’s had a fairly

[044-109]comfortable life—up to now—if she was disposed to take it that way.”

[044-110]“But ‘up to now’ is the important thing,” George said. “Now is now—and

[044-111]you see I can’t wait two years to be admitted to the bar and begin to

[044-112]practice. I’ve got to start in at something else that pays from the

[044-113]start, and that’s what I’ve come to you about. I have an idea, you see.”

[044-114]“Well, I’m glad of that!” said old Frank, smiling. “I can’t think of

[044-115]anything just at this minute that pays from the start.”

[044-116]“I only know of one thing, myself.”

[044-117]“What is it?”

[044-118]George flushed again, but managed to laugh at his own embarrassment. “I

[044-119]suppose I’m about as ignorant of business as anybody in the world,” he

[044-120]said. “But I’ve heard they pay very high wages to people in dangerous

[044-121]trades; I’ve always heard they did, and I’m sure it must be true. I

[044-122]mean people that handle touchy chemicals or high explosives—men in

[044-123]dynamite factories, or who take things of that sort about the country

[044-124]in wagons, and shoot oil wells. I thought I’d see if you couldn’t tell

[044-125]me something more about it, or else introduce me to someone who could,

[044-126]and then I thought I’d see if I couldn’t get something of the kind to

[044-127]do as soon as possible. My nerves are good; I’m muscular, and I’ve got

[044-128]a steady hand; it seemed to me that this was about the only line of

[044-129]work in the world that I’m fitted for. I wanted to get started to-day

[044-130]if I could.”

[044-131]Old Frank gave him a long stare. At first this scrutiny was sharply

[044-132]incredulous; then it was grave; finally it developed into a threat

[044-133]of overwhelming laughter; a forked vein in his forehead became more

[044-134]visible and his eyes seemed about to protrude.

[044-135]But he controlled his impulse; and, rising, took up his hat and

[044-136]overcoat. “All right,” he said. “If you’ll promise not to get blown up,

[044-137]I’ll go with you to see if we can find the job.” Then, meaning what he

[044-138]said, but amazed that he did mean it, he added: “You certainly are the

[044-139]most practical young man I ever met!”

Chapter XXXIII

[044-140]They found the job. It needed an apprenticeship of only six weeks,

[044-141]during which period George was to receive fifteen dollars a week; after

[044-142]that he would get twenty-eight. This settled the apartment question,

[044-143]and Fanny was presently established in a greater contentment than she

[044-144]had known for a long time. Early every morning she made something she

[044-145]called (and believed to be) coffee for George, and he was gallant

[044-146]enough not to undeceive her. She lunched alone in her “kitchenette,”

[044-147]for George’s place of employment was ten miles out of town on an

[044-148]interurban trolley-line, and he seldom returned before seven. Fanny

[044-149]found partners for bridge by two o’clock almost every afternoon, and

[044-150]she played until about six. Then she got George’s “dinner clothes” out

[044-151]for him—he maintained this habit—and she changed her own dress. When he

[044-152]arrived he usually denied that he was tired, though he sometimes looked

[044-153]tired, particularly during the first few months; and he explained to

[044-154]her frequently—looking bored enough with her insistence—that his work

[044-155]was “fairly light, and fairly congenial, too.” Fanny had the foggiest

[044-156]idea of what it was, though she noticed that it roughened his hands and

[044-157]stained them. “Something in those new chemical works,” she explained to

[044-158]casual inquirers. It was not more definite in her own mind.

[044-159]Respect for George undoubtedly increased within her, however, and

[044-160]she told him she’d always had a feeling he might “turn out to be a

[044-161]mechanical genius, or something.” George assented with a nod, as the

[044-162]easiest course open to him. He did not take a hand at bridge after

[044-163]dinner: his provisions for Fanny’s happiness refused to extend that

[044-164]far, and at the table d’hote he was a rather discouraging boarder. He

[044-165]was considered “affected” and absurdly “up-stage” by the one or two

[044-166]young men, and the three or four young women, who enlivened the elderly

[044-167]retreat; and was possibly less popular there than he had been elsewhere

[044-168]during his life, though he was now nothing worse than a coldly polite

[044-169]young man who kept to himself. After dinner he would escort his aunt

[044-170]from the table in some state (not wholly unaccompanied by a leerish

[044-171]wink or two from the wags of the place) and he would leave her at the

[044-172]door of the communal parlours and card rooms, with a formality in his

[044-173]bow of farewell which afforded an amusing contrast to Fanny’s always

[044-174]voluble protests. (She never failed to urge loudly that he really must

[044-175]come and play, just this once, and not go hiding from everybody in his

[044-176]room every evening like this!) At least some of the other inhabitants

[044-177]found the contrast amusing, for sometimes, as he departed stiffly

[044-178]toward the elevator, leaving her still entreating in the doorway

[044-179](though with one eye already on her table, to see that it was not

[044-180]seized) a titter would follow him which he was no doubt meant to hear.

[044-181]He did not care whether they laughed or not.
