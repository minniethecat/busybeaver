[022-001]“What ‘young fellow’?”

[022-002]“This young fellow Morgan,” laughed George; “Honestly, [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]], I

[022-003]shouldn’t be a bit surprised to have him request an interview with me

[022-004]any day, and declare that his intentions are honourable, and ask my

[022-005]permission to pay his addresses to you. What had I better tell him?”

[022-006]Fanny burst into tears.

[022-007]“Good heavens!” George cried. “I was only teasing. I didn’t mean—”

[022-008]“Let me alone,” she said lifelessly; and, continuing to weep, rose and

[022-009]began to clear away the dishes.

[022-010]“Please, Aunt Fanny—”

[022-011]“Just let me alone.”

[022-012]George was distressed. “I didn’t mean anything, Aunt Fanny! I didn’t

[022-013]know you’d got so sensitive as all that.”

[022-014]“You’d better go up to bed,” she said desolately, going on with her

[022-015]work and her weeping.

[022-016]“Anyhow,” he insisted, “do let these things wait. Let the servants

[022-017]’tend to the table in the morning.”

[022-018]“No.”

[022-019]“But, why not?”

[022-020]“Just let me alone.”

[022-021]“Oh, Lord!” George groaned, going to the door. There he turned. “See

[022-022]here, Aunt Fanny, there’s not a bit of use your bothering about those

[022-023]dishes tonight. What’s the use of a butler and three maids if—”

[022-024]“Just let me alone.”

[022-025]He obeyed, and could still hear a pathetic sniffing from the dining

[022-026]room as he went up the stairs.

[022-027]“By George!” he grunted, as he reached his own room; and his thought

[022-028]was that living with a person so sensitive to kindly raillery might

[022-029]prove lugubrious. He whistled, long and low, then went to the window

[022-030]and looked through the darkness to the great silhouette of his

[022-031]grandfather’s house. Lights were burning over there, upstairs; probably

[022-032]his newly arrived uncle was engaged in talk with the Major.

[022-033]George’s glance lowered, resting casually upon the indistinct ground,

[022-034]and he beheld some vague shapes, unfamiliar to him. Formless heaps,

[022-035]they seemed; but, without much curiosity, he supposed that sewer

[022-036]connections or water pipes might be out of order, making necessary some

[022-037]excavations. He hoped the work would not take long; he hated to see

[022-038]that sweep of lawn made unsightly by trenches and lines of dirt, even

[022-039]temporarily. Not greatly disturbed, however, he pulled down the shade,

[022-040]yawned, and began to undress, leaving further investigation for the

[022-041]morning.

[022-042]But in the morning he had forgotten all about it, and raised his shade,

[022-043]to let in the light, without even glancing toward the ground. Not until

[022-044]he had finished dressing did he look forth from his window, and then

[022-045]his glance was casual. The next instant his attitude became electric,

[022-046]and he gave utterance to a bellow of dismay. He ran from his room,

[022-047]plunged down the stairs, out of the front door, and, upon a nearer view

[022-048]of the destroyed lawn, began to release profanity upon the breezeless

[022-049]summer air, which remained unaffected. Between his mother’s house and

[022-050]his grandfather’s, excavations for the cellars of five new houses

[022-051]were in process, each within a few feet of its neighbour. Foundations

[022-052]of brick were being laid; everywhere were piles of brick and stacked

[022-053]lumber, and sand heaps and mortar beds.

[022-054]It was Sunday, and so the workmen implicated in these defacings

[022-055]were denied what unquestionably they would have considered a treat;

[022-056]but as the fanatic orator continued the monologue, a gentleman in

[022-057]flannels emerged upward from one of the excavations, and regarded him

[022-058]contemplatively.

[022-059]“Obtaining any relief, nephew?” he inquired with some interest. “You

[022-060]must have learned quite a number of those expressions in childhood—it’s

[022-061]so long since I’d heard them I fancied they were obsolete.”

[022-062]“Who wouldn’t swear?” George demanded hotly. “In the name of God, what

[022-063]does grandfather mean, doing such things?”

[022-064]“My private opinion is,” said Amberson gravely, “he desires to increase

[022-065]his income by building these houses to rent.”

[022-066]“Well, in the name of God, can’t he increase his income any other way

[022-067]but this?”

[022-068]“In the name of God, it would appear he couldn’t.”

[022-069]“It’s beastly! It’s a damn degradation! It’s a crime!”

[022-070]“I don’t know about its being a crime,” said his uncle, stepping over

[022-071]some planks to join him. “It might be a mistake, though. Your mother

[022-072]said not to tell you until we got home, so as not to spoil commencement

[022-073]for you. She rather feared you’d be upset.”

[022-074]“Upset! Oh, my Lord, I should think I would be upset! He’s in his

[022-075]second childhood. What did you let him do it for, in the name of—”

[022-076]“Make it in the name of heaven this time, George; it’s Sunday. Well, I

[022-077]thought, myself, it was a mistake.”

[022-078]“I should say so!”

[022-079]“Yes,” said Amberson. “I wanted him to put up an apartment building

[022-080]instead of these houses.”

[022-081]“An apartment building! Here?”

[022-082]“Yes; that was my idea.”

[022-083]George struck his hands together despairingly. “An apartment house! Oh,

[022-084]my Lord!”

[022-085]“Don’t worry! Your grandfather wouldn’t listen to me, but he’ll wish he

[022-086]had, some day. He says that people aren’t going to live in miserable

[022-087]little flats when they can get a whole house with some grass in front

[022-088]and plenty of backyard behind. He sticks it out that apartment houses

[022-089]will never do in a town of this type, and when I pointed out to him

[022-090]that a dozen or so of ’em already are doing, he claimed it was just the

[022-091]novelty, and that they’d all be empty as soon as people got used to

[022-092]’em. So he’s putting up these houses.”

[022-093]“Is he getting miserly in his old age?”

[022-094]“Hardly! Look what he gave [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] and Amelia!”

[022-095]“I don’t mean he’s a miser, of course,” said George. “Heaven knows he’s

[022-096]liberal enough with mother and me; but why on earth didn’t he sell

[022-097]something or other rather than do a thing like this?”

[022-098]“As a matter of fact,” Amberson returned coolly, “I believe he has sold

[022-099]something or other, from time to time.”

[022-100]“Well, in heaven’s name,” George cried, “what did he do it for?”

[022-101]“To get [[wealth-and-fortune|money]],” his uncle mildly replied. “That’s my deduction.”

[022-102]“I suppose you’re joking—or trying to!”

[022-103]“That’s the best way to look at it,” Amberson said amiably. “Take the

[022-104]whole thing as a joke—and in the meantime, if you haven’t had your

[022-105]breakfast—”

[022-106]“I haven’t!”

[022-107]“Then if I were you I’d go in and get some. And”—he paused, becoming

[022-108]serious—“and if I were you I wouldn’t say anything to your grandfather

[022-109]about this.”

[022-110]“I don’t think I could trust myself to speak to him about it,”

[022-111]said George. “I want to treat him respectfully, because he is my

[022-112]grandfather, but I don’t believe I could if I talked to him about such

[022-113]a thing as this!”

[022-114]And with a gesture of despair, plainly signifying that all too soon

[022-115]after leaving bright college years behind him he had entered into the

[022-116]full tragedy of life, George turned bitterly upon his heel and went

[022-117]into the house for his breakfast.

[022-118]His uncle, with his head whimsically upon one side, gazed after him not

[022-119]altogether unsympathetically, then descended again into the excavation

[022-120]whence he had lately emerged. Being a philosopher he was not surprised,

[022-121]that afternoon, in the course of a drive he took in the old carriage

[022-122]with the Major, when George was encountered upon the highway, flashing

[022-123]along in his runabout with [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] beside him and Pendennis doing better

[022-124]than three minutes.

[022-125]“He seems to have recovered,” Amberson remarked: “Looks in the highest

[022-126]good spirits.”

[022-127]“I beg your pardon.”

[022-128]“Your grandson,” Amberson explained. “He was inclined to melancholy

[022-129]this morning, but seemed jolly enough just now when they passed us.”

[022-130]“What was he melancholy about? Not getting remorseful about all the

[022-131]money he’s spent at college, was he?” [[major-amberson|The Major]] chuckled feebly, but

[022-132]with sufficient grimness. “I wonder what he thinks I’m made of,” he

[022-133]concluded querulously.

[022-134]“Gold,” his son suggested, adding gently, “And he’s right about part of

[022-135]you, father.”

[022-136]“What part?”

[022-137]“Your heart.”

[022-138]The Major laughed ruefully. “I suppose that may account for how heavy

[022-139]it feels, sometimes, nowadays. This town seems to be rolling right

[022-140]over that old heart you mentioned, George—rolling over it and burying

[022-141]it under! When I think of those devilish workmen digging up my lawn,

[022-142]yelling around my house—”

[022-143]“Never mind, father. Don’t think of it. When things are a nuisance it’s

[022-144]a good idea not to keep remembering ’em.”

[022-145]“I try not to,” the old gentleman murmured. “I try to keep remembering

[022-146]that I won’t be remembering anything very long.” And, somehow convinced

[022-147]that this thought was a mirthful one, he laughed loudly, and slapped

[022-148]his knee. “Not so very long now, my boy!” he chuckled, continuing to

[022-149]echo his own amusement. “Not so very long. Not so very long!”

Chapter XVII

[022-150]Young George paid his respects to his grandfather the following

[022-151]morning, having been occupied with various affairs and engagements

[022-152]on Sunday until after the Major’s bedtime; and topics concerned with

[022-153]building or excavations were not introduced into the conversation,

[022-154]which was a cheerful one until George lightly mentioned some new

[022-155]plans of his. He was a skillful driver, as the Major knew, and he

[022-156]spoke of his desire to extend his proficiency in this art: in fact,

[022-157]he entertained the ambition to drive a four-in-hand. However, as the

[022-158]Major said nothing, and merely sat still, looking surprised, George

[022-159]went on to say that he did not propose to “go in for coaching just

[022-160]at the start”; he thought it would be better to begin with a tandem.

[022-161]He was sure Pendennis could be trained to work as a leader; and all

[022-162]that one needed to buy at present, he said, would be “comparatively

[022-163]inexpensive—a new trap, and the harness, of course, and a good bay

[022-164]to match Pendennis.” He did not care for a special groom; one of the

[022-165]stablemen would do.

[022-166]At this point the Major decided to speak. “You say one of the stablemen

[022-167]would do?” he inquired, his widened eyes remaining fixed upon his

[022-168]grandson. “That’s lucky, because one’s all there is, just at present,

[022-169]George. Old fat Tom does it all. Didn’t you notice, when you took

[022-170]Pendennis out, yesterday?”

[022-171]“Oh, that will be all right, sir. My mother can lend me her man.”

[022-172]“Can she?” The old gentleman smiled faintly. “I wonder—” He paused.

[022-173]“What, sir?”

[022-174]“Whether you mightn’t care to go to law-school somewhere perhaps. I’d

[022-175]be glad to set aside a sum that would see you through.”

[022-176]This senile divergence from the topic in hand surprised George

[022-177]painfully. “I have no interest whatever in the law,” he said. “I

[022-178]don’t care for it, and the idea of being a professional man has never

[022-179]appealed to me. None of the family has ever gone in for that sort

[022-180]of thing, to my knowledge, and I don’t care to be the first. I was

[022-181]speaking of driving a tandem—”

[022-182]“I know you were,” the Major said quietly.

[022-183]George looked hurt. “I beg your pardon. Of course if the idea doesn’t

[022-184]appeal to you—” And he rose to go.

[022-185]The Major ran a tremulous hand through his hair, sighing deeply. “I—I

[022-186]don’t like to refuse you anything, Georgie,” he said. “I don’t know

[022-187]that I often have refused you whatever you wanted—in reason—”

[022-188]“You’ve always been more than generous, sir,” George interrupted

[022-189]quickly. “And if the idea of a tandem doesn’t appeal to you, why—of

[022-190]course—” And he waved his hand, heroically dismissing the tandem.

[022-191]The Major’s distress became obvious. “Georgie, I’d like to, but—but

[022-192]I’ve an idea tandems are dangerous to drive, and your mother might be

[022-193]anxious. She—”

[022-194]“No, sir; I think not. She felt it would be rather a good thing—help to

[022-195]keep me out in the open air. But if perhaps your finances—”

[022-196]“Oh, it isn’t that so much,” the old gentleman said hurriedly. “I

[022-197]wasn’t thinking of that altogether.” He laughed uncomfortably. “I guess

[022-199]“I thought you said—”
