[016-001]“What investments?” George demanded. “He hasn’t gone into Mr. Morgan’s

[016-002]automobile concern, has he?”

[016-003]“No,” Isabel smiled. “The ‘automobile concern’ is all [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s, and

[016-004]it’s so small I understand it’s taken hardly anything. No; your father

[016-005]has always prided himself on making only the most absolutely safe

[016-006]investments, but two or three years ago he and your [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] both

[016-007]put a great deal—pretty much everything they could get together, I

[016-008]think—into the stock of rolling-mills some friends of theirs owned, and

[016-009]I’m afraid the mills haven’t been doing well.”

[016-010]“What of that? Father needn’t worry. You and I could take care of him

[016-011]the rest of his life on what grandfather—”

[016-012]“Of course,” she agreed. “But your father’s always lived so for his

[016-013]business and taken such pride in his sound investments; it’s a passion

[016-014]with him. I—”

[016-015]“Pshaw! He needn’t worry! You tell him we’ll look after him: we’ll

[016-016]build him a little stone bank in the backyard, if he busts up, and he

[016-017]can go and put his pennies in it every morning. That’ll keep him just

[016-018]as happy as he ever was!” He kissed her. “Good-night, I’m going to tell

[016-019][[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] good-bye. Don’t sit up for me.”

[016-020]She walked to the front gate with him, still holding his hand, and he

[016-021]told her again not to “sit up” for him.

[016-022]“Yes, I will,” she laughed. “You won’t be very late.”

[016-023]“Well—it’s my last night.”

[016-024]“But I know Lucy, and she knows I want to see you, too, your last

[016-025]night. You’ll see: she’ll send you home promptly at eleven!”

[016-026]But she was mistaken: Lucy sent him home promptly at ten.

Chapter XII

[016-027]Isabel’s uneasiness about her husband’s health—sometimes reflected in

[016-028]her letters to George during the winter that followed—had not been

[016-029]alleviated when the accredited Senior returned for his next summer

[016-030]vacation, and she confided to him in his room, soon after his arrival,

[016-031]that “something” the doctor had said to her lately had made her more

[016-032]uneasy than ever.

[016-033]“Still worrying over his rolling-mills investments?” George asked, not

[016-034]seriously impressed.

[016-035]“I’m afraid it’s past that stage from what Dr. Rainey says. His worries

[016-036]only aggravate his condition now. Dr. Rainey says we ought to get him

[016-037]away.”

[016-038]“Well, let’s do it, then.”

[016-039]“He won’t go.”

[016-040]“He’s a man awfully set in his ways; that’s true,” said George. “I

[016-041]don’t think there’s anything much the matter with him, though, and he

[016-042]looks just the same to me. Have you seen Lucy lately? How is she?”

[016-043]“Hasn’t she written you?”

[016-044]“Oh, about once a month,” he answered carelessly. “Never says much

[016-045]about herself. How’s she look?”

[016-046]“She looks—pretty!” said Isabel. “I suppose she wrote you they’ve

[016-047]moved?”

[016-048]“Yes; I’ve got her address. She said they were building.”

[016-049]“They did. It’s all finished, and they’ve been in it a month. Lucy is

[016-050]so capable; she keeps house exquisitely. It’s small, but oh, such a

[016-051]pretty little house!”

[016-052]“Well, that’s fortunate,” George said. “One thing I’ve always felt they

[016-053]didn’t know a great deal about is architecture.”

[016-054]“Don’t they?” asked Isabel, surprised. “Anyhow, their house is

[016-055]charming. It’s way out beyond the end of [[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]]; it’s quite

[016-056]near that big white house with a gray-green roof somebody built out

[016-057]there a year or so ago. There are any number of houses going up, out

[016-058]that way; and the trolley-line runs within a block of them now, on the

[016-059]next street, and the traction people are laying tracks more than three

[016-060]miles beyond. I suppose you’ll be driving out to see Lucy to-morrow.”

[016-061]“I thought—” George hesitated. “I thought perhaps I’d go after dinner

[016-062]this evening.”

[016-063]At this his mother laughed, not astonished. “It was only my feeble joke

[016-064]about ‘to-morrow,’ Georgie! I was pretty sure you couldn’t wait that

[016-065]long. Did Lucy write you about the factory?”

[016-066]“No. What factory?”

[016-067]“The automobile shops. They had rather a dubious time at first, I’m

[016-068]afraid, and some of Eugene’s experiments turned out badly, but this

[016-069]spring they’ve finished eight automobiles and sold them all, and

[016-070]they’ve got twelve more almost finished, and they’re sold already!

[016-071]Eugene’s so gay over it!”

[016-072]“What do his old sewing-machines look like? Like that first one he had

[016-073]when they came here?”

[016-074]“No, indeed! These have rubber tires blown up with air—pneumatic! And

[016-075]they aren’t so high; they’re very easy to get into, and the engine’s

[016-076]in front—Eugene thinks that’s a great improvement. They’re very

[016-077]interesting to look at; behind the driver’s seat there’s a sort of box

[016-078]where four people can sit, with a step and a little door in the rear,

[016-079]and—”

[016-080]“I know all about it,” said George. “I’ve seen any number like that,

[016-081]East. You can see all you want of ’em, if you stand on Fifth Avenue

[016-082]half an hour, any afternoon. I’ve seen half-a-dozen go by almost at the

[016-083]same time—within a few minutes, anyhow; and of course electric hansoms

[016-084]are a common sight there any day. I hired one, myself, the last time I

[016-085]was there. How fast do Mr. Morgan’s machines go?”

[016-086]“Much too fast! It’s very exhilarating—but rather frightening; and they

[016-087]do make a fearful uproar. He says, though, he thinks he sees a way to

[016-088]get around the noisiness in time.”

[016-089]“I don’t mind the noise,” said George. “Give me a horse, for mine,

[016-090]though, any day. I must get up a race with one of these things:

[016-091]Pendennis’ll leave it one mile behind in a two-mile run. How’s

[016-092]grandfather?”

[016-093]“He looks well, but he complains sometimes of his heart: I suppose

[016-094]that’s natural at his age—and it’s an Amberson trouble.” Having

[016-095]mentioned this, she looked anxious instantly. “Did you ever feel any

[016-096]weakness there, Georgie?”

[016-097]“No!” he laughed.

[016-098]“Are you sure, dear?”

[016-099]“No!” And he laughed again. “Did you?”

[016-100]“Oh, I think not—at least, the doctor told me he thought my heart was

[016-101]about all right. He said I needn’t be alarmed.”

[016-102]“I should think not! Women do seem to be always talking about health: I

[016-103]suppose they haven’t got enough else to think of!”

[016-104]“That must be it,” she said gayly. “We’re an idle lot!”

[016-105]George had taken off his coat. “I don’t like to hint to a lady,” he

[016-106]said, “but I do want to dress before dinner.”

[016-107]“Don’t be long; I’ve got to do a lot of looking at you, dear!” She

[016-108]kissed him and ran away singing.

[016-109]But his [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]] was not so fond; and at the dinner-table there came

[016-110]a spark of liveliness into her eye when George patronizingly asked her

[016-111]what was the news in her own “particular line of sport.”

[016-112]“What do you mean, Georgie?” she asked quietly.

[016-113]“Oh I mean: What’s the news in the fast set generally? You been causing

[016-114]any divorces lately?”

[016-115]“No,” said Fanny, the spark in her eye getting brighter. “I haven’t

[016-116]been causing anything.”

[016-117]“Well, what’s the gossip? You usually hear pretty much everything that

[016-118]goes on around the nooks and crannies in this town, I hear. What’s the

[016-119]last from the gossips’ corner, auntie?”

[016-120]Fanny dropped her eyes, and the spark was concealed, but a movement of

[016-121]her lower lip betokened a tendency to laugh, as she replied, “There

[016-122]hasn’t been much gossip lately, except the report that Lucy Morgan and

[016-123]Fred Kinney are engaged—and that’s quite old, by this time.”

[016-124]Undeniably, this bit of mischief was entirely successful, for there was

[016-125]a clatter upon George’s plate. “What—what do you think you’re talking

[016-126]about?” he gasped.

[016-127]Miss Fanny looked up innocently. “[[About]] the report of Lucy Morgan’s

[016-128]engagement to Fred Kinney.”

[016-129]George turned dumbly to his mother, and Isabel shook her head

[016-130]reassuringly. “People are always starting rumours,” she said. “I

[016-131]haven’t paid any attention to this one.”

[016-132]“But you—you’ve heard it?” he stammered.

[016-133]“Oh, one hears all sorts of nonsense, dear. I haven’t the slightest

[016-134]idea that it’s true.”

[016-135]“Then you have heard it!”

[016-136]“I wouldn’t let it take my appetite,” his father suggested drily.

[016-137]“There are plenty of girls in the world!”

[016-138]George turned pale.

[016-139]“Eat your dinner, Georgie,” his aunt said sweetly. “Food will do you

[016-140]good. I didn’t say I knew this rumour was true. I only said I’d heard

[016-141]it.”

[016-142]“When? When did you hear it!”

[016-143]“Oh, months ago!” And Fanny found any further postponement of laughter

[016-144]impossible.

[016-145]“Fanny, you’re a hard-hearted creature,” Isabel said gently. “You

[016-146]really are. Don’t pay any attention to her, George. Fred Kinney’s only

[016-147]a clerk in his uncle’s hardware place: he couldn’t marry for ages—even

[016-148]if anybody would accept him!”

[016-149]George breathed tumultuously. “I don’t care anything about ‘ages’!

[016-150]What’s that got to do with it?” he said, his thoughts appearing to be

[016-151]somewhat disconnected. “‘Ages,’ don’t mean anything! I only want to

[016-152]know—I want to know—I want—” He stopped.

[016-153]“What do you want?” his father asked crossly. “Why don’t you say it?

[016-154]Don’t make such a fuss.”

[016-155]“I’m not—not at all,” George declared, pushing his chair back from the

[016-156]table.

[016-157]“You must finish your dinner, dear,” his mother urged. “Don’t—”

[016-158]“I have finished. I’ve eaten all I want. I don’t want any more than

[016-159]I wanted. I don’t want—I—” He rose, still incoherent. “I prefer—I

[016-160]want—Please excuse me!”

[016-161]He left the room, and a moment later the screens outside the open front

[016-162]door were heard to slam.

[016-163]“Fanny! You shouldn’t—”

[016-164]“Isabel, don’t reproach me. He did have plenty of dinner, and I only

[016-165]told the truth: everybody has been saying—”

[016-166]“But there isn’t any truth in it.”

[016-167]“We don’t actually know there isn’t,” Miss Fanny insisted, giggling.

[016-168]“We’ve never asked Lucy.”

[016-169]“I wouldn’t ask her anything so absurd!”

[016-170]“George would,” George’s father remarked. “That’s what he’s gone to do.”

[016-171][[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]] was not mistaken: that was what his son had gone to do.

[016-172]Lucy and her father were just rising from their dinner table when the

[016-173]stirred youth arrived at the front door of the new house. It was a

[016-174]cottage, however, rather than a house; and Lucy had taken a free hand

[016-175]with the architect, achieving results in white and green, outside, and

[016-176]white and blue, inside, to such effect of youth and daintiness that her

[016-177]father complained of “too much spring-time!” The whole place, including

[016-178]his own bedroom, was a young damsel’s boudoir, he said, so that nowhere

[016-179]could he smoke a cigar without feeling like a ruffian. However, he was

[016-180]smoking when George arrived, and he encouraged George to join him in

[016-181]the pastime, but the caller, whose air was both tense and preoccupied,

[016-182]declined with something like agitation.

[016-183]“I never smoke—that is, I’m seldom—I mean, no thanks,” he said. “I mean

[016-184]not at all. I’d rather not.”

[016-185]“Aren’t you well, George?” Eugene asked, looking at him in perplexity.

[016-186]“Have you been overworking at college? You do look rather pa—”

[016-187]“I don’t work,” said George. “I mean I don’t work. I think, but I don’t

[016-188]work. I only work at the end of the term. There isn’t much to do.”

[016-189]Eugene’s perplexity was little decreased, and a tinkle of the door-bell

[016-190]afforded him obvious relief. “It’s my foreman,” he said, looking at

[016-191]his watch. “I’ll take him out in the yard to talk. This is no place

[016-192]for a foreman.” And he departed, leaving the “living room” to Lucy and

[016-193]George. It was a pretty room, white panelled and blue curtained—and no

[016-194]place for a foreman, as Eugene said. There was a grand piano, and Lucy

[016-195]stood leaning back against it, looking intently at George, while her

[016-196]fingers, behind her, absently struck a chord or two. And her dress was

[016-197]the dress for that room, being of blue and white, too; and the high

[016-198]colour in her cheeks was far from interfering with the general harmony

[016-199]of things—George saw with dismay that she was prettier than ever, and

[016-200]naturally he missed the reassurance he might have felt had he been

[016-201]able to guess that Lucy, on her part, was finding him better looking

[016-202]than ever. For, however unusual the scope of George’s pride, vanity of

[016-203]beauty was not included; he did not think about his looks.
