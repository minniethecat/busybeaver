[006-001]He had one of those curious moments that seem to have neither a

[006-002]cause nor any connection with actual things. While it lasted, he was

[006-003]disquieted not by thoughts—for he had no definite thoughts—but by a

[006-004]slight emotion like that caused in a dream by the presence of something

[006-005]invisible, soundless, and yet fantastic. There was nothing different

[006-006]or new about his mother, except her new black and silver dress: she

[006-007]was standing there beside him, bending her head a little in her

[006-008]greetings, smiling the same smile she had worn for the half-hour that

[006-009]people had been passing the “receiving” group. Her face was flushed,

[006-010]but the room was warm; and shaking hands with so many people easily

[006-011]accounted for the pretty glow that was upon her. At any time she could

[006-012]have “passed” for twenty-five or twenty-six—a man of fifty would have

[006-013]honestly guessed her to be about thirty but possibly two or three years

[006-014]younger—and though extraordinary in this, she had been extraordinary in

[006-015]it for years. There was nothing in either her looks or her manner to

[006-016]explain George’s uncomfortable feeling; and yet it increased, becoming

[006-017]suddenly a vague resentment, as if she had done something unmotherly to

[006-018]him.

[006-019]The fantastic moment passed; and even while it lasted, he was doing

[006-020]his duty, greeting two pretty girls with whom he had grown up, as

[006-021]people say, and warmly assuring them that he remembered them very

[006-022]well—an assurance which might have surprised them “in anybody but

[006-023][[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]]!” It seemed unnecessary, since he had spent many hours

[006-024]with them no longer ago than the preceding August. They had with them

[006-025]their parents and an uncle from out of town; and George negligently

[006-026]gave the parents the same assurance he had given the daughters, but

[006-027]murmured another form of greeting to the out-of-town uncle, whom he

[006-028]had never seen before. This person George absently took note of as

[006-029]a “queer-looking duck.” Undergraduates had not yet adopted “bird.”

[006-030]It was a period previous to that in which a sophomore would have

[006-031]thought of the Sharon girls’ uncle as a “queer-looking bird,” or,

[006-032]perhaps a “funny-face bird.” In George’s time, every human male was

[006-033]to be defined, at pleasure, as a “duck”; but “duck” was not spoken

[006-034]with admiring affection, as in its former feminine use to signify

[006-035]a “dear”—on the contrary, “duck” implied the speaker’s personal

[006-036]detachment and humorous superiority. An indifferent amusement was

[006-037]what George felt when his mother, with a gentle emphasis, interrupted

[006-038]his interchange of courtesies with the nieces to present him to

[006-039]the queer-looking duck, their uncle. This emphasis of Isabel’s,

[006-040]though slight, enabled George to perceive that she considered the

[006-041]queer-looking duck a person of some importance; but it was far from

[006-042]enabling him to understand why. The duck parted his thick and longish

[006-043]black hair on the side; his tie was a forgetful looking thing, and his

[006-044]coat, though it fitted a good enough middle-aged figure, no product of

[006-045]this year, or of last year either. One of his eyebrows was noticeably

[006-046]higher than the other; and there were whimsical lines between them,

[006-047]which gave him an apprehensive expression; but his apprehensions were

[006-048]evidently more humorous than profound, for his prevailing look was

[006-049]that of a genial man of affairs, not much afraid of anything whatever.

[006-050]Nevertheless, observing only his unfashionable hair, his eyebrows, his

[006-051]preoccupied tie and his old coat, the olympic George set him down as

[006-052]a queer-looking duck, and having thus completed his portrait, took no

[006-053]interest in him.

[006-054]The Sharon girls passed on, taking the queer-looking duck with them,

[006-055]and George became pink with mortification as his mother called his

[006-056]attention to a white-bearded guest waiting to shake his hand. This was

[006-057]George’s great-uncle, old John Minafer: it was old John’s boast that in

[006-058]spite of his connection by marriage with the Ambersons, he never had

[006-059]worn and never would wear a swaller-tail coat. Members of his family

[006-060]had exerted their influence uselessly—at eighty-nine conservative

[006-061]people seldom form radical new habits, and old John wore his “Sunday

[006-062]suit” of black broadcloth to [[the-last-ball|the Amberson ball]]. The coat was square,

[006-063]with skirts to the knees; old John called it a “Prince Albert” and was

[006-064]well enough pleased with it, but his great-nephew considered it the

[006-065]next thing to an insult. George’s purpose had been to ignore the man,

[006-066]but he had to take his hand for a moment; whereupon old John began to

[006-067]tell George that he was looking well, though there had been a time,

[006-068]during his fourth month, when he was so puny that nobody thought he

[006-069]would live. The great-nephew, in a fury of blushes, dropped old John’s

[006-070]hand with some vigour, and seized that of the next person in the line.

[006-071]“Member you v’ry well ’ndeed!” he said fiercely.

[006-072]The large room had filled, and so had the broad hall and the rooms

[006-073]on the other side of the hall, where there were tables for whist.

[006-074]The imported orchestra waited in the ballroom on the third floor,

[006-075]but a local harp, ’cello, violin, and flute were playing airs from

[006-076]“The Fencing Master” in the hall, and people were shouting over the

[006-077]music. Old John Minafer’s voice was louder and more penetrating than

[006-078]any other, because he had been troubled with deafness for twenty-five

[006-079]years, heard his own voice but faintly, and liked to hear it. “Smell o’

[006-080]flowers like this always puts me in mind o’ funerals,” he kept telling

[006-081]his niece, [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]], who was with him; and he seemed to get a

[006-082]great deal of satisfaction out of this reminder. His tremulous yet

[006-083]strident voice cut through the voluminous sound that filled the room,

[006-084]and he was heard everywhere: “Always got to think o’ funerals when I

[006-085]smell so many flowers!” And, as the pressure of people forced Fanny and

[006-086]himself against the white marble mantelpiece, he pursued this train

[006-087]of cheery thought, shouting, “Right here’s where the Major’s wife was

[006-088]laid out at her funeral. They had her in a good light from that big

[006-089]bow window.” He paused to chuckle mournfully. “I s’pose that’s where

[006-090]they’ll put the Major when his time comes.”

[006-091]Presently George’s mortification was increased to hear this sawmill

[006-092]droning harshly from the midst of the thickening crowd: “Ain’t the

[006-093]dancin’ broke out yet, Fanny? Hoopla! Le’s push through and go see the

[006-094]young women-folks crack their heels! Start the circus! Hoopse-daisy!”

[006-095]Miss Fanny Minafer, in charge of the lively veteran, was almost as

[006-096]distressed as her nephew George, but she did her duty and managed to

[006-097]get old John through the press and out to the broad stairway, which

[006-098]numbers of young people were now ascending to the ballroom. And here

[006-099]the sawmill voice still rose over all others: “Solid black walnut

[006-100]every inch of it, balustrades and all. Sixty thousand dollars’ worth

[006-101]o’ carved woodwork in the house! Like water! Spent [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] like water!

[006-102]Always did! Still do! Like water! God knows where it all comes from!”

[006-103]He continued the ascent, barking and coughing among the gleaming young

[006-104]heads, white shoulders, jewels, and chiffon, like an old dog slowly

[006-105]swimming up the rapids of a sparkling river; while down below, in the

[006-106]drawing room, George began to recover from the degradation into which

[006-107]this relic of early settler days had dragged him. What restored him

[006-108]completely was a dark-eyed little beauty of nineteen, very knowing in

[006-109]lustrous blue and jet; at sight of this dashing advent in the line of

[006-110]guests before him, George was fully an Amberson again.

[006-111]“Remember you very well _indeed!_” he said, his graciousness more

[006-112]earnest than any he had heretofore displayed. Isabel heard him and

[006-113]laughed.

[006-114]“But you don’t, George!” she said. “You don’t remember her yet, though

[006-115]of course you _will!_ Miss Morgan is from out of town, and I’m afraid

[006-116]this is the first time you’ve ever seen her. You might take her up to

[006-117]the dancing; I think you’ve pretty well done your duty here.”

[006-118]“Be d’lighted,” George responded formally, and offered his arm, not

[006-119]with a flourish, certainly, but with an impressiveness inspired partly

[006-120]by the appearance of the person to whom he offered it, partly by

[006-121]his being the hero of this fête, and partly by his youthfulness—for

[006-122]when manners are new they are apt to be elaborate. The little beauty

[006-123]entrusted her gloved fingers to his coat-sleeve, and they moved away

[006-124]together.

[006-125]Their progress was necessarily slow, and to George’s mind it did not

[006-126]lack stateliness. How could it? Musicians, hired especially for him,

[006-127]were sitting in a grove of palms in the hall and now tenderly playing

[006-128]“Oh, Promise Me” for his pleasuring; dozens and scores of flowers had

[006-129]been brought to life and tended to this hour that they might sweeten

[006-130]the air for him while they died; and the evanescent power that music

[006-131]and floral scents hold over youth stirred his appreciation of strange,

[006-132]beautiful qualities within his own bosom: he seemed to himself to be

[006-133]mysteriously angelic, and about to do something which would overwhelm

[006-134]the beautiful young stranger upon his arm.

[006-135]Elderly people and middle-aged people moved away to let him pass with

[006-136]his honoured fair beside him. Worthy middle-class creatures, they

[006-137]seemed, leading dull lives but appreciative of better things when they

[006-138]saw them—and George’s bosom was fleetingly touched with a pitying

[006-139]kindness. And since the primordial day when caste or heritage first

[006-140]set one person, in his own esteem, above his fellow-beings, it is to

[006-141]be doubted if anybody ever felt more illustrious, or more negligently

[006-142]grand, than [[george-amberson|George Amberson]] Minafer felt at this party.

[006-143]As he conducted Miss Morgan through the hall, toward the stairway, they

[006-144]passed the open double doors of a card room, where some squadrons of

[006-145]older people were preparing for action, and, leaning gracefully upon

[006-146]the mantelpiece of this room, a tall man, handsome, high-mannered,

[006-147]and sparklingly point-device, held laughing converse with that

[006-148]queer-looking duck, the Sharon girls’ uncle. The tall gentleman waved a

[006-149]gracious salutation to George, and Miss Morgan’s curiosity was stirred.

[006-150]“Who is that?”

[006-151]“I didn’t catch his name when my mother presented him to me,” said

[006-152]George. “You mean the queer-looking duck.”

[006-153]“I mean the aristocratic duck.”

[006-154]“That’s my Uncle George. Honourable George Amberson. I thought

[006-155]everybody knew him.”

[006-156]“He looks as though everybody ought to know him,” she said. “It seems

[006-157]to run in your family.”

[006-158]If she had any sly intention, it skipped over George harmlessly. “Well,

[006-159]of course, I suppose most everybody does,” he admitted—“out in this

[006-160]part of the country especially. Besides, Uncle George is in Congress;

[006-161]the family like to have someone there.”

[006-162]“Why?”

[006-163]“Well, it’s sort of a good thing in one way. For instance, my Uncle

[006-164][[sydney-amberson|Sydney Amberson]] and his wife, [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]], they haven’t got much of

[006-165]anything to do with themselves—get bored to death around here, of

[006-166]course. Well, probably Uncle George’ll have Uncle Sydney appointed

[006-167]minister or ambassador, or something like that, to Russia or Italy or

[006-168]somewhere, and that’ll make it pleasant when any of the rest of the

[006-169]family go travelling, or things like that. I expect to do a good deal

[006-170]of travelling myself when I get out of college.”

[006-171]On the stairway he pointed out this prospective ambassadorial couple,

[006-172]Sydney and Amelia. They were coming down, fronting the ascending tide,

[006-173]and as conspicuous over it as a king and queen in a play. Moreover,

[006-174]as the clear-eyed Miss Morgan remarked, the very least they looked

[006-175]was ambassadorial. Sydney was an Amberson exaggerated, more pompous

[006-176]than gracious; too portly, flushed, starched to a shine, his stately

[006-177]jowl furnished with an Edward the Seventh beard. Amelia, likewise

[006-178]full-bodied, showed glittering blond hair exuberantly dressed; a pink,

[006-179]fat face cold under a white-hot tiara; a solid, cold bosom under a

[006-180]white-hot necklace; great, cold, gloved arms, and the rest of her

[006-181]beautifully upholstered. Amelia was an Amberson born, herself, Sydney’s

[006-182]second-cousin: they had no children, and Sydney was without a business

[006-183]or a profession; thus both found a great deal of time to think about

[006-184]the appropriateness of their becoming Excellencies. And as George

[006-185]ascended the broad stairway, they were precisely the aunt and uncle

[006-186]he was most pleased to point out, to a girl from out of town, as his

[006-187]appurtenances in the way of relatives. At sight of them the grandeur of

[006-188][[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] family was instantly conspicuous as a permanent thing: it

[006-189]was impossible to doubt that the Ambersons were entrenched, in their

[006-190]nobility and riches, behind polished and glittering barriers which were

[006-191]as solid as they were brilliant, and would last.
