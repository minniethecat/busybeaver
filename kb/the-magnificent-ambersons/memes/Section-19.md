[019-001]“Wait a minute! My idea is—don’t forget it’s a confidential one, but

[019-002]I’m devilish right about it, young Georgie!—it’s this: [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] uses your

[019-003]mother for a decoy duck. She does everything in the world she can to

[019-004]keep your mother’s friendship with [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] going, because she thinks

[019-005]that’s what keeps Eugene about the place, so to speak. Fanny’s always

[019-006]with your mother, you see; and whenever he sees Isabel he sees Fanny.

[019-007]Fanny thinks he’ll get used to the idea of her being around, and some

[019-008]day her chance may come! You see, she’s probably afraid—perhaps she

[019-009]even knows, poor thing!—that she wouldn’t get to see much of Eugene if

[019-010]it weren’t for Isabel’s being such a friend of his. There! D’you see?”

[019-011]“Well—I suppose so.” George’s brow was still dark, however. “If you’re

[019-012]sure whatever talk there is, is about Aunt Fanny. If that’s so—”

[019-013]“Don’t be an ass,” his uncle advised him lightly, moving away. “I’m off

[019-014]for a week’s fishing to forget that woman in there, and her pig of a

[019-015]husband.” (His gesture toward the Mansion indicated Mr. and Mrs. [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]]

[019-016]Amberson.) “I recommend a like course to you, if you’re silly enough to

[019-017]pay any attention to such rubbishings! Good-bye!”

[019-018]George was partially reassured, but still troubled: a word haunted him

[019-019]like the recollection of a nightmare. “Talk!”

[019-020]He stood looking at the houses across the street from the Mansion; and

[019-021]though the sunshine was bright upon them, they seemed mysteriously

[019-022]threatening. He had always despised them, except the largest of them,

[019-023]which was the home of his henchman, Charlie Johnson. The Johnsons had

[019-024]originally owned a lot three hundred feet wide, but they had sold all

[019-025]of it except the meager frontage before the house itself, and five

[019-026]houses were now crowded into the space where one used to squire it so

[019-027]spaciously. Up and down the street, the same transformation had taken

[019-028]place: every big, comfortable old brick house now had two or three

[019-029]smaller frame neighbours crowding up to it on each side, cheap-looking

[019-030]neighbours, most of them needing paint and not clean—and yet, though

[019-031]they were cheap looking, they had cost as much to build as the big

[019-032]brick houses, whose former ample yards they occupied. Only where George

[019-033]stood was there left a sward as of yore; the great, level, green lawn

[019-034]that served for both the Major’s house and his daughter’s. This serene

[019-035]domain—unbroken, except for the two gravelled carriage-drives—alone

[019-036]remained as it had been during the early glories of [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]]

[019-037]Addition.

[019-038]George stared at the ugly houses opposite, and hated them more than

[019-039]ever; but he shivered. Perhaps the riffraff living in those houses sat

[019-040]at the windows to watch their betters; perhaps they dared to gossip—

[019-041]He uttered an exclamation, and walked rapidly toward his own front

[019-042]gate. The victoria had returned with Miss Fanny alone; she jumped out

[019-043]briskly and the victoria waited.

[019-044]“Where’s mother?” George asked sharply, as he met her.

[019-045]“At [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]’s. I only came back to get some embroidery, because we found

[019-046]the sun too hot for driving. I’m in a hurry.”

[019-047]But, going into the house with her, he detained her when she would have

[019-048]hastened upstairs.

[019-049]“I haven’t time to talk now, Georgie; I’m going right back. I promised

[019-050]your mother—”

[019-051]“You listen!” said George.

[019-052]“What on earth—”

[019-053]He repeated what Amelia had said. This time, however, he spoke coldly,

[019-054]and without the emotion he had exhibited during the recital to his

[019-055]uncle: Fanny was the one who showed agitation during this interview,

[019-056]for she grew fiery red, and her eyes dilated. “What on earth do you

[019-057]want to bring such trash to me for?” she demanded, breathing fast.

[019-058]“I merely wished to know two things: whether it is your duty or mine to

[019-059]speak to father of what [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]]—”

[019-060]Fanny stamped her foot. “You little fool!” she cried. “You awful little

[019-061]fool!”

[019-062]“I decline—”

[019-063]“Decline, my hat! Your father’s a sick man, and you—”

[019-064]“He doesn’t seem so to me.”

[019-065]“Well, he does to me! And you want to go troubling him with an Amberson

[019-066]family row! It’s just what that cat would love you to do!”

[019-067]“Well, I—”

[019-068]“Tell your father if you like! It will only make him a little sicker to

[019-069]think he’s got a son silly enough to listen to such craziness!”

[019-070]“Then you’re sure there isn’t any talk?” Fanny disdained a reply in

[019-071]words. She made a hissing sound of utter contempt and snapped her

[019-072]fingers. Then she asked scornfully: “What’s the other thing you wanted

[019-073]to know?”

[019-074]George’s pallor increased. “Whether it mightn’t be better, under the

[019-075]circumstances,” he said, “if this family were not so intimate with the

[019-076]Morgan family—at least for a time. It might be better—”

[019-077]Fanny stared at him incredulously. “You mean you’d quit seeing Lucy?”

[019-078]“I hadn’t thought of that side of it, but if such a thing were

[019-079]necessary on account of talk about my mother, I—I—” He hesitated

[019-080]unhappily. “I suggested that if all of us—for a time—perhaps only for a

[019-081]time—it might be better if—”

[019-082]“See here,” she interrupted. “We’ll settle this nonsense right now. If

[019-083]Eugene Morgan comes to this house, for instance, to see me, your mother

[019-084]can’t get up and leave the place the minute he gets here, can she? What

[019-085]do you want her to do: insult him? Or perhaps you’d prefer she’d insult

[019-086]Lucy? That would do just as well. What is it you’re up to, anyhow? Do

[019-087]you really love your Aunt Amelia so much that you want to please her?

[019-088]Or do you really hate your Aunt Fanny so much that you want to—that you

[019-089]want to—”

[019-090]She choked and sought for her handkerchief; suddenly she began to cry.

[019-091]“Oh, see here,” George said. “I don’t hate you,” Aunt Fanny. “That’s

[019-092]silly. I don’t—”

[019-093]“You do! You do! You want to—you want to destroy the only thing—that

[019-094]I—that I ever—” And, unable to continue, she became inaudible in her

[019-095]handkerchief.

[019-096]George felt remorseful, and his own troubles were lightened: all at

[019-097]once it became clear to him that he had been worrying about nothing.

[019-098]He perceived that his Aunt Amelia was indeed an old cat, and that to

[019-099]give her scandalous meanderings another thought would be the height of

[019-100]folly. By no means unsusceptible to such pathos as that now exposed

[019-101]before him, he did not lack pity for Fanny, whose almost spoken

[019-102]confession was lamentable; and he was granted the vision to understand

[019-103]that his mother also pitied Fanny infinitely more than he did. This

[019-104]seemed to explain everything.

[019-105]He patted the unhappy lady awkwardly upon her shoulder. “There, there!”

[019-106]he said. “I didn’t mean anything. Of course the only thing to do about

[019-107]Aunt Amelia is to pay no attention to her. It’s all right, Aunt Fanny.

[019-108]Don’t cry. I feel a lot better now, myself. Come on; I’ll drive back

[019-109]there with you. It’s all over, and nothing’s the matter. Can’t you

[019-110]cheer up?”

[019-111]Fanny cheered up; and presently the customarily hostile aunt and

[019-112]nephew were driving out [[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]] amiably together in the hot

[019-113]sunshine.

Chapter XIV

[019-114]“Almost” was Lucy’s last word on the last night of George’s

[019-115]vacation—that vital evening which she had half consented to agree

[019-116]upon for “settling things” between them. “Almost engaged,” she meant.

[019-117]And George, discontented with the “almost,” but contented that she

[019-118]seemed glad to wear a sapphire locket with a tiny photograph of George

[019-119]Amberson Minafer inside it, found himself wonderful in a new world at

[019-120]the final instant of their parting. For, after declining to let him

[019-121]kiss her “good-bye,” as if his desire for such a ceremony were the most

[019-122]preposterous absurdity in the world, she had leaned suddenly close to

[019-123]him and left upon his cheek the veriest feather from a fairy’s wing.

[019-124]She wrote him a month later:

[019-125]No. It must keep on being almost.

[019-126]Isn’t almost pretty pleasant? You know well enough that I care

[019-127]for you. I did from the first minute I saw you, and I’m pretty

[019-128]sure you knew it—I’m afraid you did. I’m afraid you always knew

[019-129]it. I’m not conventional and cautious about being engaged, as you

[019-130]say I am, dear. (I always read over the “dears” in your letters

[019-131]a time or two, as you say you do in mine—only I read all of your

[019-132]letters a time or two!) But it’s such a solemn thing it scares

[019-133]me. It means a good deal to a lot of people besides you and me,

[019-134]and that scares me, too. You write that I take your feeling for

[019-135]me “too lightly” and that I “take the whole affair too lightly.”

[019-136]Isn’t that odd! Because to myself I seem to take it as something

[019-137]so much more solemn than you do. I shouldn’t be a bit surprised

[019-138]to find myself an old lady, some day, still thinking of you—while

[019-139]you’d be away and away with somebody else perhaps, and me forgotten

[019-140]ages ago! “Lucy Morgan,” you’d say, when you saw my obituary. “Lucy

[019-141]Morgan? Let me see: I seem to remember the name. Didn’t I know some

[019-142]Lucy Morgan or other, once upon a time?” Then you’d shake your

[019-143]big white head and stroke your long white beard—you’d have such a

[019-144]distinguished long white beard! and you’d say, ‘No. I don’t seem to

[019-145]remember any Lucy Morgan; I wonder what made me think I did?’ And

[019-146]poor me! I’d be deep in the ground, wondering if you’d heard about

[019-147]it and what you were saying! Good-bye for to-day. Don’t work too

[019-148]hard—dear!

[019-149]George immediately seized pen and paper, plaintively but vigorously

[019-150]requesting Lucy not to imagine him with a beard, distinguished or

[019-151]otherwise, even in the extremities of age. Then, after inscribing his

[019-152]protest in the matter of this visioned beard, he concluded his missive

[019-153]in a tone mollified to tenderness, and proceeded to read a letter from

[019-154]his mother which had reached him simultaneously with Lucy’s. Isabel

[019-155]wrote from Asheville, where she had just arrived with her husband.

[019-156]I think your father looks better already, darling, though we’ve

[019-157]been here only a few hours. It may be we’ve found just the place

[019-158]to build him up. The doctors said they hoped it would prove to be,

[019-159]and if it is, it would be worth the long struggle we had with him

[019-160]to get him to give up and come. Poor dear man, he was so blue,

[019-161]not about his health but about giving up the worries down at his

[019-162]office and forgetting them for a time—if he only will forget them!

[019-163]It took the pressure of the family and all his best friends, to

[019-164]get him to come—but father and brother George and Fanny and Eugene

[019-165]Morgan all kept at him so constantly that he just had to give in.

[019-166]I’m afraid that in my anxiety to get him to do what the doctors

[019-167]wanted him to, I wasn’t able to back up brother George as I should

[019-168]in his difficulty with Sydney and Amelia. I’m so sorry! George is

[019-169]more upset than I’ve ever seen him—they’ve got what they wanted,

[019-170]and they’re sailing before long, I hear, to live in Florence.

[019-171]Father said he couldn’t stand the constant persuading—I’m afraid

[019-172]the word he used was “nagging.” I can’t understand people behaving

[019-173]like that. George says they may be Ambersons, but they’re vulgar!

[019-174]I’m afraid I almost agree with him. At least, I think they were

[019-175]inconsiderate. But I don’t see why I’m unburdening myself of all

[019-176]this to you, poor darling! We’ll have forgotten all about it long

[019-177]before you come home for the holidays, and it should mean little or

[019-178]nothing to you, anyway. Forget that I’ve been so foolish!

[019-179]Your father is waiting for me to take a walk with him—that’s a

[019-180]splendid sign, because he hasn’t felt he could walk much, at

[019-181]home, lately. I mustn’t keep him waiting. Be careful to wear your

[019-182]mackintosh and rubbers in rainy weather, and, as soon as it begins

[019-183]to get colder, your ulster. Wish you could see your father now.

[019-184]Looks so much better! We plan to stay six weeks if the place agrees

[019-185]with him. It does really seem to already! He’s just called in the

[019-186]door to say he’s waiting. Don’t smoke too much, darling boy.
