## Role in the Novel

Major Amberson is the founding patriarch of the Amberson family and the architect of its magnificence. His fortune, his mansion, and his name define the social world of Midland for a generation — and his decline, both physical and financial, traces the novel's central arc of decay. He is at once a figure of genuine achievement and a symbol of the fatal Amberson blindness: the conviction that what has been built will last, that what has been earned need never be defended, and that change is something that happens to other people.

The novel opens with the Major's defining act. "Major Amberson had 'made a fortune' in 1873, when other people were losing fortunes, and the magnificence of the Ambersons began then" (001-001). This single sentence establishes both his acumen and his timing — he was a man who saw opportunity where others saw catastrophe. A veteran of the Civil War who came home invalided after the Gettysburg campaign and went into business (039-084 to 039-085), the Major embodied the postbellum American archetype of the self-made man. His military title, earned in the defining national conflict of his generation, gave his civilian achievements an additional patina of authority.

His great material creation was Amberson Addition: two hundred acres at the end of National Avenue, transformed into broad streets paved with cedar block, adorned with fountains and a gallery of cast-iron statuary — Minerva, Mercury, Hercules, Venus, and others — that the local newspapers proudly declared "Versailles outdone" (002-062 to 002-076). At the heart of the Addition he reserved four acres for himself and built the Amberson Mansion, a house of "arches and turrets and girdling stone porches" with the first porte-cochere in town, a ballroom on the third story, and sixty thousand dollars in black walnut woodwork alone (002-083 to 002-091). The Mansion became "the pride of the town" and the obligatory climax of any citizen's tour of the city (002-103 to 002-107).

But the Major's greatness contained the seeds of its own destruction. He kept his own books and refused to share his financial information with anyone, including his son. He "still refuses to let the Mansion be torn up for wiring" (037-089), working by an old gas drop-light while the world electrified around him. His final business decision — building five new houses on the Mansion's lawn when the market had shifted to apartment houses — was a catastrophic miscalculation. "He guessed wrong," his son said, "He guessed wrong at just the wrong time!" (037-083 to 037-084). The Major's lifelong habit of solitary control had left him without counsel when judgment began to fail.

## Relationships

The Major's family relationships define his character and, through inheritance, the fate of the Amberson line.

**Isabel Amberson (daughter)**: The Major's closest bond. When Isabel marries Wilbur Minafer, the Major builds her a house as a wedding gift — a miniature version of the Mansion, "only not as large and hasn't got a regular ballroom" (010-060 to 010-062). When Isabel returns from Europe mortally ill, the Major — "his figure, after all its years of proud soldierliness, had grown stooping at last, and his untrimmed white hair straggled over the back of his collar" (038-126 to 038-128) — shuffles forward on a shaking cane, querulously asking, "When are they going to let me see my daughter?" (038-120). Their reunion behind the closed door of her sickroom is the novel's most tender moment of paternal love.

After Isabel's death, the Major "remained dry-eyed through the time that followed: he knew that this separation from his daughter would be short, that the separation which had preceded it was the long one" (039-063 to 039-065). He stops working at his ledgers entirely and sits staring into the fire, not speaking. The family mistakes this for dazed grief, but "they were mistaken. The Major was engaged in the profoundest thinking of his life" — planning how to enter the unknown country "where he was not even sure of being recognized as an Amberson" (039-077 to 039-081).

**George Amberson Minafer (grandson)**: The Major's only grandchild, and the repository of all his dynastic hopes. Citizens point out the boy with the showman's announcement: "Major Amberson's only grandchild!" (004-073). The Major spoils George thoroughly, laughing when the boy orders the stable-men about and calling from the window, "That's right, Georgie. Make those lazy rascals jump!" (047-113 to 047-114). In the Major's final months, he "seemed to like best to have young George with him" (040-175), and it is to George that he delivers his last fragment of cosmic speculation. The inheritance George receives from his grandfather — not wealth, which has vanished, but pride, isolation, and the refusal to adapt — proves fatal.

**George Amberson (son)**: The Major's charming but feckless son, a man of "altogether gallant in spirit, and beautifully game under catastrophe" (040-068). The son's perpetual business failures — the electric headlight scheme being only the last — stand in sharp contrast to the father's fortune-building, yet the Major finances them all without demanding accountability, a pattern of enabling that extends across the family.

**Sydney and Amelia Amberson**: The Major's other children, living abroad in Italy. When the estate is settled, they unhesitatingly claim their legal third, stripping what little remains. Their polished refusal to help the destitute George Amberson Minafer provides one of the novel's bitterest revelations about the hollowness of Amberson family solidarity (041-034 to 041-036).

## Key Scenes

**The Fortune Made (Chapter 1)**: The novel opens with the Major's foundational act — making a fortune in the panic year of 1873. The narrator's tone mingles admiration with elegy, establishing the Major as both hero of a vanished age and unwitting architect of a doomed dynasty (001-001 to 001-006).

**The Amberson Addition and Mansion (Chapter 2)**: The physical monument to the Major's ambition. The Addition's fountains, statues, and cedar-block pavement, crowned by the sixty-thousand-dollar Mansion, represent everything the Amberson name means in Midland — and everything that will be eroded, vandalized, and eventually erased (002-062 to 002-102).

**The Serenade and the Dressing-Gown (Chapter 3)**: A revealing domestic scene. When one of his sons comes home incapacitated after a serenade, "the Major going down in a dressing-gown and slippers to bring him in, and scolding mildly, while imperfectly concealing strong impulses to laughter" (003-022 to 003-024). This is the Major as an indulgent father — warm, amused, incapable of real severity — and the moment foreshadows the family-wide inability to impose discipline that will doom the next generation.

**Isabel's Return (Chapter 29)**: The Major's last meeting with his daughter. His querulous demand — "When are they going to let me see my daughter? ... I think she wants to see me" (038-120 to 038-123) — strips away the patriarchal dignity to reveal a frightened old man. The physical description — stooping, white-haired, shuffling on a cane — is the novel's most vivid portrait of what the years have done to him.

**The Death and the Vanished Estate (Chapters 30-31)**: The Major's death occupies the slimmest of narrative space. His final words are a haunting cosmological fragment:

> "It must be in the sun," he said. "There wasn't anything here but the sun in the first place, and the earth came out of the sun, and we came out of the earth. So, whatever we are, we must have been in the sun. We go back to the earth we came out of, so the earth will go back to the sun that it came out of. And time means nothing — nothing at all — so in a little while we'll all be back in the sun together. I wish — I wish — somebody could tell me!"
> (041-004 to 041-017)

He dies the next morning, alone, dressed in his chair. Old Sam, the family's black servant, "could see instantly that the Major was not there" (041-026). The chapter break that follows delivers the novel's cruelest irony in the language of probate:

> When the great Amberson Estate went into court for settlement, "there wasn't any," George Amberson said — that is, when the settlement was concluded there was no estate.
> (041-027 to 041-029)

The man who built a fortune, a mansion, and a name had, in the end, built on air.

## Themes

The Major embodies the novel's central thesis: that all magnificence is temporary and all fortunes comparative. The opening line's quiet qualification — "Magnificence, like the size of a fortune, is always comparative" (001-003) — prefigures the Major's arc entirely. In the Midland of 1873 he was a titan; by the Midland of 1906 he was an irrelevance.

His military title is itself a thematic device. The Major earned his rank in the Civil War — the one great national convulsion that this novel never directly narrates — and his fortune in its economic aftermath. He represents a generation that built, conquered, and assumed permanence. The irony is that the war that defined him was fought over precisely the kind of ossified social order — an aristocracy built on inherited wealth and inherited assumptions — that the Ambersons came to replicate in miniature.

The Major's most telling characteristic is his solitude. He does his own figuring, keeps his own books, trusts no one with his affairs. This self-reliance, which in his prime was the cornerstone of his success, becomes in his decline the mechanism of his family's destruction. Because no one knew the true state of the Amberson finances, no one could arrest the drift. The estate was "as involved and mixed-up as an estate can well get" (040-161), and the Major's death left behind only questions his ledgers could not answer.

In the novel's final pages, George Amberson Minafer, lying in a hospital bed with both legs broken, remembers his grandfather: "'As rich as Major Amberson!' they used to say" (047-103 to 047-104). But the phrase has been rewritten by time. Now it is Eugene Morgan — the inventor, the automobile manufacturer, the man who built the new kind of house in the new kind of world — whose name replaces the Major's in the civic vocabulary. The Major's fortune, like his statues and his Mansion and his name on the street signs, has been swept into the oblivion that awaits "where there is growth" (047-128).
