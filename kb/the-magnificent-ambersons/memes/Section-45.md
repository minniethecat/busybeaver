[045-001]And once, as he passed the one or two young men of the place

[045-002]entertaining the three or four young women, who were elbowing and

[045-003]jerking on a settee in the lobby, he heard a voice inquiring quickly,

[045-004]as he passed:

[045-005]“What makes people tired?”

[045-006]“Work?”

[045-007]“No.”

[045-008]“Well, what’s the answer?”

[045-009]Then, with an intentional outbreak of mirth, the answer was given by

[045-010]two loudly whispering voices together:

[045-011]“A stuck-up boarder!”

[045-012]George didn’t care.

[045-013]On Sunday mornings [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] went to church and George took long walks. He

[045-014]explored the new city, and found it hideous, especially in the early

[045-015]spring, before the leaves of the shade trees were out. Then the town

[045-016]was fagged with the long winter and blacked with the heavier smoke that

[045-017]had been held close to the earth by the smoke-fog it bred. Every-thing

[045-018]was damply streaked with the soot: the walls of the houses, inside and

[045-019]out, the gray curtains at the windows, the windows themselves, the

[045-020]dirty cement and unswept asphalt underfoot, the very sky overhead.

[045-021]Throughout this murky season he continued his explorations, never

[045-022]seeing a face he knew—for, on Sunday, those whom he remembered, or who

[045-023]might remember him, were not apt to be found within the limits of the

[045-024]town, but were congenially occupied with the new outdoor life which

[045-025]had come to be the mode since his boyhood. He and Fanny were pretty

[045-026]thoroughly buried away within the bigness of the city.

[045-027]One of his Sunday walks, that spring, he made into a sour pilgrimage.

[045-028]It was a misty morning of belated snow slush, and suited him to a

[045-029]perfection of miserableness, as he stood before the great dripping

[045-030]department store which now occupied the big plot of ground where once

[045-031]had stood both [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Hotel and the Amberson Opera House. From

[045-032]there he drifted to the old “Amberson Block,” but this was fallen into

[045-033]a back-water; business had stagnated here. The old structure had not

[045-034]been replaced, but a cavernous entryway for trucks had been torn in

[045-035]its front, and upon the cornice, where the old separate metal letters

[045-036]had spelt “Amberson Block,” there was a long billboard sign: “Doogan

[045-037]Storage.”

[045-038]To spare himself nothing, he went out [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] and saw the piles

[045-039]of slush-covered wreckage where the Mansion and his mother’s house had

[045-040]been, and where the Major’s ill-fated five “new” houses had stood;

[045-041]for these were down, too, to make room for the great tenement already

[045-042]shaped in unending lines of foundation. But the Fountain of Neptune was

[045-043]gone at last—and George was glad that it was!

[045-044]He turned away from the devastated site, thinking bitterly that the

[045-045]only Amberson mark still left upon the town was the name of the

[045-046]boulevard—[[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]]. But he had reckoned without the city

[045-047]council of the new order, and by an unpleasant coincidence, while the

[045-048]thought was still in his mind, his eye fell upon a metal oblong sign

[045-049]upon the lamppost at the corner. There were two of these little signs

[045-050]upon the lamp-post, at an obtuse angle to each other, one to give

[045-051]passers-by the name of National Avenue, the other to acquaint them with

[045-052]Amberson Boulevard. But the one upon which should have been stenciled

[045-053]“Amberson Boulevard” exhibited the words “Tenth Street.”

[045-054]George stared at it hard. Then he walked quickly along the boulevard to

[045-055]the next corner and looked at the little sign there. “Tenth Street.”

[045-056]It had begun to rain, but George stood unheeding, staring at the little

[045-057]sign. “Damn them!” he said finally, and, turning up his coat-collar,

[045-058]plodded back through the soggy streets toward “home.”

[045-059]The utilitarian impudence of the city authorities put a thought into

[045-060]his mind. A week earlier he had happened to stroll into the large

[045-061]parlour of the apartment house, finding it empty, and on the center

[045-062]table he noticed a large, red-bound, gilt-edged book, newly printed,

[045-063]bearing the title: “A Civic History,” and beneath the title, the

[045-064]rubric, “Biographies of the 500 Most Prominent Citizens and Families

[045-065]in the History of the City.” He had glanced at it absently, merely

[045-066]noticing the title and sub-title, and wandered out of the room,

[045-067]thinking of other things and feeling no curiosity about the book.

[045-068]But he had thought of it several times since with a faint, vague

[045-069]uneasiness; and now when he entered the lobby he walked directly into

[045-070]the parlour where he had seen the book. The room was empty, as it

[045-071]always was on Sunday mornings, and the flamboyant volume was still upon

[045-072]the table—evidently a fixture as a sort of local Almanach de Gotha, or

[045-073]Burke, for the enlightenment of tenants and boarders.

[045-074]He opened it, finding a few painful steel engravings of placid,

[045-075]chin-bearded faces, some of which he remembered dimly; but much

[045-076]more numerous, and also more unfamiliar to him, were the pictures

[045-077]of neat, aggressive men, with clipped short hair and clipped short

[045-078]moustaches—almost all of them strangers to him. He delayed not long

[045-079]with these, but turned to the index where the names of the five hundred

[045-080]Most Prominent Citizens and Families in the History of the City were

[045-081]arranged in alphabetical order, and ran his finger down the column of

[045-082]A’s:

[045-083]Abbett Adams

[045-084]Abbott Adams

[045-085]Abrams Adler

[045-086]Akers Andrews

[045-087]Albertsmeyer Appenbasch

[045-088]Alexander Archer

[045-089]Allen Arszman

[045-090]Ambrose Ashcraft

[045-091]Ambuhl Austin

[045-092]Anderson Avey

[045-093]George’s eyes remained for some time fixed on the thin space between

[045-094]the names “Allen” and “Ambrose.” Then he closed the book quietly, and

[045-095]went up to his own room, agreeing with the elevator boy, on the way,

[045-096]that it was getting to be a mighty nasty wet and windy day outside.

[045-097]The elevator boy noticed nothing unusual about him and neither did

[045-098]Fanny, when she came in from church with her hat ruined, an hour later.

[045-099]And yet something had happened—a thing which, years ago, had been the

[045-100]eagerest hope of many, many good citizens of the town. They had thought

[045-101]of it, longed for it, hoping acutely that they might live to see the

[045-102]day when it would come to pass. And now it had happened at last:

[045-103][[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]] had got his come-upance.

[045-104]He had got it three times filled and running over. The city had rolled

[045-105]over his heart, burying it under, as it rolled over the Major’s and

[045-106]buried it under. The city had rolled over the Ambersons and buried them

[045-107]under to the last vestige; and it mattered little that George guessed

[045-108]easily enough that most of the five hundred Most Prominent had paid

[045-109]something substantial “to defray the cost of steel engraving, etc.”—the

[045-110]Five Hundred had heaved the final shovelful of soot upon that heap

[045-111]of obscurity wherein the Ambersons were lost forever from sight and

[045-112]history. “Quicksilver in a nest of cracks!”

[045-113]Georgie Minafer had got his come-upance, but the people who had so

[045-114]longed for it were not there to see it, and they never knew it. Those

[045-115]who were still living had forgotten all about it and all about him.

Chapter XXXIV

[045-116]There was one border section of the city which George never explored

[045-117]in his Sunday morning excursions. This was far out to the north where

[045-118]lay the new Elysian Fields of the millionaires, though he once went

[045-119]as far in that direction as the white house which [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] had so admired

[045-120]long ago—her “Beautiful House.” George looked at it briefly and turned

[045-121]back, rumbling with an interior laugh of some grimness. The house was

[045-122]white no longer; nothing could be white which the town had reached, and

[045-123]the town reached far beyond the beautiful white house now. The owners

[045-124]had given up and painted it a despairing chocolate, suitable to the

[045-125]freight-yard life it was called upon to endure.

[045-126]George did not again risk going even so far as that, in the direction

[045-127]of the millionaires, although their settlement began at least two miles

[045-128]farther out. His thought of Lucy and her father was more a sensation

[045-129]than a thought, and may be compared to that of a convicted cashier

[045-130]beset by recollections of the bank he had pillaged—there are some

[045-131]thoughts to which one closes the mind. George had seen [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] only once

[045-132]since their calamitous encounter. They had passed on opposite sides

[045-133]of the street, downtown; each had been aware of the other, and each

[045-134]had been aware that the other was aware of him, and yet each kept his

[045-135]eyes straight forward, and neither had shown a perceptible alteration

[045-136]of countenance. It seemed to George that he felt emanating from the

[045-137]outwardly imperturbable person of his mother’s old friend a hate that

[045-138]was like a hot wind.

[045-139]At his mother’s funeral and at the Major’s he had been conscious that

[045-140]Eugene was there: though he had afterward no recollection of seeing

[045-141]him, and, while certain of his presence, was uncertain how he knew

[045-142]of it. Fanny had not told him, for she understood George well enough

[045-143]not to speak to him of Eugene or Lucy. Nowadays Fanny almost never

[045-144]saw either of them and seldom thought of them—so sly is the way of

[045-145]time with life. She was passing middle age, when old intensities and

[045-146]longings grow thin and flatten out, as Fanny herself was thinning and

[045-147]flattening out; and she was settling down contentedly to her apartment

[045-148]house intimacies. She was precisely suited by the table-d’hote life,

[045-149]with its bridge, its variable alliances and shifting feuds, and the

[045-150]long whisperings of elderly ladies at corridor corners—those eager but

[045-151]suppressed conversations, all sibilance, of which the elevator boy

[045-152]declared he heard the words “she said” a million times and the word

[045-153]“she,” five million. The apartment house suited Fanny and swallowed her.

[045-154]The city was so big, now, that people disappeared into it unnoticed,

[045-155]and the disappearance of Fanny and her nephew was not exceptional.

[045-156]People no longer knew their neighbours as a matter of course; one lived

[045-157]for years next door to strangers—that sharpest of all the changes since

[045-158]the old days—and a friend would lose sight of a friend for a year, and

[045-159]not know it.

[045-160]One May day George thought he had a glimpse of Lucy. He was not

[045-161]certain, but he was sufficiently disturbed, in spite of his

[045-162]uncertainty. A promotion in his work now frequently took him out of

[045-163]town for a week, or longer, and it was upon his return from one of

[045-164]these absences that he had the strange experience. He had walked

[045-165]home from the station, and as he turned the corner which brought him

[045-166]in sight of the apartment house entrance, though two blocks distant

[045-167]from it, he saw a charming little figure come out, get into a shiny

[045-168]landaulet automobile, and drive away. Even at that distance no one

[045-169]could have any doubt that the little figure was charming; and the

[045-170]height, the quickness and decision of motion, even the swift gesture

[045-171]of a white glove toward the chauffeur—all were characteristic of Lucy.

[045-172]George was instantly subjected to a shock of indefinable nature,

[045-173]yet definitely a shock: he did not know what he felt—but he knew

[045-174]that he felt. Heat surged over him: probably he would not have come

[045-175]face to face with her if the restoration of all the ancient Amberson

[045-176]magnificence could have been his reward. He went on slowly, his knees

[045-177]shaky.

[045-178]But he found Fanny not at home; she had been out all afternoon; and

[045-179]there was no record of any caller—and he began to wonder, then to doubt

[045-180]if the small lady he had seen in the distance was Lucy. It might as

[045-181]well have been, he said to himself—since any one who looked like her

[045-182]could give him “a jolt like that!”

[045-183]Lucy had not left a card. She never left one when she called on Fanny;

[045-184]though she did not give her reasons a quite definite form in her own

[045-185]mind. She came seldom; this was but the third time that year, and,

[045-186]when she did come, George was not mentioned either by her hostess or

[045-187]by herself—an oddity contrived between the two ladies without either

[045-188]of them realizing how odd it was. For, naturally, while Fanny was

[045-189]with Lucy, Fanny thought of George, and what time Lucy had George’s

[045-190]aunt before her eyes she could not well avoid the thought of him.

[045-191]Consequently, both looked absent-minded as they talked, and each often

[045-192]gave a wrong answer which the other consistently failed to notice.
