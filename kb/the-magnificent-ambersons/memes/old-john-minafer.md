# Old John Minafer

Old John Minafer was the eighty-nine-year-old great-uncle of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]], a member of the Minafer side of the family who served as a comic foil at the grand occasion of [[the-last-ball|the Last Ball]]. His presence at the family event highlighted the divide between the pioneer generation's thrift and the Amberson side's taste for magnificence.

## The Great-Uncle at the Ball

At the Amberson ball in the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]], old John Minafer appeared as a distinctly uncomfortable figure. George's great-uncle made it his lifelong boast that, in spite of his connection by marriage with the Ambersons, he never had worn and never would wear a swallow-tail coat (006-057). At age eighty-nine, conservative people seldom form radical new habits, and old John wore his Sunday suit of black broadcloth to the event, a "Prince Albert" coat with skirts to the knees (006-062). His great-nephew George considered this attire the next thing to an insult.

## A Voice That Cut Through

Old John Minafer's deafness, from which he had suffered for twenty-five years, meant that he heard his own voice only faintly and therefore liked to hear it (006-078). His strident voice cut through the voluminous sound of the crowded hall, and he was heard everywhere. He took a peculiar satisfaction in reminding his niece, Fanny Minafer, that the smell of flowers always put him in mind of funerals, and he pointed out the precise spot where the Major's wife had been laid out at her funeral (006-087). He paused to observe, with mournful cheer, that the same spot would probably serve for the Major when his time came (006-089).

## Departure and Death

Old John Minafer left the festivities early. At the strains of "La Paloma," he was evidently surfeited and announced his intention to depart, declaring that he would rather see a jig any day in the world than this modern sliding about (007-006). He died the previous autumn, and his grave lay beside those of George's grandfather and grandmother Minafer, and of his grandfather Minafer's second wife and her three sons, George's half-uncles, who had been burned in the great railway accident in 1872 (020-060).
