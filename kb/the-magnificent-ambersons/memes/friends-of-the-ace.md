# Friends of the Ace

The Friends of the Ace (F.O.T.A.) was a secret boys' club founded by [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]] and housed on the fourth floor of [[the-amberson-block|the Amberson Block]] in downtown Midland. The club served as an early showcase of the young Amberson heir's domineering personality and his reliance on the family name and property to assert authority over his peers.

## Founding and Headquarters

George Amberson Minafer founded the F.O.T.A. and obtained the clubroom rent-free from his grandfather, [[major-amberson|Major Amberson]]. He also secured furniture from the janitor and planned further expansions, including an extra room across the hall and a billiard table from his Uncle George. The clubroom served as a gathering place for a circle of Midland boys who enacted elaborate ceremonies under the title of "Friends of the Ace."

## Rituals and Rivalry

The club's proceedings opened with a ceremonial greeting: "Welcome, Friend of the Ace" (005-001). The presiding officer, in lieu of a gavel, wielded a Civil War relic known as a "horse-pistol" that belonged to Major Amberson. During George's absence at school, the members elected [[fred-kinney-jr|Fred Kinney Jr.]] as president, a development that provoked an immediate confrontation upon George's return. George burst into a meeting, challenged Kinney's authority, and reclaimed control by reminding the members that he had founded the club and controlled the room through his grandfather's ownership of the building (005-044). The boys also had a side interest in card-playing, specifically euchre at a nickel a corner.

## Membership and Later Years

The core membership included [[charlie-johnson|Charlie Johnson]], who served as a peacemaker during the presidential dispute, and Fred Kinney Jr., who attempted to hold the presidency in George's absence. Years later, long after the club had dissolved and the Amberson fortune had vanished, the former Friends of the Ace, once bound by oath to succour one another, had largely forgotten their youthful pledges (042-066). The fate of the club mirrored the larger arc of George Amberson Minafer's life: a boyhood of entitled command followed by a manhood of obscurity.
