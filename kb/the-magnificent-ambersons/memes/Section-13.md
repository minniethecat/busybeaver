[013-001]Georgie had carried out his rehearsed projects with precision, he had

[013-002]given Miss Morgan a nod studied into perfection during his lengthy

[013-003]toilet before dinner. “Oh, yes, I do seem to remember that curious

[013-004]little outsider!” this nod seemed to say. Thereafter, all cognizance of

[013-005]her evaporated: the curious little outsider was permitted no further

[013-006]existence worth the struggle. Nevertheless, she flashed in the corner

[013-007]of his eye too often. He was aware of her dancing demurely, and of

[013-008]her viciously flirtatious habit of never looking up at her partner,

[013-009]but keeping her eyes concealed beneath downcast lashes; and he had

[013-010]over-sufficient consciousness of her between the dances, though it

[013-011]was not possible to see her at these times, even if he had cared to

[013-012]look frankly in her direction—she was invisible in a thicket of young

[013-013]dresscoats. The black thicket moved as she moved and her location was

[013-014]hatefully apparent, even if he had not heard her voice laughing from

[013-015]the thicket. It was annoying how her voice, though never loud, pursued

[013-016]him. No matter how vociferous were other voices, all about, he seemed

[013-017]unable to prevent himself from constantly recognizing hers. It had a

[013-018]quaver in it, not pathetic—rather humorous than pathetic—a quality

[013-019]which annoyed him to the point of rage, because it was so difficult to

[013-020]get away from. She seemed to be having a “wonderful time!”

[013-021]An unbearable soreness accumulated in his chest: his dislike of the

[013-022]girl and her conduct increased until he thought of leaving this

[013-023]sickening Assembly and going home to bed. _That_ would show her! But

[013-024]just then he heard her laughing, and decided that it wouldn’t show her.

[013-025]So he remained.

[013-026]When the young couples seated themselves in chairs against the walls,

[013-027]round three sides of the room, for the cotillion, George joined

[013-028]a brazen-faced group clustering about the doorway—youths with no

[013-029]partners, yet eligible to be “called out” and favoured. He marked that

[013-030]his uncle placed the infernal Kinney and Miss Morgan, as the leading

[013-031]couple, in the first chairs at the head of the line upon the leader’s

[013-032]right; and this disloyalty on the part of [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] was inexcusable,

[013-033]for in the family circle the nephew had often expressed his opinion

[013-034]of Fred Kinney. In his bitterness, George uttered a significant

[013-035]monosyllable.

[013-036]The music flourished; whereupon Mr. Kinney, Miss Morgan, and six of

[013-037]their neighbours rose and waltzed knowingly. Mr. Amberson’s whistle

[013-038]blew; then the eight young people went to the favour-table and were

[013-039]given toys and trinkets wherewith to delight the new partners it

[013-040]was now their privilege to select. Around the walls, the seated

[013-041]non-participants in this ceremony looked rather conscious; some

[013-042]chattered, endeavouring not to appear expectant; some tried not to

[013-043]look wistful; and others were frankly solemn. It was a trying moment;

[013-044]and whoever secured a favour, this very first shot, might consider the

[013-045]portents happy for a successful evening.

[013-046]Holding their twinkling gewgaws in their hands, those about to

[013-047]bestow honour came toward the seated lines, where expressions became

[013-048]feverish. Two of the approaching girls seemed to wander, not finding a

[013-049]predetermined object in sight; and these two were Janie Sharon, and her

[013-050]cousin, [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]. At this, [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]], conceiving that he had

[013-051]little to anticipate from either, turned a proud back upon the room and

[013-052]affected to converse with his friend, Mr. Charlie Johnson.

[013-053]The next moment a quick little figure intervened between the two. It

[013-054]was Lucy, gaily offering a silver sleighbell decked with white ribbon.

[013-055]“I almost couldn’t find you!” she cried.

[013-056]George stared, took her hand, led her forth in silence, danced

[013-057]with her. She seemed content not to talk; but as the whistle blew,

[013-058]signalling that this episode was concluded, and he conducted her to her

[013-059]seat, she lifted the little bell toward him. “You haven’t taken your

[013-060]favour. You’re supposed to pin it on your coat,” she said. “Don’t you

[013-061]want it?”

[013-062]“If you insist!” said George stiffly. And he bowed her into her chair;

[013-063]then turned and walked away, dropping the sleighbell haughtily into his

[013-064]trousers’ pocket.

[013-065]The figure proceeded to its conclusion, and George was given other

[013-066]sleighbells, which he easily consented to wear upon his lapel; but, as

[013-067]the next figure began, he strolled with a bored air to the tropical

[013-068]grove, where sat his elders, and seated himself beside his Uncle

[013-069][[sydney-amberson|Sydney]]. His mother leaned across Miss [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]], raising her voice over the

[013-070]music to speak to him.

[013-071]“Georgie, nobody will be able to see you here. You’ll not be favoured.

[013-072]You ought to be where you can dance.”

[013-073]“Don’t care to,” he returned. “Bore!”

[013-074]“But you ought—” She stopped and laughed, waving her fan to direct his

[013-075]attention behind him. “Look! Over your shoulder!”

[013-076]He turned, and discovered Miss Lucy Morgan in the act of offering him a

[013-077]purple toy balloon.

[013-078]“I found you!” she laughed.

[013-079]George was startled. “Well—” he said.

[013-080]“Would you rather ‘sit it out?’” Lucy asked quickly, as he did not

[013-081]move. “I don’t care to dance if you—”

[013-082]“No,” he said, rising. “It would be better to dance.” His tone was

[013-083]solemn, and solemnly he departed with her from the grove. Solemnly he

[013-084]danced with her.

[013-085]Four times, with not the slightest encouragement, she brought him a

[013-086]favour: four times in succession. When the fourth came, “Look here!”

[013-087]said George huskily. “You going to keep this up all night? What do you

[013-088]mean by it?”

[013-089]For an instant she seemed confused. “That’s what cotillions are for,

[013-090]aren’t they?” she murmured.

[013-091]“What do you mean: what they’re for?”

[013-092]“So that a girl can dance with a person she wants to?”

[013-093]George’s huskiness increased. “Well, do you mean you—you want to dance

[013-094]with me all the time—all evening?”

[013-095]“Well, this much of it—evidently!” she laughed.

[013-096]“Is it because you thought I tried to keep you from getting hurt this

[013-097]afternoon when we upset?”

[013-098]She shook her head.

[013-099]“Was it because you want to even things up for making me angry—I mean,

[013-100]for hurting my feelings on the way home?”

[013-101]With her eyes averted—for girls of nineteen can be as shy as boys,

[013-102]sometimes—she said, “Well—you only got angry because I couldn’t dance

[013-103]the cotillion with you. I—I didn’t feel terribly hurt with you for

[013-104]getting angry about _that!_”

[013-105]“Was there any other reason? Did my telling you I liked you have

[013-106]anything to do with it?”

[013-107]She looked up gently, and, as George met her eyes, something

[013-108]exquisitely touching, yet queerly delightful, gave him a catch in the

[013-109]throat. She looked instantly away, and, turning, ran out from the palm

[013-110]grove, where they stood, to the dancing-floor.

[013-111]“Come on!” she cried. “Let’s dance!”

[013-112]He followed her.

[013-113]“See here—I—I—” he stammered. “You mean—Do you—”

[013-114]“No, no!” she laughed. “Let’s dance!”

[013-115]He put his arm about her almost tremulously, and they began to waltz.

[013-116]It was a happy dance for both of them.

[013-117]Christmas day is the children’s, but the holidays are youth’s

[013-118]dancing-time. The holidays belong to the early twenties and the ’teens,

[013-119]home from school and college. These years possess the holidays for

[013-120]a little while, then possess them only in smiling, wistful memories

[013-121]of holly and twinkling lights and dance-music, and charming faces

[013-122]all aglow. It is the liveliest time in life, the happiest of the

[013-123]irresponsible times in life. Mothers echo its happiness—nothing is like

[013-124]a mother who has a son home from college, except another mother with a

[013-125]son home from college. Bloom does actually come upon these mothers; it

[013-126]is a visible thing; and they run like girls, walk like athletes, laugh

[013-127]like sycophants. Yet they give up their sons to the daughters of other

[013-128]mothers, and find it proud rapture enough to be allowed to sit and

[013-129]watch.

[013-130]Thus Isabel watched George and Lucy dancing, as together they danced

[013-131]away the holidays of that year into the past.

[013-132]“They seem to get along better than they did at first, those two

[013-133]children,” Fanny Minafer said sitting beside her at the Sharons’ dance,

[013-134]a week after the Assembly. “They seemed to be always having little

[013-135]quarrels of some sort, at first. At least George did: he seemed to be

[013-136]continually pecking at that lovely, dainty, little Lucy, and being

[013-137]cross with her over nothing.”

[013-138]“_Pecking?_” Isabel laughed. “What a word to use about Georgie! I think

[013-139]I never knew a more angelically amiable disposition in my life!”

[013-140]Miss Fanny echoed her sister-in-law’s laugh, but it was a rueful echo,

[013-141]and not sweet. “He’s amiable to you!” she said. “That’s all the side of

[013-142]him you ever happen to see. And why wouldn’t he be amiable to anybody

[013-143]that simply fell down and worshipped him every minute of her life? Most

[013-144]of us would!”

[013-145]“Isn’t he worth worshipping? Just look at him! Isn’t he _charming_ with

[013-146]Lucy! See how hard he ran to get it when she dropped her handkerchief

[013-147]back there.”

[013-148]“Oh, I’m not going to argue with you about George!” said Miss Fanny.

[013-149]“I’m fond enough of him, for that matter. He can be charming, and he’s

[013-150]certainly stunning _looking_, if only—”

[013-151]“Let the ‘if only’ go, dear,” Isabel suggested good-naturedly. “Let’s

[013-152]talk about that dinner you thought I should—”

[013-153]“I?” Miss Fanny interrupted quickly. “Didn’t you want to give it

[013-154]yourself?”

[013-155]“Indeed, I did, my dear!” said Isabel heartily. “I only meant that

[013-156]unless you had proposed it, perhaps I wouldn’t—”

[013-157]But here [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] came for her to dance, and she left the sentence

[013-158]uncompleted. Holiday dances can be happy for youth renewed as well as

[013-159]for youth in bud—and yet it was not with the air of a rival that Miss

[013-160]Fanny watched her brother’s wife dancing with the widower. Miss Fanny’s

[013-161]eyes narrowed a little, but only as if her mind engaged in a hopeful

[013-162]calculation. She looked pleased.

Chapter X

[013-163]A few days after George’s return to the university it became evident

[013-164]that not quite everybody had gazed with complete benevolence upon the

[013-165]various young collegians at their holiday sports. The Sunday edition of

[013-166]the principal morning paper even expressed some bitterness under the

[013-167]heading, “Gilded Youths of the Fin-de-Siècle”—this was considered the

[013-168]knowing phrase of the time, especially for Sunday supplements—and there

[013-169]is no doubt that from certain references in this bit of writing some

[013-170]people drew the conclusion that Mr. George Amberson Minafer had not yet

[013-171]got his comeuppance, a postponement still irritating. Undeniably, Fanny

[013-172]Minafer was one of the people who drew this conclusion, for she cut the

[013-173]article out and enclosed it in a letter to her nephew, having written

[013-174]on the border of the clipping, “I wonder whom it can mean!”

[013-175]George read part of it.

[013-176]We debate sometimes what is to be the future of this nation when

[013-177]we think that in a few years public affairs may be in the hands

[013-178]of the _fin-de-siècle_ gilded youths we see about us during the

[013-179]Christmas holidays. Such foppery, such luxury, such insolence, was

[013-180]surely never practised by the scented, overbearing patricians of

[013-181]the Palatine, even in Rome’s most decadent epoch. In all the wild

[013-182]orgy of wastefulness and luxury with which the nineteenth century

[013-183]reaches its close, the gilded youth has been surely the worst

[013-184]symptom. With his airs of young milord, his fast horses, his gold

[013-185]and silver cigarette-cases, his clothes from a New York tailor, his

[013-186]recklessness of [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] showered upon him by indulgent mothers or

[013-187]doting grandfathers, he respects nothing and nobody. He is blase if

[013-188]you please. Watch him at a social function, how condescendingly he

[013-189]deigns to select a partner for the popular waltz or two step, how

[013-190]carelessly he shoulders older people out of his way, with what a

[013-191]blank stare he returns the salutation of some old acquaintance whom

[013-192]he may choose in his royal whim to forget! The unpleasant part of

[013-193]all this is that the young women he so condescendingly selects as

[013-194]partners for the dance greet him with seeming rapture, though in

[013-195]their hearts they must feel humiliated by his languid hauteur, and

[013-196]many older people beam upon him almost fawningly if he unbends so

[013-197]far as to throw them a careless, disdainful word! One wonders what

[013-198]has come over the new generation. Of such as these the Republic was

[013-199]not made. Let us pray that the future of our country is not in the

[013-200]hands of these _fin-de-siècle_ gilded youths, but rather in the

[013-201]calloused palms of young men yet unknown, labouring upon the farms

[013-202]of the land. When we compare the young manhood of Abraham Lincoln

[013-203]with the specimens we are now producing, we see too well that it

[013-204]bodes ill for the twentieth century—
