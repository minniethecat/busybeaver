[020-001]Devotedly, your mother

[020-002]Isabel.

[020-003]But she did not keep her husband there for the six weeks she

[020-004]anticipated. She did not keep him anywhere that long. Three weeks after

[020-005]writing this letter, she telegraphed suddenly to George that they were

[020-006]leaving for home at once; and four days later, when he and a friend

[020-007]came whistling into his study, from lunch at the club, he found another

[020-008]telegram upon his desk.

[020-009]He read it twice before he comprehended its import.

[020-010]Papa left us at ten this morning, dearest.

[020-011]Mother.

[020-012]The friend saw the change in his face. “Not bad news?”

[020-013]George lifted utterly dumfounded eyes from the yellow paper.

[020-014]“My father,” he said weakly. “She says—she says he’s dead. I’ve got to

[020-015]go home.”

[020-016]His [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] and the Major met him at the station when he

[020-017]arrived—the first time the Major had ever come to meet his grandson.

[020-018]The old gentleman sat in his closed carriage (which still needed paint)

[020-019]at the entrance to the station, but he got out and advanced to grasp

[020-020]George’s hand tremulously, when the latter appeared. “Poor fellow!” he

[020-021]said, and patted him repeatedly upon the shoulder. “Poor fellow! Poor

[020-022]Georgie!”

[020-023]George had not yet come to a full realization of his loss: so far, his

[020-024]condition was merely dazed; and as the Major continued to pat him,

[020-025]murmuring “Poor fellow!” over and over, George was seized by an almost

[020-026]irresistible impulse to tell his grandfather that he was not a poodle.

[020-027]But he said “Thanks,” in a low voice, and got into the carriage, his

[020-028]two relatives following with deferential sympathy. He noticed that the

[020-029]Major’s tremulousness did not disappear, as they drove up the street,

[020-030]and that he seemed much feebler than during the summer. Principally,

[020-031]however, George was concerned with his own emotion, or rather, with

[020-032]his lack of emotion; and the anxious sympathy of his grandfather and

[020-033]his uncle made him feel hypocritical. He was not grief-stricken; but

[020-034]he felt that he ought to be, and, with a secret shame, concealed his

[020-035]callousness beneath an affectation of solemnity.

[020-036]But when he was taken into the room where lay what was left of Wilbur

[020-037]Minafer, George had no longer to pretend; his grief was sufficient.

[020-038]It needed only the sight of that forever inert semblance of the quiet

[020-039]man who had been always so quiet a part of his son’s life—so quiet a

[020-040]part that George had seldom been consciously aware that his father was

[020-041]indeed a part of his life. As the figure lay there, its very quietness

[020-042]was what was most lifelike; and suddenly it struck George hard. And in

[020-043]that unexpected, racking grief of his son, [[wilbur-minafer|Wilbur Minafer]] became more

[020-044]vividly George’s father than he had ever been in life.

[020-045]When George left the room, his arm was about his black-robed mother,

[020-046]his shoulders were still shaken with sobs. He leaned upon his mother;

[020-047]she gently comforted him; and presently he recovered his composure

[020-048]and became self-conscious enough to wonder if he had not been making

[020-049]an unmanly display of himself. “I’m all right again, mother,” he

[020-050]said awkwardly. “Don’t worry about me: you’d better go lie down, or

[020-051]something; you look pretty pale.”

[020-052]Isabel did look pretty pale, but not ghastly pale, as [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] did.

[020-053]Fanny’s grief was overwhelming; she stayed in her room, and George

[020-054]did not see her until the next day, a few minutes before the funeral,

[020-055]when her haggard face appalled him. But by this time he was quite

[020-056]himself again, and during the short service in the cemetery his

[020-057]thoughts even wandered so far as to permit him a feeling of regret

[020-058]not directly connected with his father. Beyond the open flower-walled

[020-059]grave was a mound where new grass grew; and here lay his great-uncle,

[020-060]old John Minafer, who had died the previous autumn; and beyond this

[020-061]were the graves of George’s grandfather and grandmother Minafer, and

[020-062]of his grandfather Minafer’s second wife, and her three sons, George’s

[020-063]half-uncles, who had been drowned together in a canoe accident when

[020-064]George was a child—Fanny was the last of the family. Next beyond was

[020-065][[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] family lot, where lay the Major’s wife and their sons

[020-066]Henry and Milton, uncles whom George dimly remembered; and beside

[020-067]them lay Isabel’s older sister, his Aunt Estelle, who had died, in

[020-068]her girlhood, long before George was born. The Minafer monument was a

[020-069]granite block, with the name chiseled upon its one polished side, and

[020-070]the Amberson monument was a white marble shaft taller than any other

[020-071]in that neighbourhood. But farther on there was a newer section of the

[020-072]cemetery, an addition which had been thrown open to occupancy only

[020-073]a few years before, after dexterous modern treatment by a landscape

[020-074]specialist. There were some large new mausoleums here, and shafts

[020-075]taller than the Ambersons’, as well as a number of monuments of some

[020-076]sculptural pretentiousness; and altogether the new section appeared to

[020-077]be a more fashionable and important quarter than that older one which

[020-078]contained the Amberson and Minafer lots. This was what caused George’s

[020-079]regret, during the moment or two when his mind strayed from his father

[020-080]and the reading of the service.

[020-081]On the train, going back to college, ten days later, this regret

[020-082](though it was as much an annoyance as a regret) recurred to his mind,

[020-083]and a feeling developed within him that the new quarter of the cemetery

[020-084]was in bad taste—not architecturally or sculpturally perhaps, but in

[020-085]presumption: it seemed to flaunt a kind of parvenu ignorance, as if

[020-086]it were actually pleased to be unaware that all the aristocratic and

[020-087]really important families were buried in the old section.

[020-088]The annoyance gave way before a recollection of the sweet mournfulness

[020-089]of his mother’s face, as she had said good-bye to him at the station,

[020-090]and of how lovely she looked in her mourning. He thought of [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], whom

[020-091]he had seen only twice, and he could not help feeling that in these

[020-092]quiet interviews he had appeared to her as tinged with heroism—she had

[020-093]shown, rather than said, how brave she thought him in his sorrow. But

[020-094]what came most vividly to George’s mind, during these retrospections,

[020-095]was the despairing face of his Aunt Fanny. Again and again he thought

[020-096]of it; he could not avoid its haunting. And for days, after he got

[020-097]back to college, the stricken likeness of Fanny would appear before

[020-098]him unexpectedly, and without a cause that he could trace in his

[020-099]immediately previous thoughts. Her grief had been so silent, yet it had

[020-100]so amazed him.

[020-101]George felt more and more compassion for this ancient antagonist of

[020-102]his, and he wrote to his mother about her:

[020-103]I’m afraid poor Aunt Fanny might think now father’s gone we won’t

[020-104]want her to live with us any longer and because I always teased

[020-105]her so much she might think I’d be for turning her out. I don’t

[020-106]know where on earth she’d go or what she could live on if we did do

[020-107]something like this, and of course we never would do such a thing,

[020-108]but I’m pretty sure she had something of the kind on her mind. She

[020-109]didn’t say anything, but the way she looked is what makes me think

[020-110]so. Honestly, to me she looked just scared sick. You tell her there

[020-111]isn’t any danger in the world of my treating her like that. Tell

[020-112]her everything is to go on just as it always has. Tell her to cheer

[020-113]up!

Chapter XV

[020-114]Isabel did more for Fanny than telling her to cheer up. Everything that

[020-115]Fanny inherited from her father, old Aleck Minafer, had been invested

[020-116]in Wilbur’s business; and Wilbur’s business, after a period of illness

[020-117]corresponding in dates to the illness of Wilbur’s body, had died just

[020-118]before Wilbur did. George Amberson and Fanny were both “wiped out to a

[020-119]miracle of precision,” as Amberson said. They “owned not a penny and

[020-120]owed not a penny,” he continued, explaining his phrase. “It’s like the

[020-121]moment just before drowning: you’re not under water and you’re not out

[020-122]of it. All you know is that you’re not dead yet.”

[020-123]He spoke philosophically, having his “prospects” from his father to

[020-124]fall back upon; but Fanny had neither “prospects” nor philosophy.

[020-125]However, a legal survey of Wilbur’s estate revealed the fact that

[020-126]his life insurance was left clear of the wreck; and Isabel, with the

[020-127]cheerful consent of her son, promptly turned this salvage over to her

[020-128]sister-in-law. Invested, it would yield something better than nine

[020-129]hundred dollars a year, and thus she was assured of becoming neither

[020-130]a pauper nor a dependent, but proved to be, as Amberson said, adding

[020-131]his efforts to the cheering up of Fanny, “an heiress, after all, in

[020-132]spite of rolling mills and the devil.” She was unable to smile, and he

[020-133]continued his humane gayeties. “See what a wonderfully desirable income

[020-134]nine hundred dollars is, Fanny: a bachelor, to be in your class, must

[020-135]have exactly forty-nine thousand one hundred a year. Then, you see,

[020-136]all you need to do, in order to have fifty thousand a year, is to be a

[020-137]little encouraging when some bachelor in your class begins to show by

[020-138]his haberdashery what he wants you to think about him!”

[020-139]She looked at him wanly, murmured a desolate response—she had “sewing

[020-140]to do”—and left the room; while Amberson shook his head ruefully at his

[020-141]sister. “I’ve often thought that humor was not my forte,” he sighed.

[020-142]“Lord! She doesn’t ‘cheer up’ much!”

[020-143]The collegian did not return to his home for the holidays. Instead,

[020-144]Isabel joined him, and they went South for the two weeks. She was proud

[020-145]of her stalwart, good-looking son at the hotel where they stayed, and

[020-146]it was meat and drink to her when she saw how people stared at him

[020-147]in the lobby and on the big verandas—indeed, her vanity in him was

[020-148]so dominant that she was unaware of their staring at her with more

[020-149]interest and an admiration friendlier than George evoked. Happy to have

[020-150]him to herself for this fortnight, she loved to walk with him, leaning

[020-151]upon his arm, to read with him, to watch the sea with him—perhaps most

[020-152]of all she liked to enter the big dining room with him.

[020-153]Yet both of them felt constantly the difference between this

[020-154]Christmastime and other Christmas-times of theirs—in all, it was a

[020-155]sorrowful holiday. But when Isabel came East for George’s commencement,

[020-156]in June, she brought Lucy with her—and things began to seem different,

[020-157]especially when George Amberson arrived with Lucy’s father on Class

[020-158]Day. [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] had been in New York, on business; Amberson easily

[020-159]persuaded him to this outing; and they made a cheerful party of it,

[020-160]with the new graduate of course the hero and center of it all.

[020-161]His uncle was a fellow alumnus. “Yonder was where I roomed when I was

[020-162]here,” he said, pointing out one of the university buildings to Eugene.

[020-163]“I don’t know whether George would let my admirers place a tablet to

[020-164]mark the spot, or not. He owns all these buildings now, you know.”

[020-165]“Didn’t you, when you were here? Like uncle, like nephew.”

[020-166]“Don’t tell George you think he’s like me. Just at this time we should

[020-167]be careful of the young gentleman’s feelings.”

[020-168]“Yes,” said Eugene. “If we weren’t he mightn’t let us exist at all.”

[020-169]“I’m sure I didn’t have it so badly at his age,” Amberson said

[020-170]reflectively, as they strolled on through the commencement crowd. “For

[020-171]one thing, I had brothers and sisters, and my mother didn’t just sit

[020-172]at my feet as George’s does; and I wasn’t an only grandchild, either.

[020-173]Father’s always spoiled Georgie a lot more than he did any of his own

[020-174]children.”

[020-175]Eugene laughed. “You need only three things to explain all that’s good

[020-176]and bad about Georgie.”

[020-177]“Three?”

[020-178]“He’s Isabel’s only child. He’s an Amberson. He’s a boy.”
