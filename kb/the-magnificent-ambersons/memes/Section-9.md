[009-001]“What do you mean: ‘after you got to know them a—’”

[009-002]She was departing to the dance. “Janie and Mary Sharon told me all

[009-003]about what sort of a _little_ boy you were,” she said, over her

[009-004]shoulder. “You must think it out!” She took wing away on the breeze

[009-005]of the waltz, and George, having stared gloomily after her for a few

[009-006]moments, postponed filling an engagement, and strolled round the

[009-007]fluctuating outskirts of the dance to where his uncle, [[george-amberson|George Amberson]],

[009-008]stood smilingly watching, under one of the rose-vine arches at the

[009-009]entrance to the room.

[009-010]“Hello, young namesake,” said the uncle. “Why lingers the laggard heel

[009-011]of the dancer? Haven’t you got a partner?”

[009-012]“She’s sitting around waiting for me somewhere,” said George. “See

[009-013]here: Who is this fellow Morgan that [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]] Minafer was dancing

[009-014]with a while?”

[009-015]Amberson laughed. “He’s a man with a pretty daughter, Georgie. Meseemed

[009-016]you’ve been spending the evening noticing something of that sort—or do

[009-017]I err?”

[009-018]“Never mind! What sort is he?”

[009-019]“I think we’ll have to give him a character, Georgie. He’s an old

[009-020]friend; used to practice law here—perhaps he had more debts than cases,

[009-021]but he paid ’em all up before he left town. Your question is purely

[009-022]mercenary, I take it: you want to know his true worth before proceeding

[009-023]further with the daughter. I cannot inform you, though I notice signs

[009-024]of considerable prosperity in that becoming dress of hers. However,

[009-025]you never can tell, it is an age when every sacrifice is made for the

[009-026]young, and how your own poor mother managed to provide those genuine

[009-027]pearl studs for you out of her allowance from father, I can’t—”

[009-028]“Oh, dry up!” said the nephew. “I understand this Morgan—”

[009-029]“[[eugene-morgan|Mr. Eugene Morgan]],” his uncle suggested. “Politeness requires that the

[009-030]young should—”

[009-031]“I guess the ‘young’ didn’t know much about politeness in your day,”

[009-032]George interrupted. “I understand that Mr. Eugene Morgan used to be a

[009-033]great friend of the family.”

[009-034]“Oh, the Minafers?” the uncle inquired, with apparent innocence. “No, I

[009-035]seem to recall that he and your father were not—”

[009-036]“I mean the Ambersons,” George said impatiently. “I understand he was a

[009-037]good deal around the house here.”

[009-038]“What is your objection to that, George?”

[009-039]“What do you mean: my objection?”

[009-040]“You seemed to speak with a certain crossness.”

[009-041]“Well,” said George, “I meant he seems to feel awfully at home here.

[009-042]The way he was dancing with Aunt Fanny—”

[009-043]Amberson laughed. “I’m afraid your Aunt Fanny’s heart was stirred by

[009-044]ancient recollections, Georgie.”

[009-045]“You mean she used to be silly about him?”

[009-046]“She wasn’t considered singular,” said the uncle “He was—he was

[009-047]popular. Could you bear a question?”

[009-048]“What do you mean: could I bear—”

[009-049]“I only wanted to ask: Do you take this same passionate interest in the

[009-050]parents of every girl you dance with? Perhaps it’s a new fashion we old

[009-051]bachelors ought to take up. Is it the thing this year to—”

[009-052]“Oh, go on!” said George, moving away. “I only wanted to know—” He

[009-053]left the sentence unfinished, and crossed the room to where a girl sat

[009-054]waiting for his nobility to find time to fulfil his contract with her

[009-055]for this dance.

[009-056]“Pardon f’ keep’ wait,” he muttered, as she rose brightly to meet him;

[009-057]and she seemed pleased that he came at all—but George was used to

[009-058]girls’ looking radiant when he danced with them, and she had little

[009-059]effect upon him. He danced with her perfunctorily, thinking the while

[009-060]of Mr. Eugene Morgan and his daughter. Strangely enough, his thoughts

[009-061]dwelt more upon the father than the daughter, though George could

[009-062]not possibly have given a reason—even to himself—for this disturbing

[009-063]preponderance.

[009-064]By a coincidence, though not an odd one, the thoughts and conversation

[009-065]of Mr. Eugene Morgan at this very time were concerned with George

[009-066]Amberson Minafer, rather casually, it is true. Mr. Morgan had retired

[009-067]to a room set apart for smoking, on the second floor, and had found a

[009-068]grizzled gentleman lounging in solitary possession.

[009-069]“‘Gene Morgan!” this person exclaimed, rising with great heartiness.

[009-070]“I’d heard you were in town—I don’t believe you know me!”

[009-071]“Yes, I do, Fred Kinney!” Mr. Morgan returned with equal friendliness.

[009-072]“Your real face—the one I used to know—it’s just underneath the one

[009-073]you’re masquerading in to-night. You ought to have changed it more if

[009-074]you wanted a disguise.”

[009-075]“Twenty years!” said Mr. Kinney. “It makes some difference in faces,

[009-076]but more in behaviour!”

[009-077]“It does so!” his friend agreed with explosive emphasis. “My own

[009-078]behaviour began to be different about that long ago—quite suddenly.”

[009-079]“I remember,” said Mr. Kinney sympathetically. “Well, life’s odd enough

[009-080]as we look back.”

[009-081]“Probably it’s going to be odder still—if we could look forward.”

[009-082]“Probably.”

[009-083]They sat and smoked.

[009-084]“However,” Mr. Morgan remarked presently, “I still dance like an

[009-085]Indian. Don’t you?”

[009-086]“No. I leave that to my boy Fred. He does the dancing for the family.”

[009-087]“I suppose he’s upstairs hard at it?”

[009-088]“No, he’s not here.” Mr. Kinney glanced toward the open door and

[009-089]lowered his voice. “He wouldn’t come. It seems that a couple of years

[009-090]or so ago he had a row with young [[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]]. Fred was president

[009-091]of a literary club they had, and he said this young Georgie got himself

[009-092]elected instead, in an overbearing sort of way. Fred’s red-headed, you

[009-093]know—I suppose you remember his mother? You were at the wedding—”

[009-094]“I remember the wedding,” said Mr. Morgan. “And I remember your

[009-095]bachelor dinner—most of it, that is.”

[009-096]“Well, my boy Fred’s as red-headed now,” Mr. Kinney went on, “as his

[009-097]mother was then, and he’s very bitter about his row with Georgie

[009-098]Minafer. He says he’d rather burn his foot off than set it inside any

[009-099]Amberson house or any place else where young Georgie is. Fact is, the

[009-100]boy seemed to have so much feeling over it I had my doubts about coming

[009-101]myself, but my wife said it was all nonsense; we mustn’t humour Fred in

[009-102]a grudge over such a little thing, and while she despised that Georgie

[009-103]Minafer, herself, as much as any one else did, she wasn’t going to miss

[009-104]a big Amberson show just on account of a boys’ rumpus, and so on and so

[009-105]on; and so we came.”

[009-106]“Do people dislike young Minafer generally?”

[009-107]“I don’t know about ‘generally.’ I guess he gets plenty of toadying;

[009-108]but there’s certainly a lot of people that are glad to express their

[009-109]opinions about him.”

[009-110]“What’s the matter with him?”

[009-111]“Too much Amberson, I suppose, for one thing. And for another, his

[009-112]mother just fell down and worshipped him from the day he was born.

[009-113]That’s what beats me! I don’t have to tell you what [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]] is,

[009-114]Eugene Morgan. She’s got a touch of [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] high stuff about her,

[009-115]but you can’t get anybody that ever knew her to deny that she’s just

[009-116]about the finest woman in the world.”

[009-117]“No,” said Eugene Morgan. “You can’t get anybody to deny that.”

[009-118]“Then I can’t see how she doesn’t see the truth about that boy. He

[009-119]thinks he’s a little tin god on wheels—and honestly, it makes some

[009-120]people weak and sick just to think about him! Yet that high-spirited,

[009-121]intelligent woman, Isabel Amberson, actually sits and worships him! You

[009-122]can hear it in her voice when she speaks to him or speaks of him. You

[009-123]can see it in her eyes when she looks at him. My Lord! What _does_ she

[009-124]see when she looks at him?”

[009-125]Morgan’s odd expression of genial apprehension deepened whimsically,

[009-126]though it denoted no actual apprehension whatever, and cleared away

[009-127]from his face altogether when he smiled; he became surprisingly winning

[009-128]and persuasive when he smiled. He smiled now, after a moment, at this

[009-129]question of his old friend. “She sees something that we don’t see,” he

[009-130]said.

[009-131]“What does she see?”

[009-132]“An angel.”

[009-133]Kinney laughed aloud. “Well, if she sees an angel when she looks at

[009-134]Georgie Minafer, she’s a funnier woman than I thought she was!”

[009-135]“Perhaps she is,” said Morgan. “But that’s what she sees.”

[009-136]“My Lord! It’s easy to see you’ve only known him an hour or so. In that

[009-137]time have you looked at Georgie and seen an angel?”

[009-138]“No. All I saw was a remarkably good-looking fool-boy with the pride

[009-139]of Satan and a set of nice new drawing-room manners that he probably

[009-140]couldn’t use more than half an hour at a time without busting.”

[009-141]“Then what—”

[009-142]“Mothers are right,” said Morgan. “Do you think this young George is

[009-143]the same sort of creature when he’s with his mother that he is when

[009-144]he’s bulldozing your boy Fred? Mothers see the angel in us because the

[009-145]angel is there. If it’s shown to the mother, the son has got an angel

[009-146]to show, hasn’t he? When a son cuts somebody’s throat the mother only

[009-147]sees it’s possible for a misguided angel to act like a devil—and she’s

[009-148]entirely right about that!”

[009-149]Kinney laughed, and put his hand on his friend’s shoulder. “I remember

[009-150]what a fellow you always were to argue,” he said. “You mean Georgie

[009-151]Minafer is as much of an angel as any murderer is, and that Georgie’s

[009-152]mother is always right.”

[009-153]“I’m afraid she always has been,” Morgan said lightly.

[009-154]The friendly hand remained upon his shoulder. “She was wrong once, old

[009-155]fellow. At least, so it seemed to me.”

[009-156]“No,” said Morgan, a little awkwardly. “No—”

[009-157]Kinney relieved the slight embarrassment that had come upon both of

[009-158]them: he laughed again. “Wait till you know young Georgie a little

[009-159]better,” he said. “Something tells me you’re going to change your mind

[009-160]about his having an angel to show, if you see anything of him!”

[009-161]“You mean beauty’s in the eye of the beholder, and the angel is all in

[009-162]the eye of the mother. If you were a painter, Fred, you’d paint mothers

[009-163]with angels’ eyes holding imps in their laps. Me, I’ll stick to the Old

[009-164]Masters and the cherubs.”

[009-165]Mr. Kinney looked at him musingly. “Somebody’s eyes must have been

[009-166]pretty angelic,” he said, “if they’ve been persuading you that Georgie

[009-167]Minafer is a cherub!”

[009-168]“They are,” said Morgan heartily. “They’re more angelic than ever.”

[009-169]And as a new flourish of music sounded overhead he threw away his

[009-170]cigarette, and jumped up briskly. “Good-bye, I’ve got this dance with

[009-171]her.”

[009-172]“With whom?”

[009-173]“With Isabel!”

[009-174]The grizzled Mr. Kinney affected to rub his eyes. “It startles me, your

[009-175]jumping up like that to go and dance with Isabel Amberson! Twenty years

[009-176]seem to have passed—but have they? Tell me, have you danced with poor

[009-177]old Fanny, too, this evening?”

[009-178]“Twice!”

[009-179]“My Lord!” Kinney groaned, half in earnest. “Old times starting all

[009-180]over again! My Lord!”

[009-181]“Old times?” Morgan laughed gaily from the doorway. “Not a bit! There

[009-182]aren’t any old times. When times are gone they’re not old, they’re

[009-183]dead! There aren’t any times but new times!”

[009-184]And he vanished in such a manner that he seemed already to have begun

[009-185]dancing.

[009-186]The appearance of [[lucy-morgan|Miss Lucy Morgan]] the next day, as she sat in George’s

[009-187]fast cutter, proved so charming that her escort was stricken to soft

[009-188]words instantly, and failed to control a poetic impulse. Her rich

[009-189]little hat was trimmed with black fur; her hair was almost as dark as

[009-190]the fur; a great boa of black fur was about her shoulders; her hands

[009-191]were vanished into a black muff; and George’s laprobe was black. “You

[009-192]look like—” he said. “Your face looks like—it looks like a snowflake on

[009-193]a lump of coal. I mean a—a snowflake that would be a rose-leaf, too!”

[009-194]“Perhaps you’d better look at the reins,” she returned. “We almost

[009-195]upset just then.”

[009-196]George declined to heed this advice. “Because there’s too much pink in

[009-197]your cheeks for a snowflake,” he continued. “What’s that fairy story
