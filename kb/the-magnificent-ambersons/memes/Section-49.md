[049-001]This was the strangest moment of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s life, because, while it

[049-002]lasted, he believed that [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]], who was dead, had found means

[049-003]to speak to him. Though within ten minutes he doubted it, he believed

[049-004]it then.

[049-005]His elbows pressed hard upon the table, and, his head between his

[049-006]hands, he leaned forward, staring at the commonplace figure in the

[049-007]easy-chair. “What does she wish to say to me?”

[049-008]“She is happy because you know her. No—she is troubled. Oh—a great

[049-009]trouble! Something she wants to tell you. She wants so much to tell

[049-010]you. She wants Lopa to tell you. This is a great trouble. She says—oh,

[049-011]yes, she wants you to be—to be kind! That’s what she says. That’s it.

[049-012]To be kind.”

[049-013]“Does she—”

[049-014]“She wants you to be kind,” said the voice. “She nods when I tell you

[049-015]this. Yes; it must be right. She is a very fine lady. Very pretty. She

[049-016]is so anxious for you to understand. She hopes and hopes you will.

[049-017]Someone else wants to speak to you. This is a man. He says—”

[049-018]“I don’t want to speak to any one else,” said Eugene quickly. “I want—”

[049-019]“This man who has come says that he is a friend of yours. He says—”

[049-020]Eugene struck the table with his fist. “I don’t want to speak to any

[049-021]one else, I tell you!” he cried passionately. “If she is there I—” He

[049-022]caught his breath sharply, checked himself, and sat in amazement. Could

[049-023]his mind so easily accept so stupendous a thing as true? Evidently it

[049-024]could!

[049-025]Mrs. Horner spoke languidly in her own voice: “Did you get anything

[049-026]satisfactory?” she asked. “I certainly hope it wasn’t like that other

[049-027]time when you was cross because they couldn’t get anything for you.”

[049-028]“No, no,” he said hastily. “This was different It was very interesting.”

[049-029]He paid her, went to his hotel, and thence to his train for home. Never

[049-030]did he so seem to move through a world of dream-stuff: for he knew that

[049-031]he was not more credulous than other men, and, if he could believe what

[049-032]he had believed, though he had believed it for no longer than a moment

[049-033]or two, what hold had he or any other human being on reality?

[049-034]His credulity vanished (or so he thought) with his recollection that it

[049-035]was he, and not the alleged “Lopa,” who had suggested the word “amber.”

[049-036]Going over the mortifying, plain facts of his experience, he found that

[049-037]Mrs. Horner, or the subdivision of Mrs. Horner known as “Lopa,” had

[049-038]told him to think of a bell and of a colour, and that being furnished

[049-039]with these scientific data, he had leaped to the conclusion that he

[049-040]spoke with Isabel Amberson!

[049-041]For a moment he had believed that Isabel was there, believed that she

[049-042]was close to him, entreating him—entreating him “to be kind.” But with

[049-043]this recollection a strange agitation came upon him. After all, had

[049-044]she not spoken to him? If his own unknown consciousness had told the

[049-045]“psychic’s” unknown consciousness how to make the picture of the pretty

[049-046]brown-haired, brown-eyed lady, hadn’t the picture been a true one? And

[049-047]hadn’t the true Isabel—oh, indeed her very soul!—called to him out of

[049-048]his own true memory of her?

[049-049]And as the train roared through the darkened evening he looked out

[049-050]beyond his window, and saw her as he had seen her on his journey, a

[049-051]few days ago—an ethereal figure flying beside the train, but now it

[049-052]seemed to him that she kept her face toward his window with an infinite

[049-053]wistfulness.

[049-054]“To be kind!” If it had been Isabel, was that what she would have said?

[049-055]If she were anywhere, and could come to him through the invisible wall,

[049-056]what would be the first thing she would say to him?

[049-057]Ah, well enough, and perhaps bitterly enough, he knew the answer to

[049-058]that question! “To be kind”—to Georgie!

[049-059]A red-cap at the station, when he arrived, leaped for his bag,

[049-060]abandoning another which the Pullman porter had handed him. “Yessuh,

[049-061]Mist’ Morgan. Yessuh. You’ car waitin’ front the station fer you, Mist’

[049-062]Morgan, suh!”

[049-063]And people in the crowd about the gates turned to stare, as he passed

[049-064]through, whispering, “That’s Morgan.”

[049-065]Outside, the neat chauffeur stood at the door of the touring-car like a

[049-066]soldier in whip-cord.

[049-067]“I’ll not go home now, Harry,” said Eugene, when he had got in. “Drive

[049-068]to the City Hospital.”

[049-069]“Yes, sir,” the man returned. “Miss [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]’s there. She said she expected

[049-070]you’d come there before you went home.”

[049-071]“She did?”

[049-072]“Yes, sir.”

[049-073]Eugene stared. “I suppose [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]] must be pretty bad,” he said.

[049-074]“Yes, sir. I understand he’s liable to get well, though, sir.” He moved

[049-075]his lever into high speed, and the car went through the heavy traffic

[049-076]like some fast, faithful beast that knew its way about, and knew its

[049-077]master’s need of haste. Eugene did not speak again until they reached

[049-078]the hospital.

[049-079][[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] met him in the upper corridor, and took him to an open door.

[049-080]He stopped on the threshold, startled; for, from the waxen face on the

[049-081]pillow, almost it seemed the eyes of Isabel herself were looking at

[049-082]him: never before had the resemblance between mother and son been so

[049-083]strong—and Eugene knew that now he had once seen it thus startlingly,

[049-084]he need divest himself of no bitterness “to be kind” to Georgie.

[049-085]George was startled, too. He lifted a white hand in a queer gesture,

[049-086]half forbidding, half imploring, and then let his arm fall back upon

[049-087]the coverlet. “You must have thought my mother wanted you to come,” he

[049-088]said, “so that I could ask you to—to forgive me.”

[049-089]But Lucy, who sat beside him, lifted ineffable eyes from him to her

[049-090]father, and shook her head. “No, just to take his hand—gently!”

[049-091]She was radiant.

[049-092]But for Eugene another radiance filled the room. He knew that he had

[049-093]been true at last to his true love, and that through him she had

[049-094]brought her boy under shelter again. Her eyes would look wistful no

[049-095]more.

[049-096]The End
