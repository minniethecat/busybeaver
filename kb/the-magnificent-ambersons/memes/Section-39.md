[039-002]“All you—needed?”

[039-003]“Yes, mother.”

[039-004]She did not speak again for a time; then, “Are you sure you

[039-005]didn’t—didn’t catch cold coming home?”

[039-006]“I’m all right, mother.”

[039-007]“That’s good. It’s sweet—it’s sweet—”

[039-008]“What is, mother darling?”

[039-009]“To feel—my hand on your cheek. I—I can feel it.”

[039-010]But this frightened him horribly—that she seemed so glad she could feel

[039-011]it, like a child proud of some miraculous seeming thing accomplished.

[039-012]It frightened him so that he could not speak, and he feared that she

[039-013]would know how he trembled; but she was unaware, and again was silent.

[039-014]Finally she spoke again:

[039-015]“I wonder if—if [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] know that we’ve come—home.”

[039-016]“I’m sure they do.”

[039-017]“Has he—asked about me?”

[039-018]“Yes, he was here.”

[039-019]“Has he—gone?”

[039-020]“Yes, mother.”

[039-021]She sighed faintly. “I’d like—”

[039-022]“What, mother?”

[039-023]“I’d like to have—seen him.” It was just audible, this little regretful

[039-024]murmur. Several minutes passed before there was another. “Just—just

[039-025]once,” she whispered, and then was still.

[039-026]She seemed to have fallen asleep, and George moved to go, but a faint

[039-027]pressure upon his fingers detained him, and he remained, with her hand

[039-028]still pressed against his cheek. After a while he made sure she was

[039-029]asleep, and moved again, to let the nurse come in, and this time there

[039-030]was no pressure of the fingers to keep him. She was not asleep, but

[039-031]thinking that if he went he might get some rest, and be better prepared

[039-032]for what she knew was coming, she commanded those longing fingers of

[039-033]hers—and let him go.

[039-034]He found the doctor standing with the nurse in the hall; and, telling

[039-035]them that his mother was drowsing now, George went back to his own

[039-036]room, where he was startled to find his grandfather lying on the bed,

[039-037]and his uncle leaning against the wall. They had gone home two hours

[039-038]before, and he did not know they had returned.

[039-039]“The doctor thought we’d better come over,” Amberson said, then was

[039-040]silent, and George, shaking violently, sat down on the edge of the bed.

[039-041]His shaking continued, and from time to time he wiped heavy sweat from

[039-042]his forehead.

[039-043]The hours passed, and sometimes the old man upon the bed would snore

[039-044]a little, stop suddenly, and move as if to rise, but [[george-amberson|George Amberson]]

[039-045]would set a hand upon his shoulder, and murmur a reassuring word or

[039-046]two. Now and then, either uncle or nephew would tiptoe into the hall

[039-047]and look toward Isabel’s room, then come tiptoeing back, the other

[039-048]watching him haggardly.

[039-049]Once George gasped defiantly: “That doctor in New York said she might

[039-050]get better! Don’t you know he did? Don’t you know he said she might?”

[039-051]Amberson made no answer.

[039-052]Dawn had been murking through the smoky windows, growing stronger for

[039-053]half an hour, when both men started violently at a sound in the hall;

[039-054]and the Major sat up on the bed, unchecked. It was the voice of the

[039-055]nurse speaking to [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]], and the next moment, Fanny appeared in

[039-056]the doorway, making contorted efforts to speak.

[039-057]Amberson said weakly: “Does she want us—to come in?”

[039-058]But Fanny found her voice, and uttered a long, loud cry. She threw her

[039-059]arms about George, and sobbed in an agony of loss and compassion:

[039-060]“She loved you!” she wailed. “She loved you! She loved you! Oh, how she

[039-061]did love you!”

[039-062]Isabel had just left them.

[039-063][[major-amberson|Major Amberson]] remained dry-eyed through the time that followed: he

[039-064]knew that this separation from his daughter would be short, that

[039-065]the separation which had preceded it was the long one. He worked

[039-066]at his ledgers no more under his old gas drop-light, but would sit

[039-067]all evening staring into the fire, in his bedroom, and not speaking

[039-068]unless someone asked him a question. He seemed almost unaware of what

[039-069]went on around him, and those who were with him thought him dazed by

[039-070]Isabel’s death, guessing that he was lost in reminiscences and vague

[039-071]dreams. “Probably his mind is full of pictures of his youth, or the

[039-072]Civil War, and the days when he and mother were young married people

[039-073]and all of us children were jolly little things—and the city was a

[039-074]small town with one cobbled street and the others just dirt roads

[039-075]with board sidewalks.” This was George Amberson’s conjecture, and

[039-076]the others agreed; but they were mistaken. The Major was engaged in

[039-077]the profoundest thinking of his life. No business plans which had

[039-078]ever absorbed him could compare in momentousness with the plans that

[039-079]absorbed him now, for he had to plan how to enter the unknown country

[039-080]where he was not even sure of being recognized as an Amberson—not

[039-081]sure of anything, except that Isabel would help him if she could. His

[039-082]absorption produced the outward effect of reverie, but of course it

[039-083]was not. The Major was occupied with the first really important matter

[039-084]that had taken his attention since he came home invalided, after the

[039-085]Gettysburg campaign, and went into business; and he realized that

[039-086]everything which had worried him or delighted him during this lifetime

[039-087]between then and to-day—all his buying and building and trading and

[039-088]banking—that it all was trifling and waste beside what concerned him

[039-089]now.

[039-090]He seldom went out of his room, and often left untouched the meals they

[039-091]brought to him there; and this neglect caused them to shake their heads

[039-092]mournfully, again mistaking for dazedness the profound concentration

[039-093]of his mind. Meanwhile, the life of the little bereft group still

[039-094]forlornly centering upon him began to pick up again, as life will, and

[039-095]to emerge from its own period of dazedness. It was not Isabel’s father

[039-096]but her son who was really dazed.

[039-097]A month after her death he walked abruptly into Fanny’s room, one

[039-098]night, and found her at her desk, eagerly adding columns of figures

[039-099]with which she had covered several sheets of paper. This mathematical

[039-100]computation was concerned with her future income to be produced by

[039-101]the electric headlight, now just placed on the general market; but

[039-102]Fanny was ashamed to be discovered doing anything except mourning, and

[039-103]hastily pushed the sheets aside, even as she looked over her shoulder

[039-104]to greet her hollow-eyed visitor.

[039-105]“George! You startled me.”

[039-106]“I beg your pardon for not knocking,” he said huskily. “I didn’t think.”

[039-107]She turned in her chair and looked at him solicitously. “Sit down,

[039-108]George, won’t you?”

[039-109]“No. I just wanted—”

[039-110]“I could hear you walking up and down in your room,” said Fanny. “You

[039-111]were doing it ever since dinner, and it seems to me you’re at it almost

[039-112]every evening. I don’t believe it’s good for you—and I know it would

[039-113]worry your mother terribly if she—” Fanny hesitated.

[039-114]“See here,” George said, breathing fast, “I want to tell you once more

[039-115]that what I did was right. How could I have done anything else but what

[039-116]I did do?”

[039-117]“[[About]] what, George?”

[039-118]“About everything!” he exclaimed; and he became vehement. “I did the

[039-119]right thing, I tell you! In heaven’s name, I’d like to know what else

[039-120]there was for anybody in my position to do! It would have been a

[039-121]dreadful thing for me to just let matters go on and not interfere—it

[039-122]would have been terrible! What else on earth was there for me to do?

[039-123]I had to stop that talk, didn’t I? Could a son do less than I did?

[039-124]Didn’t it cost me something to do it? Lucy and I’d had a quarrel, but

[039-125]that would have come round in time—and it meant the end forever when I

[039-126]turned her father back from our door. I knew what it meant, yet I went

[039-127]ahead and did it because I knew it had to be done if the talk was to be

[039-128]stopped. I took mother away for the same reason. I knew that would help

[039-129]to stop it. And she was happy over there—she was perfectly happy. I

[039-130]tell you, I think she had a happy life, and that’s my only consolation.

[039-131]She didn’t live to be old; she was still beautiful and young looking,

[039-132]and I feel she’d rather have gone before she got old. She’d had a good

[039-133]husband, and all the comfort and luxury that anybody could have—and how

[039-134]could it be called anything but a happy life? She was always cheerful,

[039-135]and when I think of her I can always see her laughing—I can always hear

[039-136]that pretty laugh of hers. When I can keep my mind off of the trip

[039-137]home, and that last night, I always think of her gay and laughing.

[039-138]So how on earth could she have had anything but a happy life? People

[039-139]that aren’t happy don’t look cheerful all the time, do they? They look

[039-140]unhappy if they are unhappy; that’s how they look! See here”—he faced

[039-141]her challengingly—“do you deny that I did the right thing?”

[039-142]“Oh, I don’t pretend to judge,” Fanny said soothingly, for his voice

[039-143]and gesture both partook of wildness. “I know you think you did,

[039-144]George.”

[039-145]“Think I did!” he echoed violently. “My God in heaven!” And he began to

[039-146]walk up and down the floor. “What else was there to do? What choice

[039-147]did I have? Was there any other way of stopping the talk?” He stopped,

[039-148]close in front of her, gesticulating, his voice harsh and loud: “Don’t

[039-149]you hear me? I’m asking you: Was there any other way on earth of

[039-150]protecting her from the talk?”

[039-151]Miss Fanny looked away. “It died down before long, I think,” she said

[039-152]nervously.

[039-153]“That shows I was right, doesn’t it?” he cried. “If I hadn’t acted as

[039-154]I did, that slanderous old Johnson woman would have kept on with her

[039-155]slanders—she’d still be—”

[039-156]“No,” Fanny interrupted. “She’s dead. She dropped dead with apoplexy

[039-157]one day about six weeks after you left. I didn’t mention it in my

[039-158]letters because I didn’t want—I thought—”

[039-159]“Well, the other people would have kept on, then. They’d have—”

[039-160]“I don’t know,” said Fanny, still averting her troubled eyes. “Things

[039-161]are so changed here, George. The other people you speak of—one hardly

[039-162]knows what’s become of them. Of course not a great many were doing the

[039-163]talking, and they—well, some of them are dead, and some might as well

[039-164]be—you never see them any more—and the rest, whoever they were, are

[039-165]probably so mixed in with the crowds of new people that seem never even

[039-166]to have heard of us—and I’m sure we certainly never heard of them—and

[039-167]people seem to forget things so soon—they seem to forget anything. You

[039-168]can’t imagine how things have changed here!”

[039-169]George gulped painfully before he could speak. “You—you mean to sit

[039-170]there and tell me that if I’d just let things go on—Oh!” He swung away,

[039-171]walking the floor again. “I tell you I did the only right thing! If

[039-172]you don’t think so, why in the name of heaven can’t you say what else

[039-173]I should have done? It’s easy enough to criticize, but the person who

[039-174]criticizes a man ought at least to tell him what else he should have

[039-175]done! You think I was wrong!”

[039-176]“I’m not saying so,” she said.

[039-177]“You did at the time!” he cried. “You said enough then, I think! Well,

[039-178]what have you to say now, if you’re so sure I was wrong?”

[039-179]“Nothing, George.”

[039-180]“It’s only because you’re afraid to!” he said, and he went on with a

[039-181]sudden bitter divination: “You’re reproaching yourself with what you

[039-182]had to do with all that; and you’re trying to make up for it by doing

[039-183]and saying what you think mother would want you to, and you think I

[039-184]couldn’t stand it if I got to thinking I might have done differently.

[039-185]Oh, I know! That’s exactly what’s in your mind: you do think I was

[039-186]wrong! So does Uncle George. I challenged him about it the other

[039-187]day, and he answered just as you’re answering—evaded, and tried to

[039-188]be gentler. I don’t care to be handled with gloves! I tell you I was

[039-189]right, and I don’t need any coddling by people that think I wasn’t! And

[039-190]I suppose you believe I was wrong not to let Morgan see her that last

[039-191]night when he came here, and she—she was dying. If you do, why in the
