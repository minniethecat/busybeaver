# Women's Tennyson Club

The Women's Tennyson Club was a literary and cultural organization in Midland, founded and led by [[mrs-henry-franklin-foster|Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster]]. Named after Alfred, Lord Tennyson, the Poet Laureate of Victorian England, the club served as the highest court of artistic and literary judgment in the community, and was the institutional foundation of Mrs. Foster's extraordinary cultural authority.

## Arbiter of Taste

The club was more than a social gathering; it was the source from which Mrs. Foster's pronouncements on art, letters, and the drama derived their weight. The narrator notes that "her word upon art, letters, and the drama was accepted more as law than as opinion" (002-169), and the Women's Tennyson Club was the platform that gave her this authority. When a new play arrived in town, many citizens deferred their own opinions until they knew what the Tennyson Club — and Mrs. Foster — thought of it (002-172).

## Social Function

Beyond its literary pretensions, the Women's Tennyson Club was a central institution in Midland's social calendar. A meeting of the club was enough to draw certain members of the community away from other obligations (003-038). It represented the aspiration toward cultivation and refinement that characterized the town's older elite, whose claim to superiority rested as much upon cultural distinction as upon wealth. In this sense, the Tennyson Club was a pillar of the social order that the novel depicts in its long decline.
