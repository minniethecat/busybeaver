[025-001]“[[fanny-minafer|Fanny]]!” exclaimed her sister-in-law. “You’re not in earnest?”

[025-002]“I am, though!”

[025-003]Isabel’s sweet-toned laugh came out of the dusk where she sat. “Then

[025-004]you didn’t mean it when you told [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] you’d enjoyed the drive this

[025-005]afternoon?”

[025-006]“I didn’t say it so very enthusiastically, did I?”

[025-007]“Perhaps not, but he certainly thought he’d pleased you.”

[025-008]“I don’t think I gave him any right to think he’d pleased me” Fanny

[025-009]said slowly.

[025-010]“Why not? Why shouldn’t you, Fanny?”

[025-011]Fanny did not reply at once, and when she did, her voice was almost

[025-012]inaudible, but much more reproachful than plaintive. “I hardly think

[025-013]I’d want any one to get the notion he’d pleased me just now. It hardly

[025-014]seems time, yet—to me.”

[025-015]Isabel made no response, and for a time the only sound upon the dark

[025-016]veranda was the creaking of the wicker rocking-chair in which Fanny

[025-017]sat—a creaking which seemed to denote content and placidity on the part

[025-018]of the chair’s occupant, though at this juncture a series of human

[025-019]shrieks could have been little more eloquent of emotional disturbance.

[025-020]However, the creaking gave its hearer one great advantage: it could be

[025-021]ignored.

[025-022]“Have you given up smoking, George?” Isabel asked presently.

[025-023]“No.”

[025-024]“I hoped perhaps you had, because you’ve not smoked since dinner. We

[025-025]shan’t mind if you care to.”

[025-026]“No, thanks.”

[025-027]There was silence again, except for the creaking of the rocking-chair;

[025-028]then a low, clear whistle, singularly musical, was heard softly

[025-029]rendering an old air from “Fra Diavolo.” The creaking stopped.

[025-030]“Is that you, George?” Fanny asked abruptly.

[025-031]“Is that me what?”

[025-032]“Whistling ‘On Yonder Rock Reclining’?”

[025-033]“It’s I,” said Isabel.

[025-034]“Oh,” Fanny said dryly.

[025-035]“Does it disturb you?”

[025-036]“Not at all. I had an idea George was depressed about something, and

[025-037]merely wondered if he could be making such a cheerful sound.” And Fanny

[025-038]resumed her creaking.

[025-039]“Is she right, George?” his mother asked quickly, leaning forward in

[025-040]her chair to peer at him through the dusk. “You didn’t eat a very

[025-041]hearty dinner, but I thought it was probably because of the warm

[025-042]weather. Are you troubled about anything?”

[025-043]“No!” he said angrily.

[025-044]“That’s good. I thought we had such a nice day, didn’t you?”

[025-045]“I suppose so,” he muttered, and, satisfied, she leaned back in her

[025-046]chair; but “Fra Diavolo” was not revived. After a time she rose, went

[025-047]to the steps, and stood for several minutes looking across the street.

[025-048]Then her laughter was faintly heard.

[025-049]“Are you laughing about something?” Fanny inquired.

[025-050]“Pardon?” Isabel did not turn, but continued her observation of what

[025-051]had interested her upon the opposite side of the street.

[025-052]“I asked: Were you laughing at something?”

[025-053]“Yes, I was!” And she laughed again. “It’s that funny, fat old Mrs.

[025-054]Johnson. She has a habit of sitting at her bedroom window with a pair

[025-055]of opera-glasses.”

[025-056]“Really!”

[025-057]“Really. You can see the window through the place that was left when we

[025-058]had the dead walnut tree cut down. She looks up and down the street,

[025-059]but mostly at father’s and over here. Sometimes she forgets to put out

[025-060]the light in her room, and there she is, spying away for all the world

[025-061]to see!”

[025-062]However, Fanny made no effort to observe this spectacle, but continued

[025-063]her creaking. “I’ve always thought her a very good woman,” she said

[025-064]primly.

[025-065]“So she is,” Isabel agreed. “She’s a good, friendly old thing, a

[025-066]little too intimate in her manner, sometimes, and if her poor old

[025-067]opera-glasses afford her the quiet happiness of knowing what sort of

[025-068]young man our new cook is walking out with, I’m the last to begrudge it

[025-069]to her! Don’t you want to come and look at her, George?”

[025-070]“What? I beg your pardon. I hadn’t noticed what you were talking about.”

[025-071]“It’s nothing,” she laughed. “Only a funny old lady—and she’s gone

[025-072]now. I’m going, too—at least, I’m going indoors to read. It’s cooler

[025-073]in the house, but the heat’s really not bad anywhere, since nightfall.

[025-074]Summer’s dying. How quickly it goes, once it begins to die.”

[025-075]When she had gone into the house, Fanny stopped rocking, and, leaning

[025-076]forward, drew her black gauze wrap about her shoulders and shivered.

[025-077]“Isn’t it queer,” she said drearily, “how your mother can use such

[025-078]words?”

[025-079]“What words are you talking about?” George asked.

[025-080]“Words like ‘die’ and ‘dying.’ I don’t see how she can bear to use them

[025-081]so soon after your poor father—” She shivered again.

[025-082]“It’s almost a year,” George said absently, and he added: “It seems to

[025-083]me you’re using them yourself.”

[025-084]“I? Never!”

[025-085]“Yes, you did.”

[025-086]“When?”

[025-087]“Just this minute.”

[025-088]“Oh!” said Fanny. “You mean when I repeated what she said? That’s

[025-089]hardly the same thing, George.”

[025-090]He was not enough interested to argue the point. “I don’t think you’ll

[025-091]convince anybody that mother’s unfeeling,” he said indifferently.

[025-092]“I’m not trying to convince anybody. I mean merely that in my

[025-093]opinion—well, perhaps it may be just as wise for me to keep my opinions

[025-094]to myself.”

[025-095]She paused expectantly, but her possible anticipation that George would

[025-096]urge her to discard wisdom and reveal her opinion was not fulfilled.

[025-097]His back was toward her, and he occupied himself with opinions of his

[025-098]own about other matters. Fanny may have felt some disappointment as she

[025-099]rose to withdraw.

[025-100]However, at the last moment she halted with her hand upon the latch of

[025-101]the screen door.

[025-102]“There’s one thing I hope,” she said. “I hope at least she won’t leave

[025-103]off her full mourning on the very anniversary of Wilbur’s death!”

[025-104]The light door clanged behind her, and the sound annoyed her nephew. He

[025-105]had no idea why she thus used inoffensive wood and wire to dramatize

[025-106]her departure from the veranda, the impression remaining with him

[025-107]being that she was critical of his mother upon some point of funeral

[025-108]millinery. Throughout the desultory conversation he had been profoundly

[025-109]concerned with his own disturbing affairs, and now was preoccupied

[025-110]with a dialogue taking place (in his mind) between himself and Miss

[025-111][[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]]. As he beheld the vision, Lucy had just thrown herself at

[025-112]his feet. “George, you must forgive me!” she cried. “Papa was utterly

[025-113]wrong! I have told him so, and the truth is that I have come to rather

[025-114]dislike him as you do, and as you always have, in your heart of hearts.

[025-115]George, I understand you: thy people shall be my people and thy gods my

[025-116]gods. George, won’t you take me back?”

[025-117]“Lucy, are you sure you understand me?” And in the darkness George’s

[025-118]bodily lips moved in unison with those which uttered the words in his

[025-119]imaginary rendering of this scene. An eavesdropper, concealed behind

[025-120]the column, could have heard the whispered word “sure,” the emphasis

[025-121]put upon it in the vision was so poignant. “You say you understand me,

[025-122]but are you sure?”

[025-123]Weeping, her head bowed almost to her waist, the ethereal Lucy made

[025-124]reply: “Oh, so sure! I will never listen to father’s opinions again. I

[025-125]do not even care if I never see him again!”

[025-126]“Then I pardon you,” he said gently.

[025-127]This softened mood lasted for several moments—until he realized that it

[025-128]had been brought about by processes strikingly lacking in substance.

[025-129]Abruptly he swung his feet down from the copestone to the floor of the

[025-130]veranda. “Pardon nothing!” No meek Lucy had thrown herself in remorse

[025-131]at his feet; and now he pictured her as she probably really was at

[025-132]this moment: sitting on the white steps of her own front porch in the

[025-133]moonlight, with red-headed Fred Kinney and silly Charlie Johnson and

[025-134]four or five others—all of them laughing, most likely, and some idiot

[025-135]playing the guitar!

[025-136]George spoke aloud: “Riffraff!”

[025-137]And because of an impish but all too natural reaction of the mind, he

[025-138]could see Lucy with much greater distinctness in this vision than in

[025-139]his former pleasing one. For a moment she was miraculously real before

[025-140]him, every line and colour of her. He saw the moonlight shimmering

[025-141]in the chiffon of her skirts brightest on her crossed knee and the

[025-142]tip of her slipper; saw the blue curve of the characteristic shadow

[025-143]behind her, as she leaned back against the white step; saw the watery

[025-144]twinkling of sequins in the gauze wrap over her white shoulders as she

[025-145]moved, and the faint, symmetrical lights in her black hair—and not one

[025-146]alluring, exasperating twentieth-of-an-inch of her laughing profile was

[025-147]spared him as she seemed to turn to the infernal Kinney—

[025-148]“Riffraff!” And George began furiously to pace the stone floor.

[025-149]“Riffraff!” By this hard term—a favourite with him since childhood’s

[025-150]scornful hour—he meant to indicate, not Lucy, but the young gentlemen

[025-151]who, in his vision, surrounded her. “Riffraff!” he said again, aloud,

[025-152]and again:

[025-153]“Riffraff!”

[025-154]At that moment, as it happened, Lucy was playing chess with her father;

[025-155]and her heart, though not remorseful, was as heavy as George could have

[025-156]wished. But she did not let Eugene see that she was troubled, and he

[025-157]was pleased when he won three games of her. Usually she beat him.

Chapter XIX

[025-158]George went driving the next afternoon alone, and, encountering

[025-159]Lucy and her father on the road, in one of Morgan’s cars, lifted

[025-160]his hat, but nowise relaxed his formal countenance as they passed.

[025-161]Eugene waved a cordial hand quickly returned to the steering-wheel;

[025-162]but Lucy only nodded gravely and smiled no more than George did. Nor

[025-163]did she accompany Eugene to the Major’s for dinner, the following

[025-164]Sunday evening, though both were bidden to attend that feast, which

[025-165]was already reduced in numbers and gayety by the absence of George

[025-166]Amberson. Eugene explained to his host that Lucy had gone away to visit

[025-167]a school-friend.

[025-168]The information, delivered in the library, just before old Sam’s

[025-169]appearance to announce dinner, set Miss Minafer in quite a flutter.

[025-170]“Why, George!” she said, turning to her nephew. “How does it happen

[025-171]you didn’t tell us?” And with both hands opening, as if to express her

[025-172]innocence of some conspiracy, she exclaimed to the others, “He’s never

[025-173]said one word to us about Lucy’s planning to go away!”

[025-174]“Probably afraid to,” the Major suggested. “Didn’t know but he might

[025-175]break down and cry if he tried to speak of it!” He clapped his grandson

[025-176]on the shoulder, inquiring jocularly, “That it, Georgie?”

[025-177]Georgie made no reply, but he was red enough to justify the Major’s

[025-178]developing a chuckle into laughter; though Miss Fanny, observing her

[025-179]nephew keenly, got an impression that this fiery blush was in truth

[025-180]more fiery than tender. She caught a glint in his eye less like

[025-181]confusion than resentment, and saw a dilation of his nostrils which

[025-182]might have indicated not so much a sweet agitation as an inaudible

[025-183]snort. Fanny had never been lacking in curiosity, and, since her

[025-184]brother’s death, this quality was more than ever alert. The fact that

[025-185]George had spent all the evenings of the past week at home had not

[025-186]been lost upon her, nor had she failed to ascertain, by diplomatic

[025-187]inquiries, that since the day of the visit to Eugene’s shops George had

[025-188]gone driving alone.

[025-189]At the dinner-table she continued to observe him, sidelong; and toward

[025-190]the conclusion of the meal she was not startled by an episode which

[025-191]brought discomfort to the others. After the arrival of coffee the Major

[025-192]was rallying Eugene upon some rival automobile shops lately built in a

[025-193]suburb, and already promising to flourish.

[025-194]“I suppose they’ll either drive you out of the business,” said the old

[025-195]gentleman, “or else the two of you’ll drive all the rest of us off the

[025-196]streets.”

[025-197]“If we do, we’ll even things up by making the streets five or ten times

[025-198]as long as they are now,” Eugene returned.

[025-199]“How do you propose to do that?”

[025-200]“It isn’t the distance from the center of a town that counts,” said

[025-201]Eugene; “it’s the time it takes to get there. This town’s already

[025-202]spreading; bicycles and trolleys have been doing their share, but the

[025-203]automobile is going to carry city streets clear out to the county line.”
