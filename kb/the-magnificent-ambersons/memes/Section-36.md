[036-001]“No.”

[036-002]“And even if you were, of course you’d be coming back to visit your

[036-003]relatives every now and then.”

[036-004]“I don’t know when I’m coming back. Mother and I are starting to-morrow

[036-005]night for a trip around the world.”

[036-006]At this she did look thoughtful. “Your mother is going with you?”

[036-007]“Good heavens!” he groaned. “[[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], doesn’t it make any difference to

[036-008]you that I am going?”

[036-009]At this her cordial smile instantly appeared again. “Yes, of course,”

[036-010]she said. “I’m sure I’ll miss you ever so much. Are you to be gone

[036-011]long?”

[036-012]He stared at her wanly. “I told you indefinitely,” he said. “We’ve made

[036-013]no plans—at all—for coming back.”

[036-014]“That does sound like a long trip!” she exclaimed admiringly. “Do you

[036-015]plan to be travelling all the time, or will you stay in some one place

[036-016]the greater part of it? I think it would be lovely to—”

[036-017]“Lucy!”

[036-018]He halted; and she stopped with him. They had come to a corner at the

[036-019]edge of the “business section” of the city, and people were everywhere

[036-020]about them, brushing against them, sometimes, in passing.

[036-021]“I can’t stand this,” George said, in a low voice. “I’m just about

[036-022]ready to go in this drug-store here, and ask the clerk for something to

[036-023]keep me from dying in my tracks! It’s quite a shock, you see, Lucy!”

[036-024]“What is?”

[036-025]“To find out certainly, at last, how deeply you’ve cared for me! To see

[036-026]how much difference this makes to you! By Jove, I have mattered to you!”

[036-027]Her cordial smile was tempered now with good-nature. “George!” She

[036-028]laughed indulgently. “Surely you don’t want me to do pathos on a

[036-029]downtown corner!”

[036-030]“You wouldn’t ‘do pathos’ anywhere!”

[036-031]“Well—don’t you think pathos is generally rather fooling?”

[036-032]“I can’t stand this any longer,” he said. “I can’t! Good-bye, Lucy!” He

[036-033]took her hand. “It’s good-bye—I think it’s good-bye for good, Lucy!”

[036-034]“Good-bye! I do hope you’ll have the most splendid trip.” She gave his

[036-035]hand a cordial little grip, then released it lightly. “Give my love to

[036-036]your mother. Good-bye!”

[036-037]He turned heavily away, and a moment later glanced back over his

[036-038]shoulder. She had not gone on, but stood watching him, that same

[036-039]casual, cordial smile on her face to the very last; and now, as he

[036-040]looked back, she emphasized her friendly unconcern by waving her

[036-041]small hand to him cheerily, though perhaps with the slightest hint of

[036-042]preoccupation, as if she had begun to think of the errand that brought

[036-043]her downtown.

[036-044]In his mind, George had already explained her to his own poignant

[036-045]dissatisfaction—some blond pup, probably, whom she had met during that

[036-046]“perfectly gorgeous time!” And he strode savagely onward, not looking

[036-047]back again.

[036-048]But Lucy remained where she was until he was out of sight. Then she

[036-049]went slowly into the drugstore which had struck George as a possible

[036-050]source of stimulant for himself.

[036-051]“Please let me have a few drops of aromatic spirits of ammonia in a

[036-052]glass of water,” she said, with the utmost composure.

[036-053]“Yes, ma’am!” said the impressionable clerk, who had been looking at

[036-054]her through the display window as she stood on the corner.

[036-055]But a moment later, as he turned from the shelves of glass jars

[036-056]against the wall, with the potion she had asked for in his hand, he

[036-057]uttered an exclamation: “For goshes’ sake, Miss!” And, describing this

[036-058]adventure to his fellow-boarders, that evening, “Sagged pretty near

[036-059]to the counter, she was,” he said. “’F I hadn’t been a bright, quick,

[036-060]ready-for-anything young fella she’d ‘a’ flummixed plum! I was watchin’

[036-061]her out the window—talkin’ to some young s’iety fella, and she was all

[036-062]right then. She was all right when she come in the store, too. Yes,

[036-063]sir; the prettiest girl that ever walked in our place and took one good

[036-064]look at me. I reckon it must be the truth what some you town wags say

[036-065]about my face!”

Chapter XXVIII

[036-066]At that hour the heroine of the susceptible clerk’s romance was

[036-067]engaged in brightening the rosy little coal fire under the white

[036-068]mantelpiece in her pretty white-and-blue boudoir. Four photographs

[036-069]all framed in decorous plain silver went to the anthracite’s fierce

[036-070]destruction—frames and all—and three packets of letters and notes in

[036-071]a charming Florentine treasure-box of painted wood; nor was the box,

[036-072]any more than the silver frames, spared this rousing finish. Thrown

[036-073]heartily upon live coal, the fine wood sparkled forth in stars, then

[036-074]burst into an alarming blaze which scorched the white mantelpiece, but

[036-075]Lucy stood and looked on without moving.

[036-076]It was not [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] who told her what had happened at Isabel’s door.

[036-077]When she got home, she found [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]] waiting for her—a secret

[036-078]excursion of Fanny’s for the purpose, presumably, of “letting out”

[036-079]again; because that was what she did. She told Lucy everything (except

[036-080]her own lamentable part in the production of the recent miseries) and

[036-081]concluded with a tribute to George: “The worst of it is, he thinks he’s

[036-082]been such a hero, and Isabel does, too, and that makes him more than

[036-083]twice as awful. It’s been the same all his life: everything he did was

[036-084]noble and perfect. He had a domineering nature to begin with, and she

[036-085]let it go on, and fostered it till it absolutely ruled her. I never saw

[036-086]a plainer case of a person’s fault making them pay for having it! She

[036-087]goes about, overseeing the packing and praising George and pretending

[036-088]to be perfectly cheerful about what he’s making her do and about the

[036-089]dreadful things he’s done. She pretends he did such a fine thing—so

[036-090]manly and protective—going to Mrs. Johnson. And so heroic—doing what

[036-091]his ‘principles’ made him—even though he knew what it would cost him

[036-092]with you! And all the while it’s almost killing her—what he said to

[036-093]your father! She’s always been lofty enough, so to speak, and had

[036-094]the greatest idea of the Ambersons being superior to the rest of the

[036-095]world, and all that, but rudeness, or anything like a ‘scene,’ or any

[036-096]bad manners—they always just made her sick! But she could never see

[036-097]what George’s manners were—oh, it’s been a terrible adulation!... It’s

[036-098]going to be a task for me, living in that big house, all alone: you

[036-099]must come and see me—I mean after they’ve gone, of course. I’ll go

[036-100]crazy if I don’t see something of people. I’m sure you’ll come as often

[036-101]as you can. I know you too well to think you’ll be sensitive about

[036-102]coming there, or being reminded of George. Thank heaven you’re too

[036-103]well-balanced,” Miss Fanny concluded, with a profound fervour, “you’re

[036-104]too well-balanced to let anything affect you deeply about that—that

[036-105]monkey!”

[036-106]The four photographs and the painted Florentine box went to their

[036-107]cremation within the same hour that Miss Fanny spoke; and a little

[036-108]later Lucy called her father in, as he passed her door, and pointed

[036-109]to the blackened area on the underside of the mantelpiece, and to the

[036-110]burnt heap upon the coal, where some metallic shapes still retained

[036-111]outline. She flung her arms about his neck in passionate sympathy,

[036-112]telling him that she knew what had happened to him; and presently he

[036-113]began to comfort her and managed an embarrassed laugh.

[036-114]“Well, well—” he said. “I was too old for such foolishness to be

[036-115]getting into my head, anyhow.”

[036-116]“No, no!” she sobbed. “And if you knew how I despise myself for—for

[036-117]ever having thought one instant about—oh, Miss Fanny called him the

[036-118]right name: that monkey! He is!”

[036-119]“There, I think I agree with you,” Eugene said grimly, and in his eyes

[036-120]there was a steady light of anger that was to last. “Yes, I think I

[036-121]agree with you about that!”

[036-122]“There’s only one thing to do with such a person,” she said vehemently.

[036-123]“That’s to put him out of our thoughts forever—forever!”

[036-124]And yet, the next day, at six o’clock, which was the hour, Fanny

[036-125]had told her, when George and his mother were to leave upon their

[036-126]long journey, Lucy touched that scorched place on her mantel with

[036-127]her hand just as the little clock above it struck. Then, after this

[036-128]odd, unconscious gesture, she went to a window and stood between the

[036-129]curtains, looking out into the cold November dusk; and in spite of

[036-130]every reasoning and reasonable power within her, a pain of loneliness

[036-131]struck through her heart. The dim street below her window, the dark

[036-132]houses across the way, the vague air itself—all looked empty, and cold

[036-133]and (most of all) uninteresting. Something more sombre than November

[036-134]dusk took the colour from them and gave them that air of desertion.

[036-135]The light of her fire, flickering up behind her, showed suddenly a

[036-136]flying group of tiny snowflakes nearing the window-pane; and for an

[036-137]instant she felt the sensation of being dragged through a snowdrift

[036-138]under a broken cutter, with a boy’s arms about her—an arrogant,

[036-139]handsome, too-conquering boy, who nevertheless did his best to get hurt

[036-140]himself, keeping her from any possible harm.

[036-141]She shook the picture out of her eyes indignantly, then came and sat

[036-142]before her fire, and looked long and long at the blackened mantelpiece.

[036-143]She did not have the mantelpiece repainted—and, since she did not,

[036-144]might as well have kept his photographs. One forgets what made the scar

[036-145]upon his hand but not what made the scar upon his wall.

[036-146]She played no marche funebre upon her piano, even though Chopin’s

[036-147]romantic lamentation was then at the top of nine-tenths of the

[036-148]music-racks in the country, American youth having recently discovered

[036-149]the distinguished congeniality between itself and this deathless bit

[036-150]of deathly gloom. She did not even play “Robin Adair”; she played

[036-151]“Bedelia” and all the new cake-walks, for she was her father’s

[036-152]housekeeper, and rightly looked upon the office as being the same as

[036-153]that of his heart-keeper. Therefore it was her affair to keep both

[036-154]house and heart in what state of cheerfulness might be contrived. She

[036-155]made him “go out” more than ever; made him take her to all the gayeties

[036-156]of that winter, declining to go herself unless he took her, and, though

[036-157]Eugene danced no more, and quoted Shakespeare to prove all lightfoot

[036-158]caperings beneath the dignity of his age, she broke his resolution for

[036-159]him at the New Year’s Eve “Assembly” and half coaxed, half dragged him

[036-160]forth upon the floor, and made him dance the New Year in with her.

[036-161]New faces appeared at the dances of the winter; new faces had been

[036-162]appearing everywhere, for that matter, and familiar ones were

[036-163]disappearing, merged in the increasing crowd, or gone forever and

[036-164]missed a little and not long; for the town was growing and changing as

[036-165]it never had grown and changed before.

[036-166]It was heaving up in the middle incredibly; it was spreading

[036-167]incredibly; and as it heaved and spread, it befouled itself and

[036-168]darkened its sky. Its boundary was mere shapelessness on the run; a

[036-169]raw, new house would appear on a country road; four or five others

[036-170]would presently be built at intervals between it and the outskirts of

[036-171]the town; the country road would turn into an asphalt street with a

[036-172]brick-faced drugstore and a frame grocery at a corner; then bungalows

[036-173]and six-room cottages would swiftly speckle the open green spaces—and

[036-174]a farm had become a suburb which would immediately shoot out other

[036-175]suburbs into the country, on one side, and, on the other, join itself

[036-176]solidly to the city. You drove between pleasant fields and woodland

[036-177]groves one spring day; and in the autumn, passing over the same ground,

[036-178]you were warned off the tracks by an interurban trolley-car’s gonging,

[036-179]and beheld, beyond cement sidewalks just dry, new house-owners busy

[036-180]“moving in.” Gasoline and electricity were performing the miracles

[036-181]Eugene had predicted.

[036-182]But the great change was in the citizenry itself. What was left of

[036-183]the patriotic old-stock generation that had fought the Civil War, and

[036-184]subsequently controlled politics, had become venerable and was little

[036-185]heeded. The descendants of the pioneers and early settlers were merging

[036-186]into the new crowd, becoming part of it, little to be distinguished

[036-187]from it. What happened to Boston and to Broadway happened in degree to

[036-188][[downtown-midland|the Midland city]]; the old stock became less and less typical, and of

[036-189]the grown people who called the place home, less than a third had been

[036-190]born in it. There was a German quarter; there was a Jewish quarter;

[036-191]there was a negro quarter—square miles of it—called “Bucktown”; there

[036-192]were many Irish neighbourhoods; and there were large settlements

[036-193]of Italians, and of Hungarians, and of Rumanians, and of Serbians

[036-194]and other Balkan peoples. But not the emigrants, themselves, were

[036-195]the almost dominant type on the streets downtown. That type was the

[036-196]emigrant’s prosperous offspring: descendant of the emigrations of the

[036-197]Seventies and Eighties and Nineties, those great folk-journeyings in

[036-198]search not so directly of freedom and democracy as of more [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] for

[036-199]the same labour. A new Midlander—in fact, a new American—was beginning

[036-200]dimly to emerge.
