[046-001]At other times [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]’s thoughts of George were anything but continuous,

[046-002]and weeks went by when he was not consciously in her mind at all. Her

[046-003]life was a busy one: she had the big house “to keep up”; she had a

[046-004]garden to keep up, too, a large and beautiful garden; she represented

[046-005]her father as a director for half a dozen public charity organizations,

[046-006]and did private charity work of her own, being a proxy mother of

[046-007]several large families; and she had “danced down,” as she said,

[046-008]groups from eight or nine classes of new graduates returned from the

[046-009]universities, without marrying any of them, but she still danced—and

[046-010]still did not marry.

[046-011]Her father, observing this circumstance happily, yet with some

[046-012]hypocritical concern, spoke of it to her one day as they stood in her

[046-013]garden. “I suppose I’d want to shoot him,” he said, with attempted

[046-014]lightness. “But I mustn’t be an old pig. I’d build you a beautiful

[046-015]house close by—just over yonder.”

[046-016]“No, no! That would be like—” she began impulsively; then checked

[046-017]herself. [[george-amberson|George Amberson]]’s comparison of the Georgian house to the

[046-018][[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]] had come into her mind, and she thought that another

[046-019]new house, built close by for her, would be like the house the Major

[046-020]built for Isabel.

[046-021]“Like what?”

[046-022]“Nothing.” She looked serious, and when he reverted to his idea of

[046-023]“some day” grudgingly surrendering her up to a suitor, she invented a

[046-024]legend. “Did you ever hear the Indian name for that little grove of

[046-025]beech trees on the other side of the house?” she asked him.

[046-026]“No—and you never did either!” he laughed.

[046-027]“Don’t be so sure! I read a great deal more than I used to—getting

[046-028]ready for my bookish days when I’ll have to do something solid in

[046-029]the evenings and won’t be asked to dance any more, even by the very

[046-030]youngest boys who think it’s a sporting event to dance with the oldest

[046-031]of the ‘older girls’. The name of the grove was ‘Loma-Nashah’ and it

[046-032]means ‘They-Couldn’t-Help-It’.”

[046-033]“Doesn’t sound like it.”

[046-034]“Indian names don’t. There was a bad Indian chief lived in the

[046-035]grove before the white settlers came. He was the worst Indian

[046-036]that ever lived, and his name was—it was ‘Vendonah.’ That means

[046-037]‘Rides-Down-Everything’.”

[046-038]“What?”

[046-039]“His name was Vendonah, the same thing as Rides-Down-Everything.”

[046-040]“I see,” said [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] thoughtfully. He gave her a quick look and then

[046-041]fixed his eyes upon the end of the garden path. “Go on.”

[046-042]“Vendonah was an unspeakable case,” Lucy continued. “He was so proud

[046-043]that he wore iron shoes and he walked over people’s faces with them. He

[046-044]was always killing people that way, and so at last the tribe decided

[046-045]that it wasn’t a good enough excuse for him that he was young and

[046-046]inexperienced—he’d have to go. They took him down to the river, and put

[046-047]him in a canoe, and pushed him out from shore; and then they ran along

[046-048]the bank and wouldn’t let him land, until at last the current carried

[046-049]the canoe out into the middle, and then on down to the ocean, and he

[046-050]never got back. They didn’t want him back, of course, and if he’d been

[046-051]able to manage it, they’d have put him in another canoe and shoved him

[046-052]out into the river again. But still, they didn’t elect another chief in

[046-053]his place. Other tribes thought that was curious, and wondered about

[046-054]it a lot, but finally they came to the conclusion that the beech grove

[046-055]people were afraid a new chief might turn out to be a bad Indian, too,

[046-056]and wear iron shoes like Vendonah. But they were wrong, because the

[046-057]real reason was that the tribe had led such an exciting life under

[046-058]Vendonah that they couldn’t settle down to anything tamer. He was

[046-059]awful, but he always kept things happening—terrible things, of course.

[046-060]They hated him, but they weren’t able to discover any other warrior

[046-061]that they wanted to make chief in his place. I suppose it was a little

[046-062]like drinking a glass of too strong wine and then trying to take the

[046-063]taste out of your mouth with barley water. They couldn’t help feeling

[046-064]that way.”

[046-065]“I see,” said Eugene. “So that’s why they named the place

[046-066]‘They-Couldn’t-Help-It’!”

[046-067]“It must have been.”

[046-068]“And so you’re going to stay here in your garden,” he said musingly.

[046-069]“You think it’s better to keep on walking these sunshiny gravel paths

[046-070]between your flower-beds, and growing to look like a pensive garden

[046-071]lady in a Victorian engraving.”

[046-072]“I suppose I’m like the tribe that lived here, papa. I had too much

[046-073]unpleasant excitement. It was unpleasant—but it was excitement. I don’t

[046-074]want any more; in fact, I don’t want anything but you.”

[046-075]“You don’t?” He looked at her keenly, and she laughed and shook her

[046-076]head; but he seemed perplexed, rather doubtful. “What was the name of

[046-077]the grove?” he asked. “The Indian name, I mean.”

[046-078]“Mola-Haha.”

[046-079]“No, it wasn’t; that wasn’t the name you said.”

[046-080]“I’ve forgotten.”

[046-081]“I see you have,” he said, his look of perplexity remaining. “Perhaps

[046-082]you remember the chief’s name better.”

[046-083]She shook her head again. “I don’t!”

[046-084]At this he laughed, but not very heartily, and walked slowly to the

[046-085]house, leaving her bending over a rose-bush, and a shade more pensive

[046-086]than the most pensive garden lady in any Victorian engraving.

[046-087]... Next day, it happened that this same “Vendonah” or

[046-088]“Rides-Down-Everything” became the subject of a chance conversation

[046-089]between Eugene and his old friend Kinney, father of the fire-topped

[046-090]Fred. The two gentlemen found themselves smoking in neighbouring

[046-091]leather chairs beside a broad window at the club, after lunch.

[046-092]Mr. Kinney had remarked that he expected to get his family established

[046-093]at the seashore by the Fourth of July, and, following a train of

[046-094]thought, he paused and chuckled. “Fourth of July reminds me,” he said.

[046-095]“Have you heard what that [[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]] is doing?”

[046-096]“No, I haven’t,” said Eugene, and his friend failed to notice the

[046-097]crispness of the utterance.

[046-098]“Well, sir,” Kinney chuckled again, “it beats the devil! My boy Fred

[046-099]told me about it yesterday. He’s a friend of this young Henry Akers,

[046-100]son of F. P. Akers of the Akers Chemical Company. It seems this

[046-101]young Akers asked Fred if he knew a fellow named Minafer, because

[046-102]he knew Fred had always lived here, and young Akers had heard some

[046-103]way that Minafer used to be an old [[pride-and-arrogance|family name]] here, and was sort of

[046-104]curious about it. Well, sir, you remember this young Georgie sort of

[046-105]disappeared, after his grandfather’s death, and nobody seemed to know

[046-106]much what had become of him—though I did hear, once or twice, that

[046-107]he was still around somewhere. Well, sir, he’s working for the Akers

[046-108]Chemical Company, out at their plant on the Thomasvile Road.”

[046-109]He paused, seeming to reserve something to be delivered only upon

[046-110]inquiry, and Eugene offered him the expected question, but only after a

[046-111]cold glance through the nose-glasses he had lately found it necessary

[046-112]to adopt. “What does he do?”

[046-113]Kinney laughed and slapped the arm of his chair.

[046-114]“He’s a nitroglycerin expert!”

[046-115]He was gratified to see that Eugene was surprised, if not, indeed, a

[046-116]little startled.

[046-117]“He’s what?”

[046-118]“He’s an expert on nitroglycerin. Doesn’t that beat the devil! Yes,

[046-119]sir! Young Akers told Fred that this George Minafer had worked like

[046-120]a houn’-dog ever since he got started out at the works. They have

[046-121]a special plant for nitroglycerin, way off from the main plant, o’

[046-122]course—in the woods somewhere—and George Minafer’s been working

[046-123]there, and lately they put him in charge of it. He oversees shooting

[046-124]oil-wells, too, and shoots ’em himself, sometimes. They aren’t allowed

[046-125]to carry it on the railroads, you know—have to team it. Young Akers

[046-126]says George rides around over the bumpy roads, sitting on as much as

[046-127]three hundred quarts of nitroglycerin! My Lord! Talk about romantic

[046-128]tumbles! If he gets blown sky-high some day he won’t have a bigger

[046-129]drop, when he comes down, than he’s already had! Don’t it beat the

[046-130]devil! Young Akers said he’s got all the nerve there is in the world.

[046-131]Well, he always did have plenty of that—from the time he used to ride

[046-132]around here on his white pony and fight all the Irish boys in Can-Town,

[046-133]with his long curls all handy to be pulled out. Akers says he gets a

[046-134]fair salary, and I should think he ought to! Seems to me I’ve heard

[046-135]the average life in that sort of work is somewhere around four years,

[046-136]and agents don’t write any insurance at all for nitroglycerin experts.

[046-137]Hardly!”

[046-138]“No,” said Eugene. “I suppose not.”

[046-139]Kinney rose to go. “Well, it’s a pretty funny thing—pretty odd, I

[046-140]mean—and I suppose it would be pass-around-the-hat for old [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]]

[046-141]Minafer if he blew up. Fred told me that they’re living in some

[046-142]apartment house, and said Georgie supports her. He was going to study

[046-143]law, but couldn’t earn enough that way to take care of Fanny, so he

[046-144]gave it up. Fred’s wife told him all this. Says Fanny doesn’t do

[046-145]anything but play bridge these days. Got to playing too high for awhile

[046-146]and lost more than she wanted to tell Georgie about, and borrowed a

[046-147]little from old Frank Bronson. Paid him back, though. Don’t know how

[046-148]Fred’s wife heard it. Women do hear the darndest things!”

[046-149]“They do,” Eugene agreed.

[046-150]“I thought you’d probably heard about it—thought most likely Fred’s

[046-151]wife might have said something to your daughter, especially as they’re

[046-152]cousins.”

[046-153]“I think not.”

[046-154]“Well, I’m off to the store,” said Mr. Kinney briskly; yet he lingered.

[046-155]“I suppose we’ll all have to club in and keep old Fanny out of the

[046-156]poorhouse if he does blow up. From all I hear it’s usually only a

[046-157]question of time. They say she hasn’t got anything else to depend on.”

[046-158]“I suppose not.”

[046-159]“Well—I wondered—” Kinney hesitated. “I was wondering why you hadn’t

[046-160]thought of finding something around your works for him. They say he’s

[046-161]an all-fired worker and he certainly does seem to have hid some decent

[046-162]stuff in him under all his damfoolishness. And you used to be such a

[046-163]tremendous friend of the family—I thought perhaps you—of course I know

[046-164]he’s a queer lot—I know—”

[046-165]“Yes, I think he is,” said Eugene. “No. I haven’t anything to offer

[046-166]him.”

[046-167]“I suppose not,” Kinney returned thoughtfully, as he went out. “I don’t

[046-168]know that I would myself. Well, we’ll probably see his name in the

[046-169]papers some day if he stays with that job!”

[046-170]However, the nitroglycerin expert of whom they spoke did not get into

[046-171]the papers as a consequence of being blown up, although his daily

[046-172]life was certainly a continuous exposure to that risk. Destiny has

[046-173]a constant passion for the incongruous, and it was George’s lot to

[046-174]manipulate wholesale quantities of terrific and volatile explosives

[046-175]in safety, and to be laid low by an accident so commonplace and

[046-176]inconsequent that it was a comedy. Fate had reserved for him the final

[046-177]insult of riding him down under the wheels of one of those juggernauts

[046-178]at which he had once shouted “Git a hoss!” Nevertheless, Fate’s ironic

[046-179]choice for Georgie’s undoing was not a big and swift and momentous car,

[046-180]such as Eugene manufactured; it was a specimen of the hustling little

[046-181]type that was flooding the country, the cheapest, commonest, hardiest

[046-182]little car ever made.

[046-183]The accident took place upon a Sunday morning, on a downtown crossing,

[046-184]with the streets almost empty, and no reason in the world for such a

[046-185]thing to happen. He had gone out for his Sunday morning walk, and he

[046-186]was thinking of an automobile at the very moment when the little car

[046-187]struck him; he was thinking of a shiny landaulet and a charming figure

[046-188]stepping into it, and of the quick gesture of a white glove toward the

[046-189]chauffeur, motioning him to go on. George heard a shout but did not

[046-190]look up, for he could not imagine anybody’s shouting at him, and he was

[046-191]too engrossed in the question “Was it Lucy?” He could not decide, and

[046-192]his lack of decision in this matter probably superinduced a lack of

[046-193]decision in another, more pressingly vital. At the second and louder

[046-194]shout he did look up; and the car was almost on him; but he could not

[046-195]make up his mind if the charming little figure he had seen was Lucy’s

[046-196]and he could not make up his mind whether to go backward or forward:

[046-197]these questions became entangled in his mind. Then, still not being

[046-198]able to decide which of two ways to go, he tried to go both—and the

[046-199]little car ran him down. It was not moving very rapidly, but it went

[046-200]all the way over George.
