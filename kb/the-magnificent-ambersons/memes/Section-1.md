Chapter I

[001-001][[major-amberson|Major Amberson]] had “made a fortune” in 1873, when other people were

[001-002]losing fortunes, and the magnificence of the Ambersons began then.

[001-003]Magnificence, like the size of a fortune, is always comparative, as

[001-004]even Magnificent Lorenzo may now perceive, if he has happened to haunt

[001-005]New York in 1916; and the Ambersons were magnificent in their day and

[001-006]place. Their splendour lasted throughout all the years that saw their

[001-007][[downtown-midland|Midland town]] spread and darken into a city, but reached its topmost

[001-008]during the period when every prosperous family with children kept a

[001-009]Newfoundland dog.

[001-010]In that town, in those days, all the women who wore silk or velvet

[001-011]knew all the other women who wore silk or velvet, and when there was

[001-012]a new purchase of sealskin, sick people were got to windows to see

[001-013]it go by. Trotters were out, in the winter afternoons, racing light

[001-014]sleighs on [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] and Tennessee Street; everybody recognized

[001-015]both the trotters and the drivers; and again knew them as well on

[001-016]summer evenings, when slim buggies whizzed by in renewals of the

[001-017]snow-time rivalry. For that matter, everybody knew everybody else’s

[001-018]family horse-and-carriage, could identify such a silhouette half a

[001-019]mile down the street, and thereby was sure who was going to market, or

[001-020]to a reception, or coming home from office or store to noon dinner or

[001-021]evening supper.

[001-022]During the earlier years of this period, elegance of personal

[001-023]appearance was believed to rest more upon the texture of garments than

[001-024]upon their shaping. A silk dress needed no remodelling when it was a

[001-025]year or so old; it remained distinguished by merely remaining silk.

[001-026]Old men and governors wore broadcloth; “full dress” was broadcloth

[001-027]with “doeskin” trousers; and there were seen men of all ages to whom

[001-028]a hat meant only that rigid, tall silk thing known to impudence as a

[001-029]“stove-pipe.” In town and country these men would wear no other hat,

[001-030]and, without self-consciousness, they went rowing in such hats.

[001-031]Shifting fashions of shape replaced aristocracy of texture:

[001-032]dressmakers, shoemakers, hatmakers, and tailors, increasing in cunning

[001-033]and in power, found means to make new clothes old. The long contagion

[001-034]of the “Derby” hat arrived: one season the crown of this hat would

[001-035]be a bucket; the next it would be a spoon. Every house still kept

[001-036]its bootjack, but high-topped boots gave way to shoes and “congress

[001-037]gaiters”; and these were played through fashions that shaped them now

[001-038]with toes like box-ends and now with toes like the prows of racing

[001-039]shells.

[001-040]Trousers with a crease were considered plebeian; the crease proved

[001-041]that the garment had lain upon a shelf, and hence was “ready-made”;

[001-042]these betraying trousers were called “hand-me-downs,” in allusion to

[001-043]the shelf. In the early ’eighties, while bangs and bustles were having

[001-044]their way with women, that variation of dandy known as the “dude”

[001-045]was invented: he wore trousers as tight as stockings, dagger-pointed

[001-046]shoes, a spoon “Derby,” a single-breasted coat called a “Chesterfield,”

[001-047]with short flaring skirts, a torturing cylindrical collar, laundered

[001-048]to a polish and three inches high, while his other neckgear might be

[001-049]a heavy, puffed cravat or a tiny bow fit for a doll’s braids. With

[001-050]evening dress he wore a tan overcoat so short that his black coat-tails

[001-051]hung visible, five inches below the over-coat; but after a season

[001-052]or two he lengthened his overcoat till it touched his heels, and he

[001-053]passed out of his tight trousers into trousers like great bags. Then,

[001-054]presently, he was seen no more, though the word that had been coined

[001-055]for him remained in the vocabularies of the impertinent.

[001-056]It was a hairier day than this. Beards were to the wearers’ fancy,

[001-057]and things as strange as the Kaiserliche boar-tusk moustache were

[001-058]commonplace. “Side-burns” found nourishment upon childlike profiles;

[001-059]great Dundreary whiskers blew like tippets over young shoulders;

[001-060]moustaches were trained as lambrequins over forgotten mouths; and it

[001-061]was possible for a Senator of the United States to wear a mist of white

[001-062]whisker upon his throat only, not a newspaper in the land finding the

[001-063]ornament distinguished enough to warrant a lampoon. Surely no more is

[001-064]needed to prove that so short a time ago we were living in another age!

[001-065]At the beginning of the Ambersons’ great period most of the houses of

[001-066]the Midland town were of a pleasant architecture. They lacked style,

[001-067]but also lacked pretentiousness, and whatever does not pretend at

[001-068]all has style enough. They stood in commodious yards, well shaded by

[001-069]leftover forest trees, elm and walnut and beech, with here and there a

[001-070]line of tall sycamores where the land had been made by filling bayous

[001-071]from the creek. The house of a “prominent resident,” facing Military

[001-072]Square, or National Avenue, or Tennessee Street, was built of brick

[001-073]upon a stone foundation, or of wood upon a brick foundation. Usually

[001-074]it had a “front porch” and a “back porch”; often a “side porch,” too.

[001-075]There was a “front hall”; there was a “side hall”; and sometimes a

[001-076]“back hall.” From the “front hall” opened three rooms, the “parlour,”

[001-077]the “sitting room,” and the “library”; and the library could show

[001-078]warrant to its title—for some reason these people bought books.

[001-079]Commonly, the family sat more in the library than in the “sitting

[001-080]room,” while callers, when they came formally, were kept to the

[001-081]“parlour,” a place of formidable polish and discomfort. The upholstery

[001-082]of the library furniture was a little shabby; but the hostile chairs

[001-083]and sofa of the “parlour” always looked new. For all the wear and tear

[001-084]they got they should have lasted a thousand years.

[001-085]Upstairs were the bedrooms; “mother-and-father’s room” the largest; a

[001-086]smaller room for one or two sons, another for one or two daughters;

[001-087]each of these rooms containing a double bed, a “washstand,” a “bureau,”

[001-088]a wardrobe, a little table, a rocking-chair, and often a chair or two

[001-089]that had been slightly damaged downstairs, but not enough to justify

[001-090]either the expense of repair or decisive abandonment in the attic. And

[001-091]there was always a “spare-room,” for visitors (where the sewing-machine

[001-092]usually was kept), and during the ‘seventies there developed an

[001-093]appreciation of the necessity for a bathroom. Therefore the architects

[001-094]placed bathrooms in the new houses, and the older houses tore out a

[001-095]cupboard or two, set up a boiler beside the kitchen stove, and sought a

[001-096]new godliness, each with its own bathroom. The great American plumber

[001-097]joke, that many-branched evergreen, was planted at this time.

[001-098]At the rear of the house, upstairs was a bleak little chamber, called

[001-099]“the girl’s room,” and in the stable there was another bedroom,

[001-100]adjoining the hayloft, and called “the hired man’s room.” House and

[001-101]stable cost seven or eight thousand dollars to build, and people with

[001-102]that much [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] to invest in such comforts were classified as the Rich.

[001-103]They paid the inhabitant of “the girl’s room” two dollars a week, and,

[001-104]in the latter part of this period, two dollars and a half, and finally

[001-105]three dollars a week. She was Irish, ordinarily, or German or it might

[001-106]be Scandinavian, but never native to the land unless she happened to be

[001-107]a person of colour. The man or youth who lived in the stable had like

[001-108]wages, and sometimes he, too, was lately a steerage voyager, but much

[001-109]oftener he was coloured.

[001-110]After sunrise, on pleasant mornings, the alleys behind the stables were

[001-111]gay; laughter and shouting went up and down their dusty lengths, with

[001-112]a lively accompaniment of curry-combs knocking against back fences and

[001-113]stable walls, for the darkies loved to curry their horses in the alley.

[001-114]Darkies always prefer to gossip in shouts instead of whispers; and they

[001-115]feel that profanity, unless it be vociferous, is almost worthless.

[001-116]Horrible phrases were caught by early rising children and carried to

[001-117]older people for definition, sometimes at inopportune moments; while

[001-118]less investigative children would often merely repeat the phrases in

[001-119]some subsequent flurry of agitation, and yet bring about consequences

[001-120]so emphatic as to be recalled with ease in middle life.

[001-121]They have passed, those darky hired-men of the Midland town; and the

[001-122]introspective horses they curried and brushed and whacked and amiably

[001-123]cursed—those good old horses switch their tails at flies no more. For

[001-124]all their seeming permanence they might as well have been buffaloes—or

[001-125]the buffalo laprobes that grew bald in patches and used to slide from

[001-126]the careless drivers’ knees and hang unconcerned, half way to the

[001-127]ground. The stables have been transformed into other likenesses, or

[001-128]swept away, like the woodsheds where were kept the stove-wood and

[001-129]kindling that the “girl” and the “hired-man” always quarrelled over:

[001-130]who should fetch it. Horse and stable and woodshed, and the whole tribe

[001-131]of the “hired-man,” all are gone. They went quickly, yet so silently

[001-132]that we whom they served have not yet really noticed that they are

[001-133]vanished.

[001-134]So with other vanishings. There were the little bunty street-cars

[001-135]on the long, single track that went its troubled way among the

[001-136]cobblestones. At the rear door of the car there was no platform, but a

[001-137]step where passengers clung in wet clumps when the weather was bad and

[001-138]the car crowded. The patrons—if not too absent-minded—put their fares

[001-139]into a slot; and no conductor paced the heaving floor, but the driver

[001-140]would rap remindingly with his elbow upon the glass of the door to his

[001-141]little open platform if the nickels and the passengers did not appear

[001-142]to coincide in number. A lone mule drew the car, and sometimes drew it

[001-143]off the track, when the passengers would get out and push it on again.

[001-144]They really owed it courtesies like this, for the car was genially

[001-145]accommodating: a lady could whistle to it from an upstairs window, and

[001-146]the car would halt at once and wait for her while she shut the window,

[001-147]put on her hat and cloak, went downstairs, found an umbrella, told the

[001-148]“girl” what to have for dinner, and came forth from the house.

[001-149]The previous passengers made little objection to such gallantry on

[001-150]the part of the car: they were wont to expect as much for themselves

[001-151]on like occasion. In good weather the mule pulled the car a mile in a

[001-152]little less than twenty minutes, unless the stops were too long; but

[001-153]when the trolley-car came, doing its mile in five minutes and better,

[001-154]it would wait for nobody. Nor could its passengers have endured such

[001-155]a thing, because the faster they were carried the less time they had

[001-156]to spare! In the days before deathly contrivances hustled them through

[001-157]their lives, and when they had no telephones—another ancient vacancy

[001-158]profoundly responsible for leisure—they had time for everything: time

[001-159]to think, to talk, time to read, time to wait for a lady!

[001-160]They even had time to dance “square dances,” quadrilles, and “lancers”;

[001-161]they also danced the “racquette,” and schottisches and polkas, and

[001-162]such whims as the “Portland Fancy.” They pushed back the sliding doors

[001-163]between the “parlour” and the “sitting room,” tacked down crash over

[001-164]the carpets, hired a few palms in green tubs, stationed three or four

[001-165]Italian musicians under the stairway in the “front hall”—and had great

[001-166]nights!

[001-167]But these people were gayest on New Year’s Day; they made it a true

[001-168]festival—something no longer known. The women gathered to “assist” the

[001-169]hostesses who kept “Open House”; and the carefree men, dandified and

[001-170]perfumed, went about in sleighs, or in carriages and ponderous “hacks,”

[001-171]going from Open House to Open House, leaving fantastic cards in fancy

[001-172]baskets as they entered each doorway, and emerging a little later, more

[001-173]carefree than ever, if the punch had been to their liking. It always

[001-174]was, and, as the afternoon wore on, pedestrians saw great gesturing and

[001-175]waving of skin-tight lemon gloves, while ruinous fragments of song were

[001-176]dropped behind as the carriages rolled up and down the streets.
