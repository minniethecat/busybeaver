[042-001]Other houses had become boarding-houses too genteel for signs, but

[042-002]many were franker, some offering “board by the day, week or meal,” and

[042-003]some, more laconic, contenting themselves with the label: “Rooms.”

[042-004]One, having torn out part of an old stone-trimmed bay window for

[042-005]purposes of commercial display, showed forth two suspended petticoats

[042-006]and a pair of oyster-coloured flannel trousers to prove the claims of

[042-007]its black-and-gilt sign: “French Cleaning and Dye House.” Its next

[042-008]neighbour also sported a remodelled front and permitted no doubt that

[042-009]its mission in life was to attend cosily upon death: “J. M. Rolsener.

[042-010]Caskets. The Funeral Home.” And beyond that, a plain old honest

[042-011]four-square gray-painted brick house was flamboyantly decorated with a

[042-012]great gilt scroll on the railing of the old-fashioned veranda: “Mutual

[042-013]Benev’t Order Cavaliers and Dames of Purity.” This was the old Minafer

[042-014]house.

[042-015]George passed it without perceptibly wincing; in fact, he held his head

[042-016]up, and except for his gravity of countenance and the prison pallor he

[042-017]had acquired by too constantly remaining indoors, there was little to

[042-018]warn an acquaintance that he was not precisely the same [[george-amberson|George Amberson]]

[042-019]Minafer known aforetime. He was still so magnificent, indeed, that

[042-020]there came to his ears a waft of comment from a passing automobile.

[042-021]This was a fearsome red car, glittering in brass, with half-a-dozen

[042-022]young people in it whose motorism had reached an extreme manifestation

[042-023]in dress. The ladies of this party were favourably affected at sight

[042-024]of the pedestrian upon the sidewalk, and, as the machine was moving

[042-025]slowly, and close to the curb, they had time to observe him in detail,

[042-026]which they did with a frankness not pleasing to the object of their

[042-027]attentions. “One sees so many nice-looking people one doesn’t know

[042-028]nowadays,” said the youngest of the young ladies. “This old town of

[042-029]ours is really getting enormous. I shouldn’t mind knowing who he is.”

[042-030]“I don’t know,” the youth beside her said, loudly enough to be heard at

[042-031]a considerable distance. “I don’t know who he is, but from his looks

[042-032]I know who he thinks he is: he thinks he’s the Grand Duke Cuthbert!”

[042-033]There was a burst of tittering as the car gathered speed and rolled

[042-034]away, with the girl continuing to look back until her scandalized

[042-035]companions forced her to turn by pulling her hood over her face.

[042-036]She made an impression upon George, so deep a one, in fact, that he

[042-037]unconsciously put his emotion into a muttered word:

[042-038]Riffraff!

[042-039]This was the last “walk home” he was ever to take by the route he was

[042-040]now following: up [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] to [[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]] and the two big

[042-041]old houses at the foot of [[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]]; for tonight would be

[042-042]the last night that he and [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] were to spend in the house which the

[042-043]Major had forgotten to deed to Isabel. To-morrow they were to “move

[042-044]out,” and George was to begin his work in Bronson’s office. He had not

[042-045]come to this collapse without a fierce struggle—but the struggle was

[042-046]inward, and the rolling world was not agitated by it, and rolled calmly

[042-047]on. For of all the “ideals of life” which the world, in its rolling,

[042-048]inconsiderately flattens out to nothingness, the least likely to retain

[042-049]a profile is that ideal which depends upon inheriting [[wealth-and-fortune|money]]. George

[042-050]Amberson, in spite of his record of failures in business, had spoken

[042-051]shrewdly when he realized at last that money, like life, was “like

[042-052]quicksilver in a nest of cracks.” And his nephew had the awakening

[042-053]experience of seeing the great Amberson Estate vanishing into such

[042-054]a nest—in a twinkling, it seemed, now that it was indeed so utterly

[042-055]vanished.

[042-056]His uncle had suggested that he might write to college friends; perhaps

[042-057]they could help him to something better than the prospect offered

[042-058]by Bronson’s office; but George flushed and shook his head, without

[042-059]explaining. In that small and quietly superior “crowd” of his he had

[042-060]too emphatically supported the ideal of being rather than doing. He

[042-061]could not appeal to one of its members now to help him to a job.

[042-062]Besides, they were not precisely the warmest-hearted crew in the world,

[042-063]and he had long ago dropped the last affectation of a correspondence

[042-064]with any of them. He was as aloof from any survival of intimacy with

[042-065]his boyhood friends in the city, and, in truth, had lost track of

[042-066]most of them. “The Friends of the Ace,” once bound by oath to succour

[042-067]one another in peril or poverty, were long ago dispersed; one or two

[042-068]had died; one or two had gone to live elsewhere; the others were

[042-069]disappeared into the smoky bigness of the heavy city. Of the brethren,

[042-070]there remained within his present cognizance only his old enemy, the

[042-071]red-haired Kinney, now married to Janie Sharon, and Charlie Johnson,

[042-072]who, out of deference to his mother’s memory, had passed [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]]

[042-073]Mansion one day, when George stood upon the front steps, and, looking

[042-074]in fiercely, had looked away with continued fierceness—his only token

[042-075]of recognition.

[042-076]On this last homeward walk of his, when George reached the entrance

[042-077]to Amberson Addition—that is, when he came to where the entrance had

[042-078]formerly been—he gave a little start, and halted for a moment to

[042-079]stare. This was the first time he had noticed that the stone pillars,

[042-080]marking the entrance, had been removed. Then he realized that for

[042-081]a long time he had been conscious of a queerness about this corner

[042-082]without being aware of what made the difference. National Avenue met

[042-083]Amberson Boulevard here at an obtuse angle, and the removal of the

[042-084]pillars made the Boulevard seem a cross-street of no overpowering

[042-085]importance—certainly it did not seem to be a boulevard!

[042-086]At the next corner Neptune’s Fountain remained, and one could still

[042-087]determine with accuracy what its designer’s intentions had been. It

[042-088]stood in sore need of just one last kindness; and if the thing had

[042-089]possessed any friends they would have done that doleful shovelling

[042-090]after dark.

[042-091]George did not let his eyes linger upon the relic; nor did he look

[042-092]steadfastly at the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]]. Massive as the old house was, it

[042-093]managed to look gaunt: its windows stared with the skull emptiness of

[042-094]all windows in empty houses that are to be lived in no more. Of course

[042-095]the rowdy boys of the neighbourhood had been at work: many of these

[042-096]haggard windows were broken; the front door stood ajar, forced open;

[042-097]and idiot salacity, in white chalk, was smeared everywhere upon the

[042-098]pillars and stonework of the verandas.

[042-099]George walked by the Mansion hurriedly, and came home to his mother’s

[042-100]house for the last time.

[042-101]Emptiness was there, too, and the closing of the door resounded through

[042-102]bare rooms; for downstairs there was no furniture in the house except

[042-103]a kitchen table in the dining room, which Fanny had kept “for dinner,”

[042-104]she said, though as she was to cook and serve that meal herself George

[042-105]had his doubts about her name for it. Upstairs, she had retained her

[042-106]own furniture, and George had been living in his mother’s room, having

[042-107]sent everything from his own to the auction. Isabel’s room was still

[042-108]as it had been, but the furniture would be moved with Fanny’s to new

[042-109]quarters in the morning. Fanny had made plans for her nephew as well

[042-110]as herself; she had found a three-room “kitchenette apartment” in an

[042-111]apartment house where several old friends of hers had established

[042-112]themselves—elderly widows of citizens once “prominent” and other

[042-113]retired gentry. People used their own “kitchenettes” for breakfast

[042-114]and lunch, but there was a table-d’hote arrangement for dinner on

[042-115]the ground floor; and after dinner bridge was played all evening, an

[042-116]attraction powerful with Fanny. She had “made all the arrangements,”

[042-117]she reported, and nervously appealed for approval, asking if she hadn’t

[042-118]shown herself “pretty practical” in such matters. George acquiesced

[042-119]absent-mindedly, not thinking of what she said and not realizing to

[042-120]what it committed him.

[042-121]He began to realize it now, as he wandered about the dismantled

[042-122]house; he was far from sure that he was willing to go and live in a

[042-123]“three-room apartment” with Fanny and eat breakfast and lunch with her

[042-124](prepared by herself in the “kitchenette”) and dinner at the table

[042-125]d’hote in “such a pretty Colonial dining room” (so Fanny described it)

[042-126]at a little round table they would have all to themselves in the midst

[042-127]of a dozen little round tables which other relics of disrupted families

[042-128]would have all to themselves. For the first time, now that the change

[042-129]was imminent, George began to develop before his mind’s eye pictures

[042-130]of what he was in for; and they appalled him. He decided that such a

[042-131]life verged upon the sheerly unbearable, and that after all there were

[042-132]some things left that he just couldn’t stand. So he made up his mind to

[042-133]speak to his aunt about it at “dinner,” and tell her that he preferred

[042-134]to ask Bronson to let him put a sofa-bed, a trunk, and a folding rubber

[042-135]bathtub behind a screen in the dark rear room of the office. George

[042-136]felt that this would be infinitely more tolerable; and he could eat at

[042-137]restaurants, especially as about all he ever wanted nowadays was coffee.

[042-138]But at “dinner” he decided to put off telling Fanny of his plan until

[042-139]later: she was so nervous, and so distressed about the failure of her

[042-140]efforts with sweetbreads and macaroni; and she was so eager in her talk

[042-141]of how comfortable they would be “by this time to-morrow night.” She

[042-142]fluttered on, her nervousness increasing, saying how “nice” it would

[042-143]be for him, when he came from work in the evenings, to be among “nice

[042-144]people—people who know who we are,” and to have a pleasant game of

[042-145]bridge with “people who are really old friends of the family?”

[042-146]When they stopped probing among the scorched fragments she had set

[042-147]forth, George lingered downstairs, waiting for a better opportunity

[042-148]to introduce his own subject, but when he heard dismaying sounds from

[042-149]the kitchen he gave up. There was a crash, then a shower of crashes;

[042-150]falling tin clamoured to be heard above the shattering of porcelain;

[042-151]and over all rose Fanny’s wail of lamentation for the treasures saved

[042-152]from the sale, but now lost forever to the “kitchenette.” Fanny was

[042-153]nervous indeed; so nervous that she could not trust her hands.

[042-154]For a moment George thought she might have been injured, but, before he

[042-155]reached the kitchen, he heard her sweeping at the fragments, and turned

[042-156]back. He put off speaking to Fanny until morning.

[042-157]Things more insistent than his vague plans for a sofa-bed in Bronson’s

[042-158]office had possession of his mind as he went upstairs, moving his hand

[042-159]slowly along the smooth walnut railing of the balustrade. Half way to

[042-160]the landing he stopped, turned, and stood looking down at the heavy

[042-161]doors masking the black emptiness that had been the library. Here he

[042-162]had stood on what he now knew was the worst day of his life; here he

[042-163]had stood when his mother passed through that doorway, hand-in-hand

[042-164]with her brother, to learn what her son had done.

[042-165]He went on more heavily, more slowly; and, more heavily and slowly

[042-166]still, entered Isabel’s room and shut the door. He did not come forth

[042-167]again, and bade Fanny good-night through the closed door when she

[042-168]stopped outside it later.

[042-169]“I’ve put all the lights out, George,” she said. “Everything’s all

[042-170]right.”

[042-171]“Very well,” he called. “Good-night.”

[042-172]She did not go. “I’m sure we’re going to enjoy the new little home,

[042-173]George,” she said timidly. “I’ll try hard to make things nice for you,

[042-174]and the people really are lovely. You mustn’t feel as if things are

[042-175]altogether gloomy, George. I know everything’s going to turn out all

[042-176]right. You’re young and strong and you have a good mind and I’m sure—”

[042-177]she hesitated—“I’m sure your mother’s watching over you, Georgie.

[042-178]Good-night, dear.”
