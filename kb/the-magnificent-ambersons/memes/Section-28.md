[028-001]The two began to walk on toward the gate, where they stopped again,

[028-002]turning to face each other, and Isabel’s glance, passing [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]], fell

[028-003]upon George. Instantly she smiled and waved her hand to him; while

[028-004]Eugene turned and nodded; but George, standing as in some rigid trance,

[028-005]and staring straight at them, gave these signals of greeting no sign of

[028-006]recognition whatever. Upon this, Isabel called to him, waving her hand

[028-007]again.

[028-008]“Georgie!” she called, laughing. “Wake up, dear! Georgie, hello!”

[028-009]George turned away as if he had neither seen nor heard, and stalked

[028-010]into the house by the side door.

Chapter XXI

[028-011]He went to his room, threw off his coat, waistcoat, collar, and tie,

[028-012]letting them lie where they chanced to fall, and then, having violently

[028-013]enveloped himself in a black velvet dressing-gown, continued this

[028-014]action by lying down with a vehemence that brought a wheeze of protest

[028-015]from his bed. His repose was only a momentary semblance, however, for

[028-016]it lasted no longer than the time it took him to groan “Riffraff!”

[028-017]between his teeth. Then he sat up, swung his feet to the floor, rose,

[028-018]and began to pace up and down the large room.

[028-019]He had just been consciously rude to his mother for the first time in

[028-020]his life; for, with all his riding down of populace and riffraff, he

[028-021]had never before been either deliberately or impulsively disregardful

[028-022]of her. When he had hurt her it had been accidental; and his remorse

[028-023]for such an accident was always adequate compensation—and more—to

[028-024]Isabel. But now he had done a rough thing to her; and he did not

[028-025]repent; rather he was the more irritated with her. And when he heard

[028-026]her presently go by his door with a light step, singing cheerfully to

[028-027]herself as she went to her room, he perceived that she had mistaken his

[028-028]intention altogether, or, indeed, had failed to perceive that he had

[028-029]any intention at all. Evidently she had concluded that he refused to

[028-030]speak to her and Morgan out of sheer absent-mindedness, supposing him

[028-031]so immersed in some preoccupation that he had not seen them or heard

[028-032]her calling to him. Therefore there was nothing of which to repent,

[028-033]even if he had been so minded; and probably Eugene himself was unaware

[028-034]that any disapproval had recently been expressed. George snorted. What

[028-035]sort of a dreamy loon did they take him to be?

[028-036]There came a delicate, eager tapping at his door, not done with a

[028-037]knuckle but with the tip of a fingernail, which was instantly clarified

[028-038]to George’s mind’s eye as plainly as if he saw it: the long and

[028-039]polished white-mooned pink shield on the end of his [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]]’s right

[028-040]forefinger. But George was in no mood for human communications, and

[028-041]even when things went well he had little pleasure in Fanny’s society.

[028-042]Therefore it is not surprising that at the sound of her tapping,

[028-043]instead of bidding her enter, he immediately crossed the room with the

[028-044]intention of locking the door to keep her out.

[028-045]Fanny was too eager, and, opening the door before he reached it, came

[028-046]quickly in, and closed it behind her. She was in a street dress and a

[028-047]black hat, with a black umbrella in her black-gloved hand—for Fanny’s

[028-048]heavy mourning, at least, was nowhere tempered with a glimpse of white,

[028-049]though the anniversary of Wilbur’s death had passed. An infinitesimal

[028-050]perspiration gleamed upon her pale skin; she breathed fast, as if she

[028-051]had run up the stairs; and excitement was sharp in her widened eyes.

[028-052]Her look was that of a person who had just seen something extraordinary

[028-053]or heard thrilling news.

[028-054]“Now, what on earth do you want?” her chilling nephew demanded.

[028-055]“George,” she said hurriedly, “I saw what you did when you wouldn’t

[028-056]speak to them. I was sitting with Mrs. Johnson at her front window,

[028-057]across the street, and I saw it all.”

[028-058]“Well, what of it?”

[028-059]“You did right!” Fanny said with a vehemence not the less spirited

[028-060]because she suppressed her voice almost to a whisper. “You did exactly

[028-061]right! You’re behaving splendidly about the whole thing, and I want to

[028-062]tell you I know your father would thank you if he could see what you’re

[028-063]doing.”

[028-064]“My Lord!” George broke out at her. “You make me dizzy! For heaven’s

[028-065]sake quit the mysterious detective business—at least do quit it around

[028-066]me! Go and try it on somebody else, if you like; but I don’t want to

[028-067]hear it!”

[028-068]She began to tremble, regarding him with a fixed gaze. “You don’t care

[028-069]to hear then,” she said huskily, “that I approve of what you’re doing?”

[028-070]“Certainly not! Since I haven’t the faintest idea what you think I’m

[028-071]‘doing,’ naturally I don’t care whether you approve of it or not. All

[028-072]I’d like, if you please, is to be alone. I’m not giving a tea here,

[028-073]this afternoon, if you’ll permit me to mention it!”

[028-074]Fanny’s gaze wavered; she began to blink; then suddenly she sank into a

[028-075]chair and wept silently, but with a terrible desolation.

[028-076]“Oh, for the Lord’s sake!” he moaned. “What in the world is wrong with

[028-077]you?”

[028-078]“You’re always picking on me,” she quavered wretchedly, her voice

[028-079]indistinct with the wetness that bubbled into it from her tears. “You

[028-080]do—you always pick on me! You’ve always done it—always—ever since you

[028-081]were a little boy! Whenever anything goes wrong with you, you take it

[028-082]out on me! You do! You always—”

[028-083]George flung to heaven a gesture of despair; it seemed to him the last

[028-084]straw that Fanny should have chosen this particular time to come and

[028-085]sob in his room over his mistreatment of her!

[028-086]“Oh, my Lord!” he whispered; then, with a great effort, addressed her

[028-087]in a reasonable tone: “Look here, Aunt Fanny; I don’t see what you’re

[028-088]making all this fuss about. Of course I know I’ve teased you sometimes,

[028-089]but—”

[028-090]“‘Teased’ me?” she wailed. “‘Teased’ me! Oh, it does seem too hard,

[028-091]sometimes—this mean old life of mine does seem too hard! I don’t think

[028-092]I can stand it! Honestly, I don’t think I can! I came in here just

[028-093]to show you I sympathized with you—just to say something pleasant

[028-094]to you, and you treat me as if I were—oh, no, you wouldn’t treat

[028-095]a servant the way you treat me! You wouldn’t treat anybody in the

[028-096]world like this except old Fanny! ‘Old Fanny’ you say. ‘It’s nobody

[028-097]but old Fanny, so I’ll kick her—nobody will resent it. I’ll kick her

[028-098]all I want to!’ You do! That’s how you think of me—I know it! And

[028-099]you’re right: I haven’t got anything in the world, since my brother

[028-100]died—nobody—nothing—nothing!”

[028-101]“Oh my Lord!” George groaned.

[028-102]Fanny spread out her small, soaked handkerchief, and shook it in the

[028-103]air to dry it a little, crying as damply and as wretchedly during this

[028-104]operation as before—a sight which gave George a curious shock to add

[028-105]to his other agitations, it seemed so strange. “I ought not to have

[028-106]come,” she went on, “because I might have known it would only give you

[028-107]an excuse to pick on me again! I’m sorry enough I came, I can tell

[028-108]you! I didn’t mean to speak of it again to you, at all; and I wouldn’t

[028-109]have, but I saw how you treated them, and I guess I got excited about

[028-110]it, and couldn’t help following the impulse—but I’ll know better next

[028-111]time, I can tell you! I’ll keep my mouth shut as I meant to, and as I

[028-112]would have, if I hadn’t got excited and if I hadn’t felt sorry for you.

[028-113]But what does it matter to anybody if I’m sorry for them? I’m only old

[028-114]Fanny!”

[028-115]“Oh, good gracious! How can it matter to me who’s sorry for me when I

[028-116]don’t know what they’re sorry about!”

[028-117]“You’re so proud,” she quavered, “and so hard! I tell you I didn’t mean

[028-118]to speak of it to you, and I never, never in the world would have told

[028-119]you about it, nor have made the faintest reference to it, if I hadn’t

[028-120]seen that somebody else had told you, or you’d found out for yourself

[028-121]some way. I—”

[028-122]In despair of her intelligence, and in some doubt of his own, George

[028-123]struck the palms of his hands together. “Somebody else had told me

[028-124]what? I’d found what out for myself?”

[028-125]“How people are talking about your mother.”

[028-126]Except for the incidental teariness of her voice, her tone was casual,

[028-127]as though she mentioned a subject previously discussed and understood;

[028-128]for Fanny had no doubt that George had only pretended to be mystified

[028-129]because, in his pride, he would not in words admit that he knew what he

[028-130]knew.

[028-131]“What did you say?” he asked incredulously.

[028-132]“Of course I understood what you were doing,” Fanny went on, drying her

[028-133]handkerchief again. “It puzzled other people when you began to be rude

[028-134]to Eugene, because they couldn’t see how you could treat him as you

[028-135]did when you were so interested in [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]. But I remembered how you came

[028-136]to me, that other time when there was so much talk about Isabel; and I

[028-137]knew you’d give Lucy up in a minute, if it came to a question of your

[028-138]mother’s reputation, because you said then that—”

[028-139]“Look here,” George interrupted in a shaking voice. “Look here, I’d

[028-140]like—” He stopped, unable to go on, his agitation was so great. His

[028-141]chest heaved as from hard running, and his complexion, pallid at

[028-142]first, had become mottled; fiery splotches appearing at his temples

[028-143]and cheeks. “What do you mean by telling me—telling me there’s talk

[028-144]about—about—” He gulped, and began again: “What do you mean by using

[028-145]such words as ‘reputation’? What do you mean, speaking of a ‘question’

[028-146]of my—my mother’s reputation?”

[028-147]Fanny looked up at him woefully over the handkerchief which she now

[028-148]applied to her reddened nose. “God knows I’m sorry for you, George,”

[028-149]she murmured. “I wanted to say so, but it’s only old Fanny, so whatever

[028-150]she says—even when it’s sympathy—pick on her for it! Hammer her!” She

[028-151]sobbed. “Hammer her! It’s only poor old lonely Fanny!”

[028-152]“You look here!” George said harshly. “When I spoke to my [[george-amberson|Uncle George]]

[028-153]after that rotten thing I heard [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]] say about my mother, he

[028-154]said if there was any gossip it was about you! He said people might be

[028-155]laughing about the way you ran after Morgan, but that was all.”

[028-156]Fanny lifted her hands, clenched them, and struck them upon her knees.

[028-157]“Yes; it’s always Fanny!” she sobbed. “Ridiculous old Fanny—always,

[028-158]always!”

[028-159]“You listen!” George said. “After I’d talked to Uncle George I saw

[028-160]you; and you said I had a mean little mind for thinking there might be

[028-161]truth in what Aunt Amelia said about people talking. You denied it.

[028-162]And that wasn’t the only time; you’d attacked me before then, because

[028-163]I intimated that Morgan might be coming here too often. You made me

[028-164]believe that mother let him come entirely on your account, and now you

[028-165]say—”

[028-166]“I think he did,” Fanny interrupted desolately. “I think he did come

[028-167]as much to see me as anything—for a while it looked like it. Anyhow,

[028-168]he liked to dance with me. He danced with me as much as he danced with

[028-169]her, and he acted as if he came on my account at least as much as he

[028-170]did on hers. He did act a good deal that way—and if Wilbur hadn’t died—”

[028-171]“You told me there wasn’t any talk.”

[028-172]“I didn’t think there was much, then,” Fanny protested. “I didn’t know

[028-173]how much there was.”

[028-174]“What!”

[028-175]“People don’t come and tell such things to a person’s family, you know.

[028-176]You don’t suppose anybody was going to say to George Amberson that his

[028-177]sister was getting herself talked about, do you? Or that they were

[028-178]going to say much to me?”
