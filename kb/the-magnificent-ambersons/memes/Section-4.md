[004-001]“Georgie, you mustn’t say ‘liar.’ Isn’t this letter the truth?”

[004-002]“Well,” said Georgie, “how old am I?”

[004-003]“Ten.”

[004-004]“Well, look how he says I’m older than a boy eleven years old.”

[004-005]“That’s true,” said Isabel. “He does. But isn’t some of it true,

[004-006]Georgie?”

[004-007]Georgie felt himself to be in a difficulty here, and he was silent.

[004-008]“Georgie, did you say what he says you did?”

[004-009]“Which one?”

[004-010]“Did you tell him to—to—Did you say, ‘Go to hell?’”

[004-011]Georgie looked worried for a moment longer; then he brightened. “Listen

[004-012]here, mamma; grandpa wouldn’t wipe his shoe on that ole story-teller,

[004-013]would he?”

[004-014]“Georgie, you mustn’t—”

[004-015]“I mean: none of the Ambersons wouldn’t have anything to do with him,

[004-016]would they? He doesn’t even know _you_, does he, mamma?”

[004-017]“That hasn’t anything to do with it.”

[004-018]“Yes, it has! I mean: none of [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] family go to see him, and

[004-019]they never have him come in their house; they wouldn’t ask him to, and

[004-020]they prob’ly wouldn’t even let him.”

[004-021]“That isn’t what we’re talking about.”

[004-022]“I bet,” said Georgie emphatically, “I bet if he wanted to see any of

[004-023]’em, he’d haf to go around to the side door!”

[004-024]“No, dear, they—”

[004-025]“Yes, they would, mamma! So what does it matter if I did say somep’m’

[004-026]to him he didn’t like? That kind o’ people, I don’t see why you can’t

[004-027]say anything you want to, to ’em!”

[004-028]“No, Georgie. And you haven’t answered me whether you said that

[004-029]dreadful thing he says you did.”

[004-030]“Well—” said Georgie. “Anyway, he said somep’m’ to me that made me

[004-031]mad.” And upon this point he offered no further details; he would not

[004-032]explain to his mother that what had made him “mad” was Mr. Smith’s

[004-033]hasty condemnation of herself: “Your mother ought to be ashamed,” and,

[004-034]“A woman that lets a bad boy like you—” Georgie did not even consider

[004-035]excusing himself by quoting these insolences.

[004-036]Isabel stroked his head. “They were terrible words for you to use,

[004-037]dear. From his letter he doesn’t seem a very tactful person, but—”

[004-038]“He’s just riffraff,” said Georgie.

[004-039]“You mustn’t say so,” his mother gently agreed “Where did you learn

[004-040]those bad words he speaks of? Where did you hear any one use them?”

[004-041]“Well, I’ve heard ’em several places. I guess [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] Amberson was

[004-042]the _first_ I ever heard say ’em. Uncle George Amberson said ’em to

[004-043]papa once. Papa didn’t like it, but Uncle George was just laughin’ at

[004-044]papa, an’ then he said ’em while he was laughin’.”

[004-045]“That was wrong of him,” she said, but almost instinctively he detected

[004-046]the lack of conviction in her tone. It was Isabel’s great failing

[004-047]that whatever an Amberson did seemed right to her, especially if the

[004-048]Amberson was either her brother George, or her son George. She knew

[004-049]that she should be more severe with the latter now, but severity with

[004-050]him was beyond her power; and the Reverend Malloch Smith had succeeded

[004-051]only in rousing her resentment against himself. Georgie’s symmetrical

[004-052]face—altogether an Amberson face—had looked never more beautiful to

[004-053]her. It always looked unusually beautiful when she tried to be severe

[004-054]with him. “You must promise me,” she said feebly, “never to use those

[004-055]bad words again.”

[004-056]“I promise not to,” he said promptly—and he whispered an immediate

[004-057]codicil under his breath: “Unless I get mad at somebody!” This

[004-058]satisfied a code according to which, in his own sincere belief, he

[004-059]never told lies.

[004-060]“That’s a good boy,” she said, and he ran out to the yard, his

[004-061]punishment over. Some admiring friends were gathered there; they had

[004-062]heard of his adventure, knew of the note, and were waiting to see what

[004-063]was going to “happen” to him. They hoped for an account of things, and

[004-064]also that he would allow them to “take turns” riding his pony to the

[004-065]end of the alley and back.

[004-066]They were really his henchmen: Georgie was a lord among boys. In fact,

[004-067]he was a personage among certain sorts of grown people, and was often

[004-068]fawned upon; the alley negroes delighted in him, chuckled over him,

[004-069]flattered him slavishly. For that matter, he often heard well-dressed

[004-070]people speaking of him admiringly: a group of ladies once gathered

[004-071]about him on the pavement where he was spinning a top. “I _know_

[004-072]this is Georgie!” one exclaimed, and turned to the others with the

[004-073]impressiveness of a showman. “[[major-amberson|Major Amberson]]’s only grandchild!” The

[004-074]others said, “It is?” and made clicking sounds with their mouths; two

[004-075]of them loudly whispering, “So handsome!”

[004-076]Georgie, annoyed because they kept standing upon the circle he had

[004-077]chalked for his top, looked at them coldly and offered a suggestion:

[004-078]“Oh, go hire a hall!”

[004-079]As an Amberson, he was already a public character, and the story of

[004-080]his adventure in the Reverend Malloch Smith’s front yard became a town

[004-081]topic. Many people glanced at him with great distaste, thereafter,

[004-082]when they chanced to encounter him, which meant nothing to Georgie,

[004-083]because he innocently believed most grown people to be necessarily

[004-084]cross-looking as a normal phenomenon resulting from the adult state;

[004-085]and he failed to comprehend that the distasteful glances had any

[004-086]personal bearing upon himself. If he had perceived such a bearing,

[004-087]he would have been affected only so far, probably, as to mutter,

[004-088]“Riffraff!” Possibly he would have shouted it; and, certainly, most

[004-089]people believed a story that went round the town just after Mrs.

[004-090]Amberson’s funeral, when Georgie was eleven. Georgie was reported to

[004-091]have differed with the undertaker about the seating of the family; his

[004-092]indignant voice had become audible: “Well, who is the most important

[004-093]person at my own grandmother’s funeral?” And later he had projected

[004-094]his head from the window of the foremost mourners’ carriage, as the

[004-095]undertaker happened to pass.

[004-096]“_Riffraff!_”

[004-097]There were people—grown people they were—who expressed themselves

[004-098]longingly: they _did_ hope to live to see the day, they said, when

[004-099]that boy would get his come-upance! (They used that honest word, so

[004-100]much better than “deserts,” and not until many years later to be more

[004-101]clumsily rendered as “what is coming to him.”) _Something_ was bound

[004-102]to take him down, some day, and they only wanted to be there! But

[004-103]Georgie heard nothing of this, and the yearners for his taking down

[004-104]went unsatisfied, while their yearning grew the greater as the happy

[004-105]day of fulfilment was longer and longer postponed. His grandeur was not

[004-106]diminished by the Malloch Smith story; the rather it was increased, and

[004-107]among other children (especially among little girls) there was added to

[004-108]the prestige of his gilded position that diabolical glamour which must

[004-109]inevitably attend a boy who has told a minister to go to hell.

Chapter III

[004-110]Until he reached the age of twelve, Georgie’s education was a domestic

[004-111]process; tutors came to the house; and those citizens who yearned

[004-112]for his taking down often said: “Just wait till he has to go to

[004-113]public school; _then_ he’ll get it!” But at twelve Georgie was sent

[004-114]to a private school in the town, and there came from this small and

[004-115]dependent institution no report, or even rumour, of Georgie’s getting

[004-116]anything that he was thought to deserve; therefore the yearning still

[004-117]persisted, though growing gaunt with feeding upon itself. For, although

[004-118]Georgie’s pomposities and impudence in the little school were often

[004-119]almost unbearable, the teachers were fascinated by him. They did

[004-120]not like him—he was too arrogant for that—but he kept them in such

[004-121]a state of emotion that they thought more about him than they did

[004-122]about all of the other ten pupils. The emotion he kept them in was

[004-123]usually one resulting from injured self-respect, but sometimes it was

[004-124]dazzled admiration. So far as their conscientious observation went, he

[004-125]“studied” his lessons sparingly; but sometimes, in class, he flashed an

[004-126]admirable answer, with a comprehension not often shown by the pupils

[004-127]they taught; and he passed his examinations easily. In all, without

[004-128]discernible effort, he acquired at this school some rudiments of a

[004-129]liberal education and learned nothing whatever about himself.

[004-130]The yearners were still yearning when Georgie, at sixteen, was sent

[004-131]away to a great “Prep School.” “Now,” they said brightly, “he’ll get

[004-132]it! He’ll find himself among boys just as important in their home towns

[004-133]as he is, and they’ll knock the stuffing out of him when he puts on

[004-134]his airs with them! Oh, but that _would_ be worth something to see!”

[004-135]They were mistaken, it appeared, for when Georgie returned, a few

[004-136]months later, he still seemed to have the same stuffing. He had been

[004-137]deported by the authorities, the offense being stated as “insolence

[004-138]and profanity”; in fact, he had given the principal of the school

[004-139]instructions almost identical with those formerly objected to by the

[004-140]Reverend Malloch Smith.

[004-141]But he had not got his come-upance, and those who counted upon it

[004-142]were embittered by his appearance upon the down-town streets driving

[004-143]a dog-cart at criminal speed, making pedestrians retreat from the

[004-144]crossings, and behaving generally as if he “owned the earth.” A

[004-145]disgusted hardware dealer of middle age, one of those who hungered for

[004-146]Georgie’s downfall, was thus driven back upon the sidewalk to avoid

[004-147]being run over, and so far forgot himself as to make use of the pet

[004-148]street insult of the year: “Got ’ny _sense!_ See here, bub, does your

[004-149]mother know you’re out?”

[004-150]Georgie, without even seeming to look at him, flicked the long lash of

[004-151]his whip dexterously, and a little spurt of dust came from the hardware

[004-152]man’s trousers, not far below the waist. He was not made of hardware:

[004-153]he raved, looking for a missile; then, finding none, commanded himself

[004-154]sufficiently to shout after the rapid dog-cart: “Turn down your pants,

[004-155]you would-be dude! Raining in dear ole Lunnon! Git off the earth!”

[004-156]Georgie gave him no encouragement to think that he was heard. The

[004-157]dog-cart turned the next corner, causing indignation there, likewise,

[004-158]and, having proceeded some distance farther, halted in front of the

[004-159]“Amberson Block”—an old-fashioned four-story brick warren of lawyers’

[004-160]offices, insurance and realestate offices, with a “drygoods store”

[004-161]occupying the ground floor. Georgie tied his lathered trotter to

[004-162]a telegraph pole, and stood for a moment looking at the building

[004-163]critically: it seemed shabby, and he thought his grandfather ought to

[004-164]replace it with a fourteen-story skyscraper, or even a higher one,

[004-165]such as he had lately seen in New York—when he stopped there for a few

[004-166]days of recreation and rest on his way home from the bereaved school.

[004-167][[About]] the entryway to the stairs were various tin signs, announcing the

[004-168]occupation and location of upper-floor tenants, and Georgie decided to

[004-169]take some of these with him if he should ever go to college. However,

[004-170]he did not stop to collect them at this time, but climbed the worn

[004-171]stairs—there was no elevator—to the fourth floor, went down a dark

[004-172]corridor, and rapped three times upon a door. It was a mysterious door,

[004-173]its upper half, of opaque glass, bearing no sign to state the business

[004-174]or profession of the occupants within; but overhead, upon the lintel,

[004-175]four letters had been smearingly inscribed, partly with purple ink and

[004-176]partly with a soft lead pencil, “F. O. T. A.” and upon the plaster

[004-177]wall, above the lintel, there was a drawing dear to male adolescence: a

[004-178]skull and crossbones.

[004-179]Three raps, similar to Georgie’s, sounded from within the room. Georgie

[004-180]then rapped four times the rapper within the room rapped twice, and

[004-181]Georgie rapped seven times. This ended precautionary measures; and

[004-182]a well-dressed boy of sixteen opened the door; whereupon Georgie

[004-183]entered quickly, and the door was closed behind him. Seven boys of

[004-184]congenial age were seated in a semicircular row of damaged office

[004-185]chairs, facing a platform whereon stood a solemn, red-haired young

[004-186]personage with a table before him. At one end of the room there was

[004-187]a battered sideboard, and upon it were some empty beer bottles, a

[004-188]tobacco can about two-thirds full, with a web of mold over the surface

[004-189]of the tobacco, a dusty cabinet photograph (not inscribed) of Miss

[004-190]Lillian Russell, several withered old pickles, a caseknife, and a

[004-191]half-petrified section of icing-cake on a sooty plate. At the other end

[004-192]of the room were two rickety card-tables and a stand of bookshelves

[004-193]where were displayed under dust four or five small volumes of M. Guy

[004-194]de Maupassant’s stories, “Robinson Crusoe,” “Sappho,” “Mr. Barnes

[004-195]of New York,” a work by Giovanni Boccaccio, a Bible, “The Arabian

[004-196]Nights’ Entertainment,” “Studies of the Human Form Divine,” “The Little

[004-197]Minister,” and a clutter of monthly magazines and illustrated weeklies

[004-198]of about that crispness one finds in such articles upon a doctor’s

[004-199]ante-room table. Upon the wall, above the sideboard, was an old framed

[004-200]lithograph of Miss Della Fox in “Wang”; over the bookshelves there was

[004-201]another lithograph purporting to represent Mr. John L. Sullivan in a

[004-202]boxing costume, and beside it a halftone reproduction of “A Reading

[004-203]From Horner.” The final decoration consisted of damaged papiermache—a

[004-204]round shield with two battle-axes and two cross-hilted swords, upon

[004-205]the wall over the little platform where stood the red-haired presiding

[004-206]officer. He addressed Georgie in a serious voice:
