[011-001]He said, “Indeed, I’m sorry,” in a nice way, and looked very strikingly

[011-002]handsome when he said it, she thought. No doubt it is true that

[011-003]there is more rejoicing in heaven over one sinner repented than over

[011-004]all the saints who consistently remain holy, and the rare, sudden

[011-005]gentlenesses of arrogant people have infinitely more effect than the

[011-006]continual gentleness of gentle people. Arrogance turned gentle melts

[011-007]the heart; and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] gave her companion a little sidelong, sunny nod of

[011-008]acknowledgment. George was dazzled by the quick glow of her eyes, and

[011-009]found himself at a loss for something to say.

[011-010]Having turned about, he kept his horse to a walk, and at this gait the

[011-011]sleighbells tinkled but intermittently. Gleaming wanly through the

[011-012]whitish vapour that kept rising from the trotter’s body and flanks,

[011-013]they were like tiny fog-bells, and made the only sounds in a great

[011-014]winter silence. The white road ran between lonesome rail fences; and

[011-015]frozen barnyards beyond the fences showed sometimes a harrow left to

[011-016]rust, with its iron seat half filled with stiffened snow, and sometimes

[011-017]an old dead buggy, its wheels forever set, it seemed, in the solid ice

[011-018]of deep ruts. Chickens scratched the metallic earth with an air of

[011-019]protest, and a masterless ragged colt looked up in sudden horror at the

[011-020]mild tinkle of the passing bells, then blew fierce clouds of steam at

[011-021]the sleigh. The snow no longer fell, and far ahead, in a grayish cloud

[011-022]that lay upon the land, was the town.

[011-023]Lucy looked at this distant thickening reflection. “When we get this

[011-024]far out we can see there must be quite a little smoke hanging over the

[011-025]town,” she said. “I suppose that’s because it’s growing. As it grows

[011-026]bigger it seems to get ashamed of itself, so it makes this cloud and

[011-027]hides in it. Papa says it used to be a bit nicer when he lived here:

[011-028]he always speaks of it differently—he always has a gentle look, a

[011-029]particular tone of voice, I’ve noticed. He must have been very fond

[011-030]of it. It must have been a lovely place: everybody must have been so

[011-031]jolly. From the way he talks, you’d think life here then was just one

[011-032]long midsummer serenade. He declares it was always sunshine, that

[011-033]the air wasn’t like the air anywhere else—that, as he remembers it,

[011-034]there always seemed to be gold-dust in the air. I doubt it! I think it

[011-035]doesn’t seem to be duller air to him now just on account of having a

[011-036]little soot in it sometimes, but probably because he was twenty years

[011-037]younger then. It seems to me the gold-dust he thinks was here is just

[011-038]his being young that he remembers. I think it was just youth. It is

[011-039]pretty pleasant to be young, isn’t it?” She laughed absently, then

[011-040]appeared to become wistful. “I wonder if we really do enjoy it as much

[011-041]as we’ll look back and think we did! I don’t suppose so. Anyhow, for

[011-042]my part I feel as if I must be missing something about it, somehow,

[011-043]because I don’t ever seem to be thinking about what’s happening at the

[011-044]present moment; I’m always looking forward to something—thinking about

[011-045]things that will happen when I’m older.”

[011-046]“You’re a funny girl,” George said gently. “But your voice sounds

[011-047]pretty nice when you think and talk along together like that!”

[011-048]The horse shook himself all over, and the impatient sleighbells made

[011-049]his wish audible. Accordingly, George tightened the reins, and the

[011-050]cutter was off again at a three-minute trot, no despicable rate of

[011-051]speed. It was not long before they were again passing Lucy’s Beautiful

[011-052]House, and here George thought fit to put an appendix to his remark.

[011-053]“You’re a funny girl, and you know a lot—but I don’t believe you know

[011-054]much about architecture!”

[011-055]Coming toward them, black against the snowy road, was a strange

[011-056]silhouette. It approached moderately and without visible means of

[011-057]progression, so the matter seemed from a distance; but as the cutter

[011-058]shortened the distance, the silhouette was revealed to be Mr. Morgan’s

[011-059][[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons|horseless carriage]], conveying four people atop: Mr. Morgan with

[011-060]George’s mother beside him, and, in the rear seat, [[fanny-minafer|Miss Fanny Minafer]]

[011-061]and the [[george-amberson|Honourable George Amberson]]. All four seemed to be in the

[011-062]liveliest humour, like high-spirited people upon a new adventure; and

[011-063]Isabel waved her handkerchief dashingly as the cutter flashed by them.

[011-064]“For the Lord’s sake!” George gasped.

[011-065]“Your mother’s a dear,” said Lucy. “And she does wear the most

[011-066]bewitching _things!_ She looked like a Russian princess, though I doubt

[011-067]if they’re that handsome.”

[011-068]George said nothing; he drove on till they had crossed Amberson

[011-069]Addition and reached the stone pillars at the head of [[national-avenue|National Avenue]].

[011-070]There he turned.

[011-071]“Let’s go back and take another look at that old sewing-machine,” he

[011-072]said. “It certainly is the weirdest, craziest—”

[011-073]He left the sentence unfinished, and presently they were again in sight

[011-074]of the old sewing-machine. George shouted mockingly.

[011-075]Alas! three figures stood in the road, and a pair of legs, with the

[011-076]toes turned up, indicated that a fourth figure lay upon its back in the

[011-077]snow, beneath a horseless carriage that had decided to need a horse.

[011-078]George became vociferous with laughter, and coming up at his trotter’s

[011-079]best gait, snow spraying from runners and every hoof, swerved to the

[011-080]side of the road and shot by, shouting, “Git a hoss! Git a hoss! Git a

[011-081]hoss!”

[011-082]Three hundred yards away he turned and came back, racing; leaning

[011-083]out as he passed, to wave jeeringly at the group about the disabled

[011-084]machine: “Git a hoss! Git a hoss! Git a—”

[011-085]The trotter had broken into a gallop, and Lucy cried a warning: “Be

[011-086]careful!” she said. “Look where you’re driving! There’s a ditch on that

[011-087]side. _Look_—”

[011-088]George turned too late; the cutter’s right runner went into the ditch

[011-089]and snapped off; the little sleigh upset, and, after dragging its

[011-090]occupants some fifteen yards, left them lying together in a bank

[011-091]of snow. Then the vigorous young horse kicked himself free of all

[011-092]annoyances, and disappeared down the road, galloping cheerfully.

Chapter VIII

[011-093]When George regained some measure of his presence of mind, Miss Lucy

[011-094]Morgan’s cheek, snowy and cold, was pressing his nose slightly to one

[011-095]side; his right arm was firmly about her neck; and a monstrous amount

[011-096]of her fur boa seemed to mingle with an equally unplausible quantity of

[011-097]snow in his mouth. He was confused, but conscious of no objection to

[011-098]any of these juxtapositions. She was apparently uninjured, for she sat

[011-099]up, hatless, her hair down, and said mildly:

[011-100]“Good heavens!”

[011-101]Though her father had been under his machine when they passed, he was

[011-102]the first to reach them. He threw himself on his knees beside his

[011-103]daughter, but found her already laughing, and was reassured. “They’re

[011-104]all right,” he called to Isabel, who was running toward them, ahead of

[011-105]her brother and Fanny Minafer. “This snowbank’s a feather bed—nothing

[011-106]the matter with them at all. Don’t look so pale!”

[011-107]“Georgie!” she gasped. “_Georgie!_”

[011-108]Georgie was on his feet, snow all over him.

[011-109]“Don’t make a fuss, mother! Nothing’s the matter. That darned silly

[011-110]horse—”

[011-111]Sudden tears stood in Isabel’s eyes. “To see you down

[011-112]underneath—dragging—oh—” Then with shaking hands she began to brush the

[011-113]snow from him.

[011-114]“Let me alone,” he protested. “You’ll ruin your gloves. You’re getting

[011-115]snow all over you, and—”

[011-116]“No, no!” she cried. “You’ll catch cold; you mustn’t catch cold!” And

[011-117]she continued to brush him.

[011-118]Amberson had brought Lucy’s hat; Miss Fanny acted as lady’s-maid; and

[011-119]both victims of the accident were presently restored to about their

[011-120]usual appearance and condition of apparel. In fact, encouraged by the

[011-121]two older gentlemen, the entire party, with one exception, decided that

[011-122]the episode was after all a merry one, and began to laugh about it. But

[011-123]George was glummer than the December twilight now swiftly closing in.

[011-124]“That darned horse!” he said.

[011-125]“I wouldn’t bother about Pendennis, Georgie,” said his uncle. “You can

[011-126]send a man out for what’s left of the cutter tomorrow, and Pendennis

[011-127]will gallop straight home to his stable: he’ll be there a long while

[011-128]before we will, because all we’ve got to depend on to get us home is

[011-129]Gene Morgan’s broken-down chafing-dish yonder.”

[011-130]They were approaching the machine as he spoke, and his friend, again

[011-131]underneath it, heard him. He emerged, smiling. “She’ll go,” he said.

[011-132]“What!”

[011-133]“All aboard!”

[011-134]He offered his hand to Isabel. She was smiling but still pale, and her

[011-135]eyes, in spite of the smile, kept upon George in a shocked anxiety.

[011-136]Miss Fanny had already mounted to the rear seat, and George, after

[011-137]helping Lucy Morgan to climb up beside his aunt, was following. Isabel

[011-138]saw that his shoes were light things of patent leather, and that snow

[011-139]was clinging to them. She made a little rush toward him, and, as one

[011-140]of his feet rested on the iron step of the machine, in mounting, she

[011-141]began to clean the snow from his shoe with her almost aerial lace

[011-142]handkerchief. “You mustn’t catch cold!” she cried.

[011-143]“Stop that!” George shouted, and furiously withdrew his foot.

[011-144]“Then stamp the snow off,” she begged. “You mustn’t ride with wet feet.”

[011-145]“They’re not!” George roared, thoroughly outraged. “For heaven’s sake

[011-146]get in! You’re standing in the snow yourself. Get in!”

[011-147]Isabel consented, turning to Morgan, whose habitual expression of

[011-148]apprehensiveness was somewhat accentuated. He climbed up after her,

[011-149]George Amberson having gone to the other side. “You’re the same Isabel

[011-150]I used to know!” he said in a low voice. “You’re a divinely ridiculous

[011-151]woman.”

[011-152]“Am I, [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]?” she said, not displeased. “‘Divinely’ and ‘ridiculous’

[011-153]just counterbalance each other, don’t they? Plus one and minus one

[011-154]equal nothing; so you mean I’m nothing in particular?”

[011-155]“No,” he answered, tugging at a lever. “That doesn’t seem to be

[011-156]precisely what I meant. There!” This exclamation referred to the

[011-157]subterranean machinery, for dismaying sounds came from beneath the

[011-158]floor, and the vehicle plunged, then rolled noisily forward.

[011-159]“Behold!” George Amberson exclaimed. “She does move! It must be another

[011-160]accident.”

[011-161]“Accident?” Morgan shouted over the din. “No! She breathes, she stirs;

[011-162]she seems to feel a thrill of life along her keel!” And he began to

[011-163]sing “The Star Spangled Banner.”

[011-164]Amberson joined him lustily, and sang on when Morgan stopped. The

[011-165]twilight sky cleared, discovering a round moon already risen; and the

[011-166]musical congressman hailed this bright presence with the complete text

[011-167]and melody of “The Danube River.”

[011-168]His nephew, behind, was gloomy. He had overheard his mother’s

[011-169]conversation with the inventor: it seemed curious to him that this

[011-170]Morgan, of whom he had never heard until last night, should be using

[011-171]the name “Isabel” so easily; and George felt that it was not just the

[011-172]thing for his mother to call Morgan “Eugene;” the resentment of the

[011-173]previous night came upon George again. Meanwhile, his mother and Morgan

[011-174]continued their talk; but he could no longer hear what they said; the

[011-175]noise of the car and his uncle’s songful mood prevented. He marked how

[011-176]animated Isabel seemed; it was not strange to see his mother so gay,

[011-177]but it was strange that a man not of the family should be the cause of

[011-178]her gaiety. And George sat frowning.

[011-179]Fanny Minafer had begun to talk to Lucy. “Your father wanted to prove

[011-180]that his horseless carriage would run, even in the snow,” she said. “It

[011-181]really does, too.”

[011-182]“Of course!”

[011-183]“It’s so interesting! He’s been telling us how he’s going to change it.

[011-184]He says he’s going to have wheels all made of rubber and blown up with

[011-185]air. I don’t understand what he means at all; I should think they’d

[011-186]explode—but Eugene seems to be very confident. He always was confident,

[011-187]though. It seems so like old times to hear him talk!”
