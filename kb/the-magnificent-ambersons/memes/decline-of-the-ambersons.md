## Overview

[[The Decline of the Ambersons]] is the central narrative arc of Booth Tarkington's *The Magnificent Ambersons* (1918). The novel traces the fall of a once-dominant Midwestern aristocratic family as the world around them transforms through industrialization, [[The Automobile in The Magnificent Ambersons|the rise of the automobile]], and shifting social values.

## In the Novel

The Ambersons are introduced at the height of their magnificence in the 1870s. [[Major Amberson]] "had 'made a fortune' in 1873, when other people were losing fortunes" (001-001). Their grandeur defines the [[Downtown Midland|Midland town]]'s social landscape, centered on the [[Amberson Mansion]] at the head of [[National Avenue]] and the [[Amberson Addition]].

However, this magnificence proves fragile. As industrialization accelerates and automobiles replace carriages, the family's resistance to change — embodied most starkly in [[George Amberson Minafer]]'s [[Pride and Arrogance|pride]] and refusal to adapt — leads to their financial and social ruin. The family's trajectory is marked by key events: [[The Last Ball]] (the peak of their social power), [[Isabel and Eugene's Engagement]] (a moment of romantic possibility thwarted by George's pride), and finally [[The Automobile Accident]] (the physical destruction of the last Amberson heir).

The decline operates on multiple levels: financial (the dissipation of the Major's fortune), social (the loss of the family's status in a growing city), and physical (the loss of the [[Amberson Mansion]] itself). [[Eugene Morgan]]'s success with [[Morgan's Automobile Factory]] mirrors the Ambersons' decline — his rise is their fall.

## Key Passages

The novel's opening establishes the Amberson dynasty:

> Major Amberson had "made a fortune" in 1873, when other people were losing fortunes, and the magnificence of the Ambersons began then. (001-001)

The theme of transience is woven through the narrative:

> They have passed, those darky hired-men of the Midland town; and the introspective horses they curried and brushed and whacked and amiably cursed — those good old horses switch their tails at flies no more. (001-160)

The automobile — symbol of the new era — appears as both technological marvel and agent of destruction:

> The manufacture of automobiles, which he says is a term he prefers to 'horseless carriages' (014-023)

## Historical Context

Published in 1918 but set primarily in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the novel captures a pivotal moment in American history: the transition from a landed gentry model of [[Wealth and Fortune|wealth]] to industrial capitalism. The Ambersons represent the old order — wealth derived from property and social position — while [[Eugene Morgan]]'s automobile manufacturing represents the new. Tarkington, who himself grew up in an affluent Indianapolis family, drew on first-hand observation of this transition. The novel's treatment of this theme parallels broader cultural anxieties about [[Fashion and Social Status|social change]] in the Progressive Era.
