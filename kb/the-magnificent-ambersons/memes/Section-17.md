[017-001]“What’s wrong, George?” she asked softly.

[017-002]“What do you mean: ‘What’s wrong?’”

[017-003]“You’re awfully upset about something. Didn’t you get though your

[017-004]examination all right?”

[017-005]“Certainly I did. What makes you think anything’s ‘wrong’ with me?”

[017-006]“You do look pale, as papa said, and it seemed to me that the way you

[017-007]talked sounded—well, a little confused.”

[017-008]“‘Confused’! I said I didn’t care to smoke. What in the world is

[017-009]confused about that?”

[017-010]“Nothing. But—”

[017-011]“See here!” George stepped close to her. “Are you glad to see me?”

[017-012]“You needn’t be so fierce about it!” [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] protested, laughing at his

[017-013]dramatic intensity. “Of course I am! How long have I been looking

[017-014]forward to it?”

[017-015]“I don’t know,” he said sharply, abating nothing of his fierceness.

[017-016]“How long have you?”

[017-017]“Why—ever since you went away!”

[017-018]“Is that true? Lucy, is that true?”

[017-019]“You are funny!” she said. “Of course it’s true. Do tell me what’s the

[017-020]matter with you, George!”

[017-021]“I will!” he exclaimed. “I was a boy when I saw you last. I see that

[017-022]now, though I didn’t then. Well, I’m not a boy any longer. I’m a man,

[017-023]and a man has a right to demand a totally different treatment.”

[017-024]“Why has he?”

[017-025]“What?”

[017-026]“I don’t seem to be able to understand you at all, George. Why

[017-027]shouldn’t a boy be treated just as well as a man?”

[017-028]George seemed to find himself at a loss. “Why shouldn’t—Well, he

[017-029]shouldn’t, because a man has a right to certain explanations.”

[017-030]“What explanations?”

[017-031]“Whether he’s been made a toy of!” George almost shouted. “That’s what

[017-032]I want to know!”

[017-033]Lucy shook her head despairingly. “You are the queerest person! You say

[017-034]you’re a man now, but you talk more like a boy than ever. What does

[017-035]make you so excited?”

[017-036]“‘Excited!’” he stormed. “Do you dare to stand there and call me

[017-037]‘excited’? I tell you, I never have been more calm or calmer in my

[017-038]life! I don’t know that a person needs to be called ‘excited’ because

[017-039]he demands explanations that are his simple due!”

[017-040]“What in the world do you want me to explain?”

[017-041]“Your conduct with Fred Kinney!” George shouted.

[017-042]Lucy uttered a sudden cry of laughter; she was delighted. “It’s

[017-043]been awful!” she said. “I don’t know that I ever heard of worse

[017-044]misbehaviour! Papa and I have been twice to dinner with his family, and

[017-045]I’ve been three times to church with Fred—and once to the circus! I

[017-046]don’t know when they’ll be here to arrest me!”

[017-047]“Stop that!” George commanded fiercely. “I want to know just one thing,

[017-048]and I mean to know it, too!”

[017-049]“Whether I enjoyed the circus?”

[017-050]“I want to know if you’re engaged to him!”

[017-051]“No!” she cried and lifting her face close to his for the shortest

[017-052]instant possible, she gave him a look half merry, half defiant, but all

[017-053]fond. It was an adorable look.

[017-054]“Lucy!” he said huskily.

[017-055]But she turned quickly from him, and ran to the other end of the room.

[017-056]He followed awkwardly, stammering:

[017-057]“Lucy, I want—I want to ask you. Will you—will you—will you be engaged

[017-058]to me?”

[017-059]She stood at a window, seeming to look out into the summer darkness,

[017-060]her back to him.

[017-061]“Will you, Lucy?”

[017-062]“No,” she murmured, just audibly.

[017-063]“Why not?”

[017-064]“I’m older than you.”

[017-065]“Eight months!”

[017-066]“You’re too young.”

[017-067]“Is that—” he said, gulping—“is that the only reason you won’t?”

[017-068]She did not answer.

[017-069]As she stood, persistently staring out of the window, with her back to

[017-070]him, she did not see how humble his attitude had become; but his voice

[017-071]was low, and it shook so that she could have no doubt of his emotion.

[017-072]“Lucy, please forgive me for making such a row,” he said, thus gently.

[017-073]“I’ve been—I’ve been terribly upset—terribly! You know how I feel about

[017-074]you, and always have felt about you. I’ve shown it in every single

[017-075]thing I’ve done since the first time I met you, and I know you know it.

[017-076]Don’t you?”

[017-077]Still she did not move or speak.

[017-078]“Is the only reason you won’t be engaged to me you think I’m too young,

[017-079]Lucy?”

[017-080]“It’s—it’s reason enough,” she said faintly.

[017-081]At that he caught one of her hands, and she turned to him: there were

[017-082]tears in her eyes, tears which he did not understand at all.

[017-083]“Lucy, you little dear!” he cried. “I knew you—”

[017-084]“No, no!” she said, and she pushed him away, withdrawing her hand.

[017-085]“George, let’s not talk of solemn things.”

[017-086]“‘Solemn things!’ Like what?”

[017-087]“Like—being engaged.”

[017-088]But George had become altogether jubilant, and he laughed triumphantly.

[017-089]“Good gracious, that isn’t solemn!”

[017-090]“It is, too!” she said, wiping her eyes. “It’s too solemn for us.”

[017-091]“No, it isn’t! I—”

[017-092]“Let’s sit down and be sensible, dear,” she said. “You sit over there—”

[017-093]“I will if you’ll call me, ‘dear’ again.”

[017-094]“No,” she said. “I’ll only call you that once again this summer—the

[017-095]night before you go away.”

[017-096]“That will have to do, then,” he laughed, “so long as I know we’re

[017-097]engaged.”

[017-098]“But we’re not!” she protested. “And we never will be, if you don’t

[017-099]promise not to speak of it again until—until I tell you to!”

[017-100]“I won’t promise that,” said the happy George. “I’ll only promise

[017-101]not to speak of it till the next time you call me ‘dear’; and you’ve

[017-102]promised to call me that the night before I leave for my senior year.”

[017-103]“Oh, but I didn’t!” she said earnestly, then hesitated. “Did I?”

[017-104]“Didn’t you?”

[017-105]“I don’t think I meant it,” she murmured, her wet lashes flickering

[017-106]above troubled eyes.

[017-107]“I know one thing about you,” he said gayly, his triumph increasing.

[017-108]“You never went back on anything you said, yet, and I’m not afraid of

[017-109]this being the first time!”

[017-110]“But we mustn’t let—” she faltered; then went on tremulously, “George,

[017-111]we’ve got on so well together, we won’t let this make a difference

[017-112]between us, will we?” And she joined in his laughter.

[017-113]“It will all depend on what you tell me the night before I go away. You

[017-114]agree we’re going to settle things then, don’t you, Lucy?”

[017-115]“I don’t promise.”

[017-116]“Yes, you do! Don’t you?”

[017-117]“Well—”

Chapter XIII

[017-118]Tonight George began a jubilant warfare upon his [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]], opening

[017-119]the campaign upon his return home at about eleven o’clock. Fanny had

[017-120]retired, and was presumably asleep, but George, on the way to his own

[017-121]room, paused before her door, and serenaded her in a full baritone:

[017-122]“As I walk along the Boy de Balong

[017-123]With my independent air,

[017-124]The people all declare,

[017-125]‘He must be a millionaire!’

[017-126]Oh, you hear them sigh, and wish to die,

[017-127]And see them wink the other eye

[017-128]At the man that broke the bank at Monte Carlo!”

[017-129]Isabel came from George’s room, where she had been reading, waiting for

[017-130]him. “I’m afraid you’ll disturb your father, dear. I wish you’d sing

[017-131]more, though—in the daytime! You have a splendid voice.”

[017-132]“Good-night, old lady!”

[017-133]“I thought perhaps I—Didn’t you want me to come in with you and talk a

[017-134]little?”

[017-135]“Not to-night. You go to bed. Good-night, old lady!”

[017-136]He kissed her hilariously, entered his room with a skip, closed his

[017-137]door noisily; and then he could be heard tossing things about, loudly

[017-138]humming “The Man that Broke the Bank at Monte Carlo.”

[017-139]Smiling, his mother knelt outside his door to pray; then, with her

[017-140]“Amen,” pressed her lips to the bronze door-knob; and went silently to

[017-141]her own apartment.

[017-142]After breakfasting in bed, George spent the next morning at his

[017-143]grandfather’s and did not encounter his Aunt Fanny until lunch, when

[017-144]she seemed to be ready for him.

[017-145]“Thank you so much for the serenade, George!” she said. “Your poor

[017-146]father tells me he’d just got to sleep for the first time in two

[017-147]nights, but after your kind attentions he lay awake the rest of last

[017-148]night.”

[017-149]“Perfectly true,” [[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]] said grimly.

[017-150]“Of course, I didn’t know, sir,” George hastened to assure him. “I’m

[017-151]awfully sorry. But Aunt Fanny was so gloomy and excited before I went

[017-152]out, last evening, I thought she needed cheering up.”

[017-153]“I!” Fanny jeered. “I was gloomy? I was excited? You mean about that

[017-154]engagement?”

[017-155]“Yes. Weren’t you? I thought I heard you worrying over somebody’s

[017-156]being engaged. Didn’t I hear you say you’d heard [[eugene-morgan|Mr. Eugene Morgan]] was

[017-157]engaged to marry some pretty little seventeen-year-old girl?”

[017-158]Fanny was stung, but she made a brave effort. “Did you ask Lucy?” she

[017-159]said, her voice almost refusing the teasing laugh she tried to make it

[017-160]utter. “Did you ask her when Fred Kinney and she—”

[017-161]“Yes. That story wasn’t true. But the other one—” Here he stared at

[017-162]Fanny, and then affected dismay. “Why, what’s the matter with your

[017-163]face, Aunt Fanny? It seems agitated!”

[017-164]“Agitated!” Fanny said disdainfully, but her voice undeniably lacked

[017-165]steadiness. “Agitated!”

[017-166]“Oh, come!” Mr. Minafer interposed. “Let’s have a little peace!”

[017-167]“I’m willing,” said George. “I don’t want to see poor Aunt Fanny all

[017-168]stirred up over a rumour I just this minute invented myself. She’s so

[017-169]excitable—about certain subjects—it’s hard to control her.” He turned

[017-170]to his mother. “What’s the matter with grandfather?”

[017-171]“Didn’t you see him this morning?” Isabel asked.

[017-172]“Yes. He was glad to see me, and all that, but he seemed pretty

[017-173]fidgety. Has he been having trouble with his heart again?”

[017-174]“Not lately. No.”

[017-175]“Well, he’s not himself. I tried to talk to him about the estate;

[017-176]it’s disgraceful—it really is—the way things are looking. He wouldn’t

[017-177]listen, and he seemed upset. What’s he upset over?”

[017-178]Isabel looked serious; however, it was her husband who suggested

[017-179]gloomily, “I suppose the Major’s bothered about this [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] and Amelia

[017-180]business, most likely.”

[017-181]“What Sydney and Amelia business?” George asked.

[017-182]“Your mother can tell you, if she wants to,” Minafer said. “It’s not my

[017-183]side of the family, so I keep off.”

[017-184]“It’s rather disagreeable for all of us, Georgie,” Isabel began. “You

[017-185]see, your Uncle Sydney wanted a diplomatic position, and he thought

[017-186]brother George, being in Congress, could arrange it. George did get him

[017-187]the offer of a South American ministry, but Sydney wanted a European

[017-188]ambassadorship, and he got quite indignant with poor George for

[017-189]thinking he’d take anything smaller—and he believes George didn’t work

[017-190]hard enough for him. George had done his best, of course, and now he’s

[017-191]out of Congress, and won’t run again—so there’s Sydney’s idea of a big

[017-192]diplomatic position gone for good. Well, Sydney and your [[amelia-amberson|Aunt Amelia]]

[017-193]are terribly disappointed, and they say they’ve been thinking for years

[017-194]that this town isn’t really fit to live in—‘for a gentleman,’ Sydney

[017-195]says—and it is getting rather big and dirty. So they’ve sold their

[017-196]house and decided to go abroad to live permanently; there’s a villa

[017-197]near Florence they’ve often talked of buying. And they want father to

[017-198]let them have their share of the estate now, instead of waiting for him

[017-199]to leave it to them in his will.”

[017-200]“Well, I suppose that’s fair enough,” George said. “That is, in case he

[017-201]intended to leave them a certain amount in his will.”

[017-202]“Of course that’s understood, Georgie. Father explained his will to us

[017-203]long ago; a third to them, and a third to brother George, and a third

[017-204]to us.”

[017-205]Her son made a simple calculation in his mind. [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] was a

[017-206]bachelor, and probably would never marry; Sydney and Amelia were

[017-207]childless. [[major-amberson|The Major]]’s only grandchild appeared to remain the eventual

[017-208]heir of the entire property, no matter if the Major did turn over

[017-209]to Sydney a third of it now. And George had a fragmentary vision

[017-210]of himself, in mourning, arriving to take possession of a historic

[017-211]Florentine villa—he saw himself walking up a cypress-bordered path,

[017-212]with ancient carven stone balustrades in the distance, and servants

[017-213]in mourning livery greeting the new signore. “Well, I suppose it’s

[017-214]grandfather’s own affair. He can do it or not, just as he likes. I

[017-215]don’t see why he’d mind much.”
