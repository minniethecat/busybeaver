[041-001]George looked at him with an air of inquiry, but said nothing. He had

[041-002]grown to be almost as silent as his grandfather. However, the Major

[041-003]spoke without being questioned.

[041-004]“It must be in the sun,” he said. “There wasn’t anything here but the

[041-005]sun in the first place, and the earth came out of the sun, and we came

[041-006]out of the earth. So, whatever we are, we must have been in the sun. We

[041-007]go back to the earth we came out of, so the earth will go back to the

[041-008]sun that it came out of. And time means nothing—nothing at all—so in a

[041-009]little while we’ll all be back in the sun together. I wish—”

[041-010]He moved his hand uncertainly as if reaching for something, and George

[041-011]jumped up. “Did you want anything, grandfather?”

[041-012]“What?”

[041-013]“Would you like a glass of water?”

[041-014]“No—no. No; I don’t want anything.” The reaching hand dropped back upon

[041-015]the arm of his chair, and he relapsed into silence; but a few minutes

[041-016]later he finished the sentence he had begun:

[041-017]“I wish—somebody could tell me!”

[041-018]The next day he had a slight cold, but he seemed annoyed when his son

[041-019]suggested calling the doctor, and Amberson let him have his own way

[041-020]so far, in fact, that after he had got up and dressed, the following

[041-021]morning, he was all alone when he went away to find out what he hadn’t

[041-022]been able to think out—all those things he had wished “somebody” would

[041-023]tell him.

[041-024]Old Sam, shuffling in with the breakfast tray, found the Major in his

[041-025]accustomed easy-chair by the fireplace—and yet even the old darkey

[041-026]could see instantly that the Major was not there.

Chapter XXXI

[041-027]When the great Amberson Estate went into court for settlement, “there

[041-028]wasn’t any,” [[george-amberson|George Amberson]] said—that is, when the settlement was

[041-029]concluded there was no estate. “I guessed it,” Amberson went on. “As

[041-030]an expert on prosperity, my career is disreputable, but as a prophet

[041-031]of calamity I deserve a testimonial banquet.” He reproached himself

[041-032]bitterly for not having long ago discovered that his father had never

[041-033]given Isabel a deed to her house. “And those pigs, [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]] and Amelia!”

[041-034]he added, for this was another thing he was bitter about. “They won’t

[041-035]do anything. I’m sorry I gave them the opportunity of making a polished

[041-036]refusal. Amelia’s letter was about half in Italian; she couldn’t

[041-037]remember enough ways of saying no in English. One has to live quite a

[041-038]long while to realize there are people like that! The estate was badly

[041-039]crippled, even before they took out their ‘third,’ and the ‘third’ they

[041-040]took was the only good part of the rotten apple. Well, I didn’t ask

[041-041]them for restitution on my own account, and at least it will save you

[041-042]some trouble, young George. Never waste any time writing to them; you

[041-043]mustn’t count on them.”

[041-044]“I don’t,” George said quietly. “I don’t count on anything.”

[041-045]“Oh, we’ll not feel that things are quite desperate,” Amberson laughed,

[041-046]but not with great cheerfulness. “We’ll survive, Georgie—you will,

[041-047]especially. For my part I’m a little too old and too accustomed to

[041-048]fall back on somebody else for supplies to start a big fight with

[041-049]life: I’ll be content with just surviving, and I can do it on an

[041-050]eighteen-hundred-dollar-a-year consulship. An ex-congressman can always

[041-051]be pretty sure of getting some such job, and I hear from Washington the

[041-052]matter’s about settled. I’ll live pleasantly enough with a pitcher of

[041-053]ice under a palm tree, and black folks to wait on me—that part of it

[041-054]will be like home—and I’ll manage to send you fifty dollars every now

[041-055]and then, after I once get settled. So much for me! But you—of course

[041-056]you’ve had a poor training for making your own way, but you’re only a

[041-057]boy after all, and the stuff of the old stock is in you. It’ll come

[041-058]out and do something. I’ll never forgive myself about that deed: it

[041-059]would have given you something substantial to start with. Still, you

[041-060]have a little tiny bit, and you’ll have a little tiny salary, too; and

[041-061]of course your [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]]’s here, and she’s got something you can fall

[041-062]back on if you get too pinched, until I can begin to send you a dribble

[041-063]now and then.”

[041-064]George’s “little tiny bit” was six hundred dollars which had come to

[041-065]him from the sale of his mother’s furniture; and the “little tiny

[041-066]salary” was eight dollars a week which old Frank Bronson was to pay

[041-067]him for services as a clerk and student-at-law. Old Frank would have

[041-068]offered more to the Major’s grandson, but since the death of that best

[041-069]of clients and his own experience with automobile headlights, he was

[041-070]not certain of being able to pay more and at the same time settle his

[041-071]own small bills for board and lodging. George had accepted haughtily,

[041-072]and thereby removed a burden from his uncle’s mind.

[041-073]Amberson himself, however, had not even a “tiny bit”; though he got his

[041-074]consular appointment; and to take him to his post he found it necessary

[041-075]to borrow two hundred of his nephew’s six hundred dollars. “It makes me

[041-076]sick, George,” he said. “But I’d better get there and get that salary

[041-077]started. Of course [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] would do anything in the world, and the fact

[041-078]is he wanted to, but I felt that—ah—under the circumstances—”

[041-079]“Never!” George exclaimed, growing red. “I can’t imagine one of the

[041-080]family—” He paused, not finding it necessary to explain that “the

[041-081]family” shouldn’t turn a man from the door and then accept favours from

[041-082]him. “I wish you’d take more.”

[041-083]Amberson declined. “One thing I’ll say for you, young George; you

[041-084]haven’t a stingy bone in your body. That’s [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] stock in

[041-085]you—and I like it!”

[041-086]He added something to this praise of his nephew on the day he left for

[041-087]Washington. He was not to return, but to set forth from the capital on

[041-088]the long journey to his post. George went with him to the station, and

[041-089]their farewell was lengthened by the train’s being several minutes late.

[041-090]“I may not see you again, Georgie,” Amberson said; and his voice was a

[041-091]little husky as he set a kind hand on the young man’s shoulder. “It’s

[041-092]quite probable that from this time on we’ll only know each other by

[041-093]letter—until you’re notified as my next of kin that there’s an old

[041-094]valise to be forwarded to you, and perhaps some dusty curios from

[041-095]the consulate mantelpiece. Well, it’s an odd way for us to be saying

[041-096]good-bye: one wouldn’t have thought it, even a few years ago, but here

[041-097]we are, two gentlemen of elegant appearance in a state of bustitude.

[041-098]We can’t ever tell what will happen at all, can we? Once I stood where

[041-099]we’re standing now, to say good-bye to a pretty girl—only it was in

[041-100]the old station before this was built, and we called it the ‘depot.’

[041-101]She’d been visiting your mother, before Isabel was married, and I was

[041-102]wild about her, and she admitted she didn’t mind that. In fact, we

[041-103]decided we couldn’t live without each other, and we were to be married.

[041-104]But she had to go abroad first with her father, and when we came to

[041-105]say good-bye we knew we wouldn’t see each other again for almost a

[041-106]year. I thought I couldn’t live through it—and she stood here crying.

[041-107]Well, I don’t even know where she lives now, or if she is living—and

[041-108]I only happen to think of her sometimes when I’m here at the station

[041-109]waiting for a train. If she ever thinks of me she probably imagines

[041-110]I’m still dancing in the ballroom at the [[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]], and she

[041-111]probably thinks of the Mansion as still beautiful—still the finest

[041-112]house in town. Life and [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] both behave like loose quicksilver in a

[041-113]nest of cracks. And when they’re gone we can’t tell where—or what the

[041-114]devil we did with ’em! But I believe I’ll say now—while there isn’t

[041-115]much time left for either of us to get embarrassed about it—I believe

[041-116]I’ll say that I’ve always been fond of you, Georgie, but I can’t say

[041-117]that I always liked you. Sometimes I’ve felt you were distinctly

[041-118]not an acquired taste. Until lately, one had to be fond of you just

[041-119]naturally—this isn’t very ‘tactful,’ of course—for if he didn’t, well,

[041-120]he wouldn’t! We all spoiled you terribly when you were a little boy and

[041-121]let you grow up en prince—and I must say you took to it! But you’ve

[041-122]received a pretty heavy jolt, and I had enough of your disposition,

[041-123]myself, at your age, to understand a little of what cocksure youth has

[041-124]to go through inside when it finds that it can make terrible mistakes.

[041-125]Poor old fellow! You get both kinds of jolts together, spiritual and

[041-126]material—and you’ve taken them pretty quietly and—well, with my train

[041-127]coming into the shed, you’ll forgive me for saying that there have been

[041-128]times when I thought you ought to be hanged—but I’ve always been fond

[041-129]of you, and now I like you! And just for a last word: there may be

[041-130]somebody else in this town who’s always felt about you like that—fond

[041-131]of you, I mean, no matter how much it seemed you ought to be hanged.

[041-132]You might try—Hello, I must run. I’ll send back the money as fast as

[041-133]they pay me—so, good-bye and God bless you, Georgie!”

[041-134]He passed through the gates, waved his hat cheerily from the other side

[041-135]of the iron screen, and was lost from sight in the hurrying crowd. And

[041-136]as he disappeared, an unexpected poignant loneliness fell upon his

[041-137]nephew so heavily and so suddenly that he had no energy to recoil from

[041-138]the shock. It seemed to him that the last fragment of his familiar

[041-139]world had disappeared, leaving him all alone forever.

[041-140]He walked homeward slowly through what appeared to be the strange

[041-141]streets of a strange city; and, as a matter of fact, the city was

[041-142]strange to him. He had seen little of it during his years in college,

[041-143]and then had followed the long absence and his tragic return. Since

[041-144]that he had been “scarcely outdoors at all,” as Fanny complained,

[041-145]warning him that his health would suffer, and he had been downtown only

[041-146]in a closed carriage. He had not realized the great change.

[041-147]The streets were thunderous; a vast energy heaved under the universal

[041-148]coating of dinginess. George walked through the begrimed crowds of

[041-149]hurrying strangers and saw no face that he remembered. Great numbers of

[041-150]the faces were even of a kind he did not remember ever to have seen;

[041-151]they were partly like the old type that his boyhood knew, and partly

[041-152]like types he knew abroad. He saw German eyes with American wrinkles

[041-153]at their corners; he saw Irish eyes and Neapolitan eyes, Roman eyes,

[041-154]Tuscan eyes, eyes of Lombardy, of Savoy, Hungarian eyes, Balkan eyes,

[041-155]Scandinavian eyes—all with a queer American look in them. He saw Jews

[041-156]who had been German Jews, Jews who had been Russian Jews, Jews who

[041-157]had been Polish Jews but were no longer German or Russian or Polish

[041-158]Jews. All the people were soiled by the smoke-mist through which they

[041-159]hurried, under the heavy sky that hung close upon the new skyscrapers;

[041-160]and nearly all seemed harried by something impending, though here and

[041-161]there a woman with bundles would be laughing to a companion about

[041-162]some adventure of the department stores, or perhaps an escape from

[041-163]the charging traffic of the streets—and not infrequently a girl, or a

[041-164]free-and-easy young matron, found time to throw an encouraging look to

[041-165]George.

[041-166]He took no note of these, and, leaving the crowded sidewalks, turned

[041-167]north into [[national-avenue|National Avenue]], and presently reached the quieter but no

[041-168]less begrimed region of smaller shops and old-fashioned houses. Those

[041-169]latter had been the homes of his boyhood playmates; old friends of his

[041-170]grandfather had lived here;—in this alley he had fought with two boys

[041-171]at the same time, and whipped them; in that front yard he had been

[041-172]successfully teased into temporary insanity by a Sunday-school class

[041-173]of pinky little girls. On that sagging porch a laughing woman had fed

[041-174]him and other boys with doughnuts and gingerbread; yonder he saw the

[041-175]staggered relics of the iron picket fence he had made his white pony

[041-176]jump, on a dare, and in the shabby, stone-faced house behind the fence

[041-177]he had gone to children’s parties, and, when he was a little older

[041-178]he had danced there often, and fallen in love with Mary Sharon, and

[041-179]kissed her, apparently by force, under the stairs in the hall. The

[041-180]double front doors, of meaninglessly carved walnut, once so glossily

[041-181]varnished, had been painted smoke gray, but the smoke grime showed

[041-182]repulsively, even on the smoke gray; and over the doors a smoked sign

[041-183]proclaimed the place to be a “Stag Hotel.”
