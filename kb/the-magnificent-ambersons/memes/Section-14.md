[014-001]George yawned, and tossed the clipping into his waste-basket, wondering

[014-002]why his aunt thought such dull nonsense worth the sending. As for

[014-003]her insinuation, pencilled upon the border, he supposed she meant to

[014-004]joke—a supposition which neither surprised him nor altered his lifelong

[014-005]opinion of her wit.

[014-006]He read her letter with more interest:

[014-007]... The dinner your mother gave for the Morgans was a lovely

[014-008]affair. It was last Monday evening, just ten days after you left.

[014-009]It was peculiarly appropriate that your mother should give this

[014-010]dinner, because her brother George, your uncle, was Mr. Morgan’s

[014-011]most intimate friend before he left here a number of years ago, and

[014-012]it was a pleasant occasion for the formal announcement of some news

[014-013]which you heard from [[lucy-morgan|Lucy Morgan]] before you returned to college. At

[014-014]least she told me she had told you the night before you left that

[014-015]her father had decided to return here to live. It was appropriate

[014-016]that your mother, herself an old friend, should assemble a

[014-017]representative selection of Mr. Morgan’s old friends around him

[014-018]at such a time. He was in great spirits and most entertaining. As

[014-019]your time was so charmingly taken up during your visit home with a

[014-020]younger member of his family, you probably overlooked opportunities

[014-021]of hearing him talk, and do not know what an interesting man he

[014-022]can be. He will soon begin to build his factory here for the

[014-023]manufacture of _automobiles_, which he says is a term he prefers

[014-024]to “horseless carriages.” Your [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] told me he would like

[014-025]to invest in this factory, as George thinks there is a future for

[014-026]automobiles; perhaps not for general use, but as an interesting

[014-027]novelty, which people with sufficient means would like to own for

[014-028]their amusement and the sake of variety. However, he said Mr.

[014-029]Morgan laughingly declined his offer, as Mr. M. was fully able to

[014-030]finance this venture, though not starting in a very large way. Your

[014-031]uncle said other people are manufacturing automobiles in different

[014-032]parts of the country with success. Your father is not very well,

[014-033]though he is not actually ill, and the doctor tells him he ought

[014-034]not to be so much at his office, as the long years of application

[014-035]indoors with no exercise are beginning to affect him unfavourably,

[014-036]but I believe your father would die if he had to give up his work,

[014-037]which is all that has ever interested him outside of his family. I

[014-038]never could understand it. Mr. Morgan took your mother and me with

[014-039]Lucy to see Modjeska in “Twelfth Night” yesterday evening, and Lucy

[014-040]said she thought the Duke looked rather like you, only much more

[014-041]democratic in his manner. I suppose you will think I have written a

[014-042]great deal about the Morgans in this letter, but thought you would

[014-043]be interested because of your interest in a _younger member_ of his

[014-044]family. Hoping that you are finding college still as attractive as

[014-045]ever,

[014-046]Affectionately,

[014-047][[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]].

[014-048]George read one sentence in this letter several times. Then he dropped

[014-049]the missive in his wastebasket to join the clipping, and strolled

[014-050]down the corridor of his dormitory to borrow a copy of “Twelfth

[014-051]Night.” Having secured one, he returned to his study and refreshed his

[014-052]memory of the play—but received no enlightenment that enabled him to

[014-053]comprehend Lucy’s strange remark. However, he found himself impelled

[014-054]in the direction of correspondence, and presently wrote a letter—not a

[014-055]reply to his Aunt Fanny.

[014-056]Dear Lucy:

[014-057]No doubt you will be surprised at hearing from me so soon again,

[014-058]especially as this makes two in answer to the one received from

[014-059]you since getting back to the old place. I hear you have been

[014-060]making comments about me at the theatre, that some actor was more

[014-061]democratic in his manners than I am, which I do not understand. You

[014-062]know my theory of life because I explained it to you on our first

[014-063]drive together, when I told you I would not talk to everybody about

[014-064]things I feel like the way I spoke to you of my theory of life. I

[014-065]believe those who are able should have a true theory of life, and I

[014-066]developed my theory of life long, long ago.

[014-067]Well, here I sit smoking my faithful briar pipe, indulging in

[014-068]the fragrance of my tobacco as I look out on the campus from my

[014-069]many-paned window, and things are different with me from the way

[014-070]they were way back in Freshman year. I can see now how boyish in

[014-071]many ways I was then. I believe what has changed me as much as

[014-072]anything was my visit home at the time I met you. So I sit here

[014-073]with my faithful briar and dream the old dreams over as it were,

[014-074]dreaming of the waltzes we waltzed together and of that last night

[014-075]before we parted, and you told me the good news you were going to

[014-076]live there, and I would find my friend waiting for me, when I get

[014-077]home next summer.

[014-078]I will be glad my friend will be waiting for me. I am not capable

[014-079]of friendship except for the very few, and, looking back over my

[014-080]life, I remember there were times when I doubted if I could feel

[014-081]a great friendship for anybody—especially girls. I do not take a

[014-082]great interest in many people, as you know, for I find most of

[014-083]them shallow. Here in the old place I do not believe in being

[014-084]hail-fellow-well-met with every Tom, Dick, and Harry just because

[014-085]he happens to be a classmate, any more than I do at home, where I

[014-086]have always been careful who I was seen with, largely on account of

[014-087]the family, but also because my disposition ever since my boyhood

[014-088]has been to encourage real intimacy from but the few.

[014-089]What are you reading now? I have finished both “Henry Esmond”

[014-090]and “The Virginians.” I like Thackeray because he is not trashy,

[014-091]and because he writes principally of nice people. My theory of

[014-092]literature is an author who does not indulge in trashiness—writes

[014-093]about people you could introduce into your own home. I agree with

[014-094]my Uncle [[sydney-amberson|Sydney]], as I once heard him say he did not care to read a

[014-095]book or go to a play about people he would not care to meet at his

[014-096]own dinner table. I believe we should live by certain standards and

[014-097]ideals, as you know from my telling you my theory of life.

[014-098]Well, a letter is no place for deep discussions, so I will not go

[014-099]into the subject. From several letters from my mother, and one from

[014-100]Aunt Fanny, I hear you are seeing a good deal of the family since I

[014-101]left. I hope sometimes you think of the member who is absent. I got

[014-102]a silver frame for your photograph in New York, and I keep it on my

[014-103]desk. It is the only girl’s photograph I ever took the trouble to

[014-104]have framed, though, as I told you frankly, I have had any number

[014-105]of other girls’ photographs, yet all were only passing fancies, and

[014-106]oftentimes I have questioned in years past if I was capable of much

[014-107]friendship toward the feminine sex, which I usually found shallow

[014-108]until our own friendship began. When I look at your photograph, I

[014-109]say to myself, “At last, at last here is one that will not prove

[014-110]shallow.”

[014-111]My faithful briar has gone out. I will have to rise and fill it,

[014-112]then once more in the fragrance of My Lady Nicotine, I will sit and

[014-113]dream the old dreams over, and think, too, of the true friend at

[014-114]home awaiting my return in June for the summer vacation.

[014-115]Friend, this is from your friend,

[014-116]G.A.M.

[014-117]George’s anticipations were not disappointed. When he came home in

[014-118]June his friend was awaiting him; at least, she was so pleased to see

[014-119]him again that for a few minutes after their first encounter she was a

[014-120]little breathless, and a great deal glowing, and quiet withal. Their

[014-121]sentimental friendship continued, though sometimes he was irritated by

[014-122]her making it less sentimental than he did, and sometimes by what he

[014-123]called her “air of superiority.” Her air was usually, in truth, that

[014-124]of a fond but amused older sister; and George did not believe such an

[014-125]attitude was warranted by her eight months of seniority.

[014-126]Lucy and her father were living at [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Hotel, while Morgan

[014-127]got his small machine-shops built in a western outskirt of the town;

[014-128]and George grumbled about the shabbiness and the old-fashioned look of

[014-129]the hotel, though it was “still the best in the place, of course.” He

[014-130]remonstrated with his grandfather, declaring that the whole Amberson

[014-131]Estate would be getting “run-down and out-at-heel, if things weren’t

[014-132]taken in hand pretty soon.” He urged the general need of rebuilding,

[014-133]renovating, varnishing, and lawsuits. But the Major, declining to hear

[014-134]him out, interrupted querulously, saying that he had enough to bother

[014-135]him without any advice from George; and retired to his library, going

[014-136]so far as to lock the door audibly.

[014-137]“Second childhood!” George muttered, shaking his head; and he thought

[014-138]sadly that the Major had not long to live. However, this surmise

[014-139]depressed him for only a moment or so. Of course, people couldn’t be

[014-140]expected to live forever, and it would be a good thing to have someone

[014-141]in charge of the Estate who wouldn’t let it get to looking so rusty

[014-142]that riffraff dared to make fun of it. For George had lately undergone

[014-143]the annoyance of calling upon the Morgans, in the rather stuffy red

[014-144]velours and gilt parlour of their apartment at the hotel, one evening

[014-145]when Mr. Frederick Kinney also was a caller, and Mr. Kinney had not

[014-146]been tactful. In fact, though he adopted a humorous tone of voice, in

[014-147]expressing his sympathy for people who, through the city’s poverty in

[014-148]hotels, were obliged to stay at the Amberson, Mr. Kinney’s intention

[014-149]was interpreted by the other visitor as not at all humorous, but, on

[014-150]the contrary, personal and offensive.

[014-151]George rose abruptly, his face the colour of wrath. “Good-night, Miss

[014-152]Morgan. Good-night, Mr. Morgan,” he said. “I shall take pleasure in

[014-153]calling at some other time when a more courteous sort of people may be

[014-154]present.”

[014-155]“Look here!” the hot-headed Fred burst out. “Don’t you try to make me

[014-156]out a boor, [[george-amberson-minafer|George Minafer]]! I wasn’t hinting anything at you; I simply

[014-157]forgot all about your grandfather owning this old building. Don’t you

[014-158]try to put me in the light of a boor! I won’t—”

[014-159]But George walked out in the very course of this vehement protest, and

[014-160]it was necessarily left unfinished.

[014-161]Mr. Kinney remained only a few moments after George’s departure; and

[014-162]as the door closed upon him, the distressed Lucy turned to her father.

[014-163]She was plaintively surprised to find him in a condition of immoderate

[014-164]laughter.

[014-165]“I didn’t—I didn’t think I could hold out!” he gasped, and, after

[014-166]choking until tears came to his eyes, felt blindly for the chair from

[014-167]which he had risen to wish Mr. Kinney an indistinct good-night. His

[014-168]hand found the arm of the chair; he collapsed feebly, and sat uttering

[014-169]incoherent sounds.

[014-170]“Papa!”

[014-171]“It brings things back so!” he managed to explain, “This very Fred

[014-172]Kinney’s father and young George’s father, [[wilbur-minafer|Wilbur Minafer]], used to do

[014-173]just such things when they were at that age—and, for that matter, so

[014-174]did George Amberson and I, and all the rest of us!” And, in spite of

[014-175]his exhaustion, he began to imitate: “Don’t you try to put me in the

[014-176]light of a boor!” “I shall take pleasure in calling at some time when a

[014-177]more courteous sort of people—” He was unable to go on.

[014-178]There is a mirth for every age, and Lucy failed to comprehend her

[014-179]father’s, but tolerated it a little ruefully.
