[032-001]The car was quicker than its owner; it shot away as soon as he had sunk

[032-002]into his seat; and George, having watched its impetuous disappearance

[032-003]from his field of vision, ceased to haunt the window. He went to the

[032-004]library, and, seating himself beside the table whereon he had placed

[032-005]the photograph of his father, picked up a book, and pretended to be

[032-006]engaged in reading it.

[032-007]Presently Isabel’s buoyant step was heard descending the stairs, and

[032-008]her low, sweet whistling, renewing the air of “Lord Bateman.” She

[032-009]came into the library, still whistling thoughtfully, a fur coat over

[032-010]her arm, ready to put on, and two veils round her small black hat,

[032-011]her right hand engaged in buttoning the glove upon her left; and, as

[032-012]the large room contained too many pieces of heavy furniture, and the

[032-013]inside shutters excluded most of the light of day, she did not at once

[032-014]perceive George’s presence. Instead, she went to the bay window at the

[032-015]end of the room, which afforded a view of the street, and glanced out

[032-016]expectantly; then bent her attention upon her glove; after that, looked

[032-017]out toward the street again, ceased to whistle, and turned toward the

[032-018]interior of the room.

[032-019]“Why, Georgie!”

[032-020]She came, leaned over from behind him, and there was a faint, exquisite

[032-021]odour as from distant apple blossoms as she kissed his cheek. “Dear, I

[032-022]waited lunch almost an hour for you, but you didn’t come! Did you lunch

[032-023]out somewhere?”

[032-024]“Yes.” He did not look up from the book.

[032-025]“Did you have plenty to eat?”

[032-026]“Yes.”

[032-027]“Are you sure? Wouldn’t you like to have Maggie get you something now

[032-028]in the dining room? Or they could bring it to you here, if you think it

[032-029]would be cozier. Shan’t I—”

[032-030]A tinkling bell was audible, and she moved to the doorway into the

[032-031]hall. “I’m going out driving, dear. I—” She interrupted herself to

[032-032]address the housemaid, who was passing through the hall: “I think it’s

[032-033]Mr. Morgan, Mary. Tell him I’ll be there at once.”

[032-034]“Yes, ma’am.”

[032-035]Mary returned. “’Twas a pedlar, ma’am.”

[032-036]“Another one?” Isabel said, surprised. “I thought you said it was a

[032-037]pedlar when the bell rang a little while ago.”

[032-038]“Mister George said it was, ma’am; he went to the door,” Mary informed

[032-039]her, disappearing.

[032-040]“There seem to be a great many of them,” Isabel mused. “What did yours

[032-041]want to sell, George?”

[032-042]“He didn’t say.”

[032-043]“You must have cut him off short!” she laughed; and then, still

[032-044]standing in the doorway, she noticed the big silver frame upon the

[032-045]table beside him. “Gracious, Georgie!” she exclaimed. “You have

[032-046]been investing!” and as she came across the room for a closer view,

[032-047]“Is it—is it [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]?” she asked half timidly, half archly. But the

[032-048]next instant she saw whose likeness was thus set forth in elegiac

[032-049]splendour—and she was silent, except for a long, just-audible “Oh!”

[032-050]He neither looked up nor moved.

[032-051]“That was nice of you, Georgie,” she said, in a low voice presently. “I

[032-052]ought to have had it framed, myself, when I gave it to you.”

[032-053]He said nothing, and, standing beside him, she put her hand gently upon

[032-054]his shoulder, then as gently withdrew it, and went out of the room.

[032-055]But she did not go upstairs; he heard the faint rustle of her dress

[032-056]in the hall, and then the sound of her footsteps in the “reception

[032-057]room.” After a time, silence succeeded even these slight tokens of her

[032-058]presence; whereupon George rose and went warily into the hall, taking

[032-059]care to make no noise, and he obtained an oblique view of her through

[032-060]the open double doors of the “reception room.” She was sitting in the

[032-061]chair which he had occupied so long; and she was looking out of the

[032-062]window expectantly—a little troubled.

[032-063]He went back to the library, waited an interminable half hour, then

[032-064]returned noiselessly to the same position in the hall, where he could

[032-065]see her. She was still sitting patiently by the window.

[032-066]Waiting for that man, was she? Well, it might be quite a long wait! And

[032-067]the grim George silently ascended the stairs to his own room, and began

[032-068]to pace his suffering floor.

Chapter XXV

[032-069]He left his door open, however, and when he heard the front door-bell

[032-070]ring, by and by, he went half way down the stairs and stood to listen.

[032-071]He was not much afraid that Morgan would return, but he wished to make

[032-072]sure.

[032-073]Mary appeared in the hall below him, but, after a glance toward the

[032-074]front of the house, turned back, and withdrew. Evidently Isabel had

[032-075]gone to the door. Then a murmur was heard, and [[george-amberson|George Amberson]]’s voice,

[032-076]quick and serious: “I want to talk to you, Isabel”... and another

[032-077]murmur; then Isabel and her brother passed the foot of the broad, dark

[032-078]stairway, but did not look up, and remained unconscious of the watchful

[032-079]presence above them. Isabel still carried her cloak upon her arm,

[032-080]but Amberson had taken her hand, and retained it; and as he led her

[032-081]silently into the library there was something about her attitude, and

[032-082]the pose of her slightly bent head, that was both startled and meek.

[032-083]Thus they quickly disappeared from George’s sight, hand in hand; and

[032-084]Amberson at once closed the massive double doors of the library.

[032-085]For a time all that George could hear was the indistinct sound of

[032-086]his uncle’s voice: what he was saying could not be surmised, though

[032-087]the troubled brotherliness of his tone was evident. He seemed to be

[032-088]explaining something at considerable length, and there were moments

[032-089]when he paused, and George guessed that his mother was speaking, but

[032-090]her voice must have been very low, for it was entirely inaudible to him.

[032-091]Suddenly he did hear her. Through the heavy doors her outcry came,

[032-092]clear and loud:

[032-093]“Oh, no!”

[032-094]It was a cry of protest, as if something her brother told her must be

[032-095]untrue, or, if it were true, the fact he stated must be undone; and it

[032-096]was a sound of sheer pain.

[032-097]Another sound of pain, close to George, followed it; this was a

[032-098]vehement sniffling which broke out just above him, and, looking up,

[032-099]he saw [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]] on the landing, leaning over the banisters and

[032-100]applying her handkerchief to her eyes and nose.

[032-101]“I can guess what that was about,” she whispered huskily. “He’s just

[032-102]told her what you did to [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]!”

[032-103]George gave her a dark look over his shoulder. “You go on back to your

[032-104]room!” he said; and he began to descend the stairs; but Fanny, guessing

[032-105]his purpose, rushed down and caught his arm, detaining him.

[032-106]“You’re not going in there?”, she whispered huskily. “You don’t—”

[032-107]“Let go of me!”

[032-108]But she clung to him savagely. “No, you don’t, [[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]]! You’ll

[032-109]keep away from there! You will!”

[032-110]“You let go of—”

[032-111]“I won’t! You come back here! You’ll come upstairs and let them alone;

[032-112]that’s what you’ll do!” And with such passionate determination did she

[032-113]clutch and tug, never losing a grip of him somewhere, though George

[032-114]tried as much as he could, without hurting her, to wrench away—with

[032-115]such utter forgetfulness of her maiden dignity did she assault him,

[032-116]that she forced him, stumbling upward, to the landing.

[032-117]“Of all the ridiculous—” he began furiously; but she spared one hand

[032-118]from its grasp of his sleeve and clapped it over his mouth.

[032-119]“Hush up!” Never for an instant in this grotesque struggle did Fanny

[032-120]raise her voice above a husky whisper. “Hush up! It’s indecent—like

[032-121]squabbling outside the door of an operating-room! Go on to the top of

[032-122]the stairs—go on!”

[032-123]And when George had most unwillingly obeyed, she planted herself in

[032-124]his way, on the top step. “There!” she said. “The idea of your going

[032-125]in there now! I never heard of such a thing!” And with the sudden

[032-126]departure of the nervous vigour she had shown so amazingly, she began

[032-127]to cry again. “I was an awful fool! I thought you knew what was going

[032-128]on or I never, never would have done it. Do you suppose I dreamed you’d

[032-129]go making everything into such a tragedy? Do you?”

[032-130]“I don’t care what you dreamed,” George muttered.

[032-131]But Fanny went on, always taking care to keep her voice from getting

[032-132]too loud, in spite of her most grievous agitation. “Do you dream I

[032-133]thought you’d go making such a fool of yourself at Mrs. Johnson’s?

[032-134]Oh, I saw her this morning! She wouldn’t talk to me, but I met George

[032-135]Amberson on my way back, and he told me what you’d done over there! And

[032-136]do you dream I thought you’d do what you’ve done here this afternoon to

[032-137]Eugene? Oh, I knew that, too! I was looking out of the front bedroom

[032-138]window, and I saw him drive up, and then go away again, and I knew

[032-139]you’d been to the door. Of course he went to George Amberson about it,

[032-140]and that’s why George is here. He’s got to tell Isabel the whole thing

[032-141]now, and you wanted to go in there interfering—God knows what! You stay

[032-142]here and let her brother tell her; he’s got some consideration for her!”

[032-143]“I suppose you think I haven’t!” George said, challenging her, and at

[032-144]that Fanny laughed witheringly.

[032-145]“You! Considerate of anybody!”

[032-146]“I’m considerate of her good name!” he said hotly. “It seems to me

[032-147]that’s about the first thing to be considerate of, in being considerate

[032-148]of a person! And look here: it strikes me you’re taking a pretty

[032-149]different tack from what you did yesterday afternoon!”

[032-150]Fanny wrung her hands. “I did a terrible thing!” she lamented. “Now

[032-151]that it’s done and too late I know what it was! I didn’t have sense

[032-152]enough just to let things go on. I didn’t have any business to

[032-153]interfere, and I didn’t mean to interfere—I only wanted to talk, and

[032-154]let out a little! I did think you already knew everything I told you.

[032-155]I did! And I’d rather have cut my hand off than stir you up to doing

[032-156]what you have done! I was just suffering so that I wanted to let out a

[032-157]little—I didn’t mean any real harm. But now I see what’s happened—oh, I

[032-158]was a fool! I hadn’t any business interfering. Eugene never would have

[032-159]looked at me, anyhow, and, oh, why couldn’t I have seen that before!

[032-160]He never came here a single time in his life except on her account,

[032-161]never! and I might have let them alone, because he wouldn’t have looked

[032-162]at me even if he’d never seen Isabel. And they haven’t done any harm:

[032-163]she made Wilbur happy, and she was a true wife to him as long as he

[032-164]lived. It wasn’t a crime for her to care for Eugene all the time; she

[032-165]certainly never told him she did—and she gave me every chance in the

[032-166]world! She left us alone together every time she could—even since

[032-167]Wilbur died—but what was the use? And here I go, not doing myself a

[032-168]bit of good by it, and just”—Fanny wrung her hands again—“just ruining

[032-169]them!”

[032-170]“I suppose you mean I’m doing that,” George said bitterly.

[032-171]“Yes, I do!” she sobbed, and drooped upon the stairway railing,

[032-172]exhausted.

[032-173]“On the contrary, I mean to save my mother from a calamity.”

[032-174]Fanny looked at him wanly, in a tired despair; then she stepped by him

[032-175]and went slowly to her own door, where she paused and beckoned to him.

[032-176]“What do you want?”

[032-177]“Just come here a minute.”

[032-178]“What for?” he asked impatiently.

[032-179]“I just wanted to say something to you.”

[032-180]“Well, for heaven’s sake, say it! There’s nobody to hear.”

[032-181]Nevertheless, after a moment, as she beckoned him again, he went to

[032-182]her, profoundly annoyed. “Well, what is it?”

[032-183]“George,” she said in a low voice, “I think you ought to be told

[032-184]something. If I were you, I’d let my mother alone.”
