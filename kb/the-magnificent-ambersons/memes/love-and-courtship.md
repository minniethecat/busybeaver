## Overview

Love and courtship in Booth Tarkington's *The Magnificent Ambersons* (1918) function not merely as romantic subplot but as the novel's central emotional engine. The story is structured around four interlocking love affairs, each illuminating a different dimension of how desire, pride, and social convention interact in the turn-of-the-century Midwestern town of Midland. Two pairs of lovers -- one young, one middle-aged -- mirror and obstruct each other: the youthful courtship of [[george-amberson-minafer]] and [[lucy-morgan]] runs parallel to the middle-aged romance of [[isabel-amberson]] and [[eugene-morgan]], and each becomes the obstacle to the other. Courtship in the Amberson world is never purely personal; it is always freighted with family reputation, social status, and the Amberson pride that proves fatal to human happiness.

Tarkington's treatment of love is notable for its frank acknowledgment of romance among the middle-aged. The novel insists -- against its own young protagonist's deepest convictions -- that romantic love is not the exclusive province of youth. In doing so, it stakes out a thematic position that was unusual for American fiction of its era and remains one of the novel's most distinctive achievements.

## In the Novel

### Youthful Courtship: George and Lucy

The novel's most conventional romance -- and the one most familiar to its contemporary readers -- is the courtship of George Amberson Minafer and Lucy Morgan. They meet at the last of the great Amberson balls, and their attraction is immediate, physical, and irrational. The narrator, in one of Tarkington's most famous passages, reflects on the mysterious nature of young love:

> Age, confused by its own long accumulation of follies, is everlastingly inquiring, "What does _she_ see in _him?_" as if young love came about through thinking -- or through conduct. Age wants to know: "What on earth can they _talk_ about?" as if talking had anything to do with April rains! (007-115)

George and Lucy's courtship unfolds through dances, sleigh rides, cotillions, and letters written from college. Lucy, the more emotionally honest of the two, is clear-eyed about her predicament: she has "fallen in love with George Amberson Minafer at first sight" (035-075), a condition she describes as having been ambushed by "the riskiest of all the moonshine ambushes wherein tricky romance snares credulous young love" (035-079). George, for his part, is possessive, imperious, and prone to quarrelling -- but genuinely smitten. Their relationship reaches its ambiguous climax when Lucy offers the word "Almost" (019-114) as a description of their engagement, followed by the letter in which she writes: "No. It must keep on being almost. Isn't almost pretty pleasant?" (019-125).

### Middle-Aged Romance: Isabel and Eugene

The true heart of the novel's treatment of love, however, lies in the relationship between Isabel Amberson and Eugene Morgan. Their story spans more than two decades. In the 1870s, Eugene courted the young Isabel with sonnets and german-leading; he was "generous, poor, well-dressed, and his amazing persuasiveness was one reason why he was always in debt" (003-014). No one doubted he would win her -- until the night he stepped through a bass viol during a drunken serenade, and Isabel, mortified not by his wildness but by the public spectacle of it all, refused to see him again. Two weeks later her engagement to the "persistent one," Wilbur Minafer, was announced (003-027).

When Eugene returns to Midland twenty years later -- now a widower, a successful manufacturer of automobiles, and a figure of the emerging twentieth century -- he resumes his place in Isabel's life. His declaration to an old friend captures his forward-looking philosophy: "There aren't any old times. When times are gone they're not old, they're dead! There aren't any times but new times!" (009-182).

The quality of their renewed connection is rendered in one of the novel's most quietly luminous images. George, coming home one afternoon, sees his mother and Eugene on the garden path:

> They had paused in their slow walk from the front door to the gate, yet still stood side by side, their shoulders almost touching, as though neither Isabel nor Eugene quite realized that their feet had ceased to bear them forward; and they were not looking at each other, but at some indefinite point before them, as people do who consider together thoughtfully and in harmony. (027-165)

The narrator adds that "somewhere about Eugene, not quite to be located, there was a romantic gravity; and Isabel, tall and graceful, with high colour and absorbed eyes, was visibly no wife walking down to the gate with her husband" (027-174). This is not flirtation; it is the shared understanding of two people who have found in each other what they have found nowhere else. When Lucy asks her father, "You liked her pretty well once, I guess, papa," Eugene's quiet reply -- "I do still" (015-031) -- shifts from past to present tense, carrying the full weight of twenty years.

### Love Destroyed: George's Possession and Isabel's Sacrifice

The central romantic tragedy of the novel lies in the collision between these two love affairs. George, who sees his mother not as a woman but "wholly a mother" (005-164), is incapable of accepting her romantic life. The narrator observes that this is a failure of imagination built into youth itself:

> Youth cannot imagine romance apart from youth. That is why the roles of the heroes and heroines of plays are given by the managers to the most youthful actors they can find among the competent. Both middle-aged people and young people enjoy a play about young lovers; but only middle-aged people will tolerate a play about middle-aged lovers. (005-172)

When George learns that Eugene has proposed marriage to Isabel, his response is not merely disapproval but a possessive fury that reduces his mother to helplessness. He threatens to leave town forever, to never see her again if she marries. Isabel's choice -- conveyed in a letter she writes to George in the night while he sleeps -- is the novel's definitive statement on the nature of maternal love and its capacity for self-annihilation:

> I love you better than anything and everything else on earth. God gave you to me -- and oh! how thankful I have been every day of my life for that sacred gift -- and nothing can ever come between me and God's gift. I cannot hurt you, and I cannot let you stay hurt as you have been -- not another instant after you wake up, my darling boy! It is beyond my power. (035-013)

### Unrequited and Companionate Love

The novel's portrait of love is rounded out by two additional variations. Fanny Minafer carries an unrequited affection for Eugene Morgan that is granted pathos rather than mockery by the narrator. Wilbur Minafer, Isabel's husband, represents a form of companionate marriage that is dutiful, decent, and entirely without passion -- "she was from the start 'a good wife to him'" (003-082), but, as Mrs. Foster had predicted, "She couldn't love Wilbur, could she?" (003-066). The novel suggests that such marriages are the normative state of the Amberson social world -- safe, respectable, and quietly tragic.

- **(003-012)** -- Eugene Morgan courts Isabel Amberson with "sonnets lacking neither music nor wit," the "sparkling gentleman" who would have won her but for the serenade disaster.
- **(005-172)** -- The narrator's meditation on youth's inability to "imagine romance apart from youth," a passage that defines the novel's generational conflict over love.
- **(007-115)** -- The famous observation that "young love came about" not "through thinking -- or through conduct," a defense of the irrationality of romantic attraction.

- **(015-031)** -- Lucy: "You liked her pretty well once, I guess, papa." Eugene: "I do still." The shift from past to present tense encapsulates two decades of enduring love.

## Historical Context

The courtship rituals depicted in *The Magnificent Ambersons* reflect the conventions of upper-middle-class American life in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The novel's world of formal balls, cotillions, serenades, calling cards, and "gentleman callers" was already passing into memory by the time Tarkington wrote the novel in 1918. The Ambersons' ball, with its imported orchestra and caterers, its receiving line, and its elaborate codified dances, represents a social form at the height of its elaboration -- and on the verge of obsolescence.

The novel registers this transition explicitly. The automobile, Eugene Morgan's invention, is not merely a technological novelty but a social disruptor. It changes courtship by extending the geographic range of young people's movements, eroding the chaperoned, household-centered rituals of an earlier era. George's sleigh ride with Lucy -- a formal, chaperone-free excursion that was already somewhat daring for the period -- is interrupted by the appearance of Eugene's horseless carriage, a moment that juxtaposes two eras of courtship in a single snow-covered road.

Tarkington's insistence on middle-aged romance was itself a quiet transgression. American literature of the period overwhelmingly treated romantic love as a youthful phenomenon; novels about older lovers were rare, and Tarkington's decision to make Isabel and Eugene's relationship the emotional center of his story represented a significant departure from convention. The novel's explicit meditation on this point -- "middle-aged lovers are a joke -- not a very funny one" (005-178) -- functions as both a diagnosis of his culture's limitation and a defense of his own narrative choice.
