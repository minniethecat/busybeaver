# Wealth and Fortune

> "Major Amberson had 'made a fortune' in 1873, when other people were losing fortunes, and the magnificence of the Ambersons began then." (001-001–001-002)

Wealth is the bedrock upon which the entire Amberson edifice stands. From the opening sentence — "Major Amberson had 'made a fortune' in 1873" (001-001) — to the final chapters in which George Amberson Minafer is reduced to an eight-dollar-a-week job, the novel is a sustained meditation on what money builds, what it cannot protect, and how fortunes made in one generation are dissipated in the next. The Amberson fortune is the source of the family's "magnificence" and the engine of its destruction — a double role that makes wealth perhaps the most structurally important concept in the novel, underpinning [[pride-and-arrogance]], enabling the lifestyle documented in [[fashion-and-social-status]], and providing the material context for the [[decline-of-the-ambersons]].

## Overview

Tarkington's treatment of wealth in *The Magnificent Ambersons* is notable for its moral complexity. The novel neither condemns wealth as inherently corrupting nor celebrates it as an unambiguous good. Instead, it presents wealth as a kind of force — like the automobile that [[eugene-morgan]] builds — that amplifies whatever human qualities already exist in its possessors. Major Amberson's fortune-making is presented as a genuine achievement, a feat of timing and judgment that occurred "when other people were losing fortunes" (001-001). His wealth is a sign of his competence, his ability to read the economic moment correctly.

But the fortune that Major Amberson builds becomes, in the hands of his descendants, something very different. For George Amberson Minafer, wealth is not an achievement but an entitlement — something one is born into, not something one earns. The novel traces this subtle but crucial shift from wealth-as-accomplishment to wealth-as-identity, and in doing so captures one of the great themes of American literature: the tension between the founding generation that builds and the inheriting generation that merely spends.

The novel's title itself encodes this tension. The Ambersons are "magnificent" — an adjective that connotes both genuine impressiveness and a certain excess, a grandeur that may already be past its prime. "Magnificence," the narrator observes, "like the size of a fortune, is always comparative" (001-003). This is not just a statement about wealth; it is a warning. What is magnificent today may not be magnificent tomorrow, because the standards by which magnificence is measured are always changing — and in the rapidly industrializing America of the early twentieth century, they are changing faster than the Ambersons can adapt.

## In the Novel

### The Founding Fortune

The Amberson fortune originates with Major Amberson, the patriarch whose business acumen established the family's position. The novel is deliberately vague about the specifics of his wealth — we are told he "made a fortune" but not how — because the mechanism of wealth creation is less important than its effects. What matters is that the fortune exists, that it is large enough to fund a way of life, and that it becomes the central fact around which the family's identity is organized.

From this fortune flow all the visible markers of Amberson magnificence: the Mansion, the Addition, the carriages, the servants, the leisure that allows George to grow up without ever having to consider what work might mean. "I don't work. I mean I don't work" (016-187–016-188), George tells Lucy Morgan, and the doubling — "I don't work. I mean I don't work" — is a perfect expression of wealth as identity. For George, not working is not a temporary condition or a phase of life; it is what he is.

### The Culture of Spending

The novel devotes considerable attention to the Ambersons' relationship with money, which is characterized not by miserly accumulation but by conspicuous expenditure. The Mansion's walnut woodwork alone costs sixty thousand dollars (002-099). The Ambersons "spent money like water" (006-101), and this spending is a form of social performance — a way of demonstrating that one belongs to a class for whom money is not a scarce resource to be managed but an abundant one to be deployed.

This culture of spending extends beyond the Ambersons themselves to become a template for the town's aspirational class. When the townspeople observe that Major Amberson "threw money around for dogs" (002-155), the observation is not entirely critical. There is a kind of admiration in it — the recognition that the Ambersons live by rules different from those that govern ordinary people. The spending is itself a form of magnificence.

Yet the novel also registers the costs of this spending culture. George's financial dependence on his grandfather — "he didn't have any mother to get money out of a grandfather for him" (012-120), Sydney Carton observes of a contrast case — is a form of prolonged infancy. He never develops the capacity to generate wealth himself, only the capacity to consume it. When the fortune that supports him begins to contract, he has no resources — financial, psychological, or practical — with which to respond.

### New Money, New Rules

The novel's treatment of wealth is inseparable from its treatment of social change. The Amberson fortune represents an older form of wealth — wealth accumulated through land, through established business, through the slow accretion of prestige. But the world around them is being reshaped by a new kind of fortune-making, exemplified by Eugene Morgan and his automobile factory. When George dismisses automobiles as "a useless nuisance" (010-184), he is not merely expressing a personal preference; he is rejecting the economic logic that will define the coming century.

Eugene Morgan represents the new economic order in several ways. First, his wealth is self-made — he is "building up a fortune out of automobiles" (037-133) through his own labor and innovation, not inheriting it. Second, his wealth is tied to a specific technology, which means it grows as the technology spreads rather than being depleted by spending. Third, and most significantly, his relationship to money is instrumental rather than symbolic: he invests, he reinvests, he builds, he expands. The Ambersons consume; Eugene produces.

The novel's judgment is clear. The narrator notes approvingly that "every day I hear of a new fortune some person has got through [Eugene's] inventions" (037-114). The old fortune, the Amberson fortune, is a finite quantity being steadily diminished. The new fortunes, the Morgan fortunes, are expanding quantities tied to the growth of the industrial economy. Between these two models of wealth, there is no contest — and the Ambersons' refusal to recognize this fact is itself a symptom of the obsolescence that will overtake them.

### The Evaporation

The novel traces the gradual, inexorable disappearance of the Amberson fortune with a restraint that makes the loss all the more powerful. We are not given a single catastrophic moment of financial ruin; instead, the fortune simply ceases to be there. The investments that Major Amberson made decades ago no longer yield what they once did. The expenses that the family's way of life requires continue regardless. And the younger generation — George, specifically — has no capacity to replenish what is being spent.

The sale of the Mansion, "once the pride of the town" (002-083), is the symbolic endpoint of this process, but it is not the process itself. The fortune was already gone before the Mansion was sold; the sale merely makes visible what has already happened. When George ends the novel as a chemical laboratory worker at eight dollars a week, and later as a moderately successful "constructor of patent screens for windows and doors" (043-104–043-107), the transformation is complete. The fortune that was "made" in 1873 has been unmade, and what remains is a young man who has finally — belatedly, painfully — learned to work.

## Historical Context

The Ambersons' financial trajectory mirrors a historical pattern well documented in studies of Gilded Age American wealth. The period between 1870 and 1920 saw both the greatest concentration of private fortunes in American history and the highest rate of fortune dissipation. The "Great Fortunes" of the Gilded Age — the Vanderbilts, the Astors, the Rockefellers — were, in many cases, substantially reduced within two or three generations, a pattern that economists have termed "regression to the mean" of wealth. The Ambersons are not a Vanderbilt-scale fortune, but their experience — rapid accumulation in one generation, lavish consumption in the second, dissipation in the third — follows the same structural logic.

The novel also captures a specific inflection point in the history of American capitalism: the transition from an economy dominated by land, trade, and small manufacturing to one dominated by large-scale industrial production. The fortune that Major Amberson "made" in 1873 was likely in real estate, railroads, or commodities — the characteristic vehicles of nineteenth-century wealth. The fortune that Eugene Morgan is building is in a transformative technology — the automobile — that represents the characteristic vehicle of twentieth-century wealth. The Ambersons' failure to adapt is not merely personal; it reflects a broader historical shift that left many old-money families stranded on the wrong side of economic change.
