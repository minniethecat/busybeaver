[043-001]“Good-night, [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]].”

[043-002]His voice had a strangled sound in spite of him; but she seemed not to

[043-003]notice it, and he heard her go to her own room and lock herself in with

[043-004]bolt and key against burglars. She had said the one thing she should

[043-005]not have said just then: “I’m sure your mother’s watching over you,

[043-006]Georgie.” She had meant to be kind, but it destroyed his last chance

[043-007]for sleep that night. He would have slept little if she had not said

[043-008]it, but since she had said it, he could not sleep at all. For he knew

[043-009]that it was true—if it could be true—and that his mother, if she still

[043-010]lived in spirit, would be weeping on the other side of the wall of

[043-011]silence, weeping and seeking for some gate to let her through so that

[043-012]she could come and “watch over him.”

[043-013]He felt that if there were such gates they were surely barred: they

[043-014]were like those awful library doors downstairs, which had shut her in

[043-015]to begin the suffering to which he had consigned her.

[043-016]The room was still Isabel’s. Nothing had been changed: even the

[043-017]photographs of George, of the Major, and of “brother George” still

[043-018]stood on her dressing-table, and in a drawer of her desk was an old

[043-019]picture of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] and [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]], taken together, which George had found, but

[043-020]had slowly closed away again from sight, not touching it. To-morrow

[043-021]everything would be gone; and he had heard there was not long to wait

[043-022]before the house itself would be demolished. The very space which

[043-023]tonight was still Isabel’s room would be cut into new shapes by new

[043-024]walls and floors and ceilings; yet the room would always live, for it

[043-025]could not die out of George’s memory. It would live as long as he did,

[043-026]and it would always be murmurous with a tragic, wistful whispering.

[043-027]And if space itself can be haunted, as memory is haunted, then some

[043-028]time, when the space that was Isabel’s room came to be made into the

[043-029]small bedrooms and “kitchenettes” already designed as its destiny,

[043-030]that space might well be haunted and the new occupants come to feel

[043-031]that some seemingly causeless depression hung about it—a wraith of the

[043-032]passion that filled it throughout the last night that [[george-amberson-minafer|George Minafer]]

[043-033]spent there.

[043-034]Whatever remnants of the old high-handed arrogance were still within

[043-035]him, he did penance for his deepest sin that night—and it may be that

[043-036]to this day some impressionable, overworked woman in a “kitchenette,”

[043-037]after turning out the light will seem to see a young man kneeling in

[043-038]the darkness, shaking convulsively, and, with arms outstretched through

[043-039]the wall, clutching at the covers of a shadowy bed. It may seem to her

[043-040]that she hears the faint cry, over and over:

[043-041]“Mother, forgive me! God, forgive me!”

Chapter XXXII

[043-042]At least, it may be claimed for George that his last night in the house

[043-043]where he had been born was not occupied with his own disheartening

[043-044]future, but with sorrow for what sacrifices his pride and youth had

[043-045]demanded of others. And early in the morning he came downstairs and

[043-046]tried to help Fanny make coffee on the kitchen range.

[043-047]“There was something I wanted to say to you last night, Aunt Fanny,” he

[043-048]said, as she finally discovered that an amber fluid, more like tea than

[043-049]coffee, was as near ready to be taken into the human system as it would

[043-050]ever be. “I think I’d better do it now.”

[043-051]She set the coffee-pot back upon the stove with a little crash, and,

[043-052]looking at him in a desperate anxiety, began to twist her dainty apron

[043-053]between her fingers without any consciousness of what she was doing.

[043-054]“Why—why—” she stammered; but she knew what he was going to

[043-055]say, and that was why she had been more and more nervous.

[043-056]“Hadn’t—perhaps—perhaps we’d better get the—the things moved to the

[043-057]little new home first, George. Let’s—”

[043-058]He interrupted quietly, though at her phrase, “the little new home,”

[043-059]his pungent impulse was to utter one loud shout and run. “It was about

[043-060]this new place that I wanted to speak. I’ve been thinking it over, and

[043-061]I’ve decided. I want you to take all the things from mother’s room and

[043-062]use them and keep them for me, and I’m sure the little apartment will

[043-063]be just what you like; and with the extra bedroom probably you could

[043-064]find some woman friend to come and live there, and share the expense

[043-065]with you. But I’ve decided on another arrangement for myself, and so

[043-066]I’m not going with you. I don’t suppose you’ll mind much, and I don’t

[043-067]see why you should mind—particularly, that is. I’m not very lively

[043-068]company these days, or any days, for that matter. I can’t imagine you,

[043-069]or any one else, being much attached to me, so—”

[043-070]He stopped in amazement: no chair had been left in the kitchen, but

[043-071]Fanny gave a despairing glance around her, in search of one, then sank

[043-072]abruptly, and sat flat upon the floor.

[043-073]“You’re going to leave me in the lurch!” she gasped.

[043-074]“What on earth—” George sprang to her. “Get up, Aunt Fanny!”

[043-075]“I can’t. I’m too weak. Let me alone, George!” And as he released the

[043-076]wrist he had seized to help her, she repeated the dismal prophecy which

[043-077]for days she had been matching against her hopes: “You’re going to

[043-078]leave me—in the lurch!”

[043-079]“Why no, Aunt Fanny!” he protested. “At first I’d have been something

[043-080]of a burden on you. I’m to get eight dollars a week; about thirty-two

[043-081]a month. The rent’s thirty-six dollars a month, and the table-d’hote

[043-082]dinner runs up to over twenty-two dollars apiece, so with my half

[043-083]of the rent—eighteen dollars—I’d have less than nothing left out of

[043-084]my salary to pay my share of the groceries for all the breakfasts

[043-085]and luncheons. You see you’d not only be doing all the housework and

[043-086]cooking, but you’d be paying more of the expenses than I would.”

[043-087]She stared at him with such a forlorn blankness as he had never seen.

[043-088]“I’d be paying—” she said feebly. “I’d be paying—”

[043-089]“Certainly you would. You’d be using more of your [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] than—”

[043-090]“My money!” Fanny’s chin drooped upon her thin chest, and she laughed

[043-091]miserably. “I’ve got twenty-eight dollars. That’s all.”

[043-092]“You mean until the interest is due again?”

[043-093]“I mean that’s all,” Fanny said. “I mean that’s all there is. There

[043-094]won’t be any more interest because there isn’t any principal.”

[043-095]“Why, you told—”

[043-096]She shook her head. “No, I haven’t told you anything.”

[043-097]“Then it was [[george-amberson|Uncle George]]. He told me you had enough to fall back on.

[043-098]That’s just what he said: ‘to fall back on.’ He said you’d lost more

[043-099]than you should, in the headlight company, but he’d insisted that you

[043-100]should hold out enough to live on, and you’d very wisely followed his

[043-101]advice.”

[043-102]“I know,” she said weakly. “I told him so. He didn’t know, or else he’d

[043-103]forgotten, how much Wilbur’s insurance amounted to, and I—oh, it seemed

[043-104]such a sure way to make a real fortune out of a little—and I thought I

[043-105]could do something for you, George, if you ever came to need it—and it

[043-106]all looked so bright I just thought I’d put it all in. I did—every cent

[043-107]except my last interest payment—and it’s gone.”

[043-108]“Good Lord!” George began to pace up and down on the worn planks of the

[043-109]bare floor. “Why on earth did you wait till now to tell such a thing as

[043-110]this?”

[043-111]“I couldn’t tell till I had to,” she said piteously. “I couldn’t till

[043-112]George Amberson went away. He couldn’t do anything to help, anyhow,

[043-113]and I just didn’t want him to talk to me about it—he’s been at me so

[043-114]much about not putting more in than I could afford to lose, and said

[043-115]he considered he had my—my word I wasn’t putting more than that in it.

[043-116]So I thought: What was the use? What was the use of going over it all

[043-117]with him and having him reproach me, and probably reproach himself?

[043-118]It wouldn’t do any good—not any good on earth.” She got out her lace

[043-119]handkerchief and began to cry. “Nothing does any good, I guess, in this

[043-120]old world. Oh, how tired of this old world I am! I didn’t know what

[043-121]to do. I just tried to go ahead and be as practical as I could, and

[043-122]arrange some way for us to live. Oh, I knew you didn’t want me, George!

[043-123]You always teased me and berated me whenever you had a chance from the

[043-124]time you were a little boy—you did so! Later, you’ve tried to be kinder

[043-125]to me, but you don’t want me around—oh, I can see that much! You don’t

[043-126]suppose I want to thrust myself on you, do you? It isn’t very pleasant

[043-127]to be thrusting yourself on a person you know doesn’t want you—but I

[043-128]knew you oughtn’t to be left all alone in the world; it isn’t good. I

[043-129]knew your mother’d want me to watch over you and try to have something

[043-130]like a home for you—I know she’d want me to do what I tried to do!”

[043-131]Fanny’s tears were bitter now, and her voice, hoarse and wet, was

[043-132]tragically sincere. “I tried—I tried to be practical—to look after your

[043-133]interests—to make things as nice for you as I could—I walked my heels

[043-134]down looking for a place for us to live—I walked and walked over this

[043-135]town—I didn’t ride one block on a street-car—I wouldn’t use five cents

[043-136]no matter how tired I—Oh!” She sobbed uncontrollably. “Oh! and now—you

[043-137]don’t want—you want—you want to leave me in the lurch! You—”

[043-138]George stopped walking. “In God’s name, Aunt Fanny,” he said, “quit

[043-139]spreading out your handkerchief and drying it and then getting it all

[043-140]wet again! I mean stop crying! Do! And for heaven’s sake, get up. Don’t

[043-141]sit there with your back against the boiler and—”

[043-142]“It’s not hot,” Fanny sniffled. “It’s cold; the plumbers disconnected

[043-143]it. I wouldn’t mind if they hadn’t. I wouldn’t mind if it burned me,

[043-144]George.”

[043-145]“Oh, my Lord!” He went to her, and lifted her. “For God’s sake, get up!

[043-146]Come, let’s take the coffee into the other room, and see what’s to be

[043-147]done.”

[043-148]He got her to her feet; she leaned upon him, already somewhat

[043-149]comforted, and, with his arm about her, he conducted her to the dining

[043-150]room and seated her in one of the two kitchen chairs which had been

[043-151]placed at the rough table. “There!” he said, “get over it!” Then

[043-152]he brought the coffee-pot, some lumps of sugar in a tin pan, and,

[043-153]finding that all the coffee-cups were broken, set water glasses upon

[043-154]the table, and poured some of the pale coffee into them. By this time

[043-155]Fanny’s spirits had revived appreciably: she looked up with a plaintive

[043-156]eagerness. “I had bought all my fall clothes, George,” she said; “and I

[043-157]paid every bill I owed. I don’t owe a cent for clothes, George.”

[043-158]“That’s good,” he said wanly, and he had a moment of physical dizziness

[043-159]that decided him to sit down quickly. For an instant it seemed to him

[043-160]that he was not Fanny’s nephew, but married to her. He passed his pale

[043-161]hand over his paler forehead. “Well, let’s see where we stand,” he said

[043-162]feebly. “Let’s see if we can afford this place you’ve selected.”

[043-163]Fanny continued to brighten. “I’m sure it’s the most practical plan we

[043-164]could possibly have worked out, George—and it is a comfort to be among

[043-165]nice people. I think we’ll both enjoy it, because the truth is we’ve

[043-166]been keeping too much to ourselves for a long while. It isn’t good for

[043-167]people.”

[043-168]“I was thinking about the money, Aunt Fanny. You see—”
