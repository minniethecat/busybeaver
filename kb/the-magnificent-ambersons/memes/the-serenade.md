# The Serenade

> "Of a summer night, young men would bring an orchestra under a pretty girl's window — or, it might be, her father's window, which was the same thing — and flute, harp, fiddle, cello, and bass viol would presently release to the dulcet stars the assurance that she was the most charming thing and the loveliest." (002-003–002-005)

The serenade is the novel's foundational event — the moment that, though it occurs years before the main action begins, determines everything that follows. Eugene Morgan, young and impetuous, joined a group of serenaders beneath Isabel Amberson's window. But Eugene had been drinking, and what should have been a romantic gesture became a public humiliation. The scandal drove Isabel to reject the man she loved and accept the steady, unremarkable [[wilbur-minafer]] instead — a choice that would shape the fates of every major character in the generations that followed.

## Overview

In the social world of late nineteenth-century Midland, the serenade was an established courtship ritual — a formalized performance in which young men declared their romantic intentions through music. The narrator describes the custom in detail in Chapter 2, establishing it as one of the "lost arts" of the pre-automobile era, along with the "winter sleighing parties" and "tearful love letters" that gave way to the faster, less ceremonious courtship of the automobile age.

What happened at the Amberson Mansion on the night of the serenade is never narrated directly. Instead, the novel approaches the event through a series of retrospective references, each adding a layer of detail and interpretation. The serenade exists in the novel as a kind of absent center — the event that everyone knows about, that everyone has an opinion on, but that the narrator never fully dramatizes. This narrative strategy makes the serenade all the more potent: it is the wound that the novel circles but never directly touches.

## Key Sources

The event is reconstructed through scattered references across multiple chapters:

**Chapter 2** establishes the cultural context: the serenade as a lost courtship ritual, a "pretty custom" that has vanished along with the slower rhythms of life before the automobile (002-003–002-008).

**Chapter 3** provides the most detailed account of what happened: "during a moonlight serenade upon the lawn before the Amberson Mansion [...] one of Isabel's brothers was among the serenaders, and that Miss Isabel's window was not the only one at which they stopped" (003-017–003-043). The detail that they stopped at multiple windows — that Eugene was not serenading Isabel exclusively — suggests the extent to which the event spiraled beyond Eugene's control. The chapter also records the town's judgment: "he got a little wild one night at a serenade!" (003-043).

**Chapter 15** adds Eugene Morgan's own retrospective account, delivered decades later to George Amberson Minafer. Eugene recalls "a long midsummer serenade" and admits with characteristic honesty that "those things used to be livelier. That serenade was just before Isabel was married" (015-084–015-090). The timing — "just before Isabel was married" — confirms the serenade's role as the proximate cause of her decision to marry Wilbur.

## Consequences

The serenade's consequences ripple through every subsequent chapter of the novel. First and most immediately, it ends Isabel's relationship with Eugene: "She took the persistent one, Wilbur Minafer, no breaker of hearts, no bewilderer of pretty girls, and they were married" (003-030–003-033). The serenade transforms Eugene from suitor to outcast, and Wilbur from an also-ran to a husband.

Second, it creates the emotional architecture of the novel's central triangle. Eugene leaves town, makes his fortune in automobiles, and returns years later a widower — only to find Isabel widowed as well. The serenade is the unfinished business between them, the wound that thirty years of separation have not healed. When Eugene resumes his courtship of Isabel in the novel's present timeline, every gesture between them is shadowed by the memory of that night.

Third, and most tragically, the serenade creates the conditions for George Amberson Minafer's intervention. It is because Isabel chose Wilbur over Eugene that George exists at all — and it is George who, when Eugene finally has a second chance at happiness with Isabel, destroys it. The serenade, in this sense, is the novel's original sin: the event whose consequences cascade through generations, beyond anyone's power to undo.

## In the Novel's Structure

The serenade exemplifies Tarkington's use of backstory as a structural device. By placing the most consequential romantic event in the novel's pre-history, he ensures that the reader always understands the present action as shadowed by the past. Isabel's present-tense scenes with Eugene carry an emotional weight that they could not possess if the reader did not know about the serenade. George Minafer's campaign against Eugene is not merely the petulance of a spoiled young man; it is the latest chapter in a story that began before he was born.

The serenade also serves a thematic function, linking the novel's romantic plot to its broader meditation on the passage of time. The serenade belongs to an older world — a world of moonlight and orchestras and formal courtship — that the automobile age has rendered obsolete. By the time Eugene returns to Midland, the custom of the serenade has vanished, replaced by faster, less ceremonious modes of romantic expression. The serenade is doubly lost: lost as a specific moment between Isabel and Eugene, and lost as a cultural form that belonged to a vanished era.
