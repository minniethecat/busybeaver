Chapter V

[007-001]The hero of the fête, with the dark-eyed little beauty upon his arm,

[007-002]reached the top of the second flight of stairs; and here, beyond a

[007-003]spacious landing, where two proud-like darkies tended a crystalline

[007-004]punch bowl, four wide archways in a rose-vine lattice framed gliding

[007-005]silhouettes of waltzers, already smoothly at it to the castanets of

[007-006]“_La Paloma_.” Old John Minafer, evidently surfeited, was in the act

[007-007]of leaving these delights. “D’want ’ny more o’ that!” he barked.

[007-008]“Just slidin’ around! Call that dancin’? Rather see a jig any day in

[007-009]the world! They ain’t very modest, some of ’em. I don’t mind _that_,

[007-010]though. Not me!”

[007-011][[fanny-minafer|Miss Fanny Minafer]] was no longer in charge of him: he emerged from

[007-012]the ballroom escorted by a middle-aged man of commonplace appearance.

[007-013]The escort had a dry, lined face upon which, not ornamentally but as

[007-014]a matter of course, there grew a business man’s short moustache; and

[007-015]his thin neck showed an Adam’s apple, but not conspicuously, for there

[007-016]was nothing conspicuous about him. Baldish, dim, quiet, he was an

[007-017]unnoticeable part of this festival, and although there were a dozen or

[007-018]more middle-aged men present, not casually to be distinguished from him

[007-019]in general aspect, he was probably the last person in the big house at

[007-020]whom a stranger would have glanced twice. It did not enter George’s

[007-021]mind to mention to Miss Morgan that this was his father, or to say

[007-022]anything whatever about him.

[007-023][[wilbur-minafer|Mr. Minafer]] shook his son’s hand unobtrusively in passing.

[007-024]“I’ll take Uncle John home,” he said, in a low voice. “Then I guess

[007-025]I’ll go on home myself—I’m not a great hand at parties, you know.

[007-026]Good-night, George.”

[007-027]George murmured a friendly enough good-night without pausing.

[007-028]Ordinarily he was not ashamed of the Minafers; he seldom thought about

[007-029]them at all, for he belonged, as most American children do, to the

[007-030]mother’s family—but he was anxious not to linger with Miss Morgan in

[007-031]the vicinity of old John, whom he felt to be a disgrace.

[007-032]He pushed brusquely through the fringe of calculating youths who were

[007-033]gathered in the arches, watching for chances to dance only with girls

[007-034]who would soon be taken off their hands, and led his stranger lady out

[007-035]upon the floor. They caught the time instantly, and were away in the

[007-036]waltz.

[007-037]George danced well, and Miss Morgan seemed to float as part of the

[007-038]music, the very dove itself of “_La Paloma_.” They said nothing as they

[007-039]danced; her eyes were cast down all the while—the prettiest gesture

[007-040]for a dancer—and there was left in the universe, for each, of them,

[007-041]only their companionship in this waltz; while the faces of the other

[007-042]dancers, swimming by, denoted not people but merely blurs of colour.

[007-043]George became conscious of strange feelings within him: an exaltation

[007-044]of soul, tender, but indefinite, and seemingly located in the upper

[007-045]part of his diaphragm.

[007-046]The stopping of the music came upon him like the waking to an alarm

[007-047]clock; for instantly six or seven of the calculating persons about the

[007-048]entry-ways bore down upon Miss Morgan to secure dances. George had to

[007-049]do with one already established as a belle, it seemed.

[007-050]“Give me the next and the one after that,” he said hurriedly,

[007-051]recovering some presence of mind, just as the nearest applicant reached

[007-052]them. “And give me every third one the rest of the evening.”

[007-053]She laughed. “Are you asking?”

[007-054]“What do you mean, ‘asking’?”

[007-055]“It sounded as though you were just telling me to give you all those

[007-056]dances.”

[007-057]“Well, I want ’em!” George insisted.

[007-058]“What about all the other girls it’s your duty to dance with?”

[007-059]“They’ll have to go without,” he said heartlessly; and then, with

[007-060]surprising vehemence: “Here! I want to know: Are you going to give me

[007-061]those—”

[007-062]“Good gracious!” she laughed. “Yes!”

[007-063]The applicants flocked round her, urging contracts for what remained,

[007-064]but they did not dislodge George from her side, though he made

[007-065]it evident that they succeeded in annoying him; and presently he

[007-066]extricated her from an accumulating siege—she must have connived in the

[007-067]extrication—and bore her off to sit beside him upon the stairway that

[007-068]led to the musicians’ gallery, where they were sufficiently retired,

[007-069]yet had a view of the room.

[007-070]“How’d all those ducks get to know you so quick?” George inquired, with

[007-071]little enthusiasm.

[007-072]“Oh, I’ve been here a week.”

[007-073]“Looks as if you’d been pretty busy!” he said. “Most of those ducks, I

[007-074]don’t know what my mother wanted to invite ’em here for.”

[007-075]“Don’t you like them?”

[007-076]“Oh, I used to see something of a few of ’em. I was president of a club

[007-077]we had here, and some of ’em belonged to it, but I don’t care much for

[007-078]that sort of thing any more. I really don’t see why my mother invited

[007-079]’em.”

[007-080]“Perhaps it was on account of their parents,” Miss Morgan suggested

[007-081]mildly. “Maybe she didn’t want to offend their fathers and mothers.”

[007-082]“Oh, hardly! I don’t think my mother need worry much about offending

[007-083]anybody in this old town.”

[007-084]“It must be wonderful,” said Miss Morgan. “It must be wonderful, Mr.

[007-085]Amberson—Mr. Minafer, I mean.”

[007-086]“What must be wonderful?”

[007-087]“To be so important as that!”

[007-088]“_That_ isn’t ‘important,’” George assured her. “Anybody that really is

[007-089]anybody ought to be able to do about as they like in their own town, I

[007-090]should think!”

[007-091]She looked at him critically from under her shading lashes—but her eyes

[007-092]grew gentler almost at once. In truth, they became more appreciative

[007-093]than critical. George’s imperious good looks were altogether manly,

[007-094]yet approached actual beauty as closely as a boy’s good looks

[007-095]should dare; and dance-music and flowers have some effect upon

[007-096]nineteen-year-old girls as well as upon eighteen-year-old boys. Miss

[007-097]Morgan turned her eyes slowly from George, and pressed her face among

[007-098]the lilies-of-the-valley and violets of the pretty bouquet she carried,

[007-099]while, from the gallery above, the music of the next dance carolled

[007-100]out merrily in a new two-step. The musicians made the melody gay for

[007-101]the Christmastime with chimes of sleighbells, and the entrance to the

[007-102]shadowed stairway framed the passing flushed and lively dancers, but

[007-103]neither George nor Miss Morgan suggested moving to join the dance.

[007-104]The stairway was draughty: the steps were narrow and uncomfortable; no

[007-105]older person would have remained in such a place. Moreover, these two

[007-106]young people were strangers to each other; neither had said anything in

[007-107]which the other had discovered the slightest intrinsic interest; there

[007-108]had not arisen between them the beginnings of congeniality, or even of

[007-109]friendliness—but stairways near ballrooms have more to answer for than

[007-110]have moonlit lakes and mountain sunsets. Some day the laws of glamour

[007-111]must be discovered, because they are so important that the world would

[007-112]be wiser now if Sir Isaac Newton had been hit on the head, not by an

[007-113]apple, but by a young lady.

[007-114]Age, confused by its own long accumulation of follies, is everlastingly

[007-115]inquiring, “What does _she_ see in _him?_” as if young love came about

[007-116]through thinking—or through conduct. Age wants to know: “What on earth

[007-117]can they _talk_ about?” as if talking had anything to do with April

[007-118]rains! At seventy, one gets up in the morning, finds the air sweet

[007-119]under a bright sun, feels lively; thinks, “I am hearty, today,” and

[007-120]plans to go for a drive. At eighteen, one goes to a dance, sits with

[007-121]a stranger on a stairway, feels peculiar, thinks nothing, and becomes

[007-122]incapable of any plan whatever. Miss Morgan and George stayed where

[007-123]they were.

[007-124]They had agreed to this in silence and without knowing it; certainly

[007-125]without exchanging glances of intelligence—they had exchanged no

[007-126]glances at all. Both sat staring vaguely out into the ballroom, and,

[007-127]for a time, they did not speak. Over their heads the music reached a

[007-128]climax of vivacity: drums, cymbals, triangle, and sleighbells, beating,

[007-129]clashing, tinkling. Here and there were to be seen couples so carried

[007-130]away that, ceasing to move at the decorous, even glide, considered

[007-131]most knowing, they pranced and whirled through the throng, from wall

[007-132]to wall, galloping bounteously in abandon. George suffered a shock of

[007-133]vague surprise when he perceived that his aunt, Fanny Minafer, was the

[007-134]lady-half of one of these wild couples.

[007-135]Fanny Minafer, who rouged a little, was like fruit which in some

[007-136]climates dries with the bloom on. Her features had remained prettily

[007-137]childlike; so had her figure, and there were times when strangers,

[007-138]seeing her across the street, took her to be about twenty; they were

[007-139]other times when at the same distance they took her to be about sixty,

[007-140]instead of forty, as she was. She had old days and young days; old

[007-141]hours and young hours; old minutes and young minutes; for the change

[007-142]might be that quick. An alteration in her expression, or a difference

[007-143]in the attitude of her head, would cause astonishing indentations

[007-144]to appear—and behold, Fanny was an old lady! But she had been never

[007-145]more childlike than she was tonight as she flew over the floor in the

[007-146]capable arms of the queer-looking duck; for this person was her partner.

[007-147]The queer-looking duck had been a real dancer in his day, it appeared;

[007-148]and evidently his day was not yet over. In spite of the headlong, gay

[007-149]rapidity with which he bore Miss Fanny about the big room, he danced

[007-150]authoritatively, avoiding without effort the lightest collision with

[007-151]other couples, maintaining sufficient grace throughout his wildest

[007-152]moments, and all the while laughing and talking with his partner. What

[007-153]was most remarkable to George, and a little irritating, this stranger

[007-154]in [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Mansion had no vestige of the air of deference proper

[007-155]to a stranger in such a place: he seemed thoroughly at home. He seemed

[007-156]offensively so, indeed, when, passing the entrance to the gallery

[007-157]stairway, he disengaged his hand from Miss Fanny’s for an instant,

[007-158]and not pausing in the dance, waved a laughing salutation more than

[007-159]cordial, then capered lightly out of sight.

[007-160]George gazed stonily at this manifestation, responding neither by word

[007-161]nor sign. “How’s that for a bit of freshness?” he murmured.

[007-162]“What was?” Miss Morgan asked.

[007-163]“That queer-looking duck waving his hand at me like that. Except he’s

[007-164]the Sharon girls’ uncle I don’t know him from Adam.”

[007-165]“You don’t need to,” she said. “He wasn’t waving his hand to you: he

[007-166]meant me.”

[007-167]“Oh, he did?” George was not mollified by the explanation. “Everybody

[007-168]seems to mean you! You certainly do seem to’ve been pretty busy this

[007-169]week you’ve been here!”

[007-170]She pressed her bouquet to her face again, and laughed into it, not

[007-171]displeased. She made no other comment, and for another period neither

[007-172]spoke. Meanwhile the music stopped; loud applause insisted upon its

[007-173]renewal; an encore was danced; there was an interlude of voices; and

[007-174]the changing of partners began.

[007-175]“Well,” said George finally, “I must say you don’t seem to be much of a

[007-176]prattler. They say it’s a great way to get a reputation for being wise,

[007-177]never saying much. Don’t you ever talk _any?_”

[007-178]“When people can understand,” she answered.

[007-179]He had been looking moodily out at the ballroom but he turned to her

[007-180]quickly, at this, saw that her eyes were sunny and content, over the

[007-181]top of her bouquet; and he consented to smile.

[007-182]“Girls are usually pretty fresh!” he said. “They ought to go to a man’s

[007-183]college about a year: they’d get taught a few things about freshness!

[007-184]What you got to do after two o’clock to-morrow afternoon?”

[007-185]“A whole lot of things. Every minute filled up.”

[007-186]“All right,” said George. “The snow’s fine for sleighing: I’ll come for

[007-187]you in a cutter at ten minutes after two.”

[007-188]“I can’t possibly go.”

[007-189]“If you don’t,” he said, “I’m going to sit in the cutter in front of

[007-190]the gate, wherever you’re visiting, all afternoon, and if you try to go

[007-191]out with anybody else he’s got to whip me before he gets you.” And as

[007-192]she laughed—though she blushed a little, too—he continued, seriously:

[007-193]“If you think I’m not in earnest you’re at liberty to make quite a big

[007-194]experiment!”
