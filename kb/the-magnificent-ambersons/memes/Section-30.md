[030-001]“Yes!” George cried. “I think you may have! That’s what I’m here about,

[030-002]and what I intend to—”

[030-003]“Don’t tell me what you intend, please,” Mrs. Johnson interrupted

[030-004]crisply. “And I should prefer that you would not make your voice quite

[030-005]so loud in this house, which I happen to own. Your aunt may have told

[030-006]you—though I think it would have been very unwise in her if she did,

[030-007]and not very considerate of me—she may have told you that we discussed

[030-008]some such topic as I have mentioned, and possibly that would have been

[030-009]true. If I talked it over with her, you may be sure I spoke in the most

[030-010]charitable spirit, and without sharing in other people’s disposition to

[030-011]put an evil interpretation on what may be nothing more than unfortunate

[030-012]appearances and—”

[030-013]“My God!” said George. “I can’t stand this!”

[030-014]“You have the option of dropping the subject,” Mrs. Johnson suggested

[030-015]tartly, and she added: “Or of leaving the house.”

[030-016]“I’ll do that soon enough, but first I mean to know—”

[030-017]“I am perfectly willing to tell you anything you wish if you will

[030-018]remember to ask it quietly. I’ll also take the liberty of reminding

[030-019]you that I had a perfect right to discuss the subject with your aunt.

[030-020]Other people may be less considerate in not confining their discussion

[030-021]of it, as I have, to charitable views expressed only to a member of the

[030-022]family. Other people—”

[030-023]“Other people!” the unhappy George repeated viciously. “That’s what I

[030-024]want to know about—these other people!”

[030-025]“I beg your pardon.”

[030-026]“I want to ask you about them. You say you know of other people who

[030-027]talk about this.”

[030-028]“I presume they do.”

[030-029]“How many?”

[030-030]“What?”

[030-031]“I want to know how many other people talk about it?”

[030-032]“Dear, dear!” she protested. “How should I know that?”

[030-033]“Haven’t you heard anybody mention it?”

[030-034]“I presume so.”

[030-035]“Well, how many have you heard?”

[030-036]Mrs. Johnson was becoming more annoyed than apprehensive, and she

[030-037]showed it. “Really, this isn’t a court-room,” she said. “And I’m not a

[030-038]defendant in a libel-suit, either!”

[030-039]The unfortunate young man lost what remained of his balance. “You may

[030-040]be!” he cried. “I intend to know just who’s dared to say these things,

[030-041]if I have to force my way into every house in town, and I’m going to

[030-042]make them take every word of it back! I mean to know the name of every

[030-043]slanderer that’s spoken of this matter to you and of every tattler

[030-044]you’ve passed it on to yourself. I mean to know—”

[030-045]“You’ll know something pretty quick!” she said, rising with difficulty;

[030-046]and her voice was thick with the sense of insult. “You’ll know that

[030-047]you’re out in the street. Please to leave my house!”

[030-048]George stiffened sharply. Then he bowed, and strode out of the door.

[030-049]Three minutes later, disheveled and perspiring, but cold all over, he

[030-050]burst into his [[george-amberson|Uncle George]]’s room at the Major’s without knocking.

[030-051]Amberson was dressing.

[030-052]“Good gracious, Georgie!” he exclaimed. “What’s up?”

[030-053]“I’ve just come from Mrs. Johnson’s—across the street,” George panted.

[030-054]“You have your own tastes!” was Amberson’s comment. “But curious as

[030-055]they are, you ought to do something better with your hair, and button

[030-056]your waistcoat to the right buttons—even for Mrs. Johnson! What were

[030-057]you doing over there?”

[030-058]“She told me to leave the house,” George said desperately. “I went

[030-059]there because [[fanny-minafer|Aunt Fanny]] told me the whole town was talking about my

[030-060]mother and that man Morgan—that they say my mother is going to marry

[030-061]him and that proves she was too fond of him before my father died—she

[030-062]said this Mrs. Johnson was one that talked about it, and I went to her

[030-063]to ask who were the others.”

[030-064]Amberson’s jaw fell in dismay. “Don’t tell me you did that!” he said,

[030-065]in a low voice; and then, seeing that it was true, “Oh, now you have

[030-066]done it!”

Chapter XXIII

[030-067]“I’ve ‘done it’?” George cried. “What do you mean: I’ve done it? And

[030-068]what have I done?”

[030-069]Amberson had collapsed into an easy chair beside his dressing-table,

[030-070]the white evening tie he had been about to put on dangling from his

[030-071]hand, which had fallen limply on the arm of the chair. The tie dropped

[030-072]to the floor before he replied; and the hand that had held it was

[030-073]lifted to stroke his graying hair reflectively. “By Jove!” he muttered.

[030-074]“That is too bad!”

[030-075]George folded his arms bitterly. “Will you kindly answer my question?

[030-076]What have I done that wasn’t honourable and right? Do you think these

[030-077]riffraff can go about bandying my mother’s name—”

[030-078]“They can now,” said Amberson. “I don’t know if they could before, but

[030-079]they certainly can now!”

[030-080]“What do you mean by that?”

[030-081]His uncle sighed profoundly, picked up his tie and, preoccupied with

[030-082]despondency, twisted the strip of white lawn till it became unwearable.

[030-083]Meanwhile, he tried to enlighten his nephew. “Gossip is never fatal,

[030-084]Georgie,” he said, “until it is denied. Gossip goes on about every

[030-085]human being alive and about all the dead that are alive enough to be

[030-086]remembered, and yet almost never does any harm until some defender

[030-087]makes a controversy. Gossip’s a nasty thing, but it’s sickly, and if

[030-088]people of good intentions will let it entirely alone, it will die,

[030-089]ninety-nine times out of a hundred.”

[030-090]“See here,” George said: “I didn’t come to listen to any generalizing

[030-091]dose of philosophy! I ask you—”

[030-092]“You asked me what you’ve done, and I’m telling you.” Amberson gave

[030-093]him a melancholy smile, continuing: “Suffer me to do it in my own way.

[030-094]Fanny says there’s been talk about your mother, and that Mrs. Johnson

[030-095]does some of it. I don’t know, because naturally nobody would come to

[030-096]me with such stuff or mention it before me; but it’s presumably true—I

[030-097]suppose it is. I’ve seen Fanny with Mrs. Johnson quite a lot; and

[030-098]that old lady is a notorious gossip, and that’s why she ordered you

[030-099]out of her house when you pinned her down that she’d been gossiping.

[030-100]I have a suspicion Mrs. Johnson has been quite a comfort to Fanny

[030-101]in their long talks; but she’ll probably quit speaking to her over

[030-102]this, because Fanny told you. I suppose it’s true that the ‘whole

[030-103]town,’ a lot of others, that is, do share in the gossip. In this town,

[030-104]naturally, anything about any Amberson has always been a stone dropped

[030-105]into the centre of a pond, and a lie would send the ripples as far as

[030-106]a truth would. I’ve been on a steamer when the story went all over

[030-107]the boat, the second day out, that the prettiest girl on board didn’t

[030-108]have any ears; and you can take it as a rule that when a woman’s past

[030-109]thirty-five the prettier her hair is, the more certain you are to meet

[030-110]somebody with reliable information that it’s a wig. You can be sure

[030-111]that for many years there’s been more gossip in this place about the

[030-112]Ambersons than about any other family. I dare say it isn’t so much so

[030-113]now as it used to be, because the town got too big long ago, but it’s

[030-114]the truth that the more prominent you are the more gossip there is

[030-115]about you, and the more people would like to pull you down. Well, they

[030-116]can’t do it as long as you refuse to know what gossip there is about

[030-117]you. But the minute you notice it, it’s got you! I’m not speaking of

[030-118]certain kinds of slander that sometimes people have got to take to the

[030-119]courts; I’m talking of the wretched buzzing the Mrs. Johnsons do—the

[030-120]thing you seem to have such a horror of—people ‘talking’—the kind of

[030-121]thing that has assailed your mother. People who have repeated a slander

[030-122]either get ashamed or forget it, if they’re let alone. Challenge them,

[030-123]and in self-defense they believe everything they’ve said: they’d rather

[030-124]believe you a sinner than believe themselves liars, naturally. Submit

[030-125]to gossip and you kill it; fight it and you make it strong. People will

[030-126]forget almost any slander except one that’s been fought.”

[030-127]“Is that all?” George asked.

[030-128]“I suppose so,” his uncle murmured sadly.

[030-129]“Well, then, may I ask what you’d have done, in my place?”

[030-130]“I’m not sure, Georgie. When I was your age I was like you in many

[030-131]ways, especially in not being very cool-headed, so I can’t say. Youth

[030-132]can’t be trusted for much, except asserting itself and fighting and

[030-133]making love.”

[030-134]“Indeed!” George snorted. “May I ask what you think I ought to have

[030-135]done?”

[030-136]“Nothing.”

[030-137]“‘Nothing?’” George echoed, mocking bitterly “I suppose you think I

[030-138]mean to let my mother’s good name—”

[030-139]“Your mother’s good name!” Amberson cut him off impatiently. “Nobody

[030-140]has a good name in a bad mouth. Nobody has a good name in a silly

[030-141]mouth, either. Well, your mother’s name was in some silly mouths, and

[030-142]all you’ve done was to go and have a scene with the worst old woman

[030-143]gossip in the town—a scene that’s going to make her into a partisan

[030-144]against your mother, whereas she was a mere prattler before. Don’t you

[030-145]suppose she’ll be all over town with this to-morrow? To-morrow? Why,

[030-146]she’ll have her telephone going to-night as long as any of her friends

[030-147]are up! People that never heard anything about this are going to hear

[030-148]it all now, with embellishments. And she’ll see to it that everybody

[030-149]who’s hinted anything about poor Isabel will know that you’re on the

[030-150]warpath; and that will put them on the defensive and make them vicious.

[030-151]The story will grow as it spreads and—”

[030-152]George unfolded his arms to strike his right fist into his left palm.

[030-153]“But do you suppose I’m going to tolerate such things?” he shouted.

[030-154]“What do you suppose I’ll be doing?”

[030-155]“Nothing helpful.”

[030-156]“Oh, you think so, do you?”

[030-157]“You can do absolutely nothing,” said Amberson. “Nothing of any use.

[030-158]The more you do the more harm you’ll do.”

[030-159]“You’ll see! I’m going to stop this thing if I have to force my way

[030-160]into every house on [[national-avenue|National Avenue]] and [[amberson-boulevard|Amberson Boulevard]]!”

[030-161]His uncle laughed rather sourly, but made no other comment.

[030-162]“Well, what do you propose to do?” George demanded. “Do you propose to

[030-163]sit there—”

[030-164]“Yes.”

[030-165]“—and let this riffraff bandy my mother’s good name back and forth

[030-166]among them? Is that what you propose to do?”

[030-167]“It’s all I can do,” Amberson returned. “It’s all any of us can do now:

[030-168]just sit still and hope that the thing may die down in time, in spite

[030-169]of your stirring up that awful old woman.”

[030-170]George drew a long breath, then advanced and stood close before his

[030-171]uncle. “Didn’t you understand me when I told you that people are saying

[030-172]my mother means to marry this man?”

[030-173]“Yes, I understood you.”

[030-174]“You say that my going over there has made matters worse,” George went

[030-175]on. “How about it if such a—such an unspeakable marriage did take

[030-176]place? Do you think that would make people believe they’d been wrong in

[030-177]saying—you know what they say.”

[030-178]“No,” said Amberson deliberately; “I don’t believe it would. There’d be

[030-179]more badness in the bad mouths and more silliness in the silly mouths,

[030-180]I dare say. But it wouldn’t hurt Isabel and [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]], if they never heard

[030-181]of it; and if they did hear of it, then they could take their choice

[030-182]between placating gossip or living for their own happiness. If they

[030-183]have decided to marry—”

[030-184]George almost staggered. “Good God!” he gasped. “You speak of it

[030-185]calmly!”

[030-186]Amberson looked up at him inquiringly. “Why shouldn’t they marry if

[030-187]they want to?” he asked. “It’s their own affair.”

[030-188]“Why shouldn’t they?” George echoed. “Why shouldn’t they?”

[030-189]“Yes. Why shouldn’t they? I don’t see anything precisely monstrous

[030-190]about two people getting married when they’re both free and care about

[030-191]each other. What’s the matter with their marrying?”
