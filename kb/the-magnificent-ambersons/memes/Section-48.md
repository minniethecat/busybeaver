[048-001]“Oh, love for a year, a week, a day,

[048-002]But alas for the love that lasts always—”

[048-003]Most plainly of all he could see her dancing; and he became articulate

[048-004]in the mourning whisper: “So graceful—oh, so graceful—”

[048-005]All the way to New York it seemed to him that Isabel was near him, and

[048-006]he wrote of her to [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] from his hotel the next night:

[048-007]I saw an account of the accident to [[george-amberson-minafer|George Minafer]]. I’m sorry,

[048-008]though the paper states that it was plainly his own fault. I

[048-009]suppose it may have been as a result of my attention falling upon

[048-010]the item that I thought of his mother a great deal on the way

[048-011]here. It seemed to me that I had never seen her more distinctly

[048-012]or so constantly, but, as you know, thinking of his mother is not

[048-013]very apt to make me admire him! Of course, however, he has my best

[048-014]wishes for his recovery.

[048-015]He posted the letter, and by the morning’s mail he received one from

[048-016]Lucy written a few hours after his departure from home. She enclosed

[048-017]the item he had read on the train.

[048-018]I thought you might not see it.

[048-019]I have seen Miss [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] and she has got him put into a room by himself.

[048-020]Oh, poor Rides-Down-Everything I have been thinking so constantly

[048-021]of his mother and it seemed to me that I have never seen her more

[048-022]distinctly. How lovely she was—and how she loved him!

[048-023]If Lucy had not written this letter [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] might not have done the odd

[048-024]thing he did that day. Nothing could have been more natural than that

[048-025]both he and Lucy should have thought intently of Isabel after reading

[048-026]the account of George’s accident, but the fact that Lucy’s letter

[048-027]had crossed his own made Eugene begin to wonder if a phenomenon of

[048-028]telepathy might not be in question, rather than a chance coincidence.

[048-029]The reference to Isabel in the two letters was almost identical: he and

[048-030]Lucy, it appeared, had been thinking of Isabel at the same time—both

[048-031]said “constantly” thinking of her—and neither had ever “seen her more

[048-032]distinctly.” He remembered these phrases in his own letter accurately.

[048-033]Reflection upon the circumstance stirred a queer spot in Eugene’s

[048-034]brain—he had one. He was an adventurer; if he had lived in the

[048-035]sixteenth century he would have sailed the unknown new seas, but having

[048-036]been born in the latter part of the nineteenth, when geography was a

[048-037]fairly well-settled matter, he had become an explorer in mechanics.

[048-038]But the fact that he was a “hard-headed business man” as well as an

[048-039]adventurer did not keep him from having a queer spot in his brain,

[048-040]because hard-headed business men are as susceptible to such spots as

[048-041]adventurers are. Some of them are secretly troubled when they do not

[048-042]see the new moon over the lucky shoulder; some of them have strange,

[048-043]secret incredulities—they do not believe in geology, for instance; and

[048-044]some of them think they have had supernatural experiences. “Of course

[048-045]there was nothing in it—still it was queer!” they say.

[048-046]Two weeks after Isabel’s death, Eugene had come to New York on urgent

[048-047]business and found that the delayed arrival of a steamer gave him a

[048-048]day with nothing to do. His room at [[amberson-hotel|the hotel]] had become intolerable;

[048-049]outdoors was intolerable; everything was intolerable. It seemed to him

[048-050]that he must see Isabel once more, hear her voice once more; that he

[048-051]must find some way to her, or lose his mind. Under this pressure he

[048-052]had gone, with complete scepticism, to a “trance-medium” of whom he

[048-053]had heard wild accounts from the wife of a business acquaintance. He

[048-054]thought despairingly that at least such an excursion would be “trying

[048-055]to do something!” He remembered the woman’s name; found it in the

[048-056]telephone book, and made an appointment.

[048-057]The experience had been grotesque, and he came away with an encouraging

[048-058]message from his father, who had failed to identify himself

[048-059]satisfactorily, but declared that everything was “on a higher plane”

[048-060]in his present state of being, and that all life was “continuous

[048-061]and progressive.” Mrs. Horner spoke of herself as a “psychic”; but

[048-062]otherwise she seemed oddly unpretentious and matter-of-fact; and Eugene

[048-063]had no doubt at all of her sincerity. He was sure that she was not an

[048-064]intentional fraud, and though he departed in a state of annoyance with

[048-065]himself, he came to the conclusion that if any credulity were played

[048-066]upon by Mrs. Horner’s exhibitions, it was her own.

[048-067]Nevertheless, his queer spot having been stimulated to action by

[048-068]the coincidence of the letters, he went to Mrs. Horner’s after his

[048-069]directors’ meeting today. He used the telephone booth in the directors’

[048-070]room to make the appointment; and he laughed feebly at himself, and

[048-071]wondered what the group of men in that mahogany apartment would think

[048-072]if they knew what he was doing. Mrs. Horner had changed her address,

[048-073]but he found the new one, and somebody purporting to be a niece of hers

[048-074]talked to him and made an appointment for a “sitting” at five o’clock.

[048-075]He was prompt, and the niece, a dull-faced fat girl with a magazine

[048-076]under her arm, admitted him to Mrs. Horner’s apartment, which smelt of

[048-077]camphor; and showed him into a room with gray painted walls, no rug on

[048-078]the floor and no furniture except a table (with nothing on it) and two

[048-079]chairs: one a leather easy-chair and the other a stiff little brute

[048-080]with a wooden seat. There was one window with the shade pulled down to

[048-081]the sill, but the sun was bright outside, and the room had light enough.

[048-082]Mrs. Horner appeared in the doorway, a wan and unenterprising looking

[048-083]woman in brown, with thin hair artificially waved—but not recently—and

[048-084]parted in the middle over a bluish forehead. Her eyes were small and

[048-085]seemed weak, but she recognized the visitor.

[048-086]“Oh, you been here before,” she said, in a thin voice, not unmusical.

[048-087]“I recollect you. Quite a time ago, wa’n’t it?”

[048-088]“Yes, quite a long time.”

[048-089]“I recollect because I recollect you was disappointed. Anyway, you was

[048-090]kind of cross.” She laughed faintly.

[048-091]“I’m sorry if I seemed so,” Eugene said. “Do you happen to have found

[048-092]out my name?”

[048-093]She looked surprised and a little reproachful. “Why, no. I never try

[048-094]to find out people’s name. Why should I? I don’t claim anything for

[048-095]the power; I only know I have it—and some ways it ain’t always such a

[048-096]blessing, neither, I can tell you!”

[048-097]Eugene did not press an investigation of her meaning, but said vaguely,

[048-098]“I suppose not. Shall we—”

[048-099]“All right,” she assented, dropping into the leather chair, with her

[048-100]back to the shaded window. “You better set down, too, I reckon. I hope

[048-101]you’ll get something this time so you won’t feel cross, but I dunno. I

[048-102]can’t never tell what they’ll do. Well—”

[048-103]She sighed, closed her eyes, and was silent, while Eugene, seated in

[048-104]the stiff chair across the table from her, watched her profile, thought

[048-105]himself an idiot, and called himself that and other names. And as the

[048-106]silence continued, and the impassive woman in the easy-chair remained

[048-107]impassive, he began to wonder what had led him to be such a fool. It

[048-108]became clear to him that the similarity of his letter and Lucy’s needed

[048-109]no explanation involving telepathy, and was not even an extraordinary

[048-110]coincidence. What, then, had brought him back to this absurd place

[048-111]and caused him to be watching this absurd woman taking a nap in a

[048-112]chair? In brief: What the devil did he mean by it? He had not the

[048-113]slightest interest in Mrs. Horner’s naps—or in her teeth, which were

[048-114]being slightly revealed by the unconscious parting of her lips, as her

[048-115]breathing became heavier. If the vagaries of his own mind had brought

[048-116]him into such a grotesquerie as this, into what did the vagaries of

[048-117]other men’s minds take them? Confident that he was ordinarily saner

[048-118]than most people, he perceived that since he was capable of doing a

[048-119]thing like this, other men did even more idiotic things, in secret. And

[048-120]he had a fleeting vision of sober-looking bankers and manufacturers and

[048-121]lawyers, well-dressed church-going men, sound citizens—and all as queer

[048-122]as the deuce inside!

[048-123]How long was he going to sit here presiding over this unknown woman’s

[048-124]slumbers? It struck him that to make the picture complete he ought to

[048-125]be shooing flies away from her with a palm-leaf fan.

[048-126]Mrs. Horner’s parted lips closed again abruptly, and became compressed;

[048-127]her shoulders moved a little, then jerked repeatedly; her small

[048-128]chest heaved; she gasped, and the compressed lips relaxed to a

[048-129]slight contortion, then began to move, whispering and bringing forth

[048-130]indistinguishable mutterings.

[048-131]Suddenly she spoke in a loud, husky voice:

[048-132]“Lopa is here!”

[048-133]“Yes,” Eugene said dryly. “That’s what you said last time. I remember

[048-134]‘Lopa.’ She’s your ‘control’ I think you said.”

[048-135]“I’m Lopa,” said the husky voice. “I’m Lopa herself.”

[048-136]“You mean I’m to suppose you’re not Mrs. Horner now?”

[048-137]“Never was Mrs. Horner!” the voice declared, speaking undeniably from

[048-138]Mrs. Horner’s lips—but with such conviction that Eugene, in spite of

[048-139]everything, began to feel himself in the presence of a third party,

[048-140]who was none the less an individual, even though she might be another

[048-141]edition of the apparently somnambulistic Mrs. Horner. “Never was Mrs.

[048-142]Horner or anybody but just Lopa. Guide.”

[048-143]“You mean you’re Mrs. Horner’s guide?” he asked.

[048-144]“Your guide now,” said the voice with emphasis, to which was

[048-145]incongruously added a low laugh. “You came here once before. Lopa

[048-146]remembers.”

[048-147]“Yes—so did Mrs. Horner.”

[048-148]Lopa overlooked his implication, and continued, quickly: “You build.

[048-149]Build things that go. You came here once and old gentleman on this

[048-150]side, he spoke to you. Same old gentleman here now. He tell Lopa he’s

[048-151]your grandfather—no, he says ‘father.’ He’s your father.”

[048-152]“What’s his appearance?”

[048-153]“How?”

[048-154]“What does he look like?”

[048-155]“Very fine! White beard, but not long beard. He says someone else wants

[048-156]to speak to you. See here. Lady. Not his wife, though. No. Very fine

[048-157]lady! Fine lady, fine lady!”

[048-158]“Is it my sister?” Eugene asked.

[048-159]“Sister? No. She is shaking her head. She has pretty brown hair. She is

[048-160]fond of you. She is someone who knows you very well but she is not your

[048-161]sister. She is very anxious to say something to you—very anxious. Very

[048-162]fond of you; very anxious to talk to you. Very glad you came here—oh,

[048-163]very, glad!”

[048-164]“What is her name?”

[048-165]“Name,” the voice repeated, and seemed to ruminate. “Name hard to

[048-166]get—always very hard for Lopa. Name. She wants to tell me her name to

[048-167]tell you. She wants you to understand names are hard to make. She says

[048-168]you must think of something that makes a sound.” Here the voice seemed

[048-169]to put a question to an invisible presence and to receive an answer. “A

[048-170]little sound or a big sound? She says it might be a little sound or a

[048-171]big sound. She says a ring—oh, Lopa knows! She means a bell! That’s it,

[048-172]a bell.”

[048-173]Eugene looked grave. “Does she mean her name is Belle?”

[048-174]“Not quite. Her name is longer.”

[048-175]“Perhaps,” he suggested, “she means that she was a belle.”

[048-176]“No. She says she thinks you know what she means. She says you must

[048-177]think of a colour. What colour?” Again Lopa addressed the unknown, but

[048-178]this time seemed to wait for an answer.

[048-179]“Perhaps she means the colour of her eyes,” said Eugene.

[048-180]“No. She says her colour is light—it’s a light colour and you can see

[048-181]through it.”

[048-182]“Amber?” he said, and was startled, for Mrs. Horner, with her eyes

[048-183]still closed, clapped her hands, and the voice cried out in delight:

[048-184]“Yes! She says you know who she is from amber. Amber! Amber! That’s it!

[048-185]She says you understand what her name is from a bell and from amber.

[048-186]She is laughing and waving a lace handkerchief at me because she is

[048-187]pleased. She says I have made you know who it is.”
