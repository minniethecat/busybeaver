# Reverend Malloch Smith

The Reverend Malloch Smith was a Methodist minister in Midland whose front yard became the scene of one of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]'s most notorious childhood exploits. Described as "that grim-bearded Methodist," he was an unwilling participant in an episode that would be told and retold throughout the town for years afterward, contributing significantly to George's fearsome reputation (003-143).

## The Famous Fight

The incident began on a summer afternoon when a strange boy, sitting bored upon the gate-post of the Reverend Malloch Smith, beheld George Amberson Minafer rapidly approaching on his white pony (003-125). The boy shouted an insult, and George immediately dismounted to answer the challenge. The visiting boy — the Reverend's nephew — descended from the gate-post but remained inside the gate, daring George to come in after him (003-137). George vaulted the fence at once, and the fight commenced on the minister's own lawn.

Mrs. Malloch Smith heard the strange noises and looked forth from a window before dashing for the pastor's study. The Reverend emerged to find his nephew being thoroughly thrashed by the young Amberson heir (003-143). The minister intervened — "after a grotesque tussle" — and managed to separate the combatants, but the affair was not yet over (003-148).

## "You Pull Down Your Vest, You Ole Billygoat"

Before remounting his pony and riding off at his accustomed gallop, George paused to deliver a parting shot at the minister himself: "You pull down your vest, you ole Billygoat, you!" (003-161). This astonishing piece of precocity — a boy of eleven addressing a clergyman in such terms — became the talk of the town and confirmed everything that was said about the young heir's imperious character.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The Reverend Malloch Smith was left in a state of excitement by the encounter. As the narrator observes, "it was a new experience for the Reverend Malloch Smith, and left him in a state of excitement" (003-165). He at once wrote a note to George's mother describing the crime, though it appears to have done little to curb George's behavior. The story of the fight "became a town legend" and was told at the [[the-last-ball|Last Ball]] and other social gatherings (004-080). Rather than diminishing George's standing, the Malloch Smith story only increased his notoriety, for the narrator notes that "inevitably attend a boy who has told a minister to go to hell" (004-109).
