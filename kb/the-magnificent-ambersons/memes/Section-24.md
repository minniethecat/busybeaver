[024-001]“Oh, not for years! So there’s the answer, and let’s trot again.”

[024-002]But George was persistent; moreover, he had become serious during the

[024-003]last minute or two. “I want to know,” he said. “I really mean it.”

[024-004]“Let’s don’t be serious, George,” she begged him hopefully. “Let’s talk

[024-005]of something pleasant.”

[024-006]He was a little offended. “Then it isn’t pleasant for you to know that

[024-007]I want to marry you?”

[024-008]At this she became as serious as he could have asked; she looked down,

[024-009]and her lip quivered like that of a child about to cry. Suddenly she

[024-010]put her hand upon one of his for just an instant, and then withdrew it.

[024-011]“[[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]!” he said huskily. “Dear, what’s the matter? You look as if

[024-012]you were going to cry. You always do that,” he went on plaintively,

[024-013]“whenever I can get you to talk about marrying me.”

[024-014]“I know it,” she murmured.

[024-015]“Well, why do you?”

[024-016]Her eyelids flickered, and then she looked up at him with a sad

[024-017]gravity, tears seeming just at the poise. “One reason’s because I have

[024-018]a feeling that it’s never going to be.”

[024-019]“Why?”

[024-020]“It’s just a feeling.”

[024-021]“You haven’t any reason or—”

[024-022]“It’s just a feeling.”

[024-023]“Well, if that’s all,” George said, reassured, and laughing

[024-024]confidently, “I guess I won’t be very much troubled!” But at once he

[024-025]became serious again, adopting the tone of argument. “Lucy, how is

[024-026]anything ever going to get a chance to come of it, so long as you keep

[024-027]sticking to ‘almost’? Doesn’t it strike you as unreasonable to have a

[024-028]‘feeling’ that we’ll never be married, when what principally stands

[024-029]between us is the fact that you won’t be really engaged to me? That

[024-030]does seem pretty absurd! Don’t you care enough about me to marry me?”

[024-031]She looked down again, pathetically troubled. “Yes.”

[024-032]“Won’t you always care that much about me?”

[024-033]“I’m—yes—I’m afraid so, George. I never do change much about anything.”

[024-034]“Well, then, why in the world won’t you drop the ‘almost’?”

[024-035]Her distress increased. “Everything is—everything—”

[024-036]“What about ‘everything’?”

[024-037]“Everything is so—so unsettled.”

[024-038]And at that he uttered an exclamation of impatience. “If you aren’t the

[024-039]queerest girl! What is ‘unsettled’?”

[024-040]“Well, for one thing,” she said, able to smile at his vehemence, “you

[024-041]haven’t settled on anything to do. At least, if you have you’ve never

[024-042]spoken of it.”

[024-043]As she spoke, she gave him the quickest possible side glance of hopeful

[024-044]scrutiny; then looked away, not happily. Surprise and displeasure were

[024-045]intentionally visible upon the countenance of her companion; and he

[024-046]permitted a significant period of silence to elapse before making any

[024-047]response. “Lucy,” he said, finally, with cold dignity, “I should like

[024-048]to ask you a few questions.”

[024-049]“Yes?”

[024-050]“The first is: Haven’t you perfectly well understood that I don’t mean

[024-051]to go into business or adopt a profession?”

[024-052]“I wasn’t quite sure,” she said gently. “I really didn’t know—quite.”

[024-053]“Then of course it’s time I did tell you. I never have been able to

[024-054]see any occasion for a man’s going into trade, or being a lawyer,

[024-055]or any of those things if his position and family were such that he

[024-056]didn’t need to. You know, yourself, there are a lot of people in the

[024-057]East—in the South, too, for that matter—that don’t think we’ve got any

[024-058]particular family or position or culture in this part of the country.

[024-059]I’ve met plenty of that kind of provincial snobs myself, and they’re

[024-060]pretty galling. There were one or two men in my crowd at college,

[024-061]their families had lived on their income for three generations, and

[024-062]they never dreamed there was anybody in their class out here. I had to

[024-063]show them a thing or two, right at the start, and I guess they won’t

[024-064]forget it! Well, I think it’s time all their sort found out that three

[024-065]generations can mean just as much out here as anywhere else. That’s the

[024-066]way I feel about it, and let me tell you I feel it pretty deeply!”

[024-067]“But what are you going to do, George?” she cried.

[024-068]George’s earnestness surpassed hers; he had become flushed and his

[024-069]breathing was emotional. As he confessed, with simple genuineness, he

[024-070]did feel what he was saying “pretty deeply”; and in truth his state

[024-071]approached the tremulous. “I expect to live an honourable life,” he

[024-072]said. “I expect to contribute my share to charities, and to take part

[024-073]in—in movements.”

[024-074]“What kind?”

[024-075]“Whatever appeals to me,” he said.

[024-076]Lucy looked at him with grieved wonder. “But you really don’t mean to

[024-077]have any regular business or profession at all?”

[024-078]“I certainly do not!” George returned promptly and emphatically.

[024-079]“I was afraid so,” she said in a low voice.

[024-080]George continued to breathe deeply throughout another protracted

[024-081]interval of silence. Then he said, “I should like to revert to the

[024-082]questions I was asking you, if you don’t mind.”

[024-083]“No, George. I think we’d better—”

[024-084]“Your father is a business man—”

[024-085]“He’s a mechanical genius,” Lucy interrupted quickly. “Of course he’s

[024-086]both. And he was a lawyer once—he’s done all sorts of things.”

[024-087]“Very well. I merely wished to ask if it’s his influence that makes you

[024-088]think I ought to ‘do’ something?”

[024-089]Lucy frowned slightly. “Why, I suppose almost everything I think or say

[024-090]must be owing to his influence in one way or another. We haven’t had

[024-091]anybody but each other for so many years, and we always think about

[024-092]alike, so of course—”

[024-093]“I see!” And George’s brow darkened with resentment. “So that’s it, is

[024-094]it? It’s your father’s idea that I ought to go into business and that

[024-095]you oughtn’t to be engaged to me until I do.”

[024-096]Lucy gave a start, her denial was so quick. “No! I’ve never once spoken

[024-097]to him about it. Never!”

[024-098]George looked at her keenly, and he jumped to a conclusion not far from

[024-099]the truth. “But you know without talking to him that it’s the way he

[024-100]does feel about it? I see.”

[024-101]She nodded gravely. “Yes.”

[024-102]George’s brow grew darker still. “Do you think I’d be much of a man,”

[024-103]he said, slowly, “if I let any other man dictate to me my own way of

[024-104]life?”

[024-105]“George! Who’s ‘dictating’ your—”

[024-106]“It seems to me it amounts to that!” he returned.

[024-107]“Oh, no! I only know how papa thinks about things. He’s never, never

[024-108]spoken unkindly, or ‘dictatingly’ of you.” She lifted her hand in

[024-109]protest, and her face was so touching in its distress that for the

[024-110]moment George forgot his anger. He seized that small, troubled hand.

[024-111]“Lucy,” he said huskily. “Don’t you know that I love you?”

[024-112]“Yes—I do.”

[024-113]“Don’t you love me?”

[024-114]“Yes—I do.”

[024-115]“Then what does it matter what your father thinks about my doing

[024-116]something or not doing anything? He has his way, and I have mine. I

[024-117]don’t believe in the whole world scrubbing dishes and selling potatoes

[024-118]and trying law cases. Why, look at your father’s best friend, my Uncle

[024-119][[george-amberson|George Amberson]]—he’s never done anything in his life, and—”

[024-120]“Oh, yes, he has,” she interrupted. “He was in politics.”

[024-121]“Well, I’m glad he’s out,” George said. “Politics is a dirty business

[024-122]for a gentleman, and Uncle George would tell you that himself. Lucy,

[024-123]let’s not talk any more about it. Let me tell mother when I get home

[024-124]that we’re engaged. Won’t you, dear?”

[024-125]She shook her head.

[024-126]“Is it because—”

[024-127]For a fleeting instant she touched to her cheek the hand that held

[024-128]hers. “No,” she said, and gave him a sudden little look of renewed

[024-129]gayety. “Let’s let it stay ‘almost’.”

[024-130]“Because your father—”

[024-131]“Oh, because it’s better!”

[024-132]George’s voice shook. “Isn’t it your father?”

[024-133]“It’s his ideals I’m thinking of—yes.”

[024-134]George dropped her hand abruptly and anger narrowed his eyes. “I know

[024-135]what you mean,” he said. “I dare say I don’t care for your father’s

[024-136]ideals any more than he does for mine!”

[024-137]He tightened the reins, Pendennis quickening eagerly to the trot; and

[024-138]when George jumped out of the runabout before Lucy’s gate, and assisted

[024-139]her to descend, the silence in which they parted was the same that had

[024-140]begun when Pendennis began to trot.

Chapter XVIII

[024-141]That evening, after dinner, George sat with his mother and his Aunt

[024-142][[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] upon the veranda. In former summers, when they sat outdoors in

[024-143]the evening, they had customarily used an open terrace at the side of

[024-144]the house, looking toward the Major’s, but that more private retreat

[024-145]now afforded too blank and abrupt a view of the nearest of the new

[024-146]houses; so, without consultation, they had abandoned it for the

[024-147]Romanesque stone structure in front, an oppressive place.

[024-148]Its oppression seemed congenial to George; he sat upon the copestone of

[024-149]the stone parapet, his back against a stone pilaster; his attitude not

[024-150]comfortable, but rigid, and his silence not comfortable, either, but

[024-151]heavy. However, to the eyes of his mother and his aunt, who occupied

[024-152]wicker chairs at a little distance, he was almost indistinguishable

[024-153]except for the stiff white shield of his evening frontage.

[024-154]“It’s so nice of you always to dress in the evening, Georgie,” his

[024-155]mother said, her glance resting upon this surface. “Your Uncle George

[024-156]always used to, and so did father, for years; but they both stopped

[024-157]quite a long time ago. Unless there’s some special occasion, it seems

[024-158]to me we don’t see it done any more, except on the stage and in the

[024-159]magazines.”

[024-160]He made no response, and Isabel, after waiting a little while, as if

[024-161]she expected one, appeared to acquiesce in his mood for silence, and

[024-162]turned her head to gaze thoughtfully out at the street.

[024-163]There, in the highway, the evening life of [[downtown-midland|the Midland city]] had begun.

[024-164]A rising moon was bright upon the tops of the shade trees, where their

[024-165]branches met overhead, arching across the street, but only filtered

[024-166]splashings of moonlight reached the block pavement below; and through

[024-167]this darkness flashed the firefly lights of silent bicycles gliding by

[024-168]in pairs and trios—or sometimes a dozen at a time might come, and not

[024-169]so silent, striking their little bells; the riders’ voices calling and

[024-170]laughing; while now and then a pair of invisible experts would pass,

[024-171]playing mandolin and guitar as if handle-bars were of no account in the

[024-172]world—their music would come swiftly, and then too swiftly die away.

[024-173]Surreys rumbled lightly by, with the plod-plod of honest old horses,

[024-174]and frequently there was the glitter of whizzing spokes from a runabout

[024-175]or a sporting buggy, and the sharp, decisive hoof-beats of a trotter.

[024-176]Then, like a cowboy shooting up a peaceful camp, a frantic devil would

[024-177]hurtle out of the distance, bellowing, exhaust racketing like a machine

[024-178]gun gone amuck—and at these horrid sounds the surreys and buggies would

[024-179]hug the curbstone, and the bicycles scatter to cover, cursing; while

[024-180]children rushed from the sidewalks to drag pet dogs from the street.

[024-181]The thing would roar by, leaving a long wake of turbulence; then the

[024-182]indignant street would quiet down for a few minutes—till another came.

[024-183]“There are a great many more than there used to be,” Miss Fanny

[024-184]observed, in her lifeless voice, as the lull fell after one of these

[024-185]visitations. “[[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] is right about that; there seem to be at least

[024-186]three or four times as many as there were last summer, and you never

[024-187]hear the ragamuffins shouting ‘Get a horse!’ nowadays; but I think he

[024-188]may be mistaken about their going on increasing after this. I don’t

[024-189]believe we’ll see so many next summer as we do now.”

[024-190]“Why?” asked Isabel.

[024-191]“Because I’ve begun to agree with George about their being more a fad

[024-192]than anything else, and I think it must be the height of the fad just

[024-193]now. You know how roller-skating came in—everybody in the world seemed

[024-194]to be crowding to the rinks—and now only a few children use rollers for

[024-195]getting to school. Besides, people won’t permit the automobiles to be

[024-196]used. Really, I think they’ll make laws against them. You see how they

[024-197]spoil the bicycling and the driving; people just seem to hate them!

[024-198]They’ll never stand it—never in the world! Of course I’d be sorry to

[024-199]see such a thing happen to Eugene, but I shouldn’t be really surprised

[024-200]to see a law passed forbidding the sale of automobiles, just the way

[024-201]there is with concealed weapons.”
