[026-001][[major-amberson|The Major]] was skeptical. “Dream on, fair son!” he said. “It’s lucky for

[026-002]us that you’re only dreaming; because if people go to moving that far,

[026-003]real estate values in the old residence part of town are going to be

[026-004]stretched pretty thin.”

[026-005]“I’m afraid so,” [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] assented. “Unless you keep things so bright and

[026-006]clean that the old section will stay more attractive than the new ones.”

[026-007]“Not very likely! How are things going to be kept ‘bright and clean’

[026-008]with soft coal, and our kind of city government?”

[026-009]“They aren’t,” Eugene replied quickly. “There’s no hope of it, and

[026-010]already the boarding-house is marching up [[national-avenue|National Avenue]]. There

[026-011]are two in the next block below here, and there are a dozen in the

[026-012]half-mile below that. My relatives, the Sharons, have sold their house

[026-013]and are building in the country—at least, they call it ‘the country.’

[026-014]It will be city in two or three years.”

[026-015]“Good gracious!” the Major exclaimed, affecting dismay. “So your little

[026-016]shops are going to ruin all your old friends, Eugene!”

[026-017]“Unless my old friends take warning in time, or abolish smoke and get

[026-018]a new kind of city government. I should say the best chance is to take

[026-019]warning.”

[026-020]“Well, well!” the Major laughed. “You have enough faith in miracles,

[026-021]Eugene—granting that trolleys and bicycles and automobiles are

[026-022]miracles. So you think they’re to change the face of the land, do you?”

[026-023]“They’re already doing it, Major; and it can’t be stopped. Automobiles—”

[026-024]At this point he was interrupted. George was the interrupter. He had

[026-025]said nothing since entering the dining room, but now he spoke in a loud

[026-026]and peremptory voice, using the tone of one in authority who checks

[026-027]idle prattle and settles a matter forever.

[026-028]“Automobiles are a useless nuisance,” he said.

[026-029]There fell a moment’s silence.

[026-030]Isabel gazed incredulously at George, colour slowly heightening upon

[026-031]her cheeks and temples, while [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] watched him with a quick eagerness,

[026-032]her eyes alert and bright. But Eugene seemed merely quizzical, as

[026-033]if not taking this brusquerie to himself. The Major was seriously

[026-034]disturbed.

[026-035]“What did you say, George?” he asked, though George had spoken but too

[026-036]distinctly.

[026-037]“I said all automobiles were a nuisance,” George answered, repeating

[026-038]not only the words but the tone in which he had uttered them. And he

[026-039]added, “They’ll never amount to anything but a nuisance. They had no

[026-040]business to be invented.”

[026-041]The Major frowned. “Of course you forget that Mr. Morgan makes them,

[026-042]and also did his share in inventing them. If you weren’t so thoughtless

[026-043]he might think you rather offensive.”

[026-044]“That would be too bad,” said George coolly. “I don’t think I could

[026-045]survive it.”

[026-046]Again there was a silence, while the Major stared at his grandson,

[026-047]aghast. But Eugene began to laugh cheerfully.

[026-048]“I’m not sure he’s wrong about automobiles,” he said. “With all their

[026-049]speed forward they may be a step backward in civilization—that is,

[026-050]in spiritual civilization. It may be that they will not add to the

[026-051]beauty of the world, nor to the life of men’s souls. I am not sure.

[026-052]But automobiles have come, and they bring a greater change in our life

[026-053]than most of us suspect. They are here, and almost all outward things

[026-054]are going to be different because of what they bring. They are going to

[026-055]alter war, and they are going to alter peace. I think men’s minds are

[026-056]going to be changed in subtle ways because of automobiles; just how,

[026-057]though, I could hardly guess. But you can’t have the immense outward

[026-058]changes that they will cause without some inward ones, and it may be

[026-059]that George is right, and that the spiritual alteration will be bad for

[026-060]us. Perhaps, ten or twenty years from now, if we can see the inward

[026-061]change in men by that time, I shouldn’t be able to defend the gasoline

[026-062]engine, but would have to agree with him that automobiles ‘had no

[026-063]business to be invented.’” He laughed good-naturedly, and looking at

[026-064]his watch, apologized for having an engagement which made his departure

[026-065]necessary when he would so much prefer to linger. Then he shook

[026-066]hands with the Major, and bade Isabel, George, and Fanny a cheerful

[026-067]good-night—a collective farewell cordially addressed to all three of

[026-068]them together—and left them at the table.

[026-069]Isabel turned wondering, hurt eyes upon her son. “George, dear!” she

[026-070]said. “What did you mean?”

[026-071]“Just what I said,” he returned, lighting one of the Major’s cigars,

[026-072]and his manner was imperturbable enough to warrant the definition

[026-073](sometimes merited by imperturbability) of stubbornness.

[026-074]Isabel’s hand, pale and slender, upon the tablecloth, touched one

[026-075]of the fine silver candlesticks aimlessly: the fingers were seen to

[026-076]tremble. “Oh, he was hurt!” she murmured.

[026-077]“I don’t see why he should be,” George said. “I didn’t say anything

[026-078]about him. He didn’t seem to me to be hurt—seemed perfectly cheerful.

[026-079]What made you think he was hurt?”

[026-080]“I know him!” was all of her reply, half whispered.

[026-081]The Major stared hard at George from under his white eyebrows. “You

[026-082]didn’t mean ‘him,’ you say, George? I suppose if we had a clergyman as

[026-083]a guest here you’d expect him not to be offended, and to understand

[026-084]that your remarks were neither personal nor untactful, if you said the

[026-085]church was a nuisance and ought never to have been invented. By Jove,

[026-086]but you’re a puzzle!”

[026-087]“In what way, may I ask, sir?”

[026-088]“We seem to have a new kind of young people these days,” the old

[026-089]gentleman returned, shaking his head. “It’s a new style of courting

[026-090]a pretty girl, certainly, for a young fellow to go deliberately out

[026-091]of his way to try and make an enemy of her father by attacking his

[026-092]business! By Jove! That’s a new way to win a woman!”

[026-093]George flushed angrily and seemed about to offer a retort, but held

[026-094]his breath for a moment; and then held his peace. It was Isabel who

[026-095]responded to the Major. “Oh, no!” she said. “Eugene would never be

[026-096]anybody’s enemy—he couldn’t!—and last of all Georgie’s. I’m afraid he

[026-097]was hurt, but I don’t fear his not having understood that George spoke

[026-098]without thinking of what he was saying—I mean, with-out realizing its

[026-099]bearing on Eugene.”

[026-100]Again George seemed upon the point of speech, and again controlled the

[026-101]impulse. He thrust his hands in his pockets, leaned back in his chair,

[026-102]and smoked, staring inflexibly at the ceiling.

[026-103]“Well, well,” said his grandfather, rising. “It wasn’t a very

[026-104]successful little dinner!”

[026-105]Thereupon he offered his arm to his daughter, who took it fondly, and

[026-106]they left the room, Isabel assuring him that all his little dinners

[026-107]were pleasant, and that this one was no exception.

[026-108]George did not move, and Fanny, following the other two, came round the

[026-109]table, and paused close beside his chair; but George remained posed in

[026-110]his great imperturbability, cigar between teeth, eyes upon ceiling, and

[026-111]paid no attention to her. Fanny waited until the sound of Isabel’s and

[026-112]the Major’s voices became inaudible in the hall. Then she said quickly,

[026-113]and in a low voice so eager that it was unsteady:

[026-114]“George, you’ve struck just the treatment to adopt: you’re doing the

[026-115]right thing!”

[026-116]She hurried out, scurrying after the others with a faint rustling

[026-117]of her black skirts, leaving George mystified but incurious. He did

[026-118]not understand why she should bestow her approbation upon him in the

[026-119]matter, and cared so little whether she did or not that he spared

[026-120]himself even the trouble of being puzzled about it.

[026-121]In truth, however, he was neither so comfortable nor so imperturbable

[026-122]as he appeared. He felt some gratification: he had done a little to

[026-123]put the man in his place—that man whose influence upon his daughter

[026-124]was precisely the same thing as a contemptuous criticism of George

[026-125]Amberson Minafer, and of [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]]’s “ideals of life.”

[026-126][[lucy-morgan|Lucy]]’s going away without a word was intended, he supposed, as a bit of

[026-127]punishment. Well, he wasn’t the sort of man that people were allowed to

[026-128]punish: he could demonstrate that to them—since they started it!

[026-129]It appeared to him as almost a kind of insolence, this abrupt

[026-130]departure—not even telephoning! Probably she wondered how he would take

[026-131]it; she even might have supposed he would show some betraying chagrin

[026-132]when he heard of it.

[026-133]He had no idea that this was just what he had shown; and he was

[026-134]satisfied with his evening’s performance. Nevertheless, he was not

[026-135]comfortable in his mind; though he could not have explained his inward

[026-136]perturbations, for he was convinced, without any confirmation from his

[026-137]Aunt Fanny, that he had done “just the right thing.”

Chapter XX

[026-138]Isabel came to George’s door that night, and when she had kissed him

[026-139]good-night she remained in the open doorway with her hand upon his

[026-140]shoulder and her eyes thoughtfully lowered, so that her wish to say

[026-141]something more than good-night was evident. Not less obvious was her

[026-142]perplexity about the manner of saying it; and George, divining her

[026-143]thought, amiably made an opening for her.

[026-144]“Well, old lady,” he said indulgently, “you needn’t look so worried. I

[026-145]won’t be tactless with Morgan again. After this I’ll just keep out of

[026-146]his way.”

[026-147]Isabel looked up, searching his face with the fond puzzlement which her

[026-148]eyes sometimes showed when they rested upon him; then she glanced down

[026-149]the hall toward Fanny’s room, and, after another moment of hesitation,

[026-150]came quickly in, and closed the door.

[026-151]“Dear,” she said, “I wish you’d tell me something: Why don’t you like

[026-152]Eugene?”

[026-153]“Oh, I like him well enough,” George returned, with a short laugh, as

[026-154]he sat down and began to unlace his shoes. “I like him well enough—in

[026-155]his place.”

[026-156]“No, dear,” she said hurriedly. “I’ve had a feeling from the very

[026-157]first that you didn’t really like him—that you really never liked him.

[026-158]Sometimes you’ve seemed to be friendly with him, and you’d laugh with

[026-159]him over something in a jolly, companionable way, and I’d think I was

[026-160]wrong, and that you really did like him, after all; but to-night I’m

[026-161]sure my other feeling was the right one: you don’t like him. I can’t

[026-162]understand it, dear; I don’t see what can be the matter.”

[026-163]“Nothing’s the matter.”

[026-164]This easy declaration naturally failed to carry great weight, and

[026-165]Isabel went on, in her troubled voice, “It seems so queer, especially

[026-166]when you feel as you do about his daughter.”

[026-167]At this, George stopped unlacing his shoes abruptly, and sat up. “How

[026-168]do I feel about his daughter?” he demanded.

[026-169]“Well, it’s seemed—as if—as if—” Isabel began timidly. “It did seem—At

[026-170]least, you haven’t looked at any other girl, ever since they came here

[026-171]and—and certainly you’ve seemed very much interested in her. Certainly

[026-172]you’ve been very great friends?”

[026-173]“Well, what of that?”

[026-174]“It’s only that I’m like your grandfather: I can’t see how you could be

[026-175]so much interested in a girl and—and not feel very pleasantly toward

[026-176]her father.”

[026-177]“Well, I’ll tell you something,” George said slowly; and a frown of

[026-178]concentration could be seen upon his brow, as from a profound effort

[026-179]at self-examination. “I haven’t ever thought much on that particular

[026-180]point, but I admit there may be a little something in what you say.

[026-181]The truth is, I don’t believe I’ve ever thought of the two together,

[026-182]exactly—at least, not until lately. I’ve always thought of Lucy just

[026-183]as Lucy, and of Morgan just as Morgan. I’ve always thought of her as

[026-184]a person herself, not as anybody’s daughter. I don’t see what’s very

[026-185]extraordinary about that. You’ve probably got plenty of friends, for

[026-186]instance, that don’t care much about your son—”

[026-187]“No, indeed!” she protested quickly. “And if I knew anybody who felt

[026-188]like that, I wouldn’t—”

[026-189]“Never mind,” he interrupted. “I’ll try to explain a little more. If

[026-190]I have a friend, I don’t see that it’s incumbent upon me to like that

[026-191]friend’s relatives. If I didn’t like them, and pretended to, I’d be a

[026-192]hypocrite. If that friend likes me and wants to stay my friend he’ll

[026-193]have to stand my not liking his relatives, or else he can quit. I

[026-194]decline to be a hypocrite about it; that’s all. Now, suppose I have

[026-195]certain ideas or ideals which I have chosen for the regulation of my

[026-196]own conduct in life. Suppose some friend of mine has a relative with

[026-197]ideals directly the opposite of mine, and my friend believes more in

[026-198]the relative’s ideals than in mine: Do you think I ought to give up

[026-199]my own just to please a person who’s taken up ideals that I really

[026-200]despise?”
