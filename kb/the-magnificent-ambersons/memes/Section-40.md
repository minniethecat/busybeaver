[040-001]Miss [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] looked startled. “You think—”

[040-002]“She told me so!” And the tortured young man choked. “She said—‘just

[040-003]once.’ She said ‘I’d like to have seen him—just once!’ She meant—to

[040-004]tell him good-bye! That’s what she meant! And you put this on me, too;

[040-005]you put this responsibility on me! But I tell you, and I told Uncle

[040-006]George, that the responsibility isn’t all mine! If you were so sure I

[040-007]was wrong all the time—when I took her away, and when I turned Morgan

[040-008]out—if you were so sure, what did you let me do it for? You and Uncle

[040-009]George were grown people, both of you, weren’t you? You were older than

[040-010]I, and if you were so sure you were wiser than I, why did you just

[040-011]stand around with your hands hanging down, and let me go ahead? You

[040-012]could have stopped it if it was wrong, couldn’t you?”

[040-013]Fanny shook her head. “No, George,” she said slowly. “Nobody could have

[040-014]stopped you. You were too strong, and—”

[040-015]“And what?” he demanded loudly.

[040-016]“And she loved you—too well.”

[040-017]George stared at her hard, then his lower lip began to move

[040-018]convulsively, and he set his teeth upon it but could not check its

[040-019]frantic twitching.

[040-020]He ran out of the room.

[040-021]She sat still, listening. He had plunged into his mother’s room, but

[040-022]no sound came to Fanny’s ears after the sharp closing of the door;

[040-023]and presently she rose and stepped out into the hall—but could hear

[040-024]nothing. The heavy black walnut door of Isabel’s room, as Fanny’s

[040-025]troubled eyes remained fixed upon it, seemed to become darker and

[040-026]vaguer; the polished wood took the distant ceiling light, at the

[040-027]end of the hall, in dim reflections which became mysterious; and to

[040-028]Fanny’s disturbed mind the single sharp point of light on the bronze

[040-029]door-knob was like a continuous sharp cry in the stillness of night.

[040-030]What interview was sealed away from human eye and ear within the lonely

[040-031]darkness on the other side of that door—in that darkness where Isabel’s

[040-032]own special chairs were, and her own special books, and the two great

[040-033]walnut wardrobes filled with her dresses and wraps? What tragic

[040-034]argument might be there vainly striving to confute the gentle dead? “In

[040-035]God’s name, what else could I have done?” For his mother’s immutable

[040-036]silence was surely answering him as Isabel in life would never have

[040-037]answered him, and he was beginning to understand how eloquent the dead

[040-038]can be. They cannot stop their eloquence, no matter how they have loved

[040-039]the living: they cannot choose. And so, no matter in what agony George

[040-040]should cry out, “What else could I have done?” and to the end of his

[040-041]life no matter how often he made that wild appeal, Isabel was doomed to

[040-042]answer him with the wistful, faint murmur:

[040-043]“I’d like to have—seen him. Just—just once.”

[040-044]A cheerful darkey went by the house, loudly and tunelessly whistling

[040-045]some broken thoughts upon women, fried food and gin; then a group of

[040-046]high school boys, returning homeward after important initiations, were

[040-047]heard skylarking along the sidewalk, rattling sticks on the fences,

[040-048]squawking hoarsely, and even attempting to sing in the shocking new

[040-049]voices of uncompleted adolescence. For no reason, and just as a poultry

[040-050]yard falls into causeless agitation, they stopped in front of the

[040-051]house, and for half an hour produced the effect of a noisy multitude in

[040-052]full riot.

[040-053]To the woman standing upstairs in the hall, this was almost unbearable;

[040-054]and she felt that she would have to go down and call to them to stop;

[040-055]but she was too timid, and after a time went back to her room, and sat

[040-056]at her desk again. She left the door open, and frequently glanced out

[040-057]into the hall, but gradually became once more absorbed in the figures

[040-058]which represented her prospective income from her great plunge in

[040-059]electric lights for automobiles. She did not hear George return to his

[040-060]own room.

[040-061]A superstitious person might have thought it unfortunate that her

[040-062]partner in this speculative industry (as in Wilbur’s disastrous

[040-063]rolling-mills) was that charming but too haphazardous man of the world,

[040-064][[george-amberson|George Amberson]]. He was one of those optimists who believe that if you

[040-065]put [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] into a great many enterprises one of them is sure to turn out

[040-066]a fortune, and therefore, in order to find the lucky one, it is only

[040-067]necessary to go into a large enough number of them. Altogether gallant

[040-068]in spirit, and beautifully game under catastrophe, he had gone into a

[040-069]great many, and the unanimity of their “bad luck,” as he called it,

[040-070]gave him one claim to be a distinguished person, if he had no other. In

[040-071]business he was ill fated with a consistency which made him, in that

[040-072]alone, a remarkable man; and he declared, with some earnestness, that

[040-073]there was no accounting for it except by the fact that there had been

[040-074]so much good luck in his family before he was born that something had

[040-075]to balance it.

[040-076]“You ought to have thought of my record and stayed out,” he told Fanny,

[040-077]one day the next spring, when the affairs of the headlight company had

[040-078]begun to look discouraging. “I feel the old familiar sinking that’s

[040-079]attended all my previous efforts to prove myself a business genius.

[040-080]I think it must be something like the feeling an aeronaut has when

[040-081]his balloon bursts, and, looking down, he sees below him the old home

[040-082]farm where he used to live—I mean the feeling he’d have just before he

[040-083]flattened out in that same old clay barnyard. Things do look bleak, and

[040-084]I’m only glad you didn’t go into this confounded thing to the extent I

[040-085]did.”

[040-086]Miss Fanny grew pink. “But it must go right!” she protested. “We saw

[040-087]with our own eyes how perfectly it worked in the shop. The light was so

[040-088]bright no one could face it, and so there can’t be any reason for it

[040-089]not to work. It simply—”

[040-090]“Oh, you’re right about that,” Amberson said. “It certainly was a

[040-091]perfect thing—in the shop! The only thing we didn’t know was how fast

[040-092]an automobile had to go to keep the light going. It appears that this

[040-093]was a matter of some importance.”

[040-094]“Well, how fast does one have to—”

[040-095]“To keep the light from going entirely out,” he informed her with

[040-096]elaborate deliberation, “it is computed by those enthusiasts who have

[040-097]bought our product—and subsequently returned it to us and got their

[040-098]money back—they compute that a [[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons|motor car]] must maintain a speed of

[040-099]twenty-five miles an hour, or else there won’t be any light at all. To

[040-100]make the illumination bright enough to be noticed by an approaching

[040-101]automobile, they state the speed must be more than thirty miles an

[040-102]hour. At thirty-five, objects in the path of the light begin to become

[040-103]visible; at forty they are revealed distinctly; and at fifty and above

[040-104]we have a real headlight. Unfortunately many people don’t care to drive

[040-105]that fast at all times after dusk, especially in the traffic, or where

[040-106]policemen are likely to become objectionable.”

[040-107]“But think of that test on the road when we—”

[040-108]“That test was lovely,” he admitted. “The inventor made us happy with

[040-109]his oratory, and you and Frank Bronson and I went whirling through the

[040-110]night at a speed that thrilled us. It was an intoxicating sensation: we

[040-111]were intoxicated by the lights, the lights and the music. We must never

[040-112]forget that drive, with the cool wind kissing our cheeks and the road

[040-113]lit up for miles ahead. We must never forget it and we never shall. It

[040-114]cost—”

[040-115]“But something’s got to be done.”

[040-116]“It has, indeed! My something would seem to be leaving my watch at my

[040-117]uncle’s. Luckily, you—”

[040-118]The pink of Fanny’s cheeks became deeper. “But isn’t that man going to

[040-119]do anything to remedy it? can’t he try to—”

[040-120]“He can try,” said Amberson. “He is trying, in fact. I’ve sat in the

[040-121]shop watching him try for several beautiful afternoons, while outside

[040-122]the windows all Nature was fragrant with spring and smoke. He hums

[040-123]ragtime to himself as he tries, and I think his mind is wandering to

[040-124]something else less tedious—to some new invention in which he’d take

[040-125]more interest.”

[040-126]“But you mustn’t let him,” she cried. “You must make him keep on

[040-127]trying!”

[040-128]“Oh, yes. He understands that’s what I sit there for. I’ll keep

[040-129]sitting!”

[040-130]However, in spite of the time he spent sitting in the shop, worrying

[040-131]the inventor of the fractious light, Amberson found opportunity to

[040-132]worry himself about another matter of business. This was the settlement

[040-133]of Isabel’s estate.

[040-134]“It’s curious about the deed to her house,” he said to his nephew.

[040-135]“You’re absolutely sure it wasn’t among her papers?”

[040-136]“Mother didn’t have any papers,” George told him. “None at all. All she

[040-137]ever had to do with business was to deposit the cheques grandfather

[040-138]gave her and then write her own cheques against them.”

[040-139]“The deed to the house was never recorded,” Amberson said thoughtfully.

[040-140]“I’ve been over to the courthouse to see. I asked father if he never

[040-141]gave her one, and he didn’t seem able to understand me at first. Then

[040-142]he finally said he thought he must have given her a deed long ago; but

[040-143]he wasn’t sure. I rather think he never did. I think it would be just

[040-144]as well to get him to execute one now in your favour. I’ll speak to him

[040-145]about it.”

[040-146]George sighed. “I don’t think I’d bother him about it: the house is

[040-147]mine, and you and I understand that it is. That’s enough for me, and

[040-148]there isn’t likely to be much trouble between you and me when we come

[040-149]to settling poor grandfather’s estate. I’ve just been with him, and I

[040-150]think it would only confuse him for you to speak to him about it again.

[040-151]I notice he seems distressed if anybody tries to get his attention—he’s

[040-152]a long way off, somewhere, and he likes to stay that way. I think—I

[040-153]think mother wouldn’t want us to bother him about it; I’m sure she’d

[040-154]tell us to let him alone. He looks so white and queer.”

[040-155]Amberson shook his head. “Not much whiter and queerer than you do,

[040-156]young fellow! You’d better begin to get some air and exercise and quit

[040-157]hanging about in the house all day. I won’t bother him any more than I

[040-158]can help; but I’ll have the deed made out ready for his signature.”

[040-159]“I wouldn’t bother him at all. I don’t see—”

[040-160]“You might see,” said his uncle uneasily. “The estate is just about

[040-161]as involved and mixed-up as an estate can well get, to the best of my

[040-162]knowledge; and I haven’t helped it any by what he let me have for this

[040-163]infernal headlight scheme which has finally gone trolloping forever to

[040-164]where the woodbine twineth. Leaves me flat, and poor old Frank Bronson

[040-165]just half flat, and Fanny—well, thank heaven! I kept her from going in

[040-166]so deep that it would leave her flat. It’s rough on her as it is, I

[040-167]suspect. You ought to have that deed.”

[040-168]“No. Don’t bother him.”

[040-169]“I’ll bother him as little as possible. I’ll wait till some day when he

[040-170]seems to brighten up a little.”

[040-171]But Amberson waited too long. [[major-amberson|The Major]] had already taken eleven months

[040-172]since his daughter’s death to think important things out. He had got as

[040-173]far with them as he could, and there was nothing to detain him longer

[040-174]in the world. One evening his grandson sat with him—the Major seemed

[040-175]to like best to have young George with him, so far as they were able

[040-176]to guess his preferences—and the old gentleman made a queer gesture:

[040-177]he slapped his knee as if he had made a sudden discovery, or else

[040-178]remembered that he had forgotten something.
