[027-001]“No, dear; of course people can’t give up their ideals; but I don’t see

[027-002]what this has to do with dear little [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] and—”

[027-003]“I didn’t say it had anything to do with them,” he interrupted. “I

[027-004]was merely putting a case to show how a person would be justified in

[027-005]being a friend of one member of a family, and feeling anything but

[027-006]friendly toward another. I don’t say, though, that I feel unfriendly to

[027-007]Mr. Morgan. I don’t say that I feel friendly to him, and I don’t say

[027-008]that I feel unfriendly; but if you really think that I was rude to him

[027-009]to-night—”

[027-010]“Just thoughtless, dear. You didn’t see that what you said to-night—”

[027-011]“Well, I’ll not say anything of that sort again where he can hear it.

[027-012]There, isn’t that enough?”

[027-013]This question, delivered with large indulgence, met with no response;

[027-014]for Isabel, still searching his face with her troubled and perplexed

[027-015]gaze, seemed not to have heard it. On that account, George repeated it,

[027-016]and rising, went to her and patted her reassuringly upon the shoulder.

[027-017]“There, old lady, you needn’t fear my tactlessness will worry you

[027-018]again. I can’t quite promise to like people I don’t care about one way

[027-019]or another, but you can be sure I’ll be careful, after this, not to

[027-020]let them see it. It’s all right, and you’d better toddle along to bed,

[027-021]because I want to undress.”

[027-022]“But, George,” she said earnestly, “you would like him, if you’d just

[027-023]let yourself. You say you don’t dislike him. Why don’t you like him? I

[027-024]can’t understand at all. What is it that you don’t—”

[027-025]“There, there!” he said. “It’s all right, and you toddle along.”

[027-026]“But, George, dear—”

[027-027]“Now, now! I really do want to get into bed. Good-night, old lady.”

[027-028]“Good-night, dear. But—”

[027-029]“Let’s not talk of it any more,” he said. “It’s all right, and nothing

[027-030]in the world to worry about. So good-night, old lady. I’ll be polite

[027-031]enough to him, never fear—if we happen to be thrown together. So

[027-032]good-night!”

[027-033]“But, George, dear—”

[027-034]“I’m going to bed, old lady; so good-night.”

[027-035]Thus the interview closed perforce. She kissed him again before going

[027-036]slowly to her own room, her perplexity evidently not dispersed; but the

[027-037]subject was not renewed between them the next day or subsequently. Nor

[027-038]did [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] make any allusion to the cryptic approbation she had bestowed

[027-039]upon her nephew after the Major’s “not very successful little dinner”;

[027-040]though she annoyed George by looking at him oftener and longer than

[027-041]he cared to be looked at by an aunt. He could not glance her way, it

[027-042]seemed, without finding her red-rimmed eyes fixed upon him eagerly,

[027-043]with an alert and hopeful calculation in them which he declared would

[027-044]send a nervous man into fits. For thus, one day, he broke out, in

[027-045]protest:

[027-046]“It would!” he repeated vehemently. “Given time it would—straight into

[027-047]fits! What do you find the matter with me? Is my tie always slipping up

[027-048]behind? Can’t you look at something else? My Lord! We’d better buy a

[027-049]cat for you to stare at, Aunt Fanny! A cat could stand it, maybe. What

[027-050]in the name of goodness do you expect to see?”

[027-051]But Fanny laughed good-naturedly, and was not offended. “It’s more as

[027-052]if I expected you to see something, isn’t it?” she said quietly, still

[027-053]laughing.

[027-054]“Now, what do you mean by that?”

[027-055]“Never mind!”

[027-056]“All right, I don’t. But for heaven’s sake stare at somebody else

[027-057]awhile. Try it on the house-maid!”

[027-058]“Well, well,” Fanny said indulgently, and then chose to be more obscure

[027-059]in her meaning than ever, for she adopted a tone of deep sympathy for

[027-060]her final remark, as she left him: “I don’t wonder you’re nervous these

[027-061]days, poor boy!”

[027-062]And George indignantly supposed that she referred to the ordeal of

[027-063]Lucy’s continued absence. During this period he successfully avoided

[027-064]contact with Lucy’s father, though [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]] came frequently to the

[027-065]house, and spent several evenings with Isabel and Fanny; and sometimes

[027-066]persuaded them and the Major to go for an afternoon’s motoring. He

[027-067]did not, however, come again to the Major’s Sunday evening dinner,

[027-068]even when [[george-amberson|George Amberson]] returned. Sunday evening was the time, he

[027-069]explained, for going over the week’s work with his factory managers.

[027-070]When Lucy came home the autumn was far enough advanced to smell of

[027-071]burning leaves, and for the annual editorials, in the papers, on the

[027-072]purple haze, the golden branches, the ruddy fruit, and the pleasure

[027-073]of long tramps in the brown forest. George had not heard of her

[027-074]arrival, and he met her, on the afternoon following that event, at

[027-075]the Sharons’, where he had gone in the secret hope that he might hear

[027-076]something about her. Janie Sharon had just begun to tell him that

[027-077]she heard Lucy was expected home soon, after having “a perfectly

[027-078]gorgeous time”—information which George received with no responsive

[027-079]enthusiasm—when Lucy came demurely in, a proper little autumn figure in

[027-080]green and brown.

[027-081]Her cheeks were flushed, and her dark eyes were bright indeed;

[027-082]evidences, as George supposed, of the excitement incidental to the

[027-083]perfectly gorgeous time just concluded; though Janie and Mary Sharon

[027-084]both thought they were the effect of Lucy’s having seen George’s

[027-085]runabout in front of the house as she came in. George took on colour,

[027-086]himself, as, he rose and nodded indifferently; and the hot suffusion to

[027-087]which he became subject extended its area to include his neck and ears.

[027-088]Nothing could have made him much more indignant than his consciousness

[027-089]of these symptoms of the icy indifference which it was his purpose not

[027-090]only to show but to feel.

[027-091]She kissed her cousins, gave George her hand, said “How d’you do,” and

[027-092]took a chair beside Janie with a composure which augmented George’s

[027-093]indignation.

[027-094]“How d’you do,” he said. “I trust that ah—I trust—I do trust—”

[027-095]He stopped, for it seemed to him that the word “trust” sounded idiotic.

[027-096]Then, to cover his awkwardness, he coughed, and even to his own rosy

[027-097]ears his cough was ostentatiously a false one. Whereupon, seeking to be

[027-098]plausible, he coughed again, and instantly hated himself: the sound he

[027-099]made was an atrocity. Meanwhile, Lucy sat silent, and the two Sharon

[027-100]girls leaned forward, staring at him with strained eyes, their lips

[027-101]tightly compressed; and both were but too easily diagnosed as subject

[027-102]to an agitation which threatened their self-control. He began again.

[027-103]“I er—I hope you have had a—a pleasant time. I er—I hope you are well.

[027-104]I hope you are extremely—I hope extremely—extremely—” And again he

[027-105]stopped in the midst of his floundering, not knowing how to progress

[027-106]beyond “extremely,” and unable to understand why the infernal word kept

[027-107]getting into his mouth.

[027-108]“I beg your pardon?” Lucy said.

[027-109]George was never more furious; he felt that he was “making a spectacle

[027-110]of himself”; and no young gentleman in the world was more loath than

[027-111][[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]] to look a figure of fun. And while he stood

[027-112]there, undeniably such a figure, with Janie and Mary Sharon threatening

[027-113]to burst at any moment, if laughter were longer denied them, Lucy sat

[027-114]looking at him with her eyebrows delicately lifted in casual, polite

[027-115]inquiry. Her own complete composure was what most galled him.

[027-116]“Nothing of the slightest importance!” he managed to say. “I was just

[027-117]leaving. Good afternoon!” And with long strides he reached the door and

[027-118]hastened through the hall; but before he closed the front door he heard

[027-119]from Janie and Mary Sharon the outburst of wild, irrepressible emotion

[027-120]which his performance had inspired.

[027-121]He drove home in a tumultuous mood, and almost ran down two ladies

[027-122]who were engaged in absorbing conversation at a crossing. They were

[027-123]his Aunt Fanny and the stout Mrs. Johnson; a jerk of the reins at the

[027-124]last instant saved them by a few inches; but their conversation was so

[027-125]interesting that they were unaware of their danger, and did not notice

[027-126]the runabout, nor how close it came to them. George was so furious with

[027-127]himself and with the girl whose unexpected coming into a room could

[027-128]make him look such a fool, that it might have soothed him a little if

[027-129]he had actually run over the two absorbed ladies without injuring them

[027-130]beyond repair. At least, he said to himself that he wished he had; it

[027-131]might have taken his mind off of himself for a few minutes. For, in

[027-132]truth, to be ridiculous (and know it) was one of several things that

[027-133]George was unable to endure. He was savage.

[027-134]He drove into the Major’s stable too fast, the sagacious Pendennis

[027-135]saving himself from going through a partition by a swerve which

[027-136]splintered a shaft of the runabout and almost threw the driver to the

[027-137]floor. George swore, and then swore again at the fat old darkey, Tom,

[027-138]for giggling at his swearing.

[027-139]“Hoopee!” said old Tom. “Mus’ been some white lady use Mist’ Jawge

[027-140]mighty bad! White lady say, ‘No, suh, I ain’ go’n out ridin’ ’ith Mist’

[027-141]Jawge no mo’!’ Mist’ Jawge drive in. ‘Dam de dam worl’! Dam de dam

[027-142]hoss! Dam de dam nigga’! Dam de dam dam!’ Hoopee!”

[027-143]“That’ll do!” George said sternly.

[027-144]“Yessuh!”

[027-145]George strode from the stable, crossed the Major’s back yard, then

[027-146]passed behind the new houses, on his way home. These structures were

[027-147]now approaching completion, but still in a state of rawness hideous to

[027-148]George—though, for that matter, they were never to be anything except

[027-149]hideous to him. Behind them, stray planks, bricks, refuse of plaster

[027-150]and lath, shingles, straw, empty barrels, strips of twisted tin and

[027-151]broken tiles were strewn everywhere over the dried and pitted gray

[027-152]mud where once the suave lawn had lain like a green lake around those

[027-153]stately islands, the two Amberson houses. And George’s state of mind

[027-154]was not improved by his present view of this repulsive area, nor by his

[027-155]sensations when he kicked an uptilted shingle only to discover that

[027-156]what uptilted it was a brickbat on the other side of it. After that,

[027-157]the whole world seemed to be one solid conspiracy of malevolence.

[027-158]In this temper he emerged from behind the house nearest to his own,

[027-159]and, glancing toward the street, saw his mother standing with Eugene

[027-160]Morgan upon the cement path that led to the front gate. She was

[027-161]bareheaded, and Eugene held his hat and stick in his hand; evidently he

[027-162]had been calling upon her, and she had come from the house with him,

[027-163]continuing their conversation and delaying their parting.

[027-164]They had paused in their slow walk from the front door to the gate, yet

[027-165]still stood side by side, their shoulders almost touching, as though

[027-166]neither Isabel nor Eugene quite realized that their feet had ceased

[027-167]to bear them forward; and they were not looking at each other, but at

[027-168]some indefinite point before them, as people do who consider together

[027-169]thoughtfully and in harmony. The conversation was evidently serious;

[027-170]his head was bent, and Isabel’s lifted left hand rested against her

[027-171]cheek; but all the significances of their thoughtful attitude denoted

[027-172]companionableness and a shared understanding. Yet, a stranger, passing,

[027-173]would not have thought them married: somewhere about Eugene, not

[027-174]quite to be located, there was a romantic gravity; and Isabel, tall

[027-175]and graceful, with high colour and absorbed eyes, was visibly no wife

[027-176]walking down to the gate with her husband.

[027-177]George stared at them. A hot dislike struck him at the sight of Eugene;

[027-178]and a vague revulsion, like a strange, unpleasant taste in his mouth,

[027-179]came over him as he looked at his mother: her manner was eloquent of

[027-180]so much thought about her companion and of such reliance upon him.

[027-181]And the picture the two thus made was a vivid one indeed, to George,

[027-182]whose angry eyes, for some reason, fixed themselves most intently upon

[027-183]Isabel’s lifted hand, upon the white ruffle at her wrist, bordering the

[027-184]graceful black sleeve, and upon the little indentations in her cheek

[027-185]where the tips of her fingers rested. She should not have worn white at

[027-186]her wrist, or at the throat either, George felt; and then, strangely,

[027-187]his resentment concentrated upon those tiny indentations at the tips

[027-188]of her fingers—actual changes, however slight and fleeting, in his

[027-189]mother’s face, made because of Mr. Eugene Morgan. For the moment, it

[027-190]seemed to George that Morgan might have claimed the ownership of a face

[027-191]that changed for him. It was as if he owned Isabel.
