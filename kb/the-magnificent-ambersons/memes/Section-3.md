[003-001]“I didn’t see the play,” she informed them.

[003-002]“What! Why, we saw you, right in the middle of the fourth row!”

[003-003]“Yes,” she said, smiling, “but I was sitting just behind Isabel

[003-004]Amberson. I couldn’t look at anything except her wavy brown hair and

[003-005]the wonderful back of her neck.”

[003-006]The ineligible young men of the town (they were all ineligible) were

[003-007]unable to content themselves with the view that had so charmed Mrs.

[003-008]Henry Franklin Foster: they spent their time struggling to keep Miss

[003-009]Amberson’s face turned toward them. She turned it most often, observers

[003-010]said, toward two: one excelling in the general struggle by his sparkle,

[003-011]and the other by that winning if not winsome old trait, persistence.

[003-012]The sparkling gentleman “led germans” with her, and sent sonnets to

[003-013]her with his bouquets—sonnets lacking neither music nor wit. He was

[003-014]generous, poor, well-dressed, and his amazing persuasiveness was one

[003-015]reason why he was always in debt. No one doubted that he would be able

[003-016]to persuade Isabel, but he unfortunately joined too merry a party

[003-017]one night, and, during a moonlight serenade upon the lawn before the

[003-018][[amberson-mansion|Amberson Mansion]], was easily identified from the windows as the person

[003-019]who stepped through the bass viol and had to be assisted to a waiting

[003-020]carriage. One of Miss Amberson’s brothers was among the serenaders,

[003-021]and, when the party had dispersed, remained propped against the front

[003-022]door in a state of helpless liveliness; the Major going down in a

[003-023]dressing-gown and slippers to bring him in, and scolding mildly, while

[003-024]imperfectly concealing strong impulses to laughter. Miss Amberson also

[003-025]laughed at this brother, the next day, but for the suitor it was a

[003-026]different matter: she refused to see him when he called to apologize.

[003-027]“You seem to care a great deal about bass viols!” he wrote her. “I

[003-028]promise never to break another.” She made no response to the note,

[003-029]unless it was an answer, two weeks later, when her engagement was

[003-030]announced. She took the persistent one, [[wilbur-minafer|Wilbur Minafer]], no breaker of

[003-031]bass viols or of hearts, no serenader at all.

[003-032]A few people, who always foresaw everything, claimed that they were not

[003-033]surprised, because though Wilbur Minafer “might not be an Apollo, as it

[003-034]were,” he was “a steady young business man, and a good church-goer,”

[003-035]and [[isabel-amberson|Isabel Amberson]] was “pretty sensible—for such a showy girl.” But

[003-036]the engagement astounded the young people, and most of their fathers

[003-037]and mothers, too; and as a topic it supplanted literature at the next

[003-038]meeting of the “Women’s Tennyson Club.”

[003-039]“Wilbur _Minafer!_” a member cried, her inflection seeming to imply

[003-040]that Wilbur’s crime was explained by his surname. “Wilbur _Minafer!_

[003-041]It’s the queerest thing I ever heard! To think of her taking Wilbur

[003-042]Minafer, just because a man _any_ woman would like a thousand times

[003-043]better was a little wild one night at a serenade!”

[003-044]“No,” said Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster. “It isn’t that. It isn’t even

[003-045]because she’s afraid he’d be a dissipated husband and she wants to be

[003-046]safe. It isn’t because she’s religious or hates wildness; it isn’t even

[003-047]because she hates wildness in _him_.”

[003-048]“Well, but look how she’s thrown him over for it.”

[003-049]“No, that wasn’t her reason,” said the wise Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster.

[003-050]“If men only knew it—and it’s a good thing they don’t—a woman doesn’t

[003-051]really care much about whether a man’s wild or not, if it doesn’t

[003-052]affect herself, and Isabel Amberson doesn’t care a thing!”

[003-053]“Mrs. _Foster!_”

[003-054]“No, she doesn’t. What she minds is his making a clown of himself in

[003-055]her front yard! It made her think he didn’t care much about _her_.

[003-056]She’s probably mistaken, but that’s what she thinks, and it’s too late

[003-057]for her to think anything else now, because she’s going to be married

[003-058]right away—the invitations will be out next week. It’ll be a big

[003-059]Amberson-style thing, raw oysters floating in scooped-out blocks of

[003-060]ice and a band from out-of-town—champagne, showy presents; a colossal

[003-061]present from the Major. Then Wilbur will take Isabel on the carefulest

[003-062]little wedding trip he can manage, and she’ll be a good wife to him,

[003-063]but they’ll have the worst spoiled lot of children this town will ever

[003-064]see.”

[003-065]“How on earth do you make _that_ out, Mrs. Foster?”

[003-066]“She couldn’t love Wilbur, could she?” Mrs. Foster demanded, with no

[003-067]challengers. “Well, it will all go to her children, and she’ll ruin

[003-068]’em!”

[003-069]The prophetess proved to be mistaken in a single detail merely: except

[003-070]for that, her foresight was accurate. The wedding was of Ambersonian

[003-071]magnificence, even to the floating oysters; and the Major’s colossal

[003-072]present was a set of architect’s designs for a house almost as

[003-073]elaborate and impressive as the Mansion, the house to be built in

[003-074][[amberson-addition|Amberson Addition]] by the Major. The orchestra was certainly not that

[003-075]local one which had suffered the loss of a bass viol; the musicians

[003-076]came, according to the prophecy and next morning’s paper, from afar;

[003-077]and at midnight the bride was still being toasted in champagne, though

[003-078]she had departed upon her wedding journey at ten. Four days later

[003-079]the pair had returned to town, which promptness seemed fairly to

[003-080]demonstrate that Wilbur had indeed taken Isabel upon the carefulest

[003-081]little trip he could manage. According to every report, she was from

[003-082]the start “a good wife to him,” but here in a final detail the prophecy

[003-083]proved inaccurate. Wilbur and Isabel did not have children; they had

[003-084]only one.

[003-085]“Only one,” Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster admitted. “But I’d like to know

[003-086]if he isn’t spoiled enough for a whole carload!”

[003-087]Again she found none to challenge her.

[003-088]At the age of nine, [[george-amberson-minafer|George Amberson Minafer]], the Major’s one

[003-089]grandchild, was a princely terror, dreaded not only in Amberson

[003-090]Addition but in many other quarters through which he galloped on

[003-091]his white pony. “By golly, I guess you think you own this town!” an

[003-092]embittered labourer complained, one day, as Georgie rode the pony

[003-093]straight through a pile of sand the man was sieving. “I will when I

[003-094]grow up,” the undisturbed child replied. “I guess my grandpa owns it

[003-095]now, you bet!” And the baffled workman, having no means to controvert

[003-096]what seemed a mere exaggeration of the facts could only mutter “Oh,

[003-097]pull down your vest!”

[003-098]“Don’t haf to! Doctor says it ain’t healthy!” the boy returned

[003-099]promptly. “But I’ll tell you what I’ll do: I’ll pull down my vest if

[003-100]you’ll wipe off your chin!”

[003-101]This was stock and stencil: the accustomed argot of street badinage of

[003-102]the period; and in such matters Georgie was an expert. He had no vest

[003-103]to pull down; the incongruous fact was that a fringed sash girdled the

[003-104]juncture of his velvet blouse and breeches, for the Fauntleroy period

[003-105]had set in, and Georgie’s mother had so poor an eye for appropriate

[003-106]things, where Georgie was concerned, that she dressed him according to

[003-107]the doctrine of that school in boy decoration. Not only did he wear a

[003-108]silk sash, and silk stockings, and a broad lace collar, with his little

[003-109]black velvet suit: he had long brown curls, and often came home with

[003-110]burrs in them.

[003-111]Except upon the surface (which was not his own work, but his mother’s)

[003-112]Georgie bore no vivid resemblance to the fabulous little Cedric. The

[003-113]storied boy’s famous “Lean on _me_, grandfather,” would have been

[003-114]difficult to imagine upon the lips of Georgie. A month after his ninth

[003-115]birthday anniversary, when the Major gave him his pony, he had already

[003-116]become acquainted with the toughest boys in various distant parts of

[003-117]the town, and had convinced them that the toughness of a rich little

[003-118]boy with long curls might be considered in many respects superior to

[003-119]their own. He fought them, learning how to go berserk at a certain

[003-120]point in a fight, bursting into tears of anger, reaching for rocks,

[003-121]uttering wailed threats of murder and attempting to fulfil them. Fights

[003-122]often led to intimacies, and he acquired the art of saying things more

[003-123]exciting than “Don’t haf to!” and “Doctor says it ain’t healthy!” Thus,

[003-124]on a summer afternoon, a strange boy, sitting bored upon the gate-post

[003-125]of the Reverend Malloch Smith, beheld [[george-amberson|George Amberson]] Minafer rapidly

[003-126]approaching on his white pony, and was impelled by bitterness to shout:

[003-127]“Shoot the ole jackass! Look at the girly curls! Say, bub, where’d you

[003-128]steal your mother’s ole sash!”

[003-129]“Your sister stole it for me!” Georgie instantly replied, checking the

[003-130]pony. “She stole it off our clo’es-line an’ gave it to me.”

[003-131]“You go get your hair cut!” said the stranger hotly. “Yah! I haven’t

[003-132]got any sister!”

[003-133]“I know you haven’t at home,” Georgie responded. “I mean the one that’s

[003-134]in jail.”

[003-135]“I dare you to get down off that pony!”

[003-136]Georgie jumped to the ground, and the other boy descended from the

[003-137]Reverend Mr. Smith’s gatepost—but he descended inside the gate. “I dare

[003-138]you outside that gate,” said Georgie.

[003-139]“Yah! I dare you half way here. I dare you—”

[003-140]But these were luckless challenges, for Georgie immediately vaulted

[003-141]the fence—and four minutes later Mrs. Malloch Smith, hearing strange

[003-142]noises, looked forth from a window; then screamed, and dashed for the

[003-143]pastor’s study. Mr. Malloch Smith, that grim-bearded Methodist, came to

[003-144]the front yard and found his visiting nephew being rapidly prepared by

[003-145]Master Minafer to serve as a principal figure in a pageant of massacre.

[003-146]It was with great physical difficulty that Mr. Smith managed to give

[003-147]his nephew a chance to escape into the house, for Georgie was hard and

[003-148]quick, and, in such matters, remarkably intense; but the minister,

[003-149]after a grotesque tussle, got him separated from his opponent, and

[003-150]shook him.

[003-151]“You stop that, you!” Georgie cried fiercely; and wrenched himself

[003-152]away. “I guess you don’t know who I am!”

[003-153]“Yes, I do know!” the angered Mr. Smith retorted. “I know who you are,

[003-154]and you’re a disgrace to your mother! Your mother ought to be ashamed

[003-155]of herself to allow—”

[003-156]“Shut up about my mother bein’ ashamed of herself!”

[003-157]Mr. Smith, exasperated, was unable to close the dialogue with dignity.

[003-158]“She ought to be ashamed,” he repeated. “A woman that lets a bad boy

[003-159]like you—”

[003-160]But Georgie had reached his pony and mounted. Before setting off at his

[003-161]accustomed gallop, he paused to interrupt the Reverend Malloch Smith

[003-162]again. “You pull down your vest, you ole Billygoat, you!” he shouted,

[003-163]distinctly. “Pull down your vest, wipe off your chin—an’ go to hell!”

[003-164]Such precocity is less unusual, even in children of the Rich, than most

[003-165]grown people imagine. However, it was a new experience for the Reverend

[003-166]Malloch Smith, and left him in a state of excitement. He at once wrote

[003-167]a note to Georgie’s mother, describing the crime according to his

[003-168]nephew’s testimony; and the note reached Mrs. Minafer before Georgie

[003-169]did. When he got home she read it to him sorrowfully.

[003-170]Dear Madam:

[003-171]Your son has caused a painful distress in my household. He made

[003-172]an unprovoked attack upon a little nephew of mine who is visiting

[003-173]in my household, insulted him by calling him vicious names and

[003-174]falsehoods, stating that ladies of his family were in jail. He

[003-175]then tried to make his pony kick him, and when the child, who is

[003-176]only eleven years old, while your son is much older and stronger,

[003-177]endeavoured to avoid his indignities and withdraw quietly, he

[003-178]pursued him into the enclosure of my property and brutally

[003-179]assaulted him. When I appeared upon this scene he deliberately

[003-180]called insulting words to me, concluding with profanity, such as

[003-181]“go to hell,” which was heard not only by myself but by my wife and

[003-182]the lady who lives next door. I trust such a state of undisciplined

[003-183]behaviour may be remedied for the sake of the reputation for

[003-184]propriety, if nothing higher, of the family to which this unruly

[003-185]child belongs.

[003-186]Georgie had muttered various interruptions, and as she concluded the

[003-187]reading he said: “He’s an ole liar!”
