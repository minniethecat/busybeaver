# Isabel and Eugene's Engagement

The love affair between [[isabel-amberson]] and [[eugene-morgan]] is the emotional heart of Booth Tarkington's *The Magnificent Ambersons* -- a romance that spans more than two decades, traverses youthful passion and middle-aged longing, and ends in permanent sacrifice. It is at once the engine of the novel's deepest tensions and the clearest illustration of its central theme: the collision between individual desire and the fierce, possessive pride of the Amberson family, embodied in the unstoppable will of [[george-amberson-minafer]]. What begins in the serenades and cotillions of the 1870s becomes, by the 1890s, a story of what might have been -- and what is deliberately, agonizingly given up.

## Summary

The relationship between Isabel Amberson and Eugene Morgan unfolds in three distinct phases across the novel's timeline. In the first phase, during the early 1870s, the young Eugene Morgan -- a "sparkling gentleman" who "led germans with her, and sent sonnets to her with his bouquets" (003-012) -- courts Isabel Amberson with charm and passion. He is, by every indication, her preferred suitor. But one night, during a moonlight serenade before the Amberson Mansion, Eugene, intoxicated, steps through a bass viol and must be assisted to a carriage. Isabel, mortified not by his wildness but by the public humiliation he has brought upon her, refuses to see him again. Two weeks later, her engagement to the "persistent one," Wilbur Minafer, is announced (003-029).

The second phase begins some twenty years later when Eugene returns to Midland as a widower with a young daughter, Lucy. He is now a successful manufacturer of automobiles, prosperous and genial, a figure of the emerging new century. At the Ambersons' great ball -- the last of the legendary Amberson dances -- he resumes his place in Isabel's life. "There aren't any old times," he declares to an old friend before going to dance with her. "When times are gone they're not old, they're dead! There aren't any times but new times!" (009-182). This declaration sets the tone for the renewed courtship that follows: Eugene and Isabel fall into a companionable intimacy, sustained through Sunday dinners, afternoon drives, and quiet conversations. The relationship deepens, and Eugene, a man who knows what he lost, is determined not to lose it again.

The third phase is one of destruction. Isabel's son George, now a young man whose pride and possessiveness toward his mother are boundless, discovers the depth of the connection between Isabel and Eugene. He perceives it not as love but as a threat to the Amberson name -- and to his own primacy in his mother's heart. When Eugene proposes marriage, George responds with an ultimatum of devastating cruelty. Isabel chooses her son, and the engagement that might have been -- for Eugene has effectively proposed, and Isabel has effectively accepted before being forced to retreat -- is annihilated. Within a few years, Isabel is dead; Eugene carries his bitterness toward George, and his undimmed love for Isabel, to the novel's end.

## Context

### The First Courtship and the Bass Viol

In the social world of Midland in the 1870s, Isabel Amberson was the undisputed belle of the town. Observers noted that she turned her face most often toward two suitors: "one excelling in the general struggle by his sparkle, and the other by that winning if not winsome old trait, persistence" (003-010). The sparkling one was Eugene Morgan; the persistent one was Wilbur Minafer.

Eugene's courtship was by all accounts romantic and intoxicating. He was "generous, poor, well-dressed, and his amazing persuasiveness was one reason why he was always in debt. No one doubted that he would be able to persuade Isabel" (003-014). But the serenade proved his undoing. The incident -- stepping through the bass viol in a state of inebriation -- was not, for Isabel, about the drinking or the wildness. As the perceptive Mrs. Henry Franklin Foster explained: "What she minds is his making a clown of himself in her front yard! It made her think he didn't care much about her" (003-054).

The wound was one of pride -- specifically, Isabel's pride in being properly valued. Eugene's transgression was not moral but aesthetic; he had made himself, and by extension her, ridiculous. She could forgive wildness; she could not forgive the violation of the public dignity that an Amberson woman demanded. Her choice of Wilbur Minafer was therefore a choice of safety, predictability, and social propriety over romantic risk.

Years later, at a Sunday dinner at the Amberson Mansion, the Major would confirm the pivotal role of the bass viol with characteristic bluntness: "I believe if Eugene hadn't broken that bass fiddle and given himself away, Isabel would never have taken Wilbur! I shouldn't be surprised if that was about all the reason that Wilbur got her!" To which Wilbur himself replied placidly, "If your notion is right, I'm glad 'Gene broke the fiddle. He was giving me a hard run!" (015-094). Isabel blushed; Eugene turned pink; the past lay between them like a half-healed wound.

### The Marriage to Wilbur

Isabel's marriage to Wilbur Minafer was by all accounts dutiful and correct. "According to every report, she was from the start 'a good wife to him'" (003-082). They had one child: George Amberson Minafer. Mrs. Foster had prophesied that Isabel "couldn't love Wilbur" and that "it will all go to her children, and she'll ruin 'em!" (003-066). The prophecy proved largely accurate: George grew into a "princely terror," spoiled beyond measure, convinced of his own supremacy in the world, and possessed of a devotion to his mother that bordered on the pathological.

The marriage was not unhappy, but it was not a love match. Wilbur was a quiet, careful man who "never wants to go anywhere" (012-151) and who believed that "God made money to be invested and saved" (015-047). Isabel, an Amberson to her core, belonged to a family that believed in spending, display, and social magnificence. The marriage produced no great passion, but it produced George -- and George became, for Isabel, the vessel into which she poured all the emotional intensity that her marriage lacked.

### Eugene's Return

When Eugene Morgan reappeared in Midland at the Ambersons' great ball, he was a transformed figure. No longer the debt-ridden young lawyer, he was now a widower of substance, an inventor and manufacturer whose automobiles were beginning to reshape the world. He carried himself with the confidence of a man who has built something, and he moved through the Amberson house not as a supplicant but as an equal.

His exchange with old Fred Kinney in the smoking room is revealing. Kinney, teasing him about his eagerness to dance with Isabel, groans, "Old times starting all over again!" Eugene's reply -- "There aren't any old times. When times are gone they're not old, they're dead! There aren't any times but new times!" (009-182) -- is both a philosophy and a statement of intent. He is not returning to reclaim the past; he is claiming the present.

And yet, despite this forward-looking philosophy, the past would prove inescapable.

## The Renewed Courtship

The courtship that developed between Isabel and Eugene in the years following his return was conducted in plain sight, under the guise of friendship. Eugene became a fixture at the Amberson household: he attended Sunday dinners at the Mansion, took Isabel and Fanny driving in his automobiles, and spent quiet evenings in conversation. The relationship was one of deepening companionship -- a shared understanding that spoke of two people who had always been, in some fundamental sense, meant for each other.

The narrator captures the quality of their connection in a single, luminous image. George, coming home one afternoon, sees his mother and Eugene standing together on the garden path:

> They had paused in their slow walk from the front door to the gate, yet still stood side by side, their shoulders almost touching, as though neither Isabel nor Eugene quite realized that their feet had ceased to bear them forward; and they were not looking at each other, but at some indefinite point before them, as people do who consider together thoughtfully and in harmony. The conversation was evidently serious; his head was bent, and Isabel's lifted left hand rested against her cheek; but all the significances of their thoughtful attitude denoted companionableness and a shared understanding. (027-165)

The narrator notes that "a stranger, passing, would not have thought them married: somewhere about Eugene, not quite to be located, there was a romantic gravity; and Isabel, tall and graceful, with high colour and absorbed eyes, was visibly no wife walking down to the gate with her husband" (027-173). This is not the posture of flirtation or of illicit passion -- it is something rarer and more threatening: the posture of two people who have found in each other what they have found nowhere else.

At a Sunday dinner, the Major's champagne-fueled reminiscences bring the unspoken into the open. When he teasingly recounts the bass viol story, Isabel blushes. Eugene, equally pink, offers the most telling remark of all: "There's another important thing -- that is, for me. It's the only thing that makes me forgive that bass viol for getting in my way." He pauses, then says gently, "Lucy" (015-112). The logic is unmistakable: had the bass viol not intervened, Isabel would have married Eugene; Lucy would not exist; and the loss of Isabel, measured against the gift of his daughter, is a loss Eugene has spent twenty years learning to forgive.

Isabel's response to this remark is immediate and revealing: "Isabel gave him a quick glance, all warm approval, and there was a murmur of friendliness round the table" (015-113). In that glance, the reader sees everything: the acknowledgment of what might have been, the tenderness of a connection that has survived decades, and the quiet joy of having Eugene back in her life.

Lucy herself perceives the depth of her father's feeling. She asks him directly: "You liked her pretty well once, I guess, papa." His reply is quiet but absolute: "I do still" (015-031). The shift from past to present tense says everything.

## The Obstacle: George Amberson Minafer

If Eugene and Isabel's love is the novel's emotional engine, George Amberson Minafer is the obstacle that halts it. George's relationship with his mother is one of the most psychologically acute portraits in American fiction. From his earliest childhood, he has been the sole recipient of Isabel's adoration: "she just fell down and worshipped him from the day he was born" (009-112). He sees her not as a woman but "wholly a mother... nor could he imagine her thinking or doing anything -- falling in love, walking with a friend, or reading a book -- as a woman, and not as his mother" (005-164). The woman, Isabel, is "a stranger to her son" (005-167).

When George first glimpses this stranger -- seeing his mother at the ball, "her eyes were brilliant, that she was graceful and youthful -- in a word, that she was romantically lovely" (005-185) -- he is disturbed. "Youth cannot imagine romance apart from youth," the narrator observes. "Young people will not come to see a play about middle-aged lovers; because, for them, middle-aged lovers are a joke -- not a very funny one" (005-175). George's disturbance is not simply jealousy; it is a fundamental inability to conceive of his mother as a person with desires, with a history, with the capacity for romantic love that exists independently of her role as his mother.

When George learns that Eugene has effectively proposed marriage -- conveyed through a letter that George's uncle urged Isabel to share with her son -- his response is volcanic. He confronts his mother in her bedroom with a ferocity that reduces her to helplessness:

> "Do you think if you did what he wants you to I could bear to stay another day in this town, mother? Do you think I could ever bear even to see you again if you married him? I'd want to, but you surely know I just -- couldn't!" (034-107)

The ultimatum is absolute: choose Eugene and lose your son. George frames his demand in the language of family honour, invoking the Amberson name as sacred. "There never was an Amberson yet that would let the Amberson name go trailing in the dust like that! It's the proudest name in this town and it's going to stay the proudest" (034-076). But his concern is not truly for the family name -- it is for his own possession of his mother. The passage that follows makes this devastatingly clear: "It was as if he owned Isabel" (027-191).

George, in a moment of grotesque self-dramatization, even compares himself to Hamlet. Standing before his mirror in a black velvet dressing-gown, he whispers:

> "'Tis not alone my inky cloak, good mother,
> Nor customary suits of solemn black..." (034-148)

The narrator's irony is surgical: George is indeed "that other gentle prince and heir whose widowed mother was minded to marry again" (034-154). But the comparison, intended by George to ennoble his cause, serves only to expose his self-absorption.

## Isabel's Choice

Isabel's response to her son's ultimatum is the moral and emotional climax of the novel. She is caught between two loves, two obligations -- and she knows, from the moment George speaks, what her decision must be. The narrator captures the collapse of her spirit in a single image:

> George was right about his mother's being proud. And even when she laughed with a negro gardener, or even those few times in her life when people saw her weep, Isabel had a proud look -- something that was independent and graceful and strong. But she did not have it now: she leaned against the wall, beside his dressing-table, and seemed beset with humility and with weakness. Her head drooped. (034-095)

She tries to find a middle ground -- suggesting that perhaps marriage was not necessary, that they could continue as they had been, seeing each other daily, enjoying each other's company. But George forecloses even this: "Do you think you could go on seeing him after this!" (034-122). The question is not a question; it is a command. Eugene is to be banished from her life entirely.

That night, while George sleeps, Isabel writes two letters. The first, to Eugene, ends their relationship. The second, left on George's dressing-table, explains:

> I have been out to the mail-box, darling, with a letter I've written to Eugene, and he'll have it in the morning. It would be unfair not to let him know at once, and my decision could not change if I waited. It would always be the same... I love you better than anything and everything else on earth. God gave you to me -- and oh! how thankful I have been every day of my life for that sacred gift -- and nothing can ever come between me and God's gift. I cannot hurt you, and I cannot let you stay hurt as you have been -- not another instant after you wake up, my darling boy! It is beyond my power. (035-001)

The letter is heartbreaking in its clarity. Isabel does not pretend that she has chosen George freely; she acknowledges that the choice was made for her, by the very nature of her maternal love. "Your suffering shows how deep-seated the feeling is within you," she writes, implicitly acknowledging that George's feeling -- his possessive rage -- is the force that has determined the outcome. And she adds a small, devastating detail: "I have -- I seem to have that family trouble -- like father's -- that I spoke to you about once... Marrying doesn't mean so much, after all -- not at my age" (034-113). Isabel has a weak heart, like the Major. She does not have long to live.

## Consequences

The consequences of Isabel's decision reverberate through the remainder of the novel.

**For Isabel:** Her health declines steadily. She has, as she told George, "that family trouble" -- the heart condition that afflicts the Major. But the novel strongly implies that the emotional devastation of losing Eugene accelerates her decline. Within a few years, Isabel is dead.

**For Eugene:** His bitterness toward George becomes permanent and absolute. The narrator states it plainly:

> Nothing that Georgie would ever do in this world or the next could change Eugene Morgan's feeling toward him... His bitterness for himself might have worn away, but never his bitterness for Isabel... He had never blamed Isabel for the weakness which had cost them the few years of happiness they might have had together; he had put the blame all on the son, and it stayed there. (047-062, 047-160)

Yet Eugene's love for Isabel endures. On the train to New York, years after her death, he remembers her as she was when they first met: "the brown-eyed, brown-haired, proud, gentle, laughing girl he had known when first he came to town, a boy just out of the State College" (047-171). The memory is as vivid as if she were "just on the other side of the glass, looking in at him" (047-166).

**For George:** His victory is pyrrhic. He has preserved his mother for himself, but in doing so he has destroyed her. He has also lost Lucy Morgan -- for when Eugene tells Lucy what George has done, any possibility of reconciliation between the young lovers is extinguished. George is left, at the novel's end, broken in body (after an automobile accident) and beginning, only beginning, to understand what he has done. His hospital reverie shows him the truth he could not face: "Nothing stays or holds or keeps where there is growth... The Ambersons had passed, and the new people would pass, and the new people that came after them" (047-128). The pride that cost his mother her happiness was, in the end, defending nothing at all.

**For the Amberson family:** The episode marks the final assertion of the old Amberson pride -- and its ultimate emptiness. The Major's fortune dissipates; the Mansion is abandoned; the name fades from the city. Eugene Morgan, the man George barred from the house, becomes the new great man of Midland, his "Morgan Place" the new Amberson Mansion (047-109). History vindicates Eugene, but it is a vindication that brings no happiness.

## Literary Significance

The story of Isabel and Eugene's engagement -- both the engagement that happened (to Wilbur) and the one that did not (to Eugene) -- is Tarkington's most sophisticated treatment of the novel's master theme: the conflict between past and future, between stasis and change. Eugene Morgan, the automobile manufacturer, represents the new century -- a world of speed, innovation, and social mobility. Isabel Amberson, the proud daughter of the old aristocracy, is caught between the world she was born into and the world that Eugene offers.

Her choice to remain with George -- to sacrifice her own happiness for the sake of a son who cannot conceive of her as a person -- is the novel's definitive statement on the Amberson tragedy. The family's greatness, its pride, has become a prison. Isabel cannot escape it; Eugene cannot breach it; and George, the ultimate product of Amberson indulgence, cannot see that he is destroying what he most claims to love.

The novel offers no resolution, only endurance. Eugene's love for Isabel survives her death, survives decades, survives even the impossibility of fulfillment. George's understanding comes too late. And Isabel, the woman at the center of it all, remains what she was from the first: a woman who gave everything to her son, including the one thing that was hers alone to give.
