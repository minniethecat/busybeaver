# Pride and Arrogance

> "It's the proudest name in this town and it's going to stay the proudest; and I tell you that's the deepest thing in my nature — not that I'd expect Eugene Morgan to understand — the very deepest thing in my nature is to protect that name, and to fight for it to the last breath when danger threatens it." (034-078–034-081)

From the opening paragraphs of the novel, pride is the lens through which everything Amberson is seen. The house is "the pride of the town" (002-083); the family name is the "proudest" in the city (034-078); and Isabel Amberson carries herself with a "proud look — something that was independent and graceful and strong" (034-097). This concept traces the trajectory of Amberson pride from the source of their magnificence, through its toxic crystallization in [[george-amberson-minafer]], to the bitter penance of the final chapters — a central thread in the larger narrative of [[decline-of-the-ambersons]] and the social transformations heralded by [[the-automobile-in-the-ambersons]].

## Overview

Pride in *The Magnificent Ambersons* operates on three interconnected levels, each reinforcing the others to create what Tarkington portrays as an almost architectural edifice of self-regard.

First, there is **institutional pride** — the pride of a family that has built, named, and dominated a city. The Ambersons are not merely wealthy; they are definitional. Their Mansion is the destination of every guided tour of the town (002-103–002-107). Their Addition, with its fountains and classical statuary, is the neighborhood where every prosperous citizen longs to build. Their customs — the afternoon tea, the olive-oil-dressed salad, the purchased Saint Bernard — become templates that the rest of society strains to imitate (002-126–002-139). This is pride not as personal vanity but as civic identity: to be an Amberson is to be the standard against which all else is measured.

Second, there is **familial pride** — the fiercely guarded sanctity of the Amberson name. This is the pride that Isabel carries in her bearing, that George inherits in its most belligerent form, and that ultimately governs the plot's central crisis. When George learns that Eugene Morgan's renewed friendship with his widowed mother has become the subject of town gossip, he does not first ask whether the gossip is true; he asks what must be done to silence it. "There never was an Amberson yet that would let the Amberson name go trailing in the dust like that!" he declares (034-076–034-077). The family name is not a reputation to be managed but a sacred object to be defended against any breath of perceived dishonor, and George's entire course of action — his rudeness to Eugene, his confrontation with his mother, his self-appointment as guardian of family honor — flows from this conviction.

Third, and most subtly, there is **the pride of the past** — the conviction that being an Amberson means belonging to an order of things that is rapidly vanishing and must therefore be defended all the more strenuously. The novel is saturated with the consciousness of change: "the magnificence of the Ambersons began then" (001-002), Tarkington writes of Major Amberson's fortune, but the grammar of decline is already present. The Ambersons were "magnificent in their day" (001-005) — the past tense is deliberate. George's refusal to engage with Eugene Morgan's automobile business, his contempt for the "riffraff" who are changing the character of the town (028-020), his insistence on the old codes of honor — all of these are expressions of a pride that knows itself to be threatened by time itself.

## In the Novel

### The Architecture of Pride

The novel opens by establishing what might be called the physical manifestation of Amberson pride. Major Amberson's purchase of two hundred acres, his carving of Amberson Boulevard and Amberson Addition from the forest, his placement of classical statuary at intersections — Minerva, Mercury, Hercules, Venus — all of this is an assertion of identity through geography. "Against so homespun a background the magnificence of the Ambersons was as conspicuous as a brass band at a funeral" (002-061–002-062). The Amberson Mansion itself, with its porte-cochere, its sixty-thousand-dollar walnut woodwork, its three-story Great Dome of green glass, is not merely a house; it is a monument — and monuments are built by the proud.

This architectural pride passes into the civic folklore of the town. Citizens tell visitors that the President of the United States would trade the White House for the Mansion, and that Major Amberson would refuse the offer (002-099–002-102). The town's pride in the Ambersons is indistinguishable from the Ambersons' pride in themselves; they are the same phenomenon, mirrored. And this mirroring is crucial to the tragedy, because it means the Ambersons have never had to distinguish between their worth as people and their worth as symbols. When the symbols begin to crumble, so too must everything else.

### George Amberson Minafer: Pride Incarnate

If institutional pride is the inheritance, George Amberson Minafer is the inheritor — and the novel's most devastating study of what happens when pride is allowed to operate without check. From childhood, George is a creature of imperious self-assurance: "He's just riffraff," young Georgie says of a playmate (004-038), and the word "riffraff" will follow him through the novel as the signature of his class-bound consciousness. The narrative notes that he is given to "riding down of populace and riffraff" (028-020) as a matter of reflex.

George's pride manifests most dangerously not as active cruelty but as an absolute inability to conceive that he might be wrong. When his aunt Fanny tells him there is gossip about his mother's relationship with Eugene Morgan, George's response is not to seek the truth but to mount a campaign of silence and severance. He cuts Lucy Morgan — the woman he loves — out of his life without explanation. He confronts Mrs. Johnson, a neighbor, demanding the names of every person who has spoken the rumor (030-031–030-044). He tells his mother that if she marries Eugene Morgan, he will never "bear even to see you again" (034-107–034-109).

The novel diagnoses this behavior with clinical precision. Eugene Morgan, observing George's fury at a perceived slight, offers what is perhaps the book's most penetrating psychological insight:

> "In all my life, the most arrogant people that I've known have been the most sensitive. The people who have done the most in contempt of other people's opinion, and who consider themselves the highest above it, have been the most furious if it went against them. Arrogant and domineering people can't stand the least, lightest, faintest breath of criticism. It just kills them." (015-019–015-024)

This is the paradox at the heart of Amberson pride: the louder the claim of superiority, the more fragile the self that makes it. George's furious sensitivity is not strength but the opposite — it is the terror of a young man whose entire identity depends on the world agreeing that he is what his name says he is. The narrative notes that "however unusual the scope of George's pride, vanity of beauty was not included; he did not think about his looks" (016-202–016-203). His vanity is deeper and more dangerous: it is vanity of position, of name, of caste — the things that can be taken away.

### Isabel Amberson: The Cost of Pride

Isabel Amberson carries pride differently — quietly, gracefully, and with a willingness to sacrifice herself for it that is perhaps more tragic than George's bombast. "George was right about his mother's being proud. And even when she laughed with a negro gardener, or even those few times in her life when people saw her weep, Isabel had a proud look — something that was independent and graceful and strong" (034-095–034-097).

Isabel's pride is of a piece with her dignity. She cannot bring herself to disregard her son's wishes, even when those wishes demand that she give up the man she has loved since girlhood, the man whose proposal of marriage she now receives and must refuse. George tells her: "You are my mother and you're an Amberson — and I believe you're too proud! You're too proud to care for a man who could write such a letter as that!" (034-090–034-092). The accusation is devastating and, in its way, true — Isabel's pride will not permit her to override her son's will, even for her own happiness. But it is a pride that serves others, not herself, and that is what makes her fate the novel's deepest sorrow.

Her brother George Amberson delivers the clearest summary of the cost, in a conversation with Eugene after Isabel's death: "I'm afraid she hasn't been really well for several years" (038-062). The reader understands that Isabel's decline is not merely physical; it is the gradual extinguishing of a woman who gave up everything for the honor of a name.

### Fanny Minafer: The Pride of Exclusion

Even Fanny Minafer, the perpetual outsider within the Amberson household, is shaped by the family's pride — though in her case the dynamic is one of desperate aspiration rather than secure possession. Fanny clings to the Amberson connection because it is all the status she has. When George treats her with contempt, her lament is that she is "only old Fanny" — a person the Amberson pride has defined as beneath notice (028-096–028-114). Her tragedy is to have internalized the family's hierarchy of worth so completely that she cannot even resent it without also craving its approval.

### The Reckoning

The final chapters of the novel constitute a systematic dismantling of every structure that Amberson pride has built. The Mansion, once "the pride of the town" (002-083), is sold and slated for demolition. Isabel dies in exile. The family fortune evaporates. George, the young man who was too proud to work — "I don't work. I mean I don't work" (016-187–016-188) — is reduced to an eight-dollar-a-week job in a chemical laboratory. And he is faced with the full moral weight of what his pride has cost:

> "Whatever remnants of the old high-handed arrogance were still within him, he did penance for his deepest sin that night — and it may be that to this day some impressionable, overworked woman in a 'kitchenette,' after turning out the light will seem to see a young man kneeling in the darkness, shaking convulsively, and, with arms outstretched through the wall, clutching at the covers of a shadowy bed." (043-034–043-037)

The language of "high-handed arrogance" is deliberate: Tarkington is telling us that pride is not simply an attitude but a form of action — "high-handed" — and that its consequences are visited on the bodies and lives of other people. The penance scene is the novel's most explicit moral reckoning:

> "At least, it may be claimed for George that his last night in the house where he had been born was not occupied with his own disheartening future, but with sorrow for what sacrifices his pride and youth had demanded of others." (043-042–043-045)

The word "sacrifices" is carefully chosen. Throughout the novel, George has framed his actions in terms of sacrifice — he tells his mother, "I would, quickly enough, if I thought it necessary in a matter of honour like this" (034-009–034-010). But what George has actually sacrificed is not his own happiness, or at least not only his own. What he has sacrificed is his mother's life, Lucy's love, and the possibility of a future that is not defined by the dead weight of a family name.

### Pride and the Amberson Name

> "There never was an Amberson yet that would let the Amberson name go trailing in the dust like that! It's the proudest name in this town and it's going to stay the proudest; and I tell you that's the deepest thing in my nature — not that I'd expect Eugene Morgan to understand — the very deepest thing in my nature is to protect that name, and to fight for it to the last breath when danger threatens it, as it does now — through my mother!" (034-076–034-082)

This passage represents the apex of George's rhetoric of family honor. Note the progression: from the family name as abstract value ("proudest name"), to the personal investment ("deepest thing in my nature"), to the call to action ("fight for it to the last breath"), to the identification of the threat ("through my mother"). George constructs a syllogism of pride: the name is sacred; the name is threatened; therefore any action taken in defense of the name is justified. The emotional force of the passage — George is "striding up and down and tossing his arms about, in a tumult of gesture" (034-083–034-084) — belies the coldness of the logic.

### Arrogance and Sensitivity

> "That's one of the greatest puzzles of human vanity, dear; and I don't pretend to know the answer. In all my life, the most arrogant people that I've known have been the most sensitive. The people who have done the most in contempt of other people's opinion, and who consider themselves the highest above it, have been the most furious if it went against them. Arrogant and domineering people can't stand the least, lightest, faintest breath of criticism. It just kills them." (015-017–015-024)

Eugene Morgan speaks here to his daughter Lucy, who has asked why George — who "does anything he likes to, without any regard for what people think" — should be so "furiously" affected by the "least little thing" that reflects upon him (015-013–015-016). Eugene's answer is the novel's psychological thesis: arrogance is not strength but a form of extreme vulnerability. The passage is structurally important because it comes early, before the major crises of the plot, and establishes a framework through which the reader is meant to interpret George's subsequent behavior.

## Historical Context

### The Gilded Age and Aristocratic Pretension

The world of the Ambersons belongs to what historians call the Gilded Age — the decades after the Civil War when rapid industrialization created vast new fortunes and a self-conscious class of American aristocrats. Major Amberson's fortune was made "in 1873" (001-001), the year of a great financial panic, and the timing is suggestive: the Major profited when others lost, and his magnificence was built on the differential that such moments create.

The Ambersons' style of pride draws on distinctly European models. The classical statuary in Amberson Addition — Minerva, Mercury, Hercules, Venus, Gladiator, Emperor Augustus — is a deliberate invocation of European aristocratic taste, translated to the American Midwest. The afternoon tea ritual, the olive oil on the salad, the imported Saint Bernard — all are markers of a class reaching beyond native American traditions toward the forms of older hierarchies. Tarkington's irony is gentle but unmistakable: the citizens who "had not seen Versailles" but declared it "outdone" (002-074–002-076) are complicit in the same pretension they mock.

### The Cult of the Family Name

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw the consolidation of what might be called the "name" culture among wealthy American families — the belief that a surname carried not merely social weight but moral and even spiritual significance. The Amberson name functions in the novel as a kind of secular religion: it demands sacrifices (034-043–034-045), inspires crusades (034-078–034-082), and induces guilt when its commandments are violated (043-034–043-041). George's framing of his mother's potential remarriage as "sacrilege" (034-085) is not merely rhetorical excess; it reflects a genuine cultural logic in which family honor has been elevated to the status of the sacred.

This was also the era in which the concept of the "gentleman" underwent its most intense American negotiation. The novel is populated with references to the gentleman ideal — from the "sparkling gentleman" who courted Isabel in her youth (003-012) to George's obsession with what a gentleman "ought to do" (023-057). The gentleman code, with its emphasis on honor, restraint, and the protection of women's reputations, is the scaffolding upon which George builds his campaign against his mother's happiness. But the novel suggests that the code has curdled into something rigid and destructive — a set of rules applied without compassion or proportionality.

### The Rise of the Automobile and the Death of Old Pride

The automobile serves in the novel as the material symbol of everything that Amberson pride cannot withstand. Eugene Morgan's rise from "a man" who made "sewing-machines" (012-011) to the owner of a thriving automobile factory mirrors the ascent of a new kind of wealth — wealth based on innovation, practical skill, and the future rather than on inheritance and the past. George's contempt for automobiles — "Give me a horse, for mine, though, any day" (016-089–016-090) — is a refusal of the future, and since pride that cannot adapt is pride that must break, the automobile becomes an agent of the Ambersons' downfall.

The new Morgan mansion, "a great Georgian picture in brick, five miles north of Amberson Addition" (038-008–038-009), is the completed reversal: the new money has built what the old money once built, and the old Amberson Mansion, with its "grand old expanses of plate glass we used to be so proud of" (038-031), now looks out on "smoke and the old Johnson house, which is a counter-jumper's boardinghouse now" (038-034–038-035). Pride, Tarkington seems to say, is always vulnerable to time — and time moves faster than any pride can run.
