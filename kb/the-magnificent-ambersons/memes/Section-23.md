[023-001]“It’s not important enough to bother about, really, of course.”

[023-002]“Well, let’s wait till autumn then,” said the Major in a tone of

[023-003]relief. “We’ll see about it in the autumn, if you’re still in the mind

[023-004]for it then. That will be a great deal better. You remind me of it,

[023-005]along in September—or October. We’ll see what can be done.” He rubbed

[023-006]his hands cheerfully. “We’ll see what can be done about it then,

[023-007]Georgie. We’ll see.”

[023-008]And George, in reporting this conversation to his mother, was ruefully

[023-009]humorous. “In fact, the old boy cheered up so much,” he told her,

[023-010]“you’d have thought he’d got a real load off his mind. He seemed to

[023-011]think he’d fixed me up perfectly, and that I was just as good as

[023-012]driving a tandem around his library right that minute! Of course I

[023-013]know he’s anything but miserly; still I can’t help thinking he must

[023-014]be salting a lot of [[wealth-and-fortune|money]] away. I know prices are higher than they

[023-015]used to be, but he doesn’t spend within thousands of what he used to,

[023-016]and we certainly can’t be spending more than we always have spent.

[023-017]Where does it all go to? [[george-amberson|Uncle George]] told me grandfather had sold

[023-018]some pieces of property, and it looks a little queer. If he’s really

[023-019]‘property poor,’ of course we ought to be more saving than we are, and

[023-020]help him out. I don’t mind giving up a tandem if it seems a little too

[023-021]expensive just now. I’m perfectly willing to live quietly till he gets

[023-022]his bank balance where he wants it. But I have a faint suspicion, not

[023-023]that he’s getting miserly—not that at all—but that old age has begun

[023-024]to make him timid about money. There’s no doubt about it, he’s getting

[023-025]a little queer: he can’t keep his mind on a subject long. Right in the

[023-026]middle of talking about one thing he’ll wander off to something else;

[023-027]and I shouldn’t be surprised if he turned out to be a lot better off

[023-028]than any of us guess. It’s entirely possible that whatever he’s sold

[023-029]just went into government bonds, or even his safety deposit box. There

[023-030]was a friend of mine in college had an old uncle like that: made the

[023-031]whole family think he was poor as dirt—and then left seven millions.

[023-032]People get terribly queer as they get old, sometimes, and grandfather

[023-033]certainly doesn’t act the way he used to. He seems to be a totally

[023-034]different man. For instance, he said he thought tandem driving might be

[023-035]dangerous—”

[023-036]“Did he?” Isabel asked quickly. “Then I’m glad he doesn’t want you to

[023-037]have one. I didn’t dream—”

[023-038]“But it’s not. There isn’t the slightest—”

[023-039]Isabel had a bright idea. “Georgie! Instead of a tandem wouldn’t it

[023-040]interest you to get one of [[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s automobiles?”

[023-041]“I don’t think so. They’re fast enough, of course. In fact, running

[023-042]one of those things is getting to be quite on the cards for sport, and

[023-043]people go all over the country in ’em. But they’re dirty things, and

[023-044]they keep getting out of order, so that you’re always lying down on

[023-045]your back in the mud, and—”

[023-046]“Oh, no,” she interrupted eagerly. “Haven’t you noticed? You don’t

[023-047]see nearly so many people doing that nowadays as you did two or three

[023-048]years ago, and, when you do, Eugene says it’s apt to be one of the

[023-049]older patterns. The way they make them now, you can get at most of the

[023-050]machinery from the top. I do think you’d be interested, dear.”

[023-051]George remained indifferent. “Possibly—but I hardly think so. I know a

[023-052]lot of good people are really taking them up, but still—”

[023-053]“‘But still’ what?” she said as he paused.

[023-054]“But still—well, I suppose I’m a little old-fashioned and fastidious,

[023-055]but I’m afraid being a sort of engine driver never will appeal to me,

[023-056]mother. It’s exciting, and I’d like that part of it, but still it

[023-057]doesn’t seem to me precisely the thing a gentleman ought to do. Too

[023-058]much overalls and monkey-wrenches and grease!”

[023-059]“But Eugene says people are hiring mechanics to do all that sort of

[023-060]thing for them. They’re beginning to have them just the way they have

[023-061]coachmen; and he says it’s developing into quite a profession.”

[023-062]“I know that, mother, of course; but I’ve seen some of these mechanics,

[023-063]and they’re not very satisfactory. For one thing, most of them only

[023-064]pretend to understand the machinery and they let people break down a

[023-065]hundred miles from nowhere, so that about all these fellows are good

[023-066]for is to hunt up a farmer and hire a horse to pull the automobile. And

[023-067]friends of mine at college that’ve had a good deal of experience tell

[023-068]me the mechanics who do understand the engines have no training at all

[023-069]as servants. They’re awful! They say anything they like, and usually

[023-070]speak to members of the family as ‘Say!’ No, I believe I’d rather wait

[023-071]for September and a tandem, mother.”

[023-072]Nevertheless, George sometimes consented to sit in an automobile,

[023-073]while waiting for September, and he frequently went driving in one of

[023-074]Eugene’s cars with [[lucy-morgan|Lucy]] and her father. He even allowed himself to

[023-075]be escorted with his mother and [[fanny-minafer|Fanny]] through the growing factory,

[023-076]which was now, as the foreman of the paint shop informed the visitors,

[023-077]“turning out a car and a quarter a day.” George had seldom been

[023-078]more excessively bored, but his mother showed a lively interest in

[023-079]everything, wishing to have all the machinery explained to her. It was

[023-080]Lucy who did most of the explaining, while her father looked on and

[023-081]laughed at the mistakes she made, and Fanny remained in the background

[023-082]with George, exhibiting a bleakness that overmatched his boredom.

[023-083]From the factory Eugene took them to lunch at a new restaurant,

[023-084]just opened in the town, a place which surprised Isabel with its

[023-085]metropolitan air, and, though George made fun of it to her, in a

[023-086]whisper, she offered everything the tribute of pleased exclamations;

[023-087]and her gayety helped Eugene’s to make the little occasion almost a

[023-088]festive one.

[023-089]George’s ennui disappeared in spite of himself, and he laughed to see

[023-090]his mother in such spirits. “I didn’t know mineral waters could go to a

[023-091]person’s head,” he said. “Or perhaps it’s this place. It might pay to

[023-092]have a new restaurant opened somewhere in town every time you get the

[023-093]blues.”

[023-094]Fanny turned to him with a wan smile. “Oh, she doesn’t ‘get the

[023-095]blues,’ George!” Then she added, as if fearing her remark might be

[023-096]thought unpleasantly significant, “I never knew a person of a more

[023-097]even disposition. I wish I could be like that!” And though the tone of

[023-098]this afterthought was not so enthusiastic as she tried to make it, she

[023-099]succeeded in producing a fairly amiable effect.

[023-100]“No,” Isabel said, reverting to George’s remark, and overlooking

[023-101]Fanny’s. “What makes me laugh so much at nothing is Eugene’s factory.

[023-102]Wouldn’t anybody be delighted to see an old friend take an idea out of

[023-103]the air like that—an idea that most people laughed at him for—wouldn’t

[023-104]any old friend of his be happy to see how he’d made his idea into such

[023-105]a splendid, humming thing as that factory—all shiny steel, clicking and

[023-106]buzzing away, and with all those workmen, such muscled looking men and

[023-107]yet so intelligent looking?”

[023-108]“Hear! Hear!” George applauded. “We seem to have a lady orator among

[023-109]us. I hope the waiters won’t mind.”

[023-110]Isabel laughed, not discouraged. “It’s beautiful to see such a thing,”

[023-111]she said. “It makes us all happy, dear old Eugene!”

[023-112]And with a brave gesture she stretched out her hand to him across

[023-113]the small table. He took it quickly, giving her a look in which his

[023-114]laughter tried to remain, but vanished before a gratitude threatening

[023-115]to become emotional in spite of him. Isabel, however, turned instantly

[023-116]to Fanny. “Give him your hand, Fanny,” she said gayly; and, as Fanny

[023-117]mechanically obeyed, “There!” Isabel cried. “If brother George

[023-118]were here, Eugene would have his three oldest and best friends

[023-119]congratulating him all at once. We know what brother George thinks

[023-120]about it, though. It’s just beautiful, Eugene!”

[023-121]Probably if her brother George had been with them at the little table,

[023-122]he would have made known what he thought about herself, for it must

[023-123]inevitably have struck him that she was in the midst of one of those

[023-124]“times” when she looked “exactly fourteen years old.” Lucy served

[023-125]as a proxy for Amberson, perhaps, when she leaned toward George and

[023-126]whispered: “Did you ever see anything so lovely?”

[023-127]“As what?” George inquired, not because he misunderstood, but because

[023-128]he wished to prolong the pleasant neighbourliness of whispering.

[023-129]“As your mother! Think of her doing that! She’s a darling! And

[023-130]papa”—here she imperfectly repressed a tendency to laugh—“papa looks as

[023-131]if he were either going to explode or utter loud sobs!”

[023-132]Eugene commanded his features, however, and they resumed their

[023-133]customary apprehensiveness. “I used to write verse,” he said—“if you

[023-134]remember—”

[023-135]“Yes,” Isabel interrupted gently. “I remember.”

[023-136]“I don’t recall that I’ve written any for twenty years or so,” he

[023-137]continued. “But I’m almost thinking I could do it again, to thank you

[023-138]for making a factory visit into such a kind celebration.”

[023-139]“Gracious!” Lucy whispered, giggling. “Aren’t they sentimental”

[023-140]“People that age always are,” George returned. “They get sentimental

[023-141]over anything at all. Factories or restaurants, it doesn’t matter what!”

[023-142]And both of them were seized with fits of laughter which they managed

[023-143]to cover under the general movement of departure, as Isabel had risen

[023-144]to go.

[023-145]Outside, upon the crowded street, George helped Lucy into his runabout,

[023-146]and drove off, waving triumphantly, and laughing at Eugene who was

[023-147]struggling with the engine of his car, in the tonneau of which Isabel

[023-148]and Fanny had established themselves. “Looks like a hand-organ man

[023-149]grinding away for pennies,” said George, as the runabout turned the

[023-150]corner and into [[national-avenue|National Avenue]]. “I’ll still take a horse, any day.”

[023-151]He was not so cocksure, half an hour later, on an open road, when a

[023-152]siren whistle wailed behind him, and before the sound had died away,

[023-153]Eugene’s car, coming from behind with what seemed fairly like one long

[023-154]leap, went by the runabout and dwindled almost instantaneously in

[023-155]perspective, with a lace handkerchief in a black-gloved hand fluttering

[023-156]sweet derision as it was swept onward into minuteness—a mere white

[023-157]speck—and then out of sight.

[023-158]George was undoubtedly impressed. “Your Father does know how to drive

[023-159]some,” the dashing exhibition forced him to admit. “Of course Pendennis

[023-160]isn’t as young as he was, and I don’t care to push him too hard. I

[023-161]wouldn’t mind handling one of those machines on the road like that,

[023-162]myself, if that was all there was to it—no cranking to do, or fooling

[023-163]with the engine. Well, I enjoyed part of that lunch quite a lot, Lucy.”

[023-164]“The salad?”

[023-165]“No. Your whispering to me.”

[023-166]“Blarney!”

[023-167]George made no response, but checked Pendennis to a walk. Whereupon

[023-168]Lucy protested quickly: “Oh, don’t!”

[023-169]“Why? Do you want him to trot his legs off?”

[023-170]“No, but—”

[023-171]“‘No, but’—what?”

[023-172]She spoke with apparent gravity: “I know when you make him walk it’s so

[023-173]you can give all your attention to—to proposing to me again!”

[023-174]And as she turned a face of exaggerated color to him, “By the Lord, but

[023-175]you’re a little witch!” George cried.

[023-176]“George, do let Pendennis trot again!”

[023-177]“I won’t!”

[023-178]She clucked to the horse. “Get up, Pendennis! Trot! Go on! Commence!”

[023-179]Pendennis paid no attention; she meant nothing to him, and George

[023-180]laughed at her fondly. “You are the prettiest thing in this world,

[023-181]Lucy!” he exclaimed. “When I see you in winter, in furs, with your

[023-182]cheeks red, I think you’re prettiest then, but when I see you in

[023-183]summer, in a straw hat and a shirtwaist and a duck skirt and white

[023-184]gloves and those little silver buckled slippers, and your rose-coloured

[023-185]parasol, and your cheeks not red but with a kind of pinky glow about

[023-186]them, then I see I must have been wrong about the winter! When are you

[023-187]going to drop the ‘almost’ and say we’re really engaged?”
