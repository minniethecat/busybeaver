[047-001]He was conscious of gigantic violence; of roaring and jolting and

[047-002]concussion; of choking clouds of dust, shot with lightning, about his

[047-003]head; he heard snapping sounds as loud as shots from a small pistol,

[047-004]and was stabbed by excruciating pains in his legs. Then he became aware

[047-005]that the machine was being lifted off of him. People were gathering in

[047-006]a circle round him, gabbling.

[047-007]His forehead was bedewed with the sweat of anguish, and he tried to

[047-008]wipe off this dampness, but failed. He could not get his arm that far.

[047-009]“Nev’ mind,” a policeman said; and George could see above his eyes the

[047-010]skirts of the blue coat, covered with dust and sunshine. “Amb’lance be

[047-011]here in a minute. Nev’ mind tryin’ to move any. You want ’em to send

[047-012]for some special doctor?”

[047-013]“No.” George’s lips formed the word.

[047-014]“Or to take you to some private hospital?”

[047-015]“Tell them to take me,” he said faintly, “to the City Hospital.”

[047-016]“A’ right.”

[047-017]A smallish young man in a duster fidgeted among the crowd, explaining

[047-018]and protesting, and a strident voiced girl, his companion, supported

[047-019]his argument, declaring to everyone her willingness to offer testimony

[047-020]in any court of law that every blessed word he said was the God’s truth.

[047-021]“It’s the fella that hit you,” the policeman said, looking down on

[047-022]George. “I guess he’s right; you must of been thinkin’ about somep’m’

[047-023]or other. It’s wunnerful the damage them little machines can do—you’d

[047-024]never think it—but I guess they ain’t much case ag’in this fella that

[047-025]was drivin’ it.”

[047-026]“You bet your life they ain’t no case on me!” the young man in the

[047-027]duster agreed, with great bitterness. He came and stood at George’s

[047-028]feet, addressing him heatedly: “I’m sorry fer you all right, and I

[047-029]don’t say I ain’t. I hold nothin’ against you, but it wasn’t any more

[047-030]my fault than the statehouse! You run into me, much as I run into you,

[047-031]and if you get well you ain’t goin’ to get not one single cent out

[047-032]o’ me! This lady here was settin’ with me and we both yelled at you.

[047-033]Wasn’t goin’ a step over eight mile an hour! I’m perfectly willing to

[047-034]say I’m sorry for you though, and so’s the lady with me. We’re both

[047-035]willing to say that much, but that’s all, understand!”

[047-036]George’s drawn eyelids twitched; his misted glance rested fleetingly

[047-037]upon the two protesting motorists, and the old imperious spirit within

[047-038]him flickered up in a single word. Lying on his back in the middle

[047-039]of the street, where he was regarded by an increasing public as an

[047-040]unpleasant curiosity, he spoke this word clearly from a mouth filled

[047-041]with dust, and from lips smeared with blood.

[047-042]It was a word which interested the policeman. When the ambulance

[047-043]clanged away, he turned to a fellow patrolman who had joined him.

[047-044]“Funny what he says to the little cuss that done the damage. That’s all

[047-045]he did call him—nothin’ else at all—and the cuss had broke both his

[047-046]legs fer him and God-knows-what-all!”

[047-047]“I wasn’t here then. What was it?”

[047-048]“Riffraff!”

Chapter XXXV

[047-049][[eugene-morgan|Eugene]]’s feeling about George had not been altered by his talk with

[047-050]Kinney in the club window, though he was somewhat disturbed. He was

[047-051]not disturbed by Kinney’s hint that [[fanny-minafer|Fanny Minafer]] might be left on

[047-052]the hands of her friends through her nephew’s present dealings with

[047-053]nitroglycerin, but he was surprised that Kinney had “led up” with

[047-054]intentional tact to the suggestion that a position might be made

[047-055]for George in the [[morgans-automobile-factory|Morgan factory]]. Eugene did not care to have any

[047-056]suggestions about [[george-amberson-minafer|Georgie Minafer]] made to him. Kinney had represented

[047-057]Georgie as a new Georgie—at least in spots—a Georgie who was proving

[047-058]that decent stuff had been hid in him; in fact, a Georgie who was doing

[047-059]rather a handsome thing in taking a risky job for the sake of his aunt,

[047-060]poor old silly Fanny Minafer! Eugene didn’t care what risks Georgie

[047-061]took, or how much decent stuff he had in him: nothing that Georgie

[047-062]would ever do in this world or the next could change Eugene Morgan’s

[047-063]feeling toward him.

[047-064]If Eugene could possibly have brought himself to offer Georgie a

[047-065]position in the automobile business, he knew full well the proud devil

[047-066]wouldn’t have taken it from him; though Georgie’s proud reason would

[047-067]not have been the one attributed to him by Eugene. George would never

[047-068]reach the point where he could accept anything material from Eugene and

[047-069]preserve the self-respect he had begun to regain.

[047-070]But if Eugene had wished, he could easily have taken George out of

[047-071]the nitroglycerin branch of the chemical works. Always interested in

[047-072]apparent impossibilities of invention, Eugene had encouraged many

[047-073]experiments in such gropings as those for the discovery of substitutes

[047-074]for gasoline and rubber; and, though his mood had withheld the

[047-075]information from Kinney, he had recently bought from the elder Akers

[047-076]a substantial quantity of stock on the condition that the chemical

[047-077]company should establish an experimental laboratory. He intended to buy

[047-078]more; Akers was anxious to please him; and a word from Eugene would

[047-079]have placed George almost anywhere in the chemical works. George need

[047-080]never have known it, for Eugene’s purchases of stock were always quiet

[047-081]ones: the transaction remained, so far, between him and Akers, and

[047-082]could be kept between them.

[047-083]The possibility just edged itself into Eugene’s mind; that is, he let

[047-084]it become part of his perceptions long enough for it to prove to him

[047-085]that it was actually a possibility. Then he half started with disgust

[047-086]that he should be even idly considering such a thing over his last

[047-087]cigar for the night, in his library. “No!” And he threw the cigar into

[047-088]the empty fireplace and went to bed.

[047-089]His bitterness for himself might have worn away, but never his

[047-090]bitterness for Isabel. He took that thought to bed with him—and it was

[047-091]true that nothing George could do would ever change this bitterness of

[047-092]Eugene. Only George’s mother could have changed it.

[047-093]And as Eugene fell asleep that night, thinking thus bitterly of

[047-094]Georgie, Georgie in the hospital was thinking of Eugene. He had come

[047-095]“out of ether” with no great nausea, and had fallen into a reverie,

[047-096]though now and then a white sailboat staggered foolishly into the small

[047-097]ward where he lay. After a time he discovered that this happened only

[047-098]when he tried to open his eyes and look about him; so he kept his eyes

[047-099]shut, and his thoughts were clearer.

[047-100]He thought of Eugene Morgan and of the Major; they seemed to be the

[047-101]same person for awhile, but he managed to disentangle them and even

[047-102]to understand why he had confused them. Long ago his grandfather had

[047-103]been the most striking figure of success in the town: “As rich as

[047-104][[major-amberson|Major Amberson]]!” they used to say. Now it was Eugene. “If I had Eugene

[047-105]Morgan’s [[wealth-and-fortune|money]],” he would hear the workmen day-dreaming at the chemical

[047-106]works; or, “If Eugene Morgan had hold of this place you’d see things

[047-107]hum!” And the boarders at the table d’hôte spoke of “the Morgan Place”

[047-108]as an eighteenth-century Frenchman spoke of Versailles. Like his uncle,

[047-109]George had perceived that the “Morgan Place” was the new Amberson

[047-110]Mansion. His reverie went back to the palatial days of the Mansion, in

[047-111]his boyhood, when he would gallop his pony up the driveway and order

[047-112]the darkey stable-men about, while they whooped and obeyed, and his

[047-113]grandfather, observing from a window, would laugh and call out to him,

[047-114]“That’s right, Georgie. Make those lazy rascals jump!” He remembered

[047-115]his gay young uncles, and how the town was eager concerning everything

[047-116]about them, and about himself. What a clean, pretty town it had been!

[047-117]And in his reverie he saw like a pageant before him the magnificence of

[047-118]the Ambersons—its passing, and the passing of the Ambersons themselves.

[047-119]They had been slowly engulfed without knowing how to prevent it, and

[047-120]almost without knowing what was happening to them. The family lot,

[047-121]in the shabby older quarter, out at the cemetery, held most of them

[047-122]now; and the name was swept altogether from the new city. But the new

[047-123]great people who had taken their places—the Morgans and Akerses and

[047-124]Sheridans—they would go, too. George saw that. They would pass, as the

[047-125]Ambersons had passed, and though some of them might do better than the

[047-126]Major and leave the letters that spelled a name on a hospital or a

[047-127]street, it would be only a word and it would not stay forever. Nothing

[047-128]stays or holds or keeps where there is growth, he somehow perceived

[047-129]vaguely but truly. Great Caesar dead and turned to clay stopped no hole

[047-130]to keep the wind away. Dead Caesar was nothing but a tiresome bit of

[047-131]print in a book that schoolboys study for awhile and then forget. The

[047-132]Ambersons had passed, and the new people would pass, and the new people

[047-133]that came after them, and then the next new ones, and the next—and the

[047-134]next—

[047-135]He had begun to murmur, and the man on duty as night nurse for the ward

[047-136]came and bent over him.

[047-137]“Did you want something?”

[047-138]“There’s nothing in this family business,” George told him

[047-139]confidentially. “Even George Washington is only something in a book.”

[047-140]Eugene read a report of the accident in the next morning’s paper. He

[047-141]was on the train, having just left for New York, on business, and with

[047-142]less leisure would probably have overlooked the obscure item:

[047-143]LEGS BROKEN

[047-144]G. A. Minafer, an employee of the Akers Chemical Co., was run

[047-145]down by an automobile yesterday at the corner of Tennessee and

[047-146]Main and had both legs broken. Minafer was to blame for the

[047-147]accident according to patrolman F. A. Kax, who witnessed the

[047-148]affair. The automobile was a small one driven by Herbert Cottleman

[047-149]of 9173 Noble Avenue who stated that he was making less than 4

[047-150]miles an hour. Minafer is said to belong to a family formerly of

[047-151]considerable prominence in the city. He was taken to the City

[047-152]Hospital where physicians stated later that he was suffering from

[047-153]internal injuries besides the fracture of his legs but might

[047-154]recover.

[047-155]Eugene read the item twice, then tossed the paper upon the opposite

[047-156]seat of his compartment, and sat looking out of the window. His feeling

[047-157]toward Georgie was changed not a jot by his human pity for Georgie’s

[047-158]human pain and injury. He thought of Georgie’s tall and graceful

[047-159]figure, and he shivered, but his bitterness was untouched. He had never

[047-160]blamed Isabel for the weakness which had cost them the few years of

[047-161]happiness they might have had together; he had put the blame all on the

[047-162]son, and it stayed there.

[047-163]He began to think poignantly of Isabel: he had seldom been able to

[047-164]“see” her more clearly than as he sat looking out of his compartment

[047-165]window, after reading the account of this accident. She might have been

[047-166]just on the other side of the glass, looking in at him—and then he

[047-167]thought of her as the pale figure of a woman, seen yet unseen, flying

[047-168]through the air, beside the train, over the fields of springtime green

[047-169]and through the woods that were just sprouting out their little leaves.

[047-170]He closed his eyes and saw her as she had been long ago. He saw the

[047-171]brown-eyed, brown-haired, proud, gentle, laughing girl he had known

[047-172]when first he came to town, a boy just out of the State College. He

[047-173]remembered—as he had remembered ten thousand times before—the look she

[047-174]gave him when her brother George introduced him to her at a picnic; it

[047-175]was “like hazel starlight” he had written her, in a poem, afterward.

[047-176]He remembered his first call at [[amberson-hotel|the Amberson]] Mansion, and what a great

[047-177]personage she seemed, at home in that magnificence; and yet so gay and

[047-178]friendly. He remembered the first time he had danced with her—and the

[047-179]old waltz song began to beat in his ears and in his heart. They laughed
