# Kosekin Culture

## Definition

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] are a fictional people inhabiting a subterranean world near the South Pole, discovered by the English narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]] in James De Mille's *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder* (1888). Their defining characteristic is a complete inversion of nineteenth-century Western values: they love darkness, worship death, despise wealth, and elevate poverty and self-abnegation to the highest social ideals (016-001).

The term "Kosekin" derives from the people's own self-designation (016-001). Linguistically, the Kosekin language bears systematic relationships to Hebrew, identifiable through Grimm's Law consonant shifts — for example, Kosekin "Op" corresponds to Hebrew "Oph," and Kosekin "[[athon-class|Athon]]" to Hebrew "Adon" (017-071). This linguistic evidence leads the frame-narrator's companions to speculate that the Kosekin may be one of the lost Ten Tribes of Israel (017-069).

## In the Kosekin World

### The Inversion Principle

Every aspect of Kosekin society is governed by an inversion of conventional values. Where Western culture prizes life, the Kosekin "love death as we love life" (016-005). Where Westerners seek wealth, the Kosekin regard riches as a curse and poverty as the highest blessing (016-008). This systematic reversal serves De Mille's satirical purpose: by holding up a mirror in which every Western value appears backwards, the novel questions the arbitrary nature of social conventions.

The inversion extends to the most intimate human relationships. When two people fall in love, each insists on surrendering the beloved to a rival; "the more passionate the love is, the more eager is the desire to have the loved one married to someone else" (016-006). Poets celebrate unhappy love, defeats rather than victories, and the lowest laborers rather than the powerful (016-006).

### Social Hierarchy

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] social order is a precise mirror image of Victorian class structure. The paupers form the most honorable and envied class, while the wealthy constitute the unfortunate masses (016-008). The hierarchy descends from pauper (highest) through laborers, artisans, farmers, merchants, authors, and finally to Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s — the rulers who occupy the lowest rung (016-015). This inversion means that the officers of state "have the command and the charge of affairs as with us; yet this is consistent with their position, for here to obey is considered nobler than to command" (016-015).

A stark consequence of this system is that "capital punishment is imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues" (016-011). What Western society considers the highest reward — luxury and power — the Kosekin deploy as the ultimate punishment.

### Economy of Self-Denial

Economic life among the Kosekin is driven by a perpetual effort to give away wealth rather than accumulate it. "Each one tries not to make money, but to lose it; but as the competition is sharp and universal, this is difficult, and the larger portion are unsuccessful" (016-015). Workers refuse adequate pay and insist on maximal labor, so that "the wealth thus created is pushed from these incessantly till it all falls upon the lowest class — namely, the rich" (016-015).

Crime is correspondingly redefined. "Instead of robbers, the Kosekin punished the secret bestowers of their wealth on others" (016-010). Murder has its counterpart when one person forces money on another and then kills himself (016-010). Only about two percent of the population ever succeeds in reaching the envied pauper class (016-015).

### Gender Relations

Women and men hold "absolutely equal" status, "holding precisely the same offices and doing the same work" (016-015). Women serve in the army and navy, and from their "lack of ambition or energetic perseverance" — which in the Kosekin value system is a virtue — they are "usually relegated to the lower ranks, such as officers and generals" (016-015). In the household, husbands insist on giving everything to their wives, with the result that wives "are therefore universally the rulers of the household while the husbands have an apparently subordinate, but, to the Kosekin, a more honorable position" (016-015).

### Religion

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] belief system centers on a "world of darkness" that awaits after death. "The death they long for leads to the darkness that they love; and the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss" (016-016). They are "eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land," though the narrator finds this indistinguishable from "a state of nothingness" (016-016). The central religious ceremony is the "[[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]]" or "[[mista-kosek|Feast of Darkness]]," a ritual involving [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|human sacrifice]] that horrifies the English narrator (013-009).

## Key Passages

> These people call themselves the Kosekin. Their chief characteristic, or, at least, their most prominent one, is their love of darkness, which perhaps is due to their habit of dwelling in caves. Another feeling, equally strong and perhaps connected with this, is their love of death and dislike of life. (016-001)

> For it must never be forgotten that the Kosekin love death as we love life; and this accounts for all those ceremonies which to me were so abhorrent, especially the scenes of the Mista Kosek. (016-005)

> The paupers here form the most honorable class. Next to these are the laborers. These have strikes as with us; but it is always for harder work, longer hours, or smaller pay. The contest between capital and labor rages, but the conditions are reversed. (016-008)

> There are among the Kosekin the unfortunate many who are cursed with wealth, and the fortunate few who are blessed with poverty. These walk while the others ride, and from their squalid huts look proudly and contemptuously upon the palaces of their unfortunate fellow-countrymen. (016-008)

> Among the Kosekin capital punishment is imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues; for that which we consider the highest they regard as the lowest. (016-011)

> Women and men are in every respect absolutely equal, holding precisely the same offices and doing the same work. In general, however, it is observed that women are a little less fond of death than men, and a little less unwilling to receive gifts. For this reason they are very numerous among the wealthy class. (016-015)

## Analysis

De Mille's Kosekin society functions as a sustained Swiftian satire targeting multiple dimensions of Western culture. Most directly, the inversion of wealth and poverty critiques Victorian capitalism and class structure: by showing a society where the wealthy are pitied and paupers envied, De Mille exposes the arbitrary moral weight attached to economic status.

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] love of death parodies religious asceticism and the Christian glorification of martyrdom. By making death-worship literal and extending it to every dimension of social life, the satire reveals the psychological contradictions in a value system that treats suffering as virtue. The narrator's horror at the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] ceremony mirrors the Western reader's own discomfort at discovering that their society's deepest values, when taken to their logical conclusion, produce results as alien as those of the Kosekin.

The linguistic connection between Kosekin and Hebrew — through Grimm's Law consonant shifts — adds a layer of anthropological satire, mocking nineteenth-century obsessions with racial and linguistic theories of cultural origins. The suggestion that the Kosekin are descended from the lost tribes of Israel (017-069) both parodies contemporary biblical anthropology and implies that the inverted values of the Kosekin may be closer to Western religious roots than the reader wishes to acknowledge.
