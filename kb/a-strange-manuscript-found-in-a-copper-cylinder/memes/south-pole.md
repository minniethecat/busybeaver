# South Pole

The **South Pole** serves as the primary geographical setting of James De Mille's *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder*. Contrary to the frozen wasteland of common imagination, the novel's South Pole contains a warm, habitable volcanic region accessible through a subterranean passage — a world "by itself" inhabited by the Kosekin people.

## Geographic Features

The narrator Adam More and his companion Agnew are carried toward the South Pole by a powerful southward ocean current while sailing near the Antarctic Circle. Rather than encountering ice and desolation, they discover an opening at the pole leading to a vast underground sea [003-063]. The polar region is described as flat and temperate — "how different from that terrible, that iron land on the other side of the mountains!—not a world of ice and frost, but one of fire and warmth" [006-008].

> "Here, then, was the South Pole — a world by itself: and how different from that terrible, that iron land on the other side of the mountains!—not a world of ice and frost, but one of fire and warmth." [006-008]

## Climate

The climate at the South Pole is notably mild, sustained by subterranean volcanic activity. De Mille explicitly contrasts the expected polar severity with the observed warmth: observations of the region indicate that "at the South Pole it is different" — mild rather than severe [007-067]. This inversion of expectation is a recurring theme throughout the novel.

> "As far as I have observed it, the climate severe. But at the South Pole it is different." [007-067]

## Inhabitants

The warm polar region is home to the Kosekin, a subterranean race with a unique culture organized around the principle of inverted values — poverty as honor, wealth as shame. The South Pole setting thus functions not merely as exotic geography but as the foundation for the novel's satirical exploration of social values [017-010].

## Narrative Significance

The South Pole setting serves multiple narrative functions. It isolates the protagonists from familiar civilization, provides a plausible mechanism for the discovery of a hidden world (the southward current), and establishes the novel's pattern of systematic inversion — even the climate defies expectations. The frame narrator's incredulity ("Who ever dreamed of people living at the South Pole—and in a warm climate, too?") mirrors the reader's response [007-004].
