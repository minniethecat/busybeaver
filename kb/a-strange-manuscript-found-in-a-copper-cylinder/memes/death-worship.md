# Death-Worship (Kosekin)

## Definition

Death-worship is the foundational religious principle of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]], defined by the conviction that death is the highest blessing a person can attain. As the narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]] observes: "For it must never be forgotten that the Kosekin love death as we love life; and this accounts for all those ceremonies which to me were so abhorrent" (016-005). This principle is not a marginal cult or esoteric doctrine — it is the "master-passion" that animates every Kosekin institution, from family life to warfare, from criminal justice to religious ritual.

The love of death is "connected with" the Kosekin love of darkness (016-001), and the two together form their eschatology: after death, the Kosekin believe they enter a "world of darkness" where "the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss" (016-016). The pursuit of this bliss shapes every conscious choice a Kosekin makes.

## The Master-Passion

### Death as Desire

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] orientation toward death is not passive acceptance but active, passionate longing. "Each one hates life and longs for death" (016-001). This desire is so powerful that it explains the entire fabric of social behavior: "This desire for death is, then, a master-passion, and is the key to all their words and acts" (016-005). [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] "are terrible in battle" because of their "passionate love of death" (016-012), which eliminates the fear that constrains ordinary soldiers.

### The Logic of Self-Sacrifice

The love of death "leads to perpetual efforts on the part of each to lay down his life for another" (016-009). This creates a paradoxical dynamic in which the highest altruism — giving one's life — is simultaneously the pursuit of one's own deepest desire. The self-sacrificing Kosekin who dies for another achieves both the moral ideal of self-denial and the personal goal of death, making the act doubly gratifying.

This logic produces dangerous practical consequences. "Confined prisoners dare not fly, for in such an event the guards kill themselves" (016-009). If a prisoner persists in escaping and is recaptured, they receive escalating "punishments" of luxury and honor until, as a last resort, "he is solemnly put to death, not, as with us, by way of severity, but as the last and greatest honor" (016-009). The prisoner who achieves this "lofty destiny" is somewhat embittered only by knowing that their family must now be "doomed, not, as with us, to poverty and want, but, on the contrary, to boundless wealth and splendor" (016-009).

## Social Manifestations

### Nursing the Sick

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] attitude toward illness is one of the most striking illustrations of death-worship in daily life. "Among the Kosekin the sick are objects of the highest regard. All classes vie with one another in their attentions" (016-004). Those suffering from contagious diseases are the most sought-after of all, "for in waiting on these there is the chance of gaining the blessing of death" (016-004). The practical result is a rush of volunteer nurses so intense that "much trouble is usually experienced from the rush of those who insist on offering their services" at the bedside of the contagiously ill (016-004).

When the sick die, those around them "rejoice, since death is a blessing," yet nurses "labor hard to preserve them in life, so as to prolong the enjoyment of the high privilege of nursing" (016-004). The incurable are the most honored patients precisely because they require perpetual care.

### Ambivalence in Battle

In warfare, the love of death produces the paradoxical result that Kosekin armies are both eager to die and almost invariably victorious. "The object of each army is to honor the other and benefit it by giving it the glory of defeat. The contest is thus most fierce" (016-012). [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] fight with lethal effectiveness because they want to confer honor on their enemies by losing; their very desire for defeat makes them win (016-012).

This produces a distinctive emotional state: "If they are defeated they rejoice, since defeat is their chief glory; but if they are victorious they rejoice still more in the benevolent thought that they have conferred upon the enemy the joy, the glory, and the honor of defeat" (016-012). Either outcome is emotionally satisfying, making the Kosekin soldier both fearless and unbeatable.

### Crime and Capital Punishment

The love of death transforms criminal justice into its mirror image. Murder, in the Kosekin sense, occurs when one person "forces money on him, and kills himself" — thus burdening the victim with both wealth and the obligation of revenge-giving (016-010). Capital punishment is "imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues" — because the Kosekin deploy as punishment exactly what Western society considers the highest reward (016-011). Actual death is reserved "for desperate cases" as "the last and greatest honor" (016-009), placing lethal punishment beyond the reach of all but the most incorrigible offenders.

## Religious Dimension

### Eschatology of Darkness

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] "believe that after death they go to what they call the world of darkness" (016-016). In this world, "the death they long for leads to the darkness that they love," producing a unified state of bliss. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] are described as "eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land" (016-016).

The narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]], representing the rational Western perspective, finds this eschatology hollow: "for my own part, it always seemed to me a state of nothingness" (016-016). This observation is a key satirical moment — the Kosekin organize their entire civilization around the pursuit of what the narrator sees as annihilation, mirroring the way Western religious devotion might appear to an outsider who does not share its premises.

### The Mista Kosek

The love of death finds its ritual culmination in the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]], or [[mista-kosek|Feast of Darkness]], a ceremony of public [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|human sacrifice]] that horrifies the narrator (013-001–035). The [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] embodies death-worship in its most concentrated form: a community gathering where death is not merely contemplated but enacted, and where the victims go to their fate as participants in a shared celebration of the darkness they love.

### The Chief Pauper's Longing

The Chief Pauper — the highest magistrate among the Kosekin — expresses death-worship as a personal melancholy. Listing his blessings of "poverty, squalor, cold, perpetual darkness, the privilege of killing others, the near prospect of death, and the certainty of the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]]," he confesses that despite possessing all these, "after all, I am not happy" (029-003). His unhappiness is framed not as doubt about the Kosekin value system but as a personal failure to achieve satisfaction — a satirical mirror of the wealthy Westerner who, possessing everything, still feels empty.

## Satirical Significance

Death-worship is De Mille's most radical satirical device. By constructing a society whose members long for death as passionately as Westerners cling to life, the novel forces readers to confront the arbitrary nature of their own deepest values. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] death-worshiper is not a monster but a mirror: their devotion to death is as reasoned, institutionalized, and emotionally authentic as Western devotion to life.

The satire operates at multiple levels. It parodies Christian asceticism and the glorification of martyrdom: the Kosekin who kill themselves to benefit others literalize the Christian ideal of self-sacrificial love. It mocks the death-drive in Victorian culture — the sentimental cult of mourning, the romanticization of consumption and early death. And it exposes the paradox at the heart of any value system: that what a culture calls "good" is ultimately a matter of collective belief, not objective truth.

The narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s recurring horror at [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin customs]] is itself part of the satire. His inability to see his own culture's death-related practices — war, capital punishment, religious martyrdom — as equally strange to an outsider reveals the blind spot at the center of cultural self-understanding.

## Key Passages

> For it must never be forgotten that the Kosekin love death as we love life; and this accounts for all those ceremonies which to me were so abhorrent, especially the scenes of the Mista Kosek. To them a dead human body is no more than the dead body of a bird: there is no awe felt, no sense of sanctity, of superstitious horror; and so I learned, with a shudder, that the hate of life is a far worse thing than the fear of death. This desire for death is, then, a master-passion, and is the key to all their words and acts. (016-005)

> The love of death leads to perpetual efforts on the part of each to lay down his life for another. This is a grave difficulty in hunts and battles. Confined prisoners dare not fly, for in such an event the guards kill themselves. (016-009)

> The Kosekin, through their passionate love of death, are terrible in battle; and when they are also animated by the desire to confer glory on their enemies by defeating them, they generally succeed in their aim. This makes them almost always victorious, and when they are not so not a soul returns alive. (016-012)

> Among the Kosekin the sick are objects of the highest regard. All classes vie with one another in their attentions. The rich send their luxuries; the paupers, however, not having anything to give, go themselves and wait on them and nurse them. (016-004)

> They believe that after death they go to what they call the world of darkness. The death they long for leads to the darkness that they love; and the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss. (016-016)
