## Definition

The **Jom** is the Kosekin word for a day, explicitly identified as "the division of time corresponding to a day" [009-011]. It is one of the most frequently used Kosekin terms in the narrative, appearing across nearly every chapter of the middle and latter portions of the story. The narrator, Adam More, adopts the term early in his captivity and uses it interchangeably with "day" for the remainder of the manuscript.

## In the Kosekin World

The jom structures the rhythm of Kosekin life in the subterranean world. It marks the interval between Layelah's daily visits to crown the narrator with flowers [009-016], the scheduling of sacrifices and ceremonies, and the countdown to significant events. The Kosekin measure time in joms just as surface-dwellers measure time in days:

> "Every jom I must come here and crown thee with flowers," said she. "It is my duty." [009-016]

The plural form "joms" is used for spans of multiple days. The narrator counts the passage of his captivity, voyages, and periods of waiting in joms, demonstrating the term's integration into his own mental framework.

## Key Passages

> "So, why, you may come the next jom." [009-010]

This is the first use of "jom" in the narrative, spoken by Layelah during her initial visit to Adam More. The narrator immediately explains the term to the reader.

> This meant the next day, jom being the division of time corresponding to a day. [009-011]

The narrator's explicit gloss establishes the term for the reader and marks the beginning of his linguistic acculturation.

> "In a few more joms the dark season will begin, and then we should be destroyed before one jom." [011-010]

Layelah uses joms to count down to the dark season, linking the Kosekin time unit to their seasonal calendar.

> Each jom as it came and went brought us nearer to our fate. [020-013]

The jom becomes the narrator's measure of impending doom, showing how thoroughly the Kosekin time unit has replaced his own.

> Our voyage occupied several joms; but our progress was continuous. [018-020]

The term is applied to travel and navigation, demonstrating its versatility as a general temporal measure.

> Many joms passed, and there was only one jom now before the great sacrifice. [029-002]

Joms mark the countdown to the climactic sacrifice, heightening narrative tension through temporal precision.

## Analysis

The jom serves both a linguistic and narrative function. Linguistically, it is part of the Semitic-flavored Kosekin vocabulary that the narrator notes as "a strange compound of Hebrew and some other language" — though no direct Hebrew cognate for "jom" exists (*yom* means "day" in Hebrew, but the narrator does not make this connection explicitly). The term signals the cultural otherness of the Kosekin while remaining transparent enough that the reader easily assimilates it.

Narratologically, the narrator's shift from "day" to "jom" tracks his psychological transformation. Early chapters use "day" exclusively; by the middle chapters, "jom" predominates. This linguistic code-switching mirrors the narrator's gradual acceptance of — and even preference for — Kosekin values and customs over his English ones.
