# Athon Class

## Definition

The Athon class occupies an intermediate position in the Kosekin social hierarchy — wealthy enough to be pitied by those above them, but not yet wealthy enough to have reached the degrading heights of the Melek and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] classes. The Athons form part of the "unfortunate many who are cursed with wealth" (016-008), in contrast to "the fortunate few who are blessed with poverty." The term "Athon" is itself a Kosekin word that [[oxenden|Oxenden]] later connects to the Hebrew "Adon" (lord) through Grimm's Law (017-071).

The narrator, [[adam-more|Adam More]], experiences the Athon class from within when he is involuntarily promoted through the Kosekin social ranks. He records with dismay: "I became a capitalist, an Athon, a general officer, and finally a [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]]" (015-059). Each promotion represents, from the Kosekin perspective, a degradation — the acquisition of wealth and power that drives the unfortunate upward (or, in Western terms, downward) through the social hierarchy.

## Position in the Social Hierarchy

The Athon class is one of several wealthy classes arrayed below the honored paupers. The full hierarchy descends from pauper through laborer, artisan, farmer, merchant, and author to Athon, then to Melek, and finally to [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] (016-014). The Athons thus occupy the first rung of the "lowest class — namely, the rich" (016-014), forming the boundary between the productive classes and the purely wealthy.

The Athons are not merely passive victims of their wealth. They actively seek to divest themselves of it, participating in the same competitive economy of self-impoverishment that characterizes all Kosekin economic life. Yet the narrator reports that "this is difficult, and the larger portion are unsuccessful" (016-015), suggesting that even the desire to be poor cannot overcome the structural forces that push wealth upward through the social hierarchy.

## Linguistic Significance

The term "Athon" carries special weight in the novel's linguistic subplot. When [[oxenden|Oxenden]] demonstrates the connection between Kosekin and Hebrew through Grimm's Law, "Athon" is one of his key examples: "Kosekin 'Athon,' Hebrew 'Adon'" (017-071). This correspondence supports the broader argument that the Kosekin are a Semitic people — perhaps the lost tribes of Israel — whose language has undergone systematic consonant shifts over millennia of isolation.

## The Athon as Cautionary Figure

For the narrator, the prospect of becoming an Athon is a source of terror. Each elevation in Kosekin social status brings him closer to the dreaded honor of the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] sacrifice, as the status of sacrificial victim is reserved for those of highest rank. The Athon class thus represents not merely social disgrace but an existential threat: the wealthier one becomes, the more likely one is to be selected for the blessing of death.
