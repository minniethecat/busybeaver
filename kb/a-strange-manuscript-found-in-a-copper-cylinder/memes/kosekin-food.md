## Definition

**Kosekin Food and Subsistence** refers to the dietary practices, food sources, and culinary customs of the Kosekin people, including their reliance on fishing and marine resources from the underground sea, their ceremonial feasting traditions, and the social meaning attached to food distribution. Consistent with Kosekin cultural inversion, the provision of food — an act of service — carries high honor.

## In the Kosekin World

The Kosekin diet draws primarily from the resources of the underground sea. Fishing is a major subsistence activity, with the narrator observing boats equipped for "fishing purposes" [004-005] and a variety of marine life including fish and wild-fowl. The first meal the narrator receives from the Kosekin consists of roasted fowl with a "savory odor" and a flavor "rather fishy" [004-025], suggesting a diet in which seafood predominates.

Food in Kosekin society carries deep social significance. The act of providing food to others is an honor, consistent with the Kosekin principle that service and giving confer status rather than receiving. When the narrator is first brought to the Kosekin, the immediate provision of food is a gesture of welcome and honor:

> If they wanted to harm us, would they feast us in this style? [004-031]

Feasting is central to Kosekin ceremonial life. The dark season festival — the most important event in the Kosekin calendar — is inaugurated with a great communal feast:

> The event of the dark season was to be celebrated with a feast. [013-009]

The narrator describes the preparations for this feast in detail, including "cooking the food of this banquet" [013-035] at large-scale cooking facilities. The feast is attended by a "vast multitude" [013-035] and marks the beginning of the dark season festivities.

The largest feasts follow significant public events. After a major ceremony, the narrator notes that it was "followed by the greatest of feasts" [030-002], indicating that feasting scales with the importance of the occasion. These banquets serve as communal gatherings that reinforce social bonds and the Kosekin value of shared privation.

Beyond feasting, daily food distribution follows the Kosekin inversion: "Some, indeed, may be forced to receive enough for food and clothing" [016-011] — the language of compulsion ("forced to receive") reflects how Kosekin consider accepting provisions to be a burden rather than a benefit, while those who give food away earn honor.

Food sources in the subterranean world include fish, birds, and possibly cultivated plants. The narrator mentions "the birds of the air" [011-009] as a food source, and trees are noted in the landscape. The underground sea provides the bulk of protein through fishing, with fish species apparently adapted to the lightless environment.

## Key Passages

> A savory odor came through the cave, and a repast was spread before us. The fare consisted of some kind of roasted fowl, the flavor of which was not at all that of a wild-goose, but rather fishy. Still to us it seemed delicious. [004-025]

The narrator's first Kosekin meal reveals key aspects of their cuisine: roasted preparation, marine-influenced flavors, and generous hospitality.

> If they wanted to harm us, would they feast us in this style? Nonsense. [004-031]

Agnew's rhetorical question captures the Kosekin practice of honoring guests with abundant food — a form of service that, in their inverted value system, confers status on the host.

> The event of the dark season was to be celebrated with a feast. [013-009]

Feasting is institutionally linked to the most important Kosekin seasonal observance.

> This was followed by the greatest of feasts. [030-002]

The scale of Kosekin feasting escalates with civic importance, culminating in massive communal banquets.
