## Overview

The Kosekin are a subterranean people dwelling in a vast network of caverns beneath the Antarctic region, discovered by the narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]] after his descent through a volcanic passage. Their name, self-designated, appears throughout the narrative as the collective identity of a civilization organized around systematic value inversions. Chapter 16 of the manuscript provides the most concentrated ethnographic account, framed as [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s observational report on a people whose every institution appears to him as a grotesque mirror of Victorian society.

> These people call themselves the Kosekin. Their chief characteristic, or, at least, their most prominent one, is their love of darkness, which perhaps is due to their habit of dwelling in caves. [016-001]

## Physical Characteristics

### Adaptation to Darkness

The Kosekin have evolved or adapted to permanent subterranean life. Their most distinctive physical trait is extreme photophobia — they blink painfully in sunlight and navigate best in near-total darkness:

> I saw also that from the nature of their eyes the sunlight distressed them, and in this cavern gloom they found their most congenial dwelling-place. … It was a puzzle to me whether the weakness of their eyes had caused this dislike of light, or the habit of cave-dwelling had caused this weakness of eyes. [008-001]

In the darkness of the caverns, their vision functions as well as [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s does in daylight — a complete adaptive reversal:

> Here, in this darkness, where there was but a faint twinkle from the feeble lamps, their eyes seemed to serve them as well as mine did in the outer light of day. [008-001]

### Contrast with Almah

The character of [[almah|Almah]] provides a striking physical contrast to the general Kosekin population. She is described as taller, lighter-complexioned, and with large dark eyes that do not shrink from light — suggesting she may belong to a different racial stock or caste:

> Her face and form, but especially her eyes, showed her to be of quite a different race from these others. … Her complexion was much lighter; her hair was dark, luxuriant, and wavy. … her eyes … were large, dark, liquid, with long lashes, and with a splendid glow in their lustrous depths. [008-004]

## Habitat and Lifestyle

### Cavern Civilization

The Kosekin inhabit an immense system of caverns, organized into subterranean streets and passages. The darkness is punctuated only by a few twinkling lamps:

> After going a little farther we came to a long, broad passage-way like a subterranean street, about twenty feet in width, and as many in height. Here there were discernible a few twinkling lamps, which served to make the darkness less intense. [008-001]

Surface travel, when necessary, is conducted along roads shaded by giant ferns, minimizing exposure to sunlight. The Kosekin have constructed an entire civilization adapted to darkness — from architecture to transportation to social rhythms.

### Time-Keeping Without Sun

Despite the absence of diurnal sunlight, the Kosekin maintain regular cycles of waking and sleeping through unspecified means:

> Here in these caverns there was no difference between day and night, but, by modes of which I was ignorant, a regular succession was observed of waking times and sleeping times. [008-018]

## Core Value System

### The Master-Passion: Love of Death

The Kosekin worldview is organized around a single governing principle — the love of death — from which all other inversions derive:

> This desire for death is, then, a master-passion, and is the key to all their words and acts. They rejoice over the death of friends, since those friends have gained the greatest of blessings; they rejoice also at the birth of children, since those who are born will one day gain the bliss of death. [016-005]

Because death is the greatest good, anything associated with death (darkness, poverty, self-denial) becomes desirable, while anything associated with life (light, wealth, self-interest) becomes contemptible. This single axiom generates the entire Kosekin cultural logic.

### Systematic Inversions

The Kosekin value system inverts virtually every Western norm:

| Domain | Western Norm | Kosekin Inversion |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Death/Life | Life is good | Death is the supreme blessing |
| Light/Dark | Light is good | Darkness is loved |
| Wealth/Poverty | Wealth is good | Poverty is the highest honor |
| Self/Other | Self-interest first | Self-sacrifice for others |
| Command/Obey | To command is honor | To serve is the highest glory |
| Victory/Defeat | Victory is glory | Defeat is the greatest honor |
| Love/Marriage | Marry the beloved | Give the beloved to another |

## Social Organization

The Kosekin maintain a complex, literate civilization with clearly defined institutions: a priesthood (the [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s), a governing council (the [[the-epet|Epet]]), a military, a merchant marine, a legal system, and a class hierarchy — all organized on inverted principles. The [[kosekin-social-classes|class structure]] places paupers at the apex of society and the wealthy at the bottom. [[kosekin-crimes|Crime]] is defined as the bestowal of benefits, and punishment consists of increased wealth and luxury.

## Gender Relations

Women and men are described as "in every respect absolutely equal, holding precisely the same offices and doing the same work" [016-015]. However, women are observed to be slightly less ardent in their love of death and less resistant to receiving gifts, which results in their overrepresentation among the wealthy (i.e., lower-status) classes and in administrative offices — an ironic equality in which formal parity produces systematically inverted outcomes.

## Language

The Kosekin language is described as "full of gutturals and broad vowels" [008-006]. A more euphonious variant is spoken by [[almah|Almah]]. The Kosekin language follows [[grimms-law|Grimm's Law in reverse]], a linguistic curiosity noted by the narrator — where sound changes operate in the opposite direction from Indo-European languages, mirroring the broader cultural inversion pattern.

## Narrative Significance

The Kosekin serve as De Mille's vehicle for satirizing Victorian society. By constructing a civilization that is the systematic negative of nineteenth-century England, the novel exposes the contingency of Western values — suggesting that what Victorians took as natural (the love of life, the pursuit of wealth, self-interest) is merely one possible configuration among others. The Kosekin are not simply "opposites"; they are a fully realized society with internal logic, dissenting factions, and structural contradictions — making them one of the more sophisticated utopian/dystopian inventions in nineteenth-century fiction.
