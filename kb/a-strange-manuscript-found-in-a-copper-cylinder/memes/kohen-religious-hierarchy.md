## Definition

The [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] religious hierarchy is the central governing institution of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]], headed by the [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]] ([[kohen-gadol|High Priest]]) and underpinned by a doctrine of death-worship and self-sacrifice. The term "[[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]" — a direct borrowing from the Hebrew word for priest — signals the author's satirical intent: the [[kohen-priesthood|Kosekin priesthood]] inverts Judeo-Christian values while retaining the formal structure of a priestly caste. The hierarchy's authority extends beyond religious ritual into every aspect of Kosekin life, from the scheduling of the sacred hunt to the administration of [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|human sacrifice]].

## In the Kosekin World

At the apex of the hierarchy stands the [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]], who exercises absolute authority over ritual and moral life. The narrator encounters him as a figure of "melancholy" serenity [011-022], whose personal gentleness contrasts starkly with the violence he orchestrates. The [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] presides over the "sacred hunt" [010-001], commands the sacrificial ceremonies atop the pyramid [012-005], and serves as the ultimate interpreter of Kosekin doctrine. His "inexorable" nature [015-001] — his refusal to exempt the narrator from the sacrificial system despite personal friendship — illustrates the hierarchy's doctrinal rigidity. The priesthood's power structure extends downward through lesser functionaries who manage specific rituals, including the "[[separation-ceremony|Ceremony of Separation]]" [029-001] and the administration of the prison system. The hierarchy's moral authority is founded on the [[kosekin-value-system|Kosekin inversion]] of values: what surface-dwellers consider evil (death, poverty, darkness) the priesthood elevates as sacred.

## Analysis

The [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] hierarchy functions as a satire of organized religion, particularly of the kind of doctrinal absolutism that prioritizes theological consistency over human compassion. The narrator's relationship with the [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] follows a tragic arc: initially viewing him as a kind host, More gradually realizes that the [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]'s personal warmth cannot override his institutional role. This tension between personal virtue and systemic cruelty mirrors contemporary critiques of religious institutions in Victorian England. The use of Hebrew terminology ("[[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]") suggests a specific target: the author draws a satirical parallel between [[love-and-death|Kosekin death-worship]] and certain interpretations of Christian sacrificial theology. The hierarchy's comprehensive social control — from the sacred hunt through the judicial system to the "Day of Sacrifice" [030-001] — presents religion as a total social system, anticipating later sociological theories of religion.
