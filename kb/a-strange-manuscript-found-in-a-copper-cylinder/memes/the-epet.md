## Overview

**Epet** is a Kosekin title meaning "servant." In the inverted value system of the Kosekin, service to others is the highest virtue, and therefore the title of servant is among the most honored social distinctions. The term epitomizes the principle that "to obey is considered nobler than to command" (016-013).

> He was proud of his title of "Epet," which means servant, and more than usually obliging. (022-009)

## Etymology and Usage

The word "Epet" appears in the Kosekin language as a title of honor. Unlike Western societies, where "servant" connotes low social status, among the Kosekin the designation marks its bearer as occupying a position of privilege — one permitted to serve others, which is the highest aspiration.

The narrator, [[adam-more|Adam More]], encounters an Epet in Chapter 22 who serves as the Feeder of the [[athaleb|Athaleb]]s — a role analogous to a stable-hand or groom in Western society, but which "here indicated high rank and honor" (022-009).

## Related Terms

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] word "sepet-ram" (meaning "rod of thunder," applied to [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s rifle) shares the "-epet" root, though the etymological connection is not explicitly established in the text. The [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] in Chapter 10 first uses this term:

> He showed some curiosity about my rifle, which he called a sepet-ram, or "rod of thunder." (010-023)

## Social Significance

The title "Epet" illustrates the Kosekin principle of inverted social valuation. In a society where the highest ranks are paupers and the lowest are rulers, the title of "servant" naturally occupies a position of esteem. The Feeder of [[athaleb|Athaleb]]s who bears the title is described as "more than usually obliging" — his eagerness to serve exemplifying Kosekin virtue.

The Epet's unquestioning obedience to [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s commands demonstrates the Kosekin ideal: a servant who responds to requests "with perfect coolness, as though they conveyed the most natural request in the world" (022-018).

## The Epet in the Escape: A Step-by-Step Exploitation

The Epet's role in facilitating the protagonists' escape from the Kosekin city provides the most detailed illustration of Kosekin servitude in action — and its vulnerability to exploitation by those who do not share its values. The episode unfolds as a series of escalating requests, each met with immediate compliance.

### Harnessing the Athaleb

When Adam More requests to see the athalebs, the Epet's response is instantaneous and proactive:

> "Oh yes," said the Epet, and without waiting for any further request he proceeded to pull at the loose fan-like wing of the nearest one. The monster drew himself together, gave a flutter with his wings, and then moved back from the wall. (022-011)

The Epet guides the creature with minimal effort — "still holding the athaleb's wing, pulled at him, and thus guided him toward the portal" (022-015) — in a display of expert handling that the narrator compares to the docility of an elephant led by a child:

> A child may lead a vast elephant with ease, and here with equal ease the Epet led the athaleb. (022-015)

### The Harnessing

When More then requests the athaleb be harnessed, the Epet complies without question, though the narrator's mind races with anxiety:

> So the Epet proceeded to harness the athaleb, and I watched him in silence; but it was the silence of deep suspense, and my heart throbbed painfully. There was yet much to be risked. The gates had to be opened. Others might interfere. Layelah might come. (022-019)

The contrast is sharp: the Epet works with serene efficiency while the narrator trembles with the fear of discovery. The harnessing itself is "simple and soon performed" (022-019) — the collar secured, the grapple attached, the reins fastened to the wings — and yet "the time seemed long" to the fugitives.

### The Final Requests

When the athaleb refuses to move, More must return to the Epet for instruction:

> I had once more to apply for help to the Epet. (022-024)

The Epet provides the simple command: "Pull at the collar to make him start, and pull at both reins to make him stop" (022-026). Throughout the entire interaction, the Epet "stood waiting for further orders" (022-020) after each completed task — the embodiment of perfect Kosekin obedience.

### The Satirical Mechanism

The escape episode functions as a controlled experiment in cultural collision. The Epet, operating entirely within Kosekin norms, cannot conceive that a request for an athaleb ride might mask an intention to flee. His virtue — eager, unquestioning service — is precisely what makes him useful to those who exploit it. The satirical point extends beyond the Kosekin: De Mille suggests that any moral system that values obedience above discernment creates subjects who can be weaponized against their own society by anyone willing to speak the language of that system.

## Role in the Escape

The Epet (Feeder of the [[athaleb|Athaleb]]s) plays a crucial narrative role as the unwitting facilitator of [[adam-more|Adam More]] and [[almah|Almah]]'s escape. His Kosekin virtue — eager service without question — enables the protagonists to harness the [[athaleb-class|athalebs]] and flee toward [[magones|Magones]]:

> The Epet heard my words with perfect coolness … and then proceeded to obey me, just as at home a servant might hear and obey his master, who might say, "I should like to take a ride; will you harness the bay mare?" (022-018)

This episode exemplifies the satirical dimension: the Kosekin compulsion to serve makes them exploitable by anyone who does not share their values.
