## Definition

The **Kosekin social class system** is the hierarchical organization of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]], structured as a complete inversion of conventional class systems. In [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]], wealth is despised and poverty is revered; therefore, the poorest members of society — the paupers — occupy the highest social rank, while the wealthiest — including the ruling classes of [[athon-class|Athon]]s, Meleks, and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s — are considered the lowest and most unfortunate classes.

The narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]] initially misreads the Kosekin hierarchy, assuming that those in positions of visible authority and wealth must be the elite. Only gradually does he discover that "the Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s, whom I at first considered the highest, are really the lowest orders" [016-013].

## In the Kosekin World

### The Inverted Ladder

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] class hierarchy, from highest (most honored) to lowest (most despised), is:

1. **Paupers** — the "aristocratic few," "greatly envied," who "walk while the others ride, and from their squalid huts look proudly and contemptuously upon the palaces of their unfortunate fellow-countrymen" [016-008]
2. **Laborers** — those who perform physical work, honored for their direct contribution to others' welfare
3. **Artisans** — skilled workers, slightly less honored than laborers
4. **Farmers** — food producers, who create wealth that flows upward to burden the wealthy
5. **Merchants** — traders who, despite their best efforts to lose money, "grow rich in spite of their utmost endeavors" [016-014]
6. **Authors** — intellectuals and writers, considered above merchants but below the true elite
7. **Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s** — the ruling and priestly classes, who hold "the lowest orders" [016-013]
8. **[[athon-class|Athon]]s** — the wealthiest class, bearing the heaviest burden of riches

### The Rarity of Pauperdom

Attaining the pauper class is extraordinarily difficult. More reports that "it has been calculated that only two per cent in every community succeed in reaching the pauper class" [016-014]. This exclusivity makes paupers the equivalent of a hereditary aristocracy — envied, emulated, and politically powerful.

The difficulty arises from the systemic flow of wealth: "The workmen being the creators of wealth, and refusing to take adequate pay, cause a final accumulation of the wealth of the community in the hands of the mass of the non-producers, who thus are fixed in their unhappy position, and can hope for no escape except by death" [016-014].

### Class Conflict

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] experience class conflict, but with inverted dynamics. The wealthy masses murmur against the "obstinacy of the paupers" who refuse to accept gifts. "Secret movements are sometimes set on foot which aim at a redistribution of property and a levelling of all classes, so as to reduce the haughty paupers to the same condition as the mass of the nation" [016-008].

Revolutionary movements have been attempted — but their goal is to *force wealth onto the paupers*, not to take it from the rich. When these movements fail, the "unhappy conspirators have been condemned to increased wealth and luxury, while the leaders have been made Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s" [016-008] — promotion to the ruling class being the Kosekin equivalent of a prison sentence.

### Occupational Prestige

The inversion extends to specific occupations. "Poets here among the Kosekin celebrate defeats instead of victories... Poets also celebrate street-sweepers, scavengers, lamp-lighters, laborers, and above all, paupers, and pass by as unworthy of notice the authors, Meleks, and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s of the land" [016-006].

In the military, "the privates here are highest in rank, and the officers come next in different graduations" [016-013]. However, the officers still command — "for here to obey is considered nobler than to command" [016-013], so the officer's position of issuing orders is consistent with their lower status.

### Labor Relations

The contest between capital and labor "rages, but the conditions are reversed; for the grumbling capitalist complains that the laborer will not take as much pay as he ought to, while the laborer thinks the capitalist too persistent in his efforts to force money upon him" [016-007].

Laborers have strikes "as with us; but it is always for harder work, longer hours, or smaller pay" [016-007]. The goal of collective action is not to improve conditions but to worsen them — to increase the laborers' contribution and decrease their compensation, thereby elevating their social standing.

## Key Passages

> Here among the Kosekin the wealthy class forms the mass of the people, while the aristocratic few consist of the paupers. [016-008]

The concise formulation of the inversion: the many are the wealthy, the few are the poor — exactly reversing the distribution of class in Western societies.

> Happy the aristocratic, the haughty, the envied paupers! [016-013]

More's ironic exclamation captures the genuine Kosekin sentiment: paupers are not pitied but admired.

> The paupers have shown no mercy in their hour of triumph; they have not conceded one jot to the public demand, and the unhappy conspirators have been condemned to increased wealth and luxury. [016-008]

The language of class warfare is preserved but inverted: paupers show "no mercy" by *refusing* to accept wealth, and punishment takes the form of enforced riches.

> The highest wish of each is to serve. The Meleks and Kohens, whom I at first considered the highest, are really the lowest orders. [016-013]

More corrects his initial misreading, revealing that the apparently powerful are actually the socially degraded.

## Analysis

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] class system is the novel's most thoroughgoing satirical construction. By inverting every element of class hierarchy while preserving its structural logic — competition for status, class conflict, occupational prestige, elite exclusivity — the narrative demonstrates that social hierarchy is not a natural order but a contingent construction that could, in principle, be organized on entirely different principles.

The system functions as a critique of Victorian class ideology on multiple levels. It exposes the arbitrariness of equating wealth with virtue by presenting a society in which the opposite equation holds with equal internal consistency. It satirizes the Victorian cult of philanthropy by showing a society in which giving is a form of aggression. And it mocks the romanticization of poverty in Christian and artistic discourse by literalizing it: in the Kosekin world, poverty genuinely is the highest state, and the consequences are absurd rather than edifying.

The "two per cent" statistic [016-014] is a particularly sharp satirical detail — it mirrors the actual concentration of wealth among a small elite in capitalist societies, but here the elite are the poor rather than the rich, suggesting that any system of extreme inequality, regardless of which direction it points, produces an entrenched oligarchy.
