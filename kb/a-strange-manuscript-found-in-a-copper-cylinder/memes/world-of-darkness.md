# World of Darkness

## Definition

The World of Darkness is the Kosekin conception of the afterlife — a realm of eternal darkness into which the faithful pass through death. It represents the ultimate expression of the [[kosekin-value-system|Kosekin inversion]] of values: where nearly every human culture imagines paradise as a realm of light, the Kosekin paradise is defined by the complete absence of light. The narrator records that the Kosekin "are eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land" (016-016), though he himself finds this vision indistinguishable from annihilation.

The concept unifies the two poles of Kosekin theology — darkness and death — into a single eschatological vision. "The death they long for leads to the darkness that they love; and the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss" (016-016). This formulation reveals the internal coherence of Kosekin belief: every value converges on the World of Darkness as the ultimate destination and reward.

## Darkness as Beatitude

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] equation of darkness with happiness challenges the most fundamental sensory and symbolic associations of human experience. For surface-dwelling humans, darkness connotes danger, ignorance, and evil; for the Kosekin, it is the medium of the sacred. Their "love of darkness, which perhaps is due to their habit of dwelling in caves" (016-001), has been elevated from environmental adaptation to cosmic principle.

The World of Darkness is described as a "sunless land" — a formulation that would strike a Western reader as dystopian but which, for the Kosekin, names the essence of paradise. The absence of the sun, which surface cultures worship as the source of life and goodness, is for the Kosekin the defining feature of beatitude. This inversion of solar symbolism represents one of De Mille's most thorough acts of imaginative worldbuilding.

## The Narrator's Skepticism

[[adam-more|Adam More]], the English narrator who records Kosekin beliefs, remains unconvinced by their eschatology. He observes that the happiness the Kosekin describe in the World of Darkness seems to him "nothing more than a state of nothingness" (016-016) — unconsciousness rather than bliss. His skepticism introduces a note of rationalist critique into the narrative, suggesting that the Kosekin afterlife may be an elaborate cultural construction masking a void.

This skepticism is characteristic of the novel's multi-layered approach to belief. The frame-narrative characters debate the authenticity of More's account, the narrator himself questions Kosekin theology, and the reader is left to navigate between competing interpretive frameworks. The World of Darkness may be a genuine metaphysical reality within the novel's fiction, or it may be a cultural delusion — De Mille never resolves this ambiguity.

## Thematic Significance

The World of Darkness completes the [[kosekin-value-system|Kosekin inversion]] by extending it into eternity. If death is the supreme good and darkness the supreme condition, then an afterlife of eternal darkness is the logical culmination of the Kosekin value system. The concept serves both as worldbuilding — filling out the theological infrastructure of the Kosekin world — and as satire, exposing the conventional nature of Western assumptions about the afterlife by constructing a paradise that is its exact opposite.
