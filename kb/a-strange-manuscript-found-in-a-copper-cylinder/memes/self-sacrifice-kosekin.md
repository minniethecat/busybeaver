## Definition

**Self-sacrifice** in Kosekin culture is the ultimate expression of the society's foundational value inversion. Rooted in the "love of death" that characterizes all Kosekin behavior, self-sacrifice is the "passionate self-denial" and "incessant effort to benefit others at their own expense" that drives every member of Kosekin society [016-001]. It is not the exception but the norm — a universal aspiration that governs individual conduct, family relations, warfare, and religious practice.

The concept encompasses both voluntary death for others and the broader practice of self-abnegation: giving away one's possessions, refusing honors, seeking the lowest social position, and ultimately welcoming death as "the greatest of blessings" [016-005].

## In the Kosekin World

### The Master-Passion

Self-sacrifice emerges directly from the Kosekin's metaphysical orientation toward death: "Each one hates life and longs for death. He, therefore, hates riches, and all things that are associated with life" [016-001]. Because life itself is contemptible and death is desirable, sacrificing one's life or well-being for another is not a cost but a reward.

The narrator observes with horror that this desire for death "is a master-passion, and is the key to all their words and acts" [016-005]. Every aspect of Kosekin behavior — their economic system, their family structure, their military strategy — flows from this single principle.

### Self-Sacrifice in War and Hunting

The most dramatic manifestations of self-sacrifice occur in collective violence. On the sacred hunt, More witnesses "the horrible cruelty, the needless slaughter, the mad self-sacrifice" that results in half the hunting party being killed [010-???]. The Kohen, who survives, greets More with "a melancholy smile" born not of grief for the dead but of disappointment "simply and solely because he himself had not gained his death."

In warfare, the self-sacrificial impulse makes the Kosekin "terrible in battle" [016-012]. Soldiers compete to die for the enemy, viewing defeat — which confers "the joy, the glory, and the honor of defeat" upon the opposing force — as the highest objective. A Kosekin army that fails to achieve defeat "returns not a soul alive" [016-012], having succeeded in its true aim.

### Self-Sacrifice as Social Control

The universal desire for self-sacrifice creates perverse incentives in law enforcement. "Confined prisoners dare not fly, for in such an event the guards kill themselves" [016-009]. A prisoner who escapes causes the guards to achieve the death they desire, burdening the escapee with guilt and triggering harsher punishment in the form of "fresh luxuries and increased splendors" upon recapture [016-009].

If a prisoner persists in escaping and is repeatedly recaptured, the ultimate sanction is applied: "he is solemnly put to death, not, as with us, by way of severity, but as the last and greatest honor" [016-009]. Death becomes simultaneously the highest reward and the final punishment — a convergence that More finds bewildering.

### The Kohen's Self-Denial

The Kohen embodies self-sacrifice as personal discipline. When curious about More's rifle, the Kohen "whose self-denial was always most wonderful to me, at once checked his curiosity" [004-???]. The ability to suppress personal desire — even the desire to handle a dangerous weapon that might accidentally grant him death — marks the Kohen's spiritual attainment.

## Key Passages

> Each one hates life and longs for death. He, therefore, hates riches, and all things that are associated with life. It leads to a passionate self-denial, an incessant effort to benefit others at their own expense. [016-001]

The foundational statement of the Kosekin ethos: the hatred of life generates self-sacrifice, and self-sacrifice generates the entire social structure.

> For it must never be forgotten that the Kosekin love death as we love life; and this accounts for all those ceremonies which to me were so abhorrent, especially the scenes of the Mista Kosek. [016-005]

More explicitly connects the Kosekin love of death to specific ritual practices, emphasizing that the emotional orientation is the key to understanding everything that follows.

> These people were the most gentle, the most self-sacrificing, and the most generous in the world; yet their strange and unnatural love of death made them capable of endless atrocities. [010-???]

The paradox at the heart of the Kosekin character: the same impulse that produces tenderness and generosity also produces what More perceives as atrocity. The novel refuses to resolve this tension.

> The love of death leads to perpetual efforts on the part of each to lay down his life for another. [016-009]

The self-sacrificial impulse as a universal social force, creating a society in which everyone is simultaneously trying to die for everyone else.

## Analysis

The Kosekin self-sacrifice ethic serves as the novel's most sustained satirical critique of Christian self-denial and Victorian moral sentiment. By taking the Christian ideal of self-sacrifice to its logical conclusion — a society where everyone competes to sacrifice for everyone else — the narrative exposes the absurdity that would result if such values were universally and sincerely held.

The paradox More repeatedly encounters — that the "most gentle, the most self-sacrificing, and the most generous" people are also "capable of endless atrocities" — functions as a critique of moral absolutism. A value taken to its extreme, without counterbalancing principles, produces outcomes indistinguishable from its opposite. The Kosekin love of death leads them to kill with as little compunction as other societies show in preserving life.

The concept also prefigures twentieth-century critiques of totalitarian ideologies that glorify self-sacrifice for the collective. The Kosekin's willingness to die — and to kill — in service of their inverted values anticipates the dark side of utopian thinking that would become a central theme in dystopian literature.
