## Overview

**Agnew** is the second mate of the ship from which [[adam-more|Adam More]] is separated in the opening chapters. He serves as More's sole companion through the Antarctic drift, the discovery of the volcanic passage, and the descent into the subterranean world. His defining characteristic is an unshakeable optimism that contrasts with More's despair — a narrative function that makes his eventual loss all the more devastating.

## Biography

Agnew is introduced as the second mate who, alongside [[adam-more|Adam More]], proposes the seal-hunting expedition that sets the entire narrative in motion:

> Upon this I proposed to Agnew, the second mate, that we should go ashore, shoot some seals, and bring them back. This was partly for the excitement of the hunt, and partly for the honor of landing in a place never before trodden by the foot of man. [002-028]

When the storm separates them from the ship, Agnew and More drift together for days in the Antarctic ocean, surviving on seal meat and melted ice. Throughout this ordeal, Agnew maintains a deliberate and self-conscious optimism that sustains both men:

> "There's no use rowing any more," said Agnew. "If it runs south we can't resist it. It's too strong. But I always like to look on the bright side, and so I believe it runs north. In that case there is no use rowing, for it will carry us along fast enough." [002-038]

He persistently argues they are drifting north toward warmer waters, even when all evidence points south — a willed optimism he explicitly acknowledges as a survival strategy:

> "Mad?" said he; "I should certainly go mad if I hadn't hope." [003-014]

> "Oh, well," said he, "enjoy your despair, and don't try to deprive me of my consolation. My hope sustains me, and helps me to cheer you up. It would never do, old fellow, for both of us to knock under." [003-016]

## Character Traits

### The Optimist

Agnew embodies the role of the hopeful companion in adversity literature. His optimism is presented not as naivety but as a conscious philosophical choice — a deliberate strategy against despair. [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s narration frames this as a source of both comfort and exasperation:

> So we drifted for some days, Agnew all the time maintaining that we were going north, while I was sure that we were going south. [002-040]

### Humanity and Decency

When the castaways discover the body of a dead sailor on the volcanic shore, Agnew's response reveals his essential decency. It is he who initiates the burial:

> "More," said he, "do you remember any of the burial-service?" [003-031]

He then locates crumbled pumice-stone to scoop a grave, and together the two men give the unknown sailor a Christian burial [003-036–037].

## Death

Agnew does not survive the subterranean passage. [[adam-more|Adam More]] awakens in the polar basin alone, and Agnew's fate is implied rather than depicted — he is lost in the darkness of the channel, his absence marking the transition from the survival narrative of the early chapters to the solitary exploration of the Kosekin world.

His death serves a crucial narrative function: it isolates [[adam-more|Adam More]] completely in an alien civilization, removing the last companion who shared his Western values and making More's cultural disorientation absolute.

## Relationship with Adam More

Agnew's role as counterpoint to More is established through dialogue. Where More is analytical and prone to despair, Agnew is instinctive and determinedly hopeful. Their debates about their direction (north vs. south) dramatize the tension between evidence-based pessimism and willed optimism.

Agnew's loss is significant partly because it happens offstage — More awakens alone, with no farewell, no burial, and no closure. This abrupt disappearance deepens the novel's sense of radical dislocation.
