## Overview

The Troglodytes are a classical people invoked by Oxenden in the frame narrative of the manuscript as a comparative case for understanding the Kosekin. Drawing on the ancient historian Agatharcides of Cnidos, Oxenden argues that the historical Troglodytes — a Semitic, cave-dwelling people of the Red Sea region — provide a crucial "connecting link" between the Kosekin and their presumed Semitic brethren in the northern hemisphere [^017-080].

## Oxenden's Argument

Oxenden presents the Troglodyte comparison as part of his broader theory of Kosekin Semitic origins. He identifies several parallels:

**Cave-dwelling**: The Troglodytes "all lived in caverns, which were formed partly by art and partly by nature" and "lived in great communities in caverns, and they had long tunnels passing from one community to another" [^017-080]. This directly mirrors the Kosekin subterranean civilization.

**Semitic affiliation**: The Troglodytes "belonged to the Arabian race, and were consequently a Semitic people" [^017-080], which Oxenden presents as a point "of the utmost importance" for establishing the Kosekin connection.

**Attitudes toward death**: Oxenden draws particular attention to Troglodyte burial customs, citing Agatharcides's account that "in their burials they were accustomed to fasten the corpse to a stake, and then gathering round, to pelt it with stones amid shouts of laughter and wild merriment" [^017-081]. He also notes they "used to strangle the old and infirm, so as to deliver them from the evils of life" [^017-081]. While Oxenden concedes this is "not exactly loving death," he argues it approximates Kosekin attitudes and represents a cultural antecedent.

## Agatharcides's Account

The primary classical source Oxenden cites is Agatharcides of Cnidos, identified as "the earliest writer who mentions the Troglodytes" [^017-081]. According to this account, the Troglodytes were chiefly herdsmen who consumed cattle flesh and a mixture of milk and blood, dressed in cattle skins, tattooed their bodies, and were renowned for their swiftness in hunting. They were also "greatly given to robbery," preying on passing caravans [^017-081].

## Narrative Function

The Troglodyte discussion serves several functions within the frame narrative:

**Scholarly credibility**: By invoking classical sources and historical parallels, Oxenden attempts to lend academic legitimacy to the manuscript's account, situating it within the discourse of 19th-century comparative anthropology.

**Racial theorizing**: The Troglodyte-Kosekin connection reflects the period's preoccupation with racial classification and diffusionist theories of cultural transmission, with the Semitic race cast as the carrier of civilization across hemispheres.

**Metafictional depth**: The scholarly debate about Troglodytes adds a layer of metafictional complexity, reminding readers that the Kosekin account is being filtered through the interpretive frameworks of the frame narrative's Victorian gentlemen.

## Melick's Satirical Extension

Melick later parodies Oxenden's Troglodyte theory with a deliberately absurd extension: "Your friends the pterodactyls probably lingered longest among the Troglodytes, who, seeing that they were rapidly dying out, concluded to depart to another and a better world" [^026-036]. He sarcastically adds that this theory "cannot possibly be disproved" and "satisfies all the requirements of the case" [^026-036] — a pointed critique of the unfalsifiability of Oxenden's comparative method.
