## Definition

The **Dark Season** is the six-month period of continuous darkness that alternates with the Light Season in the subterranean world of the Kosekin. During this period, the sun does not rise above the volcanic crater rim that forms the Kosekin sky, plunging the entire civilization into perpetual night. The dark season is not merely an astronomical phenomenon but the central organizing principle of Kosekin ceremonial life.

## In the Kosekin World

The dark season is the most culturally significant period in the Kosekin calendar. Far from being dreaded — as the narrator initially expects — it is welcomed and celebrated:

> It was the advent of the dark season, and this was the occasion of a feast. [013-009]

The Kosekin regard the dark season as their natural element, perfectly aligned with their inverted value system. While the light season brings the discomfort of the sun, the dark season returns them to the darkness they prefer:

> The arrival of the dark season brought only additional gloom, yet for the Kosekin this was the happiest time of the year. [013-002]

The dark season culminates in the most important ritual of Kosekin society: the great sacrifice at the amir, in which the Chief Pauper of the season is selected for the honor of death.

## Key Passages

> "In a few more joms the dark season will begin, and then we should be destroyed before one jom." [011-010]

Layelah warns Adam More of the urgency posed by the approaching dark season, linking it to the sacrificial calendar.

> This was the dark season, and, as I had already learned, it was a time of general gloom in external nature, but of joyous excitement among the Kosekin. [012-002]

The narrator explicitly notes the inversion: environmental gloom produces social joy.

> It was the advent of the dark season, and this was the occasion of a feast. From what I knew of the Kosekin, I was sure that this feast celebrated the arrival of a welcome season. [013-009]

The feast celebrating darkness underscores the cultural inversion.

> They are to be kept until the end of the dark season, and then we were both to be sacrificed. [018-001]

Adam More and Almah learn they are reserved as sacrificial victims for the dark season's climactic ceremony — the highest "honor" in Kosekin culture.

> It was the end of the dark season. Then, as the sun rises for its six months' course, a great festival is held. [030-002]

The dark season's conclusion marks the moment of liberation for the protagonists, as the seasonal sacrifice becomes the vehicle for their escape.

## Seasonal Sacrifice

The dark season is inextricably linked to the Kosekin sacrificial system. The Chief Pauper — the individual selected for the highest "honor" of death — is chosen each season:

> "At the end of the dark season there is the great sacrifice. It is at the amir. We must go there." [014-031]

The sacrifice at the dark season's end is the climactic event of the Kosekin year. The entire society gathers at the amir to witness and participate in the ritual, which involves not only the Chief Pauper but also the sacrifice of victims from among the most honored members of society.

## Contrast with Light Season

The dark season and light season form a binary pair central to Kosekin cosmology:

| Aspect | Light Season | Dark Season |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Duration | Six months | Six months |
| Kosekin attitude | Endured with patience | Celebrated with joy |
| Activities | Quiet, subdued | Feasting, ceremonies, sacrifice |
| Sacrificial status | Preparatory | Climactic (great sacrifice) |
| Narrator's experience | Relative calm | Mounting dread |

> The light season had almost ended. The sun had sunk beneath the mountain crest, and the season of darkness would end. [030-007]

The cyclical alternation of light and dark structures the entire temporal framework of the subterranean world.

## Analysis

The dark season is a masterstroke of satirical inversion. In Western culture, darkness is associated with evil, fear, and death — yet among the Kosekin, darkness is the celebrated norm and light the uncomfortable aberration. This seasonal inversion serves as the temporal dimension of the novel's broader value inversion: the Kosekin world is literally and figuratively upside-down.

The dark season also serves a narrative function as the ticking clock of the plot. The protagonists' plight is measured against the progression of the dark season — they learn of their fate at its beginning [011-010], suffer through its duration [018-001], and make their escape at its climax [030-002]. The season becomes the structural backbone of the novel's second half.
