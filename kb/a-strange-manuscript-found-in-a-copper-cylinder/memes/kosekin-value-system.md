# Kosekin Value System

## Definition

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] value system is the organizing moral framework of the subterranean civilization discovered by [[adam-more|Adam More]] in James De Mille's *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder*. It operates as a complete and systematic inversion of nineteenth-century Western values: every moral, social, and economic principle is reversed, so that "that which we consider the highest they regard as the lowest" (016-011). This inversion is not arbitrary or superficial — it is relentlessly applied to every dimension of Kosekin life, from romantic love to warfare, from crime to commerce, from family structure to state politics.

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] themselves articulate their value system as a doctrine of contempt for earthly things: "State politics here move, like individual affairs, upon the great principle of contempt for earthly things" (016-012). Yet this "contempt" produces a fully functioning civilization capable of agriculture, trade, naval warfare, and complex governance — demonstrating that the Kosekin inversion is not chaos but an alternative order with its own internal coherence.

## Core Inversions

### Death over Life

The foundational axiom of the Kosekin value system is that "the Kosekin love death as we love life" (016-005). This single principle explains the entire fabric of their society. Each person "hates life and longs for death," which leads to "a passionate self-denial, an incessant effort to benefit others at their own expense" (016-001). The desire for death is "a master-passion" and "the key to all their words and acts" (016-005).

This death-worship manifests in paradoxical behaviors. They "rejoice over the death of friends, since those friends have gained the greatest of blessings," yet simultaneously "rejoice also at the birth of children, since those who are born will one day gain the bliss of death" (016-005). The sick — particularly those with contagious diseases — are the most sought-after members of society, because nursing them offers the chance of contracting the illness and thereby gaining "the blessing of death" (016-004).

### Darkness over Light

Connected to the love of death is an equally fervent love of darkness. "Their chief characteristic, or, at least, their most prominent one, is their love of darkness, which perhaps is due to their habit of dwelling in caves" (016-001). [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] religion centers on a promised "world of darkness" where "the death they long for leads to the darkness that they love; and the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss" (016-016). Throughout the novel, characters speak of darkness with the reverence that Western religion reserves for light, and the extinguishing of lamps is a sacred act (029-031–032).

### Poverty over Wealth

The economic inversion is the most elaborately detailed aspect of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]]. "The paupers here form the most honorable class" (016-007), while the wealthy constitute the unfortunate masses "cursed with wealth" (016-008). The ideal Kosekin life is one of progressive divestment: each person tries "not to make money, but to lose it" (016-015). Workers refuse adequate pay and insist on maximal labor, so that wealth accumulates against their will in the hands of the wealthy class, who bear it as a "burden too heavy to be borne" (016-015).

### Self-Denial over Self-Interest

Self-denial is not merely admired but competitively practiced. "Each one tries not to make money, but to lose it; but as the competition is sharp and universal, this is difficult, and the larger portion are unsuccessful" (016-015). This creates an economy of reversed incentives: "Each one tries not to take as much pay as he ought to," "the workmen being the creators of wealth, and refusing to take adequate pay, cause a final accumulation of the wealth of the community in the hands of the mass of the non-producers" (016-015). Only about two percent of the population ever succeeds in reaching the envied pauper class (016-015).

### Obedience over Authority

Social hierarchy is inverted: "Here to obey is considered nobler than to command" (016-013). The Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s — who appear to be the ruling class — are actually "the lowest orders" (016-013). In the army, privates outrank officers; in the navy, "the rowers are the highest class; next come the fighting-men; and lowest of all are the officers" (016-013). Yet this does not produce anarchy — officers "have the command and the charge of affairs as with us" (016-013), maintaining functional hierarchy while inverting its symbolic meaning.

## Mechanisms of Inversion

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] value system operates through several distinctive social mechanisms:

**Kosekin Revenge**: When one person has been benefited by another, they are "filled with a passion which may be called Kosekin revenge — namely, a sleepless and vehement desire to bestow some adequate and corresponding benefit on the other" (016-002). Feuds and wars arise from this competitive beneficence, as each side strives to out-give the other.

**Inverted Crime**: Criminal law is defined by its mirror image of Western jurisprudence. "Instead of robbers, the Kosekin punished the secret bestowers of their wealth on others" (016-010). Murder's counterpart occurs when one person "forces money on him, and kills himself" (016-010). Capital punishment means "imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues" (016-011).

**Inverted Romance**: Romantic love triggers mutual self-surrender: "Each insists on giving up the loved one; and the more passionate the love is, the more eager is the desire to have the loved one married to someone else" (016-006). Lovers have "died broken-hearted from being compelled to marry one another" (016-006). This creates the central romantic tension of the novel, as [[adam-more|Adam More]] and [[almah|Almah]] must navigate a system where loving someone means trying to give them away.

**Inverted Warfare**: Armies compete to confer the "honor of defeat" on their enemies (016-012). Soldiers fight with terrible ferocity because "their passionate love of death" makes them fearless, and their desire to benefit enemies by losing to them drives them to win (016-012). Victory and defeat are both occasions for rejoicing — the defeated celebrate their honor, while the victors rejoice "in the benevolent thought that they have conferred upon the enemy the joy, the glory, and the honor of defeat" (016-012).

## Kosekin Religion

The religious dimension anchors the entire value system in a cosmic framework. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] "believe that after death they go to what they call the world of darkness" — a "sunless land" where "the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss" (016-016). The narrator, however, dryly observes that this seems indistinguishable from "a state of nothingness" (016-016). The central religious ceremony is the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]], or [[mista-kosek|Feast of Darkness]], involving public [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|human sacrifice]].

## Satirical Function

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] value system is James De Mille's vehicle for Swiftian satire of Victorian society. By constructing a coherent society in which every moral premise is reversed, De Mille achieves two satirical effects simultaneously:

First, he exposes the arbitrary nature of Western moral assumptions. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] inversion demonstrates that values like the pursuit of wealth or the fear of death are not natural laws but cultural conventions — by showing an alternative, the novel invites readers to question their own certainties.

Second, he satirizes the logical extremes of Western ideals. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] love of death parodies Christian asceticism and glorification of martyrdom; their hatred of wealth satirizes both the capitalist gospel of accumulation and its religious critique. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] are not simply "opposite" to Westerners — they embody the logical conclusion of values that Western civilization professes but does not fully practice.

The frame narrative reinforces this satire through the scholarly discussions of the yacht companions, who debate whether the [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]] represents "a satire on our own" or a genuine anthropological discovery (007-001). Their earnest application of Grimm's Law to [[grimms-law|Kosekin linguistics]] (017-065-071, 026-028-056) both authenticates the fictional world and mocks nineteenth-century philological obsessions.

## Key Passages

> These people call themselves the Kosekin. Their chief characteristic, or, at least, their most prominent one, is their love of darkness, which perhaps is due to their habit of dwelling in caves. Another feeling, equally strong and perhaps connected with this, is their love of death and dislike of life. (016-001)

> For it must never be forgotten that the Kosekin love death as we love life; and this accounts for all those ceremonies which to me were so abhorrent, especially the scenes of the Mista Kosek. (016-005)

> There are among the Kosekin the unfortunate many who are cursed with wealth, and the fortunate few who are blessed with poverty. These walk while the others ride, and from their squalid huts look proudly and contemptuously upon the palaces of their unfortunate fellow-countrymen. (016-008)

> State politics here move, like individual affairs, upon the great principle of contempt for earthly things. The state is willing to destroy itself for the good of other states; but as other states are in the same position, nothing can result. (016-012)

> The death they long for leads to the darkness that they love; and the death and the darkness together form a state of bliss. They are eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land — the world of darkness; but for my own part, it always seemed to me a state of nothingness. (016-016)
