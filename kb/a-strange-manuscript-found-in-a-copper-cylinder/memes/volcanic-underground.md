## Overview

The **Volcanic Underground World** is the primary setting of the novel, a vast habitable basin at the South Pole reached by the narrator [[adam-more|Adam More]] after his passage through a subterranean channel beneath the Antarctic mountains. It constitutes a self-contained ecosystem and civilization, geographically isolated from the rest of the Earth by encircling mountains and accessible only through the volcanic tunnel.

De Mille's conception draws on nineteenth-century theories of a hollow or temperate polar interior — a speculation given dramatic form through the novel's science-fictional geography.

> I had reached the antarctic pole. Here the earth was flat — an immense level with no roundness to lessen the reach of the horizon but an almost even surface that gave an unimpeded view for hundreds of miles. [006-007]

## Geography

### The Passage

Entry to the subterranean world is achieved through a channel that runs beneath the Antarctic mountains. The passage is a violent, water-filled tunnel driven by immense currents, carrying [[adam-more|Adam More]] and Agnew through darkness punctuated by volcanic glow:

> The subterranean channel had rushed through the mountains and had carried me here. Here came all the waters of the Northern ocean pouring into this vast polar sea, perhaps to issue forth from it by some similar passage. [006-008]

The channel constitutes a one-way passage — once through, return is physically impossible, which establishes the narrator's permanent exile in this new world.

### The Polar Basin

The interior world is a vast basin-shaped depression at the South Pole. Because the Earth is flattened at the poles, the horizon appears elevated and the visible surface extends to "immeasurable distances":

> It seemed like a vast basin-shaped world, for all around me the surface appeared to rise, and I was in what looked like a depression; yet I knew that the basin and the depression were an illusion, and that this appearance was due to the immense extent of level surface with the environment of lofty mountains. [006-007]

### The Subterranean Sea

An immense body of water — "an expanse of water intensely blue" [006-005] — fills much of the basin. This sea is navigated by Kosekin galleys and contains islands such as [[magones|Magones]], a volcanic island used for exile.

### The Mountains

The basin is encircled by mountains of "immeasurable heights, their summits covered with eternal ice and snow" [006-003]. These mountains form the barrier between the outer Antarctic wasteland and the warm interior, constituting the rim of the polar depression.

## Climate

### Tropical Warmth

Despite its Antarctic location, the interior world possesses a tropical climate. [[adam-more|Adam More]] compares it to India:

> It was an amazing and an unaccountable thing, and I could only attribute it to the flattening of the poles, which brought the surface nearer to the supposed central fires of the earth, and therefore created a heat as great as that of the equatorial regions. Here I found a tropical climate — a land warmed not by the sun, but from the earth itself. [006-008]

De Mille offers two scientific hypotheses: geothermal heating from proximity to the Earth's core, or warm ocean currents. Both reflect nineteenth-century scientific discourse about polar geography.

### Perpetual Daylight and Shadow

The interior is in a state of constant twilight or perpetual daylight (during the Antarctic summer), with the sun circling low on the horizon. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]], adapted to darkness, dwell in caverns and travel along roads shaded by giant tree-ferns, avoiding the sunlight that distresses their sensitive eyes.

## Volcanic Features

### The Twin Volcanoes

Two active volcanoes mark the entrance to the subterranean passage. They are among the first land features the castaways observe:

> The fire came from two points, at some distance apart — two peaks rising above the horizon, from which there burst forth flames and smoke with incessant explosions. [003-002]

[[adam-more|Adam More]] identifies them with the volcanoes discovered by Sir James Ross in the Antarctic, grounding the fantastic geography in contemporary exploration.

### Magones

[[magones|Magones]] is a volcanic island within the subterranean sea, used by the Kosekin as a place of exile. Its fiery nature makes it simultaneously terrifying (to the narrator) and desirable (to the Kosekin, who love death and darkness).

### Geothermal Basis

The volcanic activity throughout the region provides the geothermal energy that sustains the tropical climate. The "central fires of the earth" [006-008] warm the polar basin, creating conditions for the rich vegetation and large fauna that characterize the interior ecosystem.

## Ecosystem

### Flora

The vegetation is dominated by giant tree-ferns that arch overhead, creating shaded roadways and terraces. These ferns are described as resembling palms but of immense size:

> I now found them to be giant ferns, arching overhead with their broad fanlike leaves and branches in dense masses, making the roadway quite dark in the shadow. [006-019]

Cultivated fields, vineyards, orchards, and forests of palm cover the mountain slopes up to the snow line [006-003].

### Fauna

The interior world hosts megafauna including:
- **[[athaleb|Athaleb]]s** — giant flying creatures, resembling enormous bats, used as mounts
- **[[opkuk|Opkuk]]** — giant flightless birds (dodo-like)
- **[[opmahera|Opmahera]]** — giant rideable birds (dinornis/moa-like)
- Marine life in the subterranean sea

These creatures suggest an isolated ecosystem evolving along distinct lines, comparable to the fauna of isolated islands like New Zealand or Madagascar.

## Civilization

The interior world is densely populated by the Kosekin, who have constructed cities on the terraced mountain slopes. Their architecture combines excavated cavern dwellings with massive stone edifices featuring pyramidal roofs. Roads, quays, terraces, and cultivated fields attest to an advanced and organized civilization.

The narrator observes that "one half its habitations consisting of caverns, while the other half were pavilions and massive stone structures" [006-020] — a dual architecture reflecting the Kosekin adaptation to darkness while maintaining surface structures for agriculture and transport.

## Nineteenth-Century Context

De Mille's subterranean world draws on several contemporary scientific and literary traditions:

| Tradition | Manifestation |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Hollow Earth theory** | Warm polar interior accessible through volcanic passages |
| **Antarctic exploration** | Ross's discovery of Antarctic volcanoes (1841) |
| **Lost world romance** | Isolated civilization with unique fauna |
| **Geological uniformitarianism** | Rational explanations for fantastic geography |

The novel belongs to the tradition of subterranean fiction that includes works by Jules Verne (*Journey to the Center of the Earth*, 1864) and Edward Bulwer-Lytton (*The Coming Race*, 1871), though De Mille's focus on social satire through the Kosekin distinguishes his approach from purely adventure-oriented hollow-earth narratives.
