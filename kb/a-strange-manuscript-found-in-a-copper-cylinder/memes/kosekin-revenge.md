## Definition

**Kosekin revenge** is the term Adam More uses to describe the peculiar Kosekin emotional response to receiving a benefit or kindness from another person. Unlike the conventional human notion of revenge — which seeks to harm or retaliate against someone who has wronged you — Kosekin revenge is the inverse: a passionate and compulsive drive to reciprocate kindness by bestowing an even greater benefit upon the benefactor.

The concept is introduced explicitly in Chapter 16, where More describes it as "a sleepless and vehement desire to bestow some adequate and corresponding benefit on the other" [016-002]. This feeling arises whenever a Kosekin receives a gift, kindness, or favor, and it functions as the primary engine of social interaction in Kosekin society.

## In the Kosekin World

Kosekin revenge is not a minor social quirk but the foundational mechanism driving all Kosekin social relations. Feuds between families and wars between nations arise from this impulse: "No one is willing to accept from another any kindness, any gift, or any honor, and all are continually on the watch to prevent themselves from being overreached in this way" [016-002].

The system creates a perpetual social contest in reverse — each person tries to give more than they receive. Those who are "less watchful" find themselves "overwhelmed with gifts by designing men, who wish to attain to the pauper class" [016-002]. In this way, Kosekin revenge serves as the mechanism that drives wealth from individuals toward the community, since everyone is trying to unload their possessions onto others.

More and Almah, through their ignorance of Kosekin customs, become unwitting beneficiaries: "Our ignorance of the blessings and honors of poverty led us to receive whatever was offered us. Taking advantage of our innocence and ignorance, the whole city thereupon proceeded to bestow their property upon us, and all became paupers through our fortunate arrival" [016-002].

## Key Passages

> Among the Kosekin everyone makes perpetual efforts to serve others, which, however, are perpetually baffled by the unselfishness of these others. People thus spend years in trying to overreach one another, so as to make others richer than themselves. [016-002]

This passage captures the paradoxical nature of Kosekin revenge: a society-wide effort to benefit others that is constantly frustrated because everyone is trying to do the same thing simultaneously.

> When one has been benefited by another, he is filled with a passion which may be called Kosekin revenge — namely, a sleepless and vehement desire to bestow some adequate and corresponding benefit on the other. Feuds are thus kept up among families and wars among nations. [016-002]

The explicit definition of the concept, showing how what would be a mechanism of social harmony in conventional terms becomes, in the Kosekin inversion, a source of perpetual conflict.

> Those who are less watchful than others are overwhelmed with gifts by designing men, who wish to attain to the pauper class. [016-002]

The language of predation — "overwhelmed," "designing men" — is applied to acts of giving rather than taking, heightening the satirical inversion.

## Analysis

Kosekin revenge is one of the novel's most ingenious satirical devices. By inverting the logic of revenge from harm to benefit, the narrative exposes the arbitrary nature of social conventions. The Kosekin system is presented as logically consistent — if poverty is honor and wealth is shame, then forcing wealth onto someone is an act of aggression. The satire works by showing that a completely inverted value system can be internally coherent while appearing absurd from an outside perspective.

The concept also functions as a critique of Western gift economies and the social obligations that accompany receiving favors. In Victorian society, as in many cultures, receiving a gift creates an obligation to reciprocate. The Kosekin system merely takes this principle to its logical extreme, revealing the underlying anxiety and competition that can accompany gift exchange.

More's observation that he and Almah inadvertently triggered a city-wide "pauperization" by accepting gifts highlights the fragility of the Kosekin system — it depends on universal participation in the inversion. Outsiders who do not share the Kosekin values can destabilize the entire social order simply by accepting what is offered.
