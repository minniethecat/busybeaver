# Magones

## Definition

Magones, called the Island of Fire, is a volcanic island in the subterranean sea of the Kosekin world. It serves a dual function in the novel: as a place of exile where the Kosekin send honored paupers to die of starvation, and as the seasonal destination of the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] migration, which provides the mechanism for [[adam-more|Adam More]] and [[the-great-escape|Layelah's escape]]. The narrator describes it as a "great island" with "mountains of fire" and notes that the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] goes there "every season" (021-035, 021-040).

The island's desolation is absolute. Arriving there, the narrator records: "We were at last on Magones, the Island of Fire" and describes the "sharp rocks" and volcanic terrain that "added to the horror of Magones" (023-002, 023-003). For the Kosekin, this barrenness is precisely what makes it a suitable place of exile — the honored paupers sent there can achieve the blessed state of death through starvation in a setting free from the corrupting influence of wealth.

## The Island's Ecology

Despite its apparent barrenness, Magones sustains a unique ecology centered on the [[athaleb-class|athalebs]]. The flying reptiles migrate to the island each season to feed on fish found in its surrounding waters (021-035). This seasonal pattern is crucial to the plot, as it allows [[layelah|Layelah]] — who has studied the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]]'s habits — to plan an escape timed to coincide with the migration. "Here we must rest, and feed the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] on fish, which are to be found in abundance" (021-035), she explains to the narrator.

The island thus functions as a liminal space within the novel's geography: neither fully within the Kosekin civilization nor entirely outside it, accessible to the [[athaleb-class|athalebs]] but desolate enough to serve as a place of exile. This ambiguity makes it the ideal launch point for the escape that carries the narrator back toward the surface world.

## Exile and Honor

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] practice of exiling honored paupers to Magones represents another dimension of the inversion of values. In Kosekin terms, being sent to Magones to die is an honor, not a punishment — it is the final step in the renunciation of worldly goods and the embrace of darkness. The narrator, however, views the island with unmitigated horror, describing how "better to perish there of starvation" than to face the alternative of the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] (022-002). The gap between the Kosekin and Western valuations of the same place encapsulates the novel's satirical method.
