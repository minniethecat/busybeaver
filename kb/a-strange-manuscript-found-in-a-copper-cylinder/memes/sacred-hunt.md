# Sacred Hunt

## Definition

The sacred hunt is one of the most distinctive rituals of the Kosekin people, occurring "on the jom following the sacrifice" — that is, immediately after the seasonal period of darkness begins (010-001). Unlike an ordinary hunt, the sacred hunt is conducted with the paradoxical aim of being killed by the prey rather than killing it, an expression of the [[death-worship|Kosekin love of death]] in which the most glorious outcome is to be devoured by the great sea-monsters known as jantannin.

The narrator records his horror at witnessing this ritual for the first time, describing how "the contest seemed too unequal" and "the sacrifice of life was horrible" (011-005). Yet for the Kosekin participants, the sacred hunt represents the highest form of honor — an opportunity to receive the blessing of death in the most direct and immediate way possible.

## Ritual Structure

The hunt is conducted from boats on the subterranean sea that fills the Kosekin cavern world. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] hunters, armed only with light weapons, set out in small craft to confront the jantannin — enormous marine predators that dwarf the hunters and their vessels. The narrator describes how "the [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] was in command" (010-003) and the hunters awaited his orders as the monsters approached.

The objects of the sacred hunt are the great sea-creatures themselves — the narrator records that "these were the objects of the sacred hunt" (010-004), creatures so vast and powerful that a confrontation with them seems suicidal to Western eyes but, to the Kosekin, represents the chance of gaining the ultimate blessing. The [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] wields a knife and performs some ritual act upon the slain beast after the encounter (010-008, 010-009).

## The Narrator's Response

[[adam-more|Adam More]] attends the sacred hunt as an observer but is deeply affected by what he witnesses. After participating in several hunts, he records: "I resolved to go on no more sacred hunts. I was sickened at the horrid slaughter, the mad self-sacrifice which distinguished them" (012-001). His reaction embodies the gap between Western and Kosekin moral frameworks: what the Kosekin celebrate as the highest virtue, the narrator can only perceive as senseless violence.

The sacred hunt also establishes narrative context for later developments. The frame-narrator [[melick|Melick]] later asks whether one of the creatures described "speaks of in the sacred hunt" is the same as the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] (017-032), linking the hunt to the broader prehistoric ecosystem of the subterranean world.

## Connection to the Athaleb

The sacred hunt is indirectly connected to the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]], the flying reptile. It is "by this" — the creature encountered in the sacred hunt — that "the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] descended" (023-014), suggesting that the jantannin and [[athaleb-class|athalebs]] share the same marine ecosystem and that the hunt's rituals intersect with the domestication and capture of the flying creatures. This interconnection reveals the coherence of De Mille's worldbuilding: the sacred hunt, the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]], and the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] ceremony are not isolated elements but parts of an integrated cultural system centered on the worship of death.

## Satirical Function

The sacred hunt functions satirically by literalizing the logic of military heroism and martyrdom. Western cultures celebrate soldiers who sacrifice their lives in battle; the Kosekin simply extend this principle to its logical conclusion by designing a ritual in which self-sacrifice is the explicit and primary objective. The narrator's horror at the hunt mirrors the unease that might attend a clear-eyed view of any culture's glorification of death in combat. By stripping away the rhetorical justifications that typically accompany military sacrifice, De Mille exposes the underlying irrationality of the death-as-glory equation.
