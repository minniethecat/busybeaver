## Overview

The **Amir** is the central metropolis and capital city of the [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin]] people [012-001]. As Almah explains to [[adam-more|Adam More]], "It is the metropolis" — the political, ceremonial, and social heart of the [[kosekin-cavern-world|subterranean Kosekin world]] [012-001]. The city serves as the primary setting for much of the novel's middle section (Chapters 14–28), hosting the central events of the narrative: the imprisonment of [[adam-more|More]] and [[almah|Almah]], the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] sacrifice, and the eventual [[the-great-escape|great escape]].

The term "amir" is used by the Kosekin both as a proper name for their capital and as a generic term for an urban center of sufficient size — "a mighty amir" [014-001]. The city is described as comparable in activity and density to a major European commercial center of the 19th century.

## Geography and Layout

The amir is situated on the shores of the [[underground-sea|subterranean sea]], accessed by galley. The city's waterfront features wharves and caverns that function as warehouses:

> The galley hauled alongside her wharf, and we found ourselves at length in the mighty amir of the Kosekin... On the wharf workmen were moving about. Just beyond were caverns that looked like warehouses. Above these was a terraced street, where a vast multitude moved to and fro — a living tide as crowded and as busy as that in Cheapside. [014-001]

The city is built on terraced levels carved into the cavern walls, with stone houses facing outward during the dark season [014-001].

## Ceremonial and Political Function

The amir is the seat of Kosekin governance and the location of their most important rituals. It is here that:

- The **[[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]]** (High Priest/Pauper) holds court
- The **[[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]]** sacrificial ceremony takes place
- The **[[the-day-of-sacrifice|Day of Sacrifice]]** is observed at the end of the dark season
- The **[[kohen-priesthood|paupers' senate]]** deliberates on matters of state

[[adam-more|Adam More]] and [[almah|Almah]] are brought to the amir as sacrificial victims, kept in relative luxury until their appointed doom [015-001].

## The Amir and the Escape

The amir serves as both a prison and the launching point for [[adam-more|More]] and [[almah|Almah]]'s escape. After [[layelah|Layelah]] reveals her plan to fly on [[athaleb|athalebs]] to the [[magones|Island of Fire (Magones)]], the protagonists flee the city on winged mounts. The irony of the escape is magnified when their mounts instinctually fly back to the amir, demonstrating the inescapable pull of the metropolis:

> Had you tried to leave this place he would have brought you back to the amir. [022-001]

## Narrative Significance

The amir represents the apex of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin civilization]] — orderly, populous, and utterly alien to Western values. Its descriptions serve as a vehicle for the novel's satirical inversion of 19th-century urban life: the crowded streets, the busy wharves, the civic ceremonies — all mirroring London or New York, yet animated by the Kosekin's inverted value system where poverty is honor and death is the supreme blessing.
