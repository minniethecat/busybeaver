## Overview

**Desolation Island** is the name given in the narrative to the Kerguelen Islands — a real-world subantarctic archipelago in the southern Indian Ocean. It serves as the last known geographic landmark before Adam More's ship drifts into the uncharted Antarctic waters, marking the boundary between the known world and the fantastic geography of the novel's interior.

> At length, on the 6th of January, we sighted Desolation Island. We found it, indeed, a desolate spot. In its vicinity we saw a multitude of smaller islands, perhaps a thousand in number, which made navigation difficult, and forced us to hurry away as fast as possible. [Ch. 2]

## Geographic Description

The island is described as a barren, treeless landscape of moss-covered rocks, with temperatures between 38°F and 58°F even in the Antarctic summer. Its multitude of surrounding islets — "perhaps a thousand in number" — creates treacherous navigation conditions that drive the ship southward into unknown waters.

## Narrative Function

### Boundary Marker

Desolation Island functions as the threshold between the known and unknown worlds. After sighting it, the Trevelyan stands south and west, and Adam More's subsequent drift carries him beyond the reach of established geography into the subterranean polar basin. It is the last point at which the narrative is anchored to real-world cartography.

### Authentication Device

In the frame narrative, Dr. Congreve uses the accurate description of Desolation Island as evidence for the manuscript's authenticity:

> "Now, in the first place, there is the description of Desolation Island, which is perfectly accurate. But it is on his narrative beyond this that I lay chief stress. I can prove that the statements here are corroborated by those of Captain Ross in his account of that great voyage from which he returned not very long ago." [Ch. 31]

Congreve cites the island's coordinates (50°S, 70°E) and uses it as the starting point for his theory of a south-polar current that carried More to the interior world.

## Real-World Counterpart

Desolation Island corresponds to the **Kerguelen Islands** (Îles Kerguelen), a French subantarctic territory discovered in 1772 by Yves-Joseph de Kerguelen-Trémarec, who named them "Isles de la Désolation." The archipelago was well-known to nineteenth-century mariners, particularly whalers and sealers operating in southern waters. De Mille's choice of this specific landmark demonstrates familiarity with contemporary Antarctic exploration narratives, including the voyages of Sir James Clark Ross (1839–1843).

The real Kerguelen Islands are located at approximately 49°S, 69°E — closely matching Congreve's coordinates.
