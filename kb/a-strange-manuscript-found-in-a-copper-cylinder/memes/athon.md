## Overview

The Athon is a Kosekin social rank designating the wealthy and military officers. Under the Kosekin inversion of values, Athons are among the lowest-status members of society despite their material wealth and authority. The rank sits below Melek and Kohen in the Kosekin hierarchy.

## Etymology and Linguistic Context

The Kosekin term "Athon" corresponds to the Hebrew "Adon" (lord/master), as noted in the narrator's comparative linguistic analysis. The narrator observes: "'Op,' Hebrew 'Oph;' Kosekin 'Athon,' Hebrew 'Adon;' Kosekin 'Sal...'" [017-071], suggesting a shared Semitic root that was linguistically inverted in meaning within Kosekin culture.

## Social Position

Athons occupy a paradoxical social position. They possess material wealth and often hold military commands, yet these very attributes make them objects of contempt in Kosekin society:

> Among the Kosekin, the rich, including Athons, Meleks, and Kohens, are considered the most unfortunate members of society. [016-014]

The narrator Adam More himself was elevated to the rank of Athon upon accumulating wealth:

> I became a capitalist, an Athon, a general officer, and finally found myself one of the most despised men in all the country. [015-059]

## Progression and Hierarchy

The Athon rank is the lowest tier in a progression that leads upward (in Kosekin terms) through Melek to Kohen Gadol. The narrator describes the career path of another Englishman who had preceded him:

> He had become Athon, Melek, and at last Kohen Gadol. It was a terrible shock. [015-060]

The horror expressed at becoming Kohen Gadol reflects the inversion: each "promotion" in conventional terms represents a degradation in Kosekin social standing.

## Political Role

Athons, along with Meleks, held certain deliberative and administrative functions:

> To the Meleks and Athons was left the exercise of authority. [029-002]

However, their authority was subordinate to that of the Kohens and ultimately to the Chief Pauper (the highest magistrate). By the end of the narrative, the social order undergoes further transformation:

> The soldiers tradesmen, the Athons soldiers, the Kohens Athons. [031-014]

This re-shuffling of ranks following the social revolution brought about by Adam More and Layelah indicates the fluidity of Kosekin social structures when challenged by outside value systems.

## Satirical Function

The Athon rank serves De Mille's satire of capitalist values: those who would be most honored in Western society for their wealth and military prowess are, in Kosekin society, the most pitied and despised. This inversion invites the reader to question the foundations of their own social hierarchies.

## Athons in Narrative Context

The narrative provides several vignettes that illustrate the practical position of Athons in Kosekin society, beyond their formal rank definition.

### Military Exploits and the Mudecheb

An Athon's military action could earn the coveted "mudecheb" (death recompense), a reward that, under Kosekin inversion, constituted the highest honor:

> There was an Athon last year who attacked a pehmet with forty men and one hundred and twenty rowers. All were killed or drowned except himself. In reward for this he gained the mudecheb, or death recompense. In addition to this he was set apart for the Mista Kosek. (018-005)

This episode illustrates how military valor — normally a source of prestige in Western societies — was, for Kosekin Athons, merely a path toward the honor of sacrificial death.

### Social Mobility Through Service

The Athon rank was not terminal; it served as a stepping-stone to higher (i.e., more degraded) stations. A former Athon describes his elevation:

> He had recently been raised from the low position of Athon to that of Feeder of the Athalebs, a post involving duties like those of ostlers or grooms among us, but which here indicated high rank and honor. (022-009)

He wore the title of "Epet" (servant) with pride — a direct inversion of the Western expectation that advancement brings greater autonomy rather than greater servitude.

### Exclusion from Sacrifice

Perhaps the most pointed illustration of the Athon's degraded status is their exclusion from the honor of ritual sacrifice. When the narrator inquires about avoiding sacrifice by becoming an Athon, he is rebuffed:

> "Oh yes; the Athons, Meleks, and Kohens — these are not worthy of the honor. The artisans and tradesmen are sometimes permitted to attain to this honor; the laborers in greater numbers; but it is the paupers who are chiefly favored." (021-062)

When he suggests that he himself might be made an Athon or Kohen to evade sacrifice, the reply is definitive:

> "Oh, that would be too great a dishonor; it would be impossible. On the contrary, the whole people are anxious to honor you to the very uttermost, and to bestow upon you the greatest privileges and blessings which can possibly be given." (021-064)

The paradox is complete: the highest honor the Kosekin can bestow is sacrifice, and Athons are deemed unworthy of it.

### Role in the Conspiracy

The Kohen Gadol's reform movement actively recruited Athons, along with Kohens and Meleks, converting them to lower social stations:

> Through his assistance many Athons and Kohens and Meleks had become artisans laborers, and even paupers; but all were bound by him to the strictest secrecy. (019-027)

This mass downward mobility, conducted in secret, underscores the inversion: those at the "bottom" of Kosekin society were, in fact, the most eager to escape their privileged misery.

### The Revolution's Aftermath

In the final scenes, as light floods the Kosekin world and the social order collapses, Athons and Meleks linger at the margins:

> Soon nearly all were gone except the paupers at the foot of the pyramid, who were awaiting our commands, and a crowd of Meleks and Athons at a distance. (031-019)

The image is striking: the wealthy and powerful, once the center of society, are reduced to watching from the periphery as the paupers take command.
