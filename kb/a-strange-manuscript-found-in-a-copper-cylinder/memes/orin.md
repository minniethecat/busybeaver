## Overview

The Orin are a distinct people inhabiting a distant land in the subterranean Kosekin world, notable for their inversion of Kosekin values: where the Kosekin worship death and darkness, the Orin "love life and light" [^021-037]. Their land serves as the intended destination of Layelah's escape plan and represents the narrative's hope for a society more aligned with the protagonist Adam More's own values.

## Relation to the Gojin

The Orin are described as "a people among the Gojin who love life and light" [^021-037], establishing a taxonomic hierarchy in the subterranean world's ethnic geography. The Gojin represent a broader category of non-Kosekin peoples, within which the Orin are a specific subgroup distinguished by their life-affirming orientation.

This classification system suggests a diverse subterranean world beyond the Kosekin, with multiple ethnic groups and nations organized under the umbrella term "Gojin." The Orin's unique characteristic — their love of life — sets them apart not only from the Kosekin but also from potentially hostile Gojin groups [^022-002].

## Geographic Location

The land of the Orin is distant and difficult to reach. Layelah describes the route as requiring an athaleb flight over the sea, with a rest stop at Magones (the volcanic island), before proceeding to the Orin territory [^021-035]. The journey requires considerable skill in navigating the athaleb, and Layelah claims that even the athaleb does not inherently know the way — she must guide it [^024-012].

The remoteness of the Orin's land is emphasized throughout the narrative. Adam More confesses he "could not guess where to go to find the land of the Orin" [^023-021], underscoring both the geographic isolation and the dependence on Layelah's knowledge for reaching it.

## Cultural Significance

The Orin's love of life and light represents a crucial counterpoint to Kosekin society. Where the Kosekin embrace darkness, poverty, and death as the highest goods, the Orin affirm life and light — values that align with Adam More's own English sensibilities. This makes their land a symbolic promised land within the narrative, a place where escape from the Kosekin sacrificial system is possible.

Layelah frames the Orin's land in explicitly contrastive terms: "There they all hate death, they love life, they live in the light. There you will find those who are like yourself; there you can love and be happy" [^024-035]. This description establishes the Orin as a utopian alternative to Kosekin society.

## Plot Function

The Orin serve as the primary motivation for the escape plot. Layelah offers to transport Adam More to the land of the Orin as an alternative to his sacrificial death at the amir [^024-035]. The promise of reaching the Orin's territory drives the narrative tension of the middle chapters, as Adam More weighs escape with Layelah against loyalty to Almah and the moral implications of desertion.

After the escape from the amir and the harrowing experience at Magones, the protagonists find themselves on a fertile island. Almah declares this new land "shall be better to us than the lands of the Orin, for we shall be alone, and we shall be all in all to one another" [^025-007], suggesting that the Orin function as a narrative MacGuffin — a desired destination that is never reached, replaced instead by the paradise the protagonists create for themselves.
