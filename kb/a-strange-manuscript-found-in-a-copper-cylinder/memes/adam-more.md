# Adam More

## Biography

Adam More is the first-person narrator and protagonist of *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder*. He identifies himself in the opening of his manuscript: "My name is Adam More. I am the son of Henry More" (002-001). An English sailor, More becomes separated from his ship in the Antarctic Ocean and is carried by a subterranean current through a volcanic channel into the vast cavern world inhabited by the Kosekin.

As narrator, More serves as the reader's proxy within the Kosekin world. His reactions of horror, bewilderment, and reluctant fascination mirror the responses De Mille expects from his Victorian audience. His gradual comprehension of Kosekin values — and his growing recognition that these values are not merely alien but systematically opposite to his own — structures the novel's satirical argument.

## Journey into the Subterranean World

More's journey begins when his ship founders in the Southern Ocean. Adrift in a small boat, he is swept through a channel beneath the Antarctic mountains into a world illuminated by volcanoes and inhabited by a civilization that inverts every value he holds. His first encounters with the Kosekin — initially through the figure of the [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]] who serves as his guide — are marked by linguistic incomprehension and cultural shock.

Through immersion, More gradually learns the Kosekin language and begins to understand their society. His relationship with [[almah|Almah]], a Kosekin woman who exhibits ambivalence toward her culture's values, provides him with an emotional anchor and a partner in resistance. Later, [[layelah|Layelah]] — the daughter of the [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]] — offers him a more direct path to escape, using her knowledge of the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]]'s seasonal migration.

## Role as Satirical Lens

More's narrative voice is essential to De Mille's satirical method. As an Englishman of conventional Victorian sensibilities, More's horror at Kosekin practices exposes the arbitrary nature of Western values. When he describes the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] as "abhorrent" (016-005), the reader is positioned to recognize that the ceremony is abhorrent only from a particular cultural standpoint — from the Kosekin perspective, it is the highest expression of love and reverence.

More's own values are not presented as obviously superior. His attachment to life, his desire for wealth and comfort, his assumption of male authority — all are exposed as equally conventional, equally arbitrary. The novel's satire thus operates not by condemning More but by revealing that his perspective is no less culturally constructed than the Kosekin one he finds so horrifying.
