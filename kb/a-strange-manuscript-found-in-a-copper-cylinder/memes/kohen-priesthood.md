# The Kohen Priesthood

The Kohen priesthood is the institutional core of [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]] — the religious hierarchy that administers the [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|sacrificial system]], interprets the [[inversion-principle|cultural inversion]] as divine mandate, and embodies in its own behavior the unsettling combination of personal benevolence and institutional violence that defines Kosekin civilization.

## Structure

### The Kohen Gadol

At the apex of the priesthood stands the [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]] — the high priest, "the chief of the Kohens" [019-007], who presides over the great sacrifices and serves as the ultimate religious authority. His position is hereditary, passing to his daughter [[layelah|Layelah]], who serves as a priestess in his household [019-008].

The Kohen Gadol is described with characteristic ambiguity:

> "He had a shrewd, cunning face — the face of a man who understood human nature, and could see through any pretense." [019-007]

This description suggests a man who privately perceives the absurdities of the system he publicly represents — an ambiguity that makes him more interesting, and more troubling, than a simple villain.

### The ordinary Kohen

The rank-and-file Kohen whom More encounters personally is a figure of jarring contradictions. He is unfailingly kind and solicitous toward More, yet presides over the [[sacred-hunt|sacred hunt]] with ritualized violence:

> "The Kohen drew his knife, stooped over the monster, and plunged it into its body." [010-008]

And immediately afterward:

> "Suddenly the Kohen fell prostrate on his knees before the dying monster and burst into a passion of tears." [010-009]

This oscillation between violence and grief, between presiding over death and mourning it, is the priesthood's defining characteristic.

## Functions

### Sacrificial administration

The priesthood's primary public function is the administration of the [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|sacrificial system]]. The Kohen presides at the pyramid, performs the ritual killing, and mediates between the community and the sacred act:

| Function | Description | PN Source |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| Sacrificial presidency | Performs the ritual killing at the pyramid | [012-005], [012-007] |
| Hunt leadership | Commands the [[sacred-hunt|sacred hunt]] | [010-003] |
| Ceremonial guidance | Oversees the [[ceremony-of-separation|ceremony of separation]] | [029-001] |
| Pastoral care | Visits the sick and consoles the dying | [013-046] |
| Social authority | Ranks above secular officials | [013-003] |

### Pastoral care

What makes the Kohen so disorienting to More — and to the reader — is the genuine pastoral care they provide. The Kohen visits More when he is distressed, expresses concern for his wellbeing, and tends to him with "tender" solicitude:

> "The Kohen himself came with me as I was led away, and raised me most tenderly." [013-046]

This tenderness is not hypocrisy — it is the sincere expression of a value system in which preparing someone for death is the highest form of love. The Kohen genuinely *cares* — and the caring leads to the killing.

## The Kohen's Character

### Amiability and violence

More's struggle to reconcile the Kohen's personal amiability with his institutional function is one of the novel's most sustained passages of moral bewilderment:

> "This amiable, and self-sacrificing Kohen, how was it possible that he could perform such deeds?" [012-013]

The question has no answer within the novel's framework — and that is the point. The Kosekin system works precisely because it transforms moral impulses (care, generosity, love) into actions that horrify the Western observer. The Kohen is not evil; he is *inverted*.

### The joy of death

The Kohen's relation to death is not grim duty but genuine joy:

> "Not long after, the Kohen came in, looking as quiet, as amiable, and as cheerful as ever." [010-023]

After presiding over a violent ritual killing, the Kohen's mood is serene. This is not dissociation — it is the authentic emotional response of someone whose values align perfectly with his actions. The horror More feels is the horror of encountering a conscience that operates on entirely different principles.

## The Kohen Gadol's Ambiguity

The [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]] introduces a further layer of complexity. His "shrewd, cunning face" [019-007] and his intellectual curiosity suggest a man who may privately doubt the system he publicly enforces. His relationship with Layelah — he obeys her command to leave the room [019-010] — hints at power dynamics more complex than formal hierarchy suggests.

Is the Kohen Gadol a true believer? A skeptic playing his role? A man trapped in a system he can neither change nor escape? The novel provides evidence for all three readings and settles on none.

## Satirical Function

The Kohen priesthood serves De Mille's satirical project at multiple levels:

1. **Religious satire**: The inversion of pastoral care into ritual killing satirizes the way religious institutions can sanctify violence
2. **Institutional critique**: The Kohen's personal decency combined with institutional horror satirizes the "banality of evil" — good people doing terrible things because the system defines them as good
3. **Cultural relativism**: The reader's horror at the Kohen forces the question: what Western institutions combine benevolence with violence, and are simply normalized?
