## Overview

The Gojin are a broad ethnic and geographic category in the subterranean Kosekin world, encompassing diverse peoples and nations distinct from the Kosekin themselves. The term appears to function as an umbrella classification for non-Kosekin inhabitants of the subterranean realm, within which subgroups like the Orin are nested [^021-037]. The Gojin represent the ethnic diversity that exists beyond the Kosekin civilization, complicating the subterranean world's social geography.

## Layelah's Account

Layelah first introduces the Gojin when proposing escape to Adam More: "There are other nations beside ours. We can find some land among the Gojin where we can live in peace. The Gojin are not like us" [^020-080]. This characterization establishes the Gojin as fundamentally different from the Kosekin — a difference that makes them a viable refuge for someone fleeing Kosekin sacrificial customs.

The "not like us" formulation is significant: it implies that the Kosekin are aware of their own cultural exceptionalism (their love of death and darkness) and recognize that other peoples do not share these values.

## Internal Diversity

The Gojin are not a monolithic group. They are described as inhabiting "different lands" [^021-039], suggesting a distributed geography of multiple territories and peoples. Some Gojin groups are described as "hostile" [^022-002], indicating that the Gojin encompass both peaceful and warlike societies. This internal diversity makes the Gojin a complex category — not all Gojin lands are safe havens, and reaching a friendly group like the Orin requires navigation through potentially dangerous territory.

The distinction between different Gojin subgroups is critical for the escape plot: Adam More and Almah must reach the specific Gojin subgroup (the Orin) that values life, rather than landing among hostile Gojin who might kill them.

## As a Destination of Hope

Throughout the middle chapters of the narrative, "the lands of the Gojin" function as a generalized destination of hope. Adam More lies awake pondering the escape plan, resolving "to question her as to the lands of the Gojin, to learn all her purpose" [^021-001]. The Gojin lands represent an alternative to the sacrificial doom awaiting at the amir, even before the specific destination (the Orin) is fully articulated.

The narrator's internal calculus weighs the risks: "better to perish there of starvation, or to be killed by the hands of hostile Gojin, than to wait here and be destroyed at last by the sacrificial knife" [^022-002]. Even death at the hands of hostile Gojin is framed as preferable to the ritualized sacrifice of the Kosekin — a stark measure of the horror the Mista Kosek inspires.

## Taxonomic Function

The Gojin serve a taxonomic function in the novel's worldbuilding, establishing that the subterranean world contains multiple ethnic groups organized in a nested hierarchy. The Kosekin are one people; the Gojin are a broader category containing the Orin and other unnamed groups. This taxonomy mirrors real-world ethnographic classification while remaining organic to the fictional setting, lending credibility to the manuscript's purported authenticity.
