# Poverty as Virtue

## Definition

The elevation of poverty to the status of highest social virtue constitutes one of the most elaborate dimensions of [[inversion-principle|Kosekin value inversion]]. In the subterranean society discovered by [[adam-more|Adam More]], wealth is regarded as a curse and poverty as the supreme blessing. The entire economic and social system is organized around the principle that to possess nothing is to be honored, while to possess much is to be pitied — a complete reversal of Victorian capitalist values (016-008).

The narrator records that "the paupers here form the most honorable class" (016-008). This is not merely a different distribution of prestige but a comprehensive revaluation in which every economic behavior is inverted: people compete to give away wealth, workers demand harder labor for less pay, and the desire to accumulate is treated as a moral failing parallel to greed in Western society.

## Social Hierarchy

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] class structure is a precise mirror image of Victorian social stratification. The hierarchy descends from pauper (highest) through laborers, artisans, farmers, merchants, authors, and finally to Meleks and [[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]s — the wealthy rulers who occupy the lowest rung (016-015). This inversion means that "to obey is considered nobler than to command" (016-015), and those who exercise political power do so from a position of social disgrace rather than privilege.

A stark consequence of this system is the redefinition of punishment: "capital punishment is imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues" (016-011). What Western society considers the highest reward — luxury, power, comfort — the Kosekin deploy as the ultimate penalty.

## The Competition to Give

Economic life among the Kosekin is driven by a perpetual effort to divest wealth rather than accumulate it. The narrator observes that "each one tries not to make money, but to lose it; but as the competition is sharp and universal, this is difficult, and the larger portion are unsuccessful" (016-015). Workers refuse adequate pay, insist on maximal labor, and push wealth upward through the social hierarchy until "it all falls upon the lowest class — namely, the rich" (016-015).

This system creates a paradoxical situation where only a small fraction of the population ever achieves the envied state of true poverty. The narrator estimates that "about two percent" of Kosekin succeed in reaching the pauper class (016-015), making genuine poverty about as rare as genuine wealth in capitalist societies.

## Crime and Deviance

The inversion of economic values extends to the definition of crime. "Instead of robbers, the Kosekin punished the secret bestowers of their wealth on others" (016-010). The act of forcing money on another person — which in Western society would be considered charity — is treated as a serious offense among the Kosekin. This redefinition exposes the cultural contingency of moral categories that are typically assumed to be natural.

## Satirical Function

The poverty-as-virtue system is perhaps De Mille's most direct satire of Victorian capitalism. By constructing a society that simply reverses the poles of economic valuation — making poverty the goal and wealth the punishment — the novel reveals the arbitrary moral weight attached to economic status. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] economic system is functionally identical to capitalism (competition, scarcity, class stratification) but with the valuation signs flipped, demonstrating that the moral framework surrounding economic behavior is a matter of convention rather than nature.
