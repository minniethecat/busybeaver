# Subterranean Ecosystem

## Definition

The subterranean ecosystem of James De Mille's novel is a vast cavern world located beneath the Antarctic ice cap, warmed by active volcanoes and illuminated by their perpetual glow. This hidden realm sustains a complete ecosystem including prehistoric flora and fauna — giant ferns, marine reptiles, pterodactyl-like flying creatures, and enormous sea-monsters — alongside the Kosekin human civilization. The environment represents one of the most fully realized settings in nineteenth-century fantastic literature, combining scientific plausibility with imaginative speculation.

The narrator, [[adam-more|Adam More]], enters this world accidentally, carried by a subterranean current through a volcanic channel beneath the mountains. His initial impressions are of fire and desolation: "on either side arose the lofty volcanoes vomiting forth flames and smoke" (003-058). But this fiery landscape gradually reveals itself as a complete and habitable world, illuminated by "the lurid glow of the volcanoes, which grew brighter as the darkness deepened" (004-023).

## Geological Structure

The cavern system is vast enough to contain an inland sea, traversed by the Kosekin in boats and bordered by settlements and ritual sites. The volcanic activity that warms the caverns also illuminates them, creating a perpetual twilight in which the Kosekin have adapted to thrive. The narrator describes how "far away the volcanoes blazed, and the northern sky was all aglow with the red glare of the eternal fires" (004-041).

The geological formation that contains this ecosystem is described as an immense hollow within the earth's crust, accessed through a channel that sweeps beneath the mountains from the Antarctic Ocean (005-005). The existence of such a space would have seemed more plausible to nineteenth-century readers familiar with the hollow earth theories then current in scientific speculation.

## Flora and Fauna

The ecosystem preserves forms of life that have become extinct on the surface world. The most dramatic of these is the [[athaleb-class|athaleb]] — an enormous flying reptile resembling a pterodactyl — but the caverns also host giant ferns and cycads suggestive of a Carboniferous-era environment. The subterranean sea teems with the jantannin, vast marine predators that the Kosekin hunt in their sacred hunts, and other creatures of prehistoric scale.

This preservation of ancient life forms reflects the nineteenth-century scientific context of the novel, particularly the contemporary excitement surrounding paleontology and the discovery of dinosaur fossils. De Mille incorporates these scientific elements into his adventure narrative, using the cavern ecosystem to literalize the Victorian fascination with prehistoric worlds.

## Environmental Determinism

The subterranean environment is not merely a backdrop but a determining factor in Kosekin culture. The narrator speculates that the Kosekin love of darkness "is perhaps due to their habit of dwelling in caves" (016-001), suggesting an environmental determinism to their value system. The perpetual twilight of the caverns, the absence of natural sunlight, and the dependence on volcanic illumination all shape Kosekin physiology, psychology, and social organization.
