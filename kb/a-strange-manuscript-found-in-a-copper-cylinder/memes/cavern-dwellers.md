## Definition

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] are explicitly described as "a nation of cave-dwellers, loving the dark" [017-082]. Their entire civilization exists within a vast subterranean network of caverns, reached through a volcanic passage beneath the Antarctic Ocean. The narrator, [[adam-more|Adam More]], discovers this underground world after being swept through a volcanic vent, finding himself in "a vast cavern of unknown extent" filled with "eternal night" [005-005]. The cavern-dwelling nature of the Kosekin is not incidental but foundational — their preference for darkness, their physical adaptation to it, and their social structures all derive from this habitat.

## In the Kosekin World

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] live in excavated caverns along terraced streets carved into the walls of a colossal underground basin [009-005]. Their homes are "caverns on one side and massive stone houses on the other" [009-005]. During the light season, they retreat into these caverns, "shunning the sunlight" [012-013], emerging only during the dark season to occupy stone dwellings on the terraces. The narrator observes "a vast cavern, full of dim, sparkling light" [009-014], illuminated by phosphorescent minerals rather than sunlight. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] language itself reflects their underground existence — their word for the subterranean light-season bird, "opkuk" [009-005], is one of many terms for phenomena unknown to surface-dwellers.

## Analysis

The cavern-dwelling motif serves multiple functions in the novel. As satire, it inverts the Enlightenment association of light with knowledge and civilization — here, the most advanced society lives in darkness. The cavern setting recalls Plato's allegory of the cave but reverses its meaning: the Kosekin do not mistake shadows for reality; rather, their darkness has produced a coherent (if inverted) value system. The biological argument advanced by the narrator and his companions — that "dwelling in caves for many generations" has produced "a congenital and hereditary feeling" for darkness [017-082], [017-047] — reflects nineteenth-century Lamarckian evolutionary theories, adding pseudo-scientific weight to the satirical premise. The cavern environment also enables the novel's key narrative device: a hidden world beneath the familiar surface, accessible only through a perilous journey, echoing the voyage extraordinaire tradition of Jules Verne and the hollow-earth theories popular in the period.
