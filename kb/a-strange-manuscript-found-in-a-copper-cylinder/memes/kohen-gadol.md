# Kohen Gadol

## Definition

The Kohen Gadol is the supreme religious and political authority in [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]] — the high priest and de facto ruler of the subterranean nation. In keeping with the [[kosekin-value-system|Kosekin inversion]] of values, the position of Kohen Gadol is not a privileged office but a crushing burden: the holder is forced to live in the greatest splendor, surrounded by wealth and power, which the Kosekin regard as the most degrading condition imaginable. The narrator describes the Kohen Gadol he meets as having "a shrewd, cunning face" (019-007), and notes that despite the office's apparent degradation, its holders exercise genuine political control.

The name incorporates the Hebrew-derived term "[[kohen-priesthood|Kohen]]" (priest) with "Gadol" (great), reflecting the linguistic connection between Kosekin and Hebrew that [[oxenden|Oxenden]] later analyzes through Grimm's Law (017-071). The office exemplifies how the [[kosekin-value-system|Kosekin inversion]] does not eliminate hierarchy — it merely reverses its social valuation while preserving its structural function.

## The Paradox of Power

The Kohen Gadol embodies the central paradox of Kosekin governance: those who rule are the most despised, yet they rule nonetheless. The narrator observes that Kosekin officials "have the command and the charge of affairs as with us; yet this is consistent with their position, for here to obey is considered nobler than to command" (016-015). The Kohen Gadol therefore exercises authority precisely because authority is considered degrading — someone must perform this lowly function, just as someone must dispose of the wealth that the virtuous paupers refuse.

This paradox creates a hidden ambiguity in Kosekin politics. The narrator suspects that the Kohen Gadol, despite his public degradation, may not entirely believe in the values he officially upholds. The "shrewd, cunning" expression on his face suggests a man who understands the game he plays and may secretly manipulate it to his own — from the Kosekin perspective, improper — advantage (019-007).

## Ascent to the Office

The path to becoming Kohen Gadol involves a progression through the wealthy classes, each step representing a descent in social honor. [[adam-more|Adam More]], the narrator, learns with horror that he is being promoted upward through this hierarchy: from [[athon-class|Athon]] to Melek to Kohen Gadol — the ultimate disgrace (015-027, 015-060). Each promotion brings greater wealth and power, and therefore greater shame in Kosekin eyes. The narrator's terror at being "raised to the rank of Kohen Gadol" (015-027) mirrors the Western reader's horror at being condemned to poverty — the same psychological mechanism, merely with the valuation reversed.

## The Kohen Gadol in the Narrative

The Kohen Gadol whom the narrator encounters is revealed to be the father of [[layelah|Layelah]], the dark maiden who seeks escape from [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin society]] (019-006). This family relationship introduces a human dimension to the institutional role: the Kohen Gadol is not merely a functionary but a father whose daughter conspires against the system he officially represents. [[layelah|Layelah]]'s ability to command her father — "at a word from her, the Kohen Gadol rose, with many apologies, and retired" (019-010) — suggests that even the highest official is subject to the domestic inversion of gender relations in Kosekin households.

The Kohen Gadol serves as the gatekeeper of the narrator's fate, controlling access to the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]] ceremony and ultimately determining whether [[adam-more|Adam More]] will live or die. His official role as executor of Kosekin religious law places him in direct opposition to the narrator's survival, yet his private relationship with [[layelah|Layelah]] creates the possibility of subversion from within.

## Satirical Function

The Kohen Gadol serves as De Mille's satirical portrait of institutional authority. By making the ruler the most pitiable figure in society, the novel questions whether the powerful in any society are truly to be envied or whether the burdens of office outweigh its privileges. The office exposes the arbitrary nature of prestige: if wealth and power can be redefined as punishments, then the entire apparatus of social status rests on nothing more solid than collective agreement.
