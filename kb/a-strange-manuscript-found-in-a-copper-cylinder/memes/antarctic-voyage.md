## Overview

The Antarctic Voyage of 1843 is the framing event of the entire narrative. The ship *Trevelyan*, with Adam More serving as mate, embarks on an Antarctic exploration expedition. A series of maritime disasters separates More and Agnew from their ship, leading to their discovery of a warm subterranean sea and, ultimately, the Kosekin civilization.

## The Ship and Crew

The expedition was conducted aboard the ship *Trevelyan*, under Captain Bennet. Adam More served as first mate, and Agnew as second mate. The voyage was part of the broader era of Antarctic exploration in the 1840s:

> I was mate of the ship Trevelyan (Bennet, master), which sailed from England in 1843 on a voyage of discovery to the Antarctic Ocean. [002-001]

The ship's name is commemorated in Trevelyan Peak, a landmark named during the voyage:

> We named it, after our ship, Trevelyan Peak. [002-024]

## The Separation

The pivotal moment of the voyage occurs when More and Agnew become separated from the *Trevelyan*. While exploring in a small boat, a south wind drives the ship away from them:

> This south wind drove the ship farther from us, and at the same time the fog settled down. [002-031]

Despite following the sound of the ship's signal gun, the two men find themselves increasingly isolated:

> The ship was nowhere visible, nor were any of the boats. Nothing could be seen of her. [002-034]

## The Descent into the Subterranean World

Separated from their vessel, More and Agnew drift on ocean currents into an ice tunnel that descends into a warm subterranean sea. The passage through this tunnel — a harrowing journey through darkness and extreme conditions — marks the transition from the known world to the hidden Kosekin realm:

> This, I thought, was to be the end of my voyage; this was my goal — a pit of fire that yawned in the midst of a world of ice. [005-005]

Agnew dies during this subterranean passage:

> Agnew, who had endured the horrors of our desperate voyage, had been stilled in death. [004-051]

## Narrative Significance

The Antarctic Voyage serves multiple narrative functions:

- **Framing device**: The voyage and its aftermath provide the literal framework in which the manuscript (discovered in a copper cylinder) is introduced
- **Transition mechanism**: The voyage transports the protagonist from the familiar world of 19th-century England to the radically inverted world of the Kosekin
- **Isolation device**: By separating More from his ship and civilization, the voyage creates the conditions for his total immersion in — and eventual transformation by — Kosekin society
