# Kosekin Language

## Definition

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] language is the tongue spoken by the subterranean people discovered by [[adam-more|Adam More]]. Although the narrator himself learns the language through immersion and records it without systematic analysis, the frame-narrative characters — particularly [[oxenden|Oxenden]] — subject the Kosekin vocabulary preserved in More's manuscript to philological examination. Their analysis reveals that the Kosekin language bears a systematic relationship to Hebrew, governed by the consonant-shift principles of Grimm's Law (017-065, 017-071).

This linguistic discovery serves both as a feat of scholarly detection within the frame narrative and as a satirical commentary on nineteenth-century philology and racial anthropology. The suggestion that the Kosekin may descend from the lost Ten Tribes of Israel (017-069) both parodies contemporary theories and adds a layer of theological irony to the [[inversion-principle|Kosekin value inversion]].

## Grimm's Law Application

Grimm's Law, first formulated by Jacob Grimm to describe consonant shifts between Indo-European languages, is repurposed by [[oxenden|Oxenden]] to connect Kosekin to Hebrew. He argues that "the same relation between the Kosekin language and the Hebrew that there is between the Saxon and the classical German" exists (017-065), meaning that systematic consonant substitutions can map Kosekin words onto their Hebrew cognates.

[[oxenden|Oxenden]] provides specific examples of this correspondence. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] word "Op" corresponds to Hebrew "Oph," and "[[athon-class|Athon]]" to Hebrew "Adon" — in each case, the Kosekin form represents a regular consonant shift from the Hebrew original (017-071). The narrator's companions are particularly struck by the discovery that the Kosekin word for the flying monster, "[[athaleb-class|athaleb]]," corresponds to the Hebrew word "ataleph" — meaning darkness or a night-creature (026-028).

## The Lost Tribes Theory

The linguistic evidence leads [[oxenden|Oxenden]] and his companions to a dramatic conclusion: the Kosekin may be one of the lost Ten Tribes of Israel. "The only conclusion is that they are a Semitic people," [[oxenden|Oxenden]] declares, and "then, of course, the only Semitic people that could possibly be in that place are the lost Ten Tribes" (017-069). This theory acquires further plausibility from the Kosekin's subterranean location — if the lost tribes migrated northward into Central Asia and then further into the polar regions, they might indeed have found their way into the cavern world beneath the South Pole.

The theory is supported by a detailed reconstruction of the Kosekin alphabet using the old Hebrew alphabet as a template. [[oxenden|Oxenden]] demonstrates that "in the first place we must take the old Hebrew alphabet" and maps Kosekin letters onto it "substantially in the order of the old Hebrew alphabet" (026-042, 026-049). The consonant classes identified by Grimm's Law — medials, aspirates, and tenues — all find their systematic correspondences between the two languages (026-055).

## Satirical Dimension

The linguistic plotline functions as De Mille's satire of nineteenth-century scholarly enthusiasm for grand philological theories. The ease with which [[oxenden|Oxenden]] constructs an elaborate historical narrative from a handful of vocabulary items — identifying a remote underground civilization as the lost tribes based on a few consonant shifts — parodies the speculative excesses of contemporary philology and biblical anthropology. The novel presents the theory with apparent seriousness while allowing the reader to perceive its absurdity, a technique De Mille employs throughout.

At the same time, the linguistic connection to Hebrew reinforces the novel's thematic interest in inversion. If the Kosekin are indeed descended from the ancient Israelites, then their inverted value system represents not an alien perversion but a transformation of Western religious roots. The values that horrify the narrator may be closer to his own cultural heritage than he wishes to acknowledge.

## Key Passages

> Oxenden said he had found out the same relation between the Kosekin language and the Hebrew that there is between the Saxon and the classical German — both being connected by means of what is called "Grimm's Law." (017-065)

> He was going in for a great linguistic triumph — he was intending to make them out to be all Hebrew; and then, of course, the only conclusion is that they are a Semitic people. (017-069)

> This word "Op" is the very word that is used in the book, which corresponds to a kindred Hebrew word in accordance with "Grimm's Law." (017-071)

> In the first place we must take the old Hebrew alphabet. I will write down the letters in the order of that alphabet. (026-042)

> Grimm's Law can unlock any language. (026-056)
