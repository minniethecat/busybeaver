# Grimm's Law (Kosekin)

## Definition

Grimm's Law is a philological principle invoked by the frame-narrative characters in James De Mille's *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder* to demonstrate a systematic linguistic relationship between the Kosekin language and Hebrew. [[melick|Melick]], the most enthusiastic proponent of this theory, uses Grimm's Law — the historical linguistics principle describing consonant shifts between Indo-European languages — to argue that Kosekin words are Hebrew words transformed by regular sound changes, and that the Kosekin people may therefore be descended from the lost Ten Tribes of Israel (017-065–071).

The application of Grimm's Law in the novel serves a dual function. Within the story, it provides a pseudo-scholarly framework that authenticates the Kosekin world and connects it to known human history. At the metafictional level, it satirizes nineteenth-century obsessions with racial and linguistic theories of cultural origins.

## Within the Narrative

### The Kosekin-Hebrew Connection

The linguistic argument is first presented in Chapter XVII, "Belief and Unbelief," where the yacht companions debate the authenticity of [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s manuscript. [[melick|Melick]], who has been the most skeptical of the group, suddenly reverses his position after applying Grimm's Law to Kosekin vocabulary:

> Melick had applied the test of "Grimm's Law." "The fact is," said he, "that the language of the Kosekin is a complete corroboration of the whole narrative. Bear in mind that Grimm's Law establishes a regular interchange of consonants between different languages. Now you know that in the languages of the different Aryan nations there are regular interchanges of consonants. Well, now, if we take Hebrew and apply this same test to it, we get at the Kosekin language." (017-065)

[[melick|Melick]] proceeds to demonstrate the principle with specific examples. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] word "Op" corresponds to Hebrew "Oph," and "[[athon-class|Athon]]" corresponds to Hebrew "Adon" (017-071). This pattern of consonant shifts is presented as evidence that the Kosekin language is not an arbitrary invention but a systematic derivation — and therefore that [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s narrative is authentic.

### Reinforcement in Chapter XXVI

The linguistic argument returns prominently in Chapter XXVI, "Grimm's Law Again," where the discussion of the manuscript resumes after a reading session. The chapter title itself signals the importance of this theme, and the discussion deepens the philological case:

> "Grimm's Law!" cried Melick. "This is another exemplification of 'Grimm's Law.' The Hebrew word is 'ashar,' meaning 'to be straight, to go forward, to advance.' Now the Kosekin word is 'asher,' and it means 'to be crooked, to go backward, to retreat.'" (026-028)

The systematic nature of these correspondences convinces the companions that the manuscript cannot be a modern hoax. Featherstone reacts with repeated exclamations: "'By Jove!' cried Featherstone, 'Grimm's Law again!'" (026-054). The cumulative effect is to build a case for the Kosekin language as a real linguistic system rather than a fictional invention.

### The Ten Tribes Theory

[[melick|Melick]] pushes the implications of his linguistic discovery to a dramatic conclusion: the Kosekin may be "the Ten Tribes" — descendants of the lost tribes of Israel who, according to tradition, were scattered after the Assyrian conquest (017-069). This theory accounts for the [[inversion-principle|Kosekin value inversion]]: if the Ten Tribes went into exile and, over centuries in a subterranean environment, progressively inverted their values, the resulting culture would be simultaneously connected to and opposite from its Hebrew origins.

The narrative does not confirm or deny this theory — it remains a speculation by the frame-narrative characters. This ambiguity is crucial to the novel's satirical method: the theory is plausible enough to entertain but outlandish enough to raise questions about the reliability of the philological reasoning that produced it.

## Satirical Function

### Philological Satire

De Mille uses Grimm's Law to satirize nineteenth-century philology's pretensions to scientific certainty. Grimm's Law was one of the great intellectual achievements of historical linguistics, establishing regular sound correspondences that demonstrated the genetic relationships among Indo-European languages. By having [[melick|Melick]] apply it mechanically to a fictional language and Hebrew — languages from entirely different families — De Mille mocks the overreach of the comparative method when removed from its evidentiary foundations.

[[melick|Melick]]'s reasoning is formally correct (consonant shifts are regular) but premised on a category error (applying Indo-European patterns to Semitic). The satire is not that Grimm's Law is wrong but that its mechanical application can produce persuasive pseudo-knowledge when the underlying assumptions go unexamined.

### Lost Tribes Satire

The "Ten Tribes" theory parodies a popular nineteenth-century preoccupation. Throughout the Victorian era, explorers, missionaries, and armchair anthropologists "discovered" the lost tribes of Israel in locations ranging from Afghanistan to Native American reservations. British Israelism and related movements treated such identifications as serious scholarship. De Mille's novel, by placing this theory in the mouth of the most cynical character ([[melick|Melick]]), exposes the wishful thinking and circular reasoning that often underlay such claims.

### Meta-Fictional Authentication

At the same time, Grimm's Law functions as a narrative device for authenticating the fictional world. By giving the Kosekin language a systematic derivation, De Mille makes it feel real — not a random collection of exotic-sounding words but a language with discoverable rules. The fact that the frame-narrative characters can debate the linguistic evidence mirrors real scholarly practice and lends the manuscript a patina of authenticity that a purely fantastical account would lack.
