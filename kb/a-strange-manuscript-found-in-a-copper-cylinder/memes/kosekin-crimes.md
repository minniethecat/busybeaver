## Definition

The **Kosekin crime system** is the inverted legal framework of the Kosekin people, in which acts of unselfishness and wealth-transfer are criminalized while what Western societies would consider crimes — theft, violence, murder — have no equivalent. Instead, the Kosekin punish "the secret bestowers of their wealth on others," forcible self-enslavement, and other actions that benefit others at the giver's expense [016-010].

The system represents one of the most thorough and logically developed satirical inversions in [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s narrative. Every conventional category of crime finds its Kosekin counterpart in an act of excessive generosity or self-abnegation, and punishment takes the form not of deprivation but of enforced luxury.

## In the Kosekin World

### Analogous Crimes

The narrative provides a detailed mapping of conventional crimes to their Kosekin equivalents:

"Instead of robbers, the Kosekin punished the secret bestowers of their wealth on others. This is regarded as a very grave offence" [016-010]. Theft's analogue is secret giving — the unauthorized transfer of wealth to another person, which violates the recipient's right to remain in honorable poverty.

"Analogous to our crime of piracy is the forcible arrest of ships at sea and the transfer to them of valuables" [016-010]. Kosekin pirates do not steal cargo; they force cargo upon unwilling vessels, burdening the recipients with unwanted wealth.

"Kidnapping, assault, highway robbery, and crimes of violence have their parallel here in cases where a strong man, meeting a weaker, forces himself upon him as his slave or compels him to take his purse" [016-010]. Physical coercion is criminalized not because it harms the victim but because it forces the victim to receive a benefit — a slave's labor or the contents of a purse.

"Murder has its counterpart among the Kosekin in cases where one man meets another, forces money on him, and kills himself" [016-010]. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] equivalent of murder is not killing another but killing oneself after enriching another — the ultimate act of benefit-bestowal at the ultimate cost.

Forgery, in the Kosekin system, "occurs where one uses another's name so as to confer money on him" [016-010], transforming the crime of false representation into the crime of unauthorized generosity.

### Punishment Through Luxury

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] penal system inverts punishment as thoroughly as it inverts crime: "The worse the offence is, the better is the offender treated. Among the Kosekin capital punishment is imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues" [016-011].

This inversion extends to all levels of punishment. What Western societies consider the highest honors — wealth, luxury, power — the Kosekin consider the most severe penalties. The "chief post of honor" among the Kosekin is "what we would call the lowest menial office" [016-011].

## Key Passages

> Among so strange a people it seemed singular to me what offences could possibly be committed which could be regarded and punished as crimes. These, however, I soon found out. [016-010]

More's initial bewilderment reflects the reader's own disorientation. If everyone already wants to give away their possessions and die for others, what could possibly constitute a crime? The answer — that criminality consists in doing these things too effectively — is the logical endpoint of the inversion.

> Instead of robbers, the Kosekin punished the secret bestowers of their wealth on others. [016-010]

The concise formulation of the inversion's core principle: the criminal is not the taker but the secret giver.

> Among the Kosekin capital punishment is imprisonment amid the greatest splendor, where the prisoner is treated like a king, and has many palaces and great retinues. [016-011]

The ultimate punishment is not execution but enforced royalty — a fate the Kosekin consider worse than death because it condemns the prisoner to a life of luxury and service from others.

> There are many other crimes, all of which are severely punished. The worse the offence is, the better is the offender treated. [016-011]

The proportionality principle remains intact — worse crimes receive worse punishments — but the scale of what constitutes "worse" is completely inverted.

## Analysis

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] crime system serves as both satire and thought experiment. As satire, it targets Victorian England's punitive attitudes toward poverty and its equation of wealth with virtue. By presenting a society where poverty is the highest honor and enforced wealth the worst punishment, the novel exposes the contingency of these moral assumptions.

As a thought experiment, the system demonstrates remarkable logical consistency. De Amicis (via More) works through the implications systematically: if the society's foundational value is inverted (poverty=good, wealth=bad), then every aspect of the legal system must follow. The thoroughness of the mapping — robbers → secret givers, murder → self-killing after enrichment, piracy → forced cargo transfer — shows a commitment to satirical world-building that elevates the novel beyond mere whimsy.

The system also serves a character function: More's horror at learning these customs reinforces his role as the "normal" observer through whose eyes the Kosekin world is rendered strange. His inability to comprehend crimes of generosity mirrors the reader's own disorientation, making the satire more effective than direct commentary would be.
