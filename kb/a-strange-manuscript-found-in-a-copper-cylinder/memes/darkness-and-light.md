# Darkness and Light

## Definition

The opposition between darkness and light forms the foundational symbolic axis of Kosekin cosmology. In a complete inversion of universal human experience — where light represents safety, knowledge, and the divine — the Kosekin people love darkness and despise light. This reversal permeates every dimension of Kosekin life: their religion, their social structure, their geography, and their understanding of the afterlife (016-001).

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] preference for darkness is intimately connected to their subterranean existence. Dwelling in vast caverns illuminated only by volcanic glow, the Kosekin have developed a cultural system in which darkness is not merely tolerated but actively celebrated (016-016).

## Cosmological Framework

[[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] envision the afterlife as a "world of darkness" — a "sunless land" of perfect bliss (016-016). This represents the most radical inversion in their value system: where nearly every human culture imagines paradise as a realm of light, the [[world-of-darkness|Kosekin paradise]] is defined by the absence of light. The darkness they love in life becomes the darkness they enter in death, making the two states continuous rather than opposed.

The volcanic light that illuminates the Kosekin caverns serves a complex symbolic function. It is necessary for biological survival yet culturally degraded, creating a permanent tension between physical need and ideological preference. The narrator observes that the Kosekin "are eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land," though he finds this indistinguishable from "a state of nothingness" (016-016).

## Light as Corruption

In Kosekin thought, light carries associations of corruption and fallenness. The narrator notes that "their love of darkness" is "perhaps due to their habit of dwelling in caves" (016-001), suggesting an environmental determinism to their cosmology. The period of extended daylight during the polar summer, which the English narrator welcomes, is for the Kosekin a time of diminished religious intensity, culminating in the joyful return of darkness and the celebration of the [[mista-kosek|Mista Kosek]].

This inversion operates as pointed satire. By presenting a society that finds in darkness exactly what Western cultures find in light — comfort, divinity, truth — De Mille exposes the conventional nature of symbolic associations that are typically treated as universal.

## Polar Context

The novel's Antarctic setting amplifies the darkness-light theme through the natural phenomenon of [[underground-sea|polar sea]]sons. [[kosekin-race|The Kosekin]] dwell in a world where the alternation between extended light and extended darkness mirrors their value structure. The narrator's journey into the interior of the earth is simultaneously a journey from light into darkness — and, in Kosekin terms, from the profane into the sacred.

## Key Passages

> They are eloquent about the happiness that awaits them there in the sunless land. (016-016)
