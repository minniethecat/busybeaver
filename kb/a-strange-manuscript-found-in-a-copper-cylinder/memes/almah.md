# Almah

Almah is the emotional and thematic center of *A Strange Manuscript Found in a Copper Cylinder*. She is at once the object of [[adam-more|Adam More]]'s love, the catalyst of his moral education in [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin]] values, and — uniquely among the Kosekin — a figure who shares the narrator's horror at the [[love-and-death|death-cult]] that surrounds her. Her character bridges the novel's satirical project and its romantic plot.

## Biography

### First encounter

Almah is the first Kosekin whom [[adam-more|Adam More]] encounters as an individual rather than as part of an anonymous group. Their meeting establishes the pattern of their relationship — mutual incomprehension bridged by emotional recognition:

> "She looked fixedly at me and said, 'Almah, Almah!'" [008-007]

> "I repeated these words after her, saying, 'Almah, Almah!' She smiled and nodded." [008-008]

This exchange of names — the most primitive form of communication — becomes the foundation of a love that will ultimately place both of them in mortal danger. Almah's smile and nod, after the terrors More has endured, signals the first human warmth he has encountered in the Kosekin world.

### Position in Kosekin society

Almah occupies a liminal position. She outwardly conforms to [[kosekin-culture|Kosekin]] norms, participating in ceremonial life and accepting her designated social role. Yet inwardly she rejects the fundamental [[inversion-principle|Kosekin inversion]]:

> "She loved the light, she feared death, and she had never been able to understand her people or get rid of her own secret horror of their ways." [011-001]

This makes her the novel's sole Kosekin character who shares the reader's normative framework. She is not a satirical target like the [[kohen-gadol|Kohen Gadol]] — she is a fellow observer, trapped inside the system she secretly judges.

## The Love Story

### A forbidden attachment

Almah's love for [[adam-more|Adam More]] is doubly transgressive. By Kosekin standards, loving a foreigner is itself irregular; loving someone and *wishing to preserve their life* is a violation of the most fundamental cultural premise. The [[poverty-as-virtue|Kosekin ethic]] demands that one surrender what one loves to others, and the greatest gift one can give a beloved is death:

> "Oh, Atam-or, our love means death; for the very fact that you are mine makes the paupers seek your life, and the nobles seek mine." [014-028]

This is the novel's central tragic paradox: the more completely Almah and More love each other, the more certainly they are condemned.

### The confession scene

The pivotal emotional scene occurs when Almah reveals she has loved More since their first meeting:

> "But I now learned that she had loved me from the first — when I came to her in the darkness — and that her life was bound up with mine." [014-016]

This revelation transforms More's understanding of their entire history. What he had experienced as a series of terrifying encounters with an alien culture is recast as a love story that was already underway before he knew it.

## The Separation Ceremony

The [[ceremony-of-separation|ceremony of separation]] — the Kosekin equivalent of marriage — is the institutional mechanism that formalizes Almah and More's doom. In a culture that inverts every value, marriage means *separation* of the lovers:

> "You are to have your ceremony of separation." [029-015]

More's reaction captures the reader's: "I did not like the idea of a ceremony of separation" [029-018]. The months between this ceremony and the [[kosekin-sacrifice-rituals|day of sacrifice]] are a period of enforced apartness during which Almah and More are kept separated — a torment that, in Kosekin terms, represents the highest expression of marital devotion [030-003, 030-004].

### The structural irony

| Western Marriage | Kosekin "Separation" |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Unites the couple | Divides the couple |
| Celebrated with joy | Celebrated with solemnity |
| Beginning of shared life | Prelude to sacrificial death |
| Private commitment | Public ritual |

This table of inversions is not merely clever — it reveals the systematic nature of the Kosekin worldview. No single custom can be changed without unraveling the entire fabric.

## Relationship with Layelah

Almah's relationship with [[layelah|Layelah]] introduces the novel's most psychologically complex dynamic. [[layelah|Layelah]], the [[layelah|Kohen Gadol's daughter]], offers Almah "the blessing of death" [024-023]:

> "Layelah offers to give Almah the blessing of death — ostensibly as an act of love." [024-023]

The irony is multilayered: [[layelah|Layelah]]'s "gift" would remove Almah as a romantic rival while conforming perfectly to Kosekin notions of generosity. Almah, who secretly fears death, must navigate this offer with the appearance of gratitude while working to escape it.

### The triangulation

The Almah-More-[[layelah|Layelah]] triangle replays a standard romantic configuration through a culturally inverted lens. [[layelah|Layelah]]'s pursuit of More is not mere jealousy — it is the Kosekin way of demonstrating love by *taking* what another values. The triangle thus becomes another instance of the [[inversion-principle]] operating at the interpersonal level.

## Character Significance

### As moral bridge

Almah is the only Kosekin character through whom the Western reader can access the emotional reality of Kosekin life. Her secret fear of death and love of light make her comprehensible; her Kosekin upbringing makes her an authentic guide to the culture. She translates both ways — explaining More to the Kosekin (as best she can) and explaining the Kosekin to More.

### As satirical device

Almah's exceptional status also serves De Mille's satirical project. By creating one Kosekin who shares Western values, De Mille demonstrates that the inversion is *cultural*, not biological. Almah proves that a Kosekin can love life and fear death — the fact that she must hide this proves that the inversion is maintained by social pressure, not nature.

### As romantic heroine

Within the adventure-novel framework, Almah fulfills the conventional role of the exotic beloved. But De Mille subverts the convention: the "exotic" beloved turns out to be the one character who shares the hero's values, while the surrounding culture provides the true exoticism.
