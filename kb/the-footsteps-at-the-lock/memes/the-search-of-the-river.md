# The Search of the River

"You want to drag the river above Shipcote" (017-017).
Miles Bredon's reconstruction of the crime led to one inescapable conclusion: if Derek was dead, his body had been deposited in the river somewhere upstream of Shipcote Lock.
The search of that stretch of water became the final act of the investigation.

## Summary

Bredon's theory was that Nigel had killed Derek **above** Shipcote Lock, then passed through the lock with an accomplice posing as Derek in the canoe, scuttling the boat below.
If this was correct, the body must be somewhere above the lock. "We'll drag the river above Shipcote" (017-017) — the search was the physical confirmation of Bredon's deductive reconstruction.

## Timeline

- **Before the search**: Bredon reconstructs the crime, establishing that the body must be above the lock (014-025)
- **The decision**: Bredon convinces Leyland to authorize the search (017-017)
- **The dragging**: Police drag the river above Shipcote Lock, searching the deep pools and reed beds
- **After the search**: The evidence of the body's location completes the case against Nigel

## Participants

| Character | Role | Significance |

| Inspector Leyland | Authorizer | County police, authorized the dragging operation |

## Location

The search concentrated on the stretch of the Thames above Shipcote Lock — the reach between the lock and the point where Nigel had rejoined the river.
The area includes deep pools, reed beds, and the approaches to Shipcote Island (002-005). The towpath along this stretch provided access for the searchers.

## Evidence & Clues

- **Bredon's logic**: "He paddles down to Shipcote Lock, and just above the lock…" (014-025)
- **The river currents**: Understanding of water flow, eddies, and where a body would settle
- **The island geography**: Shipcote Island and its wooded area could conceal evidence
- **The weir stream**: The bifurcation of the river at the lock — one branch through the lock, the other over the weir (002-005)

## Impact on the Investigation

The search of the river was the physical culmination of Bredon's deductive method.
He had worked backward from the evidence — the empty canoe, the lock-keeper's testimony, the railway timetable — to a theory of the crime. The search was the test of that theory.
Its success (or the evidence it produced) validated the entire intellectual structure of the investigation.
