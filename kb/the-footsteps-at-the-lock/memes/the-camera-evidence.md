# The Camera Evidence

A camera found abandoned at Shipcote Lock, containing a spool of exposed film, which becomes a central piece of evidence in the investigation of Derek Burtell's disappearance.
The camera and its contents represent the novel's engagement with modern forensic technology (002-007).

## The Camera at the Lock

Nigel Burtell was carrying a camera when he and Derek reached Shipcote Lock.
He stopped to photograph the lock — a picturesque subject — while Derek lay in the bottom of the canoe: "he had brought out his camera as the boat drew near the lock" (002-007).
It was while Nigel was ashore with the camera that the canoe drifted into the current and began its fateful journey downstream (002-008).

The camera was later found "held in position by the framework of the bridge" at the lock (007-035).
Its placement was suspicious — "you cannot drop a camera into the river and then retrieve it dry" (007-033) — suggesting it had been deliberately wedged there rather than accidentally dropped.

## The Film

Inside the camera was a spool of exposed film. "It proved to be a spool of camera-films — one that had been used" (005-043). When developed, the photographs told a crucial story.
The camera "takes the same size of film as a cinematograph camera" (007-043), which meant the images were of unusually high quality for the era.

The key photograph showed the lock as Nigel claimed to have taken it — but careful examination revealed that "a few of the film were exposed with the camera tilted up in the air,
and the remainder were taken quite at random" (007-044). The pattern of exposures did not match Nigel's account of a single photograph of the lock.

## The Timeline Problem

The camera evidence intersected with the alibi question. Nigel claimed to have been photographing the lock when the accident happened.
But the film's exposure pattern suggested otherwise: some frames were deliberately exposed skyward, as if to use up film and create the appearance of normal use (007-044).

If Nigel's camera was at the lock when he claimed it was, his alibi was sound.
But the film evidence suggested "you cannot take a photograph unless you are with the camera at the time" (007-016) — raising the possibility that the entire photographic episode was a fabrication.

## Bredon's Analysis

Bredon approached the camera evidence with characteristic care. He recognized that "the camera cannot lie" is a deceptive maxim — cameras record what is in front of them, but they do not interpret context or intention.
"You assume that the camera is on the bridge — but the camera may be in the canoe, propped up to look as if it were on the bridge" (007-035).

His reconstruction of events suggested that Nigel had placed the camera in position on the bridge framework, then returned to the canoe, leaving the camera behind as a prop —
a piece of staged evidence designed to support his account of the accident (007-005).

## Significance

The camera evidence represents a transitional moment in detective fiction — the camera as a witness, but an unreliable one.
Knox, writing in 1928, is among the first mystery writers to treat photographic evidence as both probative and potentially deceptive.
The camera's mechanical objectivity is subverted by the human intention behind its placement.

The episode also reflects broader cultural anxieties about technology and truth.
Bredon's method — treating the camera as a source of clues rather than answers — anticipates later forensic approaches to photographic evidence.
