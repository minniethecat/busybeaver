## Definition and Context

Acrasia (also spelled *akrasia*, from Greek ἀκρασία) is the philosophical term for "weakness of will" — the state of acting against one's better judgment, of knowing what is right but doing what is wrong. In the context of the novel, it is the diagnosis applied to [[derek-burtell]] by the medical assessors of [[the-indescribable]] insurance company.

## Medical-Legal Application

The Indescribable, a company that insures against "almost any conceivable risk" [001-014], makes one condition before accepting Derek Burtell as a client: he must place himself under the care of a medical adviser. The company's own doctor declines (it being "a matter of honour, and indeed of income" to refuse outside practice), but recommends [[dr-simmonds]], "a man who had made a lifelong study of acrasia" [001-014].

This referral brings Derek to Dr. Simmonds' consulting room in [[wigpole-street]], where the doctor prescribes "open air combined with exercise" and specifically recommends the river: "The river, now; ever go on the river?" [001-020].

## Narrative Function

Acrasia serves as the unseen mechanism that sets the entire plot in motion. The concept is mentioned only once directly in the narrative, but its consequences ripple through every subsequent event:

1. The diagnosis of acrasia brings Derek to Dr. Simmonds
2. Dr. Simmonds prescribes the river expedition
3. Derek invites [[nigel-burtell]] on the canoe trip
4. The canoe trip results in Derek's disappearance at [[shipcote-lock]]
5. The disappearance triggers the investigation by [[miles-bredon]] and [[inspector-leyland]]

## Philosophical Resonance

The choice of acrasia as Derek's diagnosis carries philosophical weight. In Aristotelian ethics, the *akrates* (incontinent person) knows the good but fails to act on it — a condition distinct from vice, where one acts wrongly in ignorance. Derek's acrasia thus frames his character as fundamentally knowable yet uncontrollable, a man whose actions are predictable in their unpredictability.

The term also carries literary associations with Spenser's *The Faerie Queene*, where Acrasia is the enchantress of the Bower of Bliss who tempts knights away from virtuous action. While the novel does not explicitly invoke this reference, the resonance is consistent with the Burtell family's pattern of willful self-destruction.
