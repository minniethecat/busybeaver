# The Body Problem

The investigative and legal challenge created by the absence of Derek Burtell's corpse. In detective fiction, the discovery of a body typically initiates the inquiry.
But in *The Footsteps at the Lock*, the body is never found — making the entire investigation an exercise in proving a negative (003-007).

## What Was Missing

After the canoe was found abandoned at Eaton Bridge, the river was searched. Divers went down. The banks were scoured. Days passed. No body surfaced (003-010).

The absence of a corpse had cascading consequences:
- **No autopsy**: cause of death could not be established
- **No murder charge**: without a body, the police could not bring a homicide case
- **No insurance payout**: the Indescribable needed proof of death to pay the claim
- **No inheritance**: Nigel could not claim the legacy without proof that Derek was dead

The body problem made the investigation circular: "If they fish out a corpse, it's all up.
If they don't we shall have to presume death after a time, unless you can produce the man alive, or evidence that he was alive on September the third" (004-024).

## Bredon's Approach

Bredon recognized that the missing body was both a problem and an opportunity. If Derek had died in the river, the body should have been found — the Thames was searched thoroughly.
The absence of a body suggested survival.

His method inverted the standard detective procedure. Instead of looking for evidence of death, he looked for evidence of life after the supposed date of drowning.
The Bradshaw cipher became crucial here — it proved communication between Derek and an accomplice after the canoe trip (016-010). The camera film showed tampering that suggested staging (007-044).
And the financial records traced Derek's movements after his "death" (024-009).

## The Presumption of Death

The legal doctrine of presumption of death — that a missing person is presumed dead after a certain period — hung over the investigation. The Indescribable could wait for this presumption and then pay the claim.
But Bredon's task was to short-circuit this process: "It doesn't do for the Indescribable to keep people waiting" (004-024).

The temporal pressure was significant. The presumption period would take years. Derek's twenty-fifth birthday — after which he could legally claim his inheritance — was September the third.
The investigation raced against both legal and financial clocks.

## The Stakes

The body problem created a unique set of stakes for each party:
- **The Indescribable**: paying fifty thousand pounds without a body felt premature
- **Nigel**: inheriting required proof of death
- **Derek (if alive)**: the longer he hid, the closer he came to his twenty-fifth birthday and financial freedom
- **The police**: without a body, they had no homicide case — only a missing person

## Significance

The body problem is Knox's most original contribution to the detective genre in this novel.
By removing the corpse — the traditional starting point of detective fiction — Knox forces both his detective and his reader to work from absence rather than presence.
This negative-space investigation anticipates later developments in the genre, particularly the "inverted detective story."

The body problem also reflects Knox's theological background: the central question is not "who killed Derek?" but "is Derek alive?" — a question about ontology rather than morality.
