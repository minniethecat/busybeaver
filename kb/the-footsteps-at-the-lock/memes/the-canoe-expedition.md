# The Canoe Expedition

The canoe trip down the River Thames, proposed as a therapeutic measure for Derek Burtell's failing health,
becomes the central mechanism of his disappearance and the mystery that drives the entire investigation (001-020).

## The Proposal

Dr.
Simmonds, the physician to whom the Indescribable Company entrusted Derek's care, prescribed "open air combined with exercise" and specifically recommended the river: "The river, now; ever go on the river?" (001-020).
He suggested a canoe rather than a motor-boat as "you don't want to take any risks" (001-020). The recommendation was for a trip up the Thames to Cricklade, the river's navigable limit (001-021).

Nigel Burtell found the suggestion remarkable: "One moment he was a doctor, and the next he was a combined encyclopaedia and guide-book to the Upper Thames" (001-020).
Yet he himself could "see no harm in anything so innocuous as a canoe trip up the river" (001-021).

## The Journey

The cousins set out from Oxford, travelling upstream. The Thames above Oxford is described as a peaceful waterway where "you see nothing but high banks and rushes, and willow-groves overhanging the stream" (002-002).
The canal section above Oxford — "a narrow, artificial waterway, with an unambitious lock at intervals of a mile or two" (002-002) — would become crucial to the mystery.

The fateful moment came at Shipcote Lock. Nigel went ashore to photograph the lock, leaving Derek lying "in the bottom of the canoe, a dead-weight amidships, the whole balance of the canoe was altered" (002-004).
When Nigel looked back, "the canoe was being swept by the current downstream" (002-004) through the half-open lock gates.

## The Disappearance

The canoe was found abandoned further downstream, but Derek's body was not initially recovered. This created the central puzzle: was Derek alive or dead?
The canoe's discovery "at a point known as Eaton Bridge, some three miles down from Shipcote Lock" (003-009) suggested he might have gone overboard.
The canoe was described as having "no mark of any collision" (002-005), which made the disappearance more mysterious.

The search yielded nothing: "still no sign of the canoe. Nigel wandered up and down restlessly, scanning the river reach after reach with his glasses" (003-007).

## The Camera Evidence

A camera was found abandoned at Shipcote Lock — the same camera Nigel had used to photograph the lock at the moment of the accident. The film inside would become critical evidence (005-043).
The camera was described as being "held in position by the framework of the bridge" (007-035), suggesting it had been deliberately placed rather than accidentally dropped.

## Significance

The canoe expedition is more than a plot mechanism — it embodies the novel's central themes of appearance versus reality.
What seems "innocuous" becomes deadly, and the peaceful Thames becomes the scene of a possible crime.
The expedition's dual nature — a genuine health measure that also creates the perfect conditions for a staged death — mirrors the book's preoccupation with the gap between surface and substance (001-021).
