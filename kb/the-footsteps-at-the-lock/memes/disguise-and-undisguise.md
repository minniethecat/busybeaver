# Disguise and Undisguise

The theme of hidden and transformed identity that runs through the novel at multiple levels — from literal physical disguises to the more subtle art of hiding in plain sight,
which Bredon calls being "in undisguise" (018-003).

## Literal Disguises

The most explicit disguise in the novel is the "river-girl" transformation.
Angela Bredon, Miles's wife, adopts the costume and manner of a Thames boating girl to gather information among the river community: "the complete river-girl; your disguise takes in everybody" (005-044).
This disguise works because it is not merely physical — it involves adopting a social role, a way of moving and speaking.

The river-girl disguise is effective precisely because it is complete: "I suppose you wouldn't know Angela if you met her on the river," Bredon remarks (005-044).
The transformation is so thorough that it passes among people who might otherwise recognize her.

## In Undisguise

Bredon develops a more sophisticated concept: the disguise that consists of no disguise at all.
"You could easily suspect a person of being in disguise; not nearly so easy to suspect that a person is in undisguise" (018-003).

An "undisguise" is a performance of ordinariness so complete that it becomes invisible. Mr. Quirk, the American insurance agent, is Bredon's primary example.
Quirk presents himself as a stereotypical American — loud, curious, slightly out of place — and this persona is so convincing that no one questions it.
"What an excellent disguise it was, after all!" Bredon reflects (018-035).

The concept extends beyond Quirk. Derek's disappearance is itself an undisguise — he didn't hide behind a false identity but simply vanished from his own life, becoming absent rather than becoming someone else.

## Disguise and Class

Disguise in the novel is closely tied to class. The river-girl disguise works because it crosses class boundaries — Angela, an upper-middle-class woman, becomes a working-class river girl.
Quirk's American persona works because it exploits British stereotypes about Americans.

Derek's "disguise" — if he staged his death — was the ultimate class transformation: from living heir to legally dead man, a status change accomplished through paperwork and absence rather than costume.

## The Philosophy of Undisguise

Bredon's concept of undisguise has philosophical implications. In a detective story, we expect disguise to be something added — a false beard, a different accent.
But Bredon's insight is that the most effective deception is often subtractive: removing identifying markers, performing ordinariness, becoming unremarkable.

This connects to the novel's broader theme of what is visible and what is hidden. The camera "cannot lie" but it can be manipulated (007-016). The alibi seems solid but is suspicious in its solidity (006-013).
The corpse is never found — its absence is the most eloquent fact (003-007). Disguise and undisguise are not opposites but points on a spectrum of visibility and invisibility.

## Significance

The disguise theme elevates *The Footsteps at the Lock* above a mere puzzle mystery.
By linking the detective plot to questions of identity, performance, and social role, Knox creates a novel that works on multiple levels — as entertainment and as a meditation on how we know who anyone really is.
