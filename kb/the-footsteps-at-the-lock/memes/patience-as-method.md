# Patience as Method

Miles Bredon's habitual card game — Patience (known in America as Solitaire) — is more than a pastime.
It is his method of thought: a rhythmic, meditative activity that frees his mind to work on the case while his hands occupy themselves with cards (004-002).

## The Ritual

Bredon's domestic life revolves around Patience.
When not on a case, "his round of golf, an evening spent over his favourite and unintelligible form of patience, his country cottage, and the unstated companionship of his really admirable wife —
this was all Bredon asked" (004-002).

During the investigation, patience becomes Bredon's constant companion. He plays while thinking, while listening, while waiting for inspiration.
Angela, his wife, often sits beside him with her own deck — a silent partnership in parallel play (016-020).

## The Method

Patience is not idleness for Bredon — it is a cognitive technology. The card game occupies the conscious mind just enough to let the unconscious work freely on the problem.
"You sit over the cipher and try to worry it out, and you find you can't get it out of your head" (016-019) — but with Patience, the worrying is indirect, the mind oscillating between cards and case.

This method has several virtues:
- **Patience from impatience**: the game forces a rhythm that resists the pressure for quick answers
- **Pattern recognition**: dealing and arranging cards trains the eye for pattern — essential for cipher-breaking
- **Oscillation**: shifting between cards and case prevents fixation on false leads
- **Performance**: playing patience in front of suspects creates an impression of indolence that encourages them to underestimate him

## Patience and Detection

Bredon's method is explicitly contrasted with conventional detection. Leyland, the police inspector, is always in motion — visiting sites, interviewing witnesses, checking alibis. Bredon sits and plays patience.
But it is Bredon who solves the case.

The cipher episode is the clearest example. Bredon works on the Bradshaw cipher while playing patience late into the night with Angela.
The solution comes not from intense concentration but from the relaxed oscillation between two card games — the literal one on the table and the metaphorical one in the cipher (016-037).

## Character as Method

Patience is not merely a technique — it expresses Bredon's character. His "indolence of taste" (004-002) is both genuine and strategic.
He genuinely prefers the quiet life of his cottage and his patient wife; but this preference also makes him a more effective detective than someone driven by ambition or excitement.

The title of the novel's chapter "Bredon Plays Patience" (Chapter 24) connects the card game to the detective's ultimate intellectual triumph.
In the climactic chapter, patience becomes the metaphor for Bredon's entire approach — waiting, watching, letting events unfold until the pattern becomes visible.

## Significance

Patience as method is one of Knox's most original contributions to the detective genre. It represents a departure from the action-oriented detection of Sherlock Holmes and the clinical observation of Hercule Poirot.
Bredon's patience is recursive: the game is called Patience, and the method requires patience to play it. This doubling — form and content unified — is characteristic of Knox's literary approach to the genre.
