# The Gudgeon Inn

A riverside inn near Eaton Bridge, the "quite decent sort of pub" Nigel mentioned to Derek as a meeting place (002-011). Run by Mrs.
Barley, it becomes Miles Bredon's headquarters for his investigation on the upper Thames.

## Location

The inn stands near Eaton Bridge, on the stretch of the upper Thames between Shipcote Lock and the weir. It is the only accommodation available to river travellers in the immediate vicinity.
The inn faces the river, with the towpath passing nearby.

## Description

The inn is "just like a thousand other inns; the creaking signboard" outside (006-001).
Bredon complains, "It isn't an inn" — finding the place lacking the proper atmosphere he associates with a traditional English public house (006-002).
Yet its very ordinariness makes it a perfect base of operations: unremarkable, quiet, and close to the scene of the crime.

Mrs.
Barley, the innkeeper, does not serve teas — "no demand for it" at Shipcote (005-007) — but she provides rooms and meals to the Bredons and serves as an unwitting source of local intelligence about the lock-keepers,
river-men, and the comings and goings along the Thames.

## Role in the Story

The inn serves as Bredon's field headquarters. He and Angela stay there because "the nearer the scene of the crime… the better" (004-041). From the inn, Bredon can:

- Interview the lock-keeper at Shipcote about the day Derek disappeared
- Walk the towpath to reconstruct Derek and Nigel's journey
- Visit Spinnaker's Farm and question its inhabitants
- Study the river currents, weirs, and lock mechanisms at first hand
- Observe the local traffic on the river unnoticed

The inn is also where Bredon and Angela discuss the case over dinner — "ask me after dinner.
I want to try and work the thing out" (005-046) — and where Angela takes her own initiative in the investigation, talking to the locals and gathering information her husband might miss.

## Significance to the Mystery

The inn anchors the investigation physically in the landscape of the crime.
By staying at the inn, Bredon gains access to the local knowledge network — the lock-keepers, farmers, and station staff whose testimony is essential to reconstructing the timeline.
The inn's ordinariness allows Bredon to work without drawing attention, and its proximity to the river enables him to test his theories on the water itself.
