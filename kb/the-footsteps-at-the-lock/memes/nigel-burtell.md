# Nigel Burtell

The younger cousin of [[Derek Burtell]], an [[oxford|Oxford]] undergraduate who stands to inherit the fifty-thousand-pound [[the-burtell-legacy|legacy]] if [[derek-burtell|Derek]] dies before his twenty-fifth birthday.
An amateur [[the-camera-evidence|photograph]]er who "held that the [[the-camera-evidence|camera]] never tells the truth," an aesthete who painted his rooms mauve and served absinthe,
and the man whose apparently perfect alibi at [[Shipcote Lock]] makes him the central figure of suspicion throughout the investigation.
Nigel is the novel's most sophisticated character creation — neither straightforwardly guilty nor straightforwardly innocent, but something more unsettling: a man intelligent enough to commit the perfect crime,
and self-aware enough to stage his own [[nigels-mock-funeral|mock funeral]] before any crime has occurred (001-012).

## Role

Nigel occupies the classic position of the detective-fiction suspect: he has motive, opportunity, and an alibi that seems too good to be true.
He is the person with the most to gain from [[the-death-of-derek-burtell|Derek's death]] — [[the-burtell-legacy|fifty thousand pounds]], freedom from the cousin whose indolence had become an intolerable burden —
and he was the last person to see [[derek-burtell|Derek]] alive (002-008).

Yet Nigel is not a simple villain. He is intelligent, articulate, and genuinely fond of his cousin in a complicated way. His account of the disappearance is detailed and consistent — perhaps too consistent.
[[Miles Bredon|Bredon]]'s investigation revolves around testing whether Nigel's story can survive close scrutiny,
and whether the [[the-alibi|alibi]] that "seems all right" can survive the methodical pressure [[miles-bredon|Bredon]] app[[the-lie|lies]] (006-012).

## Character and Background

Nigel is introduced as a young man of ironic detachment.
At [[simon-magus-college-org|Simon Magus College]], he transformed his rooms with mauve walls and blank cartridge paper, offered absinthe to visitors,
and "complained that undergraduates come up to [[oxford|Oxford]] in order to teach" (001-007).
His theatricality is at once a pose and a genuine expression — a young man trying on identities in the tolerant atmosphere of [[oxford-university|the older University]].

For his twenty-first birthday, "he celebrated by a kind of mock funeral, at which he lay, in ghastly splendour, on a black catafalque,
while his friends stood over him and drank absinthe to the memory of his departed youth" (001-012). This macabre wit — staging his own mock death — becomes retrospectively significant when his cousin apparently dies.
The mock funeral, originally a Wildean jest, reads in hindsight as a rehearsal.

As a character, Nigel represents a particular type of [[oxford|Oxford]] undergraduate: clever, slightly dissipated, observant of social forms while privately mocking them.
His commentary on [[derek-burtell|Derek]]'s situation — "With a chap like [[derek-burtell|Derek]], the world wants to be insured against his life rather than his death" (001-013) —
shows both wit and a certain coldness that the investigation will test to its limits.

## The Alibi

Nigel's alibi is the novel's central puzzle. He was [[the-camera-evidence|photograph]]ing [[shipcote-lock|Shipcote Lock]] when [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] drifted through the gates.
The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]] saw him on the bridge. The [[the-camera-evidence|camera]] [[the-camera-evidence|film]] ostensibly confirmed his account.
The timing of his arrival at [[the-gudgeon-inn|the Gudgeon Inn]], wet from swimming after [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]], checked out (009-044).

But [[miles-bredon|Bredon]] finds this perfection suspicious.
"He left school just at the right moment — got leave from his commanding officer at exactly the right time — went down to [[oxford|Oxford]], where his cousin happened to be" (006-012).
Every element of Nigel's alibi is precisely what it should be — and therefore precisely what a clever man would have arranged.

[[miles-bredon|Bredon]]'s maxim on [[the-alibi|alibis]] is directed squarely at Nigel: "You don't suspect a man if he's got a good alibi? I suspect a man all the more if he's got a very good alibi" (006-013).

## The Photograph

Nigel's identity as a [[the-camera-evidence|photograph]]er is central to both his character and his crime.
"Photography, he held, was the highest of all the arts, because the [[the-camera-evidence|camera]] never tells the truth" (002-007).
This aesthetic credo — the [[the-camera-evidence|camera]] as the ultimate liar —
becomes grimly practical when Nigel uses a [[the-camera-evidence|photograph]] of a [[the-thames-weir|lock]] "very much like the one at [[shipcote-lock|Shipcote Lock]]" to fabricate his alibi (024-021).

The irony is complete: Nigel, who celebrated the [[the-camera-evidence|camera]]'s capacity for [[the-lie|deception]], is destroyed by his own belief in that capacity.
The [[the-camera-evidence|camera]] that "never tells the truth" tells exactly enough truth — in the subtle differences between two [[the-thames-weir|lock]]s of "a common type" — to hang him.

## Significance

Nigel is the novel's most sophisticated character creation. He is neither straightforwardly guilty nor straightforwardly innocent.
His intelligence makes him capable of the crime; his wit makes him likeable despite the suspicion; his theatricality makes him compelling even when he is [[the-lie|lying]].
The reader's shifting assessment of Nigel — is he a murderer, an accomplice, or an innocent man whose life has been upended by his cousin's recklessness? — is the novel's primary source of narrative tension.

In the broader context of golden age detective fiction, Nigel represents an evolution beyond the simple villain. He has psychology, motivation, and a kind of tragic dignity.
He is not evil; he is trapped — by [[the-burtell-legacy|inheritance]] law, by family obligation, by the character of the cousin he both loves and despises.
His crime, when it is finally revealed, is less a monstrous act than the logical endpoint of a relationship that was always, in some sense, murderous.
