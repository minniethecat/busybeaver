# The Alibi

The concept of the alibi — proof that a suspect was elsewhere at the time of a crime — is the central logical structure of the investigation into [[Derek Burtell]]'s disappearance.
[[Miles Bredon]]'s approach to alibis demonstrates Knox's original contribution to detective fiction: the alibi that is too good to be true is not a shield but a symptom.
"I suspect a man all the more if he's got a very good alibi" (006-013).

## Nigel's Alibi

[[Nigel Burtell]]'s alibi appeared perfect. He was ashore, photographing [[Shipcote Lock]], when Derek's canoe drifted through the gates. The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]] saw him on the bridge. The camera film confirmed his presence. "His alibi is surely pretty sound," [[Inspector Leyland|Leyland]] remarked (006-011).

But Bredon was skeptical precisely because the alibi was so complete: "It looks so confoundedly like an alibi, if you see what I mean.
He left school just at the right moment — got leave from his commanding officer at exactly the right time — went down to [[Oxford]], where his cousin happened to be" (006-012).
The perfection of the alibi was itself suspicious — too many coincidences, too many pieces falling exactly into place.

## Testing the Alibi

Bredon tested Nigel's alibi by examining its components independently, refusing to accept any single source for multiple elements:

| Component | Question | Method |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| The lock-keeper's testimony | Could he be mistaken about time? | Cross-reference with train schedules and sunset (006-011) |
| The photographic evidence | Did the film confirm Nigel's account? | Comparative photography — Bredon took his own photograph of Shipcote (007-016) |
| The river timeline | Could Nigel have swum downstream and intercepted the canoe? | Physical reconstruction of the river journey (007-016) |
| The return journey | Did Nigel's arrival at [[the-gudgeon-inn|the Gudgeon Inn]] match the timeline? | Timing analysis of the swim from the lock (009-044) |

Each element held up individually, but Bredon's method was to look at the pattern: "He's got a perfect alibi, as far as we know. Yet he still looks to me like the kind of man who would do it" (007-037).
The alibi was not a fact to be verified but a construction to be deconstructed.

## The Alibi at Both Ends

A crucial refinement emerged as the investigation deepened: Nigel needed an alibi not only at the upper end (Shipcote Lock, where the canoe passed through) but at the lower end (the Gudgeon Inn,
where he arrived after the supposed accident). "What about his alibi at the other end? He arrived at the Gudgeon Inn, wet to the skin, at just about the right time" (009-044).

The dual alibi was Bredon's breakthrough. A man who has arranged to be seen in two places at two times has arranged both sightings.
The very comprehensiveness of Nigel's coverage — Shipcote for the departure, the Gudgeon Inn for the arrival, Oxford for the examination — was the pattern of guilt.

Bredon tested this second alibi independently. The timing was tight — just possible, but only just. The margin was suspicious: "That alibi seems all right, don't it?" said Leyland.
"Yes," Bredon replied, "it seems all right" (010-012) — his tone implying exactly the opposite. A man whose alibi only *just* works at every point is a man who has calculated the minimum margins.

## The Photographic Alibi

The most elegant component of Nigel's alibi — and the one that ultimately destroyed it — was the [[the-photograph-of-the-lock|photograph of the lock]].
Nigel, an enthusiastic photographer who "held that the camera never tells the truth" (002-007), produced a photograph of the lock-keeper bending over his geraniums as proof of his presence at Shipcote.

Bredon's counter-move was simple and devastating: he visited Shipcote Lock and took his own photograph from the same angle. Comparing the two images, he discovered they showed **different locks**.
"It's a common type, you know" (024-021) — the Thames Conservancy had built many locks to the same design, and Nigel had photographed one of them, but not Shipcote.
The camera that "never tells the truth" had told exactly enough truth to hang its operator.

## The Philosophy of the Alibi

Bredon's approach to alibis reflects a deeper philosophy of detection that distinguishes Knox's method from his predecessors:

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| **Temporal precision** | Testing whether timing really works, down to the minute |
| **Independent verification** | Never accepting one source for multiple alibi elements |
| **Pattern recognition** | Seeing that "too perfect" is itself a clue |
| **Inversion** | Treating the alibi as incrimination rather than exoneration |

"Assume that his alibi is all wrong; that he didn't take that photograph" (007-016). This willingness to assume the alibi is false — and then work backward to the truth — is Bredon's signature method.
It inverts the conventional detective's approach. The alibi is not a neutral fact to be established or broken; it is the primary crime scene, the place where the murderer has done his most careful work.

## Significance

Knox's treatment of the alibi in *The Footsteps at the Lock* represents a sophisticated departure from earlier detective fiction.
Rather than treating the alibi as a simple fact to be established or broken, Knox presents it as a symptom: the too-perfect alibi is the surest sign of guilt.
This inversion — the alibi as incrimination rather than exoneration — would become a staple of golden age detective fiction, influencing writers from Agatha Christie to Michael Innes.

Knox's innovation is to make the alibi not merely a plot device but a philosophical problem. What does it mean to prove you were somewhere? What kind of evidence can establish presence?
And what happens when that evidence — a photograph, a witness, a train ticket — is itself a construction?
The novel's deep subject is the epistemology of criminal investigation: how we know what we think we know about where people were and what they did.
