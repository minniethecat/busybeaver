# Messrs. Wickstead

Messrs. Wickstead is a well-established Oxford boot-making firm, described as "Mr. Wickstead's well-known boot-shop" (022-023). The firm serves the university's most fashionable young gentlemen, including [[Nigel Burtell]], and maintains meticulous records of every customer's foot measurements. It is this record-keeping practice that proves instrumental in solving the mystery.

## The Chiropodic Dossier

Wickstead's maintains what is essentially a forensic database long before such a thing existed in professional policing. For each client, the firm keeps a page in a portentous volume containing a complete chiropodic dossier. There was "not a corn, it seemed, in any of the more exclusive Colleges which was not on record here" (022-023).

The dossier is maintained with institutional rigour: a vast book, alphabetically arranged, from which a customer's name never disappeared until they had paid off their bill or taken their custom elsewhere (022-023). For each client the firm records a pencil outline of each foot, exact measurements, and notes on any distinguishing characteristics.

## Role in the Investigation

Wickstead's enters the investigation through Bredon's interest in footprints. Bredon first mentions the firm when discussing heel-prints: "I dare say Messrs. Wickstead would be able to provide us with a very nice sketch of Mr. Nigel Burtell's foot" (007-021).

Bredon later visits the shop with a note from Inspector Leyland, inquiring whether Nigel Burtell is a customer and what measurements are on record. He is assured "in horror-stricken accents" that Mr. Burtell dealt there as "one of the best-dressed young gentlemen in Oxford" (022-023).

Examining the Burtell page, Bredon makes a decisive discovery: Derek Burtell had hammer-toes, and Nigel did not. Their foot-statistics were close together "on opposite sides of the same page" (024-030). This revelation enables Bredon to determine that Derek — not Nigel — was the one who left barefoot marks at the scene, identifying Derek as the scheme's true orchestrator.

Bredon describes this as the turning point: "That was when I really cottoned on to its being Derek who worked the whole plant" (024-030).

## Relevance to the Plot

Wickstead's meticulous chiropodic records distinguish Derek's foot conformation (hammer-toes) from Nigel's, allow Bredon to attribute the barefoot prints to Derek rather than Nigel, and reverse the direction of suspicion — from Nigel-as-perpetrator to Derek-as-mastermind.

## Significance

Wickstead's exemplifies a recurring motif in the novel: the transformation of mundane institutional records into forensic gold. The boot-shop's commercial ledger — kept for billing, not detection — proves more useful than any police database. Knox uses this to illustrate Bredon's method: the detective must know where to look for information, not merely how to interpret it.
