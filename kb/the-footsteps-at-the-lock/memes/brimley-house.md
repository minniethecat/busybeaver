## Role

Brimley House in Wallingford is the residence of [Mrs. Coolman](/person/mrs-coolman-person), the elderly widow whose inheritance drives the [Burtell family](/person/nigel-burtell) conflict.
It is the domestic centre of the legacy plot — the house where she lives out her final years, where her adopted protégé [Edward Farris](/person/edward-farris) serves as secretary, where the contested will is made,
and where the documents that could alter the Burtells' fortunes are kept.
The house sits apart from the river action yet shapes every move on it — from Farris's covert journey downstream to the telegram that interrupts his surveillance of the cousins.

## Narrative Significance

Brimley House is the administrative and emotional centre of the inheritance subplot. Mrs. Coolman's attachment to her grandnephew Derek grows here from a distance, fed by euphemistic reports from the solicitors.
The house is where [Edward Farris](/person/edward-farris) receives the telegram summoning him to her deathbed —
the event that interrupts his covert observation of the Burtell cousins on the river and leads to the photograph of the lock being discovered.

The house also plays a decisive role in the novel's legal mechanics. It is at Brimley House, on the Wednesday before her Saturday death, that Mrs.
Coolman executes a fresh will — a document whose contents, if known to Nigel, would provide a powerful motive.
Farris's departure from Wallingford "in a distressed state of mind" (022-006) sets in motion the chain of events that puts him on the river in disguise, directly intersecting with Derek's disappearance.

The geographical remove of Wallingford from the Thames lock sequence — requiring a railway journey and a break of journey — makes Brimley House a site of motive rather than action,
yet its influence reaches every key location in the novel.
