# The County Police

The county constabulary assigned to investigate the disappearance of Derek Burtell.
Competent within their limits, but those limits — no body, no Scotland Yard involvement until too late — constrained what they could achieve.

## Overview

The county police were the first authority notified of Derek's disappearance. "The lock-keeper, of course, communicated with the police" (003-011).
They searched the river, questioned witnesses, and examined Nigel's alibi. But without a body, they could not charge anyone with murder.
"As usual the county police didn't call in Scotland Yard till it was too late" (009-017).

Inspector Leyland led the investigation. Unlike the Scotland Yard detectives of classic fiction, Leyland is neither a fool nor a genius — he is a capable provincial officer who knows his jurisdiction's limits.

## Role in the Story

The county police represent official law enforcement, and their limitations create the space for Bredon's private investigation.
They gather the basic facts — the canoe, the station records, the witness statements — but they cannot press beyond what the evidence will bear.
It is Bredon, with the Indescribable's resources and no procedural constraints, who solves the case.

Leyland's relationship with Bredon is cooperative rather than competitive. He recognizes Bredon's abilities and is willing to authorize the river search, the reconstruction, and ultimately the arrest.
The county police provide the official framework within which Bredon's unofficial investigation operates.

## Significance

The county police embody the tension between institutional procedure and individual initiative that runs through the novel. The police can only act on evidence; Bredon can act on intuition.
The police need a body; Bredon only needs a theory.
The story is not an indictment of the police — Leyland is portrayed as competent and fair-minded — but a demonstration of what private investigation can achieve when public investigation is procedurally bound.
