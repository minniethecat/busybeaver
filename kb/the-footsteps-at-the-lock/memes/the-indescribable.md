# The Indescribable

A vast and secretive insurance agency that plays the role of plot engine in Ronald Knox's detective mystery.
The Indescribable will underwrite any risk — insuring Derek Burtell's life against his own self-destructive habits — and employs Miles Bredon as its private detective to protect its financial interests (001-013).

## Definition

The Indescribable is a fictional insurance company of enormous wealth and reach, whose name is never revealed.
It insures against risks that conventional companies refuse: "Human ingenuity has still failed to imagine any form or any degree of danger which the Indescribable are not prepared (for a consideration) to underwrite" (001-013).
Its headquarters, "Indescribable House" (004-003), is described as a vast labyrinth where clients are never admitted beyond the second floor.
The company operates on a matter of pure principle — it pursues even relatively small claims (fifty thousand pounds) with the same rigour as major ones (004-001).

## In the Story

The Indescribable enters the narrative when Derek Burtell's creditors, panicked that he might die before his twenty-fifth birthday (voiding their claims to his inheritance), insist he insure his life (001-012).
A conventional insurance company declines the risk after their doctor finds Derek's constitution "so seriously undermined" (001-013).
The Indescribable, however, accepts — for a "very reasonable premium" — on the single condition that Derek place himself under the care of Dr. Simmonds, a specialist in acrasia (001-014).

When Derek disappears on a canoe trip down the Thames, the Indescribable dispatches Miles Bredon to investigate. As Sholto explains: "If they fish out a corpse, it's all up.
If they don't we shall have to presume death after a time, unless you can produce the man alive, or evidence that he was alive on September the third" (004-024).

Throughout the investigation, the Indescribable prefers to remain anonymous — Bredon cannot present himself as its agent, leading him to remark: "I can't send up a card with 'Indescribable Company' marked on it,
as if I'd come to see about the electric light" (004-043).

## Key Details

- **Headquarters**: Indescribable House, a vast London building of gold ashtrays, real oak panelling, and (Bredon imagines) Rubenses on the walls (004-003)
- **Private detective**: Miles Bredon, "paid handsomely to do their work for them, and paid still more handsomely to do nobody else's" (004-002)
- **Doctor**: Dr. Tremayne, "that eminent practitioner who had been so highly paid to leave off saving life, and devote his talents to prophesying the probabilities of death" (004-003)
- **Key official**: Sholto, "an important cog in the business and a personal friend" of Bredon (004-003)
- **Operating principle**: "Business is business; just as a prudent housewife will waste hours tracing a missing sixpence in the accounts sooner than pay in sixpence from her own purse" (004-001)

## Significance

The Indescribable serves as the institutional motive force behind the entire investigation.
Without its financial stake in Derek's survival, there would be no detective sent to the Thames, no professional inquiry into the disappearance.
The company's combination of immense power and deliberate anonymity creates both the resources and the constraints under which Bredon operates (004-043).

The name itself — "the Indescribable" — is a running joke throughout the novel.
The company is never named, always referred to by this epithet, which the narrator deploys with ironic reverence: "neither you, reader, nor I are ever likely to be shown up beyond the second floor" (004-003).
This mock-heroic treatment of an insurance company epitomises Knox's narrative tone.

The Indescribable also reflects the period's fascination with large, impersonal financial institutions as forces that shape human affairs — a theme that runs through much interwar detective fiction.
The company's willingness to insure anything, at a price, mirrors the era's complex relationship with risk and speculation.
