# The Photograph of the Lock

On the morning of July 3, as the canoe approached [[Shipcote Lock]], [[Nigel Burtell]] brought out his camera.
He photographed [[the-lock-keeper|the lock-keeper]] bending over his gardening — a "Design for an arch," Nigel murmured (002-007).
That photograph, innocuous in itself, became the most damning piece of evidence in the case. The camera that "never tells the truth" — Nigel's own aesthetic creed — told exactly enough truth to hang its operator.

## Summary

Nigel Burtell was an enthusiastic amateur photographer who "held that the camera never tells the truth" — and styled photography as "the highest of all the arts, because the camera never tells the truth" (002-007).
As the canoe drew near Shipcote Lock, Nigel photographed the lock-keeper from behind, bent double over his geraniums.
The photograph, he claimed, proved he was at Shipcote Lock on the morning of July 3 — the foundation of his [[the-alibi|alibi]].

Later, [[Miles Bredon|Bredon]] visited the same spot and took a photograph of the lock from the same angle. Comparing the two images, he discovered they showed **different locks**.
Nigel's photograph was of a lock "very much like the one at Shipcote Lock; it's a common type" — but it was not Shipcote (024-021). The lie of the photograph exposed the lie of the alibi.

## Location

The photograph was supposedly taken at Shipcote Lock, but the actual location was a different lock — one of similar Conservancy design. "It's a common type, you know" (024-021).
The [[the-thames-conservancy|Thames Conservancy]] locks on the upper Thames share a standard design, making the substitution plausible — but not perfect.
The architectural details — stonework, garden layout, lock-gate configuration — betrayed the difference.

The existence of multiple locks of the same type is both the novel's most elegant plot point and its most damning commentary on the nature of photographic evidence.
Nigel believed the camera could be made to lie; he did not anticipate that the camera's truth — the specific details of stone and water and garden — would be what exposed him.

## Irony and Method

The photograph of the lock is Knox's most elegantly constructed piece of irony.
Nigel — the aesthete who believed the camera was the supreme artistic medium precisely because it could be made to lie — is destroyed by the camera's inability to lie completely.
The stonework, the angle of the lock gates, the arrangement of the flower beds — these details, too small for Nigel to have noticed or controlled, told the truth he was trying to suppress.

Bredon's method is characteristically patient: rather than confronting Nigel with the discrepancy directly, he took his own photograph and compared.
The comparison — silent, methodical, incontrovertible — is Bredon's signature. He does not accuse; he demonstrates. And the demonstration makes accusation unnecessary.

The episode also represents Knox's commentary on the epistemology of photography.
In an era when photographic evidence was becoming central to criminal investigation, Knox's novel reminds us that photographs do not speak for themselves. They must be interpreted, compared, and contextualized.
A photograph proves nothing — until it is proved to be of what it claims to be of.

## Impact on the Investigation

The photograph is the turning point. Until this moment, Nigel's alibi — the 9:14 train, the Oxford examination, the taxi to [[Eaton Bridge]] — held together.
The photograph appeared to place him at Shipcote Lock at a time when Derek was still alive. Bredon's discovery that the photograph was of a different lock meant:
1. Nigel was never at Shipcote Lock that morning
2. His entire alibi was fabricated
3. He must have been elsewhere — on the river, with Derek, at the time of the death
