# Nigel's Mock Funeral

"Nigel was running his course at Oxford: he celebrated his twenty-first birthday by a kind of mock funeral, at which he lay, in ghastly splendour, on a black catafalque,
while his friends stood over him and drank absinthe to the memory of his departed youth" (001-012).

## Summary

Nigel Burtell's twenty-first birthday was celebrated not with champagne and congratulations but with a theatrical funeral.
He lay on a black catafalque in his rooms at Simon Magus College, dressed in "ghastly splendour," while his circle of aesthetic disciples — the dozen "half-literary,
half-histrionic young men" who formed his movement (001-008) — drank absinthe and mourned his "departed youth."

The event was quintessentially Nigel: desperate for originality, borrowing from Wilde, staging a performance that was simultaneously a joke and a genuine expression of his alienation.
It was also, in retrospect, a grim foreshadowing. Within a few years, a real death would enter his orbit — not his own, but his cousin Derek's. And Nigel would be accused of engineering it.

## Location

The mock funeral took place in Nigel's rooms at Simon Magus College, Oxford — the same rooms where he had painted the walls mauve, hung blank cartridge paper, and offered absinthe to visitors (001-007).
The college where "men cared little what their neighbours did, short of the bagpipes" (001-008) provided a tolerant backdrop for his theatrical excesses.

## Evidence & Clues

- **The mock funeral itself**: Reveals Nigel's obsession with death as performance
- **The absinthe**: His signature affectation — "he offered absinthe to all his visitors, usually explaining that he did not really care for it" (001-007)
- **The timing**: Twenty-one — the age of legal majority in England, and a symbolic death of youth
- **The foreshadowing**: Within a few years, Nigel would be the prime suspect in a real death

## Impact on the Investigation

The mock funeral is never explicitly cited as evidence, but it colors everything the reader knows about Nigel.
This is a young man who dramatized death, who treated it as an aesthetic gesture, who surrounded himself with the props and rituals of mortality.
When Derek dies — when the performance of death becomes the fact of death — the mock funeral hangs over the investigation like a prophecy fulfilled.
