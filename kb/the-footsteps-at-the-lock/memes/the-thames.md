# The Thames

The central geographic setting of the novel, whose upper reaches between Oxford and Shipcote Lock form the physical stage on which the mystery unfolds.
The river's locks, currents, islands, and seasonal rhythms are woven into the mechanics of the plot — from the canoe expedition to the recovery of evidence.

## Location and Geography

The novel's action takes place along the upper Thames, a stretch characterized by locks, weirs, islands, and backwaters.
The river in its upper reaches is described as having a world of its own, distinct from the busier lower Thames [002-001].
Key landmarks along this stretch include Shipcote Lock, Eaton Bridge, Millington Bridge, and the Windrush tributary.
The river mingles with "the haunts and the homes of men" in its lower course, but in its upper reaches it remains relatively secluded [002-002].

## Role in the Narrative

The Thames is not merely a backdrop but an active participant in the plot.
The mechanisms of the river — its currents, the operation of its locks, the difficulty of navigating its weirs — all bear directly on the central question of whether Derek Burtell's death was accident or design.

The Thames Conservancy Board, responsible for the river's management, appears repeatedly in connection with the investigation.
A member of the Board explains the mechanical impossibility of a punt passing through the lock undetected [003-008], and the lock-keepers are said to receive a commission from the Conservancy [004-016].

## Key Events on the River

- **The Canoe Expedition**: Derek and Nigel Burtell set out in a canoe from Oxford, intending to travel down the Thames. The expedition ends in disaster at Shipcote Lock [002-004].
- **The Photograph at the Lock**: Nigel Burtell photographs the lock with his camera; the undeveloped prints later become crucial evidence [002-007].
- **The Punt Discovery**: A punt is found adrift on the river, contributing to the investigation [003-009].
- **The Island Search**: An island in the Thames is searched as part of the inquiry [016-002].
- **The Body Recovery**: The recovery of bodies from the Thames is discussed; a member of the Conservancy explains the patterns of river recovery [019-007].

## The Thames in the Investigation

The river's physical properties are interrogated as evidence. The question of whether a canoe could pass through Shipcote Lock without the lock-keeper's knowledge becomes a pivotal point [003-008].
The direction and speed of the current bear on the timeline of the disappearance.
The seasonal influx of trippers — "trippers will be coming up the Thames before long" [004-024] — adds urgency to the search, as increased river traffic threatens to disturb potential evidence.
