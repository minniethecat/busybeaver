# The Shipcote Lock-Keeper

The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]] at [[shipcote-lock|Shipcote Lock]] is a key [[the-oath-and-testimony|witness]] to the events surrounding [[derek-burtell|Derek Burtell]]'s disappearance on the upper Thames.
First encountered by [[nigel-burtell|Nigel Burtell]] and [[miles-bredon|Miles Bredon]] during their initial visit to [[shipcote-lock|the lock]],
he provides critical [[the-oath-and-testimony|testimony]] about the movements of [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] on the day [[derek-burtell|Derek]] vanished. He is later identified as the husband of [[mrs-burgess|Mrs.
Burgess]], connecting him to the [[london-place|London]] investigation network through [[the-indescribable-company|the Indescribable Company]].

## Role

The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]] serves as both a fixed point of observation in the river geography and a crucial source of timeline [[the-oath-and-testimony|evidence]].
Stationed at [[shipcote-lock|Shipcote Lock]], he [[the-oath-and-testimony|witness]]es the comings and goings of boats and canoes, and his [[the-oath-and-testimony|testimony]] about what he saw — and crucially,
what he did not see — shapes the investigation's understanding of the sequence of events.
His physical position at [[shipcote-lock|the lock]] makes him an inadvertent sentinel, his back turned to the water as he operates the winches, creating both opportunity and limitation in his observations.

## Character Traits

The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]] is characterized by the [[accident-or-design|deliberate]], methodical nature of his profession.
His movements at [[shipcote-lock|the lock]] are described as unhurried and precise, reflecting a lifetime of working with the Thames's waters.

The narrative emphasizes his physical presence — the "back view" that [[nigel-burtell|Nigel]] and [[miles-bredon|Bredon]] first see — suggesting a man defined by his work rather than his personality.
He is portrayed as willing to talk and share his observations, a natural informant whom [[miles-bredon|Bredon]] knows how to cultivate.

The detail that his wife is "[[mrs-burgess|Mrs. Burgess]]" adds an unexpected domestic dimension to his character, linking the remote [[the-thames-weir|lock]] to the wider network of the investigation.

## Significance to the Mystery

The [[the-lock-keeper|lock-keeper]]'s [[the-oath-and-testimony|testimony]] is central to reconstructing the timeline of [[the-disappearance|Derek's disappearance]].
What he saw — one man in [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] at [[shipcote-lock|the lock]] — and what he did not see — any sign of a second person or unusual activity —
form the factual foundation upon which competing theories are built.

His position, with his back to [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] while operating the winches, creates a crucial window of unobserved time during which events could have occurred.
[[miles-bredon|Bredon]]'s [[the-camera-evidence|photograph]] of the lock-keeper at work becomes a piece of [[the-oath-and-testimony|evidence]] in its own right,
and the lock-keeper's subsequent conversations with investigators reveal both the value and the limitations of eye[[the-oath-and-testimony|witness]] [[the-oath-and-testimony|testimony]].
