# Suspicion

The mental state and investigative principle that drives detection in the novel.
Knox, through Bredon, develops a sophisticated theory of suspicion that inverts conventional wisdom: the person with the best alibi is the person most worth suspecting (006-013).

## The Problem of Suspicion

Suspicion is both necessary and dangerous in detective work. It focuses attention but can also misdirect it. Leyland, the police inspector, suspects the obvious: the person with motive and opportunity.
Bredon suspects the person with the perfect alibi (006-013).

This inversion is Bredon's key insight: "You don't suspect a man if he's got a good alibi?" — "I suspect a man all the more if he's got a very good alibi" (006-013).
The logic is that a genuinely innocent person rarely has a perfect alibi. Life is messy; alibis are usually partial, qualified, uncertain. A perfect alibi is a constructed artifact — and construction implies intention.

## Nigel as the Object of Suspicion

Nigel Burtell is the primary object of Bredon's suspicion.
From the beginning, Nigel seems suspicious but is protected by a seemingly unbreakable alibi: he was on the bridge at Shipcote Lock, photographing the scene, while Derek's canoe drifted away (002-008).

Bredon's suspicion of Nigel is a kind of aesthetic judgment: "He's got a perfect alibi, as far as we know. Yet he still looks to me like the kind of man who would do it" (007-037).
This is not evidence — it is intuition shaped by experience. But for Bredon, intuition is the starting point of investigation, not its end.

## The Distribution of Suspicion

Suspicion in the novel is distributed across multiple characters:
- **Nigel**: the contingent heir, with the most to gain (006-012)
- **Derek himself**: what if he staged his own disappearance? (019-006)
- **The creditors**: they had financial motive to keep Derek alive (001-012)
- **Dr. Simmonds**: why did he specifically recommend a canoe trip? (004-015)
- **Mr. Quirk**: the American insurance agent who seems too helpful (009-030)

Each suspect has grounds for suspicion and grounds for exoneration. The novel's art lies in the shifting weight Bredon assigns to each.

## Suspicion and Certainty

The novel's deepest insight about suspicion is its relationship to certainty. Suspicion is, by nature, uncertain — it is a hypothesis, not a conclusion.
Bredon maintains multiple suspicions simultaneously, testing each against evidence without committing to any.

This methodological pluralism is his strength. Leyland, by contrast, tends to fixate on one suspect at a time.
Bredon's patience (both the method and the virtue) allows him to hold open multiple possibilities until evidence forecloses them (006-011).

## Significance

Knox's treatment of suspicion reflects the sophistication of golden age detective fiction at its best. Suspicion is not merely a plot mechanism but a philosophical problem. How do we know someone is guilty?
What counts as evidence? When does suspicion become paranoia? The novel uses the detective form to explore these questions without sacrificing narrative momentum.
