# Dr. Simmonds

The physician on [[wigpole-street|Wigpole Street]] who treated [[derek-burtell|Derek Burtell]] at the [[the-indescribable|Indescribable]]'s insistence. His medical advice — "Open air, that's what you want.
The river, now; ever go on the river?" — initiated [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] expedition that ended in [[the-disappearance|Derek's disappearance]] (001-015).

## Role

Dr. Simmonds occupies an ambiguous position.
He was recommended by the [[the-indescribable|Indescribable]] as a condition of [[derek-burtell|Derek]]'s insurance policy, but he was not officially an [[the-indescribable|Indescribable]] employee.
"He was not exactly an official of [[the-indescribable-company|the Indescribable Company]], but he was (how shall we say it?) in close touch with them" (001-017).

His prescription of [[the-canoe-expedition|the canoe]] trip is, in retrospect, the most consequential medical advice in the novel. Was it genuinely therapeutic, or was it suggested with ulterior motives?
The novel leaves this question unresolved, adding to the atmosphere of pervasive uncertainty (004-015).

## Character

Simmonds is a specialist in acrasia — the philosophical term for weakness of will. His entire practice is built on treating patients who cannot control their own behavior.
This makes him an ambiguous figure: he profits from the very condition he treats, and his patients' continued weakness is his continued income (001-014).

His recommendation of the river trip is described in detail: "Take a sea-voyage, and the first thing you know you'll find you're edging round to the saloon. Don't mind my speaking frankly, do you?
No, it must be open air combined with exercise; not very hard exercise, you ain't fit for it, but something that'll keep you occupied" (001-018).
The advice is sensible, specific, and — in hindsight — [[luck-and-chance|fate]]ful.

## Significance

Dr. Simmonds represents the medical dimension of the institutional web surrounding [[derek-burtell|Derek]].
[[the-indescribable|The Indescribable]] required a doctor; the doctor prescribed a river trip; the river trip became a disappearance. Each institution did its job competently, and the result was a possible crime.
Knox's point is not that any individual was corrupt but that institutional rationality can produce catastrophic outcomes when no one is looking at the whole picture.
