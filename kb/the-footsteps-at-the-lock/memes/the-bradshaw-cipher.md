# The Bradshaw Cipher

A coded message embedded in the personal column of a newspaper, which Miles Bredon deciphers using a Bradshaw railway timetable as the key text.
The cipher is the novel's signature puzzle — a demonstration of Bredon's methodical brilliance and a critical turning point in the investigation (016-006).

## Discovery

The cipher was found among Nigel Burtell's papers after his arrest, in the form of a newspaper clipping from the personal column. The irregular spacing and arrangement of words "clearly indicated a cipher" (016-006).
Bredon, confronted with it by Inspector Leyland, remarked with characteristic understatement that he "had a throb at the old M.C.S." (016-006) — a reference to his Ministry of Covert Services days during the war.

Mr. Quirk, the American insurance agent, was dubious: "Do they use ciphers much in the States, Mr.
Quirk?" Bredon asked, to which Quirk replied, "Yes, sir, a good deal — but nothing so elaborate as your European" (016-009).

## The Solution

Bredon determined that this was not a simple substitution cipher — "it was quite obviously impossible that one cipher should stand for one letter, because there were about forty cipher-words in all,
and nearly all of them began with a capital letter" (016-012). He recognized it as a word-cipher — a system where each code-word represents a different plaintext word, keyed to a specific book.

The breakthrough came when Bredon recalled Quirk mentioning Bradshaw: "Most country hotels don't keep Bradshaw" (016-037).
Realizing that a railway timetable would make an ideal cipher key — universally available yet inconspicuous —
Bredon obtained a copy and began the painstaking work of matching cipher-words to corresponding entries in Bradshaw (016-039).

## The Message

The deciphered message revealed a communication between conspirators, providing evidence that Derek's disappearance had been staged. The cipher had been exchanged between Derek and an accomplice before the canoe trip.
Its content confirmed that Derek was alive after the supposed date of his death — which meant the Indescribable was not liable for the insurance payout.

Bredon explained the system to Leyland: "the whole system of the Bradshaw cipher which you solved this morning" (017-013).
The decipherment was the intellectual climax of the investigation — a feat of pure deduction that broke the case open.

## Bredon's Method

The cipher-breaking process reveals Bredon's character: "You sit over the cipher and try to worry it out, and you find you can't get it out of your head" (016-019).
He worked late into the night with Angela, his wife, who played patience — Bredon's method was to work at the cipher between games, rotating his attention between the puzzle and the cards.
This mental oscillation was his signature problem-solving technique (016-020).

## Significance

The Bradshaw Cipher is more than a plot device — it is Knox's homage to the golden age of detective fiction's love of codes and puzzles.
The choice of a Bradshaw railway guide as the key text is quintessentially English, rooted in the infrastructure of everyday Edwardian life.
The cipher episode also demonstrates the collaborative nature of Bredon's detection: he works with Angela beside him, with Leyland and Quirk looking on, making the intellectual triumph a social one (016-039).
