## Role in the Initial Search

George Lowther is a serving-man employed at [[the-gudgeon-inn]], the riverside public house near [[eaton-bridge]]. He enters the narrative during the initial search for [[derek-burtell]], when [[nigel-burtell]] returns to the river after failing to meet his cousin at Eaton Bridge.

When Nigel "proceeded upstream by the towpath in the direction of Shipcote," he was "accompanied by George Lowther, a serving-man at the Gudgeon Inn" [003-008]. Lowther thus serves as both guide and witness to the crucial first moments of the search.

## The Canoe Recovery

At approximately half-past one, the search party sighted Derek Burtell's hat floating in the centre of the stream, and shortly afterwards the canoe itself came into view — still afloat but filled with water to the gunwale.

> "Lowther immediately stripped and swam out to the canoe, which he brought in to shore without difficulty; then he pluckily commenced diving near the spot where the canoe had been found, to see if any further signs of the missing gentleman were forthcoming." [003-008]

Lowther's actions constitute the first physical intervention in the investigation. His recovery of the canoe revealed the critical piece of evidence: "a jagged hole of considerable size had been made in one of the planks of its hull" [003-008]. This discovery transformed the incident from a simple drowning into a matter requiring further investigation.

## The Hole in the Canoe

The damage Lowther discovered became a central point of contention. The hole had "jagged, splintered edges, as if it had been freshly made — there was no question of an old piece of caulking having come loose" [003-010]. Watermen who inspected the canoe expressed unanimous difficulty in understanding how so deep a cut could result from impact with river gravel, particularly if the canoe was drifting rather than being paddled at speed.

Lowther's prompt action in retrieving and emptying the canoe preserved this evidence. Had the canoe drifted further downstream or sunk entirely, the nature of the hull damage — and its implications — might never have been established.

## Significance

Lowther represents the first of several local inhabitants drawn into the investigation — like [[the-lock-keeper]], the [[the-boy-scouts-encampment]], and the fishermen along the banks. His role is brief but consequential: without his swimming out to the canoe, the crucial evidence of the jagged hole might have been lost or delayed.
