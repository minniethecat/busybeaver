# Luck and Chance

The role of fortune — good and bad — in the events of the novel.
Knox structures the plot so that luck and design are constantly confused: what looks like chance may be conspiracy, and what looks like conspiracy may be the genuine randomness of the world (001-021).

## The Accidental and the Intentional

The novel's pivotal moments all involve the question of luck:

- **Derek's health**: It was bad luck that Derek's "constitution was so seriously undermined" (001-013) — or was it deliberate self-destruction?
- **The canoe trip**: It was good luck that Dr. Simmonds prescribed a river trip (001-020) — or was the recommendation made with ulterior motives?
- **The open lock gates**: It was bad luck that the lock gates were half-open when the canoe approached (002-004) — or had someone arranged them that way?
- **The missing body**: It was luck that Derek's body was never found (003-007) — or careful planning to ensure it wouldn't be?

Each moment presents the same interpretive choice: attribute to chance or attribute to intention. The novel's suspense derives from the reader's inability to choose reliably between these alternatives.

## Bredon and Luck

Bredon does not believe in luck — at least, not in luck as an explanation. His method treats every coincidence as a clue. If two events align suspiciously, the alignment itself is evidence that one caused the other.

This methodological refusal of chance distinguishes Bredon from other characters. Leyland, the police inspector, is willing to accept that some things happen by accident. Bredon is not.
For Bredon, "accident" is a hypothesis of last resort, to be adopted only when all explanations involving intention have been exhausted (006-013).

## The Gambler's Logic

The novel's treatment of luck is connected to its interest in financial speculation.
Derek's creditors took a gamble on his survival — "a young man who was no longer a minor, and who had less than two years to wait" (001-012). The Indescribable took a calculated risk on Derek's life insurance (001-013).
Even Nigel's position — waiting to see whether his cousin would live to twenty-five — has the structure of a wager.

This financialized luck contrasts with the genuine randomness of the river. The Thames current does not calculate odds. The lock gates open or close according to physical forces, not financial interests.
The novel's philosophical depth comes from this contrast between human schemes and natural forces — between the luck we make and the luck that makes us.

## The Reader's Luck

Knox plays a sophisticated game with the reader's expectations about luck in detective fiction. Genre convention suggests that coincidences in a detective story are never really coincidences — they are clues.
But Knox seeds the novel with genuine randomness alongside planted evidence, making it impossible for the reader to distinguish between them without Bredon's help.

This makes the reading experience unusually participatory. The reader, like Bredon, must hold open the possibility that any given event is either chance or design — and must accept the discomfort of not knowing which.
