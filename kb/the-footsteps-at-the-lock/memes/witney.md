# Witney

A market town in Oxfordshire, inland from the Thames valley.
Witney's significance to the investigation lies in its role as the location where Luke Wallace stayed on the Sunday night when Derek Burtell was at Millington Bridge —
a parallel movement that becomes relevant to the alibi timeline.

## Location

Witney is situated inland from the upper Thames, reachable by road rather than river.
It is sufficiently distant from the river to require a deliberate expedition — "a little expedition to Witney" [023-033] — rather than a casual visit.
The town has several pubs, though the narrative notes drily that "there can't be many pubs in Witney — decent ones, I mean" [023-050].

## Luke Wallace's Visit

The central fact about Witney in the narrative is that Luke Wallace stayed there on the Sunday night — the same night Derek Burtell slept at Millington Bridge. This parallel is carefully established:

- "Mr. Luke Wallace's visit to Witney" is introduced as a matter requiring examination [024-012].
- Wallace "went all the way on to Witney" and "at Witney, did he give..." [024-024].
- Crucially, "L. Wallace stayed at Witney on the Sunday night when Derek Burtell was at Millington Bridge" [024-036].

The significance of this parallel is that it places two key figures at different locations on the same night, establishing a geographical separation that bears on the question of opportunity and alibi.

## Role in the Investigation

Bredon and Leyland's interest in Witney is twofold. First, they need to verify Wallace's movements independently.
Second,
the fact that Wallace was at Witney while Derek was at Millington Bridge creates a pattern of dispersed locations on the critical Sunday night that must be reconciled with the timeline of the following day's canoe incident.

The inquiry at Witney involves questioning at the pubs and establishments where Wallace might have been seen, part of the methodical verification of alibis that characterizes the later stages of the investigation.
