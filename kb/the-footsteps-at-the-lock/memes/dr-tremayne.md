# Dr. Tremayne

[[the-indescribable|The Indescribable]]'s chief medical officer, present at [[miles-bredon|Bredon]]'s initial briefing. A man "who had been so highly paid to leave off saving life, and devote his talents to prophesying the probabilities of death" (004-003).

## Role

Dr. Tremayne represents the medical expertise that undergirds the insurance industry.
His job is not to heal but to calculate — to assess how long a given life will last and what premium should be charged for insuring it.
The irony of his position — a doctor paid to predict death rather than prevent it — is characteristic of Knox's satirical eye (004-003).

In the Burtell case, Tremayne would have been involved in assessing [[derek-burtell|Derek]]'s insurability.
His professional judgment — that [[derek-burtell|Derek]] was a bad risk but not an impossible one — enabled the policy that set the investigation in motion (004-012).

## Character

Tremayne appears only briefly, but his presence adds dimension to the [[the-indescribable|Indescribable]]'s operations.
The company employs not just detectives and clerks but also doctors — an entire ecosystem of professionals whose skills are directed toward financial rather than humanitarian ends.
Tremayne's career trajectory — from saving lives to pricing them — encapsulates the novel's theme of commodification.

## Significance

Dr. Tremayne represents the moral complexity of professional expertise. He is good at his job, and his job serves a legitimate social function (insurance).
But his job also involves reducing human lives to actuarial tables. Knox does not condemn this — the novel's tone is too urbane for moralizing — but he makes the reader aware of it.
Tremayne is the human face of the calculation that [[derek-burtell|Derek]]'s life is worth [[the-burtell-legacy|fifty thousand pounds]].
