## Role

Wheathampton is a small settlement near the upper reaches of the Thames, a short distance from the [Boy Scout encampment](/place/the-island-in-the-thames) opposite [Shipcote Lock](/place/shipcote-lock).
It appears only peripherally in the narrative — as a supply point for the scouts and as a transport node in the hypothetical reconstruction of the murder —
yet its role in both the alibi timeline and the escape theory makes it a quietly significant location.

## Quotations

> "We had only just moved in, and that morning the bigger boys had gone over to Wheathampton with the trek-cart to bring our stores over. Only the little ones were here, cleaning up and so on." — The Scout leader, explaining why the camp could not assist [Nigel Burtell](/person/nigel-burtell) on the morning of the incident [005-003]

> "Then you were over at Wheathampton yourself?" — [Miles Bredon](/person/miles-bredon), questioning the scout leader [005-004]

> "He ties a weight to the body, digs a hole in the bottom of the canoe, gets out and makes tracks for the high road, or perhaps for Wheathampton Station." — Bredon's reconstruction of how the murderer might have escaped [007-016]

## Narrative Significance

Wheathampton serves two linked narrative functions.
First, the scouts' supply run to Wheathampton explains why the encampment was short-handed on the morning of the canoe incident — the older, more capable boys were away fetching stores,
leaving only younger scouts who could not have rendered effective assistance to [Nigel Burtell](/person/nigel-burtell). This detail is critical to the alibi timeline.

Second, Wheathampton Station provides a hypothetical escape node in [Miles Bredon](/person/miles-bredon)'s reconstruction of the crime.
If the murderer needed to leave the river unseen, Wheathampton Station offered a rail connection away from the more conspicuous [Shipcote Station](/place/shipcote-station).
The distance between the two stations — and the choice between them — becomes part of the geographical logic of the reconstruction.
