## Discovery

The footprints at [[shipcote-lock]] are first referenced obliquely when [[miles-bredon]] and [[angela-bredon]] plan their reconnaissance of the lock. Bredon suggests a cover story involving the gardener's husband, who is "looking for footprints on the back lawn" [004-046] — an ironic foreshadowing of the footprints that will become central to the case.

The footprints themselves enter the investigation through photography. Film Number Five on the spool recovered from [[derek-burtell]]'s camera contains an image "taken at an irregular angle which played havoc with the perspective," looking down stone steps "on each of which a footprint was discernible" [007-006]. The photograph appears to show the footprints of someone who stood on [[the-weir-bridge]] to photograph the corpse.

## The Photographic Evidence

The footprint photograph is one of five exposures on Derek Burtell's film. Its anomalous character — the tilted angle, the poor focus except on the footprints themselves — immediately attracts Bredon's attention. He examines the photograph with the insight of a photographic expert, recognizing that the image is not the accidental exposure it purports to be.

The key observation is that the footprints were photographed *before* the photograph of the canoe: "He took a photograph of the footprints before he took a photograph of the canoe" [007-017]. This temporal sequence is inconsistent with the theory that someone discovered the corpse, photographed it, and left footprints in the process. The footprints were there *first*.

## Bredon's Reconstruction

Bredon's analysis of the footprint evidence proceeds through several stages:

**The Staging**: Bredon concludes that the footprints were deliberately created. [[nigel-burtell]] made the footprints "in order to suggest that somebody had been standing on the bridge with bare feet to photograph the corpse" [024-030]. They were a theatrical prop, part of an elaborate deception.

**The Photographer's Mistake**: In his careful staging of the crime scene, Nigel made one critical error — he photographed his *own* footprints. This was "a deliberate snapshot of his own footprints on the bridge — footprints which he had deliberately made" [024-030]. The photograph was not evidence of a murderer but evidence of a fabrication.

**The Purpose**: The footprints were "meant to look like the footprints of a man walking across, barefoot" [020-029]. They were designed to suggest that someone — an unknown third party — had stood on the bridge to photograph the scene of the crime, thereby directing suspicion away from Nigel and toward a hypothetical intruder.

## The Evidential Paradox

The footprints present an intriguing evidential paradox. They were fabricated evidence, yet their very fabrication becomes the evidence that unravels the case. As Bredon explains:

> "The footprints on the bridge had, after all, a meaning of their own" [025-005].

What was intended to deceive becomes, under Bredon's analysis, the instrument of detection. The footprints point not to a murderer but to the person who staged the murder scene — and in doing so, they point to the murderer.

## The Wickstead Connection

The footprint evidence is linked to another investigative thread: the records at [[wickstead]], the Oxford boot-shop. Bredon discovers that [[derek-burtell]] had hammertoes while [[nigel-burtell]] did not — a fact recorded in Wickstead's "portentous volume" of client measurements.

> "Derek Burtell had hammertoes; Nigel hadn't. And, oddly enough, it was in looking to see whether Nigel had that I found out about Derek. Their foot-statistics were close together at Wickstead's, on opposite sides of the same page." [024-030]

The Wickstead records provide the anatomical key to the footprint puzzle. The footprints on the bridge could not be Derek's because their conformation matched Nigel's feet. The physical evidence and the documentary record converge.

## Title Resonance

The novel's title — *The Footsteps at the Lock* — names this central piece of evidence. The "footsteps" are both literal (the footprints photographed on the bridge) and metaphorical (the traces left by the criminal, the "footsteps" of the investigation itself). Throughout the narrative, characters search for footsteps: the gardener's husband looks for them on the lawn, Bredon looks for them on the bridge, and the investigation follows the footsteps of the perpetrator through a labyrinth of deception.
