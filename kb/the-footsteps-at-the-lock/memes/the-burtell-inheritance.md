# The Burtell Inheritance

"Such doubts, entirely methodical in character, are suggested by the last will and testament of Sir John Burtell" (001-002).
The old barrister's will, written after his two sons were killed in the Great War, left his fortune — some fifty thousand pounds — to his elder grandson Derek,
with a contingent remainder to Nigel should Derek die before reaching the age of twenty-five.

## Summary

Sir John Burtell, a barrister of note in the late Victorian era, died in the influenza epidemic of 1918 (001-002). Both his sons had predeceased him, killed in action in 1917.
His will left the bulk of his property to his elder grandson Derek, then a child; if Derek died before twenty-five, the fortune would pass to Charles' son Nigel.

The old man had been wise enough to place the money in trust until the heir reached twenty-five: "Very wisely, then, he left the fifty thousand in trust" (001-003).
He had not been wise enough to foresee what his grandsons would become.

## Timeline

- **1918**: Sir John Burtell dies; will established with trust provisions (001-002)
- **Following years**: Derek and Nigel grow up estranged, "little in the character of either to make it desirable" that they meet (001-004)
- **Derek's Oxford years**: He runs up debts, borrows heavily from moneylenders against his expected inheritance (001-012)
- **Nigel's twenty-first birthday**: Celebrated with a mock funeral (001-012)
- **Derek approaches twenty-five**: Moneylenders panic, force him to take out life insurance with the Indescribable (001-013)
- **July**: Derek disappears on the river; Nigel stands to inherit

## Participants

| Character | Role | Significance |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Sir John Burtell | Testator | Created the financial structure that would become a motive for murder |
| Derek Burtell | Primary heir | The spendthrift whose death would enrich his cousin |
| Nigel Burtell | Contingent heir | Stood to gain £50,000 if Derek died before twenty-five |
| The moneylenders | Creditors | Lent Derek money against his future inheritance; insured his life |
| The Indescribable | Insurer | Underwrote Derek's life; employed Bredon to investigate |

## Location

The inheritance had no fixed location — it was a legal construct that followed the cousins wherever they went.
But its effects were felt in Oxford (where Derek ran up bills), London (where the moneylenders operated), and ultimately on the upper Thames (where Derek died).

## Evidence & Clues

- **The will itself**: Left fifty thousand pounds in trust until the heir reached twenty-five (001-002)
- **Derek's debts**: He "applied for financial help to strangers, less Gentile than genteel" (001-012)
- **The insurance policy**: The Indescribable insured Derek's life "for a very reasonable premium" (001-013)
- **The timing**: Derek was within a month of his twenty-fifth birthday when he disappeared
- **Nigel's knowledge**: He was "reported" of the insurance arrangements — he knew the financial stakes (001-013)

## Impact on the Investigation

The inheritance is the motive. Bredon establishes early that "by death of D., N. stands to gain £50,000" (013-001). But this alone is not sufficient — the will's terms require proof of death.
Without a body, Nigel cannot claim the money. This paradox becomes central: Nigel had motive to kill Derek but also motive to ensure the death was provable. The missing body is the puzzle within the puzzle.
