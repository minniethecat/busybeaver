# The Canoe Accident

The event at [[Shipcote Lock]] that triggers the entire mystery. [[Derek Burtell]]'s canoe drifted through half-open lock gates while [[Nigel Burtell|Nigel]] was ashore with his camera — or so Nigel's account claims.
The question of whether this was a genuine [[accident-or-design|accident or a designed fraud]] is the novel's central puzzle, and the lock's physical geography — the straight channel, the winding weir stream, the island,
the bridge — provides the key to its solution (002-004).

## Summary

Derek and Nigel Burtell set out from [[Oxford]] on a canoe expedition up the Thames, prescribed by [[dr-simmonds|Dr. Simmonds]] as health therapy for Derek's unspecified complaints (001-020).
At Shipcote Lock, Nigel went ashore to photograph the structure. The lock gates were half-open; the current caught the canoe and swept it downstream with Derek lying motionless in the bottom (002-008).

The lock-keeper watched the canoe vanish below the lock wall — the last confirmed sighting of Derek alive. Nigel ran along the towpath but lost sight of the canoe around a bend.
Three days later, the canoe was found abandoned at [[Eaton Bridge]], three miles downstream. Despite extensive dragging of the river, no body was ever recovered (003-010).

## Evidence & Clues

The physical evidence from the canoe accident is sparse but significant:

| Evidence | Description | Interpretive Significance |
|----------|------------|--------------------------|
| The undamaged canoe | No sign of collision or struggle (002-005) | Argues against accidental drowning — a man falling from a canoe would typically capsize it |
| The camera | Found wedged in bridge framework (007-035) | Deliberately placed or genuinely dropped? |
| The lock gates | Half-open — by accident or design? (002-004) | The central physical question: who left the gates half-open, and why? |
| No body | Despite extensive dragging of the river (003-010) | The absence that structures the entire investigation |
| Nigel's wet clothes | Arrived at [[the-gudgeon-inn]] soaked (009-044) | Consistent with swimming after the canoe — or with a deliberate wetting |

## Impact on the Investigation

The accident's ambiguity — [[accident-or-design]] — defines the entire investigation. Every piece of evidence can be read both ways. The absence of a body makes it impossible to determine whether a crime occurred.
The undamaged canoe suggests no struggle, but also suggests no accident. The lock gates were half-open — but who opened them, and when?

[[Miles Bredon|Bredon]]'s reconstruction of the accident — paddling the same route, timing the same journey, photographing the same lock — is the novel's methodological centerpiece. By recreating the physical conditions of the accident, he demonstrates that Nigel's account is physically impossible: the timing, the river currents, and the lock geography cannot be reconciled with Nigel's story (014-025).
