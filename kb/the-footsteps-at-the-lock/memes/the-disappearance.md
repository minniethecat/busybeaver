# The Disappearance

The vanishing of Derek Burtell from a drifting canoe at Shipcote Lock on the River Thames.
This event — part accident, part possible crime — is the central mystery that drives the entire narrative, and its ambiguity is the novel's structural principle (002-004).

## The Event

Derek Burtell and his cousin Nigel set out from Oxford on a canoe expedition up the Thames, prescribed by Dr. Simmonds as a health measure (001-020).
At Shipcote Lock, Nigel went ashore to photograph the lock while Derek remained in the canoe. The lock gates were half-open; the current caught the canoe and swept it downstream with Derek lying in the bottom (002-008).

Nigel, standing on the bank, watched as "the canoe, with its remaining occupant, was being swept by the current downstream through the half-open lock gates" (002-008).
He ran along the towpath after it, but could not catch up.

## The Search

The canoe was found abandoned at Eaton Bridge, some three miles downstream from Shipcote Lock (003-009). Derek was not in it.
The canoe showed "no mark of any collision" (002-005), making it unclear whether Derek had fallen overboard, jumped, or been pushed.

Nigel "wandered up and down restlessly, scanning the river reach after reach with his glasses" (003-007). No body was found. The river was dragged. Nothing was recovered (003-010).

## The Ambiguity

The disappearance's defining feature is its ambiguity. No body was found, which meant:
- Derek might be dead (drowned, perhaps accidentally)
- Derek might be alive (having staged his death to escape creditors or defraud the insurance company)
- Derek might have been murdered (by Nigel, to claim the legacy)

This ambiguity was precisely what made the Indescribable's position difficult: they needed to prove Derek was alive after his twenty-fifth birthday to avoid paying the insurance claim.
Bredon was tasked with finding proof of life — or, failing that, proof of death before the critical date (004-024).

## The Theories

Three theories competed throughout the investigation:

1. **Accident**: Derek, weakened by his "ruined health" (001-012) and possibly intoxicated, genuinely fell from the canoe and drowned
2. **Suicide**: Derek, facing financial ruin and physical collapse, chose to end his life in the river
3. **Faked Death**: Derek staged his disappearance — with or without Nigel's help — to defraud the Indescribable and escape his debts (019-006)

Bredon systematically tested each theory against the evidence.
The camera film, the Bradshaw cipher, and the testimony of key witnesses gradually eliminated the first two possibilities, pointing toward a staged disappearance (017-037).

## The Resolution

The disappearance was resolved when the evidence showed that Derek had been alive after the supposed date of his death.
The Bradshaw cipher contained messages exchanged between Derek and an accomplice after the canoe trip (016-010). The camera film showed deliberate manipulation (007-044).
And the financial motive — Nigel's inheritance and the insurance payout — explained the conspiracy.

Derek had not disappeared at all. He had gone into hiding, aided by Nigel, to collect the insurance money through a presumed death. The "disappearance" was a performance, and the Thames was its stage.

## Significance

The disappearance structure is Knox's most sophisticated plot mechanism.
Unlike a simple murder mystery where the crime is established and the question is "who did it," Knox's novel sustains a more fundamental uncertainty: was there even a crime?
This ontological ambiguity — is Derek alive or dead? — makes *The Footsteps at the Lock* a pioneering example of the "did a crime occur?" subgenre of detective fiction.
