# The Police Investigation

The official investigation into the disappearance and presumed death of Derek Burtell, conducted by the county police under Inspector Leyland — first as a missing person inquiry, then as a murder investigation.

## Summary

When Derek Burtell failed to reappear after his canoe trip, the lock-keeper communicated with the police (003-011). The initial investigation treated the case as a missing person — a young man lost on the river.
But as evidence accumulated, attention turned to Nigel Burtell, who "stood to win a legacy by his death, if that death could be proved" (009-039).

## Timeline

- **July 5**: Derek reported missing to the county police (003-011)
- **Early investigation**: Canoe found near Eaton Bridge; river searched; no body recovered
- **Nigel's alibi examined**: He had taken the 9:14 train from Shipcote to Oxford, sat his viva voce, and was supposedly in Oxford all day (010-002)
- **Police constraint**: Without a body, the police could not charge Nigel with murder. "Nigel, after all, was not even asked to give an account of his movements until some days after the event" (010-002)
- **Bredon's involvement**: "As usual the county police didn't call in Scotland Yard" (009-017). Instead, the Indescribable insurance company sent Miles Bredon to investigate
- **Reconstruction**: Bredon's private investigation reconstructed the crime, proving Nigel's guilt (014-025, 018-039)

## Participants

| Character | Role | Significance |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Inspector Leyland | Lead investigator | County police inspector; competent but limited by the lack of a body |
| Miles Bredon | Private investigator | Indescribable agent who solved the case where the official investigation stalled |
| Nigel Burtell | Suspect | Never formally charged until Bredon's reconstruction proved his guilt |
| The lock-keeper | Witness | Provided timeline testimony about the canoe's passage |
| Shipcote Station staff | Witnesses | Remembered Nigel catching the 9:14 train (009-036) |

## Location

The investigation spanned the upper Thames: Shipcote Lock, Eaton Bridge, the river between them, Shipcote Station (009-036), and Oxford. The police also searched downstream toward London (010-001).

## Evidence & Clues

- **No body**: The fundamental difficulty — "pretty poor evidence of the death" (009-042)
- **The canoe**: Found intact near Eaton Bridge — no sign of violence
- **Nigel's photograph**: Of the lock-keeper at Shipcote — later revealed to be of a different lock (024-021)
- **Train records**: Confirmed Nigel took the 9:14 from Shipcote (009-036)
- **The Bradshaw cipher**: Nigel's annotated railway timetable (003-001)
- **Angela's testimony**: About the "Mr. X" who joined Nigel on the river (015-005)

## Impact on the Investigation

The police investigation was hamstrung by procedure. Without a body, there could be no murder charge.
"Nigel Burtell is going to tell us the whole story from his point of view, and I don't see how the police can stop him" (018-036).
Bredon's private inquiry succeeded precisely because he was not constrained by police procedure — he could reconstruct, speculate, and set traps that the official investigation could not.
