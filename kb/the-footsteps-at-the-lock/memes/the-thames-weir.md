# The Thames Weir

The system of locks and weirs that controls the Upper Thames, making it navigable for pleasure boats and providing the physical mechanism for Derek Burtell's disappearance.
The lock system is both setting and plot engine — without it, there could be no mystery (002-002).

## The Lock System

The Thames above Oxford is described as "a narrow, artificial waterway, with an unambitious lock at intervals of a mile or two" (002-002).
These locks regulate the river's flow, creating pools of navigable water between weirs. Each lock has gates that can be opened to let boats through, and a weir that allows excess water to flow over.

Shipcote Lock, where Derek disappeared, is described with particular attention: "Shipcote Lock is among the most ambitious of these structures, a wedding of water with stone" (002-006).
The lock gates were half-open when the accident occurred — a detail that Bredon later interrogates: who opened them? When? Was it the lock-keeper, or someone else? (002-004).

## The Weir as Danger

In the peaceful landscape of the Upper Thames, the weir represents hidden danger.
The river appears placid — "you see nothing but high banks and rushes, and willow-groves overhanging the stream" (002-002) — but the weir is a point where the current concentrates,
where water rushes over stone with enough force to drown a man or sweep away a canoe.

This duality — surface calm, hidden danger — mirrors the novel's thematic structure. The Thames is both the idyllic setting of a summer boating holiday and the scene of a possible crime.
The weir is both a feat of civil engineering and a mortal hazard.

## The Lock-Keeper

The lock-keeper is a figure of ambiguous significance. He saw Nigel on the bridge with his camera (002-008). His testimony supports Nigel's alibi.
But Bredon questions whether the lock-keeper's account can be relied upon — did he really see what he thinks he saw, or was his attention elsewhere?
"One can always get conversation out of a lock-keeper" (004-043), Bredon remarks, acknowledging both the lock-keeper's value as a witness and his fallibility.

## The Infrastructure of Mystery

Knox's attention to the physical infrastructure of the river — locks, weirs, towpaths, bridges — represents an important innovation in detective fiction.
Rather than treating setting as mere backdrop, Knox makes the river's engineering an active participant in the plot.
The half-open lock gates are not just scenic detail; they are the precise mechanism that makes the disappearance possible.

This attention to infrastructure also serves the novel's realism. The Thames is not a generic river but a specific, engineered waterway with its own rules and rhythms.
Bredon's investigation must account for these physical facts — the speed of the current, the distance between locks, the time it takes a canoe to drift from Shipcote to Eaton Bridge.
