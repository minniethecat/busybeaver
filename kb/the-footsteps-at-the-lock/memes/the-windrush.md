# The Windrush

A tributary flowing into the upper Thames, referenced in the investigation as a geographical marker delimiting the search area and in the narrative as a site for riverside leisure activities that reveal character.

## Location and Geography

The Windrush is a tributary that joins the upper Thames.
It is mentioned as a geographical boundary in the context of the search for evidence related to the canoe incident: "they were, you see, not nearer than the Windrush" [005-010].
The river is sufficiently shallow and narrow in places that a punt cannot navigate up it — as Bredon notes, "you couldn't take a punt up the Windrush, for example; he can't leave the main stream" [014-002].
This navigability constraint is significant for the reconstruction of the crime: it limits the possible movements of a canoe or punt, confining the relevant action to the main Thames channel.

## Role in the Narrative

The Windrush serves two functions in the story:

1. **Search Boundary**: During the inquest and investigation, the Windrush is referenced as a geographical marker delimiting the search area for evidence connected to the canoe incident [005-010].
It establishes the upstream limit of the zone within which physical evidence might be found.

2. **Recreational Setting**: Angela Bredon spends an afternoon "mud-larking on the Windrush," an outing that showcases her familiarity with the river and its environs [016-002].
This scene provides character texture — Angela is portrayed as possessing "the gift, rare in her sex, of being able to amuse herself" — and reinforces the Bredons' intimate knowledge of the Thames valley,
a knowledge that proves essential to the investigation.

## Quotations

> "They were, you see, not nearer than the Windrush, and if they done the damage they must have come a long way." — A witness at the inquest, establishing the Windrush as the nearest plausible point of origin for floating debris [005-010]

> "You couldn't take a punt up the Windrush, for example; he can't leave the main stream." — Bredon, explaining the navigational constraints that shape his reconstruction of the murderer's movements [014-002]

> "Angela had spent an afternoon mud-larking on the Windrush. Angela had the gift, rare in her sex, of being able to amuse herself." [016-002]
