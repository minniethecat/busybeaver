# White Bracton

A village inland from the upper Thames that becomes significant in the investigation when a letter addressed to Derek Burtell is discovered at its inn.
The journey to White Bracton and the inquiries made there provide a turning point in Bredon's reconstruction of events.

## Location

White Bracton is described as a village "inland" from the river, "a mile or two away" from the Thames [019-008]. It is reachable from the river by a journey across country.
The village has several pubs, but the inn in question — "The Inn, White Bracton" — is the one that plays a role in the plot [017-011].

## The White Bracton Letter

The central significance of White Bracton is a letter addressed to Derek Burtell at the inn. This letter becomes a crucial piece of the investigative puzzle:

- The letter was written to Derek at the White Bracton inn, under the belief that he would be staying there [017-022].
- Bredon makes a journey to White Bracton to retrieve the letter and investigate [017-004].
- The existence and contents of the letter bear directly on the question of whether Derek knew about certain developments before his disappearance [017-029].
- Nigel later comments that if he had known about the letter, he "would never have known anything about White Bracton" [018-031].

The letter's role is discussed extensively across multiple chapters (16–19), making White Bracton one of the more narratively significant locations despite its physical remove from the river.

## Bredon's Inquiries

Bredon travels to White Bracton to investigate the circumstances of the letter and to trace Derek's movements.
He questions the innkeeper and examines the premises, discovering that the inn is "a pretty putrid little place" according to the description relayed through the narrative [019-009].
His inquiries at White Bracton help establish the timeline and confirm aspects of the alibi.
