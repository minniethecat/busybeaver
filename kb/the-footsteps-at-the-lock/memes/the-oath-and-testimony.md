# The Oath and Testimony

The formal mechanism by which witnesses' accounts are gathered and tested in the investigation.
The oath — the sworn promise to tell the truth — is both the foundation of legal evidence and, as the novel demonstrates, a surprisingly fragile guarantee of veracity (007-016).

## The Forms of Testimony

The novel features several forms of testimony:

1. **The lock-keeper's account**: Informal, given to Nigel and later to the police and Bredon. Not sworn, but treated as reliable because the lock-keeper has no apparent motive to lie (002-008)
2. **Nigel's narrative**: Given repeatedly — to the lock-keeper, to the police, to Bredon. Inconsistent in small details, which Bredon notes but does not immediately challenge (003-008)
3. **The photographic evidence**: A mechanical form of testimony — the camera as silent witness. Bredon treats it as testimony rather than fact, subject to cross-examination like any human witness (007-006)
4. **The Bradshaw cipher**: Documentary testimony — a written record that must be interpreted. Its meaning is not self-evident but must be extracted through decoding (016-010)

## The Limits of the Oath

The oath is supposed to guarantee truth through the threat of divine or legal sanction. But the novel shows how this guarantee is limited:

- **Truthful but incomplete**: A witness may tell the truth as far as it goes, while omitting crucial context
- **Truthful but misleading**: Nigel's account of photographing the lock may be literally true while creating a false impression of his actions (007-016)
- **Truthful but constructed**: The lock-keeper's testimony may accurately report what he saw, but what he saw may itself have been staged (004-043)

The oath can compel honesty but not completeness, precision, or context.

## Bredon as Cross-Examiner

Bredon's approach to testimony resembles cross-examination more than interrogation. He does not accuse witnesses of lying. Instead, he explores the limits of their knowledge: what exactly did you see?
When exactly did you see it? How certain are you?

This Socratic method exposes the gaps in testimony without requiring witnesses to contradict themselves.
The lock-keeper's account weakens not because he is caught in a lie but because Bredon demonstrates that the account cannot bear the weight placed on it (004-043).

## Testimony and the Novel's Form

The novel's form is itself an exercise in testimony.
The narrator, who addresses the reader directly, is a kind of witness — someone who claims knowledge of events but whose relationship to those events is never fully specified.
The narrator's testimony about Bredon's investigation is layered on top of the witnesses' testimony about the disappearance.

This Chinese-box structure of embedded testimony — narrator reports Bredon's cross-examination of Nigel's account of the lock-keeper's testimony —
creates a hall-of-mirrors effect that is characteristic of Knox's narrative technique.
