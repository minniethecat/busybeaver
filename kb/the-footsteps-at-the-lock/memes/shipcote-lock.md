# Shipcote Lock

A lock on the upper reaches of the Thames, situated between [[Oxford]] and Lechlade where "the villages stand to one side and let it pass" (002-002).
Shipcote Lock is at once a triumph of Victorian engineering and a fairy garden — "a galleon of flowers, with crimson ramblers for its rigging" — whose stone collar, roaring waterfall,
and half-open gates become the physical stage on which the central mystery of [[the-canoe-accident|Derek Burtell's disappearance]] is enacted (002-006).
It is the lock where [[Nigel Burtell]]'s alibi was forged, where [[the-lock-keeper]] saw the canoe drift through, and where [[Miles Bredon]]'s reconstruction finally exposed the truth.

## Location

Shipcote Lock lies on an isolated stretch of the upper Thames where the river runs through country that "the villages stand to one side and let it pass" (002-002).
The lock and weir are both at the higher end of their respective channels, and behind them stretches "a considerable island, the further part of which is woody and uncultivated" (002-005).
A narrow plank bridge crosses the weir, and a light iron bridge spans the lock-stream about a hundred yards below.
The lock-keeper's house stands on the mainland to the left, but the greater part of his garden covers the upper end of Shipcote Island.

Three miles downstream lies [[Eaton Bridge]] (003-009). The nearest inn above the lock is at Millington Bridge (009-012).
Shipcote Station serves the branch railway line to Oxford — "a mighty one-horse sort of station" from which [[Nigel Burtell|Nigel]] claimed to have caught the train on the day Derek vanished (009-037).
The geography of the lock — the straight lock channel versus the winding weir stream, the island, the bridges — becomes the physical framework within which every element of the mystery must be tested.

## Description

The lock is a sanctuary of water, stone, and flowers. A waterfall roars at the bifurcation of the stream, heralding the approach (002-005).
The lock channel — "not a mere precaution against floods; it is also a shortcut" — runs dead straight for nearly a mile before rejoining the weir-stream after unnecessary windings (002-005).
The lock-keeper's [[the-thames-conservancy|Conservancy]] garden is "among the most ambitious of these fairy gardens; its crowded beds of pinks and sweetwilliam, stocks and nasturtium, snapdragon and Noah's-nightcap,
seem to rise out of the water's edge like a galleon of flowers, with crimson ramblers for its rigging" (002-006).

Man has divided the stream, dammed one half and forced the other "into a stone collar" — then, adding to the violence, out-dared "with this profusion of blooms the paler glories of the river bank" (002-006).
This tension between engineering and nature, between the stone collar and the fairy garden, is the lock's defining characteristic — and the novel's.

## Role in the Story

Shipcote Lock is the pivot of the narrative. On the morning of July 3, [[Nigel Burtell|Nigel]] and [[Derek Burtell]] pass through the lock in their canoe.
Nigel disembarks to catch the train to Oxford from Shipcote Station, leaving Derek alone in the canoe drifting downstream (002-013).
The lock-keeper watches the canoe vanish below the lock wall — the last confirmed sighting of Derek alive.

Three days later, Derek is reported missing. The discovery of the abandoned canoe floating near [[Eaton Bridge]], some three miles below the lock, triggers the inquest (003-008).
The central mystery — [[accident-or-design|accident or design]]? — revolves around what happened between Shipcote Lock and Eaton Bridge.

The lock-keeper's testimony becomes critical. He last saw Derek lying motionless in the canoe: "Well, he ain't in much of a hurry, anyway" (002-023).
He remembers Nigel's camera, Nigel's inquiry about the train, and the exact time the canoe passed through (002-019, 002-008). Every detail of his testimony must be tested against the physical reality of the lock.

## Significance to the Mystery

Shipcote Lock is the fulcrum of the entire mystery. The critical question is whether Derek was alive or dead when he passed through.
Bredon's reconstruction proves that Nigel must have deposited Derek's body somewhere **above** the lock, then passed through with a live accomplice, before scuttling the canoe below (014-025).
The lock geography — the island, the weir, the bridge, the railway path — forms the physical frame within which the alibi must be tested.

The [[the-photograph-of-the-lock|photograph of the lock]] becomes a key piece of evidence.
Nigel photographed the lock-keeper bending over his gardening — but the photograph, when compared with Bredon's own photograph of Shipcote,
turns out to be of a different lock altogether: "very much like the one at Shipcote Lock; it's a common type, you know" (024-021).

The lock — unique in its particularity, generic in its Conservancy type — is both the scene of the crime and the instrument of its exposure.
