# The Dragging of the Weir

The weir at Shipcote Lock — where "lock and weir are both at the higher end of their respective channels" (002-005) — became a focus of the search for evidence.
The weir stream, with its waterfall roar and treacherous currents, could trap or conceal objects carried downstream from above the lock.

## Summary

The weir at Shipcote diverts part of the Thames into a parallel channel that winds for nearly a mile before rejoining the lock-stream below the lock (002-005).
The weir itself is crossed by "a narrow plank bridge" that renders Shipcote Island accessible from the right bank (002-005).
When Bredon organized the search of the river, the weir pool — with its deep water and turbulent currents — was a natural place to look for evidence.

## Location

The weir is at the higher end of the channel opposite the lock. Behind both lock and weir stretches Shipcote Island, "the further part of which is woody and uncultivated" (002-005).
The weir bridge — a narrow plank structure — crosses the weir itself. The water below the weir is deep and turbulent, creating a natural trap for objects carried by the current.

## Evidence & Clues

- **The weir's hydrology**: Water passing over the weir creates a recirculating current that can trap objects indefinitely
- **The island**: The wooded upper portion of Shipcote Island could conceal evidence on the bank
- **The weir bridge**: A vantage point for observing the river in both directions

## Impact on the Investigation

The dragging of the weir was part of the systematic physical search that followed Bredon's deductive reconstruction.
While the intellectual case against Nigel was built on logic and testimony, the physical evidence from the river — the weir, the lock, the island —
provided the material confirmation needed to bring the case to its conclusion.
