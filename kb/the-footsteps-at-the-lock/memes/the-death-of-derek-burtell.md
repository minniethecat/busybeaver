# The Death of Derek Burtell

The central mystery of the case: Derek Burtell vanished from a canoe on the upper Thames between Shipcote Lock and Eaton Bridge on July 3. His body was never found.
He was twenty-four years old, less than a year from his inheritance.

## Summary

Derek Burtell and his cousin Nigel set out on a canoe trip upstream from Oxford. On the return journey, Nigel disembarked at Shipcote Lock to catch the train to Oxford for his viva voce examination.
Derek continued downstream alone. He was never seen alive again (002-013, 002-023). Three days later, the empty canoe was discovered floating near Eaton Bridge — the paddle missing, no sign of its occupant (003-008).
The presumption of death was established through investigation, though the body was never recovered (004-024).

## Timeline

- **July 2**: Derek and Nigel paddled up from Oxford toward Cricklade (002-001)
- **July 3, morning**: The cousins passed through Shipcote Lock heading downstream. Nigel left for the Oxford train (002-013)
- **July 3, after Nigel left**: Derek drifted downstream alone; the lock-keeper watched the canoe vanish below the lock wall (002-023)
- **July 3–4**: No contact from Derek. The canoe found adrift near Eaton Bridge (003-008)
- **July 5**: Derek reported missing; police notified (003-011)
- **Subsequent weeks**: Investigation by Inspector Leyland and Miles Bredon; Derek's death established but body never found

## Location

The death occurred on the upper Thames, on the stretch between Shipcote Lock and Eaton Bridge (002-005, 003-009). The lock is upstream, the bridge approximately three miles downstream.
The river here is isolated — "the villages stand to one side and let it pass" (002-002). Shipcote Island, the weir, and the towpath all become critical to reconstructing the crime.

## Evidence & Clues

- **The empty canoe**: Found near Eaton Bridge, no paddle, no body (003-008)
- **The lock-keeper's testimony**: Last saw Derek lying motionless in the canoe — "Well, he ain't in much of a hurry, anyway" (002-023)
- **Nigel's movements**: Caught the 9:14 train from Shipcote Station to Oxford; the station staff remembered him (009-036)
- **The camera photograph**: Nigel photographed the lock-keeper at Shipcote — but the photograph turned out to be of a different lock (024-021)
- **Insurance motive**: Derek's life was insured by the Indescribable for £50,000; Nigel was the contingent beneficiary of Sir John Burtell's will (001-002)
- **The Bradshaw cipher**: Nigel's alibi relied on train timetables encoded in a Bradshaw (003-001)

## Impact on the Investigation

Derek's death is the fulcrum of the entire investigation. Every other event — the canoe expedition, the discovery, the cipher correspondence, the arrest, the reconstruction — orbits this central fact.
The question is not whether Derek died but **how**: was it an accident, a suicide, or murder?
Bredon's reconstruction proves that Derek must have been killed before the canoe passed through Shipcote Lock — meaning Nigel was present at the time of death (014-025).

## Quotations

> "If he should die before his twenty-fifth birthday, the fifty thousand would go to Nigel, and the moneylenders would have no assets to satisfy their claims." (001-012)

> "I pointed out that, as far as material considerations went, Nigel Burtell stood to gain nothing by Derek's death unless it could be proved that Derek was dead." (013-001)
