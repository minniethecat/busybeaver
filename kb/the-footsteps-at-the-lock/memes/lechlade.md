# Lechlade

A town on the upper Thames above Oxford, near the head of the river's navigable course.
It serves as the upstream limit of the novel's geographical setting and is noted for its distinctive church — described by Nigel Burtell as "a fantasy" and by Bredon as an aid to identifying a crucial piece of evidence.

## Location

Lechlade is situated on the Thames above Oxford, described as one terminus of the stretch of river within which the story's action takes place: "But here, in these upper waters,
it is divorced from the companionship of human life; the villages stand to one side and the river runs alone" [002-002].
The journey "down to Oxford from Cricklade and Lechlade way" establishes it as the practical head of navigation [001-020].

## The Church at Lechlade

The church at Lechlade receives particular attention in the narrative and plays an unexpected role in the investigation.
Nigel Burtell, with characteristic enthusiasm, describes it as "a fantasy, you know; an idea made real in stone" [008-010].
Its porch is noted as a distinctive architectural feature: "The porch at Lechlade was clearly the porch they wanted" [007-044].
The church's architectural character — including what is described as "the Lechlade gargoyle" [008-027] — provides one of the novel's few purely aesthetic observations,
but it also serves a forensic purpose: Bredon uses the church as a landmark to identify the location of photographs found in the film roll,
comparing its features to confirm the pictures were taken along the upper Thames.

## Role in the Narrative

Lechlade appears as both a geographical marker and a destination.
Bredon announces: "Very well then, you and I are going to take the car over to Lechlade" [007-043], making it the objective of an investigative motor excursion.
Having "been up the river as far as Lechlade" [007-046] is mentioned as a credential of familiarity with the Thames valley — a claim that Bredon challenges by suggesting the photographs might not belong to Nigel at all.
