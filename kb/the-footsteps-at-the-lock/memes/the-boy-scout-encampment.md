# The Boy Scout Encampment

A troop of approximately sixteen [[Boy Scouts]] encamped on the riverbank a little way downstream of [[Shipcote Lock]] throughout the summer of 1928.
The scouts' constant presence on the river — diving, swimming, and observing all traffic on the towpath — makes them an essential element of [[the-police-investigation|the investigation]],
functioning simultaneously as alibi witnesses, evidence discoverers, and [[Miles Bredon|Bredon's]] informal intelligence network (003-010).

## Summary

The scout encampment is first noted as a feature of the Thames landscape — one of the "encampments of boy scouts, mud-larking in the shallows" that dot the upper river (002-002).
Their position downstream of [[Shipcote Lock]] places them at a strategic vantage point: anyone moving along the towpath must pass their camp,
and anything dropped or thrown into the river might wash downstream into their diving zone.

The scouts enter the investigation in three distinct capacities.
First, as alibi witnesses: it was "unlikely that they would have let a dripping stranger go past unnoticed" (003-010), making their failure to see [[Derek Burtell]] significant.
Second, as evidence finders: they discovered the second notecase/purse in the river after days of diving (018-015).
Third, as Bredon's informal allies: he "had not been slow to cultivate the acquaintance of the boy scouts" and used them as an extension of his investigative reach (010-029).

## Impact on the Investigation

The scouts' role in the investigation pivots on one crucial question: why did it take them nearly a week of diving in the same spot to find the second notecase?
Bredon's analysis of this question is characteristically precise. "It was nonsense supposing that Derek Burtell carried two purses. That meant that one or the other was a fraud, a blind" (018-015).
The visiting-card found in one notecase was "so obviously been put there" — planted evidence.

The timing of the discovery forces a critical conclusion: the notecase was dropped into the river not on the day of the disappearance, but later — "calmly overnight" (018-015).
This transforms the scouts from mere witnesses into the key to understanding that evidence was being deliberately manufactured and placed. Bredon traces the planting to [[mr-quirk|Mr.
Quirk]], who had visited the area the night before the discovery.

The scouts also serve as a running verification mechanism throughout the novel.
[[Angela Bredon|Angela]] notes that anyone fleeing the Gudgeon Inn would have to "pass a whole encampment of boy scouts" (009-045), making the scouts an ever-present observational constraint on the characters' movements.
Their encampment functions as a natural surveillance checkpoint on the otherwise lonely upper Thames.

## Quotations

> "You'll find the spot marked, not with a cross, but with a troop of about sixteen boy scouts with no clothes on, diving into the river all day in the hopes of fishing something up." (010-007)

> "Bredon had not been slow to cultivate the acquaintance of the boy scouts, and he had little doubt that it was one of these unofficial allies who was looking for him." (010-029)

> "The curious thing was that those scouts had been diving in that precise spot from Monday till Saturday, but it wasn't till Saturday they came across the notecase. Was it possible, I asked myself, that the notecase had been dropped in calmly overnight?" (018-015)

> "If he went along the towpath, he had to pass a whole encampment of boy scouts." (009-045)
