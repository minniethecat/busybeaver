## Description

Litchfield is a town in Hillsborough County, New Hampshire, situated on the east bank of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. It was once called Brenton's Farm, being part of a land grant made in 1656 to a fur-trader named Brenton who traded among the Native Americans. (005-404) The area was anciently the Indian territory of [[naticook|Naticook]]. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the township contained about five hundred inhabitants, of whom he and his brother saw none, and but few of their dwellings, because the high banks of the river concealed the houses. (005-404)

## On the River Route

The brothers' course on Monday morning lay between the territories of [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] on the west and Litchfield on the east. (005-404) Being on the river, whose banks were always high and generally concealed the few houses, the country appeared "much more wild and primitive than to the traveller on the neighboring roads." (005-404)

On the return journey, they passed the "humble village of Litchfield, with its steepleless meeting-house" standing on the opposite or east bank, near where a dense grove of willows backed by maples skirted the shore. (005-981) They also tasted the waters of [[naticook|Naticook Brook]] on the Litchfield side, near where French and his companions were ambuscaded by the Indians. (005-981)

## Historical Context

Litchfield's history is rich with frontier drama. At Cromwell's Falls — the first falls the brothers encountered on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], known to the Indians as Nesenkeag — [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recorded the story of a trader named Cromwell, who in the years after 1665 carried on a "lucrative trade" with the Indians, "weighing their furs with his foot." (005-474) When the Indians, enraged at his supposed or real deception, formed a resolution to murder him, Cromwell buried his wealth and escaped. Within hours, a party of the [[penacook-tribe-history|Penacook tribe]] arrived, and not finding him, burnt his habitation. The cellar of Cromwell's trading post, now overgrown with trees, was still visible on the high bank in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time. (005-474)

The lock-man at the falls told [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] that Cromwell's shovel and tongs had been ploughed up nearby, as well as a stone bearing his name. The New Hampshire Historical Collections recorded that "Some time after pewter was found in the well, and an iron pot and trammel in the sand." (005-474) On the opposite bank, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother found four arrow-heads and a small Indian stone tool — evidence that a wigwam had once stood there, the dwelling of the Indians with whom Cromwell traded.

Litchfield also claimed notable residents. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] read that "The Hon. Wyseman Clagett closed his life in this town" — described as "a classical scholar, a good lawyer, a wit, and a poet," whose old gray house was visible just below Great Nesenkeag Brook. (006-516) Across the river in [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], Hon. Mathew Thornton, one of the signers of the Declaration of American [[freedom|Independence]], had also resided.

## Thoreau's Observations

Near Cromwell's Falls, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed an extensive desert by the side of the road in Litchfield, where sand had been blown off to the depth of ten or twelve feet, leaving grotesque hillocks where clumps of bushes were firmly rooted. (005-522) The area had been a sheep-[[history|past]]ure thirty or forty years earlier, but the sheep, being worried by fleas, began to paw the ground until they broke the sod; the sand then began to blow, extending over forty or fifty acres. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted that the evil "might easily have been remedied, at first, by spreading birches with their leaves on over the sand, and fastening them down with stakes, to break the wind." (005-522) His laconic summary — "The fleas bit the sheep, and the sheep bit the ground" — exemplifies his eye for chains of unintended ecological consequence.
