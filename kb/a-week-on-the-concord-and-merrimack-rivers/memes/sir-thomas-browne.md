## Biography

Sir Thomas Browne was born in London and educated at Oxford, Montpellier, Padua, and Leiden, where he received his medical degree. He settled in Norwich in 1637 and practiced medicine there for the remainder of his life. A polymath with wide-ranging interests in science, religion, antiquarianism, and literature, Browne wrote some of the most distinctive prose of the seventeenth century, marked by a baroque Latinate style and an inquiring, skeptical sensibility.

## Works / Significance

Browne's most famous work, *Religio Medici* (1643), is a personal spiritual testament that reconciles scientific inquiry with [[religion|religious]] [[religion|faith]]. His *Pseudodoxia Epidemica* (1646) is an encyclopedic debunking of popular errors and superstitions. *Hydriotaphia, or Urn Burial* (1658) is a meditation on mortality and the vanity of human monuments, containing some of the most celebrated prose in the English language. His writing influenced [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]], the Romantics, and later writers including Virginia Woolf and Jorge Luis Borges.

## In Thoreau's Text

Browne appears in the "Sunday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, though [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references him indirectly through [[samuel-johnson|Dr. Johnson]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] paraphrases Johnson expressing wonder at Browne's assertion (003-692):

> which Dr. Johnson expresses at the assertion of Sir Thomas Browne that "his life has been a miracle of thirty years, which to relate, were not history but a piece of poetry, and would sound like a fable." (003-692–694)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], with characteristic inversion, argues that the wonder is not that Browne claimed his life was miraculous but "rather, that all men do not assert as much" (003-695). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Browne's statement as a springboard for his own conviction that every ordinary life, properly regarded, contains the material of poetry and fable.
