# Sophocles

## Biography

Sophocles (c. 497–406 BCE) was one of the three great tragedians of classical Athens, alongside Aeschylus and Euripides. He wrote over 120 plays, of which seven survive complete, including *Oedipus Rex*, *Antigone*, and *Electra*. Sophocles introduced innovations in dramatic structure — among them the addition of a third actor and the use of painted scenery — that shaped the development of Western theater. His plays explore the tension between human law and divine will, individual conscience and the demands of the state.

## Works / Significance

Sophocles's *Antigone* is among the most enduring works of Greek tragedy, dramatizing the conflict between Antigone's [[religion|religious]] duty to bury her brother Polynices and King Creon's edict forbidding it. The play has inspired philosophers from Hegel to Kierkegaard to Judith Butler, who have each found in it a profound meditation on law, kinship, and moral obligation. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], in *A Week*, draws on this classic work to illuminate his own [[thought|reflection]]s on duty and the claims of the dead.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] alludes to Sophocles in *Monday* during a passage reflecting on the moral imperative to honor the dead. "There are some passages in the Antigone of Sophocles, well known to scholars, of which I am re[[thought|mind]]ed in this connection," he writes, before noting that "Antigone has resolved to sprinkle sand on the dead body of her brother Polynices, notwithstanding the edict of King Creon condemning to death that one who should perform this service, which the Greeks deemed so important" (004-435). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Sophocles to connect his own river meditations — on what is owed to the dead, on the [[religion|sacred]]ness of certain duties beyond civil law — to the deepest currents of Western literature and moral thought.
