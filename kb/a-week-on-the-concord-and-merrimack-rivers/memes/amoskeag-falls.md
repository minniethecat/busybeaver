## Description

The Amoskeag Falls are rapids and falls on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at [[manchester-nh|Manchester, New Hampshire]]. The name comes from "Namaskeak," Abenaki for "great fishing place." (006-305) They were a major gathering point for Native Americans fishing for salmon and shad. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the falls had been modified by canals and mills.

## On the River Route

The brothers hear the Falls of Amoskeag above as they approach [[manchester-nh|Manchester]]. (006-270) They observe the artificial falls where mill canals drop the water in a spectacular cascade. (006-274) The pot-holes in the brown-stone are described. (006-343) Above Amoskeag, the river spreads wider. (006-611)

## Historical Context

Amoskeag Falls was a major Abenaki settlement and fishing ground for thousands of years. After European settlement, the falls were harnessed for industrial power. The Amoskeag [[menu|Manu]]facturing Company redirected the falls, moving them a mile downstream. (006-283)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the descent of Amoskeag Falls as "about fifty feet in all." (006-299) He examines pot-holes worn into the brown-stone by the ancient river. (006-343) The water is "clear, lucid, and greenish" (006-320). The name Namaskeak means "great fishing place." (006-305)
