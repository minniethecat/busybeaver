## Biography

**Nathaniel Hawthorne** (July 4, 1804 – May 19, 1864) was an American novelist, dark romantic, and short story writer. Born in Salem, Massachusetts, he was a contemporary and [[friendship|friend]] of [[henry-david-thoreau|Henry David Thoreau]] and [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]].

Hawthorne briefly lived at Brook Farm, a transcendentalist utopian community, and later settled in [[concord-mass|Concord]], where he was part of the town's literary circle.

## Works / Significance

His major works include *The Scarlet Letter* (1850), *The House of the Seven Gables* (1851), and *Mosses from an Old Manse* (1846). Hawthorne's fiction often explores the psychological complexity of sin, guilt, and morality in Puritan New England.

## In Thoreau's Text

Hawthorne appears in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* as a gentle, [[history|past]]oral presence. As [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother pass near [[concord-mass|Concord]], the text invokes Hawthorne:

> Here was our Hawthorne in the dale, (002-119)

The reference portrays Hawthorne as a shepherd figure tending his lambs — a poetic image that aligns with his residence at the Old Manse in [[concord-mass|Concord]], where he wrote *Mosses from an Old Manse*. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s brief but [[love|affection]]ate mention reflects their neighborly [[love|friendship]] in [[concord-mass|Concord]]'s literary community.
