# Penacook Tribe History

## Overview

The [[penacook|Penacook]] (also spelled Pennacook) were an Algonquian-speaking people who inhabited the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] Valley prior to European colonization. Their name derives from the Abenaki word meaning "at the bottom of the hill" or "place of the downward slope," reflecting their settlement at the falls and bends of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references the Penacook people and their leaders — most notably [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] and [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] — throughout [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]], treating them as emblematic figures of a disappearing world.

The [[penacook|Penacook]] were part of the larger Abenaki confederacy and maintained a network of villages along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] from its mouth at [[newburyport|Newburyport]] to the great bend at what is now [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire. They were hunter-gatherers who also practiced seasonal agriculture, planting corn, beans, and squash in the fertile river meadows.

**Sunday** touches on the broader context of Native American presence in New England, establishing the [[history|historical]] backdrop against which the journey unfolds. (003-1833) The [[penacook|Penacook]] are part of this larger tapestry of indigenous life that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] seeks to recover from the [[history|historical]] record.

**Tuesday** presents two extended passages on [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]], the great [[penacook|Penacook]] sachem, and his son [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] as "the greatest Indian that ever lived in these p[[art|arts]]" and recounts traditions of his magical powers — that he could make water burn, trees dance, and cause the rocks to move. (005-481) These stories, blending history and mythology, exemplify [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in the [[truth|poetical truth]] that emerges from [[history|historical]] tradition.

**Wednesday** continues the [[penacook|Penacook]] thread. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] meditates on the relationship between the Native inhabitants and the [[nature|natural world]], suggesting that the [[penacook|Penacook]] possessed a closeness to nature that the European settlers lacked. (006-489) (006-490)

**Thursday** brings the narrative to its most poignant moment concerning the [[penacook|Penacook]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts the story of [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]]'s departure from his ancestral lands — a [[silence|quiet]]ly devastating episode in which the old sachem, after years of attempting peaceful coexistence, finally abandons the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. (007-132) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account is notable for its sympathy: he frames [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]]'s departure not as a military defeat but as a spiritual withdrawal, the dignified retreat of a people who have been pushed beyond endurance.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also refers to the [[penacook|Penacook]] in the context of [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]]'s later years, when the old chief lived in a hut near [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]] and was visited by settlers who came to regard him with a mixture of curiosity and respect. ## Historical Background

The Penacook tribe's recorded history begins with first European contact in the early 1600s. At their peak, they controlled much of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley and maintained diplomatic relations with neighboring tribes as well as with English and French colonists.

**[[passaconaway|Passaconaway]]** (c. 1570–1666) was the most powerful sachem of the [[penacook|Penacook]] confederacy. He maintained a policy of neutrality and peace with the English colonists, even as wars between settlers and other Native groups erupted around him. According to tradition, he was also a powerful shaman — "a great magician" in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s phrase — whose feats of magic were remembered in local folklore long after his death. He is said to have made a formal speech to his people around 1660, urging them to live peacefully with the English and then disappearing into the forest, never to be seen again.

**[[wannalancet|Wannalancet]]** (c. 1610–1697), [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]]'s son, succeeded his father as sachem. He continued the policy of neutrality but faced increasing pressure as English settlement expanded. During [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]] (1675–1678), [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] attempted to keep the [[penacook|Penacook]] neutral, but the tribe was caught between conflicting forces. After the war, the [[penacook|Penacook]] confederacy fragmented, with many members joining other Abenaki communities or moving to Canada. [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] himself converted to [[religion|Christianity]] and lived out his years near [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]].

By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time (the 1830s–1840s), the [[penacook|Penacook]] as a distinct political entity had long since ceased to exist. The [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley, once their homeland, was now entirely occupied by New England towns and farms. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s melancholy passages about the tribe reflect a consciousness of this [[history|historical]] loss and a desire to preserve something of the [[penacook|Penacook]] legacy in writing.
