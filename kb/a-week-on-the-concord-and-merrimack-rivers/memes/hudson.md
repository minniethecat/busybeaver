## Description

Hudson is a town in Hillsborough County, New Hampshire, situated on the east bank of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. Originally chartered as part of a larger tract and once known as [[nottingham|Nottingham]], the town was later renamed Hudson. It lies across the river from [[nashua|Nashua]] and is tra[[poetry|verse]]d by several tributary streams including Otternic Pond's outflow, which enters the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] just above [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]]. (004-730) (004-983)

## On the River Route

The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers passed through this section of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] on their journey upstream. Approaching from [[nashua|Nashua]], they would have seen Hudson on the eastern bank. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the town's location in relation to [[nashua|Nashua]] and the surrounding natural features, remarking on the rich hazel growth along the riverbanks in Hudson. (004-730) (004-771) (004-795)

The brothers' passage through this stretch of the river provided an opportunity for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] to reflect on the layers of history embedded in the [[nature|landscape]] — the town having been known successively as [[nottingham|Nottingham]], then Hudson, each name carrying its own [[history|historical]] associations. (004-983)

## Historical Context

Hudson was originally part of a large tract known as [[nottingham|Nottingham]], named after [[nottingham|Nottingham]], England. The area was first settled by Europeans in the early eighteenth century. The town later took the name Hudson, and the original [[nottingham|Nottingham]] designation was applied to a different New Hampshire town further north. (004-983)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s references to Hudson are woven into his broader meditation on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley's settlement history, noting how place names shift and accumulate meaning over time. The [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] itself, flowing [[history|past]] Hudson, was a major artery for trade and travel, with [[hudson-bay|Hudson Bay]] and the great northern waterways forming the distant horizon of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s geographical imagination. (005-1064) (005-1080)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] found the stretch of river near Hudson refreshing to the eye, noting the wild hazels growing along the bank. He was struck by the sense that even in this settled [[nature|landscape]], something of the primeval — what he called "India" — still persisted. (004-771) (004-795)

> The place where we sit is called Hudson,—once it was Nottingham,—once —
> — (004-983)

The chickaree, or red squirrel, which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] calls the "[[hudson-bay|Hudson Bay]] squirrel" (*Sciurus Hudsonius*), made its presence known as the brothers passed by, chattering a warning of their approach. (005-460)
