## Biography

**William Ellery Channing** (November 29, 1817 – December 23, 1901) was an American transcendentalist poet, a nephew of the Unitarian preacher Dr. William Ellery Channing. He was a close [[friendship|friend]] and frequent walking companion of [[henry-david-thoreau|Henry David Thoreau]].

Channing lived in [[concord-massachusetts|Concord, Massachusetts]], where he was part of the literary circle that included [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]] and [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Nathaniel Hawthorne]].

## Works / Significance

Channing published several volumes of poetry, including *Poems* (1843) and *The Woodman* (1849). His poetry focused on nature, rural life, and transcendental themes. Though less famous than Emerson or [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], he was an intimate of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] circle. He later wrote *[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]: The Poet-Naturalist* (1873), the first biography of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]].

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] includes a poetic epigraph attributed to Channing at the opening of the *Sunday* chapter (003-001). The lines evoke a [[silence|quiet]], reflective [[nature|landscape]]:

> "Has stirred its mute repose, / Still if you should walk there, you would go there again." (003-001)

The attribution to "CHANNING" underscores [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s esteem for his [[friendship|friend]]'s [[poetry|verse]] and the shared sensibility of the Transcendentalist circle — a deep attunement to the spiritual presence within nature.
