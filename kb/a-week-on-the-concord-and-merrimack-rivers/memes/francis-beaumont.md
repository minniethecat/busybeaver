## Biography

Francis Beaumont (1584–1616) was an English playwright of the Jacobean era, best known for his collaborations with John Fletcher. Together, Beaumont and Fletcher wrote some of the most popular plays of the early seventeenth century, including *The Knight of the Burning Pestle*, *Philaster*, and *The Maid's Tragedy*. Beaumont was part of the vibrant London theatrical scene that followed [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] and included [[ben-jonson|Ben Jonson]], with whom he was acquainted.

Beaumont's plays were known for their blending of tragic and comic elements, their dramatic intensity, and their exploration of honor, love, and political power. He ceased writing around 1613 and died in 1616 at the age of thirty-two. Despite his short career, his works remained on the English stage for generations.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Francis Beaumont in the *Sunday* chapter within a meditation on the nature of praise and the worth of this world. He quotes what he calls "rare praise, if it were true, which was addressed to Francis Beaumont: 'Spectators sate part in your tragedies'" (003-696–003-697).

The quotation — suggesting that Beaumont's tragedies were so powerful that spectators became participants — introduces [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[thought|reflection]] on the "mean and wretched place this world is" and the consolations of daylight, lamp-light, and poetry (003-698–003-702). Beaumont thus appears as a touchstone for the transformative power of art within [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s philosophical journey.
