## Description

The Souhegan River is a tributary of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] in southern New Hampshire. Its name, of Native American origin, is interpreted by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as meaning "Crooked River," a fitting description of its winding course through the New Hampshire countryside. (005-1095) The river joins the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at the village of [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], opposite the town of [[litchfield|Litchfield]]. (008-020)

## On the River Route

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] first lists the Souhegan among the tributaries of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], alongside the [[nashua|Nashua]], [[piscataquoag|Piscataquoag]], and other rivers that feed the main stream. (003-1152) When the brothers reached the mouth of the Souhegan on their northward journey, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] devoted sustained attention to its character and history.

The Souhegan, though a rapid river, seemed to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] to possess a quality of ancientness and permanence. (005-1144) He invokes it [[poetry|poetical]]ly: "By the Souhegan's side" the [[nature|landscape]] takes on a timeless quality. (005-1151) In [[poetry|verse]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that "Souhegan soundeth old," suggesting the river's deep [[history|historical]] resonance. (005-1178)

On the return journey, the brothers passed the mouth of the Souhegan and the village of [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as they drifted southward toward [[nashua|Nashua]]. (008-020)

## Historical Context

The Souhegan was known to early colonial settlers and appears in records of the late seventeenth century. A skirmish occurred on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] "over against Souhegan upon the 22 day of this instant" during the period of frontier conflicts. (005-1109) The river's name preserves the indigenous heritage of the region.

## Thoreau's Observations

The Souhegan holds a special place in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[imagination|poetic imagination]]. By calling it the "Crooked River," he emphasizes its meandering character, which contrasts with the more direct course of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. The line "Souhegan soundeth old" from [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[poetry|verse]] suggests he heard in the river's name an echo of an ancient, pre-colonial [[nature|landscape]]. (005-1178)
