# Revolutionary Battles

## Overview

The [[american-revolution|American Revolution]]'s key battles in New England — [[lexington|Lexington]] and [[concord-mass|Concord]] (April 19, 1775), [[bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]] (June 17, 1775), and Bennington (August 16, 1777) — form the most directly referenced military [[history|historical]] events in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*. These battles, fought on or near the very terrain [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] navigates, represent the culmination of the colonial history he traces and the birth of the nation whose [[nature|landscape]] he celebrates.

## Relevance to the Journey

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] directly engages with these battles throughout his narrative, weaving the memory of revolutionary sacrifice into his observations of the contemporary [[nature|landscape]]. He evokes the martial spirit of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] militia, men who "were as often as any else the subjects of his satire and criticism," yet who rose to the occasion when the call to arms came (002-054). The events at [[concord-mass|Concord]]'s North Bridge, where "the embattled farmers stood / And fired the shot heard round the world," are present in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s consciousness as he passes through the town (002-099). He reflects on the revolutionary dead as a permanent presence in the [[nature|landscape]], their graves marked by stones that "tell of the [[history|past]]" (002-538). The battle of Bennington, fought in the hills of what is now Vermont, is recalled as a decisive turning point in the northern theater, a victory won by New Hampshire and Massachusetts militia under General John Stark (006-503). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] meditates on the courage required of those who fought at [[bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]], men who "could not be frightened" by the British regulars (006-547). He notes the character of these citizen-soldiers, who fought not for glory but for a cause they understood in deeply personal terms (006-549). The transformation from colonial subjects to revolutionary citizens represents for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] the same kind of profound change as the shift from "bear-skins, parched corn, bows and arrows to tiled roofs, wheat-fields, guns and swords" (003-1102) — a metamorphosis of society itself.

## Historical Background

This event belongs to the complex history of New England — a history marked by conflict, displacement, and the gradual transformation of the region from Native American homeland to colonial territory to American state. The Revolutionary battles represent the climax of this process, the moment when the colonies asserted their independence and began the long project of creating a new nation. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s engagement with this history is characteristically nuanced, acknowledging both the heroism of the revolutionaries and the costs of the conflict.
