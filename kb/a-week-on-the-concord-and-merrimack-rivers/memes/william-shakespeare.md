# William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) is the poet [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] pairs most consistently with [[homer|Homer]] throughout *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*.

While [[homer|Homer]] represents the serene, nature-fused poet of [[history|antiquity]], Shakespeare stands for the dramatist of human life — the poet whose books *are men*.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s engagement with Shakespeare is less about specific plays than about the **quality of attention** Shakespeare brings to human experience.

## Biography

William Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564, the son of a glove-maker.

By the 1590s he had established himself in London as an actor and playwright, and over the next two decades produced the body of work — tragedies, comedies, histories, and sonnets — that would make him the most celebrated writer in the English language.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] knew Shakespeare's works intimately and read them as part of his lifelong engagement with the great poets.

## Works / Significance

Shakespeare's canon spans approximately 38 plays and 154 sonnets. For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], the significance of Shakespeare lay not in any single work but in the comprehensive **humanity** of his [[dream|vision]]:

| Quality | How It Appears in *A Week* |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Dramatic range | From tragedy to comedy, Shakespeare mirrors the full span of human life |
| [[truth|Truth]]-telling | [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] values Shakespeare for seeing "truth and beauty from his position" |
| Lived wisdom | "Not Shakespeare's books, unless his books were men" — the life behind the work matters |

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Shakespeare at critical moments in *A Week*, often pairing him with [[homer|Homer]] as twin pillars of the poetic tradition.

### The Two Supreme Poets

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s most sweeping statement on poetry subordinates even [[homer|Homer]] and Shakespeare to a larger [[imagination|creative]] force:

> But, after all, man is the great poet, and not Homer nor Shakespeare; and our language itself, and the common arts of life, are his work. (003-1430)

The ranking is clear: [[homer|Homer]] and Shakespeare are the two names that must be named and then *surpassed* by the recognition that ordinary human life is itself the greatest poem.

### Shakespeare's Books Were Men

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes his own [[poetry|verse]] to articulate what distinguishes Shakespeare:

> Our Shakespeare's life were rich to live again,
> What Plutarch read, that was not good nor true,
> Nor Shakespeare's books, unless his books were men. (007-076~078)

The progression is revealing: [[plutarch|Plutarch]]'s books are dismissed, but Shakespeare's are saved — *if* they are read as "men," as living testimony rather than dead text.

### A Personal Address

In a passage that reveals [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s almost conversational relationship with the great poets:

> Tell Shakespeare to attend some leisure hour,
> For now I've business with this drop of dew... (007-087~088)

Shakespeare can wait. The present moment — "this drop of dew" — takes priority. This is quintessential [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]: the immediate, the natural, the *now* always outweighs even the greatest cultural inheritance.

### The Rustling of Leaves

By the end of the journey, all poets are folded into a single natural image:

> Homer and Shakespeare and Milton and Marvell and Wordsworth are but the rustling of leaves and crackling of twigs in the forest, and there is not yet the sound of a bird. (007-265~267)

The image is double-edged. It honors the poets by making them part of nature, but it also suggests that their music is still only the *prelude* to something greater — the bird's [[music|song]] that has not yet begun.
