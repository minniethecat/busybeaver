## Biography

Decimus Junius Juvenalis, known in English as Juvenal, was a Roman poet of the late first and early second century CE, and one of the greatest satirists in the Latin literary tradition. Little is known of his life beyond what can be gleaned from his poetry and a few ancient references. He is believed to have been born in Aquinum (modern Aquino, Italy) around 55 CE and lived into the reign of Emperor Hadrian. His *Satires*, sixteen poems in five books, are his only surviving works, and they constitute the last great flowering of Roman [[poetry|verse]] satire.

## Works / Significance

Juvenal's *Satires* are a series of fierce, rhetorically brilliant attacks on Roman society under the Empire. They range from the corruption of the imperial court to the daily indignities of urban life in Rome. Juvenal is the source of many enduring phrases, including "bread and circuses" (*panem et circenses*), "a sound [[thought|mind]] in a sound body" (*mens sana in corpore sano*), and "who watches the watchers?" (*quis custodiet ipsos custodes?*). His satirical mode — indignant, hyperbolic, morally outraged — set the standard for formal [[poetry|verse]] satire in the Western tradition, influencing [[john-dryden|Dryden]], [[alexander-pope|Pope]], Johnson, and Swift.

## In Thoreau's Text

Juvenal appears twice in *A Week*, each time in a passage that reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s ambivalence toward satire as a poetic mode.

In *Thursday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Juvenal at the terminus of the classical poetic tradition, framing the descent from epic grandeur to satirical critique:

> That highway down from Homer and Hesiod to Horace and Juvenal is more attractive than the Appian. (005-1284)

The comparison of literary tradition to a Roman road — the Appian Way being the most famous — is doubly apt: the classics are a highway through time, and Juvenal represents the later, satirical end of that journey, far from [[homer|Homer]]'s epic heights but still worth traveling.

In *Friday*, however, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] is less generous. Reflecting on the nature of music and poetry, he judges satire incapable of true [[music|song]]:

> Most of all, satire will not be sung. A Juvenal or Persius do not marry music to their verse, but are measured fault-finders at best. (007-269)

The verdict is characteristic: [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] values music and lyricism above moral indignation, and Juvenal — for all his rhetorical power — fails the test of [[music|song]]. Together with his fellow satirist [[persius|Persius]], Juvenal represents the limit of a mode [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] ultimately finds constraining.
