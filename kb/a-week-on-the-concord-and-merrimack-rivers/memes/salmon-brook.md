## Description

Salmon Brook is a stream that enters the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] from the west under the railroad, approximately a mile and a half below the village of [[nashua|Nashua]]. Its name is said to be a translation from the Indian language. (004-1155) The brook meanders through woods and meadows and was described by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as a solitary stream. A stream from Otternic Pond in [[hudson|Hudson]] comes in just above Salmon Brook on the opposite side of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (004-1186)

## On the River Route

The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers passed the mouth of Salmon Brook on both legs of their journey. On Sunday, they rowed up far enough into the bordering meadows to learn its "piscatorial history" from a haymaker on its banks, who told them that the silver eel was formerly abundant there, pointing to sunken creels at the mouth. (004-1144)

On the return voyage, after passing the mouth of the [[nashua|Nashua]], they soon passed Salmon Brook again. (008-472) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also referenced the brook in connection with [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]], the famous hunter and soldier, mentioning "old [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]]'s house on Salmon Brook." (007-678)

## Historical Context

Salmon Brook was a favorite haunt of the Native Americans, and here the first white settlers of [[nashua|Nashua]] planted their early homesteads. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed "dents in the earth where their houses stood and the wrecks of ancient apple-trees" still visible in his time. (004-1155)

[[About]] one mile up this stream stood the house of old [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]], the celebrated hunter and ranger whose expeditions against the Native Americans made him a legendary figure of the New England frontier. Close by could be seen the cellar and gravestone of Joseph Hassell, who with his wife Anna, son Benjamin, and Mary Marks, were "slain by our Indian enemies on September 2d, [1691,] in the evening." (004-1155) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], invoking [[gookin|Gookin]], observed: "The Indian rod upon the English backs had not yet done God's errand." (004-1182)

## Thoreau's Observations

Though the brothers never trod in those meadows, but only touched their margin with their hands, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] wrote that they still retained a pleasant memory of them. (004-1155) The haymaker they encountered possessed what [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] described as fertile memory and imagination, rich in fishermen's tales — a type of local knowledge that the philosopher-traveler valued. The stream at that time remained solitary, meandering through woods and meadows, while "the then uninhabited mouth of the [[nashua|Nashua]] now resounds with the din of a manufacturing town." (004-1182)
