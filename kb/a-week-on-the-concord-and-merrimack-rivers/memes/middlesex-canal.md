## Description

The Middlesex Canal was an early American transportation canal connecting the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] at [[billerica-dam|Billerica Falls]] to the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] at Middlesex, above [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]]. Built to bypass the falls and facilitate trade between the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley and Boston, it was one of the oldest canals in the United States. The canal passage took a little more than an hour. (003-525)

## On the River Route

The brothers leave the broader and [[silence|quiet]]er part of the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] without regret and enter the canal at [[billerica-dam|Billerica Falls]] to bypass [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]] and reach the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (003-509, 003-525) They pass under the last bridge over the canal before reaching the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] and are let down into the larger river through the locks at Middlesex. (003-558, 003-994)

## Historical Context

This canal, one of the oldest in the country, played a crucial role in the region's early industrial development. (003-530) It enabled transport between the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley and Boston, predating the railroads. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the canal was in decline, superseded by the railroad network. The factories at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] soon overshadowed its commercial importance. (002-512)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the passage through the canal with interest, observing canal-boats traveling by means of locks. (003-1197) He sees canal-boats with their sails set (004-718) and remarks on the [[silence|quiet]], artificial waterway as a transition between the gentle [[concord-mass|Concord]] and the vigorous [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. The canal serves as a symbolic gateway from the familiar [[concord-mass|Concord]] [[nature|landscape]] to the broader [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] world.
