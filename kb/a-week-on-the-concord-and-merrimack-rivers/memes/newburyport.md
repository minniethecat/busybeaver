## Description

Newburyport is a coastal city in Essex County, Massachusetts, situated at the mouth of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] where it empties into the Atlantic Ocean. It marks the terminus of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s approximately 113-mile course from the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] to the sea. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] described approaching Newburyport by boat: "at last, you glide under the famous Chain Bridge, and are landed at Newburyport." (003-1168)

## On the River Route

Though the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers' [[the-1839-boat-journey|1839 voyage]] only reached as far upriver as [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] was intimately familiar with the full extent of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. He had "traced its stream from where it bubbles out of the rocks of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] above the clouds, to where it is lost amid the salt billows of the ocean on [[plum-island|Plum Island]] beach." (003-1116) Newburyport represents the river's final transformation — from mountain stream to industrial waterway to tidal estuary.

A small steamboat ran from Newburyport to [[haverhill|Haverhill]] in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s day, serving the commerce of the lower [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (003-1201) But [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed that the river's "real vessels are railroad cars, and its true and main stream, flowing by an iron channel farther south, may be traced by a long line of vapor amid the hills." (003-1219)

## Historical Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invoked [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]]'s description of the River Forth to describe the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s transformation by the time it reaches Newburyport: she who at first was "poore of waters, naked of renowne," having received many fair tributaries, "Doth grow the greater still, the further downe; Till that abounding both in power and fame, She long doth strive to give the sea her name." (003-1168)

From the steeples of Newburyport, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted, one could review the river stretching far up into the country with many a white sail glancing over it like an inland sea. A writer born on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s headwaters described the view: "Down out at its mouth, the dark inky main blending with the blue above. [[plum-island|Plum Island]], its sand ridges scolloping along the horizon like the sea-serpent." (003-1175)

Standing at the river's mouth and looking upstream, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] beheld "a city on each successive plateau, a busy colony of human beaver around every fall" — Newburyport, [[haverhill|Haverhill]], [[lawrence|Lawrence]], [[lowell-mass|Lowell]], [[nashua|Nashua]], [[manchester-nh|Manchester]], and [[concord-mass|Concord]], gleaming one above the other. (003-1213)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] regarded the mouth of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] with a mixture of awe and regret. The river, having escaped from under the last of the factories, had "a level and unmolested passage to the sea, a mere waste water, as it were, bearing little with it but its fame." (003-1219) The morning fog hanging over its course and the sails of the few small vessels transacting the commerce of [[haverhill|Haverhill]] and Newburyport were the last visible traces of the river's independent life before it surrendered to the ocean.

Instead of the scream of a fish-hawk scaring the fishes, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted, was now heard "the whistle of the steam-engine, arousing a country to its progress." (003-1219) This observation ties Newburyport to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s broader meditation on the tension between nature and industrial civilization.
