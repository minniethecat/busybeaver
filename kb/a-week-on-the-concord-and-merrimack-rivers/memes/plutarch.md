Plutarch of Chaeronea was a Greek historian, biographer, and priest at the Temple of Apollo at Delphi. His most famous work, the *Parallel Lives*, pairs biographies of eminent Greeks and Romans to explore moral character through [[history|historical]] example. His *Moralia* is a vast collection of essays and dialogues on ethics, religion, politics, and natural philosophy.

## Works / Significance

Plutarch's *Parallel Lives* shaped Western biographical writing for centuries, influencing [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]'s Roman plays and providing moral exemplars for the Renaissance and Enlightenment. His method of comparing Greek and Roman figures side by side established biography as a vehicle for ethical [[thought|reflection]] rather than mere chronicle.

Plutarch's essay on music in the *Moralia* preserves important fragments of ancient Greek [[music|musical]] theory, including his report of [[plato|Plato]]'s views on the divine origin of music.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Plutarch at several points in *A Week*, treating him as a touchstone of moral and intellectual authority.

In Monday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Plutarch's report of [[plato|Plato]]'s philosophy of music: "Plutarch says that '[[plato|Plato]] thinks the gods never gave men music, the science of [[music|melody]] and [[music|harmony]], for mere delectation or to tickle the ear.'" (004-1581) (004-1582) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] shares this conviction that music and poetry serve a higher, moral purpose rather than mere entertainment. For him the science of [[music|melody]] has a divine and educational function.

In Thursday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] praises Plutarch directly in [[poetry|verse]]: "Plutarch was good, and so was [[homer|Homer]] too." (007-075) The line places the Greek historian alongside the epic poet as a moral exemplar. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] continues: "What Plutarch read, that was not good nor true, / Nor [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]'s books, unless his books were men." (007-077) (007-078) Here [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] echoes Plutarch's biographical method — the belief that books are valuable only insofar as they reveal the character of living human beings.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s admiration for Plutarch aligns with his broader reverence for the classical moralists who sought wisdom through the study of exemplary lives.
