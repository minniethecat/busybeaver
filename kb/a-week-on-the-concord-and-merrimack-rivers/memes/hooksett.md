## Description

Hooksett is a town on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] between [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] and [[concord-mass|Concord]] (NH). Hooksett Pinnacle served as a landmark to boatmen. (007-020) The town has falls on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at Hooksett Falls. (007-112)

## On the River Route

The brothers pass through Hooksett on their journey up the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], arriving after about eight miles. (006-613) The village sits on the shore, nearly surrounding the stream. (007-120) They return a week afterward to find the melon man with his patch for the Hooksett and [[concord-mass|Concord]] markets. (006-1531, 007-438)

## Historical Context

Hooksett developed as a mill town on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], its falls providing water power. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it was a small settlement compared to [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] and [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] downstream. The town was known for granite quarries and as a stop along the river route.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes Hooksett Pinnacle as "a landmark to boatmen" (007-020) and the falls where the river drops about one hundred feet. (007-112) He describes the melon grower who sends produce to Hooksett and [[concord-mass|Concord]] markets. (006-1531) Between [[goffstown|Goffstown]] and Hooksett, he observes the [[nature|landscape]] at four miles' distance. (006-292) Canal-boats at Hooksett travel with a light breeze. (006-623)
