## Overview

The East India Company (EIC), chartered in 1600 by Queen Elizabeth I, was a British joint-stock company that traded primarily with the Indian subcontinent and East Asia. Over time it evolved from a commercial enterprise into a governing power, ruling large portions of India until the British Crown assumed direct control in 1858.

## In Thoreau's Text

The East India Company appears in *A Week* within [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s discussion of Eastern philosophy during the *Monday* chapter. While reflecting on the Bhagavad Gita, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes:

> Warren Hastings, in his sensible letter recommending the translation of this book to the Chairman of the East India Company, declares the original to be "of a sublimity of conception, reasoning, and diction almost unequalled." (004-522)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] continues the quotation: Hastings predicted the writings of the Indian philosophers "will survive when the British dominion in India shall have long ceased to exist, and when the sources which it once yielded of wealth and power are lost to remembrance."

The passage contrasts two kinds of legacy. The ephemeral wealth and power of the East India Company is set against the enduring wisdom of the Bhagavad Gita. Hastings, the first Governor-General of Bengal, recommended that the Company sponsor an English translation — an acknowledgment that Eastern spiritual texts held value beyond anything the commercial enterprise could produce.

## Historical Context

At its height, the East India Company commanded a private army larger than that of most nations and effectively governed the Indian subcontinent. The Company's monopoly on British trade with India and China made it one of the most powerful corporations in history.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s citation of Hastings' remark is layered with irony: the Chairman of the EIC received a letter from his Governor-General arguing that Hindu philosophy would outlast British rule. That prediction proved true when the Company was dissolved in 1874, less than thirty years after *A Week* was published.
