## Biography

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) was an English Romantic poet who, with Samuel Taylor Coleridge, launched the Romantic Age in English literature with the publication of *Lyrical Ballads* (1798). Born in Cockermouth, Cumberland, in the Lake District, Wordsworth's poetry celebrated the [[nature|natural world]], ordinary people, and the power of memory and imagination. He served as Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death. His major works include *The Prelude*, *Tintern Abbey*, and *Ode: Intimations of Immortality*.

## Works / Significance

Wordsworth revolutionized English poetry by insisting that poetry should use "the real language of men" rather than the artificial diction of neoclassical [[poetry|verse]]. His definition of poetry as "emotion recollected in tranquility" shaped the Romantic understanding of [[imagination|creative]] process. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], as a nature writer and transcendentalist, found in Wordsworth a kindred spirit — both writers sought spiritual renewal through immersion in the [[nature|natural world]]. Emerson and the American transcendentalists held Wordsworth in high regard, seeing him as a bridge between European romanticism and American literary independence.

## In Thoreau's Text

Wordsworth is referenced three times in *A Week*, each time as a benchmark of poetic achievement.

In *Thursday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Wordsworth among the immortals of English poetry. Describing how the greatest poets make themselves felt even in subtle ways, he writes:

> Homer and Shakespeare and Milton and Marvell and Wordsworth are but the rustling of leaves and crackling of twigs in the forest, and there is not yet the sound of any bird. (007-266)

In *Friday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] praises Wordsworth's linguistic choice — his preference for plain Saxon-derived English over courtly Latinisms:

> He was as simple as Wordsworth in preferring his homely but vigorous Saxon tongue, when it was neglected by the court, and had not yet attained to the dignity of a literary language. (008-961)

Later in the same chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Wordsworth as a yardstick for a particular quality of pathos he finds in another writer:

> A simple pathos and feminine gentleness, which Wordsworth only occasionally approaches, but does not equal, are peculiar to him. (008-1050)

Taken together, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s references position Wordsworth as a poet of nature's subtlety, linguistic authenticity, and emotional depth — qualities [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] himself aspired to in his own writing.
