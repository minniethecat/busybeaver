## Biography

John Lovewell was an English soldier and colonial settler who served as an ensign in Oliver Cromwell's army before emigrating to New England. He fought in the Great Swamp Fight of 1675 during [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]], one of the bloodiest engagements in colonial New England history (004-1159).

[[love|Love]]well settled along the [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]], a tributary of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] near present-day [[nashua|Nashua]], before 1690. There he built a house that became a well-known landmark on the river. He was the father of the celebrated Captain John Lovewell (1691–1725), who led a famous expedition against the Abenaki and was killed at the [[lovewells-fight|Battle of Pequawket]].

The elder [[love|Love]]well was remarkable for his longevity: he died about 1754 at the reported age of one hundred and twenty years. Local tradition held that the Indians spared him for his kindness to them. "Even in 1700 he was so old and gray-headed that his scalp was worth nothing," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes (004-1159).

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions old John Lovewell twice in *A Week*. In the *Monday* chapter, passing the [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]], he notes the site of [[love|Love]]well's house and recounts his remarkable life story. In the *Thursday* chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] imagines the escaping [[hannah-dustan|Hannah Dustan]] making her way toward [[love|Love]]well's house on [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]], a beacon of safety on the darkened river (007-678).
