## Description

Nottingham is the former name of the New Hampshire town now called [[hudson|Hudson]], situated on the east bank of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] across from [[nashua|Nashua]].

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] was attentive to the layered naming of this place, recording that the settlement "is called [[hudson|Hudson]],—once it was Nottingham." (004-983)

## On the River Route

The brothers passed by Nottingham on the outward leg of their journey. Traveling upstream, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted "the territories of [[nashua|Nashua]] on the one hand, and [[hudson|Hudson]], once Nottingham, on the other." (004-730)

The river formed a natural boundary between the two townships, with [[nashua|Nashua]] on the west bank and Nottingham on the east. This stretch of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] marked the transition from the more settled Massachusetts towns to the sparser settlements of New Hampshire.

## Historical Context

Nottingham carries a rich literary and [[history|historical]] association. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], ever alert to literary echoes, recorded that "They carried these foresters into fair Nottingham," a reference to the legendary Robin Hood and the English Sherwood Forest transplanted [[imagination|imaginative]]ly to the New England frontier. (004-1352)

He observed wryly that "Nottingham is only the other side of the river, and they were not," underscoring the distance between the romantic English names bestowed on American [[nature|wilderness]] and the realities of frontier life. (004-1356)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] was characteristically alert to the poetry embedded in American place names. He drew a deliberate contrast between the Sherwood Forest of English legend and the New England forest that the actual settlers encountered, a [[nature|landscape]] as wild and challenging as any the legendary outlaws faced. (004-1352) (004-1356)
