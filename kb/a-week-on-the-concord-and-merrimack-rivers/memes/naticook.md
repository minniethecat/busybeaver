## Description

Naticook was the name of an indigenous people and the township associated with them near the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] in what is now southern New Hampshire. Naticook Brook, a small stream, feeds into the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] and still bears the name. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions the Naticook twice in the Tuesday chapter of *A Week*, connecting them to the region's colonial land grants and his own sensory experience of the brook's waters.

## On the River Route

The Naticook appear early in the Tuesday chapter as [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts the colonial history of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley's townships. He notes that these townships "were anciently the Indian Naticook," and that a fur-trader named Brenton received the lands through a colonial grant (005-405). This brief mention embeds the Naticook within the layered history of indigenous displacement and European settlement.

Later in the same chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records that the brothers "tasted of the waters of Naticook Brook" on the same side of the river where a companion of the early settler John French lay buried in [[dunstable|Dunstable]] (005-979). The act of tasting the brook's waters exemplifies [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s characteristic desire to experience a place directly, not merely observe it.
