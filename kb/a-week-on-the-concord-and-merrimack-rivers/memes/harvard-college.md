## Overview

Harvard College, founded in 1636 in Cambridge, Massachusetts, is the oldest institution of higher education in the United States. [[henry-david-thoreau|Henry David Thoreau]] entered Harvard in 1833 and graduated in 1837. The college was a center of New England intellectual life and produced many of the region's ministers, scholars, and writers.

## In Thoreau's Text

Harvard appears in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* only once, in a poetic fragment embedded in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account of [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]] (the [[lovewells-fight|Battle of Pequawket]], 1725). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes a poem commemorating a chaplain who died in the expedition:

> Old Harvard's learned halls he left
> Far in the wilds a grave to find. (004-092)

The passage describes a Harvard-educated chaplain who joined Captain [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]]'s company in their campaign against the Abenaki. The brief reference captures the contrast between the cultivated life of the college and the harsh reality of the New England frontier, where even the learned could meet violent ends.

## Historical Context

At the time of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s attendance (1833–1837), Harvard was transitioning from its original mission of training ministers toward a broader liberal [[art|arts]] curriculum. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] studied classics, rhetoric, and natural philosophy but grew skeptical of institutional education.

His ambivalent relationship with Harvard is well-documented. Though he valued some aspects of his education, he famously refused to pay the five-dollar fee for his diploma. The college nonetheless provided him with access to [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]]'s circle and the broader transcendentalist movement that shaped his literary career.
