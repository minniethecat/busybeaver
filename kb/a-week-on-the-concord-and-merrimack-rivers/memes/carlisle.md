## Description

Carlisle is a town in Middlesex County, Massachusetts, situated northwest of [[concord-mass|Concord]]. During [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time it was a small, rural community of woods and farms, connected to the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] watershed. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] characterized it as the "city of the woods, which, if it is less civil, is the more natural." (003-224) The [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] flows through Carlisle on its course toward the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], passing through Billerica before emptying at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]]. (001-020)

## On the River Route

The brothers encountered Carlisle on the first day of their journey. On Saturday, the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] ran perfectly straight for a mile or more to Carlisle Bridge, a twenty-pier wooden structure whose surface, when viewed from a distance, appeared "like a cobweb gleaming in the sun." (002-219) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] described it as a long pull from Ball's Hill to Carlisle Bridge, sitting with their faces to the south under a slight breeze rising from the north. (002-649)

Having passed the bridge between Carlisle and [[bedford|Bedford]], they observed men haying far off in the meadow, their heads "waving like the grass which they cut." (002-652) As night stole over, freshness was wafted across the meadow, and cow-bells tinkled louder along the shore.

## Historical Context

Carlisle appears in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative as a place of patient persistence. The "building of the temple" — the meeting-house — was, according to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], "many wearisome years delayed" in Carlisle. (003-251) The delay was not due to any want of Shittim wood or the gold of Ophir, but rather because the inhabitants could not agree on a site convenient to all the worshippers — whether on "Buttrick's Plain" or rather on "[[poplar-hill|Poplar Hill]]." [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] dryly noted: "It was a tedious question." (003-251)

The town's early planters, like those of neighboring [[bedford|Bedford]], had petitioned the selectmen of [[concord-mass|Concord]] for recognition as a separate parish, in language that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] found both plaintive and dignified. This ecclesiastical history connected Carlisle to the broader pattern of New England town formation through the multiplication of parish churches.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] held a philosophical [[love|affection]] for Carlisle precisely because of its smallness. He wrote: "It gets laughed at because it is a small town, I know, but nevertheless it is a place where great men may be born any day, for fair winds and foul blow right on over it without distinction." (003-224) This observation reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s broader theme that spiritual greatness is not the prerogative of populous cities.

The town, in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s description, possessed a "meeting-house and horse-sheds, a tavern and a blacksmith's shop, for centre, and a good deal of wood to cut and cord yet." (003-224) The phrase "city of the woods" captures [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s paradoxical regard for the place — a settlement that held the earth together more by its natural character than by its civil institutions.
