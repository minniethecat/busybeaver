## Description

Wayland is a town in Middlesex County, Massachusetts, located west of Boston and east of the [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]]. The [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]] — sometimes called the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] in its lower stretches — forms part of Wayland's western boundary. The town's riparian meadows, particularly those between [[sudbury|Sudbury]] and Wayland, are among the widest and most fertile along the river system, a feature [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted in his opening chapter. (001-016) (001-024)

## On the River Route

Wayland lies near the very beginning of the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] system. The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers, setting out from [[concord-mass|Concord]], would have passed near Wayland's meadows in the earliest hours of their journey. The river's shore on the Wayland side is notably flatter than the opposite bank, and this geographic feature shapes the character of the [[nature|landscape]] [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes as the brothers depart. (001-034)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Wayland as one of several towns whose men gathered at the river, alongside those of [[sudbury|Sudbury]], [[concord-mass|Concord]], and other neighboring communities. The river served as a common boundary and meeting point for these towns, with Bound Rock marking the intersection of [[lincoln|Lincoln]], Wayland, [[sudbury|Sudbury]], and [[concord-mass|Concord]]. (001-050) (001-051)

Later in the narrative, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes the men of Wayland again in a political context, citing them alongside those of [[sudbury|Sudbury]] and [[concord-mass|Concord]] in his discussion of representative government and the demands of the people. (002-629)

## Historical Context

Wayland was settled in 1638 and incorporated in 1780. During [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it was an agricultural community whose meadows along the [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]] were prized for hay. The town's position near the junction of several waterways made it part of the natural corridor that the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers would follow northward.
