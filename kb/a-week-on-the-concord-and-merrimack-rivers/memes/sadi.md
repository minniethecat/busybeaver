Sadi of Shiraz, born Muslih-ud-Din Mushrif ibn-Abdullah around 1210 in Shiraz, Persia, is one of the most celebrated figures of classical Persian literature. His two major works, the *Gulistan* (The Rose Garden) and the *Bustan* (The Orchard), blend prose and [[poetry|verse]] with moral [[thought|reflection]], and have been read across cultures for centuries.

## Works / Significance

Sadi's *Gulistan* (1258) is a collection of moral tales and aphorisms in prose interspersed with poetry. It covers themes of justice, humility, love, and the conduct of rulers. The work became a standard text for ethical education throughout the Persian-speaking world and was among the first Persian books translated into European languages.

Doulatshah's biography of Sadi records the poet's life and the esteem in which he was held. Sadi's tomb in Shiraz remains a place of pilgrimage to this day.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Sadi's *Gulistan* in the Sunday chapter, drawing on the Persian poet's moral wisdom. He cites a passage on divine providence and forgiveness: "Providence but me; therefore did I pardon him." (003-726) Later in the same chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Sadi again in a brief exclamation: "thee!" (003-993)

In Thursday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Sadi's wisdom about travel and [[friendship|companionship]]. He writes that Sadi tells who may travel — among others, "A common mechanic, who can earn a subsistence by the industry of his hands, and not have to beg for the favor of anyone." (007-172) This democratic ideal of self-reliant travel resonated deeply with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]].

In Friday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] relates a passage from the life of Sadi by Doulatshah, recounting: "The eagle of the immaterial soul of Shaikh Sadi shook from his plumage the dust of his body." (008-1483) (008-1484) This image of the soul shedding its earthly form reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s transcendentalist sympathies with Sadi's Sufi mysticism and the universal theme of spiritual liberation.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s inclusion of Sadi alongside Western classical and English poets demonstrates his wide-ranging literary reach and his recognition of the universal quality of Persian moral wisdom.
