## Overview

The Suncook River is a tributary of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] in central New Hampshire,
flowing southward for approximately thirty-six miles from its headwaters at Crystal
Lake in Gilmanton through the Suncook Valley. It joins the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] in the village
of Suncook, which straddles the towns of Pembroke and Allenstown.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] lists the Suncook among the many tributaries that swell the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as
it descends through New Hampshire, enumerating the rivers in a single breathless
catalogue:

> (003-1153) Piscataquoag, and Suncook and Soucook and Contoocook, mingled in
> incalculable proportions, still fluid, yellowish, restless all, with an
> ancient, ineradicable inclination to the sea.

The passage captures [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s sense of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as a living composite
of its many feeder streams, each with its own character and history.

## Course and Watershed

Rising at Crystal Lake in the Belknap County town of Gilmanton, the Suncook
flows south through Barnstead, Pittsfield, and Chichester before turning
southwest through Epsom, Pembroke, and Allenstown. Its watershed covers
approximately two hundred and fifty square miles of the New Hampshire
lake country, a region of low hills, wetlands, and glacial outwash.

## Industrial History

Like many New England rivers, the Suncook powered mills in the nineteenth
century. The village of Suncook at the river's mouth became a center for
textile manufacturing, with the Suncook Mills and China Mills employing
hundreds of workers. The river's reliable flow and modest gradient made
it well suited for water-powered industry.

## Natural Features

The Suncook Valley, through which the river passes for much of its lower
course, is a broad and gently sloping basin. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s day, the riverbanks
were largely forested, and the stream supported runs of migratory fish —
alewives, shad, and salmon — that ascended from the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] each spring.
