[004-001]When the first light dawned on the earth and the birds, awoke, and the

[004-002]brave river was heard rippling confidently seaward, and the nimble

[004-003]early rising wind rustled the oak leaves about our tent, all men having

[004-004]reinforced their bodies and their souls with sleep, and cast aside

[004-005]doubt and fear, were invited to unattempted adventures.

[004-006]“All courageous knichtis

[004-007]Agains the day dichtis

[004-008]The breest-plate that bricht is,

[004-009]To feght with their foue.

[004-010]The stoned steed stampis

[004-011]Throw curage and crampis,

[004-012]Syne on the land lampis;

[004-013]The night is neir gone.”

[004-014]One of us took the boat over to the opposite shore, which was flat and

[004-015]accessible, a quarter of a mile distant, to empty it of water and wash

[004-016]out the clay, while the other kindled a fire and got breakfast ready.

[004-017]At an early hour we were again on our way, rowing through the fog as

[004-018]before, the river already awake, and a million crisped waves come forth

[004-019]to meet the sun when he should show himself. The countrymen, recruited

[004-020]by their day of rest, were already stirring, and had begun to cross the

[004-021]ferry on the business of the week. This ferry was as busy as a beaver

[004-022]dam, and all the world seemed anxious to get across the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]

[004-023]at this particular point, waiting to get set over,—children with their

[004-024]two cents done up in paper, jail-birds broke loose and constable with

[004-025]warrant, travellers from distant lands to distant lands, men and women

[004-026]to whom the Merrimack River was a bar. There stands a gig in the gray

[004-027]morning, in the mist, the impatient traveller pacing the wet shore with

[004-028]whip in hand, and shouting through the fog after the regardless Charon

[004-029]and his retreating ark, as if he might throw that passenger overboard

[004-030]and return forthwith for himself; he will compensate him. He is to

[004-031]break his fast at some unseen place on the opposite side. It may be

[004-032]Ledyard or the Wandering Jew. Whence, pray, did he come out of the

[004-033]foggy night? and whither through the sunny day will he go? We observe

[004-034]only his transit; important to us, forgotten by him, transiting all

[004-035]day. There are two of them. May be, they are [[virgil|Virgil]] and Dante. But when

[004-036]they crossed the Styx, none were seen bound up or down the stream, that

[004-037]I remember. It is only a _transjectus_, a transitory voyage, like life

[004-038]itself, none but the long-lived gods bound up or down the stream. Many

[004-039]of these Monday men are ministers, no doubt, reseeking their parishes

[004-040]with hired horses, with sermons in their valises all read and gutted,

[004-041]the day after never with them. They cross each other’s routes all the

[004-042]country over like woof and warp, making a garment of loose texture;

[004-043]vacation now for six days. They stop to pick nuts and berries, and

[004-044]gather apples by the wayside at their leisure. Good [[religion|religious]] men, with

[004-045]the love of men in their hearts, and the means to pay their toll in

[004-046]their pockets. We got over this ferry chain without scraping, rowing

[004-047]athwart the tide of travel,—no toll for us that day.

[004-048]The fog dispersed and we rowed leisurely along through Tyngsborough,

[004-049]with a clear sky and a mild atmosphere, leaving the habitations of men

[004-050]behind and penetrating yet farther into the territory of ancient

[004-051]Dunstable. It was from Dunstable, then a frontier town, that the famous

[004-052]Captain Lovewell, with his company, marched in quest of the Indians on

[004-053]the 18th of April, 1725. He was the son of “an ensign in the army of

[004-054]Oliver Cromwell, who came to this country, and settled at Dunstable,

[004-055]where he died at the great age of one hundred and twenty years.” In the

[004-056]words of the old nursery tale, sung about a hundred years ago,—

[004-057]“He and his valiant soldiers did range the woods full wide,

[004-058]And hardships they endured to quell the Indian’s pride.”

[004-059]In the shaggy pine forest of Pequawket they met the “rebel Indians,”

[004-060]and prevailed, after a bloody fight, and a remnant returned home to

[004-061]enjoy the fame of their victory. A township called Lovewell’s Town, but

[004-062]now, for some reason, or perhaps without reason, Pembroke, was granted

[004-063]them by the State.

[004-064]“Of all our valiant English, there were but thirty-four,

[004-065]And of the rebel Indians, there were about four-score;

[004-066]And sixteen of our English did safely home return,

[004-067]The rest were killed and wounded, for which we all must mourn.

[004-068]“Our worthy Capt. Lovewell among them there did die,

[004-069]They killed Lieut. Robbins, and wounded good young Frye,

[004-070]Who was our English Chaplin; he many Indians slew,

[004-071]And some of them he scalped while bullets round him flew.”

[004-072]Our brave forefathers have exterminated all the Indians, and their

[004-073]degenerate children no longer dwell in garrisoned houses nor hear any

[004-074]war-whoop in their path. It would be well, perchance, if many an

[004-075]“English Chaplin” in these days could exhibit as unquestionable

[004-076]trophies of his valor as did “good young Frye.” We have need to be as

[004-077]sturdy pioneers still as Miles Standish, or Church, or Lovewell. We are

[004-078]to follow on another trail, it is true, but one as convenient for

[004-079]ambushes. What if the Indians are exterminated, are not savages as grim

[004-080]prowling about the clearings to-day?—

[004-081]“And braving many dangers and hardships in the way,

[004-082]They safe arrived at Dunstable the thirteenth (?) day of May.”

[004-083]But they did not all “safe arrive in Dunstable the thirteenth,” or the

[004-084]fifteenth, or the thirtieth “day of May.” Eleazer Davis and Josiah

[004-085]Jones, both of [[concord-massachusetts|Concord]], for our native town had seven men in this

[004-086]fight, Lieutenant Farwell, of Dunstable, and Jonathan Frye, of Andover,

[004-087]who were all wounded, were left behind, creeping toward the

[004-088]settlements. “After travelling several miles, Frye was left and lost,”

[004-089]though a more recent poet has assigned him company in his last hours.

[004-090]“A man he was of comely form,

[004-091]Polished and brave, well learned and kind;

[004-092]Old Harvard’s learned halls he left

[004-093]Far in the wilds a grave to find.

[004-094]“Ah! now his blood-red arm he lifts;

[004-095]His closing lids he tries to raise;

[004-096]And speak once more before he dies,

[004-097]In supplication and in praise.

[004-098]“He prays kind Heaven to grant success,

[004-099]Brave Lovewell’s men to guide and bless,

[004-100]And when they’ve shed their heart-blood true,

[004-101]To raise them all to happiness.” . . .

[004-102]“Lieutenant Farwell took his hand,

[004-103]His arm around his neck he threw,

[004-104]And said, ‘Brave Chaplain, I could wish

[004-105]That Heaven had made me die for you.’”

[004-106]Farwell held out eleven days. “A tradition says,” as we learn from the

[004-107][[history|History]] of Concord, “that arriving at a pond with Lieut. Farwell, Davis

[004-108]pulled off one of his moccasins, cut it in strings, on which he

[004-109]fastened a hook, caught some fish, fried and ate them. They refreshed

[004-110]him, but were injurious to Farwell, who died soon after.” Davis had a

[004-111]ball lodged in his body, and his right hand shot off; but on the whole,

[004-112]he seems to have been less damaged than his companion. He came into

[004-113]Berwick after being out fourteen days. Jones also had a ball lodged in

[004-114]his body, but he likewise got into Saco after fourteen days, though not

[004-115]in the best condition imaginable. “He had subsisted,” says an old

[004-116]journal, “on the spontaneous vegetables of the forest; and cranberries

[004-117]which he had eaten came out of wounds he had received in his body.”

[004-118]This was also the case with Davis. The last two reached home at length,

[004-119]safe if not sound, and lived many years in a crippled state to enjoy

[004-120]their pension.

[004-121]But alas! of the crippled Indians, and their adventures in the woods,—

[004-122]“For as we are informed, so thick and fast they fell,

[004-123]Scarce twenty of their number at night did get home well,”—

[004-124]how many balls lodged with them, how fared their cranberries, what

[004-125]Berwick or Saco they got into, and finally what pension or township was

[004-126]granted them, there is no journal to tell.

[004-127]It is stated in the History of Dunstable, that just before his last

[004-128]march, Lovewell was warned to beware of the ambuscades of the enemy,

[004-129]but “he replied, ‘that he did not care for them,’ and bending down a

[004-130]small elm beside which he was standing into a bow, declared ‘that he

[004-131]would treat the Indians in the same way.’ This elm is still standing

[004-132][in Nashua], a venerable and magnificent tree.”

[004-133]Meanwhile, having passed the Horseshoe Interval in Tyngsborough, where

[004-134]the river makes a sudden bend to the northwest,—for our reflections

[004-135]have anticipated our progress somewhat,—we were advancing farther into

[004-136]the country and into the day, which last proved almost as golden as the

[004-137]preceding, though the slight bustle and activity of the Monday seemed

[004-138]to penetrate even to this scenery. Now and then we had to muster all

[004-139]our energy to get round a point, where the river broke rippling over

[004-140]rocks, and the maples trailed their branches in the stream, but there

[004-141]was generally a backwater or eddy on the side, of which we took

[004-142]advantage. The river was here about forty rods wide and fifteen feet

[004-143]deep. Occasionally one ran along the shore, examining the country, and

[004-144]visiting the nearest farm-houses, while the other followed the windings

[004-145]of the stream alone, to meet his companion at some distant point, and

[004-146]hear the report of his adventures; how the farmer praised the coolness

[004-147]of his well, and his wife offered the stranger a draught of milk, or

[004-148]the children quarrelled for the only transparency in the window that

[004-149]they might get sight of the man at the well. For though the country

[004-150]seemed so new, and no house was observed by us, shut in between the

[004-151]banks that sunny day, we did not have to travel far to find where men

[004-152]inhabited, like wild bees, and had sunk wells in the loose sand and

[004-153]loam of the Merrimack. There dwelt the subject of the Hebrew

[004-154]scriptures, and the Esprit des Lois, where a thin vaporous smoke curled

[004-155]up through the noon. All that is told of mankind, of the inhabitants of

[004-156]the Upper Nile, and the Sunderbunds, and Timbuctoo, and the Orinoko,

[004-157]was experience here. Every race and class of men was represented.

[004-158]According to Belknap, the historian of New Hampshire, who wrote sixty

[004-159]years ago, here too, perchance, dwelt “new lights,” and free [[thought|thinking]]

[004-160]men even then. “The people in general throughout the State,” it is

[004-161]written, “are professors of the Christian religion in some form or

[004-162]other. There is, however, a sort of _wise men_ who pretend to reject

[004-163]it; but they have not yet been able to substitute a better in its

[004-164]place.”

[004-165]The other voyageur, perhaps, would in the mean while have seen a brown

[004-166]hawk, or a woodchuck, or a musquash creeping under the alders.

[004-167]We occasionally rested in the shade of a maple or a willow, and drew

[004-168]forth a melon for our refreshment, while we contemplated at our leisure

[004-169]the lapse of the river and of human life; and as that current, with its

[004-170]floating twigs and leaves, so did all things pass in review before us,

[004-171]while far away in cities and marts on this very stream, the old routine

[004-172]was proceeding still. There is, indeed, a tide in the affairs of men,

[004-173]as the poet says, and yet as things flow they circulate, and the ebb

[004-174]always balances the flow. All streams are but tributary to the ocean,

[004-175]which itself does not stream, and the shores are unchanged, but in

[004-176]longer periods than man can measure. Go where we will, we discover

[004-177]infinite change in particulars only, not in generals. When I go into a

[004-178]museum and see the mummies wrapped in their linen bandages, I see that

[004-179]the lives of men began to need reform as long ago as when they walked

[004-180]the earth. I come out into the streets, and meet men who declare that

[004-181]the time is near at hand for the redemption of the race. But as men

[004-182]lived in Thebes, so do they live in Dunstable to-day. “[[time|Time]] drinketh up

[004-183]the essence of every great and noble action which ought to be

[004-184]performed, and is delayed in the execution.” So says Veeshnoo Sarma;

[004-185]and we perceive that the schemers return again and again to common

[004-186]sense and labor. Such is the evidence of history.

[004-187]“Yet I doubt not through the ages one increasing purpose runs,

[004-188]And the thoughts of men are widened with the process of the Suns.”

[004-189]There are secret articles in our treaties with the gods, of more

[004-190]importance than all the rest, which the historian can never know.

[004-191]There are many skilful apprentices, but few master workmen. On every

[004-192]hand we observe a truly wise practice, in education, in morals, and in

[004-193]the [[art|arts]] of life, the embodied wisdom of many an ancient philosopher.

[004-194]Who does not see that heresies have some time prevailed, that reforms

[004-195]have already taken place? All this worldly wisdom might be regarded as

[004-196]the once unamiable heresy of some wise man. Some interests have got a

[004-197]footing on the earth which we have not made sufficient allowance for.

[004-198]Even they who first built these barns and cleared the land thus, had

[004-199]some valor. The abrupt epochs and chasms are smoothed down in history

[004-200]as the inequalities of the plain are concealed by distance. But unless

[004-201]we do more than simply learn the trade of our time, we are but

[004-202]apprentices, and not yet masters of the art of life.

[004-203]Now that we are casting away these melon seeds, how can we help feeling

[004-204]reproach? He who eats the fruit, should at least plant the seed; aye,

[004-205]if possible, a better seed than that whose fruit he has enjoyed. Seeds!

[004-206]there are seeds enough which need only to be stirred in with the soil

[004-207]where they lie, by an inspired voice or pen, to bear fruit of a divine

[004-208]flavor. O thou spendthrift! Defray thy debt to the world; eat not the

[004-209]seed of institutions, as the luxurious do, but plant it rather, while

[004-210]thou devourest the pulp and tuber for thy subsistence; that so,

[004-211]perchance, one variety may at last be found worthy of preservation.

[004-212]There are moments when all anxiety and stated toil are becalmed in the

[004-213]infinite leisure and repose of nature. All laborers must have their

[004-214]nooning, and at this season of the day, we are all, more or less,

[004-215]Asiatics, and give over all work and reform. While lying thus on our

[004-216]oars by the side of the stream, in the heat of the day, our boat held

[004-217]by an osier put through the staple in its prow, and slicing the melons,

[004-218]which are a fruit of the East, our thoughts reverted to Arabia, Persia,

[004-219]and Hindostan, the lands of contemplation and dwelling-places of the

[004-220]ruminant nations. In the experience of this noontide we could find some

[004-221]apology even for the instinct of the opium, betel, and tobacco chewers.

[004-222]Mount Sabér, according to the French traveller and naturalist, Botta,

[004-223]is celebrated for producing the Kát-tree, of which “the soft tops of

[004-224]the twigs and tender leaves are eaten,” says his reviewer, “and produce

[004-225]an agreeable soothing excitement, restoring from fatigue, banishing

[004-226]sleep, and disposing to the enjoyment of conversation.” We thought that

[004-227]we might lead a dignified Oriental life along this stream as well, and

[004-228]the maple and alders would be our Kát-trees.

[004-229]It is a great pleasure to escape sometimes from the restless class of

[004-230]Reformers. What if these grievances exist? So do you and I. Think you

[004-231]that sitting hens are troubled with ennui these long summer days,

[004-232]sitting on and on in the crevice of a hay-loft, without active

[004-233]employment? By the faint cackling in distant barns, I judge that dame

[004-234][[nature|Nature]] is interested still to know how many eggs her hens lay. The

[004-235]Universal Soul, as it is called, has an interest in the stacking of

[004-236]hay, the foddering of cattle, and the draining of peat-meadows. Away in

[004-237]Scythia, away in India, it makes butter and cheese. Suppose that all

[004-238]farms _are_ run out, and we youths must buy old land and bring it to,

[004-239]still everywhere the relentless opponents of reform bear a strange

[004-240]resemblance to ourselves; or, perchance, they are a few old maids and

[004-241]bachelors, who sit round the kitchen hearth and listen to the singing

[004-242]of the kettle. “The oracles often give victory to our choice, and not

[004-243]to the order alone of the mundane periods. As, for instance, when they

[004-244]say that our voluntary sorrows germinate in us as the growth of the

[004-245]particular life we lead.” The reform which you talk about can be

[004-246]undertaken any morning before unbarring our doors. We need not call any

[004-247]convention. When two neighbors begin to eat corn bread, who before ate

[004-248]wheat, then the gods smile from ear to ear, for it is very pleasant to

[004-249]them. Why do you not try it? Don’t let me hinder you.

[004-250]There are theoretical reformers at all times, and all the world over,

[004-251]living on anticipation. Wolff, travelling in the deserts of Bokhara,

[004-252]says, “Another party of derveeshes came to me and observed, ‘The time

[004-253]will come when there shall be no difference between rich and poor,

[004-254]between high and low, when property will be in common, even wives and

[004-255]children.’” But forever I ask of such, What then? The derveeshes in the

[004-256]deserts of Bokhara and the reformers in Marlboro’ Chapel sing the same

[004-257][[music|song]]. “There’s a good time coming, boys,” but, asked one of the

[004-258]audience, in good faith, “Can you fix the date?” Said I, “Will you help

[004-259]it along?”

[004-260]The nonchalance and _dolce-far-niente_ air of nature and society hint

[004-261]at infinite periods in the progress of mankind. The States have leisure

[004-262]to laugh from Maine to Texas at some newspaper joke, and New England

[004-263]shakes at the double-entendres of Australian circles, while the poor

[004-264]reformer cannot get a hearing.

[004-265]Men do not fail commonly for want of knowledge, but for want of

[004-266]prudence to give wisdom the preference. What we need to know in any

[004-267]case is very simple. It is but too easy to establish another durable

[004-268]and harmonious routine. Immediately all parts of nature consent to it.

[004-269]Only make something to take the place of something, and men will behave

[004-270]as if it was the very thing they wanted. They _must_ behave, at any

[004-271]rate, and will work up any material. There is always a present and

[004-272]extant life, be it better or worse, which all combine to uphold. We

[004-273]should be slow to mend, my friends, as slow to require mending, “Not

[004-274]hurling, according to the oracle, a transcendent foot towards piety.”

[004-275]The language of excitement is at best picturesque merely. You must be

[004-276]calm before you can utter oracles. What was the excitement of the

[004-277]Delphic priestess compared with the calm wisdom of [[socrates|Socrates]]?—or whoever

[004-278]it was that was wise.—Enthusiasm is a supernatural serenity.

[004-279]“Men find that action is another thing

[004-280]Than what they in discoursing papers read;

[004-281]The world’s affairs require in managing

[004-282]More arts than those wherein you clerks proceed.”

[004-283]As in geology, so in social institutions, we may discover the causes of

[004-284]all past change in the present invariable order of society. The

[004-285]greatest appreciable physical revolutions are the work of the

[004-286]light-footed air, the stealthy-paced water, and the subterranean fire.

[004-287]Aristotle said, “As time never fails, and the universe is eternal,

[004-288]neither the Tanais nor the Nile can have flowed forever.” We are

[004-289]independent of the change we detect. The longer the lever the less

[004-290]perceptible its motion. It is the slowest pulsation which is the most

[004-291]vital. The hero then will know how to wait, as well as to make haste.

[004-292]All good abides with him who waiteth _wisely_; we shall sooner overtake

[004-293]the dawn by remaining here than by hurrying over the hills of the west.

[004-294]Be assured that every man’s success is in proportion to his _average_

[004-295]ability. The meadow flowers spring and bloom where the waters annually

[004-296]deposit their slime, not where they reach in some freshet only. A man

[004-297]is not his hope, nor his despair, nor yet his past deed. We know not

[004-298]yet what we have done, still less what we are doing. Wait till evening,

[004-299]and other parts of our day’s work will shine than we had thought at

[004-300]noon, and we shall discover the real purport of our toil. As when the

[004-301]farmer has reached the end of the furrow and looks back, he can tell

[004-302]best where the pressed earth shines most.

[004-303]To one who habitually endeavors to contemplate the true state of

[004-304]things, the political state can hardly be said to have any existence

[004-305]whatever. It is unreal, incredible, and insignificant to him, and for

[004-306]him to endeavor to extract the truth from such lean material is like

[004-307]making sugar from linen rags, when sugar-cane may be had. Generally

[004-308]speaking, the political news, whether domestic or foreign, might be

[004-309]written to-day for the next ten years, with sufficient accuracy. Most

[004-310]revolutions in society have not power to interest, still less alarm us;

[004-311]but tell me that our rivers are drying up, or the genus pine dying out

[004-312]in the country, and I might attend. Most events recorded in history are

[004-313]more remarkable than important, like eclipses of the sun and moon, by

[004-314]which all are attracted, but whose effects no one takes the trouble to

[004-315]calculate.

[004-316]But will the government never be so well administered, inquired one,

[004-317]that we private men shall hear nothing about it? “The king answered: At

[004-318]all events, I require a prudent and able man, who is capable of

[004-319]managing the state affairs of my kingdom. The ex-minister said: The

[004-320]criterion, O Sire! of a wise and competent man is, that he will not

[004-321]meddle with such like matters.” Alas that the ex-minister should have

[004-322]been so nearly right!

[004-323]In my short experience of human life, the _outward_ obstacles, if there

[004-324]were any such, have not been living men, but the institutions of the

[004-325]dead. It is grateful to make one’s way through this latest generation

[004-326]as through dewy grass. Men are as innocent as the morning to the

[004-327]unsuspicious.

[004-328]“And round about good morrows fly,

[004-329]As if day taught humanity.”

[004-330]Not being Reve of this Shire,

[004-331]“The early pilgrim blithe he hailed,

[004-332]That o’er the hills did stray,

[004-333]And many an early husbandman,

[004-334]That he met on the way”;—

[004-335]thieves and robbers all, nevertheless. I have not so surely foreseen

[004-336]that any Cossack or Chippeway would come to disturb the honest and

[004-337]simple commonwealth, as that some monster institution would at length

[004-338]embrace and crush its free members in its scaly folds; for it is not to

[004-339]be forgotten, that while the law holds fast the thief and murderer, it

[004-340]lets itself go loose. When I have not paid the tax which the State

[004-341]demanded for that protection which I did not want, itself has robbed

[004-342]me; when I have asserted the liberty it presumed to declare, itself has

[004-343]imprisoned me. Poor creature! if it knows no better I will not blame

[004-344]it. If it cannot live but by these means, I can. I do not wish, it

[004-345]happens, to be associated with Massachusetts, either in holding slaves

[004-346]or in conquering Mexico. I am a little better than herself in these

[004-347]respects.—As for Massachusetts, that huge she Briareus, Argus and

[004-348]Colchian Dragon conjoined, set to watch the Heifer of the Constitution

[004-349]and the Golden Fleece, we would not warrant our respect for her, like

[004-350]some compositions, to preserve its qualities through all weathers.—Thus

[004-351]it has happened, that not the Arch Fiend himself has been in my way,

[004-352]but these toils which tradition says were originally spun to obstruct

[004-353]him. They are cobwebs and trifling obstacles in an earnest man’s path,

[004-354]it is true, and at length one even becomes attached to his unswept and

[004-355]undusted garret. I love man—kind, but I hate the institutions of the

[004-356]dead un-kind. Men execute nothing so faithfully as the wills of the

[004-357]dead, to the last codicil and letter. _They_ rule this world, and the

[004-358]living are but their executors. Such foundation too have our lectures

[004-359]and our sermons, commonly. They are all _Dudleian;_ and piety derives

[004-360]its origin still from that exploit of _pius Æneas_, who bore his
