## Biography

Hannah Dustan (also spelled Dustin or Duston) was born in 1657 in [[haverhill|Haverhill, Massachusetts]] Bay Colony. She was the wife of Thomas Dustan, a farmer and brickmaker, and the mother of nine children. On March 15, 1697, during King William's War, an Abenaki raiding party attacked [[haverhill|Haverhill]]. Dustan, who had recently given birth, was captured along with her nurse Mary Neff. Her husband Thomas managed to escape with their seven elder children, but the raiders killed her infant by dashing its brains against an apple-tree (007-609).

Dustan, Neff, and a young English boy named Samuel Lennardson were taken northward toward the Abenaki settlement on an island in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. During the journey, Dustan resolved to escape. On the night of March 30, while their captors slept, she, Neff, and Lennardson killed ten of the sleeping Abenaki—men, women, and children—with tomahawks, then fled down the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] in a canoe with the scalps as proof (007-606).

The party reached safety in [[haverhill|Haverhill]], and the General Court of Massachusetts awarded Dustan fifty pounds for the scalps (007-682). Her story became one of the most famous captivity narratives in colonial New England, retold by Cotton Mather and later by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Hawthorne]], and Whittier. She died in 1736.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts the Hannah Dustan story in the *Thursday* chapter as he and his brother pass the mouth of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] ([[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s tributary), eighteen miles below [[haverhill|Haverhill]]. He describes the party paddling "unskilfully, but with nervous energy and determination," the still-bleeding scalps at the bottom of their canoe. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] emphasizes the dramatic contrast: Dustan had been "compelled to rise from child-bed, and half dressed, with one foot bare," and marched through snow and [[nature|wilderness]].

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] frames the event in terms of [[history|historical]] time, noting that "this seems a long while ago, and yet it happened since Milton wrote his Paradise Lost" — a characteristic [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]vian meditation on the relativity of [[history|historical]] distance (007-682). He remarks that the apple-tree against which Dustan's infant was killed still bore fruit eaten by later generations.
