“He trode the unplanted forest floor, whereon

The all-seeing sun for ages hath not shone,

Where feeds the moose, and walks the surly bear,

And up the tall mast runs the woodpecker.

* * * * *

Where darkness found him he lay glad at night;

There the red morning touched him with its light.

* * * * *

Go where he will, the wise man is at home,

His hearth the earth,—his hall the azure dome;

Where his clear spirit leads him, there’s his road,

By God’s own light illumined and foreshowed.”

EMERSON.

[007-001]When we awoke this morning, we heard the faint, deliberate, and ominous

[007-002]sound of rain-drops on our cotton roof. The rain had pattered all

[007-003]night, and now the whole country wept, the drops falling in the river,

[007-004]and on the alders, and in the pastures, and instead of any bow in the

[007-005]heavens, there was the trill of the hair-bird all the morning. The

[007-006]cheery [[religion|faith]] of this little bird atoned for the silence of the whole

[007-007]woodland choir beside. When we first stepped abroad, a flock of sheep,

[007-008]led by their rams, came rushing down a ravine in our rear, with

[007-009]heedless haste and unreserved frisking, as if unobserved by man, from

[007-010]some higher pasture where they had spent the night, to taste the

[007-011]herbage by the river-side; but when their leaders caught sight of our

[007-012]white tent through the mist, struck with sudden astonishment, with

[007-013]their fore-feet braced, they sustained the rushing torrent in their

[007-014]rear, and the whole flock stood stock-still, endeavoring to solve the

[007-015]mystery in their sheepish brains. At length, concluding that it boded

[007-016]no mischief to them, they spread themselves out quietly over the field.

[007-017]We learned afterward that we had pitched our tent on the very spot

[007-018]which a few summers before had been occupied by a party of Penobscots.

[007-019]We could see rising before us through the mist a dark conical eminence

[007-020]called Hooksett Pinnacle, a landmark to boatmen, and also Uncannunuc

[007-021]Mountain, broad off on the west side of the river.

[007-022]This was the limit of our voyage, for a few hours more in the rain

[007-023]would have taken us to the last of the locks, and our boat was too

[007-024]heavy to be dragged around the long and numerous rapids which would

[007-025]occur. On foot, however, we continued up along the bank, feeling our

[007-026]way with a stick through the showery and foggy day, and climbing over

[007-027]the slippery logs in our path with as much pleasure and buoyancy as in

[007-028]brightest sunshine; scenting the fragrance of the pines and the wet

[007-029]clay under our feet, and cheered by the tones of invisible waterfalls;

[007-030]with visions of toadstools, and wandering frogs, and festoons of moss

[007-031]hanging from the spruce-trees, and thrushes flitting silent under the

[007-032]leaves; our road still holding together through that wettest of

[007-033]weather, like faith, while we confidently followed its lead. We managed

[007-034]to keep our thoughts dry, however, and only our clothes were wet. It

[007-035]was altogether a cloudy and drizzling day, with occasional brightenings

[007-036]in the mist, when the trill of the tree-sparrow seemed to be ushering

[007-037]in sunny hours.

[007-038]“Nothing that naturally happens to man can _hurt_ him, earthquakes and

[007-039]thunder-storms not excepted,” said a man of genius, who at this time

[007-040]lived a few miles farther on our road. When compelled by a shower to

[007-041]take shelter under a tree, we may improve that opportunity for a more

[007-042]minute inspection of some of [[nature|Nature]]’s works. I have stood under a tree

[007-043]in the woods half a day at a time, during a heavy rain in the summer,

[007-044]and yet employed myself happily and profitably there prying with

[007-045]microscopic eye into the crevices of the bark or the leaves or the

[007-046]fungi at my feet. “Riches are the attendants of the miser; and the

[007-047]heavens rain plenteously upon the mountains.” I can [[imagination|fancy]] that it would

[007-048]be a luxury to stand up to one’s chin in some retired swamp a whole

[007-049]summer day, scenting the wild honeysuckle and bilberry blows, and

[007-050]lulled by the minstrelsy of gnats and mosquitoes! A day passed in the

[007-051]society of those Greek sages, such as described in the Banquet of

[007-052]Xenophon, would not be comparable with the dry wit of decayed cranberry

[007-053]vines, and the fresh Attic salt of the moss-beds. Say twelve hours of

[007-054]genial and familiar converse with the leopard frog; the sun to rise

[007-055]behind alder and dogwood, and climb buoyantly to his meridian of two

[007-056]hands’ breadth, and finally sink to rest behind some bold western

[007-057]hummock. To hear the evening chant of the mosquito from a thousand

[007-058]green chapels, and the bittern begin to boom from some concealed fort

[007-059]like a sunset gun!—Surely one may as profitably be soaked in the juices

[007-060]of a swamp for one day as pick his way dry-shod over sand. Cold and

[007-061]damp,—are they not as rich experience as warmth and dryness?

[007-062]At present, the drops come trickling down the stubble while we lie

[007-063]drenched on a bed of withered wild oats, by the side of a bushy hill,

[007-064]and the gathering in of the clouds, with the last rush and dying breath

[007-065]of the wind, and then the regular dripping of twigs and leaves the

[007-066]country over, enhance the sense of inward comfort and sociableness. The

[007-067]birds draw closer and are more familiar under the thick foliage,

[007-068]seemingly composing new strains upon their roosts against the sunshine.

[007-069]What were the amusements of the drawing-room and the library in

[007-070]comparison, if we had them here? We should still sing as of old,—

[007-071]My books I’d fain cast off, I cannot read,

[007-072]’Twixt every page my thoughts go stray at large

[007-073]Down in the meadow, where is richer feed,

[007-074]And will not [[thought|mind]] to hit their proper targe.

[007-075]Plutarch was good, and so was [[homer|Homer]] too,

[007-076]Our Shakespeare’s life were rich to live again,

[007-077]What Plutarch read, that was not good nor true,

[007-078]Nor Shakespeare’s books, unless his books were men.

[007-079]Here while I lie beneath this walnut bough,

[007-080]What care I for the Greeks or for Troy town,

[007-081]If juster battles are enacted now

[007-082]Between the ants upon this hummock’s crown?

[007-083]Bid Homer wait till I the issue learn,

[007-084]If red or black the gods will favor most,

[007-085]Or yonder Ajax will the phalanx turn,

[007-086]Struggling to heave some rock against the host.

[007-087]Tell Shakespeare to attend some leisure hour,

[007-088]For now I’ve business with this drop of dew,

[007-089]And see you not, the clouds prepare a shower,—

[007-090]I’ll meet him shortly when the sky is blue.

[007-091]This bed of herd’s-grass and wild oats was spread

[007-092]Last year with nicer skill than monarchs use,

[007-093]A clover tuft is pillow for my head,

[007-094]And violets quite overtop my shoes.

[007-095]And now the cordial clouds have shut all in

[007-096]And gently swells the wind to say all’s well

[007-097]The scattered drops are falling fast and thin,

[007-098]Some in the pool, some in the flower-bell.

[007-099]I am well drenched upon my bed of oats;

[007-100]But see that globe come rolling down its stem

[007-101]Now like a lonely planet there it floats,

[007-102]And now it sinks into my garment’s hem.

[007-103]Drip drip the trees for all the country round,

[007-104]And richness rare distils from every bough,

[007-105]The wind alone it is makes every sound,

[007-106]Shaking down crystals on the leaves below.

[007-107]For shame the sun will never show himself,

[007-108]Who could not with his beams e’er melt me so,

[007-109]My dripping locks,—they would become an elf,

[007-110]Who in a beaded coat does gayly go.

[007-111]The Pinnacle is a small wooded hill which rises very abruptly to the

[007-112]height of about two hundred feet, near the shore at Hooksett Falls. As

[007-113]Uncannunuc Mountain is perhaps the best point from which to view the

[007-114]valley of the Merrimack, so this hill affords the best view of the

[007-115]river itself. I have sat upon its summit, a precipitous rock only a few

[007-116]rods long, in fairer weather, when the sun was setting and filling the

[007-117]river valley with a flood of light. You can see up and down the

[007-118]Merrimack several miles each way. The broad and straight river, full of

[007-119]light and life, with its sparkling and foaming falls, the islet which

[007-120]divides the stream, the village of Hooksett on the shore almost

[007-121]directly under your feet, so near that you can converse with its

[007-122]inhabitants or throw a stone into its yards, the woodland lake at its

[007-123]western base, and the mountains in the north and northeast, make a

[007-124]scene of rare beauty and completeness, which the traveller should take

[007-125]pains to behold.

[007-126]We were hospitably entertained in [[concord-massachusetts|Concord]], New Hampshire, which we

[007-127]persisted in calling _New_ Concord, as we had been wont, to distinguish

[007-128]it from our native town, from which we had been told that it was named

[007-129]and in part originally settled. This would have been the proper place

[007-130]to conclude our voyage, uniting Concord with Concord by these

[007-131]meandering rivers, but our boat was moored some miles below its port.

[007-132]The richness of the intervals at Penacook, now Concord, New Hampshire,

[007-133]had been observed by explorers, and, according to the historian of

[007-134]Haverhill, in the

[007-135]“year 1726, considerable progress was made in the settlement, and a

[007-136]road was cut through the wilderness from Haverhill to Penacook. In the

[007-137]fall of 1727, the first family, that of Captain Ebenezer Eastman, moved

[007-138]into the place. His team was driven by Jacob Shute, who was by birth a

[007-139]Frenchman, and he is said to have been the first person who drove a

[007-140]team through the wilderness. Soon after, says tradition, one Ayer, a

[007-141]lad of 18, drove a team consisting of ten yoke of oxen to Penacook,

[007-142]swam the river, and ploughed a portion of the interval. He is supposed

[007-143]to have been the first person who ploughed land in that place. After he

[007-144]had completed his work, he started on his return at sunrise, drowned a

[007-145]yoke of oxen while recrossing the river, and arrived at Haverhill about

[007-146]midnight. The crank of the first saw-mill was manufactured in

[007-147]Haverhill, and carried to Penacook on a horse.”

[007-148]But we found that the frontiers were not this way any longer. This

[007-149]generation has come into the world fatally late for some enterprises.

[007-150]Go where we will on the _surface_ of things, men have been there before

[007-151]us. We cannot now have the pleasure of erecting the _last_ house; that

[007-152]was long ago set up in the suburbs of Astoria City, and our boundaries

[007-153]have literally been run to the South Sea, according to the old patents.

[007-154]But the lives of men, though more extended laterally in their range,

[007-155]are still as shallow as ever. Undoubtedly, as a Western orator said,

[007-156]“Men generally live over about the same surface; some live long and

[007-157]narrow, and others live broad and short”; but it is all superficial

[007-158]living. A worm is as good a traveller as a grasshopper or a cricket,

[007-159]and a much wiser settler. With all their activity these do not hop away

[007-160]from drought nor forward to summer. We do not avoid evil by fleeing

[007-161]before it, but by rising above or diving below its plane; as the worm

[007-162]escapes drought and frost by boring a few inches deeper. The frontiers

[007-163]are not east or west, north or south, but wherever a man _fronts_ a

[007-164]fact, though that fact be his neighbor, there is an unsettled

[007-165]wilderness between him and Canada, between him and the setting sun, or,

[007-166]farther still, between him and _it_. Let him build himself a log-house

[007-167]with the bark on where he is, _fronting_ IT, and wage there an Old

[007-168]French war for seven or seventy years, with Indians and Rangers, or

[007-169]whatever else may come between him and the reality, and save his scalp

[007-170]if he can.

[007-171]We now no longer sailed or floated on the river, but trod the

[007-172]unyielding land like pilgrims. Sadi tells who may travel; among others,

[007-173]“A common mechanic, who can earn a subsistence by the industry of his

[007-174]hand, and shall not have to stake his reputation for every morsel of

[007-175]bread, as philosophers have said.” He may travel who can subsist on the

[007-176]wild fruits and game of the most cultivated country. A man may travel

[007-177]fast enough and earn his living on the road. I have at times been

[007-178]applied to to do work when on a journey; to do tinkering and repair

[007-179]clocks, when I had a knapsack on my back. A man once applied to me to

[007-180]go into a factory, stating conditions and wages, observing that I

[007-181]succeeded in shutting the window of a railroad car in which we were

[007-182]travelling, when the other passengers had failed. “Hast thou not heard

[007-183]of a Sufi, who was hammering some nails into the sole of his sandal; an

[007-184]officer of cavalry took him by the sleeve, saying, Come along and shoe

[007-185]my horse.” Farmers have asked me to assist them in haying, when I was

[007-186]passing their fields. A man once applied to me to mend his umbrella,

[007-187]taking me for an umbrella-mender, because, being on a journey, I

[007-188]carried an umbrella in my hand while the sun shone. Another wished to

[007-189]buy a tin cup of me, observing that I had one strapped to my belt, and

[007-190]a sauce-pan on my back. The cheapest way to travel, and the way to

[007-191]travel the farthest in the shortest distance, is to go afoot, carrying

[007-192]a dipper, a spoon, and a fish-line, some Indian meal, some salt, and

[007-193]some sugar. When you come to a brook or pond, you can catch fish and

[007-194]cook them; or you can boil a hasty-pudding; or you can buy a loaf of

[007-195]bread at a farmer’s house for fourpence, moisten it in the next brook

[007-196]that crosses the road, and dip into it your sugar,—this alone will last

[007-197]you a whole day;—or, if you are accustomed to heartier living, you can

[007-198]buy a quart of milk for two cents, crumb your bread or cold pudding

[007-199]into it, and eat it with your own spoon out of your own dish. Any one

[007-200]of these things I mean, not all together. I have travelled thus some

[007-201]hundreds of miles without taking any meal in a house, sleeping on the

[007-202]ground when convenient, and found it cheaper, and in many respects more

[007-203]profitable, than staying at home. So that some have inquired why it

[007-204]would not be best to travel always. But I never thought of travelling

[007-205]simply as a means of getting a livelihood. A simple woman down in

[007-206]Tyngsborough, at whose house I once stopped to get a draught of water,

[007-207]when I said, recognizing the bucket, that I had stopped there nine

[007-208]years before for the same purpose, asked if I was not a traveller,

[007-209]supposing that I had been travelling ever since, and had now come round

[007-210]again; that travelling was one of the professions, more or less

[007-211]productive, which her husband did not follow. But continued travelling

[007-212]is far from productive. It begins with wearing away the soles of the

[007-213]shoes, and making the feet sore, and erelong it will wear a man clean

[007-214]up, after making his heart sore into the bargain. I have observed that

[007-215]the after-life of those who have travelled much is very pathetic. True

[007-216]and sincere travelling is no pastime, but it is as serious as the

[007-217]grave, or any part of the human journey, and it requires a long

[007-218]probation to be broken into it. I do not speak of those that travel

[007-219]sitting, the sedentary travellers whose legs hang dangling the while,

[007-220]mere idle symbols of the fact, any more than when we speak of sitting

[007-221]hens we mean those that sit standing, but I mean those to whom

[007-222]travelling is life for the legs, and death too, at last. The traveller

[007-223]must be born again on the road, and earn a passport from the elements,

[007-224]the principal powers that be for him. He shall experience at last that

[007-225]old threat of his mother fulfilled, that he shall be skinned alive. His

[007-226]sores shall gradually deepen themselves that they may heal inwardly,

[007-227]while he gives no rest to the sole of his foot, and at night weariness

[007-228]must be his pillow, that so he may acquire experience against his rainy

[007-229]days.—So was it with us.

[007-230]Sometimes we lodged at an inn in the woods, where trout-fishers from

[007-231]distant cities had arrived before us, and where, to our astonishment,

[007-232]the settlers dropped in at nightfall to have a chat and hear the news,

[007-233]though there was but one road, and no other house was visible,—as if

[007-234]they had come out of the earth. There we sometimes read old newspapers,

[007-235]who never before read new ones, and in the rustle of their leaves heard

[007-236]the dashing of the surf along the Atlantic shore, instead of the sough

[007-237]of the wind among the pines. But then walking had given us an appetite

[007-238]even for the least palatable and nutritious food.

[007-239]Some hard and dry book in a dead language, which you have found it

[007-240]impossible to read at home, but for which you have still a lingering

[007-241]regard, is the best to carry with you on a journey. At a country inn,

[007-242]in the barren society of ostlers and travellers, I could undertake the

[007-243]writers of the silver or the brazen age with confidence. Almost the

[007-244]last regular service which I performed in the cause of literature was

[007-245]to read the works of

[007-246]AULUS PERSIUS FLACCUS.

[007-247]If you have imagined what a divine work is spread out for the poet, and

[007-248]approach this author too, in the hope of finding the field at length

[007-249]fairly entered on, you will hardly dissent from the words of the

[007-250]prologue,

[007-251]“Ipse semipaganus

[007-252]Ad sacra Vatum carmen affero nostrum.”

[007-253]I half pagan

[007-254]Bring my verses to the shrine of the poets.

[007-255]Here is none of the interior dignity of [[virgil|Virgil]], nor the elegance and

[007-256]vivacity of Horace, nor will any sibyl be needed to remind you, that

[007-257]from those older Greek poets there is a sad descent to Persius. You can

[007-258]scarcely distinguish one harmonious sound amid this unmusical bickering

[007-259]with the follies of men.

[007-260]One sees that music has its place in thought, but hardly as yet in

[007-261]language. When the Muse arrives, we wait for her to remould language,

[007-262]and impart to it her own rhythm. Hitherto the [[poetry|verse]] groans and labors

[007-263]with its load, and goes not forward blithely, singing by the way. The

[007-264]best ode may be parodied, indeed is itself a parody, and has a poor and

[007-265]trivial sound, like a man stepping on the rounds of a ladder. Homer and

[007-266]Shakespeare and Milton and Marvell and Wordsworth are but the rustling

[007-267]of leaves and crackling of twigs in the forest, and there is not yet

[007-268]the sound of any bird. The Muse has never lifted up her voice to sing.

[007-269]Most of all, satire will not be sung. A Juvenal or Persius do not marry

[007-270]music to their verse, but are measured fault-finders at best; stand but

[007-271]just outside the faults they condemn, and so are concerned rather about

[007-272]the monster which they have escaped, than the fair prospect before

[007-273]them. Let them live on an age, and they will have travelled out of his

[007-274]shadow and reach, and found other objects to ponder.

[007-275]As long as there is satire, the poet is, as it were, _particeps

[007-276]criminis_. One sees not but he had best let bad take care of itself,

[007-277]and have to do only with what is beyond suspicion. If you light on the

[007-278]least vestige of truth, and it is the weight of the whole body still

[007-279]which stamps the faintest trace, an [[time|eternity]] will not suffice to extol

[007-280]it, while no evil is so huge, but you grudge to bestow on it a moment

[007-281]of hate. [[truth|Truth]] never turns to rebuke falsehood; her own

[007-282]straightforwardness is the severest correction. Horace would not have

[007-283]written satire so well if he had not been inspired by it, as by a

[007-284]passion, and fondly cherished his vein. In his odes, the love always

[007-285]exceeds the hate, so that the severest satire still sings itself, and

[007-286]the poet is satisfied, though the folly be not corrected.

[007-287]A sort of necessary order in the development of Genius is, first,

[007-288]Complaint; second, Plaint; third, [[love|Love]]. Complaint, which is the

[007-289]condition of Persius, lies not in the province of poetry. Erelong the

[007-290]enjoyment of a superior good would have changed his disgust into

[007-291]regret. We can never have much sympathy with the complainer; for after

[007-292]searching nature through, we conclude that he must be both plaintiff

[007-293]and defendant too, and so had best come to a settlement without a

[007-294]hearing. He who receives an injury is to some extent an accomplice of

[007-295]the wrong-doer.

[007-296]Perhaps it would be truer to say, that the highest strain of the muse

[007-297]is essentially plaintive. The saint’s are still _tears_ of joy. Who has

[007-298]ever heard the _Innocent_ sing?

[007-299]But the divinest poem, or the life of a great man, is the severest

[007-300]satire; as impersonal as Nature herself, and like the sighs of her

[007-301]winds in the woods, which convey ever a slight reproof to the hearer.

[007-302]The greater the genius, the keener the edge of the satire.

[007-303]Hence we have to do only with the rare and fragmentary traits, which

[007-304]least belong to Persius, or shall we say, are the properest utterances

[007-305]of his muse; since that which he says best at any time is what he can

[007-306]best say at all times. The Spectators and Ramblers have not failed to

[007-307]cull some quotable sentences from this garden too, so pleasant is it to

[007-308]meet even the most familiar truth in a new dress, when, if our neighbor

[007-309]had said it, we should have passed it by as hackneyed. Out of these six

[007-310]satires, you may perhaps select some twenty lines, which fit so well as

[007-311]many thoughts, that they will recur to the scholar almost as readily as

[007-312]a natural image; though when translated into familiar language, they

[007-313]lose that insular emphasis, which fitted them for quotation. Such lines

[007-314]as the following, translation cannot render commonplace. Contrasting

[007-315]the man of true religion with those who, with jealous privacy, would

[007-316]fain carry on a secret commerce with the gods, he says:—

[007-317]“Haud cuivis promptum est, murmurque humilesque susurros

[007-318]Tollere de templis; et aperto vivere voto.”

[007-319]It is not easy for every one to take murmurs and low

[007-320]Whispers out of the temples, and live with open vow.

[007-321]To the virtuous man, the universe is the only _sanctum sanctorum_, and

[007-322]the penetralia of the temple are the broad noon of his existence. Why

[007-323]should he betake himself to a subterranean crypt, as if it were the

[007-324]only holy ground in all the world which he had left unprofaned? The

[007-325]obedient soul would only the more discover and familiarize things, and

[007-326]escape more and more into light and air, as having henceforth done with

[007-327]secrecy, so that the universe shall not seem open enough for it. At

[007-328]length, it is neglectful even of that silence which is consistent with

[007-329]true modesty, but by its independence of all confidence in its

[007-330]disclosures, makes that which it imparts so private to the hearer, that

[007-331]it becomes the care of the whole world that modesty be not infringed.

[007-332]To the man who cherishes a secret in his breast, there is a still

[007-333]greater secret unexplored. Our most indifferent acts may be matter for

[007-334]secrecy, but whatever we do with the utmost truthfulness and integrity,

[007-335]by virtue of its pureness, must be transparent as light.

[007-336]In the third satire, he asks:—

[007-337]“Est aliquid quò tendis, et in quod dirigis arcum?

[007-338]An passim sequeris corvos, testâve, lutove,

[007-339]Securus quò pes ferat, atque ex tempore vivis?”

[007-340]Is there anything to which thou tendest, and against which thou

[007-341]directest thy bow?

[007-342]Or dost thou pursue crows, at random, with pottery or clay,

[007-343]Careless whither thy feet bear thee, and live _ex tempore_?

[007-344]The bad sense is always a secondary one. Language does not appear to

[007-345]have justice done it, but is obviously cramped and narrowed in its

[007-346]significance, when any meanness is described. The truest construction

[007-347]is not put upon it. What may readily be fashioned into a rule of

[007-348]wisdom, is here thrown in the teeth of the sluggard, and constitutes

[007-349]the front of his offence. Universally, the innocent man will come forth

[007-350]from the sharpest inquisition and lecturing, the combined din of

[007-351]reproof and commendation, with a faint sound of eulogy in his ears. Our

[007-352]vices always lie in the direction of our [[virtue|virtues]], and in their best

[007-353]estate are but plausible imitations of the latter. Falsehood never

[007-354]attains to the dignity of entire falseness, but is only an inferior

[007-355]sort of truth; if it were more thoroughly false, it would incur danger

[007-356]of becoming true.

[007-357]“Securus quo pes ferat, atque ex tempore _vivit_,”

[007-358]is then the motto of a wise man. For first, as the subtle discernment

[007-359]of the language would have taught us, with all his negligence he is

[007-360]still secure; but the sluggard, notwithstanding his heedlessness, is
