# Poetry

## Definition

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], poetry is not merely a literary genre but the ultimate mode of perceiving and expressing truth. He declares that "the wisest definition of poetry the poet will instantly prove false by setting aside its requisitions" (003-1317), suggesting that poetry resists all categorical boundaries. It is "the last and finest result of all human culture" (003-1324) — the distillation of wisdom, experience, and [[dream|vision]] into language that approaches the condition of music.

Yet poetry is also, in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s view, miraculously rare. "It is only by a miracle that poetry is written at all. It is not recoverable by thought, but by a miracle" (007-814). This sacramental view places the poet in the role of prophet or seer — one who receives truth and transmits it through the medium of inspired language. [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]], for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], exemplifies this power: he "saw truth and beauty from his position" (008-181), achieving a synthesis that mere [[art|craftsmanship]] cannot produce.

## Across the Journey

**Saturday** introduces poetry as a cosmic force. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reads "star-poetry" from deep wells (002-137), suggesting that the universe itself is composed in verse — that poetry is not a human invention but a discovery of the fundamental language of [[art|creation]].

**Sunday** offers the most sustained meditation on the nature of poetry. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] distinguishes between the "white man's poetry" (003-369) and the more primal voices of [[homer|Homer]] and [[ossian|Ossian]], setting European literary tradition against older, wilder forms. He notes that "ethics is but a handful of parallel things to poetry" (003-491) — the two are adjacent but distinct domains. The great poets "extracted the pleasure of poetry" (003-1291) from all subjects, while [[virgil|Virgil]]'s poetry "serves a very different use" (003-1300) than [[homer|Homer]]'s. Yet "the rays of Greek poetry struggle down" (003-1417) even to the present age, suggesting an enduring fire that cannot be extinguished.

**Monday** takes a more critical view. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] laments that there is "no poetry in repentance" (004-514), that "we look in vain through English poetry for a single memorable verse" (004-678) on certain themes, and that even Goethe "wanted that universal consent" (004-681) that marks the highest poetry. The farmer, he suggests, is "competent to write the poetry of the grave" (004-1455) — the authentic experience of life and death that city-dwelling poets lack.

**Friday** is the great chapter of poetry. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] opens with the question "what poetry is" and finds that "[[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] is the only biographer of [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]" (008-177) — the poet's life and work are one. He distinguishes poetry from the poet: "poetry is a mysterious, unsleeping revelation in man" (008-184), while the poet himself is merely its vessel. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] surveys English poetry from Chaucer to [[ossian|Ossian]], praising Chaucer as "the father of English poetry" (008-918) and the "first name" in the "archipelago of English poetry" (008-922). Yet he also critiques "shallowest contemporary poetry" (008-1160) for its lack of depth, finding the truest poetry in the "earliest English poetry" (008-977) where "there is less to re[[thought|mind]] the reader of the author" (008-978). In the end, "all that we call poetry, is a particle of such" (008-1404) — a fragment of a greater, unutterable whole.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s theory of poetry belongs to the Romantic tradition that stretches from [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] and Coleridge through to the American Transcendentalists. Like Emerson, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] sees the poet as a "representative man" who speaks for all humanity. His insistence that poetry is a "miracle" rather than a craft echoes the Romantic emphasis on inspiration over technique.

His extensive survey of English poetry on Friday — from the earliest Anglo-Saxon fragments through Chaucer, [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]], [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]], Milton, Gray, and [[ossian|Ossian]] — constitutes one of the most sustained critical meditations in American literature. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] judges poetry not by formal standards but by its authenticity, its proximity to lived experience, its power to reveal truth.
