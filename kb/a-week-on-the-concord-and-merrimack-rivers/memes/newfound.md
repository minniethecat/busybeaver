## Description

Newfound Lake is one of several large lakes in central New Hampshire whose waters contribute to the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] system. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups it with [[squam|Squam Lake]] and Lake Winnipesaukee as sources of the river's flow. (003-1150) The [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] draws its waters from "[[squam|Squam]], and Winnipiseogee, and Newfound, and [[massabesic|Massabesic Lake]]s for its headwaters." (003-1206)

Situated in Grafton County in the Lakes Region of New Hampshire, Newfound Lake lies west of Lake Winnipesaukee, connected to the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset River]] watershed that forms one of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s primary branches.

## On the River Route

Newfound Lake is not directly on the brothers' river route but appears in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s geographical survey of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s source waters. In the Sunday chapter, he traces the river's origins to the network of New Hampshire lakes—[[squam|Squam]], Winnipesaukee, and Newfound among them—that feed the growing stream the brothers are navigating. (003-1150) (003-1206)

## Historical Context

The lake was originally known to Native Americans as "Pasquaney" and was later renamed Newfound by colonial surveyors. The lake's crystal-clear waters and mountain backdrop made it a notable landmark in early New England geography.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s mention of Newfound Lake is brief but significant within his ecological awareness of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] as a unified watershed. By naming the headwater lakes, he situates the brothers' journey within the larger hydrological system, from mountain lakes to the sea at [[newburyport|Newburyport]]. (003-1150) (003-1206)
