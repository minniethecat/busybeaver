## Description

Massabesic Lake, also called Massabesic Pond in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, is a lake in southern New Hampshire east of [[manchester-nh|Manchester]]. It is one of several lakes whose waters feed the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] system—[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups it with [[squam|Squam]], Winnipesaukee, and [[newfound|Newfound]] as headwater sources. (003-1206)

The lake's outlet is Cohass Brook, which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes as flowing into the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] near [[manchester-nh|Manchester]]. He notes that Cohass Brook is "the outlet of Massabesic Pond,—which last is five or six" miles distant. (006-042) The lake lies in the watershed between the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] to the west and the coastal rivers to the east.

## On the River Route

Massabesic Lake appears in two contexts in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative. In the Sunday chapter's geographical survey, it is listed among the headwater lakes that supply the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]—a distant source whose waters the brothers will encounter indirectly as they travel the river. (003-1206)

On Wednesday, passing through the [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] area, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes Cohass Brook entering the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] and identifies it as the outlet of Massabesic Pond, situating the lake within the local geography visible from the river. (006-042)

## Historical Context

Massabesic Lake derives its name from a Native American term. By the mid-19th century, the area around the lake remained relatively undeveloped compared to the manufacturing center of [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] downstream. In later years, Massabesic Lake became the primary water supply for the city of [[manchester-nh|Manchester]], a role it continues to serve.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s reference to Massabesic is characteristically concise and geographical—a notation that fits within his systematic tracing of the river's sources. The mention comes amid his observations of the [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] area, where the natural [[nature|landscape]] of brooks and ponds coexists uneasily with the "din of a manufacturing town." (006-042) The lake represents one of the [[silence|quiet]] headwaters that continue to feed the industrialized river, a counterpoint between source and transformation.
