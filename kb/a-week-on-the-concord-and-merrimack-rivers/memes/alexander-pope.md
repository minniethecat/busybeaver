## Biography

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the preeminent English poet of the early eighteenth century and the master of the heroic couplet. Born in London to a Catholic family — a circumstance that barred him from university and public office under the Test Acts — he was largely self-educated.

Despite chronic ill health and a height of only four feet six inches, Pope achieved literary celebrity by his mid-twenties. His sharp satirical wit made him both the most admired and the most feared poet of his age.

## Works / Significance

Pope's major works include *The Rape of the Lock* (1712), a mock-epic satire of aristocratic frivolity; *The Dunciad* (1728–43), a savage attack on literary dullness; and *An Essay on Man* (1733–34), a philosophical poem on the order of the uni[[poetry|verse]]. His translations of [[homer|Homer]]'s *Iliad* and *Odyssey* — though criticized for their free handling of the Greek — defined English neoclassicism and made him financially independent.

## In Thoreau's Text

Pope appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] surveys the tradition of English poetry and finds it in autumnal decline (008-888):

> Dryden, and Pope, and Gray. Our summer of English poetry like the Greek and Latin before it, seems well advanced toward its fall, and laden with the fruit and foliage of the season, with bright autumnal tints, but soon the winter will scatter its myriad clustering and shading leaves, and leave only a few desolate and fibrous boughs to sustain the sky.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups Pope with [[john-dryden|Dryden]] and Gray as representatives of an English poetic tradition that, in his view, had passed its zenith. The seasonal metaphor — summer giving way to autumn and then to barren winter — expresses his critique that neoclassical polish had supplanted the vital, spring-like energy of earlier poetry.
