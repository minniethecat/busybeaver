## Biography

Myles Standish (c. 1584–1656) was an English military officer hired by the Pilgrims as military advisor for [[pilgrims-landing|Plymouth Colony]]. Though not a Separatist himself, he served the colony from its founding in 1620 until his death, leading exploratory expeditions, organizing the colony's defenses, and negotiating — sometimes aggressively — with Native American tribes.

Standish is remembered for his military expeditions and for his complex role in early colonial-Native relations, including the controversial raid at Wessagusset (1623). Henry Wadsworth Longfellow later fictionalized him in *The Courtship of Miles Standish* (1858).

## In Thoreau's Text

Standish appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Monday" chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] lists him among the hardy colonial figures of New England's [[history|past]] (004-077):

> sturdy pioneers still as Miles Standish, or Church, or Lovewell.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups Standish with Captain Benjamin Church, the noted Indian fighter, and Captain [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]], leader of the 1725 [[lovewells-fight|Battle of Pequawket]]. The three are cited as exemplars of the rugged colonial spirit — "sturdy pioneers" whose names still echoed in the [[nature|landscape]] [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] tra[[poetry|verse]]d. The brief, almost offhand mention reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s characteristic compression of colonial history into vivid shorthand.
