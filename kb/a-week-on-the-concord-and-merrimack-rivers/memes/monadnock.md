## Description

[[mount-monadnock|Mount Monadnock]] is a solitary peak rising to 3,165 feet in southwestern New Hampshire. It is known for its bare, rocky summit and panoramic views. The mountain gave its name to the geological term "monadnock," meaning an isolated hill or mountain of resistant rock rising above a peneplain. Monadnock is visible from much of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley.

## On the River Route

Monadnock appears as a distant landmark during the brothers' journey up the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions it alongside the [[peterborough|Peterborough Hills]] (004-1219) and groups it with [[mount-wachusett|Wachusett]] as mountains that have "retreated" westward from the travelers' native hills. (004-1323) It is visible from the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]] valley and the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]].

## Historical Context

Monadnock has been an important landmark for Native Americans and later European settlers. The name comes from the Abenaki word "menonadenak," meaning "smooth mountain." It was a major landmark for travelers along the Native American trail routes later used by colonists. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it was already a well-known natural feature.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes Monadnock and the [[peterborough|Peterborough Hills]] (004-1219) as part of the western [[nature|landscape]] visible from the river. He pairs [[mount-wachusett|Wachusett]] and Monadnock as mountains that have "retreated" from the perspective of the journey, suggesting the way distant landmarks seem to recede as one travels. (004-1323) The mountain serves as a fixed point in the changing [[nature|landscape]].
