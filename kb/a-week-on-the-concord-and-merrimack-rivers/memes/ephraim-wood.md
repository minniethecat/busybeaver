## Biography

Ephraim Wood, Esquire, was a justice of the peace in colonial Massachusetts during the late seventeenth or early eighteenth century. He resided in the region between the growing settlements of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley, where he exercised the authority of the colonial legal system over travellers and residents alike.

The [[history|historical]] record captured by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] shows Wood questioning a man named Richardson of Shirley, Massachusetts, who was travelling westward with "teams with rigging such as is used to carry barrels" (003-573). Richardson identified his fellow-traveller as [[jonas-parker|Jonas Parker]] and his employer as a Mr. Longley. This minor roadside interrogation, preserved in the annals of [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]], captures the texture of everyday colonial governance on the Massachusetts frontier.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites the Honorable Ephraim Wood in the *Sunday* chapter as a foil for his own journey. Whereas Richardson and Parker were stopped and questioned by the Squire about their business and their loads, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother glided northward on September 1, 1839, "unquestioned by any Squire or Church Deacon and ready to bear ourselves out if need were." Wood thus serves as a symbol of the regulated, scrutinized life of settled society — the very thing [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s river journey was a temporary escape from.
