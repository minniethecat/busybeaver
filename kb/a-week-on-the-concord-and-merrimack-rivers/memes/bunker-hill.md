## Description

Bunker Hill is a low rise in Charlestown, now part of Boston, Massachusetts, famous as the site of the [[revolutionary-battles|Battle of Bunker Hill]] (June 17, 1775), one of the earliest major engagements of the [[american-revolution|American Revolutionary War]]. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the hill was crowned by the Bunker Hill Monument, a granite obelisk commemorating the battle.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Bunker Hill not as a direct stop on his river journey but as a cultural and [[history|historical]] landmark within the broader New England [[nature|landscape]]. (003-1102) He also references "the battle of Bunker Hill" as a touchstone of the Revolutionary War. (006-503)

## On the River Route

Bunker Hill is not on the [[concord-mass|Concord]]-[[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] river route, but [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references it early in the narrative as a figurative destination. He writes of setting out "to find our Bunker Hill"—using the landmark as a metaphor for the heroic ambition and patriotic sentiment that animate the journey's opening. (002-099)

The reference to coming "from Montaup to Bunker Hill, from bear-skins, parched" corn to the monuments of civilization sketches a rapid progression from the Native American world of King Philip (Montaup) to the Revolutionary iconography of Bunker Hill. (003-1102)

## Historical Context

The [[revolutionary-battles|Battle of Bunker Hill]], though technically a British tactical victory, demonstrated that colonial militia could stand against British regulars and inflicted heavy casualties on the Crown forces. The battle became a rallying symbol of American resistance, and the construction of the Bunker Hill Monument (completed 1843, just a few years before [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s book was published) fixed the site in the national imagination.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s use of Bunker Hill is characteristically layered. On one level, he acknowledges the patriotic significance of the site. On another, his phrase "to find our Bunker Hill" (002-099) suggests that the brothers seek their own form of heroic encounter—not on a battlefield but on a river, where the conquest is of perception rather than territory. The juxtaposition of Montaup (King Philip's seat) and Bunker Hill (Revolutionary symbol) collapses two centuries of New England history into a single sentence, implying that both Native and colonial [[history|past]]s inhabit the same [[nature|landscape]]. (003-1102)
