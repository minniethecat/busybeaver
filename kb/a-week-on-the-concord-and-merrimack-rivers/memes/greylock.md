## Description

Mount Greylock is the highest natural point in Massachusetts, rising to 3,489 feet in the Berkshire Hills of western Massachusetts. Known for its distinctive shape and panoramic views, Greylock is visible from great distances across the state. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] climbed Greylock in 1844, five years after his river journey, and wrote about it in "A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s."

## On the River Route

Greylock lies far to the west of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] river route and is not directly on the brothers' path. However, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] includes his [[thought|reflection]]s on Greylock and the Berkshire [[nature|landscape]] within the broader philosophical and geographical context of "A Week," connecting [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]]s of his native region to the larger New England [[nature|landscape]].

## Historical Context

Mount Greylock has been known by various names, including "Saddleback Mountain" for its double-peaked profile. The mountain was an important landmark for Native American tribes including the Mohican. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s 1844 ascent of Greylock, described in his writings, was part of his exploration of the Massachusetts [[nature|wilderness]].

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[thought|reflection]]s on Greylock in "A Week" connect the specific [[nature|landscape]] of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] to the broader New England [[nature|wilderness]]. He was drawn to mountains as places of expanded perspective and spiritual [[wisdom|insight]], and Greylock represented one of the wildest and most remote places in Massachusetts during his time.
