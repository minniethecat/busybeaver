## Overview

Amesbury is a town in Essex County, Massachusetts, situated on the north bank of
the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] near its mouth at the Atlantic Ocean. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s day, the
river at Amesbury was already a broad tidal estuary, bearing ship traffic and
reflecting the town's maritime orientation.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as it broadens below [[haverhill|Haverhill]], noting the
transformation of the river into a tidal waterway between Amesbury and its
neighboring town:

> (003-1158) ocean. Between the towns of Amesbury and Newbury it is a broad
> commercial river, from a third to half a mile in width, no longer skirted
> with yellow and crumbling banks, but backed by high green hills.

The passage marks the transition from the inland river to the sea, with the
river becoming "commercial" — a working waterway rather than the [[history|past]]oral
stream of the upper reaches.

## Geography

Amesbury occupies the point where the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] widens dramatically before
emptying into the Atlantic at [[plum-island|Plum Island]] Sound. The town's early settlement,
in the seventeenth century, centered on the falls of the Powwow River, a small
tributary that enters the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at Amesbury's western edge. The "high
green hills" [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted are drumlins and glacial features characteristic
of the lower [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] valley.

## Early Industry

The Powwow River falls provided water power that made Amesbury an early center
of milling and manufacturing. By the early nineteenth century, the town was
known for its nail factories, hat manufactories, and — most enduringly —
carriage building. The Amesbury carriage industry would later supply vehicles
across the United States.

## Relationship to the Merrimack

Unlike the towns of the upper river, which used the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] primarily for
transport and fishing, Amesbury's relationship to the river was colored by
its proximity to the sea. The broad tidal reach at Amesbury meant that
shipbuilding and maritime commerce were part of the town's economy alongside
the water-powered mills on its smaller tributaries.
