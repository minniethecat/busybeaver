_“Man is man’s foe and destiny.”_

COTTON.

[006-001]Early this morning, as we were rolling up our buffaloes and loading our

[006-002]boat amid the dew, while our embers were still smoking, the masons who

[006-003]worked at the locks, and whom we had seen crossing the river in their

[006-004]boat the evening before while we were examining the rock, came upon us

[006-005]as they were going to their work, and we found that we had pitched our

[006-006]tent directly in the path to their boat. This was the only time that we

[006-007]were observed on our camping-ground. Thus, far from the beaten highways

[006-008]and the dust and din of travel, we beheld the country privately, yet

[006-009]freely, and at our leisure. Other roads do some violence to [[nature|Nature]], and

[006-010]bring the traveller to stare at her, but the river steals into the

[006-011]scenery it traverses without intrusion, silently creating and adorning

[006-012]it, and is as free to come and go as the zephyr.

[006-013]As we shoved away from this rocky coast, before sunrise, the smaller

[006-014]bittern, the genius of the shore, was moping along its edge, or stood

[006-015]probing the mud for its food, with ever an eye on us, though so

[006-016]demurely at work, or else he ran along over the wet stones like a

[006-017]wrecker in his storm-coat, looking out for wrecks of snails and

[006-018]cockles. Now away he goes, with a limping flight, uncertain where he

[006-019]will alight, until a rod of clear sand amid the alders invites his

[006-020]feet; and now our steady approach compels him to seek a new retreat. It

[006-021]is a bird of the oldest Thalesian school, and no doubt believes in the

[006-022]priority of water to the other elements; the relic of a twilight

[006-023]antediluvian age which yet inhabits these bright American rivers with

[006-024]us Yankees. There is something venerable in this melancholy and

[006-025]contemplative race of birds, which may have trodden the earth while it

[006-026]was yet in a slimy and imperfect state. Perchance their tracks too are

[006-027]still visible on the stones. It still lingers into our glaring summers,

[006-028]bravely supporting its fate without sympathy from man, as if it looked

[006-029]forward to some second advent of which _he_ has no assurance. One

[006-030]wonders if, by its patient study by rocks and sandy capes, it has

[006-031]wrested the whole of her secret from Nature yet. What a rich experience

[006-032]it must have gained, standing on one leg and looking out from its dull

[006-033]eye so long on sunshine and rain, moon and stars! What could it tell of

[006-034]stagnant pools and reeds and dank night-fogs! It would be worth the

[006-035]while to look closely into the eye which has been open and seeing at

[006-036]such hours, and in such solitudes, its dull, yellowish, greenish eye.

[006-037]Methinks my own soul must be a bright invisible green. I have seen

[006-038]these birds stand by the half-dozen together in the shallower water

[006-039]along the shore, with their bills thrust into the mud at the bottom,

[006-040]probing for food, the whole head being concealed, while the neck and

[006-041]body formed an arch above the water.

[006-042]Cohass Brook, the outlet of Massabesic Pond,—which last is five or six

[006-043]miles distant, and contains fifteen hundred acres, being the largest

[006-044]body of fresh water in Rockingham County,—comes in near here from the

[006-045]east. Rowing between Manchester and Bedford, we passed, at an early

[006-046]hour, a ferry and some falls, called Goff’s Falls, the Indian Cohasset,

[006-047]where there is a small village, and a handsome green islet in the

[006-048]middle of the stream. From Bedford and Merrimack have been boated the

[006-049]bricks of which Lowell is made. [[About]] twenty years before, as they told

[006-050]us, one Moore, of Bedford, having clay on his farm, contracted to

[006-051]furnish eight millions of bricks to the founders of that city within

[006-052]two years. He fulfilled his contract in one year, and since then bricks

[006-053]have been the principal export from these towns. The farmers found thus

[006-054]a market for their wood, and when they had brought a load to the kilns,

[006-055]they could cart a load of bricks to the shore, and so make a profitable

[006-056]day’s work of it. Thus all parties were benefited. It was worth the

[006-057]while to see the place where Lowell was “dug out.” So likewise

[006-058]Manchester is being built of bricks made still higher up the river at

[006-059]Hooksett.

[006-060]There might be seen here on the bank of the Merrimack, near Goff’s

[006-061]Falls, in what is now the town of Bedford, famous “for hops and for its

[006-062]fine domestic manufactures,” some graves of the aborigines. The land

[006-063]still bears this scar here, and time is slowly crumbling the bones of a

[006-064]race. Yet, without fail, every spring, since they first fished and

[006-065]hunted here, the brown thrasher has heralded the morning from a birch

[006-066]or alder spray, and the undying race of reed-birds still rustles

[006-067]through the withering grass. But these bones rustle not. These

[006-068]mouldering elements are slowly preparing for another metamorphosis, to

[006-069]serve new masters, and what was the Indian’s will erelong be the white

[006-070]man’s sinew.

[006-071]We learned that Bedford was not so famous for hops as formerly, since

[006-072]the price is fluctuating, and poles are now scarce. Yet if the

[006-073]traveller goes back a few miles from the river, the hop-kilns will

[006-074]still excite his curiosity.

[006-075]There were few incidents in our voyage this forenoon, though the river

[006-076]was now more rocky and the falls more frequent than before. It was a

[006-077]pleasant change, after rowing incessantly for many hours, to lock

[006-078]ourselves through in some retired place,—for commonly there was no

[006-079]lock-man at hand,—one sitting in the boat, while the other, sometimes

[006-080]with no little labor and heave-yo-ing, opened and shut the gates,

[006-081]waiting patiently to see the locks fill. We did not once use the wheels

[006-082]which we had provided. Taking advantage of the eddy, we were sometimes

[006-083]floated up to the locks almost in the face of the falls; and, by the

[006-084]same cause, any floating timber was carried round in a circle and

[006-085]repeatedly drawn into the rapids before it finally went down the

[006-086]stream. These old gray structures, with their [[silence|quiet]] arms stretched over

[006-087]the river in the sun, appeared like natural objects in the scenery, and

[006-088]the kingfisher and sandpiper alighted on them as readily as on stakes

[006-089]or rocks.

[006-090]We rowed leisurely up the stream for several hours, until the sun had

[006-091]got high in the sky, our thoughts monotonously beating time to our

[006-092]oars. For outward variety there was only the river and the receding

[006-093]shores, a vista continually opening behind and closing before us, as we

[006-094]sat with our backs up-stream; and, for inward, such thoughts as the

[006-095]muses grudgingly lent us. We were always passing some low, inviting

[006-096]shore, or some overhanging bank, on which, however, we never landed.

[006-097]Such near aspects had we

[006-098]Of our life’s scenery.

[006-099]It might be seen by what tenure men held the earth. The smallest stream

[006-100]is _mediterranean_ sea, a smaller ocean creek within the land, where

[006-101]men may steer by their farm-bounds and cottage-lights. For my own part,

[006-102]but for the geographers, I should hardly have known how large a portion

[006-103]of our globe is water, my life has chiefly passed within so deep a

[006-104]cove. Yet I have sometimes ventured as far as to the mouth of my Snug

[006-105]Harbor. From an old ruined fort on Staten Island, I have loved to watch

[006-106]all day some vessel whose name I had read in the morning through the

[006-107]telegraph-glass, when she first came upon the coast, and her hull

[006-108]heaved up and glistened in the sun, from the moment when the pilot and

[006-109]most adventurous news-boats met her, [[history|past]] the Hook, and up the narrow

[006-110]channel of the wide outer bay, till she was boarded by the

[006-111]health-officer, and took her station at Quarantine, or held on her

[006-112]unquestioned course to the wharves of New York. It was interesting,

[006-113]too, to watch the less adventurous newsman, who made his assault as the

[006-114]vessel swept through the Narrows, defying plague and quarantine law,

[006-115]and, fastening his little cockboat to her huge side, clambered up and

[006-116]disappeared in the cabin. And then I could imagine what momentous news

[006-117]was being imparted by the captain, which no American ear had ever

[006-118]heard, that Asia, Africa, Europe—were all sunk; for which at length he

[006-119]pays the price, and is seen descending the ship’s side with his bundle

[006-120]of newspapers, but not where he first got up, for these arrivers do not

[006-121]stand still to gossip; and he hastes away with steady sweeps to dispose

[006-122]of his wares to the highest bidder, and we shall erelong read something

[006-123]startling,—“By the latest arrival,”—“by the good ship——.” On Sunday I

[006-124]beheld, from some interior hill, the long procession of vessels getting

[006-125]to sea, reaching from the city wharves through the Narrows, and [[time|past]]

[006-126]the Hook, quite to the ocean stream, far as the eye could reach, with

[006-127]stately march and silken sails, all counting on lucky voyages, but each

[006-128]time some of the number, no doubt, destined to go to Davy’s locker, and

[006-129]never come on this coast again. And, again, in the evening of a

[006-130]pleasant day, it was my amusement to count the sails in sight. But as

[006-131]the setting sun continually brought more and more to light, still

[006-132]farther in the horizon, the last count always had the advantage, till,

[006-133]by the time the last rays streamed over the sea, I had doubled and

[006-134]trebled my first number; though I could no longer class them all under

[006-135]the several heads of ships, barks, brigs, schooners, and sloops, but

[006-136]most were faint generic _vessels_ only. And then the temperate twilight

[006-137]light, perchance, revealed the floating home of some sailor whose

[006-138]thoughts were already alienated from this American coast, and directed

[006-139]towards the Europe of our [[dream|dreams]]. I have stood upon the same hill-top

[006-140]when a thunder-shower, rolling down from the Catskills and Highlands,

[006-141]passed over the island, deluging the land; and, when it had suddenly

[006-142]left us in sunshine, have seen it overtake successively, with its huge

[006-143]shadow and dark, descending wall of rain, the vessels in the bay. Their

[006-144]bright sails were suddenly drooping and dark, like the sides of barns,

[006-145]and they seemed to shrink before the storm; while still far beyond them

[006-146]on the sea, through this dark veil, gleamed the sunny sails of those

[006-147]vessels which the storm had not yet reached. And at midnight, when all

[006-148]around and overhead was darkness, I have seen a field of trembling,

[006-149]silvery light far out on the sea, the [[thought|reflection]] of the moonlight from

[006-150]the ocean, as if beyond the precincts of our night, where the moon

[006-151]traversed a cloudless heaven,—and sometimes a dark speck in its midst,

[006-152]where some fortunate vessel was pursuing its happy voyage by night.

[006-153]But to us river sailors the sun never rose out of ocean waves, but from

[006-154]some green coppice, and went down behind some dark mountain line. We,

[006-155]too, were but dwellers on the shore, like the bittern of the morning;

[006-156]and our pursuit, the wrecks of snails and cockles. Nevertheless, we

[006-157]were contented to know the better one fair particular shore.

[006-158]My life is like a stroll upon the beach,

[006-159]As near the ocean’s edge as I can go,

[006-160]My tardy steps its waves sometimes o’erreach,

[006-161]Sometimes I stay to let them overflow.

[006-162]My sole employment ’t is, and scrupulous care,

[006-163]To place my gains beyond the reach of tides,

[006-164]Each smoother pebble, and each shell more rare,

[006-165]Which ocean kindly to my hand confides.

[006-166]I have but few companions on the shore,

[006-167]They scorn the strand who sail upon the sea,

[006-168]Yet oft I think the ocean they’ve sailed o’er

[006-169]Is deeper known upon the strand to me.

[006-170]The middle sea contains no crimson dulse,

[006-171]Its deeper waves cast up no pearls to view,

[006-172]Along the shore my hand is on its pulse,

[006-173]And I converse with many a shipwrecked crew.

[006-174]The small houses which were scattered along the river at intervals of a

[006-175]mile or more were commonly out of sight to us, but sometimes, when we

[006-176]rowed near the shore, we heard the peevish note of a hen, or some

[006-177]slight domestic sound, which betrayed them. The lock-men’s houses were

[006-178]particularly well placed, retired, and high, always at falls or rapids,

[006-179]and commanding the pleasantest reaches of the river,—for it is

[006-180]generally wider and more lake-like just above a fall,—and there they

[006-181]wait for boats. These humble dwellings, homely and sincere, in which a

[006-182]hearth was still the essential part, were more pleasing to our eyes

[006-183]than palaces or castles would have been. In the noon of these days, as

[006-184]we have said, we occasionally climbed the banks and approached these

[006-185]houses, to get a glass of water and make acquaintance with their

[006-186]inhabitants. High in the leafy bank, surrounded commonly by a small

[006-187]patch of corn and beans, squashes and melons, with sometimes a graceful

[006-188]hop-yard on one side, and some running vine over the windows, they

[006-189]appeared like beehives set to gather honey for a summer. I have not

[006-190]read of any Arcadian life which surpasses the actual luxury and

[006-191]serenity of these New England dwellings. For the outward gilding, at

[006-192]least, the age is golden enough. As you approach the sunny doorway,

[006-193]awakening the echoes by your steps, still no sound from these barracks

[006-194]of repose, and you fear that the gentlest knock may seem rude to the

[006-195]Oriental dreamers. The door is opened, perchance, by some Yankee-Hindoo

[006-196]woman, whose small-voiced but sincere hospitality, out of the

[006-197]bottomless depths of a quiet nature, has travelled quite round to the

[006-198]opposite side, and fears only to obtrude its kindness. You step over

[006-199]the white-scoured floor to the bright “dresser” lightly, as if afraid

[006-200]to disturb the devotions of the household,—for Oriental dynasties

[006-201]appear to have passed away since the dinner-table was last spread

[006-202]here,—and thence to the frequented curb, where you see your

[006-203]long-forgotten, unshaven face at the bottom, in juxtaposition with

[006-204]new-made butter and the trout in the well. “Perhaps you would like some

[006-205]molasses and ginger,” suggests the faint noon voice. Sometimes there

[006-206]sits the brother who follows the sea, their representative man; who

[006-207]knows only how far it is to the nearest port, no more distances, all

[006-208]the rest is sea and distant capes,—patting the dog, or dandling the

[006-209]kitten in arms that were stretched by the cable and the oar, pulling

[006-210]against Boreas or the trade-winds. He looks up at the stranger, half

[006-211]pleased, half astonished, with a mariner’s eye, as if he were a dolphin

[006-212]within cast. If men will believe it, _sua si bona nôrint_, there are no

[006-213]more quiet Tempes, nor more poetic and Arcadian lives, than may be

[006-214]lived in these New England dwellings. We thought that the employment of

[006-215]their inhabitants by day would be to tend the flowers and herds, and at

[006-216]night, like the shepherds of old, to cluster and give names to the

[006-217]stars from the river banks.

[006-218]We passed a large and densely wooded island this forenoon, between

[006-219]Short’s and Griffith’s Falls, the fairest which we had met with, with a

[006-220]handsome grove of elms at its head. If it had been evening we should

[006-221]have been glad to camp there. Not long after, one or two more were

[006-222]passed. The boatmen told us that the current had recently made

[006-223]important changes here. An island always pleases my imagination, even

[006-224]the smallest, as a small continent and integral portion of the globe. I

[006-225]have a [[imagination|fancy]] for building my hut on one. Even a bare, grassy isle,

[006-226]which I can see entirely over at a glance, has some undefined and

[006-227]mysterious charm for me. There is commonly such a one at the junction

[006-228]of two rivers, whose currents bring down and deposit their respective

[006-229]sands in the eddy at their confluence, as it were the womb of a

[006-230]continent. By what a delicate and far-stretched contribution every

[006-231]island is made! What an enterprise of Nature thus to lay the

[006-232]foundations of and to build up the future continent, of golden and

[006-233]silver sands and the ruins of forests, with ant-like industry! Pindar

[006-234]gives the following account of the origin of Thera, whence, in after

[006-235]times, Libyan Cyrene was settled by Battus. Triton, in the form of

[006-236]Eurypylus, presents a clod to Euphemus, one of the Argonauts, as they

[006-237]are about to return home.

[006-238]“He knew of our haste,

[006-239]And immediately seizing a clod

[006-240]With his right hand, strove to give it

[006-241]As a chance stranger’s gift.

[006-242]Nor did the hero disregard him, but leaping on the shore,

[006-243]Stretching hand to hand,

[006-244]Received the mystic clod.

[006-245]But I hear it sinking from the deck,

[006-246]Go with the sea brine

[006-247]At evening, accompanying the watery sea.

[006-248]Often indeed I urged the careless

[006-249]Menials to guard it, but their minds forgot.

[006-250]And now in this island the imperishable seed of spacious Libya

[006-251]Is spilled before its hour.”

[006-252]It is a [[beauty|beautiful]] fable, also related by Pindar, how Helius, or the

[006-253]Sun, looked down into the sea one day,—when perchance his rays were

[006-254]first reflected from some increasing glittering sandbar,—and saw the

[006-255]fair and fruitful island of Rhodes

[006-256]“springing up from the bottom,

[006-257]Capable of feeding many men, and suitable for flocks;

[006-258]and at the nod of Zeus,

[006-259]“The island sprang from the watery

[006-260]Sea; and the genial Father of penetrating beams,

[006-261]Ruler of fire-breathing horses, has it.”

[006-262]The shifting islands! who would not be willing that his house should be

[006-263]undermined by such a foe! The inhabitant of an island can tell what

[006-264]currents formed the land which he cultivates; and his earth is still

[006-265]being created or destroyed. There before his door, perchance, still

[006-266]empties the stream which brought down the material of his farm ages

[006-267]before, and is still bringing it down or washing it away,—the graceful,

[006-268]gentle robber!

[006-269]Not long after this we saw the Piscataquoag, or Sparkling Water,

[006-270]emptying in on our left, and heard the Falls of Amoskeag above. Large

[006-271]quantities of lumber, as we read in the Gazetteer, were still annually

[006-272]floated down the Piscataquoag to the Merrimack, and there are many fine

[006-273]mill privileges on it. Just above the mouth of this river we passed the

[006-274]artificial falls where the canals of the Manchester Manufacturing

[006-275]Company discharge themselves into the Merrimack. They are striking

[006-276]enough to have a name, and, with the scenery of a Bashpish, would be

[006-277]visited from far and near. The water falls thirty or forty feet over

[006-278]seven or eight steep and narrow terraces of stone, probably to break

[006-279]its force, and is converted into one mass of foam. This canal-water did

[006-280]not seem to be the worse for the wear, but foamed and fumed as purely,

[006-281]and boomed as savagely and impressively, as a mountain torrent, and,

[006-282]though it came from under a factory, we saw a rainbow here. These are

[006-283]now the [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag Falls]], removed a mile down-stream. But we did not

[006-284]tarry to examine them minutely, making haste to get past the village

[006-285]here collected, and out of hearing of the hammer which was laying the

[006-286]foundation of another Lowell on the banks. At the time of our voyage

[006-287]Manchester was a village of about two thousand inhabitants, where we

[006-288]landed for a moment to get some cool water, and where an inhabitant

[006-289]told us that he was accustomed to go across the river into Goffstown

[006-290]for his water. But now, as I have been told, and indeed have witnessed,

[006-291]it contains fourteen thousand inhabitants. From a hill on the road

[006-292]between Goffstown and Hooksett, four miles distant, I have seen a

[006-293]thunder-shower pass over, and the sun break out and shine on a city

[006-294]there, where I had landed nine years before in the fields; and there

[006-295]was waving the flag of its Museum, where “the only perfect skeleton of

[006-296]a Greenland or river whale in the United States” was to be seen, and I

[006-297]also read in its directory of a “Manchester Athenæum and Gallery of the

[006-298]Fine Arts.”

[006-299]According to the Gazetteer, the descent of Amoskeag Falls, which are

[006-300]the most considerable in the Merrimack, is fifty-four feet in half a

[006-301]mile. We locked ourselves through here with much ado, surmounting the

[006-302]successive watery steps of this river’s staircase in the midst of a

[006-303]crowd of villagers, jumping into the canal to their amusement, to save

[006-304]our boat from upsetting, and consuming much river-water in our service.

[006-305]Amoskeag, or Namaskeak, is said to mean “great fishing-place.” It was

[006-306]hereabouts that the Sachem Wannalancet resided. Tradition says that his

[006-307]tribe, when at war with the Mohawks, concealed their provisions in the

[006-308]cavities of the rocks in the upper part of these falls. The Indians,

[006-309]who hid their provisions in these holes, and affirmed “that God had cut

[006-310]them out for that purpose,” understood their origin and use better than

[006-311]the Royal Society, who in their Transactions, in the last century,

[006-312]speaking of these very holes, declare that “they seem plainly to be

[006-313]artificial.” Similar “pot-holes” may be seen at the Stone Flume on this

[006-314]river, on the Ottaway, at Bellows’ Falls on the Connecticut, and in the

[006-315]limestone rock at Shelburne Falls on Deerfield River in Massachusetts,

[006-316]and more or less generally about all falls. Perhaps the most remarkable

[006-317]curiosity of this kind in New England is the well-known Basin on the

[006-318]Pemigewasset, one of the head-waters of this river, twenty by thirty

[006-319]feet in extent and proportionably deep, with a smooth and rounded brim,

[006-320]and filled with a cold, pellucid, and greenish water. At Amoskeag the

[006-321]river is divided into many separate torrents and trickling rills by the

[006-322]rocks, and its volume is so much reduced by the drain of the canals

[006-323]that it does not fill its bed. There are many pot-holes here on a rocky

[006-324]island which the river washes over in high freshets. As at Shelburne

[006-325]Falls, where I first observed them, they are from one foot to four or

[006-326]five in diameter, and as many in depth, perfectly round and regular,

[006-327]with smooth and gracefully curved brims, like goblets. Their origin is

[006-328]apparent to the most careless observer. A stone which the current has

[006-329]washed down, meeting with obstacles, revolves as on a pivot where it

[006-330]lies, gradually sinking in the course of centuries deeper and deeper

[006-331]into the rock, and in new freshets receiving the aid of fresh stones,

[006-332]which are drawn into this trap and doomed to revolve there for an

[006-333]indefinite period, doing Sisyphus-like penance for stony sins, until

[006-334]they either wear out, or wear through the bottom of their prison, or

[006-335]else are released by some revolution of nature. There lie the stones of

[006-336]various sizes, from a pebble to a foot or two in diameter, some of

[006-337]which have rested from their labor only since the spring, and some

[006-338]higher up which have lain still and dry for ages,—we noticed some here

[006-339]at least sixteen feet above the present level of the water,—while

[006-340]others are still revolving, and enjoy no respite at any season. In one

[006-341]instance, at Shelburne Falls, they have worn quite through the rock, so

[006-342]that a portion of the river leaks through in anticipation of the fall.

[006-343]Some of these pot-holes at Amoskeag, in a very hard brown-stone, had an

[006-344]oblong, cylindrical stone of the same material loosely fitting them.

[006-345]One, as much as fifteen feet deep and seven or eight in diameter, which

[006-346]was worn quite through to the water, had a huge rock of the same

[006-347]material, smooth but of irregular form, lodged in it. Everywhere there

[006-348]were the rudiments or the wrecks of a dimple in the rock; the rocky

[006-349]shells of whirlpools. As if by force of example and sympathy after so

[006-350]many lessons, the rocks, the hardest material, had been endeavoring to

[006-351]whirl or flow into the forms of the most fluid. The finest workers in

[006-352]stone are not copper or steel tools, but the gentle touches of air and

[006-353]water working at their leisure with a liberal allowance of time.

[006-354]Not only have some of these basins been forming for countless ages, but

[006-355]others exist which must have been completed in a former geological

[006-356]period. In deepening the Pawtucket Canal, in 1822, the workmen came to

[006-357]ledges with pot-holes in them, where probably was once the bed of the

[006-358]river, and there are some, we are told, in the town of Canaan in this

[006-359]State, with the stones still in them, on the height of land between the

[006-360]Merrimack and Connecticut, and nearly a thousand feet above these

[006-361]rivers, proving that the mountains and the rivers have changed places.

[006-362]There lie the stones which completed their revolutions perhaps before
