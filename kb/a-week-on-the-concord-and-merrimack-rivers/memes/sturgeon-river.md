## Description

"Sturgeon River" is an alternate name for the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], recorded by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] in his opening geographical survey: "The MERRIMACK, or Sturgeon River, is formed by the confluence of the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset]] and Winnipesaukee rivers." (003-1115) The name derives from the Atlantic sturgeon, a large anadromous fish that [[history|historical]]ly migrated up the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] to spawn.

The brothers were re[[thought|mind]]ed of this alternate name during their journey when they were "tempted to pursue what seemed a sturgeon or larger fish, for we remembered that this was the Sturgeon River," suggesting the name still carried local currency in the 1830s. (003-1925) (003-1926)

## On the River Route

The name "Sturgeon River" accompanies the brothers throughout their journey on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. The Sunday chapter, which opens with the geographical survey, establishes the alternate name early. (003-1115) Later in the same chapter, while passing fishing spots along the river, the brothers recall the sturgeon association and are momentarily deceived by the appearance of a large fish—a living re[[thought|mind]]er of the name's origin. (003-1925) (003-1926)

## Historical Context

Atlantic sturgeon were once abundant in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], with individuals reaching lengths of ten feet or more. Their presence was significant enough to give the river its alternate name. By the mid-19th century, however, dam construction at [[lawrence|Lawrence]] and [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] had begun to block the sturgeon's spawning runs, and the population declined sharply.

The name "Sturgeon River" reflects a pre-industrial understanding of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] defined by its ecological character rather than by the manufacturing towns that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed transforming its banks. The river that "now resounds with the din of a manufacturing town" was once known primarily for the great fish that swam its waters. (003-1925)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s use of the name "Sturgeon River" is both a factual notation and an elegy. By recording the alternate name in his opening survey, he preserves a layer of ecological meaning that was already fading under the pressure of industrialization. The anecdote of the deceptive fish—"we remembered that this was the Sturgeon River"—captures the tension between expectation and reality that runs through the journey: the river remembered in names and stories is not always the river encountered. (003-1925) (003-1926)
