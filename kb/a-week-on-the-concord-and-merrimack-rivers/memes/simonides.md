## Biography

Simonides of Ceos (c. 556–468 BCE) was a Greek lyric poet renowned as one of the nine canonical lyric poets of ancient Greece. Born on the Aegean island of Ceos, he spent much of his career in Athens under the patronage of the Pisistratids and later in Thessaly. He was celebrated in [[history|antiquity]] for his elegies, victory odes, and epigrams — including the famous epitaph for the Spartans at Thermopylae. His nephew Bacchylides was also a noted lyric poet, and Simonides was said to have been the first poet to compose odes for pay, pioneering the professionalization of poetic craft.

## Works / Significance

Simonides was a master of the epigram and the threnody (funeral lament). His epigram on the Spartan dead at Thermopylae — "Go tell the Spartans, stranger passing by, that here obedient to their laws we lie" — remains one of the most enduring epitaphs in Western literature. He was credited in [[history|antiquity]] with inventing the art of memory (the "memory palace" technique) and with adding letters to the Greek alphabet. His poetry, though now largely lost, was admired for its emotional restraint, clarity, and moral seriousness — qualities that distinguish him from the more passionate [[pindar|Pindar]].

## In Thoreau's Text

Simonides appears twice in *A Week*, both times in the *Thursday* chapter, amid a long [[thought|reflection]] on Greek lyric poetry sparked by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s reading of the *Greek Minor Poets*.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Simonides's epigram on the poet [[anacreon|Anacreon]]:

> _Simonides' Epigram on Anacreon._ (005-1269)

The epigram, which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] leaves untranslated, celebrates [[anacreon|Anacreon]]'s lyre — the idea that his music continues even in death. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses the Greek text as a touchstone for his meditation on the immortality of [[music|song]]: ancient poets whose work survives across millennia still speak with living voices.

Later in the same meditation, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] returns to Simonides while pondering why lyric poetry has not flourished in modern times. The ancients had the advantage of singing only "light subjects":

> …only light subjects, and which Simonides tells us does not sleep in Hades. (005-1296)

The passage connects Simonides's claim that [[anacreon|Anacreon]]'s lyre "sleeps not in Hades" with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s broader argument that the lyric mode — intimate, occasional, unburdened by philosophical weight — may be the most enduring form of poetry. Simonides serves as an ancient witness to the idea that [[music|song]] outlasts the singer.

The wider passage (005-1267 through 005-1297) in which Simonides appears is one of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s most sustained engagements with Greek lyric poetry, placing Simonides alongside [[anacreon|Anacreon]] and the other *Greek Minor Poets* as exemplars of a tradition [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] deeply admired.
