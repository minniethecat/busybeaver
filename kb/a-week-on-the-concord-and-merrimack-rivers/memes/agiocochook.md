## Description

Agiocochook is the Abenaki name for the highest peak in the northeastern United States, now called [[mount-washington|Mount Washington]]. Rising to 6,288 feet in the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] of New Hampshire, it is known for its extreme weather and expansive views. The name Agiocochook comes from the Abenaki language, meaning "home of the Great Spirit" or "place of the Great Spirit."

## On the River Route

Agiocochook lies more than sixty miles north of the brothers' river route, but the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] are referenced in "A Week" as the source region of the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset River]], which rises "near the Notch of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]]." (003-1116) The distant mountains form the northern horizon of the journey's [[nature|landscape]].

## Historical Context

Agiocochook was considered a [[religion|sacred]] mountain by the Abenaki people, who gave it its name. It was later renamed [[mount-washington|Mount Washington]] in 1784. The mountain and the surrounding [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] were explored by early European settlers and became a destination for naturalists, artists, and tourists in the 19th century.

## Thoreau's Observations

Though [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] did not climb Agiocochook on his 1839 journey, the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] appear as a distant reference point. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset]] rising "near the Notch of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]]" (003-1116) and describes the river's course through "many a [[history|past]]ured Pelion and Ossa." (003-1132) The mountains symbolize the wild, untamed northern [[nature|landscape]].
