## Biography

Sir Walter Raleigh (c. 1552–1618) was an English statesman, soldier, explorer, and writer. A favorite of Queen Elizabeth I, he led expeditions to the New World, sponsored the ill-fated Roanoke Colony, and popularized tobacco in England. His fortunes re[[poetry|verse]]d under James I, who imprisoned him in the Tower of London for over a decade. During his imprisonment, Raleigh wrote his monumental *[[history|History]] of the World*. He was executed in 1618 after a failed expedition to Guiana.

Raleigh embodied the Renaissance ideal of the versatile courtier: soldier, sailor, poet, historian, and adventurer. His life oscillated between royal favor and disgrace, and his death on the scaffold became one of the defining moments of Jacobean England.

## Works / Significance

Raleigh's principal literary work is the *[[history|History]] of the World* (1614), written during his long imprisonment in the Tower. The work covers ancient history from Creation to the second century BCE, and is notable for its somber [[thought|reflection]]s on fortune, power, and human mortality. Raleigh also wrote poetry, including "The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd" (a response to Marlowe's "The Passionate Shepherd"), and accounts of his voyages to Guiana.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in Raleigh stems from his combination of active life and contemplative thought — the explorer who also meditated on the rise and fall of empires.

## In Thoreau's Text

Raleigh appears in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* as a figure of heroic stature. In "Sunday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that "Sir Walter Raleigh might well be studied if only" for the richness of his experience and the breadth of his learning. (003-1660) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] presents Raleigh as a model of the well-stocked [[thought|mind]] — a man whose life of action was matched by depth of [[thought|reflection]]:

> Thoreau imagines "a design, when, having stored Raleigh so richly with the" materials of experience and study, Providence created a figure capable of both exploration and contemplation. (003-1693)

In "Thursday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes a saying attributed to Raleigh about the Romans, drawing on the explorer's sense of [[history|historical]] perspective. (007-690) And in "Friday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Raleigh as a point of contrast, noting that unlike certain other writers, Raleigh was "heroic" in character — a quality [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] admires but distinguishes from the [[silence|quiet]]er [[virtue|virtues]] of poets like Chaucer. (008-1036)

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Raleigh represents a type of active greatness — the life lived at full intensity, accumulating experience that becomes the raw material of wisdom.
