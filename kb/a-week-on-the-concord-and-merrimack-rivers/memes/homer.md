# Homer

Homer (~800–701 BCE), the legendary Greek epic poet, stands at the summit of [[poetry|Thoreau's poetic pantheon]].

Across *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Homer more frequently than any other ancient poet.

He invokes Homer not as a distant classic, but as a living exemplar of poetry's highest aspiration: to be as "serene as nature" itself.

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Homer represents a standard against which all later poets — [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]], [[milton|Milton]], even [[nature|Nature]] — must be measured.

## Biography

Homer is the presumed author of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey*, the two epic poems that stand at the fountainhead of the Western literary tradition.

Though almost nothing certain is known of his life, tradition places him in the 8th century BCE, living in Ionia on the coast of Asia Minor.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] accepted Homer as a [[history|historical]] poet, but what mattered to him was not biographical detail but the **quality of [[dream|vision]]** he found in the Homeric poems.

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Homer was the poet who achieved the most complete fusion of human consciousness with the [[nature|natural world]].

## Works / Significance

Homer's two epics defined the shape of Western literature:

| [[labor|Work]] | Subject | Significance in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[books|Reading]] |
|------|---------|----------------------------------|
| **Iliad** | The wrath of Achilles in the Trojan War | Heroic energy, the "morning" of Western consciousness |
| **Odyssey** | Odysseus's ten-year journey home | The archetypal journey narrative; parallels [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own river voyage |

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] read Homer in Greek and quotes him throughout the journals that fed into *A Week*.

He regarded the Homeric poems as possessing a freshness and immediacy that later, more self-conscious poetry had lost.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s engagement with Homer in *A Week* moves through several registers — from direct quotation to metaphorical invocation to critical judgment.

### Homer as Nature's Poet

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s deepest admiration for Homer rests on the perception that Homeric poetry shares the serenity and impersonality of nature itself:

> It is enough if Homer but say the sun sets. He is as serene as nature, and we can hardly detect the enthusiasm of the bard. (003-1338)

This is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s highest praise. Homer does not need to strain for effect; like the sunset, his words simply *are*.

### Homer and the Question of Originality

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] raises the unsettling possibility that even Homer was not original:

> "Homer is gone; and where is Jove? and where The rival cities seven? His song outlives Time, tower, and god,—all that then was, save Heaven." (003-1421)

> So too, no doubt, Homer had his Homer, and Orpheus his Orpheus, in the dim antiquity which preceded them. (003-1424)

This recognition — that Homer himself had precursors — does not diminish Homer in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s eyes.

Rather, it places all human creativity within a continuous chain, where "man is the great poet, and not Homer nor [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]" (003-1430).

### Homer and the Limits of Tradition

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] is not uncritical. He recognizes that Homer belongs to a specific cultural tradition:

> It is only white man's poetry. Homer and Ossian even can never revive in London or Boston. (003-369)

This passage reveals [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s characteristic ambivalence: he reveres Homer while also seeing the limits of the Western poetic tradition.

### Homer in the Poet's Pantheon

In the later chapters, Homer recurs as a touchstone in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s running meditation on poetry and mortality.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes his own earlier [[poetry|verse]], embedding Homer within the living fabric of the journey:

> Plutarch was good, and so was Homer too... (007-075)

> Bid Homer wait till I the issue learn... (007-083)

By the journey's end, Homer has become for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] a figure for the entire tradition:

> Homer and Shakespeare and Milton and Marvell and Wordsworth are but the rustling of leaves... (007-265)

The image is characteristically [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]vian: the greatest poets are reduced — or rather, elevated — to the sound of wind through trees.
