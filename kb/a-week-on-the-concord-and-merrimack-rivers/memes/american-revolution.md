# American Revolution

## Overview

The American Revolutionary War (1775–1783), in which the Thirteen
Colonies won independence from Great Britain. The [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[lexington|Lexington]]
battles of April 1775 are directly referenced in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes the Revolution not merely as a [[history|historical]] event but as
a living presence in the [[nature|landscape]] and consciousness of New England.
It serves as a touchstone for his [[thought|reflection]]s on liberty, destiny,
and the moral character of the American people (001-120).

## Relevance to the Journey

The [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] itself was the stage for the opening act of the
Revolution. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the significance of the North Bridge in
[[concord-mass|Concord]], where the "shot heard round the world" was fired, and reflects
on how the river's once-peaceful banks witnessed the birth of a
nation (001-120).

Passing the sites of the conflict, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] considers the gap between
the heroic ideals of the Revolution and the mundane realities of the
present (002-054). The farmer guiding his plow over fields once soaked
with patriot blood becomes a symbol of how revolution recedes into the
rhythms of everyday life (002-099).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also reflects on the broader implications of the Revolution,
connecting the struggle for political independence with the individual's
struggle for spiritual and intellectual self-reliance (002-538).

In the Wednesday chapter, the Revolution serves as a point of departure
for meditations on truth and poetry: the revolutionary moment, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]
suggests, is one in which a people speaks with its most authentic
voice (003-1102).

Later, noting the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s role as a corridor for military movements,
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes how the same waters that carried revolutionary armies
now carry commerce and leisure (006-498, 006-503).

## Historical Background

The American Revolution began with the [[revolutionary-battles|Battles of Lexington and Concord]]
on April 19, 1775, when British troops attempted to seize colonial
military supplies stored at [[concord-mass|Concord]]. The colonial victory that day
marked the beginning of open armed conflict.

Over the next eight years, the war expanded into a global struggle
involving France, Spain, and the Netherlands. The Revolution concluded
with the Treaty of Paris in 1783, establishing the United States as an
independent nation.

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], writing in the 1840s, the Revolution was recent enough to
be within living memory of older residents yet distant enough to have
acquired the patina of legend. He exploits this dual perspective
throughout *A Week*.
