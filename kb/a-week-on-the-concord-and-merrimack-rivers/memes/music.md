# Music

## Definition

Music, for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], is both a literal presence on [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]] — the sounds of wind, water, birdsong — and a metaphor for the highest mode of perception. "We seem to hear the music of a thought, and care not if the understanding be not gratified," he writes, using music to describe a kind of knowing that bypasses the discursive intellect and speaks directly to the soul. (003-429)

This "music of a thought" is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s most powerful image for the union of form and content, beauty and truth. Music, unlike argument, does not proceed by logical steps but by direct intuition — and this, for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], is the model for all genuine understanding.

## Across the Journey

**Saturday** introduces music through natural sound — the river's murmur, the wind in the reeds, the rhythm of oars.

**Sunday** develops music as a philosophical metaphor. The "music of a thought" that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] hears in Greek myth is a kind of knowledge that operates through beauty rather than demonstration. (003-429) The fables of Narcissus, Endymion, and Pan are musical in their capacity to convey meaning without argument.

**Monday** contrasts the music of Eastern [[religion|scripture]] — the "pure intellectuality" of the *Bhagavad Gita* — with the relative silence of Western [[religion|religious]] discourse.

**Thursday** explores the relationship between music and poetry. Both are rhythmic, both operate through pattern and variation, and both aim at the "music of a thought" that is the highest achievement of language.

**Friday** returns to music as the ultimate metaphor for cosmic order. The river's flow, the farmer's labor, the poet's [[poetry|verse]] — all are, in their way, musical.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s musical metaphor draws on a long tradition — from [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]]'s "music of the spheres" through the Romantic elevation of music as the highest art form. For German Romantics like E.T.A. Hoffmann, music was "the most romantic of all the [[art|arts]]" because it bypassed representation and spoke directly to the infinite. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s "music of a thought" is a characteristically American, democratic version of this Romantic ideal: available not in the concert hall but on a river in New England.
