## Description

Wamesit was a significant Native American settlement located at the confluence of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] rivers, where the [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]] provided an abundant fishing ground. The 17th-century missionary [[gookin|Daniel Gookin]] described it as "an ancient and capital seat of Indians" where the native people gathered to fish. (003-1037) The location later became the city of [[lowell-massachusetts|Lowell, Massachusetts]], the great textile-manufacturing center. As [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed, "Pawtucket and Wamesit, where the Indians resorted in the fishing season, are now [[lowell-mass|Lowell]], the city of spindles and [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] of America, which sends its cotton cloth round the globe." (003-1104)

## On the River Route

The brothers passed the site of Wamesit on their outward journey along the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] rivers. The [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]] at the confluence marked a natural gathering place that had drawn people for centuries — first Native Americans to fish, then colonial settlers, and finally the industrial city of [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] with its canals and mills. Having grown up partly in the village of [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] knew this territory well; he noted that even the youthful voyagers "had spent a part of our lives in the village of [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]], when the present city, whose bells we heard, was its obscure north district." (003-1104)

## Historical Context

In the 1670s, Wamesit became a site of missionary activity. The Puritan missionary [[john-eliot|John Eliot]], known as the "[[john-eliot|Apostle to the Indians]]," traveled to Wamesit with [[gookin|Daniel Gookin]] on May 5, 1674, to preach the gospel to the gathered Indians. [[gookin|Gookin]] recorded that "according to our usual custom, Mr. [[john-eliot|Eliot]] and myself took our journey to Wamesit, or Pawtuckett; and arriving there that evening, Mr. [[john-eliot|Eliot]] preached to as many of them as could be got together." (003-1041) [[john-eliot|Eliot]] brought with him the Bible, a Catechism, and other [[religion|religious]] tracts translated into the Massachusetts tongue, seeking — in [[gookin|Gookin]]'s words — to "spread the net of the gospel, to fish for their souls." (003-1037)

[[gookin|Gookin]] also recorded a striking testimony of one Wamesit sachem who, after his conversion, "sanctifieth the Sabbath, though he doth travel to Wamesit meeting every Sabbath, which is above two miles; and though sundry of his people have deserted him, since he subjected to the gospel, yet he continues and persists." (003-1067)

The frontier was violent as well. In 1670, a Mohawk warrior scalped a Naamkeak or Wamesit Indian maiden near where [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] now stands, though she survived the attack. (005-1119) The incident underscores the territorial conflicts that played out across this region in the 17th century.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflected on the dramatic transformation of Wamesit from a Native American fishing ground to an industrial city. He contrasted the two worlds: the ancient seat where Indians once gathered for their seasonal fisheries, and the modern [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] filled with spindles and cotton mills. This juxtaposition of pre-colonial and industrial America was a recurring theme in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s writing — the sense that the [[nature|landscape]] was layered with prior lives and cultures now largely invisible to the casual observer. (003-1104)
