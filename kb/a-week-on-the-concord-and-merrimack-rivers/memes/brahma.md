## Biography

Brahma is the Hindu god of [[art|creation]] and the first member of the Trimurti (trinity), alongside Vishnu the preserver and Shiva the destroyer.
In Hindu cosmology, Brahma is the creator of the uni[[poetry|verse]] and all beings, born from a lotus emerging from the navel of Vishnu.
The Brahmanical tradition, rooted in the Vedas, represents one of the world's oldest continuous spiritual and philosophical systems.
The concept of Brahman — the ultimate, unchanging reality — is central to Hindu thought and is distinct from the personified deity Brahma, though [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses the terms somewhat interchangeably in his [[thought|reflection]]s on Eastern spirituality.

## Works / Significance

Brahma is primarily known through the Vedas and the Laws of [[menu|Menu]] (*[[menu|Manu]]smṛti*),
a foundational Hindu legal and ethical text said to have been taught by Brahma himself to [[menu|Manu]], the progenitor of humanity.
The Brahmanical tradition emphasizes spiritual discipline, austerity, and the pursuit of absorption into the divine essence.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] engages with this tradition in *A Week*, referencing both Brahma as deity and the Brahmans as spiritual practitioners,
finding in their contemplative rigor a parallel to his own transcendentalist ideals.

## In Thoreau's Text

Brahma and the Brahmanical tradition appear predominantly in the "Monday" chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Hindu cosmology to frame his [[thought|reflection]]s on time, spirituality, and the eternal.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes "absorption in Brahma" as the ultimate goal of Hindu speculation (004-489), noting the Brahmans' contemplative discipline.
He references the Laws of [[menu|Menu]] as a divinely revealed text, noting that [[menu|Menu]] was "son or grandson of Brahma" (004-823) and that Brahma taught his laws (004-824) in a hundred thousand [[poetry|verse]]s.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] admires the Brahmans' austerity, remarking on "the very austerity of the Brahmans" (004-943) as a mark of sincere spiritual seeking.
He also invokes the cosmic "day of Brahma" (004-957), which "endures to the end of a thousand such ages" — an immense [[time|temporal]] scale that dwarfs mortal reckoning.
In the final chapter, Brahma appears as part of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s panoramic sweep through world civilizations, "through Babylon and Thebes, Brahma" (007-709), positioning Hindu thought within the grand arc of human spiritual history.
