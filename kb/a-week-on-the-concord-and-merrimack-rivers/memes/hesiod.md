## Biography

Hesiod (fl. c. 700 BCE) is one of the earliest Greek poets whose work survives. A native of Boeotia, he composed two major poems: *Theogony*, a genealogy of the gods, and *[[labor|Work]]s and Days*, a didactic poem blending agricultural wisdom with moral [[thought|reflection]].

Alongside [[homer|Homer]], Hesiod stands at the head of the Western poetic tradition. His voice is more personal and grounded in everyday rural life than the heroic mode of [[homer|Homer]].

## Works / Significance

*[[labor|Work]]s and Days* is notable for its intimate portrayal of archaic Greek life and its explicit moral voice. Hesiod introduces the concept of the Five Ages of Man and articulates a philosophy of labor and justice. His di[[dream|vision]] of the month into [[religion|sacred]] and profane days — including the seventh day as holy — entered Greek religion.

## In Thoreau's Text

Hesiod is referenced twice in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, appearing as both a [[religion|religious]] authority and a literary progenitor.

In "Sunday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws on the testimony of Hesiod for the ancient sanctity of the seventh day: "According to Hesiod, 'The seventh is a holy day, / For then Latona brought forth golden-rayed Apollo'" (003-562). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites this to show that the concept of a sabbath rest predates [[religion|Christianity]].

In "Tuesday," Hesiod is placed in the great river of classical poetry: "That highway down from [[homer|Homer]] and Hesiod to [[horace|Horace]] and [[juvenal|Juvenal]] is more attractive than the Appian" (005-1284). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] contrasts the physical infrastructure of empire with the enduring legacy of literature.
