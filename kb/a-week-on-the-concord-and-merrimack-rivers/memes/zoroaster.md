## Biography

Zoroaster (Zarathustra in Avestan, Zoroaster in Greek) was an ancient Persian prophet who founded Zoroastrianism, one of the world's oldest continuously practiced religions.
The precise dates of his life are uncertain, with scholarly estimates ranging from 1500 to 1000 BCE.

He is believed to have been born in what is now northeastern Iran or Afghanistan, into a priestly family of the ancient Iranian religion.
Zoroaster's central revelation was the duality of good and evil, personified by Ahura Mazda (the Wise Lord) and Angra Mainyu (the Destructive Spirit).

Humanity is called to choose righteousness (*asha*) in this cosmic struggle between truth and falsehood.

## Works / Significance

Zoroaster's teachings are preserved in the *Avesta*, particularly in the *Gathas* — seventeen hymns believed to have been composed by Zoroaster himself.
The *Gathas* articulate a monotheistic [[dream|vision]] centered on Ahura Mazda as supreme creator, emphasizing good thoughts, good words, and good deeds.

Zoroastrianism influenced later Abrahamic religions through its concepts of heaven and hell, final judgment, and cosmic dualism.
The "Oracles of Zoroaster" (the *Chaldean Oracles*) were Neoplatonic texts erroneously attributed to Zoroaster, through which many Western thinkers — including [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] — encountered his name.

## In Thoreau's Text

Zoroaster appears at multiple points in *A Week* as one of the great spiritual teachers of [[history|antiquity]], alongside figures of Hinduism, Confucianism, and [[religion|Christianity]].
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] first references "the oracles of Zoroaster, still surviving after a thousand" years (004-701), noting the endurance of ancient Persian wisdom.

In "Monday," he places Zoroaster alongside [[confucius|Confucius]] as a world teacher (004-937), illustrating the universality of genuine spiritual [[wisdom|insight]] across cultures.
He later invokes an impulse reaching "further back than Zoroaster each instant" (007-364), using the prophet's name to signify the deepest [[history|antiquity]] of human spiritual striving.

In the concluding chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] assembles the great teachers: "Veias, [[menu|Menu]], Zoroaster, [[socrates|Socrates]], [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (008-1415), positioning Zoroaster among a handful of figures who have shaped human moral consciousness across millennia.
