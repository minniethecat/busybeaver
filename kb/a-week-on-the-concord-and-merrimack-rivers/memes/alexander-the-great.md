## Biography

Alexander III of Macedon (356–323 BCE), known as Alexander the Great, was a king of the ancient Greek kingdom of Macedon. Tutored by [[aristotle|Aristotle]] until the age of 16, he succeeded his father Philip II to the throne in 336 BCE and went on to create one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece to northwestern India. He was undefeated in battle and is considered one of history's greatest military commanders.

Alexander died in Babylon at the age of 32, his empire quickly fragmenting among his generals. His legacy includes the spread of Greek culture throughout the Near East and into Asia, a process known as Hellenization, which profoundly shaped the development of Western and Middle Eastern civilizations.

## Works / Significance

Alexander's significance lies not in written works but in his military campaigns and their cultural consequences. His conquests opened trade routes, founded cities (most notably Alexandria in Egypt), and disseminated Greek language, philosophy, and art across three continents. The accounts of his campaigns, written by Arrian, [[plutarch|Plutarch]], and others, became classical models of military history and biography.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in Alexander is characteristically ambivalent: he acknowledges the conqueror's greatness while questioning the value of martial ambition measured against the [[silence|quiet]]er achievements of poets and philosophers.

## In Thoreau's Text

Alexander appears in *A Week* in contexts that subtly question the value of conquest. In "Monday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "Alexander, Onesicritus was sent forward" — a reference to the explorer Onesicritus, whom Alexander dispatched to scout ahead during his Indian campaign. (004-394) The detail emphasizes Alexander's restless forward momentum, his need always to see what lay beyond.

In "Tuesday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes "Alexander Henry's Adventures here, as a" point of reference for the travelers' own experience navigating the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (005-1060) The juxtaposition of Alexander the Great with Alexander Henry — a fur trader and explorer of the American frontier — [[silence|quiet]]ly suggests that the spirit of discovery operates at multiple scales, and that the exploration of one's own river may be as meaningful as the conquest of empires.

In "Wednesday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes that "in us, and _there_ is room for Alexander" — a meditation on the inner space that ambition occupies in the human soul. (006-1584) The passage suggests that the impulse to conquer is not merely [[history|historical]] but psychological: each person contains within themselves the potential for Alexander's ambition, for better or worse.
