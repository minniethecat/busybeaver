# French and Indian War

## Overview

The North American theater of the Seven Years' War (1754–1763) between
France and Great Britain. Its frontier conflicts and ranger traditions
are invoked in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative.

The war determined which European power would dominate North America.
Its aftermath — the expulsion of France from the continent — reshaped
the political geography of the rivers [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] traveled (007-168).

## Relevance to the Journey

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references the French and Indian War primarily through its
connection to the military history of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley and the
traditions of Rogers' Rangers, the provincial irregular forces that
operated along the frontier.

The same woodlands and waterways that the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers navigate
in peace were once corridors of military movement: French raiding
parties descending from Canada, British columns advancing up the river
valleys, Native scouts guiding both sides (007-168).

In the Wednesday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws a parallel between the colonial
soldiers who fought in these frontier wars and the [[silence|quiet]] heroism of
the farmers and laborers he encounters along the river (006-502).

The war also figures in his meditation on the passage of time: the
forts and blockhouses that once guarded the frontier have crumbled,
but the [[nature|landscape]] itself retains the memory of conflict (003-1858).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes how the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s islands and bends served as strategic
points during the war, and how the names of French commanders and
battles linger in local place-names (004-1328).

The connection to [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]] is also underscored: the same
frontier dynamics that produced [[love|Love]]well's expedition in 1725 continued
through the French and Indian War, forming a continuous chain of
colonial violence (004-051).

## Historical Background

The French and Indian War (1754–1763) was the North American front of
the global Seven Years' War. It began over disputed territory in the
Ohio Valley and escalated into a continent-wide struggle.

British forces, initially suffering a series of defeats under General
Edward Braddock, eventually prevailed after the capture of Quebec in
1759 and Montreal in 1760. The Treaty of Paris (1763) ceded Canada and
all French territory east of the Mississippi to Britain.

The war also imposed a massive debt on the British Crown, leading
directly to the taxation policies that would spark the American
Revolution barely a decade later.
