## Biography

John Josselyn (fl. 1638–1675) was an English gentleman, traveler, and amateur naturalist who made two voyages to New England in the seventeenth century. His first visit, in 1638–1639, brought him to the Massachusetts Bay Colony where his brother was a settler.

He returned for a longer stay from 1663 to 1671, during which he compiled detailed observations of the region's flora, fauna, and indigenous peoples. His two published works — *New England's Rarities Discovered* (1672) and *An Account of Two Voyages to New-England* (1674) — are among the earliest natural-history accounts of the region.

## Works / Significance

*New England's Rarities* is a pioneering work of American natural history, cataloging the plants, animals, fish, and minerals of the colony, as well as remedies used by Native Americans. Josselyn's writing blends practical observation with folklore and hearsay, making it a vivid if not always reliable portrait of seventeenth-century New England.

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Josselyn represents a crucial link in the chain of New England writing — a precursor to the tradition of local natural observation that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] himself would bring to its fullest expression two centuries later.

## In Thoreau's Text

Josselyn is cited twice in the *Saturday* chapter, both times as an authority on the fish of New England waters.

In the first instance, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Josselyn while describing the shiner, a small fish common in the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]:

> Old Josselyn in his "New England's Rarities," published in 1672, mentions the Perch or River… (002-393)

The passage situates Josselyn as a [[history|historical]] baseline: the fish Josselyn observed in the 1670s are the same species [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] catches and examines two centuries later.

Later in the same chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] turns to the pickerel:

> [The Pickerel], the swiftest, wariest, and most ravenous of fishes, which Josselyn calls the Fresh-Water or River Wolf… (002-441)

Josselyn's evocative name for the pickerel — "the Fresh-Water or River Wolf" — appeals to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s sense of wildness. The pickerel is not merely a fish but a predator, and Josselyn's name captures exactly this quality.

Josselyn's presence in *A Week* is brief but significant: he stands as the earliest English-language naturalist [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] can cite for his own neighborhood waters.
