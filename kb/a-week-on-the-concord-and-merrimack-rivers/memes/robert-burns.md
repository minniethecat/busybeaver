## Biography

Robert Burns (1759–1796) was a Scottish poet and lyricist widely regarded as the national poet of Scotland. Born in Alloway, Ayrshire, to a tenant-farmer father, he received an irregular education but read widely in English literature while working the land.

His first collection, *Poems, Chiefly in the Scottish Dialect* (1786) — the "Kilmarnock Edition" — brought him immediate fame. Burns spent his final years collecting and adapting traditional Scottish folk [[music|song]]s, preserving hundreds of lyrics that might otherwise have been lost.

## Works / Significance

Burns's best-known poems include "To a Mouse," "Tam o' Shanter," "A Red, Red Rose," and "Auld Lang Syne." He wrote in a distinctive blend of Scots dialect and standard English, fusing folk traditions with the sensibility of the Enlightenment.

His influence extended to the Romantic poets — [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] and Keats admired him — and his birthday is celebrated worldwide with Burns Suppers. The democratic sympathy of poems like "A Man's a Man for A' That" made him a hero to reformers and radicals.

## In Thoreau's Text

Burns appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. In one of the book's most concentrated meditations on poetic genius, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] names Burns as a vessel for a universal [[imagination|creative]] principle (008-1120):

> in Shakespeare, Alpheus, in Burns, Arethuse; but ever the same.

The allusion is to the Greek myth of the river Alpheus, which flows underground and beneath the sea to emerge as the fountain Arethuse in Sicily — the same water, different names. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s metaphor is profound: poetic genius is one continuous current, flowing now through [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]], now through Burns, unaltered in essence. Burns, the rustic Scottish [[poetry|bard]], is placed in direct continuity with the greatest English poet.
