John Eliot was a Puritan minister and missionary who emigrated from England to the Massachusetts Bay Colony in 1631. He is best remembered for his decades of missionary work among the Native American peoples of New England, earning him the title "Apostle to the Indians." His most enduring achievement was the translation of the entire Bible into the Massachusett language (the *Eliot Indian Bible*, published 1663), the first complete Bible printed in the Americas.

## Works / Significance

Eliot's missionary enterprise included the establishment of "praying towns" — settlements where converted Native Americans could live in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities while maintaining some degree of autonomy. He produced not only the Algonquian Bible but also catechisms and [[religion|religious]] tracts in the Massachusett language, including Baxter's *Call to the Unconverted*.

His work represents one of the most ambitious cross-cultural [[religion|religious]] projects of colonial New England, and his linguistic achievements provided one of the earliest systematic records of an Algonquian language.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encounters John Eliot in the Sunday chapter through the writings of [[gookin|Daniel Gookin]], who accompanied Eliot on his missionary journeys along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]].

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the [[history|historical]] presence at the river's falls: "here also came John Eliot, with the Bible and Catechism, and Baxter's Call to the Unconverted, and other tracts." (003-1034) (003-1035) The specificity of the book list — Bible, catechism, Baxter's treatise — conveys the Puritan missionary's arsenal as he approached the Native peoples.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts [[gookin|Gookin]]'s record of a missionary journey: "our usual custom, Mr. Eliot and myself took our journey to [[wamesit|Wamesit]], or Pawtuckett; and arriving there that evening, Mr. Eliot preached to as many of them as could be got together, out of Matt. xxii. 1-14." (003-1041) (003-1042) Eliot chose the parable of the wedding feast — a narrative of universal invitation — as his text for preaching to the Pawtucket people.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also records a speech by a Native sachem who, after hearing Eliot preach, "desired brother Eliot to tell the sachem from him, that it may be, while he went in his old canoe, he passed in a [[silence|quiet]] stream." (003-1060) (003-1061) This image — the sachem navigating the [[silence|quiet]] stream of his traditional canoe while the missionary brings a new spiritual current — captures with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]vian sympathy the confluence of cultures along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]].
