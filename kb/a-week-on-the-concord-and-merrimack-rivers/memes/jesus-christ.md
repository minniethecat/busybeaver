## Biography

Jesus of Nazareth, known to his followers as the Christ ("the Anointed One"), was a first-century Jewish preacher and [[religion|religious]] leader whose life and teachings became the foundation of [[religion|Christianity]]. Born in Bethlehem and raised in Nazareth in the Roman province of Judea, he began his public ministry around the age of thirty, gathering disciples and teaching through parables and sermons. He was crucified by the Roman authorities in Jerusalem around 30 CE. His followers proclaimed his resurrection, and the movement that grew around his teachings spread throughout the Roman Empire and eventually the world.

## Works / Significance

Jesus left no writings of his own; his teachings are preserved in the four canonical Gospels of the New Testament (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John), written decades after his death. His moral teachings — centered on love, forgiveness, and the coming Kingdom of God — have shaped Western civilization, philosophy, law, and art for two millennia. The religion founded in his name is the world's largest.

## In Thoreau's Text

Jesus Christ and [[religion|Christianity]] receive sustained attention in the "Sunday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] meditates on [[religion|religious]] truth while resting on the riverbank. He opens with a [[thought|reflection]] on the story of Christ as part of universal history (003-651):

> Such a story as that of Jesus Christ,—the history of Jerusalem, say, being a part of the Universal History.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] marvels at [[religion|Christianity]]'s endurance across time and space, but his tone carries a critical edge toward organized religion (003-655):

> What are time and space to Christianity, eighteen hundred years, and a new world?—that the humble life of a Jewish peasant should have force to make a New York bishop so bigoted.

He insists that spiritual appreciation does not require formal affiliation (003-668):

> It is necessary not to be Christian to appreciate the beauty and significance of the life of Christ.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s most striking move is to place Christ alongside [[buddha|Buddha]], asserting that the love behind [[love|devotion]] matters more than the object of [[love|devotion]] (003-670):

> I know that some will have hard thoughts of me, when they hear their Christ named beside my Buddha, yet I am sure that I am willing they should love their Christ more than my Buddha, for the love is the main thing, and I like him too.

Later in the same chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] returns to his critique of [[religion|religious]] conformity, suggesting that even Christ was constrained by tradition (003-745):

> Examine your authority. Even Christ, we fear, had his scheme, his conformity to tradition, which slightly vitiates his teaching. He had not swallowed all formulas. He preached some mere doctrines.

Throughout these passages, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws a distinction between the beauty of Christ's life and the dogmatism of the institutional church — a characteristically Transcendentalist position that values direct spiritual experience over doctrine.
