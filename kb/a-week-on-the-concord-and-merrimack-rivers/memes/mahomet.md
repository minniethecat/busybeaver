## Biography

Muhammad (c. 570–632 CE), known in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time as Mahomet, was the founder of Islam and is regarded by Muslims as the last prophet sent by God. Born in Mecca in the Arabian Peninsula, he received his first revelation at the age of 40 and spent the remainder of his life preaching monotheism and establishing the [[religion|religious]] and political foundations of the Islamic community. The Quran, Islam's [[religion|sacred]] [[religion|scripture]], is believed by Muslims to be the word of God as revealed to Muhammad.

Within a century of his death, Islam had spread from Arabia across the Middle East, North Africa, and into Spain and Persia, becoming one of the world's major civilizations. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encountered Islam primarily through European translations and Orientalist scholarship.

## In Thoreau's Text

Mahomet appears in *A Week* in a passage from "Sunday" that reflects on the circulation of texts across [[religion|religious]] traditions. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that certain [[religion|sacred]] texts "were read by Mahometans in the market-places of" their cities, observing how [[religion|religious]] ideas travel across cultures. (003-464) He specifically references the dissemination of translated [[religion|scripture]]s among Islamic communities:

> Thoreau writes of "Mahometans, and the same translated" scripture being read publicly, suggesting a shared textual culture that transcends the boundaries between faiths. (003-459)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s treatment of Islam is characteristic of his comparative approach to religion: he is interested in what different traditions share rather than what divides them. The image of [[religion|sacred]] texts being read aloud in marketplaces resonates with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own belief in the public, accessible nature of spiritual truth — a truth not confined to churches or mosques but available wherever people gather to hear wisdom.

The brief references to Mahomet in *A Week* reflect the broader nineteenth-century Transcendentalist interest in world religions, which saw figures like [[confucius|Confucius]], [[brahma|Brahma]], and Muhammad as part of a universal spiritual heritage.
