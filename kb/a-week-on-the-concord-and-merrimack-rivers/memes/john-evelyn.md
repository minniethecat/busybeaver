## Biography

John Evelyn was born in Wotton, Surrey, into a wealthy gentry family. He studied at Balliol College, Oxford, and the Middle Temple. A founding member of the Royal [[civilization|Society]], Evelyn was a central figure in the intellectual life of Restoration England.

He traveled extensively on the Continent during the Civil War period, and after the Restoration served on various public commissions. His *Diary*, kept over sixty-six years, is one of the principal sources for the social, cultural, and political history of seventeenth-century England.

## Works / Significance

Evelyn's most influential work was *Sylva, or a [[conversation|Discourse]] of Forest-Trees* (1664), written to encourage the replanting of England's forests depleted by the navy. It became a foundational text of English forestry and arboriculture.

He also wrote *Acetarium: A [[conversation|Discourse]] of Sallets* (1699), a pioneering work on salad vegetables, and the *Kalendarium Hortense*, a gardener's almanac. His writings embody the Royal [[civilization|Society]]'s Baconian commitment to empirical observation and practical improvement.

## In Thoreau's Text

Evelyn appears in the "Sunday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses his gardening books as examples of civilized cultivation that lacks the vigor of wild nature (003-333):

> We are apt enough to be pleased with such books as Evelyn's Sylva, Acetarium, and Kalendarium Hortense, but they imply a relaxed nerve in the reader. Gardening is civil and social, but it wants the vigor and freedom of the forest and the outlaw. (003-333–336)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s criticism is characteristic: while he acknowledges the appeal of Evelyn's horticultural writings, he finds them tame compared to untamed nature. The passage continues with a warning against "an excess of cultivation," reflecting [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s central conviction that civilization must be balanced by contact with the wild.
