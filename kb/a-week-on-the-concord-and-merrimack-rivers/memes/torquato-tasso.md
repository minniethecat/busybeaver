## Biography

Torquato Tasso (1544–1595) was the greatest Italian poet of the late Renaissance. Born in Sorrento to a noble family, he received a classical education at the courts of Urbino and Ferrara, where he became the court poet to Duke Alfonso II d'Este.

Tasso's life was marked by profound [[imagination|creative]] achievement and deep personal suffering. In the midst of completing his epic *Jerusalem Delivered*, he experienced severe mental disturbance — possibly paranoid schizophrenia — and was confined by the Duke to a hospital for seven years. He escaped, wandered across Italy, and died in Rome days before he was to be crowned poet laureate.

## Works / Significance

*Jerusalem Delivered* (*Gerusalemme Liberata*, 1581) is Tasso's masterpiece: an epic poem in twenty cantos recounting the First Crusade and the capture of Jerusalem by Godfrey of Bouillon. Blending [[history|historical]] narrative with chivalric romance and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian allegory, the poem influenced [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]], Milton, and the development of the European epic tradition.

Tasso also wrote lyric poetry, a [[history|past]]oral drama (*Aminta*), and a treatise on epic theory (*[[conversation|Discourse]]s on the Heroic Poem*). His troubled life and romantic legend inspired Goethe's play *Torquato Tasso* and Byron's poem *The Lament of Tasso*.

## In Thoreau's Text

Tasso appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Sunday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] is meditating on [[religion|Christianity]], mortality, and the embalming power of art when he invokes Tasso (003-654):

> Tasso's poem I trust some things are sweetly buried.

The line follows a [[thought|reflection]] on "the naked, the embalmed, unburied death of Jerusalem amid its desolate hills." [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] turns to Tasso — whose epic concerns the very city of Jerusalem — as a counterpoint: what [[religion|Christianity]] and history leave naked and exposed, poetry can bury "sweetly," transforming violent history into redemptive art. The brief, almost whispered aside reveals [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s deep [[religion|faith]] in the restorative, preserving function of poetry.
