## Overview

Andover is a town in Essex County, Massachusetts, situated on the Shawsheen
River, a tributary of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. Founded in 1646, it is one of the older
interior settlements of Massachusetts and was, in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s day, a farming
community with a modest manufacturing sector.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s sole reference to Andover appears in his account of [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]]
(1725), a celebrated frontier engagement of Dummer's War. He lists the
casualties by their towns, naming Jonathan Frye among the wounded:

> (004-086) fight, Lieutenant Farwell, of Dunstable, and Jonathan Frye, of Andover,
> who were all wounded, were left behind, creeping toward the settlements.
> "After travelling several miles, Frye was left and lost," and the party
> continued their retreat.

Jonathan Frye was a young man from Andover who served as a chaplain's assistant
or soldier in Captain [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]]'s company. His death on the retreat from
Pequawket (present-day Fryeburg, Maine) became part of the lore of the battle,
which was commemorated in ballads and histories that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] knew well.

## Lovewell's Fight Connection

[[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]] (May 8, 1725) was one of the most famous incidents of the
frontier wars between English colonists and the Wabanaki Confederacy. Frye's
death was recounted in the popular ballad "The Song of [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]]," which
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes at length in the Thursday chapter. Frye's connection to Andover
reflected the town's role as a source of militia recruits for frontier
expeditions.

## Geography and Early History

Andover was originally a much larger tract that included what is now North
Andover (the original settlement site). The Shawsheen River, which flows
through the center of town, provided water power for early gristmills and
sawmills. By the mid-nineteenth century, Andover was also known as the home
of Phillips Academy (founded 1778) and Andover Theological Seminary (founded
1807).
