## Biography

Passaconaway (whose name may derive from "Papisse Conewa," meaning "Child of the Bear") was the grand sachem of the [[penacook|Penacook]] Confederacy, a powerful alliance of Algonquian-speaking tribes that dominated the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] valley in present-day New Hampshire and Massachusetts. He was renowned both as a political leader and as a *powwow* (shaman), credited by his people with supernatural powers including the ability to make water burn and trees dance.

Under Passaconaway's leadership, the [[penacook|Penacook]] maintained generally peaceful relations with the English colonists during the early decades of settlement. He reportedly counseled his people to maintain peace with the English, recognizing the inevitability of colonial expansion. He abdicated his authority to his son [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] before his death around 1669.

Passaconaway's legacy extended through his children: [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]], who succeeded him as sachem and maintained the peace policy, and another son whose imprisonment for debt in 1663 led to the sale of [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]] by [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] to secure his release (003-1832).

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Passaconaway in the *Sunday* chapter while recounting the history of [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]] in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. Citing [[gookin|Gookin]]'s *[[history|History]] of [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]]*, he notes that around 1663, Passaconaway's eldest son was imprisoned for a debt of forty-five pounds — a debt [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] satisfied by selling the island. This brief mention exemplifies [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in the complex web of colonial-Indigenous legal and economic relations that shaped the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] [[nature|landscape]] he was traversing.
