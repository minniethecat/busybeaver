## Description

Mount Wachusett is the highest peak in eastern Massachusetts, rising to 2,006 feet in Princeton, Massachusetts. It is the source of the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]], which flows from the mountain through [[lancaster|Lancaster]] toward the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (004-1192) Wachusett is visible from many points along the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] rivers and serves as a landmark for travelers.

## On the River Route

The brothers see Mount Wachusett from the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] valley. The mountain is referenced as a landmark in the western distance, retreating westward from the travelers as they proceed up the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes of "Wachusett, who like me" (004-1288) and notes that "Wachusett and [[monadnock|Monadnock]] have retreated" (004-1323) westward from their native hills.

## Historical Context

Mount Wachusett has been a landmark for Native Americans and later European settlers for centuries. Its name comes from the Algonquian language, possibly meaning "great hill" or "near the mountain." The mountain was a gathering place for the Nipmuc people and later a site of early colonial settlement. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it was a well-known natural landmark.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] personifies Wachusett in his poetic [[thought|reflection]]s, writing "Wachusett, who like me" (004-1288) as part of a meditation on the relationship between the traveler and the [[nature|landscape]]. He groups Wachusett with [[monadnock|Monadnock]] as mountains that have "retreated" from the viewer's perspective as the journey progresses. (004-1323) The [[nashua-river|Nashua River]] rises from its slopes. (004-1192)
