## Description

[[concord-massachusetts|Concord, Massachusetts]], is a historic town twenty miles west of Boston on the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]. Settled in 1635, it was the first inland plantation of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. Concord is known for its role in the Revolution and as the home of the Transcendentalist movement, including Emerson, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Hawthorne]], and Alcott.

## On the River Route

Concord is the starting point of the journey. On Saturday, August 31, 1839, the brothers "weighed anchor in this river port; for Concord, too, lies under the low hills of the river's bank." (002-001) They were "natives of Concord" (002-002) and the town serves as both start and end of their circular route.

## Historical Context

Concord was settled in 1635 by English colonists who named it to signify peace with the indigenous people. (001-005) By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it had become the intellectual capital of New England Transcendentalism. It was also the site of the Battle of Concord in 1775, which began the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]].

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes his native town with [[love|affection]], noting its place "on the low hills of the river's bank." (002-001) He refers to the "firm lands of Concord, both peopled cape and lonely farm" (002-047) and notes its literary heritage, mentioning a "Concord poet." (002-058)
