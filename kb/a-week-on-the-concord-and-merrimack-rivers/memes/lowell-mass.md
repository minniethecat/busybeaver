## Description

Lowell is a city at the confluence of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s, twenty-five miles northwest of Boston. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, Lowell was the fastest-growing industrial city in America, with textile mills powered by the [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]]. (001-020)

## On the River Route

The brothers pass Lowell on their journey. It marks where the [[concord-mass|Concord]] meets the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (001-020) They note the factories that ended fish migrations. (002-512) A fire in Lowell is visible during their first night. (002-702) They reflect on Lowell as "the city of spindles and [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] of America." (003-1105)

## Historical Context

Lowell was founded in the 1820s as a planned industrial city, harnessing the [[pawtucket-falls|Pawtucket Falls]]. It became the largest industrial center in the United States by the 1840s, with mills employing "Lowell mill girls." [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] viewed Lowell with ambivalence, admiring its energy but questioning the cost to nature and human life.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the "bricks of which Lowell is made" (006-049) and reflects it was worth seeing "the place where Lowell was 'dug out.'" (006-057) He describes it as the "city of spindles" (003-1105) and observes how industry transformed the [[nature|landscape]]. Factory dams blocked fish migrations, particularly shad. (002-512, 002-524)
