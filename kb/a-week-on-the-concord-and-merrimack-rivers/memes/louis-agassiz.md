# Louis Agassiz

## Biography

Louis Agassiz (1807–1873) was a Swiss-born naturalist who became one of the most prominent scientists in nineteenth-century America. Trained in medicine and natural history at the universities of Zurich, Heidelberg, and Munich, he established his reputation with a monumental study of fossil fishes before accepting a professorship at [[harvard-college|Harvard University]] in 1847. At [[harvard-college|Harvard]], he founded the Museum of Comparative Zoology and became a towering figure in American natural science, known for his work in ichthyology, glaciology, and his controversial advocacy of polygenism.

## Works / Significance

Agassiz's most enduring scientific contributions were in ichthyology, the study of fishes. His systematic classification of both fossil and living fish species established him as the leading authority of his era. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], an attentive observer of the [[nature|natural world]], was aware of Agassiz's work and cited his expertise when cataloguing the fish species of the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]. Agassiz's *Lake Superior* (1850) and his *Contributions to the Natural [[history|History]] of the United States* (1857–1862) were major scientific publications of the period.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Agassiz twice in *Saturday*, both times in connection with fish species. First, describing a red spot on the operculum of a fish, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "according to M. Agassiz, is undescribed" — a gesture toward the scientific incompleteness of local natural history even as [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] undertakes his own detailed observations (002-371). Later in the same chapter, he observes more broadly that "according to M. Agassiz, several of the species found in this town are yet undescribed" (002-506). These passing citations reveal [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s characteristic synthesis of poetic observation and scientific curiosity, invoking the era's greatest ichthyologist to underscore the richness and mystery of his own native waters.
