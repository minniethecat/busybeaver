Pindar, born near Thebes in Boeotia around 518 BC, is the greatest of the ancient Greek lyric poets. His surviving works consist primarily of the *Epinikia* — victory odes celebrating winners at the four Panhellenic games. Pindar's style is characterized by dense mythological allusion, striking imagery, and a lofty and complex poetic diction.

## Works / Significance

Pindar's victory odes elevated athletic achievement into a meditation on human [[virtue|excellence]], divine favor, and the fragility of mortal fortune. His poetry weaves together myth, praise of the victor, and moral [[thought|reflection]]. The Roman poet [[horace|Horace]] compared Pindar's [[poetry|verse]] to a mighty river sweeping down from the mountains.

Pindar's mythological narratives — including the origin of the island Thera and the fable of Helius looking down into the sea — are among his most memorable contributions to Greek myth.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Pindar in several chapters of *A Week*, drawing on the Greek poet's mythological imagination and his celebration of human striving.

In Sunday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes of "the victor, whom all Pindars praised," who has now "won another palm" — invoking Pindar's odes to athletic victory as a metaphor for enduring human achievement. (003-1558) The plural "Pindars" suggests that the laureate's praise continues in every generation.

In Wednesday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] relates Pindar's account of the origin of Thera, where settlers worked upon "silver sands and the ruins of forests, with ant-like industry." (006-233) He also tells Pindar's "[[beauty|beautiful]] fable" of how Helius, or the Sun, looked down into the sea, "when perchance his rays were reflected back to the heavens." (006-252) These mythological passages resonate with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own poetic engagement with the [[nature|natural world]].

In Friday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups Pindar with Isaiah and the American Indian as examples of poetry in which "only the simplest and most enduring features of humanity are seen." (008-266) This striking pairing places the Greek lyric poet in a universal tradition of primal, elemental expression that transcends cultural and [[time|temporal]] distance.
