# The 1839 Boat Journey

The 1839 boat journey is the foundational event of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* — the two-week voyage that [[henry-david-thoreau|Henry David Thoreau]] and his brother John undertook in late summer 1839, and that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] later compressed into a single symbolic week, using each day as a frame for philosophical meditation, [[history|historical]] research, and natural observation.

The journey was at once a physical adventure, a fraternal rite, and — after John's death from tetanus in 1842 — a memorial. The book that records it is not a travelogue but an elegy, and the river that carries the brothers northward also carries [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s grief.

## Overview

On August 31, 1839, Henry and John [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] pushed off from [[concord-massachusetts|Concord, Massachusetts]], in a homemade fifteen-foot skiff — the *[[concord-river-mass|Musketaquid]]* — built by Henry himself. They rowed and sailed up the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] to its confluence with the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]], then turned northward into New Hampshire, following the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as far as the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] before turning back.

The actual journey lasted approximately two weeks. In the book, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] compresses it into seven days — Saturday through Friday — with each day serving as a vessel for digression. The physical journey provides structure; the digressions provide substance.

## The Journey in the Book

Each chapter of *A Week* corresponds to a day of the journey, but [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s method is not chronological reportage. He uses the river's flow as a structuring metaphor, allowing the current to carry him between topics as it carries the boat between towns.

**Saturday — Departure.** The brothers set out under "a fresh and favorable wind" (002-001), passing through [[concord-mass|Concord]]'s meadows and locks. The day establishes the book's characteristic movement: observation of the immediate scene giving way to [[thought|reflection]] on what it evokes.

The fish in the river become "the fish principle in nature" (002-294); the riverbanks become an occasion to discuss [[history]], [[poetry]], and the proper relation between civilization and the wild.

**Sunday — The River Deepens.** As the brothers enter the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], the book takes on greater philosophical weight. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on religion — comparing [[religion|Christianity]] unfavorably with the "serene and complete" gods of Greece (003-597) — and on the nature of [[history|historical]] memory.

The towns along the river — [[nashua|Nashua]], [[manchester-nh|Manchester]], [[concord-mass|Concord]] — appear "gleaming one above the other" (003-1214), beacons of the industrial transformation reshaping New England.

**Monday — [[labor|Labor]] and [[civilization|Society]].** The river becomes a lens through which to examine work, commerce, and reform. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] contrasts the "infinite leisure and repose of nature" (004-213) with the busyness of human society.

The chapter reflects on the limitations of philanthropy and organized reform, arguing that true improvement occurs in intimate relation, not through institutional action — a theme that culminates in Tuesday's meditation on [[friendship]].

**Tuesday — [[friendship|Friendship]].** The philosophical heart of the book. As the brothers drift downstream, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] launches into a sustained meditation on [[love|friendship]] — the longest [[thought|reflection]] on any single topic in the entire work.

Drawing on [[confucius|Confucius]], Mencius, and the story of the fur-trader Henry and his Native [[friendship|friend]] Wawatam, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] develops a radical account of [[love|friendship]] as "a relation of perfect equality" (006-1066), rarer and more demanding than the world acknowledges.

**Wednesday — Landscape and [[history|History]].** The river narrows, the [[nature|landscape]] becomes wilder, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[history|historical]] imagination ranges across the colonial [[history|past]]. The chapter is rich with frontier violence — [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]], [[hannah-dustan-captivity|Hannah Dustan's captivity]].

"Other roads do some violence to [[nature|Nature]]" (006-009), he observes, but the river "steals into the scenery it tra[[poetry|verse]]s without intrusion" — a [[silence|quiet]] celebration of the river's way of moving through [[nature|landscape]] without dominating it.

**Thursday — The Return Begins.** The brothers turn homeward. The downstream drift is faster, more reflective. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] explores the history of [[penacook|Penacook]] (007-132), the settlement of the intervals, and the transformation of Indigenous homeland into colonial farmland.

The chapter is elegiac in tone — the journey is ending, and with it the "long afternoon" of the brothers' shared enterprise.

**Friday — Homecoming.** The final chapter blends arrival with anticlimax. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on the nature of journeys and their endings, on what it means to return.

The book's closing image — the boat slipping into its mooring at [[concord-mass|Concord]] — is [[silence|quiet]], unresolved, the journey dissolving back into ordinary life.

## The Journey as Memorial

The 1839 journey is inseparable from its aftermath. John [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] died of tetanus on January 11, 1842, at the age of twenty-seven, after cutting himself while shaving. Henry was devastated.

The book he wrote in the years following — partly during his stay at Walden Pond — is an act of memory and love. The "you" addressed in the book's prefatory poem is John:

> Though now thou climbest loftier mounts,
> And fairer rivers dost ascend,
> Be thou my Muse, my Brother —

The journey of 1839 becomes, in the writing, a journey through grief. The river's flow is time's flow; the places the brothers visited together become stations of mourning. The most personal passage in the book is also its most restrained — [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] never names his loss directly, but it colors every page.

## Historical Background

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] undertook the journey at a pivotal moment in his life. He had graduated from [[harvard-college|Harvard College]] in 1837 and was searching for a vocation. He had met [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]], whose essay *[[nature|Nature]]* (1836) had deeply influenced him, and was beginning to find his voice as a writer.

The boat journey with John was both a brotherly adventure and an act of deliberate self-education in the Emersonian mode — an immersion in experience from which literature might be made. Emerson encouraged [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] to keep a journal, and the raw material of the 1839 voyage filled its pages.

The book that resulted was published in 1849 by James Munroe and Company, at [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own expense. It sold fewer than 300 copies. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] famously remarked that he had "now a library of nearly nine hundred volumes, over seven hundred of which I wrote myself" — a wry acknowledgment of the unsold copies returned to him by the publisher.

Despite its commercial failure, *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* has since been recognized as a distinctive contribution to American letters. It blends genres — travel writing, natural history, philosophy, literary criticism, spiritual autobiography — in a way that was novel in its time and that prefigures [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s later masterpiece, *Walden* (1854).

The 1839 journey is the seed from which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s entire literary career grew. [[walden-pond|Walden Pond]] lies just a few miles from the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]], and the method [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] perfected in *A Week* — using a specific place and time as a frame for universal [[thought|reflection]] — would reach its fullest expression in *Walden*.
