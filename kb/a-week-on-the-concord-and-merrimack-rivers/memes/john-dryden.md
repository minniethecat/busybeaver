## Biography

John Dryden (1631–1700) was the dominant literary figure of Restoration England. Born in Northamptonshire and educated at Westminster School and Trinity College, Cambridge, he established himself as a playwright before turning increasingly to poetry and translation.

Appointed the first Poet Laureate of England in 1668, Dryden navigated the shifting political and [[religion|religious]] [[nature|landscape]] from Cromwell's Commonwealth through the Restoration and the Glorious Revolution — converting to Catholicism under James II and losing the laureateship as a consequence.

## Works / Significance

Dryden's major works include *Absalom and Achitophel* (1681), a political satire in heroic couplets; *Mac Flecknoe* (1682), a mock-epic ridiculing a rival poet; and *The Hind and the Panther* (1687), an allegorical defense of Catholicism. His translation of [[virgil|Virgil]]'s *Aeneid* (1697) remains a landmark of English [[poetry|verse]] translation.

As a critic, Dryden's *Essay of Dramatic Poesy* (1668) established principles of literary judgment that shaped English neoclassicism. His mastery of the heroic couplet paved the way for [[alexander-pope|Pope]] and the Augustan school.

## In Thoreau's Text

Dryden appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], reflecting on the trajectory of English poetry, names Dryden among its late practitioners (008-888):

> Dryden, and Pope, and Gray. Our summer of English poetry like the Greek and Latin before it, seems well advanced toward its fall, and laden with the fruit and foliage of the season, with bright autumnal tints, but soon the winter will scatter its myriad clustering and shading leaves, and leave only a few desolate and fibrous boughs to sustain the sky.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Dryden at the head of a trio of poets who, for all their polish and accomplishment, represent a poetic tradition in decline. The metaphor is seasonal: Dryden's poetry, like autumn fruit, is rich but carries the scent of decay — a harvest gathered before the coming winter.
