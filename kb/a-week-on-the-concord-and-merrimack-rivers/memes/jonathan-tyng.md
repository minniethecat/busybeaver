## Biography

Jonathan Tyng was an English colonist who became the first permanent settler of [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]], Massachusetts (which at the time encompassed present-day [[tyngsborough|Tyngsborough]] and several neighboring towns). He maintained a garrison at his house near [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Falls]] on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], for which service the General Court granted him [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]] in 1683 after the Indians had departed the area (003-1838).

Tyng employed Native American laborers, and [[gookin|Gookin]] records that in 1675, at the outbreak of [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]], seven Indians "had all been at work about seven weeks with one Mr. Jonathan Tyng, of [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]], upon [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]" before departing secretly through the woods (003-1846). An Indian named Elisha, who was in Tyng's service, was later killed by members of his own tribe in one of the Indian wars. The apple-tree marking the site, known as "Elisha's apple-tree," still stood near the riverbank at the time of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s journey (008-621).

Tyng was a prominent figure in the colonial establishment of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. The town of [[tyngsborough|Tyngsborough, Massachusetts]], bears his family name. He died in 1724.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions Jonathan Tyng in two distinct passages of *A Week*. In the *Sunday* chapter, while discussing the history of [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]], he recounts how the island was granted to Tyng for his colonial service after the Indians had departed. In the *Friday* chapter, nearing the end of his journey, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes "Elisha's apple-tree," memorializing the Indian who served Tyng and was killed there — a [[silence|quiet]] monument to the tangled history of colonial-Indigenous relations on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]].
