## Biography

Edmund Spenser (c. 1552–1599) was an English poet and one of the foremost figures of the English Renaissance. Educated at Cambridge, he served in Ireland as a colonial administrator while producing his major work, *The Faerie Queene*, an allegorical epic celebrating Queen Elizabeth I and Protestant virtue. His other works include *The Shepheardes Calender* and *Amoretti*, a sonnet sequence. [[ben-jonson|Ben Jonson]] described him as one who "writ no language," reflecting Spenser's deliberate use of archaic diction.

## Works / Significance

*The Faerie Queene* is one of the longest and most influential poems in the English language, establishing the Spenserian stanza as a major [[poetry|verse]] form. Spenser's work bridges medieval allegory and Renaissance humanism, combining chivalric romance with Protestant moral seriousness. His influence extends through Milton, the Romantic poets, and into the American literary tradition that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] inherited. The [[music|musical]]ity of his [[poetry|verse]], his invention of vivid symbolic [[nature|landscape]]s, and his fusion of classical and English traditions made him a central figure in the English literary canon.

## In Thoreau's Text

Spenser appears in *A Week* within a quotation that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] deploys to reflect on literary fame and obscurity. In the *Sunday* chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes lines that name Spenser alongside Sir Philip Sidney:

> "How many thousands never heard the name / Of Sidney, or of Spenser, or their books? / And yet brave fellows, and presume of fame, / And seem to bear down all the world with looks." (003-1646)

The quotation serves [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s meditation on the transience of literary reputation. Even great poets are unknown to vast numbers of people — yet those same people walk through the world with the confidence of fame. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Spenser as an emblem of the paradox of literary immortality: celebrated in the canon, yet invisible to the multitude.

Later in *Thursday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes a passage from *The Faerie Queene* describing a boatman rowing:

> "His tryed armes for toylesome wearinesse; / But with his oares did sweepe the watry wildernesse."

The river imagery of Spenser's epic resonates with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own journey — both are travelers sweeping through a "watry [[nature|wilderness]]e," and the quotation bridges the classical epic tradition with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s more intimate, personal mode of travel writing.
