## Biography

Hafiz (Khwāja Shams-ud-Dīn Muḥammad Ḥāfeẓ-e Shīrāzī, 1315–1390) is among the most celebrated poets of Persia. Born and buried in Shiraz, he composed some five hundred ghazals — lyrical poems blending earthly love, wine, and mystical [[love|devotion]] to the Divine. His pen name, *Hafiz*, designates one who has memorized the entire Qur'an.

Hafiz's *Divan* is a cornerstone of Persian literature, and his influence extends from Goethe's *West-östlicher Divan* to the American Transcendentalists. [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]] translated several of his poems, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] read Emerson's versions with admiration.

## In Thoreau's Text

Hafiz appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the closing pages of the "Friday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes him directly, coupling the Persian poet with a line from the English Quaker William Penn (008-1480):

> "Yesterday, at dawn," says Hafiz, "God delivered me from all worldly care."

The quotation, brief but luminous, captures a moment of spiritual release — the dawn as a figure for divine grace. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places it among a cluster of final [[thought|reflection]]s in the book's last chapter, where Eastern and Western wisdom converge. The appeal of Hafiz for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] lies in the same qualities Emerson admired: a fusion of sensuous immediacy and transcendent longing, unmediated by doctrinal apparatus.
