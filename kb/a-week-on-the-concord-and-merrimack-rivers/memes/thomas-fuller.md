## Biography

Thomas Fuller (1608–1661) was an English churchman, historian, and biographer. Educated at Queens' College, Cambridge, he served as a curate and lecturer before the English Civil War. A moderate royalist, he continued writing during the Commonwealth period. His most famous works include *The Worthies of England* (1662), a county-by-county survey of notable English figures and landmarks, and *The Holy State and the Profane State* (1642), a collection of character sketches and moral essays.

## Works / Significance

Fuller was one of the first English writers to earn a living by his pen. His prose is notable for its wit, anecdotal richness, and encyclopedic range. *The Worthies of England* is considered the first attempt at a comprehensive biographical dictionary in English. Fuller's style — mixing erudition with colloquial energy — influenced later English prose writers and antiquarians. [[john-aubrey|John Aubrey]], the 17th-century biographer, recorded personal observations of Fuller's habits, which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Thomas Fuller three times in *A Week*, drawing on his wit, his thoughts on evidence, and his observations on education.

In *Sunday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Fuller to illustrate the ceaseless activity of a productive [[thought|mind]]. Quoting [[john-aubrey|John Aubrey]]'s biography of Fuller, he notes:

> Aubrey relates of Thomas Fuller that his was "a very working head, insomuch that, walking and meditating before dinner, he would eat up a penny loaf, not knowing that he did it." (003-1789)

In *Wednesday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] turns to Fuller's thoughts on [[history|historical]] evidence, quoting his praise of the antiquarian William Camden:

> As Fuller said, commenting on the zeal of Camden, "A broken urn is a whole evidence; or an old gate still surviving out of which the city is run out." (006-404)

In *Friday*, reflecting on higher education, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Fuller's pragmatic wisdom about teaching:

> What Fuller says of masters of colleges is universally applicable, that "a little alloy of dulness in a master of a college makes him more apt to govern scholars." (008-1458)

Across these three citations, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] treats Fuller as a source of aphoristic wisdom — a writer whose observations on intellectual labor, [[history|historical]] evidence, and education remain timely.
