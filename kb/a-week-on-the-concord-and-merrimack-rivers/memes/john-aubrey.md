## Biography

John Aubrey (1626–1697) was an English antiquarian, natural philosopher, and writer, best known for his collection of biographical sketches published posthumously as *Brief Lives*. Aubrey was a pioneering figure in English antiquarianism, archaeology, and folklore studies. He carried out some of the earliest systematic surveys of Stonehenge and Avebury, and his biographical writings preserved vivid, informal portraits of many of his eminent contemporaries, including Thomas Hobbes, [[john-milton|John Milton]], and Robert Boyle.

Aubrey's method was to gather anecdotes, personal details, and curious facts about his subjects, valuing the intimate and the particular over formal biography. His *Brief Lives* were not published in his lifetime but became a beloved source for later historians and writers for their unvarnished, gossipy detail.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites John Aubrey in the *Sunday* chapter as a witness to the character of the English churchman and historian [[thomas-fuller|Thomas Fuller]]. He records Aubrey's description: [[thomas-fuller|Fuller]] had "a very working head, insomuch that, walking and meditating before dinner, he would eat up a penny loaf, not knowing that he did it" (003-1789–003-1792). Aubrey further noted that [[thomas-fuller|Fuller]]'s "natural memory was very great, to which he added the art of memory," and that [[thomas-fuller|Fuller]] "would repeat to you forwards and backwards all the signs" from one end of London to the other (003-1792–003-1793).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] presents Aubrey's anecdote as part of a [[thought|reflection]] on concentrated thought and the life of the [[thought|mind]] — themes central to the *Sunday* chapter's broader meditation on intellectual labor and spiritual [[thought|contemplation]].
