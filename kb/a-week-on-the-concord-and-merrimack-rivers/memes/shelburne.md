## Description

Shelburne Falls is a waterfall on the Deerfield River in western Massachusetts, notable for a geological formation that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] studied with great interest: pot-holes. These are cylindrical cavities worn into the limestone bedrock by the grinding action of stones caught in eddies at the base of the falls. (006-315)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the pot-holes at Shelburne Falls as ranging from one foot to four or five feet in diameter and as many feet in depth, "perfectly round and regular, with smooth and gracefully curved brims, like goblets." (006-324) Some have worn entirely through the rock, allowing a portion of the river to leak through "in anticipation of the fall." (006-341)

Similar pot-holes exist at other falls in New England—[[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag]] on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], Bellows Falls on the Connecticut, and the Basin on the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset]]—but Shelburne Falls is where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] "first observed them." (006-315)

## On the River Route

Shelburne Falls is not on the [[concord-mass|Concord]]-[[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] route itself but appears in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s extended meditation on the geological features of New England rivers, prompted by his observations at [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag Falls]]. He draws comparisons between the pot-holes at [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag]] and those he had previously studied at Shelburne, using the latter as a reference point for understanding the formation of similar features throughout the region. (006-315) (006-324) (006-341)

## Historical Context

The pot-holes at Shelburne Falls have been a subject of geological curiosity since the early 19th century. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that some observers had declared them "plainly to be artificial," reflecting the difficulty early naturalists had in accounting for their formation. He himself understood them as the product of fluvial erosion—stones and pebbles "kept in motion by the velocity of the current" that gradually drill into the bedrock.

Some pot-holes [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed had been cut as deep as sixteen feet above the current water level, "in still and dry for ages," evidence of the river's ancient course at much higher levels. Others were "still revolving, and enjoy no respite at any season," actively being deepened by the ongoing action of the falls. (006-341)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in the Shelburne pot-holes reflects his characteristic attention to the intersection of geology, time, and [[nature|landscape]]. He sees in these "perfectly round and regular" cavities the patient work of natural forces across immense spans of time—a perspective that aligns with his transcendental view of nature as both artist and architect.

His comparison between the pot-holes at Shelburne, [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag]], and elsewhere reveals his method of building geological knowledge through comparative observation across multiple sites, assembling a regional picture from local details.
