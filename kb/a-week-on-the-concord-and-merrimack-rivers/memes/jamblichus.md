# Jamblichus

## Biography

Jamblichus (c. 245–325 CE) was a Neo-[[plato|Plato]]nic philosopher born in Chalcis, Syria. A student of Porphyry, he became one of the most influential figures in the later [[plato|Plato]]nic tradition. His major works include *On the Mysteries of the Egyptians*, *On the Pythagorean Way of Life*, and extensive commentaries on [[plato|Plato]] and [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. Jamblichus systematized the metaphysical hierarchy of Neo-[[plato|Plato]]nism and placed greater emphasis on theurgy — ritual practices aimed at achieving union with the divine — than his teacher Porphyry.

## Works / Significance

Jamblichus's philosophical writings bridged the classical [[plato|Plato]]nic tradition and the mystical, theurgic concerns of late [[history|antiquity]]. His biography of [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]] served as a key source for the Pythagorean tradition, and his metaphysical framework — elaborating a series of emanations from the One through the intellectual and psychic realms — shaped subsequent [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian and Islamic philosophy. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], through his own immersion in classical literature, encountered Jamblichus as a representative of profound but sometimes impenetrable philosophical prose he both admired and gently satirized.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Jamblichus twice in *Sunday*, both times in the context of reflecting on the character of philosophical writing. He groups Jamblichus with [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]] and [[plato|Plato]] as thinkers whose discourse reaches a "full tide," but whose sentences are "long, stringy, slimy" — flowing together with a consistency that lacks the dispatch of writing meant for practical men (003-1637). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s imagery is characteristically double-edged: "The wise Jamblichus eddies and gleams like a watery slough," suggesting both depth and a certain obscurity (003-1644).

Later, in *Monday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] returns to Jamblichus approvingly, citing his biographical account of [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]]: "According to Jamblichus, '[[pythagoras|Pythagoras]] did not procure for himself a thing of any price.'" (004-1609) This citation places Jamblichus among [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s authorities on the ancient ideal of the philosophical life — one lived with simplicity and principled detachment from material concerns.
