## Description

Hudson Bay is a large saltwater body in northeastern Canada, connected to the Atlantic Ocean via the [[hudson|Hudson]] Strait. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, it was a hub of the fur trade and a distant landmark in the geographical imagination of New Englanders. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references it twice in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, associating it with wildlife and the continent's vast interior waterways.

## On the River Route

Hudson Bay lies far to the north of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s, but it appears in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[thought|reflection]]s during the Tuesday chapter. Near the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes a small creature he calls the "Hudson Bay squirrel" — the chickaree or red squirrel (*Sciurus [[hudson|Hudson]]ius*) — whose chattering marked the brothers' passage along the wooded banks (005-460).

Later, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] situates Hudson Bay within a broader geographical meditation, connecting it to Lake Winnipeg and the great chain of lakes and portages that define the northern interior (005-1064). For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Hudson Bay represented the remote northern limit of the continent's interconnected waters.
