## Overview

Windham is a town in Rockingham County, New Hampshire, situated in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]
Valley near the Massachusetts border. The town lies between the Spicket River
and Beaver Brook, both tributaries of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], and its early development
was shaped by Scotch-Irish settlement patterns.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions Windham in a topographical catalogue of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s
tributary watersheds, grouping it with neighboring towns drained by Beaver
Brook:

> (003-1293) Pelham, Windham, and Londonderry. The Scotch-Irish settlers of the
> latter town, according to this authority, were the first to introduce the
> potato into New England, as well as the manufacture of linen cloth.

The reference is brief but situates Windham within the Scotch-Irish settlement
region of southern New Hampshire. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], ever attentive to agricultural and
cultural history, notes the introduction of the potato — a crop that would
become central to New England farming.

## Settlement History

Windham was originally part of [[londonderry|Londonderry]], which was granted in 1719 to a
group of Scotch-Irish immigrants from the north of Ireland. The town was
separately incorporated in 1742 and named after Sir Charles Wyndham, a British
statesman. The Scotch-Irish settlers brought with them linen-weaving skills
and the potato, both of which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records as having spread from this
region into the broader New England economy.

## Geography

The town occupies an area of approximately twenty-eight square miles in the
southeastern corner of New Hampshire. Beaver Brook, mentioned by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as
draining the meadows of the area, flows through the western part of Windham
before joining the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]]. The terrain is rolling and marked by
glacial features, including Cobbett's Pond and Canobie Lake.
