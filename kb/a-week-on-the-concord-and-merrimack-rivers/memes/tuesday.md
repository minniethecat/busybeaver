“On either side the river lie

Long fields of barley and of rye,

That clothe the wold and meet the sky;

And through the fields the road runs by

To many-towered Camelot.”

TENNYSON.

[005-001]Long before daylight we ranged abroad, hatchet in hand, in search of

[005-002]fuel, and made the yet slumbering and [[dream|dreaming]] wood resound with our

[005-003]blows. Then with our fire we burned up a portion of the loitering

[005-004]night, while the kettle sang its homely strain to the morning star. We

[005-005]tramped about the shore, waked all the muskrats, and scared up the

[005-006]bittern and birds that were asleep upon their roosts; we hauled up and

[005-007]upset our boat and washed it and rinsed out the clay, talking aloud as

[005-008]if it were broad day, until at length, by three o’clock, we had

[005-009]completed our preparations and were ready to pursue our voyage as

[005-010]usual; so, shaking the clay from our feet, we pushed into the fog.

[005-011]Though we were enveloped in mist as usual, we trusted that there was a

[005-012]bright day behind it.

[005-013]Ply the oars! away! away!

[005-014]In each dew-drop of the morning

[005-015]Lies the promise of a day.

[005-016]Rivers from the sunrise flow,

[005-017]Springing with the dewy morn;

[005-018]Voyageurs ’gainst time do row,

[005-019]Idle noon nor sunset know,

[005-020]Ever even with the dawn.

[005-021]Belknap, the historian of this State, says that, “In the neighborhood

[005-022]of fresh rivers and ponds, a whitish fog in the morning lying over the

[005-023]water is a sure indication of fair weather for that day; and when no

[005-024]fog is seen, rain is expected before night.” That which seemed to us to

[005-025]invest the world was only a narrow and shallow wreath of vapor

[005-026]stretched over the channel of the Merrimack from the seaboard to the

[005-027]mountains. More extensive fogs, however, have their own limits. I once

[005-028]saw the day break from the top of Saddle-back Mountain in

[005-029]Massachusetts, above the clouds. As we cannot distinguish objects

[005-030]through this dense fog, let me tell this story more at length.

[005-031]I had come over the hills on foot and alone in serene summer days,

[005-032]plucking the raspberries by the wayside, and occasionally buying a loaf

[005-033]of bread at a farmer’s house, with a knapsack on my back which held a

[005-034]few traveller’s books and a change of clothing, and a staff in my hand.

[005-035]I had that morning looked down from the Hoosack Mountain, where the

[005-036]road crosses it, on the village of North Adams in the valley three

[005-037]miles away under my feet, showing how uneven the earth may sometimes

[005-038]be, and making it seem an accident that it should ever be level and

[005-039]convenient for the feet of man. Putting a little rice and sugar and a

[005-040]tin cup into my knapsack at this village, I began in the afternoon to

[005-041]ascend the mountain, whose summit is three thousand six hundred feet

[005-042]above the level of the sea, and was seven or eight miles distant by the

[005-043]path. My route lay up a long and spacious valley called the Bellows,

[005-044]because the winds rush up or down it with violence in storms, sloping

[005-045]up to the very clouds between the principal range and a lower mountain.

[005-046]There were a few farms scattered along at different elevations, each

[005-047]commanding a fine prospect of the mountains to the north, and a stream

[005-048]ran down the middle of the valley on which near the head there was a

[005-049]mill. It seemed a road for the pilgrim to enter upon who would climb to

[005-050]the gates of heaven. Now I crossed a hay-field, and now over the brook

[005-051]on a slight bridge, still gradually ascending all the while with a sort

[005-052]of awe, and filled with indefinite expectations as to what kind of

[005-053]inhabitants and what kind of nature I should come to at last. It now

[005-054]seemed some advantage that the earth was uneven, for one could not

[005-055]imagine a more noble position for a farm-house than this vale afforded,

[005-056]farther from or nearer to its head, from a glen-like seclusion

[005-057]overlooking the country at a great elevation between these two mountain

[005-058]walls.

[005-059]It reminded me of the homesteads of the Huguenots, on Staten Island,

[005-060]off the coast of New Jersey. The hills in the interior of this island,

[005-061]though comparatively low, are penetrated in various directions by

[005-062]similar sloping valleys on a humble scale, gradually narrowing and

[005-063]rising to the centre, and at the head of these the Huguenots, who were

[005-064]the first settlers, placed their houses quite within the land, in rural

[005-065]and sheltered places, in leafy recesses where the breeze played with

[005-066]the poplar and the gum-tree, from which, with equal security in calm

[005-067]and storm, they looked out through a widening vista, over miles of

[005-068]forest and stretching salt marsh, to the Huguenot’s Tree, an old elm on

[005-069]the shore at whose root they had landed, and across the spacious outer

[005-070]bay of New York to Sandy Hook and the Highlands of Neversink, and

[005-071]thence over leagues of the Atlantic, perchance to some faint vessel in

[005-072]the horizon, almost a day’s sail on her voyage to that Europe whence

[005-073]they had come. When walking in the interior there, in the midst of

[005-074]rural scenery, where there was as little to remind me of the ocean as

[005-075]amid the New Hampshire hills, I have suddenly, through a gap, a cleft

[005-076]or “clove road,” as the Dutch settlers called it, caught sight of a

[005-077]ship under full sail, over a field of corn, twenty or thirty miles at

[005-078]sea. The effect was similar, since I had no means of measuring

[005-079]distances, to seeing a painted ship passed backwards and forwards

[005-080]through a magic-lantern.

[005-090]was well kept and so nobly placed, the next day, and perhaps remaining

[005-101]As I passed the last house, a man called out to know what I had to

[005-102]sell, for seeing my knapsack, he thought that I might be a pedler who

[005-103]was taking this unusual route over the ridge of the valley into South

[005-104]Adams. He told me that it was still four or five miles to the summit by

[005-105]the path which I had left, though not more than two in a straight line

[005-106]from where I was, but that nobody ever went this way; there was no

[005-107]path, and I should find it as steep as the roof of a house. But I knew

[005-108]that I was more used to woods and mountains than he, and went along

[005-109]through his cow-yard, while he, looking at the sun, shouted after me

[005-110]that I should not get to the top that night. I soon reached the head of

[005-111]the valley, but as I could not see the summit from this point, I

[005-112]ascended a low mountain on the opposite side, and took its bearing with

[005-113]my compass. I at once entered the woods, and began to climb the steep

[005-114]side of the mountain in a diagonal direction, taking the bearing of a

[005-115]tree every dozen rods. The ascent was by no means difficult or

[005-116]unpleasant, and occupied much less time than it would have taken to

[005-117]follow the path. Even country people, I have observed, magnify the

[005-118]difficulty of travelling in the forest, and especially among mountains.

[005-119]They seem to lack their usual common sense in this. I have climbed

[005-120]several higher mountains without guide or path, and have found, as

[005-121]might be expected, that it takes only more time and patience commonly

[005-122]than to travel the smoothest highway. It is very rare that you meet

[005-123]with obstacles in this world which the humblest man has not faculties

[005-124]to surmount. It is true we may come to a perpendicular precipice, but

[005-125]we need not jump off nor run our heads against it. A man may jump down

[005-126]his own cellar stairs or dash his brains out against his chimney, if he

[005-127]is mad. So far as my experience goes, travellers generally exaggerate

[005-128]the difficulties of the way. Like most evil, the difficulty is

[005-129]imaginary; for what’s the hurry? If a person lost would conclude that

[005-130]after all he is not lost, he is not beside himself, but standing in his

[005-131]own old shoes on the very spot where he is, and that for the time being

[005-132]he will live there; but the places that have known him, _they_ are

[005-133]lost,—how much anxiety and danger would vanish. I am not alone if I

[005-134]stand by myself. Who knows where in space this globe is rolling? Yet we

[005-135]will not give ourselves up for lost, let it go where it will.

[005-136]I made my way steadily upward in a straight line through a dense

[005-137]undergrowth of mountain laurel, until the trees began to have a scraggy

[005-138]and infernal look, as if contending with frost goblins, and at length I

[005-139]reached the summit, just as the sun was setting. Several acres here had

[005-140]been cleared, and were covered with rocks and stumps, and there was a

[005-141]rude observatory in the middle which overlooked the woods. I had one

[005-142]fair view of the country before the sun went down, but I was too

[005-143]thirsty to waste any light in viewing the prospect, and set out

[005-144]directly to find water. First, going down a well-beaten path for half a

[005-145]mile through the low scrubby wood, till I came to where the water stood

[005-146]in the tracks of the horses which had carried travellers up, I lay down

[005-147]flat, and drank these dry, one after another, a pure, cold, spring-like

[005-148]water, but yet I could not fill my dipper, though I contrived little

[005-149]siphons of grass-stems, and ingenious aqueducts on a small scale; it

[005-150]was too slow a process. Then remembering that I had passed a moist

[005-151]place near the top, on my way up, I returned to find it again, and

[005-152]here, with sharp stones and my hands, in the twilight, I made a well

[005-153]about two feet deep, which was soon filled with pure cold water, and

[005-154]the birds too came and drank at it. So I filled my dipper, and, making

[005-155]my way back to the observatory, collected some dry sticks, and made a

[005-156]fire on some flat stones which had been placed on the floor for that

[005-157]purpose, and so I soon cooked my supper of rice, having already

[005-158]whittled a wooden spoon to eat it with.

[005-159]I sat up during the evening, reading by the light of the fire the

[005-160]scraps of newspapers in which some party had wrapped their luncheon;

[005-161]the prices current in New York and Boston, the advertisements, and the

[005-162]singular editorials which some had seen fit to publish, not foreseeing

[005-163]under what critical circumstances they would be read. I read these

[005-164]things at a vast advantage there, and it seemed to me that the

[005-165]advertisements, or what is called the business part of a paper, were

[005-166]greatly the best, the most useful, natural, and respectable. Almost all

[005-167]the opinions and sentiments expressed were so little considered, so

[005-168]shallow and flimsy, that I thought the very texture of the paper must

[005-169]be weaker in that part and tear the more easily. The advertisements and

[005-170]the prices current were more closely allied to nature, and were

[005-171]respectable in some measure as tide and meteorological tables are; but

[005-172]the reading-matter, which I remembered was most prized down below,

[005-173]unless it was some humble record of science, or an extract from some

[005-174]old classic, struck me as strangely whimsical, and crude, and

[005-175]one-idea’d, like a school-boy’s theme, such as youths write and after

[005-176]burn. The opinions were of that kind that are doomed to wear a

[005-177]different aspect to-morrow, like last year’s fashions; as if mankind

[005-178]were very green indeed, and would be ashamed of themselves in a few

[005-179]years, when they had outgrown this verdant period. There was, moreover,

[005-180]a singular disposition to wit and humor, but rarely the slightest real

[005-181]success; and the apparent success was a terrible satire on the attempt;

[005-182]the Evil Genius of man laughed the loudest at his best jokes. The

[005-183]advertisements, as I have said, such as were serious, and not of the

[005-184]modern quack kind, suggested pleasing and poetic thoughts; for commerce

[005-185]is really as interesting as nature. The very names of the commodities

[005-186]were poetic, and as suggestive as if they had been inserted in a

[005-187]pleasing poem,—Lumber, Cotton, Sugar, Hides, Guano, Logwood. Some

[005-188]sober, private, and original thought would have been grateful to read

[005-189]there, and as much in [[music|harmony]] with the circumstances as if it had been

[005-190]written on a mountain-top; for it is of a fashion which never changes,

[005-191]and as respectable as hides and logwood, or any natural product. What

[005-192]an inestimable companion such a scrap of paper would have been,

[005-193]containing some fruit of a mature life. What a relic! What a recipe! It

[005-194]seemed a divine invention, by which not mere shining coin, but shining

[005-195]and current thoughts, could be brought up and left there.
