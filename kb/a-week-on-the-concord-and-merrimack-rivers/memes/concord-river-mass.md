## Description

The Concord River, originally called the Musketaquid (Grass-ground River) by Native Americans, is a relatively small but [[history|historical]]ly significant river in eastern Massachusetts. It rises in the south part of [[hopkinton|Hopkinton]], with another branch from a pond and large cedar-swamp in Westborough, flowing through Framingham, [[sudbury|Sudbury]], and [[wayland|Wayland]] (where it is sometimes called the Sudbury River) before entering [[concord-mass|Concord]] at the south part of the town. After receiving the North or [[assabet-river|Assabeth River]], it flows between [[bedford|Bedford]] and [[carlisle|Carlisle]], through Billerica, and empties into the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]]. In [[concord-mass|Concord]] during summer, it is from four to fifteen feet deep and from one hundred to three hundred feet wide, but in spring freshets it overflows its banks and in some places is nearly a mile wide. (001-001)

## On the River Route

The Concord River forms the beginning and end of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s journey. The voyage commences on the Concord River at [[concord-massachusetts|Concord, Massachusetts]], on Saturday, August 31, 1839, with the brothers weighing anchor in this river port. (002-001) The river carries them through the [[great-meadows|Great Meadows]] and [[history|past]] the villages of [[bedford|Bedford]] and [[carlisle|Carlisle]] before they reach the Billerica dam and the canal that connects to the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]].

## Historical Context

The river received its English name of CONCORD from the first plantation on its banks in 1635, which appears to have been commenced in a spirit of peace and [[music|harmony]]. (001-005) The Musketaquid itself is probably as old as the Nile or Euphrates, though it did not begin to have a place in civilized history until the fame of its grassy meadows and its fish attracted settlers out of England. (001-001) The Concord River has been called "the most abused river in the most abusive history" (001-185) and is remarkable for the gentleness of its descent. (001-117)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes that the Concord River will be "Grass-ground River as long as grass grows and water runs here; it will be Concord River only while men lead peaceable lives on its banks." (001-007) He notes that shad are still taken in the basin of Concord River at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]], where they once came in multitudes. (002-594) The river's gentle current and grassy meadows form a central character in the narrative, a connecting thread for the entire week's journey. One of these boats came up the Concord River and was seen on its [[silence|quiet]] waters. (005-868)
