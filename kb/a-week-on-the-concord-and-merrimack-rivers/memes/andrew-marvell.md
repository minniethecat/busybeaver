## Biography

Andrew Marvell was born in Winestead, Yorkshire, the son of a Church of England clergyman. He was educated at Trinity College, Cambridge. During the English Civil War he traveled on the Continent, and upon his return served as tutor to Mary Fairfax (daughter of the Parliamentary general Sir Thomas Fairfax) and later to Cromwell's ward. He served as Member of Parliament for Hull from 1659 until his death. Though associated with the Puritan cause, Marvell navigated the political complexities of the Commonwealth and Restoration with unusual dexterity.

## Works / Significance

Marvell is celebrated as one of the finest metaphysical poets. His best-known poems — "To His Coy Mistress," "The Garden," "Upon Appleton House," and the "Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland" — combine intellectual wit, sensuous imagery, and political engagement. T.S. [[john-eliot|Eliot]]'s influential essay on Marvell (1921) helped revive interest in his work, praising his quality of "wit" as a "tough reasonableness beneath the slight lyric grace."

## In Thoreau's Text

Marvell appears in the "Thursday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] groups him among the greatest poets of the Western tradition only to assert that even they are insufficient (007-266):

> Homer and Shakespeare and Milton and Marvell and Wordsworth are but the rustling of leaves and crackling of twigs in the forest, and there is not yet the sound of any bird. (007-266–268)

This remarkable passage places Marvell in the highest literary company — [[homer|Homer]], [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]], Milton, [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] — yet [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s purpose is to suggest that even the greatest poetry falls short of true [[music|song]]. His inclusion of Marvell in this select list testifies to the high esteem in which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] held the metaphysical poet.
