# Wannalancet

## Biography

Wannalancet (c. 1610–1696) was a Sachem of the Pawtucket people who lived along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] in what is now New Hampshire and Massachusetts. He was the eldest son of [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]], the chiefest sachem of the Pawtucket, and inherited his father's policy of maintaining peaceful relations with English colonists. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes him as "a sober and grave person, and of years, between fifty and sixty" at the time of the colonial records (003-1048).

## Works / Significance

Wannalancet's [[history|historical]] significance lies in his steadfast commitment to peace during the turbulent period of [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]] (1675–1676). Following his father's advice to avoid war with the English, he withdrew his followers to [[penacook|Penacook]] (now [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire), keeping them out of the conflict that devastated many Native communities in southern New England. His diplomacy and restraint preserved the Pawtucket through a period when many neighboring tribes were destroyed or dispersed. His residence was near Pawtuckett Falls on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], about two miles from the colonial town (003-1045).

## In Thoreau's Text

Wannalancet appears throughout *A Week* as a figure of [[silence|quiet]] dignity, closely associated with the [[nature|landscape]] of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] first mentions him as part of the human history of the river: "for here came the Sachem Wannalancet, and his people, and sometimes Tahatawan, our [[concord-mass|Concord]] Sachem" to catch fish at the falls, alongside [[john-eliot|John Eliot]] with the Bible (003-1032). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes his dwelling place "called Wannalancet, about two miles from the town, near Pawtuckett falls, and bordering upon Merrimak river" (003-1045–003-1046).

In *Wednesday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] expands on Wannalancet's role in regional history. He records that "the Sachem Wannalancet resided" near the falls, and relates how his people "when at war with the Mohawks, concealed their pro[[dream|vision]]s in the cavities of the rocks" (006-306). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] tells how Wannalancet "carefully followed his advice" — his father [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]]'s counsel against war — and "when [[king-philips-war|Philip's War]] broke out, he withdrew his followers to [[penacook|Penacook]]" (006-488–006-490).

One of the most striking passages records Wannalancet's response when told that other Indians had submitted to the English: "'Me next'" — a [[silence|quiet]], dignified reply suggesting both his independence and his awareness of his people's precarious position (006-494). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] follows this with a characteristically elegiac observation: "The graves of Pasaconaway and Wannalancet are marked by no monument on the bank of their native river" (006-511).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also records a family detail: Wannalancet's brother, along with others, sold [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]] to relieve [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] from imprisonment for debt (003-1835), though the island was later restored to the Indians by the General Court in 1665.
