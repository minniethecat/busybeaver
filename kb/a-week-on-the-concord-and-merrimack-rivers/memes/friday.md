“The Boteman strayt

Held on his course with stayed stedfastnesse,

Ne ever shroncke, ne ever sought to bayt

His tryed armes for toylesome wearinesse;

But with his oares did sweepe the watry wildernesse.”

SPENSER.

“Summer’s robe grows

Dusky, and like an oft-dyed garment shows.”

DONNE.

[008-001]As we lay awake long before daybreak, listening to the rippling of the

[008-002]river, and the rustling of the leaves, in suspense whether the wind

[008-003]blew up or down the stream, was favorable or unfavorable to our voyage,

[008-004]we already suspected that there was a change in the weather, from a

[008-005]freshness as of autumn in these sounds. The wind in the woods sounded

[008-006]like an incessant waterfall dashing and roaring amid rocks, and we even

[008-007]felt encouraged by the unusual activity of the elements. He who hears

[008-008]the rippling of rivers in these degenerate days will not utterly

[008-009]despair. That night was the turning-point in the season. We had gone to

[008-010]bed in summer, and we awoke in autumn; for summer passes into autumn in

[008-011]some unimaginable point of time, like the turning of a leaf.

[008-012]We found our boat in the dawn just as we had left it, and as if waiting

[008-013]for us, there on the shore, in autumn, all cool and dripping with dew,

[008-014]and our tracks still fresh in the wet sand around it, the fairies all

[008-015]gone or concealed. Before five o’clock we pushed it into the fog, and,

[008-016]leaping in, at one shove were out of sight of the shores, and began to

[008-017]sweep downward with the rushing river, keeping a sharp lookout for

[008-018]rocks. We could see only the yellow gurgling water, and a solid bank of

[008-019]fog on every side, forming a small yard around us. We soon passed the

[008-020]mouth of the Souhegan, and the village of Merrimack, and as the mist

[008-021]gradually rolled away, and we were relieved from the trouble of

[008-022]watching for rocks, we saw by the flitting clouds, by the first russet

[008-023]tinge on the hills, by the rushing river, the cottages on shore, and

[008-024]the shore itself, so coolly fresh and shining with dew, and later in

[008-025]the day, by the hue of the grape-vine, the goldfinch on the willow, the

[008-026]flickers flying in flocks, and when we passed near enough to the shore,

[008-027]as we fancied, by the faces of men, that the Fall had commenced. The

[008-028]cottages looked more snug and comfortable, and their inhabitants were

[008-029]seen only for a moment, and then went quietly in and shut the door,

[008-030]retreating inward to the haunts of summer.

[008-031]“And now the cold autumnal dews are seen

[008-032]To cobweb ev’ry green;

[008-033]And by the low-shorn rowens doth appear

[008-034]The fast-declining year.”

[008-035]We heard the sigh of the first autumnal wind, and even the water had

[008-036]acquired a grayer hue. The sumach, grape, and maple were already

[008-037]changed, and the milkweed had turned to a deep rich yellow. In all

[008-038]woods the leaves were fast ripening for their fall; for their full

[008-039]veins and lively gloss mark the ripe leaf, and not the sered one of the

[008-040]poets; and we knew that the maples, stripped of their leaves among the

[008-041]earliest, would soon stand like a wreath of smoke along the edge of the

[008-042]meadow. Already the cattle were heard to low wildly in the pastures and

[008-043]along the highways, restlessly running to and fro, as if in

[008-044]apprehension of the withering of the grass and of the approach of

[008-045]winter. Our thoughts, too, began to rustle.

[008-046]As I pass along the streets of our village of [[concord-massachusetts|Concord]] on the day of our

[008-047]annual Cattle-Show, when it usually happens that the leaves of the elms

[008-048]and buttonwoods begin first to strew the ground under the breath of the

[008-049]October wind, the lively spirits in their sap seem to mount as high as

[008-050]any plough-boy’s let loose that day; and they lead my thoughts away to

[008-051]the rustling woods, where the trees are preparing for their winter

[008-052]campaign. This autumnal festival, when men are gathered in crowds in

[008-053]the streets as regularly and by as natural a law as the leaves cluster

[008-054]and rustle by the wayside, is naturally associated in my [[thought|mind]] with the

[008-055]fall of the year. The low of cattle in the streets sounds like a hoarse

[008-056]symphony or running bass to the rustling of the leaves. The wind goes

[008-057]hurrying down the country, gleaning every loose straw that is left in

[008-058]the fields, while every farmer lad too appears to scud before

[008-059]it,—having donned his best pea-jacket and pepper-and-salt waistcoat,

[008-060]his unbent trousers, outstanding rigging of duck or kerseymere or

[008-061]corduroy, and his furry hat withal,—to country fairs and cattle-shows,

[008-062]to that Rome among the villages where the treasures of the year are

[008-063]gathered. All the land over they go leaping the fences with their

[008-064]tough, idle palms, which have never learned to hang by their sides,

[008-065]amid the low of calves and the bleating of sheep,—Amos, Abner,

[008-066]Elnathan, Elbridge,—

[008-067]“From steep pine-bearing mountains to the plain.”

[008-068]I love these sons of earth every mother’s son of them, with their great

[008-069]hearty hearts rushing tumultuously in herds from spectacle to

[008-070]spectacle, as if fearful lest there should not be time between sun and

[008-071]sun to see them all, and the sun does not wait more than in

[008-072]haying-time.

[008-073]“Wise [[nature|Nature]]’s darlings, they live in the world

[008-074]Perplexing not themselves how it is hurled.”

[008-075]Running hither and thither with appetite for the coarse pastimes of the

[008-076]day, now with boisterous speed at the heels of the inspired negro from

[008-077]whose larynx the melodies of all Congo and Guinea Coast have broke

[008-078]loose into our streets; now to see the procession of a hundred yoke of

[008-079]oxen, all as august and grave as Osiris, or the droves of neat cattle

[008-080]and milch cows as unspotted as Isis or Io. Such as had no love for

[008-081]Nature

[008-082]“at all,

[008-083]Came lovers home from this great festival.”

[008-084]They may bring their fattest cattle and richest fruits to the fair, but

[008-085]they are all eclipsed by the show of men. These are stirring autumn

[008-086]days, when men sweep by in crowds, amid the rustle of leaves, like

[008-087]migrating finches; this is the true harvest of the year, when the air

[008-088]is but the breath of men, and the rustling of leaves is as the

[008-089]trampling of the crowd. We read now-a-days of the ancient festivals,

[008-090]games, and processions of the Greeks and Etruscans, with a little

[008-091]incredulity, or at least with little sympathy; but how natural and

[008-092]irrepressible in every people is some hearty and palpable greeting of

[008-093]Nature. The Corybantes, the Bacchantes, the rude primitive tragedians

[008-094]with their procession and goat-[[music|song]], and the whole paraphernalia of the

[008-095]Panathenæa, which appear so antiquated and peculiar, have their

[008-096]parallel now. The husbandman is always a better Greek than the scholar

[008-097]is prepared to appreciate, and the old custom still survives, while

[008-098]antiquarians and scholars grow gray in commemorating it. The farmers

[008-099]crowd to the fair to-day in obedience to the same ancient law, which

[008-100]Solon or Lycurgus did not enact, as naturally as bees swarm and follow

[008-101]their queen.

[008-102]It is worth the while to see the country’s people, how they pour into

[008-103]the town, the sober farmer folk, now all agog, their very shirt and

[008-104]coat-collars pointing forward,—collars so broad as if they had put

[008-105]their shirts on wrong end upward, for the fashions always tend to

[008-106]superfluity,—and with an unusual springiness in their gait, jabbering

[008-107]earnestly to one another. The more supple vagabond, too, is sure to

[008-108]appear on the least rumor of such a gathering, and the next day to

[008-109]disappear, and go into his hole like the seventeen-year locust, in an

[008-110]ever-shabby coat, though finer than the farmer’s best, yet never

[008-111]dressed; come to see the sport, and have a hand in what is going,—to

[008-112]know “what’s the row,” if there is any; to be where some men are drunk,

[008-113]some horses race, some cockerels fight; anxious to be shaking props

[008-114]under a table, and above all to see the “striped pig.” He especially is

[008-115]the creature of the occasion. He empties both his pockets and his

[008-116]character into the stream, and swims in such a day. He dearly loves the

[008-117]social slush. There is no reserve of soberness in him.

[008-118]I love to see the herd of men feeding heartily on coarse and succulent

[008-119]pleasures, as cattle on the husks and stalks of vegetables. Though

[008-120]there are many crooked and crabbled specimens of humanity among them,

[008-121]run all to thorn and rind, and crowded out of shape by adverse

[008-122]circumstances, like the third chestnut in the burr, so that you wonder

[008-123]to see some heads wear a whole hat, yet fear not that the race will

[008-124]fail or waver in them; like the crabs which grow in hedges, they

[008-125]furnish the stocks of sweet and thrifty fruits still. Thus is nature

[008-126]recruited from age to age, while the fair and palatable varieties die

[008-127]out, and have their period. This is that mankind. How cheap must be the

[008-128]material of which so many men are made.

[008-129]The wind blew steadily down the stream, so that we kept our sails set,

[008-130]and lost not a moment of the forenoon by delays, but from early morning

[008-131]until noon were continually dropping downward. With our hands on the

[008-132]steering-paddle, which was thrust deep into the river, or bending to

[008-133]the oar, which indeed we rarely relinquished, we felt each palpitation

[008-134]in the veins of our steed, and each impulse of the wings which drew us

[008-135]above. The current of our thoughts made as sudden bends as the river,

[008-136]which was continually opening new prospects to the east or south, but

[008-137]we are aware that rivers flow most rapidly and shallowest at these

[008-138]points. The steadfast shores never once turned aside for us, but still

[008-139]trended as they were made; why then should we always turn aside for

[008-140]them?

[008-141]A man cannot wheedle nor overawe his Genius. It requires to be

[008-142]conciliated by nobler conduct than the world demands or can appreciate.

[008-143]These winged thoughts are like birds, and will not be handled; even

[008-144]hens will not let you touch them like quadrupeds. Nothing was ever so

[008-145]unfamiliar and startling to a man as his own thoughts.

[008-146]To the rarest genius it is the most expensive to succumb and conform to

[008-147]the ways of the world. Genius is the worst of lumber, if the poet would

[008-148]float upon the breeze of popularity. The bird of paradise is obliged

[008-149]constantly to fly against the wind, lest its gay trappings, pressing

[008-150]close to its body, impede its free movements.

[008-151]He is the best sailor who can steer within the fewest points of the

[008-152]wind, and extract a motive power out of the greatest obstacles. Most

[008-153]begin to veer and tack as soon as the wind changes from aft, and as

[008-154]within the tropics it does not blow from all points of the compass,

[008-155]there are some harbors which they can never reach.

[008-156]The poet is no tender slip of fairy stock, who requires peculiar

[008-157]institutions and edicts for his defence, but the toughest son of earth

[008-158]and of Heaven, and by his greater strength and endurance his fainting

[008-159]companions will recognize the God in him. It is the worshippers of

[008-160]beauty, after all, who have done the real pioneer work of the world.

[008-161]The poet will prevail to be popular in spite of his faults, and in

[008-162]spite of his beauties too. He will hit the nail on the head, and we

[008-163]shall not know the shape of his hammer. He makes us free of his hearth

[008-164]and heart, which is greater than to offer one the freedom of a city.

[008-165]Great men, unknown to their generation, have their fame among the great

[008-166]who have preceded them, and all true worldly fame subsides from their

[008-167]high estimate beyond the stars.

[008-168]Orpheus does not hear the strains which issue from his lyre, but only

[008-169]those which are breathed into it; for the original strain precedes the

[008-170]sound, by as much as the echo follows after. The rest is the perquisite

[008-171]of the rocks and trees and beasts.

[008-172]When I stand in a library where is all the recorded wit of the world,

[008-173]but none of the recording, a mere accumulated, and not truly cumulative

[008-174]treasure, where immortal works stand side by side with anthologies

[008-175]which did not survive their month, and cobweb and mildew have already

[008-176]spread from these to the binding of those; and happily I am reminded of

[008-177]what poetry is,—I perceive that Shakespeare and Milton did not foresee

[008-178]into what company they were to fall. Alas! that so soon the work of a

[008-179]true poet should be swept into such a dust-hole!

[008-180]The poet will write for his peers alone. He will remember only that he

[008-181]saw truth and beauty from his position, and expect the time when a

[008-182][[dream|vision]] as broad shall overlook the same field as freely.

[008-183]We are often prompted to speak our thoughts to our neighbors, or the

[008-184]single travellers whom we meet on the road, but poetry is a

[008-185]communication from our home and solitude addressed to all Intelligence.

[008-186]It never whispers in a private ear. Knowing this, we may understand

[008-187]those sonnets said to be addressed to particular persons, or “To a

[008-188]Mistress’s Eyebrow.” Let none feel flattered by them. For poetry write

[008-189]love, and it will be equally true.

[008-190]No doubt it is an important difference between men of genius or poets,

[008-191]and men not of genius, that the latter are unable to grasp and confront

[008-192]the thought which visits them. But it is because it is too faint for

[008-193]expression, or even conscious impression. What merely quickens or

[008-194]retards the blood in their veins and fills their afternoons with

[008-195]pleasure they know not whence, conveys a distinct assurance to the

[008-196]finer organization of the poet.

[008-197]We talk of genius as if it were a mere knack, and the poet could only

[008-198]express what other men conceived. But in comparison with his task, the

[008-199]poet is the least talented of any; the writer of prose has more skill.

[008-200]See what talent the smith has. His material is pliant in his hands.

[008-201]When the poet is most inspired, is stimulated by an _aura_ which never

[008-202]even colors the afternoons of common men, then his talent is all gone,

[008-203]and he is no longer a poet. The gods do not grant him any skill more

[008-204]than another. They never put their gifts into his hands, but they

[008-205]encompass and sustain him with their breath.

[008-206]To say that God has given a man many and great talents, frequently

[008-207]means that he has brought his heavens down within reach of his hands.

[008-208]When the poetic frenzy seizes us, we run and scratch with our pen,

[008-209]intent only on worms, calling our mates around us, like the cock, and

[008-210]delighting in the dust we make, but do not detect where the jewel lies,

[008-211]which, perhaps, we have in the mean time cast to a distance, or quite

[008-212]covered up again.

[008-213]The poet’s body even is not fed like other men’s, but he sometimes

[008-214]tastes the genuine nectar and ambrosia of the gods, and lives a divine

[008-215]life. By the healthful and invigorating thrills of inspiration his life

[008-216]is preserved to a serene old age.

[008-217]Some poems are for holidays only. They are polished and sweet, but it

[008-218]is the sweetness of sugar, and not such as toil gives to sour bread.

[008-219]The breath with which the poet utters his [[poetry|verse]] must be that by which

[008-220]he lives.

[008-221]Great prose, of equal elevation, commands our respect more than great

[008-222]verse, since it implies a more permanent and level height, a life more

[008-223]pervaded with the grandeur of the thought. The poet often only makes an

[008-224]irruption, like a Parthian, and is off again, shooting while he

[008-225]retreats; but the prose writer has conquered like a Roman, and settled

[008-226]colonies.

[008-227]The true poem is not that which the public read. There is always a poem

[008-228]not printed on paper, coincident with the production of this,

[008-229]stereotyped in the poet’s life. It is _what he has become through his

[008-230]work_. Not how is the idea expressed in stone, or on canvas or paper,

[008-231]is the question, but how far it has obtained form and expression in the

[008-232]life of the artist. His true work will not stand in any prince’s

[008-233]gallery.

[008-234]My life has been the poem I would have writ,

[008-235]But I could not both live and utter it.

[008-236]THE POET’S DELAY.

[008-237]In vain I see the morning rise,

[008-238]In vain observe the western blaze,

[008-239]Who idly look to other skies,

[008-240]Expecting life by other ways.

[008-241]Amidst such boundless wealth without,

[008-242]I only still am poor within,

[008-243]The birds have sung their summer out,

[008-244]But still my spring does not begin.

[008-245]Shall I then wait the autumn wind,

[008-246]Compelled to seek a milder day,

[008-247]And leave no curious nest behind,

[008-248]No woods still echoing to my lay?

[008-249]This raw and gusty day, and the creaking of the oaks and pines on

[008-250]shore, reminded us of more northern climes than Greece, and more wintry

[008-251]seas than the Ægean.

[008-252]The genuine remains of [[ossian|Ossian]], or those ancient poems which bear his

[008-253]name, though of less fame and extent, are, in many respects, of the

[008-254]same stamp with the Iliad itself. He asserts the dignity of the bard no

[008-255]less than [[homer|Homer]], and in his era we hear of no other priest than he. It

[008-256]will not avail to call him a heathen, because he personifies the sun

[008-257]and addresses it; and what if his heroes did “worship the ghosts of

[008-258]their fathers,” their thin, airy, and unsubstantial forms? we worship

[008-259]but the ghosts of our fathers in more substantial forms. We cannot but

[008-260]respect the vigorous [[religion|faith]] of those heathen, who sternly believed

[008-261]somewhat, and are inclined to say to the critics, who are offended by

[008-262]their superstitious rites,—Don’t interrupt these men’s prayers. As if

[008-263]we knew more about human life and a God, than the heathen and ancients.

[008-264]Does English theology contain the recent discoveries!

[008-265]Ossian reminds us of the most refined and rudest eras, of Homer,

[008-266]Pindar, Isaiah, and the American Indian. In his poetry, as in Homer’s,

[008-267]only the simplest and most enduring features of humanity are seen, such

[008-268]essential parts of a man as Stonehenge exhibits of a temple; we see the

[008-269]circles of stone, and the upright shaft alone. The phenomena of life

[008-270]acquire almost an unreal and gigantic size seen through his mists. Like

[008-271]all older and grander poetry, it is distinguished by the few elements

[008-272]in the lives of its heroes. They stand on the heath, between the stars

[008-273]and the earth, shrunk to the bones and sinews. The earth is a boundless

[008-274]plain for their deeds. They lead such a simple, dry, and everlasting

[008-275]life, as hardly needs depart with the flesh, but is transmitted entire

[008-276]from age to age. There are but few objects to distract their sight, and

[008-277]their life is as unencumbered as the course of the stars they gaze at.

[008-278]“The wrathful kings, on cairns apart,

[008-279]Look forward from behind their shields,

[008-280]And mark the wandering stars,

[008-281]That brilliant westward move.”

[008-282]It does not cost much for these heroes to live; they do not want much

[008-283]furniture. They are such forms of men only as can be seen afar through

[008-284]the mist, and have no costume nor dialect, but for language there is

[008-285]the tongue itself, and for costume there are always the skins of beasts

[008-286]and the bark of trees to be had. They live out their years by the vigor

[008-287]of their constitutions. They survive storms and the spears of their

[008-288]foes, and perform a few heroic deeds, and then

[008-289]“Mounds will answer questions of them,

[008-290]For many [[time|future]] years.”

[008-291]Blind and infirm, they spend the remnant of their days listening to the

[008-292]lays of the bards, and feeling the weapons which laid their enemies

[008-293]low, and when at length they die, by a convulsion of nature, the bard

[008-294]allows us a short and misty glance into futurity, yet as clear,

[008-295]perchance, as their lives had been. When Mac-Roine was slain,

[008-296]“His soul departed to his warlike sires,

[008-297]To follow misty forms of boars,

[008-298]In tempestuous islands bleak.”

[008-299]The hero’s cairn is erected, and the bard sings a brief significant

[008-300]strain, which will suffice for epitaph and biography.

[008-301]“The weak will find his bow in the dwelling,

[008-302]The feeble will attempt to bend it.”

[008-303]Compared with this simple, fibrous life, our civilized history appears

[008-304]the chronicle of debility, of fashion, and the [[art|arts]] of luxury. But the

[008-305]civilized man misses no real refinement in the poetry of the rudest

[008-306]era. It reminds him that civilization does but dress men. It makes

[008-307]shoes, but it does not toughen the soles of the feet. It makes cloth of

[008-308]finer texture, but it does not touch the skin. Inside the civilized man

[008-309]stand the savage still in the place of honor. We are those blue-eyed,

[008-310]yellow-haired Saxons, those slender, dark-haired Normans.

[008-311]The profession of the bard attracted more respect in those days from

[008-312]the importance attached to fame. It was his province to record the

[008-313]deeds of heroes. When Ossian hears the traditions of inferior bards, he

[008-314]exclaims,—

[008-315]“I straightway seize the unfutile tales,

[008-316]And send them down in faithful verse.”

[008-317]His philosophy of life is expressed in the opening of the third Duan of

[008-318]Ca-Lodin.

[008-319]“Whence have sprung the things that are?

[008-320]And whither roll the passing years?

[008-321]Where does Time conceal its two heads,

[008-322]In dense impenetrable gloom,

[008-323]Its surface marked with heroes’ deeds alone?

[008-324]I view the generations gone;

[008-325]The [[history|past]] appears but dim;

[008-326]As objects by the moon’s faint beams,

[008-327]Reflected from a distant lake.

[008-328]I see, indeed, the thunderbolts of war,

[008-329]But there the unmighty joyless dwell,

[008-330]All those who send not down their deeds

[008-331]To far, succeeding times.”

[008-332]The ignoble warriors die and are forgotten;

[008-333]“Strangers come to build a tower,

[008-334]And throw their ashes overhand;

[008-335]Some rusted swords appear in dust;

[008-336]One, bending forward, says,

[008-337]‘The arms belonged to heroes gone;

[008-338]We never heard their praise in song.’”

[008-339]The grandeur of the similes is another feature which characterizes

[008-340]great poetry. Ossian seems to speak a gigantic and universal language.

[008-341]The images and pictures occupy even much space in the landscape, as if

[008-342]they could be seen only from the sides of mountains, and plains with a

[008-343]wide horizon, or across arms of the sea. The machinery is so massive

[008-344]that it cannot be less than natural. Oivana says to the spirit of her

[008-345]father, “Gray-haired Torkil of Torne,” seen in the skies,

[008-346]“Thou glidest away like receding ships.”

[008-347]So when the hosts of Fingal and Starne approach to battle,

[008-348]“With murmurs loud, like rivers far,

[008-349]The race of Torne hither moved.”

[008-350]And when compelled to retire,

[008-351]“dragging his spear behind,

[008-352]Cudulin sank in the distant wood,

[008-353]Like a fire upblazing ere it dies.”

[008-354]Nor did Fingal want a proper audience when he spoke;

[008-355]“A thousand orators inclined

[008-356]To hear the lay of Fingal.”

[008-357]The threats too would have deterred a man. Vengeance and terror were

[008-358]real. Trenmore threatens the young warrior whom he meets on a foreign

[008-359]strand,

[008-360]“Thy mother shall find thee pale on the shore,

[008-361]While lessening on the waves she spies

[008-362]The sails of him who slew her son.”

[008-363]If Ossian’s heroes weep, it is from excess of strength, and not from

[008-364]weakness, a sacrifice or libation of fertile natures, like the

[008-365]perspiration of stone in summer’s heat. We hardly know that tears have

[008-366]been shed, and it seems as if weeping were proper only for babes and

[008-367]heroes. Their joy and their sorrow are made of one stuff, like rain and

[008-368]snow, the rainbow and the mist. When Fillan was worsted in fight, and

[008-369]ashamed in the presence of Fingal,

[008-370]“He strode away forthwith,

[008-371]And bent in grief above a stream,

[008-372]His cheeks bedewed with tears.

[008-373]From time to time the thistles gray

[008-374]He lopped with his inverted lance.”

[008-375]Crodar, blind and old, receives Ossian, son of Fingal, who comes to aid

[008-376]him in war;—

[008-377]“‘My eyes have failed,’ says he, ‘Crodar is blind,

[008-378]Is thy strength like that of thy fathers?

[008-379]Stretch, Ossian, thine arm to the hoary-haired.’

[008-380]I gave my arm to the king.

[008-381]The aged hero seized my hand;
