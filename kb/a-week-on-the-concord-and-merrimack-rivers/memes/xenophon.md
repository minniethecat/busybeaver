## Biography

Xenophon (c. 430–354 BCE) was an Athenian historian, soldier, and philosopher. A student of [[socrates|Socrates]], he is best known for the *Anabasis*, his firsthand account of the expedition of the Ten Thousand — a Greek mercenary army's march into Persia and their harrowing retreat to the Black Sea. His other works include the *Hellenica* (a history of Greece), the *Cyropaedia* (a fictionalized biography of Cyrus the Great), and the *Symposium* (a Socratic dialogue on love and virtue).

## Works / Significance

The *Anabasis* is one of the most celebrated adventure narratives of [[history|antiquity]]. Its account of leadership, survival, and the difficulties of moving an army through hostile territory has influenced military and travel writing for millennia. Xenophon's Socratic writings, though less philosophically rigorous than [[plato|Plato]]'s, provide valuable firsthand portraits of Athenian intellectual life. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in Xenophon reflects his broader engagement with classical literature as a source of models for the examined life.

## In Thoreau's Text

Xenophon is referenced twice in *A Week*, each citation drawing on a different aspect of his work.

In *Tuesday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] compares the delays and obstacles of his own river journey to the military maneuvers of the *Anabasis*:

> A good share of our interest in Xenophon's story of his retreat is in the manœuvres to get the army safely over the rivers. (005-1010)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s identification with Xenophon is playful but pointed: his own boat journey, like the Greek army's retreat, involves navigating waterways — though his "army" consists only of himself and his brother.

In *Thursday*, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Xenophon's *Symposium* for a characteristically irreverent comparison. Reflecting on the beauties of a cranberry bog, he remarks that even the most celebrated classical dialogues cannot match nature's wit:

> The society of those Greek sages, such as described in the Banquet of Xenophon, would not be comparable with the dry wit of decayed cranberry vines, and the fresh Attic salt of the moss-beds. (007-052)

The pun on "Attic salt" — the proverbial wit of Athens — applied to moss-beds rather than Athenian symposiasts, is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] at his most playful. Xenophon's banquet of philosophers is outdone by the simple, wordless conversation of the [[nature|natural world]].
