## Biography

Francesco Petrarca (1304-1374), known in English as Petrarch, was an Italian poet, scholar, and one of the earliest humanists. Born in Arezzo, he spent much of his life in Avignon and the Vaucluse region of France.

His vernacular poetry, particularly the *Canzoniere* — a sequence of 366 poems addressed to his idealized beloved Laura — established the sonnet form that dominated European love poetry for centuries.

His rediscovery of the letters of Cicero and his own extensive Latin writings helped ignite the Renaissance revival of classical learning.

## Works / Significance

The influence of Petrarch on Western literature is difficult to overstate. The Petrarchan sonnet became the dominant lyric form of the Renaissance, shaping poets from Wyatt and Sidney to Ronsard. His *Secretum*, a dialogue with Saint Augustine, invented a new mode of introspective autobiography.

## In Thoreau's Text

Petrarch appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] surveys the literary and [[history|historical]] [[nature|landscape]] of the fourteenth century, tracing the simultaneous emergence of vernacular literature across Europe.

He writes of England's literary awakening "while Petrarch and Boccacio lived in Italy, and Tell and Tamerlane in Switzerland and in Asia" (008-994). Petrarch is placed within a constellation of figures — poets, patriots, conquerors — who defined an age.

The passage creates a synchronic map of the fourteenth century, collapsing geographical distance to reveal a world undergoing simultaneous transformation.
