## Description

Wicasuck Island contains approximately seventy acres, situated in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] on the right-hand side as one travels upstream, between present-day [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]] and [[tyngsborough|Tyngsborough, Massachusetts]]. (003-1829) The island is associated with Wicasuck Falls, a natural feature near the island that marked this stretch of the river. (003-1840)

The island's name derives from the Native American presence that persisted there well into the colonial period. It was "a favorite residence of the Indians," serving as a settlement site for the [[penacook-tribe-history|Pennacook]] people and their allies in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. (003-1829)

## On the River Route

The brothers passed Wicasuck Island on Sunday, the second day of their journey, entering a narrower part of the river near the [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]] granite quarries. (003-1829) The island marks the transition from the broader reaches of the lower [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] to the more constricted channel approaching the New Hampshire border.

On the return journey, on Friday, the brothers again encountered Wicasuck Island. They passed "just above the head of Wicasuck Island" where scows were loading clay from the opposite shore, and paused to observe the activity. (008-637) During this passage, they encountered a pleasure-boat and made inquiries about the island from its occupants, who confirmed details of its history. (008-661) (008-665)

## Historical Context

Wicasuck Island figures prominently in the colonial and Native American history of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. Around 1663, the island became entangled in a debt dispute: a Native American man was imprisoned for a debt of £45 owed to John Tinker, and his brother [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] and other tribal members sold Wicasuck Island to satisfy the obligation. (003-1836) The General Court restored the island to the Indians in 1665.

After the Native Americans departed the region in 1683, the island was granted to [[jonathan-tyng|Jonathan Tyng]] in payment for his services to the colony in maintaining a garrison at his house. Tyng's house stood not far from Wicasuck Falls. (003-1840) During [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]] in 1675, the area around the island saw military activity, as recorded by [[gookin|Daniel Gookin]] in his [[history|historical]] account of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian Indians.

On the return journey, the brothers observed the island still covered with wood, as men had been avoiding the current by passing on the far side. (003-1882)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account of Wicasuck Island weaves together the natural [[nature|landscape]] and the human history inscribed upon it. The island serves as a touchpoint for [[thought|reflection]]s on the displacement of Native Americans, the transactions of colonial land tenure, and the persistence of the [[history|past]] in the present [[nature|landscape]]. His reference to [[gookin|Gookin]]'s "Epistle Dedicatory to Robert Boyle," in which [[gookin|Gookin]] apologizes for presenting his "matter clothed in a [[nature|wilderness]] dress," captures the tension between colonial order and the wildness of the frontier. (003-1840)
