## Description

Londonderry is a town in Rockingham County, New Hampshire, located west of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. Originally chartered in 1722 as Nutfield, it was renamed Londonderry after the city in Northern Ireland, reflecting the origins of its early Scotch-Irish settlers. The town was one of several in the region — alongside Pelham, [[windham|Windham]], and others — established by Presbyterian immigrants of Scottish descent who had previously settled in Ulster. (003-1293) (006-499)

## On the River Route

Londonderry lies several miles east of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. While not directly on the river route, the town is part of the broader [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley region through which the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers traveled. Its Scotch-Irish heritage connects it to the colonial settlement patterns that shaped the communities along the river, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references its history and notable residents in his [[thought|reflection]]s on New England's early character. (003-1293)

## Historical Context

The town was first settled under the name Nutfield in 1719 by a group of Scotch-Irish families led by James McKeen. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that as early as 1752, a young resident of Londonderry was taken captive, reflecting the frontier dangers of the period. (006-499)

Londonderry produced several notable military figures of the Revolutionary era. One resident, born in Ireland, came to Londonderry with his father and later served with distinction. Another Londonderry native had been an officer in the siege of the city of Londonderry in Ireland during the Williamite War (1689), when the city held out against the army of King James II. These men brought their military experience to the American cause, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records that the officers from Londonderry were "distinguished and brave."
