# Dream

## Definition

Dream and dreaming occupy a liminal space in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s imagination — between sleep and waking, between mere [[imagination|fancy]] and genuine vision. The river journey itself has a dreamlike quality: the slow drift of the boat, the play of light on water, the way thoughts merge with perceptions until the boundary between inner and outer blurs.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s "faint and dream-like effort" to perceive beauty and truth is not a confession of failure but a description of the proper mode of perception — a receptive,半conscious attention that allows meaning to arise rather than forcing it into predetermined categories. (003-443)

**Friday** returns to dream as a mode of poetic perception. The poet's vision is dream-like in its intensity and its independence from ordinary logic.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conception of dream draws on the Romantic valorization of the unconscious and the half-waking state. Coleridge's "Kubla Khan" — composed, he claimed, in an opium dream — had already established dream as a legitimate mode of poetic composition. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] extends this Romantic logic: if dream can produce poetry, it can also produce truth.

The Transcendentalist emphasis on intuition over discursive reason similarly valorizes dreamlike modes of knowing. Emerson's "transparent eyeball" passage in *[[nature|Nature]]* describes a state not unlike lucid dreaming — a dissolution of self into pure perception.
