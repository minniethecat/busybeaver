## Biography

Sir Walter Scott was born in Edinburgh, Scotland, in 1771. Trained as a lawyer, he rose to prominence first as a poet with works such as *The Lay of the Last Minstrel* (1805) and *Marmion* (1808), then turned to the novel and became the most widely read novelist of his age. His Waverley novels — including *Ivanhoe*, *Rob Roy*, and *The Heart of Midlothian* — established the [[history|historical]] novel as a major literary genre. Scott's influence extended across Europe and America; his depiction of medieval chivalry, Highland scenery, and national character shaped the Romantic imagination throughout the nineteenth century. He died at Abbotsford, his estate in the Scottish Borders, in 1832.

## Works / Significance

Scott's major achievement was the invention of the [[history|historical]] novel. Beginning anonymously with *Waverley* (1814), he produced over twenty novels that recreated [[history|past]] eras with unprecedented vividness, from the Crusades (*The Talisman*) to the Jacobite rebellions (*Waverley*, *Redgauntlet*). His novels influenced writers from Balzac and Hugo in France to Cooper and [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Hawthorne]] in America. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who read widely in English literature, would have encountered Scott as a ubiquitous presence in early-nineteenth-century literary culture — the most popular novelist writing in English.

## In Thoreau's Text

Scott appears twice in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*. In the "Wednesday" chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] includes Scott's novels in a list of works that "entertain us" while reflecting that every person is already a dramatist and fabler in their own life (006-830):

> No wonder that the Mythology, and Arabian Nights, and Shakespeare, and Scott's novels entertain us,—we are poets and fablers and dramatists and novelists ourselves.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s point is not that fiction is escapist, but that its appeal lies in its correspondence to the [[imagination|imaginative]] activity we already practice in daily life. In the "Friday" chapter, Scott appears in a different context: [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] surveys early English and "contemporary Scottish" poetry while discussing Chaucer's superiority to the [[poetry|verse]] that preceded him (008-977).
