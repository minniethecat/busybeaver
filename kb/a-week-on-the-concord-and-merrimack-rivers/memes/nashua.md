# Nashua

Nashua, New Hampshire, stands at the confluence of the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]] and the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] — a place where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s river journey intersected with layers of colonial history, Abenaki presence, and the burgeoning industrial transformation of New England.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother John passed through Nashua on both the outward and return legs of their [[the-1839-boat-journey|1839 voyage]].

The town serves as a pivot point in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*: below it, the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] broadens into the industrialized corridor of [[lowell-massachusetts|Lowell]]; above it, the river narrows into a wilder, less settled [[nature|landscape]].

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account weaves together the place's [[beauty|natural beauty]], its early settlement by Europeans, and the lingering presence of the Indigenous peoples who fished and farmed the valley long before the first white settlers arrived.

## Description

Nashua occupies a strategic position in southern New Hampshire, straddling the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] just below the mouth of the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]] — one of the largest tributaries of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]].

The [[nashua-river|Nashua River]] itself rises from [[mount-wachusett|Wachusett Mountain]] in central Massachusetts, flowing through [[lancaster|Lancaster]], Groton, and other towns before joining the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at Nashua.

In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the town was transitioning from its agricultural and trading roots into a manufacturing center. He notes this transformation with characteristic ambivalence:

> ...while the then uninhabited mouth of the Nashua now resounds with the din of a manufacturing town. (004-1184)

The "din" of industry had replaced the [[silence|quiet]] of the earlier settlement, a shift [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] registers without fully condemning — it is simply the fact of the place as he found it.

## On the River Route

The brothers encountered Nashua across multiple days of their journey, approaching it from downstream on Sunday and passing through on Thursday.

**Sunday — First Sight from Below.** Coming upriver from [[lowell-massachusetts|Lowell]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the string of manufacturing towns along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]:

> ...Nashua, and Manchester, and Concord, gleaming one above the other. (003-1214)

From the river, the towns appeared as points of light — "gleaming" suggests both the visual impression of white buildings on the hillsides and the emerging industrial character of these settlements.

**Thursday — The Village and Beyond.** On the return journey, the brothers passed the village of Nashua and its covered bridge. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the geographical significance of the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]]'s mouth:

> We soon after passed the village of Nashua, on the river of the same name, where there is a covered bridge over the Merrimack. The Nashua, which is one of the largest tributaries, flows from Wachusett Mountain, through Lancaster, Groton, and other towns, where it has formed well-known elm-shaded meadows... (004-1190)

But he also records the obstruction at the river's mouth — "falls and factories" that prevented exploration upstream — a [[silence|quiet]] lament for a river whose [[beauty|natural beauty]] had been compromised by industry.

**The Hidden Valley.** Before the journey, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] had often gazed westward from the [[concord-mass|Concord]] hills toward the distant blue mountains, unaware of the Nashua valley concealed between:

> ...between two neighboring pines in the horizon, lay the valley of the Nashua, and this very stream was even then winding at its bottom, and then, as now, it was here silently mingling its waters with the Merrimack. (004-1204)

The valley, hidden "as it were, by a turf wall," had been gradually revealed through the journey — a fitting metaphor for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s entire project of discovery through travel.

## Historical Context

Nashua's history, as [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encountered it, was layered: Indigenous presence, early colonial settlement, and the scars of frontier violence.

**Indigenous Presence.** [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]], which enters the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at Nashua, was "a favorite haunt of the aborigines" (004-1157). Its name is said to be a translation from the Indian language, and the brook's fertile meadows had drawn Native fishermen and farmers for generations before European contact.

**First White Settlement.** The first white settlers planted at the mouth of [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]], and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records the archaeological traces still visible in his time:

> Here, too, the first white settlers of Nashua planted, and some dents in the earth where their houses stood and the wrecks of ancient apple-trees are still visible. (004-1157)

**[[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]]'s Homestead.** [[About]] a mile up [[salmon-brook|Salmon Brook]] stood the house of old [[john-lovewell|John Lovewell]], father of the famous Captain [[lovewells-fight|John Lovewell]] of Pequawket fame. An ensign in Oliver Cromwell's army, the elder [[love|Love]]well settled here before 1690 and lived to the extraordinary age of one hundred and twenty:

> He cobbled shoes without glasses at a hundred, and cut a handsome swath at a hundred and five! (004-1157)

[[love|Love]]well's house was said to be the first which [[hannah-dustan-captivity|Hannah Dustan]] reached on her escape from Indian captivity — a crossing of two of the most famous narratives of frontier New England, both anchored to this single point on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]].

**Frontier Violence.** Nearby stood the gravestone of Joseph Hassell and his family, "slain by our Indian enemies on September 2d, [1691,] in the evening" (004-1157). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes [[gookin|Gookin]]'s somber observation: "The Indian rod upon the English backs had not yet done God's errand."

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s treatment of Nashua is characteristic of his method throughout *A Week*: the place is not described in isolation but as a node in a network of natural, [[history|historical]], and personal associations.

**The Railroad and Change.** The [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] and Nashua railroad was a recent development when [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] made his journey, and he notes how it had already transformed travel along the river corridor:

> ...between Lowell and Nashua, before the railroad was built, and one now feels as if he were nearer the ocean. (003-1200)

The railroad compressed distance—made Nashua feel closer to the sea—but also marked the loss of an older, slower mode of travel that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s boat journey was, in part, an attempt to recover.

**A View from the Return Journey.** On the final day of the voyage (Friday), as the brothers drifted back toward [[concord-mass|Concord]], Nashua appeared in the distance as a point of orientation — one of the landmarks by which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] measured the closing arc of the journey.

**The Stream as [[memory|Memory]].** Perhaps the most elegiac passage about Nashua comes when [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on the valley as it appeared to him in childhood:

> The clouds which floated over its meadows and were born there, seen far in the west, gilded by the rays of the setting sun, had adorned a thousand evening skies for us. (004-1204)

Nashua, in this light, is not only a place [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] passed through on the river but a [[nature|landscape]] that shaped his imagination long before the journey began — a valley hidden between the pines, slowly revealed through the course of a life.
