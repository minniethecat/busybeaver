## Overview

The Great Meadows are a broad wetland floodplain along the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] in
[[concord-massachusetts|Concord, Massachusetts]]. The meadows form a wide, level expanse where the
[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] spreads across the floodplain, creating fertile hay lands that
have been harvested for generations.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions the Great Meadows multiple times in describing the [[concord-mass|Concord]]
River [[nature|landscape]]. In the opening chapter, he notes the meadows as part of the
river's character:

> (001-011) Great Meadows, and get the hay from year to year. "One branch of it,"
> according to the historian of Concord, for I love to quote so good
> authority, "rises in the south part of Hopkinton, and another from a
> pond and a large cedar-swamp in Westborough."

Later, on Saturday, he describes the view as the river enters the Great
Meadows near the [[poplar-hill|Poplar Hill]]:

> (002-123) Ponkawtasset and the Poplar Hill, into the Great Meadows, which,
> like a broad moccason print, have levelled a fertile and juicy place in
> nature.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s comparison of the meadow to a "broad moccason print" evokes the
Native American presence on the land, and his description emphasizes the
natural richness of the floodplain.

## Geography

The Great Meadows lie along the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] between the mouths of the
[[assabet-river|Assabet River]] (north) and the [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]] (south). The meadows flood
seasonally during spring freshets, when the river "overflows its banks"
and becomes "nearly a mile wide" in places (001-023). This annual flooding
deposits silt and nutrients that maintain the meadow's fertility.

## Historical Use

The Great Meadows have long been valued for their hay crop. [[concord-mass|Concord]] farmers
harvested the meadows annually, relying on the rich alluvial soil replenished
by seasonal flooding. The hay from these meadows supported local agriculture
and livestock through the colonial period and into [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time.
