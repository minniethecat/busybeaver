## Biography

Anacreon was a Greek lyric poet born in Teos, an Ionian city on the coast of Asia Minor, around 582 BCE.
He spent much of his career at the courts of patrons, including Polycrates of Samos and Hipparchus of Athens, where he composed [[music|song]]s celebrating love, wine, and the pleasures of life.

Anacreon lived to an unusually old age — reportedly eighty-five — and his reputation as a poet of conviviality persisted long after his death.
The *Anacreontea*, a collection of about sixty poems in his style, were composed by later imitators and were long mistaken for his genuine work.

These imitations shaped the European image of "Anacreontic" [[poetry|verse]] for centuries, influencing poets from the Renaissance through the Romantic era.

## Works / Significance

Anacreon's authentic surviving work consists of fragments — elegies, iambic poems, and monodic lyrics — that reveal a craftsman of considerable range.
His characteristic themes include the pleasures of the symposium, the pains and joys of love, and the rejection of martial subjects in favor of the personal and sensual.

The Anacreontic meter was widely imitated, and the "Anacreontic" style became a recognized mode in European poetry from the Renaissance onward.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], well-read in classical poetry, encounters Anacreon through [[simonides|Simonides]]'s commemorative epigram in the "Tuesday" chapter.

## In Thoreau's Text

Anacreon appears in the "Tuesday" chapter through [[simonides|Simonides]]'s epigram, one poet commemorating another across the ages.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records "[[simonides|Simonides]]' Epigram on Anacreon" (005-1269), using the commemoration as an emblem of literary immortality and [[love|friendship]].

The epigram describes how "Anacreon sent me" poetic gifts across time (005-1366), his [[poetry|verse]]s reaching posterity centuries after his death.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] further invokes the gift "for Anacreon" (005-1372) and the enduring presence "of Anacreon himself" (005-1385), emphasizing the poet's lasting influence across ages.

Though the references are brief, they contribute to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s broader theme of poetry as a bridge across cultures, a continuous human conversation sustained through [[music|song]] and memory.
