## Biography

Marcus Terentius Varro (116–27 BC) was Rome's most learned antiquarian — a polymath who wrote over seventy works covering agriculture, linguistics, Roman religion, history, and philosophy. Julius Caesar appointed him to o[[poetry|verse]]e Rome's first public library, and Cicero praised him as "the most acute student of [[history|antiquity]]."

Varro's *Antiquitates Rerum Humanarum et Divinarum* (Antiquities of Human and Divine Things) was his masterwork, a comprehensive encyclopedia of Roman civilization now almost entirely lost. Only his *De Re Rustica* (On Agriculture) and part of *De Lingua Latina* (On the Latin [[language|Language]]) survive intact. Augustine later relied heavily on Varro's *Antiquitates* in *The City of God*.

## In Thoreau's Text

Varro is mentioned once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes his authority as an antiquarian to illustrate a point about Roman virtue (008-655):

> described by Varro:—Cæsar Vopiscus Ædilicius, when he pleaded before the censors, said that the thing was a sign of frugality.

The fragment [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] alludes to comes from Varro's anecdotal repertoire — a vignette of Republican Roman character. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who admired the austerity of ancient Rome, deploys Varro as a window onto a world of civic simplicity that resonates with his own ideals. The erudition is characteristic: [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws on the most learned Roman source available to buttress his argument.
