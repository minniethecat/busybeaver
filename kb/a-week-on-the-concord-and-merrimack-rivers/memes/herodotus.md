## Biography

Herodotus (c. 484–425 BCE) was a Greek historian born in Halicarnassus (modern Bodrum, Turkey) in the Persian Empire. He is the author of the *Histories*, a sprawling account of the Greco-Persian Wars that also encompasses the geography, customs, and history of the known world from Egypt to Scythia. Cicero called him the "Father of [[history|History]]," a title he retains in the Western tradition.

The *Histories* is notable for its ethnographic breadth and Herodotus's practice of reporting what he was told, even when he himself was skeptical. He traveled extensively throughout the Mediterranean and Near East, gathering stories and observations that make the *Histories* an invaluable window into the sixth and fifth centuries BCE. His work established history as a distinct genre of inquiry (*historia* meaning "inquiry" or "investigation").

## Works / Significance

Herodotus's *Histories* is divided into nine books, each named after one of the Muses. It traces the rise of the Persian Empire and its conflict with the Greek city-states, culminating in the battles of Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis, and Plataea. Beyond military history, the work is filled with ethnographic digressions, marvels, and moral [[thought|reflection]]s on the instability of human fortune.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who read widely in classical literature, would have encountered Herodotus as part of the Greek [[history|historical]] canon that included Thucydides and [[xenophon|Xenophon]]. Herodotus's method — traveling, observing, and recording — resonates with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own practice of attentive travel and [[thought|reflection]].

## In Thoreau's Text

Herodotus receives a single but significant acknowledgment in *A Week*. In "Sunday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes that "if Herodotus carried his history to" Olympia and read it aloud at the games of Greece — an image of the historian as public performer, sharing his inquiries with the assembled Greek world. (003-1553) The passage evokes the communal, almost festive context in which ancient [[history|historical]] knowledge was first transmitted.

Though the reference to Herodotus in *A Week* is brief, it reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s engagement with classical [[history|historiography]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own method in *A Week* — blending observation, digression, quotation, and moral [[thought|reflection]] — owes something to the Herodotean model of inquiry that moves freely between narrative and meditation. Herodotus traveled, listened, recorded, and performed his findings; [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] traveled, observed, reflected, and published his.

The single reference is consistent with the spare, allusive quality of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s classical citations throughout *A Week*. Like many ancient authors, Herodotus is present more as a horizon of learning than as a sustained subject of discussion, yet the allusion to the historian reading his work at the Olympic games suggests [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own aspiration: that his river journey, too, might find an audience worthy of its measured attention to the world.
