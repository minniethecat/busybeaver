## Biography

Emanuel Swedenborg (1688-1772) was a Swedish scientist, philosopher, and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian mystic. Initially trained in physics and engineering, he served on the Board of Mines of Sweden and published extensively on natural philosophy.

In his mid-fifties, Swedenborg experienced a spiritual awakening and devoted the remainder of his life to theological writing. His major works include *Arcana Cœlestia* and *Heaven and Hell*.

His [[dream|vision]]s of the afterlife influenced Romantic and Transcendentalist thought in the nineteenth century. Emerson and the elder Henry James engaged seriously with Swedenborgian ideas, though [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] maintained a more skeptical distance.

## Works / Significance

The theological writings of Swedenborg describe a detailed spiritual cosmology of heavens, hells, and intermediate states. His doctrine of correspondences — that every natural object has a spiritual counterpart — resonated with Transcendentalist ideas of nature as divine symbol.

## In Thoreau's Text

Swedenborg appears twice in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in both cases positioned among the most significant thinkers of human history.

In the "Sunday" chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on the relationship between institutional religion and genuine spiritual [[wisdom|insight]]: "Swedenborg, who are without the pale of their churches" (003-667). The passage suggests that authentic spiritual [[dream|vision]] does not require orthodox affiliation.

In "Friday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Swedenborg in a catalog of luminous [[thought|mind]]s: "[[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]], Swedenborg,—these are some of our astronomers" (008-1416). The term "astronomers" is used metaphorically for explorers of the inner cosmos. The pairing with [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] underscores the stature of Swedenborg in the intellectual uni[[poetry|verse]] of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]].
