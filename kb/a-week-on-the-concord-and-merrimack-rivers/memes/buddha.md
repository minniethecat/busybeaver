## Biography

Siddhartha Gautama, known as the Buddha ("the Awakened One"), was a spiritual teacher in ancient India whose teachings form the foundation of Buddhism. Born a prince in the Shakya clan in present-day Nepal, he renounced his royal life at the age of twenty-nine to seek liberation from suffering. After years of ascetic practice and meditation, he attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya. For the remaining forty-five years of his life, he traveled the Gangetic Plain teaching the Four Noble [[truth|Truth]]s and the Eightfold Path.

## Works / Significance

The Buddha wrote nothing himself; his teachings were preserved orally by his disciples and compiled into the Tripitaka ("Three Baskets") centuries later. His central [[wisdom|insight]] — that suffering arises from craving and can be ended through ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom — became one of the world's great [[religion|religious]] and philosophical traditions, spreading from India throughout East and Southeast Asia.

## In Thoreau's Text

Buddha appears in the "Sunday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places him alongside [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as an equal spiritual figure. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes (003-666):

> I trust that some may be as near and dear to Buddha, or Christ, or Swedenborg, who are without the pale of their churches.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] deepens this comparison in the following passage, declaring his willingness to honor both figures without partisan preference (003-670):

> I know that some will have hard thoughts of me, when they hear their Christ named beside my Buddha, yet I am sure that I am willing they should love their Christ more than my Buddha, for the love is the main thing, and I like him too. (003-670–671)

This pairing of Buddha with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — and the insistence that "love is the main thing" rather than sectarian allegiance — reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s universalist [[religion|religious]] sensibility. He praises the capacity for spiritual [[love|devotion]] regardless of tradition, and his mention of Buddha is notable in a mid-nineteenth-century New England context where knowledge of Buddhism was still uncommon among his readership.
