# Early Exploration of New England

## Overview

The European exploration of the New England coast began in earnest in the early seventeenth century, with several expeditions mapping the coastline and making first contact with Native American inhabitants. The most significant of these for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s purposes was [[john-smith|Captain John Smith]]'s 1614 voyage, during which Smith explored and mapped the coast from Penobscot Bay to Cape Cod, giving the region the name "New England." [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references this expedition in the Sunday chapter as part of his broader meditation on the encounter between European and Native American worlds.

Earlier explorers had touched on the New England coast — including Giovanni da Verrazzano in 1524 and Bartholomew Gosnold in 1602 — but Smith's 1614 expedition produced the first detailed map of the region and the first systematic account of its geography and peoples.

## Relevance to the Journey

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes the early exploration of New England in several contexts throughout [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]]:

**Sunday** contains the most direct engagement with [[john-smith|John Smith]]'s expedition. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that Smith explored the coast "in 1614, when he made a map of the country" and gave it the name New England. (003-1267) This places Smith's voyage as a pivotal moment in the transformation of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley from Native American homeland to English colonial territory. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Smith's map and descriptions as part of the [[history|historical]] record that overlays his own experience of the [[nature|landscape]]. (003-1268) The explorer's accounts serve as a baseline against which [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] measures the changes wrought by two centuries of settlement. (003-1269)

**Thursday** returns to the theme of early exploration in the context of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s discovery by Europeans. The river, so intimately known to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] by the 1840s, was once an unknown quantity to the first European explorers who traced its course. (007-132)

**[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]** introduces the geographical stage on which exploration occurred. The first English settlement on the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] was established "in 1635," within living memory of Smith's voyage, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that the earliest European visitors were attracted by "the fame of the meadows and the fish" — practical motivations that contrast with the more poetic relationship he cultivates with the same [[nature|landscape]]. (001-003) (001-004)

The early exploration narratives serve [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as a counterpoint to his own journey. Where Smith and his contemporaries sailed the coast as strangers, mapping an unknown territory, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] travels the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] as a native son, intimately familiar with its banks and currents. Yet paradoxically, he feels a deeper kinship with the Native inhabitants who knew the river before the Europeans arrived than with the explorers who first recorded it in writing. This tension between discovery and belonging runs throughout *A Week*.

## Historical Background

[[john-smith|Captain John Smith]] (1580–1631) is best known for his role in the founding of Jamestown, Virginia, but his later voyages to New England were equally significant. In 1614, Smith sailed with two ships to the coast of what was then called "Northern Virginia." He explored and mapped the coastline, trading with Native Americans and gathering detailed information about the geography and resources of the region.

Smith's map of New England, published in 1616, was remarkably accurate for its time and remained the standard map of the region for decades. It was the first to use the name "New England" (which Smith himself proposed) and included place names that have survived to the present day, including [[plymouth|Plymouth]], Cape Cod, and the Charles River.

Smith never explored the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] beyond its mouth — he mapped the coastline but did not venture inland. However, his descriptions of the coast and its inhabitants provided the first detailed European account of the region that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] would later make his home and his subject.

The early exploration period (c. 1600–1620) represents a brief window of contact before large-scale English settlement began. When Smith visited in 1614, the Native American population of coastal New England was still largely intact. Within a generation, epidemic disease — particularly the 1616–1619 plague that devastated coastal tribes — and the arrival of the Pilgrims (1620) and Puritans (1630) would transform the region beyond recognition.
