## Description

Plum Island is a barrier island at the mouth of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], stretching along the Massachusetts coast near [[newburyport|Newburyport]]. Its defining features are the "salt billows of the ocean" breaking on its beach and its characteristic "sand ridges scolloping along the" shoreline. (003-1122) (003-1179)

The island serves as the natural terminus of the entire [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] system—the point where the fresh water that began in the New Hampshire lakes finally meets the Atlantic Ocean. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that Plum Island stands "at the mouth of this river, to whose formation, perhaps," the river's sediments have contributed over geological time. (005-555)

## On the River Route

Plum Island is not directly visited by the brothers on their 1839 journey, which reached only as far as the New Hampshire [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] region. However, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes the island in his opening geographical survey of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], tracing the full course from headwaters to sea. The island represents the river's ultimate destination and the point where the inland journey gives way to the ocean. (003-1122) (003-1179) (005-555)

## Historical Context

Plum Island has long served as a navigational landmark for vessels entering the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. Its shifting sand ridges and barrier beach ecology reflect the dynamic interaction between river sediment and ocean currents. The island's name likely derives from the beach plum shrubs that grow along its dunes.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] evokes Plum Island as the oceanic counterpoint to the inland riverscape he is navigating. The contrast between the "salt billows" of Plum Island beach and the gentle freshwater reaches of the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] underscores the journey's arc from inland [[thought|contemplation]] to the hint of the sea beyond. (003-1122) The island's sand ridges, shaped by wind and wave, embody the patient geological processes that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes throughout the watershed. (003-1179)
