“Come, come, my lovely fair, and let us try

Those rural delicacies.”

_Christ’s Invitation to the Soul._ QUARLES

[002-001]At length, on Saturday, the last day of August, 1839, we two, brothers,

[002-002]and natives of [[concord-massachusetts|Concord]], weighed anchor in this river port; for Concord,

[002-003]too, lies under the sun, a port of entry and departure for the bodies

[002-004]as well as the souls of men; one shore at least exempted from all

[002-005]duties but such as an honest man will gladly discharge. A warm

[002-006]drizzling rain had obscured the morning, and threatened to delay our

[002-007]voyage, but at length the leaves and grass were dried, and it came out

[002-008]a mild afternoon, as serene and fresh as if [[nature|Nature]] were maturing some

[002-009]greater scheme of her own. After this long dripping and oozing from

[002-010]every pore, she began to respire again more healthily than ever. So

[002-011]with a vigorous shove we launched our boat from the bank, while the

[002-012]flags and bulrushes courtesied a God-speed, and dropped silently down

[002-013]the stream.

[002-014]Our boat, which had cost us a week’s labor in the spring, was in form

[002-015]like a fisherman’s dory, fifteen feet long by three and a half in

[002-016]breadth at the widest part, painted green below, with a border of blue,

[002-017]with reference to the two elements in which it was to spend its

[002-018]existence. It had been loaded the evening before at our door, half a

[002-019]mile from the river, with potatoes and melons from a patch which we had

[002-020]cultivated, and a few utensils, and was provided with wheels in order

[002-021]to be rolled around falls, as well as with two sets of oars, and

[002-022]several slender poles for shoving in shallow places, and also two

[002-023]masts, one of which served for a tent-pole at night; for a buffalo-skin

[002-024]was to be our bed, and a tent of cotton cloth our roof. It was strongly

[002-025]built, but heavy, and hardly of better model than usual. If rightly

[002-026]made, a boat would be a sort of amphibious animal, a creature of two

[002-027]elements, related by one half its structure to some swift and shapely

[002-028]fish, and by the other to some strong-winged and graceful bird. The

[002-029]fish shows where there should be the greatest breadth of beam and depth

[002-030]in the hold; its fins direct where to set the oars, and the tail gives

[002-031]some hint for the form and position of the rudder. The bird shows how

[002-032]to rig and trim the sails, and what form to give to the prow that it

[002-033]may balance the boat, and divide the air and water best. These hints we

[002-034]had but partially obeyed. But the eyes, though they are no sailors,

[002-035]will never be satisfied with any model, however fashionable, which does

[002-036]not answer all the requisitions of art. However, as art is all of a

[002-037]ship but the wood, and yet the wood alone will rudely serve the purpose

[002-038]of a ship, so our boat, being of wood, gladly availed itself of the old

[002-039]law that the heavier shall float the lighter, and though a dull

[002-040]water-fowl, proved a sufficient buoy for our purpose.

[002-041]“Were it the will of Heaven, an osier bough

[002-042]Were vessel safe enough the seas to plough.”

[002-043]Some village friends stood upon a promontory lower down the stream to

[002-044]wave us a last farewell; but we, having already performed these shore

[002-045]rites, with excusable reserve, as befits those who are embarked on

[002-046]unusual enterprises, who behold but speak not, silently glided [[history|past]] the

[002-047]firm lands of Concord, both peopled cape and lonely summer meadow, with

[002-048]steady sweeps. And yet we did unbend so far as to let our guns speak

[002-049]for us, when at length we had swept out of sight, and thus left the

[002-050]woods to ring again with their echoes; and it may be many russet-clad

[002-051]children, lurking in those broad meadows, with the bittern and the

[002-052]woodcock and the rail, though wholly concealed by brakes and hardhack

[002-053]and meadow-sweet, heard our salute that afternoon.

[002-054]We were soon floating [[time|past]] the first regular battle ground of the

[002-055]Revolution, resting on our oars between the still visible abutments of

[002-056]that “North Bridge,” over which in April, 1775, rolled the first faint

[002-057]tide of that war, which ceased not, till, as we read on the stone on

[002-058]our right, it “gave peace to these United States.” As a Concord poet

[002-059]has sung:—

[002-060]“By the rude bridge that arched the flood,

[002-061]Their flag to April’s breeze unfurled,

[002-062]Here once the embattled farmers stood,

[002-063]And fired the shot heard round the world.

[002-064]“The foe long since in silence slept;

[002-065]Alike the conqueror silent sleeps;

[002-066]And Time the ruined bridge has swept

[002-067]Down the dark stream which seaward creeps.”

[002-068]Our reflections had already acquired a historical remoteness from the

[002-069]scenes we had left, and we ourselves essayed to sing.

[002-070]Ah, ’t is in vain the peaceful din

[002-071]That wakes the ignoble town,

[002-072]Not thus did braver spirits win

[002-073]A patriot’s renown.

[002-074]There is one field beside this stream,

[002-075]Wherein no foot does fall,

[002-076]But yet it beareth in my dream

[002-077]A richer crop than all.

[002-078]Let me believe a dream so dear,

[002-079]Some heart beat high that day,

[002-080]Above the petty Province here,

[002-081]And Britain far away;

[002-082]Some hero of the ancient mould,

[002-083]Some arm of knightly worth,

[002-084]Of strength unbought, and [[religion|faith]] unsold,

[002-085]Honored this spot of earth;

[002-086]Who sought the prize his heart described,

[002-087]And did not ask release,

[002-088]Whose free-born valor was not bribed

[002-089]By prospect of a peace.

[002-090]The men who stood on yonder height

[002-091]That day are long since gone;

[002-092]Not the same hand directs the fight

[002-093]And monumental stone.

[002-094]Ye were the Grecian cities then,

[002-095]The Romes of modern birth,

[002-096]Where the New England husbandmen

[002-097]Have shown a Roman worth.

[002-098]In vain I search a foreign land

[002-099]To find our Bunker Hill,

[002-100]And Lexington and Concord stand

[002-101]By no Laconian rill.

[002-102]With such thoughts we swept gently by this now peaceful pasture-ground,

[002-103]on waves of Concord, in which was long since drowned the din of war.

[002-104]But since we sailed

[002-105]Some things have failed,

[002-106]And many a dream

[002-107]Gone down the stream.

[002-108]Here then an aged shepherd dwelt,

[002-109]Who to his flock his substance dealt,

[002-110]And ruled them with a vigorous crook,

[002-111]By precept of the sacred Book;

[002-112]But he the pierless bridge passed o’er,

[002-113]And solitary left the shore.

[002-114]Anon a youthful pastor came,

[002-115]Whose crook was not unknown to fame,

[002-116]His lambs he viewed with gentle glance,

[002-117]Spread o’er the country’s wide expanse,

[002-118]And fed with “Mosses from the Manse.”

[002-119]Here was our Hawthorne in the dale,

[002-120]And here the shepherd told his tale.

[002-121]That slight shaft had now sunk behind the hills, and we had floated

[002-122]round the neighboring bend, and under the new North Bridge between

[002-123]Ponkawtasset and the Poplar Hill, into the Great Meadows, which, like a

[002-124]broad moccason print, have levelled a fertile and juicy place in

[002-125]nature.

[002-126]On Ponkawtasset, since, we took our way,

[002-127]Down this still stream to far Billericay,

[002-128]A poet wise has settled, whose fine ray

[002-129]Doth often shine on Concord’s twilight day.

[002-130]Like those first stars, whose silver beams on high,

[002-131]Shining more brightly as the day goes by,

[002-132]Most travellers cannot at first descry,

[002-133]But eyes that wont to range the evening sky,

[002-134]And know celestial lights, do plainly see,

[002-135]And gladly hail them, numbering two or three;

[002-136]For lore that’s deep must deeply studied be,

[002-137]As from deep wells men read star-poetry.

[002-138]These stars are never paled, though out of sight,

[002-139]But like the sun they shine forever bright;

[002-140]Ay, _they_ are suns, though earth must in its flight

[002-141]Put out its eyes that it may see their light.

[002-142]Who would neglect the least celestial sound,

[002-143]Or faintest light that falls on earthly ground,

[002-144]If he could know it one day would be found

[002-145]That star in Cygnus whither we are bound,

[002-146]And pale our sun with heavenly radiance round?

[002-147]Gradually the village murmur subsided, and we seemed to be embarked on

[002-148]the placid current of our [[dream|dreams]], floating from past to future as

[002-149]silently as one awakes to fresh morning or evening thoughts. We glided

[002-150]noiselessly down the stream, occasionally driving a pickerel or a bream

[002-151]from the covert of the pads, and the smaller bittern now and then

[002-152]sailed away on sluggish wings from some recess in the shore, or the

[002-153]larger lifted itself out of the long grass at our approach, and carried

[002-154]its precious legs away to deposit them in a place of safety. The

[002-155]tortoises also rapidly dropped into the water, as our boat ruffled the

[002-156]surface amid the willows, breaking the reflections of the trees. The

[002-157]banks had passed the height of their beauty, and some of the brighter

[002-158]flowers showed by their faded tints that the season was verging towards

[002-159]the afternoon of the year; but this sombre tinge enhanced their

[002-160]sincerity, and in the still unabated heats they seemed like the mossy

[002-161]brink of some cool well. The narrow-leaved willow (_Salix Purshiana_)

[002-162]lay along the surface of the water in masses of light green foliage,

[002-163]interspersed with the large balls of the button-bush. The small

[002-164]rose-colored polygonum raised its head proudly above the water on

[002-165]either hand, and flowering at this season and in these localities, in

[002-166]front of dense fields of the white species which skirted the sides of

[002-167]the stream, its little streak of red looked very rare and precious. The

[002-168]pure white blossoms of the arrow-head stood in the shallower parts, and

[002-169]a few cardinals on the margin still proudly surveyed themselves

[002-170]reflected in the water, though the latter, as well as the

[002-171]pickerel-weed, was now nearly out of blossom. The snake-head, _Chelone

[002-172]glabra_, grew close to the shore, while a kind of coreopsis, turning

[002-173]its brazen face to the sun, full and rank, and a tall dull red flower,

[002-174]_Eupatorium purpureum_, or trumpet-weed, formed the rear rank of the

[002-175]fluvial array. The bright blue flowers of the soap-wort gentian were

[002-176]sprinkled here and there in the adjacent meadows, like flowers which

[002-177]Proserpine had dropped, and still farther in the fields or higher on

[002-178]the bank were seen the purple Gerardia, the Virginian rhexia, and

[002-179]drooping neottia or ladies’-tresses; while from the more distant

[002-180]waysides which we occasionally passed, and banks where the sun had

[002-181]lodged, was reflected still a dull yellow beam from the ranks of tansy,

[002-182]now past its prime. In short, Nature seemed to have adorned herself for

[002-183]our departure with a profusion of fringes and curls, mingled with the

[002-184]bright tints of flowers, reflected in the water. But we missed the

[002-185]white water-lily, which is the queen of river flowers, its reign being

[002-186]over for this season. He makes his voyage too late, perhaps, by a true

[002-187]water clock who delays so long. Many of this species inhabit our

[002-188]Concord water. I have passed down the river before sunrise on a summer

[002-189]morning between fields of lilies still shut in sleep; and when, at

[002-190]length, the flakes of sunlight from over the bank fell on the surface

[002-191]of the water, whole fields of white blossoms seemed to flash open

[002-192]before me, as I floated along, like the unfolding of a banner, so

[002-193]sensible is this flower to the influence of the sun’s rays.

[002-194]As we were floating through the last of these familiar meadows, we

[002-195]observed the large and conspicuous flowers of the hibiscus, covering

[002-196]the dwarf willows, and mingled with the leaves of the grape, and wished

[002-197]that we could inform one of our friends behind of the locality of this

[002-198]somewhat rare and inaccessible flower before it was too late to pluck

[002-199]it; but we were just gliding out of sight of the village spire before

[002-200]it occurred to us that the farmer in the adjacent meadow would go to

[002-201]church on the morrow, and would carry this news for us; and so by the

[002-202]Monday, while we should be floating on the Merrimack, our friend would

[002-203]be reaching to pluck this blossom on the bank of the Concord.

[002-204]After a pause at Ball’s Hill, the St. Ann’s of Concord voyageurs, not

[002-205]to say any prayer for the success of our voyage, but to gather the few

[002-206]berries which were still left on the hills, hanging by very slender

[002-207]threads, we weighed anchor again, and were soon out of sight of our

[002-208]native village. The land seemed to grow fairer as we withdrew from it.

[002-209]Far away to the southwest lay the [[silence|quiet]] village, left alone under its

[002-210]elms and buttonwoods in mid afternoon; and the hills, notwithstanding

[002-211]their blue, ethereal faces, seemed to cast a saddened eye on their old

[002-212]playfellows; but, turning short to the north, we bade adieu to their

[002-213]familiar outlines, and addressed ourselves to new scenes and

[002-214]adventures. Naught was familiar but the heavens, from under whose roof

[002-215]the voyageur never passes; but with their countenance, and the

[002-216]acquaintance we had with river and wood, we trusted to fare well under

[002-217]any circumstances.

[002-218]From this point, the river runs perfectly straight for a mile or more

[002-219]to Carlisle Bridge, which consists of twenty wooden piers, and when we

[002-220]looked back over it, its surface was reduced to a line’s breadth, and

[002-221]appeared like a cobweb gleaming in the sun. Here and there might be

[002-222]seen a pole sticking up, to mark the place where some fisherman had

[002-223]enjoyed unusual luck, and in return had consecrated his rod to the

[002-224]deities who preside over these shallows. It was full twice as broad as

[002-225]before, deep and tranquil, with a muddy bottom, and bordered with

[002-226]willows, beyond which spread broad lagoons covered with pads,

[002-227]bulrushes, and flags.

[002-228]Late in the afternoon we passed a man on the shore fishing with a long

[002-229]birch pole, its silvery bark left on, and a dog at his side, rowing so

[002-230]near as to agitate his cork with our oars, and drive away luck for a

[002-231]season; and when we had rowed a mile as straight as an arrow, with our

[002-232]faces turned towards him, and the bubbles in our wake still visible on

[002-233]the tranquil surface, there stood the fisher still with his dog, like

[002-234]statues under the other side of the heavens, the only objects to

[002-235]relieve the eye in the extended meadow; and there would he stand

[002-236]abiding his luck, till he took his way home through the fields at

[002-237]evening with his fish. Thus, by one bait or another, Nature allures

[002-238]inhabitants into all her recesses. This man was the last of our

[002-239]townsmen whom we saw, and we silently through him bade adieu to our

[002-240]friends.

[002-241]The characteristics and pursuits of various ages and races of men are

[002-242]always existing in epitome in every neighborhood. The pleasures of my

[002-243]earliest youth have become the inheritance of other men. This man is

[002-244]still a fisher, and belongs to an era in which I myself have lived.

[002-245]Perchance he is not confounded by many knowledges, and has not sought

[002-246]out many inventions, but how to take many fishes before the sun sets,

[002-247]with his slender birchen pole and flaxen line, that is invention enough

[002-248]for him. It is good even to be a fisherman in summer and in winter.

[002-249]Some men are judges these August days, sitting on benches, even till

[002-250]the court rises; they sit judging there honorably, between the seasons

[002-251]and between meals, leading a civil politic life, arbitrating in the

[002-252]case of Spaulding _versus_ Cummings, it may be, from highest noon till

[002-253]the red vesper sinks into the west. The fisherman, meanwhile, stands in

[002-254]three feet of water, under the same summer’s sun, arbitrating in other

[002-255]cases between muckworm and shiner, amid the fragrance of water-lilies,

[002-256]mint, and pontederia, leading his life many rods from the dry land,

[002-257]within a pole’s length of where the larger fishes swim. Human life is

[002-258]to him very much like a river,

[002-259]—“renning aie downward to the sea.”

[002-260]This was his observation. His honor made a great discovery in

[002-261]bailments.

[002-262]I can just remember an old brown-coated man who was the Walton of this

[002-263]stream, who had come over from Newcastle, England, with his son,—the

[002-264]latter a stout and hearty man who had lifted an anchor in his day. A

[002-265]straight old man he was who took his way in silence through the

[002-266]meadows, having passed the period of communication with his fellows;

[002-267]his old experienced coat, hanging long and straight and brown as the

[002-268]yellow-pine bark, glittering with so much smothered sunlight, if you

[002-269]stood near enough, no work of art but naturalized at length. I often

[002-270]discovered him unexpectedly amid the pads and the gray willows when he

[002-271]moved, fishing in some old country method,—for youth and age then went

[002-272]a fishing together,—full of incommunicable thoughts, perchance about

[002-273]his own Tyne and Northumberland. He was always to be seen in serene

[002-274]afternoons haunting the river, and almost rustling with the sedge; so

[002-275]many sunny hours in an old man’s life, entrapping silly fish; almost

[002-276]grown to be the sun’s familiar; what need had he of hat or raiment any,

[002-277]having served out his time, and seen through such thin disguises? I

[002-278]have seen how his coeval fates rewarded him with the yellow perch, and

[002-279]yet I thought his luck was not in proportion to his years; and I have

[002-280]seen when, with slow steps and weighed down with aged thoughts, he

[002-281]disappeared with his fish under his low-roofed house on the skirts of

[002-282]the village. I think nobody else saw him; nobody else remembers him

[002-283]now, for he soon after died, and migrated to new Tyne streams. His

[002-284]fishing was not a sport, nor solely a means of subsistence, but a sort

[002-285]of solemn sacrament and withdrawal from the world, just as the aged

[002-286]read their Bibles.

[002-287]Whether we live by the seaside, or by the lakes and rivers, or on the

[002-288]prairie, it concerns us to attend to the nature of fishes, since they

[002-289]are not phenomena confined to certain localities only, but forms and

[002-290]phases of the life in nature universally dispersed. The countless

[002-291]shoals which annually coast the shores of Europe and America are not so

[002-292]interesting to the student of nature, as the more fertile law itself,

[002-293]which deposits their spawn on the tops of mountains, and on the

[002-294]interior plains; the fish principle in nature, from which it results

[002-295]that they may be found in water in so many places, in greater or less

[002-296]numbers. The natural historian is not a fisherman, who prays for cloudy

[002-297]days and good luck merely, but as fishing has been styled “a

[002-298]contemplative man’s recreation,” introducing him profitably to woods

[002-299]and water, so the fruit of the naturalist’s observations is not in new

[002-300]genera or species, but in new contemplations still, and science is only

[002-301]a more contemplative man’s recreation. The seeds of the life of fishes

[002-302]are everywhere disseminated, whether the winds waft them, or the waters

[002-303]float them, or the deep earth holds them; wherever a pond is dug,

[002-304]straightway it is stocked with this vivacious race. They have a lease

[002-305]of nature, and it is not yet out. The Chinese are bribed to carry their

[002-306]ova from province to province in jars or in hollow reeds, or the

[002-307]water-birds to transport them to the mountain tarns and interior lakes.

[002-308]There are fishes wherever there is a fluid medium, and even in clouds

[002-309]and in melted metals we detect their semblance. Think how in winter you

[002-310]can sink a line down straight in a pasture through snow and through

[002-311]ice, and pull up a bright, slippery, dumb, subterranean silver or

[002-312]golden fish! It is curious, also, to reflect how they make one family,

[002-313]from the largest to the smallest. The least minnow that lies on the ice

[002-314]as bait for pickerel, looks like a huge sea-fish cast up on the shore.

[002-315]In the waters of this town there are about a dozen distinct species,

[002-316]though the inexperienced would expect many more.

[002-317]It enhances our sense of the grand security and serenity of nature, to

[002-318]observe the still undisturbed economy and content of the fishes of this

[002-319]century, their happiness a regular fruit of the summer. The Fresh-Water

[002-320]Sun-Fish, Bream, or Ruff, _Pomotis vulgaris_, as it were, without

[002-321]ancestry, without posterity, still represents the Fresh-Water Sun-Fish

[002-322]in nature. It is the most common of all, and seen on every urchin’s

[002-323]string; a simple and inoffensive fish, whose nests are visible all

[002-324]along the shore, hollowed in the sand, over which it is steadily poised

[002-325]through the summer hours on waving fin. Sometimes there are twenty or

[002-326]thirty nests in the space of a few rods, two feet wide by half a foot

[002-327]in depth, and made with no little labor, the weeds being removed, and

[002-328]the sand shoved up on the sides, like a bowl. Here it may be seen early

[002-329]in summer assiduously brooding, and driving away minnows and larger

[002-330]fishes, even its own species, which would disturb its ova, pursuing

[002-331]them a few feet, and circling round swiftly to its nest again: the

[002-332]minnows, like young sharks, instantly entering the empty nests,

[002-333]meanwhile, and swallowing the spawn, which is attached to the weeds and

[002-334]to the bottom, on the sunny side. The spawn is exposed to so many

[002-335]dangers, that a very small proportion can ever become fishes, for

[002-336]beside being the constant prey of birds and fishes, a great many nests

[002-337]are made so near the shore, in shallow water, that they are left dry in

[002-338]a few days, as the river goes down. These and the lamprey’s are the

[002-339]only fishes’ nests that I have observed, though the ova of some species

[002-340]may be seen floating on the surface. The breams are so careful of their

[002-341]charge that you may stand close by in the water and examine them at

[002-342]your leisure. I have thus stood over them half an hour at a time, and

[002-343]stroked them familiarly without frightening them, suffering them to

[002-344]nibble my fingers harmlessly, and seen them erect their dorsal fins in

[002-345]anger when my hand approached their ova, and have even taken them

[002-346]gently out of the water with my hand; though this cannot be

[002-347]accomplished by a sudden movement, however dexterous, for instant

[002-348]warning is conveyed to them through their denser element, but only by

[002-349]letting the fingers gradually close about them as they are poised over

[002-350]the palm, and with the utmost gentleness raising them slowly to the

[002-351]surface. Though stationary, they keep up a constant sculling or waving

[002-352]motion with their fins, which is exceedingly graceful, and expressive

[002-353]of their humble happiness; for unlike ours, the element in which they

[002-354]live is a stream which must be constantly resisted. From time to time

[002-355]they nibble the weeds at the bottom or overhanging their nests, or dart

[002-356]after a fly or a worm. The dorsal fin, besides answering the purpose of

[002-357]a keel, with the anal, serves to keep the fish upright, for in shallow

[002-358]water, where this is not covered, they fall on their sides. As you

[002-359]stand thus stooping over the bream in its nest, the edges of the dorsal

[002-360]and caudal fins have a singular dusty golden [[thought|reflection]], and its eyes,

[002-361]which stand out from the head, are transparent and colorless. Seen in

[002-362]its native element, it is a very [[beauty|beautiful]] and compact fish, perfect in

[002-363]all its parts, and looks like a brilliant coin fresh from the mint. It

[002-364]is a perfect jewel of the river, the green, red, coppery, and golden

[002-365]reflections of its mottled sides being the concentration of such rays

[002-366]as struggle through the floating pads and flowers to the sandy bottom,

[002-367]and in [[music|harmony]] with the sunlit brown and yellow pebbles. Behind its

[002-368]watery shield it dwells far from many accidents inevitable to human

[002-369]life.

[002-370]There is also another species of bream found in our river, without the

[002-371]red spot on the operculum, which, according to M. Agassiz, is

[002-372]undescribed.

[002-373]The Common Perch, _Perca flavescens_, which name describes well the

[002-374]gleaming, golden reflections of its scales as it is drawn out of the

[002-375]water, its red gills standing out in vain in the thin element, is one

[002-376]of the handsomest and most regularly formed of our fishes, and at such

[002-377]a moment as this reminds us of the fish in the picture which wished to

[002-378]be restored to its native element until it had grown larger; and indeed

[002-379]most of this species that are caught are not half grown. In the ponds

[002-380]there is a light-colored and slender kind, which swim in shoals of many

[002-381]hundreds in the sunny water, in company with the shiner, averaging not

[002-382]more than six or seven inches in length, while only a few larger

[002-383]specimens are found in the deepest water, which prey upon their weaker

[002-384]brethren. I have often attracted these small perch to the shore at
