## Description

Uncannunuc Mountain rises in [[goffstown|Goffstown, New Hampshire]], west of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes it as "the most conspicuous mountain in these p[[art|arts]]," visible from numerous vantage points along the river's course. (004-1187) The mountain has a distinctive double-peaked profile, with its two summits rising above the surrounding New England [[nature|landscape]]. (006-556)

Known to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as "the Indian Uncannunuc," the mountain bears a name of Native American origin. (005-439) It forms part of a chain of hills that define the western horizon of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley in southern New Hampshire.

## On the River Route

The mountain appears repeatedly throughout the brothers' journey as a constant landmark on the western skyline. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] first notes a good view of Uncannunuc from the river bank near the mouth of the [[nashua-river|Nashua River]], seeing it "rising over the west end of the bridge above." (004-1187)

On Monday, as the travelers continued upriver, they caught sight of "the [[goffstown|Goffstown]] mountain, the Indian Uncannunuc, rising before us on the west side" whenever the river offered a suitable reach. (005-439) By Wednesday, Uncannunuc Mountain was visible from [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag Falls]], five or six miles westward, described as "the north-easternmost in the horizon, which we see from our native town." (006-556)

On the return journey, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the mountain again, along with [[hooksett|Hooksett Pinnacle]], as a "landmark to boatmen." (007-020) Their boat was ultimately moored safely "under Uncannunuc Mountain" during the final leg of their voyage. (007-448)

## Historical Context

A story recounted by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places an early settler's dwelling "between the river and Uncannunuc Mountain," where the resident lived in a log house. (006-1542) The mountain's slopes were the site of early homesteads as settlement pushed into the New Hampshire interior.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "about sixty years ago" (circa 1780), a notable incident occurred "a little south of Uncannunuc," suggesting the mountain served as a geographic reference point in local oral history long before it appeared on survey maps. (006-566)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] regards Uncannunuc with the eye of both a naturalist and a transcendentalist. He observes that from the mountain's summit one gains "perhaps the best point from which to view the" surrounding country—a vantage that reveals the full sweep of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. (007-113)

The mountain's persistent presence throughout the narrative embodies [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conception of [[nature|landscape]] as companion to the traveler. Finding their boat "safe in its harbor, under Uncannunuc Mountain," the mountain becomes not merely a landmark but a guardian presence watching over the conclusion of their journey. (007-448)
