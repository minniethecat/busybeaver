Dante Alighieri was the preeminent poet of the Italian Middle Ages and the author of the *Divine Comedy*, widely considered the greatest work of Italian literature. Writing in the Tuscan vernacular rather than Latin, Dante established Italian as a literary language and shaped the course of European poetry for centuries.

## Works / Significance

Dante's *Divine Comedy* is an epic journey through the afterlife that synthesizes medieval [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian theology with classical learning. The work comprises three p[[art|arts]]: *Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, and *Paradiso*.

His choice to write in vernacular Italian was revolutionary, democratizing literature and laying the foundation for national literatures across Europe. His influence extends through Chaucer, Milton, and the Romantic poets.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Dante at three points in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, always in contexts that elevate the poet's cultural importance.

In the Monday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] imagines two figures on the river who "may be, they are [[virgil|Virgil]] and Dante," drawing a parallel between his river journey and Dante's guided journey through the afterlife. (004-035)

In the Friday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] discusses vernacular literature, noting the service "which Dante rendered to Italy" by establishing Italian as a literary language. (008-964) He places Dante alongside Chaucer as a figure whose achievement was cultural, not merely literary.

Later in Friday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that "the name of Dante still possessed the influence of a living presence" in the century after his death. (008-999) This serves [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s argument about the permanence of genuine literary achievement.
