## Biography

George Herbert (1593–1633) was an English poet, orator, and Anglican priest. Born into a distinguished Welsh border family — his mother was a patron of [[john-donne|John Donne]] — he was educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he excelled in classics and rhetoric. He served briefly as a Member of Parliament and as Public Orator at Cambridge before turning decisively to a life of [[religion|religious]] service. Ordained in 1630, he spent his final three years as the rector of Bemerton, a small rural parish near Salisbury, where he was known for his [[history|past]]oral [[love|devotion]] and charitable care of his parishioners.

## Works / Significance

Herbert's poetic reputation rests on a single volume, *The Temple* (1633), published shortly after his death. The collection — a sequence of [[love|devotion]]al poems structured around the architecture of a church — explores the soul's relationship with God through a remarkable range of forms and tones: lyric, complaint, dialogue, and hymn. Poems such as "The Altar," "Easter Wings" (shaped as pattern poems), "The Collar," and "[[love|Love]] (III)" display Herbert's mastery of formal invention and his intimate, often anguished, spirituality. His work influenced later [[love|devotion]]al poets including Henry Vaughan and Thomas Traherne.

## In Thoreau's Text

George Herbert appears in the "Friday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, during [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s extended survey of English poetry. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Herbert as a point of contrast, naming him as a poet defined by piety rather than heroism (008-1036):

> He is not heroic, as Raleigh, nor pious, as Herbert, nor philosophical, as Shakespeare.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] returns to Herbert several paragraphs later to make a larger claim about the rarity of [[love|devotion]]al feeling in English [[poetry|verse]] (008-1064):

> How rarely in our English tongue do we find expressed any affection for God. Certainly, there is no sentiment so rare as the love of God. Herbert almost alone expresses it, "Ah, my dear God!"

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Herbert stands out among English poets for his direct and personal expression of love for the divine — a quality [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] finds largely absent even in [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] and Milton. The quotation "Ah, my dear God!" captures the intimate, [[love|affection]]ate tone of Herbert's [[love|devotion]]al voice that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] admires.
