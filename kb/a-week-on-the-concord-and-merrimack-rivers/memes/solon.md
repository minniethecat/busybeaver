## Biography

Solon (c. 630–560 BCE) was an Athenian statesman, lawmaker, and poet, counted among the Seven Sages of ancient Greece. Appointed archon of Athens in 594 BCE during a period of severe social crisis, Solon instituted a series of constitutional and economic reforms that laid the foundations for Athenian democracy. He canceled debts, freed those enslaved for debt, reorganized the citizenry into property classes, and established a council and popular law courts.

Solon's reforms were moderate by design — he sought a middle path between the demands of the poor for radical redistribution and the desire of the aristocracy to preserve its privileges. After enacting his laws, he left Athens for ten years to travel, reportedly to avoid pressure to amend his constitution.

## Works / Significance

Solon's poetry, composed in elegiac and iambic meters, survives only in fragments but was highly regarded in [[history|antiquity]]. His [[poetry|verse]]s addressed political themes — justice, good governance, the dangers of greed — and were quoted by later writers including [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and [[plutarch|Plutarch]]. Solon's name became synonymous with wise legislation, and his principle of moderation influenced subsequent political thought from [[aristotle|Aristotle]] to the American founders.

## In Thoreau's Text

Solon appears twice in *A Week*, each time in contexts that reflect [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest in the relationship between law and human nature. In "Wednesday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes that "Solon endeavored to prove that Salamis" — referring to the Athenian statesman's legendary effort to persuade his countrymen to recapture the island of Salamis. (006-407) The story, recounted in [[plutarch|Plutarch]]'s *Life of Solon*, tells how Solon feigned madness to deliver a patriotic poem urging the Athenians to renew their war for Salamis, a ruse that succeeded where ordinary political speech had failed.

In "Friday," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Solon alongside [[lycurgus|Lycurgus]], the legendary Spartan lawgiver, as figures who enacted fundamental laws: "Solon or [[lycurgus|Lycurgus]] did not enact, as" — the passage suggesting that certain [[truth|truths]] are older than legislation, grounded in nature rather than human convention. (008-100)

Both references frame Solon as an exemplar of the lawgiver whose wisdom operates at the boundary between human institutions and eternal principles — a figure who embodies [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own belief that just laws must align with higher, natural [[truth|truths]].
