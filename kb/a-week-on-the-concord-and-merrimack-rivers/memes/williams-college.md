## Overview

Williams College, founded in 1793 in Williamstown, Massachusetts, is a private liberal [[art|arts]] college located in the Berkshire Mountains of western Massachusetts. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time it was well-established as one of the leading colleges in New England.

## In Thoreau's Text

Williams College appears twice in *A Week*, both in the *Tuesday* chapter, during [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account of a mountain ascent in the Berkshires.

The first reference is incidental. While walking toward the mountain, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encounters a local woman who:

> at first had taken me for a student from Williamstown, for they went by in parties, she said, either riding or walking, almost every pleasant day, and were a pretty wild set of fellows. (005-098)

The second reference describes the summit itself, where an observatory built by Williams students crowns the peak:

> This observatory was a building of considerable size, erected by the students of Williamstown College, whose buildings might be seen by daylight gleaming far down in the valley. (005-219)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that it would be no small advantage if every college were located at the base of a mountain — as good as a well-endowed professorship. He suggests it is as well to be educated in the shadow of a mountain as in more classical shades.

This [[thought|reflection]] is characteristically transcendentalist: proximity to nature offers an education as valuable as formal instruction. The observatory becomes a symbol of the relationship between institutional learning and direct experience. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] slept in this observatory during his ascent, making the college's structure part of his own journey.

## Historical Context

Williams College was founded with funds from the estate of Colonel Ephraim Williams, who died in the [[french-and-indian-war|French and Indian War]]. By the 1830s and 1840s, the college was a center of intellectual and [[religion|religious]] life in western Massachusetts.

The mountain observatory [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] described reflected the era's growing interest in both natural science and scenic tourism. The college's location at the foot of the Taconic Range made mountain expeditions a regular activity for students — a tradition [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] witnessed firsthand and wove into his philosophical [[thought|reflection]]s on education.
