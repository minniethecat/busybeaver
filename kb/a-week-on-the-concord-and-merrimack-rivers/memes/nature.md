# Nature

Nature in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* is far more than a setting or backdrop — it is the central character, a living presence that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] approaches with reverence and intimacy.

Across all seven days of [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]], nature is the constant companion and teacher whose lessons unfold with the current. It is the standard against which all human endeavors — [[poetry]], [[friendship]], religion, commerce — are measured.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conception draws on the [[transcendentalist-movement|Transcendentalist]] conviction that the natural world mediates the divine. But his treatment is concrete and physical, grounded in the specific plants, fish, birds, and weather of the [[concord-river|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] valleys.

## Definition

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s definition of nature is both philosophical and experiential. Nature is not an abstraction but a living presence, encountered moment by moment on the river:

> The life that is in nature is perennial, young, divine. (001-092)

This "perennial" quality places nature outside of time. It is older than human civilization and will outlast it. The river, "as old as the Nile or Euphrates" (001-001), embodies this timelessness. Yet nature is also immediate and personal — it is the "constant lure" (001-198) that draws the traveler onward.

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], nature is both a physical reality and a spiritual principle. He finds in the river's "broad meadows, adorned with scattered oaks" (001-132) a model of harmonious being. The natural world is not fallen but inherently pure:

> ...as if it dated from earlier than the fall of man, and still preserved a heathen integrity. (003-006)

This "heathen integrity" is a recurrent theme. Nature exists before and beyond the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian narrative of fall and redemption. It is pagan, ancient, whole — and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] finds this liberating.

## Across the Journey

Nature evolves as a theme across the seven chapters, each day revealing a different facet of the natural world.

**[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]** establishes nature as the ground of all experience. The "[[concord-river-mass|Musketaquid]], or [[concord-river-mass|Grass-ground River]]" (001-001) is a primordial presence that precedes human history. "It is worth the while to make a voyage up this stream" (001-046), [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes, because nature reveals "how much country there is in the rear of us."

**Saturday** develops nature's health-giving qualities.
The rain-soaked landscape begins "to respire again more healthily than ever" (002-010). The "serene and fresh" afternoon reveals "nature maturing some greater scheme of her own." The fish embody "the fish principle in nature" (002-294), and "the grand security and serenity of nature" (002-317) provides emotional equilibrium.

**Sunday** is the philosophical heart of the book's treatment of nature. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] compares nature to "a god of the Greeks" (003-597) — serene and complete.

"Nature stoops the lowest and is most readily seen" (003-914) to the attentive observer. Yet a tension persists: "man is never allowed to be simply a man, but must be some kind of wood" (003-315). The "new nature" that emerges from the river is inexhaustible.

**Monday** aligns nature with labor and society. "The infinite leisure and repose of nature" (004-213) contrasts with human busyness. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "all p[[art|arts]] of nature consent" (004-268) to [[music|harmony]] when undisturbed.

**Tuesday** links nature to human character. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on "what kind of nature I should come to at last" (005-053), framing the journey as self-transformation through immersion in the wild.

**Wednesday** shows nature's vulnerability. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes that "other roads do some violence to Nature" (006-009), but the river "steals into the scenery it tra[[poetry|verse]]s without intrusion." Nature is not merely wild — it is gentle, subtle, and easily violated.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s view of nature is the foundation of his Transcendentalism. Following Emerson's *Nature* (1836), he sees the natural world as a medium through which the divine shines.

Where Emerson's nature can seem abstract, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s is concrete and intimate — he names specific plants, fish, and birds with the precision of a naturalist.

The book participates in a longer tradition of river literature, from [[homer|Homer]]'s Scamander to the [[hudson|Hudson]] River School. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] is both a specific waterway and an archetypal river of the [[thought|mind]].

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s deepest [[wisdom|insight]] connects nature to creativity. [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] and [[homer|Homer]] earn praise because they achieve nature's serenity in art — "as serene as nature" (003-1339) is his highest compliment. The poet who matches nature's impersonality outlasts towers, cities, and gods.
