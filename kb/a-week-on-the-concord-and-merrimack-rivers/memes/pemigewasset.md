## Description

The Pemigewasset River is one of the two principal headwaters of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], the other being the Winnipesaukee. It rises near the Notch of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] in New Hampshire and flows southward to join the Winnipesaukee at [[benjamin-franklin|Franklin]], forming the main stem of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (003-1116) The name "Pemigewasset" is of Native American origin and, as [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed, remains "untranslatable" — flowing "long and full of meaning" through a [[nature|landscape]] of [[history|past]]ured mountains and unnamed streams. (003-1132)

## On the River Route

The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers did not travel on the Pemigewasset during their [[the-1839-boat-journey|1839 voyage]] — their journey on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] only reached as far as the Pemigewasset's region during a later excursion described in the "Thursday" chapter. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] traced the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] from its mouth at [[plum-island|Plum Island]] "to where it bubbles out of the rocks of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]] above the clouds." (003-1116) He described a forest path that "meanders up the Pemigewasset, now more like an otter's or a marten's trail." (007-397)

The Pemigewasset represents for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] the transformation of the river itself: "from [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] it became the Pemigewasset that leaped by our side." (007-428) After passing the Pemigewasset's fountain-head, the travelers reached the [[amonooosuc|Wild Amonoosuck]] and ultimately the summit of [[mount-washington|Mount Washington]].

## Historical Context

The Pemigewasset, like all New England rivers, is subject to freshets, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recorded that it "has been known to rise twenty-five feet in a few hours." (003-1195) The river flows through the iron region of [[franconia|Franconia]], through uncut forests and [[history|past]] inexhaustible ledges of granite — a [[nature|landscape]] that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] saw as offering its natural privileges "in vain for ages, until at last the Yankee race came to improve them." (003-1213)

A notable natural feature on the Pemigewasset is the Basin, described by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as a remarkable curiosity: "one of the head-waters of this river, twenty by thirty feet in extent and proportionably deep, with a smooth and rounded brim, and filled with a cold, pellucid, and greenish water." (006-318) This geological formation, carved by the river's own action over millennia, represented for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] the patient sculpting power of water over stone.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s description of the Pemigewasset is among his most lyrical river writing. He imagined it "by many a [[history|past]]ured Pelion and Ossa, where unnamed muses haunt, tended by Oreads, Dryads, Naiads, and receiving the tribute of many an untasted Hippocrene." (003-1132) This classical imagery — likening New Hampshire mountains to the homes of Greek gods — reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conviction that the American [[nature|landscape]] deserved the same reverence and mythological richness as the ancient Mediterranean world.

He described the solitary upper reaches: "where the bear still drinks it, and the cabins of settlers are far between, and there are few to cross its stream; enjoying in solitude its cascades still unknown to fame." (003-1116) The river at its source embodies the wild, untamed nature that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] most valued.
