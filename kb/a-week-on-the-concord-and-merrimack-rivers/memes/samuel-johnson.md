## Biography

Samuel Johnson (1709–1784) was an English writer, critic, essayist, poet, and lexicographer, widely regarded as one of the most commanding figures of eighteenth-century English letters. Born in Lichfield, Staffordshire, he overcame poverty, illness, and physical disability to become the dominant literary critic of his age. His major works include *A Dictionary of the English [[language|Language]]* (1755), *The Rambler* essays, *Rasselas*, and *Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets*. His conversation, preserved in James Boswell's celebrated *Life of Johnson*, made him as famous for his wit and pronouncements as for his published works.

## Works / Significance

Johnson's *Dictionary* was a monumental achievement — the first comprehensive dictionary of the English language, setting standards for lexicography that endured for a century. His literary criticism, particularly the *Lives of the Poets*, established principles of judgment that shaped English criticism through the Romantic period. Johnson's combination of profound moral seriousness, vigorous common sense, and stylistic elegance made him the archetype of the English man of letters. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], though temperamentally far from Johnson's urban and orthodox sensibility, shared his commitment to intellectual independence and moral clarity.

## In Thoreau's Text

Johnson appears twice in *A Week*, in two very different contexts, each reflecting [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s complex engagement with English literary authority.

In the opening chapter (*[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]*), [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] turns to the early chronicles of New England to set the [[history|historical]] stage for his journey. Johnson's *Wonder-working Providence* — a history of New England from 1628 to 1652 — serves as one of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s sources for the colonial [[nature|landscape]]:

> Let us here read what old Johnson says of these meadows in his "Wonder-working Providence," which gives the account of New England from 1628 to 1652… (001-145)

The reference is to Edward Johnson (1598–1672), the colonial historian, not Samuel Johnson — though [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s casual "old Johnson" risks confusion between the two. Edward Johnson's chronicle, with its providential reading of American settlement, exemplifies the early Puritan perspective that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] both draws on and distances himself from.

In the *Sunday* chapter, it is indeed Samuel Johnson whom [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes. Reflecting on the nature of biography and the miraculous quality of a well-lived life, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] takes up a remark of Johnson's about [[sir-thomas-browne|Sir Thomas Browne]]:

> That is a superfluous wonder, which Dr. Johnson expresses at the assertion of Sir Thomas Browne that "his life has been a miracle of thirty years, which to relate, were not history but a piece of poetry, and would sound like a fable." (003-692)

The passage reveals [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s subtle disagreement with Johnson. Where Johnson dismisses Browne's claim of a miraculous life as "superfluous wonder," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] is inclined to agree with Browne — that any life, properly examined, is extraordinary enough to read like poetry rather than bare history. Johnson's skepticism becomes, in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s handling, a foil for the Transcendentalist conviction that the miraculous is immanent in the ordinary.
