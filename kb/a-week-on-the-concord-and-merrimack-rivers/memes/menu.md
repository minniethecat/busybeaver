## Biography

Menu, or Manu, is the legendary progenitor of humanity and the author of the *Manusmṛti* (Laws of Menu), one of the most important texts in the Hindu legal and ethical tradition.
In Hindu mythology, Manu is the first man and the survivor of a great flood, analogous to the Biblical Noah.
The Laws of Menu are said to have been revealed by [[brahma|Brahma]] himself and transmitted through Menu to humanity.
The text covers a comprehensive range of topics including cosmology, social order, ethics, ritual, and law, forming the foundation of traditional Hindu jurisprudence.

## Works / Significance

The Laws of Menu (*Manusmṛti*) consists of over 2,600 [[poetry|verse]]s organized into twelve chapters.
It addresses dharma (duty, righteousness) across the four stages of life and the four social classes.
The text was first translated into English by Sir William Jones in 1794, bringing it to the attention of Western intellectuals including the Transcendentalists.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] read the Laws of Menu through Jones's translation and found in it a model of spiritual law that resonated with his own belief in higher moral principles transcending human legislation.
The text's emphasis on austerity, self-discipline, and [[thought|contemplation]] deeply influenced [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s [[thought|thinking]] about the relationship between spiritual practice and daily life.

## In Thoreau's Text

Menu and the Laws of Menu feature prominently in the "Monday" chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mines Hindu [[religion|scripture]] for [[wisdom|insight]]s into spiritual discipline and the nature of law.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] introduces the Laws of Menu as a text of "transcendent law" (004-471), emphasizing its divine origin and universal scope.
He quotes Sir William Jones's assessment that the Laws of Menu, along with the Vedas and other ancient texts, were among the foundational works of human civilization (004-820).
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records the tradition that Menu was "son or grandson of [[brahma|Brahma]]" and "first of created beings" (004-823), and that [[brahma|Brahma]] taught his laws "in a hundred thousand [[poetry|verse]]s, which Menu explained to the primitive" people (004-825).
He finds the text speaks "to our privacy more than" public institutions (004-864), valuing its direct spiritual address over its social prescriptions.
In the concluding chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] places Menu alongside other world teachers — "Veias, Menu, [[zoroaster|Zoroaster]], [[socrates|Socrates]], [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (008-1415) — affirming his place in the universal tradition of spiritual wisdom.
