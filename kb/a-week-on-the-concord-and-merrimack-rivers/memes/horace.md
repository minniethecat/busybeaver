Quintus Horatius Flaccus, known in English as Horace, was one of the greatest poets of the Augustan age of Rome. The son of a freedman, he rose to become a [[friendship|friend]] of Maecenas and the Emperor Augustus himself. His works include the *Odes*, *Epodes*, *Satires*, and *Epistles*, which together established him as a master of lyric grace, urbane satire, and moral [[thought|reflection]].

## Works / Significance

Horace's lyric poetry brought Greek metrical forms into Latin [[poetry|verse]] with unprecedented skill. His *Odes* celebrate love, [[love|friendship]], wine, and the passing of time with a poised, philosophical lightness that has influenced poets from the Renaissance to the present.

His *Satires* and *Epistles* offer a more conversational mode, blending moral counsel with humorous observation of Roman life. Horace coined the phrase *carpe diem* ("seize the day") and the ideal of the "golden mean" — both of which resonate with [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s philosophy of deliberate living.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Horace across several chapters of *A Week*, treating him as a representative of the classical literary tradition and as a model of poetic directness.

In Tuesday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the highway of reading that runs "down from [[homer|Homer]] and [[hesiod|Hesiod]] to Horace and [[juvenal|Juvenal]]" as "more attractive than the Appian." (005-1284) The metaphor suggests that the literary journey through the classics surpasses even the grandeur of Rome's most famous road. [[books|Reading]] becomes a form of travel in its own right.

In Thursday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] praises the "vivacity of Horace," noting that no sibyl is needed "to re[[thought|mind]] you, that from those older Greek poets there is a sad descent to [[persius|Persius]]." (007-256) (007-257) Compared to the primal freshness of the Greeks, later Roman satirists can feel diminished — but Horace retains the vital spark.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] deepens his appreciation: "straightforwardness is the severest correction. Horace would not have written satire so well if he had not been inspired by it, as by a muse." (007-282) For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Horace's plain-spoken honesty is itself the highest literary virtue — a quality [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] himself strove for in his own prose.
