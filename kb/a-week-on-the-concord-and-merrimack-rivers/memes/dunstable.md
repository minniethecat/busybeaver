## Description

Dunstable is a town in Middlesex County, Massachusetts, situated on the north bank of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] near the New Hampshire border. During the colonial period, it was a frontier settlement that encompassed a much larger territory than its present boundaries, including p[[art|arts]] of what is now [[nashua|Nashua]] and other surrounding towns. The town's early history was marked by conflicts during the [[french-and-indian-war|French and Indian War]]s, and it served as an important waypoint along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] trade route.

## On the River Route

Dunstable lies along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]], downstream from [[concord-mass|Concord]]. The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers would have passed through or near Dunstable on their 1839 journey up the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references the town across multiple chapters of the book, drawing heavily on its colonial history. As a frontier town, it marked a transition point between the settled Massachusetts interior and the more sparsely populated New Hampshire territory upstream. (003-580) (004-051)

## Historical Context

Dunstable was first permanently settled around 1663, when the eldest son of [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]], chief of the [[penacook-tribe-history|Pennacook]] Confederacy, deeded land in the area. The town's early decades were defined by its position on the frontier of English settlement. (003-1832) (003-1847)

During the colonial wars, Dunstable was the site of several skirmishes and raids. Lieutenant Farwell of Dunstable and Jonathan Frye of [[andover|Andover]] were among those who fought against Native American raiding parties. Ten inhabitants of Dunstable were once ambushed while searching for stray horses, and the town's churchyard contains memorials to those who fell in these frontier conflicts. (004-086) (004-1332) (004-1374)

> As the temple of Juno at Argos contained the shield of Croesus, so the churchyard at Dunstable contains the dust of Lovewell and his companions. (004-1350)

A party pursuing Native Americans from Dunstable was killed in what became known as [[lovewells-fight|Lovewell's Fight]]. (005-517)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on Dunstable as a place where the [[history|past]] and present intermingle. He draws a parallel between the ancient city of Thebes and the seemingly ordinary New England town, suggesting that even a small frontier settlement contains its own epic history. (004-182)

> As they lived in Thebes, so do they live in Dunstable to-day. "Time drinketh up the essence of every great and noble action, which ought to be performed, and is not, but the little, which is done, he lets stand as a monument."
> — (004-182)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s meditation on Dunstable extends to its churchyard, where he contemplates the graves of fallen soldiers and the frontier's violent history. He also references the town in connection with Mount Auburn cemetery, drawing a contrast between the rural churchyard and the fashionable urban cemetery. (004-1452) (005-980)
