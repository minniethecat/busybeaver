## Biography

Galileo Galilei (1564-1642) was an Italian astronomer, physicist, and mathematician central to the Scientific Revolution. Born in Pisa, he studied medicine before turning to mathematics and natural philosophy.

His improvements to the telescope in 1609 enabled the first detailed observations of the surface of the Moon, the moons of Jupiter, and the phases of Venus. These discoveries provided empirical evidence supporting the Copernican heliocentric model.

His advocacy for heliocentrism brought him into conflict with the Roman Inquisition, resulting in his condemnation in 1633. Beyond astronomy, his studies of motion and falling bodies laid groundwork for Newtonian physics.

## Works / Significance

The major works of Galileo include *Sidereus Nuncius* (The Starry Messenger, 1610) and *[[conversation|Dialogue]] Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632). His insistence that nature is written in the language of mathematics fundamentally reshaped scientific methodology.

## In Thoreau's Text

Galileo appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. As [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] nears the end of his journey, he turns to consider what lies beyond the visible world.

He writes: "verified, by Galileo,—I am not without hope that we may, even here and now obtain some accurate information concerning that OTHER WORLD which the instinct of mankind has so long predicted" (008-1401).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] invokes Galileo as a symbol of verified [[dream|vision]] — the capacity to see what others had not yet seen. The "OTHER WORLD" is not a planet but a spiritual realm, and Galileo serves as the model of the observer extending human perception. This exemplifies the characteristic move of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]: enlisting scientific authority in the service of Transcendentalist inquiry.
