# Virtue

## Definition

For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], virtue is not a passive conformity to social expectations but an active, almost martial quality of character — something to be "whetted" and cultivated through deliberate effort. He speaks of "every time we whet our virtue" (005-1224), suggesting that moral excellence requires constant sharpening against the grindstone of experience. Virtue is the inner constitution that enables a person to act rightly, not from compulsion but from the natural expression of a well-ordered soul.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s virtue is deeply personal and yet fundamentally relational. In the chapter on Wednesday, dedicated to [[love|friendship]], he explores how virtue forms the bedrock of genuine human connection: "The virtue which we appreciate we to some extent appropriate" (006-1151). A [[friendship|friend]]'s virtues must be "distinctly balanced by corresponding virtues" (006-1295), as [[love|friendship]] is a contract grounded in mutual moral recognition. Yet virtue is also something that, at its highest pitch, transcends social bonds entirely: "an unwavering and commanding virtue would compel even its accessories to respect it" (006-1725).

## Across the Journey

**Sunday** introduces virtue through the lens of classical and biblical wisdom. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that "heaven-born virtues are but good manners" (003-339), suggesting that even the highest moral qualities, when adopted as mere social graces, lose their transformative power. He declares with determination: "I will find goodness, I will find virtue" (003-708), framing virtue as something to be actively sought rather than passively received.

**Monday** examines virtue through Eastern philosophy, particularly the Bhagavad Gita and the Laws of [[menu|Menu]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "humility is still a very human virtue" (004-418) and observes that for the Brahmin, "virtue consists in doing, not in believing" (004-624) — an emphasis on action over creed that resonates deeply with his own pragmatic moral [[dream|vision]]. Yet he also warns that even virtue can decay: "Even virtue is no longer preserved" (004-386), when it becomes mere habit without living spirit.

**Tuesday** presents virtue in its more vulnerable aspect. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes how his "wavering virtue" nearly gave way, and he "sank down again into that modern Erie Canal" (005-287), acknowledging the fragility of moral resolve in the face of life's enervating currents.

**Wednesday** is the richest chapter for virtue, where it becomes the very substance of [[love|friendship]]. "If I but love that virtue which he is" (006-725), [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] writes, expressing a [[plato|Plato]]nic ideal where love is directed at the moral essence of the beloved. Yet he also notes the paradox: "Why love among the virtues is not known" (006-887) — suggesting that love itself is a virtue beyond conventional moral categorization. He concludes that "all the virtues of the heart cannot supply" (006-1268) the need for active moral connection. [[friendship|Friendship]], he insists, must be "contracted with his virtue" (006-1284).

**Thursday** and **Friday** offer a more measured, mature view. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes that "those things for which the most money is demanded are the cheapest" (007-352), a paradox that applies to virtue as well. On Friday, he [[dream|dreams]] of rising "above the necessity of virtue into an unchangeable morning" (008-946), suggesting a state of spiritual perfection beyond even moral striving.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s treatment of virtue draws on classical and Eastern sources alike. His emphasis on virtue as active practice rather than passive belief echoes [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s ethics, while his integration of virtue with [[love|friendship]] recalls both the [[plato|Plato]]nic Symposium and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian agape tradition. The Bhagavad Gita's influence is palpable in the Monday chapter, where virtue is defined through action and duty.

Within American Transcendentalism, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s virtue ethics stands apart from the more abstract moralism of his contemporaries. For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], virtue is not a set of rules but a quality of being — tested, refined, and proven through the concrete circumstances of [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]]. It is the moral counterpart to truth: as truth is the intellect's highest achievement, virtue is the will's.
