## Description

Goffstown is a town in Hillsborough County, New Hampshire, situated near the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] at the mouth of the [[piscataquoag|Piscataquoag River]]. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time it was a rural community whose hills and mountains formed a picturesque backdrop to [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]]. The [[piscataquoag|Piscataquoag River]] flows through Goffstown before joining the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. (006-289)

## On the River Route

As the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers rowed northward, they caught sight of the Goffstown mountain range rising in the distance. (005-438) On the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], the travelers were tempted to go across the river into Goffstown. (006-289) The distance between Goffstown and [[hooksett|Hooksett]] was noted as four miles. (006-292)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed that [[uncannunuc|Uncannunuc Mountain]] in Goffstown was visible from [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag]], five miles distant. (006-556) The boundary between [[manchester-nh|Manchester]] and Goffstown marked a transition along the river. (006-645) In a moment of elevated [[thought|reflection]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] wrote of being in "the middle of a prosaic Goffstown day, equal to any of the gods." (006-923)

## Historical Context

Goffstown was settled in the early eighteenth century and incorporated in 1761. One notable resident was a hunter of extraordinary skill who resided in the town and was celebrated as one of the oldest hunters in the region. (006-524) The town's location at the confluence of the [[piscataquoag|Piscataquoag]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s made it a natural waypoint for river travelers.

## Thoreau's Observations

The sight of the Goffstown range from afar and the visibility of [[uncannunuc|Uncannunuc Mountain]] from [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag]] frame Goffstown within the larger geography of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s remark about the "prosaic Goffstown day" being "equal to any of the gods" captures his transcendentalist belief that the divine can be encountered in the most ordinary moments and places. (006-923)
