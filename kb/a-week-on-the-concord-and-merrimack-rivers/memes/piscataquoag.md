## Description

The Piscataquoag River is a tributary of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] in southern New Hampshire. Its name, recorded by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as meaning "Sparkling Water," captures the character of this clear-flowing stream. (006-269)

The river drains the western slopes of the [[uncannunuc|Uncanoonuc]] Mountains and flows through [[goffstown|Goffstown]] before emptying into the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] listed it among the notable tributaries joining the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]: "Piscataquoag, and [[suncook-river|Suncook]] and Soucook and [[contoocook|Contoocook]], mingled in." (003-1153)

## On the River Route

The brothers first encountered the Piscataquoag on their outward journey upstream. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted that "not long after this we saw the Piscataquoag, or Sparkling Water," recording the Native American name and its translation. (006-269)

Many a village-bell had "floated down the Piscataquoag to the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]," carrying the sounds of settled life along the river valley. (006-272) On the return journey, passing through the [[amoskeag-falls|Amoskeag Falls]] region, they observed the mouth of the Piscataquoag joining the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]], one of several tributaries whose waters had accompanied them on their voyage. (007-492)

## Historical Context

The area around the Piscataquoag was the territory of the [[penacook|Penacook]] confederacy, who knew the river long before European settlement.

The river's banks, like much of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] watershed, saw early colonial settlement in the 18th century. By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, the river drained farming communities whose church bells sent their sound down the valley, a detail [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] wove into his meditation on how rivers bind communities together. (006-272)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] was particularly attentive to river names and their Native American origins. By pairing "Piscataquoag" with its translation "Sparkling Water," he preserved both the original name and its meaning, a pattern he repeated with other geographic features along the voyage. (006-269)
