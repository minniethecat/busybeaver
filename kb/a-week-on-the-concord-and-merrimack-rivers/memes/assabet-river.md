## Overview

The Assabet River is a small river in eastern Massachusetts that rises in Westborough
and flows northeast through the towns of Northborough, Marlborough, [[hudson|Hudson]], Stow,
Maynard, and Acton before joining the [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]] at Egg Rock in [[concord-mass|Concord]] to form
the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s era, the river's confluence lay a little north and
west of [[concord-mass|Concord]] center.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions the river in the opening chapter, describing the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]'s
tributaries. The Assabet enters from the south and west:

> (001-018) Assabeth River, which has its source a little farther to the north and
> west, goes out at the northeast angle, and flowing between Bedford and Carlisle,
> and through Billerica, empties into the Merrimack at Lowell.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses the nineteenth-century spelling "Assabeth," consistent with
contemporary maps and gazetteers of the 1840s.

## Headwaters and Course

The Assabet rises near Westborough and flows in a generally northeast direction
for approximately thirty-four miles. It drains a watershed of about one hundred
and seventy-seven square miles across central Middlesex County. Unlike the
sluggish [[sudbury|Sudbury]], the Assabet has a steeper gradient and a more rapid current
in its upper reaches.

## Historical Significance

During the colonial period, the Assabet powered gristmills and sawmills along
its course. The river's name derives from the Algonquian word for "the place
where the river turns back," reflecting Native American use of the waterway
before European settlement. By the mid-nineteenth century, small manufacturing
concerns — including powder mills and paper mills — had been established along
its banks.

## Confluence at Egg Rock

The Assabet meets the [[concord-river-mass|Sudbury River]] at Egg Rock in [[concord-mass|Concord]], forming the [[concord-mass|Concord]]
River that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] knew intimately. This confluence lies approximately half a
mile from [[concord-mass|Concord]] center and was a familiar landmark to [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who passed it
regularly on his walks and river excursions. From this point, the combined waters
flow north to join the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] at [[lowell-mass|Lowell]].
