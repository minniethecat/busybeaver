Daniel Gookin was a colonial magistrate, soldier, and historian who served as Superintendent of the Indians in the Massachusetts Bay Colony. His most significant work, *Historical Collections of the Indians in New England* (1674), provides a detailed first-hand account of Native American life, language, and culture in 17th-century New England.

## Works / Significance

Gookin's *Historical Collections* is notable for its sympathetic and detailed portrayal of Native Americans. At a time when most colonial writers dismissed indigenous peoples, Gookin documented their language, customs, and beliefs with unusual care.

His writings served as a crucial source for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who admired Gookin's observational precision and his humane regard for the indigenous peoples whose world was being displaced by colonial settlement.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws on Gookin throughout *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, quoting him directly and at length.

In the Sunday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Gookin's description of [[wamesit|Wamesit]] as an ancient seat of the Indians. (003-1037) He recounts Gookin's account of a Pawtucket sachem who made a speech embracing [[religion|Christianity]] after deliberation. (003-1051)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes from *Gookin's Hist. Coll. of the Indians in New England* to illustrate the persistence of native identity alongside [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian conversion. (003-1070)

In Wednesday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions Gookin's description of Tyng's house near [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Falls]], noting Gookin's apology to Robert Boyle for his material "clothed in a [[nature|wilderness]] dress." (003-1840)

In Monday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Gookin's observation on [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]]. (004-1181) Gookin remarked that "The Indian rod upon the English backs had not yet done God's errand." He also noted that English soldiers underestimated their Indian adversaries. (004-1381)

In Tuesday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts Gookin's account of the sachem Pasaconaway, seen at Pawtucket when about one hundred and twenty years old. (006-470) Pasaconaway was reputed a wise man and powwow who restrained his people from war with the English. (006-476) Gookin thought Pasaconaway might have had a spirit like Balaam's. (006-485)
