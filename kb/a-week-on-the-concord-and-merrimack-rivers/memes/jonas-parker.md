## Biography

Jonas Parker was a resident of Shirley, Massachusetts, in the early nineteenth century. On Sunday, December 18, 1803, Parker was travelling westward alongside [[jeremiah-richardson|Jeremiah Richardson]], with teams and rigging used to carry barrels. They were stopped and questioned by the Honorable [[ephraim-wood|Ephraim Wood]], Esquire, a justice of the peace, regarding their travel on the Sabbath.

Richardson, in his testimony before Squire Wood, identified Parker as "his fellow-traveller" and stated that a Mr. Longley was their employer, "who promised to bear him out" (003-571, 003-574). The incident survives in the [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]] annals and was transcribed by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], who preserved the original spelling and grammar of the colonial record.

## In Thoreau's Text

Parker appears in the *Sunday* chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* as part of a document [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] transcribes from the [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]] records. The story of Parker, Richardson, and Squire Wood serves as a symbol of the regulated and scrutinized existence of settled New England life — a world of Sabbath laws, roadside interrogations, and official oversight. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses this vignette to contrast with the liberty of his own river journey, where he and his brother passed "unquestioned by any Squire or Church Deacon" (003-577).
