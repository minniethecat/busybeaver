## Biography

Roger Bacon (c. 1219–1292) was an English philosopher and Franciscan friar who emphasized empirical methods and mathematical reasoning in natural philosophy, earning him the title Doctor Mirabilis ("Wonderful Teacher"). Educated at Oxford and the University of Paris, he was one of the earliest European advocates of the scientific method, arguing that knowledge should be based on observation and experiment rather than received authority. His outspoken criticism of scholastic methods and his interest in alchemy, optics, and astronomy brought him into conflict with his Franciscan superiors, and he spent years under confinement in a Paris convent.

## Works / Significance

Bacon's major works include the *Opus Majus*, *Opus Minus*, and *Opus Tertium* (c. 1267), written at the request of [[alexander-pope|Pope]] Clement IV. In these he laid out a comprehensive reform of learning that encompassed grammar, mathematics, optics, experimental science, and moral philosophy. He is credited with being among the first in Europe to describe gunpowder and to outline the principles of the magnifying glass. Although largely forgotten in the centuries after his death, he was rediscovered in the Renaissance and recognized as a forerunner of modern empirical science.

## In Thoreau's Text

Roger Bacon appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Friday" chapter. During a [[thought|reflection]] on fame and the endurance of great names, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] lists Bacon among figures whose reputation has survived the passage of time (008-998):

> The fame of Roger Bacon came down from the ages.

This single line appears in a passage surveying the legacy of distinguished English figures. By placing Bacon alongside other "stout and stirring names," [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] acknowledges the medieval philosopher's lasting place in intellectual history — a fitting tribute to a thinker who, like [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] himself, valued direct observation over received authority.
