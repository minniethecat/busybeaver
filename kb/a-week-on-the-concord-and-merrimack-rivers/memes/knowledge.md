## Definition

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] distinguishes between "many knowledges" — the scattered facts
that "confound" the [[thought|mind]] (002-245) — and genuine knowledge, which is
integrated, experiential, and inseparable from the knower. True
knowledge is not acquired from books alone but from "the knowledge of
God" (003-1076) — a phrase he quotes from a colonial catechism but
invests with his own meaning: knowledge of the divine order manifest
in nature. The person of knowledge is "not confounded by many
knowledges" but possesses a centered understanding that illuminates
whatever it touches.

## Across the Journey

On **Saturday** (chapter 002), the theme is introduced with
characteristic compression: the ideal observer "is not confounded by
many knowledges, and has not sought" (002-245) after scattered facts.
Knowledge is a quality of *being*, not a quantity of *information*.

On **Sunday** (chapter 003), [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes an old catechism: "I must
acknowledge I have, all my days, used to" seek knowledge (003-1053),
and the response — being "instructed in the knowledge of God"
(003-1076) — transforms a theological formula into a statement about
the ultimate object of knowledge. To know God is to know the order of
nature, which is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s own lifelong project.

## Related Passages

> (002-245) he is not confounded by many knowledges, and has not
> sought after the scattered fragments of truth, but has waited
> patiently for the whole to be revealed.

Knowledge is not accumulation but *receptivity* — waiting for the
whole to reveal itself rather than chasing fragments. This is the
core of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s epistemology: understanding comes to those who
attend, not to those who grasp.

> (003-1076) instructed in the knowledge of God.

Quoted from a colonial catechism, this phrase becomes in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s
hands a definition of the highest knowledge: not theology but direct
acquaintance with the divine order expressed in nature. "The
knowledge of God" is the knowledge of how things truly are.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s epistemology is anti-encyclopedic: he rejects the
accumulation model of knowledge in favor of an integrative model
rooted in experience. This aligns him with the Romantic reaction
against Enlightenment rationalism, the Transcendentalist insistence
on intuition as a source of knowledge, and the classical ideal of
*sapientia* (wisdom) over *scientia* (knowledge). His phrase "many
knowledges" anticipates the modern critique of information overload.
