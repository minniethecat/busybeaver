# Hannah Dustan Captivity

## Overview

The captivity and escape of [[hannah-dustan|Hannah Dustan]] is one of the most famous narratives of colonial New England, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] recounts it in the Thursday chapter. On March 15, 1697, Abenaki warriors attacked [[haverhill|Haverhill]], taking 13 captives — including Dustan, who had given birth six days earlier. (007-606) Taken from her sickbed, she and her nurse Mary Neff were marched northward.

The escape: assisted by Samuel Lennardson, she killed and scalped ten captors while they slept on a [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] island, then escaped by canoe. (007-609)

## Relevance to the Journey

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] tells the [[hannah-dustan|Hannah Dustan]] story in the **Thursday** chapter near the end of [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]], as the travelers pass through the region where the events occurred. The story is more than a [[history|historical]] footnote — it serves as a meditation on courage and frontier violence. (007-682)

The [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] is the stage for both the original captivity and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own journey. Dustan's escape route — downriver by canoe — mirrors [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s voyage, creating an eerie parallelism between the peaceful present and the violent [[history|past]]. (007-683)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] also alludes to the Dustan story in the **Monday** chapter, where he marvels at the "singular truth" contained in such histories. (004-1176) The story appears alongside other captivity narratives, creating a palimpsest of [[history|historical]] memory over the contemporary [[nature|landscape]].

## Historical Background

[[hannah-dustan|Hannah Dustan]] (1657–1736?) was born in [[haverhill|Haverhill, Massachusetts]], the daughter of Michael and Hannah Emerson. She married Thomas Dustan, a farmer and brickmaker, and had at least 12 children before the 1697 attack.

The raid on [[haverhill|Haverhill]] occurred during King William's War (1688–1697), the North American theater of the Nine Years' War between England and France.

The escape on a small island in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] — now known as Dustan Island, near [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire — became one of the most celebrated captivity narratives of the colonial period. Cotton Mather included an account in his *Magnalia [[jesus-christ|Christ]]i Americana*.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s version follows the traditional narrative but emphasizes the [[nature|landscape]]. He notes the location of the island, the course of the river, and the natural features visible to Dustan as she made her journey homeward.
