## Description

Tyngsborough (also spelled Tyngsboro) is a town in Middlesex County, Massachusetts, situated on the east bank of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. It lies between [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]] to the south and the New Hampshire state line to the north. Originally part of the [[dunstable|Dunstable]] grant, the town was incorporated in 1809 and named after the Tyng family, early settlers of the region. (003-1830)

## On the River Route

The [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] brothers passed through Tyngsborough on both the outward and return legs of their [[the-1839-boat-journey|1839 voyage]]. On Saturday, after passing through [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]], they entered the Tyngsborough stretch of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] where the river widened to sixty rods across. (003-1959) They pitched their tent on the east bank in Tyngsborough, above patches of beach plum nearly ripe. (003-1959)

On Sunday morning, after the fog dispersed, they rowed leisurely through Tyngsborough with a clear sky and mild atmosphere, leaving the habitations of men behind. (004-048) They passed the Horseshoe Interval in Tyngsborough, where the river makes a sudden bend to the northwest. (004-133) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] noted a small desert on the east bank between Tyngsborough and [[hudson|Hudson]], which struck the travelers as interesting and refreshing. (004-770)

On the return journey, having passed the New Hampshire line and reached the Horseshoe Interval in Tyngsborough, they climbed a high and regular second bank to get a nearer sight of the autumnal flowers. (008-529) Gliding rapidly by Tyngsborough and [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]] with a bending sail, each held half a tart country apple-pie. (008-704)

## Historical Context

Tyngsborough was originally within the bounds of [[dunstable|Dunstable]], whose first permanent settler was recorded in the late seventeenth century. The area that became Tyngsborough was part of the frontier during [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]] in 1675, when many settlers fled the region. (003-1853)

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records several anecdotes from Tyngsborough. One elderly inhabitant told him a story of captives taken during the Indian wars who, escaping their captors, discovered that the hoops of their pro[[dream|vision]] barrel had been cut and turpentine spread on the ground. (004-1334) This story had been passed down through generations.

According to the record of another old inhabitant, now dead, one of the greatest freshets on record took place on the Tyngsborough farm. (008-596) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects that even if the soil had not been remarkably fertile, "at this distance we thought that this anecdote might be told of the Tyngsborough farm." (008-660)

From the hill on the road to Tyngsborough, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observed that one could get a good view of the Delectable Mountains. (004-1201)
