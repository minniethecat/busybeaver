# Penacook

Penacook appears throughout *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* in multiple layers: as the name of an Algonquian people who inhabited the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley, as a place on the river (now [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire), and as a site where Indigenous presence, colonial settlement, and frontier memory converge.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encounters Penacook not as a single event but as a palimpsest — the river carries him [[history|past]] places where the [[penacook-tribe-history|Penacook people]] fished and governed, where they resisted and were displaced, and where later settlers drove oxen through the [[nature|wilderness]] to claim the fertile intervals.

## Overview

The Penacook were a powerful Algonquian confederacy whose territory spanned the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] from present-day [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire, southward into Massachusetts. Under the leadership of the sagamore [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] — a figure of legendary status among both Natives and colonists — the Penacook maintained complex diplomatic relations with the Massachusetts Bay Colony, the Mohawks to the west, and neighboring Algonquian groups.

By the time of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s 1839 journey, the [[penacook-tribe-history|Penacook people]] had long been displaced from the river that bore their name. But their presence lingers in his narrative — in the place-names, in the stories of captivity and rescue, and in the archaeological traces visible along the riverbanks.

The site of Penacook itself — the "intervals" at what became [[concord-mass|Concord]], New Hampshire — had been transformed from an Indigenous settlement into a colonial frontier town, and then into a [[silence|quiet]] New England village. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] registers each layer of this transformation with his characteristic blend of [[history|historical]] attention and elegiac awareness.

## Relevance to the Journey

Penacook surfaces at critical moments throughout [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative, each chapter revealing a different facet of the place and its people.

**Sunday — [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] and [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]].** Passing [[wicasuck|Wicasuck Island]] between [[chelmsford|Chelmsford]] and [[tyngsborough|Tyngsborough]], [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records one of the earliest documented Penacook land transactions:

> "About 1663, the eldest son of Passaconaway [Chief of the Penacooks] was thrown into jail for a debt of £45, due to John Tinker, by one of his tribe, and which he had promised verbally should be paid. To relieve him from his imprisonment, his brother Wannalancet and others, who owned Wicasuck Island, sold it and paid the debt." (003-1833)

The island, once "a favorite residence of the Indians," was later restored by the General Court in 1665, but after the Indians' departure in 1683, it was granted to [[jonathan-tyng|Jonathan Tyng]]. The passage from Indigenous possession to colonial ownership is compressed into a single paragraph — a pattern [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] observes throughout the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] Valley.

**Tuesday — The Penacook and the Fur Trade.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] records a dramatic episode from the early contact period:

> Within a few hours after his flight, a party of the Penacook tribe arrived, and, not finding the object of their resentment, burnt his habitation. (005-481)

The story concerns a trader — possibly named Cromwell — who had cheated the Penacook in the fur trade. The Penacook pursued him, and when he fled, they burned his house. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that the cellar of this habitation was still visible in his time, "now overgrown with trees," a physical trace of the violence that accompanied contact.

**Tuesday — John Hogkins's Letter.** One of the most extraordinary documents [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] preserves is a letter from John Hogkins, a Penacook Indian, to the governor of Massachusetts in 1685:

> "May 15th, 1685. Honor governor my friend, — You my friend I desire your worship and your power, because I hope you can do som great matters this one. I am poor and naked and I have no men at my place because I afraid allwayes Mohogs he will kill me every day and night." (005-1121)

Hogkins describes his grandfather as having lived "at place called Malamake rever, other name chef Natukkog and Panukkog, that one rever great many names." The letter — with its broken English, its desperate plea for protection against Mohawk raiders, and its multiple names for the river — is a rare first-person Indigenous voice preserved within [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative. It documents the precarious position of the Penacook in the late 17th century, caught between colonial authority and intertribal warfare.

**Tuesday — Penacooks and Mohawks.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the broader context of intertribal conflict: "Penacooks and Mohawks! _ubique gentium sunt?_ In the year 1670, a Mohawk warrior scalped a Naamkeak or else a [[wamesit|Wamesit]] Indian maiden near where [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] now stands. She, however, recovered" (005-1118). The Latin phrase — "are they everywhere?" — captures the sense of pervasive, inescapable violence that characterized the region during the colonial period.

**Wednesday — The Geography of Penacook.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes the shift in place-names as the river carries him northward: "to Penacook, now [[concord-mass|Concord]] in New Hampshire, from the scene of the war" (006-490). The parenthetical "now [[concord-mass|Concord]]" is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s constant refrain — the colonial name superimposed on the Indigenous one, the present layered over the [[history|past]].

**Thursday — The Settlement of Penacook.** The most extended treatment of Penacook comes in the Thursday chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes the historian of [[haverhill|Haverhill]] on the founding of the settlement:

> The richness of the intervals at Penacook, now Concord, New Hampshire, had been observed by explorers, and, according to the historian of Haverhill, in the "year 1726, considerable progress was made in the settlement, and a road was cut through the wilderness from Haverhill to Penacook." (007-132)

The detail of the settlement is almost comic in its specificity: "a lad of 18, drove a team consisting of ten yoke of oxen to Penacook, swam the river, and ploughed a portion of the interval. He is supposed to have been the first person who ploughed land in that place" (007-141). After completing his work, "he started on his return at sunrise, drowned a yoke of oxen while recrossing the river, and arrived at [[haverhill|Haverhill]] about midnight. The crank of the first saw-mill was manufactured in [[haverhill|Haverhill]], and carried to Penacook on a horse" (007-147).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] presents these details without commentary, but the accumulation creates a vivid picture: the rich intervals that had sustained the Penacook for generations, now being ploughed by a teenager driving ten yoke of oxen through the [[nature|wilderness]]. The transformation from Indigenous homeland to colonial farmland is rendered through the material facts of settlement — roads, oxen, ploughs, saw-mills.

**Thursday — Bows and Arrows.** The final Penacook reference in the book is a technological note: "the Penacooks and Pawtuckets using bows and arrows and hatchets of stone" (007-695). Placed among a catalog of Indian tools and practices, this image of stone-age technology contrasts sharply with the saw-mill cranks being carried on horseback — two technological worlds in collision along the same river.

## Historical Background

The Penacook confederacy was one of the major political entities of 17th-century New England. Under [[passaconaway|Passaconaway]] — a leader of such stature that legends grew around him, including stories that he could make water burn and trees dance — the Penacook navigated the impossible pressures of colonial expansion, epidemic disease, and Mohawk raiding from the west.

[[passaconaway|Passaconaway]]'s strategy was accommodation. He submitted to Massachusetts Bay Colony authority in 1644, converted (at least nominally) to [[religion|Christianity]], and urged his people to maintain peace with the English. His son [[wannalancet|Wannalancet]] continued this policy, relocating the remnant of the Penacook to Canada after [[king-philips-war|King Philip's War]] (1675–1678) made remaining in New England untenable.

The Penacook story is thus a compressed version of the larger Indigenous history of the Northeast: first contact, diplomatic maneuvering, land loss, displacement, and the erasure of names — "Penacook" becoming "[[concord-mass|Concord]]" — that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s narrative both records and resists.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] preserves fragments of this history not out of systematic [[history|historical]] interest — he is no ethnographer — but because the river compels him to. The places he passes carry the names and stories of the people who preceded him, and his method in *A Week* is to let those stories surface as the current carries him [[history|past]] their sites.
