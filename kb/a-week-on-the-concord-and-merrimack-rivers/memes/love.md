# Love

## Definition

Love, for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], is not sentiment but the foundation of all genuine human relation. "Between whom there is hearty truth, there is love," he writes, making love conditional upon honesty rather than emotion. (006-917) This is characteristically [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]vian: love is not a feeling one falls into but a quality one cultivates through truthfulness.

"In proportion to our truthfulness and confidence in one another," love grows or diminishes — love and truth are directly proportional. (006-918) This formulation removes love from the realm of mere passion and places it in the domain of ethics, where it belongs alongside virtue and integrity.

## Across the Journey

**Saturday** introduces love indirectly through the brothers' shared journey — their [[friendship|companionship]] is a form of love that needs no declaration.

**Sunday** touches on love through classical mythology and [[religion|religious]] [[thought|reflection]]. The love of the gods for mortals, the love celebrated in Greek poetry — these are forms of love [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] treats with respect but also with critical distance.

**Wednesday** is the pivotal chapter for love. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] develops his ethics of love through the lens of friendship: "Why love among the [[virtue|virtues]] is not known" — suggesting that love deserves a place in the catalog of [[virtue|virtues]] but is too often excluded. (006-887) "Only lovers know the value and magnanimity of truth," he continues, binding love and truth together as inseparable. (006-896)

**Friday** considers love in relation to beauty. "Would have a love for beauty and goodness" — the cultivated soul loves what is worthy of love. (008-1069)

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s ethics of love reflects both classical and Transcendentalist sources. From [[aristotle|Aristotle]] he inherits the idea that friendship (*philia*) is the highest expression of ethical life; from [[religion|Christianity]] he retains the association of love with truth and virtue. But [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s synthesis is distinctive: love is not self-sacrifice but mutual truthfulness — a relationship between equals rather than between benefactor and recipient.

The Wednesday chapter, with its extended meditation on friendship and love, is arguably the emotional and ethical center of *A Week*. Here [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] most fully articulates his [[dream|vision]] of love as the culmination of the examined life.
