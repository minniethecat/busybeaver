## Description

Penichook Brook is a tributary entering the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] on the eastern bank, on the confines of what is now Nashville, New Hampshire. It is described as "a wild salmon-stream," indicating both its natural character and its ecological significance in the early 19th century. (005-367)

The brook flows through a [[nature|landscape]] of pine woods and deep ravines, its banks carpeted with dead pine needles. The area around its mouth is marked by a distinctive ravine under the skirts of a pine wood. (004-1463)

## On the River Route

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother first encountered Penichook Brook on Monday evening, when they camped near its mouth. They set their tent "near Penichook Brook, on the confines of what is now Nashville, by a deep ravine, under the skirts of a pine wood, where the dead pine-leaves were our carpet, and their tawny boughs stretched overhead." (004-1463)

On Tuesday morning, they passed the mouth of Penichook Brook in heavy fog "without seeing it," the brook hidden by the river mist that also forced them to leave their tent spread over the boat until the sun could dry it. (005-367)

On the return journey, by the time they reached Penichook Brook again the weather had turned cool and breezy, and "we were obliged to sit muffled in our cloaks, while the wind and current carried us along." The travelers bounded swiftly over the rippling surface [[history|past]] cultivated lands on both shores. (008-464)

## Historical Context

The brook's name is of Native American origin, like many waterways in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] watershed. "Penichook" may derive from an Algonquian term, though its precise etymology is uncertain. The area around the brook's mouth was still sparsely settled in [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time—the reference to "what is now Nashville" indicates that the modern place name had not yet been established in 1839.

The brook's designation as a "salmon-stream" reflects the historic abundance of Atlantic salmon in the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] system before the construction of dams at [[lawrence|Lawrence]] and [[lowell-mass|Lowell]] blocked their passage in the 19th century.

## Thoreau's Observations

Penichook Brook serves as the setting for one of the journey's most atmospheric campsites—a deep ravine beneath pines where the accumulated needles formed a natural carpet. The fog that obscured the brook on the following morning, requiring the tent to be aired and dried, conveys the damp, intimate conditions of river travel.

The contrast between the two passages of Penichook—the warm evening of the outward journey and the cold, wind-driven return—mirrors the larger arc of the narrative from departure to homecoming. On the return, the travelers were "obliged to sit muffled in our cloaks" as wind and current carried them swiftly [[history|past]]. (008-464)
