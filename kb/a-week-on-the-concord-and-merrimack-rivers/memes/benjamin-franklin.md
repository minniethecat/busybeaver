## Biography

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) was an American polymath: a printer, scientist, inventor, statesman, and one of the Founding Fathers of the United States. Born in Boston and raised in a modest Puritan household, he apprenticed as a printer before establishing himself in Philadelphia, where he published the *Pennsylvania Gazette* and *Poor Richard's Almanack*.

His scientific inquiries into electricity — culminating in the famous kite experiment — earned him international renown. As a diplomat, he secured crucial French support during the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]] and helped negotiate the Treaty of Paris (1783).

## Works / Significance

Franklin's *Autobiography* remains a classic of American letters, embodying the self-made ideal. His inventions include the lightning rod, bifocal glasses, and the Franklin stove. He founded the American Philosophical [[civilization|Society]] and what became the University of Pennsylvania.

## In Thoreau's Text

Franklin appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, in the "Sunday" chapter. As [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] meditates on how [[history|historical]] figures become mythologized — comparing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]opher Columbus to Jason of the Argonauts — he turns to Franklin with speculative admiration (003-485):

> Franklin,—there may be a line for him in the future classical dictionary, recording what that demigod did, and referring him to some new genealogy. "Son of —— and ——. He aided the Americans to gain their independence, instructed mankind in economy, and drew down lightning from the clouds."

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] casts Franklin not as a mere politician but as a figure of mythic proportions — a "demigod" whose deeds merit entry into a [[time|future]] classical canon. The mock-genealogical formula ("Son of —— and ——") playfully adopts the diction of ancient mythology while honoring Franklin's distinctly modern achievements.
