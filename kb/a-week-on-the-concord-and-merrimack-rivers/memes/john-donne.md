## Biography

John Donne was born in London into a recusant Catholic family at a time when the practice of Catholicism was illegal in England. He studied at Oxford and Cambridge but could not take a degree due to his religion. After a youthful period of worldly ambition, secret marriage, and financial hardship, he converted to Anglicanism and took holy orders in 1615, eventually becoming Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London. He is equally celebrated for his erotic and [[religion|religious]] poetry and for his powerful sermons.

## Works / Significance

Donne is regarded as the preeminent metaphysical poet, known for his intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of passionate emotion with philosophical argument. His major works include the *Songs and Sonnets*, the *Holy Sonnets*, and his *Devotions upon Emergent Occasions* (which includes the famous meditation "No man is an island"). His influence extended through T.S. [[john-eliot|Eliot]] and the modernist revival of metaphysical poetry in the twentieth century.

## In Thoreau's Text

Donne appears in the "Wednesday" chapter of *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes a single line as he meditates on the significance of [[dream|dreams]] (006-1714):

> We bless and curse ourselves. Some dreams are divine, as well as some waking thoughts. Donne sings of one / "Who dreamt devoutlier than most use to pray." (006-1713–1715)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Donne's line to support his argument that [[dream|dreams]] may be as spiritually meaningful as waking consciousness. The passage continues: "[[dream|Dream]]s are the touchstones of our characters" (006-1716), extending Donne's poetic [[wisdom|insight]] into a broader moral and psychological claim about the revelatory nature of the [[dream|dreaming]] [[thought|mind]].
