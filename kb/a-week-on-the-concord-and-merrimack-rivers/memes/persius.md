## Biography

Aulus Persius Flaccus (34–62 CE) was a Roman poet of Etruscan origin, known for his six satires written in the tradition of Lucilius and [[horace|Horace]].
Born in Volterra, Etruria, to a wealthy equestrian family, Persius was educated in Rome, where he studied under the Stoic philosopher Lucius Annaeus Cornutus, who became a lifelong influence.
Persius died young at the age of twenty-eight, leaving a small but intensely crafted body of work.
His satires are characterized by their moral seriousness, Stoic philosophy, and compressed, difficult style — qualities that distinguished him from the more urbane [[horace|Horace]] and the ferocious [[juvenal|Juvenal]].

## Works / Significance

Persius's surviving works consist of six satires, totaling about 650 lines, along with a brief prologue in choliambic meter.
The satires address themes of literary pretension, moral hypocrisy, the proper use of prayer, self-knowledge, and Stoic freedom.
Persius's dense, allusive style made him a challenging read even in [[history|antiquity]], but he was admired for his uncompromising moral [[dream|vision]].
His work was preserved and circulated widely in the Middle Ages and Renaissance, influencing European satirical tradition.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] encountered Persius as part of his classical education and uses him in *A Week* as a touchstone for discussing the relationship between moral severity and [[art|artistic]] expression.

## In Thoreau's Text

Persius appears in the "Thursday" chapter, where [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] reflects on the nature of satire and the solitary integrity of the serious artist.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] introduces Persius with a note of descent: "there is a sad descent to Persius" (007-257) from the heights of earlier poetry, suggesting the diminished scope of Roman satire compared to epic or lyric forms.
Yet this descent is not without value — [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] notes that "a [[juvenal|Juvenal]] or Persius do not marry" (007-269), meaning their satirical vocation demands a certain ascetic detachment from the world's pleasures.
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] probes the inner condition of the satirist: the "condition of Persius" lies "not in the province of" external circumstance but in the integrity of the inner self (007-289).
He acknowledges that Persius's satires are "the furthest possible from" popular appeal (007-385), yet insists that the core of the work — "that which alone we can call Persius, which is forever independent" (007-388) — transcends its [[history|historical]] moment.
For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], Persius exemplifies the stoic ideal of the writer who remains true to his [[dream|vision]] regardless of audience or reception.
