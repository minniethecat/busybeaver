## Biography

Captain John Smith (1580–1631) was an English soldier, explorer, and colonial governor best known for his role in the founding of Jamestown, Virginia (1607), and for his exploration and mapping of the New England coast in 1614. It was Smith who gave New England its name, publishing *A Description of New England* in 1616 to promote colonization of the region.

Smith's 1614 voyage took him along the coast from Penobscot Bay to Cape Cod. He traded with Native peoples, charted harbors and rivers, and returned to England with furs, fish, and a detailed map. His writings on the natural abundance of the New England coast — particularly its fisheries — were widely read and helped fuel English interest in the region.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Captain John Smith in the *Sunday* chapter to celebrate the pleasures of fishing along the New England coast. Smith wrote enthusiastically of the sport: "And is it not pretty sport to pull up twopence, sixpence, and twelvepence, as fast as you can haul and veer a line?" (003-1268–003-1269). He continued: "And what sport doth yield a more pleasing content, and less hurt or charge, than angling with a hook, and crossing the sweet air from isle to isle, over the silent streams of a calm sea" (003-1270–003-1272).

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses Smith's words to frame his own experience of fishing on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s. The Captain's praise of angling, written two centuries before [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s journey, connects the river voyage to a longer tradition of English exploration and enjoyment of the New England waters.
