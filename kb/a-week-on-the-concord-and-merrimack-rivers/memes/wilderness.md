## Definition

Wilderness in *A Week* is not simply untamed land; it is a quality
that adheres to places, poems, and persons. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses the word
to describe the pre-settlement American [[nature|landscape]] ("this late howling
[[nature|wilderness]]"), the unexpected force in poetry ("such strong and
[[nature|wilderness]] tints on any poet’s string"), and the existential
condition of those who live at the edge of cultivation. Wilderness
is what remains when human order recedes — a reservoir of power,
danger, and revelation that civilization cannot fully domesticate.

## Across the Journey

Wilderness appears early in the journey as a physical reality. On
**Saturday** (chapter 002), the brothers pass through a [[nature|landscape]]
that is still "a place for fauns and satyrs" (002-679) — the
classical [[history|past]]oral imagination layered over the American wild.

On **Sunday** (chapter 003), the concept thickens. The land is "this
late 'howling [[nature|wilderness]]'" (003-192), the phrase echoing Puritan
descriptions of the New World as a place of spiritual trial. Yet
[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] transforms the Puritan dread into something fertile: the
[[nature|wilderness]] "gives its perfume" (003-263) and produces "such strong
and [[nature|wilderness]] tints on any poet’s string" (003-386). The wild
becomes an aesthetic and spiritual resource.

Throughout the journey, the [[nature|wilderness]] is also the site of [[history|historical]]
violence: settlers "alone, in the [[nature|wilderness]], to defend his home"
(003-1857), captives "taken captive and carried to Canada" through
"the [[nature|wilderness]]" (004-1331). These narratives of conflict re[[thought|mind]] us
that the "howling [[nature|wilderness]]" was also a contested ground between
colonists and Native peoples.

By **Monday** (chapter 004), [[nature|wilderness]] has become almost
metaphysical: "even far in the [[nature|wilderness]], and have passed a" life
there (004-1321). The wild is no longer just a place but a condition
of existence — something one can inhabit, be shaped by, and carry
forward.

## Related Passages

> (003-192) in this late "howling wilderness"; yet to all intents and
> purposes it is as wild as ever. The Indian corn is grown there
> still. The wilderness gives its perfume to the settler's apple
> orchard.

The "howling [[nature|wilderness]]" of the Puritans persists into the present;
cultivation does not erase wildness but mingles with it. The "Indian
corn" growing still is a trace of the land's deeper history, and the
"[[nature|wilderness]] perfume" that reaches the orchard suggests an
interpenetration of wild and cultivated that is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s ideal.

> (003-386) such strong and wilderness tints on any poet’s string.

Wilderness here becomes a literary quality — a "tint" or tone that
the best poetry carries. It is not the subject matter but the
*pressure* of the wild that distinguishes strong writing.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]’s [[nature|wilderness]] concept sits at the intersection of the Puritan
"errand into the [[nature|wilderness]]," the Romantic sublime, and the emerging
American discourse of wild nature as national identity. His
innovation is to treat [[nature|wilderness]] not as an absence (of civilization)
but as a presence — a positive quality that nourishes art, thought,
and character. This idea would later be developed more fully in his
essay "Walking" (1862), with its famous declaration that "in
Wildness is the preservation of the World."
