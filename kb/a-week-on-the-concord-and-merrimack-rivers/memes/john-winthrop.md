## Biography

John Winthrop (1588–1649) was an English Puritan lawyer who served as the first governor of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. Born into a prosperous Suffolk family, he was educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, and practiced law before leading the Winthrop Fleet to Massachusetts in 1630.

His lay sermon "A Model of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian Charity," with its [[dream|vision]] of the colony as a "city upon a hill," is one of the foundational texts of American civil religion. Winthrop served as governor for twelve terms over the colony's first two decades, shaping its institutions and its relations with Native American tribes.

## Works / Significance

Winthrop's *Journal* (also known as *The [[history|History]] of New England*) is the single most important primary source for the early history of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. Covering the years 1630 to 1649, it records political events, theological disputes, natural phenomena, and encounters with Native peoples with the immediacy of a participant writing close to the moment.

The *Journal* is both a [[history|historical]] record and a spiritual autobiography — Winthrop interpreted events within a providential framework, seeing God's hand in settlements, storms, and battles alike. For [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], as for later historians, Winthrop's *Journal* is an indispensable window into the world of seventeenth-century New England.

## In Thoreau's Text

Winthrop appears in the *Sunday* chapter in a passage that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes from the colonial record to illustrate early English-Native American relations:

> "So," says Winthrop, in his Journal, "we causing them to understand the articles, and all the ten commandments of God, and they freely assenting to all, they were solemnly received." (003-1080)

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Winthrop's account of the formal reception of Native Americans into the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian fold. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s interest is characteristically ambivalent: the passage appears in a meditation on [[religion|religious]] coercion and the cultural gap between Puritan settlers and indigenous peoples. Winthrop's narrative of consent is troubled by the unequal power relations it describes.

Later in the same passage, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] compresses the arc of New England history into a list of proper names:

> Miantonimo,—Winthrop,—Webster. (003-1101)

The three names — Miantonomo the Narragansett sachem, Winthrop the Puritan governor, Daniel Webster the nineteenth-century statesman — trace the trajectory from Native sovereignty through colonial settlement to American nationhood. Winthrop stands at the pivot between two worlds.
