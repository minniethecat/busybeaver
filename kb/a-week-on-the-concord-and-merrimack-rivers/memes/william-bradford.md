## Biography

William Bradford (1590–1657) was an English Separatist who sailed on the *Mayflower* in 1620 and served as governor of [[pilgrims-landing|Plymouth Colony]] for over thirty years. Born in Austerfield, Yorkshire, he joined the Scrooby Separatist congregation as a young man and fled with them to the Netherlands before the voyage to America.

Bradford's *Of [[plymouth|Plymouth]] Plantation* (written 1630–1651) is the single most important primary source for the early history of [[pilgrims-landing|Plymouth Colony]] and the first Thanksgiving. His leadership guided the struggling colony through its precarious first decades.

## In Thoreau's Text

Bradford does not appear personally in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, but the town of Bradford, Massachusetts — named for the Bradford family — is mentioned in the "Friday" chapter as a landmark on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] route (008-603):

> at Bradford in the year 1818.

This passing reference situates Bradford among the river towns [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] catalogues along the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]]'s course. Though the governor himself goes unnamed, the persistence of his name on the [[nature|landscape]] is itself a record of the Puritan settlement [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] often reflects upon.
