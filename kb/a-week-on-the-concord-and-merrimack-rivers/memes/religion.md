# Religion

## Definition

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s engagement with religion in *A Week* is characteristically independent: he reads the New Testament alongside the *Bhagavad Gita*, treats Greek mythology as seriously as [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian scripture, and judges all religious traditions by their capacity to convey truth and inspire virtue. "A true assertion respecting God, is an assertion of God," he quotes [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]] approvingly — but immediately adds, "we may well doubt if there is any example of this in literature." (003-763)

Religion, for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], is not about doctrine or institution but about the quality of [[wisdom|insight]] a tradition makes available. He is equally critical of the "church and the Sabbath school" that prejudiced him against the Bible and of any uncritical reverence that mistakes poetry for divine revelation. (003-764)

## Across the Journey

**Sunday** is the pivotal chapter for religion. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] engages deeply with the New Testament, finding it "an invaluable book" despite his early prejudices. (003-764) He contrasts it with Hindu scripture, noting "the best of the Hindo Scripture" excels in "pure intellectuality" while the New Testament is distinguished by "pure morality." (004-516)

**Monday** develops the comparative religion theme. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] praises the *Bhagavad Gita* as raising the reader into "a higher, purer, or rarer region of thought." (004-516) He contrasts the [[brahma|Brahman]]'s active virtue with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian passive morality, and finds wisdom in both traditions.

**Tuesday** touches on religion through the lens of local history and legend — the way religious narratives become embedded in the New England [[nature|landscape]].

**Thursday** reflects on the decay of religious institutions. The "adamantine everlasting truth" that religion claims often proves to be "some old poet's grand imagination imposed on us." (003-763)

**Friday** synthesizes the religious themes, returning to the idea that genuine religious [[wisdom|insight]] is indistinguishable from poetry and truth.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s religious [[thought|thinking]] was shaped by the Unitarian liberalism of his upbringing and his discovery of Eastern texts through the Transcendentalist circle. Emerson's "Divinity School Address" (1838) had already called for a religion of direct intuition rather than [[history|historical]] revelation, and [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] applies this principle with characteristic independence.

His reading of the *Bhagavad Gita* (in Charles Wilkins's 1785 translation) was particularly consequential. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] found in it a model of religious discourse that integrated philosophy, ethics, and poetry — a model he felt Western Christianity had lost.
