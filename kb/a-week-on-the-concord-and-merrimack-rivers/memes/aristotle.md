Aristotle was a Greek philosopher and polymath during the Classical period in Ancient Greece. A student of [[plato|Plato]] and tutor to [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]], he founded the Lyceum and the Peripatetic school of philosophy. His writings span physics, metaphysics, ethics, politics, rhetoric, poetics, and biology, making him one of the most influential thinkers in Western history.

## Works / Significance

Aristotle's corpus established the foundational categories of Western philosophy and science. His *Poetics* defined art as *mimesis* and provided the first systematic theory of tragedy. His *Physics* and *Metaphysics* shaped medieval scholasticism for centuries.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]], educated in the classical curriculum at [[harvard-college|Harvard]], encountered Aristotle through both original texts and the tradition of commentary that Aristotle's work had generated over two millennia.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Aristotle three times in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, each time in a philosophical context.

In the Monday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes Aristotle's observation on the nature of time: "As time never fails, and the uni[[poetry|verse]] is eternal, neither the Tanais nor the Nile can have flowed forever." (004-287) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses this to reflect on the relationship between the eternal and the [[time|temporal]].

Also in Monday, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] lists Aristotle among the great philosophers — [[plato|Plato]], Aristotle, Leucippus, Democritus, [[pythagoras|Pythagoras]], [[zoroaster|Zoroaster]], and [[confucius|Confucius]] — whose di[[poetry|verse]] systems of thought he finds compatible. (004-936) This embrace of philosophical diversity reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conviction that truth transcends any single system.

In the Friday chapter, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] cites Aristotle's definition of art as "the principle of the work without the wood." (008-753) [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] uses this distinction to argue for the primacy of the ideal over the material in [[art|artistic]] [[art|creation]].
