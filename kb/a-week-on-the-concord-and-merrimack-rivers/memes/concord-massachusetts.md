# Concord, Massachusetts

## Description

Concord, Massachusetts, is a historic New England town situated at the confluence of the [[sudbury|Sudbury]] and Assabet rivers, which form the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time (the 1830s–1840s), it was a center of intellectual and literary activity, home to [[ralph-waldo-emerson|Ralph Waldo Emerson]], the [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] family, and the burgeoning Transcendentalist movement. The town's [[nature|landscape]] — its rivers, meadows, and woodlands — provided the setting for [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s most famous works.

## On the River Route

[[concord-mass|Concord]] marks the starting point of the 1839 journey. The brothers launched their boat on the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]], which flows north from the town toward the [[middlesex-canal|Middlesex Canal]] and eventually the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]. The "[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]]" chapter opens the narrative with the natural and [[history|historical]] richness of this waterway.

## Historical Context

Founded in 1635 as the first inland settlement of the Massachusetts Bay Colony, [[concord-mass|Concord]] played significant roles in both colonial settlement and the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]] (the Battle of [[concord-mass|Concord]], April 19, 1775). By [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s day, it had become a [[silence|quiet]] agricultural town with a notable literary community.

## Thoreau's Observations

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] describes the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] with intimate familiarity — its plant life, its fish, its seasonal changes, and the "life that is in nature" that belongs "veritably to the [[time|future]]." (001-092) The river serves as both physical starting point and philosophical grounding for the entire journey.
