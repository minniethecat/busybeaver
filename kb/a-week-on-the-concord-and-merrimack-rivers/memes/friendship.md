# Friendship

Friendship in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s* is the most sustained philosophical meditation in the book — a theme [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] explores over more than forty paragraphs in the "Tuesday" chapter, building toward one of the most radical accounts of human relationship in American literature.

The essay-within-the-book unfolds as [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother John drift downstream, the river providing the physical setting for a [[thought|reflection]] that ranges from classical philosophy to frontier encounters, from [[confucius|Confucius]] to the fur-trader Henry. At its heart is [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s conviction that true [[love|friendship]] is extraordinarily rare — a relation not of utility or even of [[music|harmony]], but of "[[music|melody]]," in which two souls resonate as one.

## Definition

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] opens his treatment of [[love|friendship]] with a declaration of its fleeting, almost mystical quality:

> Friendship is evanescent in every man's experience, and remembered like heat lightning in past summers. Fair and flitting like a summer cloud;—there is always some vapor in the air, no matter how long the drought; there are even April showers. (006-841)

The image of "heat lightning" — distant, silent flashes that illuminate without striking — captures [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s sense that [[love|friendship]], in its true form, is glimpsed more often than possessed. It "takes place, like vegetation in so many materials, because there is such a law, but always without permanent form."

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] distinguishes true [[love|friendship]] sharply from its common counterfeits. What passes for [[love|friendship]] in daily life is, in his view, "no very profound or powerful instinct":

> What is commonly honored with the name of Friendship is no very profound or powerful instinct. Men do not, after all, _love_ their Friends greatly. I do not often see the farmers made seers and wise to the verge of insanity by their Friendship for one another. (006-888)

The neighbor who gives a barrel of apples or a vote at town-meeting — this is civility, not [[love|friendship]]. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] is after something far more demanding. True [[love|friendship]] is not a matter of mutual assistance but of mutual elevation: "We do not wish for Friends to feed and clothe our bodies, — neighbors are kind enough for that, — but to do the like office to our spirits" (006-930).

## Across the Journey

Friendship is not confined to the Tuesday chapter. Its thread runs through the entire book, surfacing in different keys across the days of the voyage.

**[[concord-river-mass|Concord River]].** The opening chapter establishes the journey itself as an act of [[love|friendship]] — the brothers' shared enterprise on the river. "For all are their debtors, and all their friends" (001-116) introduces the theme of obligation and connection that will later deepen into philosophy.

**Saturday.** The first full day on the river is light with the pleasure of companionship. "Some village friends stood upon a promontory lower down the stream to wave their hands to us" (002-043). These early gestures of [[love|friendship]] are social, uncomplicated — the departure still fresh, the philosophy yet to come.

**Sunday.** As the river carries the brothers deeper into the journey, [[love|friendship]] takes on a more searching quality. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] asks pointedly: "Whose friend are you?" (003-756). He describes reading aloud to friends as one of life's great pleasures: "I should love dearly to read it aloud to my friends" (003-789). A friend presents oranges as a gift, but the journey spoils them — a small parable of the difficulty of conveying [[love|affection]] across the gap between souls (003-984).

**Monday.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] interweaves [[love|friendship]] with the theme of reform and social improvement. "One should be slow to mend, my friends, as slow to require mending" (004-273). The critique of busy reform is grounded in the conviction that true improvement happens in the intimate space between friends, not through institutional action.

**Tuesday.** This is the philosophical heart of the book's treatment of [[love|friendship]]. In an extended meditation — perhaps the longest sustained [[thought|reflection]] on any single topic in the entire work — [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] develops a rigorous, demanding [[dream|vision]] of what [[love|friendship]] requires. The passage moves through several movements:

1. **The Rarity of True Friendship.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] insists that the world's talk of [[love|friendship]] vastly exceeds its reality. "No word is oftener on the lips of men than Friendship, and indeed no thought is more familiar to their aspirations" — yet "I can remember only two or three essays on this subject in all literature" (006-974). The disproportion between the word's frequency and the thing's scarcity is itself evidence of how deeply the ideal is felt.

2. **Friendship as Melody, Not Harmony.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] makes a crucial distinction: "Friends do not live in [[music|harmony]] merely, as some say, but in [[music|melody]]" (006-930). Harmony implies two distinct notes that sound agreeable together; [[music|melody]] implies a single line in which the notes merge. True [[love|friendship]] dissolves the boundary between self and other.

3. **Friendship as Education.** "Think of the importance of Friendship in the education of men" (006-948). In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s educational philosophy, [[love|friendship]] is the highest school: "It will make a man honest; it will make him a hero; it will make him a saint. It is the state of the just dealing with the just, the magnanimous with the magnanimous, the sincere with the sincere, man with man."

4. **Equality.** "Friendship is, at any rate, a relation of perfect equality. It cannot well spare any outward sign of equal obligation and advantage" (006-1066). [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] radicalizes this principle: a king cannot have a friend among his subjects, nor a nobleman among his retainers. The "hydrostatic paradox" — by which a slender column of water balances an ocean — is "the symbol of love's law" (006-1080).

5. **The [[language|Language]] Beyond [[language|Words]].** "The language of Friendship is not words, but meanings. It is an intelligence above language" (006-1171). True friends communicate in a register deeper than speech — their understanding is immediate, requiring no translation.

6. **Eastern [[wisdom|Wisdom]].** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws on [[confucius|Confucius]] and Mencius to ground his account in ancient tradition. Mencius speaks of seeking "the sentiments of the heart which we have lost" (006-909). [[confucius|Confucius]] warns: "Never contract Friendship with a man who is not better than thyself" (006-1095). The Eastern sages confirm what [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s own experience teaches: [[love|friendship]] is an aspirational discipline, not a casual comfort.

**Wednesday through Friday.** The later chapters show [[love|friendship]]'s shadow side — loss, memory, elegy. The journey, undertaken with his brother John, becomes, in the writing, a memorial. John died of tetanus in 1842, and the entire book is suffused with the knowledge of that loss. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] quotes a poem that speaks directly to his brother: "Though now thou climbest loftier mounts, / And fairer rivers dost ascend, / Be thou my Muse, my Brother —." The dedication transforms the entire work into an act of [[love|friendship]] across the barrier of death.

## Context

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s philosophy of [[love|friendship]] stands at the intersection of several traditions.

**Classical Antiquity.** The distinction between true [[love|friendship]] and its common counterfeits echoes [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s *Nicomachean Ethics*, which distinguishes [[love|friendship]]s of utility, [[love|friendship]]s of pleasure, and [[love|friendship]]s of virtue. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] pushes the Aristotelian scheme further: even virtue-[[love|friendship]], as commonly understood, is insufficient. He demands a fusion of souls that [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s more moderate account does not claim.

**Romanticism.** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s language of "[[music|melody]]" and his insistence on the ineffability of true [[love|friendship]] belong to the Romantic tradition. Like [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] and Coleridge, he sees the deepest human bonds as resisting articulation. But where the Romantics often celebrated [[love|friendship]] between poets as a [[imagination|creative]] collaboration, [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s account is more solitary — the Friend is an ideal one seeks alone, a "fair floating isle of palms eluding the mariner in Pacific seas" (006-868).

**Eastern [[philosophy|Philosophy]].** [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s deployment of [[confucius|Confucius]] and Mencius is not decorative. The Confucian emphasis on self-cultivation through right relationship resonates deeply with his own conviction that [[love|friendship]] makes one "honest," "a hero," "a saint." The Eastern framework also supports his critique of Western [[religion|Christianity]]'s failure to institutionalize [[love|friendship]]: "There is on the earth no institution which Friendship has established; it is not taught by any religion; no [[religion|scripture]] contains its maxims. It has no temple, nor even a solitary column" (006-879).

**Personal Biography.** The essay on [[love|friendship]] cannot be separated from [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s relationship with his brother John. The journey of 1839 was their shared enterprise, and John's death three years later transformed it into a memorial. The intensity of [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s philosophy of [[love|friendship]] — its demand for perfect equality, its refusal of utility, its elegiac undertone — is the philosophy of a man who has lost the friend he loved most.
