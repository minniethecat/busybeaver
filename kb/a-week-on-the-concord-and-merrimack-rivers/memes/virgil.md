# Virgil

## Biography

Virgil (70 BCE–19 BCE) was a Roman figure of lasting significance. Roman poet, author of the Aeneid — [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s exemplar of poetry serving a different use than [[homer|Homer]]'s.

## Works / Significance

Virgil's contributions to literature and Western intellectual tradition are foundational. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] draws on their work and example at pivotal moments in *A Week*, finding in them models of intellectual integrity and spiritual [[wisdom|insight]].

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] references Virgil during his [[thought|reflection]]s on [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]], weaving their ideas and example into his meditations on truth, virtue, and the examined life. Their presence in the text reflects [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s characteristic synthesis of classical learning with immediate, personal experience — the ancient philosopher or poet speaks across centuries to illuminate the [[concord-river-mass|Concord River]] in 1839.
