## Biography

Jeremiah Richardson was a resident of Shirley, Massachusetts, in the early nineteenth century. On the Sabbath, December 18, 1803, Richardson was travelling westward with a team and rigging used to carry barrels when he was stopped and questioned by the Honorable [[ephraim-wood|Ephraim Wood]], Esquire, a justice of the peace.

Richardson stated under questioning that his fellow-traveller was [[jonas-parker|Jonas Parker]], also of Shirley, and that a Mr. Longley was his employer, "who promised to bear him out" (003-570). The incident was recorded in the annals of [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]], Massachusetts, and preserved by [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] as a glimpse into the texture of colonial and early republican governance on the Massachusetts frontier.

Richardson's name appears alongside [[jonas-parker|Jonas Parker]] in a single paragraph of the [[dunstable-settlement|Dunstable]] record that [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] transcribes in full, noting the spelling and grammar of the original document (003-569).

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] includes the record of Jeremiah Richardson in the *Sunday* chapter as a counterpoint to his own journey on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s. Richardson, questioned by the Squire about his business on the Sabbath, stands for the regulated life of settled New England society. [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] contrasts this with his own northward glide on September 1, 1839, "unquestioned by any Squire or Church Deacon and ready to bear ourselves out if need were" (003-576–003-578). The Richardson incident thus becomes a foil for the freedom of [[the-1839-boat-journey|the river journey]].
