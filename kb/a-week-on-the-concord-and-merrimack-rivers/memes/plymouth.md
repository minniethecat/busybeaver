## Overview

Plymouth is a town in Grafton County, New Hampshire, situated on the
[[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset River]], which becomes the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] downstream at its
confluence with the Winnipesaukee River. In [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s time, Plymouth marked
the uppermost point of navigation on the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]] system.

## In Thoreau's Text

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] mentions Plymouth in his description of the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]'s
navigable reach during the Sunday chapter. He notes the distances that
vessels could travel upstream from the river's mouth:

> (003-1199) Plymouth, one hundred and thirteen miles. A small steamboat
> once plied between Lowell and Nashua, before the railroad was built,
> and one now runs from Newburyport to Haverhill.

The passage places Plymouth as the farthest point upriver that small boats
could reach, at 113 miles from the Atlantic. Above Plymouth, the river
narrows into its mountain headwaters and becomes impassable to commercial
traffic.

## Geography

Plymouth lies at the confluence of the [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset]] and Baker Rivers, in
the foothills of the [[white-mountains|White Mountains]]. The [[pemigewasset|Pemigewasset]] here is still a
mountain river — swifter and narrower than the broad industrial waterway
it becomes downstream at [[benjamin-franklin|Franklin]], where it joins the Winnipesaukee to
form the [[merrimack-river|Merrimack]] proper.
