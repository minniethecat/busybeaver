## Biography

Publius Ovidius Naso (43 BCE-17 CE), known as Ovid, was one of the three canonical poets of Latin literature alongside [[virgil|Virgil]] and [[horace|Horace]]. Born to a wealthy equestrian family in Sulmo, he trained in rhetoric before devoting himself to poetry.

His early works — the *Amores*, *Ars Amatoria*, and *Heroides* — established him as the leading elegist of Rome. His masterpiece, the *Metamorphoses*, is a 15-book epic weaving together over 250 myths of transformation.

In 8 CE, Augustus banished Ovid to Tomis on the Black Sea, where he spent his final years composing the *Tristia*.

## Works / Significance

The *Metamorphoses* is among the most-read classical poems after the Bible, profoundly shaping Western art and literature. Its tales — Apollo and Daphne, Narcissus, Pyramus and Thisbe — have inspired countless painters, poets, and composers.

## In Thoreau's Text

Ovid appears once in *A Week on the [[concord-mass|Concord]] and [[merrimack-river|Merrimack River]]s*, directly quoted in the "Tuesday" chapter.

As [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] and his brother navigate the river, he reflects on the condition of the traveller moving "over unknown waves" (005-1003), citing the *Metamorphoses* of Ovid (Book I, line 133). The quotation comes from the [[art|creation]] passage where primordial earth is still subject to returning floods.

[[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]] appropriates the image to meditate on the necessary vulnerability of the traveller: to journey is to entrust oneself to forces not fully known. The classical allusion elevates a local river journey into a figure for the human condition.
