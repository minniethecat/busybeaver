## Overview

Somnambulism in *Ursule Mirouët* refers to the state of magnetic or clairvoyant sleep in which a subject gains access to perceptions beyond ordinary sensory channels — seeing at a distance, reading sealed documents, or communicating with the dead. Balzac presents this phenomenon not as fantasy but as a scientifically credible reality, drawing on the researches of Mesmer, de Puységur, and Deleuze to construct a narrative in which somnambulism is the mechanism through which divine justice operates (001-283). The novel's plot turns on Ursule's somnambulistic vision, which reveals the location of the stolen bonds and triggers the novel's resolution.

## Role in the Novel

Balzac introduces the scientific context of somnambulism through an extended historical passage that traces magnetic phenomena from Mesmer through the early nineteenth century. "The phenomena of somnambulism, hardly suspected by Mesmer, were detected by MM. de Puységur and Deleuze" (001-283), he notes, positioning the concept within a progressive scientific discovery narrative. The Revolution interrupted these researches, but by the time of the novel's events, "magnetic facts too, the miracles of somnambulism, and those of divination and ecstasy, allowing us to enter into the world of spirit, were multiplying" (001-285).

> Since Doctor Minoret's retirement to Nemours, the science of imponderable agents—the only name applicable to magnetism of which the phenomena ally it so closely with electricity and light—had made immense progress, in spite of the unfailing mockery of the Paris world of science.
>
> (001-285)

The novel's plot pivots on somnambulism as a forensic instrument. When Ursule, weakened by illness and the injustice of the stolen inheritance, enters a somnambulistic state, she sees — at a distance — the exact location of the stolen bonds and the identity of the thief. This is not presented as a random dream but as a controlled phenomenon of magnetic perception: "In this state of physical prostration, which left her soul and mind free, she became the passive medium of certain phenomena" (001-343). The passivity is crucial — Ursule does not actively seek the vision; she receives it, in keeping with the magnetic theory that the subject's will must be suspended for clairvoyance to operate.

The figure of the "Great Unknown" — the magnetic practitioner who Dr. Minoret consults in Paris — represents the apotheosis of somnambulistic practice. This extraordinary man "could produce at any moment the most curious phenomena of somnambulism by quelling the most refractory wills" (001-305). His power is described in explicitly religious terms: like Swedenborg, he claims to be "commissioned by God and in communion with the angels" (001-305). The implication is that somnambulism is not merely a scientific curiosity but a spiritual gift — a contemporary manifestation of apostolic powers.

## Key Passages

The novel's most sustained treatment of somnambulism comes in its defense of magnetic science against the skepticism of the age. Balzac laments that "magnetism was put out of court by the twofold attainder of religious believers and of materialist philosophers, both equally alarmed" (001-280). This is a remarkable passage: the Church and the Enlightenment, normally opposed, converge in their rejection of magnetic phenomena — the Church because somnambulism threatens its monopoly on the miraculous, the materialists because it threatens their metaphysics of dead matter.

The practical application of somnambulism to plot resolution comes through Ursule's vision, which operates as a kind of supernatural deposition. She sees, while entranced, the precise mechanism of the theft: the bonds hidden in a specific volume in the doctor's library. This vision, verified by Bongrand, provides the evidence that breaks Minoret-Levrault's resistance. The novel insists that this is not coincidence but causation: the magnetic fluid, channeled through Ursule's passive receptivity, reveals truth that ordinary investigation could not uncover (001-343).

## Context

Animal magnetism and somnambulism occupied a curious position in early nineteenth-century French intellectual life. Officially condemned by the Academy of Medicine in 1784, they nevertheless attracted serious practitioners and theorists throughout the Romantic period. Balzac was a committed believer: his literary universe consistently treats magnetic phenomena as real forces operating within a spiritualized physics. The preface to the *Comédie Humaine* explicitly frames Balzac's project as an investigation of the "imponderable fluids" — thought, will, magnetic influence — that he believed governed human affairs alongside the more visible forces of money and social ambition. *Ursule Mirouët* is the novel in which this thesis receives its most concentrated fictional treatment (001-277).
