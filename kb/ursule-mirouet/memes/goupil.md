# Goupil

## Overview

Goupil is the novel's most purely malicious character — a clerk of
exceptional intelligence and no moral scruple whatsoever (001-047). Trapped by his
humble birth in a position far beneath his talents, he has developed a
comprehensive contempt for humanity that expresses itself in manipulation and
sabotage. Balzac introduces him with a devastating physical description that
establishes both his abjection and his menace (001-044).

> This rascally fellow, named Goupil, was head clerk to Monsieur
> Crémière-Dionis, the notary of the town. Only to see Goupil was enough to
> tell you that he had made haste to enjoy life; for, to procure himself
> pleasure, he must have paid dearly for it. This aggregate of sinister
> details was completed by a pair of goat's eyes, the iris set in yellow
> rings, at once lascivious and cowardly.
>
> (001-044)–(001-045)

His position as clerk gives him intimate knowledge of every family's legal
and financial vulnerabilities — knowledge he deploys not for profit alone but
for the aesthetic pleasure of watching others squirm (001-100). His involvement in the
conspiracy against [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] is motivated less by greed
than by the pleasure of exercising power over those he despises — which is to
say, everyone (001-062).

## Life and Role in the Novel

Goupil works as a clerk for [[dionis|Maître Dionis]], [[dionis|the notary]] of
[[nemours|Nemours]]. His position gives him access to the legal machinery of
provincial life and an intimate knowledge of everyone's financial affairs.
His intelligence is widely recognized but his character is universally
distrusted — he is the man everyone needs and no one likes.

> No man in Nemours was more feared or more respectfully treated than Goupil.
> Strong in pretensions which his ugliness allowed, he had the detestable wit
> that is peculiar to persons who take every liberty, and he used it to be
> revenged for the mortifications of his permanent jealousy.
>
> (001-045)

His role in the [[inheritance]] plot is that of the technical expert. He
understands the legal vulnerabilities in [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s
position, knows how to exploit them, and has no compunction about doing so.
His advice to the relatives is always accurate and always malign. He is, in
effect, [[theft-of-the-will|the conspiracy]]'s brain — [[zelie-minoret|Zélie]] provides the energy,
but Goupil provides the strategy.

> "You will not live by it either," replied Goupil, making the two women
> shiver; for they, more rapidly than their husbands, interpreted as privation
> the loss of the inheritance on which they had counted for comfort. "But we
> will drown this little grief in a bottle of good wine."
>
> (001-062)

Goupil's malice is distinguished from the relatives' [[greed]] by its purity.
The relatives want money; Goupil wants to see others suffer. His pleasure in
[[theft-of-the-will|the conspiracy]] is aesthetic as much as material — he enjoys watching the
relatives squirm, enjoys the irony of their position, and enjoys most of all
the knowledge that he is smarter than everyone involved. This intellectual
contempt makes him a more dangerous antagonist than simple greed would.

His eventual fate is handled with characteristic Balzacian irony. He does
not repent — he is incapable of [[conversion|repentance]] — but he does not
prosper either. His intelligence, unmoored from any moral principle,
ultimately limits his advancement. The novel suggests that pure malice,
however clever, is self-defeating.

## Relationships

Goupil's relationship with **[[dionis|Dionis]]** is that of a resentful
subordinate to a complacent superior. He despises his employer's mediocrity
while depending on him for a living. [[dionis|Dionis]], for his part, keeps Goupil as
much from fear as from appreciation of his talents.

With **the relatives** — [[massin|Massin]], [[cremiere|Crémière]],
[[zelie-minoret|Zélie]], and the others — Goupil maintains a posture of
helpful service that conceals his contempt. He advises them because it amuses
him to do so, and because their schemes give scope to his talents.

His relationship with **[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]** is one of abstract
malice. He has no personal grievance against her; he simply finds her
innocence offensive and her destruction entertaining.

## Significance

Goupil belongs to Balzac's extensive gallery of dangerous subordinates —
intelligent men trapped by social circumstance whose resentment curdles into
malice. He prefigures characters like Vautrin (the criminal mastermind of
*Le Père Goriot* and *Illusions perdues*) in his combination of intelligence
and amorality, though on a smaller, provincial scale. His presence in the
novel serves as a reminder that evil is not always grand — sometimes it wears
a clerk's coat and amuses itself with the ruin of the innocent.

Within the moral economy of the novel, Goupil serves as a [[faith|foi]]l to
[[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]] — where the priest embodies spiritual wisdom
and selfless guidance, the clerk embodies intellectual cunning without moral
foundation. The contrast between these two figures, both acutely intelligent
but oriented toward opposite moral poles, is one of the novel's most sharply
drawn oppositions.
