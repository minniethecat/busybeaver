# Madame Massin

## Overview

Madame Massin, née Minoret, is [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s niece and one of the
collateral relatives whose hopes of inheritance are disappointed by his
decision to favor [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] (001-079). Together with her sister [[madame-cremiere|Madame Crémière]] and her
cousin-by-marriage [[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret]], she forms the trio of female relatives
whose greed drives much of the novel's plot. Her respectability — she is the
wife of a court clerk, a regular churchgoer (001-217), and a person of apparent
probity — makes her participation in [[theft-of-the-will|the conspiracy]] all the more damning
(001-069).

## Life and Role in the Novel

Madame Massin is introduced as one of the relatives who have been counting
on [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s death to improve their circumstances. Her claim on his
estate is legitimate under the Napoleonic Code — she is a blood relation —
but her attitude toward her uncle is purely instrumental (001-513). She does not love
him; she loves what he will leave behind.

Her role in [[theft-of-the-will|the conspiracy]] is that of the respectable accomplice. Unlike
[[zelie-minoret|Zélie]], whose energy drives the plot forward, or [[goupil|Goupil]], whose intelligence
devises the strategy, Madame Massin provides the cover of normality. Her
presence among the conspirators reassures the others that what they are
doing cannot be so very wrong — after all, a respectable woman like Madame
[[massin|Massin]] is part of it.

Balzac's treatment of Madame Massin is characteristic of his approach to the
provincial bourgeoisie. She is not a monster; she is an ordinary woman whose
moral sense has been eroded by years of coveting what belongs to others (001-039). Her
crime, when it comes, is not spectacular — it is the quiet, almost
unremarkable crime of a person who has persuaded herself that what she wants
is what she deserves.

Madame Massin's hypocrisy is on full display throughout the novel. She
presents herself as solicitous of her uncle's welfare while in fact
maneuvering for his money, and her piety is a thin veil over her greed:

> "You would be wise, uncle," said Madame Massin, "not to go to church without a little black velvet cap; the parish church is very damp."
>
> (001-217)

## Relationships

Madame Massin's marriage to **[[massin|Massin]]** is a partnership of mutual interest.
They share a household, a social position, and an obsession with the
inheritance that binds them more tightly than affection ever could.

With her sister **[[madame-cremiere|Madame Crémière]]**, she shares the bond of common grievance.
Their conversations — imagined rather than quoted — are presumably dominated
by the injustice of their uncle's preference for an unrelated orphan over his
own flesh and blood.

## Significance

Madame Massin represents the respectability that enables evil — the
ordinary, churchgoing, socially conventional person whose participation in
wrongdoing makes it seem normal. She is a minor figure in the novel's economy
but a significant one in its moral argument: evil is not always spectacular;
sometimes it wears a bonnet and sits in a pew.
