## Overview

The clairvoyant vision is the novel's pivotal supernatural event and the mechanism by which Balzac dismantles Doctor Minoret's Voltairean materialism. Summoned to Paris by his dying friend Doctor Bouvard, a believer in Mesmerism, Minoret witnesses a somnambulist — the "Great Unknown" of Paris — who, while asleep in a room near the Church of the Assumption, describes in precise detail what Ursule Mirouët is doing and saying at that exact moment in Nemours, many leagues away. The demonstration shatters Minoret's skepticism, setting him on the path to religious conversion.

## The Summons

At the beginning of the year, the elderly Doctor Minoret receives a letter from his old friend and former adversary Bouvard, a committed Mesmerist whom Minoret had persecuted during the magnetic controversies of the 1780s:

> My old Comrade — Every friendship, even a lost friendship, has rights which it is not easy to set aside. I know that you are still alive, and I remember less of our hostilities than of our happy days… I can wreck your incredulity by positive proofs. (001-596)

Minoret, "stung as a lion by a gadfly," rushes to Paris. Bouvard brings him to a room opposite the Church of the Assumption, where they meet the extraordinary mesmerist — "an extraordinary man, endowed by faith with tremendous powers, and a master of magnetic forces in every form of their application" (001-631).

## The Magnetic Session

The mesmerist puts his subject — a woman — into a state of clairvoyant somnambulism. She begins by revealing facts that Minoret believes could have been obtained by research: his house in Nemours, his ward Ursule, his relations. But then she goes further, describing the precise location of his hidden money:

> "You keep it in a large book bound in red, called *The Pandects of Justinian*, vol. II, between the two last pages; the book is above the sideboard with glass doors, in the division for folios." (001-733)

Minoret is shaken but demands a decisive test. He asks Bouvard to have the clairvoyant describe what Ursule will do that evening at nine o'clock — something that cannot possibly be known to anyone in Paris.

## The Vision of Ursule

At the appointed hour, the clairvoyant describes Ursule at her evening prayers with astonishing precision and tenderness:

> "Her evening prayers; she commends herself to God; she beseeches Him to keep her soul free from evil thoughts; she examines her conscience, going over all she has done during the day… she is stripping her heart bare, poor dear little thing." There were tears in the clairvoyant's eyes. (001-777)

She continues, revealing Ursule's innermost thoughts:

> "She has committed no sin; but she blames herself for having thought too much of Monsieur Savinien. She stops to wonder what he is doing in Paris, and prays to God to make him happy. She ends with you, and says a prayer aloud." (001-777)

The clairvoyant then imitates Ursule's gestures and words so exactly that "Doctor Minoret's eyes filled with tears" (001-787). He returns to Nemours with a written record of the session and verifies every detail — including the fact that Ursule had marked Saint Savinien's day with a red dot in her prayer book, something the clairvoyant had seen from across the vast distance.

## The Crisis of Unbelief

The demonstration has a devastating effect on Minoret's worldview:

> He was forced to yield to evidence. A thick wall, within himself, as it were, crumbled down; for he had lived on the double foundation of his indifference to religion and his denial of magnetism. (001-871)

By proving that the senses — purely physical organs — could participate in the attributes of infinity, magnetism overthrew Spinoza's logic for Minoret. The Finite and the Infinite, which Spinoza had declared incompatible, were shown to exist one within the other. "All his scientific theory, based on the statements of the school of Locke and Condillac, lay in ruins" (001-871).

Later, in a telling conversation with the Abbé Chaperon, the doctor reveals the depth of his crisis:

> "Do you believe in apparitions?" the infidel suddenly asked his pastor, pausing in his game. (001-875)

The abbé's response — that the Church believes in the apparitions of Our Lord and that miracles are not lacking — opens the door to the doctor's eventual conversion.

## Significance

The clairvoyant vision is the structural hinge of *Ursule Mirouët*. Before it, Minoret is a serene unbeliever; after it, he is a man whose intellectual foundations have collapsed, searching for meaning. The vision also establishes the novel's central thesis: that the material and spiritual worlds are not separate domains but interpenetrating realities, and that love — Ursule's love for Savinien, visible to the clairvoyant across space itself — is one of the bridges between them. Balzac uses the mesmeric phenomenon to argue, through fiction, for a universe in which providence, prayer, and supernatural knowledge are as real as the physical sciences.
