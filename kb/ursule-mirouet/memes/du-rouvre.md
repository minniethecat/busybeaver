## Overview

The Marquis du Rouvre is an impoverished aristocrat whose financial ruin serves as a critical plot mechanism in *[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule Mirouët]]*. Owner of the magnificent Château du Rouvre — described as "one of the finest in all the Gatinais" — the Marquis is nevertheless deep in debt, his estate "eaten up by [[death|mort]]gages" (001-065). His financial entanglement with the Portenduère family and his daughter's marriage prospects drive much of the novel's second half.

A representative of the declining ancien régime nobility, the Marquis du Rouvre embodies the post-Revolutionary aristocracy's struggle to maintain its estates in an age of bourgeois ascendance. His predicament — land-rich but cash-poor, proud but powerless against creditors — creates the pressure that forces [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien de Portenduère]] toward a marriage of convenience and, ultimately, sets the stage for the novel's resolution.

## Role in the Novel

The Marquis first enters the narrative as a former client of [[bongrand|Monsieur Bongrand]], the [[justice]] of the peace. [[bongrand|Bongrand]] describes him as a man "within the power" of his creditors, his estate [[death|mort]]gaged to its limit:

> Among his former clients Bongrand counted the Marquis du Rouvre, on whose property, eaten up by mortgages, he had claims for a considerable sum.
> — (001-065)

The Marquis's crushing debt becomes the engine of the novel's central romantic crisis. [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien de Portenduère]]'s mother, seeking to restore the family [[fortune]], pressures her son to marry Mademoiselle du Rouvre, an only daughter whose uncles — including the Marquis de Ronquerolles — could settle a substantial dowry:

> He can marry Mademoiselle du Rouvre any day he likes, an only daughter.
> — (002-402)

[[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]], however, loves [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule Mirouët]] and resists the arranged marriage. The tension between love and financial necessity, centered on the du Rouvre alliance, dominates the middle section of the novel.

In the novel's dénouement, the Marquis du Rouvre's estate becomes part of the Portenduère family's recovery. After [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]'s marriage to [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]], the Château du Rouvre and its lands pass into their possession through a deed of gift — ironically, the very symbol of aristocratic decline becomes the foundation of the young couple's prosperity:

> The deed of gift, his estate of le Rouvre, and twenty-eight thousand francs a year.
> — (002-846)

## Relationships

**Mademoiselle du Rouvre** — His daughter, whom he hopes to marry advantageously to restore the family [[fortune]]s (002-402). She represents the traditional aristocratic strategy of using marriage to resolve financial difficulties.

**[[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien de Portenduère]]** — The young vicomte pressured by his mother to marry Mademoiselle du Rouvre as a financial expedient (002-458). [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]'s refusal, rooted in his love for [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]], is a key moral choice in the novel.

**Marquis de Ronquerolles** — His brother-in-law, one of the powerful relatives whose influence is expected to facilitate Mademoiselle du Rouvre's marriage settlement (002-414).

**[[bongrand|Monsieur Bongrand]]** — The [[justice]] of the peace and the Marquis's former legal counsel, who understands the full extent of his financial ruin (001-058).

**[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]** — The wealthy doctor whose [[fortune]], ultimately inherited by [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]], enables the Portenduère family to acquire the du Rouvre estate without the loveless marriage (002-584).

## Significance

The Marquis du Rouvre exemplifies a central theme of Balzac's *Comédie Humaine*: the transfer of property from the old aristocracy to the rising bourgeoisie and, in this case, its eventual recovery through a morally-deserving alliance. The Château du Rouvre — "a real royal château" with eighty thousand francs a year in land (002-738) — passes from a bankrupt nobleman to a couple whose union represents the novel's ideal synthesis of spiritual virtue ([[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]) and reformed aristocracy ([[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]). This transfer of property through love rather than calculation affirms the novel's moral vision against the cynical marriage-market represented by the du Rouvre match.

The Marquis himself remains an off-stage figure for most of the novel, his presence felt through his debts and his daughter rather than through direct action — a narrative technique that emphasizes how the old aristocracy has become a passive force, acted upon by financial realities it can no longer control.
