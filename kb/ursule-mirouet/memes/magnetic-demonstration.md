# The Magnetic Demonstration

## Overview

The magnetic demonstration in [[paris|Paris]] is the pivotal event of
Balzac's *Ursule Mirouët* — the moment when
[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s materialist worldview is confronted with
evidence it cannot explain. A somnambulist, under magnetic trance, accurately
describes events occurring at Minoret's home in [[nemours|Nemours]], providing
proof of [[clairvoyance]] so precise that the old physician must either deny
his own empirical standards or revise his philosophy. He chooses revision
(001-287).

## Narrative Account

Summoned by his dying friend Bouvard, Minoret travels to
[[paris|Paris]] and is taken to the Church of the Assumption (001-289), where the
"Great Unknown" — a magnetic healer of extraordinary powers — conducts
demonstrations. A woman in magnetic sleep is asked to describe what is
happening at that moment in Minoret's house in [[nemours|Nemours]].

> The clairvoyant so exactly imitated the child's innocent gestures and
> saintly aspirations that Doctor Minoret's eyes filled with tears.
>
> (001-383)

The somnambulist describes [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] at her evening
[[prayer|prayers]] with uncanny precision — her gestures, her examination of
conscience, even her silent thoughts about
[[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]. Minoret, initially skeptical, is moved
to tears by the accuracy of the description.

Upon returning home, he verifies every detail: the displacement of two
volumes of the Pandects (001-356), the difference between two banknotes, and the small
red dots on Ursule's almanac marking saints' days. The verification is
complete and devastating.

> A thick wall, within himself, as it were, crumbled down; for he had lived
> on the double foundation of his indifference to religion and his denial of
> magnetism. By proving that the senses — a purely physical structure, mere
> organs whose effects can all be explained — were conterminous with some of
> the attributes of infinity, magnetism overthrew, or at any rate seemed to
> him to overthrow, Spinoza's powerful logic.
>
> (001-425)

## Significance

The magnetic demonstration is the hinge on which the novel's spiritual plot
turns. Before it, Minoret is a benevolent materialist; after it, he is on the
path to Catholic [[faith]]. The event encapsulates Balzac's thesis: that
genuine empiricism, honestly pursued, leads toward rather than away from
spiritual reality. It is also a remarkable piece of narrative construction —
a scene that works simultaneously as scientific experiment, psychological
drama, and spiritual revelation.

The demonstration also establishes the novel's supernatural logic. Once
[[clairvoyance]] is proven as a real phenomenon, the later revelations about
the [[theft-of-the-will|stolen will]] — communicated through the same
magnetic faculty — carry conviction rather than absurdity. The Paris
demonstration is thus the narrative precondition for the novel's entire
supernatural resolution.
