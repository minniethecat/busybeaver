# Greed

## Overview

Greed — the disordered desire for money and the things money can buy — is the defining vice of the Minoret relatives in Balzac's novel. Their greed is not the spectacular avarice of a Grandet or a Gobseck; it is the more ordinary, more insidious greed of provincial bourgeois who have counted on an inheritance for fifteen years and cannot bear to see it slip away. Balzac's genius is to show how this ordinary greed, given the right circumstances, escalates into conspiracy, theft, and a crime that only supernatural intervention can expose (001-062)(002-076).

The relatives are not monsters. Minoret-Levrault, the postmaster and tax-collector, has built his own fortune through trade — "in thirty-six years, the Revolution helping, he had made a fortune of thirty thousand francs a year" (001-012) — yet this financial success only feeds his appetite for more. His wife Zélie is consumed by the desire for wealth; the family's grasping ambition forms the novel's primary antagonistic force. The novel's realism lies in its refusal to make the relatives exceptional villains. They are ordinary people whose greed, given the opportunity of an unguarded fortune, becomes criminal.

## Role in the Novel

The greed of the relatives is established in the novel's opening pages as a fact of provincial life. Everyone in Nemours knows that the Minoret collaterals are waiting for the doctor to die; the relatives themselves discuss the inheritance openly, calculating shares and debating strategies. This is greed normalized — so embedded in the social fabric that no one, including the relatives themselves, recognizes it as a moral problem. The heirs' sense of entitlement is greed disguised as justice: "The loss of the inheritance on which they had counted for fifteen years seemed to them as great a privation as the loss of their daily bread" (001-062).

Balzac traces the progressive corruption that greed produces. At first, the relatives merely hope and wait. Then they scheme — consulting lawyers, spreading rumors about Ursule, attempting to manipulate the old man. The extended Minoret family "nourished a ferment of jealousy against the doctor and his protégée" (001-108); this jealousy rapidly becomes the engine of the plot. When the doctor's Sunday visits to church prompt alarm among the heirs, the revenue collector Crémière demands, "What do you say to your uncle's conversion?" (001-041) — the religious transformation registered only as a threat to their financial expectations.

The escalation accelerates when Minoret's will is made and their disinheritance is confirmed. The relatives commit a crime: stealing and destroying the will, defrauding an orphan of her legacy. Each step is rationalized, each escalation justified by the previous one. The novel's psychological realism is most acute in this portrayal of how ordinary greed, unchecked, becomes criminal. Goupil, the notary's clerk, facilitates the crime not for money alone but for the pleasure of watching respectable people debase themselves — "Where is my money?" he demands (001-353), his greed stripped of all pretense.

The counterexample is Ursule, whose complete indifference to money is not naivete but virtue. She never asks about the inheritance, never calculates her share, and would be content with whatever her guardian chose to provide. When she learns that Savinien is in debt, her instinct is generosity: "Give him all you intended to leave me. What can a poor girl like me want of money! — and he, in prison!" (001-353). Her disinterestedness is the moral standard against which the relatives' greed is measured. The novel's resolution — the fortune goes to Ursule, the guilty are punished — affirms a providential order in which greed is its own undoing.

The ironic spectacle of the heirs in church, their piety a mask for avarice:

> On seeing every face turned to look at her, Zélie hastily retired, and came out on to the Square again less precipitately than she had gone into the church; she had counted on that inheritance, and the inheritance was becoming problematical.
>

The novel's moral judgment on greed is clear throughout. The inheritance is described as a "comedy" not because it is trivial but because the relatives' behavior — their schemes, their anxiety, their ultimate crime — has the structure of farce even as its consequences are tragic. Massin's wife warns the doctor "not to go to church without a little black velvet cap" (001-217), a detail that captures the provincial conflation of physical comfort with spiritual practice — greed wearing the mask of solicitude.

## Context

Greed (*avarice*) is a recurring theme throughout *La Comédie Humaine*, treated most famously in *Eugénie Grandet* (1833) and *Gobseck* (1830). Balzac's treatment of greed reflects both his Catholic moral framework (avarice as one of the seven deadly sins) and his sociological observation of the French bourgeoisie under the July Monarchy, when the pursuit of wealth had largely replaced older forms of social ambition. In *Ursule Mirouët*, greed is domesticated — brought into the provincial family circle and shown to be not a grand passion but a petty vice, all the more destructive for its ordinariness.

The novel's specific contribution to Balzac's anatomy of greed is its portrayal of collective avarice. The relatives are not isolated misers; they form a community of greed, reinforcing each other's expectations and rationalizations. This social dimension distinguishes the greed of *Ursule Mirouët* from the individual obsessions of Grandet or Gobseck. Here, greed is a family disease, passed through generations (001-010), normalized by provincial culture, and ultimately exposed by the supernatural intervention that human justice cannot accomplish alone (002-573).
