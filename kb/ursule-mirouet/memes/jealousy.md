# Jealousy

## Overview

Jealousy in *Ursule Mirouët* is the primary emotional engine of the novel's antagonists. The Minoret heirs' resentment of Ursule, Goupil's professional and personal envy, and the petty jealousies of provincial life together create the atmosphere of hostility that threatens the heroine. Balzac presents jealousy as the characteristic vice of provincial France — a compound of material greed, social resentment, and spiritual barrenness that stands in direct opposition to the generosity and innocence embodied by Ursule.

## Role in the Novel

### Goupil's Permanent Jealousy

The clerk Goupil embodies jealousy as a permanent condition. He endures "the mortifications of his permanent jealousy" (001-045), a state that finds outlet in his "satirical couplets" and sharp-edged wit. His jealousy is both social — resentment of those above him — and personal, directed at anyone who enjoys the advantages he lacks. Goupil's position as notary's clerk places him in daily proximity to wealth he cannot possess, and this proximity intensifies rather than soothes his envy. His "satirical couplets" function as a weapon of the powerless, a way of wounding those he cannot surpass.

### The Heirs' Material Envy

The extended Minoret family "nourished a ferment of jealousy against the doctor and his protégée" (001-108). This jealousy operates on multiple levels. Most immediately, it is material: each heir calculates what share of Doctor Minoret's fortune should be theirs by right of kinship, and Ursule's presence threatens that calculation. Zélie Minoret in particular embodies this pecuniary jealousy: "the postmistress had a special hatred of Ursule" (001-108), a hatred indistinguishable from her fear of losing the inheritance.

Beyond the material, the heirs' jealousy carries a social dimension. Ursule represents cultivation, taste, and spiritual refinement — qualities the heirs neither possess nor value, yet instinctively resent. Their jealousy is thus a form of class anxiety disguised as family grievance.

### Escalation into Conspiracy

The heirs' jealousy does not remain passive. It escalates from muttered complaints in the Nemours post-office to active conspiracy after the doctor's death. When the will is stolen, it is the jealousy of the heirs — their conviction that Ursule has no right to what they regard as family property — that provides the moral justification for the crime. Jealousy thus transforms from an emotion into an action, from private resentment into public calumny.

### Romantic Jealousy

The novel also explores jealousy in the realm of love. Savinien de Portenduère becomes "acquainted with the torments of jealousy, pangs hitherto unknown" (002-419), experiencing for the first time the vulnerability that comes with deep attachment. Unlike the heirs' corrosive envy, romantic jealousy in the novel is portrayed as a natural, if painful, dimension of love — a sign of genuine feeling rather than moral failure. It humanizes Savinien, revealing depths of emotion beneath his aristocratic reserve.
