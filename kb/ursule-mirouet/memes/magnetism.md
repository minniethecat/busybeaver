# Magnetism

## Overview

Magnetism (also called animal magnetism or mesmerism) is a theory and
practice centered on the existence of an invisible, imponderable fluid that
permeates all living beings and can be directed by human will. In Balzac's
*Ursule Mirouët*, magnetism serves as both a plot mechanism and a
philosophical argument — it is the force that shatters
[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s lifelong materialist skepticism and opens him
to the spiritual dimension of existence. The novel treats magnetism not as
quackery but as a genuine [[science]] awaiting proper recognition, closely
allied to electricity and light (001-285).

## Role in the Novel

Magnetism enters the narrative through [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]], a
retired physician and convinced atheist who has lived his life by the
rationalist principles of Locke, Condillac, and the Encyclopedists. His
worldview is challenged when an old friend from his student days, Bouvard,
writes to announce that he has proof of magnetism's reality and wishes to
demonstrate it before dying (001-287).

At Bouvard's urging, Minoret travels to [[paris|Paris]] and witnesses a
somnambulist (clairvoyant) under magnetic trance. The somnambulist accurately
describes events occurring at that very moment in Minoret's home in
[[nemours|Nemours]] — including details about his ward
[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]'s evening [[prayer|prayers]] and the precise
contents of his study (001-378). She also reveals that Ursule has a secret
attachment to young [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien de Portenduère]],
something Minoret had not yet fully recognized (001-419).

The clairvoyant's revelations are minutely verified upon Minoret's return.
She correctly identified the displacement of two volumes of the Pandects, the
difference between two banknotes, and — most tellingly — the small red dots
Ursule had marked on her almanac for saints' days, including Savinien's
(001-425). For a man who had built his intellectual life on materialist
philosophy, this evidence is devastating.

Minoret's [[conversion]] experience is described as the collapse of an inner
fortress. The novel presents this not as superstition overcoming
[[reason]], but as a broader and truer empiricism — one that accepts evidence
even when it contradicts philosophical presuppositions. Magnetism thus
becomes the bridge between Minoret's scientific past and the religious
[[faith]] he will eventually embrace through Ursule's influence (001-425).

## Key Passages

The novel provides a sweeping historical account of magnetism's reception:

> At the end of the eighteenth century science was as deeply rent by the
> apparition of Mesmer as art was by that of Gluck. After his rediscovery of
> magnetism, Mesmer came to France, whither from time immemorial inventors
> have resorted to find protection for their discoveries.
>
> (001-277)

Balzac situates magnetism within a long tradition:

> Magnetism has more applications; in Mesmer's hands it was in relation to
> its future development what a principle is to results. But though the
> discoverer lacked genius, it is sad for human reason and for France to have
> to own that a science contemporaneous with the earliest civilization,
> cultivated in Egypt and Chaldea, in Greece and in India, met in Paris at
> the high tide of the eighteenth century with the same fate as the truth
> embodied in Galileo in the sixteenth.
>
> (001-280)

The Great Unknown articulates the theory in explicitly spiritual terms:

> "I am not in the habit of prostituting a power which, it is my full
> conviction, emanates from God; if I made a frivolous or evil use of it, it
> might be taken from me."
>
> (001-312)

The aftermath of proof is described as a philosophical earthquake:

> A thick wall, within himself, as it were, crumbled down; for he had lived
> on the double foundation of his indifference to religion and his denial of
> magnetism. By proving that the senses — a purely physical structure, mere
> organs whose effects can all be explained — were conterminous with some of
> the attributes of infinity, magnetism overthrew, or at any rate seemed to
> him to overthrow, Spinoza's powerful logic.
>
> (001-425)

## Context

Mesmerism was a live controversy in Balzac's France. Franz Anton Mesmer
(1734–1815) had proposed the theory of animal magnetism in the 1770s, and
though officially discredited by a royal commission in 1784, his ideas
continued to attract followers. By the 1830s and 1840s, when Balzac was
writing *La Comédie Humaine*, a revival of interest in magnetic phenomena —
somnambulism, clairvoyance, and healing trances — was underway.

Balzac himself was a convinced believer in animal magnetism and incorporated
magnetic themes throughout his work, notably in *Louis Lambert*, *Séraphîta*,
and *Le Cousin Pons*. In *Ursule Mirouët*, magnetism serves a distinct
narrative purpose: it is the empirical bridge that allows a scientific
materialist to move toward religious [[faith]] without renouncing his
commitment to evidence. This aligns with Balzac's broader project of
reconciling [[science]] and spirituality within a unified vision of human
nature.
