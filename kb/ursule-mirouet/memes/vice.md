Throughout *Ursule Mirouët*, **vice** appears as the counterpart to [[virtue]], forming the moral axis along which Balzac judges his characters. The novel presents a gallery of vices — greed, envy, hypocrisy, and cruelty — concentrated in the provincial heirs who encircle Doctor Minoret's fortune.

## The Heirs' Vices

The Minoret heirs embody the vices of the provincial bourgeoisie. Goupil's cunning malice, [[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret-Levrault]]'s avarice, and the heirs' collective envy of Ursule form a portrait of human selfishness. Balzac describes their world as one where the same blood produces both "the finest lawn or the coarsest lining" (001-067), suggesting virtue and vice are choices within shared circumstances.

Doctor Minoret's modest fortune inspires speculation about his moral character:

> "To have laid by no more than eight thousand francs a year, the doctor must have had many vices or virtues to indulge." (001-094)

The neighborhood cannot decide whether his thrift indicates hidden vice or hidden virtue — a ambiguity that Balzac uses to contrast true morality with its social appearance.

## Greed and Envy

The most prominent vices in the novel are financial: the townsfolk "kept a greedy eye" (001-065) on the doctor's fortune, and they speak "with covert envy of the day when the old man's" wealth would be dispersed (001-134). This predatory anticipation marks the provincial world as one where money governs all relationships. Even after the will's resolution, Dionis and Crémière are still found "envying her lot" (002-473) — Ursule's happiness and fortune remain objects of covetous resentment.

## Vice and Social Order

In Balzac's taxonomy, vice is closely tied to class. The Parisian aristocracy (the Portenduères) may suffer from pride and ruin, but the provincial bourgeoisie suffer from pettier vices — greed, envy, and spite. The novel suggests that vice flourishes where education and refinement are absent, as exemplified by the contrast between the cultivated Ursule and the ignorant heirs. The doctor himself, aware of the grasping nature surrounding him, took precautions so that "he might not be the victim of greed" (001-113).

## Vice as Plot Mechanism

The theft of the will is the novel's defining act of vice (001-273). It sets in motion the supernatural intervention of Ursule's vision and the eventual legal and moral resolution. Vice, in Balzac's scheme, inevitably produces its own punishment — Minoret-Levrault's remorse, Zélie's terror, and the family's disgrace.
