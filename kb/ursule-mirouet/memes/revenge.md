# Revenge

## Overview

Revenge is the engine of villainy in *[[Ursule-Mirouet|Ursule Mirouët]]*, concentrated almost
entirely in the character of [[goupil|Goupil]]. Balzac traces revenge to its
psychological root — wounded vanity — and follows its trajectory from petty
malice through systematic conspiracy to ultimate self-destruction. The novel's
moral architecture is revealed in the contrast between human revenge ([[goupil|Goupil]]'s
plot against [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] and her guardian) and divine retribution
(the providential exposure of the crime). The final irony is [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]'s
recognition that his revenge "had been taken in hand by One more powerful than
he" (002-839) — human vengeance is not merely wicked but superfluous.

## Role in the Novel

Goupil's character is introduced as a study in [[jealousy|resentment]]. His physical ugliness
and social position as a clerk feed a permanent state of grievance: "he had the
detestable wit that is peculiar to persons who take every liberty, and he made
use of it to be revenged for the mortifications of his permanent jealousy"
(001-045). From his first appearance, revenge is Goupil's primary motivation — he
"watched Madame [[dionis|Dionis]] with an eye to revenge" (001-045) even against his own
employer.

Goupil's instinct for revenge is strategic rather than impulsive. When he
approaches [[massin|Massin]], the [[justice|justice]]'s registrar, he does so by "insinuating an idea
of revenge" (001-060), weaponizing the registrar's own grievances against Doctor
Minoret. Revenge is contagious — Goupil spreads it like an infection through the
network of heritors.

The novel's most psychologically acute moment comes when Goupil, frustrated in
his schemes, restrains himself with cold calculation: "I should lose all chance
of revenge" (002-313). This internal discipline makes him more dangerous than a
merely passionate villain — his patience is a form of malice. When he finally
arrives at the moment of triumph, his face is "beaming with the thoughts of
revenge that had blossomed in his heart in the course of his walk" (002-517),
and his question — "Can I be revenged?" (002-525) — is followed by savage irony:
"Are you sufficiently revenged?" (002-548).

But revenge in Balzac's moral universe is ultimately self-defeating. Savinien
refuses to duel Goupil himself — he will demand satisfaction from Goupil's son
instead, a revenge deferred across a generation (002-577). And before that
confrontation can occur, divine justice intervenes. The accident that costs
Minoret both his legs, coming immediately after the exposure of the crime, is
recognized by all as a judgment. Savinien sees "that his revenge had been taken
in hand by One more powerful than he" (002-839), and the recognition transforms
his anger into something closer to awe.

## Key Passages

Goupil's character is defined from the outset by his relationship to revenge:

> He had the detestable wit that is peculiar to persons who take every liberty,
> and he made use of it to be revenged for the mortifications of his permanent
> jealousy.
>
> (001-045)

The cold discipline of resentment is captured in a single line:

> "I should lose all chance of revenge," and his dry heart swelled in his bosom
> like a sponge.
>
> (002-313)

The novel's resolution transfers vengeance from human to divine hands:

> Savinien saw that his revenge had been taken in hand by One more powerful
> than he.
>
> (002-839)

## Context

Revenge as a literary theme has deep roots in French drama, from Corneille's *Le
Cid* to the revenge tragedies of the seventeenth century. Balzac's innovation is
to relocate revenge from the aristocratic duel to the bourgeois conspiracy — his
avenger is not a nobleman with a sword but a [[lawyer|notary]]'s clerk with legal documents
and forged signatures. This modernization of the revenge plot reflects the
social transformation of post-Revolutionary France, where power had shifted from
the sword to the quill. At the same time, the novel's Catholic framework
insists on the ultimate inadequacy of human vengeance: the final scene at
Minoret's gate, with the crowd gathering around the crushed colossus, is a
judgment scene in which the agent is not man but God.
