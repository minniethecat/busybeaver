# The Theft of the Will

## Overview

The theft of Doctor Minoret's will is the criminal act at the center of the
novel's final crisis. After [[dr-minoret|Minoret]]'s [[minoret-death|death]],
his collateral relatives — enraged by their disinheritance — conspire to
steal and destroy the will that leaves his [[fortune]] to
[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]. Orchestrated by [[goupil|Goupil]] and executed
with [[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret]]'s ruthless energy, the crime is legally
perfect (no evidence, no witnesses) but morally untenable: a "sacred contract, destroyed afterwards" (001-599) cannot be undone in the court of conscience, even if it leaves no trace in the court of law: the
[[remorse]] it generates becomes the instrument of divine [[justice]]
(002-239).

## Narrative Account

Minoret's final will, made shortly before his death with
[[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]] as witness, leaves the bulk of his estate to
[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]. The relatives, who have counted on the
[[inheritance]] for fifteen years, are stunned. In the confusion following
the funeral, they locate and steal the document. The will is destroyed;
Ursule is left destitute; the crime appears perfect (002-779).

> The comedy of the inheritance will begin. — God grant that the wicked may
> repent.
>
> (002-239)

But the theft generates consequences its perpetrators did not anticipate.
[[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret]], the most forceful of the conspirators,
begins to experience supernatural visitations.
[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s ghost appears to her, demanding justice (002-752). Her
physical and mental deterioration — sleeplessness, wasting, terror — drives
her to confession.

The confession, made to the [[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]], reveals the
crime and restores Ursule's [[inheritance]] (002-832). The novel's resolution thus
operates on two levels: human justice (the legal system, which failed) is
overridden by divine justice (conscience, activated by
[[clairvoyance|supernatural intervention]]).

## The Conspirators and Their Roles

| Conspirator | Role in the Theft | Outcome |
|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| [[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret]] | Primary executor; physically steals and destroys the will | Haunted by ghost; driven to confession by [[remorse]] |
| [[goupil|Goupil]] | Strategic architect; identifies legal vulnerability and plans the theft | Self-defeated; malice ultimately limits his advancement |
| [[massin|Massin]] | Complicit beneficiary; provides access and cover | Exposed; loses ill-gotten gains |
| [[cremiere|Crémière]] | Passive conspirator; benefits from the crime without direct action | Shares in the moral and material consequences |

## Significance

The theft of the will demonstrates Balzac's conviction that crime contains
the seeds of its own exposure. The relatives' [[greed]], which drives them to
theft, also makes them vulnerable to the [[remorse]] that the theft
generates. The event is the novel's moral climax — the moment when the
conflict between greed and [[justice]] is resolved in favor of justice, not
through human institutions but through the operation of conscience and grace.

Within the novel's providential framework, the theft serves as the
indispensable dark moment that makes the eventual restoration meaningful. The
crime's exposure through [[magnetism|magnetic]] [[clairvoyance]] confirms the
novel's central argument: that invisible realities — whether magnetic fluids
or divine justice — are no less real for being imponderable.
