## Overview

The Swedenborgian — referred to in the novel as "the Great Unknown" — is a magnetic healer and clairvoyance practitioner living in Paris in the 1820s. Like Emanuel Swedenborg, the eighteenth-century Swedish mystic, he claims to be in direct communion with God and the angels, and to possess supernatural powers of healing and second sight (001-305). Doctor Bouvard, a lifelong believer in Mesmer's theories, brings his old friend and adversary Doctor Minoret to meet the Swedenborgian, hoping to shatter Minoret's materialist scepticism through direct experience (001-308).

## Role in the Novel

The Swedenborgian appears in a single extended scene but exerts an enormous influence on the novel's spiritual trajectory. Doctor Minoret, a disciple of the Encyclopedists and an ardent anti-Mesmerist, is brought by Bouvard to the Swedenborgian's modest apartments near the Church of the Assumption in Paris (001-308). There, the Swedenborgian demonstrates the reality of clairvoyance through a somnambulist woman who, while in a magnetic trance, perceives events occurring miles away in Nemours — specifically, the actions of Minoret's ward Ursule (001-317).

The demonstration shakes Minoret's scepticism to its foundation. The Swedenborgian's somnambulist correctly describes banknotes Minoret is carrying, then recounts Ursule's exact actions at that moment in Nemours, including her prayer for Minoret's conversion (001-363). When Minoret returns home and verifies that Ursule indeed did and said precisely what the clairvoyant reported, his materialist worldview begins to crumble (001-374).

The Swedenborgian's influence extends beyond this single scene. After the demonstration, Doctor Minoret undertakes an intensive course of spiritual reading, studying the works of Swedenborg himself alongside Pascal, Bossuet, Bonald, and Saint-Augustine (001-426). The concept of the "inner self" that the Swedenborgian articulated — a spiritual faculty capable of perceiving truths beyond the physical senses — resurfaces in Part II, when Ursule and Savinien feel an instinctive revulsion toward Goupil, a presentiment "which might be accounted for by an impulse of that inner self of which the Swedenborgian had spoken to Doctor Minoret" (002-036).

## Key Passages

The Swedenborgian is introduced as a figure of immense spiritual power who has withdrawn from public life:

> At this time there was in Paris an extraordinary man, endowed by faith with tremendous powers, and a master of magnetic forces in every form of their application. Not only did this Great Unknown, who is still living, cure unaided, and at any distance, the most painful and inveterate diseases — cure them suddenly and radically, as of old did the Redeemer of man — but he also could produce at any moment the most curious phenomena of somnambulism by quelling the most refractory wills. (001-305)

His physical presence is described in commanding terms:

> The countenance of the Unknown, who, like Swedenborg, declares himself to be commissioned by God and in communion with the angels, is that of a lion; it is radiant with concentrated and irresistible energy. His features, of a singular cast, have a terrible and overwhelming power; his voice, coming from the depths of his being, seems charged with magnetic fluid, and enters the listener by every pore. (001-305)

When Minoret mockingly asks about Mesmer's famous "tub" (baquet), the Swedenborgian's reply establishes the theological basis of his practice:

> "Nothing but the power of God," gravely replied the Swedenborgian, whom Minoret supposed to be a man of about fifty. (001-310)

## Context

The Swedenborgian movement in early nineteenth-century France emerged from the writings of Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772), a Swedish scientist and theologian who claimed to have visited Heaven and Hell and to have conversed with angels and spirits. His teachings gained a significant following in Romantic-era Europe, particularly among those seeking a reconciliation of science and spirituality. Balzac himself was deeply interested in Swedenborgian and Mesmeric ideas, exploring them in several novels of *La Comédie Humaine*, including *Louis Lambert* and *Séraphîta*.

The fictional Swedenborgian of *Ursule Mirouët* represents the convergence of Mesmeric science and Christian mysticism. By attributing his powers to God rather than to Mesmer's "imponderable fluids," he offers a spiritually acceptable path for Minoret's conversion — bridging the gap between the doctor's scientific training and the religious faith that Ursule and the Abbé Chaperon embody.
