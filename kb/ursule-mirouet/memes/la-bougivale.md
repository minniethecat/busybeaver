# La Bougival

## Overview

La Bougival is [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s housekeeper and one of the novel's most
memorable minor characters. A woman of the people — vigorous, plain-spoken,
and absolutely loyal — she represents the virtues that Balzac associates
with those uncorrupted by money and social ambition. Her devotion to [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]
is total; her contempt for the scheming relatives is unconcealed; and her
role in the novel's resolution, though modest, is decisive.

## Life and Role in the Novel

La Bougival enters the novel as an established presence in Minoret's
household. She has served the doctor for years and regards herself — with
some justification — as the true guardian of the household's moral order.
Her first loyalty is to Minoret; her second, and increasingly her first in
practice, is to [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] (001-110).

Bougival's narrative function is partly comic. Her blunt assessments of the
relatives provide a running commentary on their scheming that is both
accurate and entertaining. She says what the reader is thinking, and her
lack of social polish is presented not as a defect but as evidence of her
authenticity (001-035). In a world of calculation and hypocrisy, Bougival is
refreshingly incapable of dissimulation.

But Bougival is more than comic relief. Her fierce protectiveness toward
[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] makes her a moral force in the household. She recognizes the
relatives' schemes before Minoret does and never hesitates to voice her
suspicions (001-256). After [[minoret-death|Minoret's death]], when [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] is defrauded of her
inheritance, Bougival's loyalty becomes practical — she stays with the
impoverished young woman, sharing her privations and sustaining her spirits (002-244). During the doctor's final illness, Ursule assumed the sickroom duties herself, having "relieved La Bougival by taking care of the old man" (001-274) — a reversal of roles that deepened their bond.

Bougival's role in the novel's resolution, while modest, is real. Her
knowledge of the household's affairs, her memory of conversations and events,
and her willingness to testify to the truth all contribute to the eventual
exposure of the relatives' crime. She represents the incorruptibility of
simple people — those whom money cannot buy because they have never learned
to value it above loyalty.

> La Bougival, well lectured on the subject—and, indeed, far too simple in mind and nature to interfere—did nothing to mar the work of these great spirits.
>
> (001-256)

## Relationships

Bougival's relationship with **[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule Mirouët]]** is maternal. Having no
children of her own, she has transferred her considerable capacity for love
to the orphaned girl. Her protectiveness is fierce and total.

With **[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]**, Bougival maintains the formal respect due to an
employer, but her long service has given her a familiarity that borders on
partnership. She manages his household, anticipates his needs, and speaks
her mind with a frankness he tolerates and even values.

Toward **the relatives**, Bougival's attitude is one of unconcealed contempt.
She sees through their polite manners to their greedy hearts and makes no
effort to hide her opinion. This honesty — which the relatives interpret as
impertinence — is one of the novel's running jokes.

## Significance

La Bougival belongs to Balzac's extensive gallery of servants whose loyalty
and common sense contrast favorably with the corruption of their social
superiors. Like the servant figures in *Eugénie Grandet* and *Le Père
Goriot*, Bougival represents a standard of decency that is independent of
social class. Her presence in the novel serves as a reminder that virtue is
not a function of education or rank — and that the simple may sometimes see
more clearly than the sophisticated.
