The concept of **grace** operates on multiple levels in *Ursule Mirouët* — theological, social, and aesthetic. As a religious term it describes divine favor and the power of conversion; as a social quality it marks the refinement of character that distinguishes [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] and her allies from the coarse provincial heirs.

## Divine Grace and Conversion

The novel's most dramatic instance of grace is the conversion of Minoret-Levrault. After Ursule's vision reveals his crime, the [[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]] prays:

> "This sudden effect of grace was almost electrical. The curé was so greatly rejoiced that he was near fainting." (001-446)

Minoret-Levrault's repentance is presented not as reasoned moral choice but as an overwhelming influx of divine grace, consistent with the novel's [[magnetism|Mesmeric]] and [[spiritual]] framework. The Abbé's prayer invokes "Thy grace, that he may dwell in Thee" (001-382), framing the ex-atheist's conversion as a triumph of the supernatural over materialism.

## Social Grace

Beyond theology, grace describes the refined bearing of the novel's sympathetic characters. [[jordy|Monsieur de Jordy]] possessed "courage, grace, learning, and all the most delightful qualities" (001-120) that made his hidden past all the more mysterious. Ursule displays "the native grace with which graceful women manage" (001-199), her physical poise expressing inner virtue.

## Melancholy Grace

Balzac extends the concept to the aesthetics of decline. [[madame-de-portenduere|Madame de Portenduère]], in her old age, is "not devoid of a certain melancholy grace in spite of a front of dejected curls" (001-222). This grace-in-ruin suggests that refinement persists even as the aristocratic world crumbles — a theme that connects to the novel's broader treatment of the old nobility's fate.

## Grace and Physical Appearance

Grace in the novel is not merely abstract; it manifests in physical bearing and dress. Ursule's appearance at Mass is described with precise attention to the elements that compose her charm: she wore a dress of "gauze, with white satin shoes, graced with white ribbons, a fillet" (001-260), and she "committed with the best possible grace, and sat down by the window" (001-263) — each detail reinforcing the correspondence between outward elegance and inward purity. Balzac explicitly connects the two registers: Ursule displayed "the native grace with which graceful women manage" (001-199) and possessed "one of the most seductive graces of woman" (001-201), her straw hat and simple adornments conveying the natural refinement that distinguishes her from the calculating Minoret heirs.
