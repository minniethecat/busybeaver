# The Massin Family

## Overview

The Massin family is one of the four founding bourgeois stocks of Nemours,
descended from the merchants who established themselves in the town. Along
with the Minorets, Crémières, and Levraults, the Massins are woven into the
town's identity through generations of intermarriage — a dense cousinage
that makes every significant event in Nemours a family affair (001-065).
In the novel, Massin (the registrar) and his wife Madame Massin are among
the most vocal of Doctor Minoret's co-heirs, their resentment sharpened by
relative poverty.

## Organizational Structure

The Massins' social position reflects the ambiguous status of the provincial
bourgeoisie under the Restoration. Massin himself is a functionary — a
registrar (001-098) — whose modest salary places him below the more prosperous
Minoret-Levraults and Crémières in the hierarchy of the co-heirs. His wife,
Madame Massin, compensates for their financial anxieties with the sharpest
tongue among the relatives; her speeches, the narrator notes, "were so many
arrows" (001-039).

> Madame Massin, whose speeches were so many arrows, was the spokeswoman of
> the family's grievances. Her husband, the registrar, looked askance at
> the Justice of the Peace — a Massin-Levrault junior — whose elevation he
> envied. The Massins were not rich.
> [001-039, 001-049, 001-097–001-098]

The family's structure, like all the co-heir families, is defined by the
Napoleonic Code's rules of collateral succession. Massin's claim to the
Minoret fortune rests on his status as a nephew of the doctor — a legal
relationship that carries weight in the courts but none in the doctor's
affections (001-080).

## Role in the Novel

The Massin family, and particularly Madame Massin, provides the novel with
its most vivid portrait of provincial resentment. While the Crémières are
anxious and the Minoret-Levraults are brutish, the Massins are bitter — their
relative poverty among the heirs sharpening their sense of grievance and
making them the most persistent advocates of the claim against Ursule. Madame
Massin's intervention at the family council — "It is robbery!" (001-036) —
sets the tone for the collective campaign against the doctor's ward.

The Massins' role illustrates a recurring Balzacian theme: that the sense of
injury, rather than actual deprivation, is the most powerful motive in human
affairs. Massin is not poor by any objective standard — he holds a public
office — but his proximity to greater wealth transforms his modest comfort
into felt poverty, and his envy into action.

## Narrative Function

The Massin family adds depth and texture to the novel's social portrait.
Where [[zelie-minoret]] drives the criminal conspiracy,
and [[goupil]] supplies its intelligence, the Massins provide its
emotional fuel — the collective resentment that makes the theft of the
will feel, to its perpetrators, not like a crime but like justice. Their
presence in the novel completes Balzac's taxonomy of provincial greed.
