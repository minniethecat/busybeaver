# Fortune

## Overview

Fortune — the accumulated wealth whose disposition after
[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s death drives the plot — is the material axis
around which *Ursule Mirouët* turns. Balzac treats fortune not as inherently
evil but as a revealer of character: the same money that enables Minoret's
generosity to [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] also exposes the [[greed]] of his
relatives. The novel's moral economy is simple and consistent — those who
love money for its own sake are corrupted by it; those who value it only as
a means to protect and provide for others are blessed by it (001-062). Even the postmaster Minoret-Levrault "had made a fortune of thirty thousand francs a year, in meadow land" (001-012) — a substantial sum that nevertheless fails to satisfy, illustrating the novel's thesis that fortune sought for its own sake is a hunger that grows by feeding.

## Role in the Novel

Doctor Minoret's fortune is established early as the object of universal
interest in [[nemours|Nemours]]. His return from Paris with a substantial
medical practice behind him immediately draws the attention of relatives who
have been counting on their share. The fortune is described as having been
augmented by careful investment and several smaller inheritances, making it
a substantial provincial estate — large enough to transform the circumstances
of whoever receives it (001-629).

The relatives' relationship to the fortune is purely instrumental. They do
not love [[dr-minoret|Minoret]]; they love what he will leave behind. Their
calculations, their anxieties, and ultimately their crimes all flow from this
obsession. Balzac's psychological acuity is evident in his portrayal of how
the mere prospect of money transforms ordinary provincial bourgeois into
conspirators like [[goupil|Goupil]], [[zelie-minoret|Zélie]],
[[massin|Massin]], and [[cremiere|Crémière]].

[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]'s relationship to the fortune is the polar
opposite. She never asks about money, never calculates her prospects, and
would be content with whatever her guardian chose to provide. When Minoret
tells her that he intends to make her his principal heir, her response is
distress rather than delight — she fears the resentment it will provoke. This
indifference to wealth, far from being naive, is portrayed as the deepest
wisdom.

The fortune's final disposition — restored to Ursule after the
[[theft-of-the-will]] is exposed through supernatural intervention — affirms
the novel's providential framework. The money goes where it morally belongs,
not because the legal system functions perfectly (quite the opposite) but
because divine justice intervenes to correct human corruption.

## Fortune as Moral Test

| Character | Attitude Toward Fortune | Moral Outcome |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------|
| [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule Mirouët]] | Indifference; accepts whatever is given | Blessed with the fortune |
| [[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]] | Stewardship; wealth as means to protect others | Dies in peace after [[conversion]] |
| [[goupil|Goupil]] | Cunning exploitation; wealth through manipulation | Self-defeated |
| [[zelie-minoret|Zélie Minoret]] | Obsessive greed; fortune as life's purpose | Exposed and punished |
| [[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]] | Voluntary poverty; gives everything away | Spiritual authority and peace |

## Key Passages

The relatives' sense of entitlement is expressed without shame:

> The loss of the inheritance on which they had counted for fifteen years
> seemed to them as great a wrong as the privation of their daily bread.
>
> (001-062)

Minoret's directness about his intentions is characteristically blunt:

> "I shall deprive them of their inheritance to make you the richer."
>
> (001-538)

The moral test posed by fortune is made explicit in the novel's climax:

> The comedy of the inheritance will begin. — God grant that the wicked may
> repent.
>
> (002-239)

## Context

The fortune at the center of *Ursule Mirouët* reflects the economic realities
of the French bourgeoisie under the July Monarchy (1830–1848). This was an
era in which wealth — rather than birth — increasingly determined social
standing. Balzac, who spent his entire adult life in debt and pursued
financial success with an energy matching that of his literary production,
understood the power of money from the inside. His portrayal of fortune as a
moral test reflects both his Catholic convictions and his hard-won knowledge
that money, while necessary, is spiritually dangerous.
