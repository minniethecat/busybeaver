# The Portenduère Family

## Overview

The Portenduère family is an ancient noble house of Breton origin, now
reduced to poverty and represented in Nemours by the aged widow,
[[madame-de-portenduere|Madame de Portenduère]], and her son,
[[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]. Their decline from feudal magnificence
to genteel destitution parallels the transformation of French society in the
decades following the Revolution; their presence in the novel provides a
counterpoint to the bourgeois Minoret heirs, whose wealth is new and whose
values are mercantile (001-065).

## Description in the Novel

### Noble Origins and Decline

Balzac introduces the Portenduères within a panoramic overview of Nemours's
social structure, positioning them as the sole surviving representatives of
the old order:

> At Nemours there are not more than three or four noble families, of no
> great rank or fame; among them, at the time of our story, shone that of the
> Portenduères. These exclusive families visit the nobility who possess lands
> and châteaux in the neighboring country... The nobility who live in the
> towns have no wealth. Madame de Portenduère's whole estate consisted of a
> farm, yielding four thousand seven hundred francs a year, and her house in
> the town.
>
> (001-065)

The family's lineage is elaborated later, when Doctor Minoret must explain
to Ursule why a marriage with Savinien is socially impossible:

> "Never would Madame de Portenduère — a Kergarouët — if she had but three
> hundred francs a year to live on, consent to see the Vicomte Savinien de
> Portenduère, grandnephew of the late Comte de Portenduère, lieutenant-general
> of the king's naval forces, and son of the Vicomte de Portenduère, ship's
> captain, married to — whom? Ursule Mirouët, the daughter of a regimental
> bandmaster, without a fortune..."
>
> (001-617)

This recitation of titles — however impoverished the current holders —
demonstrates the rigid social hierarchy that Ursule must overcome.

### The Two Branches

The family is divided into two branches, a structural detail with
significant plot consequences:

> Savinien, then, was great-nephew to Vice-Admiral Kergarouët, and cousin
> to the Comte de Portenduère, the Admiral's grandson, both of them very
> rich. The Vice-Admiral lived in Paris, and the Comte de Portenduère at his
> château of the same name in Dauphiné. The Count, his cousin, represented
> the elder branch, and Savinien was the only scion of the younger branch of
> the Portenduères.
>
> (001-628)

The elder branch is wealthy and politically connected — the Comte sits as
a deputy, has repurchased the family's Paris mansion under Villèle's
indemnity act, and enjoys an income of sixty thousand francs a year
(001-629). The younger branch — Savinien and his mother — has been left
with only the farm at Bordières and a small house in town. When Savinien is
imprisoned for debt, both branches of the family are asked for help. Both
refuse. The Comte offers only the cold comfort of an invitation to sell the
farm and come live at his estate (001-672). The family's solidarity, so
vaunted in aristocratic ideology, proves hollow when money is required.

## Narrative Function

The Portenduère family serves as a foil to the Minoret heirs. Where the
Minoret relatives scheme to seize the doctor's fortune through legal
manipulation, the Portenduères struggle to preserve their dignity in the
face of financial ruin. Yet Balzac does not romanticize the aristocracy:
the family's refusal to help Savinien exposes the coldness beneath the
veneer of honor. When the notary Dionis speaks of "the honor of the
Portenduères" (001-581), the phrase carries an irony the reader is meant to
perceive.

The cross-class romance between Savinien and Ursule — the penniless noble
and the illegitimate heiress — is one of the novel's central emotional
threads. By marrying Savinien, Ursule — the daughter of an illegitimate
line, with no legal claim to the Minoret fortune under the Code but
possessing it through the doctor's properly executed will — becomes the
instrument through which the two classes are joined.

The family's social trajectory illustrates a broader historical theme:
the transfer of power from the old aristocracy to the new bourgeoisie.
The Portenduère name, which once "shone" in Nemours, is now kept alive not
by aristocratic patronage but by bourgeois money. Savinien's redemption
through Ursule's love and fortune suggests that personal virtue, rather
than lineage, is the true foundation of honor — a characteristically
Balzacian resolution that reconciles the old order with the new.
