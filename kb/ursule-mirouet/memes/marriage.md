# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in *[[Ursule-Mirouet|Ursule Mirouët]]* functions on three distinct levels. First, it
is the romantic culmination of [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] and [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien]]'s love — the union of two
[[youth|young]] people whose affection has been tested by adversity and proven genuine.
Second, it is a financial and social transaction, carefully negotiated by
guardians and notaries, involving dowries, property settlements, and
calculations of family standing. Third, it is a moral index: the contrast
between Ursule's marriage (founded on mutual love and religious commitment)
and the marriages of the Minoret relatives (founded on interest and
calculation) serves to measure the spiritual health of the community.

## Role in the Novel

The love between Ursule and Savinien de Portenduère is introduced early and
develops with a delicacy that distinguishes it from the mercenary
relationships surrounding them. Ursule's feelings for Savinien are revealed
through the clairvoyant's description of her private prayers — a disclosure
that both embarrasses and validates her. Savinien, for his part, is genuinely
drawn to Ursule's beauty and goodness, not to any prospective [[fortune|fortune]]
(001-378)(001-419).

The path to marriage is obstructed by the Portenduère family's financial
difficulties and [[madame-de-portenduere|Madame de Portenduère]]'s initial resistance to a match with
a girl of uncertain birth and modest fortune (002-199). The obstacles are overcome
through a combination of Minoret's generosity, Savinien's maturation during
his military service, and the providential resolution of the inheritance
dispute. The marriage, when it finally occurs, represents the triumph of
moral order over the schemes of the greedy relatives.

In contrast to Ursule and Savinien's union, the novel presents the marriages
of the Minoret relatives as models of what marriage should not be. [[zelie-minoret|Zélie]]
Minoret's marriage to [[minoret-levrault|the tax-receiver]] is a business partnership disguised
as a sacrament; [[cremiere-family|the Crémières]]' union is marked by petty calculation. These
marriages produce not happiness but anxiety, as the spouses share not love
but a common obsession with the inheritance.

The novel's epilogue traces the subsequent history of Ursule and Savinien's
marriage, confirming that it has been fruitful, happy, and blessed —
precisely the outcome that the scheming relatives sought for themselves and
failed to achieve. The marriage thus functions as a final vindication of the
novel's moral vision.

## Key Passages

The clairvoyant reveals Ursule's [[secret|hidden]] feelings:

> "She has committed no sin; but she blames herself for having thought too
> much of Monsieur Savinien." (001-378)

Minoret discovers the depth of Ursule's attachment:

> The guardian convinced himself that from her window Ursule could see across
> to Madame de Portenduère's house. (001-419)

The marriage sacrament is presented as the natural culmination of virtuous
love, though Balzac's treatment is more social than sacramental — the
ceremony is mentioned but not described in detail, consistent with the
novel's focus on the moral rather than ritual dimensions of marriage.

## Context

Marriage in post-Revolutionary France was governed by the Napoleonic Code,
which established it as a civil contract subject to strict legal regulation.
The Code required parental consent for minors, regulated dowries and
community property, and made divorce difficult (it was abolished entirely
in 1816 under the Restoration). Balzac's treatment of marriage reflects this
[[law|legal framework]] while also drawing on the Romantic idealization of love that
was a hallmark of nineteenth-century French literature. The tension between
marriage-as-contract and marriage-as-sacrament is a recurring theme
throughout *La Comédie Humaine*.
