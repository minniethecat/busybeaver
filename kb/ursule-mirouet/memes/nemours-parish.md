# The Parish of Nemours

## Overview

The Catholic parish of [[nemours|Nemours]] is the institutional setting for
the novel's religious life. Under the pastoral care of
[[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]], it serves the spiritual needs of a
community that is nominally Catholic but largely indifferent to the demands
of genuine [[faith]]. The parish's modesty — its priest is poor, its
[[nemours-church|church]] unremarkable — contrasts with its spiritual
significance: it is the institution through which
[[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] is formed in faith and
[[dr-minoret|Minoret]] is received into the Church through
[[conversion]] (001-041).

## Organizational Structure

The parish is a standard French Catholic parish of the Restoration period,
part of the diocese of Meaux. Its single priest, the
[[abbe-chaperon|Abbé Chaperon]], is responsible for the full range of
pastoral duties: Mass, confession, baptism, marriage, extreme unction, and
the religious education of the young (001-032). His Jansenist sympathies have made him
suspect to his bishop and cost him any prospect of advancement — a detail
that reveals both the tensions within the Restoration Church and Chaperon's
integrity (001-110) (he will not compromise his theological convictions for
preferment).

## Role in the Novel

The parish functions as the institutional counterweight to the materialist
values that dominate [[nemours|Nemours]]. Where the relatives' world is
governed by money, the parish represents an alternative order of value — the
spiritual economy of grace and merit. This alternative is embodied in
Chaperon's person ("the parish" and "the priest" are nearly synonymous in the
novel's treatment) but it is institutionally grounded in the parish's
sacramental life (001-122).

> Three months after these events, in January 1837, Ursule married Savinien, with Madame de Portenduère's consent. Minoret intervened at the signing of the contract to settle on Mademoiselle Mirouët, by deed of gift, his estate of le Rouvre, and twenty-eight thousand francs a year in consols, reserving of all his fortune only his uncle's house and six thousand francs a year. He is become the most charitable and pious man in Nemours, churchwarden of the parish, and the providence of the unfortunate.
>
> (002-846)


The novel's treatment of the parish is notably idealizing. There is no
clerical corruption, no ecclesiastical politics, no tension between the
parish and its parishioners beyond the general indifference of the
bourgeoisie. The parish is the Church as it should be — a community of
[[faith]] rather than a branch of the state apparatus — and Chaperon is the
priest as he should be.
