# Duty

Duty in *Ursule Mirouët* operates on multiple levels — religious, professional, familial, and moral. The novel's virtuous characters are defined by their fidelity to duty, while its antagonists treat duty as a constraint to be evaded or manipulated.

## Religious Duty

The Abbé Chaperon exemplifies religious duty, fulfilling obligations "that fall on the head of a dean" (001-110) in ministering to his fellow clergy and parishioners. His sense of duty is neither rigid nor self-righteous but arises from genuine pastoral care. [[ursule-mirouet|Ursule]], shaped by his tutelage, internalises a similar orientation "towards feeling rather than duty" (001-254) — a formulation that suggests true duty is animated by love rather than mere obligation.

## Professional Duty

The novel's legal professionals navigate competing claims of duty. Doctor Minoret acts on "what I believe it to be my duty to do for that child" (001-566), framing his guardianship of Ursule as a moral imperative. The notary Dionis, by contrast, is "grieved to fulfil a painful duty" (002-072) when the duty in question serves his clients' interests rather than abstract justice. The investigating magistrate acknowledges that "my strict duty would be to demand a warrant" (002-826), yet exercises discretion — suggesting that true duty requires judgment, not mechanical application of rules.

> "If either of them should make any change in what I believe it to be my duty to do for that child, I will come back from the other world to torment him!"
>
> (001-566)

## Duty and Conscience

The novel's resolution hinges on the intersection of duty and conscience. When Zélie is "enlightened as to her duty" (002-832), it is conscience — awakened by Ursule's magnetic vision — that reveals what duty requires. The heirs who insist "we do no more than our duty" (002-049) in pursuing their inheritance exemplify how the language of duty can be co-opted to justify self-interest, a critique Balzac levels at provincial bourgeois morality.
