# The Crémière Family

## Overview

The Crémière family is one of the four founding bourgeois families of Nemours,
descended from the millers who settled the region (001-066). Along with the Minorets,
Massins, and Levraults, the Crémières form the social fabric of the town — a
dense network of intermarried cousins whose collective identity is defined
by property and prudence rather than lineage or culture (001-041). In the
novel, Antoine Crémière and his wife represent the grasping, resentful
spirit of the provincial bourgeoisie who feel entitled to a share of
[[dr-minoret|Doctor Minoret]]'s fortune.

## Organizational Structure

> Of the four families of Nemours, the Crémières were the millers. The
> Minorets held the tanneries, the Massins went into business, the Levraults
> remained farmers; and by intermarriage among these four stocks, the whole
> population of the town was one great family.
> [001-065–001-067]

The Crémières, like their fellow co-heir families, are structured around the
logic of the Napoleonic Code: collateral relatives who would inherit by law
if the patriarch died intestate. Antoine Crémière-Dionis, the notary of
Nemours (001-044), is the professional head of the family line and the legal
instrument through which the heirs pursue their claims. His cousin Antoine
Crémière (the usher) is a less prominent figure, but his wife, the "very
fat" Madame Crémière, is a conspicuous participant in the family councils
(001-049–001-050).

## Role in the Novel

The Crémière family embodies the provincial bourgeoisie's relationship to
money: cautious, accumulative, and perpetually fearful of being cheated.
Their participation in the plot against [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] is driven
less by active malice than by a settled conviction that any fortune that
escapes the family circle is a theft — a conviction that the narrator
presents as characteristic of the class (001-036).

> "It is robbery!" said Madame Massin.
> (001-036)

Madame Crémière's role in the novel, though minor, is telling. Her physical
description — "doubtfully white" — and her anxious attendance at every family
gathering mark her as a representative type: the provincial bourgeoise whose
world is bounded by the family ledger and whose sentiments are entirely
governed by the expectation of inheritance. The Crémières are not villains;
they are products of a system that Balzac anatomizes with clinical precision.

## Narrative Function

The Crémière family illustrates the collective psychology of the provincial
bourgeoisie in Balzac's world: the suspicion of outsiders, the obsessive
tracking of property, and the assumption that self-interest is the only
rational motive. Their presence in the novel thickens the social texture of
Nemours and provides a chorus of ordinary greed against which the
exceptional virtue of characters like [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]] and
[[abbe-chaperon]] stands out in relief.
