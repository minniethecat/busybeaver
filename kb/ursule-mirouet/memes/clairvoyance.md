# Clairvoyance

## Overview

Clairvoyance — the capacity of a magnetized subject to see and hear events occurring at a great distance — is the specific magnetic phenomenon that convinces Doctor Minoret of the reality of imponderable forces and, by extension, of the soul and Providence. In Balzac's *Ursule Mirouët*, clairvoyance serves as the empirical bridge between materialist science and spiritual belief. The somnambulist in Paris, under magnetic trance, accurately describes events transpiring in Minoret's home in Nemours, providing evidence so precise and verifiable that the old physician's philosophical defenses crumble (001-328)(001-425).

The novel treats clairvoyance not as a supernatural marvel to be accepted on faith but as a natural phenomenon to be tested empirically. Dr. Minoret, a hardened materialist, demands a "decisive and irrefutable test" (001-370), and the test is constructed with scientific rigor: the somnambulist must describe something that no one in the room could possibly know, and the doctor himself will verify the results. This empiricist framing is crucial to Balzac's philosophical argument: true science follows evidence even when it contradicts established theory.

## Role in the Novel

Clairvoyance is introduced through the figure of the "Great Unknown," a magnetic healer in Paris — a Swedenborgian who possesses extraordinary powers and whom Minoret visits at the urging of his old friend Bouvard. When Minoret witnesses a somnambulist — a woman in magnetic sleep — demonstrating the ability to perceive across space, the scene is carefully constructed to preempt charges of fraud:

> "If you discern in this woman a real power to annihilate space, if you can but convince yourself that she, here, from the Church of the Assumption, can see and hear what is going on at Nemours, you must then admit all other effects of magnetism; they are to a sceptic quite as impossible as these."
> — (001-373)

The test is ingenious in its simplicity. The somnambulist is asked to describe what Ursule — Minoret's ward in Nemours, whom no one in the room has ever met — is doing at that very moment. The somnambulist describes Ursule at her evening prayers with such precise detail — her gestures, her examination of conscience, even her silent thoughts about young Savinien de Portenduère — that Minoret is moved to tears:

> "Her evening prayers; she commends herself to God; she beseeches Him to keep her soul free from evil thoughts; she examines her conscience, going over all she has done during the day to see whether she has failed in obedience to His commandments or to those of the Church."
> — (001-378)

The clairvoyant then dictates, word for word, the prayer that Ursule recites — a prayer composed by the Abbé Chaperon that no one in Paris could possibly know:

> The clairvoyant so exactly imitated the child's innocent gestures and saintly aspirations that Doctor Minoret's eyes filled with tears.
> — (001-383)

Upon returning home, Minoret verifies every detail the somnambulist revealed. The displacement of two volumes of the Pandects, the difference between two banknotes, and — most tellingly — the small red dots Ursule had marked on her almanac for saints' days, including that of Saint Savinien. The clairvoyant had perceived a pinhead-sized mark from across the city, through walls and distance (001-425). The narrator notes that Minoret "went downstairs again, carrying away the convicting document, shut himself into his room, looked for Saint Savinien, and found, as the clairvoyant had said, a small red dot on the 19th of October" (001-425).

The evidence of clairvoyance functions in the novel as what we might now call a "paradigm shift." Minoret's entire intellectual framework — built on the premise that matter is all there is — cannot accommodate the fact of clairvoyant perception. Rather than deny the evidence, he revises the framework:

> And yet, the stiff-necked old man tried to dispute his own doubts. Though stricken to the heart, he could not make up his mind; he still strove with God. At the same time his mind seemed to vacillate; he was not the same man. He became unnaturally perturbed.
> — (001-426)

This is the novel's philosophical argument in action: true empiricism must follow evidence even when it contradicts established theory. The doctor's conversion to Catholicism is not a rejection of science but its completion — he follows the evidence of clairvoyance to its logical conclusion, which is the existence of the soul and, ultimately, of God.

Clairvoyance also foreshadows the novel's supernatural resolution. Ursule herself experiences magnetic dreams — somnambulistic visions in which she sees the theft of the bonds, the hiding place within the Pandects, and the burning of the will. These visions, which she reports to the Abbé Chaperon and to Bongrand, provide the evidence that leads to the recovery of the stolen inheritance. The novel thus establishes a symmetry: the doctor's conversion through clairvoyant evidence in Paris is mirrored by the restoration of justice through clairvoyant evidence in Nemours.

## Key Passages

The test of clairvoyance is proposed as a direct challenge to Minoret's skepticism:

> "If you discern in this woman a real power to annihilate space, if you can but convince yourself that she, here, from the Church of the Assumption, can see and hear what is going on at Nemours, you must then admit all other effects of magnetism; they are to a sceptic quite as impossible as these."
> — (001-373)

The somnambulist's description of Ursule at prayer contains the novel's most intimate portrait of its heroine's spiritual life:

> "She is stripping her heart bare, poor dear little thing." There were tears in the clairvoyant's eyes. "She has committed no sin; but she blames herself for having thought too much of Monsieur Savinien."
> — (001-378)

The dramatic confirmation of the clairvoyant's accuracy is rendered with the precision of a scientific report:

> He went downstairs again, carrying away the convicting document, shut himself into his room, looked for Saint Savinien, and found, as the clairvoyant had said, a small red dot on the 19th of October; he found such another at Saint Denis' day, his own patron; and holes that showed where the pins had been that Ursule had used, to mark the days by pricking them in a pattern.
> — (001-425)

The philosophical consequence of the clairvoyant evidence is the doctor's conversion from materialism to belief — a conversion that the novel presents as the triumph of genuine empiricism over dogmatic skepticism (001-426).

## Context

The phenomenon of clairvoyance in magnetic sleep was a subject of serious investigation in early nineteenth-century France. The Marquis de Puységur had discovered "artificial somnambulism" in the 1780s, demonstrating that magnetized subjects could apparently perceive at a distance, diagnose illnesses, and prescribe treatments. By Balzac's time, numerous practitioners claimed to induce clairvoyant states, and a significant minority of intellectuals — including Balzac himself — took these claims seriously as data requiring explanation rather than dismissal.

Balzac's interest in magnetism and clairvoyance was not merely literary. He was a dedicated reader of Swedenborg, Mesmer, and the mystical philosophers, and he believed that imponderable fluids and magnetic influences were natural phenomena that science had not yet adequately explained. In the preface to *Ursule Mirouët*, he explicitly frames the novel as a contribution to the debate between materialism and spiritualism, offering clairvoyance as empirical evidence for the existence of forces beyond the material.

The novel's epistemological argument is sophisticated: it does not ask the reader to accept clairvoyance on faith but rather dramatizes the skeptical Doctor Minoret's reluctant acceptance of evidence that he himself has verified. The test is controlled, the verification is thorough, and the conclusion is presented as rationally inescapable. This is not credulity but empiricism honestly applied — and it is Balzac's challenge to the materialist science of his day, which he believed had hardened into a dogma as inflexible as the religious dogmas it had displaced.
