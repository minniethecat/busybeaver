# The Marriage of Ursule and Savinien

## Overview

The marriage of [[Ursule-Mirouet|Ursule Mirouët]] to [[savinien-de-portenduere|Savinien de Portenduère]] is the novel's
resolution — the event that rewards virtue, restores the social order, and
confirms the providential pattern that the novel has traced. After the
exposure of [[theft-of-the-will|the conspiracy]] and the restoration of her inheritance, [[ursule-mirouet-p|Ursule]]
is free to marry the man she has loved since girlhood (001-341). The marriage unites
old [[nobility|aristocracy]] (the Portenduère name) with genuine virtue (Ursule's
character) — a union the novel presents as the ideal reconciliation of
traditional and modern France.

## Narrative Account

Balzac handles the marriage with characteristic restraint. The ceremony
itself is not described; the novel's interest is in its meaning rather than
its details. The epilogue traces the couple's subsequent happiness: their
children, Savinien's naval career, their life together in [[nemours|Nemours]] and
occasionally in [[paris|Paris]] (002-095).

The marriage functions as a moral bookend to the novel's opening. Where the
novel began with greed, calculation, and the relatives' predatory watching
of Minoret's [[fortune|fortune]], it ends with love, generosity, and the natural
fruition of virtuous affection. The fortune — now Ursule's — serves its
proper purpose: supporting a family rather than feeding [[greed|avarice]].

> Two years, full of secret delight, thus slipped away, unbroken by any event but the useless efforts of the young man to obtain his mother's consent to his marriage with Ursule. He would sometimes talk for the whole morning, his mother listening to his entreaties and arguments, but making no reply but by the obstinate silence of a Bretonne or by curt refusals.
>
> (002-199)


## Significance

The marriage of Ursule and Savinien is Balzac's answer to the question the
novel has posed: can virtue prosper in a world dominated by greed? The
answer is a qualified yes — qualified because the marriage is made possible
only by supernatural intervention (the ghost that torments [[zelie-minoret|Zélie]] into
confession). Human institutions alone — the law, [[dionis|the notary]], the family
council — would have failed (001-667). [[providence|Providence]], operating through conscience and
grace, succeeds where the machinery of [[justice|justice]] could not (001-550).
