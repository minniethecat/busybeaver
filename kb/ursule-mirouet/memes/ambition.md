# Ambition

Ambition in *Ursule Mirouët* takes a distinctly provincial form. Unlike the grand Napoleonic ambitions of Balzac's Paris novels, ambition in Nemours is narrow, grasping, and defined by the desire to acquire — or prevent others from acquiring — the doctor's fortune. (002-146) Balzac portrays provincial ambition as a corrosive force that reveals character rather than elevating it.

## The Heirs' Hungry Ambition

The [[minoret-family|Minoret]] heirs, led by [[zelie-minoret|Zélie]], embody a particular species of ambition: the desire for unearned wealth. Their ambition is not to create or to achieve but to inherit (001-093). Each heir schemes to maximize their share of the doctor's estate, their ambition sharpened by the knowledge that the fortune is finite and that another's gain is their loss. (001-062) Zélie Levrault, perhaps the most openly ambitious of the heirs, measures every social interaction by its potential financial return.

> All the townsfolk rushed to the Square, as much to see the doctor's carriage and cross-question the coachman, as to comment on his ward, to whose excessive ambition Massin, Crémière, and the postmaster ascribed their uncle's follies.
>
> (002-146)

This form of ambition is fundamentally reactive and defensive (001-047). The heirs do not aspire to greatness; they aspire to comfort, to the consolidation of what they believe is rightfully theirs. Their ambition is the ambition of the counting-house, not the battlefield — a characteristically Balzacian observation about the bourgeois values that were reshaping post-Revolutionary France.

## Goupil's Ruthless Drive

Goupil, head clerk to Monsieur Crémière, (001-044) represents ambition of a different order — the ambition of the intelligent outsider who sees social advancement blocked by reputation and birth. Denied the legitimate path of professional respectability, Goupil channels his ambition into manipulation. He serves the heirs' interests while pursuing his own, understanding that in the provincial game of inheritance, the clerk who holds the documents holds the power.

Goupil's tragedy, in Balzac's rendering, is that his intelligence outstrips his moral sense. His ambition, untethered from principle, leads him to acts of increasing desperation. He is the novel's cautionary exemplar of ambition without conscience — a type Balzac would develop more fully in characters like Vautrin elsewhere in *La Comédie Humaine*.
