# Orberosia

Orberosia is the most complex female character in [[book-ii|Book II: The Ancient Times]] and one of Anatole France's most audacious satirical creations. Clothed by the monk Magis in the first "garments of chastity," she becomes the wife of [[kraken|Kraken]], the mistress of at least two other men, an unwitting accomplice to the Dragon of Alca terror, and — through the alchemy of historical memory — **Saint Orberosia**, patroness of the [[draco-the-great|Draconides]] dynasty. Her trajectory from village beauty to venerated saint embodies France's critique of how religious and political institutions manufacture sanctity from profoundly human, often disreputable, raw material.

## Role in the Narrative

Orberosia appears primarily in Book II's central chapters: "The Marriage of Kraken and Orberosia" (015) and the Dragon of Alca sequence (016–023). She also receives posthumous veneration in Book III through [[draco-the-great|Draco the Great]]'s translation of her relics (026). The narrative treats her with a characteristic blend of mock-heroic diction and clinical candor — calling her "the fair Orberosia" in one breath and cataloguing her adulteries in the next.

Her narrative function is tripartite: she is the **romantic catalyst** who enables Kraken's dragon scheme, the **unwitting intelligence asset** whose affairs protect her husband, and the **symbolic raw material** from which the Draconides manufacture a saint.

## Key Actions and Appearances

### The First Clothed Woman

Orberosia enters the narrative with an origin story that is itself a satire of foundation myths. She is the Penguin woman upon whom the monk Magis personally placed the first garment of chastity:

> She was the one that the monk Magis had clothed with his own hands and thus was the first to have worn the garments of chastity. In remembrance of the day when the astonished crowd of Penguins had seen her moving gloriously in her robe tinted like the dawn, this maiden had received the name of Orberosia. (015-001)

The etymological footnote — "Orb, poetically, a globe when speaking of the heavenly bodies" (015-002) — immediately inflates her name with cosmic significance while the narrative simultaneously prepares to catalogue her thoroughly terrestrial behavior.

### Marriage to Kraken

Encountering Kraken on the deserted Beach of Shadows, Orberosia initially fears he is a wandering spirit ("Is it you, I see, sir, or is it not rather your troubled spirit?"), as the Penguins of Alca believed Kraken had died (015-003, 015-007). When Kraken offers to make her his companion, "casting down her eyes, she murmured: 'I will follow thee, master'" (015-022–015-023).

The marriage is conducted without ceremony — hidden in Kraken's cavern where he "planned great designs" (015-024). This secrecy enables the dragon imposture that follows.

### The Adulteries That Saved a Hero

France devotes sustained attention to Orberosia's romantic life outside her marriage, treating it with mock-epic gravity:

> Orberosia loved her husband, but she did not love him alone. At the hour when Venus lightens in the pale sky, whilst Kraken scattered terror through the villages, she used to visit in his moving hut, a young shepherd of Dalles called Marcel, whose pleasing form was invested with inexhaustible vigour. (023-007)

Orberosia adopts the alias "Bridget" and claims to be a gardener's daughter, concealing her identity from Marcel. When leaving his arms, if she encountered a belated peasant, she "immediately spread out her garments like great wings" (023-007) — an angelic gesture masking earthly conduct.

Her second affair, with a hunchbacked neatherd from Belmont, proves providential. From this lover she learns of [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]]'s proclamation against dragons, realizing that Kraken's costume scheme is endangered. The narrative frames this as "lucky infidelity" that "was destined soon to save the hero from a great peril and to assure his fortune and his glory for ever" (023-007).

### The Virgin of the Dragon

When the Penguins search for the missing Orberosia (who disappeared during Kraken's dragon rampages), they believe she has been devoured by the monster. The narrative notes dryly:

> Her absence had at first caused no uneasiness because on several occasions she had been carried off by violent men who were consumed with love. And thoughtful people were not astonished at this, reflecting that the maiden was the most beautiful of the Penguins. (016-003)

The community's matter-of-fact acceptance of Orberosia's periodic abductions — "for none of us can escape his destiny" — is a characteristic Francean irony: the future saint's sexual history is an open secret that no one considers incompatible with her eventual sanctification.

### Posthumous Sanctification

In Book III, Orberosia has been transformed into Saint Orberosia. [[draco-the-great|Draco the Great]] — her son, and the first historical Draconide king — solemnly translates her relics, cementing the family's religious legitimacy (026). France does not narrate the process of canonization itself; the gap between the adulterous village beauty of Book II and the venerated saint of Book III is left for the reader to contemplate.

## Character Traits and Satirical Function

| Target | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Hagiography** | A woman whose conduct would scandalize any congregation becomes an object of religious veneration |
| **Dynastic legitimacy** | The Draconides derive their sacred authority from a mother whose "miracles" were accidents of infidelity |
| **The Madonna/whore dichotomy** | Orberosia simultaneously occupies the roles of virgin, temptress, and saint |
| **Historical memory** | The community conveniently forgets what "everyone knew" about Orberosia once she becomes politically useful to remember differently |

Orberosia's duality — faithful wife and serial adulteress, dragon victim and dragon accomplice, village beauty and canonized saint — is not presented as hypocrisy but as the normal operation of social myth-making. France's satire targets not Orberosia herself but the institutional machinery that transforms her into something she never was.

## Historical Parallels

Orberosia does not satirize a specific saint but the broader **hagiographic tradition** in which medieval chroniclers transformed local figures — often with checkered histories — into objects of veneration to serve institutional needs. The translation of relics by Draco the Great (026) parallels the medieval practice of *furta sacra* (holy theft) and the political deployment of saints' cults by ruling dynasties.

France, writing in the aftermath of the Church-State conflicts of the French Third Republic, targets the mechanism by which religious authority launders worldly power into sacred legitimacy. Orberosia is the raw material; the Church and the Draconides are the manufacturing plant.
