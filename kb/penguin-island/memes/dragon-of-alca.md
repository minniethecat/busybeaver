# Dragon of Alca

> "We afterwards went to visit the cabinet of natural history. . . . The care-taker showed us a sort of packet bound in straw that he told us contained the skeleton of a dragon; a proof, added he, that the dragon is not a fabulous animal."
> — *Memoirs of Jacques Casanova*, epigraph to Chapter VI, Book II

The Dragon of Alca is the central event of [[book-ii|Book II: The Ancient Times]] and the foundational trauma around which Penguin dynastic legitimacy is constructed. For seven consecutive chapters (VI–XIII), a "frightful dragon" ravages the villages of [[alca|Alca]] — devouring livestock, abducting children, and spreading terror across the island. The crisis is resolved when the hero [[kraken|Kraken]] slays the monster, earning eternal glory and founding the [[draco-the-great|Draconides]] dynasty. The official story, preserved in Penguin chronicles for centuries, conceals the satirical truth: **Kraken was the dragon**, wearing a sealskin helmet with bull's horns, sea-bird beak gloves, and a coiled green tail to terrorize the very communities from which he later claimed to have delivered the nation.

## The Official Account

The canonical version, preserved by the Draconides and later Penguin historians, presents the following sequence:

1. A terrible dragon appears in Alca, devastating farms and villages
2. The monster devours livestock, abducts the maiden [[orberosia|Orberosia]], and carries off children including the orphan Elo
3. The Elders of Alca consult [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]], who proclaims that the dragon must be destroyed
4. The hero Kraken — "whose arm was strong and whose mind was subtle" (015-001) — confronts and slays the dragon
5. Kraken marries the rescued Orberosia and becomes the progenitor of the Draconides dynasty
6. The Dragon's crest is adopted as the royal symbol of Penguinia (039-015, 044-020)

## The Satirical Reality

### The Costume

France systematically undercuts the official narrative through the accumulation of contradictory witness testimony and domestic detail. The dragon's physical composition is revealed through Kraken's domestic routine:

- A **sealskin helmet** adorned with two bull's horns and a visor trimmed with "terrible hooks"
- **Gloves** ending in "horrible claws" — actually sea-bird beaks
- A **belt** from which hangs "a long green tail twisted into many folds"
- The costume is so cumbersome that Kraken requires his captive page Elo to help him remove his boots (023-005)

### The Witnesses

The Elders' investigation (VI) produces testimony that, read against the eventual revelation, becomes a dark comedy of misrecognition:

| Witness | Testimony | Actual Meaning |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Villagers | "He has the head of a dragon, the claws of a lion, and the tail of a fish" | Kraken in full costume |
| Woman of Anis | "He is formed like a man. The proof is that I thought he was my husband, and I said to him, 'Come to bed, you old fool'" | Saw Kraken without the helmet |
| Another villager | "I saw the dragon taking off his head in the barn so that he might give a kiss to my sister Minnie" | Caught Kraken mid-costume-change, pursuing a romantic interlude |

The Elders "remained uncertain as to what should be done" (VI) — a satire of institutional paralysis in the face of evidence that contradicts received categories. A dragon that takes off its head to kiss a local girl does not fit the conceptual framework available to them.

### The Collapse

Kraken's dragon enterprise eventually fails not through heroic opposition but through **familiarity breeding contempt**. As he complains to Orberosia:

> "They fear me no longer. Formerly everyone fled at my approach. I carried away hens and rabbits in my bag; I drove sheep and pigs, cows, and oxen before me. To-day these clod-hoppers keep a good guard; they sit up at night. Just now I was pursued in the village of Anis by doughty labourers armed with flails and scythes and pitchforks." (023-005)

The comic image of a dragon "putting his tail under his arm" to run faster from pitchfork-wielding farmers is the novel's masterstroke of myth-deflation.

### Orberosia's Role

[[orberosia|Orberosia]]'s "lucky infidelity" proves decisive. Through her affair with the hunchbacked neatherd of Belmont, she learns of Saint Maël's proclamation against the dragon and warns Kraken (023-007). Her adultery — which the narrative presents as providential — saves the hero and enables the Draconides' future glory.

## Satirical Targets

| Target | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Foundational myths** | Every nation's "monster-slaying" origin story conceals a more cynical reality |
| **Dynastic legitimacy** | The Draconides derive their authority from a costume party |
| **Historical method** | The Penguin chronicles accept the official version without question for centuries |
| **Heraldry and symbolism** | The "Dragon's crest" — proudly displayed on royal banners — is the emblem of a livestock thief |
| **Religious authority** | Saint Maël's holy proclamation is triggered by a fraudulent crisis and resolved by fraudulent heroism |

The Casanova epigraph extends the satire beyond Penguinia: a European museum proudly displays a straw-wrapped "dragon skeleton" as proof that dragons are real. The credulity is universal.

## The Dragon's Crest: Afterlife of a Costume

Centuries after Kraken, the Dragon's crest persists as the central symbol of Penguin monarchism. In Book V, conspirators toast "the Dragon's crest" while plotting to restore Prince Crucho (039-023). In Book VI, military officers swear by it. The transformation is complete: a terroristic fraud has become a sacred national emblem.

France's point is not merely that founding myths are false, but that their falsity **does not matter** to their institutional function. The Draconides dynasty rules effectively regardless of its origins; the Dragon's crest inspires loyalty regardless of what it originally signified. The machinery of legitimacy operates independently of truth.
