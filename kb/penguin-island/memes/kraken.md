# Kraken

A Penguin of [[alca|Alca]] renowned for his strength and cunning, Kraken is the legendary progenitor of the [[draco-the-great|Draconides]] dynasty and husband of [[orberosia|Orberosia]]. His enduring significance lies in his dual identity: publicly revered as a dragon-slaying hero, he was secretly the "[[dragon-of-alca|Dragon of Alca]]" himself — a man in an elaborate monster costume who terrorized Penguin villages to steal their livestock and accumulate wealth. Anatole France uses Kraken to satirize the violent and fraudulent origins that underlie myths of dynastic legitimacy.

## Role in the Narrative

Kraken appears exclusively in [[book-ii|Book II: The Ancient Times]], where his story forms the foundational myth of Penguin civilization. He dwells on the Beach of Shadows in an inaccessible cavern — a liminal space between the civilized Penguin world and the untamed supernatural realm, inhabited by the restless souls of unbaptized Penguins (015-001). The narrator presents him as a "hero" in the classical sense, yet systematically undermines this designation through ironic revelation of his methods.

Kraken's narrative arc encompasses three phases: the solitary cunning hunter, the costumed dragon-terror, and the posthumous dynastic symbol. Each phase deepens France's satire of how societies construct their origin stories.

## Key Actions and Appearances

### The Marriage to Orberosia

Encountering Orberosia — the first Penguin woman clothed in the garments of chastity by the monk Magis — on the deserted plain near his cavern, Kraken seizes her by her floating garments and persuades her to become his companion (015-003–015-024). When she asks how he plans to acquire riches, he answers simply: "By my intelligence" (015-011). This laconic reply foreshadows the elaborate deception that follows.

The marriage is conducted in secret — "not celebrated with songs and torches because Kraken did not consent to show himself to the people of the Penguins" (015-024) — a narrative detail that positions Kraken outside the social contract from the beginning.

### The Dragon Scheme

Kraken's "intelligence" manifests as an audacious imposture: fashioning a sealskin helmet with bull's horns, gloves tipped with sea-bird beaks, and a long green tail, he terrorizes the villages of Alca as the "Dragon." The costume's components are carefully enumerated to emphasize both its crude artifice and the credulity of its victims:

- A sealskin helmet adorned with two bull's horns and a visor trimmed with "terrible hooks"
- Gloves ending in "horrible claws" made from sea-bird beaks
- A belt from which hangs "a long green tail twisted into many folds" (015-024, 023-005)

The scheme initially succeeds brilliantly — Kraken steals hens, sheep, pigs, and cattle while the terrified Penguins believe a supernatural monster ravages their land. A young orphan named Elo is abducted and forced into service as Kraken's page (023-005).

### The Unraveling

Kraken's dragon enterprise eventually collapses as the Penguins grow less fearful. As he complains to Orberosia:

> "They fear me no longer. Formerly everyone fled at my approach. I carried away hens and rabbits in my bag; I drove sheep and pigs, cows, and oxen before me. To-day these clod-hoppers keep a good guard; they sit up at night. Just now I was pursued in the village of Anis by doughty labourers armed with flails and scythes and pitchforks. I had to drop the hens and rabbits, put my tail under my arm, and run as fast as I could." (023-005)

The image of the fearsome Dragon of Alca fleeing "with his tail under his arm" exemplifies France's method of deflating myth through comic physical detail.

## Character Traits and Satirical Function

Kraken embodies several satirical targets simultaneously:

| Target | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Foundational myths of monarchy** | The Draconides dynasty traces its lineage to a costumed livestock thief |
| **The "great man" theory of history** | Kraken's "intelligence" consists of theatrical fraud, not statecraft |
| **Religious credulity** | The Penguins interpret Kraken's costume as a supernatural scourge requiring saintly intervention |
| **Heroic narrative conventions** | The narrator persistently calls Kraken a "hero" while describing purely self-interested violence |

Kraken's name — evoking the Norse sea-monster — inflates his stature ironically. Unlike the mythological Kraken that drags ships to the depths, this Kraken hunches in a cave, gets a pitchfork in his thigh, and flees from farmers with flails (023-005). The bathos is deliberate and devastating.

His relationship with Orberosia further undercuts heroic pretense. While Kraken is "a hero" who "never discover[s] the secrets of their wives" (023-007), Orberosia conducts affairs with a young shepherd and a hunchbacked neatherd. Kraken unknowingly "reap[s] the benefit" of these infidelities through his wife's improved mood (023-007).

## Historical Parallels

Kraken does not satirize a single historical figure but rather the entire category of **legendary founding fathers** — Romulus, Aeneas, King Arthur — whose violent and often criminal origins are retroactively ennobled by dynastic propaganda. The Draconides' appropriation of the "Dragon's crest" as a royal symbol (039-015, 044-020) mirrors the historical practice of transforming embarrassing ancestral details into heraldic glory.

The satire gains additional resonance in the context of the French Third Republic (1870–1940), during which monarchist movements continued to invoke the sacred origins of royal houses. France, a committed Dreyfusard and republican, systematically demolishes the logic by which "might makes right" becomes "tradition makes legitimacy."
