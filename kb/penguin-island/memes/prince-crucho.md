# Prince Crucho

Prince Crucho is the last descendant of the Draconides, the ancient ruling dynasty of Penguinia, and the figurehead of the royalist conspiracy that runs through Books V and VI of *Penguin Island*. Living in exile, Crucho is the object of "regret and hope" [038-001] for those Penguins who look backward to monarchy. Anatole France uses him to satirize the various pretenders to the French throne who persisted as political forces well into the Third Republic.

## Role in the Narrative

Crucho is introduced at the opening of Book V as a magnetic absence: "Many Penguins, anxious about the problems of their age, looked with regret and hope to Prince Crucho, the last of the Draconides" [038-001]. He never appears directly in the narrative — like Pyrot, he is an object around which others organize their ambitions.

The royalist conspirators, led by Father Agaric and Father Cornemuse, see in Crucho a vehicle for restoring the old order. Agaric asks Cornemuse for his assessment: "Cornemuse, what do you think of Prince Crucho?" [038-021]. Cornemuse replies with careful approval: "Prince Crucho has sound views; he respects the clergy; he is attached to the traditions of his race" [038-022].

As the conspiracy develops, Crucho becomes a symbolic figure. His name is invoked in "many homes, both rich and poor" as one "uttered with hope and blessing" [038-023]. The royalists organize around his claim, securing financing from the Viscountess Olive and support from military figures like the Emiral Chatillon.

When Prince Crucho and his wife, Princess Gudrune, make a public appearance, the narrative notes pointedly that they participate in the religious ceremony of the sovereign's foot-washing: "it is a sign of sovereignty" [039-023] — a gesture that simultaneously asserts royal legitimacy and reveals its theatrical character.

## Key Actions and Appearances

**The Pretender in Exile (Book V)**: Crucho lives outside Penguinia, the last of his line, awaiting the call to return. The royalists — Agaric, Cornemuse, the Viscountess Olive — build their conspiracy around his claim.

**The Public Appearance**: Crucho and Princess Gudrune perform the ritual foot-washing, a calculated public gesture that asserts their royal dignity [039-023].

**The Conspiracy's Object**: Throughout the machinations of Agaric, Cornemuse, and their allies, Crucho remains the ostensible goal. The conspirators seek "the restoration of Prince Crucho," which in the eyes of Father Agaric represents not merely a political change but a moral and religious restoration [040-003].

**The Failed Enterprise**: As the conspiracy unravels amid the chaos of the Pyrot affair, Crucho's restoration recedes. The pretender, who was always more symbol than agent, fades from the narrative as the forces he represented prove unable to recapture power.

## Character Traits and Satirical Function

Prince Crucho is a satirical composite of the French royalist pretenders of the late nineteenth century:

- **The absent pretender**: Crucho's non-appearance in direct narrative is the point. Like the Comte de Chambord (the Legitimist claimant) or the Comte de Paris (the Orléanist claimant), Crucho exercises influence entirely through the hopes and ambitions others project onto him.

- **The weight of tradition**: Crucho is defined entirely by his ancestry — he is "the last of the Draconides" [038-001], a living relic. France suggests that monarchism in a modern republic is fundamentally nostalgic, sustained by ritual rather than political reality.

- **The empty symbol**: The foot-washing ceremony [039-023] captures the contradiction of royalist politics: Crucho performs sovereignty as theater, and the audience that takes it seriously is precisely the audience the satire targets.

- **The Draconides irony**: Crucho's dynasty descends from Draco the Great — a name linking Penguin royalty to dragons, monsters, and the mythic-absurd origins of all political legitimacy in France's satire.

## Historical Parallels

Prince Crucho satirizes the French royalist movement of the Third Republic, particularly:

| Penguin Element | Historical Parallel |
|----------------|-------------------|
| The Draconides dynasty | The House of Bourbon (Legitimists) and House of Orléans |
| Crucho's exile | The Comte de Chambord's exile in Frohsdorf |
| Royalist conspiracy | The monarchist leagues and publications of the 1870s–1890s |
| The foot-washing ceremony | Royalist ritualism and Catholic-monarchist symbolism |

France, a committed republican, treats the royalist cause as inherently absurd — a political movement sustained by nostalgia, ritual, and the ambitions of clergymen who seek to recover the Church's lost privileges.
