# Gesta Penguinorum

The *Gesta Penguinorum* (also titled *De Gestis Penguinorum*, "On the Deeds of the Penguins") is the foundational chronicle of Penguin history, composed in Latin by the Benedictine monk Johannes Talpa during the minority of King Gun. Written at the monastery of Beargarden over the course of the author's later life, the twelve-book chronicle is the principal surviving narrative source for early and medieval Penguinia. Its original manuscript is preserved in the National Library under the shelfmark *Monumenta Peng*., *K*. *L*6., 12390.

## In the Narrative

Johannes Talpa composed the *Gesta Penguinorum* at the monastery of Beargarden, "where at the age of fourteen he had made his profession and from which he never departed for a single day throughout his life" [028-001]. The chronicle was written during the minority of King Gun, a period of disorder in Penguinia.

Talpa began the work in his old age. He describes himself with characteristic simplicity: "My head has long since lost its adornment of fair hair, and my scalp resembles those convex mirrors of metal which the Penguin ladies consult with so much care and zeal. My stature, naturally small, has with years become diminished and bent. My white beard gives warmth to my breast" [028-003].

The composition of the chronicle was interrupted by a terrible war. Soldiers of Queen Crucha occupied the monastery of Beargarden, fortifying its walls and turning its cloisters into encampments. The Porpoises, northern warriors clad in copper armour, besieged the monastery for six months. Sixty times they attempted to scale its walls and sixty times they were repulsed — until a shepherd revealed a hidden path into the vaults [028-004, 028-006, 028-007].

As the abbey burned around him and monks "ran in thousands like ants, and fell into the valley," Johannes Talpa "kept on writing his Chronicle" [028-009]. Even after the destruction of the entire monastery — "soon there was left nothing of the rich and extensive abbey but the cell of Johannes Talpa, which, by a marvellous chance, hung from the ruin of a smoking gable" — "the old chronicler still kept writing" [028-009].

The chronicle, Anatole France notes, "suddenly comes to an end at the third year of Draco the Simple, the successor of Gun the Weak," leaving later historians to lament "the loss of an agreeable and trustworthy guide" [027-008].

## Content and Nature

The *Gesta Penguinorum* spans twelve books covering the history of the Penguins from their legendary origins through the medieval period, terminating abruptly in the reign of Draco the Simple. It was composed in Latin and served as the principal source for all subsequent Penguin historiography.

Anatole France, who claims to have personally consulted the manuscript, provides a detailed physical description:

> I have consulted the original manuscript of Johannes Talpa in the National Library, where it is preserved (*Monumenta Peng*., *K*. *L*6., 12390 *four*). It is a parchment manuscript of 628 leaves. The writing is extremely confused, the letters instead of being in a straight line, stray in all directions and are mingled together in great disorder, or, more correctly speaking, in absolute confusion. They are so badly formed that for the most part it is impossible not merely to say what they are, but even to distinguish them from the splashes of ink with which they are plentifully interspersed. [028-010]

France interprets the chaotic penmanship as material evidence of the historical disruptions amid which the chronicle was produced: "Those inestimable pages bear witness in this way to the troubles amid which they were written" [028-010].

Despite the physical disorder of the manuscript, the literary style of the *Gesta* is notably restrained:

> The tone of the "Gesta Penguinorum" never departs from simplicity. The narration is rapid and of a conciseness that sometimes approaches dryness. The reflections are rare and, as a rule, judicious. [028-010]

The chronicle incorporates autobiographical passages in which Talpa reflects on his own life. He recounts his harsh education — "I learnt to read under the guidance of a master who was called Amicus, and who would have been better named Inimicus. As I did not easily attain to a knowledge of my letters, he beat me violently with rods so that I can say that he printed the alphabet in strokes upon my back" [028-003] — and confesses, with disarming honesty, the ardours of his youth: "In my youth the ardour of my senses was such that in the shadow of the woods I experienced a sensation of boiling in a pot rather than of breathing the fresh air. I fled from women, but in vain, for every object recalled them to me" [028-004].

## Satirical Function

The *Gesta Penguinorum* functions as Anatole France's affectionate parody of the medieval monastic chronicle tradition. Talpa embodies the ideal of the devoted annalist — continuing to write as the world burns around him — pushed to absurdity. The image of the old monk composing his Latin history while his monastery is destroyed by siege engines and flaming wax is at once heroic and ridiculous.

The physical description of the manuscript serves a double satirical function. On one level, it mocks the apparatus of scholarly authentication — the shelfmark, the parchment count, the pseudo-precise codicological detail. On another, the "confused" and "indecipherable" handwriting becomes a metaphor for the inherent opacity of historical sources: the more closely one examines the documentary record, the less legible it becomes.

The abrupt termination of the chronicle at "the third year of Draco the Simple" [027-008] parodies the arbitrary incompleteness of all historical records. France's lament — "I deplore the loss of an agreeable and trustworthy guide" — is delivered with ironic self-awareness, since the narrator of *Penguin Island* is himself fabricating the very history he pretends to mourn losing.

Talpa's autobiographical digressions — the sadistic schoolmaster "Inimicus," the frank confession of sexual desire — satirize the medieval convention of authorial self-deprecation by pushing it into the realm of the comically personal. The monk who presents himself as humble and insignificant reveals, in the process, a vivid and entirely human self-absorption.

## Key Passages

> When he began to write his "Gesta Penguinorum," Johannes Talpa was already old. The good monk has taken care to tell us this in his book: "My head has long since lost its adornment of fair hair, and my scalp resembles those convex mirrors of metal which the Penguin ladies consult with so much care and zeal." [028-003]

> Yet Johannes Talpa kept on writing his Chronicle. The soldiers of Crucha retreated speedily and filled up all the issues from the monastery with pieces of rock so as to shut up the Porpoises in the burning buildings. [028-009]

> The tone of the "Gesta Penguinorum" never departs from simplicity. The narration is rapid and of a conciseness that sometimes approaches dryness. The reflections are rare and, as a rule, judicious. [028-010]
