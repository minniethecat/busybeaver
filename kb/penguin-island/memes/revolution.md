# Revolution in Penguin Island

Revolution in *Penguin Island* appears as both threat and practice, culminating in the socialist-revolutionary movements of modern Penguinia. France portrays revolution not as liberation but as a cyclical pattern of violence and replacement — one set of rulers substituting for another.

## In the Narrative

The peasants initially "thought that the revolution was a good thing for acquiring land" (034-001), reflecting the material motivations behind revolutionary movements. But the novel's most sustained treatment appears in Book VI (Modern Times: The Affair of the Eighty Thousand Trusses of Hay), where Bidault-Coquille and Maniflore lead a socialist-revolutionary faction.

The revolutionaries' rhetoric is scathing: "Revolution is impotence. Progress has no need of revolution" (056-012). France gives the counter-revolutionary position equal weight, while the revolutionaries themselves are portrayed as ideologues whose "revolutionary task" is indistinguishable from ambition. The general strike and revolution are proposed as tactics (052-027), but the narrative reveals these as theatrical gestures.

In the final book, revolution becomes farce: the revolutionists sing "revolutionary hymns" while looting barrels of wine (069-085). The tragic grandeur attributed to revolutions (056-016) is exposed as self-dramatization.

## Satirical Treatment

France's satire of revolution is fundamentally conservative in its implications: revolution changes nothing because human nature doesn't change. The revolutionaries mirror their oppressors in tactics and ambition. The "revolutionary manner" they adopt (052-026) is shown to be indistinguishable from the violence they claim to oppose.

The novel's treatment reflects France's own political evolution from socialist sympathizer to disillusioned skeptic after the failure of revolutionary movements in France.

## Historical Context

*Penguin Island* was published in 1908, after the Paris Commune (1871), the Dreyfus Affair (1894–1906), and during the rise of revolutionary syndicalism in France. The socialist movement was split between reformists and revolutionaries. France satirizes both the revolutionary left and the conservative establishment that fears it.
