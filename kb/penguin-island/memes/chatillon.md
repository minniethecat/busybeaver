# Chatillon

Chatillon, who bears the title of Emiral, is a celebrated military commander in Penguinia and a central figure in Book V of *Penguin Island*. His good looks, military fame, and entanglement with the wealthy Viscountess Olive make him both a romantic figure and a political pawn in the royalist conspiracy to restore [[prince-crucho|Prince Crucho]]. Anatole France uses Chatillon to satirize the cult of military celebrity in the French Third Republic.

## Role in the Narrative

Chatillon enters the narrative as the commander of the Penguin navy: "The navy was commanded by the famous Chatillon, who bore the title of Emiral" [041-002]. France describes him as "good-looking and fortune had showered her gifts upon him" [041-003], immediately establishing him as a figure of martial glamour.

His entanglement with the [[viscountess-olive|Viscountess Olive]] becomes the narrative engine of Book V. The Viscountess, a wealthy and well-connected woman involved in the royalist conspiracy, "cast her eyes upon Emiral Chatillon" [041-004] and subsequently "asked a large sum of money" from him — blending romantic intrigue with political financing.

Chatillon becomes a public hero of the royalist (Dracophil) faction. "The Dracophils proclaimed Chatillon's praises and hurled shame and opprobrium upon his enemies" [042-001]. His daily rides upon his white horse become a political ritual: "Every evening Chatillon rode upon his white horse round the Queen's Meadow" [042-002], attracting crowds who "cried at the top of their voices, 'Long live the Emiral!'" [042-003].

The Prince des Boscénos, a rival, visits Chatillon each morning, "whom he treated with the cordiality of a friend and the deference of a superior" [042-004] — a relationship that encodes the complex hierarchy of royalist politics.

## Key Actions and Appearances

**The Viscountess's Patronage**: Chatillon becomes the object of the Viscountess Olive's romantic and political interest. Their relationship intertwines personal desire with political finance — the Viscountess asks him for money while simultaneously advancing his public career [041-004].

**The Cult of the Emiral**: Chatillon's daily rides on his white horse become political theater. The crowds that cheer him are simultaneously celebrating military glory and expressing royalist sympathies [042-002, 042-003].

**The Political Pawn**: Despite his public prominence, Chatillon is ultimately a figure moved by others — the Viscountess, the Dracophils, the royalist conspirators. His military fame makes him useful to the conspiracy, but his own agency within it remains ambiguous.

**The Rivalry**: Chatillon's relationship with the Prince des Boscénos introduces the theme of rivalry within the royalist camp — even as they conspire together, they compete for status and the Viscountess's favour.

## Character Traits and Satirical Function

Chatillon satirizes the cult of the military hero in French political culture:

- **The glamour of command**: Chatillon's good looks and military bearing are inseparable from his political function. France implies that the public's adoration of military figures is fundamentally aesthetic rather than rational — they cheer the white horse as much as the man.

- **The empty uniform**: For all his fame, Chatillon has no discernible political principles. He is a vessel for the ambitions of others — the Viscountess, the Dracophils, the royalists. His celebrity is a form of availability.

- **The "Emiral" title**: France's coinage "Emiral" — a portmanteau of "Emir" and "Admiral" — satirizes the inflation of military titles and the exoticism of French colonial military culture.

- **The white horse**: Chatillon's ritual rides around the Queen's Meadow echo the ceremonial displays of military power that characterized the Third Republic's public culture — reviews, parades, and the cult of the *sabre*.

## Historical Parallels

Chatillon satirizes the type of the military celebrity in the Third Republic, particularly figures like General Boulanger — the "man on horseback" whose personal popularity briefly threatened the Republic in the late 1880s. Like Boulanger, Chatillon's appeal crosses political lines, combining military glamour with royalist sympathies. France's point is that the cult of the military hero is inherently anti-republican, bypassing democratic institutions in favor of personal charisma.

The title "Emiral" specifically satirizes the proliferation of exotic military ranks in France's colonial empire, blending European and Middle Eastern forms.
