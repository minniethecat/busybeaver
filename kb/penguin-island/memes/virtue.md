# Virtue in Penguin Island

Virtue is everywhere claimed and nowhere practiced in *Penguin Island*. From Saint Maël's holy reputation to the civic virtues proclaimed by Penguin politicians, France systematically exposes the gap between virtuous rhetoric and human behavior.

## In the Narrative

Virtue is first attributed to Saint Maël, who "advanced from virtue to virtue" (002-005), becoming renowned for his holiness. But the very mechanism of virtue signaling is immediately satirized: Maël's miracles are reported by followers with vested interests, and his virtue is a narrative construct.

The concept of civic virtue appears in Book V, where the Reverend Fathers invoke "the honour of religion and the exaltation of the Catholic faith" to justify political conspiracy. The revolutionary leaders of Book VI claim to fight for virtue while practicing violence. Even Madame Clarence, in her modern drawing-room, performs virtue as social currency.

The most explicit satire comes in the final book: "Jealousy is a virtue of democracies" (066-001) — an ironic declaration that equates virtue with petty social competition. The penguin civilization discovers that "virtue has been acquired, having been unknown to Greece" (069-004) — meaning that what passes for virtue is merely the absence of opportunity for vice.

## Satirical Treatment

France's treatment of virtue follows a consistent pattern: a character or group claims moral superiority, and the narrative reveals their underlying self-interest. The hagiographic tradition around Saint Maël is shown to be legendary embellishment. Political virtue is exposed as rhetorical cover for ambition. Even religious virtue — the monk's celibacy and poverty — is motivated by institutional power.

## Related Characters

- **Saint Maël** — Embodiment of claimed virtue, whose sanctity is partly legendary
- **Father Agaric** — Claims religious virtue while conspiring politically
- **Madame Clarence** — Performs social virtue as a drawing-room accomplishment
- **The Penguins** — Collective subject of virtue discourse that masks baser instincts
