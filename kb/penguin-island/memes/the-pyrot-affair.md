# The Pyrot Affair

The Pyrot Affair is the central political event of *Penguin Island* Book VI — a transparent allegory of the Dreyfus Affair that divided France from 1894 to 1906. France uses the absurd charge (stealing eighty thousand trusses of hay) to expose the machinery of military justice, political corruption, and public credulity.

## In the Narrative

Pyrot, an innocent penguin, is accused of stealing eighty thousand trusses of hay. General Greatauk delivers him "to justice as guilty" (046-020) despite the complete absence of evidence. The prosecutor tells the Minister of Justice that "very little is needed for a prosecution to be set on foot" (043-047).

The case divides Penguin society into Pyrotists (who believe in Pyrot's innocence) and anti-Pyrotists (who support the military's authority). The debate consumes the nation, exposing deep social fissures. The Colomban trial (053-033) extends the affair's logic into new domains of persecution.

## Historical Context

The Dreyfus Affair began in 1894 when Captain Alfred Dreyfus was wrongly convicted of treason. The case split France into Dreyfusards (including Anatole France, Émile Zola) and anti-Dreyfusards (military, Church, conservatives). Dreyfus was finally exonerated in 1906. France's allegory follows the actual events closely while transforming them into absurdist satire.
