## Statement

"Do not attempt too much at once" is the explicit moral of The Boy and the Filberts (075-002). A Boy thrusts his hand into a jar of nuts and grasps as many as his fist can hold — but the neck of the jar is too narrow to let the bulging fist pass. Unwilling to sacrifice any of his [[reward|prize]], he is trapped. A bystander advises him to take half, and the lesson lands: greed transforms a gain into a cage.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Boy and the Filberts

A Boy reaches into a jar of Filberts and grabs as many as his fist will hold. He cannot withdraw his hand because the neck is too narrow. He cries in frustration, unwilling to let go of a single nut.

> A bystander, who saw where the trouble lay, said to him, "Come, my boy, don't be so greedy: be content with half the amount, and you'll be able to get your hand out without difficulty." (075-001)

The fable's compact structure — three sentences — makes the mechanism transparent: the same hand that grasped the [[reward|prize]] is what keeps it prisoner.

### The Goose That Laid the Golden Eggs

A Man owns a Goose that lays a golden egg every day. Dissatisfied with the daily rate, he kills the Goose to extract all the gold at once — and finds nothing inside. By attempting too much, he loses even what he had (070-001). The filberts jar and the goose's belly operate on the same principle: the attempt to maximize destroys the source of value.

### The Discontented Tortoise

A Tortoise, "discontented with his lowly life," begs Jupiter to teach him to fly. Jupiter refuses. The Tortoise's des[[anger|ire]] to attempt what is beyond its nature mirrors the Boy's overreach — both mistake [[much-wants-more-and-loses-all|wanting more]] for being able to have more (081-001).

## Mechanism

The moral's mechanism is **geometric**: the hand that grasps is also the hand that blocks. The Boy's fist is the same object inside and outside the jar — the constraint is not external but self-created. This distinguishes "do not attempt too much" from simple warnings about greed. The Boy does not lose his filberts to a thief or to circumstance; he loses them to his own unwillingness to release any part of his claim.

Aesop embeds the same geometry in other fables: the Milkmaid loses everything by mentally spending her egg money before the eggs exist ([[do-not-count-your-chickens]]). In both cases, the grasping mind creates the trap that the grasping hand then springs.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[do-not-count-your-chickens]] — Both morals address premature or excessive claiming. The Milkmaid attempts too much in imagination; the Boy attempts too much in physical fact. The mechanism is identical: overreach converts a potential gain into an actual loss.
- [[greed]] — [[greed|Greed]] is the impulse; "do not attempt too much at once" is the structural [[punishment|consequence]] that greed produces. The Boy's greed is straightforward; the structural trap that greed sets is Aesop's distinctive contribution.
- [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race]] — The Tortoise's gradual approach is the inverse of the Boy's all-at-once grasp. Patience and moderation, in Aesop's world, are not just virtues but survival strategies.

## Enduring Influence

"Don't bite off more than you can chew" is the closest English proverbial descendant, though it shifts the domain from grasping to eating. The fable's core image — a hand trapped by its own greed — appears in modern behavioral economics as the "endowment effect": people overvalue what they already possess and refuse to trade down even when it would benefit them. The Boy and the Filberts appears in the Perry Index as number 301.
