## Role in Aesop

The Bee appears in Aesop's Fables as the emblem of **collective industry** — a creature whose labor produces sweetness ([[honey]]) but whose defense of that sweetness proves fatal. Unlike the [[the-ant|Ant]], whose industry is purely self-preserving, the Bee's work benefits others (gods and men alike), and her story is one of unintended consequences. The fable of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] and the Bee (040-001) is the definitive statement: the bee asks for the power to harm those who steal her honey, and receives a sting that kills her upon use.

The Bee's role is small but morally concentrated. She appears in three contexts: as petitioner to the gods, as accidental savior of the fruitless tree, and as the unwitting enforcer in the [[the-beekeeper|Bee-keeper]]'s tragedy. Across all three, the same pattern holds: the bee's nature — to build, to defend, to die for the hive — produces outcomes that no single bee intended.

## Key Appearances

### The Bee and Jupiter

A Queen Bee brought [[honey]] to [[jupiter|Jupiter]] on Olympus, and the god, pleased with the gift, offered her any wish. She asked for stings to kill those who robbed the hive. Jupiter, though he loved mankind, had given his word — so he granted the request, but with a fatal condition:

> The stings he gave them, however, were of such a kind that whenever a bee stings a man the sting is left in the wound and the bee dies. (040-001)

The fable's moral is direct: evil wishes come home to roost. The bee's defensive impulse, pushed to murderous extremes, rebounds upon her own kind. [[jupiter|Jupiter]]'s wisdom lies not in refusing the wish outright, but in making the weapon self-consuming — a divine compromise between promise-keeping and protecting mankind.

### The Bee-Keeper's Unjust Punishment

In a later fable, a thief steals all the [[honey]] from an apiary while the Bee-keeper is away. When the bees return to find their hives empty, they attack — not the thief, but the innocent keeper standing beside the wreckage. The keeper's cry captures the irony:

> "You ungrateful scoundrels, you let the thief who stole my honey get off scot-free, and then you go and sting me who have always taken such care of you!" (218-001)

Here the bee's famous sting, granted by [[jupiter|Jupiter]], is turned against the wrong target — the bees cannot distinguish thief from keeper, and their blind defensive instinct punishes the one person who served them.

### The Fruitless Apple-Tree

A Peasant prepares to cut down a barren apple-tree, despite the sparrows and [[grasshopper|grasshoppers]] begging him to spare it. A few strokes of the axe reveal that the hollow trunk contains a swarm of bees and a large store of [[honey]]. The peasant throws down his axe: the tree is worth keeping after all (069-001). Here the Bee is the silent, invisible agent of salvation — her honey, not her sting, is what saves the tree.

## Relationship with Jupiter

The Bee's relationship with [[jupiter|Jupiter]] is the axis of her fable identity. Unlike the [[eagle]], who enjoys Jupiter's direct protection, or the [[beetle]], who outwits him through persistence, the Bee approaches Jupiter as a petitioner bearing gifts. She gives [[honey]]; she asks for stings. Jupiter's response — granting the request but making it self-destructive — is the archetypal Aesopic judgment: the wish is fulfilled, but the wisher learns the cost too late. The Bee and [[jupiter|Jupiter]] together embody the fable's warning that the power to harm is never separable from the vulnerability to be harmed.

## Symbolism

The Bee in Aesop symbolizes **industry whose defense becomes self-destruction**. She is the creature whose sweetness attracts plunder, and whose response to plunder — the sting — is a weapon that kills the wielder. In a broader sense, the Bee represents the paradox of collective labor: the hive produces abundance, but the individual bee who defends that abundance pays with her life. Her fables are meditations on the limits of defensive [[violence]] and the [[irony]] of [[revenge|vengeance]] that costs more than it recovers.
