## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], a quarrel is never a victory for either side. It is a state of mutual [[ruin|destruction]] — two parties so consumed by their dispute that they become blind to the predator watching from above, the enemy waiting at the gate, or the simple tool that could solve their problem.

The corpus treats quarrelling as a form of **collective [[foolishness|folly]]**: the combatants pour their energy into harming one another while the real [[danger|threat]] goes unnoticed. Whether between Dolphins and Whales, brothers, or a [[the-lion|Lion]] and a Boar, the quarrel always ends with the same lesson — stop fighting before someone else benefits.

> The quarrels of friends are the opportunities of foes. ([[fable-122|122-002]])

## Varieties

**The Endless Quarrel.** The Dolphins and Whales in [[fable-030|030-001]] fight "for some time without any sign of coming to an end." When a Sprat attempts to mediate, the Dolphins respond with [[contempt]]: "We would rather go on fighting till we're all killed than be reconciled by a Sprat like you!" The quarrel has become its own purpose — neither side can accept peace if it means accepting the mediator. [[pride|Pride]] in the quarrel outlasts the quarrel's original cause.

**The Preventable Quarrel.** [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Boar in [[fable-078|078-001]] come to a spring at the same moment on a hot day and immediately begin "quarrelling as to who should drink first." The quarrel escalates to violence before either drinks. Only the sight of vultures — "evidently waiting for one of them to be killed" — sobers them. The quarrel is not resolved by wisdom but by the sudden visibility of its cost.

**The Familial Quarrel.** The Sons in [[fable-058|058-001]] are "always quarrelling with one another." Their father demonstrates the lesson with a bundle of sticks: individually, each stick snaps easily; together, the bundle is unbreakable. The quarrel among brothers is the quarrel that most delights their enemies — internal division is the precondition for external conquest.

**The Irreconcilable Quarrel.** The Labourer and the Snake in [[fable-188|188-001]] reach a state beyond mediation. The Snake's verdict — "I can never be your friend because of my lost tail, nor you mine because of your lost child" — describes a quarrel where both parties have suffered irreparable loss. Some quarrels, once begun, cannot be undone; the injury on each side makes reconciliation impossible.

## Consequences

Every quarrel in Aesop produces the same structural outcome: the combatants weaken each other while a third party — vultures, enemies, or [[fortune|fate]] itself — profits from their exhaustion. The fables do not [[advice|counsel]] against all conflict, but against conflict that is disproportionate to its cause. To quarrel over who drinks first when vultures are circling is not cou[[anger|rage]] — it is self-[[ruin|destruction]] by mutual consent.

## Relation to Anger

Quarrel and [[anger]] form a tight feedback loop in Aesop. [[Anger]] provokes the quarrel; the quarrel intensifies the anger; the escalating cycle continues until external reality intervenes — a vulture, a bundle of sticks, a dead child. The fables offer one exit from this loop: the moment of sober recognition when combatants look up and see what their quarrel has cost them. But as the Labourer and the Snake show, that moment can come too late.
