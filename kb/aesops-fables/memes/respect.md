## Definition

Respect in [[Aesop's Fables]] is never self-assigned; it is granted by others in response to demonstrated merit, and its misattribution — whether by the respecter or the respected — leads invariably to [[shame|humiliation]]. Unlike [[flattery]], which is manufactured and deceptive, genuine respect arises from observed competence: the Owl earns it through [[wisdom|foresight]], the Raven is granted it for symbolic [[strength|power]], and the Ass who mistakes ceremonial reverence for personal esteem is beaten for his presumption.

The fables draw a sharp line between respect that is **bestowed** (by the comm[[harmony|unity]], for real or perceived value) and respect that is **appropriated** (by an individual who confuses proximity to honor with personal desert). The Ass Carrying the Image captures this distinction with surgical precision: the animal believes the bows intended for a sacred object are d[[anger|ire]]cted at him, and his driver's stick delivers the corrective (208-001). The fable's moral — "Rude shocks await those who take to themselves the credit that is due to others" — is the closest Aesop comes to a theory of respect.

## Respect in the Fables

Aesop explores respect through three distinct scenarios, each illuminating a different failure mode:

> The Owl is a very wise bird; and once, long ago, when the first oak sprouted in the forest, she called all the other Birds together and said to them, "You see this tiny tree? If you take my advice, you will destroy it now when it is small."... they changed their minds and conceived a great respect for her wisdom. (060-002)

> A certain man put an Image on the back of his Ass to take it to one of the temples of the town. As they went along the road all the people they met uncovered and bowed their heads out of reverence for the Image; but the Ass thought they were doing it out of respect for himself, and began to give himself airs accordingly. (208-001)

## Varieties of Respect

The fables distinguish several forms of respect, each with its own vulnerability:

- **Earned respect** — granted after demonstrated competence, as when the Birds finally acknowledge the Owl's wisdom (060-002). This is the ideal, but the fable warns it may arrive only after the respected party has ceased to care.
- **Misattributed respect** — when the recipient of ceremonial deference confuses it for personal esteem, as with the Ass (208-001). The boundary between the office and the occupant is one Aesop insists upon.
- **Envious respect** — the des[[anger|ire]] to possess the regard others enjoy without earning it, exemplified by the Crow who covets the Raven's status as an omen-bird (259-001). The Crow cannot become what she is not, and her attempt exposes the gap between imitation and substance.
- **Institutional respect** — the social deference owed to a role or symbol rather than to the individual bearing it. The fables treat this as morally neutral but practically [[danger|hazard]]ous: those who carry it may forget they are merely carriers.

## Consequences

When respect is misd[[anger|ire]]cted in Aesop, the [[punishment|consequence]]s follow a predictable pattern: **exposure through performance**. The Ass is beaten when he stops moving. The Crow is dismissed the moment she opens her beak. The would-be respected party must eventually *act*, and the action reveals the emptiness of the claim.

This pattern is structural rather than punitive — respect in the fables is not a moral reward but an **emergent property of function**. The Owl is respected because her predictions are accurate; the moment she stops [[sacrifice|offering]] them, the respect becomes inert. The Raven is respected because humans have assigned him symbolic meaning; a crow attempting the same performance cannot replicate the under[[deception|lying]] social architecture.
