## Statement

"Once bitten, twice shy" is the explicit moral of The Dog and the Wolf (221-002). A Dog who talks his way out of a Wolf's jaws by pleading thinness and promising a future feast learns, after his narrow escape, that no promise of reward is worth the risk of a second encounter. The moral captures a universal truth of [[danger|threat]]-learning: a single exposure to danger permanently recalibrates behaviour, making future caution involuntary rather than deliberative.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Dog and the Wolf

A hungry Wolf catches a thin Dog and prepares to eat him. The Dog pleads for his life with an argument from deferred gratification:

> A Dog was lying in the sun before a farmyard gate when a Wolf pounced upon him and was just going to eat him up; but he begged for his life and said, "You see how thin I am and what a wretched meal I should make you now: but if you will only wait a few days, my master is going to give a feast, and I shall grow fat on all the scraps." (221-001)

The Wolf relents and departs. When he returns, the Dog is safely inside the farmyard, and delivers the fable's closing judgment:

> Once bitten, twice shy. (221-002)

The Dog has learned his lesson: safety, once recovered, is not to be traded away. The Wolf's mistake is not greed but a failure of [[danger|threat]]-assessment — he underestimates the Dog's capacity to learn from danger.

## Mechanism

The moral operates on a simple but [[strength|power]]ful learning model: **single-trial [[danger|threat]] conditioning**. Unlike other Aesopic morals that requ[[anger|ire]] repeated demonstration (e.g., [[habits-change-nature-stays]]), "once bitten, twice shy" asserts that one exposure is sufficient. The Dog does not need to be caught twice; a single brush with death permanently alters his risk calculus.

Three structural features:

1. **Asymmetric stakes**: The Wolf's cost of waiting is low (he can hunt elsewhere); the Dog's cost of trusting is fatal. The asymmetry guarantees the Dog will not repeat his mistake, even if the Wolf's offer is genuine.
2. **The safety boundary**: The farmyard gate marks the transition from vulnerability to safety. Once behind it, the Dog is insulated — and the insulation itself provides the clarity to refuse. [[safety|Safety]] enables refusal.
3. **Involuntary caution**: "Shy" here does not mean timid but **wary** — an automatic, uncalculating aversion to a known [[danger|threat]]. The Dog does not weigh pros and cons; he simply refuses. Threat-learning bypasses deliberation.
