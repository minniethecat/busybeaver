The Monkey is a distinctive character in [[Aesop's Fables]], appearing in fables that explore the tension between sincere devotion and foolish pretension. Unlike the more consistently drawn animal archetypes — the [[the-fox|Fox]] as cunning, the [[the-lion|Lion]] as powerful — the Monkey embodies a more complex duality: capable of touching parental love in one fable and fatal pretentiousness in another. The Monkey's versatility as a character reflects the animal's proximity to humanity — close enough to mimic, distant enough to fail.

## Role in Aesop

Within the Aesopic bestiary, the Monkey occupies a unique position as the animal most like humans — and therefore most exposed to human vices. The Monkey can be a devoted mother whose love transcends aesthetic judgment, or a pretentious liar whose claims to social status prove fatal. This duality makes the Monkey one of Aesop's most psychologically complex characters: neither wholly sympathetic nor wholly villainous, but recognizably human in both its virtues and its flaws.

The Monkey's proximity to humanity is central to its symbolic function. In both major appearances, the Monkey is judged by gods or by nature — and found wanting precisely where it most aspires to human status. The Monkey's tragedy (and comedy) is that it cannot help being what it is, even when what it is brings about its destruction.

## Key Appearances

### Jupiter and the Monkey (Fable 57)

In one of Aesop's most touching fables, a Monkey mother brings her baby to a divine beauty contest, and her love transcends the mockery of the gods:

> Jupiter issued a proclamation to all the beasts, and offered a prize to the one who, in his judgment, produced the most beautiful offspring. Among the rest came the Monkey, carrying a baby monkey in her arms, a hairless, flat-nosed little fright. When they saw it, the gods all burst into peal on peal of laughter. (057-001)

The gods' cruel laughter sets up the fable's emotional climax — the Monkey's defiant declaration of unconditional love:

> But the Monkey hugged her little one to her, and said, "Jupiter may give the prize to whomsoever he likes: but I shall always think my baby the most beautiful of them all." (057-001)

This fable presents the Monkey at her most sympathetic — a mother whose love is immune to social judgment, whose devotion transcends aesthetic standards. The gods' laughter reveals their limitations, not the Monkey's.

### The Monkey and the Dolphin (Fable 229)

In stark contrast, the Monkey appears as a pretentious fool whose lies lead directly to his death. A shipwrecked Monkey, rescued by a Dolphin who mistakes him for a man, cannot resist the temptation to inflate his status:

> When they got near the Piraeus, which is the port of Athens, the Dolphin asked the Monkey if he was an Athenian. The Monkey replied that he was, and added that he came of a very distinguished family. "Then, of course, you know the Piraeus," continued the Dolphin. The Monkey thought he was referring to some high official or other, and replied, "Oh, yes, he's a very old friend of mine." (229-002)

The Monkey's ignorance — mistaking the port of Athens for a person — exposes his lies with devastating efficiency:

> At that, detecting his hypocrisy, the Dolphin was so disgusted that he dived below the surface, and the unfortunate Monkey was quickly drowned. (229-002)

The punishment is swift and fatal. The Monkey's pretension — claiming distinguished Athenian lineage and familiarity with the port he has never seen — is both comic and tragic, a warning about the dangers of pretending to knowledge one does not possess.

## Relationship with Jupiter

The Monkey's relationship with [[jupiter]], king of the gods, is defined by a single, poignant encounter. In "Jupiter and the Monkey" (Fable 57), Jupiter sets the terms of the beauty contest — he will judge and award the prize — but the Monkey mother refuses to accept his judgment. Her love for her child is not contingent on divine approval. This is one of the few instances in Aesop where a mortal creature effectively defies a god and emerges morally triumphant. Jupiter does not punish the Monkey for her defiance; the fable ends with her declaration of love, not with divine retribution. The relationship thus suggests that parental love operates outside the hierarchy of divine judgment — a realm where even Jupiter's authority finds its limit.

## Symbolism

The Monkey symbolizes the duality of imitation — the capacity for both authentic love and inauthentic pretension. As the animal closest to human form, the Monkey embodies the promise and peril of likeness: in one fable, likeness enables devotion that transcends mockery; in another, it enables lies that lead to death. The Monkey is Aesop's exploration of what it means to be "almost human" — capable of a mother's unconditional love, yet also of a fool's fatal vanity. In this sense, the Monkey may be Aesop's most human character: flawed, loving, pretentious, and ultimately subject to judgment by forces — gods, dolphins, nature — that see through all disguise.
