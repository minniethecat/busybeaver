## Statement

"[[misfortune|Misfortune]] tests the [[honesty|sincerity]] of friendship" is the explicit moral of The Bear and the Travellers (037-002). Two Travellers are on the road together when a Bear appears. One climbs a tree and hides; the other, too slow to follow, throws himself to the ground and holds his breath, pretending to be dead, "for they say that a bear will not touch a dead body" (037-001). The Bear sniffs him all over, takes him for a corpse, and departs. When the tree-climber descends and asks jokingly what the Bear whispered, the other Traveller replies: "He told me never again to travel with a friend who deserts you at the first sign of danger" (037-001). The moral's [[strength|power]] lies in its framing: the Bear — the instrument of danger itself — becomes the revealer of character. The cowardly friend does not merely fail to help; his failure is dramatised and exposed by the very [[danger|threat]] he fled.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Bear and the Travellers

Two Travellers are walking together when a Bear appears:

> "Before he observed them, one made for a tree at the side of the road, and climbed up into the branches and hid there. The other was not so nimble as his companion; and, as he could not escape, he threw himself on the ground and pretended to be dead." (037-001)

The Bear approaches the prostrate Traveller, sniffs him, and — believing him dead — departs without harming him. The tree-climber then comes down, evidently amused, and asks what the Bear whispered. The reply is the moral itself:

> "He told me never again to travel with a friend who deserts you at the first sign of danger." (037-001)

The ingenious reply turns the question back on the questioner: the Bear is the vehicle of judgment, not merely of danger.

### The Two Fellows and the Bear

A structurally identical fable (not in this corpus but widely circulated in Aesop's collections) rein[[strength|force]]s the same point: two men promise to face danger together, but when a bear appears, one flees upward while the other is left below. The motif is stable across variants — the tree is the coward's [[safety|refuge]], the ground is where [[honesty|sincerity]] is tested.

### The Fox and the Monkey

In [[the-fox|The Fox]] and the Monkey (fable-031), the two animals [[quarrel|dispute]] their ancestry. When they pass through a cemetery, the Monkey points to the tombs and claims they honour his forefathers. [[the-fox|The Fox]] retorts: "You lie so boldly because you know the dead cannot rise to contradict you." The test here is not physical danger but a social one: the [[the-fox|Fox]] exposes the Monkey's claim as unverifiable, just as the Bear exposes the tree-climber's friendship as performative.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **danger as a sorting device**. [[friendship|Friendship]], in ordinary circumstances, is cheap to profess — anyone can declare themselves a friend when the cost of friendship is zero. [[danger|Danger]] introduces a cost: the friend who genuinely values the relationship will bear that cost (risk of injury, loss of time, shared [[danger|peril]]). The friend who values only the benefits of [[friendship|companionship]] will not.

The mechanism has three components:

1. **Cost asymmetry**: Climbing a tree costs nothing; staying on the ground risks being mauled. The tree-climber chooses the zero-cost option, revealing that he values his own safety above his companion's.
2. **Revelation through crisis**: The Bear is an unwitting diagnostician. Without the Bear's appearance, the tree-climber's [[coward|cowardice]] would remain latent, undiscovered. The crisis does not create the flaw; it reveals it.
3. **The test is retrospective**: The abandoned Traveller does not know in advance that his companion will desert him. He learns it only through the event. [[friendship|Friendship]] cannot be tested in advance; it can only be tested by [[fortune|misfortune]] when [[fortune|misfortune]] arrives.

## Enduring Influence

The moral has crystallised into the English proverb "A friend in need is a friend indeed" — a line whose oldest recorded form (Ennius, 3rd century BCE) runs: *Amicus certus in re incerta cernitur* ("A sure friend is known in unsure times"). The Traveller on the ground acqu[[anger|ire]]s knowledge that no amount of fair-weather [[friendship|companionship]] could have provided. The fable also anticipates the social-psychological concept of *costly signalling*: only actions that impose a genuine cost on the actor can reliably signal commitment, because cheap signals can be faked. Climbing a tree is cheap; standing one's ground is costly — and honesty is what the costly signal reveals.
