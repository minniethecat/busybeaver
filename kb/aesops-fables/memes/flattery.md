Flattery in [[Aesop's Fables]] is never innocent. It is the signature move of the predator — the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s preferred tactic, the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s fallback when [[strength|force]] fails. The fables document the flattery transaction in precise detail: the flatterer identifies the victim's vanity, applies praise to that exact point, and strikes when the victim's guard is down ([[009-001]], [[241-001]], [[267-001]]).

## Definition

Aesopian flattery is strategic false praise deployed to disarm a target. It has three components: (1) **a vulnerability is identified** — the Crow's vanity about her voice, the Bull's pride in his horns, the Stag's ambition; (2) **praise is applied to that vulnerability** — excessive, specific, designed to inflate; (3) **the target is exploited** while basking in the praise. Flattery works because the victim *wants* the praise to be true, and wanting is the crack through which the predator enters.

> The Fox said, "What a noble bird I see above me! Her beauty is without equal. If only her voice is as sweet as her looks are fair, she ought without doubt to be Queen of the Birds." The Crow was hugely flattered by this, and just to show the Fox that she could sing, she gave a loud caw. Down came the cheese. ([[009-001]])

> The Lion said, "I cannot help saying how much I admire your magnificent figure. What a fine head! What powerful shoulders! What splendid legs!" ([[241-001]])

## Varieties

**Flattery That Succeeds.** [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s approach to the Crow ([[009-001]]) is the canonical case: the Crow is vain about her voice, the [[the-fox|Fox]] praises it, the Crow demonstrates it, the cheese falls. Every element is visible to the reader, and yet the mechanism works. [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Bull ([[241-001]]) follows the identical template: flattery disarms, the predator takes.

**Flattery That Fails.** The Grasshopper ([[205-001]]) receives the same treatment — the [[the-fox|Fox]] praises her song — but sees through it. She replies: "You are very much mistaken, my dear sir, if you imagine I am going to come down: I keep well out of your way." This is the rare counter-example, and it proves the rule: flattery works only on those who are ready to believe it.

**Flattery as Recruitment.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] flattering the Stag ([[267-001]]) extends the pattern: the Stag is told the [[the-lion|Lion]] has appointed him successor to the throne. This is flattery of ambition rather than vanity, but the mechanism is identical — the Stag wants the news to be true, follows the [[the-fox|Fox]] to the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den, and is killed.

## Consequences

The flattered character who believes the praise is destroyed — the Crow loses her cheese ([[009-001]]), the Bull loses his life ([[241-001]]), the Stag loses his life ([[267-001]]). The one character who sees through flattery survives ([[205-001]]). Flattery is thus the purest test of self-knowledge in the Aesopian corpus: those who know their own worth accurately are immune to flattery; those who do not are its prey. This links flattery d[[anger|ire]]ctly to [[pride]] — flattery exploits pride, and the proud are the easiest marks.

## Relation to Persuasion

[[Flattery]] is a species of [[persuasion]], distinguished by its method. [[Persuasion]] appeals to reason or interest; flattery appeals to vanity. [[the-fox|The Fox]] uses flattery to persuade the Crow ([[009-001]]) and [[persuasion]] to convince the Dogs ([[184-001]]). The two overlap — the Bull is both flattered and persuaded ([[241-001]]) — but the distinction matters: flattery is per[[persuasion|suasion]] by false praise, and it works only where the target's self-estimation is already inflated.
