## Statement

"Do not count your chickens before they are hatched" is the explicit moral of The Milkmaid and Her Pail (029-002). A farmer's daughter, carrying a pail of milk on her head, daydreams her way through an ent[[anger|ire]] chain of future transactions and spills the milk when she acts out her imagined triumph.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Milkmaid and Her Pail

A farmer's daughter plans her future [[fortune|prosperity]] while walking home with a pail of milk. Her chain of reasoning is logical at each step, but the ent[[anger|ire]] edifice rests on unhatched eggs.

> Forgetting all about the pail, and suiting the action to the word, she tossed her head. Down went the pail, all the milk was spilled, and all her fine castles in the air vanished in a moment! (029-001)

### The Greedy Boy

A boy thrusts his hand into a jar of nuts and grasps too many. His hand becomes stuck in the narrow neck. His greed for the full handful loses him everything — reaching for more than the present moment can deliver (075-001).

### The Discontented Peacock

A Peacock, discontented with his lack of a beautiful voice, demands one from Juno. The reply is a template for the Milkmaid's error: be content with what you have, because wanting what you cannot have destroys your enjoyment of what you do (097-001).

## Mechanism

Aesop's diagnosis of the Milkmaid's error is not that she is ambitious but that she acts on an outcome before it exists. Each step in her chain is individually rational; the error is treating the chain as already-realized.

The physical mechanism mirrors the psychological one: she suits the action to the word, and the real pail — the only real thing in the story — falls. The unreal future destroys the real present.

## Enduring Influence

"Do not count your chickens before they are hatched" is a widely-adopted Aesopic expression in everyday English. It survives as a living proverb, used without awareness of its fable origin.

The expression "castles in the air," which the fable also coins, is similarly durable. The fable is Perry Index number 340.
