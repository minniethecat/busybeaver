## Statement

"Two blacks do not make a white" is the explicit moral of The Sheep, the Wolf, and the Stag (121-002). A Stag asks a Sheep for a loan of wheat, [[sacrifice|offering]] the Wolf as surety. The Sheep refuses: the Wolf seizes what he wants without paying, and the Stag can outrun any pursuer — so how could a creditor ever collect?

> Two blacks do not make a white. (121-002)

The Sheep's reasoning is probabilistic, not moralistic. She does not accuse either party of a specific scheme. She observes their known behavior — the Wolf's bolt-and-grab, the Stag's speed — and calculates that the combination of two flight risks does not produce one reliable debtor.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Sheep, the Wolf, and the Stag (primary)

The fable is Aesop's most concise lesson in credit analysis. The Sheep evaluates (a) character: the Wolf's known habits, (b) capability: the Stag's speed advantage, and (c) structure: the surety arrangement that supposedly mitigates risk. She concludes that the arrangement is a farce — the surety makes the deal worse, not better (121-001).

### The Sick Stag

A Stag, too sick to fo[[anger|rage]], is slowly starved by the friends who gather to visit him — each eating the grass within his reach. The moral's irony is sharper: the surety who offers help can be worse than the original [[danger|threat]] (078-001).

### The Nurse and the Wolf

A Wolf overhears a Nurse [[danger|threat]]ening to throw a crying child to the wolves. He waits all night at the door, only to be driven off by dogs in the morning. The Nurse's words were not payment — one black [[danger|threat]] does not guarantee one white meal (047-001).

## Mechanism

The fable operates on a logical rather than ethical plane. The Sheep does not say "two wrongs don't make a right" in the sense of moral accounting. She says two **debt-dodging vectors** don't cancel — each party's flight risk is independently assessed, and the Wolf's surety adds no constraint on the Stag.

This is Aesop's version of the principal-agent problem: a surety who cannot be caught is no surety at all. The fable implies a general principle about compounding unreliability — adding a second unreliable party does not diversify risk; it multiplies it.
