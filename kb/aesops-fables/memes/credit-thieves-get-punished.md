## Statement

"Rude shocks await those who take to themselves the credit that is due to others" is the explicit moral of The Ass Carrying the Image (208-002). A man places a sacred Image on the back of his Ass to carry it to the temple. Along the road, passersby bow reverently before the Image. The Ass, observing the bows, mistakes them for homage d[[anger|ire]]cted at himself:

> "As they went along the road all the people they met uncovered and bowed their heads out of reverence for the Image; but the Ass thought they were doing it out of respect for himself, and began to give himself airs accordingly." (208-001)

The Ass becomes so [[pride|conceit]]ed that he stops dead and refuses to proceed. His driver beats him and delivers the corrective:

> "Oh, you dunder-headed idiot, do you suppose it's come to this, that men pay worship to an Ass? Rude shocks await those who take to themselves the credit that is due to others." (208-002)

The Ass's error is a **causal misattribution**: he observes an effect (people bowing) and assigns it to the wrong cause (his own presence rather than the sacred Image). The corrective is not merely verbal — it is physical. The driver's stick teaches what argument cannot: that the Ass's self-conception does not survive contact with reality.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass Carrying the Image

The Ass's [[pride|conceit]] follows a predictable sequence: observation → misattribution → inflated self-[[respect|regard]] → refusal to work → violent correction. Each step follows logically from the one before. The Ass observes the bows correctly — people *are* bowing. Where he errs is in the causal inference. He notices that bows occur when he passes, and concludes (by *post hoc ergo propter hoc*) that he is the cause.

This is the cognitive error of **egocentric attribution bias**: the tendency to credit oneself for positive outcomes that one merely happened to be present for. The Ass did not craft the Image, did not earn the worship, did not even choose the route — he was led. Yet the mere contiguity of "I am here" and "they are bowing" is enough to generate the illusion of desert.

### The Jackdaw and the Peacocks

A Jackdaw finds discarded peacock feathers and sticks them among his own, then struts among the Peacocks as if he were one of them. The Peacocks see through the disguise, p[[fortune|luck]] out the borrowed feathers, and drive him away (PN=199-001). When he returns to his fellow Jackdaws, they too reject him. The Jackdaw's punishment is double: exposed by those he imitated, rejected by those he abandoned. The Ass's punishment is simpler but follows the same logic: the borrowed credit is torn away, and the claimant is left with nothing.

### The Oxen and the Axle-Trees

A team of Oxen drag a heavy wagon. The Axle-Trees groan loudly under the weight, attracting attention. The Oxen remark: "We bear all the labor, and the Axles do the complaining" (PN=052-001). This is the mirror image of the Ass's error: the Ass claims credit for what the Image earned; the Axles claim suffering for what the Oxen endured. Both are forms of **credit misallocation** — one taking unearned honor, the other claiming unearned grievance.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **credit attribution failure under observational ambiguity**. When an outcome is produced by multiple factors (the Image's sacredness + the Ass's presence), the observer — including the participant — must correctly partition credit among causes. The Ass's error has three components:

1. **Spatial contiguity**: The Ass is physically present when the bows occur. He uses spatial proximity as a proxy for causal contribution, which is the simplest (and often wrong) attribution heuristic.
2. **Absence of corrective feedback**: No one tells the Ass "we are bowing to the Image, not to you" — until the driver's stick. The lack of verbal feedback allows the misattribution to compound into [[pride|conceit]].
3. **Action as test**: The Ass's refusal to move is, in his mind, a test of his status. "If people truly worship me," he thinks, "they will not [[strength|force]] me to carry a load." The driver's stick is the experimental result: the worship was never his.

The driver's beating serves as both punishment and education — it forcibly realigns the Ass's self-model with reality. The "rude shock" is the sudden collision between self-conception and external fact.

## Enduring Influence

The moral survives in the English idiom "riding on someone's coattails" and the workplace complaint of "taking credit for my work." The Ass's error is a perennial feature of organizations: those in visible positions (managers, spokespeople, figureheads) are often credited with outcomes produced by those who are less visible. The corrective — the driver's stick — takes the modern form of 360-degree reviews, attribution tracking in version control, and the social media call-out. The under[[deception|lying]] logic is unchanged: credit misattribution is a standing temptation, and exposure is the standing risk.
