## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], reward is never simple. The corpus treats reward as a test of perception: can the recipient tell the difference between genuine honor and disguised [[shame|disgrace]]? The fables share a structural pattern — someone performs an action, expects a reward, and receives something that [[strength|force]]s them to reconsider what they thought they knew. Reward is thus not a transaction but a revelation. It exposes the gap between how we see ourselves and how others see us.

## Varieties

**False Reward.** The Mischievous Dog ([[fable-004\|004-001]]) believes the bell is a decoration — a mark of his importance. In reality it is a warning to others. The dog's pride in his "reward" is the fable's [[ridicule|mockery]]: he prizes exactly what should shame him. Aesop draws no line between reward and punishment — they are distinguished only by the recipient's self-awareness.

**Merited Reward.** The [[truth|Truth]]-Teller in [[fable-044\|044-001]] receives a splendid reward from the king for reporting honestly. But this is the exception that proves the rule: reward works only when uncalculated. The Liar, seeing his companion rewarded, tries to replicate the formula and fails — because he approached reward as a strategy rather than a [[punishment|consequence]].

**Inverted Reward.** The Eagle who eats the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s fruit ([[fable-079\|079-001]]) is asked for gratitude but returns contempt: "those who enjoy my fruit should thus reward me with insults and blows." Here reward is subverted — the beneficiary refuses the obligation. The fable exposes reward as a social contract that one party can simply refuse to honor.

## Consequences

The fables consistently demonstrate that reward-seeking is self-defeating. The Dog who loves his bell becomes an object of ridicule; the Liar who chases reward receives nothing. Only the [[truth|Truth]]-Teller, who never calculated a return, is rewarded — and even then, his reward is accidental. Aesop's position is clear: **reward cannot be pursued d[[anger|ire]]ctly; it is a byproduct of action pursued for its own sake.** This places [[reward]] in d[[anger|ire]]ct opposition to [[greed]], which mistakes accumulation for desert.

## Relation to Gratitude

[[Reward]] and [[gratitude]] are mirror concepts in Aesop. [[gratitude|Gratitude]] is the proper response to receiving a benefit — it closes the loop. Reward is what the benefactor receives when the loop closes. The fables are filled with characters who demand reward but refuse [[gratitude]], severing the chain that makes social exchange possible. The Eagle in [[fable-079\|079-001]] enjoys the fruit as if it were entitled to it; the Farmer in [[fable-056\|056-001]] thanks the Earth rather than [[fortune|Fortune]]. In both cases, the refusal to acknowledge debt creates the very [[blame]] that the characters fear.
