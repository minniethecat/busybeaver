## Role in Aesop

The Traveller is one of the most frequently recurring human archetypes in Aesop's Fables. Unlike the Farmer, the Shepherd, or the Master — who are defined by their social roles — the Traveller is defined by motion and vulnerability.

On the road, stripped of the protections of home and status, the Traveller becomes a test subject for fortune. Aesop uses the travelling figure to explore themes of hospitality, boasting, the value of shade, and the capriciousness of luck. The road is a moral laboratory where character is revealed.

## Key Appearances

### The Bear and the Travellers

Two Travellers encounter a Bear. One climbs a tree and hides; the other, unable to escape, throws himself on the ground and plays dead. The Bear sniffs at him and departs, for bears will not touch a corpse. When the first Traveller descends and jokingly asks what the Bear whispered, his companion replies: "He told me never to trust a friend who deserts you in danger" (042-001).

### The Apes and the Two Travellers

Two Travellers, one truthful and one given to lying, find themselves in the land of the Apes. The Ape King, seated on his throne, asks each Traveller what they think of him and his court. The lying Traveller praises them extravagantly and is richly rewarded; the truthful one speaks plainly and is nearly torn to pieces (049-001).

### The Boasting Traveller

A Man returns from foreign travel and boasts that he once made an extraordinary leap in Rhodes, witnessed by many. A bystander replies: "If it is true, you don't need witnesses. Imagine this is Rhodes, and leap now." Deeds, not words, are the proof of ability (054-001).

### The Traveller and His Dog

A Traveller preparing for a journey finds his Dog stretching by the door. "Come, what are you yawning for? Hurry up!" The Dog replies: "I'm ready, master: it's you I'm waiting for." The lesson is that the impatient often blame others for delays they themselves cause (090-001).

### The Travellers and the Plane-tree

Two Travellers rest under a Plane-tree on a hot day. Looking up, they remark what a useless tree it is — it bears no fruit. The Plane-tree replies: "Ungrateful creatures! At the very moment you are benefiting from my shade, you call me useless." Some people are so thankless that they despise the very benefits they are enjoying (171-001).

## Character Profile

| Trait | Manifestation | Example Fable |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| Vulnerable | Exposed to dangers and dependent on companions | The Bear and the Travellers |
| Truthful-to-a-fault | Speaks honestly to the Ape King and suffers for it | The Apes and the Two Travellers |
| Boastful | Claims impossible feats that cannot be proven | The Boasting Traveller |
| Impatient | Blames his Dog for delay when he himself is unprepared | The Traveller and His Dog |
| Ungrateful | Enjoys the Plane-tree's shade while calling it useless | The Travellers and the Plane-tree |

## Relationship with Fortune

The Traveller's condition is fundamentally defined by his exposure to Fortune — the capricious force that governs roads, weather, and chance encounters. Unlike the Farmer who can prepare his fields or the Shepherd who knows his flock, the Traveller enters each new situation without reliable knowledge.

This vulnerability makes him an ideal vehicle for Aesop's lessons about preparedness, loyalty, and gratitude. The road strips away pretense and leaves only character.

## Symbolism

The Traveller symbolizes the human condition of moral testing through circumstance. The road is life itself — unpredictable, sometimes dangerous, and always revealing of who we truly are.

Each fable tests a different virtue: loyalty (the Bear), honesty (the Apes), humility (the boasting Traveller), patience (the Dog), and gratitude (the Plane-tree). The Traveller sometimes passes and sometimes fails, but the road never stops asking.
