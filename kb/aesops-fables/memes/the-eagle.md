## Role in Aesop

The Eagle occupies a unique position in the Aesopic cosmos: she is the creature to whom Fate itself has assigned **strength** as her defining gift. As Juno declares in her arbitration of the Peacock's complaint: "Fate has allotted to all their destined gifts: to yourself beauty, to the eagle strength, to the nightingale song, and so on to all the rest in their degree" (097-001). This divine distribution of natural endowments makes the Eagle the paradigm of **fixed, non-transferable power** — the Jackdaw who tries to imitate her seizing a lamb learns that one cannot acquire another creature's gifts by ambition alone (170-001).

Yet the Eagle's strength, for all its magnificence, repeatedly proves insufficient protection against forces she fails to anticipate. She is protected by [[jupiter|Jupiter]] himself, the special guardian of eagles, and yet she loses her eggs to a beetle's persistence (223-001), her fledglings to the fire her own greed kindled (250-001), and her life to an arrow fletched with eagle feathers (110-001). The Eagle in Aesop embodies a central paradox: she is the mightiest of birds, sovereign of the sky, endowed by the gods — and she is brought down, again and again, by what she cannot see, cannot imagine, and cannot control.

## Key Appearances

### The Eagle and the Fox (250-001)

An Eagle and a [[the-fox|Fox]] become neighbours — the Eagle nesting at the top of a high tree, the Fox in a thicket below. While the Fox is out foraging, the Eagle, needing food for her own young, swoops down, seizes the Fox's cubs, and feeds them to her family. The Fox returns and, powerless to reach the nest, sits and curses her betrayer:

> "When the Fox came back, and found out what had happened, she was not so much sorry for the loss of her cubs as furious because she couldn't get at the Eagle and pay her out for her treachery. So she sat down not far off and cursed her." (250-001)

Soon after, the Eagle steals a piece of burning flesh from a sacrificial altar. The wind catches her nest on fire, her half-roasted fledglings fall to the ground, and the Fox devours them in full sight of the Eagle. The moral — "False faith may escape human punishment, but cannot escape the divine" (250-002) — frames the Eagle's downfall as cosmic retribution. She assumed the Fox was her only possible enemy; she never considered that fire, wind, and the architecture of her own greed might be her undoing.

### The Eagle and the Beetle (223-001)

An Eagle chases a [[the-hare|Hare]], which begs a [[the-beetle|Beetle]] for protection. The Beetle warns the Eagle not to touch the Hare, but:

> "The Eagle never noticed the Beetle because it was so small, seized the hare and ate her up. The Beetle never forgot this, and used to keep an eye on the Eagle's nest, and whenever the Eagle laid an egg it climbed up and rolled it out of the nest and broke it." (223-001)

Desperate, the Eagle appeals to Jupiter, who lets her lay her eggs in his lap. But the Beetle makes a ball of dirt the size of an eagle's egg and deposits it there; Jupiter stands to shake it out, forgetting the real eggs, and they are broken too. The moral — "The weak will sometimes find ways to avenge an insult, even upon the strong" (223-002) — is one of Aesop's most sobering lessons about power. The Eagle's contempt for the insignificant proves fatal; the smallest creature, with sufficient memory and method, can humble even Jupiter's favourite.

### The Eagle and the Arrow (110-001)

An Eagle sits perched on a lofty rock, watching for prey. A huntsman shoots an arrow that pierces her through the breast:

> "As he lay in the agonies of death, he turned his eyes upon the Arrow. 'Ah! cruel fate!' he cried, 'that I should perish thus: but oh! fate more cruel still, that the Arrow which kills me should be winged with an Eagle's feathers!'" (110-001)

This is the Eagle's most tragic moment — destroyed not by a worthy adversary but by her own nature turned against itself. The arrow flies on eagle feathers; the instrument of death is made of the victim's own strength. [[fate|Fate]] in Aesop is defined partly through this fable: the boundary that circumscribes even the mightiest, and the cosmic irony that makes destruction self-referential.

### The Serpent and the Eagle (272-001)

An Eagle seizes a [[the-serpent|Serpent]], intending to devour it, but the Serpent coils around her in a life-and-death struggle. A Countryman frees the Eagle; in revenge, the Serpent spits poison into the man's drinking-horn. As the man is about to drink, the Eagle knocks the horn from his hand, spilling the poisoned contents and saving his life. The moral — "One good turn deserves another" (272-002) — shows the Eagle at her moral best: a creature capable of gratitude and reciprocal loyalty, repaying rescue with rescue.

### Other Appearances

In **The Eagle and His Captor** (091-001), a captured Eagle, his wings clipped and confined among barnyard fowls, has his wings restored by a new owner and brings a hare as a gift of thanks — though a Fox cynically advises him to appease his original captor instead. In **The Eagle, the Cat, and the Wild Sow** (135-001), a [[the-cat|Cat]] manipulates the Eagle and a Sow into mutual starvation by lying to each about the other's intentions. In **The Eagle and the Cocks** (144-001), the Eagle functions as an instrument of nemesis, swooping down to seize a crowing victor — "Pride comes before a fall" (144-002). In **The Tortoise and the Eagle** (081-001), the Eagle reluctantly grants a [[the-tortoise|Tortoise]]'s foolish wish to learn to fly, carrying him aloft and releasing him to his inevitable death — the Eagle as reluctant agent of natural law.

## Relationship with the Fox

The Eagle's most consequential relationship is with [[the-fox|the Fox]], which spans two opposing configurations. In **The Eagle and the Fox** (250-001), the Eagle is the treacherous friend who exploits a power asymmetry — she holds the sky, the Fox holds the earth — to murder her neighbour's children with impunity. The Fox cannot reach the Eagle's nest, and the Eagle believes this physical immunity grants her moral immunity as well. She is wrong: the cosmos intervenes, and the Fox devours the Eagle's fledglings as the Eagle once devoured the Fox's cubs. The punishment mirrors the crime with exact symmetry.

In **The Eagle and His Captor** (091-001), the Fox reappears as a cynical counsellor. After the Eagle brings a gift of thanks to the man who restored his wings, the Fox advises him to redirect his gratitude to his original captor: "make him your friend, and then perhaps he won't catch you and clip your wings a second time" (091-001). The Fox here is the voice of strategic self-interest, while the Eagle operates on honour. Between these two fables, the Eagle-Fox relationship maps the full spectrum from mortal enmity to unsolicited counsel — the Fox understands the Eagle better than the Eagle understands herself.

## Symbolism

The Eagle symbolizes **power without wisdom** — a creature whose physical supremacy is repeatedly circumvented by forces she cannot anticipate: a beetle's persistence, a cat's lies, the wind and fire, her own feathers on a hunter's shaft. She is protected by Jupiter and endowed with strength by Fate, yet this divine patronage becomes almost ironic: the gods favour her, but they cannot save her from herself.

At her best, the Eagle represents **grateful nobility** — the creature who repays rescue with rescue, who brings thanks-gifts to benefactors, who warns the foolish against impossible ambitions. This duality — predator and protector, betrayer and loyal ally — makes the Eagle one of Aesop's most complex characters: not merely a symbol of power but a sustained meditation on its limits.
