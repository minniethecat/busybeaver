## Statement

"Show gratitude where gratitude is due" is the explicit moral of The Farmer and [[fortune|Fortune]] (056-002). A Farmer, ploughing his field, turns up a pot of golden coins. Overjoyed, he makes daily [[sacrifice|offering]]s at the shrine of the Goddess of the Earth — until [[fortune|Fortune]] appears to rebuke him.

> Fortune was displeased at this, and came to him and said, "My man, why do you give Earth the credit for the gift which I bestowed upon you? You never thought of thanking me for your good luck; but should you be unlucky enough to lose what you have gained I know very well that I, Fortune, should then come in for all the blame." (056-001)

[[fortune|Fortune]]'s complaint is specific: **credit and blame are asymmetrically distributed**. Earth receives gratitude for the gain; [[fortune|Fortune]] would receive blame for the loss. Neither is correct — the Farmer owes thanks to the one who actually acted.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Farmer and Fortune (primary)

The fable is structured as a **misattribution problem**. The Farmer's reasoning is associative: the gold came from the earth, therefore Earth provided it. But [[fortune|Fortune]]'s agency was the cause; Earth was merely the medium. Aesop draws a distinction between **location of discovery** and **source of benefit** that applies broadly to [[fortune|luck]], inheritance, and unearned advantage.

> "You never thought of thanking me for your good luck; but should you be unlucky enough to lose what you have gained I know very well that I, Fortune, should then come in for all the blame." (056-001)

### The Fighting Cocks and the Eagle (supporting)

Two Cocks fight for dominance; the victor crows from a high perch, where an Eagle seizes him (028-001). The Cock who achieved victory through combat now suffers from the elevation that victory gave him. If he had thanked [[fortune|Fortune]] for his win rather than his own strength, the moral logic would be different — but his misattribution of success to his own [[strength|power]] sets up his fall.

### The Horse and the Stag (parallel)

A Horse, seeking revenge against a Stag, asks a Hunter for help. The Hunter agrees, mounts the Horse, and never dismounts — the Horse trades [[freedom|independence]] for [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]] (060-002). The Horse's error is the mirror of the Farmer's: the Farmer thanks the wrong benefactor; the Horse accepts aid from someone who becomes his master. Both fail to identify the **true source** of what they receive.

| Case | Agent | Attribution | Consequence |
|------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Farmer finds gold | [[fortune|Fortune]] | Thanks Earth | [[fortune|Fortune]]'s rebuke |
| Horse seeks revenge | Hunter | Sees ally | Permanent servitude |
| Victor Cock crows | Eagle | Sees triumph | Seized and eaten |

## Mechanism

The moral operates through an **attribution asymmetry**: people credit proximate or visible causes (the earth, the ally, the victory) and overlook the actual source of agency ([[fortune|Fortune]], the hidden master, the predator above).

Aesop identifies this as both a cognitive error and a moral failing. The Farmer is not merely mistaken but **unjust**, because his misattribution of gratitude imposes a symmetrical misattribution of blame.

The mechanism is structural, not emotional. [[gratitude|Gratitude]] in Aesop is not a warm feeling but a **transaction of accurate credit assignment**. To thank the wrong party is to create a debt relationship with someone who did not confer the benefit, while leaving the true benefactor unacknowledged — a double failure of reciprocity.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[ingratitude-brings-its-own-punishment]] — In that fable, ingratitude is active and punished: the Eagle who eats the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s cubs is repaid by the [[the-fox|Fox]] burning the Eagle's nest (173-002). "Show gratitude where due" addresses the subtler case of gratitude that is genuinely felt but wrongly aimed.

- [[service-met-with-ingratitude]] — The service that receives no thanks (166-002) is the benefactor's perspective on the same moral. The Farmer would be the subject of this moral from [[fortune|Fortune]]'s point of view: she provided a service and received no acknowledgment.

- [[one-good-turn-deserves-another]] — If gratitude is the correct response to a benefit, then recognizing the correct benefactor is prerequisite to the chain of reciprocal good turns. Misattributed gratitude breaks the chain at the first link.

- [[fortune]] — [[fortune|Fortune]] as a concept in Aesop is the personification of [[fortune|luck]]'s agency. The Farmer's error is failing to recognize [[fortune|Fortune]] as a legitimate actor with claims on human acknowledgment.
