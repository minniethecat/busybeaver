Persuasion in [[Aesop's Fables]] is speech aimed at changing another's mind or conduct. The corpus does not condemn persuasion as such — it condemns persuasion deployed in the service of self-interest at another's expense. The Sprat who tries to persuade warring Dolphins and Whales to make peace ([[030-001]]) is laughed at, but the [[the-fox|Fox]] who persuades his fellows to cut off their tails ([[083-002]]) is exposed as a manipulator. The difference is in the persuader's motive and the target's vulnerability.

## Definition

Aesopian persuasion is the use of words to produce action in another. Three dimensions determine its moral character: (1) **the persuader's stake** — does the persuader benefit from the action urged? (the [[the-fox|Fox]] at [[083-002]] does; the Sprat at [[030-001]] does not); (2) **the target's capacity** — can the target assess the argument? (the Crow is too vain to evaluate at [[009-001]]; the Grasshopper at [[205-001]] can and does); (3) **the truth of the appeal** — the Wolves' promise of freedom ([[184-001]]) is a lie; the Mouse's plea for [[pity|mercy]] ([[019-001]]) is honest.

## Varieties

**Honest Persuasion.** The Sprat ([[030-001]]) attempts persuasion with no personal stake — it gains nothing from peace between Dolphins and Whales. The persuasion fails, but not because it is dishonest; it fails because the Sprat lacks **standing**. The Dolphins would rather die than be lectured by a creature they consider beneath them. The fable's lesson is subtle: even honest persuasion requ[[anger|ire]]s that the persuader be taken seriously.

**Interested Persuasion.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] advocating tail-cutting ([[083-002]]) is the textbook case of persuasion that serves the persuader. He presents his argument in universal terms — tails are ugly, tails are heavy — but the old [[the-fox|Fox]] sees through it instantly. The fable's maxim could not be clearer: when a persuader stands to gain, test the argument twice.

**Predatory Persuasion.** The Wolves' offer to the Dogs ([[184-001]]) and the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s lure to the Stag ([[267-001]]) represent persuasion as a weapon. The Wolves frame servitude as intolerable and freedom as natural, knowing that their real goal is the flock. This is [[deception]] wearing the c[[fate|lot]]hes of argument — the most dangerous form because it sounds reasonable.

## Consequences

Persuasion in the fables succeeds in proportion to the target's weakness, not the argument's strength. The vain Crow ([[009-001]]) and ambitious Stag ([[267-001]]) are persuaded easily; the suspicious Grasshopper ([[205-001]]) is not. The honest Sprat ([[030-001]]) fails where the dishonest [[the-fox|Fox]] succeeds. The fables do not offer a comforting lesson — they show that persuasion is a tool, and tools serve the hands that wield them, not the truth. The only defense is the listener's own judgment.

## Relation to Flattery

[[Persuasion]] is the genus; [[flattery]] is the species. Both aim to move the target to action, but [[flattery]] does so exclusively through [[flattery|false praise]], while persuasion may use argument, appeal to interest, or appeal to emotion. [[the-fox|The Fox]] is the master of both: he flatters the Crow ([[009-001]]) and persuades the Stag ([[267-001]]), blending the two as circumstances requ[[anger|ire]]. The old [[the-fox|Fox]] who sees through the tail-cutting argument ([[083-002]]) is immune to [[flattery]] because he is immune to persuasion in any form — he judges arguments, not speakers.
