## Statement

"Look and see which way the wind blows before you commit yourself" is the closing moral of [[the-bat-and-the-weasels|The Bat and the Weasels]] (007-002).

A Bat, caught by a Weasel that hates all birds, pleads that it is not a bird but a mouse — and the Weasel releases it. Later, caught by a different Weasel that hates all mice, the Bat claims to be a bird — and is released again. The Bat survives not by being stronger or faster but by being more *accurate*: it reads each captor's prejudice and presents the identity that fits.

The moral is not about dishonesty. The Bat is not [[deception|lying]] — it *is* both mammal and winged creature. It is about **situational reading**: before you declare yourself, understand the audience you are declaring to.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Bat and the Weasels

The Bat falls and is caught — twice. Each time, the Weasel announces its extermination policy before attacking:

> A Bat fell to the ground and was caught by a Weasel, and was just going to be killed and eaten when it begged to be let go. The Weasel said he couldn't do that because he was an enemy of all birds on principle. "Oh, but," said the Bat, "I'm not a bird at all: I'm a mouse." "So you are," said the Weasel, "now I come to look at you"; and he let it go. (007-001)

The second encounter is a mirror:

> Some time after this the Bat was caught in just the same way by another Weasel, and, as before, begged for its life. "No," said the Weasel, "I never let a mouse go by any chance." "But I'm not a mouse," said the Bat; "I'm a bird." "Why, so you are," said the Weasel; and he too let the Bat go. (007-001)

> Look and see which way the wind blows before you commit yourself. (007-002)

The Bat's genius is in letting the Weasel declare first. Had the Bat announced "I am a bird" to the first Weasel, it would be dead. By listening before speaking, the Bat converts the Weasel's prejudice from a death sentence into a loophole.

### The Fox and the Lion

A [[the-fox|Fox]], encountering a [[the-lion|Lion]] for the first time, flees in [[fear|terror]]. The second time, he watches from a safe distance. The third time, he approaches and converses calmly (090-001). [[the-fox|The Fox]] learns the [[the-lion|Lion]] before acting — exactly what the Bat does with each Weasel. Situational reading requ[[anger|ire]]s exposure, not avoidance.

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

A Wolf dresses in a sheepskin and infiltrates the flock, devouring sheep at will — until the Shepherd, choosing a sheep for dinner, slaughters the disguised Wolf by accident (027-001). The Wolf reads the situation correctly (sheep are not killed by the Shepherd)

but fails to account for a second layer (the Shepherd kills *some* sheep). The Bat's success depends on reading *all* relevant layers of the situation.

### The Chameleon

Though not one of Aesop's own fables, the Chameleon's color-changing ability makes it the natural emblem of this moral. The Bat is Aesop's chameleon: it changes its declared identity to match the environment, not out of [[disguise|deception]] but out of survival.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **audience-first communication**: the Bat never speaks first. It lets each Weasel announce its extermination policy, then presents itself as whatever the Weasel does *not* exterminate. This is not [[deception|lying]]; it is strategic framing.

The Bat's strategy works because of three conditions:

| Condition | Bat's Case |
|-----------|------------|

| **Ambiguous identity** | The Bat is genuinely both mammal and bird — it has a menu of honest identities to choose from |

| **Declared criteria** | Each Weasel announces its rule before acting — the Bat never has to guess |

| **Commitment before disclosure** | The Weasel commits to "I kill all birds" *before* the Bat says "I am a mouse" — so releasing the Bat is consistent with the Weasel's stated principle |

If any condition fails — if the Bat had only one identity, if the Weasel killed without announcing, or if the Weasel killed indiscriminately — the Bat is dead. The moral is not "lie to survive" but "understand the rules before you play."

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[adaptability]] — The Bat's survival depends on adaptability: the capacity to present different aspects of a single identity in response to different [[danger|threat]]s. Adaptability is not [[deception|duplicity]]; it is range.

- [[cunning]] — The Bat is [[cleverness|cunning]], but [[cleverness|cunning]] in Aesop is often punished (the [[the-fox|Fox]] who betrays the Ass is eaten, 018-001). The Bat's [[cleverness|cunning]] is different — it harms no one.

It merely navigates.
- [[disguise]] — [[disguise|Disguise]] in Aesop is usually a trap (the Wolf in sheep's c[[fate|lot]]hing dies, 076-001; the Ass in the [[the-lion|lion]]'s skin is exposed, 126-001). The Bat's "disguise" is not a costume but a selection among true identities — closer to [[appearance]] management than [[disguise|deception]].

- [[fortune]] — [[fortune|Fortune]] is unpredictable; the Bat is the creature best adapted to [[fortune|Fortune]]'s variability. Reading the wind is the skill that survives [[fortune|Fortune]]'s turns.

## Enduring Influence

"Look which way the wind blows" has become an English idiom, though often with a pejorative connotation (imp[[deception|lying]] unprincipled opportunism).

The original Aesopic sense is more neutral and more useful: before you act, understand the [[strength|force]]s in play. In negotiation theory, the Bat's strategy is "reactive de-escalation" — let the other party state their position first,

then frame your offer within it. The fable is Perry Index 172. Its survival as both proverb and strategic principle testifies to the universality of being caught by someone who hates what you are — and discovering you can be something else.
