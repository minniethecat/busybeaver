## Statement

"It's no use trying to hide what can't be hidden" is the explicit moral of The G[[promise|oath]]erd and the Goat (210-003). A G[[promise|oath]]erd, frustrated that one of his goats refuses to return to the fold, throws a stone and breaks off one of her horns. Stricken with fear of his master's [[anger|wrath]], he begs the goat not to tell anyone what happened. The goat's reply is a masterpiece of compressed logic: "You silly fellow, my horn would cry aloud even if I held my tongue" (210-001). The goat is saying something more precise than "the evidence is obvious." She is saying that **the fact itself is the testimony** — the broken horn does not need her voice because the horn's absence is a louder announcement than any spoken [[blame|accusation]] could be. The G[[promise|oath]]erd's plea to the goat to "not tell" treats the goat's speech as the only channel through which the master could learn of the broken horn, ignoring the channel of d[[anger|ire]]ct visual evidence that requ[[anger|ire]]s no intermediary.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Goatherd and the Goat

The fable unfolds in two swift movements — the deed and its impossible [[disguise|concealment]]:

> A Goatherd was one day gathering his flock to return to the fold, when one of his goats strayed and refused to join the rest. He tried for a long time to get her to return by calling and whistling to her, but the Goat took no notice of him at all; so at last he threw a stone at her and broke one of her horns. In dismay, he begged her not to tell his master: but she replied, "You silly fellow, my horn would cry aloud even if I held my tongue." (210-001)

The G[[promise|oath]]erd's mental model of detection is **testimonial** — truth reaches the master only through intentional speech. The goat's reply replaces this with a **forensic** model — truth reaches the master through physical evidence that does not requ[[anger|ire]] anyone's cooperation. The broken horn is what lawyers call *res ipsa loquitur*: the thing speaks for itself.

### The Widow and the Sheep

A Widow, left alone with a single Sheep, shears it close to the skin each year. The Sheep, unable to bear the pain, complains: "Why do you cut me so close? My wool will not pay for the cost of my blood. If you want my flesh, call the butcher; if my wool, the shearer can shear me without wounding me" (fable-174). The Sheep's wounds are like the goat's broken horn: they speak a language that requ[[anger|ire]]s no translation. The Widow cannot persuade her neighbours that she is a gentle shepherdess when the Sheep bears the evidence of her cruelty on its body.

### The Butcher and His Customers

A Butcher, questioned by two customers about the weight of his meat, takes an [[promise|oath]] to confirm his honesty (251-001). The customers watch him swear — one about the fat, one about the suet — and the moral concludes: "Prevarication often amounts to perjury." The Butcher, like the G[[promise|oath]]erd, tries to manage testimony (his [[promise|oath]]) while ignoring the physical evidence (his scales). The customers, like the goat, can read the fact without help from the perpetrator.

### The Fox and the Stork

A [[the-fox|Fox]] invites a Stork to dinner and serves soup in a flat dish that the Stork cannot drink from (020-001). The Stork later serves the [[the-fox|Fox]] minced meat in a narrow-necked jar. Neither says a word about the insult — but the vessel itself is the [[blame|accusation]]. The broken horn and the narrow-necked jar speak in the same silent language of physical facts that requ[[anger|ire]] no verbal testimony.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **testimonial-channel myopia**: the cognitive error of believing that information about one's misdeeds can only travel through the intentional speech of witnesses. The G[[promise|oath]]erd's plea — "don't tell my master" — assumes that:

1. **Speech is the only channel**: The G[[promise|oath]]erd treats the goat as the sole conduit of information. If the goat stays silent, the master will never know. This is wrong not because the goat [[power|might]] speak, but because the broken horn is itself a channel that bypasses the goat's volition ent[[anger|ire]]ly.

2. **Silence equals [[disguise|concealment]]**: The G[[promise|oath]]erd conflates the absence of testimony with the absence of evidence. But the evidence (the broken horn) is permanently inscribed on the goat's body. It will be visible to the master the moment he looks at his flock.

3. **The witness can be controlled**: The G[[promise|oath]]erd treats the goat as a switch he can turn on or off — "please don't tell" implies that telling is a voluntary act the goat can choose not to perform. The goat's reply reveals that this model of communication is inadequate: the horn is telling the story already, continuously, to anyone with eyes.

The mechanism appears wherever people try to conceal facts that are inherently visible, audible, or otherwise detectable by means that bypass human testimony: the broken window, the empty vault, the deleted file that leaves a log entry, the bruise that cannot be explained away.

## Enduring Influence

The moral has passed into law through the Latin maxim *res ipsa loquitur* ("the thing speaks for itself") — the doctrine in tort law that some accidents are so obviously negligent that the mere fact of their occurrence constitutes evidence of [[blame|fault]]. A barrel that rolls out of a warehouse window and strikes a passer-by does not need a witness to explain that someone was careless; the barrel's trajectory *is* the testimony. The goat's broken horn is *res ipsa loquitur* in animal form. The fable also underlies the English proverb "murder will out" — the conviction that serious misdeeds carry within them the seeds of their own exposure. In forensic science, the principle is known as *Locard's exchange principle*: every contact leaves a trace. The G[[promise|oath]]erd's stone left a trace not on the ground but on the goat's head, and no plea for silence can erase what is already written in bone.
