## Statement

"Every man for himself" is the explicit moral of The Three Tradesmen (138-002). The citizens of a city debate what material to use for fortifications to be erected "for the greater [[safety|security]] of the town." A Carpenter rises and advises wood, which is "readily procurable and easily worked." A Stone-mason objects that wood is inflammable and recommends stone instead. Then a Tanner stands up and declares: "In my opinion there's nothing like leather" (138-001). Each man nominates the material of his own trade — not from malice or even conscious self-dealing, but because his professional experience makes that material the most salient. The moral is an observation about **structural bias**: what you know best is what you will advocate for, and what you advocate for will tend to align with what benefits you.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Three Tradesmen

The debate is elegantly compressed into a single paragraph:

> The citizens of a certain city were debating about the best material to use in the fortifications which were about to be erected for the greater security of the town. A Carpenter got up and advised the use of wood, which he said was readily procurable and easily worked. A Stone-mason objected to wood on the ground that it was so inflammable, and recommended stones instead. Then a Tanner got on his legs and said, "In my opinion there's nothing like leather." (138-001)

The fable's brevity is its genius. No one accuses anyone of corruption. The Carpenter, Stone-mason, and Tanner each make arguments that are *sincere and correct within their domain of expertise*. Wood is indeed easily worked; stone is indeed less inflammable; leather is indeed tough. The problem is not dishonesty — it is that each speaker's professional experience supplies him with the reasons that favour his own material while leaving him less equipped to evaluate the alternatives.

### The Lion and the Wild Ass

In [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Wild Ass (107-001), a [[the-lion|Lion]] and a Wild Ass hunt together. [[the-lion|The Lion]] divides the spoils into three portions: one for himself "as the first," a second "as the most cou[[anger|rage]]ous," and a third that he warns the Ass not to touch "unless you want to be killed." [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s division is nakedly self-serving, but it is presented under a veneer of principle. Both the Tradesmen and the [[the-lion|Lion]] exemplify the same mechanism: the rationalisation of self-interest into a framework that sounds objective.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

The Wolf, seeking a pretext to eat the Lamb (015-001), invents a series of escalating [[blame|accusation]]s — the Lamb muddying his water, insulting him the year before — each transparently false. The Wolf and the Tradesmen are cousins in self-justification: the Wolf fabricates reasons, while the Tradesmen choose from the inventory of reasons supplied by their own experience. Both arrive at the same destination: the conclusion that serves them best.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **domain-congruent reasoning bias**. Each tradesman's cognitive apparatus is shaped by his daily work — the carpenter sees the world through joinery, the stonemason through quarrying, the tanner through hides. When asked to reason about a problem outside any single trade (city defence), each de[[blame|fault]]s to the material he knows:

1. **Salience effect**: The carpenter does not *choose* to favour wood — wood simply presents itself to his mind as the obvious answer, because his brain has been trained by years of working with wood to see "material problem → wood solution" as natural.
2. **Evaluative asymmetry**: The stonemason can articulate the weaknesses of wood (inflammability) more readily than his companions can, because his own expertise includes comparing stone favourably to wood. The carpenter could, in theory, articulate stone's weaknesses — but his mind doesn't reach for them spontaneously.
3. **Collective failure**: If you assemble a group of domain experts, each biased toward their own domain, their collective output will be a stalemate of competing biases rather than a synthesis. The city's fortifications never get built in the fable — the debate is the whole story.

The fable is a compact demonstration of what modern psychology calls *motivated reasoning* stripped of any active dishonesty. No one lies. Everyone is sincere. And [[honesty|sincerity]], when pa[[anger|ire]]d with structurally biased cognition, produces exactly the same result as corruption would.

## Enduring Influence

"Every man for himself" has survived in English as a proverbial expression of the de[[blame|fault]] state of competitive self-interest — the condition that prevails when collective norms break down, as in the cry *sauve qui peut* ("save himself who can") on a sinking ship. The phrase appears in John Dryden's *The Spanish Friar* (1681): "Every man for himself, and God for us all." Adam Smith's invisible hand is the optimistic inversion of this fable's insight: Smith argues that self-interest, channelled through markets, produces collective good; the Three Tradesmen show that self-interest, channelled through public deliberation, produces only self-interest. The difference — markets vs. councils — is the difference between competition that aggregates into efficiency and competition that aggregates into paralysis. The fable also anticipates the modern concept of *regulatory capture*, in which industries [[persuasion|influence]] the rules that are meant to govern them, each advocating for standards that happen to align with their existing production capabilities.
