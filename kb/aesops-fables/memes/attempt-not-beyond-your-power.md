## Statement

"If you attempt what is beyond your [[strength|power]], your [[misfortune|trouble]] will be wasted and you court not only [[fortune|misfortune]] but ridicule" is the explicit moral of The Eagle, the Jackdaw, and the Shepherd (170-002). Unlike morals that warn against moral vices (greed, ingratitude, treachery), this moral targets a **capacity error**: the failure to correctly assess one's own abilities relative to a task. The Jackdaw does not act from malice or greed — it acts from a mistaken self-assessment. The moral's dual [[punishment|penalty]] ([[fortune|misfortune]] *and* ridicule) is distinctive: the overreacher is not merely punished by the world but becomes an object of [[ridicule|mockery]]. The Shepherd's verdict — "nothing but a Jackdaw: but it wants to be taken for an Eagle" (170-001) — is not just a physical capture but a social verdict that strips the Jackdaw of its pretension.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Eagle, the Jackdaw, and the Shepherd

A Jackdaw witnesses an Eagle seizing a lamb and resolves to do the same. It flies high, swoops down, and lands on a ram — only to find its claws hopelessly tangled in the wool:

> "It had no sooner alighted than its claws got caught fast in the wool, and nothing it could do was of any use: there it stuck, flapping away, and only making things worse instead of better." (170-001)

The Shepherd captures it, clips its wings, and brings it home to his children. When they ask what kind of bird it is, he delivers the fable's devastating closing line:

> "It's a Jackdaw," he replied, "and nothing but a Jackdaw: but it wants to be taken for an Eagle." (170-001)

The Jackdaw's punishment is not merely defeat but **public exposure**: it is rendered flightless, displayed to children, and identified precisely as the thing it tried not to be. The ridicule is worse than the physical harm.

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin

An Ass finds a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s skin and wears it, terrifying all who encounter him — until he opens his mouth and brays. The disguise works only so long as the Ass remains silent; the moment he speaks, his true nature is revealed. Both the Jackdaw and the Ass attempt to pass as something greater than themselves. The Jackdaw's failure is one of capacity (its claws cannot hold a ram); the Ass's failure is one of essence (its voice cannot be disguised). Both fables converge on the same insight: pretending to be what you are not is not merely unsuccessful — it makes you ridiculous.

### The Frog and the Ox

A Frog, jealous of an Ox's size, inflates itself to match the Ox's bulk — and bursts. The Frog, like the Jackdaw, attempts what is beyond its [[strength|power]]. But the Frog's failure is one of **scale** (trying to match physical magnitude), while the Jackdaw's is one of **kind** (trying to be an Eagle when it is a Jackdaw). The Frog is destroyed; the Jackdaw is humiliated. Both fables together map the spectrum of overreaching: from physical impossibility to social exposure.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **capacity-exposure**: attempting a task beyond one's [[strength|power]] does not stay private — it inevitably exposes the attempt and the attempter to a public that judges both. Three structural features are at work:

1. **The imitation gap**: The Jackdaw sees the Eagle's success (seizing a lamb) but fails to see the Eagle's capacity (talons built for gripping prey, not tangling in wool). The observable outcome conceals the enabling infrastructure.
2. **The self-revealing failure**: The Jackdaw's failure is not a quiet collapse but a **spectacle** — flapping, stuck, making things worse. Overreaching does not merely fail; it fails visibly and noisily, attracting the very audience whose judgment constitutes the ridicule.
3. **The verdict from outside**: The Shepherd names the Jackdaw precisely — "nothing but a Jackdaw: but it wants to be taken for an Eagle." The moral is not self-recognised (contrast [[ingratitude-brings-its-own-punishment]], where the Stag says "I deserve my [[fortune|fate]]"). It is pronounced by an external judge, making the punishment social as well as physical.

## Enduring Influence

The fable coins a durable social diagnosis: the person who "wants to be taken for" something they are not. The phrase captures a specific kind of social exposure — not the exposure of a liar (who knows he is [[deception|lying]]) but of a self-deceiver (who believes his own pretension). The Jackdaw's [[fortune|fate]] — being brought home as a pet, flightless, displayed as a curiosity — is the fable's most chilling detail: the overreacher survives, but survives as a diminished thing, stripped of the very agency (flight) that made the original ambition thinkable.
