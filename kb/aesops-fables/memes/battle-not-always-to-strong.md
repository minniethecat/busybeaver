## Statement

"The battle is not always to the strong" is the explicit moral of The Mouse and the Bull (139-002). A Bull, en[[anger|rage]]d after a Mouse bites his nose, charges the Mouse's hole in the wall until he collapses from exhaustion. When all is quiet, the Mouse darts out and bites him again. From inside his hole, the Mouse delivers the verdict: "You big fellows don't always have it your own way, you see: sometimes we little ones come off best." [[strength|Strength]] without reach is impotence; weakness with sanctuary is invincibility.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Mouse and the Bull

A Bull is bitten on the nose by a Mouse. The Mouse darts into a hole in the wall. The Bull charges the wall "furiously again and again" until he collapses from exhaustion. When the Bull lies still, the Mouse emerges and bites him once more. As the Bull [[anger|rage]]s helplessly, a small voice comes from within the wall:

> "You big fellows don't always have it your own way, you see: sometimes we little ones come off best." (139-001)

The Bull's superior strength is perfectly real — but it cannot reach the Mouse. The wall transforms the contest: the Mouse is not [[quarrel|fighting]] the Bull; the Mouse is letting the Bull fight the wall. The Bull's strength, turned against an immovable object, becomes the instrument of his own defeat.

### The Lion and the Mouse

A [[the-lion|Lion]] catches a Mouse who pleads for his life, promising future help. [[the-lion|The Lion]], amused, releases him. Later, the [[the-lion|Lion]] is caught in a hunter's net. The Mouse gnaws through the ropes and frees him (059-001). The Mouse's strength is not in combat but in access — he can reach the ropes that the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[strength|power]] cannot touch. This fable and the Bull fable form a complementary pair: the Mouse can hurt the strong (by biting the Bull) and save the strong (by gnawing the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s bonds).

### The Hare and the Tortoise

A Hare, confident in his superior speed, takes a nap mid-race and wakes to find the Tortoise has already won (117-001). [[strength|Strength]] here is not physical [[strength|force]] but natural speed — and it fails for the same reason the Bull's strength fails: the strong party assumes the contest will be decided on his terms and neglects the terms that actually decide it. The Tortoise, like the Mouse, wins by playing a different game than his opponent.

### The Oak and the Reed

An Oak, proud of its strength, [[contempt|scorn]]s a Reed for bending before the [[insult|slight]]est breeze. When a great storm comes, the Oak stands rigid and is uprooted; the Reed bends and survives (086-001). The Oak, like the Bull, believes that strength means refusing to yield — and loses because of it. The Reed and the Mouse share the same strategy: yield to avoid [[ruin|destruction]], then return when the [[strength|force]] is spent.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **asymmetric engagement**: the weak prevail not by matching the strong on the strong's terms, but by changing the terms of the contest. The Mouse does not fight the Bull; the Mouse makes the Bull fight himself. The strategy has three components:

1. **Sanctuary**: The Mouse operates from a position the Bull cannot physically reach (the hole in the wall).
2. **Provocation**: The Mouse delivers small, precise bites that cost the Bull his composure.
3. **Exhaustion**: The Bull expends his own strength against an immovable obstacle (the wall) while the Mouse waits.

The moral generalizes beyond physical contest: any party that can choose the terrain, control the pace, and [[strength|force]] the opponent to exhaust himself [[quarrel|fighting]] the environment has a structural advantage that raw [[strength|power]] cannot overcome.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race]] — Both morals invert the expected hierarchy of advantage. The Tortoise's steady [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race|persistence]] and the Mouse's strategic sanctuary both defeat superior natural gifts. Persistence and position matter more than speed or strength.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Bull's [[anger|rage]] is a form of pride: the assumption that his strength entitles him to victory, and the refusal to accept that a smaller creature could [[danger|threat]]en him. His collapse is pride's fall.
- [[example-is-better-than-precept]] — The Mouse does not argue with the Bull about theory; he demonstrates the truth by repeated action. The Bull learns not from being told "the battle is not always to the strong" but from experiencing it.

## Enduring Influence

"The battle is not always to the strong" has passed into English as a proverb, rein[[strength|force]]d by the Biblical echo in Ecclesiastes 9:11 ("the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong"). The fable is Perry Index number 353. The strategic insight — that asymmetric warfare [[kindness|favor]]s the party that can choose the terrain — has been rediscovered in military theory (guerrilla warfare), game theory (the weaker player's advantage in repeated games), and organizational behavior (the small competitor who redefines the market rather than competing on the incumbent's terms). The Mouse's taunt from the safety of his hole is the soul of asymmetric strategy: you cannot win if you cannot be reached.
