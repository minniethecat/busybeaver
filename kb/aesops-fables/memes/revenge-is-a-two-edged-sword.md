## Statement

"[[revenge|Revenge]] is a two-edged sword" is the closing moral of [[the-farmer-and-the-fox|The Farmer and the Fox]] (146-002).

A Farmer, plagued by a [[the-fox|Fox]] that raids his henhouse, traps the [[the-fox|Fox]] and devises a spectacular revenge: he ties burning tow to the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s tail and releases it. The terrified [[the-fox|Fox]] bolts straight into the Farmer's own wheat field, which stands ripe and dry. The flames spread. The Farmer loses his ent[[anger|ire]] harvest — a punishment far exceeding the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s original depredations.

The moral is not that revenge is morally wrong. It is that revenge is **structurally dangerous**: once you set a destructive [[strength|force]] in motion, you cannot control where it goes. [[the-fox|The Fox]] does not target the wheat — the [[the-fox|Fox]] simply runs. But a burning animal running through dry wheat does not need to *intend* [[ruin|destruction]] for [[ruin|destruction]] to occur.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Farmer and the Fox

> A Farmer was greatly annoyed by a Fox, which came prowling about his yard at night and carried off his fowls. So he set a trap for him and caught him; and in order to be revenged upon him, he tied a bunch of tow to his tail and set fire to it and let him go. (146-001)

The Farmer's revenge is theatrical: he does not simply kill the [[the-fox|Fox]], which would solve the problem. He stages a punishment — f[[anger|ire]], flight, [[fear|terror]] — that transforms the [[the-fox|Fox]] from pest to projectile. But a projectile cannot be steered:

> As ill-luck would have it, however, the Fox made straight for the fields where the corn was standing ripe and ready for cutting. It quickly caught fire and was all burnt up, and the Farmer lost all his harvest. (146-001)

> Revenge is a two-edged sword. (146-002)

### The Horse and the Stag

A Horse, seeking revenge against a Stag that has wronged him, allows a Man to put a bridle on him and ride him into battle. The Horse defeats the Stag — and finds himself permanently enslaved to the Man (075-001). [[revenge|Revenge]] here does not burn a field; it burns freedom. The Horse achieves his goal, but the price is permanent servitude.

### The Eagle and the Fox

An Eagle, nesting high in a tree, steals a [[the-fox|Fox]]'s cubs and feeds them to her young. [[the-fox|The Fox]] cannot climb to reach the nest. But when the Eagle next steals a burning sacrifice from an altar and drops it onto her own nest, the flames consume the eaglets (013-001).

[[the-fox|The Fox]] does not take revenge d[[anger|ire]]ctly; the Eagle's own greed becomes the agent of her [[ruin|destruction]]. But the principle is the same: a destructive act set in motion returns to its origin.

### The Serpent and the File

A Serpent, finding a File in a smithy, bites it repeatedly. The File does not bleed or flinch; it is steel. The Serpent's revenge accomplishes nothing except to wear down its own fangs (115-001). This is the other edge of the revenge sword: sometimes the target is impervious, and the only damage is to the avenger.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **boomerang escalation**: a destructive act, once released, operates under its own logic and cannot be recalled. The Farmer's f[[anger|ire]] does not ask whether the wheat is the Farmer's or the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s — it burns whatever is dry. Three features make boomerang escalation unavoidable in revenge scenarios:

| Feature | Farmer's Case |
|---------|---------------|
| **Loss of control** | The Farmer releases a burning [[the-fox|Fox]]; the [[the-fox|Fox]] decides where to run |
| **Asymmetric stakes** | The Farmer risks his ent[[anger|ire]] harvest to punish a few stolen fowls |
| **Irreversibility** | F[[anger|ire]] cannot be un-set; the harvest cannot be un-burned |

The Farmer's error is not in *wanting* revenge but in designing a revenge that outsources its execution to an uncontrollable agent — a terrified animal on f[[anger|ire]].

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[injuries-never-forgotten]] — "Injuries may be forgiven, but not forgotten" . [[revenge|Revenge]] is the act of not forgetting. But the Farmer, by not forgetting a few stolen fowls, loses everything.
- [[contempt]] — [[contempt|Contempt]] is often the emotional fuel of revenge. The Farmer does not just want to stop the [[the-fox|Fox]]; he wants to humiliate it. [[contempt|Contempt]] makes revenge theatrical,

and theatrical revenge is the most dangerous kind.
- [[betray-friend-ruin-yourself]] — [[the-fox|The Fox]] who betrays the Ass to the [[the-lion|Lion]] is eaten first (018-002). The betrayer, like the avenger, sets a destructive [[strength|force]] in motion and discovers it does not discriminate.
- [[foolishness]] — [[revenge|Revenge]] that destroys the avenger is a species of [[foolishness]]: the failure to calculate [[punishment|consequence]]s before acting.

## Enduring Influence

"[[revenge|Revenge]] is a two-edged sword" has joined the English lexicon as a caution against retaliatory escalation. The image of the burning [[the-fox|Fox]] in the wheat field — [[ruin|destruction]] returning to its sender — appears in contexts from international relations (the "revenge spiral" in tit-for-tat sanctions)

to workplace [[quarrel|conflict]]. The moral's durability lies in its structural truth: any revenge that releases an uncontrolled [[strength|force]] risks becoming self-revenge.
