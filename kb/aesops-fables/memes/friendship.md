## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **friendship** is not a sentimental bond but a practical alliance subjected to the hardest test: [[misfortune|adversity]]. Aesop treats friendship as a provisional state — declared in peace, proven in crisis, and often found wanting. "[[misfortune|Misfortune]] tests the [[honesty|sincerity]] of friendship" (037-002) functions as the corpus's definitive moral on the subject. Unlike modern romanticized friendship, Aesop's friendship is a contract of mutual obligation; when one party breaks that contract, the relationship is revealed as counterfeit.

## Varieties

Aesop presents friendship in three distinct modes:

1. **False Friendship** (fable-037): The Traveller who climbs the tree leaves his companion to face the Bear alone. When the coast clears, he returns with feigned concern. The survivor's reply — that the Bear "told me never again to travel with a friend who deserts you at the first sign of danger" (037-001) — exposes the hollowness of fair-weather fellowship.

2. **Ill-Matched Friendship** (fable-067): The Mouse and Frog are sincere in their bond — they literally tie themselves together. But their natures are incompatible: the Frog's aquatic impulse drowns the land-bound Mouse, and the tether drags the Frog to the Hawk's talons. Friendship without compatibility of circumstance is self-destructive.

3. **Universal Friendship as Ideal** (fable-181): Under the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s just reign, predator and prey "dwell side by side in unbroken peace and friendship" (181-001). This is Aesop's vision of friendship at societal scale — only possible under a ruler who suppresses natural predation through law.

## Consequences

> He told me never again to travel with a friend who deserts you at the first sign of danger. (037-001)

> The Frog was unable to loose the knot which bound him to the Mouse, and thus was carried off along with him and eaten by the Hawk. (067-001)

The [[punishment|consequence]]s in Aesop are stark. False friendship leads to renunciation — the betrayed survivor walks away wiser. Ill-matched friendship leads to mutual [[ruin|destruction]] — the Frog's well-intentioned bond kills both parties. Only the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s artificial peace (fable-181) offers a happy outcome, and even that is contingent on the ruler's continued justice.

## Relation to Loyalty

Friendship and loyalty are inseparably linked in Aesop. The false friend in fable-037 is disloyal at the first test; the Mouse and Frog in fable-067 are loyal to the point of self-[[ruin|destruction]]. Aesop thus draws a distinction: loyalty to an ill-chosen friend is as foolish as disloyalty to a true one. The implicit standard is the Traveller's lesson — a true friend is one who stays when danger appears.
