## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], contempt is the error of the high looking down. It differs from [[anger]] (which is reactive) and [[pride]] (which is self-referential) — contempt is **d[[anger|ire]]cted outward**, a verdict delivered against another creature's worth. The corpus is ruthless toward the contemptuous. Every character who speaks or acts from contempt is defeated, and the agent of their defeat is invariably the creature they held in contempt. Contempt in Aesop is not merely a vice — it is a **prediction error** that the p[[fate|lot]] reliably punishes.

## Varieties

**Contempt of Size.** The most common pattern in the corpus: a large or [[strength|power]]ful creature dismisses a small one as beneath consideration. The Fir-tree mocks the Bramble's uselessness ([[fable-033\|033-001]]); the Hawk eyes the Nightingale "with some contempt" ([[fable-232\|232-001]]); the [[the-lion|Lion]] dismisses the Gnat as a "contemptible insect" ([[fable-162\|162-003]]). In every case, the small creature proves lethal. The Fir-tree is cut down by the very axes it boasts about providing; the Gnat stings the [[the-lion|Lion]] until he tears himself bloody. Aesop's pattern is invariant: **size is not strength, and contempt is not accuracy.**

**Contempt as Exclusion.** The Dolphin in [[fable-030\|030-001]] refuses to carry the Monkey back to shore because the Monkey had lied about being Athenian. The Dolphin's contempt is social — the liar is beneath the comm[[harmony|unity]] of truth-tellers. This is the one form of contempt the corpus treats as justified, though even here it comes at a cost: "we would rather go on our way with a liar than turn back with you."

**Contempt [[disguise|Disguise]]d as [[pity|Pity]].** The servant in [[fable-115\|115-001]] replies to the Ass "in a tone of [[mercy|pity]]ing contempt: 'What a simpleton you must be.'" This is contempt's most insidious form — it wears the mask of kindness. The Ass, who merely wanted affection, is doubly humiliated: first rejected, then pitied for wanting what was never available to him.

## Consequences

Contempt consistently produces the opposite outcome from what the contemptuous character expects. The Fir-tree who scorns the Bramble is cut down while the Bramble survives. [[the-lion|The Lion]] who scorns the Gnat is tormented to exhaustion. The Hawk who scorns the Nightingale misses a meal. The pattern is so consistent that it functions as a **law of the fable universe**: contempt is a guarantee of reversal. The mechanism is simple — contempt prevents the contemptuous from perceiving the real [[danger|threat]], so they take no precautions. The Bramble they mocked is the one they grab when falling; the Gnat they dismissed is the one who defeats them.

## Relation to Wisdom

[[Contempt]] is, in Aesop's taxonomy, the precise opposite of [[wisdom]]. [[Wisdom]] is the capacity to perceive accurately, without the distortions of vanity or hierarchy. Contempt is the refusal to perceive at all — it substitutes a verdict for an observation. The Fir-tree does not *look at* the Bramble; it classifies the Bramble and discards it. The [[wisdom|wise]] character, by contrast, sees what is there, [[respect|regard]]less of size or status. The Mouse who defeats the Bull ([[fable-139\|139-001]]) does so not through [[contempt]] but through clear-eyed assessment of the Bull's weakness. That is the Aesopian distinction that matters: [[wisdom]] sees, contempt refuses to look.
