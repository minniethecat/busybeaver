## Statement

"One good turn deserves another" is the explicit moral of The Eagle and the Serpent (272-002). An Eagle seizes a Serpent but the Serpent coils around him, and the two are locked in a death struggle. A Countryman intervenes and frees the Eagle. Later, the Serpent, seeking revenge, spits poison into the Countryman's drinking-horn. As the Countryman is about to drink, the Eagle swoops down and knocks the horn from his hand, spilling the poisoned water on the ground. The Eagle repays the Countryman's rescue with a rescue of his own. The moral is a statement about the **structure of obligation**: a good turn creates a debt, and the honorable party pays it.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Eagle and the Serpent

An Eagle catches a Serpent and tries to carry it off. The Serpent coils around the Eagle, and the two are locked in combat. A Countryman witnesses the struggle and helps the Eagle escape (272-001). The Serpent, en[[anger|rage]]d, spits poison into the man's drinking-horn. As the heated Countryman raises the horn to drink:

> The Eagle knocked it out of his hand, and spilled its contents upon the ground. (272-001)

The Eagle's repayment is exact: the Countryman saved the Eagle from death by Serpent; the Eagle saves the Countryman from death by Serpent's poison. The symmetry is the mechanism: a good turn creates an obligation, and the Eagle is not too proud to discharge it.

### The Slave and the Lion (Androcles)

A runaway Slave removes a thorn from a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s paw. Later, the Slave is captured and thrown to the beasts in the arena — and the [[the-lion|Lion]] he is thrown to is the very one he helped. Instead of attacking, the [[the-lion|Lion]] fawns at the Slave's feet (038-001). [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s repayment is a mirror of the Eagle's: a life saved is a life owed. [[gratitude|Gratitude]] operates across species and across time, and the debt does not diminish with distance.

### The Fowler and the Ringdove

A Fowler catches a Ringdove, who pleads for his life and promises to lure other doves into the Fowler's nets in exchange for his freedom (056-001). The Fowler replies with the moral: "Show gratitude where gratitude is due" — and kills the Ringdove anyway, because the Ringdove's "gratitude" takes the form of betraying his own kind. This fable illustrates the negative case: **a good turn that is repaid with treachery is not gratitude but a second crime**.

### The Ox and the Heifer

An Ox, straining at the plow, advises a Heifer who has never worked that the yoke is not so bad. When festival day comes and the Heifer is chosen for sacrifice while the Ox is spared, the Ox says: "You were spared the yoke only to be brought to the altar" (028-001). This fable is a darker version of "one good turn deserves another" — the Ox's labor, which seemed like suffering, proves to be his salvation. The Ox's "good turn" of pulling the plow earns him a life the Heifer's ease cannot buy.

## Mechanism

The moral describes **reciprocal altruism**: a good deed creates a social debt that the beneficiary is obligated to repay. The mechanism has three components:

1. **Debt recognition**: The beneficiary must remember the good turn and feel the weight of the obligation. The Eagle, unlike the Ringdove, recognizes his debt and acts on it.
2. **Delayed repayment**: The debt may be held for a long time before repayment is possible. Androcles' [[the-lion|Lion]] waits until the arena; the Eagle waits until the poisoned horn.
3. **Asymmetric capacity**: The repayment may take a different form than the original good turn. The Countryman used his hands to free the Eagle; the Eagle uses his wings and beak to knock away the horn. The form differs but the value is preserved.

This mechanism contrasts with [[gratitude]], the internal feeling. "One good turn deserves another" is gratitude **in action**, not just in sentiment.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[gratitude]] — The emotional companion to this moral. [[gratitude|Gratitude]] is the internal state; one good turn deserves another is its external expression. The Eagle feels gratitude but, more importantly, acts on it.
- [[kindness]] — [[kindness|Kindness]] is the initiating act — the Countryman's intervention, the Slave's removal of the thorn. "One good turn deserves another" is the closing act that completes the circuit that kindness opens.
- [[mercy]] — The Slave shows [[pity|mercy]] to the [[the-lion|Lion]] by removing the thorn; the [[the-lion|Lion]] returns [[pity|mercy]] by [[mercy|sparing]] the Slave. The two morals are interlocking: [[pity|mercy]] initiates, reciprocity completes.
- [[friendship]] — Reciprocal good turns are the foundation of friendship in Aesop's world. [[friendship|Friendship]] without reciprocity is merely patronage.

## Enduring Influence

"One good turn deserves another" is one of the most durable Aesopic proverbs in English, listed in the Perry Index as fable 573. The moral underlies the anthropological concept of **reciprocal altruism** and the economic principle of **gift exchange** — the obligation to reciprocate is a universal human norm studied by Marcel Mauss and subsequent anthropologists. The Eagle's swoop is the original "pay it forward."
