## Statement

"Greatness carries its own penalties" is the explicit moral of The Mice and the Weasels (096-002). The Mice, losing their war with the Weasels, appoint leaders distinguished by large plumed helmets. In the next battle they are defeated again — but this time only the leaders die, because their badges of rank trap them at the entrance to their holes.

> All made their way to safety without difficulty except the leaders, who were so hampered by the badges of their rank that they could not get into their holes, and fell easy victims to their pursuers. (096-001)

The moral is not that leadership is wrong but that its **visible markers** create asymmetric vulnerability. In normal times the helmets confer [[power|authority]] and distinction; in catastrophe they become the mechanism of [[ruin|destruction]].

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Mice and the Weasels (primary)

This is the sole fable from which the moral is drawn, but its structure is generalizable. A council of war identifies the problem: "we have no generals to plan our battles" (096-001). The solution — promotion with visible insignia — solves one problem while creating another. The leaders' helmets make them visible to the enemy and, more fatally, too wide to enter the mouse-holes.

> It's no wonder we are always beaten, for we have no generals to plan our battles and direct our movements in the field. (096-001)

The irony is complete: the generals were chosen to reduce casualties, and their distinction increased them — but only for the generals themselves.

### The Stag at the Pool (supporting)

The Stag adm[[anger|ire]]s his antlers and despises his legs, but when the [[the-lion|Lion]] attacks, his legs carry him to safety while his antlers catch in the branches and [[fate|doom]] him (095-001). The structural parallel is exact: what is [[reward|prize]]d — antlers, plumed helmets — becomes the instrument of death.

| Fable | Displayed Trait | Instrument of Death | Escape Mechanism |
|-------|----------------|---------------------|------------------|
| The Mice and the Weasels | Plumed helmets | Badges block escape holes | Ordinary mice flee freely |
| The Stag at the Pool | Spreading antlers | Antlers catch in branches | Swift legs |

### The Peacock and Juno (parallel)

The Peacock complains to Juno that he lacks the nightingale's song. Juno replies that each creature receives its al[[fate|lot]]ted gift — beauty, strength, song — and discontent with one's portion brings only fresh [[discontent|dissatisfaction]] (097-001). The Peacock is not destroyed by his beauty as the Mice generals are by their helmets, but the mechanism is identical: **the gift is also the constraint**.

## Mechanism

Aesop's moral operates through a **visibility paradox**: the same marker that signals [[power|authority]] in peacetime becomes a targeting mechanism in crisis. The plumed helmets are not inherently dangerous — they are dangerous because they make the wearer distinguishable, and distinguishability is fatal when the group flees.

This mechanism is structural, not punitive. The Weasels do not deliberately target the generals; they simply catch whoever is slowest. The helmets make the generals slowest **by geometry**, not by moral judgment. Greatness does not attract punishment — it impedes escape.

Compare [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]], which is moral and psychological: the proud are humbled because their [[pride|arrogance]] blinds them. "Greatness carries its own penalties" is mechanical and spatial: the distinguished are killed because their distinction has **physical dimensions**.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Peacock's discontent (097-001) is the pride case: [[discontent|dissatisfaction]] with one's gifts is a form of pride. The Mice generals do not display pride — they are simply promoted — so the moral warnings are adjacent but distinct.

- [[be-content-with-your-lot]] — Juno's reply to the Peacock is the contentment moral in divine voice: "[[fate|Fate]] has al[[fate|lot]]ted to all their destined gifts." The Mice fable adds the hard corollary: not only should you be [[be-content-with-your-lot|content with your lot]], but elevation above it carries hidden cost.

- [[battle-not-always-to-strong]] — The Mice were beaten by the Weasels despite their generals, demonstrating that organization does not guarantee victory. Both morals undercut the assumption that visible strength predicts outcomes.

- [[vanity]] — The Stag's antlers are adm[[anger|ire]]d for their beauty; the Dog's bell is mistaken for a mark of honor. When the marker of distinction is confused with a reward, the error is vanity.
