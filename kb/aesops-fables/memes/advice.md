## Definition

[[Advice]] in [[Aesop's Fables]] is never neutral. It carries the weight of the giver's intentions and demands the discernment of the receiver. Aesop treats advice as a moral test that cuts both ways: the giver reveals their character through the counsel they offer, and the receiver reveals theirs through the counsel they heed or reject.

The fables establish a clear hierarchy: the best advice is ignored, the worst advice is followed, and the rarest advice is both wise and accepted. The Owl who warns of the tiny acorn — a [[danger|threat]] invisible to the unwise — is the paradigmatic good advisor: prescient, disinterested, and unheeded (060-002). The man who shouts advice from the riverbank while the drowning boy struggles is the paradigmatic bad advisor: well-intentioned but useless, mistaking words for deeds.

> "Give assistance, not advice, in a crisis." (064-002)

> Some Birds consulted the Owl about the acorn that had just begun to sprout. "If you take my advice," said the Owl, "you will destroy it now when it is small. For once it has grown into a tree, it will produce birdlime, and you will all be caught." But the Birds laughed at the Owl's fears. The acorn grew into a mighty oak, birdlime was made from its sap, and the Birds, caught in snares, remembered the Owl's warning too late. (060-002)

## Varieties of Advice

Aesop's taxonomy of advice is as much about motive as about content:

- **Wise but rejected advice** — the Owl's warning about the acorn is the archetype. The advice is genuinely prescient, offered without self-interest, and rejected precisely because the [[danger|threat]] it names is not yet visible. Aesop's irony is sharp: the smaller the present evidence, the more valuable the warning, and the more likely it is to be dismissed (060-002).
- **Self-serving advice** — the [[the-fox|Fox]] in the well advises the Goat to jump down and join him, praising the water's sweetness. The advice is superficially plausible but serves only the advisor. Aesop's warning is clear: always ask *why* someone is giving you advice before asking *what* the advice contains (176-001). Similarly, the [[the-fox|Fox]] advises the [[quarrel|fighting]] [[the-lion|Lion]] and Bear to "come and see" their [[reward|prize]] — and takes it himself (267-001).
- **Useless advice** — the man on the riverbank who lectures the drowning boy instead of reaching out his hand. Aesop's verdict is unambiguous and compressed into a single line: "Give assistance, not advice, in a crisis." The word "assistance" is an action; "advice" in this context is merely noise (064-002).
- **Impossible advice** — the Mice in Council unanimously agree to bell the Cat. The advice is excellent in theory and impossible in practice. Aesop draws a line between the wisdom of a plan and the wisdom of its execution — the two are not the same thing (096-001).

## Consequences

Advice in Aesop has three possible outcomes, each carrying its own lesson:

- **Good advice rejected** leads to preventable disaster. The Owl's warning comes true because the Birds could not imagine a [[danger|threat]] so small growing so large. The moral is not about the advisor's failure but the receiver's failure of imagination (060-002).
- **Bad advice accepted** leads to entrapment. The Goat, persuaded by the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s plausible recommendation, finds himself trapped in the well. The receiver bears responsibility for failing to discern the gap between the advisor's words and the advisor's situation (176-001).
- **Good advice accepted** is so rare in Aesop that its examples are nearly invisible. When it does occur — the Mice who actually escape through small holes to safety — the advice is not verbal at all but structural, embedded in the design of the world (096-001).

## Related Concepts

- [[wisdom]] — the quality that enables one to give good advice and to recognize it in others; the Owl possesses wisdom but not persuasiveness
- [[deception]] — the category into which self-serving advice falls; the [[the-fox|Fox]] in the well is not giving advice at all but laying a trap
- [[trust]] — the bond that advice exploits or strengthens; good advice builds trust, bad advice exploits it
- [[folly]] — the state of rejecting good advice or accepting bad; the Goat and the Birds are both fools in different ways
- [[danger]] — what good advice protects against; what bad advice delivers the receiver into
