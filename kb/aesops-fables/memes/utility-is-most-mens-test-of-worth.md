## Statement

"Utility is most men's test of worth" is the closing moral of The Peasant and the Apple-Tree (069-002). In one sentence it distills a hard truth about human nature: people judge value by what something does for them, not by what it is in itself. The principle operates at every level of Aesop's world — peasants evaluate trees by their yield, thieves evaluate a cock by whether it helps or hinders their trade, and [[the-lion|lion]]s evaluate hunting partners by what share of the kill they can claim.

The moral is not a celebration of utility as the highest virtue. It is an observation about how valuation actually works in practice. Aesop does not say utility *should* be the test of worth; he says it *is*.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Peasant and the Apple-Tree

> A Peasant had an Apple-tree growing in his garden, which bore no fruit, but merely served to provide a shelter from the heat for the sparrows and grasshoppers which sat and chirped in its branches. Disappointed at its barrenness he determined to cut it down, and went and fetched his axe for the purpose. (069-001)

> But when the sparrows and the grasshoppers saw what he was about to do, they begged him to spare it, and said to him, "If you destroy the tree we shall have to seek shelter elsewhere, and you will no longer have our merry chirping to enliven your work in the garden." (069-001)

The Peasant ignores their pleas — birdsong has no utility. He swings his axe: "A few strokes showed that it was hollow inside and contained a swarm of bees and a large store of honey. Delighted with his find he threw down his axe, saying, 'The old tree is worth keeping after all'" (069-001).

The pivot is instant and absolute. Birdsong = worthless. Honey = worth keeping. Same tree, same man, same axe — only the discovery of a useful product changes the verdict.

### The Thieves and the Cock

A Cock pleads for his life by arguing his utility: "I rouse honest men to their work in the morning by my crowing" (055-002). But the Thief who is about to wring his neck sees the same utility from the opposite side: crowing makes it "still harder for us to get a livelihood." What the Cock presents as a virtue — waking honest men — is to the Thief the very reason to kill him. Utility is relative to the user's interests, not an objective property of the thing itself.

### The Swollen Fox

A [[the-fox|Fox]], trapped in a hollow tree after gorging on a shepherd's bread and meat, cannot escape because his belly is too full. Another [[the-fox|Fox]] advises him: "Wait till you get thin again; you'll be able to get out then" (066-001). The moral turns on timing and use: a hollow tree that provided easy access is now a prison. Utility is not fixed — what serves you at one moment traps you at the next. The Peasant's apple-tree is hollow *and* useful; the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s hollow tree is hollow *and* a cage.

## Mechanism

Utility-as-worth operates through three linked dynamics:

| Dynamic | How It Works | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Interest-dependence** | The same quality is evaluated oppositely depending on who benefits | Cock's crowing: virtuous to the farmer, fatal to the thief |
| **Discoverability** | Worth is only recognized when utility becomes visible | Apple-tree spared only after honey is found inside |
| **Temporal instability** | What is useful now may not be useful later | Hollow tree: entrance → prison |

The mechanism is not moral but cognitive. People do not ask "What is this thing?" They ask "What can this thing do for me right now?" The Peasant does not reassess the tree itself after finding honey; he reassesses *his relationship to it*. The tree has not changed — his knowledge of it has.

Aesop's insight is that this is not hypocrisy. It is how the human mind economizes attention. We cannot value everything; we value what serves our purposes. The danger is not in using utility as a test but in forgetting that others — sparrows, thieves, [[the-fox|fox]]es — have different utilities, and what is worthless to us may be life itself to them.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[what-is-worth-most-is-often-valued-least]] — The complementary moral. The Stag adm[[anger|ire]]s his antlers (ornamental) and despises his legs (useful), only to discover that legs save him and antlers trap him. Together these two morals form a complete theory of value: people undervalue what is genuinely useful and overvalue what merely looks impressive.
- [[no-virtue-in-giving-useless]] — Utility ethics applied to [[kindness|generosity]]. Giving away what serves no purpose to the giver is not virtue but waste disposal.
- [[much-wants-more-and-loses-all]] — The dark side of utility thinking. When the Peasant discovers honey, he spares the tree. When the Goose-owner wants all the gold at once, he kills the goose. The difference between wise utility assessment and destructive greed is the difference between harvesting honey and cutting open the source.

## Enduring Influence

"Utility is most men's test of worth" has not entered common English as a proverb in the way "Look before you leap" has, but its sentiment pervades modern discourse. Every cost-benefit analysis, every ROI calculation, every debate about whether the arts "deserve" funding is a replay of the Peasant and his apple-tree. The sparrows and grasshoppers — the creatures who benefit from the tree without extracting a measurable product — are the artists, the philosophers, the unmonetized contributors to a garden whose owner only values honey.

The fable is a warning disguised as an observation. Aesop does not tell us to stop measuring worth by utility. He tells us to notice that we do, and to remember that some things — like birdsong in a garden — may be worth keeping even if they produce no honey at all.
