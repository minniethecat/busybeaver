## Role in Aesop

The Ass is [[Aesop's Fables]]' most consistent embodiment of foolish self-deception. Unlike the [[the-fox|fox]], who deceives others, or the [[wolf]], who simply takes what he wants, the Ass deceives himself — mistaking a lion's skin for [[authority]], a lap-dog's tricks for [[affection]], and another creature's burden for an easier [[fate]]. He is the beast who works hard, learns nothing, and suffers precisely because he cannot accept what he is.

[[G. K. Chesterton]]'s introduction invokes the Ass as a foundational fable-image: "As the child learns A for Ass or B for Bull or C for Cow, so man has learnt here to connect the simpler and stronger creatures with the simpler and stronger truths" (P03-006). The Ass is elementary — the first letter of the moral alphabet — and what he teaches is the cost of not knowing your place. The fables are not cruel to the Ass because he is weak; they are honest about him because he is foolish, and [[folly]] has consequences that [[strength]] does not.

## Key Appearances

### The Ass and the Lap-Dog

A man kept both an Ass and a Lap-dog. The Lap-dog was petted and fed from his master's table; the Ass, seeing this, decided to imitate the dog's playful behavior — leaping up, braying, and putting his heavy hooves on his master's shoulders. The servants beat him back to the stable. "The Ass was not made to be a lap-dog" (032-001). The fable is the definitive Ass story: the creature who envies another's role without understanding that roles are not interchangeable, and that what charms in a small [[dog]] is [[danger|dangerous]] in a large beast of burden.

> There was once a man who had an Ass and a Lap-dog. The Ass was housed in the stable with plenty of oats and hay, and was as well off as an ass could be. The little Dog was made a great pet of. (032-001)

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin

An Ass found a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s skin and dressed himself in it, strutting about the forest and terrifying every animal he met. But when he opened his mouth to roar, he brayed — and the [[the-fox|Fox]], who had fled at first, turned back and said: "I would have been frightened too, if I had not heard your voice" (061-002). The Ass with a lion's skin is the archetype of imposture exposed: appearance without substance fools only for as long as the mouth stays shut.

### The Ass and the Fox

An Ass and a [[the-fox|Fox]] went into partnership and went out hunting together. When a [[the-lion|Lion]] appeared, the [[the-fox|Fox]] betrayed the Ass to save himself — promising the [[the-lion|Lion]] an easy meal if he would spare the [[the-fox|Fox]]. The [[the-lion|Lion]] agreed, seized the Ass, and then turned on the [[the-fox|Fox]] as well (018-001). In this pairing, the Ass is the naive partner who trusts a creature known to be crooked — and pays with his life for believing that [[treachery]] would not be turned on him.

### The Ass and His Purchaser

A man who wanted to buy an Ass took him home on trial. He put the Ass in the stable with his other asses, and the Ass immediately went and stood beside the laziest and greediest of the lot. The man returned him to the seller: "I can see what kind of creature he is by the company he chooses" (190-001). The Ass here is not the victim but the self-revealer: his nature betrays itself through his choice of companions.

## Relationship with the Fox

The Ass and the [[the-fox|Fox]] are the odd couple of the Aesopic bestiary — the fool and the schemer. Where the [[the-fox|Fox]] calculates, the Ass blunders. Where the [[the-fox|Fox]] dissembles, the Ass is transparent. Their partnership in "The Ass and the Fox" (018-001) is doomed from the start: the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s cunning requires a victim, and the Ass is the most available. But the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s betrayal of the Ass also contains a warning for the clever — the [[the-fox|Fox]] himself is eaten by the [[the-lion|Lion]] immediately after, because predators do not honor treaties with smaller predators. The Ass's [[folly]] gets him killed; the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s [[treachery]] gets him killed too. In the end, the Ass is the more dignified of the two, because he at least was honest.

## Symbolism

The Ass symbolizes **the creature who cannot learn from his own nature**. He is not evil, not predatory, not even lazy. He is simply incapable of the one thing that would save him: self-knowledge. The Ass who envies the lap-dog (032-001), the Ass who wears the lion's skin (061-002), the Ass who trusts the fox (018-001) — all make the same error in different costumes. They believe they can be something other than what they are. Aesop's verdict on this ambition is unforgiving: the Ass who forgets himself is beaten back to the stable, eaten by the lion, or returned to his seller. The Ass's [[fate]] is not tragic, because tragedy requires [[self-awareness]]. The Ass is comedy — the comedy of the creature who looks in the water and sees a [[the-lion|lion]].
