## Statement

"One swallow does not make summer" is the closing line of [[the-spendthrift-and-the-swallow|The Spendthrift and the Swallow]], stated as an explicit moral at the fable's end (014-002).

The proverb has outlasted the fable itself — it is now a standalone English idiom, used to caution against [[hasty-generalization|hasty generalization]] and the human tendency to treat a single [[kindness|favor]]able sign as a guarantee of lasting change.

The moral targets a specific cognitive error: **premature pattern recognition**. When we want something to be true, we lower the evidentiary threshold for believing it. The Spendthrift *wants* summer to have arrived, so one Swallow is enough to convince him. The fable punishes this error not with mere disappointment but with death — the Spendthrift freezes along with the Swallow.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Spendthrift and the Swallow

A Spendthrift who has squandered everything but the c[[fate|lot]]hes on his back spots a Swallow in early spring. Convinced that summer has arrived, he sells his coat for whatever it will fetch. But the weather turns: a sharp frost kills the Swallow and leaves the Spendthrift shivering:

> A Spendthrift, who had wasted his fortune, and had nothing left but the clothes in which he stood, saw a Swallow one fine day in early spring. Thinking that summer had come, and that he could now do without his coat, he went and sold it for what it would fetch. A change, however, took place in the weather, and there came a sharp frost which killed the unfortunate Swallow.

> When the Spendthrift saw its dead body he cried, "Miserable bird! Thanks to you I am perishing of cold myself." (014-001)

> One swallow does not make summer. (014-002)

The Spendthrift's error is a triple failure: he misreads the season, he bets everything on that misreading, and he blames the Swallow rather than his own judgment.

### The Milkmaid and Her Pail

A Milkmaid balances a pail of milk on her head and walks to market, daydreaming about the profits she will make, the eggs she will buy, the chickens she will raise, and the gown she will wear — until she tosses her head at the imagined suitors, the pail falls, and all her plans spill into the dirt (029-001).

She has built a future on a single assumption: that the milk will sell at a good price. One successful market day does not make a [[fate|fortune]].

### The Goose That Laid the Golden Eggs

A Man and his Wife possess a Goose that lays a golden egg every day. But one golden egg is not enough — they want all the gold at once. They kill the Goose, cut it open, and find nothing but goose innards (002-001). One golden egg is a sign; a thousand golden eggs are a [[fate|fortune]]. The Man and his Wife confuse the sign for the source, just as the Spendthrift confuses the Swallow for summer.

### The Boy Who Cried Wolf

A Shepherd's Boy, bored on the hillside, cries "Wolf!" to raise the villagers. They come running — twice. The third time, when a real Wolf appears, nobody comes (099-001). The villagers treat two false [[fear|alarm]]s as a pattern; the Boy treats one successful prank as an invitation to repeat it. Both errors arise from mistaking a single data point for a trend.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **sample-size failure**: the tendency to generalize from insufficient evidence. The Swallow is a valid signal — swallows do return in spring — but a single Swallow is not a *reliable* signal. The Spendthrift treats it as conclusive because the alternative (remaining cold and coatless through a long winter) is too painful to contemplate.

Three conditions make sample-size failure fatal in Aesop:

| Condition | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **High stakes** | The Spendthrift bets his only coat on the Swallow's appearance |
| **Motivated reasoning** | He *wants* it to be summer, so he suppresses doubt |
| **No recovery mechanism** | Once the coat is sold, there is no way to get it back |

When all three conditions hold — high stakes, wishful thinking, and irreversibility — a single Swallow is a death sentence.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[look-before-your-leap]] — The complementary moral: "Look before your leap" (047-002) [[advice|counsel]]s caution before action, while "one swallow does not make summer" [[advice|counsel]]s caution before conclusion. One is about physical risk;

the other is about epistemic risk.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Spendthrift's error has a pride component: he is too confident in his own reading of the season.

[[pride|Pride]] suppresses the doubt that would have saved him.
- [[fortune]] — [[fortune|Fortune]], in Aesop, is a distribution game: some get golden eggs, some get frost. But the Spendthrift's [[fortune|misfortune]] is self-inflicted — he gambled on [[fortune|Fortune]]'s consistency, and [[fortune|Fortune]] has none.
- [[appearance]] — The Swallow *appears* to signal summer. Appearance and reality diverge;

treating appearance as reality is the root of the Spendthrift's ruin.

## Enduring Influence

"One swallow does not make a summer" has become a standalone English proverb, cited in philosophy (Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* uses the same image), law (warning against single-data-point precedents),

and everyday caution against overgeneralization. The proverb's survival testifies to the universality of the cognitive error it names: every generation has its Spendthrifts, and every Spendthrift has his Swallow.
