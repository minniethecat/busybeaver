## Statement

"Advantages that are dearly bought are doubtful blessings" (199-002) is the explicit moral of The Wild Ass and the Pack-Ass. A Wild Ass, wandering free, encounters a Pack-Ass luxuriating in the sun. The Pack-Ass's sleek coat suggests a life of ease, and the Wild Ass envies him openly. But when the Wild Ass next sees his acquaintance, the Pack-Ass is bent under a heavy load while a driver beats him with a thick stick:

> "Ah, my friend," said the Wild Ass, "I don't envy you any more: for I see that you pay dearly for your good things." (199-001)

The moral is not that all advantages are fraudulent but that **visible benefits obscure invisible costs**. The Pack-Ass's sleek coat is real — he is well-fed — but the price is servitude and the stick. The Wild Ass's freedom comes with lean fodder, but he has no master and no beating.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Wild Ass and the Pack-Ass (primary)

The structure is an [[jealousy|envy]]-correction arc. First encounter: Wild Ass sees Pack-Ass resting in sunshine and envies his comfort. Second encounter: Wild Ass sees Pack-Ass laboring under a load, being beaten, and revises his judgment. The Pack-Ass's advantage — regular food, a master's care — is purchased with [[freedom|autonomy]] and physical safety. The Wild Ass's disadvantage — wild foraging, no shelter — purchases freedom from the stick.

### The Fir-tree and the Bramble (parallel)

A Fir-tree boasts to a Bramble about its usefulness for building houses, dismissing the Bramble as "of no use whatever." The Bramble replies:

> "Ah, that's all very well: but you wait till they come with axes and saws to cut you down, and then you'll wish you were a Bramble and not a Fir." (033-001)

The Fir-tree's utility is its [[ruin|destruction]]: the moment it is useful enough to notice, it is useful enough to cut down. The Bramble's uselessness is its [[safety|protection]]. This is the same cost-benefit structure as the Pack-Ass: the Fir-tree pays for its stature with its life.

### The Fox and the Lion (parallel)

A [[the-fox|Fox]], serving a [[the-lion|Lion]] as attendant, grows dissatisfied with his meager share of the hunt and strikes out on his own. He tries to steal a lamb but is caught by the shepherd. Returning to the [[the-lion|Lion]], he is rebuffed:

> "Better servitude with safety than freedom with danger." (253-001)

[[the-fox|The Fox]] discovers, like the Wild Ass, that freedom carries hidden costs — in this case, vulnerability to predators and shepherds. His servitude to the [[the-lion|Lion]] was the price of safety.

### The Wolf and the House Dog (parallel)

A lean Wolf meets a well-fed House Dog and envies him — until he notices the chafed neck where the collar rubs. The Dog's full belly is bought with the chain, and the Wolf's hunger with his [[freedom|liberty]]. The Wild Ass and the Wolf reach the same conclusion through identical discovery: first [[jealousy|envy]], then inspection, then rejection.

| Creature | Visible Advantage | Hidden Cost | Verdict |
|----------|------------------|-------------|---------|
| Pack-Ass | Sleek coat, regular food | Heavy loads, beating | "Don't [[jealousy|envy]] you any more" |
| Fir-tree | Useful for building | Cut down with axes | "Wish you were a Bramble" |
| [[the-fox|Fox]] (servant) | [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[safety|protection]] | Meager share | Returns to servitude |
| House Dog | Full belly | Collar, chain | Wolf chooses hunger |

## Mechanism

The moral operates through an **asymmetric visibility principle**: advantages are displayed, costs are concealed. The Pack-Ass's sleek coat is visible at a glance; the beating he took that morning is not. This asymmetry is not accidental — it is structural. Creatures advertise their benefits and hide their sufferings, so the first encounter always misleads.

The Wild Ass's [[jealousy|envy]] is rational given what he can see. His correction comes only with **additional information** — the second encounter that reveals the load and the stick. The moral therefore carries a methodological warning: do not judge a condition by its surface; wait for the moment when its costs become visible. The second encounter is the truth.

Compare [[better-poverty-without-care]], which makes the same argument from the opposite d[[anger|ire]]ction: poverty is not a curse if it buys freedom from obligation. The Wild Ass *is* the Bramble — lean, wild, unburdened — and his initial [[jealousy|envy]] is the Fir-tree's boast reversed.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[be-content-with-your-lot]] — The Wild Ass's initial [[jealousy|envy]] is a violation of contentment, and his correction is contentment's affirmation. "Be [[be-content-with-your-lot|content with your lot]]" is the positive framing; "dearly bought advantages are doubtful blessings" is the negative one — the reason *why* contentment is wise.

- [[better-poverty-without-care]] — The Bramble's reply to the Fir-tree is both morals at once: poverty without care is better than wealth with obligations, and the Fir-tree's advantages are dearly bought. These two morals are twins, one stated from the poor creature's perspective and one from the envious observer's.

- [[better-servitude-with-safety]] — [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s failed [[freedom|independence]] is the safety case: servitude is the price of [[safety|protection]] from greater dangers. The Wild Ass lacks the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s predators, so his calculus differs: the Pack-Ass's stick is a *higher* risk than wild foraging. Both morals agree that the trade-off must be assessed, not assumed.

- [[no-use-being-own-master]] — The companion fable in which a Wild Ass taunts a Pack-Ass, only to flee from a [[the-lion|Lion]]. Being your own master requ[[anger|ire]]s the ability to defend yourself. The Pack-Ass's master beats him, but the master also keeps the [[the-lion|Lion]] at bay.
