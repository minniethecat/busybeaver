## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], [[fortune|misfortune]] is never merely random. It is a **revealer** — a sudden reversal that strips away pretense and shows the true character of persons and situations. When [[fortune|misfortune]] strikes, friends reveal whether they are sincere or self-interested; the overconfident discover the limits of their [[strength|power]]s; and the wise are distinguished from the foolish by whether they learn from their own suffering or only from the suffering of others.

The corpus treats [[fortune|misfortune]] as a universal experience that no one escapes, but that not everyone experiences in the same way. The question Aesop poses is not "will [[fortune|misfortune]] come?" but "what will [[fortune|misfortune]] reveal when it arrives?"

> Misfortune tests the sincerity of friendship. ([[fable-066|066-001]])

## Varieties

**Self-Inflicted Misfortune.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] in [[fable-066|066-001]] finds bread and meat left by shepherds and "greedily devoured it all." Having eaten, he is too swollen to squeeze back through the narrow aperture. His [[fortune|misfortune]] is the d[[anger|ire]]ct [[punishment|consequence]] of his [[greed]] — a [[fortune|misfortune]] that could have been avoided by moderation. The other [[the-fox|Fox]]'s advice — "stay where you are till you shrink to your former size" — is the grim logic of self-inflicted trouble: the only cure is time and discomfort.

**Misfortune as Test.** "Misfortune tests the [[honesty|sincerity]] of friendship" ([[fable-066|066-001]]). This single line distills a recurring Aesopian theme: [[fortune|prosperity]] attracts companions; adversity reveals which of them are genuine. [[the-fox|The Fox]] trapped in the tree receives honest but unhelpful advice from a passing stranger — not the rescue a true friend [[power|might]] attempt. Misfortune asks a question that fair weather never poses: who will stay when staying costs something?

**Misfortune from Overreach.** The Jackdaw in [[fable-170|170-002]] sees an Eagle carry off a lamb and attempts the same feat with a ram — only to find his claws tangled in the fleece. The Shepherd catches him, clips his wings, and gives him to his children. The fable's moral is blunt: "If you attempt what is beyond your [[strength|power]], your trouble will be wasted and you court not only [[fortune|misfortune]] but ridicule." The [[fortune|misfortune]] of overreach is double: failure itself, plus the [[shame|humiliation]] of having attempted at all.

**Learning from Others' Misfortune.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] in [[fable-246|246-002]] stands at the entrance to the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den and observes "many tracks pointing inwards but none coming out." He draws the correct conclusion — the [[the-lion|Lion]] is not sick but hungry — and survives. "Happy is he who learns from the [[fortune|misfortune]]s of others" is Aesop's praise for the observational [[wisdom]] that turns other people's suffering into one's own survival guide.

## Consequences

Misfortune in Aesop produces two possible outcomes. The foolish suffer and learn nothing — or worse, learn the wrong lesson, redoubling their efforts in the same [[fate|doom]]ed d[[anger|ire]]ction. The wise observe [[fortune|misfortune]] — their own or others' — and adjust. The Miser's neighbour points out that the gold "was of no earthly use" even when possessed; the [[the-fox|Fox]] at the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den reads the tracks and walks away.

The fables do not promise that [[fortune|misfortune]] can be avoided. They promise that [[fortune|misfortune]], properly read, is a curriculum — and that those who refuse to learn from it will repeat it.

## Relation to Fortune

Misfortune and [[fortune]] are the negative and positive faces of the same Aesopian preoccupation with reversal. [[Fortune]] represents the unexpected benefit — the golden axe rising from the river, the gold coins spilling from the shattered Image. Misfortune represents the unexpected cost — the swollen belly that traps you, the ram's fleece that entangles your claws.

But Aesop draws a subtle distinction: [[fortune]] often arrives undeserved ([[fable-017|017-001]] shows Mercury rewarding [[honesty]] with unearned wealth), while [[fortune|misfortune]] often arrives precisely because it *is* deserved — the [[punishment|consequence]] of [[greed]], overreach, or [[foolishness|folly]]. In this sense, [[fortune|misfortune]] in Aesop is more just than [[fate|fortune]]. It comes when called.
