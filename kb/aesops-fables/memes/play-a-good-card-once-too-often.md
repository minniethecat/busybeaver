## Statement

"You may play a good card once too often" is the closing moral of The Ass Carrying Salt (045-002). A Pedlar's Ass discovers, by accident, that falling into a stream dissolves much of the salt he carries and lightens his load considerably. Delighted with this discovery, he deliberately repeats the trick on the next journey.

But his master, having caught on, loads him with sponges instead of salt. When the Ass falls again, the sponges absorb water and become heavier than ever.

The trick that brought relief now brings ruin. **The Ass's error is not that the trick was immoral — it is that it was legible.** A trick observed is a trick disarmed.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass Carrying Salt

> A Pedlar who owned an Ass one day bought a quantity of salt, and loaded up his beast with as much as he could bear. On the way home the Ass stumbled as he was crossing a stream and fell into the water. The salt got thoroughly wetted and much of it melted and drained away, so that, when he got on his legs again, the Ass found his load had become much less heavy. (045-001)

> His master, however, drove him back to town and bought more salt… The Ass, noticing this, fell down on purpose, and got rid of most of the salt once again. But the Pedlar saw through the trick and drove the Ass back to town once more, and bought a lot of sponges and loaded him with them instead. When they reached the stream, the Ass fell down again as usual. But this time the sponges absorbed the water and became so heavy that he could not rise, and the Pedlar beat him for his pains. (045-001)

> You may play a good card once too often. (045-002)

The fable is a three-act structure: discovery, exploitation, cost. The Ass's first fall is innocent; the second is strategic; the third is catastrophic. Aesop compresses all three into one paragraph of perfect economy.

### The Shepherd Boy and the Wolf

The boy who cries "Wolf!" for amusement finds that the villagers stop coming — and when the Wolf actually arrives, no one believes him (092-001). The logic is identical: a signal that works once loses its [[strength|power]] when repeated. Both the Ass and the Boy discover that **[[trust|credibility]] is a non-renewable resource**.

### The Fox and the Crow

A Crow holds a piece of cheese; a [[the-fox|Fox]] flatters her into singing, and the cheese drops (001-001). [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s trick works brilliantly — once. The Crow, having learned, will not be flattered again. [[the-fox|The Fox]], unlike the Ass, takes his winnings and leaves. He knows that a good card played twice is a losing hand.

### Contrast and Refinement

| Character | Trick | Repeated? | Outcome |
|-----------|-------|-----------|---------|
| The Ass | Falling in stream | Yes — ×2 | Drowns in sponges |
| The Shepherd Boy | False [[fear|alarm]] | Yes — ×3 | Eaten by wolf |
| [[the-fox|The Fox]] (Crow) | [[flattery|Flattery]] | Once only | Gets cheese, leaves |

[[the-fox|The Fox]] who flatters the Crow is the Ass's wiser cousin: he knows when to stop playing the card. The moral is not "never use tricks" — it is "never use the *same* trick twice against the *same* opponent."

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **adversarial learning asymmetry**. The Ass discovers a trick; the Pedlar discovers the Ass. Each round, both parties learn — but the Pedlar learns *about the Ass*, while the Ass learns only *about salt*.

| Round | Ass's Knowledge | Pedlar's Knowledge |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Salt dissolves in water (accidental) | Ass fell — load lightened |
| 2 | Falling works reliably | Ass is faking — time for countermove |
| 3 | — (doesn't reach round 3) | Load is now sponges |

The Ass's fatal assumption is that the environment is static. He models the world as "salt + stream = lighter," failing to account for the Pedlar as an intelligent adversary. This is the same error that appears in games theory: an exploitable strategy attracts counter-adaptation. The Ass discovers an edge and assumes it is permanent.

The Pedlar, meanwhile, demonstrates the counter-strategy: **observe, confirm, exploit**. He does not punish the Ass immediately; he verifies the trick first, then substitutes sponges. His patience is what makes his revenge complete.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[look-before-your-leap]] — The Ass leaps (falls) without looking at what he carries. Had he checked the cargo first, he would have survived round 3.
- [[much-wants-more-and-loses-all]] — The Ass wants *more* relief than his situation naturally provides.

[[greed|Greed]] is the motive; overplaying the card is the method.
- [[false-confidence-leads-to-disaster]] — The Ass's [[trust|confidence]] in his trick is genuine — it really worked twice.

False [[trust|confidence]] is not about being wrong; it is about being right in a world that has changed.
- [[be-content-with-your-lot]] — If the Ass had accepted his salt load after the first accidental fall, he would have been fine. [[discontent|Discontent]] drives the second, deliberate fall.

## Enduring Influence

"You may play a good card once too often" has not become a standalone idiom in the way "[[the-fox-and-the-grapes|sour grapes]]" or "crying wolf" have, but its structure — the trick that backfires on repetition — is one of the most replicated patterns in world folklore.

The fable d[[anger|ire]]ctly anticipates the strategic concept of "diminishing returns to [[disguise|deception]]" studied in evolutionary biology: organisms that signal deceptively (bluffing in animal contests, mimicry in insects) succeed only as long as their signals are rare. Once the trick becomes common, counter-adaptation eliminates the advantage.

The fable is Perry Index 211. Its modern equivalent is the poker maxim: "You can't bluff the same player twice."
