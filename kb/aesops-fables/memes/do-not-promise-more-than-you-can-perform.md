## Statement

"Do not promise more than you can perform" is the explicit moral of The Sick Man and the Vow (244-002). A man gravely ill promises the gods a hundred oxen if they restore his health. When they do, he realizes he owns no oxen, so he fabricates a hundred tiny tallow oxen and offers those instead. The gods, unamused, send him a dream d[[anger|ire]]cting him to a shore where he will find a hundred crowns — but when he hurries there, robbers seize him and sell him into slavery for exactly one hundred crowns. The symmetry is the point: the price of the broken [[promise|vow]] is the [[promise|vow]]'s exact value.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Sick Man and the Vow

A man at death's door makes a desperate [[promise|vow]]: a hundred oxen to the gods for his recovery. He recovers, but owns no oxen. His solution — a hundred miniature oxen made of tallow — is ingenious but transparently dishonest. He even declares his obligation discharged:

> "Ye gods, I call you to witness that I have discharged my vow." (244-001)

The gods are not impressed. They send him to the shore for a hundred crowns, where robbers capture him and sell him for exactly that sum. The broken [[promise|vow]] is repaid in kind — not by divine lightning but by the world's own machinery of [[punishment|consequence]].

### The Man Who Promised the Impossible

A poor Man, hoping to win [[kindness|favor]], promises a rich benefactor that he can perform an impossible feat — teaching an Ass to speak. When challenged, he claims it will take twenty years, by which time one of them (the Ass, the benefactor, or himself) will surely be dead. The story mocks the logic of overpromising: the promisor knows he cannot deliver and builds the escape into the promise itself (194-001).

### The Fox Who Promised the Moon

A [[the-fox|Fox]], trapped and desperate, promises extravagant rewards to anyone who frees her. When freed, she immediately reneges, claiming the promise was made under duress. The fable captures the promisor's convenient amnesia — and the promissee's failure to recognize that no en[[strength|force]]ment mechanism exists. The Sick Man's gods, by contrast, *do* en[[strength|force]] (063-001).

## Mechanism

The moral's mechanism is **exact reciprocity**: the punishment matches the promise in both kind and scale. The sick man promises a hundred oxen; he is sold for a hundred crowns. The gods do not punish him with illness or death but with an economic transaction that mirrors his own fraud. This is not [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]] — it is accounting.

Aesop embeds a subtle theological claim here that differs from later religious traditions. The gods are not merciful; they are auditors. They grant the request not out of [[mercy|compassion]] but to test the [[promise|vow]]. And their en[[strength|force]]ment is not supernatural thunderbolt but the ordinary machinery of the world — robbers, slave markets, the price of a human being. The promise-breaker is not smitten; he is simply handed over to [[punishment|consequence]].

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[do-not-count-your-chickens]] — Both morals warn against treating the future as already settled. The Milkmaid spends imaginary egg-money; the sick man spends imaginary oxen. The difference is that the Milkmaid's loss is intrinsic (the eggs spill), while the sick man's is extrinsic (the gods collect).
- [[promise]] — This moral is the dark corollary to the broader concept of promise. Where promise creates obligation, overpromising creates obligation that cannot be discharged — and the gap between promise and performance is where punishment enters.
- [[better-poverty-without-care]] — The sick man's [[promise|vow]] creates obligations he cannot meet — the same dynamic as the Fir-tree boasting of usefulness until the axes arrive. Both morals warn that obligations freely undertaken can become traps.

## Enduring Influence

"Do not promise more than you can perform" exists in English as a proverb, though in practice it is more often violated than observed. The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 28. The tale's structure — a desperate [[promise|vow]], a miraculous recovery, a cheap substitute, and a symmetrical punishment — has [[persuasion|influence]]d cautionary tales about divine bargains from medieval exempla to modern fiction.
