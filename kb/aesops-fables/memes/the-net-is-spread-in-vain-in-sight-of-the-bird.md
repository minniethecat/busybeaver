## Statement

"The net is spread in vain in sight of the bird" is the explicit moral of [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Bull (142-002). A [[the-lion|Lion]] sees a fine fat Bull and "cast about for some means of getting him into his clutches" (142-001). He sends an invitation to dine, claiming he is sacrificing a sheep. The Bull accepts, but on arriving at the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den:

> "He saw a great array of saucepans and spits, but no sign of a sheep; so he turned on his heel and walked quietly away. The Lion called after him in an injured tone to ask the reason, and the Bull turned round and said, 'I have reason enough. When I saw all your preparations it struck me at once that the victim was to be a Bull and not a sheep.'" (142-001)

The Bull's escape is ent[[anger|ire]]ly cognitive: he does not fight, flee, or call for help. He observes, infers, and walks away. [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s preparations — meant to facilitate the killing — are precisely what prevent it. The trap, by being visible, announces itself.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Lion and the Bull

[[the-lion|The Lion]]'s error is not in his des[[anger|ire]] to eat the Bull but in his inability to separate the preparation for the deed from the deed itself. The saucepans and spits are excessive for a single sheep; the mismatch between the claimed sacrifice and the visible apparatus is the gap through which the Bull walks to safety. [[the-lion|The Lion]] compounds his error by calling after the Bull "in an injured tone" — he cannot help signalling his own frustrated intent. The Bull's quiet exit ("turned on his heel and walked quietly away") is the precise opposite of the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s elaborate scheming: simplicity defeats complexity.

### The Fox and the Lion

In [[the-fox|The Fox]] and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (fable-033), a [[the-fox|Fox]] sees a [[the-lion|Lion]] for the first time and is terrified. The second time, he is less afraid. The third time, he approaches and speaks to the [[the-lion|Lion]]. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s progressive desensitisation is the inverse of the Bull's instant recognition: the [[the-fox|Fox]] learns through repeated exposure; the Bull sees the truth in a single glance. Both fables value observation over action — the survivor is the one who looks carefully before committing.

### The Fox and the Goat

In [[the-fox|The Fox]] and the Goat (fable-024), a [[the-fox|Fox]] trapped in a well convinces a Goat to jump in, promising a way out. The Goat jumps in; the [[the-fox|Fox]] climbs out on the Goat's back and leaves him trapped. The Goat fails to see the net spread before him — the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s story is the bait, and the well is the trap. The Goat, unlike the Bull, does not inspect the scene before committing. The contrast is instructive: the Bull survives because he looks; the Goat is trapped because he listens.

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

In The Wolf in Sheep's C[[fate|lot]]hing (fable-033b), a Wolf disguises himself as a sheep to get close to the flock. The Shepherd, unable to distinguish the Wolf from the sheep, kills the Wolf by accident while slaughtering a sheep for dinner. The Wolf's disguise — his "invisible" trap — backf[[anger|ire]]s because it works too well. The Shepherd, unlike the Bull, does not see the net because the net is too well hidden. But the Wolf, like the [[the-lion|Lion]], dies by his own contrivance.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **self-betraying preparation**. Any trap requ[[anger|ire]]s preparation (the saucepans, the spits, the absence of a sheep), and preparation leaves visible traces. The would-be predator faces a trade-off:

1. **Prepare adequately** → visible traces alert the prey → prey escapes
2. **Conceal preparation** → trap is inadequate → prey may not be caught

[[the-lion|The Lion]] chooses option 1 and loses the Bull. The mechanism applies wherever hostile intent requ[[anger|ire]]s advance preparation: the [[deception|lying]] suitor who rehearses his story too carefully, the ambush that requ[[anger|ire]]s too many soldiers to hide, the fraud that requ[[anger|ire]]s too many documents to forge. Visibility of preparation is inversely proportional to likelihood of success.

The Bull's method — observation followed by quiet withdrawal — is the optimal response. He does not confront the [[the-lion|Lion]] (which would be dangerous), does not flee (which would trigger pursuit), and does not argue (which would give the [[the-lion|Lion]] a [[fortune|chance]] to improvise). He walks away. The moral's avian metaphor — "in sight of the bird" — emphasises the importance of the prey's perspective: the net is only invisible from the hunter's side.

## Enduring Influence

The image of the bird seeing the net and f[[deception|lying]] past it has become a proverb in its own right across multiple languages. The fable underlies the strategic principle that *surprise is the prerequisite of ambush* — once the target sees the preparation, the ambush has failed. In military thought, the fable is cited in discussions of operational [[safety|security]]: visible logistics betray intent. In everyday ethics, the fable validates the instinct to walk away from situations whose visible apparatus does not match their stated purpose — the job offer that promises advancement but shows signs of exploitation, the partnership that claims mutual benefit but reveals one-sided preparation. The Bull's quiet exit is the model: no confrontation, no explanation, just observation and departure.
