## Statement

"Equals make the best friends" is the explicit moral of The Two Pots (125-003). Two Pots — one of earthenware, one of brass — are swept away by a flood. The Brazen Pot offers [[safety|protection]]: "Keep close by my side." The Earthenware Pot replies that proximity alone is the greatest danger: "One touch from you and I should be broken in pieces."

> The Brazen Pot urged his companion to keep close by his side, and he would protect him. The other thanked him, but begged him not to come near him on any account. (125-002)

The Two Pots do not part in anger. The Earthenware Pot thanks the Brazen Pot for the offer. The refusal is structural, not personal — the inequality of their material makes friendship impossible.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Two Pots (primary)

The fable strips friendship down to physics. Two vessels in the same current, one of brass and one of clay, cannot travel together because contact alone — not malice — will destroy the weaker. The earthenware pot's wisdom is recognizing that the offer of [[safety|protection]] is the offer of [[ruin|destruction]] (125-002).

### The Lion in Love

A [[the-lion|Lion]], courting a Woodman's daughter, agrees to have his claws and teeth removed to make himself acceptable to her family. The Woodman then beats him with a club — the [[the-lion|Lion]] made himself unequal in the wrong d[[anger|ire]]ction, and the friendship cost him everything (028-001).

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf upstream falsely accuses a Lamb downstream of muddying his water. The Lamb rebuts each charge with perfect logic. The Wolf eats him anyway. The fable is the negative case: inequality makes predation inevitable, [[respect|regard]]less of the weaker party's reasonableness (001-001).

## Mechanism

Aesop's Two Pots diagnosis is more specific than "[[a-man-is-known-by-company|birds of a feather]]." The inequality is not one of virtue or character but of **material constitution**. The Brazen Pot means well — its offer is sincere. But good intention cannot override physics. The Earthenware Pot's refusal is a kind of cou[[anger|rage]]: it resists the flattery of being offered [[safety|protection]] and names the structural danger.

The fable also implies a critique of patronage: the offer of [[safety|protection]] from a more [[strength|power]]ful party is often the mechanism of [[ruin|destruction]]. The Brazen Pot is not the enemy, but its presence is fatal.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[union-is-strength]] — The Two Pots complement the Bundles of Sticks: strength through [[harmony|unity]] works only among equals. A bundle of brass and clay cannot be bound.

- [[bad-companions-corrupt]] — The Two Pots is the structural version, independent of moral character. The Brazen Pot is not bad — but contact with it destroys the weak.

- [[a-man-is-known-by-company]] — Company shapes identity; unequal company destroys it ent[[anger|ire]]ly.
