## Role in Aesop

The Sheep is the moral baseline of [[Aesop's Fables]] — the creature who does nothing wrong and suffers everything. As [[G. K. Chesterton]]'s introduction puts it, "The sheep in a fable must march on, as the pawn in chess must march on" (P03-004). The Sheep does not choose, does not scheme, does not resist. It is the object of predation, not the subject of action. And yet this very passivity makes the Sheep the ethical center of the fable universe: every harm done to a [[sheep]] is a pure injustice, uncomplicated by provocation.

The Sheep's appearances are almost always as the victim — of the [[wolf]], of the [[thief]], of the false [[shepherd]], sometimes even of the [[dog]] that should protect it. The Sheep rarely speaks, and when it does, its words are simple and true — and ignored. In a world of clever [[the-fox|foxes]] and strong [[the-lion|lions]], the Sheep is the creature who can only trust and suffer.

## Key Appearances

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

A [[wolf]] wrapped himself in a sheep's skin to infiltrate the flock. The disguise was so effective that the [[shepherd]] himself killed the disguised [[wolf]], mistaking him for a [[sheep]] (027-001). The fable is uniquely revealing: the Sheep's identity is so pure and defenseless that even impersonating it is dangerous. To be [[sheep]]-like is to be vulnerable to everyone — including one's own protectors.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

The [[wolf]] accuses a Lamb of muddying his water, then of insulting him — each charge more absurd than the last — and finally devours the Lamb. The Lamb offers reasoned defenses to each accusation, but the [[wolf]] is not interested in [[truth]]: "Any excuse will serve a [[tyrant]]" (120-001). The Lamb is the voice of [[reason]] — and reason is powerless against appetite.

### The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf

When a Shepherd's Boy cried "Wolf!" as a joke, the villagers rushed to protect the [[sheep]] — and found nothing. When the real [[wolf]] came, no one answered, and "the Wolf made a hearty meal of the boy's sheep" (046-001). The Sheep are the collateral damage of human [[lying|falsehood]]: they die not because they are guilty, but because their protectors have been conditioned to disbelieve the alarm.

> The Wolf made a hearty meal of the boy's sheep, and went off unmolested. (046-001)

### The Sheep and the Injured Wolf

A [[wolf]] who had been injured and could not hunt asked a passing [[sheep]] to bring him water. The [[sheep]] refused, recognizing that the [[wolf]]'s request was merely a preliminary to eating him. "If I bring you water," said the [[sheep]], "you will make a meal of me" (152-001). This is a rare moment of [[sheep]]-ish wisdom — the prey who sees through the predator's ruse.

## Relationship with the Wolf

The Sheep and the [[wolf]] are Aesop's irreducible pair — the simplest and most terrible relationship in the fable canon. The [[wolf]] eats; the Sheep is eaten. There is no negotiation, no justice, no appeal. When the Sheep speaks reason to the [[wolf]] (120-001), it is to demonstrate that reason has no jurisdiction over hunger. When the Sheep sees through the [[wolf]]'s deception (152-001), the insight saves it, but only for a moment. The Sheep's relationship with the [[wolf]] is the [[truth]] that all the other fables elaborate: in a world without protection, the innocent are food.

## Symbolism

The Sheep represents **pure innocence in a predatory world** — the moral reproach that vulnerability levels against [[strength]]. The Sheep does not need to argue because its existence is the argument: every harm to a [[sheep]] is a visible injustice. In Chesterton's chessboard reading of the fables, the Sheep is the pawn — it can only move forward, it cannot retreat, and it is sacrificed most often. But the pawn is also the piece that, against all odds, can reach the far rank and become something greater. The Sheep's silence is not weakness but witness: it testifies that [[cruelty]] is real, and that some creatures bear it without deserving it.
