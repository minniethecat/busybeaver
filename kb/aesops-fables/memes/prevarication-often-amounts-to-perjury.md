## Statement

"Prevarication often amounts to perjury" is the explicit moral of The Two Men and the Butcher (251-002). Two thieves collaborate in stealing meat: one snatches the joint, the other conceals it under his cloak. When the Butcher accuses them, each offers a technically true but substantively false defence — the taker says he hasn't *got* it, the hider says he hasn't *taken* it. The moral diagnoses a specific form of dishonesty: the use of verbal precision to evade moral truth. Quibbling over words does not erase the under[[deception|lying]] lie; it compounds it.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Two Men and the Butcher

Two Men consp[[anger|ire]] to steal a joint of meat while the Butcher's back is turned. One snatches, the other conceals:

> Two Men were buying meat at a Butcher's stall in the market-place, and, while the Butcher's back was turned for a moment, one of them snatched up a joint and hastily thrust it under the other's cloak, where it remained hidden. (251-001)

When the Butcher discovers the theft, each man deploys a technically accurate denial that together amounts to a complete lie:

> When the Butcher turned round, he missed the meat at once, and charged them with having stolen it: but the one who had taken it said he hadn't got it, and the one who had got it said he hadn't taken it. (251-001)

The Butcher is not deceived. He delivers a verdict that elevates the crime from theft to something graver — a lie told in the face of the gods:

> The Butcher felt sure they were deceiving him, but he only said, "You may cheat me with your lying, but you can't cheat the gods, and they won't let you off so lightly." (251-001)

The fable closes:

> Prevarication often amounts to perjury. (251-002)

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **distributed dishonesty**: two people, each telling a half-truth, together produce a whole lie. Neither man individually utters a false statement — the taker truly does not *have* the meat at the moment he speaks, and the hider truly did not *take* it. But this verbal precision is a moral evasion, not an honest defence.

Three structural features:

1. **The division of labour in [[deception|lying]]**: One man performs the physical act (taking); the other performs the physical [[disguise|concealment]] (hiding). Their verbal defences mirror this division: each defends the act he did not perform. The result is a seamless lie constructed from truthful pieces — a demonstration that truth-telling at the atomic level does not guarantee truth-telling at the molecular level.
2. **The Butcher's upgrade**: The Butcher does not merely call them thieves — he calls them liars who "can't cheat the gods." His [[blame|accusation]] escalates from a property crime to a cosmic [[insult|offence]]. Prevarication, in this framing, is not merely dishonest but *impious* — an attempt to cheat the divine as well as the human.
3. **Perjury as the limit case**: Perjury is [[deception|lying]] under [[promise|oath]] — [[deception|lying]] in the most formal, witnessed, consequential context. The moral's claim that prevarication "often amounts to" perjury means that the moral weight of a statement does not depend on whether an [[promise|oath]] was sworn but on whether the speaker knowingly used words to deceive. The courtroom is merely the formalisation of a moral reality that exists wherever language is used to mislead.
