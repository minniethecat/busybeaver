## Definition

[[Pity]] occupies an uneasy place in [[Aesop's Fables]]. Unlike [[kindness]] or [[mercy]], which are presented as unambiguous virtues, [[mercy|pity]] is treated with a distinctive wariness. Aesop repeatedly warns that [[mercy|pity]], when d[[anger|ire]]cted at the wrong object, is not merely wasted but actively harmful — to the pitier, to the pitied, or to both.

The fables draw a sharp line between [[mercy|pity]] as an emotion (involuntary, morally neutral) and [[mercy|pity]] as an action (voluntary, morally consequential). Feeling [[mercy|pity]] for a scamp is natural; acting on that [[mercy|pity]] is disastrous. The instruction "Do not waste your [[mercy|pity]] on a scamp" is not a call to hard-heartedness but a call to discernment — to distinguish between those who suffer [[fortune|misfortune]] and those who manufacture suffering as a strategy (039-002).

> "Do not waste your pity on a scamp." (039-002)

## Varieties of Pity

Aesop's taxonomy of [[mercy|pity]] is, at its core, a taxonomy of the objects of [[mercy|pity]]:

- **Pity wasted on the ungrateful** — the most emphatically warned-against form of [[mercy|pity]] in the fables. The Countryman who warms the frozen Serpent in his bosom is Aesop's paradigmatic case. The Serpent did not ask for [[mercy|pity]], did not pretend to deserve it, and bit as soon as it was physically capable of doing so. The Countryman's error is not in feeling [[mercy|pity]] but in failing to check *what* he was [[mercy|pity]]ing. A frozen Serpent is still a Serpent; warmth does not change nature, it only restores capacity (157-001).
- **Pity wasted on the self-inflicted** — the Boy who complains of nettle-stings after brushing them lightly. His mother's response is instructive: the nettles stung because you failed to grasp them firmly. Pity here is not merely wasted but counterproductive — it rewards and rein[[strength|force]]s the very weakness that caused the injury. Aesop's insight is that misplaced [[mercy|pity]] enables incompetence (039-002).
- **Pity as contempt** — the File sneers at the Viper who wastes its venom on unyielding metal. The File's "[[mercy|pity]]ing contempt" is not [[mercy|compassion]] but condescension — [[mercy|pity]] mixed with superiority. Aesop recognizes this as a distinct emotional register: "What a simpleton you are to expect to hurt me" (115-001). This is [[mercy|pity]] as [[strength|power]] dynamic, not [[mercy|pity]] as virtue.
- **Pity as genuine virtue** — the Mouse who gnaws through the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s net does so out of a combination of [[mercy|pity]] and gratitude (the [[the-lion|Lion]] had earlier spared the Mouse). This is the rare case where [[mercy|pity]] is correctly d[[anger|ire]]cted and correctly acted upon. The object of [[mercy|pity]] is genuinely suffering, genuinely blameless, and genuinely capable of being helped without danger to the helper (017-001).

> The Lion, trapped in the hunter's net, roared in anguish. The Mouse, hearing the roar and recognizing the Lion who had spared her life, came running. Seeing what had happened, out of pity for his distress she gnawed at the ropes until the Lion was free. (017-001)

## Consequences

The [[punishment|consequence]]s of [[mercy|pity]] in Aesop map d[[anger|ire]]ctly to the discernment of the pitier:

- **Pity wisely given** leads to reciprocal benefit. The Mouse who pities the [[the-lion|Lion]] in the net gains nothing d[[anger|ire]]ctly but solidifies a relationship of mutual [[safety|protection]]. Pity here is not charity but investment (017-001).
- **Pity unwisely given** leads to the [[ruin|destruction]] of the pitier. The Countryman who pities the Serpent dies of its venom. The point is not that [[mercy|pity]] is wrong but that [[mercy|pity]] must be accompanied by accurate assessment of its object (157-001).
- **Pity withheld** — the Mother who refuses to [[mercy|pity]] her son's nettle-stings — is Aesop's most counterintuitive [[advice|recommendation]]. Not-[[mercy|pity]]ing can be the truest form of care, because [[mercy|pity]] sometimes enables the very weakness it means to soothe (039-002).

## Related Concepts

- [[compassion]] — distinct from [[mercy|pity]]; [[mercy|compassion]] in Aesop implies action to relieve suffering, while [[mercy|pity]] may remain purely emotional
- [[mercy]] — the decision not to harm when one has the [[strength|power]] to do so; the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s mercy to the Mouse is the precondition for the Mouse's [[mercy|pity]] toward the [[the-lion|Lion]]
- [[kindness]] — the broader virtue of which wisely-d[[anger|ire]]cted [[mercy|pity]] is one expression; the Countryman's kindness is genuine but fatally misd[[anger|ire]]cted
- [[ingratitude]] — what [[mercy|pity]] risks enabling; the Serpent's bite is ingratitude in its purest form
- [[cruelty]] — what the refusal of [[mercy|pity]] may look like from the outside; but Aesop insists that not all refusals of [[mercy|pity]] are cruel — some are merely discerning
