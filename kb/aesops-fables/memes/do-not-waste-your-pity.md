## Statement

"Do not waste your [[mercy|pity]] on a scamp" is the explicit moral of The Flea and the Man (039-002). When a Flea that has bitten a Man repeatedly pleads for [[pity|mercy]] on the grounds of being too small to do real harm, the Man refuses: whatever is deliberately harmful must be destroyed, no matter how [[insult|slight]] the damage. The moral is not about cruelty — it is about the asymmetry between the harm-doer's self-assessment and the victim's experience.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Flea and the Man

A Flea bites a Man repeatedly until he can stand it no longer. He catches the Flea and holds it between finger and thumb. The Flea whimpers in [[fear|terror]]: "Oh, sir! pray let me go; don't kill me! I am such a little thing that I can't do you much harm."

> The Man laughed and said, "I am going to kill you now, at once: whatever is bad has got to be destroyed, no matter how slight the harm it does." (039-001)

The Man's reasoning is categorical — not proportional. The Flea's argument is quantitative (small harm), but the Man's judgment is qualitative (bad intent). This distinction is what the moral encodes: a scamp will always minimize their own [[guilt|wrongdoing]].

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf, determined to eat a Lamb, fabricates a series of [[blame|accusation]]s. When each is refuted, the Wolf simply invents another. The Lamb's innocence is irrelevant — the Wolf's intent is fixed, and no appeal to reason or [[mercy|pity]] can change it (009-001). Like the Flea, the Wolf is a scamp; unlike the Flea, the Wolf is strong. But the moral applies equally: recognizing a scamp and withholding [[pity|sympathy]] is the victim's only defense.

### The Fox and the Eagle (By Cunning, Not Force)

[[the-fox|The Fox]], unable to procure food by honest means, feigns death in a cave and catches prey that come to investigate. [[the-fox|The Fox]] is a scamp who succeeds by [[disguise|deception]] rather than [[strength|force]], and those who [[mercy|pity]] the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s apparent weakness walk into a trap (063-001). The fable rein[[strength|force]]s the moral: [[mercy|pity]] for the [[cleverness|cunning]] is self-destructive.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **asymmetric minimization**. A scamp will always describe their harm as trivial ("I am such a little thing"), while the victim experiences it as intolerable. The Flea's plea recasts the Man's suffering as overreaction — a rhetorical move that every bully and cheat makes. Aesop's insight is that the plea itself is evidence of the scamp's character: someone who genuinely regretted the harm would not argue about its magnitude.

This mechanism operates across the fable corpus whenever a weaker wrongdoer appeals for [[pity|mercy]]. The Man's response — "I am going to kill you now, at once" — is swift and final, not because he is cruel but because he recognizes that negotiation with a scamp is a trap.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[persuasion-is-better-than-force]] — [[persuasion|Persuasion]] is the legitimate alternative to [[strength|force]]; the Flea's plea is a counterfeit of per[[persuasion|suasion]], deploying the form without the substance. The Sun persuades; the Flea manipulates.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Flea's overestimation of its own importance (thinking it too small to harm) mirrors [[The Eagle and the Arrow]]'s fatal pride. Scamps are often proud scamps.
- [[better-servitude-with-safety]] — Both morals address the terms of survival under [[danger|threat]]. The Flea asks for safety without servitude; the Man answers that safety is earned, not begged.

## Enduring Influence

"Do not waste your [[mercy|pity]] on a scamp" has not survived as an English proverb with the same prominence as other Aesopic morals, but its logic pervades English common law — the principle that a defendant's character and past conduct are relevant to sentencing. The fable itself appears in the Perry Index as number 272.
