## Statement

"Men do not see their own [[blame|fault]]s, but never fail to see those of others" is the explicit moral of The Two Bags (073-001). Aesop gives the diagnosis in a single compressed image:

> "Every man carries Two Bags about with him, one in front and one behind, and both are packed full of faults. The Bag in front contains his neighbours' faults, the one behind his own. Hence it is that men do not see their own faults, but never fail to see those of others." (073-001)

The fable is not a narrative but an allegorical description — a diagram of human moral perception. No animal disguises the point; the statement is delivered as anthropological fact. The spatial arrangement is precise: the neighbour's [[blame|fault]]s are *in front* where they fill the visual field; one's own [[blame|fault]]s are *behind* where they are invisible. This is not a moral failure so much as a structural feature of human cognition — the mind is arranged so that self-examination requ[[anger|ire]]s an effort that [[blame|fault]]-finding in others does not.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Two Bags

The fable itself is a single-paragraph diagnosis rather than a narrative. Its [[strength|force]] comes from the image of the two bags, which externalises an internal asymmetry. The man does not choose to ignore his own [[blame|fault]]s; the bag containing them is physically behind him. He would need to turn around — to make a deliberate effort at self-scrutiny — to see them. The neighbour's bag, by contrast, is always in view.

### The Fox and the Mask

In [[the-fox|The Fox]] and the Mask (fable-029), a [[the-fox|Fox]] finds an actor's mask and remarks, "What a fine face this is! What a [[mercy|pity]] it has no brains." [[the-fox|The Fox]] sees the mask's emptiness immediately, but the observation is d[[anger|ire]]cted outward — at someone else's surface without substance. The fable mirrors the Two Bags mechanism: it is easier to diagnose hollowness in others than to ask whether one's own face conceals a vacancy.

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin

In The Ass in the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s Skin (fable-194), an Ass disguises himself as a [[the-lion|Lion]] and terrifies everyone until his ears give him away. The Ass sees the [[fear|terror]] he produces but not the absurdity of his disguise. He cannot see his own ears — they are in the bag behind his back. [[the-fox|The Fox]] who unmasks him sees what the Ass cannot: the gap between performance and reality.

### The Mole and His Mother

In The Mole and His Mother (fable-050), a young Mole insists he can see, contradicting his mother's knowledge that moles are blind. The mother tests him by asking what a certain object is; the Mole guesses incense and is proved wrong. The Mole's insistence on a faculty he lacks — and his inability to perceive his own blindness — is the Two Bags in miniature: the Mole's [[blame|fault]] is invisible to himself and obvious to everyone else.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **perceptual asymmetry by position**. The two-bag image is not a metaphor for hypocrisy (conscious [[disguise|concealment]] of [[blame|fault]]) but for a deeper cognitive condition: the informational architecture of self-perception makes self-[[blame|fault]] invisible by de[[blame|fault]]. Three structural factors are at work:

1. **Field of view**: The neighbour's [[blame|fault]]s are placed in the visual field; one's own are placed outside it. The asymmetry is spatial before it is moral.
2. **No feedback loop**: The bags are carried, not inspected. There is no mechanism in the image for the man to see what is behind him — no mirror, no companion, no turn of the head.
3. **Equal distribution of [[blame|fault]]**: Both bags are "packed full of [[blame|fault]]s." The distribution is symmetric; only the perception is asymmetric. Every man has [[blame|fault]]s equal in quantity to his neighbour's — this is a remarkably egalitarian diagnosis.

The moral's brilliance is that it indicts the observer along with the observed. To say "men do not see their own [[blame|fault]]s" is to include oneself in the diagnosis. The reader who nods in agreement is simultaneously confessing.

## Enduring Influence

The Two Bags is one of Aesop's most enduring images because it converts a moral observation into a spatial diagram. The fable has passed into common idiom: "seeing the mote in your brother's eye but not the beam in your own" (Matthew 7:3) is the same structure with different furniture. In modern psychology, the fable anticipates the *fundamental attribution error* — the tendency to attribute one's own failures to circumstances and others' failures to character. The Two Bags gives the same insight without experimental apparatus: the neighbour's [[blame|fault]]s are character; my own are luggage I cannot see. The fable also underlies the classical injunction *know thyself* (γνῶθι σεαυτόν), which assumes that self-knowledge requ[[anger|ire]]s active effort precisely because the de[[blame|fault]] state is ignorance of one's own interior.
