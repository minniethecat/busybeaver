## Statement

"[[honesty|Honesty]] is the best policy" is the explicit moral of Mercury and the Woodman (017-002). A Woodman loses his axe in a river. Mercury retrieves a golden axe and asks: was this yours? The Woodman says no. Mercury then retrieves a silver axe; again, the Woodman says no. Only when his own ordinary axe is recovered does he claim it. Mercury, pleased, gives him all three.

> Mercury was so pleased with his honesty that he made him a present of the other two axes. (017-001)

The dishonest companion, attempting the same trick, screams "That's mine!" at the golden axe — and Mercury gives him nothing, refusing even to recover the axe he actually lost.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### Mercury and the Woodman (primary)

The fable is structurally a **test**. Mercury does not reward the Woodman for piety or service but for accuracy: the Woodman correctly identifies which axe is his. The reward for honesty is surplus — the Woodman came to recover one axe and leaves with three. The dishonest companion came to game the system and leaves with nothing (017-001).

### The Eagle and the Arrow

An Eagle, shot by an arrow fletched with his own feathers, laments that he has furnished his enemy with the means of his [[ruin|destruction]]. [[honesty|Honesty]] about one's own complicity is the bitterest truth (028-001).

### Truth and the Traveller

Aesop gives truth its own fable in The Man and the Satyr, where a man who blows both hot and cold with the same breath is banished: "I will have no dealings with a man who can blow hot and cold with the same breath" (044-001). [[dishonesty|Dishonesty]] as inconsistency is structurally recognized and socially rejected.

## Mechanism

Aesop's version of this moral is not a broad endorsement of truthfulness. It operates as a **transaction rule**: when a more [[strength|power]]ful party offers a test, honesty produces surplus; [[disguise|deception]] produces deficit. The Woodman's reward is not abstract virtue but concrete material gain — the moral earns its "policy" label because it is instrumentally rational, not just ethically correct.

This mechanism differs from the related moral [[dishonest-get-no-credit]] (143-002), which addresses reputation effects: the liar who reforms is still not believed. "[[honesty|Honesty]] is the best policy" is about the **single encounter** — even without a reputation history, the honest choice pays better.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[dishonest-get-no-credit]] — The Woodman's companion is the profile of the person who gets no credit: he lies once and loses everything. Reputation amplifies honesty's returns across repeated encounters.

- [[truth]] — [[truth|Truth]] in Aesop is not a passive description but an active allegiance. The Woodman aligns his words with reality and is enriched; the companion aligns his words with his des[[anger|ire]]s and is impoverished.

- [[greed]] — The companion's error is fundamentally greed: he wants what is not his. [[honesty|Honesty]] and greed are mutually exclusive stances toward possessions.
