## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], discontent is the great self-inflicted wound. It is not imposed by enemies or circumstance — it is generated from within, a restless conviction that one's present condition is insufficient and that any change must be an improvement. The corpus treats this conviction as almost always wrong.

Discontent in Aesop is **perpetual**: the discontented character is never satisfied. The Frogs trade one King for another and then for a third. The Ass changes masters twice and each new master is worse. The Peacock, given beauty beyond all other birds, fixates on the one gift he lacks. The fables converge on a single lesson: those who cannot be content with what they have will end with less.

> Fate has allotted to all their destined gifts... but you alone are dissatisfied with your portion. ([[fable-097|097-001]])

## Varieties

**Political Discontent.** The Frogs in [[fable-076|076-002]] are "discontented because they had no one to rule over them." Jupiter, "despising the [[foolishness|folly]] of their request," gives them a Log-King. The Frogs grow bolder, show [[contempt]] for the Log, and demand a replacement — receiving the Stork, who devours them. Political discontent, Aesop suggests, is a des[[anger|ire]] that can always rationalize itself but can never be satisfied: each new ruler will be worse than the last, because the problem is not the ruler but the restlessness that demands one.

**Aspirational Discontent.** The Tortoise in [[fable-081|081-001]] is "discontented with his lowly life, and envious of the birds." He begs the Eagle to teach him to fly, ignoring the Eagle's protest that "nature had not provided him with wings." The Tortoise insists it is "only a question of learning the craft of the air." The Eagle releases him from a great height, and he is "dashed to pieces on a rock." The discontent that denies natural limits is the discontent that destroys.

**Aesthetic Discontent.** The Peacock in [[fable-097|097-001]] is "greatly discontented because he had not a beautiful voice." Juno enumerates his gifts — "your neck flashes like the emerald and your splendid tail is a marvel of gorgeous colour" — but the Peacock is not appeased. Juno's reply is Aesop's definitive statement on discontent: each creature has its al[[fate|lot]]ted gifts; to fixate on the one gift withheld is to poison enjoyment of all gifts received.

**Chronic Discontent.** The Ass in [[fable-200|200-001]] and the Slave in [[fable-270|270-001]] illustrate the pattern of perpetual dissatisfaction. The Ass, transferred from Gardener to Potter to Tanner, is "as discontented as ever" — each new situation is worse, not because the Ass is un[[fortune|luck]]y but because discontent guarantees dissatisfaction with any situation. The Runaway Slave, fleeing his master, hides in a treadmill — and his master, finding him there, remarks with grim irony: "that's the very place for you."

## Consequences

Discontent in Aesop consistently produces **worsening conditions**. The discontented character begins in a tolerable situation and, through the pursuit of something better, arrives at something worse. This is not [[misfortune|bad luck]] — it is structural. Discontent narrows perception to what is lacking and blinds the discontented to what they risk losing. The Frogs do not see that the Log-King, however undignified, does not eat them; the Tortoise does not see that walking on earth is safer than falling from the sky.

Aesop's [[advice|counsel]] is not passivity but clarity: to distinguish between what can be changed and what must be accepted. The fables do not condemn ambition — they condemn the ambition that is really just restlessness in disguise.

## Relation to Envy

Discontent and [[envy]] are the twin engines of dissatisfaction in Aesop. [[Envy]] looks outward — at what another possesses. Discontent looks inward — at what oneself lacks. The Tortoise in [[fable-081|081-001]] is both discontented with his own life and envious of the birds; the two conditions feed each other.

But Aesop draws a distinction: [[envy]] is d[[anger|ire]]cted at a specific other and can sometimes be cured by seeing that other's disadvantages. Discontent is d[[anger|ire]]cted at existence itself and has no cure except the wisdom that Juno offers the Peacock — to see one's own gifts clearly, and to stop comparing.
