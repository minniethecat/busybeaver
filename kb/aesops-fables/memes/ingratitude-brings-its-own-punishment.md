## Statement

"[[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] sometimes brings its own punishment" is the explicit moral of The Stag and the Vine (173-002). A Stag pursued by huntsmen conceals himself under a thick Vine. The huntsmen pass by without detecting him. "Supposing all danger to be over, he presently began to browse on the leaves of the Vine" (173-001). The movement draws the huntsmen back; they shoot an arrow into the foliage and pierce the Stag to the heart. As he exp[[anger|ire]]s, the Stag speaks his own verdict: "I deserve my [[fortune|fate]] for my treachery in feeding upon the leaves of my protector" (173-001). The moral is distinctive among Aesop's pronouncements because the punishment is not dispensed by a judge, a god, or a stronger party — it is an automatic [[punishment|consequence]] of the ingratitude itself.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Stag and the Vine

The Vine shelters the Stag from death. The Stag repays this shelter by eating the Vine's leaves — an act that is not merely ungrateful but self-destructive:

> "They lost track of him and passed by his hiding-place without being aware that he was anywhere near. Supposing all danger to be over, he presently began to browse on the leaves of the Vine. The movement drew the attention of the returning huntsmen, and one of them, supposing some animal to be hidden there, shot an arrow at a venture into the foliage." (173-001)

The Vine does not need to punish the Stag; the Stag punishes himself. His ingratitude — eating the leaves — is the very act that betrays his position. The mechanism is not revenge but **causal feedback**: the ingratitude itself creates the conditions of punishment.

> "The unlucky Stag was pierced to the heart, and, as he expired, he said, 'I deserve my fate for my treachery in feeding upon the leaves of my protector.'" (173-001)

### The Farmer and the Viper

In the fable of The Farmer and the Viper, a Farmer rescues a frozen snake and carries it to his hearth. Revived by the warmth, the snake bites its rescuer. The Viper, like the Stag, repays kindness with harm. But the structural difference is instructive: the Viper bites because it is a viper — its nature dictates its conduct. The Stag eats the Vine's leaves not because it is a stag but because it is ungrateful. The Viper's act is predation; the Stag's act is ingratitude — a moral failing, not an instinctive one.

### The Wolf and the Crane

A Wolf gets a bone stuck in his throat and h[[anger|ire]]s a Crane to extract it with her long beak, promising a reward. When the Crane succeeds and asks for payment, the Wolf replies that not being eaten is payment enough. The Crane, unlike the Stag, performs a service and is cheated — not punished. The Stag receives shelter and repays it with [[ruin|destruction]]. Both fables concern the relationship between service and reciprocity, but they occupy opposite poles of the moral spectrum.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **self-defeating ingratitude**: the act of ingratitude d[[anger|ire]]ctly triggers the punishment. This is not a moralising claim that "the universe punishes ingratitude." It is a structural claim: biting the hand that feeds you (or, in this case, eating the leaves that hide you) exposes you to the very danger from which you were rescued.

Three structural features are at work:

1. **The benefactor-protector dual role**: The Vine is simultaneously the Stag's benefactor (providing shelter) and a vulnerable party (its leaves are food). The Stag fails to perceive that harming the Vine in its role as food-source eliminates its role as shelter-provider.
2. **Consequence as d[[anger|ire]]ct causation**: The huntsmen do not track the Stag by scent or skill; they are drawn by the movement of the Vine's leaves. The Stag's ingratitude is not detected by a moral observer — it is detected by the physical world, in the form of rustling foliage.
3. **Self-condemnation**: The Stag does not die blaming the huntsmen, [[misfortune|bad luck]], or the Vine. He dies saying "I deserve my [[fortune|fate]]." The punishment is not only causal — it is recognised as just by the punished party himself.

## Enduring Influence

The fable is a paradigmatic illustration of what game theorists call a *self-undermining strategy*: a move that yields a short-term benefit (a mouthful of leaves) at the cost of destroying the conditions that made the benefit possible (the Vine's shelter). The phrase "biting the hand that feeds you" captures the same logic in English, and the Stag's dying confession — "I deserve my [[fortune|fate]]" — makes this fable one of the few in the Aesopic corpus where the victim of punishment explicitly endorses the justice of his punishment. In this sense the fable anticipates the concept of *tragic recognition* (anagnorisis): the moment when a character realises that their own actions, not external [[fortune|fate]], have brought about their [[ruin|destruction]].
