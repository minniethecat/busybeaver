## Statement

"Precautions are useless after the event" is the explicit moral of The Cage-Bird and the Bat (189-002). A Singing-bird, kept in a cage hanging outside a window, sings only at night. A Bat, clinging to the bars, asks why. The Bird explains: she was caught by a fowler who was attracted by her daytime singing. Now, to avoid the same [[fortune|fate]], she sings only in darkness. The Bat's reply is devastating: "It is no use your doing that now when you are a prisoner: if only you had done so before you were caught, you [[power|might]] still have been free" (189-001). The Bird's precaution is perfectly sensible — *for a free bird*. For a caged one, it accomplishes nothing. The risk it guards against (being caught) has already materialised. The moral is not that caution is wrong, but that caution applied to a risk that has already crystallised is not caution at all — it is theatre.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Cage-Bird and the Bat

The fable's structure is a Socratic dialogue in miniature: the Bird states her policy, and the Bat reveals its absurdity with a single sentence:

> A Singing-bird was confined in a cage which hung outside a window, and had a way of singing at night when all other birds were asleep. One night a Bat came and clung to the bars of the cage, and asked the Bird why she was silent by day and sang only at night. "I have a very good reason for doing so," said the Bird: "it was once when I was singing in the daytime that a fowler was attracted by my voice, and set his nets for me and caught me. Since then I have never sung except by night." But the Bat replied, "It is no use your doing that now when you are a prisoner: if only you had done so before you were caught, you might still have been free." (189-001)

The Bat's reply is the fable's ent[[anger|ire]] moral in one line. The Bird has treated the cause of her imprisonment (daytime singing) as a variable she can still control, rather than as a condition that applied only in her previous state. The fowler is no longer a [[danger|threat]] — she is already caught. The [[danger|threat]] she faces now is not capture, but permanent imprisonment.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

The Lamb is the Cage-Bird's tragic precursor (015-001). When the Wolf accuses the Lamb of muddying his drinking water, the Lamb points out that the water flows from Wolf to Lamb, not the reverse — making it impossible for the Lamb to be the cause. The Wolf ignores the logic and eats the Lamb anyway. The Lamb's defence is factually correct but practically useless, because the Wolf never needed a real reason. The Bird's precaution is the same kind of error: it addresses a [[danger|threat]] that no longer exists while ignoring the [[danger|threat]] that does.

### The Fox Who Lost His Tail

A [[the-fox|Fox]] caught in a trap escapes but leaves his tail behind (023-001). Rather than admit his loss, he tries to persuade the other [[the-fox|fox]]es that taillessness is fashionable. A wise old [[the-fox|Fox]] replies: "You only advise us to cut off our tails because you have lost your own." [[the-fox|The Fox]] Who Lost His Tail is the Cage-Bird's mirror image: the Bird adopts a useless precaution for herself; the [[the-fox|Fox]] urges all [[the-fox|fox]]es to adopt the condition he was [[strength|force]]d into. Both are post-hoc adaptations to irreversible losses.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **[[danger|hazard]] inversion**: treating a [[danger|threat]] that has already materialised as if it were still prospective. The structure has three components:

1. **Temporal displacement**: The Bird's mind remains in the pre-capture state, when the fowler was a prospective [[danger|threat]]. She has not updated her [[danger|threat]] model to account for the fact that "being caught" is no longer a risk but a reality. The fowler cannot catch her again — she is already caught.

2. **Substitution of the obsolete precaution**: Unable to undo the capture, the Bird does the only thing she can do — she replicates the precaution that *would have worked* if applied earlier. The act of singing only at night is the Bird's penance: it makes her feel as though she is doing something, when in fact she is doing nothing that matters.

3. **Cosmetic agency**: The precaution provides the illusion of control without any actual control. The Bird's policy change — "I have a very good reason" — sounds rational. The Bat's reply shows that it is merely rational-seeming behaviour produced by a decision-theoretic framework that has not been updated to reflect the changed state of the world.

This mechanism is visible wherever people install burglar [[fear|alarm]]s after being robbed, take vitamins after falling ill, or resolve to save money after going bankrupt. The precaution would have been valuable *ex ante*; *ex post*, it is empty ritual.

## Enduring Influence

The moral has become a proverb in English through the expression "closing the stable door after the horse has bolted" — the image of a farmer locking the door once the animal is already gone. Both the fable and the proverb capture the same ironic futility: the act of securing is still taking place, but the thing to be secured has already been lost. In risk management, the concept is formalised as the distinction between *prospective risk* (a [[danger|threat]] that may materialise) and *crystallised risk* (a loss that has already occurred); precautions that address the former are valuable, while precautions that address the latter are an exercise in delusion. The fable has also been applied to public policy: a government that tightens [[safety|security]] regulations only after a disaster has already occurred is the Cage-Bird — the policy is correct in form but irrelevant to the event that prompted it, because that event has already passed.
