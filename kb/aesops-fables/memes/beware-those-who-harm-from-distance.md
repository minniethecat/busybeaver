## Statement

"Give a wide berth to those who can do damage at a distance" is the explicit moral of [[The Archer and the Lion]] (175-002). An Archer goes hunting in the hills. All the animals flee at the sight of him — except the [[the-lion|Lion]], who stands his ground and challenges the man to fight. The Archer shoots an arrow that strikes the [[the-lion|Lion]], and says:

> "There, you see what my messenger can do: just you wait a moment and I'll tackle you myself." (175-001)

[[the-lion|The Lion]], feeling the arrow's sting, runs. A [[the-fox|Fox]] taunts him for [[coward|cowardice]]. [[the-lion|The Lion]] replies: "You won't get me to stay, not you: why, when he sends a messenger like that before him, he must himself be a terrible fellow to deal with."

[[the-lion|The Lion]] is not a coward. He is a **strategist** who reasons from the arrow to the archer: if the Archer's projectile — something the Archer dispatches without risking himself — can wound, then the Archer in d[[anger|ire]]ct combat must be far more dangerous. The moral captures the asymmetry of risk in ranged combat.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Archer and the Lion

The Archer shoots from a distance. [[the-lion|The Lion]], built for close combat, cannot reach him. The arrow is a "messenger" — a proxy that extends the Archer's reach beyond the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s. [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s decision to flee is not panic but inference: the proxy is formidable. [[the-fox|The Fox]], who has not been shot, misreads the situation as [[coward|cowardice]] because he only sees the flight, not the wound (175-001).

### The Lion and the Mouse

A [[the-lion|Lion]] spares a Mouse, who later gnaws through the net that traps the [[the-lion|Lion]] (019-001). The Mouse's harm comes from a position of insignificance — the [[the-lion|Lion]] cannot fight a mouse, because it operates at a scale below the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s capabilities.

### The Eagle and the Arrow

An Eagle is shot by an arrow fletched with eagle feathers. Dying, he says: "It is a double grief to me that I should perish by an arrow feathered from my own wings" (110-001).

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf, wanting an excuse to eat a Lamb, accuses the Lamb of various offenses from a position of [[strength|power]]. The Lamb answers each [[blame|accusation]] logically, but logic is no defense (011-001). The fable illustrates the same asymmetry as the Archer: one party can harm the other without [[punishment|consequence]]. [[the-lion|The Lion]] at least can run; the Lamb cannot.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **reach asymmetry**: the Archer can harm the [[the-lion|Lion]] without entering the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s attack radius. In close combat, the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s strength would be decisive; at range, the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s strength is irrelevant. The Arrow is a technology that collapses the spatial gap between predator and prey.

[[the-lion|The Lion]]'s reasoning is an example of **transitive inference from proxy to principal**: the arrow is an extension of the Archer, and the Archer must be at least as dangerous as his extension. This is not [[coward|cowardice]] — it is rationality. [[the-lion|The Lion]] correctly assesses that the expected outcome of closing the distance is worse than the expected outcome of retreat.
