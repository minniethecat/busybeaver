## Statement

"Heaven helps those who help themselves" is the explicit moral of [[Hercules and the Waggoner]] (102-002). A Waggoner's cart sinks deep into the mud. His horses cannot move it. Rather than pushing, the Waggoner stands helplessly and calls upon Hercules — the god of strength — to rescue him. Hercules appears:

> "Put your shoulder to the wheel, man, and goad on your horses, and then you may call on Hercules to assist you. If you won't lift a finger to help yourself, you can't expect Hercules or any one else to come to your aid." (102-001)

Hercules does not refuse to help. He refuses to help **first**. The Waggoner must exhaust his own effort before the god will add his. The moral is not that heaven is indifferent but that heaven's help is a multiplier, not a substitute, and multiplication by zero yields zero.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### Hercules and the Waggoner

The Waggoner's wagon is stuck. He calls to Hercules without touching the wheel. Hercules appears and delivers the moral as a d[[anger|ire]]ct command: act first, then pray. The fable is a theological argument in miniature — it redefines the relationship between human agency and divine intervention from substitution to partnership (102-001).

### The Lark and Her Young Ones

A Lark nests in a field of green corn. When the corn ripens, she tells her young ones to listen for the farmer's plans. The farmer first says he will ask his friends to help reap; the Lark does nothing. Then he says he will ask his relatives; still the Lark waits.

Finally the farmer says, "Tomorrow I will come and reap the corn myself." At this the Lark says, "Now it is time to go," and moves her nest (209-001). The Lark knows what the Waggoner does not: help from others is unreliable; only when the actor commits to acting himself does the situation become real.

### The Father and His Sons (The Bundle of Sticks)

A dying Father asks his sons to break a bundle of sticks. None can. He unties the bundle and gives each son a single stick, which they break easily (058-001).

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **effort-gated assistance**. Hercules will add his strength only after the Waggoner has exhausted his own. The god's [[strength|power]] is not denied — it is conditionally offered. This is a sophisticated model of aid that avoids two opposite errors: the error of believing no help is available (despair) and the error of believing help will come without effort (entitlement).

The fable's position is that the Waggoner's prayer is not wrong — it is **premature**. He has skipped the step where he demonstrates that he is serious. The demonstration serves two functions: it proves need (if the Waggoner could free the cart himself, he wouldn't need Hercules) and it proves commitment (the Waggoner is not merely lazy).

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[do-not-attempt-too-much]] — The complementary moral: while the Waggoner errs by attempting too little, the moral of [[The Boy and the Filberts]] warns against attempting too much at once (075-002). Together they define the optimal zone of effort.

- [[example-is-better-than-precept]] — Hercules does not merely tell the Waggoner to push. He models the principle that the god himself will not act without human effort first. The god's own behavior is the example that teaches the precept.

- [[persuasion-is-better-than-force]] — Hercules could have freed the wagon by [[strength|force]]. Instead he persuades the Waggoner to act. The god of strength chooses per[[persuasion|suasion]] over [[strength|force]], showing that even omnipotence respects the boundary of human agency.

## Enduring Influence

"Heaven helps those who help themselves" (often "God helps those who help themselves") has become one of the most widely quoted proverbs in English. It is frequently misattributed to the Bible and appears in Benjamin Franklin's *Poor Richard's Almanack*. The phrase "put your shoulder to the wheel" has entered English as an idiom. The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 291.
