The [[the-lion|Master]] appears throughout Aesop's Fables as the archetypal authority figure — the one who owns, commands, judges, and ultimately decides the fate of those beneath him. Unlike the [[the-lion|Lion]] who rules by raw strength in the wild, the Master operates within the human social order, representing the power dynamics between owner and owned, employer and servant, ruler and subject. His presence in the fables illuminates timeless questions about [[justice]], [[loyalty]], and the moral obligations that power entails.

## Role in Aesop

The Master serves as both a narrative device and a moral touchstone in Aesop's Fables. He appears as the owner of domestic animals — dogs, asses, horses, hounds — and as the holder of authority over slaves and servants. In this role, the Master's decisions and judgments become the mechanism through which moral lessons about power, gratitude, and desert are delivered. The fables consistently probe whether the Master is just or arbitrary, grateful or ungrateful, perceptive or blind to the true character of those he commands.

> "A Hound who had served his master well for years, and had run down many a quarry in his time, began to show the first signs of age." (126-001)

The Master figure recurs across at least seventeen fables (PN=004-001, 028-001, 032-001, 038-001, 045-001, 053-001, 085-001, 092-001, 099-001, 105-001, 118-001, 126-001, 144-001, 150-001, 190-001, 200-001, 201-001), making him one of the most frequently appearing human archetypes in the collection.

## Key Appearances

**The Hound and His Master** (Fable 126). An aging Hound who has served his master faithfully for years can no longer hunt as he once did. When he fails to hold a quarry, his master beats him. The Hound protests that he should be honored for what he was, not punished for what he is. This fable exposes the Master's ingratitude — valuing utility over loyalty.

**The Ass and His Masters** (Fable 099). An Ass, burdened by a cruel master, prays for a new one. [[jupiter|Jupiter]] grants his wish, but the new master proves even harsher. When the Ass prays again, he receives a tanner who works him to death. The fable teaches that change is not always improvement: one may exchange a known burden for a worse one.

> "Servants don't know a good master till they have served a worse." (200-002)

**The Runaway Slave** (Fable 038). A Slave flees his cruel master and hides in a cave, only to find a [[the-lion|Lion]] inside. Trapped between two dangers, he learns that escaping one master may lead straight into the jaws of another — a stark parable about the limits of flight from authority.

**The Ass in the Lion's Skin** (Fable 190). When an Ass disguises himself in a lion's skin, his master sees through the deception and puts a halter on him, saying, "You may frighten others with that skin, but I know you for the ass you are." The Master here embodies discernment — the ability to see through pretense.

**The Horse and His Rider** (Fable 118). A war-horse, once swift and proud in battle, is reduced to carrying heavy loads for his master in old age. The Master's treatment shifts with utility — a commentary on how power relationships erode when the subordinate's usefulness declines.

## Relationship with the Ass

The [[the-master|Master]]–[[ass]] relationship is the most instructive in the collection. The Ass, a beast of burden, is entirely dependent on his master's will, yet the fables consistently show the Master undervaluing or overworking the Ass. In Fable 045, the Master overloads the Ass until he collapses; in Fable 099, each successive master proves crueler than the last. The dynamic embodies an asymmetric power relation where the subordinate's welfare depends entirely on the master's character — and that character is often found wanting.

## Symbolism

The Master symbolizes the hierarchical principle itself — the inescapable truth that most creatures live under the authority of another. In Aesop's moral universe, the Master is neither inherently good nor evil; rather, his virtue depends on how he exercises power. The fables collectively warn that those in authority are prone to ingratitude, short-sightedness, and a tendency to value present utility over past service. At the same time, fables like PN=200-002 suggest that servants are equally fallible — they fail to recognize a good master until they have experienced a worse one, making the Master a mirror for human discontent as much as for tyranny.
