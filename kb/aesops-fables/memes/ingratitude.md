# Ingratitude

Ingratitude — the refusal to acknowledge or repay a benefit — appears in [[Aesop's Fables]] as a uniquely offensive vice, one that attacks not just the benefactor but the ent[[anger|ire]] fabric of mutual obligation. Unlike [[greed]], which simply wants more, ingratitude **negates what has already been given**. The fables present it as a short-sighted [[treachery|betrayal]]: the ungrateful character gains a momentary advantage but destroys the relationship that could have provided future benefits.

## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, ingratitude is the active negation of a received benefit — not merely forgetting to say thank you, but denying that the benefit was given at all, or turning against the benefactor after receiving help. The corpus identifies two characteristic forms: (1) **abusive ingratitude** — attacking the benefactor while still enjoying the benefit, and (2) **abandoning ingratitude** — accepting benefits during hardship and then deserting the benefactor when conditions improve. Both forms are treated as not just morally wrong but **practically self-defeating**: they destroy the reputation on which future help depends.

## Varieties

**Abusive Ingratitude.** The Traveller rests under the Plane-tree's shade while calling it useless ([[166-001]]). He is not merely unthankful — he actively insults the benefactor while still receiving the benefit. This is the most offensive form because it combines ingratitude with contempt: the benefactor is not just forgotten but devalued. The Plane-tree's retort — spoken while still providing shade — captures the absurdity of the position.

**Misd[[anger|ire]]cted Ingratitude.** The Bees sting their Bee-keeper instead of the actual thief ([[218-001]]). Their ingratitude is compounded by error: they attack the wrong target, punishing the person who cared for them while the real culprit escapes. This variety shows that ingratitude often carries an information cost — the ungrateful character, in the heat of grievance, fails to identify who actually deserves blame.

**Strategic Ingratitude.** The Wild Goats accept the G[[promise|oath]]erd's extra rations during the storm but flee the moment they reach the hills ([[282-001]]). Their ingratitude is calculating rather than emotional — they took what was offered with no intention of staying. But the Goat's parting words reveal the strategic flaw: by treating newcomers better than his own flock, the G[[promise|oath]]erd signaled that loyalty is punished in his household. The Goats' ingratitude was, in their own frame, a rational response to the G[[promise|oath]]erd's [[treachery|faithlessness]].

## Consequences

Ingratitude in the fables is consistently exposed and rebuked but rarely punished in material terms. The Traveller is not struck by lightning for insulting the Plane-tree — he simply stands exposed as ungrateful. The Bees are not destroyed — they are called "ungrateful scoundrels." The Wild Goats are not captured — they leave, and the G[[promise|oath]]erd is left to reflect. The fables treat the punishment for ingratitude as **reputational rather than material**: the ungrateful character loses the future [[kindness|goodwill]] of the benefactor and any who witness the ingratitude. In a moral universe where reputation determines survival, this is punishment enough.

## Relation to Gratitude

[[Ingratitude]] is the negation of [[gratitude]], and the fables treat the two as the poles of a single moral axis. [[Gratitude]] acknowledges that a benefit was freely given and creates an obligation to reciprocate or at least remember. Ingratitude denies the benefit or the obligation. The Plane-tree fable ([[166-001]]) makes the contrast explicit: the Traveller enjoys the shade (receiving the benefit) while denying the tree's value (negating the obligation). [[Gratitude]] would have been a simple acknowledgment — "this tree gives us shade, therefore it is useful." Ingratitude is a **performative blindness** — the ungrateful character cannot see the benefit because acknowledging it would create an unwelcome debt.
