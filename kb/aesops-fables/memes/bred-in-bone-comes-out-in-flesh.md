## Statement

"What's bred in the bone is sure to come out in the flesh" (212-002) is the explicit moral of The Wolf's Cub and the Shepherd. A Shepherd finds a stray Wolf's Cub, rears it with his dogs, and the Cub grows up joining the dogs in hunting down sheep-stealing wolves. But when the dogs abandon a chase, the Cub continues alone — and when it catches the thief-wolf, shares the feast with him before returning to the Shepherd:

> When he overtook the culprit, he would stop and share the feast with him, and then return home. If no wolf was observed, he would fall on the sheep himself and kill one for his own dinner. (212-001)

The moral is not that nurture has no effect — the Cub hunted wolves alongside the dogs for years — but that **innate nature re-emerges under pressure**. The Cub cooperates with dogs when the hunt is active; when the hunt fails, the dog-role dissolves and the wolf-nature takes over.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Wolf's Cub and the Shepherd (primary)

The Cub's trajectory is not a sudden [[treachery|betrayal]] but a **gradual revelation**. He hunts wolves with the dogs — this is genuine cooperation. He finishes the chase alone — this is plausible diligence. He shares the feast with the thief-wolf — here the wolf-nature emerges but is still ambiguous. Finally, in the absence of any wolf to hunt, he kills a sheep himself. Nature did not override nurture all at once; it leaked through the seams.

### The Blind Man and the Wolf's Cub (parallel)

A Blind Man with an extraordinary sense of touch can identify any animal by feel. Given a Wolf's Cub to identify, he hesitates and then says:

> "Indeed, I am not sure whether it is a Wolf's Cub or a Fox's: but this I know — it would never do to trust it in a sheepfold." (042-001)

The Blind Man cannot name the species but can **feel the disposition**. His caution is the practical application of the moral: you do not need to know exactly what a creature is to know what it will do. The shepherd in the primary fable lacked this caution.

### The Crow and the Swan (parallel)

A Crow, [[jealousy|envy]]ing the Swan's white plumage, abandons his altar-picking life to bathe in pools and streams, believing the water will whiten his feathers. After many days of washing, he is thinner, hungrier, and just as black:

> "You may change your habits, but not your nature." (148-002)

The Crow's blackness is not a habit — it is bred in the bone. Unlike the Wolf's Cub, the Crow's nature is visible (black feathers), but he refuses to believe what his own eyes tell him. The Wolf's Cub's nature is invisible until it acts.

### The Farmer, the Cranes, and the Stork (supporting)

A Stork, caught in a trap among cranes, begs the Farmer to release him: "I am not a crane, but a Stork... the most honest and harmless of birds." The Farmer replies:

> "You may be all you say, but I only know that I have caught you in the same net with these cranes, the thieves, and in their company you must die." (153-001)

The Stork's protest is that his nature is different from the cranes' — he is innocent by disposition. The Farmer's reply is that **company proves nature** or, at minimum, makes disposition irrelevant: what you are caught doing defines you, [[respect|regard]]less of what is bred in your bone.

| Creature | Innate Nature | Nurture Attempt | Does Nature Win? |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Wolf's Cub | Wolf (predator) | Reared with dogs | Yes — kills sheep alone |
| Crow | Black feathers | Bathes like Swan | Yes — stays black |
| Stork | Claimed harmless | Travels with cranes | Irrelevant — judged by company |

## Mechanism

The moral operates through a **latency model** of nature: innate disposition is not always active, but it is always present. The Wolf's Cub hunts alongside the dogs — wolf-nature is latent. When the dogs give up the chase, the Cub is alone with his nature and no witnesses. That is when the wolf emerges.

This distinguishes "bred in the bone" from [[evil-tendencies-shown-early]], which holds that character is visible from childhood. The Blind Man detects the Wolf Cub's nature by touch — early detection is possible, but it requ[[anger|ire]]s specialized perception. Most observers see only the Cub's cooperative behavior and conclude, as the Shepherd did, that nurture has succeeded.

The fable also implies a **test condition**: you do not know a creature's nature until you observe it **unobserved**. The Wolf's Cub only kills sheep when no dogs or shepherd are watching. The private moment — the chase continued alone — is the revelatory test.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[evil-tendencies-shown-early]] — Both morals assert that character is innate and detectable before action. "Evil tendencies are early shown" emphasizes detection; "bred in the bone" emphasizes the inevitability of emergence. The Blind Man could detect the Cub's nature; the Shepherd could not.

- [[habits-change-nature-stays]] — The Crow and the Swan is the d[[anger|ire]]ct companion fable: habits (bathing) are surface-level; nature (blackness) is immutable. The Wolf's Cub changes his habits (hunting with dogs) but not his nature (taste for sheep).

- [[villain-may-disguise]] — The villain can disguise himself temporarily, but the disguise will not hold. The Wolf's Cub's cooperation with dogs is a form of disguise — not deliberate, but functionally equivalent. Both morals converge on the same warning: do not trust surface behavior.

- [[bad-companions-corrupt]] — The Stork is caught in the cranes' net, and his innocence — even if real — cannot save him. The corollary: if you are judged by your company, then choosing company wisely is as important as having a good nature in the first place.
