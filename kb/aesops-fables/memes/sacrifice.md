## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **sacrifice** operates in two registers: the literal ritual sacrifice of animals to the gods, and the figurative sacrifice of one good for another through [[folly|poor judgment]]. The fables are populated with oxen, goats, and calves being led to altars — but Aesop's interest is not in the ritual itself. He uses sacrifice as a narrative device to expose human calculation: who makes the [[promise|vow]], what they offer, and whether they actually pay when the gods deliver.

## Sacrifice in the Fables

| Fable | Sacrificial Act | Motivation | Ironic Outcome |
|-------|----------------|------------|----------------|
| The Vine and the Goat (fable-124) | Goat browses vine shoots; Vine says it will provide wine for the goat's sacrifice | Vine's prophecy of revenge | The goat, feeding now, will be the offering later (124-001) |
| The Heifer and the Ox (fable-180) | Heifer mocks ploughing Ox, then is led to sacrifice while Ox returns to work | Heifer's false superiority | Idleness ends at the altar (180-001) |
| The Herdsman's Vow (fable-194) | Herdsman [[promise|vow]]s a calf to find lost Bull; finds it being eaten by a [[the-lion|lion]] | Desperate bargain | He loses both the Bull and the calf he promised (194-001) |
| The Sick Man's Vow (fable-244) | Sick man [[promise|vow]]s a hundred oxen for recovery; gods cure him; he sacrifices tiny clay oxen | Cheap repayment | Gods punish his [[ridicule|mockery]] (244-001) |

## Varieties

1. **Sacrifice as Inevitable Cost** (fable-124, fable-180): The Vine tells the browsing Goat that it will supply the wine for the goat's own sacrifice. The Heifer who mocks the working Ox is soon led to the altar. In both, sacrifice is the endpoint of a life lived without [[wisdom|foresight]].

2. **Sacrifice as Desperate Bargain** (fable-194): The Herdsman [[promise|vow]]s to "sacrifice a calf to Jupiter" if he finds his lost Bull (194-001). When he discovers the Bull being devoured by a [[the-lion|lion]], the [[promise|vow]] evaporates — he has lost both what he sought and what he promised. Aesop's implicit judgment: do not bargain with gods over what you cannot afford to lose.

3. **Sacrifice as Mocked Obligation** (fable-244): The sick man [[promise|vow]]s a hundred oxen, recovers, and offers clay figurines instead. The fable is a caution against fraudulent piety — the gods are not mocked.

## Consequences

> "What have I done to you," said the Vine, "that you should harm me thus? ... the time will come when you shall be led to the altar to be sacrificed." (124-001)

> Not long afterwards the Heifer was seized and led off to sacrifice. "Ah," said the Ox, with a grim smile, "that's why you were allowed to live in idleness." (180-001)

Aesop's sacrifices rarely console anyone. Animals led to altars are not martyrs — they are [[punishment|consequence]]s. The Heifer's idleness was not rewarded but deferred. The Herdsman's [[promise|vow]] was not answered but pre-empted by a [[the-lion|lion]]. Sacrifice in Aesop reveals the gap between what humans promise and what the world actually delivers.
