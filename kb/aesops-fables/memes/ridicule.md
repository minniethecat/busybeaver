## Definition

Ridicule in [[Aesop's Fables]] is a social [[strength|force]] rather than an individual emotion: it is mockery delivered by a group, and its target is always someone who has stepped outside their proper place. It is not cruel laughter at the weak but corrective laughter at the presumptuous — the Camel who tries to dance, the Jackdaw who tries to be an [[Eagle]].

The fables treat ridicule as a boundary-en[[strength|force]]ment mechanism. When the Camel's grotesque performance excites "ridicule" from the assembled beasts, the mockery reasserts the natural order: camels do not dance (164-001). When the Jackdaw's failed imitation draws "not only [[fortune|misfortune]] but ridicule," the laughter confirms that a jackdaw cannot be an eagle (170-002). In both cases, ridicule is the symptom of a category violation — and the comm[[harmony|unity]]'s instinctive response to it.

## Varieties of Ridicule

Aesop's treatment of ridicule reveals several distinct social functions:

- **Ridicule as boundary en[[strength|force]]ment** — the Camel's dancing is not merely bad art; it is a category error. The beasts' ridicule communicates: *this is not what camels do*. The laughter restores the violated taxonomy of the animal kingdom (164-001).
- **Ridicule as a [[punishment|consequence]] of [[envy]]** — the Camel is motivated by [[jealousy|envy]] of the Monkey's applause. Ridicule is thus the second-order punishment: [[jealousy|envy]] drives the transgression, and ridicule is what the transgression earns. The emotion and the punishment form a closed loop.
- **Ridicule as the lighter half of punishment** — in the Jackdaw's case, ridicule is pa[[anger|ire]]d with "[[fortune|misfortune]]" as a joint [[punishment|consequence]] of overreaching (170-002). The fable suggests a spectrum: [[fortune|misfortune]] is what happens to the body (clipped wings, capture); ridicule is what happens to the reputation. Both are equally certain.

## Consequences

Ridicule in the fables is **public, immediate, and irreversible**. The Camel is not merely laughed at — he is "driven away," expelled from the assembly. The Jackdaw is carried home as an object of confused curiosity: "What sort of bird is it, father?" The ridicule converts the transgressor from a participant into a spectacle.

This permanence is what distinguishes Aesopic ridicule from mere humor. It is not a passing reaction but a **status reassignment**: after ridicule, the target occupies a permanently lower position in the social order.

## Related Concepts

- [[pride]] — the internal disposition that precedes public ridicule; the Camel's pride in imagining he can dance
- [[envy]] — the motivator: the Camel envies the Monkey's success, the Jackdaw envies the Eagle's [[strength|power]]
- [[folly]] — the broader category of foolish action; ridicule is the social [[punishment|consequence]] of [[foolishness|folly]] enacted in public
- [[flattery]] — the opposite of ridicule: manufactured praise rather than authentic mockery
