## Statement

"Betray a friend, and you'll often find you have ruined yourself" is the explicit moral of The Ass, the [[the-fox|Fox]], and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (018-002). An Ass and a [[the-fox|Fox]] form a partnership and go out foraging together. When they encounter a [[the-lion|Lion]], the [[the-fox|Fox]], thinking to save himself, approaches the [[the-lion|Lion]] and offers to deliver the Ass into the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[strength|power]] — in exchange for his own freedom. [[the-lion|The Lion]] agrees. [[the-fox|The Fox]] leads the Ass into a hidden pit, and the Ass is trapped. [[the-lion|The Lion]] then turns not on the Ass, but on the [[the-fox|Fox]]: he devours the [[the-fox|Fox]] first, and only then feasts on the trapped Ass. The traitor is the first to die, because a betrayer is useful only until the [[treachery|betrayal]] is complete — and after that, he is merely another piece of prey.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass, the Fox, and the Lion

An Ass and a [[the-fox|Fox]], out foraging, spot a [[the-lion|Lion]] and are "[[fear|dread]]fully [[fear|fright]]ened" (018-001). [[the-fox|The Fox]] whispers to the [[the-lion|Lion]] that he can deliver the Ass into the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[strength|power]] — if the [[the-lion|Lion]] promises to let the [[the-fox|Fox]] go free. [[the-lion|The Lion]] consents. [[the-fox|The Fox]] guides the Ass to a hidden pit trap left by hunters. The Ass falls in and is trapped. [[the-lion|The Lion]], seeing the Ass is secured, wastes no time:

> When the Lion saw that the Ass was safely caught and couldn't get away, it was to the Fox that he first turned his attention, and he soon finished him off, and then at his leisure proceeded to feast upon the Ass. (018-001)

[[the-fox|The Fox]]'s miscalculation is devastatingly simple: he assumed the [[the-lion|Lion]] would honor a promise to a traitor. But a [[the-lion|Lion]] who would break no promise to prey has no reason to keep a promise to a collaborator. [[the-fox|The Fox]] made himself useful — and the moment his usefulness ended, he became dinner.

### The Double-Faced Bat

A Bat, caught between the birds and the beasts during their great war, switches allegiance depending on which side is winning. When the birds prevail, he flies with them; when the beasts prevail, he walks among them. After the war ends in peace, both sides reject the Bat, and he is condemned to fly only in darkness (168-001). The Bat, like the [[the-fox|Fox]], believes that allegiance is a strategy rather than a commitment. Both learn that **those who betray one side will never be trusted by the other**.

### The Wolf and the Crane

A Wolf, choking on a bone, promises a Crane a great reward if she will extract it. The Crane puts her head in the Wolf's mouth and pulls out the bone. When she asks for her reward, the Wolf replies: "You have your reward: I let you take your head out of my mouth without biting it off" (062-001). The Wolf, like the [[the-lion|Lion]], treats promises to the weak as instruments to be discarded. The Crane is not a traitor, but she trusts a predator — and the lesson is the same: **promises from the [[strength|power]]ful to the weak are only as durable as the [[strength|power]] differential that en[[strength|force]]s them**.

### The Horse and the Stag

A Horse, seeking revenge on a Stag, allows a Man to put a bridle on him and ride him into battle. The Horse defeats the Stag — and finds himself permanently enslaved to the Man (075-001). The Horse is not a traitor to another creature, but he betrays his own freedom for the sake of revenge. Like the [[the-fox|Fox]], he exchanges a partnership (with his own [[freedom|liberty]]) for an [[friendship|alliance]] with a more [[strength|power]]ful party — and discovers that the [[strength|power]]ful party never lets go.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **collaborator disposal**: a traitor is a tool that becomes a liability the moment the [[treachery|betrayal]] is complete. The logic has three steps:

1. **Instrumental value**: The traitor is useful because he provides access — he knows the victim's weaknesses, locations, and trust patterns.
2. **Value exhaustion**: Once the victim is delivered, the traitor's instrumental value drops to zero. He has done the one thing he was kept alive to do.
3. **Liability conversion**: The traitor now becomes a liability: he knows what the [[the-lion|Lion]] did, he knows the [[the-lion|Lion]] broke a promise, and he has demonstrated that his loyalty is for sale.

[[the-fox|The Fox]]'s error is in believing that a promise from a predator to a prey-species is binding. But the [[the-lion|Lion]] never stopped being a predator. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s [[treachery|betrayal]] of the Ass did not change the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s category — it merely proved that the [[the-fox|Fox]] belongs in the "prey" column.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[trust]] — [[trust|Trust]] is the commodity the [[the-fox|Fox]] spends. He trusts the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s promise, and the trust is betrayed. The moral cuts both ways: the [[the-fox|Fox]] betrays the Ass and is betrayed by the [[the-lion|Lion]]. A chain of broken trust destroys everyone in it.
- [[treachery]] — The abstract concept this moral instantiates. [[the-fox|The Fox]] is treachery in action: the offer, the whispered deal, the guided steps into the pit. The concept page catalogs what the fable dramatizes.
- [[friendship]] — The Ass and the [[the-fox|Fox]] are explicitly described as "partners." [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s [[treachery|betrayal]] of partnership is what condemns him. [[friendship|Friendship]], in Aesop, is not an emotion but a structural commitment — and breaking it has structural [[punishment|consequence]]s.
- [[reward]] — [[the-fox|The Fox]] expects a reward (his life) in exchange for his service. [[the-lion|The Lion]] gives him a different reward: being eaten first. The gap between expected and actual reward is the space where [[treachery|betrayal]] dies.

## Enduring Influence

The fable is Perry Index 195, classified under "The Ass, the [[the-fox|Fox]], and the [[the-lion|Lion]]." The strategic logic — that a traitor becomes a liability after the [[treachery|betrayal]] is complete — has been observed in organized crime, intelligence operations, and authoritarian politics. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s calculation error is a case study in **strategic naivety**: he assumed that because he and the [[the-lion|Lion]] had a deal, he was no longer prey. [[the-lion|The Lion]] assumed differently.
