Pride in [[Aesop's Fables]] is the cardinal error of self-estimation. The fables do not merely condemn pride — they document its **mechanics**: how it rises, how it expresses itself, and how it collapses. The Lamp that swells with pride and is blown out a moment later ([[059-001]]) is the paradigm: pride inflates, then reality punctures.

## Definition

Aesopian pride is the overestimation of one's own standing, [[strength|power]], or permanence. Three features recur: (1) **pride is comparative** — the proud always measure themselves against others and find the others wanting (the Horse against the Ass at [[274-002]]); (2) **pride is fragile** — it rests on circumstances that can reverse in an instant (the War-horse at [[154-001]]); (3) **pride precedes a fall** — the corpus explicitly states "[[pride-comes-before-a-fall|pride goes before a fall]]" ([[P03-006]]), and every proud character proves the maxim true.

> A Lamp, well filled with oil, burned with a clear and steady light, and began to swell with pride and boast that it shone more brightly than the sun himself. Just then a puff of wind came and blew it out. ([[059-001]])

> "You don't think, do you, that your bell was given you as a reward of merit? On the contrary, it is a badge of disgrace." ([[004-001]])

## Varieties

**Pride of Appearance.** The Dog ([[004-001]]) takes the warning-bell for an ornament, strutting as though it were a medal. This is pride that misreads signs — the very thing the proud man displays as proof of his worth is evidence against him. The Horse in his fine harness ([[274-002]]) makes the same error, [[contempt|scorn]]ing the laden Ass, only to meet the Ass again when his own circumstances have collapsed.

**Pride of Position.** The War-horse ([[154-001]]) recalls his battle days with longing, having once been caparisoned and attended by a groom, now grinding corn in a mill. His pride was tied to a role that time and age inevitably stripped away. The fable's lesson is that pride in station is pride in something borrowed — the station is not the self, and when it goes, the pride goes with it.

**Pride of Performance.** The Lamp ([[059-001]]) shines steadily and concludes it outshines the sun. This is pride that forgets its dependence — the Lamp burns only because someone filled it with oil; it is extinguished by the merest breath. Pride of performance is the most foolish variety because the performance is always contingent, never sovereign.

## Consequences

Pride always ends in exposure. The pattern is invariant: the proud character is shown a truth they refused to see, and the revelation is humiliating. The Dog learns his bell is a warning, not a reward ([[004-001]]). The Horse draws a dung-cart past the same Ass he once [[contempt|scorn]]ed ([[274-002]]). The War-horse grinds corn ([[154-001]]). The Lamp goes out ([[059-001]]). There is no exception — pride in Aesop is never vindicated, never rewarded, never left standing. It is the root of [[folly]] and the seedbed of [[shame]].

## Relation to Shame

[[Pride]] and [[shame]] are the two poles of Aesopian self-[[respect|regard]]. Pride is the false high; [[shame]] is the true low that follows. [[the-fox|The Fox]] who loses his tail ([[083-002]]) is driven by [[shame]]; the Dog who wears his bell ([[004-001]]) is driven by pride. Both are forms of self-[[disguise|deception]] — one inflating, one deflating — and both are cured only by a clear-eyed view of one's actual position. The fables recommend neither pride nor [[shame]] but **honest self-assessment**, the quiet middle that neither boasts nor cowers.
