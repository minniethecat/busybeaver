## Statement

"He is no friend who plays double" is the explicit moral of The Hound and the Hare (131-002). A young Hound catches a Hare but cannot decide what to do with her: he snaps at her teeth as if to kill, then releases and frisks about her as if playing. The Hare speaks:

> I wish you would show yourself in your true colours! If you are my friend, why do you bite me? If you are my enemy, why do you play with me? (131-001)

The Hare does not ask for [[pity|mercy]]. She asks for **clarity** — the one thing the Hound cannot give, because his double behavior is not indecision but a refusal to commit to either role.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Hound and the Hare (primary)

The Hound's error is not aggression but **role ambiguity**. He occupies two mutually exclusive positions — friend and enemy — and switches between them without warning. The Hare's protest is epistemological: a double player makes the world unreadable, and an unreadable world is more dangerous than a hostile one (131-001).

### The Man and the Serpent

A Man finds a frozen Serpent, takes [[mercy|pity]], and warms it in his bosom. The Serpent revives and bites him. The Serpent played double: it accepted the warmth of friendship and responded with the venom of enmity (045-001).

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

A Wolf dresses in a sheep's skin and infiltrates the flock. He is the literal double player — dressed as friend, acting as enemy — and his discovery and death at the shepherd's hands is the fable tradition's verdict on the double game (044-001).

## Mechanism

The Hare's challenge is a piece of moral logic: friend implies no-bite; enemy implies no-play. The Hound's behavior violates the excluded middle. In Aesop's world, such violations are not tolerated — characters who refuse to choose a role ultimately serve neither.

This is distinct from hypocrisy ([[hypocrite-deceives-himself]]). The Hypocrite wears a mask and believes in it. The double player wears two masks and believes in neither — he acts on impulse, switching between self-images as convenience dictates. The Hypocrite deceives others; the double player deceives himself about his own indecision.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[betray-friend-ruin-yourself]] — The double player who is discovered betrays the friend-role; the betrayer who acts against a friend destroys the relationship. Both end alone.

- [[traitors-deserve-their-fate]] — The double player is the traitor who hasn't yet chosen sides. The Hound is a traitor to both the hunter (he doesn't kill) and the hunted (he doesn't spare).

- [[injuries-never-forgotten]] — The Hare will never forget the Hound's bite. A double player accumulates injuries on both sides without building loyalty on either.
