## Statement

"Look before your leap" is the explicit closing moral of [[the-fox|The Fox]] and the Goat (047-002). It is one of the few Aesopic morals that has entered English as an independent proverb — so thoroughly that most speakers do not know it originated in a fable.

A [[the-fox|Fox]] falls into a well. A Goat, thirsty and impulsive, asks whether the water is good. [[the-fox|The Fox]], seeing his opport[[harmony|unity]], praises the water extravagantly. The Goat jumps in. Only after drinking does he ask the obvious question: how do we get out? [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s answer is chilling: "I have an idea. You stand on your hind legs and place your fore-legs against the side of the well.

I will climb up your back, and then I will help you out." The Goat agrees. [[the-fox|The Fox]] climbs out — and walks away (047-001).

**The Goat's error is not [[folly|stupidity]] but sequential myopia:** he solves his immediate need (thirst) before checking whether the solution creates a larger problem.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Fox and the Goat

> A Fox fell into a well and was unable to get out again. By and by a thirsty Goat came by, and seeing the Fox in the well asked him if the water was good. "Good?" said the Fox, "it's the best water I ever tasted in all my life. Come down and try it yourself." The Goat thought of nothing but the prospect of quenching his thirst, and jumped in at once. (047-001)

> When he had had enough to drink, he looked about, like the Fox, for some way of getting out, but could find none. Presently the Fox said, "I have an idea. You stand on your hind legs, and plant your fore-legs firmly against the side of the well, and then I'll climb on to your back, and, from there, by stepping on your horns, I can get out. And when I'm out, I'll help you out too." The Goat did as he was asked, and the Fox climbed on to his back and so out of the well. (047-001)

> And then he coolly walked away. The Goat called loudly after him and reminded him of his promise. The Fox turned round and said, "You silly old Goat! If your brain could only have been equal to your beard, you'd never have gone down there without seeing how you were going to get out again." (047-001)

> Look before your leap. (047-002)

### The Hares and the Frogs

The Hares, persecuted on all sides, resolve to drown themselves en masse. They rush toward a pool — but the Frogs at the edge leap away in [[fear|terror]] at their approach. The Hares stop: "There are creatures even more [[fear|fright]]ened than us" (025-001). They reconsidered before the final leap. They passed the test the Goat failed.

### The Ass on the Precipice

An Ass, driven down a mountain, "suddenly quitted the track and rushed to the edge of a precipice." His Driver catches his tail and pulls with all his [[power|might]], but the Ass refuses to budge. At last the Driver gives up, saying: "All right, then, get to the bottom your own way; but it's the way to sudden death" (182-001).

The Ass is the Goat's even more reckless cousin: he leaps without even the excuse of thirst.

### The Fox Who Passed His Own Test

[[the-fox|The Fox]] in this fable is *also* a jumper. He fell into the well first — possibly through carelessness. But [[the-fox|fox]]es in Aesop rarely make the same mistake twice. Having landed at the bottom, the [[the-fox|Fox]] does not panic; he waits. When the Goat arrives, the [[the-fox|Fox]] does not think about water — he thinks about exit.

The contrast between the two characters is not intelligence but **temporal horizon**: the [[the-fox|Fox]] sees two moves ahead; the Goat sees one.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **sequential myopia with sunk-cost escalation**:

| Step | Goat's Decision | What He Ignores |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1. See well | "Is the water good?" | "How deep is the well?" |
| 2. Jump in | Immediate gratification | Exit strategy |
| 3. Drink | Satisfy thirst | Growing entrapment |
| 4. Ask [[the-fox|Fox]] | [[trust|Trust]] the deceiver | [[the-fox|Fox]]'s incentive to betray |

At each step, the Goat makes a locally rational decision — satisfying the most urgent need — while ignoring how each decision compounds into a trap. By step 4, the Goat has invested so much (he is now at the bottom of a well) that he must trust the [[the-fox|Fox]]: the sunk-cost fallacy in its purest form.

[[the-fox|The Fox]] exploits this by controlling the Goat's attention. "The best water I ever tasted" is not a lie about water; it is a distraction from the exit problem. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s genius is knowing that a thirsty creature cannot think about anything except thirst.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[think-twice-before-you-act]] — A d[[anger|ire]]ct synonym, reinforcing the same principle from a different fable tradition.
- [[false-confidence-leads-to-disaster]] — The Goat is confident that jumping into a well is fine because he trusts the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s endorsement.

False [[trust|confidence]] + no verification = trap.
- [[play-a-good-card-once-too-often]] — [[the-fox|The Fox]] plays his "escape on someone else's back" card exactly once. He does not repeat the trick on the same Goat.
- [[precautions-are-useless-after-the-event]] — The Goat asks "how do we get out?" *after* jumping.

Precautions post-leap are useless — the moral pairs with this one as cause and effect.
- [[necessity-is-the-mother-of-invention]] — [[the-fox|The Fox]], trapped and alone, invents an escape. Necessity sharpens his mind; the Goat, not yet trapped when he jumps, has no necessity to sharpen his.

## Enduring Influence

"Look before you leap" is one of a handful of Aesopic morals that have become fully naturalized English proverbs. It appears in John Heywood's 1546 collection of proverbs, in Shakespeare, and in virtually every modern English idiom dictionary. The phrase has outlived its fable: most users have no idea it originated with a [[the-fox|Fox]] and a Goat at the bottom of a well.

The fable is Perry Index 9, one of the earliest and most widely attested in the Aesopic corpus. Its survival is partly due to its structural perfection: two characters, one well, one exit, one [[treachery|betrayal]]. No detail is wasted. Any child can understand it; any adult who ignores it will eventually meet their own [[the-fox|Fox]] at the bottom of their own well.
