## Statement

"Those who will not work deserve to starve" is the explicit moral of The Blacksmith and His Dog (092-002). A Blacksmith has a Dog that sleeps all day while the master hammers at the anvil, but springs awake the moment food appears. The Blacksmith rebukes the Dog: "What on earth is the good of a lazy cur like you?" Aesop draws no distinction between inability to work and unwillingness — the judgment is binary and final.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Blacksmith and His Dog

A Blacksmith's Dog curls up and sleeps while his master labors, but is "very wide awake indeed when it was time for meals." One day the Blacksmith confronts him:

> "What on earth is the good of a lazy cur like you? When I am hammering away at my anvil, you just curl up and go to sleep: but no sooner do I stop for a mouthful of food than you wake up and wag your tail to be fed." (092-001)

The Dog offers no defense. The moral is not an argument but an observation: the Dog's behavior is self-indicting.

### The Ass and the Lap-Dog

An Ass sees a Lap-Dog receiving affection and treats for its playful antics, while the Ass performs heavy labor for nothing but blows. He attempts to imitate the Lap-Dog's behavior, leaping onto his master — and is beaten for his [[misfortune|trouble]]. The Ass learns that labor, not mimicry, is his path to tolerable treatment (154-001). Those who will not work in their proper capacity cannot expect reward.

### The Oxen and the Butchers

Oxen p[[fate|lot]] to destroy the Butchers who slaughter them, until an old Ox points out that the Butchers at least kill skillfully and quickly — if the Butchers are destroyed, less skilled men will do the killing worse. The fable is not d[[anger|ire]]ctly about laziness but about the paradox of labor: even those who seem to exploit may provide a form of [[safety|protection]] (018-001).

## Mechanism

The moral operates on a principle of **reciprocal obligation**: the Dog's right to eat flows from a duty to work that the Dog never discharges. Aesop does not frame this as a social contract — the Blacksmith is not a political philosopher — but the structure is unmistakable. The Blacksmith's hammering is the condition that makes the bone possible; the Dog's sleeping is the failure that makes the rebuke inevitable.

This mechanism differs from modern "work ethic" in one crucial respect: Aesop does not say the Dog *should* work. He says those who *will not* work deserve to starve — a descriptive claim about [[punishment|consequence]]s, not a prescriptive claim about virtue. The fable is a miniature sociology of labor.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[better-poverty-without-care]] — The Fir-tree's boast about usefulness (033-001) is the opposite error: working to excess for status. Both morals converge on a warning about miscalibrating one's contribution.
- [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race]] — The Tortoise works steadily while the Hare idles. The moral of the race is a softer version of the same principle: idleness loses the [[reward|prize]].
- [[example-is-better-than-precept]] — The Crab mother cannot teach sideways walking by precept; the Blacksmith's Dog needs no precept at all — he simply ignores what he sees. Action teaches where words cannot.

## Enduring Influence

The sentiment appears in the New Testament ("If anyone is not willing to work, let him not eat," 2 Thessalonians 3:10) — whether Aesop [[persuasion|influence]]d Paul or whether both draw from a common Mediterranean folk tradition is unclear. The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 300. In modern usage, the moral has been adopted by both sides of welfare debates, often stripped of the original fable's compact narrative [[strength|power]].
