## Statement

"Traitors richly deserve their [[fortune|fate]]" is the explicit moral of The Wolves and the Dogs (184-002). The Wolves approach the Dogs with a seductive argument: "You are very like us in most ways: the main difference between us is one of training only. We live a life of freedom; but you are enslaved to mankind, who beat you, and put heavy collars round your necks" (184-001). They offer the Dogs a share of the flocks in exchange for betraying their human masters. The Dogs accept the deal, accompany the Wolves into their den — and are immediately torn to pieces. The moral is not a warning that [[treachery|betrayal]] *[[power|might]]* be punished; it is the stronger claim that [[treachery|betrayal]] *deserves* punishment, and that the punisher is often the very party who solicited the [[treachery|betrayal]]. The traitor is not a tragic figure caught between two loyalties but a fool who believes that those who urge disloyalty against others will remain loyal to him.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Wolves and the Dogs

The Wolves come to the Dogs with an argument from shared nature: both are canids, both are hunters, both lived free before men put collars on the Dogs. The Wolves frame the arrangement as liberation:

> "Why should we continue to be enemies any longer? You are very like us in most ways: the main difference between us is one of training only. We live a life of freedom; but you are enslaved to mankind, who beat you, and put heavy collars round your necks, and compel you to keep watch over their flocks and herds for them, and, to crown all, they give you nothing but bones to eat. Don't put up with it any longer, but hand over the flocks to us, and we will all live on the fat of the land and feast together." (184-001)

The Dogs allow themselves to be persuaded. But the promise of shared abundance is a trap:

> "But no sooner were they well inside than the Wolves set upon them and tore them to pieces. Traitors richly deserve their fate." (184-002)

The Wolves' argument contains a structural flaw that the Dogs miss: if the Wolves are willing to betray their natural prey (sheep), what reason is there to think they will honor a deal with fellow predators? The Wolves' own logic of predation extends to anyone weaker — including collaborators.

### The Farmer and the Viper

The structural parallel to The Wolves and the Dogs is [[betray-friend-ruin-yourself]], drawn from a different fable but reaching the same conclusion: placing trust in a party whose nature is predatory invites [[ruin|destruction]]. In The Farmer and the Viper, the Farmer rescues a frozen snake, only to be bitten when it revives. The Viper, like the Wolves, does not pretend to be something other than what it is — it is the Farmer and the Dogs who mistake kindness (or collusion) for a binding contract. Both fables teach that those who deal with predators on the predators' terms will end up as prey.

### The Serpent and the Eagle

An Eagle drops a Serpent near a Traveller, who is about to be bitten when another Traveller intervenes (PN=069-001). The Serpent, grateful, offers a reward — but the moral is that a creature whose nature is to strike cannot be trusted to change. The Wolves' offer to the Dogs is structurally identical: a predator offers a benefit, and the recipient forgets what the offeror is.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **[[treachery|betrayal]] as a transitive relationship**. If A induces B to betray C, then A has demonstrated that [[treachery|betrayal]] is a legitimate tool in their reperto[[anger|ire]]. When B has served their purpose, there is nothing — no contract, no gratitude, no shared interest — preventing A from deploying the same tool against B.

The mechanism has three structural features:

1. **Inducement asymmetry**: The Wolves offer the Dogs something the Dogs cannot obtain on their own (freedom from collars, access to the fat of the land). This asymmetry gives the Wolves leve[[anger|rage]] that persists after the Dogs have betrayed their masters.
2. **Instrumental value**: The Dogs are valuable to the Wolves only as a means to acqu[[anger|ire]] the flocks. Once the flocks are handed over, the Dogs' instrumental value drops to zero — indeed turns negative, since the Dogs are now competitors for the same meat.
3. **No third-party en[[strength|force]]ment**: The Wolves and Dogs operate outside human law. There is no contract, no court, no neutral arbiter to en[[strength|force]] the Wolves' promises. The only en[[strength|force]]ment mechanism is the Wolves' [[kindness|goodwill]] — which, by their own logic, they do not possess.

The Dogs' error is not moral but cognitive: they mistake the Wolves' rhetoric of shared nature for a bond of solidarity, when in fact the only bond the Wolves recognize is the food chain.

## Enduring Influence

The moral has passed into English through the proverb "Give a traitor enough rope and he will hang himself," and its logic underpins the legal doctrine of *criminal conspiracy*: those who consp[[anger|ire]] together are jointly liable for each other's acts, because the law recognizes that conspirators are, by their nature, prepared to betray anyone — including each other. The Wolves' den is a Hobbesian state of nature where promises are en[[strength|force]]d only by [[strength|power]], and the Dogs enter it unarmed.
