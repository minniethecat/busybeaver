## Statement

"A man is known by the company he keeps" is the explicit moral of The Ass and His Purchaser (190-002). A Man takes an Ass home on trial and puts him in the stable. The Ass immediately chooses to stand beside the laziest and greediest beast there. The purchaser returns him instantly: "I could see what sort of beast he is from the companion he chose for himself." Character reveals itself not in words or even in deeds, but in the silent, spontaneous choice of who to stand beside.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass and His Purchaser

A Man buying an Ass takes one home on trial and places him among his other asses. The newcomer surveys the stable and — without hesitation — positions himself next to the laziest and greediest animal present. When the owner comes to collect the Ass, the purchaser explains his verdict:

> "I don't want to put him through any more tests. I could see what sort of beast he is from the companion he chose for himself." (190-001)

No formal test could be more revealing. The Ass incriminates himself in a single, un[[strength|force]]d decision.

### The Two Travellers and the Purse

Two Travellers walking together find a purse. The one who picks it up claims it as "I have found it," but when a mob approaches shouting "thief," he immediately shifts to "we are undone." His companion replies: "No — say rather *I* am undone. He who will not share the [[reward|prize]] must not share the danger" (088-001). The fable tests the principle in reverse: refusing good [[friendship|companionship]] reveals character as clearly as choosing bad.

### The Wolf and the Crane

A Wolf with a bone stuck in his throat h[[anger|ire]]s a Crane to extract it with her long bill. When the Crane demands payment, the Wolf replies that having her head in a Wolf's mouth and pulling it out again *is* her payment. Choosing to do business with a Wolf is itself the mistake — the [[bad-companions-corrupt|company you keep]] determines the contract (027-001).

## Mechanism

The moral's mechanism is **spontaneous sorting**: character expresses itself most truthfully when no one is watching. The Ass is not trying to impress anyone; he simply gravitates toward the creature most like himself. The purchaser understands that this uncoerced choice is better evidence than any controlled test could provide.

This mechanism also works in the negative d[[anger|ire]]ction implied by the moral: if a man is known by his company, then choosing bad company is indistinguishable from being bad. Aesop does not allow the modern distinction between "guilt by association" and actual guilt — in the fable world, the association *is* the guilt.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Ass's instinctive gravitation toward the worst companion is a form of pride: the assumption that one belongs among the best, betrayed by the reality of where one actually goes. The Crow in borrowed feathers (013-001) makes the same mistake in a different register.
- [[example-is-better-than-precept]] — The Crab mother cannot teach sideways walking by command; the Ass's companion-choice is an example that cannot be hidden. Both morals converge on the same insight: what you *do* reveals what you *are*, [[respect|regard]]less of what you say.
- [[do-not-waste-your-pity]] — The scamp who pleads for [[pity|mercy]] is known by the company of his own prior actions. Both morals are tests of character: one by past deeds, one by present associates.

## Enduring Influence

"A man is known by the company he keeps" has entered English as a fully naturalized proverb, largely detached from its Aesopic source. The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 589. The principle is embedded in modern juris[[wisdom|prudence]] (character witnesses, association evidence) and in folk wisdom about peer groups — though modern sensibilities are more likely to challenge "guilt by association" than to endorse it as Aesop does.
