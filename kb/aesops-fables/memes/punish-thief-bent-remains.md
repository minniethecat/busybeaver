## Statement

"You may punish a thief, but his bent remains" is the explicit moral of The Ant (202-002). Ants were once men who made their living by farming — but not content with their own harvest, they stole from their neighbors. Jupiter, angered by their [[envy|covetousness]], transformed them into Ants as punishment.

> But, though their forms were changed, their nature remained the same: and so, to this day, they go about among the cornfields and gather the fruits of others' labour, and store them up for their own use. (202-001)

The punishment is total — transformation of species — and it fails completely. The Ants' *form* is different; their *behavior* is identical. Aesop's point is bleak: punishment changes what you are, not who you are.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ant (primary)

The fable is an origin myth in miniature. Ants were once men who farmed — so they know what labor is. Their theft is not necessity but **[[envy|covetousness]]**: they had enough from their own fields but "were always casting longing eyes upon the crops and fruits of their neighbours" (202-001). Jupiter's response is metamorphosis, the most extreme punishment short of death available to a god.

> At last their covetousness made Jupiter so angry that he changed them into Ants. (202-001)

The failure of the punishment is structural. Jupiter changed their bodies but could not — or chose not to — change their *bent*, their inclination. The fable implies that bent is more fundamental than form, more durable than species, and beyond the reach even of divine punishment.

### The Wolf and the Lamb (parallel structure)

A Wolf, determined to eat a Lamb, manufactures [[blame|accusation]]s. The Lamb refutes each one, and the Wolf eats him anyway (008-001). The Wolf's bent — predation — is unaffected by argument, law, or the Lamb's innocence. The Wolf is not punished in the fable, but the implication is clear: if he were, his bent would persist, just as the Ants' does.

### The Cat and Aphrodite (inverted case)

A young man falls in love with a Cat and begs Aphrodite to change her into a woman. Aphrodite grants the wish. But when a mouse runs across the floor, the former cat leaps up to chase it — her form is changed, her bent is not (113-001). This is the same mechanism in reverse: the Ants are punished down into insects; the Cat is rewarded up into humanity. Neither change affects the essential inclination.

## Mechanism

The Ant fable draws a radical distinction between **form** (morphē) and **bent** (tropos). Form is the physical body, subject to divine [[strength|power]]: Jupiter can reshape a man into an ant. Bent is the inclination of character, and it appears to be **immutable** — even Jupiter cannot erase it, or chooses not to because the punishment is meant to be visible.

The mechanism differs sharply from [[habits-change-nature-stays]], which contrasts habit (surface behavior) with nature (deep character). "You may punish a thief, but his bent remains" goes further: it says that *bent* survives even divine punishment. The Ants' thieving is not habit — it is bent, the core orientation of the self, and it persists across the boundary between species.

[[punishment|Punishment]] in Aesop is thus a **signal**, not a cure. The Ants' continued thieving in insect form is a visible reminder to mankind of what [[envy|covetousness]] looks like when stripped of human dignity. [[punishment|Punishment]] exposes bent; it does not correct it.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[evil-wishes-come-home-to-roost]] — The Bee asks Jupiter for a weapon and receives a self-destructive one; the Ants are punished by Jupiter and retain their nature. Both fables show Jupiter's style of ind[[anger|ire]]ct governance: he grants or transforms in ways that make the moral visible, not in ways that eliminate the problem.

- [[habits-change-nature-stays]] — "You may change your habits, but not your nature" (148-002) is the gentler version of the Ant's moral. Habits are acqu[[anger|ire]]d; bent is innate. [[punishment|Punishment]] can suppress habits but cannot touch bent.

- [[evil-tendencies-shown-early]] — The Wolf cub, identified by touch as a danger to the sheepfold (042-002), has a bent that is recognized before it can act. The Ants had a bent that was recognized, punished, and *still* acted. The early detection moral and the punishment moral together form a pessimistic arc: bent can be spotted early, but it cannot be removed.

- [[greed]] — The Ants' [[envy|covetousness]] is greed in its structural form: not wanting *more* but wanting *what belongs to others*. Their continued thieving as insects demonstrates that greed is not a human failing but a **natural [[strength|force]]** that survives the boundary between species.
