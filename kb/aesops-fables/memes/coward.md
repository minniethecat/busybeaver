# Coward

The coward in [[Aesop's Fables]] is not defined by the presence of fear — everyone in Aesop feels fear — but by the **failure to act despite it**. The corpus is particularly interested in a specific subspecies: the **boastful coward**, who talks of [[courage|bravery]] in safety and flees at the first test. The fable does not blame the coward for being afraid; it blames him for pretending he is not, and for abandoning those who depend on him when the pretending fails.

## Definition

Aesopian cowardice is the triumph of fear over obligation. It has three signatures in the corpus: (1) **the boast before the test** — the coward announces his cou[[anger|rage]] loudly when there is no danger; (2) **the flight during the test** — when danger appears, the coward is the first to run; and (3) **the rationalisation after the test** — the coward explains his flight as wisdom, not weakness. The fables are un[[mercy|sparing]] in their judgment: the coward is worse than the openly fearful, because his pretence endangers others who [[power|might]] have relied on him.

## Varieties

**The Boastful Coward.** The Hunter ([[089-001]]) boasts of his skill in hunting, but at the sight of a hound he flees. His slave, witnessing the scene, asks the obvious question: why boast if you cannot stand? This is the most common coward in Aesop — the one whose words and legs move in opposite d[[anger|ire]]ctions.

**The Taunting Coward.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] ([[175-001]]) watches a [[the-lion|Lion]] and a Bear [[quarrel|fighting]] over a kid and calls out, "Don't be a coward: why don't you stay and show fight?" He taunts them into continuing their mutual [[ruin|destruction]], then takes the kid for himself. The taunting coward is doubly condemned: he not only refuses to fight but exploits the [[quarrel|fighting]] of others.

**The Exposed Coward.** The Hunter ([[196-001]]) tells everyone he chased a [[the-lion|lion]]. When asked to show the [[the-lion|lion]]'s tracks, he can only point to tracks leading away from the forest — his own footprints, running. The exposed coward is a recurring Aesopian type: the boaster whose own physical evidence contradicts his story.

**The Universal Coward.** Even the [[the-lion|Lion]] — king of beasts, with sharp teeth and claws — "is a coward in one thing: he can't bear the sound of a cock crowing" ([[213-002]]). Aesop's inclusion of the [[the-lion|Lion]] among cowards is deliberate: cowardice is not a condition of the weak alone. Every creature has its fear; what distinguishes the coward is not the fear but the response.

## Consequences

The coward in Aesop is never rewarded, but he is also not always punished — he is simply **exposed**. The Hunter who fled the hound ([[089-001]]) is exposed by his slave. The Hunter who ran from the [[the-lion|lion]] ([[196-001]]) is exposed by his own tracks. [[the-fox|The Fox]] who calls others cowards ([[267-001]]) is exposed by the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s observation that the [[the-fox|Fox]] himself never enters the den. Cowardice in the corpus is structurally unstable: it generates a false reputation that reality eventually collapses.

## Relation to Courage

[[Cowardice]] and [[courage]] are not opposites distinguished by the presence or absence of fear. Aesop's [[courage]] is fear plus action; cowardice is fear plus flight. Both the cou[[anger|rage]]ous character and the coward feel the same fear; what separates them is the decision made in the moment of testing. The Boy Bathing ([[171-001]]) p[[fortune|luck]]ed up cou[[anger|rage]] while drowning and called for help; the Hunter ([[089-001]]) had every advantage and fled. The distinction is not in the fear but in the will.
