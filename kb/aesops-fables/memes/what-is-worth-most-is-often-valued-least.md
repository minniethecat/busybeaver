## Statement

"What is worth most is often valued least" is the closing moral of [[the-stag-at-the-pool|The Stag at the Pool]] (093-002).

A thirsty Stag bends to drink and sees his reflection: he adm[[anger|ire]]s the spreading majesty of his antlers and feels disgust at the thinness of his legs. Moments later, pursued by a [[the-lion|Lion]], those "disgusting" legs carry him clear of danger — until his "glorious" antlers snag in a tree's branches and deliver him to the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s jaws.

The moral is a systematic critique of human valuation. We consistently [[reward|prize]] the ornamental over the functional, the visible over the vital, and the beautiful over the useful — not because we are stupid, but because beauty is conspicuous and utility is silent.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Stag at the Pool

A Stag, bending to drink, studies his own reflection with a critic's eye:

> A thirsty Stag went down to a pool to drink. As he bent over the surface he saw his own reflection in the water, and was struck with admiration for his fine spreading antlers, but at the same time he felt nothing but disgust for the weakness and slenderness of his legs. (093-001)

A [[the-lion|Lion]] appears. The Stag bolts — and the legs he despised carry him swiftly beyond the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s reach across open ground. But when he enters a wood, his antlers, the object of his pride, become tangled in the branches. [[the-lion|The Lion]] catches him. With his last breath the Stag cries: "I despised my legs, which [[power|might]] have saved my life: but I gloried in my horns, and they have proved my ruin" (093-001).

> What is worth most is often valued least. (093-002)

The Stag's error is not ignorance — he *knows* his legs are thin and his antlers are grand. His error is a category mistake: he evaluates by aesthetic criteria when he should evaluate by survival criteria.

### The Peacock and Juno

A Peacock complains to Juno that he was not given the Nightingale's voice. Juno replies that each creature received its gift: the Eagle has strength, the Nightingale has song, and the Peacock has beauty. To demand another's gift is to despise one's own (080-001). The Peacock, like the Stag, measures what he lacks against what others possess, blind to what he himself has been given.

### The Fox and the Lion

A [[the-fox|Fox]], seeing a [[the-lion|Lion]] for the first time, is terrified. The second time, he watches from a distance. The third time, he approaches and speaks (090-001).

What the [[the-fox|Fox]] initially valued — the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s visible [[fear|terror]] — proves to be a surface judgment. [[the-lion|The Lion]] himself has not changed; only the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s valuation has. [[the-fox|The Fox]] learns [[familiarity-breads-contempt|familiarity breeds contempt]], but the deeper lesson is that first impressions are systematically wrong.

### The Oak and the Reeds

A great Oak [[contempt|scorn]]s the bending Reeds for their weakness — until a gale uproots the Oak while the Reeds survive by yielding (041-001). The Oak's strength is visible and adm[[anger|ire]]d; the Reeds' flexibility is invisible and despised. The wind reveals which is worth more.

## Mechanism

The moral's mechanism is **visibility bias**: traits that are visually conspicuous receive disproportionate valuation, while traits that operate silently receive none. Antlers are large, branching, and crown-like — they *look* valuable. Legs are thin, spindly, and unremarkable — they *look* worthless. But legs run; antlers snag.

The bias has three components:

1. **Aesthetic capture**: The Stag is captivated by the *appearance* of his antlers, mistaking visual grandeur for functional value.

2. **Utility invisibility**: The legs work silently. They have carried the Stag his ent[[anger|ire]] life without once demanding admiration. Their reliability makes them invisible.
3. **Delayed feedback**: The valuation error is only revealed under stress — the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s pursuit. In calm conditions, the Stag can adm[[anger|ire]] his antlers forever without [[punishment|consequence]].

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[utility-is-most-mens-test-of-worth]] — "Utility is most men's test of worth" (069-002) is the positive formulation of the same principle: what is useful is genuinely valuable, [[respect|regard]]less of appearance.

The Stag learns this too late.
- [[appearance]] — The moral is fundamentally about the gap between appearance and reality.

The antlers *appear* valuable; the legs *appear* worthless. Reality reverses both judgments.

- [[vanity]] — Vanity is the engine of misvaluation. The Stag spends his last free moments admiring his reflection, and vanity selects the antlers as the object of admiration because they are the most striking feature.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — [[pride|Pride]] in antlers leads to death by antlers. The fall is built into the object of pride.

## Enduring Influence

The Stag at the Pool is Perry Index 74. The image of the Stag admiring his antlers while despising his legs has become a standard illustration of misvaluation in economics (the "antlers and legs" problem in cost-benefit analysis), organizational behavior (rewarding visible over functional contributions),

and personal development. The moral's endurance lies in its uncomfortable accuracy: each of us can name something we [[reward|prize]]d for its appearance that ultimately trapped us, and something we dismissed whose value we only recognized when it saved us.
