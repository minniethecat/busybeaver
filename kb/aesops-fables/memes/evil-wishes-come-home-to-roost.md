## Statement

"Evil wishes, like fowls, come home to roost" is the explicit moral of The Bee and Jupiter (040-002). A Queen Bee brings Jupiter a gift of fresh honey from Hymettus. Pleased, Jupiter offers her anything she wishes. She asks for stings to kill people who steal honey from the hive. Jupiter, who loves mankind, is displeased — but he has given his word.

> The stings he gave them, however, were of such a kind that whenever a bee stings a man the sting is left in the wound and the bee dies. (040-001)

Jupiter fulfills the letter of the request while nullifying its spirit. The Bee wanted a weapon; she received a suicide pact. The wish came true, and the wisher was its first victim.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Bee and Jupiter (primary)

The fable is a study in **poisoned-gift diplomacy**. Jupiter cannot refuse — he has promised — but he can shape the fulfillment so that the wisher's malice becomes the wisher's punishment. The mechanism is elegant: the Bee's sting kills the thief *and* the bee, so each act of defense is also an act of self-[[ruin|destruction]].

> A Queen Bee from Hymettus flew up to Olympus with some fresh honey from the hive as a present to Jupiter, who was so pleased with the gift that he promised to give her anything she liked to ask for. (040-001)

The gift-giving frame is essential. The Bee approaches Jupiter through [[kindness|generosity]] — honey from Hymettus, the finest in Greece — and uses the [[kindness|goodwill]] to request a weapon. The moral does not condemn self-defense but the conversion of **gift into [[danger|threat]]**.

### The Horse and the Stag (parallel)

The Horse, seeking [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]] against the Stag, asks a Hunter for help. The Hunter mounts the Horse and never dismounts (060-002). Like the Bee, the Horse wishes harm upon a rival and receives it — but the price is his own freedom. The wish is granted; the wisher is caught in the same mechanism.

### The Kites and the Swans (related)

A similar structure appears when the Kites, wishing to be treated like Swans, imitate their song. The horses, who had been fed, hear the Kites' cry, recognize it, and flee (105-001). The wish to be something one is not — like the wish for a weapon —produces the opposite of the intended result: instead of being fed, the Kites are exposed and abandoned.

| Wisher | Wish | Fulfillment | Cost to Wisher |
|--------|------|-------------|----------------|
| Queen Bee | Stings to kill thieves | Sting given | Bee dies with each sting |
| Horse | Vengeance on Stag | Hunter intervenes | Horse becomes slave |
| Kites | Sing like Swans | Imitated song | Exposed as impostors |

## Mechanism

Aesop's "evil wishes come home to roost" operates through a **boomerang structure** in three stages: (1) the wisher identifies an enemy; (2) the wisher appeals to a higher [[strength|power]] for a weapon against that enemy; (3) the higher [[strength|power]] grants the weapon in a form that also destroys the wielder.

The mechanism is distinct from simple revenge or punishment because the [[ruin|destruction]] is **built into the wish itself**. Jupiter does not punish the Bee — he grants her wish exactly, and the wish is its own punishment. The moral is not "the gods will punish evil wishes" but rather "evil wishes, once granted, contain their own [[ruin|destruction]] of the wisher."

This is a more specific mechanism than [[evil-tendencies-shown-early]], which is about essential nature revealing itself over time. The Bee's nature is not evil — she wants to protect her hive. But her wish converts protective instinct into destructive request, and the [[ruin|destruction]] circles back.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[punish-thief-bent-remains]] — The Ant was punished by Jupiter (changed into insect form) but retained its thieving nature (202-002). The Bee was granted, not punished — but the grant functioned as punishment. Both fables show Jupiter's ind[[anger|ire]]ct governance: he does not refuse requests but shapes outcomes so the requestor learns the cost.

- [[evil-tendencies-shown-early]] — "Evil tendencies are early shown" (042-002) describes a wolf cub identified by touch. The Bee's wish is not an early tendency but an adult choice, making the moral graver: the Bee is not discovered evil but **becomes** self-destructive through her own request.

- [[honesty-is-the-best-policy]] — Both fables involve a test before a [[strength|power]]ful figure (Jupiter, Mercury). The Woodman passes by being honest; the Bee fails by asking for a weapon disguised as a gift-return. The Bee's honey is the equivalent of the dishonest companion's claim to the golden axe — both use a false premise to seek gain.

- [[revenge]] — [[revenge|Revenge]] in Aesop is external: the [[the-fox|Fox]] burns the Eagle's nest in retaliation. The Bee's wish is preemptive revenge, and its special horror is that it succeeds — the bee does kill the thief, and the bee does die.
