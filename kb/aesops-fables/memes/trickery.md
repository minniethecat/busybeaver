# Trickery

Trickery — the art of [[disguise|deception]] and [[cleverness|cunning]] — appears throughout [[Aesop's Fables]] as both a survival strategy and a moral failing. From the [[the-fox]]'s clever schemes to the Cat's failed ruse in [[003-001]], trickery drives the p[[fate|lot]]s of many fables and often serves as the pivot on which the [[moral]] turns.

## Definition

In the context of Aesop's Fables, trickery means the deliberate use of [[disguise|deception]], disguise, or clever manipulation to achieve a goal — whether it be escaping danger, obtaining food, or outwitting a rival. Unlike outright [[strength|force]], trickery relies on intelligence and misd[[anger|ire]]ction. The fables present trickery as a morally ambivalent tool: sometimes rewarded (when used by the weak against the strong), sometimes punished (when motivated by [[greed]] or [[pride|arrogance]]). The key distinction is whether the trick serves survival or exploitation.

## Varieties

Aesop's Fables distinguish several varieties of trickery, each with different moral weight:

**[[disguise|Disguise]] and Impersonation.** The trickster adopts a false appearance — the Ass wearing the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s skin ([[061-002]]), or the Cat feigning death ([[003-001]]). These tricks exploit the gap between appearance and reality. The moral consistently warns that borrowed [[power|authority]] is fragile and eventually collapses.

**[[flattery|Flattery]] and Luring.** The trickster uses sweet words to draw the victim within reach — the Owl flattering the Grasshopper ([[155-001]]), the [[the-fox|Fox]] attempting the same ([[205-001]]). Unlike disguise, flattery works by exploiting the victim's vanity rather than their fear.

**Strategic Feigning.** The trickster pretends weakness or [[folly|stupidity]] to gain advantage — the Ass falling into the stream to lighten his salt load ([[045-001]]). This variety tests whether the trickster can outsmart not just the victim but also the master.

## Consequences

Trickery in the fables rarely goes unpunished. The Ass in the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s Skin is exposed when he brays, revealing his true nature ([[061-002]]). The Cat's ruse is seen through by experienced Mice who have learned to distrust ([[003-001]]). Even successful tricks — like the Owl's consumption of the Grasshopper — carry an undertone of cruelty that the fable implicitly condemns. The overarching message is that trickery may bring short-term gain but erodes trust and invites eventual retaliation.

## Relation to Deception

While trickery and [[deception]] are closely related, the fables treat them with a subtle distinction. Trickery implies an **active scheme** — a planned, multi-step maneuver (the Cat hanging from the peg, the Ass finding and wearing a skin). [[deception|Deception]], by contrast, can be a single **untruth** — a lie told in the moment, a false promise made without elaborate staging. Both concepts overlap with [[folly]] when the trickster underestimates the victim's intelligence, and with [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] when over[[trust|confidence]] leads to exposure.
