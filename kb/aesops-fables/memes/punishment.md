## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **punishment** is the inexorable consequence of [[guilt|wrongdoing]] — not always delivered by an external [[power|authority]], but often built into the structure of the wrong act itself. "False [[trust|faith]] may escape human punishment, but cannot escape the divine" (250-002) neatly captures Aesop's layered view: there is human punishment (which can be evaded) and there is cosmic or self-inflicted punishment (which cannot).

## Punishment in the Fables

| Fable | Wrongdoer | Offense | Form of Punishment |
|-------|----------|---------|-------------------|
| The Sick Man and the Gods (fable-244) | The Sick Man | Fraudulent [[promise|vow]] (clay oxen for real) | Divine punishment (250-002) |
| The Slave and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (fable-173) | The Slave's master (implicit) | [[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] | "[[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] sometimes brings its own punishment" (173-002) |
| The Innkeeper and the Wolf (fable-198) | The Innkeeper | [[fear|Fear]]-driven misdeeds | Internal: "maybe they are sent as a punishment for my misdeeds" (198-001) |

## Varieties

1. **Divine Punishment** (fable-244, fable-250): The gods punish fraudulent [[promise|vow]]s. This is punishment from above — external, authoritative, inescapable in the long run even if delayed.

2. **Built-in Punishment** (fable-173): "[[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] sometimes brings its own punishment" — the offense carries its own penalty. No external agent is needed. The ingrate destroys their own future prospects of kindness.

3. **Self-Imagined Punishment** (fable-198): The Innkeeper fears a wolf and imagines his [[fortune|misfortune]]s are "sent as a punishment for my misdeeds." Here Aesop shows punishment as a psychological state — guilt projecting consequences onto random events.

## Consequences

> False faith may escape human punishment, but cannot escape the divine. (250-002)

> Ingratitude sometimes brings its own punishment. (173-002)

Aesop's theory of punishment is remarkably modern: it is more often systemic than personal. The wrong act changes the world in ways that harm the wrongdoer, with or without a punishing agent. The gods en[[strength|force]] the cosmic order, but even without them, "ingratitude brings its own punishment" — the structure of the act contains its own penalty.
