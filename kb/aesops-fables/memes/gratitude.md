## Definition

Gratitude in [[Aesop's Fables]] is not a passive sentiment but an active moral debt — a duty to acknowledge, repay, and protect those who have rendered service. When gratitude is honored, [[friendship|alliance]]s form and lives are spared. When it is violated, the ungrateful suffer swift [[punishment|retribution]], often at the very hands they failed to thank.

The fable "Androcles and the [[the-lion|Lion]]" (later tradition, but thematically central) captures the ideal: the slave removes a thorn from the [[the-lion|lion]]'s paw, and the [[the-lion|lion]] later refuses to harm him in the arena. The bond of gratitude transcends species, [[strength|power]], and circumstance.

## Gratitude in the Fables

Aesop returns to gratitude repeatedly, often through its violation — ingratitude — which serves as an even sharper narrative device.

| Fable | Act of Gratitude or [[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] | Outcome | PN |
|-------|-------------------------------|---------|-----|
| The Oxen and the Butchers | Calf expresses gratitude to Oxen for shelter | Welcomed, but warned of danger ahead | 028-001 |
| Androcles and the [[the-lion|Lion]] | [[the-lion|Lion]] shows "unbounded gratitude" to Androcles | [[the-lion|Lion]]'s gratitude saves both | 038-001 |
| The Serpent and the Eagle | Eagle spared, later shows gratitude | "Show gratitude where gratitude is due" | 056-002 |
| The Serpent and the File | Serpent bites file in ingratitude | "Many a service is met with ingratitude" | 166-002 |
| The Fowler and the Ringdove | Ringdove spared by fowler, shows gratitude | [[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] brings "its own punishment" | 173-002 |

The pattern is clear: Aesop treats ingratitude as a special category of [[foolishness|folly]] — not merely rude but structurally dangerous, because it severs the very bonds that sustain life in a world of predators and prey.

## Varieties

The fables distinguish between:

- **D[[anger|ire]]ct gratitude** — the simple obligation to thank a benefactor. The Oxen episode (028-001) illustrates that even a word of thanks carries weight.
- **Lifesaving gratitude** — the gravest form, when a life has been spared. The ingrate who turns on a rescuer commits the worst [[insult|offence]] in the Aesopic moral universe (166-002).
- **Collective ingratitude** — when a group fails to acknowledge service, as when "Rascals!" cried the man to the runaway servants who "abused them for their ingratitude" (282-001).

> A Serpent was once attacked by an Eagle, but, coiling itself about the Eagle's neck, it was on the point of strangling the bird when a countryman came by and freed the Eagle. The Serpent, angry at the escape of its prey, spat poison at the man. Show gratitude where gratitude is due. (056-002)

> The Lion's gratitude was unbounded; he looked upon the man as his friend. (038-001)

## Consequences

[[ingratitude|Ingratitude]] in Aesop is punished not by divine judgment but by **the natural collapse of the social fabric**. The ungrateful character is abandoned, hunted, or killed — not because the gods intervene, but because no one remains to help. Gratitude, by contrast, creates a network of obligation that is the closest thing to safety the fable world offers.

## Relation to Greed

Where [[greed]] reaches for more than is due, ingratitude refuses to acknowledge what has already been given. Both violate the principle of sufficiency and balance that underlies Aesop's moral universe. [[greed|Greed]] grasps forward; ingratitude erases backward. Together they form the two faces of insatiability.
