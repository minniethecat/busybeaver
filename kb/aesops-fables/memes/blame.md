## Definition

In [[Aesop's Fables]], blame is not a neutral assessment of cause but a contest of positioning. Every character who blames another is simultaneously claiming innocence for themselves. The fables expose this maneuver with surgical clarity: the one who blames first is usually the one most at fault. Blame in Aesop is thus a **second-order signal** — to watch who blames whom is to learn the true distribution of guilt in any situation.

## Varieties

**Pre-emptive Blame.** The Goddess [[fortune|Fortune]] in [[fable-056\|056-001]] delivers one of Aesop's most penetrating insights about human psychology: the Farmer thanks Earth for good [[fortune|luck]] but will blame [[fortune|Fortune]] for [[misfortune|bad luck]]. The blame has not yet occurred — [[fortune|Fortune]] predicts it. The structure is consistent across the corpus: success is attributed to oneself, failure to external [[strength|force]]s. Blame, in this pattern, is not about causation but about self-[[safety|protection]].

**Displaced Blame.** The Sailor in [[fable-086\|086-001]] blames the Wind for his [[fortune|misfortune]] at sea, avoiding the uncomfortable truth that he chose to set sail. The fable does not need to state the moral explicitly — the structure alone exposes it. Blame displaced onto natural [[strength|force]]s, gods, or [[fortune|luck]] is blame that belongs to the blamer.

**Honest Self-Blame.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] in [[fable-186\|186-001]] is the rare exception that proves the rule. She acknowledges: "we are ourselves to blame. The little we gave has cost us all that we had." This moment of self-recognition is so unusual in the corpus that it reads as a lesson in itself — the character who can accept [[blame]] has already transcended it.

## Consequences

The fable universe punishes false blame and rewards honest self-assessment. The Farmer who will blame [[fortune|Fortune]] for his losses is, in [[fortune|Fortune]]'s prediction, destined to repeat the pattern — because he never learns. Blame that is misplaced prevents learning; it is not merely a moral failure but a **cognitive error** that guarantees future failure. The only character who escapes this trap is the [[the-fox|Fox]] in [[fable-186\|186-001]], who accepts blame and, in doing so, gains the clarity to see what went wrong.

## Relation to Guilt

[[Blame]] and [[guilt]] are distinct in Aesop — and the distinction is critical. [[Guilt]] is a state of being (one *is* guilty); blame is an act of speech (one *assigns* blame). A character can be guilty without being blamed, and blamed without being guilty. The Sailor in [[fable-086\|086-001]] blames the Wind but is himself guilty of [[folly|poor judgment]]. [[the-fox|The Fox]] in [[fable-186\|186-001]] is guilty of short-sightedness but, by accepting blame, becomes the only character in the corpus who can genuinely improve. Aesop's lesson: guilt is a fact; blame is a story we tell about that fact.
