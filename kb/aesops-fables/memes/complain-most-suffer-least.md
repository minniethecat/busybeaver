## Statement

"They complain most who suffer least" is the explicit moral of [[The Oxen and the Axletrees]] (074-002). A pair of Oxen strain under a heavy load while the Axletrees, which contribute nothing but friction, creak and groan loudly. The Oxen, exhausted, turn and rebuke them:

> "Hullo, you there! Why do you make such a noise when we do all the work?" (074-001)

The Axletrees are not suffering — they are functioning exactly as designed, rotating freely on their grease. The noise they make is not a signal of distress but a byproduct of their position. The Oxen, who bear the ent[[anger|ire]] weight of the wagon in silence, articulate the moral themselves: **volume of complaint is inversely proportional to magnitude of suffering**.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Oxen and the Axletrees

The Oxen pull the wagon; the Axletrees squeal. The fable is barely a narrative — it is closer to a vignette, a single scene. Its [[strength|force]] comes from the precision of the observation: anyone who has heard a heavy cart pass knows the sound of groaning axles, and anyone who has worked in a group knows the person who makes the most noise about the workload while doing the least (074-001).

### The Ass and the Lapdog

An Ass sees the Farmer's Lapdog petted and indulged while he himself bears heavy loads. The Ass decides to imitate the Lapdog — he clambers onto the Farmer's lap, braying and pawing, thinking he too will be treated as a pet. The Farmer beats him instead (032-001). The Ass's complaint is real — he does do all the work — but his attempt to express it through imitation rather than d[[anger|ire]]ct communication makes him ridiculous. The Axletrees complain without cause; the Ass has cause but complains in the wrong register.

### The Fawn and His Mother

A Fawn asks his mother why she, who is larger and swifter than any hound, always flees from the hunting pack. The Hind replies that she cannot explain — all she knows is that when she hears the hounds, she runs (246-001). The Hind is like the Oxen: she has the most to lose (her life), yet she makes no complaint and offers no theory. The Fawn, who has never been hunted, is the one asking questions — like the Axletrees, the one furthest from danger makes the most noise about it.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **attention asymmetry**: those who suffer genuinely are fully occupied with bearing the suffering. The Oxen are straining; they have no breath for complaint. The Axletrees, under no real load, have the luxury of producing sound. This is a structural, not a moral, relationship. It does not requ[[anger|ire]] malice or laziness — the Axletrees are not choosing to be noisy. It is simply that noise-production and work-production draw from different resources, and the work absorbs the resources that [[power|might]] otherwise go into noise.

The corollary is that complaints should be **discount-weighted by the complainer's proximity to the load**. A complaint from someone carrying nothing is less informative than silence from someone carrying everything.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[do-not-waste-your-pity]] — The moral that [[mercy|pity]] should not be wasted on scamps echoes the Oxen's frustration: the villagers' [[mercy|pity]] is wasted on the Axletrees (who groan) while the real sufferers (the Oxen) go unnoticed.
- [[those-who-will-not-work]] — The idler who refuses to work and the Axletree that groans without bearing weight are cousins. But the Axletree is not refusing — it is simply in a structural position where noise exceeds contribution.
- [[gratitude]] — The Oxen's complaint is not ingratitude but a demand for proportionality: if the Axletrees received the same reward (grease, maintenance) while contributing nothing, the injustice would be clearer. The moral exposes a gap in gratitude — we often thank the noisy rather than the burdened.

## Enduring Influence

The image of the creaking wheel that gets the grease (or, in this case, the creaking axle that draws attention) has a parallel in the English proverb "the squeaky wheel gets the grease." Aesop's version, however, inverts the valence: in the English proverb, squeaking is rewarded; in the fable, squeaking is exposed as fraudulent. The fable endures in political and organizational commentary: the loudest complainers in any group are rarely those bearing the heaviest burdens.
