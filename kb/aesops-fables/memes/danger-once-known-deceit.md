## Statement

"If you are wise you won't be deceived by the innocent airs of those whom you have once found to be dangerous" is the explicit moral of The Cat and the Mice (003-002). A Cat, having eaten the Mice of a house one by one, finds that the survivors have retreated to their holes and refuse to come out. She climbs a wall, hangs herself upside-down from a peg, and pretends to be dead. A Mouse peeps out to inspect the scene and delivers the verdict:

> "Aha! You're very clever, madam, no doubt: but you may turn yourself into a bag of meal hanging there, if you like, yet you won't catch us coming anywhere near you." (003-001)

The Mouse has learned what the Cat is, and no amount of theatrical re-framing can overwrite that knowledge. A known danger cannot deceive by changing costume.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Cat and the Mice

A Cat takes up residence in a house overrun with Mice and eats them one by one. The surviving Mice retreat to their holes. The Cat, realizing she can no longer reach them, devises a trick: she climbs a wall and hangs herself by her hind legs from a peg, pretending to be dead (003-001). A Mouse emerges and sees the Cat hanging there. He is not fooled: "You may turn yourself into a bag of meal, if you like, yet you won't catch us coming anywhere near you." The Mouse's wisdom is experiential: he has seen the Cat hunt, and that knowledge is permanent.

### The Bear and the Travelers

Two Travelers encounter a Bear. One climbs a tree; the other, unable to climb, throws himself on the ground and pretends to be dead. The Bear sniffs the prone Traveler, decides he is dead, and leaves — for a Bear, it is said, will not touch a dead body (037-001). The Traveler who climbed the tree descends and asks jokingly what the Bear whispered in his companion's ear. The reply: "He gave me this piece of advice: never trust a friend who deserts you in danger." The "known danger" here is not the Bear but the **false friend** — and the moral is structurally identical: once you have found someone dangerous, do not be deceived by their subsequent friendliness.

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

A Wolf finds a sheep's skin and wears it to infiltrate the flock. He deceives the Shepherd and kills many sheep before being discovered and hung from a tree (067-001). The Wolf, like the Cat, uses disguise to overcome the natural wariness of his prey. But whereas the Cat's disguise is seen through by the Mouse's experience, the Wolf's disguise works until physical discovery. The difference is instructive: **disguise works on the naive, but the experienced see through the costume**.

### The Fox and the Lion

A [[the-fox|Fox]], meeting a [[the-lion|Lion]] for the first time, is terrified. The second time, he is [[fear|fright]]ened but less so. The third time, he walks up and speaks to the [[the-lion|Lion]] (007-001). This fable is the inverse of the Cat and the Mice: familiarity with danger reduces fear, whereas in the Cat fable, familiarity with danger increases caution. Both are true: experience teaches you what to fear and what not to fear.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **one-shot learning from [[danger|threat]] experience**: a single exposure to a predator's true nature is sufficient to immunize against all future disguises. The logic is:

1. **Classification is permanent**: Once an entity is classified as "dangerous," that classification is not revised downward — the cost of a false negative (being eaten) vastly exceeds the cost of a false positive (missing an opport[[harmony|unity]] to trust).
2. **[[disguise|Disguise]]s are surface features**: Hanging from a peg, wearing a sheepskin, playing dead — these are variations in presentation that do not alter the under[[deception|lying]] entity. The Mice treat the Cat's "dead" posture as noise, not signal.
3. **Shared knowledge**: The Mice collectively know what the Cat is. Knowledge of danger spreads through social learning, so even Mice who did not personally witness the Cat's hunting benefit from the classification.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[wisdom]] — [[wisdom|Wisdom]] in Aesop reduces to "learning from experience," and this moral is wisdom applied to [[danger|threat]] assessment. The Mouse who speaks is not the smartest Mouse — he is the one who has seen the Cat hunt.
- [[safety]] — The companion concept. [[safety|Safety]] is achieved not by eliminating danger (the Cat is still there) but by recognizing it and staying out of reach.
- [[cunning]] — The Cat displays [[cleverness|cunning]] in devising the "pretend to be dead" trick. But [[cleverness|cunning]], the moral teaches, has a shelf life: it works once, maybe twice, but experience neutralizes it.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — The Cat's failure is a form of pride: she assumes the Mice are too simple to see through her trick. The Mouse who speaks punctures that pride with one line.

## Enduring Influence

The moral is the Aesopic origin of the proverb "once bitten, twice shy" and the psychological concept of **preparedness for [[danger|threat]] learning** — the finding that certain fears (snakes, predators, heights) are acqu[[anger|ire]]d in a single trial and never extinguished. The Cat's trick is the original experiment in [[danger|threat]] memory: a single exposure produces lasting imm[[harmony|unity]]. Perry Index 573.
