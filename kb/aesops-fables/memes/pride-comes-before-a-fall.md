## Statement

"[[pride|Pride]] goes before a fall" is the single moral that [[G. K. Chesterton]], in his 1912 introduction, identifies as the unifying truth of the ent[[anger|ire]] fable tradition:

> …the upshot is everywhere essentially the same: that superiority is always insolent, because it is always accidental; that pride goes before a fall; and that there is such a thing as being too clever by half. You will not find any other legend but this written upon the rocks by any hand of man. (P03-006)

Chesterton's claim is audacious: of all the morals in [[Aesop's Fables]], this one is fundamental. Every fable, in his reading, is a variation on the theme that the high will be brought low — not by divine [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]] but by the structural logic of pride itself.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Fighting Cocks and the Eagle

Two Cocks fight for supremacy in the farmyard. The victor flies to a rooftop to crow his triumph — and is immediately seized by an Eagle. The defeated Cock, who had been hiding quietly in a corner, inherits the farmyard. [[pride|Pride]] announces; humility survives. (059-001)

> The victor flew up to a high wall, flapped his wings, and crowed with all his might. An Eagle, sailing by, heard him and made a swoop, carrying him off. (059-001)

### The Hare and the Tortoise

The Hare's pride in his speed causes him to nap mid-race, confident of victory. The Tortoise's steady [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race|persistence]] wins. In the fable's own words: "[[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race|Slow and steady wins the race]]" (117-002).

### The Oxen and the Butchers

The Oxen, proud in their rebel[[the-lion|lion]], sharpen their horns for a massacre — until an old Ox points out that killing the skilled butchers will only bring worse, inexperienced ones. [[pride|Pride]] in rebel[[the-lion|lion]] blinds them to the cost of success.

### The Lion and the Bull

[[the-lion|The Lion]], bloated with self-[[respect|regard]], cannot perceive his own ridiculousness. "Fancy my being afraid of a [[the-lion|lion]]!" (238-002).

## Mechanism

Aesop does not present pride as a sin to be punished but as a **perceptual error** — a misreading of one's position in the world. The proud character stops looking around. The Eagle misses the sky; the Hare misses the finish line; the [[the-lion|Lion]] misses the trap. [[pride|Pride]] is not pride of being wrong but pride of having stopped checking whether one [[power|might]] be wrong.

This is why [[the-fox]] — the most cynical and self-aware of Aesop's characters — rarely falls to pride. [[cunning|Cunning]] is incompatible with self-satisfaction. The [[the-fox|fox]] is always looking over his shoulder.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[greed]] — [[pride|Pride]] and greed often co-occur: the proud believe they deserve more, and the greedy believe they already have more coming
- [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race]] — The Tortoise's victory is a d[[anger|ire]]ct refutation of pride; humility plus [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race|persistence]] outweighs talent plus [[pride|arrogance]]
- [[gratitude]] — [[pride|Pride]] makes gratitude impossible, because the proud believe they earned what they received
