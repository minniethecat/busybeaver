## Statement

"Think twice before you act" is the explicit moral of The Frogs and the Well (203-003). Two Frogs, [[strength|force]]d from their marsh by drought, search for a new home. They come upon a deep well. One Frog peers down and says, "This looks a nice cool place: let us jump in and settle here." The other Frog, described as having "a wiser head on his shoulders," replies with a single devastating question: "Not so fast, my friend: supposing this well dried up like the marsh, how should we get out again?" (203-001). The wiser Frog has identified the **irreversibility** of the decision. A marsh can be walked out of; a well can only be jumped into. The question is not whether the well is pleasant — it is — but whether the commitment is escapable. The moral is less about caution in general than about the specific danger of decisions that close off future options.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Frogs and the Well

The fable's structure is a dialogue between impulse and reflection, compressed into four sentences:

> Two Frogs lived together in a marsh. But one hot summer the marsh dried up, and they left it to look for another place to live in: for frogs like damp places if they can get them. By and by they came to a deep well, and one of them looked down into it, and said to the other, "This looks a nice cool place: let us jump in and settle here." But the other, who had a wiser head on his shoulders, replied, "Not so fast, my friend: supposing this well dried up like the marsh, how should we get out again?" (203-001)

The question is an exercise in what decision theorists call **backward induction**: the wiser Frog projects forward to a future state in which the well has become as inhospitable as the marsh and asks whether the path from that state is traversable. The answer — no, because wells have vertical walls — reveals the well as a trap dressed as a [[safety|refuge]]. The fable ends without telling us what the Frogs did, and that is its strength: the question itself is the resolution. Once the irreversibility is named, the decision answers itself.

### The Fox and the Goat

The companion fable (047-001) enacts what the Frogs avoided. A [[the-fox|Fox]], trapped in a well, persuades a thirsty Goat to jump in by praising the water. The Goat leaps without thinking. Once both are trapped, the [[the-fox|Fox]] stands on the Goat's back to escape — promising to return and help, but of course departing permanently. The Goat is the hasty Frog who did jump. His final predicament is the answer to the wiser Frog's question: "How should we get out again?" The Goat learns that wells have vertical walls only after it is too late.

### The Stag at the Pool

A Stag adm[[anger|ire]]s his antlers in a pool but despises his thin legs (093-001). When hunters appear, his legs carry him to safety, but his antlers become entangled in branches, and he is caught. The Stag's error is the inverse of the Frogs': he evaluated the *present* (antlers are beautiful, legs are ugly) without considering the *future* (antlers will ensnare him, legs will save him). The wiser Frog succeeds by projecting into a hypothetical future; the Stag fails by remaining in the aesthetic present.

### The Hen That Laid the Golden Eggs

The owner of the golden-egg-laying hen, suspecting a treasure inside, kills the hen to extract it all at once (002-001). He finds nothing inside. Like the hasty Frog, the owner acts on a hypothesis without considering the irreversibility of the action — once the hen is dead, there is no undoing the cut. "Think twice" is the prophylaxis against "I should have thought once."

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **irreversibility blindness**: the cognitive failure to distinguish decisions that preserve future options from those that eliminate them. The structure has three elements:

1. **Salience of the attractive present**: The well is cool and damp — exactly what a frog des[[anger|ire]]s. The immediate sensory appeal crowds out consideration of what [[power|might]] follow. The hasty Frog sees "good for right now" and stops thinking.

2. **Invisibility of exit costs**: Entry costs are visible (jumping into the well is easy); exit costs are deferred and invisible (climbing out is near-impossible). Humans and frogs alike systematically undervalue options that are not currently priced.

3. **Symmetry assumption**: The hasty Frog implicitly assumes that leaving the well would be as easy as leaving the marsh — a symmetrical path in and out. The wiser Frog sees the asymmetry: wells are designed to be entered from above and exited only with assistance.

The mechanism is fundamental to irreversible decisions of all kinds: signing a long-term contract, burning a bridge, publishing a statement that cannot be retracted. The wiser Frog's question — "How do we get out?" — is the universal test for commitment decisions: if there is no exit path, the entry had better be worth a permanent stay.

## Enduring Influence

"Think twice" and its twin "look before you leap" are among the most widely cited Aesopic morals in English, functioning as everyday proverbs that requ[[anger|ire]] no knowledge of their source. The concept has been formalised in decision theory as the **option value of waiting** — the insight that deferring an irreversible decision preserves the value of information that may arrive later. In law, the principle appears as *contra proferentem* (interpret ambiguous contracts against the drafter) and similar doctrines that penalise those who rush others into commitments. In software engineering, the maxim "you can't un-ring a bell" captures the same asymmetry: a production database deletion, like a jump into a well, cannot be reversed by wishing. The wiser Frog would have made an excellent engineer.
