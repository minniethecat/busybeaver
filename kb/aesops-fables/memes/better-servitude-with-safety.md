## Statement

"Better servitude with safety than freedom with danger" is the explicit moral of [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the [[the-fox|Fox]] (253-002). A [[the-fox|Fox]], resentful of the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s disproportionate share of their hunt, leaves to hunt alone. He tries to steal a lamb, is spotted by the shepherd, and is killed by dogs. The hunter becomes the hunted.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Lion and the Fox

A [[the-lion|Lion]] and a [[the-fox|Fox]] hunt together — the [[the-fox|Fox]] finds prey, the [[the-lion|Lion]] kills it, and they divide the spoils. [[the-lion|The Lion]] always takes the larger share. Resentful, the [[the-fox|Fox]] sets off alone but is quickly caught and killed.

> The hunter was now the hunted, and was very soon caught and despatched by the dogs. (253-001)

### The Fox Who Served the Lion

A [[the-fox|Fox]], appointed as the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s attendant, discovers that the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[safety|protection]] comes at the cost of [[freedom|independence]]. The fable dramatizes the same trade-off: in a world of predators, the safest place may be beside the strongest predator (P03-004).

### The Wolf and the Housedog

A lean Wolf meets a well-fed Housedog and envies his comfort. But when the Wolf notices the mark of the collar on the Dog's neck — the sign of his servitude — he chooses hunger over chains. This fable presents the opposite choice: freedom, even at cost, over servitude (noted as a counter-example).

## Mechanism

Aesop's logic is **Hobbesian avant la lettre**: the state of nature (freedom without [[safety|protection]]) is worse than subjection to an unequal sovereign. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s complaint — that the [[the-lion|Lion]] takes too large a share — is reasonable but beside the point. [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s share is the price of survival.

This is one of Aesop's darker morals. It does not celebrate servitude; it merely notes that the alternative is worse. [[the-fox|The Fox]] is not wrong to resent the [[the-lion|Lion]] — he is wrong to act on that [[envy|resentment]] without calculating the cost.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[better-poverty-without-care]] — A companion trade-off, but in the economic register: simplicity-vs-obligation rather than safety-vs-freedom. Together these two morals bracket Aesop's deep pessimism about the cost of improvement.
- [[safety]] — The concept page explores the broader theme of [[safety|security]] versus risk across the fable corpus. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s calculation is a case study.
- [[freedom]] — [[freedom|Freedom]] in Aesop is rarely celebrated uncritically. [[the-fox|The Fox]] gains freedom and loses his life; the Housedog loses freedom and gains [[safety|security]].

## Enduring Influence

The tension between [[safety|security]] and freedom this moral articulates is a perennial political theme. Hobbes's Leviathan (1651) makes essentially the same argument: subjects trade freedom for the sovereign's [[safety|protection]], and the bargain is rational even when the sovereign takes an unfair share.
