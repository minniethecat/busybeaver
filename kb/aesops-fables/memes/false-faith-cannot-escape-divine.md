## Statement

"False [[trust|faith]] may escape human punishment, but cannot escape the divine" is the explicit moral of The Eagle and the [[the-fox|Fox]] (250-002). An Eagle and a [[the-fox|Fox]] become friends and decide to live near one another: the Eagle builds a nest at the top of a high tree, the [[the-fox|Fox]] settles in a thicket at the foot. When the [[the-fox|Fox]] goes out foraging, the Eagle — needing food for her own young — flies down, seizes the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s cubs, and carries them up to the nest. [[the-fox|The Fox]] returns to find her cubs gone. She cannot reach the Eagle's nest; she has no human court to appeal to; she is, by any earthly measure, [[strength|power]]less. She sits and curses her betrayer. But the curse finds its vehicle: the Eagle steals a piece of burning flesh from a village sacrifice, the wind ignites her nest, and her half-roasted fledglings fall to the ground, where the [[the-fox|Fox]] devours them "in full sight of the Eagle" (250-001).

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Eagle and the Fox

The fable sets up a complete asymmetry of [[strength|power]]: the Eagle holds the sky; the [[the-fox|Fox]] holds the earth. The Eagle can take from the [[the-fox|Fox]] with imp[[harmony|unity]]; the [[the-fox|Fox]] cannot reciprocate:

> "One day the Fox went out foraging for food, and the Eagle, who also wanted food for her young, flew down into the thicket, caught up the Fox's cubs, and carried them up into the tree for a meal for herself and her family." (250-001)

[[the-fox|The Fox]]'s response is not strategy but lament — the only weapon available to the [[strength|power]]less:

> "When the Fox came back, and found out what had happened, she was not so much sorry for the loss of her cubs as furious because she couldn't get at the Eagle and pay her out for her treachery. So she sat down not far off and cursed her." (250-001)

The curse is not magic. It is a prediction that becomes a fact through an almost accidental chain of causation:

> "Some villagers happened to be sacrificing a goat on a neighbouring altar, and the Eagle flew down and carried off a piece of burning flesh to her nest. There was a strong wind blowing, and the nest caught fire, with the result that her fledglings fell half-roasted to the ground. Then the Fox ran to the spot and devoured them in full sight of the Eagle." (250-001)

### The Wolves and the Dogs

In The Wolves and the Dogs (fable-184), the Wolves persuade the Dogs to betray their human masters, then tear the Dogs apart. The mechanism is structurally similar: [[treachery|betrayal]] is punished, but in that fable the punishment is dispensed by the stronger party (the Wolves). Here the punishment comes from no party at all — it arrives through f[[anger|ire]], wind, and the Eagle's own greed.

### The Eagle and the Beetle

In The Eagle and the Beetle (fable-223), another Eagle wrongs a Beetle by eating the Hare under its [[safety|protection]], dismissing the Beetle as too small to matter. The Beetle exacts a slow, methodical revenge — rolling the Eagle's eggs out of the nest, then tricking Jupiter into shaking them from his own lap. Both Eagle fables share a structural arc: the [[power|might]]y wrong the weak, the weak curse or calculate, and the [[power|might]]y are destroyed by [[strength|force]]s they could not have anticipated.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **divinely mediated [[punishment|retribution]]** — but "divine" here does not mean a god personally intervenes. The burning flesh comes from a sacrifice to a god; the wind that ignites the nest is a natural [[strength|force]]; the [[the-fox|Fox]] who devours the fallen fledglings is an earthly agent. "Divine punishment" operates through the causal fabric of the world, not through miraculous intervention.

Three structural features are at work:

1. **[[power|Power]] asymmetry that invites imp[[harmony|unity]]**: The Eagle believes — rationally, on earthly terms — that she can betray the [[the-fox|Fox]] without [[punishment|consequence]], because the [[the-fox|Fox]] cannot fly. This belief is correct as far as it goes; the [[the-fox|Fox]] never does learn to fly. The Eagle's error is in assuming that the [[the-fox|Fox]] is her only possible enemy.
2. **The curse as diagnosis, not weapon**: [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s curse does not cause the nest-f[[anger|ire]]. The Eagle's own greed causes it — she steals burning meat for herself, just as she stole cubs for herself. The curse is a true description of what the [[the-fox|Fox]] believes must happen, not an incantation that makes it happen.
3. **The cosmic symmetry of punishment**: The Eagle ate the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s children; the [[the-fox|Fox]] eats the Eagle's children. The punishment mirrors the crime. This is not revenge administered by a stronger party but *poetic justice* — a structural symmetry that the world, through f[[anger|ire]] and wind, delivers without any human court or en[[strength|force]]ment mechanism.

## Enduring Influence

The fable is one of the earliest articulations in Western literature of the principle that earthly imp[[harmony|unity]] does not equal cosmic imp[[harmony|unity]]. It stands behind the Greek concept of *nemesis* (the goddess who punishes [[pride|hubris]]) and the broader Indo-European motif of the *unreachable avenger* — the party who cannot strike back d[[anger|ire]]ctly but whose grievance is heard by [[strength|force]]s beyond the human. The image of the [[the-fox|Fox]] watching her own cubs devoured by a "friend" and then devouring that friend's young "in full sight" is one of the most visually symmetrical [[punishment|retribution]]s in the Aesopic corpus — not justice as a legal proceeding but justice as a completed pattern.
