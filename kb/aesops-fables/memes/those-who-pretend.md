## Statement

"Those who pretend to be something they are not only make themselves ridiculous" is the explicit moral of The Crow and the Raven (259-002). A Crow, envious of the Raven's reputation as a bird of omen, positions herself on a branch and caws at passing travellers. Rather than receiving the [[respect|reverence]] she seeks, she is dismissed with contempt: "It's only a crow and that means nothing." The pretension does not elevate the Crow — it makes her more ridiculous than if she had simply remained silent.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Crow and the Raven

A Crow grows jealous of the Raven because men [[respect|regard]] the latter as a bird of omen that foretells the future. Determined to acqu[[anger|ire]] the same reputation, she flies to a branch by the roadside and caws as loudly as she can at some approaching travellers. The travellers are momentarily [[fear|alarm]]ed — until one of them spies the Crow and says:

> "It's all right, my friends, we can go on without fear, for it's only a crow and that means nothing." (259-001)

The Crow's pretension is exposed by a single glance. She is not merely ignored; she is worse off than before, having drawn attention to her own insignificance. The fable draws a sharp line between earned reputation and borrowed performance: the Raven's status rests on genuine cultural recognition, while the Crow's attempt is a theatrical gesture with no substance behind it.

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin

An Ass finds a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s skin and puts it on, striding through the forest terrifying every animal he meets. He enjoys the [[strength|power]] of his borrowed identity until a [[the-fox|Fox]] hears his bray and says, "I [[power|might]] have been [[fear|fright]]ened too, if I had not heard your voice" (027-002). The Ass's natural voice — the one thing he cannot disguise — gives him away. The fable teaches the same lesson as the Crow: the true self will always leak through the costume.

### The Jackdaw and the Doves

A Jackdaw, seeing that the Doves in a dovecote are well-fed, paints himself white and joins them. He keeps silent, and for a while the disguise works. But one day he forgets himself and cries out in his natural voice. The Doves, recognizing a stranger among them, drive him out. He returns to the Jackdaws, but they no longer accept him either — now he belongs nowhere (070-001). Pretension here carries a double cost: not only exposure, but irrecoverable loss of one's original identity.

### The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

A Wolf wraps himself in a sheep's skin and infiltrates the flock. His disguise succeeds for a time, and he devours sheep unnoticed. But the Shepherd, wanting meat for supper, seizes the disguised Wolf and kills him before realizing what he has caught (013-001). Unlike the Crow's harmless pretension, the Wolf's disguise is lethal — but it ends the same way: the costume cannot protect the wearer from those who know how to look.

## Mechanism

The mechanism behind this moral is **irrepressible authenticity**: every creature possesses an irreducible core — a voice, a bray, a nature — that cannot be hidden or altered. The Crow cannot change her caw; the Ass cannot change his bray; the Jackdaw cannot change his cry. The pretension collapses at the moment when the creature forgets to perform, and the true self leaks through.

Aesop's formulation is distinctive because it frames pretension not as a moral failing deserving punishment, but as a practical error that produces its own [[shame|humiliation]]. The Crow is not punished by an external judge — she is exposed by the travellers' casual observation. The punishment is built into the act: pretenders "make themselves ridiculous." There is no need for a punitive god or a moral arbiter; reality does the work.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[villain-may-disguise]] — The companion moral to this one: villains disguise themselves, but the wise see through the mask. The Crow's pretension fails because the travellers are observant; the Cat-as-doctor fails because the Birds are cautious. Both morals converge on the same insight: disguise only works on the inattentive.
- [[hypocrite-deceives-himself]] — The hypocrite, unlike the simple pretender, believes his own performance. The Bear who claims to be refined genuinely thinks he is; the Crow knows she is not a Raven. The hypocrite's delusion is internal, while the pretender's is external — but both fail in the end.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — Pretension is a form of pride: the Crow's des[[anger|ire]] for Raven-status is a refusal to accept her own portion, much like the Peacock's complaint about his voice. The fall from pretension is a fall from pride.

## Enduring Influence

The principle that pretenders expose themselves has become folk wisdom well beyond Aesop: "You can't make a silk purse out of a sow's ear," "You are what you do, not what you say," and the modern idiom "fake it till you make it" (which Aesop would have [[respect|regard]]ed as disastrous advice). The fable is Perry Index number 201. The Crow-and-Raven motif reappears in La Fontaine and in medieval bestiaries, where the crow's caw is consistently a symbol of vain noise without substance.
