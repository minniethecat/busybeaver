## Statement

"There is no virtue in giving to others what is useless to oneself" is the explicit moral of The Wolf and the Horse (219-003). A Wolf, wandering through a field of oats, encounters a Horse and declares that he has left the field untouched "for your sake," adding that he "shall greatly enjoy the sound of your teeth munching the ripe grain." The Horse replies with devastating precision: if wolves could eat oats, the Wolf would hardly have indulged his ears at the cost of his belly. The Wolf's offer costs him nothing and therefore proves nothing. Generosity is measured by what you give up, not by what you let pass.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Wolf and the Horse

A Wolf finds a field of ripe oats. Being unable to eat grain, he is about to continue on his way when a Horse approaches. The Wolf seizes the moment:

> "Look," said the Wolf, "here's a fine field of oats. For your sake I have left it untouched, and I shall greatly enjoy the sound of your teeth munching the ripe grain." (219-001)

The Horse is not deceived. He replies that if wolves could eat oats, the Wolf would hardly have chosen to indulge his ears rather than his belly. The Wolf's gesture is exposed as costless theater: he "gives" what he cannot keep, and "sacrifices" what he never wanted.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf, seeking to eat a Lamb, fabricates a series of [[blame|accusation]]s: the Lamb muddied his drinking water, insulted him last year, and so on (041-001). When each charge is disproved, the Wolf eats the Lamb anyway. The pattern is the same as the oats fable: the Wolf offers reasons as "gifts" of justification — but the reasons cost him nothing because he never intended to be bound by them. His offer of fair argument is as empty as his offer of oats.

### The Fox and the Grapes

A [[the-fox|Fox]], unable to reach a bunch of grapes, declares them sour and walks away (001-001). [[the-fox|The Fox]], like the Wolf, converts inability into a display of choice: "I don't want these" is presented as "these are not worth wanting." [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s dismissal of the grapes is a mirror image of the Wolf's offer of the oats — both transform incapacity into pretense, but one pretends to reject and the other pretends to give.

### The Lion and the Mouse

A [[the-lion|Lion]], having caught a Mouse who begs for his life, releases him — not from [[pity|mercy]], but from amusement at the idea that the Mouse could ever help him (059-001). [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s "gift" of life costs him nothing because he does not believe the Mouse has any value. When the Mouse later gnaws through the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s net and frees him, the [[the-lion|Lion]] learns that **a gift you believe is useless may prove invaluable — the opposite of the Wolf's empty [[sacrifice|offering]]**.

## Mechanism

The moral describes a **cost-signaling failure**: genuine [[kindness|generosity]] requ[[anger|ire]]s a transfer of value from the giver to the receiver. When the transfer costs the giver nothing, it sends no signal of benevolent intent. The mechanism has three conditions for a valid gift:

| Condition | Wolf's Offer | Result |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Giver bears cost | Wolf cannot eat oats — cost is zero | ✗ |
| Receiver gains benefit | Horse can eat oats — benefit is real | ✓ |
| Giver's intent is revealed by cost | No cost → no signal of sacrifice | ✗ |

The Wolf fails on two of three conditions. The Horse, app[[deception|lying]] the same analysis intuitively, dismisses the offer. The moral generalizes: **if it cost you nothing, it proves nothing**.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[gratitude]] — [[gratitude|Gratitude]], the companion concept, requ[[anger|ire]]s that a genuine benefit has been conferred. The Wolf's "gift" of oats cannot generate gratitude because it requ[[anger|ire]]d no sacrifice. [[gratitude|Gratitude]] and empty giving are mutually exclusive.
- [[hypocrisy]] — The Wolf's empty offer is a form of hypocrisy: he performs the role of a generous benefactor while making no actual transfer. The Horse identifies the gap between performance and substance.
- [[sacrifice]] — The fable of the [[the-lion|Lion]] and the Mouse shows the opposite case: the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s release of the Mouse costs nothing at the time, but the narrative honors it because it later proves valuable. [[sacrifice|Sacrifice]] that seems useless may be the most meaningful gift of all.

## Enduring Influence

The phrase "there is no virtue in giving what is useless to oneself" parallels modern concepts of **opport[[harmony|unity]] cost** and **skin in the game**. The Horse's reply — "if you could eat oats, you wouldn't be [[sacrifice|offering]] them" — is an intuitive cost-benefit analysis that cuts through performative altruism. The fable is Perry Index 333.
