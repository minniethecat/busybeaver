[[hercules|Hercules]] (Heracles) is the greatest of the Greek heroes, and his appearances in Aesop's Fables draw on this legendary stature to deliver moral lessons about [[strength]], self-reliance, and the proper limits of mortal ambition. Unlike the animal characters that dominate the fables, Hercules represents the divine-heroic order — a bridge between [[jupiter|Jupiter]]'s authority and the human world.

## Role in Aesop

Hercules appears in four fables (PN=102-001, 209-001, 252-001, 256-001), each using his legendary identity to make a distinct moral point. In these stories, Hercules functions not as a developing character but as a fixed moral reference — the embodiment of heroic virtue against which human failings are measured. His presence in the fable collection reflects Aesop's engagement with Greek mythology as a shared cultural vocabulary for moral instruction.

> "Calling loudly at intervals upon Hercules for assistance, the god himself appeared and said..." (102-001)

## Key Appearances

**The Wagoner and Hercules** (Fable 102). A wagoner whose cart is stuck in the mud prays to Hercules for help. The god appears and tells him to put his own shoulder to the wheel first: "Heaven helps those who help themselves." This is among the most famous morals in the entire Aesopic corpus — a rebuke to passive prayer and an affirmation of human agency.

> "Put your shoulder to the wheel, man, and goad your oxen, and then pray to me for help. For until then your prayers are useless." (102-001)

**The Theban and the Athenian** (Fable 209). A Theban boasts that Hercules was the greatest hero, while an Athenian claims [[theseus|Theseus]]. Their debate about ancestral glory — each claiming a mythic hero for their city — becomes a satire of civic pride. Hercules here serves as the ultimate status symbol, his greatness appropriated for local vanity.

**Hercules and the Wagon of Oxen** (Fable 252). Hercules, travelling along a narrow road, encounters a wagoner who refuses to yield. When Hercules warns him, the wagoner challenges the hero's divine parentage. Hercules responds by crushing the wagon — a demonstration that divine power does not tolerate insolence, no matter how stubborn the mortal.

**Hercules Among the Gods** (Fable 256). When Hercules is received among the gods on Olympus, he greets each deity with courtesy — until [[plutus|Plutus]] (Wealth) approaches. Hercules turns his back, explaining to [[jupiter|Jupiter]] that in his mortal life, wealth was always associated with wicked men. This fable uses Hercules to deliver a sharp critique of wealth's moral corruption.

## Relationship with Jupiter

[[hercules|Hercules]]'s relationship with [[jupiter|Jupiter]] (Zeus) is foundational to his identity as the greatest of [[jupiter|Jupiter]]'s mortal sons. In Fable 256, it is [[jupiter|Jupiter]] who receives Hercules among the gods — the ultimate reward for a life of heroic labor. In Fable 252, Hercules's divine lineage is challenged by a mortal, and his response reaffirms that parentage. The relationship models the proper hierarchy: the hero derives his authority from the supreme god, yet exercises it independently in the mortal realm.

## Symbolism

Hercules symbolizes the ideal of earned divinity — greatness achieved through labor and moral integrity rather than birth or fortune. His rejection of [[plutus|Plutus]] (Wealth) in Fable 256 is the capstone of his Aesopic characterization: true heroism is incompatible with moral compromise. At the same time, his most famous fable (102) insists that even divine help has limits — the mortal must first exert himself. Hercules thus embodies both aspiration and accountability, a divine figure who demands that humans rise to meet him rather than passively receive his aid.
