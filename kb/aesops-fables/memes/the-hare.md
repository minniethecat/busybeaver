## Role in Aesop

The Hare in [[Aesop's Fables]] is the creature defined by flight. Unlike the [[the-fox|Fox]] (who escapes by wit) or the [[the-stag|Stag]] (who fights or hides), the Hare has only one strategy: speed. Across the fables, the Hare is chased by the [[the-eagle|Eagle]] (223-001), caught by the [[hunter]] (281-001), and longs for a kingdom where predator and prey might dwell in peace (181-001).

The Hare's slim corpus — only three significant appearances — reflects its nature: it flees even from narrative centrality. But each appearance is carefully placed in Aesop's moral architecture, testing the possibility of [[protection]], the reality of [[betrayal]], and the limits of [[justice]] for the powerless.

## Key Appearances

### The Eagle and the Beetle

An Eagle chased a Hare that was running for its life. The Hare begged a [[the-beetle|Beetle]] for protection, and the Beetle warned the Eagle not to touch the Hare. But the Eagle, contemptuous of so small a protector, seized and ate the Hare anyway. The Beetle took a terrible revenge: it tracked the Eagle's nest and rolled her eggs out, one by one, until the god [[jupiter|Jupiter]] himself intervened (223-001). The Hare is the catalyst — its death sets in motion a cosmic [[revenge]] drama in which the smallest creature humbles the greatest bird of prey.

> "Presently she espied a Beetle, and begged it to aid her... But the Eagle never noticed the Beetle because it was so small, seized the hare and ate her up." (223-001)

### The Kingdom of the Lion

When the [[the-lion|Lion]] reigned as a just and gentle king, he called an assembly of beasts and drew up a code of laws: "the wolf and the lamb, the tiger and the stag, the leopard and the kid, the dog and the hare, all should dwell side by side in unbroken peace and friendship." The Hare cried out: "Oh! how I have longed for this day when the weak shall take their place without [[fear]] by the side of the strong!" (181-001). The moment is utopian — and by the logic of the fable world, fragile. The Hare yearns for a peace that only a uniquely virtuous [[the-lion|Lion]] can guarantee.

### The Hunter and the Horseman

A [[Hunter]] caught a Hare and was carrying it home. A man on [[the-horse|horseback]] offered to buy it, but the moment the Hare changed hands, the horseman galloped away — stealing both hare and purchase. The Hunter gave up pursuit, realizing he had been [[trickery|tricked]] (281-001). In this fable, the Hare is already dead — a commodity, not a character. It is the object over which [[men]] cheat each other, its body the site of [[human]] dishonesty even after the hunt is over.

## Relationship with the Eagle

The Hare and the [[the-eagle|Eagle]] form the purest predator-prey dyad in the Aesopic corpus. The Eagle is everything the Hare is not: armed with talons, sovereign of the sky, protected by [[jupiter|Jupiter]] himself. Yet the Hare's plea to the Beetle (223-001) reveals the hidden network of the weak: even the smallest creature can avenge what the powerful destroy. The Hare's death is not the end of the story — it is the beginning of the Beetle's war against the [[heavens]]. Through the Hare, Aesop teaches that the powerful should respect even the humblest protector of the humble.

## Symbolism

The Hare in Aesop symbolizes **defenseless swiftness** — the creature whose only gift is flight, and who finds that even flight is not enough. It runs from the Eagle (223-001) and is caught; it is caught by the Hunter and stolen by the Horseman (281-001); it dreams of a peaceable kingdom and is heard only once (181-001). The Hare is the test case for [[justice]]: if the weakest cannot be protected, what does the law protect? The Lion's utopian kingdom (181-001) and the Beetle's terrible revenge (223-001) are two answers — one hopeful, one ferocious — and the Hare's body lies between them.
