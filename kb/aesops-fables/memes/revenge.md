# Revenge

Revenge — the drive to retaliate for harm suffered — runs through [[Aesop's Fables]] as one of the most dangerous impulses in the moral universe. Unlike [[justice]], which seeks proportionate redress, revenge in the fables exceeds its original grievance, setting off chains of damage that consume the avenger along with the target. The fables do not condemn the des[[anger|ire]] for revenge as unnatural; they condemn it as **self-destructive**.

## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, revenge is the act of inflicting harm in return for a perceived injury or injustice. The corpus distinguishes revenge from [[justice]] by its excess: revenge does not restore balance but escalates the [[quarrel|conflict]]. The fables present a consistent pattern — the avenger may succeed in hurting the offender, but the act of revenge invariably produces collateral damage that rebounds upon the avenger. The moral framing treats this not as tragic [[fortune|fate]] but as structural inevitability: revenge is engineered to escape proportionality, and therefore cannot be contained.

> Once upon a time the Oxen determined to be revenged upon the Butchers for the havoc they wrought in their ranks, and plotted to put them to death on a given day. They were all gathered together discussing how best to carry out the plan, and the more violent of them were engaged in sharpening their horns for the fray, when an old Ox got up upon his feet and said, "My brothers, you have good reason, I know, to hate these Butchers, but, at any rate, they understand their trade and do what they have to do without causing unnecessary pain. ## Varieties

**Thwarted Revenge.** The Oxen p[[fate|lot]] revenge against the Butchers ([[119-001]]) but are stopped by the wisdom of an elder who recognizes that the current oppressors, however bad, are known quantities — their replacements would be worse. This variety treats revenge not as morally wrong but as practically foolish: the avenger fails to consider the full cost.

**Escalating Revenge.** The Farmer ties burning tow to the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s tail ([[146-001]]) and the Serpent spits poison into the rescuer's drinking-horn ([[272-001]]). Both cases show revenge exceeding its original grievance — the Farmer loses his ent[[anger|ire]] harvest to f[[anger|ire]]; the Serpent nearly kills an innocent bystander. The fable's verdict, "Revenge is a two-edged sword" ([[146-002]]), captures the structural danger.

**Self-Enslaving Revenge.** The Horse enlists Man's help to expel the Stag ([[264-001]]) and succeeds — but at the cost of accepting bridle and rider, trading one enemy for a permanent master. This variety warns that the ally recruited for revenge may prove a greater [[danger|threat]] than the original enemy.

## Consequences

Revenge in the fables never concludes cleanly. The Farmer's revenge on the [[the-fox|Fox]] burns his own harvest ([[146-001]]). The Horse's revenge on the Stag leaves him enslaved to Man ([[264-001]]). Even when revenge appears justified — the Eagle devoured the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s cubs — the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s satisfaction in devouring the Eagle's fledglings ([[250-001]]) does not restore her loss. The consistent verdict is that revenge creates more damage than it repairs, and the avenger's own interests are among the casualties.

## Relation to Justice

[[Revenge]] and [[justice]] are opposites in the Aesopian moral framework, though they may appear similar from the outside. Justice restores balance: the wrong is acknowledged, the remedy is proportionate, and the cycle closes. Revenge escalates imbalance: the wrong is amplified by retaliation, the remedy exceeds the injury, and the cycle accelerates. The old Ox who dissuades his fellows from killing the Butchers ([[119-001]]) articulates the practical wisdom that underpins this distinction — the current state, however bad, is preferable to the unpredictable cascade that revenge would unleash.
