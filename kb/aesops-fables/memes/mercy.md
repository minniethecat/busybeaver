## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **[[pity|mercy]]** is the restraint of [[strength|power]] — a decision by the strong not to harm the weak when harm would be easy. Unlike [[kindness]], which is proactive [[kindness|benevolence]], [[pity|mercy]] in Aesop is reactive: it is always *asked for* by a victim facing imminent [[ruin|destruction]].

The corpus presents [[pity|mercy]] as conditional and strategic rather than absolute — it is extended only when it aligns with the [[strength|power]]ful party's interest.

## Varieties

Aesop's treatment of [[pity|mercy]] reveals two hard truths:

1. **Mercy Denied When Interests Conflict** (fable-055): The Cock's plea is perfectly rational — "I rouse honest men to their work in the morning" (055-002) — but this very usefulness [[fate|doom]]s him. The Thief's logic is brutal but consistent: "making it still harder for us to get a livelihood. Into the pot you go!"

Mercy requ[[anger|ire]]s the [[strength|power]]ful party to value the victim's utility above their own immediate interest, and the Thief will not.

2. **Mercy as Strategic Choice** (fable-058, by inversion): The Father teaches that [[harmony|unity]] prevents being "at the [[pity|mercy]] of those who attack you" (058-001). The proverb implies that [[pity|mercy]] is not a virtue enemies possess — it is a vulnerability the weak must avoid needing. "Union is strength" (058-002) is, in effect, advice to never be in a position where you must ask for [[pity|mercy]].

## Consequences

> "Pray do not kill me: you will find me a most useful bird, for I rouse honest men to their work in the morning by my crowing." But the Thief replied with some heat, "Yes, I know you do, making it still harder for us to get a livelihood. Into the pot you go!" (055-002)

> "United you will be more than a match for your enemies: but if you quarrel and separate, your weakness will put you at the mercy of those who attack you." (058-001)

Aesop's fables do not feature a single clear instance of [[pity|mercy]] being granted. The Cock's eloquent plea fails. The Father's proverb treats [[pity|mercy]] as something enemies never show. This is a notably grim vision: the weak cannot rely on the compassion of the strong; they must either make themselves useful (and even that may fail) or make themselves strong.

## Relation to Kindness

Mercy and [[kindness]] differ in [[strength|power]] dynamics. [[kindness|Kindness]] is voluntary [[kindness|benevolence]] without a prior [[danger|threat]] — the [[the-lion|Lion]] sparing the Mouse in the famous fable is an act of [[kindness]] (or strategic [[wisdom|foresight]]), not [[pity|mercy]], because the Mouse was not at the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[pity|mercy]] to begin with; he was simply beneath notice.

Mercy requ[[anger|ire]]s the shadow of imminent harm; it is asked for when the knife is already at the throat.
