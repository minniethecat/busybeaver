## Statement

"Notoriety is often mistaken for fame" is the explicit moral of The Mischievous Dog (004-002). A Dog who habitually bites everyone is fitted with a bell by his master so people can hear him coming and avoid him. The Dog, oblivious to the bell's purpose, struts about tinkling it with pride — until an old dog explains its true meaning.

> The Dog was very proud of the bell, and strutted about tinkling it with immense satisfaction. But an old dog came up to him and said, "The fewer airs you give yourself the better, my friend. You don't think, do you, that your bell was given you as a reward of merit? On the contrary, it is a badge of disgrace." (004-001)

The bell is public visibility without distinction of source. The Mischievous Dog commits a **category error**: he reads any sign of noticeability as a sign of honor.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Mischievous Dog (primary)

The fable is tight and two-beat. Beat one: the Dog bites everyone, and his master's solution is a warning bell — a public-health measure, not a decoration.

> There was once a Dog who used to snap at people and bite them without any provocation, and who was a great nuisance to every one who came to his master's house. So his master fastened a bell round his neck to warn people of his presence. (004-001)

Beat two: the Dog reinterprets the bell as an ornament and performs pride. The old dog's correction is clinical: the bell announces your presence so people can **flee**, not so they can adm[[anger|ire]].

| Reading | Author | Effect |
|---------|--------|--------|
| The bell is a medal | The Mischievous Dog | [[pride|Pride]], strutting |
| The bell is a warning | His master | People avoid him |
| The bell is a badge of disgrace | The old dog | Diagnosis delivered |

### The Jackdaw and the Pigeons (parallel)

A Jackdaw paints himself white to pass as a pigeon and join their dovecote. The pigeons discover him by his voice and drive him out. Returning to the jackdaws, they too reject him — he belongs nowhere now (100-001). The Jackdaw mistakes **passing** for **belonging**, just as the Dog mistakes **visibility** for **honor**. Both discover that the signal they thought was status was actually exposure.

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin (parallel)

An Ass finds a [[the-lion|lion]]'s skin and terrifies everyone — until his bray gives him away (138-001). The skin is notoriety mistaken for [[strength|power]]; the bray is the truth that pierces the disguise. Like the Dog's bell, the [[the-lion|lion]]'s skin creates a false reading of identity that collapses under scrutiny.

## Mechanism

Notoriety-mistaken-for-fame operates through **signal-source blindness**. The Dog observes that he has a bell — a visible, audible marker of specialness — and de[[blame|fault]]s to the interpretation that flatters him. He never asks the diagnostic question: *what is this signal for?*

The mechanism is universal because most social signals — titles, uniforms, decorations, followers — are ambiguous between earned honor and assigned warning. The receiver of the signal has an incentive to choose the [[kindness|favor]]able interpretation; the old dog, as an external observer with no stake in the Dog's self-image, can afford accuracy.

The moral is distinct from [[those-who-pretend]]: the Dog is not pretending to be something he is not. He genuinely believes his own misreading. The error is cognitive, not performative — self-[[disguise|deception]] rather than [[disguise|deception]] of others.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[those-who-pretend]] — The person who pretends to be something they are not is engaged in deliberate performance; the Dog is engaged in innocent self-[[disguise|deception]]. Both end in exposure, but the internal mechanism differs.

- [[villain-may-disguise]] — If a [[villain-may-disguise|villain cannot deceive the wise]], the Mischievous Dog confirms the corollary: the unwise can deceive themselves. The Dog is his own easiest victim.

- [[hypocrite-deceives-himself]] — "A hypocrite deceives no one but himself" (098-003). The Dog fits this profile exactly: he alone is fooled by his bell-wearing. Everyone else — his master, the old dog, the people who flee — understands the signal correctly.

- [[greatness-carries-penalties]] — Both morals concern visible markers with hidden meanings. The Mice generals' helmets are genuinely honorific *and* structurally fatal; the Dog's bell is genuinely visible *and* structurally shameful. In both cases the bearer misreads what they carry.
