## Statement

"Be shy of favours bestowed at the expense of others" is the explicit moral of The Dog and the Cook (053-002). A rich man gives a banquet, and his Dog invites a friend: "My master is giving a feast: there'll be a fine spread, so come and dine with me to-night" (053-001). The invited Dog arrives and is delighted by the preparations, wagging his tail "by way of showing his friend how delighted he was to have been asked." But the Cook, seeing a strange Dog in the kitchen, "caught him up by the hind legs and threw him out of the window." The Dog limps away; when other dogs ask how the dinner was, he claims he drank so much wine he cannot remember how he left. The moral's [[strength|force]] lies in the distinction between the host Dog's [[kindness|generosity]] and the Cook's en[[strength|force]]ment of property rights: the host Dog offers what is not his to give, and the guest Dog pays for the host's presumption.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Dog and the Cook

The host Dog extends an invitation in good [[trust|faith]], but the feast is not his to share:

> "A rich man once invited a number of his friends and acquaintances to a banquet. His dog thought it would be a good opportunity to invite another Dog, a friend of his; so he went to him and said, 'My master is giving a feast: there'll be a fine spread, so come and dine with me to-night.'" (053-001)

The invited Dog is innocent — he accepts what appears to be a genuine invitation. But the Cook's perspective is different: a strange Dog in the kitchen is a thief, not a guest. The structural irony is that the host Dog's friendship is genuine but his [[power|authority]] is nonexistent. He acts as if the feast belongs to him, and his friend pays the physical [[punishment|penalty]] for that presumption.

### The Oxen and the Butchers

In The Oxen and the Butchers (fable-018), the Oxen p[[fate|lot]] to kill the Butchers who slaughter them — but an old Ox warns that if the Butchers are killed, less skilled men will take their place and the slaughter will be messier and more painful. The Butchers provide a service at the Oxen's expense, but removing them makes things worse. The parallel is structural: in both fables, the beneficiary of a relationship that harms a third party must consider that the alternative may be worse, or that they themselves may become the third party.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

In The Wolf and the Lamb (fable-008), the Wolf fabricates [[blame|accusation]]s against the Lamb to justify eating him. The Lamb is innocent of every charge, but the Wolf's logic is designed to produce a predetermined outcome. Both fables illustrate that [[strength|power]], not righteousness, determines outcomes when one party controls the terms of the encounter. The invited Dog is not eaten, but he is thrown out the window — the Cook's [[strength|power]] over the kitchen is as absolute as the Wolf's [[strength|power]] over the stream bank.

### The Lion's Share

In [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s Share (fable-017), the [[the-lion|Lion]] goes hunting with several weaker animals and claims the ent[[anger|ire]] kill for himself, inverting the idea of shared enterprise. The invited Dog experiences the inverse: he expects to share in another's feast but finds that the feast's true owner never consented to share. Both fables teach that those who control the resources control the outcome, [[respect|regard]]less of what intermediate promises are made.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **delegated [[power|authority]] without delegated ownership**. The host Dog has access to the feast but not ownership of it. He can extend an invitation, but he cannot guarantee its acceptance by the actual owner. This creates a triangular relationship:

1. **The benefactor** (host Dog): acts with genuine [[kindness|goodwill]] but without [[power|authority]]
2. **The beneficiary** (invited Dog): accepts in good [[trust|faith]], unaware of the [[power|authority]] gap
3. **The true owner** (Cook): en[[strength|force]]s property rights without [[respect|regard]] to intermediate promises

The invited Dog suffers not for his own [[blame|fault]] but for the host Dog's overreach. The moral is not "do not accept favours" but "be shy of favours bestowed at the expense of others" — scrutinise the giver's [[power|authority]] to give. A favour that costs the giver nothing but harms a third party is not a favour; it is complicity in theft, and the complicit party may be the one who pays.

## Enduring Influence

The fable captures a principle that extends well beyond literal feasts: *never accept a benefit that the giver has no right to confer*. The invited Dog's [[shame|humiliation]] — limping away and [[deception|lying]] about how much he drank — is the face-saving response of someone who has learned that his friend's [[kindness|generosity]] was counterfeit. The moral is echoed in modern cautions against accepting gifts from those who are spending other people's money: corporate hospitality, political favours, and borrowed [[kindness|generosity]] all carry the risk that the true owner will arrive to throw the recipient out the window. The Dog's invented excuse — "the wine was so good I don't remember how I got out" — is perhaps the most enduring detail: the human instinct to disguise [[shame|humiliation]] as indulgence.
