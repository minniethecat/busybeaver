## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **[[folly|foolishness]]** denotes a failure of judgment that causes a character to act against their own self-interest. Unlike [[folly]] — which in Aesop often refers to misplaced pride or vanity — [[folly|foolishness]] is rooted in simple credulity, lack of [[wisdom|foresight]], or an inability to learn from warning. Jupiter himself attributed the world's suffering to human [[folly|foolishness]], noting that Ills multiplied "owing to the [[folly|foolishness]] of mankind" (024-001). The foolish character is not necessarily wicked; rather, they fail to see through [[disguise|deception]], ignore wise [[advice|counsel]], or act on impulse without considering [[punishment|consequence]]s.

## Foolishness in the Fables

Aesop portrays [[folly|foolishness]] across diverse scenarios — from diplomatic naivety to filial hypocrisy — always with the same outcome: the foolish suffer exactly the outcome they should have foreseen.

| The Crab and His Mother (fable-050) | Old Crab | Criticized her child for walking sideways while unable to walk straight herself | Exposed her own hypocrisy (050-002) |

## Varieties

Foolishness in Aesop is not monolithic. The fables distinguish several sub-types:

1. **Gullibility** (fable-235, fable-267): The victim believes a predator's promise. The Sheep trust the Wolves' peace proposal; the Stag twice believes the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s flattery. In both cases, the target's own des[[anger|ire]] (peace, honor) blinds them to obvious danger.

2. **Short-sightedness** (fable-161): The Body's Members see only their immediate grievance and fail to grasp systemic interdependence. Their "rebel[[the-lion|lion]]" harms no one but themselves.

3. **Hypocritical Criticism** (fable-050): The Old Crab rebukes her son for a flaw she herself possesses. The folly lies not in the criticism but in the failure of self-awareness.

4. **Collective Complacency** (fable-024): Mankind's passivity allows Ills to proliferate. This is [[folly|foolishness]] at scale — not a single bad decision but a persistent failure of vigilance.

## Consequences

> The result was just what might have been expected: the whole Body soon began to fail, and the Members and all shared in the general collapse. And then they saw too late how foolish they had been. (161-001)

> How can we expect to live at peace with you? Why, even with the dogs at hand to protect us, we are never secure from your murderous attacks! (235-001)

The [[punishment|consequence]]s of [[folly|foolishness]] in Aesop are remarkably consistent: the foolish character invariably suffers the predictable outcome of their [[folly|poor judgment]]. There is no redemption arc. In fable-267, the Stag is given a second [[fortune|chance]] after the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s first pounce misses — and returns to the den anyway. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s post-mortem quip — "a creature who twice walked into a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den can't have got any brains" (267-001) — is Aesop's own verdict on the irredeemably foolish.

## Relation to Wisdom

Foolishness and [[wisdom]] form a d[[anger|ire]]ct opposition in Aesop. In fable-235, the foolish Sheep agree to sacrifice their protectors, while the old Ram — "whose years had brought him wisdom" (235-001) — immediately recognizes the Wolves' [[disguise|deception]]. Similarly, Jupiter's decree in fable-024 frames the human condition as a struggle between Goods (associated with wisdom and divine [[wisdom|foresight]]) and Ills (enabled by [[folly|foolishness]]). Across the corpus, wisdom is consistently associated with age and experience, while [[folly|foolishness]] is the de[[blame|fault]] state of the young, the flattered, and the unwary.
