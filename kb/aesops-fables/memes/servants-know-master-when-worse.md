## Statement

"Servants don't know a good master till they have served a worse" is the explicit moral of The Ass and His Masters (200-002). A Gardener's Ass endures "scanty food, heavy loads, and constant beating" and begs Jupiter for a new master. Jupiter obliges — the Ass is sold to a Potter, where the work is harder still (200-001). The Ass begs again and is sold to a Tanner. On discovering his new master's trade, the Ass cries in despair: "Why wasn't I content to serve either of my former masters, hard as I had to work and badly as I was treated? for they would have buried me decently, but now I shall come in the end to the tanning-vat" (200-001). The moral's [[strength|power]] lies in its trajectory: each change makes things worse, not better, and the Ass only recognises the relative goodness of his first master when faced with the absolute badness of his last.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass and His Masters

The progression [[out-of-the-frying-pan-into-the-fire|from bad to worse]] is charted through three masters:

> "A Gardener had an Ass which had a very hard time of it, what with scanty food, heavy loads, and constant beating. The Ass therefore begged Jupiter to take him away from the Gardener and hand him over to another master." (200-001)

Jupiter sends Mercury to arrange the sale to a Potter. But the Ass finds the Potter even more demanding. He begs Jupiter a second time:

> "But the Ass was as discontented as ever, for he had to work harder than before: so he begged Jupiter for relief a second time, and Jupiter very obligingly arranged that he should be sold to a Tanner." (200-001)

The Tanner's trade is the worst of all — his business is turning animal hides into leather. The Ass realises with horror that under the Gardener or Potter, he would at least have been buried when he died; under the Tanner, his body will be rendered into the very product his master sells. The trajectory is relentlessly downward: Gardener → Potter → Tanner, each step a degradation.

### The Pack-Ass and the Wild Ass

In a complementary fable (fable-200, following the Ass and His Masters), a Wild Ass taunts a Pack-Ass for his servitude: "I am free as the air, and never do a stroke of work" (200-004). But a [[the-lion|Lion]] appears and — because the Pack-Ass has a human driver to protect him — devours only the Wild Ass. The twin fables together form a diptych: the Pack-Ass who envies others learns through changing masters that his [[fate|lot]] was better than he knew; the Wild Ass who boasts of freedom learns that freedom without [[safety|protection]] is fatal.

### The Town Mouse and the Country Mouse

Though a different type (concept/fable), The Town Mouse and the Country Mouse (fable-141) shares the identical structure. The Country Mouse envies the Town Mouse's luxury until he experiences its cost — constant danger from humans. He returns to his simple dinner of barleycorns, now understanding its value. Both fables teach that the goodness of one's own [[fate|lot]] is invisible until compared against a worse alternative.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **comparative [[gratitude|appreciation]] through sequential degradation**. The Ass cannot evaluate his first master objectively because he has no basis for comparison. His suffering under the Gardener is real, but he has no way of knowing whether all masters are equally harsh, or whether some are harsher. The mechanism has two components:

1. **The baseline problem**: Without experience of alternatives, any condition feels like the worst possible. The Ass mistakes "bad" for "worst" because he has no data. Each change provides data — but the data point is always worse than the previous one, so the Ass's baseline keeps ratcheting downward.
2. **Irreversibility of knowledge**: Once the Ass knows that masters can be worse, he wishes he had been content with the Gardener. But this knowledge is useless because he cannot return to the Gardener. The fable is a t[[anger|rage]]dy not of suffering but of *retrospective recognition* — the Ass learns the truth only when it can no longer benefit him.

The fable also contains a structural feature sometimes overlooked: Jupiter grants the Ass's requests. The Ass is not punished for asking — he is given exactly what he asks for, and it is worse each time. The moral is not "don't ask for change" but "ask with accurate information about alternatives."

## Enduring Influence

The Ass's cry — "why wasn't I content?" — is the ancestor of the modern proverb "you don't know what you've got till it's gone" and the closely related "the grass is always greener on the other side." The fable's tripartite structure (three masters, each worse) has become a narrative archetype for the "be careful what you wish for" story. In behavioural economics, the fable illustrates the *focusing illusion*: when we consider a change, we focus on the features that will improve and neglect those that will worsen. The Ass focuses on escaping the Gardener's beatings and fails to consider what a Potter or Tanner [[power|might]] do instead. The fable also illuminates the concept of *reference dependence* in prospect theory — satisfaction is not absolute but relative to a reference point, and the Ass's reference point keeps shifting downward until the final, irreversible realisation.
