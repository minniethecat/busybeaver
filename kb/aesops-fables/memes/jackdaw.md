## Role in Aesop

The Jackdaw occupies a distinctive niche in Aesop's avian hierarchy. Neither a noble raptor like the Eagle nor a beautiful songbird, the Jackdaw is a small corvid whose defining trait is his unwillingness to accept his own station.

Across four fables, the Jackdaw repeatedly attempts to pass himself off as something greater than he is. He disguises himself among Pigeons, adorns himself with borrowed feathers, and imitates the Eagle's hunting prowess. Each time, his pretensions are unmasked and he suffers humiliation or death.

In the preface, G.K. Chesterton names the jackdaw among the symbolic animals through which moral certainties are conveyed (P03-006). The Jackdaw is the Aesopian archetype of the arriviste: dissatisfied with his natural gifts, he reaches for a status he cannot sustain.

## Key Appearances

### The Jackdaw and the Pigeons

A Jackdaw, envious of the well-fed Pigeons in a farmyard, paints himself white and joins their flock. So long as he remains silent, the deception holds.

But the moment he opens his beak to chatter, the Pigeons recognize him as an interloper and peck him mercilessly. Fleeing back to his own kind, he finds the other jackdaws no longer recognize him in his white disguise and drive him away.

He becomes "a homeless wanderer for his pains" — rejected by both the society he tried to infiltrate and the one he abandoned (075-001).

### The Vain Jackdaw

Jupiter announces a contest to appoint a king over the birds, the winner to be the most beautiful. All the birds preen themselves at a stream. The Jackdaw, knowing his "ugly plumage" gives him no chance, waits until the others depart.

He gathers "the most gaudy of the feathers they had dropped" and fastens them about his own body. Before Jupiter's throne, he appears "gayer than any of them." Jupiter is about to crown him king — when the other birds set upon him, "stripped him of his borrowed plumes, and exposed him for the Jackdaw that he was" (089-001).

### The Escaped Jackdaw

A Man catches a Jackdaw and ties a string to its leg, giving it to his children as a pet. Though the Jackdaw becomes fairly tame, he longs for freedom and slips away.

The string still trails from his leg, and before long it entangles in a tree branch. Unable to free himself, the Jackdaw cries in despair: "Alas, in gaining my freedom I have lost my life" (150-001).

### The Eagle, the Jackdaw, and the Shepherd

Seeing an Eagle swoop down and carry off a lamb, a Jackdaw declares he will do the same and plunges at a large ram. His claws become hopelessly tangled in the wool, and he flaps helplessly until the Shepherd arrives.

The Shepherd clips the Jackdaw's wings and takes him home. When his children ask what sort of bird it is, the Shepherd replies: "It's a Jackdaw, and nothing but a Jackdaw: but it wants to be taken for an Eagle" (175-001).

## Relationship with the Eagle

The Jackdaw's most revealing relationship is with the Eagle, the sovereign of the Aesopian sky. In their shared fable (175-001), the Jackdaw does not merely admire the Eagle — he attempts to become him, replicating the Eagle's hunting technique on a ram with disastrous results.

The Eagle is everything the Jackdaw is not: powerful, naturally magnificent, a legitimate raptor. The Shepherd's verdict — "It wants to be taken for an Eagle" — defines the Jackdaw by the distance between his ambition and his nature.

## Symbolism

The Jackdaw is Aesop's emblem of presumptuous imitation. His borrowed plumes — literal feathers, white paint, or the Eagle's hunting technique — stand for all forms of unearned pretension.

His inevitable humiliation reinforces the Aesopian conviction that identity is not elective. As the Shepherd declares, he is "a Jackdaw, and nothing but a Jackdaw" (175-001). The attempt to transcend one's nature through disguise ends not in transformation but in exposure.
