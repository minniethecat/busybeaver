# Promise

A promise in [[Aesop's Fables]] is a fragile thing. Characters make vows to gods, pledge favours to friends, and swear oaths to strangers — but the corpus treats every promise as a test of character that most speakers fail. The fables distinguish sharply between the **promise made in desperation** and the **promise kept at cost**, and they reward only the latter. To promise in Aesop is to enter a debt, and the debtor who forgets his word is seldom forgiven.

## Definition

In Aesop, a promise is a verbal commitment that creates a moral obligation independent of any written contract. The corpus reveals three defining properties: (1) a promise is **public** — it is witnessed by gods, bystanders, or the reader, so evasion is always exposed; (2) a promise is **asymmetric** — the promiser acqu[[anger|ire]]s an obligation while the promisee acqu[[anger|ire]]s an expectation, and the tension between these is the engine of the narrative; (3) a promise is **irreversible** — once spoken, it cannot be unspoken, and the only question is whether it will be kept or broken.

## Varieties

**The Desperate Promise.** The Herdsman who loses his Bull ([[194-001]]) promises first a calf, then, upon seeing the [[the-lion|lion]] devouring it, a full-grown Bull — all for his own escape. Aesop's treatment is clinical: the desperate promiser escalates his offer as danger escalates, but the promise is never tested because the gods never receive it. The Herdsman escapes with nothing to show because he never intended to pay.

**The Mocked Obligation.** The Sick Man ([[244-001]]) vows a hundred oxen to the gods for recovery, then offers clay figurines instead. The gods' response is punitive: he is taught that a promise to the divine is not a negotiation tactic. The moral is explicit: "Do not promise more than you can perform" ([[244-002]]).

**The Kept Promise.** The Mouse ([[019-001]]) is the corpus's exemplar. When the [[the-lion|Lion]] spares her, she promises to repay the kindness. [[the-lion|The Lion]] laughs, but when he is entangled in a hunter's net, the Mouse gnaws through the ropes and frees him. The promise kept restores order; the small creature rises to equal the great.

**The Broken Promise.** [[the-fox|The Fox]] ([[047-001]]) persuades the Goat to help him escape a well, promising to help the Goat out afterwards. Once free, the [[the-fox|Fox]] walks away, and when the Goat protests, the [[the-fox|Fox]] replies with a proverb that shifts blame to the victim. The broken promise in Aesop is never repaid — it is simply exposed.

**The Impossible Promise.** The Tortoise ([[081-001]]) promises treasure to the Eagle in exchange for f[[deception|lying]] lessons. The promise is impossible to fulfill — Tortoises have no treasure. The Eagle, seeing this, drops the Tortoise to his death. Aesop's implicit warning: do not promise what you do not possess.

> "There!" said the Mouse, "you laughed at me when I promised I would repay you: but now you see, even a Mouse can help a Lion." ([[019-001]])

> The Goat called loudly after him and reminded him of his promise to help him out: but the Fox merely turned and said, "If you had as much sense in your head as you have hair in your beard you wouldn't have got into the well without making certain that you could get out again." ([[047-001]])

## Consequences

The Aesopian promise has three outcomes, and two of them are catastrophic. The kept promise ([[019-001]]) rescues both parties and proves that worth is measured by action, not size. The broken promise ([[047-001]]) severs the social bond and leaves the betrayed party stranded. The foolish promise ([[081-001]], [[040-001]]) destroys the promiser, who asked for something that could only harm him. The fables' cumulative message is clear: **the promise binds the speaker more tightly than the listener**, and the one who forgets this learns it at his own expense. The [[truth]]-teller speaks of what is; the promise-maker speaks of what will be — and Aesop is far more suspicious of the future tense.

## Relation to Trust

[[Promise]] is the act; [[trust]] is the enduring disposition that makes promises receivable. Aesop's fables show that a single broken promise destroys trust permanently — the Goat will never again believe the [[the-fox|Fox]], and the gods will not accept the Sick Man's next vow. Conversely, the Mouse's kept promise establishes a bond between the weak and the strong that endures beyond the moment of rescue. In the Aesopian calculus, **promises create trust, but trust is a stock that depreciates with every [[treachery|betrayal]]** and cannot be replenished by further promises alone.
