## Statement

"When you hit back make sure you have got the right man" (218-002) is the explicit moral of The Thief and the Bee-keeper. A thief steals all the honey from an apiary while the Bee-keeper is away. When the bees return and find their hives empty, they attack the innocent Bee-keeper — not the thief who is long gone. The Bee-keeper, in his pain, [[anger|rage]]s at the bees:

> "You ungrateful scoundrels, you let the thief who stole my honey get off scot-free, and then you turn on the man who has always taken such care of you, and sting him to death!" (218-001)

The moral is not that retaliation is wrong but that **misd[[anger|ire]]cted retaliation is self-defeating**. The bees are not wrong to defend their hive — they are wrong to attack the one person most invested in their welfare. The fable construes the Bee-keeper as the bees' caretaker, making the misidentification especially costly: by destroying their keeper, the bees harm themselves.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Thief and the Bee-keeper (primary)

This is the fable from which the moral is drawn. The thief steals honey; the bees return, see empty hives, and attack the only human present. Their grievance is legitimate — their honey is gone — but their targeting is catastrophic. The Bee-keeper's indignant speech makes the point: harm the guilty, not the caretaker.

### The Snake and the Labourer's Son (supporting)

A Labourer's son is bitten by a Snake and dies. The father, in grief-stricken [[anger|rage]], hacks at the Snake with an axe but only cuts off its tail tip. Later, fearing further attacks, he offers the Snake peace. The Snake replies:

> "Take away your gifts. There can never be peace between us: for whenever I see you, I shall remember the loss of my tail; and whenever you see me, you will be thinking of the death of your son." (188-001)

The father's retaliation hit the Snake — the right target — but only partially, and the cycle of injury continues. This is the complementary case: even correct targeting may not end the [[quarrel|conflict]] if the first injury is mortal.

### The Man and the Image (parallel)

A man loads an Image onto his Ass to carry to a temple. Passers-by bow to the Image, but the Ass mistakes the [[respect|reverence]] as d[[anger|ire]]cted at himself and becomes insufferably proud — until the man beats him for his presumption (208-001). The moral is about credit theft, but the targeting error is the same structure: the Ass hits back (with [[pride|arrogance]]) at the wrong party (the worshippers), and the wrong party (his master) punishes him.

### The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf (inversion)

The Shepherd's Boy cries "Wolf! wolf!" falsely, and when a real Wolf comes, no one believes his genuine cry (046-001). The villagers who *fail* to respond are making the inverse error: they fail to hit back at all, because past false [[fear|alarm]]s have taught them to suspect every cry. The targeting error here is not misd[[anger|ire]]ction but inaction — the wrong-man problem inverted.

| Fable | Aggrieved Party | Target Struck | Right Target? | Consequence |
|-------|----------------|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| The Thief and the Bee-keeper | Bees | Bee-keeper | No (thief) | Keeper destroyed |
| The Snake and the Labourer's Son | Father | Snake | Yes, but partial | Cycle continues |
| The Man and the Image | Ass | Passers-by | No | Master punishes Ass |
| The Shepherd's Boy | Villagers | No one (stay home) | Avoidance | Sheep eaten |

## Mechanism

The moral operates through a **misidentification trap**: the aggrieved party acts on the most *visible* target rather than the *correct* one. The thief who stole the honey is invisible — he has already fled. The Bee-keeper standing by the empty hives is visible and present, so the bees attack him. Visibility substitutes for evidence.

This mechanism is distinct from the revenge-cycle moral of [[injuries-never-forgotten]]. There, the problem is that injuries *cannot* be forgotten, so peace offers fail. Here, the problem is that injuries are *assigned to the wrong recipient*. The bees' [[anger|fury]] is real and justified — they are just aiming at the wrong man.

The fable adds a further layer: the Bee-keeper is not merely an innocent bystander but the bees' **steward**. By destroying him, the bees destroy their own care infrastructure. Misd[[anger|ire]]cted retaliation is not only unjust — it is self-wounding.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[injuries-never-forgotten]] — Both morals concern the aftermath of harm. "Injuries are never forgotten" addresses the [[slow-and-steady-wins-the-race|persistence]] of memory between the right two parties; "hit back at the right man" addresses misidentification of the other party. A feud can be both correctly targeted and still unending.

- [[credit-thieves-get-punished]] — The Ass in The Man and the Image claims credit not his due, a mirror of the bees' misplaced blame. [[blame|Blame]] and credit are symmetrical: misd[[anger|ire]]cted blame creates a scapegoat; misd[[anger|ire]]cted credit creates an impostor. Both end in the impostor's punishment.

- [[greatness-carries-penalties]] — The Bee-keeper's position of stewardship makes him vulnerable, just as the Mice generals' plumed helmets make them targets. In both cases, the victim's *role* — steward, leader — creates the vulnerability, though the mechanism differs (visibility of rank vs. visibility of presence).

- [[revenge]] — [[revenge|Revenge]] as a concept presupposes correct targeting: you must know whom to strike. This fable warns that the emotional urgency of revenge short-circuits the identification step. The bees want [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]]; they seize the nearest target. The result is not revenge but collateral damage.
