# Courage

Courage in [[Aesop's Fables]] is not the absence of fear but the mastery of it. The corpus draws a sharp line between **genuine cou[[anger|rage]]** — quiet, tested, rooted in capability — and **boastful cou[[anger|rage]]** — loud, untested, rooted in vanity. Again and again, the loudest boaster proves the weakest in the moment of truth, while the quiet one acts without fanfare.

## Definition

Aesopian cou[[anger|rage]] is the capacity to act rightly in the face of danger. It has three defining features: (1) it is **tested** — cou[[anger|rage]] unproven is merely talk; (2) it is **proportional** — cou[[anger|rage]] that exceeds capability becomes recklessness; (3) it is **quiet** — the truly cou[[anger|rage]]ous do not boast. The fables consistently reward the character who acts bravely without announcing it and punish the one who announces bravery without possessing it.

## Varieties

**Tested Courage.** The Boy who cries for help despite fear ([[171-001]]) exemplifies cou[[anger|rage]] in its simplest form: doing what must be done despite the [[fear|terror]] of the moment. The Boy does not fight the current — he recognizes his limits and acts within them, which is itself a form of cou[[anger|rage]] that the fable endorses.

**Boastful Courage.** The Bull claiming superior strength and cou[[anger|rage]] ([[080-001]]) represents the empty variety. The fable's structure is consistent: the boaster is challenged, fails the test, and is exposed. Boastful cou[[anger|rage]] is cou[[anger|rage]] that exists only in words.

**Delegated Courage.** The Trumpeter who stirs others to fight while staying safely behind ([[133-001]]) embodies a particularly condemned form — using cou[[anger|rage]]-language to send others into danger while avoiding it oneself. The fable's judgment is harsh: such a person is responsible for the bloodshed he enables.

## Consequences

True cou[[anger|rage]] in the fables is rewarded with rescue and survival ([[171-001]]). Boastful cou[[anger|rage]] is punished with exposure and [[shame|humiliation]] ([[080-001]]). The pattern aligns cou[[anger|rage]] with [[wisdom]] — the cou[[anger|rage]]ous character assesses the situation realistically before acting, while the boastful character acts on a self-flattering fiction. This makes [[courage]] the positive counterpart to [[folly]]: [[foolishness|folly]] acts without thinking; cou[[anger|rage]] thinks, then acts despite fear.

## Relation to Folly

[[Courage]] and [[folly]] appear similar from the outside — both involve risk-taking — but the fables separate them sharply. [[Folly]] takes risks without assessing them (the Sheep dismissing their guard-dogs at [[235-001]]). Courage assesses the risk, acknowledges the fear, and acts anyway (the Boy crying for help at [[171-001]]). The distinction is not in the action but in the **awareness** that precedes it.
