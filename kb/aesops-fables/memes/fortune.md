# Fortune

Fortune in [[Aesop's Fables]] is not a goddess but a test. When good [[fate|fortune]] arrives — the Goose that lays golden eggs, the Spendthrift who inherits wealth — the fable immediately asks: will the recipient keep it or destroy it? And when [[fate|fortune]] turns to misfortune, the fable asks an even harder question: who stays? Aesop is relentlessly interested in what prosperity and [[misfortune|adversity]] reveal about the people they visit.

## Definition

Aesopian [[fate|fortune]] is the external circumstance — wealth, luck, favourable or unfavourable events — that befalls a character independently of merit. The corpus treats [[fate|fortune]] as (1) **morally neutral** — the good are not always fortunate, nor the wicked always unfortunate; (2) **testing** — [[fate|fortune]] reveals character rather than creates it; and (3) **transient** — what [[fate|fortune]] gives, [[fate|fortune]] can take back, often through the very instrument that delivered it.

## Varieties

**Fortune as Test of Character.** The Woodcutter sees a [[the-fox|Fox]] fleeing hunters and, asked where the [[the-fox|Fox]] went, points to his hut while saying aloud he saw nothing. [[the-fox|The Fox]] escapes, and the fable's moral — "[[misfortune|Misfortune]] tests the [[honesty|sincerity]] of friends" ([[037-002]]) — is one of Aesop's most quoted lines. Fortune neither helps nor harms d[[anger|ire]]ctly; it creates the conditions under which true character reveals itself.

**Fortune as Destroyer.** The Man and Wife who possess the golden-egg-laying Goose ([[002-001]]) cannot tolerate the slow pace of [[fate|fortune]]. They kill the goose to extract all the gold at once and find nothing inside. Fortune does not destroy them; their own impatience, catalysed by [[fate|fortune]], does.

**Fortune as Illusion.** The Spendthrift ([[014-001]]) has squandered his inheritance and sees a swallow in winter. He sells his coat, believing spring has come. The cold returns and he freezes. Fortune in Aesop is never to be read from signs — the swallow does not make spring any more than a single good day makes a rich man wise.

**Fortune as Pretence.** The Soothsayer ([[130-001]]) tells [[fate|fortune]]s in the marketplace while his own house is being robbed. Aesop's irony is pointed: the man who claims to foretell the future cannot see what is happening to him in the present.

> Misfortune tests the sincerity of friends. ([[037-002]])

> The Miser told him of his misfortune; but the other replied, "Don't grieve about it. Take a stone and put it in the hole, and fancy the gold is still there. It will do you just as much good." ([[262-002]])

## Consequences

The fables do not promise that the virtuous will enjoy good [[fate|fortune]] or that the wicked will suffer misfortune. What they promise is subtler: **[[fate|fortune]] is a lens**. It magnifies what was already there — the miser's fear, the spendthrift's carelessness, the friend's loyalty. When [[fate|fortune]] visits, the only safe posture is [[wisdom|prudence]], because [[fate|fortune]] is never permanent and gratitude for what one has is wiser than scheming for what one lacks. The Goose's owners had everything; they wanted more; they ended with nothing. Aesop returns to this pattern so often it becomes a law: **the inability to bear good [[fate|fortune]] is the surest path to misfortune**.

## Relation to Greed

[[Fortune]] and [[greed]] are neighbours in Aesop's moral landscape, and proximity is dangerous. [[Greed]] is the response to [[fate|fortune]] that cannot leave well enough alone — the Goose's owners are not poor; they are impatient. Fortune provides; greed accelerates. In fable after fable, it is not misfortune that ruins people but their own response to good [[fate|fortune]]. The moral of [[002-001]] is a warning about [[fortune]]: prosperity is a gift that must be received with restraint, because the hand that reaches for more of it may drop what it already holds.
