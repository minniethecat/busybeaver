## Role in Aesop

The Weasel appears in Aesop's Fables as a small but ferocious predator. Unlike the Fox who relies on cunning, or the Lion who commands by natural authority, the Weasel is defined by rigid adherence to principle — a rigidity that proves to be his undoing.

In all three of his appearances, the Weasel functions as an antagonist whose fixed categories ("I am an enemy of all birds") are easily exploited by more flexible adversaries. He is a cautionary figure: the danger of a mind that cannot adapt its principles to circumstances.

## Key Appearances

### The Bat and the Weasels

A Weasel catches a Bat and declares he cannot let it go because he is "an enemy of all birds on principle." The Bat protests that it is a mouse, not a bird, and the Weasel releases it. Later, a different Weasel catches the same Bat and declares he never lets a mouse go. The Bat now claims to be a bird, and this Weasel too is deceived (012-001).

### The Mice and the Weasels

A war between the Mice and the Weasels goes badly for the Mice, who are consistently defeated. The Mice appoint generals from among their largest members, who wear straw-plumed helmets to distinguish themselves. But when the next battle is lost and the Mice flee to their holes, the generals cannot fit through the narrow entrances and are easily caught (101-001).

### The Weasel and the Man

A Man catches a Weasel that has been sneaking about his house and prepares to drown it. The Weasel pleads for its life, arguing that it has been useful in clearing the house of mice. The Man replies: "You have not been altogether useless, I grant you. But who killed the fowls? Who stole the meat? No, no! You do much more harm than good, and die you shall" (231-001).

## Relationship with the Bat

The Weasel's most memorable encounters are with the Bat, who twice escapes death by exploiting the Weasel's categorical thinking (012-001). The Weasels in this fable are not stupid — they inspect the Bat carefully — but they are prisoners of their own principles.

The first Weasel cannot conceive that a creature might be both bird-like and mouse-like; the second Weasel cannot imagine that the same creature who claimed to be a mouse might also credibly claim to be a bird. The Weasel's tragedy is that his principles, which should protect him from error, instead make him predictable and exploitable.

## Symbolism

The Weasel represents the limits of rigid principle untempered by discernment. His predation is natural and, in the case of mice, even useful to humans. But his inability to weigh competing claims — to see that a Bat is neither purely bird nor purely mouse — makes him easy to manipulate.

The Man's verdict in "The Weasel and the Man" (231-001) is the final word: utility in one domain does not excuse harm in another. The Weasel's fate is to be judged by the net balance of his actions, a standard his own categorical thinking cannot accommodate.
