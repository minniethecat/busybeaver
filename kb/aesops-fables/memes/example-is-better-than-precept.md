## Statement

"Example is better than precept" is the explicit moral of The Crab and His Mother (050-003). When the Old Crab commands her son to "walk straight," the Young Crab replies: "Show me how, dear mother, and I'll follow your example." The mother tries — and fails. Her precept was correct, but without example it is worthless.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Crab and His Mother

An Old Crab criticizes her son's sideways walk. The son's response is devastating in its simplicity: demonstrate. When the mother cannot, the lesson transfers from son to mother — she now sees "how foolish she had been to find [[blame|fault]] with her child" (050-002).

> "Why do you walk sideways like that, my son? You ought to walk straight." The Young Crab replied, "Show me how, dear mother, and I'll follow your example." (050-002)

### The North Wind and the Sun

[[persuasion-is-better-than-force]] is itself a lesson in example-over-precept. The Sun does not argue with the North Wind about the superiority of gentleness — it simply demonstrates, and the traveller's discarded cloak is the proof (022-001).

### The Lion and the Bull

A [[the-lion|Lion]] who preaches cou[[anger|rage]] but cannot recognize his own ridiculousness embodies the gap between precept and example. "Fancy my being afraid of a [[the-lion|lion]]!" he says — a precept about [[courage|bravery]] that his own example undermines (238-002).

## Mechanism

Aesop's insight is epistemological: precept without example is **information without demonstration**, and therefore not knowledge but noise. The Crab mother knows the right answer (walk straight) but lacks the capacity to enact it. Her precept is true but useless.

This mechanism is unusually subtle for Aesop — it concerns not moral failure but the structure of teaching itself. You cannot teach what you cannot do. The Old Crab's real [[folly|foolishness]] was not her sideways walk but her assumption that saying something makes it teachable.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[persuasion-is-better-than-force]] — Both morals are about the ineffectiveness of demands. The Sun persuades by demonstrating warmth; the Crab fails because she cannot demonstrate straight walking.
- [[hypocrisy]] — The Crab mother is a hypocrite in the strict sense: she demands of her son what she cannot do herself. But Aesop's point is more practical than moral — hypocrisy fails not because it is wrong but because it is ineffective.

## Enduring Influence

"Example is better than precept" has become a standard English proverb, often rendered as "practice what you preach" or "lead by example." The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 322. Its pedagogical insight — that demonstration trumps instruction — is foundational to modern learning theory.
