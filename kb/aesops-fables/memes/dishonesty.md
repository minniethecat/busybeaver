## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **dishonesty** is the knowing substitution of [[deception|falsehood]] for truth, typically for immediate advantage. Unlike [[deception]] — which in Aesop often involves elaborate stratagems — dishonesty is the simpler sin of the false word: the broken promise, the pretended piety, the lie told for gain. Aesop treats dishonesty as self-defeating in the long run, even when it succeeds in the moment.

## Dishonesty in the Fables

| Fable | Dishonest Character | The Lie | Consequence |
|-------|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| The Sick Man and the Gods (fable-244) | The Sick Man | Vowed a hundred oxen; offered clay figurines when healed | Gods punished his [[ridicule|mockery]] (244-001) |
| The Partridge and the Fowler (fable-269) | The Partridge | Offered to betray its kin to save itself | Fowler refused: "after that treacherous speech you thoroughly deserve your [[fortune|fate]]" (269-001) |

## Varieties

1. **Fraudulent Piety** (fable-244): The sick man [[promise|vow]]s a hundred oxen, recovers, and substitutes clay figurines. "False [[trust|faith]] may escape human punishment, but cannot escape the divine" (250-002). The lie is to the gods themselves.

2. **Treacherous Self-Preservation** (fable-269): The Partridge offers to "decoy other partridges into your nets" (269-001) to save its own life. The Fowler's refusal establishes Aesop's principle: dishonesty that betrays one's own kind earns contempt, not [[pity|mercy]].

## Consequences

> "No," said the Fowler, "I will not spare you. I was going to kill you anyhow, and after that treacherous speech you thoroughly deserve your fate." (269-001)

Dishonesty in Aesop rarely succeeds on its own terms. The Partridge dies not because it was caught, but because its lie revealed its character. The Sick Man is punished not for the illness but for the fraudulent [[promise|vow]]. Aesop's dishonesty is a boomerang: thrown at others, it returns to strike the thrower.

## Relation to Honesty

Dishonesty and [[honesty]] form a moral axis in Aesop. Where honesty is associated with honor and reputation, dishonesty is associated with self-[[ruin|destruction]]. Notably, Aesop does not praise honesty as an abstract virtue — he warns against its opposite as a practical liability.
