## Statement

"If you choose bad companions no one will believe that you are anything but bad yourself" is the explicit moral of The Farmer, the Cranes, and the Stork (153-002). A Farmer sets traps in his newly sown field to catch the cranes that steal his seed. Among the trapped birds is a Stork, who protests that he is not a crane but "the most honest and harmless of birds." The Farmer replies with the moral's verdict: "It's nothing to me what you are: I find you among these cranes, who ruin my crops, and, like them, you shall suffer." The Stork's inner virtue is irrelevant — what registers is his position among the guilty. The moral is not about the corruption of character by bad company, but about the **social epistemology of association**: observers cannot read your heart, so they read your companions.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Farmer, the Cranes, and the Stork

A Farmer sets traps in a cornfield to catch the cranes that pick up his seed. When he checks the traps he finds several cranes — and a Stork. The Stork begs for [[pity|mercy]], arguing that he is not a crane: he is a different species, a harmless bird, "the most honest and harmless of birds." The Farmer is unmoved:

> "It's nothing to me what you are: I find you among these cranes, who ruin my crops, and, like them, you shall suffer." (153-002)

The Farmer's logic is cold but practical: the cost of distinguishing the innocent from the guilty in a mixed group exceeds the benefit. The Stork has chosen to feed in the same field as the crop-destroyers, and choice of feeding ground is all the evidence the Farmer needs.

### The Woodman and His Companions

A Woodman, working in the forest, loses his axe in a deep pool. When the god Mercury retrieves first a golden axe and then a silver axe, the Woodman honestly says neither is his. For his honesty, Mercury grants him all three (017-001). The Woodman's companions, hearing the story, rush to the pool to try the same trick — but when Mercury offers each a golden axe and asks "Is this yours?" they greedily claim it, and Mercury gives them nothing but blows. The companions are not woodcutters but opportunists — and by acting as companions to a lie, they reveal their own nature.

### The Man and His Two Wives

A Man with one gray-ha[[anger|ire]]d wife and one young wife finds himself p[[fortune|luck]]ed bald: each wife, wanting him to look like a man of her own age, pulls out hairs of the opposite color (042-001). The fable illustrates the same structural principle as the Stork's predicament — being caught between two groups can destroy you, [[respect|regard]]less of your own intentions. Association is not just about judgment; it is about the [[strength|force]]s that others' expectations exert on you.

### The Ass and the Lapdog

An Ass, seeing the Farmer's Lapdog fawned over, decides to imitate its behavior and climbs onto the Farmer's lap. The result is not affection but a beating (032-001). The Ass is like the Stork in reverse: the Stork is innocent but judged guilty by association; the Ass is well-intentioned but punished because his behavior is read in the wrong frame. Both fables teach that **context determines how you are read, not your private nature**.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **associational inference**: observers with limited information use the company you keep as a proxy for your character. The logic has three steps:

1. **Sampling constraint**: The observer cannot inspect every individual's inner nature — it costs too much time and attention.
2. **Heuristic substitution**: Instead of evaluating you d[[anger|ire]]ctly, the observer evaluates the ave[[anger|rage]] of your visible companions.
3. **Irreversible judgment**: Once the heuristic returns its verdict, the cost of revisiting it exceeds the benefit, so the judgment sticks.

The Stork's defense — "I am not a crane; look at my feathers" — is factually correct but practically irrelevant. The Farmer has already made the judgment that matters: the Stork was found in the field where the damage was done. The rest is detail.

This mechanism operates in reputation markets, judicial systems, hiring decisions, and social networks. The moral's enduring truth is that **you cannot control who you are — you can only control where you stand**.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[a-man-is-known-by-company]] — The companion moral (built from fable 190), which states the same principle from a different fable. Together they form the strongest Aesopic endorsement of the associational inference model.
- [[honesty]] — The Woodman's honesty is what distinguishes him from his companions. The Woodman wins treasure by telling the truth; his companions lose everything by [[deception|lying]]. The contrast shows that inner virtue does matter — but only when an external test reveals it.
- [[hypocrisy]] — The Stork is not a hypocrite: he genuinely is what he claims to be. But the Farmer treats him as if he were a hypocrite, because the distinction between "I am innocent" and "I look exactly like the guilty" is invisible to external judgment.

## Enduring Influence

The moral has passed into English through the proverb "A man is known by the company he keeps" and the Biblical parallel in 1 Corinthians 15:33: "Bad company corrupts good character." The Latin tag *noscitur a sociis* ("he is known by his associates") is a principle of statutory interpretation in law — a word's meaning is determined by the words around it. The Stork's feathers are a legal brief that never gets filed.
