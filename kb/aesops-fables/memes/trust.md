# Trust

In [[Aesop's Fables]], trust is the bridge between characters — and the bridge is always being tested. The Wolf asks to be trusted with the sheep. [[the-fox|The Fox]] asks to be trusted as a friend. The captive asks to be trusted with a mission. In nearly every case, the one who extends trust to the wrong party pays with his life or his flock. Aesop does not [[advice|counsel]] paranoia, but he insists that trust must be **earned, verified, and never given blindly to the [[strength|power]]ful**.

## Definition

Aesopian trust is the decision to make oneself vulnerable to another's future actions based on an assessment of that other's character. The corpus distinguishes three elements: (1) **the trustee's reputation** — what is known of their past behaviour; (2) **the stakes** — what the truster stands to lose; and (3) **the alignment** — whether the trustee's interests align with or oppose the truster's. Trust placed where alignment is poor is called [[folly|foolishness]]; trust placed where alignment is good is called [[wisdom|prudence]].

## Varieties

**Trust Denied.** The Dog approaches the Sow and asks why she will not trust him near her piglets ([[042-001]]). The Sow's reply is curt: she knows what dogs do in sheepfolds. Here trust is correctly withheld — the Dog's nature, known from past observation, disqualifies him [[respect|regard]]less of his present protestations.

**Trust Betrayed.** The Shepherd ([[152-001]]) raises a Wolf cub among his dogs, believing the Wolf has been domesticated. When the flock is [[danger|threat]]ened and the Shepherd flees for his life, the Wolf leads the other wolves to devour the sheep. The Shepherd's epitaph — "It serves me right for trusting my flock to a Wolf" — is an admission that trust cannot override nature. The Wolf did not change; the Shepherd only believed he had.

**Trust Weaponised.** The Horse, wounded by a Stag's trespass, asks a Man to help him take revenge ([[159-001]]). The Man agrees, mounts the Horse, and from that moment the Horse is a servant, not an ally. Trust extended in anger becomes a chain.

**Trust as Reputation.** The fables treat trust not as a feeling but as a **public record**. The Boy Who Cried Wolf destroyed his trustworthiness through repeated false [[fear|alarm]]s; the Mouse ([[019-001]]) built hers through a single kept promise. Trust, in Aesop, is the sum of all past promises kept or broken. It compounds, and when it goes negative, it is almost never restored.

## Consequences

Trust misplaced in Aesop is nearly always fatal. The Sheep who trust the Wolves ([[122-001]]) are eaten. The Shepherd who trusts the Wolf ([[152-001]]) loses his flock. The Horse who trusts the Man ([[159-001]]) becomes a slave. And yet the corpus is not nihilistic: the Mouse's trust in the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[pity|mercy]] is repaid with rescue ([[019-001]]). The difference is that the Mouse trusted a party whose interests were aligned with her own — the [[the-lion|Lion]] had already spared her, proving his character. **Trust is rational in Aesop only when it is evidence-based**; trust extended on hope or flattery alone is called [[foolishness|folly]].

## Relation to Promise

Where [[promise]] is the act of committing one's word, [[trust]] is the accumulated record of commitments kept or broken. A single promise fulfilled creates a grain of trust; a single promise broken destroys the whole store. The Aesopian calculus is unbearably asymmetric: trust takes a lifetime to build and a moment to destroy. The Mouse ([[019-001]]) illustrates both sides — her promise to repay the [[the-lion|Lion]] seemed absurd, but she kept it, and a bond of [[trust]] formed between the smallest and the greatest creature in the forest.
