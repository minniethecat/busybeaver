## Statement

"The dishonest get no credit, even if they act honestly" is the explicit moral of The Wolf, the [[the-fox|Fox]], and the Ape (143-003). A Wolf charges a [[the-fox|Fox]] with theft before an Ape acting as judge. The Ape hears both sides and delivers a verdict that condemns both: "I do not think that you, O Wolf, ever lost what you claim; but all the same I believe that you, [[the-fox|Fox]], are guilty of the theft, in spite of all your denials" (143-003). The judgment is paradoxical — the [[the-fox|Fox]] may be innocent of this particular charge, but his reputation for dishonesty makes it impossible for him to receive a favourable verdict. The Ape does not judge the case on its facts; he judges it on the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s character, which the [[the-fox|Fox]] himself has established through past conduct.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Wolf, the Fox, and the Ape

A Wolf accuses a [[the-fox|Fox]] of theft. [[the-fox|The Fox]] denies the charge, and the case comes before an Ape:

> "A Wolf charged a Fox with theft, which he denied, and the case was brought before an Ape to be tried. When he had heard the evidence on both sides, the Ape gave judgment as follows: 'I do not think that you, O Wolf, ever lost what you claim; but all the same I believe that you, Fox, are guilty of the theft, in spite of all your denials.'" (143-003)

The Ape's ruling is structurally remarkable: he does not believe the Wolf (the accuser) and yet still believes the [[the-fox|Fox]] is guilty. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s reputation for dishonesty is so entrenched that it overrides the specific evidence of the case. Even when the [[the-fox|Fox]] may be telling the truth *this time*, his past lies have consumed his [[trust|credibility]].

### The Boy Who Cried Wolf

Though not in this corpus, the archetypal parallel — The Shepherd's Boy (the "[[liar-never-believed|boy who cried wolf]]") — shares the identical mechanism. A shepherd boy repeatedly raises false [[fear|alarm]]s of a wolf attack for his own amusement. When a wolf actually appears, no one believes his cries, and the flock is destroyed. In both fables, the dishonest person discovers that truth-telling is not a switch they can flip at will — [[trust|credibility]], once spent, cannot be instantly replenished.

### The Woodman and Mercury

In The Woodman and Mercury (fable-143, preceding the Wolf-[[the-fox|Fox]]-Ape), a Woodman honestly admits that a golden axe and a silver axe are not his, acknowledging only the plain iron axe he lost. Mercury rewards his honesty with all three axes. Another man, seeing this, tries to claim a golden axe as his own, and Mercury denies him not only the [[reward|prize]] but also refuses to recover the axe he had actually lost. The dishonest man gets nothing — not even what was rightfully his. The contrast between the two fables is intentional: honesty earns a bonus; dishonesty forfeits even the baseline.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **reputational discounting**: past dishonesty functions as a coefficient that reduces the [[trust|credibility]] of every future statement, [[respect|regard]]less of its actual truth value. The mechanism operates on three levels:

1. **Character as evidence**: The Ape uses the [[the-fox|Fox]]'s known character as evidence in lieu of facts. This is technically a logical fallacy (appeal to character), but it is rational as a heuristic: if someone has lied in the past, the probability that they are [[deception|lying]] now is higher than for a person with no record of [[deception|lying]].
2. **Irreversibility of reputation**: [[the-fox|The Fox]] cannot undo his reputation by telling the truth in one instance. Credibility is built slowly through repeated honest behaviour and destroyed quickly through dishonesty. A single truthful statement does not restore what years of [[deception|deceit]] have eroded.
3. **The accuser's irrelevance**: The Ape does not even need to believe the Wolf to rule against the [[the-fox|Fox]]. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s character is so tainted that the [[blame|accusation]], however baseless, sticks. The dishonest person has made himself vulnerable to any [[blame|accusation]], true or false.

## Enduring Influence

The phrase "give credit where credit is due" and its negative counterpart — withholding credit from the undeserving — are rooted in fables like this one. The concept of *reputational capital* in modern economics and game theory formalises what the Ape intuits: reputation is an asset accumulated through honest dealing and depleted by dishonesty. Once depleted, it cannot be borrowed against. The fable also anticipates the legal principle that a witness's [[trust|credibility]] may be impeached by evidence of prior dishonest conduct — the Ape is doing informally what modern courts do through cross-examination about character. Perhaps the most enduring insight is the asymmetry: a dishonest person can maintain a false reputation for honesty only so long as they are never tested, but once tested and found wanting, no subsequent test can be passed — because no one will administer it.
