## Statement

"The quarrels of friends are the opportunities of foes" is the explicit moral of [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Three Bulls (122-002). Three Bulls graze together, watched by a [[the-lion|Lion]] who "longed to capture and devour them, but who felt that he was no match for the three so long as they kept together" (122-001). [[the-lion|The Lion]], unable to defeat the Bulls by [[strength|force]], turns to strategy: "he began by false whispers and malicious hints to foment jealousies and distrust among them" (122-001). The stratagem works: the Bulls grow cold, unfriendly, and eventually feed apart. [[the-lion|The Lion]] then falls upon them one by one and kills them all. The moral is defined by its inversion — it is not a statement about what friends *do* but about what foes *exploit*. The friends' quarrel is the condition; the foe's opport[[harmony|unity]] is the [[punishment|consequence]]. This makes it distinct from morals that simply condemn quarrelling: it warns not from sentiment but from strategic self-interest.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Lion and the Three Bulls

Three Bulls graze together, united against a common predator:

> "Three Bulls were grazing in a meadow, and were watched by a Lion, who longed to capture and devour them, but who felt that he was no match for the three so long as they kept together." (122-001)

[[the-lion|The Lion]] knows he cannot defeat the Bulls if they stand together. So he does not attack — he talks:

> "So he began by false whispers and malicious hints to foment jealousies and distrust among them. This stratagem succeeded so well that ere long the Bulls grew cold and unfriendly, and finally avoided each other and fed each one by himself apart." (122-001)

Once the Bulls are isolated, the [[the-lion|Lion]] attacks each in turn and kills them all. [[the-lion|The Lion]] never becomes stronger — he simply waits until the Bulls make themselves weaker by division.

### The Father and His Sons

In a closely related fable (058-001), a father teaches his quarrelsome sons the same lesson through demonstration rather than t[[anger|rage]]dy. He asks each son to break a bundle of sticks: none can. Then he separates the sticks and each snaps easily. "United you will be more than a match for your enemies: but if you quarrel and separate, your weakness will put you at the [[pity|mercy]] of those who attack you" (058-002). The father's sticks and the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s Bulls are the same argument — one told as warning-precept, the other as warning-t[[anger|rage]]dy.

### The Sheep, the Wolf, and the Stag

In The Sheep, the Wolf, and the Stag (fable-122, preceding the Bulls fable), a Stag asks a Sheep for a loan with a Wolf as surety. The Sheep refuses, recognising that both the Wolf and the Stag can outrun her: "How shall I be able to come up with either of you when the debt falls due?" (122-pre). The Sheep's instinct — distrust those who can outrun accountability — is the individual version of the Bulls' mistake. The Sheep stays safe by refusing the partnership; the Bulls perish by allowing theirs to be broken.

## Mechanism

The mechanism operates through **structural weakness introduced by division**. Three Bulls united are stronger than a [[the-lion|Lion]]; three Bulls divided are weaker than a [[the-lion|Lion]]. [[the-lion|The Lion]] does not change the Bulls' individual strength — he changes their relationship. The mechanism has three stages:

1. **Fragmentation**: [[the-lion|The Lion]] introduces false information designed to create suspicion. Each Bull receives whispers that make him doubt the others. The information is false, but the suspicion is real.
2. **Self-enforcing isolation**: Once the Bulls begin to separate, the isolation rein[[strength|force]]s itself. Each Bull, already suspicious, interprets the others' distance as confirmation of the whispers. [[the-lion|The Lion]] need not continue his work — the Bulls maintain the division themselves.
3. **Sequential vulnerability**: [[the-lion|The Lion]] attacks each Bull individually. The t[[anger|rage]]dy is that any one Bull, if still allied with the others, would have been protected — but by the time the first Bull falls, the remaining two are too estranged to reunite.

The fable is a study in *pretextual manipulation*: the [[the-lion|Lion]] needs no [[power|authority]] over the Bulls, only the ability to place false information between them. The lesson is not merely "don't quarrel" — it is "recognise that quarrels, once started, benefit the enemy who may have started them."

## Enduring Influence

[[the-lion|The Lion]]-and-Bulls fable is the archetypal "divide and conquer" narrative, predating by centuries the Latin *divide et impera* attributed to Philip of Macedon and later adopted as a principle of Roman statecraft. The phrase "divide and conquer" itself has become a stock political and military term, but the fable reminds us that its original application was interpersonal — a [[the-lion|Lion]] whispering, not an emperor commanding. The psychological mechanism (creating suspicion through false information) anticipates the modern concept of *social undermining* in organisational psychology, where third-party gossip is used to fracture workplace [[friendship|alliance]]s. The fable's durability lies in its pessimism: the Bulls' [[harmony|unity]] was real, but it was fragile — and the [[the-lion|Lion]] knew exactly where to press.
