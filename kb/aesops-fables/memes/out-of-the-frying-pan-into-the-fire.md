## Statement

"Out of the frying-pan into the f[[anger|ire]]" is the explicit moral of The Stag and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (243-002). Fleeing one danger without pausing to assess what lies ahead can deliver the fugitive into an even worse [[danger|peril]]. The Stag, running from hounds, seeks [[safety|refuge]] in a cave — and finds a [[the-lion|Lion]]. The hounds would have killed him; the [[the-lion|Lion]] does kill him. The moral is not that one should not flee danger but that unexamined flight is as dangerous as unexamined advance.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Stag and the Lion

A hunted Stag makes a split-second decision to take shelter — without checking what the shelter contains:

> A Stag was chased by the hounds, and took refuge in a cave, where he hoped to be safe from his pursuers. Unfortunately the cave contained a Lion, to whom he fell an easy prey. (243-001)

The Stag's dying recognition is a cry against the irony of his [[fortune|fate]]:

> "Unhappy that I am," he cried, "I am saved from the power of the dogs only to fall into the clutches of a Lion." (243-001)

The fable closes with the proverbial judgment:

> Out of the frying-pan into the fire. (243-002)

The Stag's error is not [[coward|cowardice]] but **incomplete [[danger|threat]]-assessment**: he evaluates the danger he is fleeing (the hounds) but fails to evaluate the danger he is entering (the cave). Flight without reconnaissance is as fatal as standing still.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **asymmetric danger substitution**: the fugitive trades one danger for another, but the second danger is not merely different — it is *worse*. Three structural features:

1. **The visibility asymmetry**: The hounds are visible and audible; the [[the-lion|Lion]] is hidden and silent until it is too late. The Stag flees what he can see into what he cannot see. Visibility is not a reliable proxy for severity.
2. **The speed trap**: The Stag must decide at sprinting speed. Deliberation is a luxury the hunted do not have — but the absence of deliberation makes a bad situation worse. The moral exposes the cruelty of emergencies: they demand instant decisions but punish wrong ones with finality.
3. **The cave as false sanctuary**: The cave looks like safety (enclosure, darkness, narrow entrance) but is in fact a trap (one exit, occupied by a predator). The Stag's instinct — hide in enclosed space — is correct against hounds but fatal against a [[the-lion|Lion]]. Instinct calibrated to one [[danger|threat]] fails against another.
