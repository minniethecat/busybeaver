## Definition

In Aesop's Fables, **[[envy|jealousy]]** is the [[envy|resentment]] aroused by another's possession of a des[[anger|ire]]d quality, status, or condition. Unlike [[envy]] — which in Aesop's usage often encompasses a broader [[envy|covetousness]] — [[envy|jealousy]] specifically involves a comparative gaze: the jealous character measures their own [[fate|lot]] against another's and finds it wanting. This resentful comparison invariably leads to self-defeating action, as Aesop illustrates with surgical clarity.

|-------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------|
| The Ass and the Lap-dog (fable-032) | The Ass | The Lap-dog's pampered life | Mimics the dog, smashes furniture, tries to sit on master's lap | Beaten and driven back to the stable (032-001) |
| [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Three Bulls (fable-122) | The Bulls (victims of sown [[envy|jealousy]]) | Each other (suspicion sown by [[the-lion|Lion]]) | Bulls separate and feed apart | [[the-lion|Lion]] picks them off one by one (122-001) |
| The Crow and the Raven (fable-259) | The Crow | The Raven's prophetic reputation | Caws loudly at travellers to seem ominous | Mocked: "it's only a crow and that means nothing" (259-001) |

## Varieties

Aesop distinguishes between [[envy|jealousy]] as an internal failing and [[envy|jealousy]] as a weapon wielded by enemies:

1. **Self-Destructive Jealousy** (fable-032, fable-259): The jealous character acts on their [[envy|resentment]] unprovoked — the Ass tries to become a lap-dog; the Crow tries to be an omen. Both fail because they abandon their own nature to pursue what they envy. The Ass's cry — "Why could I not be satisfied with my natural and honourable position?" (032-001) — is Aesop's own verdict.

2. **Jealousy as a Weapon** (fable-122): [[the-lion|The Lion]] cannot defeat the three Bulls together. His strategy is not [[strength|force]] but division — "false whispers and malicious hints to foment jealousies and distrust among them" (122-001). Here [[envy|jealousy]] is an external tool, injected by a predator who understands that [[harmony|unity]] is the only defense. The moral "the quarrels of friends are the opportunities of foes" (122-002) elevates this to a general principle of vulnerability.

## Consequences

> "Alas!" he cried, "all this I have brought on myself. Why could I not be satisfied with my natural and honourable position, without wishing to imitate the ridiculous antics of that useless little Lap-dog?" (032-001)

> No sooner did the Lion see this than he fell upon them one by one and killed them in turn. (122-001)

The jealous in Aesop never obtain what they covet. The Ass returns to the stable beaten; the Crow is laughed at; the Bulls are eaten. The lesson is not merely moral — it is strategic: [[envy|jealousy]] makes you vulnerable, whether to your own [[folly|foolishness]] (fable-032) or to external predators who exploit division (fable-122).

## Relation to Envy

Jealousy and [[envy]] are closely related in Aesop but carry different narrative functions. [[envy|Envy]] in the fables typically involves wanting what another *has* (possessions, status), whereas [[envy|jealousy]] involves resenting what another *is* (beloved, respected, safe). The Ass is jealous of the dog's *position*, not its food. The Crow envies the Raven's *reputation*, not its nest. This distinction, while subtle, shapes the remedy: the cure for envy may be satisfaction with one's [[fate|lot]]; the cure for [[envy|jealousy]] is acceptance of one's own nature.
