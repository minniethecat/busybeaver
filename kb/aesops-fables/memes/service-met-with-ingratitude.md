## Statement

"Many a service is met with ingratitude" is the explicit moral of The Travellers and the Plane-Tree (166-002). Unlike the Viper's lethal response or the Stag's self-defeating ingratitude, the Plane-tree's moral describes a quieter but more pervasive form of ingratitude: the casual, unreflective dismissal of a benefit while it is still being received. The Travellers are not malicious. They are thoughtless. They enjoy the shade, look up at the tree that provides it, and pronounce it useless. The Plane-tree's reply — "You come and take shelter under me from the scorching sun, and then, in the very act of enjoying the cool shade of my foliage, you abuse me and call me good for nothing!" (166-001) — is the voice of the unappreciated benefactor.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Travellers and the Plane-Tree

Two Travellers on a hot summer's day find shelter under a Plane-tree. Resting in its shade, one turns to the other and dismisses the tree as useless because it bears no fruit:

> "Two Travellers were walking along a bare and dusty road in the heat of a summer's day. Coming presently to a Plane-tree, they joyfully turned aside to shelter from the burning rays of the sun in the deep shade of its spreading branches. As they rested, looking up into the tree, one of them remarked to his companion, 'What a useless tree the Plane is! It bears no fruit and is of no service to man at all.'" (166-001)

The tree answers — and its answer is the fable's structural pivot. The benefactor speaks:

> "The Plane-tree interrupted him with indignation. 'You ungrateful creature!' it cried: 'you come and take shelter under me from the scorching sun, and then, in the very act of enjoying the cool shade of my foliage, you abuse me and call me good for nothing!'" (166-001)

The Traveller's error is a **valuation blindness**: he measures usefulness by a single metric (edible fruit) and is blind to the benefit he is currently consuming (shade). The Plane-tree does not need to punish him — the rebuke itself is the fable's moral action.

### The Fox and the Grapes

[[the-fox|The Fox]] des[[anger|ire]]s grapes he cannot reach. Rather than admit failure, he dismisses the grapes as sour. The Traveller and the [[the-fox|Fox]] are mirror images: the Traveller dismisses what he *is* receiving (shade, while craving fruit); the [[the-fox|Fox]] dismisses what he *cannot* receive (grapes, sour because unreachable). Both fables diagnose the human tendency to devalue what does not meet a narrow, self-serving criterion.

### The Hen with the Golden Eggs

A man owns a Hen that lays one golden egg daily. Dissatisfied with the rate of return, he kills the Hen to extract all the gold at once — and finds nothing inside. The man and the Traveller share the same cognitive error: undervaluing a reliable benefit (daily gold, cool shade) because it falls short of a more ambitious des[[anger|ire]] (instant wealth, edible fruit). Both fail to perceive that the benefit they dismiss is the only benefit they will ever receive from that source.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **consumption-blind ingratitude**: the beneficiary fails to perceive the benefit as a benefit because it does not match a preconceived category of value. Three structural features:

1. **The simultaneity paradox**: The Traveller complains about the tree *while* enjoying its shade. The ingratitude is not retrospective (complaining after the benefit ends) but concurrent — he is ungrateful for the very thing he is using. This makes the ingratitude more cognitively striking: the benefit is present, tangible, undeniable, and still he does not see it.
2. **The single-metric fallacy**: The Traveller judges the Plane-tree by one standard (fruit production) and is blind to all others. The tree provides shade, rest, shelter from the scorching sun — but none of these count on the Traveller's ledger, because his ledger has only one column.
3. **The speaking benefactor**: The Plane-tree is one of the few non-animal entities in Aesop that speaks. Its indignation is not revenge — it is **correction**. The tree does not withdraw its shade; it speaks to teach. The fable is a lesson, not a punishment.

## Enduring Influence

The Plane-tree's rebuke is a compressed lesson in value pluralism: the tree provides shade, not fruit, and shade is what the Traveller needs. The fable's insight — that we often dispa[[anger|rage]] what serves us while craving what does not — anticipates the psychological concept of *hedonic adaptation*: the tendency to take present benefits for granted while fixating on absent ones. The Plane-tree's voice is the voice of every unappreciated provider — parent, teacher, institution — who watches their gift being consumed while being told it is not enough.
