## Statement

"There are two sides to every question" is the explicit moral of The Man and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (080-002). A Man and a [[the-lion|Lion]] travel together, each claiming to be superior in strength and cou[[anger|rage]]:

> "They were still arguing with some heat when they came to a cross-road where there was a statue of a Man strangling a Lion. 'There!' said the Man triumphantly, 'look at that! Doesn't that prove to you that we are stronger than you?' 'Not so fast, my friend,' said the Lion: 'that is only your view of the case. If we Lions could make statues, you may be sure that in most of them you would see the Man underneath.'" (080-001)

[[the-lion|The Lion]]'s reply is epistemologically devastating. He does not [[quarrel|dispute]] the statue's existence or the Man's interpretation of it. Instead he points out that the statue is itself the product of human making, and that evidence produced by one side of a [[quarrel|dispute]] cannot settle the [[quarrel|dispute]]. The fable is less about strength than about the relationship between [[strength|power]] and representation: whoever makes the statues makes the truth.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Man and the Lion

The Man sees the statue and treats it as neutral evidence. [[the-lion|The Lion]] sees the statue and treats it as a human artifact — a product of human hands and human perspective. [[the-lion|The Lion]] cannot sculpt, so his version of the encounter does not exist in stone. His counterfactual — "if we [[the-lion|Lion]]s could make statues" — is unanswerable because it cannot be tested. The fable does not resolve the argument; it ends with the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s rebuttal, leaving the reader to draw the conclusion: the absence of a counter-narrative does not prove the existing narrative is true.

### The Huntsman and the Fisherman

In the fable immediately following (fable-081), a Huntsman and a Fisherman exchange their catches and grow t[[anger|ire]]d of the same food every day. A bystander advises them to stick to their own trades. Each man sees only the desirability of the other's catch and the monotony of his own — a milder version of the perspectival asymmetry in The Man and the [[the-lion|Lion]]. Both fables appear in the same chapter, suggesting a conscious pairing around the theme of limited perspective.

### The Fox and the Grapes

In [[the-fox-and-the-grapes|The Fox and the Grapes]] (fable-001), the [[the-fox|Fox]] cannot reach the grapes and declares them sour. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s statement is a self-serving narrative — he constructs a version of reality in which his failure is not a failure but a wise rejection of inferior fruit. The grapes have no voice to tell their side of the story. The parallel to the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s rebuttal is exact: the [[the-fox|Fox]] controls the narrative because the grapes cannot sculpt statues.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

In The Wolf and the Lamb (fable-008), the Wolf accuses the Lamb of various offenses, each disproved, until he simply eats the Lamb anyway. The Lamb presents a competing narrative — but unlike the [[the-lion|Lion]], the Lamb has no counter-evidence that the Wolf must respect. The Wolf can ignore the Lamb's "side" because the Wolf holds all the [[strength|power]]. The Man and the [[the-lion|Lion]] is the gentler version: the [[the-lion|Lion]] survives because the argument remains verbal and the statue is only a statue.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **representational capture**. One party controls the medium of representation (statues, narratives, official histories), and that party's version of events becomes what passes for truth. The mechanism has three components:

1. **Evidence production asymmetry**: Only one side can produce durable evidence (statues, documents, monuments). The other side's evidence, if it exists, is ephemeral or nonexistent.
2. **Neutrality illusion**: The existing evidence appears neutral because its origin is forgotten or ignored. The Man treats the statue as *evidence* rather than as *human testimony in stone*.
3. **Counterfactual invisibility**: The losing side's version is not false — it is absent. [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s counterfactual cannot be seen, so it cannot compete with what is seen.

The fable's enduring [[strength|power]] is that it does not settle the question of who is actually stronger. [[the-lion|The Lion]] [[power|might]] be right that [[the-lion|Lion]]s would win in a fair fight, or the Man [[power|might]] be right that human [[cleverness|ingenuity]] overcomes brute strength. The point is that the statue cannot settle the question — evidence produced by one party to a [[quarrel|dispute]] is not evidence at all.

## Enduring Influence

The Man and the [[the-lion|Lion]] has become a foundational text for critiques of [[strength|power]] and representation. "Who makes the statues?" is shorthand for asking whose version of events has been preserved and whose has been erased. The fable is regularly cited in historiography (the problem of sources written by the victors), media studies (who controls the narrative), and legal theory (the importance of hearing both sides — *audi alteram partem*). [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s reply anticipates by two and a half millennia the insight that the archive is not neutral: the absence of a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s statue does not mean [[the-lion|Lion]]s never won. In contemporary discourse, the fable is invoked whenever a dominant group's narrative is challenged by a counter-narrative from those who were previously excluded from the means of representation — the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s descendants have, in some domains, finally learned to sculpt.
