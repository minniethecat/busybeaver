# Envy

Envy — the resentful des[[anger|ire]] for another's advantages — is one of the most self-destructive vices in [[Aesop's Fables]]. Unlike [[greed]], which drives one to acqu[[anger|ire]] more, [[jealousy|envy]] fixates on what others have and poisons the envious from within. Across the corpus, the envious character consistently brings ruin upon **themselves**, not upon the object of their [[jealousy|envy]].

## Definition

In the moral universe of Aesop, [[jealousy|envy]] is the emotion that arises when one creature perceives another's superiority — in beauty, possessions, talent, or [[fate|fortune]] — and responds not with admiration or emulation, but with bitter resentment. The key characteristic of Aesopian [[jealousy|envy]] is its **self-destructive** nature: the envious character wastes energy comparing, scheming, and ultimately destroying their own peace, while the envied party remains untouched by the drama.

## Varieties

Aesop distinguishes several manifestations of [[jealousy|envy]]:

**Envy of Appearance.** The Crow [[jealousy|envy]]ing the Swan's whiteness ([[148-001]]) shows the futility of [[jealousy|envy]]ing inborn traits. No amount of washing can change a crow's feathers. This variety warns against [[discontent|dissatisfaction]] with one's natural endowments.

**Envy of Status.** The Ass [[jealousy|envy]]ing the Lap-Dog's privileged position in the household ([[070-001]]) demonstrates how the envious misunderstand what they [[jealousy|envy]]. The Ass saw only the Lap-Dog's rewards, not that they came from being a different kind of creature ent[[anger|ire]]ly.

**Envy of Talent.** The Camel [[jealousy|envy]]ing the Monkey's dancing ([[164-001]]) shows that [[jealousy|envy]]ing another's skill without possessing the under[[deception|lying]] aptitude leads to [[shame|humiliation]] rather than achievement.

## Consequences

The fables are remarkably consistent: the envious character suffers, while the envied remains unaffected. The Crow dies of starvation. The Ass gets beaten. The Camel becomes a laughingstock. This pattern rein[[strength|force]]s the moral that [[envy]] is a self-inflicted wound — it consumes the envious while leaving the envied intact. Unlike [[trickery]], which can occasionally succeed, [[jealousy|envy]] in Aesop has **zero** success rate.

## Relation to Greed

[[Envy]] and [[greed]] are twin vices but operate differently. [[Greed]] reaches outward — the greedy want *more* for themselves [[respect|regard]]less of what others have. Envy looks sideways — the envious want what *someone else* has, often without even knowing what to do with it. The Crow did not need white feathers; the Ass could not use the Lap-Dog's privileges. This distinction is why [[greed]] in Aesop occasionally leads to loss (through overreaching), while [[jealousy|envy]] always leads d[[anger|ire]]ctly to self-[[ruin|destruction]].
