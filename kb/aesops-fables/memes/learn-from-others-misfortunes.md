## Statement

"Happy is he who learns from the [[fortune|misfortune]]s of others" is the explicit moral of [[the-lion|The Lion]], the [[the-fox|Fox]], and the Ass (246-002). A [[the-lion|Lion]], a [[the-fox|Fox]], and an Ass hunt together and take a large booty.

[[the-lion|The Lion]] asks the Ass to divide the spoils. The Ass, app[[deception|lying]] the principle of fairness, splits everything into three equal parts:

> "The Ass divided it all into three equal parts, and modestly begged the others to take their choice; at which the Lion, bursting with fury, sprang upon the Ass and tore him to pieces." (246-001)

[[the-lion|The Lion]] then asks the [[the-fox|Fox]] to divide. [[the-fox|The Fox]] gives almost everything to the [[the-lion|Lion]], keeping only a tiny morsel. When asked how he learned to divide so well, the [[the-fox|Fox]] replies:

> "Me? Oh, I took a lesson from the Ass." (246-001)

The moral is about the **economic value of observation**. [[the-fox|The Fox]] paid nothing for the Ass's lesson. The Ass paid with his life. [[the-fox|The Fox]] simply watched, understood, and acted accordingly.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Lion, the Fox, and the Ass

The fable is a miniature t[[anger|rage]]dy in three acts. Act One: the hunt succeeds and the Ass is asked to divide the booty. He applies the only principle he knows — equality — and the [[the-lion|Lion]] tears him to pieces. Act Two: the [[the-fox|Fox]] is asked to divide. He has just watched the Ass die. He does not divide equally.

Act Three: the [[the-lion|Lion]] asks how he learned. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s reply — "I took a lesson from the Ass" — is both true and evasive. It is literally correct that he observed the Ass's [[fortune|fate]] and adjusted.

But it is also a deflection. [[the-fox|The Fox]] does not say "I saw you are a tyrant," which would be the complete truth. Instead, he credits the Ass. [[the-fox|The Fox]] has learned a second lesson: do not state the truth about [[strength|power]] in the presence of [[strength|power]].

### The Sick Stag

A Stag, too sick to find fresh pasture, lies down in a field where cattle feed. When the cattle return, they trample him (PN=076-001). The lesson is clear: do not bed down where the [[strength|power]]ful gather. But unlike the [[the-fox|Fox]], no one in the Stag's fable learns until it is too late.

### The Lion and the Mouse

In [[one-good-turn-deserves-another]], the [[the-lion|Lion]] learns that the small can help the great — but he learns from his own experience, not observation. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s method is superior: he learns without risk, by watching.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **vicarious learning through costly observation**.

First, the Ass's death provides **zero-cost information**. The Ass bore the full cost of the experiment (testing whether the [[the-lion|Lion]] tolerates equal division). [[the-fox|The Fox]] got the result for free.

Second, the [[the-fox|Fox]] demonstrates **single-trial learning**. One death is enough to teach that the [[the-lion|Lion]] does not share. This is model-based learning from an unambiguous outcome, not statistical learning requiring multiple trials.

Third, the [[the-fox|Fox]] **generalizes**: he learns not merely "equal division is dangerous" but the deeper principle — an absolute ruler takes everything. This allows him to adapt to future interactions beyond division of spoils.

The Ass's error is the mirror image. A creature who hunts with a [[the-lion|Lion]] should know [[the-lion|Lion]]s do not share. App[[deception|lying]] abstract fairness principles in the presence of [[strength|power]] is a dangerous act.

## Enduring Influence

[[the-fox|The Fox]]'s method — learn from the dead to stay alive — is the foundation of case-based reasoning. Every post-mortem, every autopsy, every after-action review attempts exactly what the [[the-fox|Fox]] did: extract a general lesson from a specific [[fortune|misfortune]]. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s reply is the motto of every survivor who watched a predecessor fail and silently adjusted course.
