## Statement

"You may change your habits, but not your nature" is the explicit moral of The Crow and the Swan (148-002). Among the Aesopic morals that address the fixity of character, this one makes the subtlest distinction: between **habit** (what you do) and **nature** (what you are). The Crow does change his habits — he leaves the altars, moves to the pools, bathes repeatedly. His behaviour is genuinely different. But his nature — the blackness of his feathers — is unaffected by any amount of washing. The fable does not say change is impossible; it says change is bounded by essence. The Crow's error is not that he wants to improve — it is that he mistakes a surface behaviour (bathing) for a transformative act (becoming white).

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Crow and the Swan

A Crow, observing a Swan's beautiful white plumage, attributes the whiteness to the Swan's aquatic habitat rather than to the Swan's nature:

> "A Crow was filled with envy on seeing the beautiful white plumage of a Swan, and thought it was due to the water in which the Swan constantly bathed and swam. So he left the neighbourhood of the altars, where he got his living by picking up bits of the meat offered in sacrifice, and went and lived among the pools and streams." (148-001)

The Crow's causal reasoning is wrong — the Swan is white because it is a Swan, not because it swims. But the Crow does not learn this until he has abandoned his food source:

> "But though he bathed and washed his feathers many times a day, he didn't make them any whiter, and at last died of hunger into the bargain." (148-001)

The double [[punishment|penalty]] is instructive: the Crow does not merely fail to become white — he also loses the life he had. The attempt to transcend his nature costs him the sustenance that his nature did provide.

### The Ass in the Lion's Skin

An Ass wears a [[the-lion|Lion]]'s skin and terrifies everyone — until he brays. The skin is a habit (an external behaviour); the bray is nature (an internal essence). The Ass can change what he wears but not what he sounds like. The Crow and the Ass are parallel cases: both adopt new external behaviours (bathing daily, wearing a skin) that fail to alter the internal reality. The Ass's exposure is more dramatic (he is beaten); the Crow's is more gradual (he starves). Both fables teach the same architecture: behaviour can mask nature temporarily, but nature will reassert itself.

### The Cat and Aphrodite

A Cat falls in love with a handsome young man and prays to Aphrodite to be turned into a woman. The goddess grants the wish, and the Cat-woman marries the man. But when a mouse runs across the floor, the transformed Cat leaps after it. Aphrodite, offended, turns her back. The Cat's nature — the hunting instinct — survives even divine transformation. The Crow, the Ass, and the Cat form a trilogy: nature cannot be washed off, disguised, or transformed by a goddess. It is the bedrock beneath all surface change.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **nature-habit asymmetry**: habits are acqu[[anger|ire]]d, reversible, and external; nature is innate, fixed, and internal. Three structural features:

1. **The causal misattribution**: The Crow sees correlation (Swan + water = white) and infers causation (water → whiteness). He fails to consider the alternative hypothesis: the Swan is white because it is a Swan. The fable diagnoses a cognitive error — the confusion of enabling conditions (the Swan bathes in water) with determining causes (the Swan is a Swan).
2. **The opport[[harmony|unity]] cost of transformation**: The Crow does not merely fail to become white — he dies of hunger. His attempt to change his nature costs him the ecological niche (altar scraps) that sustained his old life. The fable's warning is not merely "you cannot change" but "attempting to change will cost you what you have."
3. **Effort is not sufficient**: The Crow bathes "many times a day" — he is diligent, persistent, committed. His failure is not laziness. The fable distinguishes between effort (which the Crow has) and efficacy (which the Crow lacks). Not all effort is productive; effort d[[anger|ire]]cted against nature is wasted.

## Enduring Influence

The Crow and the Swan addresses a question that runs through philosophy from Plato's *Phaedrus* (the soul's fixed nature) to modern debates about personality change. The fable's answer — nature is fixed, only habits can change — is notably conservative. But the fable's deepest insight is not the fixity of nature but the **cost of attempting to change it**. The Crow does not merely fail to become a Swan; he dies. The fable's warning is ecological as much as moral: every creature has a niche (the Crow's altar-scraps), and abandoning that niche for an impossible ambition is not merely futile — it is fatal. The Crow who wanted to be a Swan ends up neither a Swan nor a living Crow.
