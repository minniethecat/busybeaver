## Statement

"[[kindness|Kindness]] is thrown away upon the evil" is the explicit moral of The Farmer and the Viper (157-002). The moral is among the bleakest in the Aesopic corpus: it does not say kindness is *risky* or kindness *may backf[[anger|ire]]* — it says kindness is *wasted*, a null investment, producing no return and in fact producing [[ruin|destruction]]. The Viper does not hesitate; it does not weigh gratitude against instinct. It is revived by warmth and it bites. The Farmer's dying words — "I have only got what I deserved, for taking [[mercy|compassion]] on so villainous a creature" (157-001) — are a self-condemnation, not an [[blame|accusation]] against the Viper. The Farmer blames himself for the category error of treating a villain as a worthy recipient of [[mercy|compassion]].

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Farmer and the Viper

The fable is brutally compressed — a single paragraph containing the ent[[anger|ire]] arc of rescue, [[treachery|betrayal]], and deathbed recognition:

> "One winter a Farmer found a Viper frozen and numb with cold, and out of pity picked it up and placed it in his bosom. The Viper was no sooner revived by the warmth than it turned upon its benefactor and inflicted a fatal bite upon him; and as the poor man lay dying, he cried, 'I have only got what I deserved, for taking compassion on so villainous a creature.'" (157-001)

The Viper's action is not explained, justified, or moralised — it simply *happens*, as if the bite were a law of nature rather than a choice. The Farmer's [[mercy|pity]], which would be a virtue in any other recipient-context, becomes the instrument of his death.

### The Wolf and the Crane

A Wolf gets a bone stuck in his throat and h[[anger|ire]]s a Crane to extract it. The Crane succeeds and asks for the promised reward. The Wolf replies: "You have put your head inside a Wolf's mouth and taken it out again in safety; that ought to be reward enough for you." The Crane is not killed, but she is cheated — the Wolf's nature does not change because it has been helped. The Viper kills; the Wolf defrauds. Both fables converge on the same architecture: a party in need receives help, and repays that help with harm. The form of harm varies (fatal bite vs. broken promise), but the mechanism is identical: **the helper misreads the helped**.

### The Frogs Who Asked for a King

The Frogs, weary of anarchy, petition Jupiter for a king. He sends a Log — harmless, silent, contemptible. They petition again and receive a Stork, who immediately begins eating them. Unlike the Viper, the Stork does not pretend to be anything else — it is a stork, and storks eat frogs. The Frogs' error, like the Farmer's, is asking the wrong recipient: they petitioned for governance, and what they received was predation. The moral extends the Viper's logic beyond individual kindness to collective political choice.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **category-independent nature**: the Viper's villainy is not a choice it makes in response to kindness but a fixed feature of what it is. Three structural features are at work:

1. **The warmth test**: Warmth is the condition that revives the Viper. The Viper's nature is latent in the cold; warmth merely activates it. The Farmer's [[mercy|pity]] is the medium through which the Viper becomes capable of biting — the very act of kindness creates the conditions for the bite.
2. **No deliberation**: The Viper does not pause. There is no paragraph describing internal [[quarrel|conflict]], no weighing of gratitude. The bite is immediate, as mechanical as a reflex. The Viper acts according to its nature, and its nature is ent[[anger|ire]]ly unaffected by the kindness it received.
3. **Self-blame as verdict**: The dying Farmer does not curse the Viper. He curses his own judgment: "I have only got what I deserved." This is not Christian forgiveness — it is Aesopic self-indictment. The moral's target is not the Viper (villains will be villains) but the Farmer (the good must learn to recognise the evil).

## Enduring Influence

The Farmer and the Viper is one of Aesop's most proverbial fables. The image of warming a snake in one's bosom has become a metaphor across languages for nurturing a danger that will destroy you. The fable's distinctive contribution is the dying man's self-condemnation — "I have only got what I deserved" — which transforms the story from a warning about snakes into a warning about **the duty of discrimination**. [[kindness|Kindness]], in the Aesopic worldview, is not an absolute virtue; it is a resource that must be d[[anger|ire]]cted at worthy recipients. D[[anger|ire]]cted at the evil, it is not merely wasted — it is self-destructive.
