## Statement

"The want of a good excuse never kept a villain from crime" is the explicit moral of The Cat and the Cock (116-002). A Cat catches a Cock and, knowing that Cats do not customarily eat Cocks, searches for a justification. She accuses the Cock of disturbing people's sleep with his crowing. The Cock replies that he crows precisely so that men will wake in time for their day's work — his noise is a public service. The Cat replies: "Whether they can do without you or not, I'm not going without my dinner" — and kills and eats him. The excuse was never the point; the appetite was.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Cat and the Cock

A Cat pounces on a Cock and casts about for a plausible excuse for making a meal of him. Cats do not, as a rule, eat Cocks, and she knows she ought not to. She accuses the Cock of being a nocturnal nuisance. The Cock's defense is elegant — he crows for the benefit of mankind, to wake them for honest labor:

> "That may be," said the Cat, "but whether they can or not, I'm not going without my dinner." (116-001)

The Cat's answer exposes the ent[[anger|ire]] structure of villainous self-justification: the excuse is a costume, worn for a moment and discarded the instant it becomes inconvenient. The villain does not need the excuse to be good; she only needs it to exist. A bad excuse is as useful as a good one — because after the crime, no one will remember the excuse anyway.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf encounters a Lamb straying from the flock and feels "some compunction" about killing so helpless a creature without cause. He invents three [[blame|accusation]]s: the Lamb insulted him last year, feeds in his pastures, drinks from his spring. The Lamb refutes each one — he was not born last year, has never tasted grass, has only drunk his mother's milk. The Wolf replies: "Well, anyhow, I'm not going without my dinner" — and devours him (003-001). The Cat and the Wolf are identical in structure: the excuse is a formality performed for the predator's own conscience, not for the victim's benefit.

### The Cat and the Birds

A Cat, dressed as a doctor, seeks entry to an aviary where the Birds are ailing. The Birds refuse to open the door. Here the excuse never even gets a hearing — the Birds preempt the ent[[anger|ire]] theater by denying access before the performance begins (013-001). It is the same moral principle in reverse: if a villain will make do with any excuse, the wise response is not to evaluate the excuse but to refuse the encounter ent[[anger|ire]]ly.

### The Lion and the Mouse

A [[the-lion|Lion]] catches a Mouse and is about to eat him when the Mouse pleads for [[pity|mercy]], promising to repay the [[kindness|favor]] someday. [[the-lion|The Lion]] laughs at the absurdity of a Mouse helping a [[the-lion|Lion]] — but releases him anyway. [[the-lion|The Lion]]'s excuse for [[mercy|sparing]] the Mouse is as arbitrary as the Cat's excuse for eating the Cock; the outcome turns on the predator's whim, not on the quality of the argument (059-001). The fable shows the same mechanism in reverse: just as a bad excuse is sufficient for crime, an arbitrary reason is sufficient for [[pity|mercy]].

## Mechanism

The moral reveals a structural truth about [[guilt|wrongdoing]]: **the excuse is not a premise but a ritual**. The villain does not reason from excuse to action; the villain decides on action first, then manufactures an excuse to dress the decision in the appearance of reason. The Cat is looking for a justification *after* she has already caught the Cock. The Wolf invents [[blame|accusation]]s *after* he has decided to eat the Lamb.

Aesop's insight is that this temporal order — action first, excuse second — makes the quality of the excuse irrelevant. A good excuse and a bad excuse are equally useless to the victim, because the decision has already been made. The Cock's eloquent defense, like the Lamb's logical refutations, is wasted breath. The only relevant question is who has the [[strength|power]] — and the appetite.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[villain-may-disguise]] — The villain's excuse is a verbal disguise, parallel to the Cat's doctor costume. Both are external performances that say nothing about the internal reality. The wise (the Birds, the [[the-fox|Fox]] at the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s den) ignore the performance and attend to what they already know.
- [[those-who-pretend]] — The villain who makes a bad excuse is a species of pretender: he pretends to be reasoning when he is merely rationalizing. The gap between the claimed reason and the real motive is the same gap that exposes the Crow's pretension.
- [[justice]] — The Cat-and-Cock fable touches the boundary of justice as a concept in the fable world. The Cat knows she "ought not" to eat the Cock, which implies a normative order — but the fable teaches that this normative order has no en[[strength|force]]ment mechanism except [[strength|power]]. Justice without en[[strength|force]]ment is just an excuse.

## Enduring Influence

"The want of a good excuse never kept a villain from crime" appears in the Perry Index as number 16. The fable's cynical clarity about the relationship between justification and action has made it a touchstone in discussions of moral reasoning and motivated cognition. The Cat's trajectory — from "I need an excuse" through "here is a bad excuse" to "I'll do it anyway" — is a miniature map of moral disengagement that psychologists still study today. The Cock's elegant self-defense, and its complete ineffectuality, remains one of Aesop's darkest jokes.
