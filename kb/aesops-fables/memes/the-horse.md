## Role in Aesop

The Horse in [[Aesop's Fables]] is an aristocrat among animals — associated with warfare, wealth, and the pride of the mounted class. Unlike the humble [[the-ass|Ass]] or the hybrid [[mule]], the horse is born to command respect. Yet Aesop consistently undercuts this nobility: the horse is spirited but uncontrollable (123-001), glorious in youth but reduced to servitude in age (154-001), and a tool for the powerful rather than an agent in his own right.

The horse appears both as a literal animal and as an emblem of a class — the horseman, the cavalryman, the master who rides in pursuit of the [[the-thief|fugitive]] (270-001). In this sense the horse is never truly free: his strength and speed belong always to the man on his back.

## Key Appearances

### The Charger and the Miller

A war-horse grown old was put to work in a mill, grinding corn day after day. He lamented to the Miller: "I was once a splendid war-horse, gaily caparisoned, and attended by a groom whose sole duty was to see to my wants. How different is my present lot!" The Miller replied bluntly, "Ah, my friend, we must look forward, not backward" (154-001). This fable distills the horse's symbolic role — past glory cannot prevent present [[drudgery]].

> "Ah me! I was once a splendid war-horse, gaily caparisoned, and attended by a groom whose sole duty was to see to my wants. How different is my present lot!" (154-001)

### The Horse and His Rider

A young man mounted a horse that had not been properly broken in, and the horse bolted uncontrollably. "Where are you off to in such a hurry?" called a friend. "Don't ask me," the rider replied, "ask the horse!" (123-001). The fable is a parable of [[power]] without restraint — when the spirited nature of the horse is unchecked, even the master becomes a passenger.

### The Hunter and the Horseman

A [[hunter]] caught a [[the-hare|hare]] and was carrying it home when a man on horseback offered to buy it. But no sooner had the horseman taken the hare than he set spurs to his horse and galloped away, leaving the hunter duped (281-001). The horse here is the instrument of [[theft]] and [[deception]] — his speed enabling dishonesty rather than honorable pursuit.

### Hercules and the Waggoner

A waggoner's horses were stuck in deep mire, unable to move the load. The man called upon [[hercules|Hercules]] for help, but the god appeared and said: "Put your shoulder to the wheel, man, and goad on your horses, and then you may call on Hercules to assist you" (102-001). Even the finest horses need human effort behind them — a fable about the partnership, and limits, of animal strength.

## Relationship with the Ass

The Horse and the [[the-ass|Ass]] form a dyad of elevation and humiliation. The horse is what the ass is not — noble where the ass is humble, swift where the ass is plodding. But Aesop unsettles this hierarchy: the old war-horse grinding corn (154-001) is no better off than the miller's ass. And the Mule — offspring of a horse — boasts of his noble parentage only to be exhausted by labor by day's end (195-001). The horse's nobility, the fables suggest, is contingent and fragile, not intrinsic.

## Symbolism

The Horse in Aesop symbolizes **nobility in harness** — strength and speed that are never truly autonomous. Unlike the [[wolf]] (free but hungry) or the [[the-lion|lion]] (king by nature), the horse's power is always mediated by the man who bridles him. His tragedy is that of all domesticated [[greatness]]: he is remembered for what he was — a war-horse, a charger — and measured against what he has become. The Miller's counsel (154-001) is the horse's epitaph: forward, not backward.
