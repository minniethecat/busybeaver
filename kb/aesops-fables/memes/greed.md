## Definition

Greed is the immoderate appetite for possession — whether of food, wealth, [[strength|power]], or advantage — unchecked by reason or justice. In [[Aesop's Fables]], greed is consistently depicted not as ambition but as self-defeating blindness. The greedy character reaches for too much and ends with nothing, or wolfs down what is available and cannot escape the [[punishment|consequence]]s.

The [[G. K. Chesterton]] introduction to the 1912 edition locates this pattern at the very heart of the fable tradition, observing that a [[the-fox|fox]] who gets most out of a flat dish may easily get least out of a deep dish (P03-006). The fable form, he argues, imposes a rigid moral algebra in which "the [[the-lion|lion]] must always be stronger than the wolf, just as four is always double of two" (P03-004). Greed disturbs this algebra, and the fable restores it — usually at the greedy character's expense.

## Greed in the Fables

Aesop treats greed across a range of situations — hunger, opport[[harmony|unity]], inheritance, and bargain — always with the same outcome: the greedy lose what they have.

| Fable | Greedy Act | Consequence | PN |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----|
| [[the-fox-and-the-grapes|The Swollen Fox]] | [[the-fox|Fox]] devours all the food in a hollow tree | Swells up, cannot exit, must starve back to size | 066-001 |
| [[the-hare-and-the-tortoise|The Boy and the Filberts]] | Boy grabs a fistful of nuts, cannot withdraw hand | Told "be content with half" | 075-001 |
| The Goose That Laid the Golden Eggs | Couple kills the goose to get all gold at once | No gold, no daily egg — "Much wants more and loses all" | — |
| The Dog and the Shadow | Dog drops real meat to snatch a larger reflection | Loses both | — |

Each narrative follows the same arc: possession → des[[anger|ire]] for more → total loss. The moral is never "greed is wrong" stated abstractly; it is demonstrated as a mechanical law of the fable world.

## Varieties of Greed

Aesop distinguishes several faces of greed, each with its own warning:

- **Gluttonous greed** — the physical impulse to consume more than the body can hold. Exemplified by the Swollen [[the-fox|Fox]], who "greedily devoured it all" and found himself trapped (066-001).
- **Acquisitive greed** — the impulse to seize what one has not earned. The Boy with the Filberts refuses to "be content with half the amount" and so loses everything (075-001).
- **Speculative greed** — the belief that destroying present wealth will yield greater hidden wealth. The couple with the golden goose kill their daily [[fate|fortune]] hoping for a lump sum, and receive neither.

> A hungry Fox found in a hollow tree a quantity of bread and meat, which some shepherds had placed there against their return. Delighted with his find he slipped in through the narrow aperture and greedily devoured it all. But when he tried to get out again he found himself so swollen after his big meal that he could not squeeze through the hole. (066-001)

> A bystander, who saw where the trouble lay, said to him, "Come, my boy, don't be so greedy: be content with half the amount, and you'll be able to get your hand out without difficulty." (075-001)

## Consequences

The [[punishment|consequence]]s of greed in Aesop follow a rigid pattern: **loss exceeding the original des[[anger|ire]]**. The [[the-fox|fox]] who wanted to be full must now be empty. The boy who wanted all the nuts must now have none. The couple who wanted a lump sum of gold lose even the daily egg.

This pattern is not punitive but **structural** — greed, in the fable world, is a miscalculation of constraints. The narrow aperture, the jar's neck, the dog's reflection: each is an unyielding reality that appetite cannot alter.
