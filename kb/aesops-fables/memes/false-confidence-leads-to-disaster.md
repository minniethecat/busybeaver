## Statement

"False [[trust|confidence]] often leads to disaster" is the explicit moral of The Ass, the Cock, and the [[the-lion|Lion]] (160-002). An Ass and a Cock share a cattle-pen. A starving [[the-lion|Lion]] arrives, ready to devour the Ass — but the Cock crows, and the [[the-lion|Lion]], terrified of the sound, flees. The Ass, witnessing this, draws a catastrophic inference: if the [[the-lion|Lion]] runs from a Cock, surely he will be even more [[fear|fright]]ened of an Ass. The Ass charges after the [[the-lion|Lion]] in pursuit. Once they are beyond sight and hearing of the Cock, the [[the-lion|Lion]] turns and eats him (160-001). The Ass's error is a reasoning failure so elementary it has a name in logic — the **fallacy of composition**: the property of the part (the Cock terrifies the [[the-lion|Lion]]) does not transfer to the whole (the Ass terrifies the [[the-lion|Lion]]). The Ass was always prey; the Cock's crowing was just a temporary reprieve that the Ass mistook for a permanent advantage.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Ass, the Cock, and the Lion

The fable is structured as a three-act t[[anger|rage]]dy of inference:

> An Ass and a Cock were in a cattle-pen together. Presently a Lion, who had been starving for days, came along and was just about to fall upon the Ass and make a meal of him when the Cock, rising to his full height and flapping his wings vigorously, uttered a tremendous crow. Now, if there is one thing that frightens a Lion, it is the crowing of a Cock: and this one had no sooner heard the noise than he fled. The Ass was mightily elated at this, and thought that, if the Lion couldn't face a Cock, he would be still less likely to stand up to an Ass: so he ran out and pursued him. But when the two had got well out of sight and hearing of the Cock, the Lion suddenly turned upon the Ass and ate him up. (160-001)

The Ass's reasoning is superficially logical — "A [[fear|fright]]ens B, I am greater than A, therefore I [[fear|fright]]en B even more" — but it fails to recognise that the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s fear is **specific to the stimulus**, not proportional to size or strength. The Cock's crowing is an evolutionary trigger for the [[the-lion|Lion]], not a rational assessment. The Ass mistakes a phobia for a general policy and pays with his life.

### The Lion, Jupiter, and the Elephant

In a complementary fable (206-001), a [[the-lion|Lion]] complains to Jupiter that he is terrified of cock-crowing, which he considers beneath his dignity. Jupiter tells him this is his only failing and he should be content. Later, the [[the-lion|Lion]] meets an Elephant who is afraid of gnats getting into his ear. [[the-lion|The Lion]] realises: if the enormous Elephant fears a gnat, he need not be ashamed of fearing a Cock. This fable presents the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s perspective on the same weakness — and shows that even the [[the-lion|Lion]] himself understands the irrationality of his fear. The Ass's error is more damning by contrast: the [[the-lion|Lion]] *knows* his fear is irrational but cannot overcome it; the Ass *does not know* the basis of his temporary safety and cannot preserve it.

### The Eagle and the Cocks

Two Cocks fight for mastery of the dungheap (161-001). The victor crows in triumph from a high wall, only to be seized by an Eagle. The defeated Cock, who had been hiding, inherits the dungheap by de[[blame|fault]]. Both this fable and the Ass's story illustrate how victory that rests on a fragile foundation can reverse in an instant. The Ass thinks he has won; the Cock thinks he rules. Both are wrong.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **borrowed-[[strength|power]] illusion**: a weaker party misattributes to itself the [[strength|power]] that actually belongs to a proximate stronger party. The structure has three stages:

1. **Co-location**: The weaker party (Ass) is in proximity to the true source of [[strength|power]] (Cock) when the [[danger|threat]] ([[the-lion|Lion]]) appears. The visual perspective makes it look like "we [[fear|fright]]ened the [[the-lion|Lion]]" rather than "the Cock [[fear|fright]]ened the [[the-lion|Lion]]."

2. **False generalisation**: The weaker party extrapolates from the specific case — "the [[the-lion|Lion]] fled from our pen" — to a general claim — "the [[the-lion|Lion]] fears me." This erases the critical detail of *which element* in "our pen" caused the flight.

3. **Context-dependent reversal**: As soon as the context that supplied the true [[strength|power]] (the Cock's crowing range) is removed, the illusion collapses. The Ass's pursuit carries him beyond the Cock's protective radius, at which point he is simply an Ass facing a [[the-lion|Lion]].

This mechanism operates wherever individuals confuse the respect commanded by their office, their patron, or their institution with respect commanded by themselves. The civil servant who thinks his memos carry weight because people listen to him may learn, upon ret[[anger|ire]]ment, that they were listening to his position, not to him.

## Enduring Influence

The fable's insight into borrowed [[trust|confidence]] has proved remarkably durable. The psychological phenomenon is now known as the *Dunning-Kruger effect* in one d[[anger|ire]]ction (overestimating one's own competence) and *vicarious self-[[respect|esteem]]* in another (deriving self-worth from association with a superior). The Ass mistakes the Cock's [[strength|power]] for his own in the same way that a sports fan mistakes his team's victory for his own achievement. The fable also underlies the military maxim that one should not pursue a retreating enemy beyond the range of one's supply lines — the Ass charges beyond the Cock's crowing radius, which is his only logistical advantage. In organisational behaviour, the fable is a warning against *[[strength|power]] by association*: the junior employee who mistakes the CEO's attention for his own irreplaceability.
