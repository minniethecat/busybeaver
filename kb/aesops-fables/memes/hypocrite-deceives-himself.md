## Statement

"A hypocrite deceives no one but himself" is the explicit moral of The Bear and the [[the-fox|Fox]] (098-003). A Bear boasts about his generous, refined nature — citing the tradition that Bears will never touch a dead body — while a [[the-fox|Fox]] listens and smiles. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s reply is devastating in its politeness: "My friend, when you are hungry, I only wish you would confine your attention to the dead and leave the living alone." The Bear believes his own press release; the [[the-fox|Fox]] has seen the receipts.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Bear and the Fox

A Bear brags about his generous feelings and refined nature compared with other animals. He invokes the tradition that a Bear will never touch a dead body as proof of his superior character. A [[the-fox|Fox]], hearing this performance, offers a reply that punctures the ent[[anger|ire]] edifice:

> "My friend, when you are hungry, I only wish you would confine your attention to the dead and leave the living alone." (098-002)

[[the-fox|The Fox]] does not argue. He does not [[quarrel|dispute]] the tradition about dead bodies. He simply reframes the Bear's claimed virtue as a [[danger|threat]]: if you are so noble, why are we — the living — afraid of you? The Bear's hypocrisy is not exposed through logical refutation but through the gap between his self-description and everyone else's experience.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

A Wolf, feeling some compunction about killing a Lamb without cause, invents a series of justifications: the Lamb insulted him last year, feeds in his pastures, drinks from his spring. Each [[blame|accusation]] is disproven by the Lamb, but the Wolf concludes: "I'm not going without my dinner" — and devours the Lamb anyway (003-001). The Wolf is a different moral type from the Bear: he knows he is [[deception|lying]]. But the structure is the same: the self-justification is for the predator's own comfort, not for anyone else's belief.

### The Peacock and Juno

The Peacock complains to Juno that while the Nightingale has a beautiful voice, he himself becomes a laughing-stock whenever he utters a sound. Juno replies that [[fate|Fate]] has al[[fate|lot]]ted different gifts to all — beauty to the Peacock, strength to the Eagle, song to the Nightingale — and that the Peacock alone is dissatisfied. "If your present wish were granted, you would quickly find cause for fresh discontent" (098-001). The Peacock is not a predator like the Bear, but his hypocrisy is the same structure: he cannot see that his complaint is unwarranted, that others would be grateful for what he already has.

### The Fir-tree and the Bramble

A Fir-tree boasts to a Bramble about its superior usefulness in building houses. The Bramble replies that when the axes come for the Fir-tree, it will regret its importance. The Fir-tree's pride in its utility is genuine self-[[disguise|deception]] — it cannot see that the very quality it boasts about will be its [[ruin|destruction]]. The hypocrite, like the Fir-tree, mistakes vulnerability for virtue.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **asymmetric perception**: the hypocrite and his audience occupy different information positions. The Bear sees himself through the lens of his own noble self-conception; the [[the-fox|Fox]] sees the Bear through the lens of d[[anger|ire]]ct experience (or well-founded fear). The hypocrite's self-[[disguise|deception]] is possible because he controls the narrative he tells himself — but he does not control the narratives that others tell about him.

Aesop adds a distinctive twist: the hypocrite's audience is not deceived, and they know that the hypocrite is not deceiving them. But they also know that the hypocrite *is* deceiving himself. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s smile is the universal response to watching someone perform a role they genuinely believe, while everyone else sees the gap between the performance and the reality.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[those-who-pretend]] — The pretender knows he is performing (the Crow knows she is not a Raven). The hypocrite *believes* his own performance (the Bear genuinely thinks he is refined). The pretender fails when his true nature leaks out; the hypocrite fails because his true nature was never hidden in the first place.
- [[villain-may-disguise]] — The villain in disguise is a conscious deceiver trying to fool others. The hypocrite is an unconscious deceiver who has already fooled himself. The Cat knows he is a Cat in doctor's robes; the Bear has forgotten he is a Bear. Both fail, but by different routes: the villain is seen through, the hypocrite is seen around.
- [[pride-comes-before-a-fall]] — Hypocrisy is pride's cognitive manifestation. The Bear's boast and the Peacock's complaint both arise from an inflated self-conception that reality cannot sustain. The fall, when it comes, is the gap between self-image and what others actually see.

## Enduring Influence

"A hypocrite deceives no one but himself" has a long afterlife in moral philosophy and psychology. The concept maps onto the modern psychological notion of "self-[[disguise|deception]]" and "cognitive dissonance" — the hypocrite maintains an inconsistent belief system by hiding one part of it from himself. The fable is Perry Index number 485. [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s dry, polite demolition of the Bear's self-image is one of Aesop's most sophisticated narrative moments — a single sentence that does more work than any sermon could.
