## Statement

"You cannot believe a liar even when he tells the truth" is the explicit moral of [[The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf]] (046-002), one of the most widely known of all Aesopic fables. A Shepherd's Boy, tending his flock near a village, thinks it amusing to trick the villagers by crying "Wolf! wolf!" and laughing when they come running. He repeats the hoax several times. When a wolf actually appears and the Boy cries for help, the villagers ignore him, and the wolf destroys the flock at leisure (046-001).

The moral is brutal in its simplicity: **[[trust|credibility]] is a non-renewable resource**. Each false [[fear|alarm]] withdraws from a fixed account; when the balance reaches zero, no genuine emergency can restore it. The Boy does not merely waste the villagers' time — he destroys the signaling system itself.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf

The Boy cries "Wolf!" as a prank. The villagers respond. He repeats the prank. They respond again but with diminishing enthusiasm. On the third iteration, when the wolf is real, they do not come. The wolf kills the sheep:

> "the people were so used to hearing him call that they took no notice of his cries for help. And so the Wolf had it all his own way, and killed off sheep after sheep at his leisure." (046-001)

The fable models the t[[anger|rage]]dy as a sequence: (1) the Boy discovers that a false [[fear|alarm]] produces an amusing response; (2) he exploits the response repeatedly; (3) the villagers learn that the signal is unreliable; (4) when the signal becomes genuine, the learning cannot be undone fast enough.

### The Fox and the Monkey

The Monkey's boast about his ancestors in the cemetery is a different type of lie — one that cannot be detected (031-001). But the connection to the Shepherd's Boy is instructive: the Monkey's lie survives because it is unverifiable, while the Boy's lies collapse because they are highly verifiable. The two morals together describe the full spectrum of deceptive speech: lies about verifiable events destroy the liar's future [[trust|credibility]]; lies about unverifiable claims preserve it indefinitely (see [[boasters-brag-when-undetected]]).

### The Thief and His Mother

A Boy steals a book from a schoolmate and shows it to his Mother, who does not punish him. Encou[[anger|rage]]d, he progresses to stealing c[[fate|lot]]hes, then money, and finally, as a young man, is caught in a major theft and condemned to death. On the way to execution he bites his mother's ear, blaming her for not correcting him at the first small theft (199-001). The fable is structurally parallel to the Shepherd's Boy: a sequence of small untruths or transgressions, each tolerated, builds toward an irreversible catastrophe that could have been prevented only at the first step.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **signal degradation through false positives**. The villagers' response to "Wolf!" is a rational Bayesian update: each time the Boy cries wolf and no wolf appears, the conditional probability that a future cry indicates a real wolf decreases. After enough false [[fear|alarm]]s, the expected cost of responding (dropping work, running to the hills) exceeds the expected benefit (saving sheep multiplied by the now-tiny probability that the cry is genuine), and non-response becomes rational.

The Boy's error is not that he lies. It is that he lies about a **verifiable, high-stakes event** using a **single, non-resettable signal channel**. Had he varied his pranks — sometimes crying "Bear!" or "F[[anger|ire]]!" — the villagers [[power|might]] not have learned to ignore him specifically. But the fable's signal is always "Wolf!", which means the learning is concentrated on that one word.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[honesty]] — The Boy's lie is not about concealing a fact (as in dishonesty to avoid punishment) but about fabricating an event for entertainment. The motive distinguishes this moral from general honesty morals: the damage is not to the liar's soul but to the **information channel** between the liar and his audience.
- [[trust]] — [[trust|Trust]] is the substrate that the Boy destroys. Once destroyed, no appeal to truth can rebuild it. The fable is a case study in the irreversibility of trust erosion.
- [[those-who-pretend]] — The Boy pretends there is a wolf; the villagers treat the pretense as reality. When the pretense becomes reality, the distinction has been erased by the Boy's own hand.

## Enduring Influence

"The Boy Who Cried Wolf" is among the most widely retold of all Aesop's fables. It appears in children's literature across languages, has been adapted into animated shorts and picture books, and is routinely invoked in journalism and politics to describe false-[[fear|alarm]] scenarios — from [[safety|security]] [[danger|threat]]s to financial panics. The expression "crying wolf" has entered English as an idiom meaning "raising a false [[fear|alarm]]." The fable appears in the Perry Index as number 210.
