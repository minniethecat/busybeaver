## Role in Aesop

The Cat in [[Aesop's Fables]] is the predator who lives indoors — a miniature [[wolf]] with the privileges of domesticity. Unlike the [[dog]], whose loyalty can be counted on, the Cat is always working an angle. She hunts the [[the-mouse|mice]] who hold council against her (006-001), disguises herself as a [[the-doctor|doctor]] to get at the birds (013-001), and manipulates her neighbors — the [[the-eagle|Eagle]] and the Sow — into mutual [[destruction]] for her own benefit (135-001).

Most telling is the fable of Venus and the Cat (147-001): a cat in love with a young man begs the goddess [[venus|Venus]] to change her into a woman. The transformation is perfect — until a mouse runs across the floor and the "bride" leaps after it on all fours. Nature, Aesop concludes, cannot be changed by a change of shape.

## Key Appearances

### Belling the Cat

The Mice held a council to discuss how to protect themselves against the Cat. A mouse of experience proposed fastening a bell around the Cat's neck to warn of her approach. All applauded the plan — until an old mouse asked: "Who is to bell the Cat?" (006-001). No one volunteered. The fable captures the Cat's role as the ever-present threat that inspires grand [[plans]] and no [[action]].

> "I think I have hit upon a plan which will ensure our safety... It is that we should fasten a bell round the neck of our enemy the cat." — "That is very well said, but... who is to bell the Cat?" (006-001)

### Venus and the Cat

A Cat fell in love with a handsome young man and begged [[venus|Venus]] to change her into a woman. Venus obliged, and the young man married the beautiful maiden at first sight. But the goddess, curious, let a mouse run loose in the room. The bride immediately sprang after it on all fours. Disgusted, Venus changed her back into a cat (147-001). The fable is one of Aesop's sharpest statements on the immutability of [[nature]]: "Nature will out."

### The Eagle, the Cat, and the Wild Sow

An Eagle nesting at the top of a tree, a Cat in the hollow trunk, and a Wild Sow at the foot might have lived as neighbors — but the Cat had other plans. She told the Eagle the Sow meant to uproot the tree, and told the Sow the Eagle was waiting to eat her young. Both stayed in their places, paralyzed by [[fear]], while the Cat and her kittens lived comfortably in the hollow — the Eagle and Sow eventually starving (135-001). The Cat here is the architect of mutual [[suspicion]], profiting from lies told in both directions.

### The Cat and the Cock

A Cat pounced on a [[Cock]] and cast about for a good excuse to eat him. She accused him of crowing at night and disturbing people. The Cock defended himself: he crowed so men might wake for the day's work. "That may be," said the Cat, "but I can't go without my dinner" — and ate him anyway (116-001). The fable exposes the Cat's hypocrisy: the reasons are invented, the appetite is real.

## Relationship with the Fox

The Cat and the [[the-fox|Fox]] are Aesop's two great [[cunning|cunning ones]] — but their cunning serves different ends. The Fox is the satirist's surrogate: he exposes fraud, speaks truth to [[power]], and often escapes by wit. The Cat is the satirist's target: she embodies fraud, manufactures lies, and escapes by making others [[ruin|ruin]] each other. Where the Fox unmasks the doctor (065-002), the Cat becomes one — the doctor's gown is just another hunting blind (013-001). Together they mark the boundary between intelligence used for [[truth]] and intelligence used for [[deception]].

## Symbolism

The Cat in Aesop symbolizes **nature wearing a mask of domestication** — and the mask never fits. She is the predator at the hearth, the bride who cannot stop hunting, the neighbor who destroys both sides of a triangle while appearing innocent. The lesson of [[venus|Venus]] and the Cat (147-001) is the lesson of the Cat in every fable: the form may change, but the essence endures. You cannot bell the Cat, you cannot reform the Cat, and — as Venus learned — you cannot even make her human.
