## Statement

"Injuries are never forgotten in the presence of those who caused them" is the explicit moral of The Labourer and the Snake (188-002). A Labourer's son is bitten by a Snake and dies. The father, beside himself with grief, takes an axe to the Snake's hole. When the Snake emerges, the Labourer strikes but only severs the tip of its tail. The Snake retreats. Some time later, the Labourer tries to make peace, but the Snake refuses:

> "I can never be your friend because of my lost tail, nor you mine because of your lost child." (188-002)

The moral is not about forgiveness — it is about the **physiology of grievance**. An injury, once inflicted, cannot be unseen or unfelt. Both parties carry the evidence of the wrong in their own bodies: the Snake carries a shortened tail, the Labourer carries the absence of his son. Even if both wish to reconcile, the physical fact of the injury makes it impossible to return to the prior state. The presence of the injurer is a standing reminder of the loss, and the reminder is itself a form of repeated injury.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Labourer and the Snake

The Labourer's attack is an act of grief, not justice. He does not sue the Snake or demand compensation; he takes an axe and waits at the hole. This is [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]], not law — and [[revenge-is-a-two-edged-sword|vengeance]] leaves a wound that law, at least in principle, could close:

> "The father was beside himself with grief, and in his anger against the Snake he caught up an axe and went and stood close to the Snake's hole, and watched for a chance of killing it. Presently the Snake came out, and the man aimed a blow at it, but only succeeded in cutting off the tip of its tail before it wriggled in again." (188-001)

The Snake's reply reveals the structural problem: both parties have lost something irreplaceable. The Snake lost its tail-tip, which will not grow back. The Labourer lost his son, who will not return. Neither can restore what the other lost, so neither can offer the material precondition of reconciliation.

### The Farmer and the Viper

[[betray-friend-ruin-yourself]] describes the same dynamic from the victim's side. The Farmer rescues a frozen Viper and is bitten for his [[misfortune|trouble]]. The Viper, dying, asks: "Why did you pick me up, knowing what I am?" The Snake in the Labourer fable does not ask such a question — it simply states the fact: we are now enemies, and nothing can change that. Both fables converge on the insight that **harm creates a one-way door**: you can enter the state of enmity instantly, but you cannot exit it.

### The Man and the Serpent

In a related fable (PN=069), a Traveller who is about to be bitten by a Serpent is saved by a companion. The Serpent, however, never forgets and never forgives. The lesson is consistent across the Aesopic corpus: the memory of injury is the most durable of all memories, more durable than gratitude, more durable than fear.

## Mechanism

The mechanism of this moral is **irreversible state transition under mutual harm**. A relationship before injury exists in a state of *optional future* — the parties may cooperate or compete, trust or distrust, depending on circumstances. After injury, the relationship enters a new state — one where the injury is *visible* in the presence of the injurer:

1. **Bilateral scarring**: Both the Snake and the Labourer carry visible marks of the [[quarrel|conflict]]. Even if both wish to forget, the scar makes forgetting impossible.
2. **Presence as trigger**: The moral specifies "in the presence of those who caused them." The injury is not forgotten *when the injurer is present*. The injurer's face, voice, and body are cues that reactivate the memory of the wrong.
3. **No restorative transaction**: For reconciliation to occur, at least one party must be able to restore what was taken. Here, neither can — a lost tail and a lost child are equally irrecoverable.

The mechanism explains why "sorry" is often insufficient: an apology does not restore what was lost, and the presence of the apologizer remains a trigger for the memory of loss.

## Enduring Influence

The moral underpins the modern concept of **trauma triggers** — the clinical observation that the presence of a person, place, or object associated with a past injury can reactivate the emotional state of the original wound. It also explains the deep reluctance of parties in long-running [[quarrel|conflict]]s (ethnic, familial, political) to accept gestures of reconciliation: the gesture cannot undo the physical facts of the injury, and the injurer's continued presence is itself a provocation. The Snake's reply is the most honest statement in the Aesopic corpus: reconciliation is not always possible, and pretending otherwise is a second injury.
