## Statement

"Might makes right" is the closing moral of [[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Wild Ass (107-002). It is among the bluntest statements in the Aesopic corpus — not a proverb [[advice|counsel]]ing virtue but an observation about how [[strength|power]] actually operates when unchecked. [[the-lion|The Lion]] does not pretend to be just; he simply takes what he wants and dares the weaker party to object.

Aesop returns to this theme across multiple fables, always with the same structure: a strong party and a weak party, a nominal agreement, and a foreordained outcome in which justice is whatever the strong party says it is.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Lion and the Wild Ass

> A Lion and a Wild Ass went out hunting together: the latter was to run down the prey by his superior speed, and the former would then come up and despatch it. They met with great success; and when it came to sharing the spoil the Lion divided it all into three equal portions. (107-001)

> "I will take the first," said he, "because I am King of the beasts; I will also take the second, because, as your partner, I am entitled to half of what remains; and as for the third — well, unless you give it up to me and take yourself off pretty quick, the third, believe me, will make you feel very sorry for yourself!" (107-001)

[[the-lion|The Lion]]'s logic is a parody of legal reasoning. Each claim invokes a different principle — kingship, partnership, [[danger|threat]] — as if they were cumulative justifications. But the under[[deception|lying]] mechanism is pure [[danger|threat]]: "make you feel very sorry for yourself." The Wild Ass contributed speed; the [[the-lion|Lion]] contributed killing [[strength|power]]. But the division is not proportional to contribution. It is proportional to capacity for violence.

### The Wolf and the Lamb

> A Wolf came upon a Lamb straying from the flock, and felt some compunction about taking the life of so helpless a creature without some plausible excuse; so he cast about for a grievance. (011-001)

The Wolf manufactures three pretexts — an insult last year (impossible, the Lamb was not yet born), grazing in his pastures (impossible, the Lamb has never eaten grass), drinking from his spring (impossible, the Lamb drinks only milk). Each [[blame|accusation]] is refuted. The Wolf's reply is the moral in miniature: "Well, anyhow, I'm not going without my dinner" (011-001).

The Wolf's "compunction" is revealing. He wants the *appearance* of justice even when he has no intention of being bound by it. Might does not simply override right; it goes through the motions of respecting it before overriding it.

### The Wolf and the Lion

A Wolf steals a lamb and is carrying it off when a [[the-lion|Lion]] takes it from him. The Wolf protests: "It is most unjust of you to take what's mine away from me like that." [[the-lion|The Lion]] laughs: "It was justly yours, no doubt! The gift of a friend, perhaps, eh?" (120-001).

[[the-lion|The Lion]]'s [[ridicule|mockery]] exposes the absurdity of the Wolf's appeal to justice. The Wolf acqu[[anger|ire]]d the lamb through theft. Now the Wolf invokes property rights. [[the-lion|The Lion]], standing one rung higher on the food chain, sees the joke immediately: [[power|might]] makes right all the way down.

## Mechanism

The mechanism operates as a **nested hierarchy of predation**, with each level justifying itself by a different rhetorical strategy:

| Level | Actor | Method | Rhetoric |
|-------|-------|--------|----------|
| Top predator | [[the-lion|Lion]] | D[[anger|ire]]ct seizure | "I am King" — no pretense |
| Mid predator | Wolf (to Lamb) | Fabricated charges | "You insulted me" — sham trial |
| Mid predator | Wolf (to [[the-lion|Lion]]) | Appeal to rights | "It's unjust" — hypocrisy |
| Prey | Lamb, Wild Ass | None available | Rational refutation — ignored |

The structure is fractal. The Wolf uses [[power|might]] against the Lamb and complains about [[power|might]] when facing the [[the-lion|Lion]]. [[the-lion|The Lion]] is at least honest about it. Aesop's most chilling observation is that the mid-level predator is the most dangerous — strong enough to kill, weak enough to need a cover story.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[battle-not-always-to-strong]] — The necessary counterweight. The battle is *not always* to the strong, which means it usually is. The exceptions (the Mouse freeing the [[the-lion|Lion]], the Beetle avenging itself on the Eagle) are memorable precisely because they violate the de[[blame|fault]].
- [[weak-may-avenge-upon-strong]] — The mechanism by which the hierarchy occasionally inverts. Weakness is not helplessness; it is a different kind of resource.
- [[union-is-strength]] — The collective solution to individual weakness. A single Wild Ass cannot resist the [[the-lion|Lion]]; a herd of Wild Asses [[power|might]].
- [[persuasion-is-better-than-force]] — The counter-ideal. Aesop does not endorse [[power|might]]-makes-right; he documents it and offers alternatives where they exist.

## Enduring Influence

"Might makes right" has entered English as a proverb in its own right, though its Aesopic origin is less widely known than "Look before you leap." The phrase appears in political philosophy from Thrasymachus in Plato's *Republic* ("justice is the advantage of the stronger") to modern realist international relations theory. The Wolf and the Lamb, in particular, has become the archetypal fable of [[strength|power]] masquerading as justice — cited in legal opinions, political cartoons, and protest literature for over two millennia.

The fable's endurance owes to its uncomfortable accuracy. Every reader recognizes the Wolf and the Lamb because every society produces both. The question Aesop leaves unanswered is whether this hierarchy is a law of nature or a failure of collective will.
