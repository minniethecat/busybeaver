## Statement

"The covetous are poor givers" is the closing moral of The Viper and the File (115-002). The fable is short — a single paragraph — but its structure is precisely engineered to make the moral land. A Viper, hungry, goes from tool to tool in a carpenter's shop begging for food. The File's reply is the key: "What a simpleton you must be if you imagine you will get anything from me, who invariably take from every one and never give anything in return" (115-001).

The File's self-description is also a description of the Viper. The Viper, a predator that takes without giving, goes begging to a tool that also takes without giving. The File recognizes the absurdity immediately. The Viper does not. This asymmetry of self-awareness is the fable's engine: the covetous person does not see that his own nature, applied to others, would leave him with nothing.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Viper and the File

> A Viper entered a carpenter's shop, and went from one to another of the tools, begging for something to eat. Among the rest, he addressed himself to the File, and asked for the favour of a meal. The File replied in a tone of pitying contempt, "What a simpleton you must be if you imagine you will get anything from me, who invariably take from every one and never give anything in return." (115-001)

The File's "tone of [[mercy|pity]]ing contempt" is the emotional core. The File does not hate the Viper; it pities it. The Viper is a simpleton — not wicked but stupid, unable to see that the world mirrors its own nature back at it.

### The Goose That Laid the Golden Eggs

> A Man and his Wife had the good fortune to possess a Goose which laid a Golden Egg every day. Lucky though they were, they soon began to think they were not getting rich fast enough, and, imagining the bird must be made of gold inside, they decided to kill it in order to secure the whole store of precious metal at once. (002-001)

The couple are covetous in the purest sense: they have a daily gift and it is not enough. Their [[envy|covetousness]] makes them poor givers — to themselves. By trying to take everything at once, they destroy the source of all future giving. The Goose was a giver (one golden egg per day); the couple were takers (they wanted the whole goose). The File's rule applies: from takers, expect nothing.

### The Swollen Fox

A [[the-fox|Fox]] finds bread and meat left by shepherds in a hollow tree, eats until his belly swells, and cannot escape. He is the Viper's cousin — a taker who has taken too much. The hollow tree, like the File, takes and does not give back: what it gave (easy access) it now withholds (exit). [[the-fox|The Fox]]'s [[envy|covetousness]] has made him a poor giver of patience, of restraint, of [[wisdom|foresight]].

## Mechanism

Covetousness-as-poor-giving operates through a **mirror-world logic**:

| Step | The Covetous Person's View | Reality |
|------|--------------------------|---------|
| 1. Need | "I am hungry; others should feed me" | Others are also takers, not givers |
| 2. Approach | "This tool / person will help" | Takers recognize takers and refuse |
| 3. Outcome | Indignation: "How stingy!" | The File's [[mercy|pity]]ing contempt |
| 4. Blind spot | Never sees own nature in others | The world is a mirror; the Viper never looks |

The Viper's error is not moral but cognitive. He applies one standard to himself ("I need, therefore I should receive") and another to the world ("others should give to me"). He does not see that if everyone followed his own principle — take, don't give — he would starve. The File, a literal piece of metal, has more self-awareness: "I invariably take from every one and never give anything in return" (115-001).

The fable's deepest insight is that [[envy|covetousness]] is not just a character flaw but an **epistemic failure**: the covetous person cannot model the minds of others because he assumes others operate differently from himself. The Viper cannot imagine the File is also a taker because the Viper does not think of *himself* as a taker.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[much-wants-more-and-loses-all]] — Covetousness in action. The Goose-owners want more than the daily golden egg; they end with nothing. Covetousness is the engine; losing all is the destination.
- [[no-virtue-in-giving-useless]] — The mirror image. If [[no-virtue-in-giving-useless|useless gifts]] confer no virtue, then covetous non-gifts confer no shame. The File refusing the Viper is not stingy; it is honest about its nature.
- [[kindness-wasted-on-the-evil]] — The Viper is the recipient in this moral. [[kindness|Kindness]] to the evil is wasted because the evil, like the File, take and do not give. The Viper would receive kindness and return venom.

## Enduring Influence

"The covetous are poor givers" has not become a standalone English proverb, but its sentiment survives in phrases like "you can't get blood from a stone" and "what goes around comes around." The File's reply has a particular resonance in modern discourse about wealth and philanthropy: those who accumulate by taking from others are rarely those who give back.

The fable's deeper [[persuasion|influence]] is psychological. The Viper who cannot see the File as a mirror is the ancestor of every literary character whose selfishness blinds them to the selfishness of others. The fable says in one paragraph what novels take three hundred pages to unfold: that the covetous person's real poverty is not material but imaginative.
