## Statement

"Give assistance, not advice, in a crisis" is the explicit moral of The Boy Bathing (064-002). Among Aesop's morals, this is one of the most practically applicable — it describes a common social failure ([[sacrifice|offering]] commentary when action is needed) and prescribes a simple corrective (help first, lecture later). The Boy is drowning. The Man who passes by does not deny the danger, does not refuse to engage — he *engages wrongly*. He speaks when he should act. The Boy's reply — "please help me first and scold me afterwards" (064-001) — does not [[quarrel|dispute]] the content of the scolding. He concedes that he may have been careless. His objection is to the timing: in a crisis, the correct response is assistance, not analysis.

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Boy Bathing

A Boy, out of his depth, cries for help. A passer-by hears him, approaches — and begins to lecture:

> "A Boy was bathing in a river and got out of his depth, and was in great danger of being drowned. A man who was passing along a road heard his cries for help, and went to the riverside and began to scold him for being so careless as to get into deep water, but made no attempt to help him." (064-001)

The Boy's reply is the fable's ent[[anger|ire]] moral payload:

> "'Oh, sir,' cried the Boy, 'please help me first and scold me afterwards.'" (064-001)

The fable ends there — we are not told whether the Man helps or the Boy drowns. The open ending is deliberate: the moral is not about the outcome but about the **order of operations**. The Man's scolding, even if correct in content, is a category error. A crisis calls for rescue, not review.

### The Fox and the Bramble

A [[the-fox|Fox]], pursued by hounds, seeks shelter behind a Bramble. The Bramble consents but warns: "I help everyone in my own way — if you grasp me, I shall tear your flesh." [[the-fox|The Fox]], once the hounds pass, tries to detach himself and is scratched. He complains that he came for help and received injury. The Bramble replies: "You made a mistake, my friend, in laying hold of me, who lay hold of everyone myself." This fable inverts the Boy Bathing: the Bramble *does* provide assistance (shelter), but the assistance comes with a cost. [[the-fox|The Fox]], like the drowning Boy, needs rescue — but unlike the Man who offers only scolding, the Bramble offers genuine help with transparent terms.

### The Lark and Her Young Ones

A mother Lark, hearing that the farmer will ask friends and relatives to help reap the field, tells her chicks not to worry — help asked of others is slow to arrive. But when the farmer says he will reap the field himself, the Lark says: "Now it is time to be off." The Lark understands what the Man in The Boy Bathing does not: action taken by the person present is the only action that counts. [[advice|Advice]] from afar, promises of help, and scolding from the riverbank are all the same thing — words that do not move water.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **crisis category collapse**: a crisis is defined by urgency — the need for an *immediate* intervention to prevent an *imminent* harm. The Man's scolding, however correct, does not address urgency. Three structural features:

1. **The timing error**: The Man's scolding may be valid — perhaps the Boy was careless to swim in deep water. But validity is not the relevant criterion in a crisis. The relevant criterion is **effectiveness**: does the action reduce the harm? Scolding, delivered from the riverbank, does not.
2. **The Boy's rhetorical competence**: The Boy does not argue with the scolding's content. He accepts the premise ("perhaps I was careless") and reframes the issue as one of sequence: "help me first and scold me afterwards." This is not a child's plea but a logical argument about the structure of crisis response.
3. **The neutrality of inaction**: The Man is not malicious. He does not push the Boy under. But his inaction — standing on the riverbank delivering a lecture — is functionally equivalent to abandonment. The fable's lesson is that *doing the wrong thing* ([[sacrifice|offering]] advice instead of assistance) is not morally neutral, even if the advice is correct.

## Enduring Influence

The Boy's plea — "help me first and scold me afterwards" — is one of Aesop's most transferable lines. It encodes a principle of crisis management: triage by urgency, not by causal attribution. The person drowning does not need a root-cause analysis; they need a rope. The fable's wisdom is routinely rediscovered in disaster-response protocols, medical triage, and the management principle that post-mortems belong *after* the incident, not during it. The Man on the riverbank is not a villain — he is a well-meaning person who has confused the time for reflection with the time for action.
