## Statement

"Deeds, not words" is the closing moral of The [[boasting|Boasting]] Traveler (049-002). It is the Aesopic formulation of what would become, millennia later, the pragmatist's motto: a claim without a demonstration is indistinguishable from a lie.

A Man returns from his travels and regales his audience with tales of his exploits abroad. In Rhodes, he claims, he performed a jump so extraordinary that no one else could match it. "Just go to Rhodes and ask them," he says; "everyone will tell you it's true." A listener replies: "If you can jump as well as all that, we needn't go to Rhodes to prove it.

Let's just imagine this is Rhodes for a moment: and now — jump!" (049-001).

The Traveler cannot jump. His words were the jump; and words, unlike jumps, do not requ[[anger|ire]] witnesses.

**The Traveler's error is not [[deception|lying]] — it is assuming that words can substitute for deeds in a world where deeds leave evidence and words leave only air.**

## Fables That Illustrate This Moral

### The Boasting Traveler

> A Man once went abroad on his travels, and when he came home he had wonderful tales to tell of the things he had done in foreign countries. Among other things, he said he had taken part in a jumping-match at Rhodes, and had done a wonderful jump which no one could beat. "Just go to Rhodes and ask them," he said; "every one will tell you it's true." But one of those who were listening said, "If you can jump as well as all that, we needn't go to Rhodes to prove it. Let's just imagine this is Rhodes for a moment: and now — jump!" (049-001)

> Deeds, not words. (049-002)

### The Crab and His Mother

An Old Crab tells her son to walk straight instead of sideways. The Young Crab replies, "Show me how, dear mother, and I'll follow your example." The Old Crab tries — and fails (050-001). She cannot walk straight either. Her words ("walk straight") are contradicted by her body, which can only walk sideways.

> Example is better than precept. (050-003)

This moral is the positive counterpart to "Deeds, not words." Where the Traveler uses words to *claim* deeds he never did, the Old Crab uses words to *demand* deeds she cannot perform. Both fail the same test: the body cannot lie.

### The Quack Frog

A Frog proclaims himself a physician, skilled in all drugs and able to cure all diseases. A [[the-fox|Fox]] calls out: "You a doctor! Why, how can you set up to heal others when you cannot even cure your own lame legs and b[[fate|lot]]ched and wrinkled skin?" (065-002). The Frog's medical credentials are all words; his legs are deeds.

### The Lion and the Mouse

The positive exemplar of "Deeds, not words" is the Mouse in [[the-lion-and-the-mouse|The Lion and the Mouse]]. The Mouse does not *promise* to repay the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s [[pity|mercy]]; she simply says she will. When the [[the-lion|Lion]] is trapped in the hunter's net, the Mouse does not give a speech — she gnaws through the ropes (P03-008). Her deeds make her words retroactively true.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is **verifiability asymmetry**. A claim about the past made in a distant location ("Rhodes") is cheap because verification is costly. The listener's counter-move — "Here is Rhodes; jump!" — eliminates the asymmetry by collapsing the verification distance to zero.

| Component | Traveler's Strategy | Listener's Counter |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Venue | Rhodes (distant, unverifiable) | "Here is Rhodes" (present, verifiable) |
| Witnesses | "Everyone in Rhodes" (absent) | "We are here now" (present) |
| Action | Claimed jump (past, invisible) | "Jump!" (present, visible) |
| Cost of [[deception|lying]] | Zero (words are free) | Infinite (body cannot lie) |

The Traveler exploits a gap between *claim-cost* and *verification-cost*. In modern terms, he is running a cheap-talk strategy. The listener closes the gap by making the claim immediately testable.

The Crab-and-Mother variant (050) adds a second dimension: the speaker may be sincere but self-deceived. The Old Crab genuinely believes she can walk straight; she is not [[deception|lying]], just wrong. But the Young Crab's test — "Show me how" — treats [[honesty|sincerity]] and [[disguise|deception]] identically. If you cannot demonstrate it, your belief in your own ability is irrelevant.

## Relation to Other Morals

- [[example-is-better-than-precept]] — The positive counterpart. "Deeds, not words" is the negative injunction; "Example is better than precept" is the positive guide. Both locate [[power|authority]] in demonstration rather than assertion.
- [[boasting]] — [[boasting|Boasting]] is the preceding vice; "Deeds, not words" is the cure. The Traveler boasts; the listener demands a deed.

The Boaster who cannot deliver is exposed as a Traveler to Rhodes.
- [[honesty-is-the-best-policy]] — Words that match deeds are honest words. The Traveler's words mismatch his deeds (or his absence of deeds); the moral diagnoses the mismatch.
- [[no-use-hiding-what-cant-be-hidden]] — The Traveler tries to hide his lack of jumping ability behind the distance to Rhodes.

The listener brings Rhodes to him, revealing what cannot be hidden: the Traveler cannot jump.

## Enduring Influence

"Deeds, not words" and "Actions speak louder than words" are now independent English proverbs, though the latter formulation postdates Aesop by centuries. The fable is Perry Index 33.

Its structure — the boast challenged by the demand for immediate proof — is one of the most durable comic patterns in Western literature, appearing in everything from Plautus to Shakespeare's *Henry V* (where Henry's claim to [[courage|valor]] at Agincourt is validated by deeds, not words) to modern job interviews.

The phrase "Hic Rhodus, hic salta" (Here is Rhodes; jump here) became a Latin proverb in its own right, used by Hegel in the preface to the *Philosophy of Right* to mean: do not appeal to distant ideals — demonstrate your principles in the world as it actually exists.
