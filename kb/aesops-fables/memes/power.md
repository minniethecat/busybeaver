## Definition

Power in [[Aesop's Fables]] is not a single attribute but a **relational condition** — the capacity of one being to affect another. The corpus distinguishes natural [[strength|power]] (given by nature or the gods), usurped [[strength|power]] (seized beyond one's station), and lost [[strength|power]] (forfeited through age, [[foolishness|folly]], or [[disguise|deception]]). Aesop's treatment of [[strength|power]] is fundamentally conservative: the natural order assigns each creature its proper scope, and any attempt to exceed it invites ruin.

The [[G. K. Chesterton]] introduction codifies this as a rigid hierarchy: "The [[the-lion|lion]] must always be stronger than the wolf, just as four is always double of two" (P03-004). But the fables themselves complicate this picture — [[strength|power]] can be undermined by [[flattery]], overestimated through pride, or nullified by circumstance.

## Varieties

**Natural Power.** The [[strength|power]] each creature receives by nature — the Eagle's strength to carry a lamb (170-001), the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s royal authority (267-001), the Bull's horns (241-001). This form of [[strength|power]] is distributed by [[fate|Fate]] and is, in principle, **inalienable** — as Juno tells the Peacock: your gifts are your gifts, and discontent with them is the only true [[strength|power]]lessness (097-001). Yet natural [[strength|power]] can be lost: the Old Hound, who "began to lose his strength and speed owing to age," speaks for all creatures whose [[strength|power]] is borrowed from youth (126-001).

**Usurped Power.** When a creature reaches beyond its natural station to claim [[strength|power]] it does not possess. The Jackdaw who tries to carry off a ram like an Eagle is the paradigm: "It's a Jackdaw, and nothing but a Jackdaw: but it wants to be taken for an Eagle" (170-001). The Shepherd clips its wings — a symbolic amputation of even the limited [[strength|power]] the Jackdaw did have. The fable world punishes usurpation not with proportionate correction but with **[[shame|humiliation]] and permanent diminishment**.

**Power Undermined.** Even legitimate [[strength|power]] is fragile. The Bull in "The Wily [[the-lion|Lion]]" possesses horns that make him match the [[the-lion|Lion]] — but he is "foolish enough to be persuaded by this flattery to have his horns cut off" (241-001), surrendering his [[strength|power]] voluntarily. [[the-lion|The Lion]], for all his [[strength|power]], fails to catch the Stag on his first attempt, misjudging his spring (267-001). Power in Aesop is never absolute; it is contingent on circumstance, awareness, and the absence of deceivers.

## Consequences

The fables draw two conclusions about [[strength|power]] that stand in productive tension. First, [[strength|power]] must be **accepted within its bounds** — the Peacock should not wish for the nightingale's voice, the Jackdaw should not try to be an Eagle. To resent one's al[[fate|lot]]ted portion is [[foolishness|folly]]; to exceed it is disaster.

Second, [[strength|power]] must be **actively defended** — the Bull's [[strength|power]] evaporates the moment he surrenders his horns; the Old Hound's [[strength|power]] fades with age; the Stag, who "was saved from the [[strength|power]] of the dogs only to fall into the clutches of a [[the-lion|Lion]]" (243-001), discovers that escaping one [[danger|threat]] does not guarantee safety from another. Power without vigilance is merely deferred weakness.

## Relation to Strength

Power and [[strength]] are often conflated but Aesop distinguishes them. [[Strength]] is the **inherent capacity** — the Oak's trunk, the [[the-lion|Lion]]'s frame. Power is the **operative capacity** — the ability to bring that strength to bear on a situation. The Wind has strength but fails to exercise [[strength|power]] over the traveller; the Sun's gentler strength translates into effective [[strength|power]] (022-001). A creature may possess great strength and yet be [[strength|power]]less (the Old Hound at 126-001), or possess modest strength and yet exert considerable [[strength|power]] (the [[the-fox|Fox]] manipulating the Stag at 267-001). The relationship is d[[anger|ire]]ctional: strength is input, [[strength|power]] is output; one is a gift of nature, the other is a product of circumstances and skill.
