## Overview

The tank was one of the few genuinely new weapons to emerge from the First World War. Conceived as a solution to the trench stalemate, it combined armor protection, caterpillar traction, and mounted machine guns or cannon to cross No Man's Land, crush barbed wire, and suppress enemy strongpoints. Gibbs, as a war correspondent, was among the first to describe these "new engines of war" to the British public (109-001).

## Definition

A tank is a tracked, armored fighting vehicle designed for front-line combat. Early British tanks — designated "Mark I" through "Mark V" — were rhomboid-shaped steel boxes weighing up to 28 tons, crewed by eight men, and capable of crossing trenches up to 10 feet wide. Their name originated from the secrecy of their development: they were shipped in crates labeled "tank" (water tank) to disguise their purpose.

## In the War

Tanks made their combat debut at the Battle of Flers-Courcelette on 15 September 1916, part of the Somme offensive. Gibbs's dispatches "caused a sensation, a sense of excitement, laughter which shook the nation because of the comicality, the grotesque surprise, the possibility of quicker victory" (109-001). Early models were slow and mechanically unreliable, but their psychological impact was considerable.

By 1917, tanks had become central to offensive planning. At Cambrai, four hundred tanks were brought up secretly by night. The Hindenburg Line had been constructed "behind broad hedges of barbed wire with such wide ditches that no tank could cross" — but engineers devised a solution: each tank carried a massive fascine on its nose, "a big bundle of fagots about a yard and a half in diameter," dropped into the ditch to create a bridge (129-005, 129-006).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs captures both the technological wonder and the grotesque reality of tank warfare. He describes the machines as "so beast-like in their mysterious powers of trespassing over the ways of men, that they seemed as though the monsters of the Stone Age had been released from some hidden cave" (109-001). His account of the tanks' debut conveys the mix of hope, comedy, and horror with which they were received by soldiers and civilians alike.
