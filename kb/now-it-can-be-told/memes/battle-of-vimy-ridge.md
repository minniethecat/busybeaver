## Overview

The Battle of Vimy Ridge, fought from April 9 to 12, 1917, was the Canadian Corps's most celebrated victory of the First World War. Four Canadian divisions, fighting together for the first time, assaulted the heavily fortified Vimy Ridge, a seven-kilometer escarpment that dominated the Douai Plain in northeastern France. The ridge had resisted all previous attacks, including costly French assaults in 1915 that cost thousands of lives. Through meticulous preparation, including extensive tunneling, a devastating creeping barrage, and platoon-level tactical innovation, the Canadians captured the ridge in four days — a feat that helped forge Canada's national identity and demonstrated what careful planning could achieve in trench warfare. Gibbs had observed the ridge from the early French attacks through its eventual capture, recording the transformation from an impregnable German fortress to "the friendly old hill of Vimy" (071-020).

## Background

Vimy Ridge rose 145 meters above the Douai Plain, giving its occupiers commanding observation for miles in every direction. The Germans had seized it in October 1914 and spent two years fortifying it with three lines of trenches, deep dugouts, concrete machine-gun emplacements, and miles of tunnels. The French Tenth Army had attacked the ridge repeatedly in 1915, "suffering horrible casualties and failing to gain the heights in spite of the reckless valor of their men" (052-002). Gibbs noted that "the French Tenth Army had failed, too, at Vimy, thus failing to give the British troops protection on their right flank" during the Battle of Loos (057-025). By 1917, the ridge had become a symbol of German impregnability.

The British took over the sector from the French in early 1916, and the Canadian Corps, under Lieutenant-General Sir Julian Byng, was assigned to capture it as part of the larger Battle of Arras offensive in April 1917. Major-General Arthur Currie, commanding the 1st Canadian Division, insisted on meticulous preparation. The Canadians built a full-scale replica of the ridge behind their lines for rehearsals, dug extensive tunnel systems to bring troops close to the front line, and developed new infantry tactics based on platoon-level fire and maneuver.

Gibbs first saw the area during the French tenure: "I went there first — to the hill of Notre Dame de Lorette looking northward to Lens, and facing the Vimy Ridge, which the enemy held as a strong barrier against us above the village of Souchez" (071-006). The landscape was already devastated. Neuville St.-Vaast, once "a large village with a fine church... under the shelter of the friendly old hill of Vimy," had become "a frightful rubbish heap mingled with unexploded shells, the twisted iron of babies' perambulators, bits of dead bodies, and shattered farm-carts" (071-020).

## The Event

The assault began at 5:30 a.m. on Easter Monday, April 9, 1917, with a massive artillery bombardment that had been pounding German positions for two weeks. All four Canadian divisions advanced simultaneously behind a creeping barrage that moved forward at a precise rate of 100 yards every three minutes — a technical achievement requiring exact coordination between infantry and gunners.

The Canadians employed new tactics: each platoon was assigned a specific objective, and infantry sections advanced using fire-and-movement techniques rather than the traditional wave attacks. Tunnel systems brought the leading battalions within yards of the German front line before the attack began. The results were dramatic. By midday, the Canadians had captured most of the ridge's crest. Hill 145, the highest point, fell the next day; the "Pimple," the northernmost strongpoint, was taken on April 12.

Gibbs had observed the earlier, failed French attacks from the mining country around Lens: "From the flat country below the distant ridges of Notre Dame de Lorette and Vimy there rose a number of high black cones made by the refuse of the coal-mines" (048-001). After the Canadian victory, he passed through the ridge repeatedly: "Then for the first time I went into Ablain St.-Nazaire, which afterward I passed through scores of times on the way to Vimy when that ridge was ours" (071-012).

The early mining warfare presaging the battle was savage. Gibbs recorded an action where "some mines were exploded under the German earthworks on the lower slopes of the Vimy Ridge" and British troops rushed the craters — "the perilous adventure of driving in the mine shafts, putting tremendous charges of high explosives in the sapheads, and rushing the German positions" (077-004). Before the Canadian assault, a French sergeant told Gibbs of his men's misery in the shadow of the ridge: "That dirty Boche up there on Vimy looks out of his deep tunnels and laughs up his sleeve and says those poor devils of Frenchmen are not gay today! That is true, mon Capitaine. Mais, que voulez-vous? C'est pour la France" (071-016).

## Aftermath

The capture of Vimy Ridge cost the Canadian Corps approximately 10,600 casualties, including 3,598 dead. German losses were estimated at 20,000. But the victory was unambiguous: the ridge was taken, and it remained in Allied hands for the remainder of the war. Four Canadians were awarded the Victoria Cross for actions during the battle.

The battle has assumed a mythic place in Canadian national memory. It was the first time all four Canadian divisions fought together as a unified corps, and the victory was widely seen as a coming-of-age for Canada as a nation. The ridge itself was ceded to Canada by France in perpetuity, and the Canadian National Vimy Memorial now stands on Hill 145 — the highest point of the ridge.

General Currie's role in the preparation was critical. Gibbs observed that "General Currie, who became the Canadian Corps Commander, did not spare his men from training and work... The Canadian Corps became a powerful engine on our side" (065-005). The Vimy methods — rehearsal, creeping barrage coordination, delegated tactical authority — became a template for the Canadian Corps's subsequent successes and influenced British offensive doctrine.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's treatment of Vimy in the memoir is woven through multiple chapters and perspectives. He saw the ground from the French trenches before the ridge was captured, witnessed the devastation of the villages below it, and returned many times after the Canadian victory. His sympathy for the French poilus who died in earlier assaults is evident; so is his recognition of the Canadian achievement. The ridge appears as both a geographical feature and a symbol — of German strength before 1917, of Allied capability afterward. Gibbs's inclusion of the French sergeant's voice — speaking of his cold, wet soldiers and the Boche "up there on Vimy" who "laughs up his sleeve" — is one of the memoir's most effective uses of direct quotation to convey the human texture of the war.
