## Overview

Ypres (Ieper in Flemish) was a prosperous medieval cloth-trading city in the Belgian province of West [[Flanders]]. Before 1914 it was known for its magnificent Cloth Hall (Lakenhalle) and St. Martin's Cathedral, both masterpieces of Gothic architecture dating from the 13th century. By November 1918, after four years as the focal point of the Ypres Salient, the city had been reduced to a field of rubble and dust — "a heap of ruins and a graveyard of old memories" (012-018). Today the city has been painstakingly rebuilt, but its name remains synonymous with the industrial-scale destruction of the First World War.

## Geography

Ypres lies on the flat, low-lying plain of western [[Flanders]], approximately 30 km southwest of Bruges and 12 km from the French border. The surrounding terrain is composed of clay soils that drain poorly — a critical factor in the battlefield conditions of the Ypres Salient. To the east and south-east, a low semicircular ridge rises gently from the plain, encompassing the villages of Passchendaele, Messines, and Wytschaete. This ridge, the only high ground in the region, gave German forces commanding views over the city and the Allied positions below. The Yser Canal, running north from the city, and the Comines Canal, connecting Ypres to the River Lys, were important features of the defensive geography. Gibbs describes the fields east of the city in summer as covered with "wild flowers growing in the long grass, and the corn that never would be harvested" (004-001).

## Role in the War

Ypres was the pivot of the northern sector of the [[Western Front]] from October 1914 until the final Allied advance in September 1918. The German Schlieffen Plan had aimed to sweep through this region to capture the Channel ports of Dunkirk, Calais, and [[Boulogne]] — objectives that would have severed the British Expeditionary Force's lines of communication with England. The First Battle of Ypres (October–November 1914) halted this advance, establishing a defensive salient that bulged eastward around the city. For the remainder of the war, Ypres was held by the Allies while German forces occupied the high ground on three sides.

The city itself became a ghost town. Its civilian population was evacuated, and its streets — or what remained of them — were traversed only by soldiers, supply columns, and the occasional war correspondent. Gibbs recalls "strange nights in Ypres in those early days" when the ruined city was "a place of ghosts and shadows, lit by the white glare of German star-shells" (022-010). The Cloth Hall and Cathedral were systematically destroyed by German [[artillery]], their ruins becoming iconic images of the war's cultural vandalism. The Menin Gate, the eastern exit from the city through which hundreds of thousands of British soldiers marched toward the front, was later chosen as the site of the memorial to the missing.

The battles that defined the Salient — the gas attack of April 1915 (Second Ypres), the mining offensive at Messines in June 1917, and the Third Battle of Ypres (Passchendaele) from July to November 1917 — were fought on the ground immediately east of the city. In the spring of 1918, the German Lys Offensive pushed the line dangerously close, but Ypres remained in Allied hands. Gibbs notes that "the whole sweep of the Ypres salient" was visible from the high ground, a landscape "pitted and gouged with thousands of shell craters" (022-010).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs returned repeatedly to Ypres throughout his time as a war correspondent. He knew the city in its dying and death — its shell-shattered streets, its cellars where troops sheltered, its eerily intact fragments of domestic life amid the ruins. He compares the city to "a skeleton of its former self, with its great Cloth Hall standing roofless against the sky, and its cathedral towers chipped and scarred by shellfire" (024-001). The memoir devotes significant attention to the Salient not merely as a battlefield but as a place of particular horror — a low, wet killing ground overlooked by German observers, where men "stood in waterlogged trenches" and "the mud was a worse enemy than the German bullets" (012-018).

Gibbs's descriptions emphasize the absolute desolation: "There were no women in the Ypres salient. Nothing pretty or soft or womanly remained in that desert of the world" (012-018). This absence of the feminine and the domestic becomes, in his prose, a measure of how completely war had erased ordinary life from the landscape. The ruined Cloth Hall, built by medieval burghers who "had faith in the beauty of life," stands as Gibbs's central symbol for what civilization lost in the war.
