## Overview

Field Marshal Sir John French was the first Commander-in-Chief of the British Expeditionary Force on the Western Front. His tenure from August 1914 to December 1915 encompassed the transition from mobile warfare to the trench stalemate. Gibbs records how French's initial personal magnetism, derived from his Boer War reputation, was gradually eroded by the grinding attrition of trench warfare and costly, unsuccessful offensives (016-006).

## Biography

John Denton Pinkstone French was born in 1852 in Kent. He joined the Royal Navy at age 14 before transferring to the Army. He earned his reputation as a cavalry commander during the Second Boer War (1899–1902), where his bold leadership around Colesberg and in the relief of Kimberley brought him public acclaim. Promoted to field marshal in 1913, he was the natural choice to command the BEF when war broke out in 1914.

## Role in the War

French commanded the BEF during the opening campaigns of 1914 — Mons, the Marne, the Aisne, and First Ypres — where the small British professional army played a critical role in halting the German advance. However, as the war settled into trench warfare, French's leadership came under increasing strain. Gibbs notes that "when trench warfare began, and the daily routine of slaughter under German gunfire, when our artillery was weak... the prestige of the Commander-in-Chief faded from men's minds and he lost place in their admiration" (016-006).

The costly battles of 1915 — Neuve Chapelle, Festubert, and Loos — further damaged his standing. The failure at Loos, in particular, led to his replacement by Sir Douglas Haig in December 1915. He was subsequently created Earl of Ypres and served as Commander-in-Chief of Home Forces.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's treatment of French is measured but critical. He acknowledges French's early personal magnetism — "during the retreat from Mons, Sir John French had a touch of that personal power — his presence meant something to the men because of his reputation in South Africa" (016-006). But he is candid that French's prestige "was washed out in blood and mud" (016-006), a verdict that captures the fate of many 1914 commanders who proved unable to adapt to industrial warfare.
