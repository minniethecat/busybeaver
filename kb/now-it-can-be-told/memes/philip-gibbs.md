## Overview

Sir Philip Gibbs (1877–1962) was a British journalist and author who served as one of the first five officially accredited war correspondents on the Western Front during the First World War. His memoir *Now It Can Be Told* (1920) was one of the most significant postwar accounts of the conflict — a book in which Gibbs, freed from wartime censorship, revealed the "ghastly failures of distinguished generals," the staggering human cost of the Somme and Flanders offensives, and the daily suffering of the men in the trenches (003-001, 016-014).

## Biography

Philip Gibbs was born in 1877 in London. He began his journalistic career at the *Daily Chronicle* and later worked for the *Daily Express* and the *Daily Graphic*, establishing himself as one of Fleet Street's most capable reporters before the war. His early career included coverage of the Balkan Wars (1912–13), which gave him direct experience of modern combat before 1914.

During the war, Gibbs was attached to the British armies as an accredited correspondent from March 1915 — initially on an experimental basis, then with full credentials from June 1915. He served continuously on the Western Front through the Armistice and followed the Army of Occupation into Germany beyond the Rhine (003-005). For his wartime reporting and subsequent literary work, he was knighted (KBE) in 1920.

After the war, Gibbs published *Now It Can Be Told* (1920), followed by numerous other works of fiction and non-fiction. He died in 1962 at the age of 85.

## Role in the War

### Accreditation and Censorship

Gibbs's accreditation came after a prolonged struggle between the press and the military authorities. The War Office, and Lord Kitchener in particular, was "prejudiced against" war correspondents. The High Command possessed, in Gibbs's phrase, an "enormous, impregnable stupidity... on all matters of psychology" — unable to understand that a nation sending its sons to war "desired with a deep and poignant craving to know how those boys of theirs were living and how they were dying" (003-001).

The compromise reached in 1915 allowed a handful of correspondents to operate "under the strictest censorship," tasked with writing up "the glorious side of war... without, of course, any allusion to dead or dying men, to the ghastly failures of distinguished generals, or to the filth and horror of the battlefields" (003-001). Gibbs chafed under these restrictions, and the memoir he wrote after the war was in many ways a response to them — the title *Now It Can Be Told* is an explicit declaration that what follows was what he could not say at the time.

### Life as a War Correspondent

As a correspondent, Gibbs held an anomalous position. By "custom and tradition," correspondents had honorary rank as captains, but Gibbs describes this as amounting "to nothing, here or there." They were "civilians in khaki, with green bands round our right arms, and uncertain status." This uncertainty was in fact a privilege: it allowed correspondents "to speak to Tommies and sergeants as human beings, to be on terms of comradeship with junior subalterns and battalion commanders, and to sit at the right hand of generals without embarrassment to them or to ourselves" (016-001).

Gibbs dined in the messes of generals, including Adjutant-General Sir Neville Macready, and had direct access to the High Command — yet he consistently aligned his sympathies with the fighting men. His description of the "savage irony" of soldiers passing peaceful headquarters "where their doom was being planned" (019-006) captures this dual perspective.

### The Case Against British Generalship

One of Gibbs's most significant contributions in *Now It Can Be Told* was his candid assessment of British generalship — material that would never have passed wartime censorship. He notes that among the senior commanders "there was not one whose personality had that mysterious but essential quality of great generalship — inspiring large bodies of men with exalted enthusiasm, devotion, and faith." No British general, in Gibbs's judgment, possessed the personal magnetism of a Wellington or a Napoleon until Foch assumed supreme command (016-004, 016-006).

His critique is balanced but sharp. He acknowledges the industry, courage, and devotion of many staff officers, and praises the administrative machinery — transport, maps, intelligence — that kept the armies functioning. But he is unsparing on the strategic failures: the costly obsession with "small V's" (minor salients seized at appalling cost), the piecemeal attacks on the Somme that allowed the enemy to concentrate fire on narrow fronts, and the insistence on "aggressive" postures during periods when artillery inferiority made such tactics suicidal (016-012–016-014).

His verdict on the early battles is devastating: "The battles of Neuve Chapelle, Festubert, and Loos, in 1915, cost us thousands of dead and gave us no gain of any account; and both generalship and staff-work were, in the opinion of most officers who know anything of those battles, ghastly" (016-014).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs is simultaneously the memoir's narrator and one of its subjects. He writes in the first person throughout, placing himself in the scenes he describes — dining with generals, walking through ruined villages, watching the flash of artillery from observation posts, and bearing witness to the "dreadful fields" of the Somme strewn with dead. The memoir's emotional power derives from this dual role: Gibbs is both an insider (trusted by generals, privy to strategic discussions) and an outsider (a civilian among soldiers, a writer among men of action) who chooses to adopt "the point of view of the men who were to die" (019-006).

The single direct reference to Gibbs by name in the text occurs when a soldier "in his steel hat" recognizes him in passing: "Hullo, Gibbs!" — a small moment that underscores his presence among the troops, not just in headquarters (125-020).

The memoir's title is itself a statement of purpose. *Now It Can Be Told* promises a truth that wartime conditions had suppressed, and the book delivers: not a dispassionate history but a personal testament, written by a man who spent nearly four years watching, listening, and — by his own account — choosing sides.
