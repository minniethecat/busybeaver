## Overview

Barbed wire was one of the defining material features of trench warfare on the Western Front. Originally developed for agricultural fencing in the American West, it became a ubiquitous military obstacle, strung in dense tangles before trench parapets and across No Man's Land. For the infantryman, barbed wire represented both a physical barrier to assault and a psychological symbol of the static, entrapping nature of the war (076-001).

## Definition

Barbed wire consists of twisted steel strands with sharp barbs spaced at regular intervals. In its military application, it was deployed in dense entanglements—coils and concertinas—anchored by iron posts and designed to slow, entangle, and expose advancing infantry to machine-gun and artillery fire. Cutting through enemy wire under fire was among the most dangerous tasks of trench raiding (066-003).

## In the War

Barbed wire became nearly synonymous with the Western Front's defensive stalemate. The streets of French towns like Beauvais were "strewn with barbed wire and broken bottles" in 1914 as the Germans advanced (002-021). As the front stabilized, wire entanglements grew into elaborate defensive systems. Before attacks, artillery barrages attempted—often unsuccessfully—to cut paths through the wire. Gibbs recorded that at the Hohenzollern Redoubt, "intense as our artillery fire had been, it failed to destroy the enemy's barbed wire and front trenches sufficiently to clear the way" (062-006).

Night raids through the wire required extraordinary courage. Gibbs describes a lieutenant who "went out first and alone to reconnoiter the ground and to find the best way through the German barbed wire," returning with a wound but vital intelligence (066-003). Sappers performed "astounding feats," putting up wire beyond the front line "under a devilish fire" (032-043).

The wire also shaped the cavalry's fate. Gibbs records that the cavalry "believed they were 'bunkered' forever" in "that warfare of trenches and barbed wire," their training and tradition rendered futile (076-001).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs uses barbed wire as a recurring visual and symbolic motif. In quiet moments, snow gave "a beauty even to No Man's Land, making a lacework pattern of barbed wire" that hid "all the ugliness of destruction and death" (068-005). But he also invokes it as a metaphor for the war's moral entrapment: "the barbed wire which hedged in patriotism" (135-001) and the "spreading poison" of "a body of men behind barbed wire" (141-003).

Studying trench maps before the Somme offensive, Gibbs was "appalled at the task which lay before our men" when he saw "one vast maze of trenches and redoubts and barbed wire and tunnels" stretching thirty miles deep (100-004). The tanks of 1917 were designed specifically to overcome this obstacle, "plowing through the barbed wire" with fascines mounted on their noses (129-006).
