## Overview

The Battle of the Somme, fought between July 1 and November 18, 1916, was the largest battle of the First World War and one of the bloodiest military operations in human history. The first day alone — July 1, 1916 — remains the worst day in British military history, with nearly 60,000 casualties, including 19,240 dead. [[Philip Gibbs]] covered the battle as a [[war correspondent]] and his memoir captures both the strategic scale and the intimate horror: "in the battlefields of the Somme and [[Flanders]], of [[Picardy]] and Artois, they attacked isolated objectives and died in heaps" (016-008).

## Background

By late 1915, the [[Western Front]] had settled into a bloody stalemate. The Allies planned a joint offensive at the Somme for 1916, intending to relieve pressure on the French at [[Battle of Verdun|Verdun]] and break through German lines. The German attack at Verdun in February 1916 transformed the Somme from a primarily French-led offensive into a British one. The British New Armies — [[Lord Kitchener|Kitchener]]'s volunteer recruits — would bear the brunt of the fighting.

## The Event

The offensive began at 7:30 a.m. on July 1, 1916, with the detonation of massive mines under German positions and an [[artillery]] bombardment that had lasted seven days. The infantry advanced in waves and was met by German machine-gunners who had survived the bombardment in deep dugouts. The first day was catastrophic; subsequent months saw incremental gains at hideous cost.

Gibbs moved through the Somme headquarters during the battle: "many times, during the battles of the Somme, I passed the headquarters of the 14th Corps" (019-001). He observed the transformation of the landscape: "digging in the salient or beyond the Somme, where dead bodies lay in unburied heaps" (011-003). The battle ground on through the summer and autumn until November mud made further operations impossible.

## Aftermath

The Somme offensive gained approximately six miles of territory at the cost of over a million casualties on all sides. British losses of over 400,000 men staggered the nation. Yet the battle inflicted severe damage on the German army — losses from which, many historians argue, it never fully recovered. The German withdrawal to the Hindenburg Line in early 1917 was a direct consequence. The Somme also marked the combat debut of the [[tank]], first used on September 15, 1916 at Flers-Courcelette.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's experience of the Somme permeates his memoir. He was present through the preparations, the fighting, and the aftermath. His descriptions move between strategic overview and devastating personal detail. He records the soldiers' hope and subsequent disillusionment, the physical destruction of the French countryside, and the sheer industrial scale of the killing. "Ammunition columns went up to the Somme front in long, unending lines" (016-008). The Somme represents, in Gibbs's narrative, the moment when the war's true nature became undeniable.
