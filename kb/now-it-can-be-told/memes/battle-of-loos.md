## Overview

The Battle of Loos, fought from September 25 to October 8, 1915, was the largest British offensive of that year and the first major action for [[Lord Kitchener|Kitchener]]'s New Armies. It marked the first British use of poison gas on the Western Front. For [[Philip Gibbs]], Loos was a pivotal experience — he witnessed its "ghastly failure" (009-004) and the battle became a reference point throughout his memoir for the consequences of poor generalship and inadequate preparation.

## Background

By autumn 1915, the French were pressing the British to take a larger share of the fighting. [[Sir John French]], commanding the BEF, was under political pressure to demonstrate British commitment. The Loos sector was chosen despite [[Douglas Haig|Haig]]'s reservations about the terrain — open ground overlooked by German positions. The British planned to use chlorine gas to compensate for the shortage of [[artillery]] shells.

## The Event

On September 25, 1915, the British released chlorine gas from cylinders and attacked on a wide front. In some sectors the gas drifted back onto British troops; elsewhere it achieved initial surprise. The 15th (Scottish) Division broke through the German front line and captured the village of Loos itself. But reserves — including the 21st and 24th Divisions of the New Army — were held too far back and arrived exhausted after night marches. When they attacked on September 26 against intact German defenses, the result was massacre.

Gibbs records how the New Army, composed of Kitchener volunteers, suffered at Loos: "How the New Army Went to Loos" (043-002). The battle came to symbolize the gap between the courage of the volunteer soldier and the failures of the system that sent them into battle. "Neuve Chapelle, Festubert, and Loos, in 1915, cost us thousands of lives for bits of ground that were not worth a single drop of blood" (016-014).

## Aftermath

Loos cost the British approximately 60,000 casualties, including many battalion commanders and staff officers whose loss would be felt for years. Sir John French was relieved of command two months later, replaced by Haig. The failure at Loos, combined with the shell scandal of 1915, contributed to the fall of the Liberal government and the formation of a coalition under Asquith. For the soldiers, Loos was a bitter lesson in the cost of inadequate artillery preparation.

## In the Memoir

Loos haunts Gibbs's memoir. He returned to the battle repeatedly — not as a tactical analysis but as a moral reference point. The "ghastly failure" at Loos, the needless slaughter of the New Army, the ground that was "not worth a single drop of blood" — these phrases recur because Loos crystallized for Gibbs everything that was wrong with the conduct of the war. It was at Loos, perhaps more than any other single battle, that his disillusionment with the High Command took root.
