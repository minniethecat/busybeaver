## Overview

Brigadier-General John Charteris occupied one of the war's most sensitive positions: Chief of Intelligence at General Headquarters. For Gibbs and his fellow war correspondents, Charteris was the man who controlled their access to the front and decided what they could publish. The memoir depicts him as a complex figure — sometimes obstructive, often nervous, but ultimately a man doing an impossible job at the intersection of military secrecy and public knowledge.

## Biography

John Charteris was born in 1877 in Glasgow. A Royal Engineers officer, he served in India before becoming Haig's aide-de-camp and trusted confidant. When Haig assumed command of the BEF in December 1915, Charteris was appointed Chief of Intelligence — a role that went far beyond traditional military intelligence to encompass censorship, propaganda, and the management of press relations. His optimistic intelligence assessments, which consistently underestimated German strength and morale, became controversial after the war. He later served as a Conservative Member of Parliament. He died in 1946.

## Role in the War

Charteris's office was the nexus through which all war correspondents had to pass. Gibbs describes him positioned at Haig's side during a meeting about press censorship: "He was very handsome as he sat behind a Louis XIV table, with General Charteris — his Chief of Intelligence, who was our chief, too — behind him at one side, for prompting and advice" (010-003).

The Commander-in-Chief's relationship with his intelligence chief shaped what journalists could report. Haig "knew but vaguely through General Charteris, who looked upon us as his special 'cross'" (010-005) about the correspondents' frustrations with censorship. Gibbs and his colleagues "had fought hard for liberty in mentioning units, to give the honor to the troops, and for other concessions which would free our pens."

During the tense meeting when correspondents pressed their grievances, Gibbs noticed that Haig "made a note of our grievances, turning now and again to General Charteris, who was extremely nervous at our frankness of speech" (010-008). The scene captures the dynamic: Charteris as the nervous gatekeeper, Haig as the commander who could be persuaded.

## In the Memoir

Charteris appears in Gibbs's memoir not as a villain but as a man caught in the structural tension between military necessity and journalistic truth-telling. The correspondents' campaign for greater freedom succeeded — "in later stages of the war I personally had no great complaint against the censorship, and wrote all that was possible to write of the actual conditions of the fighting" (010-008) — but Charteris's nervousness at their frank speech reveals how unusual such access was.

Later in the memoir, Gibbs records a revealing anecdote about Charteris's possessiveness over access: "after so much civility to him. Charteris would be furious. He would bar the way" (089-010). This flashes of temper humanize the intelligence chief and remind readers that the censorship system was ultimately personal — a matter of individual relationships rather than abstract rules.
