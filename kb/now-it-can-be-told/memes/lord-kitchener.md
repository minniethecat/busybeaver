## Overview

Horatio Herbert Kitchener, 1st Earl Kitchener, was one of the few British leaders who foresaw a long war in 1914. As Secretary of State for War, he launched the volunteer recruitment campaign that created Britain's mass citizen army — the "New Armies" or "Kitchener's Army." His death at sea in June 1916 was one of the war's great shocks.

## Biography

Kitchener was born in Ireland in 1850. He made his reputation as the conqueror of the Sudan (1898) and as commander-in-chief during the Second Boer War (1900–1902). By 1914 he was Britain's most famous soldier. Appointed Secretary of State for War on the outbreak of hostilities, he immediately predicted a conflict lasting at least three years and requiring armies of millions — a forecast his cabinet colleagues initially dismissed.

## Role in the War

Kitchener's "Your Country Needs You" poster campaign and his call for 100,000 volunteers produced an overwhelming response. The "New Armies" that fought on the Somme in 1916 were his creation. Gibbs records the cost these volunteer divisions paid: battalions composed of friends and neighbors from the same towns, who enlisted together and died together (016-014).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's memoir is populated by Kitchener's volunteers — the Pals battalions, the citizen-soldiers who had been civilians two years before they went over the top at the Somme. The memoir's account of tactical failures that cost "thousands of dead" (016-014) is indirectly a judgment on the system Kitchener built: an army of unprecedented size but variable training, thrown against German defensive positions of terrible strength.
