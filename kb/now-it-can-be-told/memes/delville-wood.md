## Overview

Delville Wood was a dense copse on the Somme battlefield, east of Longueval, that became one of the most notorious killing grounds of the 1916 campaign. Its name was transformed by the troops who fought there into "Devil's Wood" — an apt description for a place that Gibbs records was "captured and lost several times" (108-019), each cycle consuming more lives. The wood is principally remembered for the stand of the South African Brigade of the 9th (Scottish) Division, who held it through six days and five nights of concentrated German shellfire in July 1916, emerging with barely a quarter of their strength. Today it is the site of the South African National Memorial and the Delville Wood Cemetery.

## Geography

Delville Wood lies approximately 1 kilometer east of the village of Longueval and 3 kilometers east of Bazentin-le-Petit, on gently rising ground of the Somme plateau. Before the battle, the wood covered roughly 170 acres of mature hornbeam and oak, with thick undergrowth. By the autumn of 1916, it was a wasteland of shattered stumps and shell craters. A defining geographic feature was the ride or clearing that ran north-south through its center — known as "Princes Street" — which became a boundary between British and German-held sectors during the fighting. The terrain's gentle slope toward the northeast gave the wood strategic value as an observation point.

## Role in the War

Delville Wood was attacked on July 14, 1916, as part of the Battle of Bazentin Ridge, the second major British push on the Somme. The 9th (Scottish) Division captured Longueval and the southern edge of the wood, but the Germans held the northern and eastern portions. The South African Brigade — the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 4th South African Infantry — was ordered into the wood on July 15 to complete its capture.

What followed was one of the most concentrated artillery bombardments of the war. Gibbs describes how the German response to these piecemeal attacks allowed "a vast concentration of explosives which plowed up our men" (108-018). The South Africans endured six days of shellfire in the wood, and by the time they were relieved on July 20, of the approximately 3,150 men who had entered, only about 750 emerged unwounded. "In Delville Wood the South African Brigade of the 9th Division was cut to pieces, and I saw the survivors come out with few officers to lead them" (108-019).

The fighting continued through August. Gibbs describes the recurring pattern: "Delville Wood was captured and lost several times and became 'Devil's' Wood to men who lay there under the crash and fury of massed gunfire until a wretched remnant of what had been a glorious brigade of youth crawled out stricken and bleeding when relieved by another brigade ordered to take their turn in that devil's cauldron" (108-019).

Gibbs also records an episode revealing the High Command's lingering delusions about cavalry warfare: a major on General Aylmer Haldane's staff "blurted out his desire. Could he lead a squadron round Delville Wood? Could he take that ride into the blue? He would give his soul to do it" (108-014). General Haldane's response — "Get on with your job" — was a terse rebuke to outdated notions of war.

In March 1918, during the German Spring Offensive, Delville Wood was lost again. Gibbs returned to find that "once again after many battles the enemy was in Courcelette and High Wood and Delville Wood" (130-023).

## In the Memoir

Delville Wood appears in Gibbs's memoir as one of the "woods of death" (019-006) that came to define the Somme campaign. His account emphasizes several of his recurring themes: the wasteful nature of the piecemeal attacks that allowed German batteries to concentrate their fire, the extraordinary endurance of the infantry, and the gap between the High Command's cheerful optimism and the reality faced by fighting men. The transformation of "Delville" into "Devil's" Wood is a motif that Gibbs deploys to underscore the infernal quality of the battlefield, aligning with his broader characterization of the Somme as a "bloodbath." The South African Brigade's destruction carries particular weight in his narrative, representing the sacrifice of distant imperial troops in a European war not of their making.
