## Overview

The Zeppelin and airship raids of the First World War represented a new dimension of warfare: the deliberate bombing of civilian populations far behind the battle lines. German airships, named after their inventor Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin, crossed the North Sea to drop bombs on English towns and cities beginning in 1915. Later, Gotha and Giant bombers continued the campaign. Behind the Western Front, towns like Amiens and Dickebusch endured fierce air raids that destroyed buildings "before the paint was dry" (091-001). Gibbs observed both the physical destruction and the psychological effects of this novel terror — noting the strange paradox that soldiers in the trenches sometimes "prayed God to get the Germans to send zeppelins to England — to make the people know what war meant" (042-021).

## Background

Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin had developed rigid airships before the war, and the German military saw them as a weapon to strike at the British homeland. The Kaiser initially restricted bombing to military targets, but by 1915 these restrictions were lifted. The raids were intended to terrorize the civilian population, disrupt industrial production, and divert British resources from the Western Front. At first, British air defenses were almost nonexistent — searchlights, anti-aircraft guns, and fighter aircraft had to be developed from scratch in response to the threat.

Gibbs observed the impact of air raids on both sides of the Channel. In England, "government offices were scared of air raids" (015-004), and the civilian population experienced for the first time the direct impact of modern warfare. Behind the front, the fear was constant and justified: Amiens suffered repeated bombing, and forward-area towns lived under the perpetual threat of destruction from the air.

## The Event

German naval Zeppelins conducted their first raid on England in January 1915, bombing Great Yarmouth and King's Lynn. Over the following three years, more than fifty Zeppelin raids struck English towns and cities, killing approximately 500 people. The raids reached their peak in 1916, before improved British air defenses — including incendiary bullets that could ignite the hydrogen-filled airships — made Zeppelin operations increasingly costly.

By 1917, the Germans shifted to Gotha heavy bombers and later Giant (Riesenflugzeug) aircraft. These twin-engined bombers conducted daylight raids on London in June and July 1917, causing panic and leading to the creation of the London Air Defence Area. Gibbs recorded the peculiar atmosphere behind the lines: "A good night for a German air raid" became a grimly familiar observation among officers (091-018). At Amiens, the air raids repeatedly struck the city. Gibbs saw a building there "wrecked, too, in a fierce air raid, before the paint was dry" (091-001). The bombing disrupted daily life entirely — nights in one week saw repeated raids, "and as the German mark was falling in value, the enemy was paying for bombs instead of selling them" (094-046).

Gibbs also described airships visible from the battlefield, capturing the strange beauty of these deadly machines: soldiers "were more interested in an airship, a silver fish with shining gills" (042-027).

## Aftermath

The Zeppelin and bomber raids failed to break British morale — indeed, they arguably strengthened it by giving civilians a direct stake in the war. The raids did succeed in diverting significant British resources to home defense: by 1918, more than 12,000 officers and men were assigned to anti-aircraft duties in Britain, along with hundreds of fighter aircraft and searchlights. The psychological legacy was profound; the raids established the template for the strategic bombing campaigns of the Second World War, demonstrating that twentieth-century warfare would not spare civilian populations.

For soldiers at the front, the air raids created a complex emotional response. As Gibbs recorded with unflinching honesty, some men who had endured the horror of the trenches harbored a bitter wish: they "prayed God to get the Germans to send zeppelins to England — to make the people know what war meant" (042-021). This sentiment captured the profound gulf between the front line and the home front that would characterize the war's legacy.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's treatment of air raids is characteristically layered. He does not devote a single narrative to the Zeppelin campaign but weaves observations about aerial bombing throughout his memoir. His accounts capture both the strategic novelty and the human response — from the terror of civilians to the sardonic humor of soldiers who treated air raids as just another hazard of war. His mention of government offices being "scared of air raids" (015-004) is notably ironic, contrasting the relative safety of London bureaucrats with the conditions of the men they directed.
