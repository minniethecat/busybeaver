## Overview

The First Battle of Ypres, fought in October–November 1914, was the climactic engagement of the "Race to the Sea" and the last major battle of 1914 on the Western Front. The British Expeditionary Force, reduced to a shadow of its original strength after Mons and the Marne, held the line around the Belgian city of Ypres against overwhelming German forces. Gibbs knew the Ypres Salient intimately and returned to it throughout the war. "There were no women in the Ypres salient. Nothing pretty or soft about that desolate stretch of country which will be forever famous in the history of the world" (012-018).

## Background

After the German advance on Paris was halted at the Marne in September 1914, both sides attempted to outflank each other to the north. The British moved from the Aisne to Flanders, anchoring their line near Ypres. The Germans, determined to break through to the Channel ports of Calais and Dunkirk, massed newly formed reserve divisions for a final offensive before winter.

## The Event

The battle raged from October 19 to November 22, 1914. German attacks fell with furious intensity on the thin British line. The professional soldiers of the BEF — the "Old Contemptibles" — held against repeated assaults despite crippling losses. At Gheluvelt, a handful of Worcesters counterattacked and saved the line. Gibbs describes the broader arc: "the battle of the Marne and the first battle of Ypres" (003-005) as the twin moments that saved the Allies in 1914. From Cassel Hill, observers could see "the whole sweep of the Ypres salient" (022-010).

## Aftermath

The First Ypres ended with the Allies holding the city but the Germans occupying the surrounding ridges. The Salient — a bulge in the Allied line — would remain one of the war's most dangerous sectors for four years. Second Ypres (1915) saw the first use of poison gas. Third Ypres (1917), known as Passchendaele, became synonymous with mud and slaughter. The town of Ypres itself was reduced to rubble, its Cloth Hall a skeletal ruin visible for miles.

## In the Memoir

The Ypres Salient is a recurring presence in Gibbs's memoir — a landscape of desolation that he visited again and again. His descriptions of the salient's geography, its destroyed villages, its constant shellfire, and the men who lived and died there form one of the memoir's most sustained narrative threads. "The early battles round Ypres, and the slaughter at Neuve Chapelle" (024-001) marked, for Gibbs, the transition from the war of movement to the war of attrition.
