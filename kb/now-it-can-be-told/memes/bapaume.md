## Overview

Bapaume was a small market town in the Pas-de-Calais that became, for the British army on the Somme, a symbol of the objective that seemed always just out of reach. Lying on the straight Roman road running northeast from Albert, it was the natural axis of the British advance throughout the 1916 campaign. Gibbs called it "the goal of our desire" (117-001), attained at last in March 1917 when Australian troops entered the ruins. Its loss during the German Spring Offensive of 1918 and subsequent recapture mirrored the violent pendulum of the Western Front.

## Geography

Bapaume sits astride the Albert–Bapaume road (the D929), which runs in a nearly straight line across the Somme plateau. The town is located on relatively level ground at approximately 120 meters elevation, with gentle rises to the south and west. Before the war, it was a modest agricultural center, a stopping point on the road from Arras to Péronne. The road itself became one of the defining features of the Somme battlefield — Gibbs refers to it repeatedly as a landmark and axis of movement. "Often on the way to Bapaume I stepped off the road to look into that white gulf" of the Lochnagar mine crater (104-006).

## Role in the War

Bapaume served as a German administrative and logistical center behind the Somme front from 1914 onward. During the Battle of the Somme, it lay at the heart of the German second and third defensive lines. "Every hillock a machine-gun fortress and every wood a deathtrap" guarded the approach, as Gibbs noted of the defenses "all the way back to Bapaume" (019-006). The town was the objective toward which the British army fought yard by yard through High Wood, Delville Wood, the Pozières ridge, Martinpuich, and Courcelette.

The fall of Bapaume in March 1917, when the Germans withdrew to the Hindenburg Line, was the tangible — if belated — fruit of the Somme offensive. Gibbs was present: "I was with the Australians on that day when they swarmed into Bapaume, and they brought out trophies like men at a country fair" (117-002). He records an Australian colonel "who came riding with a German beer-mug at his saddle" and Australian soldiers setting up "some painted scenery which they had found among the rubbish" to create the "Coo-ee Theater" (117-002).

In the Spring Offensive of March 1918, the fortunes reversed. Gibbs recounts the desperate days: "I went into the swirl of our retreat day after day up by Guiscard and Hum; then, as the line moved back, by Péronne and Bapaume" (130-023). The town fell to the Germans once more, only to be retaken in the final Allied advance later that year.

## In the Memoir

Bapaume serves in Gibbs's narrative as both a geographic waypoint and a symbol of grinding perseverance. It appears first in the context of the great mine explosion at La Boisselle — "Often on the way to Bapaume I stepped off the road to look into that white gulf" (104-006) — linking the road to the battle's most spectacular moment of destruction. Throughout the autumn of 1916, Gibbs traveled the Albert–Bapaume road repeatedly, watching the slow progress of the offensive. "Up by Pozières or Martinpuich or beyond, by Ligny-Tilloy, on the way to Bapaume, in the long struggle and slaughter over every inch of ground" (086-001), he wrote his dispatches by night in Amiens.

The liberation of the town, when it finally came, is rendered with palpable relief: "Our men who had escaped death and shell-shock kept their sanity through all this wilderness of slaughter, kept — oh, marvelous! — their spirit of humor, their faith in some kind of victory" (117-001). The Australians' theatrical dragshow in the ruins captures the irrepressible spirit that Gibbs admired. Yet the account is shadowed by what came next: the town's loss in 1918, when "once again after many battles the enemy was in Courcelette and High Wood and Delville Wood" (130-023), and the ghosts of the old Somme dead seemed to rise again.
