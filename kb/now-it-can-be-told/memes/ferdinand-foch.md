## Overview

Ferdinand Foch was the French general who, in the crisis of March 1918, was appointed Supreme Allied Commander — the first unified command of the war. Gibbs singles out Foch as the exception to the pattern of uninspiring British generalship, noting that "no such man appeared on the western front until Foch obtained the supreme command" (016-006).

## Biography

Foch was born in Tarbes, France, in 1851. A military theorist before the war, he taught at the École de Guerre and wrote influential works on the principles of war. He commanded at the Marne (1914) and coordinated the French, British, and Belgian forces during the Race to the Sea. After setbacks in 1915–1916, he was recalled to prominence and ultimately given supreme command when the German Spring Offensive of 1918 threatened to split the Allied armies.

## Role in the War

Foch's appointment as Supreme Commander in March 1918 was the turning point of the final year. The German offensives of spring 1918 — Operation Michael and its successors — had shattered the Allied line and threatened Paris. Foch coordinated the Allied defense and then orchestrated the counter-offensive that became the Hundred Days, forcing Germany to seek an armistice.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs presents Foch as the commander the British Army had lacked. The memoir's observation that the British front had "no general with the gift of speech — a gift too much despised by our British men of action — or with a character and prestige which could raise him to the highest rank in popular imagination" is implicitly contrasted with Foch's personal force (016-006).
