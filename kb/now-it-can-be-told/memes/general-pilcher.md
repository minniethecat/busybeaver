## Overview

Major-General Thomas David Pilcher commanded the 17th (Northern) Division of the British Army on the Western Front. Gibbs encountered him twice — once during the Ypres Salient operations of March 1916, when Pilcher was visibly affected by heavy shelling at St.-Eloi (074-001, 074-008), and again during the Somme offensive in July 1916, when the 17th Division's attacks on Contalmaison were marred by command confusion and heavy losses (108-003, 108-004).

## Role in the War

Pilcher's 17th Division arrived in the Ypres Salient to relieve the 3rd Division under General Haldane in early 1916. The relief itself became a German opportunity: "The Germans took advantage of the change in defense by a sudden attack after the explosion of a mine, and the men of the 17th Division, new to this ground, abandoned a position of some local importance" (074-001). The position was the Bluff, a contested hillock near St.-Eloi that cost many lives for its dubious value.

When Gibbs visited Pilcher's headquarters at Reninghelst on March 4th, he found an atmosphere of tension. The staff of the 17th Division were "frosty in their greeting," while a brigadier from the displaced 3rd Division was still conducting operations in their sector. Gibbs's portrait of Pilcher at this moment is unusually personal and unguarded:

> "General Pilcher himself was much shaken. The old gentleman had been at St.-Eloi when the bombardment had begun on his men. With Captain Rattnag his A.D.C. he lay for an hour in a ditch with shells screaming overhead and bursting close."
> — [074-008]

Pilcher's nervousness lingered: "More than once when I talked with him he raised his head and listened nervously and said: 'Do you hear the guns? … They are terrible'" (074-008). It is one of the memoir's few depictions of a general officer visibly traumatized by bombardment.

Four months later, during the Somme offensive, Pilcher's division was thrown into the attack on Contalmaison on July 7th. The operation was chaotic: "Whatever division of opinion existed in the High Command I do not know; it was visible to all of us that for some days there were uncertainty of direction, hesitation, conflicting orders" (108-003). A battalion of the Prussian Guard, rushed from Valenciennes "without maps or guidance, walked into the barrage which covered the advance of our men and were almost annihilated." But the 17th Division fared no better: although some British troops briefly entered Contalmaison, "they were smashed out of it again by storms of fire followed by masses of men who poured out from Mametz Wood" (108-003).

Gibbs's verdict on the day's command was scathing: "The 17th Division, under General Pilcher, did not attack at the expected time. There was no coordination of divisions; no knowledge among battalion officers of the strategy or tactics of a battle in which their men were involved" (108-004).

## In the Memoir

Pilcher appears in Gibbs's narrative at two distinct moments that together form an unflattering portrait of divisional command under the strain of industrial warfare. The first episode — Pilcher cowering in a ditch, then nervously listening for the guns — is rendered with a reporter's eye for the human cost borne by commanders themselves. The second — the bungled Contalmaison attack — is rendered with a critic's eye for systemic failure. Gibbs does not single Pilcher out for blame; the "uncertainty of direction, hesitation, conflicting orders" appear to originate higher up. But the two episodes, read together, suggest a general worn down by a war that had outpaced his generation's military training.
