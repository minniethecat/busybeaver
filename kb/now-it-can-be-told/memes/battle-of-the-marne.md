## Overview

The First Battle of the Marne, fought from September 5 to 12, 1914, was the battle that saved Paris and determined the character of the entire war. After a month of retreat from Mons, the exhausted British Expeditionary Force and the French army turned and struck the overextended German right wing. The German plan — the Schlieffen-Moltke envelopment of Paris — collapsed. But the Allies lacked the strength to exploit their victory, and the Germans withdrew to the Aisne heights, where both sides dug in. For Philip Gibbs, the Marne was a foundational memory of the war: "the victory of the Marne" (024-001) was the pivot on which everything that followed turned.

## Background

In August 1914, the German armies swept through Belgium and northern France according to the Schlieffen Plan, a vast wheeling movement designed to envelop Paris and knock France out of the war in six weeks. The BEF fought a delaying action at Mons on August 23, then fell back in the epic retreat that Gibbs's generation would never forget. "Germans from Mons to the Marne" (042-001) — the retreat was a national trauma for the British and an existential crisis for the French. By early September, German cavalry patrols were within sight of the Eiffel Tower.

## The Event

The German First Army under von Kluck, pursuing the retreating Allies, swung east of Paris instead of enveloping it — exposing its flank to the newly formed French Sixth Army. On September 5, General Joffre launched the counter-offensive. The BEF, which had been retreating for two weeks, turned and advanced into the gap between the German First and Second Armies.

Gibbs records the desperation of the moment: "during the first battle of the Marne" (002-012). The French requisitioned Paris taxicabs to rush reserves to the front. The fighting along the Marne and its tributaries was confused and savage. By September 9, von Moltke suffered a nervous collapse. His staff officers ordered a general retreat to the Aisne. Paris was saved.

## Aftermath

The Marne was a decisive strategic victory — but an incomplete one. The Germans withdrew only forty miles to the Aisne heights, where they dug defensive positions. The Allies, exhausted, could not dislodge them. The Race to the Sea followed, as both sides attempted to outflank each other northward, ending only when the trench lines reached the Channel coast in October. "The sun before the battle of the Marne these cries went up" (002-019) — the brief war of movement gave way to positional warfare. The Marne had saved France, but it also ensured four more years of slaughter.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs did not witness the Marne; he was not yet accredited as a war correspondent in 1914. But the battle is foundational to his memoir because it shaped everything he would later observe. "From Mons, the victory of the Marne, the early battles round Ypres" (024-001) — the Marne was the moment the German offensive scheme failed and the contours of the Western Front were set. Gibbs's entire subsequent experience — trench warfare on the Somme, in Flanders, at Arras and Passchendaele — was a consequence of the outcome at the Marne.
