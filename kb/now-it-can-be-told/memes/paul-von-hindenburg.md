## Overview

Field Marshal Paul von Hindenburg was recalled from retirement in 1914 and rose to command all German forces as Chief of the General Staff. In [[Philip Gibbs]]'s memoir, Hindenburg appears primarily through the great defensive system that bore his name — the Hindenburg Line — and through his tactical responses to Allied offensives. His "elastic system of defense" at the Chemin des Dames in 1917 demonstrated the operational ingenuity that prolonged the war on the [[Western Front]].

## Biography

Paul von Hindenburg was born in Posen, Prussia in 1847. A career officer in the Prussian Army, he served in the Austro-Prussian War (1866) and Franco-Prussian War (1870–71), retiring as a corps commander in 1911. Recalled at the outbreak of war in 1914, he achieved early fame with victory at Tannenberg on the Eastern Front alongside [[Erich Ludendorff]]. In August 1916 he succeeded von Falkenhayn as Chief of the General Staff, becoming the effective military dictator of Germany. After the war, he served as President of the Weimar Republic from 1925 until his death in 1934.

## Role in the War

As Chief of the General Staff, Hindenburg directed German strategy on all fronts. His most visible legacy on the Western Front was the Hindenburg Line (Siegfriedstellung), a fortified defensive system to which German forces withdrew in early 1917. Gibbs describes the retreat: "the enemy, stricken by the prolonged fury of our attack, fell back in a far and wide retreat across a country which he laid waste, to the shelter of his Hindenburg line, from Bullecourt to St.-Quentin" (116-004).

When General Nivelle launched his catastrophic French offensive in April 1917, Hindenburg was prepared. Gibbs records: "Hindenburg was ready. He adopted his 'elastic system of defense,' which consisted in withdrawing the main body of his troops beyond the range of the French barrage fire, leaving only a few outposts to camouflage the withdrawal" (125-002). This tactical innovation turned Nivelle's offensive into a bloody failure.

The Hindenburg Line was finally broken by British forces in late 1918. Gibbs captures the moment: "squadrons of [[tank|tanks]] broke through the Hindenburg line, and infantry streamed through the breach, captured hundreds of guns, ten thousand prisoners" (129-001).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs writes of Hindenburg with a mixture of professional respect for his military skill and moral condemnation of the German High Command's willingness to sacrifice men. The memoir vividly describes the German scorched-earth retreat to the Hindenburg Line: "The enemy was in retreat to his Hindenburg line, over a wide stretch of country which he laid waste behind him, making a desert of French villages and orchards and parks" (117-003).

Gibbs also records how Hindenburg and his staff were fully informed of Nivelle's plans before the offensive — intelligence that enabled the elastic defense: "The 'secret' of his preparations was known by every officer in the French army and by Hindenburg and his staff, who prepared a new method of defense to meet it" (125-001).
