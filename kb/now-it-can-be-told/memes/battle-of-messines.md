## Overview

The Battle of Messines, fought in June 1917, was one of the most meticulously planned and successfully executed operations of the First World War. Under the command of General Sir Herbert Plumer's Second Army, the battle opened with the detonation of 17 enormous mines placed under the German positions on the Messines-Wytschaete Ridge — an explosion that Gibbs witnessed and described in unforgettable terms. The attack succeeded brilliantly in its immediate objectives, capturing the ridge that the Germans had held since 1914. Gibbs, who had watched the preparations and aftermath, called it a "model in organization and method" and compared its precision to "the clockwork of a death machine" (022-001).

## Background

The Messines Ridge, extending from the village of Messines in the south to Wytschaete in the north, was a long, low rise in the Flanders landscape that gave German artillery observers commanding views over the Ypres Salient. From the ridge, "they could see for miles across our territory, not only the trenches, but the ways up to the trenches, and the villages behind them and the roads through the villages" (028-005). The British had been tunneling under the ridge since early 1916, constructing 21 mine shafts — some extending more than 2,000 feet — and packing them with nearly one million pounds of ammonal high explosive. The operation was intended as a preliminary to the larger Flanders offensive (the Third Battle of Ypres) that would begin in July.

General Sir Herbert Plumer commanded the Second Army. Gibbs was struck by Plumer's unassuming appearance — "in appearance he was almost a caricature of an old-time British general, with his ruddy, pippin-cheeked face, with white hair, and a fierce little white mustache, and blue, watery eyes, and a little potbelly and short legs" (022-001). Yet behind this avuncular exterior lay a commander known for his meticulous attention to detail and his refusal to squander his men's lives.

## The Event

On June 7, 1917, at 3:10 a.m., 17 of the 21 mines (two had been discovered by the Germans, one had detonated prematurely, and one was positioned outside the attack zone) were fired simultaneously. Gibbs was there and saw it happen:

> "I saw the seventeen mines go up, and earth and flame gush out of them as though the fires of hell had risen. A terrible sight, as the work of men against their fellow-creatures... It was the signal for seven hundred and fifty of our heavy guns and two thousand of our field-guns to open fire, and behind a moving wall of bursting shells English, Irish, and New Zealand soldiers moved forward in dense waves" (126-004).

The explosion was reportedly heard in London and remains one of the largest non-nuclear man-made explosions in history. An estimated 10,000 German soldiers were killed instantly in the blasts. The attacking infantry, advancing behind a carefully coordinated creeping barrage, met only scattered resistance. "It was almost a 'walkover.' Only here and there groups of Germans served their machine-guns to the death. Most of the living were stupefied amid their dead in the upheaved trenches, slashed woods, and deepest dugouts" (126-004).

Gibbs walked across the captured ground the next day, past Wytschaete Wood to the ruins of the Hospice on the ridge. "I walked to the edge of the mine-craters," he wrote, "and stared into their great gulfs, wondering how many German bodies had been engulfed there" (126-004). The craters, some of which remain visible to this day, were immense cavities blasted out of the earth.

The mining operations that made the battle possible had been conducted with extraordinary courage and engineering skill over more than a year. Gibbs described the grim struggle underground: "They mined beneath this place and it blew up with a roaring blast which flung up tons of soil in a black mass" (073-015).

## Aftermath

The battle achieved its objectives within days. The Messines-Wytschaete Ridge was fully in British hands, and the German positions that had dominated the Ypres Salient since 1914 were gone. British casualties were approximately 25,000 — heavy by any measure, but remarkably light for a major First World War offensive. The success demonstrated the effectiveness of mining warfare, meticulous planning, and the "bite and hold" tactical doctrine associated with Plumer. Gibbs noted that the battle "was the beginning of a vastly ambitious scheme to capture the whole line of ridges through Flanders, of which this was the southern hook" (126-001).

Tragically, the success of Messines was not repeated. The subsequent Flanders offensive — the Third Battle of Ypres, or Passchendaele — that began on July 31 bogged down in mud and blood. Gibbs observed that "even the battles of Flanders in the autumn of '17, ghastly as they were in the losses of our men in the state of the ground through which they had to fight, and in futile results, were well organized by the Second Army headquarters" (022-001).

The battle also had a poignant human cost. Among the dead was Major Willie Redmond, an Irish nationalist Member of Parliament serving with the Royal Irish Regiment, "whose death in Wytschaete Wood was heard with grief in Ireland" (126-004).

## In the Memoir

Messines occupies a special place in Gibbs's narrative, representing what was possible when planning, method, and attention to detail replaced the wasteful improvisation that characterized earlier battles. His vivid description of the mine explosions is among the memoir's most dramatic passages. Gibbs's admiration for Plumer's Second Army is unmistakable, and he explicitly contrasts the Messines operation with "the abominable mismanagement of other troops" (022-001). The battle serves in the memoir as proof that even in the ghastliest of wars, competence could triumph over chaos.
