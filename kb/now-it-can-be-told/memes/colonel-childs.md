## Overview

Colonel Childs was a British staff officer who served as Deputy Assistant Judge Advocate at General Headquarters in the early months of the First World War. He appears in Philip Gibbs's memoir through a single, memorable anecdote from the retreat from Mons, in which his alertness to an ominous silence saved the adjutant-general and other staff officers from being abandoned to the advancing German forces (018-001–018-017).

## Role in the War

Childs served at G.H.Q. under Adjutant-General Sir Neville Macready during the period when Sir John French commanded the British Expeditionary Force. As Deputy Assistant Judge Advocate, he worked in the legal and administrative branch of the headquarters staff — a role removed from the trenches but, as the retreat from Mons demonstrated, not without its own dangers.

The defining episode of Childs's war occurred during the retreat from Mons, when G.H.Q. was in constant motion ahead of the German advance. Gibbs describes the retreat not as the "orderly" affair of official accounts, but as at times "a wild stampede from the tightening loop of a German advance, with lorries and motorcycles and transport wagons going helter-skelter among civilian refugees" (018-001). One night, Macready, the judge advocate, and Childs billeted in a French château near Creil, while the Commander-in-Chief was quartered some distance away.

As Childs worked opposite the adjutant-general, an unusual quiet settled over the village. Twice he remarked on the silence; twice Macready brushed him off — "So much the better. Get on with your job" and "Well, don't go making a noise" (018-004, 018-008). Something in that silence "scared him," and Childs decided to investigate. Walking toward Sir John French's quarters, he found no sentry and, inside the château, only a soldier licking out a jam-pot who informed him: the Commander-in-Chief had been "gone hours ago" and the Germans "ought to be here by now" (018-011–018-013).

Childs hurried back and reported to Macready: "General, the whole box of tricks has gone. We've been left behind. Forgotten!" There was barely time to pack. The party — Macready, the judge advocate, Childs, and one or two orderlies — crammed into a single car with a single rifle. They ran the gauntlet of German patrols, were "sniped all the way through a wood," and took "flying shots at moving figures" before finally locating G.H.Q. miles away (018-017). Macready later recounted the tale with characteristic irony: "And weren't they sorry to see me again! They thought they had lost me forever" (018-018).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs introduces Childs in a single parenthetical phrase — "a hotheaded fellow!" — but the narrative quickly complicates this characterization. Childs's "hotheadedness" proves to be vigilance bordering on prescience: twice ignored, he insisted on investigating the silence, and his insistence saved the lives or liberty of the adjutant-general's entire party.

The anecdote serves a dual function in Gibbs's memoir. On the surface, it is one of Macready's "innumerable anecdotes" — a comic tale of staff-officer incompetence and narrow escape, capped by Macready's wry punchline. At a deeper level, it exposes the chaos behind the official narrative of an "orderly retreat." The fact that G.H.Q. could simply forget an entire branch of its own staff — including the adjutant-general — and drive off without them reveals the disorganization that attended the BEF's first encounter with German mobile warfare.
