## Overview

General Erich Ludendorff, as First Quartermaster General under [[Paul von Hindenburg|Hindenburg]], was the operational brain of the German army from 1916 until his dismissal in October 1918. [[Philip Gibbs]]'s memoir reserves some of its harshest language for Ludendorff, whose post-war *Memoirs* revealed a chilling calculus of human sacrifice that the author found "more horrible" than anything else he had read about the war.

## Biography

Erich Ludendorff was born in Kruszewnia, Prussia in 1865. A brilliant staff officer, he distinguished himself early in the war at the capture of Liège (1914) and subsequently became [[Paul von Hindenburg|Hindenburg]]'s chief of staff on the Eastern Front, where they won the decisive victory at Tannenberg. In August 1916, with Hindenburg's elevation to Chief of the General Staff, Ludendorff was appointed First Quartermaster General, making him the effective operational commander of all German forces. He directed the 1918 Spring Offensive and was dismissed in October 1918 as defeat loomed. He died in 1937.

## Role in the War

Ludendorff directed German strategy during the war's most critical phase. He oversaw the withdrawal to the Hindenburg Line, the unrestricted submarine warfare campaign, and the transfer of Lenin to Russia. His most ambitious gamble was the 1918 Spring Offensive — a series of massive attacks beginning in March 1918 designed to win the war before American forces arrived in strength.

Gibbs quotes Ludendorff's own accounting of British losses: "our losses of three to one (as Ludendorff reckons them, and as many of us guessed) were in our favor, because we could afford the difference of exchange" (123-002). The German command coldly calculated that killing three Allied soldiers for every German loss was a winning ratio.

Ludendorff himself later admitted the severity of British blows. Gibbs records: "Ludendorff admits the severity of that defeat" after the [[Battle of Arras]] in April 1917 (125-004), and "Ludendorff admits the severity of the blow" of the [[Battle of Messines]] (126-005). Regarding American entry: "Ludendorff has described the German agony of mind after the United States had entered the war" (127-017).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's moral outrage at Ludendorff is unmistakable. Having read Ludendorff's post-war memoirs, he writes: "I have read nothing more horrible than the cold-blooded cruelty of Ludendorff's *Memoirs*, in which, without any attempt at self-excuse, he reveals himself as using the lives of millions of men upon a gambling chance of victory with the hazards weighted against him, as he admits" (123-005).

Yet Gibbs acknowledges Ludendorff's professional competence. The scorched-earth retreat to the Hindenburg Line was, as Gibbs quotes: "Ludendorff claims, quite justly, that it was in accordance with the laws of war" (124-001). The memoir captures the tragedy of a brilliant military mind serving a cause that consumed millions: "Ludendorff played his trump cards and they failed" (130-015), and ultimately "Ludendorff's great offensive had failed" (131-001).
