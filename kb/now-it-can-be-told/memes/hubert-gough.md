## Overview

General Sir Hubert Gough commanded the British Fifth Army during some of the war's most desperate fighting. Gibbs interviewed him personally and recorded his grim assessment of the German Spring Offensive. Gough appears in the memoir as a courteous and dutiful commander who was, in Gibbs's words, "badly served" — a figure whose professional competence was overwhelmed by circumstances and the sheer weight of the German assault.

## Biography

Hubert de la Poer Gough was born in London in 1870 into a distinguished Anglo-Irish military family. He served in the Boer War and commanded cavalry formations in the early years of the Great War. Promoted rapidly, he took command of the Fifth Army when it was formed in 1916. He led it through Passchendaele (Third Ypres) and the German Spring Offensive of March 1918, after which he was relieved of command — widely regarded as a scapegoat for failures of the High Command. He lived to be 93, dying in 1963 as one of the last surviving senior British commanders of the war.

## Role in the War

Gough's Fifth Army held the southern sector of the British line when Ludendorff launched Operation Michael on March 21, 1918. The German attack, delivered by overwhelming forces, shattered the Fifth Army's positions. Gibbs writes: "the Fifth Army, under General Gough, was entrusted with the defense of the southern sector of the British line" (130-001), and it was there that the storm broke.

The situation was dire. Gibbs records his personal meeting with Gough: "I had an interview with General Gough, commanding the Fifth Army. He was confident that his men would hold, but the odds against them were overwhelming" (130-016). What Gough told him "staggered my imagination" — the general "had suggested terrible possibilities" (130-019) that would soon become reality.

Gibbs's assessment of Gough's character is measured but sympathetic. He described him as "extremely courteous, of most amiable character, with a high sense of duty" but concluded that he "was badly served" by subordinates and circumstances (127-009).

## In the Memoir

Gough is one of the few senior commanders whom Gibbs depicts with a degree of personal warmth. Their interview revealed a general who understood the gravity of his position and did not hide it. Gibbs's treatment of Gough contrasts with his more generalized criticism of "G.H.Q." — Gough emerges as a man of character caught in an impossible situation, not a remote staff officer indifferent to the suffering of his troops.

The Fifth Army's ordeal in March 1918 is one of the memoir's most dramatic sequences. Gibbs witnessed the human cost of the retreat and understood that Gough, like many British commanders, was paying for decisions made far from the battlefield.
