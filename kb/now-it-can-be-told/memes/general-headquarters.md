## Overview

General Headquarters — universally known as G.H.Q. — was the supreme command center of the [[British Expeditionary Force]] on the [[Western Front]]. From its château near [[Montreuilsurmer|Montreuil]], it directed the operations of British and Empire forces across France and [[Flanders]]. In Philip Gibbs's memoir, G.H.Q. appears as an almost mythic institution: distant, secretive, staffed by "mysterious people in a mysterious town" (004-009), and the target of bitter resentment from frontline soldiers who believed its staff lived in comfort while men died in the trenches.

## Organization

G.H.Q. was organized into specialized branches — Operations, Intelligence, Signals, Quartermaster-General, Adjutant-General — each occupying separate buildings across Montreuil and its surroundings. "Other branches of G.H.Q. were billeted in private houses in the town, and all day long, from early morning to late at night, motor-cars and motor-cycles, with loud, strident hooters and clattering exhaust-pipes, dashed through the narrow streets" (018-001). The staff wore the distinctive "blue-and-red armlet of G.H.Q." which gave them privileged status behind the lines (011-002). Gibbs notes the social hierarchy within: "The smart society of G.H.Q. was best seen at the Officers' Club, where youthful 'Staff Captains' and 'G.S.O.3's' dined with elderly colonels and generals" (011-006).

## Role in the War

G.H.Q. directed British military operations on the Western Front from 1914 to 1919. Under [[Sir John French]] and then [[Douglas Haig]], it planned the major offensives — [[Battle of Loos|Loos]], the [[Battle of the Somme|Somme]], [[Battle of Arras|Arras]], [[Battle of Passchendaele|Passchendaele]] — and managed the vast logistical apparatus that sustained the [[British Expeditionary Force]]. Its Intelligence branch assessed German capabilities; its Operations branch issued the fateful orders that sent divisions into battle.

The headquarters had a reputation for being out of touch with trench reality. Gibbs records a visiting officer's devastating critique: "G.H.Q. was a close corporation in this war... They have no idea of the real conditions in which we live... We are expected to die if G.H.Q. tells us so" (069-003). When a staff officer defended the High Command, another guest retorted: "Then God help our British G.H.Q.!" (070-016).

During the 1918 German Spring Offensive, G.H.Q. itself was threatened: "G.H.Q. has got the windup" (130-007), and the staff prepared for the possibility of evacuation. The crisis passed, and G.H.Q. directed the final Allied advances that ended the war.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's relationship with G.H.Q. was complex and personal. As a war correspondent, he was subject to its [[censorship]] regime: "Censorship under the orders of G.H.Q." (113-001). He visited Montreuil many times, observing the "military inhabitants of G.H.Q." who were "great and glorious soldiers" yet also "sheltered from the dirty business of war" (011-004). He recorded the frontline soldiers' "deadly hatred of G.H.Q." — a resentment born of the gulf between those who gave the orders and those who died carrying them out (010-002).

The memoir captures the surreal contrast between G.H.Q.'s world and the trenches: casualties "were reported back to G.H.Q. — where good fires were burning" (060-013). Despite his criticisms, Gibbs also conveyed the professional respect that G.H.Q.'s planning deserved, particularly during the 1918 crisis when "G.H.Q. was convinced" of the German offensive and prepared accordingly (130-008).
