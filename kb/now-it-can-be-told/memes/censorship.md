## Overview

Censorship was the invisible framework within which all war correspondence operated on the Western Front. Military authorities controlled what journalists could see, where they could go, and what they could publish. Philip Gibbs, as one of the five accredited British war correspondents, worked under this system throughout the war. His memoir, published after the Armistice, is in part an act of retrospective honesty — telling what censorship had prevented him from saying at the time.

## Definition

Military press censorship involved prior restraint on journalists' dispatches, restrictions on naming units and locations, prohibitions on reporting casualties, and a general ban on "criticism, protest, and of the figures of loss" (008-009). Correspondents were escorted by military "conducting officers" who controlled their movements and access to the front.

## In the War

Gibbs describes the censorship regime as evolving over the course of the war. "After early, hostile days it allowed nearly all but criticism, protest, and of the figures of loss" (008-009). The system was designed to maintain civilian morale by concealing the true scale of casualties and the often disastrous tactical decisions of commanders.

The strain of working under censorship was considerable. Gibbs describes his colleague Percival Phillips as turning "pink-and-white under the hideous strain of nervous control, with an hour and a half for two columns in The Morning Post" (008-009). The correspondents' dispatches had to be both truthful enough to maintain credibility and circumspect enough to pass the censor's pen.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's memoir is explicit about the constraints censorship placed on his wartime writing. "My duty, then, was that of a chronicler, not arguing why things should have happened so nor giving reasons why they should not happen so, but describing faithfully many of the things I saw, and narrating the facts as I found them, as far as the censorship would allow" (008-009). The phrase "as far as the censorship would allow" is the key admission — the published record was incomplete by design.

The very existence of this memoir, titled *Now It Can Be Told*, is a declaration that the censorship era has ended and the full truth can finally be revealed. Gibbs frames his book as getting "deeper into the truth of this war and of all war" (008-009), beyond what wartime dispatches could convey.
