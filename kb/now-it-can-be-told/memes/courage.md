## Overview

Courage is the central moral quality that Philip Gibbs sought to document and understand in his war memoir. Not the romanticized courage of patriotic propaganda, but the unglamorous, often bitter endurance of ordinary men under conditions of industrialized slaughter. Gibbs distinguishes between the physical courage of the battlefield, the moral courage to question authority, and the quiet courage of simply carrying on (062-059).

## Definition

In the context of Gibbs's memoir, courage is not a single virtue but a spectrum of human responses to mortal danger. It includes the instinctive bravery of the soldier going "over the top," the calculated risk-taking of trench raiders, the steady endurance of men under prolonged bombardment, and the moral courage of those who questioned the war's conduct. Gibbs is particularly interested in the relationship between courage and its opposites — fear, boredom, despair — and how ordinary men managed the psychological burden of daily exposure to death (041-001).

## In the War

The war demanded courage on an industrial scale. Gibbs records that the 12th Division's honors in months of trench warfare — "one V.C., sixteen D.S.O.'s, forty-five Military Crosses, thirty-four Military Medals" — "were won by the loss in casualties of more than fourteen thousand men" (062-059). The decorations and the casualties were two sides of the same coin: "It was valor that was paid for by flesh and blood" (062-059).

Not all courage was recognized by medals. Gibbs describes the Bantam battalions of undersized men, "stunted men" who "cursed the war with the foulest curses" yet held their ground under "damnable fire, until many of them were blown to bits" (114-005). There was "no 'glory' in their job, only filth and blood" (114-005). The jester among them, a "five-foot hero," declared with "ironical laughter, outrageous blasphemy, grotesque imagery" that "there will be a lot of murder after this bloody war" — a testament to how courage and despair coexisted in the same soul (114-005).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's treatment of courage is deliberately anti-heroic. He was present at the war as a chronicler, not a combatant, and he describes the courage of war correspondents under fire — Percival Phillips "turned pink-and-white under the hideous strain of nervous control" while writing his columns (008-009). Gibbs himself sought cover when he "heard a shrill 'coo-ee!' from a shell" (030-023). This honesty about his own fear gives his observations on soldiers' courage their moral authority.

The memoir also records the perversion of courage into brutality. Colonel Ronald Campbell — "'O.C. Bayonets'" — was tasked with inspiring "bloodlust in the brains of gentle boys who instinctively disliked butcher's work" (141-003). The armies established "an intensive culture of brutality" that transformed natural courage into trained killing instinct, a process Gibbs views with deep ambivalence (141-003).
