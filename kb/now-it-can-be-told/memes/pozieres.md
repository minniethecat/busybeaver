## Overview

Pozières was a small farming village on the Albert–Bapaume road that became, during the summer of 1916, the scene of one of the most sustained and costly battles fought by Australian forces on the Western Front. Standing on the highest point of the Somme ridge, the village and its windmill were fought over for six weeks with a ferocity that Gibbs called "the last word in frightfulness" (116-002). The Australians suffered 23,000 casualties in the struggle, and the finely pulverized earth of the ridge became an "Australian graveyard" (116-004), now commemorated by the Pozières Memorial and the site of the Windmill.

## Geography

Pozières lies on the Albert–Bapaume road, approximately six kilometers northeast of Albert. The village occupies a crest of the Somme plateau at roughly 150 meters elevation, commanding views in all directions. To the northwest lay Mouquet Farm ("Mucky Farm" to the troops), a German stronghold built around deep tunnels. The highest point was marked by the Pozières Windmill, a mound that became, in Gibbs's words, "the high ground of Pozières, for which there was unceasing slaughter on both sides" (116-001). The German defensive position known as the "Leipzig Salient" lay to the south. The soil, chalk overlaid with clay, was reduced by continuous shelling to a fine white dust that choked men and obliterated landmarks.

## Role in the War

The Battle of Pozières began on July 23, 1916, when the 1st Australian Division launched its first major attack on the Western Front. The Australians captured the village in fierce fighting, then held it against repeated German counterattacks. Gibbs describes the six-week ordeal: "The struggle for the Pozières ridge and High Wood lasted from the beginning of August until the middle of September — six weeks of fighting as desperate as any in the history of the world until that time" (116-001).

The Germans, recognizing the tactical importance of the high ground, "counterattacked again and again, and waves of men surged up and fell around that mound of forsaken brick, which I saw as a reddish cone through flame and smoke" (116-001). The intensity of the shellfire was unprecedented. "The intensity of the shellfire under which they lay shook them, if it did not kill them. Many of their wounded told me that it had broken their nerve. They would never fight again without a sense of horror" (116-002).

By the time the fighting moved on in September, the Australians had suffered twenty thousand casualties in six weeks. The German 15th Reserve Infantry Regiment, sent to reinforce the Leipzig salient, "had suffered casualties to the extent of over 50 percent west of Pozières during the middle of July, and showed no eagerness to return to the fight" (118-044).

In March 1918, during the German Spring Offensive, Gibbs returned to Pozières to witness its loss: "on a dreadful day by the windmill at Pozières, our old heroic fighting-ground, where once again after many battles the enemy was in Courcelette and High Wood and Delville Wood" (130-023).

## In the Memoir

Pozières appears in Gibbs's narrative as a place of extremes: extreme courage, extreme suffering, and extreme destruction. He records the Australian officers' own astonishment at their ordeal: "Those Australians whom I had seen arrive in France had proved their quality. They had come believing that nothing could be worse than their ordeal in the Dardanelles. Now they knew that Pozières was the last word in frightfulness" (116-002).

The physical devastation is rendered in unforgettable imagery. "They fought stubbornly, grimly, in ground so ravaged with fire that the earth was finely powdered" (116-004). The Australian officer who observed, "Our men are more highly strung than the English," surprised Gibbs: "I was astonished to hear these words, because those Australians seemed to me without nerves, and as tough as gristle in their fiber" (116-003). Yet even they were broken by the shellfire.

Gibbs also records a more intimate moment connected to Pozières. In Amiens, he helped a drunken young Australian officer searching for his missing horse: "How can I get back to Pozières?" the boy asked (090-043). The episode, in its mingling of farce and pathos, captures the precarious humanity of the men who fought and died on the ridge. "I wonder if he died at Pozières, or farther on by the Butte de Warlencourt" (090-052), Gibbs reflects, letting the question hang.
