## Overview

The Armistice of November 11, 1918, ended four years of slaughter on the Western Front. Signed in a railway carriage in the Forest of Compiegne at 5:00 a.m., it came into effect at 11:00 a.m. — the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month. For Philip Gibbs, who had spent nearly the entire war at the front, the moment was not the triumphant celebration his readers at home imagined. It was, instead, a strange and haunted silence. "After November 11th, when the armistice brought us to a halt" (131-010), Gibbs writes, the guns simply stopped.

## Background

By late 1918, Germany's military position had become untenable. The Allied counter-offensive of the Hundred Days had shattered the Hindenburg Line. Bulgaria had surrendered in September, Turkey in October, and Austria-Hungary on November 3. Germany's allies were gone, its army was in retreat, and revolution was spreading through its cities. On November 9, Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicated and fled to Holland. A German delegation, led by the Catholic politician Matthias Erzberger, crossed the lines to meet Marshal Foch.

## The Event

In the predawn darkness of November 11, 1918, the German delegation signed the armistice terms in Foch's railway carriage at Compiegne. The terms were harsh: Germany must evacuate all occupied territory within fifteen days, surrender vast quantities of war materiel, and accept Allied occupation of the Rhineland. Hostilities would cease at 11:00 a.m.

Gibbs records that men were still dying in those final hours: "at Maubeuge on the day before the armistice I saw the last dead German" (131-006). Units that had survived four years of war perished in the last minutes before eleven o'clock. "Close calls of death before the armistice" (040-004) haunted those who had survived. At the appointed hour, the guns fell silent. Gibbs notes the quiet was almost unbearable to men who had learned to live within the rhythm of shellfire.

## Aftermath

The Armistice brought not peace but a prolonged state of negotiation and blockade. Germany remained under the threat of resumed hostilities until the Treaty of Versailles was signed in June 1919. The Allied blockade continued, contributing to widespread hunger and bitterness in Germany. For the victorious armies, the aftermath brought a slow, disorganized demobilization that left many soldiers feeling abandoned. Gibbs accompanied the British Army of Occupation into Germany: "after the armistice I went with our troops to the Rhine" (131-010). The peace he found there felt fragile and incomplete.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's treatment of the Armistice is characteristically ambivalent. He records no cheering crowds, no flag-waving celebration. Instead, he describes the strange silence, the anticlimax, the men who had survived years of slaughter only to die in the last hours. "They had peace long before the armistice" (042-008) — Gibbs applies this phrase to the dead, but the living too had been permanently changed. His march to the Rhine, undertaken days after the ceasefire, reads not as a victory parade but as a journey into a broken country.
