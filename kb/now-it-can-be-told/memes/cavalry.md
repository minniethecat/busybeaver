## Overview

The [[cavalry]] represented the martial tradition of mobility, dash, and decisive charges that defined nineteenth-century warfare. On the [[Western Front]] of 1914–1918, however, the cavalry found itself "bunkered" — rendered largely useless by [[trench-warfare|trenches]], [[barbed-wire]], and machine guns. Gibbs's memoir captures the cavalryman's despair at technological obsolescence and his struggle to find a role in modern industrialized war (076-001).

## Definition

Cavalry are mounted soldiers trained to fight from horseback. In the British Army of the First World War, cavalry regiments retained their traditional arms — sabers and lances — along with carbines. Cavalry divisions were held in reserve behind the front, waiting for the "breakthrough" that would allow them to exploit gaps in the enemy line and pursue retreating forces (044-002).

## In the War

The war's opening months in 1914 saw the last large-scale cavalry operations on the Western Front. Once the trenches were dug, cavalrymen spent most of the war waiting behind the lines. Gibbs recorded the cavalry's bitter realization: "in that winter of discontent there was one great body of splendid men whose spirits had sunk to zero, seeing no hope ahead of them in that warfare of trenches and barbed wire" (076-001). Cavalry officers believed they were "bunkered forever, and that all their training and tradition were made futile by the digging in of armies" (076-001).

When opportunities did arise, cavalry were used in dismounted roles. At the [[Battle of the Somme|Somme]], dismounted cavalry under Brigadier-General Seely fought in the line (095-002). The cavalry also provided the army's power structure — Gibbs notes that "the cavalry, above all, held the power of high appointment" in the British military hierarchy (024-004).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs visited cavalry officers in their mess and recorded conversations that revealed a profound disillusionment. "I am all for revolution after the war," one cavalry officer told him — "strange words to hear in a cavalry mess!" (076-021). The cavalry's predicament became for Gibbs a symbol of the war's destruction of old certainties and traditions. Yet the cavalry still made its contribution: "So the cavalry did its 'bit' again, though not with horses" (076-014).
