## Overview

Field Marshal Sir Douglas Haig succeeded Sir John French as Commander-in-Chief of the British Expeditionary Force in December 1915 and held that command through the war's most costly battles — the Somme, Arras, Passchendaele, and the final Hundred Days offensive. Gibbs's memoir captures the soldiers' complex attitudes toward Haig and the high command, reflecting the tension between strategic necessity and the staggering human cost.

## Biography

Douglas Haig was born in Edinburgh in 1861. A cavalry officer like French, he served in the Sudan and the Boer War before holding key staff appointments. He played a central role in the creation of the British Expeditionary Force and commanded I Corps in 1914. After French's removal, Haig assumed command with the support of King George V.

## Role in the War

Haig presided over the vast expansion of the BEF from a small professional force into a mass citizen army. The Somme offensive of 1916, the largest battle in British military history, was fought under his direction. Gibbs records the soldiers' hope that the Somme would be the last great battle: "The British, stronger than they had ever been, in men, and guns, and shells, and aircraft... were going to be launched in a great offensive. No more trench warfare. No more dying in ditches" (078-002).

The reality was different. The Somme produced over 400,000 British casualties. Gibbs's account of the tactical failures of 1915 at Neuve Chapelle, Festubert, and Loos — where "both generalship and staff-work were, in the opinion of most officers who know anything of those battles, ghastly" (016-014) — reflects a wider critique of the high command's learning curve.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs does not name Haig frequently, but his criticism of "G.H.Q." — the General Headquarters — runs through the memoir. He contrasts the staff officers "where good fires were burning under dry roofs" with the soldiers in the line (060-013). The memoir's treatment of the high command is one of its most politically sensitive dimensions: Gibbs could not criticize commanders openly during the war, and the book represents his post-censorship reckoning.
