## Overview

General Sir Aylmer Haldane was one of the British commanders whom Philip Gibbs knew personally and admired. A former war correspondent himself before his military career, Haldane welcomed journalists to his headquarters — a rare courtesy in an army where the staff often treated correspondents with suspicion. Gibbs's memoir preserves several vivid memories of Haldane: his grief over a promising young officer's death, his fury at a lost position, and his quiet kindness in warning Gibbs of the bad news about to fall on his men.

## Biography

James Aylmer Lowthorpe Haldane was born in 1862 into a Scottish military family. He served in the Anglo-Egyptian War, the Boer War, and the Boxer Rebellion. Before his military career, he had experience as a war correspondent — a background that made him uniquely sympathetic to Gibbs and his colleagues. During the Great War, he commanded the 3rd Division from 1914 and later took command of VI Corps. After the war he served in India and retired as a full General. He died in 1950 at the age of 87.

## Role in the War

Haldane commanded the 3rd Division through some of the war's fiercest fighting on the Western Front, including the battles of the Somme and Arras. Under his command, the division's men — Suffolks, King's Own Liverpools, Gordons, and Royal Scots — held ground they knew intimately. When a relief division lost a position of "local importance" through inexperience, Gibbs records Haldane's reaction: "General Haldane was annoyed. It was ground of which he knew every inch. It was ground which men of his had died to hold" (074-002).

His command was not without tragedy. Gibbs describes how Haldane had identified a young officer as "the most promising young soldier in the whole army" — "wonderfully handsome, elegant even when covered from head to foot in wet mud, fearless, or at least scornful of danger, to the verge of recklessness" (017-002). When a shell killed that officer, the general "was saddened more than by the death of other gallant officers."

## In the Memoir

Haldane appears in Gibbs's memoir as an unusually human commander. Gibbs writes directly: "General Haldane was friendly to the war correspondents — he had been something of the kind himself in earlier days — and we were welcomed at his headquarters" (017-001). This friendship gave Gibbs access to moments of command-pathos rarely captured in war memoirs.

The most poignant Haldane memory concerns a boxing match Gibbs attended at the general's invitation. The men of the 3rd Division, enjoying a day of sports, were about to receive devastating orders: "General Haldane had given me a quiet word about it, and I watched the boxing, and the faces of all those men, crowded round the ring, with pity for the frightful disappointment that was about to fall on them, like a sledgehammer" (074-003). This scene — the general quietly warning the correspondent, the men cheering unaware — captures the terrible intimacy of command in the Great War.
