## Overview

Amiens, the capital of the Somme department and the historic capital of Picardy, was the chief city behind the British sector of the Western Front during the great battles of 1916. A Roman settlement (Samarobriva) and medieval cathedral city, Amiens possessed good railway connections, a navigable river, and a large civilian hospital infrastructure that made it ideally suited to serve as a military hub. During the war it became the rest-and-recuperation destination for British troops rotating out of the Somme trenches, the administrative center for the BEF's logistical apparatus, and the site of numerous base hospitals. Gibbs devotes an entire section of his memoir to the city, observing that "during the battles of the Somme in 1916, and afterward in periods of progress and retreat, the city of Amiens was the capital of the British armies" (079-002).

## Geography

Amiens sits at the confluence of the Somme River and its tributary the Avre, approximately 120 km north of Paris and 50 km inland from the English Channel. The terrain is the rolling chalk plateau of Picardy, cut by the wide, marshy valley of the Somme. The city lies on the great trunk railway line between Paris and Calais — the line that carried the bulk of British reinforcements and supplies to the front. To its east, the ground rises gently toward the Santerre plateau, beyond which lay the 1916 battlefields of the Somme and the old front line. To the west, the valley opens toward Abbeville and the sea. The network of roads radiating from Amiens to Albert, Bapaume, Péronne, and Montdidier made it the natural transport node for the entire central sector of the British front.

## Role in the War

Amiens was never a front-line city in the sense that Ypres or Arras were, but its strategic importance was immense. It served as the headquarters of the French Grand Quartier Général (GQG) from late 1914, and later became the pivot of British logistics in the Somme region. During the 1916 Somme offensive, Amiens's railway station handled enormous volumes of troop movements, ammunition supply, and medical evacuation trains. The city's hospitals — the Hôtel-Dieu, the civilian hospital, and numerous converted schools and hotels — treated tens of thousands of wounded. Gibbs describes "long convoys of ambulances" passing through the streets, carrying "men who had been smashed and broken in the Somme fighting" (002-008).

The most dangerous moment for Amiens came in March 1918, during the German Spring Offensive. The German advance pushed to within 20 km of the city, and long-range railway guns — including the famous "Paris Gun" type — shelled the outskirts. Gibbs records that the city was "under shellfire" and that civilians were evacuated en masse. The critical Allied defensive stand at Villers-Bretonneux on April 24, 1918 — fought largely by Australian troops — prevented the Germans from capturing Amiens. Had the city fallen, the entire British line in the Somme sector might have collapsed. On August 8, 1918, the city served as the launching point for the Battle of Amiens, the opening phase of the Hundred Days Offensive that would end the war.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs spent considerable time in Amiens throughout the war, and his memoir paints a vivid portrait of a city transformed by its role behind the lines. The "Street of the Three Pebbles" — the rue des Trois Cailloux — was, for Gibbs, the central artery of the wartime city: "the crowded highway" where "British staff officers, French poilus, and civilians jostled in a cosmopolitan throng" (079-002). He describes the estaminets and cafés where soldiers and war correspondents congregated, the "old château on the Amiens–Albert road" used as a headquarters (019-005), and the quieter precincts around the Cathedral — a 13th-century Gothic masterpiece of such height that its nave could accommodate the tallest of war machines.

Gibbs renders the human dimension of the city with particular care: a French poilu on leave, "grizzled and weather-beaten," sharing a glass of wine; "Australians slouching up the street with a grim look under their wide-brimmed hats"; and the constant procession of the wounded. He also captures the city's resilience. Despite the shelling of 1918, Amiens survived the war largely intact, its Cathedral and its medieval quarters spared the total destruction that befell Ypres and Arras. "At Flixecourt, near Amiens," Gibbs recalls a moment of almost painful normality — a Sunday scene of French families walking by the river, a reminder of the world that had been suspended (033-001).
