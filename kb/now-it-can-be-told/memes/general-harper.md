## Overview

Major-General George Montague Harper commanded the 51st (Highland) Division through some of the hardest fighting on the Western Front, including the Somme and the Battle of Arras, and later rose to command IV Corps. Gibbs's memoir captures him as a divisional commander who earned the affection of his kilted soldiers — they called him "Uncle" — and who was willing to speak candidly about the human cost of operations that official communiqués portrayed in purely optimistic terms (127-006).

## Role in the War

Harper was one of several divisional generals Gibbs encountered regularly at the front, alongside Hull of the 56th (London) Division, Hickey of the 16th (Irish), Nugent of the 36th (Ulster), and Pinnie of the 35th (Bantams) Division (020-015). His 51st Division was among the most distinguished Scottish formations in the BEF. Gibbs records the division's fearsome reputation:

> "The Highland division, under his command, fought many battles and gained great honor, even from the enemy, who feared them and called the kilted men 'the ladies from hell.'"
> — [020-020]

The Germans acknowledged the 51st's tenacity with a grudging salute: during the retreat from the Somme, they sent a message by balloon — "Poor old 51st. Still sticking it!" (020-020).

At the Battle of Arras, which opened on Easter Sunday, April 9th, 1917, in a gale of sleet and snow, Harper's Highlanders fought alongside the Canadian Corps in the capture of Vimy Ridge:

> "The Vimy Ridge was captured from end to end by the Canadians on the left and the 51st Division of Highlanders on the right. By the afternoon the entire living German population, more than seven thousand in the tunnels of Vimy, were down below in the valley on our side of the lines."
> — [125-003]

Gibbs notes that the Highlanders "had done as well as the Canadians, though they had less honor, and took as many prisoners." The divisional sign "H.D." — stenciled on ruined walls across the front — had once been mocked as "Harper's Duds," but after Vimy, a Scottish sergeant told the general: "Well, General, they don't call us Harper's Duds any more!" (125-003).

Harper's candor set him apart from many commanders Gibbs interviewed. In a conversation recorded late in the war, Harper "protested that there was not enough liaison between the fighting-officers and the Higher Command, and could not blame them for their hatred of 'the Staff'" (127-006). He acknowledged that his own brigades had suffered staggering losses — "one of my brigades has lost eighty-seven officers and two thousand men since the spring" — and challenged the language of official reporting:

> "We tell the public that an enemy division has been 'shattered.' That is true. But so is mine."
> — [127-006]

## In the Memoir

Harper appears in Gibbs's narrative as an exemplar of the competent, plain-spoken divisional commander — respected by his men, clear-eyed about the cost of the war, and skeptical of the polished narratives produced by headquarters and the press. Gibbs, whose own dispatches were "accused of being black in pessimism," did not tell Harper that his articles shared the general's misgivings about "glowing optimism" (127-006).

The two Harper episodes also illustrate the memoir's broader structural technique: the brisk, affectionate sketch of the general and his "boys" (020-020) is followed, hundreds of pages later, by the same general's sober reckoning with what those "boys" had endured (127-006). The cumulative effect tracks the arc of the war itself — from early élan through grinding attrition to exhausted honesty.
