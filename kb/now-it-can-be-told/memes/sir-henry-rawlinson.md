## Overview

General Sir Henry Rawlinson was a senior British Army officer who commanded the Fourth Army during the Battle of the Somme and later led the breakthrough offensive of 1918. In Philip Gibbs's *Now It Can Be Told*, he appears as a central figure of the High Command — portrayed with a mixture of affection for his jovial personality and quiet criticism of the staggering human cost of his attritional strategy on the Somme (019-006).

## Biography

Henry Seymour Rawlinson was born in 1864 into a military family; his father, Sir Henry Creswicke Rawlinson, was a distinguished Orientalist and army officer. The younger Rawlinson was educated at Eton and Sandhurst before joining the King's Royal Rifle Corps. He served in the Burma Expedition (1886–87), the Sudan Campaign (1898), and the Second Boer War (1899–1902), steadily rising through the ranks. At the outbreak of the First World War he commanded the 7th Division, leading it with distinction in the early open-warfare phase before the Western Front solidified into trench stalemate. He was created Baron Rawlinson of Trent in 1919 and died in 1925, having served briefly as Commander-in-Chief in India.

## Role in the War

Rawlinson's most consequential command was the British Fourth Army, which he organized in early 1916 for the forthcoming Somme offensive. The army was designated — with what Gibbs calls "unconscious irony" — the "Army of Pursuit," though the Somme battles would prove to be "a siege rather than a pursuit" (019-001).

His headquarters at Querrieux, on the Amiens–Albert road, was a château surrounded by pleasant fields — a detail that Gibbs notes with pointed contrast to the "dreadful fields" where thousands of British soldiers lay dead after the "bloodbath of the Somme" (019-005, 019-006). Gibbs records the "savage irony" of the fighting men who passed such peaceful headquarters knowing their doom was being planned inside.

In the early days of the war, commanding the 7th Division, Rawlinson had "done well" — a "gallant soldier, with initiative and courage of decision and a quick intelligence in open warfare." But Gibbs identifies his central limitation: "his trouble on the Somme was that the enemy did not permit open warfare" (019-006). The German defensive system of interconnected machine-gun fortresses, woods turned into deathtraps, and successive fortified lines back to Bapaume turned Rawlinson's planned breakthrough into a siege measured in yards of blood-soaked ground.

During the planning phase, Rawlinson attempted to requisition the war correspondents' château at Tilques as his own headquarters, provoking a standoff with the press corps. Gibbs records the episode with humor, including an accidental informal greeting — "Hullo! You here again?" — that Rawlinson deflected with genial good nature: "Hullo, young feller!" (019-001–019-004).

In the final phase of the war, Rawlinson's Fourth Army at last became a real "Army of Pursuit," breaking through the German lines and driving toward victory. Gibbs credits him for showing "his qualities of generalship and once again that driving purpose" in the open warfare of 1918 — though with the somber addendum that such purpose "was achieved only by prodigious cost of life" on the Somme (019-006).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's portrait of Rawlinson is notably ambivalent. On one hand, he describes a commander of personal charm — "a roguish eye," a habit of speaking of the enemy affectionately as "the old Hun" or "old Fritz," and an "amazing, overweening optimism" that sustained morale at headquarters (019-006). Rawlinson is presented as shrewd in conversation, possibly feigning ignorance of certain map locations as "camouflage" to avoid revealing tactical knowledge to a war correspondent who might inadvertently pass it to the enemy.

On the other hand, Gibbs aligns his own perspective with that of the common soldier, writing: "As onlookers of sacrifice some of us — I, for one — adopted the point of view of the men who were to die, finding some reason in their hatred of the staffs" (019-006). This dual perspective — personal liking for the general, moral solidarity with his casualties — is characteristic of Gibbs's memoir as a whole.

Elsewhere, Gibbs notes that Rawlinson was "arguing, persuading" among fellow officers (108-002), and records the general's report to the King: "Three shots to one, sir, that's the stuff to give 'em!" (126-010).
