## Overview

The War Office was the British government department in Whitehall responsible for the administration of the Army. For Philip Gibbs and other war correspondents, it was the gatekeeper — the institution that issued or denied military passports, controlled access to the front, and shaped the official narrative of the war. Its "sacred portals" in London represented the civilian bureaucracy that, in Gibbs's view, was often as remote from battlefield reality as [[General Headquarters (G.H.Q.)|G.H.Q.]] itself (140-002).

## Organization

The War Office was a sprawling civil-military bureaucracy headed by the Secretary of State for War — throughout most of the conflict, [[Lord Kitchener]]. It administered recruitment, training, equipment supply, and the movement of troops to the fighting fronts. Its press section controlled war correspondents' credentials: in the early months of the war, "the War Office was not giving any" military passports, effectively banning journalists from the front (002-002). The department also issued official communiqués — Gibbs notes a report "dated September 15, 1915, and issued by the War Office on November 1st of that year" (056-001).

## Role in the War

The War Office's most immediate impact on Gibbs's experience was its control of press access. At the outbreak of war, British journalists were barred from the front — a restriction that provoked fury in [[Fleet Street]] and drove enterprising correspondents to attempt dangerous independent journeys to the war zone (002-004). In 1915, "the War Office at last moved in the matter of correspondents" and Gibbs was among the first five accredited British war correspondents (003-001).

Beyond press control, the War Office oversaw the vast expansion of the British Army from a small professional force to a mass citizen army. It administered the recruitment campaigns that produced [[Lord Kitchener|Kitchener]]'s New Armies, managed the procurement crisis that led to the Shell Scandal of 1915, and coordinated with the Treasury and Foreign Office on war policy. "Lord Kitchener's word" at the War Office carried immense authority throughout the Empire (138-002).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's memoir portrays the War Office as a remote, almost sacred institution — "the holy ground of the Horse Guards, the sacred portals of the War Office" (140-002) — whose decisions shaped the lives and deaths of millions. His personal experience was one of navigating its restrictions: the initial ban on correspondents, the gradual relaxation, and the ongoing [[censorship]] regime that G.H.Q. enforced on the War Office's behalf. The War Office appears less frequently than G.H.Q. in the memoir, but it stands behind the entire system of press control and official narrative that Gibbs chafed against.
