## Overview

Lieutenant-General Sir Cecil Frederick Nevil Macready served as Adjutant-General of the British Expeditionary Force in the opening months of the First World War. Gibbs's memoir devotes an extended passage to Macready — the son of the famous Shakespearean actor William Charles Macready — presenting him as a figure of ironic wit amid the chaos of the retreat from Mons. Through Macready's own anecdotes, retold by Gibbs, the memoir exposes the disorganization behind the official narrative of an "orderly retreat" (018-001–018-018).

## Role in the War

As Adjutant-General under Sir John French, Macready was responsible for the BEF's personnel administration — discipline, promotions, casualties, and the machinery of military justice. It was a headquarters role far from the fighting line, but as the retreat from Mons demonstrated, the line between headquarters and the front could collapse with startling speed.

Gibbs's portrait of Macready begins not with his duties but with his character. The general's parentage — "the son of Macready, the actor" — is presented as source and explanation for his most distinctive quality: "a subtlety of mind not common among British generals, to whom 'subtlety' in any form is repulsive" (018-001). His "sense of humor was developed upon lines of irony and he had a sly twinkle in his eyes before telling one of his innumerable anecdotes" (018-001).

The chief anecdote Gibbs preserves is Macready's account of being abandoned during the retreat. Gibbs sets the scene by undercutting the official version of events:

> "It was not, to tell the truth, that 'orderly' retreat which is described in secondhand accounts. There were times when it was a wild stampede from the tightening loop of a German advance, with lorries and motorcycles and transport wagons going helter-skelter among civilian refugees."
> — [018-001]

One night, Macready, the judge advocate, and Colonel Childs billeted in a French château near Creil, while the Commander-in-Chief was quartered some distance away. Childs twice remarked on an ominous silence; twice Macready dismissed him: "So much the better. Get on with your job" and "Well, don't go making a noise" (018-004, 018-008). But Childs investigated and discovered that Sir John French's entire party had decamped hours earlier — the Germans were expected at any moment. The party crammed into a single car with a single rifle, ran the gauntlet of German patrols, and eventually found G.H.Q. miles away.

Macready's punchline, delivered with the actor's son's sense of timing, closes the episode:

> "And weren't they sorry to see me again! They thought they had lost me forever."
> — [018-018]

The anecdote is also preserved in a fragment that Gibbs quotes directly — Macready's growled reaction to a German atrocity rumor: "The dirty dogs!" (018-016). These small, recorded utterances give Macready a vividness unusual even for the memoir's more sharply-drawn characters.

## In the Memoir

Macready enters Gibbs's narrative as the source of self-deprecating humor — the general who was "forgotten" by his own headquarters — but the anecdote does subtler work. It exposes the fragility of the BEF's command structure in the war's first weeks: even the Adjutant-General could be misplaced in the fog of retreat. And it humanizes the staff officer, presenting Macready not as a bureaucratic functionary but as a man whose wit survived circumstances that might have crushed a less resilient temperament.

The literary quality of the Macready passage — its pacing, its punchline structure, its ear for spoken dialogue — suggests that Gibbs the war correspondent was also Gibbs the novelist (he had published fiction before the war). Macready is less a historical subject than a character in a well-told tale, and the tale's reliability is complicated by its very artfulness. But as evidence of how the BEF's senior officers wanted to be remembered — ironic, unflappable, capable of laughing at their own near-disasters — the Macready anecdotes are invaluable.
