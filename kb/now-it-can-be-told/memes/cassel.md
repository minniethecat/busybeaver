## Overview

Cassel is a small, ancient hill town in French Flanders, perched on a prominent 176-metre hill that overlooks the Flanders plain. Throughout the First World War it served as the headquarters of the British Second Army under General [[Herbert Plumer]], making it one of the most important command centres on the Western Front. Gibbs describes it as "the little old town of Cassel on the hill — where once a Duke of Marlborough and a King of France had kept their headquarters" (128-012), drawing a line from the campaigns of the past to the industrial warfare of the present.

## Geography

Cassel stands on a steep outlier hill (Mont Cassel) in the Nord department of northern France, approximately 18 miles west of [[Ypres]] and 24 miles south of Dunkirk. Its elevation, rising abruptly from the flat Flanders landscape, gives it a commanding view extending across the Ypres Salient to the east, over the plains of French [[Flanders]] to the north, and south toward the mining country of Artois. The hill's strategic vantage point had been recognized since antiquity — the Romans, medieval counts, and Louis XIV's military engineers all used it as an observation post. Gibbs notes the "five old windmills of Cassel that wave their arms up the hill" (042-035), a distinctive landmark visible for miles around.

## Role in the War

Cassel's military significance derived entirely from its topography. The hilltop provided an unparalleled observation platform over the Ypres Salient, allowing the Second Army's commander [[Herbert Plumer]] and his staff to survey the battlefield from relative safety. Gibbs describes the headquarters as "a big building which had been a French military college" (022-010), where Plumer conducted the methodical operations — Messines Ridge (1917) and the Third Battle of Ypres (Passchendaele) — for which his army was responsible. The town's ancient streets filled with billeted officers, staff cars, and dispatch riders. Gibbs records that "for some strange reason Cassel itself was never bombed" (128-019), a remarkable immunity for such an important target, which he attributes partly to luck and partly to the Germans' reluctance to waste shells on a target beyond effective observation. From Cassel, staff officers made regular trips to the forward areas — "a quick ride over Cassel Hill to the edge of the Ypres salient" (004-001) — and maintained communication with higher echelons through "staff-officers from Cassel to Boulogne, and away back to Whitehall" (128-025).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's portrait of Cassel is unusually intimate for a headquarters location. He was billeted there and describes the town with an affection born of familiarity: "we were billeted in the old town of Cassel, where, perched on a hill which in time of peace would be a place of pilgrimage for tourists" (128-001). His memoir captures the peculiar contrast between the medieval streets and the modern war being directed from within them — the Second Army headquarters where "many of the battles of the Ypres salient were directed" (127-013), the room "where many of the generals I knew sat at the council table" (127-013). He conveys the strange quiet of the place: "the little old town of Cassel on the hill" (128-012) preserved in an atmospheric pocket above the devastation below. The journey to the front from Cassel took him "along the road through Ypres to Cassel" (128-018) — the ruined city of [[Ypres]] a constant, smoking presence on the horizon, visible from Cassel's high windows. The Scottish officers who "rode down into Cassel" from the forward area (128-025) brought with them the smells and dust of the line, an intrusion of the war's reality into the headquarters enclave.
