## Overview

The dugout was the soldier's underground home on the Western Front — a shelter excavated into trench walls or tunneled deep below the surface, lined with timber or corrugated iron, and offering the only protection from shellfire, weather, and the constant exposure of trench life. In Gibbs's memoir, dugouts appear as settings for conversations, councils of war, and the rare moments of physical comfort that punctuated the misery of the trenches (060-013).

## Definition

Dugouts ranged from shallow "funk-holes" scraped into trench walls — barely large enough for one man to curl up in — to elaborate underground complexes with electric lighting, bunks, and multiple rooms. Deep dugouts, sometimes thirty or forty feet below ground, could survive direct hits from all but the heaviest shells. They were accessed by wooden steps and lit by candles or carbide lamps.

## In the War

Dugouts were essential to survival. Gibbs records that after the capture of the Hohenzollern Redoubt, men worked through the night to consolidate their position, "flung out barbed wire, and dug themselves into this ground" (062-001). The German dugouts were often superior: "In some places they had the advantage of our men in better trenches, with better drains and dugouts — due to an industry with which ours could never compete" (060-005).

Gibbs contrasts the comfort of headquarters dugouts with the conditions of front-line troops. G.H.Q. officers enjoyed "good fires... burning under dry roofs" while issuing orders that exposed men in forward positions to enemy fire (060-013).

## In the Memoir

Dugouts feature in Gibbs's memoir as places of both sanctuary and surreal domesticity. He describes conversations with soldiers in their underground shelters, the adjutant in his dugout asking casually "'What casualties?'" as though inquiring about the weather (041-001). The dugout was also where the war's informal codes developed — the "fraternization" between enemies, the gallows humor, the philosophical conversations that Gibbs captures as the war's hidden human dimension.
