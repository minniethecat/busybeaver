## Overview

The Third Battle of Ypres, commonly known as Passchendaele, was fought from July 31 to November 10, 1917, in the low-lying Flanders plain around the shattered city of Ypres. Sir Douglas Haig's objective was to break through the German lines, capture the Belgian ports from which U-boats operated, and turn the German flank. Instead, the offensive became a four-month ordeal of mud, artillery, and incremental slaughter. Philip Gibbs observed the battle from the press billet at Cassel and recorded the grinding advance: "the ridge which led at last to Passchendaele" (031-050).

## Background

Haig had long wanted a Flanders offensive, believing a breakthrough in the north could roll up the German line and capture the Channel ports. The destruction of the French army's offensive spirit in the 1917 mutinies made a British offensive politically essential. The Flanders plain was chosen despite warnings that its drainage system, destroyed by years of shelling, would turn the battlefield into a swamp with autumn rains. The preliminary attack at Messines Ridge on June 7, 1917, succeeded brilliantly — nineteen mines detonated beneath the German lines. But the main offensive, delayed until July 31, gave the Germans weeks to prepare.

## The Event

The battle opened on July 31, 1917, with Sir Hubert Gough's Fifth Army leading the assault. Early rains turned the shell-churned ground into a quagmire. Tanks bogged down. Men and horses drowned in the mud. In August, Haig transferred command of the main effort to Sir Herbert Plumer's Second Army, whose bite-and-hold tactics achieved limited successes at the Menin Road Ridge, Polygon Wood, and Broodseinde.

By October, the objective had narrowed to the village of Passchendaele itself, standing on a slight rise above the flooded plain. Gibbs records the final phase: "the heights at Passchendaele were stormed and won, though at terrible cost" (127-002). The Canadian Corps, under Arthur Currie, took over the assault and captured Passchendaele on November 6. Haig could report that his armies had captured the ridge. He did not emphasize that they had advanced only five miles in over three months.

## Aftermath

Passchendaele cost approximately 250,000 British and Empire casualties for five miles of ground. The German army suffered similarly, and its reserves were exhausted — but it had not broken. The offensive had drained British manpower at a time when the army was already weakened by the Somme and Arras. The name Passchendaele, like the Somme, became emblematic of the war's horror. Gibbs wrote that Plumer "hurled it against Poelcapelle, Passchendaele, Becelaere, Gheluvelt, and Zandvoorde" (127-022) — a list of names that had become cenotaphs.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs is ambivalent about Passchendaele. He acknowledges the courage of the troops and the tactical skill of commanders like Plumer and Currie. He records that Plumer "was glad that Passchendaele had been captured before his departure" (127-013). But the battle confirmed his deepening skepticism about the High Command's strategy. The mud, the slaughter, the negligible gains — Passchendaele was for Gibbs the Somme repeated, only wetter. "Where his dead lay in the swamp" (127-003) — the phrase captures both the physical reality and the moral judgment.
