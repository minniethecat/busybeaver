## Overview

The Battle of Cambrai, fought from November 20 to December 7, 1917, marked a revolution in warfare: the first massed use of tanks as the primary instrument of attack. Nearly 400 tanks, supported by new artillery techniques, smashed through the formidable Hindenburg Line defenses in a matter of hours — gains that would have cost hundreds of thousands of lives on the Somme. The church bells rang in England for the first time since the war began. But German counter-attacks ten days later recaptured most of the ground. Gibbs observed the "Cambrai advance of November, '17" (020-002) and later assessed that "great hopes were entertained in the first battle of Cambrai" (109-007).

## Background

By late 1917, the Third Battle of Ypres (Passchendaele) had bogged down in Flanders mud. The Tank Corps, whose new weapon had been used piecemeal since the Somme, sought a battlefield where massed armor could demonstrate its full potential. The rolling chalk downland near Cambrai offered ideal tank country. General Sir Julian Byng's Third Army planned a surprise attack using predicted artillery fire — without preliminary bombardment — followed by a massed tank advance through the Hindenburg Line.

## The Event

At dawn on November 20, 1917, 378 British tanks rumbled toward the German lines across a six-mile front. The silence of the guns before the attack ensured complete surprise. German infantry, faced with armored monsters emerging from the mist, broke and fled. By midday, the British had advanced five miles in places — gains that on the Somme had taken months and cost a hundred thousand lives.

The breakthrough proved too deep for exploitation. Cavalry reserves, still waiting for the great gap, were held up by a single German field battery. The advance stalled. On November 30, the Germans launched a devastating counter-attack using new infiltration tactics — *Hutier* stormtrooper methods — that recaptured much of the lost ground. Gibbs notes how the tank had not yet matured: the "troops who had been trained with high hopes...were unprepared for the German counter-stroke" (109-007).

## Aftermath

Cambrai ended with both sides roughly where they had started, at a cost of approximately 44,000 British and 45,000 German casualties. But the battle had demonstrated that the Hindenburg Line was not impregnable and that tanks, used correctly, could restore mobility to the battlefield. The German counter-attack, meanwhile, previewed the stormtrooper tactics that would nearly win the war in March 1918. Cambrai was also where the Gothic Line tactics of predicted artillery fire — shooting without prior registration — proved their worth.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs covers Cambrai in the context of the evolution of armored warfare. He had witnessed the first tanks on the Somme and watched them develop: "the Tanks, as we called them, were no longer experimental toys but weapons of decision" (109-002). Yet he records the defensive failure that followed as a caution: "in the Cambrai salient...the Tank had not yet come into its own" (109-002). The battle left him with a sense of promise unfulfilled — a glimpse of what mechanized warfare might become, had the exploitation been properly organized.
