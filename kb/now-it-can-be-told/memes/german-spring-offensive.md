## Overview

The German Spring Offensive of 1918 — called the *Kaiserschlacht* ("Emperor's Battle") by the Germans — was a series of massive attacks launched between March and July 1918. Freed from the Eastern Front after Russia's collapse, the German High Command under General Erich Ludendorff transferred some 50 divisions westward for a decisive blow before American forces could arrive in strength. The offensive opened on March 21 with Operation Michael, smashing through the British Fifth Army on the old Somme battlefield, and over the following months launched additional offensives in Flanders (Operation Georgette) and on the Aisne. For a time, the German advance threatened to split the British and French armies and capture Amiens. Gibbs, who witnessed the preparations and aftermath, wrote that "the events of March, 1918, were not foreseen nor prevented" (123-002) — a judgment on the Allied command's failure to anticipate the blow. Ultimately, the offensive exhausted Germany's last reserves of manpower and morale; the Allied counteroffensive in July marked the beginning of the war's final act.

## Background

By the end of 1917, the strategic situation had shifted decisively. Russia had collapsed into revolution and signed the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk in March 1918, freeing nearly a million German soldiers for service in the west. The United States had entered the war in April 1917, but its army would not be ready for large-scale combat until mid-1918. Ludendorff saw a narrow window of opportunity: strike now, before the Americans arrived in force, and win the war.

The German tactical approach had evolved dramatically since 1916. Stormtrooper (*Stosstruppen*) tactics, developed on the Eastern Front and at Caporetto, emphasized infiltration over mass assault. Specially trained assault units would bypass strongpoints, penetrate deep into the enemy rear, and disrupt communications and command. A short, intense artillery bombardment using poison gas would precede the infantry.

Gibbs was not at the front during the March 21 attack — he had been reassigned — but he reconstructed the events through interviews with survivors and his knowledge of the ground. He noted the failure of Allied intelligence and planning: "The events of March, 1918, were not foreseen nor prevented" (123-002).

## The Event

At 4:40 a.m. on March 21, 1918, more than 6,600 German guns opened fire along a 40-mile front between Arras and La Fère. The bombardment, lasting only five hours, was a masterpiece of brevity and concentration: high explosive mixed with gas shells saturated the British forward zone. Then, through thick fog, specially trained stormtrooper units advanced.

Gibbs recorded the shock of that morning: "March 21st came, as we knew it would come" (130-015). The British Fifth Army under General Sir Hubert Gough, holding recently taken-over French trenches that were poorly fortified, crumbled under the assault. The Germans advanced up to 40 miles in some sectors — distances unseen on the Western Front since 1914. The old Somme battlefields, purchased at such hideous cost in 1916, were abandoned. "Ammunition columns went up to the Somme front in long, unending lines" had been Gibbs's image of the 1916 offensive (016-008); now the same roads carried British troops in retreat.

By late March, the Germans had reached the outskirts of Amiens. Gibbs described the scenes there: "in 1918, when the enemy came nearly to the gates of Amiens" (107-009), the city became a ghost of its wartime self. In the memoir, he paints a picture of the crisis: "The night in Amiens was dark and sinister when rain fell heavily out of a moonless sky. Hardly a torch-lamp flashed out except where a solitary woman scurried down the wet streets to lonely rooms" — an evocation of a city threatened by imminent capture.

The situation was so grave that on March 26, the Allies appointed General Ferdinand Foch as Supreme Commander of all Allied forces on the Western Front — the first unified command of the war. Ludendorff launched additional offensives: Operation Georgette in Flanders in April, and Operation Blücher-Yorck on the Aisne in May, which brought the Germans to the Marne River, within 40 miles of Paris.

But the German advance was running out of momentum. Supply lines lengthened; stormtrooper casualties mounted; the troops, finding Allied supply depots well stocked with food and luxuries they had not seen in years, stopped to loot. Gibbs noted the turning point: "The German offensive had been checked and the American troops were coming in" (096-010). By July, the last German offensive — the Second Battle of the Marne — was decisively defeated by a French-American counterattack. The initiative had passed to the Allies.

## Aftermath

The Spring Offensive was one of the costliest campaigns of the war. German casualties exceeded 688,000, including many of the irreplaceable stormtroopers who had spearheaded the attacks. Allied losses were similarly heavy: approximately 418,000 British and 433,000 French. But the Allies could replace their losses with American manpower; Germany could not.

Ludendorff himself described March 21 as the German army's "finest achievement" in his postwar memoirs, but the strategic result was disaster. The offensive had gambled everything on a knockout blow and failed. The German army, exhausted and demoralized, began to disintegrate under the Allied Hundred Days Offensive that began in August. By November, Germany had sued for an armistice.

Gibbs was unsparing in his analysis of Ludendorff's role and his postwar self-justifications. Quoting from the German general's memoirs, he wrote of "the cold-blooded cruelty of Ludendorff's Memoirs, in which, without a pang of remorse, he boasts of the enormous casualties suffered by the German troops" (123-005). Gibbs contrasted Ludendorff's "scientific" attitude toward slaughter with the moral and human reality of what had been done: "Of those things General Ludendorff does not write in his Memoirs" (124-015).

## In the Memoir

The Spring Offensive appears in Gibbs's memoir less as a single narrative than as a constellation of moments — the foreboding before the attack, the chaos of the retreat, the ghostliness of threatened Amiens, and the fraught debates over responsibility. Gibbs acknowledges Ludendorff's tactical achievement: "Ludendorff claims, quite justly, that it was the German army's greatest feat of arms" (124-001). But his ultimate judgment is that the offensive was a crime against the soldiers — German as well as Allied — driven by the same cold calculus that had characterized German strategy throughout the war.

His engagement with Ludendorff's memoirs is particularly significant. Gibbs wrote *Now It Can Be Told* in 1920, the year after Ludendorff's own memoirs were published, and he uses them as a foil — quoting the German general's boasts to expose what he sees as a moral void at the heart of German high command. The Spring Offensive, in Gibbs's telling, is not just a military campaign but a parable about the relationship between generalship and humanity.
