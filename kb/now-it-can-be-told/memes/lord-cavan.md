## Overview

Frederick Lambart, 10th Earl of Cavan, commanded the Guards Division during the Battle of the Somme and would later rise to become one of the most successful British corps commanders of the war. Gibbs's memoir captures Cavan at two contrasting moments during the 1916 offensive: a peacetime dispute over captured artillery trophies that reveals the strange normalcy that persisted at headquarters (055-027), and a wartime encounter with a shell-shocked officer that reveals the quiet professionalism Cavan brought to his command (057-017, 057-018).

## Role in the War

Cavan's Guards Division was among the elite formations committed to the Somme offensive. Gibbs first encounters him in an almost incongruously domestic scene at General Rawlinson's château:

> "General Rawlinson was taking tea in his château when I called on him, and was having an animated argument with Lord Cavan, commanding the Guards, as to the disposal of the captured artillery and other trophies. Lord Cavan claimed some for his own, with some violence of speech."
> — [055-027]

The argument over who got to keep which captured German guns played out while "away up the line there would be more dead piled up, more filth and stench of human slaughter, but the smell of it would not reach back to headquarters" (055-027). Gibbs's juxtaposition of the tea-table squabble with the carnage up the line is one of the memoir's sharpest ironic effects: Rawlinson's staff "were anticipating the real German counterattack — a big affair" with the same matter-of-fact concern they gave to trophy allocation.

Cavan reappears later in a very different register. At his Guards divisional headquarters, a young officer arrived and delivered an impossible report:

> "Sir, I have to report that my battalion has been cut to pieces. We have been utterly destroyed."
> — [057-017]

Cavan's response was quiet and immediate: he questioned the officer, then sent for another staff officer and said, "Look after that young man. He is mad. It is a case of shell-shock" (057-018). The diagnosis was correct — the officer's battalion had not, in fact, been destroyed — but Gibbs's narration does not editorialize. The episode stands as a vignette of the psychological destruction that the Somme inflicted on those who survived.

## In the Memoir

Cavan serves a structural function in Gibbs's narrative: he is the competent, unflappable professional whose presence anchors two otherwise disorienting passages. In the first, the surreal normality of the château — tea, trophies, chaffing staff officers — contrasts with the industrial slaughter occurring a few miles away. In the second, the Guards headquarters becomes a way-station for the war's psychological casualties, and Cavan's calm becomes a form of mercy.

Gibbs does not moralize about either episode. The trophy dispute is not condemned; the shell-shock case is not sentimentalized. But the juxtaposition of the two Cavan scenes — the general arguing over guns one moment, quietly absorbing the human cost the next — captures something essential about the officer class Gibbs chronicles: capable of both pettiness and compassion, often in the same afternoon.
