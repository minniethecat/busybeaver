## Overview

The Battle of Arras, launched on April 9, 1917, was designed as a diversionary offensive to support the French Nivelle Offensive on the Aisne. The opening day saw dramatic British gains — including the Canadian capture of Vimy Ridge — but the battle soon bogged down into a grinding struggle east of Arras. Gibbs covered the battle personally and his memoir contains some of its most vivid battlefield journalism from these actions.

## Background

By early 1917, General Nivelle had convinced the French government that his planned offensive would break the German line within 48 hours. The British were asked to attack at Arras a week earlier to draw German reserves away from the French sector. Haig complied, though he and his commanders had misgivings. The British had learned from the Somme: the Arras attack employed improved artillery tactics, better coordination, and extensive tunneling.

## The Event

The offensive opened brilliantly on April 9. The Canadian Corps stormed Vimy Ridge, capturing the most formidable German position on the sector. Further south, British divisions advanced up to three miles on the first day — gains that would have been unthinkable on the Somme. But the initial success could not be sustained. Gibbs describes the "fighting that followed the battle of Arras on April 9th along the glacis east of the city" (020-001) — weeks of bitter, costly actions that achieved little.

He records General Haldane's defiant response to the German counter-offensive: "'They will not take Arras if I can help it,' he said" (017-004). Gibbs visited a "bedroom, in an old house of Arras, while he gave me an account of the fighting" (020-016), and at night "looked Arras way, and saw what seemed a burning furnace" (037-005) — the city under shellfire.

## Aftermath

The battle achieved its diversionary purpose: German reserves were drawn north, and Nivelle's offensive failed regardless. Vimy Ridge was the greatest single British success of the war to date, and it cemented the Canadian Corps' reputation. But the grinding actions east of Arras through May 1917 cost the British heavily for negligible gains. Ludendorff admitted the severity of the defeat in his memoirs.

## In the Memoir

Arras provides some of Gibbs's most direct frontline reporting. He was in the city while it was under shellfire, interviewed commanders in the thick of the battle, and watched the night sky burn. The battle embodies the pattern Gibbs observed throughout 1916–17: brilliant initial gains, followed by stagnation, followed by the weary question — was it worth it? His description of the "glacis" east of Arras as killing ground is one of the memoir's most devastating tactical critiques.
