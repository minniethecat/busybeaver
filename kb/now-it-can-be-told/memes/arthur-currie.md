## Overview

General Sir Arthur Currie commanded the Canadian Corps from 1917 through the end of the war, leading it through Vimy Ridge, Passchendaele, the Hundred Days, and into Mons on the final day. Gibbs saw in Currie something different from the traditional professional officer: a citizen-soldier who applied practical intelligence rather than rigid doctrine. His pre-war career as an estate agent made him emblematic of the Dominion officers who rose to high command not through the staff college but through demonstrated competence in the field.

## Biography

Arthur William Currie was born in 1875 in Strathroy, Ontario. Before the war he worked as a schoolteacher, insurance salesman, and estate agent in Victoria, British Columbia. He joined the militia as a part-time gunner and rose through the ranks by ability rather than connections. At the outbreak of war in 1914, he commanded a brigade; by 1917 he led the entire Canadian Corps. Under his command, the Canadians captured Vimy Ridge (April 1917), Hill 70, and Passchendaele village, and spearheaded the Hundred Days offensive with a series of brilliant combined-arms attacks. After the war he served as Principal of McGill University. He died in 1933.

## Role in the War

Currie's appointment as corps commander reflected a broader pattern Gibbs observed in the Dominion forces: "The same thing was observed in the Canadian Corps, General Currie, the corps commander, having been an estate agent, and many of his high officers having had no military training of any scientific importance before they handled their own men in France and Flanders" (021-005). Despite — or perhaps because of — this unconventional background, Currie proved to be one of the war's finest corps commanders.

Gibbs was struck by Currie upon their first meeting: "I was impressed by General Currie, whom I met for the first time in that winter of 1915–16, and wrote at the time that I saw in him 'a leader of men who in open warfare might win great victories by doing the commonsense thing rapidly and decisively, to the surprise of an enemy working by elaborate schedules'" (065-004).

## In the Memoir

Currie's final appearance in the memoir comes during the desperate fighting around Cambrai in 1918, where his Canadians suffered heavily: "General Currie, whom I saw during those days, was anxious and disheartened. He was losing more men in machine-gun actions round Cambrai than in bigger battles" (131-009). This glimpse of the commander's personal anguish contextualizes Gibbs's larger argument about the Dominion generals: "Their generals believed in common sense applied to war, and not in high mysteries and secret rites which cannot be known outside the circle of initiation" (065-004).
