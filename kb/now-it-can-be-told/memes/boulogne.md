## Overview

Boulogne (Boulogne-sur-Mer) was one of the most important Channel ports in France during the First World War, serving as the principal point of disembarkation for the British Expeditionary Force in August 1914 and remaining a vital logistics hub, hospital centre, and transit point throughout the conflict. Gibbs describes it as "a port through which all the armies passed" (102-001), capturing its role as the revolving door between England and the Western Front.

## Geography

Boulogne sits on the northern coast of France in the Pas-de-Calais department, at the mouth of the Liane River where it empties into the English Channel. The town is built around a natural harbour sheltered by two long jetties, with the old upper town (Haute-Ville) perched on an elevated promontory overlooking the port below. Its location — roughly 70 miles from the Kent coast and 120 miles from the Ypres Salient — made it the shortest and most convenient sea crossing for troops and supplies moving from southern England to the Western Front. The surrounding countryside consists of rolling chalk downs and river valleys typical of the Boulonnais region.

## Role in the War

In the first days of August 1914, Boulogne became the primary disembarkation port for the BEF as it crossed to France. Gibbs records the early arrivals: "in the first days of August of the war, landing at Boulogne and marching, as though to a great adventure" (024-002). Throughout the war, the port handled a ceaseless flow of men, munitions, food, and horses. By 1916, an elaborate infrastructure of docks, warehouses, and railway sidings had been constructed to manage the volume of traffic. The town also became a major hospital centre, with Gibbs noting that "at Etaples and other places near Boulogne hospital huts and tents were spread over wide acres" (099-009). Alongside the hospitals grew a network of support facilities — bakeries, boot-mending factories, and ordnance depots — that Gibbs inspected during his visits (011-004). Beyond the town, a great camp near Etaples housed troops in transit and recovering wounded (102-002). Boulogne's importance as a communication hub also meant it was a critical node in the road network: "all the roads to Paris and from Boulogne and Dieppe and Havre to the front were in a state of intense motion" (089-001).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's portrait of Boulogne is one of constant, restless motion. His memoir captures the port as a place of transit and waiting — the point where men arriving from England first set foot in France, and where those granted leave departed for the brief respite of home. He describes soldiers catching "a chance of a lorry-ride to Boulogne, on seven days' leave from the trenches" (011-005), the port becoming synonymous with the prospect of escape from the front. The town's role as a rear-area support hub is conveyed through vignettes of its social life: "the bun shops in Boulogne were filled with nurses, V.A.D.s, and officers on leave" (102-003), a scene of fragile normalcy behind the lines. Gibbs also records the passage of staff officers shuttling between command echelons — "staff-officers from Cassel to Boulogne, and away back to Whitehall" (128-025) — underlining Boulogne's position in the administrative geography of the war. Gibbs's descriptions of Boulogne, like those of [[Paris]] and [[London]], emphasize the contrast between the war zone and the rear areas, where life continued with a semblance of pre-war rhythms.
