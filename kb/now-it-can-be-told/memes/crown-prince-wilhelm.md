## Overview

Crown Prince Wilhelm of Prussia, the eldest son of Kaiser Wilhelm II, commanded the German 5th Army and later the Army Group named for him on the Western Front. Gibbs's memoir focuses on the Crown Prince's central role in the Battle of Verdun — the German offensive of 1916 that became synonymous with industrial-scale slaughter and strategic futility.

## Biography

Friedrich Wilhelm Victor August Ernst was born in 1882 at the Marmorpalais in Potsdam, the eldest of Kaiser Wilhelm II's seven children. Despite his royal birth, he was given command of the 5th Army at the outbreak of war in 1914, though operational decisions were largely made by his chief of staff. He commanded forces at Verdun and later on the Chemin des Dames. After the German Revolution of 1918, he went into exile in the Netherlands. He returned to Germany in 1923 and lived quietly until his death in 1951.

## Role in the War

The Crown Prince's military career was defined by the Verdun offensive. In February 1916, his 5th Army launched the assault designed by Falkenhayn to "bleed France white." Gibbs records the world's reaction: "The world — our side of it — held its breath and felt its own heartbeat when, in February of that year '15, the armies of the German Crown Prince launched their offensive against the French at Verdun" (070-001).

The offensive consumed enormous resources and produced staggering casualties on both sides. Gibbs describes how the German command "was concentrating all its energy and available strength in manpower and gun-power upon the attack of Verdun. The Crown Prince had staked his reputation upon that adventure" (118-003).

When the 1918 Spring Offensive collapsed, the Crown Prince's army group suffered catastrophic reverses: "The Crown Prince's army was attacked on both flanks and broken" (130-031), and German reserves had to be thrown "into the melting-pot to save the Crown Prince" (131-001).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs treats the Crown Prince with the restraint of a journalist writing about enemy royalty — he names the military actions without personal vilification, but the verdict on the Verdun strategy is unmistakable. The Crown Prince's reputation was "staked" on Verdun, and its failure marked the beginning of the end for German ambitions. Gibbs also notes that the Crown Prince was one of the figures whose plans were developed as "the Crown Prince's plan against the French" (130-029), framing him as an active participant in strategic decision-making rather than a mere figurehead.
