## Overview

High Wood was a small but strategically vital copse on the Somme ridge, between Bazentin-le-Petit and Martinpuich. Though modest in size, its position on the forward slope of the ridge gave it immense tactical importance during the Battle of the Somme. Gibbs describes how it provided "a fine observation of the enemy's lines above Martinpuich and Courcellette away to Bapaume" (108-020). For this reason, the Germans were ordered to hold it "at all costs," and many German batteries had registered on it. The result was months of savage, close-quarters fighting that reduced the wood to a "thin row of branchless trees" (108-020) and made it "another hell" (108-021) for the British divisions sent to capture it.

## Geography

High Wood is located on the Somme plateau, approximately 1.5 kilometers northeast of Bazentin-le-Petit and 2 kilometers southwest of Martinpuich. Before the battle, it was a dense copse of mature trees with heavy undergrowth — typical of the Somme's patchwork of small woodlands. Its eastern edge overlooked a reverse slope that offered clear observation toward the German rear positions. After months of shelling, Gibbs records the transformation: "All that was left of High Wood in the autumn of 1916 was a thin row of branchless trees" (108-020), while earlier in the summer "there were still glades under heavy foliage, until the branches were lopped off and the leaves scattered by our incessant fire" (108-020). The London Cemetery and Extension now stand at the wood's southern edge.

## Role in the War

High Wood was contested from mid-July 1916, following the capture of Bazentin Ridge on July 14. The first attack was made by cavalry — a squadron of the 7th Dragoon Guards and the 20th Deccan Horse — who briefly entered the wood on July 14 but were withdrawn, marking the last British cavalry charge on the Western Front. Subsequent infantry assaults through July and August were repelled with heavy losses.

The main assault came on September 15, 1916 — the first day tanks were used in battle. Gibbs records the grim result: "High Wood became another hell, on a day of great battle — September 14, 1916 — when for the first time tanks were used" (108-021). Actually the date is likely the 15th. The Londoners of the 47th Division "gained part of High Wood at frightful cost and then were blown out of it. Other divisions followed them and found the wood stuffed with machine-guns which they had to capture through hurricanes of bullets before they crouched in craters amid dead Germans and dead English, and then were blown out like the Londoners, under shellfire, in which no human life could stay for long" (108-021).

The 7th Division was "cut up there." The 33rd Division "lost six thousand men in an advance against uncut wire in the wood, which they were told was already captured" (108-022). Casualty figures were compounded by gas attacks: "Hundreds of men were vomiting from the effect of gas-shells, choking and blinded" (108-023).

A tragic confusion plagued the fighting. Gibbs records an overheard telephone conversation in which a staff officer learned that "our men seen leaving High Wood" were being "shelled by our artillery" (108-027). The officer's grim question — "How is one to tell?" (108-031) — captures the chaos of battlefield communication. "The Londoners said so from High Wood. The Australians said so from Mouquet Farm. The Scots said so from Longueval! They said: 'Why the hell do we get murdered by British gunners?'" (108-033).

In the Spring Offensive of 1918, High Wood was lost again: "once again after many battles the enemy was in Courcelette and High Wood and Delville Wood" (130-023).

## In the Memoir

High Wood concentrates many of the bitterest themes in Gibbs's account of the Somme. It exemplifies the piecemeal, uncoordinated nature of the fighting: "separate attacks on places like Trones Wood and Delville Wood, and later on High Wood" which allowed the Germans to concentrate their artillery on a single point (108-018). It illustrates the gruesome toll on specific divisions, recorded with a journalist's attention to unit identity. And it exposes the chaotic communication failures that led British artillery to shell its own infantry — a recurring trauma that haunted the survivors.

Gibbs's treatment of High Wood also foregrounds the physical transformation of the battlefield landscape. The wood's reduction from a living copse to a "thin row of branchless trees" stands as a synecdoche for the devastation of the Somme itself — and for the war's capacity to erase even the most basic features of the natural world.
