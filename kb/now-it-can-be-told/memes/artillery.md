## Overview

Artillery was the supreme weapon of the First World War. It caused more casualties than rifles, machine guns, bayonets, and gas combined. The relentless bombardment of trench lines transformed the landscape of France and Belgium into cratered moonscapes, and the sound of shellfire became the constant background noise of soldiers' lives. Gibbs, who witnessed innumerable bombardments, describes [[artillery]] as both the instrument of destruction and the focus of the industrial war's inhuman scale.

## Definition

Artillery encompasses large-caliber firearms — field guns, howitzers, and mortars — designed to fire explosive shells over long distances using indirect fire. On the [[Western Front]], artillery ranged from light 18-pounder field guns to massive 15-inch howitzers nicknamed "Grandmother." The artillery arm consumed the largest share of industrial production and military manpower of any combat branch.

## In the War

The artillery war was one of constant escalation. In the early war, British artillery was "much weaker than ours" according to German assessments, and British troops suffered grievously from German gunfire (016-014). By 1916, the British had built up enormous artillery parks. Gibbs describes the [[Battle of the Somme|Somme]] bombardment as "a vast orchestra of drumfire" where "the tumult of the field-guns merged into thunderous waves" and "a fifteen-inch 'Grandmother' fired single strokes, and each one was an enormous clap of sound" (104-003).

The physical destruction wrought by artillery was total. After bombardments, "where there had been a crisscross of trenches and sandbag shelters for their machine-guns and a network of [[barbed-wire]], there was now an enormous crater" (032-013). The artillery duel became the defining rhythm of [[trench-warfare|trench]] life — periods of relative quiet punctuated by "casual shellfire" that inflicted a steady toll of casualties (041-001).

## In the Memoir

Gibbs conveys both the physical terror and the surreal detachment that artillery produced. He "ducked my head behind a curtain of bricks as I heard a shrill 'coo-ee!' from a shell" (030-023). The noise was omnipresent: "the wood resounded with sharp reports as though hard filbert nuts were being cracked by giant teeth" (037-021). The "drumfire" of concentrated bombardments became one of the memoir's most vivid auditory motifs — a sound that, for Gibbs's generation, would forever define the memory of the Western Front.
