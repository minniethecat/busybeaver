## Overview

The Battle of Mons, fought on August 23, 1914, was the first major action of the British Expeditionary Force in the Great War. Outnumbered and outflanked, the professional soldiers of the BEF fought a skilled defensive action before beginning the long retreat that took them to the outskirts of Paris. In Gibbs's memoir, "the retreat from Mons" is not merely a military event but a symbolic touchstone — the moment the war moved from plan to catastrophe.

## Background

The BEF, consisting of four infantry divisions and one cavalry division under Sir John French, took up position on the left flank of the French Fifth Army near the Belgian mining town of Mons. They faced General von Kluck's German First Army — vastly superior in numbers. The British mission was to hold the Mons-Condé Canal and protect the French flank.

## The Event

On the morning of August 23, German infantry advanced in dense formations and were met by the BEF's rapid, accurate rifle fire — so fast that German reports spoke of machine-gun batteries where there were only riflemen. The British held through the day but were forced to withdraw as the French Fifth Army on their right fell back. What followed was the two-week "Retreat from Mons" — a fighting withdrawal in August heat over nearly 200 miles. "That was after the retreat from Mons, and the French retreat along their own lines" (002-020). The retreat ended at the Marne, where the Allies turned and counterattacked.

## Aftermath

British casualties at Mons were relatively light — about 1,600 — but the psychological impact was enormous. The retreat established the character of the "Old Contemptibles" in British memory: outnumbered but undefeated, professional soldiers sacrificed in a war that would consume millions. The Kaiser's supposed reference to "Sir John French's contemptible little army" was transformed by the soldiers themselves into a badge of honor. Gibbs records a veteran who "described how he had run away from Mons 'with the rest of you'" (038-003) — the self-deprecating humor of survivors.

## In the Memoir

"The retreat from Mons" recurs throughout Gibbs's memoir as a reference point for everything that followed. It was "not, to tell the truth, a glorious episode" (018-001) in the conventional sense, but it shaped the generation of soldiers and journalists who experienced it. Gibbs, writing in 1920, could assume that every reader knew what "Mons" meant: the beginning of the British Army's war, the first encounter with the reality of German military power, and the first of many retreats.
