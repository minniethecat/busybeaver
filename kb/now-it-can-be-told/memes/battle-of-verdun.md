## Overview

The Battle of Verdun, fought from February 21 to December 18, 1916, was the longest single engagement of the First World War and one of the most devastating. German Chief of Staff Erich von Falkenhayn's objective was not territorial but attritional — to "bleed France white" by forcing her to defend a symbol of national pride. Philip Gibbs recorded the anxiety the battle provoked across the entire Allied front: "the German Crown Prince launched their great offensive against the French at Verdun. It was the biggest offensive the world had then seen" (070-001).

## Background

By early 1916, Falkenhayn had concluded that Britain was Germany's principal enemy but could not be directly attacked across the Channel. Instead, he would strike France at a point she could not abandon: the fortress city of Verdun, surrounded by a ring of forts and invested with centuries of patriotic significance. The German Fifth Army under Crown Prince Wilhelm massed over 1,200 artillery pieces for the offensive.

The British army, though not directly engaged at Verdun, watched the battle with intense anxiety. Gibbs records how "the news of that conflict round Verdun" reached the waiting British armies (070-003). The Somme offensive was accelerated specifically to relieve pressure on the French at Verdun.

## The Event

The German bombardment began on February 21, 1916, with a concentration of fire unprecedented in history. Within days, Fort Douaumont — the key to Verdun's defences — fell without a fight. The French government rushed General Philippe Petain to command the defence with his famous order: "They shall not pass" (*Ils ne passeront pas*).

For ten months, the battle ground on across a landscape reduced to lunar desolation. Petain organized a supply route — the *Voie Sacree* — that kept Verdun alive. Gibbs conveyed the mood of the watching Allies: "The French will never lose Verdun," said the optimist. But Gibbs recorded the darker analysis too: "They are just pouring their men into a furnace," said the pessimist (070-005, 070-007). The fighting continued through the summer until French counter-offensives in October and December recaptured most lost ground.

## Aftermath

Verdun cost approximately 700,000 French and German casualties. The fortress city held, and the French army had proven its resilience. But the damage to French military morale was profound, contributing to the mutinies of 1917. The German army, too, had been exhausted — losses Falkenhayn could not afford given the simultaneous demands of the Somme. Falkenhayn was replaced by Hindenburg and Ludendorff in August 1916. Verdun became the supreme symbol of French sacrifice in the Great War.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs did not witness Verdun firsthand — the British sector lay to the north — but the battle dominates his narrative of early 1916. He records how British officers and men discussed its progress constantly. "I agree. But the forces at Verdun are about equal, and the French are holding" (070-011). For Gibbs, Verdun represented both the horror of attritional warfare and the stubborn courage that defied it. The battle provided the grim backdrop against which the British Somme offensive was launched.
