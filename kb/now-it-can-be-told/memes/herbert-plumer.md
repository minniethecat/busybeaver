## Overview

Field Marshal Sir Herbert Plumer commanded the British Second Army on the Western Front, notably during the Battle of Messines (1917) — one of the war's most meticulously planned and successful set-piece operations. Gibbs's memoir offers a memorable physical portrait of Plumer and records the dramatic moment when he was ordered to Italy at the height of the Passchendaele campaign, reflecting both his importance and the strains on Allied command.

## Biography

Herbert Charles Onslow Plumer was born in 1857 in Torquay. A career infantry officer, he served in the Sudan, the Second Matabele War, and the Boer War. During the Great War, he commanded V Corps at the Second Battle of Ypres (1915) before assuming command of the Second Army. His masterpiece was the Battle of Messines in June 1917 — a meticulously prepared offensive that opened with the simultaneous detonation of 19 mines under the German lines. After the war he served as Governor of Malta and High Commissioner in Palestine. He was raised to the peerage as Baron Plumer and later Viscount Plumer. He died in 1932.

## Role in the War

Plumer's Second Army held the Ypres Salient, one of the most difficult and costly sectors of the Western Front. His planning for Messines Ridge in 1917 became a model of thorough preparation — the mines, laid over more than a year of tunneling, produced the largest man-made explosion before the nuclear age. The attack succeeded brilliantly: the ridge was captured in hours.

At the height of the Third Battle of Ypres (Passchendaele), Plumer was abruptly reassigned. Gibbs records: "Sir John Harington made the dramatic announcement that Sir Herbert Plumer, and he, as General Plumer's chief of staff, had been ordered to Italy — in the middle of a battle" (127-013). The Italian front had collapsed at Caporetto, and Allied command needed Plumer's steady hand to stabilize the situation.

## In the Memoir

Gibbs's portrait of Plumer is one of the memoir's most vivid character sketches: "I often used to wonder what qualities belonged to Sir Herbert Plumer, the army commander. In appearance he was almost a caricature of an old-time British general, with his ruddy, pippin-cheeked face, with white hair, and a fierce little white mustache, and blue, watery eyes, and a little potbelly and short legs. He puffed and panted when he walked" (022-001).

Gibbs also records the faith placed in Plumer by the civilians of Cassel: "others had faith in a miraculous statue, and still others in Sir Herbert Plumer" (128-020). This trust — from generals, soldiers, and civilians alike — captures Plumer's reputation as one of the war's more dependable commanders.
