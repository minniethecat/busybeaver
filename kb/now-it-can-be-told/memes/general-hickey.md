## Overview

Major-General William Bernard Hickey commanded the 16th (Irish) Division, one of the emblematic formations of the New Army raised from Irish volunteers. Gibbs, who felt a personal affinity for the Irish troops, portrays Hickey as a commander whose morale was inseparable from that of his men — "gay-hearted" when his "boys" were thriving, and "very sad" when the division was consumed by the attritional machine of the Western Front (020-019).

## Role in the War

Hickey appears first in Gibbs's roll-call of divisional generals encountered along the front, alongside Hull, Harper, Nugent, and Pinnie (020-015). But the portrait that follows is more personal and more tender than any other divisional sketch in the memoir:

> "I liked the love of General Hickey for his Irish division. An Irishman himself, with a touch of the old Irish soldier as drawn by Charles Lever, gay-hearted, proud of his boys, he was always pleased to see me because he knew I had a warm spot in my heart for the Irish troops."
> — [020-019]

Hickey served as a conduit between Gibbs and the soldiers: "He had a good story to tell every time, and passed me on to 'the boys' to get at the heart of them" (020-019). The general functioned as a kind of guarantor for the war correspondent — his trust in Gibbs opened doors to the men in the ranks.

But the war that made such bonds possible also destroyed them. The 16th (Irish) Division suffered heavily at Guillemont and Ginchy during the Somme, and the difficulty of finding Irish replacements — recruitment in Ireland had collapsed after the Easter Rising of 1916 — meant that the division's Irish character was slowly diluted. "It was long before he lost hope of keeping the division together, though it was hard to get recruits and losses were high at Guillemont and Ginchy" (020-019).

The breaking point came in a Flanders battle. Gibbs records the numbers with a reporter's precision:

> "It lost 2,000 men and 162 officers before the battle began — they were shelled to death in the trenches — and 2,000 men and 170 officers more during the progress of the battle. It was murderous and ghastly."
> — [020-019]

The pre-battle losses — men killed by shellfire before the attack even started — were a particular horror of trench warfare. The total toll of 4,000 men and 332 officers effectively destroyed the division as a coherent Irish fighting unit. "For the first time he lost heart and was very sad when the division was cut to pieces in a Flanders battle" (020-019).

Hickey himself was absent during at least one critical period: Gibbs notes that on one occasion before the German spring offensive of March 1918, "he was commanding the 16th (Irish) Division in the absence of General Hickey, who was ill" (020-016). General Hull filled the gap, but Hull's scepticism about the coming German offensive — he "gave many reasons for thinking it was all 'bluff'" — proved catastrophically wrong. "A few days later the enemy had rolled over his lines" (020-016).

## In the Memoir

Hickey occupies a unique place in Gibbs's memoir: he is the only divisional commander described primarily in terms of emotional temperament rather than military competence. The language Gibbs uses — "gay-hearted," "proud of his boys," "lost heart," "very sad" — is the language of affection and loss, not of strategic assessment. This reflects both Gibbs's own Irish sympathies (he was of Irish extraction on his mother's side) and his recognition that the 16th Division's story was a tragedy in the literary sense: a community of men bound by shared identity was fed into a war that consumed it, and the general who loved them could do nothing but watch.

Gibbs's departure from Hickey is also notable: the general passes him on to "the boys" — the men themselves — as if to say that the story worth telling is theirs, not his. It is one of the memoir's most generous authorial gestures.
