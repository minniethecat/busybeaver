# Crusade

The series of religious-military campaigns waged by Latin Christendom against Muslim powers in the Holy Land, spanning 1095–1291. In *Ivanhoe*, set in 1194, the Third Crusade (1189–1192) has just concluded, and Richard the Lionheart's return provides the novel's political frame. The crusading experience shapes nearly every knightly character, from the returned pilgrim to the Templar zealot.

## Historical Context

The Third Crusade, led by Richard I of England, Philip II of France, and Frederick Barbarossa, aimed to recapture Jerusalem after Saladin's conquest of 1187. Though the crusaders failed to retake the city, Richard negotiated a treaty securing Christian access to the holy sites. Scott's narrator is acutely aware of the crusades' mixed legacy: they elevated the ideal of knightly service to Christendom but also licensed violence, greed, and political opportunism disguised as piety.

## Crusaders in Ivanhoe

The novel opens as returning crusaders travel through England. Brian de Bois-Guilbert, a Knight Templar freshly returned from Palestine, embodies the crusade's dark side — a warrior-monk whose religious vows mask worldly ambition and predatory desire. The Templars "whom the crusades had turned back on their country" are described as "lawless resolutes" (007-004), men for whom holy war had become an excuse for lawlessness.

By contrast, the Palmer (the disguised Ivanhoe) represents the penitential ideal of crusading — a pilgrim who has fought in the Holy Land and returns in humility, his identity concealed. King Richard himself, also returning in disguise, is sought by his "needy and impoverished crusaders" (015-004) whom he must support at the expense of his Saxon subjects.

Scott's portrayal is nuanced. The crusaders are not uniformly wicked; they include genuine pilgrims and penitents. But the institution of crusading — and particularly the military orders it spawned — comes under sustained critique. The Templar preceptory of Templestowe, where Rebecca is tried for witchcraft, represents the fusion of religious and military power at its most corrupt.

> The "lawless resolutes," whom the crusades had turned back on their country, were thus left to subsist by plunder, and to recruit their wasted numbers from the hordes of banditti and outlaws who infested the country. (007-004)

> The Prior was assured that the crusaders, under Richard, had never passed the sea so ill-provided with means as on the present occasion. (013-008)
