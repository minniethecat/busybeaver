# Tournament

A formal contest of martial skill among knights, conducted according to elaborate rules of chivalry and serving as both military training and public spectacle. In *Ivanhoe*, the great tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche occupies Chapters 7–14 and functions as the novel's primary arena for testing honour, revealing identity, and dramatising the Norman-Saxon divide.

## Historical Practice

By the late 12th century, the tournament had evolved from chaotic mock battles (*mêlée*) into more regulated affairs, though fatalities remained common. Richard I, unusually for an English monarch, licensed tournament grounds in England — previously, knights had to cross to the Continent to compete. Scott's narrator notes the tournament's dual role as both sport and preparation for crusading warfare, observing that the knights who gathered at Ashby included veterans of the Holy Land "whose renown in all games of chivalry" was continental in scope (007-009).

## The Ashby Tournament in Ivanhoe

The Ashby tournament unfolds over two days. The first day features single combats with lances and swords; the second, a general *mêlée* of greater violence. Scott structures the event as a narrative of revelation: the mysterious Disinherited Knight defeats the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert and the other challengers, earning the right to crown the Queen of Love and Beauty — whom he names as the Lady Rowena.

The formal apparatus of the tournament is minutely described. Heralds, marshals, and pursuivants administer the rules: "any knight proposing to combat, might, if he pleased, select a special antagonist from the challengers" (008-019). The ritual exchange of weapons is measured: "the trial of skill was made with what were called the arms of courtesy, namely with lances, at the extremity of which were flat pieces of wood" (008-019).

The tournament's economic dimension is equally important. Defeated knights forfeit their armour and horses to the victor — a custom that makes successful champions wealthy. The Disinherited Knight, having defeated five challengers, returns the ransom money to his vanquished opponents with chivalric largesse, preserving their honour while asserting his own superiority.

> The heralds finished their proclamation with their usual cry of "Largesse, largesse, gallant knights!" and gold and silver pieces were showered on them from the galleries, it being a high point of chivalry to exhibit liberality towards those whom the age accounted at once the secretaries and the historians of honour. (008-023)

> The marshals of the field were the first to offer their congratulations to the victor, praying him, at the same time, to suffer his helmet to be unlaced, or, at least, that he would raise his visor ere they conducted him to receive the prize of the day's tourney. (009-005)
