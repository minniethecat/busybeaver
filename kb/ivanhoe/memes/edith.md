# Edith of Coningsburgh

Edith of Coningsburgh is the mother of Athelstane, the last representative of the Saxon royal line. Though she appears in relatively few scenes, her influence on the novel's political resolution is decisive: she is the character who persuades Athelstane to renounce his claim to the Anglo-Saxon throne and thus remove the final obstacle to King Richard's restoration.

## 角色概述

Edith is introduced as a noble Saxon lady of Coningsburgh Castle, the ancestral seat of her family. Her political significance derives not from personal action but from her moral authority over her son Athelstane. Where Athelstane is indolent, vacillating, and dangerously ambitious, Edith represents the older Saxon ideal of dignity and restraint:

> （032-043）… I speak in name of the noble Edith, mother of the fallen prince,…

This reference—where Cedric invokes Edith's authority in negotiations following the siege of Torquilstone—places her at the centre of the novel's political dénouement. Athelstane's claim to the Saxon throne has been a recurring threat throughout the novel; Edith's intervention removes it.

## 外貌与形象

Scott provides minimal direct description of Edith's physical appearance. She is consistently referred to through her son's identity—"the noble Edith, mother of the fallen prince"—rather than through independent physical detail. Her presence is felt through her moral and political weight rather than her visual distinctiveness.

> （042-010）"Noble Edith," said Cedric, after having s…

> （042-049）…to whom it was commenced, for Edith, having given certain necessa…

The scene at Coningsburgh Castle (Ch. 42) in which Edith interacts with Rebecca reveals her as a woman of genuine Christian charity—she receives Rebecca warmly despite the social conventions that would otherwise dictate hostility toward a Jewess. Her treatment of Rebecca demonstrates that Edith's Saxon identity is defined by courtesy and principle rather than by racial prejudice.

## 情节中的关键行动

### The Siege of Torquilstone (Ch. 28–29)

Edith's first appearance in the narrative follows the capture of Front-de-Boeuf's castle. At this point she is acting as the voice of reason in negotiations:

> （032-043）… I speak in name of the noble Edith, mother of the fallen prince,…

Her authority is such that Cedric invokes her name as a guarantee of good faith—a remarkable indication of her political weight in the Saxon community.

### Coningsburgh Castle: The Denouement (Ch. 42)

Edith's most important scene occurs at Coningsburgh Castle, where she receives Rebecca (disguised as a shepherdess after her trial) and Athelstane simultaneously:

> （042-055）"But, my son," said Edith, "consider his sacred office.…

Here Edith plays her decisive role. Athelstane, having been rescued from Torquilstone, has been restored to his ancestral claim and is being pressed by his followers to assert his right to the Anglo-Saxon throne. Edith intervenes:

> （042-063）"My son," said Edith, "think on thy royal rights!"

Her persuasion is not coercion but moral reasoning: she reminds Athelstane that true nobility consists in accepting the current order rather than in pursuing a hopeless political fantasy. This intervention removes the last political obstacle to the novel's happy ending.

### Reception of Rebecca

Edith's treatment of Rebecca at Coningsburgh is one of the novel's most quietly progressive moments. She receives the Jewish woman not merely with tolerance but with genuine hospitality, insisting that Rebecca be given shelter and care:

> （042-010）"Noble Edith," said Cedric, after having s…

This scene contrasts sharply with the anti-Jewish sentiment expressed by other characters throughout the novel and suggests that Edith represents an ideal of Saxon nobility that transcends communal prejudice.

## 历史/文学背景

Edith's name derives from the Old English *Ēadgȳð* ("rich-war"), a name borne by several Anglo-Saxon noblewomen including Edith of Wessex (c. 1025–1075), queen of Edward the Confessor. Scott's choice of this name for the mother of the last Saxon prince is clearly deliberate: it connects Athelstane's line to the pre-Conquest royal house and reinforces the novel's concern with Anglo-Saxon identity.

Scott's depiction of Edith as the moral force behind Athelstane's renunciation reflects a broader literary convention: the "wise mother" who guides her son away from destructive ambition. This motif appears in medieval romance from the *Matter of Britain* (Igraine counselling Arthur) through to Shakespeare's *King Lear* (the Fool as conscience-voice).

Edith's role is more subtle than these precedents: she does not lecture or command but reasons with Athelstane in a private conversation that changes the course of the novel.
