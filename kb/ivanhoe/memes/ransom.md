# Ransom

The custom by which a captured knight, instead of being killed, was held for a monetary payment. Ransom was a defining economic institution of medieval warfare and tournament — a system that made combat profitable and, paradoxically, may have saved lives by incentivising capture over killing.

## The Economics of Capture

In the 12th century, a knight's ransom could be his most significant financial liability — or his captor's greatest windfall. The sum was negotiated according to the captive's rank and wealth. Richard the Lionheart himself was held for ransom by Duke Leopold of Austria on his return from the Crusades, an event that forms the political backdrop of *Ivanhoe*. Scott's narrator notes that Richard sought to finance his "needy and impoverished crusaders at the expense of those who had not joined the expedition" (015-004), a policy that fuels the resentment driving the novel's political conflict.

In the tournament at Ashby, the ransom convention governs the aftermath of each combat: "the vanquished knight was, by the laws of chivalry, at the disposal of the victor" (009-024). The Disinherited Knight's decision to return the horses and armour of his defeated opponents — accepting only a portion of their ransom — is a calculated act of chivalric generosity that earns him the admiration of the spectators and the suspicion of Prince John.

> Prince John wished to banish from the minds of the chivalry around him his own indecent and unlawful conduct, and was glad to appear to take interest in the fortunes of a knight who had shown such liberality with his ransoms. (009-024)

## Ransom as Plot Mechanism

The ransom economy drives key plot points. When Cedric and his party are captured by De Bracy and the Templars at Torquilstone, the Norman lords calculate their prisoners' ransom value against the political risks of holding them. The wounded Ivanhoe, revealed to be Cedric's son, becomes simultaneously a valuable hostage and a political liability.

Rebecca's offer to pay Bois-Guilbert for her freedom — offering her father Isaac's wealth as ransom — is refused on terms that expose the limits of the ransom system: a Jewess, however wealthy, cannot purchase her liberty from a Templar who desires her for other reasons. The scene dramatises how the chivalric-economic order excluded those outside its Christian, noble framework.

> The vanquished knight was, by the laws of chivalry, at the disposal of the victor, who might dispose of his person and arms at pleasure — a custom which, however inconsistent with the laws of modern warfare, was then universally recognised. (009-024)
