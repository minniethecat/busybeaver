# Heraldry

The formal system of hereditary symbols, colours, and devices painted on shields and embroidered on surcoats, by which knights were identified in battle and tournament. In *Ivanhoe*, heraldry is the language of recognition — and misrecognition — that drives the plot's central mystery: the identity of the Disinherited Knight.

## The Function of Heraldry

By the late 12th century, heraldry had developed from simple battlefield identification into a sophisticated code. Each element — tincture (colour), charge (symbol), ordinaries (geometric divisions) — carried meaning. A knight's arms proclaimed his lineage, alliances, and deeds. The heralds who administered tournaments were also the keepers of armorial records, functioning as "the secretaries and the historians of honour" (008-023).

Shields were painted so that knights could be recognised even when fully armoured. Scott exploits this for dramatic effect: when the Disinherited Knight appears at Ashby with a shield bearing a young oak-tree torn up by the roots and the Spanish word *Desdichado*, his anonymity becomes a narrative device. No one — not even his father Cedric — recognises Ivanhoe beneath the unfamiliar arms.

> The marshals of the field were the first to offer their congratulations to the victor, praying him, at the same time, that he would raise his visor ere they conducted him to receive the prize. (009-005)

## Heraldic Disclosure as Narrative Climax

The tournament's crowning moment occurs when, at the command of Prince John and the marshals, the Disinherited Knight's helmet is unlaced. His identity revealed, the assembled "chivalry of this land" (006-015) see Wilfred of Ivanhoe — an act of heraldic unveiling that transforms the narrative from tournament spectacle to personal drama. The device on his shield, which had concealed his identity, now proclaims it.

Heraldry also marks the novel's villains. Brian de Bois-Guilbert's Templar mantle and the eight-pointed cross identify him before he speaks, signalling his allegiance to an order whose reputation precedes him. The symbols are not decorative but functional: they tell the reader, and the characters, what to expect.

> He rode, not a mule, like his companion, but a strong hackney for the road, to save his gallant war-horse, which a squire led behind, fully accoutred for battle, with a chamfron or plaited head-piece upon his head. (002-008)
