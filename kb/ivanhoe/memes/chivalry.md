# Chivalry

The code of conduct governing the knightly class in medieval Europe, combining martial valour, personal honour, and service to God and liege lord. In *Ivanhoe*, Scott depicts the late 12th-century chivalric world as a complex arena where Norman and Saxon values collide, and where the ideal of knighthood is tested by greed, religious zealotry, and political intrigue.

## Definition and Historical Background

Chivalry in 12th-century England was both a military institution and a social code. The Norman Conquest of 1066 had reshaped English knighthood, importing Continental ideals of mounted warfare, heraldry, and tournament culture. By the time of Richard I (r. 1189–1199), the chivalric code had matured into a system governing a knight's conduct in war, in court, and in personal honour (001-006).

The Crusades internationalised chivalric culture. Knights from across Christendom gathered in the Holy Land, competing in tournaments even amid siege warfare: "King Richard himself, and five of his knights, held a tournament after the taking of St John-de-Acre, as challengers against all comers" (005-032).

Scott's narrator introduces chivalry as the lens through which Norman dominance was maintained and expressed. The Norman French language itself became "the language of honour, of chivalry, and even of justice" (001-006), deepening the cultural divide between conqueror and conquered.

## Chivalry in Ivanhoe

Scott uses chivalry as the moral engine of the novel. Three dimensions stand out:

**Tournament as chivalric theatre.** The great tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche (Chapters 7–14) is the novel's most sustained display of chivalric spectacle. Brian de Bois-Guilbert, "whose renown in all games of chivalry" was unmatched (007-009), leads the challengers against the Saxon knights.

The Disinherited Knight's victories are triumphs of merit over Templar arrogance. Scott emphasises the ritual of largesse: "it being a high point of chivalry to exhibit liberality towards those whom the age accounted at once the secretaries and the historians of honour" (008-023).

**Chivalry and justice.** The concept reaches its apex in the trial by combat at Templestowe (Chapters 37–38). Bois-Guilbert, bound by Templar vows, must fight *against* Rebecca, while Ivanhoe appears as her champion. The scene tests chivalry's highest claim: that God grants victory to the righteous cause.

**The dark side of chivalry.** Scott does not present chivalry uncritically. Cedric dismisses "the fantastic fashions of Norman chivalry" (005-052) as foreign impositions. Prince John's court reveals chivalry's vulnerability to manipulation. The narrator notes that in "the days of chivalry, there were none more common than rivals in love and rivals in fame, meeting in mortal combat" (009-005).

## Chivalric Virtues and Vices

| Virtue | Norman Expression | Saxon Equivalent | Key Scene |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Martial prowess | Tournament victory at Ashby | Thane's combat in the hall | Disinherited Knight's triumph |
| Honour | Wager of battle, sworn oaths | Boasting in the mead-hall | Cedric's pledge for Ivanhoe |
| Loyalty | Fealty to liege lord | Kinship bonds, clan duty | Gurth's loyalty to Cedric |
| Generosity | Largesse to minstrels | Hospitality to all guests | Cedric's feast at Rotherwood |
| Courtly love | Service to lady | Domestic duty to family | Ivanhoe's conflicted devotion |

> "I say that the English chivalry were second to NONE who ever drew sword in defence of the Holy Land." (005-032)

> "Lady," said Cedric, "were further pledge necessary, I myself would yet gage my honour for the honour of Ivanhoe. But the wager of battle is complete, even according to the fantastic fashions of Norman chivalry." (005-052)
