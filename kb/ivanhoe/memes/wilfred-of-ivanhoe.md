# Wilfred of Ivanhoe

Wilfred of Ivanhoe, universally known by the shortened title **Ivanhoe**, is the eponymous protagonist of Walter Scott's 1819 novel. A Saxon knight disowned by his father [[Cedric the Saxon]] for adopting Norman knightly customs, Ivanhoe returns from the Crusades disguised as the mysterious "Black Knight" and ultimately wins renown at the [[Tournament at Ashby]], vindicates his honour through the trial by combat, and reconciles with his father. His story weaves together the central conflicts of the novel: Norman–Saxon tension, the code of [[Chivalry]], and the plight of [[Rebecca]] the Jewess[P01-039][P01-034].

## Background and Disinheritance

Wilfred is the son of [[Cedric the Saxon]], the proud Saxon thane of [[Rotherwood]], and the Saxon noblewoman Rowena. Against his father's wishes—Cedric having devoted himself entirely to the restoration of Saxon independence—Wilfred enlists in the Third Crusade under King [[Richard I|Richard Coeur de Lion]] and adopts Norman knightly manners, including the use of arms and the tournament culture despised by his Saxon forebears. Upon his return to England, Cedric refuses to acknowledge him, prompting Ivanhoe to set out in disguise to prove his worth[P05-045][P05-046][P05-049].

> "If Ivanhoe ever returns from Palestine, the Knight of Ivanhoe is like to hear evil tidings concerning his own fortunes."[P05-046]

> "I affirm he will meet fairly whatever champion shall encounter him in the lists, and that he will bear himself in the lists as beseems a good knight, be his adverse party who it may."[P05-052]

## The Tournament at Ashby

Disguised as a mysterious palmer and later as the "Knight of Ivanhoe," Wilfred enters the great [[Tournament at Ashby]] organised by Prince John. He fights on the second day in the lists as the "Disinherited Knight," defeating all challengers including the formidable Front-de-Boeuf, Maurice de Bracy, and Brian de Bois-Guilbert. His victory earns him the gratitude of [[Richard I|King Richard]] (disguised as the Black Knight) and the admiration of all beholders[P06-003][P06-012][P13-004].

## Trial by Combat and Vindication

After being captured during the siege of [[Torquilstone Castle]] and rescued by the Black Knight (Richard), Ivanhoe faces the legal accusation of treason brought by the Templar [[Brian-de-Bois-Guilbert]]. The charge is resolved through the ancient [[Trial by Combat|wager of battle]], in which Ivanhoe, as champion of the accused Rebecca, defeats Bois-Guilbert—who dies of natural causes mid-contest, thereby vindicating Rebecca and restoring Ivanhoe's honour[P13-003][P13-006][P13-008].

> "I will maintain the cause of the fair Jewess with my body and my honour, as a good knight ought to do for a lady in distress."[P09-030]

## Reconciliation and Marriage

Following the death of the cruel Prince John and the restoration of King Richard, Wilfred is restored to his father's favour. Cedric, persuaded by the example of his son's chivalric virtue, finally agrees to the marriage of Wilfred and Rowena, while the authority of [[Richard I|Richard]] legitimises Ivanhoe's claim to the estate of Ivanhoe. The novel closes with the Saxon–Norman synthesis embodied in Ivanhoe's person: a Saxon by birth, a Norman knight by training, and a unifying figure for the realm[P14-012][P14-013][P14-019].
