# Black Knight (King Richard I)

King Richard I of England (1157–1199), known as Richard the Lionheart, appears throughout *Ivanhoe* in the disguise of a mysterious black-armoured knight. The Black Knight is simultaneously a plot catalyst, a commentary on feudal obligation, and the agent of the novel's resolution.

## 角色概述

The Black Knight first appears at the Tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche (Ch. 13–16), where he competes in the second day's melee as one of the challengers and distinguishes himself through extraordinary prowess. Declining to name himself before receiving the promised prize—a Chaplet of Flowers from the Queen of Beauty—he later describes himself in the following terms:

> （013-017）…an, and the Saxon ox whom the Black Knight struck down in the tournament…

The Black Knight's conduct at the tournament reveals a man who prizes martial honour above reputation. Refusing to disclose his name until the Chaplet is awarded, he departs without learning who presented it:

> （016-041）en call me in these parts the Black Knight,—many, sir, add to it the epithe…

His nominal epithet—"Black Knight"—thus becomes his primary public identity throughout the novel, known to virtually every major character who encounters him.

## 外貌与形象

Walter Scott provides remarkably little direct description of the Black Knight's physical appearance, consistent with the novel's broader pattern of leaving Richard's disguise unelaborated. What emerges from the text is a warrior of imposing presence:

> （014-012）… in the archery games, of the Black Knight, whose self-denial had induce…

> （016-005）ak-tree for a canopy. But the Black Knight either had no mistress to med…

The Black Knight's equipment and bearing are those of a seasoned crusader: heavily armed, riding a powerful warhorse, and displaying a mastery of the lance that immediately distinguishes him from lesser knights. At the lists of Ashby, he fights with a restraint that marks him as a commander accustomed to ordering battles rather than merely participating in them.

## 情节中的关键行动

### Tournament at Ashby (Ch. 13–16)

The Black Knight's debut at the tournament establishes his credentials as the novel's finest living warrior. On the second day's melee, he unhorses numerous opponents and fights with a ferocity that astonishes the spectators:

> （020-028）"But who is he?" said the Black Knight; "it imports me much to know.…

> （025-043）he person calling himself the Black Knight have indeed a claim to the ho…

Declining to reveal his identity at the lists, the Black Knight departs Ashby still anonymous—an absence that allows Ivanhoe's identity as the Disinherited Knight to be temporarily misattributed.

### Alliance with the Saxons and Outlaws (Ch. 25–29)

Following the tournament, the Black Knight takes refuge with Locksley's outlaw band in the Forest of Copmanhurst. There he encounters Gurth and Wamba, whom he befriends:

> （025-045）…ust be clerk, then," said the Black Knight; and taking the letter from L…

> （020-065）…erk of Copmanhurst?" said the Black Knight.

His relationship with Locksley (Robin Hood) and the Saxon outlaws is one of mutual respect; the Black Knight treats them as worthy allies rather than social inferiors. When Front-de-Boeuf sends a letter to demand the surrender of the castle, the Black Knight participates in the council of war:

> （025-051）"I would," said the Black Knight, "there were some one among u…

### Siege of Torquilstone Castle (Ch. 28–29)

During the siege of Front-de-Boeuf's castle, the Black Knight leads the assault on the postern gate and engages Front-de-Boeuf in single combat:

> （029-041）he law! Front-de-Bœuf and the Black Knight fight hand to hand on the bre…

> （029-052）themselves right yeomanly—the Black Knight approaches the postern with h…

> （029-043）"The Black Knight," answered Rebecca, faintly; …

Rebecca's recognition of the Black Knight from the tournament—and her awareness that his presence constitutes Richard's intervention—heightens the dramatic irony of the scene. The reader knows who the Black Knight is; Rebecca begins to suspect.

### Identity Reveal and Resolution (Ch. 32–44)

The Black Knight's identity is revealed at the hut of the Hermit (actually the Clerk of Copmanhurst), where Ivanhoe encounters him and the two are joyfully reunited. Richard immediately assumes the role of king-in-exile whose return will restore order to England:

> （032-038）…culiar gratitude to the Black Champion, and earnestly entreated him …

At the novel's conclusion, Richard attempts to reconcile with Cedric the Saxon and to reward those who served him during his disguise:

> — King Richard's reconciliation with Cedric at the end of the novel resolves the Norman-Saxon conflict.

## 历史/文学背景

King Richard I (1157–1199) was a historical figure whose absence from England during the Third Crusade (1189–1192) created the political conditions that Scott exploits in *Ivanhoe*.

Richard's real-life captivity in Austria and Germany upon his return from the Holy Land (1192–1194) is briefly referenced in the novel's Dedictory Epistle, where Scott's fictional "Ivanhoe" narrator places Richard's ransom as a contemporary concern.

Scott's decision to represent Richard in disguise as the Black Knight follows a well-established literary tradition.

The "disguised king" motif appears in medieval romance from *Le Morte d'Arthur* (Arthur among the shepherds) through Shakespeare's *Henry V* (Prince Hal among the common soldiers). Scott's innovation is to make the Black Knight's identity simultaneously known and unknown: the reader recognises him early from contextual clues, while the characters within the novel debate his identity.
