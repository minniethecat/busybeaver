# Urfried (Ulrica)

Urfried—later known as Ulrica—is a Saxon noblewoman who has spent twenty years disguised as a housekeeper in Torquilstone Castle, the fortress built by her family's Norman murderers. Her revelation of identity, her account of her family's destruction, and her dramatic self-immolation in the burning castle constitute one of the most powerful scenes in *Ivanhoe*.

## 角色概述

Urfried first appears as "the old dame" or "the housekeeper" who serves the Norman knights at Torquilstone Castle. Her humble appearance and silent efficiency make her seem an unremarkable domestic servant—until her conversation with Rebecca reveals the extraordinary woman beneath:

> （024-006）"Good Dame Urfried," said the other man, "stand …

This early reference to her birth name—Urfried—hints at a Saxon identity that the castle's Norman inhabitants have not recognised. Rebecca, sheltered in the castle after being wounded at the tournament, encounters Urfried and gradually draws out her story.

Urfried's narrative is the novel's most sustained account of Norman atrocities against the Saxon people. She describes how her family—the Lords of Torquilstone—were murdered by Reginald Front-de-Boeuf and his accomplices, and how she alone survived by feigning death:

> （024-012）… the survivors of my father's house were all but myself…

Her twenty-year concealment in the castle of her enemies, as a domestic servant, represents the novel's most extreme example of Saxon suffering under Norman rule. It also provides the moral justification for her final act of vengeance.

## 外貌与形象

Urfried's physical description is deliberately ambiguous in Scott's text. She appears first as an aged woman—"old" and "withered" in the descriptions offered by the castle's Norman inhabitants—but Rebecca perceives something more:

> （024-006）"Good Dame Urfried," said the other man, "stand …

Her aged appearance is partly a deliberate disguise: Ulrica has preserved her youth and vigour through twenty years of disciplined self-concealment, and her true self emerges when she is finally provoked to reveal herself. The contrast between the humble housekeeper and the avenging Saxon noblewoman is one of the novel's most striking transformations.

## 情节中的关键行动

### First Conversation with Rebecca (Ch. 24)

Urfried's initial conversations with Rebecca in the castle are coded and elliptical. She speaks in riddles about her identity, dropping hints that gradually lead Rebecca (and the reader) toward the truth:

> （024-012）… the survivors of my father's house were all but myself…

Rebecca's sensitivity and intelligence allow her to penetrate Urfried's disguise; she is the first character in the novel to recognise Urfried's true identity and to understand her purpose.

### The Revelation (Ch. 24)

Urfried's full revelation to Rebecca is one of the novel's great narrative climaxes. She recounts the massacre of her family by Front-de-Boeuf:

> （024-030）…uess so truly," said Brian de Bois-Guilbert, dropping the mantle from his…

> （024-042）…o stood perfectly motionless, Bois-Guilbert had neither time to intercept…

> （024-047）…e dishonoured," said Brian de Bois-Guilbert, "if thou shalt have reason t…

Urfried's account is remarkable for its specificity: she names each victim, describes the manner of each death, and identifies the particular cruelty of each Norman perpetrator. The detail transforms Urfried's desire for revenge from a generic emotional response into a precisely documented historical grievance.

### The Burning of Torquilstone (Ch. 29)

Urfried's final action—setting fire to the castle—fulfils the promise of her narrative. Having communicated her story to Rebecca and secured Rebecca's understanding of her motives, she then acts:

> （029-006）…nly to this, that the Templar Bois-Guilbert, and the Baron Front-de-Bœuf,…

The burning of Torquilstone Castle is Urfried's vengeance made visible: the castle built by her family's murderers is consumed by the same element that consumed her family. Her self-immolation—she perishes in the flames—represents the self-sacrifice that Scott associates with the highest form of Saxon heroism.

## 历史/文学背景

Urfried's character draws on two distinct literary traditions. First, she is the archetypal "noble savage turned avenger"—the Saxon noblewoman reduced to servitude who recovers her identity and exacts retribution.

This motif has its origins in medieval Germanic legend (the Nibelungenlied's Kriemhild) and its modern expression in Scott's own *The Lady of the Lake* (1810) and *The Bride of Lammermoor* (1819).

Second, Urfried's role as a housekeeper-spy recalls the figure of the "faithful servant" in medieval romance—the humble domestic who is secretly of noble birth and who ultimately saves the hero through secret knowledge. Urfried subverts this convention: rather than saving a hero, she destroys the villains' fortress.

Scott's depiction of Urfried's narrative of massacre and survival has been read by critics as a commentary on the historical displacement of the Anglo-Saxon aristocracy after 1066.

Urfried's twenty-year disguise represents the persistence of Saxon identity beneath Norman superficiality—a theme that Cedric also embodies but with less dramatic intensity.
