# Justice

The concept and administration of law in the world of *Ivanhoe*, where multiple legal systems — Norman feudal law, Church canon law, Saxon custom, and the judicial duel — compete for authority. Scott uses justice (and its perversion) as a central thematic concern, testing whether chivalric ideals can produce fair outcomes.

## Competing Legal Orders

Post-Conquest England operated under layered jurisdictions. Norman barons held court over their fiefs; ecclesiastical courts claimed jurisdiction over clergy and matters of faith; and remnants of Anglo-Saxon customary law persisted among the conquered population. Cedric the Saxon laments that a Norman lord may hang a Saxon "at his own door without any form of trial" (P02-008), highlighting the legal asymmetry between conqueror and conquered.

The novel's two great trials — the tournament judgment at Ashby and the witchcraft trial at Templestowe — exemplify different modes of justice. The tournament, governed by heralds and marshals, represents chivalric justice at its most ritualised. The Templestowe trial, by contrast, reveals ecclesiastical justice at its most corrupt: the Grand Master of the Templars condemns Rebecca based on superstition and coerced testimony.

## Trial by Combat

The judicial duel — *wager of battle* — was the ultimate appeal in medieval law. The theory held that God would grant victory to the righteous cause. In *Ivanhoe*, Rebecca demands trial by combat as her final recourse: "I challenge the privilege of trial by combat, and will appear by my champion" (037-052). The scene crystallises the novel's treatment of justice: a system that claims divine sanction but depends on the skill and availability of a champion.

Ivanhoe's appearance as Rebecca's champion — wounded, exhausted, barely able to sit his horse — is the novel's most dramatic assertion that justice, however imperfectly administered, can sometimes prevail. Bois-Guilbert's death, attributed by the narrator to "the violence of his own contending passions" rather than Ivanhoe's lance, suggests that divine judgment operates through moral rather than physical causality.

> "I challenge the privilege of trial by combat, and will appear by my champion. It is enough that I challenge the trial by combat — there lies my gage." (037-052)

> "To whom, noble sir, but to this woman, the benefit of the trial by combat — but though a Jewess and an unbeliever, she shall have her champion." (038-014)
