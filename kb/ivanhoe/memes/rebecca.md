# Rebecca

Rebecca, the daughter of the Jewish merchant [[Isaac of York]], is one of the most compelling figures in *Ivanhoe*. Her beauty, intelligence, and moral fortitude contrast sharply with the bigotry of medieval Christian society, and her trial for sorcery at [[Templestowe Preceptory|Templestowe]] constitutes the novel's most powerful critique of religious intolerance. Though ultimately reprieved through the intervention of [[Wilfred of Ivanhoe|Ivanhoe]], who champions her in the [[Trial by Combat|wager of battle]], Rebecca's story ends with her departure from England, having refused to abandon her faith even for the man she loves[P01-039][P07-016][P07-022].

## Character and Virtue

Rebecca is introduced at the Tournament at Ashby, where her beauty immediately captivates the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert. Unlike the novel's Christian women—Rowena, Ulrica—Rebecca is distinguished by her active intelligence: she reads medicine, tends to the wounded Ivanhoe when he is disguised as a palmer, and debates theology with Bois-Guilbert on equal terms. Her father Isaac observes that she "was seated on a heap of embroidered cushions, surrounded by all the apparatus of Eastern luxury" yet her character remains modest and devout[P10-031][P10-034][P10-036].

> "My father," answered Rebecca, "we also have advantages. The same God who has given them power has given us patience and fortitude."[P10-036]

> "Assuredly," said Rebecca, "you shall not repent you of your confidence. Place your daughter under the protection of the blessed Virgin, and of the good St. John of [[Templestowe Preceptory|Templestowe]], and fear nothing."[P10-040]

## Relationship with Ivanhoe

Rebecca's bond with Ivanhoe begins when she and Isaac encounter the wounded knight (disguised as a palmer) after the Tournament. She nurses him back to health using her knowledge of Eastern medicine, establishing the emotional core of the novel: a love that can never be fulfilled because of the religious divide between Jew and Christian. When Bois-Guilbert pursues her, she repeatedly rejects him, declaring that she would "rather die than become the prey of a man who has no belief in the faith of her fathers"[P09-024][P09-030][P09-031].

| Aspect | Rebecca's Stance | Contrast |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Faith | Devout Jewess, refuses conversion | Opposed by Christian bigotry |
| Love for Ivanhoe | Deep but unrequited | Ivanhoe bound to Rowena by duty |
| Response to Bois-Guilbert | Defiant rejection | He sees her as prey/trophy |
| Fate | Exile from England | Ivanhoe remains |

## The Trial at Templestowe

Accused by Lucas Beaumanoir, the bigoted Grand Master of the Templars, of using sorcery to enchant Bois-Guilbert, Rebecca is imprisoned at [[Templestowe Preceptory]]. She defends herself with eloquence, citing Hebrew scripture and appealing to natural justice. When the trial by combat is decreed, she chooses Ivanhoe as her champion, fully aware that Bois-Guilbert is the Templars' finest fighter. Ivanhoe's victory—Bois-Guilbert dying mid-combat from natural causes—saves Rebecca, but she refuses to exploit the victory to force a Christian marriage, choosing instead to rejoin her father and leave England for the East[P10-066][P10-067].

> "I am the daughter of that people to whom God gave his oracles, and from whom the Saviour of the world himself sprang."[P10-066]

## Legacy and Significance

Rebecca stands as Scott's most nuanced exploration of religious tolerance in the novel. Through her, Scott critiques the hypocrisy of medieval Christendom while celebrating the universal virtues of courage, loyalty, and compassion. Her departure from England at the novel's close—"bearing with her the gratitude of the brave Ivanhoe and the blessings of her father"—leaves the reader with the sense that the true moral victory belongs not to the Norman conquerors but to the Jewess who preserved her integrity against overwhelming odds[P10-042].
