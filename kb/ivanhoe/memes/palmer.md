# The Palmer

The Palmer is the pilgrim disguise assumed by Wilfred of Ivanhoe upon his clandestine return from the Holy Land. Disguised as a humble pilgrim, the Palmer travels through the north of England observing Saxon-Norman relations and gathering intelligence before his appearance at the Tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouche.

## 角色概述

The Palmer first appears in Chapter 2 at the meeting between Cedric the Saxon and his retinue at the town of Sheffield, where he seeks guidance from Wamba the Jester:

> （002-064）"A Palmer, just returned from the Holy …

> （002-066）…end Sir Knight," answered the Palmer, to whom the appearance of th…

His designation as "a Palmer, just returned from the Holy Land" immediately identifies him as a veteran of the Crusades—a veteran whose particular identity the novel conceals until much later. The Palmer's knowledge of the Holy Land and his physical condition (wounded, wearied from travel) confirm his authenticity.

The Palmer's disguise serves a specific narrative function: as a pilgrim, he can move through Norman territory without raising suspicion and observe the political situation without being recognised.

His apparent anonymity also allows Scott to establish the Norman-Saxon tension through dialogue between the Palmer and characters such as Wamba and Gurth, who represent the Saxon underclass.

## 外貌与形象

Scott describes the Palmer as a "man of tall and commanding stature, though the extent of his personal strength could only be conjectured from the bulk and sinewy appearance of his limbs." His pilgrim's habit—a coarse woollen gown with the palm-branch emblem of a returned crusader—conceals the physique of a knight:

> （005-035）… for you, if you will, friend Palmer," said Wamba.

> （005-044）"The sixth," said the Palmer, after a pause, in which he s…

> （005-045）"Sir Palmer," said Sir Brian de Bois-Guil…

The Palmer's physical presence is that of a man accustomed to command: when he answers "Sir Brian de Bois-Guilbert" correctly to a question about the sixth Knight of the Tournament, his confidence marks him as someone who knows the tournament's composition—suggesting he is no ordinary pilgrim but a trained military observer.

## 情节中的关键行动

### Journey Through Yorkshire (Ch. 2–5)

The Palmer's journey takes him from Sheffield toward Coningsburgh, accompanied by Wamba and Gurth. During this journey, he gathers information about the tournament and the political situation:

> （005-048）"This reliquary," said the Palmer, taking a small ivory box fro…

The Palmer displays a reliquary containing relics from the Holy Land—a plot device that both confirms his crusader credentials and establishes the novel's motif of sacred objects carrying narrative weight. The reliquary becomes the mechanism by which the Palmer later reveals himself to Isaac of York and ultimately to Rebecca.

### The Tournament at Ashby (Ch. 8–16)

At the Tournament, the Palmer is a spectator rather than a competitor—his disguise forbidding open combat. However, he strategically deploys the reliquary to secure Isaac of York's assistance for Rebecca, who has been wounded by a fall during the lists:

> （006-003）As the Palmer, lighted by a domestic with a…

The Palmer's intervention on Rebecca's behalf initiates one of the novel's most important narrative threads: the bond between Rebecca and her unknown benefactor, who is later revealed to be the Disinherited Knight himself.

### The Siege of Torquilstone (Ch. 28–29)

Though the Palmer's disguise is largely abandoned once he fights as the Black Knight at Torquilstone, his earlier identity as a pilgrim connects to the novel's larger moral framework.

The Palmer—like the Black Knight—represents a Christianity tempered by contact with the Holy Land: tolerant, pragmatic, and opposed to the fanaticism of characters like Bois-Guilbert and the Grand Master.

## 历史/文学背景

The pilgrim's habit—bearing a palm-branch from the Holy Land—was a genuine medieval practice: crusaders who returned from Jerusalem carried palm fronds as proof of their pilgrimage, and the palm became a recognised symbol of the returned crusader. Walter Scott's contemporary readers would have immediately recognised the Palmer's significance from this emblem.

The "disguised hero" narrative convention, of which the Palmer is a classic instance, has deep roots in medieval romance.

Scott's particular variation—making the hero's disguise a religious pilgrim's habit rather than a courtly disguise—grounds *Ivanhoe* in the novel's central concern with religious identity and its social consequences. The Palmer's ease among both Saxons and Normans, Christians and Jews, reflects Scott's vision of a post-Crusade England that must learn religious tolerance.
