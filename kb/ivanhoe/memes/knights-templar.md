# Knights Templar

The Knights Templar (officially the Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and of the Temple of Solomon) were a Catholic military order founded in 1119 to protect Christian pilgrims travelling to the Holy Land. In *Ivanhoe*, Scott depicts the Templars as a powerful and politically influential order whose members—above all Brian de Bois-Guilbert—embody the dangerous intersection of religious fanaticism, feudal ambition, and personal desire. The Temple at [[Templestowe Preceptory|Templestowe]], the novel's English headquarters, becomes the setting for Rebecca's trial and Bois-Guilbert's final confrontation with Ivanhoe[P01-016][P02-032][P02-040][P03-021].

## Historical Context

The Templars were founded by nine French knights in Jerusalem shortly after the First Crusade, taking vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience while committing themselves to the defence of the Holy Land. By the late 12th century, when *Ivanhoe* is set, the order had grown into one of the most powerful institutions in Christendom—owning vast estates across Europe, operating a primitive banking system, and fielding elite military units. Pope Innocent II granted the Templars exemptions from tithes and the right to build their own churches, making them answerable only to the papacy[P02-032][P03-021].

## The Templars in Ivanhoe

Scott's portrayal of the Templars is deeply ambivalent. On one hand, the order represents the martial ideals of the Crusades—courage, discipline, and devotion to a sacred cause. On the other, it reveals the corruption that power and fanaticism can breed. Bois-Guilbert, the order's most prominent representative, is "a man of gallantry" whose pursuit of Rebecca reduces chivalric courtesy to predatory lust. Lucas Beaumanoir, the Grand Master of the English Templars, is a figure of chilling bigotry whose persecution of Rebecca exposes the order's capacity for cruelty in the name of faith[P02-040][P02-044][P02-046][P36-028].

> "He is of the order of Knights Templars, whom you may have heard of; and he is now on his way to the Preceptory of Templestowe, which is situated in the neighbourhood of Ashby-de-la-Zouche." — The description of the Templar knight at Rotherwood[P02-032].

> "Prior Aymer," said the Templar, "you are a man of gallantry; I pray you to tell me whether the beauty of this Jewess is not sufficient to make even a Templar forget his vow?" — Bois-Guilbert's cynical test of his own order's discipline[P02-044].

## Bois-Guilbert: The Templar as Tragic Antagonist

Brian de Bois-Guilbert is the novel's most complex villain—a man torn between his Templar vows and his passion for Rebecca. His fatal obsession drives the novel's central crisis: he arranges Rebecca's seizure, conspires with Front-de-Boeuf, and ultimately demands that Beaumanoir put her on trial for sorcery. Yet his final scene—dying in the lists at Templestowe, his last words a confused cry for Rebecca—reveals a man destroyed by the very passions his order was sworn to suppress. Through Bois-Guilbert, Scott interrogates the compatibility of religious austerity and human desire[P07-017][P09-024][P36-028].

## Conflict with Authority

The Templars in *Ivanhoe* exist in uneasy tension with both secular and ecclesiastical authority. Prince John manipulates the Templars for political gain, while Beaumanoir asserts the order's independence from both king and bishop. This autonomy—rooted in the Templars' direct answerability to the Pope—makes them a state-within-a-state, capable of defying English law when it conflicts with their own interests. Rebecca's trial at Templestowe, conducted under Templar jurisdiction, exposes the dangers of such unchecked power[P02-040][P36-028].

## Legacy and Disbandment

Scott's depiction of the Templars draws on the historical record of their suppression. Philip IV of France, motivated by debt and political rivalry, arrested all Templars in his kingdom in 1307, extracting confessions under torture and ultimately persuading the Pope to disband the order in 1312. Scott's novel, set in 1194, dramatises the tensions—between martial valour and religious hypocrisy, between individual desire and institutional discipline—that would ultimately destroy the Templars as a political force[P03-021].
