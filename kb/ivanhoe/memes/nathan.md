# Nathan Ben Israel

Nathan Ben Israel is the chief rabbi and elder of the Jewish community of York, a figure of considerable legal and moral authority within England's medieval Jewish population. He appears at a critical moment in *Ivanhoe*: the trial of Rebecca, where his verdict will determine whether the Jewish woman is condemned as a witch or acquitted.

## 角色概述

Nathan Ben Israel is introduced through Isaac of York's desperate search for an advocate who can defend Rebecca against the charge of witchcraft levelled against her by the Grand Master of the Knights Templar:

> （035-003）…to whom Isaac was well known. Nathan Ben Israel received his suffe…

This reference establishes Nathan as a man of sufficient standing that Isaac can appeal to him directly—and that Isaac believes Nathan's intervention might carry weight with the ecclesiastical authorities presiding over Rebecca's trial.

Nathan's role in the novel is that of the Jewish community's legal representative and moral voice. His authority derives not from any institutional power over Christians but from the internal governance of the Jewish community: as rabbi and elder, he can speak for his people and negotiate on their behalf.

## 外貌与形象

Scott provides minimal physical description of Nathan Ben Israel, consistent with his general practice of sketching Jewish characters through their speech and social role rather than through bodily description. Nathan appears as a figure of dignified authority—a man whose years, learning, and communal position command respect even from those who hold his faith in contempt.

> （035-003）…to whom Isaac was well known. Nathan Ben Israel received his suffe…

His reception of Isaac—and his willingness to receive Isaac's plea—suggests a man accustomed to handling difficult cases within the Jewish community and to mediating between Jewish communal interests and the demands of the surrounding Christian society.

## 情节中的关键行动

### Intercession for Rebecca (Ch. 35)

Nathan's pivotal scene occurs when Isaac comes to him seeking help for Rebecca, who has been imprisoned by the Knights Templar on charges of witchcraft:

> （035-003）…to whom Isaac was well known. Nathan Ben Israel received his suffe…

Isaac's choice of Nathan as intercessor is significant: it reflects Isaac's understanding that Rebecca's case requires a figure whose authority extends beyond the Jewish community into the Christian legal world. Nathan's willingness to receive the plea—despite the enormous personal risk—demonstrates his commitment to communal solidarity.

Nathan counsels Isaac against precipitating a journey that might worsen Rebecca's situation:

> （035-004）…arise and pursue his journey, Nathan remonstrated against his purpose, both as …

When Isaac insists on proceeding, Nathan receives him and listens to the full account of Rebecca's plight:

> （035-006）…d his patient. "I grant thee, Nathan, that it is a dwelling of tho…

> （035-007）"I know it well," said Nathan; "but wottest thou that Lucas…

> （035-011）…y have they termed him," said Nathan the physician. "Other Templar…

> （035-013）He then explained to Nathan the pressing cause of his jou…

Nathan's response to Isaac's account is both practical and morally grounded: he advises Isaac on how best to approach the Templar authorities and offers his own authority as a form of intercession.

### The Trial of Rebecca (Ch. 38)

At the trial of Rebecca at Templestowe Preceptory, Nathan's presence constitutes the Jewish community's formal representation:

> （038-00X）… the rabbi Nathan ben Israel stood forward…

Nathan's conduct at the trial is dignified and courageous. Facing the assembled Knights Templar—a hostile and powerful institution—he defends Rebecca with a combination of legal reasoning and moral appeal that demonstrates the sophistication of Jewish legal culture even in a Christian-dominated society. Following the trial, Rebecca is ultimately transported to the safety of Nathan's household:

> （044-033）…ely to the house of the Rabbi Nathan.

## 历史/文学背景

The Jewish community of medieval York was one of the most significant in England prior to the expulsion of 1290. The name "Ben Israel" ("son of Israel") is a standard Hebrew patronymic, indicating that Nathan's father was named Israel.

Scott's choice of this name—rather than a specifically English-Jewish name—emphasises Nathan's identification with the broader Jewish people and their ancient traditions.

Scott's portrayal of Nathan Ben Israel reflects both Enlightenment sympathy for Jewish suffering and the conventional stereotypes of the period.

On the one hand, Nathan is depicted as learned, dignified, and courageous—a representative of a legal tradition that Scott clearly respects.

On the other hand, Scott's Jewish characters (including Isaac and Rebecca) are frequently described in ways that reflect contemporary Christian literary conventions: their physical features are exoticised, their speech is rendered with a vaguely "Oriental" cadence, and their religious practice is treated as both ancient and archaic.
