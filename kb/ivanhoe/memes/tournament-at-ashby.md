# Tournament at Ashby

The Tournament at Ashby (formally the "Passage of Arms at Ashby-de-la-Zouche") is the grand chivalric spectacle at the heart of *Ivanhoe*'s opening movement. Organised by Prince John near the town of Ashby in Leicestershire, the tournament brings together the Norman aristocracy of England for three days of martial display, political intrigue, and romantic encounter. It is here that the mysterious Disinherited Knight—Wilfred of [[Wilfred of Ivanhoe|Ivanhoe]] in disguise—wins the prize and establishes his knightly renown, while [[Rebecca]] first enters the novel's emotional core[P02-048][P03-021][P07-006][P07-007].

## Organisation and Setting

The tournament is announced as a "Passage of Arms" to be held "not far from Ashby-de-la-Zouche, on the second day of the ensuing month" (Ch. III). Prince John, seeking to consolidate Norman support against the absent King Richard, sponsors the event with characteristic extravagance. The lists are prepared "within a mile of the town of Ashby," an extensive meadow chosen for its capacity to hold thousands of spectators. [[Brian-de-Bois-Guilbert]], "whose renown in all games of chivalry" was unrivalled, leads the challengers, while the mysterious Disinherited Knight appears as the sole Saxon defender[P07-009][P09-039][P09-048][P09-052].

| Day | Combatants | Key Outcomes |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| 1st | Challengers vs. defenders | Several knights unhorsed; Bois-Guilbert dominant |
| 2nd | Disinherited Knight vs. all comers | Ivanhoe unhorses Bois-Guilbert, de Bracy, Front-de-Boeuf |
| 3rd | Final passage | Disinherited Knight declared champion |

## The Disinherited Knight's Triumph

Ivanhoe, still in disguise and recovering from wounds sustained on crusade, enters the lists on the second day as "the Knight of Ivanhoe" or "the Disinherited Knight." His identity is unknown to the spectators, but his prowess is unmistakable. He defeats Bois-Guilbert, Maurice de Bracy, and Front-de-Boeuf in succession, each victory accompanied by the heralds' cry and the acclaim of the crowd. Cedric the Saxon, watching from the stands, is torn between pride in his son's martial skill and horror at his adoption of Norman customs[P06-052][P07-016][P14-013][P14-019][P14-021].

> "The Knight of Ivanhoe is like to hear evil tidings concerning his own fortunes" — a prophecy that foreshadows Cedric's disowning of his son[P05-049].

> "I will maintain the cause of the fair Jewess with my body and my honour, as a good knight ought to do for a lady in distress." — Rebecca's plea, echoed through the tournament's aftermath[P09-030].

## Rebecca and the Tournament

Rebecca's presence at Ashby, as the companion of her father Isaac, introduces the novel's most sustained exploration of religious difference. Her beauty captivates Bois-Guilbert, setting in motion the fatal obsession that will drive the novel's central crisis. Her observation of Ivanhoe's victories—and her tender care for him when he is wounded—establishes the emotional bond that will define her character. The tournament thus functions simultaneously as chivalric spectacle, political theatre, and the site of a love that cannot be acknowledged[P07-016][P07-022][P07-026][P07-029][P08-004][P08-006].

| Character | Role at Tournament | Significance |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Wilfred of Ivanhoe | Disinherited Knight (disguised) | Wins the prize; establishes chivalric renown |
| Rebecca | Spectator, later nurse | Begins her tragic attachment to Ivanhoe |
| Brian de Bois-Guilbert | Challenger leader | Defeated by Ivanhoe; obsessed with Rebecca |
| Prince John | Sponsor/patron | Uses tournament to rally Norman barons |
| King Richard | Black Knight (disguised) | Tests loyalty of subjects; intervenes covertly |
| Cedric the Saxon | Spectator | Torn between paternal pride and Saxon principle |

## Political and Dramatic Functions

The tournament serves as the novel's central narrative hinge. It brings together all the principal characters in a single location, sets in motion the love quadrangle (Ivanhoe–Rowena–Rebecca–Bois-Guilbert), and establishes the political conflict between Norman usurpers (Prince John) and the rightful king (Richard). Scott's detailed description of the lists, the costumes, the heralds' cries, and the crowd's reactions creates a vivid evocation of medieval public spectacle that remains one of the novel's most celebrated passages[P06-003][P06-012][P06-013][P06-014][P06-015].
