# Faubourg Saint-Germain Heist

The midnight burglary of [[madame-omber|Madame Hélène Omber]]'s mansion in the Faubourg Saint-Germain district of Passy, executed by [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] — [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] — with [[lucy-shannon|Lucy Shannon]] as his lookout. Covering Chapters XV and XVI ("Sheer Impudence" and "Restitution"), the heist represents [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s return to his craft not for profit but for restitution.

## Preparation

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] explains the [[criminal-code|professional ethics]] of his trade to [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] with characteristic dry humour: "The gentle art of housebreaking has its technique, you know, its [[criminal-code|professional ethics]]: we can't well violate the privacy of a house before midnight" ([015-003]). They wait in a café until the appointed hour, [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] writing notes while [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] keeps watch for Pack agents through the window.

The target is the Passy mansion of [[madame-omber|Madame Omber]] — "a souvenir of those days when Passy had been suburban. A survival of the Revolution, a vast, dour pile" ([016-008]). Its walls are aged but its contents are legendary: "the work of generations of passionate collectors" — paintings, furniture, and a famous safe containing jewels ([016-040]).

## The Heist

In the darkness of the mansion, "the obscurity on every hand seemed alive with stealthy footfalls, whispers, rustlings, sighs" ([016-035]). [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] navigates by torchlight, moving "confidently" through rooms he has studied in advance. He parts heavy draperies, swings aside a massive screen, and uncovers "the face of a considerable built-in safe, stoutly constructed of steel" ([016-042]).

At the safe, he works with the precision of a master. The pigeonholes are "stored tight with an extraordinary array of leather jewel-cases, packets of stout paper bound with red tape, and boxes of diverse sizes and shapes" ([016-059]). He finds "nothing but diamond jewellery, mostly in antique settings" — [[madame-omber|Madame Omber]]'s lesser collection, left behind when she took her finest pieces to [[london|England]] ([016-062]).

## The Tempation

The moment of temptation arrives when [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] opens a case of diamonds. From the first, "the light smote radiance in blinding, coruscant welter" ([016-062]). The old instinct stirs — "that mutinous and rebellious spirit, [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]" — but he masters it. He takes only what is needed for restitution, not for gain ([016-070]).

This self-denial is the heist's true purpose. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is not stealing; he is returning what was stolen. The jewels are destined for their owner through the intermediary of the French Minister of War, not for any [[paris|Paris]] fence.

## Escape Through the Faubourg

The exit is as methodical as the entry: drapes restored, screen replaced, safe closed, all traces eliminated. "They had to walk as far as the [[trocadero-apartment|Trocadéro]] before [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] found a fiacre, which he later dismissed at the corner in the Faubourg St. Germain" ([016-083]).

## Significance

The Faubourg Saint-Germain heist is the novel's demonstration that skill is morally neutral — the same hands that once stole for profit now steal for justice. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s technical mastery remains undiminished, but its purpose has inverted. The heist also cements [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]]'s role: she is no longer a passive observer of [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s world but an active participant, sharing both its danger and its strange exhilaration.

The chapter title "Restitution" (Chapter XVI) makes the moral frame explicit: this is not theft but restoration, not crime but its undoing.
