# Bourke

An Irishman of mysterious means and uncertain temper who gave [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] his name, his first foothold in respectable society, and his introduction to the shadow world between gentility and crime.

## Overview

Bourke is the man who first said "that even if you knew your way about [[paris|Paris]] you had to lose it in order to find it to [[troyons|Troyon's]]" — a paradox that perfectly captures his role in the novel. He is the gateway figure: the one who brought the child [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] to [[troyons|Troyon's]], gave him a name, and set him on the path that would lead to [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]. (001-001) (001-058)

## Background

Bourke was proud to be Irish, spoke "the purest English known," and had received most of his education at Dublin University. He was "a quick, compact, dangerous little Irishman who had fallen into the habit of 'resting' at [[troyons|Troyon's]] whenever a vacation from [[london|London]] seemed a prescription apt to prove wholesome." His daily routine varied but slightly — breakfasting abed around half-past ten — and his red hair was "the spring of his secret sorrow," a mark by which he was known. (001-034) (001-035) (001-036)

## Role in the Story

Bourke appears primarily in the novel's opening chapters, establishing the world of [[troyons|Troyon's]] and [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s place within it. When the [[the-omber-affair|Omber jewel theft]] brings [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] to [[paris|Paris]], Bourke hastily packs to leave "[[paris|Paris]], [[paris|France]] and Europe by the fastest feasible route" — a revealing reaction that hints at his own ambiguous relationship with the law. (001-057)

Though Bourke largely exits the narrative after the early chapters, his influence persists: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s gentlemanly manner, his name, and his knowledge of how to move between social worlds all trace back to Bourke's tutelage.
