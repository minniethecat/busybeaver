# Ducroy

Monsieur Hector Ducroy, the French Minister of War, a pompous and bewildered official whose midnight encounter with [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] triggers a chain of events leading to the novel's aerial climax.

## Overview

Ducroy is the embodiment of official [[paris|France]] — dignified, slow-witted, and easily flustered when confronted outside the comfortable rituals of his office. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s nocturnal visit to the Minister's bedroom, bearing stolen war plans that prove a German spy ring has penetrated the French military, is one of the novel's most memorable set-pieces. Ducroy's subsequent role in arranging the aviation demonstration at [[port-aviation|Port Aviation]] proves pivotal to the story's resolution. (016-022)

## Background

Established behind his splendid mahogany desk at the Ministère de la Guerre, Ducroy moves through life with the assurance of a man who has never been seriously challenged. He is described as lending "the dignity of his presence to some formal ceremony in that beautiful uniform which appertained unto his office." His world is one of protocol and procedure — utterly unprepared for a midnight intrusion by a notorious criminal bearing state secrets. (017-001)

## Role in the Story

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] first mentions Ducroy as his intended target: he plans to break into the Minister's residence to deliver information "to a person of considerable consequence — nobody less, in fact, than Monsieur Ducroy" (016-022). This initiates the extraordinary scene in which [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] enters Ducroy's bedroom, presents him with stolen military documents, and forces the flustered Minister to examine photographic evidence of German espionage.

The scene plays as high comedy: Ducroy splutters, goggles resentfully, and protests while [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] patiently explains that [[paris|France]]'s war plans have been photographed by enemy agents. When Ducroy finally grasps the gravity of the situation, his transformation is immediate. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] entrusts him with the recovered plans: "You *must* accomplish it, Monsieur Ducroy — for if you fail, you make yourself the instrument of my death" (017-030).

Later in the novel, Ducroy redeems his earlier befuddlement by arranging the aviation test at [[port-aviation|Port Aviation]], providing [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] with the military aircraft and facilities needed to demonstrate the stolen engine design that proves critical to the plot's climax (025-003). He appears at the airfield in fur-lined coat and shining top-hat, "all the majesty of Mars incarnate," chatting with [[captain-vauquelin|Captain Vauquelin]] before the test flight. As the crowd gathers, Ducroy lifts his hat in salute, cries "Bon voyage!" and "scuttled clear like a startled rooster before a motorcar" (025-020).

His bargain with [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] becomes a point of tension in the final chapter, when [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] reveals that [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] and [[ekstrom|Ekstrom]] learned of the arrangement and spent each morning in the hangars at St. Germain, setting the stage for the novel's final confrontation (027-040).
