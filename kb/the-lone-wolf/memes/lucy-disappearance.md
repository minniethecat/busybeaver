# Lucy's Disappearance

The unexplained disappearance of [[lucy-shannon|Lucy Shannon]] during the events of Chapter XVIII ("Enigma"), when she vanishes from the garden of the [[impasse-stanislas|Impasse Stanislas]] hideout without a word, leaving [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] to search [[paris|Paris]] for three anguished days.

## The Vanishing

After the escape from the [[impasse-stanislas|Impasse Stanislas]] and the [[rive-droite-confrontation|Rive Droite]] confrontation, [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] leaves [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] briefly in a garden. When he returns, she is gone. He "found no reason to believe she had left him other than voluntarily" — no sign of struggle, no evidence of abduction by [[the-pack|the Pack]] ([018-002]).

The absence of explanation is its own cruelty. He tells himself there is "every reason to believe she hadn't left him to go back to [[bannon|Bannon]]; concerning whom she had expressed herself too forcibly to excuse a surmise that she had preferred his protection to [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]'s" ([018-003]).

## Three Days of Searching

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] spends three days scouring [[paris|Paris]]. "It wasn't easy; drowsiness buzzed in his brain and weighted his eyelids; now and again, involuntarily, he nodded over his glass of black coffee" ([018-007]). By the close of the third day, he is "ready to concede that she had managed to escape without his aid" ([018-012]).

His search takes him past [[troyons|Troyon's]], now "nothing more than a gaping excavation choked with ashes and charred timbers" — the physical destruction of his childhood home mirroring the emotional devastation of her absence ([018-014]).

## The Discovery

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] finds [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] by chance — or fate — while driving his taxicab past a jeweller's shop on the boulevard Malesherbes. Looking through a plate-glass window, he sees her inside: transformed, dressed in fine clothes, seated at a desk. "A great wonder obsessed him that she should have opened that door to him no less than that he should have entered through it" ([018-059]).

When he demands, "Why did you do that?" — meaning, why did you leave — [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] answers: "I couldn't go without knowing what had become of you" ([018-076]).

## The Explanation

Pressed further, [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]] resists: "I can't tell you... I mean, I don't know how to tell you!" ([018-079]). She reveals only that she left because of a "repugnance for criminal associations" — not rejection of [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] himself, but fear of the world he inhabits ([018-112]).

The scene ends in unresolved anguish: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] steps out of the cab, "standing with the pose, with the gesture of one who has just heard his sentence of death pronounced" ([018-144]).

## Significance

[[lucy-shannon|Lucy]]'s disappearance is the emotional fulcrum of the novel's second half. It dramatizes the cost of [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s criminal past: not legal punishment, but the barrier it erects between him and love. The three days of searching are his purgatory — a trial by absence that tests the sincerity of his reformation.

The reunion scene in the jeweller's shop, with its plate glass barrier between the lovers and the [[the-underworld|underworld]], is one of Vance's most visually symbolic passages: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] can see his redemption but cannot yet touch it.
