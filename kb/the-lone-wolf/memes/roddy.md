# Roddy

A habitué of [[troyons|Troyon's restaurant]] who appears harmless and inconsequential but secretly serves as an informant for [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], his surveillance contributing to the exposure of [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]]'s identity as [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]].

## Overview

Roddy is the first face [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] sees upon returning to [[troyons|Troyon's]] after years abroad — "almost the first face he saw turned his way was that of Roddy" ([002-001]). He is described as "a square-set man with a square jaw and a square manner" ([003-011]), a figure of "essentially British intelligence—something as self-contained as a [[london|London]] flat" ([004-006]). A man who drinks too much, talks too freely, and seems of no consequence to anyone — an appearance that makes him one of the most dangerous presences in the novel. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] has known him for years and never considered him a threat. This underestimation proves costly.

## Role in the Story

Roddy is present at [[troyons|Troyon's]] from the moment of [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s return in Chapter II. His initial reaction is telling: "an almost imperceptible narrowing of Roddy's eyes when they met his own, as if the Englishman were struggling with a faulty memory" ([002-003]). [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]], preoccupied with his own concerns, dismisses the warning sign.

Throughout the evening, Roddy positions himself to observe. He "paused long enough to instruct the vestiaire to have a fire laid in his room" ([003-007]), establishing his presence at the hotel. At dinner, he "had ordered a meal that matched his aspect well—both of true British simplicity" ([003-011]), all the while methodically trailing his quarry.

Roddy overhears the conversation in which [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s identity as [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] is discussed, and "Roddy was listening" ([003-102]). This single act of passive betrayal — he does not actively seek the information but does not hesitate to sell it — sets in motion [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]'s renewed pursuit of [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] observes Roddy through a mirror's reflection: "a sidelong glance into a mirror at his elbow showed Roddy still absorbed in the newspaper" ([003-015]) — a pose of studied indifference that conceals his true attention.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s counter-manoeuvre exploits his intimate knowledge of [[troyons|Troyon's]] architecture: "[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] knew that warren which was [[troyons|Troyon's]] as no one else knew it; Roddy would find it hard to detect his going" ([003-025]).

## Character

Roddy is defined by weakness rather than malice. He drifts between patrons, selling information to whoever pays. [[the-pack|The Pack]] uses him; [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] uses him; and he serves both without loyalty to either. This makes him, in his way, more dangerous than a principled antagonist: he can be neither reasoned with nor trusted.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s assessment of Roddy's limitations is ironically incomplete: "considering Roddy, he believed it to be impossible to gauge the limitations of that essentially British intelligence" ([004-006]). The things [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] cannot gauge prove to be the most dangerous.

## Fate

[[the-pack|The Pack]] ultimately eliminates Roddy when he becomes a liability — "Roddy proving a menace to [[the-pack|the Pack]] and requiring elimination" ([010-034]). His death is treated with indifference by all parties, a testament to his place at the margins of every world he inhabited.
