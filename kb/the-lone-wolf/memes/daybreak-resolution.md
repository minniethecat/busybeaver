# Daybreak Resolution

The climactic revelation of Chapter XXVII ("Daybreak"), in which the blackmailer [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] discloses his true identity — not a criminal but a [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] special agent who has spent years undercover in the European [[the-underworld|underworld]]. This revelation retroactively transforms the novel's entire conflict.

## The Revelation

The Englishman smiles cheerfully at [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s astonishment: "'[[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]' isn't my name, but it is so closely identified with my — ah — insinuative personality as to warrant the misapprehension" ([027-003]).

His work in [[paris|Paris]] completed, he can now discard the persona: "there's no saying when it may not be convenient to be able to go back without establishing a new identity" ([027-005]). The blackmailer, the syndicate organizer, the enemy who dispatched [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] to murder [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] — all of it was a cover.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] demands confirmation: "[[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], eh?" ([027-007]). [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] bows: "Special agent" ([027-008]).

## The Recontextualization

[[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] explains his infiltration: "I had to establish myself firmly as a shining light of the swell mob over here before [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] would take any notice of me" ([027-010]). Every interaction between [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] and [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is recast — the blackmail was a test, the murder attempt perhaps a miscalculation by [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] acting beyond orders, the [[the-pack|International Underworld]] Unlimited a fiction invented to gather intelligence.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s bitter response — "I might have guessed, if I'd had the wit of a goose!" — acknowledges that the clues were always present: [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]'s intimate knowledge of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] operations, his English mannerisms, his peculiar immunity from prosecution ([027-009]).

## The Blurring of Lines

The revelation is the novel's final and most radical moral inversion. The criminal [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] sought reformation; the respectable [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] was a police agent all along — yet [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]'s methods (blackmail, conspiracy, association with murderers) were indistinguishable from crime. The distinction between law and lawlessness collapses entirely.

[[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] acknowledges this with characteristic understatement: "I did pull your leg — didn't I? But not more than our other friends" ([027-010]). The police agent and the master thief have been playing the same game by the same rules; only their institutional affiliations differ.

## Lanyard's Position

For [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]], the revelation offers both vindication and danger. His enemy was never truly his enemy — but a police agent's report could still destroy him. The resolution leaves his fate suspended between [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]'s gratitude for his unwitting assistance and its obligation to arrest a notorious jewel thief.

## Significance

"Daybreak" as a chapter title carries multiple meanings: the literal dawn after the Channel crossing, the revelation of hidden truths, and the possibility of a new beginning for [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]. The resolution does not provide closure — [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is neither arrested nor exonerated — but instead offers the more complex ending of ambiguous possibility.
