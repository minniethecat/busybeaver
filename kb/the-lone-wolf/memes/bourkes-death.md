# Bourke's Death

The death of Bourke — [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]]'s earliest associate in [[paris|Paris]] and his closest friend — serves as the emotional catalyst for the events of Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*.

## Overview

[[bourke|Bourke]] is described as "a quick, compact, dangerous little Irishman" who was [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s first real connection in the [[paris|Paris]]ian [[the-underworld|underworld]]. He "first said that even if you knew your way about [[paris|Paris]] you had to lose it in order to find it to [[troyons|Troyon's]]" ([001-001]). His death, occurring before the novel's main action, leaves a void that draws [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] back to [[paris|Paris]].

The novel opens with the statement that [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s return was "an enterprise which he had been contemplating for several years — in fact, ever since the death of Bourke" ([002-001]). This places [[bourke|Bourke]]'s death as the primary motivation for the entire narrative.

## Bourke and Lanyard

[[bourke|Bourke]] was more than a criminal associate — he was family to the orphaned [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]. "Receiving most of his education in Dublin University, [[bourke|Bourke]] had the habit of 'resting' at [[troyons|Troyon's]] whenever a vacation from [[london|London]] permitted" ([001-034]–[001-035]). He gave [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] his last breakfast at [[troyons|Troyon's]] before departing — and was killed shortly afterward.

The nature of [[bourke|Bourke]]'s death is only hinted at. An Irishman with enemies, he was shot: "This was no chance shot, and took the Irishman between wind and water" ([001-052]). The killing is never fully explained, contributing to the novel's atmosphere of shadowy threats and unfinished business.
