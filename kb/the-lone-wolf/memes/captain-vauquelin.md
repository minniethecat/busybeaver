# Captain Vauquelin

A French military aviator attached to the Aviation Corps who pilots the [[channel-crossing|cross-Channel flight]] that carries [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] to safety — and to his new life — in Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*.

## Overview

Captain Vauquelin appears in the novel's penultimate chapters as the pilot of the *Parrott* biplane attempting a nonstop flight from [[paris|Paris]] to [[london|London]]. He is introduced in Chapter XVII when [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] proposes the arrangement to [[ducroy|Ducroy]]: "It is on schedule, is it not, that Captain Vauquelin of the Aviation Corps is to attempt a nonstop flight from [[paris|Paris]] to [[london|London]] this morning, with two passengers, in a new *Parrott* biplane?" ([017-052]).

Vauquelin is a figure of quiet professionalism in a novel populated by thieves and spies — a military officer performing a sanctioned mission who becomes an unwitting accomplice to [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s escape. His courage and competence under fire are essential to the novel's resolution: without his steady hand at the controls, the aerial duel over the Channel would have ended in disaster.

## Role in the Escape

At [[port-aviation|Port Aviation]], Vauquelin keeps himself professionally aloof while [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] and a young officer help [[lucy-shannon|Lucy Shannon]] into the passenger seat and strap her in ([025-008]). Once airborne, he "was wheedling every ounce of power out of its willing motor" ([025-016]), pushing the *Parrott* to its eighty-mile-per-hour limit as [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]]'s pursuing *Valkyr* monoplane closes in.

The aerial pursuit that follows is the novel's most spectacular set-piece. Vauquelin "manipulating the levers, the plane tilted its nose and swam higher and yet higher. The song of the motor dropped an octave to a richer tone" ([025-010]). When the wind creates dangerous pockets — "those holes in the air so dreaded by cloud pilots" — Vauquelin returns his entire attention to the management of the biplane ([025-015]).

The pursuit turns lethal when gunfire from the *Valkyr* pierces the *Parrott*'s canvas and tears through Vauquelin's leather surtout, "a ragged perforation in the sleeve of the pilot's leather surtout, just above the elbow" ([025-018]). Vauquelin ducks as another bullet "came slamming past his head" ([025-019]). Despite being wounded, he maintains control.

The climactic moment comes in Chapter XXVI when Vauquelin, "steadying himself with a splendid display of self-control and sheer courage," concentrates on flying while [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] prepares to return fire ([026-038]). Together they ascend, the monoplane unable to match their climb rate — "[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] had read somewhere, or heard it said, that monoplanes were poor machines for climbing. He told himself that, if this were true, Vauquelin knew his business" ([026-044]).

After the duel ends and the Valkyr plunges into the Channel, Vauquelin's composure returns: "You feel better now, mademoiselle?" he enquires, hastening to loosen [[lucy-shannon|Lucy]]'s fastenings ([026-055]). Landing somewhere on the South Downs, he assures them: "Oh, we'll find a way out of this somehow. [[london|England]] isn't big enough for anybody to remain lost in it" ([026-058]).

## Relationship to Lanyard

Vauquelin is not knowingly [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s ally. He is a military officer performing a sanctioned mission; [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s presence in the aeroplane is a private arrangement brokered through [[ducroy|Ducroy]]. This ambiguity — Vauquelin as unwitting accomplice — is characteristic of the novel's moral complexity: the most honourable characters are often the most deceived. Yet in the crucible of aerial combat, the distinction between willing and unwitting ally dissolves; Vauquelin and [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] become brothers in survival.
