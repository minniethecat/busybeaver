# The Apaches

The criminal street gangs of [[paris|Paris]] — known as *Les Apaches* — who serve as [[the-pack|the Pack]]'s enforcement arm and represent the violent, undisciplined face of crime in Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*.

## Overview

The Apaches are mentioned several times in the novel as the lowest tier of the [[paris|Paris]]ian criminal hierarchy. They are the ones who patrol the stations, watch the exits, and do [[the-pack|the Pack]]'s dirty work. [[popinot|Popinot]] is described as commanding "[[popinot|Popinot]]'s Apaches" ([013-076]), indicating that each Pack lieutenant has a cohort of these street-level operatives at their disposal.

## Role in the Pack

Within [[the-pack|the Pack]]'s structure, the Apaches function as muscle. They have no voice in the council, no share in the profits beyond their wages, and no protection when they become inconvenient. They are, in [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s hierarchy of criminals, barely above vermin: "rats of Belleville" ([008-115]).

## Lanyard's Contempt

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s dismissal of the Apaches is not merely snobbery but a professional judgment. The Apaches represent everything the [[gentleman-thief|gentleman thief]] is not: unskilled, violent, disloyal, and lacking any code of [[criminal-code|professional ethics]]. They are criminals without craft — a distinction that matters deeply to [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s self-conception.
