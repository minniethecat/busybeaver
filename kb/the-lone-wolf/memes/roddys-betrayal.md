# Roddy's Betrayal

The discovery that [[roddy|Roddy]] — a fixture at [[troyons|Troyon's]] and a man [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] considered harmless — has been serving as an informant for [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], marking a critical moment in the unravelling of [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]'s carefully constructed identity.

## Overview

[[roddy|Roddy]] appears throughout the novel as a background figure — a drunk, a hanger-on, a man of no particular consequence. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] has known him for years and has never considered him a threat. But [[roddy|Roddy]] has been watching, listening, and reporting to [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] all along.

The revelation comes in Chapter III, when it is disclosed that [[roddy|Roddy]] overheard the conversation identifying [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] with [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] and that "[[roddy|Roddy]] was listening" ([003-102]). This single detail shatters [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s assumption of security at [[troyons|Troyon's]].

## The Betrayal

[[roddy|Roddy]]'s betrayal is not motivated by malice but by weakness — he is easily bought, easily controlled. [[the-pack|The Pack]] uses him, [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] uses him, and he drifts between them without loyalty to anyone. This makes him in some ways more dangerous than a principled enemy: he cannot be reasoned with, only managed.

## Narrative Function

[[roddy|Roddy]]'s betrayal serves as a reminder that [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s world is permeable — that even the familiar faces at [[troyons|Troyon's]] cannot be trusted. The betrayal transforms [[troyons|Troyon's]] from sanctuary to trap, one of several such transformations in a novel about the impossibility of hiding.
