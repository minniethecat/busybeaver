# Aviation

**Aviation** — the technology of powered flight — plays a pivotal role in the climax of *The Lone Wolf*, serving as both the means of Michael Lanyard's escape from France and a symbol of the modern era dawning over the old world of gentleman thieves and European intrigue.

## In the Novel

The novel is set in 1914, on the eve of the Great War, when aviation was still a daring and dangerous frontier. Aeroplanes appear as fragile machines of wood, wire, and canvas, piloted by men in heavy wind-resisting garments. The aerodrome at Port Aviation becomes the stage for the final act of Lanyard's French adventure.

### The Escape

Lanyard's bargain with Monsieur Ducroy secures him a place in an aviation test at Port Aviation — a public demonstration of a new aeroplane. This provides the cover he needs to leave France, as the French government cannot arrest a man participating in an official military test. Captain Vauquelin is the designated pilot.

### The Sabotage

Unknown to Lanyard, De Morbihan and Ekstrom have spent each morning in the hangars at St. Germain preparing a trap. They have tampered with Vauquelin's aeroplane, intending for it to fail catastrophically mid-flight. Lucy Shannon, discovering the plot, rushes to Port Aviation to warn Lanyard. She arrives too late — Lanyard has already taken Vauquelin's place in the cockpit. Vauquelin himself survives, having been delayed, and Lanyard flies the machine successfully despite the sabotage.

## Symbolism

Aviation represents the modern world pressing in upon Lanyard's existence. The aeroplane's "mighty roar comparable only… to the chant of ten thousand riveting locusts" (PN 027-051) is the sound of a new century — one in which the old codes of honour among thieves may no longer suffice. Lanyard, who began the novel as a master of old-world arts (safe-cracking, disguise, the gentleman's code), survives by embracing the new technology.
