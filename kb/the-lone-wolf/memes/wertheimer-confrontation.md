# Wertheimer Confrontation

The confrontation between [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] — [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] — and [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]], a [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] informant and blackmailer who attempts to coerce [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] into the service of a criminal syndicate called the [[the-pack|International Underworld]] Unlimited. The encounter spans Chapters XIX–XXI and ends in a violent chase through [[paris|Paris]].

## The Proposition

[[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] intercepts [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]], who is working incognito as a [[paris|Paris]] taxi driver after his reformation. Posing as an amiable Englishman of "the better caste" with a "personality uncommonly insinuating," [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] reveals that he knows [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s true identity and past ([019-080]).

His proposition is simple: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] will resume his career as a jewel thief, channeling the proceeds through [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]: "When you've done your work, you'll come to me and split the proceeds fairly — and as long as you do that, never a word will pass my lips!" ([019-176]).

## The Blackmail

[[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]'s leverage is [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]. As he explains with a "quizzical glance": "I'm hoping to be able to run home for an occasional weekend without vexing [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]" ([019-099]). He offers [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] "protection" — protection from the very authorities [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] himself informs for.

The arrangement would require [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] to work under [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]'s supervision, insulated from the other syndicate leaders: "you can work with us without being obliged to deal in any way with the Old Man or [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]], or [[popinot|Popinot]]" ([019-149]).

## Lanyard's Response

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s reaction surprises even himself. Rather than the "cool disdain" befitting a master cracksman of the highest caste, he is consumed by "blind rage" — a fury that puzzles him in retrospect ([020-002]). He storms out, slamming the door "on a room ringing with [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]]'s unaffected laughter" ([019-180]).

## The Attempted Murder

Refusing to accept rejection, [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] dispatches [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] and a carload of [[the-apaches|Apaches]] to eliminate [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]. The pursuit is a "hideous clamour" through [[paris|Paris]] streets — [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]]'s car hunting [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s taxi with "the report of a revolver-shot... drowned out" by the noise of engines and horns ([020-029]).

The chase culminates at the Bois de Boulogne, where [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] turns the tables: he flings a heavy cushion "directly into the face of [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]]," causing the pursuer's car to crash ([020-062]). The would-be murderers are destroyed by their own violence.

## Aftermath

In the aftermath, [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is "singularly free from any sort of exultation." He reflects on the inexplicable nature of the feud — why "this necessity of striking in self-defence" when he "asked nothing better than to be let alone" ([021-002]). He resolves to complete one final coup, then retreat to [[london|England]] until the affair blows over, vowing to "exterminate [[popinot|Popinot]] utterly, drive [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] into permanent retirement" upon his return ([021-009]).

## Significance

The [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] confrontation crystallizes the novel's central conflict: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s attempt at reformation versus the [[the-underworld|underworld]]'s refusal to release him. [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] represents the institutional corruption that blurs the line between criminal and law enforcement — a [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] informant who is himself a blackmailer. The confrontation forces [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] to recognize that passivity is not an option; he must actively fight to defend his new life.
