# Social Class

The stratified social order of early twentieth-century [[paris|Paris]] that shapes the world of Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*, determining access, suspicion, and the very possibility of a [[gentleman-thief|gentleman thief]]'s existence.

## Overview

The novel's world is rigidly stratified. At the top are aristocrats like [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]], whose titles and connections make them effectively immune to suspicion. Below them are the wealthy bourgeoisie like [[madame-omber|Madame Omber]], who command respect through fortune rather than birth. And below them all are the criminals — though even here, class distinctions apply.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s genius is to exploit the blind spots created by class. A gentleman, by definition, is above suspicion. The police do not look for [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] in the drawing rooms of the wealthy; they look in the alleyways of Belleville. This is "the immunity ascribed to [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] as long as he maintained his social camouflage" ([004-003]).

## Class Structure in the Novel

| Level | Examples | Relation to Crime |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| [[high-society|Aristocracy]] | [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] | Immune, can conspire openly |
| Bourgeoisie | [[madame-omber|Madame Omber]] | Target, protected by wealth |
| Working class | [[troyons|Troyon's]] staff | Invisible, notice everything |
| Criminal elite | [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] ([[gentleman-thief|gentleman thief]]) | Hidden among the respectable |
| Criminal underclass | [[the-apaches|Apaches]] | Visible, disposable |

## Lanyard's Position

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] occupies an ambiguous position in this hierarchy. He is not an aristocrat by birth, but his manners, dress, and comportment allow him to pass. He is a criminal by profession, but his technique and ethics place him above the common thief. This liminality is both his greatest asset and his deepest vulnerability.
