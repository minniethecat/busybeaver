# Madame Troyon

The proprietress of [[troyons|Troyon's restaurant]]-hotel, a maternal figure who raised the orphaned Marcel ([[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]]) from boyhood and represents the sole constant presence in his life through all its transformations.

## Overview

Madame Troyon is introduced in the novel's opening chapter as the woman who took charge of the abandoned child who would become [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]. "It was Madame who took charge of him when the strange man dragged him in from the rain" ([001-012]). From that moment, she becomes his surrogate mother — feeding him, clothing him, and, in her way, loving him. She gives him the name "Marcel, which wasn't his name" ([001-026]), and the boy "before long was unaware he had ever had another."

Her relationship with [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is one of the few genuinely warm connections in the novel. She asks no questions about his activities, makes no judgments about his character, and welcomes him back whenever he returns — the unconditional acceptance of a mother for her son. In a world of thieves and spies, Madame Troyon represents a moral simplicity: love expressed through daily care rather than grand pronouncements.

## Madame Troyon and Lanyard's Childhood

The orphaned child's relationship with Madame Troyon shapes his entire understanding of womanhood. Vance writes that "his concept of womanhood was incarnate in Madame Troyon; so he gave all the hotel women a wide berth" ([001-026]). She is, in effect, his entire template for femininity — both sheltering him and isolating him from normal relationships with women.

The boy "gave all the hotel women the respect and affection due Madame Troyon" ([001-026]), a statement that reveals both his devotion to her and his undiscriminating view of women, shaped entirely by her example. She remains the one fixed point in a childhood of transience — the guests come and go, but Madame is always there.

## The Reunion

When [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] returns to [[troyons|Troyon's]] as an adult, the reunion is quiet and natural. She does not demand explanations for his years of absence; she simply welcomes him home. "Heaving in his luggage — '[[troyons|Troyon's]]!' he told the cocher" ([002-014]), [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] finds that despite the electric lights now brightening the windows, the essential character of Madame's establishment remains unchanged.

The continuity she represents is precisely what [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] needs. After years of living as [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] — solitary, hunted, forever calculating — the simple rhythm of [[troyons|Troyon's]] under Madame's stewardship offers a temporary respite. She represents a world where he was once simply Marcel, where his identity was not a mask but a given.
