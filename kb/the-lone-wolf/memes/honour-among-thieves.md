# Honour Among Thieves

The principle of professional respect and mutual recognition among criminals — a concept invoked and ultimately betrayed in Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*, where [[the-pack|the Pack]]'s violation of this code marks them as beyond redemption.

## Overview

"It was a matter of professional honour," [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] explains when another thief recognizes him on a transatlantic crossing and refrains from exposing him ([001-055]). This brief encounter establishes a rule that governs [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s world: thieves recognize each other, and recognition carries obligations.

[[the-pack|The Pack]]'s violation of this code — their willingness to blackmail, betray, and kill their own kind — is what makes them truly dangerous. They are not merely criminals; they are criminals without honour, and therefore without limit.

## The Code in Practice

| Principle | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Recognition | A thief identifies another but does not expose him |
| Territory | Each professional respects another's domain |
| Non-interference | One does not poach another's target |
| Mutual protection | What threatens one threatens all |

## The Pack's Violation

[[the-pack|The Pack]] systematically violates every principle of the code. They attempt to coerce [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] rather than negotiate with him. They threaten to expose him to the police when he refuses. They attempt to kill him when exposure fails. Each violation deepens [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s contempt and reinforces his determination to remain independent.

The novel ultimately suggests that the [[criminal-code|code of honour]] among thieves, while real, is insufficient — it can regulate behaviour but cannot justify it. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s reformation is the recognition that honour requires more than professional etiquette.
