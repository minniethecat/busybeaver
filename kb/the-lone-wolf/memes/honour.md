# Honour

**Honour** — the gentleman's code among thieves — is the moral framework that distinguishes Michael Lanyard from common criminals in *The Lone Wolf*. It is the principle that allows him to be both a master thief and a sympathetic protagonist.

## Lanyard's Code

Lanyard operates by a personal code that sets him apart from men like Popinot, De Morbihan, and Ekstrom:

- He steals property but never takes a life
- He respects women and refuses to harm them
- He keeps his word once given
- He protects the innocent, even at risk to himself
- He despises gratuitous cruelty and treachery

This code is not a formal creed but an instinctive inheritance — what Lanyard calls "the tradition of a gentleman." It is what compels him to return the Huysman war plans to France, to warn Lucy Shannon when she is in danger, and ultimately to reject the criminal life entirely.

## In Conflict with the Underworld

The criminal Pack operates by a different code — one of fear, loyalty enforced by violence, and betrayal as a tool of management. De Morbihan and Popinot mock Lanyard's scruples as weakness. Yet it is precisely Lanyard's honour that saves him: Lucy Shannon trusts him because she recognizes his code; Captain Vauquelin admires his courage; even Ducroy, though baffled, fulfills his promise.

## The Transformation

The novel traces Lanyard's evolution from a practitioner of this code within the underworld to a man who can no longer reconcile his honour with a life of crime. The final chapters show him choosing love, honesty, and a fresh start over the wealth and power of the Pack. Honour, in the end, demands that the Lone Wolf cease to be a wolf at all.
