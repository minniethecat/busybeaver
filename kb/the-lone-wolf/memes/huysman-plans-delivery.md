# Huysman Plans Delivery

The midnight intrusion of [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] — [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] — into the bedchamber of Monsieur Ducroy, the French Minister of War, to deliver the stolen Huysman military plans. This act of patriotic restitution, depicted in Chapter XVII ("The Forlorn Hope"), is one of the novel's most politically charged scenes.

## The Plans

The Huysman plans — stolen military documents of critical importance to French national defense — are the MacGuffin of the novel's political subplot. Their theft and intended sale to a foreign power by [[the-pack|the Pack]]'s agents represents the intersection of ordinary crime with international espionage.

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]], having recovered the plans during the Faubourg Saint-Germain heist, resolves to return them directly to the French government — bypassing both the criminal [[the-underworld|underworld]] that would sell them and the police who would arrest him.

## The Intrusion

"Established behind his splendid mahogany desk in his office at the Ministère de la Guerre," Ducroy is a man of "distinguished corpulence" whose dignity deserts him entirely when awakened at midnight by an armed intruder ([017-002]).

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] sits beside the bed, a pistol visible but not aimed. The Minister's reaction is immediate terror: "There are eighteen hundred francs in my pocketbook — about... My watch is on the stand here. You will find the family plate in the dining-room safe" ([017-013]).

## The Exchange

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] must repeatedly correct the Minister's assumption that he is a common burglar: "Pardon: monsieur labours under a misapprehension. Had one desired these valuables, one would readily have taken them without going to the trouble of disturbing the repose of monsieur" ([017-014]).

The Minister, still bewildered, protests: "Why — if your errand be peaceable — break into my house?" ([017-020]). [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s answer — that he has come to deliver, not to steal — is so counterintuitive that Ducroy struggles to comprehend it.

## The Delivery

[[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] hands over the Huysman plans without demanding payment or immunity. The act is pure restitution: the criminal returning what other criminals stole, not for reward but for country. It is [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]'s most explicit declaration that his reformation is genuine — that patriotism and honor are not the exclusive properties of the respectable.

## Significance

The Huysman plans delivery is the novel's most direct engagement with the theme of patriotism. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]], the international fugitive, acts in [[paris|France]]'s national interest while the "respectable" [[de-morbihan|De Morbihan]] and [[wertheimer|Wertheimer]] conspire to sell French military secrets. Vance inverts the expected moral hierarchy: the thief is the patriot, and the gentlemen are the traitors.

The chapter title — "The Forlorn Hope," a military term for a suicidal vanguard action — casts [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s reformation as a doomed but noble assault on the fortress of his own past.
