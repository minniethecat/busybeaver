# Scotland Yard

The [[london|London]] Metropolitan Police detective force whose emissaries are dispatched to continental Europe in pursuit of [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]], representing the long arm of British law enforcement in Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel.

## Overview

Scotland Yard serves as the primary institutional antagonist throughout the novel — the organized force of law that pursues [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] across [[paris|Paris]] and [[london|London]]. Its emissaries operate under legal constraints that [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] exploits, knowing they must move "gingerly to keep within the laws they serve." The Yard's presence creates constant tension, forcing [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] to remain vigilant even in apparently safe settings. (002-004)

## Significance in the Story

Scotland Yard represents the civilized world's response to [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]]'s depredations. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s opinion of the Yard is characteristically low — he reckons it requires "at least twenty-four hours to unlimber for action" (002-003) — yet its agents prove persistent and dangerous adversaries. The man from Scotland Yard appears at railway platforms, in restaurants, and at social gatherings, a recurring reminder that the law is always closing in. (002-008) (003-001)

The Yard's presence also creates a layered threat environment: [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] must evade not only Scotland Yard but also the French [[la-tour-pointue|Préfecture de Police]] and the agents of various Continental secret services, all of whom operate with different rules and jurisdictions. (002-004)
