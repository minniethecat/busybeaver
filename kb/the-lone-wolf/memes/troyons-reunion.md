# Troyon's Reunion

[[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]]'s emotional reunion with [[troyons|Troyon's]] — the modest [[paris|Paris]]ian restaurant and hotel that had been his home for two decades — serves as both a homecoming and a portent in the opening chapters of Louis Joseph Vance's 1914 novel *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]*.

## Overview

When [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] returns to [[troyons|Troyon's]] after years abroad, he finds it remarkably unchanged. "Heaving in his luggage — '[[troyons|Troyon's]]!' he told the cocher" ([002-026]). The familiar setting — the dingy drab exterior, the dull green shutters, the maze of corridors — offers a temporary illusion that the past can be recovered.

The reunion with the staff is warm: [[madame-troyon|Madame Troyon]], who had cared for him as a child, still presides; Papa Troyon still runs the establishment with gruff efficiency. For a brief moment, [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] is not [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]] but Marcel — the boy who grew up in these corridors.

## The Illusion of Return

The reunion's warmth is undercut by what [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] knows but cannot acknowledge: the world has changed. [[bourke|Bourke]] is dead. [[the-pack|The Pack]] has grown more powerful. And his own identity — so carefully compartmentalized — is beginning to fracture.

The presence of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]'s man at [[troyons|Troyon's]], watching him, confirms what [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] fears: the past cannot be reclaimed, only revisited briefly before the present intrudes ([003-045]).

## Thematic Significance

The [[troyons|Troyon's]] reunion embodies the novel's meditation on nostalgia. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] returns seeking the comfort of the familiar, but finds that familiarity is itself a trap. The very qualities that made [[troyons|Troyon's]] a sanctuary — its obscurity, its constancy — now make it a cage that his enemies know how to find.
