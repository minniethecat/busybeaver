# Troyon's

A three-storey restaurant and hotel tucked away in a jungle of side-streets near the Luxembourg Gardens, known only to a select circle of patrons, where [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] lived and operated for most of his adult life.

## Overview

Troyon's occupied a corner "in a jungle of side-streets, well withdrawn from the bustle of the adjacent boulevards of St. Germain and St. Michel." Its cooking was the best in [[paris|Paris]], its cellar second to none, its rates ridiculously reasonable — yet Baedeker knew it not. This obscurity was deliberate: the cognoscenti who patronized Troyon's guarded its reputation jealously, preferring to keep the swarms of tourists away. (001-002)

## Description

The building was three storeys, painted a dingy drab with dull green shutters. The restaurant occupied most of the ground floor street front. A squat, broad doorway opened into a stone-walled passageway leading to a semi-roofed courtyard — "a shadowy place, cool on the hottest day." From the court, a staircase climbed lazily to the second storey. (001-003) (001-004)

Above stairs, "a medieval maze of corridors long and short, complicated by many unexpected steps and staircases and turns and enigmatic doors, ran every-which-way and as a rule landed one in the wrong room." There were no salons, no reception-rooms, never a bathroom — not even running water beyond two hallway taps. Gas burned only in the corridors and the restaurant, with "asthmatic jets that, spluttering blue within globes obese, semi-opaque, and yellowish, went well with furnishings and decorations of the Second Empire to which years had lent a mellow and somehow rakish dinginess." (001-005)

## Significance in the Story

Troyon's is the novel's gravitational centre — the place [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] always returns to. It is where [[bourke|Bourke]] brought him as a child in 1893; where he learned to be a gentleman while practising as a thief; where [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] detectives dine unaware that their quarry sits at the next table. The building's labyrinthine architecture mirrors [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s own psychology: a man full of hidden passages, unexpected turns, and doors that lead to the wrong room. (001-007) (003-001)

Its eventual destruction by fire marks the end of an era — [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] can never go back, and the burning of Troyon's is the burning of his old life. (018-013)

## Key Events

- **1893**: The child who would become [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]] arrives at Troyon's on a wet winter night (001-007)
- **The [[the-omber-affair|Omber affair]]**: [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]'s detective enters the restaurant while [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] dines (003-001)
