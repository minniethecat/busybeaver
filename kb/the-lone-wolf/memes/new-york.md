# New York

The city where the second stage of [[michael-lanyard|Michael Lanyard]]'s criminal education began, and a destination that reappears in his reflections throughout *[[the-lone-wolf|The Lone Wolf]]* as a symbol of escape and possibility.

## Overview

New York enters [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s life when [[bourke|Bourke]] takes him there after leaving [[paris|Paris]]. "In New York began the second stage in the education of a professional criminal" ([001-060]). It is in New York that [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] acquired his English fluency, his cosmopolitan polish, and the skills — both legitimate and otherwise — that made him [[michael-lanyard|the Lone Wolf]].

Though never visited "on screen" during the novel's main action (which unfolds entirely in [[paris|Paris]], [[london|London]], and the airspace between them), New York exerts a gravitational pull on [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s imagination. When his circumstances in Europe grow untenable, his thoughts turn westward.

## New York in Lanyard's Development

[[bourke|Bourke]]'s tutelage in New York transformed the [[paris|Paris]]ian street urchin into an international operator. [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]] "learned to speak English, what clothes to wear and when to wear them, and the civilized practice with knife and fork at table" ([001-060]). He came to know that "a self-made millionaire, taken the right way, is as approachable as one whose millions date back even unto the third generation; he could order a dinner at Sherry's as readily as drinks at Sharkey's" ([001-060]).

## As a Symbol

In [[michael-lanyard|Lanyard]]'s internal geography, New York represents the road not taken — a life he might have lived had he stayed with [[bourke|Bourke]]'s tutelage and never returned to [[troyons|Troyon's]]. When the novel's climax forces him to contemplate permanent flight, New York is one of the destinations he considers.
