# Lord W

The wealthy owner of [[Earlshall Park]], who purchases [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] from [[Squire Gordon]] and introduces the bearing rein into their lives.

## 角色概述

Lord W—— (full name withheld in the narrative) is an aristocratic estate owner who visits [[Squire Gordon]]'s property and, impressed by the horses, purchases [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] for his own stables at [[Earlshall Park]]. (024-011) He represents the Victorian upper class's attitude toward horses as status symbols, and his household's insistence on the bearing rein becomes a source of great suffering for both horses.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Lord W first appears when he visits Squire Gordon to inspect the horses. [[John Manly]] presents [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] to him, and Lord W is favorably impressed, commenting on their excellent character as vouched for by Squire Gordon. (024-011) (024-012)

### 关键转折

Lord W purchases Black Beauty and Ginger, and they are sent to [[Earlshall Park]], where [[Mr. York]] serves as coachman. Under Lord W's household, the horses are subjected to the bearing rein — a fashionable but painful device that forces horses to hold their heads high. (025-007) When Ginger rebels against the bearing rein, Lord W is "much put out," demonstrating his prioritization of fashion over animal welfare. His younger sons also ride the horses recklessly, causing Ginger further injury. (025-008)

### 结局

Lord W and part of his family depart [[Earlshall Park]] in early spring, leaving the stables under the management of the household staff. (026-001) His departure does not end the horses' suffering, as the practices he established — particularly the bearing rein — continue under the remaining grooms.

## 性格特征

- Wealthy and status-conscious, valuing appearance over substance
- Not intentionally cruel, but indifferent to the suffering caused by fashionable equipment
- Representative of the Victorian aristocracy's detachment from the working animals that serve them

## 主题意义

Lord W embodies the systemic nature of equine mistreatment in Victorian society. He is not depicted as a villain — he is a gentleman who trusts Squire Gordon's judgment — but his casual acceptance of the bearing rein and his household's rough treatment of horses illustrates how cruelty was embedded in fashion and class expectations. His character demonstrates that animal suffering was often caused not by malice but by indifference and social convention.
