# Horse Accidents

Horse accidents — falls, collisions, and injuries to both horses and riders — recur throughout *Black Beauty* as warnings about the consequences of carelessness, recklessness, and poor treatment. Each accident in the novel serves a moral purpose, illustrating that when humans fail in their duty of care, both horses and people suffer.

## 定义

In the Victorian era, horse accidents were common and often severe. Riders were thrown at fences, carriages overturned on slippery streets, horses fell on cobblestones, and collision between vehicles were frequent. The causes ranged from reckless riding to poorly maintained harness to simple misfortune. The consequences could be fatal for both horse and human.

## 在书中的体现

The novel opens with a fatal hunting accident: a young rider pushes his horse too hard at a fence, the horse falls, and the rider is killed (003-014, 003-016). Beauty's mother calls it "that sad, sad sight" (003-015), establishing early that recklessness kills.

Later, Beauty himself is involved in a dangerous night ride for the doctor, galloping through darkness with an urgent mission (019-004, 019-012). He returns safely, but the hard ride and subsequent neglect leave him with a life-threatening fever: "I was now very ill; a strong inflammation had attacked my lungs" (019-017). "Mr. Bond, the horse-doctor, came every day. One day he bled me" (020-001).

The most consequential accident occurs when a drunken rider, Reuben Smith, takes Beauty out and, riding recklessly, causes a fall that breaks Beauty's knees. This injury transforms Beauty from a gentleman's horse into a cab horse — a single accident redirecting an entire life.


> As for me, I was so astonished that I did not at first see what was going on by the brook; but when I did look there was a sad sight; two fine horses were down, one was struggling in the stream, and the other was groaning on the grass. One of the riders was getting out of the water covered with mud, the other lay quite still.
>
> (003-008)


### 关键场景

**The fatal hunt.** Squire Gordon's son is killed in a hunting accident (003-014, 003-016). The church bell tolls, and the funeral procession passes. The novel's first major event is a death caused by recklessness on horseback.

**Beauty's fever.** After the night ride for the doctor, Beauty's severe illness (019-017, 020-001) demonstrates that even necessary risks carry consequences when aftercare is neglected.

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell does not condemn riding or driving — she condemns the carelessness that turns these activities deadly. Each accident in the novel is traced to a specific human failure: recklessness, drunkenness, ignorance, or neglect. The message is that accidents are not random misfortunes but the predictable results of human choices.
