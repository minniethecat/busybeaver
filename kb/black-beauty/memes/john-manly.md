# John Manly

John Manly is the coachman at Birtwick Park, the estate of Squire Gordon. More than any other human character in *Black Beauty*, John articulates and demonstrates the novel's central argument: that kindness is both a moral duty and the most effective way to manage horses.

## 角色概述

John is described as a skilled, principled coachman who lives with his wife and child in the coachman's cottage near the stables (006-001). He is "very particular" about every aspect of horse care (006-003), from fitting bridles to grooming coats. His philosophy is practical rather than sentimental: he argues that kindness produces better, safer horses, and that cruelty is not only wrong but counterproductive.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

John tells his own backstory to young Joe Green, explaining how he was orphaned at a young age when his parents died "of the fever within ten days of each other" (018-013). Taken in by a kind farmer, he learned the value of helping others — a principle he applies throughout his life.

### 关键转折

John's most significant intervention is his handling of Ginger. When the chestnut mare bites James, rather than punishing her, John says: "Try her with kindness" (009-009). He brings her a bran mash and strokes her, and she never bites again. His diagnosis — "Kindness is all the physic she wants" (009-011) — proves correct, transforming a horse labeled "irritable" into a reliable companion.

### 结局

When Squire Gordon must sell his horses and leave England, John is separated from the horses he has cared for (022-002). He receives offers of good positions but his attachment to Birtwick is clear.

## 性格特征

- **Principled** — his treatment of horses is governed by consistent moral standards
- **Skilled** — meticulous in every aspect of horse care and driving
- **Compassionate** — recognizes that horses have individual needs and temperaments
- **Articulate** — able to explain and defend his philosophy to others

## 主题意义

John Manly is Sewell's ideal horseman. He demonstrates that the best horsemanship is inseparable from moral character — that treating horses well is not merely a technical skill but an ethical practice. His practical arguments for kindness (that it produces better results than force) are the novel's most accessible rhetoric, making the case that virtue and utility align.
