# John Manly's Farewell

When [[Squire Gordon]]'s household was broken up, [[John Manly]] personally delivered [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] to [[Earlshall Park]] — then, voice sounding "very sad," patted each horse for the last time and walked away, leaving Beauty never to see him again. (024-009)

## 概述

After three happy years at [[Birtwick Park]], the news came that Squire Gordon's wife was gravely ill and must leave England for a warm country. The household was to be broken up. Beauty and Ginger were sold to the Earl of W——; [[Merrylegs]] was given to the vicar. John Manly, who had cared for the horses with exceptional skill and kindness throughout their time at Birtwick, rode the horses to Earlshall himself — and there, after warning the new coachman about the check-rein, said his final, wordless goodbye. (022-003)

## 背景

Squire Gordon's wife had been ill for some time. The doctor was often at the house, and the master looked grave and anxious. Then the verdict came: she must leave her home at once and go to a warm country for two or three years. The news "fell upon the household like the tolling of a deathbell." (022-001)

John went about his work silent and sad. Joe scarcely whistled. There was a great deal of coming and going as the master began making arrangements for breaking up his establishment and leaving England. (022-002)

## 事件经过

**The Dispersal**

The first to leave were [[Miss Jessie]] and [[Miss Flora]] with their governess. They came to the stable to bid the horses goodbye, hugging Merrylegs like an old friend. (022-003)

Then the horses' fates were announced. Squire Gordon had sold Beauty and Ginger to his old friend the Earl of W——, believing they would have a good place there. Merrylegs was given to the vicar, [[Mr. Blomefield]], for his wife — on the strict condition that he should never be sold, and when past work should be shot and buried. (022-003)

Joe was engaged to take care of Merrylegs and help in the vicarage house, so the pony was well provided for. John, offered several good places, said he would wait and look round — he had resolved to find work with a first-rate colt-breaker and horse-trainer, believing he could help young horses get a fair start in life. (022-007)

**The Last Evening**

The evening before departure, Squire Gordon came into the stable to give his horses a last pat. He seemed very low-spirited — Beauty could tell by his voice, believing that horses can tell more by the voice than many men can. (022-005)

When the master thanked John for his long and faithful service, John broke down:

> "Pray, don't, sir, I can't bear it; you and my dear mistress have done so much for me that I could never repay it. But we shall never forget you, sir, and please God, we may some day see mistress back again like herself; we must keep up hope, sir." (022-009)

Master gave John his hand without speaking. They both left the stable. (022-009)

**The Final Departure**

On the last sad day, Beauty and Ginger brought the carriage to the hall door for the final time. Squire Gordon carried his wife down the steps in his arms and placed her carefully in the carriage, while the house servants stood round crying. (022-010)

They trotted slowly through the park and village, where people stood at their doors to have a last look and say "God bless them." (022-012)

At the railway station, the mistress spoke her last words to John: "Good-by, John. God bless you." Beauty felt the rein twitch, but John made no answer — perhaps he could not speak. Joe stood close up to the horses' heads to hide his tears. The train doors slammed, the guard whistled, and the train glided away, "leaving behind it only clouds of white smoke and some very heavy hearts." (022-013)

When it was out of sight, John said, "We shall never see her again — never." He drove slowly home. "But it was not our home now." (022-014)

**The Delivery to Earlshall**

The next morning, John put the saddle on Ginger and the leading rein on Beauty and rode them fifteen miles across country to Earlshall Park. He asked for Mr. York, the new coachman, and carefully briefed him on each horse's character — Beauty's perfect temper, Ginger's history of bad treatment and her need for gentle handling. (024-001)

Most importantly, John warned York that neither horse had ever worn a check-rein. When York replied that Lady W—— would insist on it, John could only say, "I am sorry for it, very sorry." (024-006)

Then came the final moment:

> He came round to each of us to pat and speak to us for the last time; his voice sounded very sad. I held my face close to him; that was all I could do to say good-by; and then he was gone, and I have never seen him since. (024-009)

## 后果与影响

John Manly's departure marked the definitive end of Beauty's golden age at Birtwick. Everything that followed — the bearing rein at Earlshall, the drunken Reuben Smith's fatal ride, the years as a London cab horse under harsh masters — happened in a world without John's steady, knowledgeable kindness.

John himself went on to become a colt-breaker and horse-trainer, using his gift with horses to give young animals the fair start he believed every horse deserved. His career represents Sewell's ideal of what horse management could be in the right hands — and his absence from Beauty's life thereafter is a measure of what the horse lost.

For Beauty, the farewell was absolute: "I have never seen him since." The finality of that sentence, placed at the end of Chapter 22 (and the beginning of Part II), marks the permanent closure of the Birtwick era.

## 主题意义

John Manly's farewell distills the novel's deepest theme: that the quality of a horse's life depends entirely on the character of the humans who control it. John was the ideal horseman — knowledgeable without being harsh, firm without being cruel, gentle without being weak. His departure is the novel's true tragedy: not a death, but the permanent loss of the one person who could have protected Beauty from everything that followed.

The contrast between John's way and Lady W——'s way — presented back-to-back in the same chapter — is the novel's structural argument. John pleads against the check-rein; Lady W—— demands it. John pats each horse with a voice "very sad"; Lady W—— speaks in an "imperious voice." The two value systems — horsemanship versus fashion, care versus control — meet in the stable at Earlshall, and fashion wins.

The final image — Beauty holding his face close to John, knowing this was goodbye, and the quiet words "I have never seen him since" — is among the most restrained and powerful moments in the novel. Sewell trusts her readers to feel the loss without having to describe it.
