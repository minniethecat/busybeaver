# The Fire at the Hotel

The fire at the hotel is one of the novel's most suspenseful episodes, occurring during an overnight journey when [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] are stabled at an inn.

In the middle of the night, a fire breaks out in the stable. James Howard, the young stable boy from [[Birtwick Park]], risks his life to rescue both horses — an act of courage that [[John Manly]] later holds up as the model of selfless duty.

The episode demonstrates that the trust built through gentle training has life-saving practical value when crisis strikes.

## 背景

Beauty and [[Ginger]] are traveling with [[Squire Gordon]] and John Manly when they stop for the night at a large hotel in a market town.

The hotel has "a long stable, with six or eight stalls in it, and two or three horses" (016-010). Beauty notes that the head hostler is "a pleasant, active little man, with a crooked leg" who handles the horses with practiced efficiency.

The other hostler, however, is less attentive — a detail that proves significant when the fire's cause is later revealed to be a careless hand smoking in the hayloft.

## 事件经过

Beauty wakes in the darkness to find the air "all thick and choking" (017-004). He hears [[Ginger]] coughing and senses the other horses' restlessness.

> "I cannot say how long I had slept, nor what time in the night it was, but I woke up very uncomfortable. The air seemed all thick and choking. I heard Ginger coughing." (017-004)

The stable is filled with smoke, but the horses are trapped — their stall doors latched, their instincts screaming at them to flee while their training holds them in place.

James Howard, alerted by the commotion, rushes into the burning stable. He leads Beauty out first, then — against the urging of bystanders who tell him not to go back — darts into the smoke-filled building a second time.

> "I saw James coming through the smoke leading Ginger with him; she was coughing violently, and he was not able to speak." (017-013)

Two other horses, trapped in the far end of the stable, do not survive. Their screams haunt the survivors: "it was very terrible! and made both Ginger and me feel very bad" (017-020).

## 后果与影响

The fire's aftermath reveals the cost of carelessness. The stable hand's pipe or lantern, left unattended in the hayloft, caused the blaze.

John Manly uses the episode to teach a moral lesson. When someone remarks that every man looks out for "number one," John responds with righteous anger.

He points to James's courage as the counter-example: "Where would Black Beauty and Ginger have been if you had only thought of number one? Why, roasted to death!" (018-015).

The fire thus becomes a parable about moral responsibility. James's willingness to risk himself for the horses is not foolishness but duty — the same duty that the novel argues every horse owner owes to the animals in their care.

## 主题意义

The fire episode distills several of the novel's core arguments into a single dramatic scene.

First, it demonstrates the practical value of trust-based training. Beauty follows human direction even in terror because his training has taught him that human commands are trustworthy. The horses that panic and perish are those whose experience has taught them to fear.

Second, it models moral courage. James Howard is young and physically small — the last person one would expect to charge into a burning building. But his courage comes from character, not physique, reinforcing the novel's argument that moral quality transcends social station.

Third, it provides the novel's most direct rejection of selfishness. John Manly's speech against "number one" thinking is Sewell's clearest articulation of her ethical framework: humans have duties to animals, and those duties cannot be set aside for convenience.
