## Role in the Story

Samson was the son of the old master Mr. Ryder, a skilled and gentle horse-breaker. Unlike his father, Samson was "a strong, tall, bold man" who boasted that he had never found a horse that could throw him. (008-005)

Ginger described him as having no gentleness — only "a hard voice, a hard eye, a hard hand." She believed his goal was to wear all the spirit out of her and make her into "a quiet, humble, obedient piece of horseflesh." (008-005)

When Ginger first arrived at the stable, Samson attempted to break her by force. He put a severe bit in her mouth and drove her around a field until she was exhausted and bleeding. When the old master returned and saw Ginger's condition, he confronted Samson at the stable door.

Ginger snapped at Samson, and the old master rebuked his son: "A bad-tempered man will never make a good-tempered horse. You've not learned your trade yet, Samson." (008-007)

Samson's reputation for harshness followed him. Later, when Ginger described another cruel groom, she simply said he was "as hard-tempered and hard-handed as Samson." (009-006)

## The Father-Son Contrast

Samson serves as a foil to his father, [[mr-ryder|Mr. Ryder]], and their contrast is one of the novel's most pointed lessons about horse-breaking. The father believed in patience and kindness; the son believed in force and domination.

Mr. Ryder's famous rebuke — "A bad-tempered man will never make a good-tempered horse" (008-007) — is not merely a reprimand but a statement of philosophy. The horse reflects the handler. Samson's method produced resistance and blood; Mr. Ryder's method produced trust. The novel leaves no doubt about which approach Sewell endorsed.
