# Farewell to Ginger

[[Black Beauty]]'s final encounters with [[Ginger]] form one of the novel's most poignant arcs. After their shared ruin at [[Earlshall Park]] — Beauty with scarred knees, Ginger with broken wind — they are separated. Beauty last sees Ginger on a London street, her once-glossy chestnut coat now dull and thin, her proud neck lank and fallen, her bright eye hopeless. (040-001)

## 概述

Ginger and Beauty's friendship was forged through shared suffering at Earlshall — both victims of the bearing rein, both subject to a system that valued appearance over welfare. Their bond deepened in the meadow where they recuperated together, heads close under the lime-tree. But the friendship's true emotional weight comes from what follows: Ginger's long, slow decline through a succession of ever-lower owners, and the devastating moment when Beauty recognizes her — now a "poor old worn-out chestnut" — on a London street, only to lose her forever. (040-001)

## 背景

Ginger's story began badly. Taken from her mother as a foal, passed through a series of trainers who used force rather than patience, she developed the defensive habits — biting, kicking, rearing — that earned her the label "vicious." It was only under [[John Manly]]'s care at [[Birtwick Park]] that she began to trust humans. Even then, her spirit remained fierce; she was never the placid, accommodating horse that Beauty was. (008-001)

At Earlshall, Ginger suffered most from the bearing rein. [[York]] tightened it against his own judgment, under orders from [[Lady W]]. The rein exacerbated Ginger's natural high-spiritedness into something like desperation — a famous scene shows her rearing, plunging, and kicking over the carriage pole in protest, while York sat on her head to control her. (024-010)

When [[Lord George]] rode her in the steeplechase despite her being strained, he broke her wind and injured her back. This was the beginning of her end. (027-003)

## 事件经过

**The Meadow: First Farewell**

After her twelve-month run at Earlshall, Ginger was deemed fit for work and sold. Beauty, meanwhile, had been sold at York's recommendation to the livery stables in Bath. Their paths diverged — and they would not meet again until London, years later. (027-004)

**The London Street: Last Meeting**

One day, while Beauty's cab waited outside a London park where music was playing, a shabby old cab drew up beside them. The horse was "an old worn-out chestnut, with an ill-kept coat, and bones that showed plainly through it, the knees knuckled over, and the fore-legs were very unsteady." (040-001)

The wind blew a wisp of hay toward her, and "the poor creature put out her long thin neck and picked it up, and then turned and looked about for more." Then she looked full at Beauty and spoke: (040-001)

> "Black Beauty, is that you?" (040-002)

It was Ginger — but how changed:

> "The beautifully arched and glossy neck was now straight, and lank, and fallen in; the clean straight legs and delicate fetlocks were swelled; the joints were grown out of shape with hard work; the face, that was once so full of spirit and life, was now full of suffering, and I could tell by the heaving of her sides, and her frequent cough, how bad her breath was." (040-002)

Ginger told her story: after Earlshall, she was sold to a gentleman and did well for a while, but "after a longer gallop than usual the old strain returned." She changed hands several times, "always getting lower down," until a cab-owner bought her. When they discovered her weakness, they put her in one of the low cabs — "and just be used up; that is what they are doing, whipping and working with never one thought of what I suffer." (040-003)

Ginger said she wished she might die in the streets: "I wish the end was come, I wish I was dead. I have seen dead horses, and I am sure they do not suffer pain." (040-003)

The drivers returned, and the two horses were parted:

> "There was no time to say more; she was soon out of sight, and I saw her no more. A short time after this a cart with a dead horse in it passed our cab-stand. The head hung out of the cart-tail, the lifeless tongue was slowly dropping with blood; and the sunken eyes! but I can't speak of them, the sight was too dreadful. It was a chestnut horse with a long thin neck. I saw a white streak down the forehead. I believe it was Ginger; I hoped it was, for then her troubles would be over." (041-001)

## 后果与影响

Ginger's death is the novel's darkest moment and its most powerful argument. She is not killed in a single dramatic event — she is used up, worn down, piece by piece, over years. The novel treats her death with restraint: Beauty sees a dead horse on a cart and believes it is Ginger, but Sewell never confirms it. The ambiguity makes the loss more haunting, not less.

Ginger's arc serves as the counterpoint to Beauty's. Both horses start at Birtwick, both go to Earlshall, both suffer under the bearing rein, both are ruined by human carelessness. But Beauty is repeatedly saved by good masters — [[Jerry Barker]], [[Mr. Thoroughgood]], the Misses Blomefield — while Ginger slides inexorably downward. The difference is not character (Ginger was as noble as Beauty) but luck: Beauty happened to fall into kind hands at crucial moments, while Ginger did not.

## 主题意义

The farewell to Ginger is the novel's emotional pivot. Until this point, the story has been a series of episodes — some happy, some sad — in a horse's life. Ginger's death transforms the novel into something more urgent: a protest against a system that treats living creatures as disposable tools.

Sewell's restraint is her greatest rhetorical weapon. She does not describe Ginger's actual death; she shows Beauty seeing a dead horse on a cart and hoping it is Ginger, "for then her troubles would be over." The sentence is devastating precisely because it is understated. Hope — that a friend is dead so she will stop suffering — is the most damning indictment of the system Sewell could make.

Ginger's story also makes visible what Beauty's story obscures: the role of sheer luck in a horse's fate. Beauty is not morally superior to Ginger; he is luckier. The novel's happy ending — Beauty in a green meadow with kind owners — is shadowed by the knowledge that Ginger earned no such ending, and that thousands of real horses earned none either.
