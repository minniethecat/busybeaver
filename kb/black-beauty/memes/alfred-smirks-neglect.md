# Alfred Smirk's Neglect

After Filcher's imprisonment, Mr. Barry hired Alfred Smirk — a tall, handsome groom whose polished manner concealed laziness so profound that it nearly crippled Black Beauty. Smirk's neglect caused a severe case of thrush, exposing the gap between appearances and genuine horse care. (033-001)

## Overview

Alfred Smirk presented a perfect facade: always touching his cap with "Yes, sir; yes, sir," always brushing Beauty's mane and tail with water and oiling his hoofs before bringing him to the door so that the horse gleamed. (033-001) But behind this performance, Smirk was "the laziest, most conceited fellow I ever came near." (033-003) He never cleaned Beauty's feet, checked his shoes, or groomed him thoroughly. He left the bit rusty, the saddle damp, the crupper stiff. He never cleaned out the loose box, allowing fouled straw to accumulate underneath. (033-003) The resulting ammonia vapors inflamed Beauty's eyes, and the constant moisture rotted his feet — producing a severe case of thrush that made him stumble dangerously on the streets of Bath.

## Background

Mr. Barry, still recovering from the Filcher scandal, hired Smirk as his second groom. Smirk's deferential manner — touching his hat at every word — convinced everyone that "Mr. Barry was very fortunate to meet with him." (033-003) But Smirk spent his time preening before a looking-glass in the harness-room, attending to his "hair, whiskers and necktie" rather than to the horse. (033-002)

## The Neglect

Smirk's negligence took multiple forms. He never exercised Beauty, leaving him standing for days at a time while feeding him the same high ration as if he were at hard work — a combination that disordered Beauty's health, making him "sometimes heavy and dull, but more often restless and feverish." (033-010) He never provided green food or bran mashes to cool the horse. Instead, when Beauty showed signs of illness, Smirk dosed him with "horse balls and draughts" — medicines that "used to make me feel ill and uncomfortable." (033-010)

When Mr. Barry noticed the stable's foul smell, Smirk deflected blame:

> "Well, sir, I'll do so if you please, sir; but it is rather dangerous, sir, throwing down water in a horse's box; they are very apt to take cold, sir. I should not like to do him an injury." (033-006)

He then suggested the drains might be faulty. A bricklayer was summoned, pulled up bricks, found nothing wrong, laid down lime, and charged five shillings — but "the smell in my box was as bad as ever." (033-009)

## The Crisis

The neglect culminated when Beauty's feet became so tender from standing in fouled straw that he stumbled twice while carrying Mr. Barry over fresh stones. Alarmed, Mr. Barry stopped at a farrier's, who examined Beauty's feet and delivered a blunt diagnosis:

> "Your horse has got the 'thrush', and badly, too; his feet are very tender; it is fortunate that he has not been down. I wonder your groom has not seen to it before. This is the sort of thing we find in foul stables, where the litter is never properly cleaned out." (033-012)

## Consequences and Impact

The farrier prescribed thorough cleaning of Beauty's feet, removal of all litter day by day, bran mashes, green food, and reduced corn. (033-014) Under this treatment Beauty's spirits returned, but the damage to Mr. Barry's trust was permanent. "Mr. Barry was so much disgusted at being twice deceived by his grooms that he determined to give up keeping a horse, and to hire when he wanted one." (033-015) Beauty was kept until his feet healed, then sold again — his third sale since leaving Birtwick Park.

## Thematic Significance

The Smirk episode deepens the novel's exploration of equine vulnerability. Where Filcher's crime was active theft, Smirk's was passive neglect — harder to detect, easier to excuse. His "humbug" represents a type Sewell clearly wishes to expose: the servant who performs diligence for the master's eye while neglecting the creature in his charge. Smirk's suggestion that cleaning would "give the horse a cold" exemplifies how superficial knowledge, deployed to excuse laziness, can be more dangerous than open cruelty — it cloaks abuse in the language of care. (033-006)

The episode also demonstrates the structural vulnerability of horses in Victorian England: two bad grooms in succession were enough to convince a well-meaning owner to abandon horse-keeping entirely, sending Beauty back into the uncertain market where each sale risked a worse fate.
