# The Flood at the Wooden Bridge

Returning to [[Birtwick Park]] on a stormy night, [[Black Beauty]] senses danger at [[The Wooden Bridge|wooden-bridge]] and refuses to cross — saving [[John Manly]] and [[Squire Gordon]] from being swept into the rising river.

## 概述

After a long day's journey, John Manly and Squire Gordon were riding Beauty home through heavy rain. As they approached the wooden bridge over the swollen river, Beauty stopped dead and refused to go forward. Despite John's urging — and then his whip — Beauty held his ground. Moments later, the bridge, undermined by the flood, collapsed into the river (013-010). Had Beauty crossed, all three would have been swept away.

## 背景

The day had been stormy with heavy rain; the river had been rising steadily. The man at the toll-gate warned John that "the river was rising fast" and he feared there would be trouble at the bridge (013-002). John, confident in his knowledge of the route, pressed on along the river road. But Beauty, with senses more acute than any human's, felt the danger in the ground beneath him.

John's apology to Beauty — "I am very sorry I struck you, Beauty; you saved our lives" (013-012) — is one of the novel's most powerful moments of human-horse understanding.


> The moment my feet touched the first part of the bridge I felt sure there was something wrong. I dare not go forward, and I made a dead stop. "Go on, Beauty," said my master, and he gave me a touch with the whip, but I dare not stir; he gave me a sharp cut; I jumped, but I dare not go forward.
>
> "There's something wrong, sir," said John, and he sprang out of the dog-cart and came to my head and looked all about. He tried to lead me forward. "Come on, Beauty, what's the matter?" Of course I could not tell him, but I knew very well that the bridge was not safe.
>
> (013-009–013-010)

## 后果与影响

The event cemented Beauty's reputation as a horse of extraordinary sense and reliability. Squire Gordon declared that "no horse ever served a man better" (013-018). The story of "the horse who refused to cross the bridge" became known throughout Birtwick village.

For Beauty, the episode was remembered as a moment when his instinct was vindicated against human authority — a rare reversal of the usual power dynamic.

## 主题意义

The flood at the wooden bridge is the novel's most celebrated argument for listening to animals. Beauty "knew" the danger in a way that John — with all his experience — could not. The episode inverts the usual hierarchy: the horse is the teacher, the human the student. John's apology — a grown man, an expert horseman, admitting he was wrong to whip his horse — models the humility that Sewell believed was essential to ethical animal care.
