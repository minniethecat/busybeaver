# Beauty Reunites with Ruined Ginger

After recovering from the injuries caused by [[Reuben Smith]]'s drunken ride, [[Black Beauty]] was turned out into a small meadow—where he was soon joined by his old friend [[Ginger]], who had been ruined by the hard riding of [[Lord George]]. (029-001)

## 概述

Following the accident that left him with permanently blemished knees, Black Beauty was placed in a small meadow for recovery. His loneliness was broken when Ginger, his closest companion from [[Birtwick Park]] and [[Earlshall Park]], was turned into the same field. The joyful reunion quickly turned bittersweet as Beauty learned that Ginger had been ruined—her wind and back damaged beyond repair by Lord George's reckless riding at a steeplechase. (029-001)

## 背景

Beauty and Ginger had formed a deep bond during their time together at Birtwick Park under [[Squire Gordon]]'s care, and later at Earlshall Park. They were separated when Beauty was sent away for the fateful ride with Reuben Smith. (028-001)

During the separation, each horse suffered catastrophic injury caused by human failings:

- **Black Beauty** fell on sharp stones during Reuben Smith's drunken ride, shattering his hoof and permanently scarring both knees. (028-007) The injury left him unfit for the Earl's stables, where appearance was paramount. (029-004)

- **Ginger** was pushed by Lord George to compete in a steeplechase despite her groom's warnings that she was strained. She finished among the top three, but the effort destroyed her wind and strained her back. (029-002)

## 事件经过

**The Lonely Recovery**

Beauty spent a month or two alone in a small meadow after his knees had healed sufficiently. Though he enjoyed the liberty and sweet grass, the isolation weighed on him:

> Ginger and I had become fast friends, and now I missed her company extremely. I often neighed when I heard horses' feet passing in the road, but I seldom got an answer. (029-001)

**The Reunion**

One morning, the gate opened and Ginger was led in. Beauty trotted up to her with a joyful whinny—but quickly realized the meeting was not for pleasure:

> Her story would be too long to tell, but the end of it was that she had been ruined by hard riding, and was now turned off to see what rest would do. (029-001)

**Ginger's Account**

Ginger told Beauty what had happened. Lord George, young and heedless of warnings, entered her in a steeplechase. When the groom told him she was not fit, he ignored the advice:

> With her high spirit, she strained herself to the utmost; she came in with the first three horses, but her wind was touched, besides which he was too heavy for her, and her back was strained. (029-002)

Ginger drew a bitter parallel between their fates:

> "And so," she said, "here we are, ruined in the prime of our youth and strength, you by a drunkard, and I by a fool; it is very hard." (029-002)

**Quiet Days Together**

Despite their diminished conditions, the two horses found comfort in each other's company. They no longer galloped as they once did, but fed together, lay down together, and stood for hours beneath a shady lime tree with their heads close. (029-002)

**The Earl's Inspection**

One day, the Earl of W—— came into the meadow with [[York]], the coachman. They examined both horses:

> "There is three hundred pounds flung away for no earthly use," said he; "but what I care most for is that these horses of my old friend, who thought they would find a good home with me, are ruined." (029-004)

The Earl decided Ginger would have a twelve-month run to see if she could recover, but Beauty—with his visible knee scars—had to be sold. York recommended a livery stable master in Bath known for treating horses well. (029-005)

**The Final Separation**

About a week later, Robert came with a halter to take Beauty away. There was no formal leave-taking:

> We neighed to each other as I was led off, and she trotted anxiously along by the hedge, calling to me as long as she could hear the sound of my feet. (029-009)

They would never see each other again.

## 后果与影响

The reunion and final parting had profound consequences:

1. **Beauty's sale to Bath**: Through York's recommendation, Beauty was bought by a livery stable master in Bath, beginning the next chapter of his life as a job horse. (029-010)

2. **Ginger's uncertain fate**: Ginger remained at Earlshall for a year's rest, but her ultimate fate—as revealed later in the novel—was far darker. She would eventually be seen by Beauty in London, reduced to a skeletal cab horse, her spirit completely broken.

3. **A shared indictment**: Both horses' ruination in their prime stands as one of the novel's strongest indictments of human carelessness. The contrast between the Earl's concern for "appearance" (Beauty's scarred knees) and his tolerance of Lord George's destructive behavior underscores the hypocrisy of a system that valued horses as status symbols rather than living creatures.

4. **Emotional resonance**: The image of Ginger trotting along the hedge, calling out as Beauty is led away, is one of the novel's most emotionally affecting moments, cementing the depth of equine friendship and the pain of forced separation.

## 主题意义

The reunion at Earlshall crystallizes the novel's argument about the consequences of human folly on animals. Ginger's bitter summary—"you by a drunkard, and I by a fool"—reduces two complex narratives of suffering to their common denominator: human irresponsibility. Both horses were in their prime, both were valuable, and both were destroyed not by age or illness but by the very people entrusted with their care.

The scene also deepens the novel's portrayal of equine emotional life. The quiet companionship Beauty and Ginger share under the lime tree—feeding together, standing head-to-head—demonstrates that horses form meaningful bonds and experience grief at separation. Anna Sewell invests these moments with a dignity that challenges the Victorian tendency to view animals as unfeeling property.

The Earl's remark that he "care[s] most" about the horses of his "old friend" is an uncomfortable moment of performative concern: he expresses regret yet takes no action against Lord George, and his primary concern is that the horses' appearance no longer befits his stables. The novel invites the reader to question whether such concern, unaccompanied by accountability, is worth anything at all.
