After his desperate midnight gallop to save [[Mrs. Gordon]]'s life, [[Black Beauty]] is left in the care of young [[Joe Green]], whose inexperience leads to a near-fatal illness. The incident becomes a defining lesson about the dangers of ignorance.

## 概述

Joe Green's Mistake is a pivotal event in Chapters 19–20 of *Black Beauty*. After Beauty rides through the night to fetch [[Dr. White]] for the gravely ill Mrs. Gordon, the exhausted horse is handed over to young Joe Green, who has recently replaced [[James Howard]] at the lodge. Joe, though well-meaning, fails to cover the overheated horse with a warm cloth and gives him cold water to drink. Beauty develops severe lung inflammation and nearly dies. The incident sparks a profound conversation between [[John Manly]] and Thomas Green about whether ignorance can ever be an acceptable excuse.

## 背景

The event takes place shortly after James Howard's departure from [[Birtwick Park]] to take up a new position at [[Clifford Hall]]. Joe Green, a young boy from the lodge, has taken over James's duties. Joe is described as "young and small" and "knew very little" about horse care (019-015). His father, who would normally guide him, had been sent away to the next village that night.

On the night in question, Mrs. Gordon falls suddenly and dangerously ill. The squire wakes John Manly and sends him galloping for Dr. White, with the urgent instruction: "ride for your life — that is, for your mistress' life; there is not a moment to lose" (019-002).

## 事件经过

Beauty and John ride eight miles through the frosty moonlit night to the doctor's town, arriving as the church clock strikes three in the morning (019-005). The doctor's own horse is exhausted, so Beauty must carry him back — a heavier and less skilled rider than John — while John walks the eight miles home on foot (019-009).

The return journey is brutal: "I was nearly spent" (019-015). When Joe leads Beauty into the stable, the horse is in severe distress: "my legs shook under me, and I could only stand and pant. I had not a dry hair on my body, the water ran down my legs, and I steamed all over" (019-015).

Joe does his best with limited knowledge. He rubs Beauty's legs and chest but, reasoning that the overheated horse would not want a warm covering, fails to put on the essential warm cloth. He then gives Beauty a pail of cold water (019-015). Soon after, Beauty begins to shake and tremble, turning deadly cold. His legs, loins, and chest ache severely.

When John finally returns, he finds Beauty in great pain. He immediately covers him with warm cloths and makes him warm gruel. John mutters repeatedly: "Stupid boy! stupid boy! no cloth put on, and I dare say the water was cold, too" (019-016).


> "I wish, John, you'd say a bit of a kind word to Joe. The boy is quite broken-hearted; he can't eat his meals, and he can't smile. He says he knows it was all his fault, though he is sure he did the best he knew, and he says if Beauty dies no one will ever speak to him again. It goes to my heart to hear him."
>
> (020-004)

## 后果与影响

Beauty develops a severe lung inflammation and cannot breathe without pain (019-017). John nurses him night and day, getting up two or three times each night. The horse-doctor, [[Mr. Bond]], visits daily and bleeds Beauty at one point, after which Beauty feels faint and believes he may die (020-001).

Ginger and Merrylegs are moved to another stable so Beauty can rest quietly (020-002). The master visits often, telling Beauty: "My poor Beauty, my good horse, you saved your mistress' life, Beauty; yes, you saved her life" (019-017). The doctor confirms that any longer delay would have been fatal for Mrs. Gordon.

A crucial conversation takes place between John Manly and Thomas Green (Joe's father) as they watch over Beauty. When Tom asks John to say a kind word to the heartbroken Joe, John delivers one of the novel's most important speeches on the subject of ignorance:

| 要点 | John Manly 的论述 |
|------|------------------|
| 无知与恶意的区别 | "Only ignorance! how can you talk about only ignorance? Don't you know that it is the worst thing in the world, next to wickedness?" (020-008) |
| 后果不因动机减轻 | If a nurse kills a baby with wrong medicine, or a prank gives a child brain damage — the harm is real regardless of intent |
| 各领域的共通教训 | Tom's own plants were killed when young ladies left a hothouse door open — "it was only ignorance" (020-012) |

Joe is eventually forgiven. John acknowledges: "I know he meant no harm, I never said he did; I know he is not a bad boy" (020-005). Beauty recovers, and the experience shapes Joe into a more careful and knowledgeable horseman. He later redeems himself, growing into a competent groom who appears at the novel's close to recognize and reunite with the aged Beauty (053-013).

## 主题意义

Joe Green's Mistake crystallizes one of the novel's central themes: **the danger of ignorance and the moral responsibility to acquire knowledge**. Anna Sewell uses this episode to argue that "I didn't know" is not an acceptable defense when harm results — whether to horses or to people. John Manly's passionate speech ("the worst thing in the world, next to wickedness") elevates a stable-yard accident into a universal ethical principle.

The event also demonstrates the **reciprocal bond** between Beauty and his human companions: Beauty risks his life for Mrs. Gordon, and John and the master in turn risk exhaustion to save Beauty. This mutual devotion between horse and human is the ideal that Sewell holds up throughout the novel.

Finally, the episode serves as Joe Green's **coming-of-age moment** — a harsh education that transforms a well-meaning but ignorant boy into a responsible horseman, as confirmed by his appearance in the novel's final chapters.
