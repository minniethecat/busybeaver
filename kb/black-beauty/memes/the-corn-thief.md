# The Corn Thief

While employed by a well-meaning but horse-ignorant master in Bath, Black Beauty suffers a stealthy decline in health as his groom Filcher steals his oats — feeding them to his own rabbits and poultry. The theft is exposed only through the intervention of a sharp-eyed gentleman farmer and a police investigation. (032-013)

## Overview

After being sold to an unmarried businessman in Bath, Beauty initially believed he had landed in a good situation: his new master ordered the best hay, plenty of oats, crushed beans, and bran. (032-001) For a few days all went well. But then Beauty noticed his oats diminishing — bran was mixed in as filler, and the corn ration shrank to "not more than a quarter of what there should have been." (032-003) Within two to three weeks his strength and spirits declined. Unable to speak, Beauty could only watch as Filcher's young son arrived each morning with a covered basket, filled a bag with oats from the corn-bin, and departed. (032-011) The theft continued for two months until a visit to a gentleman farmer and a police raid exposed the crime.

## Background

Beauty's new master, a businessman living in Bath, had bought him on medical advice to take horse exercise. He knew "very little about horses" but treated Beauty well and gave generous feeding orders. (032-001) He hired a man named Filcher as groom — a former hotel hostler who now also cultivated fruit, vegetables, poultry, and rabbits for market. The master's ignorance of equine care and Filcher's apparent competence created the perfect conditions for systematic theft.

## The Discovery

The turning point came when Beauty's master rode out to visit a gentleman farmer on the road to Wells. The farmer, a man with "a very quick eye for horses," immediately noticed something wrong:

> "It seems to me, Barry, that your horse does not look so well as he did when you first had him; has he been well?" (032-005)

When Beauty's master relayed Filcher's excuse — that "horses are always dull and weak in the autumn" — the farmer exploded: "Autumn, fiddlesticks! Why, this is only August; and with your light work and good food he ought not to go down like this." (032-007) Feeling Beauty's warm, damp neck and shoulder — a sign of a horse surviving on grass rather than corn — he delivered the crucial diagnosis: "I can't say who eats your corn, my dear fellow, but I am much mistaken if your horse gets it." (032-008)

## The Exposure

Five or six mornings after the farmer's warning, as Filcher's boy was leaving the stable with his basket, a policeman walked in holding the child by the arm. Another policeman followed, locked the stable door, and said: "Show me the place where your father keeps his rabbits' food." (032-013) The frightened boy led them to the corn-bin, where police found "another empty bag like that which was found full of oats in the boy's basket." (032-013)

> Filcher was cleaning my feet at the time, but they soon saw him, and though he blustered a good deal they walked him off to the "lock-up", and his boy with him. I heard afterward that the boy was not held to be guilty, but the man was sentenced to prison for two months. (032-014)

## Consequences and Impact

Filcher was sentenced to two months in prison; his son was not held guilty. (032-014) For Beauty, the exposure ended two months of slow starvation. However, the episode was the first of two betrayals by grooms in Bath — Alfred Smirk's neglect would follow immediately after — that ultimately convinced Mr. Barry to give up horse-keeping entirely. (033-015) This pattern of trusting masters being deceived by dishonest servants recurs throughout the novel, highlighting the vulnerability of horses who cannot speak to report abuse.

## Thematic Significance

The episode dramatizes the novel's recurring theme of equine voicelessness. Beauty observes Filcher's theft but cannot report it: "Dumb beasts! Yes, we are; but if I could have spoken I could have told my master where his oats went to." (032-011) The story also illustrates the danger of the ignorant but well-intentioned owner — a type distinct from the deliberately cruel — whose lack of knowledge makes him dependent on servants he cannot evaluate. The gentleman farmer's intervention represents the novel's advocacy for knowledgeable horsemanship: his quick diagnosis of Beauty's condition ("he is as warm and damp as a horse just come up from grass") demonstrates the observational skill that all horse-owners should cultivate. (032-008)
