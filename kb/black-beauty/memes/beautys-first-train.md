# Beauty's First Train

During his breaking-in period at [[Farmer Grey]]'s farm, young [[Black Beauty]] was sent to a neighboring meadow beside a railway line as part of his desensitization training—an experience that gave him a lifelong fearlessness around steam engines. (004-008)

## 概述

As part of a carefully planned training regimen, Farmer Grey sent the four-year-old Black Beauty to spend a fortnight in a meadow that bordered a railway line. The purpose was to accustom the young horse to the sights and sounds of passing trains—a practice that proved highly effective when Beauty later traveled by train without panic. (004-008)

## 背景

By the time he was four years old, Black Beauty had already undergone several stages of breaking in at [[Farmer Grey]]'s hands: accepting the bit and bridle, wearing a saddle, being shod with iron shoes, and learning to work in harness. (004-004) Farmer Grey believed that a young horse should not only learn to carry a rider and pull a carriage, but also become accustomed to potentially frightening sights and sounds in a controlled environment. (004-012)

After the initial breaking-in stages, Beauty's master sent him away from the home farm:

> My master sent me for a fortnight to a neighboring farmer's, who had a meadow which was skirted on one side by the railway. Here were some sheep and cows, and I was turned in among them. (004-008)

## 事件经过

**The First Encounter**

Beauty's first sight of a train was a moment of pure terror. He was grazing peacefully near the fence separating the meadow from the railway when the train approached:

> I shall never forget the first train that ran by. I was feeding quietly near the pales which separated the meadow from the railway, when I heard a strange sound at a distance, and before I knew whence it came—with a rush and a clatter, and a puffing out of smoke—a long black train of something flew by, and was gone almost before I could draw my breath. (004-009)

Beauty's instinctive reaction was to flee to the farthest side of the meadow, where he stood snorting with astonishment and fear. (004-009)

**Gradual Habituation**

Throughout the day, more trains passed. Some drew up at the nearby station with what Beauty described as "an awful shriek and groan." (004-009) He observed that the cows in the same meadow continued eating calmly, barely raising their heads at the passing trains. (004-009)

Over the following days, Beauty's fear diminished as he learned through repeated exposure that the "terrible creature" never entered the field or caused him any harm. (004-010)

**The Outcome**

The training was a complete success. Beauty became as indifferent to trains as the cows and sheep, and the experience provided a lasting benefit:

> Since then I have seen many horses much alarmed and restive at the sight or sound of a steam engine; but thanks to my good master's care, I am as fearless at railway stations as in my own stable. (004-011)

## 后果与影响

The railway desensitization had practical benefits throughout Beauty's life:

1. **Immediate training value**: Beauty completed his breaking-in without developing any fear of mechanical noises, allowing him to work safely on roads shared with railway crossings.

2. **Later travel**: When Beauty was sold to the master of the livery stables in Bath, he traveled by train for the first time. Though he found it "new" and requiring "a good deal of courage," he "soon took it quietly" because he had already learned that trains posed no real danger. (029-010)

3. **Contrast with other horses**: Beauty frequently observed other horses that panicked at the sight or sound of steam engines, highlighting the value of Farmer Grey's foresight. (004-011)

Farmer Grey's approach to training was held up as a model:

> Now if any one wants to break in a young horse well, that is the way. (004-012)

## 主题意义

The railway desensitization episode exemplifies one of the novel's central themes: that good horsemanship is rooted in patience, foresight, and an understanding of equine psychology. Farmer Grey's method—controlled exposure in a safe environment—stands in deliberate contrast to the harsh "breaking" methods employed by less thoughtful horsemen throughout the book.

The episode also marks a subtle technological transition. The steam railway, cutting through rural England, represents the encroachment of industrialization on the pastoral world. Beauty's successful adaptation to this new reality parallels his journey through a changing Victorian society where horses increasingly shared roads and duties with machines.

The cows' calm indifference to the trains provides an implicit commentary: animals (and people) take their cues from those around them. Beauty learns not only from direct experience but also from observing the placid behavior of his meadow companions.
