# Black Beauty

Black Beauty is the narrator and protagonist of [[Anna Sewell]]'s novel.

He is a handsome black horse with a white star and one white foot.

His first-person account is the vehicle for Sewell's exploration of [[animal-cruelty|animal welfare] and moral responsibility.

His life — from colt on a farm, to carriage horse at [[Birtwick Park]], to [[London]] cab horse, to peaceful retirement — surveys the entire [[victorian-horse-economy|Victorian horse economy]].

## 角色概述

Beauty is born on a farm, the son of a wise mother who tells him: "I hope you will grow up gentle and good, and never learn bad ways" (001-005).

He is well-bred, with "a great deal of breeding about that horse" (052-005), and his quality is recognized even in old age by those who know horses.

His temperament is shaped by early kindness: broken gently by Farmer Grey, he develops a trust in humans that sustains him through later hardships.

Unlike [[Ginger]], whose spirit is crushed by cruelty, Beauty's gentle nature endures — a testament to the power of early kindness and to the luck of landing with good owners at critical moments.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Beauty's first home is a farm with meadows, a pond, and a grove of trees. His mother teaches him the values of good character: "Do your best wherever it is, and keep up your good name" (001-007).

Farmer Grey breaks him gently, "never allowing the whip" (006-007). This early gentleness establishes the baseline against which all later suffering is measured.

The first violence Beauty witnesses — a [[the-hunt-accident|hunt accident]] that kills a horse and its rider — foreshadows the novel's critique of human carelessness with animal life (003-007).

### Birtwick Park

At [[Birtwick Park]], Beauty finds his happiest home. Under [[John Manly]]'s care, he is groomed, exercised, and treated with consistent kindness.

> "I was quite happy in my new place; and if there was one thing more than another that I missed, it must not be thought I was discontented." (007-002)

He forms a close friendship with [[Ginger]] and [[Merrylegs]]. The three horses represent different temperaments — gentle, spirited, wise — and together form a miniature community.

Beauty's years at Birtwick demonstrate Sewell's ideal: a horse properly cared for, usefully employed, and treated as a sentient being rather than a machine.

### Earlshall 与下坡路

When [[Squire Gordon]] must sell his horses, Beauty and [[Ginger]] are sent to [[Earlshall Park]].

There the [[bearing-rein]] is tightened daily by Lady W——'s orders.

> "Day by day, hole by hole, our bearing reins were shortened, and instead of looking forward with pleasure to having my harness put on, I began to dread it." (024-020)

From Earlshall, Beauty passes through a series of owners.

Reuben Smith's drunken ride causes a fall that permanently scars Beauty's knees — marking him as damaged goods in the horse market (026-003).

The sequence of sales illustrates the Victorian horse economy's brutal logic: a horse is only as valuable as his last soundness, and a single accident can trigger an irreversible decline.

### London Cab Horse

Beauty's years as a London cab horse under [[Jerry Barker]] are grinding but dignified.

Jerry's rule — "If a man will not work, he shall not eat" (037-005) — is balanced by his genuine care for his horses. He provides regular rest, good food, and kindness.

Beauty reflects on the worth of honest labor: even a broken-down cab horse can serve with dignity when treated fairly.

The contrast between Jerry and the novel's other cab drivers — drunkards, cheats, and abusers — reinforces Sewell's argument that character, not class, determines how a man treats his animals.

### 退休

Beauty's final home is with Farmer Thoroughgood and his grandson Willie, who recognize the old horse's quality despite his ruined condition.

> "Poor old fellow! see, grandpapa, how well he understands kindness. Could not you buy him and make him young again?" (052-006)

The farmer's grandson articulates the novel's closing hope: kindness can restore what cruelty has broken.

Restored to health through good pasture and gentle care, Beauty spends his final years in peace.

The novel closes with his signature line of contentment: "I have nothing to fear; and here my story ends. My troubles are all over, and I am at home" (053-019).

## 主题意义

Black Beauty is both a character and a narrative device.

His first-person voice compels the reader to see the world through a horse's eyes, making abstract questions of [[animal-cruelty|animal welfare] viscerally felt. When Beauty suffers, the reader suffers with him.

His life journey through all levels of Victorian society allows Sewell to survey the entire horse economy — from the gentry's stables to the London streets — revealing how kindness and cruelty cross class lines.

Beauty's survival while [[Ginger]] dies in the streets is not a judgment on character but on luck.

The novel insists that both horses deserved a happy ending. The system, not their natures, determined their fates.

This structural argument — that cruelty is systemic, not personal — is what made *Black Beauty* a reform text, not merely a sentimental animal story.
