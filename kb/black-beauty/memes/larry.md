## 角色概述

Larry is a London cab driver who works alongside [[Jerry Barker]] at the cab stand. He represents the more cynical, financially driven side of the cab trade, contrasting sharply with Jerry's principled approach to work and animal welfare.

Larry is depicted as a man who prioritizes earning a shilling over the wellbeing of his horse, frequently using his whip and changing horses due to their rapid deterioration under his care. His exchanges with Jerry and Governor Grant provide some of the novel's most direct moral debates about religion, work ethics, and the treatment of working animals.

## 故事经历

Larry appears primarily in Chapter 38, where he interacts with Jerry Barker and other cab drivers at the cab stand. His first appearance shows him aggressively pursuing a fare:

> (038-009)Larry's cab was standing next to ours; he flung open the door, and said, "I'm your man, gentlemen! take my cab, my horse will get you there all right;" and as he shut them in, with a wink toward Jerry, said, "It's against his conscience to go beyond a jog-trot." Then slashing his jaded horse, he set off as hard as he could.

This scene establishes his character: willing to overwork his horse to secure a fare, and mockingly aware of Jerry's more ethical standards.

Later conversations reveal his worldview. When Jerry declines extra payment for speeding, Larry remarks bluntly:

> (038-032)"Well," said Larry, "you'll never be a rich man."

Governor Grant predicts Larry's financial fate:

> (038-034)As for you, Larry, you'll die poor; you spend too much in whipcord.

Larry defends himself by blaming his horses rather than his methods:

> (038-035)"Well," said Larry, "what is a fellow to do if his horse won't go without it?"

The most substantive exchange comes when the drivers discuss Sunday rest. Larry openly rejects religious conviction:

> (039-021)"All very well for you religious chaps to talk so," said Larry; "but I'll turn a shilling when I can. I don't believe in religion, for I don't see that your religious people are any better than the rest."

And cynically questions the value of Sunday rest for churchgoers:

> (039-026)"And what would all the good people do if they could not get to their favorite preachers?" said Larry.

## 性格特征

Larry embodies several key traits that serve as a foil to Jerry Barker:

| Trait | Manifestation | Contrast with Jerry |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Cynicism** | Dismisses religion and ethical principles | Jerry's sincere faith |
| **Profit-driven** | Prioritizes fares over horse welfare | Jerry refuses extra pay for speeding |
| **Self-justifying** | Blames horses for his own harsh methods | Jerry takes responsibility for training |
| **Rough with animals** | Heavy whip use, frequent horse changes | Jerry's gentle, patient handling |
| **Fatalistic** | Attributes his struggles to "bad luck" | Jerry believes in personal accountability |

## 与其他角色的关系

- **[[Jerry Barker]]** — Larry's primary foil. Their debates about religion, money, and horse treatment highlight Jerry's moral integrity. Larry respects Jerry enough to engage with him but dismisses his principles as impractical.
- **[[Governor Grant]]** — An older, wiser cab driver who directly criticizes Larry's methods, predicting he will "die poor" from excessive whipcord spending.
- **Truman** — A fellow cab driver who argues for Sunday rest, prompting Larry's most explicit rejection of religious principles.
- **His horses** — Though unnamed, Larry's horses suffer from his rough treatment, requiring frequent replacement — a fact Governor Grant points out as evidence of Larry's poor horsemanship.

## 主题意义

Larry serves as an important thematic device in *Black Beauty*. Through his dialogues, Anna Sewell addresses:

1. **The economic pressures on working-class men** — Larry's desperation for fares reflects real Victorian cab drivers' struggles.
2. **The moral dimension of animal treatment** — His whip use shows how financial pressure can lead to cruelty, even without malice.
3. **Religious hypocrisy vs. genuine faith** — Larry's critique that "religious people are no better than the rest" prompts Jerry's nuanced defense of true religion as distinct from empty churchgoing.
