On New Year's Eve, [[Jerry Barker]] waits for over four hours in freezing sleet while his wealthy clients enjoy a card party — a night of exposure that leaves him with life-threatening bronchitis, forces him to abandon cab work, and ultimately leads his family to a new life in the country. (048-001)

## 概述

The Christmas and New Year season, a time of merriment for the wealthy, was for London cabmen and their horses a season of grueling labor. Jerry Barker, already suffering from a bad cough, took [[Black Beauty]] out on New Year's Eve to drive two gentlemen to a West End party. Told to return at eleven, they waited — as the clock struck twelve, then half-past twelve, then a quarter past one — in driving sleet with no shelter. By the time the men emerged, Jerry's voice was nearly gone. He struggled home, collapsed, and was diagnosed with severe bronchitis. The doctor warned that if Jerry had been a drinking man, "it would have burned him up like a piece of paper." Though he survived, he was ordered never to return to cab work — a decree that reshaped the Barker family's entire future. (048-001) (048-005) (048-007) (048-012) (048-017) (048-028)

> For some people Christmas and the New Year are very merry times; but for cabmen and cabmen's horses it is no holiday, though it may be a harvest. There are so many parties, balls, and places of amusement open that the work is hard and often late. Sometimes driver and horse have to wait for hours in the rain or frost, shivering with the cold, while the merry people within are dancing away to the music. I wonder if the beautiful ladies ever think of the weary cabman waiting on his box, and his patient beast standing, till his legs get stiff with cold.
>
> (048-001)

## 背景

Jerry Barker had worked as a London cabman for years, maintaining his principles — no Sunday work, no hard driving for extra pay, no drinking — despite the profession's pressures. His health had already shown signs of strain: during the previous winter he had developed "a bad cough that followed me up quite into the warm weather." [[Polly Barker]], his wife, worried about him constantly.

The Christmas season was particularly hard. Beauty notes that "for cabmen and cabmen's horses it is no holiday, though it may be a harvest." Parties and balls meant late nights; drivers and horses waited for hours in rain or frost "while the merry people within are dancing away to the music." Jerry took most of the evening work himself, fearing that his younger horse [[Hotspur]] might take cold. His cough was already bad as Christmas week wore on, but Polly sat up for him every night, coming out with a lantern, "looking anxious and troubled." (048-001) (048-002)

## 事件经过

### The Endless Wait

On New Year's Eve, Jerry and Beauty took two gentlemen to a house in a West End Square. They were told to return at eleven, with the casual warning that "as it is a card party, you may have to wait a few minutes, but don't be late." Jerry, always punctual, was at the door as the clock struck eleven. The quarters chimed — one, two, three — and then the clock struck twelve. The door did not open. (048-003) (048-004)

The night grew vicious. Squalls of rain during the day gave way to "sharp, driving sleet, which seemed to come all the way round; it was very cold, and there was no shelter." Jerry got off his box and pulled a cloth more tightly around Beauty's neck. He paced up and down, stamping his feet; he beat his arms against his body, but that set him coughing. Eventually he opened the cab door and sat on the floor with his feet on the pavement, finding what little shelter he could. (048-005)

At half-past twelve Jerry rang the bell. The servant's response was dismissive: "Oh, yes, you'll be wanted safe enough; you must not go, it will soon be over." Jerry sat down again, his voice by now "so hoarse I could hardly hear him." (048-006)

### The Release and the Journey Home

At a quarter past one — over four hours after their arrival — the two gentlemen emerged. They got into the cab without a word of apology. They directed Jerry to drive nearly two miles. Beauty's legs were "numb with cold," and he thought he would stumble. When they arrived, the men complained about the charge rather than expressing any regret for the wait. Jerry, who "never charged more than was his due, so he never took less," collected his fare — "hard-earned money" indeed. (048-007)

When they finally reached home, Jerry could hardly speak. His cough was "dreadful." Polly, asking no questions, opened the door and held the lantern. Jerry managed only a hoarse whisper: "Can't I do something? ... Yes; get Jack something warm, and then boil me some gruel." Even in his extremity, he gave Beauty his usual rub-down and climbed to the hayloft for an extra bundle of straw. Polly brought Beauty a warm mash, and then they locked the door. (048-008) (048-009) (048-010) (048-011)

### The Crisis

The next morning no one came until late. Harry, Jerry's son, cleaned and fed the horses in silence — no whistling, no singing. At noon Dolly came with him, crying. From their hushed conversation Beauty gathered that "Jerry was dangerously ill, and the doctor said it was a bad case." For two days the household was in crisis; Polly stayed constantly at Jerry's side. (048-012)

On the third day, [[Governor Grant]] — a fellow cabman and friend — came to the stable. Harry told him the diagnosis: "bronchitis." The doctor thought it would "turn one way or another tonight." Grant shook his head: "I know two men who died of that last week; it takes 'em off in no time." But Harry clung to hope: "The doctor said that father had a better chance than most men, because he didn't drink. He said yesterday the fever was so high that if father had been a drinking man it would have burned him up like a piece of paper." (048-015) (048-016) (048-017)

Grant's response captured the community's regard for Jerry: "If there's any rule that good men should get over these things, I'm sure he will, my boy; he's the best man I know." (048-019)

### Recovery and New Life

The next morning brought better news: "Father is better. Mother hopes he will get over it." Jerry survived, but the doctor's order was absolute — he "must never go back to the cab work again if he wished to be an old man." (048-022) (048-028)

The crisis triggered an extraordinary chain of kindness and community support. Governor Grant volunteered to work Hotspur daily, bringing Polly half the earnings to help feed the horses while Jerry recovered. Meanwhile, Polly had written to her former mistress, [[Mrs. Fowler]], who responded with an offer: a cottage on her country estate, a position as coachman for Jerry, and opportunities for the children. The family would leave London for a healthier, more stable life in the countryside. (048-025) (048-039)

## 后果与影响

For Jerry, the illness was a turning point that ended his cab career but opened a safer, healthier chapter as a country coachman. For his family, the move to Mrs. Fowler's estate offered [[Harry Barker]] a future as a groom or gardener rather than a cabman, and Dolly access to a good school. (048-039) (048-040)

For Beauty, however, Jerry's illness was catastrophic. The horses and cab had to be sold. Though Grant took Hotspur and Jerry insisted Beauty should not return to "cab work again with just anybody," the separation was permanent. Beauty records: "I never saw him after that New Year's eve." Polly and the children came to say goodbye; Polly kissed his neck, Dolly wept, and Harry stroked him in silence. Beauty was led away to an uncertain future. "Since I left Birtwick I had never been so happy as with my dear master Jerry," he reflects, "but three years of cab work, even under the best conditions, will tell on one's strength, and I felt that I was not the horse that I had been." (048-042) (048-044)

## 主题意义

Jerry's dangerous illness crystallizes several of the novel's most important themes. First, it exposes the brutal asymmetry of Victorian class relations: wealthy men enjoying a card party think nothing of leaving a cabman and his horse to freeze for over four hours in sleet, and when they finally emerge, they complain about the fare rather than apologize. Second, it demonstrates the physical cost of cab work — a profession that, even under the best master, wore down both men and horses.

Most significantly, the episode illustrates the novel's moral economy. Jerry's survival is explicitly attributed to his sobriety — the doctor says a drinking man would have died. The community's response — Grant's volunteer labor, Mrs. Fowler's offer — rewards Jerry's lifetime of integrity. And yet the resolution is bittersweet: Jerry is saved but Beauty is sold, a reminder that in the equine world of the novel, even the best human relationships are temporary, and a working horse's fate always hangs by a thread.
