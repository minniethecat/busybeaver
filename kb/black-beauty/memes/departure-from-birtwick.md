After three happy years at [[Birtwick Park]], [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] learn that their beloved mistress is gravely ill. The [[Squire Gordon|Gordon family]] must move abroad, and the horses — along with their human companions — are scattered to uncertain futures.

## 概述

The Departure from Birtwick, narrated in Chapter 22, marks the end of the novel's first act and the happiest period of Beauty's life. Mrs. Gordon's worsening illness forces the family to relocate to a warmer climate. The estate is broken up: Beauty and Ginger are sold to [[Earlshall Park]], [[Merrylegs]] is given to the vicar, and [[John Manly]] leaves to become a horse-trainer. It is one of the most emotionally charged events in the novel, portraying the bond between the Gordons and all who served them — both human and equine.

## 背景

Beauty has lived at Birtwick Park for three years, experiencing kindness, good treatment, and the companionship of Ginger and Merrylegs under the care of John Manly and [[James Howard]] (022-001). But reports of Mrs. Gordon's declining health grow increasingly grave: "The doctor was often at the house, and the master looked grave and anxious" (022-001). Eventually, the doctors order the family to move abroad for the sake of the mistress's health.

Plans are made for everyone and every animal. The master personally arranges for each horse's future. Ginger and Beauty are sold together to Earlshall Park. Merrylegs is given to the vicar, Mr. Blomefield, where Joe Green will continue to care for him. John Manly receives offers of several positions but declines them all, holding out for the right opportunity (022-005).

## 事件经过

The departure unfolds over several days. First, Miss Jessie and Miss Flora leave with their governess, coming to the stable to bid farewell: "They hugged poor Merrylegs like an old friend, and so indeed he was" (022-003).

On the final evening, the master visits the stable to give his horses a last pat. "He seemed very low-spirited; I knew that by his voice" (022-005). He asks John about his plans, and John reveals he wishes to find work with a first-rate colt-breaker and horse-trainer — a role the master wholeheartedly endorses, saying: "You understand horses, and somehow they understand you" (022-008). He provides John with a reference and tries to thank him for his years of faithful service, but John cannot bear it: "Pray, don't, sir, I can't bear it; you and my dear mistress have done so much for me that I could never repay it" (022-009).

On the last morning, Ginger and Beauty bring the carriage to the hall door for the final time. The servants carry the mistress out, and the master presses something into John's hand, saying: "Good-by, John; God bless you" (022-010). He adds: "We shall not forget any of you" (022-011).

The carriage drives slowly through the park and village, past people standing at their doors for a last glimpse, calling out "God bless them" (022-012). At the railway station, the mistress speaks her final words to John: "Good-by, John. God bless you" (022-013). John makes no answer — perhaps he cannot speak.

After the train disappears, John returns to the carriage. His words are devastating in their simplicity: "We shall never see her again... never" (022-015). He drives slowly home, but as Beauty observes: "it was not our home now" (022-015).


> I held my face close to him; that was all I could do to say good-by; and then he was gone, and I have never seen him since.
>
> (024-010)

## 后果与影响

The departure scatters the Birtwick community. Beauty and Ginger are sent to Earlshall Park, where they encounter the bearing rein and the harsh regime of Lady W——, setting in motion the events that lead to Ginger's eventual decline and death. Beauty's life enters a long period of instability, passing through a series of owners of varying kindness.

John Manly's words about horses and mutual understanding prove prophetic: he does become a successful horse-trainer. The endorsement from Squire Gordon — a respected judge of both horses and men — opens doors for him.

The departure also reinforces the novel's message about the fragility of a good situation for working animals. Beauty's happiness at Birtwick was entirely dependent on the character of his owners; when circumstances beyond anyone's control force a change, the horses have no say in their fate.

## 主题意义

The Departure from Birtwick embodies the novel's recurring theme of **powerlessness**: no matter how well a horse serves, its fate rests entirely in human hands. Beauty has done everything right — he saved Mrs. Gordon's life, served faithfully for three years — yet he cannot prevent being sold.

The event also illustrates **the ideal master-servant relationship** that Sewell advocates. Squire Gordon does not simply dispose of his property; he takes personal responsibility for finding good situations for every horse and every servant. His farewell gifts, his reference for John, and his arrangements for Merrylegs all reflect the kind of conscientious stewardship that the novel holds up as a model.

Finally, the departure scene is one of the novel's most powerful depictions of **mutual grief across class and species boundaries**. The master is "low-spirited," John cannot speak, and even Beauty — a horse — perceives the finality of the moment. Sewell suggests that the bonds formed through kindness and good treatment are real, deep, and painful to break.
