# Horse Care

Horse care encompasses the daily practices of feeding, watering, grooming, stabling, and exercising horses. In *Black Beauty*, the quality of care a horse receives serves as a direct index of its owner's character: good owners provide clean stables, regular meals, thorough grooming, and rest; bad owners neglect these duties, leading to illness, exhaustion, and early death.

## 定义

Proper horse care in the Victorian era included: feeding hay, oats, and bran mashes at regular intervals; providing clean, fresh water; grooming the coat to maintain circulation and prevent skin disease; cleaning hooves; maintaining clean, dry bedding; ensuring adequate ventilation in stables; and granting sufficient rest between periods of work. A well-cared-for horse could work reliably for fifteen to twenty years; a neglected one might break down within a few.


> Kindness is all the physic she wants, and patience is all the physic she needs.
>
> (009-011)

## 在书中的体现

At Birtwick Park, horse care is exemplary. John Manly maintains immaculate stables, feeds the horses on schedule, and ensures they receive proper exercise and rest. When Ginger struggles, John prescribes not punishment but a "bran mash" and gentle treatment (009-009) — care as medicine.

The contrast is stark at other stables. Ginger describes her previous owner's establishment where horses were valued only for appearance, not health. The fashionable stable at Earlshall prioritizes style over welfare, tightening bearing reins at the expense of the horses' comfort (024-020). Later in Beauty's life, a negligent stable boy fails to provide proper care, nearly killing Beauty through ignorance.

### 关键场景

**The Birtwick standard.** Squire Gordon's stable is the novel's ideal. Horses are well-fed, comfortably stabled, and treated with consistent kindness. John Manly's philosophy — "kindness is all the physic she wants" (009-011) — encapsulates the preventive value of good care.

**The consequences of neglect.** At various points in the narrative, poor stable management leads to illness and injury. Wet bedding causes hoof problems; irregular feeding weakens horses; lack of grooming allows disease; overwork without rest causes collapse. Each instance reinforces Sewell's argument that good care is not a luxury but a necessity.

**Jerry's Sunday rest.** Jerry Barker refuses to work his cab horses on Sundays, understanding that regular rest is essential: "the horses would have a whole day's rest" each week. This practice, unusual among cab drivers, keeps his horses healthy and willing.

## 历史背景

Victorian horse care knowledge varied enormously. Skilled grooms and coachmen understood equine needs, but many horse owners — particularly those who viewed horses as disposable assets — provided only minimal care. The RSPCA and other reform organizations published guides on proper horse management. Sewell's detailed descriptions of both good and bad care practices served an educational purpose, teaching readers what to look for and demand.
