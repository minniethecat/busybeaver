# Duchess

The mother of [[Black Beauty]] and [[Rob Roy]], owned by [[Farmer Grey]]. A gentle and wise old mare who gave Beauty his earliest lessons in good behavior.

## 角色概述

Duchess was an old mare owned by [[Farmer Grey]], known for her gentle temperament and wisdom. She was the mother of both [[Black Beauty]] and [[Rob Roy]], and Farmer Grey affectionately called her "Pet." (002-007) Her early guidance to the young colt Beauty — advising him to be good, gentle, and hardworking — echoes throughout the novel as Beauty navigates the challenges of his life.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Duchess lived at Farmer Grey's farm as a valued broodmare. She gave birth to [[Rob Roy]] first, and later to [[Black Beauty]], who was her last foal. She spent her days grazing in the meadow with Beauty, teaching him about the world through conversation. (002-007)

### 关键转折

Duchess's most significant contribution comes through her advice to young Beauty. She teaches him that a horse's future depends on his character and behavior — if he is good, gentle, and willing, he will be well-treated; if he is ill-tempered, he will suffer. This formative lesson shapes Beauty's approach to every master and situation he encounters throughout the novel.

### 结局

By the time [[John Manly]] mentions her to [[James Howard]], Duchess is referred to as "Farmer Grey's old Duchess," suggesting she has either passed away or grown very old by then. (006-019) Her legacy lives on through Black Beauty, who frequently recalls her advice during difficult times.

## 性格特征

- Gentle and wise, embodying maternal patience
- Practical and realistic about a horse's place in the world
- Affectionate — Farmer Grey's pet name for her ("Pet") reflects their bond
- A moral teacher whose simple philosophy guides Beauty throughout his life

## 主题意义

Duchess represents the importance of early moral education and the transmission of values across generations. Her advice to Beauty — that goodness and hard work are rewarded — forms the ethical backbone of the novel. At the same time, the story subtly questions her philosophy: Beauty is good and gentle throughout his life, yet he still suffers greatly, suggesting that individual virtue alone is not enough to protect against systemic cruelty. Her character anchors the novel's exploration of nature versus nurture in shaping a horse's fate.
