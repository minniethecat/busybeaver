# Rory

A gentle and honest horse who shared a livery stable with [[Black Beauty]] during his time with [[Mr. Barry]], tragically disabled in a reckless driving accident.

## 角色概述

Rory was a livery horse kept at the same stable as [[Black Beauty]] when both were under the ownership — or rather, the hiring — of [[Mr. Barry]]. (031-007) He is described as a good-natured horse whose life was cut short by the carelessness of a drunk driver, illustrating the vulnerability of hired horses in Victorian England.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Rory was kept at a livery stable where gentlemen could hire horses for their own use. He was paired with [[Black Beauty]] on several occasions, and the two horses shared a quiet companionship. Unlike Beauty, who was owned directly by Mr. Barry, Rory belonged to the livery stable and was available for hire by any paying customer.

### 关键转折

One spring evening, Rory and [[Black Beauty]] had been out together for the day. (031-007) Later, Rory was hired by a man who drove recklessly and at high speed. The driver lost control, and Rory's flesh was torn open in the resulting crash. Despite the severity of his injury, the driver showed no remorse — he simply remarked that he didn't care. (031-008)

### 结局

After the accident, Rory was permanently disabled and could no longer work. His fate after being disabled is not explicitly described, but the implication is that a horse who could no longer earn his keep at a livery stable faced a grim future — likely sold for whatever little he could fetch, or worse. (031-010)

## 主题意义

Rory's story exposes the dangers of the livery stable system, where horses were treated as disposable rental equipment. Unlike [[Black Beauty]], who eventually finds his way to better circumstances, Rory is destroyed by a single act of recklessness — and no one is held accountable. His fate illustrates how the anonymity of hired horses made cruelty invisible: no owner was there to demand accountability, and the horse had no one to speak for him. Within the novel's moral framework, Rory is the silent victim, the horse whose suffering goes unrecorded and unpunished.
