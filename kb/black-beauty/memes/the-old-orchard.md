# The Old Orchard

A sunlit orchard at [[Birtwick Park]] where [[Black Beauty]], [[Merrylegs]], and the Gordon children spent their leisure hours. It became the setting for some of Beauty's most cherished memories, including conversations with [[John Manly]] and a pivotal talk with [[Ginger]].

## 概述

The old orchard lay adjacent to [[The Home Paddock|home-paddock]] and the stables at Birtwick Park (005-002). It was a place of rest and recreation for the horses, with cool soft grass under the apple-trees where they could graze and socialize freely (007-004). The orchard features throughout the early chapters of the novel and reappears in Beauty's final nostalgic reverie, where he imagines himself "still in the orchard at Birtwick, standing with my old friends under the apple-trees" (053-022).

## 地理位置

The orchard was situated within the grounds of Birtwick Park, adjacent to the home paddock and close to the stables (005-002). It was part of the larger estate belonging to [[Squire Gordon]], encompassing the park, the common, the meadow, and the Highwood. The layout allowed horses to move freely between the orchard and the home paddock during their leisure hours (007-004).

> It was a great treat to us to be turned out into the home paddock or the old orchard; the grass was so cool and soft to our feet, the air so sweet, and the freedom to do as we liked was so pleasant—to gallop, to lie down, and roll over on our backs, or to nibble the sweet grass. Then it was a very good time for talking, as we stood together under the shade of the large chestnut tree.
>
> (007-004)

The horses spent many happy hours in the orchard. The grass was "so cool and soft to our feet" and the air "so sweet" (007-004). Beauty describes how he, Ginger, and Merrylegs "used to gallop all together round and round the field" before settling in the orchard (010-008). The orchard functioned as a social space where the horses could interact freely, away from the formality of the stable.

## 象征意义

The orchard represents the ideal of pastoral happiness — a space of freedom, companionship, and gentle treatment that defines the Birtwick chapters. It stands in deliberate contrast to the harsh stables and city streets that Beauty later endures. That Beauty's very last thought in the novel returns him to the orchard (053-022) underlines its thematic role as the symbolic heart of the novel's vision of a good life for horses.

The orchard also functions as a narrative device: it is the setting where Ginger reveals her tragic backstory (011-011), making the orchard a place where the novel's central animal-welfare concerns emerge through personal testimony rather than authorial preaching.
