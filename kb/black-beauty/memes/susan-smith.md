# Susan Smith

Susan Smith was the wife of [[Reuben Smith]], the head groom at [[Earlshall Park]], and the mother of their six children. Her story is one of the novel's most poignant illustrations of how the consequences of drink ripple outward from the individual to destroy entire families. (028-013)

## 角色概述

Susan Smith appears only briefly in the narrative — in the aftermath of her husband's death — but her fate embodies the novel's temperance message in human terms. She is first mentioned by name when Ned and Robert, sent by "Smith's anxious wife" to search for him, discover Reuben's body on the road. (028-007) The narrative describes her as "awfully pale" when she came to ask if Reuben had not come home, trying to "make believe she was not a bit anxious." (028-007)

## 故事经历

### 丈夫之死

Reuben Smith, though a skilled and knowledgeable horseman, had a fatal weakness for drink. After being left in charge at Earlshall, he rode [[Black Beauty]] — whom Lady Anne called "Black Auster" — to town, got drunk at the inn, and insisted on riding home despite having lost a shoe. On the rough stones, Beauty's unprotected hoof was cut to pieces, and Reuben was thrown and killed. (028-002) (028-007)

Susan had sensed something was wrong that evening and went to Ned's house asking about her husband. The following day, the inquest returned a verdict of "accidental death," and Susan was left a widow with six children and no means of support. (028-013)

### 进入济贫院

After Reuben was buried, Susan — "as she had no home or relations" — was forced to take her six children into the grim Union House, the Victorian workhouse for the destitute. (028-013) Her anguished cry at the inquest crystallizes the novel's systemic indictment of the alcohol trade:

> Everybody pitied Susan. She was nearly out of her mind; she kept saying over and over again, "Oh! he was so good—so good! It was all that cursed drink; why will they sell that cursed drink?"
>
> (028-013)

## 性格特征

- **Devoted wife**: Her anxious search for Reuben and her grief after his death show deep love and devotion
- **Perceptive**: She sensed something was wrong before anyone else, showing an intuitive awareness of danger
- **Victim of systemic failure**: Her question — "why will they sell that cursed drink?" — shifts blame from the individual to the system

## 主题意义

Susan Smith's story extends the novel's temperance message beyond the individual drinker to the collateral damage inflicted on families. Her descent into the Union House with six children represents the complete social death that awaited working-class widows in Victorian England. The narrative's choice to give her the final, anguished question — "why will they sell that cursed drink?" — redirects moral judgment from the drunkard to the society that permits and profits from the liquor trade. (028-013)
