# Dolly Barker and the Election Mob

On the morning before the election, [[Dolly Barker]] ran into the yard sobbing, her dress spattered with mud—the victim of political street boys who had harassed her for wearing blue. Her brother [[Harry Barker|Harry]] retaliated with his fists, and [[Jerry Barker]] used the incident to teach his children a lesson about the hollowness of party loyalty. (045-007)

## 概述

As [[Jerry Barker]] was harnessing [[Black Beauty]] for the day's work, his young daughter Dolly came into the yard in tears, her blue frock and white pinafore covered in mud. A group of "orange" political boys had thrown dirt at her and called her a "blue ragamuffin." Harry, her brother, had already given the bullies a thrashing. Rather than simply comforting his children, Jerry used the moment to deliver a pointed critique of electoral partisanship—arguing that party colors were meaningless symbols that turned ordinary people against each other. (045-007)

## 背景

The Barker household had already been caught up in the election preparations. Earlier that day, Polly Barker had come to the yard to tell Jerry that a local gentleman—"Mr. B——"—had asked about Jerry's vote and wanted to hire his cab for election day. Jerry refused firmly: "I should not like to have it pasted over with their great bills, and as to making Jack and Captain race about to the public-houses to bring up half-drunken voters, why, I think 'twould be an insult to the horses." (045-002)

When [[Polly Barker|Polly]] asked if Jerry would at least vote for the gentleman, who claimed to share Jerry's politics, Jerry refused on principle. He knew the man's trade and believed he was "blind as to what workingmen want." Jerry concluded: "I dare say they'll be angry, but every man must do what he thinks to be the best for his country." (045-006)

## 事件经过

**The Attack on Dolly**

The morning before the election, as Jerry was putting Beauty into the shafts, Dolly burst into the yard:

> Dolly came into the yard sobbing and crying, with her little blue frock and white pinafore spattered all over with mud. (045-007)

When Jerry asked what had happened, Dolly could barely speak through her tears:

> "Those naughty boys," she sobbed, "have thrown the dirt all over me, and called me a little raga—raga—" (045-009)

**Harry's Defense**

Harry rushed in, still angry from the confrontation, and finished his sister's sentence:

> "They called her a little 'blue' ragamuffin, father," said Harry, who ran in looking very angry; "but I have given it to them; they won't insult my sister again. I have given them a thrashing they will remember; a set of cowardly, rascally 'orange' blackguards." (045-010)

**Jerry's Response**

Jerry first comforted Dolly with a kiss and sent her inside to her mother. (045-011) Then he turned to Harry with a more complex message. He approved of Harry defending his sister—"I hope you will always defend your sister, and give anybody who insults her a good thrashing"—but immediately drew a firm line: "mind, I won't have any election blackguarding on my premises." (045-013)

Jerry then delivered a critique that cut across both political factions:

> "There are as many 'blue' blackguards as there are 'orange', and as many white as there are purple, or any other color, and I won't have any of my family mixed up with it. Even women and children are ready to quarrel for the sake of a color, and not one in ten of them knows what it is about." (045-013)

When Harry protested that he thought blue stood for Liberty, Jerry dismantled the notion with bitter wit:

> "My boy, Liberty does not come from colors, they only show party, and all the liberty you can get out of them is, liberty to get drunk at other people's expense, liberty to ride to the poll in a dirty old cab, liberty to abuse any one that does not wear your color, and to shout yourself hoarse at what you only half-understand—that's your liberty!" (045-015)

Jerry concluded with his genuine political conviction: "An election is a very serious thing; at least it ought to be, and every man ought to vote according to his conscience, and let his neighbor do the same." (045-017)

## 后果与影响

The incident reveals several important dimensions of the Barker family and the novel's social vision:

1. **Family solidarity**: The Barkers function as a unit—Harry defends Dolly, Jerry defends both, Polly provides comfort inside. The family is a bulwark against the chaotic forces of the wider world.

2. **Jerry's moral authority**: Jerry navigates a difficult line—endorsing Harry's physical defense of his sister while rejecting the partisan logic that provoked the attack. His ability to separate the principle (defending family) from the context (political tribalism) demonstrates the independent thinking that characterizes him throughout the novel.

3. **Working-class political consciousness**: Jerry's refusal to rent his cab for election work, his rejection of the gentleman candidate who doesn't understand workingmen's needs, and his critique of performative partisanship all paint a portrait of a politically engaged but independent-minded working man—a rare and sympathetic representation in Victorian literature.

4. **Children as moral barometers**: The attack on Dolly—a small child whose only "crime" was wearing a blue dress—exposes the absurdity and cruelty of adult political divisions when they filter down to street level.

## 主题意义

The election-mob episode operates on two levels. On the surface, it is a domestic scene—a father comforting his daughter and instructing his son—that showcases the warmth and moral clarity of the Barker household. But Jerry's speech to Harry transforms the moment into one of the novel's most explicit political statements.

Sewell published *Black Beauty* in 1877, in the wake of the Second Reform Act (1867) that had extended the franchise to many working-class men. Jerry Barker, a cab driver who owns his own horse and cab, is precisely the kind of newly enfranchised voter the Reform Act created. His political philosophy—vote your conscience, ignore party colors, see through the performative theatrics of electioneering—reflects both the hopes and the anxieties of the era about what working-class political participation would mean.

Jerry's critique of "colors" is also a class critique. The wealthy "gentleman" candidate who "is blind as to what workingmen want" mirrors the actual political dynamics of the period, where working-class voters were often expected to defer to their social superiors. Jerry refuses this deference without becoming a partisan himself—he will not vote for the gentleman, but he will not let his family become "blue" partisans either.

The episode's final turn—from political critique back to the everyday reality of harnessing a cab horse—is characteristically Sewell. Jerry's politics are not abstract; they are grounded in his daily work, his family, and his care for his horses. The election, like everything else, is measured by its impact on the vulnerable: Dolly in her mud-spattered pinafore, and the cab horses who would be raced to public houses if Jerry had accepted the election hire.
