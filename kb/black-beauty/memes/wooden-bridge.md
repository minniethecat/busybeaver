# The Wooden Bridge

The Wooden Bridge is a low, flood-prone bridge near the toll-bar on the road from [[Birtwick Park]]. It is the setting for one of the novel's most dramatic episodes, in which [[Black Beauty]]'s instinctive refusal to cross saves the lives of [[Squire Gordon]] and [[John Manly]].

## Overview

The bridge spans a river on the route from Birtwick Park to the nearby town. It is described as a "low wooden bridge" (013-001) susceptible to flooding during heavy rain. Its structural vulnerability makes it a place of hidden danger, and it becomes the stage for Beauty's most heroic act.

## Location

The bridge lies beyond the toll-gate on the road from Birtwick. It crosses a river whose banks become treacherous in wet weather. During the storm episode, many of the surrounding meadows are flooded, and the river rises dangerously high:

> "The river banks were rather high, and the bridge, instead of rising, went across just level, so that in the middle, if the river was full, the water would be half way up the bridge." (013-013)

## In the Story

### The Stormy Day

On a stormy autumn evening, [[John Manly]] is driving [[Squire Gordon]] back to [[Birtwick Park]] when they approach the wooden bridge. Despite the urgency of getting home, Beauty senses danger and refuses to cross:

> "He gave me a sharp cut; I jumped, but I dared not go forward." (013-010)

John's faith in his horse proves decisive. Rather than forcing Beauty onward, he trusts the animal's judgment. Moments later, the bridge collapses into the flooded river:

> "The bridge at the wooden bridge has been carried away by the flood." (013-018)

The toll-bar man confirms that had they attempted crossing, all three would have drowned. The Squire later reflects on the debt he owes his horse: "You saved our lives" (013-020).

## Symbolic Significance

The Wooden Bridge episode serves as one of Sewell's strongest arguments for listening to animals. Beauty's refusal is not disobedience but intelligence — he perceives a danger his human companions cannot see. John Manly's trust in his horse, rather than punishing him for balking, models the ideal human-animal relationship the novel advocates.

The bridge itself symbolizes the fragility of human constructions and the wisdom of natural instinct. In a novel that repeatedly criticizes fashionable human follies — bearing-reins, docked tails, blinkers — the bridge collapse stands as a literal and figurative indictment of ignoring warnings.
