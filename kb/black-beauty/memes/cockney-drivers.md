# Cockney Drivers

In *Black Beauty*, the term "cockney drivers" refers to a category of London cab and carriage hirers who — having never owned or cared for a horse themselves — treated horses as machines rather than living creatures. (031-006) Their ignorance and recklessness were a distinct source of equine suffering in the novel's portrayal of urban horse life.

## 定义

Cockney drivers were city people, typically from the working or lower-middle classes of London, who hired "job horses" for short-term use. They had no long-term stake in the animals' welfare and drove them hard and carelessly, with no understanding of equine needs. (029-002)

The term appears in Chapter 29, titled "Cockneys," and Chapter 31, "A Humbug," where Black Beauty catalogues the "different kinds of bad and ignorant driving" to which job horses were subjected. (031-006)

## 在书中的体现

As a "job horse" let out to all manner of hirers, Black Beauty experienced the full range of bad driving firsthand. He notes that he was "oftener let out to the ignorant drivers than some of the other horses" precisely because of his good temper and gentleness. (028-001)

### 蒸汽机式驾驶

Beauty describes the "steam-engine style of driving" as the most wearing of all. Drivers who "never had a horse of their own and generally traveled by rail" treated a horse as they would a locomotive — starting at full speed and maintaining a relentless pace with no consideration for hills, road surface, or the animal's stamina. (029-003)

> Then these cockneys, instead of starting at an easy pace, as a gentleman would do, generally set off at full speed from the very stable-yard; and when they want to stop, they haul the reins so suddenly that we are nearly thrown on our haunches.
>
> (031-006)

This style of driving "wears us up faster than any other kind," Beauty observes. "I would far rather go twenty miles with a good considerate driver than I would go ten with some of these." (029-003)

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell's critique of cockney drivers is not purely a class argument — she does not condemn city people as inherently cruel. Rather, she indicts a system in which horses were treated as disposable machines, rented out to anyone who could pay. The "steam-engine style" is a product of ignorance, but also an indictment of a society where cheap horse hire made such ignorance lethal.
