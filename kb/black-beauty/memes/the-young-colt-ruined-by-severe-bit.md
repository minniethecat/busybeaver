# The Young Colt Ruined by the Severe Bit

A young horse named [[Hotspur]], stabled in a nobleman's coach-house, was driven into a frenzy by an overly sharp curb bit and over-tightened check-rein — bolting, smashing into another carriage, and throwing his lordship to the ground. Sold cheap as damaged goods, he found redemption under [[Jerry Barker]]'s gentle hand. (047-014)

## 概述

After [[Captain]]'s death, Jerry Barker needed a replacement cab horse. Through an acquaintance working as an under-groom in a nobleman's stables, he learned of Hotspur — a fine five-year-old brown horse, valuable but now blemished and disgraced. The horse had run away, smashed into another carriage, and flung his lordship out. The under-groom believed the accident was caused not by vice but by the horse's tender mouth being tortured with a sharp curb, tight check-rein, and martingale — literally driving the spirited young horse mad. Jerry, who understood high-spirited horses, bought him and proved the groom right. (047-014)

## 背景

The accident happened after Captain, Jerry's old white cab horse and Beauty's stable companion, was crippled in a collision with a drunk drayman's runaway dray. The farrier said Captain might recover enough for carting but never for cab work. Jerry, refusing to sell his old friend into hard labor, had Captain humanely put down. (047-004)

Jerry then needed to find a replacement. An acquaintance who worked in a nobleman's stables told him about a young horse that had to be sold. The coachman had orders to get rid of him as well as he could. (047-014)

## 事件经过

**The Groom's Account**

The under-groom described what happened with unusual insight:

> "There is not a bit of vice in him. His mouth is very tender, and I think myself that was the cause of the accident; you see he had just been clipped, and the weather was bad, and he had not had exercise enough, and when he did go out he was as full of spring as a balloon. Our governor (the coachman, I mean) had him harnessed in as tight and strong as he could, with the martingale, and the check-rein, a very sharp curb, and the reins put in at the bottom bar. It is my belief that it made the horse mad, being tender in the mouth and so full of spirit." (047-016)

The groom's analysis reveals the chain of causation: a young, spirited horse, freshly clipped and under-exercised, was then subjected to maximum restraint — martingale, check-rein, sharp curb, and bottom-bar reins. The combination of pent-up energy and mouth pain drove the horse into a frenzy.

**The Bolt**

When harnessed and taken out, Hotspur — full of spring, mouth in agony from the sharp bit — ran away. He smashed into another carriage, flung his lordship to the ground, and in the chaos cut and blemished himself so badly that he was no longer considered fit for a gentleman's stables. (047-014)

The irony was stark: a horse ruined not by any fault of his own, but by the very restraints meant to control him. The coachman's solution to a spirited horse — more restraint, tighter rein, sharper bit — had created the catastrophe it was supposed to prevent.

**Jerry's Redemption**

Jerry, who had said "I can do with high spirits if a horse is not vicious or hard-mouthed," went to see Hotspur. The next day the horse came home. Beauty describes him:

> He was a fine brown horse, without a white hair in him, as tall as Captain, with a very handsome head, and only five years old. (047-018)

The first night Hotspur was restless — jerking his halter rope, knocking the block about. But after five or six hours in the cab the next day, he came in quiet and sensible. Jerry patted and talked to him, and very soon they understood each other.

> Jerry said that with an easy bit and plenty of work he would be as gentle as a lamb; and that it was an ill wind that blew nobody good, for if his lordship had lost a hundred-guinea favorite, the cabman had gained a good horse with all his strength in him. (047-018)

## 后果与影响

Hotspur settled in well. Though he initially thought it a great come-down to be a cab-horse and was disgusted at standing in the rank, he soon confessed to Beauty:

> An easy mouth and a free head made up for a great deal, and after all, the work was not so degrading as having one's head and tail fastened to each other at the saddle. (047-019)

Hotspur's story became a living proof of Jerry's philosophy: that high spirits in a horse are not a vice to be suppressed with mechanical force, but a quality to be managed with understanding and a gentle hand. The horse the nobleman's coachman had ruined was, under Jerry's care, as gentle as a lamb.

When Jerry later fell gravely ill and had to give up cab work, Hotspur was bought by a kind gentleman who had employed Jerry's son Harry — ensuring the horse who had been ruined by one set of humans was saved by another. (048-043)

## 主题意义

Hotspur's story is one of the novel's most pointed critiques of fashionable horse management among the wealthy. The coachman's "governor" represents the institutional cruelty of the aristocratic stable: a system that, faced with a spirited horse, reaches for more restraint rather than more understanding. The cascade — clip the horse, deny him exercise, then strap him down with every mechanical device available — is presented not as individual cruelty but as systemic folly.

The contrast between the nobleman's approach (maximum restraint → catastrophe) and Jerry's approach (easy bit, plenty of work, patience → gentle as a lamb) encapsulates Sewell's central argument: that kindness and understanding produce better results than force and fashion. Jerry's aphorism — "an ill wind that blew nobody good" — reframes the accident as providential: it removed Hotspur from a system that was destroying him and delivered him to one that would restore him.
