# The Election

A parliamentary election day in London when cab horses were in high demand and many were driven to exhaustion — but [[Jerry Barker]] refused to hire out [[Black Beauty]] beyond his strength, even when offered premium fares.

## 概述

During a London election, the demand for cabs surged as voters needed transport to the polls. Other cab drivers pushed their horses mercilessly to maximize earnings, but Jerry Barker — true to his principles — limited Beauty's work to a reasonable number of journeys. He refused to take fares that would overtax his horse. (042-003)

The episode contrasts Jerry's ethical treatment of his horse with the exploitation practiced by his competitors. It is one of the novel's most direct illustrations of how economic pressure tests moral character.

## 背景

Jerry Barker was a London cab driver who owned Beauty during his cab horse years. He was known for his integrity, his care for his horses, and his refusal to work on Sundays — a commitment that cost him income but earned him respect. The election created a temporary windfall for cab drivers, and many succumbed to the temptation to overwork their horses.

## 后果与影响

Beauty was spared the worst of the election-day overwork that damaged or exhausted many other cab horses. Jerry's refusal to prioritize profit over his horse's welfare reinforced his character as one of the novel's most admirable human figures. The episode also exposed the systemic exploitation of working horses during public events.

## 主题意义

The election episode is a study in ethical consistency under economic pressure. Jerry's refusal to exploit the windfall — even when "every other cab was hired" — demonstrates that good treatment of animals is not a luxury reserved for wealthy estate owners. It is a moral choice available to working men as well.

Elections in Victorian England were notoriously rowdy affairs involving bribery, intimidation, and heavy drinking. The surge in cab demand came from party agents transporting voters to the polls — sometimes the same voters making the journey multiple times. This context makes Jerry's restraint all the more striking: he stood against not just individual greed but an entire system of exploitation.

## 原文引用

Jerry refuses to hire his cab for the election:

> "Well, Polly, you may say that my cab will be otherwise engaged. I should not like to have it pasted over with their great bills, and as to making Jack and Captain race about to the public-houses to bring up half-drunken voters, why, I think 'twould be an insult to the horses. No, I shan't do it."
>
> (045-002)

Jerry explains the true meaning of liberty to his son Harry:

> "My boy, Liberty does not come from colors, they only show party, and all the liberty you can get out of them is, liberty to get drunk at other people's expense, liberty to ride to the poll in a dirty old cab, liberty to abuse any one that does not wear your color, and to shout yourself hoarse at what you only half-understand—that's your liberty!"
>
> (045-015)
