## Role in the Story

Mr. Ryder was an experienced horse-breaker described by [[ginger|Ginger]] as "the old master" — a man who "could soon have brought me round, and could have done anything with me." By the time Ginger arrived at his stable, he had given up most of the hard work to his son [[samson|Samson]] and another man, coming only at times to oversee. (008-005)

Unlike his harsh and brutal son, Mr. Ryder was gentle and patient. He was described as "a very fine old gentleman with quite white hair," whose voice was "full, and clear, and kind."

When he returned one evening and found Ginger bloodied and exhausted from Samson's rough treatment, he was deeply upset. He gently led her to her stall, personally removed her saddle, and spent time sponging her wounds with warm water, speaking tenderly. (008-007)

Mr. Ryder's philosophy of horse-breaking was founded on kindness rather than force. He famously told Samson: "A bad-tempered man will never make a good-tempered horse. You've not learned your trade yet, Samson." He also observed that if a high-mettled creature "can't be broken by fair means, she will never be good for anything." (008-007)

> He was a very fine old gentleman with quite white hair, but his voice was what I should know him by among a thousand. It was not high, nor yet low, but full, and clear, and kind, and when he gave orders it was so steady and decided that every one knew, both horses and men, that he expected to be obeyed. He came quietly along, now and then shaking the oats about that he had in the sieve, and speaking cheerfully and gently to me: "Come along, lassie, come along, lassie; come along, come along."
>
> (008-007)

## The Voice of Wisdom

Mr. Ryder speaks only a handful of lines in the novel, but each carries the weight of a lifetime's experience with horses. His rebuke to Samson is arguably the novel's thesis statement on horse-breaking: the handler's temperament shapes the horse's temperament.

The physical contrast between father and son is telling — the old man with white hair, the young man tall and bold; the father gentle with warm water and a sponge, the son brutal with a severe bit and a whip. Sewell does not present Mr. Ryder's gentleness as weakness but as the hard-won wisdom of a man who has spent his life among horses and learned what actually works.
