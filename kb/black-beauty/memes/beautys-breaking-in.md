# Beauty's Breaking In

Black Beauty's formal training at Farmer Grey's hands — from his first bit and bridle through saddle, iron shoes, harness, and railway desensitization — stands as the novel's model of humane horse breaking. (004-002)

## Overview

When Black Beauty turned four, Farmer Grey personally undertook his breaking in — the process of teaching a young horse to accept saddle, bridle, harness, and human commands. Rather than sending Beauty to a professional trainer who might use force, Farmer Grey performed the training himself with patience, oats, and kind words, ensuring the young horse was never frightened or hurt. (004-002) The training progressed methodically: bit and bridle first, then saddle and rider, then iron shoes at the smith's forge, and finally full harness with collar, crupper, and blinkers. (004-003) As a final and unusual step, Beauty was sent to a neighboring farm beside a railway line for two weeks to become accustomed to trains — a precaution that left him "as fearless at railway stations as in my own stable." (004-012)

## Background

Before training began, Beauty had spent his first four years at Farmer Grey's meadow with his mother Duchess and the other colts. Farmer Grey believed that "lads ought not to work like men, and colts ought not to work like horses till they were quite grown up." (004-001) When Squire Gordon came to inspect the four-year-old Beauty — examining his eyes, mouth, and legs, and watching him walk, trot, and gallop — he pronounced: "When he has been well broken in he will do very well." (004-002) Farmer Grey declared he would break Beauty in himself, "as he should not like me to be frightened or hurt," and began the very next day. (004-002)

## The Training Process

### Bit and Bridle

The first step was the bit — "a great piece of cold hard steel as thick as a man's finger to be pushed into one's mouth, between one's teeth, and over one's tongue." (004-005) Held fast by straps over the head, under the throat, round the nose, and under the chin, it was "very bad! yes, very bad!" But Beauty knew his mother and all grown horses wore one; combined with oats, pats, and gentle words, he learned to accept it. (004-005)

### Saddle and Rider

The saddle was easier. Farmer Grey placed it gently on Beauty's back while old Daniel held his head, fastening the girths with constant encouragement. (004-006) After days of practice, Beauty began to look forward to the saddle for the oats that accompanied it. One morning his master mounted and rode him round the meadow on soft grass; though it felt "queer," Beauty "felt rather proud to carry my master." (004-006)

### Iron Shoes

Farmer Grey accompanied Beauty to the smith's forge personally. The blacksmith cut away portions of each hoof — which did not hurt — then fitted and nailed on iron shoes. Beauty's feet felt "very stiff and heavy" at first, but he grew accustomed in time. (004-007)

### Harness, Blinkers, and Crupper

The final equipment was the most unpleasant: a "stiff heavy collar," a bridle with blinkers that blocked side vision, and a crupper — "a nasty stiff strap that went right under my tail." (004-008) Beauty hated the crupper especially: "to have my long tail doubled up and poked through that strap was almost as bad as the bit. I never felt more like kicking, but of course I could not kick such a good master." (004-008)

### Railway Desensitization

In what Beauty considered "a very great advantage," Farmer Grey sent him for a fortnight to a meadow beside a railway line. (004-009) The first train terrified him — "with a rush and a clatter, and a puffing out of smoke — a long black train of something flew by." (004-010) But observing the cows and sheep eating quietly, Beauty realized the train never entered the field or harmed him, and soon "cared as little about the passing of a train as the cows and sheep did." (004-011)

## Consequences and Impact

> "Since then I have seen many horses much alarmed and restive at the sight or sound of a steam engine; but thanks to my good master's care, I am as fearless at railway stations as in my own stable." (004-012)

The training established the novel's gold standard for humane horse handling. Ginger later tells Beauty that her own breaking in was harsh and forceful, leaving her resentful of human control (008-004) — a direct contrast illustrating that a horse's character is shaped by its treatment. Duchess reinforced the lesson with a parting warning: "there are a great many kinds of men; there are good thoughtful men like our master, that any horse may be proud to serve; and there are bad, cruel men, who never ought to have a horse or dog to call their own." (004-014)

## Thematic Significance

The breaking-in episode establishes the novel's core argument through concrete demonstration: humane treatment produces willing, reliable horses. The systematic description of each piece of tack — bit, saddle, shoes, harness, blinkers, crupper — educates the Victorian reader about what a working horse endures, building empathy through knowledge rather than abstract preaching. Farmer Grey's railway desensitization shows that thoughtful prevention is more effective than harsh correction — a principle that echoes through the novel's many episodes of cruelty and neglect.
