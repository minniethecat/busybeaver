# The Devil's Trade Mark

Riding home with [[John Manly]], [[Black Beauty]] witnesses a young farmer's son — Bill Bushby — brutally whipping a small pony for refusing to leap a gate too high for it. John's response, and [[James Howard]]'s later recollection of a schoolroom lesson about cruelty being "the devil's own trade-mark," make this one of the novel's most explicit moral teachings. (013-001)

## 概述

While returning from an errand on a long straight road, John and Beauty see a boy trying to force a pony over a gate. The pony refuses, and the boy escalates: whipping, kicking, knocking the pony about the head. When the pony finally throws the boy into a thorn hedge and runs home, John pointedly refuses to help him out. The incident, reported to Farmer Bushby, leads James Howard to recall a schoolroom lesson: that cruelty is the devil's trade-mark, and kindness to man and beast is God's mark. (013-002)

## 背景

Beauty had been at [[Birtwick Park]] long enough to know its values. Squire Gordon and his household believed that kindness was not just a personal virtue but a moral obligation. John Manly, the coachman, was the daily embodiment of this philosophy — firm but never harsh, knowledgeable but never arrogant. He had no patience for cruelty, and he believed that those who were cruel to animals would inevitably be cruel to people too. (013-001)

The incident with Bill Bushby was not an isolated case. As the farmer would later admit, "this is not the first time, nor the second, that he has ill-used that pony." The boy had a pattern of cruelty that his parents had allowed to continue unchecked. (013-007)

## 事件经过

**The Confrontation**

John and Beauty were returning gently on a long straight road when they saw a boy at some distance, trying to leap a pony over a gate. "The pony would not take the leap, and the boy cut him with the whip, but he only turned off on one side. He whipped him again, but the pony turned off on the other side." Then the boy got off and "gave him a hard thrashing, and knocked him about the head." (013-002)

When they drew near, the pony — a good little creature who had shown patience far beyond what was reasonable — "put down his head and threw up his heels, and sent the boy neatly over into a broad quickset hedge, and with the rein dangling from his head he set off home at a full gallop." John laughed out loud: "Served him right." (013-002)

The boy, struggling among the thorns, called for help. John's response was withering:

> "Thank ye, I think you are quite in the right place, and maybe a little scratching will teach you not to leap a pony over a gate that is too high for him." (013-003)

John rode on, suspecting the boy might lie about what happened. He decided to go home by way of Farmer Bushby's — "and then if anybody wants to know you and I can tell 'em, ye see." (013-004)

**The Report to Farmer Bushby**

At the farm, they found the farmer rushing out into the road, his wife at the gate looking frightened — the pony had come home riderless. John told the farmer exactly what he had seen: (013-005)

> "Well, sir, I saw your son whipping, and kicking, and knocking that good little pony about shamefully because he would not leap a gate that was too high for him. The pony behaved well, sir, and showed no vice; but at last he just threw up his heels and tipped the young gentleman into the thorn hedge. He wanted me to help him out, but I hope you will excuse me, sir, I did not feel inclined to do so. There's no bones broken, sir; he'll only get a few scratches. I love horses, and it riles me to see them badly used; it is a bad plan to aggravate an animal till he uses his heels; the first time is not always the last." (013-006)

The mother began to cry, wanting to run to her son, but the farmer stopped her: "Bill wants a lesson about this, and I must see that he gets it; this is not the first time, nor the second, that he has ill-used that pony, and I shall stop it. I am much obliged to you, Manly." (013-007)

**James Howard's Recollection**

Back at the stable, John told James Howard what had happened. James laughed — he knew Bill Bushby from school. Bill "took great airs on himself because he was a farmer's son; he used to swagger about and bully the little boys." (013-009)

Then James shared a memory that gave the chapter its title. One day at school, he had found Bill catching flies and pulling off their wings. James gave him "a box on the ears that laid him sprawling on the floor." When the master investigated, James showed him the flies — "some crushed and some crawling about helpless" — and the wings on the window sill. (013-010)

The master's response became the novel's moral centerpiece:

> "He said that cruelty was the devil's own trade-mark, and if we saw any one who took pleasure in cruelty we might know who he belonged to, for the devil was a murderer from the beginning, and a tormentor to the end. On the other hand, where we saw people who loved their neighbors, and were kind to man and beast, we might know that was God's mark." (013-010)

## 后果与影响

Bill Bushby's story is the first explicit illustration of the novel's thesis that cruelty to animals is not a minor vice but a mark of profound moral corruption. The master's lesson — that cruelty is "the devil's own trade-mark" — is the theological formulation of what Sewell has been showing through narrative: that how a person treats animals reveals their essential character.

The incident also demonstrates John Manly's approach to moral education. He does not punish Bill himself (that is the farmer's responsibility) but he ensures the truth is told and that natural consequences — a few scratches, parental discipline — follow. His refusal to help Bill out of the hedge is not cruelty but pedagogical restraint: some lessons must be learned through discomfort.

Farmer Bushby's response is equally important. He does not defend his son or make excuses. He thanks John for the report and commits to stopping the behavior. The novel presents good parenting — like good horsemanship — as a matter of firm, consistent correction applied early, before habits harden.

## 主题意义

The Devil's Trade Mark chapter is the novel's most concentrated statement of its moral philosophy. Sewell, a devout Quaker, saw cruelty not as a failure of manners but as a sign of spiritual corruption. The master's lesson — that those who take pleasure in hurting the weak belong to the devil — is uncompromising, and it is meant to be.

But the chapter is subtle as well as forceful. Bill Bushby is not a monster; he is a boy who has been allowed to develop cruel habits because no one stopped him earlier. "This is not the first time, nor the second," his father says — acknowledging parental failure as much as filial fault. The novel's reformist optimism lies in this acknowledgment: cruelty is learned, and therefore it can be unlearned, if adults take responsibility for teaching kindness early and consistently.

The chapter also links cruelty to animals with bullying of other children. Bill pulled wings off flies and bullied smaller boys — the two behaviors came from the same source. Sewell's integrated moral vision treats kindness as indivisible: you cannot be kind to people while being cruel to animals, or vice versa.
