# Victorian Horse Economy

The Victorian horse economy was the vast commercial infrastructure that depended on horses for transport, agriculture, industry, and leisure. In *Black Beauty*, the narrator's journey through this economy — from country estate to London cab stand — reveals a system that treated horses as both indispensable and disposable.

## 定义

By the 1870s, Britain's economy ran on horse power. London alone had an estimated 300,000 horses, employed in everything from elegant private carriages to grinding cab work, from heavy cartage to omnibus service. The horse industry encompassed breeding farms, dealers, auctioneers, feed merchants, harness makers, farriers, veterinary surgeons, stable builders, and the vast workforce of grooms, coachmen, and drivers.

## 在书中的体现

Black Beauty's life traces the full arc of the horse economy. Born on a breeding farm, he is broken by a skilled trainer, sold to a country gentleman, resold to a fashionable estate, passed through a livery stable, auctioned at a horse fair, purchased for cab work, and finally rescued by a kind farmer. Each transaction reveals a different sector of the horse economy and a different set of incentives — some aligned with welfare, most not.

Ginger describes a parallel journey through the same system: "I was taken from my mother as soon as I was weaned" (008-004), broken by a dealer, sold to a fashionable owner who valued appearance over welfare (009-005), and eventually worked to death.

### 关键场景

**The horse fair.** Beauty's sale at the horse fair is the most explicit depiction of the horse market — a rough, commercial environment where horses are examined like merchandise. A salesman appraises Beauty: "if you want him for cab work he's a bargain" (035-009), and "the money was paid on the spot" (035-010).

**The cab stand economy.** Jerry Barker's work at the London cab stand (036-001 through 036-012) reveals the daily economics of urban horse labor: the pressure to maximize fares, the cost of feed and stabling, and the constant temptation to overwork horses for profit.

## 历史背景

The horse economy reached its peak in the 1880s–1890s, just as Sewell's novel was gaining influence. Within a generation, the automobile would begin its displacement of the horse, but at the time of *Black Beauty*, the horse was as essential to the economy as the combustion engine is today. The sheer scale of horse use made welfare reform both urgent and difficult, as economic interests resisted regulation.
