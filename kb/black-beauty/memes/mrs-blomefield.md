## 角色概述

Mrs. Blomefield is the wife of [[Mr. Blomefield]], the vicar of the parish near [[Birtwick Park]]. She appears briefly in the narrative as a member of the local gentry connected to Squire Gordon's household. Her role, though small, helps establish the social world of Birtwick Park and its network of neighboring families.

## 故事经历

Mrs. Blomefield is first mentioned when Squire Gordon speaks to her about the horses at Birtwick, reassuring her about their temperament:

> (010-010)"My dear madam, you need not be alarmed. I always drive these horses myself, and they are the safest in the world."

This exchange, overheard by Black Beauty, suggests that Mrs. Blomefield had expressed concern about the spirited horses — a reasonable worry in an era when carriage accidents were common.

Later, when Merrylegs is being rehomed after the Gordon family's departure from Birtwick, Mrs. Blomefield is the recipient:

> (022-003)The vicar, who was wanting a pony for Mrs. Blomefield, received Merrylegs on the condition that the pony should never be sold.

This condition — that Merrylegs must never be sold — echoes Squire Gordon's protective attitude toward his animals and suggests that Mrs. Blomefield's household was trusted to provide a good permanent home.

Mrs. Blomefield is also connected to the novel's ending. Her daughters, [[Miss Blomefield]] and Miss Lavinia, are among the women who give the aged Black Beauty his final, peaceful home.

## 与其他角色的关系

- **[[Mr. Blomefield]]** — Her husband, the vicar. The Blomefields are a respected local family connected to Birtwick Park.
- **[[Squire Gordon]]** — Their neighbor and social equal at Birtwick Park. He speaks to her with the courteous address "my dear madam."
- **[[Merrylegs]]** — The pony given to her for riding, under the condition of permanent care.
- **[[Miss Blomefield]]** and **Miss Lavinia** — Her daughters (or possibly sisters-in-law), who feature in the novel's conclusion.
- **[[Black Beauty]]** — She is part of the network that ultimately gives Beauty his final home.

## 在故事中的角色

Mrs. Blomefield is a representative of the decent, humane gentry class that Sewell portrays positively throughout *Black Beauty*. Unlike the fashionable wealthy who treat horses as disposable status symbols, the Blomefield family is associated with long-term care and the novel's happiest ending — Beauty's peaceful retirement.
