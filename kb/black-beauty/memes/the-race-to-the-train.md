A young man carrying a heavy portmanteau slips on a piece of orange peel, and [[Jerry Barker]] — with [[Black Beauty]] at his best — races through the tangled London traffic to get him to the South-Eastern Railway in time for the twelve o'clock Dover train, then quietly refuses the extra half-crown reward. (038-011)

## 概述

One morning on the cab stand, a young man carrying a heavy portmanteau stepped on a piece of orange peel on the pavement and fell hard. He was stunned and in visible pain, but his urgency was absolute: he had to catch the twelve o'clock train at the South-Eastern Railway. Jerry, who normally refused to "put on the steam" for careless passengers offering extra shillings, made an exception — not for the money, but because he understood the young man's genuine need. What followed was a masterful display of teamwork between driver and horse through the most congested streets in London, ending at the station with eight minutes to spare. When the grateful passenger offered an extra half-crown, Jerry refused it: "So glad we hit the time, sir." (038-011) (038-012) (038-013) (038-014) (038-022) (038-024)

## 背景

Jerry Barker was known on the cab stand for his principled refusal to drive hard merely to please passengers who offered extra money. Earlier that same period, two wild-looking young men had demanded he "put on the steam" to get them to the Victoria station, offering a shilling extra. Jerry had refused: "Shillings don't pay for putting on the steam like that." Larry, a less scrupulous cabman, had snatched the fare, slashing his jaded horse and dashing off — while Jerry patted Beauty's neck and said, "A shilling would not pay for that sort of thing, would it, old boy?" (038-007) (038-008) (038-009)

This established a clear principle: Jerry would not abuse his horse for money. But the orange-peel incident tested that principle in a new way. Here was a passenger in genuine distress, not a pair of careless thrill-seekers. Jerry's willingness to drive fast "if only he knew why" proved to be the key distinction. (038-010)

## 事件经过

### The Accident

One morning on the stand, a young man carrying a heavy portmanteau trod on a piece of orange peel lying on the pavement and "fell down with great force." Jerry was the first to run and lift him up. The young man seemed badly stunned and walked as if in great pain as he was helped into a nearby shop. (038-011) (038-012)

Ten minutes later, a shopman summoned Jerry. The young man, "dreadfully white and ill," explained his predicament: the fall had made him late, and it was "of great importance that I should not lose the twelve o'clock train." He would gladly pay an extra fare. (038-013)

Jerry's response was immediate: "I'll do my very best, if you think you are well enough, sir." (038-014)

### The London Gauntlet

The next minute Jerry was on the box, and with "a cheery chirrup to me, and a twitch of the rein that I well understood," they were off. "Now then, Jack, my boy, spin along, we'll show them how we can get over the ground, if we only know why." (038-016) (038-017)

Driving fast through the City of London at midday required extraordinary skill. Beauty describes the chaos: carriages, omnibuses, carts, vans, trucks, cabs, and great wagons "creeping along at a walking pace; some going one way, some another, some going slowly, others wanting to pass them; omnibuses stopping short every few minutes... perhaps you try to pass, but just then something else comes dashing in through the narrow opening." A driver had to be "ready for any chance — to dash forward if there be an opening, and be quick as a rat-dog to see if there be room." (038-018)

Jerry and Beauty were a seasoned team: "no one could beat us at getting through when we were set upon it. I was quick and bold and could always trust my driver; Jerry was quick and patient at the same time, and could trust his horse." Jerry almost never used the whip — Beauty responded to his voice, his "click, click," and the subtle guidance of the rein. (038-019)

### The Block-Up

At the bottom of Cheapside they hit a block. For three or four minutes they stood still. The young man, growing desperate, put his head out: "I think I had better get out and walk; I shall never get there if this goes on." (038-020)

Jerry replied with steady reassurance: "I'll do all that can be done, sir; I think we shall be in time. This block-up cannot last much longer, and your luggage is very heavy for you to carry, sir." (038-021)

### The Final Dash

The cart ahead began to move, and "then we had a good turn. In and out, in and out we went, as fast as horseflesh could do it." For a wonder, London Bridge was clear — "a whole train of cabs and carriages all going our way at a quick trot, perhaps wanting to catch that very train." They "whirled into the station with many more, just as the great clock pointed to eight minutes to twelve o'clock." (038-022)

### The Refusal

The young man, overcome with relief and gratitude, said: "Thank God! we are in time, and thank you, too, my friend, and your good horse. You have saved me more than money can ever pay for. Take this extra half-crown." (038-023)

Jerry's refusal was gentle but absolute: "No, sir, no, thank you all the same; so glad we hit the time, sir; but don't stay now, sir, the bell is ringing. Here, porter! take this gentleman's luggage — Dover line twelve o'clock train — that's it." Without waiting for another word, Jerry wheeled Beauty around to make way for other cabs and drew aside. (038-024)

## 后果与影响

Back at the cab stand, Jerry faced teasing from the other drivers for "driving hard to the train for an extra fare, as they said, all against his principles." They wanted to know how much he had pocketed. Jerry's reply was characteristically principled: "The gentleman offered me half a crown extra, but I didn't take it; 'twas quite pay enough for me to see how glad he was to catch that train; and if Jack and I choose to have a quick run now and then to please ourselves, that's our business and not yours." (038-027) (038-031)

This distinction — between driving hard for hire and driving hard for a good reason, freely chosen — was too subtle for Larry, who retorted that Jerry would "never be a rich man." Jerry's reply carried the novel's moral: "I have heard the commandments read a great many times and I never noticed that any of them said, 'Thou shalt be rich'; and there are a good many curious things said in the New Testament about rich men that I think would make me feel rather queer if I was one of them." (038-033) (038-034)

[[Governor Grant|Governor Gray]] had the last word: "If you ever do get rich, Jerry, you'll deserve it, and you won't find a curse come with your wealth. As for you, Larry, you'll die poor; you spend too much in whipcord." (038-034)

## 主题意义

The race to the train is the novel's most vivid demonstration of Jerry's ethical framework in action. His refusal to drive hard "to please careless people" is not stubbornness but discernment: when a passenger's need is real, he will do everything in his power to help — and he will do it for free. The extra half-crown, which any other cabman would have pocketed without a thought, represents for Jerry a corruption of the act. He drove fast not for money but because it was the right thing to do.

The scene also celebrates the partnership between a good driver and a good horse. Jerry's ability to navigate London traffic without using the whip — guided by voice, click, and rein — demonstrates a level of communication and mutual trust that the novel presents as the ideal human-animal relationship. Beauty's pride in their performance is palpable: "no one could beat us at getting through when we were set upon it."

The coda on the cab stand, with its mix of mockery and respect from fellow drivers, places Jerry in the tradition of the principled outsider — a man who lives by a code his peers find quaint but cannot quite dismiss. Governor Gray's parting observation seals the theme: wealth gained without cruelty "won't find a curse come with it," while Larry's whipcord spending presages his own destruction.
