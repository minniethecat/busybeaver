## Role in the Story

Robert was a groom at [[earlshall-park|Earlshall Park]] who appears during the aftermath of Reuben Smith's fatal riding accident. After Smith was thrown from Black Beauty and killed on the stony road, Robert arrived with another man to recover the body.

He attempted to lead Black Beauty forward, but the horse's injured hoof made it nearly impossible to walk. (028-006) Robert and his companion Ned discussed what to do, and it was agreed that "Robert, as the groom, should lead me" while Ned transported the body. (028-008)

Robert then examined Black Beauty's injured foot, bound it with his handkerchief, and slowly led the limping horse home through a painful three-mile night walk. During the journey, Robert showed genuine compassion — he "often patted and encouraged me, talking to me in a pleasant voice." (028-009)

About a week later, Robert came into the field with a halter, marking Black Beauty's return to work after the injury. (029-009)

## A Groom's Quiet Compassion

Robert occupies a small but significant space in the narrative. Unlike the named grooms whose actions directly cause suffering — [[reuben-smith|Reuben Smith]]'s drunkenness, [[samson|Samson]]'s brutality — Robert appears solely as a helper in a moment of crisis.

His tenderness with the injured Beauty stands out: binding the wounded foot with his own handkerchief, speaking in a pleasant voice, offering encouragement through a long, painful walk in darkness. (028-009) Anna Sewell grants Robert no backstory or further appearances, but in this single scene he embodies the quiet competence and kindness that the novel consistently elevates.
