# The Fire Investigation

The morning after the hotel stable fire that nearly killed [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]], [[James Howard]] investigated the cause—tracing the blaze to a careless pipe left by [[Dick Towler]] in the hay loft, in direct violation of [[John Manly]]'s standing rule. (017-021)

## 概述

After the dramatic rescue of the horses from the burning stable, James Howard spent the morning gathering information about how the fire started. The investigation revealed that Dick Towler had gone into the hay loft with a lit pipe, despite being told to put it down, and that the fire had originated from the trapdoor area where he had been working. The tragedy claimed the lives of two horses that could not be rescued and validated John Manly's strict rule against smoking in stables. (017-021)

## 背景

The previous night, a traveler's horse had been brought into the hotel stable. While the second hostler was cleaning the horse, a young man named Dick Towler entered the stable with a pipe in his mouth to gossip. The hostler asked Towler to climb into the loft and put hay down into the rack, explicitly instructing him to put down his pipe first. (017-001)

Later that night, the stable filled with smoke. The horses panicked, and the situation escalated quickly as flames broke through the trapdoor. James Howard, staying at the hotel with [[Squire Gordon]]'s party, rushed into the burning stable and led Beauty and Ginger to safety. The fire engine arrived but could not save the structure. Two horses from another stable perished in the flames. (017-004)

## 事件经过

**The Morning Inquiry**

The next morning, Squire Gordon came to check on the horses and speak with James. Because their mistress had been alarmed by the fire, the party's journey was postponed until the afternoon, giving James the morning to investigate. (017-021)

James went first to the inn to check on the harness and carriage, then gathered accounts of the fire from those present. The investigation pieced together the sequence of events:

> At first no one could guess how the fire had been caused, but at last a man said he saw Dick Towler go into the stable with a pipe in his mouth, and when he came out he had not one, and went to the tap for another. (017-021)

**The Key Testimony**

The under hostler provided the critical evidence. He had asked Towler to go up the ladder to put hay into a rack, but had specifically warned him to put down his pipe first. (017-021) Towler denied taking the pipe with him up the ladder, but the circumstantial evidence was overwhelming: he had been seen entering the stable with a pipe, leaving without one, and immediately obtaining another.

**The Aftermath**

The damage was total:

> James said the roof and floor had all fallen in, and that only the black walls were standing; the two poor horses that could not be got out were buried under the burnt rafters and tiles. (017-022)

Beauty, reflecting on the investigation, connected it to John Manly's firm rule:

> I remember our John Manly's rule, never to allow a pipe in the stable, and thought it ought to be the rule everywhere. (017-021)

## 后果与影响

The fire investigation had several lasting effects:

1. **Vindication of John Manly's rule**: The strict no-smoking policy at [[Birtwick Park]] stables, which Manly enforced without exception, was proven tragically correct. A single moment of carelessness in a wooden stable full of dry hay had catastrophic consequences.

2. **Two horses lost**: Despite James Howard's heroism in saving Beauty and Ginger, two other horses—those that "could not be got out"—died in the fire. (017-022)

3. **James Howard's reputation**: The fire and its aftermath consolidated James's standing as a capable and brave young man. Squire Gordon's pride in him was evident, and the hostler's remarks confirmed the wider community's admiration. (017-016)

4. **A lasting lesson**: Beauty's reflection that Manly's rule "ought to be the rule everywhere" frames the fire as a preventable tragedy—the result not of bad luck but of systemic carelessness that good stable management could and should prevent. (017-021)

## 主题意义

The fire investigation serves as a structural counterpoint to the dramatic rescue scene that precedes it. Where the rescue is all action and chaos, the investigation is methodical and retrospective—a search for accountability. The novel's narrative structure thus models the process of moving from disaster to understanding, from emotional response to systemic analysis.

Dick Towler's denial ("Dick denied taking the pipe with him, but no one believed him") introduces a minor but significant moral thread: the refusal to accept responsibility. Towler's lie stands in contrast to the honesty and integrity that characters like John Manly, James Howard, and Jerry Barker embody throughout the novel.

Most importantly, the investigation transforms a singular tragedy into a general principle. Beauty's thought—that Manly's rule "ought to be the rule everywhere"—extends the lesson beyond the burned-out stable to all places where horses are kept. It is one of the novel's clearest arguments for standardized safety practices, anticipating modern animal welfare regulations.
