The plowboy Dick, who habitually threw stones at the young colts for his own amusement, is caught in the act by [[Farmer Grey]] and summarily dismissed — an early lesson in the novel's uncompromising stand against cruelty to animals. (002-009)

## 概述

During [[Black Beauty]]'s colthood on Farmer Grey's farm, a plowboy named Dick would enter the colts' field ostensibly to pick blackberries. Once he had eaten his fill, he would amuse himself by throwing stones and sticks at the young horses to make them gallop. Though the colts could usually outrun him, the stones sometimes struck and injured them. One day Farmer Grey, working unseen in the adjacent field, witnessed the abuse. He vaulted over the hedge, seized Dick by the arm, boxed his ear, and dismissed him on the spot with the words: "This is not the first time, nor the second, but it shall be the last." (002-009) (002-010) (002-011)

## 背景

The event takes place during Beauty's early life at Farmer Grey's meadow, a setting defined by the gentle and principled management of both the farmer and his head horseman, Old Daniel. Beauty had already described his master as "a very good, kind man" who "would never sell me, or part with me" — a man who fed his horses well, spoke kindly to them, and expected the same standard of care from everyone on his farm. Dick's casual cruelty stood in direct violation of the farm's ethos.

Dick was a plowboy — a low-ranking farm laborer — and his visits to the field were ostensibly to pick blackberries from the hedge. His stone-throwing was not a single impulsive act but a pattern: "This is not the first time, nor the second," Farmer Grey observed, indicating repeated, deliberate cruelty over an extended period. (002-009) (002-011)

## 事件经过

On the day of his dismissal, Dick was at his usual game, throwing stones and sticks to make the colts gallop. He was unaware that Farmer Grey was working in the next field, watching the entire scene. The farmer "jumped in a snap" over the hedge, caught Dick by the arm, and gave him "such a box on the ear as made him roar with the pain and surprise." The colts, curious, trotted closer to watch. (002-010)

Farmer Grey's judgment was swift and final: "Bad boy! Bad boy! to chase the colts. This is not the first time, nor the second, but it shall be the last. There — take your money and go home; I shall not want you on my farm again." (002-011)

The farmer's actions combined three elements: physical punishment (the box on the ear), moral condemnation ("Bad boy!"), and economic consequence (dismissal with immediate payment of wages owed). The colts never saw Dick again. (002-011)

## 后果与影响

Dick's dismissal removed an immediate threat to the young horses' well-being, and Beauty records that afterward "we were well off," with Old Daniel proving "just as gentle as our master." The episode serves a narrative function beyond the immediate incident: it establishes Farmer Grey's character as a man of principle who will not tolerate cruelty on his farm, setting the moral baseline against which later owners — both good and bad — will be measured. (002-011)

The episode also foreshadows the novel's recurring pattern: those who mistreat animals face consequences, whether immediate (Dick's dismissal), deferred (Reuben Smith's fatal accident), or systemic (the fate of horses under cruel masters). It is the first of many such object lessons in the book.

## 主题意义

Dick's dismissal, though a brief episode occupying only three paragraphs in the novel, carries disproportionate thematic weight. It is the first instance of justice for animal cruelty in the book, establishing a moral framework that will govern the entire narrative: cruelty is always seen, always judged, and always punished — if not by human authority, then by the narrative's moral arc. Farmer Grey's "this shall be the last" echoes the novel's larger argument that cruelty must not be tolerated, excused, or ignored.

The episode also introduces the theme of economic justice: Dick is paid his wages ("take your money") even as he is dismissed, demonstrating that Farmer Grey's justice is not vindictive but principled. The swiftness of the punishment — no warning, no second chance — reflects the seriousness with which the novel treats animal abuse, treating it not as a minor infraction but as a disqualifying moral failure.
