# Jakes and the Lady

A well-dressed lady stops her carriage on a country road to remonstrate with a carter whose horse is suffering under the [[bearing rein]] — and by quiet reasoning, succeeds where a direct order might have failed.

## 概述

While [[Black Beauty]] was working as a job horse, he was hired by a carter named Jakes who drove with a tight bearing rein. On a steep hill, a lady in a passing carriage noticed the horse's distress. She stepped down and spoke to Jakes — not with anger, but with calm authority and practical reasoning. She explained how the bearing rein prevented the horse from using his full strength on the hill and offered to hold the reins herself to demonstrate that the horse would be perfectly manageable without the device (046-006). Convinced by her manner and her argument, Jakes removed the bearing rein — and the horse climbed the hill with ease.

## 背景

The bearing rein was widely used in Victorian England to force carriage horses to hold their heads high — a fashion that caused significant pain and restricted breathing, especially on hills. Beauty had suffered under the bearing rein at Earlshall (see [[A Strike for Liberty|a-strike-for-liberty]]) and knew its torments firsthand.

## 后果与影响

The episode is presented as a model of effective advocacy. The lady did not scold Jakes or invoke her social superiority — she reasoned with him, appealed to his practical sense, and treated him as a fellow human being capable of understanding. Jakes, for his part, was not villainous — merely ignorant, and willing to change when shown a better way.

For Beauty, the episode was a rare moment of vindication: a human being had noticed his suffering and acted to relieve it, not from duty or economic interest but from simple compassion.

## 主题意义

"Jakes and the Lady" is the novel's most practical lesson in how to advocate for animals. The lady's method — respectful, factual, collaborative rather than confrontational — reflects Anna Sewell's own approach to reform: change hearts through reason and empathy, not through coercion. The episode also shows that the bearing rein problem was not limited to cruel aristocrats; working men like Jakes used it too, often from ignorance rather than malice.
