# London Cab Stand

The London cab stand is the workplace of Jerry Barker and the setting for Black Beauty's later years as a cab horse. It serves as a microcosm of Victorian urban life, where drivers of varying character gather to wait for fares, and the pressures of the horse economy are most visible.

## 地点描述

The cab stand is located on a wide London street: "On one side of this wide street were high houses with smart shops" (036-006). Drivers wait with their cabs, talking, arguing, and observing the passing world. The stand is Jerry's workplace, where he debates Sunday observance with fellow drivers and where the contrast between his well-kept horses and others' exhausted animals is most apparent.

## 在故事中的角色

The cab stand is where the novel's moral debates take place. Jerry refuses Sunday work (039-003), debates religion and workers' rights with his fellow drivers, and intervenes when he sees cruelty. The stand represents the public sphere where principles are tested against economic pressure.

Beauty's first week at the stand is overwhelming: "I had never been used to London, and the noise, the hurry, the crowds of horses, carts, and carriages" (036-012).

> I should be glad to oblige the lady, sir, but I had a seven-days' license once, and the work was too hard for me, and too hard for my horses. Year in and year out, not a day's rest, and never a Sunday with my wife and children; and never able to go to a place of worship, which I had always been used to do before I took to the driving box. So for the last five years I have only taken a six-days' license, and I find it better all the way round.
>
> (039-006)
