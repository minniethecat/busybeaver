# The Horse Fair Sale

The horse fair sale is the transaction in which Black Beauty is purchased by Jerry Barker, redirecting his life from gentleman's horse to London cab horse. The scene is the novel's most detailed depiction of the commercial horse trade — a rough marketplace where a horse's fate hangs on the character of the next buyer.

## 事件经过

Beauty, now aged and diminished, stands at the horse fair among many other horses. Potential buyers examine him: some roughly, some with practiced eyes. A salesman pitches him to a quiet, kind-looking man: "if you want him for cab work he's a bargain" (035-009). The man — Jerry Barker — examines Beauty carefully but gently, and the transaction is completed: "The money was paid on the spot" (035-010).

Jerry leads Beauty to an inn, fits him with a saddle and bridle, and rides him home to meet his family. Beauty notes the man's light hands and kind voice — small signs that prove to be accurate indicators of his character.

## 影响与意义

The horse fair sale is a turning point in Beauty's life, marking the transition from private ownership to commercial labor. The randomness of the transaction — Beauty could as easily have been bought by a cruel master — underscores the novel's critique of a system in which horses have no control over their fates. Jerry's kindness is a stroke of luck, not a guarantee.

## 原文引用

Beauty meets the gray-eyed man who will become his new master:

> There was one man, I thought, if he would buy me, I should be happy. He was not a gentleman, nor yet one of the loud, flashy sort that call themselves so. He was rather a small man, but well made, and quick in all his motions. I knew in a moment by the way he handled me, that he was used to horses; he spoke gently, and his gray eye had a kindly, cheery look in it. It may seem strange to say—but it is true all the same—that the clean, fresh smell there was about him made me take to him; no smell of old beer and tobacco, which I hated, but a fresh smell as if he had come out of a hayloft.
>
> (035-005)

The bargain is struck:

> "Well, old chap," he said, "I think we should suit each other. I'll give twenty-four for him."
>
> "Say twenty-five and you shall have him."
>
> "Twenty-four ten," said my friend, in a very decided tone, "and not another sixpence—yes or no?"
>
> "Done," said the salesman; "and you may depend upon it there's a monstrous deal of quality in that horse, and if you want him for cab work he's a bargain."
>
