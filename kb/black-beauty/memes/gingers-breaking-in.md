Ginger tells [[Black Beauty]] the traumatic story of her breaking in under the brutal trainer Samson, the violent rebellion that left her bleeding and alone in a field, and her rescue by the gentle old [[Mr. Ryder]]. (008-001)

## 概述

While standing together in the shade, Ginger confides in Black Beauty about her past. Unlike Beauty's gentle upbringing under [[Farmer Grey]], Ginger was taken from her mother as a weanling, placed among indifferent colts, and subjected to a breaking-in process that relied entirely on force. The head trainer, Samson, sought to crush her spirit and reduce her to "a quiet, humble, obedient piece of horseflesh." After a brutal day of overwork, Ginger fought back — rearing, plunging, and kicking until she threw Samson off. She spent the rest of the day alone in the field, bleeding from spur wounds, until old Mr. Ryder found her at sunset and treated her with the kindness she had never known. (008-001) (008-004) (008-005) (008-006) (008-007)

## 背景

Ginger's breaking in occurred after a childhood devoid of affection. Taken from her mother immediately after weaning, she was placed with a group of young colts where "none of them cared for me, and I cared for none of them." The man responsible for them provided food and shelter but never a kind word. Boys passing through the field would fling stones to make the colts gallop, and though Ginger was never hit, a fine young colt was badly cut in the face. These experiences planted in Ginger's mind the conviction that "boys were our enemies" — a formative belief that would later generalize to humans in general. (008-004)

The training establishment was run by old Mr. Ryder, a man whose voice and manner could calm any horse. But he had handed over the "hard part of the trade" to his son Samson and another experienced man, retaining only a supervisory role. This abdication of the most delicate work to a man utterly unsuited for it set the stage for disaster. (008-005)

## 事件经过

### The Brutal Capture and Early Training

When breaking-in time came, several men cornered Ginger in the field. One caught her by the forelock, another held her nose so tightly she could hardly breathe, and a third wrenched her mouth open to force in the halter and bit. One man dragged her by the halter while another flogged from behind. "This was the first experience I had of men's kindness," Ginger recalled bitterly; "it was all force." She was shut in a stall day after day, pining for her lost liberty, with no trace of the coaxing and gentleness Beauty had received from Farmer Grey. (008-004)

### Samson's Regime

Samson was a strong, tall, bold man who boasted that no horse could throw him. Unlike his father, there was "no gentleness in him... only hardness, a hard voice, a hard eye, a hard hand." His objective, as Ginger felt from the first, was to "wear all the spirit out of me, and just make me into a quiet, humble, obedient piece of horseflesh." Ginger noted that Samson drank, and "the oftener he drank the worse it was for me." (008-005)


> "If I did not do exactly what he wanted he would get put out, and make me run round with that long rein in the training field till he had tired me out. I think he drank a good deal, and I am quite sure that the oftener he drank the worse it was for me. One day he had worked me hard in every way he could, and when I laid down I was tired, and miserable, and angry; it all seemed so hard."
>
> (008-006)

### The Breaking Point

One day Samson worked Ginger hard in every possible way. The next morning he came for her early and ran her round again for a long time. After scarcely an hour's rest, he returned with a saddle, bridle, and a painful new bit. Something Ginger did — she could never quite tell what — put him out of temper, and he "chucked me hard with the rein." The new bit caused intense pain, and Ginger reared up suddenly. Enraged, Samson began to flog her. (008-006)

Ginger's "whole spirit set against him." She kicked, plunged, and reared as never before. Samson clung to the saddle and punished her "cruelly with his whip and spurs," but her "blood was thoroughly up." After a terrible struggle, she threw him off backward. She heard him fall heavily on the turf and galloped to the far end of the field without looking back. (008-006)

### The Afternoon Alone

Ginger stood under an oak tree and watched. No one came to catch her. The hours passed; the sun grew hot, and flies swarmed around her bleeding flanks where Samson's spurs had dug in. Hungry — she had not eaten since early morning — she found the meadow too sparse to sustain a goose. The saddle remained strapped tightly on her back, denying her any comfort. She watched the other colts being led in for their evening feed. (008-006)

### Mr. Ryder's Rescue

Just as the sun went down, Ginger saw the old master emerge with a sieve of oats. His voice — "not high, nor yet low, but full, and clear, and kind" — was one she "should know him by among a thousand." He approached quietly, shaking the oats and speaking gently: "Come along, lassie, come along, lassie." Ginger stood still and let him come; his voice "took all my fear away." (008-007)

Seeing the clots of blood on her side, Mr. Ryder was visibly distressed. "Poor lassie! it was a bad business, a bad business." At the stable door stood Samson. Ginger laid her ears back and snapped at him. "Stand back," said the master, "and keep out of her way; you've done a bad day's work for this filly." When Samson muttered about a "vicious brute," the old man delivered a rebuke that encapsulates the novel's moral philosophy: "A bad-tempered man will never make a good-tempered horse. You've not learned your trade yet, Samson." (008-007)

Mr. Ryder personally removed Ginger's saddle and bridle, sponged her bruised sides with warm water, and, seeing that the corners of her mouth were so torn she could not eat hay, ordered a soft bran mash. His verdict: "If a high-mettled creature like this can't be broken by fair means, she will never be good for anything." (008-007)

## 后果与影响

After Ginger's mouth healed, a different trainer — Job, described as "steady and thoughtful" — took over her education, and she "soon learned what he wanted." Mr. Ryder continued to visit her regularly. The experience, however, left deep psychological scars. Ginger's later defensiveness — her biting, snapping, and general mistrust — can be traced directly to Samson's brutality. (008-008)

When Ginger later arrived at [[Birtwick Park]], the contrast between Samson's methods and [[John Manly]]'s philosophy of kindness was overwhelming. John's maxim — "kindness is all the physic she wants" — proved correct, but Ginger herself acknowledged that the damage ran deep: "I had made up my mind that men were my natural enemies and that I must defend myself." Her breaking-in story stands as the novel's most powerful argument against forceful training methods. (009-007) (009-009)

## 主题意义

Ginger's breaking-in story is the novel's most concentrated critique of forceful horse-training methods. The contrast between Samson's brutality and Mr. Ryder's kindness illustrates the book's central argument: that harsh treatment creates viciousness, while gentleness produces willing service. Mr. Ryder's aphorism — "a bad-tempered man will never make a good-tempered horse" — and his insistence on "fair means" encapsulate Anna Sewell's entire philosophy of humane treatment. The episode also establishes the formative trauma that explains Ginger's later behavior throughout the novel, making her eventual decline all the more tragic: she was not born vicious, but made so by human cruelty.
