# The Meadow

The Meadow is the large pleasant meadow at Farmer Grey's farm where [[Black Beauty]] is born and spends his earliest years with his mother [[Duchess]] and six other young colts. It is the novel's first setting and establishes the pastoral ideal of natural, unhurried equine life.

## Overview

The meadow is described in the novel's opening paragraph as "a large pleasant meadow with a pond of clear water in it" (002-001). Shaded by trees around the pond, it provides the colts with fresh grass, clean water, and the freedom to run and play. [[Duchess]] watches over her son with wise counsel, shaping Beauty's character before he enters the world of human ownership.

## Location

The meadow lies on the farm of [[Farmer Grey]], Beauty's first owner. It borders a plowed field on one side, a railway on another, and is separated from the railway by "pales" (a wooden fence). A plantation of fir trees stands nearby, where the colts take shelter from the sun and wind.

> "At the part where the high bank and the hedge are nearest the railway, there were some strong wooden pales." (004-009)

## In the Story

### Beauty's Early Life

The meadow is the nursery of Beauty's character. Here he learns his mother's key lessons: to be gentle, to do his best, and to remember that his grandfather "won the cup two years at the Newmarket races" (003-008).

The colts' play — "galloping all together round and round the field as hard as we could go" (002-004) — builds Beauty's physical strength and his social confidence.

### The Hunt Accident

The meadow is also the vantage point from which Beauty witnesses his first traumatic event: the hunt. When the hare is killed and a rider thrown to his death, Beauty watches from beyond the pales, and his mother's somber words mark the end of his pastoral innocence.

### Parting

When Beauty turns four, [[Farmer Grey]] begins to gentle-break him. The meadow is where [[Squire Gordon]] first sees Beauty ridden and decides to buy him (004-005). This marks the end of Beauty's life in the meadow and the beginning of his journey through the novel's chain of owners.

## Symbolic Significance

The meadow represents the natural state that Sewell argues every horse deserves: freedom, companionship, clean water, and maternal care. Beauty's memory of the meadow recurs throughout the novel, most poignantly when he is suffering as a cab horse in London and dreams of returning:

> "I dreamed I was back in the meadow." (029-001)

The transition from meadow to stable is the novel's structural arc in miniature — from freedom to servitude, from nature to civilization. Beauty's life is shaped by how closely each subsequent home approximates the meadow's conditions of kindness and space.
