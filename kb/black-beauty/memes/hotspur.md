# Hotspur

Hotspur is a gentleman's horse — "a fine big brown horse, high bred" (048-033) — who is temporarily left in [[Jerry Barker]]'s stable while his owner travels abroad. His name suggests spirit and fire, fitting his proud temperament.

## 角色概述

Hotspur arrives at Jerry's stable with aristocratic airs: "Hotspur thought it a great come-down to be brought among a set of cab horses" (047-019). He is accustomed to better treatment and initially looks down on his working-class stablemates. Despite this, [[Harry Barker]] tends him with the same care he gives all horses.

## 故事经历

### 来到 Jerry 的马厩

Hotspur's owner goes abroad, leaving his horse in Jerry's care. "The next day Hotspur, that was his name, came home" (047-018), Beauty records, noting the newcomer's proud bearing. Hotspur is clearly unused to the rough-and-tumble of cab-horse life.

### Harry 的照料

When Jerry falls ill, Harry takes over all stable duties. Hotspur benefits from Harry's diligent care: "Harry scrubbed away at Hotspur with a huish! huish! like any old ostler" (048-038). Despite his initial disdain, Hotspur comes to rely on the boy's attention.

### 寒冬困境

"One afternoon Hotspur was brought in very wet and dirty" (048-029), and Jerry — already unwell — "was also more afraid of Hotspur taking cold" (048-002). Harry and Dolly work together to clean him: "Dolly came in" while Harry was "sponging off the mud from Hotspur's body and legs" (048-033). This episode reveals both Hotspur's vulnerability and the Barker family's commitment to every horse in their charge.

### 结局

When a gentleman "said at once that he would take Hotspur" (048-043), the horse finds a new home — presumably more to his liking than cab work.

## 性格特征

- **Proud** — carries himself with the bearing of a gentleman's horse
- **High-bred** — accustomed to finer treatment than a cab stable provides
- **Adaptable** — ultimately accepts the care he is given, however humble

## 主题意义

Hotspur's brief tenure in Jerry's stable serves as a contrast between classes — even among horses, Sewell suggests, distinctions of breeding and station matter. His presence also highlights the Barker family's democratic kindness: they treat a gentleman's horse with the same care they give their own working animals, refusing to let class dictate compassion.
