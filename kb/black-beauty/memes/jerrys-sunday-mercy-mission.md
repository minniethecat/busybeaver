[[Jerry Barker]] makes a rare exception to his firm rule against Sunday work when [[Polly Barker]] convinces him that taking a poor neighbor, Dinah Brown, to visit her dying mother is an act of mercy, not a violation of the Sabbath. (040-009)

## 概述

On a Sunday morning, Polly Barker approaches Jerry with a request from their neighbor Dinah Brown, a poor woman with a four-week-old baby whose mother is dangerously ill ten miles away. Though Jerry is tired and the horses need their day of rest, Polly invokes the Golden Rule, arguing that helping Dinah is no different from pulling a beast out of a pit on the Sabbath. Jerry agrees, borrows a light trap to ease the load on his horse, and makes the journey—demonstrating that his principles are rooted in compassion, not rigid legalism. (040-009) (040-011) (040-012)

## 背景

Jerry Barker was known on the cab stand for his strict refusal to work on Sundays. He and Polly were devout Christians who observed the Sabbath as a day of rest for themselves and their horses. Mrs. Briggs, a regular customer, had previously been turned away for requesting Sunday service. Jerry believed that "if the Sunday was not made for rest, what was it made for?"—a principle that cost him business but earned him the respect of those who knew him. (040-006) (040-007)

## 事件经过

### Polly's Appeal

Polly came to Jerry in the yard on Sunday morning, looking full of something important. She explained that Dinah Brown had just received a letter: her mother was dangerously ill, and Dinah must go immediately if she wished to see her alive. The place was over ten miles away, and even if Dinah took the train, she would have four miles to walk—impossible for a woman so weak, with a baby only four weeks old. She promised to pay faithfully. (040-008) (040-009)

### Jerry's Dilemma

Jerry's immediate concern was not the money but the principle: "It was not the money I was thinking about, but of losing our Sunday; the horses are tired, and I am tired, too—that's where it pinches." It pinched in another way too: spending half Sunday apart from Polly and the children. (040-010)

### The Golden Rule Decides

Polly responded with the argument that settled the matter. She acknowledged that "it pinches all round," but reminded Jerry of the Golden Rule: "we should do to other people as we should like they should do to us." She added that she knew very well what she would want if her own mother were dying. Crucially, she framed the act not as a Sabbath violation but as a work of mercy: "I am sure it won't break the Sabbath; for if pulling a poor beast or donkey out of a pit would not spoil it, I am quite sure taking poor Dinah would not do it." (040-011)

### Jerry's Practical Compassion

Jerry accepted Polly's reasoning with characteristic warmth: "Why, Polly, you are as good as the minister, and so, as I've had my Sunday-morning sermon early to-day, you may go and tell Dinah that I'll be ready for her as the clock strikes ten." But his compassion extended further—he asked Polly to borrow butcher Braydon's light trap, which was never used on Sundays. A light trap would make "a wonderful difference to the horse," showing that even in an act of mercy, Jerry thought first of his horse's welfare. (040-012) (040-014)

> “It pinches all round, for that matter,” said Polly, “for it’s only half Sunday without you, but you know we should do to other people as we should like they should do to us; and I know very well what I should like if my mother was dying; and Jerry, dear, I am sure it won’t break the Sabbath; for if pulling a poor beast or donkey out of a pit would not spoil it, I am quite sure taking poor Dinah would not do it.”

## 后果与影响

The journey was made successfully, and Jerry returned in the afternoon. The event deepened the respect of those who knew him, reinforcing his reputation as a man of genuine principle. It also illustrated the practical working of the Golden Rule in everyday life—a theme that runs throughout the novel. This episode later helps explain why Jerry was willing to take other mercy missions, and why his customers trusted him absolutely.

## 主题意义

This episode directly embodies the novel's Christian ethic of compassionate action. Anna Sewell uses Jerry and Polly to demonstrate that true religious observance is not about rigid rule-following but about embodying the Golden Rule. Polly's argument—comparing Dinah's need to pulling a beast from a pit on the Sabbath—echoes Jesus's own teaching in the Gospels. The episode also reinforces Jerry's character as the moral center of the London cab stand sequences, showing that kindness to horses and kindness to humans spring from the same source.
