# Jakes

Jakes was a corn-dealer's carter who drove [[Black Beauty]] during the horse's final period of heavy work. He appears in the chapter "Jakes and the Lady," where his encounter with a compassionate gentlewoman becomes a small but significant victory for the novel's argument against the bearing rein. (050-001)

## 角色概述

Jakes is not portrayed as a cruel man. Like many working carters, he used the check-rein not out of malice but out of custom — "it is the fashion, you see." His character arc in a single brief scene shows how persuasion and example, rather than condemnation, can change a working man's practice. (050-013)

## 故事经历

### 艰难的工作

By the time Beauty came to Jakes, he was much reduced — his strength failing from years of hard work and the constant strain of the bearing rein. Jakes often said the load was "more than the horse ought to be asked to draw," but as a working man himself, he had little choice in the matter. (050-001) (050-002)

### 一位女士的干预

One day, as Jakes was struggling to get a heavy load up a hill, whipping Beauty to force him forward against the check-rein, a lady approached. She spoke to Jakes not with anger but with gentle reason, asking him to try removing the check-rein to see if Beauty could pull better with his head free. (050-006)

Jakes was skeptical but agreed to try. The result was immediate: "having his head has helped him up the hill." The lady then made her larger point:

> "Is it not better to lead a good fashion than to follow a bad one? A great many gentlemen do not use check-reins now; our carriage horses have not worn them for fifteen years."
>
> (050-013)

She added, more seriously, that "we have no right to distress any of God's creatures without a very good reason." Jakes, to his credit, listened. He thanked her and promised to "remember it another time." (050-017)

## 性格特征

- **Not cruel, just conventional**: He used the check-rein because everyone did, not from malice
- **Pragmatic**: When shown a better way, he was willing to try it
- **Teachable**: Unlike many characters in the novel, Jakes actually listened and changed his behavior
- **Working-class dignity**: He is portrayed as a man doing hard work under hard conditions

## 主题意义

Jakes embodies the novel's argument that cruelty to horses was often systemic rather than individual — a matter of "fashion" and custom rather than personal vice. The lady's approach to Jakes — patient, reasonable, and persuasive rather than condemnatory — models the reform strategy Sewell advocates: change minds one driver at a time, and the fashion itself will change.
