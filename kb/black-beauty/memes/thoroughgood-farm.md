# Thoroughgood Farm

Thoroughgood Farm was the rural property of [[Mr. Thoroughgood]] and his grandson [[Willie|Willie Thoroughgood]], where an exhausted and broken-down [[Black Beauty]] was brought to recover — and where, against all odds, the old horse found his way back to health and happiness. (052-023)

## 概述

After years of grueling work as a London cab horse and a series of increasingly harsh owners following [[Jerry Barker]]'s illness, Beauty reaches his lowest point: starved, overworked, and barely able to stand. It is at a horse sale that Mr. Thoroughgood — a kindly farmer — notices the horse's "gentle, intelligent eye" despite his wretched condition and decides to buy him. (052-023)

The farm becomes the site of Beauty's recovery. With good food, rest, and the devoted care of young Willie, Beauty slowly regains his strength. The meadows and pastures of the farm are the setting for the novel's final, hopeful turn. (052-026) (053-003)

### 重要事件

Mr. Thoroughgood purchased Beauty at a sale where the horse was in pitiable condition — thin, weak, and with "old sores on his back and hips." (052-023) He entrusted Beauty's daily care to his grandson Willie, who fed him, groomed him, and walked him gently through the meadows. Willie's patient tending — and his proud declaration "he's growing young" — marked the beginning of Beauty's physical and spiritual recovery. (052-026)

When Beauty was sufficiently restored, Mr. Thoroughgood took him to be inspected by the three ladies — Miss Blomefield, Miss Ellen, and their friend — who were looking for a gentle horse. Recognizing Beauty's quality beneath his scars, they purchased him and gave him his final, peaceful home. (053-003) (053-004)

## 象征意义

Thoroughgood Farm represents the possibility of redemption and recovery in the novel's moral economy. After the brutal trajectory of Beauty's life — from the kindness of Birtwick Park through the cruelty of Earlshall and the grinding hardship of London cab work — the farm is a liminal space between suffering and final peace. Its name — "Thoroughgood" — is an almost allegorical signal: the goodness that runs through and through is the quality that reverses Beauty's downward spiral, embodied in the farmer, his grandson, and the pastoral landscape they inhabit.
