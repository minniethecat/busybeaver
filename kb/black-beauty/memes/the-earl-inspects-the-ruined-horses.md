# The Earl Inspects the Ruined Horses

After [[Black Beauty]]'s knees healed from [[Reuben Smith]]'s drunken ride, he was turned into a small meadow — and was soon joined by [[Ginger]], ruined by [[Lord George]]'s hard riding. When the Earl of W—— and [[York]] came to inspect them, the verdict was swift: Ginger got a twelve-month's rest; Beauty, with his permanently scarred knees, must be sold. (027-004)

## 概述

Following the accident at the inn — when a drunken Reuben Smith rode Beauty over sharp stones and permanently damaged his knees — Beauty was placed in a small meadow to recover. Though physically healed, he was no longer the flawless carriage horse the Earl of W—— required. Soon Ginger joined him, her wind broken and back strained from Lord George's reckless steeplechase riding. The two ruined horses kept each other company until the earl and York arrived to assess their condition and decide their fates. (027-001)

## 背景

Beauty's troubles began when Reuben Smith, a skilled groom with a drinking problem, took him to town and got drunk. On the ride home, Smith failed to notice a loose nail in Beauty's shoe; the shoe came off, and riding on the sharp stones without protection ruined Beauty's knees. Smith was thrown and killed, but Beauty's knees were permanently scarred — "a great blemish in a handsome horse" that would seal his fate. (027-001)

Ginger's ruin came from a very different cause. [[Lord George]], the earl's son, was "young and would take no warning; he was a hard rider, and would hunt whenever he could get the chance, quite careless of his horse." When a steeplechase was announced, the groom told him Ginger was strained and unfit — but Lord George refused to believe it. He "urged Ginger to keep up with the foremost riders." With her "high spirit, she strained herself to the utmost; she came in with the first three horses, but her wind was touched, besides which he was too heavy for her, and her back was strained." (027-003)

## 事件经过

**Reunion in the Meadow**

After his knees healed, Beauty was turned into a small meadow alone. Though he enjoyed the liberty and sweet grass, he had been "so long used to society that I felt very lonely." One morning the gate opened and Ginger — now "dear old Ginger" — was led in. "With a joyful whinny I trotted up to her; we were both glad to meet." (027-002)

But Beauty soon understood this was no happy reunion. Ginger explained her ruin: Lord George's recklessness at the steeplechase. She summarized their shared tragedy with bitter clarity:

> "Here we are, ruined in the prime of our youth and strength, you by a drunkard, and I by a fool; it is very hard." (027-003)

The two broken horses passed their days quietly: feeding together, lying down in each other's company, and standing for hours under a shady lime-tree with their heads close together. (027-003)

**The Inspection**

One day the earl came into the meadow with York. Seeing them, the two horses stood still under their lime-tree and let the men approach. The earl examined them carefully and "seemed much annoyed." (027-004)

His judgment was blunt and practical:

> "There is three hundred pounds flung away for no earthly use; but what I care most for is that these horses of my old friend, who thought they would find a good home with me, are ruined." (027-004)

For Ginger, the earl gave a reprieve: "The mare shall have a twelve-month's run, and we shall see what that will do for her." A year of rest in the meadow might restore her. (027-004)

For Beauty, the verdict was harsher. The scarred knees were a permanent blemish: "The black one, he must be sold; 'tis a great pity, but I could not have knees like these in my stables." Appearance mattered more than soundness in the earl's establishment. (027-004)

York, showing the practical kindness that distinguished him from his employer, offered a solution: "I know a man in Bath, the master of some livery stables, who often wants a good horse at a low figure; I know he looks well after his horses. The inquest cleared the horse's character, and your lordship's recommendation, or mine, would be sufficient warrant." (027-004)

## 后果与影响

The inspection was the formal end of Beauty's and Ginger's life at Earlshall. Though the inquest had cleared Beauty's character — ruling Smith's death an accident, not the horse's fault — the scarred knees were a visible brand that no aristocrat would tolerate in his stables. Beauty was sold to the livery stables in Bath, beginning his long descent through a succession of ever-lower positions: job horse, cab horse, and eventually the exhausted animal bought by [[Mr. Thoroughgood]] at the horse fair.

Ginger's twelve-month run did her no lasting good. After the year, she was deemed fit for work again and sold — but the old strain soon returned. She changed hands repeatedly, always "getting lower down," until she ended up a broken-down cab horse in London, where Beauty would encounter her one last time. (040-001)

The earl's words — "these horses of my old friend, who thought they would find a good home with me, are ruined" — carry a bitter irony. Squire Gordon had entrusted his beloved horses to the earl believing they would be well cared for. Instead, one was ruined by the earl's own son, and the other by the earl's employee. The earl's primary concern, however, was not the suffering of the horses but the waste of "three hundred pounds" and the inconvenience of having blemished animals in his stables.

## 主题意义

The inspection scene crystallizes the novel's class-based critique of horse welfare. Both horses are ruined — but by two different kinds of human failing. Beauty is ruined by drunkenness (a working-class vice), Ginger by aristocratic arrogance (an upper-class vice). Sewell's symmetry is deliberate: no class has a monopoly on cruelty, and no class is exempt from responsibility.

The earl's reaction reveals the transactional nature of horse ownership among the aristocracy. His first concern is the monetary loss ("three hundred pounds flung away"); his second, a vague regret about letting down his "old friend" Squire Gordon; and only implicitly, and distantly, the suffering of the animals themselves. That York — a servant — is the one who proposes a kind placement for Beauty underscores Sewell's argument that kindness is a matter of character, not station.

Ginger's summary — "you by a drunkard, and I by a fool" — is one of the novel's most quoted lines, and for good reason. It compresses the entire moral architecture of the first half of the book into a single sentence: good horses ruined by bad men, regardless of the men's rank.
