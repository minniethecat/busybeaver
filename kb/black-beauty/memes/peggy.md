# Peggy

A gentle-natured mare with unusually short legs who worked alongside [[Black Beauty]] pulling carriages from the livery stable, her physical limitations making her a target for driver's whips.

## 角色概述

Peggy was a mare who stood in the stall next to [[Black Beauty]] during his time at the livery stable under [[Mr. Barry]]. (031-010) Her short legs made her naturally slower than other carriage horses, and she suffered greatly from being harnessed alongside faster-paced horses. Peggy's story is brief but poignant — a portrait of quiet suffering borne with dignity.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Peggy entered [[Black Beauty]]'s life after [[Rory]] was disabled in a road accident. (031-010) Beauty was paired with Peggy in the carriage, and the two horses shared the stable and the road together. Peggy was already well aware of her own limitations, speaking of her "very bad" paces with troubled resignation. (031-012)

### 关键转折

The central tragedy of Peggy's working life was structural: in the livery system, horses were paired for convenience, not compatibility. Peggy, with her short legs and slow pace, was inevitably yoked alongside faster horses. In such pairings, when the driver whipped to increase speed, the whipping "all comes to their share" — the slow horse, unable to match the pace of her partner, received the brunt of the punishment. (031-017)

### 结局

Peggy's ultimate fate is not recorded in the novel, but [[Black Beauty]]'s sorrowful reflection — "I was very sorry for her, and I could not comfort her" — suggests that horses like Peggy passed through the system anonymously, their suffering unremarked and unrelieved. (031-017)

## 性格特征

- Gentle and self-aware, conscious of her physical shortcomings
- Despondent but not bitter — her sadness is directed inward, not at others
- Embodies the quiet endurance of horses who know they cannot win
- Her short legs are a physical metaphor for being born at a disadvantage in a system that values speed

## 关键引文

> "Ah," she said in a troubled manner, "I know my paces are very bad, but what can I do? It isn't my fault; my legs are so short." (031-012)

> "I was very sorry for her, and I could not comfort her, for I knew how hard it was upon slow-paced horses to be put with fast ones; all the whipping comes to their share, and they can't help it." (031-017)

## 主题意义

Peggy's short legs are among the novel's most quietly devastating metaphors. She is not mistreated because anyone is cruel — she is mistreated because the system is designed for uniformity and speed, and she does not fit. Her case illustrates what [[Anna Sewell]] implies throughout the novel: that much equine suffering is structural, not personal. Peggy cannot grow longer legs, and the livery system cannot accommodate a slow horse. The result is inevitable suffering — a horse punished for the body she was born with.
