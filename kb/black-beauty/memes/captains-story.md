# Captain's Story

At the [[London Cab Stand|london cab stand]], the old war horse Captain tells [[Black Beauty]] the story of his life — a narrative spanning the Crimean War, the cavalry, and his eventual decline to cab work — in the novel's most complete embedded autobiography of a horse.

## 概述

Captain was a tall, dignified old horse who shared a stable with Beauty during his London cab horse period. One evening, Captain told Beauty his life story: born and bred on a nobleman's estate, trained for the cavalry, and shipped to the Crimea. He served through the war, carrying his master through battles, witnessing the deaths of men and horses, and learning both the nobility and the horror of military service (034-005). After the war, he passed through several owners, each step a descent, until he ended up pulling a cab in London.

## 背景

Captain was one of Jerry Barker's horses, stabled alongside Beauty. He was noted for his patience, dignity, and the respect he commanded even from rough cab drivers. Jerry treated him with particular care, aware of his age and his distinguished past.

Captain's narrative introduced the Crimean War into the novel — a conflict that had ended only two decades before the book's publication and would have been fresh in the memory of many readers.

## 后果与影响

Captain's story expanded the novel's scope from domestic animal welfare to the broader question of what humans ask of animals in war. The cavalry horse — trained for courage, tested in battle, and then discarded to the cab trade — embodied the novel's critique of a system that used animals and then abandoned them.

Captain's dignity in his reduced circumstances also served as a model for Beauty during his own cab horse years. The old horse's ability to endure hardship without losing his sense of self-worth was a lesson Beauty carried forward.

## 主题意义

Captain's story is the novel's most sustained embedded narrative — a convention of Victorian fiction that Sewell adapts to give voice to a horse. By having Captain narrate his own history, the novel grants him the dignity of a full life story. The Crimean War setting connects the novel to contemporary British history and suggests that the nation's debt to its animals extends beyond the farm and the street to the battlefield.

> Captain had been broken in and trained for an army horse; his first owner was an officer of cavalry going out to the Crimean war. He said he quite enjoyed the training with all the other horses, trotting together, turning together, to the right hand or the left, halting at the word of command, or dashing forward at full speed at the sound of the trumpet or signal of the officer. He was, when young, a dark, dappled iron-gray, and considered very handsome. His master, a young, high-spirited gentleman, was very fond of him, and treated him from the first with the greatest care and kindness. He told me he thought the life of an army horse was very pleasant; but when it came to being sent abroad over the sea in a great ship, he almost changed his mind.
