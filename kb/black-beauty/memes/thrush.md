# Thrush

Thrush is a bacterial infection that affects the frog of a horse's hoof, producing a foul-smelling discharge and causing lameness. In *Black Beauty*, the condition serves as a diagnostic marker of equine neglect — an ailment that reveals the filthy conditions in which working horses were kept. (033-014)

## 定义

Thrush is caused by bacteria that thrive in moist, dirty, and poorly ventilated environments. The frog — the V-shaped structure on the underside of the hoof — becomes infected, producing a black, foul-smelling discharge. If left untreated, the infection can eat into the sensitive tissues of the hoof, causing significant pain and lameness.

## 在书中的体现

The condition appears in the novel when Black Beauty, newly arrived at a London cab stable, is examined by a knowledgeable horseman who immediately identifies the problem:

> "Your horse has got the 'thrush', and badly, too; his feet are very tender, and it is a shame to drive him over these stones without shoes."
>
> (033-014)

The diagnosis comes as part of a broader assessment of Beauty's poor condition after being sold from owner to owner. The thrush is presented not as an isolated medical issue but as evidence of systematic neglect — a direct consequence of the dirty, cramped stabling conditions that urban working horses endured.

## 与清洁和马房管理的关系

Thrush in the novel is inseparable from the theme of stable management. At [[Birtwick Park]], where [[John Manly]] kept the stables meticulously clean, Beauty never suffered from hoof problems. It is only in the London cab stables — where horses stood in their own filth with inadequate bedding and no turn-out — that the infection takes hold. The contrast implicitly argues that thrush is a management disease: preventable through good husbandry, inevitable under neglect.

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell uses thrush as part of her systematic catalogue of the physical consequences of bad horse-keeping. The disease appears alongside other ailments — sore shoulders from ill-fitting collars, damaged wind from overwork, bruised soles from unshod hooves on cobblestones — as one more way in which the urban horse economy literally broke animals' bodies down.
