# Mr. Thoroughgood

A kind farmer who purchased the aged and worn-down [[Black Beauty]] at a horse sale, recognizing the quality beneath his poor condition, and patiently nursed him back to health. (052-023)

## 角色概述

Mr. Thoroughgood appears late in the novel as a compassionate and experienced horseman. Unlike the buyers who saw only a broken-down cab horse, he recognized Black Beauty's breeding and potential. His intervention saves Beauty from a grim fate and sets up the novel's happy ending. (052-023) (052-026)

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Mr. Thoroughgood was an established farmer with a good eye for horses. He had a grandson, Willie, who shared his grandfather's love for animals and helped care for the horses. (053-003)

### 关键转折

At a horse sale, Mr. Thoroughgood saw Black Beauty — now thin, worn, and miserable after years of abuse under [[Nicholas Skinner]]. Where others saw a spent animal, Thoroughgood recognized "many first-rate horses have been sold as worn out only because their legs have not been properly cared for." (053-004) He bought Beauty and brought him to his farm.

With patient care, proper feeding, and rest, Mr. Thoroughgood gradually restored Beauty to health. In March, he determined Beauty was ready for light work. (052-026) His grandson Willie proudly rode the recovered horse.

### 结局

Through Mr. Thoroughgood's connections, Black Beauty was sold to the three ladies at the novel's end — Miss Blomefield, Miss Ellen, and their friend. There, Beauty was reunited with [[Joe Green]], now a grown and skilled groom. Mr. Thoroughgood's act of kindness had given Beauty a dignified retirement. (053-020)

## 性格特征

- **Perceptive**: Saw quality in a horse others had written off. (053-004)
- **Patient**: Gave Beauty months of rest and recovery without rushing. (052-026)
- **Kind**: Motivated by genuine care for horses, not profit.
- **Experienced**: Knew that many "worn out" horses simply needed proper care. (053-004)

## 主题意义

Mr. Thoroughgood embodies the novel's closing argument: that kindness and proper care can reverse even severe neglect. His perceptive eye — seeing the thoroughbred beneath the broken-down exterior — is a metaphor for Sewell's broader point about seeing the inherent worth in all working animals. His role as Beauty's final human benefactor brings the novel's moral arc full circle: from Farmer Grey's idyllic meadow to near-destruction on London's streets, and finally back to green fields through one man's compassion.
