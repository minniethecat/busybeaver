# Squire Gordon

Squire Gordon is the owner of Birtwick Park, where Black Beauty spends his happiest years. A country gentleman of means and principle, Squire Gordon maintains a stable where kindness is the governing rule, and his coachman John Manly is empowered to treat every horse with patience and respect.

## 角色概述

The Squire is introduced early, inspecting the newly arrived Black Beauty after John Manly's grooming: "with my coat soft and bright, the squire came to look at me" (006-002). He demonstrates practical horsemanship and takes genuine interest in his animals' welfare. Unlike fashionable owners who value appearance over substance, Squire Gordon judges his horses by their character and condition.


> When I was four years old Squire Gordon came to look at me. He examined my eyes, my mouth, and my legs; he felt them all down; and then I had to walk and trot and gallop before him. He seemed to like me, and said, "When he has been well broken in he will do very well."
>
> (004-002)

## 故事经历

Squire Gordon's wife falls ill, compelling the family to leave England for a warmer climate (022-001). The horses must be sold — "sad changes were about to come over us" (022-001) — and the parting scene marks the end of Beauty's happiest period. John Manly "went about his work silent and sad" (022-002) as the stable is dismantled.

The Squire's departure is not a moral failure but a circumstance beyond his control, underscoring the novel's recognition that even the best situations are fragile and that a horse's welfare depends entirely on forces it cannot influence.

## 性格特征

- **Humane** — treats his horses as sentient beings, not property
- **Discerning** — values skilled horsemen like John Manly and gives them autonomy
- **Principled** — maintains high standards of care regardless of fashion

## 主题意义

Squire Gordon represents the ideal horse owner — wealthy enough to provide excellent care, principled enough to demand it, and humble enough to trust his coachman's expertise. His forced departure from England demonstrates that good intentions alone cannot protect animals from the vicissitudes of fortune.
