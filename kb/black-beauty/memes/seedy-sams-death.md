# Seedy Sam's Death

Seedy Sam was a shabby, miserable-looking cab driver who worked the London streets under [[Nicholas Skinner]], one of the most brutal cab-owners in the city. His desperate speech at the cab stand — heard by [[Jerry Barker]] and [[Black Beauty]] — laid bare the economics of exploitation that crushed both working men and their horses. He died not long after, worked to exhaustion in a system that gave him no chance of survival. (039-001)

## 概述

The London cab trade had two classes of driver. Men like Jerry Barker owned their own cab and horses, and had "a chance of getting on and a chance of doing right." But the majority drove for large cab-owners like Skinner, who charged drivers a fixed daily fee for the use of a cab and two horses. These drivers had to earn the master's fee before they earned a penny for themselves — and if the horses didn't work, their families didn't eat. Seedy Sam, with six children at home and only one earning anything, was trapped in this system. His speech on the cab stand is the novel's most explicit social-protest passage, and his death is its most damning verdict on the system. (039-002)

## 背景

Beauty had been working as a cab horse under Jerry Barker for some time when he first heard Seedy Sam's story. Jerry was an exceptional master — kind, fair, and a strict observer of Sunday rest. But Beauty understood that many horses were far less fortunate: "There were a great many horses which belonged to the large cab-owners, who let them out to their drivers for so much money a day. As the horses did not belong to these men the only thing they thought of was how to get their money out of them." (039-001)

The economics were brutal. In the busy season, a driver might have to pay eighteen shillings a day for a cab and two horses — "nine shillings a day to get out of each horse before you begin to get your own living." Fares were fixed by law at sixpence a mile within the four-mile radius. A driver might go six miles and take only three shillings — twelve miles for the horse, three shillings for the man. (039-002)

## 事件经过

**The Speech**

One day Seedy Sam brought his horse to the stand "looking dreadfully beat." The governor — a kind-hearted man who ran the cab stand — told him he looked more fit for the police station than for the rank. Sam turned on him with a voice "that sounded almost desperate": (039-001)

> "If the police have any business with the matter it ought to be with the masters who charge us so much, or with the fares that are fixed so low." (039-002)

He described his situation: working fourteen or sixteen hours a day, no Sunday off for ten or twelve weeks, six children at home with only one earning anything. He had pawned his clock to pay Skinner's fee. "I want a warm coat and a mackintosh, but with so many to feed how can a man get it?" (039-002)

The other drivers stood around nodding — they knew he was right. Sam drove for Skinner, who "never gives a day if he can help it." The system gave him no choice: "if the horses don't work we must starve, and I and my children have known what that is before now." (039-002)

Sam contrasted his lot with those who owned their own cabs: "You that have your own horses and cabs, or drive for good masters, have a chance of getting on and a chance of doing right; I haven't." (039-003)

He described a morning's work: a six-mile fare that paid only three shillings, no return fare, twelve miles for the horse and three shillings for him. Then a three-mile fare overloaded with luggage — the passengers piled everything inside instead of putting bags on top where they'd earn extra twopences. The driver's income depended on passengers' whims, not on the actual work done. (039-003)

**The Death**

Sam's prediction came true. Some time later, Beauty's new master was [[Nicholas Skinner]] — "the man that poor Seedy Sam drove for." (046-001) Beauty describes Skinner with visceral revulsion: "He had black eyes and a hooked nose, his mouth was as full of teeth as a bull-dog's, and his voice was as harsh as the grinding of cart wheels over graveled stones." Under Skinner, Beauty too wished he "might, like Ginger, drop down dead at my work and be out of my misery." (047-001)

Sam did not survive the system. The novel does not describe his actual death — Sewell's restraint again — but the implication is clear: he was worked to death, just like the horses. His fate is the human parallel to Ginger's: both were used up by a system that valued profit over life.

## 后果与影响

Seedy Sam's speech is the most explicit piece of social criticism in the novel. It connects the suffering of horses directly to the suffering of the working poor — the same economic forces that destroyed Ginger and nearly destroyed Beauty also destroyed Sam. The fixed fares, the daily fees, the long hours with no rest — these were real features of the Victorian cab trade, and Sewell used Sam to expose them.

Sam's death reinforces the novel's core argument: cruelty to animals is not just a matter of individual wickedness but of systemic exploitation. Skinner is a villain, but the system that allows Skinner to operate is the larger villain. Sam's ghost haunts the later chapters — when Beauty himself falls into Skinner's hands, the reader already knows what that means.

## 主题意义

Seedy Sam's death is the novel's bridge between animal welfare and labor reform. Sewell does not treat the suffering of horses and the suffering of poor men as separate issues — they are products of the same economic logic. The man who must work his horse to death to feed his children is as much a victim of the system as the horse itself.

This was a radical argument in 1877. The RSPCA had been founded in 1824, but its early campaigns treated animal cruelty primarily as a moral failing of individuals. Sewell's insight — articulated through Sam — was that much cruelty was structural: baked into fares, fees, and working hours that gave men no choice but to be cruel.

Sam's speech also serves as a foil for Jerry Barker. Jerry could afford to be kind because he owned his cab, observed Sunday rest, and had a wife who managed their modest finances carefully. Sam had none of these advantages. The novel does not condemn Sam for what he became; it condemns the system that made him that way.
