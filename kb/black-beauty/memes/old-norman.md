## 角色概述

Old Norman was the coachman at [[Birtwick Park]] under Squire Gordon's father, serving before [[John Manly]] took over the position. Though long retired by the time of the main narrative, Norman's influence persists through the stable practices and values he passed down to his successor. He represents an intergenerational tradition of responsible horsemanship at Birtwick.

## 故事经历

Old Norman's legacy is recalled by John Manly during a conversation about stable management. When Black Beauty first arrives at Birtwick, John describes how Norman received him:

> (018-013)He put me into the stable under old Norman, the coachman that was then. I had my food and water brought to me, and was kept warm and comfortable.

This careful initial treatment of a newly arrived horse reflects Norman's methodical approach to stable management. John elaborates on Norman's philosophy:

> (018-015)The old master and mistress and old Norman had only taken care of number one, and never thought of making money out of the horses.

This principle — prioritizing the horses' welfare over profit — became the foundation of Birtwick's reputation as an ideal stable. John Manly, trained under Norman's supervision, carried these values forward:

> (018-013)I was stable-boy under old Norman, and when he died, master promoted me.

The smooth transition from Norman to John ensured continuity in Birtwick's high standards of animal care across two generations of service.

## 性格特征

Though Old Norman never appears in the present action of the novel, his character emerges through John Manly's recollections:

| Trait | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| **Experienced** | Served the Gordon family for decades, from the old master's time |
| **Methodical** | Established systematic stable routines that John later followed |
| **Humane** | Prioritized horse welfare over profit — "never thought of making money out of the horses" |
| **Mentor** | Trained John Manly, who became the novel's model coachman |
| **Loyal** | Served one family for his entire career |

## 与其他角色的关系

- **[[John Manly]]** — Norman's stable-boy, protégé, and successor. John credits Norman for his training and frequently references Norman's standards as the benchmark for proper horse care.
- **[[Squire Gordon]]** — Norman served Squire Gordon's father, establishing the family tradition of excellent horsemanship that the younger Squire Gordon continued.
- **[[Black Beauty]]** — Though Beauty never met Norman, he benefited from the stable culture Norman established.
- **[[Ginger]]** — John contrasts Norman's methods with the harsh treatment Ginger received at other stables, highlighting what proper training looks like.

## 在故事中的角色

Old Norman serves as an invisible foundation for Birtwick's reputation as the novel's model stable. Anna Sewell uses him to demonstrate that good horsemanship is taught, not innate — it is a tradition passed from one generation of stable workers to the next. Norman's legacy shows that responsible animal care requires both knowledge and a moral commitment to the animals' welfare.

The contrast between Norman's standards and those of later stables (Earlshall, the livery stable, Skinner's cab yard) underscores the novel's central argument: the difference between good and bad treatment of horses is a matter of human character and training, not circumstance.
