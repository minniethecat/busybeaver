# Nicholas Skinner

A harsh and mercenary London cab owner who briefly owned [[Black Beauty]] in the final year of his working life, personifying the system of equine exploitation that the novel condemns.

## 角色概述

Nicholas Skinner ran a low-end cab operation in [[London]], hiring out worn-out horses to equally desperate drivers. (051-003) He was described in grotesque physical terms — black eyes, a hooked nose, a mouth "as full of teeth as a bull-dog's," and a voice "as harsh as the grinding of cart wheels over graveled stones" — external features that mirror his inner brutality. (051-001) Skinner's business model was simple: work horses until they could no longer stand, then sell them for whatever they would fetch at the knacker's.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Skinner's reputation preceded him. Long before [[Black Beauty]] fell into his hands, the cab driver [[Seedy Sam]] spoke bitterly of Skinner's practice of denying his horses any rest, even on Sundays. (042-005) Another driver, Grant, died raving about Skinner in his final hours, his last words being "I never had a Sunday's rest." (042-018)

### 关键转折

Skinner purchased [[Black Beauty]] when the horse was already worn down from years of cab work. (051-001) Under Skinner's ownership, Beauty was paired with a driver who had a family to feed and was forced by economic necessity to push his horses beyond their limits. The combination of Skinner's indifference and the driver's desperation created a regime of relentless overwork.

One day, overloaded with a heavy trunk on the roof of the cab, Beauty collapsed in the street, unable to rise. (051-017) A veterinary examination determined that Beauty's wind was broken and he could no longer work as a cab horse.

### 结局

When told that [[Black Beauty]] needed rest, pasture, and months of recovery, Skinner's response revealed his entire philosophy: "Then he must just go to the dogs. I have no meadows to nurse sick horses in — he might get well or he might not; that sort of thing don't suit my business; my plan is to work 'em as long as they'll go, and then sell 'em for what they'll fetch, at the knacker's or elsewhere." (051-019) Only reluctantly, on the advice of his farrier, did Skinner agree to give Beauty proper care and feeding so he could be sold at the upcoming horse fair. (051-021) This pragmatic concession — motivated entirely by profit — inadvertently saved Beauty's life by allowing him to recover enough to be purchased by [[Mr. Thoroughgood]].

## 性格特征

- Mercenary and profit-driven, with no regard for animal suffering
- Physically repellent, his appearance described in grotesque detail
- Treats horses as disposable assets to be wrung dry and discarded
- Represents the worst of the commercial exploitation system

## 主题意义

Nicholas Skinner embodies the profit-driven cruelty that *Black Beauty* identifies as the root cause of equine suffering. He is not a cartoon villain but a chillingly realistic product of a system where horses were commodities. Skinner's famous dismissal — "work 'em as long as they'll go" — encapsulates the economic logic that the novel's moral argument must overcome. Tellingly, Skinner's own drivers are also victims: they are "hard on the horses" because Skinner is "hard on the men," revealing how exploitation cascades downward through the social hierarchy. (051-003)
