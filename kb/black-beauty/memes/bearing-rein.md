# Bearing Rein

The bearing rein (also called the check-rein or gag-rein) is a strap used in horse harnesses to prevent the horse from lowering its head. In Victorian England, it was fashionable for carriage horses to hold their heads high, and the bearing rein was tightened to enforce this posture. In *Black Beauty*, Anna Sewell portrays the bearing rein as one of the most painful and unnecessary cruelties inflicted on horses.

## 定义

A bearing rein runs from the horse's bit, through the crownpiece of the bridle, to the saddle or harness pad. When tightened, it forces the horse's head upward and backward, preventing the natural lowering of the head needed for balance, breathing, and pulling strength. The tighter the rein, the higher and more rigid the head position — and the greater the horse's suffering.

## 在书中的体现

Black Beauty's companion Ginger describes her hatred of the check-rein from her earliest experience: "I had been driven with a check-rein by the dealer, and I hated it worse than anything else." (009-002) She recounts how her previous owner's coachman claimed she "had not been well broken to the check-rein" (009-005), using it as an excuse for her resistance — when in fact the device itself was causing her distress.

At Earlshall Park, the bearing rein becomes a daily torment for both Black Beauty and Ginger. The coachman York tightens the reins progressively: "Day by day, hole by hole, our bearing reins were shortened, and instead of looking forward with pleasure to having my harness put on, I began to dread it." (024-020)

### 关键场景

**Ginger's account.** Ginger tells Black Beauty that her first owner's wife insisted on a tight check-rein for fashion. When Ginger fought against it, she was sold to a dealer who "drove me quite without a check-rein, and then sold me as a perfectly quiet horse" (009-006) — revealing that the device itself, not the horse's nature, was the cause of her restlessness.

**The old horse's wisdom.** An old horse at Birtwick explains the bearing rein's origin succinctly: "For fashion!" (011-007). He elaborates: "I suppose it is fashion that makes them strap our heads up" (011-008), and declares "to my mind, fashion is one of the wickedest things in the world" (011-009).

**The Earlshall consequences.** The progressive tightening at Earlshall leads directly to Ginger's rebellion and injury, which results in her being sold. This chain of events — fashion → bearing rein → suffering → rebellion → sale → decline — is the novel's clearest indictment of fashionable cruelty.

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell uses the bearing rein as the central symbol of how fashion overrides compassion. The old horse's observation — "if we should be a good deal less according to fashion, we should find many things work easier" (012-013) — is presented as simple common sense that fashionable society ignores. Sewell's critique is not merely about one piece of equipment but about a system where appearance is valued above welfare.

## 历史背景

The bearing rein was standard equipment for fashionable carriage horses throughout the 19th century. Despite criticism from veterinarians and horsemen, it remained popular because a high-headed horse was considered elegant. Sewell's novel contributed significantly to public awareness of its cruelty, and the bearing rein fell out of fashion in the decades following the book's 1877 publication. The campaign against it became one of the early successes of the animal welfare movement.
