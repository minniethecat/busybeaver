During a Sunday holiday in the orchard at [[Birtwick Park]], [[Sir Oliver]] reveals the cruel truth about his shortened tail, igniting a passionate discussion among [[Ginger]], [[Merrylegs]], and [[Black Beauty]] about tail-docking, check-reins, blinkers, and the casual cruelty inflicted on animals in the name of fashion. (011-003)

## 概述

On one of their free Sundays in the orchard, Black Beauty asks Sir Oliver how he lost his tail. Sir Oliver reveals it was no accident—his tail was cut off through flesh and bone in a cruel practice called docking. This revelation prompts Ginger to connect it to her own suffering with the check-rein, and the horses engage in a philosophical discussion about fashion's cruelty, with Merrylegs ultimately calling for gratitude rather than bitterness toward their kind masters. (011-003) (011-008) (011-013)

## 背景

The horses at Birtwick Park enjoyed occasional Sunday liberty in the home paddock or old orchard, where they could graze freely and talk under the shade of the large chestnut tree. It was during one such holiday that Beauty, who had often wondered about Sir Oliver's unusually short tail, finally ventured to ask about it. Sir Oliver, a gentle but fiery old horse, was deeply fond of his master but carried the permanent mark of a cruel practice. (011-003) (007-004)

## 事件经过

### Sir Oliver's Revelation

When Beauty asked what accident had cost Sir Oliver his tail, the old horse snorted fiercely: "Accident! it was no accident! it was a cruel, shameful, cold-blooded act!" He described being taken as a young horse to a place where he was tied up immovably, and then his long, beautiful tail was cut off through the flesh and through the bone. Beyond the terrible pain and the indignity of losing his best ornament, Sir Oliver described the lifelong practical consequence: without a full tail, he could never properly brush the flies off his sides and hind legs. "I tell you it is a lifelong wrong, and a lifelong loss; but thank heaven, they don't do it now." (011-003) (011-004) (011-005)

### Fashion as the Enemy

When Ginger asked why such a thing was done, Sir Oliver stamped his foot and declared: "For fashion! if you know what that means." He described how every well-bred young horse in his time had his tail docked, "just as if the good God that made us did not know what we wanted and what looked best." Ginger immediately connected this to her own experience: "I suppose it is fashion that makes them strap our heads up with those horrid bits that I was tortured with in London." (011-006) (011-007) (011-008)

### The Skye Story

Sir Oliver broadened the indictment by telling the story of his dear friend Skye, a brown terrier who lived in his stall. Skye had a litter of five puppies, but one day a man took them all away and brought them back bleeding and crying—each had a piece of its tail cut off and the soft flap of its ears cut away. Sir Oliver's righteous anger at human hypocrisy boiled over: "Why don't they cut their own children's ears into points to make them look sharp? Why don't they cut the end off their noses to make them look plucky? One would be just as sensible as the other. What right have they to torment and disfigure God's creatures?" (011-009)

### Ginger's Fury and the Blinkers Question

Ginger was deeply stirred by Sir Oliver's story, flinging up her head with flashing eyes and declaring that men were both brutes and blockheads. Beauty, troubled by these revelations, tried to turn the subject by asking about the use of blinkers. Sir Oliver replied bluntly that they had no use. The discussion grew heated until Merrylegs arrived from the old apple-tree and gently reproached them: "talking against men in such a place as this doesn't seem fair or grateful," reminding everyone that their own master, John, and James were always good to them. (011-010) (011-013) (011-014) (011-015)

## 后果与影响

Merrylegs's wise speech cooled the discussion, particularly affecting Sir Oliver, who was dearly fond of his master. The conversation strengthened the bond among the Birtwick horses and deepened Beauty's understanding of the wider world of equine suffering. It also reinforced the novel's argument that cruelty to animals is often casual and institutionalized—driven by fashion, ignorance, and indifference rather than active malice. The discussion foreshadows later events where these same issues (check-reins, harsh bits, ignorant treatment) recur throughout the novel.

## 主题意义

This orchard discussion is one of the novel's most explicit philosophical passages. Through the horses' conversation, Anna Sewell catalogs the casual cruelties of Victorian animal treatment—tail-docking, check-reins, ear-cropping, and blinkers—all justified by "fashion." Sir Oliver's rhetorical questions about human hypocrisy ("Why don't they cut their own children's ears?") are a direct challenge to the reader's moral complacency. The passage exemplifies Sewell's technique of using animal narrators to expose human cruelty, while Merrylegs's moderating voice prevents the critique from becoming misanthropic, acknowledging that good masters and grooms do exist.
