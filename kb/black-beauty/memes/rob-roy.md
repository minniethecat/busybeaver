# Rob Roy

A good hunter horse and the older brother of [[Black Beauty]], killed tragically in a hunting accident early in the novel.

## 角色概述

Rob Roy was a hunter horse owned by [[Farmer Grey]] and was the son of [[Duchess]], making him [[Black Beauty]]'s older brother by the same mother. He was described as a good horse with a gentle temperament. (003-015) According to [[John Manly]], Rob Roy and Black Beauty shared a striking resemblance, both inheriting their mother's qualities. (006-018) His death in a hunting accident serves as one of the novel's earliest lessons about the dangers horses face at the hands of men.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Rob Roy was born to [[Duchess]] at Farmer Grey's farm, where he grew up alongside his mother before being trained as a hunter. He was several years older than [[Black Beauty]], and by the time Beauty was old enough to remember him, Rob Roy was already an established hunter in Farmer Grey's stable. (003-015)

### 关键转折

During a hunt near Farmer Grey's farm, a young rider named George Gordon, the son of [[Squire Gordon]], was thrown from Rob Roy. The horse fell while jumping a fence and was fatally injured. (003-016) The accident had a profound impact on young Black Beauty, who witnessed the commotion as the injured horse was brought back. Later, Beauty learned that this horse was his own brother — a revelation that [[John Manly]] shared with [[James Howard]] while grooming Beauty. (006-020)

### 结局

Rob Roy was killed in the hunting accident. His body was taken away, and Black Beauty never learned what became of it. The tragedy foreshadows many of the novel's later lessons about the fragility of a horse's life and the consequences of reckless riding. (003-016) (006-020)

## 性格特征

- A good-natured and reliable horse, well-suited to hunting
- Shared his mother [[Duchess]]'s gentle temperament
- Physically resembled [[Black Beauty]] in appearance and movement

## 主题意义

Rob Roy's death represents one of the novel's central themes: the vulnerability of horses to human carelessness. Unlike [[Black Beauty]], who survives through many trials, Rob Roy dies young because of a riding accident — a stark illustration of how even well-meaning humans can cause equine suffering. His death also establishes an early emotional connection for the reader, making the stakes of horse welfare personal before the novel launches into its broader critique of Victorian equine treatment.
