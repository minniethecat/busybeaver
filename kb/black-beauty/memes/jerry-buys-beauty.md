At a London horse fair, [[Jerry Barker]] — a small, gray-eyed cabman with a kind manner and a fresh, clean smell — buys [[Black Beauty]] for £24.10 after a tense bidding contest, beginning the happiest period of Beauty's working life. (035-005)

## 概述

After being sold from [[Earlshall Park]], Black Beauty finds himself at a London horse fair, placed among "strong, useful-looking horses" whose best days are behind them. His broken knees — the legacy of [[Reuben Smith]]'s fatal drunk ride — cause gentleman buyers to turn away. But a compact, quick-moving cabman with kind gray eyes sees past the blemishes. After a tense negotiation in which a hard-faced dealer nearly buys Beauty, Jerry Barker secures him for £24.10, pays on the spot, and rides him through the London twilight to a warm welcome from his wife Polly and children Harry and Dolly. (035-003) (035-004) (035-005) (035-010) (035-015)

## 背景

The horse fair followed a period of decline for Beauty. After the catastrophic night ride that killed Reuben Smith and ruined Beauty's knees, he had been sold down through a succession of increasingly humble owners: from Earlshall to a livery stable, then to a corn dealer and a baker who overworked and underfed him, and finally to the fair — the last resort for horses whose visible blemishes made them unsellable through private channels.

The fair itself was a morally corrupt place. Beauty observed that "there were more lies told and more trickery at that horse fair than a clever man could give an account of." Sellers concealed injuries, exaggerated capabilities, and passed off broken-down animals as sound. Beauty's salesman falsely claimed his broken knees were "only a slip in the stall." (035-003)

## 事件经过

### The Examination

Prospective buyers subjected Beauty to a rough inspection: pulling his mouth open, examining his eyes, feeling down his legs, testing his skin and flesh, and trying his paces. Beauty noted with remarkable sensitivity the difference in how this was done — some men handled him "in a rough, offhand way, as if one was only a piece of wood," while others "would take their hands gently over one's body, with a pat now and then, as much as to say, 'By your leave.'" Beauty judged buyers by their manners, and most were found wanting. (035-004)

### Jerry's First Offer

Then came a man unlike the others. Not a gentleman, nor one of the "loud, flashy sort that call themselves so," Jerry was "rather a small man, but well made, and quick in all his motions." Beauty knew instantly from his handling that he was experienced with horses. He spoke gently, and his "gray eye had a kindly, cheery look in it." Most remarkably, there was "the clean, fresh smell there was about him... no smell of old beer and tobacco, which I hated, but a fresh smell as if he had come out of a hayloft." (035-005)

Jerry offered twenty-three pounds. The salesman refused. Jerry walked away.

### The Tension

A "very hard-looking, loud-voiced man" now approached. Beauty was "dreadfully afraid he would have me," but the man walked off. Two or three more came who "did not mean business." Then the hard-faced man returned and matched Jerry's offer at twenty-three pounds. The salesman, sensing he might not get his asking price, began to waver. (035-005)

Just then, the gray-eyed man came back. Beauty could not help reaching out his head toward him — an involuntary gesture of hope. Jerry stroked his face kindly. (035-005)

### The Deal

"Well, old chap," Jerry said, "I think we should suit each other. I'll give twenty-four for him." (035-006)

The salesman countered: "Say twenty-five and you shall have him." (035-007)

"Twenty-four ten," said Jerry in a "very decided tone," "and not another sixpence — yes or no?" (035-008)

"Done," said the salesman. "And you may depend upon it there's a monstrous deal of quality in that horse, and if you want him for cab work he's a bargain." (035-009)

### Arrival in London

The money was paid immediately. Jerry led Beauty to an inn where he had a saddle and bridle ready. He gave Beauty a good feed of oats and "stood by while I ate it, talking to himself and talking to me" — a small gesture of companionship that marked him as different from the purely transactional dealers. (035-010)

They rode through "pleasant lanes and country roads" until they reached the great London thoroughfare. In the twilight the gas lamps were lit; streets branched to the right and left "for mile upon mile." Finally, passing a long cab stand, Jerry called out cheerfully: "Good-night, governor!" (035-010)

"Halloo!" a voice replied. "Have you got a good one?" (035-011)

"I think so," replied Jerry. (035-012)

### The Welcome Home

Jerry led Beauty through a narrow side street to a modest house. He whistled, and "the door flew open." A young woman — Polly — followed by a little girl and boy, ran out to greet him. With lively excitement they opened the stable gates, and the next minute "they were all standing round me in a small stable-yard." Little Dolly asked if he was gentle, and Jerry replied: "Yes, Dolly, as gentle as your own kitten; come and pat him." (035-015) (035-016) (035-017) (035-018) (035-019)

## 后果与影响

The purchase marked the beginning of Beauty's three happiest years since leaving [[Birtwick Park]]. Under Jerry's care — with regular hours, a warm stable, no Sunday work, and a driver who refused to whip him — Beauty thrived as a London cab horse. The Barker family's collective care (Polly and Dolly cleaned the cab cushions, Harry groomed the horses) gave Beauty a sense of belonging he had not felt since Birtwick.

Jerry's investment of £24.10 — a substantial sum for a cabman — proved that good horsemanship and kindness were not incompatible with commercial viability. The purchase also introduced Beauty to a community of cab horses and drivers, including the old war horse [[Captain]] and the wise Governor Grant, whose stories enriched the novel's portrait of working-class London.

## 主题意义

The purchase scene is one of the novel's most quietly profound moments. Beauty, an animal supposedly without agency, exercises a form of choice: he reaches out his head toward Jerry, wordlessly indicating his preference. Jerry responds with a gentleness that distinguishes him from every other buyer at the fair. The transaction is a meeting of mutual recognition — the kind cabman and the willing horse, each seeing something trustworthy in the other.

Economically, the scene is equally significant. Jerry's £24.10 is a serious investment — not an impulse purchase but a calculated bet that a horse with "quality" could still work, broken knees notwithstanding. His willingness to pay for quality, combined with his refusal to exploit his horse through overwork, models an ethical capitalism that the novel holds up as an ideal: profit earned without cruelty.

The contrast between Jerry's fresh, hay-scented presence and the beer-and-tobacco reek of other cabmen is a sensory detail that carries moral weight. It signals sobriety, cleanliness, and self-respect — qualities that will directly save Jerry's life when he falls ill in a later chapter, and that make him the novel's most fully realized example of a good master.
