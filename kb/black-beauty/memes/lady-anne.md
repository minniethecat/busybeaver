# Lady Anne

Lady Anne is a young aristocratic woman who rides at [[Earlshall Park]], where she is known for her skill on horseback and her kind treatment of horses. Her riding accident on the nervous mare [[Lizzie]] becomes one of the most dramatic episodes in Black Beauty.

## 角色概述

Lady Anne is described as an accomplished rider who "preferred riding on horseback" (026-002) to the carriage. She is notably kind to the horses: the groom later declares, "I'd risk my neck for the Lady Anne; she was uncommon good to my wife and children" (026-022). Her status as an aristocrat does not make her distant — she is genuinely liked by the household staff.

## 故事经历

### 在 Earlshall 的时光

Lady Anne frequently rides at Earlshall, choosing between [[Ginger]] and Lizzie for her outings. She "ordered the side-saddle to be put on Lizzie" (026-003) after hearing others praise the mare, and despite her cousin's warnings, insists on trying the horse herself.

### 骑行事故

Lady Anne decides to ride Lizzie despite being told the mare is nervous. She rides out with a companion, and when they stop to look at a view, "Lizzie was standing quietly by the side" (026-013). But something startles the mare, who bolts suddenly — "It was so sudden that Lady Anne was nearly unseated" (026-013). Lizzie gallops off with Lady Anne struggling to maintain control, ultimately throwing her. Lady Anne is found lying injured on the ground.

### 事故后

After the accident, Lady Anne shows no resentment toward the horse. She is carried back to the hall, and her first concern is for others. The groom's devotion to her — he declares he would "risk his neck" for her — demonstrates how her kindness has earned lasting loyalty.

## 性格特征

- **Fearless** — willing to ride a horse others warn her about
- **Kind** — treats servants and horses with genuine warmth
- **Dignified** — bears her accident without self-pity or blame
- **Competent rider** — skilled enough to nearly control a bolting horse

## 主题意义

Lady Anne's accident illustrates Sewell's argument that even the best-intentioned riders can be endangered by nervous or poorly trained horses — the responsibility lies with those who handle horses, not with the animals. Her kindness toward grooms and horses alike demonstrates that aristocratic status need not entail cruelty, contrasting with other wealthy characters in the novel who treat horses as disposable property.
