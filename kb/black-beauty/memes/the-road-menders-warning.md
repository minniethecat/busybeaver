# The Road-Mender's Warning

During [[Lady Anne]]'s near-fatal riding accident on [[Lizzie]], an anonymous road-mender standing by a heap of stones became the critical link in the rescue chain — pointing [[Colonel Blantyre]] and [[Black Beauty]] toward the common where Lady Anne lay unconscious. (026-014)

## 概述

Lady Anne insisted on riding the nervous mare Lizzie despite Colonel Blantyre's warnings. When a bolting colt startled Lizzie into a headlong gallop, Lady Anne was carried across rough heathland at breakneck speed. Blantyre and Beauty gave chase, but lost sight of her at a fork in the road. An old road-mender, seeing the emergency, dropped his shovel and signaled the direction — "To the common, sir" — allowing the pursuit to continue. Though Lady Anne was thrown when Lizzie stumbled at a dike, the road-mender's quick thinking prevented a far longer and potentially fatal delay in reaching her. (026-014)

## 背景

In early spring, Lord W—— and part of his family went to London, taking coachman [[York]] with them. [[Lady Harriet]], an invalid, remained at the hall and never went out in the carriage. Lady Anne preferred riding on horseback with her brother or cousins, and she chose Beauty — whom she named "Black Auster" — as her horse. (026-001)

[[Colonel Blantyre]], a gentleman staying at the hall, always rode Lizzie, a bright bay mare almost thoroughbred. [[Ginger]] had warned Beauty that Lizzie was "rather nervous." (026-002)

## 事件经过

**The Fateful Decision**

One day Lady Anne ordered the side-saddle put on Lizzie and the other saddle on Beauty, intending to swap horses. Colonel Blantyre was deeply uneasy:

> "Do let me advise you not to mount her. She is a charming creature, but she is too nervous for a lady. I assure you, she is not perfectly safe; let me beg you to have the saddles changed." (026-006)

Lady Anne laughed off his concern, insisting she had been a horsewoman since childhood and had followed the hounds many times. Blantyre had no choice but to help her mount. (026-007)

**The Bolt**

The pair rode to Dr. Ashley's house to deliver a message for Lady Harriet. Blantyre dismounted at the gate, hung Beauty's rein on an iron spike, and went inside. Lady Anne waited on Lizzie with a loose rein, humming a little song. (026-010)

Then disaster struck. Cart horses and several young colts came trotting out of a meadow in a disorderly manner, a boy cracking a great whip behind them. One colt bolted across the road and blundered up against Lizzie's hind legs. Whether it was the stupid colt or the loud whip crack, Lizzie gave a violent kick and dashed off into a headlong gallop. Lady Anne was nearly unseated but recovered herself. (026-013)

Beauty neighed desperately for help, pawing the ground. Blantyre came running, sprang to the saddle, and the chase began. (026-013)

**The Road-Mender's Gesture**

For about a mile and a half the road ran straight, then bent right and divided. Lady Anne and Lizzie were already out of sight. At the fork, a woman at her garden gate pointed them to the right-hand road. They caught glimpses of the green riding habit ahead, but could not close the distance. (026-014)

Then came the crucial moment:

> An old road-mender was standing near a heap of stones, his shovel dropped and his hands raised. As we came near he made a sign to speak. Blantyre drew the rein a little. "To the common, to the common, sir; she has turned off there." (026-014)

The common was the worst possible terrain for a headlong gallop — uneven ground covered with heather, furze bushes, scrubby thorn-trees, ant-hills, and mole-turns everywhere. (026-014)

**The Rescue**

On the common they caught sight of Lady Anne again — her hat gone, long brown hair streaming behind her, head and body thrown back as if pulling with all her remaining strength that was nearly exhausted. The rough ground had slowed Lizzie, giving them a chance. (026-015)

Blantyre guided Beauty masterfully across the treacherous terrain. They reached a wide dike recently cut, with earth cast up roughly on the other side. Lizzie took the leap but stumbled among the rough clods and fell. Blantyre urged Beauty on: "Now, Auster, do your best!" With one determined leap, Beauty cleared both dike and bank. (026-017)

> Motionless among the heather, with her face to the earth, lay my poor young mistress. Blantyre kneeled down and called her name: there was no sound. Gently he turned her face upward: it was ghastly white and the eyes were closed. (026-018)

Blantyre sent a turf-cutter on Beauty to fetch the doctor and alert the hall. The man, though no horseman, rode bravely, and Beauty carried him safely. (026-021)

## 后果与影响

Two days after the accident, Blantyre visited Beauty and praised him warmly, telling Lord George that "the horse knew of Annie's danger as well as he did." He declared she ought never to ride any other horse. (026-032)

Lady Anne survived — she was not dead when the doctor arrived, and was soon out of danger and expected to ride again. The accident demonstrated both the danger of nervous horses under inexperienced riders and the critical importance of even the humblest bystanders — a garden-gate woman and an old road-mender — in moments of crisis.

## 主题意义

The road-mender — unnamed, working-class, standing by a heap of stones — embodies Sewell's democratic vision of moral responsibility. In a novel where titled gentlemen and ladies cause accidents through pride and fashion, it is the humble road-mender who provides the decisive information that saves Lady Anne's life. His dropped shovel and raised hands are a quiet rebuke to the hierarchy that Lady Anne's recklessness represents.

The accident also reinforces the novel's persistent warning about matching horse to rider. Lady Anne's insistence on riding Lizzie despite expert advice mirrors other moments in Black Beauty where human vanity overrides equine welfare — and both horse and human pay the price.
