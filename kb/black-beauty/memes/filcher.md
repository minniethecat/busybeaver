## 角色概述

Filcher is a groom engaged by [[Mr. Barry]] to care for [[Black Beauty]] during the period after Beauty's sale from the Gordon household. His brief but impactful appearance in Chapter 32 illustrates a specific danger faced by working horses in the Victorian era: theft and neglect by the very people paid to care for them.

Filcher's name is suggestive — Sewell chose it deliberately to signal his dishonest character. He represents a type of groom who exploits both horse and master, stealing the horse's food for personal profit while the animal goes hungry.

## 故事经历

Mr. Barry, a gentleman who had previously kept his horses at a livery stable, decided to bring Beauty home and engage a private groom:

> (032-001)He brought me home to his own premises, and engaged a man named Filcher as groom. My master knew very little about horses, or their care, and trusted entirely to the groom.

This setup — an inexperienced owner relying completely on a groom — created the conditions for Filcher's exploitation. Mr. Barry's ignorance of horse care made him easy to deceive.

The theft is discovered through a chance observation:

> (032-014)Filcher was cleaning my feet at the time, and when the policeman laid his hand on his shoulder, he started up and began to excuse himself.

A policeman had been watching the stable and observed Filcher carrying away a bag of Beauty's oats each evening. Filcher was caught in the act and taken into custody. The investigation revealed he had been systematically stealing Beauty's feed — a serious matter that directly affected the horse's health and condition.

Mr. Barry, shocked by the betrayal, resolved to be more careful in selecting his next groom. However, his subsequent hire would also prove problematic, showing that owner ignorance — not just groom dishonesty — was the root cause.

## 与其他角色的关系

- **[[Mr. Barry]]** — Filcher's employer. Mr. Barry's ignorance of horse care made him an easy target. The episode taught Mr. Barry a painful lesson about the importance of personal oversight.
- **[[Black Beauty]]** — The direct victim of Filcher's theft. Beauty was being underfed while Filcher profited from selling the stolen oats.
- **The policeman** — The observant officer who caught Filcher in the act, representing the law's role in protecting animals from abuse.

## 主题意义

Filcher's story contributes to several of *Black Beauty*'s central themes:

1. **Owner responsibility** — Sewell argues that owners cannot simply delegate care to hired help; they must know enough about horses to supervise properly.
2. **The vulnerability of horses** — A horse cannot report abuse or theft; it depends entirely on its owner's vigilance.
3. **Class and trust** — The episode complicates Sewell's generally pro-working-class stance by showing that working-class grooms could also be sources of equine suffering.
4. **Systemic vs. individual failings** — While Filcher was individually dishonest, Mr. Barry's ignorance created the opportunity. The system of hiring grooms without oversight was the deeper problem.
