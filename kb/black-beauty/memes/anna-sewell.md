# Anna Sewell

The English novelist (1820–1878) who wrote *Black Beauty*, her only published work. She composed the novel between 1871 and 1877 while confined to her home by illness, and died just five months after its publication. Her name appears on the title pages of all four parts of the novel. (001-001)(023-001)(034-001)(049-001)

## 角色概述

Anna Sewell was born in Great Yarmouth, England, to a Quaker family. An ankle injury in childhood left her severely disabled for much of her life, and she relied heavily on horse-drawn carriages for mobility. This intimate dependence on horses shaped her empathy for their treatment and directly inspired the animal welfare message of her novel.

## 创作背景

Sewell began writing *Black Beauty* in 1871, dictating passages to her mother from her sickbed. By 1876, her health had deteriorated so severely that she could barely leave her room. She continued working on the manuscript until 1877, the year of its publication.

The novel was originally intended not as a children's book but as a manual for working horse owners — grooms, stablemen, and cab drivers — to encourage kinder treatment of horses. Its subtitle was "The Autobiography of a Horse, Translated from the Original Equine."

## 作品影响

*Black Beauty* became one of the best-selling novels of all time, with over 50 million copies sold worldwide. The book's vivid depictions of cruel practices — the bearing rein, tail docking, blinkers — contributed to real-world reforms in animal welfare, including the abolition of the bearing rein in England.

Sewell died on 25 April 1878, just five months after her book was published. She lived long enough to see its initial success but not its lasting legacy.

## 性格特征

- **Empathetic**: Her own physical suffering gave her a profound connection to the suffering of working horses.
- **Determined**: She completed a 50,000-word novel while bedridden and in constant pain.
- **Reformer**: She wrote not for literary fame but to change how horses were treated, targeting working-class readers.

## 主题意义

Anna Sewell's life story is inseparable from the message of her book. Her disability gave her a unique perspective on dependence and vulnerability — themes that run throughout *Black Beauty*. The novel's first-person equine narration was a radical literary device for its time, forcing human readers to see the world through the eyes of a creature they controlled. Sewell's Quaker upbringing, with its emphasis on kindness to all living beings, permeates the moral framework of the story.
