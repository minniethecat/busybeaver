# Mr. Bushby

A farmer living near [[Birtwick Park]], whose young son Bill was caught whipping and kicking their pony by [[John Manly]]. Unlike some parents who might have defended their child, Bushby responded with resolve, declaring that his son "wants a lesson" and thanking John for reporting the incident.

## 角色概述

Farmer Bushby was a neighbor of [[Squire Gordon]], living on a farm along the route that John Manly and [[Black Beauty]] sometimes took when returning from business. (014-003) His brief appearance in the novel shows a working farmer caught between parental concern and a willingness to discipline his son for cruelty — a miniature moral drama that Sewell uses to illustrate the importance of raising children to be kind to animals.

## 故事经历

### 事发

One day, John Manly and Black Beauty were returning along a straight road when they saw a boy trying to force a pony to leap over a gate. When the pony refused, the boy whipped it, kicked it, "knocked him about the head," and tried again. The pony eventually "put down his head and threw up his heels, and sent the boy neatly over into a broad quickset hedge." (014-001) John laughed and refused to help the boy out of the thorns, saying "I think you are quite in the right place, and maybe a little scratching will teach you not to leap a pony over a gate that is too high for him." (014-003)

John decided to go home by Farmer Bushby's farm so that if anyone asked, he could tell them exactly what happened. (014-003)

### 父亲的反应

When John and Beauty arrived at the Bushby farm, the farmer was hurrying out into the road, and his wife stood at the gate "looking very frightened." The pony had come home without its rider. "Have you seen my boy?" Bushby asked anxiously. (014-004)

John told him plainly: his son had been "whipping, and kicking, and knocking that good little pony about shamefully." He described how the pony had behaved well and shown "no vice," but finally threw the boy into a thorn hedge. John added pointedly: "I love horses, and it riles me to see them badly used; it is a bad plan to aggravate an animal till he uses his heels; the first time is not always the last." (014-007)

While Mrs. Bushby wanted to run to her son, the farmer stopped her. "You had better go into the house, wife," he said. "Bill wants a lesson about this, and I must see that he gets it; this is not the first time, nor the second, that he has ill-used that pony, and I shall stop it." (014-009) He thanked John Manly for the information, and they parted.

### 结局

The novel does not revisit the Bushby household, but the farmer's final words suggest that Bill's pattern of cruelty would be confronted — perhaps for the first time with real consequences. [[James Howard]], who knew Bill from school, later confirmed the boy's character: he had been a bully who "took great airs on himself" and was once caught by the schoolmaster pulling the wings off flies. (014-010)

## 性格特征

- **公正的父亲**：Unlike Mrs. Bushby, who reacted with maternal panic, the farmer recognized that his son needed discipline
- **有原则**：He acknowledged that this was "not the first time, nor the second" and committed to stopping the pattern
- **通情达理**：He thanked John for reporting the incident rather than becoming defensive

## 主题意义

Farmer Bushby's scene is a quietly radical moment in the novel — a parent who, when told that his child has been cruel to an animal, does not make excuses but resolves to act. His response contrasts sharply with the many adults in the novel who enable or ignore cruelty, and his gratitude to John Manly for speaking up models the kind of community accountability that Sewell believed was essential to changing how animals were treated. The backstory from James — that Bill had been pulling wings off flies at school — reinforces one of Sewell's deepest convictions: that cruelty to animals in childhood, if unchecked, becomes cruelty to animals in adulthood.
