## Role in the Story

Dick Towler was a hostler at an inn where [[black-beauty|Black Beauty]] and [[ginger|Ginger]] were stabled during a journey with their master and mistress. Towler was the man responsible for causing the terrible stable fire.

On the night of the fire, the under hostler asked Towler to go up the ladder into the hay loft to put down hay for the horses, but told him to "lay down your pipe" first. (017-021) Towler appears to have ignored this warning.

At first, no one could determine how the fire started, but eventually a witness reported that he had seen Dick Towler go into the stable with a pipe in his mouth, and come out without it, going to the tap for another. When questioned, Towler denied taking the pipe with him into the stable, but "no one believed him." (017-021)

The fire that Towler caused nearly killed Black Beauty and Ginger, who were trapped in the burning stable until James Howard bravely rescued them. The incident reinforced [[john-manly|John Manly]]'s strict rule of never allowing a pipe in the stable — "a rule everywhere" that Black Beauty later reflected upon. (017-021)


> Later on in the evening a traveler's horse was brought in by the second hostler, and while he was cleaning him a young man with a pipe in his mouth lounged into the stable to gossip.
>
> "I say, Towler," said the hostler, "just run up the ladder into the loft and put some hay down into this horse's rack, will you? only lay down your pipe."
>
> "All right," said the other, and went up through the trapdoor; and I heard him step across the floor overhead and put down the hay. James came in to look at us the last thing, and then the door was locked.
>
> (017-001–017-003)

## A Cautionary Figure

Towler is a minor character who appears only in a single paragraph, yet his careless act has outsized consequences. A moment of negligence with a lit pipe in a hay loft endangers two of the novel's central characters and nearly results in their deaths.

His denial when confronted — "no one believed him" — adds a moral dimension to what might otherwise be read as a simple accident. Sewell does not portray Towler as malicious, but as careless and dishonest when caught. The episode functions as a warning about the devastating consequences that can follow from a single moment of inattention.
