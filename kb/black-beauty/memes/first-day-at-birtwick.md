[[Black Beauty]] arrives at [[Birtwick Park]] and meets his new stablemates—the good-natured pony [[Merrylegs]] and the ill-tempered chestnut mare [[Ginger]]—learning the first lessons about his new home. (006-001)

## 概述

At four years old, after being sold by Farmer Grey to Squire Gordon, Black Beauty is brought to Birtwick Park. He is placed in a spacious loose box—the finest accommodation he has ever known—and meets his two new neighbors: Merrylegs, a cheerful fat gray pony, and Ginger, a tall chestnut mare who is immediately hostile because Beauty has displaced her from the best stall. Through his first interactions with both horses, Beauty begins to understand the dynamics of his new home. (006-001) (006-005) (006-012)

## 背景

Beauty had spent his early life at Farmer Grey's farm, raised kindly by a good master. At four years old, Squire Gordon came to examine him and, satisfied with his quality, purchased him. The breaking-in process was thorough but gentle, and Beauty had been well prepared for his new life. His mother had taught him that "there are a great many kinds of men"—good, bad, and foolish—and that a horse must always do his best wherever he finds himself. Beauty left his first home with Farmer Grey's parting words: "Good-by, Darkie; be a good horse, and always do your best." (006-001) (004-002) (004-013)

## 事件经过

### Arrival at Birtwick Park

In early May, a man from Squire Gordon's came and took Beauty to the hall. The park was entered through a large iron gate and a smooth road wound between clumps of old trees. Beauty was led to a roomy stable with four good stalls, and placed in the largest one—a loose box, where a horse was not tied up but left free to move as he liked. It was clean, sweet, and airy, with iron rails at the top through which he could see everything. Beauty had never been in a better box. (006-001) (006-002) (006-003) (006-004)

### Meeting Merrylegs

After eating his corn, Beauty looked around and saw, in the next stall, a little fat gray pony with a thick mane and tail, a pretty head, and a pert nose. Beauty introduced himself, and the pony replied: "My name is Merrylegs. I am very handsome; I carry the young ladies on my back, and sometimes I take our mistress out in the low chair. They think a great deal of me, and so does James." Merrylegs immediately warned Beauty that he hoped the newcomer was good-tempered—"I do not like any one next door who bites." ### Confrontation with Ginger

Just then, a horse's head looked over from the stall beyond—ears laid back, eyes ill-tempered. It was a tall chestnut mare with a long, handsome neck. She glared at Beauty and said: "So it is you who have turned me out of my box; it is a very strange thing for a colt like you to come and turn a lady out of her own home." Beauty politely explained he had nothing to do with the arrangement, and that at four years old he was a grown-up horse who wished to live at peace. Ginger replied skeptically: "Well, we shall see." (006-011) (006-012) (006-013) (006-014)

### Merrylegs Explains

After Ginger went out, Merrylegs explained the situation. Ginger had a bad habit of biting and snapping—which was why she was called Ginger—and when she occupied the loose box, she used to snap so much that the young ladies, Miss Flora and Miss Jessie, were afraid to come into the stable. They used to bring Merrylegs treats, but had stopped visiting after Ginger took the box. Merrylegs hoped they would return now that Beauty—who never bit anything but grass, hay, and corn—was in the loose box. He also offered a wise assessment, at twelve years old: "I can tell you there is not a better place for a horse all round the country than this." (006-015) (006-016) (006-017) (006-018)

## 后果与影响

Beauty's first day establishes the social dynamics of the Birtwick stable that will shape the entire first part of the novel. His polite, peaceable nature immediately contrasts with Ginger's defensive hostility, setting up the relationship that will develop throughout their time together. Merrylegs's explanation of Ginger's background—"she must have been very ill-used before she came here"—plants the seed of understanding that later grows into compassion when Ginger finally tells her full story. The description of Birtwick as the finest place for a horse establishes the baseline against which all Beauty's subsequent homes will be measured.

## 主题意义

This arrival scene introduces the novel's central contrast between nature and nurture. Beauty, raised with kindness, is trusting and gentle; Ginger, who has known only harshness, is defensive and hostile. The loose box—a symbol of considerate horse-keeping—represents the Birtwick philosophy: horses treated well will behave well. Merrylegs's role as the wise elder statesman of the stable establishes a theme that runs throughout the novel: experienced horses are often the best judges of character, both equine and human.
