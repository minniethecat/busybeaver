# Hunting Field

The hunting field was the setting for fox hunting, the premier country sport of Victorian England. Mounted riders followed hounds across fields, hedges, and streams in pursuit of a fox. In *Black Beauty*, the hunt features prominently in the early chapters, serving as both an exciting spectacle and the novel's first encounter with death and the consequences of reckless riding.

## 定义

Fox hunting involved a pack of foxhounds, a huntsman, and a field of mounted followers. The hunt typically met in the morning and could last for hours, covering many miles of countryside. Horses needed to be fit, bold, and sure-footed to handle the demands of jumping hedges, ditches, and fences at speed. The sport was central to the social life of the rural gentry.

## 在书中的体现

In an early chapter, Black Beauty witnesses a hunt from his pasture. His mother explains: "They have found a hare, and if they come this way we shall see the hunt." (003-002) Beauty watches the hounds and the terrified hare: "a hare wild with fright rushed by and made for the woods" (003-007). But the scene turns tragic when a horse falls at a fence and a young rider — Squire Gordon's son — is killed. Riders scatter "in all directions to the doctor's, to the farrier's" (003-014), and later "we heard the church-bell tolling" (003-016) as the funeral procession passes.

Beauty's mother reflects on the hunt with characteristic Sewell morality, declining to blame the sport itself but noting that young lives are lost to recklessness (003-012). The incident introduces the novel's theme that sport and fashion can have deadly consequences for both horses and riders.

### 关键场景

**The fatal hunt.** A young man, eager to keep up with the hunt, pushes his horse too hard at a fence. The horse falls, and the rider is killed (003-014, 003-016). The scene serves as a warning about the human cost of recklessness — just as later scenes warn of the equine cost of fashion.

**The hare's perspective.** The hunt is experienced through the eyes of the horses and, implicitly, the hare — a characteristic Sewell move of asking the reader to see the world from the victim's point of view (003-002, 003-007).

> "They have found a hare," said my mother, "and if they come this way we shall see the hunt." And just then a hare wild with fright rushed by and made for the woods. On came the dogs; they burst over the bank, leaped the stream, and came dashing across the field followed by the huntsmen.
>
> (003-002) (003-007)
