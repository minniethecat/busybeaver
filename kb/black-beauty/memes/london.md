# London

London is the setting for the novel's later chapters, where [[Black Beauty]] works as a cab horse under [[Jerry Barker]].

The city represents the harshest test of Sewell's argument about animal welfare — a place where horses are worked to exhaustion, where kindness is rare, and where the system of commercial transport grinds both horses and humans alike.

Yet London is also where Beauty encounters some of his kindest owners, revealing Sewell's core insight: character, not class, determines how a person treats animals.

## 地理描述

Beauty's London is the Victorian metropolis of the 1870s — crowded, noisy, and built around horse-drawn transport.

His first impression captures the city's overwhelming sensory assault: "I had never been used to London, and the noise, the hurry, the crowds of people, astonished and terrified me" (036-012).

The geography of Beauty's London centers on [[London]] Bridge, the cab stands where drivers wait for fares, and the railway stations that generate constant traffic.

The cab stand itself becomes a microcosm of London life: drivers like [[Jerry Barker]] wait alongside drunkards and cheats, and the quality of care a horse receives depends entirely on which driver owns him.

## 在故事中的角色

London functions as the novel's moral testing ground. Away from the protected environment of [[Birtwick Park]], horses are exposed to the full brutality of the commercial horse economy.

The bearing-rein — the novel's central symbol of fashionable cruelty — is described as particularly prevalent in London: "the gag bit in London, that her mouth was not so perfect as mine" (011-002).

Ginger's account of London torture foreshadows her own fate: "those horrid bits that I was tortured with in London" (011-008).

Yet London is not uniformly cruel. [[Jerry Barker]] proves that a cab driver can be both commercially successful and humane.

His rule — treating his horses well and refusing Sunday work — demonstrates that kindness is compatible with city life. The problem is not the city itself but the choices people make within it.

## 重要事件

London is the setting for several of the novel's most powerful scenes.

Beauty's reunion with [[Ginger]] occurs on a London street, where he sees his old friend reduced to a skeleton pulling a cart (Chapter 40, "Poor Ginger").

The moment captures London's brutal logic: the city consumes horses and discards them when they can no longer work.

The election sequence (Chapter 42) shows London's political life from a cab horse's perspective, with drunken passengers and all-night work.

The encounter with the lady who recognizes Beauty's quality despite his broken-down appearance also occurs in London, offering a glimpse of redemption within the urban grind.

## 象征意义

London is the novel's structural counterpoint to [[Birtwick Park]].

Where Birtwick is rural, sheltered, and governed by personal relationships, London is urban, exposed, and governed by commercial transactions.

Sewell does not romanticize the countryside or demonize the city. Good masters exist in both places — [[John Manly]] at Birtwick and [[Jerry Barker]] in London are moral equivalents.

The novel's argument is not that cities are inherently cruel but that systems of commerce, when unchecked by conscience, inevitably become cruel. London merely makes the system's logic more visible.
