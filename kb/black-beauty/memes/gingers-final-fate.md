## Overview

While waiting at a London cab stand, [[Black Beauty]] spots a shabby old cab with a worn-out chestnut mare. To his horror, he recognizes the horse as [[Ginger]] — his former companion from [[Birtwick Park]] and [[Earlshall Park]]. In their brief, sorrowful reunion, Ginger recounts the downward spiral that has brought her to ruin, and Beauty later sees her lifeless body on a cart, a devastating testament to the cruelty of the cab-horse system.

## Background

Black Beauty had last seen Ginger at Earlshall, where both horses suffered under the [[Bearing Rein|bearing rein]] regime imposed by the Countess of W——. After [[A Strike for Liberty|Beauty's rebellion against the bearing rein]], Ginger remained at Earlshall while Beauty was sold on. Ginger's own health had already begun to deteriorate — the strain of the bearing rein had damaged her wind and spirit (024-004).

In the intervening years, Beauty had found a good home with [[Jerry Barker]], working as a London cab horse. Ginger, however, had descended through a succession of ever-worsening owners.

## The Encounter at the Cab Stand

One day, while Beauty's cab waited outside a park where music was playing, an old cab drew up beside them. The horse was a shocking sight:

> "The horse was an old worn-out chestnut, with an ill-kept coat, and bones that showed plainly through it, the knees knuckled over, and the fore-legs were very unsteady." (043-001)

When a stray wisp of hay blew toward the mare and she reached for it, Beauty recognized her:

> "It was Ginger! but how changed! The beautifully arched and glossy neck was now straight, and lank, and fallen in; the clean straight legs and delicate fetlocks were swelled; the joints were grown out of shape with hard work; the face, that was once so full of spirit and life, was now full of suffering." (043-002)

## Ginger's Story

With their drivers occupied, the two horses managed a quiet conversation. Ginger recounted the relentless decline that followed her departure from Earlshall. After a year's rest, she was sold to a gentleman — but the old strain soon returned during a long gallop (043-004). She changed hands repeatedly, "always getting lower down" (043-004), until she ended up with a cab owner who rented out horses:

> "When they found out my weakness they said I was not worth what they gave for me, and that I must go into one of the low cabs, and just be used up; that is what they are doing, whipping and working with never one thought of what I suffer — they paid for me, and must get it out of me, they say." (043-005)

Ginger described a life of unending labor: "all the week round and round, with never a Sunday rest" (043-005). The proud mare who once declared "men are both brutes and blockheads" was now utterly defeated:

> "Ah! I did once, but it's no use; men are strongest, and if they are cruel and have no feeling, there is nothing that we can do, but just bear it — bear it on and on to the end. I wish the end was come, I wish I was dead." (043-007)

Ginger called Beauty "the only friend I ever had" (043-008) before her driver roughly backed her out of the line and drove off.

## The Aftermath

Shortly after this meeting, a cart passed the cab stand carrying a dead horse:

> "The head hung out of the cart-tail, the lifeless tongue was slowly dropping with blood; and the sunken eyes! but I can't speak of them, the sight was too dreadful. It was a chestnut horse with a long, thin neck. I saw a white streak down the forehead. I believe it was Ginger; I hoped it was, for then her troubles would be over." (043-010)

Beauty's hope that the dead horse was Ginger — so that her suffering might end — is one of the novel's most devastating moments. It crystallizes the argument that death can be a mercy compared to the prolonged torment of overwork and neglect.

## Thematic Significance

Ginger's fate represents the logical endpoint of the **use-them-up philosophy** that the novel condemns. Unlike Beauty, who is repeatedly saved by good masters, Ginger encounters no such mercy after leaving Birtwick. The bearing rein damages her wind; each successive sale lowers her value; and the cab-rental system — where drivers must extract maximum work to cover the owner's daily fee — ensures she is worked to death.

The scene also exemplifies Sewell's technique of **emotional juxtaposition**: Beauty's comfortable life with Jerry Barker stands in stark contrast to Ginger's misery, demonstrating that a horse's fate depends entirely on the character of the humans who control it. Ginger's line "I wish I was dead" and Beauty's quiet hope that the corpse is hers transform the novel's animal-welfare argument into an unflinching indictment of systemic cruelty.
