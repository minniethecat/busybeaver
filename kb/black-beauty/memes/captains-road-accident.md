Returning from the railway station with [[Jerry Barker]], the old war horse [[Captain]] is caught in a violent collision when a runaway brewer's dray crashes into their cab. The accident ends Captain's working life and illustrates the brutal dangers faced by London's cab horses.

## 概述

Captain's Road Accident occurs in Chapter 47 ("Hard Times") and serves as a stark illustration of the physical hazards that working horses faced daily on the streets of Victorian London. Captain and Jerry are returning from a fare when a brewery dray — its driver drunk — careers out of control, drawn by powerful horses at full gallop. The collision wrecks Jerry's cab, drags Captain down, and leaves the old horse with injuries from which he can never fully recover for cab work.

## 背景

Captain, a former war horse who served in the Crimean War, has found a dignified retirement pulling Jerry Barker's cab through London. He is described as "a noble old fellow" and "very good company" (047-001). Jerry treats him with the same kindness and consideration he shows all his horses.

The streets of London in this era were chaotic and dangerous. Heavy brewery drays, drawn by the largest and strongest horses, were a particular menace — the drivers were often paid partly in beer and could be intoxicated. There were no traffic lights, no speed limits, and no legal protections for horses injured in the course of work.

## 事件经过

Jerry and Captain had taken passengers to the great railway station over London Bridge. On the return journey, between the bridge and the monument, Jerry spotted a brewer's empty dray coming toward them at terrifying speed: "two huge horses, with the dray behind them, at a furious gallop" (047-002). The driver was drunk and had lost all control.

Jerry tried to pull out of the way, but there was no time and no space on the crowded street. The dray's horses "dashed up against our cab; both the wheels were torn off and the cab was thrown over" (047-003). Captain was dragged down, the shafts splintering around him, and suffered serious injuries.

The aftermath was devastating. Jerry and the farrier "did the best they could to ease his pain and make him comfortable" (047-004). The cab needed extensive repairs, and for several days Jerry earned nothing. The farrier's assessment was grim: "He'll never get over it, at least not for my work" (047-005). Captain might manage light carting, but his days as a cab horse were over.

> He and Jerry had taken a party to the great railway station over London Bridge, and were coming back, somewhere between the bridge and the monument, when Jerry saw a brewer's empty dray coming along, drawn by two powerful horses. The drayman was lashing his horses with his heavy whip; the dray was light, and they started off at a furious rate; the man had no control over them, and the street was full of traffic.
>
> One young girl was knocked down and run over, and the next moment they dashed up against our cab; both the wheels were torn off and the cab was thrown over. Captain was dragged down, the shafts splintered, and one of them ran into his side. Jerry, too, was thrown, but was only bruised; nobody could tell how he escaped; he always said 'twas a miracle. When poor Captain was got up he was found to be very much cut and knocked about. Jerry led him home gently, and a sad sight it was to see the blood soaking into his white coat and dropping from his side and shoulder. The drayman was proved to be very drunk, and was fined, and the brewer had to pay damages to our master; but there was no one to pay damages to poor Captain.
>
> (047-002)

## 后果与影响

Captain's injury is a turning point for Jerry Barker's business. He has already lost one horse and now loses a second through no fault of his own. The financial pressure mounts, contributing to the difficult decisions Jerry faces in later chapters.

For Captain, the accident means leaving the stable and the companions he has grown to love — [[Black Beauty]] among them. Beauty captures the sadness of the moment: "I never thought that he would have to leave his home and go down the hill; but his turn came" (047-001). Captain's fate after the accident is not detailed, but the implication is clear: a working horse too injured to work has few good options.

The accident also strengthens the novel's critique of **public safety failures**. The drunk drayman is not pursued or punished; the brewery faces no consequences. The entire cost — in equine suffering, lost human earnings, and damaged property — is borne by the victims.

## 主题意义

Captain's accident is one of the novel's most direct critiques of **industrialized urban danger**. The runaway dray is the product of a system that values speed and profit over safety — a system in which a brewery can send a drunk driver onto crowded streets with massive, powerful horses and face no accountability.

The event also illustrates the **precarity of working horses' lives**. Captain is a decorated war veteran who has served his country faithfully; in peacetime, he is pulling a cab. One moment of someone else's negligence ends that career, and he is simply replaced. His fate echoes the novel's warning: a horse's value in this society is only as great as its current usefulness.

For Jerry Barker, the accident demonstrates the **interconnected vulnerability** of working people and working animals. When Captain is injured, Jerry cannot earn. Their fates are bound together — a reality that the novel insists must inform how horses are treated.
