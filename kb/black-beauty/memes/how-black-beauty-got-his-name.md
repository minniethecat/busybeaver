## Overview

Upon arriving at [[Birtwick Park]], the young black horse undergoes his first trial under [[John Manly]] and then [[Squire Gordon]]. His exceptional performance — speed, steadiness, and calm in the face of gunfire — impresses both men. That evening, Squire Gordon and his wife select the name "Black Beauty," marking the horse's formal entry into the Gordon household and establishing the identity by which he will be known throughout his life.

## Background

The horse had been purchased from [[Farmer Grey]], who had raised him from a foal and given him his early training (001-005). His mother, Duchess, had instructed him to behave well and always do his best (001-008). Now, in a new stable with unfamiliar companions — [[Ginger]] and [[Merrylegs]] — he was eager to prove himself.

The coachman, John Manly, lived with his wife and child in the coachman's cottage near the stables, and the stable boy [[James Howard]] assisted with the horses (006-001, 006-022).

## John's Trial

The morning after his arrival, John groomed the new horse and was about to put him in his box when the Squire appeared. Business prevented the master from riding, so he directed John to take the horse out:

> "You may as well take him around after breakfast; go by the common and the Highwood, and back by the watermill and the river; that will show his paces." (006-002)

John fitted the horse carefully with a bridle, adjusting the straps to fit comfortably, and found a saddle broad enough for his back (006-003). They went out — first slowly, then a trot, then a canter. On the common, John gave a light touch of the whip, and "we had a splendid gallop" (006-003).

The trial revealed the horse's character as much as his speed:

> "Down at the end of the common we met one of those traveling carts hung all over with baskets, rugs, and such like; you know, sir, many horses will not pass those carts quietly; he just took a good look at it, and then went on as quiet and pleasant as could be. They were shooting rabbits near the Highwood, and a gun went off close by; he pulled up a little and looked, but did not stir a step to right or left." (006-007)

John's assessment was unequivocal: the horse was "as fleet as a deer" with "a fine spirit too," yet guided by "the lightest touch of the rein" (006-007). He concluded that the horse "has not been frightened or ill-used while he was young" (006-007).

## The Master's Ride and the Naming

The following day, Squire Gordon himself rode the horse. Beauty remembered his mother's counsel and his old master's training, and tried "to do exactly what he wanted me to do" (006-009). The Squire proved to be "a very good rider, and thoughtful for his horse too" (006-009).

Upon returning, the Squire found his wife at the hall door. Their conversation turned to the subject of a name:

> "He is exactly what John said," he replied; "a pleasanter creature I never wish to mount. What shall we call him?" (006-011)

Mrs. Gordon suggested "Ebony" because of the horse's black coat. The Squire declined. She proposed "Blackbird," after his uncle's old horse, but the Squire found the new arrival "far handsomer than old Blackbird ever was" (006-015). Then Mrs. Gordon made the decisive observation:

> "Yes," she said, "he is really quite a beauty, and he has such a sweet, good-tempered face, and such a fine, intelligent eye — what do you say to calling him Black Beauty?" (006-016)

The Squire agreed immediately: "If you like it shall be his name" (006-017).

## A Family Connection Revealed

After the naming, John told James in the stable that the master and mistress "had chosen a good, sensible English name for me, that meant something; not like Marengo, or Pegasus, or Abdallah" (006-018). James remarked that if it weren't for the painful association, he would have named the horse "Rob Roy," for "I never saw two horses more alike" (006-018).

John's reply revealed a connection Beauty had not known:

> "That's no wonder," said John; "didn't you know that Farmer Grey's old Duchess was the mother of them both?" (006-019)

Beauty thus learned that Rob Roy — the horse killed in the hunt that had so distressed his mother — was his own brother. "It seems that horses have no relations; at least they never know each other after they are sold" (006-020).

## Thematic Significance

The naming scene establishes several key themes that run through the novel. John's careful fitting of the bridle and saddle, and his observation that the horse "has not been frightened or ill-used while he was young" (006-007), connect **good early training** to **good behavior** — a principle the novel returns to repeatedly.

The Squire and his wife choosing "a good, sensible English name" rather than a grandiose foreign one reflects the Gordon household's values: substance over show, plainness over fashion. This contrasts sharply with the Earlshall obsession with appearance that later torments Beauty and Ginger.

The revelation that Rob Roy was Beauty's brother — and that "horses have no relations" after being sold — introduces the theme of **severed family bonds** that the novel frames as one of the unspoken cruelties of the horse trade.
