# Reuben Smith

A skilled and knowledgeable groom at [[Earlshall Park]] whose fatal weakness for alcohol led to tragedy when he rode [[Black Beauty]] while drunk. (027-001)

## 角色概述

Reuben Smith was left in charge of the stables at Earlshall Park when [[Mr. York]] was away. He was described as a man who "had the care of the stables" and knew his business well — when sober. (027-001) His story serves as one of the novel's most powerful cautionary tales about the dangers of alcohol.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Reuben Smith was a capable and trusted groom, knowledgeable about horses and generally reliable. The master at Earlshall trusted him enough to leave him in charge of the stables, and he was specifically instructed to care for Black Beauty and the young mistress's horse. (027-002)

### 关键转折

The fatal night came when Reuben, having been drinking in town, insisted on riding Black Beauty home despite his intoxicated state. He rode recklessly, and one of Beauty's shoes — loosened by a sharp stone on the road — came off on the rough ground. Reuben, too drunk to notice or control the horse, was thrown when Beauty stumbled. (028-002)

A search party found Reuben lying on the road, having been there for hours in the cold night. He was carried home but the exposure and injuries proved fatal. (028-005) His wife's anguished cries — "Oh Reuben, Reuben!" — echoed through the house as she blamed the drink that had destroyed him. (028-013)

### 结局

Reuben Smith died from his injuries. Black Beauty, who had thrown him while stumbling on the damaged hoof, was blamed by some, though the true cause was Reuben's drunkenness. His death cast a shadow over Earlshall and contributed to the instability that eventually led to Beauty being sold.

## 性格特征

- **Skilled**: A competent groom who knew horses well when sober. (027-001)
- **Weak-willed**: Unable to resist alcohol despite the responsibilities entrusted to him.
- **Tragic**: His death was preventable — a waste of talent and life.

## 主题意义

Reuben Smith's story is the novel's most direct moral warning about alcohol. Unlike the subtle cruelties of fashion (bearing reins, tail docking) that require persuasion to correct, Reuben's drunkenness is presented as an unambiguous evil that destroys a good man and harms innocent animals. His wife's grief (028-013) personalizes the collateral damage of addiction. Sewell, writing in the temperance era, uses Reuben as a cautionary example for the working-class grooms and stablemen who were among her intended readers.
