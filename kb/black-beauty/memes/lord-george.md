## Role in the Story

Lord George was a young nobleman living at [[earlshall-park|Earlshall Park]], the son of the Earl of W——. He was known as a hard, reckless rider who cared little for the welfare of his horses.

When Lady Anne suffered a riding accident, Ginger was sent in haste to fetch Lord George. (026-027) He later took Ginger hunting despite the groom York's warning that she needed a steady hand for her first season. (026-030)

After the hunting season, Lord George's reckless riding left Ginger permanently damaged. Ginger's wind was touched and her back was strained from carrying his heavy weight in a steeplechase. (029-002)

The novel records: "Lord George was young and would take no warning; he was a hard rider, and would hunt whenever he could get the chance, quite careless of his horse." (029-002) Ginger reflected that while Black Beauty was ruined by a drunkard, she was ruined "by a fool."

Despite his faults, Lord George did express concern after Lady Anne's accident. He was told by Blantyre that Black Beauty had sensed Annie's danger and could not be held back during the emergency ride. (026-032)

## The Cost of Reckless Riding

Lord George exemplifies a cruelty different from the overt brutality of men like [[samson|Samson]] — the cruelty of thoughtless privilege. He did not set out to harm Ginger; he simply valued his own sport above the animal's welfare. York warned that Ginger needed a steady hand, but Lord George ignored him. (026-030)

The result was permanent damage: Ginger's wind was touched and her back was strained. (029-002) The narrative distinguishes between ruin by vice and ruin by thoughtlessness. Both destroyed horses, but the latter was more socially acceptable — and therefore more insidious.
