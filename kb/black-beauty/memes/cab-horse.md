# Cab Horse

The cab horse was a working horse employed to pull hackney carriages for hire on the streets of Victorian [[London]].

In *Black Beauty*, the narrator spends his later years as a cab horse under [[Jerry Barker]], providing [[Anna Sewell]] with a platform to expose the harsh conditions faced by London's countless working horses.

The cab horse chapters are the novel's most detailed portrait of urban working-class life — and its most direct argument for the compatibility of commerce and conscience.

## 定义

A cab horse pulled a two-wheeled hansom cab or four-wheeled growler through London's congested streets, typically working long shifts in all weather.

The work demanded strength, stamina, and steady temperament. Cab horses were hired from proprietors who owned fleets, and drivers were under constant pressure to maximize fares — often at the horses' expense.

Sewell's description of cab horse life is grounded and specific: the weight of the cab, the condition of the streets, the rhythm of waiting at the stand and responding to fares, and the toll that years of such work take on a horse's body.

## 在书中的体现

[[Black Beauty]] enters cab work after being sold at a horse fair. A salesman notes his suitability: "if you want him for cab work he's a bargain" (035-009).

The transition is physically and psychologically jarring. Beauty had never experienced urban life before:

> "The first week of my life as a cab horse was very trying. I had never been used to London, and the noise, the hurry, the crowds of horses, carts, and carriages, that seemed to have no end, quite bewildered me." (036-012)

Yet under [[Jerry Barker]]'s care, Beauty adjusts and finds dignity in honest work. Jerry's principle is simple: treat horses well and they will serve well.

The cab stand becomes a microcosm of London life. Alongside Jerry are drivers of varying character — some kind, some drunk, some merely struggling to survive.

Captain, an old war horse who shares Jerry's stable, tells Beauty: "I have often thought that if men knew what we horses go through, they would be more merciful" (037-001).

## 历史背景

By the 1870s, tens of thousands of horses worked on London's streets, pulling cabs, omnibuses, trams, carts, and carriages.

The Hansom cab, introduced in 1834, was the most common type — a light two-wheeled vehicle that a single horse could pull at speed.

Cab horses faced particular hardships: long hours in all weather, heavy loads on slippery cobblestones, and drivers incentivized by the fare system to push their animals beyond safe limits.

Sewell's portrayal of cab horse life — its grinding routines, its rare kindnesses, its brutal economics — contributed to public concern about urban horse welfare.

The novel appeared at a pivotal moment. Within decades of its publication, motor vehicles would begin replacing horse-drawn transport, but the welfare principles Sewell advocated outlasted the specific technology she described.
