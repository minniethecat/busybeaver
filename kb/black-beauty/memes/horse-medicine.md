# Horse Medicine

Horse medicine in Victorian England was practiced by a range of practitioners — from formally trained veterinary surgeons to farriers, grooms, and horse-dealers with folk knowledge. In *Black Beauty*, medical treatment appears at critical moments, and the quality of care a horse receives when sick or injured reveals its owner's true character.

## 定义

Victorian horse medicine included the treatment of lameness, respiratory conditions, wounds, colic, and chronic ailments caused by overwork or poor stable management. Trained veterinary surgeons were increasingly common after the founding of the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons in 1844, but many horse owners still relied on farriers (who combined shoeing with basic medical treatment) or on home remedies passed down through generations of grooms.

## 在书中的体现

When Black Beauty falls seriously ill after a hard ride and improper cooling, a veterinarian is summoned. The doctor proves to be "a heavier man than John, and not so good a rider" (019-015), but his medical intervention is critical. John nurses Beauty through a severe fever: "I could not draw my breath without pain" (019-017). "Mr. Bond, the horse-doctor, came every day. One day he bled me" (020-001). Ginger and Merrylegs are moved to another stable "so that I might be quiet, for the fever made me very quick of hearing" (020-002).

The incident illustrates both the strengths and limitations of Victorian horse medicine. Bleeding was a standard treatment despite its questionable efficacy. Yet the attentive nursing — John staying with Beauty through the crisis — reflects the holistic care that Sewell valued most.

### 关键场景

**The fever.** After being ridden hard and then left in a cold stable, Beauty develops a severe fever. The doctor's intervention saves him, but the incident nearly results in his death — a cautionary tale about the importance of proper post-exercise care.

> I do not know how long I was ill. Mr. Bond, the horse-doctor, came every day. One day he bled me; John held a pail for the blood. I felt very faint after it and thought I should die, and I believe they all thought so too.
>
> (020-001)

**Ginger's decline.** Ginger's physical deterioration from harsh treatment, overwork, and the bearing rein is a medical case study in the cumulative effects of cruelty. No doctor treats her, and she dies in the streets — a fate that proper care throughout her life could have prevented.

**Rest as medicine.** John Manly's observation that "kindness is all the physic she wants" (009-011) regarding Ginger expresses the preventive principle that good care is the best medicine.

## 历史背景

The veterinary profession was still establishing itself in the 1870s. The Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons had been founded in 1844, but many horse owners were skeptical of "book-learned" veterinarians and preferred traditional farriers. The RSPCA and similar organizations promoted better veterinary care as part of their animal welfare mission, and Sewell's novel contributed to public awareness of equine health needs.
