# Sunday Rest

Sunday rest was the practice of abstaining from work on the Christian Sabbath — a principle that [[Jerry Barker]], the novel's moral exemplar among working men, observes strictly even at significant financial cost.

Sewell presents Sunday rest as the intersection of religious conviction and [[animal-cruelty|animal welfare]]: a weekly demonstration that rest is a right, not a privilege, and that commercial pressure should not be allowed to consume every hour of a living creature's life.

> "We can't help it, Jack, old boy; we must have our Sundays." (039-010)

## 定义

In Victorian England, Sunday observance was widely practiced by the middle and upper classes but less so among the working poor, who could not afford to lose a day's income.

For cab drivers, Sunday was often the busiest and most profitable day. Churchgoers and pleasure-seekers alike required transport, and a driver willing to work seven days could earn substantially more.

Jerry's decision to take only a six-days' license — legally limiting himself to Monday through Saturday — is therefore a meaningful moral stand. He sacrifices real income for principle.

His wife Polly supports him unconditionally: "I would not have you a seven-days' license for all the money in London" (039-015). The family's unity on this point reinforces the novel's argument that moral decisions are made in community, not in isolation.

## 在书中的体现

The Sunday rest theme is developed most fully in Chapter 39, "The Sunday Cab," but its roots extend throughout the novel.

At [[Birtwick Park]], the horses enjoy "liberty for a few hours" on fine Sundays (007-003) — a gentry version of the same principle that Jerry practices in the city.

Jerry's confrontation with the wealthy Mrs. Briggs is the novel's most extended ethical discussion. She demands that he drive her to church every Sunday, offering steady employment at good pay.

Jerry refuses, explaining: "I have only taken out a six-days' license, and therefore I could not take a fare on a Sunday" (039-003). When she persists, he adds that "the work was too hard for me, and too hard for my horses" (039-006).

A fellow workingman, Truman, articulates the broader right at stake: "If workingmen don't stick to their Sunday, they'll soon have none left; it is every man's right and every beast's right" (039-020).

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell was a devout Quaker, and her religious convictions permeate the novel. But she presents Sunday rest not primarily as a theological requirement but as a practical act of mercy.

The rest day serves multiple purposes in her argument. It gives working animals physical recovery time. It asserts that commercial life has limits. And it demonstrates that conscientious people can thrive economically without exploiting those in their power.

Jerry's willingness to break his rule for a genuine emergency — driving to fetch a doctor when a neighbor's wife falls dangerously ill (040-006) — shows that Sewell valued compassionate flexibility over rigid legalism.

The principle extends beyond horses. When Jerry is eventually forced to give up cab work due to his own failing health, the novel quietly acknowledges that human bodies, too, need the rest that a six-day week provides.

## 历史背景

The Sunday observance movement was active in Victorian England, though it often focused on human Sabbath-keeping rather than animal welfare.

Sewell's innovation was to connect the two: if Sunday rest is good for human souls, it is also good for animal bodies.

The cab trade's seven-day culture was driven by market logic — more days of work meant more fares. Jerry's example challenged that logic by demonstrating that a six-day driver could still support a family while treating his horses better than his seven-day competitors.

This economic argument — that kindness is compatible with commerce — is central to the novel's reform strategy. Sewell did not ask people to stop using horses; she asked them to use horses well.
