# Lady Anne's Accident

A near-tragic riding accident at [[Earlshall Park|Earlshall]] in which Lady Anne, riding the nervous mare Lizzie against advice, is thrown and injured — witnessed by [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]].

## 概述

Lady Anne, a spirited young noblewoman visiting Earlshall, insisted on riding Lizzie — a high-strung mare known to be difficult — despite warnings from the grooms.

While riding on [[Birtwick Common|Birtwick Common]], Lizzie bolted when startled and threw Lady Anne, who was seriously injured (024-012). Beauty and Ginger, who were also being ridden on the common, witnessed the accident; Beauty's rider, [[Governor Grant|Captain Langley]], rode to the rescue.

## 背景

Lady Anne was staying at Earlshall as a guest. She was an accomplished rider but had a taste for challenging horses. Lizzie was a "bright bay mare" with a nervous disposition — "she was a very pretty creature, but she had a temper of her own" (024-003). The Earlshall grooms warned that Lizzie was not safe for a lady to ride on the open common, but Lady Anne overruled them.

## 后果与影响

Lady Anne survived but was badly hurt. The accident reinforced the grooms' warnings about matching horse temperament to rider skill. For Beauty, the episode was a reminder of the dangers horses could pose when mismanaged — and of the trust he himself had earned through his reliable temperament.

## 主题意义

The accident illustrates the danger of ignoring a horse's temperament for the sake of sport or fashion. Lady Anne's insistence on riding a difficult horse — overruling the knowledge of experienced grooms — echoes the novel's broader critique of human arrogance toward animals.

Sewell's treatment of the episode is notably even-handed: Lady Anne is not vilified but presented as brave and well-meaning, if headstrong. The real fault lies in a culture that normalizes risk-taking with animals' lives. Lizzie is not innately bad but made dangerous by human choices — to ignore the grooms, to ride a nervous horse on open ground.
