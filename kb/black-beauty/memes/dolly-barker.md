# Dolly Barker

The young daughter of [[Jerry Barker]], a gentle child whose kindness to the cab horses exemplifies the values of the Barker household in London. (036-001)

## 角色概述

Little Dorothy Barker, called Dolly by everyone, was the daughter of Jerry and [[Polly Barker]]. She was "her mother over again" — a testament to the family's shared warmth and kindness. (036-001) Dolly, along with her mother and brother, made the Barker home a haven for the hard-working cab horses. (036-002)

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Dolly grew up in the modest but loving Barker household. From an early age, she was comfortable around the large cab horses, showing no fear of [[Captain]] or [[Black Beauty]]. She would come into the yard with her mother Polly to see the horses after they were groomed. (036-002)

### 关键转折

Dolly's gentle nature was most evident in her interactions with the horses. Jerry once told her that Captain was "as gentle as your own kitten," drawing a comparison that delighted the child. (035-019) She and Polly would bring small treats and flowers to the stable, brightening the horses' day. (038-003) (040-024)

One cold windy day, Dolly brought her father a basin of hot soup, showing her caring nature even in uncomfortable weather. (041-004) Her small acts of kindness were noticed by the horses, who looked forward to her visits.

### 结局

Dolly's happiness was shattered when her father fell ill and the family faced hardship. She came into the yard "sobbing and crying" — a heartbreaking moment that marked the end of the family's stable life. (045-007) (045-008)

## 性格特征

- **Gentle and kind**: Her sweet nature earned her the affection of both family and horses. (035-019)
- **Brave**: Comfortable around large horses from a very young age. (036-002)
- **Loving daughter**: Brought hot soup to her father on cold days and wept at his illness. (041-004) (045-007)
- **Cheerful**: Brought joy and brightness to the working stable. (038-003)

## 主题意义

Dolly Barker represents the domestic happiness that makes ethical labor bearable. The Barker household, with its loving children and kind parents, is the human equivalent of the ideal stable — a place where care and mutual respect create a refuge from the harshness of the world. Her tears at her father's illness (045-007) mark the novel's emotional pivot from stability to crisis, reminding readers that working-class families were vulnerable to disaster despite their virtue.
