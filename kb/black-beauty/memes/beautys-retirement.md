# Beauty's Retirement

Black Beauty's retirement is the novel's closing episode, in which the aged and broken-down horse is recognized, purchased, and restored to health by a kind farmer and his grandson. It is the novel's quiet, redemptive conclusion — an affirmation that kindness persists even in a world of cruelty.

## 事件经过

Beauty, now old, thin, and worn from years of hard work, stands at yet another sale. He is purchased by Farmer Thoroughgood, who recognizes the quality beneath the neglect: "he might have been anything when he was young; look at his nostrils and his ears, the shape of his neck and shoulder; there's a deal of breeding about that horse" (052-005).

The farmer's grandson Willie becomes Beauty's devoted caretaker. The boy pleads: "Poor old fellow! see, grandpapa, how well he understands kindness. Could not you buy him and make him young again?" (052-006). Under their patient care — good food, rest, gentle exercise — Beauty recovers his health and spirits. His final home is modest but kind, and he ends his days in peace.

In a final stroke of narrative closure, Beauty is reunited with Joe Green — now a grown man — who was the young stable boy at Birtwick. Joe recognizes him and ensures his continued care, bringing the story full circle.

## 影响与意义

Beauty's retirement is not a grand triumph but a quiet affirmation. The novel does not promise that all horses will be saved — Ginger was not — but it insists that rescue remains possible, and that kindness, when offered, can restore what cruelty has broken. Willie's question — "Could not you buy him and make him young again?" — is answered not literally (youth cannot be restored) but morally: kindness cannot undo the past, but it can redeem the present.

## 原文引用

Joe Green, now grown, recognizes his old friend:

> "White star in the forehead, one white foot on the off side, this little knot just in that place;" then looking at the middle of my back—"and, as I am alive, there is that little patch of white hair that John used to call 'Beauty's three-penny bit'. It must be 'Black Beauty'! Why, Beauty! Beauty! do you know me?—little Joe Green, that almost killed you?" And he began patting and patting me as if he was quite overjoyed.
>
> (053-013)

The novel closes on a note of peace and security:

> I have now lived in this happy place a whole year. Joe is the best and kindest of grooms. My work is easy and pleasant, and I feel my strength and spirits all coming back again.
>
> (053-020)

> My ladies have promised that I shall never be sold, and so I have nothing to fear; and here my story ends. My troubles are all over, and I am at home; and often before I am quite awake, I fancy I am still in the orchard at Birtwick, standing with my old friends under the apple-trees.
>
> (053-022)
