# Going for the Doctor

In one of the novel's most dramatic episodes, [[Black Beauty]] gallops eight miles through the night to summon Dr. White for the gravely ill [[Squire Gordon|Mrs. Gordon]], pushing himself to the limit of his endurance — only to be brought near death afterward by the ignorance of a young stable boy.

## 概述

Late one night after [[James Howard]]'s departure from [[Birtwick Park]], the stable bell rang out urgently. Mrs. Gordon had fallen dangerously ill, and [[John Manly]] was tasked with riding Beauty to fetch Dr. White from the town eight miles away (019-001). Beauty ran "as fast as I could lay my feet to the ground," covering the distance at a speed John declared he had "never saw a horse go" (019-005; 019-017). After returning with the doctor, Beauty was left in the care of young [[Joe Green]], who — through inexperience, not malice — failed to put a warm cloth on the exhausted, steaming horse and gave him cold water to drink. Beauty developed a severe lung inflammation and nearly died (019-015; 020-001). His recovery was prolonged and painful, but his heroic service saved Mrs. Gordon's life (019-017).


> "Now, Beauty, do your best," and so I did; I wanted no whip nor spur, and for two miles I galloped as fast as I could lay my feet to the ground; I don't believe that my old grandfather, who won the race at Newmarket, could have gone faster. When we came to the bridge John pulled me up a little and patted my neck. "Well done, Beauty! good old fellow," he said. He would have let me go slower, but my spirit was up, and I was off again as fast as before.
>
> (019-005)

## 背景

James Howard had recently left Birtwick Park to take up a position at [[Clifford Hall]] (015-017). Joe Green, the young son of the lodge gardener, had been hired as his replacement. Joe was willing and kind-hearted but had little experience with horses (018-005). Mrs. Gordon had been unwell for some time; the doctor had been a frequent visitor at the hall (022-001). On the night of the crisis, her condition worsened suddenly, and Squire Gordon dispatched John to summon Dr. White immediately (019-002).

The ride itself was a feat of extraordinary endurance. Beauty ran the eight miles without whip or spur — "I wanted no whip nor spur" (019-005) — and John later testified that "he never saw a horse go so fast in his life" (019-017). The chapter juxtaposes Beauty's willing heroism against Joe Green's devastating mistake, giving John Manly the platform for one of the novel's most memorable moral reflections: "Only ignorance! only ignorance! how can you talk about only ignorance? Don't you know that it is the worst thing in the world, next to wickedness?" (020-008).

## 后果与影响

Beauty's illness lasted an extended period. [[Ginger]] and [[Merrylegs]] were moved to another stable so he could rest undisturbed (020-002). The near-death experience cemented Beauty's bond with John Manly, who nursed him night and day (019-017), and with Squire Gordon, who told him, "My good horse, you saved your mistress' life" (019-017).

The episode also served as Joe Green's moral education. His father Thomas Green appealed to John to speak kindly to the devastated boy (020-004). John's eventual reconciliation with Joe — tempered by his famous discourse on "Only Ignorance" — becomes one of the novel's most-quoted passages on the ethics of animal care and human responsibility (020-008–020-012).

## 主题意义

"Going for the Doctor" is the novel's central dramatization of the horse as willing servant — Beauty runs himself to collapse not from fear of the whip but from his own "spirit" (019-005) and his intuitive understanding of the emergency: "at least I knew as much as this — that John and I must go at the top of our speed, and that it was for the sake of the mistress" (019-017).

The episode's second half pivots to the novel's explicit moral theme: the danger of ignorance. John Manly's speech about Martha Mulwash, Bill Starkey, and the frozen hothouse plants (020-008–020-012) is Anna Sewell's most direct statement of the book's educational mission — that ignorance, while not malicious, can be as destructive as deliberate cruelty. The episode models the book's ideal of informed, empathetic animal care through John's attentive nursing, contrasted with Joe's well-meaning but disastrous mistakes.
