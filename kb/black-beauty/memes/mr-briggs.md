# Mr. Briggs

A gentleman who regularly engaged [[Jerry Barker]]'s cab to take Mrs. Briggs to church on Sunday mornings, and whose negotiations with Jerry over the propriety of Sabbath work illuminate the moral economy of Victorian London cab driving.

## 角色概述

Mr. Briggs was one of [[Jerry Barker]]'s regular clients, a gentleman who had made an arrangement for Jerry to drive Mrs. Briggs to church every Sunday. (039-002) He appears in the narrative primarily as a foil for Jerry's principles regarding Sunday work — Briggs represents the respectable client who neither understands nor particularly cares about the moral cost of his convenience, while Jerry's responses reveal the careful balancing act between earning a living and honoring the Sabbath.

## 故事经历

### 早期经历

Mr. Briggs had an established arrangement with [[Jerry Barker]] to take Mrs. Briggs regularly to church on Sunday mornings. (039-002) The arrangement was a significant piece of Jerry's regular income, and Mrs. Briggs very much preferred Jerry to drive her, considering him a safe and reliable driver. (039-005)

### 关键转折

The conflict with Mr. Briggs arose when Jerry, a devout man who observed the Sabbath, expressed reluctance to continue Sunday work. Briggs, a practical man of business, saw the matter in purely contractual terms. He argued that it was very proper for everyone to go to church, and that cab drivers must be there to take them. (039-007) When Jerry countered that if Sunday working were given up, people would find other ways to get to church, Briggs could not see the logic — to him, the arrangement was simple: a service rendered for payment.

The matter escalated when Mr. Briggs wanted Jerry to start taking Mrs. Briggs on additional weekday trips. (039-014) Jerry's wife Polly suggested that if Mrs. Briggs would pay a sovereign every Sunday morning, it would be worth the sacrifice, but Jerry's conscience would not allow him to put a price on his principles. (039-015) In the end, Jerry held firm: he told Mr. Briggs he could not continue the Sunday arrangement. (039-016)

### 结局

On the Sunday when no order came from Mrs. Briggs, Jerry was free to spend the day with his family. (039-017) The following Monday, however, Mrs. Briggs sent her servant with a message asking Jerry to call — she still wanted his cab, but on weekdays instead. (040-002) The arrangement was restructured to satisfy both parties: Jerry kept a valued client without compromising his Sabbath observance, and Mrs. Briggs continued to have the driver she preferred. After this, Mrs. Briggs wanted Jerry's cab quite as often as before. (040-006)

## 性格特征

- Respectable, practical, and business-minded
- Not cruel or unreasonable, but limited in moral imagination
- Views cab driving as a straightforward commercial transaction without spiritual dimensions
- Capable of compromise once Jerry's principles were clearly stated
- Represents the well-meaning client whose demands nevertheless create moral pressure on working people

## 主题意义

Mr. Briggs functions in the novel as a representative of respectable society's quiet complicity in the exploitation of working people and animals. He is not a villain on the scale of [[Nicholas Skinner]] or the fashionable ladies who demand the bearing rein — he is merely a gentleman who wants things to be convenient. Yet in his assumption that cab drivers ought to be available whenever he needs them, including the Sabbath, he embodies the subtle pressure that the comfortable classes exert on the working poor. Jerry's successful negotiation with Briggs — maintaining both his principles and the client relationship — is one of the novel's small moral victories, showing that integrity and commerce can coexist when both parties are willing to adapt.
