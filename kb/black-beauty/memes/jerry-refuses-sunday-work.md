# Jerry Refuses Sunday Work

When a wealthy customer offers Jerry Barker a standing Sunday engagement, Jerry declines despite the financial risk — sparking a debate among the cab stand drivers about religion, rest, and the rights of working men and their horses. (039-003)

## Overview

One morning Mr. Briggs, a gentleman whose wife was one of Jerry's best customers, approached him with an offer: drive Mrs. Briggs to church every Sunday morning. The Briggs family were "very good customers" — Mrs. Briggs paid fairly, never haggled, and her work was "easy work for the horses; not like tearing along to catch trains." (039-009) But Jerry held only a six-day cab license, deliberately chosen to guarantee a day of rest. When Mr. Briggs offered to make the switch to a seven-day license "worth your while," Jerry refused — and his wife Polly backed him with equal conviction. (039-008)

## Background

Jerry Barker had been a seven-day cabman earlier in his career and knew the toll it exacted. "Year in and year out, not a day's rest, and never a Sunday with my wife and children; and never able to go to a place of worship." (039-005) Five years before these events, he had switched to a six-day license and found "it better all the way round" — he was stronger, his horses lasted longer, and he had actually saved more money. (039-005)

## The Offer and Refusal

Mr. Briggs framed his offer as mutually beneficial: a short distance, only once a day, leaving Jerry the afternoon and evening free. But Jerry's response was unwavering:

> "I can't give up my Sundays, sir, indeed I can't. I read that God made man, and he made horses and all the other beasts, and as soon as He had made them He made a day of rest, and bade that all should rest one day in seven; and I think, sir, He must have known what was good for them." (039-008)

When Jerry consulted Polly, her answer was even firmer: "I say, Jerry, if Mrs. Briggs would give you a sovereign every Sunday morning, I would not have you a seven-days' cabman again." (039-010) She recalled the "horrid times when you hardly had a minute to look at your own children, and we never could go to a place of worship together, or have a happy, quiet day." (039-010)

## The Cab-Stand Debate

After losing the Briggs account, Jerry became the subject of debate among the drivers on the stand. Most called him a fool, but two or three defended his position:

> "If workingmen don't stick to their Sunday," said Truman, "they'll soon have none left; it is every man's right and every beast's right. By God's law we have a day of rest, and by the law of England we have a day of rest; and I say we ought to hold to the rights these laws give us and keep them for our children." (039-020)

When another driver, Larry, dismissed religion as hypocrisy, Jerry delivered the novel's most direct statement of faith:

> "Real religion is the best and truest thing in the world, and the only thing that can make a man really happy or make the world we live in any better." (039-021)

The debate also exposed a structural injustice: churchgoers themselves created the demand for Sunday cab work. "If it was not for the church and chapel-goers it would be hardly worth while our coming out on a Sunday," Jones observed bitterly. (039-023) Jerry's response placed responsibility on both sides: "If you Sunday drivers would all strike for a day of rest the thing would be done," and as for the churchgoers, "If a thing is right it can be done, and if it is wrong it can be done without; and a good man will find a way." (039-025)

## Consequences and Impact

Three weeks passed with no order from Mrs. Briggs, and Jerry felt the financial loss keenly. "The work was harder for horse and man" relying solely on stand jobs. (039-016) Yet Polly cheered him with her rhyme: "Do your best, And leave the rest, 'Twill all come right Some day or night." (039-017) Jerry's stand was vindicated in the long run — his horses remained healthy, his family life intact, and his character respected by the better sort of men on the stand.

## Thematic Significance

This episode is the novel's most concentrated expression of its religious and social philosophy. Jerry's refusal operates on three levels: as personal piety (obedience to the Fourth Commandment), as labor advocacy (the right to a day of rest for working men), and as animal welfare (horses too deserve rest). Sewell weaves these threads together through Jerry's simple, unapologetic testimony. The cab-stand debate that follows transforms a private decision into a public argument, giving voice to skeptics whose objections — that religious people create the demand for Sunday labor — the novel takes seriously rather than dismissing.

The episode also reinforces the economic argument for ethical treatment: Jerry's six-day system left him healthier, his horses fresher, and his savings larger — a practical demonstration that virtue and prosperity need not conflict.
