# Mr. Sawyer

A builder by trade who became the target of one of [[Squire Gordon]]'s most forceful public rebukes for his violent and "unmanly" treatment of a small pony. His brief appearance in the novel illustrates the casual cruelty that [[Anna Sewell]] sought to expose among those who worked with horses daily.

## 角色概述

Mr. Sawyer was a builder who had business at [[Birtwick Park]] and was known to the household. (012-005) His single scene in the novel — driving a "beautiful little bay pony" in a light chaise — becomes a set-piece demonstration of how ordinary working men could inflict terrible suffering on horses through temper and thoughtlessness.

## 故事经历

### 暴力的一幕

One morning, as Squire Gordon was riding [[Black Beauty]] home toward Birtwick Park, they saw a man driving a light pony chaise with "a beautiful little bay pony, with slender legs and a high-bred sensitive head and face." (012-003) As the pony approached the park gates, it naturally turned toward them — recognizing the familiar route. The driver, without a word of warning, "wrenched the creature's head round with such a force and suddenness that he nearly threw it on its haunches." When the pony tried to go on, the man began to "lash it furiously," holding it back "with force almost enough to break its jaw, while the whip still cut into him." (012-003)

Squire Gordon spurred Black Beauty forward and confronted the man directly. "Sawyer," he cried in a stern voice, "is that pony made of flesh and blood?" (012-004) Sawyer replied angrily that the pony was "too fond of his own will" — a common excuse used to justify horse-beating. (012-005)

### 公开谴责

Squire Gordon's response was devastating in its clarity. He pointed out that the pony's turn toward the park gates only showed "the creature's memory and intelligence" — it knew the route it had often traveled. (012-008) Then he delivered his judgment: "I must say, Mr. Sawyer, that a more unmanly, brutal treatment of a little pony it was never my painful lot to witness, and by giving way to such passion you injure your own character as much, nay more, than you injure your horse." (012-008)

Gordon's final words linked the act to eternal judgment: "Remember, we shall all have to be judged according to our works, whether they be toward man or toward beast." (012-008)

### 结局

The novel does not record Sawyer's response or whether he changed his behavior. Like [[Captain Langley]], his narrative function is not as a developing character but as a representative type: the ordinary man who has never reflected on the suffering he inflicts.

## 性格特征

- **暴躁易怒**：He reacted to the pony's entirely reasonable turn with explosive violence, "as if he was in a strong passion" (012-005)
- **缺乏自省**：His immediate defense — blaming the pony's "temper" — reveals a refusal to examine his own behavior
- **代表型人物**：He personifies the casual, thoughtless cruelty that Sewell believed was endemic among those who handled working animals

## 主题意义

Mr. Sawyer's brief appearance encapsulates one of the novel's most urgent arguments: that cruelty to animals was not solely a vice of the wealthy or the fashionable. Ordinary working men — builders, carters, tradesmen — could be just as brutal, and their violence was often excused as necessary discipline. Squire Gordon's public rebuke models the active, vocal opposition that Sewell believed every decent person owed to suffering animals. His framing of the issue in moral and even religious terms ("we shall all have to be judged") reflects Sewell's own devout Quaker beliefs.
