# Reuben Smith's Downfall

A trusted groom's lapse into drink leads to his own death, [[Black Beauty]]'s permanent knee injury, and a decisive turn for the worse in Beauty's fortunes — one of the novel's starkest cautionary tales about the cost of human weakness on animals.

## 概述

[[Reuben Smith]] was the best groom at [[Earlshall Park|Earlshall]] — knowledgeable, gentle, and trusted by [[Mr. York]] to drive the carriage in his absence (025-001). But Smith had one fatal flaw: he drank. After a long period of sobriety, Smith took Beauty to [[The White Lion Inn|the White Lion Inn]] on an errand and — left alone with access to drink — got drunk. Despite the warnings of the innkeeper, he insisted on riding home. He spurred Beauty into a reckless gallop; a loose shoe caught and tore off, throwing Beauty to his knees and Smith headfirst onto the road (025-012). Smith died of his injuries; Beauty's knees were permanently scarred — a blemish that would mark him as damaged goods for the rest of his working life.

## 背景

Reuben Smith was a skilled horseman who had been in Mr. York's service for years. He was "a first-rate driver" and "could doctor a horse almost as well as a farrier" (025-001). His only weakness was drink — he had been dismissed from a previous position for it. Mr. York had given him a second chance on the condition of total abstinence, and Smith had kept sober for a considerable period. On the day of the accident, Mr. York was away, and Smith was sent on a journey to a distant town — a trip that required an overnight stay at an inn where drink was available (025-005).

Beauty's own account of the fall is vivid: "I felt the shoe loosen, and before I could check myself I was down on my knees. The violence of the fall threw him off my back" (025-012). The physical description emphasizes that it is the horse who suffers for the human's mistake — Beauty's knees, already weakened by [[A Strike for Liberty|a-strike-for-liberty]], are now permanently scarred, reducing his market value.

## 后果与影响

Smith's death and Beauty's injury triggered a cascade of consequences:

- **Beauty's devaluation**: Scarred knees made Beauty unsellable as a gentleman's carriage horse. He was sold to a livery stable, beginning his descent into the world of job horses and cab work (027-001).
- **The end of Earlshall**: Beauty left Earlshall Park forever, never to return to even the qualified comfort of Mr. York's care.
- **Mr. York's guilt**: Though not directly responsible, Mr. York had trusted Smith and the weight of the tragedy fell heavily on him (026-005).
- **Ginger's separation**: Beauty's departure from Earlshall separated him from Ginger for the second time, leaving her alone in an increasingly harsh environment.

The accident also functions as a narrative hinge: it is the moment Beauty transitions from a horse with prospects to a horse in decline. Everything before this — Birtwick, Earlshall, even the bearing rein rebellion — belonged to his life as a valued animal. Everything after — livery stables, cab stands, hard labor — is the consequence of this one night's tragedy.

## 主题意义

Reuben Smith's downfall is the novel's most concentrated treatment of the temperance theme — a cause Anna Sewell supported alongside animal welfare. Smith is not a villain; he is a skilled, kind man destroyed by a single weakness. The tragedy is that his weakness is visited not only on himself but on the innocent animal in his charge.

The episode also advances the novel's critique of the horse-as-commodity system: Beauty's permanent injury is evaluated not in terms of his suffering but in terms of his reduced market value. The scarred knees — "blemishes" in the language of the trade — become a stigma that follows him from owner to owner, showing how the Victorian horse economy priced living creatures by their surface perfection.
