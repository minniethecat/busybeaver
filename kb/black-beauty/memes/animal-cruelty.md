# Animal Cruelty

Animal cruelty is the infliction of suffering or harm upon animals. In *Black Beauty*, Anna Sewell uses the first-person narrative of a horse to expose the routine cruelties inflicted on working horses in Victorian England, and to argue that kindness is both a moral duty and the most effective way to manage animals.

## 定义

The concept of animal cruelty in *Black Beauty* encompasses physical abuse (whipping, overworking, neglect), the use of fashionable but painful equipment such as the bearing rein, and the systemic indifference that allows such treatment to continue. Sewell presents cruelty not as isolated acts of malice but as a widespread social problem rooted in ignorance, fashion, and economic exploitation.

The novel's counterpoint to cruelty is kindness — portrayed not merely as the absence of harm but as an active, attentive practice. Throughout the book, characters who treat horses with patience and respect are rewarded with loyalty and good service, while those who use force provoke resistance and suffering.

## 在书中的体现

The narrative traces the consequences of both cruelty and kindness through Black Beauty's successive owners. The horse's early experiences of harsh treatment serve as a baseline against which later kindness is measured: "The first experience I had of men's kindness; it was all force." (008-004)

Ginger, Black Beauty's companion at Birtwick Park, embodies the destructive effects of systematic cruelty. Taken from her mother early, broken harshly, and passed between indifferent owners, she becomes resentful and defensive — a temperament that John Manly diagnoses as treatable only through patience: "Try her with kindness" (009-009). Ginger's eventual decline and death is the novel's starkest warning about the long-term consequences of mistreatment.

The bearing rein — a fashionable device that forced horses to hold their heads in an unnaturally high position — is the novel's central symbol of cruelty disguised as refinement. At Earlshall, Black Beauty and Ginger suffer its effects daily: "Day by day, hole by hole, our bearing reins were shortened, and instead of looking forward with pleasure to having my harness put on, I began to dread it." (024-020)

### 关键场景

**Ginger's story.** Ginger recounts her life to Black Beauty, describing how she was taken from her mother, broken by force, and passed through a succession of owners who valued appearance over welfare (009-005). Her narrative establishes the novel's thesis: cruelty breeds resistance, while kindness earns trust.

**The Birtwick model.** At Birtwick Park, Squire Gordon and John Manly demonstrate that gentle treatment produces willing, capable horses. John's philosophy is explicit: "Kindness is all the physic she wants" (009-011). This household serves as Sewell's ideal of humane horse management.

**Joe Green's intervention.** When young Joe Green sees a carter beating his horses, he intervenes despite his own inexperience. John Manly affirms this moral courage: "With cruelty and oppression it is everybody's business to interfere when they see it." (021-014)

**Jerry Barker's principle.** The London cab driver Jerry Barker articulates the doctrine most clearly: "If we see cruelty or wrong that we have the power to stop, and do nothing, we make ourselves sharers in the guilt." (041-017) This transforms kindness from a private virtue into a public responsibility.

**The final decline.** In his later years, Black Beauty — now reduced to a cab horse — endures overwork and neglect: "I did my best, as I always had done, in spite of cruelty and injustice." (051-015) His eventual rescue and retirement with the Thoroughgood family (052-005, 052-006) provides the novel's redemptive conclusion, affirming that kindness can still be found even after great suffering.

## Anna Sewell 的立场

Sewell wrote *Black Beauty* explicitly to promote the humane treatment of horses. Through the device of a horse narrator, she compels the reader to see the world from the victim's perspective, making cruelty viscerally felt rather than abstractly described. Her approach is not merely sentimental: she offers practical arguments that kindness produces better and safer horses, and moral arguments that witnessing cruelty without intervening makes one complicit.

The novel does not present cruelty as the province of villains alone. Even well-intentioned owners — motivated by fashion, economy, or simple ignorance — cause suffering. This broadening of responsibility was radical for its time and contributed directly to the animal welfare reforms that followed the book's publication.

## 历史背景

*Black Beauty* was published in 1877, at the height of the horse-drawn era in Britain. Horses were essential to transport, agriculture, and industry, yet their welfare was poorly protected by law. The bearing rein was fashionable among the wealthy, causing chronic pain and respiratory difficulty. Working horses in cities faced grueling conditions: long hours, heavy loads, poor stabling, and callous treatment.

Sewell's novel had a measurable impact. It influenced the abolition of the bearing rein in fashionable circles and contributed to the growing animal welfare movement. The Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (RSPCA), founded in 1824, gained public support partly through the awareness the book raised.
