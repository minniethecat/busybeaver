# The Wet Stable Incident

While stabled with [[Mr. Barry]] in London, [[Black Beauty]] fell ill after groom [[Alfred Smirk]] poured water into his stall under the pretext of cleaning, leaving the horse standing on moist straw that caused painful thrush and dangerous stumbling. (033-009)

## 概述

After Mr. Barry complained about the stable smell, Alfred Smirk warned that throwing down water would give horses a cold — then did it anyway at his master's insistence, but with deliberate excess. Beauty developed thrush in his feet, began stumbling dangerously on the road, and became alternately heavy and feverish from lack of exercise. When the farrier diagnosed the problem and exposed Smirk's negligence, Mr. Barry — already burned once by a dishonest groom — gave up on horse ownership entirely and sold Beauty. (033-009)

## 背景

Mr. Barry had hired Alfred Smirk as Beauty's groom after being previously deceived by [[Filcher]], who had been stealing Beauty's oats. Smirk presented himself as a respectful, attentive young man, always touching his cap and saying "Yes, sir" at every word. Everyone thought he was a very nice young man and that Mr. Barry was fortunate to have him. (033-001)

In reality, Smirk was the laziest, most conceited fellow Beauty ever encountered. He never cleaned Beauty's feet, looked to his shoes, or groomed him thoroughly. He left the bit rusty, the saddle damp, and the crupper stiff. More seriously, Smirk never properly cleaned out the loose box — leaving old straw to rot underneath, creating strong vapors that made Beauty's eyes smart and inflamed his appetite. (033-002)

## 事件经过

**The Complaint**

One day Mr. Barry entered the stable and noticed the strong smell. He instructed Smirk to give the stall a good scrub and throw down plenty of water. (033-003)

Smirk responded with a show of concern:

> "Well, sir, I'll do so if you please, sir; but it is rather dangerous, sir, throwing down water in a horse's box; they are very apt to take cold, sir. I should not like to do him an injury, but I'll do it if you please, sir." (033-004)

When Mr. Barry, worried about the horse's health, instead suggested the drains might be faulty, Smirk seized on this and a bricklayer was summoned. The bricklayer pulled up many bricks, found nothing wrong, and charged five shillings — but the smell remained as bad as ever. (033-009)

**The Damage**

Smirk's neglect went beyond the stable. He hardly ever exercised Beauty, leaving the horse standing for days without stretching his legs, yet feeding him the same high ration as if he were at hard work. This disordered Beauty's health, making him sometimes heavy and dull, more often restless and feverish. Smirk never gave him green food or a bran mash that would have cooled him. Instead, Beauty had to take horse balls and draughts — unpleasant medicines poured down his throat that made him feel ill. (033-012)

The moist straw did its damage:

> Standing as I did on a quantity of moist straw my feet grew unhealthy and tender, and the master used to say: "I don't know what is the matter with this horse; he goes very fumble-footed. I am sometimes afraid he will stumble." (033-009)

**The Farrier's Diagnosis**

One day, Beauty's feet became so tender that he stumbled twice seriously while trotting over fresh stones with Mr. Barry on his back. Alarmed, Mr. Barry stopped at a farrier's and asked him to examine the horse. (033-013)

The farrier immediately identified the problem:

> "Your horse has got the 'thrush', and badly, too; his feet are very tender; it is fortunate that he has not been down. I wonder your groom has not seen to it before. This is the sort of thing we find in foul stables, where the litter is never properly cleaned out." (033-014)

The farrier prescribed daily cleaning of the box, removal of all old litter, bran mashes, green food, and reduced corn rations. With this proper treatment, Beauty soon regained his spirits. (033-016)

## 后果与影响

The incident was the final straw for Mr. Barry. Having been deceived twice — first by a thief (Filcher) and then by a lazy, ignorant pretender (Smirk) — he was so disgusted that he determined to give up keeping a horse entirely. He would hire one when needed instead. (033-016)

Beauty was kept until his feet were fully healed and then sold again — beginning the next chapter of his uncertain life as a London cab horse.

The incident also illustrates a broader pattern in Beauty's London experience: grooms who looked good on the surface but neglected the fundamentals of horse care. Both Filcher and Smirk cost Mr. Barry his confidence in horse ownership, and both contributed to Beauty's downward trajectory through successive buyers.

## 主题意义

This incident exemplifies Anna Sewell's recurring critique of appearance versus substance in horse care. Alfred Smirk is a "humbug" — all surface polish (brushing mane and tail with water before showing the horse) with no real knowledge or diligence. The episode also demonstrates how quickly a horse's health can deteriorate under poor stable management, and the cascading consequences of neglect: foul stable → thrush → stumbling → a horse sold on to an uncertain fate.

The story also reflects the vulnerability of first-time horse owners like Mr. Barry, who lacked the experience to distinguish between a genuinely good groom and a smooth-talking pretender. His decision to abandon horse-keeping altogether represents a small but meaningful failure in the human–horse relationship that Sewell sought to strengthen throughout the novel.
