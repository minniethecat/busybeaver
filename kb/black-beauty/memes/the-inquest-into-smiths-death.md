# The Inquest into Smith's Death

After [[Reuben Smith]] was found dead on the road with a fractured skull following a riding accident, a coroner's inquest was held to determine the circumstances of his death—and to establish whether [[Black Beauty]] bore any responsibility. (028-012)

## 概述

Reuben Smith, a groom at [[Earlshall Park]], had been sent to collect a broken fore shoe from the blacksmith and was expected to return within a few hours. Instead, he stopped at the White Lion inn and became intoxicated. Riding barefoot on sharp stones, Beauty fell, throwing Smith and lacerating his own knees and hoof. Smith was found dead hours later. Because the death was sudden and unwitnessed, a formal inquest was convened. Testimony from the inn staff and the toll-gate keeper established Smith's intoxication, and Beauty was exonerated. (028-012)

## 背景

Reuben Smith was a skilled and knowledgeable groom—"a first-rate driver" who "could dress a horse beautifully"—but he had one fatal weakness: drink. (026-003) [[York]], the Earlshall coachman, had entrusted him with the responsibility of taking Beauty to the forge because Smith had been sober for some time and "nobody thought there would be any danger." (026-010)

But Smith did not go directly to the forge. He stopped at the White Lion, where he drank heavily. When he finally set out again, he raced Beauty at a hard gallop along the stony road. One of Beauty's shoes—the fore shoe that had been the very reason for the journey—came loose and was later found among the stones. (028-012)

Riding barefoot on sharp flints, Beauty stumbled and fell, throwing Smith onto the stones. Smith struck his head and died where he lay. Beauty, injured and unable to move far, stood by the body through the night until the search party found them near midnight. (028-001)

## 事件经过

**The Discovery**

Robert and Ned, sent by Smith's anxious wife Susan to search for him, found the body on the road through the Earl's woods. Smith's hair was "soaked with blood" and his hands were cold. (028-003) Beauty stood nearby with cut knees and a shattered hoof, barely able to walk. (028-007)

**The Inquest**

Because Smith's death was sudden and unwitnessed, the law required an inquest—a formal inquiry by a coroner to determine the cause and circumstances of death:

> As Smith's death had been so sudden, and no one was there to see it, there was an inquest held. The landlord and hostler at the White Lion, with several other people, gave evidence that he was intoxicated when he started from the inn. The keeper of the toll-gate said he rode at a hard gallop through the gate; and my shoe was picked up among the stones, so that the case was quite plain to them, and I was cleared of all blame. (028-012)

Three pieces of evidence were decisive:

1. **The White Lion testimony**: The landlord and hostler confirmed Smith had been drinking heavily before departing.
2. **The toll-gate keeper's account**: Smith rode through the gate at a hard gallop, inconsistent with responsible horsemanship on a stony road.
3. **The physical evidence**: Beauty's shoe, picked up among the stones, proved the horse had been ridden unshod over a surface that would inevitably cause injury.

**The Verdict**

The case was straightforward. The evidence established that Smith's intoxication caused him to ride recklessly, resulting in the fall that killed him. Beauty was "cleared of all blame"—an important legal finding, as a horse deemed responsible for a rider's death could face severe consequences. (028-012)

**The Human Cost**

The inquest's finding did little to ease the tragedy for Smith's family:

> Everybody pitied Susan. She was nearly out of her mind; she kept saying over and over again, "Oh! he was so good—so good! It was all that cursed drink; why will they sell that cursed drink?" (028-013)

After Smith was buried, Susan—with no home and no relations—was forced to take her six children into the Union House, the Victorian workhouse for the destitute. (028-013)

## 后果与影响

1. **Beauty's exoneration**: The verdict that cleared Beauty of blame was referenced by York when later recommending Beauty to the Bath livery stable master: "The inquest cleared the horse's character, and your lordship's recommendation, or mine, would be sufficient warrant for him." (029-005) Without this legal clearing, Beauty might have been destroyed or sold into the harshest forms of labor.

2. **Beauty's permanent injury**: Although exonerated, Beauty carried the physical consequences for life. His scarred knees made him unacceptable for the Earl's stables and reduced his market value permanently. (029-004)

3. **Susan Smith's destitution**: The human cost extended far beyond the man who died. Susan's fate—a widow with six children in the workhouse—illustrates the cascading consequences of alcohol abuse in Victorian society, a theme Sewell returns to throughout the novel.

4. **A systemic indictment**: Susan's anguished cry—"why will they sell that cursed drink?"—shifts blame from the individual to the system that enabled and profited from his addiction, echoing the temperance themes that run through the novel.

## 主题意义

The inquest into Reuben Smith's death is one of the novel's most explicit engagements with the relationship between individual moral failing and systemic responsibility. On one level, the inquest establishes a clear chain of causation: Smith drank → Smith rode recklessly → Smith died. The legal process delivers a clean verdict, and Beauty is cleared.

But the novel does not let the matter rest with that verdict. Susan Smith's anguished question—"why will they sell that cursed drink?"—opens a wider inquiry that the coroner's court never addresses. The alcohol trade, the social conditions that drive men to drink, and the lack of safety nets for working-class families are all implicated. The Union House, where Susan and her six children end up, was the Victorian equivalent of a social death sentence.

Beauty's exoneration is also tinged with irony. The legal system clears him of "blame"—as if a horse could be morally culpable for falling on sharp stones while being ridden at a gallop without shoes. Yet the same system regards horses as property, not beings with rights. The inquest serves a human need for narrative closure and accountability, but the justice it delivers is strictly for humans. The horse, cleared of blame, is still sold at a discount because his knees are scarred—punished, in economic terms, for a crime he did not commit.
