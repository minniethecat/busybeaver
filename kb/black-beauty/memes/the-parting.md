# The Parting

The breaking up of [[Squire Gordon]]'s household at [[Birtwick Park]] — and the sale of [[Black Beauty]] and [[Ginger]] — marks the novel's most significant emotional turning point, closing the door on Beauty's years of happiness and beginning his long descent through progressively harsher owners.

## 概述

After Mrs. Gordon's health crisis (see [[Going for the Doctor|going-for-the-doctor]]), the doctor advised that she could no longer endure the cold climate of Birtwick. Squire Gordon made the painful decision to sell the estate and move abroad (022-001). The household was dispersed: servants were let go, furniture was sold, and — most wrenchingly for Beauty — the horses were to be sold at auction. Beauty and Ginger were taken to the horse fair together, an experience Ginger greeted with bitter resignation: "Here we are, ruined in a fair" (021-006).


> We had been in that happy place three years, but sad changes were about to come over us. We heard that our mistress was ill, and the doctor said she must leave the country and go to a warmer climate for the winter.
>
> (022-001)

## 背景

Mrs. Gordon's illness had been worsening. The doctor was a frequent visitor to the hall throughout the preceding weeks (022-001). Squire Gordon's finances — already strained by the failing estate — could not support the move abroad and the maintenance of Birtwick. The parting was therefore both medical and financial in its motivation. For the horses and servants, who had known only the kindness of Birtwick, the news came as a devastating shock.

The emotional heart of the parting is the moment Beauty and Ginger realize their shared life at Birtwick is over. Ginger, who had suffered so much before arriving at Birtwick, told Beauty that "here we are, ruined in a fair" — a phrase that captures the helplessness of horses in a system where their fate is determined entirely by human circumstance (021-006). Their separation at the fair, never to meet again until their tragic final encounter in London years later, is one of the novel's quietest and most painful moments.

## 后果与影响

The parting ended the Birtwick era — the book's moral baseline of good treatment. Everything that follows — Earlshall's bearing reins, Reuben Smith's drunken accident, the livery stables, the cab stand, Ginger's death — unfolds against the memory of what was lost. Beauty's new owner, the Earl of Earlshall, would prove to be a harsher master who insisted on the [[bearing rein]], leading to [[A Strike for Liberty|a-strike-for-liberty]] (023-006).

The parting also separated Beauty from John Manly, Joe Green, Merrylegs, and the entire Birtwick community. John's farewell to Beauty — checking his shoes, giving final instructions to the new owner — is one of the novel's most understatedly moving scenes (022-010).

## 主题意义

"The Parting" establishes the novel's central structural pattern: the loss of a good home. Each subsequent phase of Beauty's life — Earlshall, the livery stable, the cab stand — will be measured against the Birtwick ideal. The parting also foregrounds the economic reality of horse ownership in Victorian England: no matter how deep the bond between horse and owner, horses remained property to be bought, sold, and separated at human convenience.

Ginger's bitter line — "ruined in a fair" — encapsulates the novel's critique of the horse market, where sentient beings are reduced to commodities. That she and Beauty are separated at the fair, their futures determined by different buyers' whims, dramatizes the random cruelty of a system that treats horses as interchangeable.
