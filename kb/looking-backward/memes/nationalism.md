# Nationalism

The political ideology that animated the Great Transition in Bellamy's *Looking Backward*: not nationalism in the 19th-century sense of militaristic patriotism, but the principle that the nation should become a genuine economic community — "a family, a vital union, a common life" (024-003).

## Overview

Bellamy redefines nationalism as the moral and political foundation of the cooperative commonwealth. When Dr. Leete describes the "national party" that carried out the revolutionary reorganization of society, he emphasizes that the name was chosen deliberately: "It probably took that name because its aim was to nationalize the functions of production and distribution" (024-003). This was nationalism not as chauvinism but as the recognition that the nation — rather than private capital or the market — should be the organizing principle of economic life.

## The National Party

The vehicle of the transition was a political movement that transcended class. "For purposes of national scope," Dr. Leete explained, "their basis as merely class organizations was too narrow. It was not till a rearrangement of the industrial and social system on a higher ethical basis, and for the more efficient production of wealth, was recognized as the interest, not of one class, but equally of all classes... that there was any prospect that it would be achieved" (024-003). The labor parties, organized around the interests of workers alone, could never have accomplished the transformation; the National Party succeeded precisely because it appealed to a universal interest.

This was an insight with profound implications: a revolution that benefits only one class invites counter-revolution; a reorganization that demonstrably benefits all classes — even those who must surrender their privileges — can achieve lasting consensus.

## The Nation as Family

Bellamy's nationalism reimagines the nation not as a political abstraction but as a living community. Dr. Leete's description is among the novel's most lyrical passages:

> "Its purpose was to realize the idea of the nation with a grandeur and completeness never before conceived, not as an association of men for certain merely political functions affecting their happiness only remotely and superficially, but as a family, a vital union, a common life, a mighty heaven-touching tree whose leaves are its people, fed from its veins, and feeding it in turn." (024-003)

The metaphor of the tree — each leaf both receiving sustenance from the trunk and contributing to it — captures the reciprocity at the heart of the utopian economic system. Every citizen receives from the nation; every citizen contributes to the nation. The relationship is direct, not mediated by employer, landlord, or market.

## The Most Patriotic of All Parties

In a striking inversion of 19th-century rhetoric, Bellamy presents the National Party as "the most patriotic of all possible parties" because "it sought to justify patriotism and raise it from an instinct to a rational devotion, by making the native land truly a father land, a father who kept the people alive and was not merely an idol for which they were expected to die" (024-003).

The 19th-century nation demanded sacrifice from its citizens — in war, in taxes, in obedience to law — but offered little in return beyond the abstractions of patriotism and glory. The 20th-century nation, by contrast, earned loyalty by providing the material basis for life itself: food, shelter, education, meaningful work. Patriotism was no longer an instinct to be manipulated but a rational appreciation of a community that genuinely served its members.

## From Competition to Cooperation

Nationalism in Bellamy's sense was the ideological framework that made the economic transition intelligible. The consolidation of capital into ever-larger units — the historical process described in the novel's account of 19th-century monopolization — made the nation the natural unit of economic organization. Once the entire capital of the nation was concentrated in a single trust, the only question was whether it would serve private interests or public ones. Nationalism provided the answer: it would serve all.
