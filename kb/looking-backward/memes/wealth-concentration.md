# Wealth Concentration

The extreme disparity of economic resources that defined 19th-century society, where "the many pulled at the rope and the few rode" (001-004) — eliminated in Bellamy's utopia by making every citizen an equal shareholder in the national trust.

## Overview

In the 19th century Bellamy describes, wealth was concentrated in the hands of a small capitalist class while the vast majority lived precariously. "Unequal distribution of wealth, and, still more effectually, unequal opportunities of education and culture, divided society in your day into classes which in many respects regarded each other as distinct races" (014-019). This concentration was not an accident of capitalism but its intended result: the profit motive incentivized accumulation, and inheritance laws allowed fortunes to compound across generations.

## The Consolidation of Capital

Bellamy traces the historical process by which wealth concentrated inexorably. Small capitalists, "with their innumerable petty concerns, had in fact yielded the field to the great aggregations of capital, because they belonged to a day of small things and were totally incompetent to the demands of an age of steam and telegraphs and the gigantic scale of its enterprises" (005-023). The logic was economic and irresistible: larger concentrations of capital were more efficient, and competition ensured that the larger would swallow the smaller. The process culminated in what Dr. Leete calls "the final consolidation of the entire capital of the nation" (005-025) — The Great Trust — at which point the nation itself became the sole capitalist, and the distinction between public and private ownership dissolved.

> The fact that the desperate popular opposition to the consolidation of business in a few powerful hands had no effect to check it proves that there must have been a strong economical reason for it.
> (005-023)

## In Bellamy's Utopia

The utopia dismantled wealth concentration through three structural changes. First, the nation became the sole owner of all productive capital — "the epoch of trusts had ended in The Great Trust" (005-025) — so no individual could own the means of production. Second, every citizen receives an equal annual credit from the national product regardless of their work, eliminating income inequality. Third, inheritance was abolished: since all citizens are maintained equally by the nation, there is no private fortune to pass on. The result is a society where "the equal wealth and equal opportunities of culture which all persons now enjoy have simply made us all members of one class" (014-019). Without concentrated wealth, the social dynamics that divided the 19th century — the servility of the poor, the arrogance of the rich — simply disappear.
