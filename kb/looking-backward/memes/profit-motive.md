# Profit Motive

The principle that individuals and corporations in Bellamy's 19th century produced goods not to meet human needs but to enrich themselves — a system the utopian 20th century replaces with production for the common good under a single national syndicate.

## Overview

In Bellamy's *Looking Backward*, the profit motive was the central organizing principle of 19th-century economic life. Under capitalism, industry and commerce were "conducted by a set of irresponsible corporations and syndicates of private persons at their caprice and for their profit" (005-025). The pursuit of private gain, not public need, determined what was produced, how it was distributed, and who benefited. Bellamy portrays this as a fundamental irrationality — entrusting the community's livelihood to private persons managing it for private profit was "a folly similar in kind, though vastly greater in magnitude, to that of surrendering the functions of political government to kings and nobles to be conducted for their personal glorification" (005-025).

## In Bellamy's Utopia

The utopian solution was to eliminate the profit motive entirely by restructuring the ownership of capital. The nation itself became "the one capitalist in the place of all other capitalists, the sole employer, the final monopoly in which all previous and lesser monopolies were swallowed up, a monopoly in the profits and economies of which all citizens shared" (005-025). Because every citizen is an equal shareholder in the national enterprise, the distinction between private profit and public good collapses. As Dr. Leete explained, "there is absolutely no way in which an official, however ill-disposed, could possibly make any profit for himself or anyone else by a misuse of his power. Let him be as bad an official as you please, he cannot be a corrupt one. There is no motive to be. The social system no longer offers a premium on dishonesty" (006-009). With the profit motive gone, the entire structure of incentives changes: production serves need rather than accumulation, and cooperation replaces competition as the natural mode of economic life.

> The conditions of human life have changed, and with them the motives of human action.
> (006-009)
