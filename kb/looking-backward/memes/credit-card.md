# Credit Card

The mechanism by which every citizen in Bellamy's utopia receives and spends their equal share of the nation's annual product, replacing the entire apparatus of 19th-century money, banking, wages, and private finance with a simple public accounting system.

## Overview

The credit card is the instrument through which the utopian society solves the problem of distribution — a problem that, as Dr. Leete explains, plagued 19th-century capitalism despite its productive capacity. Each year, every citizen receives a credit on the public books "corresponding to his share of the annual product of the nation," and a credit card is issued "with which he procures at the public storehouses, found in every community, whatever he desires whenever he desires it" (009-014). This system eliminates the entire machinery of buying and selling, currency exchange, banking, and debt that dominated economic life in Julian West's era.

## In Bellamy's Utopia

### How the Credit Card Works

The credit card functions as a simple accounting tool rather than a financial instrument. When a citizen makes a purchase at a sample store, the clerk "punched the value of her purchase out of the credit card" (010-027), and a duplicate receipt is given to the purchaser. There is no exchange of money, no bargaining, no credit checks — the transaction is purely administrative, recording what goods each citizen has drawn from the common stock.

The system extends beyond retail purchases. Professional services such as medical care are obtained through the same mechanism: "Their services can be obtained by application at the proper bureau, and their value is pricked off the credit card of the applicant" (011-037). However, for physicians, the system allows patients to choose their preferred doctor rather than being assigned one arbitrarily (011-043).

### International Use

Remarkably, the credit card system operates internationally, at least within the developed world. Dr. Leete explains that "An American credit card is just as good in Europe as American gold used to be, and on precisely the same condition, namely, that it be exchanged into the currency of the country you are traveling in" (013-027). This suggests that the utopian economic system has been adopted broadly enough to enable seamless international exchange.

### Women's Economic Independence

Credit cards are issued to women "just as to the men" (025-014), ensuring full economic independence regardless of marital status. This represents a radical departure from 19th-century norms, where married women often had no independent access to financial resources.

## Contrast with the 19th Century

In Julian West's 19th century, "endless exchanges between individuals were requisite in order that they might supply themselves with what they needed" (009-012). These exchanges required money, banks, credit instruments, and an entire financial infrastructure that employed millions in unproductive labor. The credit card eliminates all of this, reducing the act of distribution to a simple administrative record-keeping function that costs society virtually nothing.

Perhaps most importantly, the credit card is not a reward for labor performed — it is an entitlement of citizenship. Every citizen receives the same credit regardless of their occupation, health, or productivity. This principle of absolute economic equality is the foundation upon which all other utopian institutions rest.

## Notes

Bellamy's credit card remarkably anticipates the 20th-century consumer credit card in name and form, though his version functions as a debit system against a universal basic income rather than a revolving loan. The concept influenced later distributist and social credit movements, though Bellamy's egalitarian intent was often lost in these adaptations.
