# Guild System

## Overview

In Edward Bellamy's utopian vision of the year 2000, the industrial workforce is organized into a system of trade guilds. Each worker, after completing an apprenticeship, becomes a full workman and a member of their trade or guild (007-783). This guild structure provides both the organizational framework for industrial production and a career ladder based on merit.

## Organization and Ranking

The industrial army is structured hierarchically. New recruits spend three years in an "unclassified grade of common laborers," which serves as "a sort of school, and a very strict one, in which the young men are taught habits of obedience, subordination, and devotion to duty" (007-783). After this period, workers select their preferred occupation and enter as apprentices. The length of apprenticeship varies by trade, and "upon the average of his record during apprenticeship the standing given the apprentice among the full workmen depends" (007-783).

Above the rank of full workman, the line of promotion progresses through three grades to officer status, then through lieutenancies to captaincy or foremanship, and superintendent or colonel's rank (012-1185).

## Guild Leadership

Each guild is headed by a general, who is responsible for all operations of the trade and reports to the national administration. The general "holds a splendid position, and one which amply satisfies the ambition of most men" (012-1185). Above the guild generals are the chiefs of ten great departments, and above them the general-in-chief, who is the President of the United States (012-1185).

## Election by Honorary Members

The guild general is chosen not by active workers but by the honorary members — those who have served their full term in the guild and been discharged at age forty-five. Dr. Leete explains: "The nation entrusts to the honorary members of each guild the election of its general, and I venture to claim that no previous form of society could have developed a body of electors so ideally adapted to their office, as regards absolute impartiality, knowledge of the special qualifications and record of candidates, solicitude for the best result, and complete absence of self-interest" (012-1191).

## Guild Clubhouses

All industrial and professional guilds maintain extensive clubhouses for their members, as well as "country, mountain, and seaside houses for sport and rest in vacations" (010-1008). These facilities exemplify the contrast between the simplicity of private home life and the ornate, luxurious quality of public and common life in the twentieth century.


> “You find illustrated here,” said Dr. Leete, when I had expressed my admiration, “what I said to you in our first conversation, when you were looking out over the city, as to the splendor of our public and common life as compared with the simplicity of our private and home life, and the contrast which, in this respect, the twentieth bears to the nineteenth century. To save ourselves useless burdens, we have as little gear about us at home as is consistent with comfort, but the social side of our life is ornate and luxurious beyond anything the world ever knew before. All the industrial and professional guilds have clubhouses as extensive as this, as well as country, mountain, and seaside houses for sport and rest in vacations.”
