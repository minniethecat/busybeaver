# The Music Room

The music room is finished in wood with a polished parquet floor for acoustic purposes (011-011). A telephone-like instrument provides access to live orchestral performances. The room represents cultural democratization — professional music available to every citizen in their own home.

## The Telephonic Music System

Edith Leete leads West into the music room and demonstrates the system. "She made me sit down comfortably, and, crossing the room, so far as I could see, merely touched one or two screws, and at once the room was filled with the music of a grand organ anthem" (011-015). The volume fills the room not from any visible instrument but from the walls themselves — the entire apartment functions as a speaker. The effect astounds West, who has never encountered anything like it.

## Musical Repertoire and Access

The system offers more than organ music. Edith plays a waltz at West's request: "I want to have you listen to this waltz before you ask any questions. I think it is perfectly charming" (011-017). The violins fill the room with an intimacy that suggests live performers. Later, Dr. Leete instructs West on "the adjustment of the musical telephone" in his bedroom (013-001), showing how a screw controls volume and selection. The technology makes professional music a private, on-demand commodity — a radical democratization of cultural access unthinkable in the 19th century.
