# Dr. Pillsbury

The mesmerist and "Professor of Animal Magnetism" whose hypnotic treatment of Julian West results in a 113-year sleep — the accidental mechanism of time travel that launches the entire narrative of *Looking Backward*.

## Character Overview

Dr. Pillsbury is one of the novel's strangest characters: a practitioner of "animal magnetism" (mesmerism or early hypnosis) who treats West for insomnia in the months before his long sleep. West describes him as "a doctor by courtesy only, what was called in those days an 'irregular' or 'quack' doctor" who "called himself a 'Professor of Animal Magnetism'" (002-009). Despite this dismissive characterization, Pillsbury's technique works — perhaps too well, as West's hypnotic trance proves far deeper and longer than intended.

## Background

Pillsbury belongs to the tradition of 19th-century mesmerists and animal magnetists — practitioners who claimed to manipulate invisible "magnetic fluids" in the body to induce healing trances. The movement, pioneered by Franz Mesmer in the late 18th century, had largely been discredited by mainstream medicine by the 1880s, but it retained a popular following. Pillsbury represents this fringe of medical practice — skilled enough to genuinely induce trance states, but operating outside the boundaries of respectable medicine.

## Role in the Narrative

Pillsbury appears in only a few paragraphs, but his role is pivotal: he induces the trance from which West does not awaken for 113 years. The trance is intended as a treatment for West's chronic insomnia — a nightly ritual during which West, "lying down on the bed in my dressing-gown," would surrender himself "to the manipulations of Pillsbury" (002-013). On the night of May 30, 1887, Pillsbury performs his usual treatment and departs. That night, West's house burns, and the secret of the underground sleeping chamber is lost. Pillsbury presumably dies or disappears in the aftermath, never knowing the extraordinary consequence of his final treatment.

## Character as Social Commentary

Pillsbury serves as a reminder of the intellectual fringe from which genuine discoveries sometimes emerge. Hypnosis — which Bellamy himself apparently believed in as a genuine phenomenon — was indeed a contested practice in the 1880s, hovering between medical treatment, stage entertainment, and pseudoscience. By using mesmerism as the mechanism for West's time travel, Bellamy simultaneously exploits contemporary fascination with the technique and signals the novel's willingness to deploy quasi-scientific premises in service of social argument.
