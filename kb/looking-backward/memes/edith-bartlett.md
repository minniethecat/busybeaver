# Edith Bartlett

Julian West's beloved fiancée in 1887, and the emotional anchor of his pre-sleep existence. Though she appears only briefly in the narrative, her presence haunts the novel — first as the object of West's longing for his lost world, and ultimately as the genealogical link that connects his old life to his new one in the utopian future.

## Character Overview

Edith Bartlett is introduced early in the novel as West's intended bride. "She, like myself, rode on the top of the coach. That is to say... her family was wealthy. In that age, when money alone commanded all that was agreeable and refined in life, it was enough for a woman to be rich to have suitors; but Edith Bartlett was beautiful and graceful also" (001-009). This description — affectionate but tinged with West's retrospective awareness of his class privilege — establishes Edith as both a genuine love object and a representative of the 19th-century elite.

## Background

Like West, Edith Bartlett belongs to the upper class of Gilded Age Boston. Her family is wealthy, and she has been raised to occupy the position of a society wife — beautiful, cultivated, and economically dependent. The narrative provides few details of her personal history, but the world she inhabits is clearly delineated: it is a world of fine houses, fashionable costumes, and the unquestioned assumption that the fortunate few are entitled to their privilege.

## Role in the Narrative

Edith Bartlett appears directly in only a few scenes — notably in West's recollection of their engagement and in the locket containing her picture that West wears throughout his long sleep. When West awakens in 2000, the locket becomes a tangible link to his lost world. In a pivotal scene, he shows it to Edith Leete, who recognizes the face as strikingly similar to her own — leading to the revelation that she is the great-granddaughter of Edith Bartlett (020-019).

This genealogical connection serves multiple narrative purposes: it provides a satisfying romantic resolution (West has not betrayed his first love but found her descendant), it symbolizes the continuity between the old world and the new, and it suggests that even in the radical transformation of society, the best of the past — love, beauty, family — endures.

## Character as Social Commentary

Edith Bartlett is less a character than a symbol — of the world West has lost, of the love that transcends the transformation of society, and of the continuity Bellamy wished to emphasize between the 19th-century elite and the utopian future. Unlike many revolutionary visions, Bellamy's utopia does not condemn the wealthy to destruction; it offers them conversion. Through the Edith Bartlett → Edith Leete lineage, Bellamy suggests that the best qualities of the old elite — beauty, grace, refinement — can survive the transition to equality, stripped of the economic privilege that previously accompanied them.
