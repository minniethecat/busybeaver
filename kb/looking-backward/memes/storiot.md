# Storiot

**Storiot** was a twentieth-century historian and author known for chronicling the social and economic conditions of the nineteenth century.

## Historical Role

Storiot wrote an account of the nineteenth-century era that emphasized the "gloom and confusion of men's minds" during the period of industrial capitalism. His portrayal was "generally thought exaggerated" by his twentieth-century contemporaries, who — living in the peace and prosperity of the new order — found it difficult to credit the depth of misery and social dysfunction that characterized the earlier age (005-012).

## Vindication by Julian West

Storiot's historical account received an extraordinary validation when [[julian-west|Julian West]], a nineteenth-century man who had slept through the Great Transition, awakened in the year 2000. After hearing West's firsthand description of the anxieties and despair prevalent in 1887, Dr. Leete declared that West's words "will be regarded as a most valuable vindication of Storiot" (005-012).

West's testimony confirmed that Storiot's supposedly exaggerated picture was, if anything, understated — the nineteenth-century mind had indeed been gripped by a pervasive sense of impending social collapse.


> Dr. Leete had listened to me with close attention and nodded thoughtfully as I finished speaking. “What you have said,” he observed, “will be regarded as a most valuable vindication of Storiot, whose account of your era has been generally thought exaggerated in its picture of the gloom and confusion of men’s minds. That a period of transition like that should be full of excitement and agitation was indeed to be looked for; but seeing how plain was the tendency of the forces in operation, it was difficult to account for the mingled hope and fear with which men awaited the future.”
>
> (005-012)


## The Problem of Historical Distance

Dr. Leete's observation about Storiot touches on a deeper epistemological problem: how can a society that has transcended an earlier age's suffering truly understand it? Dr. Leete notes that the period of transition was "full of excitement and agitation" (005-012), a fact that made it difficult for those born into the calm of the twentieth century to credit the intensity of feeling that accompanied the death of the old order. Storiot's achievement was to resist the natural tendency of his contemporaries to smooth over the past — to insist, against the grain of his own time, that the nineteenth century had been a genuinely desperate era. West's testimony did not so much correct Storiot as confirm what he had been saying all along.

## Legacy

Storiot's work serves as an important bridge between the old and new eras. His willingness to document the harsher truths of the nineteenth century, despite the skepticism of his contemporaries, preserved an accurate historical record that later generations could verify through the unique testimony of Julian West.
