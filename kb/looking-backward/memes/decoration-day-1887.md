# Decoration Day 1887

Decoration Day (now Memorial Day) provides the temporal setting for West's final day in the 19th century. 'It was one of the annual holidays of the nation in the latter third of the nineteenth century, being set apart under the name of Decoration Day' (002-001). West's fiancée Edith Bartlett and her family had departed the city for the holiday, and the dinner with Edith's family that West attended that evening delayed his return home — and his hypnotic treatment — until late that night.

## The Day's Events

The thirtieth of May, 1887, fell on a Monday. "The survivors of the war, escorted by military and civic processions and bands of music, were wont on this occasion to visit the cemeteries and lay wreaths of flowers upon the graves of their fallen comrades" (002-001). The holiday meant that West's household was disrupted — his servant Sawyer had the day off, and his fiancée Edith Bartlett and her family had left the city to observe the holiday elsewhere. West attended a dinner at the Bartlett family home that evening, a social obligation that kept him from his usual early bedtime and delayed the hypnotic treatment that Dr. Pillsbury was to administer. In retrospect, every detail of this day's schedule proved consequential: had it not been a holiday, West would not have needed the hypnotic sleep, and the century-long suspended animation would never have occurred.

## The Temporal Anchor

When West awakens in the year 2000, he initially believes he has merely overslept. "It was Decoration Day that I went to sleep," he insists to Dr. Leete, struggling to comprehend that an entire century has passed (003-022). The incredulity of his claim is underlined by Dr. Leete's observation that "had its theme been yet more incredible, the circumstantiality of this narrative, as well as the impressive manner and personality of the narrator, might have staggered a listener" (003-051). Decoration Day thus serves as both the literal date of West's departure from the 19th century and the narrative device that anchors the reader's acceptance of the novel's central fantastic premise.
