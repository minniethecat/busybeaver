# The National Government

The radically simplified federal government of Bellamy's utopia, transformed from the complex and often corrupt political apparatus of the 19th century into a streamlined administrative body whose primary function is economic management. State governments have been abolished, traditional partisan politics has withered, and the government's legitimacy rests on its efficient administration of the [[industrial-army|national economy]] rather than on electoral competition.

## Transformation of Government

The transformation of American government is one of the most striking elements of Bellamy's vision — and one of the most radical:

| Aspect | 19th Century | 20th Century |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Primary Function | Lawmaking, defense, limited services | Economic administration |
| States | Semi-sovereign with independent powers | Abolished (interfered with central control) |
| Parties | Competitive, partisan | None (economic administration is non-partisan) |
| Presidency | Political leader, commander-in-chief of military | General-in-Chief of Industrial Army |
| Military | Standing army and navy | Disbanded; industrial army replaces |
| Taxation | Complex system of tariffs, excise, property | Single national accounting system |
| Corruption | Endemic (patronage, spoils system) | Nonexistent (no private wealth to seek favors) |

Dr. Leete explains the logic of centralization: state governments were eliminated because they "would have interfered with the control and discipline of the industrial army, which required to be central and uniform" (019-030). Economic efficiency, not political ideology, drove the restructuring of American federalism.

## Structure

### The Presidency

The [[the-president|President]] remains the head of state, but the office has been transformed. Its most important function is now "the headship of the industrial army" (017-012) — the President serves as General-in-Chief, the supreme commander of the nation's economic organization. This transforms the presidency from a political office into an administrative one, with leadership qualifications based on demonstrated economic competence rather than political popularity.

### Congress

Congress continues to exist but its function has shifted dramatically. Rather than passing laws in a competitive partisan environment, Congress functions as an economic planning and oversight body. Its members are "the leading men of the nation in all departments of industry and the liberal professions" (017-009), suggesting a technocratic rather than representative character.

### The Judiciary

The court system remains in place but with a reduced caseload. Dr. Leete notes that "the volume of civil litigation has shrunk to insignificant proportions" (019-025) because the abolition of private property and commerce has eliminated the primary sources of legal conflict — contracts, property disputes, inheritance, and commercial fraud. Criminal law has similarly contracted: with economic motives for crime removed, "the class of criminals in the old sense no longer exists" (019-027).

### Administrative Departments

The government's administrative apparatus manages the [[industrial-army]], the [[credit-card|credit system]], [[vocational-education|education]], public health, infrastructure, and international relations. These departments are staffed by career civil servants promoted through merit, analogous to the Industrial Army's own rank system.

## Functions

The national government's core functions in the utopia:

1. **Economic Planning**: Determining national production targets based on consumption data from the [[credit-card|credit system]]
2. **Labor Management**: Overseeing the [[industrial-army]], including recruitment, training, assignment, promotion, and retirement
3. **Distribution**: Managing the [[credit-card]] system and [[the-central-warehouse|central warehouses]]
4. **Education**: Administering the [[vocational-education|universal education system]] through age 21
5. **Public Health**: Maintaining hospitals, sanitation, and preventive medicine
6. **Infrastructure**: Building and maintaining transportation, communications, and public works
7. **International Relations**: Coordinating with other nations through the [[international-council|International Council]]

The government notably does **not** engage in:
- Military operations (the standing army is disbanded)
- Criminal prosecution (crime has largely vanished)
- Civil litigation management (private property disputes are gone)
- Welfare administration (universal [[credit-card|credit]] replaces means-tested programs)

## The Abolition of States

The elimination of state governments is one of Bellamy's most audacious proposals:

> "The state governments would have interfered with the control and discipline of the industrial army, which required to be central and uniform."
>
> — Dr. Leete, (019-030)

This was not, Bellamy argues, a power grab but an efficiency measure — the same logic that had driven the consolidation of industries into trusts now applied to government itself. The states, with their varying laws and jurisdictions, represented friction in the smooth operation of a nationally integrated economy. Their abolition was thus a natural consequence of economic rationalization, not a political coup.

## International Relations

The national government coordinates with other nations through the [[international-council|International Council]], which manages trade, travel, and mutual recognition of [[credit-card|credit systems]]. Dr. Leete describes a world in which "The great nations of Europe, as well as Australia, Mexico, and parts of South America, are now organized industrially like the United States" (013-023), suggesting that the American model has been widely adopted voluntarily rather than imposed through conquest.

## Notes

Bellamy's vision of government represents a distinctive American strand of utopian thought — technocratic, centralizing, and confident that administration can replace politics. It reflects the Progressive Era's faith in expertise and efficiency, anticipating the administrative state that would develop in the 20th century. Critics have noted the authoritarian potential of a government that manages the entire economy while facing no partisan challenge — a concern Bellamy addresses through the democratic elements of the Industrial Army's officer elections, though whether these are sufficient safeguards is a question the novel leaves unresolved.
