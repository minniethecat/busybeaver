# The President

The chief executive of the utopian United States, who serves simultaneously as the head of state and the general-in-chief of the industrial army — a position that can only be attained by a citizen who has risen through every rank from common laborer to the highest office.

## Character Overview

The President in Bellamy's utopia is not an individual character but an institutional role, described by Dr. Leete in his explanations of the utopian government. Nevertheless, the qualifications for the office — and the career path that leads to it — reveal so much about the values of the new society that the position merits its own examination. The President embodies the utopian principle that leadership should be earned through demonstrated excellence at every level of the system one is to lead.

## Career Path

The path to the presidency is the longest and most demanding career in the utopian society. "The general-in-chief of the industrial army must have passed through all the grades below him, from the common laborers up" (017-015). A candidate rises through the three grades of the rank and file, then through the officer grades, then through the departmental headships, and only then — after a period out of office — becomes eligible for the presidency. "It is rarely that a man passes through all the grades to the headship of the industrial army before he is forty" (017-020).

This system ensures that the President has direct experience of every kind of work the nation performs, from the most menial to the most complex. No one can govern the industrial army who has not served in its lowest ranks. The presidency is thus the ultimate expression of the meritocratic principle: it is open to every citizen, but attainable only by those of exceptional ability and dedication.

## The Dual Role

The President's dual role — head of state and general-in-chief of the industrial army — reflects the centrality of economic organization in the utopian society. "He is the President of the United States," Dr. Leete explains, "or rather the most important function of the presidency is the headship of the industrial army" (017-012). This formulation is revealing: the traditional political functions of the presidency have been subordinated to the economic function, suggesting that in a society where material production has been rationalized, politics itself becomes largely administrative.
