# Labor Strikes

## Overview

Labor strikes were a defining feature of 19th-century industrial society in Edward Bellamy's *Looking Backward*. They represented the breakdown of relations between capital and labor, and served as a symptom of the deeper structural crisis that ultimately led to the Great Transition.

## In Bellamy's Narrative

The strikes directly affect the protagonist Julian West, delaying the completion of his new house and thus postponing his marriage to Edith Bartlett. West describes the strikes as "concerted refusals to work on the part of the bricklayers, masons, carpenters, painters, plumbers, and other trades concerned in house building" (001-011). The personal inconvenience fuels West's animosity toward the working classes: "The particular grievance I had against the working classes at the time of which I write, on account of the effect of their strikes in postponing my wedded bliss, no doubt lent a special animosity to my feeling toward them" (001-017).

## As a Historical Phenomenon

West notes that strikes had become endemic: "Strikes had become so common at that period that people had ceased to inquire into their particular grounds. In one department of industry or another, they had been nearly incessant ever since the great business crisis of 1873" (001-011). The working classes had become "infected with a profound discontent with their condition" and demanded "higher pay, shorter hours, better dwellings, better educational advantages" (001-012).

## Dr. Leete's Analysis

From the perspective of the year 2000, Dr. Leete identifies strikes as the most prominent feature of 19th-century labor troubles (005-014). He explains that strikes and labor organizations were an effect of the concentration of capital: "The organization of labor and the strikes were an effect, merely, of the concentration of capital in greater masses than had ever been known before" (005-020). As capital consolidated into vast corporations, the individual worker "was reduced to insignificance and powerlessness," and "self-defense drove him to union with his fellows" (005-020).

## Role in the Great Transition

The strikes of the 1880s are retrospectively understood as "the first and incoherent phase of the great movement which ended in the establishment of the modern industrial system with all its social consequences" (001-012). The labor movement's ultimate inability to achieve its goals through strikes demonstrated the need for fundamental systemic change rather than incremental reform.

> The reader who observes the dates alluded to will of course recognize in these disturbances of industry the first and incoherent phase of the great movement which ended in the establishment of the modern industrial system with all its social consequences.
> (001-012)
