# Berrian

A literary figure of Bellamy's 20th-century utopia, Berrian is the author of the celebrated romance *Penthesilia* — referenced in the novel as an example of the creative flourishing made possible by the abolition of economic want.

## In the Novel

Berrian is not a character who appears in the narrative but a cultural reference point. His name is invoked to illustrate the literary achievements of the utopian era: freed from the economic anxieties that constrained 19th-century writers, the artists of the 20th century produce work of a quality and humanity unattainable under capitalism. *Penthesilia*, Berrian's romance, stands as a symbol of this cultural renaissance.

The reference serves Bellamy's broader argument that economic reorganization would not merely satisfy material needs but unlock creative potential. When no one must write for the market, literature can aspire to higher aims.

## Penthesilia: The Masterpiece

*Penthesilia* is introduced to Julian West by Edith Leete on his first night in the 20th century. Edith remarks, "Ah, what would I give if I had not already read Berrian's novels" (015-004), before offering him the book with the note that "it is considered his masterpiece, and will at least give you an idea what the stories nowadays are like" (015-031).

West reads through the night and into dawn, finding the experience revelatory:

> I sat up in my room that night reading Penthesilia till it grew gray in the east, and did not lay it down till I had finished it. And yet let no admirer of the great romancer of the twentieth century resent my saying that at the first reading what most impressed me was not so much what was in the book as what was left out of it. The story-writers of my day would have deemed the making of bricks without straw a light task compared with the construction of a romance from which should be excluded all effects drawn from the contrasts of wealth and poverty, education and ignorance, coarseness and refinement, high and low, all motives drawn from social pride and ambition, the desire of being richer or the fear of being poorer, together with sordid anxieties of any sort for one's self or others; a romance in which there should, indeed, be love galore, but love unfretted by artificial barriers created by differences of station or possessions, owning no other law but that of the heart.
>
> (015-032)

What West notices is not what *Penthesilia* contains but what it omits — all the social anxieties and class tensions that 19th-century fiction took as its essential material. The book thus serves as an education in utopian consciousness, showing West what human relationships look like when economic inequality no longer shapes them. As West himself reflects, "Berrian put them together for me in a picture" (015-032).

## Ruth Elton and Later Works

Berrian appears again later in the novel, when West reads another of his romances, *Ruth Elton*. This work turns on "a situation suggested by his last words, concerning the modern view of parental responsibility" (025-031). The narrator contrasts how a 19th-century writer would have treated the same material — by exciting "the morbid sympathy of the reader with the sentimental selfishness of the lovers" — with Berrian's approach, which enforces a higher ethical standard:

> "Over the unborn our power is that of God, and our responsibility like His toward us. As we acquit ourselves toward them, so let Him deal with us."
>
> (025-031)

Through Berrian's fiction, Bellamy demonstrates that the utopian transformation is not merely economic but moral — a reorientation of human relationships toward responsibility, equality, and genuine freedom.

## Key Passage

> The novel references Berrian and his work *Penthesilia* as examples of utopian-era literature — a romance that would have been impossible to produce under the economic pressures of the 19th century.
