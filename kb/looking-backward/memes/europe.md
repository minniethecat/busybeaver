# Europe

The European continent as it appears in Bellamy's *Looking Backward*: a region that follows America's example in transitioning to the cooperative commonwealth, referenced in discussions of international relations.

## In Looking Backward

Europe figures in the novel primarily as the recipient of America's example. Dr. Leete explains that the cooperative system, once established in the United States, was adopted by "the great nations of Europe" and eventually by the whole civilized world. The spread of the system beyond America serves to validate its universality — the utopia is not an American peculiarity but a model applicable to all industrial societies.

## The International Spread

When Julian West asks about the state of Europe, Dr. Leete provides a comprehensive account of the global transformation:

> "The great nations of Europe as well as Australia, Mexico, and parts of South America, are now organized industrially like the United States, which was the pioneer of the evolution. The peaceful relations of these nations are assured by a loose form of federal union of worldwide extent. An international council regulates the mutual intercourse and commerce of the members of the union and their joint policy toward the more backward races, which are gradually being educated up to civilized institutions. Complete autonomy within its own limits is enjoyed by every nation."
>
> (013-011)

The passage is significant for what it implies: the cooperative system is not imposed by conquest or imperial expansion but spreads by example. America was "the pioneer," and Europe followed. The "loose form of federal union" preserves national autonomy while ensuring peaceful relations — a middle path between the war-prone competitive nationalism of the 19th century and a homogenizing world government.

## Travel and Commerce

The 20th-century international system extends to practical conveniences. An American traveling in Europe uses a credit card that is "just as good in Europe as American gold used to be" (013-027). The mechanism is elegantly simple: at the local office of the international council, the traveler exchanges their national credit card for the local equivalent, and the amount is debited against their home nation on the international account. Money — "as superfluous in our foreign as in our internal relations" (013-013) — has been entirely eliminated from international commerce.

This detail underscores the thoroughness of Bellamy's utopian vision: the abolition of money is not merely domestic but global, requiring an international institutional framework to manage what private banking and currency exchange once handled.

## International Context

The novel's references to Europe are sparse and general, reflecting its focus on the domestic transformation of America. European nations are mentioned in aggregate rather than individually discussed, with the implication that their adoption of the cooperative model followed a similar trajectory of industrial consolidation and nationalization. The international order that emerges is one of peaceful cooperation among nations organized on the same principles — a contrast to the competitive nationalism and imperial rivalry that characterized the 19th century.
