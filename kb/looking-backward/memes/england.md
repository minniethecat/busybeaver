# England

England as it appears in Bellamy's *Looking Backward*: among the European nations that adopted the cooperative industrial system following its successful implementation in the United States.

## In Looking Backward

England is referenced in the context of the international spread of the cooperative model. As one of the major industrial powers of the 19th century, England's adoption of the system is particularly significant — it demonstrates that even societies with deeply entrenched class hierarchies and traditions of private capitalism can undergo the transformation to economic cooperation.

## 19th-Century Context

In Dr. Leete's critique of 19th-century militarism, England appears alongside France and Germany as a nation whose government was accustomed "on the slightest international misunderstanding, to seize upon the bodies of citizens and deliver them over by hundreds of thousands to death and mutilation, wasting their treasures the while like water" (006-005). Leete contrasts this with the 20th-century state, whose only function is "to protect every citizen against hunger, cold, and nakedness, and provide for all his physical and mental needs" (006-005).

A 19th-century newspaper from West's own time reinforces the indictment, recording "Great suffering among the unemployed in London. They demand work. Monster demonstration to be made. The authorities uneasy" (028-007). The juxtaposition — imperial power abroad and destitution at home — underscores Bellamy's argument that the competitive system served neither peace nor prosperity.


> “Yes,” replied Dr. Leete, “the great nations of Europe as well as Australia, Mexico, and parts of South America, are now organized industrially like the United States. The peaceable federation of the world is assured by the fact that the international relations of the nations are now entirely controlled by the same supreme tribunal which has charge of our internal affairs.”
