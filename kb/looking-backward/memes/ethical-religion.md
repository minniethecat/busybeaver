# Ethical Religion

The transformed religious culture of Bellamy's utopia: a secular, ethically-oriented civic faith that retains the emotional and communal functions of traditional Christianity while replacing its theological content with the worship of human solidarity and the celebration of economic brotherhood.

## Overview

Religion in Bellamy's utopia has undergone a transformation as profound as the economic system. Traditional Christianity, with its focus on individual salvation and otherworldly rewards, has given way to an ethical religion centered on "the solidarity of the race and the brotherhood of man." As Dr. Leete explains, "The religious practices of the people have naturally changed considerably in a century" (026-008), but the social system "would accommodate them perfectly" — a revealing formulation that suggests the new religion has been shaped to fit the new economic order rather than the reverse.

## In Bellamy's Utopia

### Mr. Barton's Sermon

The fullest exposition of the ethical religion comes through the sermon of Mr. Barton, which Julian West attends with the Leete family. Barton preaches not about sin, redemption, or the afterlife, but about the moral transformation effected by economic equality. He argues that "the present economic arrangement of society, by which the means of life are made as free to all as the air we breathe, has given the true basis of human brotherhood" (026-017). The implication is clear: genuine spiritual fraternity requires material equality as its precondition.

### From Theology to Ethics

The ethical religion retains the forms of Christian worship — sermons, congregations, moral exhortation — but has emptied them of supernatural content. Barton's preaching is essentially secular humanism delivered in a religious idiom. He uses words like "soul" and "spirit," but they refer to ethical consciousness and social solidarity rather than immortal essences. The sermon functions as a civic ritual that reinforces the values on which utopian society depends: cooperation, mutual obligation, and the dignity of labor.

### Religious Freedom

Bellamy is careful to note that the ethical religion is not coercive. "The nation supplies any person or number of persons" with whatever religious facilities they desire (026-008). Traditional denominations can continue to exist, but they have withered naturally as the social conditions that sustained them — poverty, fear, the longing for otherworldly compensation — have been eliminated by economic equality.

## Contrast with the 19th Century

In the 19th century, religion served what Bellamy saw as a compensatory function: it promised in heaven the justice denied on earth. The poor were exhorted to accept their lot with patience, for their reward would come in the afterlife. This made religion, in the eyes of many radicals, an instrument of social control — an "opiate of the people," as Marx had described it four decades before *Looking Backward*.

The ethical religion inverts this relationship. Rather than compensating for injustice, it celebrates the achievement of justice. Rather than reconciling the poor to their poverty, it exhorts the fortunate to recognize their obligations to all. Religion becomes not a consolation for suffering but a celebration of human achievement — a civic faith for a society that has solved the material problems of existence.

## Notes

Bellamy's ethical religion was deeply influential on the Social Gospel movement, which sought to apply Christian ethics to social problems. Figures like Walter Rauschenbusch and Washington Gladden explicitly cited Bellamy in their arguments that Christianity demanded economic justice. At the same time, Bellamy's secularization of religion alarmed orthodox believers, who saw in his ethical religion a reduction of transcendent faith to mere social ethics. The debate anticipated 20th-century tensions between liberal and conservative Christianity over the relative priority of social reform versus personal salvation.
