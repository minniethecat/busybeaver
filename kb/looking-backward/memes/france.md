# France

France as it appears in Bellamy's *Looking Backward*: among the major European nations that followed the United States in reorganizing society on cooperative principles.

## In Looking Backward

France is mentioned alongside England, Germany, and other European powers as having adopted the industrial army model and the cooperative commonwealth after its successful establishment in America. The reference is brief but significant — it positions the utopian transformation as a global phenomenon rather than an American anomaly.

## 19th-Century Context

In the 19th-century newspaper from Julian West's time, France appears in the context of international tension: "The impending war between France and Germany. The French Chambers asked for new military credits to meet Germany's increase of her army. Probability that all Europe will be involved in case of war" (028-007). The contrast with Bellamy's 20th century — where nations trade peacefully through the international council — is deliberate and striking. The newspaper, read by West on the eve of his long sleep, catalogues a civilization defined by military rivalry, labor unrest, and systematic corruption.

## International Economic Relations

In the 20th-century cooperative system, international trade operates without money. When Julian West asks how trade balances are handled — "Supposing we import more from France than we export to her" (013-020) — Dr. Leete explains that the international council examines each nation's books annually and may require settlement of imbalances "lest feelings unfavorable to amity should be engendered" (013-021). The system is designed to prevent the trade rivalries and economic resentments that characterized 19th-century international relations. The council also inspects interchanged commodities to ensure quality, transforming international commerce from a field of competition into one of cooperation.

## From Enemy to Partner

Dr. Leete's rhetorical question — "Are they France, England, Germany, or hunger, cold, and nakedness?" (006-005) — encapsulates the transformation. In the 19th century, France was a potential military enemy, one of the nations against which America armed itself. In the 20th, France is a partner in the international cooperative system, trading goods through the international council without money, tariffs, or the threat of war. The arc from "public enemy" to economic partner is Bellamy's answer to the nationalism of his own day.

> And, in heaven's name, who are the public enemies? Are they France, England, Germany, or hunger, cold, and nakedness?
> (006-005)
