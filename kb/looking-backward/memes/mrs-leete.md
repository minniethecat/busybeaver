# Mrs. Leete

The wife of Dr. Leete and mother of Edith Leete, a gracious maternal figure who, along with her husband, welcomes Julian West into their home and represents the quiet dignity of the older generation of utopian citizens.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Leete is a background presence throughout West's stay with the Leete family — always present at meals, always solicitous of West's comfort, but rarely the center of attention. She embodies the domestic virtues of the utopia: warmth, hospitality, and the easy competence of someone who has never known economic anxiety. Along with Dr. Leete, she models the companionate marriage that economic equality makes possible — a partnership of equals rather than a hierarchy of provider and dependent.

## Background

Mrs. Leete belongs to the generation that experienced the transition to the new order, or at least grew up in its immediate aftermath. She has raised her daughter Edith in a world of complete gender equality, and the contrast between Edith's unselfconscious independence and Mrs. Leete's more traditional maternal role suggests the rapidity of social change across a single generation.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Leete's narrative function is understated but important. She provides the domestic warmth that makes the Leete household feel like a home rather than a lecture hall. Her gentle surprise at West's solo exploration of the city — "Dr. and Mrs. Leete were evidently not a little startled to learn... that I had been all over the city alone that morning" — reveals the protective concern that characterizes utopian social relations (009-001). Her comment that West's stroll "could scarcely have failed to be a very interesting one" reflects the quiet pride that utopian citizens take in their world (009-002).

## Character as Social Commentary

Mrs. Leete represents the transformation of middle-class womanhood under economic equality. In the 19th century, a woman of her station would have been a household manager, supervising servants and organizing domestic life. In the utopia, with domestic labor socialized through communal dining and public services, she is free to pursue her own interests and to participate as an equal partner in the intellectual and social life of her family.
