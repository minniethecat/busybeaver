# Medical System

## Overview

In Edward Bellamy's *Looking Backward*, the medical profession occupies a distinctive position in utopian society. Unlike most workers who belong to the industrial army, physicians are part of the liberal professions and are governed by their own independent board of regents.

## Physicians as a Liberal Profession

Dr. Leete explains that "the members of the liberal professions, the doctors and teachers, as well as the artists and men of letters who obtain remissions of industrial service, do not belong to the industrial army" (012-1205). While they vote for the President, they are not eligible for that office, since "one of its main duties being the control and discipline of the industrial army, it is essential that the President should have passed through all its grades to understand his business" (012-1205).


> “That rule would not work well in the case of physicians,” replied Dr. Leete. “The good a physician can do a patient depends largely on his acquaintance with his constitutional tendencies and condition. The patient must be able, therefore, to call in a particular doctor, and he does so just as patients did in your day. The only difference is that, instead of collecting his fee for himself, the doctor collects it for the nation by pricking off the amount, according to a regular scale for medical attendance, from the patient’s credit card.”
>
> (011-043)


## Governance by Board of Regents

The medical and educational faculties are "controlled by boards of regents of their own, in which the President is ex-officio chairman, and has the casting vote" (012-1209). These regents are "chosen by the honorary members of the guilds of education and medicine, the retired teachers and doctors of the country" (012-1209). This ensures that the governance of medicine remains in the hands of those with deep professional experience.

## Dr. Leete as Exemplar

Dr. Leete himself embodies the utopian physician. As a retired doctor, he devotes his leisure to scientific pursuits, including chemical experiments. His discovery of Julian West's sealed chamber occurred during excavation for a private laboratory (003-202). His calm, rational manner and devotion to both science and the welfare of his patient-guest exemplify the ideal of the physician in a society free from commercial pressures.

## Nineteenth-Century Contrast

The 19th-century medical landscape included both regular physicians and "irregular" practitioners like Dr. Pillsbury, the "Professor of Animal Magnetism" who treated West's insomnia through mesmerism (001-089). West notes that Dr. Pillsbury "didn't know anything about medicine, but he was certainly a remarkable mesmerist" (001-089). This contrast highlights the professionalization and standardization of medicine achieved by the 20th-century system.

## Professional Training

Medical education, like all professional training, remains open to every citizen until the age of thirty. As Dr. Leete explains, "this opportunity for a professional training remains open to every man till the age of thirty is reached" (004-412), allowing for the later development of natural aptitudes.
