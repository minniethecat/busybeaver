# National Solidarity

The ethical and spiritual foundation of Bellamy's utopian society: the recognition that all human beings are members of one family, bound by ties of mutual obligation that transcend the narrow individualism and class antagonism of the capitalist era.

## Overview

National solidarity — sometimes called the "solidarity of the race" or "human brotherhood" — is the moral sentiment that animates all the institutional arrangements of the utopian society. It is the answer to the question that Julian West repeatedly asks: what motivates people to work hard and behave ethically in a society without fear of poverty or hope of great wealth? Dr. Leete's answer is that the competitive, self-interested motives of capitalism have been replaced by a higher ethical consciousness, one in which every citizen genuinely feels their interests to be identical with those of every other citizen.

## In Bellamy's Utopia

### From Competition to Cooperation

The key to understanding the utopian ethical transformation, Dr. Leete tells West, can be summarized in a single sentence: "that what is now regarded as an object of ambition, namely, to outshine your fellows, was in your day an object of ambition likewise, but that then the means to that end were private accumulation, while now they are public service" (012-033). The fundamental human drive for distinction and recognition has not been eliminated — it has been redirected from private wealth accumulation toward public service and contribution to the common good.

### The Religion of Solidarity

Solidarity in Bellamy's vision has a quasi-religious character. The sermon that West hears from Mr. Barton articulates what might be called the "religion of solidarity": a secular ethical faith that replaces traditional Christianity as the moral compass of society. Barton preaches that "the present economic arrangement of society, by which the means of life are made as free to all as the air we breathe, has given the true basis of human brotherhood" (026-017). Economic equality is not merely a practical arrangement — it is the material precondition for genuine spiritual fraternity.

### The National Family

The metaphor of the nation as a family pervades the utopian discourse. Just as family members do not compete with one another for a larger share of the household budget, citizens of the utopian nation do not compete for a larger share of the national product. The "solidarity of the race" means that the success of any citizen is the success of all, and the suffering of any is the concern of all. As Dr. Leete explains, the competitive struggle for existence has been replaced by a cooperative effort for the common welfare.

## Contrast with the 19th Century

In the 19th century, society was organized around what Bellamy saw as a fundamental lie: "a singular hallucination which those on the top of the coach generally shared, that they were not exactly like their brothers and sisters who pulled at the rope, but of finer clay, in some way belonging to a higher order of beings who might justly expect to be drawn" (001-008). This delusion of essential difference between rich and poor justified the most extreme inequality and blunted the natural human impulse toward compassion.

The transformation from competitive individualism to national solidarity was not, in Bellamy's telling, a matter of moral exhortation. It was a structural change: once economic equality was established, the material basis for class distinction disappeared, and the ethical superstructure of solidarity followed naturally. "It was not till a rearrangement of the industrial and social system on a higher ethical basis" that genuine solidarity became possible (024-010).

## Notes

Bellamy's "religion of solidarity" was deeply influential on the Social Gospel movement in American Protestantism, which sought to apply Christian ethics to social and economic problems. The concept also influenced the British Fabian Society and the early British Labour Party, though these movements generally adopted more pragmatic, less millenarian versions of solidarity than Bellamy's utopian vision.
