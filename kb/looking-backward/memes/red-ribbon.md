# Red Ribbon

The **Red Ribbon** is the highest honor in Bellamy's utopian society, surpassing even the presidency in prestige. It is awarded by direct popular vote to the nation's most distinguished creative and intellectual contributors — great authors, artists, engineers, physicians, and inventors — whose work has enriched the common life.

## Nature of the Honor

Dr. Leete describes the red ribbon as the supreme recognition of individual excellence:

> "The highest of all honors in the nation, higher than the presidency, which calls merely for good sense and devotion to duty, is the red ribbon awarded by the vote of the people to the great authors, artists, engineers, physicians, and inventors of the generation." (015-006)

The honor is intentionally exclusive — "not over a certain number wear it at any one time" — creating a genuine distinction that inspires ambition. Dr. Leete himself confesses that "every bright young fellow in the country loses innumerable nights' sleep dreaming of it. I even did myself" (015-006).

## The Blue Ribbon

Below the red ribbon, a secondary honor — the blue ribbon — recognizes a broader tier of achievement. Dr. Leete wryly notes that his wife "would never have had me if I had not assured her that I was bound to get the red ribbon or at least the blue" (015-008), suggesting that even the second-tier honor carries significant social weight.

## Merit Badges in the Industrial Army

The red and blue ribbons exist within a broader system of visible merit markers. Every industry has its own emblematic device, worn as a metallic badge by workers in the [[industrial-army]]. These badges are hierarchical: iron for the third grade, silver for the second, and gilt for the first (012-018). When a worker is promoted, "the results of each regrading, giving the standing of every man in his industry, are gazetted in the public prints, and those who have won promotion since the last regrading receive the nation's thanks and are publicly invested with the badge of their new rank" (012-017).

## Function in a Moneyless Society

In a society without [[wealth-concentration|wealth inequality]] or poverty, traditional material incentives lose their force. The red ribbon system channels competitive ambition into socially valuable achievement, replacing the crude drive for personal enrichment with the pursuit of honor and public recognition. As Dr. Leete observes, this taps "the same principle... which in all ages has been the strongest incentive to effort" (012-015).
