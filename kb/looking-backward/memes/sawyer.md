# Sawyer

Julian West's personal servant in 1887, whose quiet fidelity and competence represent the anonymous working-class figures whose labor sustained the luxury of the 19th-century elite.

## Character Overview

Sawyer appears only in the preparatory scenes before West's long sleep, but his role is symbolically significant. He is the "faithful servant" — the unnamed and largely invisible working-class presence whose labor made possible the leisured existence of men like West. West instructs Sawyer "to rouse me at nine o'clock next morning" (002-013), a simple command that, through the accident of the house fire that night, is never fulfilled — with consequences that span more than a century.

## Background

Sawyer is a domestic servant in the West household, one of the vast army of working-class men and women whose labor sustained the comfortable lives of the Gilded Age elite. The narrative provides no details of his personal history, his family, or his aspirations — an absence that is itself significant, reflecting the 19th-century elite's blindness to the humanity of those who served them.

## Role in the Narrative

Sawyer's narrative function is brief but crucial. He assists Dr. Pillsbury in preparing West's hypnotic chamber, and he is the person instructed to awaken West the following morning. When the house burns that night — with West sealed in his underground sleeping chamber — Sawyer presumably perishes or flees, and the secret of West's location is lost. Sawyer thus unwittingly becomes the proximate cause of West's 113-year sleep.

## Character as Social Commentary

Sawyer represents the invisible working class of the 19th century — the millions whose labor sustained civilization but whose names and stories were rarely recorded. West's relationship with Sawyer is benevolent but entirely hierarchical: Sawyer is loyal, competent, and wholly subordinate. The utopian society that West awakens to has eliminated this relationship entirely — there are no servants in the 20th century, only fellow citizens performing their chosen work with dignity. The contrast between Sawyer's anonymous servitude and the self-respecting independence of utopian workers like the waiter at the dining hall is one of Bellamy's most pointed social critiques.
