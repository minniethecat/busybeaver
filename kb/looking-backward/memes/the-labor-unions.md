# The Labor Unions

The labor unions of West's era represent the first organized resistance to capitalist exploitation. Their strikes were 'nearly incessant ever since the great business crisis of 1873' (001-011). Though the unions' aspirations were impossible under capitalism, their militancy demonstrated 'dead earnestness' and prepared the working class for the transformation to come. Bellamy portrays them sympathetically but as ultimately incapable of achieving systemic change on their own.

## Labor Organization and the Concentration of Capital

Dr. Leete explains that the labor unions and strikes were not the cause of social transformation but an effect of deeper economic forces. "The organization of labor and the strikes were an effect, merely, of the concentration of capital in greater masses than had ever been known before" (005-020). Before this concentration, when commerce and industry were conducted by innumerable petty concerns with small capital, "the individual workman was relatively important and independent in his relations to the employer" (005-020). The rise of great monopolies destroyed that independence, forcing workers to organize collectively in self-defense. The unions were thus a rational response to the consolidation of economic power — a defensive measure that, paradoxically, demonstrated the very principle of collective action that would later underpin the utopian industrial army.

## The Limits of Class Organization

Despite their militancy, the unions could never have achieved national transformation on their own. Dr. Leete observes that "the labor parties, as such, never could have accomplished anything on a large or permanent scale. For purposes of national scope, their basis as merely class organizations was too narrow" (024-010). Real systemic change required "a rearrangement of the industrial and social system on a higher ethical basis" (024-010) — something that transcended the interests of any single class. Bellamy's argument is that the unions were a necessary but insufficient step: they awakened working-class consciousness and demonstrated the feasibility of mass organization, but the final transition from capitalism to the cooperative commonwealth required a national, cross-class movement grounded in ethical principle rather than economic grievance.


> “Oh no!” replied the doctor. “The labor parties, as such, never could have accomplished anything on a large or permanent scale. For purposes of national scope, their basis as merely class organizations was too narrow. It was not till a rearrangement of the industrial and social system on a higher ethical basis, and for the more efficient production of wealth, was recognized as the interest, not of one class, but equally of all classes, of rich and poor, cultured and ignorant, old and young, weak and strong, men and women, that there was any prospect that it would be achieved.”
