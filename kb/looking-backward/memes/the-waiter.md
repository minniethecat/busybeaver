# The Waiter

A young man serving in the unclassified grade of the industrial army who waits tables at the communal dining hall, and whose bearing and dignity provide Julian West with his first direct observation of the transformed character of labor in the utopia.

## Character Overview

The waiter appears in a single scene — the Leete family's dinner at the communal dining hall — but his significance in the novel is disproportionate to his brief appearance. West observes him "closely, as it was the first time I had been able to study particularly the bearing of one of the enlisted members of the industrial army" (014-009). What West sees astonishes him: a young man who performs the work of a servant with the dignity and self-possession of a gentleman.

## Background

The waiter is a young man in the "unclassified grade" of the industrial army — the entry level for workers who have not yet specialized in a trade. "The waiters are young men in the unclassified grade of the industrial army who are assignable to all sorts of miscellaneous occupations not requiring special skill" (014-021). Waiting tables is not his permanent career but a temporary assignment, rotated among young workers as part of their general experience of productive labor.

## Role in the Narrative

The waiter serves as West's first concrete evidence that the status hierarchy of 19th-century labor has been abolished. In West's world, a waiter was a social inferior — someone whose occupation marked him as belonging to a lower class. The utopian waiter, by contrast, is a citizen performing a valued service, whose dignity derives from his membership in the national community rather than from the prestige of his occupation. "Waiting on table is not deemed a menial occupation but a service like any other" (014-021) — this simple statement encapsulates the revolution in social status that economic equality enables.

## Character as Social Commentary

The waiter is Bellamy's most vivid illustration of the transformed meaning of work in the utopia. In the 19th century, work carried a double stigma: it was both economically precarious (you could be fired) and socially degrading (service work marked you as inferior). The utopian waiter experiences neither: his economic security is absolute, and his social status is equal to every other citizen's. The result is a young man who performs the physical tasks of a servant with the bearing of an equal — because he is one.
