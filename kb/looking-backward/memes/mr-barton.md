# Mr. Barton

The preacher whose sermon Julian West attends with the Leete family, and through whom Bellamy articulates the "religion of solidarity" — the secular ethical faith that serves as the spiritual dimension of the utopian social order.

## Character Overview

Mr. Barton appears in a single extended scene — the Sunday sermon that West attends — but his function in the novel is crucial. He is the voice of the ethical religion, translating the institutional arrangements that Dr. Leete has explained into the language of moral and spiritual conviction. Where Leete provides the "how" of the utopia, Barton provides the "why" — the ethical imperative that gives the system its moral legitimacy.

## Background

Barton is a preacher, but not in the traditional Christian sense. He belongs to the "ethical religion" that has evolved from Christianity in the century since West's time — a faith that has shed supernatural doctrines while retaining the emotional and communal functions of religious worship. He is described as having "earnest sympathetic tones which had already impressed me most favorably" (026-017), and his sermon is delivered not in a church but in a public hall, reflecting the civic rather than sectarian character of the new religion.

## Role in the Narrative

Barton's sermon provides the spiritual climax of the novel's exposition. He argues that "the present economic arrangement of society, by which the means of life are made as free to all as the air we breathe, has given the true basis of human brotherhood" (026-017). Where traditional Christianity had promised brotherhood in heaven while accepting inequality on earth, the new religion celebrates the achievement of brotherhood in the here and now. Economic equality is not merely a practical convenience — it is the material precondition for genuine spiritual community.

## Character as Social Commentary

Barton is the least individualized of Bellamy's characters — he exists purely as a vehicle for ideas. But those ideas were among the most influential in the novel. The concept of a "religion of solidarity" that replaced otherworldly theology with this-worldly ethics resonated deeply with the Social Gospel movement and with progressive religious thinkers who sought to reconcile Christianity with social reform. Barton's sermon — and the attentive, diverse congregation that hears it — models the kind of civic ritual that Bellamy believed could replace traditional religious observance in a rational, egalitarian society.
