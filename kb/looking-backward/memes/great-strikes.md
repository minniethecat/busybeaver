# The Great Strikes of the 1880s

The wave of strikes, lockouts, and industrial conflicts that convulsed American industry during the last decades of the 19th century. In Bellamy's narrative, the strikes serve as the most visible symptom of competitive capitalism's terminal crisis — and as the immediate catalyst for [[julian-west]]'s fateful decision to build the [[underground-chamber]] and undergo hypnotic treatment.

## Historical Context

The strikes of the 1870s and 1880s represented an unprecedented escalation of class conflict in the United States. Major confrontations included:

| Conflict | Year | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| Great Railroad Strike | 1877 | Nationwide railroad walkout; first general strike in US history; over 100 killed |
| Haymarket Affair | 1886 | Chicago labor rally bombed; eight anarchists convicted; became international cause célèbre |
| Building Trades Strikes | 1887 | Boston-area construction walkouts; directly affect West's house and marriage plans |
| Southwest Railroad Strike | 1886 | 200,000 workers walk out against Jay Gould's railroad empire |
| Homestead Strike | 1892 | (After novel's publication) Bloody confrontation at Carnegie steel works |

Bellamy published *Looking Backward* in 1888, at the peak of this labor conflict. The novel's first readers would have recognized the strikes West describes as current events, not historical fiction.

## Role in the Narrative

### Catalyst for the Plot

The strikes serve as the novel's inciting incident. Construction on West's new house — the house he is building for his marriage to [[edith-bartlett]] — has been repeatedly delayed by the building trades strikes. West describes his situation:

> "The building trades were in a state of great disturbance all that summer. Strikes were of such common occurrence that people had ceased to inquire into their particular grounds. The building of my house had been delayed through the whole season by a succession of strikes and lockouts."
>
> — Julian West, (001-005)

To escape the noise and disruption, West has constructed the [[underground-chamber]] — a soundproofed sleeping room beneath the foundations of his house. It is there that he undergoes Dr. Pillsbury's hypnotic treatment on Decoration Day, 1887, and it is there that he sleeps for 113 years while the old world collapses and the new one is built above him (001-006).

### Symbol of Systemic Failure

Bellamy presents the strikes not as isolated incidents of labor unrest but as symptoms of a dying economic order:

> "Strikes had become so common at that period that people had ceased to inquire into their particular grounds. In the larger view, it was recognized that the labor question was insoluble under the existing system, and that strikes were but the symptoms of a disease for which no cure had been found."
>
> — Julian West, (001-011)

This framing is crucial to Bellamy's argument: the strikes represent not the irrationality of workers but the irrationality of the system. The laborers "knew what they wanted — more of the product of their labor — but they knew nothing of how to accomplish it" (001-011). Their instincts were correct; their methods, within the constraints of capitalism, were futile.

## Bellamy's Analysis of the Strikes

### The Diagnosis

Bellamy's narrator offers a layered analysis of the labor crisis:

1. **Structural Inevitability**: Strikes are not aberrations but the predictable outcome of a system in which "the producing and employing classes" have fundamentally antagonistic interests
2. **Scale of Crisis**: Strikes have become so pervasive that "people had ceased to inquire into their particular grounds" — the system is in permanent crisis, not episodic disruption
3. **Rational Impulse, Futile Method**: Workers are right to demand more of what they produce, but strikes cannot deliver lasting improvement within the capitalist framework
4. **The Coach Metaphor**: West's famous metaphor (001-004) depicts the masses as beasts of burden dragging a coach of privilege — the strikes are the moment when "the dumb beasts" begin to resist their harness

### The Prescription

The strikes' ultimate remedy, in Bellamy's vision, is not unionization or reform but systemic transformation. The [[the-great-transition|Great Transition]] from capitalism to the [[industrial-army]] system eliminates strikes entirely — not by suppressing workers but by eliminating the employer-employee relationship that generates conflict:

> "Under the new order, the very idea of a strike became as meaningless as a quarrel between the right hand and the left, for there were no longer employers and employed, but only fellow-workers in a common enterprise."
>
> — Dr. Leete, (007-008)

## Contrast with Utopian Labor Relations

| Aspect | Strikes (19th c.) | Industrial Army (20th c.) |
|--------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Worker-Employer | Adversarial | Nonexistent (all are public servants) |
| Wages | Negotiated in conflict | Equal [[credit-card|credit]] for all |
| Working Conditions | Imposed by market | Democratically determined |
| Job Security | None (at-will employment) | Guaranteed (right of citizenship) |
| Dispute Resolution | Strikes, lockouts, violence | Administrative review, reassignment |
| Motivation | Fear of hunger | Honor, pride, social esteem |

## Notes

Bellamy's portrayal of the strikes reflects both sympathy for workers' grievances and skepticism about labor militancy as a path to change. This ambivalence was characteristic of middle-class reform thought in the Gilded Age: horrified by the violence and disruption of strikes, yet convinced that the underlying grievances were legitimate. Bellamy's solution — complete systemic transformation through democratic means — offered a vision that appealed to both labor sympathizers and those who feared class war. It is notable that the novel, while clearly anti-capitalist, does not depict a single worker character or revolutionary figure — the transformation is narrated entirely from the perspective of the educated middle class, with workers remaining an abstract mass whose grievances are acknowledged but whose agency is displaced onto systemic evolution.
