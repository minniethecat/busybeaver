# Mr. Barton's Sermon

The sermon represents the spiritual climax of the novel. Mr. Barton preaches that economic equality has 'given the true basis of human brotherhood' (026-017). The congregation listens via telephonic transmission, and the sermon is delivered not in a traditional church but in a public hall. The event demonstrates the transformed religious culture of the utopia — secularized in doctrine but retaining the communal and emotional functions of traditional worship.

## The Telephonic Service

Mr. Barton's sermon is delivered through the same telephonic network that transmits music. Dr. Leete explains that West need only accompany the family "to the music room at the proper hour and selecting an easy chair" (026-010). Though some still prefer to attend church in person, "most of our preaching, like our musical performances, is not in public, but delivered in acoustically prepared chambers, connected by wire with subscribers' houses" (026-010). West, intrigued by "the novelty of the experience of hearing a sermon under such circumstances" (026-011), chooses to hear Mr. Barton. An hour later, as West sits reading in the library, Edith comes for him, and the family gathers in the music room — the same wood-paneled room where Edith had demonstrated the musical telephone — to listen to the sermon.

> Most of our preaching, like our musical performances, is not in public, but delivered in acoustically prepared chambers, connected by wire with subscribers' houses.
> (026-010)

## Barton's Message and West's Response

Dr. Leete, concerned that the sermon's theme might embarrass West, offers to switch to another preacher: "Edith suggests that you may find it slightly embarrassing to listen to a discourse on the lines Mr. Barton is laying down" (026-014). West declines emphatically, insisting he would "much rather hear what Mr. Barton has to say" (026-015). The sermon's theme — the ethical foundation of the new social order — directly challenges the moral assumptions of West's 19th-century world, yet West's willingness to hear it signals his growing openness to the utopian perspective. When the sermon concludes, Edith touches a screw and the room fills again with "the earnest sympathetic tones" (026-017) of the preacher's voice as he delivers his peroration on human brotherhood.
