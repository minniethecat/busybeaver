# Penthesilia

*Penthesilia* is the celebrated romance by Berrian, considered the great romancer of the twentieth century in Bellamy's utopia. The novel serves within *Looking Backward* as a device for illustrating the cultural transformation wrought by economic equality — a book that, by what it omits as much as what it contains, reveals the character of the new society.

## Introduction to Julian West

On his first night in the 20th century, Julian West is offered *Penthesilia* by Edith Leete. She observes wistfully, "Ah, what would I give if I had not already read Berrian's novels" (015-004), and introduces it as "his masterpiece, and will at least give you an idea what the stories nowadays are like" (015-031).

## What the Book Leaves Out

West reads through the night and into dawn, and what most strikes him is not the book's content but its omissions:

> The story-writers of my day would have deemed the making of bricks without straw a light task compared with the construction of a romance from which should be excluded all effects drawn from the contrasts of wealth and poverty, education and ignorance, coarseness and refinement, high and low, all motives drawn from social pride and ambition, the desire of being richer or the fear of being poorer, together with sordid anxieties of any sort for one's self or others; a romance in which there should, indeed, be love galore, but love unfretted by artificial barriers created by differences of station or possessions, owning no other law but that of the heart.
>
> (015-032)

The passage is a structural analysis of how economic conditions shape literary form. Nineteenth-century fiction — from Dickens to Balzac to Howells — drew its dramatic energy from the very inequalities that *Penthesilia*'s world has abolished. To write a compelling romance without class difference, without financial anxiety, without social ambition, is, West realizes, a fundamentally different artistic enterprise.

## As Social Education

West concludes that *Penthesilia* was "of more value than almost any amount of explanation would have been in giving me something like a general impression of the social aspect of the twentieth century" (015-032). Where Dr. Leete's explanations had left him with "so many separate impressions," Berrian's narrative "put them together for me in a picture" (015-032). Fiction, in Bellamy's vision, is not escapism but the most effective vehicle for understanding a society's inner life. The arts, freed from market pressures and class preoccupations, become instruments of moral and social education — not because they preach, but because they show what human life looks like when stripped of the anxieties that had defined all previous literature.
