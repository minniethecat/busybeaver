# Wage System

The 19th-century method of compensating labor through variable cash payments — a system Dr. Leete's utopia replaces entirely, to the point that "there is no idea in the modern social economy which at all corresponds with what was meant by wages in your day" (009-029).

## Overview

In Bellamy's 19th century, the wage system determined how workers were paid: employers paid as little as possible, workers demanded as much as they could, and the market set the rate. Julian West, trying to understand the utopian economy, keeps returning to "the wages question" as the central puzzle: "How, I ask once more, can you adjust satisfactorily the comparative wages or remuneration of the multitude of avocations?" (009-024). The wage system was, in West's own description, "not a pretty system ethically" but seemed the only practical way to allocate labor across thousands of different trades.

## The Market Rate and Its Defects

West presses Dr. Leete on what could possibly replace the market mechanism for setting wages. He acknowledges the market's ethical deficiencies but insists on its practicality: "with all its defects, the plan of settling prices by the market rate was a practical plan; and I cannot conceive what satisfactory substitute you can have devised for it" (009-026). With the government as the sole employer, wages "must be arbitrarily fixed by the government," which West regards as "a more complex and perilous a problem than any your solution of the labor question avoids" (009-026). Dr. Leete's response — that a board of sensible people could set initial rates and let voluntary movement between trades correct any errors (009-027) — reveals the utopian confidence that cooperation, not competition, produces the better equilibrium.

## In Bellamy's Utopia

The utopia does not reform the wage system — it abolishes it. Dr. Leete explains: "we have nothing at all answering to your idea of wages" (009-035). Instead of variable cash wages, all citizens receive equal maintenance from the national product in exchange for equal labor service. The differential "price" of different kinds of labor is handled not by paying some workers more money but by adjusting the hours required: difficult or dangerous trades require fewer hours per day, making them attractive to volunteers without creating income inequality. The incentives that wages provided in the 19th century — the fear of want and the hope of luxury — are replaced by "higher motives wholly unknown to the mere wage earners of your age": patriotism, the passion for humanity, and the desire for social distinction through excellent service (009-047).

> Does it then really seem to you that human nature is insensible to any motives save fear of want and love of luxury, that you should expect security and equality of livelihood to leave them without possible incentives to effort?
> (009-047)
