# Distribution System

The mechanism by which the 20th-century utopia distributes goods to all citizens: a centralized, moneyless system of national warehouses that replaced the chaotic market economy of the 19th century.

## Overview

In Bellamy's *Looking Backward*, the distribution of goods underwent a transformation as radical as the reorganization of production. "A system of direct distribution from the national storehouses took the place of trade, and for this money was unnecessary" (009-008). The entire apparatus of retail — the "miles of stores" and "ten thousand stores to distribute the goods needed by this one city" that West later marveled at in his nightmare return to 19th-century Boston — was replaced by a single coordinated system. Distribution became a branch of public administration rather than a field of private enterprise.

## The Mechanism

### National Warehouses and Sample Stores

Every ward or district maintains a single sample store where citizens examine goods and place orders. As Edith Leete explained to Julian West during his first visit to a sample store, consumers inspect product samples and place orders that are communicated directly to the central warehouse. "Everything was procurable from one source, and nothing could be procured anywhere else" (009-008). The goods are delivered the same day from the warehouse to the consumer's home.

Dr. Leete elaborated that the system eliminated the massive duplication of the 19th century: "the work done once by the merchants, traders, storekeepers, with their various grades of jobbers, wholesalers, retailers, agents, commercial travelers, and middlemen of all sorts, with an excessive waste of energy in needless transportation and interminable handlings, is performed by one-tenth the number of hands" (022-007). The statisticians of the utopia calculated "that one eightieth part of our workers suffices for all the processes of distribution which in your day required one eighth of the population, so much being withdrawn from the force engaged in productive labor" (022-007).

### The Credit Card System

Instead of money, each citizen receives an annual credit — an equal share of the national product. The credit card serves as the instrument of allocation, debited at the point of order. There is neither selling nor buying in the traditional sense; as Dr. Leete put it, "There is neither selling nor buying nowadays; the distribution of goods is effected in another way. As to the bankers, having no money we have no use for those gentry" (009-005). The abolition of money removes the entire apparatus of banking, credit, and speculation.

### Demand-Driven Production

The distribution system is directly linked to production planning. "For by the connection between distribution and production supply is geared to demand like an engine to the governor which regulates its speed" (023-008). The Department of Distribution maintains precise records of every item dispensed from national warehouses, enabling production estimates to be based on actual consumption data rather than speculation. For staples with steady demand, estimates are prepared a year in advance; for smaller industries where fashion and novelty fluctuate, "production is kept barely ahead of consumption, the distributive department furnishing frequent estimates based on the weekly state of demand" (017-012).

## Contrast with 19th-Century Distribution

Bellamy's 19th-century distribution was a system of organized waste. West's nightmare return to Washington Street in 1887 Boston provides the novel's most vivid contrast: "Stores! stores! stores! miles of stores! ten thousand stores to distribute the goods needed by this one city, which in my dream had been supplied with all things from a single warehouse" (028-012). In the old system, "the mere distribution of the goods, their handling alone, added a fourth, a third, a half and more, to the cost" (028-013). The utopian system eliminates this waste entirely.

The difference was not merely economic but moral. The old system, Dr. Leete observed, substituted "a plan of hand to hand distribution, involving the maximum of personal humiliation to all classes of recipients" for the "most natural and obvious method" of distributing the national product directly to its members (025-008). Under the new system, "the account of every person, man, woman, and child... is always with the nation directly, and never through any intermediary" (025-008).

## Economic Principles

The distribution system operates on the principle of equal purchasing power for all. Prices for goods are set based on the cost of the labor required to produce them — measured in hours, not wages, since the maintenance of all workers is equal. "The cost of a man's work in a trade so difficult that in order to attract volunteers the hours have to be fixed at four a day is twice as great as that in a trade where the men work eight hours" (017-016). The factor of scarcity affects some prices — fresh fish, dairy products, and goods requiring rare materials command higher prices — but for staples, "there is always a large surplus kept on hand from which any fluctuations of demand or supply can be corrected" (017-017).
