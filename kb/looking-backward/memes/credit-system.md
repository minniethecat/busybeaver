# Credit System

The mechanism by which Bellamy's utopia allocates goods without money: each citizen receives an equal annual credit representing their share of the national product, debited at the point of consumption through a system of national accounting.

## Overview

The credit system is the practical instrument of economic equality in Bellamy's *Looking Backward*. Having abolished money, private property in capital, and the entire apparatus of banking and finance, the utopia needed a mechanism to distribute goods. The solution was elegantly simple: each citizen receives an equal annual credit — their share of the national product — recorded on a perforated card. Purchases are debited against this credit at national warehouses and sample stores. There is no money, no price negotiation, no debt, and no possibility of accumulation beyond one's equal share.

## How It Works

Dr. Leete explained the system to Julian West in the simplest terms: "A credit corresponding to his share of the annual product of the nation is given to every citizen on the public books at the beginning of each year, and a credit card issued him with which he procures at the public storehouses, found in every community, whatever he desires whenever he desires it" (009-010). The card is perforated at the time of purchase — a physical debiting of the citizen's annual allocation.

The system operates on the principle that all citizens are entitled to equal maintenance from the common product in exchange for their equal contribution of labor. The credit is not a wage — it is not proportional to the quantity or quality of work performed — but a share, equal for all, based on citizenship itself. "The account of every person, man, woman, and child... is always with the nation directly, and never through any intermediary" (025-008).

## Elimination of Money

The credit system is not a substitute for money but the abolition of it. "There is neither selling nor buying nowadays; the distribution of goods is effected in another way. As to the bankers, having no money we have no use for those gentry" (009-005). Because the nation is the sole producer and sole distributor of goods, there is no need for a medium of exchange. The credit card is simply a record-keeping device — a way to track each citizen's consumption against their annual allocation.

The consequences of eliminating money are far-reaching. Without money, there can be no accumulation of wealth, no inheritance of fortunes, no lending at interest, no financial speculation, no banking crises. The entire apparatus of 19th-century finance — which employed millions and periodically devastated the economy — simply vanishes when the medium it operated on is withdrawn.

## Prices Without Money

While there is no money, there are still prices — calculated based on the cost of labor required to produce each good. As Dr. Leete explained, "The cost of the labor which produced it was recognized as the legitimate basis of the price of an article in your day, and so it is in ours" (017-016). The difference is that in the utopia, the "cost" is measured in labor hours, adjusted for the difficulty of the work, rather than in wages determined by the market.

This system of labor-cost pricing ensures that goods requiring more labor — either in quantity or difficulty — "cost" more in credit terms, naturally rationing them to those who value them most. But because all citizens have equal credits, this rationing is by preference rather than by class. "High prices in your day meant restriction of the articles affected to the rich, but nowadays, when the means of all are the same, the effect is only that those to whom the articles seem most desirable are the ones who purchase them" (017-017).

## Moral Implications

The credit system embodies a moral principle: that every citizen's claim on the national product is equal because every citizen's obligation to contribute is equal. The 19th-century system, by contrast, made a person's claim on goods proportional not to their humanity but to their wealth — a principle the utopians found both irrational and unjust. The credit system replaces the morality of the market with the morality of the commonwealth.
