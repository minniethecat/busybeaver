# Equality of Distribution

**Equality of Distribution** is the foundational economic principle of Bellamy's utopia — the idea that every citizen receives an identical share of the national product. This replaces the 19th-century system of unequal wages, inherited wealth, and class stratification with a society in which all members share alike from the common stock.

## The Moral Argument

Dr. Leete presents equality of condition as an ethical imperative, not merely an economic arrangement. He explains that unequal distribution of wealth and unequal opportunities for education and culture had divided 19th-century society into classes that "regarded each other as distinct races" (014-002). The wealthy would not accept services from their own class that they would not return, yet they freely exploited the poor — treating them as beings of a fundamentally different kind (014-002).

Only when equality of condition was established could the abstract ideals of human solidarity become practical reality:

> "Until this equality of condition had come to pass, the idea of the solidarity of humanity, the brotherhood of all men, could never have become the real conviction and practical principle of action it is nowadays. In your day the same phrases were indeed used, but they were phrases merely." (014-002)

## The Economic Rationale

Beyond the moral case, Dr. Leete advances a pragmatic argument: the principle of "share and share alike" is economically efficient. Under the old system of competing private enterprises, "selfishness was their only science, and in industrial production selfishness is suicide" (022-005). Competition dissipated energy, while combination enabled efficient production. Only by subordinating individual hoarding to the growth of the common stock could real wealth creation begin (022-005).

The [[credit-card]] system implements this equality — each citizen receives an identical annual credit, drawn against the national product. Differences in consumption preferences are accommodated, but everyone's basic claim on society's output is the same.

## From Class Division to One Class

Under equality of distribution, the class distinctions of the 19th century disappear entirely. As Dr. Leete puts it, equal wealth and equal opportunities of culture have "simply made us all members of one class, which corresponds to the most fortunate class with you" (014-002). The nation becomes "a family, a vital union, a common life" rather than a collection of competing economic interests (024-005).
