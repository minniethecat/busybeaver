# The 19th-Century Trusts

The trusts represent the transitional phase between capitalism and the utopia. Through 'the absorption of business by ever larger monopolies' (005-023), the small capitalists were eliminated by the large, and the large by the larger, until the entire capital of the nation was consolidated. These trusts were the organizational precursors of the nationalized economy — private monopolies that made the final step to public ownership both natural and inevitable.

## The Inexorable Logic of Consolidation

Dr. Leete traces the relentless process by which competitive capitalism destroyed itself. "Meanwhile, without being in the smallest degree checked by the clamor against it, the absorption of business by ever larger monopolies continued" (005-022). In the United States, after the last quarter of the 19th century, there ceased to be "any opportunity whatever for individual enterprise in any important field of industry" (005-022). The small capitalist, far from being the hero of the system, was its first victim — swallowed by the larger fish in an increasingly concentrated pond. This process, though lamented by contemporaries as the death of economic freedom, was in Bellamy's view the necessary prelude to genuine freedom: the trusts created organizational forms and logistical systems that, once transferred to public ownership, became the infrastructure of the cooperative commonwealth.


> “Meanwhile, without being in the smallest degree checked by the clamor against it, the absorption of business by ever larger monopolies continued. In the United States there was not, after the beginning of the last quarter of the century, any opportunity whatever for individual enterprise in any important field of industry, unless backed by a great capital. During the last decade of the century, such small businesses as still remained were fast-failing survivals of a past epoch, or mere parasites on the great corporations.”
>
> (005-022)


## From Private Monopoly to Public Trust

The final stage of consolidation was the nation's assumption of control. "Early in the last century the evolution was completed by the final consolidation of the entire capital of the nation" (005-025). Industry and commerce, ceasing to be "conducted by a set of irresponsible corporations and syndicates of private persons at their caprice and for their profit, were entrusted to a single syndicate representing the people, to be conducted in the common interest for the common profit" (005-025). The nation became "the one great business corporation in which all other corporations were absorbed" (005-025). The trusts, in Bellamy's dialectical vision, were history's unwitting agents: their greed-driven consolidation built the organizational machinery that a democratized state would inherit and redirect toward universal welfare.
