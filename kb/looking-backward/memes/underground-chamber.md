# Underground Chamber

West's sleeping chamber is the physical mechanism of the novel's time travel. Built beneath his 19th-century house, it is a sealed, soundproof room where Dr. Pillsbury induces hypnotic trance. When the house burns in 1887, the chamber preserves West in suspended animation until Dr. Leete discovers it in the year 2000.

## Discovery and Awakening

When West awakens in a strange house with an unknown companion, Dr. Leete explains the extraordinary truth: "You are a century older than when you lay down to sleep in that underground chamber, your appearance is unchanged" (003-052). The Leete family is deeply interested in "the circumstances under which I had gone to sleep in the underground chamber" (004-022), and all have suggestions to account for West's survival. The chamber had preserved him while his house burned above — an accident of architecture and hypnotic science that bridged two centuries.


> “You are probably surprised,” said my companion, “to see that, although you are a century older than when you lay down to sleep in that underground chamber, your appearance is unchanged. That should not amaze you. It is by virtue of the total arrest of the vital functions that you have survived this great period of time. If your body could have undergone any change during your trance, it would long ago have suffered dissolution.”
>
> (003-052)


## The Nightmare Return

The chamber haunts West's psychology long after his awakening. One night he dreams he has returned to the 19th century, and the dream begins with him bolt upright in bed: "I was in my underground chamber. The mellow light of the lamp which always burned in the room when I occupied it was shining" (028-002). The voice of his servant Sawyer calls to him — but this is a nightmare vision of the old world's misery, not reality. The chamber thus serves a dual narrative function: the literal mechanism of time travel, and the psychological anchor of West's old identity.

## Revisiting the Chamber

Edith Leete casually asks West whether he has "yet revisited the underground chamber in the garden in which I had been found" (020-001). The question reveals how matter-of-factly the Leete family regards what to West is a traumatic and miraculous space. The chamber, buried beneath the garden of the new Leete house built on the site of West's old home, is both a historical curiosity and a personal wound.
