# Araucaria

## Definition

The araucaria — the potted evergreen plant on the landing of the aunt's [[boarding-house|boarding house]] — is one of [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s most delicately handled symbols. It stands for the entire bourgeois order: cleanliness, regularity, the quiet cultivation of domestic peace. [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s relationship to the araucaria is complex and revealing. He waters it carefully, observes it with affection, and yet knows he does not truly belong in its world. The plant is [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s secret shrine — a token of the orderly life he can appreciate but never fully possess.

## In the Novel

The araucaria is introduced in the narrator's preface as a detail of the household:

> "Look at this little vestibule," Haller went on, "with the araucaria and its wonderful smell. Many a time I can't go by without pausing a moment." (P02-029)

This offhand remark reveals much. [[harry-haller|Haller]] pauses before the araucaria — not because he needs to but because he wants to. The plant, like the household it ornaments, represents something he lacks: rootedness, domestic continuity, a life lived without metaphysical anguish. His appreciation of the araucaria is genuine but also ironic, for he knows that such appreciation is all he can manage. He can pause and admire; he cannot plant his own.

Later, the narrator observes:

> He called himself the Steppenwolf, and this too estranged and disturbed them, but they noticed that he carefully watered the araucaria and kept his room immaculate. (P02-035)

The juxtaposition is the point. The man who calls himself a wolf of the steppes — a wild, solitary, untamable creature — is also the man who faithfully waters a houseplant. The [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] identity, [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] implies, is not the whole truth about [[harry-haller|Haller]]. The araucaria witnesses to another side of him — the "bourgeois" side that the Treatise would later identify as one of his [[soul|thousand souls]].

## Symbolic Reading

In a novel of grand philosophical gestures, the araucaria stands as a small, stubborn counter-symbol. It does not point toward [[immortals|the Immortals]] or the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] or the dissolution of the ego. It points toward the ordinary, the mundane, the daily round. Its message is not transcendence but immanence: the peace that comes from tending something small and living, from participating in the quiet rituals of domesticity.

This makes it a surprisingly subversive presence in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]. Against [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s grandiose suffering and the Treatise's intellectual sophistication, the araucaria simply exists — green, watered, unremarkable. It is Hesse's reminder that the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois world]], for all its limitations, is not merely an obstacle to transcendence. It is also what makes transcendence possible — the ground from which the spirit rises and to which, in its better moments, it returns with gratitude.
