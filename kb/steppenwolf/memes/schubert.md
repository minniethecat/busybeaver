## In the Novel

Franz Schubert is referenced in *Steppenwolf* as a composer whose lieder belong to the world of authentic, soulful music that Harry Haller treasures. Schubert's song "Again Thou Fillest Brake and Vale" ("Füllest wieder Busch und Tal") is specifically mentioned, placing his art song tradition in contrast to the popular dance music that Hermine introduces Harry to.(001-142)

> "heard Mozart's 'Violets' and Schubert's 'Again Thou Fillest Brake and Vale'" (001-142)

The mention of Schubert occurs in a context where Harry is reflecting on the music that once gave his life meaning — the classical tradition represented by Mozart, Schubert, and others. This music stands in stark opposition to the jazz, foxtrot, and popular tunes that Harry initially despises but must learn to accept as part of his sensual re-education.

Schubert is later referenced by Mozart in the Magic Theater, alongside Hugo Wolf, in a discussion of what came after the classical masters. The implication is that Schubert represents a continuation of the Viennese musical lineage, even as it transforms into Romanticism.(001-598)

> "Certainly, there was Schubert to come. Hugo Wolf also, and..." (001-598)

## Character Portrait

Franz Schubert (1797–1828) was an Austrian composer of the late Classical and early Romantic eras. Despite his short life, he produced a vast body of work including over six hundred lieder, symphonies, chamber music, and piano works. In *Steppenwolf*, Schubert represents the Romantic transition in music — where the formal perfection of Haydn and Mozart gives way to a more personal, emotionally expressive art.

Schubert's inclusion alongside Mozart in Harry's musical memories is significant: it suggests that Harry's musical taste spans both the Classical and early Romantic periods, and that his attachment is to the emotional authenticity of art music rather than to any narrow stylistic allegiance.

## Key Passages

Schubert's "Again Thou Fillest Brake and Vale" is heard alongside Mozart's "Violets" (001-142), suggesting that Harry associates Schubert's lieder with intimate, private musical experience rather than public concert performance. The later mention by Mozart in the Magic Theater (001-598) places Schubert in the historical lineage of great composers who followed the classical masters, hinting at the continuity of artistic tradition that Harry's Steppenwolf philosophy sometimes denies.
