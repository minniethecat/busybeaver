# War

## Definition

In Hesse's *[[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]]*, war functions as both historical backdrop and spiritual metaphor. World War I (1914–1918) has just ended when [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s records are set; its shadow hangs over every page — in the newspapers the professor reads, in the jingoism [[harry-haller|Harry]] despises, and in the profound disillusionment that shapes his crisis. War for Hesse is not merely a political event but the outward expression of an inner sickness: the same fragmentation that tears Europe apart also tears [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s soul between man and wolf.

## In the Novel

The novel's anti-war stance surfaces most explicitly through [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s encounter with a former professor. The professor brandishes a militarist newspaper and praises the coming war, triggering in [[harry-haller|Harry]] a visceral revulsion. This passage crystallizes the novel's critique:

The professor "pointed to it and commented on it with enthusiasm; for, being a patriot and a militarist, he was anti-British in his views" — a stance that [[harry-haller|Harry]] finds not merely wrong but symptomatic of the same [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] blindness he has spent his life fleeing.

War also appears as metaphor: [[harry-haller|Harry]] describes his inner state in martial terms — "the battle between man and wolf," the "war" within his own personality. The [[chess-personality|chess game of personality]] in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] literalizes this metaphor, breaking the self into warring fragments.

## Philosophical Background

Hesse wrote *Steppenwolf* in the aftermath of World War I and his own severe psychological crisis. The novel's anti-war position aligns with Hesse's actual biography: he was condemned as a traitor in Germany for his pacifist writings during the war. The character of [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] — a middle-aged intellectual who rejects nationalist fervor — reflects Hesse's own experience of being alienated from a society rushing toward violence. This dimension of the novel connects it to the broader tradition of postwar European literature that questioned the values that led to the trenches.

## Critical Reception

Critics have noted that *Steppenwolf*'s anti-war theme is less foregrounded than its psychological themes, but no less important. The novel's critique of militarism functions as part of its larger indictment of [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] conformity — war being, in Hesse's view, the ultimate expression of the herd mentality that [[harry-haller|Harry]] struggles to escape. The 1960s counterculture rediscovered this dimension of the novel, reading it as an anti-establishment text that spoke to the Vietnam era as powerfully as it had to the interwar period.
