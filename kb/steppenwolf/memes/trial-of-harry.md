The **Trial of Harry Haller** is a dream sequence in which the [[immortals|Immortals]] — the eternal spirits of great artists and thinkers — put [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] on trial. The trial serves as a reckoning with Harry's deepest flaw: his inability to see beyond the binary of man and wolf, and his secret attachment to the very [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] order he claims to despise.

## The Court

The trial is presided over by [[goethe|Goethe]] and [[mozart|Mozart]], who appear as representatives of the Immortals — those who have transcended the limitations of individual personality and achieved a kind of eternal, humorous serenity. The atmosphere is both solemn and absurd, blending the gravity of a courtroom with the playfulness of a carnival.

Harry stands accused not of specific criminal acts but of a deeper spiritual failure: his lifelong insistence on seeing himself as a divided being, a Steppenwolf torn between man and beast, rather than recognising the [[personality-disintegration|multiplicity of his soul]].

## The Charges

The Immortals charge Harry with:

1. **Bourgeois hypocrisy** — Despite his professed contempt for the bourgeoisie, Harry has never truly broken free of its values. He keeps money in the bank, dresses respectably, and lodges in respectable middle-class houses, secretly comforted by their order and cleanliness.

2. **The wolf-man simplification** — Harry has reduced the rich complexity of his soul to a crude binary of "man" and "wolf," using this simplification as both a self-justification and a self-condemnation. The Immortals reveal that the self is not two but a "hundred or a thousand selves" (001-142).

3. **Self-hatred as egoism** — Harry's relentless self-criticism is unmasked as a form of vanity, a preoccupation with the self that is no different in kind from the smug self-satisfaction of the bourgeois he despises. As the [[Preface|narrator]] observes, "self-hate is really the same thing as sheer egoism, and in the long run breeds the same cruel isolation and despair" (P02-022).

## The Verdict

The Immortals do not condemn Harry to punishment. Instead, they sentence him to continue living — to endure his suffering, to learn to [[laughter|laugh]], and to enter the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] where he may finally confront the full multiplicity of his self.

The trial is thus not a condemnation but an education. The Immortals' laughter is not cruel but liberating: it dissolves Harry's tragic self-seriousness and opens the possibility of humour as a mode of transcendence.

## Symbolic Meaning

The trial dramatises the novel's central philosophical argument: that the self is not a fixed unity but a "constellated heaven, a chaos of forms, of states and stages, of inheritances and potentialities" (001-144). Harry's crime is not having a wolf inside him but believing that the wolf and the man are the whole of his being.

The trial by the Immortals represents Harry's first genuine encounter with the possibility of seeing himself differently — not as a tragic figure torn between two poles, but as a being of infinite facets, each capable of being embraced with the humour that is "the most inborn and brilliant achievement of the spirit" (001-126).
