# Suicide

## Definition

In *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]*, suicide is not simply the act of taking one's own life but a mode of existence. The "suicide" — as defined by the [[treatise-on-the-steppenwolf|Treatise on the Steppenwolf]] — is one who lives in conscious proximity to death, who regards self-annihilation not as a catastrophe but as a permanent exit strategy, and who draws from this knowledge a peculiar form of freedom. [[harry-haller|Haller]] is a suicide not because he has attempted to kill himself but because he has structured his identity around the possibility of doing so.

## In the Novel

The Treatise explicitly categorizes [[harry-haller|Haller]] among the suicides:

> Little more is needed to show that the Steppenwolf lived a suicidal existence. But do not imagine that he actually ended his life. The suicide does not necessarily die. (P02-042)

This paradox is essential: the suicide lives — sometimes for many years — but lives *as if* death were an ever-present option. The suicide's relationship to death is not one of despair (though despair may accompany it) but one of freedom. Knowing that he can leave at any time, the suicide is oddly liberated. He need not compromise, need not accommodate, need not lower his spiritual demands to fit the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois world]].

The Treatise further refines this by distinguishing the suicide from the ordinary depressive. The suicide's suffering is not merely psychological but metaphysical: it stems from the discrepancy between the intensity of his inner experience and the poverty of what the external world offers in return. [[harry-haller|Haller]] does not want to die because life is painful; he wants to die because life, as ordinarily lived, is insufficient:

> He must with his own hand make an end of the personality he so hated, or else learn to die — to die to himself and his own ego — and be reborn. (001-035)

This formulation — "die to himself and his own ego" — reveals that the suicide's true task is not physical death but ego-death: the dissolution of the fixed identity that causes such suffering. The novel's resolution does not involve [[harry-haller|Haller]] killing himself but rather *dying* to [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] identity, allowing it to be dismantled in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] so that a more fluid, multiplicitous self can emerge.

## Philosophical Dimensions

The suicide concept engages deeply with existentialist thought. It anticipates Camus's opening line in *The Myth of Sisyphus* — "There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide" — and shares Camus's insight that confronting the possibility of suicide can lead not to despair but to a more authentic mode of living.

It also draws on Nietzsche's idea of "free death" — dying at the right time, on one's own terms — and on the Buddhist concept of *the death of the ego* as the gateway to liberation. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s "suicidal existence" is thus not a pathology to be cured but a threshold to be crossed. The novel's genius is to show that crossing it requires not an act of violence but an act of imagination — not killing oneself but letting the self one has constructed die into a larger, more playful multiplicity.
