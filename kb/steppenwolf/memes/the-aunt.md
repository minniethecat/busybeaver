## In the Novel

The Aunt is the first character encountered in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] after the narrator himself, appearing in the opening lines of the Preface when [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]—"a man of nearly fifty"—calls on her "to inquire for a furnished room" [P02-002]. The narrator, her nephew, records that Haller "came at noon" and "became my aunt's lodger" [P02-003], establishing the domestic arrangement that frames the entire narrative. She provides Haller with the attic room that becomes his solitary refuge, filled with books, cigar ash, and wine bottles—a physical manifestation of his disordered inner life at the boundary between the bourgeois world and his Steppenwolf existence.

Throughout the Preface, the Aunt serves as the narrator's primary source of information about Haller's habits. The narrator acknowledges that his understanding comes "partly from my own espionage, partly from my aunt's remarks" [P02-023], positioning her as an indispensable witness to Haller's daily existence. Her observations are practical and domestic: "Sometimes my aunt found nothing but a banana peel" [P02-024] in Haller's room the morning after his sleepless nights.

In the main narrative, the Aunt reappears briefly when Haller encounters her "above the araucaria"—the potted plant that symbolises bourgeois domestic order throughout [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]. He notes that he "saw her seldom" (001-175), yet their one recorded conversation reveals her character: "the aunt had a slight leaning to piety" (001-181), and she spoke to Haller about her nephew with maternal pride.

## Character Portrait

The Aunt embodies the bourgeois world that [[harry-haller|Haller]] simultaneously depends upon and despises. She is described as kind, orderly, and genuinely concerned for her lodger's welfare, yet entirely incapable of understanding the spiritual turmoil that consumes him. Her home is immaculate—"a wonderfully clean and well-kept house" as Haller himself acknowledges—with the [[araucaria]] plant standing as the emblem of her domestic order [P02-029].

She and her nephew "bothered our heads a lot" about Haller, genuinely wanting to help but fundamentally unable to comprehend what they observed [P02-014]. The narrator notes that "when my aunt had any little occasion to talk to Haller, he was invariably polite and showed genuine respect" [P02-034], suggesting that Haller recognised her goodness even as he remained fundamentally estranged from her world.

Her relationship with Haller is characterised by a mixture of maternal concern and respectful distance. When Haller caused some unspecified disturbance, he "begged my aunt's pardon for days after" [P02-043], demonstrating his awareness of how his Steppenwolf existence disrupted her orderly life. The narrator concludes that "she would have more to tell about him than I can" [P02-044], hinting at depths of observation that remain unrecorded.

## Key Passages

> A man of nearly fifty called on my aunt to inquire for a furnished room. [P02-002]

The novel's first introduction to Haller, filtered through the Aunt's domestic world.

> Sometimes my aunt found nothing but a banana peel in the morning. [P02-024]

A small domestic detail that reveals Haller's disordered nocturnal existence glimpsed through the lens of bourgeois housekeeping.

> At your aunt's too, there reigns a wonderfully clean and well-kept house. [P02-029]

Haller's own acknowledgment of the orderly world he inhabits but cannot truly belong to.

## Relationships

The Aunt's primary relationship is to **[[harry-haller]]**, her lodger, for whom she provides the attic room that becomes his sanctuary and prison. She is also connected to her **nephew** (the unnamed narrator of the Preface), who gathers information about Haller partly through her observations. Her house—with its [[araucaria]] plant, its cleanliness, and its orderly routines—represents the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] world that the entire novel critiques and Haller cannot escape.
