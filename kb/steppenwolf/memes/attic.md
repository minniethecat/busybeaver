# Attic

## Definition

[[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s attic room is the novel's central spatial symbol — the scholar's cell from which he descends into a world he cannot inhabit and to which he retreats when that world becomes unbearable. Perched at the top of the boarding house, above the [[araucaria]] and the clean staircases of [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] order, the attic is both refuge and prison: the only place where [[harry-haller|Harry]] can be himself, and the place where being himself means being alone.

## In the Novel

The attic is introduced through the narrator's detailed inventory of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s rented rooms — "a large and comfortable attic room with two windows" that, within days of his arrival, "was not at all the same as when occupied by other tenants." The transformation is significant: [[harry-haller|Harry]] fills the space with books, papers, cigar smoke, and [[wine]] bottles, converting a generic rented room into the dense material expression of a personality.

The attic's vertical position encodes its meaning. It is above the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] world of the lower floors, above the [[araucaria]] and the clean landings, above the domestic life of the [[the-aunt|aunt]] and her household. This elevation is both privilege and curse: [[harry-haller|Harry]] can look down on the world he has rejected, but he cannot enter it without descending stairs that leave him breathless. The [[gout]] that makes climbing painful reinforces the metaphor — the life of the mind, the novel suggests, is unsustainable without a body that can move through the world.

The attic is also the site of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s suicide resolution. It is here, alone, that he decides to end his life at fifty — a decision made in the very space that has made that life both possible and unbearable.

## Symbolic Significance

The attic room belongs to a long literary tradition of the scholar's garret — from Faust's study to the bohemian attics of nineteenth-century Paris. Hesse draws on this tradition while complicating it: [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s attic is not romanticized poverty but spiritual isolation, a space that protects him from the world at the cost of separating him from it. The novel's resolution requires him to leave it — to descend the stairs and enter the [[dance-halls|dance hall]], the [[masked-ball|Masked Ball]], the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]].

## Critical Reception

Critics have read the attic as a spatial correlative of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s psychological condition — the architectural expression of a self that has withdrawn from life. The novel's arc from attic to [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] traces a movement from enclosure to expansion, from the solitary cell to the infinite interior of the imagination.
