## In the Novel

Dostoevsky appears in [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] as one of the select authors whose works form the intellectual foundation of [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s inner life. The [[treatise-on-the-steppenwolf|Treatise on the Steppenwolf]] notes that "a few volumes of Dostoevsky bristled with pencilled slips" in [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s room [P02-023], the "pencilled slips" (annotations) indicating an active, engaged reading rather than passive ownership. This detail places Dostoevsky among the writers—alongside [[goethe|Goethe]], [[novalis|Novalis]], and [[jean-paul|Jean Paul]]—whom [[harry-haller|Haller]] has read with the intensity of personal need.

Dostoevsky is invoked again when [[harry-haller|Haller]] describes his study as a sanctuary: "the ink-pot and the paintbox, [[novalis|Novalis]] and Dostoevsky, awaited me" (001-030). The pairing of the writer with ordinary domestic objects (ink-pot, paintbox) suggests that Dostoevsky is not merely an author [[harry-haller|Haller]] admires but part of the familiar texture of his daily existence—a presence as reliable and necessary as the tools of his trade.

## Character Portrait

Hesse's brief references to Dostoevsky carry considerable weight for readers familiar with the Russian novelist's themes. Dostoevsky's explorations of the divided self (the "underground man"), the crisis of faith in modernity, and the tension between reason and irrational impulse resonate deeply with [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]'s central concerns. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s own double nature—the "man" and the "wolf"—echoes Dostoevsky's fascination with the multiplicity and contradictoriness of the human [[soul]].

The "pencilled slips" in [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s volumes [P02-023] imply that Dostoevsky has been read not for pleasure but for survival—the marginalia of a man seeking answers to existential questions. This mode of reading aligns [[harry-haller|Haller]] with Dostoevsky's own characters, who likewise treat ideas as matters of life and death.

## Key Passages

> A few volumes of Dostoevsky bristled with pencilled slips. [P02-023]

The vivid image of annotated pages conveys [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s intensive, need-driven engagement with Dostoevsky's works.

> The ink-pot and the paintbox, Novalis and Dostoevsky, awaited me just as do the mother and the wife, the children and the maid. (001-030)

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s domestic analogy positions Dostoevsky as a member of his intellectual household, a constant presence in his solitary life.

## Cultural Context

Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881) was one of the greatest Russian novelists, author of *Crime and Punishment*, *The Brothers Karamazov*, *Notes from Underground*, and *The Idiot*. His exploration of the divided self, the problem of evil, and the search for faith in a godless world made him a central figure for twentieth-century existentialist thought. Hermann Hesse was a lifelong admirer of Dostoevsky and wrote several essays on his work, including "The Brothers Karamazov, or The Decline of Europe" (1919).

Dostoevsky's concept of the "underground" consciousness—the self that rebels against rational self-interest and bourgeois order—is a clear precursor to [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] identity. Both figures experience their own complexity as a source of suffering and superiority.
