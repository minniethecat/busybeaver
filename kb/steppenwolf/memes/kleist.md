## In the Novel

Heinrich von Kleist appears in the Steppenwolf novel not as a character but as a cultural reference point alongside Ludwig van Beethoven. Harry Haller associates himself with Kleist as one of the figures of romantic despair and self-destruction — Kleist committed suicide in 1811 alongside Henriette Vogel at the Wannsee. The reference to Kleist places Harry's suicidal contemplation within a broader tradition of romantic poets and thinkers who saw death as a solution to existential suffering.(001-134)

> "in yourself only, but also in Kleist and Beethoven. Year after year..." (001-134)

Kleist's presence in the novel serves as a mirror for Harry's self-perception. Just as Kleist represented the extreme romantic temperament — passionate, idealistic, and ultimately self-destructive — Harry sees his own fate intertwined with those who could not reconcile their inner vision with the demands of the bourgeois world.(001-138)

## Character Portrait

Heinrich von Kleist (1777–1811) was a German dramatist, poet, and novelist known for his intense, psychologically complex works such as *Penthesilea*, *The Broken Jug*, and *Michael Kohlhaas*. His literary career was marked by struggle with convention and an inability to find peace in society. Kleist's suicide has become emblematic of the romantic artist unable to bear the dissonance between inner truth and external reality — precisely the condition that Harry Haller diagnoses in himself.

Though Kleist is not physically present in the novel, his symbolic weight is substantial. Harry invokes him as part of a lineage of tormented spirits, positioning himself within a tradition of romantic despair. The reference also underscores the Germanic cultural context of Harry's intellectual world.

> "agrees neither with Herr von Kleist nor with Herr Beethoven. It is precisely a point of transition between two ages..." (001-138)

## Key Passages

Kleist is mentioned twice in the novel, both times in connection with Beethoven, suggesting that Harry views these two figures as parallel cases of romantic genius undone by their times. The association is not merely biographical but philosophical — Kleist's work dramatizes the collision between absolute moral demands and imperfect human reality, a theme central to Harry Haller's own inner conflict.
