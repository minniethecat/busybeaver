## About the Work

*Faust* is [[goethe|Johann Wolfgang von Goethe]]'s two-part tragic drama, widely regarded as the greatest work of German literature. The first part (published 1808) tells the story of the scholar Faust, who makes a pact with Mephistopheles in exchange for unlimited knowledge and worldly pleasure. The second part (published posthumously in 1832) expands into an allegorical exploration of human striving, redemption, and the nature of modern civilization.

In [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]], *Faust* is mentioned in a single but significant passage: "If Faust is treated in this way, Faust, Mephistopheles, Wagner and the rest form a unity and are more real and genuine than most of the people one meets in the flesh" (001-033). [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s assertion—that literary characters possess a reality superior to that of living people—encapsulates his alienation from the social world and his investment in the life of the imagination.

## Role in the Narrative

The novel's single explicit reference to *Faust* opens onto a larger thematic resonance. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s divided self—the man and the wolf—echoes Faust's famous lament: "Two [[soul|souls]], alas, are dwelling in my breast." [[goethe|Goethe]]'s Faust, like [[harry-haller|Haller]], is a man of immense learning who finds that knowledge alone cannot satisfy the deepest human longings. Both figures must pass through sensuality, guilt, and despair before achieving any measure of integration or redemption.

[[goethe|Goethe]] himself appears in [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] as one of the "[[immortals|Immortals]]"—a transcendent figure who has passed beyond the struggles that torment [[harry-haller|Haller]]. The presence of *Faust* in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s imaginative background thus connects the living [[goethe|Goethe]] (an Immortal who laughs at earthly concerns) with his greatest fictional creation (a man who, like [[harry-haller|Haller]], must suffer through earthly experience to achieve wisdom).

## Key Passages

> If Faust is treated in this way, Faust, Mephistopheles, Wagner and the rest form a unity and are more real and genuine than most of the people one meets in the flesh. (001-033)

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s provocative claim—that fictional characters are "more real" than living people—is a distillation of his [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] condition: he has invested so deeply in art and ideas that the ordinary social world has become spectral by comparison.

## Cultural Context

[[goethe|Goethe]] worked on *Faust* for over sixty years, from the 1770s until his death in 1832. The work spans genres (tragedy, comedy, opera, allegory) and registers (from earthy [[laughter|humor]] to sublime mysticism), embodying the range of human experience that [[harry-haller|Haller]], in his fragmented state, can only aspire to. Hesse was a lifelong admirer of [[goethe|Goethe]] and wrote extensively about him; the figure of [[goethe|Goethe]] in [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]—at once revered and treated with ironic [[laughter|humor]]—reflects Hesse's complex relationship with the towering presence of German Classicism.
