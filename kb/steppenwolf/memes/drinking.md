# Drinking

## Definition

In *[[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]]*, drinking is never simple. It functions on multiple registers simultaneously: as self-medication (the nightly [[wine]] that dulls the edge of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s despair), as social ritual (the drinks at the [[dance-halls|dance hall]] that lubricate his entry into company), and as spiritual technique (the deliberate intoxication that dissolves the boundaries of the isolated self). The novel neither condemns nor celebrates drinking; it maps its complex role in a life that has few other consolations.

## In the Novel

Drinking structures [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s day from beginning to end. The morning begins with the razor and the memory of the previous night's bottle; the afternoon passes in abstinent suffering; the evening brings the first glass, the transition from endurance to temporary relief. This rhythm is so deeply ingrained that it has become indistinguishable from [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s identity.

> "There was a straw-covered bottle usually containing Italian red wine... often, too, a bottle of Burgundy as well."

The pivotal transformation in [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s drinking occurs under [[hermine|Hermine]]'s influence. Solitary drinking — the [[wine]] in the attic room, the brandy at the tavern — gives way to social drinking, alcohol consumed in company and in service of connection rather than escape. The [[masked-ball|Masked Ball]] represents the apotheosis of this transformation: drinking becomes collective, ecstatic, a shared ritual of self-dissolution rather than a private ritual of self-destruction.

## Philosophical Background

The novel's treatment of drinking engages with a long tradition of thinking about intoxication and creativity. From the Dionysian rites of ancient Greece through Nietzsche's philosophy of ecstatic dissolution to the Parisian café culture of the 1920s, drinking has been associated with the relaxation of the ego boundaries that both protect and imprison the self. [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s trajectory — from solitary drinking as escape to social drinking as participation — echoes this tradition while insisting on its limits: drinking can open a door, but it cannot walk through it.

## Critical Reception

Critics have read the novel's treatment of drinking as part of its broader engagement with altered states of consciousness. Unlike the Beats who would later celebrate intoxication as a permanent lifestyle, Hesse presents it as a temporary measure — useful, even necessary, for someone in [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s condition, but not sufficient for the transformation he needs. The [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] represents the possibility of achieving through imagination what drinking can only approximate.
