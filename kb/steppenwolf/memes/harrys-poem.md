**Harry's Poem** is an untitled poem written by [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] in the days before his encounter with [[hermine|Hermine]] and his entry into the sensual world. The poem is a self-portrait — a literary attempt to capture the [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] condition that Harry believes defines his existence. Though the poem is mentioned multiple times in the novel, its text is never revealed to the reader.

## Context of Composition

Harry writes the poem during one of his long, solitary nights, at a time when his [[suicide|suicidal]] despair is reaching its peak. It is a period when Harry is, in his own words, "living a suicidal existence" — withdrawing from society, cultivating his loneliness, and counting the days to his fiftieth birthday, which he has set as the deadline for taking his own life (Preface, P02-042).

The poem thus belongs to the "old" Harry — the Harry who still believes in the wolf-man [[duality]], who takes himself with tragic seriousness, and who has not yet learned to [[laughter|laugh]].

## Relation to the Treatise

Harry's poem exists in parallel with the [[treatise-on-the-steppenwolf|Treatise on the Steppenwolf]], the anonymous pamphlet Harry acquires from the sign-carrier on the night of his first walk through the [[old-town|Old Town]]. While the Treatise is an external, objective, and ultimately humorous analysis of the Steppenwolf type, Harry's poem is a subjective, lyrical, and tragic expression of the same condition.

> "And both these pictures of myself, my dispirited and halting poem and the clever study by an unknown hand, equally afflicted me." (001-035)

The poem and the Treatise together form a diptych of self-knowledge: the poem is how Harry sees himself from within, while the Treatise is how a detached observer — perhaps one of the [[immortals|Immortals]] — would describe him.

## In the Narrative

After reading the Treatise, Harry recalls his poem and compares the two works:

> "When I had read to the end it came to my mind that some weeks before I had written one night a rather peculiar poem, likewise about the Steppenwolf." (001-034)

The poem lies on Harry's bedside table alongside the Treatise, and both serve as mirrors in which Harry confronts his own image. The poem represents his own voice — halting, dispirited, but authentic — while the Treatise represents the voice of the Immortals: wise, detached, humorous.

## Significance

Though the poem's text remains hidden from the reader, its presence in the narrative serves an important function. It establishes Harry as a creative being, not merely a consumer of culture. Harry is not only a reader of [[goethe|Goethe]] and [[novalis|Novalis]] and a listener of [[mozart|Mozart]]; he is himself a maker, however modest. This creative dimension of Harry's character — his writing, his painting (watercolours mentioned in the Preface, P02-023) — is the seed of the transformation that Hermine will cultivate.

The poem also marks the beginning of Harry's self-examination, a process that the Treatise will deepen and that the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] will bring to its ultimate conclusion.
