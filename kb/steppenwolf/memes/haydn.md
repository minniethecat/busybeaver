## In the Novel

Joseph Haydn is referenced multiple times in Hermann Hesse's *Steppenwolf* as part of Harry Haller's pantheon of classical composers. Harry, a man who defines himself through high culture, carries the complete works of Haydn — along with Bach — in his head as a spiritual possession. This internal library represents Harry's refuge from the degraded modern world of jazz, radio, and mass entertainment.(001-051)

The narrator of the preface lists Haydn among those figures — "Socrates, Mozart and Haydn, Dante and Goethe" — who represent true cultural greatness. Harry's reverence for Haydn positions the composer as emblematic of an older, nobler artistic tradition that Harry sees threatened by modernity.(001-316)

> "the complete works of Bach and Haydn in my head and could say the most profound things about them" (001-316)

Harry's near-religious devotion to Haydn's music serves a dual function: it is both a genuine aesthetic attachment and a defensive posture, a way of rejecting contemporary life. The narrator notes that Harry's love for music is "a ready-at-hand religion," a substitute for genuine engagement with others.(001-321)

## Character Portrait

Joseph Haydn (1732–1809) was one of the most celebrated composers of the Classical period, often called the "Father of the Symphony" and "Father of the String Quartet." In *Steppenwolf*, Haydn symbolizes the order, clarity, and formal perfection of the Viennese classical tradition — qualities that appeal to Harry's intellectual nature.

Haydn is less frequently discussed in the novel than Mozart, but his inclusion alongside Bach and Mozart completes a musical trinity representing the summit of German-Austrian classical music. Where Mozart represents genius and transcendence, Haydn represents craft, cheerfulness, and formal mastery — qualities that Harry, in his despair, longs for but cannot attain.

> "your putting Mozart and Haydn and 'Valencia' on what levels" (001-321)

## Key Passages

Haydn appears in four passages across the novel, often in lists of great composers. His complete works form part of Harry's internal musical library (001-316). In the Magic Theater, Mozart teasingly questions Harry about the comparative value he places on Mozart, Haydn, and popular music (001-321), exposing Harry's cultural snobbery. Haydn's relationship to Bach is also noted, with Harry cherishing the "old beloved way" from Buxtehude through Pachelbel, Bach, and Haydn (001-325).

> "Buxtehude, Pachelbel, Bach and Haydn. I had gone the old beloved way" (001-325)
