# Music

## Definition

In *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]*, music functions as more than an aesthetic preference — it is a portal to transcendence. For [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]], classical music (especially [[mozart|Mozart]]) represents the highest spiritual achievement, a realm where the conflicts and trivialities of everyday existence are suspended and the [[soul]] makes contact with the immortal. Music stands in stark opposition to the [[jazz]] and popular entertainment of the modern world, which [[harry-haller|Haller]] experiences as raw, chaotic, and barbaric.

## In the Novel

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s relationship with music reveals the fundamental tension of his dual nature. Classical music nourishes the "man" — the cultivated, spiritual aspect of his being. [[jazz|Jazz]], by contrast, appeals to something primitive and bodily that he both despises and finds seductively vital:

> I let the sounds, as raw and warm as the steam of raw flesh, flood over me. It was strange and yet not wholly alien. This music was the music of decline. ... But another note was there — a note of sincerity, of an honest, childlike primitiveness. (001-021–001-022)

The novel's most ecstatic passages often occur in the presence of music. In a memory that [[harry-haller|Haller]] treasures as a rare moment of epiphany, music itself becomes the door to the divine:

> It was at a concert of lovely old music. After two or three notes of the piano the door was opened of a sudden to the other world. I sped through heaven and saw God at work. I suffered holy pains. I dropped all my defences and was afraid of nothing in the world. (001-008)

This experience — fleeting, unrepeatable, yet unforgettable — establishes music as the authentic religious experience for the secular, intellectual [[harry-haller|Haller]]. It is the closest he comes to mystical union.

The [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] sequence brings music to its fullest symbolic expression. [[mozart|Mozart]] himself appears — not as the historical composer but as one of [[immortals|the Immortals]], teaching [[harry-haller|Haller]] the art of [[laughter]]. The music of the spheres becomes literal: "star bright is our eternal [[laughter]]" (001-627). Music and [[laughter]] merge into a single transcendent principle, the wisdom [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] so desperately needs to learn.

## Musical Polarities

The novel organizes [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s musical experience around a series of polarities:

| Classical | [[jazz|Jazz]]/Modern |
|-----------|------------|
| Eternal, timeless | Ephemeral, of-the-moment |
| Spiritual transcendence | Bodily sensuality |
| Order and beauty | Raw energy and decline |
| [[mozart|Mozart]], [[goethe|Goethe]] | [[dance|Dance]] halls, radios |
| The [[immortals|Immortals]]' realm | Bourgeois entertainment |

Yet [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] subtly undermines this rigid opposition. [[harry-haller|Haller]] is drawn to [[jazz]] despite himself. [[pablo|Pablo]] [[pablo|the saxophonist]], who seems a mere purveyor of sensual pleasures, is later revealed as one of [[immortals|the Immortals]] — a master of the "music of the personality" who orchestrates [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s psychic disassembly in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]]. The lesson is not that classical music is good and [[jazz]] is bad, but that [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s rigid aesthetic categories are themselves symptoms of [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s splitting tendency.

## Philosophical Background

Hesse's elevation of music echoes Schopenhauer's metaphysics, in which music is the highest art because it directly expresses the Will — the noumenal reality behind all appearances — rather than merely representing phenomena. It also reflects the German Romantic tradition ([[novalis|Novalis]], E.T.A. Hoffmann, early Nietzsche) that saw music as the art of the infinite, uniquely capable of expressing what cannot be put into words.

Nietzsche's *The Birth of Tragedy* is particularly relevant. The Apollonian/Dionysian distinction — between form-giving beauty and ecstatic dissolution — maps onto [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s classical/[[jazz]] split. The novel's resolution involves transcending this dichotomy entirely, learning to find the divine in both [[mozart|Mozart]]'s crystalline order and [[pablo|Pablo]]'s sensuous chaos.
