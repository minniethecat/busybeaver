# Courtesan

## Definition

In *[[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]]*, prostitution appears without the moral judgment that a more conventional novel would attach to it. The women [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] encounters at the [[dance-halls|dance halls]] and the [[masked-ball|Masked Ball]] — some professional, some amateur — are part of the sensual economy that [[hermine|Hermine]] orchestrates as part of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s education. The novel treats paid love not as sin but as one of the many forms of human exchange, no more corrupt than the emotional transactions of [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] marriage.

## In the Novel

The figure of the courtesan enters the narrative through [[hermine|Hermine]], who moves easily through a world where sexuality and commerce overlap. She introduces [[harry-haller|Harry]] to women — including [[maria|Maria]] — whose company is both paid for and genuinely given, blurring the line between transaction and affection. [[harry-haller|Harry]], whose sexual life has been confined to conventional channels, is initially disoriented by this world, but he learns to accept it as part of the larger education in [[sensuality]] that [[hermine|Hermine]] prescribes.

The courtesan figures in the novel are not victims; they are agents, skilled practitioners of an art that [[harry-haller|Harry]] must learn to appreciate. [[maria|Maria]], in particular, is presented as a fully realized character whose profession is one aspect of a complex personality rather than a defining stigma.

## Cultural Context

Hesse's treatment of prostitution reflects the relative openness of Weimar-era German culture, where sexual commerce was legal and regulated. But it also serves the novel's larger philosophical project: the courtesan, like the [[saxophone]] player and the [[dance]] instructor, represents a mode of expertise that [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s intellectual culture has dismissed. Learning to value her art is part of learning to value the body and its pleasures.

## Critical Reception

Critics have noted the novel's unusually non-judgmental treatment of prostitution as part of its broader challenge to [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] morality. The courtesan is not a figure of degradation but of competence — someone who has mastered a domain of experience that [[harry-haller|Harry]] has neglected. In the novel's sensual curriculum, she is a teacher, not a cautionary tale.
