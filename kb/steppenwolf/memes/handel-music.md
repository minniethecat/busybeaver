**Handel's Music** appears in [[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]] through a symphony concert that the narrator of the [[Preface]] attends, at which he observes [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] in a rare moment of aesthetic transport. The unnamed Handel piece (likely one of the oratorio or orchestral suites) serves as a gateway to the "other world" that Harry glimpses in his fleeting hours of joy.

## The Concert Scene

The narrator encounters Harry by chance at a symphony concert in the town. The programme consists of three works:

> "First some Handel was played, noble and lovely music." (P02-036)

During the Handel, Harry sits "absorbed in his own thoughts, detached alike from the music and his surroundings. Unheeding and alone, he sat with downcast eyes, and a cold but sorrowful expression" (P02-036). The music does not yet reach him; he is too deeply enclosed in his own melancholy.

What follows is revealing: it is the second piece — a "little Symphony of Friedman Bach" — that breaks through Harry's defences. At the first notes of the Bach, Harry begins to smile and "give himself up to the music," transported into "pleasant dreams" that last the entire ten-minute performance (P02-036).

The final piece, Reger's *Variations*, finds Harry once again withdrawn, "sadly and finally irritated," his face "vacant and grey" (P02-036). The progression — Handel (unmoved), Bach (transported), Reger (irritated) — maps Harry's spiritual condition: he can be reached only by music of a particular kind, and the window of connection is narrow and fleeting.

## Handel in Harry's Inner World

Although it is the Bach that actually moves Harry at the concert, the narrator names Handel first and with the epithet "noble and lovely." Handel, for the German-speaking world of Hesse's time, represented the summit of Baroque sacred music — a music of architectural grandeur and spiritual assurance that stands in stark contrast to the "raw and savage gaiety" of the [[jazz]] Harry will later encounter in the dance halls.

Handel's music belongs to the world of the [[immortals|Immortals]] — the eternal spirits of great artists who have transcended their personal destinies. It is music that, in Harry's own words about such experiences, opens "the door to the other world" where one can "speed through heaven and see God at work" (001-008).

## The Golden Track

The concert experience exemplifies what Harry calls the [[golden-track|golden track]] — those rare moments when a glimmer of the divine breaks through the grey monotony of everyday existence:

> "It did not last very long, a quarter of an hour perhaps; but it returned to me in a dream at night, and since, through all the barren days, I caught a glimpse of it now and then. Sometimes for a minute or two I saw it clearly, threading my life like a divine and golden track." (001-008)

Handel's music, even when it fails to move Harry directly, belongs to this constellation of revelatory experiences — a reminder that the world of the spirit exists, however inaccessible it may seem during the long stretches of ordinary life.
