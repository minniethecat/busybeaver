**Harry's Attic Room** is the rented lodging where [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] lives during the period covered by the novel and its [[Preface|prefatory narrative]]. Situated on the top floor of a respectable middle-class house owned by the narrator's aunt, the room functions as both a physical refuge and a symbolic expression of Harry's inner life — a space of cultivated disorder suspended above the tidy bourgeois world below.

## Location

The room occupies the attic of a three-storey house in a quiet suburban neighbourhood of the unnamed town. The house, "let as three flats to highly respectable families" (001-006), belongs to the world of the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeoisie]] that Harry simultaneously despises and secretly craves. Harry lives on the top floor — physically above the respectable families but still within their sphere, a spatial metaphor for his ambivalent relationship to conventional life.

The narrator, who lives in an adjoining room, observes Harry's comings and goings and the gradual transformation of the attic space from an ordinary rented room into a distinctive personal environment.

## The Room's Contents

The room fills rapidly with the detritus of Harry's intellectual and sensual life:

**Books**: A large bookcase and piles on every available surface — the sofa, the bureau, the table, the chairs, the floor. The collection spans "the poets of all times and peoples," with well-worn volumes of [[goethe|Goethe]], [[novalis|Novalis]], Jean Paul, Lessing, Jacobi, Lichtenberg, and a complete edition of [[dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] "bristling with pencilled slips" (P02-023). The six volumes of *[[sophias-journey|Sophia's Journey from Memel to Saxony]]* lie on the sofa where Harry spends whole days reading.

**Art**: Photographs and pictures cover the walls — a southern landscape, photographs of Harry's home town, brightly painted watercolours that Harry himself painted, a photograph of a pretty young woman (later identified as [[hermine|Hermine]]), a Siamese Buddha replaced by Michelangelo's *Night* replaced by a portrait of Mahatma Gandhi (P02-023).

**Wine and Tobacco**: A straw-covered bottle of Italian red wine, occasionally supplemented by Burgundy, Malaga, and a squat bottle of Cherry brandy. The "all-pervading smell of cigar-smoke" and the "stumps and ash of cigars all about the room" testify to Harry's habits (P02-023).

**Paint Box**: A paint box, "generally full of dust," reposing among cigar ash — evidence of the creative impulse that survives but lies neglected in Harry's life of passive consumption and withdrawal (P02-023).

## Symbolism

The room embodies Harry's [[duality|divided]] nature. Like its occupant, it occupies a liminal space — perched above the orderly bourgeois household yet not fully separate from it. The narrator notes the contrast between "this middle-class family-life" with its "odour of quiet and order, of cleanliness and respectable domesticity" and the "disorder and neglect" of Harry's room, "where everything — books, manuscript, thoughts — is marked and saturated with the plight of lonely men" (001-006).

The room also reveals Harry's hidden creative life. The watercolours he paints, though neglected under dust, and the [[harrys-poem|poem]] he writes about the Steppenwolf show that Harry is not merely a consumer of culture but a maker — a capacity that [[hermine|Hermine]] will later cultivate through her programme of sensual re-education.

The ever-changing decorations on the walls — Buddha, Michelangelo, Gandhi — suggest a spiritual restlessness, a search for meaning that cycles through traditions without finding a permanent home in any of them. This impermanence mirrors Harry's own homelessness, his status as a "wolf of the Steppes that had lost its way and strayed into the towns and the life of the herd" (P02-035).

## In the Narrative

The attic room is the base from which Harry ventures into the nocturnal world of the town and to which he returns after his encounters with Hermine, the [[dance-halls|dance halls]], and eventually the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]]. It is the setting for his reading of the [[treatise-on-the-steppenwolf|Treatise]], his composition of the poem, and his long nights of solitary drinking and reflection.

At the novel's end, Harry vanishes from the town, leaving behind only his manuscript. The attic room, emptied of its occupant, reverts to being an ordinary space — but the narrator, and the reader, know what transpired within its walls.
