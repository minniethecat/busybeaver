# Garden

## Definition

In *[[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]]*, the garden — specifically the "little garden of order" with its [[araucaria]] tree at the center — functions as the master symbol of [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] domesticity. It represents everything [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] simultaneously longs for and despises: cleanliness, regularity, propriety, the quiet cultivation of small pleasures. The garden is the spatial expression of the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] world — bounded and cultivated, a nature tamed into respectability.

## In the Novel

The garden first appears in the boarding house where [[harry-haller|Harry]] rents his attic room. On the first floor landing stands the [[araucaria]], and outside stretches a garden that embodies the values of the house:

> "I contemplate this little garden of order and let the touching air of its respectability move me, together with its somewhat lonely and ridiculous atmosphere of solitude and the whole silly but still touching love of a little advance post of cleanliness and order." (001-006)

The garden recurs as a site of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s ambivalence. He is drawn to its quiet order — it reminds him of his [[childhood]] home — yet he recognizes that this very order is what he cannot inhabit. The [[steppenwolf]] in him yearns to trample it; the man in him yearns to rest within it. This double movement — toward and away from the garden — defines [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s relationship to the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois]] world throughout the novel.

## Symbolic Significance

The garden-as-symbol draws on a long literary tradition. The Garden of Eden represents prelapsarian wholeness; the enclosed garden (*hortus conclusus*) of medieval poetry represents spiritual purity; the cultivated garden of the Enlightenment represents reason's triumph over wild nature. Hesse layers all these associations into [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s boarding-house garden, making it at once a paradise he has lost, a purity he cannot claim, and an order he cannot accept.

## Critical Reception

Critics have noted the garden's function as a spatial correlative of [[harry-haller|Harry]]'s psychological state. The garden is not described in sensuous detail — we never smell its flowers or feel its soil — because [[harry-haller|Harry]] experiences it not as a physical place but as an idea, a projection of his own conflicted longing for and rejection of order.
