# Mozart

## In the Novel

Mozart appears in the final sections of the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] sequence as an Immortal — a being who has transcended mortal suffering and dwells in a realm of pure spirit. He is no longer the historical composer but a presence of serene, amused wisdom. His [[laughter]] — "still and soundless" (001-635) — is [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s supreme spiritual value.

Mozart is present throughout [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] as a musical ideal before he appears as a character. [[harry-haller|Haller]] listens to Mozart's music in moments of spiritual respite, "sipping the cool and noble music as gods sip nectar" (001-019), and the narrator notes that he "wanted to hear Mozart, to read poetry and to cherish human ideals" (001-033). For [[harry-haller|Haller]], Mozart's music represents a standard of purity against which all other art — and all of modern life — is measured and found wanting.

## The Radio Lesson

Mozart confronts [[harry-haller|Haller]] at a moment of crisis. [[harry-haller|Haller]] has just "killed" [[hermine|Hermine]] in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] (fulfilling her prophetic request) and is facing judgment for the murder. Mozart deflates the drama: this was not a real murder but a symbolic act, a picture in the Theater of the mind. "You have done with [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] at last. It's no good with a razor, that's only murder" (001-472). The real death was not [[hermine|Hermine]]'s but [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s attachment to his own suffering.

Mozart's teaching method is paradoxical and [[laughter|humor]]ous. He heckles [[harry-haller|Haller]], laughs at his earnestness, and demonstrates his own lesson by example rather than argument. The central teaching device is the radio: Mozart turns on a cheap wireless set playing [[handel-music|Handel]]'s music, distorted and crackling through the primitive technology, and insists that [[harry-haller|Haller]] listen:

> "The devil take your radio! ... Do you hear the basses? They stride like gods. And let this Handel shake-up enter your ears and your heart, this ridiculous trumpet that sets the divine music going behind the idiotic surface!" (001-635)

When [[harry-haller|Haller]] protests that the radio is destroying the music, Mozart laughs and delivers his final teaching: "You have heard your sentence. So, you see, you will have to learn to listen to more of the wireless music of life. It'll do you good. You are uncommonly poor in gifts, a poor blockhead" (001-645). The lesson is double: first, the sacred persists even in degradation; second, [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s demand that the sacred appear only in pristine form is itself a form of spiritual arrogance.

## Mozart and Pablo

In a final transformation, Mozart reveals his identity: "And as he spoke and conjured up a cigarette from his waistcoat pocket and offered it me, he was suddenly Mozart no longer. It was my friend Pablo looking warmly at me" (001-653). The revelation that Mozart and [[pablo|Pablo]] are one — that the Immortal composer and the jazz saxophonist are the same being — is [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s most radical teaching. [[harry-haller|Haller]] has spent the entire novel distinguishing high art ([[mozart|Mozart]]) from low entertainment ([[pablo|Pablo]]'s [[jazz]]), spirit from sensuality, the eternal from the ephemeral. Mozart-Pablo's identity collapses all these distinctions into a single, laughing presence.

This reveals that [[pablo|Pablo]] was never merely a "purveyor of pleasure" but always already an [[immortals|Immortal]], and that Mozart's [[music]] was never merely "high art" but always already sensuous and bodily. The categories [[harry-haller|Haller]] used to organize his world — [[duality|man/wolf]], spirit/flesh, art/entertainment — were the walls of his own prison.

## The Laughter

Mozart's defining quality is his [[laughter]]. Throughout the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] sequence, [[harry-haller|Haller]] hears Mozart's "soundless laughter," "all-knowing and all-mocking" (001-636). This laughter is not cruel or dismissive. It is the natural expression of a being who has seen through the comedy of selfhood and the pretensions of suffering. Mozart tells [[harry-haller|Haller]]: "You take the old Goethe much too seriously, my young friend. You should not take old people who are already dead seriously" (001-143) — and by extension, [[harry-haller|Haller]] should not take *himself* so seriously either.

## Cultural Context

Hesse's choice of Mozart as [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s supreme Immortal is significant. Mozart represented, for German culture, the union of formal perfection and emotional depth — [[music]] that is simultaneously structured and ecstatic, Apollonian and Dionysian. In contrast to [[wagner|Wagner]] (the nineteenth century's dominant musical deity), Mozart offered a model of transcendence through lightness rather than grandeur, [[laughter]] rather than pathos.

This aligns with [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s broader critique of German earnestness. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s tragedy is not that he suffers too much but that he suffers too seriously. Mozart's [[laughter]] — and the [[music]] that embodies it — is the antidote: transcendence without pomposity, wisdom without solemnity, the sacred as play.
