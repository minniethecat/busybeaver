## About the Work

*Sophia's Journey from Memel to Saxony* (*Sophiens Reise von Memel nach Sachsen*) is an eighteenth-century German novel by Johann Timotheus Hermes, first published in five volumes between 1769 and 1773. It was one of the most widely read German novels of its era—a sentimental, epistolary narrative of the virtuous Sophia's adventures and eventual marriage. By the twentieth century, it had become a literary curiosity, remembered more for its historical significance than for its artistic merit.

In [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]], [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s possession of this work is noted in the Treatise's catalogue of his library: "volumes of a work with the title Sophia's Journey from Memel to Saxony" [P02-023]. On the night of the disastrous dinner at the professor's house, [[harry-haller|Haller]] "lost himself for a moment in Sophia's Journey from Memel to Saxony" (001-051)—a telling detail that reveals his impulse to retreat into familiar literary comfort when social reality becomes unbearable.

## Role in the Narrative

The inclusion of *Sophia's Journey* in [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s library serves multiple functions. First, it establishes [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s bookish character—his tendency to "lose himself" in books rather than engage with the world (001-051). Second, [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s age and obscurity mark [[harry-haller|Haller]] as a man of antiquarian tastes, attached to the literary past rather than the cultural present. Third, the sentimental, morally conventional nature of Hermes's novel contrasts sharply with the existential radicalism of [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] itself—an ironic juxtaposition typical of Hesse's method.

The detail that [[harry-haller|Haller]] turns to this book during the excruciating evening at the professor's house is particularly revealing: when social performance becomes impossible, [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s instinct is to disappear into the printed page.

The Treatise's inventory of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s library includes this curious eighteenth-century title, mapping [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s intellectual identity through the physical evidence of his book collection.

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s reflexive retreat into an obscure eighteenth-century novel during a social crisis captures his characteristic response to discomfort: withdrawal into literature.

## Cultural Context

Johann Timotheus Hermes (1738–1821) was a German novelist and Protestant theologian. *Sophia's Journey* was his most famous work—a sprawling sentimental novel in the tradition of Samuel Richardson, enormously popular in its day but largely forgotten by Hesse's time. Its presence in [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] is a characteristically precise detail: [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s library contains not only the canonical greats ([[goethe|Goethe]], [[novalis|Novalis]]) but also the once-popular works that a genuine bibliophile would have collected.
