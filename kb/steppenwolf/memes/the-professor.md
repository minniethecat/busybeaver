## In the Novel

The Professor appears early in [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s records, encountered "by the Library" where Haller meets "a young professor of whom in earlier years I used to see a good deal" (001-050). Their reunion leads to a dinner invitation that becomes one of the novel's pivotal early scenes—the occasion of Haller's decisive break with bourgeois society. The Professor, "glad enough" to see Haller, greets him warmly and invites him home, where Haller's evening unravels into disaster (001-053).

The central incident of this encounter is the Professor's "print of Goethe"—a sentimental portrait of the poet as "an old man full of character, with a maned head and a handsome face" in a "somewhat theatrically furnished drawing room" (001-052). Haller finds the picture unbearable, seeing in it a betrayal of everything [[goethe|Goethe]] truly represents. The evening becomes a torment: "thus locked in conflict, the professor was almost forgotten" as Haller spirals into his own thoughts (001-051).

The climax comes when the Professor "spoke of Haller as a traitor to his country" (001-054), expressing a conventional nationalist sentiment that Haller finds not merely wrong but spiritually repugnant. Haller flees: "I took leave of Goethe and of the professor. I seized my hat and coat" (001-060), his departure marking the moment he definitively severs his connection to academic bourgeois society.

## Character Portrait

The Professor embodies the respectable intellectual establishment that [[harry-haller|Haller]] once belonged to and has now outgrown—or fallen away from. He is described as cordial, well-meaning, and entirely conventional in his tastes and opinions. His drawing room, with its theatrical Goethe portrait, represents everything Haller despises about the bourgeois appropriation of culture: sentimentality passing as appreciation, nationalism disguised as patriotism.

Haller's own later reflections confirm his alienation from the Professor's world: "I didn't see eye to eye with the professor. Like nearly all professors, he was a patriot" (001-107). The Professor's patriotic sentiment—which to him would seem natural and unobjectionable—strikes Haller as an irreconcilable offense, the final proof that the academic world he once inhabited offers him no home.

The Professor reappears only in memory. Pablo, in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]], refers to "that night when he visited the professor and sat through the dance" (001-618), framing the Professor's dinner as the starting point of Haller's long journey toward transformation. In [[hermine|Hermine]]'s later mockery—"You carried on in a silly way, just like a professor" (001-094)—the word "professor" becomes an epithet, shorthand for everything pedantic and lifeless that Haller must shed.

## Key Passages

> I met a young professor of whom in earlier years I used to see a good deal. (001-050)

The casual reunion that sets in motion Haller's decisive break with bourgeois intellectual life.

> He spoke of Haller as a traitor to his country. (001-054)

The moment when the Professor's conventional patriotism makes Haller's alienation irreparable.

> I took leave of Goethe and of the professor. I seized my hat and coat. (001-060)

Haller's abrupt departure, coupling Goethe with the Professor as representatives of a world he must escape.

## Relationships

The Professor's only significant relationship is to **[[harry-haller]]**, whom he once knew as a colleague and now hosts as a dinner guest—only to lose him when Haller's disgust at bourgeois culture becomes unbearable. The Professor serves as a foil to **[[pablo]]**, the saxophonist and sensualist who represents everything the academic world is not. His Goethe print directly connects him to **[[goethe]]**, the immortal poet whose true spirit Haller believes the Professor's sentimental portrait betrays.
