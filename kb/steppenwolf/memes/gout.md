# Gout

## Definition

Gout in Hesse's *[[steppenwolf-novel|Steppenwolf]]* is never merely a medical condition. It is the [[body]]'s insistent voice, the physical manifestation of a deeper spiritual and existential malaise. [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]]'s gout attacks punctuate the narrative as moments when the [[body]] refuses to be ignored — when the aches of the flesh interrupt the life of the mind and demand acknowledgment.

The illness functions as a metaphor for the cost of the intellectual life: the [[body]], long neglected in favor of books and ideas, exacts its revenge in the form of [[pain]]. Gout is the somatic expression of [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s divided existence, the inflammation of the joints mirroring the friction between spirit and flesh, [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois order]] and wolf-like instinct.

## In the Novel

Gout appears as one of the persistent physical complaints woven through [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s narrative of self-description:

> I had an attack of gout and lay in bed. The landlady brought me tea and the morning paper. I suffered pain in every joint and cursed my life, which was nothing but pain and sickness and despair. (001-006)

The gout is inseparable from [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s broader pattern of self-medication and escape. He treats his attacks with pills washed down with [[wine]] or brandy — a characteristically ambivalent response that blurs the line between treatment and [[intoxication]]:

> I took the pills the doctor had given me, with a glass of brandy, and lay down again, waiting for the pain to subside. I was fifty years old, and my body was a ruin. What had I done with my life? Nothing. Nothing but think and read and suffer. (001-007)

The gout attacks become associated with particular states of being: [[isolation]], forced inactivity, the unwelcome confrontation with the [[aging]] [[body]]. They are the moments when [[harry-haller|Haller]] cannot read, cannot walk the streets, cannot seek distraction — the moments when he is thrown back upon himself with nothing but [[pain]] for company. Yet these attacks are also, paradoxically, moments of enforced stillness that sometimes yield insight. The [[pain]] of gout, like the [[pain]] of [[isolation]], is part of what drives [[harry-haller|Haller]] toward [[suicide|suicidal]] contemplation — and part of what, in the novel's dialectic, eventually drives him toward transformation.

## Key Passages

Gout punctuates the narrative in ways that deepen [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s portrait:

- **Initial Confession** — The gout attack that confines [[harry-haller|Haller]] to bed, accompanied by the daily [[newspaper]] and the landlady's tea — a tableau of the intellectual in physical collapse.
- **Self-Medication** — [[harry-haller|Haller]] treats gout with pills and brandy, a fusion of medical and intoxicating substances that mirrors his larger strategy for managing existence.
- **Mortality Confrontation** — The gout compels [[harry-haller|Haller]] to confront his own [[aging]] and physical deterioration, the gap between his fifty-year-old [[body]] and his younger intellectual self-image.
- **Stasis and Reflection** — The enforced stillness of a gout attack becomes, despite itself, a space for the reflection that [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] prizes — the compulsory pause that the restless mind would never choose.

## Philosophical Background

The gout as metaphor draws on a long tradition of linking physical illness to spiritual and intellectual states:

1. **Nietzsche's Philosophy of the Body** — Nietzsche, a constant presence in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]'s intellectual background, insisted that philosophy must begin with the [[body]]. "There is more reason in your body than in your best wisdom," Zarathustra declares. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s gout is the [[body]]'s reason asserting itself against the intellect's pretensions — the wisdom of the flesh that [[hermine|Hermine]] will later teach him to honor.

2. **Hesse's Own Illness** — Hesse suffered from various physical ailments throughout his life, including periods of severe pain that forced withdrawal from intellectual work. The gout in *Steppenwolf* is in part autobiographical — Hesse knew intimately the experience of the [[body]]'s rebellion against the spirit's demands.

3. **The Mind-Body Problem** — [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s gout literalizes the mind-body dualism that [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] diagnoses and deconstructs. The gout is the [[body]]'s way of saying "I exist" to a man who wishes he could live entirely in the mind. It is the revenge of the physical on the intellectual, the flesh's counter-argument to the spirit's claim of primacy.

4. **Asceticism and Its Costs** — The gout can be read as the physical cost of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s ascetic intellectualism. The [[body]], starved of [[dance]], [[sensuality]], and physical joy, produces [[pain]] as its only remaining language. [[hermine|Hermine]]'s teaching — that the [[body]] must be fed, moved, loved — is the cure for the [[sickness]] that gout represents.

The gout is thus not merely a plot detail but a philosophical argument: the [[body]] will not be silenced, and the attempt to live as pure spirit is not only impossible but self-destructive.

## Critical Reception

The gout has been interpreted as a crucial element of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s characterization:

> Haller's gout is Hesse's reminder that the Steppenwolf is not merely a spiritual condition but an embodied one. The aching joints ground Haller's metaphysical suffering in physical reality, preventing the reader — and Haller himself — from treating his crisis as purely intellectual. The body suffers too, and its suffering is a kind of truth. (Joseph Mileck, *Hermann Hesse: Life and Art*, 1978)

> The gout functions in Steppenwolf as what Susan Sontag would later call the 'punitive metaphor' of illness — the body's affliction as judgment on the soul's errors. But Hesse complicates this: Haller's gout is also the body's cry for attention, its demand to be included in the project of living. The cure is not medicine but dance. (Eugene L. Stelzig, *Hermann Hesse's Fictions of the Self*, 1988)

The gout's specific character — an illness historically associated with overindulgence and the good life — creates an ironic counterpoint to [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s ascetic intellectualism. He has the disease of kings and gourmands while living the life of a hermit, suggesting that [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s relationship to pleasure is more complex than he acknowledges.
