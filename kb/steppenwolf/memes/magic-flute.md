**The Magic Flute** (Die Zauberflöte, K. 620) is [[mozart|Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart]]'s final opera, premiered in Vienna in 1791. In [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]], it is a touchstone of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s [[music]]al and spiritual life — the work that, more than any other, represents the synthesis of sensuous pleasure and metaphysical meaning that he hungers for.

## Hermine's Challenge

The Magic Flute enters the narrative through [[hermine|Hermine]], who selects it deliberately to probe [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s relationship with pleasure and art. During an evening in his apartment, she puts on a gramophone record:

> She put on another record of a Mozart opera — it was the Magic Flute, I believe — and then I experienced how enchantingly and strangely the lovely and beloved music could go through another's movements, and, mingling with them, acquire a new and wonderful grace. (001-136)

When [[harry-haller|Haller]] expresses a "strong objection" to what he perceives as the profanation of [[mozart|Mozart]]'s [[music]] through [[dance]], [[hermine|Hermine]] pushes back:

> The Magic Flute presents life to us as a wondrous song. It honours our feelings, transient as they are, just as it honours those that are eternal and sublime... Are you to say that you have a strong objection to the Magic Flute of Mozart? [001-136–001-138]

[[hermine|Hermine]]'s reading of the opera is a **direct challenge to [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s aesthetic purism**. For [[harry-haller|Haller]], great [[music]] belongs to a realm of eternal truth, separate from and superior to the transient pleasures of the body. For [[hermine|Hermine]], the Magic Flute's greatness lies precisely in its capacity to embrace **both** — [[eternity|the eternal]] and the ephemeral, the sublime and the sensual.

## Haller's Confession

Herme's question lands with unusual force. [[harry-haller|Haller]], who is rarely emotionally vulnerable, confesses:

> You hit of all things on the Magic Flute that is dearer to me than anything else in the world. (001-139)

This admission is significant. Throughout [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]], [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s relationship with [[music]] is the one sustained source of genuine feeling in his otherwise desiccated emotional life. The Magic Flute — with its Masonic symbolism, its journey from darkness to light, its union of the comic (Papageno) and the sublime (Sarastro) — represents the **wholeness** that [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s [[duality|man/wolf split]] denies him.

## Later Echoes

The Magic Flute recurs as a motif. During the [[dance]] lessons with [[hermine|Hermine]], [[harry-haller|Haller]] finds that "a melody from the Magic Flute or the Matthew Passion" (001-317) rises unbidden in his mind, mingling with the [[jazz]] and popular tunes of the [[dance]] hall. This collision of high and low, sacred and profane, is exactly the synthesis that [[hermine|Hermine]] has been trying to teach him:

> Whether it's 'Yearning' or the Magic Flute or 'Valencia.' But where do they come from, these melodies? (001-318)

The question "where do they come from?" is central. For [[harry-haller|Haller]], the Magic Flute's melodies are not merely beautiful sounds — they are **messengers from the other world**, traces of the "golden track" (001-008) that occasionally breaks through the surface of ordinary life.

## Relation to Don Giovanni

The Magic Flute and [[don-giovanni|Don Giovanni]] function as complementary symbols in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]]. [[don-giovanni|Don Giovanni]] represents the classical synthesis of comedy and tragedy, the human and the divine, viewed through the lens of the [[magic-theater|Magic Theatre]]'s visionary chaos. The Magic Flute represents a gentler ideal — the opera as "wondrous song" that honours all feelings, transient and eternal alike.

Both works are [[mozart|Mozart]]'s, and both are central to [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s inner life. But where [[don-giovanni|Don Giovanni]] appears in a hallucinatory vision, the Magic Flute appears in the intimate space of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s apartment — played on a gramophone, [[dance]]d to by [[hermine|Hermine]]. It is the more human, more accessible face of [[mozart|Mozart]]'s genius.
