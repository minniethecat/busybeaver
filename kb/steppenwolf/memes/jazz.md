# Jazz

## Definition

Jazz appears in *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]* as the [[music]]al embodiment of twentieth-century modernity — raw, improvisational, bodily, and democratic. For [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]], jazz is a site of profound ambivalence. He hears in it the "[[music]] of decline," the soundtrack of a culture he despises. Yet he is also irresistibly drawn to its vitality, its honesty, its freedom from the cerebral pretensions that imprison him. Jazz represents everything [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] cannot integrate: sensuality without guilt, community without hierarchy, pleasure without justification.

## In the Novel

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s first encounter with jazz occurs as he wanders the night streets, drawn by the sounds emanating from a [[dance]] hall:

> From a dance-hall there met me as I passed the strains of lively jazz music, hot and raw as the steam of raw flesh. I stopped a moment. This kind of music, though I detested it, had always had a secret charm for me. It was the music of decline. (001-021)

The language is telling: jazz is "hot and raw," like meat, like life itself before it has been refined and sanitized. [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s "detestation" is inseparable from attraction — [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] despises what he secretly craves. The [[music]] appeals to something in him that the cultivated "man" wishes to deny: the wolf's hunger for the unrefined, the immediate, the real.

Jazz's primary representative in [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] is [[pablo|Pablo]], [[pablo|the saxophonist]] who initially seems the epitome of superficial modern entertainment but is gradually revealed as one of [[immortals|the Immortals]]. When [[hermine|Hermine]] first mentions [[pablo|Pablo]], [[harry-haller|Haller]] dismisses him: "I was not very interested in what she told me about him. A jazz musician did not impress me" (001-236). This dismissal is ironic, for [[pablo|Pablo]] will become [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s guide into the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] — the very realm of transcendence [[harry-haller|Haller]] has been seeking all his life.

The novel's treatment of jazz thus enacts its own philosophical argument. The [[music]] [[harry-haller|Haller]] dismisses as decadent turns out to be the vehicle of his spiritual liberation. The saxophonist he disdains turns out to be a master of the psyche. The lesson is not that jazz replaces [[music|classical music]] but that [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s categorical opposition between them — his need to rank and hierarchize — is itself the pathology.

## Cultural Context

Hesse wrote *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]* in the mid-1920s, when jazz was sweeping Europe as the soundtrack of the Weimar Republic's cultural revolution. For conservative intellectuals of Hesse's generation, jazz represented Americanization, the decline of European high culture, and the triumph of the primitive over the civilized. The novel channels this anxiety through [[harry-haller|Haller]] while simultaneously subverting it: jazz is not the enemy of [[immortals|the Immortals]]' realm but one of its unexpected doorways. The saxophone, no less than the harpsichord, can be an instrument of transcendence — if one has ears to hear it.
