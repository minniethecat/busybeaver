## In the Novel

Bach appears throughout [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] as a touchstone of authentic musical experience, consistently invoked by [[harry-haller|Harry Haller]] when he wishes to distinguish "real music" from the degraded entertainments of modern life. The first reference comes in the Preface, where the narrator hears "a little Symphony of Friedman Bach"—likely referring to Wilhelm Friedemann Bach, Johann Sebastian's eldest son—and notes that "after a few notes I was already intoxicated" [P02-036].

Haller's own relationship to Bach is established in one of his most revealing confessions. He declares that he "carried the complete works of Bach and [[haydn|Haydn]] in my head and could not be taken in by the latest dance tunes" (001-316). The image of Haller carrying Bach's complete works in his memory is central to understanding his character: he is a man whose inner world is furnished with the highest achievements of Western music, making the "latest dance tunes" of the jazz age not merely unappealing but existentially threatening. The music he carries in his head is the measure against which all experience is judged—and found wanting.

Bach also appears in the description of Haller's youthful musical explorations: "I had gone the old way and played pieces by Buxtehude, Pachelbel, Bach and [[haydn|Haydn]]" (001-325). This lineage of German Baroque composers represents Haller's musical heritage—a tradition of sacred seriousness that the novel both honours and gently questions, suggesting that Haller's attachment to it may be as imprisoning as it is enriching.

In the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]], fragments of "Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier" drift through Haller's consciousness alongside Mozart's Cassations (001-407), the two composers merging into a single stream of timeless music that the [[immortals|Immortals]] inhabit and Haller aspires to join.

## Cultural Context

Johann Sebastian Bach (1685–1750) occupies a unique position in German musical culture. By the early twentieth century, Bach had been canonised not merely as a great composer but as the foundation of Western music itself—the composer whose "Well-Tempered Clavier" established the very harmonic language that all subsequent music built upon. For a figure like Haller, steeped in German intellectual tradition, Bach represents the highest synthesis of mathematical precision and spiritual depth, of craft and transcendence.

Haller's claim to carry Bach's "complete works" in his head is both a boast and a confession: it demonstrates his extraordinary cultural cultivation while also revealing the impossible standard against which he measures all experience, including his own.

## Key Passages

> I carried the complete works of Bach and Haydn in my head and could not be taken in by the latest dance tunes. (001-316)

Haller's most revealing self-portrait: a man whose inner world of Bach makes the modern world unlivable.

> Compared with Bach and Mozart and real music it is all worthless. (001-022)

Haller's absolute distinction between "real music" and everything else, the aesthetic judgment that isolates him.

> Pieces by Buxtehude, Pachelbel, Bach and Haydn. I had gone the old way. (001-325)

The German Baroque tradition as Haller's musical heritage and spiritual home.
