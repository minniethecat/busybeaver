# Bourgeois Existence

## Definition

Bourgeois existence — the orderly, comfortable, rule-bound life of the middle class — serves in *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]* as both the object of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s critique and the object of his secret longing. The [[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] defines himself in opposition to the bourgeois world, yet he is drawn to its warmth, its domesticity, its well-tended [[araucaria|araucaria plant]]s. This ambivalence is not incidental but central: [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s tragedy is that he can neither inhabit the bourgeois order nor fully transcend it.

## In the Novel

The narrator's preface provides the most sustained description of the bourgeois world [[harry-haller|Haller]] enters — his aunt's orderly household with its polished floors, its regular meals, its peaceful routines:

> I admired and loved our little bourgeois world as something solid and secure in the midst of chaos, something worthy of respect and preservation. (P02-034)

This domestic order is embodied in the [[araucaria|araucaria plant]] on the landing — a symbol of bourgeois cultivation and tidiness that [[harry-haller|Haller]] regards with a mixture of affection and irony:

> He called himself the Steppenwolf, and this too estranged and disturbed them, but they noticed that he carefully watered the araucaria and kept his room immaculate. (P02-035)

The [[treatise-on-the-steppenwolf|Treatise on the Steppenwolf]] provides an almost clinical analysis of [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s relationship to the bourgeois world. It describes the "[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]] type" as someone suspended between two worlds, capable of seeing through bourgeois conventions but incapable of establishing a viable alternative:

> In relation, for example, to the bourgeois world, his symptoms may be described as follows: he is by nature unsuited to bourgeois life, yet he constantly seeks it out. He despises it and yet is drawn to it. (001-033)

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s own meditations return repeatedly to this theme. Walking through the streets at night, he observes the middle-class interiors with a mixture of contempt and envy:

> I cannot understand what pleasures and joys they are that drive people to the overcrowded railways and hotels, into the packed cafés with the suffocating and oppressive music. ... And in fact, if the world is right, ... then I am wrong, I am crazy. I am in truth the Steppenwolf that I often call myself; that beast astray who finds neither home nor joy nor nourishment in a world that is strange and incomprehensible to him. (001-008)

## Social Critique and Self-Critique

The concept of bourgeois existence in *[[steppenwolf|Steppenwolf]]* operates on multiple levels. On one level, it is Hesse's critique of Weimar-era middle-class culture — its complacency, its spiritual poverty, its reduction of life to comfort and convention. On another level, it is [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s self-critique: much of what he calls "bourgeois" is simply what he cannot have or sustain, and his scorn often [[masks]] envy.

The novel's resolution does not involve choosing between the bourgeois world and the world of [[immortals|the Immortals]]. It involves accepting that both are real, that neither is the whole truth, and that [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]]'s suffering arises precisely from the demand that life resolve into a single coherent order. The bourgeois [[araucaria]] and [[mozart|Mozart]]'s [[laughter|immortal laughter]] are not opposites to be reconciled — they are simply different rooms in the [[magic-theater|Magic Theater]] of existence.
