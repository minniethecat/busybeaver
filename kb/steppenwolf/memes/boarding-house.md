# Boarding House

## In the Novel

The boarding house run by the narrator's aunt is [[harry-haller|Haller]]'s home for the nine to ten months recorded in the manuscript. It is a model of bourgeois domesticity: clean, orderly, peaceful, with polished floors, regular meals, and the [[araucaria|araucaria plant]] standing sentinel on the landing. The narrator's preface describes it in loving detail, establishing the household as a symbol of everything [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] is estranged from.

[[harry-haller|Haller]]'s relationship to the boarding house is ambivalent. He appreciates its quiet, its routine, its freedom from disturbance. He faithfully waters the [[araucaria]] and keeps his own room immaculate. Yet he knows he is a temporary resident — not merely of this particular house but of the bourgeois order it represents. He can pause before the [[araucaria]] and admire its scent; he cannot plant one of his own.

The boarding house functions as a frame for [[steppenwolf-novel|the novel]] — the place where [[harry-haller|Haller]] writes his manuscript and, after his departure, the place where the narrator discovers and publishes it. It is thus both a setting and a perspective: [[harry-haller|the Steppenwolf]] seen from the outside, through the eyes of someone who inhabits the [[bourgeois-existence|bourgeois world]] he can only visit.
