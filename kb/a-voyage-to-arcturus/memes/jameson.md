# Mrs. Jameson

## Role

Mrs. Jameson is a guest at [[faull]]'s séance at Prolands and, as the text reveals, [[faull|Faull]]'s sister. Her presence in the novel is confined to the framing chapters that take place on Earth (Chapters 1–2), yet within those chapters she serves an important dramatic function: she is the human barometer of the séance's emotional pressure, her reactions registering the gathering supernatural tension in a way that the more guarded characters — [[faull]] with his "stoical apathy," [[backhouse]] with his professional detachment — do not.

## Arrival at Prolands

Mrs. Jameson enters the narrative not as a formally invited guest but as a relative making an unexpected visit:

> "Well, then let's go to see my sister, Mrs. Jameson, who is in the drawing room." [001-069]

Her connection to [[faull|Faull]] is established immediately — she is his sister, and her presence at Prolands is casual, familial. This domestic framing is important because it grounds the séance in ordinary social relations. The supernatural does not arrive in a sterile laboratory but in a drawing room where family members drop in.

Her initial response to the evening's purpose is polite but slightly distanced:

> "Oh, that's beautiful," responded Mrs. Jameson. She smiled rather vaguely. [001-086]

The "rather vague" smile suggests detachment or perhaps mild scepticism. She is not a committed spiritualist; she is a guest at her brother's house, participating in his eccentric entertainment with the slightly amused tolerance of a family member who has seen his enthusiasms before.

## A Social Observer

Mrs. Jameson's role in the séance room is that of an observer and, occasionally, a commentator. When the conversation turns to [[backhouse|Backhouse]], she offers a remark that cuts through the séance's gravitas:

> "Oh, he's not coming tonight," remarked Mrs. Jameson, throwing a curious look at her brother. [001-102]

The "curious look" directed at [[faull|Faull]] is telling. She knows her brother; she reads the social dynamics of the room with a clarity that the more credulous guests lack. When this statement is contradicted by another character, she responds with good-natured humour:

> "Oh, you provoking girl," put in Mrs. Jameson. [001-161]

She is relaxed, comfortable, and slightly teasing — a woman at ease in a social situation, treating her brother's séance with the same lightly entertained attitude one might bring to amateur theatricals.

## The Crisis

The shift in Mrs. Jameson's state tracks the séance's descent from controlled social ritual into genuine supernatural crisis. Her physical appearance becomes a register of the evening's wrongness:

> She looked haggard and ghastly. [002-096]

The starkness of this description — "haggard and ghastly" — is the novel's most direct physical registration of the séance's impact on its participants. Where [[faull|Faull]] preserves his composure and [[backhouse|Backhouse]] maintains his professional facade, Mrs. Jameson shows the cost on the body. She is not a medium, not a philosopher, not a seeker; she is simply present, and the presence of the supernatural is enough to drain the colour from her face.

The crisis culminates in a full bodily collapse:

> Mrs. Jameson quietly fainted in her chair. [002-096]

The adverb "quietly" is important. She does not scream or protest or run from the room; she simply faints, as though the supernatural pressure has exceeded the capacity of her nervous system to process it. It is a passive response, but it is also the most authentic response available — a body acknowledging what the mind cannot comprehend.

> Maskull caught Mrs. Jameson in his arms; she was attacked by a sort of convulsive fit, and he had to hold her tight. [002-128]

[[maskull]] — who was not even a participant in the séance but an observer — becomes her physical support. The "convulsive fit" is the body's language when words fail. Mrs. Jameson has no theoretical vocabulary for what is happening, no philosophical framework to contain it; she can only *react*, and her reaction is total.

## Philosophical Significance

In a novel structured around philosophical positions — each major character embodies a metaphysical stance — Mrs. Jameson represents something that Lindsay treats with unusual respect: the *unmediated* human response. She has no doctrine, no system, no ambition to transcend ordinary life. She came to her brother's house for an evening's entertainment, and instead she encounters something that overwhelms her.

Her fainting is not weakness in Lindsay's scheme but honesty. Compare her to [[faull]], who preserves his "stoical apathy" and glances at [[backhouse|the medium]] to see if this is part of the show [001-058]. [[faull|Faull]]'s composure is a form of spiritual deadness; Mrs. Jameson's collapse is a form of spiritual aliveness. She *feels* the intrusion of [[krag]] into the room with her whole body, and her body tells the truth that [[faull|Faull]]'s stoicism conceals.

There is also something in her role that prefigures the novel's subsequent pattern. The cosmic journey will repeatedly expose characters — [[maskull]] most of all — to experiences that exceed their capacity to process them. Mrs. Jameson, in her brief appearance, models a kind of authentic response: not understanding, not controlling, but simply *undergoing*.

She is, in this sense, the most ordinary person in the room and therefore the truest register of the extraordinary. The philosophers and seekers will travel to [[tormance]]; Mrs. Jameson will remain in Hampstead, having seen enough.
