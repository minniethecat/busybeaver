# Section 17

[017-001]Tydomin flashed a quick, gentle smile at Maskull. “Is it necessary for

[017-002]you to listen to all this?”

[017-003]Without question, and feeling it the right thing to do, he walked away

[017-004]out of earshot.

[017-005]Tydomin approached Oceaxe. “Perhaps because my beauty fades and I’m no

[017-006]longer young, I needed him all the more.”

[017-007]Oceaxe gave a kind of snarl. “Well, he’s dead, and that’s the end of it.

[017-008]What are you going to do now, Tydomin?”

[017-009]The other woman smiled faintly and rather pathetically. “There’s nothing

[017-010]left to do, except mourn the dead. You won’t grudge me that last

[017-011]office?”

[017-012]“Do you want to stay here?” demanded Oceaxe suspiciously.

[017-013]“Yes, Oceaxe dear, I wish to be alone.”

[017-014]“Then what is to become of us?”

[017-015]“I thought that you and your lover—what is his name?”

[017-016]“Maskull.”

[017-017]“I thought that perhaps you two would go to Disscourn, and spend

[017-018]Blodsombre at my home.”

[017-019]Oceaxe called out aloud to Maskull, “Will you come with me now to

[017-020]Disscourn?”

[017-021]“If you wish,” returned Maskull.

[017-022]“Go first, Oceaxe. I must question your friend about Crimtyphon’s death.

[017-023]I won’t keep him.”

[017-024]“Why don’t you question me, rather?” demanded Oceaxe, looking up

[017-025]sharply.

[017-026]Tydomin gave the shadow of a smile. “We know each other too well.”

[017-027]“Play no tricks!” said Oceaxe, and she turned to go.

[017-028]“Surely you must be dreaming,” said Tydomin. “That’s the way—unless you

[017-029]want to walk over the cliffside.”

[017-030]The path Oceaxe had chosen led across the isthmus. The direction which

[017-031]Tydomin proposed for her was over the edge of the precipice, into empty

[017-032]space.

[017-033]“Shaping! I must be mad,” cried Oceaxe, with a laugh. And she obediently

[017-034]followed the other’s finger.

[017-035]She walked straight on toward the edge of the abyss, twenty paces away.

[017-036]Maskull pulled his beard around, and wondered what she was doing.

[017-037]Tydomin remained standing with outstretched finger, watching her.

[017-038]Without hesitation, without slackening her step once, Oceaxe strolled

[017-039]on—and when she had reached the extreme end of the land she still took

[017-040]one more step.

[017-041]Maskull saw her limbs wrench as she stumbled over the edge. Her body

[017-042]disappeared, and as it did so an awful shriek sounded.

[017-043]Disillusionment had come to her an instant too late. He tore himself out

[017-044]of his stupor, rushed to the edge of the cliff, threw himself on the

[017-045]ground recklessly, and looked over.... Oceaxe had vanished.

[017-046]He continued staring wildly down for several minutes, and then began to

[017-047]sob. Tydomin came up to him, and he got to his feet.

[017-048]The blood kept rushing to his face and leaving it again. It was some

[017-049]time before he could speak at all. Then he brought out the words with

[017-050]difficulty. “You shall pay for this, Tydomin. But first I want to hear

[017-051]why you did it.”

[017-052]“Hadn’t I cause?” she asked, standing with downcast eyes.

[017-053]“Was it pure fiendishness?”

[017-054]“It was for Crimtyphon’s sake.”

[017-055]“She had nothing to do with that death. I told you so.”

[017-056]“You are loyal to her, and I’m loyal to him.”

[017-057]“Loyal? You’ve made a terrible blunder. She wasn’t my mistress. I killed

[017-058]Crimtyphon for quite another reason. She had absolutely no part in it.”

[017-059]“Wasn’t she your lover?” asked Tydomin slowly.

[017-060]“You’ve made a terrible mistake,” repeated Maskull. “I killed him

[017-061]because he was a wild beast. She was as innocent of his death as you

[017-062]are.”

[017-063]Tydomin’s face took on a hard look. “So you are guilty of two deaths.”

[017-064]There was a dreadful silence.

[017-065]“Why couldn’t you believe me?” asked Maskull, who was pale and sweating

[017-066]painfully.

[017-067]“Who gave you the right to kill him?” demanded Tydomin sternly.

[017-068]He said nothing, and perhaps did not hear her question.

[017-069]She sighed two or three times and began to stir restlessly. “Since you

[017-070]murdered him, you must help me bury him.”

[017-071]“What’s to be done? This is a most fearful crime.”

[017-072]“You are a most fearful man. Why did you come here, to do all this? What

[017-073]are we to you?”

[017-074]“Unfortunately you are right.”

[017-075]Another pause ensued.

[017-076]“It’s no use standing here,” said Tydomin. “Nothing can be done. You

[017-077]must come with me.”

[017-078]“Come with you? Where to?”

[017-079]“To Disscourn. There’s a burning lake on the far side of it. He always

[017-080]wished to be cast there after death. We can do that after Blodsombre—in

[017-081]the meantime we must take him home.”

[017-082]“You’re a callous, heartless woman. Why should he be buried when that

[017-083]poor girl must remain unburied?”

[017-084]“You know that’s out of the question,” replied Tydomin quietly.

[017-085]Maskull’s eyes roamed about agitatedly, apparently seeing nothing.

[017-086]“We must do something,” she continued. “I shall go. You can’t wish to

[017-087]stay here alone?”

[017-088]“No, I couldn’t stay here—and why should I want to? You want me to carry

[017-089]the corpse?”

[017-090]“He can’t carry himself, and you murdered him. Perhaps it will ease your

[017-091]mind to carry it.”

[017-092]“Ease my mind?” said Maskull, rather stupidly.

[017-093]“There’s only one relief for remorse, and that’s voluntary pain.”

[017-094]“And have you no remorse?” he asked, fixing her with a heavy eye.

[017-095]“These crimes are yours, Maskull,” she said in a low but incisive voice.

[017-096]They walked over to Crimtyphon’s body, and Maskull hoisted it on to his

[017-097]shoulders. It weighed heavier than he had thought. Tydomin did not offer

[017-098]to assist him to adjust the ghastly burden.

[017-099]She crossed the isthmus, followed by Maskull. Their path lay through

[017-100]sunshine and shadow. Branchspell was blazing in a cloudless sky, the

[017-101]heat was insufferable—streams of sweat coursed down his face, and the

[017-102]corpse seemed to grow heavier and heavier. Tydomin always walked in

[017-103]front of him. His eyes were fastened in an unseeing stare on her white,

[017-104]womanish calves; he looked neither to right nor left. His features grew

[017-105]sullen. At the end of ten minutes he suddenly allowed his burden to slip

[017-106]off his shoulders on to the ground, where it lay sprawled every which

[017-107]way. He called out to Tydomin.

[017-108]She quickly looked around.

[017-109]“Come here. It has just occurred to me”—he laughed—“why should I be

[017-110]carrying this corpse—and why should I be following you at all? What

[017-111]surprises me is, why this has never struck me before.”

[017-112]She at once came back to him. “I suppose you’re tired, Maskull. Let us

[017-113]sit down. Perhaps you have come a long way this morning?”

[017-114]“Oh, it’s not tiredness, but a sudden gleam of sense. Do you know of any

[017-115]reason why I should be acting as your porter?” He laughed again, but

[017-116]nevertheless sat down on the ground beside her.

[017-117]Tydomin neither looked at him nor answered. Her head was half bent, so

[017-118]as to face the northern sky, where the Alppain light was still glowing.

[017-119]Maskull followed her gaze, and also watched the glow for a moment or two

[017-120]in silence.

[017-121]“Why don’t you speak?” he asked at last.

[017-122]“What does that light suggest to you, Maskull?”

[017-123]“I’m not speaking of that light.”

[017-124]“Doesn’t it suggest anything at all?”

[017-125]“Perhaps it doesn’t. What does it matter?”

[017-126]“Not sacrifice?”

[017-127]Maskull grew sullen again. “Sacrifice of what? What do you mean?”

[017-128]“Hasn’t it entered your head yet,” said Tydomin, looking straight in

[017-129]front of her, and speaking in her delicate, hard manner, “that this

[017-130]adventure of yours will scarcely come to an end until you have made some

[017-131]sort of sacrifice?”

[017-132]He returned no answer, and she said nothing more. In a few minutes’ time

[017-133]Maskull got up of his own accord, and irreverently, and almost angrily,

[017-134]threw Crimtyphon’s corpse over his shoulder again.

[017-135]“How far do we have to go?” he asked in a surly tone.

[017-136]“An hour’s walk.”

[017-137]“Lead on.”

[017-138]“Still, this isn’t the sacrifice I mean,” said Tydomin quietly, as she

[017-139]went on in front.

[017-140]Almost immediately they reached more difficult ground. They had to pass

[017-141]from peak to peak, as from island to island. In some cases they were

[017-142]able to stride or jump across, but in others they had to make use of

[017-143]rude bridges of fallen timber. It appeared to be a frequented path.

[017-144]Underneath were the black, impenetrable abysses—on the surface were the

[017-145]glaring sunshine, the gay, painted rocks, the chaotic tangle of strange

[017-146]plants. There were countless reptiles and insects. The latter were

[017-147]thicker built than those of Earth—consequently still more disgusting,

[017-148]and some of them were of enormous size. One monstrous insect, as large

[017-149]as a horse, stood right in the centre of their path without budging. It

[017-150]was armour-plated, had jaws like scimitars, and underneath its body was

[017-151]a forest of legs. Tydomin gave one malignant look at it, and sent it

[017-152]crashing into the gulf.

[017-153]“What have I to offer, except my life?” Maskull suddenly broke out. “And

[017-154]what good is that? It won’t bring that poor girl back into the world.”

[017-155]“Sacrifice is not for utility. It’s a penalty which we pay.”

[017-156]“I know that.”

[017-157]“The point is whether you can go on enjoying life, after what has

[017-158]happened.”

[017-159]She waited for Maskull to come even with her.

[017-160]“Perhaps you imagine I’m not man enough—you imagine that because I

[017-161]allowed poor Oceaxe to die for me—”

[017-162]“She did die for you,” said Tydomin, in a quiet, emphatic voice.

[017-163]“That would be a second blunder of yours,” returned Maskull, just as

[017-164]firmly. “I was not in love with Oceaxe, and I’m not in love with life.”

[017-165]“Your life is not required.”

[017-166]“Then I don’t understand what you want, or what you are speaking about.”

[017-167]“It’s not for me to ask a sacrifice from you, Maskull. That would be

[017-168]compliance on your part, but not sacrifice. You must wait until you feel

[017-169]there’s nothing else for you to do.”

[017-170]“It’s all very mysterious.”

[017-171]The conversation was abruptly cut short by a prolonged and frightful

[017-172]crashing, roaring sound, coming from a short distance ahead. It was

[017-173]accompanied by a violent oscillation of the ground on which they stood.

[017-174]They looked up, startled, just in time to witness the final

[017-175]disappearance of a huge mass of forest land, not two hundred yards in

[017-176]front of them. Several acres of trees, plants, rocks, and soil, with all

[017-177]its teeming animal life, vanished before their eyes, like a magic story.

[017-178]The new chasm was cut, as if by a knife. Beyond its farther edge the

[017-179]Alppain glow burned blue just over the horizon.

[017-180]“Now we shall have to make a detour,” said Tydomin, halting.

[017-181]Maskull caught hold of her with his third hand. “Listen to me, while I

[017-182]try to describe what I’m feeling. When I saw that landslip, everything I

[017-183]have heard about the last destruction of the world came into my mind. It

[017-184]seemed to me as if I were actually witnessing it, and that the world

[017-185]were really falling to pieces. Then, where the land was, we now have

[017-186]this empty, awful gulf—that’s to say, nothing—and it seems to me as if

[017-187]our life will come to the same condition, where there was something

[017-188]there will be nothing. But that terrible blue glare on the opposite side

[017-189]is exactly like the eye of fate. It accuses us, and demands what we have

[017-190]made of our life, which is no more. At the same time, it is grand and

[017-191]joyful. The joy consists in this—that it is in our power to give freely

[017-192]what will later on be taken from us by force.”

[017-193]Tydomin watched him attentively. “Then your feeling is that your life is

[017-194]worthless, and you make a present of it to the first one who asks?”

[017-195]“No, it goes beyond that. I feel that the only thing worth living for is

[017-196]to be so magnanimous that fate itself will be astonished at us.

[017-197]Understand me. It isn’t cynicism, or bitterness, or despair, but

[017-198]heroism.... It’s hard to explain.”

[017-199]“Now you shall hear what sacrifice I offer you, Maskull. It’s a heavy

[017-200]one, but that’s what you seem to wish.”

[017-201]“That is so. In my present mood it can’t be too heavy.”

[017-202]“Then, if you are in earnest, resign your body to me. Now that

[017-203]Crimtyphon’s dead, I’m tired of being a woman.”
