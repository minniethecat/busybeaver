# Section 15

[015-001]Her ironical laugh sounded out of the gloom. “Shall we go down and see?”

[015-002]“You find that amusing?”

[015-003]“No, not that. What I do find amusing is the big stranger with the

[015-004]beard, who is so keenly interested in everything except himself.”

[015-005]Maskull then laughed too. “I happen to be the only thing in Tormance

[015-006]which is not a novelty for me.”

[015-007]“Yes, but I am a novelty for you.”

[015-008]The channel went zigzagging its way through the belly of the mountain,

[015-009]and all the time they were gradually rising.

[015-010]“At least I have heard nothing like your voice before,” said Maskull,

[015-011]who, since he had no longer anything to look at, was at last ready for

[015-012]conversation.

[015-013]“What’s the matter with my voice?”

[015-014]“It’s all that I can distinguish of you now; that’s why I mentioned it.”

[015-015]“Isn’t it clear—don’t I speak distinctly?”

[015-016]“Oh, it’s clear enough, but—it’s inappropriate.”

[015-017]“Inappropriate?”

[015-018]“I won’t explain further,” said Maskull, “but whether you are speaking

[015-019]or laughing, your voice is by far the loveliest and strangest instrument

[015-020]I have ever listened to. And yet I repeat, it is inappropriate.”

[015-021]“You mean that my nature doesn’t correspond?”

[015-022]He was just considering his reply, when their talk was abruptly broken

[015-023]off by a huge and terrifying, but not very loud sound rising up from the

[015-024]gulf directly underneath them. It was a low, grinding, roaring thunder.

[015-025]“The ground is rising under us!” cried Oceaxe.

[015-026]“Shall we escape?”

[015-027]She made no answer, but urged the shrowk’s flight upward, at such a

[015-028]steep gradient that they retained their seats with difficulty. The floor

[015-029]of the canyon, upheaved by some mighty subterranean force, could be

[015-030]heard, and almost felt, coming up after them, like a gigantic landslip

[015-031]in the wrong direction. The cliffs cracked, and fragments began to fall.

[015-032]A hundred awful noises filled the air, growing louder and louder each

[015-033]second—splitting, hissing, cracking, grinding, booming, exploding,

[015-034]roaring. When they had still fifty feet or so to go, to reach the top, a

[015-035]sort of dark, indefinite sea of broken rocks and soil appeared under

[015-036]their feet, ascending rapidly, with irresistible might, accompanied by

[015-037]the most horrible noises. The canal was filled up for two hundred yards,

[015-038]before and behind them. Millions of tons of solid matter seemed to be

[015-039]raised. The shrowk in its ascent was caught by the uplifted debris.

[015-040]Beast and riders experienced in that moment all the horrors of an

[015-041]earthquake—they were rolled violently over, and thrown among the rocks

[015-042]and dirt. All was thunder, instability, motion, confusion.

[015-043]Before they had time to realise their position, they were in the

[015-044]sunlight. The upheaval still continued. In another minute or two the

[015-045]valley floor had formed a new mountain, a hundred feet or more higher

[015-046]than the old. Then its movement ceased suddenly. Every noise stopped, as

[015-047]if by magic; not a rock moved. Oceaxe and Maskull picked themselves up

[015-048]and examined themselves for cuts and bruises. The shrowk lay on its

[015-049]side, panting violently, and sweating with fright.

[015-050]“That was a nasty affair,” said Maskull, flicking the dirt off his

[015-051]person.

[015-052]Oceaxe staunched a cut on her chin with a corner of her robe.

[015-053]“It might have been far worse.... I mean, it’s bad enough to come up,

[015-054]but it’s death to go down, and that happens just as often.”

[015-055]“Whatever induces you to live in such a country?”

[015-056]“I don’t know, Maskull. Habit, I suppose. I have often thought of moving

[015-057]out of it.”

[015-058]“A good deal must be forgiven you for having to spend your life in a

[015-059]place like this, where one is obviously never safe from one minute to

[015-060]another.”

[015-061]“You will learn by degrees,” she answered, smiling.

[015-062]She looked hard at the monster, and it got heavily to its feet.

[015-063]“Get on again, Maskull!” she directed, climbing back to her perch. “We

[015-064]haven’t too much time to waste.”

[015-065]He obeyed. They resumed their interrupted flight, this time over the

[015-066]mountains, and in full sunlight. Maskull settled down again to his

[015-067]thoughts. The peculiar atmosphere of the country continued to soak into

[015-068]his brain. His will became so restless and uneasy that merely to sit

[015-069]there in inactivity was a torture. He could scarcely endure not to be

[015-070]doing something.

[015-071]“How secretive you are, Maskull!” said Oceaxe quietly, without turning

[015-072]her head.

[015-073]“What secrets—what do you mean?”

[015-074]“Oh, I know perfectly well what’s passing inside you. Now I think it

[015-075]wouldn’t be amiss to ask you—is friendship still enough?”

[015-076]“Oh, don’t ask me anything,” growled Maskull. “I’ve far too many

[015-077]problems in my head already. I only wish I could answer some of them.”

[015-078]He stared stonily at the landscape. The beast was winging its way toward

[015-079]a distant mountain, of singular shape. It was an enormous natural

[015-080]quadrilateral pyramid, rising in great terraces and terminating in a

[015-081]broad, flat top, on which what looked like green snow still lingered.

[015-082]“What mountain is that?” he asked.

[015-083]“Disscourn. The highest point in Ifdawn.”

[015-084]“Are we going there?”

[015-085]“Why should we go there? But if you were going on farther, it might be

[015-086]worth your while to pay a visit to the top. It commands the whole land

[015-087]as far as the Sinking Sea and Swaylone’s Island—and beyond. You can also

[015-088]see Alppain from it.”

[015-089]“That’s a sight I mean to see before I have finished.”

[015-090]“Do you, Maskull?” She turned around and put her hand on his wrist.

[015-091]“Stay with me, and one day we’ll go to Disscourn together.”

[015-092]He grunted unintelligibly.

[015-093]There were no signs of human existence in the country under their feet.

[015-094]While Maskull was still grimly regarding it, a large tract of forest not

[015-095]far ahead, bearing many trees and rocks, suddenly subsided with an awful

[015-096]roar and crashed down into an invisible gulf. What was solid land one

[015-097]minute became a clean-cut chasm the next. He jumped violently up with

[015-098]the shock. “This is frightful.”

[015-099]Oceaxe remained unmoved.

[015-100]“Why, life here must be absolutely impossible,” he went on, when he had

[015-101]somewhat recovered himself. “A man would need nerves of steel.... Is

[015-102]there no means at all of foreseeing a catastrophe like this?”

[015-103]“Oh, I suppose we wouldn’t be alive if there weren’t,” replied Oceaxe,

[015-104]with composure. “We are more or less clever at it—but that doesn’t

[015-105]prevent our often getting caught.”

[015-106]“You had better teach me the signs.”

[015-107]“We’ll have many things to go over together. And among them, I expect,

[015-108]will be whether we are to stay in the land at all.... But first let us

[015-109]get home.”

[015-110]“How far is it now?”

[015-111]“It is right in front of you,” said Oceaxe, pointing with her

[015-112]forefinger. “You can see it.”

[015-113]He followed the direction of the finger and, after a few questions, made

[015-114]out the spot she was indicating. It was a broad peninsula, about two

[015-115]miles distant. Three of its sides rose sheer out of a lake of air, the

[015-116]bottom of which was invisible; its fourth was a bottleneck, joining it

[015-117]to the mainland. It was overgrown with bright vegetation, distinct in

[015-118]the brilliant atmosphere. A single tall tree, shooting up in the middle

[015-119]of the peninsula, dwarfed everything else; it was wide and shady with

[015-120]sea-green leaves.

[015-121]“I wonder if Crimtyphon is there,” remarked Oceaxe. “Can I see two

[015-122]figures, or am I mistaken?”

[015-123]“I also see something,” said Maskull.

[015-124]In twenty minutes they were directly above the peninsula, at a height of

[015-125]about fifty feet. The shrowk slackened speed, and came to earth on the

[015-126]mainland, exactly at the gateway of the isthmus. They both

[015-127]descended—Maskull with aching thighs.

[015-128]“What shall we do with the monster?” asked Oceaxe. Without waiting for a

[015-129]suggestion, she patted its hideous face with her hand. “Fly away home! I

[015-130]may want you some other time.”

[015-131]It gave a stupid grunt, elevated itself on its legs again, and, after

[015-132]half running, half flying for a few yards, rose awkwardly into the air,

[015-133]and paddled away in the same direction from which they had come. They

[015-134]watched it out of sight, and then Oceaxe started to cross the neck of

[015-135]land, followed by Maskull.

[015-136]Branchspell’s white rays beat down on them with pitiless force. The sky

[015-137]had by degrees become cloudless, and the wind had dropped entirely. The

[015-138]ground was a rich riot of vividly coloured ferns, shrubs, and grasses.

[015-139]Through these could be seen here and there the golden chalky soil—and

[015-140]occasionally a glittering, white metallic boulder. Everything looked

[015-141]extraordinary and barbaric. Maskull was at last walking in the weird

[015-142]Ifdawn Marest which had created such strange feelings in him when seen

[015-143]from a distance.... And now he felt no wonder or curiosity at all, but

[015-144]only desired to meet human beings—so intense had grown his will. He

[015-145]longed to test his powers on his fellow creatures, and nothing else

[015-146]seemed of the least importance to him.

[015-147]On the peninsula all was coolness and delicate shade. It resembled a

[015-148]large copse, about two acres in extent. In the heart of the tangle of

[015-149]small trees and undergrowth was a partially cleared space—perhaps the

[015-150]roots of the giant tree growing in the centre had killed off the smaller

[015-151]fry all around it. By the side of the tree sparkled a little, bubbling

[015-152]fountain, whose water was iron-red. The precipices on all sides,

[015-153]overhung with thorns, flowers, and creepers, invested the enclosure with

[015-154]an air of wild and charming seclusion—a mythological mountain god might

[015-155]have dwelt here.

[015-156]Maskull’s restless eye left everything, to fall on the two men who

[015-157]formed the centre of the picture.

[015-158]One was reclining, in the ancient Grecian fashion of banqueters on a

[015-159]tall couch of mosses, sprinkled with flowers; he rested on one arm, and

[015-160]was eating a kind of plum, with calm enjoyment. A pile of these plums

[015-161]lay on the couch beside him. The over-spreading branches of the tree

[015-162]completely sheltered him from the sun. His small, boyish form was clad

[015-163]in a rough skin, leaving his limbs naked. Maskull could not tell from

[015-164]his face whether he were a young boy or a grown man. The features were

[015-165]smooth, soft, and childish, their expression was seraphically tranquil;

[015-166]but his violet upper eye was sinister and adult. His skin was of the

[015-167]colour of yellow ivory. His long, curling hair matched his sorb—it was

[015-168]violet. The second man was standing erect before the other, a few feet

[015-169]away from him. He was short and muscular, his face was broad, bearded,

[015-170]and rather commonplace, but there was something terrible about his

[015-171]appearance. The features were distorted by a deep-seated look of pain,

[015-172]despair, and horror.

[015-173]Oceaxe, without pausing, strolled lightly and lazily up to the outermost

[015-174]shadows of the tree, some distance from the couch.

[015-175]“We have met with an uplift,” she remarked carelessly, looking toward

[015-176]the youth.

[015-177]He eyed her, but said nothing.

[015-178]“How is your plant man getting on?” Her tone was artificial but

[015-179]extremely beautiful. While waiting for an answer, she sat down on the

[015-180]ground, her legs gracefully thrust under her body, and pulled down the

[015-181]skirt of her robe. Maskull remained standing just behind her, with

[015-182]crossed arms.

[015-183]There was silence for a minute.

[015-184]“Why don’t you answer your mistress, Sature?” said the boy on the couch,

[015-185]in a calm, treble voice.

[015-186]The man addressed did not alter his expression, but replied in a

[015-187]strangled tone, “I am getting on very well, Oceaxe. There are already

[015-188]buds on my feet. Tomorrow I hope to take root.”

[015-189]Maskull felt a rising storm inside him. He was perfectly aware that

[015-192]“What he says is quite true,” remarked the latter. “Tomorrow roots will

[015-193]reach the ground, and in a few days they ought to be well established.

[015-194]Then I shall set to work to convert his arms into branches, and his

[015-195]fingers into leaves. It will take longer to transform his head into a

[015-196]crown, but still I hope—in fact I can almost promise that within a month
