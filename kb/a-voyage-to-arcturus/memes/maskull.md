# Maskull

**Maskull** is the protagonist and central consciousness of [[David Lindsay]]'s *A Voyage to Arcturus*. Transported from Earth to the planet [[tormance|Tormance]] after a mysterious séance, he embarks on a transformative pilgrimage across an alien world where he grows new sensory organs — each expanding his perception — and encounters a succession of beings who embody distinct philosophical positions. Guided by the violent [[krag|Krag]] and shadowed by the enigmatic [[nightspore|Nightspore]], Maskull's journey is an allegory of spiritual development: the path to enlightenment lies through pain, not comfort.

## Role

Maskull is introduced at a London séance in Chapter 1, where "one rejoices in the name of Maskull" (001-162). His physical description is striking — he is "a kind of giant, but of better proportions than most giants" (002-034) — and his temperament is that of a restless seeker, initially skeptical but driven by an insatiable need to understand.

Over the course of the novel, Maskull transforms from an ordinary man into a being capable of perceiving previously unimaginable dimensions of reality. Each new organ he grows — the breve (telepathy), the poigns (moral perception), the magn (enlarged vision), the sorb (taste-based knowledge) — opens a new mode of experience, and each encounter with a [[tormance|Tormance]] inhabitant confronts him with a different philosophical worldview. His final death in Chapter 45 is not a defeat but a completion: the seeker has found what he was seeking.

## Philosophical Significance

Maskull represents the spiritual seeker — the Everyman who must undergo suffering and transformation to approach truth. His name may derive from "mask" (the persona that must be stripped away) or "masculine" (the active principle seeking union with the divine feminine, [[muspel|Muspel]]).

Each organ he grows is a philosophical instrument: the breve enables direct mind-to-mind understanding, bypassing language; the poigns reveal moral dimensions invisible to ordinary perception; the magn expands the scale at which he can perceive reality. Together, they form a composite portrait of what full human perception might be — and what it costs to achieve.
