# Broodviol

## Role

Broodviol is described as "the wisest man in [[tormance|Tormance]]" [013], a figure of legend consulted by those who undertake the three-day walk through the Wombflash Forest to find him. He appears only in backstory — a tale told to [[maskull]] — but his presence looms over the novel's conception of wisdom: gnarled, rugged, and dwelling in a deep, miry pit surrounded by trees three hundred feet high.

## In the Novel

Broodviol is introduced through a story told by a character recalling a childhood journey: "I was taken, by my father and mother, to see Broodviol, the wisest man in [[tormance|Tormance]]" [013]. The journey takes three days through trees that "grew taller as we went," culminating in the discovery of the sage standing "in a deep, miry pit in the forest, surrounded on all sides by trees three hundred feet high" [013].

Broodviol is described as "a big, gnarled, rugged, wrinkled, sturdy old man" whose age at the time was already immense [013]. When the mother rouses him from meditation, Broodviol "scowled at her savagely, and demanded what she required" [013] — his wisdom does not come with gentleness. He is a figure of the Old Testament prophet type: truth-speaking but not comforting.

The story of Broodviol serves as the novel's acknowledgment that wisdom exists in the world of [[tormance|Tormance]], but it is hard to reach, harder to rouse, and may not give the answer one seeks. The sage in the pit is an image of truth buried deep in the material world — a "vestige of [[muspel|Muspel]]" [026-085] in its most concentrated form.

## Philosophical Significance

Broodviol represents the archetype of the wise old man — the senex figure who embodies accumulated insight. His dwelling in a pit is symbolically important: wisdom is not on the surface but must be excavated, and the seeker must descend to find it. The three-day journey through the forest is an initiatory ritual; the trees that grow taller as one approaches mark the transition from ordinary to sacred space.

In Lindsay's world, where most characters embody a single philosophical position, Broodviol stands apart — he is not a doctrine but a presence, not an argument but a being. His scowl when disturbed from meditation suggests that wisdom is a state to be maintained, not a message to be delivered.
