[003-054]pulled out a pipe, and began to fill it.

[003-055]“You must have cultivated a new form of humour, Krag.”

[003-056]“I am glad if I can amuse you, Maskull, if only for a few days.”

[003-074]“Krag’s chief,” said Nightspore, turning his head away.

[003-186]Chapter 3. STARKNESS

[003-187]A couple of days later, at two o’clock in the afternoon, Maskull and

[003-188]Nightspore arrived at Starkness Observatory, having covered the seven

[003-189]miles from Haillar Station on foot. The road, very wild and lonely, ran

[003-190]for the greater part of the way near the edge of rather lofty cliffs,

[003-191]within sight of the North Sea. The sun shone, but a brisk east wind was

[003-192]blowing and the air was salt and cold. The dark green waves were flecked

[003-193]with white. Throughout the walk, they were accompanied by the plaintive,

[003-194]beautiful crying of the gulls.

[003-195]The observatory presented itself to their eyes as a self-contained

[003-196]little community, without neighbours, and perched on the extreme end of

[003-197]the land. There were three buildings: a small, stone-built dwelling

[003-198]house, a low workshop, and, about two hundred yards farther north, a

[003-199]square tower of granite masonry, seventy feet in height.

[003-200]The house and the shop were separated by an open yard, littered with

[003-201]waste. A single stone wall surrounded both, except on the side facing

[003-202]the sea, where the house itself formed a continuation of the cliff. No

[003-203]one appeared. The windows were all closed, and Maskull could have sworn

[003-204]that the whole establishment was shut up and deserted.
