# Section 7

[007-001]“This is extraordinary! What is this organ?” he asked, touching his

[007-002]forehead.

[007-003]“It is named the ‘breve.’ By means of it we read one another’s thoughts.

[007-004]Still, speech is better, for then the heart can be read too.”

[007-005]He smiled. “They say that speech is given us to deceive others.”

[007-006]“One can deceive with thought, too. But I’m thinking of the best, not

[007-007]the worst.”

[007-008]“Have you seen my friends?”

[007-009]She scrutinised him quietly, before answering. “Did you not come alone?”

[007-010]“I came with two other men, in a machine. I must have lost consciousness

[007-011]on arrival, and I haven’t seen them since.”

[007-012]“That’s very strange! No, I haven’t seen them. They can’t be here, or we

[007-013]would have known it. My husband and I—”

[007-014]“What is your name, and your husband’s name?”

[007-015]“Mine is Joiwind—my husband’s is Panawe. We live a very long way from

[007-016]here; still, it came to us both last night that you were lying here

[007-017]insensible. We almost quarrelled about which of us should come to you,

[007-018]but in the end I won.” Here she laughed. “I won, because I am the

[007-019]stronger-hearted of the two; he is the purer in perception.”

[007-020]“Thanks, Joiwind!” said Maskull simply.

[007-021]The colors chased each other rapidly beneath her skin. “Oh, why do you

[007-022]say that? What pleasure is greater than loving-kindness? I rejoiced at

[007-023]the opportunity.... But now we must exchange blood.”

[007-024]“What is this?” he demanded, rather puzzled.

[007-025]“It must be so. Your blood is far too thick and heavy for our world.

[007-026]Until you have an infusion of mine, you will never get up.”

[007-027]Maskull flushed. “I feel like a complete ignoramus here.... Won’t it

[007-028]hurt you?”

[007-029]“If your blood pains you, I suppose it will pain me. But we will share

[007-030]the pain.”

[007-031]“This is a new kind of hospitality to me,” he muttered.

[007-032]“Wouldn’t you do the same for me?” asked Joiwind, half smiling, half

[007-033]agitated.

[007-034]“I can’t answer for any of my actions in this world. I scarcely know

[007-035]where I am.... Why, yes—of course I would, Joiwind.”

[007-036]While they were talking it had become full day. The mists had rolled

[007-037]away from the ground, and only the upper atmosphere remained fog-

[007-038]charged. The desert of scarlet sand stretched in all directions, except

[007-039]one, where there was a sort of little oasis—some low hills, clothed

[007-040]sparsely with little purple trees from base to summit. It was about a

[007-041]quarter of a mile distant.

[007-042]Joiwind had brought with her a small flint knife. Without any trace of

[007-043]nervousness, she made a careful, deep incision on her upper arm. Maskull

[007-044]expostulated.

[007-045]“Really, this part of it is nothing,” she said, laughing. “And if it

[007-046]were—a sacrifice that is no sacrifice—what merit is there in that?...

[007-047]Come now—your arm!”

[007-048]The blood was streaming down her arm. It was not red blood, but a milky,

[007-049]opalescent fluid.

[007-050]“Not that one!” said Maskull, shrinking. “I have already been cut

[007-051]there.” He submitted the other, and his blood poured forth.

[007-052]Joiwind delicately and skilfully placed the mouths of the two wounds

[007-053]together, and then kept her arm pressed tightly against Maskull’s for a

[007-054]long time. He felt a stream of pleasure entering his body through the

[007-055]incision. His old lightness and vigour began to return to him. After

[007-056]about five minutes a duel of kindness started between them; he wanted to

[007-057]remove his arm, and she to continue. At last he had his way, but it was

[007-058]none too soon—she stood there pale and dispirited.

[007-059]She looked at him with a more serious expression than before, as if

[007-060]strange depths had opened up before her eyes.

[007-061]“What is your name?”

[007-062]“Maskull.”

[007-063]“Where have you come from, with this awful blood?”

[007-064]“From a world called Earth.... The blood is clearly unsuitable for this

[007-065]world, Joiwind, but after all, that was only to be expected. I am sorry

[007-066]I let you have your way.”

[007-067]“Oh, don’t say that! There was nothing else to be done. We must all help

[007-068]one another. Yet, somehow—forgive me—I feel polluted.”

[007-069]“And well you may, for it’s a fearful thing for a girl to accept in her

[007-070]own veins the blood of a strange man from a strange planet. If I had not

[007-071]been so dazed and weak I would never have allowed it.”

[007-072]“But I would have insisted. Are we not all brothers and sisters? Why did

[007-073]you come here, Maskull?”

[007-074]He was conscious of a slight degree of embarrassment. “Will you think it

[007-075]foolish if I say I hardly know?—I came with those two men. Perhaps I was

[007-076]attracted by curiosity, or perhaps it was the love of adventure.”

[007-077]“Perhaps,” said Joiwind. “I wonder... These friends of yours must be

[007-078]terrible men. Why did they come?”

[007-079]“That I can tell you. They came to follow Surtur.”

[007-080]Her face grew troubled. “I don’t understand it. One of them at least

[007-081]must be a bad man, and yet if he is following Surtur—or Shaping, as he

[007-082]is called here—he can’t be really bad.”

[007-083]“What do you know of Surtur?” asked Maskull in astonishment.

[007-084]Joiwind remained silent for a time, studying his face. His brain moved

[007-085]restlessly, as though it were being probed from outside. “I see.... and

[007-086]yet I don’t see,” she said at last. “It is very difficult.... Your God

[007-087]is a dreadful Being—bodyless, unfriendly, invisible. Here we don’t

[007-088]worship a God like that. Tell me, has any man set eyes on your God?”

[007-089]“What does all this mean, Joiwind? Why speak of God?”

[007-090]“I want to know.”

[007-091]“In ancient times, when the earth was young and grand, a few holy men

[007-092]are reputed to have walked and spoken with God, but those days are

[007-093]past.”

[007-094]“Our world is still young,” said Joiwind. “Shaping goes among us and

[007-095]converses with us. He is real and active—a friend and lover. Shaping

[007-096]made us, and he loves his work.”

[007-097]“Have you met him?” demanded Maskull, hardly believing his ears.

[007-098]“No. I have done nothing to deserve it yet. Some day I may have an

[007-099]opportunity to sacrifice myself, and then I may be rewarded by meeting

[007-100]and talking with Shaping.”

[007-101]“I have certainly come to another world. But why do you say he is the

[007-102]same as Surtur?”

[007-103]“Yes, he is the same. We women call him Shaping, and so do most men, but

[007-104]a few name him Surtur.”

[007-105]Maskull bit his nail. “Have you ever heard of Crystalman?”

[007-106]“That is Shaping once again. You see, he has many names—which shows how

[007-107]much he occupies our minds. Crystalman is a name of affection.”

[007-108]“It’s odd,” said Maskull. “I came here with quite different ideas about

[007-109]Crystalman.”

[007-110]Joiwind shook her hair. “In that grove of trees over there stands a

[007-111]desert shrine of his. Let us go and pray there, and then we’ll go on our

[007-112]way to Poolingdred. That is my home. It’s a long way off, and we must

[007-113]get there before Blodsombre.”

[007-114]“Now, what is Blodsombre?”

[007-115]“For about four hours in the middle of the day Branchspell’s rays are so

[007-116]hot that no one can endure them. We call it Blodsombre.”

[007-117]“Is Branchspell another name for Arcturus?”

[007-118]Joiwind threw off her seriousness and laughed. “Naturally we don’t take

[007-119]our names from you, Maskull. I don’t think our names are very poetic,

[007-120]but they follow nature.”

[007-121]She took his arm affectionately, and directed their walk towards the

[007-122]tree-covered hills. As they went along, the sun broke through the upper

[007-123]mists and a terrible gust of scorching heat, like a blast from a

[007-124]furnace, struck Maskull’s head. He involuntarily looked up, but lowered

[007-125]his eyes again like lightning. All that he saw in that instant was a

[007-126]glaring ball of electric white, three times the apparent diameter of the

[007-127]sun. For a few minutes he was quite blind.

[007-128]“My God!” he exclaimed. “If it’s like this in early morning you must be

[007-129]right enough about Blodsombre.” When he had somewhat recovered himself

[007-130]he asked, “How long are the days here, Joiwind?”

[007-131]Again he felt his brain being probed.

[007-132]“At this time of the year, for every hour’s daylight that you have in

[007-133]summer, we have two.”

[007-134]“The heat is terrific—and yet somehow I don’t feel so distressed by it

[007-135]as I would have expected.”

[007-136]“I feel it more than usual. It’s not difficult to account for it; you

[007-137]have some of my blood, and I have some of yours.”

[007-138]“Yes, every time I realise that, I—Tell me, Joiwind, will my blood

[007-139]alter, if I stay here long enough?—I mean, will it lose its redness and

[007-140]thickness, and become pure and thin and light-coloured, like yours?”

[007-141]“Why not? If you live as we live, you will assuredly grow like us.”

[007-142]“Do you mean food and drink?”

[007-143]“We eat no food, and drink only water.”

[007-144]“And on that you manage to sustain life?”

[007-145]“Well, Maskull, our water is good water,” replied Joiwind, smiling.

[007-146]As soon as he could see again he stared around at the landscape. The

[007-147]enormous scarlet desert extended everywhere to the horizon, excepting

[007-148]where it was broken by the oasis. It was roofed by a cloudless, deep

[007-149]blue, almost violet, sky. The circle of the horizon was far larger than

[007-150]on earth. On the skyline, at right angles to the direction in which they

[007-151]were walking, appeared a chain of mountains, apparently about forty

[007-152]miles distant. One, which was higher than the rest, was shaped like a

[007-153]cup. Maskull would have felt inclined to believe he was travelling in

[007-154]dreamland, but for the intensity of the light, which made everything

[007-155]vividly real.

[007-156]Joiwind pointed to the cup-shaped mountain. “That’s Poolingdred.”

[007-157]“You didn’t come from there!” he exclaimed, quite startled.

[007-158]“Yes, I did indeed. And that is where we have to go to now.”

[007-159]“With the single object of finding me?”

[007-160]“Why, yes.”

[007-161]The colour mounted to his face. “Then you are the bravest and noblest of

[007-162]all girls,” he said quietly, after a pause. “Without exception. Why,

[007-163]this is a journey for an athlete!”

[007-164]She pressed his arm, while a score of unpaintable, delicate hues stained

[007-165]her cheeks in rapid transition. “Please don’t say any more about it,

[007-166]Maskull. It makes me feel unpleasant.”

[007-167]“Very well. But can we possibly get there before midday?”

[007-168]“Oh, yes. And you mustn’t be frightened at the distance. We think

[007-169]nothing of long distances here—we have so much to think about and feel.

[007-170]Time goes all too quickly.”

[007-171]During their conversation they had drawn near the base of the hills,

[007-172]which sloped gently, and were not above fifty feet in height. Maskull

[007-173]now began to see strange specimens of vegetable life. What looked like a

[007-174]small patch of purple grass, above five feet square, was moving across

[007-175]the sand in their direction. When it came near enough he perceived that

[007-176]it was not grass; there were no blades, but only purple roots. The roots

[007-177]were revolving, for each small plant in the whole patch, like the spokes

[007-178]of a rimless wheel. They were alternately plunged in the sand, and

[007-179]withdrawn from it, and by this means the plant proceeded forward. Some

[007-180]uncanny, semi-intelligent instinct was keeping all the plants together,

[007-181]moving at one pace, in one direction, like a flock of migrating birds in

[007-182]flight.

[007-183]Another remarkable plant was a large, feathery ball, resembling a

[007-184]dandelion fruit, which they encountered sailing through the air. Joiwind

[007-185]caught it with an exceedingly graceful movement of her arm, and showed

[007-186]it to Maskull. It had roots and presumably lived in the air and fed on

[007-187]the chemical constituents of the atmosphere. But what was peculiar about

[007-188]it was its colour. It was an entirely new colour—not a new shade or

[007-189]combination, but a new primary colour, as vivid as blue, red, or yellow,

[007-190]but quite different. When he inquired, she told him that it was known as

[007-191]“ulfire.” Presently he met with a second new colour. This she designated

[007-192]“jale.” The sense impressions caused in Maskull by these two additional

[007-193]primary colors can only be vaguely hinted at by analogy. Just as blue is

[007-194]delicate and mysterious, yellow clear and unsubtle, and red sanguine and

[007-195]passionate, so he felt ulfire to be wild and painful, and jale

[007-196]dreamlike, feverish, and voluptuous.
