# Section 28

[028-001]“You read character well, and have great perception,” remarked Gleameil

[028-002]quietly. “Now say what I am.”

[028-003]“In place of a human heart, you have a wild harp, and that’s all I know

[028-004]about you.”

[028-005]“What was that you said to my husband about two worlds?”

[028-006]“You heard.”

[028-007]“Yes, I heard. And I also am conscious of two worlds. My husband and

[028-008]boys are real to me, and I love them fondly. But there is another world

[028-009]for me, as there is for you, Maskull, and it makes my real world appear

[028-010]all false and vulgar.”

[028-011]“Perhaps we are seeking the same thing. But can it be right to satisfy

[028-012]our self-nature at the expense of other people?”

[028-013]“No, it’s not right. It is wrong, and base. But in that other world

[028-014]these words have no meaning.”

[028-015]There was a silence.

[028-016]“It’s useless to discuss such topics,” said Maskull. “The choice is now

[028-017]out of our hands, and we must go where we are taken. What I would rather

[028-018]speak about is what awaits us on the island.”

[028-019]“I am ignorant—except that we shall find Earthrid there.”

[028-020]“Who is Earthrid, and why is it called Swaylone’s Island?”

[028-021]“They say Earthrid came from Threal, but I know nothing else about him.

[028-022]As for Swaylone, if you like I will tell you his legend.”

[028-023]“If you please,” said Maskull.

[028-024]“In a far-back age,” began Gleameil, “when the seas were hot, and clouds

[028-025]hung heavily over the earth, and life was rich with transformations,

[028-026]Swaylone came to this island, on which men had never before set foot,

[028-027]and began to play his music—the first music in Tormance. Nightly, when

[028-028]the moon shone, people used to gather on this shore behind us, and

[028-029]listen to the faint, sweet strains floating from over the sea. One

[028-030]night, Shaping (whom you call Crystalman) was passing this way in

[028-031]company with Krag. They listened a while to the music, and Shaping said

[028-032]‘Have you heard more beautiful sounds? This is my world and my music.’

[028-033]Krag stamped with his foot, and laughed. ‘You must do better than that,

[028-034]if I am to admire it. Let us pass over, and see this bungler at work.’

[028-035]Shaping consented, and they passed over to the island. Swaylone was not

[028-036]able to see their presence. Shaping stood behind him, and breathed

[028-037]thoughts into his soul, so that his music became ten times lovelier, and

[028-038]people listening on that shore went mad with sick delight. ‘Can any

[028-039]strains be nobler?’ demanded Shaping. Krag grinned and said, ‘You are

[028-040]naturally effeminate. Now let me try.’ Then he stood behind Swaylone,

[028-041]and shot ugly discords fast into his head. His instrument was so

[028-042]cracked, that never since has it played right. From that time forth

[028-043]Swaylone could utter only distorted music; yet it called to folk more

[028-044]than the other sort. Many men crossed over to the island during his

[028-045]lifetime, to listen to the amazing tones, but none could endure them;

[028-046]all died. After Swaylone’s death, another musician took up the tale; and

[028-047]so the light has passed down from torch to torch, till now Earthrid

[028-048]bears it.”

[028-049]“An interesting legend,” commented Maskull. “But who is Krag?”

[028-050]“They say that when the world was born, Krag was born with it—a spirit

[028-051]compounded of those vestiges of Muspel which Shaping did not know how to

[028-052]transform. Thereafter nothing has gone right with the world, for he dogs

[028-053]Shaping’s footsteps everywhere, and whatever the latter does, he undoes.

[028-054]To love he joins death; to sex, shame; to intellect, madness; to virtue,

[028-055]cruelty; and to fair exteriors, bloody entrails. These are Krag’s

[028-056]actions, so the lovers of the world call him ‘devil.’ They don’t

[028-057]understand, Maskull, that without him the world would lose its beauty.”

[028-058]“Krag and beauty!” exclaimed he, with a cynical smile.

[028-059]“Even so. That same beauty which you and I are now voyaging to discover.

[028-060]That beauty for whose sake I am renouncing husband, children, and

[028-061]happiness.... Did you imagine beauty to be pleasant?”

[028-062]“Surely.”

[028-063]“That pleasant beauty is an insipid compound of Shaping. To see beauty

[028-064]in its terrible purity, you must tear away the pleasure from it.”

[028-065]“Do you say I am going to seek beauty, Gleameil? Such an idea is far

[028-066]from my mind.”

[028-067]She did not respond to his remark. After waiting for a few minutes, to

[028-068]hear if she would speak again, he turned his back on her once more.

[028-069]There was no more talk until they reached the island.

[028-070]The air had grown chill and damp by the time they approached its shores.

[028-071]Branchspell was on the point of touching the sea. The Island appeared to

[028-072]be some three or four miles in length. There were first of all broad

[028-073]sands, then low, dark cliffs, and behind these a wilderness of

[028-074]insignificant, swelling hills, entirely devoid of vegetation. The

[028-075]current bore them to within a hundred yards of the coast, when it made a

[028-076]sharp angle, and proceeded to skirt the length of the land.

[028-077]Gleameil jumped overboard, and began swimming to shore. Maskull followed

[028-078]her example, and the raft, abandoned, was rapidly borne away by the

[028-079]current. They soon touched ground, and were able to wade the rest of the

[028-080]way. By the time they reached dry land, the sun had set.

[028-081]Gleameil made straight for the hills; and Maskull, after casting a

[028-082]single glance at the low, dim outline of the Wombflash Forest, followed

[028-083]her. The cliffs were soon scrambled up. Then the ascent was gentle and

[028-084]easy, while the rich, dry, brown mould was good to walk upon.

[028-085]A little way off, on their left, something white was shining.

[028-086]“You need not go to it,” said the woman. “It can be nothing else than

[028-087]one of those skeletons Polecrab talked about. And look—there is another

[028-088]one over there!”

[028-089]“This brings it home!” remarked Maskull, smiling.

[028-090]“There is nothing comical in having died for beauty,” said Gleameil,

[028-091]bending her brows at him.

[028-092]And when in the course of their walk he saw the innumerable human bones,

[028-093]from gleaming white to dirty yellow, lying scattered about, as if it

[028-094]were a naked graveyard among the hills, he agreed with her, and fell

[028-095]into a sombre mood.

[028-096]It was still light when they reached the highest point, and could set

[028-097]eyes on the other side. The sea to the north of the island was in no way

[028-098]different from that which they had crossed, but its lively colors were

[028-099]fast becoming invisible.

[028-100]“That is Matterplay,” said the woman, pointing her finger toward some

[028-101]low land on the horizon, which seemed to be even farther off than

[028-102]Wombflash.

[028-103]“I wonder how Digrung passed over,” meditated Maskull.

[028-104]Not far away, in a hollow enclosed by a circle of little hills, they saw

[028-105]a small, circular lake, not more than half a mile in diameter. The

[028-106]sunset colors of the sky were reflected in its waters.

[028-107]“That must be Irontick,” remarked Gleameil.

[028-108]“What is that?”

[028-109]“I have heard that it’s the instrument Earthrid plays on.”

[028-110]“We are getting close,” responded he. “Let us go and investigate.”

[028-111]When they drew nearer, they observed that a man was reclining on the

[028-112]farther side, in an attitude of sleep.

[028-113]“If that’s not the man himself, who can it be?” said Maskull. “Let’s get

[028-114]across the water, if it will bear us; it will save time.”

[028-115]He now assumed the lead, and took running strides down the slope which

[028-116]bounded the lake on that side. Gleameil followed him with greater

[028-117]dignity, keeping her eyes fixed on the recumbent man as if fascinated.

[028-118]When Maskull reached the water’s edge, he tried it with one foot, to

[028-119]discover if it would carry his weight. Something unusual in its

[028-120]appearance led him to have doubts. It was a tranquil, dark, and

[028-121]beautifully reflecting sheet of water; it resembled a mirror of liquid

[028-122]metal. Finding that it would bear him, and that nothing happened, he

[028-123]placed his second foot on its surface. Instantly he sustained a violent

[028-124]shock throughout his body, as from a powerful electric current; and he

[028-125]was hurled in a tumbled heap back on to the bank.

[028-126]He picked himself up, brushed the dirt off his person, and started

[028-127]walking around the lake. Gleameil joined him, and they completed the

[028-128]half circuit together. They came to the man, and Maskull prodded him

[028-129]with his foot. He woke up, and blinked at them.

[028-130]His face was pale, weak, and vacant-looking, and had a disagreeable

[028-131]expression. There were thin sprouts of black hair on his chin and head.

[028-132]On his forehead, in place of a third eye, he possessed a perfectly

[028-133]circular organ, with elaborate convolutions, like an ear. He had an

[028-134]unpleasant smell. He appeared to be of young middle age.

[028-135]“Wake up, man,” said Maskull sharply, “and tell us if you are Earthrid.”

[028-136]“What time is it?” counterquestioned the man. “Does it want long to

[028-137]moonrise?”

[028-138]Without appearing to care about an answer, he sat up, and turning away

[028-139]from them, began to scoop up the loose soil with his hand, and to eat it

[028-140]halfheartedly.

[028-141]“Now, how can you eat that filth?” demanded Maskull, in disgust.

[028-142]“Don’t be angry, Maskull,” said Gleameil, laying hold of his arm, and

[028-143]flushing a little. “It is Earthrid—the man who is to help us.”

[028-144]“He has not said so.”

[028-145]“I am Earthrid,” said the other, in his weak and muffled voice, which,

[028-146]however, suddenly struck Maskull as being autocratic. “What do you want

[028-147]here? Or rather, you had better get away as quickly as you can, for it

[028-148]will be too late when Teargeld rises.”

[028-149]“You need not explain,” exclaimed Maskull. “We know your reputation, and

[028-150]we have come to hear your music. But what’s that organ for on your

[028-151]forehead?”

[028-152]Earthrid glared, and smiled, and glared again.

[028-153]“That is for rhythm, which is what changes noise into music. Don’t stand

[028-154]and argue, but go away. It is no pleasure to me to people the island

[028-155]with corpses. They corrupt the air, and do nothing else.”

[028-156]Darkness now crept swiftly on over the landscape.

[028-157]“You are rather bigmouthed,” said Maskull coolly. “But after we have

[028-158]heard you play, perhaps I shall adventure a tune myself.”

[028-159]“You? Are you a musician, then? Do you even know what music is?”

[028-160]A flame danced in Gleameil’s eyes.

[028-161]“Maskull thinks music reposes in the instrument,” she said in her

[028-162]intense way. “But it is in the soul of the Master.”

[028-163]“Yes,” said Earthrid, “but that is not all. I will tell you what it is.

[028-164]In Threal, where I was born and brought up, we learn the mystery of the

[028-165]Three in nature. This world, which lies extended before us, has three

[028-166]directions. Length is the line which shuts off what is, from what is

[028-167]not. Breadth is the surface which shows us in what manner one thing of

[028-168]what-is, lives with another thing. Depth is the path which leads from

[028-169]what-is, to our own body. In music it is not otherwise. Tone is

[028-170]existence, without which nothing at all can be. Symmetry and Numbers are

[028-171]the manner in which tones exist, one with another. Emotion is the

[028-172]movement of our soul toward the wonderful world that is being created.

[028-173]Now, men when they make music are accustomed to build beautiful tones,

[028-174]because of the delight they cause. Therefore their music world is based

[028-175]on pleasure; its symmetry is regular and charming, its emotion is sweet

[028-176]and lovely.... But my music is founded on painful tones; and thus its

[028-177]symmetry is wild, and difficult to discover; its emotion is bitter and

[028-178]terrible.”

[028-179]“If I had not anticipated its being original, I would not have come

[028-180]here,” said Maskull. “Still, explain—why can’t harsh tones have simple

[028-181]symmetry of form? And why must they necessarily cause more profound

[028-182]emotions in us who listen?”

[028-183]“Pleasures may harmonise. Pains must clash; and in the order of their

[028-184]clashing lies the symmetry. The emotions follow the music, which is

[028-185]rough and earnest.”
