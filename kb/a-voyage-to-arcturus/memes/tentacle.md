# Tentacle

## Definition

The tentacle, also called the heart tentacle, is a sensory organ that grows from the chest of beings on Tormance. Unlike the facial organs — [[breve]], [[poigns]], [[magn]], and [[sorb]] — the tentacle is a long, whip-like appendage that serves as the organ of direct emotional connection between individuals. By touching another being with the tentacle, one can share inner states, love, and emotional truth without the mediation of language.

## In the Novel

The tentacle is one of the first new organs Maskull discovers upon waking on Tormance:

> [006-123] From the region of his heart, a tentacle had budded. It was as long as his arm, but thin, like whipcord, and soft and flexible.

Joiwind, the first Tormance native Maskull encounters, also possesses the heart tentacle [006-160]. She uses it to communicate feeling directly:

> [008-033] …she threw her tentacle around him…

> [008-046] "By means of it what we love becomes part of us."

The tentacle thus functions as the organ of love in Lindsay's physiological allegory. Just as the breve enables silent thought-exchange and the magn enables perception of inner nature, the tentacle enables the direct transmission of emotional states — particularly love — between beings.

## Philosophical Meaning

In Lindsay's system, each organ corresponds to a mode of perception or connection. The tentacle, growing from the heart rather than the head, represents the capacity for emotional rather than intellectual connection. Its whip-like, flexible nature suggests the adaptability and reach of love, while its location near the heart anchors it in the novel's consistent association of the heart with emotional truth.

The tentacle's function — making what is loved "part of us" [008-046] — speaks to a central theme of the novel: the question of whether connection with others enriches or corrupts the individual soul. For characters like Joiwind, who embodies love and self-sacrifice, the tentacle is an organ of genuine sharing. For Spadevil's ascetic philosophy, by contrast, all emotional connection is suspect because it binds the soul to the world of pleasure.
