# Barey

## Location

Barey is a region of Tormance accessible via the Mornstab Pass, situated at a lower elevation than the surrounding terrain. It is characterized by its unique atmospheric condition of receiving light from two suns simultaneously [020-006].

## Description

Barey is described as "low-lying land" [031-008] that descends from the Mornstab Pass. The region's most distinctive feature is its dual solar illumination — unlike most of Tormance, Barey receives light from both suns, creating an intensified visual environment. The landscape is obscured by a "low haze" that conceals it from view when approached from above, and the air is characterized by "complete stillness" [031-023, 031-025].

Descending into Barey reveals "a whole forest" on the left [031-008], and the approach is made via earth and rock slopes "overgrown" with vegetation [031-015]. Later, the region reveals itself as "one big mountain" with a complex landscape of "lakes and forests" [034-081].

## Narrative Events

Maskull learns of Barey's dual-sun phenomenon before arriving: "Barey, where you have two suns instead of one — but beyond that fact I know nothing" [025-024], a character tells him. The approach to Barey involves traversing the Mornstab Pass, a high mountain route that connects the uplands to the lowlands [029-096].

Upon entering Barey, Maskull experiences a kind of sensory and spiritual overload. The intensified light, the unusual stillness, and the landscape's density all contribute to a heightened state of awareness. It is in Barey that Maskull's philosophical transformation approaches its culmination, as the novel's final revelations are prepared.

## Symbolic Significance

Barey's dual-sun illumination makes it the most light-saturated region Maskull visits, and this physical brightness corresponds to spiritual intensity. In Lindsay's color-symbol system, the combination of different light sources represents the convergence of different modes of perception — the ordinary solar light and the transformative Alppain light together create conditions under which reality is seen more fully but also more challengingly.

The region's name, sounding like "bare" or "barely," suggests exposure and extremity — a stripping-away of protective illusions that prepares Maskull for the novel's climactic revelations. The low-lying nature of Barey, reached by descent rather than ascent, inverts the conventional symbolism of spiritual elevation: in Lindsay's system, the deepest truths are found not on mountain peaks but in the humble lowlands.
