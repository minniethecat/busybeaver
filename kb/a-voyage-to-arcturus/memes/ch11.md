# Section 11

[011-001]“We sat down and faced one another, about two yards apart.

[011-002]“‘Which of us walks over the other?’ asked Slofork. His manner was as

[011-003]calm as the day itself, but, to my young nature, terrible with hidden

[011-004]terrors. I smiled at him, but did not wish for this humiliation. We

[011-005]continued sitting thus, in a friendly way, for many minutes.

[011-006]“‘What is greater than Pleasure?’ he asked suddenly.

[011-007]“I was at an age when one wishes to be thought equal to any emergency,

[011-008]so, concealing my surprise, I applied myself to the conversation, as if

[011-009]it were for that purpose we had met.

[011-010]“‘Pain,’ I replied, ‘for pain drives out pleasure.’

[011-011]“‘What is greater than Pain?’

[011-012]“I reflected. ‘Love. Because we will accept our loved one’s share of

[011-013]pain.’

[011-014]“‘But what is greater than Love?’ he persisted.

[011-015]“‘Nothing, Slofork.’

[011-016]“‘And what is Nothing?’

[011-017]“‘That you must tell me.’

[011-018]“‘Tell you I will. This is Shaping’s world. He that is a good child

[011-019]here, knows pleasure, pain, and love, and gets his rewards. But there’s

[011-020]another world—not Shaping’s—and there all this is unknown, and another

[011-021]order of things reigns. That world we call Nothing—but it is not

[011-022]Nothing, but Something.’

[011-023]“There was a pause.

[011-024]“‘I have heard,’ said I, ‘that you are good at growing and ungrowing

[011-025]organs?’

[011-026]“‘That’s not enough for me. Every organ tells me the same story. I want

[011-027]to hear different stories.’

[011-028]“‘Is it true, what men say, that your wisdom flows and ebbs in pulses?’

[011-029]“‘Quite true,’ replied Slofork. ‘But those you had it from did not add

[011-030]that they have always mistaken the flow for the ebb.’

[011-031]“‘My experience is,’ said I sententiously, ‘that wisdom is misery.’

[011-032]“‘Perhaps it is, young man, but you have never learned that, and never

[011-033]will. For you the world will continue to wear a noble, awful face. You

[011-034]will never rise above mysticism.... But be happy in your own way.’

[011-035]“Before I realised what he was doing, he jumped tranquilly from the

[011-036]path, down into the empty void. He crashed with ever-increasing momentum

[011-037]toward the valley below. I screeched, flung myself down on the ground,

[011-038]and shut my eyes.

[011-039]“Often have I wondered which of my ill-considered, juvenile remarks it

[011-040]was that caused this sudden resolution on his part to commit suicide.

[011-041]Whichever it might be, since then I have made it a rigid law never to

[011-042]speak for my own pleasure, but only to help others.

[011-043]“I came eventually to the Marest. I threaded its mazes in terror for

[011-044]four days. I was frightened of death, but still more terrified at the

[011-045]possibility of losing my sacred attitude toward life. When I was nearly

[011-046]through, and was beginning to congratulate myself, I stumbled across the

[011-047]third extraordinary personage of my experience—the grim Muremaker. It

[011-048]was under horrible circumstances. On an afternoon, cloudy and stormy, I

[011-049]saw, suspended in the air without visible support, a living man. He was

[011-050]hanging in an upright position in front of a cliff—a yawning gulf, a

[011-051]thousand feet deep, lay beneath his feet. I climbed as near as I could,

[011-052]and looked on. He saw me, and made a wry grimace, like one who wishes to

[011-053]turn his humiliation into humour. The spectacle so astounded me that I

[011-054]could not even grasp what had happened.

[011-055]“‘I am Muremaker,’ he cried in a scraping voice which shocked my ears.

[011-056]‘All my life I have sorbed others—now I am sorbed. Nuclamp and I fell

[011-057]out over a woman. Now Nuclamp holds me up like this. While the strength

[011-058]of his will lasts I shall remain suspended; but when he gets tired—and

[011-059]it can’t be long now—I drop into those depths.’

[011-060]“Had it been another man, I would have tried to save him, but this ogre-

[011-061]like being was too well known to me as one who passed his whole

[011-062]existence in tormenting, murdering, and absorbing others, for the sake

[011-063]of his own delight. I hurried away, and did not pause again that day.

[011-064]“In Poolingdred I met Joiwind. We walked and talked together for a

[011-065]month, and by that time we found that we loved each other too well to

[011-066]part.”

[011-067]Panawe stopped speaking.

[011-068]“That is a fascinating story,” remarked Maskull. “Now I begin to know my

[011-069]way around better. But one thing puzzles me.”

[011-070]“What’s that?”

[011-071]“How it happens that men here are ignorant of tools and arts, and have

[011-072]no civilisation, and yet contrive to be social in their habits and wise

[011-073]in their thoughts.”

[011-074]“Do you imagine, then, that love and wisdom spring from tools? But I see

[011-075]how it arises. In your world you have fewer sense organs, and to make up

[011-076]for the deficiency you have been obliged to call in the assistance of

[011-077]stones and metals. That’s by no means a sign of superiority.”

[011-078]“No, I suppose not,” said Maskull, “but I see I have a great deal to

[011-079]unlearn.”

[011-080]They talked together a little longer, and then gradually fell asleep.

[011-081]Joiwind opened her eyes, smiled, and slumbered again.

[011-082]Chapter 8. THE LUSION PLAIN

[011-083]Maskull awoke before the others. He got up, stretched himself, and

[011-084]walked out into the sunlight. Branchspell was already declining. He

[011-085]climbed to the top of the crater edge and looked away toward Ifdawn. The

[011-086]afterglow of Alppain had by now completely disappeared. The mountains

[011-087]stood up wild and grand.

[011-088]They impressed him like a simple musical theme, the notes of which are

[011-089]widely separated in the scale; a spirit of rashness, daring, and

[011-090]adventure seemed to call to him from them. It was at that moment that

[011-091]the determination flashed into his heart to walk to the Marest and

[011-092]explore its dangers.

[011-093]He returned to the cavern to say good-by to his hosts.

[011-094]Joiwind looked at him with her brave and honest eyes. “Is this

[011-095]selfishness, Maskull?” she asked, “or are you drawn by something

[011-096]stronger than yourself?”

[011-097]“We must be reasonable,” he answered, smiling. “I can’t settle down in

[011-098]Poolingdred before I have found out something about this surprising new

[011-099]planet of yours. Remember what a long way I have come.... But very

[011-100]likely I shall come back here.”

[011-101]“Will you make me a promise?”

[011-102]Maskull hesitated. “Ask nothing difficult, for I hardly know my powers

[011-103]yet.”

[011-104]“It is not hard, and I wish it. Promise this—never to raise your hand

[011-105]against a living creature, either to strike, pluck, or eat, without

[011-106]first recollecting its mother, who suffered for it.”

[011-107]“Perhaps I won’t promise that,” said Maskull slowly, “but I’ll undertake

[011-108]something more tangible. I will never lift my hand against a living

[011-109]creature without first recollecting you, Joiwind.”

[011-110]She turned a little pale. “Now if Panawe knew that Panawe existed, he

[011-111]might be jealous.”

[011-112]Panawe put his hand on her gently. “You would not talk like that in

[011-113]Shaping’s presence,” he said.

[011-114]“No. Forgive me! I’m not quite myself. Perhaps it is Maskull’s blood in

[011-115]my veins.... Now let us bid him adieu. Let us pray that he will do only

[011-116]honourable deeds, wherever he may be.”

[011-117]“I’ll set Maskull on his way,” said Panawe.

[011-118]“There’s no need,” replied Maskull. “The way is plain.”

[011-119]“But talking shortens the road.”

[011-120]Maskull turned to go.

[011-121]Joiwind pulled him around toward her softly. “You won’t think badly of

[011-122]other women on my account?”

[011-123]“You are a blessed spirit,” answered he.

[011-124]She trod quietly to the inner extremity of the cave and stood there

[011-125]thinking. Panawe and Maskull emerged into the open air. Halfway down the

[011-126]cliff face a little spring was encountered. Its water was colourless,

[011-127]transparent, but gaseous. As soon as Maskull had satisfied his thirst he

[011-128]felt himself different. His surroundings were so real to him in their

[011-129]vividness and colour, so unreal in their phantom-like mystery, that he

[011-130]scrambled downhill like one in a winter’s dream.

[011-131]When they reached the plain he saw in front of them an interminable

[011-132]forest of tall trees, the shapes of which were extraordinarily foreign

[011-133]looking. The leaves were crystalline and, looking upward, it was as if

[011-134]he were gazing through a roof of glass. The moment they got underneath

[011-135]the trees the light rays of the sun continued to come through—white,

[011-136]savage, and blazing—but they were gelded of heat. Then it was not hard

[011-137]to imagine that they were wandering through cool, bright elfin glades.

[011-138]Through the forest, beginning at their very feet an avenue, perfectly

[011-139]straight and not very wide, went forward as far as the eye could see.

[011-140]Maskull wanted to talk to his travelling companion, but was somehow

[011-141]unable to find words. Panawe glanced at him with an inscrutable

[011-142]smile—stern, yet enchanting and half feminine. He then broke the

[011-143]silence, but, strangely enough, Maskull could not make out whether he

[011-144]was singing or speaking. From his lips issued a slow musical recitative,

[011-145]exactly like a bewitching adagio from a low toned stringed

[011-146]instrument—but there was a difference. Instead of the repetition and

[011-147]variation of one or two short themes, as in music, Panawe’s theme was

[011-148]prolonged—it never came to an end, but rather resembled a conversation

[011-149]in rhythm and melody. And, at the same time, it was no recitative, for

[011-150]it was not declamatory. It was a long, quiet stream of lovely emotion.

[011-151]Maskull listened entranced, yet agitated. The song, if it might be

[011-152]termed song, seemed to be always just on the point of becoming clear and

[011-153]intelligible—not with the intelligibility of words, but in the way one

[011-154]sympathises with another’s moods and feelings; and Maskull felt that

[011-155]something important was about to be uttered, which would explain all

[011-156]that had gone before. But it was invariably postponed, he never

[011-157]understood—and yet somehow he did understand.

[011-158]Late in the afternoon they came to a clearing, and there Panawe ceased

[011-159]his recitative. He slowed his pace and stopped, in the fashion of a man

[011-160]who wishes to convey that he intends to go no farther.

[011-161]“What is the name of this country?” asked Maskull.

[011-162]“It is the Lusion Plain.”

[011-163]“Was that music in the nature of a temptation—do you wish me not to go

[011-164]on?”

[011-165]“Your work lies before you, and not behind you.”

[011-166]“What was it, then? What work do you allude to?”

[011-167]“It must have seemed like something to you, Maskull.”

[011-168]“It seemed like Shaping music to me.”

[011-169]The instant he had absently uttered these words, Maskull wondered why he

[011-170]had done so, as they now appeared meaningless to him.

[011-171]Panawe, however, showed no surprise. “Shaping you will find everywhere.”

[011-172]“Am I dreaming, or awake?”

[011-173]“You are awake.”

[011-174]Maskull fell into deep thought. “So be it,” he said, rousing himself.

[011-175]“Now I will go on. But where must I sleep tonight?”

[011-176]“You will reach a broad river. On that you can travel to the foot of the

[011-177]Marest tomorrow; but tonight you had better sleep where the forest and

[011-178]river meet.”

[011-179]“Adieu, then, Panawe! But do you wish to say anything more to me?”

[011-180]“Only this, Maskull—wherever you go, help to make the world beautiful,

[011-181]and not ugly.”

[011-182]“That’s more than any of us can undertake. I am a simple man, and have

[011-183]no ambitions in the way of beautifying life—But tell Joiwind I will try

[011-184]to keep myself pure.”

[011-185]They parted rather coldly. Maskull stood erect where they had stopped,

[011-186]and watched Panawe out of sight. He sighed more than once.

[011-187]He became aware that something was about to happen. The air was

[011-188]breathless. The late-afternoon sunshine, unobstructed, wrapped his frame

[011-190]sky overhead.

[011-191]A single trumpet note sounded in the far distance from somewhere behind

[011-192]him. It gave him an impression of being several miles away at first; but

[011-193]then it slowly swelled, and came nearer and nearer at the same time that
