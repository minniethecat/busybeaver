# Gleameil

## Role

Gleameil is introduced in Chapter 11 as the wife of [[polecrab]] and the mother of their children, living on the shores of the Sinking Sea in the land of Ifdawn Maret. She is a figure of maternal devotion and quiet strength who makes the difficult choice to leave her family behind and travel with [[maskull]] toward the Island. Her departure scene is one of the novel's most emotionally charged domestic moments, contrasting the tenderness of family life with the inexorable pull of the journey.

## Appearance and Manner

Gleameil is first seen sitting with her legs under her, erect and attentive [011-041]. She listens intently to the conversation between her husband [[polecrab|Polecrab]] and the visitor [[maskull|Maskull]] before revealing that she has overheard everything:

> "I heard everything," repeated Gleameil. "But what I want to know is, what he means to do." [011-012]

Beneath her composed exterior, Gleameil carries long-suppressed desire. When she reproaches [[polecrab|Polecrab]] — "No, you have always put me off" — her words come "with some emotion" [011-017], suggesting years of deferred hope.

When [[maskull|Maskull]] later arrives by raft to depart with Gleameil, she laughs at [[polecrab|Polecrab]]'s protective instinct, but says nothing [011-040]. Her silence in that moment conveys more than words — she has made her decision and will not be swayed.

## The Domestic Scene

Gleameil's home is described through her interactions with her family. When [[polecrab|Polecrab]] worries about taking her on a dangerous journey — "women die too easily in [[tormance|Tormance]]" [011-027] — Gleameil's response is not submission but resolve. She listens to the conversation between the men with quiet alertness, then asserts her own will.

Her relationship with [[polecrab|Polecrab]] is affectionate but complicated by his protective overprotectiveness. When she first states her intention to go, [[polecrab|Polecrab]] "looked on stolidly," but Gleameil "glanced away from them, with a strange expression" — suggesting emotions too complex for the domestic scene to contain [011-046].

## Departure

The departure is the emotional core of Gleameil's brief but vivid presence in the novel. Walking into the sea alongside [[maskull|Maskull]], she leaves behind her children on the shore:

> Gleameil kissed her children convulsively, and broke down a little. [011-050]

> Gleameil now walked into the sea, followed by Maskull. The water covered her shoulders; she still held the boys' hands. [011-051]

Her children wave from the shore. Gleameil responds; but [[maskull|Maskull]] turns away, unable to watch [011-052]. The word "convulsively" captures the violence of the separation — this is not a serene farewell but an act of will against maternal instinct.

When [[polecrab|Polecrab]] urges her to "make your farewells," she simply replies "To the Island" [011-048] — stating her destination as though the word itself were sufficient explanation for abandoning her family.

## Philosophical Significance

Gleameil's choice — to leave her children for an unknown destination — embodies a recurring tension in the novel between domestic love and the spiritual quest. Unlike many of the philosophical pilgrims [[maskull|Maskull]] encounters, Gleameil is not driven by doctrine or cosmic theory. Her motivation remains opaque, her interior life only partly revealed. She represents the cost that the journey extracts from ordinary life and ordinary love.

> "Don't take dejected views," replied Gleameil sternly. [011-043]

This reprimand to [[maskull|Maskull]] — spoken "sternly" — reveals a woman of strong will who refuses sentimentality even as she prepares to break her own heart.
