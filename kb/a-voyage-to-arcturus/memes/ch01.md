[001-025]Chapter 1. THE SÉANCE

[001-026]On a March evening, at eight o’clock, Backhouse, the medium—a fast-

[001-027]rising star in the psychic world—was ushered into the study at Prolands,

[001-028]the Hampstead residence of Montague Faull. The room was illuminated only

[001-029]by the light of a blazing fire. The host, eying him with indolent

[001-030]curiosity, got up, and the usual conventional greetings were exchanged.

[001-031]Having indicated an easy chair before the fire to his guest, the South

[001-032]American merchant sank back again into his own. The electric light was

[001-033]switched on. Faull’s prominent, clear-cut features, metallic-looking

[001-034]skin, and general air of bored impassiveness, did not seem greatly to

[001-035]impress the medium, who was accustomed to regard men from a special

[001-036]angle. Backhouse, on the contrary, was a novelty to the merchant. As he

[001-037]tranquilly studied him through half closed lids and the smoke of a

[001-038]cigar, he wondered how this little, thickset person with the pointed

[001-039]beard contrived to remain so fresh and sane in appearance, in view of

[001-040]the morbid nature of his occupation.

[001-041]“Do you smoke?” drawled Faull, by way of starting the conversation. “No?

[001-042]Then will you take a drink?”

[001-043]“Not at present, I thank you.”

[001-044]A pause.

[001-045]“Everything is satisfactory? The materialisation will take place?”

[001-046]“I see no reason to doubt it.”

[001-047]“That’s good, for I would not like my guests to be disappointed. I have

[001-048]your check written out in my pocket.”

[001-049]“Afterward will do quite well.”

[001-050]“Nine o’clock was the time specified, I believe?”

[001-051]“I fancy so.”

[001-052]The conversation continued to flag. Faull sprawled in his chair, and

[001-053]remained apathetic.

[001-054]“Would you care to hear what arrangements I have made?”

[001-055]“I am unaware that any are necessary, beyond chairs for your guests.”

[001-056]“I mean the decoration of the siance room, the music, and so forth.”

[001-057]Backhouse stared at his host. “But this is not a theatrical

[001-058]performance.”

[001-059]“That’s correct. Perhaps I ought to explain.... There will be ladies

[001-060]present, and ladies, you know, are aesthetically inclined.”

[001-061]“In that case I have no objection. I only hope they will enjoy the

[001-062]performance to the end.”

[001-063]He spoke rather dryly.

[001-064]“Well, that’s all right, then,” said Faull. Flicking his cigar into the

[001-065]fire, he got up and helped himself to whisky.

[001-066]“Will you come and see the room?”

[001-067]“Thank you, no. I prefer to have nothing to do with it till the time

[001-068]arrives.”

[001-069]“Then let’s go to see my sister, Mrs. Jameson, who is in the drawing

[001-070]room. She sometimes does me the kindness to act as my hostess, as I am

[001-071]unmarried.”

[001-072]“I will be delighted,” said Backhouse coldly.

[001-073]They found the lady alone, sitting by the open pianoforte in a pensive

[001-074]attitude. She had been playing Scriabin and was overcome. The medium

[001-075]took in her small, tight, patrician features and porcelain-like hands,

[001-076]and wondered how Faull came by such a sister. She received him bravely,

[001-077]with just a shade of quiet emotion. He was used to such receptions at

[001-078]the hands of the sex, and knew well how to respond to them.

[001-079]“What amazes me,” she half whispered, after ten minutes of graceful,

[001-080]hollow conversation, “is, if you must know it, not so much the

[001-081]manifestation itself—though that will surely be wonderful—as your

[001-082]assurance that it will take place. Tell me the grounds of your

[001-083]confidence.”

[001-084]“I dream with open eyes,” he answered, looking around at the door, “and

[001-085]others see my dreams. That is all.”

[001-086]“But that’s beautiful,” responded Mrs. Jameson. She smiled rather

[001-087]absently, for the first guest had just entered.

[001-088]It was Kent-Smith, the ex-magistrate, celebrated for his shrewd judicial

[001-089]humour, which, however, he had the good sense not to attempt to carry

[001-090]into private life. Although well on the wrong side of seventy, his eyes

[001-091]were still disconcertingly bright. With the selective skill of an old

[001-092]man, he immediately settled himself in the most comfortable of many

[001-093]comfortable chairs.

[001-094]“So we are to see wonders tonight?”

[001-095]“Fresh material for your autobiography,” remarked Faull.

[001-096]“Ah, you should not have mentioned my unfortunate book. An old public

[001-097]servant is merely amusing himself in his retirement, Mr. Backhouse. You

[001-098]have no cause for alarm—I have studied in the school of discretion.”

[001-099]“I am not alarmed. There can be no possible objection to your publishing

[001-100]whatever you please.”

[001-101]“You are most kind,” said the old man, with a cunning smile.

[001-102]“Trent is not coming tonight,” remarked Mrs. Jameson, throwing a curious

[001-103]little glance at her brother.

[001-104]“I never thought he would. It’s not in his line.”

[001-105]“Mrs. Trent, you must understand,” she went on, addressing the ex-

[001-106]magistrate, “has placed us all under a debt of gratitude. She has

[001-107]decorated the old lounge hall upstairs most beautifully, and has secured

[001-108]the services of the sweetest little orchestra.”

[001-109]“But this is Roman magnificence.”

[001-110]“Backhouse thinks the spirits should be treated with more deference,”

[001-111]laughed Faull.

[001-112]“Surely, Mr. Backhouse—a poetic environment...”

[001-113]“Pardon me. I am a simple man, and always prefer to reduce things to

[001-114]elemental simplicity. I raise no opposition, but I express my opinion.

[001-115]Nature is one thing, and art is another.”

[001-116]“And I am not sure that I don’t agree with you,” said the ex-magistrate.

[001-117]“An occasion like this ought to be simple, to guard against the

[001-118]possibility of deception—if you will forgive my bluntness, Mr.

[001-119]Backhouse.”

[001-120]“We shall sit in full light,” replied Backhouse, “and every opportunity

[001-121]will be given to all to inspect the room. I shall also ask you to submit

[001-122]me to a personal examination.”

[001-123]A rather embarrassed silence followed. It was broken by the arrival of

[001-124]two more guests, who entered together. These were Prior, the prosperous

[001-125]City coffee importer, and Lang, the stockjobber, well known in his own

[001-126]circle as an amateur prestidigitator. Backhouse was slightly acquainted

[001-127]with the latter. Prior, perfuming the room with the faint odour of wine

[001-128]and tobacco smoke, tried to introduce an atmosphere of joviality into

[001-129]the proceedings. Finding that no one seconded his efforts, however, he

[001-130]shortly subsided and fell to examining the water colours on the walls.

[001-131]Lang, tall, thin, and growing bald, said little, but stared at Backhouse

[001-132]a good deal.

[001-133]Coffee, liqueurs, and cigarettes were now brought in. Everyone partook,

[001-134]except Lang and the medium. At the same moment, Professor Halbart was

[001-135]announced. He was the eminent psychologist, the author and lecturer on

[001-136]crime, insanity, genius, and so forth, considered in their mental

[001-137]aspects. His presence at such a gathering somewhat mystified the other

[001-138]guests, but all felt as if the object of their meeting had immediately

[001-139]acquired additional solemnity. He was small, meagre-looking, and mild in

[001-140]manner, but was probably the most stubborn-brained of all that mixed

[001-141]company. Completely ignoring the medium, he at once sat down beside

[001-142]Kent-Smith, with whom he began to exchange remarks.

[001-143]At a few minutes past the appointed hour Mrs. Trent entered,

[001-144]unannounced. She was a woman of about twenty-eight. She had a white,

[001-145]demure, saintlike face, smooth black hair, and lips so crimson and full

[001-146]that they seemed to be bursting with blood. Her tall, graceful body was

[001-147]most expensively attired. Kisses were exchanged between her and Mrs.

[001-148]Jameson. She bowed to the rest of the assembly, and stole a half glance

[001-149]and a smile at Faull. The latter gave her a queer look, and Backhouse,

[001-150]who lost nothing, saw the concealed barbarian in the complacent gleam of

[001-151]his eye. She refused the refreshment that was offered her, and Faull

[001-152]proposed that, as everyone had now arrived, they should adjourn to the

[001-153]lounge hall.

[001-154]Mrs. Trent held up a slender palm. “Did you, or did you not, give me

[001-155]carte blanche, Montague?”

[001-156]“Of course I did,” said Faull, laughing. “But what’s the matter?”

[001-157]“Perhaps I have been rather presumptuous. I don’t know. I have invited a

[001-158]couple of friends to join us. No, no one knows them.... The two most

[001-159]extraordinary individuals you ever saw. And mediums, I am sure.”

[001-160]“It sounds very mysterious. Who are these conspirators?”

[001-161]“At least tell us their names, you provoking girl,” put in Mrs. Jameson.

[001-162]“One rejoices in the name of Maskull, and the other in that of

[001-163]Nightspore. That’s nearly all that I know about them, so don’t overwhelm

[001-164]me with any more questions.”

[001-165]“But where did you pick them up? You must have picked them up

[001-166]somewhere.”

[001-167]“But this is a cross-examination. Have I sinned against convention? I

[001-168]swear I will tell you not another word about them. They will be here

[001-169]directly, and then I will deliver them to your tender mercy.”

[001-170]“I don’t know them,” said Faull, “and nobody else seems to, but, of

[001-171]course, we will all be very pleased to have them.... Shall we wait, or

[001-172]what?”

[001-173]“I said nine, and it’s past that now. It’s quite possible they may not

[001-174]turn up after all.... Anyway, don’t wait.”

[001-175]“I would prefer to start at once,” said Backhouse.

[001-176]The lounge, a lofty room, forty feet long by twenty wide, had been

[001-177]divided for the occasion into two equal parts by a heavy brocade curtain

[001-178]drawn across the middle. The far end was thus concealed. The nearer half

[001-179]had been converted into an auditorium by a crescent of armchairs. There

[001-180]was no other furniture. A large fire was burning halfway along the wall,

[001-181]between the chairbacks and the door. The room was brilliantly lighted by

[001-182]electric bracket lamps. A sumptuous carpet covered the floor.

[001-183]Having settled his guests in their seats, Faull stepped up to the

[001-184]curtain and flung it aside. A replica, or nearly so, of the Drury Lane

[001-185]presentation of the temple scene in The Magic Flute was then exposed to

[001-186]view: the gloomy, massive architecture of the interior, the glowing sky

[001-187]above it in the background, and, silhouetted against the latter, the

[001-188]gigantic seated statue of the Pharaoh. A fantastically carved wooden

[001-189]couch lay before the pedestal of the statue. Near the curtain, obliquely

[001-190]placed to the auditorium, was a plain oak armchair, for the use of the

[001-191]medium.

[001-192]Many of those present felt privately that the setting was quite

[001-193]inappropriate to the occasion and savoured rather unpleasantly of

[001-194]ostentation. Backhouse in particular seemed put out. The usual

[001-195]compliments, however, were showered on Mrs. Trent as the deviser of so

[001-196]remarkable a theatre. Faull invited his friends to step forward and

[001-197]examine the apartment as minutely as they might desire. Prior and Lang

[001-198]were the only ones to accept. The former wandered about among the

[001-199]pasteboard scenery, whistling to himself and occasionally tapping a part

[001-200]of it with his knuckles. Lang, who was in his element, ignored the rest

[001-201]of his party and commenced a patient, systematic search, on his own

[001-202]account, for secret apparatus. Faull and Mrs. Trent stood in a corner of

[001-203]the temple, talking together in low tones; while Mrs. Jameson,

[001-204]pretending to hold Backhouse in conversation, watched them as only a

[001-205]deeply interested woman knows how to watch.

[001-206]Lang, to his own disgust, having failed to find anything of a suspicious

[001-207]nature, the medium now requested that his own clothing should be

[001-208]searched.

[001-209]“All these precautions are quite needless and beside the matter in hand,

[001-210]as you will immediately see for yourselves. My reputation demands,

[001-211]however, that other people who are not present would not be able to say

[001-212]afterward that trickery has been resorted to.”

[001-213]To Lang again fell the ungrateful task of investigating pockets and

[001-214]sleeves. Within a few minutes he expressed himself satisfied that

[001-215]nothing mechanical was in Backhouse’s possession. The guests reseated

[001-216]themselves. Faull ordered two more chairs to be brought for Mrs. Trent’s

[001-217]friends, who, however, had not yet arrived. He then pressed an electric

[001-218]bell, and took his own seat.
