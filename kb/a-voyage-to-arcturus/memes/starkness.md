# Starkness

**Starkness Observatory** is the remote astronomical station on the north-east coast of Scotland where [[maskull|Maskull]], [[nightspore|Nightspore]], and [[krag|Krag]] gather before their departure for [[tormance|Tormance]]. It is the novel's threshold space — the liminal point between ordinary Earthly existence and the cosmic journey that follows. Here, Krag reveals the nature of their destination, the face of [[crystalman|Crystalman]] is first perceived, and the voyage to Arcturus is set in motion.

## Location

Starkness Observatory stands on the north-east coast of Scotland, seven miles from Haillar Station. Maskull and Nightspore arrive "on foot, having covered the seven miles from Haillar Station" (003-188), walking along "rather lofty cliffs, within sight of the North Sea" with "the plaintive, beautiful crying of the gulls" accompanying them (003-188).

The location is deliberately remote — a place suited for "curious discoveries" (003-081) made far from the distractions of civilization. Its coastal position, exposed to salt wind and the vastness of the sea, prefigures the cosmic voyage about to begin.

## Description

Starkness is introduced in Chapter 3 through Krag's question: "From the old observatory at Starkness.... Have you heard of the famous Starkness Observatory, Maskull?" (003-081). The observatory is described as a place of serious astronomical work — "Curious discoveries are made there from time to time" (003-081) — though the nature of those discoveries quickly transcends ordinary astronomy.

The journey to Starkness is itself marked by a sense of transition. The road is "very wild and lonely," the wind is "brisk" and "salt and cold," the sea's "dark green waves were flecked with white" (003-188). The gulls' crying provides an almost elegiac soundtrack to the final Earthly walk of the protagonists.

## Philosophical Significance

Starkness functions as a liminal space — a threshold between worlds. It is neither fully of Earth (its remote, wild character sets it apart from ordinary human habitation) nor of Tormance (it remains a place of stone, sea, and salt wind). This in-between quality makes it the appropriate setting for the transition from ordinary life to cosmic pilgrimage.

The observatory's function is also significant: it is a place for *looking* at the stars, not traveling to them. The transformation of Starkness from a site of distant observation to a point of actual departure mirrors Maskull's own transformation from passive observer to active seeker. The tower Maskull climbs in Chapter 4 physically enacts this shift — ascending from ground level to a height from which the cosmos is visible in a new way.

The novel's final sentence returns to Starkness: "the rays of Starkness... flashed across him with the certainty of truth" (046-021). In this closing moment, the journey's beginning and end are united — the observatory that launched the pilgrimage becomes, in retrospect, the lens through which its meaning is finally apprehended.
