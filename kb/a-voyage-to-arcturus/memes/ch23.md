# Section 23

[023-001]“It is false.”

[023-002]“Is it false to sacrifice oneself for another?” demanded Tydomin.

[023-003]“I can’t argue as yet,” said Maskull. “At this moment the world with its

[023-004]sweetness seems to me a sort of charnel house. I feel a loathing for

[023-005]everything in it, including myself. I know no more.”

[023-006]“Is there no duty?” asked Spadevil, in a harsh tone.

[023-007]“It appears to me but a cloak under which we share the pleasure of other

[023-008]people.”

[023-009]Tydomin pulled at Spadevil’s arm. “Maskull has betrayed you, as he has

[023-010]so many others. Let us go.”

[023-011]He stood fast. “You have changed quickly, Maskull.”

[023-012]Maskull, without answering him, turned to Catice. “Why do men go on

[023-013]living in this soft, shameful world, when they can kill themselves?”

[023-014]“Pain is the native air of Surtur’s children. To what other air do you

[023-015]wish to escape?”

[023-016]“Surtur’s children? Is not Surtur Shaping?”

[023-017]“It is the greatest of lies. It is Shaping’s masterpiece.”

[023-018]“Answer, Maskull!” said Spadevil. “Do you repudiate right action?”

[023-019]“Leave me alone. Go back! I am not thinking of you, and your ideas. I

[023-020]wish you no harm.”

[023-021]The darkness came on fast. There was another prolonged silence.

[023-022]Catice threw away the flint, and picked up his staff. “The woman must

[023-023]return home,” he said.

[023-024]“She was persuaded here, and did not come freely. You, Spadevil, must

[023-025]die—backslider as you are!”

[023-026]Tydomin said quietly, “He has no power to enforce this. Are you going to

[023-027]allow the truth to fall to the ground, Spadevil?”

[023-028]“It will not perish by my death, but by my efforts to escape from death.

[023-029]Catice, I accept your judgment.”

[023-030]Tydomin smiled. “For my part, I am too tired to walk farther today, so I

[023-031]shall die with him.”

[023-032]Catice said to Maskull, “Prove your sincerity. Kill this man and his

[023-033]mistress, according to the laws of Hator.”

[023-034]“I can’t do that. I have travelled in friendship with them.”

[023-035]“You denied duty; and now you must do your duty,” said Spadevil, calmly

[023-036]stroking his beard. “Whatever law you accept, you must obey, without

[023-037]turning to right or left. Your law commands that we must be stoned; and

[023-038]it will soon be dark.”

[023-039]“Have you not even this amount of manhood?” exclaimed Tydomin.

[023-040]Maskull moved heavily. “Be my witness, Catice, that the thing was forced

[023-041]on me.”

[023-042]“Hator is looking on, and approving,” replied Catice.

[023-043]Maskull then went apart to the pile of boulders scattered by the side of

[023-044]the pool. He glanced about him, and selected two large fragments of

[023-045]rock, the heaviest that he thought he could carry. With these in his

[023-046]arms, he staggered back.

[023-047]He dropped them on the ground, and stood, recovering his breath. When he

[023-048]could speak again, he said, “I have a bad heart for the business. Is

[023-049]there no alternative? Sleep here tonight, Spadevil, and in the morning

[023-050]go back to where you have come from. No one shall harm you.”

[023-051]Spadevil’s ironic smile was lost in the gloom.

[023-052]“Shall I brood again, Maskull, for still another year, and after that

[023-053]come back to Sant with other truths? Come, waste no time, but choose the

[023-054]heavier stone for me, for I am stronger than Tydomin.”

[023-055]Maskull lifted one of the rocks, and stepped out four full paces.

[023-056]Spadevil confronted him, erect, and waited tranquilly.

[023-057]The huge stone hurtled through the air. Its flight looked like a dark

[023-058]shadow. It struck Spadevil full in the face, crushing his features, and

[023-059]breaking his neck. He died instantaneously.

[023-060]Tydomin looked away from the fallen man.

[023-061]“Be very quick, Maskull, and don’t let me keep him waiting.”

[023-062]He panted, and raised the second stone. She placed herself in front of

[023-063]Spadevil’s body, and stood there, unsmiling and cold.

[023-064]The blow caught her between breast and chin, and she fell. Maskull went

[023-065]to her, and, kneeling on the ground, half-raised her in his arms. There

[023-066]she breathed out her last sighs.

[023-067]After that, he laid her down again, and rested heavily on his hands,

[023-068]while he peered into the dead face. The transition from its heroic,

[023-069]spiritual expression to the vulgar and grinning mask of Crystalman came

[023-070]like a flash; but he saw it.

[023-071]He stood up in the darkness, and pulled Catice toward him.

[023-072]“Is that the true likeness of Shaping?”

[023-073]“It is Shaping stripped of illusion.”

[023-074]“How comes this horrible world to exist?”

[023-075]Catice did not answer.

[023-076]“Who is Surtur?”

[023-077]“You will get nearer to him tomorrow; but not here.”

[023-078]“I am wading through too much blood,” said Maskull. “Nothing good can

[023-079]come of it.”

[023-080]“Do not fear change and destruction; but laughter and joy.”

[023-081]Maskull meditated.

[023-082]“Tell me, Catice. If I had elected to follow Spadevil, would you really

[023-083]have accepted his faith?”

[023-084]“He was a great-souled man,” replied Catice. “I see that the pride of

[023-085]our men is only another sprouting-out of pleasure. Tomorrow I too shall

[023-086]leave Sant, to reflect on all this.”

[023-087]Maskull shuddered. “Then these two deaths were not a necessity, but a

[023-088]crime!”

[023-089]“His part was played and henceforward the woman would have dragged down

[023-090]his ideas, with her soft love and loyalty. Regret nothing, stranger, but

[023-091]go away at once out of the land.”

[023-092]“Tonight? Where shall I go?”

[023-093]“To Wombflash, where you will meet the deepest minds. I will put you on

[023-094]the way.”

[023-095]He linked his arm in Maskull’s, and they walked away into the night. For

[023-096]a mile or more they skirted the edge of the precipice. The wind was

[023-097]searching, and drove grit into their faces. Through the rifts of the

[023-098]clouds, stars, faint and brilliant, appeared. Maskull saw no familiar

[023-099]constellations. He wondered if the sun of earth was visible, and if so

[023-100]which one it was.

[023-101]They came to the head of a rough staircase, leading down the cliffside.

[023-102]It resembled the one by which he had come up; but this descended to the

[023-103]Wombflash Forest.

[023-104]“That is your path,” said Catice, “and I shall not come any farther.”

[023-105]Maskull detained him. “Say just this, before we part company—why does

[023-106]pleasure appear so shameful to us?”

[023-107]“Because in feeling pleasure, we forget our home.”

[023-108]“And that is—”

[023-109]“Muspel,” answered Catice.

[023-110]Having made this reply, he disengaged himself, and, turning his back,

[023-111]disappeared into the darkness.

[023-112]Maskull stumbled down the staircase as best he could. He was tired, but

[023-113]contemptuous of his pains. His uninjured probe began to discharge

[023-114]matter. He lowered himself from step to step during what seemed an

[023-115]interminable time. The rustling and sighing of the trees grew louder as

[023-116]he approached the bottom; the air became still and warm. Inky blackness

[023-117]was all around him.

[023-118]*****

[023-119]He at last reached level ground. Still attempting to proceed, he began

[023-120]to trip over roots, and to collide with tree trunks. After this had

[023-121]happened a few times, he determined to go no farther that night. He

[023-122]heaped together some dry leaves for a pillow, and immediately flung

[023-123]himself down to sleep. Deep and heavy unconsciousness seized him almost

[023-124]instantly.

[023-125]Chapter 13. THE WOMBFLASH FOREST

[023-126]He awoke to his third day on Tormance. His limbs ached. He lay on his

[023-127]side, looking stupidly at his surroundings. The forest was like night,

[023-128]but that period of the night when the grey dawn is about to break and

[023-129]objects begin to be guessed at, rather than seen. Two or three amazing

[023-130]shadowy shapes, as broad as houses, loomed up out of the twilight. He

[023-131]did not realise that they were trees, until he turned over on his back

[023-132]and followed their course upward. Far overhead, so high up that he dared

[023-133]not calculate the height, he saw their tops glittering in the sunlight,

[023-134]against a tiny patch of blue sky.

[023-135]Clouds of mist, rolling over the floor of the forest, kept interrupting

[023-136]his view. In their silent passage they were like phantoms flitting among

[023-137]the trees. The leaves underneath him were sodden, and heavy drops of

[023-138]moisture splashed onto his head from time to time.

[023-139]He continued lying there, trying to reconstruct the events of the

[023-140]preceding day. His brain was lethargic and confused. Something terrible

[023-141]had happened, but what it was he could not for a long time recollect.

[023-142]Then suddenly there came before his eyes that ghastly closing scene at

[023-143]dusk on the Sant plateau—Spadevil’s crushed and bloody features and

[023-144]Tydomin’s dying sighs.... He shuddered convulsively, and felt sick.

[023-145]The peculiar moral outlook that had dictated these brutal murders had

[023-146]departed from him during the night, and now he recognised what he had

[023-147]done! During the whole of the previous day he seemed to have been

[023-148]labouring under a series of heavy enchantments. First Oceaxe had

[023-149]enslaved him, then Tydomin, then Spadevil, and lastly Catice. They had

[023-150]forced him to murder and violate; he had guessed nothing, but had

[023-151]imagined that he was travelling as a free and enlightened stranger. What

[023-152]was this nightmare journey for—and would it continue, in the same

[023-153]way?...

[023-154]The silence of the forest was so intense that he heard no sound except

[023-155]the pumping of blood through his arteries.

[023-156]Putting his hand to his face, he found that his remaining probe had

[023-157]disappeared and that he was in possession of three eyes. The third eye

[023-158]was on his forehead, where the old sorb had been. He could not guess its

[023-159]use. He still had his third arm, but it was nerveless.

[023-160]Now he puzzled his head for a long time, trying unsuccessfully to recall

[023-161]that name which had been the last word spoken by Catice.

[023-162]He got up, with the intention of resuming his journey. He had no toilet

[023-163]to make, and no meal to prepare. The forest was tremendous. The nearest

[023-164]tree appeared to him to have a circumference of at least a hundred feet.

[023-165]Other dim boles looked equally large. But what gave the scene its aspect

[023-166]of immensity was the vast spaces separating tree from tree. It was like

[023-167]some gigantic, supernatural hall in a life after death. The lowest

[023-168]branches were fifty yards or more from the ground. There was no

[023-169]underbrush; the soil was carpeted only by the dead, wet leaves. He

[023-170]looked all around him, to find his direction, but the cliffs of Sant,

[023-171]which he had descended, were invisible—every way was like every other

[023-172]way, he had no idea which quarter to attack. He grew frightened, and

[023-173]muttered to himself. Craning his neck back, he stared upward and tried

[023-174]to discover the points of the compass from the direction of the

[023-175]sunlight, but it was impossible.

[023-176]While he was standing there, anxious and hesitating, he heard the drum

[023-177]taps. The rhythmical beats proceeded from some distance off. The unseen

[023-178]drummer seemed to be marching through the forest, away from him.

[023-179]“Surtur!” he said, under his breath. The next moment he marvelled at

[023-180]himself for uttering the name. That mysterious being had not been in his

[023-181]thoughts, nor was there any ostensible connection between him and the

[023-182]drumming.

[023-183]He began to reflect—but in the meantime the sounds were travelling away.

[023-184]Automatically he started walking in the same direction. The drum beats

[023-185]had this peculiarity—though odd and mystical, there was nothing awe-

[023-186]inspiring in them, but on the contrary they reminded him of some place

[023-187]and some life with which he was perfectly familiar. Once again they

[023-188]caused all his other sense impressions to appear false.

[023-189]The sounds were intermittent. They would go on for a minute, or for five

[023-190]minutes, and then cease for perhaps a quarter of an hour. Maskull

[023-191]followed them as well as he could. He walked hard among the huge,

[023-192]indistinct trees, in the attempt to come up with the origin of the

[023-193]noise, but the same distance always seemed to separate them. The forest

[023-194]from now onward descended. The gradient was mostly gentle—about one foot

[023-195]in ten—but in some places it was much steeper, and in other parts again

[023-196]it was practically level ground for quite long stretches. There were

[023-197]great swampy marshes, through which Maskull was obliged to splash. It

[023-198]was a matter of indifference to him how wet he became—if only he could

[023-199]catch sight of that individual with the drum. Mile after mile was

[023-200]covered, and still he was no nearer to doing so.
