# Disscourn

## Location

Disscourn is the highest elevation in the Ifdawn region of Tormance, a mountain summit that towers above the surrounding landscape. From Disscourn, the terrain falls away in all directions except toward Sant, to which a narrow ridge connects [024-045].

The mountain is accessible by ascent from Ifdawn proper, and the chapter titled "On Disscourn" (Chapter 11) is devoted to the events that transpire there. Spadevil, one of Maskull's most formidable interlocutors, "lives under Disscourn" [024-053].

## Description

Disscourn is not merely a geographical feature but the symbolic apex of the Ifdawn region. As "the highest point in Ifdawn," it embodies the principle of extremity that characterizes the entire region — where Ifdawn is a place of "hammer blows" and "quick decisions," Disscourn concentrates this intensity into a single commanding summit.

The mountain's name, combining "diss" (perhaps suggesting dissolution or distance) with "courn" (echoing both "corn" and "scorn"), hints at the severe, stripping-away quality of the moral doctrine Spadevil preaches from its slopes. Under Disscourn lives Spadevil's "other wife," described as "a most dangerous woman" — the mountain's shadow harbors forces Maskull must confront [024-053].

## Narrative Events

Maskull ascends to Disscourn in Chapter 11, where he meets Spadevil and is subjected to the most uncompromising moral philosophy he has yet encountered on Tormance. Spadevil's doctrine, delivered from this mountain height, represents a form of ethical absolutism that strips away all comfort and convention.

Later, Tydomin tells Maskull: "I thought that perhaps you two would go to Disscourn, and spend the night" [024-057], suggesting that the mountain is a place of extended spiritual trial. The transition from Disscourn to Sant — across the narrow ridge that connects them — represents Maskull's passage from the realm of extreme moral philosophy to the realm of ultimate spiritual revelation.

## Symbolic Significance

Disscourn is the physical manifestation of moral extremism in Lindsay's allegorical geography. As the highest point in a region already characterized by extremity, it represents the pinnacle of philosophical absolutism — the point at which a moral system, pursued to its logical conclusion, becomes both awe-inspiring and dangerous.

Spadevil's location "under Disscourn" rather than on its peak is significant: the mountain overshadows him, suggesting that even the most rigorous moral philosopher is dwarfed by the principles he serves. The narrow ridge connecting Disscourn to Sant maps the difficult passage from moral philosophy to spiritual truth — one cannot reach Sant without first confronting what Disscourn represents.
