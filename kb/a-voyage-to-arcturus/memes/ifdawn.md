# Ifdawn

## Location

Ifdawn is a mountainous highland region of Tormance, situated between the Lusion Plain and the Sant plateau. It is bordered by the Ifdawn Marest and lies under the light of Alppain, Tormance's second sun. The Ifdawn Mountains rise prominently on the northern skyline as Maskull approaches from the south [008-086].

## Description

The Ifdawn Mountains are described as "high, wild" peaks that dominate the northern horizon [008-086]. More striking than their physical form is the quality of light that bathes them — the illumination of Alppain, Tormance's second sun, which paints the Ifdawn precipices in extraordinary, gaily colored light [008-209, 009-095].

The region is harsh. As one character explains: "Nature is all hammer blows with us. Nothing soft and gradual" [009-119]. The name Ifdawn itself — combining "if" with "dawn" — suggests contingency and emergence, a place where things come into being only through decisive action.

## Narrative Events

Maskull enters Ifdawn after crossing the Lusion Plain. Here he encounters Oceaxe, who becomes his guide and antagonist through the region's moral and physical challenges. The inhabitants of Ifdawn are described as a people of swift decisions: "One may call Ifdawn a place of quick decisions" [009-119].

The region operates under unusual social customs — as Oceaxe's blunt question, "Tell me, how do you remove husbands in Ifdawn?" reveals, the norms of Ifdawn differ radically from those of Poolingdred [009-203]. Maskull's time in Ifdawn is marked by accelerated events: "Don't wait too long, for time doesn't wait — especially in Ifdawn" [009-169].

The region's highest point is Disscourn, "the highest point in Ifdawn" [011-119], which serves as the gateway to the still more elevated Sant plateau.

## Symbolic Significance

Ifdawn represents the philosophical principle of decisive action. Where Poolingdred exemplifies gentle contemplation and the Wombflash Forest embodies deep wisdom, Ifdawn is the place of hammer blows — rapid, irreversible moral choices. Lindsay uses the region's harsh beauty and accelerated temporality to dramatize his view that spiritual growth requires not passive reception but active, even violent, engagement with reality.

The pairing of Ifdawn's name ("if-dawn") with its characterization as a place of quick decisions suggests that deliberation and hesitation — the "if" — must be resolved into the "dawn" of action. The Alppain light that illuminates the region marks it as a zone of heightened spiritual intensity.
