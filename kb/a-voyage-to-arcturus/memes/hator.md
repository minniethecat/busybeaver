# Hator

## Role

Hator is a legendary, quasi-mythical figure in the world of [[tormance|Tormance]] — the founder of the religious community of Sant and the originator of its ascetic doctrines. Though he does not appear in the narrative present (having lived "many thousands of years ago"), his teachings and his legendary confrontation with [[shaping]] are recounted in detail by [[spadevil]] in Chapters 20 and 21, making him one of the novel's most significant offstage characters.

## Founder of Sant

Hator is first introduced in connection with a physical object — "Hator's [[trifork|Trifork]]" [020-011] — which serves as the symbol of his legacy. The [[trifork|Trifork]] (a three-pronged implement) represents the doctrinal tradition Hator established. [[spadevil|Spadevil]] explains:

> "Hator was the founder of Sant — many thousands of years ago. He laid down the principles, and for a long time his followers kept them faithfully." [020-013]

But over time, [[spadevil|Spadevil]] says, his followers have "fallen off from Hator's doctrines" [020-039]. The implication is that the living tradition of Sant has become a hollow shell, and [[spadevil|Spadevil]] sees himself as the restorer of Hator's original, austere vision.

## The Legend of Hator and the Girl

The central myth of Hator's life is his encounter with a woman who was sent to tempt him by [[shaping|Shaping]]. [[spadevil|Spadevil]] narrates this story to [[maskull]] in Chapter 21:

> "Having by a false tale drawn Hator on to the summit of a high rock, she suddenly threw off her mask, and stood before him naked. 'You and I, Hator, are now together, and there is no one to see us. I am fairer than any other woman you have seen. Let us lie together, and afterward you may kill me if you wish.'" [021-038]

Hator's response is not to succumb but not to flee either. He simply "said no word" — his silence is itself his answer [021-039]. The girl continues her assault on his asceticism, trying "to force Hator to acknowledge loveliness and joy," but Hator refuses [021-042].

The story ends tragically: the girl "fell down dead." Hator "awoke from his dream" — the ordeal has been a visionary test [021-040]. He recognises that the temptation was sent by [[shaping|Shaping]] himself, and his victory over it becomes the foundation of Sant's ascetic philosophy.

## Confrontation with Shaping

The most significant account of Hator's life is his direct dialogue with [[shaping|Shaping]] (the creator-demiurge of [[tormance|Tormance]], also called [[crystalman]]). After the temptation episode, [[shaping|Shaping]] appears and challenges Hator directly:

> "My wisdom does not come from you, nor from your world, but from another world," said Hator. [021-043]

[[shaping|Shaping]] retorts:

> "What, then, do you do in my world?" [021-044]

And Hator delivers his most famous pronouncement:

> "You are the dreamer of impossible dreams." [021-045]

This exchange is the theological heart of Hator's significance. He claims access to a reality beyond [[shaping|Shaping]]'s creation — a "other world" of true wisdom — and dismisses [[shaping|Shaping]]'s entire cosmos as a delusion. When [[maskull|Maskull]] asks "What other world did Hator refer to?", the question remains partially unanswered [021-046], but the intimation is of the realm associated with [[muspel]] — the world of [[krag]] and authentic reality.

## Philosophical Significance

Hator represents the ascetic tradition in its pure, founding form. Where [[spadevil|Spadevil]] is the zealous reformer and enforcer, Hator is the original visionary — the one who saw through [[shaping|Shaping]]'s world and refused its blandishments. His silence, his refusal to argue or explain, and his direct appeal to a higher reality all mark him as a mystic rather than a systematiser.

The contrast between Hator's silence and [[spadevil|Spadevil]]'s volubility is itself a commentary on how spiritual traditions evolve. "The blossom was to Hator, the fruit is to me," [[spadevil|Spadevil]] says [021-026], claiming the role of completing what Hator began — but the reader may wonder whether the "fruit" is not a falling-away from the purity of the "blossom."
