# Section 14

[014-001]“I propose nothing—I am waiting.”

[014-002]“Don’t wait too long, for time doesn’t wait—especially in Ifdawn.”

[014-003]“Something will happen,” said Maskull.

[014-004]Oceaxe threw a subtle smile. “So you have no special destination in the

[014-005]Marest?”

[014-006]“No, and if you’ll permit me, I will come home with you.”

[014-007]“Singular man!” she said, with a short, thrilling laugh. “That’s what I

[014-008]have been offering all the time. Of course you will come home with me.

[014-009]As for Crimtyphon...”

[014-010]“You mentioned that name before. Who is he?”

[014-011]“Oh! My lover, or, as you would say, my husband.”

[014-012]“This doesn’t improve matters,” said Maskull.

[014-013]“It leaves them exactly where they were. We merely have to remove him.”

[014-014]“We are certainly misunderstanding each other,” said Maskull, quite

[014-015]startled. “Do you by any chance imagine that I am making a compact with

[014-016]you?”

[014-017]“You will do nothing against your will. But you have promised to come

[014-018]home with me.”

[014-019]“Tell me, how do you remove husbands in Ifdawn?”

[014-020]“Either you or I must kill him.”

[014-021]He eyed her for a full minute. “Now we are passing from folly to

[014-022]insanity.”

[014-023]“Not at all,” replied Oceaxe. “It is the too-sad truth. And when you

[014-024]have seen Crimtyphon, you will realise it.”

[014-025]“I’m aware I am on a strange planet,” said Maskull slowly, “where all

[014-026]sorts of unheard of things may happen, and where the very laws of

[014-027]morality may be different. Still as far as I am concerned, murder is

[014-028]murder, and I’ll have no more to do with a woman who wants to make use

[014-029]of me, to get rid of her husband.”

[014-030]“You think me wicked?” demanded Oceaxe steadily.

[014-031]“Or mad.”

[014-032]“Then you had better leave me, Maskull—only—”

[014-033]“Only what?”

[014-034]“You wish to be consistent, don’t you? Leave all other mad and wicked

[014-035]people as well. Then you’ll find it easier to reform the rest.”

[014-036]Maskull frowned, but said nothing.

[014-037]“Well?” demanded Oceaxe, with a half smile.

[014-038]“I’ll come with you, and I’ll see Crimtyphon—if only to warn him.”

[014-039]Oceaxe broke into a cascade of rich, feminine laughter, but whether at

[014-040]the image conjured up by Maskull’s last words, or from some other cause,

[014-041]he did not know. The conversation dropped.

[014-042]At a distance of a couple of miles from the now towering cliffs, the

[014-043]river made a sharp, right-angled turn to the west, and was no longer of

[014-044]use to them on their journey. Maskull stared up doubtfully.

[014-045]“It’s a stiff climb for a hot morning.”

[014-046]“Let’s rest here a little,” said she, indicating a smooth flat island of

[014-047]black rock, standing up just out of the water in the middle of the

[014-048]river.

[014-049]They accordingly went to it, and Maskull sat down. Oceaxe, however,

[014-050]standing graceful and erect, turned her face toward the cliffs opposite,

[014-051]and uttered a piercing and peculiar call.

[014-052]“What is that for?” She did not answer. After waiting a minute, she

[014-053]repeated the call. Maskull now saw a large bird detach itself from the

[014-054]top of one of the precipices, and sail slowly down toward them. It was

[014-055]followed by two others. The flight of these birds was exceedingly slow

[014-056]and clumsy.

[014-057]“What are they?” he asked.

[014-058]She still returned no answer, but smiled rather peculiarly and sat down

[014-059]beside him. Before many minutes he was able to distinguish the shapes

[014-060]and colors of the flying monsters. They were not birds, but creatures

[014-061]with long, snakelike bodies, and ten reptilian legs apiece, terminating

[014-062]in fins which acted as wings. The bodies were of bright blue, the legs

[014-063]and fins were yellow. They were flying, without haste, but in a somewhat

[014-064]ominous fashion, straight toward them. He could make out a long, thin

[014-065]spike projecting from each of the heads.

[014-066]“They are shrowks,” explained Oceaxe at last. “If you want to know their

[014-067]intention, I’ll tell you. To make a meal of us. First of all their

[014-068]spikes will pierce us, and then their mouths, which are really suckers,

[014-069]will drain us dry of blood—pretty thoroughly too; there are no half

[014-070]measures with shrowks. They are toothless beasts, so don’t eat flesh.”

[014-071]“As you show such admirable sangfroid,” said Maskull dryly, “I take it

[014-072]there’s no particular danger.”

[014-073]Nevertheless he instinctively tried to get on to his feet and failed. A

[014-074]new form of paralysis was chaining him to the ground.

[014-075]“Are you trying to get up?” asked Oceaxe smoothly.

[014-076]“Well, yes, but those cursed reptiles seem to be nailing me down to the

[014-077]rock with their wills. May I ask if you had any special object in view

[014-078]in waking them up?”

[014-079]“I assure you the danger is quite real, Maskull. Instead of talking and

[014-080]asking questions, you had much better see what you can do with your

[014-081]will.”

[014-082]“I seem to have no will, unfortunately.”

[014-083]Oceaxe was seized with a paroxysm of laughter, but it was still rich and

[014-084]beautiful. “It’s obvious you aren’t a very heroic protector, Maskull. It

[014-085]seems I must play the man, and you the woman. I expected better things

[014-086]of your big body. Why, my husband would send those creatures dancing all

[014-087]around the sky, by way of a joke, before disposing of them. Now watch

[014-088]me. Two of the three I’ll kill; the third we will ride home on. Which

[014-089]one shall we keep?”

[014-090]The shrowks continued their slow, wobbling flight toward them. Their

[014-091]bodies were of huge size. They produced in Maskull the same sensation of

[014-092]loathing as insects did. He instinctively understood that as they hunted

[014-093]with their wills, there was no necessity for them to possess a swift

[014-094]motion.

[014-095]“Choose which you please,” he said shortly. “They are equally

[014-096]objectionable to me.”

[014-097]“Then I’ll choose the leader, as it is presumably the most energetic

[014-098]animal. Watch now.”

[014-099]She stood upright, and her sorb suddenly blazed with fire. Maskull felt

[014-100]something snap inside his brain. His limbs were free once more. The two

[014-101]monsters in the rear staggered and darted head foremost toward the

[014-102]earth, one after the other. He watched them crash on the ground, and

[014-103]then lie motionless. The leader still came toward them, but he fancied

[014-104]that its flight was altered in character; it was no longer menacing, but

[014-105]tame and unwilling.

[014-106]Oceaxe guided it with her will to the mainland shore opposite their

[014-107]island rock. Its vast bulk lay there extended, awaiting her pleasure.

[014-108]They immediately crossed the water.

[014-109]Maskull viewed the shrowk at close quarters. It was about thirty feet

[014-110]long. Its bright-coloured skin was shining, slippery, and leathery; a

[014-111]mane of black hair covered its long neck. Its face was awesome and

[014-112]unnatural, with its carnivorous eyes, frightful stiletto, and blood-

[014-113]sucking cavity. There were true fins on its back and tail.

[014-114]“Have you a good seat?” asked Oceaxe, patting the creature’s flank. “As

[014-115]I have to steer, let me jump on first.”

[014-116]She pulled up her gown, then climbed up and sat astride the animal’s

[014-117]back, just behind the mane, which she clutched. Between her and the fin

[014-118]there was just room for Maskull. He grasped the two flanks with his

[014-119]outer hands; his third, new arm pressed against Oceaxe’s back, and for

[014-120]additional security he was compelled to encircle her waist with it.

[014-121]Directly he did so, he realised that he had been tricked, and that this

[014-122]ride had been planned for one purpose only—to inflame his desires.

[014-123]The third arm possessed a function of its own, of which hitherto he had

[014-124]been ignorant. It was a developed magn. But the stream of love which was

[014-125]communicated to it was no longer pure and noble—it was boiling,

[014-126]passionate, and torturing. He gritted his teeth, and kept quiet, but

[014-127]Oceaxe had not plotted the adventure to remain unconscious of his

[014-128]feelings. She looked around, with a golden, triumphant smile. “The ride

[014-129]will last some time, so hold on well!” Her voice was soft like a flute,

[014-130]but rather malicious.

[014-131]Maskull grinned, and said nothing. He dared not remove his arm.

[014-132]The shrowk straddled on to its legs. It jerked itself forward, and rose

[014-133]slowly and uncouthly in the air. They began to paddle upward toward the

[014-134]painted cliffs. The motion was swaying, rocking, and sickening; the

[014-135]contact of the brute’s slimy skin was disgusting. All this, however, was

[014-136]merely background to Maskull, as he sat there with closed eyes, holding

[014-137]on to Oceaxe. In the front and centre of his consciousness was the

[014-138]knowledge that he was gripping a fair woman, and that her flesh was

[014-139]responding to his touch like a lovely harp.

[014-140]They climbed up and up. He opened his eyes, and ventured to look around

[014-141]him. By this time they were already level with the top of the outer

[014-142]rampart of precipices. There now came in sight a wild archipelago of

[014-143]islands, with jagged outlines, emerging from a sea of air. The islands

[014-144]were mountain summits; or, more accurately speaking, the country was a

[014-145]high tableland, fissured everywhere by narrow and apparently bottomless

[014-146]cracks. These cracks were in some cases like canals, in others like

[014-147]lakes, in others merely holes in the ground, closed in all round. The

[014-148]perpendicular sides of the islands—that is, the upper, visible parts of

[014-149]the innumerable cliff faces—were of bare rock, gaudily coloured; but the

[014-150]level surfaces were a tangle of wild plant life. The taller trees alone

[014-151]were distinguishable from the shrowk’s back. They were of different

[014-152]shapes, and did not look ancient; they were slender and swaying but did

[014-153]not appear very graceful; they looked tough, wiry, and savage.

[014-154]As Maskull continued to explore the landscape, he forgot Oceaxe and his

[014-155]passion. Other strange feelings came to the front. The morning was gay

[014-156]and bright. The sun scorched down, quickly-changing clouds sailed across

[014-157]the sky, the earth was vivid, wild, and lonely. Yet he experienced no

[014-158]aesthetic sensations—he felt nothing but an intense longing for action

[014-159]and possession. When he looked at anything, he immediately wanted to

[014-160]deal with it. The atmosphere of the land seemed not free, but sticky;

[014-161]attraction and repulsion were its constituents. Apart from this wish to

[014-162]play a personal part in what was going on around and beneath him, the

[014-163]scenery had no significance for him.

[014-164]So preoccupied was he, that his arm partly released its clasp. Oceaxe

[014-165]turned around to gaze at him. Whether or not she was satisfied with what

[014-166]she saw, she uttered a low laugh, like a peculiar chord.

[014-167]“Cold again so quickly, Maskull?”

[014-168]“What do you want?” he asked absently, still looking over the side.

[014-169]“It’s extraordinary how drawn I feel to all this.”

[014-170]“You wish to take a hand?”

[014-171]“I wish to get down.”

[014-172]“Oh, we have a good way to go yet.... So you really feel different?”

[014-173]“Different from what? What are you talking about?” said Maskull, still

[014-174]lost in abstraction.

[014-175]Oceaxe laughed again. “It would be strange if we couldn’t make a man of

[014-176]you, for the material is excellent.”

[014-177]After that, she turned her back once more.

[014-178]The air islands differed from water islands in another way. They were

[014-179]not on a plane surface, but sloped upward, like a succession of broken

[014-180]terraces, as the journey progressed. The shrowk had hitherto been flying

[014-181]well above the ground; but now, when a new line of towering cliffs

[014-182]confronted them, Oceaxe did not urge the beast upward, but caused it to

[014-183]enter a narrow canyon, which intersected the mountains like a channel.

[014-184]They were instantly plunged into deep shade. The canal was not above

[014-185]thirty feet wide; the walls stretched upward on both sides for many

[014-186]hundred feet. It was as cool as an ice chamber. When Maskull attempted

[014-187]to plumb the chasm with his eyes, he saw nothing but black obscurity.

[014-188]“What is at the bottom?” he asked.

[014-189]“Death for you, if you go to look for it.”

[014-190]“We know that. I mean, is there any kind of life down there?”

[014-191]“Not that I have ever heard of,” said Oceaxe, “but of course all things

[014-192]are possible.”

[014-193]“I think very likely there is life,” he returned thoughtfully.
