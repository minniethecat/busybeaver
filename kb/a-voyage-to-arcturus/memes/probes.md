# Probes

## Definition

Probes are the most advanced sensory organs that grow on Tormance's inhabitants, described as "the gates opening into a new world" [021-002]. They represent the culmination of Maskull's organ-growing journey: where earlier organs like [[breve]] (communication), [[poigns]] (pleasure/pain), and [[magn]] (emotional perception) each opened a single dimension of experience, the probes grant access to a mode of perception that transcends self-interest entirely.

## In the Novel

Probes first appear in Chapter 21, during Maskull's encounter with [[spadevil]] and his doctrine of the new law. As Spadevil covers Maskull's [[sorb]] — the organ of pleasure and pain — the probes' latent function becomes available:

> They are probes, and they are the gates opening into a new world.
>
> [021-002]

The probes enable perception of "duty" — Spadevil's supreme ethical principle — as an objective reality rather than a subjective inclination. With his sorb suppressed, Maskull experiences the new law flowing into his consciousness "like a smooth-running stream of clean water which had hitherto been dammed by his obstructive will" [021-002].

The narrative makes clear that probes come paired — Spadevil possesses a double probe, and offers to double Maskull's so he can "see as I see" [022-152]. This doubling is significant: the probe is not a solitary organ but one that connects the perceiver to a shared spiritual vision.

## The Flinting of the Probe

In a dramatic scene in Chapter 22, the skeptic Catice strikes out one of Maskull's two probes with a flint stone, declaring:

> When you have but one, you will see with me, and you will recollect with Spadevil. Choose you then the superior faith, and I shall obey your choice.
>
> [022-152]

The flinting of the probe is a test: with only one probe, Maskull must choose between Spadevil's new faith of duty and Catice's skepticism. The physical violence done to the organ mirrors the violent spiritual choice demanded of Maskull — each reduction of perceptual faculty forces a sharper, more committed vision. This is the novel's recurring pattern: new organs bring new worlds, but also new burdens of choice.

## Philosophical Meaning

As the highest perceptual organ in Lindsay's hierarchy, the probes correspond to the capacity for direct spiritual vision — perception unclouded by desire, pleasure, or self-interest. In the framework of Spadevil's philosophy, probes reveal duty as something _seen_, not reasoned toward: an immediate intuition of what must be done.

The doubling of the probe suggests that spiritual vision is inherently social — to see truly is to see with another, in shared recognition. Catice's act of halving the probe dramatizes the tension between solitary insight and communal verification: a single probe suffices for choice, but dual probes are needed for the fullest vision.

Within the novel's broader Gnostic allegory, the probes represent the highest faculty available within Crystalman's material world — the organ that perceives the "vestiges of Muspel" [026-085] in created beings. They are the perceptual gateway through which the divine spark in each person becomes visible to others.
