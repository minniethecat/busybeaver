# Section 12

[012-001]He felt himself alone with Nature. A sacred stillness came over his

[012-002]heart. Past and future were forgotten. The forest, the sun, the day did

[012-003]not exist for him. He was unconscious of himself—he had no thoughts and

[012-004]no feelings. Yet never had Life had such an altitude for him.

[012-005]A man stood, with crossed arms, right in his path. He was so clothed

[012-006]that his limbs were exposed, while his body was covered. He was young

[012-007]rather than old. Maskull observed that his countenance possessed none of

[012-008]the special organs of Tormance, to which he had not even yet become

[012-009]reconciled. He was smooth-faced. His whole person seemed to radiate an

[012-010]excess of life, like the trembling of air on a hot day. His eyes had

[012-011]such force that Maskull could not meet them.

[012-012]He addressed Maskull by name, in an extraordinary voice. It had a double

[012-013]tone. The primary one sounded far away; the second was an undertone,

[012-014]like a sympathetic tanging string.

[012-015]Maskull felt a rising joy, as he continued standing in the presence of

[012-016]this individual. He believed that something good was happening to him.

[012-017]He found it physically difficult to bring any words out. “Why do you

[012-018]stop me?”

[012-019]“Maskull, look well at me. Who am I?”

[012-020]“I think you are Shaping.”

[012-021]“I am Surtur.”

[012-022]Maskull again attempted to meet his eyes, but felt as if he were being

[012-023]stabbed.

[012-024]“You know that this is my world. Why do you think I have brought you

[012-025]here? I wish you to serve me.”

[012-026]Maskull could no longer speak.

[012-027]“Those who joke at my world,” continued the vision, “those who make a

[012-028]mock of its stern, eternal rhythm, its beauty and sublimity, which are

[012-029]not skin-deep, but proceed from fathomless roots—they shall not escape.”

[012-030]“I do not mock it.”

[012-031]“Ask me your questions, and I will answer them.”

[012-032]“I have nothing.”

[012-033]“It is necessary for you to serve me, Maskull. Do you not understand?

[012-034]You are my servant and helper.”

[012-035]“I shall not fail.”

[012-036]“This is for my sake, and not for yours.”

[012-037]These last words had no sooner left Surtur’s mouth than Maskull saw him

[012-038]spring suddenly upward and outward. Looking up at the vault of the sky,

[012-039]he saw the whole expanse of vision filled by Surtur’s form—not as a

[012-040]concrete man, but as a vast, concave cloud image, looking down and

[012-041]frowning at him. Then the spectacle vanished, as a light goes out.

[012-042]Maskull stood inactive, with a thumping heart. Now he again heard the

[012-043]solitary trumpet note. The sound began this time faintly in the far

[012-044]distance in front of him, travelled slowly toward him with regularly

[012-045]increasing intensity, passed overhead at its loudest, and then grew more

[012-046]and more quiet, wonderful, and solemn, as it fell away in the rear,

[012-047]until the note was merged in the deathlike silence of the forest. It

[012-048]appeared to Maskull like the closing of a marvellous and important

[012-049]chapter.

[012-050]Simultaneously with the fading away of the sound, the heavens seemed to

[012-051]open up with the rapidity of lightning into a blue vault of immeasurable

[012-052]height. He breathed a great breath, stretched all his limbs, and looked

[012-053]around him with a slow smile.

[012-054]After a while he resumed his journey. His brain was all dark and

[012-055]confused, but one idea was already beginning to stand out from the

[012-056]rest—huge, shapeless, and grand, like the growing image in the soul of a

[012-057]creative artist: the staggering thought that he was a man of destiny.

[012-058]The more he reflected upon all that had occurred since his arrival in

[012-059]this new world—and even before leaving Earth—the clearer and more

[012-060]indisputable it became, that he could not be here for his own purposes,

[012-061]but must be here for an end. But what that end was, he could not

[012-062]imagine.

[012-063]Through the forest he saw Branchspell at last sinking in the west. It

[012-064]looked a stupendous ball of red fire—now he could realise at his ease

[012-065]what a sun it was! The avenue took an abrupt turn to the left and began

[012-066]to descend steeply.

[012-067]A wide, rolling river of clear and dark water was visible in front of

[012-068]him, no great way off. It flowed from north to south. The forest path

[012-069]led him straight to its banks. Maskull stood there, and regarded the

[012-070]lapping, gurgling waters pensively. On the opposite bank, the forest

[012-071]continued. Miles to the south, Poolingdred could just be distinguished.

[012-072]On the northern skyline the Ifdawn Mountains loomed up—high, wild,

[012-073]beautiful, and dangerous. They were not a dozen miles away.

[012-074]Like the first mutterings of a thunderstorm, the first faint breaths of

[012-075]cool wind, Maskull felt the stirrings of passion in his heart. In spite

[012-076]of his bodily fatigue, he wished to test his strength against something.

[012-077]This craving he identified with the crags of the Marest. They seemed to

[012-078]have the same magical attraction for his will as the lodestone for iron.

[012-079]He kept biting his nails, as he turned his eyes in that

[012-080]direction—wondering if it would not be possible to conquer the heights

[012-081]that evening. But when he glanced back again at Poolingdred, he

[012-082]remembered Joiwind and Panawe, and grew more tranquil. He decided to

[012-083]make his bed at this spot, and to set off as soon after daybreak as he

[012-084]should awake.

[012-085]He drank at the river, washed himself, and lay down on the bank to

[012-086]sleep. By this time, so far had his idea progressed, that he cared

[012-087]nothing for the possible dangers of the night—he confided in his star.

[012-088]Branchspell set, the day faded, night with its terrible weight came on,

[012-089]and through it all Maskull slept. Long before midnight, however, he was

[012-090]awakened by a crimson glow in the sky. He opened his eyes, and wondered

[012-091]where he was. He felt heaviness and pain. The red glow was a terrestrial

[012-092]phenomenon; it came from among the trees. He got up and went toward the

[012-093]source of the light.

[012-094]Away from the river, not a hundred feet off, he nearly stumbled across

[012-095]the form of a sleeping woman. The object which emitted the crimson rays

[012-096]was lying on the ground, several yards away from her. It was like a

[012-097]small jewel, throwing off sparks of red light. He barely threw a glance

[012-098]at that, however.

[012-099]The woman was clothed in the large skin of an animal. She had big,

[012-100]smooth, shapely limbs, rather muscular than fat. Her magn was not a thin

[012-101]tentacle, but a third arm, terminating in a hand. Her face, which was

[012-102]upturned, was wild, powerful, and exceedingly handsome. But he saw with

[012-103]surprise that in place of a breve on her forehead, she possessed another

[012-104]eye. All three were closed. The colour of her skin in the crimson glow

[012-105]he could not distinguish.

[012-106]He touched her gently with his hand. She awoke calmly and looked up at

[012-107]him without stirring a muscle. All three eyes stared at him; but the two

[012-108]lower ones were dull and vacant—mere carriers of vision. The middle,

[012-109]upper one alone expressed her inner nature. Its haughty, unflinching

[012-110]glare had yet something seductive and alluring in it. Maskull felt a

[012-111]challenge in that look of lordly, feminine will, and his manner

[012-112]instinctively stiffened.

[012-113]She sat up.

[012-114]“Can you speak my language?” he asked. “I wouldn’t put such a question,

[012-115]but others have been able to.”

[012-116]“Why should you imagine that I can’t read your mind? Is it so extremely

[012-117]complex?”

[012-118]She spoke in a rich, lingering, musical voice, which delighted him to

[012-119]listen to.

[012-120]“No, but you have no breve.”

[012-121]“Well, but haven’t I a sorb, which is better?” And she pointed to the

[012-122]eye on her brow.

[012-123]“What is your name?”

[012-124]“Oceaxe.”

[012-125]“And where do you come from?”

[012-126]“Ifdawn.”

[012-127]These contemptuous replies began to irritate him, and yet the mere sound

[012-128]of her voice was fascinating.

[012-129]“I am going there tomorrow,” he remarked.

[012-130]She laughed, as if against her will, but made no comment.

[012-131]“My name is Maskull,” he went on. “I am a stranger—from another world.”

[012-132]“So I should judge, from your absurd appearance.”

[012-133]“Perhaps it would be as well to say at once,” said Maskull bluntly, “are

[012-134]we, or are we not, to be friends?”

[012-135]She yawned and stretched her arms, without rising. “Why should we be

[012-136]friends? If I thought you were a man, I might accept you as a lover.”

[012-137]“You must look elsewhere for that.”

[012-138]“So be it, Maskull! Now go away, and leave me in peace.”

[012-139]She dropped her head again to the ground, but did not at once close her

[012-140]eyes.

[012-141]“What are you doing here?” he interrogated.

[012-142]“Oh, we Ifdawn folk occasionally come here to sleep, for there often

[012-143]enough it is a night for us which has no next morning.”

[012-144]“Being such a terrible place, and seeing that I am a total stranger, it

[012-145]would be merely courteous if you were to warn me what I have to expect

[012-146]in the way of dangers.”

[012-147]“I am perfectly and utterly indifferent to what becomes of you,”

[012-148]retorted Oceaxe.

[012-149]“Are you returning in the morning?” persisted Maskull.

[012-150]“If I wish.”

[012-151]“Then we will go together.”

[012-152]She got up again on her elbow. “Instead of making plans for other

[012-153]people, I would do a very necessary thing.”

[012-154]“Pray, tell me.”

[012-155]“Well, there’s no reason why I should, but I will. I would try to

[012-156]convert my women’s organs into men’s organs. It is a man’s country.”

[012-157]“Speak more plainly.”

[012-158]“Oh, it’s plain enough. If you attempt to pass through Ifdawn without a

[012-159]sorb, you are simply committing suicide. And that magn too is worse than

[012-160]useless.”

[012-161]“You probably know what you are talking about, Oceaxe. But what do you

[012-162]advise me to do?”

[012-163]She negligently pointed to the light-emitting stone lying on the ground.

[012-164]“There is the solution. If you hold that drude to your organs for a good

[012-165]while, perhaps it will start the change, and perhaps nature will do the

[012-166]rest during the night. I promise nothing.”

[012-167]Oceaxe now really turned her back on Maskull.

[012-168]He considered for a few minutes, and then walked over to where the stone

[012-169]was lying, and took it in his hand. It was a pebble the size of a hen’s

[012-170]egg, radiant with crimson light, as though red-hot, and throwing out a

[012-171]continuous shower of small, blood-red sparks.

[012-172]Finally deciding that Oceaxe’s advice was good, he applied the drude

[012-173]first to his magn, and then to his breve. He experienced a cauterising

[012-174]sensation—a feeling of healing pain.

[012-175]Chapter 9. OCEAXE

[012-176]Maskull’s second day on Tormance dawned. Branchspell was already above

[012-177]the horizon when he awoke. He was instantly aware that his organs had

[012-178]changed during the night. His fleshy breve was altered into an eyelike

[012-179]sorb; his magn had swelled and developed into a third arm, springing

[012-180]from the breast. The arm gave him at once a sense of greater physical

[012-181]security, but with the sorb he was obliged to experiment, before he

[012-182]could grasp its function.

[012-183]As he lay there in the white sunlight, opening and shutting each of his

[012-184]three eyes in turn, he found that the two lower ones served his

[012-185]understanding, the upper one his will. That is to say, with the lower

[012-186]eyes he saw things in clear detail, but without personal interest; with

[012-187]the sorb he saw nothing as self-existent—everything appeared as an

[012-188]object of importance or non-importance to his own needs.

[012-189]Rather puzzled as to how this would turn out, he got up and looked about

[012-190]him. He had slept out of sight of Oceaxe. He was anxious to learn if she

[012-191]were still on the spot, but before going to ascertain he made up his

[012-192]mind to bathe in the river.

[012-193]It was a glorious morning. The hot white sun already began to glare, but

[012-194]its heat was tempered by a strong wind, which whistled through the

[012-195]trees. A host of fantastic clouds filled the sky. They looked like

[012-196]animals, and were always changing shape. The ground, as well as the

[012-197]leaves and branches of the forest trees, still held traces of heavy dew

[012-198]or rain during the night. A poignantly sweet smell of nature entered his

[012-199]nostrils. His pain was quiescent, and his spirits were high.
