# Section 8

[008-001]The hills were composed of a rich, dark mould. Small trees, of weird

[008-002]shapes, all differing from each other, but all purple-coloured, covered

[008-003]the slopes and top. Maskull and Joiwind climbed up and through. Some

[008-004]hard fruit, bright blue in colour, of the size of a large apple, and

[008-005]shaped like an egg, was lying in profusion underneath the trees.

[008-006]“Is the fruit here poisonous, or why don’t you eat it?” asked Maskull.

[008-007]She looked at him tranquilly. “We don’t eat living things. The thought

[008-008]is horrible to us.”

[008-009]“I have nothing to say against that, theoretically. But do you really

[008-010]sustain your bodies on water?”

[008-011]“Supposing you could find nothing else to live on, Maskull—would you eat

[008-012]other men?”

[008-013]“I would not.”

[008-014]“Neither will we eat plants and animals, which are our fellow creatures.

[008-015]So nothing is left to us but water, and as one can really live on

[008-016]anything, water does very well.”

[008-017]Maskull picked up one of the fruits and handled it curiously. As he did

[008-018]so another of his newly acquired sense organs came into action. He found

[008-019]that the fleshy knobs beneath his ears were in some novel fashion

[008-020]acquainting him with the inward properties of the fruit. He could not

[008-021]only see, feel, and smell it, but could detect its intrinsic nature.

[008-022]This nature was hard, persistent and melancholy.

[008-023]Joiwind answered the questions he had not asked.

[008-024]“Those organs are called ‘poigns.’ Their use is to enable us to

[008-025]understand and sympathise with all living creatures.”

[008-026]“What advantage do you derive from that, Joiwind?”

[008-027]“The advantage of not being cruel and selfish, dear Maskull.”

[008-028]He threw the fruit away and flushed again.

[008-029]Joiwind looked into his swarthy, bearded face without embarrassment and

[008-030]slowly smiled. “Have I said too much? Have I been too familiar? Do you

[008-031]know why you think so? It’s because you are still impure. By and by you

[008-032]will listen to all language without shame.”

[008-033]Before he realised what she was about to do, she threw her tentacle

[008-034]round his neck, like another arm. He offered no resistance to its cool

[008-035]pressure. The contact of her soft flesh with his own was so moist and

[008-036]sensitive that it resembled another kind of kiss. He saw who it was that

[008-037]embraced him—a pale, beautiful girl. Yet, oddly enough, he experienced

[008-038]neither voluptuousness nor sexual pride. The love expressed by the

[008-039]caress was rich, glowing, and personal, but there was not the least

[008-040]trace of sex in it—and so he received it.

[008-041]She removed her tentacle, placed her two arms on his shoulders and

[008-042]penetrated with her eyes right into his very soul.

[008-043]“Yes, I wish to be pure,” he muttered. “Without that what can I ever be

[008-044]but a weak, squirming devil?”

[008-045]Joiwind released him. “This we call the ‘magn,’” she said, indicating

[008-046]her tentacle. “By means of it what we love already we love more, and

[008-047]what we don’t love at all we begin to love.”

[008-048]“A godlike organ!”

[008-049]“It is the one we guard most jealously,” said Joiwind.

[008-050]The shade of the trees afforded a timely screen from the now almost

[008-051]insufferable rays of Branchspell, which was climbing steadily upward to

[008-052]the zenith. On descending the other side of the little hills, Maskull

[008-053]looked anxiously for traces of Nightspore and Krag, but without result.

[008-054]After staring about him for a few minutes he shrugged his shoulders; but

[008-055]suspicions had already begun to gather in his mind.

[008-056]A small, natural amphitheatre lay at their feet, completely circled by

[008-057]the tree-clad heights. The centre was of red sand. In the very middle

[008-058]shot up a tall, stately tree, with a black trunk and branches, and

[008-059]transparent, crystal leaves. At the foot of this tree was a natural,

[008-060]circular well, containing dark green water.

[008-061]When they had reached the bottom, Joiwind took him straight over to the

[008-062]well.

[008-063]Maskull gazed at it intently. “Is this the shrine you talked about?”

[008-064]“Yes. It is called Shaping’s Well. The man or woman who wishes to invoke

[008-065]Shaping must take up some of the gnawl water, and drink it.”

[008-066]“Pray for me,” said Maskull. “Your unspotted prayer will carry more

[008-067]weight.”

[008-068]“What do you wish for?”

[008-069]“For purity,” answered Maskull, in a troubled voice.

[008-070]Joiwind made a cup of her hand, and drank a little of the water. She

[008-071]held it up to Maskull’s mouth. “You must drink too.” He obeyed. She then

[008-072]stood erect, closed her eyes, and, in a voice like the soft murmurings

[008-073]of spring, prayed aloud.

[008-074]“Shaping, my father, I am hoping you can hear me. A strange man has come

[008-075]to us weighed down with heavy blood. He wishes to be pure. Let him know

[008-076]the meaning of love, let him live for others. Don’t spare him pain, dear

[008-077]Shaping, but let him seek his own pain. Breathe into him a noble soul.”

[008-078]Maskull listened with tears in his heart.

[008-079]As Joiwind finished speaking, a blurred mist came over his eyes, and,

[008-080]half buried in the scarlet sand, appeared a large circle of dazzlingly

[008-081]white pillars. For some minutes they flickered to and fro between

[008-082]distinctness and indistinctness, like an object being focused. Then they

[008-083]faded out of sight again.

[008-084]“Is that a sign from Shaping?” asked Maskull, in a low, awed tone.

[008-085]“Perhaps it is. It is a time mirage.”

[008-086]“What can that be, Joiwind?”

[008-087]“You see, dear Maskull, the temple does not yet exist but it will do so,

[008-088]because it must. What you and I are now doing in simplicity, wise men

[008-089]will do hereafter in full knowledge.”

[008-090]“It is right for man to pray,” said Maskull. “Good and evil in the world

[008-091]don’t originate from nothing. God and Devil must exist. And we should

[008-092]pray to the one, and fight the other.”

[008-093]“Yes, we must fight Krag.”

[008-094]“What name did you say?” asked Maskull in amazement.

[008-095]“Krag—the author of evil and misery—whom you call Devil.”

[008-096]He immediately concealed his thoughts. To prevent Joiwind from learning

[008-097]his relationship to this being, he made his mind a blank.

[008-098]“Why do you hide your mind from me?” she demanded, looking at him

[008-099]strangely and changing colour.

[008-100]“In this bright, pure, radiant world, evil seems so remote, one can

[008-101]scarcely grasp its meaning.” But he lied.

[008-102]Joiwind continued gazing at him, straight out of her clean soul. “The

[008-103]world is good and pure, but many men are corrupt. Panawe, my husband,

[008-104]has travelled, and he has told me things I would almost rather have not

[008-105]heard. One person he met believed the universe to be, from top to

[008-106]bottom, a conjurer’s cave.”

[008-107]“I should like to meet your husband.”

[008-108]“Well, we are going home now.”

[008-109]Maskull was on the point of inquiring whether she had any children, but

[008-110]was afraid of offending her, and checked himself.

[008-111]She read the mental question. “What need is there? Is not the whole

[008-112]world full of lovely children? Why should I want selfish possessions?”

[008-113]An extraordinary creature flew past, uttering a plaintive cry of five

[008-114]distinct notes. It was not a bird, but had a balloon-shaped body,

[008-115]paddled by five webbed feet. It disappeared among the trees.

[008-116]Joiwind pointed to it, as it went by. “I love that beast, grotesque as

[008-117]it is—perhaps all the more for its grotesqueness. But if I had children

[008-118]of my own, would I still love it? Which is best—to love two or three, or

[008-119]to love all?”

[008-120]“Every woman can’t be like you, Joiwind, but it is good to have a few

[008-121]like you. Wouldn’t it be as well,” he went on, “since we’ve got to walk

[008-122]through that sun-baked wilderness, to make turbans for our heads out of

[008-123]some of those long leaves?”

[008-124]She smiled rather pathetically. “You will think me foolish, but every

[008-125]tearing off of a leaf would be a wound in my heart. We have only to

[008-126]throw our robes over our heads.”

[008-127]“No doubt that will answer the same purpose, but tell me—weren’t these

[008-128]very robes once part of a living creature?”

[008-129]“Oh, no—no, they are the webs of a certain animal, but they have never

[008-130]been in themselves alive.”

[008-131]“You reduce life to extreme simplicity,” remarked Maskull meditatively,

[008-132]“but it is very beautiful.”

[008-133]Climbing back over the hills, they now without further ceremony began

[008-134]their march across the desert.

[008-135]They walked side by side. Joiwind directed their course straight toward

[008-136]Poolingdred. From the position of the sun, Maskull judged their way to

[008-137]lie due north. The sand was soft and powdery, very tiring to his naked

[008-138]feet. The red glare dazed his eyes, and made him semi-blind. He was hot,

[008-139]parched, and tormented with the craving to drink; his undertone of pain

[008-140]emerged into full consciousness.

[008-141]“I see my friends nowhere, and it is very queer.”

[008-142]“Yes, it is queer—if it is accidental,” said Joiwind, with a peculiar

[008-143]intonation.

[008-144]“Exactly!” agreed Maskull. “If they had met with a mishap, their bodies

[008-145]would still be there. It begins to look like a piece of bad work to me.

[008-146]They must have gone on, and left me.... Well, I am here, and I must make

[008-147]the best of it. I will trouble no more about them.”

[008-148]“I don’t wish to speak ill of anyone,” said Joiwind, “but my instinct

[008-149]tells me that you are better away from those men. They did not come here

[008-150]for your sake, but for their own.”

[008-151]They walked on for a long time. Maskull was beginning to feel faint. She

[008-152]twined her magn lovingly around his waist, and a strong current of

[008-153]confidence and well-being instantly coursed through his veins.

[008-154]“Thanks, Joiwind! But am I not weakening you?”

[008-155]“Yes,” she replied, with a quick, thrilling glance. “But not much—and it

[008-156]gives me great happiness.”

[008-157]Presently they met a fantastic little creature, the size of a new-born

[008-158]lamb, waltzing along on three legs. Each leg in turn moved to the front,

[008-159]and so the little monstrosity proceeded by means of a series of complete

[008-160]rotations. It was vividly coloured, as though it had been dipped into

[008-161]pots of bright blue and yellow paint. It looked up with small, shining

[008-162]eyes, as they passed.

[008-163]Joiwind nodded and smiled to it. “That’s a personal friend of mine,

[008-164]Maskull. Whenever I come this way, I see it. It’s always waltzing, and

[008-165]always in a hurry, but it never seems to get anywhere.”

[008-166]“It seems to me that life is so self-sufficient here that there is no

[008-167]need for anyone to get anywhere. What I don’t quite understand is how

[008-168]you manage to pass your days without ennui.”

[008-169]“That’s a strange word. It means, does it not, craving for excitement?”

[008-170]“Something of the kind,” said Maskull.

[008-171]“That must be a disease brought on by rich food.”

[008-172]“But are you never dull?”

[008-173]“How could we be? Our blood is quick and light and free, our flesh is

[008-174]clean and unclogged, inside and out.... Before long I hope you will

[008-175]understand what sort of question you have asked.”

[008-176]Farther on they encountered a strange phenomenon. In the heart of the

[008-177]desert a fountain rose perpendicularly fifty feet into the air, with a

[008-178]cool and pleasant hissing sound. It differed, however, from a fountain

[008-179]in this respect—that the water of which it was composed did not return

[008-180]to the ground but was absorbed by the atmosphere at the summit. It was

[008-181]in fact a tall, graceful column of dark green fluid, with a capital of

[008-182]coiling and twisting vapours.

[008-183]When they came closer, Maskull perceived that this water column was the

[008-184]continuation and termination of a flowing brook, which came down from

[008-185]the direction of the mountains. The explanation of the phenomenon was

[008-186]evidently that the water at this spot found chemical affinities in the

[008-187]upper air, and consequently forsook the ground.

[008-188]“Now let us drink,” said Joiwind.

[008-189]She threw herself unaffectedly at full length on the sand, face

[008-190]downward, by the side of the brook, and Maskull was not long in

[008-191]following her example. She refused to quench her thirst until she had

[008-192]seen him drink. He found the water heavy, but bubbling with gas. He

[008-193]drank copiously. It affected his palate in a new way—with the purity and

[008-194]cleanness of water was combined the exhilaration of a sparkling wine,

[008-195]raising his spirits—but somehow the intoxication brought out his better

[008-196]nature, and not his lower.
