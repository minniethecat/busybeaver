# Section 10

[010-001]The sound appeared to him to belong to a different world from that in

[010-002]which he was travelling. The latter was mystical, dreamlike, and

[010-003]unbelievable—the drumming was like a very dim undertone of reality. It

[010-004]resembled the ticking of a clock in a room full of voices, only

[010-005]occasionally possible to be picked up by the ear.

[010-006]He rejoined Panawe and Joiwind, but said nothing to them about his

[010-007]experience. They all walked round the rim of the crater, and gazed down

[010-008]on the opposite side. Precipices similar to those that had overlooked

[010-009]the desert here formed the boundary of a vast moorland plain, whose

[010-010]dimensions could not be measured by the eye. It was solid land, yet he

[010-011]could not make out its prevailing colour. It was as if made of

[010-012]transparent glass, but it did not glitter in the sunlight. No objects in

[010-013]it could be distinguished, except a rolling river in the far distance,

[010-014]and, farther off still, on the horizon, a line of dark mountains, of

[010-015]strange shapes. Instead of being rounded, conical, or hogbacked, these

[010-016]heights were carved by nature into the semblance of castle battlements,

[010-017]but with extremely deep indentations.

[010-018]The sky immediately above the mountains was of a vivid, intense blue. It

[010-019]contrasted in a most marvellous way with the blue of the rest of the

[010-020]heavens. It seemed more luminous and radiant, and was in fact like the

[010-021]afterglow of a gorgeous blue sunset.

[010-022]Maskull kept on looking. The more he gazed, the more restless and noble

[010-023]became his feelings.

[010-024]“What is that light?”

[010-025]Panawe was sterner than usual, while his wife clung to his arm. “It is

[010-026]Alppain—our second sun,” he replied. “Those hills are the Ifdawn

[010-027]Marest.... Now let us get to our shelter.”

[010-028]“Is it imagination, or am I really being affected—tormented by that

[010-029]light?”

[010-030]“No, it’s not imagination—it’s real. How can it be otherwise when two

[010-031]suns, of different natures, are drawing you at the same time? Luckily

[010-032]you are not looking at Alppain itself. It’s invisible here. You would

[010-033]need to go at least as far as Ifdawn, to set eyes on it.”

[010-034]“Why do you say ‘luckily’?”

[010-035]“Because the agony caused by those opposing forces would perhaps be more

[010-036]than you could bear.... But I don’t know.”

[010-037]For the short distance that remained of their walk, Maskull was very

[010-038]thoughtful and uneasy. He understood nothing. Whatever object his eye

[010-039]chanced to rest on changed immediately into a puzzle. The silence and

[010-040]stillness of the mountain peak seemed brooding, mysterious, and waiting.

[010-041]Panawe gave him a friendly, anxious look, and without further delay led

[010-042]the way down a little track, which traversed the side of the mountain

[010-043]and terminated in the mouth of a cave.

[010-044]This cave was the home of Panawe and Joiwind. It was dark inside. The

[010-045]host took a shell and, filling it with liquid from a well, carelessly

[010-046]sprinkled the sandy floor of the interior. A greenish, phosphorescent

[010-047]light gradually spread to the furthest limits of the cavern, and

[010-048]continued to illuminate it for the whole time they were there. There was

[010-049]no furniture. Some dried, fernlike leaves served for couches.

[010-050]The moment she got in, Joiwind fell down in exhaustion. Her husband

[010-051]tended her with calm concern. He bathed her face, put drink to her lips,

[010-052]energised her with his magn, and finally laid her down to sleep. At the

[010-053]sight of the noble woman thus suffering on his account, Maskull was

[010-054]distressed.

[010-055]Panawe, however, endeavoured to reassure him. “It’s quite true this has

[010-056]been a very long, hard double journey, but for the future it will

[010-057]lighten all her other journeys for her.... Such is the nature of

[010-058]sacrifice.”

[010-059]“I can’t conceive how I have walked so far in a morning,” said Maskull,

[010-060]“and she has been twice the distance.”

[010-061]“Love flows in her veins, instead of blood, and that’s why she is so

[010-062]strong.”

[010-063]“You know she gave me some of it?”

[010-064]“Otherwise you couldn’t even have started.”

[010-065]“I shall never forget that.”

[010-066]The languorous heat of the day outside, the bright mouth of the cavern,

[010-067]the cool seclusion of the interior, with its pale green glow, invited

[010-068]Maskull to sleep. But curiosity got the better of his lassitude.

[010-069]“Will it disturb her if we talk?”

[010-070]“No.”

[010-071]“But how do you feel?”

[010-072]“I require little sleep. In any case, it’s more important that you

[010-073]should hear something about your new life. It’s not all as innocent and

[010-074]idyllic as this. If you intend to go through, you ought to be instructed

[010-075]about the dangers.”

[010-076]“Oh, I guessed as much. But how shall we arrange—shall I put questions,

[010-077]or will you tell me what you think is most essential?”

[010-078]Panawe motioned to Maskull to sit down on a pile of ferns, and at the

[010-079]same time reclined himself, leaning on one arm, with outstretched legs.

[010-080]“I will tell some incidents of my life. You will begin to learn from

[010-081]them what sort of place you have come to.”

[010-082]“I shall be grateful,” said Maskull, preparing himself to listen.

[010-083]Panawe paused for a moment or two, and then started his narrative in

[010-084]tranquil, measured, yet sympathetic tones.

[010-085]PANAWE’S STORY

[010-086]“My earliest recollection is of being taken, when three years old

[010-087](that’s equivalent to fifteen of your years, but we develop more slowly

[010-088]here), by my father and mother, to see Broodviol, the wisest man in

[010-089]Tormance. He dwelt in the great Wombflash Forest. We walked through

[010-090]trees for three days, sleeping at night. The trees grew taller as we

[010-091]went along, until the tops were out of sight. The trunks were of a dark

[010-092]red colour and the leaves were of pale ulfire. My father kept stopping

[010-093]to think. If left uninterrupted, he would remain for half a day in deep

[010-094]abstraction. My mother came out of Poolingdred, and was of a different

[010-095]stamp. She was beautiful, generous, and charming—but also active. She

[010-096]kept urging him on. This led to many disputes between them, which made

[010-097]me miserable. On the fourth day we passed through a part of the forest

[010-098]which bordered on the Sinking Sea. This sea is full of pouches of water

[010-099]that will not bear a man’s weight, and as these light parts don’t differ

[010-100]in appearance from the rest, it is dangerous to cross. My father pointed

[010-101]out a dim outline on the horizon, and told me it was Swaylone’s Island.

[010-102]Men sometimes go there, but none ever return. In the evening of the same

[010-103]day we found Broodviol standing in a deep, miry pit in the forest,

[010-104]surrounded on all sides by trees three hundred feet high. He was a big

[010-105]gnarled, rugged, wrinkled, sturdy old man. His age at that time was a

[010-106]hundred and twenty of our years, or nearly six hundred of yours. His

[010-107]body was trilateral: he had three legs, three arms, and six eyes, placed

[010-108]at equal distances all around his head. This gave him an aspect of great

[010-109]watchfulness and sagacity. He was standing in a sort of trance. I

[010-110]afterward heard this saying of his: ‘To lie is to sleep, to sit is to

[010-111]dream, to stand is to think.’ My father caught the infection, and fell

[010-112]into meditation, but my mother roused them both thoroughly. Broodviol

[010-113]scowled at her savagely, and demanded what she required. Then I too

[010-114]learned for the first time the object of our journey. I was a

[010-115]prodigy—that is to say, I was without sex. My parents were troubled over

[010-116]this, and wished to consult the wisest of men.

[010-117]“Old Broodviol smoothed his face, and said, ‘This perhaps will not be so

[010-118]difficult. I will explain the marvel. Every man and woman among us is a

[010-119]walking murderer. If a male, he has struggled with and killed the female

[010-120]who was born in the same body with him—if a female, she has killed the

[010-121]male. But in this child the struggle is still continuing.’

[010-122]“‘How shall we end it?’ asked my mother.

[010-123]“‘Let the child direct its will to the scene of the combat, and it will

[010-124]be of whichever sex it pleases.’

[010-125]“‘You want, of course, to be a man, don’t you?’ said my mother to me

[010-126]earnestly.

[010-127]“‘Then I shall be slaying your daughter, and that would be a crime.’

[010-128]“Something in my tone attracted Broodviol’s notice.

[010-129]“‘That was spoken, not selfishly, but magnanimously. Therefore the male

[010-130]must have spoken it, and you need not trouble further. Before you arrive

[010-131]home, the child will be a boy.’

[010-132]“My father walked away out of sight. My mother bent very low before

[010-133]Broodviol for about ten minutes, and he remained all that time looking

[010-134]kindly at her.

[010-135]“I heard that shortly afterward Alppain came into that land for a few

[010-136]hours daily. Broodviol grew melancholy, and died.

[010-137]“His prophecy came true—before we reached home, I knew the meaning of

[010-138]shame. But I have often pondered over his words since, in later years,

[010-139]when trying to understand my own nature; and I have come to the

[010-140]conclusion that, wisest of men as he was, he still did not see quite

[010-141]straight on this occasion. Between me and my twin sister, enclosed in

[010-142]one body, there never was any struggle, but instinctive reverence for

[010-143]life withheld both of us from fighting for existence. Hers was the

[010-144]stronger temperament, and she sacrificed herself—though not

[010-145]consciously—for me.

[010-146]“As soon as I comprehended this, I made a vow never to eat or destroy

[010-147]anything that contained life—and I have kept it ever since.

[010-148]“While I was still hardly a grown man, my father died. My mother’s death

[010-149]followed immediately, and I hated the associations of the land. I

[010-150]therefore made up my mind to travel into my mother’s country, where, as

[010-151]she had often told me, nature was most sacred and solitary.

[010-152]“One hot morning I came to Shaping’s Causeway. It is so called either

[010-153]because Shaping once crossed it, or because of its stupendous character.

[010-154]It is a natural embankment, twenty miles long, which links the mountains

[010-155]bordering my homeland with the Ifdawn Marest. The valley lies below at a

[010-156]depth varying from eight to ten thousand feet—a terrible precipice on

[010-157]either side. The knife edge of the ridge is generally not much over a

[010-158]foot wide. The causeway goes due north and south. The valley on my right

[010-159]hand was plunged in shadow—that on my left was sparkling with sunlight

[010-160]and dew. I walked fearfully along this precarious path for some miles.

[010-161]Far to the east the valley was closed by a lofty tableland, connecting

[010-162]the two chains of mountains, but overtopping even the most towering

[010-163]pinnacles. This is called the Sant Levels. I was never there, but I have

[010-164]heard two curious facts concerning the inhabitants. The first is that

[010-165]they have no women; the second, that though they are addicted to

[010-166]travelling in other parts they never acquire habits of the peoples with

[010-167]whom they reside.

[010-168]“Presently I turned giddy, and lay at full length for a great while,

[010-169]clutching the two edges of the path with both hands, and staring at the

[010-170]ground I was lying on with wide-open eyes. When that passed I felt like

[010-171]a different man and grew conceited and gay. About halfway across I saw

[010-172]someone approaching me a long way off. This put fear into my heart

[010-173]again, for I did not see how we could very well pass. However, I went

[010-174]slowly on, and presently we drew near enough together for me to

[010-175]recognise the walker. It was Slofork, the so-called sorcerer. I had

[010-176]never met him before, but I knew him by his peculiarities of person. He

[010-177]was of a bright gamboge colour and possessed a very long, proboscis-like

[010-178]nose, which appeared to be a useful organ, but did not add to his

[010-179]beauty, as I knew beauty. He was dubbed ‘sorcerer’ from his wondrous

[010-180]skill in budding limbs and organs. The tale is told that one evening he

[010-181]slowly sawed his leg off with a blunt stone and then lay for two days in

[010-182]agony while his new leg was sprouting. He was not reputed to be a

[010-183]consistently wise man, but he had periodical flashes of penetration and

[010-184]audacity that none could equal.
