# Section 26

[026-001]“What kind of look?”

[026-002]“A tragical look,” said Polecrab. He never even glanced at Maskull, but

[026-003]was gazing at a fixed spot on the water with unblinking eyes.

[026-004]“What lies beyond Lichstorm?” asked Maskull, after a minute or two.

[026-005]“Barey, where you have two suns instead of one—but beyond that fact I

[026-006]know nothing about it.... Then comes the ocean.”

[026-007]“And what’s on the other side of the ocean?”

[026-008]“That you must find out for yourself, for I doubt if anybody has ever

[026-009]crossed it and come back.”

[026-010]Maskull was silent for a little while.

[026-011]“How is it that your people are so unadventurous? I seem to be the only

[026-012]one travelling from curiosity.”

[026-013]“What do you mean by ‘your people’?”

[026-014]“True—you don’t know that I don’t belong to your planet at all. I’ve

[026-015]come from another world, Polecrab.”

[026-016]“What to find?”

[026-017]“I came here with Krag and Nightspore—to follow Surtur. I must have

[026-018]fainted the moment I arrived. When I sat up, it was night and the others

[026-019]had vanished. Since then I’ve been travelling at random.”

[026-020]Polecrab scratched his nose. “You haven’t found Surtur yet?”

[026-021]“I’ve heard his drum taps frequently. In the forest this morning I came

[026-022]quite close to him. Then two days ago, in the Lusion Plain, I saw a

[026-023]vision—a being in man’s shape, who called himself Surtur.”

[026-024]“Well, maybe it was Surtur.”

[026-025]“No, that’s impossible,” replied Maskull reflectively. “It was

[026-026]Crystalman. And it isn’t a question of my suspecting it—I know it.”

[026-027]“How?”

[026-028]“Because this is Crystalman’s world, and Surtur’s world is something

[026-029]quite different.”

[026-030]“That’s queer, then,” said Polecrab.

[026-031]“Since I’ve come out of that forest,” proceeded Maskull, talking half to

[026-032]himself, “a change has come over me, and I see things differently.

[026-033]Everything here looks much more solid and real in my eyes than in other

[026-034]places so much so that I can’t entertain the least doubt of its

[026-035]existence. It not only looks real, it is real—and on that I would stake

[026-036]my life.... But at the same time that it’s real, it is false.”

[026-037]“Like a dream?”

[026-038]“No—not at all like a dream, and that’s just what I want to explain.

[026-039]This world of yours—and perhaps of mine too, for that matter—doesn’t

[026-040]give me the slightest impression of a dream, or an illusion, or anything

[026-041]of that sort. I know it’s really here at this moment, and it’s exactly

[026-042]as we’re seeing it, you and I. Yet it’s false. It’s false in this sense,

[026-043]Polecrab. Side by side with it another world exists, and that other

[026-044]world is the true one, and this one is all false and deceitful, to the

[026-045]very core. And so it occurs to me that reality and falseness are two

[026-046]words for the same thing.”

[026-047]“Perhaps there is such another world,” said Polecrab huskily. “But did

[026-048]that vision also seem real and false to you?”

[026-049]“Very real, but not false then, for then I didn’t understand all this.

[026-050]But just because it was real, it couldn’t have been Surtur, who has no

[026-051]connection with reality.”

[026-052]“Didn’t those drum taps sound real to you?”

[026-053]“I had to hear them with my ears, and so they sounded real to me. Still,

[026-054]they were somehow different, and they certainly came from Surtur. If I

[026-055]didn’t hear them correctly, that was my fault and not his.”

[026-056]Polecrab growled a little. “If Surtur chooses to speak to you in that

[026-057]fashion, it appears he’s trying to say something.”

[026-058]“What else can I think? But, Polecrab, what’s your opinion—is he calling

[026-059]me to the life after death?”

[026-060]The old man stirred uneasily. “I’m a fisherman,” he said, after a minute

[026-061]or two. “I live by killing, and so does everybody. This life seems to me

[026-062]all wrong. So maybe life of any kind is wrong, and Surtur’s world is not

[026-063]life at all, but something else.”

[026-064]“Yes, but will death lead me to it, whatever it is?”

[026-065]“Ask the dead,” said Polecrab, “and not a living man.”

[026-066]Maskull continued. “In the forest I heard music and saw a light, which

[026-067]could not have belonged to this world. They were too strong for my

[026-068]senses, and I must have fainted for a long time. There was a vision as

[026-069]well, in which I saw myself killed, while Nightspore walked on toward

[026-070]the light, alone.”

[026-071]Polecrab uttered his grunt. “You have enough to think over.”

[026-072]A short silence ensued, which was broken by Maskull.

[026-073]“So strong is my sense of the untruth of this present life, that it may

[026-074]come to my putting an end to myself.” The fisherman remained quiet and

[026-075]immobile.

[026-076]Maskull lay on his stomach, propped his face on his hands, and stared at

[026-077]him. “What do you think, Polecrab? Is it possible for any man, while in

[026-078]the body, to gain a closer view of that other world than I have done?”

[026-079]“I am an ignorant man, stranger, so I can’t say. Perhaps there are many

[026-080]others like you who would gladly know.”

[026-081]“Where? I should like to meet them.”

[026-082]“Do you think you were made of one stuff, and the rest of mankind of

[026-083]another stuff?”

[026-084]“I can’t be so presumptuous. Possibly all men are reaching out toward

[026-085]Muspel, in most cases without being aware of it.”

[026-086]“In the wrong direction,” said Polecrab.

[026-087]Maskull gave him a strange look. “How so?”

[026-088]“I don’t speak from my own wisdom,” said Polecrab, “for I have none; but

[026-089]I have just now recalled what Broodviol once told me, when I was a young

[026-090]man, and he was an old one. He said that Crystalman tries to turn all

[026-091]things into one, and that whichever way his shapes march, in order to

[026-092]escape from him, they find themselves again face to face with

[026-093]Crystalman, and are changed into new crystals. But that this marching of

[026-094]shapes (which we call ‘forking’) springs from the unconscious desire to

[026-095]find Surtur, but is in the opposite direction to the right one. For

[026-096]Surtur’s world does not lie on this side of the one, which was the

[026-097]beginning of life, but on the other side; and to get to it we must

[026-098]repass through the one. But this can only be by renouncing our self-

[026-099]life, and reuniting ourselves to the whole of Crystalman’s world. And

[026-100]when this has been done, it is only the first stage of the journey;

[026-101]though many good men imagine it to be the whole journey.... As far as I

[026-102]can remember, that is what Broodviol said, but perhaps, as I was then a

[026-103]young and ignorant man, I may have left out words which would explain

[026-104]his meaning better.”

[026-105]Maskull, who had listened attentively to all this, remained thoughtful

[026-106]at the end.

[026-107]“It’s plain enough,” he said. “But what did he mean by our reuniting

[026-108]ourselves to Crystalman’s world? If it is false, are we to make

[026-109]ourselves false as well?”

[026-110]“I didn’t ask him that question, and you are as well qualified to answer

[026-111]it as I am.”

[026-112]“He must have meant that, as it is, we are each of us living in a false,

[026-113]private world of our own, a world of dreams and appetites and distorted

[026-114]perceptions. By embracing the great world we certainly lose nothing in

[026-115]truth and reality.”

[026-116]Polecrab withdrew his feet from the water, stood up, yawned, and

[026-117]stretched his limbs.

[026-118]“I have told you all I know,” he said in a surly voice. “Now let me go

[026-119]to sleep.”

[026-120]Maskull kept his eyes fixed on him, but made no reply. The old man let

[026-121]himself down stiffly on to the ground, and prepared to rest.

[026-122]While he was still arranging his position to his liking, a footfall

[026-123]sounded behind the two men, coming from the direction of the forest.

[026-124]Maskull twisted his neck, and saw a woman approaching them. He at once

[026-125]guessed that it was Polecrab’s wife. He sat up, but the fisherman did

[026-126]not stir. The woman came and stood in front of them, looking down from

[026-127]what appeared a great height.

[026-128]Her dress was similar to her husband’s, but covered her limbs more. She

[026-129]was young, tall, slender, and strikingly erect. Her skin was lightly

[026-130]tanned, and she looked strong, but not at all peasantlike. Refinement

[026-131]was stamped all over her. Her face had too much energy of expression for

[026-132]a woman, and she was not beautiful. Her three great eyes kept flashing

[026-133]and glowing. She had great masses of fine, yellow hair, coiled up and

[026-134]fastened, but so carelessly that some of the strands were flowing down

[026-135]her back.

[026-136]When she spoke, it was in a rather weak voice, but full of lights and

[026-137]shades, and somehow intense passionateness never seemed to be far away

[026-138]from it.

[026-139]“Forgiveness is asked for listening to your conversation,” she said,

[026-140]addressing Maskull. “I was resting behind the tree, and heard it all.”

[026-141]He got up slowly. “Are you Polecrab’s wife?”

[026-142]“She is my wife,” said Polecrab, “and her name is Gleameil. Sit down

[026-143]again, stranger—and you too, wife, since you are here.”

[026-144]They both obeyed. “I heard everything,” repeated Gleameil. “But what I

[026-145]did not hear was where you are going to, Maskull, after you have left

[026-146]us.”

[026-147]“I know no more than you do.”

[026-148]“Listen, then. There’s only one place for you to go to, and that is

[026-149]Swaylone’s Island. I will ferry you across myself before sunset.”

[026-150]“What shall I find there?”

[026-151]“He may go, wife,” put in the old man hoarsely, “but I won’t allow you

[026-152]to go. I will take him over myself.”

[026-153]“No, you have always put me off,” said Gleameil, with some emotion.

[026-154]“This time I mean to go. When Teargeld shines at night, and I sit on the

[026-155]shore here, listening to Earthrid’s music travelling faintly across the

[026-156]sea, I am tortured—I can’t endure it.... I have long since made up my

[026-157]mind to go to the island, and see what this music is. If it’s bad, if it

[026-158]kills me—well.”

[026-159]“What have I to do with the man and his music, Gleameil?” demanded

[026-160]Maskull.

[026-161]“I think the music will answer all your questions better than Polecrab

[026-162]has done—and possibly in a way that will surprise you.”

[026-163]“What kind of music can it be to travel all those miles across the sea?”

[026-164]“A peculiar kind, so we are told. Not pleasant, but painful. And the man

[026-165]that can play the instrument of Earthrid would be able to conjure up the

[026-166]most astonishing forms, which are not phantasms, but realities.”

[026-167]“That may be so,” growled Polecrab. “But I have been to the island by

[026-168]daylight, and what did I find there? Human bones, new and ancient. Those

[026-169]are Earthrid’s victims. And you, wife, shall not go.”

[026-170]“But will that music play tonight?” asked Maskull.

[026-171]“Yes,” replied Gleameil, gazing at him intently. “When Teargeld rises,

[026-172]which is our moon.”

[026-173]“If Earthrid plays men to death, it appears to me that his own death is

[026-174]due. In any case I should like to hear those sounds for myself. But as

[026-175]for taking you with me, Gleameil—women die too easily in Tormance. I

[026-176]have only just now washed myself clean of the death blood of another

[026-177]woman.”

[026-178]Gleameil laughed, but said nothing.

[026-179]“Now go to sleep,” said Polecrab. “When the time comes, I will take you

[026-180]across myself.”

[026-181]He lay down again, and closed his eyes. Maskull followed his example;

[026-182]but Gleameil remained sitting erect, with her legs under her.

[026-183]“Who was that other woman, Maskull?” she asked presently.

[026-184]He did not answer, but pretended to sleep.

[026-185]Chapter 15. SWAYLONE’S ISLAND

[026-186]When he awoke, the day was not so bright, and he guessed it was late

[026-187]afternoon. Polecrab and his wife were both on their feet, and another

[026-188]meal of fish had been cooked and was waiting for him.

[026-189]“Is it decided who is to go with me?” he asked, before sitting down.

[026-190]“I go,” said Gleameil.

[026-191]“Do you agree, Polecrab?”

[026-192]The fisherman growled a little in his throat and motioned to the others

[026-193]to take their seats. He took a mouthful before answering.
