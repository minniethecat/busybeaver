# Blodsombre

## Definition

Blodsombre is the name given by the inhabitants of Tormance to the transitional twilight period between the setting of the primary sun [[Branchspell]] and the rising of the second sun [[Alppain]]. The term evokes blood and shadow, suggesting a liminal state that is neither fully illuminated nor completely dark — an interval of ambiguous half-light that carries its own distinctive character in the novel's celestial hierarchy.

## In the Novel

Blodsombre occurs regularly as part of Tormance's dual-sun day cycle. After Branchspell sets in the west, the world does not descend into ordinary night; instead, it enters the blodsombre period — a twilight during which the sky holds a reddish, shadowy quality before Alppain's blue glow becomes visible in the north.

The period serves as a narrative marker of transition. When characters travel through blodsombre, they are typically moving between states of being — between the known world of Branchspell's material clarity and the stranger realms illuminated by Alppain's spiritual light. Maskull's most significant transformations often occur during or immediately after blodsombre passages.

The phenomenon is part of Tormance's unique astronomical arrangement. Because the planet orbits Arcturus and has two suns — the visible Branchspell and the invisible Alppain — its light cycle differs fundamentally from Earth's simple alternation of day and night. Blodsombre is the distinctive Tormance equivalent of twilight, but it carries none of Earth's connotations of peaceful evening; instead, it is a time of uncertainty and impending change.

## Philosophical Meaning

Blodsombre represents the intermediate state between two modes of perception in Lindsay's allegory. If Branchspell's white light corresponds to material consciousness and Alppain's blue radiance to spiritual vision, then blodsombre is the threshold state — the moment when ordinary perception falters but higher vision has not yet crystallised.

This liminality is central to Maskull's journey. He repeatedly finds himself in states of confusion, doubt, and partial understanding — the psychological equivalent of blodsombre — before each breakthrough into new perception. The twilight period thus mirrors the experience of philosophical transition: the uncomfortable interval when old certainties have dissolved but new truths have not yet fully emerged.

The name itself — suggesting blood and shadow — evokes sacrifice and obscurity, hinting that the passage from material to spiritual vision exacts a cost. One cannot move directly from Branchspell's daylight to Alppain's revelation; one must first endure the shadow.
