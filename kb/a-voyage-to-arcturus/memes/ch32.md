# Section 32

[032-001]As he continued staring into those strange, archaic eyes, he had an

[032-002]intuitive feeling that aer lover was no other than Shaping himself. It

[032-003]came to him that the design of this love was not the continuance of the

[032-004]race but the immortality on earth of the individual. No children were

[032-005]produced by the act; the lover aerself was the eternal child. Further,

[032-006]ae sought like a man, but received like a woman. All these things were

[032-007]dimly and confusedly expressed by this extraordinary being, who seemed

[032-008]to have dropped out of another age, when creation was different.

[032-009]Of all the weird personalities Maskull had so far met in Tormance, this

[032-010]one struck him as infinitely the most foreign—that is, the farthest

[032-011]removed from him in spiritual structure. If they were to live together

[032-012]for a hundred years, they could never be companions.

[032-013]Maskull pulled himself out of his trancelike meditations and, viewing

[032-014]the newcomer in greater detail, tried with his understanding to account

[032-015]for the marvellous things told him by his intuitions. Ae possessed broad

[032-016]shoulders and big bones, and was without female breasts, and so far ae

[032-017]resembled a man. But the bones were so flat and angular that aer flesh

[032-018]presented something of the character of a crystal, having plane surfaces

[032-019]in place of curves. The body looked as if it had not been ground down by

[032-020]the sea of ages into smooth and rounded regularity but had sprung

[032-021]together in angles and facets as the result of a single, sudden idea.

[032-022]The face too was broken and irregular. With his racial prejudices,

[032-023]Maskull found little beauty in it, yet beauty there was, though neither

[032-024]of a masculine nor of a feminine type, for it had the three essentials

[032-025]of beauty: character, intelligence, and repose. The skin was copper-

[032-026]coloured and strangely luminous, as if lighted from within. The face was

[032-027]beardless, but the hair of the head was as long as a woman’s, and,

[032-028]dressed in a single plait, fell down behind as far as the ankles. Ae

[032-029]possessed only two eyes. That part of the turban which went across the

[032-030]forehead protruded so far in front that it evidently concealed some

[032-031]organ.

[032-032]Maskull found it impossible to compute aer age. The frame appeared

[032-033]active, vigorous, and healthy, the skin was clear and glowing; the eyes

[032-034]were powerful and alert—ae might well be in early youth. Nevertheless,

[032-035]the longer Maskull gazed, the more an impression of unbelievable

[032-036]ancientness came upon him—aer real youth seemed as far away as the view

[032-037]observed through a reversed telescope.

[032-038]At last he addressed the stranger, though it was just as if he were

[032-039]conversing with a dream. “To what sex do you belong?” he asked.

[032-040]The voice in which the reply came was neither manly nor womanly, but was

[032-041]oddly suggestive of a mystical forest horn, heard from a great distance.

[032-042]“Nowadays there are men and women, but in the olden times the world was

[032-043]peopled by ‘phaens.’ I think I am the only survivor of all those beings

[032-044]who were then passing through Faceny’s mind.”

[032-045]“Faceny?”

[032-046]“Who is now miscalled Shaping or Crystalman. The superficial names

[032-047]invented by a race of superficial creatures.”

[032-048]“What’s your own name?”

[032-049]“Leehallfae.”

[032-050]“What?”

[032-051]“Leehallfae. And yours is Maskull. I read in your mind that you have

[032-052]just come through some wonderful adventures. You seem to possess

[032-053]extraordinary luck. If it lasts long enough, perhaps I can make use of

[032-054]it.”

[032-055]“Do you think that my luck exists for your benefit?... But never mind

[032-056]that now. It is your _sex_ that interests me. How do you satisfy your

[032-057]desires?”

[032-058]Leehallfae pointed to the concealed organ on her brow. “With that I

[032-059]gather life from the streams that flow in all the hundred Matterplay

[032-060]valleys. The streams spring direct from Faceny. My whole life has been

[032-061]spent trying to find Faceny himself. I’ve hunted so long that if I were

[032-062]to state the number of years you would believe I lied.”

[032-063]Maskull looked at the phaen slowly. “In Ifdawn I met someone else from

[032-064]Matterplay—a young man called Digrung. I absorbed him.”

[032-065]“You can’t be telling me this out of vanity.”

[032-066]“It was a fearful crime. What will come of it?”

[032-067]Leehallfae gave a curious, wrinkled smile. “In Matterplay he will stir

[032-068]inside you, for he smells the air. Already you have his eyes.... I knew

[032-069]him.... Take care of yourself, or something more startling may happen.

[032-070]Keep out of the water.”

[032-071]“This seems to me a terrible valley, in which anything may happen.”

[032-072]“Don’t torment yourself about Digrung. The valleys belong by right to

[032-073]the phaens—the men here are interlopers. It is a good work to remove

[032-074]them.”

[032-075]Maskull continued thoughtful. “I say no more, but I see I will have to

[032-076]be cautious. What did you mean about my helping you with my luck?”

[032-077]“Your luck is fast weakening, but it may still be strong enough to serve

[032-078]me. Together we will search for Threal.”

[032-079]“Search for Threal—why, is it so hard to find?”

[032-080]“I have told you that my whole life has been spent in the quest.”

[032-081]“You said Faceny, Leehallfae.”

[032-082]The phaen gazed at him with queer, ancient eyes, and smiled again. “This

[032-083]stream, Maskull, like every other life stream in Matterplay, has its

[032-084]source in Faceny. But as all these streams issue out from Threal, it is

[032-085]in Threal that we must look for Faceny.”

[032-086]“But what’s to prevent your finding Threal? Surely it’s a well-known

[032-087]country?”

[032-088]“It lies underground. Its communications with the upper world are few,

[032-089]and where they are, no one that I have ever spoken to knows. I have

[032-090]scoured the valleys and the hills. I have been to the very gates of

[032-091]Lichstorm. I am old, so that your aged men would appear newborn infants

[032-092]beside me, but I am as far from Threal as when I was a green youth,

[032-093]dwelling among a throng of fellow phaens.”

[032-094]“Then, if my luck is good, yours is very bad.... But when you have found

[032-095]Faceny, what do you gain?”

[032-096]Leehallfae looked at him in silence. The smile faded from aer face, and

[032-097]its place was taken by such a look of unearthly pain and sorrow that

[032-098]Maskull had no need to press his question. Ae was consumed by the grief

[032-099]and yearning of a lover eternally separated from the loved one, the

[032-100]scents and traces of whose person were always present. This passion

[032-101]stamped aer features at that moment with a wild, stern, spiritual

[032-102]beauty, far transcending any beauty of woman or man.

[032-103]But the expression vanished suddenly, and then the abrupt contrast

[032-104]showed Maskull the real Leehallfae. Aer sensuality was solitary, but

[032-105]vulgar—it was like the heroism of a lonely nature, pursuing animal aims

[032-106]with untiring persistence.

[032-107]He looked at the phaen askance, and drummed his fingers against his

[032-108]thigh. “Well, we will go together. We may find something, and in any

[032-109]case I shan’t be sorry to converse with such a singular individual as

[032-110]yourself.”

[032-111]“But I should warn you, Maskull. You and I are of different creations. A

[032-112]phaen’s body contains the whole of life, a man’s body contains only the

[032-113]half of life—the other half is in woman. Faceny may be too strong a

[032-114]draught for your body to endure.... Do you not feel this?”

[032-115]“I am dull with my different feelings. I must take what precautions I

[032-116]can, and chance the rest.” He bent down, and, taking hold of the phaen’s

[032-117]thin and ragged robe, tore off a broad strip, which he proceeded to

[032-118]swathe in folds around his forehead. “I’m not forgetting your advice,

[032-119]Leehallfae. I would not like to start the walk as Maskull and finish it

[032-120]as Digrung.”

[032-121]The phaen gave a twisted grin, and they began to move upstream. The road

[032-122]was difficult. They had to stride from boulder to boulder, and found it

[032-123]warm work. Occasionally a worse obstacle presented itself, which they

[032-124]could surmount only by climbing. There was no more conversation for a

[032-125]long time. Maskull, as far as possible, adopted his companion’s counsel

[032-126]to avoid the water, but here and there he was forced to set foot in it.

[032-127]The second or third time he did so, he felt a sudden agony in his arm,

[032-128]where it had been wounded by Krag. His eyes grew joyful; his fears

[032-129]vanished; and he began deliberately to tread the stream.

[032-130]Leehallfae stroked aer chin and watched him with screwed-up eyes, trying

[032-131]to comprehend what had happened. “Is your luck speaking to you, Maskull,

[032-132]or what is the matter?”

[032-133]“Listen. You are a being of antique experience, and ought to know, if

[032-134]anyone does. What is Muspel?”

[032-135]The phaen’s face was blank. “I don’t know the name.”

[032-136]“It is another world of some sort.”

[032-137]“That cannot be. There is only this one world—Faceny’s.”

[032-138]Maskull came up to aer, linked arms, and began to talk. “I’m glad I fell

[032-139]in with you, Leehallfae, for this valley and everything connected with

[032-140]it need a lot of explaining. For example, in this spot there are hardly

[032-141]any organic forms left—why have they all disappeared? You call this

[032-142]brook a ‘life stream,’ yet the nearer its source we get, the less life

[032-143]it produces. A mile or two lower down we had those spontaneous plant-

[032-144]animals appearing out of nowhere, while right down by the sea, plants

[032-145]and animals were tumbling over one another. Now, if all this is

[032-146]connected in some mysterious way or other with your Faceny, it seems to

[032-147]me he must have a most paradoxical nature. His essence doesn’t start

[032-148]creating shapes until it has become thoroughly weakened and watered....

[032-149]But perhaps both of us are talking nonsense.”

[032-150]Leehallfae shook aer head. “Everything hangs together. The stream is

[032-151]life, and it is throwing off sparks of life all the time. When these

[032-152]sparks are caught and imprisoned by matter, they become living shapes.

[032-153]The nearer the stream is to its source, the more terrible and vigorous

[032-154]is its life. You’ll see for yourself when we reach the head of the

[032-155]valley that there are no living shapes there at all. That means that

[032-156]there is no kind of matter tough enough to capture and hold the terrible

[032-157]sparks that are to be found there. Lower down the stream, most of the

[032-158]sparks are vigorous enough to escape to the upper air, but some are held

[032-159]when they are a little way up, and these burst suddenly into shapes. I

[032-160]myself am of this nature. Lower down still, toward the sea, the stream

[032-161]has lost a great part of its vital power and the sparks are lazy and

[032-162]sluggish. They spread out, rather than rise into the air. There is

[032-163]hardly any kind of matter, however delicate, that is incapable of

[032-164]capturing these feeble sparks, and they are captured in multitudes—that

[032-165]accounts for the innumerable living shapes you see there. But not only

[032-166]that—the sparks are passed from one body to another by way of

[032-167]generation, and can never hope to cease being so until they are worn out

[032-168]by decay. Lowest of all, you have the Sinking Sea itself. There the

[032-169]degenerate and enfeebled life of the Matterplay streams has for its body

[032-170]the whole sea. So weak is it’s power that it can’t succeed in creating

[032-171]any shapes at all but you can see its ceaseless, futile attempts to do

[032-172]so, in those spouts.”

[032-173]“So the slow development of men and women is due to the feebleness of

[032-174]the life germ in their case?”

[032-175]“Exactly. It can’t attain all its desires at once. And now you can see

[032-176]how immeasurably superior are the phaens, who spring spontaneously from

[032-177]the more electric and vigorous sparks.”

[032-178]“But where does the matter come from that imprisons these sparks?”

[032-179]“When life dies, it becomes matter. Matter itself dies, but its place is

[032-180]constantly taken by new matter.”

[032-181]“But if life comes from Faceny, how can it die at all?”

[032-182]“Life is the thoughts of Faceny, and once these thoughts have left his

[032-183]brain they are nothing—mere dying embers.”
