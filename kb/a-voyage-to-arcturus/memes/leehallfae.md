# Leehallfae

## Role

Leehallfae is a being of the "third sex" from the land of Phaeon, encountered by [[maskull]] in the later stages of his journey across [[tormance]]. As a guide figure in Chapters 32–33, Leehallfae explains the nature of [[faceny]] — the source-mind whose thoughts constitute all living things — and serves as the narrative vehicle for the novel's most explicit metaphysical exposition. But Leehallfae is far more than a repository of doctrine: aer physical nature, aer mode of perception, and aer lifelong quest to find [[faceny|Faceny]] make aer one of Lindsay's most radical creations, a being whose very existence challenges the categories of embodiment, desire, and knowledge.

## Appearance and Nature

Leehallfae's introduction is stark and elliptical:

> "Leehallfae." [032-049]

> "Leehallfae. And yours is Maskull. I read your mind." [032-051]

The mind-reading is the first sign of Leehallfae's difference. Ae does not need to ask [[maskull|Maskull]]'s name; ae simply knows it. This effortless penetration of mental privacy establishes Leehallfae as a being for whom the ordinary boundaries between selves are permeable.

Aer physical nature is elusive, phantasmal. When ae gestures toward the concealed organs of perception that [[tormance|Tormance]]'s beings possess, ae reveals something of aer own mode of apprehension:

> Leehallfae pointed to the concealed organs which, in a Tormance human being, occupy the position of the pineal gland. [032-058]

The reference to "concealed organs" is typical of the novel's later chapters, where the body becomes increasingly strange and the senses multiply beyond the familiar five. Leehallfae, who is *from* this world of multiplied senses, seems at home in a perceptual landscape that [[maskull|Maskull]] can only dimly apprehend.

There is also an unsettling quality to Leehallfae's expression:

> Leehallfae gave a curious, wrinkled smile. [032-067]

The smile recurs — "wrinkled," "distorted" — and carries a quality that is neither warm nor cold but simply *other*, the face of a being whose emotional life does not map onto human categories:

> Leehallfae gave another wrinkled smile. The smile appeared to be quite involuntary. [033-003]

> Leehallfae gave a distorted grin, expressing who knows what emotion. [033-043]

The narrator's frank admission — "who knows what emotion" — marks the limit of human empathy. Leehallfae's affect is opaque because aer nature is opaque. Lindsay uses aer to press against the edges of what the novel's human characters (and human readers) can understand.

Aer sensuality is equally strange. The text describes aer with a word that belongs to no human sexual category:

> A kind of radiance seemed to come from her, which showed Maskull the real Leehallfae. Aer sensuality was solitary, and yet at the same time it was not repellent. [032-104]

The pronoun shift from "her" to the invented "aer" is Lindsay's explicit linguistic marker of the third sex. "Solitary" sensuality — self-contained, not directed outward in desire — is a concept that has no counterpart in ordinary human experience. Leehallfae is neither male nor female, neither desiring nor desired in any familiar sense.

## The Quest for Faceny

Leehallfae's entire existence is oriented toward finding [[faceny|Faceny]]:

> "You said Faceny, Leehallfae." [032-081]

> Leehallfae looked at him in silence. The name seemed to produce a curious effect on aer. [032-096]

The "curious effect" is reverence, longing, and something more — an existential dependence on the object of aer quest. For Leehallfae, [[faceny|Faceny]] is not an abstract philosophical principle but a person, a presence, the source toward which aer entire being inclines:

> "I would not like to start the long journey till I have some other companionship than my own thoughts." [032-119]

Ae seeks companionship not out of loneliness in any human sense but because the quest for [[faceny|Faceny]] is too great to bear alone. [[maskull|Maskull]], as the companion ae has chosen, is both a practical ally for the journey and a witness to the quest's completion.

Leehallfae strokes aer chin as ae thinks [032-130], a small, habitual gesture that underscores aer difference — the third-sex body has its own mannerisms, its own rhythms of thought and expression:

> "I will go in with you, Leehallfae, for this valley and everything connected with it attracts me strangely." [032-139]

[[maskull|Maskull]] is drawn in, not by rational persuasion but by the "strange attraction" of Leehallfae and Leehallfae's world. The quest for [[faceny|Faceny]] exerts a gravitational pull that operates below the level of conscious decision.

When [[maskull|Maskull]] questions the purpose of the quest, Leehallfae's response is opaque:

> "Leehallfae, I fail to see what you personally are to gain in this new existence." [033-021]

> Leehallfae walked on in silence. [033-032]

The silence is the answer. What Leehallfae stands to gain cannot be articulated in language [[maskull|Maskull]] would understand — perhaps cannot be articulated in language at all. The quest is its own justification; the longing for [[faceny|Faceny]] is the deepest truth of Leehallfae's nature, and to explain it would be to reduce it.

> "Yes," answered Leehallfae, "we have now come to the region where Faceny is not a mere name." [033-067]

The journey has brought them to a threshold. [[faceny|Faceny]] is becoming real — not a concept, not a doctrine, but a presence whose proximity changes the quality of the world.

## Philosophical Significance

Leehallfae embodies one of Lindsay's most radical philosophical propositions: that the categories of human embodiment — sex, desire, identity — are contingent, not universal. The third sex is not a variation on the human but an entirely different mode of being, one that Lindsay signals through linguistic invention ("aer") and through Leehallfae's opaque, involuntary expressiveness.

Aer role as a guide to [[maskull|Maskull]] is philosophically significant because it reverses the expected relationship between knowledge and embodiment. Leehallfae knows about [[faceny|Faceny]] not through study or revelation but through *being what ae is*. Aer knowledge is not propositional but ontological; ae does not believe in [[faceny|Faceny]], ae *belongs* to [[faceny|Faceny]]. The quest to find [[faceny|Faceny]] is not a search for information but a return to the source — a journey of ontological homecoming.

There is also a crucial contrast between Leehallfae and many of the novel's other guide figures. Characters like [[panawe]] or [[oceaxe]] embody specific philosophical positions — self-sacrifice, will-to-power, and so on. Leehallfae does not offer a philosophy; ae offers a *relationship*, a mode of being-in-quest that is its own justification. In this, ae prefigures the novel's culminating movements toward a reality beyond doctrine.

The "solitary sensuality" of Leehallfae is perhaps the most philosophically dense detail. In a novel where desire is typically destructive or distorting — consider the fate of characters who give themselves to erotic longing — Leehallfae's self-contained sensuality offers an alternative: a mode of embodiment that is fully alive without being directed outward in conquest or consumption. It is a vision of what human desire might be if it were not entangled with the will to possess.

## Key Passages

> Leehallfae pointed to the concealed organs which, in a Tormance human being, occupy the position of the pineal gland. [032-058]

This passage situates Leehallfae within the novel's anatomy of transcendence — the "concealed organs" that enable perception beyond the ordinary senses are the bodily basis of spiritual vision.

> A kind of radiance seemed to come from her, which showed Maskull the real Leehallfae. Aer sensuality was solitary, and yet at the same time it was not repellent. [032-104]

The disclosure of Leehallfae's "real" nature through radiance rather than speech is characteristic of the novel's later mode, where truth is shown rather than argued.

> Leehallfae gave a distorted grin, expressing who knows what emotion. [033-043]

The narrator's frank admission of incomprehension is a moment of epistemological humility. Leehallfae's inner life is opaque to human categories of feeling.

> "Yes," answered Leehallfae, "we have now come to the region where Faceny is not a mere name." [033-067]

The transformation of "[[faceny|Faceny]]" from a name to a reality marks the threshold between doctrinal knowledge and direct encounter — the journey from theology to theophany.

## Relationships

- **[[maskull]]** — Leehallfae reads [[maskull|Maskull]]'s mind at their first meeting and chooses him as a companion for the quest toward [[faceny|Faceny]]. Their relationship is neither friendship nor alliance in any human sense; it is a convergence of trajectories, two seekers brought together by the gravitational pull of the source.
- **[[faceny]]** — The source-mind, the creator whose thoughts constitute all living things. Leehallfae's entire existence is oriented toward finding [[faceny|Faceny]], and aer knowledge of [[faceny|Faceny]] is not learned but innate — a function of aer very being as a third-sex creature.
- **[[phaon]]** — Leehallfae's homeland, a distinctive region of [[tormance|Tormance]] that produces beings of the third sex. Phaeon is mentioned in relation to Leehallfae's origin but never visited in the narrative.
