# Oceaxe

## Role

Oceaxe is the title character of Chapter 9, encountered by [[maskull]] on the Lusion Plain. She is the first fully sexualized female figure [[maskull|Maskull]] meets on [[tormance|Tormance]], and she embodies the danger of erotic attachment — a recurring theme in Lindsay's allegorical system where sensuality is both alluring and spiritually perilous.

## In the Novel

Oceaxe first appears sitting carelessly on a couch of mosses, eating, as [[maskull|Maskull]] emerges onto the Lusion Plain [009-4711]. She immediately establishes herself as confident, knowing, and physically self-possessed. When [[maskull|Maskull]] asks her name, she answers simply "Oceaxe" [009-4711], offering no explanation — her identity needs no justification.

Oceaxe advises [[maskull|Maskull]] on practical matters of survival — the application of drude, the navigation of the plain — but her assistance carries an undertow of manipulation. She studies [[maskull|Maskull]]'s body with frank appraisal: "Did some woman give you that?" [009-4911], and her presence becomes "more dangerously feminine" [009-4929] as their journey continues.

Her relationship with [[maskull|Maskull]] reaches a crisis point in Chapter 10, when [[tydomin]] appears. Oceaxe is visibly afraid of [[tydomin|Tydomin]], and her power over [[maskull|Maskull]] evaporates in [[tydomin|Tydomin]]'s moral gravity. [[tydomin|Tydomin]]'s judgment leads to Oceaxe's death — she is pushed over the precipice — and [[maskull|Maskull]] is forced to reckon with his complicity in her fate.

## Philosophical Significance

Oceaxe represents the seduction of the material and the sensual. In Lindsay's allegorical framework, she is the temptation to remain at the level of bodily desire and attachment rather than ascending toward spiritual truth. Her dangerous femininity is not a statement about women but about a mode of consciousness — one that seeks to possess and be possessed rather than to transcend.

Her death at [[tydomin|Tydomin]]'s instigation marks the novel's insistence that sensual attachment must be overcome (sometimes violently) for moral growth to proceed. Yet the cost of this overcoming — [[maskull|Maskull]]'s guilt — is real and lasting.
