# Section 45

[045-001]The dying man closed his eyes, and smiled.

[045-002]Opening them again, a few moments later, with an effort, he murmured,

[045-003]“Who are you?”

[045-004]Krag maintained a gloomy silence.

[045-005]Shortly afterward a frightful pang passed through Maskull’s heart, and

[045-006]he died immediately.

[045-007]Krag turned his head around. “The night is really past at last,

[045-008]Nightspore.... The day is here.”

[045-009]Nightspore gazed long and earnestly at Maskull’s body. “Why was all this

[045-010]necessary?”

[045-011]“Ask Crystalman,” replied Krag sternly. “His world is no joke. He has a

[045-012]strong clutch—but I have a stronger... Maskull was his, but Nightspore

[045-013]is mine.”

[045-014]Chapter 21. MUSPEL

[045-015]The fog thickened so that the two suns wholly disappeared, and all grew

[045-016]as black as night. Nightspore could no longer see his companion. The

[045-017]water lapped gently against the side of the island raft.

[045-018]“You say the night is past,” said Nightspore. “But the night is still

[045-019]here. Am I dead, or alive?”

[045-020]“You are still in Crystalman’s world, but you belong to it no more. We

[045-021]are approaching Muspel.”

[045-022]Nightspore felt a strong, silent throbbing of the air—a rhythmical

[045-023]pulsation, in four-four time. “There is the drumming,” he exclaimed.

[045-024]“Do you understand it, or have you forgotten?”

[045-025]“I half understand it, but I’m all confused.”

[045-026]“It’s evident Crystalman has dug his claws into you pretty deeply,” said

[045-027]Krag. “The sound comes from Muspel, but the rhythm is caused by its

[045-028]travelling through Crystalman’s atmosphere. His nature is rhythm as he

[045-029]loves to call it—or dull, deadly repetition, as I name it.”

[045-030]“I remember,” said Nightspore, biting his nails in the dark.

[045-031]The throbbing became audible; it now sounded like a distant drum. A

[045-032]small patch of strange light in the far distance, straight ahead of

[045-033]them, began faintly to illuminate the floating island and the glassy sea

[045-034]around it.

[045-035]“Do all men escape from that ghastly world, or only I, and a few like

[045-036]me?” asked Nightspore.

[045-037]“If all escaped, I shouldn’t sweat, my friend... There’s hard work, and

[045-038]anguish, and the risk of total death, waiting for us yonder.”

[045-039]Nightspore’s heart sank. “Have I not yet finished, then?”

[045-040]“If you wish it. You have got through. But will you wish it?”

[045-041]The drumming grew loud and painful. The light resolved itself into a

[045-042]tiny oblong of mysterious brightness in a huge wall of night. Krag’s

[045-043]grim and rocklike features were revealed.

[045-044]“I can’t face rebirth,” said Nightspore. “The horror of death is nothing

[045-045]to it.”

[045-046]“You will choose.”

[045-047]“I can do nothing. Crystalman is too powerful. I barely escaped with my

[045-048]own soul.”

[045-049]“You are still stupid with Earth fumes, and see nothing straight,” said

[045-050]Krag.

[045-051]Nightspore made no reply, but seemed to be trying to recall something.

[045-052]The water around them was so still, colourless, and transparent, that

[045-053]they scarcely seemed to be borne up by liquid matter at all. Maskull’s

[045-054]corpse had disappeared.

[045-055]The drumming was now like the clanging of iron. The oblong patch of

[045-056]light grew much bigger; it burned, fierce and wild. The darkness above,

[045-057]below, and on either side of it, began to shape itself into the

[045-058]semblance of a huge, black wall, without bounds.

[045-059]“Is that really a wall we are coming to?”

[045-060]“You will soon find out. What you see is Muspel, and that light is the

[045-061]gate you have to enter.”

[045-062]Nightspore’s heart beat wildly.

[045-063]“Shall I remember?” he muttered.

[045-064]“Yes, you’ll remember.”

[045-065]“Accompany me, Krag, or I shall be lost.”

[045-066]“There is nothing for me to do in there. I shall wait outside for you.”

[045-067]“You are returning to the struggle?” demanded Nightspore, gnawing his

[045-068]fingertips.

[045-069]“Yes.”

[045-070]“I dare not.”

[045-071]The thunderous clangor of the rhythmical beats struck on his head like

[045-072]actual blows. The light glared so vividly that he was no longer able to

[045-073]look at it. It had the startling irregularity of continuous lightning,

[045-074]but it possessed this further peculiarity—that it seemed somehow to give

[045-075]out not actual light, but emotion, seen as light. They continued to

[045-076]approach the wall of darkness, straight toward the door. The glasslike

[045-077]water flowed right against it, its surface reaching up almost to the

[045-078]threshold.

[045-079]They could not speak any more; the noise was too deafening.

[045-080]In a few minutes they were before the gateway. Nightspore turned his

[045-081]back and hid his eyes in his two hands, but even then he was blinded by

[045-082]the light. So passionate were his feelings that his body seemed to

[045-083]enlarge itself. At every frightful beat of sound, he quivered violently.

[045-084]The entrance was doorless. Krag jumped onto the rocky platform and

[045-085]pulled Nightspore after him.

[045-086]Once through the gateway, the light vanished. The rhythmical sound—blows

[045-087]totally ceased. Nightspore dropped his hands.... All was dark and quiet

[045-088]as an opened tomb. But the air was filled with grim, burning passion,

[045-089]which was to light and sound what light itself is to opaque colour.

[045-090]Nightspore pressed his hand to his heart. “I don’t know if I can endure

[045-091]it,” he said, looking toward Krag. He felt his person far more vividly

[045-092]and distinctly than if he had been able to see him.

[045-093]“Go in, and lose no time, Nightspore.... Time here is more precious than

[045-094]on earth. We can’t squander the minutes. There are terrible and tragic

[045-095]affairs to attend to, which won’t wait for us... Go in at once. Stop for

[045-096]nothing.”

[045-097]“Where shall I go to?” muttered Nightspore. “I have forgotten

[045-098]everything.”

[045-099]“Enter, enter! There is only one way. You can’t mistake it.”

[045-100]“Why do you bid me go in, if I am to come out again?”

[045-101]“To have your wounds healed.”

[045-102]Almost before the words had left his mouth, Krag sprang back on to the

[045-103]island raft. Nightspore involuntarily started after him, but at once

[045-104]recovered himself and remained standing where he was. Krag was

[045-105]completely invisible; everything outside was black night.

[045-106]The moment he had gone, a feeling shot up in Nightspore’s heart like a

[045-107]thousand trumpets.

[045-108]*****

[045-109]Straight in front of him, almost at his feet, was the lower end of a

[045-110]steep, narrow, circular flight of stone steps. There was no other way

[045-111]forward.

[045-112]He put his foot on the bottom stair, at the same time peering aloft. He

[045-113]saw nothing, yet as he proceeded upward every inch of the way was

[045-114]perceptible to his inner feelings. The staircase was cold, dismal, and

[045-115]deserted, but it seemed to him, in his exaltation of soul, like a ladder

[045-116]to heaven.

[045-117]After he had mounted a dozen steps or so, he paused to take breath. Each

[045-118]step was increasingly difficult to ascend; he felt as though he were

[045-119]carrying a heavy man on his shoulders. It struck a familiar chord in his

[045-120]mind. He went on and, ten stairs higher up, came to a window set in a

[045-121]high embrasure.

[045-122]On to this he clambered, and looked through. The window was of a sort of

[045-123]glass, but he could see nothing. Coming to him, however, from the world

[045-124]outside, a disturbance of the atmosphere struck his senses, causing his

[045-125]blood to run cold. At one moment it resembled a low, mocking, vulgar

[045-126]laugh, travelling from the ends of the earth; at the next it was like a

[045-127]rhythmical vibration of the air—the silent, continuous throbbing of some

[045-128]mighty engine. The two sensations were identical, yet different. They

[045-129]seemed to be related in the same manner as soul and body. After feeling

[045-130]them for a long time, Nightspore got down from the embrasure, and

[045-131]continued his ascent, having meanwhile grown very serious.

[045-132]The climbing became still more laborious, and he was forced to stop at

[045-133]every third or fourth step, to rest his muscles and regain breath. When

[045-134]he had mounted another twenty stairs in this way, he came to a second

[045-135]window. Again he saw nothing. The laughing disturbance of the air, too,

[045-136]had ceased; but the atmospheric throb was now twice as distinct as

[045-137]before, and its rhythm had become _double_. There were two separate

[045-138]pulses; one was in the time of a march, the other in the time of a

[045-139]waltz. The first was bitter and petrifying to feel, but the second was

[045-140]gay, enervating, and horrible.

[045-141]Nightspore spent little time at that window, for he felt that he was on

[045-142]the eve of a great discovery, and that something far more important

[045-143]awaited him higher up. He proceeded aloft. The ascent grew more and more

[045-144]exhausting, so much so that he had frequently to sit down, utterly

[045-145]crushed by his own dead weight. Still, he got to the third window.

[045-146]He climbed into the embrasure. His feelings translated themselves into

[045-147]vision, and he saw a sight that caused him to turn pale. A gigantic,

[045-148]self-luminous sphere was hanging in the sky, occupying nearly the whole

[045-149]of it. This sphere was composed entirely of two kinds of active beings.

[045-150]There were a myriad of tiny green corpuscles, varying in size from the

[045-151]very small to the almost indiscernible. They were not green, but he

[045-152]somehow saw them so. They were all striving in one direction—toward

[045-153]himself, toward Muspel, but were too feeble and miniature to make any

[045-154]headway. Their action produced the marching rhythm he had previously

[045-155]felt, but this rhythm was not intrinsic in the corpuscles themselves,

[045-156]but was a consequence of the obstruction they met with. And, surrounding

[045-157]these atoms of life and light, were far larger whirls of white light

[045-158]that gyrated hither and thither, carrying the green corpuscles with them

[045-159]wherever they desired. Their whirling motion was accompanied by the

[045-160]waltzing rhythm. It seemed to Nightspore that the green atoms were not

[045-161]only being danced about against their will but were suffering

[045-162]excruciating shame and degradation in consequence. The larger ones were

[045-163]steadier than the extremely small, a few were even almost stationary,

[045-164]and one was advancing in the direction it wished to go.

[045-165]He turned his back to the window, buried his face in his hands, and

[045-166]searched in the dim recesses of his memory for an explanation of what he

[045-167]had just seen. Nothing came straight, but horror and wrath began to take

[045-168]possession of him.

[045-169]On his way upward to the next window, invisible fingers seemed to him to

[045-170]be squeezing his heart and twisting it about here and there; but he

[045-171]never dreamed of turning back. His mood was so grim that he did not once

[045-172]permit himself to pause. Such was his physical distress by the time that

[045-173]he had clambered into the recess, that for several minutes he could see

[045-174]nothing at all—the world seemed to be spinning round him rapidly.

[045-175]When at last he looked, he saw the same sphere as before, but now all

[045-176]was changed on it. It was a world of rocks, minerals, water, plants,

[045-177]animals, and men. He saw the whole world at one view, yet everything was

[045-178]so magnified that he could distinguish the smallest details of life. In

[045-179]the interior of every individual, of every aggregate of individuals, of

[045-180]every chemical atom, he clearly perceived the presence of the green

[045-181]corpuscles. But, according to the degree of dignity of the life form,

[045-182]they were fragmentary or comparatively large. In the crystal, for

[045-183]example, the green, imprisoned life was so minute as to be scarcely

[045-184]visible; in some men it was hardly bigger; but in other men and women it

[045-185]was twenty or a hundred times greater. But, great or small, it played an

[045-186]important part in every individual. It appeared as if the whirls of

[045-187]white light, which were the individuals, and plainly showed themselves

[045-188]beneath the enveloping bodies, were delighted with existence and wished

[045-189]only to enjoy it, but the green corpuscles were in a condition of

[045-190]eternal discontent, yet, blind and not knowing which way to turn for

[045-191]liberation, kept changing form, as though breaking a new path, by way of

[045-192]experiment. Whenever the old grotesque became metamorphosed into the new

[045-193]grotesque, it was in every case the direct work of the green atoms,

[045-194]trying to escape toward Muspel, but encountering immediate opposition.

[045-195]These subdivided sparks of living, fiery spirit were hopelessly

[045-196]imprisoned in a ghastly mush of soft pleasure. They were being

[045-197]effeminated and corrupted—that is to say, absorbed in the foul, sickly

[045-198]enveloping forms.
