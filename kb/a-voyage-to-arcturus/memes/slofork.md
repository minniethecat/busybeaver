# Slofork

**Slofork** is a self-styled sorcerer whom [[maskull|Maskull]] encounters on the journey through the Sant Levels. Described by [[panawe|Panawe]] as a man who "walks over others" by force of will, Slofork challenges [[maskull|Maskull]] to a contest that dramatizes the tension between personal power and the natural order of things.

## Role

Slofork appears as an antagonist figure whose challenge to [[maskull|Maskull]] is not physical but philosophical: he claims the right to dominate others through the strength of his will. His encounter with [[maskull|Maskull]] serves as a trial that tests the protagonist's own developing sense of power and its proper limits. [[panawe|Panawe]]'s description of him as one who "walks over" others (010-175) frames him as a cautionary example of will untempered by wisdom.

## Appearances

Slofork is first mentioned by [[panawe|Panawe]] in Chapter 10, who points him out as a figure approaching in the distance: "It was Slofork, the so-called sorcerer. I had already been told about him" (010-175). [[panawe|Panawe]]'s tone in describing him is wary but not fearful — Slofork is a known quantity in this region, a man whose reputation precedes him.

The direct encounter occurs in Chapter 11. Slofork opens with an immediate challenge: "Which of us walks over the other?" (011-002), and his manner is described as "cold and masterful." [[maskull|Maskull]]'s terse response — "Nothing, Slofork" (011-015) — suggests a refusal to engage on Slofork's terms. Slofork's final line in the exchange is a pointed rejoinder: "Quite true. But those you had it from did not add..." (011-029), implying hidden knowledge that others have withheld from [[maskull|Maskull]].

## Philosophical Significance

Slofork embodies the Nietzschean will-to-power in its raw form — the claim that strength of personality justifies domination over others. His challenge "Which of us walks over the other?" is a stark reduction of social relations to a contest of wills. [[maskull|Maskull]]'s refusal to play this game marks an important moment in his philosophical development: he is beginning to sense that raw power is not the highest value.

Lindsay uses Slofork as a foil to [[panawe|Panawe]], who represents a different kind of mastery — mastery over oneself rather than over others. The sorcerer's claim to walk over others is the inverse of the self-sacrificing ethos that [[panawe|Panawe]] embodies and that [[maskull|Maskull]] is slowly learning to understand.

## Relationships

Slofork's primary relationship is adversarial: his challenge to [[maskull|Maskull]] tests the protagonist's emerging philosophical sensibility. He is known to [[panawe|Panawe]] by reputation, and [[panawe|Panawe]]'s description of him establishes the moral framework through which the reader views the encounter. Slofork has no apparent connection to the broader metaphysical conflict of the novel — he is a localized danger, a territorial power in the Sant Levels.
