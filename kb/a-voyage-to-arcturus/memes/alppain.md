# Alppain

## Definition

Alppain is the second sun of the planet Tormance, invisible to ordinary sight and perceptible only through the advanced sensory organs that grow on Maskull's body as his journey progresses. Unlike the white, physical light of [[Branchspell]], Alppain emits a blue radiance that operates on the spiritual plane — its light does not illuminate objects but rather reveals their inner nature. It is described as "beautiful, deadly, treacherous" [025-039], a transformative force that both attracts and threatens.

## In the Novel

Alppain is first named by Joiwind's people as the second sun [010-026], but Maskull cannot perceive it directly — "It's invisible here" [010-032]. The sun itself lies behind the mountains that conceal its direct rays; only its blue afterglow is visible in certain regions of Tormance.

As Maskull travels northward, Alppain's influence grows. Its blue glow appears over the northern sky [017-118, 017-179] and begins to affect his consciousness. By the later chapters, Alppain's light becomes a dominant force in Maskull's inner experience:

> [025-011] Alppain affected him like this, he thought — it was a spiritual, not a physical, sun.

The sun's light is strongest in the far north, where the novel's climactic revelations occur. As Maskull approaches the end of his journey, Alppain's blue glow increasingly replaces Branchspell's white light as the dominant celestial influence — mirroring his shift from material to spiritual perception.

## Philosophical Meaning

In Lindsay's allegorical system, Alppain represents the light of higher spiritual reality — the illumination that reveals the true nature of things beneath Shaping's pleasant surface. While Branchspell lights the world of forms and sensory pleasure, Alppain's blue radiance penetrates to the metaphysical essence.

The fact that Alppain is invisible to ordinary organs but perceptible through the new organs (such as the [[probes]]) underscores the novel's central theme: that perception itself must be transformed before truth can be apprehended. Maskull cannot see Alppain until his body has grown the necessary organs — just as the soul must develop new faculties to perceive spiritual reality.

The "beautiful, deadly, treacherous" quality attributed to Alppain [025-039] captures the double-edged nature of metaphysical truth in Lindsay's vision. To see things as they really are is both liberating and annihilating — it destroys the comfortable illusions of ordinary life while opening the way to genuine being.
