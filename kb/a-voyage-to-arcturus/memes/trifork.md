# Trifork

## Definition

The trifork is the symbolic emblem of the philosophical tradition founded by Hator and carried forward by [[Spadevil]] in the land of Sant. It is both a conceptual symbol and a physical object — a three-pronged implement carried by Spadevil as a visible token of his doctrine. The trifork's structure maps directly onto Hator's philosophical system: the stem represents hatred of pleasure, and each of the three forks represents a distinct stage of spiritual attainment [020-067, 021-062].

## In the Novel

The trifork appears when Spadevil enters the narrative. It is described as a physical object that Spadevil carries and wields — a weapon-like implement [022-109, 022-114]. When Spadevil's role as prophet of the new law is challenged, the question of succession crystallizes around the trifork:

> [022-129] "Has the trifork passed to you?" he demanded.

The transfer of the trifork from Spadevil to Maskull marks the passing of doctrinal authority and the continuation of the philosophical tradition. The physical trifork thus serves as a tangible symbol of an abstract philosophical lineage.

## Philosophical Meaning

The trifork's structure encodes Hator's complete philosophical system as explained by Spadevil:

> [021-062] The stem, Maskull, is hatred of pleasure. The first fork is disentanglement from the sweetness of the world. The second fork is power over those who still writhe in the nets of illusion. The third fork is the healthy glow of one who steps into ice-cold water.

This progression — from rejecting pleasure, through detachment and mastery, to the positive vigour of ascetic life — represents a complete spiritual path. The trifork condenses an entire philosophical system into a single symbolic object, functioning much like a mandala or icon in Eastern traditions: a visible form that encodes invisible truths.

Spadevil later modifies Hator's doctrine, replacing "hatred of pleasure" with "iron obedience to duty" [021-165]. Whether the trifork's symbolism shifts accordingly is left ambiguous — the symbol endures even as the doctrine it encodes evolves.
