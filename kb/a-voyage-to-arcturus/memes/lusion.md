# Lusion Plain

## Location

The Lusion Plain is a broad, open region of Tormance, first encountered by Maskull early in his journey. Its name, evocative of "illusion," foreshadows its role as a landscape where appearances and reality are tested.

## Description

The Lusion Plain is first mentioned by Joiwind as Maskull's next destination after leaving Poolingdred. It is characterized as an expansive, open landscape that serves as a transitional zone between settled regions and the more challenging terrain to come. The plain's name itself — with its phonetic echo of "illusion" — is thematically significant in a novel preoccupied with the distinction between perception and reality.

From the plain, Maskull can see the Ifdawn Mountains rising on the northern skyline, marking the boundary toward the next phase of his journey [008-086]. The plain is described in the chapter bearing its name: Chapter 8, "The Lusion Plain," where Maskull has one of his most significant encounters with the entity Shaping [018-021].

## Narrative Events

Maskull traverses the Lusion Plain after departing Poolingdred. Here, he encounters the entity Shaping in a dreamlike vision, where the nature of reality and illusion is tested [018-001]. The experience is pivotal — Shaping appears "stripped of illusion," offering Maskull a glimpse of the metaphysical forces operating beneath the surface of Tormance [027-072].

Later, a character recounts seeing Maskull "two days ago, in the Lusion Plain," confirming that the events there were witnessed by others and not merely Maskull's subjective experience [028-063].

## Symbolic Significance

The Lusion Plain is the novel's most explicit spatial metaphor for the theme of illusion versus reality. Lindsay uses the landscape as a stage for Maskull's first direct encounter with Shaping, the demiurgic force that governs Tormance. The plain's very name collapses the distinction between geography and philosophy — it is a "plain of illusions" where Maskull must learn to distinguish the real from the apparent.

This theme reverberates throughout the novel: "Feelings which flourish on illusions, and sicken and die on realities" [028-090], and ultimately, "You think you are thoroughly disillusioned, don't you? Well, that may prove to be the last and strongest illusion of all" [043-081].
