## Summary

Spring planting is the foundational agricultural event in the novel's seasonal cycle — the period when the family sows the fields that will sustain them through the year. It demands the labor of both the man and the mother, pulling her from the kitchen into the fields alongside her husband.

## In the Narrative

The novel references planting as part of the rhythm that structures peasant life. The mother works in the fields "until the last row" (001-005), planting beans beside her husband. The man's labor is described with tactile precision — "pressing the earth tight over the rows with his bare feet" (001-005). The work is hard, repetitive, and essential.

Planting is also inextricable from the household's reproductive cycle. The mother's pregnancy and nursing schedule is measured against agricultural labor: "when her first son had been born, she could not bear to hear him cry at all" (001-010), and the man complained that her nursing interrupted the work. This convergence of agricultural and human fertility — planting seeds in the earth, bearing children to work the land — is one of the novel's deepest patterns.

## Key Participants

- **[[the-son|The Son]]**: The primary field laborer, responsible for the heavy work of turning soil and planting rows
- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]**: Works alongside her husband in addition to her domestic duties, embodying the double burden of peasant women

## Cultural Context

Spring planting represents the beginning of the agricultural year, a time of hope and anxiety. The family's survival depends on the success of the planting; a poor planting means a hungry year. This reality underlies all the domestic dramas — the quarrels over money, the careful rationing of fuel, the attention to children's health — grounding them in the material facts of subsistence farming.

## Significance

Spring planting in the novel is both literal and metaphorical. It is the labor that sustains life, the cycle that governs time, and the bond that ties the family to the land. The mother's participation in field labor — her movement between kitchen and field — marks her as a complete agricultural being: producer as well as reproducer, worker as well as nurturer.
