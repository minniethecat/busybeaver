## Summary

The city visit is a recurring event in *The Mother* that marks the mother's movement between two worlds: the enclosed rural hamlet and the wider urban space of commerce, strangers, and possibility. These journeys — to sell grain and rice straw, to exchange currency, to purchase goods — are both practical economic necessity and the setting for her secret life. It is in the city that the landlord's agent pursues her beyond the hamlet's watchful eyes, where she buys trinkets she can never wear, and where she hires letter writers to sustain the fiction of her husband's continued existence.

## The Journey Outward

The city lies "behind a curve of the hill upon the river's edge" (176-176), accessible on foot or by animal. The mother goes there to sell what the land produces beyond what the family needs: "rice straw was too good to burn. Only city folk burned rice straw, and she or her man would carry it into the city in great bundles upon a pole and gain good silver for it" (017-017). These journeys are woven into the fabric of seasonal life, as regular as the harvests themselves:

> And there were days when she went into the town with some grain or cabbage to sell, she and the man together, and there in the town were strange sights to see and think about if ever she had time to think at all. (118-118)

## The City as Alternative World

The city represents everything the hamlet is not. The travelers at the wayside inn describe it in almost magical terms:

> Strange sights there were in that city, streets as smooth as threshing-floors and carts of every sort, houses tall as mountains and shops with windows filled with merchandise of all the world that ships brought there from over seas... Good food and plenty was there, too, sea fish and sea meats, and after he had eaten a man might enter into a great playhouse where there was every sort of play and picture. (232-232)

It is this vision of the city that seduces the husband away — the promise of "money easy come by with very little work for it and gaming houses everywhere" (232-232).

## The City as Site of Secrecy

After the husband's departure, the mother's city visits take on a new dimension. She goes not only to sell goods but to sustain her fiction:

> She carried all the rice she could spare into the city and the straw she had to spare and she sold it. When she had the silver in her hand she asked for a paper bit that is as good as silver, and with it she went to a letter writer, a strange man in that town she did not know, and he sat in his little booth beside the Confucian temple. (519-519)

There, she dictates a letter as if from her husband, enclosing the paper money to prove to the hamlet that he still works and sends wages home. Each city visit enables the lie that protects her.

## The Silversmith's Counter

It is during a city visit that the agent's pursuit crosses from the hamlet into the wider world. After exchanging her paper money, the mother turns to find him at the door:

> On a day soon after this she went into the city to change again her bit of paper... and when she had the ten pieces in her hand she turned to go and there a man stood by the door upon the street, and he stood smiling and smoothing his upper lip, and it was the landlord's agent. (743-743)

He follows her through the streets. At the silversmith's counter, while she pretends to examine brass pins, she toys with silver earrings:

> Suddenly the agent came up while she toyed and he pretended he did not know her and he said to the silversmith, "How much are these earrings?" (755-755)

He buys them — along with rings — in a transaction that the mother watches with a mixture of jealousy and longing: "such things as she would have loved and in her girlhood had often longed to have" (765-765). The trinkets become the material tokens of their secret, objects she can never wear publicly.

## The Two Worlds

The city visit dramatizes the split in the mother's life. In the hamlet, she is the abandoned wife sustained by a fiction of letters and silver. In the city, she is a woman whom a man pursues with gifts she has always desired. The city enables her secret self — but it is also where she must continually renew the lie that makes that secret self possible. The journey between hamlet and city becomes the geographical expression of her divided heart.
