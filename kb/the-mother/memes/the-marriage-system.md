## Summary

The marriage system in the novel is the social institution that governs the formation of new families — through matchmaking, bride price negotiation, the red bridal chair ritual, and the wedding ceremony itself. It structures generational transition, female economic exchange, and the transfer of domestic authority from one woman to another.

## Structure

Marriage in the hamlet operates through customary mechanisms rather than formal law. The go-between or matchmaker initiates the process, negotiating between families. The bride price is paid by the groom's family, and the bride brings her domestic skills — sewing, cooking, obedience — as her contribution. The wedding ceremony centers on the red bridal chair that transports the bride from her natal home to her husband's house.

The exchange of wedding fruits is part of the ritual economy: "the small wedding fruits, the cock she had sent to the bride's house and that according to custom they sent back and with it a hen they mated to it" (014-034). This symbolic pairing — cock and hen — enacts the union at the level of the household's material life.

## The Wedding Ritual

The son's wedding (Ch XIV) shows the full ritual apparatus. The mother "put forth more silver and hired the red marriage chair and the bead crown and the earrings of false pearls and all that was needful for the day, and especially the trousers of red which every bride must wear in those parts" (014-031). The bride's arrival is a performance of reluctance: "the chair was fetched and set down there before the door and the cousin's wife and the gossip and the other elder women of the hamlet took the bride's hand and tried to pull her forth. And she was proper and reluctant and came at last but most unwillingly" (014-034).

Following custom, the mother-in-law withdraws during the ceremony: "the mother withdrew into her cousin's house, as was the custom too in those parts where it was said a son's wife must not see too easily her husband's mother, lest she do not fear her thereafter" (014-034). This custom encodes the transfer of domestic authority — the new wife must learn to fear and respect her mother-in-law, preserving hierarchy within the household.

## Generational Transition

Marriage marks a generational handover. When the son's wife arrives, the mother surrenders the marriage bed: "It was not easy either for the mother to give up to this new pair that place which had been hers and her man's, and it made her seem old to herself at night to lie on the old woman's pallet" (014-039). She reflects that this is a recurring pattern: "Now I suppose that old soul who was mother when I came to this house felt as I do now, when I came a bride and pushed her from her bed and lay there with her son in my turn" (014-039).

## Dowry and Disability

The marriage system's treatment of the blind daughter reveals its logic of economic exchange. Because of her disability, she cannot command a bride price: "the maid was given free and there were no gifts given because she was not good bargain for the man she was to wed" (015-033). Nevertheless the mother sews her red wedding trousers, "as even the poorest maid must have" (015-033) — the minimum ritual requirement that marks every woman's passage into marriage, regardless of her economic value.

## Role in the Story

The marriage system drives key narrative transitions: the son's wedding displaces the mother from her bed and authority; the blind daughter's betrothal becomes a source of household conflict and ultimately tragedy; the mother's own remembered wedding day echoes through her experience of these events. Marriage is the mechanism by which the family reproduces itself across generations — and the site where the costs of that reproduction become visible.

## Significance

The marriage system embodies the tension between individual desire and social obligation that runs through the novel. The mother's son mocks her preoccupation with weddings: "Old women like you, mother, think of nothing but weddings and births of children" (019-012). But for the mother, marriage is not sentiment — it is the infrastructure of survival, the means by which a woman secures her place in a world that offers no security outside the family.
