# Solitude and Isolation

**Solitude** is the fundamental condition of the mother's existence in Pearl S. Buck's *The Mother*. She is surrounded by children, an old woman, and the constant demands of a peasant household, yet she is profoundly alone—emotionally, sexually, and existentially. Buck renders this isolation not as a single dramatic event but as the steady, accumulating texture of daily life, visible in the mother's silent labor, her solitary nights, and the inner world that no other character can access.

## The Architecture of Daily Solitude

The mother's solitude is built into the structure of her days. While the old grandmother talks incessantly, the mother works in silence, her consciousness unreachable:

> The old woman chattered on. She was alone all day except for the little children since her son and son's wife must labor on the land, and now it seemed there were many things she had to tell this daughter-in-law whom she loved.
>
> (001-006)

The irony is deliberate: the old woman describes herself as "alone," yet it is the mother—the listener, the silent worker—who is truly isolated. The grandmother's speech fills the air but asks for no response; it is a monologue that deepens rather than relieves the mother's solitude.

When the man abandons the family, the mother faces the harvest alone:

> Then did the mother set herself steadfastly to her life. The seven days were long past and the man did not come and the rice ripened through the days and hung heavy and yellow and ready for the harvest and he did not come. The woman reaped it alone then except for two days when the cousin came and helped her.
>
> (005-062)

The repetition—"he did not come … he did not come"—mimics the rhythm of solitary labor, each day identical to the last, the waiting stretched across seasons.

## Solitude at Night

Night is when the mother's solitude becomes most acute. The darkness strips away the busyness that fills her days and leaves only the empty space beside her:

> But in the dark night, in the still, hot night, in the silence of the closed room, her anger went out of her and she was afraid. … The bed was enormous, empty.
>
> (005-041)

The physical detail—the bed that is "enormous, empty"—grounds emotional solitude in bodily experience. She has lain against this man for six years; his absence is not an abstraction but a tactile deprivation.

The mother's nighttime solitude is also the medium of her tenderness:

> There she lay stretched and still, her strong body full of healthy weariness. Lying like this in the darkness she was filled with tenderness … tender to the children as they lay helpless in sleep, tender to the old woman if she coughed in the night.
>
> (001-001)

Her solitude at night is generative: it is the space in which her love operates, wordless and invisible to all but the reader.

## Barrenness and Loneliness

The mother's isolation takes on a specifically female dimension through her experience of barrenness. After years of near-annual childbirth, the spring when her body does not conceive becomes a crisis of solitude:

> Nearly every spring she had given birth, nearly every spring since she was wed, but this spring was her body barren. … her loneliness came over her like a pain and her breasts ached when she thought of the thing, and it was this, that she would never bear a child again in such a spring unless her man came home.
>
> (007-010)

The barrenness is not merely biological but relational: without her husband, the fertility that defined her womanhood ceases. Her body, like her bed, registers absence.

## The Solitary Self

Buck suggests that the mother's deepest solitude is constitutional—a quality she has carried since youth:

> Now from her youth up this woman had been ever a creature of deep still heats. She was not as some women are, quick to look at this young man and that … No, she was a woman of a very deep heart, shy to the depths of it, and until she was properly wed even when she was alone her thoughts had not turned to men for their own sake.
>
> (008-001)

The phrase "deep still heats" captures the paradox of the mother's solitude: she is not cold or indifferent but possesses a hidden intensity that no one sees. Her isolation is not emptiness but fullness that has no outlet.

This constitutional solitude reaches its culmination in her middle age:

> Now with her body ripe and knowing all and herself in all her prime of womanhood she was left, woman alone, and every day the children grew up taller and every day they grew further from their babyhood they seemed less her own.
>
> (008-012)

The children's growth is a second abandonment: as they become independent, the mother is once again "woman alone." The solitude that began with the man's departure extends into every relationship; she is the fixed center around which others move and from which they eventually depart.
