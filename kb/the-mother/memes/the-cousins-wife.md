## Summary

The cousin's wife is the most vividly drawn secondary female character in the early chapters — a bustling, laughing, talkative woman whose round brown face and upturned nostrils are as memorable as her generous, practical kindness. She lives across the street from the mother's house, married to [[the-cousin]], the silent basket-weaver. Their complementary marriage — the noisy woman and the quiet man — is one of the novel's quiet affirmations of how village life works. She serves as midwife at the mother's fourth childbirth, runs household errands between families, and speaks with a frank, earthy warmth that cuts through the novel's more somber passages.

## Role

The cousin's wife serves as a **bridge figure** between the mother's private domestic world and the wider hamlet community. She is the one who praises the mother publicly — "Truly a very able, forward woman!" (001-059) — and privately. She brings soup after childbirth, watches for the mother's return from the fields, and shares gossip that keeps the mother connected to village life even when the mother herself is too busy to participate.

Her most critical narrative function is as **midwife** during the mother's fourth childbirth. She catches the baby "nimbly" in her two hands, washes and wraps the newborn, and stays until the mother wakes. This scene establishes the informal support network that sustains peasant women in the absence of professional medical care.

## Character Traits

- **Talkative and cheerful**: She talks "the livelong day beside her silent man" (001-139) — her voice fills the space her husband's silence leaves.
- **Physically vivid**: Her face is "round and brown and her nostrils black and upturned above a big red mouth" (001-139) — a description that emphasizes warmth, health, and earthy vitality.
- **Bustling and practical**: She comes "running, wiping her hands on her apron" (001-139), always in motion, always mid-task.
- **Frank and opinionated**: She tells the mother bluntly that the old grandmother would be better off dead ("A very good thing it will be for you, goodwife, when that old thing is dead" [001-120]), a comment whose harshness is softened by her evident goodwill.
- **Emotionally generous**: She brings soup unprompted after the birth, laughs at the elder son's cleverness, and offers a reciprocal model of female friendship: "Do you not the same for me in my hour?" (001-165).

## Relationships

She is married to [[the-cousin]], a marriage of opposites that functions harmoniously. She talks; he works. She laughs; he is silent. Together they form a household that serves as a counterpoint to the mother's more volatile marriage.

Her relationship with [[the-mother]] is the novel's most developed female friendship. They call each other "sister" (001-163), help each other through childbirth, and maintain an easy, practical reciprocity. When the mother needs help, the cousin's wife comes. When the cousin's wife needs help, the mother comes. There is no sentimentality in this — it is simply how the hamlet functions.

With [[the-husband]], she is pragmatic and slightly dismissive, noting his gambling and his failures as a provider through village gossip. With [[the-elder-son]], she is stern but not unkind — she gives him a hard look when he intrudes upon the birthing room but does not chase him away.

## Key Passages

> "This cousin's wife was a kindly, good woman, her face round and brown and her nostrils black and upturned above a big red mouth. She was a noisy, busy soul, talking the livelong day beside her silent man." (001-139)

> "Well, goodwife, I do ever say how good a thing it is that we do not come together. I have been watching you and wondering which would come first, you or I." (001-139)

> "Then did the cousin's wife run forward and she caught nimbly in her two hands that little child for whom they waited, and it was a son." (001-157)

> "The mother drank the soup then gratefully, and she murmured to her cousin's wife, 'It is your good heart, my sister.' But the cousin's wife said, 'Do you not the same for me in my hour?'" (001-163–165)

## In the Narrative

The cousin's wife appears primarily in the domestic sequences of Chapter I — calling across the street, delivering the baby, bringing soup, gossiping about other villagers. Her absence from later chapters (as the narrative narrows its focus to the mother's solitary struggle) is itself significant: the community network of women that sustained the mother through childbirth cannot sustain her through the deeper crisis of abandonment and poverty that follows.

## Significance

In a novel centered on the mother's solitary strength, the cousin's wife represents an alternative model of female resilience — one grounded in community rather than individual fortitude. She is not the protagonist, but she is the kind of woman who makes the protagonist's survival possible. Her question — "Do you not the same for me?" — articulates the novel's understated ethic of mutual aid, an ethic that the mother herself embodies even as the narrative isolates her.
