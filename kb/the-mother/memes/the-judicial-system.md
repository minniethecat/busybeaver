## Summary

The judicial system in the novel is the distant but lethal apparatus of state power — the magistrate's court, the city gaol, and the execution ground — that intrudes into peasant life most catastrophically when the mother's younger son is arrested as a communist and beheaded. It represents an external world of authority that the hamlet's inhabitants cannot meaningfully access or influence except through bribery.

## Structure

The judicial system operates through a hierarchy: the magistrate who presides over the court, the soldiers who guard the city gate and patrol for "communists, those new robbers" (009-055), the gaolers who hold prisoners and accept bribes, and the execution ground where sentences are carried out. None of these figures are individualized — they appear as functions of a system that exists outside the hamlet's social world.

The system is geographically concentrated in the city. The gaol is "near the south gate where the beheading ground is" (019-072). This spatial concentration — court, prison, and execution ground all within the city walls — makes state violence a distinctly urban phenomenon, distant from the rural hamlet where most of the novel's life unfolds.

## Inaccessibility to the Poor

The judicial system is formally universal but practically inaccessible to those without money. When the blind daughter is lost, the mother laments: "It is true there is no use in going to law since we have no money to pay our way in to justice" (017-004). Justice is not a right but a commodity — something one must "pay [one's] way in to." This understanding is so deeply internalized that the mother does not even consider appealing to the law; she knows in advance that poverty excludes her from its protections.

## The Gaol and the Communist

The system's most devastating intervention comes when the younger son is arrested as a communist. The elder son reports: "he saw my brother this morning among many others, and his hands were tied behind him with hempen ropes and his clothes were rags and he was marching past the marketplace … the guards who walked along said they were communists sent to gaol to be killed tomorrow" (019-058). The guard explains casually: "it was a new sort of robber they had nowadays" (019-060).

The mother's instinctive response is to try bribery — the only interface with the state that her experience has taught her: "Whoever heard of guards in a gaol who are not to be bribed? But I will go and fetch that money that I have bought for this new child's birth" (019-093). Bribery is not corruption from her perspective — it is the normal operating procedure of a system that provides no other point of entry for the poor.

## The Execution

The beheading is a public spectacle: "it had been noised about the countryside that there would be this great beheading and many came to see it for a strange sight" (019-116). The mother waits at the gaol gate at dawn. When the prisoners are marched out, she tries to reach her son but is thrown aside: "a guard ran out and picked the mother up and threw her to one side upon the road and there she lay in the dust" (019-124). The image — an old woman thrown into the dust by an anonymous guard — crystallizes the relationship between the peasant and the state: absolute vulnerability meeting impersonal force.

## Role in the Story

The judicial system appears in the novel only at moments of crisis. It has no presence in the hamlet's daily life — no police, no court, no visible authority. But its sudden intrusion in the final chapters transforms the novel's emotional register. The mother's grief at her son's execution is different from every loss that came before: this was not poverty, not illness, not abandonment, but a killing authorized by an institution that claims the monopoly on legitimate violence.

## Significance

The judicial system embodies the novel's political unconscious. Pearl S. Buck never names the regime — there is no mention of dynasty, republic, or party — but the apparatus of magistrates, soldiers, gaols, and public beheadings is unmistakably that of the late imperial or early republican Chinese state. The system's treatment of the younger son — execution without trial, on the mere label of "communist" — foreshadows the political violence that would define China's twentieth century. But the novel keeps this in the background, filtering it through the mother's bewildered grief rather than political analysis.
