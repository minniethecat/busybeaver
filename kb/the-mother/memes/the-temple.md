# The Temple

The **temple** in *The Mother* is a distant sanctuary ten miles from the hamlet, housing the "living goddess" reputed to grant prayers for fertility and healing. It is the destination of the mother's most desperate pilgrimage—a journey undertaken not to the familiar roadside shrine whose gods she once covered with a garment, but to a greater, more potent sacred space.

## Description

The temple is "great and fine," its walls "painted rosy red and the gods gilded" (012-027), with many people coming and going for worship. At its entrance, incense is sold, and gray-robed priests direct petitioners. Inside, the "living goddess" sits not in a prominent position but in a dark corner—"a small old dingy goddess" seated between two lesser attendant figures (012-027–028). This contrast between the temple's grandeur and the goddess's humble placement mirrors the mother's own social position: she is directed there by a priest who "supposing her from her common looks to be but one of those many women who came each day to ask for sons" (012-028), dismisses her as ordinary.

## Role in the Story

The temple serves as the setting for the novel's central spiritual crisis. The mother journeys there to pray for her daughter's blind eyes to be healed, recalling that the same goddess "gave [[the-son|the son]] to Li the Sixth's wife when she had gone barren all her life long" (012-025). However, the mother's prayer is undercut by her guilty conscience: having committed what she believes to be an unatoned sin, she feels the goddess "looked down harshly" with a "smooth golden face" that "stared down fixed and unmoved by the sinful soul who prayed" (012-030). The temple thus becomes not a place of healing but of confrontation with the mother's unabsolved guilt. She returns home uncertain, telling her children, "How do I know what heaven wills? I could but say my prayer and it must be as heaven wills" (012-032).

## Associated Characters

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]** — makes the ten-mile pilgrimage to pray for her daughter's sight
- **[[the-daughter|The Daughter]]** — the blind girl whose condition prompts the journey; she has "no great faith in the goddess" (014-002)
- **The old woman** — a fellow petitioner who prays for her paralyzed son, voicing the temple's logic of sin and atonement: "If there be a sin in our house... I will atone—I will atone!" (012-029)

## Significance

The temple embodies the novel's treatment of folk religion as a site of unresolved anxiety rather than comfort. The mother never returns to pray there, fearing the goddess might remember her sin and "reach out and smite" her grandson (018-013). The temple, for all its painted walls and gilded gods, offers no absolution—only the mirror of the petitioner's own guilt. In the novel's moral economy, suffering itself becomes the only offering: "Does not sorrow atone?" the mother asks, but the temple provides no answer (018-016).
