# Anger and Suppressed Rage

**Anger** runs through *The Mother* as a subterranean current—rarely expressed directly, more often suppressed, redirected, or transformed into silence. Pearl S. Buck depicts the mother's anger not as a moral failing but as a structural condition of peasant womanhood: the constant pressure of poverty, abandonment, and powerlessness produces rage that has no legitimate outlet. The novel traces anger through its many forms—quarrel, sullen silence, redirected fury at children, and the strange intimacy of anger held close.

## The Economy of Quarrel

In the early chapters, anger between the mother and her husband follows a domestic rhythm. Quarrel is not rupture but release—a valve for pressures that have no other exit:

> Once or twice in a season a quarrel would go deep with her and give an unused bitterness to her tongue. When the man bought some foolish trinket in the market with the silver he had taken for the cabbage there, or when he was drunken on a day that was not a holiday, then she would grow angry and well-nigh she forgot she loved him in her heart.
>
> (004-010)

The phrase "well-nigh she forgot she loved him" is crucial: anger temporarily eclipses love but does not extinguish it. For the mother, anger and love coexist in the same emotional space, neither canceling the other.

The husband's anger, by contrast, is performative and ultimately weaker:

> On such days when all these thoughts pressed on him he was very surly … and when her quick temper rose against him it was a strange malicious pleasure to him to fall to loud quarreling with her, and it eased him somehow, although she had often the best of him, too, because her temper was more stout than his.
>
> (004-005)

The mother's "more stout" temper is a form of strength. Her anger lasts longer, roots deeper, and proves more durable than his—a pattern that will define her survival after he leaves.

## Anger as Intimacy

After the husband's abandonment, the mother's anger takes on a paradoxical function: it keeps him present. Fury becomes a form of connection:

> And she was somehow glad she could be angry at him, and she held her anger, clinging to it, because it made him seem more near, and she clung to it while she led in the beast and barred the door against the night.
>
> (005-040)

The verb "clinging" transforms anger from an emotion of rejection into one of attachment. As long as she can be angry at him, he is not truly gone. Anger fills the empty space in the bed, gives shape to the absence.

But anger cannot sustain itself through the night:

> But in the dark night, in the still, hot night, in the silence of the closed room, her anger went out of her and she was afraid. … Suddenly there fell upon her the hottest longing for that man of hers.
>
> (005-041)

Anger is a daytime emotion—it requires the busyness of labor, the distraction of tasks. At night, in solitude, it dissipates and leaves only longing. The cycle of anger-by-day and longing-by-night structures the mother's emotional life through the long months of waiting.

## Anger Turned Inward

When the mother cannot direct her anger at its proper object—the absent husband—it turns against those nearest to her:

> Times there were when this deep anger lasted over days, so that she was sullen and short with the children and with the grandmother and pushed the dog away roughly with her hoe, although she grieved her own heart the more when she had done it.
>
> (009-007)

The self-awareness here—"she grieved her own heart the more when she had done it"—is characteristic of Buck's portrayal. The mother's anger is never simple; it is always accompanied by guilt, by the knowledge that her rage is misdirected. This recursive quality—anger at her situation, followed by anger at her own anger—traps her in an emotional loop.

## The Anger of Lies

The mother constructs an elaborate fiction about her husband's absence to protect her dignity. But this necessary deception generates its own anger:

> But there were times when her lies made her angry at him and she told herself the man was not worth it and if he had come home at one such time as this she would have burst her anger full upon him and cursed him soundly while she loved him because he made her suffer so.
>
> (009-007)

The phrase "cursed him soundly while she loved him" captures the central paradox of the mother's anger: it is always entangled with love. She curses him *because* she loves him, and her anger is measured by the depth of that love. A woman who did not love would not need to be so angry.
