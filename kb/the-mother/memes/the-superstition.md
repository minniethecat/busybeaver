## Summary

Folk belief and superstition permeate the world of the novel, from the red letters of luck pasted on farm tools to the burial customs that shape the grandmother's relationship with her grave clothes. The characters inhabit a universe where fate, luck, gods, and omens are real forces requiring constant negotiation through ritual observance, propitiation, and the reading of signs.

## In the Narrative

The household god of wealth — "a merry old man he was made to be, his robes all red and blue and yellow" (178) — hangs on the earthen wall, ignored and cursed by the husband for failing to bring riches. This irreverent relationship with the gods is characteristic of the novel's pragmatic spirituality: gods who deliver receive offerings; gods who fail receive curses.

Burial customs are a central expression of folk belief. The grandmother wears her red burial coat under her blue daily coat, "as it was the custom to do in these parts" (122), and when it wears out, she frets until the mother makes a new one. The grandmother's cheerful relationship with her own grave clothes — she calls out "A-wearing them out, I be!" (122) — reflects a culture where death is not hidden but ritually prepared for and, in a sense, lived with daily.

The red letters of luck pasted on tools and farm implements at New Year (503) represent the agricultural dimension of folk belief: the conviction that ritual acts can influence material outcomes. The mother burns incense "to pray for him" (517), seeking divine intervention in her husband's return.

Fate and destiny are invoked as explanatory frameworks. The gossip speaks of the mother's "very lucky destiny" (562) in receiving paper money, while the husband is described as a man "made for losing" (155) at gambling — a fixed existential condition rather than a correctable behavior pattern.

## Key Participants

- **the-mother** — practices domestic rituals while maintaining a pragmatic, sometimes irreverent relationship with the gods
- **the-old-woman** — embodies the burial customs and death-preparation rituals of peasant culture
- **the-husband** — curses the household god for failing to deliver wealth

## Cultural Context

Folk belief in the novel is not a separate "religious" domain but woven into every aspect of daily life: agriculture (luck letters on tools), domestic economy (the god of wealth), lifecycle (burial robes), and crisis (temple pilgrimage for healing). It is a comprehensive worldview in which material and spiritual causation are inseparable.

## Significance

Superstition and folk belief provide the characters with a language for understanding forces beyond their control — poverty, illness, gambling losses, abandonment. The mother's flexible, pragmatic approach to supernatural power mirrors her flexible, pragmatic approach to survival itself: use what works, discard what does not, and never let theological consistency stand in the way of practical need.
