## Summary

The departure of the mother's husband is the pivotal event that defines the narrative arc of *The Mother*. After years of restless discontent with the unending cycle of peasant labor, the husband leaves the hamlet to seek work in a distant city — and never returns. His departure transforms the mother from a wife embedded in a traditional household into a woman who must farm the rented land alone, manage the family's public reputation, and construct an elaborate fiction of her husband's continued existence.

## The Husband's Restlessness

The husband, twenty-eight years old and "well formed and not tall, but strong and slight and full of grace," chafed against the boundaries of peasant life. The novel describes his existential dread:

> Sometimes when the man came home from a holiday in town... it fell upon him like a terror that so long as he lived there was naught for him but this, to rise in the morning and go to this land of which they owned but little and rented from a landlord who lived in pleasure in some far city. (180-180)

Travelers passing through the wayside inn told him of a "vast city full of people of many hues of skin" where "money was easy come by with very little work for it and gaming houses everywhere" (232-232). These tales fed his discontent, and he was told his gambling skill "could play in any city pleasure house" (234-234).

## The Day of Departure

The departure itself is not witnessed directly in the narrative. The mother only gradually realizes he is gone. Having quarreled with her over the purchase of a blue grass-cloth robe with her three silver coins — her most precious possession, brought from her childhood home — the husband had been sullen and distant:

> But from that day on the woman harbored this hour against the man... and because she was hard with him in these small ways the man was sullen and he did not sing and as soon as he had eaten he went away to the wayside inn. (304-304)

When he failed to return for his evening meal, the mother first assumed "he has gone into the town to game or for something or other" (345-345). The cousin had seen him "gay in his new blue robe and set for town" (349-349). But as days passed without his return, anxiety grew.

## The Reckoning of Abandonment

As the seven days passed when his money should have run out, the mother confronted the possibility of abandonment:

> That night she laid herself down most sorrowful and afraid upon her bed and this night she said openly to her own heart that none knew if he would ever come back again. (413-413)

The rice ripened and "hung heavy and yellow and ready for the harvest and he did not come" (445-445). She reaped it alone, with only the cousin's help for two days. When the time came to divide the grain with the landlord, the cousin warned her that "the new agent is a wily, clever man, and of a sort ill for a woman to do with alone" (445-445).

## The Fiction of His Continued Life

To protect herself from the hamlet's gossip, the mother invented a story: her husband had "a friend in a far city" who found him work "and the wage is good" (417-417). She even hired a letter writer in the city to forge a letter from him, enclosing paper money, so the hamlet would believe he was still working and sending wages home:

> She carried all the rice she could spare into the city and the straw she had to spare and she sold it. When she had the silver in her hand she asked for a paper bit that is as good as silver, and with it she went to a letter writer. (519-519)

This fiction sustained her for years, even as her secret hope faded. She made him new garments for the new year festival, telling everyone he would return — yet he never did. The departure became the hollow center of every festival, every harvest, and every season of waiting.

## Significance

The departure is the novel's foundational event. It sets in motion the mother's transformation from a wife defined by her relationship to a man into an independent agent who must navigate the landlord's demands, the hamlet's gossip, and her own complex desires. The absence of the husband creates the space in which all subsequent events — the rent collections, the city visits, the entanglement with the landlord's agent — unfold. The "clear and fine a blue" robe becomes the novel's recurring symbol of absence and waiting: "That blue robe of his would show a long way off, if he had a mind to turn to home again, so clear and fine a blue it was!" (679-679).
