# The Pond

The **pond** in *The Mother* is the hamlet's shared water source—a body of water at the settlement's edge where women gather to wash rice, scrub clothes, and collect shrimp. It is a site of female domestic labor conducted in common, where the rhythms of daily subsistence intersect with the hamlet's social life.

## Description

The pond sits within walking distance of the farmhouses, reached by a narrow path. It has a bank where women step "carefully down" (005-028) to dip their baskets into the water. The pond's edge is green, and it serves multiple purposes: washing rice until the grain "shone clean and white as wet pearls" (005-029), laundering clothes, catching shrimp for special dishes, and irrigating the rice seed beds via bucket and ditch. There is also "a further pond" somewhat higher than the seed beds (008-063), used for irrigation, distinct from the washing pond.

## Role in the Story

The pond appears as a constant presence in the domestic economy. The cousin's wife is introduced washing clothes "at the pond's edge" (003-023) when summoned for the mother's childbirth. The mother goes to "wash the noon's rice at the pond" (008-019), and teaches her young daughter the same task, though she makes her do it before leaving for the fields "lest the child with her half-seeing eyes fall into the pool and drown" (008-019). The pond is repeatedly invoked as a danger: the old grandmother's one remaining use is to "watch the door" and "see that the little ones do not fall into the pond" (001-013). The mother warns her youngest: "Watch the little one and see he does not fall in the pond, for he is the boldest, wilfulest of you all" (008-022).

The pond is also the setting for the agent's approach to the mother during the irrigation scene. She is "upon the green edge of the pond to rest" (008-068) when she sees him coming across the fields—a moment that rekindles the relationship leading to her pregnancy. In a rare moment of tenderness, the mother catches "some shrimps from the pond" (004-009) to make a special dish to reconcile with her husband.

## Significance

The pond is a threshold between the domestic interior and the agricultural exterior—a place of women's shared labor where the private work of feeding and cleaning becomes visible to the community. It is simultaneously a site of sustenance (water, shrimp), danger (drowning), and encounter (the agent's approach). The repeated warning about children falling into the pond creates a low thrum of anxiety beneath the daily routine, while the image of women bent over the water's edge—washing, scrubbing, dipping—constitutes one of the novel's most persistent visual motifs of female labor.
