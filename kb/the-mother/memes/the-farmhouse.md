## Summary

The farmhouse is the primary setting of the novel — a small, thatched dwelling that houses three generations of the family. It is simultaneously kitchen, bedroom, and gathering place, its cramped quarters containing the entirety of the family's domestic life.

## Description

The farmhouse is introduced in the novel's first sentence: "the small thatched farmhouse" (001-001). Its kitchen is dominated by an earthen stove into which the mother feeds grass "bit by bit" to maintain the fire (001-005). The main room where the family eats and the old woman sleeps contains a table and a pallet; [[the-son|the man]] and wife share a bed, "the blue quilt upon the bed newly covered" from their wedding (001-178). On the earthen wall hangs a paper god of wealth, "a merry old man... his robes all red and blue and yellow" who "had never brought wealth to this house" (001-178). The house is modest to the point of bareness — when [[the-son|the man]] buys a brass ring, the mother's outrage is partly about the house's lack of adornment: "whoever heard of a good and honest poor man who wore a ring upon his finger?" (001-194).

## Role in the Story

Virtually all domestic scenes take place in or immediately outside the farmhouse. The mother cooks in the kitchen, the family eats in the main room or "upon her low stool in the dooryard" when the weather permits (001-069). The old woman spends her days "in the kitchen corner," wrapped in a padded coat, watching the door (001-005). The children sleep, play, and cry within its walls; [[the-son|the man]] and wife quarrel and reconcile in its confines.

The farmhouse is the anchor of the family's identity — when [[the-son|the man]] leaves and the mother fears for the household's survival, it is the house that must be held together, the house that represents continuity across generations. It is the same house [[the-son|the man]] was born in, where his wedding was celebrated, where his children were born.

## Associated Characters

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]**: The house is her domain and her prison — she maintains it, feeds its inhabitants, and is tethered to it by duty.
- **[[the-son|The Son]]**: He returns to the house at day's end, expecting food and comfort. The house is his refuge from labor.
- **[[the-old-woman|The Old Woman]]**: She rarely leaves the house, tethered to it by near-blindness and old age. Its walls contain her entire remaining world.

## Significance

The farmhouse embodies the central tension of the novel: it is both home and cage, shelter and constraint. Its smallness and bareness reflect the family's [[poverty]]; its continuity across generations represents the endurance of peasant life. Every object within it — the stove, the bed, the quilt, the paper god — carries the weight of economic striving and emotional history.
