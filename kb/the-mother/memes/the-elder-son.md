## Summary

The elder son is the mother's firstborn child — a boy of about five years old at the start of the novel, watchful, jealous of his mother's attention, and deeply curious about the world of adults he is entering. He is present at the birth of his younger brother, where his anxious question — "Is it not ours then to keep?" (001-163) — reveals both his possessive love for his mother and his childish misunderstanding of birth. As the narrative progresses and his father disappears, the elder son begins the long, premature transition from child to household helper.

## Role

The elder son occupies the narrative role of the **child-witness** — a figure through whose eyes the reader glimpses the emotional dynamics of the household without the filtering consciousness of adult characters. His reactions are raw and unmediated: he weeps when his mother closes the door during childbirth, runs after her when she returns from the fields, and proudly announces the new baby's arrival to the cousin's wife.

His developmental arc tracks the family's trajectory: as the mother is forced to take on the father's labor after the husband's disappearance, the elder son is pushed toward an early adulthood. He is the first child to feel the weight of his father's absence, and the first to step into the role the husband has vacated.

## Character Traits

- **Watchful and perceptive**: At five, the elder son already reads the emotional landscape of the household. He sees the cousin's wife "look at him hard as if he had done some ill thing" and withdraws silently (001-153). He notices his father's new blue robe and reports its cost to his mother (001-329).
- **Possessive of his mother**: When she closes the door for the birth, he beats on it in panic and cannot be consoled — his fear is not of the unknown but of exclusion from her presence.
- **Curious about adult affairs**: His question "Is it not ours then to keep?" reveals a child's attempt to understand the mystery of a new baby's arrival through the concrete logic of ownership.
- **Gentle and tearless in his grief**: Unlike his little sister who screams and rubs her sore eyes, the elder son weeps "gently and steadily, his eyes tearless" (001-051) — a quiet, internalized sorrow that anticipates the stoicism he will need.

## Relationships

The elder son is the firstborn of [[the-mother]] and [[the-husband]]. His bond with his mother is fierce and jealous — he cannot bear to be shut out from her presence and will endure a cuff from her rather than stay away. He is the brother of [[the-daughter]] (about three years old at the start of the novel) and a new baby born in Chapter I. He also lives with [[the-grandmother]], his father's old blind mother, whose chatter fills the household.

His relationship with the [[the-cousins-wife]] is wary — he feels her stern look and withdraws — and with [[the-cousin]] it is distant but observant.

## In the Narrative

The elder son appears in the opening chapters as part of the household texture — eating from his tin bowl, trailing after his mother, running errands to summon the cousin's wife. His most memorable scene is during his younger brother's birth, where his fear of being shut out collides with his mother's need for privacy, and she cuffs him before relenting. As the narrative progresses and the father disappears, the elder son's role shifts from child to helper, mirroring the mother's own transformation from wife to sole provider.

## Significance

The elder son embodies the novel's concern with cycles of inheritance and labor. He is the next generation of peasant life, and his trajectory — from watchful child to early laborer — replicates the pattern his father fled. Pearl Buck uses him to ask, through implication, whether the cycle can be broken: will the elder son grow into his father's restlessness or his mother's steadfastness?
