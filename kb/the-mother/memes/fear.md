# Fear

In *The Mother*, **fear** is a diffuse, pervasive force that shapes behavior more powerfully than any external constraint. Characters fear abandonment, exposure, their own desires, and the simple precariousness of survival. Buck depicts fear not as a dramatic emotion confined to moments of crisis but as a constant companion to labor and daily life—a quiet hum beneath the rhythms of planting, harvest, and household. The mother's journey through the novel is, in large measure, a journey through successive fears and the strategies she develops to contain them.

## Fear of the Man's Anger

Within the household, fear operates as a discipline. The children learn early to navigate their father's volatility:

> They had crept up silently and not waking the man, fearing his anger, for with all his merry ways when he was minded to be merry, the man could be angry and cuff them furiously if he were waked before his sleep was ended.
>
> (001-001)

This fear is learned and practical—the children know the boundary and adapt their behavior to avoid crossing it. When the door is shut during the mother's childbirth, the boy "was afraid to see the door shut in the daytime" (004-004) and beats on it until the mother, in her labor pain, rises and cuffs him. Fear in the household is circular: the man's anger generates the children's fear, which generates the mother's exasperation, which falls back on the children.

## Fear of Abandonment

The mother's deepest and most sustained fear emerges when the man disappears. After the first night alone, her anger gives way to terror:

> But in the dark night, in the still, hot night, in the silence of the closed room, her anger went out of her and she was afraid. If he did not come back what would she do, a lone woman and young?
>
> (010-010)

This fear is existential rather than emotional—it is the fear of a woman in a world where survival depends on having a man to work the land, face the landlord's agent, and provide the social legitimacy that protects her from predation. As days become weeks, the fear settles into a chronic condition:

> That night she laid herself down most sorrowful and afraid upon her bed and this night she said openly to her own heart that none knew if he would ever come back again.
>
> (010-010)

## Fear of Her Own Body

The novel's most psychologically acute treatment of fear comes when the mother recognizes her own desire for the landlord's agent. After the encounter in the bamboo grove, she is terrified not of him but of herself:

> She was more frightened than she had ever been in her whole life, for she knew that such hunger as was in her now grew raving if it were not fed.
>
> (017-017)

This fear is devastating precisely because it is self-knowledge. The mother is "too simple to pretend when well she knew how it was with her" (017-017). She cannot blame the agent alone; she must reckon with the fact that her own body answered his. Her prayer—"Oh, I wish he would not have me, and that I might be saved!" (017-017)—acknowledges that she wants what she fears, and fears what she wants.

## The Certainty Beyond Fear

After the encounter, fear transforms into its aftermath:

> One month passed and she was afraid. Two months passed and three and harvest came, the grain was threshed, and what had been fear beneath her labor day by day was now a certainty.
>
> (017-017)

The progression from fear to certainty is one of the novel's most compressed narrative arcs. Fear was the anticipation; pregnancy is the inescapable fact. The mother curses "the day of the storm and her own foolish heats" (017-017), but the curse cannot undo what fear had failed to prevent.

## Fear as Cover

In a dark irony, the old woman's death gives the mother a socially acceptable reason for her visible distress:

> It was an ease to her too to have every cause to cover up her fear and melancholy. For she had cause to be afraid, and she had time now, even while she worked upon her land, to take out of her heart that fear which had been hiding there ever since the hour in the rising storm.
>
> (017-017)

Grief for the old woman becomes a mask for the fear of exposure. The mother is "glad even for the old woman's death" (017-017) because it provides cover—a thought whose cruelty she acknowledges but cannot afford to refuse. Fear makes utilitarian calculations of the heart.

## The Man's Terror

Buck extends the register of fear beyond the mother. The man experiences his own existential terror at the prospect of a life without escape:

> It fell upon him like a terror that so long as he lived there was naught for him but this, to rise in the morning and go to this land of which they owned but little … to spend his day upon that rented land even as his father had done before him … until he grew old and died.
>
> (005-005)

This claustrophobic vision—the "wheel of days, year upon year" (005-005)—drives the man's gambling, his flight to the city, and ultimately his abandonment of his family. His fear is the mirror image of the mother's: she fears losing the life she has; he fears being trapped in it.
