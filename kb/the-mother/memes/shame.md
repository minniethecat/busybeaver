# Shame

In *The Mother*, **shame** is the invisible architecture of social life in the hamlet. It determines what can be said aloud and what must be hidden, what actions can be taken and what must be refused. For the mother, shame is a constant companion—the shame of abandonment, the shame of desire, and ultimately the shame of a secret pregnancy that threatens to undo her standing in the community. Buck presents shame not as an internal feeling alone but as a social currency, traded in glances, gossip, and the ever-present possibility of exposure.

## The Shame of Abandonment

When the man disappears, the mother's first instinct is to construct a fiction that protects her from the shame of being a woman whose husband has left her:

> Thus did the mother lie steadfastly and well, and the old woman believed the tale heartily … and the mother kept her face calm and smooth and she did not weep as women do when their men run away and shame them.
>
> (014-014)

The phrase "shame them" is crucial—a man's abandonment of his wife is a mark against *her*, not him. The mother understands this social logic instinctively and builds her public composure as a shield. The elaborate fiction of the letter, the money, and the man's distant success is not for her own emotional comfort but for her social survival.

## The Cost of Public Composure

The mother's calm face is maintained at a price. At night, when the audience is gone, the shame pours out:

> But in the nights, in the dark nights, she wept silently and most bitterly. Partly she wept because he was gone, but sometimes she wept, too, because she was so put to shame.
>
> (016-016)

The doubling of "partly … but sometimes" is significant: grief for the man and shame at her position are distinct pains. The shame is not about missing him but about what her situation says about her—that she was not worth staying for, that she cannot hold a man. When she thinks of the money spent on his new garments, the cakes baked, the incense burned, she resolves to "spare herself the shame" (016-016) by stopping the rituals of waiting.

## The Shame of Desire

The novel's most psychologically complex treatment of shame appears in the mother's response to her own desire for the landlord's agent. After the encounter in the bamboo grove, shame and longing coexist without canceling each other:

> In the midst of her true shame and all her wishing she had withstood him, she yearned for him still.
>
> (017-017)

This is the shame of a woman who knows what she has done and would do it again. She is "ashamed to seek him out, and fearful too lest she be seen" (017-017), yet waits for his return. The shame does not extinguish the desire; it sharpens it by making it forbidden.

## Public Humiliation

At the harvest feast, shame becomes a public event. The mother dresses carefully, her "cheeks red with sick hope and shyness" (017-017), only to have the agent look through her as a stranger:

> And his eyes fell on her scarlet face and he looked away as if he had never known her and fell to his bowl again. And the woman, feeling the scarlet draining from her face too fast, crept out and ran away and they laughed to see her run for shame at all their merriment.
>
> (017-017)

The physical description—"scarlet face," "scarlet draining"—makes shame visible, a color that rises and falls. The laughter of the community completes the humiliation. This is shame not as private conscience but as public spectacle.

## Shame and Self-Knowledge

Shame in the novel also operates as a form of self-recognition. When the mother cuffs her child in frustration and immediately regrets it, "she blamed herself secretly with a sort of shame that she had made the child suffer for her own pain" (012-012). When she nurses another woman's baby in a moment of displaced tenderness, she is "ashamed somehow of what she had done" (012-012). These small shames are the conscience of a woman who knows her actions are misdirected expressions of larger pains.

## The Generational Transmission of Shame

The novel extends shame across generations. The blind daughter "hung her head ashamed to live as such a burden" (017-017), internalizing the community's assessment of her worth. The elder son tells his mother that her field labor "shames me to have you work in the field and others see you there when you are aged" (017-017). Shame flows downward (the mother's shame transmitted to children) and upward (children's shame constraining the mother). It is the emotional infrastructure of filial obligation.
