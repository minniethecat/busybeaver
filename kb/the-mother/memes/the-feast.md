# Feast as Communal Ritual

**Feast** events are communal rituals in the hamlet, marking the agricultural calendar and structuring social relationships. The novel uses feasts to reveal the hierarchies, obligations, and rare pleasures of peasant existence.

## The Landlord's Feast

The most politically charged feast is the one mandated by the landlord's agent after the grain is measured. Every household must contribute a dish:

> More than this, when all the grain was measured out the village must set a feast before the agent and every house must give a dish.
>
> (005-072)

The feast is an obligation, not a celebration. It formalizes the unequal relationship between landlord and tenant. The mother's contribution—a carefully prepared fowl—reflects both her skill and her anxiety about appearing before the agent:

> Even in this lonely year the mother caught a fowl and killed it and cooked it for the feast, steaming it gently and long until it was done and while the shape was whole and the skin unbroken, yet was the flesh so tender that when the first chopsticks touched it it would fall apart.
>
> (005-072)

The care taken with the fowl—steamed until the flesh falls apart at a touch—demonstrates the mother's culinary competence and her understanding of the social stakes involved in presenting food to the powerful.

## The Feast's Aftermath

After the men have eaten, the mother scavenges what remains for her children:

> Nevertheless when the feast was over she went to the littered table where the men had sat and she picked up a bone left from her fowl and a little skin was hanging to it and a shred of meat and she took it and gave it to the lad to suck.
>
> (005-074)

This image—the mother picking through leftovers to feed her son—encapsulates the economic reality behind the feast's ceremonial surface. The feast feeds the powerful; the scraps feed the peasant children.

## Harvest Feasting

The harvest feast marks the culmination of the agricultural year. It is a moment of communal celebration, but also of personal longing:

> Not even on the day of harvest feasting would he look at her, although she washed herself freshly and combed her hair and smoothed it down with oil.
>
> (011-027)

For the mother, the harvest feast becomes a measure of her [[the-husband|husband]]'s emotional distance. She prepares herself with care, but her efforts go unnoticed. The feast that should unite the community underscores her isolation.

## The New Year Feast

The approach of the [[the-new-year|New Year]] feast structures the entire hamlet's activity:

> The time drew near and everyone in the hamlet was busy for the feast, and she must needs be busy, too, not only for the children, to make them new shoes and wash their garments clean and make a new cap for the babe, but she must be busy for the man, also.
>
> (005-090)

The feast demands labor from everyone, especially the mother, who must prepare not only food but clothing and cleanliness for the entire household. The feast is an event that reorganizes village time and domestic labor around its demands.
