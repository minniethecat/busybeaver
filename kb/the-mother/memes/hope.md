# Hope and Resilience

In the harsh world of *The Mother*, **hope** is not a grand aspiration or a theological virtue — it is a survival mechanism, as essential as food and as fragile as a child's health. Pearl S. Buck traces hope through the mother's life as something that flickers and gutters but never entirely goes out, renewing itself in small forms: a letter that might arrive, a child who might thrive, a season that will eventually turn. To understand the mother's resilience is to understand how hope operates in a life where most outcomes are determined by forces beyond individual control.

## The Small Shapes of Hope

Hope in the novel rarely announces itself in dramatic form. It appears instead in small, almost invisible gestures — the mother checking the rice pot to see if there will be enough, the child waiting under the table for scraps, the anticipation of a market day:

> He had been sitting in hope under the table, but the man kicked him, and he slunk out.
>
> (005-005)

This image — the dog waiting "in hope" under the table — captures the novel's understanding of hope as a posture of waiting, vulnerable and easily crushed. The dog's hope is for food; the mother's hope is for her children's survival, for her husband's return, for a harvest that will last through winter. The dog is kicked, and hope is extinguished in that moment — but only for that moment. The dog will return to its place under the table the next day, just as the mother will rise before dawn to feed the fire once more.

## Hope Deferred: The Absent Husband

The mother's most sustained act of hope — and her most elaborate fiction — concerns her husband's return. After he abandons the family, she constructs a story: he has gone to a distant city for work, he sends money when he can, and he will come back rich one day. This story sustains not only the mother but also the old grandmother, who "believed the tale heartily" (014-014).

Yet the mother knows better than anyone that her hope is, at best, uncertain:

> Now well she knew she had no hope from this man.
>
> (004-004)

This sentence, arriving with the force of a confession, reveals the gap between the hope the mother performs for others and the hope she actually feels. She lies to the hamlet, to the old woman, and perhaps to herself — but there are moments when the lie falls away and she sees clearly what she has lost.

The seven-day rhythm of hope and disappointment structures the early period of the husband's absence:

> Nevertheless, there was the hope of the seven days when his money might be gone.
>
> (006-006)

Each week brings the possibility that he might return — and each week that passes confirms the likelihood that he will not. Hope becomes a cycle of anticipation and letdown.

## Hope for the Children

As the novel progresses, the mother's hope shifts from her own circumstances to her children's futures. Her daughter's marriage becomes a focal point:

> So garbed and her cheeks red with sick hope and shyness and her eyes bright with all her desperate secret fears, she hurried here and there.
>
> (014-014)

The phrase "sick hope" is characteristic of Buck's unsentimental treatment of this emotion. Hope is not pure or noble — it is "sick," tinged with desperation and fear. The mother hopes her daughter will find a good husband, but she also knows the statistics of peasant marriage:

> Better far to have her where I cannot see her and where I cannot know and so be saved the pain because I cannot see and so can hope.
>
> (014-014)

This is hope as willful ignorance — a deliberate turning-away from what one suspects to be true in order to preserve the possibility of a better outcome.

Her son's departure brings a different quality of hope — more anxious, less certain:

> Yet she must hope and whenever anyone went into town she always asked him when he came back again, "Saw you my little son today anywhere?"
>
> (019-019)

The persistence of this question, repeated across years, is itself a definition of maternal hope: the refusal to stop asking, even when answers never come.

## Hope and the Body

When the mother falls ill after her husband's departure, her body becomes a register of emotional depletion:

> She lay there on her bed for many days, purged body and heart, and all her sorrow and her comfort too in abeyance because she was not strong enough to feel either.
>
> (016-016)

"Purged body and heart" — this is hope exhausted, the system shutting down when emotional resources have been spent. Yet even here, hope is not dead but "in abeyance," suspended rather than extinguished. The mother will rise from this bed.

The cousin's wife offers a different kind of hope — practical intervention:

> And for the first time the mother saw a hope before her and she whispered, "But how — but how —" and the cousin's wife said, "There are simples to be taken."
>
> (010-010)

This is hope made tangible: herbs, remedies, the accumulated knowledge of peasant women. It is not the hope of escape but the hope of getting through another day.

## The Limits of Hope

Buck is too honest a writer to let hope carry the day unchallenged. The infant who dies after one breath, the daughter's sore eyes that never fully heal, the son who drifts into the anonymous life of the urban poor — these are not tragedies that hope can undo.

The mother's final posture is not triumphant but enduring. She has outlasted her husband's abandonment, raised children who have scattered, and remained rooted in the hamlet and the land:

> So she thought most heavily, "Yet I cannot hope she will never be beaten."
>
> (014-014)

This is hope stripped of illusion — a clear-eyed recognition of what is likely, coupled with the determination to continue anyway. It is not the hope of optimists but the hope of survivors.

| Form of Hope | Object | Outcome |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Domestic hope | Husband's return, family stability | Deferred indefinitely; sustained by fiction |
| Maternal hope | Children's health and futures | Partial: children survive, futures precarious |
| Practical hope | Herbs, remedies, small interventions | Mixed: some work, some do not |
| Bodily hope | Recovery from illness, strength to work | Cyclical: body recovers, then depletes |
| Hope as habit | Persistence without expectation | Endures: the mother keeps going |
