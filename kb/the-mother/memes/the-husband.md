## Summary

The husband is the mother's spouse and the father of her children — a young man of twenty-eight whose charm, wit, and physical grace coexist uneasily with a deep resentment of the life of unremitting labor that fate has dealt him. He is a talented singer and a lucky gambler who brings merriment to the inn but silence and sullenness to his own household. His defining act in the narrative is his disappearance — leaving the mother alone to harvest the rice and raise the children — a desertion that both frees and hardens her.

## Role

The husband occupies the central **antagonistic-loved** position in the mother's life. She is drawn to his passion and physical presence — their quarrels "set some edge upon their passion for each other" (001-118) — but also worn down by his unreliability. He is the source of her children, her pride, her anger, and ultimately her abandonment.

His narrative function is to embody the tension between individual desire and familial obligation in rural peasant life. He is not a villain — he is a man who "had never worked more than he must" (001-073) and who looks at his poor house and sees a lifetime of the same stretching ahead with no escape. His gambling and his final departure are attempts, however selfish and destructive, to break free of that fate.

## Character Traits

- **Physically graceful**: Small-boned, strong, and well-formed — "such a one as heaven had shaped for joy" with "bright and black" eyes and "nimble fingers" that the plough had not stiffened (001-073).
- **Charismatic in public**: At the inn he sings new songs, cracks witty jokes, and "could set a whole crowd laughing" (001-073). His public self is merry and magnetic.
- **Sullen at home**: He saves none of this charm for his own house. With his wife and children he is silent, surly, or quarrelsome. The contrast is deliberate — he "seemed to save all his good humor... for strangers and for those who were not of his own house" (001-073).
- **Lucky gambler**: He habitually returns from games with more money than he took, a skill that feeds his fantasy of living as "a lone man" with only himself to feed (001-073).
- **Restless and resentful**: He recoils at the thought that "so long as he lived there was naught for him but this" — the cycle of planting, harvesting, and handing a share to the landlord (001-073). This resentment shapes his every action.
- **A flawed but not evil man**: When the cousin accuses him of wasting the land, the husband defends himself half-heartedly, then passes on in silence. He can be shamed, even if he cannot be changed.

## Relationships

The husband is married to [[the-mother]], and their dynamic is one of the novel's central tensions. Their passion is real but volatile — she can match his temper and often best him in quarrels, "except when she was angry at a child" (001-073). She is proud of his charm and his luck, but increasingly aware that charm does not feed children.

His **cousin** ([[the-cousin]]) is his moral opposite — steady, silent, hardworking — and the husband's awareness of this contrast feeds his self-justifying anger.

His **mother**, [[the-grandmother]], is old, blind, and living in the household — a "troublesome old care" who nonetheless takes fierce pride in her son's gambling skill: "My son is very good at winning in a game" (001-155).

His **children** include [[the-elder-son]], [[the-daughter]], and a baby. He is not cruel to them but is emotionally absent, quick to anger when they cry but too restless to be consistently present.

## Key Passages

> "Sometimes when the man came home from a holiday in town... it fell upon him like a terror that so long as he lived there was naught for him but this, to rise in the morning and go to this land of which they owned but little... to come home to eat his same coarse food... to sleep, to rise again to the same next day." (001-073)

> "He was such a one as seemed to save all his good humor and his merry, lovable looks for strangers and for those who were not of his own house." (001-073)

> "When he heard them laugh his heart leaped with pleasure in this power he had, and when he came home again and saw his wife's grave face and sturdy body it seemed to him that only she did not know him for the fine man he truly was, for only she never praised him." (001-073)

## In the Narrative

The husband is present in the early chapters as a vivid physical force — eating three bowls of rice, singing under the moon, quarreling with the mother, gambling at the inn. His purchase of a new blue robe marks a turning point: the garment costs money the family cannot spare and signals his increasing estrangement from domestic life. His disappearance, which occurs between chapters, is the narrative's central rupture. The mother is left to harvest alone, to deal with the landlord alone, and to raise the children alone. His absence defines the rest of the novel.

## Significance

The husband represents the fragility of the peasant family when the man at its center cannot bear its weight. Pearl Buck does not condemn him outright — she shows the genuine terror he feels at the prospect of a life of endless, unrewarded toil. But she also shows, through the mother's steadfastness and the cousin's silent help, that the community survives even when one of its members fails it. The husband's departure is the crisis that reveals the mother's true strength.
