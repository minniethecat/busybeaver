## Summary

The Mother is the central protagonist of the novel, a peasant woman living in a small thatched farmhouse in a rural Chinese hamlet. Throughout the narrative, she embodies the archetype of maternal strength — hardworking, resilient, and fiercely devoted to her family. Her life revolves around cooking, fieldwork, child-rearing, and maintaining the household, all while navigating the tensions between her own passionate nature and the demands of her roles as wife, mother, and daughter-in-law.

## Role in the Story

The Mother serves as the narrative anchor of the novel, with much of the action filtered through her consciousness. She is introduced in the novel's opening scene, feeding grass into the earthen stove while her mother-in-law chatters endlessly and her children cling to her. This domestic tableau establishes her defining tension: she is a woman of action and silence, surrounded by demands — [[the-old-woman|the old woman]]'s ceaseless talk, the children's needs, [[the-son|the husband]]'s expectations.

Her role spans both the domestic and agricultural spheres. She cooks the family's meals, tends to the children, cares for the old woman, and feeds the animals, yet she also labors in the fields alongside her husband — planting beans, cutting grass for fuel, and working "until the last row" (001-005). She takes pride in her competence, recognizing that in their small hamlet of six or seven houses, "there was not one in which the mother did better for her children than did she for hers" (001-038).

The Mother's inner life is rich with contradiction. She quarrels passionately with her husband ("they were both young and full of temper and passion" (001-011)), yet takes pleasure in feeding him well. She is impatient with her children by day, yet at night she is "all tenderness — passionate tenderness to the man when he turned to her in need, tender to the children as they lay helpless in sleep" (001-038).

## Key Relationships

- **The Man (husband)**: A vigorous, singing farmer with whom she shares a passionate but quarrelsome marriage. They are "so well matched" that "neither was the victor and neither vanquished" (001-011). Despite their arguments, she takes quiet pleasure in feeding him an egg after hard labor.
- **The Old Woman (mother-in-law)**: Her husband's elderly mother, nearly blind from years of smoke exposure. She talks incessantly, yet the Mother dutifully serves her the first bowl of food. A relationship of patient tolerance rather than warmth.
- **The Boy (elder son)**: Her five-year-old son, healthy and well-grown. He follows her about and suckles at her dry breast for comfort at night (001-038).
- **The Girl (younger daughter)**: Her three-year-old daughter, whose chronically sore eyes trouble the Mother. The child's condition — eyes sealed shut each morning — is a source of recurring concern (001-040).

## Character Development

The Mother is not presented as static; the narrative reveals layers of her character through the accumulation of domestic detail. Initially depicted as absorbed in her endless tasks, her inner thoughts gradually emerge: her pride in her children's health, her awareness of the family's modest prosperity by comparison to neighbors, her private tenderness that surfaces only at night. She is a woman whose outward brusqueness masks a deep capacity for feeling — and for fierce protectiveness of those under her care.

## Notable Appearances

- **Chapter I**: The opening domestic scene — cooking the evening meal, feeding the family, putting the household to bed (001-001 through 001-038)
- **Chapter I**: Her reflection on her marriage and early quarrels over breastfeeding — "when her first son had been born, she could not bear to hear him cry at all" (001-010)
- **Chapter I**: Evening rest at the door, watching the sunset, taking pride in her children's well-being compared to the innkeeper's children (001-038)
- **Chapter II**: The morning routine — rising before dawn, lighting the fire, washing her daughter's sealed eyes, beginning another identical day (001-040)

## Significance

The Mother represents the universal figure of the peasant woman whose life is entirely given over to sustaining others — cooking, bearing children, laboring in the fields, tending to the old. Her significance lies in the dignity the narrative accords to this seemingly unremarkable existence. Through her, the novel explores themes of maternal sacrifice, the tension between selfhood and duty, and the quiet heroism of daily endurance.
