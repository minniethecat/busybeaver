# Voice and Oral Expression

If silence is the mother's default mode, **voice** — in its many registers — is the force that punctuates her silence and reveals her character. In *The Mother*, Pearl S. Buck constructs a rich soundscape of peasant speech: the old woman's endless chatter, the man's roaring, the cousin's wife's gossip, the children's crying, and, at the center, the mother's own voice — rough, kind, loud, and used with an economy that gives each utterance weight.

## The Mother's Voice

The mother speaks relatively little, but when she does, her voice carries authority. Buck describes it as "rough kind voice" (001-001) — an instrument shaped by labor and outdoors, not by refinement. When she calls to the restless beasts at night, "hearing her rough kind voice even they were quieted and slept again" (001-001). This is voice as command, capable of imposing order on the world simply by sounding.

Her voice is also the vehicle of tenderness. To her children, to the old woman, even to the animals, the mother's speech — though often brusque — conveys care:

> She called out to them, "Be still — sleep — day is a long way off yet —"
>
> (001-001)

The dashes in Buck's rendering of her speech suggest a voice that is unhurried, rhythmic, shaped by the bodily experience of tiredness and the maternal duty of reassurance. It is not a poetic voice but a practical one, and its beauty lies in its unadorned directness.

## Quarrel and Complaint

The mother's voice is capable of ferocity. Her quarrels with the man are among the novel's most energetic passages, two strong-willed people "well matched" in verbal combat:

> She flew back at him then as ever she did, for they were both young and full of temper and passion, and she cried at him, "And shall I not have a little something for my pains?"
>
> (001-001)

Quarreling, in this world, is not a failure of relationship but one of its modes — a way of negotiating the impossible demands of peasant life. The mother's willingness to "fly back" at the man is a sign of her vitality and her refusal to be diminished. Her voice in these passages is fierce, quick, and unafraid.

Yet after the man leaves, this dimension of her voice is largely lost. There is no one left to quarrel with on equal terms. The mother's verbal energy is redirected into lying — the elaborate fiction of the absent husband — and into the quieter tones of maternal command.

## The Old Woman's Voice

The old grandmother provides the novel's continuous vocal backdrop. Her "old cracked voice" (001-001) runs on incessantly, "pausing to cough now and again in the smoke." She is the oral historian of the household, the keeper of memory and complaint:

> "I ever did say that when a man is hungered and especially a young and hearty son like mine, an egg stirred into noodles —"
>
> (001-001)

The old woman's voice is fragmented, repetitive, often unheard — the mother "did not hear the old voice" — yet it serves an essential function. It is the sound of continuity, linking past to present, and it fills the silence that would otherwise be oppressive. When the old woman eventually dies, her voice's absence is a form of silence the mother must learn to inhabit.

## The Hamlet's Oral Culture

Beyond the farmhouse walls, the hamlet hums with talk. Women "chatter" and "gad about," exchanging news and judgment. The cousin's wife is the most vivid representative of this oral culture — voluble, indiscreet, and indispensable:

> "A very good thing it will be for you, goodwife, when that old thing is dead, so old and blind and full of aches."
>
> (002-002)

Her blunt speech, however shocking, is also a form of friendship — the willingness to say what the mother cannot. The cousin's wife's voice is the voice of the hamlet itself: practical, unsentimental, and relentlessly social.

The mother's relationship to this oral culture is ambivalent. She participates — she must — but she is never fully of it. Her relative quiet marks her as different, and it is this difference that enables her survival. By speaking less, she exposes less; by listening more, she learns more.

## Voice and Gender

Buck's novel implicitly tracks the relationship between voice and gender. The man roars and shouts; the women talk and chatter and complain. But the mother's voice occupies an intermediate register — she can roar back at the man when necessary, and she can command the children and the beasts with authority. Her voice is not "feminine" in any conventional sense; it is the voice of competence, of someone who has learned that speech is a tool like any other, to be used when useful and set aside when not.

After her husband leaves, the mother's voice becomes perhaps the most "male" voice in the household — she makes decisions, issues commands, and negotiates with the outside world. Her voice fills the vacuum left by the absent man, and in doing so it reveals that gender roles in this peasant world are more fluid than they first appear.

| Register of Voice | Character | Function |
|------------------|-----------|----------|
| Command | The mother | Orders children, calms beasts, directs labor |
| Quarrel | The mother + the man | Negotiates domestic conflict |
| Chatter | The old woman | Fills silence, preserves memory |
| Gossip | The cousin's wife, hamlet women | Circulates information and judgment |
| Lying | The mother | Defends against shame, protects children |
| Silence | The mother | Withholds, endures, preserves self |
