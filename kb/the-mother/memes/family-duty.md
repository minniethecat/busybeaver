## Summary

Family duty is the silent architecture of the novel's domestic world — an unspoken but absolute code that governs who feeds whom, who cares for whom, and who endures what. The mother's life is defined by obligations: to her children, her husband, her mother-in-law, and the household as a whole.

## In the Novel

The mother's day is a catalogue of duties: she rises before dawn to light the fire (001-040), feeds the stove, cooks the meal, serves the old woman first ("first she poured it full for the old woman" (001-011)), feeds the children, waits upon the man, and labors in the fields. None of these actions carry any suggestion of choice; they are simply what must be done.

The structure of duty follows a clear hierarchy. The old woman receives her food first — a ritual acknowledgment of seniority that persists even though she no longer contributes labor. The man eats while the mother serves him, thumping his bowl on the table "to hasten her" (001-069). The mother eats last, upon her low stool in the dooryard, rising periodically to serve others. This sequence is not presented as injustice but as the natural order of things, unquestioned by all parties.

The novel subtly registers the cost of this system. The mother's interior life — her pride in her children's health, her private tenderness at night, her suppressed anger at the man's gambling — exists in tension with the ceaseless demands of duty. "She was a woman whose outward brusqueness masked a deep capacity for feeling," yet the feeling finds expression only in stolen moments: staring into the sunset, feeding the dog scraps of rice, watching the man approach singing.

The old woman's relationship to duty is equally revealing. Having outlived her productive years, she fears becoming "a useless old crone now, who ought to die" (001-001). Her incessant talk is an effort to maintain a function — the function of memory, of warning, of commentary — in a household that no longer needs her labor. The family's continued care for her, regardless of her utility, embodies the Confucian ideal of filial piety, but the narrative does not sentimentalize this: she is tolerated more than cherished.

## Key Scenes

- **Chapter I**: The evening meal — the ritualized serving order (old woman first, then the man, then the mother last) (001-011 through 001-069)
- **Chapter I**: The old woman's monologue — her anxiety about fuel, her recollections of past days, her assertion of continued relevance through talk (001-001 through 001-005)
- **The morning routine**: The mother waking before dawn, lighting the fire, washing her daughter's sealed eyes — duty resumed before full consciousness (001-040)

## Related Characters

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]**: Her identity is inseparable from duty — she is defined by what she does for others. Her private self exists in the margins between obligations.
- **[[the-son|The Son]]**: As the male head of household, his duties (field labor) are clear and bounded. His freedom to game and sing exists because the mother's unbounded duty absorbs all else.
- **[[the-old-woman|The Old Woman]]**: She represents the far end of the duty cycle — the recipient of care who once provided it, now struggling to find purpose in dependence.

## Significance

Family duty in the novel is both structure and cage. It organizes daily life into a coherent order of obligations, ensuring that the old are fed and the young are raised. Yet it also erases the self, particularly the female self, subsuming individual desire into the collective project of survival. The novel's achievement is to show this system from within — not as a problem to be critiqued, but as the water in which its characters swim.
