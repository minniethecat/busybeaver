# Illness and Sickness in Peasant Life

**Illness** is a disruptive event in the mother's world, exposing the fragility of bodies that depend on continuous labor for survival. The novel treats illness not as a medical condition but as a social and economic crisis.

## Illness as Curse

In the hamlet's folk understanding, illness is often attributed to curses or supernatural forces:

> That woman of mine has cursed me ill over some small thing I scarcely know, and there is no pleasing her, and she cursed me so sore I had an illness in the night.
>
> (004-027)

A neighboring husband attributes his sickness to his wife's curse, revealing the pre-modern framework through which illness is understood. Physical symptoms are interpreted as moral or spiritual events. The "illness in the night" is a dramatic social event, not a biological one.

## Illness as Existential Vulnerability

For the mother, illness represents the ever-present threat to her capacity to work:

> Or even if she worked too hard or sat in any small cold wind that evil illness waited for her and made her helpless for a while again.
>
> (015-026)

The phrase "evil illness waited for her" personifies sickness as a predator stalking the margins of peasant life. A "small cold wind" is all it takes—the margin between health and helplessness is paper-thin for those who live by physical labor.

## Illness as Reproductive Shame

Illness is also weaponized as an accusation against barren women:

> It must be you have some hidden illness in you somewhere to make you barren and unnatural.
>
> (016-017)

The mother-in-law's accusation transforms infertility from a physiological condition into a moral failing. "Hidden illness" suggests something shameful and concealed, making the woman's body a site of suspicion and blame within the household.

## The Refusal of Medical Frameworks

The mother resists medicalizing her body's changes:

> She pretended nothing to herself now, as a townswoman might pretend, that there was some illness she had.
>
> (010-018)

The contrast between "townswoman" and peasant woman is crucial. Town women might seek medical explanations for their conditions; the mother accepts her body's transformations—aging, exhaustion, pregnancy—as natural facts rather than pathologies. Her refusal to name illness is a form of resilience.
