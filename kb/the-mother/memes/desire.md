# Desire

In *The Mother*, **desire** is the force that disrupts the steady rhythm of agricultural life. The mother's body, so reliable in labor and childbirth, becomes a source of longing she cannot control. Buck presents desire without romanticization — as a physical fact, a heat that rises with the seasons, a hunger that demands feeding. The novel traces the arc of desire from legitimate marital passion through dangerous extramarital longing to its final extinction, mapping the mother's emotional life through the language of the body.

## Marital Passion

The novel establishes early that the mother's marriage is grounded in physical satisfaction. Her account of her own contentment is bodily before it is emotional:

> To her — to know the fullness of the man's frequent passion, to conceive by him and know life growing within her own body, to feel this new flesh take shape and grow, to give birth and feel a child's lips drink at her breast — these were enough.
>
> (003-003)

"These were enough" — the phrase recurs as a kind of credo. Desire fulfilled within marriage is the foundation on which the mother's entire life rests. Even quarreling "set some edge upon their passion for each other" (003-003). The man's nightly turning toward her is the transaction that makes the day's labor bearable.

## The Hunger of Absence

When the man disappears, the mother discovers that desire does not vanish with its object. On the contrary, absence intensifies it:

> Suddenly there fell upon her the hottest longing for that man of hers. These six years she had lain against him. Angry she might be with him in the day, but at night she was near to him again and she forgot his idle ways and his childishness.
>
> (010-010)

The bed becomes "enormous, empty" (010-010). She need take no care, may spread her arms and legs as she will — and this very freedom is its own torment. The body accustomed to another body cannot simply adjust to solitude. She remembers "how good and fair he was to look upon, not coarse in the mouth and foul of breath as most men are" (010-010). Absence restores the man to an idealized form that his presence never quite achieved.

## The Agent's Heat

The novel's most dangerous desire is the one the mother feels for the landlord's agent. It arrives not as a decision but as a physical event — a "sweet sick heat" (014-014) that she recognizes too late. Walking behind him through the summer grass, she is undone by proximity:

> Suddenly as she walked the woman felt her blood grow all sweet and languorous in her with the hot sun … she came near to him and caught some fragrance from him, too strong for perfume, some compound of man's blood and flesh and sweat. When she caught it in her nostrils she was stirred with longing and it was such a longing she grew frightened of herself.
>
> (017-017)

The progression is entirely sensory: sun, fragrance, blood, sweat, longing, fright. The mother's body answers before her mind can object. She is "too simple to pretend when well she knew how it was with her" (017-017). Her self-knowledge is absolute even when it damns her.

## The Persistence of Wanting

What makes the novel's treatment of desire psychologically acute is its refusal of easy repentance. Even after the encounter, even after the pregnancy, even after the public humiliation at the harvest feast, desire persists:

> She had repented that she ever yielded to him and yet she yearned for him often day and night. In the midst of her true shame and all her wishing she had withstood him, she yearned for him still.
>
> (017-017)

The repetition of "yearned" — three times in two sentences — enacts the insistence of the feeling itself. Shame and longing coexist without canceling. The mother hates what she wants and wants what she hates, and Buck offers no resolution to this contradiction except exhaustion.

## The End of Desire

Desire in the novel is not conquered but outlasted. After the birth of the secret child and the agent's final rejection, the mother's longing burns itself out:

> At that hour of deep loathing she was healed of all her heat and youth, and she was young no more. For her there was no man left in the world for man's own sake, and there were only these three her children, and one blind.
>
> (017-017)

"Healed of all her heat and youth" is a phrase of terrible ambiguity. She is cured of desire, but the cure is a kind of death — the death of youth, of heat, of the self that could still want. What remains is motherhood without the woman, duty without the body's consent.
