## Summary

Market day is a recurring economic event that punctuates the agricultural calendar in the novel. The mother's journeys to sell produce — cabbages, radishes, rice, grass — at the town market represent the intersection of subsistence farming with the money economy. These trips are also moments of social exposure where the mother confronts the wider world beyond the hamlet.

## In the Narrative

The market appears first in the context of the husband's irresponsibility: "When the man bought some foolish trinket in the market with the silver he had taken for the cabbage there" (192), market silver becomes a source of marital conflict. After the husband's departure, the market becomes the mother's responsibility alone.

During the spring planting season, the mother works "the fields ploughed somehow" and plants "radishes to be sold at market" (597), integrating market production into her already crushing labor burden. The physical toll of market trips weighs heavily: the mother passes by "with a load for market across her shoulder or coming slowly home with empty baskets" (675), her weariness visible to all who see her.

After the younger son's birth and the mother's prolonged illness, the burden becomes nearly unbearable: "to lift a load and carry it to market was a torture and yet she must do it now and then" (1009). Market day thus traces the mother's physical decline and the relentless demands of peasant survival.

The market is also the site of larger events. The elder son learns of his brother's arrest when a cousin reports seeing him "marching past the marketplace" (1777) with bound hands among captured communists.

## Key Participants

- **the-mother** — primary market-goer after the husband's departure, carrying produce to sell
- **the-husband** — formerly handled market sales, spent the silver on personal indulgences

## Cultural Context

Market day in the novel's world is not merely economic but deeply gendered. When the mother must "bargain alone with men" (503), she enters a male-dominated commercial space where her negotiating position is weakened by her sex. The market-town also serves as the hamlet's window to the wider world — the place where news, letters, and strangers arrive.

## Significance

Market day embodies the economic precariousness of peasant life. Each trip converts physical labor into the silver that sustains the household, yet the market also exposes the mother to exploitation, exhaustion, and the constant reminder of her abandoned status. The market is where private survival meets public judgment.
