# Pride and Dignity

**Pride** in *The Mother* is both a weapon and a wound. The mother draws on pride to sustain herself through abandonment and poverty—pride in her strength, her children, her capacity to endure. Yet pride is also what makes her vulnerable: the need to maintain face before the hamlet's gossiping eyes drives her to construct elaborate fictions and to suffer in silence. Pearl S. Buck traces pride as a fundamentally social emotion, never purely internal; it exists in the space between the mother's self-conception and the community's judgment.

## The Grounds of Pride

The novel establishes early that the mother's pride has real foundations. She is strong, capable, and productive—a woman whose labor sustains an entire household:

> It was true she could rightly be proud, proud of her own strength, proud of her children, proud of that man of hers.
>
> (001-025)

The triple repetition—"proud … proud … proud"—anchors pride in concrete achievements rather than vanity. Her strength is visible in the fields; her children are healthy and fed; her husband, whatever his faults, is hers. This is the pride of a woman who has built something real.

The arrival of the landlord's agent provides another occasion for pride. When he writes in his little book with a pen that needs no ink, the mother's response mingles curiosity with dignity:

> She watched him write and half it made her curious and half it touched her and made her proud to think that her land, which she had labored on and made fertile with her own sweat, was written there in his book.
>
> (010-010)

Pride here attaches to recognition: her land, her labor, her sweat are *recorded*—made legible to the world beyond the hamlet.

## Pride Performed

The mother's most theatrical moment of pride comes when a letter arrives from the absent husband. She transforms private receipt into public triumph:

> Then all the crowd fell back in respect for her, and she walked proudly home, the children following her and sharing in their mother's glory, and when the mother was come she told it all to the old mother.
>
> (006-009)

The public nature of this pride—the crowd parting, the children "sharing in their mother's glory"—reveals how fragile it is. The letter is real, but its meaning is performed. The mother needs witnesses; her pride requires an audience to be fully felt.

## The Limits of Pride

Pride cannot sustain the mother indefinitely. As the years of abandonment accumulate, even public recognition loses its power:

> But this time their fresh envy and all their talk and praise put nothing in the woman's empty heart. Not even pride could comfort her this time.
>
> (009-029)

The phrase "not even pride could comfort her" marks a threshold. Pride has been the mother's primary emotional resource—the thing she reaches for when other comforts fail. When pride itself fails, she enters a new and more desolate phase of her solitude. Buck stages this as a crucial moment in the mother's emotional trajectory: pride, like anger, has its limits.

## Inherited Pride and Its Burdens

The mother's pride extends to her children, particularly her elder son, who inherits her stubborn dignity:

> But the lad was proud and wilful and he argued, "I am here, mother, and I will help you. What other do you need? See, I am here!"
>
> (009-014)

The son's pride mirrors his mother's—it is pride in capacity, in the ability to work and provide. But his pride also becomes a source of conflict, as the mother finds herself "torn between these two, her son and the poor half blind girl" whose pride has been wounded:

> So she made haste to get out of the field then, too proud to stay, and the mother was torn between these two, her son and the poor half blind girl, and she felt the hearts of both.
>
> (008-017)

Here pride is divisive: the girl's pride drives her from the field; the son's pride makes him hard. The mother, who has spent a lifetime managing her own pride, must now navigate the competing prides of the next generation.
