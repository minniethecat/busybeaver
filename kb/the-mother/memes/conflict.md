## Summary

Conflict in *The Mother* is the social expression of anger, the point at which internal emotion becomes external action. The novel is punctuated by quarrels — between husband and wife, between neighbors, between siblings — that are never mere noise but always structurally significant. Conflict reveals power relations, tests community bonds, and provides the catharsis that the novel's characters, lacking any other outlet, depend on for emotional release.

## In the Novel

The husband's quarrels with the mother are the novel's central pattern of domestic conflict. "It gave a malicious pleasure to him to fall to loud quarreling with her, and it eased him somehow, although she never understood it" (004-005). The quarrel is asymmetrical — it serves the husband's needs while confusing the mother — and this asymmetry is the conflict's social meaning. He quarrels because he can; she endures because she must.

Physical violence is part of this dynamic. When the mother discovers the husband with another woman, she "flew at him and scratched his smooth and pretty face and she beat him so heartily that he was aghast" (003-008). This rare moment of the mother's physical aggression is immediately followed by softening: "her heart grew soft and the anger in her was satisfied" (003-008), suggesting that the quarrel, even when physically expressed, is not violence for its own sake but a form of communication — a desperate, embodied attempt at resolution.

Conflict extends beyond the household. "The house was full of quarreling, the younger lad noisy and full of angry words" (013-015) — sibling conflict as a stage of family development. The whole hamlet hears "a great quarrel over the new robe" (005-058), making private conflict into public spectacle. And neighbor disputes — "the man and his wife had had a mighty quarrel for that he had taken the last bit of money" (003-010) — circulate as news, binding the community through shared knowledge of each other's ruptures.

## Key Scenes

- The husband's "malicious pleasure" in quarreling, and the mother's incomprehension (004-005)
- The mother attacking the husband physically, then her anger dissolving into softness (003-008)
- The hamlet-wide quarrel over the blue robe, private conflict as public theater (005-058)
- Sibling quarrels in the household, the noisy generational transfer of conflict (013-015)
- The neighbor's quarrel over money, community knowledge of domestic rupture (003-010)

## Significance

Conflict in *The Mother* is never gratuitous. Every quarrel serves a narrative function: revealing character, testing relationships, providing the catharsis that makes continued endurance possible. The novel treats conflict not as moral failure but as a structural feature of life in close quarters under conditions of scarcity. What matters is not the absence of conflict but what happens after — whether the quarrel hardens into lasting rupture or dissipates into renewed intimacy, as when the mother's beating of the husband ends in tenderness. This is conflict not as tragedy but as the rough texture of living together.
