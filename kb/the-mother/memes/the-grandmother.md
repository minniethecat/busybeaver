## Summary

The grandmother is the husband's elderly mother — a "very old and weazened woman" wrapped in a bright red padded coat under a patched blue one, half-blind from a disease of the eyes that has "well-nigh sealed her eyelids together." She lives in the corner of the farmhouse kitchen, as close to the fire as she can creep, and talks "incessantly throughout the day" in a wheezing, toothless voice that never needs a listener to continue. She is at once a burden, a comic presence, and a beloved fixture of the household — the mother's affection for her is one of the novel's quiet revelations of character.

## Role

The grandmother serves a triple narrative function: **comic relief**, **generational continuity**, and **a test of the mother's character**.

As comic relief, her ceaseless monologue — about peas in rice, the superiority of a new-laid egg, the honest old days when "if you left a hoe out in the night it was there at dawn" — provides the novel's few moments of humor against its backdrop of poverty and labor. Her refrain "a useless old crone now, who ought to die" (001-011) is delivered with the confidence of someone who has no intention of dying anytime soon.

As generational continuity, she bridges the novel's present (the mother's laboring years) and its remembered past (the grandmother's own youth). Her presence anchors the household in time: the same kitchen, the same stove, the same cycle of work and sleep that the husband finds so unbearable has been her entire life.

As a test of character, her presence reveals the mother's genuine tenderness. When the cousin's wife suggests that the grandmother's death would be a relief, the mother demurs: "I hope she will live until the girl is bigger" (001-120). The mother treats the old woman as "but another child of hers" — feeding her first, fetching her water in the night, coaxing and coddling her through her pettish moods.

## Physical Description

The grandmother is introduced in striking detail: "a very old and weazened woman, wrapped in a thick padded coat of bright red cotton stuff, whose edges showed under a patched coat of blue she wore over it" (001-011). She is half-blind, her eyelids nearly sealed together, able to see only through small slits. She has no teeth, speaks through "sunken, toothless gums," and her hands are "chill" and "dry." When food arrives, she trembles with greediness and cannot find her spoon.

Her red burial coat — worn daily under her blue outer coat — is a running joke in the hamlet. She has worn out two such coats already, and when villagers call out "Are you still there then, old one?" she pipes back gaily, "Aye, here I be and my good grave clothes on me!" (001-122).

## Character Traits

- **Indefatigably talkative**: She talks "incessantly throughout the day" (001-021), her voice a steady background hum that others tune out but that never stops of its own accord.
- **Tough and long-lived**: She survived a fierce flux that killed two strong men, some women, and many children. When the family bought her best coffin, she "clung to her life and came back to it" (001-122).
- **Proud of her son**: When village gossip notes another man's gambling losses, she spits and declares "not in my house, thank the gods, for my son is very good at winning in a game!" (001-155).
- **Pettish but affectionate**: She frets about spoons and food, but clearly loves her daughter-in-law, calling her "daughter" and saving up all the day's thoughts to tell her when she returns from the fields.
- **Functionally useful**: Despite her frailty, she serves as door-watcher — her one remaining task, which she performs with fierce pride: "the door must be watched in these evil times with thieves and robbers everywhere" (001-015).

## Relationships

The grandmother is the mother of [[the-husband]], and her pride in him is unconditional — she seems not to notice his sullenness, hearing only the cheerfulness in his voice. The mother calls her "old mother" and treats her with a patient tenderness that surprises even the cousin's wife.

To [[the-mother]], the grandmother is "but another child" — "childish and wanting this and that as children do" (001-122). The mother feeds her first, fetches her water in the night, and genuinely grieves at the thought of her death.

To [[the-husband]], she is a familiar burden he barely notices. When he comes home surly, she hears only what she wants to hear, and he indulges her with a perfunctory "Aye, old mother, and so I do!" before ignoring her entirely.

The grandmother also watches over [[the-elder-son]] and [[the-daughter]] during the day while their parents labor in the fields, though her ability to do so is limited by her near-blindness.

## Key Passages

> "In the corner of the kitchen as near as she could creep to the fire sat a very old and weazened woman, wrapped in a thick padded coat of bright red cotton stuff, whose edges showed under a patched coat of blue she wore over it. She was half blind with a sore disease of the eyes, and this had well-nigh sealed her eyelids together." (001-011)

> "The red coat the young mother had made to bury her in she wore under a blue coat, as it was the custom to do in these parts, until it was worn and gone and the old woman fretted and was ill at ease until the mother had made it new again, and now she wore this second one merrily and if any called out, 'Are you still there then, old one?' she would pipe back gaily, 'Aye, here I be and my good grave clothes on me! A-wearing them out, I be, and I cannot say how many more I can wear out!'" (001-122)

> "The mother never had it in her heart to be hard on an old woman like that. Women she had heard who boasted of how they waged war in a house against their mothers-in-law... But to this young mother the old woman seemed but another child of hers." (001-122)

## Significance

The grandmother is the novel's most fully realized minor character — a figure who could easily have been a mere symbol of rural agedness but who instead emerges as a distinct, humorous, and ultimately moving presence. Her survival against all odds (the flux, her blindness, her age) mirrors the mother's own resilience, and the mother's care for her — unresentful, practical, even tender — is one of the novel's clearest demonstrations of its protagonist's moral quality. Pearl Buck uses the grandmother to ask, implicitly, what society owes its old, and to answer: in a peasant village, the answer is whatever the daughter-in-law can give.
