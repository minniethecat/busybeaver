# Marriage as Social Institution

In *The Mother*, **marriage** is never abstracted into ideology—it is simply the given structure of life, as natural and inescapable as the hills that circle the valley. Pearl S. Buck portrays the marriage of the central couple through its concrete particulars: the customs that shaped its formation, the household economy that sustains it, the gender roles that divide its labor, and the extended family that expands its obligations beyond the couple alone. The novel offers a granular portrait of marriage as an economic, social, and reproductive institution in rural China, where individual desire must negotiate with collective necessity.

## Custom and Formation

The marriage is governed by customs that Buck embeds casually in the narrative, as facts of life requiring no explanation. The age convention is noted in passing:

> He was but a lad still in some ways, and younger by some two years than his wife was, as the custom was in those parts, where it was held fitting for a man to be younger than his wife.
>
> (008-008)

This detail inverts Western expectations—the wife is older, more settled, the anchor of the household, while the husband retains a boyish irresponsibility well into fatherhood. The custom shapes the couple's dynamic: she is the steady force, he the restless spirit.

The mother's dowry, her three silver coins, represents the economic foundation a woman brings into marriage:

> Now those three coins were of good value and coins the mother had brought with her when she came to be wed, and her own mother had handed them to her for her own when she left her home. They were her precious possession and she had never found the hour when she could spend them.
>
> (010-010)

These coins are not merely money—they are her mother's legacy, her emergency reserve, her one piece of property that is hers alone. When the man seizes them to buy his blue robe, the violation is as much symbolic as economic: he has raided the very foundation of her security within the marriage.

The wedding itself is remembered only through the objects it brought into the house:

> Even in the house scarcely one new thing except the few small things that had been bought at the time of his wedding, a new teapot, the blue quilt upon the bed newly covered, new candlesticks and a new god of paper on the wall.
>
> (006-006)

The god of wealth, "a merry old man … his robes all red and blue and yellow," hangs on the wall but "had never brought wealth to this house." The wedding objects, like the marriage itself, promise prosperity but deliver only the same poverty, generation after generation.

## The Extended Family Household

Marriage in this world is not a union of two individuals but the formation of a new node in an extended family network. The old grandmother lives with the couple, and the mother's relationship with her mother-in-law is an essential dimension of the marriage:

> Women she had heard who boasted of how they waged war in a house against their mothers-in-law and how they would not bear the evil tempers of the elder women. But to this young mother the old woman seemed but another child of hers.
>
> (004-004)

The mother's unusual tenderness toward her mother-in-law—treating her as "another child" rather than as an adversary—distinguishes her from other women in the hamlet. The old woman, in turn, "loved" her daughter-in-law (001-001). This cross-generational bond between women, built on shared labor and proximity, is one of the novel's quiet affirmations: marriage creates kinship between women as well as between husband and wife.

## Gender and the Division of Labor

The marriage is structured by a sharp division of labor that Buck presents without sentimentality. The mother works the fields alongside the man but also bears children, cooks, cleans, spins, and tends the animals:

> To rise at dawn and feed her house and tend the beasts, to sow the land and reap its fruit, to draw water at the well for drink, to spend days upon the hills reaping the wild grass and know the sun and wind upon her, these were enough.
>
> (003-003)

The man's labor is lighter and more intermittent—he naps in the fields, he gambles at the inn, and he dreams of escape. The mother accepts this asymmetry as part of the order of things: "She knew that men cannot work as women do, but have the hearts of children always in them" (006-006).

Childbearing is the most consequential dimension of the marriage's gendered division. The mother conceives easily—"as easily as a sound and cleanly beast does" (001-001)—and the novel counts her pregnancies as facts of her body's fertility, not as choices. The man, by contrast, is "sullen and angered against her because she bore so easily" (008-008), resenting the children who consume resources he would rather spend on himself.

## The Cycle of Reproduction

The man perceives marriage as a trap precisely because of its reproductive logic:

> Even in the coming of the children his wife loved there was no new thing, for to him they were born the same and one was like another and all were to be clothed and fed, and when they were grown they must be wed in their turn and once more children born and all was the same, each day like to another, and there was no new thing.
>
> (006-006)

This is the institution seen from its bleakest angle: marriage as a machine for producing more marriages, an endless cycle of feeding, clothing, wedding, and birthing that extinguishes all possibility of novelty. The man's desire to escape is not just a personal failing but a response to the crushing repetitiveness that the institution imposes—especially on those who, unlike the mother, find no intrinsic satisfaction in its rhythms.

## Quarrel as Institutional Feature

The novel portrays marital quarrel not as a sign of dysfunction but as a normal and even healthy feature of the institution:

> So they quarreled heartily for a while and neither was the victor and neither vanquished, they were so well matched.
>
> (001-001)

The couple are "well matched"—evenly balanced in their capacity for argument. Quarrel provides release, reestablishes equilibrium, and paradoxically affirms the bond: "even quarreling with the man was good and set some edge upon their passion for each other" (003-003). But the quarrels also reveal the fault lines of power within the marriage. When the man wants something badly enough, he takes it—the silver coins, the money for the robe—and the mother, however fierce her anger, cannot stop him.
