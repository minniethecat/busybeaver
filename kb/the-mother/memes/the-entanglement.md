## Summary

The entanglement is the romantic-moral event at the emotional center of *The Mother* — the years-long pursuit of the abandoned mother by the landlord's agent, and her complex response to that pursuit. Beginning with the "accidental" touch of hands during the rent collection, the entanglement unfolds through purchased trinkets, charged glances, secret assignations, and the mother's internal war between desire and shame. It ends with the agent's departure to another province, leaving the mother with silver earrings and rings she can never publicly wear — material proof of a secret life that has no place in the hamlet's moral order.

## The First Touch

The entanglement begins at the rent collection, when the agent hands the mother a bowl of tea:

> The man stared at her and scented her heat and he turned hot and grave and when he took the bowl he touched his hand to hers as though not knowing her hand was there. But the woman felt the touch and caught its meaning in her blood like flame. (693-693)

The touch is presented as accidental — "as though not knowing her hand was there" — but both of them know it is deliberate. The mother's response is immediate and physical: the meaning catches "in her blood like flame."

## The Agent's Knowledge

The agent knows what has befallen the mother. He has heard the hamlet's gossip:

> The woman knew from the way he stared at her from under those heavy drooping lids that he had heard her tale and how her husband was gone out to other parts and never had come back. (687-687)

The narrator describes him as having "a dog's heart in him" — "such a man as could not look at any woman left alone and not wonder secretly what she was and how her heart was made and how her body was shaped" (687-687). His interest is predatory, but the mother's loneliness makes her vulnerable to it.

## The Waived Fee

In the first significant gesture of pursuit, the agent refuses his personal fee from the mother's harvest:

> "No, I will not have it! You are a lone woman now and your man gone from home and all this is your own labor. I will take no more of it than my landlord must have." (703-703)

He pushes the grain measure away, "his hand on hers while he did" (705-705). The boy rejoices at this apparent kindness, but the mother is "suddenly afraid in the midst of the sweet sick heat that was upon her" (705-705). She knows the waived fee is not generosity but investment.

## The Trinkets

The entanglement takes material form in the city, at a silversmith's counter. The agent follows the mother there, pretends not to know her, and buys silver earrings and rings:

> The silversmith made haste to add the rings, pleased at so fine a sale, and he said, "Then let them be for the new wife." But the man said no more but stood and stared and smoothed his lip. (765-765)

The mother watches this purchase "half jealous for the one he had bought the trinkets for, such things as she would have loved and in her girlhood had often longed to have" (765-765). The gifts are never openly given — they are slipped to her in secret, objects she must hide.

## The Burden of Secrecy

The trinkets become the mother's secret burden. She cannot wear them in the hamlet, and the gossip notices:

> "Where are those rings you said you have? Let me see what their pattern is." And the mother was often hard put to it and she said, "The silversmith is making them," or "I have put them in a certain place and I have forgot where they are for the moment." (765-765)

She constructs ever more elaborate excuses, culminating in the poignant fiction: "I have not the heart and I will put them on the first day he comes home" (765-765) — referring to her absent husband, even as the rings were bought by another man.

## The Agent's Visits

The agent finds excuses to return to the hamlet throughout the year, long after the rent collection is done:

> For this excuse and that he came back to the hamlet and now it was to make sure of some account which he thought he had written wrong, and now it was to complain that such a one had given a measure short... Most often of all he went to the cousin's house, which stood near the woman's. (717-717)

The cousin grows anxious at so many visits, suspecting "some very deep and evil purpose" (717-717). But "neither cousin nor cousin's wife saw how the secret eyes of the agent went sliding under his lids toward the woman across the way" (719-719).

## The Cooling and the End

The entanglement does not end with a dramatic break but with a gradual cooling. The agent "came no more throughout that whole summer until the grain was reaped when he must come, and he came hard and quarrelsome as he used to be" (891-891). At the harvest feast, when the women mock the mother, the agent looks at her with cold indifference:

> The agent, hearing the laughter and the witty sayings that arose against the woman, looked up carelessly and haughtily from his bowl... and he said carelessly and loud enough for her to hear, "What woman is it?" And his eyes fell on her scarlet face and he looked away as if he had never known her. (901-901)

The public disavowal is the entanglement's cruel conclusion. The agent departs to another province, leaving the mother with trinkets she can never wear and a secret that has no public acknowledgment.

## The Mother's Internal War

Throughout the entanglement, the mother wages an internal war. She is neither victim nor willing participant in any simple sense. Her desire is real — she has been starved of touch and recognition for years. Her shame is equally real — she knows what the hamlet would say if they knew. The entanglement exists in this moral twilight: a relationship that is predatory in its origins, mutual in its moments of passion, and ultimately devastating in its public denial.

## Significance

The entanglement is the novel's central exploration of female desire under constraint. It poses the question: what is a woman to do with her body and her longing when her husband has abandoned her, when the social order offers no legitimate path to remarriage, and when the man who pursues her is powerful, attractive, and dangerous? The trinkets — "such things as she would have loved and in her girlhood had often longed to have" — become the novel's symbols of desire that cannot be publicly owned.
