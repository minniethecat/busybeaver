# The Execution of the Younger Son

The **execution** of the younger son is the novel's climactic event—a public beheading that marks the violent intrusion of state power into the peasant family's world. After years as a wanderer who returns occasionally to charm his mother with tales and small gifts, the younger son is arrested as a communist, imprisoned in the [[the-gaol|gaol]] near the south gate, and executed alongside twenty other condemned prisoners. The mother makes an agonizing dawn journey to witness the death she is powerless to prevent.

## Arrest and Imprisonment

The event begins with a report from a cousin who saw the younger son being marched through the marketplace:

> My cousin said—he said he saw my brother this morning among many others, and his hands were tied behind him with hempen ropes and his clothes were rags and he was marching past the marketplace where my cousin had taken the grass to sell, and there was a long line of some twenty or thirty, and when my brother saw him he turned his eyes away—but my cousin asked and the guards who walked along said they were communists sent to gaol to be killed tomorrow.
>
> (013-005–007)

The crime is vague—the younger son was found distributing books that spoke of "overturning all the state and sharing all the money and the land alike" (014-022). His fate is sealed not by evidence but by his class: a gaoler explains that if he were "son of some great rich and mighty man then doubtless silver used could set him free," but "being a country lad and poor no man would put his life in danger for all that we could give" (014-023–024).

## The Mother's Response

The mother's reaction is fierce and immediate. She offers her entire life savings—coins hidden in a hole in the wall—to bribe the guards. When told the money was refused, she refuses to accept it:

> "Then he did not offer enough," the mother shouted. "Whoever heard of guards in a gaol who are not to be bribed? But I will go and fetch that money this moment. Yes, I will dig it up and take it, old as I am, and find my little son and bring him home."
>
> (013-016)

Her faith in money as a universal solvent—born from a lifetime in which everything, from harvest shares to coffin purchases, is negotiable—collides with the rigid machinery of state justice. The execution is not a transaction she can enter.

## The Public Spectacle

The execution is staged as a public event, drawing crowds from across the countryside:

> There were many waiting there, for it had been noised about the countryside that there would be this great beheading and many came to see it for a show and brought their children.
>
> (014-026)

The crowd's appetite for violence is described with unflinching clarity: "waiting hungrily, relishing in some strange way and hating, too, the horror that they craved to see" (014-026). The mother, old and leaning on a staff, is jostled among them. When the condemned prisoners march out singing, and the headsman's blade falls, the crowd disperses with gruesome details—"Saw you that lad whose red blood spurted out so far it poured upon the headsman's foot and made him curse?" (015-012).

## Aftermath

The mother is knocked to the ground by a guard who calls her "old hag" (015-012). She lies in the dust, then is carried home on the ass by the two men who accompanied her. The execution ends not with catharsis but with exhaustion—the mother "washed by her tears, and she went almost not knowing what she did" (014-024).

## Significance

The execution transforms state violence from an abstract concept into a visceral, personal catastrophe. The younger son's vague political involvement—books, ideas, the company of a bold young woman—is met with a response utterly disproportionate: public death by beheading. The event exposes the absolute asymmetry between peasant life and state power; the mother, who has survived hunger, abandonment, and the deaths of children, encounters a force that cannot be bargained with, endured, or outlasted.
