# The Distant City

The **city** in *The Mother* is never visited, never described from direct experience—yet it exerts a gravitational pull on the narrative. It exists beyond the circle of hills that encloses the [[the-hamlet|hamlet]], a place of impossible wealth and freedom that haunts the [[the-son|husband]]'s imagination and represents everything his life is not. The city is the antipode of [[rural-life|rural existence]]: where the hamlet is small and known, the city is vast and mysterious; where the farm demands endless [[work|labor]], the city promises easy money; where peasant life is defined by family obligation, the city offers anonymous freedom.

## The City of Imagination

The most vivid description of the city comes not from observation but from travelers' tales told at the wayside inn:

> There the river met the sea, they said, and there was a vast city full of people of many hues of skin, and money was easy come by with very little work for it and gaming houses everywhere and pretty singing girls in every gaming house, such girls as the men in this hamlet had never even seen and could not hope to see their lives long. Strange sights there were in that city, streets as smooth as threshing-floors and carts of every sort, houses tall as mountains and shops with windows filled with merchandise of all the world that ships brought there from over seas.
>
> (005-005)

This is the city as fantasy—a place where every desire can be satisfied, where labor is divorced from survival, where pleasure is abundant and freely available. The comparison of streets to threshing-floors reveals the peasant frame of reference: even wonders must be understood through the familiar.

## The City of Exploitation

But the city has a darker face. It is from the city that the landlord's agent comes to collect his share of the [[harvest]]:

> When he thought of this agent he could not bear it, for this townsman was such a one as he himself would fain have been, dressed in soft silk and his skin pale and fair and with that smooth oily look that townsmen have who work at some small, light task and are well fed.
>
> (005-005)

The agent embodies the city's parasitic relationship to the countryside. His soft skin and silk clothes are purchased with the surplus extracted from peasant labor. The husband both resents and envies him—the agent represents the life he wants, even as he despises the system that makes that life possible at his expense.

## The City and Modernity

The travelers' tales include wonders that strain the peasant imagination:

> And strangest thing of all was this, that in the great city all the night was light as day with a sort of lamp they had, not made with hands nor lit with any flame, but with some pure light that was caught from out of heaven.
>
> (005-005)

Electric light—a technology utterly beyond the experience of anyone in the hamlet—is described in almost religious terms. This passage captures the vast gulf between rural and urban China in the early twentieth century, a gulf not merely of distance but of entire worldviews.

## The Town as Intermediate

Between the hamlet and the distant city lies the local town—close enough to visit, far enough to feel different:

> And there were days when she went into the town with some grain or cabbage to sell, she and the man together, and there in the town were strange sights to see and think about if ever she had time to think at all.
>
> (003-003)

For the [[the-mother|mother]], the town is the limit of her world. She goes there to sell produce, not to dream. Her relationship to the urban is practical and transactional; the husband's is imaginative and longing.
