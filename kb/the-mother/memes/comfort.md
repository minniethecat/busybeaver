## Summary

Comfort in *The Mother* is never grand — it is the breast that is "soft and gave remembered comfort" (001-039), the hot tea for "the comfort of my belly" (004-027), the vague comfort of a child at the breast even when the milk has dried (007-003). In a novel organized around deprivation, comfort is the small counter-force: not the absence of suffering but its temporary suspension, the moment of relief that makes continued endurance possible.

## In the Novel

The novel's treatment of comfort is relentlessly physical. The infant receives "remembered comfort" from the mother's breast (001-039), a comfort that precedes language and persists in the body's memory. The old woman seeks "a little hot tea for the comfort of my belly" (004-027), comfort as warmth entering the body. Even when "her breasts were well-nigh dry by now" (007-003), the mother continues to nurse because "it gave her some vague comfort" — comfort as mutual, flowing in both directions.

Comfort is also precarious, easily lost. When the elder son leaves, "even that small comfort she had had was taken from her" (007-015). This small comfort — the child who needed her — was barely acknowledged while present, registered only in its absence. The novel understands comfort as something we notice mainly when it is gone.

Self-comfort is a recurring practice. The mother "comforted herself and said in her heart, 'If he comes we can always make more'" (005-087) while sewing clothes for the absent husband. The old woman is "comforted" by the assurance that her son "will be back tomorrow" (005-038), even though this assurance is false. Self-comfort in the novel is often self-deception — but it is also survival. The lies we tell ourselves to get through another day are themselves a form of comfort.

At its bleakest, the novel registers the failure of comfort. After the husband's final betrayal, "not even pride could comfort her this time" (009-029). In this failure, we see what comfort normally does — it provides an emotional fallback position, a way of reframing loss. When even this fails, the mother is left with only "the comfort of her food when she was there, the only comfort now" (009-047) — comfort reduced to the barest material minimum.

## Significance

Comfort in *The Mother* is the inverse of deprivation — not its opposite but its complement, the small brightness that defines the darkness by contrast. Buck never allows comfort to become sentimental: the comforts she depicts are physical (the breast, hot tea, food), temporary ("back tomorrow doubtless"), and often self-deceiving. Yet this very modesty is what gives comfort its moral weight. In a world where grand consolation is unavailable — no god intervenes, no justice arrives — the small comforts of the body and of domestic routine are what make life livable. The novel's realism about comfort is itself a kind of comfort: it does not promise more than it can deliver.
