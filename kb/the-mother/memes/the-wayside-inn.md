# The Wayside Inn

The **wayside inn** in *The Mother* is the hamlet's sole social establishment—a teahouse and gambling space kept by the husband's "fifth cousin" (004-033). It is where men gather after the day's labor, where travelers pause with tales of the distant world, and where the husband's restless longing for a life beyond the fields first takes shape.

## Description

The inn is a humble structure where "tables were set under a canopy of cloth upon the street, where travelers might pass" (004-033). It serves tea rather than alcohol primarily, functioning as both teahouse and informal gambling hall. The innkeeper's wife wears "two silver rings upon her hands and rings in her ears" (002-004)—a marker of relative prosperity that distinguishes the innkeeper's family from the farming households. The inn sits along the road through the hamlet, positioned to catch travelers and provide a space where "one heard a tale or two of this strange thing and that, and even perhaps some storyteller passed and told his tales" (004-033).

## Role in the Story

The inn serves three narrative functions. First, it is the husband's preferred escape from domestic and agricultural life. After quarrels with the mother, "he went away to the wayside inn and he sat there among the men and drank his tea and gambled far into the night" (004-028). The gambling table becomes a site of masculine autonomy where the rhythms of planting and [[harvest]] do not apply.

Second, the inn is where the outside world enters the hamlet. Travelers stopping there tell "of strange and wonderful things beyond the circle of the hills"—the great city at the river's mouth with "gaming houses everywhere and pretty singing girls" and streets "as smooth as threshing-floors" (004-032). These tales kindle the husband's discontent: "it seemed to him a thing not to be borne forever that there was to be no new thing for him, and only this wheel of days, year upon year, until he grew old and died" (004-032). The inn thus becomes the narrative engine of the husband's eventual abandonment.

Third, the inn is a marker of social reputation. The mother takes pride that her husband, unlike other men, does not lose money gambling there: "it was in truth a pride to her that when other women complained aloud of their own men and how the scanty earnings of the land were lost at that table in the inn, she need not complain" (004-018).

## Associated Characters

- **[[the-husband|The Husband]]** — his primary social space outside the home; the inn's tales of the city fuel his departure
- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]** — never enters the inn herself but monitors it; asks her son, "Saw you your father on the street or in the inn?" (005-029)
- **The innkeeper** — the husband's fifth cousin, keeper of the hamlet's social center

## Significance

The wayside inn represents the threshold between the hamlet's closed agricultural world and the wider world of mobility, chance, and possibility. It is a masculine counter-space to the domestic sphere of the farmhouse—a place where the husband can be a man among men rather than a farmer bound to land. The inn's travelers' tales function as temptation narratives, offering visions of a life unmoored from the "wheel of days" that traps the husband. In the novel's moral geography, the inn is neither condemned nor celebrated—it is simply the place where dreams of elsewhere are kindled, and where those dreams begin their slow destructive work.
