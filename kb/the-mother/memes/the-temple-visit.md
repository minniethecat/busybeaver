## Summary

Religious practice in the novel takes the form of temple visits, shrine worship, and domestic offerings to household gods. These ritual events punctuate the agricultural and life-cycle calendar, serving as occasions for prayer, expiation, and the seeking of divine intervention in human troubles. The mother's relationship with the gods is pragmatic, personal, and morally complex.

## In the Narrative

Religious observance appears first in the context of the New Year festival, when the mother buys "two red candles and incense to burn before the god and red letters of luck to paste upon the tools" (503). This domestic ritual blends ancestor veneration, luck-seeking, and agricultural blessing into a single act.

The Confucian temple serves a secular function as the site where the letter writer keeps his booth (519), suggesting the interweaving of sacred space with commercial life. Religion is accessible, quotidian, not set apart.

The most dramatic religious event occurs at the wayside shrine, where the mother meets the landlord's agent for their secret rendezvous. As she enters, she sees "the three gods in the shrine, the chief a staid old man staring straight ahead of him, and by his side two small attendants, little, decent gods of the wayside for those who paused in their journey for worship or for shelter" (834). In a gesture of profound moral complexity, the mother covers the gods' faces with her garment so they cannot witness what follows.

Later, when the mother seeks an abortion, she returns to this same shrine, drinking the brew and lying "down upon the ground" (995) in agony, careful not to scream lest a passerby notice "an unaccustomed light in that shrine" (997). The shrine thus becomes the site of the novel's most secret and desperate acts.

At the novel's end, the mother determines to make a pilgrimage to "a temple far away, a whole ten miles and more, where she had heard there was a kind and potent goddess who heard women when they prayed bitterly" (1114), seeking divine help for her daughter's failing eyesight.

## Cultural Context

Folk religion in the novel is syncretic and practical. Gods are approached for specific favors — luck, healing, protection — and the mother's moral relationship with them is transactional. She covers their eyes when she sins and petitions them when she needs help, without apparent concern for theological consistency.

## Significance

Religious events in the novel reveal the mother's pragmatic spirituality and the moral flexibility that survival demands. The wayside shrine, in particular, becomes a sacred space hollowed out by human need — simultaneously a place of worship, of illicit love, and of desperate medical intervention.
