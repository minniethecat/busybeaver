# The Wayside Shrine

The **wayside shrine** in *The Mother* is a small, ruined sacred structure on the winding path between the hamlet and the fields. Unlike the grand distant temple, this shrine is proximate and quotidian—a place passed daily, housing "three gods": "the chief a staid old man staring straight ahead of him, and by his side two small attendants, little, decent gods of the wayside for those who paused in their journey for worship or for shelter" (008-030).

## Description

The shrine is "small and ruined" (008-027), windowless, its interior a twilight space. It stands at a turn in the path where it "turned past" the shrine, making it a marker on the daily route between domestic and agricultural space. The three gods within are described as modest—"little, decent gods of the wayside" (008-030)—not the gilded figures of the great temple but humble deities for travelers seeking momentary shelter or blessing. The shrine's ruin and obscurity make it a place of concealment, suited for acts that must remain hidden from the hamlet's collective gaze.

## Role in the Story

The wayside shrine is the setting for two of the novel's most consequential secret events. First, on the night of her liaison with the landlord's agent, the mother pauses at the shrine: "She went under such a sky, along the little winding turn the path took where it turned past a small and ruined shrine, and there in the door of the shrine [[the-son|the man]] stood, waiting" (008-027). Inside the "twilight of the windowless shrine" (008-028), she follows him—and afterward, overcome with shame, she throws her garment over the gods' heads, covering "their staring eyes" (008-030) so they cannot witness her sin.

Second, when she discovers she is pregnant from that encounter, the mother returns to the same shrine for a secret abortion. The cousin's wife prepares a brew, and "to that wayside shrine the two women went, and the mother drank the brew and she lay down upon the ground, and waited" (010-037). In the darkness, "she remembered not to scream" (010-038), enduring agony in silence so no passerby would see a light in the abandoned shrine.

The shrine haunts the mother thereafter. She resolves never to go to "that wayside shrine nor ever to those gods whose faces she had covered" (012-025), choosing the distant temple instead. Years later, she still fears "that old god in the shrine smelled the sin about him and told the goddess somehow" (018-016).

## Associated Characters

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]** — commits her deepest transgression here and bears its psychological weight for decades
- **[[the-cousins-wife|The Cousin's Wife]]** — the only person who knows what happens at the shrine, having helped with the abortion (010-035)

## Significance

The wayside shrine embodies the novel's treatment of sacred space as witness rather than sanctuary. The gods in the shrine do not protect or forgive—they stare, and their staring must be covered. The mother's gesture of throwing her garment over their heads is an act of desperate self-protection that paradoxically deepens her isolation: she can never confess to gods she has silenced. The shrine becomes a private theater of concealment, where the mother contends with forces—desire, shame, bodily consequence—that the hamlet's public morality cannot accommodate.
