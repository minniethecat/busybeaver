## Summary

Rent collection is the annual socioeconomic event that punctuates the agricultural calendar of the hamlet. After each harvest, the landlord's agent arrives to measure out the landlord's share of the grain and collect his own private fee. The event encompasses the formal grain measurement on the threshing-floor, the obligatory village feast, and the power dynamics between tenant farmers and the absentee landlord's representative. For the mother, the rent collection becomes the site of her first charged encounter with the agent — an encounter that will grow into a years-long entanglement.

## The Structure of Obligation

The hamlet's farmers own little land of their own. Most is rented from a landlord "who lived in pleasure in some far city" (180-180). The harvest is never fully theirs:

> Even the harvests were not his own, for he must measure out a share to that landlord and he must take another share and fee the townsman who was the landlord's agent. (180-180)

This system of extraction is the economic foundation of the narrative. Every year, the farmers must give "the landlord's share of all the harvest" (459-459), plus a private fee to the agent — "a fat fowl or two or a measure of rice or some eggs or even silver" (463-463).

## The Agent

The agent who arrives for the rent collection is "a tall man dressed in a silk robe of gray, and his face square and large and handsome in its bold way" (687-687). The tenants both hate and fear him:

> In spite of his forced courtesy and his free words the tenants hated him, and they feared him because he had a high hard temper and this big body and two large, swift fists that he clenched and held hard against his thighs if any argued against what he said. (687-687)

Yet "often they laughed at him, too, for if they gave him his fee without quarrel, he made a joke or two to salve the taking" (687-687). He is a man of power who knows how to wield charm as well as threat.

## The Mother's First Harvest Alone

The first rent collection after the husband's departure marks a critical transition. The mother reaps the harvest alone, with only the cousin's help:

> The woman reaped it alone then except for two days when the cousin came and helped her when his own rice was cut and bound in sheaves. (445-445)

When the day arrives for the grain division, the cousin warns her: "the new agent is a wily, clever man, and of a sort ill for a woman to do with alone" (445-445). She waits at her door "the grain heaped on the threshing-floor and waiting" (459-459).

## The First Touch

During the grain measurement, the agent's gaze lingers on the mother. He offers her tea, and "being so near his laughing eyes she could not but look into them, and there was that great, greedy, starving heart of hers showing in her own eyes without her knowledge that it did" (693-693):

> The man stared at her and scented her heat and he turned hot and grave and when he took the bowl he touched his hand to hers as though not knowing her hand was there. But the woman felt the touch and caught its meaning in her blood like flame. (693-693)

This touch — "as though not knowing her hand was there" — initiates the entanglement that will define years of the mother's secret life.

## The Feast

After the grain is measured, "the village must set a feast before the agent and every house must give a dish" (465-465). The mother kills and cooks a fowl, steaming it until "the flesh so tender that when the first chopsticks touched it it would fall apart" (465-465). The children "hung about the kitchen and the boy cried, 'I wish it were for us — I wish we ever could eat a fowl ourselves!'" (465-465). The feast ritual dramatizes the inequality at the heart of the rent system — the best food goes not to those who labor but to the agent who collects.

## The Waived Fee

In a gesture charged with unspoken meaning, the agent refuses his personal fee from the mother:

> "No, I will not have it! You are a lone woman now and your man gone from home and all this is your own labor. I will take no more of it than my landlord must have, or blame me if he does not. I will take no fee from you, goodwife." (703-703)

The boy cries out "A good man, mother, not to take his fee!" (709-709) — but the mother knows the waiver is not kindness but investment, a gesture that binds her to him.

## Significance

The rent collection is the novel's central economic ritual. It is where class power becomes personal, where the structural inequality of the tenant-landlord relationship is enacted in grain measures and feasts and private fees. For the mother, it is where her vulnerability as an abandoned woman intersects with the agent's predatory attention — and where her loneliness makes her receptive to a touch she knows she should refuse.
