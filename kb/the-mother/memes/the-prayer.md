# Prayer as Ritual Event

**Prayer** in the mother's world is an event—a deliberate journey to sacred space, an offering made, a petition raised. The novel presents prayer not as daily devotion but as a crisis response, undertaken when ordinary remedies have failed.

## The Temple Prayer for Fertility

The most detailed prayer event involves the young wife's desperate attempt to conceive:

> Now here was the young wife's grief, that though she had been wed near upon five years she had no child yet and not a sign of one, and she had gone secretly to a temple to pray and had done all she knew and still her body stayed as barren as it had been.
>
> (016-016)

The prayer is "secret"—a private act of desperation, not a communal ritual. The phrase "had done all she knew" underscores the prayer as a last resort after all folk remedies have been exhausted. The failure of prayer to produce a child introduces doubt into the efficacy of divine intervention.

## The Mother's Abandoned Faith

The mother's own relationship with prayer was severed by the failure of the gods to heal her [[the-daughter|daughter]]'s blindness:

> As for the girl, the mother loved her well but always with pain, for there the blind eyes were always for a reproach and she never could forget that the goddess had not heard her prayer nor had the mother ever heart to pray again, fearing now that it was her own sin come down in some way worse than she could bear because it fell upon her child.
>
> (013-018)

The unanswered prayer for her daughter's eyes becomes a permanent rupture in the mother's relationship with the divine. The blindness is a "reproach"—a daily reminder that prayer failed. The mother's conclusion that it must be "her own sin" visiting punishment on her child reflects the folk theology of inherited guilt.

## The Ritual of Incense

When the mother does return to prayer for her [[the-son|younger son]] imprisoned, she follows the prescribed ritual forms:

> There the mother went and stood and waited while an old bent woman muttered her prayers for a son who could not move and had lain these many years, she too, like her son, imprisoned in her life.
>
> (012-031)

The temple is a gathering place for the desperate—an old woman praying for a paralyzed son, the mother praying for an imprisoned son. Prayer events connect strangers through shared helplessness.

> So the mother rose at last and sighed most heavily, not knowing what her prayer was worth, and she lit her incense and went away again.
>
> (012-033)

The uncertainty—"not knowing what her prayer was worth"—is the emotional core of prayer in the novel. The incense is lit, the ritual observed, but the mother has no confidence in its efficacy. Prayer becomes an event of uncertain meaning, performed because there is nothing else to do.
