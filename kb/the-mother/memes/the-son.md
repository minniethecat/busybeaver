## Summary

The Son is the husband of the Mother and the sole son of the Old Woman. A vigorous young farmer, he works the family's land by day and enjoys singing, gaming, and idle pleasures by night. He is quick-tempered and passionate — well-matched to his equally spirited wife — yet beneath his mercurial moods lies a deep dependence on the domestic order she maintains.

## Role in the Story

The Son appears only briefly in the opening chapters, yet his presence looms over the household. He labors in the fields from dawn to dusk — "pressing the earth tight over the rows with his bare feet" (001-005) — and returns home famished, demanding his meal. The narrative presents him through [[the-mother|the Mother]]'s eyes: she sees him as both a source of exasperation and a figure to be cared for and fed.

His relationship with the Mother is defined by passionate quarrels that leave "neither the victor and neither vanquished, they were so well matched" (001-011). When their first son was born, he roared at her for stopping too often to nurse: "Here be you, but just begun your bearing and for these next twenty years shall you be suckling one or another, and am I to bear this?" (001-011). Yet the Mother's interior monologue reveals that their quarrels are "only of the lips" and that "well she loved to see him eat."

He is a singer — his "voice high and quivering and clear" — who knows "many a song" and is often asked to perform at the teashop on holidays (001-038). This musicality hints at a liveliness beyond the grinding routine of farm labor, and it provides the Mother with one of her quiet pleasures: watching him approach the house at evening, singing softly.

## Key Relationships

- **The Mother (wife)**: His partnership is passionate, quarrelsome, and interdependent. She feeds him, argues with him, shares his bed, and bears his children. He depends on her utterly for the running of the household and the raising of the children.
- **The Old Woman (mother)**: He is her sole son, and she dotes on him. When he returns home, she immediately begins speaking of his preferences — "my son likes a little peas mixed in his rice" (001-038). He responds with easy, affectionate tolerance: "Aye, old mother, and so I do!"
- **The Cousin**: A more industrious neighbor who works "even into the night… weaving a basket." The Son's awareness of this harder-working relative pricks at him, but he dismisses the comparison in favor of rest or gaming.

## Character Development

The Son is introduced as a somewhat immature figure — prone to petulance, quick to anger, eager for distraction. When the Mother fixes him with "that steadfast, angry look" as he prepares to go gaming, he "shook himself petulantly as a child does" and makes an excuse: "I am too weary to game tonight!" (001-038). This boyish quality coexists with his physical vigor as a laborer, suggesting a man not yet fully matured into the responsibilities of fatherhood and husbandry.

## Notable Appearances

- **Chapter I**: Returning from the fields at sunset, "his hoe over his shoulder," singing as he walks. He eats voraciously — "thrice the mother rose and filled the man's bowl" (001-038)
- **Chapter I**: The quarrel over breastfeeding — a flashback revealing the tensions of early marriage, when he complained about her stopping work to nurse their firstborn (001-011)
- **Chapter I**: The evening scene — he considers going to the inn for gaming, meets the Mother's silent disapproval, and retires to bed instead, muttering about the emptiness of a life of "work and sleep — work and sleep" (001-038)

## Significance

The Son represents the traditional rural Chinese husband — the laborer whose life is work and whose pleasures are simple: food, song, gaming, and conjugal comfort. His character illuminates the gender dynamics of the household: he is nominally the head, yet the Mother's will often prevails through unspoken pressure. His dependence on her domestic labor — for food, child-rearing, and emotional equilibrium — makes visible the invisible work that sustains peasant family life.
