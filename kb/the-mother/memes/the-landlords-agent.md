## Summary

The landlord's agent is the human face of the economic system that oppresses the hamlet's farmers. He lives in town, wears soft silk, and has the "smooth oily look that townsmen have who work at some small, light task and are well fed" (001-073). He never appears on stage — we encounter him only through the husband's resentful imagination and the cousin's wary warning. But his offstage presence shapes the novel's economic reality: every harvest, a share goes to the landlord through this agent, and every farmer must deal with him.

## Role

The agent represents the **extractive economic order** that keeps the peasants poor. The landlord himself "lived in pleasure in some far city" (001-073) — remote and indifferent — but the agent is the tangible intermediary who comes to measure the grain and take the share. Through him, the novel dramatizes the structural inequality of the tenant-farming system without needing to depict the landlord directly.

The cousin's warning about the agent — that he is "a wily, clever man, and of a sort ill for a woman to do with alone" (001-445) — positions the agent as a specific threat to the mother after her husband's disappearance. She must face him without a man to negotiate on her behalf, and the cousin's offer to stand beside her is motivated by this concern.

## Character Traits

- **Townsman refinement**: Dressed in "soft silk," with "skin pale and fair" — the physical opposite of the sun-darkened farmers he collects from.
- **False heartiness**: The mother "knew the agent but a little, since he was new in the last years to those parts, and a townsman who had a false heartiness in all he did and said" (001-445). His friendliness is performative, a professional mask.
- **Wily and clever**: The cousin, not given to exaggeration, describes him as "wily" and warns that he is particularly dangerous for a woman to negotiate with alone — suggesting he exploits asymmetries of gender as well as class.
- **An object of class resentment**: The husband's bitterest thoughts fix on the agent: "he could not bear it, for this townsman was such a one as he himself would fain have been" (001-073). The agent embodies everything the husband wants and cannot have.

## Relationships

The agent's relationship to [[the-husband]] is one of class antagonism mixed with envy. The husband cannot bear the sight of the agent precisely because the agent lives the easy life the husband craves.

To [[the-mother]], the agent is a practical problem to be managed. The cousin's warning suggests the agent may try to cheat a lone woman, and the mother's ability to face him successfully is one measure of her growth into self-reliance.

To [[the-cousin]], the agent is a known quantity — wily and clever — and the cousin's offer to help the mother deal with him is the cousin's most significant action in the novel.

## Key Passages

> "If he is not come when the time is here to divide the grain with the landlord, I will help you then, for the new agent is a wily, clever man, and of a sort ill for a woman to do with alone." (001-445)

> "She knew the agent but a little, since he was new in the last years to those parts, and a townsman who had a false heartiness in all he did and said." (001-445)

## In the Narrative

The agent appears in the novel's imagination before he appears in person. The husband broods on him as the symbol of an unjust system; the cousin warns about him as a specific danger to a husbandless woman. When the agent finally comes to the mother's door to measure the landlord's share of the harvest (001-445 and following), the scene is both a practical negotiation and a test of the mother's capacity to face the world of men and money alone.

## Significance

The landlord's agent is the novel's most direct representation of the extractive economic structures that hem in peasant life. Unlike the landlord, who is distant and abstract, the agent is physically present, personally known, and personally dangerous. His "false heartiness" marks him as a figure of the modern economy — the professional intermediary who profits from both sides of a transaction. In a novel otherwise populated by peasants whose relationships are governed by kinship, neighborliness, and mutual aid, the agent represents a different kind of relationship entirely: the cash nexus.
