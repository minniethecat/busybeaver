# Time and Cyclical Temporality

Time in *The Mother* is not linear but cyclical — governed by the agricultural year, the rhythms of childbirth, and the repetitive tasks of peasant survival. Pearl S. Buck structures the novel so that the reader experiences time as the mother does: not as a progression toward goals but as a series of returns. Planting follows harvest, pregnancy follows birth, and the decades accumulate not through dramatic events but through the slow sedimentation of days.

## Agricultural Time

The most fundamental temporal rhythm in the novel is the agricultural cycle. Spring planting, summer growth, autumn harvest, winter dormancy — these are not metaphors but material realities that dictate every aspect of the mother's life:

> The land changes from seedtime to harvest and there is this new thing to do and that.
>
> (001-001)

The mother's consciousness is attuned to these cycles with a precision that city dwellers cannot match. She knows "these warm days and these soft damp nights" (001-001) and what they demand. She knows how long the fuel will last, when the beans must be dropped, when the rice must be harvested. This knowledge is not intellectual but embodied — acquired through years of working the same fields, cooking at the same stove, living in the same house.

Agricultural time is also communal time. The whole hamlet plants together, harvests together, and rests together. The synchronization of labor creates a shared temporal experience that binds the community. When the mother falls out of step — through illness, through the husband's absence — she feels the dislocation acutely.

## Narrative Rhythm

Buck mirrors cyclical temporality in her narrative technique. The novel does not follow a tight plot with rising action and climax; instead, it proceeds through a series of episodes separated by temporal gaps. Chapters often begin with phrases like "Some seven days after" or "When spring came again," skipping over the uneventful intervals.

This narrative rhythm produces a distinctive reading experience. Time feels both compressed — years pass in a paragraph — and expansive, because the repetitive texture of daily life is rendered in such rich sensory detail. The reader, like the mother, becomes attuned to small changes within the cycle: a child who has grown, a new line on a face, a field that yields slightly less than the year before.

## Generational Time

The novel spans enough years that the mother moves from young wife to aging matriarch. Generational time is measured in children: their births, their growth, their departures. The mother counts years by reference to her offspring:

> She was in her forty and third year, and when she counted on her fingers sometimes in the night how many years her children's father had been gone, she could scarcely believe the sum her fingers made.
>
> (013-013)

This passage captures the strange experience of time in a life of repetition: the days are long but the years are short. The mother cannot quite believe how much time has passed because each day has been so similar to the one before. Only by counting — literally, on her fingers — can she grasp the scale of her own history.

The generational cycle also brings the novel's deepest sadnesses. Children who were once held at the breast grow up and leave for cities the mother will never see. The novel ends not with closure but with continuation — the next generation beginning its own cycle of departure and return.

## Waiting

Much of the mother's temporal experience is waiting: for the husband to return, for the harvest to ripen, for the children to come home, for the seasons to turn. Waiting is not passive in this world — it is filled with labor — but it shapes the emotional texture of the mother's life:

> Nevertheless, there was the hope of the seven days when his money might be gone.
>
> (006-006)

The "seven days" is a unit of temporal hope — a manageable span that the mother can hold in her mind. Longer spans — years, decades — are too vast to grasp. Time is broken into pieces small enough to endure: the week, the day, the hour before the rice is done.

This fragmentation of time is a survival strategy. By focusing on the immediate — the next meal, the next row of beans, the next sunrise — the mother avoids being overwhelmed by the scale of her circumstances. Time, in this sense, is a resource she manages as carefully as fuel or food.

## The Persistence of the Present

For all the novel's attention to cycles and generations, its deepest temporal truth may be this: the mother lives almost entirely in the present tense. She does not dwell on the past (though it haunts her at night) and she does not plan for a future very different from the present. Her consciousness is absorbed in the immediate task — feeding the fire, dropping the beans, wiping a child's eyes.

This present-mindedness is not a limitation but a form of wisdom. In a world where the future is largely predetermined by poverty and gender and tenancy, the present is the only domain where the mother can exercise agency. By living fully in each moment, she accumulates a life that, in retrospect, is extraordinary in its resilience:

> She relished all her life: giving birth, the labor on the land, eating and drinking and sleeping.
>
> (001-001)

"Relished" — the word is key. The mother does not merely endure; she tastes her life, moment by moment, finding satisfaction in work well done, food well cooked, children well fed. Her relationship to time is, in the end, a relationship to the present — the only tense in which a peasant woman can truly live.
