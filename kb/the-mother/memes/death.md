# Death

In *The Mother*, **death** is not a distant abstraction but a constant, intimate presence woven into the fabric of daily life. From the novel's opening page, where the old grandmother announces she "ought to die" (001-001), to the final chapters where the mother contemplates suicide and the husband casually declares his wife dead, mortality shapes the emotional and social landscape of the hamlet. Buck presents death without sentimentality—as a fact of peasant existence, a community event, and a moment that reveals character.

## The Old Woman's Long Dying

The novel's richest treatment of death centers on the old grandmother, whose relationship with mortality spans the entire narrative. From the first scene, she speaks of dying as both a burden and an identity:

> "A useless old crone now, who ought to die—"
>
> (001-001)

Yet the old woman does not die. She survives an epidemic that kills "two strong men and some women and many little children" (001-001), wearing out two burial shrouds in the process. The hamlet treats her longevity as a running joke:

> "Aye, here I be and my good grave clothes on me! A-wearing them out, I be, and I cannot say how many more I can wear out!"
>
> (001-001)

The shroud becomes the organizing symbol of her relationship to death—each red burial coat worn as everyday clothing until threadbare, each one a marker of time won against mortality.

## The Third Shroud

The old woman's death, when it finally comes, is staged as a dramatic race between the mother's needle and the failing breath. Struck by a chill after falling asleep in the sun, the old woman refuses to die until her promised third shroud is finished:

> "I will not die till it is made and on—"
>
> (017-017)

The mother digs up her hidden silver to buy the finest red cloth. She sews through the night while the entire hamlet stays awake:

> In one day and a part of the night it was done, and the cousin and the cousin's wife stood by to see it and a neighbor or two, and indeed the whole hamlet did not sleep, but stayed awake to wonder if the mother would win that race, or death.
>
> (017-017)

The old woman dies "triumphantly" (017-017) the moment the scarlet robes are drawn onto her body—a death on her own terms, having outlasted two shrouds and earned a third.

## Infant and Child Mortality

Death in the novel is especially present among children. The mother herself lost an infant:

> If that one had not come too soon, and died when it had breathed but once, it would have been a fair boy by now, too, and trying to walk soon.
>
> (001-001)

The epidemic brings wider loss: "two strong men died and some women and many little children" (001-001). The hamlet's relationship to child death is pragmatic rather than sentimental—the girl's chronically sealed eyes are dismissed by the man with "she can never die of it" (001-001), and the old woman reassures: "So was I when I was a child, and I never died of it!" (001-001). Death is a threshold measured in practical consequence, not existential weight.

## Death as Escape and Erasure

For the mother, death becomes entangled with shame and desperation. After the secret encounter leaves her pregnant with no husband to claim the child, she contemplates suicide:

> It came to her often and wildly that she would hang herself there upon the bedpost. But yet she could not. There were her own good children, and she looked upon their sleeping faces and she could not, and she could not bear to think of the neighbors' looks on her dead body when they searched her for her cause of death.
>
> (017-017)

What stays her hand is not fear of death itself but fear of posthumous exposure—the hamlet would discover her pregnancy and piece together the truth. Death would not erase her shame; it would publish it.

In a crueler register, the man weaponizes death when he tells another woman:

> "But they are not for a wife—the wife I had died a six-month ago."
>
> (016-016)

With four casual words, he declares the mother dead to claim freedom. The phrase "the wife I had" reduces her to a past-tense possession, easily discarded.

## The Wish for Honorable Widowhood

At her lowest, the mother wishes the man were actually dead rather than simply absent:

> If she could have forgotten the man and so finished with him, if he were dead and she had seen him buried in the earth and still and gone forever, if she could have been a widow and known her life with the man ended, it would have been easier for her.
>
> (014-014)

A dead husband would give her a socially legible identity—the virtuous widow, steadfast and true—instead of the ambiguous shame of a woman abandoned but not released. Death, in this calculus, would be cleaner than uncertainty.
