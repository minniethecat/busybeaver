# The Wedding of the Elder Son

The **wedding** of [[the-elder-son|the elder son]] is the novel's most detailed depiction of traditional marriage ritual. It marks a turning point for [[the-mother]], who must cede her position as sole mistress of the household to a younger woman. The event unfolds through geomancer consultation, bridal procession, and the mother's private reckoning with her own past.

## The Geomancer's Consultation

The marriage begins not with the couple but with a geomancer, who compares the birth dates of the prospective bride and groom to determine their compatibility:

> So was it done, and the days of the month and the years in which the two were born and the hours of their birth were compared upon a geomancer's table in the town, and they were all favorable. The young man was born under the sign of horse, and the maid was born under the sign of cat, which do not devour each other, and thus was harmony foretold in the marriage.
>
> (014-001–002)

With the signs found favorable, [[the-mother]] proceeds to arrange the wedding with all required ritual items, demonstrating her commitment to [[family-duty|familial obligation]] despite the personal cost.

## The Bridal Procession

The mother spares no expense for the proper ritual trappings, hiring the red marriage chair and procuring the bride's ceremonial garments:

> And the mother put forth more silver and hired the red marriage chair and the bead crown and the earrings of false pearls and all that was needful for the day, and especially the trousers of red which every bride must wear in those parts. So was the marriage day set and it drew on, and dawned at last, a clear cold day in the winter of that year.
>
> (014-003)

The red trousers, bead crown, and marriage chair are the essential symbols of a proper rural wedding, signaling that the family has not skimped on ritual propriety despite their [[poverty]].

## The Mother's Reckoning

Standing at her door to welcome the bridal chair, [[the-mother]] is overcome by a sense of displacement and recognition. She sees her own younger self in the arriving bride:

> Now was it a strange day for this mother when she must welcome to her house a new and younger woman, so long had she been master there as well as mistress. When she was dressed in her best and stood waiting at her door, when she saw the red bridal chair come near with its burden of the bride within, it seemed suddenly but a little while ago when she herself had come in that same chair, and the old woman dead stood where she stood today and her own man where her son stood.
>
> (014-004–005)

In this moment, the cyclical nature of women's lives in [[the-hamlet]] becomes visible: the mother once displaced [[the-old-woman|her own mother-in-law]], and now she is displaced in turn. This cycle of generational succession is one of the novel's most quietly devastating observations about [[rural-life|peasant existence]].

## Wedding Customs and Hamlet Gossip

The wedding unfolds according to local custom. The bride's family returns the ceremonial cock with a hen, and the village women coax the reluctant bride from her chair:

> The first of the procession came, the small wedding fruits, the cock she had sent to the bride's house and that according to custom they sent back and with it a hen they mated to it, and after these few things, the chair was fetched and set down there before the door and the cousin's wife and the gossip and the other elder women of the hamlet took the bride's hand and tried to pull her forth. And she was proper and reluctant and came at last but most unwillingly, and when she did come she made her eyes downcast and did not look up once.
>
> (014-006–007)

The mother withdraws to [[the-cousin]]'s house, as custom dictates that a daughter-in-law must not see her husband's mother too easily, "lest she do not fear her thereafter" (014-008). From her hiding place, she listens to the hamlet's judgment of the new bride's quality—her sewing skills, her earnest look, her thinness.

## Displacement

At night, [[the-mother]] sleeps on [[the-old-woman]]'s pallet while the new couple occupies the bed that was once hers. The physical displacement mirrors her emotional one:

> It was not easy either for the mother to give up to this new pair that place which had been hers and her man's, and it made her seem old to herself at night to lie on the old woman's pallet. Through the day she could be usual, busy everywhere, commanding all, her tongue quick to correct and command, but at night she was old.
>
> (014-009–010)
