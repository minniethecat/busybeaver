# The Funeral of the Grandmother

The **funeral** of the grandmother is the novel's central [[death]] ritual, structured around a recurring motif: the three burial robes (shrouds). The [[the-old-woman|old woman]] outlives two shrouds and dies triumphantly upon receiving her third, transforming her death from tragedy into a testament of stubborn vitality. The event also serves as the occasion for [[the-mother]]'s most significant release of grief in the narrative.

## The Three Shrouds

The grandmother's relationship with death is defined by her burial garments. From the novel's opening scene, she wears a red shroud beneath her patched blue coat, as local custom dictates that the elderly prepare for death by wearing their burial clothes in life:

> The old woman had worn out two burial robes, the mother was glad to have her live. It was a joke in the whole hamlet to see how the old life hung on. The red coat the young mother had made to bury her in she wore under a blue coat, as it was the custom to do in these parts, until it was worn and gone and the old woman fretted and was ill at ease until the mother had made it new again, and now she wore this second one merrily and if any called out, "Are you still there then, old one?" she would pipe back gaily, "Aye, here I be and my good grave clothes on me! A-wearing them out, I be, and I cannot say how many more I can wear out!"
>
> (001-002–003)

The shroud becomes a marker of the grandmother's improbable endurance—each worn-out burial robe signals not death's approach but life's stubborn persistence. Throughout [[the-hamlet]], the old woman's longevity in the face of death becomes a source of communal amusement and wonder.

## A Promise from the Absent Son

When [[the-mother]] receives a letter from her absent husband, she instructs the letter-writer to include a promise that will delight the old woman:

> "Say, 'Tell my old mother I shall bring red stuff for her third shroud when I come, as good stout stuff as can be bought.'"
>
> (006-005)

The grandmother's joy at this promise becomes a recurring motif. She boasts to neighbors, "I swear my son is just such a man as she says, and he is to buy me my third shroud and of the best town stuff" (006-007). The promised third shroud becomes a symbol of [[family-duty|filial piety]] from a son who otherwise contributes nothing.

## The Deathbed

When the grandmother's end finally comes, her last conscious act is to pluck at her patched shroud, signaling her final desire:

> The mother hastened to her and she saw there was a thing the old soul had to say and could not get it out, but she plucked trembling at the shroud she wore that was full of patches now, and she had laughed when every patch was set in place and cried she would outlive the garment yet. But now she plucked at it.
>
> (011-002–003)

[[the-elder-son|The elder son]] understands: "She wants her third shroud new to lie in, the one my father said he would send" (011-004). [[the-mother]] sets to work immediately, sewing the scarlet burial robes.

## The Triumphant Death

The grandmother dies as she lived—on her own terms:

> But the old soul knew when it was finished. Speak she could not, but she lay and drew one last rattling breath or two, and opened wide her eyes and smiled her toothless smile, knowing she had lived through to her third shroud, which was her whole desire, and so she died triumphantly.
>
> (011-008)

Death comes not as defeat but as completion. She has outlasted two shrouds and received a third—her "whole desire" fulfilled. In a world of [[poverty]] and struggle, the grandmother's death stands as one of the novel's few moments of unqualified triumph.

## The Burial and Aftermath

After the burial, [[the-mother]] throws herself into work, using busyness to mask grief she cannot fully own. The hamlet praises her as a devoted daughter-in-law, but her mourning carries a deeper, unspoken weight. Later, walking alone to a neglected grave, she finally weeps—not for the grandmother, but for the accumulated losses of her life:

> She felt the lump she called her heart melt somehow and the tears rushed to her eyes, and she rose and walked by a little unused path across the land to a rude lonely grave she knew, the grave of some unknown man or woman, so old none knew whose it was now, and she sat there on the grassy mound and waited. And at last she wept.
>
> (012-007–008)

This displaced weeping at a stranger's grave reveals the funeral's deeper function in the narrative: it is the occasion not only for the grandmother's ritual send-off, but for [[the-mother]]'s release of grief too long withheld.
