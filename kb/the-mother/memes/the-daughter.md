## Summary

The daughter is the mother's second child — a little girl of about three years old whose defining physical trait is her chronically sore, inflamed eyes. She chews her fist when hungry, weeps easily, and follows her mother and elder brother everywhere. Her vulnerability — captured in the repeated image of her pressing her hands against her eyes — is one of the novel's most affecting visual motifs, and her condition becomes the occasion for one of the mother's earliest and most telling confrontations with her husband's neglect.

## Role

The daughter functions as a **marker of domestic hardship and maternal tenderness**. Her sore eyes, which the husband dismisses as unworthy of medical expense, crystallize the tension between the mother's protective instinct and the household's poverty. The mother's conflicted response — she is "troubled for the moment with the child's pain" (001-051) but also too busy to dwell on it — reflects the impossible calculus of peasant motherhood, where tenderness must compete with survival.

## Physical Description

The daughter is consistently described through her affliction: "her face was caked with mud and tears" (001-051), her eyelids "turned out and raw" (001-051). She moves tentatively, "feeling her way" (001-149) as though half-blind, and habitually presses her hands against her eyes to ease the irritation. She is small for her age — "being but three and a little less" (001-051) — and clings to her mother's coat or shoulder whenever she can.

## Character Traits

- **Physically vulnerable**: Her sore eyes are a constant source of pain and make her dependent on others to guide her.
- **Emotionally expressive**: Unlike her elder brother who weeps tearlessly, the daughter "screams and rubs her eyes" (001-051) — her suffering is raw, loud, and impossible to ignore.
- **Persistently attached**: She follows her mother everywhere, even into the birthing room where she sits on the earthen floor "beside her mother and held her hands against her eyes to ease them" (001-153).
- **Resilient in habit**: Despite the smoke that aggravates her eyes, she keeps "thrusting her head into the fiery smoke" (001-051) — a child's stubbornness that mirrors her mother's own.

## Relationships

The daughter is the second child of [[the-mother]] and [[the-husband]]. Her relationship with her mother is physical and clinging — she is too young to understand adult concerns but old enough to feel excluded when doors are closed. With her brother [[the-elder-son]], she forms a small, inseparable unit: where he goes, she follows, though more slowly.

Her father [[the-husband]] dismisses her condition: "And why should we use our scanty money for sore eyes when she can never die of it? I had sore eyes when I was a child, and my father never spent his money on me" (001-067). This moment — the mother asking for medicine and the husband refusing — is one of the novel's quiet turning points, a foreshadowing of his broader failure as a parent.

[[the-grandmother]], the husband's mother, watches over the children during the day but has little practical power to soothe the daughter's physical pain.

## Key Passages

> "The child sat in the dust of the threshing-floor, and what with her tears and what with her grimy fists, her face was caked with mud and tears." (001-051)

> "It was true the child's eyes were red and tender and the edges of the lids turned out and raw, and when the child turned her head wincing and whimpering, the mother let be in pity, troubled for the moment with the child's pain." (001-051)

> "She went out and this time she found two small tin bowls and filled them and she found two small pairs of bamboo chopsticks, and she took one bowl to the girl first because she was still weeping and rubbing her eyes." (001-051)

## In the Narrative

The daughter appears in the domestic scenes of the early chapters — eating, weeping, following her mother, sitting beside her during childbirth. Her sore eyes are mentioned repeatedly, creating a cumulative pressure that the narrative never resolves. She is a child in the background of larger events, but her suffering — small, constant, and unaddressed — is emblematic of the novel's attention to the costs of poverty that accumulate in the body.

## Significance

The daughter's sore eyes serve as a somatic register of hardship. They are not dramatic enough to warrant emergency — the husband is technically correct that she "can never die of it" — but they are real enough to mark every waking hour. Pearl Buck uses this condition to illustrate how poverty distributes its damage unevenly across a household, landing hardest on those least able to protest. The daughter cannot articulate her pain except through tears; her mother can see it but cannot afford to fix it. The gap between recognition and remedy is the novel's quiet tragedy.
