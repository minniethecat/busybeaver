# Temple and Religious Life

The **temple** in the world of *The Mother* is a presence more suggested than described—a node in the rural landscape that marks the boundary between the secular routine of [[work|labor]] and the sacred rhythms of festival and ritual. Folk religion in the novel is not a matter of doctrine or theology but of seasonal observance, communal gathering, and the quiet persistence of belief in a world where survival depends on forces beyond human control.

## The Temple in the Landscape

The temple exists at the edge of the [[the-hamlet|hamlet]]'s consciousness. It is a physical landmark—a destination for festival days, a place where the rhythms of agricultural life briefly pause:

> and there are the holidays of the festivals and of the new year

> (003-003)

The [[the-mother|mother]] lists festivals alongside the agricultural seasons as one of the markers that give shape to the year. The temple is where these festivals are centered, providing the hamlet with a shared calendar of celebration that punctuates the otherwise unvarying round of days.

## The Wayside Shrine

Beyond the formal temple, folk religion in the novel finds expression in the small, intimate spaces of the landscape—the wayside shrines where travelers pause and peasants offer informal worship:

> she saw the three gods in the shrine, the chief a staid old man staring straight ahead of him, and by his side two small attendants, little, decent gods of the wayside for those who paused in their journey for worship or for shelter. She took the garment she had laid aside and went and threw it on their heads and covered up their staring eyes.

> (001-834)

This moment—the mother covering the shrine gods with her garment—is one of the novel's rare direct engagements with the divine. The "three gods" are not imposing deities but "little, decent gods of the wayside," approachable and domestic. The mother's gesture is ambiguous: part modesty (she hides from their gaze), part intimacy (she treats them as familiars rather than fearsome powers). The gods of the novel's world are not transcendent beings demanding worship but household presences, woven into the fabric of daily life.

## Folk Religion as Social Glue

Religious observance in the novel is inseparable from community. Temple festivals bring the [[the-hamlet|hamlet]] together, temporarily suspending the hierarchies and tensions of daily life:

> Yes, it is true that all days were the same for the mother, but she never felt them dull and she was well content with the round of the days … and there are the holidays of the festivals and of the new year, yes and even the children change and grow and I am busy bearing more, and to me there is naught but change, and change enough to make me work from dawn to dark, I swear.

> (003-003)

Festivals provide the variety that makes the monotony of work bearable. They are occasions for rest, for communal eating, for the brief flowering of social life that sustains the hamlet through the long stretches of labor.

## The New Year Festival

The New Year festival is the pivotal religious occasion in the hamlet's calendar—a time when all absent men return home, debts are settled, and the community renews itself:

> the time drew near and everyone in the hamlet was busy for the feast, and she must needs be busy, too, not only for the children, to make them new shoes and wash their garments clean and make a new cap for the babe, but she must be busy for the man, also.

> (001-503)

The mother's preparations for the New Year reveal the festival's layered significance. She sells rice to buy "two red candles and incense to burn before the god and red letters of luck to paste upon the tools and on the plough" (001-503). The ritual objects—candles, incense, luck-characters—bridge the sacred and the practical. The god receives his offerings, but the tools of labor are blessed too. Folk religion sanctifies work rather than transcending it.

The New Year is also when the mother enacts her fiction of the absent husband. She has a letter written to herself, as though from him, explaining that "I could not come at the new year because my master loves me so he could not spare me" (001-527). The festival's expectation of homecoming—"a time when all men go to their homes if they still live and are not beggars" (001-499)—makes the husband's absence socially conspicuous. Religion provides the calendar; social performance fills it.

## The Mid-Autumn Festival

A quieter festival appears later in the novel, when the mother, pregnant with the agent's child and isolated by shame, still observes the mid-autumn rites:

> when the festival of mid-autumn came and the hamlet feasted and each house had its own joy and the little street was merry with the pleasure and rejoicing and the houses full of grain and food, the mother, though she had no joy, yet made a few small moon cakes for her own children, too.

> (001-903)

The moon cakes are the minimum ritual observance—a gesture that connects the mother's children to the community's celebration even when she herself is excluded from its joy. The festival persists as a structural fact of hamlet life, indifferent to individual suffering. The mother participates not from devotion but from maternal duty: her children must not be denied what other children have.

## The Unseen Spiritual World

The novel's spiritual landscape is thin but present. No gods are named, no prayers are recorded, but an awareness of forces beyond the visible world runs beneath the surface of peasant consciousness. The [[the-old-woman|old grandmother]]'s folk wisdom carries faint echoes of religious belief:

> The old grandmother piped, "So was I when I was a child, and I never died of it!"

> (002-002)

Her dismissal of the girl's eye condition—treated with hot water rather than prayer or medicine—reflects a worldview in which the natural and the spiritual are not sharply distinguished. Bodily ailments are accepted as part of life's given conditions rather than as problems to be solved through religious or medical intervention.

When the cousin discovers the mother's pregnancy and her husband long absent, the supernatural is invoked as a plausible explanation before the social one:

> "It is a child, I swear, yet how can it be except it be conceived by spirit, since your man is gone these many years? But I have heard it said it does happen sometimes to women and in olden times it happened often, if they were of a saintly sort, that gods came down and visited them. Yet you be no great saint, cousin."

> (001-975)

The cousin's half-serious suggestion that "gods came down and visited" the mother reveals a folk cosmology in which divine intervention is possible but rare—a relic of "olden times" that persists as a narrative resource even when the speaker half-disbelieves it.

## The Absence of Institutional Religion

What is striking about the novel's treatment of religion is its near-absence. No priest appears, no scripture is quoted, no moral lesson is drawn from religious teaching. The temple is a place of festival, not of weekly worship. This reflects the reality of folk religion in rural China, where practice was seasonal and communal rather than doctrinal and institutional:

| Aspect | Manifestation |
|--------|--------------|
| Temple festivals | Seasonal holidays, communal celebration |
| Folk wisdom | Passed through generations, especially by the old |
| Spiritual belief | Implicit in attitudes toward birth, death, and nature |
| Institutional authority | Absent—no clergy, no formal doctrine |
| Wayside shrines | Informal worship spaces, gods as familiar presences |
| Ritual practice | Candles, incense, luck-characters—practical rather than theological |
