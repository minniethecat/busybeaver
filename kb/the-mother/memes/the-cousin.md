## Summary

The cousin is a secondary but morally significant figure in the hamlet — a silent, hardworking farmer who stands in quiet contrast to the mother's husband. Where the husband is restless, mercurial, and drawn to gambling and song, the cousin is steady, diligent, and sparing with words. He weaves baskets from willow withes in the evening while other men rest, helps the mother reap her rice when her husband abandons the family, and offers to stand beside her when she must deal with the landlord's agent. His character embodies the peasant virtues of silent labor and neighborly obligation.

## Role

The cousin serves as a **moral foil** to the mother's husband. Their first direct juxtaposition occurs early in Chapter I (001-073), where the husband, having finished his meal, looks across the street and sees only one man still working — his cousin, bent over a basket. The husband's reaction is a mixture of resentment and self-justification: "If he had worked all day, could he not game a bit at night either?" The cousin's silent diligence exposes what the husband himself dimly senses about his own character: that he lacks the steady, uncomplaining work ethic of the other men in the hamlet.

Later, in the harvest season (001-445), the cousin's role shifts from foil to **provider of practical aid**. After the husband disappears and does not return, the cousin comes to help the mother cut and bind her rice. He works silently, asks no probing questions, and offers only the words that necessity demands. His final promise — to help her deal with the landlord's wily agent — marks him as a figure of genuine community support.

## Character Traits

- **Industrious**: Works past sundown while others rest; the husband notes with envy that the cousin "would work on even into the night" (001-073).
- **Laconic**: A man of few words — "by nature a man who did not speak unless he must, and kept what few thoughts he had close in him" (001-228). His questions are simple and honest; his silence is neither hostile nor judgmental.
- **Honest and upright**: His few words carry moral weight. When the husband meets him on a morning when he should be working, the cousin's single question — "Where do you go and not at work?" (001-224) — is enough to make the husband look away, though not enough to change his course.
- **Neighborly**: His help to the mother during the harvest is given without expectation of return or even thanks. He anticipates her needs — the landlord negotiation — without being asked.

## Relationships

The cousin is married to **[[the-cousins-wife]]**, a noisy, good-natured woman who is everything he is not — talkative, laughing, socially engaged. Their marriage embodies a complementary partnership common in rural life: the silent man and the bustling woman. She serves as the community's informal midwife, while he provides the household's steady labor.

To [[the-mother]], the cousin is a source of quiet, dependable support. She is "glad of his help and yet she feared him too, for he was a man of few words, honest and few, and his questions were simple and hard not to answer truthfully" (001-445). This fear is not of harm but of being seen — the cousin's honest simplicity makes evasion difficult.

To [[the-husband]], the cousin represents a reproach. The husband cannot meet his cousin's simple work ethic with a straight answer; when asked why he is not at work, he offers a defensive evasion: "I was hungered with my work, and it was a cold day" (001-224).

## Key Passages

> "Out of the other few houses in the hamlet men began to come now also. Some shouted to each other of a game they had begun at the inn, and some stood yawning and gaping at their doors. The young husband ceased his singing suddenly and looked sharply across the street. There was only one house where a man worked on while others rested. It was his cousin. That fellow! He would work on even into the night. There he was sitting at his door, his head bent to see the weaving of a basket of some sort he made from willow withes." (001-073)

> "But as he went the man met his sober cousin coming from the field for his first morning meal, having already worked a space since dawn, and this cousin called, 'Where do you go and not at work?'" (001-224)

> "Then the cousin spat and passed on, saying nothing, for he was by nature a man who did not speak unless he must, and kept what few thoughts he had close in him." (001-228)

But he worked silently and asked her nothing and he said nothing except the few necessary words he must until he went away, and then he said, 'If he is not come when the time is here to divide the grain with the landlord, I will help you then, for the new agent is a wily, clever man, and of a sort ill for a woman to do with alone.'" (001-445)

## In the Narrative

The cousin appears at key structural points in the narrative:

- **Chapter I, evening scene** (001-073): First appearance — the husband sees him working while others rest, establishing the contrast between the two men.
- **Chapter I, morning encounter** (001-224–228): The cousin questions the husband's absence from the fields; the husband evades and passes on.
- **Chapter I, harvest** (001-445): After the husband disappears, the cousin comes to help the mother reap and bind her rice. He works two days in silence and promises to help with the landlord's agent.

## Significance

In a novel where male characters are often absent, unreliable, or predatory, the cousin stands as a rare example of steady, unobtrusive masculine virtue. He does not sing, gamble, or charm; he works, helps, and keeps his own counsel. His silence is not withholding but respectful — he does not pry into the mother's situation even as he offers practical aid. Pearl Buck uses him to suggest that community, not just individual effort, is what sustains the mother through hardship.
