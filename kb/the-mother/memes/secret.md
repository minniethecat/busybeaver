# Secrecy

In *The Mother*, **secrecy** is the hidden architecture of private life beneath the hamlet's communal surface. Characters conceal desires they cannot name, money they cannot trust others to know about, and acts whose exposure would destroy them. Buck uses secrecy as both a narrative strategy—revealing the mother's inner life to the reader while she hides it from her neighbors—and as a thematic preoccupation. The novel suggests that in a world where gossip polices every threshold, secrecy is the only form of freedom, however precarious.

## The Landscape of Hidden Things

Secrecy in the novel begins with the natural world itself. In the opening pages, the mother takes comfort in the invisible processes of growth:

> This very night life would begin to stir in those dry beans. This thought gave her content. Yes, that whole field would begin to stir with life tonight secretly in the damp warm earth.
>
> (001-001)

The beans germinating underground—hidden, silent, inevitable—become a metaphor for all the secret growths in the novel: the mother's hidden love, her buried silver, and ultimately the pregnancy she cannot show. What the earth conceals is life; what the mother conceals is survival.

## Buried Silver

The most literal secret in the novel is the mother's hidden money. After exchanging the paper note for silver coins, she buries them in the earthen floor:

> In that night while the children and the old woman slept the mother rose and lit the candle and dug a hole with her hoe into the hard earth of the floor and there she hid the ten pieces of silver … Then she laid herself down again in the darkness.
>
> (012-012)

The buffalo stares with "great dull eyes" and the fowls look "with this eye and then with that" (012-012)—the animals become witnesses to a secret that no human in the hamlet shares. The buried silver represents the mother's only autonomy: money that belongs to her alone, hidden from the man who would spend it on himself, the neighbors who would borrow it, and the landlord who would claim it.

## The Secret Pregnancy

The novel's central secret is the pregnancy that results from the encounter with the landlord's agent. The mother drives "the memory of an hour … into her secret places" (017-017), using the labor of making the old woman's shroud to bury the knowledge beneath activity:

> Here was strange motherhood now that must be so secret and watched with such dismay in the loneliness of the night while the children slept. And however she might be sickened she dared not show it.
>
> (017-017)

The pregnancy is "strange motherhood" because it is illegitimate—a child conceived outside marriage, whose father will not claim it. The secrecy required transforms the experience of pregnancy itself: where her "proper children" brought no sickness, this one makes "her food turn on her when she ate a mouthful" (017-017). The body refuses to keep the secret the mind insists upon.

## The Secret Hold of Desire

Buck is unflinching in her depiction of how secrecy intensifies rather than extinguishes desire. Even as the mother hides her pregnancy, she cannot stop wanting the man who caused it:

> Rather did it seem he held her fast now by this secret hold that grew within her. She had repented that she ever yielded to him and yet she yearned for him often day and night.
>
> (017-017)

The "secret hold" is doubly meaningful—it is the hidden pregnancy (the physical hold) and the hidden desire (the emotional hold). The secrecy of their connection, far from being a deterrent, becomes part of its power. What no one knows exerts a stronger pull than what is publicly acknowledged.

## The Agent's Secret Gaze

The agent operates almost entirely through secrecy. His desire for the mother is conveyed not in words but in hidden looks:

> Neither cousin nor cousin's wife saw how the secret eyes of the agent went sliding under his lids toward the woman across the way.
>
> (017-017)

His "secret eyes" perceive what the hamlet's open gaze does not—the mother's vulnerability, her solitude, her hunger. He is a man who "could not look at any woman left alone and not wonder secretly what she was and how her heart was made and how her body was shaped" (014-014). Secrecy is his medium; public courtesy is his mask.

## The Mother's Secret Tenderness

Not all secrets in the novel are guilty. The mother's love for the man is a secret she keeps even from herself:

> She would answer quietly, "Aye, a very merry man, truly," and would talk then of other things to hide her pain, because she loved him secretly. And she knew he was never merry when he was with her.
>
> (006-006)

The phrase "she loved him secretly" recurs throughout the early chapters. This secrecy is not shameful but protective—she hides her love because to show it openly would expose her to the pain of his indifference. Even her tenderness toward the old woman is "secretly tender" (001-001). The mother's deepest emotions are conducted in a private register that the hamlet's chattering surface cannot access.
