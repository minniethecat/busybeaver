# Ritual

In *The Mother*, **ritual** is the visible lattice of belief that structures the hamlet's relationship to forces beyond its control. The religious world of the novel is syncretic, practical, and entirely untheological — gods and goddesses are approached for results, not worshiped for their own sake. Incense is burned for the man's safe return; burial shrouds are worn as everyday clothing until needed for the grave; festival feasts mark the calendar. Ritual in this world is not separate from daily life but woven through it, a set of practices as practical as planting and as necessary as food.

## The Practicality of Belief

Religious observance in the novel is transactional. When the man disappears, the mother does not pray for his soul — she burns incense for his return and makes offerings:

> She had bought incense and burned it for his safety, and she had made cakes for the festival and set them before the gods.
>
> (010-010)

The gods are approached as powerful neighbors might be — with offerings, with respect, with the hope of favorable intervention. When the incense and cakes produce no result, the mother does not question the gods' existence; she simply stops spending money on them. Faith is measured by efficacy, and efficacy alone.

## The Goddess of Mercy

The most specific religious figure in the novel is the goddess of mercy (Guanyin), to whom the mother turns in desperation when her daughter's eyes fail:

> She went to the temple and she bought incense and burned it before the goddess of mercy, and she vowed a vow that if the girl were healed she would buy a new red robe for the goddess.
>
> (017-017)

The vow follows the logic ofall peasant bargaining: if the goddess delivers, the mother will pay. The conditional nature of the promise is not impiety but the only religion the mother knows — one in which divine and human obligations are reciprocal. When the girl is not healed, the mother "had no great faith in the goddess, somehow" (017-017), and the relationship ends not in apostasy but in abandonment, like a failed business arrangement.

## Burial Custom

The novel's richest ritual material concerns death and burial. The old woman's shrouds — red burial coats worn as everyday garments until threadbare — embody the hamlet's matter-of-fact relationship to mortality:

> The red coat the young mother had made to bury her in she wore under a blue coat, as it was the custom to do in these parts, until it was worn and gone.
>
> (001-001)

The custom of wearing one's burial clothes while alive collapses the distinction between preparation for death and continuance of life. The old woman's shroud is simultaneously her grave garment and her everyday coat — the same object serving two opposed purposes. When she dies, the entire hamlet participates in a vigil structured around the race to finish the third shroud before the failing breath. The ritual is communal, practical, and deeply attentive to the dying person's desire for a dignified exit.

## Festival and Feast

Ritual also marks the calendar of communal celebration. The harvest feast, the mid-autumn festival, the new year — these are occasions when the hamlet consumes together, when women dress carefully and men eat publicly, when the social order is displayed and briefly suspended:

> When the festival of mid-autumn came and the hamlet feasted and each house had its own joy … the mother, though she had no joy, yet made a few small moon cakes for her own children, too.
>
> (017-017)

The mother's participation in festival ritual even when she "had no joy" is telling. Ritual is obligation as much as celebration — a performance of normalcy that the community expects and that the mother, with her secret pregnancy, cannot afford to refuse. The moon cakes made without joy are perhaps the most honest ritual in the novel: form maintained when substance has fled.

## The Letter as Ritual Object

In a secular register, the false letter the mother obtains functions as a ritual object. She displays it, allows it to be handled, treats it with the reverence due a sacred text:

> She grew to hate the thing because she was ever afraid the wind might blow it away or the rats gnaw it or the children find it and think it nothing and tear it in play.
>
> (012-012)

The letter's power lies not in its content but in its existence as a physical token. It is handled, examined, discussed — a fetish object that wards off the shame of abandonment. When the mother exchanges it for silver coins, she converts ritual power into practical power, preferring the weight of metal to the fragility of paper.
