## Summary

Homecoming is a recurring narrative event in *The Mother* that structures the emotional life of the hamlet. Each day, the laborers return from the fields; each year, the festivals summon families together; and always, the mother watches for the return that never comes — her husband's. The pattern of departures and returns forms the rhythmic backbone of the novel, with each homecoming carrying the weight of the original, defining absence.

## Daily Returns from the Fields

The novel opens with the daily rhythm of work and return. The mother returns from the fields to find her children crying hungrily at the kitchen door:

> But the mother went steadily on with her task, her face quiet and in repose. Yes, she was as quiet as though she did not hear the fretting of the children... She was thinking that it was true she was a little late tonight. There was a deal to be done on the land in the spring, and she had stayed to drop the last row of beans. (023-023)

The man, too, returns from the fields — his daily homecoming marked by his high singing voice and the old woman's joy at hearing him. These ordinary returns establish the normal pattern against which the great absence will be measured.

## The Husband's Non-Return

After the husband's departure to the city, every day becomes a vigil. The mother watches the street:

> She set her stool so that she could see through the short single street of the hamlet if any came that way... with every twist she gave to the spindle she looked secretly down the road. (405-405)

The children, too, wait: "the boy cried, astonished that his father was not come, 'And where does my father sleep, then?'" (395-395). The mother invents reassurances: "Doubtless in the town, for he will come home tomorrow or in a day or so" (395-395).

## The Festival Homecoming That Never Comes

The new year festival becomes the ritual horizon of hoped-for return. The mother sews new garments for her absent husband, telling all the hamlet that "he will come home for the new year festival" (499-499). The old mother repeats it endlessly: "When my son comes at new year..." (499-499). The children hope. Even the gossip, making herself new festival shoes, cannot shake the mother's assertion.

> Yes, she was so sure by now he would come that she even set her scissors in that cloth... and so she finished the garments to the last button... Then she put the garments away against his coming, and to all of them it seemed the garments brought the man more nearly home again. (505-505)

But the festival comes and goes, and the blue robe never appears on the road.

## The Longing for Return

After five years, the mother's longing crystallizes into a bodily cry:

> Nearly every spring she had given birth... but this spring was her body barren... Suddenly her longing streamed out of her like a cry, "Oh—come home—come home!" (609-609)

Yet even this cry is ambiguous — does she long for the man himself, or for the life she had when he was present? The homecoming she desires may be the restoration of a self she has lost.

## The Younger Son's Returns

A secondary homecoming pattern emerges with the younger son, who "came but sometimes to see how she did and went away again somewhere" (1355-1355). His sporadic returns from town, dressed in clothes his laboring brother cannot afford, mirror and invert the father's departure — he returns, but he is changed, belonging now to a world beyond the hamlet.

## Significance

The homecoming pattern gives the novel its cyclical structure. Every return — from the fields, from the city, from a journey — is measured against the one return that never happens. The homecoming motif transforms absence into presence: the husband is most present in the narrative through the very fact of his not coming home. The "clear and fine a blue" robe waits unworn, a garment for a homecoming that exists only in the mother's sustained fiction.
