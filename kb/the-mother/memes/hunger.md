## Summary

Hunger in *The Mother* is the body's direct registration of poverty. It is not abstract economic deprivation but the specific sensation of an empty stomach, the calculation of whether the rice will last until harvest, the mother's decision to feed her children while she goes without. Pearl S. Buck renders hunger as a recurring physical presence — "the old woman grew hungry and faint with the smell of the food" (005-012) — making it one of the novel's most visceral registers of the characters' material condition.

## In the Novel

Hunger is threaded through the novel's domestic scenes as an ever-present threat. The mother's first duty is feeding: "Now the food in the cauldron was boiling" (001-009) opens the novel's domestic action, and the distribution of food — who eats first, who gets the "heart of the cabbage" (005-013) — is the primary theater of care and sacrifice. The mother feeds the man first, then the children, then the old woman; her own hunger is registered only indirectly, through her labor.

The husband's hunger is qualitatively different from the family's. He "ate hastily, being mightily hungered" (001-030), demanding food as his due. His hunger is appetite, not deprivation — a demand rather than a condition. The mother experiences hunger differently: as a constant background to work, as the cold rice saved for the children (005-028), as the old woman's observation that "my food does not heat me as once it did" (005-082), a hunger that is also the body's aging and decline.

Food scarcity is seasonal and structural. The land is "mortally poor" (016-045), and the daughter's new home offers only "a bit of cold food" (016-046), a geographic inheritance of poverty. Hunger in this novel is never romanticized as simplicity or asceticism — it is the body's register of injustice, the physical cost of tenancy, gambling losses, and the structural vulnerability of women in a subsistence economy.

## Key Scenes

- Opening domestic scene: food distribution as the novel's first action, the cauldron and the stove (001-009–018)
- The husband eating "mightily hungered" while the family waits (001-030)
- The old woman "hungry and faint" waiting for her son to return before eating (005-012)
- The mother saving the cabbage heart for the husband, the children eating the rest (005-013)
- Cold rice and heated water for the old woman — the mother's minimal provision (005-028)
- The old woman's observation: "food does not heat me as once it did" (005-082)
- The daughter's new home: "a bit of cold food" as evidence of diminished circumstances (016-046)

## Significance

Hunger in *The Mother* does the work that "poverty" as an abstraction cannot. Where poverty names a condition, hunger names an experience — the specific, recurring, bodily sensation that structures the mother's day from the first lighting of the stove to the last distribution of cold rice. Buck's treatment is relentlessly material: hunger is not spiritual emptiness but physical need, not a metaphor but a fact. By making hunger visible in every domestic scene, the novel refuses to let poverty remain an abstract category and insists on its lived, bodily cost.
