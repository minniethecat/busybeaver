## Summary

The Old Woman is the mother-in-law of the Mother and grandmother to the children. Nearly blind from a lifetime of feeding the earthen stove, she spends her days seated in the kitchen corner, wrapped in a padded coat, chattering incessantly to whoever will listen — or to no one at all. She is a figure of comic persistence and pathos, her ceaseless talk a testament to the loneliness of old age and her desperate need to remain relevant in a household that has moved beyond her.

## Role in the Story

The Old Woman functions as a kind of Greek chorus — a constant commentary on the household's activities, whether anyone is listening or not. Her voice is the first voice the reader hears after the narrative's opening description, and her characteristic phrase — "and I ever did say" / "I always said" — recurs throughout the early chapters, establishing the timeless quality of domestic routine.

She is "half blind with a sore disease of the eyes" that has "well-nigh sealed her eyelids together," yet "through the small slits left open she could see a great deal still" (001-001). This physical limitation anchors her to the kitchen: she "was alone all day except for the little children since her son and son's wife must labor on the land" (001-005).

Her talk serves multiple narrative functions. It provides exposition about the family's circumstances — the scarcity of fuel, the dangers of thieves, the customs of an earlier era. It also reveals her character: a woman who fears becoming useless ("a useless old crone now, who ought to die" (001-001)) and who clings to her one remaining function — watching the door and the children while the younger generation works.

## Key Relationships

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]] (daughter-in-law)**: A relationship of dutiful care rather than warmth. The Old Woman loves her daughter-in-law — "this daughter-in-law whom she loved" (001-005) — but her love expresses itself as endless talk that the Mother has learned to tune out. The Mother serves her the first bowl of rice at each meal, a ritual acknowledgment of seniority.
- **[[the-son|The Son]] (her son)**: Her devotion to him is absolute and expressed through food. "I ever did say that when a man is hungered and especially a young and hearty son like mine, an egg stirred into noodles—" (001-005). When he returns home, she wakes from her doze to resume talking as though she had never paused.
- **The Grandchildren**: She attempts to comfort them when they cry, but "they were beyond her coaxing and would not be comforted and so she let them be" (001-005). She lacks the physical capacity to care for them actively.

## Character Development

The Old Woman is presented largely as a static figure — her habits and preoccupations are unchanging, her refrain of "I always said" suggesting a mind that no longer processes new information but simply replays accumulated wisdom. Yet her fixation on her own decline ("there is only that one load — is it two? — and the spring is but newly come") reveals a sharp awareness of her place in the household economy: she was once a productive member and is now a dependent, and this change stings.

## Notable Appearances

- **Chapter I**: The opening scene — she sits by the fire and launches into her monologue about fuel scarcity, thieves, and the customs of her youth. "Be careful how you feed the fire — there is only that one load" (001-001)
- **Chapter I**: Receiving her bowl of rice — she "seized the bowl in her two chill dry hands and fell silent, suddenly trembling with greediness for the food" (001-011)
- **Chapter I**: The evening scene — when the Son returns, she "woke out of a doze that had fallen on her after she had eaten and she began to speak as though she had not left off" (001-038)

## Significance

The Old Woman embodies the predicament of the elderly in a subsistence economy: she has outlived her productive years but not her need for food, shelter, and recognition. The household's dutiful care for her — the first bowl of rice, the tolerance of her endless talk — represents the Confucian value of filial piety in practice. At the same time, her near-invisibility to the younger adults ("no one listened") gestures toward the emotional isolation that accompanies physical dependence.
