# The Fields

The **fields** in *The Mother* are the primary workspace of the mother's life—the agricultural land she cultivates, plants, weeds, and [[harvest]]s across decades. They are not merely a backdrop but an active domain where her physical endurance, economic survival, and relationship to the natural world are expressed through ceaseless labor.

## Description

The fields surround the hamlet in a fertile valley, planted with beans, cabbage, radishes, rape (for oil), wheat, and rice. The mother's connection to them is intimate and bodily: she plants beans in the "damp warm earth" knowing "this very night life would begin to stir in those dry beans" (003-005). The fields are shaped by seasonal rhythms—spring planting, summer growth, autumn [[harvest]], winter hardening—and the mother's labor follows this cycle with "the sweat of the fields" (003-005) marking every season. Between the fields run footpaths "where grass grew on the footpath between this field and the next" (004-008), and the land extends to the hills visible in the distance.

## Role in the Story

The fields are where the novel's central drama of endurance unfolds. After her husband's abandonment, the mother becomes the sole cultivator: "she must work for two and work she did" (005-065), reaping "handful by handful the stiff yellow grain" (005-065). It is in the fields that the landlord's agent first approaches her—"I came but to look and see what the wheat is this year and guess the [[harvest]] from the fields" (008-004)—initiating the relationship that leads to her deepest transgression. The fields are also where her elder son takes over his absent father's plowing, "straining himself at heavy tasks" (007-082) with "the rude wooden plough... like a yoke across his own thin shoulders" (007-082). In the fields, the mother's authority is absolute: when gleaners come, she "screamed curses at them" and drives them away with "such powerful cursing" (005-065) that they fear her.

## Associated Characters

- **[[the-mother|The Mother]]** — works the fields from before dawn; they are the measure of her labor, worth, and endurance
- **[[the-husband|The Husband]]** — ploughed the fields before his departure; his absence is felt in every furrow
- **[[the-elder-son|The Elder Son]]** — takes on his father's field labor as a child, "struggling his whole share and more" (007-082)
- **[[the-cousin|The Cousin]]** — a neighboring farmer who works adjacent fields

## Significance

The fields embody the novel's central tension between order and chaos, survival and exhaustion. They represent both the mother's competence—she knows the land, the winds, the timing of planting—and her vulnerability: a lone woman defending her [[harvest]] against gleaners and landlord alike. The fields are also the site of seasonal beauty that the mother rarely notices, absorbed as she is in labor: "she never thought to lift her head to see what the world was about her" (007-004). When she does pause to look, on a spring day after her husband's departure, she sees the willows, pear blossoms, and pomegranate trees—a fleeting recognition of the world beyond toil that the fields both contain and deny her.
