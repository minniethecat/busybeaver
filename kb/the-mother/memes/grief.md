# Grief and Emotional Endurance

In *The Mother*, **grief** is not a single catastrophic event but a continuous condition woven into the fabric of peasant life. The mother experiences loss in many registers: the miscarriage of a child, the slow approach of the old woman's death, the daily attrition of poverty, and ultimately the abandonment by the man she loves. Pearl S. Buck traces grief not as melodrama but as a quiet, persistent force that the mother absorbs into her labor, her silence, her body. The novel's achievement is to show how a woman who never names her sorrow nonetheless lives through it—and continues to feed her children, plant her fields, and spin her thread.

## The Miscarried Child

The mother's first recorded loss is mentioned almost in passing, as a fact of her reproductive life:

> Even now was her milk dry again, though one child she dropped too soon last summer when she fell and caught herself upon the point of the plough … and if that one had not come too soon, and died when it had breathed but once, it would have been a fair boy by now, too, and trying to walk soon.
>
> (001-001)

The brevity of the reference is itself an index of how grief works in this world. The mother does not dwell on the lost child, but she remembers—calculating silently what age the boy would be, imagining his face. The grief is absorbed into the ongoingness of her life, not because she is callous but because the living demand her attention. She cannot afford the luxury of prolonged mourning.

## Death and Its Nearness

Death is a constant presence in the hamlet, and the mother confronts it with characteristic practicality. When a flux sweeps through the community, the old woman nearly dies:

> Yet when one summer came and there was a fierce flux in the hamlet so that two strong men died and some women and many little children, and the old woman lay dying, or so it seemed, and so seemed that they bought the best coffin they could and set there ready, the young mother was truly glad when the old woman clung to her life and came back to it for a while longer.
>
> (004-004)

The mother purchases a coffin and burial clothes for the old woman—acts of practical love that acknowledge death's proximity without surrendering to despair. When the old woman survives, it becomes a joke in the hamlet, and the red burial coat becomes everyday wear:

> The red coat the young mother had made to bury her in she wore under a blue coat, as it was the custom to do in these parts, until it was worn and gone and the old woman fretted and was ill at ease until the mother had made it new again.
>
> (004-004)

The shroud worn as everyday clothing is a powerful image of how grief and life coexist in this world. Death is prepared for, then deferred—and life continues in the garments meant for the grave.

## The Husband's Abandonment

The novel's central grief is the husband's departure. When the man leaves for the city and does not return, the mother's sorrow is too deep for public expression:

> Thus did the mother lie steadfastly and well, and the old woman believed the tale heartily and cried out often of her son and how rich he would be, and the mother kept her face calm and smooth and she did not weep as women do when their men run away and shame them.
>
> (014-014)

She constructs an elaborate fiction—that he has found work in a far city, that he sends money—to protect herself and her children from shame. The lie is a form of grief management: by controlling the narrative, she controls how much the loss can hurt her publicly. But the private cost is immense:

> But in the nights, in the dark nights, she wept silently and most bitterly. Partly she wept because he was gone, but sometimes she wept, too, because she was so put to shame, and sometimes she wept because she was a lone woman and life seemed too hard for her with these four leaning on her.
>
> (016-016)

This is the novel's most direct rendering of the mother's grief. It comes at night, in darkness, without witnesses. Her weeping is silent—even in her most private moments, she does not allow herself the release of audible crying. The grief is layered: loss of the man she loves, humiliation before the community, and the raw terror of survival.

## Grief Displaced into Labor

The mother's primary mechanism for processing grief is work. After the man leaves, she takes on his labor as well as her own:

> Day after day the woman had risen before the dawn … and taking the babe with her in one arm and in her other hand the short curved sickle she must use in reaping she set out to the fields … She must work for two and work she did, and if the child cried he must cry until she was weary and could sit down to rest.
>
> (015-015)

The body absorbs what the heart cannot process. Her bitterness emerges not as lamentation but as fury directed at gleaners who would take what little she has:

> When gleaners came to her field to glean what she might drop … she turned on them, her face dark with sweat and earth, and drawn with the bitterness of labor, and she screamed curses at them … And she cursed them so heartily and she so cursed the mothers that bore them and the sons they had themselves that at last they let her fields be, because they were afraid of such powerful cursing.
>
> (015-015)

Grief transformed into rage, rage transformed into labor, labor transformed into survival—this is the mother's emotional metabolism. She curses not the man who left her but the beggars who would take her grain; the displacement is both psychologically accurate and socially safer.

|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Miscarriage | Quiet calculation of the child's would-be age | Absorbed into ongoing maternal labor |
| Near-death of the old woman | Practical preparation (coffin, burial clothes) | Joking about the shroud worn daily |
| Husband's abandonment | Public fiction, private weeping | Displacement into work, protection of children |
| Shame before the community | Steadfast lying, calm face | Narrative control, strategic silence |
| The terror of sole survival | Bitter cursing of gleaners | Transmutation into productive fury |
