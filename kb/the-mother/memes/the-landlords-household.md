## Summary

The landlord's household is an offstage but structuring presence in the novel — the economic power that extracts rent from the peasant family and shapes their financial precarity. Though the landlord himself never appears directly, his claim on the family's produce is an ever-present reality, enforced through the intermediary figure of the agent.

## Structure

The landlord's household represents the landowning class in the novel's rural economy. The family does not own their land; they work it and pay rent to the landlord, making them tenant farmers rather than independent proprietors. This relationship is mentioned in the context of the mother's calculations: "we have scarcely enough to feed these children and this old soul and pay the landlord too" (001-266). The landlord's claim is listed alongside feeding children and caring for the elderly — an obligation equal in weight to the most fundamental duties of kinship.

The landlord himself remains an abstraction — never named, never seen. This invisibility is itself significant: the landlord's power operates impersonally, through economic structure rather than personal relationship. He need not be present to extract his share.

## The Landlord's Agent

The landlord's economic power is personified in his agent, a townsman who visits the hamlet each harvest to collect the rent. Unlike the absent landlord, the agent is a vivid physical presence — and a deeply ambivalent one:

> this landlord of the hamlet and the fields about it never came himself to fetch his share. He lived an idle rich man in some far city or other … and he sent in his place his agent, and this year it was a new agent, for his old agent had left him the last year, being rich enough after twenty years to cease his labors.

> (001-459)

The agent embodies the economic relationship in flesh and blood. He is "a tall, smooth man, his gown gray silk and leathern shoes on his feet, and he had a large smooth hand he put often to his shaven lip, and when he moved a scent of some sort came from him" (001-461). His silk robe, pale skin, and urban mannerisms mark him as belonging to a different world from the sun-browned peasants whose grain he measures.

He commands both fear and grudging laughter among the tenants:

> the tenants hated him, and they feared him because he had a high hard temper and this big body and two large, swift fists that he clenched and held hard against his thighs if any argued against what he said. Yes, and then he lifted the lids he drooped over his eyes, and his eyes were terrible, shining and black and cruel. Yet often they laughed at him, too, for if they gave him his fee without quarrel, he made a joke or two to salve the taking.

> (001-687)

The agent occupies an ambiguous social position — a townsman with fine clothes and book-learning, yet a servant to an unseen master. His power over the tenants is real but borrowed; his urban sophistication coexists with a predatory sexuality that the mother will later experience directly.

## The Harvest Ritual

The rent collection follows an annual rhythm tied to the agricultural calendar. The harvest yields grain that is never fully the farmer's own:

> Even the harvests were not his own, for he must measure out a share to that landlord and he must take another share and fee the townsman who was the landlord's agent.

> (001-180)

The division of the grain is a ceremonialized extraction. The cousin helps the mother when her husband is absent, measuring the grain "honest" so she need not come near the agent:

> she saw the grain divided and hard it was too, as it was hard for every farmer, to give to this smooth townsman his own share in what they had labored on. But they gave grimly, and so did she, knowing that if they did not they would suffer, and besides the landlord's share they gave the agent a fat fowl or two or a measure of rice or some eggs or even silver for his private fee.

> (001-463)

The "private fee" — an informal surcharge beyond the landlord's legal share — reveals how the rent system breeds parasitism. The agent grows rich on these unofficial exactions, and after twenty years retires wealthy, replaced by a new agent who begins the cycle again.

## Personal Entanglement

The economic relationship between the mother and the agent becomes complicated by personal entanglement after her husband disappears. The agent, drawn by her beauty and vulnerability, pursues her:

> he was such a man as could not look at any woman left alone and not wonder secretly what she was and how her heart was made and how her body was shaped. There was a dog's heart in him, for all his big, good frame and his square full face and his voice he made so hearty and frank.

> (001-687)

He waives her private fee as a gesture of seduction: "I will take no more of it than my landlord must have, or blame me if he does not. I will take no fee from you, goodwife" (001-703). The economic and the sexual intertwine — the agent uses his power over rent as a tool of courtship, and the mother's economic vulnerability makes her susceptible to his advances.

Later, when the mother believes herself a widow, she imagines a future with him: "And the landlord's agent was not wed. She was widow and he not wed, for she had heard him say that his wife was dead last year but she had not heeded, for what was it to her then, who was not widow?" (001-917). The fantasy of remarriage to the agent represents a dream of economic security — a way to escape the grind of tenant farming by attaching herself to the man who collects the rent.

## Role in the Story

The landlord's household functions as an economic ceiling on the family's aspirations. No matter how hard they work, how skillfully the mother manages, how lucky the man is at gaming — a portion of their produce always goes to the landlord. This structural theft (from the peasant perspective) is accepted as inevitable, part of the natural order of things, as unquestioned as the hierarchy of the family meal.

The absence of the landlord from the narrative is a political choice by the author. In Pearl S. Buck's rendering, class relations are not dramatic confrontations but background conditions — the water the characters swim in, so pervasive as to be nearly invisible to them.

## Key Members

- **The landlord** (unseen): The absentee owner who collects rent through his agent, living "an idle rich man in some far city" (001-459)
- **The agent**: A townsman intermediary — "tall, smooth … his gown gray silk" (001-461) — who measures the grain, collects the fees, and becomes entangled with the mother
- **The tenant family**: [[the-mother|The Mother]], [[the-son|The Son]], and the old woman — three generations bound to the land they do not own

## Significance

The landlord's household represents the class structure that undergirds the novel's world. It is the reason poverty is permanent, the reason hard work does not lead to prosperity, the reason the mother must refuse "the stuffs as rich women put upon their only sons" (001-266). By keeping the landlord offstage, the novel presents class not as a relationship between people but as a condition of existence — as fixed and as natural-seeming as the hills between which the hamlet nestles.

The agent, however, brings class into the realm of the personal. His sexual predation on the mother is inseparable from his economic power. The entanglement dramatizes how thoroughly class domination penetrates intimate life — the man who takes the family's grain also takes the mother's body, and the two forms of extraction are not easily distinguished.
