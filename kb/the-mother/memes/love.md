# Love and Marriage in Peasant Life

In *The Mother*, **love** is never named directly as an abstract ideal, yet it pulses beneath every domestic scene. The relationship between the [[the-mother|mother]] and her husband is built not on romantic declarations but on shared labor, physical passion, quarrels, tenderness, and the inexorable bond of children. The novel portrays love as something embodied—expressed through feeding, through the warmth of bodies sleeping together, through the fierce pride each takes in the other's strengths—rather than something spoken.

## Passion and Quarrel

The couple's relationship is defined by its intensity. They fight with the same energy they bring to love-making. Their arguments are not signs of a failing marriage but of two strong personalities colliding within the narrow space of peasant life:

> But the truth was that this woman was such a one as could live well content with the man and children and think of nothing else at all. To her—to know the fullness of the man's frequent passion, to conceive by him and know life growing within her own body, to feel this new flesh take shape and grow, to give birth and feel a child's lips drink at her breast—these were enough.
>
> (003-003)

Physical intimacy is central to their bond. The mother's experience of love flows through her body—through pregnancy, childbirth, nursing, and the "fullness of the man's frequent passion." This is love rooted in biology and daily proximity rather than in sentimental language.

## Feeding as Love

The mother expresses love most concretely through food. When the [[the-son|man]] works hard, she gives him the best of what little they have:

> When the man worked hard he must have a bit of meat or an egg. However they might quarrel, it was a pleasure to her to see him fed well, and all their quarreling, she thought to herself, was only of the lips. Well she loved to see him eat, even if sometimes she belabored him with her tongue for something.
>
> (001-001)

This passage captures the paradox of their love: she scolds him with her tongue but feeds him with devotion. The egg she saves from their few fowls is her unspoken declaration of care. In a world of [[poverty|scarcity]], feeding someone well is the most honest expression of love.

## Jealousy and Possession

The novel's most dramatic conflict arises from the mother's jealousy. When the man sells a portion of the harvest to buy himself a robe of good cloth, the mother's anger is not about the money alone:

> And she did not blame him deeply that he could not work steadily hour after hour in the fields as she did, though at the moment she might lay about him sharply with her tongue. She knew that men cannot work as women do, but have the hearts of children always in them.
>
> (006-006)

Her hurt comes from feeling excluded—he spent something that belonged to the family on himself alone. The garment becomes a symbol of his separateness from her, and her silent refusal to forgive reveals how deeply wounded her love can be.

## The Man's Perspective

The husband's love is more restless than the mother's. While she finds fulfillment in [[family-duty|domestic routine]], he dreams of escape to the [[the-hamlet|city beyond the hills]]:

> On days like this he held as nothing even the good few holidays he took, the feast days and the long idle winter days when he did nothing but sleep, and when he could not sleep he gambled. A lucky gambler he was, too, and he always came home with more than he took, and it seemed an easy way to live, if he were a lone man and had but himself to feed.
>
> (005-005)

Yet he always returns. His love for the mother is the gravity that holds him in place even when his imagination wanders. His singing in the evening, his quick temper, his childish petulance—all are the gestures of a man who is fundamentally bound to his wife, however much he may chafe against the bond.

## Reconciliation

After their worst quarrel, the mother's anger eventually dissolves—not through words of apology, but through the softening of her heart as she works in the fields:

> But after she had worked a while on the land the gentle autumn wind blew into her angry heart and cooled it without her knowing it … And while her hand scattered the winter wheat into the soft and well-tilled soil, in her heart came quiet and she remembered that she loved the man well and his laughing face came before her and stirred her and she said to herself remorsefully, "I will make him a dainty dish for his meal this noon."
>
> (006-006)

Love in this world is renewed not through conversation but through action—through the preparation of a special dish, through the shared labor of the fields, through the warmth of bodies in the shared bed at night. The mother cannot stay angry because her life is too entangled with his; their interdependence makes sustained resentment impossible.

## Love in the Extended Family

The [[the-old-woman|old grandmother]] represents another dimension of love: the bond between mother-in-law and daughter-in-law. Though the old woman chatters endlessly, the mother tolerates her with patience rooted in the recognition of shared womanhood:

> The old woman chattered on. She was alone all day except for the little children since her son and son's wife must labor on the land, and now it seemed there were many things she had to tell this daughter-in-law whom she loved.
>
> (001-001)

The grandmother loves the daughter-in-law not only as her son's wife but as a fellow woman who understands the burdens of peasant life. This cross-generational bond, expressed through constant talk and shared domestic space, is one of the quiet strengths of the novel's emotional world.

| Expression of Love | How It Manifests | Characters Involved |
|-------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Physical passion | Frequent intimacy, bodily desire | Mother and husband |
| Feeding | Saving the best food, preparing special dishes | Mother → husband, children, old woman |
| Quarrel | Heated arguments followed by reconciliation | Mother and husband |
| Silent devotion | Working alongside, enduring together | Mother and husband |
| Filial care | Tending to the old woman's needs | Mother → grandmother |
| Maternal tenderness | Nighttime comfort, nursing, protection | Mother → children |
