# Seasons

In *The Mother*, the **agricultural year** is more than backdrop — it is the fundamental structure of existence. The mother's life, emotions, and the novel's narrative itself are organized around the cycle of planting, growing, harvesting, and waiting. Buck uses seasonal rhythms to create a sense of eternal return that both sustains and traps her characters. The land's demands are the only calendar that matters, and against its steady pulse, the novel measures every human disruption: abandonment, desire, and the secret that cannot be hidden.

## Spring: The Season of Beginning

The novel opens in spring, establishing the season's dual character of hope and precarity:

> "The spring is but newly come and we have long to go before the grass is long enough to cut."
>
> (001-001)

Spring is the season of planting — beans into the earth, life stirring secretly. The mother "had stayed to drop the last row of beans" (001-001) and takes content in knowing that "this very night life would begin to stir in those dry beans … secretly in the damp warm earth" (001-001). Spring is possibility, but also anxiety: the fuel must last, the seeds must take, the children must be fed through the lean weeks before harvest.

Childbirth, too, belongs to spring. The mother gives birth to her fourth child "upon a sweet and windy day in spring" (004-004), the fertility of the land and the fertility of the body joined in a single rhythm. A young spring moon hangs "very small and crystal pale among the stars" (001-001) as the man sings softly outside the door — spring induces dreaming even in those who cannot afford it.

## Summer: Heat and Crisis

Summer brings labor at its most intense and desire at its most dangerous. The novel's central crisis — the encounter with the landlord's agent — unfolds in summer heat:

> It was such a day in that summer as might set any man to dreaming of new joy … The air was so full of languor and soft warmth, the leaves and grass so newly green, and the sky so bright and deep a blue that scarcely could he work at all.
>
> (006-006)

The landscape becomes eroticized: "yellow fragrant lilies bloomed and wild wistaria hung in pale purple wreaths" (006-006). Summer's "sweet wind, teasing and blowing" mirrors the agent's pursuit. The heat that makes work impossible is the same heat that makes resistance impossible. When the mother feels "her blood grow all sweet and languorous in her with the hot sun" (017-017), the season itself becomes an agent of her undoing.

Summer also brings the epidemic — "a fierce flux in the hamlet so that two strong men died and some women and many little children" (001-001) — and the old woman's death, struck by a sudden chill after sitting too long in the sun. Summer gives life and takes it with equal indifference.

## Autumn: Harvest and Reckoning

Autumn is the season of consequences. The rice "hung heavy and yellow and ready for the harvest" (010-010) just as the man's absence becomes permanent. The mother must reap alone, bending "handful by handful" to the "stiff yellow grain" (010-010). The harvest that should be a celebration becomes a trial.

Yet autumn also brings its own kind of mercy. After the quarrel over the brass ring, it is the autumn landscape that heals the mother's anger:

> Drifting leaves and brown hillsides from which the green of summer had died away, the gray sky and the far cry of wild geese flying southward, the quiescent land and all the quiet melancholy of the falling year stole into her heart without her knowledge and made her kind again.
>
> (005-005)

The "quiet melancholy of the falling year" does what argument cannot — it restores the mother to herself. Autumn's beauty is elegiac, a preparation for the bareness to come.

## Winter: Endurance and Waiting

Winter is the season of reduction. The man is gone, the fields lie fallow, and the household contracts around the stove. The mother sits spinning in the doorway "to be in the southern sun and out of the sharp wind" (012-012). She takes the wadding from the old woman's summer shroud and sews it back for winter warmth — the same garment, seasonally transformed.

Winter is also the season of storytelling and survival. The mother fabricates the fiction of the letter and the money to carry them through the lean months. The paper money becomes a burden: she hides it from rats, from damp, from the children who might "find it and think it nothing and tear it in play" (012-012). Winter's scarcity sharpens every anxiety and concentrates the household into its smallest, most intimate form.

> The land changes from seedtime to harvest and there is the reaping of the harvest from our own land … and there are the holidays of the festivals and of the new year, yes and even the children change and grow and I am busy bearing more, and to me there is naught but change, and change enough to make me work from dawn to dark, I swear.
>
> (003-003)

## The Eternal Return

The mother's own account of her life — "naught but change" — captures the paradox at the heart of the novel's seasonal structure. The agricultural year is endlessly cyclical, yet within each cycle there is genuine change: children grow, babies are born, old women die, men leave. The seasons provide a framework stable enough to absorb disruption without breaking. Spring always returns, planting must always be done, and in this necessity the mother finds both her burden and her salvation.
