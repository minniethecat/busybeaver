## Summary

Memory in *The Mother* operates along a sharp gender divide: men forget, women remember. The husband "would forget easily anything that did not please him" (004-007), while the mother's narrative consciousness is defined by what she cannot forget — grievances, betrayals, moments of tenderness, the husband's "laughing face and his pleasant ways" (004-019). This asymmetry makes memory a form of labor, a burden disproportionately borne by women, and one of the novel's most subtle instruments of psychological realism.

## In the Novel

The husband's forgetting is systematic and self-serving. After his misdeeds, "the man had almost forgot what he had done, for it was his way to forget easily anything that did not please him" (004-007). His memory is selective, editing out anything that would require accountability. This is memory as privilege — the ability to erase the past that the mother must carry.

The mother's remembering is, by contrast, involuntary and total. Even in anger, she "remembered that she loved the man well and his laughing face and his pleasant ways" (004-019) — memory undermines her rage, making it impossible for her to sustain the simplifying emotions that would make her life easier. After the husband's final departure, "she remembered every word" (006-012) he had spoken, her memory becoming a form of archival duty, preserving what others would discard.

The novel also explores forgetting as a slow, organic process — not the husband's willful erasure but the natural fading of trauma. "Almost the children had forgotten that there had ever been another in the bed except their mother" (005-077) — seasonal time heals what memory cannot. By the novel's close, "very dim in her now was the memory of the man, and mingled somehow with this younger son" (018-003), suggesting that forgetting, when it comes not as evasion but as the body's own healing, is a form of mercy.

## Key Scenes

- The husband's selective forgetting after quarrels (004-007)
- The mother's involuntary remembering of love during anger (004-019)
- The mother remembering "every word" after the husband leaves (006-012)
- The children's organic forgetting of their father (005-077)
- The grandmother's failing memory in old age: "she grew more idle as she grew older, and forgetful" (007-002)
- The mother's dimming memory of the husband, merged with the son (018-003)

## Significance

Memory in *The Mother* is quietly political. The husband's ability to forget is a form of power — the power to evade consequence, to remain unburdened by the past. The mother's inability to forget is a form of subjection — she carries what others discard, her consciousness a repository of grievances that can never quite become actions. Yet Buck does not romanticize memory as virtue or forgetting as vice. The novel's final gesture toward the fading of memory — "very dim in her now was the memory of the man" — suggests that the body's slow release of the past may be, after all, a form of grace.
