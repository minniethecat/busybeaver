# Gratitude

Gratitude serves as a moral marker in _The Secret of the Old Clock_, distinguishing the deserving from the undeserving. The **[[crowley-relatives|Crowley relatives]]** express genuine thankfulness for kindness received, while the **[[topham-family|Tophams]]** display only ingratitude.

## The Grateful Relatives

**[[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]**, though impoverished, tells **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** after receiving care: "It feels better already. I dunno what I'd of done if you hadn't come when you did." After receiving her inheritance, Abigail ensures Nancy knows she is in "surprisingly good health" with proper medical attention.

The **[[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]]** are profoundly grateful. **[[allie-horner|Allie]]** declares, "We owe it all to you, Nancy Drew!" and **[[grace-horner|Grace]]** says, "We'll never be able to thank you enough." They organize with the other relatives to offer Nancy a reward.

## Nancy's Response

Nancy refuses monetary compensation, insisting she "wanted to help" and that "it was fun for me." She eventually accepts only the **[[crowley-clock|Crowley clock]]** as a memento — "the only reward I want" — valuing the personal connection over financial gain.

## The Tophams' Ingratitude

In stark contrast, the Tophams treated **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]** with ingratitude during his life. They "treated him like a prince until he made his will in their favor and then they acted as though he were dirt under their feet." This ingratitude is repaid when the second will cuts them off entirely.

The novel's moral framework is clear: gratitude and kindness are rewarded, while ingratitude and greed lead to ruin.
