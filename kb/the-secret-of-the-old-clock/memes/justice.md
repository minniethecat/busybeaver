# Justice

The pursuit of justice is the central moral motivation for **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]**'s investigation into the **[[the-lost-will|Crowley will mystery]]**. The novel presents a clear moral order in which kindness and honesty should be rewarded, and greed and cruelty should be punished.

## The Injustice

The initial situation is fundamentally unjust: the snobbish, cruel **[[topham-family|Tophams]]** stand to inherit **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]**'s entire **[[fortune|fortune]]**, while kind relatives like the **[[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]]** and **[[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]** receive nothing. The Tophams treated Crowley "like dirt under their feet" after securing his will, whereas the others loved him "for himself and not his money."

## Nancy as Agent of Justice

Nancy is driven by a sense of **[[fairness|fairness]]** from the start: "It does seem unfair that all the money should go to them." Her father warns her about the difficulty of the case, but she persists because she believes the outcome is wrong.

Her commitment to justice extends beyond the inheritance case. At the department store, she intervenes during the **[[ada-vase-incident|vase-breaking incident]]** to prevent **[[ada-topham|Ada Topham]]** from falsely accusing the saleswoman, driven by a determination "to see justice done."

## Justice Served

The novel's resolution delivers poetic justice: the Tophams are "cut off without a cent" and face financial ruin from **[[stock-market-speculation|stock market losses]]**, while the Horner sisters, Abigail Rowen, the **[[mathews-farm|Mathews brothers]]**, and the **[[turner-home|Turner sisters]]** all receive generous inheritances. The Tophams' legal challenge is "thrown out of court," affirming the triumph of justice over greed.
