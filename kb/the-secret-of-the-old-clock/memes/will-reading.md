# Will Reading

The reading of [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]'s [[crowley-will-document|second will]] is the climactic event of *The Secret of the Old Clock*. Staged at the [[drew-household|Drew home]] before an assembly of the Crowley relatives and the [[topham-family|Topham family]], the reading delivers narrative justice: the [[kindness|kindly]] relatives who cared for Crowley are rewarded, while the grasping Tophams are reduced to a token share and public humiliation.

## Background

The existence of a second will has been the novel's central mystery. [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]'s investigation — from the [[crowley-clock|mantel clock]] to the [[topham-bungalow|Moon Lake bungalow]] to the safety deposit box under the alias "[[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]" — has all led to this moment. Multiple witnesses testified that Crowley intended to change his will after the Tophams "treated him unkindly" [001-039], and [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]'s disclosure about the clock provided the critical lead.

> "Can't the present will be broken?" [001-029]

## The Reading

[[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] opens the proceedings by addressing the assembled company. When he produces the document, [[cora-topham|Cora Topham]] immediately protests: "[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] made only one will and in that he left everything to us!" [024-024]. Drew calmly ignores her and begins reading.

The will distributes the bulk of the estate to the relatives who had shown Crowley [[kindness|kindness]] during his lifetime — the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]], the [[matilda-turner|Turner sisters]], the [[william-mathews|Mathews brothers]], and [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]] — with only a small portion remaining for the Tophams [024-055].

When the will mentions household furniture previously in the Tophams' possession, [[cora-topham|Cora]] cries out at the "hint that I took his furniture" [024-049], though it was "generally known about [[river-heights|River Heights]] that the Tophams had practically confiscated Josiah Crowley's furniture" [024-050].

## The Tophams' Humiliation

After the reading, [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] warns that contesting the will would be "only a waste of your time and money" [024-064]. When the [[topham-lawyer|Topham lawyer]] agrees — "Mr. Drew is right" [024-065] — [[cora-topham|Cora Topham]] erupts: "If that's all you know about law, you're discharged! We'll get another lawyer and we'll fight to the last ditch!" [024-066]

The Tophams storm out, their scheme in ruins. The lawyer rises, gathers his briefcase, and remarks dryly: "Well, I can't say I'm sorry to lose the case." [024-068]

## Significance

The will reading functions as the novel's moral climax on multiple levels:

1. **Narrative resolution**: The central mystery — whether a second will exists — is definitively answered
2. **Poetic justice**: The kind relatives who helped Crowley are rewarded; the cruel Tophams are punished
3. **Social reversal**: The poor are elevated, the arrogant humbled — the novel's democratic moral vision
4. **Nancy's vindication**: Every step of her investigation is proven correct
5. **Public reckoning**: The Tophams' humiliation is witnessed by the very community they tried to dominate

## Aftermath

Following the reading, the beneficiaries receive their inheritances. The [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] are "fixed for life" [024-055], able to renovate their [[horner-farm|farm]] and live in security. [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]] receives financial security in her old age. The Tophams receive a token share — a reminder that Crowley did not entirely disinherit them, only corrected the injustice of the first will.
