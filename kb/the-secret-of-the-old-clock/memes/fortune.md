# Fortune

The substantial wealth and property left by Josiah Crowley upon his death — the material stakes that motivate every character's actions, from the Tophams' greed to Nancy's quest for justice. (001-005)

## Definition

A fortune, in the context of this novel, is a large amount of money and property accumulated over a lifetime and transmitted at death. Josiah Crowley's fortune is substantial enough that the Topham family has already begun spending against it — "counting on the fortune already" — before their legal claim is even secure. (002-047) The fortune's magnitude is such that it defines the social standing and life prospects of everyone connected to it, from the impoverished Horner sisters to the comfortably established Tophams.

Carson Drew describes the estate plainly: "The estate is a considerable one, I understand, Nancy." (001-026) Later, when examining the documentation, the banker remarks that "The fortune will make a nice nest egg for someone. These securities are of the best, too. All gilt edge." (022-039)

## Role in the Story

The Crowley fortune is the central object of conflict. The Tophams possess it through the first will but are morally undeserving. Grace Horner captures the injustice: "It isn't that we were expecting any of the money, but it does seem unfair that the entire fortune should go to them. They never cared a snap of their fingers for Uncle Josiah, and I don't believe he intended to leave his money to them." (004-056) The Horner sisters and Abigail Rowen, who genuinely cared for Crowley, receive nothing. Nancy's investigation aims to correct this injustice by finding the second will that redirects the fortune.

The fortune brings out the worst in the Tophams, who are already borrowing against it: "Richard Topham has been losing heavily in the stock market this last month. He has been getting credit at a number of the banks on the strength of the inheritance, and I suspect he is depending on Crowley's money to pull him through a tight place." (022-043) Carson Drew warns Nancy: "Rest assured the Tophams won't give up the fortune without a bitter struggle." (007-044)

The fortune's redistribution at the novel's end — with Allie and Grace receiving "the bulk of the fortune" and Abigail Rowen receiving a portion — represents the restoration of moral order. (024-088) (025-007)
