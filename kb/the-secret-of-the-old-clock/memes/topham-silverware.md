# Topham Silverware

The silverware and other valuables stolen from the Topham bungalow during the Moon Lake robbery — the physical evidence that allows Nancy to identify and convict the thieves. (014-033) (022-017)

## Description

The stolen items include silverware, furniture, and other household valuables that the Tophams had "sent up" to their Moon Lake bungalow from River Heights. (011-050) These are luxury goods — the kind of possessions the Tophams display to signal their wealth. After the theft, the recovered items are catalogued: items "which apparently belonged to the estate," with the total "amount reached well over" a significant sum. (022-017) The value of the stolen goods underscores the scale of the Tophams' wealth — wealth that, the novel argues, rightfully belongs to others.

## Story Role

The stolen silverware serves as both plot device and thematic symbol. As plot device, it provides the evidence Nancy needs to identify the robbers — physical objects that can be traced, recovered, and presented as proof. As symbol, the silverware represents the Tophams' materialistic values: these are people who measure worth in silver and furniture, not in kindness or justice. The theft of their possessions is a poetic foreshadowing of their larger loss — the fortune itself, stolen (in the novel's moral calculus) from its rightful heirs.

## Plot Significance

The silverware theft is the novel's secondary mystery — a conventional crime that contrasts with the more complex legal puzzle of the missing will. Solving the robbery demonstrates Nancy's competence with tangible evidence (recovering physical objects) before she tackles the more abstract challenge of proving a will's existence through testimony and deduction. The silverware's recovery also parallels the larger theme of restoration — what was stolen is returned, just as the fortune is restored to its rightful heirs.
