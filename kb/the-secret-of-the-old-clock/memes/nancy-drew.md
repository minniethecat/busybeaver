# Nancy Drew

The sixteen-year-old protagonist and amateur [[detective-work|detective]] — daughter of noted criminal lawyer [[carson-drew]], Nancy applies her natural talent for [[detective-work|investigation]] to the search for [[josiah-crowley]]'s missing [[crowley-will-document|second will]]. Her moral conviction that the [[topham-family]] do not deserve the fortune drives her pursuit of [[fairness|justice]] for the [[horner-sisters]] and [[abigail-rowen]]. (001-001) (001-029)

## Role in the Story

Nancy Drew is the central character and driving force of the narrative. Upon learning that the [[richard-topham|Topham]] family has [[inheritance|inherit]]ed [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]'s entire fortune through a likely fraudulent [[will-legal-document|will]], she determines to find the missing [[crowley-will-document|second will]] that would restore [[fairness|justice]] for the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] and [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]. (001-005) (001-029) Her [[detective-work|investigation]] takes her from [[river-heights]] to [[moon-lake]], through interviews with witnesses, a robbery [[detective-work|investigation]] at the [[topham-bungalow]], and ultimately to the discovery of the [[crowley-notebook]] hidden inside the [[crowley-clock]], which leads to the [[hidden|hidden will]] in a [[first-national-bank|bank safety deposit box]]. By the novel's end, she has proven herself as a [[detective-work|detective]] and keeps [[crowley-clock|the clock]] as a "trophy of my first venture." (025-039)

## Character Traits

- Naturally inclined toward [[detective-work|detective]] work: goes at things "like a [[detective-work|detective]]" with keen observation and logical reasoning (001-033)
- Persistent and resourceful: does not give up when initial efforts fail (003-012)
- Compassionate: genuinely [[nancy-roadster|car]]es about the impoverished [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] and elderly [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]] (005-020)
- Proud of her developing skills: takes "a certain amount of pride in her [[detective-work|detective]] work" (012-003)
- Ambitious: views this case as her "first venture as a [[detective-work|detective]]" (025-039)
- Daughter of a lawyer: mentored by her father [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]], whose professional connections exposed her to [[detective-work|detective]] methods (001-035)

## Investigative Method

Nancy's approach to the case reveals a methodical [[detective-work|detective]] process: she begins by gathering background information from her father and newspaper accounts, then conducts field interviews with the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] (Ch. 4), [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]] (Ch. 10), and other witnesses. She follows physical [[clue|clues]] — the [[crowley-notebook]], the deposit box under the alias "[[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]," and the inscription inside [[crowley-clock|the clock]] — combining documentary evidence with witness testimony. Her method is notable for its ethical boundaries: she never lies to obtain information and refuses to break laws, even when doing so might speed her [[detective-work|investigation]]. (001-029) (010-002) (023-028)

> "Oh, I don't know. A mystery always interests me, I guess, and it does seem to me that someone ought to help those poor relatives." — Nancy Drew, explaining her motivation to her father. (001-054)

## Narrative Significance

Nancy Drew is both the protagonist and the narrative lens through which the reader experiences the mystery. Her amateur status — neither a professional [[detective-work|detective]] nor a party to the dispute — makes her relatable and morally unimpeachable. As the first novel in the Nancy Drew series, this story establishes her character, method, and supporting cast for decades of subsequent adventures.

> "I'll always prize this clock as a trophy of my first venture as a detective," she said quietly, turning to Allie and Grace. — Nancy Drew, reflecting on her success at the novel's conclusion. (025-039)
