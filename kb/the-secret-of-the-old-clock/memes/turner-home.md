# Turner Home

The Masonville residence of [[matilda-turner]] and [[edna-turner]], cousins of [[josiah-crowley]] who were among the relatives visited by [[nancy-drew]] during her investigation into the missing will. The sisters lived alone at the outskirts of Masonville, and Nancy's interview with them — though yielding no direct clue about the will's whereabouts — confirmed the family-wide belief that Crowley had intended to disinherit the [[topham-family]]. (008-071) (008-073)

## Location

The Turner home was located at the outskirts of Masonville, the town where Judge Hartgrave's courthouse was situated. Nancy visited the Turners after first stopping at the [[mathews-farm|Mathews farm]] on River Road, continuing in the direction of Masonville. The home was in the city itself but on its periphery — the sisters "lived alone at the outskirts." (008-071) (008-058)

> "Nancy was directed to the home of the Turner sisters. They lived alone at the outskirts of the city." (008-069)

## The Turner Sisters

[[matilda-turner|Matilda]] and [[edna-turner|Edna Turner]] were elderly cousins of [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] — "regular old maids, but awfully nice," as [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] described them. (008-071) They lived modestly, "not having any more money than they needed," yet had been generous to Crowley during his life. [[allie-horner|Allie Horner]] had described them to [[nancy-drew|Nancy]]: "Matilda and Edna Turner — they're cousins. They live in Masonville." (008-041)

The sisters' testimony was consistent with every other relative [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] interviewed: [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] had promised to remember them in a later will. "Matilda and Edna Turner insisted that Josiah Crowley had intimated he would leave them a generous sum." (008-071) Their conviction, shared by all the Crowley relatives, formed a chorus of testimony that made the [[topham-family|Tophams]]' claim to the entire estate morally indefensible — even before the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] was found.

> "Matilda and Edna Turner insisted that Josiah Crowley had intimated he would leave them a generous sum of money." (008-071)

## Role in the Mystery

The Turner home interview was one of Nancy's systematic stops in her investigation, and it proved significant primarily through indirection. The sisters themselves had no idea where the will might be, but Matilda's suggestion that Nancy speak with [[abigail-rowen]] — "She took care of Josiah once when he was sick, and he thought a lot of her" — was the lead that sent Nancy to the [[abigail-rowen-cottage|cottage on West Lake Road]], where she received the clock clue that broke the case open. (008-074)

> "From what the Turner sisters had told her, she was more firmly convinced than ever that a second will existed." (008-076)

The sisters' testimony also reinforced the pattern: every Crowley relative Nancy interviewed believed a second will existed and that the Tophams had no moral right to the fortune. This consensus, validated when the will was found with $20,000 bequests to each sister, confirmed the legitimacy of Nancy's quest. (008-073) (024-043)

The final scene of the Turner sisters at the will reading — arriving "dressed in their best" at the [[drew-home|Drew home]] — provides a quiet emotional payoff: two modest, deserving women whose faith in their cousin's promises was finally vindicated. (024-019)
