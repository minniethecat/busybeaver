# Topham Residence Garden

The garden and grounds of the **[[topham-residence|Topham residence]]** on the avenue in **[[river-heights|River Heights]]** reflect the family's **[[snobbery|social pretensions]]** and lack of genuine taste. Even a casual glance reveals the lawn has been "landscaped with a vengeance."

## Description

In an effort to "do it in proper style," Mrs. Topham has crowded the yard with:
- Sundials
- Benches
- Bird houses
- Statues

The overabundance of decorative elements creates an impression not of elegance but of excess. **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** thinks to herself: "Such lack of taste!"

## Symbolism

The garden symbolizes the Tophams' approach to social advancement: accumulate as many status symbols as possible without understanding how to use them. Just as the garden is overcrowded with mismatched ornaments, the house interior features "expensive oriental rugs clashed with window draperies of a different hue" and paintings "entirely out of place in such a small room."

The garden, like the family's social ambitions, is a failed performance — the Tophams are "cordially welcomed" nowhere, merely "tolerated" or "barred" from the best homes. Their external display cannot compensate for their internal lack of character.
