# Professional Pride

Professional pride distinguishes the novel's virtuous characters from the **[[topham-family|Tophams]]**, who seek wealth without work. **[[grace-horner|Grace Horner]]** and **[[carson-drew|Carson Drew]]** exemplify the dignity of skilled labor.

## Grace Horner's Dressmaking

Grace earns her living as a dressmaker, helping a dressmaker in **[[masonville|Masonville]]** "whenever she can get work" and making all the sisters' clothes. When **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** commissions a dress, Grace refuses payment in advance: "Not until the work is finished. That isn't good business."

When Nancy urges her to accept help, Grace responds with quiet dignity: "Thank you for your kindness, Nancy; but we can't take your money. We'll manage somehow." Her professional pride sustains her through poverty — she will not accept charity, only honest payment for honest work.

## Carson Drew's Legal Practice

Carson Drew is "a noted criminal and mystery-case lawyer, known far and wide for his work as a former district attorney." He offers his services to the **[[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]]** without charge: "And of course there will be no charge for my services." His professional standing enables him to navigate the legal system — obtaining the **[[court-order|court order]]** and administering the estate — while his ethics ensure the will's provisions are honored.

## Contrast with the Tophams

The Tophams represent the antithesis of professional pride. **[[richard-topham|Richard Topham]]** "made his money by gambling on the stock exchange" rather than productive work. **[[cora-topham|Mrs. Topham]]** is "a vapid social climber" whose only ambition is status. They sought Crowley's fortune through manipulation, not merit, and their lack of any genuine professional identity contributes to their moral and financial collapse.
