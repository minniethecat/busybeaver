# Horner Sisters

Grace and Allie Horner — two young women farming marginal land near Moon Lake, promised an inheritance by Josiah Crowley but left with nothing under the first will. (004-005) (004-019)

## Members

- [[grace-horner]] — The more reserved sister; receives the bulk of the fortune at the novel's end (004-019) (024-088)
- [[allie-horner]] — The more outspoken sister; directly appeals to Nancy for help (004-005)

## Description

The Horner sisters are the novel's primary sympathetic victims. Living on a poor farm where "the living room was warm and comfortable" but "nearly bare of furniture," they maintain their dignity despite poverty. (004-030) Grace, the elder sister — "tall and slender, as was her sister, with dark hair and thoughtful brown eyes" and at least four years older than Allie — is the quieter, more reserved of the two. (004-019) Allie, the younger and more outspoken sister, raises chickens to help make ends meet, though "the market hasn't been so high as usual, and the price of eggs has dropped, too." (004-037) Grace helps a dressmaker at Masonville whenever she can find work, and makes all of their clothes as well. (004-036)

Their isolation is profound. As Allie tells Nancy, "Grace and I seldom have a chance to talk with anyone our own age. Sometimes a whole week will go by, and we won't see anyone but the postman." (003-053) Nancy, even in this first encounter, senses "she had encountered something unusual" — a recognition that the sisters' quiet dignity marks them as deserving of something better than the life they have been dealt. (003-053-003-054)

They "had tried hard to make their farm pay" and, despite their circumstances, offer hospitality with genuine warmth: "It's true that we're poor, but we can still offer a guest a cup of tea. Excuse me while I make it." (004-043) Allie is hopeful that with the right resources she could succeed at her poultry business: "I enjoy the work, and I could make it pay if only I could afford to start a flock of white leghorns." (004-038) They were Josiah Crowley's neighbors and "great pets of Crowley's when they were children" — the kind of genuine, caring relationship that the Tophams' opportunistic attention could never match. (001-032)

## Role in the Story

The Horner sisters are the human face of the injustice Nancy seeks to correct. Every detail of their poverty — the bare rooms, the struggling farm, the sold possessions — is a silent accusation against the Tophams' greed. Their relationship with Crowley was authentic and longstanding; he was a familiar, absent-minded presence in their lives, a man Allie remembers fondly: "I've seen him go about hunting for his spectacles when they were on his nose all the time," and "He was always putting things away in what he called a safe place. The place was usually so safe that he could never find it again!" (005-038-005-040)

Crowley himself had promised them a future. His last words to the sisters were: "I have planned a big surprise for you girls. You'll see it in my will." (004-055) When the will surfaced and the entire fortune went to the Tophams instead, Grace voiced the quiet, dignified protest that captures the moral heart of the novel: "It isn't that we were expecting any of the money, but it does seem unfair that the entire fortune should go to them." (004-056)

Their restoration at the novel's end, when Grace receives "the bulk of the fortune" and the farm flourishes with "many new hen houses," is the tangible proof that Nancy's detective work matters. (024-088) (025-013) As Nancy herself declares: "The Tophams deserved to be cut off without a cent. I'm so glad everything came out right and that Allie and Grace received the bulk of the fortune." (024-088)
