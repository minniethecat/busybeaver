# Stolen Goods

In *The Secret of the Old Clock*, **stolen goods** are the physical tokens of crime that Nancy Drew tracks and recovers across multiple chapters. The concept links the robbery subplot to the larger themes of rightful ownership and justice that drive the narrative.

## Definition

Stolen goods are items obtained through theft — property belonging lawfully to one party but held illegally by another. In the novel, the Crowley clock is the most significant stolen item because it contains evidence critical to the inheritance case, but the robbers' van contains a broader haul of merchandise stolen from multiple locations.

## The Crowley Clock as Stolen Goods

The theft of the Crowley clock transforms it from a decorative object into stolen goods with legal and investigative significance. Nancy realizes the clock "contained the notebook telling of what disposal had been made of the Crowley will and that it had been stolen by the robbers" (017-041). She immediately understands the stakes: "She must recover the clock! The thieves must be apprehended and the stolen good returned" (017-042).

Jeff Tucker, the caretaker of the Topham bungalow, confirms the clock's presence before the robbery: "I recollects dat clock just as plain as day. I wouldn't fohget dat clock" (017-040). His testimony establishes that the clock was in the bungalow and was taken by the robbers, transforming it from a missing item into stolen property.

## The Robbers' Van

The robbers' moving van serves as a mobile repository of stolen goods. When Nancy and the marshal finally locate it, "the van contained the stolen goods and it was loaded nearly to the top" (019-024), revealing the scale of the criminal operation. The van contains not only the Topham furniture but also "the goods stolen only a few hours before from" other locations (019-012).

The robbers plan to "head for a large city where they could dispose of the stolen goods" (018-033), indicating an organized fencing operation. The urgency of pursuit is driven by the knowledge that once stolen goods enter a large city's underground markets, recovery becomes nearly impossible.

## The Marshal and Legal Custody

A key tension in the narrative is the conflict between Nancy's need to recover the clock (for its evidentiary value in the inheritance case) and the marshal's duty to impound all stolen goods as evidence in the criminal case. Nancy worries: "once the marshal takes charge of the stolen goods, I'll have no opportunity" to secure the clock first (019-002).

After the robbers are captured, the marshal issues a direct order: "You stay here and guard the stolen goods until we can send another car" (021-058), assigning a deputy to secure the van. Nancy must then navigate around official custody to retrieve the clock — the stolen good that matters most to the inheritance case.

## Recovery and Justice

The recovery of stolen goods serves multiple functions in the novel's resolution:

| Function | Description | PN |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Criminal justice | The robbers are apprehended and the goods returned to owners | (017-042) |
| Inheritance evidence | The clock, recovered from stolen goods, contains the will's location | (017-041) |
| Moral vindication | Nancy's persistence recovers what the Tophams' negligence lost | (019-002) |
| Community restoration | The stolen furniture and merchandise are returned | (019-012) |

Nancy's successful recovery of the clock from among the stolen goods demonstrates her detective skill: she prioritizes the item with the highest evidentiary value from a van full of stolen merchandise, understanding that some stolen goods carry significance beyond their monetary worth.
