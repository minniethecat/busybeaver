# Crowley House

The former River Heights home of [[josiah-crowley]] — the house he lived in independently before the [[topham-family]] persuaded him to make his home with them. After Crowley moved out, he sold the house, and the Tophams got " 'most everything" — the household goods that eventually included the clock concealing the will. (010-018)

## Location

The Crowley house was located in River Heights, though the exact street is not specified in the text. It was Crowley's own property, and he lived there alone after his wife died during the influenza epidemic following World War I. From this house, he would periodically "visit around" with various relatives, letting the house sit empty: "Although well-to-do, he preferred to 'visit around.'" (001-014)

## Description

The house itself is not physically described in detail, but its significance lies in what it represented: Crowley's independence. After his wife's death, he preferred to "visit around" among relatives rather than maintain a settled existence, suggesting the house was modest enough to leave unattended for extended periods. Before moving to the Tophams, he sold the property, and when Nancy inquired about the household goods, Abigail Rowen confirmed: "Oh, the Tophams got 'most everything." (010-018)

> "His wife had died during the influenza epidemic following the World War, and since that time Crowley had made his home with various relatives. Although well-to-do, he preferred to 'visit around.'" (001-014)

> "What became of the household goods?" Nancy inquired. "Oh, the Tophams got 'most everything," Abigail replied. (010-017) (010-018)

> "There must have been a family clock," Nancy mused, half to herself. "A family clock?" Abigail repeated. "Oh, yes, there was a clock." (010-019) (010-020)

> " 'To Grace and Allie Horner, my household furniture now in the possession of Mrs. Richard Topham.'" (024-048)

> "It was generally known about River Heights that the Tophams had practically confiscated Josiah Crowley's furniture at the time they induced him to make his home with them." (024-049)

> "How insulting!" Mrs. Topham cried. "Does Josiah Crowley dare hint that I took his furniture? (024-050)

## Role in the Mystery

The Crowley house is significant as the site of Josiah Crowley's independent life — the period during which he reportedly made a second will. The fact that he sold the house and that his furniture was "practically confiscated" by the Tophams raises the possibility that the hidden will was concealed among his household goods — the very furnishings that later ended up in the Topham residence alongside [[crowley-clock|the old clock]].

This connection between the Crowley house furnishings and the Topham residence is what ultimately leads Nancy to suspect that the clock — part of Crowley's "household goods" — might contain the hidden document. The trail runs from the deserted house, through the confiscated furniture, to the clock, and finally to the will and notebook hidden within it.
