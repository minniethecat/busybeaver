# Charity

The voluntary giving of help, typically in the form of money, to those in need — a recurring motif that both advances the plot through the charity dance and illuminates character through attitudes toward generosity. (010-042)

## Definition

Charity in the novel operates on two levels: as a practical plot device (the charity dance that brings Nancy into social circulation and the Topham household) and as a moral touchstone (revealing characters' generosity or selfishness). The term also carries a pejorative sting when impoverished characters refuse "to take charity," valuing dignity over aid. (009-027) (012-034)

## Role in the Story

The charity dance serves as Nancy's entrée into investigating the Topham family. Selling tickets for the event, she visits the Topham bungalow at Moon Lake and observes their luxurious lifestyle at the expense of the rightful heirs. (010-042) (011-006) The Tophams' dismissive response — "Give it to the charity" — starkly contrasts with Nancy's genuine charitable spirit. (011-039) Beyond the plot mechanics, Crowley's original intent to leave his fortune to charitable causes rather than his relatives reveals his disillusionment with the Tophams' greed. (022-044)
