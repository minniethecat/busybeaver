# Snobbery

The **[[topham-family|Topham family]]**'s snobbery is a defining characteristic that motivates much of the novel's conflict. Nearly everyone in **[[river-heights|River Heights]]** shares "the opinion that the Tophams were snobbish and arrogant."

## Manifestations

The Tophams' snobbery pervades all their interactions. At the department store, **[[ada-topham|Ada Topham]]** demands immediate service: "Perhaps you don't know who we are!" She threatens the floor manager: "My father owns stock in this store. If we report your conduct to him, he could have you discharged."

**[[isabel-topham|Isabel Topham]]** has "acquired an artificial manner of speaking which was both irritating and amusing." Her mother's ambition is that she "marry into a wealthy family."

## Treatment of Josiah Crowley

The Tophams' treatment of **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]** reveals their snobbery most clearly. They induced him to live with them purely for his money, then "treated him unkindly" once the will was secured. They considered his possessions — including the **[[crowley-clock|heirloom clock]]** — as "old junk" unworthy of their "modern home."

## Social Rejection

Despite **[[cora-topham|Mrs. Topham]]**'s efforts to climb socially, the Tophams are never accepted by River Heights society. Carson Drew and Nancy are "cordially welcomed in River Heights homes which merely tolerated the Tophams or, in a few cases, barred them."

## Consequences

The Tophams' snobbery contributes to their downfall. Their cruelty to Crowley leads to their disinheritance. After losing the **[[fortune|fortune]]**, they face **[[stock-market-speculation|financial ruin]]** and are forced to move from their pretentious home on the avenue to a small house, losing the social standing they valued above all else.
