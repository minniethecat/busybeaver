# Richard Topham

The [[fortune|wealth]]y patriarch of the Topham family and the novel's primary antagonist — a shrewd, unscrupulous man who [[inheritance|inherit]]ed [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]'s entire fortune under the first [[will-legal-document|will]] and [[will-legal-document|will]] stop at nothing to prevent a [[crowley-will-document|second will]] from being found. (001-012) (001-028)

## Role in the Story

Richard Topham is the human face of the in[[fairness|justice]] in the novel. Described by [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] as "an old skinflint who made his [[fortune|money]] by gambling on the stock exchange," he represents the corrupting influence of [[fortune|wealth]]. (001-012) He and his family treated [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] with contempt until they learned of his fortune, then "experienced a sudden change of heart" and "begged [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] to make his home with them." (001-016) Their insincerity was transparent to the community: "[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] did not exaggerate. Nearly everyone in [[river-heights|River Heights]] shared the opinion that the [[topham-family|Tophams]] were snobbish and arrogant, and the treatment they accorded old [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] had aroused a great deal of unfavorable comment." (001-013) Once [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] had signed the [[will-legal-document|will]], "the [[topham-family|Tophams]] treated him unkindly" and it "was whispered about that frequently he slipped away to visit his old friends and that he intended to change his [[will-legal-document|will]] again, cutting [[topham-family|the Tophams]] out entirely." (001-017)

[[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] describes Richard Topham as "shrewd" and notes "he isn't noted for his honesty." (001-028) He also warns that "the [[topham-family|Tophams]] don't intend that anyone shall get a cent of it. It's my private opinion that they will take care that a [[crowley-will-document|second will]] is never found." (001-026) Under the first [[will-legal-document|will]], [[topham-family|the Tophams]] possess the fortune; under the second, they lose everything. Richard Topham's desperation to prevent the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] from surfacing drives much of the novel's conflict.

> "The Tophams aren't very well liked in our little city, are they?" — Carson Drew, commenting dryly on the family's reputation. (001-011)

## Character Traits

- Shrewd and calculating: "Richard Topham is shrewd, and he isn't noted for his honesty" (001-028)
- Greedy: accumulated [[fortune|wealth]] through market speculation — "made his [[fortune|money]] by gambling on the stock exchange" (001-012)
- Hypocritical: showed no interest in [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] until his [[fortune|wealth]] became apparent (001-015)
- Unkind: "[[topham-family|the Tophams]] treated him unkindly" when [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] lived with them (001-017)
- Legally advantaged but morally bankrupt: [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] concedes they have a "legal right" but not a moral one (001-030)
- Financially overextended: borrowing against an unsettled [[fortune|estate]], revealing his desperation (002-047) (022-043)
- Unpopular: "nearly everyone in [[river-heights|River Heights]] shared the opinion that the [[topham-family|Tophams]] were snobbish and arrogant" (001-013)

## Role in the Mystery

Richard Topham is the antagonist whose moral failure creates the mystery. Without his greed — and his family's mistreatment of [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] — there would be no need for a [[crowley-will-document|second will]]. He functions as the obstacle [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] must overcome, and his eventual loss of the fortune represents poetic [[fairness|justice]].

> "Richard Topham is an old skinflint who made his money by gambling on the stock exchange." — Nancy Drew, describing the antagonist. (001-012)

> "Well, I'm not making any accusations, Nancy. But I do know that Richard Topham is shrewd, and he isn't noted for his honesty." — Carson Drew. (001-028)

> "The estate is a considerable one, I understand, Nancy, and the Tophams don't intend that anyone shall get a cent of it. It's my private opinion that they will take care that a second will is never found." — Carson Drew. (001-026)

> "The Tophams deserved to be cut off without a cent." — Nancy Drew, after the second will is found. (024-088)

## Narrative Significance

Richard Topham embodies the novel's critique of [[fortune|wealth]] without virtue. His legal victory under the first [[will-legal-document|will]] demonstrates that what is legal is not always just. His ultimate defeat — losing the fortune when the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] is found — affirms the novel's moral universe: greed is punished, and the deserving are rewarded. The revelation that "Richard Topham has been losing heavily in the stock market this last month" and has been "getting credit at a number of the banks on the strength of the inheritance" (022-043) underscores the precariousness of ill-gotten wealth and adds dramatic urgency to [[nancy-drew|Nancy]]'s race to locate [[crowley-will-document|the will]].
