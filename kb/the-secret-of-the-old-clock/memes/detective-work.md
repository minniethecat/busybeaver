# Detective Work

The systematic investigation of a mystery through observation, questioning, and deduction — Nancy Drew's natural talent and chosen method, which she applies to locating Josiah Crowley's missing will. (001-033)

## Definition

Detective work in the context of this novel is the amateur pursuit of truth through methodical investigation. Nancy is described as approaching things "like a detective" with a natural talent for the work: "From her father she had acquired the habit of thinking things through to their logical conclusion. Frequently, Carson Drew had assured her that she went at a thing 'like a detective.'" (001-033) Her father, Carson Drew, a lawyer, has had professional associations with noted detectives who consult him on cases — giving Nancy informal exposure to investigative techniques. (001-035) Unlike a professional detective operating under formal constraints, Nancy's amateur status allows her greater freedom of movement and method. (007-014)

> "From her father she had acquired the habit of thinking things through to their logical conclusion. Frequently, Carson Drew had assured her that she went at a thing 'like a detective.'" (001-033)

## Role in the Story

Detective work is the narrative framework of the entire novel. Nancy's investigation proceeds through classic detective methods: questioning witnesses (Abigail Rowen, Jeff Tucker), examining physical evidence, visiting relevant locations (the Topham bungalow, the Crowley mansion), and assembling clues into a coherent theory. She takes pride in her developing skills: "She took a certain amount of pride in her detective work, and now that she felt she had struck a valuable clue, she did not wish to ask advice from her father. She preferred to solve the mystery herself and then surprise him with the solution." (012-003)

Nancy experiences the frustrations of real detective work as well, doubting herself after early setbacks: "I must admit I have made no progress. I guess I'm not cut out for a detective!" (003-012) Yet she perseveres, using "the instinct of a detective" to eavesdrop on the Topham sisters in the park and gather critical information. (006-058)

By the novel's end, Nancy has proven herself. She removes the Crowley clock from her roadster and "paused an instant to gloat over it," well satisfied with the result of her detective work. (022-011) Other characters recognize her talent: Jeff Tucker calls her "a regular detective," (022-032) and Carson Drew had often remarked that "he enjoyed the detective work of his cases better than the court work." (007-014) Nancy keeps the clock as "a trophy of my first venture as a detective," establishing the pattern for an entire series. (025-039)

> "She took a certain amount of pride in her detective work, and now that she felt she had struck a valuable clue, she did not wish to ask advice from her father. She preferred to solve the mystery herself and then surprise him with the solution." (012-003)

> "No, I haven't forgotten, but I must admit I have made no progress. I guess I'm not cut out for a detective!" — Nancy Drew, momentarily discouraged but never defeated. (003-012)

> "With the instinct of a detective, Nancy Drew crept cautiously closer. The bushes were thick, and by crouching low behind the bench she was able to hear without being seen." (006-058)

> "You're a regular detective, Nancy." — Jeff Tucker, acknowledging Nancy's investigative skill. (022-032)

> "I'll always prize this clock as a trophy of my first venture as a detective. It will serve as a pleasant reminder of a thrilling adventure — and, who knows? perhaps as a promise for the future!" — Nancy Drew. (025-039)
