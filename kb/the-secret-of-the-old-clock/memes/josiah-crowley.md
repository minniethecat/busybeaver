# Josiah Crowley
## Role in the Story

Josiah Crowley is dead before the novel begins, yet he is the most consequential character — every action in the story traces back to his decisions. He was a man who "had made his home with various relatives" after his wife died during the influenza epidemic following World War I, never settling permanently. (001-014) The [[richard-topham|Topham]]s initially showed no interest in him, but when they learned he was [[fortune|wealth]]y, "the [[richard-topham|Topham]] family experienced a sudden change of heart" and "begged Josiah Crowley to make his home with them." (001-016) Crowley recognized their insincerity: "the Tophams treated him unkindly" and it "was whispered about that frequently he slipped away to visit his old friends and that he intended to change his will again, cutting the Tophams out entirely." (001-017)

He told multiple witnesses he intended to change his [[will-legal-document|will]] to benefit the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] and [[charity|charitable causes]], cutting [[topham-family|the Tophams]] out. (001-047) The novel's central question — did he actually write and hide that [[crowley-will-document|second will]]? — drives [[nancy-drew]]'s entire [[detective-work|investigation]], culminating in the discovery of the [[crowley-will-document]] [[secret|hidden]] inside the [[crowley-clock]].

> "It's likely Crowley had made up his mind to change the will. I suspect he intended to cut the Tophams out, but whether or not he did, I have no way of knowing." — Carson Drew, assessing the legal situation. (001-047)

## Character Traits

- Wealthy but eccentric: described as "rather queer in many ways" (001-022)
- Shrewd judge of character: recognized [[topham-family|the Tophams]]' insincerity and planned to cut them out (001-017)
- Generous to those who genuinely [[nancy-roadster|car]]ed for him: intended to leave portions to the Horners and others (001-032)
- [[secret|Secret]]ive about his final intentions: told only a few trusted people about the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] (009-052)
- Formerly a soldier: served in World War I (001-014)
- Used aliases for secrecy: rented a safe deposit box under the name "[[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]" (022-023)


## The Secret Alias

One of Crowley's most revealing actions was his use of the alias **[[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]** when renting safe deposit box 148 at the Masonville National Bank. This secretive measure — using a variant of his own name — reflects both his eccentricity ("rather queer in many ways") and his genuine concern that [[topham-family|the Tophams]] might interfere with his final arrangements. [[nancy-drew]]'s discovery of this alias is a critical breakthrough in tracing the whereabouts of the [[crowley-will-document|second will]]. (022-023) (022-027) (023-028)

> "No wonder the will never came to light," Nancy mused. "Who would have ever thought of looking for it in a safety deposit box listed under an assumed name?" (022-027)


## The Two Wills

The central mystery of the novel revolves around Crowley's testamentary intentions:

| Will | Date | Beneficiaries | Status |
|------|------|--------------|--------|
| First [[will-legal-document|will]] | Early (pre-WWI) | [[richard-topham|Topham]] family (sole heirs) | Probated, disputed |
| Second [[will-legal-document|will]] | Later (post-WWI) | [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]], [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]], charities | [[hidden|Hidden]], sought by [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] |

The first [[will-legal-document|will]] was never updated after Crowley's relationships with his relatives changed dramatically. Multiple witnesses testified that Crowley expressed intent to create a [[crowley-will-document|second will]], but its existence was unproven until [[nancy-drew]] located it [[hidden|concealed]] within the [[crowley-clock]]. (001-043) (024-029)

> "That looks as though Mr. Crowley had made up his mind to write a new will, doesn't it?" — Nancy Drew, connecting the evidence. (001-044)


## Role in the Mystery

Crowley is the absent center of the mystery. Everything turns on what he did in the final months of his life: whether he wrote a [[crowley-will-document|second will]], where he hid it, and who knew about it. The novel is structured as a posthumous character study — [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] pieces together his intentions through the testimony of those who knew him, gradually revealing a man who tried to do the right thing but whose plans were nearly defeated by the greed of those he tried to exclude.

> "Josiah Crowley was rather queer in many ways, but he was a just man." — Community opinion, explaining why his final actions surprised many. (001-022)

> "The Tophams would get the entire fortune, even though they never treated Josiah Crowley like a human being!" — Nancy Drew, expressing the moral outrage that motivates her investigation. (001-008)


## Narrative Significance

Crowley functions as both the MacGuffin (his [[will-legal-document|will]] is the object everyone seeks) and the moral compass (his true intentions, once revealed, affirm the novel's values of [[fairness|fair]]ness and generosity). His absence from the living cast makes him a [[mystery|puzzle]] to be solved rather than a character to be developed, and the gradual revelation of his character through others' testimony is a classic mystery structure. The alias "[[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]" adds a layer of intrigue, revealing a man who, despite his eccentricity, took careful measures to protect his final wishes from those he distrusted.

> "I suspect he intended to cut the Tophams out, but whether or not he did, I have no way of knowing." — Carson Drew, capturing the central uncertainty that powers the entire novel. (001-047)
