# Will (Legal Document)

The legal instrument by which Josiah Crowley declared the disposition of his estate — the discovery of a missing second will drives the entire plot of the novel. (001-007)

## Definition

A will is a legal document in which a person (the testator) states how their property and assets should be distributed after death. In the story, Josiah Crowley drafted a first will that left his entire fortune to the Topham family. (001-018) However, multiple witnesses suggest he grew dissatisfied with the Tophams and intended to — or did — create a second will more favorable to the Horner sisters and Abigail Rowen. (001-015) (001-017) (001-039)

Carson Drew's succinct summary of the legal reality — "A will is a will, you know" — captures the novel's central predicament: an unfair document, once lawfully executed, carries binding force unless a superseding will is discovered. (001-007) Nancy immediately grasps the injustice: "But it does seem unfair that all the money should go to them. Especially when they never treated Josiah Crowley like a human being!" (001-008)

## Role in the Story

The will is the central puzzle of the novel. The Tophams enjoy Crowley's fortune under the first will, but Carson Drew tells Nancy that it could be contested if evidence of a later will emerges. (001-029) (001-030) He also reveals a crucial clue: he saw Crowley at the First National Bank with Henry Rolsted — "the attorney who specializes in wills and legal documents" — discussing a will, and Crowley made an appointment to call at Rolsted's office the following day. (001-041) (001-042) (001-043)

Nancy presses her father: "That looks as though Mr. Crowley had made up his mind to write a new will, doesn't it?" And Carson Drew concedes: "That was the thought which passed through my mind at the time." (001-044) (001-045)

At the fateful luncheon, Henry Rolsted confirms the suspicions: "About a year ago Crowley came to my office and told me that he wanted to draw up a new will. He indicated that he intended to cut the Tophams off without a cent. He expressed a desire to write the will himself, and asked me a number of questions. I told him exactly how to proceed." (002-069)

Nancy's detective work is motivated by the search for this missing second will, which she believes Crowley hid somewhere — possibly inside a clock, as Abigail Rowen cryptically recalls: "The clock. That was it! The clock!" (010-001) (010-002)

## Legal Themes

The novel explores the tension between legality and justice. The first will is legally valid but morally repugnant — the Tophams manipulated a vulnerable old man into making it, then mistreated him afterward. The missing second will represents the possibility of aligning the law with what is right. But as Carson Drew warns, "If I were you, I would forget about the case" — the law without evidence is powerless, and without the document, the Tophams keep the fortune. (002-082)

The resolution depends on the physical recovery of the legal instrument. The law, in this world, is not self-executing; it requires an active agent — Nancy — to bring the evidence to light. This makes the will concept not merely a passive MacGuffin but a test of the protagonist's determination and ingenuity.
