# Masonville National Bank

A financial institution in the neighboring town of [[masonville|Masonville]] where [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] secretly stored his [[crowley-will-document|second will]] in safety deposit box #148, registered under his alias [[josiah-harkston|Josiah Harkston]]. The discovery and retrieval of this document from the bank is the turning point that resolves the novel's central mystery.

## Description

The Masonville National Bank is a standard small-town bank located in [[masonville|Masonville]], a town within driving distance of [[river-heights|River Heights]]. Its physical appearance is unremarkable — what matters is the safety deposit service it provided. Box #148, registered under the alias "Josiah Harkston," held [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]'s true [[crowley-will-document|last will]], hidden from [[topham-family|the Tophams]] who would have destroyed it.

The bank is connected with [[judge-hartgrave|Judge Hartgrave]]'s office, also located in Masonville, suggesting that [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] chose this town deliberately — it was distant enough from [[river-heights|River Heights]] to keep his secret safe, yet accessible to those who knew where to look.

## The Safety Deposit Box

[[josiah-crowley|Crowley]]'s choice to use a safety deposit box under a false name reveals his state of mind in his final years. He trusted neither [[topham-family|the Tophams]], who had treated him "more shabbily than anyone else" (002-071), nor the [[river-heights|River Heights]] legal establishment, where word might reach the family. An out-of-town bank, a numbered box, and an alias — these were the precautions of a man who knew his true intentions might be contested.

> "To whom it may concern: My last will and testament will be found in safety deposit box number 148 in the Masonville National Bank. The box is under the name of Josiah Harkston." — Josiah Crowley's note, quoted by Carson Drew

## Role in the Story

The bank enters the narrative at the climax of [[nancy-drew|Nancy]]'s investigation. After learning of the "Josiah Harkston" alias from [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]], [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] and [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] drive to Masonville to retrieve the document.

The trip to Masonville was quickly accomplished, and Nancy and her father stopped the roadster in front of the Masonville National Bank. After locating the Crowley will in safety deposit box #148, Carson Drew and his daughter read it carefully. (018-022)–(018-024)

Without disclosing the contents to anyone beforehand, [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]] called a special meeting of all the Crowley relatives at [[drew-home|the Drew home]]. The will from the Masonville bank was the centerpiece:

> "As you have probably learned by this time, a second will of the late Josiah Crowley has been found in a safety deposit box in a Masonville bank. The will is unusually long, and with your permission I will read only the portions which have to do with the disposal of the property." — Carson Drew addressing the relatives

The reading of this will from the Masonville bank overturns the earlier will that favored [[topham-family|the Tophams]] and redirects the inheritance to the deserving relatives — [[allie-horner|the Horner sisters]], [[abigail-rowen]], and others whom [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] truly wished to benefit.

## Role in the Mystery

The Masonville National Bank is a crucial link in the chain of evidence. Without the security of its safety deposit system and the anonymity provided by [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]]'s alias, the [[crowley-will-document|true will]] would likely have been discovered and destroyed by [[topham-family|the Tophams]]. The bank represents the institutional trust that [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] could not place in individuals — a safe harbor for his true intentions until [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] could uncover them.

The bank's role is passive but essential: it preserves. Unlike the active characters who drive the plot, the bank simply holds — but what it holds changes everything.
