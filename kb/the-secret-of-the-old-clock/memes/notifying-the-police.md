# Notifying the Police

The critical decision point in [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]'s investigation when she recognizes she must involve [[melborne-marshal|Marshal Melborne]] to lawfully apprehend the robbers she has identified — a moment that tests the balance between independent detective work and legal procedure.

## Summary

After identifying the three robbers responsible for stealing [[topham-silverware|the Topham silverware]] and [[crowley-clock|the Crowley clock]], [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] faced a dilemma: attempt to recover the stolen goods herself, or notify the authorities first. Her first attempt was delayed when [[jeff-drugging|Jeff Tucker]] — whom she had stationed as a lookout — was drugged and incapacitated.

> "For goodness's sake, Jeff, don't you realize I've been waiting for you? Those robbers will probably be in the next state before we even succeed in notifying the police!" (018-030)

Nancy's frustration with [[jeff-drugging|Jeff]] underscores the urgency: with each delay, the robbers could escape. She recognized the robbers had likely parked their van in the garage behind the inn. "I must notify the marshal at once!" Nancy told herself.

> "If only I could get my hands on the clock before I notify the police! Once the marshal takes charge of the stolen goods, I'll have to wait for official procedures." (018-033)

This moment reveals Nancy's internal conflict — her desire to personally recover [[crowley-clock|the Crowley clock]] versus the necessity of lawful procedure. She ultimately chooses to notify [[melborne-marshal|the marshal]], leading to the [[capturing-the-robbers|successful capture of the robbers]].

## The Decision

[[nancy-drew|Nancy]]'s choice to notify the authorities was not automatic. As an amateur detective who had operated independently throughout the investigation — breaking into the [[topham-bungalow|Topham bungalow]], hiding in closets, and interviewing witnesses under false pretenses — she was accustomed to working alone. But the scale of the robbery operation and the physical danger posed by the [[robber-leader|robber gang]] made solitary action impractical.

Her decision reflected a maturing judgment: effective [[justice|justice]] requires both independent investigation AND institutional authority. The [[melborne-marshal|marshal]] had resources — manpower, legal authority, the power of arrest — that [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] could not replicate. Her willingness to share credit with the marshal, rather than insisting on solving everything herself, marked an important step in her development as a detective.

## Narrative Significance

Notifying the police is a pivotal character moment for [[nancy-drew|Nancy]]. Throughout the novel, she operates independently — breaking into [[topham-residence|the Topham bungalow]], hiding in closets, and conducting covert surveillance. The decision to involve [[melborne-marshal|the marshal]] marks the point where amateur detective work must yield to lawful authority for the final resolution.

This choice distinguishes Nancy Drew from purely vigilante heroes. She does not try to apprehend the robbers herself — she recognizes the limits of her role and defers to the proper authorities. The novel's implicit message is that [[justice|justice]] is achieved through a partnership: the detective finds the truth, and the law enforces it.

The sequence also serves a narrative function: by delaying the notification (through [[jeff-drugging|Jeff]]'s incapacitation), the story builds tension. The reader knows the robbers could escape at any moment, and each delay raises the stakes.
