# Evidence

Information, objects, or testimony that help establish facts in an investigation — Nancy Drew's detective method relies on gathering and interpreting evidence to reconstruct events and identify wrongdoers. (014-036)

## Definition

Evidence in detective work is any material, testimony, or observation that helps prove or disprove a hypothesis about what happened. In the novel, evidence takes multiple forms: documentary (the will itself), testimonial (Abigail Rowen's account of Crowley's intentions), physical (the stolen furniture and silverware), and circumstantial (the clues pointing to the robbers' identity). Nancy views the evidence methodically — "as she viewed the evidence, the suspicion which lurked in her mind became a certainty." (014-036)

## Role in the Story

Evidence gathering is the practical backbone of Nancy's detective work. After the Moon Lake bungalow robbery, she surveys the scene and collects evidence that points to specific suspects. (014-036) Later, she assembles "enough evidence to convict" the robbers, satisfying the legal threshold for police action. (021-039) The novel concludes with abundant "evidence" of a different kind — "the large number of chickens in evidence" at the Horner farm, signaling prosperity restored. (025-013)
