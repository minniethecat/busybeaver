# Fairness

The principle of just and equitable treatment — repeatedly invoked in the novel as the moral standard that the Crowley inheritance dispute violates and that Nancy Drew's investigation seeks to restore. (001-008) (004-056) (004-060)

## Definition

Fairness is the quality of treating people equally and without favoritism or discrimination. In the context of inheritance, it is the belief that a testator's wealth should go to those who genuinely cared for them, not merely to the nearest legal relative. The novel explicitly states this tension: "it does seem unfair that all the money should go to them" — "them" being the Tophams, who neglected Crowley in life. (001-008) Carson Drew concedes the unfairness while noting the legal reality: "No, it isn't fair. But it is legal, and I'm afraid nothing can be done about it." (001-032)

> "It does seem unfair that all the money should go to them. Especially when they never treated Josiah Crowley like a human being!" — Nancy Drew, recognizing the moral violation at the heart of the case. (001-008)

## Role in the Story

Fairness is the moral engine of the novel. Every sympathetic character recognizes the unfairness of the Tophams receiving the entire fortune while the Horner sisters and Abigail Rowen — who genuinely cared for Crowley — receive nothing. Nancy puts it plainly: "The Tophams don't deserve the fortune, father. It doesn't seem fair." (001-031) Grace Horner gives voice to the same sentiment: "It isn't that we were expecting any of the money, but it does seem unfair that the entire fortune should go to them." (004-056)

The Horner sisters embody the dignity of those who accept unfairness without resentment. Despite their poverty — "It's true that we're poor, but we can still offer a guest a cup of tea," Grace says (004-043) — they do not demand justice. Instead, Nancy advocates for them: "It seems only fair that you should have your inheritance." (004-060) The novel's resolution restores fairness: the second will is found, the estate is redistributed, and the Tophams' unjust enrichment is undone. Nancy's entire quest is an effort to make the legal outcome align with the fair outcome.

> "The Tophams don't deserve the fortune, father. It doesn't seem fair." — Nancy Drew, stating the moral problem clearly. (001-031)

> "No, it isn't fair. But it is legal, and I'm afraid nothing can be done about it." — Carson Drew, articulating the gap between law and justice. (001-032)

> "It isn't that we were expecting any of the money, but it does seem unfair that the entire fortune should go to them. They never cared a snap of their fingers for Uncle Josiah." — Grace Horner. (004-056)

> "It seems only fair that you should have your inheritance." — Nancy Drew, advocating for the Horner sisters. (004-060)

> "I'm sure you're not interested in our misfortunes. It's true that we're poor, but we can still offer a guest a cup of tea." — Grace Horner, demonstrating dignity in the face of unfairness. (004-043)
