# Josiah Harkston

The pseudonym under which [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]] rented safety deposit box 148 at the [[first-national-bank|Masonville National Bank]] — the hiding place of the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] that [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] ultimately discovers. The name "Josiah Harkston" is not a separate person but a legal shadow: Crowley's final and most effective act of concealment.

## The Alias

"Josiah Harkston" preserves Crowley's first name — Josiah — while replacing his surname with a fictional one. The choice is psychologically telling: Crowley wanted a name he could remember (his own given name) but one sufficiently different from "Crowley" to elude casual discovery. The alias was essential to his plan to keep the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] secret from the [[topham-family|Topham family]], who had already proven they would stop at nothing to secure his fortune.

## Role in the Investigation

When [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] tracks [[crowley-clock|the clock]] to the [[first-national-bank|Masonville National Bank]], she learns from [[bank-president|the bank president]] that the safety deposit box is not under Crowley's name: "according to our record a Josiah Harkston rented box 148 from us, but the past year the rent has not been paid." [023-031] The bank president is initially reluctant to allow access: "It may be that Crowley and Harkston were the same person, as you say, but we have no authority to permit you to examine the contents." [023-033]

The alias creates a legal obstacle that only [[judge-hartgrave|Judge Hartgrave]]'s authority can overcome. Without his intervention, the box — and the will within it — would have remained sealed indefinitely.

> "No wonder [[crowley-will-document|the will]] never came to light. Who would have ever thought of looking for it in a safety deposit box under the name of Josiah Harkston?" [022-027]

## Detection Analysis

The "Josiah Harkston" alias is the final puzzle piece in Nancy's investigation. Every earlier clue — [[crowley-clock|the clock]], the [[topham-bungalow|Moon Lake bungalow]], the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]]' testimony, [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]'s disclosure — pointed toward the existence of a [[crowley-will-document|second will]]. But only by understanding that Crowley used a false name could Nancy find the document itself.

The alias represents a test of detective reasoning. Nancy must connect three facts: (1) Crowley was secretive and foresighted, (2) he had a safety deposit box, and (3) the box was not under his own name. The deduction that "Harkston" must be Crowley requires confidence in her own investigative chain — the kind of confidence that comes only from having traced every other link correctly.

> "The box is under the name of Josiah Harkston." [022-023]

## Narrative Significance

The Harkston alias functions as the novel's final mystery-within-a-mystery. It demonstrates that even a seemingly straightforward act — renting a bank box — can become a crucial piece of detective work when done under a false identity. The alias also reinforces the novel's thematic interest in [[hidden|secrecy]] and revelation: what the Tophams believed was their undisputed inheritance was always resting on a secret waiting to be found, guarded by nothing more than a carefully chosen name.

The alias is also a testament to Crowley's character. Even in his final, failing days, he possessed the clarity of mind to construct an identity just different enough to protect his true intentions — a final act of defiance against the family that had treated him so [[kindness|unkindly]].
