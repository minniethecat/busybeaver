# Abigail Rowen's Cottage Interior

The interior of **[[abigail-rowen-cottage|Abigail Rowen's cottage]]** on the **[[west-lake-road|West Lake Road]]** reveals the depth of her **[[poverty|poverty]]** and isolation. The contrast between the exterior decay and Abigail's quiet dignity inside underscores the injustice of her situation.

## The Living Room

When **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** pushes open the door, she finds Abigail lying on a couch under "a ragged old shawl, her withered face drawn with pain." The room is "dreary" — shades drawn, darkness prevailing.

## The Kitchen

Nancy's inspection of the kitchen cupboards reveals they are "practically empty." The entire food supply consists of "a little flour and sugar and a cheap can of soup." In a jar in the cupboard, Abigail's life savings amount to "less than five dollars."

Nancy does not take the money but instead purchases ten dollars' worth of groceries with her own funds. She also "built a fire in the kitchen range and put on the teakettle" and "raised the drawn shades and permitted the warm sunshine to flood into the room."

## Transformation Through Care

Nancy's intervention transforms the space — from a dark sickroom to a bright, warm environment where Abigail can sit up on the couch, drink tea, and talk. This physical transformation parallels the emotional change: Abigail becomes "more cheerful and seemed to gain strength." It is in this transformed space that Abigail shares the crucial memory of **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]**'s **[[crowley-notebook|notebook]]**, triggered by the chiming of her **[[mantel-clock-abigail|mantel clock]]**.
