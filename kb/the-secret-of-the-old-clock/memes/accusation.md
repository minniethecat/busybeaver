# Accusation

In *The Secret of the Old Clock*, **accusation** serves as a tool of manipulation — primarily wielded by the Topham family to silence opposition and assert dominance over the Crowley inheritance dispute. The novel depicts accusation both as an intimidation tactic (when used falsely by the powerful) and as a call for accountability (when grounded in truth and evidence).

## Definition

An accusation is a claim that someone has done something wrong or illegal. In the novel, accusations are frequently made without evidence, functioning as intimidation tactics rather than genuine legal claims.

Carson Drew, as an experienced criminal lawyer, draws a clear distinction: "Well, I'm not making any accusations, Nancy. But I do know that..." (001-028) — he withholds judgment until evidence is established, modeling the proper legal approach to accusation.

## The Shopgirl Incident

The most vivid illustration of false accusation occurs in Chapter 6, when Nancy witnesses Ada Topham in a department store. Ada carelessly brushes against a counter of expensive vases, sweeping a large fragile bowl to the floor (006-009). When a saleswoman politely asks her to pay for the damage, Ada turns on her:

> "I'll not pay for it!" she snapped. "I didn't break it!" (006-013)

Ada then accuses the saleswoman herself: "This impudent girl claims I broke a vase," Ada stormed, addressing the floor manager. "I wasn't even near the counter at the time!" (006-016). She doubles down by demanding the clerk pay from her own salary: "If she hadn't been so stupid she wouldn't have broken the vase!" (006-020).

The floor manager is caught in a dilemma — he knows "it would be a serious error to accuse well-to-do customers falsely" (006-018) — and is inclined to give the wealthy Tophams the benefit of the doubt despite the evidence.

Nancy intervenes, speaking truth to power: "You must be mistaken, Miss Topham," she said quietly. "I am certain the saleswoman did not break the bowl, for I saw the accident myself" (006-022). She adds: "Perhaps it isn't my business, but I can't permit you to accuse this girl of something she didn't do" (006-024).

Ada is forced to pay fifty dollars for the imported Italian bowl but threatens retaliation: "But let me warn you the matter won't be dropped here" (006-038), and to Nancy: "You'll pay for this outrage!" (006-040).

## The Topham Pattern

The shopgirl incident is not an isolated case — it reveals a systematic pattern of behavior by the Topham family:

| Element | Description | PN |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Deflection | Ada breaks the bowl but immediately blames the saleswoman | (006-013) |
| Status exploitation | The floor manager hesitates because the Tophams are wealthy customers | (006-018) |
| Reversal | Ada frames herself as the victim of an "impudent" clerk | (006-016) |
| Intimidation | Threats against both the store and Nancy follow the failed accusation | (006-038) |
| False narrative | Ada lies outright about what happened, despite eyewitness testimony | (006-022) |

This pattern mirrors the Tophams' larger strategy in the Crowley inheritance dispute: making claims without evidence, bullying those who challenge them, and using their social status to shield themselves from accountability.

## Accusation and the Clock Theft

Later in the novel, the Tophams threaten to accuse Nancy of stealing the Crowley clock. However, Carson Drew counters that entering a house that was left open does not constitute breaking and entering (022-037). Here the legal perspective prevails: a valid accusation requires a legal basis, and the Tophams' threat collapses under scrutiny.

## Legal Perspective

Carson Drew embodies the novel's ethical standard regarding accusation. As "a noted criminal and mystery-case lawyer, known far and wide for his work as a former district attorney" (001-003), he represents the principle that accusations must be supported by evidence.

His restraint — refusing to make accusations without proof — stands in direct contrast to the Tophams' reckless use of false charges as weapons.
