# Capturing the Robbers

The climactic resolution event in which the gang of furniture thieves who stole [[topham-silverware|the Topham silverware]] and [[crowley-clock|the Crowley clock]] are apprehended through [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]'s investigative work and the intervention of [[melborne-marshal|Marshal Melborne]] and his deputies. The capture recovers the stolen property and brings the novel's criminal subplot to its resolution alongside the [[crowley-will-document|will]] mystery.

## Summary

After [[nancy-drew|Nancy]]'s prolonged investigation into the [[bungalow-robbery|Topham bungalow robbery]], she identifies the three robbers and the location of their hideout. Despite delays caused by [[jeff-drugging|Jeff Tucker]]'s drugged state and communication difficulties, Nancy eventually succeeds in [[notifying-the-police|notifying the authorities]].

> A buzzer rang and soon a marshal, followed by several men, entered. Nancy quickly told them all she knew about the Topham robbery. (018-011)

The pursuit spans three towns — from [[melborne|Melborne]], through the countryside, toward [[garwin|Garwin]] — with [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] leading the [[melborne-marshal|marshal]]'s convoy in her [[nancy-roadster|roadster]]. (018-023) (021-009)

> "Follow me!" Nancy cried to the marshal. (018-023)

The marshal and his deputies followed, though there were moments of doubt: word came that "the marshal and his deputies may have given up." (021-011)

> The marshal and his deputies sprang from the car. (018-029)

> "Doesn't the lefthand road lead to Garwin?" The marshal nodded. (018-032)

When [[melborne-marshal|Marshal Melborne]] finally closes in with his men, the robbers are taken into custody. In the aftermath, Nancy deflects praise with characteristic modesty:

> "It was only an accident that took me to the Topham bungalow at the critical moment. I don't deserve any credit for the roundup." — Nancy Drew, modestly. (021-044)

The marshal, however, sees her clearly:

> "Not many girls would have used their brains the way you did." — Marshal Melborne to Nancy. (021-045)

The marshal is astonished that [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] does not seek public credit — or any reward at all:

> "I don't want a reward." — Nancy Drew. (021-047)

> "You don't want any credit for capturing the robbers?" the marshal gasped in astonishment. (021-050)

Nancy's refusal of credit — captured in the marshal's astonished reaction — is a testament to her character: she pursued [[justice|justice]], not fame.

The capture recovers the stolen goods — including [[topham-silverware|the Topham silverware]] and [[crowley-clock|the Crowley clock]] — and brings the criminal subplot to its resolution. The event occurs in parallel with the resolution of the [[crowley-will-document|will]] mystery in [[drew-home|the Drew home]], together forming the novel's dual climax.

## Narrative Significance

The capture of the robbers is the action climax of the novel, resolving the criminal investigation that runs parallel to the [[crowley-will-document|will]] mystery. Unlike the legal resolution of the will, which unfolds in [[drew-home|the Drew home]] through discussion and legal procedure, this climax involves physical pursuit, danger, and direct confrontation — providing narrative balance between intellectual and physical problem-solving.

[[nancy-drew|Nancy]]'s refusal of credit is the moral heart of the scene. Where the [[topham-family|Tophams]] spent the entire novel grasping for [[fortune|wealth]] they did not deserve, Nancy solves the crime and steps back — demonstrating that justice, not recognition, is the detective's true reward. The marshal's astonishment at this choice underscores how unusual her values are in a world driven by self-interest.
