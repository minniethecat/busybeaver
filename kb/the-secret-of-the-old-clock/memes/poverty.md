# Poverty

**Poverty** serves as both a narrative engine and a moral divider in *The Secret of the Old Clock*. The Crowley fortune's disposition matters most to those who have least, and the story repeatedly tests whether wealth or character defines a person's worth.

## The Crowley Relatives' Circumstances

Josiah Crowley's relatives — the Horner sisters, the Turner sisters, the Mathews brothers, and Abigail Rowen — are all described as living in poverty. "The other relatives are in poverty," Carson Drew notes (001-032), explaining why they cannot afford to contest the Tophams' will. Despite their circumstances, these relatives had shown kindness to Crowley when the Tophams would not.

> "Allie and Grace Horner are as poor as church mice, but they're proud, too," Nancy ended. "If only we could do something to help them! They deserve a portion of the Crowley estate, but they'll never get it unless someone takes a hand." (005-022)
## The Horner Sisters

Allie and Grace Horner are the story's primary embodiment of dignified poverty. They live on a small farm, raise chickens and vegetables, and struggle through winters without steady income. Grace "helps a dressmaker at Masonville whenever she can get work" (004-038), while Allie manages the outdoor work. Nancy describes them as "poor as church mice, but they're proud, too" (005-022).

## Poverty and Morality

The story draws a clear contrast: the impoverished relatives treated Crowley with kindness and respect, while the wealthy Tophams "treated him unkindly" after securing his will (001-032). Poverty in the novel is associated with virtue — the poor characters are hardworking, generous, and dignified — while wealth (in the Tophams' case) correlates with greed and cruelty.

## Resolution

The discovery of the second will transforms the relatives' circumstances overnight. Each receives a substantial bequest, and by the story's end the Horner sisters are "fixed for life" (022-056), their poverty replaced by the security that justice demands.
