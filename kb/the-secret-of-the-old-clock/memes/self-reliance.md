# Self-Reliance

Self-reliance is a defining virtue in _The Secret of the Old Clock_, embodied by characters who solve problems through their own competence rather than depending on others.

## Nancy Drew

**[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** is remarkably self-reliant for a sixteen-year-old. She runs the **[[drew-household|Drew household]]** after her mother's death, manages "under her skillful direction everything ran smoothly." When she gets a **[[flat-tire-moon-lake|flat tire]]** on the way to **[[moon-lake|Moon Lake]]**, she changes it herself. When the **[[launch-engine-failure|camp launch engine fails]]**, she works on it for eight hours until it restarts. When **[[nancy-trapped-in-closet|locked in a closet]]** by robbers, she uses a **[[closet-rod|closet rod]]** as a lever to break free.

## The Horner Sisters

**[[allie-horner|Allie]]** and **[[grace-horner|Grace Horner]]** survive through their own labor after their parents' deaths. Grace makes all their clothes and takes dressmaking work in **[[masonville|Masonville]]**. Allie raises chickens. Despite their **[[poverty|poverty]]**, they repeatedly refuse financial help from Nancy: "We can't take your money. We'll manage somehow."

## The Reward of Self-Reliance

The novel rewards self-reliance. Nancy's independent investigation succeeds where professionals might have failed. The Horner sisters' **[[inheritance|inheritance]]** enables their **[[horner-farm-transformation|farm transformation]]** into a thriving chicken enterprise — but the skills and work ethic were already theirs.

In contrast, the **[[topham-family|Tophams]]** depend on inheritance and **[[stock-market-speculation|speculation]]**, producing nothing themselves. Their lack of self-reliance leaves them helpless when fortune turns against them.
