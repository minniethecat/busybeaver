# Ada Topham

The younger of the two [[richard-topham|Topham]] sisters — described by [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] as "even worse" than her father, Ada is "tall and slender to the point of being termed 'skinny'" with "dark eyes" that "flashed angrily" when she is crossed. (001-012) (002-029) She embodies the worst traits of the [[topham-family|Topham]] family: arrogance, rudeness, and an unshakeable sense of entitlement.

## Role in the Story

Ada Topham, alongside her sister [[isabel-topham|Isabel]], represents the social face of [[topham-family|Topham]] villainy. [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] describes the two sisters as "stuck-up creatures" who are "even worse" than their father [[richard-topham|Richard]]. (001-012) Ada's rudeness is on full display when [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] encounters both sisters at a department store. Impatient at having to wait for service, Ada says petulantly: "We've been standing here nearly ten minutes! Send a saleswoman to us immediately!" (002-022) When the floor manager explains the delay, "Ada interrupted rudely: 'Perhaps you don't know who we are!'" (002-024) She then escalates: "Such service! Do you realize that my father owns stock in this store? If we report your conduct to him, he could have you discharged." (002-027)

Ada's physical description reinforces her unpleasant character: "Ada tossed her head and her dark eyes flashed angrily. In spite of the expensive clothes she wore, she was anything but attractive, for she was tall and slender to the point of being termed 'skinny.' Now that her face was distorted with anger, she was positively ugly." (002-029) When she encounters [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] in the store, she "gave no indication that she had even noticed her." (002-033)

Her rudeness extends to physical carelessness: in the store, she carelessly brushes against a counter of expensive vases, causing a fifty-dollar imported Italian bowl to crash to the floor. When confronted, she denies responsibility and tries to blame the saleswoman, with [[isabel-topham|Isabel]] "solemnly" agreeing to the untruth. (006-009) (006-016)

At the novel's climax, when the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] is read and [[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]] is revealed to receive seventy-five thousand dollars, "Ada Topham demanded harshly: 'That old lady gets seventy-five thousand dollars? What did she ever do for Crowley?' Angrily, she turned to her mother. 'We kept him for years. And this is the pay we get!'" (024-040) Her outrage reveals that she values [[fortune|money]] far above human decency.

## Character Traits

- Rude and petulant: complains loudly about service and flaunts family status (002-022) (002-024)
- Arrogant and entitled: assumes her family name commands special treatment (002-024)
- Vindictive: threatens the floor manager after being forced to pay for the broken vase (006-038)
- Cold and dismissive: pointedly ignores [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] in public (002-033)
- Physically unattractive through anger: "tall and slender to the point of being termed 'skinny,'" her "face was distorted with anger" (002-029)
- Greedy: her first reaction to the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] is fury about [[fortune|money]] going to others (024-040)
- Dishonest: tries to shift blame for breaking the vase onto an innocent saleswoman (006-016)
- Social climber: part of a family described as "vapid social climbers" (001-012)

## Role in the Mystery

Ada Topham is not directly involved in the legal dispute over the estate, but she provides the novel's most vivid portrait of [[topham-family|Topham]] arrogance. Her rudeness at the shop, her dismissive treatment of [[nancy-drew|Nancy]], her attempt to frame an innocent saleswoman, and her angry reaction to losing the fortune all serve to make [[topham-family|the Tophams]]' eventual defeat satisfying. She is the character readers most love to hate, and her presence makes the novel's moral lesson — that [[fortune|wealth]] without character is repellent — visible and entertaining.

> "Perhaps you don't know who we are!" — Ada Topham, interrupting the floor manager rudely. (002-024)

> "Such service! Do you realize that my father owns stock in this store? If we report your conduct to him, he could have you discharged." — Ada Topham, wielding her family's power. (002-027)

> "We've been standing here nearly ten minutes! Send a saleswoman to us immediately!" — Ada Topham, petulantly demanding service. (002-022)

> "That old lady gets seventy-five thousand dollars? What did she ever do for Crowley? We kept him for years. And this is the pay we get!" — Ada Topham, when the second will is read. (024-040)

## Narrative Significance

Ada Topham gives the [[topham-family|Topham]] family a vivid, personal villainy that [[richard-topham|Richard Topham]]'s more abstract financial scheming lacks. Her rudeness is both entertaining and morally instructive — a demonstration that [[fortune|wealth]] without character is repellent rather than admirable. Her physical description — "tall and slender to the point of being termed 'skinny,'" her face "distorted with anger" (002-029) — makes the novel's moral judgment visible on her body, a technique consistent with the novel's approach to character. Her eventual defeat, when the [[crowley-will-document|second will]] strips her family of the fortune, provides the reader with the satisfaction of poetic justice.
