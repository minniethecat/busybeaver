# Chance Meeting

The serendipitous encounter between [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] and the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] on a train that first alerts Nancy to the injustice of [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]'s [[crowley-will-document|estate settlement]] and launches her investigation into [[the-lost-will|the missing will]].

## Summary

While traveling, [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] happened to sit near two plainly dressed girls — [[allie-horner|Allie]] and [[grace-horner|Grace Horner]] — who were discussing their family's hardship. The sisters had been promised an inheritance by their late relative, [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]], but [[topham-family|the Tophams]] had taken everything under the original [[crowley-will-document|will]]. Nancy overheard their conversation and was moved by their plight.

> Nancy's interest in the Crowley case had intensified since her chance meeting with the Horner girls, and it was with impatience that she awaited the conference.

The conversation revealed that [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]] had frequently told the [[horner-sisters|Horner sisters]] he would remember them in his will, and that they had believed him. The chance encounter transforms Nancy from a bystander into an advocate, setting the entire investigation in motion.

## Narrative Significance

The chance meeting is the story's inciting incident. Without this serendipitous encounter, [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] would not have learned of [[topham-family|the Tophams]]' injustice toward [[josiah-crowley|Crowley]]'s poorer relatives. The device of the "chance meeting" reflects the novel's theme that justice requires both opportunity and an alert observer — Nancy's willingness to listen and act on what she overheard is as important as the coincidence itself.
