# Horner Farm

The modest farmhouse where Grace and Allie Horner live — a place of dignified poverty that stands in stark contrast to the Topham bungalow and embodies the injustice of the disputed inheritance. (004-019)

## Location

The Horner farm is located in the Moon Lake area, accessible from the roads Nancy travels while investigating. It is rural farmland, consistent with the description of the poor land along the River Road. (004-019)

## Description

The Horner farmhouse is "warm and comfortable" but "nearly bare of furniture" — the sisters have sold most of their possessions to survive. (004-030) Despite their poverty, the Horner girls have "tried hard to make their farm pay," and they maintain their dignity — "It's true that we're poor, but we can still offer a guest some food." (004-030) (004-043) The farm's condition reflects the injustice: these hardworking young women, who genuinely cared for Josiah Crowley, have been reduced to near-destitution while the Tophams enjoy luxury.

> "Although the Horner girls were poor they had tried hard to make their farm pay." (004-030)

## Role in the Mystery

The Horner farm is the emotional heart of the novel's moral argument. Its bare rooms and struggling farm stand as the visible evidence of injustice — the physical counterpoint to the Topham bungalow's luxury. The farm's transformation from poverty to prosperity at the novel's end ("the large number of chickens in evidence") is the tangible proof that justice has been done. (025-013)
