# Clue

A piece of evidence or information that guides an investigation toward its solution — Nancy Drew begins the novel with "not a single clue" and methodically gathers them until the mystery of the missing will is solved. (002-086)

## Definition

A clue in detective work is any observation, document, testimony, or physical object that helps an investigator narrow possibilities and build a coherent theory of events. The novel traces Nancy's journey from cluelessness to certainty. At the outset, her father challenges her: "How do you expect to locate a missing will when you haven't even a clue?" (002-084) Nancy admits she does "not have a single clue" but remains confident: "I may stumble onto a clue one of these days." (002-086) (003-014)

## Role in the Story

The clue-gathering process is the narrative's procedural backbone. Each clue — Abigail Rowen's mention of the clock, the witness signature of Doctor Nesbitt, the notebook found in the clock, the robbery evidence — builds on previous ones. The clues follow a classic detective-story progression: early optimism ("I may stumble onto a clue"), mid-story accumulation (the clock, the notebook, the robbery evidence), and final synthesis (Nancy finds the will). Nancy's method is to pursue "something which may serve as a clue," following leads before they fully make sense. (005-025)
