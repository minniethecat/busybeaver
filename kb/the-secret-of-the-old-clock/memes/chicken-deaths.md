# Chicken Deaths

A series of suspicious chicken deaths at the Horner farm, orchestrated by the Topham family as part of their campaign to intimidate the Horners into leaving their property. The incident reveals the Tophams' ruthless tactics and strengthens Nancy's resolve to help the Crowley relatives.

## Summary

During Nancy's visit to the Horner farm, she discovers a disturbing scene: several of Allie Horner's chickens lying dead in the barnyard. Allie, distraught, explains that her chickens — many of them pets she raised herself — have been dying one after another. The deaths are not natural; they are part of a pattern of intimidation by the Topham family, who want the Horners off the farm now that the Crowley inheritance seems lost. The chicken deaths represent the Tophams' willingness to use cruel, underhanded methods against anyone standing between them and the Crowley money. (012-014) (012-016)

> "Oh, it's my chickens, Nancy. They're all dying. I'm afraid I'll lose all of my chickens before I get through," Allie said, her voice trembling. (012-016)

## Plot Significance

The chicken deaths serve as concrete evidence of the Topham family's cruelty and their campaign of intimidation against Josiah Crowley's intended beneficiaries. While not directly related to the mystery of the missing will, the incident establishes the stakes: without finding the later will, the Tophams will inherit everything, and families like the Horners — whom Crowley genuinely cared about — will be driven from their homes. This motivates Nancy to intensify her search, knowing that the Tophams are actively harming innocent people even before the estate is settled. (012-014) (012-016) (012-018)

> "She's worked so hard with her chickens. Most of them are pets." Grace's voice was tight with anger. (012-019)

By the novel's end, with the true will discovered and the Tophams' claim defeated, the chicken farm is thriving again — a symbol of the justice restored by Nancy's detective work. (025-013) (025-014) (025-015)
