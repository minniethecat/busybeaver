# An Interesting Story
“The [[storm-on-river-road|storm]] is getting worse every minute,” [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]’s companion observed.

[004-001] Nancy followed her to the barn door and looked out. The rain was now coming down in torrents, and as the girls stood in the doorway a sudden gust of cold wind blew a sheet of water into their faces.

[004-002] “Oh, let’s get back where it’s dry,” Nancy laughed. “This is terrible!”

[004-003] “It’s getting cold, too,” her companion announced, with chattering teeth. “Why not come into the house with me? It’s far more comfortable there. And unless I’m mistaken, this storm [[will-legal-document|will]] continue for an hour or so.”

[004-004] “I don’t like to trouble you—”

[004-005] “Oh, it’s no trouble at all. [[grace-horner|Grace]] will never forgive me if I don’t bring you inside.” She turned to Nancy with an apologetic smile. “I guess I forgot to introduce myself. My name is [[allie-horner|Allie]]—Allie Horner.”

[004-006] “And I am Nancy Drew.”

[004-007] “Not the daughter of [[carson-drew|Carson Drew]], the noted lawyer?”

[004-008] “Yes,” Nancy admitted, in surprise. “Do you know my father?”

[004-009] “Oh, no. But I guess everyone has heard of him.” Allie Horner began to take off the raincoat which she wore. “Here, take this. And I’ll find you a pair of rubber boots.”

[004-010] “I don’t want to take your raincoat,” Nancy protested. “What will you wear?”

[004-011] “Oh, I have an old coat hanging here on a nail. I’ll use that. Anyway, the rain won’t hurt my dress.”

[004-012] Under protest, Nancy donned the raincoat and put on a pair of rubber boots which were several sizes too large for her. She made such a grotesque picture that the two girls could not help but laugh. Then they closed the barn doors.

[004-013] “Now!” Allie directed. “Let’s make a dash for it!”

[004-014] Hand in hand they splashed through the mud toward the farmhouse. As they ran, a vivid flash of lightning momentarily illuminated the sky and caused the girls to cringe. The rain pelted down steadily, and the atmosphere was much colder than it had been ten minutes before.

[004-015] “If it gets any colder we’ll have hail,” Allie chattered, as the two girls reached the shelter of the porch.

[004-016] Dropping their muddy boots outside, Allie flung open the door, and they entered a warm kitchen.

[004-017] As the door closed behind them, an older girl who was bending over the kitchen range turned toward them in surprise.

[004-018] “Grace, I’ve brought you a visitor,” Allie said quickly. “Miss Drew, I want you to meet my sister. She’s the mainstay of our little family.”

[004-019] Grace Horner cordially acknowledged the introduction and greeted Nancy with a warm smile. She was tall and slender, as was her sister, with dark hair and thoughtful brown eyes. Nancy judged that she was at least four years older than Allie. Her face was rather serious, and it was evident that responsibility had fallen upon her shoulders at an early age.

[004-020] Nancy was at once attracted to both [[horner-sisters|Grace and Allie]], and responded to their cordial welcome.

[004-021] “It’s nice of you to take me in this way,” she told them.

[004-022] “Mercy! It’s a pleasure for us,” Grace assured her. “We don’t see a girl of our own age once in a month unless we go to Masonville, and our visits there aren’t very frequent, Miss Drew.”

[004-023] “Oh, please call me by my first name,” Nancy begged. “Everyone does.”

[004-024] The three girls were soon laughing and talking together as though they had always been friends. Nancy knew that she was going to like the Horner girls, and it was evident they were delighted with her.

[004-025] Presently, Grace deftly removed a cake from the oven and set it on the table to cool.

[004-026] “Let’s go into the living room,” she suggested. “My cake is done, so I don’t need to watch it. I’ll give you a piece before you go, Nancy.”

[004-027] “And Grace’s cakes are the real thing,” Allie declared. “I’m not much of a cook myself. I’d rather be outdoors.”

[004-028] “We don’t wish you any bad luck, Nancy,” Grace laughed as she led Nancy into the next room. “But Allie and I don’t care how long it storms!”

[004-029] “Neither do I, so long as I get home before dark,” Nancy replied.

[004-030] Although the living room was warm and comfortable, it was nearly bare of furniture. The floors had been painted and were scantily covered with handmade rag rugs. With the exception of an old fashioned divan, an inexpensive table, a few straight-back chairs, and an old wood stove which furnished heat, there was little in the room. However, dainty white curtains covered the windows, and Nancy realized that although the Horner girls were poor they had tried hard to make their home attractive.

[004-031] “Surely, you two girls don’t live here alone, do you?” Nancy inquired.

[004-032] Allie nodded.

[004-033] “Grace and I have been living here ever since father died. That was two years ago. Mother passed away just before that,” the girl added with a slight catch in her voice.

[004-034] “But how do you manage? It must be dreadfully hard for two girls to run a farm by themselves,” said Nancy, in wonder.

[004-035] “Our farm isn’t very large now,” Grace said quietly. “We have only a few acres.”

[004-036] “I know you wonder how we live, but are too polite to ask,” Allie broke in. “Grace helps a dressmaker at Masonville whenever she can get work. She makes all our clothes, too. And I raise chickens.”

[004-037] “Chickens?” Nancy asked, in surprise. “Can you make much at that?”

[004-038] “Well, it all depends. This year the market hasn’t been so high as usual, and the price of eggs has dropped, too. But I enjoy the work, and I could make it pay if only I could afford to start a flock of white leghorns.”

[004-039] “Allie is a regular outdoor girl,” Grace explained, with a smile. “We divide our work. I attend to the house, but she would rather be outside.”

[004-040] “We get along very nicely in the summer,” Allie went on. “We raise vegetables and have enough for our own use. But in the winter it’s hard. I don’t know how we’ll manage this year.”

[004-041] “We’ll find a way, Allie,” Grace told her, with a brave smile. “We always have.”

[004-042] She arose from her chair and turned to Nancy.

[004-043] “I’m sure you’re not interested in our misfortunes. It’s true that we’re poor, but we can still offer a guest a cup of tea. Excuse me while I make it.”

[004-044] It was on the tip of Nancy’s tongue to protest, but she checked the words just in time. She knew that Allie and Grace were proud and that to refuse their hospitality would hurt their feelings.

[004-045] “I wish I could help them,” Nancy thought. “But if I offer to pay for the tea they will be offended. Perhaps later on I can engage Grace to make a dress for me.”

[004-046] In a few minutes Grace returned to the living room bearing a tray which had been covered with a clean white napkin. She poured the tea and served the cake with as much poise as though she were gracing an elegant drawing room.

[004-047] “I never tasted more delicious cake in all my life,” Nancy said warmly.

[004-048] The three girls chattered over the tea cups and watched the rain beat against the windows. Presently, as Nancy noticed an unusual picture on the wall, she commented on its beauty.

[004-049] “Uncle Josiah gave it to us,” Allie told her. “If he were only alive now, things would be different.”

[004-050] At the mention of the name, Nancy started. Could it be that Allie referred to [[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]? It was very unlikely, she decided, yet only the week before her father had told her that there were two girls living on the [[river-road|River Road]] who should have figured in [[crowley-will-document|the will]]. The relationship would be worth investigating, Nancy told herself.

[004-051] “Then your uncle is dead?” she inquired sympathetically.

[004-052] “Josiah Crowley wasn’t really our uncle,” Grace replied. “But we loved him as much as though he were a relative.” Her voice broke and for a moment she could not go on. “He lived next door to us—that was when mother and father were alive. All of our misfortunes seemed to come at once.”

[004-053] “He was the dearest man you ever saw,” Allie added, taking up the story. “Some people thought him queer, but you never minded his peculiar ways after you knew him. He was so good and kind to us. We were neighbors for years—until [[topham-family|the Tophams]] induced him to live with them. After that, everything was changed.”

[004-054] “But he never liked it with the Tophams,” Grace declared. “They weren’t kind to him, and he used to sneak off to visit us. Didn’t he, Allie?”

[004-055] “Yes, he often said that we seemed like his own children. He brought us so many nice gifts. We loved him for himself and not his [[fortune|money]], but after our parents died he hinted that he intended to see to it that we never came to want. I remember the very last day we ever saw him alive, he told us that he intended to mention us in the will. ‘I have planned a big surprise for you girls. You’ll see it in my will.’ Those were his very words.”

[004-056] “And then the Tophams got everything,” Grace said somewhat bitterly. “It isn’t that we were expecting any of the money, but it does seem [[fairness|unfair]] that the entire fortune should go to them. They never cared a snap of their fingers for Uncle Josiah, and I don’t believe he intended to leave his money to them.”

[004-057] “Perhaps you were mentioned in the will they say can’t be found,” Nancy suggested.

[004-058] Grace and Allie exchanged significant glances and gravely nodded.

[004-059] “That’s just what we think,” Allie announced.

[004-060] “Can’t something be done about it? It seems only fair that you should have your inheritance.”

[004-061] “I’m afraid there’s nothing we can do,” Grace said doubtfully. “You see, although we are certain a will was made in our favor, we have no actual [[evidence|proof]]. We haven’t enough money to fight the case.”

[004-062] “The Tophams ought to do something for you girls.”

[004-063] “The Tophams?” Allie laughed scornfully. “They wouldn’t give us a cent.”

[004-064] For some time Allie and Grace Horner talked of Josiah Crowley and his queer ways. To Nancy it was evident that the girls had loved the old man a great deal.

[004-065] Presently, as the rain ceased and the sun threatened to break through the clouds, Nancy arose to leave.

[004-066] “Your story has interested me a great deal,” she assured the Horner girls. “It’s possible my father can do something to help you—he is a lawyer you know.”

[004-067] “Oh, we weren’t asking for help!” Grace interposed quickly. “I don’t know why we happened to tell you so much.”

[004-068] “I’m glad that you did, and if I can I want to help you. If my father asks you to come to his office in [[river-heights|River Heights]] will you do it?”

[004-069] “Why, yes, I suppose so,” Grace admitted slowly. “But we have told you about everything we know concerning the will.”

[004-070] “My father is a wonder at unearthing [[hidden|hidden facts]],” Nancy declared.

[004-071] “It’s kind of you to tell your father about our case,” Allie broke in. “We’d be so grateful if something could be done. We don’t want a cent that doesn’t belong to us, but it does seem as though we should have received a small inheritance.”

[004-072] “Don’t build up your hopes until after I’ve talked with my father,” Nancy advised them as she turned toward the door. “If anything can be done I promise I’ll let you know.”
