# Psychological Association

The principle of psychological association provides the critical breakthrough in **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]**'s investigation of the **[[the-lost-will|Crowley will mystery]]**. When **[[abigail-rowen|Abigail Rowen]]**'s **[[mantel-clock-abigail|mantel clock]]** chimes three, the sound triggers her long-buried memory of **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]**'s **[[crowley-notebook|notebook]]**.

## The Moment of Recognition

Abigail had been struggling to recall what Crowley had told her about the will. She knew he "did say something about what he was going to do with that will, but I can't just recollect." The effort to remember exhausted her.

Then the clock chimed: "Abigail Rowen's eyes fluttered open and an odd expression passed over her face." She stared at the clock and whispered: "The clock. That was it!" After further concentration, she exclaimed: "It was something about a notebook!"

## Nancy's Understanding

**[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** immediately grasps the significance. She "had studied psychology in school and was familiar with the power of suggestion and association." Her "keen mind jumped to the conclusion that a clock had something to do with the missing Crowley will."

Nancy realizes that the chiming clock triggered Abigail's memory because Crowley's clock — the **[[crowley-clock|family heirloom clock]]** — was associated with the hidden notebook. This insight leads her directly to search the Topham bungalow on **[[moon-lake|Moon Lake]]**.

## Significance

The psychological association serves as the dramatic pivot of the mystery. Before this moment, Nancy had only vague theories. After it, she has a concrete lead: find the Crowley clock, find the notebook. The novel thus demonstrates how understanding human psychology can be as important as physical evidence in **[[detective-work|detective work]]**.
