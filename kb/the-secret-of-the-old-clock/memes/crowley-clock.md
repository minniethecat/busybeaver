# Crowley Clock

The humble family timepiece in which [[josiah-crowley]] concealed his second will — an ordinary object overlooked by the greedy [[topham-family]], whose hidden contents ultimately restore justice to the [[horner-sisters]] and become [[nancy-drew]]'s most treasured possession. (010-001) (025-039)

## Physical Description

The Crowley clock is a family timepiece — deceptively ordinary in appearance, which is precisely why Crowley chose it as a hiding place. It is not the mantel clock that [[abigail-rowen]] initially suspects; when she studies the mantel clock, she realizes "this isn't the right one." (010-012) The actual Crowley clock is a different timepiece, one that could be moved and transported without attracting attention. When [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] first sees it, she notes: "it had a square face and the top was ornamented with a crescent." (019-029)

Its unremarkable appearance is central to its narrative function. The Tophams, who seized everything of obvious value from Crowley's estate, overlooked it entirely. The clock embodies the novel's recurring theme: what is truly valuable is hidden from those who lack the perception — or the moral character — to find it.

## The Clock as Hiding Place

Crowley's choice of the clock as a hiding place for his second will was both clever and revealing of his character. A man described as "rather queer in many ways," Crowley understood that the best concealment is in plain sight. (001-022) The clock contained not only the will but also a notebook bearing the name "Josiah Harkston" — the alias Crowley used for his safe deposit box at the [[masonville-national-bank|Masonville National Bank]]. (022-023)

Abigail Rowen's cryptic testimony provides the crucial lead. In Chapter 9, she recalls that Crowley mentioned something about "the clock" in connection with his will. (009-063) In Chapter 10, she whispers the single word that sets the entire investigation in motion:

> "The clock," Abigail whispered so softly that Nancy could scarcely hear. (010-001)

> It was on the tip of Nancy's tongue to tell Abigail that Josiah Crowley might have hidden his will inside the family clock, but she hesitated to excite the old lady. After all, it was only a theory. (010-025)

## Nancy's Relationship with the Clock

Nancy's attitude toward the clock evolves through the novel. Initially, she views it as a clue — something to be searched and investigated. When she first suspects it may be the right clock, she thinks: "I wonder if that can be the Crowley clock?" (011-010) During the robbery investigation, she is "anxious" about its fate, feeling "certain that the Crowley clock had been stolen." (015-002)

> "They must have taken the Crowley clock too," Nancy told herself with a sinking heart. "If they have, I'll never set eyes on it!" (014-037)

After the mystery is solved, her relationship with the clock transforms. When asked what reward she wants, she replies: "Just the old Crowley clock — that's all I care for." (025-030) The clock becomes her trophy — a memento of her first successful case and a symbol of her identity as a detective:

> "I'll always prize this clock as a trophy of my first venture as a detective," she said quietly, turning to Allie and Grace. "It will serve as a pleasant reminder of a thrilling adventure — and, who knows? perhaps as a promise for the future!" (025-039)

> She removed the Crowley clock from the back of the roadster and paused an instant to gloat over it. She was well satisfied with the result of her detective work. (022-011)

## Symbolic Significance

The Crowley clock is the novel's central MacGuffin and its most potent symbol. As a MacGuffin, it drives the plot — everyone is searching for something, and what they are really searching for is inside the clock, though most of them do not know it. As a symbol, the clock represents several interconnected themes:

- **Hidden justice**: The will, like justice itself, is concealed but not destroyed — available to those who look carefully enough
- **The limits of greed**: The Tophams seized everything of obvious value but missed the one object that would undo them
- **Time and truth**: A clock — literally a device for measuring time — contains the document that will finally bring the past (Crowley's true intentions) into the present

> "I wonder if that can be the Crowley clock?" Nancy thought. (011-010)
