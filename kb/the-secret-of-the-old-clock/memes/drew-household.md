# Drew Household

The household of Carson and Nancy Drew — a small but stable family unit whose mutual respect, professional ethics, and domestic warmth provide the moral foundation for the entire novel. (001-001) (002-011)

## Members

- [[carson-drew]] — Father, noted criminal lawyer, head of household (001-003)
- [[nancy-drew]] — Daughter, amateur detective, protagonist (001-001)
- [[hannah-gruen]] — Elderly maid of all work, loyal long-term employee (002-011)

## Description

The Drew household is defined by mutual respect and shared values. Carson Drew treats his sixteen-year-old daughter as an intellectual equal, discussing complex legal cases with her and taking "secret boast that he had taught her everything she knew." (001-034) Nancy, in turn, respects her father's legal expertise and seeks his counsel. The household operates smoothly thanks to Hannah Gruen, "an elderly maid of all work who had been with the family since before the death of Mrs. Drew." (002-011) The absence of Mrs. Drew — mentioned only in passing — adds poignancy to the household portrait: this is a family that has weathered loss and emerged stronger.

## Role in the Story

The Drew household is the novel's moral center. Unlike the Tophams, whose family is defined by greed and mutual exploitation, the Drews represent what a family should be: loving, honest, and generous. Carson Drew's professional ethics provide the legal framework for Nancy's investigation, while the household's domestic stability — meals prepared by Hannah, evenings in the library — gives Nancy the secure base from which to venture into the uncertainties of detective work.

> "It was his secret boast that he had taught her everything she knew." — On Carson Drew's pride in Nancy. (001-034)
