# Topham Bungalow

The Topham family's vacation home at Moon Lake — a place of leisure purchased with disputed fortune money, and the scene of a robbery that tests Nancy's detective skills. (011-050) (012-077)

## Location

The bungalow is located at Moon Lake, "set back some distance from the water" among the hills. (012-077) It is visible from a launch on the lake:

> After a time, as the launch chugged along close to the shore, Helen waved her hand toward a bungalow which was set back some distance from the water. (012-077)

Its isolated position makes it vulnerable to theft — though a caretaker is employed year-round as a protective measure.

## Description

The bungalow is a vacation property where the Tophams store possessions sent from their River Heights home. When Mrs. Topham explains what became of Josiah Crowley's old clock:

> "We considered the clock entirely out of place in our modern home," Mrs. Topham continued; "so we sent it up to our bungalow on Moon Lake." (011-050)

The property is maintained by a caretaker:

> "Oh, no, the cottage is closed. There's a negro caretaker who looks after it—they call him Jeff Tucker." (012-073)

Nancy observes it from a distance during her Moon Lake visit and notes its isolation. After the robbery, Nancy discovers that valuable items — furniture and silverware — were stored there, making it a target for thieves. The bungalow serves as a physical symbol of the Tophams' appropriation of wealth that rightfully belongs to others.

## Role in the Mystery

The Topham bungalow is the crime scene for the novel's subplot — the robbery that complicates Nancy's investigation but ultimately provides evidence against the thieves. When Nancy learns of the robbery:

> "It looks like a case of ordinary robbery to me," she reasoned. (014-033)

Yet the bungalow functions in a broader context of crime in the Moon Lake area:

> Nancy had frequently read of cases where the summer homes of wealthy persons had been stripped of furniture and rugs by unscrupulous robbers who sold the valuables to secondhand dealers and fences. In recent years there had been a number of thefts in the vicinity of Moon Lake, and as a protective measure many of the cottage owners employed caretakers the year around. (014-034)

Nancy takes quick action upon discovering the break-in:

> "I'll drive to the nearest town and report the robbery to the police," she decided. "Those men won't be able to travel very fast with a heavy moving van. There's a chance that they may be caught yet." (015-003)

The bungalow also serves as a physical manifestation of the Tophams' ill-gotten wealth: this vacation property was purchased with fortune money that rightfully belongs to the Horner sisters and Abigail Rowen, reinforcing the novel's moral argument about justice and inheritance.
