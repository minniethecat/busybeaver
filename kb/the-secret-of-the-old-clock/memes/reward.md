# Reward

The concept of **reward** runs through *The Secret of the Old Clock* as both a plot mechanism and a moral theme. Characters repeatedly offer, refuse, and debate rewards, revealing their values in the process.

## The Topham Silverware Reward

When the Topham bungalow is robbed, William Mathews offers "a substantial reward to anyone who can produce" the missing silverware and household goods (008-068). This mercenary framing contrasts with Nancy's own motivations — she pursues the robbers not for money but for justice.

> "Not many girls would have used their brains the way you did," the marshal observed. "Unless I'm mistaken, these robbers would have gotten away if it hadn't been for you." (021-047)
## Nancy's Refusal

After capturing the robbers and recovering the stolen goods, the marshal suggests Nancy deserves a "liberal reward" from Mrs. Topham (021-047). Nancy refuses: "She'll not offer a reward, and even if she did I wouldn't accept it. In fact, I must ask you not to mention my part in this" (021-047). Her insistence on anonymity underscores her altruism.

## The Horner Sisters' Gratitude

The theme culminates in Chapter 25, "A Reward," where the Horner sisters, now financially secure, insist on rewarding Nancy. They offer money, but Nancy demurs: "Oh, I don't want a reward. I wanted to help" "(025)". Ultimately she accepts a small, sentimental token — the old mantel clock that started the investigation — as a memento rather than payment.
