# Kindness

**Kindness** is the moral touchstone of *The Secret of the Old Clock*. The novel repeatedly asks who treated Josiah Crowley kindly — and the answer determines who deserves his fortune.

## Kindness to Josiah Crowley

The novel's premise rests on a simple moral calculus. Crowley "had been forced to live with kindly relatives who were scarcely able to have him with them" (001-019), while the Tophams, after securing his will, "treated him unkindly" (001-019). Henry Rolsted confirms that "the Tophams treated Crowley most unkindly after he had made his will in their favor" (002-071), motivating Crowley to draft a second, fairer document.

> "Then I will say this much—it would not surprise me if another will should come to light. The Tophams treated Crowley most unkindly after he had made his will in their favor." — Henry Rolsted (002-071)
## Nancy's Kindness

Nancy Drew's investigation is fueled by kindness rather than self-interest. She befriends the Horner sisters, brings them to meet her father, and devotes herself to uncovering the second will — not for reward, but because she believes they deserve better. When Grace refuses charity with "Thank you for your kindness, Nancy; but we can't take your money" (012-024), Nancy pivots to action rather than pity.

## Kindness as Social Marker

The novel draws a clear line between the kind and the unkind, mapping it onto wealth in surprising ways. The poor relatives — the Horners, Turners, Mathews, and Abigail Rowen — consistently show generosity and warmth, while the affluent Tophams display "snobbish" cruelty. This inversion challenges the assumption that wealth signals virtue.
