# Stinginess

**[[cora-topham|Mrs. Cora Topham]]**'s stinginess is a defining trait that reveals the hypocrisy of the **[[topham-family|Topham family]]**. Despite their wealth and social pretensions, they are notoriously tight-fisted.

## Evidence of Stinginess

When **[[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]]** visits the **[[topham-residence|Topham residence]]** selling charity dance tickets at two dollars each, Mrs. Topham protests: "Why, that's positively exorbitant!" It is "common knowledge that Mrs. Topham was decidedly stingy where other persons were concerned."

**[[richard-topham|Richard Topham]]**, by contrast, makes a show of generosity by buying all four tickets with a twenty-dollar bill and grandly declaring, "Keep the change. Give it to the charity." However, he does this not from generosity but from calculation — "This donation will get our names into the paper" — and overrules his wife's objection.

## The Paradox

Mrs. Topham's stinginess paradoxically coexists with her social ambitions. She desperately wants social acceptance, yet resents spending money even for charitable causes that would enhance her standing. Her husband's strategic generosity brings the family publicity, but their underlying meanness toward **[[josiah-crowley|Josiah Crowley]]** and his relatives ultimately costs them everything.

The Tophams' miserly treatment of Crowley — taking him in only for his money, then treating him unkindly — leads directly to their disinheritance in the second will. Their stinginess toward others is repaid with the loss of the entire **[[fortune|fortune]]**.
