# Pride

**Pride** appears in *The Secret of the Old Clock* in two sharply contrasting forms: the quiet, dignified pride of those who have little but refuse charity, and the arrogant, class-based pride of the wealthy Topham family.

## Dignified Pride

The Horner sisters embody pride as self-respect. Though "poor as church mice," they are "proud, too" (005-022), refusing to accept money or pity. When Nancy considers giving them financial help, she realizes they "were too proud to accept money" (008-037), and instead finds indirect ways to support them — buying dresses from Grace's dressmaking, for example. Their pride is a moral quality, rooted in hard work and independence.

Grace displays this dignity when she "proudly displayed a tin pail which was filled nearly to the brim with raspberries" (008-037) — taking honest satisfaction in the fruits of her labor. Even in poverty, "they had tried hard to make their home attractive" (004-032).

> "Allie and Grace Horner are as poor as church mice, but they're proud, too," Nancy ended. "If only we could do something to help them!" (005-022)
## The Horner Sisters' Refusal

When Nancy attempts to offer direct help, the sisters refuse with dignity. "Nancy saw that Grace and Allie were too proud to accept help, and regretfully abandoned the idea of trying to give them money" "(012)". Nancy recognizes that "in spite of their pride, Allie and Grace would soon be forced to accept charity" "(012)" — a tension that fuels her determination to find the second will.

## Carson Drew's Pride in Nancy

The novel also depicts parental pride. When Nancy explains how she solved the mystery, "Carson Drew stared at her with mingled pride and amusement" (022-033), then tells her: "I'm proud of what you've done, Nancy. I couldn't have done better myself" "(025)". This earned pride — rooted in accomplishment — contrasts with the Tophams' unearned arrogance.

## Topham Vanity

The Topham family's pride is the negative counterpart. Isabel is "the pride of the Topham family" (002-032) in a way that signals vanity rather than virtue. Ada's behavior at the department store — demanding special treatment, refusing to pay for a broken vase — demonstrates pride as entitlement rather than dignity.
