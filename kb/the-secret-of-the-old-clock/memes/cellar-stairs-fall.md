# Cellar Stairs Fall

Abigail Rowen falls down the cellar stairs of her rundown cottage on West Lake Road, injuring her hip and spraining her ankle. Nancy Drew finds her bedridden and alone during a crucial visit.

## Summary

When Nancy Drew first visits Abigail Rowen at her cottage, she finds the elderly woman lying in bed, injured and unable to walk. Abigail explains that she fell down the cellar stairs the day before, hurting her hip and spraining her ankle (009-015). No one has come to help her — no doctor, no neighbor (009-017). Nancy gently arranges pillows for the old woman and checks her injuries, confirming the hip is not broken but noting the ankle is not even bandaged (009-018, 009-020).

The accident underscores Abigail's profound isolation. Living alone in a dilapidated cottage on West Lake Road, she has no one to call for help, no family nearby, and no means to summon a doctor. Her situation stands in stark contrast to the wealthy [[topham-family|Topham sisters]], who inherited everything while Abigail — whom [[josiah-crowley]] had intended to provide for — was left with nothing.

## Background

Abigail Rowen is a seamstress who had been close to Josiah Crowley in his later years. Despite Crowley's promises to leave her money in his will, the first will that was probated left everything to the Topham family, and Abigail received nothing. Her poverty and the poor condition of her cottage reflect the injustice that Nancy eventually works to correct.

## Investigation Context

This event forms part of the larger pattern of criminal activity that Nancy Drew systematically uncovers. Each incident reveals another layer of the conspiracy surrounding the Crowley estate, building toward the eventual resolution of the case.

## Plot Significance

Abigail's fall creates the circumstances under which Nancy first meets her — bedridden, vulnerable, and grateful for assistance. This encounter builds trust between them, leading Abigail to share crucial information about [[josiah-crowley]] and the clock. The fall also highlights Abigail's isolation and poverty, reinforcing the contrast with the wealthy Topham sisters who sought to deny her inheritance.

Nancy's compassionate response — gently checking Abigail's injuries, arranging her pillows, and listening to her story — exemplifies the detective's combination of kindness and investigative instinct. Her willingness to help someone in need opens the door to information that proves essential to solving the case.
