# Finding the Missing Will
## The Investigation Path

The search for the missing will is the spine of the novel. Nancy's investigation follows a logical chain of evidence that begins with community rumor and ends with physical discovery:

1. **The newspaper report** — Nancy learns of the Crowley fortune and the Topham inheritance (001-001)
2. **Legal groundwork** — [[carson-drew]] explains the legal significance of finding a later will (001-030)
3. **Witness interviews** — The Horner sisters, Henry Rolsted, and other witnesses confirm that Crowley intended to write a new will (001-041) (004-060)
4. **Abigail's clue** — The elderly confidante reveals that Crowley mentioned "the clock" in connection with the will (009-063) (010-001)
5. **The alias discovery** — Nancy traces the name "Josiah Harkston" to a safe deposit box at the Masonville National Bank (022-023)
6. **Physical recovery** — Nancy recovers the Crowley clock and the notebook it contained from the robbers' moving van, then follows the notebook's clue to a safe deposit box (022-023) where the will is discovered (023-060)
7. **Verification** — Doctor Nesbitt's signature as witness confirms the will's authenticity (023-060)

## The Discovery

The actual moment of discovery turns on a detail that only Abigail Rowen could provide. She recalls that Crowley mentioned hiding the will in "the clock" — but crucially, not the mantel clock that she initially examined. (010-012) The distinction between the two clocks is one of the novel's most important clues: the obvious clock is the wrong one; the real hiding place is the clock that no one thinks to check.

> "The clock. That was it! The clock!" — Abigail Rowen, revealing the crucial clue. (010-001)

When Nancy finally recovers the correct clock from the robbers' moving van (021-006 through 022-009), she does not find the will inside it. Instead, she finds a notebook — Crowley's carefully kept record — containing a vital clue written in his own handwriting. The notebook reveals that the will is not hidden in the clock at all, but in a safe deposit box at the Masonville National Bank, registered under Crowley's alias.

> "To whom it may concern: My last will and testament will be found in safety deposit box number 148 in the Masonville National Bank. The box is under the name of Josiah Harkston." — From Josiah Crowley's notebook. (022-023)

This notebook is the true key to the mystery. Nancy and Carson Drew go to the Masonville National Bank, present the information from the notebook, and gain access to safe deposit box #148. Inside, they find the document they have been searching for — the second will, signed and witnessed, exactly as Crowley intended.

> "Oh, it must be the will!" Nancy cried before Mr. Drew could examine the paper. (023-050)

> "I see Doctor Nesbitt signed as a witness. No wonder the will never came to light. If you remember, he died a few days after Crowley." — Carson Drew, understanding why the will was hidden for so long. (023-060)


## The Notebook

The notebook proved to be the linchpin of the entire investigation. Without it, Nancy might have searched the clock endlessly and found nothing — because the will was never inside the clock. The notebook demonstrates that Josiah Crowley, despite his eccentricity, was methodical and deliberate in protecting his final intentions. He did not simply hide the will; he created a two-step process: the clock held the notebook, and the notebook held the clue that led to the will.

This layered concealment explains why the will remained undiscovered for so long. Even if someone had stumbled upon the clock and searched it, they would have found only a notebook — not the will itself. The alias "Josiah Harkston" added a second layer of protection: only someone who knew both about the clock and about Crowley's alias could connect the two clues and reach the safe deposit box.


## The Aftermath

The discovery of the will produces immediate and far-reaching consequences:

- **The Horner sisters** receive the bulk of the estate, freeing them from poverty (024-056) (024-088)
- **Abigail Rowen** receives her promised portion of the inheritance (025-007)
- **The Tophams** lose everything — the fortune, their legal claim, and their social standing
- **Charitable bequests** are honored, fulfilling Crowley's expressed wishes
- **Nancy Drew** receives the Crowley clock as her trophy, marking her transition from amateur to detective (025-028) (025-030) (025-039)

The bank acts quickly once the will is authenticated: Carson Drew advises that "it would be possible to draw on your inheritances at once." (024-056) The legal machinery, which initially protected the Tophams, now works efficiently to restore justice — affirming the novel's faith in law as an instrument of fairness when properly informed.

> "Just the old Crowley clock — that's all I care for. And if you particularly want it yourself —" — Nancy Drew, refusing financial reward and asking only for the clock as her trophy. (025-030)

> "I'll always prize this clock as a trophy of my first venture as a detective." — Nancy Drew. (025-039)


## Narrative Significance

This event is the novel's climax, resolution, and moral vindication combined. Every plot thread — the will search, the robbery subplot, the character testimonies — converges on this moment. The discovery validates Nancy's method (patient investigation, careful listening, logical deduction), rewards the deserving, punishes the greedy, and establishes the template for the dozens of Nancy Drew mysteries that would follow.

> "For a reason which she could not understand, the Crowley case had attracted Nancy's attention... She had a certain feeling that a mystery lurked behind it." (001-037)

The event also completes Nancy's character arc. She begins the novel as Carson Drew's daughter with a "certain feeling that a mystery lurked" behind the Crowley case (001-037), and she takes on the investigation out of a sense of fairness:

> "You weren't paying a bit of attention. I was saying I think it's mean if those snobbish Tophams fall heir to all of Josiah Crowley's fortune. Can't something be done about it?" — Nancy Drew. (001-005)

She ends it as a detective in her own right, with a trophy to prove it. The clock is not merely a reward — it is a symbol of her achieved identity and the beginning of her career as a detective.
