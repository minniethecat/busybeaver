# Flock

The flock in *Shepherds of the Wild* is far more than livestock — it is the moral center around which the entire narrative revolves. Three thousand ewes and lambs, "crowded so close together that they occupied, in all, a space hardly more than a hundred yards square" (004-012), become the vehicle for Hugh Gaylord's transformation from a shallow city-dweller into a man who understands duty and sacrifice.

## Definition

In the novel's world, the flock represents both vulnerability and purpose. Sheep are uniquely defenseless among domesticated animals: "sheep, above all other domestic animals, are dependent upon men for their very lives" (004-010). Unlike horses that can fight off predators or cattle that can defend themselves with horns, the flock has no natural defenses. This utter dependence is not a weakness but the very thing that gives the shepherd's life its meaning — the flock's need summons the highest virtues from those who guard it.

## Manifestations

The flock appears throughout the novel as a vast white presence on the landscape. When Hugh first sees the sheep bedding down, he mistakes them for "deep snow" covering the meadow (004-011). The flock's physical reality — the bleating of lambs, the white drift of bodies, the black sheep that serve as markers — becomes the backdrop against which all the novel's drama unfolds. The flock is tended by the herder and his dogs, threatened by predators and cattlemen alike, and ultimately becomes the test of character for everyone who encounters it.

## Key Characters/Events

Hugh Gaylord's entire arc is defined by his relationship to the flock. When he first arrives at the sheep camp, he is merely a spectator. But after the herder Dan is murdered and Hugh finds himself as the flock's sole protector, he undergoes a profound reckoning. "It did not lay with the shepherd to consider abstract issues of life and death. It was not for him to try to balance the value of a lamb's life with his own. His business was to watch the flock" (008-017). Later, Alice Crowson inherits responsibility for her father's flock, and her determination to protect the sheep — even through the catastrophic forest fire — mirrors Hugh's own transformation.

## Philosophical Significance

The flock serves as the novel's central symbol for the ancient, unchanging verities of human existence. Gazing at the sheep bedding down, Hugh has a vision of continuity: "It had been the same for uncounted centuries — since the first dim days when the nomads drove their flocks over the plains of Asia" (004-023). The flock connects Hugh to a lineage of shepherds stretching back to biblical times. "Watching the flocks — the phrase was as old as the mountains themselves" (005-042). Against the transience of cities, fashions, and civilizations, the flock endures as a symbol of what is permanent and real.

## Key Passages

> "All the basic elements of life were here: the flocks, the herder's little shelter, the fire glowing in the falling darkness, the watchful shepherd dog guarding the lambs, the beasts of prey lurking in the growing shadows." (004-022)

> "Cities are born, grow great, are cursed with wickedness, and perish. The flocks still wander on the hills. Men catch new fancies, follow new teachings, build new orders, and pursue new ways. The firelight of the herder still glows in the twilight." (004-023)

> "His business was to watch the flock. His work was to guard the sheep. Nothing could come between." (008-017)
