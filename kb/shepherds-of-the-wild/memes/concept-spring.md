# Spring

Spring in *Shepherds of the Wild* represents the promise of renewal that underpins the wilderness's eternal cycle. Though the novel's action takes place primarily in summer and fall, the idea of spring — as a time of birth, returning life, and the greening of the land — haunts its pages as both memory and hope.

## Definition

In the novel's seasonal imagination, spring is the counterweight to the drought and the fire. It is the season when the mountain springs flow full, when the deer drop their fawns, and when the high peaks emerge from their winter snows. The novel's awareness of spring as part of an annual cycle is essential to its understanding of wilderness time — a time that is cyclical rather than linear, governed by the returning seasons rather than the forward march of human history.

The Western stock business, as the novel notes, depends on this cycle: "The Western stock business depends on having acres and acres of open land for the stock to run in through spring, summer, and fall" (012-012). Spring is the beginning of the productive year, the season that makes the shepherd's life possible.

## Manifestations

Spring manifests most concretely in the novel through the image of mountain springs — the sources of water that sustain all life in Smoky Land. The land is defined by "where the little rivers rose in great springs" (001-008), and the drought's devastation is measured by the drying of these waters: "Most of the springs were dried up, the lesser tributaries of Silver Creek were only successions of stagnant pools in which the silver people were already dying" (018-006).

The novel's opening chapter establishes the seasonal calendar through concrete natural details. The huckleberries ripen "as always, in the seventh month" (001-003), and the pines change color from "deep, rich green to a strange dusky blue" (001-003) — a calendar written in the landscape itself. These markers imply the spring that preceded them and the fall that will follow.

Spring is also the season of birth in the novel's ecology. The bighorn lamb Spot was "born the following spring" (021-009) and was destined for the high peaks before human intervention altered his fate. The cycle of birth, growth, and death that spring initiates is the fundamental rhythm of the wild.

## Key Characters/Events

The drought described in Chapter 18 is the novel's most powerful meditation on what happens when spring fails. "No rain had fallen since April" (018-002) — the spring rains that should have sustained the land through summer never came. The drying of the springs, the suffering of the fish, and the triumph of the buzzards all follow from this single failure of the seasonal cycle. The drought chapter makes visible what is usually invisible: the absolute dependence of the wilderness on the spring renewal that each year brings.

Spot the bighorn ram embodies the spring theme in his very origin. Born in the spring on the high peaks, he carries the memory of his birthplace through his life among the domestic sheep. His final flight toward the crags is, in a sense, a return to the spring of his birth.

## Philosophical Significance

Spring represents the novel's faith in cyclical renewal — the conviction that the wilderness, for all its violence and death, is fundamentally a system of return and rebirth. The drought may dry the springs and the fire may blacken the forest, but the novel implies that spring will come again, the snow will melt, and the green will return. This faith in the cycle is what separates the wilderness perspective from the linear, progressive worldview that Hugh brings from the city.

## Key Passages

> "The Western stock business depends on having acres and acres of open land for the stock to run in through spring, summer, and fall, feeding them just in winter." (012-012)

> "Most of the springs were dried up, the lesser tributaries of Silver Creek were only successions of stagnant pools in which the silver people were already dying and turning white bellies to the sun." (018-006)

> "It was wholly possible that Bill Elkins, a herder who the following spring had gone over to Fargo and his gang, could remember that night also." (021-006)
