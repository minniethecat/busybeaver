# Cattlemen (Smoky Land Ranchers)


## Overview

The cattlemen of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] are less a formal organization than an economic bloc — the established ranching interests who came first to the high ranges and who regard the sheepmen as interlopers to be driven out. They are the invisible antagonist behind [[landy-fargo|Landy Fargo]]'s visible crimes, the economic and social force that makes "range law" possible. [[pete|Pete]] explains the [[struggle|conflict]] to [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] in simple terms: "Big [[struggle|fight]] — over the range. This big cattle country — cattlemen always try to keep out sheep" (005-014).

The cattlemen embody a particular form of [[dominance|power]] — not the raw violence of an outlaw gang, but the settled, systemic [[dominance|power]] of an established class defending its prerogatives. Their antagonist status is more insidious than simple villainy because their claims carry a certain legitimacy: they were first, they built the ranches, and by the customs of the frontier, priority of occupation confers moral standing. The novel's achievement is to show how this standing becomes a cover for exploitation.

## Structure

The cattlemen operate not through a formal association but through shared economic interest and the logic of first possession. The novel presents several layers of their [[dominance|power]]:

| Layer | Mechanism | Effect |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Economic | Control of grazing territory through prior use | Sheepmen cannot access public domain without [[struggle|conflict]] |
| Social | "Range law" — the unwritten [[honor|code]] that might makes right | Violence against sheepmen is tacitly condoned |
| Violent | Hiring of enforcers like [[jose-mertos|José Mertos]] | Individual [[shepherd|herder]]s are isolated and eliminated |

[[alice-crowson|Alice]] explains the cattlemen's legal and economic position to [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]: "As far as public domain is concerned, they've got a certain right to oppose the sheepmen. They were the first here, and cattle won't feed after sheep" (012-016). The phrase "a certain right" is carefully chosen — the cattlemen's claim is not legal in the strict sense (the public domain belongs to everyone) but customary, rooted in the Western [[honor|code]] of prior occupation.

[[alice-crowson|Alice]]'s father attempted to circumvent the [[struggle|conflict]] by leasing private land: "Father thought that by renting it, the cattlemen would leave him alone. They had all the public domain; and in this way, there couldn't be any particle of doubt about his being in the right" (012-017). But the legal right to private land did not deter the cattlemen — "We've been at war ever since" (012-018).

This structural analysis reveals a key irony: the cattlemen's system requires the fiction of "range law" to function, yet the system itself consistently violates any actual law. The cattlemen claim the moral authority of custom while practicing the methods of criminality. [[landy-fargo|Fargo]] is not an aberration but the system's logical endpoint — the man who does openly what the system requires to be done covertly.

## Role in the Novel

The cattlemen function as the systemic antagonist — less personal than [[landy-fargo|Fargo]] but more [[dominance|power]]ful. [[landy-fargo|Fargo]] is an individual criminal; the cattlemen are the conditions that make his criminality possible. [[pete|Pete]] notes that "the war with the cattlemen made it as [[danger|peril]]ous" as any physical danger (006-027), and the [[shepherd|herder]] Dan was murdered "not for personal reasons, but because he was a sheepman" (011-038).

The cattlemen's [[dominance|power]] is most visible in their ability to attract men like [[jose-mertos|José Mertos]] and [[bill-elkins|Bill Elkins]] — ordinary [[shepherd|herder]]s who become instruments of violence. "The cowboys knew him; they knew that if there was ever any dirty work to be done, [[landy-fargo|Landy Fargo]] could always find [[jose-mertos|José Mertos]] to do it" (022-010). The cattlemen's system does not require every rancher to be a criminal — it only requires enough criminals to do the work that the system condones.

The novel's resolution does not defeat the cattlemen as a class — they remain the dominant economic interest in [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]]. But by protecting [[alice-crowson|Alice]]'s flock through the September deadline, [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] achieves a tactical victory within the larger war. The [[sheep-camp|sheep camp]] survives; the cattlemen's monopoly is, if not broken, at least breached.

## Moral Dimension

The cattlemen represent more than an economic adversary — they embody a moral problem that the novel examines at length. Their claim to the range rests on the principle of first possession, a principle that [[alice-crowson|Alice]] herself acknowledges: "They were the first here" (012-016). But the novel forces the question: does being first confer the right to exclude? The public domain, by definition, belongs to all.

The cattlemen's moral logic collapses under its own contradictions. They claim that sheep destroy the range for cattle — a practical argument dressed in the language of [[honor|custom]]. Yet their response to this alleged threat is not negotiation or legal recourse but murder. The novel's moral taxonomy places them not as honorable opponents but as a "little, evil group" (011-038) who have corrupted the frontier's [[honor|code]] into a tool of predation.

This corruption is most visible in [[landy-fargo|Fargo]]'s casual cynicism: "Who'd ever dream that old hag would ever find a renter — and as long as we were gettin' it free, what was the use?" (013-030). The phrase "gettin' it free" reveals the cattlemen's true motivation: not principle but profit, not custom but theft. The range war is not a clash of civilizations but a land grab.

## Key Members

While the cattlemen are a diffuse group, specific figures embody their [[dominance|power]]:

| Individual | Role |

## In the Context of the Range War

The cattlemen's campaign against the sheepmen is part of a historical pattern that the novel documents with remarkable precision. The range war is fought along two fronts simultaneously: the economic (control of grazing territory) and the symbolic (the cattlemen's claim to be the "legitimate" users of the range). On both fronts, the cattlemen hold the advantage of incumbency.

Yet the novel suggests that this advantage is eroding. [[alice-crowson|Alice]]'s sheep-camp, sustained by [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s [[guardianship]], demonstrates that sheep can coexist with cattle — that the "cattle won't feed after sheep" claim is, if not false, at least exaggerated. The cattlemen's monopoly depends on a practical falsehood as much as on violence.
