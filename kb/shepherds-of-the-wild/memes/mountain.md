# Mountain

The mountains in *Shepherds of the Wild* are more than geography — they are the spiritual architecture of the novel, "sentinel mountains grand and austere" (003-019) that frame every human action against the scale of geological time. Smoky Land lies "on the great shoulders of the Rockies — a high plateau, studded here and there by grim and lofty snow peaks" (001-010), and this elevation defines both the physical and moral landscape of the story.

## Definition

In the novel's world, the mountains represent the permanent and the true, standing in stark contrast to the ephemeral concerns of human civilization. They are "a hard, grim place, a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter" (001-010), yet "through it all, dwelling like a spirit, there was a beauty that could not be denied" (001-010). The mountains do not judge; they simply endure, and in their endurance they offer a standard of permanence against which human lives can be measured.

## Manifestations

The mountains manifest as both barrier and sanctuary. They isolate Smoky Land from civilization — "beyond — beyond the last outposts of civilization, beyond the end of the trails" (001-009). Their peaks hold snow year-round, their canyons conceal predators, their slopes provide summer grazing for the flocks. The mountain sheep, the bighorn, are emblems of this high country. When Hugh first truly sees the mountains at twilight, he is "vaguely stirred" (003-019) by their receding grandeur. The mountains are also the source of the novel's most terrible threat — the forest fire sweeps down from the ridges, and survival depends on finding passes through the mountain canyons.

## Key Characters/Events

The mountains shape every character who enters their domain. The Old Colonel, who "had known the great school of the forest" (002-008), understands that the mountains test a person in ways the city never can. Old Argali, "the great mountain ram" (001-011), embodies the mountains' wild nobility. For Hugh Gaylord, the mountains are initially alien and forbidding — "in those far mountains he was a stranger and an alien" (002-006) — but they become the setting for his transformation. Alice Crowson navigates the mountain canyons with the sure knowledge of a native, leading the flock through fire toward safety.

## Philosophical Significance

The mountains serve as the novel's ultimate reference point for permanence and scale. When Hugh's urban life dissolves into meaninglessness, the mountains remain: "the long outline of the high range against the pale western sky — the one part of the firmament the darkness had not yet completely overspread" (006-002). "Watching the flocks — the phrase was as old as the mountains themselves" (005-042). The mountains connect the present moment to the deep past, reminding every character that their struggles are part of a larger, older story.
