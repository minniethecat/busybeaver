# Flock

The flock in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the gravitational center of the novel's moral universe — the living charge around which all action revolves, the reason the herder keeps his watch, the prize the predators circle, and the ancient symbol that connects Hugh Gaylord to an unbroken lineage of human stewardship stretching back to "the first dim days when the nomads drove their flocks over the plains of Asia" (004-022).

## Definition

In the novel's world, the flock is not merely a collection of sheep but a sacred trust. It is the entity that defines the roles of every creature in Smoky Land: herders protect it, predators threaten it, shepherd dogs guard it. "All the basic elements of life were here: the flocks, the herder's little shelter, the fire glowing in the falling darkness, the watchful shepherd dog guarding the lambs, the beasts of prey lurking in the growing shadows" (004-021). The flock is the constant presence around which the cosmic drama of the novel unfolds — white, moving, vulnerable, and utterly dependent on the fidelity of those who watch it.

## Manifestations

The flock appears first as a visual marvel — "a flock that could not contain less than three thousand ewes and lambs" bedding down in a space barely a hundred yards square (004-002). Its whiteness is a recurring image: "the white sheep bedding down for the night" (004-021), a living drift of white against the dark forest. Black sheep appear as markers — "animals that occur in every western flock and are generally used by the herdsmen as markers" (004-002) — a practical detail that grounds the symbolism in authentic ranching knowledge.

The flock's vulnerability is its defining characteristic. Without a herder, "the beasts of prey would find easy hunting" (004-031), and even with the dog's vigilance, the sheep remain tempting game. The puma sees them as "an easy game to kill, dying at one little touch to the head or shoulder" (018-002). This vulnerability is precisely what makes the act of watching meaningful — the flock cannot protect itself, and so it calls into being the entire structure of guardianship that the novel celebrates.

## Key Characters/Events

**Hugh Gaylord's awakening** to the meaning of the flock is the novel's central transformation. When he realizes "Watching the flock! That was it — he was watching the flock himself" (004-044), his heart gives "a great leap in his breast." This is the moment Hugh moves from being a mere observer of the wilderness to a participant in its ancient rhythms. "It was his own presence as much as the growlings of the dog that kept the puma at a distance. Except for him, white fangs even now would be tearing at the throats of the lambs" (004-044).

**Dan's murder** leaves the flock unprotected, and it is this crisis that forces Hugh to confront the question of responsibility. The murder victim was, in Hugh's judgment, "of the class of shiftless and uneducated men from which most flock owners have to recruit their herders" (004-035), yet his death matters because the flock matters — the sheep cannot be abandoned.

**The shepherd dog** defines his entire existence in relation to the flock: "from dawn till dark he was at his toil, through the blasting heat of summer and the bitter winter cold, watching through the night and running through the day" (004-040). Even wounded, "the brave animal still kept his watch over the sheep" (004-040).

## Philosophical Significance

The flock is the novel's answer to Hugh's crisis of meaning. The city life he knew — clubs, motor cars, amusements — is dismissed as "mushroom growths of a little handful of centuries that men called the age of civilization" (004-021). Against this impermanence, the novel sets the timeless image of the flock: "Cities are born, grow great, are cursed with wickedness, and perish. The flocks still wander on the hills" (004-022).

The crucial moment comes when Hugh recognizes that "it did not lay with the shepherd to consider abstract issues of life and death. It was not for him to try to balance the value of a lamb's life with his own. His business was to watch the flock. His work was to guard the sheep. Nothing could come between" (005-022). This is the novel's moral center: the act of watching the flock is its own justification. One does not weigh the value of one's life against a lamb's; one simply guards. The simplicity of this duty liberates Hugh from the moral paralysis of the over-civilized man.

The flock also provides Hugh with a new relationship to joy: "The vision of himself watching the flocks had brought a new tolerance, a new breadth of view toward all the world. Never in his life, so far as he could remember, had he laughed from the simple joy of life, the laugh of sheer delight" (005-040).
