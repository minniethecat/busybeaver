# Cry-in-the-night

Cry-in-the-night is the great wolf of Smoky Land — a predator whose name embodies both his power and his elusiveness. Unlike [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]], who is pursued and named by men, Cry-in-the-night remains a creature of darkness and distance, known more by his voice than his form. He is the voice of the [[wilderness]], the howl that carries across the darkness to remind the [[shepherd]] that the watch is never done.

## Overview

The wolf enters the narrative through the memory of conflict: Broken Fang "in one starving winter had fought Cry-in-the-night, the wolf, and had mastered him" (007-025). This single sentence establishes a hierarchy of predators — the [[cougar]] above the wolf — while also acknowledging the wolf's stature as a worthy adversary for the "monarch of the cougars." The encounter frames Cry-in-the-night as the second rung in Smoky Land's [[concept-food-chain|food chain]]: a formidable [[predator]] capable of challenging the puma himself.

Yet Cry-in-the-night is unique among the novel's named predators. Broken Fang has a body — aging, failing, singular. [[coyote|Coyotes]] are named and tracked by PNR numbers. But Cry-in-the-night is defined by absence rather than presence. He is "such beasts as Cry-in-the-night" (020-014) — a species representative rather than an individual. His name itself is onomatopoeic: he is not seen but heard, not a creature but a cry, not a body but a voice.

Cry-in-the-night appears most vividly not in action but in Hugh Gaylord's ancestral dreams. Hugh dreams himself as a prehistoric shepherd, and in those dreams "the dog was like Cry-in-the-night — that was it! He was gray and white-fanged just like the wolves themselves" (019-013). The wolf is the archetypal enemy at the fire's edge, the twin lights glowing in the darkness that have haunted shepherds since before recorded time.

In Marshall's opening description of Smoky Land, the wolf is introduced as a collective voice of death: "The wolf pack sang of death and hunger when the ridges were swept with snow" (001-009). Before Cry-in-the-night has a name, before any individual wolf appears, the pack is already singing. This establishes the novel's treatment of the wolf as plural, collective, a force rather than a character.

## Character Arc

Unlike Broken Fang, Cry-in-the-night has no individual arc — he represents the species rather than the individual. This is deliberate: while Broken Fang is singular (a named individual with a broken tooth), the wolf is plural, collective, a force rather than a character. The novel never gives Cry-in-the-night a scene of his own, never narrates from his perspective as it does for the cougar. He exists only in relation to others: as the opponent Broken Fang once defeated, as the fear in Hugh's dreams, as the scent recognized by Spot and Argali.

Yet the novel grants him moments of near-mythic presence. In Hugh's dream, "Cry-in-the-night might kill him in a fair fight, but still he could not build a fire" (019-015). The wolf is powerful but limited — fire, the human technology, is beyond him. This is the boundary that separates man from beast: not strength, not ferocity, but fire-making. The dream sequence positions Cry-in-the-night as the ultimate test of the shepherd's worth — the adversary against whom all human tools and cunning must be deployed.

Spot knows the wolf's scent — "one that remotely suggested Cry-in-the-night" (021-014) — and recognizes him as a threat. But the wolf never attacks directly in the novel's present timeline. He is a presence more than an actor, a symbol of the wild's eternal menace. When Argali the bighorn ram descends from the high peaks, he too detects the scent: "There were unfamiliar smells in the air too: one that remotely suggested Cry-in-the-night" (022-009). The wolf's scent lingers at the margins of every significant encounter between the domestic and the wild.

The wolf's defining characteristic is his voice. Hugh's ancestral dream ends with him crying out — "a hoarse, wild, savage cry" (019-018) — and it is this cry that mirrors the wolf's own howl, the sound that gives Cry-in-the-night his name. The dream-shepherd's cry and the wolf's howl are echoes of each other, suggesting that the boundary between man and predator is thinner than civilization would like to believe.

## Relationships

Cry-in-the-night's primary relationship is with **Broken Fang**, who defeated him in combat. The text specifies that this fight occurred "in one starving winter" (007-025), suggesting a desperate encounter driven by hunger rather than territoriality — both apex predators reduced to fighting each other when prey was scarce. The cougar's victory places Cry-in-the-night in the subordinated position within Smoky Land's hierarchy, yet the wolf is never diminished by this defeat; he remains a creature of dread throughout the novel.

With **Hugh Gaylord**, the connection is psychological rather than physical. Hugh never confronts Cry-in-the-night directly, yet the wolf haunts his dreams as the archetype of the predator. In Hugh's prehistoric visions, the wolf represents the eternal adversary, the twin lights watching from the darkness, the force that makes the shepherd's vigilance necessary. The relationship is one of ancestral memory — the wolf has been stalking the human imagination since before the first campfire was lit.

With **Spot**, the relationship is one of inherited recognition. Spot — the bighorn-hybrid ram — carries genetic memories of the high peaks where wolves hunt. When Spot detects the wolf's scent (021-014), it is not learned but remembered, part of the deep ancestral knowledge that drives his longing for the mountain fastnesses "where such beasts as Cry-in-the-night could scarcely follow" (020-014).

With **Argali**, the great bighorn ram, the wolf is a distant but recognized danger. Argali's detection of the wolf's scent among the unfamiliar smells of the meadow (022-009) contributes to his wariness and his decision to retreat. Even the monarch of the high peaks knows to fear the wolf.

With **Shep**, the shepherd dog, the relationship is one of dark resemblance. In Hugh's dream, Shep — the loyal guardian — appears wolf-like: "The dog was like Cry-in-the-night — that was it! He was gray and white-fanged just like the wolves themselves" (019-013). This uncanny resemblance suggests that the guardian and the predator share a common ancestor, separated only by the human alliance. The dog is the domesticated wolf; the wolf is the wild dog. Both serve the flock, but on opposite sides of the firelight.

## Thematic Significance

Cry-in-the-night embodies the wild in its most elemental form — not the singular, tragic predator (Broken Fang) but the eternal, renewable menace. His name is onomatopoeic: he is defined by his voice, by the sound that reaches the shepherd across the darkness. He is fear made audible, [[danger]] given a voice.

The wolf's appearance in Hugh's ancestral dream — "He was gray and white-fanged just like the wolves themselves" (019-013) — suggests that the conflict between shepherd and wolf is older than civilization, encoded in human DNA. Hugh's guardianship is not just a job but an inheritance, a role passed down through millennia. The dream peels back the layers of modern consciousness to reveal the prehistoric shepherd who lived in terror of the wolf-pack's circling eyes.

This is reinforced by the opening image of the novel: before any human character appears, before the flock or the shepherd, "The wolf pack sang of death and hunger when the ridges were swept with snow" (001-009). The wolf's song is the baseline music of Smoky Land — the sound that defines the wilderness as a place of predation. Cry-in-the-night's howl is the audible form of the [[law-of-the-wild|law of the wild]]: that life feeds on life, and the shepherd's task is to hold that law at bay.

The collective nature of the wolf is thematically significant. Broken Fang is singular, legendary, an individual whose death would close a chapter. But Cry-in-the-night is plural — kill one wolf, and another will howl. The pack cannot be defeated, only guarded against. This makes the wolf a more fitting symbol of the wilderness than any single predator could be: the wild is not one thing but many, not an enemy but a condition.

The wolf's relationship to fire is the novel's central philosophical distinction between man and beast. "Cry-in-the-night might kill him in a fair fight, but still he could not build a fire" (019-015). The wolf possesses strength, ferocity, cunning — everything necessary to be a predator. But fire-making is beyond him. This single technological advantage is what separates the human shepherd from the animal kingdom, what allows Hugh — and his prehistoric dream-ancestor — to hold the darkness at bay. Fire is the thin line between civilization and savagery, and the wolf is everything that fire keeps out.

Finally, Cry-in-the-night's name matters. It is not a descriptive label imposed by men (like "Broken Fang" or "Spread Horn") but a translation of experience — the shepherd hears a cry in the night, and that cry becomes the wolf's identity. The name collapses the distinction between signifier and signified: the wolf *is* the cry, the sound, the terror that travels across the darkness. In this, Cry-in-the-night is the novel's purest embodiment of the [[wilderness]] itself — a presence known only by its effects, never fully seen, never fully understood, but always, always heard.
