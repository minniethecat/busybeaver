# Dream

**Dreams** in *Shepherds of the Wild* are not mere nighttime fantasies but conduits to ancestral memory. Through dreams, the novel's characters — human and animal alike — access experiences that predate their individual lives, connecting the present-day shepherd to his prehistoric forebears and the domesticated bighorn lamb to the wild rams of the high peaks.

## Hugh's Accusing Dreams

From his first nights among the sheep, Hugh Gaylord is visited by troubling dreams. "He was troubled with curious dreams; and even on wakening he didn't know quite what they were" (001-513). A recurring theme is accusation: "someone had been trying to tell him something to which he did not want to listen... damning him for shirking a great responsibility that had been put upon him" (001-513). The wilderness itself, it seems, uses dreams to call Hugh to his duty.

A second dream pattern is more visceral: "a recurring dream that some terrible Foe was lurking in the shadows just beyond his vision, ready to swoop down on one who was very weak and helpless; yet he wouldn't stay to fight it off" (001-515). These dreams diagnose Hugh's moral condition before he has the waking self-awareness to recognize it.

## The Ancestral Dream

The novel's most sustained dream sequence is an extraordinary passage of ancestral memory. Hugh experiences "one dream in particular that came to him night after night," so vivid "it was almost as if it had been some actual experience in his own life, rather than a remembered vista from the immeasurable past" (001-1453).

In this dream, Hugh is a prehistoric shepherd — his body transformed: "His arms — curiously long and quite black with hair — could crush the ribs of Cry-in-the-night like an eggshell. His chest was huge, his legs were knotted and gigantic" (001-1459). He guards the sheep by firelight, ringed by "the deadly shadows that ever encroached upon the space of firelight, and the strange twin lights that ever glowed from their depths" (001-1459). He wields a "Death Flint" dagger and knows the wild cry that frightens the circling predators.

This dream culminates in a moment that bridges the ancient and the modern: "a strange, wild cry from his own lips wakened Hugh, in the sheep camp of the twentieth century, from his dream" (001-1463). The cry that served his ancestor now serves him — and scatters a real coyote from the flock.

## Animal Dreams

The novel extends dreams beyond human consciousness. Spot, the domesticated bighorn lamb, is defined by his dreams: "Dreams, dreams! Spot, the young ram, waking or sleeping, never escaped from them" (001-1475). His dreams of "cliffs, waterfalls, snow-capped peaks" (001-1489) are memories of the wild homeland his species was exiled from — an ancestral knowing that persists despite domestication.

Hugh recognizes this kinship: "Spot, old boy... I believe you've got memories too. I believe you've been dreaming — just as I have" (001-1467). The shared capacity for ancestral dream links human and animal in a common wilderness inheritance.

> The crowds were far, the gaieties were known at last as the dreams they really were, and in their place was the silence, the inner peace, the joy of conquest, and the white sheep, feeding in the shadow of the high peaks. (001-1425)
