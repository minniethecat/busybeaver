# Instinct

Instinct in *Shepherds of the Wild* is portrayed as nature's alternative to conscious thought — an ancient, infallible wisdom that guides the wild creatures through their lives. It is the force that makes the wilderness comprehensible and survivable, and its presence or absence distinguishes the wild from the tame.

## Manifestations

The novel opens with instinct in action. When Spread Horn catches an unfamiliar scent — man — "at once his instincts began to get busy inside of him" (003-006). This is presented not as thought but as "a form of insect-like ratiocination" — an intelligence operating below consciousness but no less effective for it.

Instinct governs the rhythms of the wild. The "gregarious instinct" (009-011) keeps herds together; the "long-remembered instinct" (010-012) enables predators to recognize prey patterns; and even the sheep, the most domesticated of creatures, are moved by "an instinctive effort: he had not the slightest idea that he was watched" (016-002).

The novel suggests that instinct is a form of knowledge more reliable than human reason. The animals' "instincts had told them true" (014-038) — a statement that implicitly contrasts with human error and misjudgment. Instinct never deceives; it is the voice of nature speaking directly to its creatures.

## Key Characters

Spread Horn, the bull elk, is the novel's most complete embodiment of instinctual wisdom. He moves through the forest reading signs "as clearly as a printed calendar" (001-003), his survival depending entirely on instincts honed by evolution.

Hugh Gaylord occupies a transitional position. As "a wilderness man" (005-007), he has learned to trust his instincts, yet his human consciousness sometimes conflicts with them. In critical moments, "all his best instincts warned him to abstain" (006-010), revealing that even a civilized man retains access to this deeper knowledge.

The shepherd dogs bridge the gap between wild instinct and trained behavior. Their bravery is "blind" (007-034) — instinctual rather than calculated — yet it is channeled into the service of their human masters.

## Philosophical Significance

Instinct represents the novel's critique of pure rationality. The wilderness does not operate on logic but on an older, deeper wisdom that the novel calls instinct. When human characters override their instincts, they court disaster; when they listen, they survive.

Marshall draws a distinction between the "gregarious instinct" (009-011) that governs herd animals and the individual instinct that guides predators. The former is collective, preserving; the latter is individual, creative. Both are natural, and the novel refuses to privilege one over the other.

The implication is that modern civilization has dulled human instinct — that "only to the eyes of human beings, badly in need of spectacles" (001-003) does the natural world seem unchanging. Instinct is what the wild creatures have and humans have lost.
