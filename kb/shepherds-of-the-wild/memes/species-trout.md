# Trout


## Overview

The trout populates the cold, clear streams of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] in two forms: the small resident trout — "quivering, timid, sparkling creatures" that never leave their home waters — and the [[species-salmon|steelhead]], an anadromous giant that makes "an occasional thousand-mile jaunt to the ocean" (001-033). The novel treats both with a blend of naturalist precision and lyricism. The trout is simultaneously a link in the food chain (prey to otter, mink, and merganser) and a source of aesthetic rapture — "sportsmen say that a five-pounder at the end of a silk line will permit, for ten tearing, [[struggle|fight]]ing, breathless moments, a glimpse into the Promised Land" (001-033).

## Natural History

The streams of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] — particularly [[silver-creek|Silver Creek]] and its tributaries — support populations of rainbow and cutthroat trout. Resident trout spend their entire lives in freshwater, feeding on aquatic insects and smaller fish. Steelhead are rainbow trout that migrate to the ocean, growing to several times the size of their resident cousins before returning to freshwater to spawn. The novel distinguishes the [[species-salmon|steelhead]] from the true salmon with affectionate condescension: "But you can imagine the mighty salmon, who have spent four years in the sea and who have swum about the reefs of Kamchatka, regarding them with some patronage" (001-033). The trout's [[predator|predators]] include otter, mink, and mergansers, all of whom hunt the riffles and pools of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]]'s rivers (001-035).

## Role in the Novel

The trout is introduced in the novel's panoramic survey of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]]'s [[wilderness|wild]]life, where [[species-elk|Spread Horn]] the elk observes the river's inhabitants during the drinking hour (001-0[[TOC|31]], 001-033). The description serves as a miniature taxonomy of the region's aquatic life, distinguishing between the salmon, the [[species-salmon|steelhead]], and the "little trout" with the precision of a field naturalist.

The trout appears again as a symbol of the outdoor life the [[old-colonel|Old Colonel]] urges upon [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]. The [[old-colonel|Colonel]] frames a Western hunting expedition in terms of sporting abundance: "There are always trout, and this is trout-time in the West" (002-123). The trout here functions as a synecdoche for the West itself — always available, always renewing, a permanent invitation to the sporting life.

In the novel's most ecstatic passage on [[wilderness|wild]]erness experience, the [[species-salmon|steelhead]]'s strike is ranked alongside the flight of mallards and the sight of [[bighorn-sheep|bighorn]] sheep as one of the experiences that grants "[[wilderness|wild]]erness ecstasy": "those who have beheld the waters break and explode as the [[species-salmon|steelhead]] strikes can comprehend this [[wilderness|wild]]erness ecstasy at all" (006-485).

## Named Individuals

The novel names no individual trout. As with the salmon and deer, the trout functions at the species level — its individuality subsumed into its ecological role and its beauty.

## Symbolic/Thematic Role

The trout occupies a middle ground in the novel's symbolic register between the salmon (cosmic repatriation) and the unnamed minnows (mere food). The resident trout embodies contentment with one's native waters — "great stay-at-homes and never going to sea" (001-033) — a [[silence|quiet]] counterpoint to the salmon's epic migratory drive. The [[species-salmon|steelhead]], by contrast, attempts the salmon's journey on a smaller scale, and the novel's gentle condescension toward it ("regarding them with some patronage") suggests that not all returns are equally mythic. The trout's vulnerability — "one little clasping of those wicked teeth into the beautiful silver shoulder — and the trout leaped no more for flies in the cool of the evening" (001-035) — makes it the river's analogue to the deer: beauty inseparable from [[danger|peril]].
