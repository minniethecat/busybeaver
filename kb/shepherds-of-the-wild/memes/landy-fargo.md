# Landy Fargo

Landy Fargo is the primary human antagonist of *Shepherds of the Wild*. A cattleman who "controls everything" (012-011) in the region, Fargo represents the forces of greed and domination that threaten both the shepherds and the wilderness they protect. He serves as the manager and part-owner of a cattle operation backed by "a number of rich men in the East" (012-011), giving his personal vendetta against the shepherds the weight of institutional power.

## Overview

Fargo is introduced indirectly, through the fear he inspires in others. "Landy Fargo — José Mertos" (007-013) are named together as threats, establishing Fargo's network of power. He is a man who operates through proxies and intimidation rather than direct confrontation — at least initially. Alice Crowson explains his position to Hugh: "they are cattlemen — managers — and they're part owners, too — for a number of rich men in the East. For years and years they've had everything their own way in regard to the range" (012-011). This dual identity — as both local strongman and agent of distant capital — makes him a distinctively modern antagonist within the framework of the traditional range-war narrative.

Fargo's philosophy is blunt and cynical. "When it comes to the shooting stage," he says, "no one will find the bodies" (013-012). He has no moral qualms about violence when it serves his interests. His house on the "lower waters of Silver Creek" (013-015) is described as a headquarters of power rather than a home — a "stuffy living room" (013-015) where he waits "dozing in a great chair" (013-015) for his enforcer to return with news of violence done on his orders. Fargo oversees his operations from this domestic command post, combining the comforts of settlement with the ruthlessness of conquest.

Fargo is described repeatedly through animalistic and brutal imagery. He has a "brutal face" (016-018), "brutal hands" (014-010), "brutal jaws" (016-018), and "dark mouth" (016-018). The novel consistently emphasizes his physical coarseness as an outward manifestation of his spiritual deformity. He is also explicitly superstitious — "Fargo was a superstitious man" (014-012) — a trait that aligns him with the darker, irrational forces of the wilderness rather than with reason or civilization.

## Character Arc

Fargo's role is defined by his conflict with the shepherds, particularly Hugh Gaylord. He sees the sheep as competition for range land and is willing to use any means — economic pressure, intimidation, and ultimately violence — to eliminate them.

### Escalation Through Proxies

His relationship with José Mertos reveals his method of operation: he hires skilled enforcers to carry out his will. When José returns from the shooting of Dan, the herder, Fargo greets him with a single question: "Clean job, eh?" (013-017) He shows no interest in the victim, only in the operational result. Fargo watches "with hard eyes" (013-018) — he is a commander, not a soldier, and his power lies in his network and resources.

When José reports that the shepherd may still be alive, Fargo's response is chillingly practical. Rather than send José back, he devises a new plan using his pack of bear dogs. In his stuffy living room, the hounds' sudden howling from behind the house inspires him: "It was as if they heard us talkin' about sheep. It's like they was tryin' to tell us what to do" (014-012). The narrative explicitly frames this moment in supernatural terms: "It might have been the voice of an evil genius, prompting their vicious designs" (014-012). Fargo is "tingling all over with hatred and malice, inspired to the depths of his wicked being by the cry in the darkness" (014-012).

### The Dog-Pack Scheme

Fargo's most methodical atrocity is the training of his bear hounds to kill sheep. He and José purchase a live lamb from a neighboring herder and throw it to the dogs in a fenced enclosure behind Fargo's house. "There was only a single second of strange and dreadful clamor within the enclosure, a glimpse of white in the ravening circle of browns and blacks, a smear of red and a faint cry that the men strained to hear but which was lost in the baying of the dogs" (014-014). The scene is described as "unmentionable" (014-014), and the narrative voice refuses to dwell on it, yet it is essential to understanding Fargo's character: he does not merely kill his enemies — he corrupts the natural order, turning guardian animals into killers for sport.

Fargo then rides his pack into Smoky Land to unleash them on the shepherd's flock. He is described as a competent mountain man, navigating by landmarks and moonlight: "Fargo was a mountain man, and he knew what to do" (016-015). This competence makes his evil more dangerous — he is not an incompetent brute but a skilled operator who has chosen malice.

### The Confrontation at Hugh's Fire

Fargo's first direct encounter with Hugh occurs when he stumbles upon Hugh's campfire while tracking his escaped hounds. The confrontation is tense and revealing. Fargo immediately asserts class superiority: "I want you to know I'm not expectin' any back talk from such as you. All I'd have to do was to say the word, and old Crowson would fire you in a minute" (016-017). When Hugh guesses his identity, "Fargo glared, and there ensued a half-second of strained silence, of curious immobility on the part of them both" (016-018).

The standoff is broken by the distant baying of the hounds, and Fargo's reaction betrays his true nature: Hugh sees "the strange, exultant look in his brutal face" (016-018). When Hugh demands Fargo's horse to ride to Alice's aid, Fargo howls defiance and reaches for his hip — and Hugh knocks him unconscious with a single blow. "He dimly heard Fargo grunt — like a beast as it falls below the butcher's stroke — then saw him reel and fall" (016-019). Hugh leaves him "sleeping peacefully" (018-014) beside the dying fire, and when he returns hours later, he delivers the devastating verdict: "You can have your horse now... And, of course, you can have your dogs, too. There's quite a little heap of them for you back there in the forest" (018-014).

Fargo's defeat is total and public: "It seemed to Fargo, when he went to look, that only a laugh followed him out of the firelight. It was to haunt him for months, that laugh" (018-014). He stands over the heap of dead hounds "clear into the dawn, strange fumes of rage and hatred in his brain. The buzzards dropped down one by one to see what had interested him" (018-014).

### The Forest Fire

Fargo's final scheme is his most ambitious: a forest fire designed to trap the shepherds and their flock. As the narrator notes, "Landy Fargo was not of a mind to have his plan go wrong, so he had given particular care to its details. He didn't want to take even the slightest chance of failure" (026-003). He personally lights "the circle of fires in front of the sheep flock" (027-003), seeking direct satisfaction: "In the hand, more than the brain, lies the gratification of vengeance; and Fargo didn't intend to miss this satisfaction" (027-003).

But the wilderness itself turns against him. His horse trips on a rodent's burrow, and Fargo is thrown, knocked unconscious. When he wakes, disoriented, he finds the fire has surrounded him: "The fiery wall was behind him, too, leaping toward him with a deadly and terrible ferocity" (027-011). His own hand — "the hand in which he had exulted such a little time before, and which had set the flame" (027-013) — is burned by the fire. The narrative makes his fate explicitly poetic: "He was helpless — powerless to aid himself as the Shropshire lamb that he had thrown, so many weeks ago, among his hounds. His own handiwork had turned against him, and the vengeance of the wilderness was complete" (027-014).

Hugh, having survived the fire with Alice, notes that they "can get Fargo too — on a charge of arson, at least" (028-010), suggesting that human justice awaits Fargo if the wilderness has not already consumed him.

## Relationships

José Mertos is Fargo's right-hand man and primary enforcer. Their relationship is transactional — Mertos rides "on orders" (023-021) — but effective. They plan together in Fargo's living room at dawn, with José providing "any little suggestions that did not occur to his chief" (014-010). Yet there is no loyalty or affection between them; Mertos questions Fargo's decisions, and Fargo issues commands without warmth. They are bound by shared malice rather than trust.

With Hugh Gaylord, Fargo represents everything the shepherd stands against: the treatment of the wilderness as territory to be exploited rather than a trust to be kept. Their physical confrontation is the novel's central moral clash. After Hugh knocks him unconscious, "Fargo remembered how Hugh — with his bleeding arm — had motioned for him to go, and how at the same time his hard, bright eyes had been watching for any offensive motion on the part of Fargo. But courage to attack simply would not come to him" (022-003). Hugh's moral and physical victory is complete — Fargo is unmanned by the encounter.

Alice Crowson is the daughter of Fargo's economic rival, Crowson. Fargo never interacts with her directly in the narrative, but she is the target of his dog-pack attack, and his hired men threaten her life. She represents everything he cannot control — youth, loyalty, and a feminine presence in the wilderness that exists outside his network of power.

Bill Elkins, "a herder who the following spring had gone over to Fargo and his gang" (020-003), represents Fargo's ability to corrupt and recruit. The defection of a shepherd to the cattlemen's side shows Fargo's economic and social reach extending even into his enemies' camp.

## Thematic Significance

Fargo embodies the threat of civilization to the wilderness — not civilization as culture or wisdom, but civilization as domination and extraction. He is what the Old Colonel's world becomes when stripped of its gentlemanly code: pure power, pure greed.

Several dimensions of Fargo's symbolic function deserve attention:

**Capital and the Range.** Fargo is not an independent rancher but a manager for absentee Eastern capital. This makes him a specifically modern villain — he exploits the land not for a living but for profit on behalf of distant owners. His conflict with the shepherds is not a traditional range war between equals but a contest between organized capital and smallholders like Crowson, whose "whole capital is tied up in this one flock" (012-011).

**The Corruption of the Natural.** Fargo's worst crime is not murder but the perversion of natural order. He turns bear hounds — animals bred to hunt predators — into sheep-killers. He uses fire, a tool of warmth and light, as a weapon of destruction. The novel's wilderness philosophy holds that nature has its own laws and balances, and Fargo violates these as thoroughly as he violates human law.

**Irony and Poetic Justice.** Fargo's fate is structured as an extended exercise in poetic irony. He throws a lamb to his dogs; he is himself thrown to the fire he started. He exults in the power of his hands; his hand is the first part of him the fire claims. The "forest-demons" (027-015) reward him with "super-consciousness — a fine keying of every nerve and an added sensitiveness to his flesh" (027-014) so that he may fully experience his own destruction. This is not mere coincidence within the novel's moral universe — it is the wilderness itself delivering justice.

**Superstition and Evil.** Fargo's superstition — his belief that the dogs' howling is a supernatural prompting — aligns him with the irrational, the demonic. The novel does not treat his superstition as folk wisdom but as a channel for "the voice of an evil genius" (014-012). In the book's moral geography, Fargo is not merely a criminal but a figure of spiritual darkness whose destruction is less a legal outcome than a cosmic necessity.

**Class and Brutality.** The novel repeatedly emphasizes Fargo's physical coarseness — his "brutal hands" (014-010), "brutal jaws" (016-018), "brutal features" (027-007). This is class-coded language: Fargo is a man who works through force rather than through the gentleman's code that Hugh, despite his poverty, instinctively honors. When Fargo expects "such as you" (016-017) to submit to his authority, he is asserting a hierarchy that Hugh's actions prove to be hollow.

The conflict between shepherds and cattlemen mirrors the historical range wars of the American West, but Marshall elevates it to a philosophical struggle between two ways of relating to nature — and Fargo is the embodiment of the way that must be destroyed for the wilderness to survive.
