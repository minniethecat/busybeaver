# Pride

Pride in *Shepherds of the Wild* is a double-edged virtue. It is the quality that makes [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] magnificent, that gives the shepherd dog his dignity, and that sustains [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] through humiliation. But it is also the quality that blinds [[landy-fargo|Fargo]] to his vulnerability, that isolates the great cougar from his own mortality, and that must be surrendered before love can enter.

## The Pride of the Predator

Broken Fang is pride personified. His voice carries "a living expression of power and pride that only one of the greatest of wilderness creatures could make heard" (005-027). This is not vanity — it is the self-knowledge of the apex predator, the earned confidence of the creature that has never been challenged. His pride is the natural extension of his power: he is magnificent, and he knows it.

Yet the novel also suggests that this pride is a form of blindness. The great [[species-puma|cougar]] cannot conceive of his own defeat. When Hugh's bullets strike, Broken Fang's response is not fear but fury — the outrage of a king who cannot believe in his own mortality. His pride, which made him beautiful, also makes him vulnerable.

The coyotes, by contrast, have had their pride stripped away. "They have been exiled to the rocky hills, they are afflicted with madness in Indian summer, their pride has been taken from them, and all of the greater forest creatures treat them with contempt" (009-008). The loss of pride is depicted as a kind of spiritual wound — the coyote is not merely hungry but humiliated, and this humiliation shapes his every action.

## Human Pride and Its Costs

Hugh arrives in [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] with the pride of his class and upbringing, but the wilderness steadily strips it away. He is a tenderfoot, a greenhorn, a man who does not know how to do the simplest things. His old pride — the pride of the Greenwood Club, of inherited wealth, of urban sophistication — proves worthless in the mountains. What replaces it is something harder and more genuine: the pride of having done a difficult thing well.

Fargo's pride is of a different order. "Part of the hunting fever is due to pride, a sense of power and might" (006-012), the novel observes. Fargo's pride is territorial — the pride of the man who has dominated a range and expects his dominion to go unchallenged. When Hugh defies him, it is not merely a practical setback but an existential wound. Fargo's pride cannot tolerate defiance, and this intolerance drives the novel's central conflict.

## Pride and Love

The novel's most delicate treatment of pride comes through Hugh's relationship with [[alice-crowson|Alice]]. In Chapter 19, Hugh confronts his own unworthiness: "Had he not been a waster, a slacker from the works of men? Had he anything in general or particular because of which he might expect a girl to love him? The things on which he used to pride himself were meaningless now" (019-004). The old pride — based on status and sophistication — has collapsed, and Hugh has not yet built a new one based on genuine achievement.

This is the pride that must be surrendered. Hugh cannot approach Alice from a position of status or entitlement; he can only offer what he has become in the wilderness. The novel suggests that authentic pride — the kind worth having — is not a claim but a consequence. It is earned through action, not inherited through position.
