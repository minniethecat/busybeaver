# Drought

The drought in *Shepherds of the Wild* is a season of ecological trial that tests every creature in Smoky Land. It withers the grass, dries the streams, drives the wild game upward toward the high peaks, and ultimately sets the stage for the catastrophic forest fire that engulfs the novel's climax. The drought is both a natural phenomenon and a narrative engine — it intensifies hunger, sharpens the predator-prey relationship, and reveals the resilience or fragility of every living thing in the wilderness.

## Definition

In the novel, drought is a prolonged dry spell that transforms the landscape of Smoky Land from a verdant range into "dry as dust" terrain where "the leaves crinkly and crisp" and "most of the springs were dried up" (008-039). It is the antithesis of the life-giving rain cycle — a condition in which "the grass was dry as dust" and the lesser tributaries became "only successions of stagnant pools in which the silver people were already dying and turning white bellies to the sun" (008-039). The drought is not a singular catastrophe but a recurring natural cycle known to the forest people, memorialized in legend and prophecy.

## Ecological Cascade

The effects of drought cascade through every level of the wilderness order. Smaller creatures suffer first — "the grubs that the old black bear loved were dried to little flakes, like grease spots, on the dead logs; and the berries withered and dropped off before they ever ripened" (008-039). The deer found poor feeding; saltlicks became "hard, dry mud" (008-039).

Yet the drought does not harm all creatures equally. The wolves "ran their game, and since in the hot, stifling days exhaustion came quickly to their prey, perhaps they benefited, rather than suffered, from the drought" (008-039). This asymmetry — predators thriving while prey weakens — is one of the novel's sharpest observations about natural systems: hardship is not distributed evenly, and the strong often gain from the suffering of the weak.

The instinct of all creatures was "to climb ever higher — into the far, lovely grass slopes of the high peaks" where "the melting snow, the colder nights and days, the moisture-laden winds that swept across them removed, in some degree, the effect of drought" (008-043). The [[bighorn-sheep]] dwelt in these high realms, surviving even in starving times.

### The Buzzard Legend

The buzzards become the drought's most memorable symbol. "For disaster to the forest creatures always means triumph to the buzzards. They are the undertakers, the followers of the dead" (008-037). The novel invests the buzzards with a dark mythology — a legend "passed down from mother to fledgling among them, that long ago such a drought did happen" and a prophecy "that some time such an hour of glory will come again" (008-037). The buzzards' patient waiting for mass death gives the drought a gothic, almost apocalyptic dimension. They are not merely scavengers but embodiments of the drought's ultimate logic: the drought is a catastrophe that some creatures welcome.

## Key Characters/Events

[[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] meets the drought with human ingenuity. Because he is "a strong man" with "a faithful dog to care for them," Crowson's flock "weathered the drought with little discomfort" (008-047). Hugh led the sheep to "the greenest glens, the richest meadows," moving camp weekly ever higher "where the effects of drought were less" (008-047). Through his vigilance, "few of his sheep died of sickness, and the lambs grew like weeds" (008-053).

The drought also attracts predators. Hugh observed that "the days of drought had brought ever more of the wild hunters about his flock, and almost anywhere he looked he saw their luminous eyes, two by two, as they waited in the darkness" (009-015).

### The Drought-Fire Connection

The drought creates the conditions for the [[forest-fire]] that becomes the novel's ultimate trial. A cowman arrives complaining that "the streams where his cattle fed were drying up" and warns: "Never seen the woods so dry in all my days. Just like tinder" (010-023). This tinder-dry forest, set alight by [[jose-mertos|José Mertos]] in his madness, becomes the inferno that nearly consumes all. The novel thus presents the drought as a compound danger: it is not merely a hardship in itself but the precondition for an even greater catastrophe. The drought strips the forest of its natural fire resistance, and human malice — José's arson — exploits this vulnerability to devastating effect.

## Philosophical Significance

The drought embodies the indifference of the wild — it is neither cruel nor merciful, only natural. It reveals character: the wolf's opportunism, the buzzard's grim patience, the bighorn's endurance, and Hugh's resourcefulness. As one of the novel's core natural cycles, drought is the season that precedes renewal, the necessary trial before the rains of October. It strips away the illusion of security and exposes the true order of survival in Smoky Land.

The drought also serves as a test of the shepherd's competence. Hugh's ability to keep his flock alive and thriving through the drought — when wild creatures are dying and cattlemen are panicking — is the novel's clearest demonstration that skillful shepherding can overcome natural adversity. This is not a denial of nature's power but an affirmation of human agency within natural constraints.
