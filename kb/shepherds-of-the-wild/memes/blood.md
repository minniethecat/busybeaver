# Blood

**Blood** runs through the wilderness of *Shepherds of the Wild* as both literal substance and symbolic force — the hot pulse of predation, the cold stain of murder, and the deep inheritance of ancient instinct that binds shepherd and predator alike.

## Blood as Primal Vitality

The novel repeatedly invokes blood as the seat of wild vitality. When Hugh Gaylord first takes up his rifle in service of the flock, "the blood leaped and sang in his veins" (001-419). This is not mere metaphor — it signals his awakening to the ancient war between shepherd and predator. Later, transformed by his life in the mountains, Hugh reflects that his friend the shepherd dog will remain faithful "as long as blood stirred in his veins" (001-1423), linking blood to the deepest bonds of loyalty.

The corporeal reality of blood also marks key narrative moments. Hugh's initial dissipation is registered in his "bloodshot" eyes (001-077), and his eventual redemption is signaled when "the little blood-splotches had quite gone from the whites" (001-1413). The physical transformation tracks the spiritual one.

## The Hunting Hour and Bloodlust

As dusk falls across the wilderness, the narrative voice describes "the hunting hour" — a time when "wild, hot blood leaped in savage veins; strange terrible lights sprang up in fierce eyes" (001-483). This bloodlust is presented not as mere savagery but as a kind of ecstasy, "a rapturous, an exultant thing" that civilized humans struggle to comprehend (001-485).

The cougar, described as "no more magnificent slayer in the whole woods," feels the blood "leap in his veins anew" when he catches the scent of the sheep flock (001-567). The coyote Running Feet makes his stalk with "the blood leaped in his veins" (001-781). These parallel descriptions unify predator species through a shared physiological response — blood as the universal conductor of the hunting impulse.

## Blood and Violence

Blood also marks the novel's moments of violence. The death of Hugh's predecessor Dan is characterized as "cold-blooded murder" (001-345). The massacre of the sheep flock leaves "bloodstained pine needles" (001-1329) as the shepherds confront the aftermath of predation. José Mertos is described as "no stranger to the blood-madness" (001-1017), a human predator who has known the same hunting ecstasy as the cougar and wolf.

## Blood as Inheritance

The novel traces blood as lineage and inherited character. Pete, Hugh's disreputable guide, is described as having "good blood in him, the blood of as brave and hardy a race as ever lived, but degeneracy was upon him" (001-171). Hugh himself is described as possessing "Northern blood" that "gave him a background of strength" (001-077). In the novel's culminating reflection, the shepherd's calling is traced to something deeper than profession — "it was in the blood" (001-1451), linking Hugh's transformation to an ancestral inheritance reaching back to "the first dawn of civilization."

> The love of strong drink was gone from his body. He didn't look a great deal like the man that the Old Colonel had sent forth from the Greenwood Club. His hands and face had been brown before, now they were almost as dark as those of Pete, his late guide. A fast walk over the ridges did not fatigue him now. He was lean and hard as hickory, the muscles rippled under his toughened skin, the sweet, mountain air rushed deep in his lungs. (001-1413)
