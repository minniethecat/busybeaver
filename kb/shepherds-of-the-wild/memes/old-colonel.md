# Old Colonel

The Old Colonel is the catalyst of *Shepherds of the Wild*. Though he appears only briefly, his decision to send Hugh Gaylord to Smoky Land sets the entire narrative in motion. "All men knew fondly as the 'Old Colonel'" (002-002) — he is a beloved figure, a man of judgment and warmth, the "gray, wise, venerable head of the board of governors" (002-002) of the Greenwood Club.

## Overview

The Colonel is introduced as a discerning observer of character. He studies Hugh's face and sees both the problem and the solution: "The Old Colonel was a little despairing: he had begun to fear that in this lay the true explanation of Hugh's growing seediness — that he lacked the basic stuff" (002-010). Rather than giving up on Hugh, the Colonel intervenes.

"You're looking a bit seedy, Hugh," the Old Colonel began in his usual straightforward way. "I'm afraid you're getting to be sort of a poor stick." (002-011). His tone is uniquely disarming: "perfectly matter-of-fact, just as if he were pronouncing a judgment on the weather. Hugh flushed to the roots of his hair but he didn't take offense. No one ever could take offense when the Colonel told them truths." (002-011). This quality of honest, painless candor is the Colonel's signature — he commands affection and respect in equal measure.

The Colonel's connection to Hugh runs deep. It traces back "to one of his own mother's girlhood romances — a rather beautiful story such as men tell their wives and sweethearts but from masculine reserve do not talk over among themselves" (002-011). The Colonel's investment in Hugh is personal and historical, not merely charitable, giving emotional weight to his intervention.

His philosophy is simple: "If you'd once learn to love the outdoors, and learn to love it well, you'd find your way clear to a happier, healthier existence" (002-027). The Colonel believes in the transformative power of wilderness experience, and his faith in Hugh proves prescient.

The Colonel's proposal is characteristically direct: "I say go to Smoky Land. These two weeks might teach you to love the woods so you'd go again and again" (002-035). "The Old Colonel always put his propositions in just that straight-out way; and it made them hard to refuse." (002-033). What the Colonel cannot foresee is that Hugh will not merely visit — he will stay.

The Colonel is a worldly man with impressive connections. Hugh knew about the "good enough beggars" that were friends to the Colonel — "many of them men of great names and titles whom lesser Americans would boast of knowing" (002-026). Yet the Colonel himself "shook his head somewhat sadly, and for a moment his eyes gazed out over the twilight grounds" (002-026), suggesting a man who has seen beyond titles and wealth to deeper truths about human nature.

The Colonel also invokes heritage and race as moral obligation: "Remember," the Old Colonel urged further, "you're an Anglo-Saxon — a white man of straight descent. It's a heritage, Hugh. And it implies an obligation." (002-037). This appeal to ancestral duty — to the outdoor life of the Anglo-Saxon tillers of the soil (009-025) — is the Colonel's deepest argument. He knows that Hugh has "had everything too easy" (002-016) and needs "to sweat once" (002-016) to recover his birthright of self-reliance.

## Character Arc

The Colonel appears primarily in Chapter 2, serving as the narrative's inciting force. His brief but vivid presence establishes the stakes of Hugh's journey and the faith placed in him. Though physically absent from the wilderness chapters, the Colonel's influence persists — Hugh thinks of him at critical moments, measuring himself against the Colonel's expectations.

In Chapter 5, as Hugh faces his first night alone by the sheep camp, "his mind flew back to the Old Colonel, sitting in the Greenwood Club" (005-033). The memory of the Colonel becomes a touchstone, a connection to the civilized world that Hugh is leaving behind even as he honors the Colonel's prescription.

In Chapter 9, the Colonel's advice proves practical as well as philosophical. Hugh recalls the pipe the Colonel selected for him: "The Old Colonel himself had passed upon it, explaining certain virtues in regard to it that Hugh had since forgotten. 'You'll like a pipe,' the old gentleman had said, 'if you ever get into a real outdoor-man's mood. Cigarettes are all right, of course — probably don't give you the nicotine kick that a pipe does — but a pipe has been the woodsman's smoke from Sir Walter Raleigh and the Indians down.'" (009-013). This small gift, a pipe costing "something less than a dollar" (009-013), becomes a symbol of Hugh's gradual transformation from clubman to outdoorsman.

In Chapter 18, Hugh reflects on how far he has come since the Colonel dispatched him: "He didn't look a great deal like the man that the Old Colonel had sent forth from the Greenwood Club" (018-018). The recollection is both a measure of his growth and a silent tribute to the Colonel's wisdom. Hugh's exultant thought echoes the Colonel's original charge: "'Go for two weeks,' the Old Colonel had said. 'And Lord knows — it might make a man of you.'" (018-019). The prophecy has been fulfilled.

The Colonel's presence then recedes entirely from the narrative — but returns at the novel's close, in a visionary passage. As Hugh and Alice stand together at the novel's end, the narrative imagines that "perhaps the spirit of the Old Colonel — sitting in the Greenwood Club and dreaming of the mountain realms he loved — came wandering over peak and plain and saw these two, their arms about each other and their eyes lighted with fond dreams — and swiftly stole back to make its report to its master. And thereupon the old man smiled in his half-doze, wondering at the mighty ways of worlds and men — and the calm spirit of the flocks, grazing in the forest." (031-022). This coda grants the Colonel a kind of omniscient benediction, confirming that his experiment succeeded beyond all expectation.

## Relationships

The Colonel's relationship with Hugh is avuncular and discerning. He sees past Hugh's surface — "the true explanation of Hugh's growing seediness" (002-010) — to the potential beneath. His trust is a gift that Hugh must earn, and the novel charts Hugh's slow, hard-won fulfillment of that trust.

The Colonel's reference to Hugh's mother reveals a deeper connection: his interest in Hugh stems from "one of his own mother's girlhood romances" (002-011). The Colonel is not merely a club acquaintance but a man with a personal stake in Hugh's fate, carrying forward a bond from the previous generation.

The Colonel also functions as the symbolic head of the Greenwood Club. He is its "gray, wise, venerable head of the board of governors" (002-002), and the Club itself — its lounging room, its champagne, its idle amusements — represents the world that both produced Hugh's malaise and provided the means of his cure.

## Thematic Significance

The Old Colonel represents civilization's best qualities — wisdom, generosity, judgment — channeled toward the wilderness. He understands that the cure for modern malaise is not more civilization but less. His prescription — send the young man west — is the novel's thesis in miniature.

The Colonel's genius lies in his diagnosis. He perceives that Hugh's problem is not moral weakness but spiritual emptiness — the result of a life too easy, too sheltered, too disconnected from physical reality. His prescription of the outdoor life reflects a philosophy that the novel will validate across every subsequent chapter: that a man finds his truest self not in comfort but in challenge, not in society but in solitude with nature.

The closing vision of the Colonel (031-022) elevates him from plot device to presiding spirit. His spirit, "dreaming of the mountain realms he loved" (031-022), transcends the physical distance between the Greenwood Club and Smoky Land. The Colonel becomes the novel's surrogate reader — the one who, like us, watches Hugh's transformation with gratified wonder, confirming that the story has been worth telling.

The Colonel's Anglo-Saxon appeal (002-037) must be understood in its historical context. Bindloss writes in 1922, and the Colonel's rhetoric draws on then-common ideas of racial heritage and ancestral calling. Modern readers may find this language uncomfortable, but within the novel's moral framework it serves a specific purpose: to give Hugh a sense of obligation larger than himself, a duty to the past that can overcome his present aimlessness.
