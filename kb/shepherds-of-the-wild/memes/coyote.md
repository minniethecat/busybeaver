# Coyote

The coyote is the ever-present minor predator of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] — "abject cowards" (015-004) when alone, but a persistent [[danger|threat]] to lambs and weak sheep that wears down the flock's defenses through sheer relentlessness. The novel uses the coyote as a deliberate foil: the predator that is merely irritating where the [[cougar]] is magnificent and the [[wolf]] is terrifying. In Marshall's moral ecology, the coyote occupies the lowest rung — not without danger, but without dignity.

## Overview

*Canis latrans*, the coyote, is North America's most adaptable predator — smaller than the [[wolf]], more opportunistic, and far more successful at coexisting with human encroachment. In [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]], coyotes "skulk" along the trails (004-005) and test the flock's defenses nightly, embodying the kind of danger that requires constant but unglamorous vigilance.

The novel's most distinctive coyote is Running Feet, a named individual who is "part of his curse was a far-reaching and calculating intelligence almost equal to that of a dog" (016-003). Unlike the anonymous coyotes that appear only as tracks and distant wails, Running Feet is characterized — and the characterization is damning: "a trait that would have been a tremendous advantage if he had been blessed with courage to go with it, but which to a coward meant only realization of a thousand dangers to make life a torment" (016-003).

[[alice-crowson|Alice Crowson]]'s assessment is definitive: "The coyotes were abject cowards" (015-004). Unlike [[wolf|wolves]], they lack the courage — or the pack coordination — to [[danger|threat]]en a well-[[guardianship|guard]]ed flock. But they are constant, and their presence requires constant vigilance.

> Here a coyote had raced along in some chase of death, and here a cougar had crept. (004-005)

## Natural History

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 20–50 pounds — much smaller than [[wolf|wolves]] |
| Diet | Small prey (rabbits, rodents), carrion, and opportunistically lambs |
| Range | Expanded across North America even as wolf populations declined |
| Social Structure | Loosely territorial; more solitary than [[wolf|wolves]] |
| Ecological Niche | Mesopredator — below [[wolf|wolves]] and cougars in the [[dominance|hierarchy]] |

The coyote's success is tied to its adaptability. Unlike the [[wolf]], which requires large territories and intact prey populations, the coyote thrives at the edges of human settlement. This adaptability is, in Marshall's framing, a kind of moral failing — the coyote survives not through courage but through evasion.

## Role in the Novel

The coyote appears primarily as background [[danger|menace]], a presence that keeps [[shep|the shepherd dog]]s alert and the [[shepherd|herder]]s vigilant. Running Feet, the named coyote, represents the predator as coward — calculating and intelligent but ultimately contemptible:

> He understood perfectly what displeasing treatment would be his if the dogs came upon him at the killing. Not even Broken Fang, the puma, can always protect himself against the onslaught of the Barking One on guard over the flocks. (016-004)

When [[spot|Spot]] confronts a coyote that has crept close to the flock, the incident reveals both the coyote's opportunism and [[spot|the young ram]]'s emerging [[courage]]. The coyote flees — as coyotes always do when faced with genuine [[struggle|resistance]].

## Symbolic Role

The coyote represents the ordinary, unglamorous dangers of the [[wilderness]] — not the epic [[struggle|battle]] but the daily skirmish. Where [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] is a worthy adversary whose defeat brings honor, the coyote is merely a pest whose defeat brings only relief.

This distinction is important to the novel's moral ecology. Not all [[savagery|wildness]] is noble, and the [[shepherd]]'s work is mostly mundane vigilance against mundane [[danger|threat]]s. The coyote is [[nature|the wild]]erness's most honest representative of this truth: most danger is not dramatic. Most danger is just... persistent.

Running Feet's characterization as a coward — "a far-reaching and calculating intelligence" married to fear — makes him the novel's explicit counterpoint to the [[guardianship|guardian]] figures. Where [[shep|Shep]] embodies courage without calculation, Running Feet embodies calculation without courage.
