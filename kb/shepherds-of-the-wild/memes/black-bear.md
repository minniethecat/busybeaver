# Black Bear


## Overview

The black bear occupies a singular place in the [[wilderness|Smoky Land wilderness]] — neither the most feared predator (that honor belongs to the [[species-grizzly|grizzly]]) nor the most efficient hunter ([[broken-fang|the cougar]] and wolf surpass it), but a creature of such distinct personality that the novel names it the "most lovable spirit in the forest" (002-0[[TOC|31]]). Known as "Growl-in-the-throat," the black bear blunders through the ridges with a gruff dignity, "grunting and mumbling and soliloquizing" (004-005), a solitary philosopher of the woods.

## Natural History

The American black bear (*Ursus americanus*) is the most common bear species in North America. Smaller than the [[species-grizzly|grizzly]], it is an opportunistic omnivore whose diet ranges from berries and grubs to occasional fawns and carrion. The novel captures its varied diet: the black bear is a "honey-grubber" who loves the grubs beneath dead logs, and the drought that parches [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] hits him hard — "the grubs that the old black bear loved were dried to little flakes, like grease spots, on the dead logs; and the berries withered and dropped off before they ever ripened" (018-0[[TOC|31]]).

## Role in the Novel

The black bear appears in several distinct roles. First, as a figure of solitary dignity. The narrative notes that "even the old black bear, the honey-grubber who is, after all, the most lovable spirit in the forest, had been known to speak politely when the two of them met on the trail" (002-0[[TOC|31]]) — a remarkable concession from a creature "who rarely goes to any particular trouble to be polite to anyone."

Second, the black bear serves as a benchmark of [[broken-fang|the cougar]] [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]]'s status. The narration boasts that in his prime, [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] had "seen the [[species-elk|bull elk]] turn from his path" and that "even the old black bear... had been known to speak politely" when they crossed paths (002-0[[TOC|31]]). The black bear's grudging respect is a credential, proof that [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] was once a force to be reckoned with.

Third, the black bear becomes a victim. The wolf pack — directed by the human antagonist [[landy-fargo|Fargo]] — had previously "chased the black bear through the ridges" (013-027). When [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] kills the [[wolf|wolves]] with his rifle, the narrative notes with finality: "No longer would the pack chase the black bear through the ridges. Their strength was broken" (013-027). The black bear's liberation from wolf persecution is one of the novel's [[silence|quiet]] triumphs — a restoration of the [[nature|natural order]] in which the bear belongs to the ridges and the [[wolf|wolves]] to their proper place.

## Named Individuals

**Growl-in-the-throat** — The novel's named black bear, an old male who "grunted and mumbled and soliloquized as he blundered along" (004-005). He is presented as a creature of habit and comfort: a lover of grubs and berries whose world is disrupted by the drought. Despite his formidable size and strength, he is characterized less by [[savagery|ferocity]] than by a kind of gruff amiability — "the most lovable spirit in the forest" (002-0[[TOC|31]]).

## Symbolic/Thematic Role

The black bear represents a third way between predator and prey — a creature [[dominance|power]]ful enough to command respect but not so driven by the "hunting fever" that consumes [[broken-fang|the cougar]] and wolf. He is the novel's image of [[nature|the wild]]erness at peace with itself: a bear grumbling through the ridges, eating grubs and berries, asking nothing of the world but to be left alone.

The bear's suffering during the drought — his grubs dried to flakes, his berries withered — ties him to the novel's broader ecological vision. The drought is not merely a plot device; it is a s[[rabbit|hare]]d affliction that touches every creature, from the smallest gopher to the great bear. The black bear's hunger is the forest's hunger.

The wolf pack's harassment of the bear — and [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s decisive intervention — also carries thematic weight. The [[wolf|wolves]], serving [[landy-fargo|Fargo]]'s corrupt purposes, have upset the natural [[dominance|hierarchy]]. By killing the [[wolf|wolves]], [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] restores the bear to its rightful place: not hunted, not harassed, but [[silence|quiet]]ly sovereign in its own domain.
