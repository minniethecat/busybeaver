# Broken Fang

Broken Fang is the legendary cougar of Smoky Land — a predator of extraordinary size and cunning whose presence haunts the novel from its earliest pages. "A giant cougar, to whom the sparse settlers have given the name of Broken Fang" (002-031) is both a concrete antagonist and a symbol of the untamed wilderness that Hugh Gaylord must confront.

## Overview

The cougar is introduced through a newspaper clipping the Old Colonel shows Hugh: "a giant cougar...doing thousands of dollars' worth of damage to cattle and sheep" and believed to be "the largest of the great cats that has ranged in Idaho for many years" (002-031). He is not merely an animal but a named adversary — "a trophy" (002-032) that draws Hugh into his journey west.

Broken Fang's identity is confirmed by his defining physical trait: "One of the great dog-fangs had been broken sharply off in some stress of years before" (006-038). He bears the marks of a lifetime of combat, and his unusual size testifies to his past prowess: "only an animal that had surpassed in all the wiles of hunting and self-preservation could live to attain such a growth" (007-025).

Bindloss presents Broken Fang through his own point of view in an extended interior chapter (Chapter VII), giving the reader access to the cougar's consciousness — his hunger, his pride, and his slow recognition of his own decline. "He was the great Broken Fang, the monarch of the cougars" (006-039), and the forest world is organized around his supremacy: "Was not the trail cleared, for long distances ahead, of all the lesser hunters?" (006-039).

## Early Reign as Forest Monarch

Before his decline, Broken Fang bestrode Smoky Land as its undisputed sovereign. His kill count over the years was staggering: "an ordinary cougar will kill two deer a week, year on year, until the buzzards come to feed upon him. Broken Fang was no ordinary cougar" (007-024). His prey extended far beyond the usual range of a cougar — "he had felled the horned cattle; in one starving winter he had fought Cry-in-the-night, the wolf, and had mastered him; he had taken the old tusked boar gone wild in the underbrush" (007-025). No creature challenged him: "In his time...he had seen the bull elk turn from his path, and *that* is a sight to pass down to one's cubs" (006-040–041). Even the black bear deferred to him: "Even the old black bear, the honey-grubber...had been known to speak politely when the two of them met on the trail" (006-041).

The hunting ecstasy was his birthright. The novel describes the primal thrill of the predator: "For sheer sensuality there are no creatures on earth to equal the cats — and he was king and monarch of all the breed" (006-040). His hunting cry was not the skulking whimper of lesser predators but "a long, wild, triumphant scream that lesser members of his family were ordinarily afraid to utter, lest it should call their enemies down upon them" (006-041).

## The Arc of Decline

Broken Fang's arc is a tragedy of aging. In his prime, he "had felled the horned cattle; in one starving winter he had fought Cry-in-the-night, the wolf, and had mastered him" (007-025). But the novel catches him in decline: "He was old: his unusual size proved that fact" (007-025).

His hunting prowess fails him. "Never in his long years could he remember a time of darkness when the hunting had been so barren" (007-024). The deer flee before he reaches leaping range; even a porcupine escapes him — a humiliation that carries forest lore significance: "When Quill-back escapes the hunter, the buzzards will be full-fed tomorrow" (007-028).

The novel traces his degeneration with clinical precision. His muscle control, once flawless, now betrays him: "Broken Fang was growing old; some of his marvelous muscle-control was breaking; and no longer could he accomplish a successful stalk" (007-026). His pride refuses to acknowledge the truth: "He flattered himself that not even the wild creatures, dreading or waiting for just this moment, had ears keen enough to hear him...Time was when his stealing feet...fell soft as pine needles on the trail" (006-039). Yet the trail reveals the bitter truth — "his feet had spoken loudly, rather than whispered, on the narrow path" (006-039).

The scent of the sheep flock offers a "new lease of life" (007-031), awakening the "death-feast" instinct — "an orgy of slaying, not for food but from desire, the tearing-out of a hundred throats in so many seconds" (007-032). Yet this is not fulfillment but desperation, the final gambit of a dying king.

## The Stream Confrontation

The first direct confrontation between Broken Fang and Hugh Gaylord comes at the river crossing in Chapter VIII. Desperate with hunger, the cougar stalks a lamb that has been swept into the torrent. The ancient forest law — "Most of all, fear men" (008-006) — wars with his starvation: "He hadn't forgotten that he was the master of the forest, who had felled even the horned steer. What were these slight figures to stand against him? Many of the forest laws he had already broken; he might even yet break the law that forbade the death-feast" (008-007).

Hugh plunges into the rapids and the two adversaries meet at close quarters: "Hugh's head, shoulders and one arm were clear of the water: a few inches distant gleamed the white fangs of the puma" (008-018). The moment is existential: "He was the great Broken Fang; and this creature that rose up from the waters was but a fawn in strength compared to him. The lamb was almost in his grasp. His sinuous tail twitched at the tip" (008-021).

Hugh prevails not by physical force but by asserting "the old elemental superiority of man over the beasts of the field" (008-022). This victory transforms Hugh: "None of his old, soft delights had yielded one part of the pleasure that had been his as his strong strokes braved the current; no false flattery had ever been so satisfying as his victory over Broken Fang" (008-022). The confrontation is the crucible in which Hugh's manhood is forged.

## The Final Hunt in the High Country

By Chapter 23, Broken Fang is "desperate and half-mad with hunger" (023-002), stalking the bighorn sheep in their own high country where he is at a "great disadvantage of fighting in the enemy's country" (023-003). His final hunt demonstrates both the persistence of his cunning and the completeness of his fall.

He stalks Spot and the bighorn band with the strategic precision of a general: "Broken Fang's glaring eyes encompassed the whole scene, and with the swiftness and accuracy of a general, he mapped out his plan of attack" (023-006). The novel pauses to note the mystery of animal intelligence: "No naturalist explains by what swift avenues of intelligence he made his plans. Animals cannot reason. Instinct alone is severely taxed to account for all the wiles and astuteness of the wild creatures" (023-006).

Yet his hunt is thwarted by the smallest creature — a pika, "only a miniature sound, really little more than a high-pitched squeak" (023-011). The irony is deliberate: "He had always had only scorn for these lesser folk — the scurrying gophers, the timid rabbits, and the furry pikas in the rocks. Yet at that instant he was to receive a taste of their might" (023-009). The pika's cry, "just enough, and no more, to put high explosives under the sheep hunt" (023-012), triggers the cougar's hair-trigger nerves and sends his prey scattering. Broken Fang's pursuit drives Spot down into the forest below — a chase that will ultimately lead both predator and prey into the path of the forest fire.

## Death in the Fire

Broken Fang's death comes in Chapter XXX, amid the great forest fire. He has driven Spot and two bighorn ewes into the burning valley, and in his frenzy he abandons his hunting craft: "He knew by the animal's frantic leaps that he was desperate with hunger, stark mad from the long chase that had never seemed to end, and frenzied, perhaps, by the fire. The felines do not often chase their game in open pursuit; but in his madness he had forgotten his hunting cunning" (030-015).

Hugh, Alice, and the shepherd dog Shep stand between the cougar and the flock. The moment distills the novel's central conflict: "Suddenly he seemed to know that in some invisible and secret way that he could not trace, the whole issue of life and death had centered down to his war with Broken Fang" (030-014). Hugh fires six shots from his revolver — the first inflicts a mortal wound, yet "the shocking power of the lead was not enough to destroy wholly the mighty engine of life in Broken Fang's body" (030-017). The cougar charges again and again, brought down only by the sixth and final bullet through the brain.

The narrator pronounces his epitaph: "Broken Fang's trail of rapine and carnage had come to an end at last. He typified all that was most deadly and terrible in the wilderness, and he had fallen in fair battle with the breed whose strength — in such regions where they venture — has conquered the wilderness. He was a forest monarch; but his foe was the shepherd. Talon and fang and supple strength had not been availing" (030-018).

His death is the key that unlocks the flock's salvation. Spot, freed from his pursuer, leads the three thousand sheep through a hidden canyon — "the Little People show the way" (031-001) — to safety beyond the fire.

## Thematic Significance

Broken Fang represents the old order of the wilderness — magnificent, terrible, and dying. His aging parallels the broader transformation of Smoky Land from untamed frontier to managed range. He is not evil but elemental: a force of nature that the shepherd must learn to face and, ultimately, outlast.

His death scene carries explicit symbolic weight. The narrator frames it as the conquest of wilderness by civilization: "He typified all that was most deadly and terrible in the wilderness, and he had fallen in fair battle with the breed whose strength — in such regions where they venture — has conquered the wilderness" (030-018). Yet this victory is rendered with ambivalence. Broken Fang is not a villain but a monarch — his defeat is necessary, but it is also a diminishment of the world.

The cougar also serves as the instrument of Hugh's moral transformation. Hugh's first victory over Broken Fang at the stream teaches him the joy of service: "It was service, it was conquest, it was manhood at last" (008-022). His final victory in the forest fire completes the arc, demonstrating that the shepherd's role is not merely to tend the flock but to face and defeat the primal forces that threaten it.

The novel does not grant him a heroic death. Instead, he falls to the shepherd's revolver, his broken fang — the mark of a warrior past his prime — a symbol of the inevitable decline that comes for all predators, human and animal alike. The forest fire that claims his life also clears the way for Spot to lead the flock to safety, suggesting that the destruction of the old wild order is the precondition for new forms of life and guardianship.

## Relationships

Broken Fang's primary relationship is with Hugh Gaylord — the hunter who comes to Smoky Land specifically to claim him as a trophy. They are adversaries in the novel's central man-versus-nature conflict, meeting twice directly: at the stream in Chapter VIII and at the forest fire in Chapter XXX. The cougar exists as much as a psychological presence as a physical one — "Hugh could imagine the tawny cougar, Broken Fang, whom he had come to slay" (004-006).

The Old Colonel serves as Broken Fang's human instigator, recognizing that "he would be a trophy" (002-032) worth pursuing — the prize that sets Hugh's journey in motion.

Broken Fang's relationship with the bighorn sheep — particularly Spot — defines the novel's predator-prey dynamics. He drives Spot from the high country down into the forest, unwittingly setting in motion the chain of events that leads to his own death and the flock's salvation. The chase culminates when Spot, pursued by the cougar, returns to the domestic flock and leads them through the fire — "It was Broken Fang, the monarch of the cougars, and he had simply driven the three bighorn down into his own hunting ground at last" (030-013).

He also has a historical relationship with Cry-in-the-night, the wolf, whom he "had fought...and had mastered him" (007-025) in a starving winter — a testament to his former supreme dominance of the Smoky Land food chain.

The shepherd dog Shep, while never confronting Broken Fang directly in combat before the final scene, is the cougar's antithesis — where Broken Fang represents predation for its own sake, Shep embodies fidelity and guardianship. In the death scene, Shep charges to Hugh's aid as the wounded cougar makes his final approach (030-017).
