# Running Feet

Running Feet is the named coyote of *Shepherds of the Wild* — a predator whose cowardice and cunning are framed by the novel's Native American cosmology. He is both a real animal (a persistent threat to the sheep flock) and a symbolic figure: the embodiment of the curse that Manitou laid upon the coyote people in the young days of the earth. Among the novel's predators, Running Feet occupies a distinct niche — not the regal power of the cougar nor the pack ferocity of the wolf, but the desperate intelligence of the underdog, surviving on wit where strength fails.

## Overview

Running Feet is introduced through Pete's Native idiom. When Hugh struggles to understand the threat to the sheep, Pete explains: "You don't know Running Feet — what he can do in one night" (005-022). Hugh intuits that "Running Feet, past all doubt, referred to some of the predatory animals that habitually preyed on the sheep" (005-023), and in this moment of comprehension, he begins to enter the wilderness worldview.

The coyote's curse is explained in mythological terms: "No one knows just what their offense was — whether they were once dogs who betrayed men, or the fathers of dogs that betrayed the wild by selling their sons into the bondage of men — but not even a tenderfoot can doubt the severity of their punishment and the depths of their remorse" (009-004). Running Feet's nightly howl — "a long wail broken by half-sobs — infinitely despairing and sad" (009-004) — expresses an ancient grief. The novel draws on Native American myth to elevate the coyote from mere pest to tragic figure, a creature whose very intelligence becomes the instrument of its torment.

Running Feet's dual identity — both "the coyote" as generic threat and "Running Feet" as Pete's named individual — reflects the novel's broader technique of personifying wild creatures without domesticating them. Pete names him as the mountain men name the peaks and passes, recognizing individual identity within the wild order.

## Character Arc

Running Feet's most developed sequence comes in Chapter 10, where he attempts to stalk a lamb from the flock. His emergence from the thicket establishes his method: "Running Feet, the coyote, emerging from the brushy thickets of the hillside in late afternoon" (010-012). The chapter offers an extended meditation on coyote psychology:

> His second name was Coward — cowardice was part of the curse that Manitou had put upon him — but even a seasonal fawn would be brave enough to attack a sheep. (010-013)

His intelligence is both his gift and his torment. "Part of his curse was a far-reaching and calculating intelligence almost equal to that of a dog, a trait that would have been a tremendous advantage if he had been blessed with courage to go with it, but which to a coward meant only realization of a thousand dangers to make life a torment" (010-014). He knows exactly how dangerous the shepherd dog is, how deadly the herder's rifle — and this knowledge paralyzes as much as it protects. "Not even Broken Fang, the puma, can always protect himself against the onslaught of the Barking One on guard over the flocks" (010-014). And "Running Feet knew quite well the deadly qualities of the glittering stick that the herder carried in the hollow of his arm" (010-015).

The entire stalk is a masterwork of coyote cunning compromised by coyote cowardice. The terrain favors him: "The brush went clear to the base of the rocks; one or two little leaps into the open would put him within killing range" (010-017). On this particular day, "the wind was right, the shadows were long and strange, and even Running Feet the coward could see no chance of failure" (010-018). When he makes his stalk — "a gray shadow few wilderness eyes were keen enough to see" (010-018) — his white fangs gleam and "the blood leaped in his veins."

But Spot, the young ram, confounds every expectation. Rather than fleeing in panic, Spot "snapped about in the trail, making no attempt whatever to flee" and "lowered his head in a posture of defense" (010-020). The coyote's blood turns to milk:

> All at once it came to him that he had made a mistake. There was something familiar about the sturdy figure, the lowered head, the curling horns. He remembered certain passes in the High Rockies — and various trim, horned creatures that might occasionally be met there. Even Broken Fang did not care to meet these people on a narrow trail. (010-022)

Running Feet's flight — "he turned and fled at the fastest pace he knew" (010-023) — is the logical conclusion to a life governed by fear. Even the near-miss of Hugh's bullet, which "whizzed hot along Running Feet's shoulder" (010-023), is barely noticed in the terror of the retreat.

## The Coyote's Worldview

Running Feet embodies a specific philosophy of survival that the novel explores in depth. Unlike the great predators who kill through power, the coyote survives through what the narrative calls "a far-reaching and calculating intelligence" that is, paradoxically, "almost equal to that of a dog" (010-014). The comparison to the dog — the coyote's domesticated cousin, now the shepherd's ally — is pointed. Running Feet's intelligence is the intelligence of the dog turned to predation rather than service, and the novel suggests that this is precisely the tragedy of the coyote's existence.

His cowardice is not mere personality but existential condition. "Part of his curse was a far-reaching and calculating intelligence" that in a courageous creature "would have been a tremendous advantage" but which "to a coward meant only realization of a thousand dangers to make life a torment" (010-014). The coyote understands exactly how dangerous the world is; this understanding, rather than empowering him, merely multiplies his fear.

The novel also notes Running Feet's place in the predator hierarchy. He is introduced as "a coyote — most despised of hunters" (001-007), and a family legend records how "a particularly bold forefather had seized a cow elk by the leg" and was dissected "beneath the front hoofs of the bull" (001-007). Running Feet has no desire to create new legends on his own account. He survives at the margins of the predator world, scavenging when he can, hunting when the opportunity seems safe, and fleeing at the first sign of resistance.

## Relationships

Running Feet exists in a web of predator and prey relationships. He fears the shepherd dog, whose onslaught even "Broken Fang, the puma, cannot always protect himself against" (010-014). He recognizes the deadly "glittering stick" — Hugh's rifle — and navigates every stalk with the hypervigilance of the hunted predator.

His relationship to Spot is the dramatic center of his arc. Spot defies every coyote expectation of sheep behavior. When the ram stands defiant on the rocky promontory — "a watchful sentinel for any danger that might threaten" (010-010) — he occupies a position "such a place as the wolf chooses for her lair" (010-008), a place Running Feet "found a convenient place to look for game" (010-008). Spot has claimed the predator's own ground, and Running Feet cannot comprehend this inversion.

His relationship to the larger predators — cougar, wolf — is one of inferiority and opportunistic scavenging. He is "most despised of hunters" (001-007) — the bottom of the predator hierarchy, surviving on cunning rather than strength.

## Thematic Significance

Running Feet represents the cowardice that is also intelligence. Unlike the cougar, who rules through sheer power, the coyote survives through wit and caution. His curse — to be smart enough to understand every danger but too afraid to face any — is a tragic version of the novel's broader theme about the relationship between knowledge and action. He is the inversion of the shepherd dog: where Shep's knowledge of danger produces courage, Running Feet's equivalent intelligence produces only fear.

His mythological dimension also introduces the Native American cosmology that underlies the novel's wilderness philosophy. Manitou's curse on the coyote is a story about betrayal, punishment, and the loss of courage — a fall narrative that parallels the human story of civilization's estrangement from the wild. The coyote is what the dog would be without its covenant with man: intelligent but purposeless, knowing but impotent.

The encounter with Spot dramatizes the novel's thesis that the wild is not simply a hierarchy of predator and prey. Spot, the sheep, defeats Running Feet, the predator, not through physical power but through a defiant posture that the coyote's cowardice cannot overcome. The tableau on the rocks — "the young ram with lowered head, the coyote — his blood turning to milk inside of him — on the trail" (010-021) — is a visual emblem of the novel's moral economy, where courage can reverse the expected order of predation.
