# Hunting

## Definition

Hunting in *Shepherds of the Wild* operates on two interconnected levels. At the surface, it is the literal predation that drives the wilderness ecosystem — the cougar stalking the lamb, the wolf pack closing on the deer, the coyote circling the sheep camp. But hunting also serves as the novel's central metaphor for human behavior: the cattlemen "hunt" the sheepmen, Fargo "hunts" the Crowson flock, and ultimately every character is either hunter or hunted. The boundary between the two states is fluid — Hugh arrives as a hunter of cougars but becomes a guardian hunted by men.

## Manifestations

The novel opens with the "hunting hour" — dusk, when every predator in the forest stirs to life: "It was the hunting hour, and Hugh could imagine the tawny cougar, Broken Fang, whom he had come to slay, responding in the same way" (005-031). Broken Fang, the puma, is the novel's archetypal hunter — patient, lethal, driven by an ancient instinct that predates human presence. But hunting takes many forms: the sheep-herder hunts with his rifle, Fargo hunts with his pack of bear dogs (029-031), and even the lynx stalks the mountain grouse from its perch (012-031). The hunt is the wilderness's fundamental rhythm.

## Key Characters/Events

**Broken Fang** the cougar embodies the pure, amoral hunt — killing because killing is what predators do (005-031). **Landy Fargo** represents the degenerate form of hunting — using the tools of predation not for survival but for economic domination and cruelty. His decision to unleash a pack of bear dogs on the sheep transforms the natural hunt into something perverse (029-031). **Hugh Gaylord** begins as a hunter — he came to Smoky Land to kill a cougar — but his transformation turns him from predator to protector. His climactic fight with Broken Fang at the river (020-031) is both a literal hunt and the moment when the hunter becomes the hunted.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel treats hunting as an existential condition rather than merely an activity. Every creature in Smoky Land is either hunting or being hunted, and the moral distinction lies not in whether one hunts but in why and how. The wild predators hunt from necessity; the shepherds guard from duty; Fargo's men hunt from greed. The novel suggests that hunting reveals character: "The hunting fever spares none of the flesh-eaters, and from the smallest to the great — from the little, deadly, white-fanged mink following a rabbit's trail beside the river to the mighty grizzly, stalking a cow elk in the thicket — they felt in their veins the age-old stir that is ever new" (012-031). This universalizing of the hunt places humans and animals on the same moral plane — both are subject to the same ancient imperatives.
