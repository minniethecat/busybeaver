# Predator

The predators of Smoky Land — the [[cougar|puma]], the [[wolf]], the [[coyote]], the [[grizzly]] — are more than characters in *Shepherds of the Wild*; they are the living expression of the wilderness's fundamental truth: that life feeds on life. They "lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep" (007-003), and their presence defines the [[shepherd|shepherd's]] role. To guard the flock is, above all, to stand between the predators and their prey.

## Definition

In Marshall's taxonomy, a predator is any creature that hunts and kills for food — but the novel's interest extends beyond biology. Predators are the agents of the [[wilderness]] order. "Running Feet, past all doubt, referred to some of the predatory animals that habitually preyed on the sheep" (005-023): this moment of comprehension, when [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] first grasps the native idiom for predation, marks his entry into the wilderness worldview.

The predator is never merely an animal in this novel. It is a symbol, a force, a test. The shepherd measures his [[courage]] by his willingness to face the predators. The flock measures its safety by the shepherd's vigilance. And the predators themselves — particularly [[cougar|Broken Fang]] — are rendered with a dignity that denies simple villainy.

> "The beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep." (007-003)

## Manifestations

**The Great Hunters**: The novel gives us a hierarchy of predators. At its apex stands [[cougar|Broken Fang]], "the great tawny king of the pumas" (018-007), whose hunting is a craft of "noiseless stalk" and explosive violence. The [[wolf]] pack hunts with collective intelligence, their winter songs filling the forest. The [[coyote]] — "most despised of hunters" (001-006) — occupies the lowest tier, a scavenger as much as a killer.

**The Predator's Worldview**: Marshall grants his predators interiority. Broken Fang's perspective on the drought reveals a world ordered entirely by the hunting calculus: "Their whole hunting success depended on a noiseless stalk upon a breed of creatures with ears sharp enough to hear the predatory beetles utter their kill screams in the air" (018-007). The predator lives or dies by the acuity of its senses.

**The Shepherd's Adversary**: For [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]], predators are the problem he exists to solve. "It seemed to him also that the wolves and other predatory animals had every opportunity in the world to lurk about the flanks of the flock and steal the lambs" (009-027). This is precisely correct — and it is why the shepherd must remain ever watchful.

**Predator as Prey**: The novel does not exempt predators from the cycle of death. Broken Fang himself becomes prey — not to another animal but to the ultimate predator, man. "He lay as if fallen in battle against the flocks — a token of man's dominance of the wild" (030-036). The predator's death is a reminder that in the wilderness, no creature is permanently safe.

The predators are not antagonists in any simple sense. Broken Fang, in particular, is rendered with something approaching admiration. His death is not a victory to be celebrated but a loss to be acknowledged — "a token of man's dominance," yes, but dominance is not the same as triumph.

## Philosophical Significance

The predator is the novel's most direct challenge to sentimental views of nature. The wilderness is not a garden; it is a hunting ground. "All night long the ancient war of the wilderness waged on, and many were its casualties" (008-008). To accept the wilderness is to accept predation — not to celebrate it, but to recognize it as the engine of the wild order.

The shepherd's relationship to predators is thus inherently tragic. He must kill them to protect the flock, but he cannot hate them. They are not evil; they are simply doing what the wilderness requires. The novel's treatment of Broken Fang's death makes this clear: it is a necessary act, not a righteous one. "Man's dominance" is asserted, but the triumph is hollow.
