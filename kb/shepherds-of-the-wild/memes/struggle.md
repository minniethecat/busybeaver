# Struggle

## Definition

Struggle in *Shepherds of the Wild* is not an occasional event but the baseline condition of life. Every creature in Smoky Land is engaged in a continuous struggle: the sheep struggle against predators, the herder struggles against human enemies and the elements, the predators themselves struggle against starvation and competition. The novel treats struggle not as a tragic circumstance but as the medium through which character is forged and meaning is created.

## Manifestations

Struggle takes many forms in the novel: Hugh's physical struggle against the wilderness — the rough trail, the freezing river, the forest fire; his moral struggle over whether to risk his life for a lamb; Alice's struggle to maintain her flock against Fargo's campaign of intimidation; Shep the dog's struggle to keep watch despite his wounds. The novel does not distinguish between these forms of struggle; all are expressions of the same fundamental law — that life in the wild is earned, not given.

## Key Characters/Events

**Hugh Gaylord** embodies the transformative power of struggle. He arrives soft and untested; through struggle, he discovers capacities he didn't know he possessed. **Shep** represents struggle in its purest form — the wounded dog who will not abandon his post, fighting to his last breath. **Alice Crowson** demonstrates that struggle can be waged with intelligence and composure, not just brute force.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel argues, through its depiction of struggle, that ease is the enemy of character. Hugh's city life was easy and meaningless; his wilderness life is hard and purposeful. Struggle clarifies values: when everything is at stake, trivial concerns fall away and essential commitments come into focus. The novel's vision is not masochistic — it does not celebrate suffering for its own sake — but it insists that a life without struggle is a life without definition, a life in which nothing is truly chosen because nothing is truly risked.
