## Definition

The sun in Smoky Land is a force of extremes — a "curse in summer" when its heat parches the high plateau, and a welcome presence when its light dapples through the pines at the drinking hour (027-001). The novel uses the sun's daily arc to mark the rhythm of wilderness life, from the "late afternoon sunlight" that signals the approach of night to the sunrise that finds Hugh Gaylord walking behind his flocks in silent vigil. The sun is both a harsh master and a source of transcendent beauty, governing the daily cycle of every creature on the high ranges.

## Manifestations

The sun governs the daily cycle of Smoky Land. In the late afternoon, "sunlight, shot and dappled by the shadows of the leaves" falls over the bull elk's body, and Spread Horn senses the approach of the drinking hour (041-001). At sunset, the twilight falls, "the tall pines seemed to darken" above them, and "the immeasurable silence of the mountain night" deepens (187-001). The novel describes the land itself as one "where great glaciers gleamed in the morning light and snow fields lay unchanging above the line where the timber became stunted and died" (027-001).

The morning sun brings a different quality — revelation and homecoming. Hugh, walking behind the flocks "in silent vigil as the sun climbed up over the far, white peaks," experiences a sense of having "returned, after long and futile wanderings, to his own home" (727-001). The sunrise becomes associated with the ancestral pull of the soil: the Anglo-Saxon, the Old Colonel had told him, "was never one to gather into cities," and when his land became too heavy with people — "so that he could no longer see the sun breaking forth in glory to the east because of city spires that rose between — he was likely to set sail for the far places of the earth" (731-001).

The sun also marks the wild creatures' deepest longings. The bighorn lamb Spot dreams of "the glory of the sun from the topmost spire of the highest peak" and "the setting sun would throw its red glow over him" — dreams that express an ancestral memory of the high country his domestic life has denied him (1489-001, 1497-001). The old bighorn who leads the flock has himself "stood on the high pinnacles and let the last red glow of the sun fall over him" (1519-001).

## Key Characters

**Spread Horn** feels the sun's signal — the late afternoon light that tells him it is the drinking hour and draws him to the spring (041-001).

**Hugh Gaylord** experiences the sunrise as a moment of revelation: walking behind the sheep as the sun climbs over the white peaks, he feels a profound connection to the land that he had never known in the city, where "the smoke that ever hung over his city mostly obscured" the stars (321-001). The sun becomes a marker of his transformation from urban alienation to wilderness belonging. His repatriation to the soil — the ancestral Anglo-Saxon life of watching "the sun breaking forth in glory to the east" — is the fulfillment of a racial memory the city had suppressed (731-001).

**Spot**, the bighorn lamb, embodies the sun's role in wild longing. His dreams of "the glory of the sun from the topmost spire" are not mere fantasy but ancestral memory — the legacy of a species that once ranged the high peaks. The sun in his dreams represents the freedom and elevation his domestic life cannot provide (1489-001, 1497-001).

## Philosophical Significance

The sun in the novel represents both the harshness and the redemptive beauty of the natural order. As a "curse in summer," it is one of the forces that make Smoky Land "not a land for gentlefolk" — part of the region's fundamental inhospitality to soft living (027-001). Yet the same sun, at sunrise and sunset, provides the novel's most transcendent moments: the drinking hour, the homecoming, the ancestral memory of a people wedded to the soil.

The sun also serves as the novel's most consistent marker of authentic versus inauthentic life. The Old Colonel's diagnosis of Anglo-Saxon destiny — to leave the cities "when he could no longer see the sun breaking forth in glory to the east because of city spires" — makes the sun's visibility the test of a life properly lived (731-001). Hugh's ability to see the sunrise over the white peaks, after a city life where smoke obscured the stars, is the novel's simplest measure of his transformation.

## Key Passages

> It was not a land for gentlefolk. It was a hard, grim place, a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter, where great glaciers gleamed in the morning light and snow fields lay unchanging above the line where the timber became stunted and died. (027-001)

> The late afternoon sunlight, shot and dappled by the shadows of the leaves, fell over the bull elk's body; and the animal sensed the approach of night. It was the drinking hour. (041-001)

> It seemed wholly natural that he should be walking here — behind the flocks — in silent vigil as the sun climbed up over the far, white peaks. He had a sense of having returned, after long and futile wanderings, to his own home. (727-001)

> When his land became too heavy with people — so that he could no longer see the sun breaking forth in glory to the east because of city spires that rose between — he was likely to set sail for the far places of the earth, still to watch the sheep as they fed at the edge of the forest. (731-001)
