# Twilight

Twilight in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the hour of revelation. As darkness gathers over Smoky Land, the artificial distinctions of civilization fade, and Hugh Gaylord begins to see the world — and himself — with a clarity he has never known. It is the threshold between the known and the unknown, the civilized and the wild.

## Definition

In the novel's temporal landscape, twilight is the liminal hour when the day's certainties dissolve and the ancient order of the wilderness reasserts itself. "The twilight was falling over Smoky Land" (003-021) signals the beginning of Hugh's transformation. It is in "this deepening twilight" that "Gaylord saw more clearly than any time since his arrival at Smoky Land" (004-004). Twilight is not merely a time of day but a state of perception — the hour when the veil between civilization and reality grows thin.

## Manifestations

Twilight appears at pivotal moments throughout the novel. Hugh's first night at the sheep camp begins in twilight: "As usual in the twilight hour, the faintest breath of wind came slipping, light as a deer-tread itself, from the further mountains" (003-024). The Colonel's melancholy gaze "out over the twilight grounds" (002-026) foreshadows Hugh's journey. The recurring image of "the firelight of the herder still glows in the twilight" (004-022) connects Hugh to every shepherd since antiquity. "Even the dim twilight and the glow of the dying fire seemed something vividly remembered from long ago" (005-003).

## Key Characters/Events

Hugh Gaylord's transformation is bookmarked by twilight. His first evening in Smoky Land is a twilight epiphany. The Colonel's pivotal conversation with Hugh occurs against a twilight backdrop. The herder's fire — the human answer to the wilderness night — is always first lit in twilight. The predators begin their stir in twilight, and the sheep are gathered close.

## Philosophical Significance

Twilight represents the threshold between two worlds: the civilized world Hugh has left behind and the wilderness reality he is entering. It is the hour of truth, when "the strong profiles of the pines dimmed and blurred, the distant peaks receded — with a curious effect of actual motion — into the further darkness" (005-003). In twilight, Hugh confronts the eternal pattern — the herder, the fire, the flock, the predators — that has repeated since "the nomad herders of Asia first led their flocks into the West" (004-022).

## Twilight and the Predator's Stir

Twilight in the novel is not only the hour of human revelation but the hour when the hunting creatures begin to move. "In that momentous twilight that brought Hugh Gaylord to the sheep camp for the first time, there was unfamiliar traffic on the brown, pine-needle trail that wound down to the meadows from the darkening forest" (013-002). This traffic is the stirring of predators and prey alike, the shift change between the diurnal and nocturnal worlds. The coyotes' twilight cries carry "the ancient sadness of existence, the old complaint of the pain of living with only Fear and Death at the end" (009-005). The novel describes "the dim, sinuous movements of the hunters' bodies" in the twilight, when they "could hardly be distinguished from the wavering shadows" (009-003). The predatory world comes alive in twilight, and the herder's fire becomes a necessity — not a comfort — against what prowls in the falling dark. This duality — twilight as both epiphany and danger — captures the novel's core tension: the wilderness is beautiful precisely because it is deadly.
