# Season

The seasons in *Shepherds of the Wild* are not measured by calendars but by the living world itself. The novel presents a wilderness where time is read in the ripening of huckleberries, the changing color of pines, and the hardening of elk antlers — a natural timekeeping system that "would have been mostly unintelligible to men" (001-002).

## Definition

In Smoky Land, the seasons are a language spoken by the land. The novel opens in July, when "signs of July were everywhere: signs that were large print and clear to the eyes of the wild creatures" (001-002). The pines serve as "a wonderful calendar in themselves" (001-002), shifting from "a deep, rich green to a strange dusky blue" (001-002). The fledglings "were just the July size — still soft with the fat of squabhood but yet big enough to make a pleasing lump in the stomach" (001-002). The season is not an abstraction but a physical reality that governs every creature's behavior.

## Summer and Winter

The novel contrasts the two extreme seasons of Smoky Land. It is "a hard, grim place, a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter" (001-009). Summer brings not only heat but fire — "at the end of the dry summer, when forest fires ranged here and there through the high ridges" (001-008) — and drought: the "arid season" when "the little underground folk, such as the digger squirrels and gophers, didn't particularly care" (023-005) but the larger creatures suffered. Winter brings the wolf pack, which "would not have their pack strength until the winter" (019-013); "the wolf pack in the snow, she knew perfectly, was not a thing to trifle with" (019-013).

The shepherd endures both extremes: "the blasting heat of summer and the bitter winter cold" (007-005). The seasons shape not only physical survival but the entire rhythm of wilderness life — mating, migration, hunger, and death.

## The Salmon's Calendar

The most precise seasonal marker in the novel is the salmon's migration. The salmon return on their "fourth-year migration to the waters in which they were born" (001-013), a cycle so exact it is measured "almost to the day" (001-013):

> They come to spawn in the waters where they were themselves spawned before they go down to the sea, and after they spawn they die. To the naturalist there are strange significances in this repatriation of the salmon. There is a sense of curious relationships — for strong men, too, always try to return to their homeland for their last days. (001-013)

This seasonal return — "up Silver Creek came the salmon, returning to their home waters for their last days" (012-012) — becomes a metaphor for Hugh's own repatriation to the wild. Just as the salmon carries "a vision of their Lost Land to which they would return to die" (012-012), Hugh finds in Smoky Land a homecoming measured not in calendar months but in the deeper seasons of the spirit.
