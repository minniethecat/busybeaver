# Pete

Pete is the wilderness guide who introduces Hugh Gaylord to the realities of Smoky Land. "Pete was a wilderness man" (005-007) — the definitive statement of his identity. He is not a romantic figure but a practical one, shaped by years of surviving in a harsh environment.

## Overview

Pete arrives as Hugh's hired guide but quickly reveals himself as much more — a representative of the life Hugh is about to enter. "Pete had good blood in him, the blood of as brave and hardy a race as ever lived" (003-013). He is a storyteller, known for embellishing tales — "Pete was given to telling lies" (004-014) — but his exaggerations are the harmless kind of a man who has seen remarkable things and knows how to make them memorable.

Pete is a half-breed guide, and "Pete the guide had an exceedingly good command of English for a half-breed" (004-026). Yet in moments of intense emotion, "the use of the language fell away from him, and his only utterance was an exclamation in his own almost-forgotten tongue" (004-026). His identity straddles two worlds — the vanishing culture of his people and the practical reality of the frontier — and neither fully claims him.

"Pete was a wilderness man, and if there is one lesson that the wilderness teaches, it is the finality and the inevitability of death" (005-007). This philosophical observation — filtered through Pete's experience — establishes him as the novel's voice of wilderness wisdom.

Pete possesses an almost preternatural awareness of his surroundings. When Hugh cannot hear anything in the forest silence, "the truth was that Pete would have found considerable difficulty in telling just how he knew. Rather it was a sixth sense, an essence in the air that blunter senses could not have perceived" (004-008). This attunement to the wilderness stands in stark contrast to Hugh's urban blindness and marks Pete as the novel's representative of older, deeper modes of knowledge.

As a guide, Pete is "inordinately lazy and a wretched guide" (003-020), spending his time in camp drinking Hugh's whisky rather than leading hunts. He "had found it much more convenient to stay in camp and drink Hugh's whisky" (003-013), revealing the corruption that alcohol — and white contact — has brought to his people. Yet despite these flaws, his wilderness competence is never in question; he is a broken vessel, but the substance within is genuine.

## Character Arc

Pete's role is largely static — he is the fixed point against which Hugh's growth is measured. He does not change; Hugh changes in relation to him. Pete's pragmatic acceptance of wilderness realities (death, danger, hardship) provides the baseline that Hugh must first understand and then transcend.

When crisis comes, Pete's response is characteristically blunt: "Big fight — over the range. There's nothing we can do" (005-014). His fatalism is both a strength (he does not waste energy on impossible fights) and a limitation (he would not attempt what Hugh attempts).

A pivotal moment in their relationship occurs over whisky. When Pete finds two bottles in the dead herder's tent and cries "Fire water" (006-012), Hugh orders him to put them back. Pete grows sullen, resisting: "I don't put 'em back. He — won't need 'em now" (006-014). Hugh invokes the "national law against giving whisky to Indians" (006-015) and threatens to fire him. The Indian's "sullenness gave way to surprise; then — to some measure, at least — to respect" (006-016). This confrontation marks the first time Hugh asserts genuine authority over Pete, and it is one of the earliest signs of Hugh's own transformation from soft urbanite to a man capable of command.

When Hugh suggests Pete could stay and take a permanent job as a sheep herder, Pete's response reveals the social hierarchy of the West: "Me no sheep herder. Only dagoes and Mexicans sheep herders. I'm a guide" (006-020). His pride in his profession — and his disdain for the degraded work of sheep herding — shows that even a half-breed guide considers himself above certain stations in the frontier's racial and occupational order.

Pete departs the narrative at dawn of the day after the murder discovery, carrying the dead herder's body toward the settlements. His final exchange with Hugh is a refusal to stay: "You don't know cattlemen — Landy Fargo — José Mertos — Besides — Pete got other work to do" (007-013). With these words, Pete exits — his practical utility exhausted, his role as catalyst for Hugh's journey complete.

## Physical Description and Skills

Pete possesses the physical characteristics of a wilderness man. He walks with the "bent-kneed, shuffling walk, shoulders sagging and feet falling lightly" (003-020) that enables him to cover "four miles in every hour through the long mountain day without fatigue" (003-020). His "swarthy face" and dark eyes (005-005) mark his Indian heritage, and by the novel's end Hugh has been so transformed by wilderness life that his skin is "almost as dark as those of Pete, his late guide" (018-010).

His tracking skills are exceptional. He identifies that the shepherd dog has been "creased" — "bullet took away a little skin" (004-028) — by touch alone in the darkness. He spots a horse grazing in the forest that Hugh's untrained eyes cannot see, teasing: "Eyes maybe half blind. Horse grazing just inside the forest, just outside meadow" (005-020). These abilities make Pete a bridge between the civilized and wild worlds, though one more at home in the latter.

## Relationships

With Hugh, Pete occupies the role of reluctant mentor. He mocks Hugh's inexperience but also recognizes his potential. Their dynamic evolves from guide-tenderfoot to near-equals, though Pete never fully abandons his sardonic posture. The whisky confrontation marks a turning point — Hugh's first exercise of moral authority over his guide.

Pete's knowledge of the local figures — "Landy Fargo — José Mertos" (007-013) — makes him an essential source of intelligence about the threats facing the shepherds. He understands the cattlemen's power and the peril of opposing them, and his warning proves prescient.

Pete is also defined by what he will *not* do. He refuses to stay as a herder, partly from professional pride and partly from fear: "Herder shot — me shot next" (007-011). His self-preservation instinct contrasts with Hugh's emerging willingness to sacrifice himself for the flock.

In the wider narrative, Pete serves as a benchmark. Alice Crowson initially assumes Hugh is merely "a humble white man, friend of Pete's" (011-015), a laborer — demonstrating how Pete's social position conditions others' perception of those associated with him.

## Thematic Significance

Pete represents the unromanticized wilderness man — competent, fatalistic, and fully adapted to his environment. He is what Hugh might become if he stayed in the mountains long enough: weathered, wise, and slightly worn. But Pete also represents the ceiling of mere survival — he has adapted to the wilderness without transcending it, and this distinction marks Hugh's different destiny.

Pete embodies several key tensions in the novel's thematic architecture:

- **Primitive vs. Civilized**: Pete's "sixth sense" and instinctual knowledge contrast with Hugh's book-learned ignorance, yet Pete is also degenerate — a man whose race and culture have been eroded by the same civilization Hugh represents.
- **Fatalism vs. Agency**: Pete accepts the wilderness's dictates ("There's nothing we can do"); Hugh learns to resist them. Pete represents the old order of passive acceptance; Hugh inaugurates the new order of active guardianship.
- **Service vs. Self-Interest**: Pete's work is transactional — he guides for pay and drinks on the job. Hugh discovers the joy of service for its own sake. Pete is a mirror in which Hugh sees what he might become, and recoils.
- **Racial Hierarchy**: Pete's half-breed status and his disdain for "dagoes and Mexicans" reveal the complex ethnic stratification of the frontier West, where even a marginalized figure participates in hierarchies of his own.

The narrator's early observation that "degeneracy was upon him and his people" (003-013) is crucial — Pete is not an idealized noble savage but a tragically diminished figure, his potential blunted by alcohol and the corrosive effects of contact with white civilization. He is both a casualty and a survivor, and his presence in the novel complicates any simple celebration of wilderness life.
