# Wind

The wind in *Shepherds of the Wild* is more than weather — it is the carrier of scent-messages, the sculptor of the Smoky Land landscape, and the force that gives the region its name. Every wild creature lives by the wind, and the novel's opening scene establishes wind-reading as the first law of wilderness awareness. To understand the wind is to understand the wilderness itself; to misread it is to court death.

## Definition

In the novel's world, the wind is the primary medium of information. "Spread Horn made it his business to walk into the face of the wind just as much as possible, and in that way he was aware of all living creatures in the foreground before they were aware of him" (001-005). To walk down the wind is fatal: "To walk down the wind, all creatures know, is to announce one's self as clearly as to wear a bell around the neck" (001-005). The wind is both ally and enemy, depending on one's position relative to it. This binary — upwind safety, downwind exposure — is the novel's most fundamental survival principle, and it governs the behavior of every creature from the bull elk to the human herder.

The wind's role as messenger extends beyond scent. It carries sound, temperature, and the intangible quality of the wilderness itself. When "the faintest breath of wind came slipping, light as a deer-tread itself, from the further mountains" (003-024), it brings with it not just information but atmosphere — the whole mood of the high peaks descending into the valleys.

## Manifestations

The wind shapes Smoky Land itself. The region's name derives from the smoke-filled winds of late summer: "Because of the structure of the passes the winds were likely to fill the region with pale, blue smoke" (001-008). In winter, the winds become a weapon: "a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter" (001-009). The wind is the region's signature — it names the land, sculpts its ecology, and defines the terms of existence for every living thing within it.

The wind carries messages across vast distances with uncanny precision. "The wind had brought him a message, unmistakably as wireless telephones bring messages of approaching foes across a battle field" (001-016). This technological metaphor — the wind as wireless telephone — underscores the wind's role as the wilderness's communication network. Every creature is a receiver; the wind is the broadcast medium. Those who cannot tune in are deaf in a world where deafness is fatal.

### The Wind and the Hunt

For the predators, the wind is the difference between a successful stalk and a humiliating failure. Broken Fang's hunt succeeds when "the wind had been right, the thickets had hidden his advance, the magic and the thrill of the hunting hour had been upon him" (007-026). But his aging body betrays him: "Broken Fang was growing old; some of his marvelous muscle-control was breaking; and no longer could he accomplish a successful stalk" (007-026). When the wind shifts, even the greatest hunter is reduced to hunger.

The cougar's desperation is measured by the wind: "just before dawn — the blood leaped in his veins anew when he caught the smell of the sheep flock on the wind" (007-025). The wind delivers the scent of prey and with it the promise of survival. For the predator, the wind is appetite itself — the medium through which hunger becomes action.

### The Wind and the Shepherd

Hugh's education in wind-reading is central to his transformation. As a city man, he was oblivious to the wind's messages; as a shepherd, he learns to lift his face to every breeze and read its contents. The shepherd dog, by contrast, is a native speaker of the wind's language. Spot, too, stands "alert, head up, nostrils open to receive any message that the wind might bring" (013-006), a sentinel whose vigilance is wind-powered.

The wind also carries the novel's most intimate moments. "As the stars emerged and the little mountain wind sprang up and crept forth in its never-ending explorations of the thickets, the shepherdess explained all things to Hugh's satisfaction" (017-005). The wind is the background music of Hugh's courtship of Alice — the invisible presence that witnesses their growing bond.

### The Forest Fire and the Wind

In the novel's climax, the wind becomes an agent of destruction. "The way of the forest fire is the way of the wind, and no man can always predict accurately where it will go" (030-029). The same force that carried scent-messages of prey now carries flame, and the creatures who lived by the wind now flee before it. The forest fire inverts the wind's meaning: what was once the medium of information becomes the medium of annihilation.

Yet even in destruction, the wind serves the novel's deeper pattern. The fire drives the flock upward toward the high peaks where the bighorns dwell, and the same winds that carry smoke also carry the promise of renewal. "In these places the melting snow, the colder nights and days, the moisture-laden winds that swept across them removed, in some degree, the effect of drought" (025-015). The wind that destroys is also the wind that restores.

## Key Characters/Events

Spread Horn, the bull elk, embodies wind-wisdom in the novel's opening pages. His practice of always walking into the wind is presented as the fundamental survival skill — and as the quality that marks him as a true native of Smoky Land. The predators use the wind too: the cougar stalks with the wind in his favor, and the wolf pack uses the wind to locate prey.

Hugh Gaylord, the tenderfoot, must learn to read the wind as the wild creatures do. His growing sensitivity to the wind's messages tracks his transformation from urban wastrel to authentic shepherd. By the novel's end, Hugh has internalized the wind's language so completely that he no longer thinks about reading it — he simply knows, as the wild creatures know, what the moving air is telling him.

Alice Crowson, the shepherdess, is a native reader of the wind from her first appearance. Her ease with the wilderness is signaled partly by her unconscious mastery of wind-reading — she adjusts her movements to the breeze without ever having to articulate the principle.

## Philosophical Significance

The wind represents the invisible order of the wilderness — the forces that humans cannot see but that govern life and death. The "faintest breath of wind" that "came slipping, light as a deer-tread itself, from the further ridges" (003-024) in the twilight hour symbolizes the interconnectedness of all things in the wild. To understand the wind is to understand the wilderness itself.

The wind is also the novel's figure for ancestry and continuity. Hugh's reflection that shepherds "had they not been out — through the long course of the ages — under the same stars, felt the same winds, endured the same dangers?" (027-010) ties the wind to the deepest human past. The wind that stirs the pines above Hugh's campfire is the same wind that stirred the acacias above the first shepherd's fire in the Neolithic. Wind is the breath of history — the invisible thread that connects every watcher of flocks across the millennia.
