# Nature

## Definition

In *Shepherds of the Wild*, nature is not a scenic backdrop but an active, governing force. It is the sum of laws — predation, reproduction, death, renewal — that operate without reference to human concepts of justice or fairness. Nature in this novel is both beautiful and terrifying, nurturing and destructive, and it demands that those who live within it abandon their civilized illusions about order and safety. Hugh Gaylord's journey is fundamentally an education in what nature really is, as opposed to the romanticized version he carried from the city.

## Manifestations

Nature's presence in the novel is most powerfully conveyed through the forest itself — the mysterious voice of the pines, the rustling thickets, the "hushed footfall of the wild creatures" (012-050). The opening chapters establish nature as the true protagonist of the story: long before human characters appear, the reader is immersed in the life of the forest, the rhythms of predator and prey, the ancient patterns of the wilderness. The forest fire (023-028) represents nature at its most indifferent — a force that consumes without discrimination, killing predator and prey, human and animal alike. Nature also manifests through the weather — the mountain wind "creeping forth in its never-ending explorations of the thickets" (012-002) — and through the seasonal cycles that govern animal behavior.

## Key Characters/Events

Unlike most concepts in the novel, nature is not embodied by any single character. Instead, it is the encompassing reality within which all characters operate. **Hugh Gaylord** serves as the reader's guide to understanding nature — his transformation from city dweller to shepherd is a process of learning nature's laws. The **forest fire** (023-028) is nature's most dramatic intervention — a reminder that the wilderness is not merely a setting but an active participant in the story, capable of reshaping the landscape and everyone in it.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel's treatment of nature is fundamentally philosophical. It presents nature as an alternative value system to civilization — not a better one, but a truer one. "The cities had been built in a day; the other factors that had been so necessary in his life — his clubs, his motor cars, his amusements, even much of the great world of business — were merely mushroom growths of a little handful of centuries that men called the age of civilization" (004-021). Against this, the novel poses nature as eternal: "Cities are born, grow great, are cursed with wickedness, and perish. The flocks still wander on the hills" (004-022). Nature is not kind, but it is real in a way that civilization's constructs are not.
