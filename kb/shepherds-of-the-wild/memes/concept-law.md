## Definition

In *Shepherds of the Wild*, "law" operates on multiple levels: the formal laws of the United States that govern the frontier, the unwritten codes of the wilderness, and the moral imperatives that guide individual conduct. The concept encompasses both the protective function of law—shielding the weak from the predatory—and its limitations in a landscape where legal authority is distant and nature's laws are immediate and brutal.

## Manifestations

Law appears in the novel primarily through its absence and through characters' varying relationships to legal and moral codes. Hugh Gaylord invokes national law to prevent the distribution of whisky to Native Americans: "I remember—I'd forgotten it until now—that there's a national law against giving whisky to Indians" (009-003). This assertion of legal authority establishes Hugh as a man who respects codified justice even in the remote wilderness.

The "forest laws" represent the natural order that governs predator-prey relationships. Broken Fang, the great cougar, has "broken" many forest laws in his long career of predation: "Many of the forest laws he had already broken; he might even yet break the law that forbade the death-feast" (012-004). These laws are not written but are understood within the novel's ecological framework as the boundaries that even wild creatures normally observe.

The protection of law is something the herders long for: "In October we start the sheep down—we'll be nearer the settlements and the protection of law" (018-007). This reveals the tension between the frontier's lawlessness and the civilization that lies beyond the mountains.

## Key Characters/Events

**Hugh Gaylord** embodies the law-bringer—a man who insists on legal and moral order even when surrounded by lawlessness. When Landy Fargo threatens the flock, Hugh's response is framed in the language of authority: Fargo wonders if "the man who had called him was an officer of the law who had been put on guard" (023-005). **Landy Fargo** represents the opposite principle—a man who operates outside legal constraints, using "bluffing" and intimidation rather than lawful authority (022-020).

The sheep dog, unnamed but pivotal, is described as "obedient to a great law within himself, true to a deathless trust that had been bestowed upon him" (011-004)—suggesting that law in its deepest sense is not external compulsion but internal fidelity.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel presents law as a spectrum: at one end, the formal statutes of civilization; at the other, the instinctual codes of the wild. Between them lies the moral law—the internalized sense of right that guides Hugh's actions. The sheep dog's obedience to "a great law within himself" (011-004) suggests that the truest law is not imposed from without but arises from loyalty and trust. In the wilderness, where sheriffs are absent, character itself becomes the only enforceable law.
