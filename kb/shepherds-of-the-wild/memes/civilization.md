# Civilization

Civilization, in *Shepherds of the Wild*, is not a place but an illusion — the "mushroom growths of a little handful of centuries" (004-022) that men mistake for permanent reality. The novel's philosophical project is, in large part, the dismantling of this illusion. [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] arrives in Smoky Land as a product of civilization — "he had never really been outside of cities" (004-021) — and his transformation into a [[shepherd]] is measured by the degree to which he learns to see civilization for what it is: a fragile, temporary construct erected against the ancient and enduring order of the wild.

## Definition

Marshall's definition of civilization is relentlessly comparative: civilization is always defined against the [[wilderness]] that precedes and will outlast it. The novel's most concentrated philosophical passage lays out the contrast explicitly:

> Cities are born, grow great, are cursed with wickedness, and perish. The flocks still wander on the hills. Men catch new fancies, follow new teachings, build new orders, and pursue new ways. The firelight of the herder still glows in the twilight. Civilization rises and falls like the tide. The beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep. (007-003)

Civilization is defined by its impermanence. Its elements — "clubs, motor cars, amusements, even much of the great world of business" (004-022) — are surface phenomena, incapable of addressing the fundamental human needs that the shepherd's fire and the grazing flock represent.

## Manifestations

**The City as Blindness**: Hugh's urban upbringing has trained his body and perceptions for a world that does not exist in Smoky Land. His physical conditioning — "swinging Indian clubs a startling number of times" (004-003) — proves useless on the game trail. More profoundly, his eyes are not "trained to penetrate the thickets" (005-032); civilization has taught him to see surfaces, not depths.

**The Return Impulse**: When Hugh first discovers the murdered herder, his immediate thought is to "return to civilization" (005-032). The phrase itself is telling — civilization is still "home," the default, the place to which one returns. The novel traces the slow dissolution of this assumption.

**Civilization's Aftertaste**: Even as Hugh immerses himself in wilderness life, civilization lingers as a memory — and increasingly, as a form of poverty. "Here, not in those swarming cities, was reality" (007-002). The city, once the totality of his experience, becomes a distant and diminished thing.

**The Hunting Hour**: Marshall's most striking image of the civilization-wilderness divide comes in the description of the "hunting hour" — the moment when "the whole wilderness thrilled and pulsed" with the bloodlust that "human beings — jaded with too many centuries of repression that men call civilization — find it hard to understand" (006-031).

## Key Characters

| Character | Relationship to Civilization |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] | Product of civilization who learns to see through it |
| [[old-colonel|Old Colonel]] | A man who has already bridged the divide — "a sportsman whose metal had been tried and proven on the game trails of two continents" (002-007) |
| [[pete|Pete]] | Born outside civilization but corrupted by its vices |

The [[old-colonel|Old Colonel]] is the novel's evidence that the civilization-wilderness divide can be crossed. He has built a fortune "by his own resistless effort" (002-007) in the world of cities, yet he possesses "X-ray eyes that could read straight into a man" (002-007) — eyes trained not by civilization but by the wilderness.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel's treatment of civilization is neither simple primitivism nor nostalgic pastoralism. Marshall does not argue that civilization is evil or that all men should abandon cities for the wilderness. Rather, he argues that civilization is *insufficient* — that it offers no answers to the fundamental questions of existence, and that those who mistake it for the whole of reality are living in a state of dangerous blindness.

The shepherd's fire is the novel's counter-symbol to the city's electric lights. "The firelight of the herder still glows in the twilight" (007-003) — *still*, because it predates civilization, and *still*, because it will outlast it. This is not a rejection of civilization but a radical recontextualization of it.

> Never did he get away from a haunting feeling that here — beside the white sheep — he was face to face with life at last. Here, not in his cities, were the realities, the essentials of life. (019-010)

## Key Passages

> All his life Hugh Gaylord had dwelt in cities. He had traveled far... Yet he had never really been outside of cities. (004-021)

> The cities had been built in a day; the other factors that had been so necessary in his life — his clubs, his motor cars, his amusements, even much of the great world of business — were merely mushroom growths of a little handful of centuries that men called the age of civilization. (004-022)

> It was a rapturous, an exultant thing, and human beings — jaded with too many centuries of repression that men call civilization — find it hard to understand. (006-031)

> Cities are born, grow great, are cursed with wickedness, and perish. The flocks still wander on the hills. (007-003)

> In the first dawn of civilization, dim and far away through the mists of the past, the herdsman cared for his sheep in the green pastures — and it was in the blood. (019-010)
