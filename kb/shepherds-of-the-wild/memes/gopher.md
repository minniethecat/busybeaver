# Gopher


## Overview

The gopher is one of the "Little People" of the [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] forest — the small, burrowing creatures whose lives play out at ground level while the dramas of [[wolf|wolves]], cougars, and men unfold above them. The novel gives the gopher a memorable introduction as a creature "so fat of cheeks that he gave the impression of being afflicted with mumps" (002-0[[TOC|31]]), establishing the affectionate, gently comic tone with which the narrative treats these minor citizens of [[nature|the wild]]erness.

## Natural History

Gophers are burrowing rodents of the family Geomyidae, adapted to a subterranean lifestyle. They have small eyes, short fur, and fur-lined cheek pouches used for carrying food. In the [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] ecosystem, they are among the most abundant small mammals, along with chipmunks and digger squirrels. The novel portrays them as creatures of limited perception — "The gopher has not particularly acute vision" (002-0[[TOC|31]]) — who survive more by concealment than awareness. During the drought that grips [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] through much of the narrative, the gophers are noted to fare relatively well: "Their small stomachs seemed to be lined with fur; and the dryer the brushwood the easier they could gnaw it" (018-0[[TOC|31]]).

## Role in the Novel

The gopher appears in two key contexts. First, as an observer — one of the small creatures who witness the passage of the larger [[predator|predators]] through the forest. When a tall, dark form (likely [[broken-fang|the puma]] [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]]) glides past at dusk, a gopher "lay rather close and still among his tree roots until the creature got past" (002-0[[TOC|31]]):

> "He didn't care to feel a puma claw impaling him as a fishhook impales a worm." (002-031)

The gopher's perspective anchors the novel's multi-layered view of [[nature|the wild]]erness — the forest is not only the arena of [[wolf|wolves]] and elk but also a world of small, fearful lives lived inches above the soil.

Second, the gopher serves as a metaphor for blind, unsuspecting prey. When [[alice-crowson|Alice Crowson]] rides toward the shadowed mouth of a canyon — unaware that she is riding into an ambush — the novel reaches for the gopher as its point of comparison:

> "No blind gopher, venturing forth from his burrow in the icy gaze of a rattler stretched still and lifeless in the moonlight, ever sped so straight and unsuspecting into an ambush as Alice, riding toward the shadowed mouth of the canyon." (013-027)

This simile elevates the humble gopher to tragic dignity: its blind vulnerability becomes the measure of human [[danger|peril]].

## Named Individuals

No individual gopher is named in the novel. The species appears as a collective presence — the "Little People" who rustle in the leaves and form the base of the forest's food chain.

## Symbolic/Thematic Role

The gopher embodies the novel's theme of limited perception. In the [[wilderness|Smoky Land wilderness]], what you cannot see can kill you. The gopher's poor vision makes it vulnerable to [[cougar|puma]] and rattler alike — just as [[alice-crowson|Alice]]'s inability to see the ambush ahead makes her vulnerable to [[landy-fargo|Fargo]]'s scheming. The gopher is the novel's emblem of innocence in a predatory world: unaware, uncomprehending, and yet somehow surviving.

The gopher also serves the novel's democratic interest in all forest creatures, great and small. The narrative takes time to note the gopher's perspective, however limited, as part of the complete picture of [[wilderness|wild]]erness life. The "Little People" — gophers, chipmunks, digger squirrels — are not merely prey; they are residents with their own small concerns, their own fears, and their own modest triumphs.
