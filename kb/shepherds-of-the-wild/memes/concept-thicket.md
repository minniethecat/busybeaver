# Thicket

The thicket in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the wilderness's hidden dimension — the dense, tangled growth where the forest's most secret dramas unfold. It is the domain of ambush and concealment, a place where creatures vanish from sight and where the boundary between the seen and unseen worlds blurs. The thicket is both shelter and danger: refuge for the hunted and hunting ground for the hunter. Throughout the novel, a character's relationship to the thicket is a reliable index of their relationship to the wild itself — those who can read its signs belong to the forest; those who cannot remain outsiders.

## Definition

In the novel's ecology, the thicket represents the opacity of the wild — the parts of the forest that resist human penetration and understanding. "His eyes were not trained to penetrate the thickets as those of the Indian" (005-032). The thicket is the measure of wilderness competence. The Indian guide moves through the thickets with silent, instinctual ease; Hugh stumbles and is scratched by berry vines, his body marked by his inexperience.

The thicket is also the architecture of predation. "The beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep" (004-022) — this recurring image establishes the thicket as the predator's natural stronghold, the place from which death springs. The novel's predators are creatures of the thicket: they emerge from it to kill and retreat into it to feed. "His long tail began to lash back and forth; his fangs caught the dim light of the encroaching dawn as he crept like a serpent through the thickets" (007-033).

## Manifestations

The novel's opening scene establishes the thicket as a fundamental element of the landscape: "The mouth of the canyon was darkened with shadows when the bull elk came stealing down the brown trail through the dusky thicket" (001-002). The thicket is present from the first sentence, framing the wilderness as a place of half-hidden movements and shadowed paths. Spread Horn the elk navigates the thickets with the confidence of a native; his movement through them establishes the baseline of wilderness competence against which all other characters will be measured.

As Hugh ventures deeper into the forest, his awareness of the thicket grows: "As they headed deeper into the thickets a strange change seemed to come over the man" (004-006). The physical penetration of the thicket mirrors an inner transformation — the civilized man awakening to the wild. He begins to sense what lies hidden: "Hugh had an instant's dim realization that thickets moved and rustled in the ultimate reaches of hearing; that dim shadows wavered so far distant that he could not be sure of them" (003-023).

For the predators, the thicket is the essential tool of the hunt. The cougar Broken Fang "crept like a serpent through the thickets" (007-033), his approach rendered invisible by the dense cover. The wind and the thicket together make the perfect ambush: "The wind had been right, the thickets had hidden his advance, the magic and the thrill of the hunting hour had been upon him" (007-026). The thicket is not passive concealment — it is an active instrument of predation, the medium through which the hunter's art is practiced.

### The Thicket as Portal

The thicket serves a deeper narrative function as a portal between worlds. When Hugh first passes through the "heavier thickets" (004-002) on the trail to the sheep camp, he crosses a threshold — on one side the world of cities, clubs, and dissipation; on the other, the world of flocks, fires, and predators. The berry vines that "scratched the face and caught at his clothes" (004-002) physically resist his passage, marking him as an outsider who has not yet earned the right to move through the wild unseen.

Later, after his transformation, Hugh moves through the thickets differently — not as an intruder but as one who belongs. The thicket that once resisted him now conceals him. His growing comfort in the dense undergrowth is a measure of his assimilation into the wilderness order.

### The Thicket and the Hunt

The thicket is the stage for the novel's central drama of predator and prey. "The smells on the winds, the little hushed noises in the thickets, the startled waverings of shadows all added their influence; and the bloodlust came upon the beasts of prey" (006-031). Every rustle in the thicket carries the possibility of death — or deliverance. Hugh learns to read these signs, distinguishing the benign movement of a gopher from the stealthy approach of a coyote.

The coyote Running Feet uses the thicket as his hunting blind: "He crept slowly from the thicket, a gray shadow few wilderness eyes were keen enough to see" (013-015). For the cowardly coyote, the thicket provides what courage cannot — the opportunity to approach unseen. But even the thicket's concealment cannot overcome Spot's vigilance on his rocky promontory.

## Key Characters/Events

The thicket serves as a threshold between Hugh's civilized self and the wild. When he first enters the forest with his guide, "the berry vines scratched the face and caught at his clothes when the trail twined between the heavier thickets" (004-002) — the thicket physically resists his passage, marking him as an outsider.

The novel's climax in the forest fire transforms the thicket from a place of concealment to a place of deadly danger. What once hid the predator now traps the fleeing creatures, the very density that provided cover becoming an instrument of destruction as the flames sweep through.

The guide's death scene is framed by the thicket. Hugh discovers the body "in a little clump of thicket" (005-024), and the murderer has fled into the same dense cover that now hides the evidence of the crime. The thicket is both crime scene and escape route — it conceals equally the victim and the killer.

## Philosophical Significance

The thicket embodies the novel's central insight: that the wilderness conceals as much as it reveals. It is the physical manifestation of the wild's essential mystery. The creatures that vanish into the thicket enter a realm beyond human knowledge, and Hugh's growth is measured by his increasing ability to read the signs that the thicket offers — the broken twig (004-025), the rustling leaves (005-040), the dim shadows that waver at the edge of vision.

The thicket also represents the limits of human mastery. No matter how skilled Hugh becomes, there will always be depths of the thicket he cannot penetrate, movements he cannot interpret, dangers he cannot see. The thicket is the wilderness's irreducible remainder — the part of the wild that forever escapes human comprehension and control. This is not a failure but a condition of existence: to live in Smoky Land is to accept that the thicket will always hold secrets.
