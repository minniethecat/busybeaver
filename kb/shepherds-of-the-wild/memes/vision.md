# Vision

**Vision** in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the clarifying power that the wilderness bestows upon those who dwell within it. It is both literal sight — the keen eyes of predator and prey — and spiritual insight, the sudden clarity that reveals life's essential truths.

## Hugh's Awakening

The novel's central transformation is Hugh Gaylord's acquisition of vision. Arriving in the wilderness as a dissipated urbanite, his first moments among the sheep bring an almost mystical clarity: "It was an impression that all his life there had been a cloud before his vision, and all at once he could see clear" (001-253). The cities, he realizes, "had been built in a day" and their apparatus — clubs, motor cars, amusements — were "merely mushroom growths of a little handful of centuries." For the first time, he is "face to face with life" (001-253).

This new vision is not merely philosophical. In a moment of intense introspection, "he had just been given a glimpse into his own soul: a sight that he had never really had before" (001-443). The wilderness strips away his self-deception about his drinking, forcing him to confront uncomfortable truths.

## Vision as Moral Clarity

When a lamb is threatened, Hugh experiences a different dimension of vision: "in that moment of inner trial a great and a serene knowledge came to him. He knew — in one instant of vision — that it did not lay with the shepherd to consider abstract issues of life and death" (001-619). His duty is simply "to watch the flock." Vision here becomes the capacity to perceive moral imperatives without the fog of rationalization.

This clarity reshapes his entire worldview. His previous cynicism — "he had thought that everything was dross" — gives way to "a new tolerance, a new breadth of view toward all the world" (001-715). Most remarkably, "never in his life, so far as he could remember, had he laughed from the simple joy of life" (001-715). Vision restores joy.

## The Vision of Home

Hugh's transformation is framed as a homecoming. "All his life, it had seemed to him, he had carried in the deepest realms of his spirit a vision that had now come true: just the feeding flocks in the deep shade of the forest" (001-725). The salmon returning to spawn become a metaphor for this repatriation — they carry "in the deepest recesses of their beings a vision of their Lost Land to which they would return to die" (001-729).

## Alice's Vision

In the novel's climax, vision is granted to Alice as well. Watching Hugh's stand against the wolf pack, "Vision had come to her, and throughout the world she saw men's works and heard women's tears" (001-1791). She perceives the grand sweep of history — Vikings, frontiersmen, armies, bridge-builders — all connected by the same impulse Hugh embodies.

> She could see the Viking, leaving the white arms of his woman and following western stars; the frontiersman, striking out from his beloved hearth to seek new dominions; the bloodstained paths of armies; the builder, stretching his bridges across roaring rivers and his railroads into uncharted lands. Through the long roll of the ages she saw the shepherd on the rugged hills, alone and wondering and full of thoughts, watching his sheep. (001-1791)
