# Bighorn Sheep

The bighorn sheep — also called mountain ram — is the novel's emblem of prey consciousness: alert, social, and defined by the constant pressure of predation.

## Definition

Bighorn sheep are the dominant herbivores of Smoky Land's high country. They are described with admiration for their physical prowess — "He would stake his horns and hoofs against any wild creature" (050-001) — but also with recognition of their vulnerability. Their existence is defined by the predators that hunt them.

## Role in the Ecosystem

As a keystone prey species, the bighorn shapes the behavior of every predator in Smoky Land. The puma, the grizzly, the wolf — all depend on the bighorn and its relatives. The bighorn's survival strategies — vigilance, flocking, sheer physical strength — drive the evolutionary arms race that defines the novel's wilderness.

## Named Individuals

### Spread Horn

Spread Horn is the great bull bighorn, "the monarch of the sheep" — a creature of immense horns and fighting spirit who commands respect even from predators. He represents the prey's capacity for defiance: not passive victims but active participants in the struggle for survival.

## Significance in the Novel

The bighorn serves as the novel's primary symbol of prey dignity. Unlike smaller animals that flee or hide, the bighorn stands and fights when necessary. Its horns are both weapon and crown — a reminder that prey animals are not merely victims but warriors in their own right. The shepherd's relationship with the bighorn is one of fellow feeling: both are guardians, both live under the shadow of predation.
