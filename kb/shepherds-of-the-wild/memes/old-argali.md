# Old Argali

Old Argali is the patriarch of the bighorn sheep in the high peaks above Smoky Land — a "wonderful and noble monarch — to whom even the wolf made obeisance" (020-018). He is Spot's wild father, and his single encounter with a band of domestic ewes sets in motion the chain of events that produces the novel's central animal character.

## Overview

Argali appears in the narrative largely through memory and dream. He is introduced in Chapter 18 alongside Old Surefoot as one of "the two greatest of the bighorn rams" who "fought their battles in the fall" (018-009). His domain is above timberline — "in the land where the great snow banks endured through the centuries" (018-009).

His defining act occurs two seasons before the main narrative: during the autumn rut, he encounters a band of domestic ewes driven into the mountains by a wolf. "He realized that these ewes were his own people — cousins far remote and yet females of his own breed" (021-007). Their mating produces Spot.

## Character Arc

Argali's arc is minimal but pivotal. His appearance in the moonlight — "he lifted high his magnificent horns" (021-010) — is one of the novel's most transcendent moments. Hugh, waking to witness the bighorn patriarch, feels "as if the mountains had opened their secret heart to him at last" (021-010).

But Argali is suspicious of the human world — the firelight and unfamiliar smells — and "even now the great ram had begun to grow suspicious" (021-014). He turns to leave, and Spot races after him: "He knew this breed that had come down from the fastnesses" (021-015). In this moment of recognition between father and son, the novel's themes of heritage and belonging crystallize.

Argali disappears back into the high peaks, and Spot is prevented from following. "Argali and his band had fled, and by driving the runaway sheep back to the flock the little bighorn — born the following spring — was to be cheated of his heritage" (021-009).

## Relationships

Argali's relationship with Spot is the novel's most poignant biological connection — a father who briefly appears, is recognized, and vanishes, leaving his son torn between two worlds. With Old Surefoot, Argali shares the rivalry of equals — "they fought their battles in the fall" (018-009). With the domestic world, he has only fleeting, consequential contact.

## Thematic Significance

Old Argali represents the wild in its most elevated form — not predatory but majestic, not menacing but remote. He is the "monarch" whose nobility commands respect from "even the wolf" (020-018). His brief appearance suggests that the wild can be glimpsed but never possessed — Hugh sees him but cannot keep him, Spot recognizes him but cannot follow.

The name "Argali" itself — referring to the great Asian mountain sheep, *Ovis ammon* — carries scientific resonance, linking the fictional bighorns of Smoky Land to their real-world counterparts across the globe.
