# Fear

Fear in *Shepherds of the Wild* is a fundamental force of wilderness psychology. Every creature — elk, cougar, dog, and man — is shaped by fear, and the novel distinguishes between those who master it and those who are ruled by it. It is presented as neither virtue nor vice but as information: a primal signal system that keeps creatures alive, a tool of threat assessment, and a moral arena where character is forged.

## Manifestations

The novel presents fear as universal but differentiating. Spread Horn, the bull elk, "had no fear of such enemies as might be lurking in the gloomy canyon" (001-002) — his confidence born of strength. Yet when he catches man's scent, his "nervous reaction was instantaneous: danger, go slow!" (001-016). Even the king of the forest knows fear when facing the unknown.

For the shepherd dogs, fearlessness is a defining trait. The dog remains "fearless and constant in his vigil" (005-051), and the narrative celebrates the "bold, fearless, domineering disposition" (010-008) of the dominant breed. Yet the novel acknowledges that "Man knows the terror of lightning, the dread (as well as the love) of fire, the fear of flooding waters" (008-012) — certain fears are natural and wise, rooted in ancestral memory that "carries them back to the first great Terror" (008-012).

Hugh Gaylord experiences fear as a moral challenge. "He was afraid, but his fear could not come between the shepherd and his work" (008-017) — the novel's definition of courage as fear overcome by duty. When confronting Broken Fang on the log, he faces "one single moment of blighting terror" (008-031) yet presses forward, discovering that "He was of the dominant breed, and if he forgot that fact for one instant — in this moment of stress — those white fangs would lash out" (008-032).

Alice Crowson endures the novel's most sustained confrontation with terror. With the dog pack closing in and her pistol empty, she faces "that forest nightmare of terror" (017-024), and an inner voice commands: "She must not yield herself to terror. To turn, to waver but an instant meant that those white fangs would flash toward her throat" (017-025). Her ordeal demonstrates that fear must be defied even when all external defenses have failed.

The cougar's scream, described as freezing "the whole forest world with horror" (005-045), is both a literal event and a symbolic expression of the ancient terror of the wilderness — "the primeval forces that war with man" (005-048). Through Broken Fang's eyes, fear is also parsed as a tactical calculation: "if the dog and the man feared him there was no further cause for him to be afraid of them" (008-023). In this reading, fear is a communication system between species, a language of power and submission.

The coyote, Running Feet, embodies cowardice as a cosmic affliction: "His second name was Coward — cowardice was part of the curse that Manitou had put upon him" (010-013). His fear is not caution but a paralysis that mocks his intelligence, for "Part of his curse was a far-reaching and calculating intelligence almost equal to that of a dog, a trait that would have been a tremendous advantage if he had been blessed with courage to go with it, but which to a coward meant only realization of a thousand dangers to make life a torment" (010-014). The coyote's fate illustrates the novel's view that fear without courage is a living death.

Landy Fargo, the antagonist, undergoes a complete arc of fear. When he first sees the campfire burning where a murdered man should have left only ashes, "A strange little shiver of cold and fear crept over him" (016-008), and "the moment was of the greatest terror he had ever known" (016-010). Yet "his fear changed in a moment to devastating rage" (016-012) when he realizes the watchman is only a lowly herder. His contemptuous conclusion — "There was nothing to fear here" (016-013) — proves to be his fatal miscalculation. Fear, for Fargo, is an emotion he cannot distinguish from anger, and his inability to read it accurately leads to his downfall.

## Fear as Hierarchy

The novel constructs a clear hierarchy of fear responses that maps directly to character and species status:

| Level | Response | Characters | Quality |
|-------|----------|------------|---------|
| Mastery | Fear acknowledged, then set aside through duty or love | Hugh, Alice, the shepherd dog | Courage |
| Instinct | Fear as immediate threat detection, followed by flight | Spread Horn, deer | Prudence |
| Calculation | Fear as tactical assessment, used to decide attack or retreat | Broken Fang | Intelligence |
| Paralysis | Fear that prevents action, despite awareness of danger | Running Feet the coyote | Cowardice |
| Transmutation | Fear that converts into rage and aggression | Landy Fargo | Corruption |

This hierarchy reveals the novel's moral framework: fear itself is neutral, but the response to fear defines character. The shepherd and his dog achieve the highest level — fear does not disappear, but it is subordinated to duty. The coyote's paralytic cowardice is lower than the cougar's calculating fear because it allows awareness of danger to prevent action. Fargo's transmutation of fear into violence occupies the lowest rung: it is fear weaponized and misdirected, the most dangerous form of all.

## Key Characters

Hugh Gaylord faces fear repeatedly — of the cougar, of the rapids, of human enemies — and each time chooses duty over self-preservation. His fear is real but never paralyzing. Significantly, when confronting Fargo, Hugh "had been a little afraid, at first" (016-028), yet he knows that "Instinctively he knew that he was face to face with a mortal foe; but he felt a miraculous trust in his own strength" (016-028). Fear does not vanish; it is simply rendered irrelevant by a deeper certainty. Hugh's transformation is marked by his growing ability to process fear without being governed by it — from the social dread of the Colonel's judgment to the existential terror of facing a predator at close quarters.

Alice Crowson represents fear confronted without physical power. Her ordeal with Fargo's dog pack strips her of every defense — she fires her last bullet and then faces the hounds with nothing but will. "Her terror cost her the steadiness of her hand" (017-024), yet in the crisis she learns that the inner command not to yield to terror is the only thing standing between her and death. Her arc mirrors Hugh's: both discover that the mastery of fear is a matter not of eliminating it but of maintaining inner command.

The shepherd dogs, particularly Shep, represent fearlessness in service. Their courage is described as instinctive precisely because it lacks the calculation that fear enables. "No wilderness voice was so terrible as to frighten him from his watch" (005-050) — the dog's fearlessness is a function of his complete devotion to the flock.

Broken Fang, the cougar, occupies the predator's perspective: fear is a tactical instrument. He knows "Most of all, fear men" (008-020) as a forest law, and he assesses threats based on whether his prey shows fear. When the dog and man plunge into the torrent — behavior he associates with fleeing prey — he concludes they fear him and attacks. But his calculation is wrong: Hugh's fear is not the fear of prey but the caution of a shepherd who still intends to fight.

Running Feet the coyote is the novel's study in terminal cowardice. He is intelligent enough to calculate danger accurately but lacks the courage to act on his intelligence, making him the most pitiable creature in the wilderness.

Landy Fargo demonstrates that the worst response to fear is to transmute it into cruelty. His superstition makes him vulnerable to terror, but his pride transforms that terror into savagery — first against the sheep, then against Alice.

## Philosophical Significance

Marshall treats fear as neither virtue nor vice but as information. The novel's wisdom is encapsulated in the rule "Most of all, fear men" (008-020) — fear as instinctive threat assessment, the wisdom of a million years of evolution compressed into a single imperative. Fear keeps creatures alive; it is the reckless who perish.

Yet the novel also celebrates the transcendence of fear through love and duty. The shepherd dog who guards "with his life if necessary" has not eliminated fear but has subordinated it to a higher loyalty. Hugh, plunging into the rapids while "the heart of the man was like ice" (008-034), has not conquered fear but has chosen to act despite it. This is the novel's moral vision: fear acknowledged, then set aside.

The novel places fear in a cosmic context. The voice of the cougar "expressed all the ancient terror of the darkness, the primeval forces that war with man" (005-048). Fear is not just personal but anthropological — it connects the individual to the long history of the species, to "other such fiery spheres, gleaming in the darkness at the mouth of the cave" (008-012). The novel suggests that to feel fear is to be fully human, to participate in the ancient struggle between civilization and the wild.

In the novel's cosmology, the wilderness itself is described as offering "the old complaint of the pain of living with only Fear and Death at the end" (009-005). Fear is the emotional currency of the wild, the price of existence. The shepherd's task is not to eliminate fear — which would be impossible — but to render it powerless against the higher imperatives of guardianship and love.
