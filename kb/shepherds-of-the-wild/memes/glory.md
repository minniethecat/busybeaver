# Glory

In the world of *Shepherds of the Wild*, **glory** is the fleeting moment of triumph — the predator's kill, the sunset on a high peak, the shepherd's victory over the menacing wild. It is distributed unevenly across the wilderness: concentrated in the hunting hour, scattered across the high ridges, and most fully realized by those who have earned it through struggle.

## The Hunting Hour of Glory

For the predators of the wilderness, glory belongs to the night. The narrative voice describes it as "the hour of glory when fang and talon and strength and stealth come into their own" (001-687). In the darkness, the dim movements of hunters become indistinguishable from shadows, and the advantage shifts decisively from prey to predator. This is not a moral judgment — the novel treats this glory as part of the natural order.

The cougar, the wolf, and even the lowly coyote all know versions of this glory. Running Feet and "his savage companions were in their glory" when stray bands of sheep became separated from the flock in box canyons (001-1403). The buzzards, those "undertakers, the followers of the dead," would be "in their glory" if all streams dried up — a dark irony embedded in drought legend and prophecy (001-1387).

## Human Glory

Hugh Gaylord comes to know glory of a different kind. In his climactic battle to save Alice from the wolf pack, "he knew the joy of victory, the glory of strength" (001-1365). This is not the predator's bloodlust but the protector's exultation — earned through courage and paid for in blood.

Earlier, the Old Colonel's wisdom frames Hugh's heritage in terms of ancestral connection to the land: the Anglo-Saxon who could no longer "see the sun breaking forth in glory to the east because of city spires that rose between" was fated to seek the far places (001-731). Glory, in this ancestral sense, is inseparable from the open sky and the feeding flock.

## The Glory of the High Peaks

The novel's most lyrical invocations of glory belong to the mountain landscape itself. Through the dreams of Spot, the bighorn lamb, the reader glimpses a world of "cliffs, waterfalls, snow-capped peaks" where "the glory of the sun from the topmost spire of the highest peak" awaits (001-1489). The old Bighorn ram Lurk-in-the-trail has known in waking life what Spot only dreams: "the glory of the sunset on the high peaks" (001-1519).

> The longing for his lost land was with him every waking moment. He loved his dreams: his reality seemed absent of all delight. He was always sorry to waken. Deep, insatiable longings and vague unhappiness filled his days. (001-1497)

Hugh himself comes to know "the peace of the stars and the glory of the moon" (001-1425), a quiet counterpoint to the violent glory of the hunt.

## The Two Faces of Glory

The novel consistently distinguishes between two kinds of glory: the predatory and the earned. The predatory glory belongs to darkness — the cougar's "long, wild, triumphant scream" (006-035), the buzzard's grim satisfaction in disaster, the wolf pack's exultation over fallen prey. As the narrator observes, "the night has always been the time of triumph to the hunting creatures" (009-003). Yet this glory is morally neutral: the wilderness distributes it without regard to virtue.

The earned glory, by contrast, comes through sacrifice. When Hugh feels "the thrill and the exultation that had passed from border to border through the wilderness world" (006-029) under the moonlight, he is participating in something larger than predation — a shared consciousness of the wild. And when he reflects that "no false flattery had ever been so satisfying as his victory over Broken Fang" (008-043), glory becomes a measure of authentic experience rather than social status. The novel's final word on glory may be its image of the bighorn ram returning to "his own snow-capped peaks" (031-006) — the quietest, deepest glory of all: the glory of belonging.
