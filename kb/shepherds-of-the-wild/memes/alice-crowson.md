# Alice Crowson

Alice Crowson is the daughter of Ezra Crowson and a capable camp-tender in Smoky Land. She becomes Hugh Gaylord's unexpected companion and eventual love interest, defying the expectation that the wilderness is "not a place for the tender flesh of girls" (011-004).

## Overview

Alice Crowson first appears when Hugh discovers that his camp-tender is not "a Mexican, nor yet a laboring man of a certain type, but a girl" (011-003). Raised in the mountains, she embodies a different kind of wilderness competence than the male characters — one born of lifelong familiarity rather than hard-won conquest. "She knew these mountains. She felt that in some measure at least she had mastered them" (015-004).

She is young — "not more than twenty-two at most" — and "pretty past all denial" (011-005), but her beauty is not her defining trait. She carries a heavy revolver, handles her own camp, and runs her flock with the same authority as any male herder. As Hugh observes, she wore "a rather heavy revolver slung at her hip" and her hand, "small and shapely, but also brown and firm," suggested that "they would make a rather dangerous combination" (011-007).

Alice works as her father's camp-tender out of necessity — "unlike many sheepmen in the West, his whole capital is tied up in this one flock. He isn't a big sheepman — only a little one" (012-004). Her "perfect health, her strong, lithe body, a skill with horses and a wholesome scarcity of nonsense in her disposition had enabled her to fill the position well" (011-008).

## Character Arc

### First Meeting and the Murder

Alice's first appearance is charged with discovery. When Hugh tells her that the previous herder Dan has been killed, she responds with mountain stoicism: "she opened her eyes; and it seemed to Hugh that she closed her lips — in a fine, hard line — just for an instant" (011-004). She wastes no time on hysteria but immediately asks the practical question: "Killed — or did he just die?" (011-004).

She hires Hugh on the spot for "two dollars a day" (011-008), and admits frankly the danger he would face: "Dan was murdered, not for personal reasons, but because the cattlemen — a little, evil group of them — want to destroy this flock of sheep" (011-009).

### The Camp-Tender's World

In Chapter 12, Alice reveals the full scope of her competence. She cooks a supper that Hugh — who "had dined in the most famous cafes and restaurants of Europe" — finds incomparable: "he couldn't remember ever experiencing the simple and healthy zest for food, the sheer delight of eating, the amazing appetite that had come upon him now" (012-001). She explains the range war that threatens her father's flock and the systematic violence the cattlemen have used: "sometimes they did it just by threatening, sometimes by poisoning the stock, and *sometimes* —" (012-005).

When a dozen sheep are lost from the flock, Alice insists on searching for them herself through the night-dark forest, telling Hugh: "You don't know sheep yet, Hugh, and likely you don't know these mountains...It's part of the life of the camp-tender" (012-010). Hugh watches her go into the darkness and feels "a strange weight of dread" (012-011).

Alice's role expands from camp-tender to active participant in the novel's central conflicts. When Jose Mertos murders the herder, Alice is the one who discovers the body upon her return — "three days later" (013-012). This places her at the center of the novel's crime narrative.

### The Hound Attack (Chapter 17)

Her most dramatic sequence comes in Chapter 17, when she runs her flock at top speed through moonlit canyons, pursued by a savage pack of enormous hounds: "These were not wolves. This fact dawned upon Alice Crowson, running her little flock at top speed toward the camp" (017-002). The hounds are Fargo's weapon, and the passage distinguishes their menace from that of wild predators: "And it would have been better were they wolves. Not for nothing has man waged immemorial centuries of warfare...They have been taught a wholesome respect for the tall breed that has come to dominate the earth...But it is not this way with dogs" (017-001).

Alice faces a harrowing choice between self-preservation and her duty. "It occurred to her that there might be time, even yet, to spring up a tree and leave the sheep to their fate. Yet was not she the shepherdess, the guardian of the flock?" (017-006). She stands her ground. The scene is a tableau: "the huddled band of white sheep, the girl — the eternal shepherdess of the wilderness — standing guard over them, the savage pack in its grim circle" (017-007).

She fires her revolver with diminishing ammunition — six shots, and only her "third shot went true" (017-008). In her moment of terror, her instinctive cry is for Hugh: "Help me, Hugh" (017-004). Hugh hears the faint sound of her pistol from nearly a mile away and runs to her rescue, realizing "that Alice was in her own being his life and death, his heaven and hell, his spirit and his world and his stars" (017-018).

### The Forest Fire (Chapters 25-31)

Alice's trial by fire forms the novel's climactic sequence. When Fargo sets the forest ablaze to destroy both the flock and the shepherds, Alice and Hugh face the flames together. "In the glow of the fire, and speaking just loud enough to be heard above its roar, Alice and Hugh made swift appraisal of their situation" (025-001). When Hugh insists that she ride for help while he stays with the sheep, Alice protests: "Then I'll stay too. I won't go alone" (025-004).

Hugh's decision to stay with the flock — "a good herder — doesn't leave his sheep" (025-005) — transforms Alice's understanding of him. "She understood. At last she knew this man who stood before her...She was simply the woman, sick at heart with fear for the man she loved" (025-005). In this moment, Hugh confesses his love: "I love you, Alice. Never doubt it — never forget it" (025-011), and Alice answers: "And I love you, Hugh" (025-011).

Riding for help, Alice is ambushed by Jose Mertos. Her horse is shot dead beneath her and "Alice was fairly hurled through the air, and it seemed an incredible thing that human flesh should endure such a fall and yet retain life" (026-007). She awakens to find herself bound and at Jose's mercy in the path of the advancing fire. In captivity, Alice shows remarkable defiance: "If you want to leave me here in the track of the fire, it's in your power to do it — but it won't make me bend to you, or plead with you, or treat you any different than I've ever treated you" (028-008).

Shep the shepherd dog rescues Alice from Jose's assault, but leaves her still tied to a tree. "Her arms still pinioned, Alice sat waiting for the end" (029-010). Hugh, led by Shep, runs desperately to find her and cuts her free. Together they return to the flock to face what they believe will be their final hour: "One more hour," Hugh whispers. "It can't be over an hour more. And then we go — some place — together" (030-002).

Their salvation comes through Spot, the bighorn ram, who leads the flock through a hidden canyon pass. "The lesser folk had shown the way...The bighorn ram — exiled by birth among strangers...was headed back to his own snow-capped peaks — and Hugh and Alice and the surging flocks had simply followed his lead" (031-003).

By the novel's end, Alice stands with Hugh at the foot of the high mountains, having survived the forest fire. She speaks the novel's most hopeful lines: "We've won. We're safe, after all" (031-009).

Alice's relationship with Hugh develops from mutual astonishment to deep partnership. Hugh's initial disbelief — "by no possible circumstance had she a right to be pretty" (011-006) — gives way to profound attachment. Their final scene together, with "her brown hand creeping into Hugh's" (031-008), confirms their union.

The bond between Hugh and Alice is built on shared labor and shared danger. "Beyond all other joys and compensations for these three months in Smoky Land, there remained — a subject for humble thanks to the skies — the comradeship of Alice. Every day it made the struggle more worth while, his happiness more complete" (019-001).

Her father, Ezra Crowson, appears briefly as a sheep rancher whose flock Hugh tends. Landy Fargo and Jose Mertos are her antagonists — Fargo's scheme explicitly counts on the murder not being "discovered before Alice Crowson returned" (013-012).

## Thematic Significance

Alice represents the possibility of human connection within the wilderness. Where the male characters are defined by their relationship to the land — guardianship, conquest, survival — Alice brings the dimension of companionship and love. She is not a romanticized figure but a practical one: she shoots straight, runs her own camp, and faces danger with composure.

Her role as "the eternal shepherdess of the wilderness" (017-007) connects her to an archetypal pastoral tradition, yet Bindloss firmly roots her in the gritty realities of frontier life. She is "of the mountains, because the spirit that dwells in the forest, the rugged places, the wilderness primeval dwelt also in her" (028-006). Unlike Hugh, whose transformation requires the wilderness to remake him, Alice is already a product of the environment — her competence is native, not acquired.

The novel's gender dynamics pivot on Alice's character. The opening assumption that Smoky Land is "a man's land, not a place for the tender flesh of girls" (011-004) is systematically undermined by her every action. She rides alone through dangerous terrain, carries a revolver she knows how to use, negotiates business with rough herders, and faces death with stoic courage. Yet Bindloss does not deny her femininity — her voice is a "tinkling, musical sound, inexpressibly girlish" (011-005), and her relationship with Hugh brings emotional depth to the survival narrative.

The novel's final affirmation — "Each other — always. Oh, shepherd of my heart!" (031-021) — transforms the shepherd narrative from solitary duty to shared purpose. Alice makes the wilderness not just a place of testing but of belonging. Hugh's declaration — "Don't you see, Alice — that this is my rightful place? With the sheep? In the wilderness?" (031-011) — is answered by her presence, her partnership, and her own commitment to the same life. Together they will "fill the hills with the feeding flocks" and "still know the comradeship of the camp fire, the night wind whispering through the secret places" (031-014).
