## Definition

In *Shepherds of the Wild*, the meadow is the central spatial setting—a grassy, treeless clearing in the high mountain ranges where the [[sheep]] are bedded at night and where the herders make their camp. It is described as "one of those grassy, treeless stretches that are so often encountered in the high ranges" (005-004). The meadow is both a practical grazing ground and a symbolic boundary between the human and the [[concept-wilderness|wilderness]].

## Manifestations

The meadow appears as the primary stage for the novel's action. It is where the flock of two thousand [[sheep]] sleeps under the watch of [[the-shepherd|the herder]] and his [[shepherd-dog|dogs]], where the camp fire burns against the [[concept-darkness|darkness]], and where predators—[[wolves]], [[coyote|coyotes]], and the great [[cougar]] [[Broken Fang]]—test the perimeter of human control. [[Hugh Gaylord]]'s first night with the flock takes place at the meadow's edge: "At the edge of the little meadow the dusk deepened between the trees. The strong profiles of the pines dimmed and blurred, the distant peaks receded" (006-005).

The meadow transforms in Hugh's perception from a mere geographical feature to a charged symbolic space. By night, with the flock sleeping, it becomes the arena where the ancient conflict between shepherd and predator plays out: "Still the sheep slept in the meadow and a great beast of prey menaced them from the shadowed forest" (008-006).

Throughout the dry season, Hugh leads the flock between meadows at ever-higher elevations, following the greenest grazing: "Hugh led them to the greenest glens, the richest meadows, and his weekly change of camp site found them ever higher on the range where the effects of drought were less" (017-008). In late September the flock had ascended so far that "the old leader of the [[species-bighorn|bighorn]] flock could look down and see these tame brethren, like moving fields of [[concept-snow|snow]], on the slopes beneath" (017-008).

The meadow at night takes on an enchanted quality under the [[moon]]: "The moonlight was a miracle in itself. It enchanted the meadows, it filled the open forest with gliding ghosts, it turned to snow the great, sleeping flock" (019-005). It is in this moonlit meadow that Hugh witnesses the [[species-bighorn|bighorn]] flock leader—an epiphany that marks his spiritual completion.

## Key Characters/Events

The meadow is the site of [[Hugh Gaylord]]'s spiritual awakening. It is here that he first senses the "mystery of the forest in the darkness" and realizes that "the domain of man... extended just to the limits of the little meadow where the sheep were bedded: beyond that lay the Kingdom of the Wild" (008-009). **[[Pete]]**, the guide, describes the horse "grazing just inside the forest, just outside meadow" (007-005), emphasizing the meadow's liminal position.

[[Landy Fargo]] approaches the meadow with instinctive caution during his midnight visit: "Landy Fargo tried to approach the sheep camp with some caution. It was an instinctive effort: he had not the slightest idea that the lone human occupant of the little meadow could waken from his deep sleep to hear him" (015-007). The meadow also becomes [[Alice Gaylord|Alice]]'s camp during her stay, with Hugh cutting "fir boughs for her bed that was to be situated nearly a quarter of a mile away, across the meadow" (012-009).

For [[Spot]], the [[species-bighorn|bighorn]] lamb, the meadow represents captivity—a place from which his dreams carry him away: "In his dreams he always left the meadows far behind him. Even the forest itself was left too, and he was set free and joyful in a more familiar land: a place of jagged cliffs and slide-rock" (019-003).

## Philosophical Significance

The meadow functions as the novel's key metaphor for the human condition in nature. It is a fragile clearing—won from the wilderness and maintained only by constant vigilance. The firelight that "leaps into the shadow and the darkness comes racing back" (008-009) parallels the precariousness of human civilization. The shepherds of Judea, Hugh realizes, "might have known the same cry" (008-006)—the meadow connects him to an ancient pastoral tradition, collapsing the distance between modern man and his ancestors.

The meadow also embodies [[concept-memory|racial memory]] and the Anglo-Saxon pastoral heritage. The Old Colonel's words to Hugh frame the meadow as an ancestral inheritance: "The Anglo-Saxon was never one to gather into cities. He knew the meadows, the forests, the feel of the earth through the handles of his plow, the sheep feeding on the hillside" (010-003). The meadow is thus not merely a geographical location but a spiritual homeland—the place where [[concept-heritage|heritage]] reasserts itself against the alienation of modern city life.
