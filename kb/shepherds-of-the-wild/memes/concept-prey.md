# Prey

Prey in *Shepherds of the Wild* encompasses all creatures that are hunted, from the domesticated sheep flocks to the wild deer, rabbit, and porcupine. The concept is essential to the novel's ecological vision: predation is not cruelty but the fundamental engine of wilderness life, a reality that Hugh Gaylord must confront directly.

## Definition

In the novel's world, prey are the animals that sustain the predators — the cougar, wolf, coyote, and buzzard. The sheep are the archetypal prey, wholly dependent on human protection. Hugh realizes that "sheep, above all other domestic animals, are dependent upon men for their very lives" (004-018). Unlike cattle or horses, sheep have no natural defenses. The wild prey — deer, elk, rabbits — are caught in the same web but possess instincts for evasion that the domestic flocks have lost.

## Manifestations

The prey-predator relationship appears in nearly every chapter. The sheep flock is beset on all sides: "the beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep" (004-022). The cougar Broken Fang hunts deer, elk, and eventually turns to the sheep when wild game fails: he recalls "the deer that had died in his talons, even the elk that he had laid low with one lightning, terrible bite to the throat" (007-025).

The buzzards occupy a special role — they are not hunters but undertakers, feeding only on the already dead: "for disaster to the forest creatures always means triumph to the buzzards" (008-037). The coyotes, in contrast, are skulking opportunists: "all the larger beasts of prey had now gone to their lairs and to sleep, but these gray skulkers still lingered about the flock" (009-006).

## Key Characters/Events

The primary prey in the novel are the sheep, whose vulnerability drives the entire plot. A lamb is nearly killed by Broken Fang in the river driftwood (008-028). The deer, the porcupine, and the smaller forest creatures all serve as prey for the novel's many predators. Broken Fang's hunting experiences illustrate the full spectrum: he has killed cattle, fought a wolf in a starving winter, and taken a wild boar (007-025).

## Philosophical Significance

Prey is not a moral category but an ecological position. The novel does not sentimentalize prey — the sheep's vulnerability is simply a fact of nature, and Hugh's role as shepherd is to stand between them and the predators. The eternal pattern — "the flocks still wander on the hills ... the beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep" (004-022) — connects Hugh to every shepherd since the nomads of Asia. In protecting the prey, Hugh finds his own redemption.

## Key Passages

> "All the basic elements of life were here: the flocks, the herder's little shelter, the fire glowing in the falling darkness, the watchful shepherd dog guarding the lambs, the beasts of prey lurking in the growing shadows." (004-021)

> "The cities had been built in a day ... The flocks still wander on the hills. Men catch new fancies, follow new teachings, build new orders, and pursue new ways. The firelight of the herder still glows in the twilight. Civilization rises and falls like the tide. The beasts of prey still lurk in the thickets to slay the sheep." (004-022)
