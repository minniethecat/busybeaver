## Definition

In *Shepherds of the Wild*, lightning serves as the novel's primary metaphor for instantaneous, irresistible natural power. It describes both the physical force of storms and the deadly swiftness of predators, bridging the gap between meteorological violence and the violence inherent in the wild's food chain.

## Manifestations

The lightning motif appears most prominently in descriptions of the great predators. Growl-in-the-throat, the black bear, is characterized as "living, forked, chain lightning in a hand-to-hand fight" (009-006)—a creature whose peaceful, honey-grubbing demeanor conceals devastating combat ability. Broken Fang, the cougar, is described as having felled elk "with one lightning, terrible bite to the throat" (011-003), his killing speed compared to a lightning strike.

The coyote Running Feet understands the danger of the shepherd dogs in similarly electrical terms: the Barking One is "fast as lightning and as terrible as the she-wolves at the lair mouth" (015-004). Even Landy Fargo's outlaw band is described with the metaphor: "once they get goin', they'd sweep through that flock of woollies like lightning" (021-008).

Hugh Gaylord himself experiences a moment of primal recognition when he sees the cougar's eyes in the darkness: "Man knows the terror of lightning, the dread (as well as the love) of fire, the fear of flooding waters, but he knows those two twin circles best of all" (012-002). The twin glowing eyes evoke the same deep fear as lightning—both are forces beyond human control.

## Key Characters/Events

**Broken Fang**, the great cougar, is most closely associated with lightning imagery. His killing bite is "lightning, terrible," and his eyes glow with the blue-green "flame that plays about an alcohol burner" (012-002). **Growl-in-the-throat** (the black bear) is "chain lightning" in combat despite his otherwise comical demeanor. **Running Feet** (the coyote) perceives sheepdogs as lightning-fast threats.

## Philosophical Significance

Lightning in the novel represents the irreducible, untamable core of wild nature—the power that cannot be negotiated with or domesticated. It is associated with the "first great Terror" (012-002), a primordial fear that connects modern humans to their ancient ancestors. The metaphor collapses the distinction between natural forces and animal predators, suggesting that both operate according to the same implacable laws. For the shepherds, lightning is what they guard against—the sudden, devastating strike from the darkness that can undo in an instant what vigilance has maintained through the long night.

## Key Passages

> Growl-in-the-throat was a honey-robber and an eater of fat grubs; he was forgetful and awkward and given to long weeks of sleeping; but he was living, forked, chain lightning in a hand-to-hand fight. (009-006)

> The deer that had died in his talons, even the elk that he had laid low with one lightning, terrible bite to the throat would make a number not pleasing to contemplate by lovers of the wild game. (011-003)

> Man knows the terror of lightning, the dread (as well as the love) of fire, the fear of flooding waters, but he knows those two twin circles best of all. They carry him back to the first great Terror; they waken memories from the depths of the germ plasm. (012-002)
