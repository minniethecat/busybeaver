# Rabbit


## Overview

The rabbit is the most universal prey animal of the [[wilderness|Smoky Land wilderness]] — a creature whose abundance and vulnerability make it the foundation of the forest's predator economy. From the "little, deadly, white-fanged mink following a rabbit's trail beside the river" to the mighty [[species-grizzly|grizzly]], every flesh-eater in the novel depends at some point on the rabbit and its kin (004-005). The novel also introduces the "rock rabbit" or pika, a high-country cousin whose squealing death at a cougar's claws becomes a small but piercing moment of tragedy.

The rabbit and the pika share a particular narrative function: they are the creatures against whom the novel's great predators measure themselves. When [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] the cougar scorns "the scurrying gophers, the timid rabbits, and the furry pikas in the rocks" (023-012), he is practicing the arrogance that will shortly undo him. The small creatures he dismisses turn out to possess a power he cannot anticipate — the power to disrupt his hunt with nothing more than a "shrilly shriek" in the stillness of the high mountains.

## Natural History

Rabbits and hares (family Leporidae) are small herbivorous mammals characterized by long ears, [[dominance|power]]ful hind legs, and high reproductive rates. The pika (*Ochotona princeps*), called "rock rabbit" by the mountaineers, is a smaller relative that inhabits talus slopes at high elevations. Both are staple prey across the [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] food web. The novel presents the rabbit primarily through the perspective of its [[predator|predators]] — the mink, the coyote, and especially [[broken-fang|the cougar]] [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]], for whom a night without even a rabbit caught is a night of hunger and humiliation.

The pika (*Ochotona princeps*) is not a true rabbit but a member of the family Ochotonidae — a small, tailless lagomorph that inhabits rock slides and talus slopes at elevations between 8,000 and 13,000 feet in the Rocky Mountains. Unlike rabbits, pikas do not burrow; they nest in natural crevices among the rocks and gather "haypiles" of dried vegetation to sustain them through the alpine winter. Their high-pitched alarm calls — the "shrill shriek" that startles the cougar in Chapter XXIII — are a well-documented behavioral adaptation: pikas act as sentinels for their talus-slope communities, alerting neighbors to the presence of predators.

The novel groups the pika with its fellow "lesser folk" — creatures that the great predator dismisses but that collectively form the ecological substrate of the high country: "He had always had only scorn for these lesser folk — the scurrying [[gopher|gophers]], the timid rabbits, and the furry pikas in the rocks" (023-012). This contempt is a narrative setup: the very creatures that seem least significant will prove decisive in the cougar's fate.

## Role in the Novel

The rabbit appears as the baseline of predatory success. When [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] returns from a night of hunting, the novel measures his failure in the simplest possible terms: "not even a rabbit had been caught to appease the [[hunger|gnawing hunger]] within him" (001-018). The rabbit is the minimum — to fall below it is to face [[hunger|starvation]].

The mink, one of the novel's smallest [[predator|predators]], is introduced in the act of "following a rabbit's trail beside the river" (004-005), establishing the food chain from its smallest link upward. The rabbit is everywhere and nowhere in the novel: a constant presence whose individual life matters little but whose collective abundance sustains the entire [[wilderness|wild]]erness.

The novel's most poignant rabbit moment involves not the common rabbit but the pika — the "rock rabbit." The cougar [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]] kills a pika, and its dying squeal becomes a small but resonant note of suffering:

> "It would have been surpassing poetic justice for it to have been the mate of the small, gray creature that had died so recently in his talons, squealing for the mother of his litter to come back to him." (002-031)

This passage gives the pika a voice and a family — transforming it from anonymous prey into a creature whose death matters, if only for a moment.

### The Pika's Revenge: A Structural Turn

The pika returns in Chapter XXIII in a structurally pivotal role. [[broken-fang|Broken Fang]], desperate and half-starved, stalks [[spot|Spot]] and the bighorn flock through the high passes. He has already "caught a little pika on the slide-rock" (023-002) the day before — a meager meal that has done nothing to satisfy him. As he creeps within striking distance of the sheep, his muscles coiled for the spring, a second pika intervenes:

> "It was only a little pika — a rock rabbit, as the mountaineers call them — such as the puma had killed the day before. It would have been surpassing poetic justice for it to have been the mate of the small, gray creature that had died so recently in his talons, squealing for the mother of his litter to come back to him. And his sharp squeal was just enough, and no more, to put high explosives under the sheep hunt." (023-014)

The pika's shriek — "really little more than a high-pitched squeak" (023-013) — triggers the cougar's over-strung nerves. He snarls involuntarily; the sheep scatter; the hunt is ruined. Bindloss constructs a miniature revenge tragedy in which the smallest creature in the scene — a rodent weighing perhaps six ounces — defeats the "monarch of the forest" by the simple mechanism of a reflexive shriek. The passage explicitly frames this as "poetic justice": the death of the first pika is avenged by the second, and the cougar's scorn for "lesser folk" is repaid in full.

The scene also demonstrates a subtle ecological truth: the pika's alarm call, evolved to warn its own kind of predators, inadvertently serves the bighorn sheep. In the interconnected web of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]], even the smallest creature's instinctive behavior can ripple outward to affect the largest dramas of survival.

## Named Individuals

No individual rabbit or pika is named in the novel. They appear as generalized prey, though the "small, gray creature" killed by [[broken-fang|the cougar]] is granted a moment of individualized pathos through its dying cry.

## Symbolic/Thematic Role

The rabbit represents the novel's concern with the food chain as both biological reality and moral structure. In [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]], everything eats and is eaten. The rabbit stands at the crucial middle tier: numerous enough to sustain the [[predator|predators]] above it, vulnerable enough to never be safe. Its abundance is its only defense.

The pika's death scene introduces a different theme: that even in a world governed by tooth and claw, individual suffering registers. The "squealing for the mother of his litter" is one of the novel's few moments of explicit sympathy for prey — a recognition that the rabbit, too, has bonds and fears that the predator's hunger [[species-deer|doe]]s not comprehend.

The novel also uses the rabbit in a moment of human characterization. When [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] first sees [[alice-crowson|Alice Crowson]], he notes that her high color was "not put on with a rabbit's foot" (006-026) — a wry observation that uses the rabbit as a figure for artificiality, contrasting the cosmetic with the genuinely [[wilderness|wild]].

The pika's structural role in Chapter XXIII introduces a third theme: the inversion of [[dominance|power]] hierarchies. The novel's food chain seems fixed — cougar eats pika, pika flees cougar — but at the critical moment the hierarchy reverses. The pika's involuntary squeak undoes the cougar's carefully planned stalk, and the "monarch of the forest" is humiliated by a creature he holds in contempt. The novel suggests that in [[nature|the wild]], power is never absolute and the small may undo the great by accident as readily as by design.

### The Food-Chain Spectrum

The novel organizes its predators along a explicit spectrum that places the rabbit — or more precisely, the predators who hunt it — in a clear hierarchical relationship. The hunting fever described in Chapter VI moves from smallest to largest:

| Tier | Predator | Typical Prey | Narrative Role |
|------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Smallest | [[mink|Mink]] (Little Death) | Rabbit, trout | River-margin hunter; anchor of the base |
| Small | [[coyote|Coyote]] | Rabbit, lamb | Constant skulker; coward but persistent |
| Medium | [[lynx|Lynx]] | Grouse, hare | Silent branch-climber of the dusk |
| Large | [[cougar|Cougar]] ([[broken-fang|Broken Fang]]) | Deer, elk, bighorn | Monarch of the hunt; aging and failing |
| Largest | [[grizzly|Grizzly]] | Cow elk, marmot | Undisputed king; fears nothing in a fair fight |

The rabbit and its kin stand at the foundation of every tier: "from the little, deadly, white-fanged mink following a rabbit's trail beside the river to the mighty [[species-grizzly|grizzly]], stalking a cow elk" (006-032). The rabbit is the common denominator — the prey that makes predation possible at every scale.
