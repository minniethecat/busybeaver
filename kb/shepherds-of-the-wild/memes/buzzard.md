# Buzzard (Turkey Vulture)


## Overview

The buzzard — the turkey vulture of the [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] skies — occupies a role unlike any other creature in the novel. It is not a hunter but a [[guardianship|watch]]er, a patient scavenger whose circling presence above the ridges serves as a constant reminder of [[nature|the wild]]erness's central truth: death is inevitable. In a world where every creature is either predator or prey, the buzzard belongs to neither category. It waits, and it knows. "Not for nothing [[species-deer|doe]]s the buzzard [[guardianship|watch]] all day from the clouds. *He knows*" (005-007).

## Natural History

The turkey vulture (*Cathartes aura*) is a New World vulture found throughout the Americas. A large soaring bird with a wingspan of up to six feet, it locates carrion by sight and by a highly developed sense of smell — uncommon among birds. In the novel's ecosystem, the buzzard serves as [[nature|the wild]]erness's undertaker, cleaning the landscape of the dead that the [[predator|predators]] leave behind. It is a creature adapted not to the thrill of [[hunting|the hunt]] but to its aftermath: the silence after [[broken-fang|the cougar]]'s claws release, the [[peace|stillness]] after [[cry-in-the-night|the wolf]] pack moves on.

## Role in the Novel

The buzzard appears in three distinct contexts, each deepening its symbolic meaning. In the first, [[pete|Pete the guide]] reflects on [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s surprising [[peace|calm]] in the face of death, and the buzzard becomes the novel's emblem of [[death|mortality]] itself (005-007):

> "Not for nothing does the buzzard watch all day from the clouds. *He knows*; and whoever listens to the wilderness voices knows too. The wild, despairing song of the pack, the wail of the coyote, even the murmur of the pines is a song of death, immutable and dark, at the end of their little days." (005-007)

The second appearance places the buzzard among the creatures untroubled by drought. The rattlesnakes on their sun-blasted rocks may "look dead as last year's leaves," deceiving even "the buzzard—in the sky" (018-004). The buzzard is patient; the drought [[species-deer|doe]]s not disturb its rhythms. What is a dry season to a creature whose meals are measured in carcasses rather than living prey?

Third, the buzzard serves as a figure of [[fear|terror]] in the mind of [[spot|Spot]] the [[bighorn-sheep|bighorn]] lamb. In a world where danger comes from above — the golden eagle, [[eagle|Swift Wing]] — even "the shadow of a lazy buzzard flicked across the meadow" is enough to make [[spot|Spot]]'s "little heart choke with [[fear|terror]]" (020-008). The buzzard may not hunt living lambs, but in the young animal's consciousness, any winged shadow is a herald of death.

## Named Individuals

No individual buzzard is named in the novel. The species appears as a generalized, symbolic presence rather than through specific characters. The buzzard is always "the buzzard" — singular and archetypal, as though [[nature|the wild]]erness needs only one to make its point.

## Symbolic/Thematic Role

The buzzard is the novel's purest symbol of death-awareness. Unlike [[broken-fang|the cougar]] or wolf — creatures that embody death as an active force, a pursuit and a kill — the buzzard represents death as knowledge, as patience, as inevitability. "He knows," the novel states, and the italics are significant. The buzzard's knowledge is not acquired through violence but through witness. It has seen every outcome, and it waits.

The idea that even "the murmur of the pines is a song of death" (005-007) extends the buzzard's symbolic domain. The buzzard [[species-deer|doe]]s not sing; it [[guardianship|watch]]es. But its patient circling teaches the same lesson that [[nature|the wild]]erness's every voice confirms: that all lives end, that "immutable and dark" conclusion waits at the end of every "little day."

The drought passage adds a layer of dark irony. The buzzard may be deceived by a rattlesnake playing dead — "even the buzzard—in the sky—is deceived" (018-004) — but the deception is temporary. The buzzard's patience outlasts all pretense. In the novel's world, death cannot be fooled forever.
