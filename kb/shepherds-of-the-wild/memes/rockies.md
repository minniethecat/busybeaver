# Rockies


## Overview

The Rockies (Rocky Mountains) are the defining geographical feature of *Shepherds of the Wild*, framing the entire narrative. The novel's setting — Smoky Land — lies "on the great shoulders of the Rockies — a high plateau, studded here and there by grim and lofty snow peaks" (001-027). This alpine wilderness serves not merely as backdrop but as an active force shaping the lives of every character, from Hugh Gaylord to the wild creatures of the forest.

The mountains are described as both beautiful and cruel, "a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter, where great glaciers gleamed in the morning light and snow fields lay unchanging above the line where the timber became stunted and died" (001-027).

## Role in the Narrative

The Rockies provide the stage for nearly every major event in the novel. Hugh Gaylord travels to the "far Rockies" (001-175) as part of the Old Colonel's experiment to cure his dissipation through wilderness immersion. His journey unfolds across the high plateau and its surrounding peaks.

The mountains also serve as the hunting ground for Broken Fang, the great cougar "that had already won a name through a thousand square miles of Idaho forest" (001-175). The high ridges and snow fields are where the sheep herds graze under Shep's watch, where Running Feet the coyote prowls, and where Fargo confronts Hugh in the climactic fire scene.

The landscape culminates in the novel's closing vision, with "the moon gleamed on the white peaks of the Rockies" (013-1269) during the final confrontation between Fargo and Hugh.

## Physical Description

The Rockies of Smoky Land are characterized by "rugged crags and impassable cliffs, deep gorges and dark, still canyons; miles of gray slide-rock and glossy grass slope" (001-027). The terrain includes:

- **Snow Peaks**: "Sentinel mountains grand and austere" (007-193) with permanent snow fields above the timberline
- **Glaciers**: Gleaming ice masses visible in the morning light
- **Rocky Eminences**: Outcroppings like the one where Spot stands watch — "an outcropping of the rocky skeleton of the hill emerging — like the end of a broken bone through the flesh — from the brown earth of the hillside" (011-765)
- **Timberline**: The elevation above which trees become "stunted and died" (001-027)

The wildlife includes grizzly bears, bull elk, Rocky Mountain goats, bighorn sheep, wolf packs, cougars, and coyotes — each occupying distinct ecological niches within the mountain landscape.

## Thematic Significance

The Rockies embody the novel's central wilderness theme. They represent both the danger and the redemptive beauty of untamed nature. The mountains are indifferent to human concerns — "through it all, dwelling like a spirit, there was a beauty that could not be denied" (001-027).

For Hugh, the mountains become a proving ground. The Old Colonel's vision of the "far Rockies" is a place where men are tested and either broken or remade. The closing image — "the moon gleamed on the white peaks of the Rockies" (013-1269) while Hugh sits redeemed beside his campfire in "the Land of Mighty Men" (031-1427) — confirms the mountains as the crucible of Hugh's transformation.

## Key Passages

> "In reality Smoky Land lay on the great shoulders of the Rockies — a high plateau, studded here and there by grim and lofty snow peaks. It was not a land for gentlefolk. It was a hard, grim place, a forbidding land where the sun was a curse in summer and the winds a stinging lash in winter, where great glaciers gleamed in the morning light and snow fields lay unchanging above the line where the timber became stunted and died." (001-027)

> "He saw the two long ridges that enclosed his particular part of the plateau, and the last light of day gleamed on their tall, white, snow-laden peaks. These were the high Rockies; sentinel mountains grand and austere." (007-193)

> "The fire blazed beside them, the shadows leaped and danced, far away the moon gleamed on the white peaks of the Rockies. The whole forest world was wrapped in impenetrable silence." (013-1269)

> "Even if he were dead — slain in some great stress in those far Rockies — he was more vital and alive in the Colonel's eye than he had ever been before." (031-1427)
