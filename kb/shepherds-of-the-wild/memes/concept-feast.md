## Definition

The feast — specifically the "feast of death" — is the novel's term for the most terrible of wilderness lusts: the orgy of killing that goes beyond honest hunting for food. It is "not the rational, honest killing of the hunt" but "a perfect orgy of slaying, not for food but from desire, the tearing-out of a hundred throats in so many seconds" (571-001). The first laws of the forest have decreed against it, marking it as the ultimate transgression of the natural order. In all the world of carnage and wickedness, the novel insists, "there is no debauch that is half so terrible" (571-001).

## Manifestations

The feast impulse is presented as a universal temptation among predators. The novel describes how "all forest creatures know this feast: they have an inborn passion for it that simply must almost tear them in twain" (571-001). The night is the predators' "time of triumph" — "the hour of glory when fang and talon and strength and stealth come into their own" (687-001). During these hours, the hunting fever "spares none of the flesh-eaters, and from the smallest to the great — from the little, deadly, white-fanged mink following a rabbit's trail beside the river to the mighty grizzly, stalking a cow elk in the thicket — they felt in their veins the age-old stir that is ever new" (487-001).

The novel's climax brings this theme to its darkest expression. As the sheep-killing dogs descend into their frenzy, the feast of death becomes a human-caused catastrophe: "The feast of death was to take place after all — the same delight of which Broken Fang, the puma, was even then dreaming beside the sheep camp on the far headwaters of Silver Creek. But men, not wild beasts, were to be the debauchers" (1097-001). The dogs, corrupted by human misuse, become agents of the very wilderness lust that the novel's wild predators — even Broken Fang — struggle to resist.

The feast reaches its apex in the novel's most harrowing scene: "The sheep were dying now. Already the scene was one of unspeakable carnage" (1317-001). The dogs "lingered to worry their dead before springing to a fresh victim," and the "pungent smell of blood" fills the meadow (1317-001, 1327-001). Alice stands alone against them, her "frail body and her pistol, oddly silent" the only barrier between the dogs and the "gratification of their lust" (1345-001).

## Key Characters

**Broken Fang**, the puma, embodies the predator's internal struggle with the death-feast impulse. He is the "monarch of the forest" who has "felled even the horned steer." He questions what laws could restrain him: "Many of the forest laws he had already broken; he might even yet break the law that forbade the death-feast — and why should he obey now?" (629-001). His internal debate between monarchal power and forest law mirrors the human characters' own struggles with restraint and transgression. The irony is sharp: while Broken Fang wrestles with the temptation, it is men — supposedly above the forest's bloody laws — who ultimately unleash the feast of death.

**Alice Gaylord** faces the feast of death as its principal human opponent. Standing in the midst of the carnage, "this slight, sobbing figure in the middle of the white band," she becomes the last defense against the dogs' "fury and their veins with lust" (1327-001). Her desperate resistance — "she alone stood in the way of the gratification of their lust" — places her in direct opposition to the primal force the novel has spent chapters developing (1345-001).

## Philosophical Significance

The feast of death is the novel's central concept for exploring the thin line between natural predation and unnatural slaughter. The wilderness operates by laws — hunting for food is honest, but killing for the joy of killing violates "the first laws of the forest" (571-001). This distinction allows the novel to honor the predator's role while condemning excess, and it provides the moral framework for the climax: when men become "the debauchers," they fall below the standards that even wild beasts struggle to maintain.

The concept also bridges the animal and human worlds. Hugh's pre-wilderness life is described in the vocabulary of debauch — he was "an honest young debaucher, taking his orgies rather seriously" (077-001). The feast of death in the sheep meadow is thus foreshadowed by the "orgies" of Hugh's city life, suggesting a continuity between urban dissipation and wilderness slaughter. Both are violations of natural law — one in the social realm, the other in the realm of tooth and claw. The novel's moral arc moves from the debauch of civilization to the feasts of the wilderness, asking whether either can be redeemed.
