# Greenwood Club

The Greenwood Club is the fashionable social club on the Atlantic seaboard from which [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh Gaylord]] is dispatched to [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]]. It represents the civilized world — wealth, comfort, and aimless leisure — that [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]] must leave behind to discover his true self in [[nature|the wild]]erness.

## Overview

"It is a far cry from the fastnesses of Idaho back to the lounging room of the Greenwood Club" (002-002). This opening of Chapter 2 establishes the vast distance — geographical, cultural, and spiritual — between [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s old life and his new one. The Club is where "all men knew fondly" the [[old-colonel|Old Colonel]] (002-002), the "gray, wise, venerable head of the board of governors" (002-002).

The Greenwood Club is [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s point of origin and the symbol of everything he transcends. Its name — "Greenwood" — ironically evokes the forest, yet the Club's greenwood is metaphorical: a place of cultivated leisure rather than [[wilderness|wild]] growth.

## Role in the Narrative

The Club appears only in flashback, in Chapter 2. The [[old-colonel|Old Colonel]], in "correctly tailored dinner clothes" (002-003), proposes [[hunting|the hunt]]ing expedition that will change [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s life. The contrast between the "lounging room" and the "dark forests" (011-004) of [[smoky-land|Smoky Land]] defines the novel's central journey.

[[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s membership in the Greenwood Club marks him as a man of his class — wealthy, athletic ("he could swing the Indian clubs a startling number of times" (004-003)), and fundamentally disconnected from the real world of labor and survival.

## Thematic Significance

The Greenwood Club embodies the thesis the novel sets out to disprove: that a man can find fulfillment in comfort and status alone. [[hugh-gaylord|Hugh]]'s transformation from clubman to shepherd is the novel's argument for the redemptive [[dominance|power]] of [[wilderness|wild]]erness and meaningful work.
