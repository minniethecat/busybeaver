# Mexican Herders

The figure of the Mexican herder runs through *Shepherds of the Wild* as both a social reality and a narrative device. In the early twentieth-century Idaho sheep industry, Mexican laborers performed the dangerous, isolated work of tending flocks in the high country — work that Anglo settlers largely refused to do. The novel engages with this ethnic dimension of frontier labor, sometimes reinforcing contemporary stereotypes and sometimes subverting them through individual characterization.

## Definition

In the novel's world, sheep-herding is coded as ethnic labor. Hugh is told bluntly: "Only dagoes and Mexicans sheep herders." The statement reveals the class and ethnic hierarchy of the range — sheep-herding is low-status work, beneath the dignity of white American men. Hugh's decision to become a herder is therefore doubly transgressive: he crosses both class and ethnic boundaries.

Fargo reinforces this hierarchy when boasting that Hugh "was giving all he possessed of time and skill to a job that as a rule only Mexicans and uneducated foreigners deigned to accept." The word "deigned" is bitterly ironic — the work is not chosen but forced by economic circumstance and social exclusion. The novel is aware of this irony, even when its characters are not.

## Manifestations

José Mendoça is the novel's most fully realized Mexican character — and also its most problematic. He embodies the stereotype of the treacherous foreign laborer: cunning, violent, and ultimately disposable. "The Mexican was the one man on whom Fargo felt he might rely. José had no ridiculous limits as to conduct, no notch of brutality and crime above which he would not go." This characterization reflects the period's racial attitudes, and the novel does not fully transcend them.

Yet the novel also provides moments of humanizing detail. José's face is "suddenly crafty," his speech carries an accent rendered phonetically, and his relationship with Fargo is one of mutual exploitation rather than simple servitude. When José is described as "a Mexican named José," the dismissive phrasing captures the casual racism of the range while also inviting the reader to see beyond it.

## Dan the Herder

The murdered herder Dan offers a counterpoint to José. Described as "a South-European, evidently of the class of shiftless and uneducated men from which most flock owners have to recruit their herders," Dan is a victim rather than a villain. His death — shot without warning while resting in his tent — exposes the vulnerability of the ethnic laborers who did the range's most dangerous work. "The murdered man looked like a South-European" — the phrase acknowledges both his foreignness and his humanity. Dan's "ludicrous look of bewilderment" at the moment of death is one of the novel's most quietly devastating images.

## Alice as Exception

Alice Crowson's role as camp-tender deliberately violates the ethnic and gender norms of the range. "His camp-tender was not a Mexican, nor yet a laboring man of a certain type, but a girl." Hugh's surprise registers the depth of the expectation: camp work was Mexican work, men's work, and certainly not work for the daughter of a flock owner. Alice's presence in this role is one of the ways the novel signals that its moral universe is not identical with the social conventions of its setting.

## Philosophical Significance

The novel's treatment of Mexican herders is a window into the racial and class structures of the frontier West. It is not a progressive treatment by contemporary standards — the stereotypes are real and the novel does not mount an explicit critique of them — but the sheer visibility of these laborers in the narrative serves a documentary function. The herder's life, whether Mexican or not, is the novel's ground truth: isolated, dangerous, poorly compensated, and essential. That these herders are "foreigners" only intensifies the novel's theme of exile and belonging.
