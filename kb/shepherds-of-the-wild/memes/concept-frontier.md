# Frontier

The frontier in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the shifting boundary where human civilization presses against the primeval wilderness of Smoky Land. It is both a geographical reality and a symbolic threshold — the place where "the last outposts of civilization" give way to "the end of the trails, and clear where the little rivers rose in great springs" (001-008). Unlike the classic Western frontier of Frederick Jackson Turner's thesis, which is defined by its movement and eventual closure, the frontier in this novel is permanent — a condition, not a stage.

## Definition

The novel's frontier is not the stereotypical Western boundary of settlement but something more layered. Smoky Land lies in a region that is legally "public domain" (001-008), yet its inhabitants are "few" and its maps scarce:

> The place was really startlingly large — distances are always rather generous in the West — but few were the maps that named it. Those that had gone to fish in its waters and hunt its mountains thought of it always as beyond — beyond the last outposts of civilization, beyond the end of the trails, and clear where the little rivers rose in great springs. (001-008)

The frontier is defined not by lines on paper but by presence: where "few frontiersmen had come to clear away the forest and till the meadows" (001-008), the wild still holds sway. The frontier is wherever human order becomes tentative. This definition has important implications: the frontier is not a place that can be permanently settled and thereby erased. It is a relationship — a quality of encounter between the human and the wild that renews itself with each new arrival.

## The Threshold of Belonging

The frontier manifests most visibly in the character of Hugh Gaylord, who crosses it in both directions. He arrives wearing "the outdoor clothes of a gentleman, which is but rarely the outdoor garb of the frontier" (003-021), marked as a tenderfoot by his very appearance. The frontier tests him: "the lean foresters, natives of the land, would not accept him" (002-014). On the frontier, stories told to outsiders "are usually, on the frontier, the 'feed' for tenderfeet" (002-014) — a form of gentle mockery that marks the boundary between those who belong and those who do not.

Alice Drayton embodies the frontier's other face — a white woman who "might stand the test of existence even on the frontier" (005-012), possessing "a suppleness, a strength and litheness of body" (005-012) that the wilderness demands. She is the proof that the frontier is not closed to civilization — it simply demands a different kind of civilized person, one who has learned the wilderness's terms. Alice's frontier competence unsettles the gendered assumptions that Hugh brings from the city: on the frontier, strength matters more than convention.

The novel thus presents the frontier as a sorting mechanism — a place that exposes pretense and reveals character. Hugh's gentleman's clothes, his "lack of frontier experience" that the foresters detected "just as certainly as the little people of the wild recognized the presence of the wolf pack" (002-014), mark him as an outsider. But the frontier does not permanently exclude him. His transformation over the course of the novel is precisely a frontier education — learning to read the wild's signs, to command respect from the creatures of the forest, to stand and face the fire.

## The Frontier as Contact Zone

The frontier is not merely a boundary but a contact zone — a place where different orders of existence meet, clash, and sometimes reconcile. The novel presents several such encounters:

| Contact | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Hugh × Wilderness | The city man encounters the primeval; transformation follows |
| Sheepmen × Cattlemen | Two economic orders compete for the same territory |
| Human × Animal | [[spread-horn|Spread Horn]] flees from the scent of man at the spring |
| Civilization × The Wild | The forest fire as the wild's ultimate assertion |

Each of these meetings occurs at the frontier — the zone where distinct worlds touch. The frontier is not a line but a space of negotiation, of testing, of potential violence and potential understanding.

## Fire and Transformation

The frontier finds its most dramatic expression in the forest fire that closes the novel. As the smoke thickens and the flames advance, Hugh and Alice stand at "the vague frontier of hearing" (028-008), listening to "the distant crackle of the fire" (028-008). The phrase "frontier of hearing" is significant — the fire creates a sensory boundary that maps onto the existential one. The fire-frontier becomes the crucible in which Hugh's transformation is completed — the point where the boundary between the man he was and the man he has become is finally and irrevocably crossed.

Alice, watching Hugh's stand against the fire, perceives the deeper meaning of the frontier across all human history: "She could see the Viking, leaving the white arms of his woman and following western stars; the frontiersman, striking out from his beloved hearth to seek new dominions" (029-016). This vision connects the Smoky Land frontier to the whole history of human movement — the frontier is not merely this particular boundary in this particular place but an eternal human impulse, the drive to cross thresholds, to face the unknown, and to be transformed by what lies beyond.

## Philosophical Significance

The frontier in this novel inverts the classic frontier narrative. Turner's thesis held that the frontier was the defining American experience because it forged democratic individualism from the confrontation with wilderness. But Turner also saw the frontier as temporary — a phase that ended with its closure. The Smoky Land frontier, by contrast, is permanent because it is defined not by the absence of settlement but by the presence of the wild. The wild cannot be fully domesticated; the forest fire cannot be fully controlled. The frontier endures.

This permanence has moral implications. If the frontier is a permanent condition rather than a temporary stage, then the task is not to close it but to learn to live within it. Hugh's journey is not about conquering the frontier but about becoming worthy of it — learning its languages, submitting to its demands, and ultimately standing with it rather than against it. The frontier is not conquered but entered.
