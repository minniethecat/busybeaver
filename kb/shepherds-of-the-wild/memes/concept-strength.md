# Strength

## Definition

Strength in *Shepherds of the Wild* is not an abstract virtue but a concrete physical reality that separates the living from the dead. It manifests in the muscles of the wolf, the swimmer fighting the current, and the Northern blood that gives Hugh Gaylord "a background of strength" (002-010). In the wilderness, strength is currency — it buys survival, dominance, and the right to feast.

## Manifestations

The novel portrays strength through physical ordeal. Hugh Gaylord plunges into dark waters where "the muscles of his body seemed to shiver and vibrate with strength" (008-019), fighting the icy river that grips him "with resistless strength" (008-025). The predators embody this quality most purely: it is "their time of triumph: stealth and strength, fang and claw, the stalk in the shadows, the leap, the blow" (006-032). Strength is not gentle — it is the force that drives the killing bite and the desperate escape alike.

## Key Characters/Events

Broken Fang the cougar represents the apex of predatory strength. When Hugh emerges from the river, even the great cat perceives that "this creature that rose up from the waters was but a fawn in strength compared to him" (008-031). Hugh's own strength is tested to its limit when rescuing a sheep from drowning, a feat that "would tax the last ounce of strength in Hugh's body" (008-015). He survives because "he had always been a strong swimmer, and he needed that strength now" (008-025).

## Philosophical Significance

The novel treats strength as a natural law rather than a moral quality. The strong do not boast; they simply prevail. The Northern blood that Hugh inherits carries "a background of strength" that his grandfather hopes will manifest (002-010), connecting physical power to ancestry and place. In the wilderness hierarchy, strength defines the order of things — the strong lead, the weak follow, and the dead are silent.
