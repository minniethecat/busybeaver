# Spirit

Spirit in *Shepherds of the Wild* is the intangible quality that separates the wilderness's true inhabitants from its visitors. It is not mere courage but a deeper attunement — a "certain hardihood of spirit" (002-014) without which the wilderness remains forever closed. "To follow the high trails, to seek the hidden people, to scale the cliffs and wade the marsh require a certain hardihood of spirit to start with" (002-014).

## Definition

The novel distinguishes between physical fitness and spiritual readiness. Hugh Gaylord arrives in Smoky Land fit — "his hands were hard and brown, his figure lithe, his face and neck were tanned in tennis court and golf links" (002-014) — yet the old woodsman sees immediately that Hugh's "spirit was dead within him" (003-021). The wilderness demands not the body of an athlete but a spiritual quality that no gymnasium can provide. "He would not be able at once to enter into the spirit of the land" (002-014).

## In the Wild Creatures

Spirit reveals itself throughout the animal kingdom of Smoky Land. The puma possesses "a sense of power and might" (008-001) that is the spirit of the apex predator. The wolf pack's "unconquerable spirit" (010-020) is matched only by "that with which the she-wolf guards her whelps" (010-020). Even the old black bear is "after all, the most lovable spirit in the forest" (008-016).

The hunting fever that drives all predators at nightfall is itself a manifestation of wilderness spirit — an "age-old stir that is ever new" (008-005) that spares none of the flesh-eaters. And the wilderness itself is described as possessing an animating presence: "through it all, dwelling like a spirit, there was a beauty that could not be denied" (001-009).

## The Voice of the Wild

The cougar's cry becomes the novel's most direct expression of wilderness spirit. When Hugh first hears it, it "typified to him the very spirit of the wilderness" (007-011):

> It was the voice not alone of a hungry creature, stalking in the shadows, but — in his thought — it expressed all the ancient terror of the darkness, the primeval forces that war with man. (007-011)

This cry — "one of the most distinctive and awe-inspiring of all the wilderness voices" (007-009) — is "not heard often. Many men have lived years in the forest without ever hearing it at all. But once heard it is never forgotten" (007-009). It becomes the auditory symbol of the spirit that Hugh must learn to face, and ultimately to embody.

## Hugh's Transformation

For Hugh, the recovery of spirit is his central transformation. Beside the camp fire, "new knowledge came to him, a realization of the great themes and purposes of existence never known before" (006-019). The Indian guide awakens to "the age-old intoxication of the falling night" (004-018), and watching him, Hugh begins to understand what he lacks. By the novel's end, Hugh has found "a spirit and pride and self-reliance" (011-008) that mark him as belonging to Smoky Land. The fight against the wilderness is "never done. Never the night descended but that the age-old battle cry of the wild — the howl of a wolf or a scream of a cougar in the gloom — would come soaring, eerie and wild past all telling, to his ears" (023-007). And Hugh welcomes it — for he has found the spirit to meet it.
