# Duty

In *The American Senator*, "duty" is the word that every character reaches for when they need to justify a course of action — whether noble, self-serving, or cruel. Trollope tracks the word's deployment across the social spectrum to reveal how moral language functions as social currency, capable of legitimizing everything from genuine sacrifice to calculated self-interest.

## Definition in Context

Duty in Trollope's world is simultaneously a genuine moral commitment and a rhetorical resource. When John Morton says "I think that it is the duty of a country gentleman to ask clergymen to his house" (009-004), he is speaking sincerely about the obligations of rank. But the same word in the mouth of his grandmother — the Honourable Mrs. Morton — becomes an instrument of social enforcement. Her "great idea of her duty" leads her to "hate everybody who differed from her with her whole heart" (008-005). The concept is capacious enough to contain both impulses.

The novel's key insight is that duty-talk is unequally distributed across the social hierarchy. Those with power invoke duty to preserve the order that benefits them; those without power invoke it to dignify their submission. The word is never neutral — it always carries the weight of the social position from which it is spoken.

## Key Passages

> "She had a great idea of her duty, and hated everybody who differed from her with her whole heart." (008-005)

Trollope's devastating characterization of Mrs. Morton — one of his finest satirical portraits — collapses the distance between moral conviction and moral arrogance. Her "great idea of her duty" is indistinguishable from her sense of superiority, and the two emotions reinforce each other to produce a character who is simultaneously righteous and monstrous.

> "I think that it is the duty of a country gentleman to ask them to his house. It shows a proper regard for religion." (009-004)

John Morton's version of duty is more benign but no less revealing. Duty here means performing the rituals of social leadership — inviting the clergyman, attending church, maintaining appearances. It is duty as social performance rather than moral imperative.

> "She had long known that it was her duty to marry, and especially her duty to marry well." (012-017)

Arabella Trefoil's internal monologue exposes the cynicism that the language of duty can mask. Her "duty" is indistinguishable from worldly calculation, and Trollope's irony is directed at a society that teaches young women to call their market participation by the name of moral obligation.

> "I have my duty to do whether it's painful or not." (013-023)

Lady Augustus's invocation of duty is the most transparently manipulative — she uses the word to pressure her daughter into a marriage she knows is against her daughter's wishes. Duty here is simply the exercise of parental power dressed in moral language.

## Character Perspectives

### The Honourable Mrs. Morton
For Mrs. Morton, duty is a closed system that always validates her own preferences. Her charitable giving — "To two charities she gave annually £5 per annum each. Duty demanded it, and the money was given" (008-005) — reveals the minimalist calculation at the heart of her morality.

### Arabella Trefoil
The most honest character in the novel about what "duty" actually means in the marriage market. She recognizes that her "duty to marry well" is a social script, not a moral imperative, and she follows it with a clear-eyed cynicism that Trollope finds more admirable than hypocritical piety (012-017).

### John Morton
Represents duty as the unexamined performance of social obligation. His sense of what a country gentleman owes to his position is genuine but shallow — it extends to inviting clergymen to dinner but not to examining whether the system that gives him his position is just.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer for whom duty cannot be separated from justice. When he questions the rector's arrangement — "if a man can't do his duty he has to give way to someone who can" (012-013) — he exposes the gap between English duty-talk and American notions of contractual fairness.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of duty is neither cynical nor naive. He recognizes that the concept is indispensable — a society without some shared notion of obligation would be ungovernable — while also exposing the ways in which the language of duty serves as a cover for self-interest. The novel's moral education consists in learning to distinguish between the two.

The transatlantic dimension is again crucial. Senator Gotobed's American perspective treats duty as a matter of contract and performance: you do your job, you earn your pay, and if you cannot do your job, someone else should. The English characters treat duty as a matter of station and inheritance: you do what your position requires, whether or not the result is just. Neither perspective is presented as wholly adequate, but the tension between them generates the novel's richest moral inquiry.
