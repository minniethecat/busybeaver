# Mrs. Masters

Mrs. Masters is one of Trollope's great domestic tyrants—a woman whose energy, intelligence, and desperation, finding no outlet beyond the household, have curdled into something corrosive. She is the novel's most vivid study of what class anxiety does to women who are expected to manage households without the resources that management requires.

## Background & Social Position

Mrs. Masters is the second wife of the Dillsborough attorney, stepmother to Mary, and mother of several younger children. Her position is defined by insufficiency: insufficient income to maintain the appearance of gentility, insufficient social standing to command the deference she craves, insufficient control over a household that includes a stepdaughter whose quiet superiority she cannot match.

Her anxiety about Mary's marriage is not mere meddling. She understands, more clearly than her husband, that an unmarried daughter is a financial liability and a social embarrassment. Her insistence that Mary accept Lawrence Twentyman is cruel, but it is not irrational—it is the logic of a woman who has learned that sentiment is a luxury the precarious cannot afford.

## Personality & Character

Mrs. Masters is defined by her energy, which has no constructive outlet. She manages, schemes, nags, and complains because these are the only forms of agency available to a woman in her position. Her attacks on her husband—"What time did you come home on Saturday night;—or Sunday morning I mean?" (014-003)—are not questions but indictments, and their relentless quality is the sound of a woman battering against the walls of her life.

Yet Trollope does not make her simply a monster. Her concern for her own children's prospects is genuine; her frustration with her husband's professional timidity is understandable; her resentment of Mary's quiet superiority is human. She is what happens when intelligence and energy are confined to a sphere too small to contain them.

## Key Relationships

- **Mary Masters**: The stepdaughter whose existence is a daily reproach. Mary's gentility—acquired at Bragton, confirmed by Lady Ushant's affection—is everything Mrs. Masters wants for her own daughters and cannot provide. Her persecution of Mary is the dark side of maternal ambition.

- **Mr. Masters**: The husband whose professional indecision and social timidity drive her to fury. Mrs. Masters is not wrong about his weaknesses—he is indecisive, he is timid, he does sacrifice the family's interests to his own comfort—but her method of addressing them makes everything worse.

- **Lawrence Twentyman**: The suitor she champions. Mrs. Masters sees in Twentyman what her husband also sees—a solvent, respectable match for Mary—but she pursues the match with an aggression that ultimately defeats its purpose.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Masters is the novel's most concentrated study of domestic unhappiness as a structural rather than personal phenomenon. She is not wicked by nature—she is made wicked by a system that gives intelligent women of her class no sphere of action beyond the household and then condemns them for the form their intelligence takes.

## Significance

Through Mrs. Masters, Trollope asks what becomes of women whom the class system places in positions of chronic insufficiency. Her cruelty is real, and the novel does not excuse it—but it shows the conditions that produce it, and in doing so it extends its social criticism to the domestic sphere that is both the novel's subject and its setting.
