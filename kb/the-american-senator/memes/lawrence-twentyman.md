# Lawrence Twentyman

Lawrence Twentyman is the novel's most sympathetic study of social aspiration and its limits. He has land, income, the hunt subscription, the red coat—everything the county's code says should make him a gentleman. Yet the gentlemen do not receive him, and his inability to understand why is both his tragedy and his innocence.

## Background & Social Position

Twentyman is "quite the gentleman-farmer. He possessed over three hundred acres of land, … and was more than a yeoman with an unaccented name" (001-007). His father was a farmer known at the Bush Inn, and Lawrence has improved upon his inheritance—more land, better farming, the hunt subscription that marks his ambition. But the class barrier he seeks to cross is not one that land alone can breach.

He "paid twenty-five pounds annually to the hunt, which entitled him to feel quite at home in his red coat" (001-007)—yet the feeling of being at home is not the same as being at home. The older gentry call him "Larry" without permission, and Lord Rufford never invites him to dine. His subscription purchases access to the hunt field but not to the houses beyond it.

## Personality & Character

Twentyman is defined by his transparency. He is "fair and above board" (007-025) in his courtship of Mary Masters, appealing not to her feelings—which he barely considers—but to the propriety of his suit. He has her parents' approval, his finances are sound, his intentions are honorable: what more could a woman in Mary's position want?

His inability to understand Mary's hesitation is not stupidity but a kind of innocence. He has internalized the market's logic so completely that he cannot imagine a rejection based on anything other than a superior offer. When Reginald Morton emerges as his rival, Twentyman's confusion gives way to a resigned dignity that is the closest he comes to self-knowledge.

## Key Relationships

- **Mary Masters**: The attorney's daughter whose refusal of his proposal is the novel's most sustained examination of what the marriage market cannot price. Twentyman's love for Mary is genuine in its way—he genuinely wants her, genuinely believes he would make her happy—but his inability to see her as anything other than an object of pursuit limits his understanding (003-022, 006-022, 007-025).

- **Lord Rufford**: The aristocrat whom Twentyman admires and emulates but can never equal. Their relationship is one of patronage without friendship—Twentyman rides in Rufford's hunt, follows Rufford's lead, but is never invited to Rufford's table (001-007).

- **Mr. Masters**: The attorney who approves Twentyman's suit and whose own class anxiety mirrors Twentyman's. Masters sees in Twentyman a version of himself—a man of professional competence and social ambition, respected but not quite received.

- **Reginald Morton**: The rival whose class position (a Morton, an Oxford man) gives him advantages Twentyman cannot match. Their rivalry for Mary is unequal not because Twentyman is less worthy but because the system weights birth over worth.

## Role in the Novel

Twentyman embodies the class system's cruelty in its most benign form. He has done everything right—worked hard, acquired land, followed the codes, paid his subscription—and it has not been enough. His failure is not personal but structural, and his dignity in accepting it is the novel's tribute to the men the system excludes.

## Significance

Through Twentyman, Trollope examines the limits of Victorian social mobility. Land and income can be acquired; acceptance cannot. The hunt subscription buys the red coat but not the dinner invitation, and the difference between the two is the distance between aspiration and belonging. Twentyman is not a tragic figure—he is too solid, too sensible for tragedy—but he is a figure of genuine pathos, and the novel's treatment of him is one of its most quietly devastating achievements.
