# Manners and Etiquette

Manners in *The American Senator* are never merely matters of politeness. They are the grammar of class identity, the mechanism by which insiders are distinguished from outsiders, and the medium through which social power is exercised without appearing to be exercised at all. Trollope's attention to the minutiae of social conduct — who calls whom by what name, who is invited to which dinner, who may ride across whose land — is not fastidiousness but precision: in the world he describes, manners are destiny.

## Definition in Context

The novel's social world is governed by codes that are nowhere written down but everywhere understood. Lawrence Twentyman's predicament — he owns land, he pays his hunt subscription, he wears the red coat, and still he does not get into the gentry's houses (001-007) — is a study in the limits of what manners can accomplish. He has mastered the outward forms of gentility, but the inner circle remains closed to him because manners, in the end, cannot compensate for birth.

The American presence exposes the arbitrariness of English manners by refusing to observe them. Senator Gotobed's directness is not rudeness — it is the application of different codes, and the English characters' discomfort with his manner reveals how dependent their social order is on everyone agreeing to play by the same rules. When Mrs. Morton says "Of course an American member of Congress can't be an English gentleman" (008-030), she is making a statement about manners that is also a statement about power: the forms of deference that constitute English gentlemanliness are not universal but local, and the Senator's failure to perform them exposes them as conventions rather than natural laws.

## Key Passages

> "He not only rode well to hounds but paid twenty-five pounds annually to the hunt, which entitled him to feel quite at home in his red coat." (001-007)

The red coat is the uniform of gentlemanly belonging, earned through the payment of a subscription. But "feeling quite at home" in it is a psychological state that the subscription cannot guarantee — Twentyman wears the coat but never quite inhabits it, and his uncertainty is itself a social marker.

> "A man should not have his Christian name used by every Tom and Dick without his sanction." (001-007)

The use of Christian names is Trollope's most precise instrument for mapping social boundaries. When Mr. Runciman calls Twentyman "Larry," he is asserting an intimacy that Twentyman finds presumptuous — but the intimacy is real, born of a shared history that predates Twentyman's ascent. The conflict between earned familiarity and claimed status is irresolvable.

> "She was the daughter of a Viscount, a fact which she never forgot for a single moment, and which she thought gave her positive superiority to all women who were not the daughters of Dukes or Marquises, or of Earls." (008-005)

Mrs. Morton's genealogical calculus is a parody of manners reduced to mathematics. Her sense of her own superiority is so complete that it constitutes a form of innocence — she is incapable of seeing that the system she inhabits might appear absurd to an outside observer.

## Character Perspectives

### Lawrence Twentyman
The man who has mastered the forms of gentility without being able to claim its substance. His manners are correct, his position is precarious, and his awareness of both facts is the source of his dignity and his pain.

### The Honourable Mrs. Morton
Manners as weapon. Her politeness is perfect, her rudeness is devastating, and both are exercised with the assurance of someone who has never questioned her right to judge.

### Senator Gotobed
The outsider whose failure to observe English manners is a form of critique. He is not impolite — he simply does not recognize the authority of codes he has not consented to, and his presence exposes the coercive dimension of manners that insiders have learned not to see.

### Arabella Trefoil
The professional performer of manners, whose entire social existence depends on her ability to navigate codes she understands perfectly. Her tragedy is that perfect manners cannot compensate for the absence of the fortune that would make manners unnecessary.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of manners reflects his understanding that social codes are simultaneously necessary and absurd. A society without manners would be unlivable; a society governed entirely by manners is unjust. His realism consists in representing the codes with precision while never pretending that they are anything other than human inventions, subject to the same vanities and cruelties as the people who enforce them.
