# The Marriage Market

The marriage market in *The American Senator* is a system in which romantic feeling provides the vocabulary but economic calculation supplies the grammar. Trollope's couples negotiate their futures with the precision of commercial contracts, and the novel's honesty about the financial foundations of marriage constitutes one of its most enduring achievements as social criticism. The market links [[dillsborough|the rural gentry]], [[london|London society]], and the transatlantic world of [[senator-gotobed|American observers]] in a web of strategic calculation.

## Definition in Context

In Dillsborough, marriage is never a private transaction between two individuals. It is, rather, a family enterprise in which parents, guardians, and interested neighbors all have stakes. Lady Augustus Trefoil manages Arabella's marital prospects as a military campaign, identifying targets and coordinating operations with tactical precision. The Masters family discusses Lawrence Twentyman's suit at the dinner table, Mrs. Masters acting as chief strategist and Mr. Masters as reluctant financier. Even the romantic Reginald Morton cannot propose to Mary Masters until his inheritance is secured — love may inspire the question, but property enables the answer.

The market operates on two currencies: money and status. The ideal match exchanges one for the other — a wealthy manufacturer's daughter for an impoverished lord, or a titled gentleman for a merchant's fortune. Pure money-for-money matches are respectable but uninspiring; pure love matches, without financial foundation, are viewed as reckless. Arabella Trefoil, the novel's most clear-eyed participant in the market, understands that her beauty and her aunt's title are her only capital: "Her own fortune on her marriage had been small, but now she was in possession of that which the world would regard as considerable wealth" (008-005).

## Key Passages

> "She had long known that it was her duty to marry, and especially her duty to marry well. Between her and her mother there had been no reticence on this subject." (012-017)

Arabella Trefoil's calculation is not coldness but clarity. Without a fortune of her own, she must trade her beauty and connections for security. The absence of "reticence" between mother and daughter is Trollope's indictment of a system that has made marriage into commerce.

> "Her own fortune on her marriage had been small, but now she was in possession of that which the world would regard as considerable wealth." (008-005)

The Lady Augustus's own experience of the marriage market — a small fortune, a titled but worthless husband — has made her the strategist she is. She understands that the market punishes the romantic and rewards the calculating.

> "He would give sage advice to all he could to promote the marriage. He would give sage advice to his daughter as to the wisdom of accepting such a man." (006-022)

Mr. Masters's promotion of Twentyman's suit reveals the parental role in the marriage market. His "sage advice" is a calculus of security over sentiment — Twentyman is solvent, respectable, and willing, which is more than most women in Mary's position can expect.

## Character Perspectives

### Arabella Trefoil
The novel's most adept player of the marriage market. She pursues John Morton as an acceptable compromise, then transfers her efforts to Lord Rufford when a better prospect appears. Her tragedy is not that she is calculating — the market demands calculation — but that her calculations are ultimately unsuccessful.

### Mary Masters
The attorney's daughter who resists the market's logic. Lawrence Twentyman offers security, respectability, and parental approval — everything the market says she should want. Her refusal of him, and her eventual union with Reginald Morton, represents the novel's argument that love can sometimes defeat calculation, but only when material circumstances align.

### Lady Augustus Trefoil
The market's cynical priestess. Having learned its rules through her own disappointing marriage, she trains her daughter with ruthless efficiency. She is cruel, but her cruelty is the market's cruelty; Trollope does not exempt her from judgment, but he makes the system that produced her visible.

### Reginald Morton
The romantic who must nevertheless play by the market's rules. His love for Mary is genuine, but he cannot act on it until his inheritance from his grandfather is secured. The market permits romance only when it is properly funded.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of the marriage market is characteristically double-edged. He does not condemn the financial logic of marriage — he presents it as a fact of social life, as natural and unavoidable as the hunt or the parish church. His critique is directed instead at those who pretend the market does not exist, or who substitute romantic rhetoric for honest negotiation.

The novel's most sympathetic characters are those who acknowledge the market's reality without being reduced by it. Mary Masters understands what Twentyman offers and refuses it anyway, for reasons she cannot fully articulate. Reginald Morton loves Mary before his inheritance and marries her after it — the market shapes the timing but not the feeling. Even Arabella, for all her calculation, is portrayed with a compassion unusual in Victorian fiction: she is not wicked, merely realistic about the system in which she must operate.

The transatlantic perspective sharpens this analysis. Senator Gotobed's American egalitarianism makes him blind to the market's nuances — he cannot see that Mary's hesitation about Twentyman is not snobbery but a recognition that marriage into the class below one's own carries costs the market does not price. Trollope suggests that neither the English accommodation to the market nor the American denial of it quite captures the truth.
