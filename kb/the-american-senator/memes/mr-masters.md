# Mr. Masters

Mr. Masters, the Dillsborough attorney, occupies the novel's most instructive social position: he is a gentleman by profession but not by birth, respected but not received, and his acute awareness of the class boundary he straddles makes him the novel's most self-conscious character. He knows what it costs to be a gentleman because he has paid the price.

## Background & Social Position

Masters is the town's attorney, a man whose professional standing places him at the border of gentility. He has "any client that isn't a gentleman, you might as well put up your shutters" (003-014)—his practice depends on the trust of the county's landowners, and that trust requires him to perform gentility while never quite possessing it.

His household reflects his ambiguous position. His first marriage produced Mary, who was taken in by Lady Ushant and exposed to a gentility beyond her father's reach. His second marriage produced more children and a wife (Mrs. Masters) whose social anxiety is sharper and less controlled than his own. The Masters household is a laboratory of class feeling.

## Personality & Character

Masters is a man of genuine integrity constrained by social circumstance. He wants to do right—by his clients, by his daughter, by his profession—but "doing right" in Dillsborough means negotiating between competing class interests. His handling of the Goarly case exemplifies his dilemma: he knows Goarly is unpopular, knows defending him will cost him socially, and knows that refusing to defend him would violate his professional duty (004-009).

His weakness is not corruption but indecision. Mrs. Masters's constant pressure—to pursue more lucrative cases, to marry Mary to Twentyman, to assert himself against the condescension of the gentry—wears him down. His "weak decision" in the Goarly matter (014-016) is the decision of a man who has been worn down by forces he cannot control.

## Key Relationships

- **Mary Masters**: His daughter by his first marriage, whose quiet integrity is both a source of pride and a source of anxiety. Masters wants Mary to marry well—that is, securely—and his inability to understand her refusal of Twentyman reveals the limits of his moral imagination (006-022).

- **Mrs. Masters**: His second wife, whose social ambition and domestic energy dominate the household. Mrs. Masters is not wrong about the family's precarious position, but her methods—nagging, scheming, emotional manipulation—alienate the daughter whose happiness she claims to pursue.

- **Lawrence Twentyman**: The suitor Masters approves. Twentyman is solvent, respectable, and willing—everything a father in Masters's position should want for his daughter. His bewilderment at Mary's refusal is genuine and painful.

- **Lord Rufford**: The great man whose patronage (direct and indirect) shapes Masters's professional world. Masters needs Rufford's goodwill but resents his dependence, and this tension informs his professional life.

## Role in the Novel

Masters functions as the novel's most detailed study of the professional classes—men who are neither gentry nor trade, whose social position depends on education and conduct rather than land or title. His struggles with the Goarly case, with Mary's marriage, and with his wife's expectations dramatize the pressures on the Victorian professional man.

## Significance

Through Masters, Trollope examines the cost of respectability. Masters has achieved what the system allows a man of his birth—a profession, a household, a place in the community—and the cost has been a lifetime of managing the gap between what he is and what the gentry require him to appear to be. His story is not tragic, but it is sobering: a testament to the quiet labor of maintaining a position that can never be secure.
