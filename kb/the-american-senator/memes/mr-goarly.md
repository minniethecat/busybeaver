# Mr. Goarly

Goarly is the novel's most politically charged figure—a tenant farmer whose alleged poisoning of a fox transforms him from an obscure agricultural laborer into the center of a community's moral panic. He hardly appears in person, but his case structures the novel's examination of class, justice, and the limits of rural solidarity.

## Background & Social Position

Goarly is a tenant on Lord Rufford's estate, a man of no property, no education, and no social standing. His name is first mentioned in connection with a dispute over land use—he has "proposed to shoot the fox" (003-049) because the hunt's depredations damage his crops—and this conflict between agricultural necessity and sporting privilege defines his position.

He is what the rural social order exists to exclude. The hunt's temporary commons—"everybody rides across everybody's land out hunting" (010-030)—is built on the assumption that tenant farmers like Goarly have no right to object. When he does object, the community's response reveals the violence that underlies the consensual fiction of country life.

## Personality & Character

Trollope gives us almost nothing of Goarly's inner life. This absence is structural—Goarly does not matter as an individual; he matters as a test case. The novel is interested not in who Goarly is but in how the community treats him, and its treatment is damning.

He is presumed guilty before any evidence is examined. Mr. Nickem, the attorney's clerk, calls him "a great blackguard" (014-021) as a statement of identity rather than accusation. The hunt community's grief at the fox's death translates immediately into persecution of the suspected poisoner, and Goarly's poverty and unpopularity make him an acceptable target.

## Key Relationships

- **Lord Rufford**: The landlord whose hunt's privileges Goarly challenges. Rufford does not actively persecute Goarly—he does not need to; the community does it for him. Their relationship is one of structural antagonism, not personal conflict.

- **Mr. Masters**: The attorney whose professional duty compels him to take Goarly's case despite the social cost. Masters's handling of the case reveals both his professional integrity and its limits—he will defend Goarly, but he will not like it.

- **Senator Gotobed**: Goarly's unlikely defender. Gotobed's insistence on due process—"if Goarly didn't poison the fox, why shouldn't he claim damages?" (014-016)—is the only principled response to the community's rush to judgment, and it is telling that the principle comes from an American outsider.

## Role in the Novel

Goarly is less a character than a structural position—the point at which the rural social order's exclusions become visible. His case exposes the class logic of the hunt community, the limits of professional integrity in a hierarchical society, and the difficulty of achieving justice when reputation substitutes for evidence.

The fox he allegedly poisoned matters more, in the community's moral calculus, than any injustice done to him. This asymmetry is Trollope's indictment: a system that values a fox's death over a tenant's rights has lost its moral bearings.

## Significance

Goarly is Trollope's most radical creation—not because he is heroic (he is not; he may well have poisoned the fox) but because his very ordinariness exposes the injustice of a system that treats men like him as expendable. The novel does not romanticize Goarly, but it insists on his right to be judged by evidence rather than reputation, and that insistence is its most fundamental political claim.
