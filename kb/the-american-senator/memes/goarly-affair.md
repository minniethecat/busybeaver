# The Goarly Affair

The Goarly affair is the legal and social conflict that occupies much of the first volume of *The American Senator*, centering on a tenant farmer's alleged poisoning of a fox in Dillsborough Wood. What begins as a local dispute between [[lord-rufford|Lord Rufford's]] hunt and a discontented farmer escalates into a cause célèbre that exposes the fault lines of rural English society — and draws the bemused attention of [[senator-gotobed]], for whom the affair becomes a parable of English injustice.

## Origins of the Dispute

Mr. Goarly is a farmer who holds land adjacent to Dillsborough Wood, a covert used by the U.R.U. Hunt. For years, Goarly and other small farmers have resented the damage done to their crops and fences by the hunt's passage across their land. The hunt's claim of customary right — "Everybody rides across everybody's land out hunting" (010-030) — masks a fundamental inequality: large landowners like Lord Rufford benefit from the hunt's access, while small farmers bear its costs.

> "Goarly declares," said Mrs. Masters, conveying the farmer's grievance to the attorney's family. (003-048)

Goarly's position is that "if his lordship hunted over his land, his lordship should pay for the privilege" (003-049). This demand — that aristocratic privilege be priced rather than assumed — is the revolutionary kernel at the heart of the affair. It threatens not merely a single hunt but the entire edifice of customary rural relations.

## The Poisoning and Its Aftermath

When a fox is found dead — poisoned — in Dillsborough Wood, the hunt community immediately identifies Goarly as the culprit. The scene of discovery is described by Trollope with ironic gravity:

> "Between them they agreed that there should be no hunting till the wood had been thoroughly searched, and that the hounds should be taken home for the day. It was as though some special friend of the U.R.U. had died that morning, and the world could hardly be borne without him." (010-016)

The community's grief for the fox is genuine, but its response is punitive. Goarly is denounced as "that scoundrel" by the Botsey brothers (010-008), and the hunt community mobilizes to crush him legally, socially, and economically. The chapter title "Goarly's Revenge" (010-001) frames the poisoning as an act of retribution, suggesting that Goarly has already suffered wrongs the narrative has not fully acknowledged.

## The Legal Case

Goarly consults Mr. Masters, the Dillsborough attorney, seeking legal representation:

> "Has Goarly been to you?" asked Twentyman, the question revealing the speed with which news of the dispute had spread through the community. (003-050)

Mr. Masters's position is professionally awkward. Taking Goarly's case would antagonize Lord Rufford, the county's most powerful figure and a source of legal business. Yet declining would confirm the suspicion that the law serves the rich at the expense of the poor. "I think Goarly ought to take his lordship's offer," Mr. Masters advises cautiously (004-030), attempting to steer between his professional duty and his social prudence.

The legal details of the case — trespass, property rights, the admissibility of customary usage — are presented with Trollope's characteristic precision, but they are not the novel's main concern. What matters is how the case reveals the networks of power and obligation that structure rural life. Every character who takes a position on the Goarly affair — Mr. Masters, Lawrence Twentyman, John Morton, Senator Gotobed — reveals their own place in the social order by how they respond to it.

## Senator Gotobed's Intervention

Senator Gotobed's encounter with the Goarly affair is one of the novel's most significant transatlantic moments. The American senator sees in the dispute a confirmation of his critique of English institutions: a poor farmer persecuted by a powerful landlord, the law serving privilege, and the entire community closing ranks against an outsider who challenges customary rights.

Gotobed's questions cut to the heart of the matter: "I don't see why Lord Rufford is to ride over everybody because he rides over the Dillsborough Wood" (003-042). His American perspective exposes what is invisible to the English characters — that the hunt's claim of customary right is, in fact, a claim of class privilege enforced by social pressure rather than legal principle.

Yet Trollope does not make Gotobed's intervention straightforwardly heroic. The senator's understanding of the case is partial — he sees class conflict where the English see custom and mutual obligation, and his inability to grasp the complexity of English social relations limits his effectiveness as a critic. The Goarly affair is not simply a case of aristocratic oppression; it is also a case of a farmer who may indeed have acted illegally, a community whose solidarity is both admirable and oppressive, and a legal system whose procedures protect property more reliably than they protect justice.

## Significance

The Goarly affair is the novel's most concentrated treatment of the conflict between custom and law, between aristocratic privilege and democratic rights, and between English tradition and American critique. It prefigures the larger conflicts that structure the novel — the marriage market's exploitation of women, the hunt's exclusion of social climbers, the Foreign Office's preference for connection over merit — and it establishes the question that Senator Gotobed carries through the entire narrative: whether a society organized around inherited privilege can be just.

The affair also functions as a character test. Every major figure in the Dillsborough world is positioned by their response to it: Masters by his professional caution, Twentyman by his awkward loyalty to the hunt, Morton by his diplomatic evasion, Gotobed by his principled interference. Only [[lady-ushant]] stands apart, declining to join the mob without mounting a defense — her characteristic moral position expressed through withdrawal rather than confrontation.
