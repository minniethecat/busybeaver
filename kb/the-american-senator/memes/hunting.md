# Fox-Hunting

Fox-hunting is the central social institution around which Trollope structures rural life in *The American Senator*. It serves simultaneously as sport, class marker, communal ritual, and moral testing ground — revealing character through how individuals relate to the hunt and to one another within its codes. From [[dillsborough|Dillsborough's]] hunting stables to [[lord-rufford|Lord Rufford's]] legendary hounds, the hunt defines the calendar, the hierarchy, and the imagination of the county.

## Definition in Context

In the world of Dillsborough and Rufford county, hunting is not merely recreation but the primary arena of social organization. The "U.R.U., or Ufford and Rufford United Hunt Club" defines the social hierarchy of the region, and one's standing within it maps almost perfectly onto class position (001-007). Hunting structures the calendar, shapes male identity, and provides the shared language through which men of different classes interact (001-002). The Master of Hounds commands greater local authority than any civic leader: Captain Glomax, though "not rich" and "no one in particular," is "the greatest man in the county on hunting days" (009-018).

Trollope presents hunting as a genuine passion and a social theater simultaneously. The sport's rules and etiquette encode the unwritten constitution of English rural society — respect for property, deference to rank, and the shared fellowship of the chase. Yet the novel also exposes fault lines within this world through hunting disputes: conflicts over trespass (010-030), the preservation of foxes versus game (004-013), and the tensions between great landlords and smaller yeomen (003-042).

## Key Passages

> "That a man with a family should cling to a house in which he has once established himself is intelligible. ... But how it came to pass that he first got himself to Dillsborough, or his father, or his grandfather before him, has always been a mystery to me." (001-001)

The novel opens not with hunting but with the puzzle of Dillsborough's existence — yet within paragraphs we learn that Mr. Runciman's recovery strategy is "putting up new stables for hunting-horses, that being the special trade for which he now finds that there is an opening" (001-002). Hunting is what keeps the town alive.

> "He was quite the gentleman-farmer. ... He not only rode well to hounds but paid twenty-five pounds annually to the hunt, which entitled him to feel quite at home in his red coat." (001-007)

Lawrence Twentyman's subscription to the hunt is the price of his social ambition — it buys him the right to wear the red coat and claim gentleman status, yet the claim is never fully honored.

> "Everybody rides across everybody's land out hunting." (010-030)

This simple declaration carries the weight of an entire social philosophy — hunting creates a temporary commons that overrides property boundaries, a collective right that stands above individual ownership.

> "Between them they agreed that there should be no hunting till the wood had been thoroughly searched, and that the hounds should be taken home for the day. It was as though some special friend of the U.R.U. had died that morning, and the world could hardly be borne without him." (010-016)

When a fox is poisoned, the hunt community experiences collective grief. Trollope's irony is gentle but unmistakable — the death of a fox occasions more genuine communal feeling than any human loss in the novel's opening chapters.

## Character Perspectives

### Lord Rufford
The hunt's chief patron, "a rich man who thinks of nothing but sport in all its various shapes." His dominance over the hunting field mirrors his dominance over the county's land and politics (001-005). Hunting is the one context in which his wealth and privilege read as legitimate rather than merely assumed.

### Lawrence Twentyman
The gentleman-farmer whose relation to hunting epitomizes his social predicament. He rides well, pays his subscription, wears the red coat — but still does not get into the houses of the gentry. Mr. Runciman, the innkeeper who knew his father, still calls him "Larry" (001-007). Hunting offers Twentyman access to a world that ultimately excludes him.

### John Morton
The absentee squire who brings Senator Gotobed to a hunt meet, hoping to display "the best side of hunting" to his American guest (010-038). Morton's attempt to mediate between English tradition and American observation reveals his own uneasy position between two worlds.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer for whom hunting becomes a lens through which to examine English social arrangements. His outsider's questions — about trespass, property rights, and class privilege — expose assumptions the English characters have never examined.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope uses hunting as a diagnostic tool for English society. The hunt is, in one sense, what he loves — a world of shared purpose, physical courage, and communal joy. Yet he also anatomizes its exclusions with clinical precision. Twentyman's subscription entitles him to the red coat but not to the dinner table; the hunt's democracy extends only to the field's edge.

The transatlantic dimension sharpens this commentary. Senator Gotobed's incomprehension of hunting becomes a way of seeing English customs from the outside. What appears natural and inevitable to the English characters appears arbitrary and exclusionary to the American observer. Trollope does not resolve this tension; he simply holds it before the reader, allowing both perspectives their full weight.

The [[goarly-affair|poisoned fox episode]] (010-016) crystallizes the novel's moral geography. The community's grief for the fox is genuine, yet their persecution of the suspected poisoner — a poor tenant farmer — reveals the class violence latent in their collective feeling. Hunting, for all its beauty and tradition, is also an instrument of social control.
