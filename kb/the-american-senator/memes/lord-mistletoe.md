# Lord Mistletoe

Lord Mistletoe appears only glancingly in *The American Senator*, but his two mentions are among Trollope's most economical acts of political satire. He is a peer who "devoted himself to parliamentary erudition" (060-003) in exactly the way one might devote oneself to stamp collecting—as a hobby rather than a calling—and his presence in the novel's margins is a quiet indictment of the political system that gives such men legislative power.

## Background & Social Position

Lord Mistletoe is a member of the House of Lords, inheriting his seat as he inherits his title—without qualification, examination, or obligation. His parliamentary activity is described not as work but as "devotion," a word that suggests hobby rather than duty. He is mentioned alongside tobacco as one of the things that define Lord Rufford's understanding of his fellow peers (060-037)—a juxtaposition that reduces political authority to personal taste.

His social position is secure in the way that only hereditary position can be. He need not accomplish anything, because his status does not depend on accomplishment. The novel's treatment of him is not hostile—he is presumably a decent enough man—but the casualness with which his political role is mentioned is itself a critique.

## Personality & Character

Trollope gives us almost nothing of Mistletoe's personality. This absence is the point. Mistletoe is defined entirely by his position—a Lord, a member of Parliament, a man of "parliamentary erudition"—without any suggestion that these attributes correspond to personal qualities. He is what the system makes of a man who inherits legislative authority: a gentleman with a hobby.

## Key Relationships

- **Lord Rufford**: His fellow peer, mentioned in the same breath as tobacco. Their relationship, such as it is, consists of shared membership in an inherited governing class whose authority requires no justification (060-037).

- **Senator Gotobed**: The novel does not bring them together directly, but Gotobed's democratic principles stand in implicit judgment of everything Mistletoe represents. The American senator who earned his position through election contrasts with the English lord who inherited his through birth.

## Role in the Novel

Mistletoe's role is structural rather than narrative. He does not appear in any scene; he is mentioned twice, both times in contexts that associate political authority with trivial pursuits. His function is to remind the reader that the English political system, for all its stability, places legislative power in the hands of men whose qualifications are purely genealogical.

## Significance

The two mentions of Lord Mistletoe are Trollope's most compressed political commentary in the novel. "Parliamentary erudition" is a phrase that sounds impressive and means nothing—like the hereditary principle itself, it substitutes form for substance. That Mistletoe is mentioned alongside tobacco completes the satire: political authority, in this system, is a matter of taste.
