# Inheritance

Inheritance in *The American Senator* is more than a legal mechanism — it is the organizing principle of a social world. Property passes from father to son, and with it passes identity, obligation, and moral character. Trollope treats inheritance as a lens through which to examine the deepest structures of English life: the relationship between land and power, the claims of family against those of justice, and the psychological burden of receiving what one has not earned.

## Definition in Context

The Morton family history, which Trollope narrates across the novel's early chapters, is a chronicle of inheritance and its discontents. The first attorney Masters "became learned in the law through the patronage of some former Morton" (002-011), establishing a multi-generational relationship of clientage that mirrors the feudal bonds of an earlier era. Old Reginald Morton's extravagance nearly destroys the estate; decades of minority and careful management restore it. The Bragton property is not merely a collection of fields and buildings but a repository of family identity that shapes the lives of everyone it touches — owners, tenants, lawyers, servants.

The novel's American perspective sharpens the question. Senator Gotobed comes from a country where inheritance is taxed and where no one assumes that the son of a rich man is entitled to anything. His presence forces the English characters to justify — or at least articulate — arrangements they have always taken for granted.

## Key Passages

> "It does not much concern us to know how far back should be dated the beginning of the connection between the Morton family and that of Mr. Masters, the attorney." (002-011)

Trollope's characteristic self-consciousness about narrative economy conceals a serious point: the Morton-Masters relationship is so old that its origins are lost. Inheritance here is not merely a transfer of property but a web of obligation that extends outward from the great house to touch every trade and profession in the neighborhood.

> "But that the squire of Bragton should be her promised husband was a marvel so great that from every short slumber, she waked with fear of treacherous dreams." (071-063)

Mary Masters's psychological response to her engagement captures the vertigo of upward mobility through marriage. Reginald Morton's inheritance of Bragton transforms him overnight from a reclusive scholar into a territorial magnate — and transforms Mary's social position with equal suddenness. The passage reveals how inheritance works on the imagination as much as on the pocketbook.

> "It never crossed her conscience that it would be wrong to cheat the revenue." (030-029)

Old Lady Ushant, "an honester or more religious or better woman" than any in Cheltenham, casually contemplates tax fraud to protect her legacy. Trollope's irony is gentle but precise: inheritance creates a moral universe in which preserving the family's wealth is a higher duty than obeying the law.

## Character Perspectives

### John Morton
The absentee heir who has inherited Bragton without inheriting its duties. His long absence in Washington has left him unfamiliar with his own estate and its tenants — he is an heir in law but not in spirit, and his attempt to reclaim his inheritance through marriage and residence is one of the novel's central plots.

### Reginald Morton
Represents inheritance as unexpected transformation. His quiet life as the occupant of Hoppet Hall is overturned when circumstances make him the squire of Bragton. Trollope uses him to explore the question of whether inheritance — the accident of birth and death — can legitimately determine a person's entire social identity.

### Mary Masters
The attorney's daughter for whom inheritance works at one remove. Her marriage to Reginald Morton is, from one perspective, the fulfillment of a Cinderella plot; from another, it is the novel's most radical challenge to the heredity principle. She brings nothing to the marriage but herself — and Trollope clearly considers that enough.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer for whom inheritance is an archaic survival rather than a natural right. His lectures on English institutions implicitly question why land should pass from father to son without regard to merit, and why the community should accept the governance of men whose only qualification is birth.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's position on inheritance is characteristically moderate. He does not attack the principle — he himself was a beneficiary of the social order that inheritance sustained — but he insists on examining its consequences with unblinking clarity. The Morton inheritance has produced both the admirable (old Reginald Morton, "the best known and the best loved of all the squires in Rufford") and the problematic (John Morton's absenteeism, the Honourable Mrs. Morton's arrogance). The system is not evil, but neither is it just — it is simply the way things are, and Trollope's realism consists in refusing to pretend otherwise.

The transatlantic dimension is, again, the novel's sharpest critical tool. Senator Gotobed does not argue against inheritance — he simply fails to see why it should command deference. His incomprehension is a solvent that dissolves the deference on which the English system depends, and his presence in the novel suggests that the system's survival depends on never being examined too closely.
