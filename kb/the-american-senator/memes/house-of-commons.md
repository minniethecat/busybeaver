# House of Commons

The House of Commons appears in *The American Senator* primarily as the object of [[senator-gotobed]]'s critical scrutiny. In a lecture delivered toward the novel's conclusion, Gotobed subjects the British constitution — and the House of Commons as its representative institution — to a sustained American critique. The Commons becomes a lens through which Trollope examines the nature of representative government, the claims of democratic legitimacy, and the gap between constitutional theory and political practice.

## Gotobed's Critique

Senator Gotobed's lecture on the House of Commons is the novel's most explicit political argument. His central claim is that the Commons, despite its reputation as the "people's house," is in fact an assembly of landed and monied interests that represents property rather than persons:

> "I am told daily that you are proud of your House of Commons — and that you are so because it is the most perfect representative assembly that ever existed. But what does it represent? It represents the land, and the titles, and the wealth of the country — not the people." (077-011)

This argument crystallizes observations that Gotobed has been making throughout the novel. The Commons, like the [[hunting|hunt]], like the [[marriage-market]], like the [[foreign-office|Foreign Office]], is an institution that claims to serve the public while in practice serving the interests of those who already hold power. The reform that has made the Commons "representative" has not, in Gotobed's view, made it democratic.

## The Historical Argument

Gotobed grounds his critique in a historical comparison between British and American constitutional development:

> "At that time your members of the House of Commons were in truth deputies of the people. But the system has changed, and the deputies have become the masters." (077-012)

The argument is that the Commons has lost its democratic character through the accumulation of landed wealth and the professionalization of politics. What began as a check on royal power has become an instrument of class rule. Gotobed's historical narrative is simplified and polemical — Trollope does not necessarily endorse it — but it poses a question the novel takes seriously: whether representative institutions can remain representative when the representatives and the represented have divergent interests.

## Trollope's Position

Trollope's treatment of Gotobed's critique is characteristically balanced. The senator's arguments are given full and fair expression — the lecture occupies a substantial portion of the novel's final volume — but they are also subjected to testing through the reactions of other characters and through the novel's own narrative practice.

Several of Gotobed's criticisms are borne out by the novel's action. The [[goarly-affair]] has indeed shown that the law serves landed interests; the [[marriage-market]] has indeed shown that women without fortunes are systematically disadvantaged; the [[hunting|hunt]] has indeed shown that aristocratic custom overrides individual rights. In these respects, the novel endorses Gotobed's diagnosis of English institutions.

Yet the novel also suggests limits to Gotobed's perspective. His American egalitarianism leads him to underestimate the value of stability, custom, and gradual reform — values that the English system, for all its injustices, has preserved. His critique of the Commons is accurate in its particulars but reductive in its conclusions: the Commons is not simply an instrument of class rule, any more than the hunt is simply a device for excluding tenant farmers.

## The Commons in the Novel's Political Geography

The House of Commons occupies a position in the novel's political geography analogous to [[london]] in its social geography — distant, powerful, and largely invisible. No scene is set in the Commons; no Member of Parliament appears as a character (except, arguably, [[lord-rufford]], who presumably sits in the Lords). The Commons exists in the novel as an idea, a reference point, a symbol of the constitutional order that the novel's provincial action both depends upon and questions.

This absence is itself significant. The Commons is supposed to represent places like [[dillsborough]], but Dillsborough's inhabitants never think about it, never appeal to it, and are never affected by its deliberations — except through the general operation of the laws it passes. The gap between the representative institution and the represented community is the gap that Gotobed's critique identifies and that the novel's structure dramatizes.
