# Justice and Law

The law in *The American Senator* is a profession, a livelihood, and a mechanism of social control — but it is rarely, if ever, an instrument of justice. Trollope, who knew the legal world intimately, treats lawyers with the affectionate cynicism of an insider: they are honest men doing necessary work, but the system they serve is designed to protect property rather than to deliver fairness.

## Definition in Context

Mr. Masters, the "well-known old-established attorney of Dillsborough," embodies the legal profession as Trollope understands it. He has inherited his practice from his father and grandfather, who were "thoroughly trusted and employed by old Reginald Morton" (002-011). His relationship to the Morton family is one of hereditary clientage — he is, in effect, a vassal of the great house who happens to charge fees for his services. His knowledge of the law is genuine, but his function is to manage the property interests of the gentry, not to pursue abstract justice.

The Goarly case is the novel's test of whether the legal system can serve a poor man against the combined forces of "aristocracy and plutocracy" (016-001). Goarly's grievance — that Lord Rufford's hunt has damaged his crops and he deserves compensation — is reasonable on its face. But the system is rigged against him: the lawyers are gentlemen who hunt with Lord Rufford, the judges are landowners, and the costs of litigation are prohibitive for a tenant farmer. Justice, as Senator Gotobed observes, "is the same everywhere" in theory, but in practice it is distributed according to the claimant's ability to pay.

## Key Passages

> "I don't understand your laws, but justice is the same everywhere. If the one man is injured and the other man has injured him, the truth will soon make itself known if the matter is inquired into." (019-006)

Senator Gotobed's faith in universal justice is both noble and naive. The English legal system is precisely designed to prevent the kind of straightforward inquiry he imagines — its complexities and costs are barriers that protect the powerful from the claims of the weak.

> "Had he not threatened to shoot foxes? But, then, an attorney must live by lawsuits, and a poor man should not be trampled on." (003-056)

Mr. Masters's internal conflict over the Goarly case captures the moral ambiguity of the legal profession. He knows Goarly is "a brute" for threatening foxes, but he also knows that "a poor man should not be trampled on." His profession requires him to serve both principles simultaneously — and the contradiction is never resolved.

> "Who do you think is to pay the bill? The lawyer won't do it for nothing." (019-009)

The most devastating critique of English justice is economic. Law costs money, and the poor do not have it. The system that promises equal justice is, in practice, available only to those who can afford it.

## Character Perspectives

### Mr. Gregory Masters
The provincial attorney as honest professional and conflicted moral agent. He knows the law, serves his clients, and tries to do right — but the system he serves is structured to protect property, and his livelihood depends on maintaining that system.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer who refuses to accept that law and justice might be different things. His questions about the Goarly case are naive in their assumption that the law ought to serve justice, but Trollope clearly considers the naivety admirable rather than foolish.

### Goarly
The tenant farmer who represents the legal system's limit case. His claim is just, but his pursuit of it marks him as a troublemaker. The community closes ranks against him not because he is wrong but because he has challenged the natural order.

### Lord Rufford
The aristocrat for whom the law is simply an extension of his social position. He does not need to think about justice — the system is designed to produce outcomes favorable to his interests, and it does so reliably without his conscious intervention.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of the law reflects his deep knowledge of the legal profession and his moderate reformism. He does not propose radical change — the legal system, like the church and the aristocracy, is part of the fabric of English life — but he insists on exposing the gap between its claims and its performance. The law promises justice; it delivers property protection. The tension is structural rather than personal: Mr. Masters is an honest man, but the system he serves is designed to serve the interests of the Morton family, and his honesty cannot transcend the structure that employs him.
