# Hunting Meets

Hunting meets in *The American Senator* are not merely sporting occasions but the central social theater of [[dillsborough|Rufford county]]. At the meet, the hierarchy of the [[hunting|hunt]] becomes visible in physical space — who rides where, who speaks to whom, who wears the red coat and who merely watches from the roadside. For [[senator-gotobed]], the meet is an anthropological exhibit; for [[lawrence-twentyman]], a stage for social aspiration; for [[lord-rufford]], the natural theater of his dominance. No other event in the novel so completely reveals the structure of rural English society.

## The Ritual of the Meet

A hunting meet follows a precise choreography that Trollope records with the care of a participant-observer. The hounds are brought to an appointed place — a crossroads, a covert's edge, a convenient field. Riders assemble from the surrounding country: great landowners on blood horses, tenant farmers on sturdy hunters, tradesmen on whatever they can afford. The Master of Hounds directs operations; the whippers-in control the pack; the field waits for the signal to move off.

The meet's social function is as important as its sporting function. It is the one occasion on which the full hierarchy of the county assembles in a single place. Introductions are made, news is exchanged, reputations are assessed. A man's standing at the meet — whether he is greeted by Lord Rufford, whether he rides near the front or hangs back with the foot-followers — is a precise index of his social position.

## Key Meets in the Novel

### The Meet at Dillsborough Wood (Ch. 10)

The most dramatically significant meet occurs at Dillsborough Wood, where the discovery of the poisoned fox interrupts the day's sport. The collective grief of the hunt community — "as though some special friend of the U.R.U. had died" (010-016) — transforms the meet from a social occasion into a scene of communal judgment. The hunt does not simply mourn the fox; it mobilizes against [[mr-goarly|Goarly]], the suspected poisoner, and the meet becomes the occasion for a collective decision to destroy him.

> "It's that scoundrel, Goarly," said one of the Botseys. "It's that scoundrel Goarly" had been repeated again and again through the entire field. (010-008, 010-010)

The repetition of the accusation — Trollope's narrative voice making audible the chorus of condemnation — shows how the meet functions as a mechanism of social enforcement. The hunt community does not need evidence or legal process; at the meet, accusation is conviction.

### The Meet with Senator Gotobed (Ch. 8–10)

[[john-morton]] brings Senator Gotobed to a hunting meet in an attempt to display "the best side of hunting" to his American guest (010-038). The result is a collision of interpretive frameworks. Gotobed sees trespass, class exclusion, and the subordination of agriculture to sport; Morton sees tradition, community, and the beauty of English country life. Neither is entirely wrong, and neither can see what the other sees.

The meet becomes, through Gotobed's eyes, a tableau of English social arrangements. He notices that [[lawrence-twentyman]] in his red coat is not treated as an equal by Lord Rufford; that farmers whose land is crossed by the hunt have no effective right of refusal; that the entire spectacle depends on the willing subordination of many to the pleasure of a few. His observations are naive in their American egalitarianism, but they expose truths that the English characters have naturalized into invisibility.

## The Meet as Social Test

For several characters, the hunting meet is the site of decisive social tests:

- **Lawrence Twentyman** rides well and wears the red coat, but the gentry's failure to treat him as an equal at the meet confirms the limits of his social ascent. His subscription buys access to the field but not to the dinner table.

- **Senator Gotobed**'s response to the meet determines how the English characters — and the reader — judge his larger critique of English society. His incomprehension of hunting's beauty limits the force of his critique of hunting's injustice.

- **John Morton**'s awkwardness at the meet reveals his estrangement from the rural world of his inheritance. He is neither a true country gentleman nor a fully metropolitan diplomat; the meet exposes his intermediate position.

## Trollope's Perspective

Trollope loved hunting and wrote about it with intimate knowledge. The hunting meets in *The American Senator* are rendered with a precision that comes from lifelong participation. Yet his affection for the institution does not prevent him from anatomizing its exclusions. The meet is, in Trollope's treatment, both genuinely beautiful and genuinely unjust — a ritual that creates real community while systematically excluding most of the population from full membership in that community.

The transatlantic perspective is Trollope's device for holding both truths in view simultaneously. Senator Gotobed cannot see what is beautiful about the hunt; the English characters cannot see what is unjust about it. The reader, positioned between them, is invited to see both.
