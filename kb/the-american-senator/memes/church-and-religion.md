# Church and Religion

The Church of England in *The American Senator* is less a spiritual institution than a department of the social order. Its clergy are gentlemen by virtue of their office, its parish churches are monuments to local hierarchy, and its services are occasions for the display of rank rather than the cultivation of piety. Trollope's treatment of the church is characteristically double: he respects the institution while exposing its accommodations with worldly power.

## Definition in Context

Dillsborough's parish church is "ancient and inconvenient," its chief glory — and Trollope's adjective is carefully chosen — but its rector, Mr. Mainwaring, is an invalid who has not officiated for six months, leaving the work to a curate paid £100 a year with the use of a pony (012-001). The arrangement is perfectly legal and perfectly unjust: the rector receives £800 a year and a house for doing nothing, while the curate performs all the duties for a fraction of the stipend. It is the church as microcosm of the English property system.

Mrs. Morton's church attendance epitomizes the conflation of religion and rank. She goes to church every Sunday, "and had no doubt of her own sincerity when she reiterated her prayer that as she forgave others their trespasses, so might she be forgiven hers" (009-004). Trollope's irony is devastating: her piety is genuine and her self-deception is complete. The church that should challenge her moral complacency instead reinforces it.

## Key Passages

> "On the Sunday the party from Bragton went to the parish church — and found it very cold. The duty was done by a young curate who lived in Dillsborough, there being no house in Bragton for him." (012-001)

The church is physically cold, spiritually cold, and institutionally cold — a place where duty is "done" rather than performed, where the rector's absence is normal rather than scandalous. Trollope's prose is flat and factual, letting the indictment emerge from the arrangement of details.

> "She always went to church, and had no doubt of her own sincerity when she reiterated her prayer that as she forgave others their trespasses, so might she be forgiven hers. As Reginald Morton had certainly never trespassed against her perhaps there was no reason why her thoughts should be carried to the necessity of forgiving him." (009-004)

The Lord's Prayer, recited without reflection, becomes a mechanism for avoiding rather than confronting moral obligation. Mrs. Morton's piety is technically correct and spiritually empty — a perfect emblem of the established church as Trollope sees it.

> "If the sick gentleman took the small portion of the stipend and the working man the larger, would not better justice be done, and the people better served?" (012-013)

Senator Gotobed's question about church patronage is unanswerable within the terms of the English system. The church's arrangements are not designed for justice or efficiency — they are designed to preserve the property rights of the clergy, and those rights happen to align with the interests of the gentry.

## Character Perspectives

### The Honourable Mrs. Morton
The church as social theater. Her attendance is impeccable, her charity is calculated to the penny, and her moral self-satisfaction is untouched by any awareness of the gap between her prayers and her conduct. She is the church's ideal parishioner and its most devastating indictment.

### Mr. Mainwaring (the Rector)
The absentee clergyman whose £800 stipend is a property right rather than a salary for services rendered. His invalidity is genuine, but the system that allows him to collect the income while a curate does the work is an inheritance arrangement dressed in ecclesiastical robes.

### The Curate
A young man "nearly wet through and muddy up to his knees" (012-001), performing two sermons a week and all the parish duties for £100 a year. His situation is the clerical equivalent of Goarly's — a man doing the work while others collect the rent.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer who immediately identifies the patronage system as a violation of contractual fairness. His suggestion that the working clergyman should receive the larger stipend is simple common sense — and utterly foreign to the English arrangement.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of the church is informed by his lifelong affection for the institution and his clear-eyed understanding of its failings. He does not advocate disestablishment — the church, like the aristocracy, is part of the fabric of English life that he would preserve. But he insists on seeing it as it is rather than as it claims to be: an institution whose spiritual authority is compromised by its entanglement with property and rank.

The American dimension sharpens this critique. Senator Gotobed's questions about the church are questions about England itself: why should anyone defer to an institution whose arrangements serve the powerful at the expense of the deserving? Trollope does not answer the question, but he makes it impossible to ignore.
