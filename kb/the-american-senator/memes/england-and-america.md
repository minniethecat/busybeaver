# England and America

The American Senator arrives in Dillsborough as an anthropologist arriving among a remote tribe — curious, respectful, and fundamentally incapable of taking the natives' customs for granted. Trollope uses Senator Gotobed's American perspective to defamiliarize English social arrangements, revealing them as conventions rather than natural laws. The result is the novel's most sustained act of social criticism, conducted not through polemic but through the comedy of mutual incomprehension.

## Definition in Context

The novel's title announces its transatlantic structure: this is a story about an English country town, but it is seen through American eyes. Senator Gotobed, "the great American Senator" from "Mickewa" (008-003), is both a character in his own right and a narrative device — a lens through which Trollope can examine English society from the outside. Every English institution that the novel anatomizes — hunting, the church, the aristocracy, the courts — is subjected to the Senator's questions, and the questions reveal what the English characters have learned not to see.

The contrast works in both directions. Just as the Senator's incomprehension exposes English assumptions, so the English characters' reactions to him reveal their own limitations. Mrs. Morton's pronouncement — "Of course an American member of Congress can't be an English gentleman" (008-030) — is a perfect specimen of unexamined prejudice, and Trollope presents it without comment, letting the reader draw the obvious conclusion.

## Key Passages

> "I'm afraid you won't like my American friend." (008-029)

John Morton's anxiety about introducing Senator Gotobed to his grandmother captures the social tension that the American presence generates. The Senator is a guest who cannot be classified within the English social taxonomy — he is a member of Congress, but he is not a gentleman; he is powerful, but he is not landed; he is respected in his own country, but that respect does not translate.

> "Of course an American member of Congress can't be an English gentleman." (008-030)

Mrs. Morton's response is the novel's most concise statement of English class ideology. "English gentleman" is an exclusive category founded on birth, not achievement. The Senator's democratic credentials are, from this perspective, evidence of his inferiority rather than his merit.

> "Mr. Gotobed, who was studying English society, made notes in his own mind." (017-047)

The Senator is not merely a visitor — he is a student, collecting data, forming hypotheses, preparing conclusions. His note-taking is a metaphor for Trollope's own method: the novelist, like the Senator, is an observer of English society who renders its customs strange by describing them with anthropological precision.

## Character Perspectives

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer as moral philosopher. His questions are naive in their assumption that institutions ought to serve justice, but Trollope clearly considers the naivety admirable. The Senator is the novel's conscience, and his incomprehension is its sharpest critical instrument.

### John Morton
The man caught between two worlds, "Secretary of Legation at Washington" (001-006) who has absorbed enough American perspective to be embarrassed by English customs but not enough to abandon them. His unease is the novel's most nuanced exploration of cultural identity.

### The Honourable Mrs. Morton
The English aristocrat for whom the transatlantic contrast is simply invisible — she cannot conceive that an American might have a valid perspective on English arrangements. Her prejudice is so deep that she experiences it as natural law.

### Arabella Trefoil
The worldly observer who recognizes that the Senator's presence changes the social dynamics even if she cannot articulate how. Her instinct for social navigation makes her more perceptive about the transatlantic dimension than the novel's more moralistic characters.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's use of the transatlantic theme reflects his extensive travel in America and his publication of *North America* (1862), a travel book that combines observation with sharp social commentary. He admired American democracy more than most of his English contemporaries, but he also saw its limitations. The Senator is neither a hero nor a fool — he is a reasonable man from a different system, and his presence forces both English and American readers to examine their assumptions.

The novel's achievement is to make the transatlantic contrast work as both comedy and criticism. The Senator's questions about the church, the hunt, and the courts are funny because they are naive — and devastating because they are unanswerable. England emerges from his scrutiny as a society held together by conventions that cannot survive rational examination, and America emerges as a society whose democratic principles founder on the persistence of human vanity and self-interest.
