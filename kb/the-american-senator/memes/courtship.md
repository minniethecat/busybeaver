# Courtship

Courtship in *The American Senator* is a transaction conducted in the language of love but governed by the logic of the market. Trollope's treatment of the subject is remarkable for its refusal to romanticize an institution that his characters themselves understand in brutally economic terms. The novel's courtship plots — Arabella Trefoil's pursuit of John Morton, Mary Masters's negotiations with Lawrence Twentyman, Reginald Morton's silent devotion — form a comprehensive anatomy of how Victorians made marriages.

## Definition in Context

In Trollope's Dillsborough, courtship is never a private matter between two individuals. It is a family enterprise in which parents, guardians, and even neighbors have a stake. Lady Augustus Trefoil manages her daughter's marital prospects as a "spotting" operation, identifying targets and coordinating campaigns with military precision (013-030). The Masters family's discussions of Lawrence Twentyman's suit unfold at the dinner table, with Mrs. Masters as chief strategist and Mr. Masters as reluctant financier. Courtship in this world is a negotiation whose terms are set by class, money, and family obligation — love is at best a welcome addition, at worst an inconvenient variable.

## Key Passages

> "She had long known that it was her duty to marry, and especially her duty to marry well. Between her and her mother there had been no reticence on this subject." (012-017)

Arabella Trefoil is the most honest character in the novel about what the marriage market requires. Her lack of romantic illusion is not a moral failing but a survival strategy — without a fortune of her own, she must trade her beauty and connections for security. The absence of "reticence" between mother and daughter is Trollope's comment on a system that has made marriage into commerce.

> "Of course I want to see you. Why shouldn't I? I'm all fair and above board; — ain't I? Your father and mother know all about it." (007-025)

Lawrence Twentyman's proposal to Mary Masters is conducted with the transparency of a business negotiation. He appeals not to her feelings but to the propriety of his suit — he has her parents' approval, he is "fair and above board," he has followed the rules. What he cannot understand is why Mary might want something other than a good offer.

> "I wish you hadn't, because then I shouldn't be going to marry Mr. Morton. But, as I have got so far, for heaven's sake let it go on." (013-030)

Arabella's confession to her mother reveals the momentum that courtship acquires once begun. She has committed herself to a course of action not because she wants its outcome but because the alternative — starting over — is unthinkable. Trollope captures the terrible logic of a system in which courtship, once initiated, is almost impossible to stop.

## Character Perspectives

### Arabella Trefoil
The novel's central study in matrimonial strategy. Her pursuit of John Morton is simultaneously a job of work and a genuine effort to secure her future. Her tragedy is not that she is mercenary — she has no choice but to be mercenary — but that the man she secures turns out to be, in her estimation, "the last man in the world" she should have chosen (013-030).

### Mary Masters
Represents the alternative possibility — a woman who refuses to treat marriage as a transaction. Her resistance to Lawrence Twentyman's suit, against her family's wishes and her own economic interest, is the novel's most powerful assertion of individual autonomy against social pressure.

### Lawrence Twentyman
The suitor as honest tradesman. His approach to courtship is as direct and transparent as his approach to farming and hunting. He cannot understand why his good offer should be refused, and his bewilderment is Trollope's gentle satire of a man who has absorbed the rules of the marriage market so thoroughly that he cannot imagine anyone refusing to play by them.

### Lady Augustus Trefoil
The mother as campaign manager. Her management of Arabella's courtship is conducted with strategic intelligence and moral blindness in equal measure. She sees her daughter as an asset to be deployed, and her apparent ruthlessness is the logical consequence of a system that has given her no other way to secure her family's future.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of courtship is characteristic of his realism: he refuses to condemn the marriage market while also refusing to prettify it. Arabella Trefoil is not a villain — she is a rational actor in a system she did not create, making the best of limited options. Mary Masters is not a heroine of romantic love — she is a young woman who simply refuses to be bought.

The novel's American dimension sharpens this commentary. Senator Gotobed's observations of English courtship customs — the chaperonage, the formal calls, the elaborate negotiations — make visible what English readers might have taken for granted. What appears as romance from inside the system appears as a strange and slightly absurd form of commerce from outside it.
