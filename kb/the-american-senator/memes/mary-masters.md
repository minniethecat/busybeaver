# Mary Masters

Mary Masters occupies the novel's moral center without ever claiming it. She is the attorney's daughter, the lady's companion, the object of two very different men's affections—and in her quiet refusals and even quieter acceptances, Trollope traces the difficult path of a woman who insists on her own feelings in a world that would prefer her to follow its calculations.

## Background & Social Position

Mary is the daughter of Mr. Masters, the Dillsborough attorney, by his first marriage. Her mother died young, and Mary was taken in by Lady Ushant at Bragton, where she lived as companion and protégée until circumstances forced her return to her father's house (002-009, 002-011). This double experience—the gentility of Bragton and the professional respectability of Dillsborough—gives Mary a social perspective broader than either world alone.

Her position at the novel's opening is precarious. She lives in her father's house under the resentful eye of her stepmother, Mrs. Masters, who sees her as a rival for the family's limited resources and a obstacle to her own daughters' prospects. Mary has neither fortune nor influential connections—her value in the marriage market is her character, and the market does not price character.

## Personality & Character

Mary's defining quality is her seriousness—not solemnity, but moral seriousness. She takes her own feelings seriously, and she refuses to pretend to feelings she does not have. When Lawrence Twentyman proposes, offering security, respectability, and her family's enthusiastic approval, she hesitates. She does not know exactly why—she has not yet acknowledged her feelings for Reginald Morton—but she knows that "yes" would be a lie, and she will not lie.

This moral seriousness makes her appear passive, but it is a passivity of refusal rather than weakness. Mary does not act; she waits. She waits for Reginald Morton to declare himself, waits for circumstances to resolve, waits for her own feelings to become clear. In a novel full of characters who act precipitously—Arabella's pursuit of Rufford, Twentyman's repeated proposals, Mrs. Masters's schemes—Mary's stillness is itself a form of integrity.

## Key Relationships

- **Reginald Morton**: The man she loves. Their relationship develops almost entirely through indirection—glances, absences, significant silences. Reginald's inability to declare himself until his inheritance is secured is as much a test of Mary's patience as of his own character (006-021, 007-001).

- **Lawrence Twentyman**: The suitor she cannot accept. Twentyman is honest, solvent, and devoted—everything the marriage market says she should want. Mary's refusal of him is the novel's most radical act, a rejection of the market's logic in favor of a feeling she can barely articulate (003-022, 006-022).

- **Lady Ushant**: The benefactress whose household gave Mary her first experience of gentility. Lady Ushant's affection for Mary is genuine, but it also places Mary in a social no-man's-land—above the Masters, below the Mortons, belonging fully to neither (002-009).

- **Mrs. Masters**: The stepmother whose hostility sharpens Mary's isolation. Mrs. Masters is not wrong to worry about Mary's marriage prospects—they are genuinely precarious—but her insistence that Mary accept Twentyman transforms a practical concern into a moral pressure Mary resists.

## Role in the Novel

Mary's plot—the quiet girl who waits while others scheme—provides the novel's counterweight to Arabella's frenetic campaign. Where Arabella calculates, Mary feels; where Arabella pursues, Mary receives. Their juxtaposition is Trollope's argument that the marriage market damages everyone, but that some forms of resistance are possible.

Mary's eventual union with Reginald Morton is the novel's only marriage that is both financially prudent and emotionally genuine. It vindicates her patience without sentimentalizing it—she has waited, and she has been lucky, and Trollope does not pretend that luck was guaranteed.

## Significance

Mary Masters is Trollope's argument for the possibility of integrity within a corrupting system. She does not reform the marriage market—she cannot—but she refuses to let it dictate her choices. Her quiet heroism consists in knowing what she feels and refusing to pretend otherwise, even when everyone around her insists that feeling is a luxury she cannot afford.
