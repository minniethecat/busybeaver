# English Society

English society in *The American Senator* is not an abstract category but a concrete, densely realized world of reciprocal obligations and rigid stratifications. Trollope anatomizes this world through the microcosm of Dillsborough and its surrounding estates — a landscape where every person knows every other person's rank, income, and claims to gentility.

## Definition in Context

The social structure of Rufford county is a pyramid of landed wealth, with Lord Rufford at its apex as the region's greatest landlord and the hunt its central ritual institution (001-005). Below him sits the gentry — the Mortons of Bragton Park, whose three-century tenure in the parish gives them a claim to social legitimacy that mere wealth cannot buy (001-006). Further down are the "gentlemen-farmers" like Lawrence Twentyman, who own land and subscribe to the hunt but are never fully admitted to the inner circle (001-007). At the base stand the tradesmen, laborers, and tenants — necessary but invisible to the social calculus of their betters.

Trollope's innovation is to present this hierarchy as both natural and perpetually contested. The "sparse aristocracy of Dillsborough" (001-008) is a phrase that captures both exclusivity and fragility — the social order is thin enough to be described in a single paragraph, yet its rules govern every interaction from dinner invitations to hunting etiquette.

## Key Passages

> "The Hamptons of Hampton Wick were Ufford men, and belonged rather to Norrington than Dillsborough. The Botseys, also from Norrington, were members of the U.R.U., or Ufford and Rufford United Hunt Club; but they did not much affect Dillsborough as a town." (001-008)

Trollope's social classification is granular: even between neighboring towns, distinctions of affiliation and standing matter. The Botseys are members of the hunt club — they have paid the subscription — but they "do not much affect Dillsborough as a town," implying a hierarchy of social spaces as well as persons.

> "Mr. Gotobed, when the persecutions of Goarly were described to him at the scene of the dead fox, had expressed considerable admiration for the man's character as portrayed by what he then heard. The man — a poor man too and despised in the land — was standing up for his rights, all alone, against the aristocracy and plutocracy of the county." (016-001)

The Senator's American perspective reveals what the English characters cannot see: that "aristocracy and plutocracy" are not just descriptive terms but a system of power. Goarly, "a poor man too and despised in the land," becomes a test case for whether English society actually offers justice to those outside its charmed circle.

> "Mr. Gotobed, who was studying English society, made notes in his own mind." (017-047)

This line captures the novel's method: English society is being studied, anatomized, and judged — not by the English characters who inhabit it unthinkingly, but by an American observer who sees its customs as strange as those of any foreign tribe.

## Character Perspectives

### Lord Rufford
The apex predator of the social system, "a rich man who thinks of nothing but sport in all its various shapes" (001-005). His power is absolute but exercised with the indifference of someone who has never known challenge. He is "much liked by all sporting men, but is not otherwise very popular" — hunting is the one arena where his wealth translates to legitimate authority.

### Lawrence Twentyman
The aspirational figure who exposes the system's limits. He owns three hundred acres, pays his hunt subscription, wears the red coat — and still cannot get into the gentry's houses (001-007). His position is the novel's most precise study of the boundary between "yeoman" and "gentleman."

### Senator Gotobed
The external observer whose incomprehension is anthropological method. His questions — about church patronage, hunting trespass, and class obligation — force the English characters to articulate rules they have never examined. He represents the possibility of a society organized on different principles.

### Mrs. Morton (the Honourable)
The guardian of hereditary rank, who "was the daughter of a Viscount, a fact which she never forgot for a single moment" (008-005). Her version of English society is entirely genealogical — merit, wealth, and character count for nothing against the accident of birth.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of English society is characteristically double. He loves the world he describes — its rituals, its unspoken codes, its web of mutual obligation — and he exposes its cruelties with equal clarity. The hunt is genuinely beautiful and genuinely exclusive; the social hierarchy preserves order and crushes ambition; tradition is both the source of stability and the instrument of injustice.

The novel's transatlantic structure is crucial to this double vision. By placing an American observer inside an English country house, Trollope achieves a defamiliarization that his readers — who would have taken hunting for granted as a social institution — might otherwise have resisted. The Senator's questions are naive, but they are also unanswerable within the terms of the society he questions. When he asks why a rector should receive £800 a year for doing nothing while his curate receives £100 for doing everything (012-013), no English character can give him an answer that satisfies any standard of justice.
