# Aristocracy

The aristocracy in *The American Senator* is simultaneously the novel's subject and its target. Trollope, himself a member of the professional classes who moved comfortably among the great, writes about the landed ruling class with the intimacy of an insider and the critical distance of an observer. His aristocracy is neither demonized nor idealized — it is simply anatomized, with all its virtues, vices, and absurdities laid out for inspection.

## Definition in Context

Dillsborough's aristocracy is, as Trollope dryly notes, "very sparse" (001-008). It consists of Lord Rufford, the great landowner; John Morton, the absentee squire of Bragton; Mr. Mainwaring the rector and Mr. Surtees his curate; and the Hamptons and Botseys from neighboring parishes. This is a thin social layer, yet it governs everything — access to the hunt, invitations to dinner, eligibility for marriage. The aristocracy's power derives not from wealth alone but from the social deference that wealth commands in a world where everyone knows everyone's rank and acts accordingly.

The novel's key insight is that this power is maintained not by force but by consent. The tradesmen, farmers, and laborers of Dillsborough participate in their own subordination by accepting the aristocracy's right to rule. When they do challenge it — as Goarly does when he takes his grievance against Lord Rufford to law — the system mobilizes against them with a unanimity that reveals how deeply the social order is internalized (016-001).

## Key Passages

> "Those whom I have named, together with Mr. Mainwaring the rector, and Mr. Surtees his curate, made up the very sparse aristocracy of Dillsborough." (001-008)

The word "sparse" does quiet satirical work — this is the entire ruling class of a market town, and it can be listed in a single sentence. The inclusion of the rector and curate as aristocracy is also telling: in Trollope's England, the clergy are part of the gentry, their spiritual authority indistinguishable from their social position.

> "The man — a poor man too and despised in the land — was standing up for his rights, all alone, against the aristocracy and plutocracy of the county." (016-001)

Senator Gotobed's framing of the Goarly affair reduces Trollope's careful social taxonomy to two words: "aristocracy and plutocracy." The elision is deliberate — for the American observer, the distinction between inherited rank and purchased power is less important than the fact that both are arrayed against "a poor man" seeking justice.

> "Of course an American member of Congress can't be an English gentleman." (008-030)

Mrs. Morton's casual dismissal of Senator Gotobed is the purest expression of aristocratic ideology in the novel. "English gentleman" is a category that cannot be earned or achieved — it is inherited, and even a member of the American Congress cannot cross its boundary. The statement is offered without malice, as a simple statement of natural fact.

## Character Perspectives

### Lord Rufford
The absentee aristocrat par excellence, who "does not live near the town, but away at the other side of the county" (001-005). His power is exercised through the hunt, where his wealth and status translate directly into authority. He is neither cruel nor generous — he is simply indifferent, the product of a system that has never required him to consider the consequences of his actions.

### Mrs. Morton (the Honourable)
The aristocracy's ideological enforcer, "the daughter of a Viscount, a fact which she never forgot for a single moment" (008-005). Her entire identity is genealogical — she judges everyone by the accident of their birth, and the judgment is always in her own favor.

### John Morton
The uneasy aristocrat, caught between his English inheritance and his American experience. His position as Secretary of Legation at Washington has exposed him to a world where rank counts for less, but he cannot fully inhabit either system. His courtship of Arabella Trefoil is an attempt to secure his position within the English system that he has been absent from for too long.

### Senator Gotobed
The external observer whose questions expose the aristocracy's unspoken assumptions. When he asks why a man who does no work should receive £800 a year while the man who does all the work receives £100 (012-013), he is not being naive — he is pointing to a contradiction that the English characters have learned not to see.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's position on the aristocracy is characteristically nuanced. He does not believe in radical reform — the system, for all its inequities, works tolerably well. But he also refuses to accept the aristocracy's own account of itself. His aristocrats are not born to rule by divine right; they have simply inherited property that gives them power, and they exercise that power with varying degrees of wisdom and justice.

The novel's deepest criticism of the aristocracy is moral rather than political. The system produces characters like Mrs. Morton — righteous, cruel, and utterly incapable of self-doubt — and characters like Lord Rufford — charming, irresponsible, and indifferent to the human consequences of their pleasures. It is not that the aristocracy is evil; it is that the aristocracy is a moral environment that makes certain kinds of goodness difficult and certain kinds of failure almost inevitable.
