# Senator Gotobed

Elias Gotobed, Senator from the Western State of Mickewa, is the structural and thematic hinge of *The American Senator*. His presence transforms what might have been a conventional Trollope chronicle of English country life into a comparative study of two civilizations—each equally convinced of its own superiority, each equally blind to its own absurdities.

## Background & Social Position

Senator Gotobed arrives in England as the guest of John Morton, whom he has known in Washington. He is "a great Republican, who had very pronounced opinions as to the political and social advantages of his own country" (008-002), and he has come to study English country life with the methodical curiosity of a naturalist examining an unfamiliar species. His position is singular: as an American senator, he carries political weight that no English character can match, yet as a foreigner in rural Rufford, he is perpetually outside the social codes he observes.

Gotobed's wealth is never specified—American senatorship implies status rather than fortune—but his independence is absolute. He owes nothing to anyone in England, which gives his observations a freedom unavailable to characters enmeshed in the county's obligations and dependencies. The old Lady Ushant, meeting him for the first time, must be told that "a member of Congress can't be an English gentleman" (008-030)—the category simply does not apply.

## Personality & Character

Gotobed combines genuine intellectual curiosity with a degree of democratic dogmatism that can make him as rigid as the hierarchy he criticizes. He is "inquisitive" (009-001) in the best sense—he wants to understand—but his understanding is constrained by premises that English life does not share. When told that the hounds cannot hunt a fox, he exclaims "Starve that for dogs!" (008-014), applying American utilitarian logic to an English ritual that resists it.

Yet Gotobed is also the novel's most principled character. His defense of Goarly, the suspected fox-poisoner, is unpopular but morally serious: "If Goarly poisoned the fox, he is a very blackguard. But … if Goarly didn't poison the fox, why shouldn't he claim damages?" (014-016). He insists on evidence where the community insists on class solidarity, and his willingness to be unpopular for the sake of principle is a form of integrity the English characters rarely display.

## Key Relationships

- **John Morton**: Gotobed's host and guide, whose own uneasy position between English tradition and American experience mirrors the novel's larger concerns. Morton attempts to mediate between Gotobed's democratic rigor and English social reality, with mixed success (008-001).

- **Lord Rufford**: The aristocrat whom Gotobed observes but cannot comprehend. Rufford's casual exercise of privilege—"everybody rides across everybody's land out hunting" (010-030)—is to Gotobed a violation of property rights that no amount of tradition can justify.

- **Lady Ushant**: The "honourable old lady" who represents English gentility at its most attractive. Her meeting with Gotobed is a collision of two forms of integrity—her settled moral authority against his restless democratic principle (008-006).

- **Arabella Trefoil**: The marriage-market strategist whose calculations Gotobed observes without fully grasping. His American assumptions about romantic freedom make him a poor reader of the economic logic governing English courtship.

## Role in the Novel

Gotobed serves as the novel's primary instrument of defamiliarization. By placing an American observer in the midst of English country life, Trollope forces his English readers to see their own customs as strange—the hunt, the class system, the marriage market, the deference to rank. Gotobed's questions are often naive, sometimes obtuse, but always useful: they expose the unexamined assumptions on which English social life depends.

Structurally, Gotobed is present for the novel's major set pieces: the hunt at Bragton, the social gatherings at Rufford, the Goarly affair, and the resolution of the marriage plots. His presence does not drive the plot—he is an observer, not an actor—but it shapes how the reader understands what happens.

His name, "Gotobed," is one of Trollope's few allegorical touches—a suggestion that the Senator's questions might disturb English sleep. Whether they should is the question the novel leaves open.

## Significance

Through Gotobed, Trollope examines what it means to judge another society. The Senator is not always right—his democratic certainties can be as unreflective as the English class prejudices he criticizes—but his presence forces the question: which customs are wisdom, and which are merely habit? The novel refuses to answer definitively, and that refusal is its deepest wisdom.

Gotobed also represents Trollope's fascination with America, a country he visited and wrote about extensively. The Senator is neither a caricature nor an idealization; he is a serious attempt to imagine what an intelligent, principled American would make of English life, and what English life would make of him.
