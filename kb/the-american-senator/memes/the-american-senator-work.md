# The American Senator (novel)

*The American Senator* is the eighty-first novel by [[anthony-trollope]], published in 1877, during the height of his powers as the Victorian era's most industrious and psychologically acute novelist. Set in the fictional hunting country of [[dillsborough]], it weaves together three narrative strands — the [[marriage-market|matrimonial campaign]] of Arabella Trefoil, the [[goarly-affair|legal dispute]] over a poisoned fox, and the transatlantic observations of the visiting [[senator-gotobed]] — into a portrait of English rural society that is at once affectionate and unsparing.

## Publication and Reception

The novel was first serialized in *Temple Bar* magazine from May 1876 to June 1877, and published in three volumes by Chapman and Hall in 1877. It belongs to Trollope's late period, after the completion of the Palliser series and during a phase when he was increasingly interested in the social and political contrasts between England and the United States — a theme he had explored at length in *North America* (1862) and would return to in this novel.

*The American Senator* was modestly successful but has never ranked among Trollope's most celebrated works. Its divided structure — part [[hunting]] chronicle, part [[marriage-market|marriage plot]], part political satire — has sometimes been read as a weakness. More recent criticism has seen in this very division a formal reflection of the novel's theme: the fragmentation of English society under the pressure of democratic critique.

## Structure and Plot

The novel's eighty chapters can be broadly divided into three interwoven narratives:

| 1 | 1–28 | The Dillsborough world established; the Goarly affair begins | [[john-morton]], [[lord-rufford]], [[mr-masters]], [[lawrence-twentyman]] |
| 2 | 29–54 | The marriage campaign: Arabella pursues Rufford | [[arabella-trefoil]], [[lord-rufford]], [[mary-masters]], [[reginald-morton]] |
| 3 | 55–80 | Resolutions and Gotobed's critique | [[senator-gotobed]], [[mary-masters]], [[reginald-morton]], [[arabella-trefoil]] |

This tripartite structure reflects Trollope's characteristic method of building a social world through the accumulation of interconnected detail. No single plotline dominates; instead, the novel's meaning emerges from the interplay of its parts — the way the hunting chronicle comments on the marriage plot, the way the legal dispute illuminates the political argument.

## Major Themes

### English Rural Society
The novel is one of Trollope's most sustained examinations of English country life. Through [[dillsborough]] and its inhabitants, he anatomizes a social order built on land, custom, and class hierarchy — an order that is beautiful in its coherence but unjust in its effects. The novel neither celebrates nor condemns this world; it renders it with a precision that makes both its virtues and its exclusions visible.

### The Anglo-American Encounter
Senator Gotobed is one of Trollope's most fully realized American characters — not a caricature but a genuine critical intelligence whose outsider perspective forces the novel's English characters (and its English readers) to see their institutions afresh. The transatlantic dimension of the novel anticipates the international theme that would become central to late Trollope and to the work of his successor [[henry-james]].

### Marriage and Money
The [[marriage-market]] plot — Arabella Trefoil's pursuit of a titled husband, Mary Masters's resistance to a financially advantageous match — continues Trollope's lifelong examination of the relationship between love and property. The novel's originality lies in its refusal to sentimentalize: Arabella is calculating, but her calculation is rational given the system she operates in; Mary's romantic resistance succeeds only because material circumstances happen to align.

### Law and Custom
The [[goarly-affair]] transforms a local hunting dispute into a meditation on the nature of law in a society governed by custom. The novel asks whether English justice is genuinely just, or merely the codification of aristocratic privilege — and it refuses to offer a simple answer.

## Critical Assessment

*The American Senator* occupies an unusual position in Trollope's canon. It has never achieved the popularity of the Barsetshire novels or the Palliser series, yet it contains some of Trollope's most sophisticated social criticism and one of his most memorable minor characters in Senator Gotobed. The novel's willingness to subject English institutions to sustained critique — and its refusal to resolve that critique into comfortable affirmation — gives it a distinctly modern quality that sets it apart from much of Trollope's other work.

The novel is also notable for its formal experimentation. By dividing its narrative among three loosely connected plotlines and by incorporating Gotobed's lecture as a set-piece political argument, Trollope tests the limits of the realist novel's capacity to accommodate explicit intellectual content. The result is uneven but ambitious — a novel that tries to do something genuinely difficult and largely succeeds.
