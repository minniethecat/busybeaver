# Social Class

Social class in *The American Senator* is neither a fixed inheritance nor a simple ladder—it is a performance that must be continually maintained through wealth, behavior, and above all recognition by others. Trollope's Dillsborough is a laboratory of class anxiety: the gentleman-farmer who cannot get into the great houses, the aristocratic daughter hunting a titled husband, the American senator who cannot comprehend English deference, and the attorney's daughter caught between two worlds.

## Definition in Context

In Trollope's rural England, class is a system of graded inclusion. At the summit stands Lord Rufford, "a rich man who thinks of nothing but sport in all its various shapes," whose dominance is so complete that even his rudeness reads as privilege (001-005). Below him are the country gentlemen—the Mortons, "so thoroughly a gentleman—one of the Mortons, and a man who had been at Oxford" (006-021)—whose status derives from land, education, and ancient name rather than mere wealth. The professional classes are represented by the attorney Mr. Masters, whose work places him perpetually at the boundary of gentility. And at the aspirational edge stands Lawrence Twentyman, "quite the gentleman-farmer," who pays his hunt subscription, wears the red coat, and never quite belongs (001-007).

The American Senator Gotobed introduces a destabilizing perspective. When Morton tells him that "a member of Congress can't be an English gentleman," Gotobed's incomprehension exposes the arbitrariness of the category (008-030). Class, Trollope implies, is not a natural order but a collective agreement—powerful precisely because it is treated as self-evident.

## Key Passages

> "He was quite the gentleman-farmer. He possessed over three hundred acres of land, ... and was more than a yeoman with an unaccented name. He not only rode well to hounds but paid twenty-five pounds annually to the hunt, which entitled him to feel quite at home in his red coat." (001-007)

Twentyman embodies the paradox of Victorian class mobility: he has every outward marker of gentility—land, the hunt subscription, the red coat—yet the narrator's description is laced with qualifiers. "Quite the gentleman-farmer" is not "quite the gentleman." His name is "unaccented," his father was known at the Bush Inn, and older families refuse the recognition his wealth has purchased.

> "A member of Congress can't be an English gentleman. You, in your position, have … advantages which have caused you to be what you are without any reference on your own part to the outside world." (008-030)

Morton's explanation of English gentility to Gotobed reveals class as a closed system: it is not what you do but what you are, and what you are is determined by birth and recognition rather than achievement. The circular logic—a gentleman is someone recognized as a gentleman—is the system's strength, making it impossible to breach from outside.

> "I don't know how a gentleman is to do so in the presence of the girl's father and mother without having some ground under his feet." (003-032)

Twentyman's courtship of Mary Masters is conducted with agonizing awareness of class protocol. He needs "ground under his feet"—the security of parental approval and financial assurance—because he cannot rely on the unspoken privileges that a Morton or a Rufford would take for granted.

## Character Perspectives

### Lord Rufford
The aristocrat for whom class is not a question but a fact of nature. He "is much liked by all sporting men, but is not otherwise very popular" (001-005)—his class position is so secure that he need not trouble himself with being liked. His rudeness to those beneath him is not contempt but simple failure of attention; they are invisible to him.

### Lawrence Twentyman
The gentleman-farmer whose every action is shadowed by class anxiety. He rides well, pays well, and follows the codes—yet Mr. Runciman still calls him "Larry" (001-007), and Lord Rufford never invites him to dinner. His tragedy is that he understands the rules perfectly and obeys them all, and it is still not enough.

### Mary Masters
The attorney's daughter who exists at the class boundary. Reginald Morton's love for her crosses class lines, and her hesitation about Twentyman is partly a hesitation about marrying into the class position he represents—secure but subordinate, respectable but excluded from the world she has glimpsed through the Mortons.

### Senator Gotobed
The American observer for whom the English class system is simultaneously fascinating and repellent. His insistence on applying democratic logic to a hierarchical society produces both comedy and insight. "There is a self-consciousness of the better class of our citizens" (029-011)—his phrasing reveals how even American equality produces its own class markers.

## Trollope's Commentary

Trollope's treatment of class is remarkable for its refusal of both radical critique and complacent acceptance. He loves the world of the English country gentleman—the hunting field, the great house, the settled order—and he sees through it with perfect clarity. His irony is directed not at the system itself but at the pretensions it generates: Twentyman's anxiety, Arabella's calculation, the minor gentry's snobbery.

The transatlantic dimension of *The American Senator* allows Trollope to examine English class from the outside without ever abandoning his insider's knowledge. Gotobed is not always right—his democratic dogmatism can be as rigid as the hierarchy he criticizes—but his presence forces the English characters to articulate what they have never had to explain, and in explaining it they often expose its contradictions.

A man "who lived on his own property" (006-010) had a status that required no justification; a man who bought his land with money earned in trade never quite achieved the same position. The novel's genius is to show that everyone understands this, no one can quite defend it, and it governs everything anyway.
