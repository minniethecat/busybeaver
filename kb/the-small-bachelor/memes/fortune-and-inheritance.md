# Fortune and Inheritance

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, inherited wealth functions as a deus ex machina that resolves the novel's central romantic conflict. [[George-Finch|George Finch]], dismissed as a penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]] by [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], is revealed to have inherited a substantial [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] from his uncle Thomas — a revelation that instantly transforms his [[class-and-society|social]] standing. Wodehouse uses inheritance to satirise a [[class-and-society|society]] in which character counts for nothing and [[money-and-wealth|money]] for everything.

## In the Novel

### The Penniless Artist

Throughout the early chapters, [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is defined by his poverty. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] makes her position brutally clear:

> "Do you imagine I intend to allow you to ruin your life by marrying a penniless artist?" (025-044)
> "He isn't a despicable fortune-hunter." (025-045)
> "He is a penniless artist." (025-046)

It is the word "penniless" — not "[[art-and-artists|artist]]" — that carries the fatal weight. In [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s [[class-and-society|social]] calculus, a wealthy [[art-and-artists|artist]] would be eccentric; a penniless one is a threat. The [[love-and-romance|romance]] between [[George-Finch|George]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] is presented as a [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehouse pairing: the unsuitable suitor blocked by a mercenary mother.

### The Secret Fortune

[[George-Finch|George]]'s wealth is introduced as a revelation, not a given. The truth emerges only when [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] intervenes at a critical moment, first [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a strategic hypothetical question:

> "I ask you, Mrs. Waddington, would you give your consent to this marriage if my friend George Finch were a wealthy man?" (025-092)

And then, having secured her answer, the triumphant revelation:

> "Then allow me to inform you," said Hamilton Beamish, triumphantly, "that George Finch is an exceedingly wealthy man. His uncle Thomas, whose entire fortune he inherited two years ago, was Finch, Finch, Finch, Butterfield and Finch, the well-known Corporation Law firm." (025-096)

The inheritance is a plot twist. [[George-Finch|George]] has been wealthy all along, but his modest life[[fashion-and-appearance|style]] and [[art-and-artists|artist]]ic identity concealed the fact from [[class-and-society|society]] — and from [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] in p[[art-and-artists|art]]icular. Wodehouse's choice of "[[George-Finch|Finch]], [[George-Finch|Finch]], [[George-Finch|Finch]], Butterfield and [[George-Finch|Finch]]" — a name that buries the surname in repetition — is a comic touch: [[George-Finch|George]]'s [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] comes from the most respectable, and most absurdly named, of [[crime-and-law|legal]] institutions.

### The Dollar-Bill Episode

[[George-Finch|George]]'s spontaneous generosity — the act that demonstrates he has never valued [[money-and-wealth|money]] properly — is narrated by [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h delighted admiration:

> "He was so happy at being engaged that he suddenly stopped at Fifty-Ninth Street and Fifth Avenue and started giving away dollar-bills to everybody who came by. In about two minutes there was a crowd stretching right across to Madison Avenue, and the traffic was blocked for miles, and they called out the police-reserves, and George was taken away in a patrol-wagon, and I telephoned to Hamilton Beamish to go and bail him out and bring him along here." (025-065)

This is one of the novel's finest comic set-pieces. A man who scatters dollar bills to strangers on [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] is a man who deserves his inheritance: his wealth has not corrupted him into the calculating miserliness of characters like [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]. The fact that his generosity results in a [[police-and-law|police]] [[police-and-law|arrest]] is pure Wodehouse — generosity is no defense against the machinery of [[new-york|New York]] [[crime-and-law|law]] enforcement.

### The Social Alchemy of Wealth

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s immediate reversal upon learning of [[George-Finch|George]]'s [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] is the novel's sharpest satirical comment on the [[class-and-society|social]] power of inherited [[money-and-wealth|money]]. She who had condemned [[George-Finch|George]] as "a penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]]" (025-046) moments before now embraces him as a suitable son-in-[[crime-and-law|law]]. Character, talent, and [[love-and-romance|love]] prove irrelevant — only wealth matters.

### Uncle Thomas Finch

Uncle Thomas Finch, though deceased before the novel begins, is a pivotal offstage presence. His decision to leave his entire [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] to [[George-Finch|George]] is the mechanism that enables the happy ending. Wodehouse presents the inheritance [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout irony or critique — it is simply the way the world works, and [[George-Finch|George]]'s good [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] is treated as a comic blessing. The name "[[George-Finch|Finch]], [[George-Finch|Finch]], [[George-Finch|Finch]], Butterfield and [[George-Finch|Finch]]" is itself a joke: the repetition of "[[George-Finch|Finch]]" suggests an institution so respectable it cannot stop naming itself.

### Wealth and Social Access

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s [[class-and-society|social]] world is defined by wealth as the sole criterion for admission:

> "Mrs. Waddington, in her selection of guests, confined herself to the extremely wealthy." (013-013)

This is the world into which [[George-Finch|George]] must be admitted, and inheritance is the only key. Wodehouse does not critique this system so much as treat it as a fixed comic premise — the absurdity is self-evident.

### Fortune as Narrative Device

The novel's use of inheritance differs from the standard Victorian inheritance plot. In Dickens or Trollope, the revelation of a hidden [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] is typically the culmination of a moral education. In Wodehouse, there is no moral education — [[George-Finch|George]] does not need to become worthy of his [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]; he simply needs to be revealed as already possessing it. The inheritance is a pure plot mechanism, a s[[humor-and-comedy|wit]]ch that flips the novel's [[class-and-society|social]] dynamics from obstacle to resolution.
