## Identity and Background

Lord Hunstanton is a British aristocrat who arrives as a house-guest at the Waddington residence on [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]. His precise title and estate are never specified, but his manners — and his verbal tic — mark him unmistakably:

> "Well, here I am," said Lord Hunstanton. He paused for a moment, then added, "What, what!" as if he felt that it was expected of him. [008-008]

The "What, what!" becomes his signature utterance throughout the novel, serving simultaneously as punctuation, affirmation, and a marker of his [[class-and-society|class]] identity. His first concern upon arrival — whether dinner will be "cold meat and a pickle" — immediately establishes the comic gap between his aristocratic pretensions and his domestic anxieties [008-017].

## Appearance and Manner

[[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]] provides the novel's most vivid description of Lord Hunstanton:

> "I don't know if you noticed a pie-faced fellow with an eyeglass and a toothbrush moustache at dinner? That was Lord Hunstanton. He keeps telling me things about etiquette." [014-038]

The eyeglass and toothbrush moustache are [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehousian markers of the affected aristocrat. Hunstanton's preoccupation [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h etiquette positions him as a foil to the informal American characters, p[[art-and-artists|art]]icularly [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee]] H., who resents being lectured on manners in his own home.

## Role in the Waddington Household

Lord Hunstanton is summoned to the Waddington residence by [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], who views him as an ideal match for her daughter [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]]:

> "I should be very happy," said Mrs. Waddington, "if a man like Lord Hunstanton asked you to be your wife." [009-029]

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s enthusiasm for the match is strategic. Facing financial difficulties — her husband [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee]] H.'s business troubles are a recurring theme — she sees a titled son-in-[[crime-and-law|law]] as a solution. [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]], however, is in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[George-Finch|George Finch]], and views Lord Hunstanton as an unwelcome suitor imposed by her mother.

> "Last night, she told me in so many words that she wanted me to marry him. He's always hanging about the house." [020-042]

## The Locked-Room Episode

Lord Hunstanton's most extended comic set-piece occurs when he and the Waddingtons are accidentally locked in a room by a [[crime-and-law|police]]man. Trapped [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee]] H. — who already despises him — Hunstanton's reaction reveals both his physical cowardice and his instinct for self-preservation:

> To Lord Hunstanton the beating on the door had sounded like the first guns of a relieving army. He felt like the girl who heard the pipers skirling as they marched on beleaguered Lucknow. [056-051]

When rescue arrives, Hunstanton wastes no time escaping: "Lord Hunstanton eyed [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Mr. Waddington]] warily, and sidled [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h an air of carelessness towards the stairway. Accelerating rapidly, he vanished" [056-122]. His undignified exit — all aristocratic dignity abandoned in favor of survival — is pure Wodehouse.

## Connection to Hamilton Beamish

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] vouches for Lord Hunstanton's identity to [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]], describing him as "a personal acquaintance of mine" [056-127]. This connection places Hunstanton [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hin the novel's [[class-and-society|social]] web — linked to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] through the shared world of London [[class-and-society|society]] and now intersecting [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the [[new-york|New York]] cast through the Waddington household.

## Narrative Function

Lord Hunstanton serves as the novel's "wrong suitor" — a stock figure in Wodehouse's romantic comedies. He is not villainous; he is merely unsuitable, a titled mannequin whose appeal is entirely external (his title) rather than internal (his character). His presence creates the romantic obstacle that drives the central plot: [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] must escape her mother's matchmaking to marry [[George-Finch|George Finch]].

His comic value derives from the gap between his [[class-and-society|social]] standing and his actual behavior — a lord who frets about cold [[food-and-dining|meals]], flees from confrontation, and cannot do more than interject "What, what!" in moments of crisis.
