# May Stubbs

## Identity and Role

May Stubbs is a figure from [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s past — a young woman [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h whom [[George-Finch|George]] once had "an [[engagement-and-courtship|understanding]]" (034-017) that has since become an impediment to his romantic pursuit of [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]]. Her name, repeatedly invoked throughout the novel, functions as a kind of comic threat: the past that refuses to stay past.

[[Hamilton-Beamish]]'s struggle to recall her name is a small comic set piece in itself:

> "And her name, if I remember rightly, was Dubbs or Tubbs or Jubbs or — no, I have it. My memory is better than I supposed. It was May Stubbs." (033-052)

The litany of near-miss surnames — Dubbs, Tubbs, Jubbs — before the triumphant arrival at "Stubbs" is [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehouse: a display of mental effort that is simultaneously impressive and ridiculous.

## The "Understanding"

The nature of [[George-Finch|George]]'s prior entanglement [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h May Stubbs is carefully calibrated for maximum comic potential. It was, as [[George-Finch|George]] insists:

> "It wasn't an engagement. It was an understanding." (034-036)

The distinction is crucial. An "[[engagement-and-courtship|engagement]]" carries formal [[class-and-society|social]] weight; an "[[engagement-and-courtship|understanding]]" is the Wodehouse equivalent of a loophole — binding enough to cause trouble, vague enough to allow maneuvering. The word allows [[George-Finch|George]] to maintain his self-image as a gentleman while acknowledging that May Stubbs may view the matter differently.

## The Revelation

The novel's most audacious plot twist involving May Stubbs is revealed when it emerges that she and [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]] are the same person:

> "My professional name. Didn't we agree that anyone who had a name like May Stubbs would want to change it as quickly as possible?" (035-087)

The name "May Stubbs" is presented as so irredeemably ordinary — so resistant to [[love-and-romance|romance]] or glamour — that its bearer has been driven to assume an entirely different identity. The line is both a joke and a genuine piece of character insight: May Stubbs has reinvented herself, and her [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling persona is the vehicle of that reinvention.

## Plot Function

May Stubbs/[[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]] serves as the novel's most intricate plot device. She is simultaneously:
- The romantic complication that threatens [[George-Finch|George]]'s happiness
- The agent of its resolution (through her influence over [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]])
- The self-reinvented figure whose double identity embodies the novel's themes of [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]] and reality

Her transformation from May Stubbs of [[east-gilead|East Gilead]] to [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]] of [[new-york|New York]] mirrors [[George-Finch|George]]'s own journey from Idaho provincial to [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]] [[art-and-artists|artist]] — both are acts of self-creation in the fluid [[class-and-society|social]] world of 1920s America.
