# Servants and Domestic Life

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, as in much of Wodehouse, servants are never merely servants. [[mullett|Mullett]], [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s valet, is a reformed ex-convict with a romantic life of his own. [[ferris|Ferris]], the Waddington butler, is a man haunted by memories of [[brangmarley-hall|Brangmarley Hall]]. These characters occupy the lower rungs of the novel's [[class-and-society|class]] ladder, but Wodehouse grants them the same interiority, the same capacity for [[love-and-romance|love]] and [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]], that he grants his protagonists.

## Mullett: The Reformed Criminal

[[mullett|Mullett]] is the novel's most developed servant character. A former burglar who served a year's imprisonment for "an inside burglary job" (004-053), he has been reformed by [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] principles and now serves as [[George-Finch|George]]'s valet, cook, and man-of-all-work. His romantic involvement with [[fanny-welch|Fanny Welch]], a pickpocket, creates a parallel love story that mirrors the main romantic plot while operating on an entirely different [[class-and-society|social]] register.

## Ferris: The Nostalgic Butler

[[ferris|Ferris]], the Waddington butler, is defined by professional composure and private nostalgia. His "marmoreal calm" (010-010) provides a still point in the Waddington household's perpetual domestic drama, while his memories of [[brangmarley-hall|Brangmarley Hall]] add a note of unexpected pathos to his characterization.

## Servants as Plot Agents

Wodehouse's servants are not passive observers; they are active agents in the plot's machinery. It is [[mullett|Mullett]] who follows [[George-Finch|George]] to East Seventy-Ninth Street and reports back to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]], setting crucial revelations in motion. The servant class, in Wodehouse's universe, sees everything and quietly facilitates — or complicates — the designs of their employers.
