# Long Island

## The Waddington Summer Home

Long Island, specifically the town of Hempstead, serves as the location of the Waddington family's summer residence in *The Small Bachelor*. It is here, away from the [[class-and-society|social]] pressures of [[new-york|New York City]], that the novel's climactic wedding takes place — and where the plot's various complications converge for their final resolution.

## A Place Apart

Long Island functions in the novel's geography as a place apart from [[new-york|New York]] — sufficiently distant to require a train journey, sufficiently pastoral to host a country wedding. The removal from the city strips away some of the [[class-and-society|social]] scaffolding that constrains the characters in Manhattan, allowing for the accelerated revelations and reversals of the novel's final movement.

[[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]] makes his desperate journey back to Hempstead after learning of the [[finer-and-better-film-co|Finer and Better Film Company]]'s success — "several hours later, when the stars had begun to peep out and the birds were rustling sleepily in the trees, a solitary figure might have been observed moving slowly up the drive towards the front door of the Waddington summer-residence at Hempstead, Long Island" (036-110).

## The Duck-Farm Dream

[[mullett|Mullett]] articulates a different vision of Long Island when describing his post-wedding plans with [[fanny-welch|Fanny Welch]]: "when we've finished honeymooning, we'll go down to Long Island and buy a little duck-farm and live happy ever after" (029-008). In this formulation, Long Island represents not [[class-and-society|social]] ambition but domestic contentment — a place where a reformed ex-convict and a reformed pickpocket can raise ducks and be happy. The duck-farm is the novel's most modest and most genuine vision of the good life.

## Significance

Long Island/Hempstead serves as the novel's pastoral alternative to [[new-york|New York]] — a place where the complications bred in the city can finally be resolved. The wedding that takes place there, despite its comic interruptions, succeeds: [[George-Finch|George]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] are married, and the novel's movement from city to country mirrors its movement from complication to resolution.
