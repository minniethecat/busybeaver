# Fanny Welch

## Identity and Role

Fanny Welch is a pickpocket introduced in *The Small Bachelor* as the fiancée of [[mullett|Mullett]], [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s valet. [[mullett|Mullett]] himself identifies her profession with startling frankness: "Fanny's her name, sir. Fanny Welch. She's a pickpocket" (004-070). She is later described as "a pretty little thing with snapping black eyes and a small face" whose most notable feature is "the slim shapeliness of her hands with their long, sensitive fingers" — hands ideally suited, the narrator notes wryly, to her chosen profession (029-002).

## Relationship with Mullett

Fanny appears at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]] for a light collation in [[George-Finch|George]]'s kitchen, where [[mullett|Mullett]] gazes at her "very much as George Finch had gazed at [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]], [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] at [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]], and as a million other young men in [[new-york|New York]] and its outskirts were or would shortly be gazing at a million other young women" (029-001). The narrator notes that "Love had come rather late to Frederick Mullett, for his had been a busy life, but it had come to stay."

Their shared background in crime — he a reformed burglar, she a pickpocket — creates a bond of mutual understanding that sets them apart from the other couples in the novel. While [[George-Finch|George]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] must navigate [[class-and-society|class]] barriers, Fanny and [[mullett|Mullett]] are simply two people who have found each other after difficult lives.

## Character and Reform

Fanny's appearance coincides with her decision to abandon her criminal career. She declares to [[mullett|Mullett]]: "I'm through with the game for good. I wouldn't steal a bone collar-stud now, not if my mother came and begged me on her bended knee. All I want is my little wife and my little home in the country." Her transformation from pickpocket to prospective wife parallels the novel's broader interest in self-reinvention — [[George-Finch|George Finch]] remakes himself from Idaho provincial to [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]] [[art-and-artists|artist]], just as Fanny remakes herself from criminal to respectable spouse.

## Plot Function

Fanny Welch serves a crucial plot function in the novel's final movement. [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]] carries three hundred dollars intended for her, and this sum becomes entangled in the climactic complications. Her presence humanizes [[mullett|Mullett]], transforming him from comic servant to a man with emotional depth and a future worth working toward.
