# Ferris

## Identity and Role

Ferris is the butler at the Waddington household, Number 16, East Seventy-Ninth Street, in *The Small Bachelor*. Like many Wodehouse butlers, he is defined by a professional impassivity — the narrator describes him as possessing "marmoreal calm," a stone-like composure that makes him the still centre of the Waddington family's perpetually agitated domestic world (010-010).

## Brangmarley Hall

Ferris's defining characteristic is his nostalgia for Brangmarley Hall, Little-Seeping-in-the-Wold, Salop, England, where he spent "the early, happy days of his butlerhood." When the wedding descends into chaos, his reaction is characteristically understated: "A very unpleasant affair, sir, and one which could never have occurred at Brangmarley Hall" (037-056). The repetition of Brangmarley Hall as a lost ideal — an England of orderly households where weddings proceeded without interruption — becomes one of the novel's gentlest running jokes.

Moonlit nights make him "a little homesick, for Brangmarley Hall had been at its best on such occasions." He remembers himself as "a careless, lighthearted footman" watching moonbeams on the moat and "pondering idly on what would win the two o'clock race at Ally Pally next day." The refrain "Happy days! Happy days!" runs through his reflections like a melancholy chime (055-019).

## Significance

In the novel's [[class-and-society|class]] structure, Ferris occupies the servant's position — but Wodehouse grants him an interior life, a remembered past, and a private melancholy that complicates the stereotype of the impassive butler. Like [[mullett|Mullett]], he is a servant with a story, and his Brangmarley Hall nostalgia adds a note of gentle pathos to the surrounding [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]].
