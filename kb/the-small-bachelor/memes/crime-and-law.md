# Crime and Law

## Overview

*The Small Bachelor* is set during Prohibition, when the Eighteenth Amendment made the manufacture and sale of alcohol illegal in the United States. This legal context creates a backdrop of criminality and law enforcement that Wodehouse exploits for comic effect. Bootleggers, police raids, [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling violations, and other minor crimes populate the novel's secondary plotlines.

## In the Novel

### Prohibition and Bootlegging

Prohibition-era laws create a world where otherwise respectable citizens become technically criminal. When a character inquires about a bootlegger (004-122), it is presented not as scandalous but as a mundane domestic concern. Wodehouse treats the absurdity of Prohibition [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h light satirical touch.

### Fortune-Telling and the Law

One of the novel's most developed comic subplots involves [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]], a [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-teller whose activities attract police attention:

> "It has been called to the attention of my superior officers, Mr. Beamish, that Madame Eulalie is in the habit of telling fortunes for a monetary consideration. Against the law, sir." (021-054)

The legal prohibition on [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling — another of the era's moral crusades — becomes a plot complication that Wodehouse mines for [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]].

### Police and Authority Figures

Police officers in the novel are neither menacing nor heroic; they are bureaucratic functionaries enforcing absurd laws. T[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] presence adds a layer of comic menace to the proceedings [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout ever becoming genuinely threatening. This treatment of authority is characteristic of Wodehouse's light satirical mode.
