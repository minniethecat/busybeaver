# Fate and Coincidence

## Overview

Chance encounters and improbable coincidences drive much of the plot in *The Small Bachelor*. Wodehouse uses coincidence not as a narrative weakness but as a deliberate comic device — the more unlikely the coincidence, the funnier its consequences. Characters constantly find themselves in the right (or wrong) place at exactly the wrong (or right) time, and the narrative treats these accidents [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a light touch that is characteristic of Wodehouse's comic worldview.

## In the Novel

### The Bus Encounter

One of the novel's most significant coincidences occurs when [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] meets an unknown woman on a bus and falls in [[love-and-romance|love]] instantly. This chance encounter — a meeting that could only happen in the crowded, random world of [[new-york|New York]] public transit — sets in motion a major romantic subplot:

> "I think I am in love with your friend, George Finch." (020-006)
> "I am in love with... I don't know her name. She is a most charming girl. I met her coming up here on the bus..." (020-007)

### Fate as Spoken Belief

Characters in the novel explicitly invoke fate as an explanation for t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] circumstances. When [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] encounters the woman he admires again, he declares:

> "We seem fated to meet." (021-008)

And immediately affirms his acceptance:

> "And I'm not quarrelling with fate." (021-009)

This direct invocation of fate — by a character who elsewhere prides himself on rational [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] — reveals how Wodehouse uses the language of destiny to frame romantic attraction. Fate becomes the vocabulary through which characters surrender to emotions they cannot logically justify.

### Comic Timing and Misunderstanding

Wodehouse's coincidences are timed for maximum comic effect. Characters arrive at precisely the wrong moment, overhear the wrong fragment of conversation, or miss one another by seconds. These near-misses and unlucky timings are the engine of Wodehousian [[humor-and-comedy|farce]].

A p[[art-and-artists|art]]icularly fine example occurs when a character's shoelace comes untied at a critical moment:

> "It chanced that as he spoke these light and casual words Hamilton Beamish, glancing down, noted that his shoelace had come untied. Stooping to attend to this he missed seeing George's face. Nor—for he was a man who concentrated even on the lightest task the full attention of a great mind—did he hear the other's sudden, whistling gasp of astonishment and horror." (034-001)

The triviality of the shoelace — a purely mechanical accident — determines whether one character perceives another's emotional crisis. This is [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehouse: cosmic consequences hinging on absurdly small physical events.

### Coincidence and the Central Park Meeting

[[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]]'s reunion [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is framed explicitly in the language of chance:

> "By a most extraordinary chance we met this afternoon in Central Park near the Zoo..." (025-025)

The Zoo setting amplifies the comic absurdity — [[love-and-romance|romance]] blossoms amid the animal enclosures, as if the natural world itself is conspiring in the comic plot.

### The Fatality of Weddings

One of the novel's darkest comic threads involves the [[marriage-and-weddings|wedding]] ceremony itself. The officiating clergyman reveals a grim pattern:

> "I have always thought it singularly unfortunate. But then it almost seems as if there were some fatality about the weddings at which I officiate. Only a week before, I had married a charming young couple, and both were dead before the month was out. A girder fell on them as they were passing a building which was under construction." (036-121)

This "singular coincidence" — a clergyman whose [[marriage-and-weddings|wedding]] ceremonies appear to curse the [[marriage-and-weddings|bride]][[marriage-and-weddings|groom]]s — is Wodehouse's way of grafting mock-tragic fatality onto the comic [[marriage-and-weddings|marriage]] plot. The clergyman's catalog of unfortunate [[marriage-and-weddings|bride]][[marriage-and-weddings|groom]]s (pink spots, falling girders, low fever) turns fate into a running joke.

### The Reversals of Luck

Luck in the novel is fickle and ironic. When a character believes fortune favors her:

> "Luck seemed to be with her. Nobody was about, the window was ajar, and there on the table lay that which she had now come to look on in the light of a present for a good girl." (034-079)

The moment of ap[[family-and-duty|parent]] good luck is immediately overturned:

> "She crept out of her hiding-place, stole round the edge of the lawn, entered the room, and had just grasped the case in her hand, when it was borne sharply in upon her that luck was not with her so much as she had supposed. A heavy hand was placed upon her shoulder..." (034-079)

This pattern — luck appears, then reverses — recurs throughout the novel. It is the structural principle of Wodehouse's comic machinery: every ap[[family-and-duty|parent]] success contains the seed of its own comic undoing.

### Fate as Comic Force

Unlike tragedy, where fate is cruel and inexorable, fate in Wodehouse is mischievous and ultimately benevolent. Coincidences create temporary chaos but ultimately lead characters toward t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] proper romantic resolutions. The universe of *The Small Bachelor* is fundamentally comic — fate may toy [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h its characters, but it will not destroy them.
