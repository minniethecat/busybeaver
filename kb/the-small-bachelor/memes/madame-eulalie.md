# Madame Eulalie

## Identity and Role

Madame Eulalie is a [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-teller operating in [[new-york|New York]] during the Prohibition era. Her profession — telling [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]s for a fee — places her in [[crime-and-law|legal]] jeopardy, as [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling was among the activities targeted by the era's moral reform movements. Despite this, she maintains a flourishing practice and counts [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] among her most devoted clients:

> "She believes everything Madame Eulalie sees in the crystal." (020-040)

## Personality and Manner

Madame Eulalie is characterized by an irrepressible amiability. Her first [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]] is marked by "that brisk amiability which was one of her main charms" (035-003). She greets clients [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h warmth, treats [[class-and-society|social]] awkwardness [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h blithe good [[humor-and-comedy|humor]], and somehow manages to make [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]], the apostle of rational [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]], forget his principles.

Her effect on [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] is p[[art-and-artists|art]]icularly notable. After t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] initial meeting, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] — who has dedicated his career to debunking irrationality — carefully preserves the match she used to light her cigarette "reverently in his left top waistcoat-pocket" (035-054). The adverb "reverently" is Wodehouse at his most precise: the [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert, undone by a [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-teller's charm.

## Plot Function

Madame Eulalie serves as a nexus for several of the novel's intersecting plotlines:

1. **The Fortune-Telling Investigation**: Her profession attracts [[crime-and-law|police]] scrutiny, [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]] informing [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] that "Madame Eulalie is in the habit of telling [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]s for a monetary consideration. Against the [[crime-and-law|law]], sir" (021-054).

2. **The Romantic Complications**: [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] hopes to use Madame Eulalie's influence over her mother to divert [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s matrimonial ambitions away from [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]]: "You must get Madame Eulalie at least to steer mother off [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]]" (020-042).

3. **The [[may-stubbs|May Stubbs]] Resolution**: Madame Eulalie's office becomes the setting for crucial plot developments involving [[may-stubbs|May Stubbs]] and [[George-Finch|George]]'s romantic entanglements.

She refers to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] as "Jimmy" — a familiarity that would be shocking from anyone else but from her seems entirely natural: "How very charming of you, Jimmy!" (035-092).

## Narrative Function

Madame Eulalie embodies a recurring Wodehouse type: the figure whose irrational profession paradoxically makes them more sensible than the rationalists around them. While [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s Booklets offer systematic solutions that consistently fail, Madame Eulalie's crystal ball — however fraudulent — manages to influence events in ways that ultimately serve the novel's comic resolution. She is a benevolent fraud, and the novel treats her as such: [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h affection rather than censure.
