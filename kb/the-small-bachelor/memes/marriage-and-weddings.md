# Marriage and Weddings

## Overview

Marriage serves as both the romantic goal and the primary plot engine of *The Small Bachelor*. [[George-Finch|George Finch]] wishes to marry [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]], but he must overcome the obstacle of her mother, [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], who judges suitors primarily by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]. The novel treats marriage not merely as a romantic conclusion but as a [[class-and-society|social]] transaction that reveals [[class-and-society|class]] prejudices and [[family-and-duty|family]] dynamics.

## In the Novel

### Marriage as Social Transaction

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] views marriage through the lens of [[class-and-society|social]] advancement. She considers [[George-Finch|George Finch]] an unsuitable match for her daughter because he is "a penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]]" (025-046). Her opposition is purely financial:

> "Do you imagine I intend to allow you to ruin your life by marrying a despicable fortune-hunter?" (025-044)

When [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] reveals that [[George-Finch|George]] is actually [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y — having inherited his uncle's entire [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]] — [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s opposition vanishes instantly. Her sudden reversal exposes the mercenary nature of her marriage politics:

> "Then allow me to inform you," said Hamilton Beamish, triumphantly, "that George Finch is an exceedingly wealthy man." (025-096)

### The Elopement Plot

When conventional [[engagement-and-courtship|courtship]] fails, the characters turn to elopement as an alternative. The novel's final act revolves around [[George-Finch|George]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s plan to marry in secret, circumventing [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s opposition. This comic elopement plot is a [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehouse device, generating mis[[engagement-and-courtship|understanding]]s, missed connections, and last-minute reversals.

### Marriage as Comic Resolution

In the Wodehouse universe, marriage represents the natural and satisfying conclusion to romantic [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]]. The obstacles to [[George-Finch|George]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s union — [[class-and-society|class]] prejudice, financial mis[[engagement-and-courtship|understanding]], and comic [[fate-and-coincidence|coincidence]] — are all resolved by the novel's end, allowing the couple to marry.
