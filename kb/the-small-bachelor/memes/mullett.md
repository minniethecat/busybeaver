## Identity and Background

Mullett is a reformed ex-convict employed as [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s valet and general servant at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]]. His precise origins remain shadowy throughout the novel, though [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] — who recommended him to [[George-Finch|George]] — is fully aware of his criminal history. [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]] of the [[new-york|New York]] [[crime-and-law|police]] seems to recognize Mullett from his past, repeatedly insisting "I have seen your face before" and inquiring whether Mullett has a brother who resembles him [004-002][004-004].

> "Reformed," said Mullett hastily. [004-038]

The word captures Mullett's central narrative function: he is a man perpetually navigating the tension between his reformed present and his unreformed impulses. His admission of reform is delivered [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h conspicuous haste, suggesting a man who wishes to close the subject as quickly as possible.

## Role and Duties

Mullett serves as [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s valet, performing the ordinary duties of a domestic servant — sweeping the roof, mixing whisky-and-sodas, and attending to his employer's wardrobe [004-001][004-124]. His position, however, extends beyond mere domestic service. He functions as an informal confidant to [[George-Finch|George]], who confides in him at length about his romantic feelings for [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]], despite Mullett's evident discomfort [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h such intimate disclosures.

[[George-Finch|George]]'s monologues to Mullett provide some of the novel's most revealing character moments. In one extended scene, [[George-Finch|George]] recounts every detail of his first encounter [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] — following her home, loitering outside her house, the wind blowing his hat off, her saying "Oh, thank you so much!" — all delivered to a presumably bewildered Mullett [004-096][004-098][004-106][004-112].

> "I don't know why I'm telling you this, Mullett." [004-098]

## The Tempter Within

Mullett's most distinctive character trait is his internal dialogue [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a personified "Tempter" — a voice that urges him toward criminal relapse. This comic device transforms what might be a dark backstory into one of the novel's running jokes:

> "Yes, sir, it's awful what that Tempter will suggest to me. Sometimes, when you're lying asleep, he says 'Slip a sponge of Chloroform under his nose, Mullett, and clear out with the swag!'" [004-060]

The Tempter represents Mullett's unreformed self, and his frank discussion of these impulses — rather than hiding them — paradoxically demonstrates his genuine commitment to honesty. [[George-Finch|George Finch]], characteristically distracted by his own romantic preoccupations, seems largely unfazed by his valet's confession of near-criminal temptation.

## Vocabulary and Speech

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] holds Mullett's vocabulary in p[[art-and-artists|art]]icular contempt. An advocate of pure English and author of a booklet on the subject, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] frequently corrects Mullett's word choices and holds him up as an example of linguistic degradation:

> "I dislike the expression. It is the sort of expression Mullett would use: and I know few things more calculated to make a thinking man shudder than Mullett's vocabulary." [005-079]

Earlier, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] instructs Mullett to "Avoid jargon. Strive for the Word Beautiful. Read my [[beamish-booklets|booklet on Pure English]]" [002-037]. Mullett's linguistic shortcomings serve as comic punctuation throughout the novel, contrasting [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s pedantic insistence on verbal precision.

## Comic Function

Mullett occupies the [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehousian role of the comic servant — a position that allows the author to deflate the pretensions of the principal characters. When [[George-Finch|George]] waxes poetic about [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s voice "like the fluting of young birds in the springtime," Mullett's presence as audience underscores the absurdity of such effusions [004-104]. His deadpan responses to [[George-Finch|George]]'s romantic monologues serve as an implicit comic commentary.

His dual role as both servant and informant — reporting [[George-Finch|George]]'s activities to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] — creates narrative irony. [[George-Finch|George]] confides in Mullett as a trusted servant, unaware that Mullett passes information to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]], who uses it to meddle in [[George-Finch|George]]'s affairs [005-005][005-007][005-028].
