# Officer Garroway

## Identity and Role

Officer Garroway is a [[crime-and-law|police]]man of the [[new-york|New York]] Police Dep[[art-and-artists|art]]ment who, through an unlikely chain of events, becomes a pupil of [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]]'s [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|self-improvement]] system. His ambition — to become a poet — is one of the novel's most charming absurdities: a uniformed officer of the [[crime-and-law|law]], studying verse composition under the guidance of the author of [[the-beamish-booklets|the Beamish Booklets]].

His full name, as he pronounces it in the grip of a sneezing fit, is a miniature comic set piece:

> "Gar-ish-wash-*wush*… Garroway," said the policeman, becoming calmer. (045-014)

## The Suffering Poet

Garroway's most distinctive characteristic is his hay fever. His [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]]s are frequently punctuated by sneezes:

> "Achoo!" replied Officer Garroway. (045-005)

The sneezing is never merely comic — it humanizes the figure of authority, reducing the [[crime-and-law|police]]man from an instrument of the [[crime-and-law|law]] to a man who cannot control his own nose. Wodehouse returns to this motif throughout the novel's later chapters, where Garroway's affliction becomes a running gag that never quite exhausts itself.

## Gallantry and Gratitude

Despite his physical afflictions, Garroway possesses an instinctive gallantry. When offered sympathy — p[[art-and-artists|art]]icularly by young women — he responds [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h touching formality:

> "Thank you, miss," said Officer Garroway gratefully. A man can do with a bit of sympathy on these occasions. (045-027)

And later:

> "I feel better already, miss," said Officer Garroway gallantly, "thanks to your kind sympathy. Good nish-nosh, miss." (045-043)

The "nish-nosh" — a garbled "night-night" filtered through hay fever — is typical Wodehouse: a formal valediction comically deformed by physical incapacity.

## The Beamish Connection

Garroway's improbable relationship [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] — the [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert teaching poetry to a [[crime-and-law|police]]man — embodies the novel's central satirical theme: the collision between systematic [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|self-improvement]] and irreducible human reality. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] believes he can "make a poet out of two sticks and a piece of orange-peel" (002-023); Garroway is the living test case. That he is a [[crime-and-law|police]]man — the embodiment of institutional authority — makes the experiment all the more piquant.

When [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] encounters Garroway in an unexpected context, his reaction captures t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] relationship in a single line:

> "Garroway!" cried Hamilton Beamish. "What are you doing here? And what the devil does this masquerade mean?" (021-033)

## The Wistful Authority Figure

Wodehouse's treatment of Garroway is consistently affectionate. He is never menacing, never corrupt, never cruel — merely a man [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a cold, a job, and an improbable dream of literary achievement:

> Officer Garroway shook his head wistfully. (045-029)

The adverb "wistfully" does a great deal of work. It transforms the [[crime-and-law|police]]man from a plot function into a person — someone who has hoped for something and been disappointed, someone whose life contains longings that his uniform cannot express.

## Plot Function

Garroway appears at crucial moments in the novel's late chapters, serving as both an obstacle (the [[crime-and-law|law]] must be satisfied) and an ally (his sympathies are easily enlisted). His climactic [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]]s at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]] roof bring the novel's farcical complications to a head, and his ultimate disposition — as [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h all Wodehouse characters — is benevolent.
