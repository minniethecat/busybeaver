# II

[025-001] Having uttered these words, [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee Horatio]] stood gazing at his wife with something of the spellbound horror of a man who has bored a hole in a dam and sees the water trickling through and knows that it is too late to stop it. He had had a sort of idea all along that the news might affect her rather powerfully, and his guess was coming true. Nothing could make a woman of Mrs. Waddington’s physique “leap from her chair”: but she had begun to rise slowly like a balloon half-filled with gas: and her face had become so contorted and her eyes so bulging that any competent medical man of sporting tastes would have laid seven to four on a fit of apoplexy in the next few minutes.

[025-002] But by some miracle this disaster—if you could call it that—did not occur. For quite a considerable time the sufferer had trouble with her vocal chords and could emit nothing but guttural croaks. Then, mastering herself with a strong effort, she spoke.

[025-003] “What did you say?”

[025-004] “You heard,” said Sigsbee H. sullenly, twisting his fingers and wishing that he was out in Utah, rustling cattle.

[025-005] Mrs. Waddington moistened her lips.

[025-006] “Did I understand you to say that [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] was engaged to be married to that [[George-Finch|Finch]]?”

[025-007] “Yes, I did. And,” added Sigsbee H., giving battle in the first line of trenches, “it’s no good saying it was all my fault, because I had nothing to do with it.”

[025-008] “It was you who brought this man into the house.”

[025-009] “Well, yes.” Sigsbee had overlooked that weak spot in his defences. “Well, yes.”

[025-010] There came upon Mrs. Waddington a ghastly calm like that which comes upon the surface of molten lava in the crater of a volcano just before the stuff shoots out and starts doing the local villagers a bit of no good.

[025-011] “Ring the bell,” she said.

[025-012] Sigsbee H. rang the bell.

[025-013] “Ferris,” said Mrs. Waddington, “ask Miss Molly to come here.”

[025-014] “Very good, madam.”

[025-015] In the interval which elapsed between the departure of the butler and the arrival of the erring daughter, no conversation brilliant enough to be worth reporting took place in the room. Once Sigsbee said “Er—” and in reply Mrs. Waddington said “Be quiet!” but that completed the dialogue. When Molly entered, Mrs. Waddington was looking straight in front of her and heaving gently, and Sigsbee H. had just succeeded in breaking a valuable china figure which he had taken from an occasional table and was trying in a preoccupied manner to balance on the end of a paper-knife.

[025-016] “Ferris says you want to see me, mother,” said Molly, floating brightly in.

[025-017] She stood there, looking at the two with shining eyes. Her cheeks were delightfully flushed: and there was about her so radiant an air of sweet, innocent, girlish gaiety that it was all Mrs. Waddington could do to refrain from hurling a bust of Edgar Allan Poe at her head.

[025-018] “I do want to see you,” said Mrs. Waddington. “Pray tell me instantly what is all this nonsense I hear about you and. …” She choked. “… and Mr. Finch.”

[025-019] “To settle a bet,” said Sigsbee H., “is his name Finch or Pinch?”

[025-020] “Finch, of course.”

[025-021] “I’m bad at names,” said Sigsbee. “I was in college with a fellow called Follansbee and do you think I could get it out of my nut that that guy’s name was Ferguson? Not in a million years! I. …”

[025-022] “Sigsbee!”

[025-023] “Hello?”

[025-024] “Be quiet.” Mrs. Waddington concentrated her attention on Molly once more. “Your father says that you told him some absurd story about being. …”

[025-025] “Engaged to George?” said Molly. “Yes, it’s quite true. I am. By a most extraordinary [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] we met this afternoon in Central Park near the Zoo. …”

[025-026] “A place,” said Sigsbee H., “I’ve meant to go to a hundred times and never seen yet.”

[025-027] “Sigsbee!”

[025-028] “All right, all right! I was only saying. …”

[025-029] “We were both tremendously surprised, of course,” said Molly. “I said ‘Fancy meeting you here!’ and he said. …”

[025-030] “I have no wish to hear what Mr. Finch said.”

[025-031] “Well, anyway, we walked round for awhile, looking at the animals, and suddenly he asked me to marry him outside the cage of the Siberian yak.”

[025-032] “No, sir!” exclaimed Sigsbee H. with a sudden strange firmness, the indulgent father who for once in his life asserts himself. “When you get married, you’ll be married in St. Thomas’s like any other nice girl.”

[025-033] “I mean it was outside the cage of the Siberian yak that he asked me to marry him.”

[025-034] “Oh, ah!” said Sigsbee H.

[025-035] A dreamy look had crept into Molly’s eyes. Her lips were curved in a tender smile, as if she were reliving that wonderful moment in a girl’s life, when the man she loves beckons to her to follow him into Paradise.

[025-036] “You ought to have seen his ears!” she said. “They were absolutely crimson.”

[025-037] “You don’t say!” chuckled Sigsbee H.

[025-038] “Scarlet! And, when he tried to speak, he gargled.”

[025-039] “The poor simp!”

[025-040] Molly turned on her father with flaming eyes.

[025-041] “How dare you call my dear darling Georgie a simp?”

[025-042] “How dare you call that simp your dear darling Georgie?” demanded Mrs. Waddington.

[025-043] “Because he is my dear darling Georgie. I [[love-and-romance|love]] him with all my heart, the precious lamb, and I’m going to marry him.”

[025-044] “You are going to do nothing of the kind!” Mrs. Waddington quivered with outraged indignation. “Do you imagine I intend to allow you to ruin your life by marrying a despicable [[money-and-wealth|fortune]]-hunter?”

[025-045] “He isn’t a despicable [[fortune-and-inheritance|fortune]]-hunter.”

[025-046] “He is a penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]].”

[025-047] “Well, I’m sure he is frightfully clever and will be able to sell his pictures for ever so much.”

[025-048] “Tchah!”

[025-049] “Besides,” said Molly defiantly, “when I marry I get that pearl necklace which father gave mother. I can sell that, and it will keep us going for years.”

[025-050] Mrs. Waddington was about to reply—and there is little reason to doubt that that reply would have been about as red-hot a comeback as any hundred and eighty pound woman had ever spoken—when she was checked by a sudden exclamation of agony that proceeded from the lips of her husband.

[025-051] “Whatever is the matter, Sigsbee?” she said, annoyed.

[025-052] Sigsbee H. seemed to be wrestling with acute mental agitation. He was staring at his daughter with protruding eyes.

[025-053] “Did you say you were going to sell that necklace?” he stammered.

[025-054] “Oh, be quiet, Sigsbee!” said Mrs. Waddington. “What does it matter whether she sells the necklace or not? It has nothing to do with the argument. The point is that this misguided girl is proposing to throw herself away on a miserable, paint-daubing, ukulele-playing artist. …”

[025-055] “He doesn’t play the ukulele. He told me so.”

[025-056] “… when she might, if she chose, marry a delightful man with a fine old English title who would. …”

[025-057] Mrs. Waddington broke off. There had come back to her the memory of that scene in [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]]’s office.

[025-058] Molly seized the opportunity afforded by her unexpected silence to make a counterattack.

[025-059] “I wouldn’t marry [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]] if he were the last man in the world.”

[025-060] “Honey,” said Sigsbee H. in a low, pleading voice, “I don’t think I’d sell that necklace if I were you.”

[025-061] “Of course I shall sell it. We shall need the money when we are married.”

[025-062] “You are not going to be married,” said Mrs. Waddington, recovering. “I should have thought any right-minded girl would have despised this wretched Finch. Why, the man appears to be so poor-spirited that he didn’t even dare to come here and tell me this awful news. He left it to you. …”

[025-063] “George was not able to come here. The poor pet has been arrested by a policeman.”

[025-064] “Ha!” cried Mrs. Waddington triumphantly. “And that is the sort of man you propose to marry! A gaol-bird!”

[025-065] “Well, I think it shows what a sweet nature he has. He was so happy at being engaged that he suddenly stopped at Fifty-Ninth Street and [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] and started giving away dollar-bills to everybody who came by. In about two minutes there was a crowd stretching right across to Madison Avenue, and the traffic was blocked for miles, and they called out the [[crime-and-law|police]]-reserves, and George was taken away in a patrol-wagon, and I telephoned to [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] to go and bail him out and bring him along here. They ought to arrive at any moment.”

[025-066] “Mr. Hamilton Beamish and Mr. George Finch,” said Ferris in the doorway. And the nicely-graduated way in which he spoke the two names would have conveyed at once to any intelligent listener that Hamilton Beamish was an honoured guest but that he had been forced to admit George Finch—against all the promptings of his better nature—because Mr. Beamish had told him to and he had been quelled by the man’s cold, spectacled eye.

[025-067] “Here we are,” said Hamilton Beamish heartily. “Just in time, I perceive, to join in a jolly [[family-and-duty|family]] discussion.”

[025-068] Mrs. Waddington looked bleachingly at George, who was trying to hide behind a gate-leg table. For George Finch was conscious of not looking his best. Nothing so disorders the outer man as the process of being arrested and hauled to the coop by a posse of [[new-york|New York]] gendarmes. George’s collar was hanging loose from its stud: his waistcoat lacked three buttons: and his right eye was oddly discoloured where a high-minded officer, piqued by the fact that he should have collected crowds by scattering dollar-bills and even more incensed by the discovery that he had scattered all he possessed and had none left, had given him a hearty buffet during the ride in the patrol-wagon.

[025-069] “There is no discussion,” said Mrs. Waddington. “You do not suppose I am going to allow my daughter to marry a man like that.”

[025-070] “Tut-tut!” said Hamilton Beamish. “George is not looking his best just now, but a wash and brush-up will do wonders. … What is your objection to George?”

[025-071] Mrs. Waddington was at a momentary loss for a reply. Anybody, suddenly questioned as to why they disliked a slug or a snake or a black-beetle, might find it difficult on the spur of the moment to analyse and dissect their prejudice. Mrs. Waddington looked on her antipathy to George Finch as one of those deep, natural, fundamental impulses which the sensible person takes for granted. Broadly speaking, she objected to George because he was George. It was, as it were, his essential Georgeness that offended her. But, seeing that she was expected to be analytical, she forced her mind to the task.

[025-072] “He is an artist.”

[025-073] “So was Michelangelo.”

[025-074] “I never met him.”

[025-075] “He was a very great man.”

[025-076] Mrs. Waddington raised her eyebrows.

[025-077] “I completely fail to understand, Mr. Beamish, why, when we are discussing this young man here with the black eye and the dirty collar, you should persist in diverting the conversation to the subject of a perfect stranger like this Mr. Angelo.”

[025-078] “I merely wished to point out,” said Hamilton Beamish stiffly, “that the fact that he is an artist does not necessarily damn a man.”

[025-079] “There is no need,” retorted Mrs. Waddington with even greater stiffness, “to use bad language.”

[025-080] “Besides, George is a rotten artist.”

[025-081] “Rotten to the core, no doubt.”

[025-082] “I mean,” said Hamilton Beamish, flushing slightly at the lapse from the English Pure into which emotion had led him, “he paints so badly that you can hardly call him an artist at all.”

[025-083] “Is that so?” said George, speaking for the first time and speaking nastily.

[025-084] “I am sure George is one of the cleverest artists living,” cried Molly.

[025-085] “He is not,” thundered Hamilton Beamish. “He is an incompetent amateur.”

[025-086] “Exactly!” said Mrs. Waddington. “And consequently can never hope to make money.”

[025-087] Hamilton Beamish’s eyes lit up behind their spectacles.

[025-088] “Is that your chief objection?” he asked.

[025-089] “Is what my chief objection?”

[025-090] “That George has no money?”

[025-091] “But. …” began George.

[025-092] “Shut up!” said Hamilton Beamish. “I ask you, Mrs. Waddington, would you give your consent to this [[marriage-and-weddings|marriage]] if my friend George Finch were a wealthy man?”

[025-093] “It is a waste of time to discuss such. …”

[025-094] “Would you?”

[025-095] “Possibly I would.”

[025-096] “Then allow me to inform you,” said Hamilton Beamish, triumphantly, “that George Finch is an exceedingly wealthy man. His uncle Thomas, whose entire fortune he inherited two years ago, was Finch, Finch, Finch, Butterfield and Finch, the well-known Corporation Law firm. George, my boy, let me congratulate you. All is well. Mrs. Waddington has withdrawn her objections.”

[025-097] Mrs. Waddington snorted, but it was the snort of a beaten woman, outgeneralled by a superior intelligence.

[025-098] “But. …”

[025-099] “No.” Hamilton Beamish raised his hand. “You cannot go back on what you said. You stated in distinct terms that, if George had money, you would consent to the marriage.”

[025-100] “And, anyway, I don’t know what all the fuss is about,” said Molly. “Because I am going to marry him, no matter what anybody says.”

[025-101] Mrs. Waddington capitulated.

[025-102] “Very well! I am nobody, I see. What I say does not matter in the slightest.”

[025-103] “Mother!” said George reproachfully.

[025-104] “Mother?” echoed Mrs. Waddington, starting violently.

[025-105] “Now that everything is so happily settled, of course I regard you in that light.”

[025-106] “Oh, do you?” said Mrs. Waddington.

[025-107] “Oh, I do,” said George.

[025-108] Mrs. Waddington sniffed unpleasantly.

[025-109] “I have been overwhelmed and forced into consenting to a marriage of which I strongly disapprove,” she said, “but I may be permitted to say one word. I have a feeling that this wedding will never take place.”

[025-110] “What do you mean?” said Molly. “Of course it will take place. Why shouldn’t it?”

[025-111] Mrs. Waddington sniffed again.

[025-112] “Mr. Finch,” she said, “though a very incompetent artist, has lived for a considerable time in the heart of [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]] and mingled daily with Bohemians of both sexes and questionable morals. …”

[025-113] “What are you hinting?” demanded Molly.

[025-114] “I am not hinting,” replied Mrs. Waddington with dignity. “I am saying. And what I am saying is this. Do not come to *me* for sympathy if this Finch of yours turns out to have the sort of moral code which you might expect in one who deliberately and of his own free-will goes and lives [[washington-square|near Washington Square]]. I say again, that I have a presentiment that this marriage will never take place. I had a similar presentiment regarding the wedding of my sister-in-law and a young man named John Porter. I said, ‘I feel that this wedding will never take place.’ And events proved me right. John Porter, at the very moment when he was about to enter the church, was arrested on a charge of bigamy.”

[025-115] George uttered protesting noises.

[025-116] “But my morals are above reproach.”

[025-117] “So you say.”

[025-118] “I assure you that, as far as women are concerned, I can scarcely tell one from another.”

[025-119] “Precisely,” replied Mrs. Waddington, “what John Porter said when they asked him why he had married six different girls.”

[025-120] Hamilton Beamish looked at his watch.

[025-121] “Well, now that everything is satisfactorily settled. …”

[025-122] “For the moment,” said Mrs. Waddington.

[025-123] “Now that everything is satisfactorily settled,” proceeded Hamilton Beamish, “I will be leaving you. I have to get back and [[fashion-and-appearance|dress]]. I am speaking at a dinner of the Great Neck Social and Literary Society tonight.”

[025-124] The silence that followed his departure was followed by a question from Sigsbee H. Waddington.

[025-125] “Molly, my dear,” said Sigsbee H., “touching on that necklace. Now that this splendid young fellow turns out to be very rich, you will not want to sell it, of course?”

[025-126] Molly reflected.

[025-127] “Yes, I think I will. I never liked it much. It’s too showy. I shall sell it and buy something very nice with the money for George. A lot of diamond pins or watches or motorcars or something. And, whenever we look at them we will think of you, daddy dear.”

[025-128] “Thanks,” said Mr. Waddington huskily. “Thanks.”

[025-129] “Seldom in my life,” observed Mrs. Waddington, coming abruptly out of the brooding coma into which she had sunk, “have I ever had a stronger presentiment than the one to which I alluded just now.”

[025-130] “Oh, mother!” said George.

[025-131] Hamilton Beamish, gathering up his hat in the hall, became aware that something was pawing at his sleeve. He looked down and perceived Sigsbee H. Waddington.

[025-132] “Say!” said Sigsbee H. in a hushed undertone. “Say, listen!”

[025-133] “Is anything the matter?”

[025-134] “You bet your tortoiseshell-rimmed spectacles something’s the matter,” whispered Sigsbee H. urgently. “Say, listen. Can I have a word with you? I want your advice.”

[025-135] “I’m in a hurry.”

[025-136] “How long will you be before you start out for this Hoboken Clambake of yours?”

[025-137] “The dinner of the Great Neck Social and Literary Society, to which I imagine you to allude, is at eight o’clock. I shall motor down, leaving my apartment at twenty minutes past seven.”

[025-138] “Then it’s no good trying to see you tonight. Say, listen. Will you be home tomorrow?”

[025-139] “Yes.”

[025-140] “Right!” said Sigsbee H.
