# Police and Law

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, [[crime-and-law|police]] officers and [[crime-and-law|legal]] enforcement function not as instruments of justice but as sources of comic disruption. Wodehouse's [[crime-and-law|police]]men are neither threatening nor heroic — they are bureaucratic functionaries enforcing absurd [[crime-and-law|law]]s, and t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] interventions invariably produce [[humor-and-comedy|farce]] rather than danger. The novel's treatment of [[crime-and-law|law]] enforcement epitomises Wodehouse's light satirical mode, in which authority figures are gently mocked but never truly condemned.

## In the Novel

### Officer Garroway

The novel's principal [[crime-and-law|police]] presence is [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]], a physically awkward constable who first appears on the roof of the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]]. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] summons him — "Ah, [[officer-garroway|Garroway]], there you are at last" (002-066) — and a [[crime-and-law|police]]man materialises:

> "He was a long, stringy policeman, who flowed out of his uniform at odd spots, as if Nature, setting out to make a constable, had had a good deal of material left over which it had not liked to throw away but hardly seemed able to fit neatly into the general scheme. He had large, knobby wrists of a geranium hue and just that extra four or five inches of neck which disqualify a man for high honours in a beauty competition." (003-001)

This is one of Wodehouse's finest character descriptions. The image of a man who "flowed out of his uniform at odd spots" — as if the uniform, not the body, were the primary form — captures the essential Wodehousian inversion: the [[crime-and-law|police]]man is a man ill-fitted to the role of enforcer, and his physical awkwardness is the outward sign of an inward gentleness.

[[officer-garroway|Garroway]] is defined by his mildness. His blue eyes and philosophical musings make him more poet than enforcer. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] immediately seizes upon him as a potential subject for [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] training, commanding him to look out at the city and report what he sees (003-006) — a question the [[crime-and-law|police]]man answers [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h dreamy aesthetic contemplation rather than investigative acumen. When pressed, he can only sigh that the view is "stark" (004-009).

### The Mullett Subplot

[[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]]'s most significant narrative function involves the exposure of [[mullett|Mullett]]'s criminal past. Recognising [[mullett|Mullett]] from the Rogues Gallery at [[crime-and-law|police]] headqu[[art-and-artists|art]]ers, [[officer-garroway|Garroway]] feels [[family-and-duty|duty]]-bound to inform [[George-Finch|George Finch]]:

> "The moment I was introduced to him, I detected something oddly familiar about Mr. Mullett's face, and I have just recollected that I recently saw a photograph of him in the Rogues Gallery at Headquarters. You are possibly aware, sir, that convicted criminals are 'mugged' — that is to say, photographed in various positions — at the commencement of their term of incarceration." (004-039)

The formal precision of the diction — "at the commencement of t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] term of incarceration" — is characteristic of [[officer-garroway|Garroway]]'s voice. He speaks like a [[crime-and-law|police]] manual written by someone who finds the manual faintly embarrassing.

[[mullett|Mullett]], it emerges, served a year's imprisonment for "an inside burglary job" (004-053). [[officer-garroway|Garroway]] delivers this information [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h genuine reluctance — "I would avoid it, if I could reconcile the act [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h my conscience, but [[family-and-duty|duty]] is [[family-and-duty|duty]]" (004-033) — yet his revelation produces no lasting harm. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s reformist philosophy, [[art-and-artists|art]]iculated in his *Yale Review* series on the "Problem of the Reformed Criminal" (005-010), ensures [[mullett|Mullett]]'s continued employment, and the ex-convict remains loyal throughout.

### The Ex-Convict's History

The novel traces [[mullett|Mullett]]'s criminal career [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h surprising specificity:

> "I used to get a place as a valet, sir, and wait till I saw my chance, and then skin out with everything I could lay my hands on." (004-054)

The bluntness of "skin out [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h everything" — the colloquial frankness of a man describing his own [[crime-and-law|crime]]s — is juxtaposed against [[officer-garroway|Garroway]]'s formal [[crime-and-law|police]] diction. The contrast between the two registers — institutional formality and criminal candor — is itself a comic effect.

### The Fortune-Telling Investigation

A second [[crime-and-law|police]] thread involves [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]]'s [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling establishment. [[officer-garroway|Garroway]] is dispatched to "effect a cop" (021-047) — a vulgarism [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] sharply corrects — because [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-telling for [[money-and-wealth|money]] is il[[crime-and-law|legal]]:

> "It has been called to the attention of my superior officers, Mr. Beamish, that Madame Eulalie is in the habit of telling fortunes for a monetary consideration. Against the law, sir." (021-054)

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] dismisses the [[crime-and-law|law]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h characteristic imperiousness — "Ridiculous! If that's the [[crime-and-law|law]], alter it!" — and [[officer-garroway|Garroway]] retreats, convinced by [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]]'s "limpid truth." The [[crime-and-law|law]], in Wodehouse's world, is a paper tiger that retreats before determined [[class-and-society|social]] superiors.

### The Arrest of George Finch

In the novel's most exuberant [[crime-and-law|legal]] set piece, [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is arrested after celebrating his [[engagement-and-courtship|engagement]] by spontaneously distributing dollar bills at the corner of Fifty-Ninth Street and [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]. The resulting crowd blocks traffic "right across to Madison Avenue," the [[crime-and-law|police]] reserves are called out, and [[George-Finch|George]] is taken away in a patrol wagon (025-065). The arrest is pure [[humor-and-comedy|farce]] — an act of generosity punished by the [[crime-and-law|law]]'s absurd machinery.

### The Law as Comic Obstacle

Throughout the novel, the [[crime-and-law|law]] serves as a comic obstacle rather than a moral force. Its representatives — [[officer-garroway|Garroway]], the [[crime-and-law|police]] reserves at the dollar-bill incident — are well-meaning but ineffectual. The novel's true [[class-and-society|social]] order is maintained not by [[crime-and-law|law]] but by the informal networks of [[class-and-society|class]], [[money-and-wealth|wealth]], and personal connection. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s "Ridiculous! If that's the [[crime-and-law|law]], alter it!" is not an act of rebellion — it is the confident assertion of someone who knows that [[crime-and-law|law]]s apply to other people.
