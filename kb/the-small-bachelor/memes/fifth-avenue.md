# Fifth Avenue

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, Fifth Avenue represents the polar opposite of [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]]: [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]], [[class-and-society|respectability]], and [[class-and-society|social]] convention. Wodehouse uses the geography of [[new-york|New York]] — [[Greenwich-Village|the Village]] below, Fifth Avenue above — to map the novel's [[class-and-society|social]] divisions onto physical space. Characters in the novel navigate between these two poles, and the movement from one to the other tracks the romantic plot's progress.

## In the Novel

### The Geography of Class

Fifth Avenue is never described at length in the novel — it functions as a shorthand for everything [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] values and [[George-Finch|George Finch]], as an [[art-and-artists|artist]], stands outside of. The Waddington world is a Fifth Avenue world: [[money-and-wealth|money]], propriety, and the relentless judgment of one's peers. When [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] dismisses [[George-Finch|George]] as "a penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]]" (025-046), she is dismissing [[Greenwich-Village|the Village]] in favour of the Avenue.

### The Dollar-Bill Incident

Fifth Avenue appears most vividly in the novel's comic climax, when [[George-Finch|George Finch]] celebrates his [[engagement-and-courtship|engagement]] by spontaneously distributing dollar bills at the corner of Fifty-Ninth Street and Fifth Avenue:

> "He suddenly stopped at Fifty-Ninth Street and Fifth Avenue and started giving away dollar-bills to everybody who came by. In about two minutes there was a crowd stretching right across to Madison Avenue, and the traffic was blocked for miles." (025-065)

The location is significant: [[George-Finch|George]] brings Village generosity to Fifth Avenue, and the result is chaos. The [[crime-and-law|police]] — instruments of Fifth Avenue order — [[police-and-law|arrest]] him for disrupting the city's proper functioning. His spontaneous generosity is illegible to the Fifth Avenue mindset.

### Jewellers and Commerce

Fifth Avenue's commercial identity appears in a throwaway joke about a "prosperous firm of jewellers on Fifth Avenue" (030-004) whose missing wristwatch remains an unsolved mystery. The reference reinforces Fifth Avenue as the domain of [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] and luxury — a place where prosperous jewellers conduct t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] trade, far from the [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] studios of [[Greenwich-Village|the Village]].

## Significance

Fifth Avenue in the novel serves as a geographical shorthand for the [[class-and-society|social]] world that [[George-Finch|George Finch]] must navigate. It is less a place than an idea: the idea that [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] determines worth, that propriety trumps passion, and that a [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] [[art-and-artists|artist]] has no business marrying into respectable [[class-and-society|society]]. When the novel's happy ending arrives, it is because [[George-Finch|George]] has been revealed to belong — financially — to Fifth Avenue after all.
