# Class and Society

## Overview

*The Small Bachelor* offers a sharp [[humor-and-comedy|satire]] of early twentieth-century [[new-york|New York]] society, where social standing is determined by [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]], [[family-and-duty|family]] connections, and outward respectability. P.G. Wodehouse uses the clash between different social worlds — the [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] [[art-and-artists|artist]]ic qu[[art-and-artists|art]]er of [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]] and the [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y upper class of [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] — to generate both [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]] and social commentary.

## In the Novel

### The Two Worlds

The novel presents two contrasting social spheres. [[George-Finch|George Finch]] inhabits the [[art-and-artists|artist]]ic, [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] world of [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]], centered on the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan]] ap[[art-and-artists|art]]ment house. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] represents the world of [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]], exclusivity, and social ambition. The conflict between these worlds drives much of the plot.

The [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan]] itself embodies this cultural geography:

> The Sheridan stands in the heart of New York's Bohemian and artistic quarter. If you threw a brick from any of its windows, you would be certain to brain some rising interior decorator, some Vorticist sculptor, or a writer of revolutionary vers libre. (002-002)

### Judgment by Association

Characters in the novel are constantly judged not by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] own merits but by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] associations and social category. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] judges [[George-Finch|George]] not as an individual but as a representative of the [[art-and-artists|artist]] class, which she despises. Her prejudice is categorical rather than personal:

> "A woman of her type looks on artists as blots on the social scheme. I told you she judged her fellow-creatures solely by the extent of their bank balances." (018-012)

### Respectability as Performance

Wodehouse exposes the performative nature of social respectability. Characters like [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] maintain elaborate facades of propriety while t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] real motivations — [[money-and-wealth|money]], status, and control — are trans[[family-and-duty|parent]]ly mercenary. The novel's comic situations often arise from the gap between social performance and genuine feeling.

### The Democratic Impulse

Beneath the [[humor-and-comedy|satire]], the novel affirms a more democratic vision. [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] [[love-and-romance|love]]s [[George-Finch|George]] regardless of his social standing or profession. Her disregard for class barriers represents the novel's implicit critique of a society organized around [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] and status.
