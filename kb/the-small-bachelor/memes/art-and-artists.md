# Art and Artists

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, art and artists occupy a central thematic position. The protagonist, [[George-Finch|George Finch]], is a painter living in [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]], [[new-york|New York]]'s [[Greenwich-Village|Bohemian quarter]]. Through [[George-Finch|George]]'s identity as an artist, P.G. Wodehouse explores themes of [[class-and-society|social]] [[class-and-society|class]], [[class-and-society|respectability]], and the clash between artistic sensibilities and conventional [[class-and-society|society]] — themes made all the sharper by the Prohibition-era setting.

## George Finch: The Artist Protagonist

[[George-Finch|George Finch]] proudly identifies as an artist. His self-conception as a painter is one of his strongest convictions:

> "No. I am an artist," said George proudly. [015-065]

This identity, however, places him at odds [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the [[class-and-society|social]] world he must navigate. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], the mother of his [[love-and-romance|love]] interest [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]], judges people primarily by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] and [[class-and-society|social]] standing — criteria by which artists fare poorly:

> And he was an artist! Mrs. Waddington shuddered. Of all the myriad individuals that went to make up the kaleidoscopic life of New York, she disliked artists most. They never had any money. [015-090]

The shudder is pure Wodehouse — a single physical gesture that encapsulates an entire worldview.

## Society's View of Artists

The novel portrays early twentieth-century upper-[[class-and-society|class]] [[new-york|New York]] [[class-and-society|society]] as deeply suspicious of the artistic temperament. Artists are seen as financially unstable and morally questionable:

> "A woman of her type looks on artists as blots on the social scheme. I told you she judged her fellow-creatures solely by the extent of their bank balances." [018-012]

Even [[George-Finch|George]]'s friend [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]], the [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert, struggles to defend [[George-Finch|George]]'s profession against [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s prejudice:

> "I merely wished to point out," said Hamilton Beamish stiffly, "that the fact that he is an artist does not necessarily damn a man." [025-078]

The stiffness of [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s delivery is revealing — even he, the apostle of rational [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]], cannot make a purely rational case for the value of art. He can only offer the negative defense: being an artist does not *necessarily* damn a man. The qualification speaks volumes about the [[class-and-society|social]] terrain.

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s private opinion is less generous. He considers [[George-Finch|George]] "a rotten artist" [025-080] and "a very incompetent artist" [025-112], judgments that add a layer of dramatic irony: the reader knows that [[George-Finch|George]]'s artistic merit is irrelevant to the question of whether he deserves happiness.

## Bohemian Setting

The novel's physical setting reinforces the artistic theme. [[George-Finch|George]] lives at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]] in the heart of [[new-york|New York]]'s artistic quarter:

> The Sheridan stands in the heart of New York's Bohemian and artistic quarter. If you threw a brick from any of its windows, you would be certain to brain some rising interior decorator, some Vorticist sculptor, or a writer of revolutionary vers libre. [002-002]

The "brick" hypothetical is a [[class-and-society|class]]ic Wodehouse exaggeration — a comic image that simultaneously defines the neighborhood, establishes the narrative tone, and introduces the reader to the world [[George-Finch|George]] inhabits.

## Art as Identity vs. Art as Profession

A key tension in the novel is the gap between [[George-Finch|George]]'s self-identification as an artist and the [[class-and-society|social]] function of that identity. For [[George-Finch|George]], being an artist is existential — it is *who he is*. For [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], it is merely an economic category — a profession that, unlike banking or real estate, produces no reliable [[money-and-wealth|income]]. This mismatch between internal identity and external perception drives much of the novel's comic conflict.

| Perspective | Art Means | Representative Character |
|-------------|----------|--------------------------|
| Internal identity | Self-expression, purpose, pride | [[George-Finch|George Finch]] |
| Social judgment | Poverty, unreliability, bohemianism | [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] |
| Rational assessment | A data point, neither damning nor redeeming | [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] |
| Romantic acceptance | Irrelevant — [[love-and-romance|love]] transcends profession | [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] |
