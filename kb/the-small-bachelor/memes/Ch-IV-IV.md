# IV

[021-001] It might have been expected that the passage of time, giving opportunity for quiet reflection on the subject of the illogical nature of the infatuation in which he had allowed himself to become involved, would have brought remorse to so clear and ruthless a thinker as [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]]. It was not so, but far otherwise. As Hamilton Beamish sat in the antechamber of [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]]’s office next day, he gloried in his folly: and when his better self endeavoured to point out to him that what had happened was that he had allowed himself to be ensnared by a girl’s face—that is to say, by a purely fortuitous arrangement of certain albuminoids and fatty molecules, all Hamilton Beamish did was to tell his better self to put its head in a bag. He was in [[love-and-romance|love]], and he liked it. He was in love, and proud of it. His only really coherent thought as he waited in the anteroom was a resolve to withdraw the booklet on “[[the-beamish-booklets|The Marriage Sane]]” from circulation and try his hand at writing a poem or two.

[021-002] “Madame Eulalie will see you now, sir,” announced the maid, breaking in upon his reverie.

[021-003] Hamilton Beamish entered the inner room. And, having entered it, stopped dead.

[021-004] “You!” he exclaimed.

[021-005] The girl gave that fleeting pat at her hair which is always Woman’s reaction to the unexpected situation. And Hamilton Beamish looking at that hair emotionally, perceived that he had been right in his yesterday’s surmise. It was, as he had suspected, a gleamy mass, sparkling with life and possessing that incomparable softness, freshness and luxuriance.

[021-006] “Why, how do you do?” said the girl.

[021-007] “I’m fine,” said Hamilton Beamish.

[021-008] “We seem fated to meet.”

[021-009] “And I’m not quarrelling with [[fate-and-coincidence|fate]].”

[021-010] “No?”

[021-011] “No,” said Hamilton Beamish. “Fancy it being you!”

[021-012] “Fancy who being me?”

[021-013] “Fancy you being you.” It occurred to him that he was not making himself quite clear. “I mean, I was sent here with a message for Madame Eulalie, and she turns out to be you.”

[021-014] “A message? Who from?”

[021-015] “From whom?” corrected Hamilton Beamish. Even in the grip of love, a specialist on Pure English remains a specialist on Pure English.

[021-016] “That’s what I said—Who from?”

[021-017] Hamilton Beamish smiled an indulgent smile. These little mistakes could be corrected later—possibly on the honeymoon.

[021-018] “From [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]]. She asked me to. …”

[021-019] “Oh, then you don’t want me to read your hand?”

[021-020] “There is nothing I want more in this world,” said Hamilton Beamish warmly, “than to have you read my hand.”

[021-021] “I don’t have to read it to tell your character, of course,” said the girl. “I can see that at a glance.”

[021-022] “You can?”

[021-023] “Oh, certainly. You have a strong, dominating nature and a keen incisive mind. You have great breadth of vision, iron determination, and marvellous insight. Yet with it all you are at heart gentle, kind and lovable; deeply altruistic and generous to a fault. You have it in you to be a leader of men. You remind me of Julius Caesar, Shakespeare and Napoleon Bonaparte.”

[021-024] “Tell me more,” said Hamilton Beamish.

[021-025] “If you ever fell in love. …”

[021-026] “If I ever fell in love. …”

[021-027] “If you ever fell in love,” said the girl, raising her eyes to his and drawing a step closer, “you would. …”

[021-028] “Mr. Delancy Cabot,” announced the maid.

[021-029] “Oh, darn it!” said Madame Eulalie. “I forgot I had an appointment. Send him in.”

[021-030] “May I wait?” breathed Hamilton Beamish devoutly.

[021-031] “Please do. I shan’t be long.” She turned to the door. “Come in, Mr. Cabot.”

[021-032] Hamilton Beamish wheeled around. A long, stringy person was walking daintily into the room. He was richly, even superbly, dressed in the conventional costume of the popular clubman and pet of Society. He wore lavender gloves and a carnation in his buttonhole, and a vast expanse of snowy collar encircled a neck which suggested that he might be a throwback to some giraffe ancestor. A pleasing feature of this neck was an Adam’s apple that could have belonged to only one man of Hamilton Beamish’s acquaintance.

[021-033] “[[officer-garroway|Garroway]]!” cried Hamilton Beamish. “What are you doing here? And what the devil does this [[deception-and-mistaken-identity|masquerade]] mean?”

[021-034] The policeman seemed taken aback. His face became as red as his wrists. But for the collar, which held him in a grip of iron, his jaw would no doubt have fallen.

[021-035] “I didn’t expect to find you here, Mr. Beamish,” he said apologetically.

[021-036] “I didn’t expect to find you here, calling yourself De Courcy Bellville.”

[021-037] “Delancy Cabot, sir.”

[021-038] “Delancy Cabot, then.”

[021-039] “I like the name,” urged the policeman. “I saw it in a book.”

[021-040] The girl was breathing hard.

[021-041] “Is this man a policeman?” she cried.

[021-042] “Yes, he is,” said Hamilton Beamish. “His name is Garroway, and I am teaching him to write poetry. And what I want to know,” he thundered, turning on the unhappy man, whose Adam’s apple was now leaping like a young lamb in the springtime, “is what are you doing here, interrupting a—interrupting a—in short interrupting, when you ought either to be about your constabulary duties or else sitting quietly at home studying John Drinkwater. That,” said Hamilton Beamish, “is what I want to know.”

[021-043] Officer Garroway coughed.

[021-044] “The fact is, Mr. Beamish, I did not know that Madame Eulalie was a friend of yours.”

[021-045] “Never mind whose friend she is.”

[021-046] “But it makes all the difference, Mr. Beamish. I can now go back to headquarters and report that Madame Eulalie is above suspicion. You see, sir, I was sent here by my superior officers to effect a cop.”

[021-047] “What do you mean, effect a cop?”

[021-048] “To make an [[police-and-law|arrest]], Mr. Beamish.”

[021-049] “Then do not say ‘effect a cop.’ Purge yourself of these vulgarisms, Garroway.”

[021-050] “Yes, sir. I will indeed, sir.”

[021-051] “Aim at the English Pure.”

[021-052] “Yes, sir. Most certainly, Mr. Beamish.”

[021-053] “And what on earth do you mean by saying that you were sent here to arrest this lady?”

[021-054] “It has been called to the attention of my superior officers, Mr. Beamish, that Madame Eulalie is in the habit of telling fortunes for a monetary consideration. Against the [[crime-and-law|law]], sir.”

[021-055] Hamilton Beamish snorted.

[021-056] “Ridiculous! If that’s the law, alter it!”

[021-057] “I will do my best, sir.”

[021-058] “I have had the privilege of watching Madame Eulalie engaged upon her [[art-and-artists|art]], and she reveals nothing but the most limpid truth. So go back to your superior officers and tell them to jump off the Brooklyn Bridge.”

[021-059] “Yes, sir. I will, sir.”

[021-060] “And now leave us. We would be alone.”

[021-061] “Yes, Mr. Beamish,” said Officer Garroway humbly. “At once, Mr. Beamish.”

[021-062] For some moments after the door had closed, the girl stood staring at Hamilton Beamish with wondering eyes.

[021-063] “Was that man really a policeman?”

[021-064] “He was.”

[021-065] “And you handled him like that, and he said, ‘Yes, sir!’ and ‘No, sir!’ and crawled out on all fours.” She drew a deep breath. “It seems to me that you are just the sort of friend a lonely girl needs in this great city.”

[021-066] “I am only too delighted that I was able to be of service.”

[021-067] “Service is right! Mr. Beamish. …”

[021-068] “My first name is Hamilton.”

[021-069] She looked at him, amazed.

[021-070] “You are not *the* Hamilton Beamish? Not the man who wrote the booklets?”

[021-071] “I have written a few booklets.”

[021-072] “Why, you’re my favourite author! If it hadn’t been for you I would still be mouldering in a little one-horse town where there wasn’t even a good soda-fountain. But I got hold of a couple of your Are You in a Groove? things, and I packed up my grip and came right along to [[new-york|New York]] to lead a larger life. If I’d known yesterday that you were Hamilton Beamish, I’d have kissed you on the doorstep!”

[021-073] It was Hamilton Beamish’s intention to point out that a curtained room with a closed door was an even more suitable place for such a demonstration, but, even as he tried to speak, there gripped him for the first time in his life a strange, almost [[George-Finch|George Finch]]-like shyness. One deprecates the modern practice of exposing the great, but candour compels one to speak out and say that at this juncture Hamilton Beamish emitted a simpering giggle and began to twiddle his fingers.

[021-074] The strange weakness passed, and he was himself again. He adjusted his glasses firmly.

[021-075] “Would you,” he asked, “could you possibly. … Do you think you could manage to come and lunch somewhere tomorrow?”

[021-076] The girl uttered an exclamation of annoyance.

[021-077] “Isn’t that too bad!” she said. “I can’t.”

[021-078] “The day after?”

[021-079] “I’m sorry. I’m afraid I shall be off the map for three weeks. I’ve got to jump on a train tomorrow and go visit the old folks back in [[east-gilead|East Gilead]]. It’s Pop’s birthday on Saturday, and I never miss it.”

[021-080] “East Gilead?”

[021-081] “Idaho. You wouldn’t have heard of the place, but it’s there.”

[021-082] “But I have heard of it. A great friend of mine comes from East Gilead.”

[021-083] “You don’t say! Who?”

[021-084] “A man named George Finch.”

[021-085] She laughed amusedly.

[021-086] “You don’t actually mean to tell me you know George Finch?”

[021-087] “He is my most intimate friend.”

[021-088] “Then I trust for your sake,” said the girl, “that he is not such a yap as he used to be.”

[021-089] Hamilton Beamish reflected. Was George Finch a yap? How precisely did one estimate the yaphood of one’s friends?

[021-090] “By the word ‘yap’ you mean. …”

[021-091] “I mean a yap. The sort of fellow who couldn’t say Bo to a goose.”

[021-092] Hamilton Beamish had never seen George Finch in conversation with a goose, but he thought he was a good enough judge of character to be able to credit him with the ability to perform the very trivial deed of daring indicated.

[021-093] “I fancy New York has changed George,” he replied, after reflection. “In fact, now that I remember, it was on more or less that very subject that I called to see you in a professional capacity. The fact is, George Finch has fallen violently in love with Molly Waddington, the stepdaughter of your client, Mrs. Waddington.”

[021-094] “You don’t say! And I suppose he’s too shy to come within a mile of her.”

[021-095] “On the contrary. The night before last he seems to have forced his way into the house—you might say, practically forced his way—and now Mrs. Waddington has forbidden him to see Molly again, fearing that he will spoil her plan of marrying the poor child to a certain [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]].”

[021-096] The girl stared.

[021-097] “You’re right! George must have altered.”

[021-098] “And we were wondering—Molly and I—if we could possibly induce you to stoop to a—shall I say a benevolent little ruse. Mrs. Waddington is coming to see you today at five, and it was Molly’s suggestion that I should sound you as to whether you would consent to take a look in the crystal and tell Mrs. Waddington that you see danger threatening Molly from a dark man with an eyeglass.”

[021-099] “Of course.”

[021-100] “You will?”

[021-101] “It isn’t much to do in return for all you have done for me.”

[021-102] “Thank you, thank you,” said Hamilton Beamish. “I knew, the moment I set eyes on you, that you were a woman in a million. I wonder—could you possibly come to lunch one day after you return?”

[021-103] “I’d love it.”

[021-104] “I’ll leave you my telephone number.”

[021-105] “Thanks. Give George my regards. I’d like to see him when I get back.”

[021-106] “You shall. Goodbye.”

[021-107] “Goodbye, Mr. Beamish.”

[021-108] “Hamilton.”

[021-109] Her face wore a doubtful look.

[021-110] “I don’t much like that name Hamilton. It’s kind of stiff.”

[021-111] Hamilton Beamish had a brief struggle with himself.

[021-112] “My name is also James. At one time in my life many people used to call me Jimmy.” He shuddered a little, but repeated the word bravely: “Jimmy.”

[021-113] “Put me on the list,” said the girl. “I like that much better. Goodbye, Jimmy.”

[021-114] “Goodbye,” said Hamilton Beamish.

[021-115] So ended the first spasm of a great man’s love-story. A few moments later, Hamilton Beamish was walking in a sort of dance-measure down the street. Near [[washington-square|Washington Square]] he gave a small boy a dollar and asked him if he was going to be President some day.
