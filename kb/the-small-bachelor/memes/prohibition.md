# Prohibition

## Overview

*The Small Bachelor* is set during Prohibition, the period from 1920 to 1933 when the Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution banned the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors. Wodehouse, writing in 1927, treats Prohibition not as a moral crusade but as a source of comic absurdity — a legal framework that turns otherwise respectable citizens into technical criminals and provides endless opportunities for the [[humor-and-comedy|comic]] complications that drive his plot.

## In the Novel

### The Purple Chicken and "It"

The novel's opening pages establish Prohibition's presence with characteristic Wodehousian indirection. The [[Purple-Chicken|Purple Chicken]] restaurant is described as "one of those numerous oases in this great city where, in spite of the law of Prohibition, you can still, so the cognoscenti whisper, 'always get it if they know you'" (002-002). The euphemism "it" — never defined, universally understood — captures the era's atmosphere of knowing winks and technical violations.

### Fortune-Telling and the Law

Prohibition-era legal culture extended beyond alcohol to other forms of moral regulation. [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]] informs [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] that [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]]'s fortune-telling practice violates the law: "It has been drawn to our attention that the Purple Chicken, defying the Eighteenth Amendment, still purveys alcoholic liquors" (003-013). The solemnity with which these laws are invoked, contrasted with their evident absurdity, is Wodehouse's primary satirical method.

### Bootleggers as Domestic Help

The novel treats the procurement of illegal alcohol as a mundane domestic concern. When a character inquires about a bootlegger, it is presented not as scandalous but as a practical household matter — another example of the gap between the law's aspirations and the city's actual life.

## Comic Treatment

Wodehouse's treatment of Prohibition is characteristic of his light satirical mode. He does not mount a political argument against the Eighteenth Amendment; instead, he simply depicts its consequences — the speakeasies, the euphemisms, the police raids that everyone knows are coming — and allows the absurdity to speak for itself. In the Wodehousian universe, laws that criminalize ordinary human pleasures are not evils to be fought but inconveniences to be navigated with [[humor-and-comedy|wit]] and style.
