# Lancelot Biffen

## Identity and Role

Lancelot Biffen is the editor-in-chief of *Town Gossip*, a scandal sheet that trades in "items of interest concerning those prominent in [[new-york|New York]] Society" (044-034). He lives in the ap[[art-and-artists|art]]ment directly below [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s rooftop bungalow at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartments]], a proximity that makes him a persistent nuisance.

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]]'s assessment of Biffen is characteristically direct:

> "A ghastly, sneaking, snooping fellow named Lancelot Biffen. I've known him to climb up before." (045-062)

The three adjectives — ghastly, sneaking, snooping — are presented [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the finality of a [[crime-and-law|legal]] judgment. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]], who reduces everything to maxims, has reduced Biffen to a trinity of disapprobation.

## The Gossip Trade

Biffen represents the underside of the novel's [[class-and-society|social]] world. While characters like [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] obsess over [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]]s and [[class-and-society|social]] standing, Biffen profits from the exposure of what lies beneath those [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]]s:

> "I understand, Mr. Biffen, that *Town Gossip* is glad to receive and pay a substantial remuneration for items of interest concerning those prominent in New York Society." (044-034)

The phrase "substantial remuneration" is the key — Biffen's interest in scandal is not moral but commercial. He is a businessman of shame, and Wodehouse treats him [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the mild contempt reserved for those who profit from others' mis[[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]s.

## Physical and Social Position

Biffen's residence — "the ap[[art-and-artists|art]]ment on the floor below" — is telling. He is positioned beneath [[George-Finch|George]] both literally and metaphorically: below him in the building's architecture and below him in the novel's moral hierarchy. His "climbing up" (045-062) is a spatial enactment of [[class-and-society|social]] climbing — the gossip columnist who ascends to the rooftop, the realm of [[love-and-romance|romance]] and [[art-and-artists|art]], to gather material for his sordid trade.

## Narrative Function

Biffen's role is that of the minor antagonist — never genuinely threatening but always inconvenient. His presence adds a note of worldly cynicism to the novel's otherwise benevolent universe. In a story where [[deception-and-mistaken-identity|deception]]s are ultimately harmless and [[love-and-romance|love]] conquers all, Biffen represents the forces that would turn private life into public spectacle.
