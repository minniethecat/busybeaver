# Hamilton Beamish

## Overview

Hamilton Beamish is [[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s friend and the novel's most developed comic character. A self-proclaimed [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert who has written [[the-beamish-booklets|booklets]] on scientific living, Beamish provides much of the novel's [[humor-and-comedy|humor]] through the collision between his rational principles and the irrational power of [[love-and-romance|love]]. His intellectual opposition to [[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]] — and his subsequent capitulation to it — is one of Wodehouse's finest comic creations.

## Role in the Novel

Beamish serves as [[George-Finch|George]]'s ally, advisor, and sometime critic. When [[George-Finch|George]] falls in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] at first sight, Beamish diagnoses the condition from his own book:

> "In that booklet I argue very strongly against love at first sight." (002-055)

Beamish tries to help [[George-Finch|George]] navigate the [[class-and-society|social]] complexities of courting [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] while dealing [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h her mother's opposition. His rational advice is often sound but frequently undermined by the chaotic realities of [[love-and-romance|romance]].

## Character and Personality

Beamish prides himself on his rationality and [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]]. Even minor frustrations register as professional offenses:

> Hamilton Beamish clicked his tongue in annoyance. It was waste motion, but even efficiency experts have their feelings. (002-015)

His commitment to his principles is genuine but fragile. When he himself falls in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h an unknown woman he meets on a bus, all his theories collapse:

> "I think I am in love with your friend, George Finch." (020-006)

The irony of the [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert — who wrote a booklet against [[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]] — falling in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a stranger on a bus is Wodehouse at his comic best:

> Which simply goes to prove that even efficiency experts, when they fall in love at first sight, can babble like any man of inferior intellect in the same circumstances. (019-048)
