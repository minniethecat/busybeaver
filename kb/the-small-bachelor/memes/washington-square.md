# Washington Square

## Overview

Washington Square is the geographic centre of *The Small Bachelor*. The novel opens by establishing the setting: "The roof of the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartment House]], near Washington Square, [[new-york|New York]]" (002-001). From this rooftop vantage point, the entire plot unfolds — and Washington Square, [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h its associations of [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]]ism, [[art-and-artists|artist]]ry, and [[class-and-society|social]] unconventionality, provides the essential counterpoint to the Waddington world of [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] and Seventy-Ninth Street.

## In the Novel

### The Sheridan and Its Roof

The [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan Apartment House]], standing near Washington Square (015-071), is the novel's primary stage. Its flat roof serves as a liminal space where characters from different [[class-and-society|social]] worlds meet. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] conducts his [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] experiments there, [[mullett|Mullett]] sweeps and philosophises, [[officer-garroway|Officer Garroway]] intrudes, and [[George-Finch|George Finch]] dreams of [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]. The roof is Washington Square in microcosm: a place where the usual rules are suspended.

### Bohemian Headquarters

Washington Square functions in the novel as shorthand for everything [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] distrusts. She refuses to sympathise [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s romantic predicament because [[George-Finch|George]] has "deliberately and of his own free-will goes and lives near Washington Square" (025-114). In her mind, the neighbourhood is synonymous [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h loose morals — a place of "Washington Square vamps" (030-018) and shadowy young women behind yellow blinds (040-065).

The irony, as always in Wodehouse, is that the Square's [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] associations are vastly overstated. [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is no seducer — he is an earnest young [[art-and-artists|artist]] in [[love-and-romance|love]]. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s horror at Washington Square reveals more about her own prejudices than about the neighbourhood itself.

### A Stage for Romance

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]], the novel's least sentimental character, finds himself walking "in a sort of dance-measure down the street" near Washington Square after his first encounter [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h his future wife — and gives a small boy a dollar, asking if he is going to be President some day (021-115). The Square's romantic atmosphere is potent enough to melt even [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]]-hardened he[[art-and-artists|art]]. Later, [[George-Finch|George]] watches his be[[love-and-romance|love]]d's car turn into Washington Square and stands looking after it "long after there was nothing to look at but empty street" (045-144).

### Lancelot Biffen's Perch

The editor [[lancelot-biffen|Lancelot Biffen]] maintains his private ad[[fashion-and-appearance|dress]] at the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan]], near Washington Square (038-058). From this vantage he edits *Town Gossip* (044-032) and p[[art-and-artists|art]]icipates — indirectly — in the novel's climactic revelations. The Square thus houses not only [[art-and-artists|artists]] and [[love-and-romance|love]]rs but also the machinery of publicity that will ultimately resolve the plot.

### Mrs. Waddington's Surveillance

In the novel's final movement, [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] takes up position outside the [[Sheridan-Apartments|Sheridan]], convinced that "in an ap[[art-and-artists|art]]ment near Washington Square where there was a young woman present events were not likely to remain static" (042-001). Her vigil at Washington Square represents the collision of two worlds: [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] propriety camped on [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] turf, [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h predictably comic results.

## Significance

Washington Square in *The Small Bachelor* is more than a setting — it is a [[class-and-society|social]] declaration. To live near Washington Square is to declare oneself outside the conventions of [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] [[class-and-society|society]]. The novel's comic engine runs on the tension between these two [[new-york|New York]]s, and Washington Square — home to [[art-and-artists|artists]], [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] experts, ex-convict valets, and gossip editors — represents a world where authentic feeling can flourish, however absurdly.
