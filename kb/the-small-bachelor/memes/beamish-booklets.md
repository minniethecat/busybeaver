# Beamish Booklets

## Overview

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] is the author of self-published [[the-beamish-booklets|booklets]] on [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] and scientific living. These pamphlets — [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] confident pronouncements on topics like [[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]] and pure English — serve as a running comic device in *The Small Bachelor*. Each booklet represents [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s attempt to impose rational order on a world that stubbornly resists it, and the gap between booklet-theory and lived-experience generates some of the novel's [[money-and-wealth|riches]]t [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]].

## The Booklet Against Love at First Sight

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s most consequential publication argues against [[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]] [002-055]. The irony, of course, is that [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] himself falls victim to precisely this phenomenon after a [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] [[beamish-bus-encounter|encounter on a city bus]]:

> Which simply goes to prove that even efficiency experts, when they fall in love at first sight, can babble like any man of inferior intellect in the same circumstances. [019-048]

The booklet's confident reasoning — whatever it might have been — evaporates the moment [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] meets an unknown woman and falls in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout learning her name. The pamphlet, once a prop of intellectual self-assurance, becomes an indictment of its author.

## The Booklet on Pure English

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s linguistic purism finds expression in a second booklet, which he invokes when correcting [[mullett|Mullett]]'s vocabulary:

> "Avoid jargon. Strive for the Word Beautiful. Read my booklet on Pure English." [002-037]

The instruction to "Strive for the Word Beautiful" is quintessential [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] — simultaneously admirable (who could object to beautiful language?) and absurd (delivered to a reformed ex-convict valet in a [[Greenwich-Village|Greenwich Village]] ap[[art-and-artists|art]]ment). [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] holds [[mullett|Mullett]]'s vocabulary in p[[art-and-artists|art]]icular contempt:

> "I dislike the expression. It is the sort of expression Mullett would use: and I know few things more calculated to make a thinking man shudder than Mullett's vocabulary." [005-079]

The "thinking man" who shudders at [[mullett|Mullett]]'s vocabulary is himself a comic creation — a man whose own [[the-beamish-booklets|booklets]], for all t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] pure English, cannot prevent him from falling in [[love-and-romance|love]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a stranger on a bus.

## The Booklet as Comic Prop

[[the-beamish-booklets|The Beamish Booklets]] belong to a venerable Wodehousian tradition: the published work that its author cannot live up to. Like the many [[art-and-artists|art]]icles, manuals, and treatises that litter the Wodehouse canon, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]]'s pamphlets exist to be disproven by the action of the novel. They represent the attempt to reduce complex human experience to simple principles — an attempt that the novel's celebration of [[love-and-romance|love]], [[art-and-artists|art]], and spontaneity gently but firmly rejects.
