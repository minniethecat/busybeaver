# Money and Wealth

## Overview

In *The Small Bachelor*, money is the primary measure by which characters judge one another. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], in p[[art-and-artists|art]]icular, evaluates potential sons-in-[[crime-and-law|law]] exclusively by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] financial standing. The novel satirizes a [[class-and-society|society]] in which [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] trumps all other qualities — including character, talent, and [[love-and-romance|love]].

## In the Novel

### The Poverty of Artists

[[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s identity as an [[art-and-artists|artist]] carries a financial stigma. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s immediate objection to [[George-Finch|George]] is economic rather than personal:

> "He is a penniless artist." (025-046)

In her world, the phrase "penniless [[art-and-artists|artist]]" is practically redundant — [[art-and-artists|art]] and poverty are inseparable in the [[class-and-society|social]] imagination. This prejudice extends beyond [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] to the broader [[class-and-society|society]] she represents.

### The Transformative Power of Wealth

When [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] reveals that [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is actually [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y, the entire [[class-and-society|social]] calculus changes. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s opposition collapses instantly, demonstrating that her principles were never about character or compatibility — only about money:

> "He is an exceedingly wealthy man. His uncle Thomas, whose entire fortune he inherited two years ago..." (025-096)

The speed and completeness of [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s conversion is one of the novel's sharpest satirical moments.

### Money and Social Standing

Wealth in the novel is not merely a means to comfort but the key to [[class-and-society|social]] acceptance. Characters are [[class-and-society|class]]ified by t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] financial [[class-and-society|status]]: the [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y are "the most [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y and exclusive people" (008-031), while the poor are excluded from polite consideration. Wodehouse exposes the absurdity of this system through [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]] — the same characters who condemn [[George-Finch|George]] as a [[fate-and-coincidence|fortune]]-hunter embrace him when they learn of his [[fortune-and-inheritance|inheritance]].
