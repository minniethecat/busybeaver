# Family and Duty

## Overview

Family relationships in *The Small Bachelor* are primarily defined by conflict between parental authority and romantic independence. [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] embodies the intrusive parent who views her daughter's [[marriage-and-weddings|marriage]] as a matter of family strategy rather than personal happiness. The novel satirizes the notion that family duty should override individual desire.

## In the Novel

### Mrs. Waddington's Maternal Authority

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] exercises near-absolute authority over her daughter [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s romantic life. She sees it as her maternal duty to prevent an unsuitable [[marriage-and-weddings|marriage]], regardless of [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s feelings. Her opposition to [[George-Finch|George Finch]] is framed not as cruelty but as responsibility:

> "Do you imagine I intend to allow you to ruin your life by marrying a despicable fortune-hunter?" (025-044)

Wodehouse exposes the self-serving nature of such "duty" — [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s concern for her daughter's welfare is indistinguishable from her own [[class-and-society|social]] ambition.

### The Absent Father

[[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]], [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]'s father, is a largely passive figure who defers to his wife's judgments. His weakness as a father figure leaves [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout a parental ally against her mother's schemes. His eventual emergence as a supportive presence comes only after the financial revelations that satisfy his wife.

### Family vs. Love

The central conflict between family obligation and romantic [[love-and-romance|love]] is resolved not through reconciliation but through the removal of the financial obstacle. Once [[George-Finch|George]] is revealed to be [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y, [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s "family duty" evaporates — revealing it was never about duty at all.
