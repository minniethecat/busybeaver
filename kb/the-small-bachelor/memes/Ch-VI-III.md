# III

[029-001] Upstairs, while the conversation just recorded was in progress, Frederick [[mullett|Mullett]] was entertaining his fiancée, Fanny Welch, to a light collation in the kitchen of [[George-Finch|George Finch]]’s apartment. It is difficult for a man to look devotional while his mouth is full of cold beef and chutney—but not impossible, for Mullett was doing it now. He gazed at Fanny very much as George Finch had gazed at [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]], [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] at [[madame-eulalie|Madame Eulalie]], and as a million other young men in [[new-york|New York]] and its outskirts were or would shortly be gazing at a million other young women. Love had come rather late to Frederick Mullett, for his had been a busy life, but it had come to stay.

[029-002] Externally, Fanny Welch appeared not unworthy of his devotion. She was a pretty little thing with snapping black eyes and a small face. The thing you noticed about her first was the slim shapeliness of her hands with their long, sensitive fingers. One of the great advantages of being a pickpocket is that you do have nice hands.

[029-003] “I like this place,” said Fanny, looking about her.

[029-004] “Do you, honey?” said Mullett tenderly. “I was hoping you would. Because I’ve got a secret for you.”

[029-005] “What’s that?”

[029-006] “This is where you and me are going to spend our honeymoon!”

[029-007] “What, in this kitchen?”

[029-008] “Of course not. We’ll have the run of the whole apartment, with the roof thrown in.”

[029-009] “What’ll Mr. Finch have to say to that?”

[029-010] “He won’t know, pettie. You see, Mr. Finch has just gone and got engaged to be married himself, and he’ll be off on his honeymoon-trip, so the whole place’ll be ours for ever so long. What do you think of that?”

[029-011] “Sounds good to me.”

[029-012] “I’ll take and show you the place in a minute or two. It’s the best studio-apartment for miles around. There’s a nice large sitting-room that looks on to the roof, with French windows so that you can stroll out and take the air when you like. And there’s a sleeping-porch on the roof, in case the weather’s warm. And a bath H. and C., with shower. It’s the snuggest place you’ll ever want to find, and you and I can stay perched up here like two little birds in a nest. And, when we’ve finished honeymooning, we’ll go down to Long Island and buy a little duck-farm and live happy ever after.”

[029-013] Fanny looked doubtful.

[029-014] “Can you see me on a duck-farm, Freddy?”

[029-015] “Can I?” Mullett’s eyes beamed adoration. “You bet I can see you there—standing in a gingham apron on the old brick path between the hollyhocks, watching little Frederick romping under the apple-tree.”

[029-016] “Little *who*?”

[029-017] “Little Frederick.”

[029-018] “Oh? And did you notice little Fanny clinging to my skirts?”

[029-019] “So she is. And William John in his cradle on the porch.”

[029-020] “I think we’d better stop looking for awhile,” said Fanny. “Our [[family-and-duty|family]]’s growing too fast.”

[029-021] Mullett sighed ecstatically.

[029-022] “Doesn’t it sound quiet and peaceful after the stormy lives we’ve led. The quacking of the ducks. … The droning of the bees. … Put back that spoon, dearie. You know it doesn’t belong to you.”

[029-023] Fanny removed the spoon from the secret places of her [[fashion-and-appearance|dress]] and eyed it with a certain surprise.

[029-024] “Now, how did that get there?” she said.

[029-025] “You snitched it up, sweetness,” said Mullett gently. “Your little fingers just hovered for a moment like little bees over a flower, and the next minute the thing was gone. It was beautiful to watch, dearie, but put it back. You’ve done with all that sort of thing now, you know.”

[029-026] “I guess I have,” said Fanny wistfully.

[029-027] “You don’t guess you have, precious,” corrected her husband-to-be. “You know you have. Same as I’ve done.”

[029-028] “Are you really on the level now, Freddy?”

[029-029] “I’m as honest as the day is long.”

[029-030] “Work at nights, eh? Mullett, the human moth. Goes through his master’s clothes like a jealous wife.”

[029-031] Mullett laughed indulgently.

[029-032] “The same little Fanny! How you do [[love-and-romance|love]] to tease. Yes, precious, I’m through with the game for good. I wouldn’t steal a bone collar-stud now, not if my mother came and begged me on her bended knee. All I want is my little wife and my little home in the country.”

[029-033] Fanny frowned pensively.

[029-034] “You don’t think it’ll be kind of quiet down on that duck-farm? Kind of slow?”

[029-035] “Slow?” said Mullett, shocked.

[029-036] “Well, maybe not. But we’re retiring from business awful young, Freddy.”

[029-037] A look of concern came into Mullett’s face.

[029-038] “You don’t mean you still have a hankering for the old game?”

[029-039] “Well, what if I do?” said Fanny defiantly. “You do, too, if you’d only come clean and admit it.”

[029-040] The look of concern changed to one of dignity.

[029-041] “Nothing of the kind,” said Mullett. “I give you my word, Fanny, that there isn’t the job on earth that could tempt me now. And I do wish you would bring yourself to feel the same, honey.”

[029-042] “Oh, I’m not saying I would bother with anything that wasn’t really big. But, honest to goodness, Freddy, it would be a [[crime-and-law|crime]] to sidestep anything worthwhile, if it came along. It isn’t as if we had all the [[money-and-wealth|money]] in the world. I’ve picked up some nice little things at the stores and I suppose you’ve kept some of the stuff you got away with, but outside of that we’ve nothing but the bit of cash we’ve managed to save. We’ve got to be practical.”

[029-043] “But, sweetie, think of the awful chances you’d be taking of getting pinched.”

[029-044] “I’m not afraid. If they ever do nab me, I’ve got a spiel about my poor old mother. …”

[029-045] “You haven’t got a mother.”

[029-046] “Who said I had? … a spiel about my poor old mother that would draw tears from the Woolworth Building. Listen! ‘Don’t turn me over to the [[police-and-law|police]], mister, I only did it for ma’s sake. If you was out of work for weeks and starvin’ and you had to sit and watch your old ma bendin’ over the washtubs. …’ ”

[029-047] “Don’t, Fanny, please! I can’t bear it even though I know it’s just a game. I. … Hello! Somebody at the front door. Probably only a model wanting to know if Mr. Finch has a job for her. You wait here, honey. I’ll get rid of her and be back in half a minute.”
