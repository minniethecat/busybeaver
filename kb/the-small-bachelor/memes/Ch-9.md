# IX

[034-001] It chanced that as he spoke these light and casual words [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]], glancing down, noted that his shoelace had come untied. Stooping to attend to this he missed seeing [[George-Finch|George]]’s face. Nor—for he was a man who concentrated even on the lightest task the full attention of a great mind—did he hear the other’s sudden, whistling gasp of astonishment and horror. A moment later, however, he observed out of the corner of his eye something moving: and, looking, perceived that George’s legs were wobbling strangely.

[034-002] Hamilton Beamish straightened himself. He was now in a position to see George steadily and see him whole: and the spectacle convinced him at once that something in the message he had just delivered must have got right in among his friend’s ganglions. George Finch’s agreeable features seemed to be picked out in a delicate Nile-green. His eyes were staring. His lower jaw had fallen. Nobody who had ever seen a motion-picture could have had the least doubt as to what he was registering. It was dismay.

[034-003] “My dear George!” said Hamilton Beamish, concerned.

[034-004] “Wok. … Wuk. … Wok. …” George swallowed desperately. “Wok name did you say?”

[034-005] “[[may-stubbs|May Stubbs]].” Hamilton Beamish’s expression grew graver, and he looked at his friend with sudden suspicion. “Tell me all, George. It is idle to pretend that the name is strange to you. Obviously it has awakened deep and unpleasant memories. I trust, George, that this is not some poor girl with whose happiness you have toyed in the past, some broken blossom that you have culled and left to perish by the wayside?”

[034-006] George Finch was staring before him in a sort of stupor.

[034-007] “All is over!” he said dully.

[034-008] Hamilton Beamish softened.

[034-009] “Confide in me. We are friends. I will not judge you harshly, George.”

[034-010] A sudden fury melted the ice of George’s torpor.

[034-011] “It’s all that parson’s fault!” he cried vehemently. “I knew all along it meant bad [[fate-and-coincidence|luck]]. Gosh, what a Paradise this world would be if only clergymen could stand on chairs without spraining their ankles! I’m done for.”

[034-012] “Who is this May Stubbs?”

[034-013] “I knew her in [[east-gilead|East Gilead]],” said George hopelessly. “We were sort of engaged.”

[034-014] Hamilton Beamish pursed his lips.

[034-015] “Apart from the slovenly English of the phrase, which is perhaps excusable in the circumstances, I cannot see how you can have been ‘sort of’ engaged. A man is either engaged or he is not.”

[034-016] “Not where I come from. In East Gilead they have what they call understandings.”

[034-017] “And there was an [[engagement-and-courtship|understanding]] between you and this Miss Stubbs?”

[034-018] “Yes. Just one of those boy and girl affairs. You know. You see a girl home once or twice from church, and you take her to one or two picnics, and people kid you about her, and … well, there you are. I suppose she thought we were engaged. And now she’s read in the papers about my [[marriage-and-weddings|wedding]], and has come to make herself unpleasant.”

[034-019] “Did you and this girl quarrel before you separated?”

[034-020] “No. We sort of drifted apart. I took it for granted that the thing was over and done with. And when I saw [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]]. …”

[034-021] Hamilton Beamish laid a hand upon his arm.

[034-022] “George,” he said, “I want you to give me your full attention: for we have arrived now at the very core of the matter. Were there any letters?”

[034-023] “Dozens. And of course she has kept them. She used to sleep with them under her pillow.”

[034-024] “Bad!” said Hamilton Beamish, shaking his head. “Very bad!”

[034-025] “And I remember her saying once that she believed in breach of promise suits.”

[034-026] Hamilton Beamish frowned. He seemed to be deploring the get-rich-quick spirit of the modern girl, who is not content to sit down and wait for her alimony.

[034-027] “You think it certain that she is coming here with the intention of making trouble?”

[034-028] “What other reason could she have?”

[034-029] “Yes, I fancy you are right. I must think. I must think. Let me think.”

[034-030] And, so saying, Hamilton Beamish turned sharply to the left and began to walk slowly round in a circle, his hands behind his back and his face bent and thoughtful. His eyes searched the ground as if to wrest inspiration from it.

[034-031] Few sights in this world are more inspiring than that of a great thinker actually engaged in thought: and yet George Finch, watching his friend, chafed. He had a perhaps forgivable craving for quick results: and Hamilton Beamish, though impressive, did not seem to be getting anywhere.

[034-032] “Have you thought of anything?” he asked, as the other came round for the third time.

[034-033] Hamilton Beamish held up a hand in silent reproof, and resumed his pacing. Presently he stopped.

[034-034] “Yes?” said George.

[034-035] “With regard to this engagement. …”

[034-036] “It wasn’t an engagement. It was an understanding.”

[034-037] “With regard to this understanding or engagement, the weak spot in your line of defence is undoubtedly the fact that it was you who broke it off.”

[034-038] “But I didn’t break it off.”

[034-039] “I used the wrong expression. I should have said that it was you who took the initiative. You left East Gilead and came to [[new-york|New York]]. Therefore, technically, you deserted this girl.”

[034-040] “I wish you wouldn’t say things like that. Can’t you understand that it was just one of those boy and girl affairs which come to an end of themselves?”

[034-041] “I was looking at the thing from a lawyer’s viewpoint. And may I point out that the affair appears not to have come to an end. What I am trying to make clear is this: that, if you had wished it to come to an end, you should, before you left East Gilead, have arranged somehow that this Miss Stubbs broke off the engagement.”

[034-042] “Understanding.”

[034-043] “The engagement or understanding. That would have cleaned the slate. You should have done something that would have made her disgusted with you.”

[034-044] “How could I? I’m not the sort of fellow who can do things like that.”

[034-045] “Even now, it seems to me, if you could do something that would revolt this Miss Stubbs … make her recoil from you with loathing. …”

[034-046] “Well, what?”

[034-047] “I must think,” said Hamilton Beamish.

[034-048] He did four more laps.

[034-049] “Suppose you had committed some sort of [[crime-and-law|crime]]?” he said, returning to the fixed point. “Suppose she were to find out you were a thief? She wouldn’t want to marry you if you were on your way to Sing-Sing.”

[034-050] “No. And neither would Molly.”

[034-051] “True. I must think again.”

[034-052] It was some moments later that George, eyeing his friend with the growing dislike which those of superior brainpower engender in us when they fail to deliver the goods in our times of crisis, observed him give a sudden start.

[034-053] “I think I have it,” said Hamilton Beamish.

[034-054] “Well?”

[034-055] “This Miss Stubbs. Tell me, is she straitlaced? Prudish? Most of those village girls are.”

[034-056] George reflected.

[034-057] “I don’t remember ever having noticed. I never did anything to make her prudish about.”

[034-058] “I think we may assume that, having lived all her life in a spot like East Gilead, she is. The solution of this difficulty, then, is obviously to lead her to suppose that you have become a reprobate.”

[034-059] “A what?”

[034-060] “A Don Juan. A Lothario. A libertine. It should be perfectly easy. She has seen motion-pictures of life in New York, and will not be hard to convince that you have deteriorated since you came to live there. Our plan of action now becomes straightforward and simple. All we have to do is to get some girl to come along and say that you have no right to marry anybody but her.”

[034-061] “What!”

[034-062] “I can see the scene now. This Miss Stubbs is sitting beside you, a dowdy figure in her homemade village gown. You are talking of the old days. You are stroking her hand. Suddenly you look up and start. The door has opened and a girl, all in black with a white face, is entering. Her eyes are haggard, her hair disordered. In her arms she clasps a little bundle.”

[034-063] “No, no! Not that!”

[034-064] “Very well, we will dispense with the bundle. She stretches out her arms to you. She totters. You rush to support her. The scene is similar to one in Haddon Chambers’ ‘Passersby.’ ”

[034-065] “What happened in that?”

[034-066] “What could happen? The fiancée saw the ruined girl had the greater claim, so she joined their hands together and crept silently from the room.”

[034-067] George laughed mirthlessly.

[034-068] “There’s just one thing you’re overlooking. Where are we going to get the white-faced girl?”

[034-069] Hamilton Beamish stroked his chin.

[034-070] “There is that difficulty. I must think.”

[034-071] “And while you’re thinking,” said George coldly, “I’ll do the only practical thing there is to be done, and go down to the station and meet her, and have a talk with her and try to get her to be sensible.”

[034-072] “Perhaps that would be as well. But I still feel that my scheme would be the ideal one, if only we could find the girl. It is too bad that you have not a dark past.”

[034-073] “My dark past,” said George bitterly, “is all ahead of me.”

[034-074] He turned and hurried down the drive. Hamilton Beamish, still meditating, made his way towards the house.

[034-075] He had reached the lawn, when, as he stopped to light a cigarette to assist thought, he saw a sight that made him drop the match and draw back into the shelter of a tree.

[034-076] Hamilton Beamish stopped, looked and listened. A girl had emerged from a clump of rhododendrons, and was stealing softly round the lawn towards the [[food-and-dining|dining]]-room window.

[034-077] Girlhood is the season of dreams. To Fanny Welch, musing over the position of affairs after [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]] had given her her final instructions, there had come a quaint, fantastic thought, creeping into her mind like a bee into a flower—the thought that if she got to the house an hour earlier than the time he had mentioned, it might be possible for her to steal the necklace and keep it for herself.

[034-078] The flaw in the scheme, as originally outlined, had seemed to her all along to lie in the fact that Mr. Waddington was to preside over the enterprise and take the loot from her the moment she had got it. The revised plan appeared immeasurably more attractive, and she proceeded to put it into action.

[034-079] Luck seemed to be with her. Nobody was about, the window was ajar, and there on the table lay that which she had now come to look on in the light of a present for a good girl. She crept out of her hiding-place, stole round the edge of the lawn, entered the room, and had just grasped the case in her hand, when it was borne sharply in upon her that luck was not with her so much as she had supposed. A heavy hand was placed upon her shoulder: and, twisting round, she perceived a majestic-looking man with a square chin and horn-rimmed spectacles.

[034-080] “Well, young lady!” said this person.

[034-081] Fanny breathed hard. These little contretemps are the risk of the profession, but that makes them none the easier to bear philosophically.

[034-082] “Put down that jewel-case.”

[034-083] Fanny did so. There was a pause. Hamilton Beamish moved to the window, blocking it up.

[034-084] “Well?” said Fanny.

[034-085] Hamilton Beamish adjusted his spectacles.

[034-086] “Well, you’ve got me. What are you going to do?”

[034-087] “What do you expect me to do?”

[034-088] “Turn me over to the [[police-and-law|police]]?”

[034-089] The figure in the window nodded curtly. Fanny clasped her hands together. Her eyes filled with tears.

[034-090] “Don’t turn me over to the bulls, mister! I only did it for ma’s sake. …”

[034-091] “All wrong!”

[034-092] “If you was out of work and starvin’ and you had to sit and watch your poor old ma bendin’ over the washtub. …”

[034-093] “All wrong!” repeated Hamilton Beamish forcefully.

[034-094] “What do you mean, all wrong?”

[034-095] “Mere crude Broadway melodrama. That stuff might deceive some people, but not me.”

[034-096] Fanny shrugged her shoulders.

[034-097] “Well, I thought it was worth trying,” she said.

[034-098] Hamilton Beamish was regarding her keenly. That busy brain was never still, and now it had begun to work with even more than its normal intensity.

[034-099] “Are you an actress?”

[034-100] “Me? I should say not. My folks are awful particular.”

[034-101] “Well, you have considerable dramatic ability. There was a ring of sincerity in that drivel you just recited which would have convinced most men. I think I could use you in a little drama which I have been planning. I’ll make a bargain with you. I have no wish to send you to prison.”

[034-102] “Spoken like a man.”

[034-103] “I ought to, of course.”

[034-104] “Yes, but it’s a lot better fun doing things that you oughtn’t, isn’t it?”

[034-105] “Well, the point is, I have a friend who is in a difficulty, and it occurs to me that you can get him out.”

[034-106] “Always glad to oblige.”

[034-107] “My friend is going to be married today, and he has just heard that a previous fiancée of his, whom in the excitement inseparable from falling in [[love-and-romance|love]] with the girl who is to be his bride he had unfortunately overlooked, is on her way here.”

[034-108] “To make trouble?”

[034-109] “Precisely.”

[034-110] “Well, what can I do about it?”

[034-111] “Just this. For five minutes I want you to play the role of my friend’s discarded victim.”

[034-112] “I don’t get it.”

[034-113] “I will put it more plainly. In a short while this girl will arrive, probably in company with my friend, who has gone to meet her at the station. You will be waiting outside here. At an appropriate moment you will rush into the room, hold out your arms to my friend and cry ‘George! George! Why did you desert me? You don’t belong to that girl there. You belong to me—the woman you have wronged!’ ”

[034-114] “Not on your life!”

[034-115] “What!”

[034-116] Fanny drew herself up haughtily.

[034-117] “Not on your life!” she said. “Suppose my husband got to hear of it!”

[034-118] “Are you married?”

[034-119] “Married this morning at the Little Church Round the Corner.”

[034-120] “And you come here and try to steal things on your wedding-day!”

[034-121] “Why not? You know as well as I do what it costs nowadays to set up house.”

[034-122] “Surely it would be a severe shock to your husband to find that you had been sent to prison! I think you had better be reasonable.”

[034-123] Fanny scraped the floor with her shoe.

[034-124] “Would this thing you want me to do get into the papers?”

[034-125] “Good heavens, no!”

[034-126] “And there’s another thing. Suppose I did come in and pull that spiel, who would believe it?”

[034-127] “The girl would. She is very simple.”

[034-128] “She must be.”

[034-129] “Just an ignorant village girl. The sort who would naturally recoil from a man in the circumstances I have outlined.”

[034-130] “Suppose they ask me questions?”

[034-131] “They won’t.”

[034-132] “But suppose they do? Suppose the girl says Where did you meet him and when did all this happen and what the hell and all like that, what do I say?”

[034-133] Hamilton Beamish considered the point.

[034-134] “I think the best plan would be for you to pretend, immediately after you have spoken the words I have indicated, that emotion has made you feel faint. Yes, that is best. Having said those words, exclaim ‘Air! Air! I want air!’ and rush out.”

[034-135] “Now you’re talking. I like that bit about rushing out. I’ll go so quick, they won’t see me.”

[034-136] “Then you are prepared to do this thing?”

[034-137] “Looks as if I’d got to.”

[034-138] “Good. Kindly run through your opening speech. I must see that you are letter-perfect.”

[034-139] “George! George! …”

[034-140] “Pause before the second George and take in breath. Remember that the intensity or loudness of the voice depends on the amplitude of the movement of the vocal chords, while pitch depends on the number of vibrations per second. Tone is strengthened by the resonance of the air on the air-passages and in the pharyngeal and oral cavities. Once more, please.”

[034-141] “George! George! Why did you desert me? …”

[034-142] “Arms extended.”

[034-143] “You don’t belong to that girl there.”

[034-144] “Pause. Breath.”

[034-145] “You belong to me—the woman you have wronged!”

[034-146] Hamilton Beamish nodded with restrained approval.

[034-147] “Not bad. Not at all bad. I should have liked, if it had been possible, to have an expert examine your thyro-arytenoid ligaments: and I wish there had been time for you to study my booklet on ‘[[the-beamish-booklets|Voice Production]].’ … However, I think it will do. Now go back and hide in the rhododendrons. This girl may be arriving at any moment.”
