# Love and Romance

## Overview

Love and romance drive the central plot of *The Small Bachelor*. P.G. Wodehouse uses romantic entanglements to create both [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]] and character development. The novel's key romantic relationships — [[George-Finch|George Finch]] and [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]], [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] and his mysterious bus companion — are built on the tension between spontaneous passion and calculated rationality. The novel's thesis, delivered through comic reversals rather than lectures, is that love cannot be reduced to an [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] equation.

## Love at First Sight

The concept of "[[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]]" is a recurring motif. When [[George-Finch|George Finch]] first sees [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] at a p[[art-and-artists|art]]y, his friend [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]] immediately recognizes the signs:

> "It sounds like love at first sight." [002-051]

Ironically, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] himself has written a booklet arguing strongly against [[beamish-bus-encounter|love at first sight]] [002-055]. This intellectual opposition to spontaneous romance becomes a source of [[humor-and-comedy|comedy]] when [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] himself falls victim to the very phenomenon he has criticized:

> Which simply goes to prove that even efficiency experts, when they fall in love at first sight, can babble like any man of inferior intellect in the same circumstances. [019-048]

The narrative voice's gentle mockery — "babble like any man of inferior intellect" — is Wodehouse at his most characteristic: the aphorism delivered [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h a straight face, the observation so perfectly phrased that the reader laughs before fully registering its truth.

## George and Molly

[[George-Finch|George Finch]]'s love for [[Molly-Waddington|Molly Waddington]] is immediate and consuming. He is incredulous that anyone — even the rational [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] — could know [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] and not be in love [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h her:

> "You stand there and tell me," said George incredulously, "that you have known this wonderful girl for many years and are not in love with her?" [005-052]

[[George-Finch|George]]'s incredulity is the comic marker of the lovestruck: the inability to imagine that anyone else could see the beloved differently. This is the sentiment that [[beamish-booklets|Beamish's booklets]], [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h t[[fortune-and-inheritance|heir]] careful reasoning about compatibility and rational choice, cannot account for.

## Beamish's Transformation

[[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]], the [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert who believes love should be a reasoned decision, undergoes a complete transformation after his own encounter [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h romance. He meets an unknown woman on a bus and falls in love instantly — [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout learning her name:

> "I think I am in love with your friend, George Finch." [020-006]
> "I am in love with... I don't know her name." [020-007]

The ellipsis in the second quotation is devastating. [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]], the man who has an [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] formula for everything, cannot even complete the sentence "I am in love [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h..." [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout confronting the absurdity of his position. When [[George-Finch|George]] reminds him of his own theories about love being a reasoned emotion, [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] can only offer weak excuses [020-009].

## The Two Models of Love

Wodehouse stages a quiet philosophical debate between two models of romance:

| Model | Proponent | Principle | Outcome |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Spontaneous passion | [[George-Finch|George Finch]] | Love strikes [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]hout warning | Triumphs — [[George-Finch|George]] wins [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]] |
| Rational choice | [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]]'s [[the-beamish-booklets|booklets]] | Love should be reasoned | Collapses — [[Hamilton-Beamish|Beamish]] falls in love on a bus |
| Social calculation | [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] | Love should improve one's position | Defeated — [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]] flees |

The novel's verdict is clear: love cannot be systematized. The [[efficiency-and-self-improvement|efficiency]] expert's [[the-beamish-booklets|booklets]] are no match for a [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] encounter on a city bus.
