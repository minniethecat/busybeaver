# The Waddington Dinner Party

## Overview

The formal dinner party at the [[Mrs-Waddington|Waddington]] residence is a pivotal early scene in *The Small Bachelor*. It brings together the [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y and exclusive [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the unwelcome [[art-and-artists|Bohemian]] element represented by [[George-Finch|George Finch]], setting the stage for the novel's central [[class-and-society|social]] conflict. The dinner table becomes a battlefield where [[class-and-society|social]] [[class-and-society|class]], [[art-and-artists|artist]]ic identity, and romantic ambition collide — all under the watchful eye of a hostess who "disliked [[art-and-artists|artists]] most" [015-090].

[[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]] hosts the dinner for "some of the most [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]]y and exclusive people" [008-031]. The guest list is itself a comic document of [[class-and-society|social]] aspiration:

## Lord Hunstanton's Entrance

The dinner party marks [[lord-hunstanton|Lord Hunstanton]]'s first [[fashion-and-appearance|appearance]], and his entrance is characteristically comic:

> "Well, here I am," said Lord Hunstanton. He paused for a moment, then added, "What, what!" as if he felt that it was expected of him. [008-008]

His immediate concern — whether dinner will involve "cold meat and a pickle" [008-017] — establishes the gap between his aristocratic title and his domestic anxieties. The British lord, imported as a [[class-and-society|social]] trophy for [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]], turns out to be preoccupied [[humor-and-comedy|wit]]h the menu.

## The Social Crucible

The dinner party establishes the [[class-and-society|social]] world that [[George-Finch|George]] must navigate. Surrounded by [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s guests, [[George-Finch|George]]'s identity as an [[art-and-artists|artist]] marks him as an outsider. [[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee H. Waddington]] later provides the dinner's most vivid summary:

> "I don't know if you noticed a pie-faced fellow with an eyeglass and a toothbrush moustache at dinner? That was Lord Hunstanton. He keeps telling me things about etiquette." [014-038]

[[Sigsbee-H-Waddington|Sigsbee]] H.'s exasperation — being lectured on etiquette in his own home by a man he describes as "pie-faced" — captures the dinner's essential dynamic: everyone is performing for someone, and nobody is comfortable.

## Consequences

The dinner party sets in motion the chain of events that drives the novel's plot: [[Mrs-Waddington|Mrs. Waddington]]'s determination to separate [[George-Finch|George]] from [[Molly-Waddington|Molly]], [[Hamilton-Beamish|Hamilton Beamish]]'s schemes to help his friend, and the eventual revelation of [[George-Finch|George]]'s [[fortune-and-inheritance|wealth]] that transforms the [[class-and-society|social]] calculus. The dinner is the dramatic inciting incident — after it, the battle lines are drawn.
