## Definition

Reward and Punishment (French: *récompense et châtiment*, Hebrew: *sachar va-onesh*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the principle of divine retribution by which [[god|God]] recompenses obedience to the [[Law]] and punishes transgression. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] reinterprets this doctrine philosophically: reward is not an extrinsic payment for good behavior but the natural consequence of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual and moral perfection — the soul's attainment of [[wisdom|knowledge]] of [[God]] and its resulting immunity to destruction.

This reinterpretation is one of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most radical philosophical moves. Against the popular conception of reward as material prosperity and punishment as material calamity, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that true reward is [[wisdom|intellect]]ual — the survival of the acquired [[wisdom|intellect]] after death — and that what Scripture describes as divine punishment is, philosophically understood, the natural destruction of the soul that has not perfected its [[wisdom|intellect]] (015-002).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of reward and punishment is distributed throughout the Guide rather than concentrated in a single section. In Part I, Chapter 2, discussing the Garden of Eden narrative, he explains that [[adam|Adam]]'s punishment — expulsion from the garden — is not an arbitrary sentence but the necessary consequence of his disobedience: "sa désobéissance lui mérita cette grande perfection propre à l'homme" (015-002). The "punishment" is the loss of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection that [[adam|Adam]] had previously enjoyed.

In Chapter 54 of Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] analyzes the thirteen attributes of divine mercy (*shelosh-esreh middot*) revealed to [[Moses]] ([[exodus|Exodus]] 34:6–7). The final attribute — "visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children" — appears to contradict divine justice. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] resolves this by explaining that "punishment" in Scripture often refers to the natural causal consequences of actions that propagate through generations, not to [[god|God]]'s arbitrary anger. The attributes must be understood as descriptions of divine *actions* in the world, not as passions in the divine essence (057-005).

> Sa désobéissance lui mérita cette grande perfection propre à l'homme — je veux dire la distinction entre le bien et le mal.
> (015-002)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] connects reward and punishment to his broader theory of [[Providence]]. Divine providence ext[[final-cause|end]]s to human beings in proportion to their [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection: the more one knows [[god|God]], the more one is under providence. The wicked, by contrast, are abandoned to chance. In this framework, reward and punishment are not external recompense but the direct result of one's relationship to the [[emanation|divine overflow]]: the righteous draw near to [[god|God]] and are thereby protected, while the wicked distance themselves and are thereby exposed to the vicissitudes of matter (050-007).

The Mosaic [[law|Law]] plays a crucial role in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' account: the commandments train the soul to turn away from bodily desires and toward [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection. Obedience to the [[law|Law]] is therefore not the condition for reward in a juridical sense but the *means* by which the soul becomes capable of receiving the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual overflow that constitutes its true reward. Punishment, conversely, is the soul's failure to achieve this perfection — equivalent to its extinction upon death (057-002).
