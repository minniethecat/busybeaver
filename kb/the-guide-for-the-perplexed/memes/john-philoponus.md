# John Philoponus

**John Philoponus** (Greek: Ἰωάννης ὁ Φιλόπονος, *Iōánnēs ho Philóponos*; c. 490 – c. 570 CE), also known as **John the Grammarian** (*Jean le grammairien*), was a Greek Christian philosopher and theologian of the Alexandrian school. [[Maimonides]] identifies him as a key source for the argumentative methods later adopted by the Islamic [[Kalām]] theologians.

## Influence on the Kalām

In Part I, Chapter 71 of the *Guide*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] traces the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual genealogy of Islamic [[kalam|dialectical theology]] back to Greek Christian writers, naming Philoponus prominently:

> [070-017] La même méthode, comme l'auteur va l'expliquer, fut suivie par les Motecallemîn musulmans, qui connaissaient les écrits de plusieurs écrivains de l'Église grecque, tels que Jean Philopone, Jean Damascène et autres.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that when the Christian Church absorbed nations among whom Greek philosophy was widespread, Christian scholars like Philoponus developed a [[knowledge|science]] of dialectical argument (*[[Kalām]]*) to def[[final-cause|end]] religious doctrine against philosophical critique. This method was later transmitted to Islamic theologians.

## His Writings and Their Transmission

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] provides a precise identification of Philoponus and explains how his works reached Islamic thinkers:

> [070-018] Cet auteur est Jean Philopone, surnommé le grammairien, qui florissait à Alexandrie dans les VIe et VIIe siècles; son commentaire sur la Physique d'Aristote porte la date de l'an 333 de l'ère de Dioclétien, ou des Martyrs, qui correspond à l'an 617 de l'ère chrét.

The works [[maimonides|Maimonides]] alludes to include Philoponus's *Refutation of Proclus on the [[eternity|Eternity]] of the World* and his *Cosmogony of [[moses|Moses]]* — texts that argued against [[Aristotle]]'s doctrine of the eternity of the world, a position [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself grapples with throughout the *Guide*.

## Philoponus as Bridge Figure

Philoponus occupies a unique position in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s [[wisdom|intellect]]ual history. He stands at the intersection of three traditions: Greek [[Philosophy]], Christian [[Theology]], and Islamic [[Kalām]]. His critiques of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics and his arguments for temporal creation provided the [[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]] with ready-made refutations that they "seized upon in the opinion of having made an important discovery" (070-018). [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s awareness of this genealogy reflects his broader project of tracing the historical transmission of ideas.
