## Overview

Joseph ben Judah (Joseph ibn Aknin) is the disciple to whom [[maimonides|Maimonides]] dedicated the *Guide for the Perplexed*. He came from Sabta (Ceuta), a city in the Maghreb al-Aksa (the far west of North Africa), and his passion for study earned him [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' esteem even before they met — [[maimonides|Maimonides]] first encountered Joseph through the poems and rhymed prose compositions (Makamat) that reached him from Alexandria. (001-001)

Joseph studied astronomy, mathematics, and logic under [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' direction. When circumstances separated them, Joseph continued his studies indep[[final-cause|end]]ently but wrote to [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with pressing philosophical questions — questions that ultimately prompted [[maimonides|Maimonides]] to compose the *Guide* as a written response. (001-002)

## In the Guide

The *Guide* opens with [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' epistle dedicatory to Joseph, in which he explains that the work was written "for you and for your like, however few they may be." [[maimonides|Maimonides]] describes Joseph as possessing both [[wisdom|intellect]]ual capacity and genuine religious commitment — the rare combination that qualifies a student for esoteric philosophical instruction. (001-002)

Joseph had reached a point in his studies where the apparent contradictions between [[truth|philosophical truth]] and the literal sense of Scripture troubled him. [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' response is the *Guide* itself: a systematic demonstration that these two sources of truth are ultimately compatible. Joseph thus stands as the p[[accident|arad]]igmatic "perplexed" reader for whom the entire work was conceived. (001-003)

> Maimonides wrote of Joseph: "already very highly placed in my esteem because of your great passion for study" — the disciple whose intellectual seriousness called the *Guide* into being. (001-002)
