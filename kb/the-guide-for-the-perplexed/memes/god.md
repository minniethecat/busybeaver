## Definition

God (Dieu), in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' "Guide for the Perplexed", is the incorporeal, eternal, and absolutely one First Cause. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that God cannot be described by positive attributes — one can only say what God is *not* (via negativa) or describe His actions. (P01-021)

The core principle is expressed in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of divine attributes: all terms that appear to describe God in scripture must be understood either as descriptions of divine actions or as negations of privation. To say "God is living" means "God is not dead"; to say "God is powerful" describes what God does, not what God is. (P01-020)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes extensive discussion to the question of divine attributes, arguing that all terms applied to God in scripture must be understood allegorically. When scripture speaks of God "speaking", "seeing", or "sitting", these are metaphors for divine actions or relations, not descriptions of God's essence. (P01-020)

> The corporeal terms applied to God in the books of the prophets are all metaphorical; they refer to divine actions or relations, never to God's essence itself.

The term "return" (shub), when applied to God, signifies a change in the relation between God and man, not a change in God Himself. Similarly, terms like "attention" or "care" designate divine [[providence|Providence]] when applied to God. (P02-003) (P02-004)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s concept of God as the Prime Mover — the unmoved mover who sets all things in motion without Himself being in motion. The Guide's treatment of God as "first mover" (P02-006) bridges [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics with biblical theology. (P02-006)

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[divine-attributes|negative theology]] was revolutionary and remains one of his most influential contributions. By insisting that God can only be described through negations, he prefigures later apophatic traditions in medieval philosophy. (P03-012)

His approach was contested: critics argued it leaves the believer with no positive content for faith, while def[[final-cause|end]]ers saw it as the only [[wisdom|intellect]]ually honest way to preserve divine transc[[final-cause|end]]ence. (P03-013)

The doctrine of [[incorporeality|divine incorporeality]] is, for [[maimonides|Maimonides]], a fundamental principle — alongside creation ex nihilo — that every Jew must affirm. To conceive of God as corporeal is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' view, worse than idolatry, be[[causality|cause]] it degrades the very concept of the Divine. (003-003)
