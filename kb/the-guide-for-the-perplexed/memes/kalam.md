## Definition

Kalam (Arabic: *Kalâm*, literally "speech" or "discourse") refers to the tradition of Islamic speculative theology whose practitioners are known as the *[[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]]* (French: *Motécallemin*, "those who engage in Kalâm"). In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes extensive attention to the Kalam, treating it as the primary representative of a theological method that, while sharing his goal of def[[final-cause|end]]ing religious belief through rational argument, diverges fundamentally from philosophical demonstration in its premises and procedures.

The Kalam theologians, as [[maimonides|Maimonides]] describes them, developed a comprehensive system of physics and metaphysics designed to support the doctrines of creation *ex nihilo*, divine volition, and the possibility of [[prophets|prophecy]]. Their method proceeds not from the nature of being but from the requirements of religious belief: they ask what the world must be like for revelation to be possible, then construct arguments to show that the world is indeed that way (001-003). [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' interest in the Kalam stems from his conviction that [[wisdom|understanding]] their errors is essential for anyone seeking to distinguish genuine philosophical [[wisdom|knowledge]] from merely persuasive dialectics.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most concentrated treatment of the Kalam appears in Part I, Chapters 51–52 and 69–70. He identifies twelve fundamental propositions that the Motécallemin universally accept as premises for their proofs, including [[atoms|atom]]ism, the denial of stable natures, and the assertion that all accidents are created anew at each moment by divine will (051-010). These premises, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues, are not demonstrated but assumed; the Kalam theologians begin with the conclusion they wish to prove and reason backward to premises that would support it.

> Ce sont là des assertions qui toutes se disent seulement, qui existent dans les paroles et non dans les esprits.
> (052-001)

The Kalam's rejection of causality is of particular importance to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]. In Chapter 69, he examines their refusal to call [[god|God]] a "[[causality|cause]]," preferring instead the term "agent" (*fāʿil*). The Motécallemin believe that calling [[god|God]] a [[causality|cause]] would imply the world's co-eternity with Him, since a [[causality|cause]] and its effect must coexist; an agent, by contrast, can precede its action temporally (069-012). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] refutes this distinction at length, arguing that the four [[causality|Aristotelian causes]] — efficient, material, for[[evil|mal]], and final — are all applicable to [[god|God]], and that the Kalam's linguistic scruple does not resolve the underlying metaphysical question (069-015).

The Kalam's influence ext[[final-cause|end]]ed beyond purely theological circles into what [[maimonides|Maimonides]] regarded as errors in biblical interpretation. He observes that certain Jewish commentators had adopted Kalam methods uncritically, borrowing arguments and terminology that originated in the polemical context of Islam but were ill-suited to Jewish scriptural exegesis (070-013).

## Interpretation

Modern scholarship has debated the extent of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' firsthand [[wisdom|knowledge]] of Kalam literature. While he names specific texts and doctrines, some scholars suggest he relied on summaries and polemical digests rather than direct study of Kalam works. Nevertheless, his critique of the Kalam serves a clear pedagogical function within the Guide: it provides a foil against which his own [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian-Neoplatonic philosophical theology can be defined. By showing what the Kalam gets wrong — its [[atoms|atom]]ism, its occasionalism, its rejection of natural causality — [[maimonides|Maimonides]] prepares the reader to understand what genuine philosophical demonstration requires.
