## Overview

Adam ('adam, in Hebrew) is the first man according to the biblical account, and his nature is a recurring subject of philosophical analysis in the Guide. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] examines Adam not merely as a historical person but as an archetype of the human condition: created with perfect [[wisdom|intellect]] in the image of [[god|God]], possessing the faculty of reason that distinguishes truth from falsehood, and subsequently losing this perfection through disobedience. (016-001)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses Adam most extensively in Part I, Chapter 2, where he addresses the objection that the serpent's promise — "you shall be as Elohim, knowing good and evil" — appears to promise a higher perfection as a punishment for disobedience. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] resolves this by distinguishing between two domains of [[wisdom|knowledge]]: the [[wisdom|intellect]] grasps necessary truths (true and false), while the conventional realm of good and evil pertains to merely probable opinions. Adam before the fall possessed only the former: his [[wisdom|intellect]] was perfect, and he had no faculty directed toward what is conventionally "beautiful" or "ugly." In this state, even uncovering his nakedness was not shameful to him, be[[causality|cause]] he did not judge by conventional standards.

> La raison que Dieu a fait émaner sur l'homme , et qui constitue sa perfection finale , est celle qu'Adam possédait avant sa désobéissance ; c'est pour elle qu'il a été dit de lui qu'il était (fait) à l'image de Dieu et à sa ressemblance. (016-001)

When Adam disobeyed by following his corporeal desires and the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]], he was punished by the loss of this [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection. He came to know good and evil — a lower form of cognition — and thereby fell from the rank of pure [[wisdom|intellect]] to that of a being governed by opinion and appetite. This loss was accompanied by his expulsion from the Garden of Eden, as [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets the verse: "When he changed his face, You sent him forth" ([[job|Job]] 14:20), meaning that when Adam changed his direction and pursued what was forbidden, he was expelled. (017-001)

In Part I, Chapter 7, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] further develops the theme by analyzing the verse "And Adam lived a hundred and thirty years, and begot in his likeness, after his image" (Genesis 5:3). Before Seth, Adam's children lacked the true human form — the [[wisdom|intellect]] that constitutes the divine image — and were therefore not fully human, though they had the shape and lineaments of men. Only when Adam instructed Seth and gave him [[wisdom|understanding]] did he produce a child truly "in his likeness, after his image." (020-001)
