## Definition

The celestial spheres (French: *sphères célestes*, Arabic: *aflāk*) are, in the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian-Ptolemaic cosmology that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] inherits, a system of concentric, transparent orbs that carry the planets, the sun, the moon, and the fixed stars in their circular motions around the earth. Each sphere is governed by a separate Intelligence — an incorporeal [[wisdom|intellect]] that serves as the sphere's mover — and together they constitute the intermediate realm between the sublunar [[sublunar-world|world of generation and corruption]] and the purely [[wisdom|intellect]]ual realm of the divine.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] treats the celestial spheres as more than astronomical constructs. For him, they are essential to [[wisdom|understanding]] the hierarchical structure of being: the spheres represent the highest level of physical reality, directly moved by [[wisdom|intellect]], and their study is a prerequisite for any comprehension of divine governance. The sphere of the moon marks the boundary between the incorruptible celestial realm and the sublunar world subject to generation and decay (014-001).

## In the Guide

The spheres appear throughout the *Guide for the Perplexed* in multiple contexts. In the cosmological discussions of Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] refers to the Intelligence of the spheres (*Intelligences des sphères*) as intermediaries between [[god|God]] and the human [[wisdom|intellect]], through which divine influence desc[[final-cause|end]]s to the sublunar realm (015-001). The movement of the spheres is described as circular and eternal — a reflection of the perfection of their movers, the separate Intelligences.

> L'auteur entend les sphères célestes et les astres fixés dans ces sphères.
> (024-002)

In Chapter 10, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses the immediate mover of the uppermost sphere (*la sphère supérieure*), linking it to the highest of the separate Intelligences (010-001). The spheres are arranged hierarchically: the ninth sphere, the diurnal sphere, imparts daily rotation to all lower spheres, while each planetary sphere has its own proper motion (028-002).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also employs the image of the spheres in his explanation of prophetic experience. In the parable of [[jacob|Jacob]]'s ladder, he interprets the ladder as representing the hierarchy of the spheres, with the angels asc[[final-cause|end]]ing and desc[[final-cause|end]]ing being the Intelligences that govern them (026-002). This identification of angels with the Intelligences of the spheres is one of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most characteristic moves: it translates biblical imagery into [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian cosmology.

The spherical structure ext[[final-cause|end]]s to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' discussion of the heavens in Part II. He notes that the celestial region is sometimes described as a "hemisphere" by those who lack astronomical [[wisdom|knowledge]], while the true picture involves nested, complete spheres surrounding one another (044-009, 044-010). The mutual positions of the spheres (*position respective de leurs sphères*) are among the matters that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] considers properly scientific [[wisdom|knowledge]], distinct from mere speculation (046-004).
