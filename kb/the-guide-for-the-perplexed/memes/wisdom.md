## Definition

Wisdom (French: *sagesse*, Hebrew: *ḥokhmah*, Arabic: *ḥikma*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the highest form of human perfection and the ultimate goal of the religious life. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes between two senses of [[knowledge|wisdom]]: theoretical [[knowledge|wisdom]], which is knowledge of [[God]] and the structure of the cosmos (what the philosophers call metaphysics and physics), and practical [[knowledge|wisdom]], which is the rational understanding of the commandments' [[final-cause|purpose]]s (*ta'amei ha-mitzvot*).

This dual sense reflects [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' synthesis of the biblical concept of [[knowledge|wisdom]] — personified in Proverbs and identified with the fear of [[god|God]] — and the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian concept of *sophia* as the intellectual virtue of grasping first principles. [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' key move is to argue that the [[law|Torah]]'s commandments are themselves vehicles for acquiring [[knowledge|wisdom]]: they train the soul away from bodily desires and toward the intellectual contemplation that constitutes true human perfection (029-001).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of [[knowledge|wisdom]] reaches its climax in Part III, Chapter 54, where he analyzes the meaning of the biblical verse "the fear of the Lord, that is [[knowledge|wisdom]]" ([[job|Job]] 28:28). He identifies four species of human perfection: perfection of possessions (the lowest), perfection of the body, perfection of [[ethics|moral virtues]], and — the highest — perfection of the intellect, which is "the true human perfection" be[[causality|cause]] it alone belongs to man as man (054-003).

The intellectual perfection [[maimonides|Maimonides]] advocates is not merely abstract contemplation. It culminates in the love of [[god|God]] (*ahavat ha-Shem*), which is proportionate to knowledge of [[god|God]]. Wisdom in this highest sense involves understanding that [[god|God]]'s attributes can only be described through negation — that we know [[god|God]] not by what He is but by what He is not. This [[divine-attributes|negative theology]] is itself the crown of [[knowledge|wisdom]] (059-002).

> Sache que les vrais attributs de Dieu sont ceux où l'attribution se fait au moyen de négations.
> (059-002)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] warns, however, that [[knowledge|wisdom]] has limits. The human intellect is finite and cannot grasp all things. He cites the Sages' injunction: "Do not study what is too obscure for you, and do not scrutinize what is too hidden for you; study what is within your capacity" (037-002). This intellectual humility is itself a mark of [[knowledge|wisdom]] — knowing where to stop inquiry is as important as knowing how to begin it (037-001).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also discusses the "[[knowledge|wisdom]]" (*ḥokhmah*) that the Talmud attributes to the Sages, distinguishing it from [[Prophecy]]. While [[prophets|prophecy]] involves the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] receiving [[emanation|divine overflow]], [[knowledge|wisdom]] — in the Talmudic sense of intellectual insight into the [[law|Law]] — involves the rational faculty alone and is accessible to any scholar who has perfected his [[Intellect]] through study (033-001).
