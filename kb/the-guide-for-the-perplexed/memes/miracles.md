# Miracles

**Miracles** (*mu'jizāt*, Hebrew: *nissim*) occupy a distinctive place in [[Maimonides]]' philosophy of nature and [[Providence|providence]]. Departing from both the popular conception of miracles as arbitrary divine interventions and the radical naturalism that denies miracles entirely, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] develops a theory according to which miracles were **pre-programmed into the natural order** at the moment of [[Creation|creation]].

## The Embedded Miracle Theory

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most original contribution to the philosophy of miracles is his argument — drawing on the rabbinic tradition — that all miraculous events were decreed and embedded in nature during the six days of creation. This theory appears in his *Commentary on the Mishnah* (Avot 5:6) and is elaborated throughout the *Guide*:

> Tous les miracles furent créés et déterminés lors des six jours de la création; leur réalisation n'est que l'actualisation de ce qui fut alors décrété. (067-005)

This position allows [[maimonides|Maimonides]] to affirm the reality of biblical miracles while preserving the immutability of natural law and [[God|divine]] [[knowledge|wisdom]]. [[god|God]] does not "change His mind" or "intervene" in history; rather, all apparent interventions were foreseen and incorporated into the original creative act.

## Miracles and the Natural Order

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes between miracles that temporarily susp[[final-cause|end]] natural regularities (such as the parting of the Red Sea) and those that operate through natural [[causality|cause]]s (such as the plagues of [[egypt|Egypt]]). In both cases, however, the event is grounded in the eternal [[Divine Will|divine will]]:

> Dieu faisant par miracle retentir l'air de sons qui frappèrent l'ouïe du prophète. (067-008)

The key distinction is not between "natural" and "supernatural," but between events that follow the **regular course** of nature (*al-ṭabī'a al-mu'tāda*) and those that follow an **exceptional course** (*khāriq al-'āda*) — both of which are equally expressions of divine [[knowledge|wisdom]].

## Miracles as Epistemological Proofs

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] assigns miracles an important epistemological function: they serve as **public verification** of prophetic claims. A prophet's ability to perform miracles — particularly in the case of [[Moses]] — confirms that the prophet's mes[[wisdom|sage]] genuinely originates from [[god|God]]:

> Il fut mis en possession d'un miracle. Il est donc clair que le miracle authentifie la mission prophétique. (067-002)

However, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cautions that miracles alone are insufficient to establish lasting faith. True conviction arises only from [[wisdom|intellect]]ual demonstration and the rational [[wisdom|understanding]] of [[Knowledge|divine science]].
