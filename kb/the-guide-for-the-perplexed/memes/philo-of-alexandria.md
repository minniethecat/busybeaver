## Overview

Philo of Alexandria (Philo Judaeus) was a Hellenistic Jewish philosopher who lived in Alexandria during the first century CE. He is the earliest Jewish thinker known to have systematically applied Greek philosophical concepts — particularly [[plato|Plato]]nic and Stoic doctrines — to the interpretation of the Hebrew Bible. Philo's central methodological innovation was [[esoteric-interpretation|allegorical interpretation]]: the conviction that beneath the literal sense of Scripture lies a deeper philosophical meaning accessible to reason. This hermeneutic principle, though never attributed to Philo by name in the *Guide*, constitutes the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual foundation upon which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] built his own project of reconciling biblical revelation with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy.

Philo's synthesis of Jerusalem and Athens — of prophetic revelation and rational inquiry — created a p[[accident|arad]]igm that shaped all subsequent Jewish philosophy. Through Arabic intermediaries and the broader tradition of Hellenistic Jewish thought, Philo's allegorical method reached the medieval Jewish philosophers, including [[saadia-gaon|Saadia Gaon]] and ultimately [[maimonides|Maimonides]], who brought it to its fullest expression.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] never names Philo in the *Guide for the Perplexed*, but Philo's [[wisdom|intellect]]ual legacy permeates the work. The *Guide*'s fundamental premise — that the apparent contradictions between Scripture and philosophy can be resolved by reading the biblical text at multiple levels of meaning — is the Philonic project recast in [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian terms.

This affinity is most visible in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of allegory and metaphor. In the Introduction and throughout Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] systematically argues that biblical language about [[god|God]] must be understood figuratively: when Scripture speaks of [[god|God]]'s hand, face, or throne, it employs metaphors whose philosophical meaning must be extracted through careful interpretation. (002-003) The same hermeneutic governs Philo's *Allegorical Commentary*, where every anthropomorphic expression becomes an invitation to philosophical reflection.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' discussion of the *[[maase-bereschith|Ma'aseh Bereshit]]h* (the Account of the Beginning) and the *Ma'aseh Mercabah* (the [[maase-mercaba|Account of the Chariot]]) likewise follows a Philonic pattern. Philo had read the creation narrative in Genesis as an allegory of the soul's ascent to [[god|God]] and identified the divine chariot of Ezekiel with the highest [[truth|philosophical truth]]s. [[maimonides|Maimonides]], while replacing Philo's Middle [[plato|Plato]]nic framework with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics and metaphysics, preserves the same structural insight: the esoteric meaning of Scripture corresponds to the highest achievements of rational inquiry. (026-005)

In Part I, Chapter 26, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] def[[final-cause|end]]s the use of figurative language in philosophical discourse, citing [[plato|Plato]]'s practice of calling matter "the fe[[evil|mal]]e" and form "the [[evil|mal]]e." This defense of esoteric expression — that philosophers, like prophets, clothe their teachings in metaphor — is thoroughly Philonic in spirit. Philo had argued that [[moses|Moses]] himself was the greatest of philosophers, who chose to express abstract truths in concrete images suited to the [[wisdom|understanding]] of the multitude. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] applies the same reasoning to the entire biblical corpus.

The *Guide*'s [[divine-attributes|negative theology]] — the doctrine that [[god|God]] can only be described by what He is not — also has Philonic antecedents. Philo had insisted that [[god|God]]'s essence is beyond all human categories and that even the divine names in Scripture denote not [[god|God]]'s nature but His activities in the world. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] develops this insight with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian rigor in Part I, Chapters 50–60, arguing that all positive attributes of [[god|God]] must be understood either as negations or as descriptions of divine actions. (058-004)

## Significance

Philo's importance for [[wisdom|understanding]] the *Guide* lies not in direct citation but in the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual genealogy he established. By demonstrating that Jewish Scripture could sustain philosophical interpretation without ceasing to be divine revelation, Philo created the conceptual space that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] would later occupy. The *Guide* is, in a meaningful sense, the culmination of the Philonic tradition — a tradition that insisted on the ultimate harmony of faith and reason, [[law|Torah]] and philosophy.

However, the differences are equally instructive. Where Philo reads [[plato|Plato]] into [[moses|Moses]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] reads [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. Where Philo's [[god|God]] remains within a Middle [[plato|Plato]]nic framework of intermediary powers (the Logos), [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[god|God]] is radically unified, beyond all multiplicity. These transformations reflect the maturation of Jewish philosophy through its encounter with Islamic *kalam* and [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian [[knowledge|science]] — a development Philo made possible but could not have anticipated.
