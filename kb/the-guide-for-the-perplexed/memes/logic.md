## Definition

Logic (Greek: *logikē*, Arabic: *mantiq*, French: *logique*) is, in the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian curriculum that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows, the [[knowledge|science]] that studies the forms of valid reasoning. It is the first of the three preparatory [[knowledge|science]]s — logic, physics, and mathematics — that must be mastered before one can approach metaphysics and theology. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents logic not as an [[final-cause|end]] in itself but as an indispensable instrument (*organon*) without which all higher inquiry is compromised.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] opens the Guide by recalling that his pupil [[joseph-ben-judah|Joseph ben Judah]] had studied logic under his direction (001-002). This personal reference establishes that the int[[final-cause|end]]ed reader has already passed through the logical training that the work presupposes. The Guide itself is not a text of logic, but it everywhere dep[[final-cause|end]]s on logical distinctions — between homonyms and univocal terms, between demonstration and dialectic, between essence and accident — that only a logically trained reader can appreciate.

## In the Guide

The role of logic in the Guide is most explicit in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' classification of the [[knowledge|science]]s. In his outline of the curriculum that leads to [[knowledge|wisdom]], he divides [[wisdom|knowledge]] into three parts: logic, physics (including what is beneath the sphere of the moon and what is above it), and metaphysics (003-002). Logic is placed first be[[causality|cause]] it provides the tools — the theory of definition, the forms of the syllogism, the analysis of fallacies — that all subsequent inquiry requires.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' reliance on logical categories pervades the Guide's method. His famous discussion of biblical language and [[anthropomorphism]] turns on the logical distinction between homonymous, metaphorical, and amphibological terms (001-004, 002-001). Without the logical training to recognize these distinctions, the reader cannot properly interpret Scripture, falling either into crude [[anthropomorphism]] or into an equally misguided literalism. The [[knowledge|science]] of logic "must be deepened before approaching the [[knowledge|science]] of divine things" (019-002).

> Tu sais que celui qui a écrit ces pages, c'est-à-dire moi, fils de Maïmoun, avait étudié avec toi l'art de la logique.
> (001-002)

The logical treatment of the categories (*catégories*) receives special attention. In Chapter 9, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] notes the dual nature — logical and ontological — of the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian categories: they are at once forms of predication (logic) and forms of being (ontology) (009-003). This intersection of logic and metaphysics is characteristic of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' approach: logical analysis is never purely for[[evil|mal]] but always directed toward ontological truth. The term "amphibological" (*amphibologique*), which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] uses frequently, itself derives from logical vocabulary and designates terms whose ambiguity is not accidental but structural, requiring careful logical analysis to disentangle (013-002).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes his own enterprise from mere philological commentary. He is not offering "philological explanations" (*explications philologiques*) of ambiguous terms — that work belongs to lexicography, not to logic or philosophy (021-002). Instead, he uses logic to expose the conceptual structures that underlie the language of Scripture, revealing the [[truth|philosophical truth]]s concealed beneath the surface of the biblical text.
