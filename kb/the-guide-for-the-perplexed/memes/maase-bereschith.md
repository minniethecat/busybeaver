## Definition

*Maasé Beréschîth* (Hebrew: מעשה בראשית, "the Work of the Beginning") refers to the biblical account of creation found in Genesis 1. In rabbinic tradition, it is classified as an esoteric doctrine whose public teaching is restricted: the Mishnah (Hagigah 2:1) prohibits expounding it before more than one student at a time (002-004).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] radically reinterprets this restriction, arguing that *Maasé Beréschîth* is nothing other than **[[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics** — the study of the natural world and its principles — encoded in scriptural language for the protection of philosophically untrained readers.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] unveils his interpretation of *Maasé Beréschîth* early in the Guide, in Part I, Chapter 2. He states explicitly that the Account of Creation corresponds to the [[knowledge|science]] of physics, the study of bodies in motion and the principles governing the sublunar world (002-003).

This identification transforms the esoteric biblical narrative into a philosophical curriculum, aligning the [[law|Torah]]'s opening chapters with Greek natural [[knowledge|science]].

The traditional rabbinic prohibition on public teaching is explained by [[maimonides|Maimonides]] as a pedagogical safeguard. Physics deals with abstract principles that contradict sensory appearances, such as the distinction between matter and form. Teaching it to unprepared minds would lead to confusion rather than enlightenment. The [[wisdom|sage]]s therefore veiled it in allegorical language (003-002).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s this framework to details of the Genesis narrative. The "firmament" (*raqia*), the "waters above," and the six days of creation are treated as allegories for physical and metaphysical truths. The "light" created on the first day is read as a reference to primeval substance.

## Interpretation

The identification of *Maasé Beréschîth* with physics serves a dual apologetic [[final-cause|purpose]]. For Jewish readers troubled by conflicts between [[law|Torah]] and Greek [[knowledge|science]], it demonstrates that Scripture itself mandates the study of [[aristotelian-physics|natural philosophy]]. For philosophically sophisticated readers, it provides a framework for engaging with the biblical text without [[wisdom|intellect]]ual compromise.
