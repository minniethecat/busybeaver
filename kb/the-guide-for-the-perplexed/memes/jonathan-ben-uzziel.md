## Overview

Jonathan ben Uzziel (Yonathan ben Uzziel, in Hebrew) was a first-century tanna, the foremost disciple of Hillel the Elder, and the traditional author of the Aramaic Targum to the [[prophets|Prophets]] (the books of Joshua, Judges, [[samuel|Samuel]], and Kings, along with Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the twelve minor prophets). In the Guide, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] consistently appeals to Jonathan ben Uzziel's Targum as an authoritative precedent for interpreting biblical [[anthropomorphism]]s in a non-corporeal sense. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] treats Jonathan's translation choices not as improvisations but as faithful transmissions of an ancient tradition of avoiding the attribution of bodily characteristics or passions to [[god|God]]. (019-006)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] references Jonathan ben Uzziel throughout Part I of the Guide, where the systematic removal of anthropomorphic language from the conception of [[god|God]] is the central philosophical project. In Chapter 19, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] introduces Jonathan as a trustworthy authority on the meaning of difficult biblical expressions: "Comme a dit Jonathan ben Uzziel dans la version de la [[law|loi]]" (As Jonathan ben Uzziel said in the version of the [[law|Law]]). This [[final-cause|end]]orsement establishes that when Jonathan's Targum paraphrases a verse rather than translating it literally, the paraphrase reveals the authentic meaning that the literal text conceals. (019-006)

> Comme a dit Jonathan ben Uzziel dans la version de la loi... leurs opinions. (019-006)

In Chapter 32, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites an interpretation "qu'a eue en vue Jonathan ben Uzziel" when r[[final-cause|end]]ering a biblical verse concerning [[god|God]]'s appearance. Jonathan's r[[final-cause|end]]ering replaces the anthropomorphic expression with language that conveys a created manifestation — a "glory" or "presence" — rather than implying that [[god|God]] Himself became visible. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] treats this as evidence that the rabbinic tradition had always understood such verses in a non-literal sense. (032-007)

In Chapter 33, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] again draws upon Jonathan's paraphrase when explaining the phrase "puiser les eaux du salut" (Isaiah 12:3). Jonathan's Chaldean version r[[final-cause|end]]ers this in terms of receiving prophetic instruction — a reading that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts in his argument that biblical metaphors for water frequently signify [[wisdom|knowledge]] and prophetic [[revelation|inspiration]]. (033-003)

Chapter 45 establishes Jonathan ben Uzziel alongside [[onkelos|Onkelos]] as one of the two principal Aramaic translators who "font tout pour éviter l'[[anthropomorphism|anthropomorphisme]]" (do everything to avoid [[anthropomorphism]]). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] considers their shared translation practice — systematically replacing corporeal language about [[god|God]] with language about created divine manifestations — as normative for all subsequent interpretation. The consistency between [[onkelos|Onkelos]] on the Pentateuch and Jonathan on the [[prophets|Prophets]] demonstrates that non-corporeal interpretation was not a philosophical innovation but the traditional Jewish [[wisdom|understanding]]. (045-001)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most revealing discussion of Jonathan's method appears in Chapter 47, where he notes that Jonathan "n'a point traduit ce pas[[wisdom|sage]]" (did not translate this pas[[wisdom|sage]]) in the case of Judges 10:16 — "Et sa [[free-will|volonté]] s'abstint d'affliger [[israel|Israël]]." According to [[maimonides|Maimonides]], Jonathan refrained from translating be[[causality|cause]] the verse, taken literally, would attribute a change of mind or emotional passivity to [[god|God]]. Jonathan's silence — his refusal to translate — is itself an interpretive act, affirming the principle that any biblical expression implying divine mutability must be understood figuratively or, where necessary, left untranslated rather than r[[final-cause|end]]ered literally. (047-003)

## Significance

Jonathan ben Uzziel's Targum to the [[prophets|Prophets]] constitutes one of the earliest systematic efforts to reconcile biblical language with monotheistic theology. His method of paraphrase — replacing anthropomorphic expressions with references to the divine word (memra), glory (yekara), or presence (shekhinta) — provided [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with an authoritative rabbinic precedent for his own philosophical reinterpretation of Scripture. By grounding his non-corporeal reading in Jonathan's Targum, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] could present his radical theology not as a departure from tradition but as the recovery of its authentic, original meaning. The Guide's frequent citation of Jonathan thus serves a strategic [[final-cause|purpose]]: it demonstrates that the philosophical interpretation of Scripture is continuous with, rather than opposed to, the rabbinic tradition of translation and exegesis.
