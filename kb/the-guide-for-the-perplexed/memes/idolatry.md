## Definition

Idolatry (French: *idolâtrie*, Hebrew: *avodah zarah*) in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed* is not merely the [[prayer|worship]] of statues but a comprehensive theological error: the belief that beings other than [[god|God]] possess indep[[final-cause|end]]ent power and are therefore worthy of veneration. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows the rabbinic tradition in treating idolatry as the arch-sin — the root from which all other transgressions grow — but he gives this traditional view a philosophical grounding by analysing idolatry's historical origins and its cognitive foundations.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' distinctive contribution is his historical-genealogical account of how idolatry arose. He argues that the earliest humans recognized [[god|God]] as the Creator but gradually came to believe that honoring [[god|God]]'s servants — the stars, the celestial spheres, and eventually their physical images — was itself a form of divine service (044-008). This incremental [[revelation|descent]] from pure monotheism to idolatry provides the historical framework within which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets the entire Mosaic legislation.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most concentrated treatment of idolatry appears in Part III, Chapters 29–49, where he offers a systematic explanation of the rational grounds — the *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* — for the [[law|Torah]]'s commandments. His governing thesis is that many commandments whose [[final-cause|purpose]] is otherwise obscure were specifically designed to counter idolatrous beliefs and practices. The table of contents signals the importance of this material: "lorsqu'il est question d'idolâtrie" — when idolatry is the subject, a special interpretive key is required (P02-003).

> Si quelqu'un croyait que Zéus est une image de Jupiter, il se livrait par là à l'idolâtrie.
> (044-009)

In Chapter 44, within his ethical framework, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that idolatry is not merely an [[error|intellectual error]] but a moral corruption that prevents the mind from attaining truth. The [[ethics|moral virtues]] — what he calls the preparatory dispositions — must be cultivated precisely be[[causality|cause]] the passions that drive idolatry (fear, desire for control, aesthetic fascination) obscure rational judgment (044-012). One who is enslaved to the [[imagination]] cannot grasp the unity and incorporeality of [[god|God]].

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] deepens this analysis in Chapter 57 by arguing that the [[law|Torah]]'s punishments for idolatry — including the destruction of the idolatrous city — are proportionate to the severity of the error. Idolatry is treated more harshly than other sins be[[causality|cause]] it strikes at the foundation of the entire [[law|Law]]: "ces peines ne se rapportent qu'au péché d'idolâtrie en particulier" (057-005). The idolater does not merely violate a single commandment but repudiates the entire system of divine governance that the commandments express (057-006).

In Chapter 66, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s the historical analysis to the generation of the Dispersion, noting that some early nations "étaient livrés à l'idolâtrie" — were given over to idolatry (066-011). This genealogical claim serves [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' larger argument that idolatry is not the original human condition but a degeneration from an earlier monotheism — a view that directly opposes the Enlightenment narrative of progress from polytheism to monotheism.
