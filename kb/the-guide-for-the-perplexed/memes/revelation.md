## Definition

Revelation (French: *révélation*, Hebrew: *hitgallut*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the process by which divine [[wisdom|knowledge]] reaches the human [[wisdom|intellect]] — understood not as a literal "descent" of [[god|God]] or a verbal communication but as the emanation of the [[emanation|divine overflow]] (*al-fayd al-ilāhī*) through the [[Active Intellect]] to the perfected human soul. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] systematically de-anthropomorphizes the biblical language of revelation, arguing that Scripture's descriptions of [[god|God]] "desc[[final-cause|end]]ing" or "speaking" are metaphors for [[wisdom|intellect]]ual emanation.

The philosophical foundation of this account is [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' theory of the separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s: the [[active-intellect|Active Intellect]], the tenth and lowest of the cosmic [[intellect|intelligence]]s, governs the sublunar world and serves as the conduit through which [[emanation|divine overflow]] reaches human [[wisdom|intellect]]s capable of receiving it. Revelation is thus a natural cosmic process, not a miraculous interruption of nature (023-002).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] introduces the concept of revelation through a philological analysis of the Hebrew verb *y[[accident|arad]]* ("to desc[[final-cause|end]]"). In Chapter 10 of Part I, he explains that Scripture uses "descent" metaphorically for the arrival of divine [[wisdom|knowledge]]: "le Très-Haut, ayant voulu faire venir de lui la connaissance et faire émaner la révélation sur quelques uns d'entre nous, a employé, en parlant de la révélation desc[[final-cause|end]]ant sur le [[prophets|prophète]] et de la vision prophétique, le verbe *y[[accident|arad]]*" (023-002).

> Le Très-Haut, ayant voulu faire venir de lui la connaissance et faire émaner la révélation sur quelques uns d'entre nous, a employé le verbe yarad (descendre).
> (023-002)

This philological strategy is central to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' method: by demonstrating that the biblical vocabulary of descent is already metaphorical when applied to revelation, he prepares the reader for his larger claim that all biblical language about [[God]]'s actions must be understood figuratively.

The relationship between revelation and Scripture is a delicate point for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]. He ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that many people err by taking the "books of revelation" — the prophetic writings and the Pentateuch — at face value, attributing literal attributes to [[god|God]]. In Chapter 52, he notes: "Ce qui a amené tout cela, c'est qu'on suivait le sens littéral des livres de la révélation" (052-004). Yet [[maimonides|Maimonides]] does not reject the revelatory status of Scripture; he insists instead that its true meaning must be recovered through [[esoteric-interpretation|allegorical interpretation]] guided by philosophical reason.

Revelation for [[maimonides|Maimonides]] is graded: the highest form is Mosaic [[prophets|prophecy]], which alone was purely [[wisdom|intellect]]ual with no imaginative mediation. Lower forms involve the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] and are expressed in the figurative language of dreams and visions. This gradation allows [[maimonides|Maimonides]] to affirm the uniqueness of the [[law|Torah]]'s revelation — given through [[Moses]], whose [[wisdom|intellect]] received the [[emanation|divine overflow]] directly — while acknowledging the authenticity of the prophetic books, whose content was mediated through the [[imagination]] (055-007).
