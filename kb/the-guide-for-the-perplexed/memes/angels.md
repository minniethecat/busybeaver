## Definition

In the Guide for the Perplexed, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] radically reinterprets the biblical concept of angels (*[[evil|mal]]'akhim*, messengers) through the lens of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian metaphysics. Angels are not winged beings or anthropomorphic messengers, but **separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s** — purely [[wisdom|intellect]]ual entities that exist without matter or corporeal form (049-002).

This identification is one of the most characteristic moves in Maimonidean thought: he reads Scripture as encoding [[truth|philosophical truth]]s, so that the "angels of [[god|God]]" who appear in biblical narratives are, in reality, references to the cosmic [[intellect|intelligence]]s that govern the celestial spheres (006-001).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] develops his angelology most systematically in Part II, Chapter 6, where he equates angels with the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s and identifies them with the "Elohim" mentioned in Scripture (032-006).

He argues that the Hebrew word *Elohim* is equivocal, sometimes referring to [[god|God]], sometimes to angels, and sometimes to human judges. Biblical pas[[wisdom|sage]]s mentioning "the angels of [[god|God]]" should be understood as referring to these cosmic [[wisdom|intellect]]s.

In Part I, Chapter 49, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses the scriptural imagery that attributes wings and flight to angels. He explains that the Hebrew verb for flight (*canaph*) when applied to angels denotes concealment or veiling, consistent with their incorporeal nature (049-002, 049-003).

He further clarifies that angels do not eat, drink, or possess bodies, rejecting the anthropomorphic conceptions held by some contemporaries (044-010).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s this rationalist approach to biblical angelophanies. When angels appear in human form, as in the account of [[abraham|Abraham]]'s visitors, he explains these as prophetic visions rather than physical encounters (051-001, 051-002).

## Interpretation

Maimonidean angelology represents a deliberate synthesis of biblical tradition and Greco-Arabic philosophy. By equating angels with the separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s of [[aristotle|Aristotle]], mediated through [[alfarabi|Alfarabi]] and [[avicenna|Avicenna]], he preserves the scriptural language while draining it of anthropomorphic content.

This approach allows [[maimonides|Maimonides]] to argue that Scripture itself, properly understood, teaches the immateriality of these cosmic intermediaries. The angels thus become a bridge between the transc[[final-cause|end]]ent [[god|God]] and the material [[sublunar-world|world of generation and corruption]].
