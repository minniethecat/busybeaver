## Definition

Ethics (French: *la morale*, Hebrew: *middōt*) in the Guide for the Perplexed refers to the cultivation of moral virtues as a necessary preparation for [[wisdom|intellect]]ual and spiritual perfection. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes between two kinds of human perfections: moral virtues (*vertus morales*), which govern the soul's appetitive and irascible faculties, and rational virtues (*vertus rationnelles*), which consist in the acquisition of true intelligible notions (044-001). The moral virtues are not an [[final-cause|end]] in themselves but a prerequisite for the higher life of the [[wisdom|intellect]].

This preparatory conception of ethics is deeply [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows the Nicomachean Ethics in treating the moral virtues as dispositions of the soul that are acquired through habituation and that enable the rational faculty to operate without the disturbance of excessive passions. However, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] gives this [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian framework a distinctive theological inflection by embedding it within his account of the human relationship to [[god|God]].

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses ethics most extensively in Part I, Chapter 34, and Part III, Chapters 51–54. In Chapter 34, he argues that [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection "is only possible for a man who has well disciplined his manners and who is calm and composed" (044-001). He illustrates this with the example of a person whose heart is "extremely hot" by nature — such a person cannot refrain from violence even with the greatest effort, and is therefore constitutionally incapable of the tranquility required for philosophical contemplation (044-001).

The moral preparation ext[[final-cause|end]]s beyond the individual to the pedagogical context: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites Proverbs to argue that it is improper to teach metaphysical truths to young people, precisely be[[causality|cause]] they have not yet undergone the necessary moral formation (044-002). One must first acquire "rectitude and extreme perfection" of character, for "the Eternal holds in abomination the devious, and His secret is with those who are upright" (044-002).

> Celui qui, de nature, a le cœur extrêmement chaud ne peut s'empêcher d'être violent, quand même il ferait les plus grands efforts sur lui-même.
> (044-001)

In Chapter 53, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] analyzes the language of moral attributes as applied to [[god|God]] in Scripture. He notes that terms like "compassionate," "merciful," and "gracious" designate, in human beings, dispositions of the soul — moral qualities that are accidents inhering in a subject. But since [[god|God]] has no soul, no accidents, and no acquired dispositions, these terms cannot be predicated of [[god|God]] in their ordinary sense. They must instead be understood as attributes of action: descriptions of the effects [[god|God]] produces in the world, not of His essence (057-002).

The ethical dimension of the Guide culminates in the discussion of the Divine Name in Chapter 62, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] reports the rabbinic tradition that the Name of forty-two letters is transmitted only to "the pious man in middle age, who does not become angry, nor drunk, nor persists in his bad habits, and who speaks gently to creatures" (066-007). The connection between moral discipline and esoteric [[wisdom|knowledge]] is not incidental: moral purification is the condition of possibility for [[wisdom|intellect]]ual illumination.

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' subordination of ethics to [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection has been a subject of scholarly debate. Some see in this a devaluation of the moral life — ethics reduced to mere means. Others argue that [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' position is more nuanced: while [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection is the highest human [[final-cause|end]], moral virtue is not merely instrumental but intrinsically connected to the [[wisdom|knowledge]] of [[god|God]], since the moral attributes of [[god|God]] (as actions) are precisely what humans are called to imitate. This "imitation of [[god|God]]" through moral action constitutes a distinct and valuable form of human perfection, even if it ranks below the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual apprehension of divine truth.
