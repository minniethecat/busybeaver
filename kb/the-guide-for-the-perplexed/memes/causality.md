## Definition

Causality (Arabic: *sabab*, French: *cause*) is the principle that every effect proceeds from a cause in a necessary and intelligible relationship. In the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian framework that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts in the *Guide for the Perplexed*, causality is structured through four modes: the efficient cause (that which brings something into being), the material cause (that out of which something is made), the for[[evil|mal]] cause (the essence or definition of a thing), and the final cause (the [[final-cause|purpose]] for which something exists). Together, these four causes provide a complete explanation of any being.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of causality is central to his philosophical theology. He identifies [[god|God]] as the "first cause" (*cause première*), the ultimate efficient cause from which all existence derives (P02-005). Unlike the [[kalam|Kalam]] theologians who deny natural causation to preserve divine omnipotence, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] affirms that [[god|God]]'s causality operates through the stable natures He has established in creation. To deny causality, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues, is to deny the intelligibility of the world and to make [[knowledge|science]] impossible (069-012).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most developed discussion of causality appears in the context of his critique of the [[kalam|Kalam]] (Part I, Chapters 69–70). The *[[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]]*, he reports, refuse to call [[god|God]] a "cause," preferring the term "agent" (*fāʿil*), because they believe that a cause must coexist with its effect and this would imply the world's eternity (069-012). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] dismisses this as a merely verbal distinction. The four Aristotelian causes, he argues, all apply to [[god|God]]: He is the efficient cause of the world's existence, the for[[evil|mal]] cause of its intelligible structure, and the final cause toward which all things t[[final-cause|end]].

> Les philosophes appellent Dieu la cause première.
> (P02-005)

The question of causality is also tied to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' discussion of the causes of contradiction and error in human [[wisdom|understanding]]. In the Introduction, he enumerates seven causes that lead authors to contradict themselves or to produce obscure texts (P02-001, P02-003). More fundamentally, in Chapter 9 he identifies five causes of contradiction and opposition in philosophical works, ranging from the author's own changes of mind to the necessities of pedagogical concealment (009-002). This meta-reflection on the causes of error serves as a hermeneutical key to the Guide itself: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] warns his reader that apparent contradictions in his own text are not accidental but result from one of these identifiable causes (009-003).

The causality of natural processes is affirmed throughout the Guide. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies matter as "the cause of all" imperfection and privation in the sublunar world (006-001). This material causality explains why physical beings are subject to generation and corruption, while the separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s, being pure form, are not.
