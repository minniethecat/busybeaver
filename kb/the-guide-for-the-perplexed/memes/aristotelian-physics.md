## Definition

[[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics (French: *la [[knowledge|science]] physique*) is the philosophical study of natural beings insofar as they are subject to motion and change. In the medieval curriculum that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] inherits, physics occupies the middle position between the mathematical [[knowledge|science]]s and metaphysics, providing the principles — concerning matter, form, motion, and causality — upon which metaphysical inquiry dep[[final-cause|end]]s. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explicitly identifies [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics with the esoteric subject matter of the *[[maase-bereschith|Maaseh Bereshit]]h*, the biblical "[[maase-bereschith|Account of Creation]]" (002-003).

This identification is hermeneutically significant: it means that for [[maimonides|Maimonides]], the opening chapters of Genesis are not a cosmology in the mythological sense but a veiled exposition of natural philosophy. The "[[esoteric-interpretation|secrets of the Torah]]" concerning creation are, in his view, the truths of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics expressed in the symbolic language of Scripture.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] introduces the distinction between physics and metaphysics early in the Guide, informing his reader that the *[[maase-bereschith|Maaseh Bereshit]]h* ([[maase-bereschith|Account of Creation]]) corresponds to natural [[knowledge|science]], while the *Maaseh Mercabah* ([[maase-mercaba|Account of the Chariot]]) corresponds to metaphysics (002-003). This interpretive key governs the entire work: pas[[wisdom|sage]]s of Scripture that appear to describe the natural world are to be understood as allusions to physical principles, just as pas[[wisdom|sage]]s describing the divine throne-chariot allude to metaphysical truths.

The preparatory role of physics is emphasized in Part I, Chapter 34, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] outlines the curriculum that the aspiring metaphysician must follow. After mastering logic and mathematics, the student must study "the mathematical [[knowledge|science]]s, then gradually the physical [[knowledge|science]]s, and after that metaphysics" (041-001). This pedagogical order is not arbitrary: the principles of physics — concerning bodies, motion, and the celestial spheres — provide the premises from which metaphysical demonstrations proceed. One cannot demonstrate the existence of a first mover without first [[wisdom|understanding]] motion; one cannot prove [[incorporeality|divine incorporeality]] without first grasping the nature of bodies.

> Le Maasé beréschîth est la science physique et le Maasé mercabâ la science métaphysique.
> (002-003)

The table of contents of the Guide (preserved in the French edition's preface) reflects this structure: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes extensive chapters to the physics of the celestial spheres, the nature of matter and form, and the principles of motion, all of which belong to the domain of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics as understood in the twelfth century (P02-002). These discussions are not digressions but integral to the work's theological argument, since many of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' proofs for [[god|God]]'s existence and unity dep[[final-cause|end]] on physical premises about the structure of the cosmos.

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' identification of Genesis with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics has been controversial from his own time to the present. Critics have argued that it reduces the biblical narrative to a mere allegory for Greek philosophy; def[[final-cause|end]]ers respond that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] saw no contradiction between the two, believing that [[moses|Moses]] and [[aristotle|Aristotle]] described the same reality in different idioms. What is clear is that for [[maimonides|Maimonides]], the study of physics was not an optional [[wisdom|intellect]]ual luxury but a religious obligation, since one cannot love and fear [[god|God]] without [[wisdom|understanding]] His works.
