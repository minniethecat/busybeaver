## Definition

The concept of atoms (Arabic: *al-juz' alladhī lā yatajazza'u*, "the part that cannot be divided") refers to indivisible particles that the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] (Islamic dialectical theologians) held to be the fundamental constituents of all physical bodies. According to this theory, all bodies are composed of atoms that are homogeneous in substance, and all diversity in the world arises from the various arrangements and accidents attached to these atoms. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses this doctrine as one of the key premises of the Kalām, which he systematically examines and refutes in the Guide (034-005).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents the atomic theory as part of his broader critique of [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] physics, which he regards as driven by theological apologetics rather than demonstrated truth. The atomism of the Kalām served their aim of proving the creation of the world ex nihilo: by positing that atoms and their accidents are continually re-created at each moment by divine will, they sought to establish [[god|God]]'s ongoing creative act and the radical contingency of the natural order (051-009).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes significant attention to atomic theory primarily in Part I, Chapters 73–76, where he enumerates and refutes the twelve premises of the Kalām. The premise of atoms is among the first he addresses. He notes that the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] believed atoms to be simple substances that occupy no space, yet through aggregation they form ext[[final-cause|end]]ed bodies. "La [[substance|substance simple]] (l'atome) n'est pas dans un espace" — the simple substance (the atom) is not in space — a p[[accident|arad]]ox [[maimonides|Maimonides]] finds incoherent (051-011).

The discussion ext[[final-cause|end]]s into Part II, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses theories of matter and creation. He contrasts the atomic theory with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian [[matter-and-form|hylomorphism]], in which bodies are continuous and infinitely divisible. For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian framework better accounts for the observed regularities of nature. He notes that the atomists were forced to deny many observable phenomena — such as the mathematical properties of geometry — be[[causality|cause]] these contradicted their atomic premises (070-019).

> Les Mutakallimun affirment que la substance du monde est composée d'atomes indivisibles, et que la génération et la corruption ne sont que la réunion et la séparation de ces atomes.
> (071-033)

A particularly revealing pas[[wisdom|sage]] appears at (052-001), where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] connects the atomic theory to the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]]'s conception of macān (spatial position), showing how their physics dep[[final-cause|end]]s on a chain of mutually reinforcing but unproven premises. The atom, conceived as a [[substance|substance simple]], is said to be either ext[[final-cause|end]]ed or non-ext[[final-cause|end]]ed — but both alternatives lead to contradictions, demonstrating for [[maimonides|Maimonides]] the weakness of the entire Kalām physics.

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of atoms must be understood in the context of his larger project: distinguishing demonstrated [[truth|philosophical truth]] from merely conventional or apologetic doctrines. He does not reject atomism be[[causality|cause]] it contradicts Scripture but be[[causality|cause]] it fails the test of rational demonstration. This reflects his [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian commitment to the infinite divisibility of continuous magnitudes (071-034).

However, some scholars note that [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' own theory of creation — which ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s divine volition as the [[causality|cause]] of the world's existence — shares a structural similarity with the occasionalist implications of Kalām atomism. Both positions ultimately ground the existence and persistence of the world in divine will, even if they arrive there through very different philosophical routes (071-037).
