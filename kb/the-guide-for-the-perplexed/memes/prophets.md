## Definition

Prophets (French: *prophètes*, Hebrew: *neviim*) are, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, individuals who have attained the highest degree of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual and imaginative perfection such that they receive the [[emanation|divine overflow]] (*al-fayd al-ilāhī*) that constitutes prophetic [[revelation|inspiration]]. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] defines prophecy not as a supernatural suspension of natural law but as the culmination of human cognitive and moral development — a natural perfection that [[god|God]] may, by an act of will, withhold from someone otherwise qualified (032-002).

This naturalistic account is one of the most distinctive features of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' philosophy. [[prophecy|Prophecy]] requires the perfection of three faculties: the rational faculty (which grasps intelligible truths), the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] (which translates abstract truths into sensory images), and the moral faculty (which restrains bodily desires). When all three are perfected and the [[emanation|divine overflow]] desc[[final-cause|end]]s upon them, the result is prophecy.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes the bulk of the *Guide* Part II, Chapters 32–48, to prophecy — the longest sustained treatment of any single topic in the work. He opens this section by distinguishing three opinions concerning prophecy: the popular view ([[god|God]] chooses anyone He wills), the philosophical view (prophecy is a natural perfection), and the view of the [[law|Law]] (which agrees with the philosophical view but adds that [[god|God]] may withhold prophecy by an act of will). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents the third as the true position of Judaism.

The most crucial distinction [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws is between [[Moses]] and all other prophets. [[moses|Moses]] alone received prophecy without the mediation of the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] — his prophecy was purely [[wisdom|intellect]]ual, which is why the [[law|Law]] he delivered is eternal and immutable. All other prophets, by contrast, received prophecy through the [[imagination]], which is why their visions are expressed in figurative and symbolic language (033-002).

> Et l'esprit retourne vers Dieu qui l'a donné. Il désigne encore l'inspiration d'intelligence divine qui émane (de Dieu) sur les prophètes, et par laquelle ils prophétisent.
> (047-001)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] further analyzes the eleven degrees of prophecy enumerated in Scripture, distinguishing the "spirit of the Lord" (*ruaḥ ha-kodesh*), dreams, visions, and the direct speech that characterizes Mosaic prophecy (047-001). He explains that prophetic visions often use anthropomorphic imagery not be[[causality|cause]] [[god|God]] has a body but be[[causality|cause]] the [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] must clothe abstract truths in sensory form — the same reason the [[law|Torah]] speaks in human language (049-002).

A critical implication of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' theory is that false prophets can arise when a person with a strong [[imagination]] but an imperfect [[wisdom|intellect]] claims divine [[revelation|inspiration]]. The test of a true prophet is both [[wisdom|intellect]]ual (the content of the prophecy must be consistent with reason) and practical (predictions must come true), though [[maimonides|Maimonides]] subordinates the latter to the former (050-003).
