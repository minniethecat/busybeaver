## Definition

Divine unity (French: *unité divine*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' philosophical theology, the doctrine that [[god|God]] is one in a sense that excludes not only polytheism but also any internal composition, multiplicity of attributes, or corporeal form. This is stronger than mere numerical oneness: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that [[god|God]]'s unity is a "true unity" (*unité véritable*) that can only be established by demonstrating that [[god|God]] has no body, no parts, and no essential attributes distinct from His essence (013-001).

The doctrine of divine unity is one of the fundamental dogmas that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies as obligatory for all [[israel|Israelites]]. In the Guide, he treats it not merely as a revealed truth to be accepted on authority but as a rationally demonstrable proposition: the rejection of corporeality logically entails the affirmation of true unity, since any composite being is necessarily corporeal or quasi-corporeal in its ontological structure.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of divine unity spans much of Part I of the Guide. After establishing in the early chapters that Scriptural terms suggesting divine corporeality must be interpreted allegorically, he turns in Chapters 50–60 to the systematic exclusion of all attributes from [[god|God]]. Chapter 51 is particularly important: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that even believing in [[god|God]]'s unity while simultaneously affirming essential attributes is incoherent — one who says "[[god|God]] is one, living, knowing, powerful" has not affirmed unity but has enumerated a plurality (051-006).

> Quant à ce qui doit être dit pour écarter la corporéité et établir l'unité véritable — qui n'a de réalité que par l'exclusion de la corporéité — tu sauras la démonstration de tout cela par le présent traité.
> (013-001)

The table of contents describes the Guide's treatment of divine unity as one of the four fundamental dogmas that [[kalam|Kalam]] methods are "powerless to demonstrate": "l'existence, l'unité et l'incorporalité de [[god|Dieu]], et la [[creation-of-the-world|création du monde]]" (P02-006). [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' ambition is to show that these dogmas are not only true but demonstrable by philosophical means, and the demonstration of divine unity proceeds primarily through the refutation of divine attributes.

In Chapter 44, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] embeds the discussion of unity within his broader pedagogical framework. The [[ethics|moral virtues]] — what he calls the "preparatory moral dispositions" — must be cultivated before one can properly grasp the concept of true unity, be[[causality|cause]] the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual act required to conceive of a being without parts or attributes demands a mind freed from the distractions of passion and appetite (044-002).

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' conception of divine unity draws on both [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian and Neoplatonic sources. From [[aristotle|Aristotle]], he takes the argument that [[god|God]], as the first mover, must be pure actuality without any potentiality — and therefore without composition, since composition implies a distinction between what a thing is and what it could be. From the Neoplatonic tradition (mediated by [[alfarabi|Al-Farabi]] and [[avicenna|Avicenna]]), he takes the more radical conclusion that [[god|God]]'s unity is so absolute that it cannot be captured by the predicate "one" in any ordinary sense of the term. This convergence of Greek and Islamic philosophical traditions produces a conception of divine unity that is among the most rigorous in medieval monotheism.
