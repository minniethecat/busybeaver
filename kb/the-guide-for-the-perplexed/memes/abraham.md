## Overview

Abraham (originally Abram), the biblical patriarch, is presented by [[maimonides|Maimonides]] as the p[[accident|arad]]igmatic figure of rational monotheism. He is the first person in the biblical narrative to recognize the unity of [[god|God]] through philosophical inquiry rather than prophetic revelation, and he serves throughout the *Guide* as a model of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection and prophetic vision. (066-011)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses Abraham in several key contexts. In *Guide* I:63, Abraham is described as the first monotheist who arrived at the [[wisdom|knowledge]] of [[god|God]] through meditation and rational demonstration — prior to any claim of prophetic mission. Unlike later prophets such as [[moses|Moses]], Abraham did not present himself as a divinely appointed messenger to the people. Instead, he and Sarah instructed individuals through teaching and persuasion, as indicated by the biblical phrase "the souls they had made in Haran" (Genesis 12:5). (066-011) (066-012)

Abraham's prophetic visions serve as important examples in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' theory of [[prophets|prophecy]]. The vision of the "smoking furnace and a flaming torch" in the covenant between the pieces (Genesis 15) is cited as a p[[accident|arad]]igmatic instance of prophetic vision, demonstrating that apparently physical theophanies occurred within the prophetic [[imagination]] rather than in external reality. (030-002) The repetition "Abraham! Abraham!" in the binding of [[isaac|Isaac]] narrative is compared to "[[moses|Moses]]! [[moses|Moses]]!" as an example of the prophetic mode of address, where doubling indicates intimacy and urgency. (030-001)

> Abraham arrived at the knowledge of God not through inherited tradition or direct revelation, but through his own rational inquiry — meditating on the order of the universe until it was demonstrated to him that a single God presided over all. (066-011)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also uses Abraham to illustrate his method of interpreting anthropomorphic biblical language. When "Abraham approached" to intercede for Sodom, the term "approach" (nagasch) is explained as [[wisdom|intellect]]ual or spiritual nearness, not physical movement. (028-001) Similarly, the phrase "[[god|God]] went up from Abraham" is analyzed as accommodating human language rather than describing any spatial change in the divine. (024-001) Abraham is further invoked as the "rock" from which [[israel|Israel]] was hewn, a metaphor discussed in *Guide* I:16 to illustrate the idea of a source or principle. (026-004)
