## Definition

Substance (Greek: *ousia*, Arabic: *jawhar*, Hebrew: *etzem*) is the primary [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian category denoting that which exists in itself and not in another — the fundamental "what-it-is" of a thing. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] employs the concept of substance across several contexts: explaining [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s categories to his reader, analyzing the nature of the soul, distinguishing [[god|God]] from created beings, and critiquing the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]]'s [[atoms|atom]]ism (009-003).

For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], substance is the category that answers the question "what is it?" and stands in contrast to accidents, which exist only in a substance. This distinction is foundational to his metaphysics, particularly in discussions of divine attributes, where he argues that [[god|God]] cannot be said to have accidents, since these would imply composition and contingency (052-008).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] first introduces substance in the context of explaining [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s ten categories to his student Joseph. He notes that the category of substance was already explained by [[aristotle|Aristotle]] in the *[[aristotelian-categories|Categories]]*, and that [[wisdom|understanding]] this distinction is essential for grasping the discussions of divine attributes that follow (009-003). The Guide systematically applies the substance-accident distinction to theological problems throughout.

In his discussion of the soul and afterlife, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses the question of whether the [[wisdom|intellect]] persists after death as a separate substance. He cites the view that the acquired [[wisdom|intellect]] "existe après la mort comme une substance séparée" (exists after death as a separate substance), though he immediately raises philosophical difficulties with this position (040-003).

> Moïse, notre Maître, s'est borné à nommer cette connaissance, et c'est la faveur que Dieu t'a accordée, en te faisant connaître sa véritable substance en tant qu'il est souverain.
> (049-007)

The most sustained discussion of substance appears in the chapters on divine attributes (Part I, Chapters 50–60). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that if [[god|God]] had attributes, these would necessarily be accidents inhering in the divine substance, which would compromise divine unity and simplicity. The substance-accident distinction thus becomes a key tool in his [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]: [[god|God]] transc[[final-cause|end]]s the categories entirely and cannot be described by any attribute that would imply composition (052-009).

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' use of substance reflects his deep engagement with the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian tradition mediated through Arabic philosophers, particularly [[alfarabi|Al-Farabi]] and [[avicenna|Avicenna]]. However, his application of the concept to theology is distinctive. By insisting that [[god|God]] is not a substance in the categorical sense — since [[god|God]] is not a member of any genus — [[maimonides|Maimonides]] pushes [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian metaphysics to its limit, using its own categories to demonstrate their inadequacy for describing the divine (054-003).
