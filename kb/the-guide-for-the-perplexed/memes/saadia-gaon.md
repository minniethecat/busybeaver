## Overview

Saadia Gaon (Saadia ben Joseph) was the foremost Jewish philosopher and exegete of the Geonic period, serving as Gaon of the Sura Academy in Babylon. He authored the first systematic work of Jewish philosophy, "Croyances et Opinions" (Emunot ve-Deot), which adapted the methods of Mutazilite [[kalam|Kalam]] to def[[final-cause|end]] and rationalize Jewish theology. [[maimonides|Maimonides]], writing three centuries later, inherited the framework Saadia established — the project of reconciling revealed religion with rational philosophy — and the Guide frequently engages with Saadia's positions, whether through explicit reference or through the [[kalam|Kalam]] tradition Saadia helped introduce into Rabbanite Judaism. (067-005)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] consistently positions Saadia within the lineage of Jewish thinkers who adopted the methods of the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] (Islamic dialectical theologians). According to the editorial apparatus of the Guide, it was under the impulse of the Karaïtes, who had already taken up Mutazilite theology, that Saadia and other Rabbanite scholars first addressed questions of [[kalam|Kalam]]. (067-005) The Guide cites Saadia's "Croyances et Opinions" on multiple points: on the nature of divine speech, where Saadia held that [[god|God]] creates audible words that travel through the air to the prophet's ear (067-008); on the created light (the Shechinah) that [[god|God]] [[causality|cause]]s to desc[[final-cause|end]] as a sign of His presence (067-004); and on the doctrine of [[atoms|atom]]s, where Saadia's objections are noted in the context of the broader [[kalam|Kalam]] critique. (071-033)

> "Par parole (divine) il faut entendre que Dieu créait des paroles qui arrivaient a travers l'air a l'oreille du prophete ou du peuple." (067-008)

Saadia's Arabic translation of the Pentateuch is also noted in the Guide. Munk's edition records that Saadia r[[final-cause|end]]ered the Hebrew verb for "speaking" in Genesis 1 as "willed" ([[god|Dieu]] voulut), a theological interpretation that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] [[final-cause|end]]orses in his own discussion of divine speech as an expression of will rather than verbal utterance. (067-010) Similarly, Saadia's translation choices in the Pentateuch are invoked in the analysis of various Hebrew terms. (064-004)

## Significance

Saadia Gaon's "Croyances et Opinions" (Emunot ve-Deot) represents the first comprehensive attempt to systematize Jewish theology using the conceptual tools of [[kalam|Kalam]]. His method of demonstrating the harmony between reason and revelation established the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual program that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] would later perfect in the Guide. Although the Guide diverges from Saadia on key points — most notably by rejecting [[kalam|Kalam]] [[atoms|atom]]ism and adopting [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics — Saadia's pioneering synthesis of Jewish faith with philosophical inquiry made him an indispensable predecessor. The editor of the Guide consistently refers to Saadia's works and his "Notice sur Rabbi Saadia Gaon" as essential context for [[wisdom|understanding]] [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' arguments against the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]]. (001-003)
