## Overview

Onkelos (Onkelos the Proselyte) is the author of the standard Aramaic translation (Targum) of the [[law|Torah]]. A convert to Judaism who mastered both Hebrew and Aramaic, Onkelos produced a translation that systematically eliminates anthropomorphic language about [[god|God]] — r[[final-cause|end]]ering "[[god|God]] desc[[final-cause|end]]ed" as "the glory of the Lord was revealed," and "[[god|God]] heard" as "it was heard before the Lord." (032-004)

For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], Onkelos is not merely a translator but the primary model of correct scriptural interpretation. His Targum demonstrates that the rejection of divine corporeality is not a philosophical innovation but a traditional Jewish obligation, practiced by the authoritative interpreters of Scripture long before the rise of systematic theology. (045-001)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes sustained attention to Onkelos throughout Part I of the *Guide*. In chapters XXVII–XXVIII, he analyzes Onkelos' method: whenever Scripture uses an expression that could imply [[god|God]] has a body, moves in space, or possesses physical organs, Onkelos interprets it according to its true (non-corporeal) sense. Onkelos typically accomplishes this by positing an omitted word — not "the Lord passed" but "the glory of the Lord passed"; not "under his feet" but "under the throne of his glory." (032-005) (032-008)

In chapter XLVIII, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] provides the most detailed analysis, examining how Onkelos handles sensory verbs applied to [[god|God]]. Onkelos consistently replaces "[[god|God]] heard" with "it was heard before the Lord" and "[[god|God]] accepted the prayer," while for "[[god|God]] saw" he substitutes "it was manifest before the Lord" — particularly when the seeing would imply divine approval of injustice or violence. (050-013) [[maimonides|Maimonides]] even examines variant readings in Onkelos manuscripts and laments that no autograph survives. (050-016)

> Onkelos "made every effort to eliminate the corporealization of God" — Maimonides treats his Targum as the definitive proof that anthropomorphic language must be reinterpreted. (032-004)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] occasionally departs from Onkelos: in chapter LIV, he disagrees with Onkelos' interpretation of the verb *naqah* (to clear or purify), and in chapter LXVI, he expresses puzzlement that Onkelos r[[final-cause|end]]ered "written with the finger of [[god|God]]" literally, contradicting his usual anti-corporeal method. (057-005) (068-002) But these exceptions only underscore the rule: Onkelos is [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' exegetical north star.
