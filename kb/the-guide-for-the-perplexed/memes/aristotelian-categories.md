## Definition

The Aristotelian Categories (Greek: *katēgoriai*, French: *catégories*) are the ten fundamental classes or "predicaments" into which, according to [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s logical works, all things that exist can be classified: substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, and affection. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on the Categories as a methodological tool for clarifying how language applies — and does not apply — to [[god|God]], arguing that terms from different categories cannot be predicated of [[god|God]] and creatures in the same sense.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' interest in the Categories is not primarily logical but theological: he uses the categorical framework to demonstrate that when Scripture attributes to [[god|God]] terms drawn from the category of quality or relation, these attributions cannot be taken literally. Since [[god|God]] is not a substance that bears accidents, no predicate belonging to any [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian category can describe [[god|God]]'s essence (054-002). The categories thus serve as a negative theological instrument — mapping what cannot be said of [[god|God]].

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses the Categories most directly in Part I, Chapters 52–58, where he systematically examines the classes of attributes that may or may not be predicated of [[god|God]]. He identifies five classes of attributes, of which the first three correspond roughly to the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian categories of substance, quantity, and quality. The fourth class — relational attributes — receives particularly detailed treatment be[[causality|cause]] [[maimonides|Maimonides]] must explain how terms like "Creator" and "Lord," which appear to be relational, can be applied to [[god|God]] without implying a real relation in [[god|God]] himself (055-001).

In Chapter 55, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that even relational attributes are problematic when applied to [[god|God]], be[[causality|cause]] true relation requires that the two terms belong to the same proximate genus. Since [[god|God]] and creatures share no common category, no real relation can obtain between them. The apparent relations expressed in Scriptural language — [[god|God]] as "Lord of [[israel|Israel]]," "Creator of the world" — are not relations in the logical sense but accommodations to human [[wisdom|understanding]] (055-004).

> Ce sont là des assertions qui toutes se disent seulement, qui existent dans les paroles et non dans les esprits.
> (052-001)

In Chapter 58, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s this analysis to the category of quality. He notes that terms like "quality" (*qualité*) are applied broadly in philosophical discourse, encompassing dispositions, capacities, sensible qualities, and figures. None of these sub-categories, he argues, can apply to [[god|God]], who is not a qualified subject (058-003). The systematic exclusion of categorical predication is central to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[divine-attributes|negative theology]].

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' use of the Categories reflects his broader strategy of employing Aristotelian logical tools to dismantle anthropomorphic conceptions of [[god|God]]. By showing that divine attributes cannot fit into any [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian category, he prepares the ground for his doctrine that [[god|God]] can only be described through attributes of action — the effects of divine causality in the world — rather than through essential attributes that would imply composition in [[god|God]]. This approach is consistent with that of Islamic [[aristotle|Aristote]]lians like [[alfarabi|Al-Farabi]] and [[avicenna|Avicenna]], who also employed the Categories in their discussions of [[divine-unity|divine simplicity]], though [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment is distinctive in its sustained focus on Scriptural vocabulary.
