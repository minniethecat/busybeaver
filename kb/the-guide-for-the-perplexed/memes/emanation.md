## Definition

Emanation (Hebrew: *shefaʿ*, Arabic: *fayḍ*, French: *émanation* or *épanchement*) is the Neoplatonic doctrine according to which being, form, and [[wisdom|intellect]] proceed from [[god|God]] not through a temporally distinct act of creation but through a continuous and necessary overflow, like light from the sun or heat from fire. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts the vocabulary of emanation extensively in the Guide, using it to explain the relationship between [[god|God]] and the world in a way that preserves divine transc[[final-cause|end]]ence while accounting for the world's ongoing dep[[final-cause|end]]ence on its [[causality|first cause]].

The term shefaʿ, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains, literally means "overflow" or "outpouring," and is used to designate the divine action that gives things their duration and harmony through wise governance rather than through any physical contact or change in [[god|God]] (060-002). Emanation thus serves as a middle path between the [[kalam|Kalam]]'s conception of [[god|God]] as a temporally prior "agent" who creates at discrete moments, and the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian conception of an eternal world that is co-existent with its [[causality|first cause]].

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] introduces the concept of emanation in Part I, Chapter 58, where he discusses how divine attributes like power, [[wisdom|knowledge]], and will are to be understood. He insists that these attributes do not imply multiplicity in [[god|God]]'s essence; rather, they describe the single divine action by which existence is communicated to all beings. This action is not like the sun's light or fire's heat, which are physical emanations, but "une action divine" — a divine action that is wholly unlike any created causality (060-002).

> Le mot shefaʿ désigne l'épanchement de la science qui provient de Dieu et se communique aux êtres.
> (060-002)

In Part I, Chapter 69, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] develops the emanation framework further by arguing that [[god|God]] is simultaneously the efficient [[causality|cause]], the for[[evil|mal]] [[causality|cause]], and the final [[causality|cause]] of the world. The for[[evil|mal]] causality of [[god|God]] — the sense in which [[god|God]] is the "form" of the universe — is understood through the model of emanation: just as form gives being to matter, so [[god|God]]'s continuous overflow gives being to the entire cosmos (069-019). This does not mean that [[god|God]] is a form inhering in a body, but that the relationship of existential dep[[final-cause|end]]ence between the world and [[god|God]] is analogous to the relationship between matter and form.

The doctrine of emanation also provides the framework for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' account of [[prophets|prophecy]] and [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection. The divine overflow that sustains the celestial spheres ext[[final-cause|end]]s downward to the human [[wisdom|intellect]]: the [[active-intellect|Active Intellect]] is the channel through which the emanation reaches the rational faculty, and [[prophets|prophecy]] is the highest degree of this [[wisdom|intellect]]ual reception (075-002).

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' use of emanationist language has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. Some read him as a full-fledged Neoplatonist who accepts emanation as a metaphysical reality; others argue that he employs emanationist vocabulary merely as a façon de parler, never committing to the ontological indep[[final-cause|end]]ence of intermediary [[causality|cause]]s. The Guide's deliberate ambiguity on this point may reflect [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' characteristic strategy of accommodating both philosophical and traditional readers within the same text.
