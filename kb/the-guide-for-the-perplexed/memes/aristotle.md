## Overview

Aristotle (Aristote) is the most frequently cited philosopher in the Guide. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] refers to him simply as "the Philosopher" and treats his works — particularly the Physics, Metaphysics, and De Anima — as the definitive expression of rational truth attainable without revelation. (P01-024)

For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], Aristotle represents the pinnacle of human reason unaided by [[prophets|prophecy]]. The Guide's project can be understood as demonstrating that Aristotelian philosophy, correctly interpreted, not only does not contradict the [[law|Torah]] but actually illuminates its deepest meanings. (001-004)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts Aristotle's cosmological framework — the celestial spheres, the [[active-intellect|Active Intellect]], the distinction between matter and form — while departing from Aristotle on two crucial points: creation ex nihilo (versus the eternity of the world) and the scope of [[providence|divine providence]] (ext[[final-cause|end]]ing to individuals, not just species). (016-001) (016-002)

The Guide frequently cites Aristotle's logical works (the [[logic|Organon]]), his Physics, and his Metaphysics. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] uses Aristotelian categories and [[logic|syllogistic]] reasoning throughout, treating them as the universal language of rational discourse. (019-002) (024-003)

> Maimonides drew upon the Arabic translations of Aristotle's works, which were the intellectual backbone of medieval philosophy from Baghdad to Cordoba to Cairo.

## Significance

Aristotle's influence on [[maimonides|Maimonides]] is pervasive but not uncritical. The Guide consistently evaluates Aristotelian claims against biblical truth: where they align (incorporeality of [[god|God]], structure of the cosmos), [[maimonides|Maimonides]] embraces Aristotle; where they conflict (eternity of the world), he argues that Aristotle himself ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]d his proofs were merely probable. (027-001)

This critical synthesis — accepting Aristotle's method while reserving judgment on his conclusions where revelation demands — became the model for subsequent Jewish, Christian, and Islamic philosophical theology.
