## Definition

Esoteric interpretation (Hebrew: *sitrei torah*, "secrets of the Torah") is the practice of decoding and transmitting the concealed philosophical content of Scripture that lies beneath its literal surface. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents this esoteric tradition as an integral part of Jewish [[wisdom|intellect]]ual heritage, deliberately excluded from written transmission and reserved for oral instruction to qualified individuals. In the Introduction to the Guide, he identifies the explanation of these hidden meanings as one of the two principal aims of his work.

The esoteric dimension of Scripture is not, for [[maimonides|Maimonides]], an optional supplement to the literal text but its true philosophical core. The [[law|Torah]] addresses two audiences simultaneously: the multitude, who receive the narrative and legal surface, and the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual elite, who are capable of perceiving the philosophical doctrines encoded beneath. The esoteric meanings are called "mysteries and secrets of the Torah" precisely be[[causality|cause]] they are inaccessible to untrained minds and require systematic preparation before they can be properly understood (039-001).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses the institutional framework of esoteric transmission in Part I, Chapter 34, drawing on Talmudic sources. He cites the rabbinic teaching that the Account of the Beginning (*[[maase-bereschith|Ma'aseh Bereshit]]*) may be taught only to a single disciple, and the [[maase-mercaba|Account of the Chariot]] (*Ma'aseh Merkavah*) only to a single qualified elder — restrictions designed to prevent the public dissemination of doctrines that could [[causality|cause]] [[wisdom|intellect]]ual harm if misunderstood (044-004).

The content of the esoteric tradition encompasses what [[maimonides|Maimonides]] calls the true [[knowledge|science]] of the [[law|Law]]: the nature of [[god|God]], the manner of creation, divine governance and providence, the nature of [[prophets|prophecy]] and its degrees, and the meaning of divine will, perception, and [[wisdom|knowledge]] (044-007). These topics correspond precisely to the central subjects of the Guide itself, confirming that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] understands his own work as an attempt to restore, in carefully controlled written form, a tradition of [[wisdom|knowledge]] that had been lost through historical disruption.

> On ne transmet les secrets de la Torah qu'à un homme de conseil, savant penseur.
> (070-012)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that the esoteric tradition was deliberately excluded from written codification. The Talmudic [[wisdom|sage]]s transmitted it orally, and the written record contains only hints and allusions that the initiated can assemble into a coherent doctrine. The Guide itself participates in this esoteric practice: its deliberate contradictions, its scattered treatment of topics, and its alternating clarity and obscurity are all literary devices designed to preserve the oral character of the tradition even in writing (070-011).

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' decision to write the Guide at all represents a departure from the strict esotericism he describes. He justifies this breach on the grounds of historical necessity: the chain of oral transmission had been broken, and the fundamental principles of Jewish theology were in danger of being lost entirely. The Guide is thus a compromise — a written work that strives to remain esoteric in its method even as it discloses esoteric content. This tension between concealment and revelation pervades the entire treatise and accounts for many of its structural peculiarities.
