## Definition

Demonstration (Greek: *apodeixis*, Arabic: *burhān*, French: *démonstration*) is, in the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian tradition that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts, the form of reasoning that proceeds from true and necessary premises to a certain conclusion. It contrasts with dialectic, which argues from merely probable or generally accepted premises, and with rhetoric, which persuades without proving. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] consistently invokes the standard of demonstration as the criterion that separates genuine philosophical [[wisdom|knowledge]] from the merely verbal assertions of the [[kalam|Kalam]] theologians.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies the absence of demonstrative rigor as the fundamental flaw in the methods of the *[[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]]*. Their arguments, he cont[[final-cause|end]]s, begin not from the nature of being but from the desired theological conclusion; they then construct premises — often physically impossible ones — to support that conclusion (001-003). This inversion of the demonstrative order distinguishes [[truth|philosophical truth]]-seeking from apologetic defense of doctrine.

## In the Guide

The question of what can and cannot be demonstrated is central to the Guide's architecture. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] states at the outset that his pupil's request was to understand the methods of the [[kalam|Kalam]] and whether they were "based on demonstration, or, in the contrary case, to what art they belonged" (001-003). This framing sets up the entire work as an inquiry into the scope and limits of demonstrative reasoning in theological matters.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] is careful to delineate which metaphysical questions admit of demonstration and which do not. In discussing the eternity of the world versus creation *ex nihilo*, he observes that neither position can be strictly demonstrated from physical premises — a rare instance where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the limits of demonstrative reason (034-004). He warns that the desire to find demonstrations for everything leads to [[wisdom|intellect]]ual overreach: those who attempt demonstrative proofs for non-demonstrable matters [[final-cause|end]] up doubting even what they once knew (035-003).

> Il n'est point au pouvoir de l'intelligence humaine d'alléguer à cet égard une preuve démonstrative.
> (034-004)

The preparatory [[knowledge|science]]s — logic, mathematics, and physics — are described by [[maimonides|Maimonides]] as training in the art of demonstration, without which one cannot properly approach metaphysics (041-001). The student who reaches metaphysical study without having first mastered demonstrative reasoning is like someone who attempts to read before learning the alphabet. In Chapter 39, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents the perfected individual as one who can recognize truth "by demonstration, when it is possible, or by solid argumentation, when that means is practicable" (039-002).

## Interpretation

Scholars have debated the extent to which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself believed that the central doctrines of Judaism could be demonstrated. His nuanced position seems to be that certain truths — the existence and unity of [[god|God]], the incorporeality of the deity — are demonstrable, while others — creation *ex nihilo*, the specifics of [[prophets|prophecy]] — rest on a combination of probable argument and prophetic tradition. This careful demarcation of the demonstrable from the non-demonstrable reflects [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' commitment to [[wisdom|intellect]]ual honesty: one should not claim certainty where only probability is attainable.
