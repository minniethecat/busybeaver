## Definition

Allegory (Hebrew: *mashal*, Arabic: *mathal*, French: *allégorie*) is the central hermeneutical method of the *Guide for the Perplexed*, by which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] decodes the hidden philosophical meanings beneath the literal surface of prophetic and biblical texts. In the Introduction to the Guide, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explicitly declares that one of the two [[final-cause|purpose]]s of his work is "l'explication des allégories très obscures qu'on rencontre dans les livres des [[prophets|prophètes]]" — the explanation of the very obscure allegories encountered in the books of the prophets (002-002).

Allegory for [[maimonides|Maimonides]] is not merely a literary device but an epistemological necessity. The truths of philosophy and the truths of Scripture are identical in substance; allegory is the form in which Scripture communicates [[truth|philosophical truth]]s to a general audience while reserving their deeper meaning for the [[wisdom|intellect]]ually qualified. The prophetic books speak "par des allégories et des énigmes" (through allegories and enigmas), and the task of the Guide is to provide the keys for their interpretation (002-006).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' method of [[esoteric-interpretation|allegorical interpretation]] is laid out programmatically in the Introduction. He distinguishes between different types of parabolic speech: some allegories are explicit and easily deciphered, while others are deliberately obscure, scattering clues across multiple pas[[wisdom|sage]]s such that only the attentive reader who "a reçu l'[[intellect|intelligence]]" (has received [[wisdom|understanding]]) can assemble the complete picture (003-001).

The Guide itself is structured as a sustained allegorical reading of Scripture. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that the prophets consistently used allegory to speak about [[god|God]], employing terms like "ascent," "[[revelation|descent]]," "seeing," and "hearing" metaphorically rather than literally. The "allégories é[[law|loi]]gnées les unes des autres" (allegories distant from one another) form a mosaic that the reader must piece together — a literary strategy that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explicitly adopts from the prophetic books themselves (003-002).

> C'est par allégorie qu'on lui donne certains attributs corporels, comme la face, les yeux, les mains, la droite, la gauche, la colère, la miséricorde.
> (P02-005)

In Part II, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] applies the allegorical method extensively to the account of creation, the story of [[adam|Adam]] and Eve, and the prophetic visions of Ezekiel and Isaiah. Each narrative is shown to contain philosophical teachings about cosmology, [[knowledge|epistemology]], or ethics encoded in figurative language. The [[esoteric-interpretation|allegorical interpretation]] of *[[maase-bereschith|Ma'aseh Bereshit]]* (the Account of the Beginning) and *Ma'aseh Merkavah* (the [[maase-mercaba|Account of the Chariot]]) constitutes the esoteric core of the Guide (P02-001).

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' allegorical method must be understood against the backdrop of medieval Jewish exegesis and its Islamic parallels. While Midrashic literature had long employed figurative interpretation, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] systematizes allegory as a rigorous philosophical instrument. His approach is closer to the allegorical methods of the Islamic *falāsifa* (philosophers) than to traditional rabbinic hermeneutics. The deliberate obscurity of the Guide's own composition — its non-linear structure, deliberate contradictions, and scattered treatment of topics — is itself an allegorical practice, training the reader in the very method the book expounds (003-003).
