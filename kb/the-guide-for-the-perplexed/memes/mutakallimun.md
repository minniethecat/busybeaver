## Definition

The Motécallemîn (Arabic: المتكلمون, *Mutakallimūn*), meaning "those who engage in discourse," are the practitioners of *Kalām*, the tradition of [[kalam|Islamic scholastic theology]]. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies them as thinkers who sought to def[[final-cause|end]] religious doctrine—particularly the creation of the world—through rational argumentation, but whose methods relied on premises chosen for their theological utility rather than their philosophical soundness (071-005). In the Guide, they serve as [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' primary foil for demonstrating the superiority of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian demonstration over dialectical reasoning driven by predetermined conclusions.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes a substantial portion of the Guide, particularly Part I Chapters 69 to 71, to a detailed exposition and critique of the Motécallemîn's system. He introduces them early as a distinct school whose fundamental principles require careful examination before his own arguments can be properly understood (P02-006).

In Chapter 71, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] outlines twelve general propositions that the Motécallemîn established as the foundation for their proofs of creation. These include the doctrine of [[atoms|atom]]s (*[[atoms|atome]]s du temps*), the denial of an actual infinite, and the principle that every possibility must at some point become actual (071-031). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] observes that the Motécallemîn were willing to accept absurd consequences—such as the denial of continuous motion—rather than abandon propositions that supported their theological conclusions (071-033).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also notes the historical transmission of Kalām methods. He suggests that the earliest Muslim Mutakallimūn may have drawn from earlier sources, and that their influence ext[[final-cause|end]]ed to Jewish thinkers in the Islamic East, as well as to figures such as [[solomon-ibn-gabirol|Solomon ibn Gabirol]] in Spain (070-016, 070-018). This historical framing serves [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' broader argument that the Motécallemîn's methodology, while widespread, does not constitute genuine philosophical demonstration.
