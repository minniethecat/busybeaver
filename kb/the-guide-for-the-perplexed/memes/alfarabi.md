## Overview

Abu Nasr al-Farabi (Alfarabi), known as the "Second Teacher" (after [[aristotle|Aristotle]], the First), was the foundational figure of Arabic Peripatetic philosophy. His works on logic, metaphysics, and political philosophy established the conceptual framework that [[maimonides|Maimonides]], [[avicenna|Avicenna]], and Averroes would later develop and contest. (P01-024)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] consistently treats Alfarabi as a philosophical authority, adopting his logical terminology, his classification of the [[knowledge|science]]s, and his integration of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian metaphysics with monotheistic theology. Unlike [[avicenna|Avicenna]], toward whom [[maimonides|Maimonides]] sometimes adopts a critical stance, Alfarabi is cited almost exclusively with approval throughout the *Guide*. (054-001)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on Alfarabi's logical works extensively, particularly his treatise on the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian categories. He notes that he uses "the same terms as Alfarabi and all the Arabic philosophers" (054-001), placing himself deliberately within the terminological tradition Alfarabi established. Alfarabi's analysis of the category of quality — dividing it into four intermediate classes — is cited in detail when [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains how predicates apply to [[god|God]]. (052-008)

Alfarabi serves as [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' primary ally in the sustained polemic against the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] (Islamic dialectical theologians). In chapter LXXII, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites Alfarabi's demonstration that the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] confound [[imagination]] with reason: they treat whatever can be imagined as rationally possible, a conflation Alfarabi exposed. (072-016) In chapter LXXIV, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] declares that "Abou-Naçr al-Farabi has already demolished" the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]]'s eleventh proposition — concerning the impossibility of an infinite series — in his work *On Changing Beings* (Kitāb al-Mawjūdāt al-Mutaghayyira). (074-019)

> Alfarabi's refutation of Mutakallimun propositions provided Maimonides with a ready-made arsenal for the *Guide*'s own anti-Kalam polemic.

## Significance

Alfarabi represents the moment when [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy was successfully naturalized within an Islamic [[wisdom|intellect]]ual framework — a synthesis that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] would replicate within Judaism. His political philosophy, which casts the prophet as the ideal philosopher-king who promulgates a perfect law, directly informs [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[wisdom|understanding]] of [[moses|Moses]] and the [[law|Torah]]. (054-001)
