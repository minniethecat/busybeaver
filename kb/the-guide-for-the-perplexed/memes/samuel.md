## Overview

Samuel (Shemuel, in Hebrew) is the prophet who anointed the first two kings of [[israel|Israel]] — Saul and [[david|David]] — and whose authority [[maimonides|Maimonides]] invokes in the Guide to illustrate the nature of divine will and the hierarchy of prophetic ranks. The biblical books of Samuel provide [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with key linguistic data for his analysis of anthropomorphic terms applied to [[god|God]], and Samuel himself appears as a figure whose prophetic stature is second only to that of [[moses|Moses]]. (025-003)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] references Samuel in two distinct contexts. First, in Part I, Chapter 41, during his analysis of the Hebrew word [[soul|néphesch]] (soul), he cites the verse from Jeremiah: "Quand [[moses|Moïse]] et Samuel se ti[[final-cause|end]]raient devant moi, mon [[soul|âme]] ne serait pas pour ce peuple" (Jeremiah 15:1). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that when [[soul|néphesch]] is attributed to [[god|God]], it signifies "will" or "intention" — not a soul or emotional faculty. Here, [[god|God]]'s [[soul|néphesch]] being "not for" the people means His will is not inclined toward preserving them, even if [[moses|Moses]] and Samuel — the two greatest intercessors — were to plead on their behalf. By pairing Samuel with [[moses|Moses]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] implicitly ratifies the rabbinic tradition that ranks Samuel as the greatest prophet after [[moses|Moses]]. (025-003)

> Toutes les fois que le mot néphesch est attribué à Dieu, il a le sens de volonté... Quand Moïse et Samuel se tiendraient devant moi, mon âme ne serait pas pour ce peuple (Jérémie, XV, 1), où le sens est: ma volonté ne serait pas pour eux, c'est-à-dire je ne voudrais point les conserver. (025-003)

The same verse is cited in Chapter 47, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] further elaborates on the meaning of [[soul|néphesch]] as divine will, drawing a parallel with the verse "Et sa [[free-will|volonté]] s'abstint d'affliger [[israel|Israël]]" (Judges 10:16). In this pas[[wisdom|sage]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] notes that [[jonathan-ben-uzziel|Jonathan ben Uzziel]] — the authoritative Aramaic translator — refrained from translating this verse literally be[[causality|cause]] a literal reading would attribute passion or passivity to [[god|God]]. This observation connects Samuel's prophetic context to the broader project of removing [[anthropomorphism]] from Scripture: the verses surrounding Samuel's role as intercessor must be interpreted through the lens of divine will, not divine emotion. (047-003)

The first book of Samuel also provides [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with linguistic evidence in his discussion of Hebrew homonyms. In Chapter 25, the verse "Quand [[moses|Moïse]] et Samuel se ti[[final-cause|end]]raient devant moi" serves as an early lexical anchor in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' sustained analysis of how biblical Hebrew uses the same words for human and divine subjects with fundamentally different meanings. (025-003)
