## Overview

Moses Maimonides (Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon, Rambam) is the author of the *Guide for the Perplexed* (Dalālat al-ḥāʾirīn), the foundational text upon which this wiki is built. Born in Cordoba in 1138, he fled the Almohad persecution and eventually settled in Fustat (Cairo), where he served as court physician to Saladin's family while composing his major philosophical and legal works. (P01-017)

Maimonides is unique in Jewish [[wisdom|intellect]]ual history for combining three roles: halakhic codifier (the *Mishneh [[law|Torah]]*), philosophical theologian (the *Guide*), and communal leader. His synthesis of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy with biblical revelation set the ag[[final-cause|end]]a for subsequent Jewish, Christian, and Islamic philosophical theology. (P01-024)

## In the Guide

Maimonides composed the *Guide* around 1190 in Judeo-Arabic for his disciple [[joseph-ben-judah|Joseph ben Judah]], who had studied logic, astronomy, and mathematics under his direction. The work aims to resolve the apparent conflicts between [[truth|philosophical truth]] and the literal sense of Scripture, guiding the "perplexed" reader toward a correct [[wisdom|understanding]] of both. (001-002)

Maimonides grounds his system in an [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian framework — the celestial spheres, the [[active-intellect|Active Intellect]], the distinction between matter and form — while departing from [[aristotle|Aristotle]] on two crucial points: creation ex nihilo (versus the eternity of the world) and the scope of [[providence|divine providence]]. His [[divine-attributes|negative theology]], which holds that no positive attributes can be predicated of [[god|God]]'s essence, remains one of the most influential doctrines in the history of philosophy. (061-003) (064-004)

> Maimonides wrote the Guide in Judeo-Arabic; it was translated into Hebrew by Samuel Ibn-Tibbon and into French by Salomon Munk (1856–1866). Munk's critical edition, based directly on the Arabic original, is the corpus used by this wiki.

## Significance

Maimonides' influence ext[[final-cause|end]]s far beyond Judaism. Thomas Aquinas and Albert the Great engaged his arguments in Latin translation; his [[divine-attributes|negative theology]] shaped subsequent discussions of divine attributes in all three [[abraham|Abraham]]ic traditions. His method of [[esoteric-interpretation|allegorical interpretation]] — treating anthropomorphic biblical language as figurative — provided a model for reconciling scripture with reason that remains relevant today. (013-001)

The *Guide*'s project can be understood as a single sustained argument: that the [[god|God]] of [[abraham|Abraham]] and the [[god|God]] of [[aristotle|Aristotle]] are the same, and that correct philosophical reasoning leads to the same truths that [[moses|Moses]] appreh[[final-cause|end]]ed through [[prophets|prophecy]].
