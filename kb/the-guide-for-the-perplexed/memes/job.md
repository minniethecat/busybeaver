## Overview

Job (Job, Iyov) is a biblical figure whose narrative of unexplained suffering, patient [[final-cause|end]]urance, and eventual restoration serves as a central case study for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of [[providence|divine providence]] and [[evil|theodicy]] in the Guide. The story of Job is discussed extensively in Part III, chapters 22–23, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets it as a parable about the true nature of providence — a doctrine that links divine care to [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection. In Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] also cites verses from Job philologically, employing them as examples in his analysis of Hebrew terms for touching, presence, and ceasing. (028-002)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most sustained treatment of Job occurs in Part III of the Guide, where he interprets the book of Job not as a literal historical account but as a philosophical parable about the problem of evil. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents five opinions on providence (the Epicurean, the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian, the Mu'tazilite, the Ash'arite, and the Mosaic view) and uses Job's story to demonstrate that the correct view — that providence is proportionate to [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection — resolves the apparent injustice of the righteous suffering.

In Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] uses verses from Job as evidence in his philological analysis of Hebrew terms. In Chapter 30, he examines the verb *naga* (to touch), citing Job 2:5: "Touch his person" (Et touche sa personne). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that when applied to [[god|God]], "touching" does not imply physical contact but rather means afflicting or causing harm, as in the command to bring down a scourge upon Job. (028-002)

> Et touche sa personne (Job, II, 5) signifient : Fais descendre ton fléau sur lui.
> (028-002)

In Chapter 47, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses the Hebrew word *panim* (face) and its meaning of "presence." He cites Job 1:11, where Satan says Job will "blaspheme you to your face" (s'il ne te blasphémera pas à ta face), explaining that "to your face" means "in your presence, you being there." This is part of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' broader argument that anthropomorphic terms applied to [[god|God]] must be understood metaphorically. (045-002)

In Chapter 67, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] discusses the verb *shavat* (to cease, to rest) and cites Job 32:1, where Job's three fri[[final-cause|end]]s "ceased to answer Job" (cessèrent de répondre à Job). He uses this to demonstrate that *shavat* can mean "to stop speaking" rather than physical rest, supporting his interpretation of [[god|God]]'s "rest" on the seventh day as the cessation of creation, not physical repose. (068-005)
