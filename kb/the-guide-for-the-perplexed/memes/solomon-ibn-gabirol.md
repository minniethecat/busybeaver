## Overview

Solomon ibn Gabirol (c. 1021–1058), known to the Latin Scholastics as Avicebron, was a Jewish Neoplatonic philosopher and poet born in Malaga, Spain. His major philosophical work, *Fons Vitae* (Source of Life), written in Arabic and preserved in Latin translation, develops a comprehensive Neoplatonic cosmology centered on the doctrine of universal [[matter-and-form|hylomorphism]] — the view that all created beings, including spiritual substances, are composed of matter and form. [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' French translator [[solomon|Salomon]] Munk references Ibn Gabirol's *Fons Vitae* in his notes to the *Guide*, citing it in discussions of metaphysical questions such as the essence/existence distinction and the macrocosm-microcosm analogy. (059-005) (070-016)

## In the Guide

Ibn Gabirol does not appear in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' own text; his presence in the *Guide* is entirely through the extensive footnotes of [[solomon|Salomon]] Munk, who draws on Ibn Gabirol's *Fons Vitae* as a key witness to the early flowering of Jewish philosophy in medieval Spain. Munk notes that Ibn Gabirol's work proves that as early as the eleventh century, before [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy had found a worthy representative among the Muslims of Spain, Jewish thinkers had already engaged successfully with the highest metaphysical questions. (070-016) This historical observation situates Ibn Gabirol as a precursor to the Peripatetic tradition that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] would later inherit.

Munk also cites Ibn Gabirol's *Fons Vitae* in connection with specific philosophical topics in the *Guide*. In his commentary on the distinction between essence and existence (the "thatness" versus "whatness" of a thing), Munk refers the reader to his own extracts from Ibn Gabirol's *Fons Vitae*, Book V, section 30. (059-005) Similarly, in discussing the doctrine of the universe as a single individual — the macrocosm-microcosm analogy that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] develops in Chapter LXXII — Munk adduces Ibn Gabirol's treatment of the same theme in Book III of the *Fons Vitae*. (071-010)

> "Salomon ibn-Gebirol, de Malaga, nous a laisse, dans sa Source de vie, un document important qui prouve que des le XI^e siecle, et avant que la philosophie peripateticienne eut trouve un digne representant parmi les musulmans d'Espagne, les juifs de ce pays s'etaient livres avec succes aux etudes philosophiques et avaient aborde les plus hautes questions metaphysiques." — Salomon Munk (070-016)

## Significance

Ibn Gabirol represents an important strand of Neoplatonic thought within medieval Jewish philosophy that predates the full reception of [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. His doctrine of universal [[matter-and-form|hylomorphism]] — the claim that matter underlies even spiritual realities — set him apart from both the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian tradition and from later Jewish philosophers such as [[maimonides|Maimonides]], who rejected this view. Although [[maimonides|Maimonides]] does not engage directly with Ibn Gabirol, Munk's footnotes reveal that Ibn Gabirol's *Fons Vitae* provides an essential comparative reference for [[wisdom|understanding]] the development of Jewish philosophy in Spain and the conceptual landscape that [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide* would later transform.
