## Overview

Jacob (Ya'aqov, in Hebrew), later named [[israel|Israel]], is the third patriarch and the father of the twelve tribes. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws upon the narratives of Jacob primarily in two contexts: as an example of prophetic vision in the analysis of biblical dream reports, and as proof that true [[prophets|prophecy]] — contra the claims of certain philosophers — was not exclusive to [[moses|Moses]] but was granted to the patriarchs as well. Jacob's ladder, his wrestling, and his role as a recipient of [[revelation|divine communication]] all enter the Guide's philosophical argument about the nature and accessibility of prophetic experience. (032-005)

## In the Guide

The most significant treatment of Jacob appears in the Guide's discussion of prophetic dreams. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets the biblical account of Jacob's ladder — "le récit de l'échelle de Jacob" (the account of Jacob's ladder) — as a prophetic vision in which the asc[[final-cause|end]]ing and desc[[final-cause|end]]ing angels represent the ranks of prophets asc[[final-cause|end]]ing through [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perception and desc[[final-cause|end]]ing to communicate divine truths. This reading integrates Jacob's experience into [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' larger theory of [[prophets|prophecy]] as [[wisdom|intellect]]ual emanation: what Jacob saw was not a physical ladder but a representation of the hierarchy of prophets and the graded nature of [[revelation|divine communication]]. (075-001)

In Part I, Chapter 31, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] employs the narrative of Jacob's departure from Laban to illustrate the metaphorical use of the Hebrew verb halakh (to go). The verse "Et Jacob alla son chemin" (Genesis 32:1) exemplifies the straightforward, non-metaphorical use of the verb — in contrast to its application to [[god|God]], where the same verb carries a transferred meaning. This example appears within [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' sustained lexicographical project of demonstrating that biblical Hebrew employs identical terms for human actions and divine attributes with fundamentally different senses. (031-001)

> Le récit de l'échelle de Jacob... est un exemple d'un récit prophétique qui doit être interprété allégoriquement. (075-001)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also cites Balaam's oracle concerning Jacob — "Il n'a pas aperçu d'iniquité dans Jacob" (Numbers 23:21) — in his discussion of [[providence|divine providence]] and the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection of the patriarchs. For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], this verse confirms that Jacob, along with [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[isaac|Isaac]], had attained a degree of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection that placed him under continuous [[providence|divine providence]]. The absence of "iniquity" is understood not as moral sinlessness in the conventional sense but as freedom from the error of [[imagination]] that distracts the [[wisdom|intellect]] from the apprehension of [[god|God]]. (019-001)

In Part I, Chapter 66, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] groups Jacob with [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[isaac|Isaac]] as the three patriarchs who attained [[prophets|prophecy]] and whose perfection serves as a model for the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual ideal: "[[abraham|Abraham]], ou [[isaac|Isaac]], ou Jacob, ou ceux qui les précédaient" — a lineage of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection that preceded and transc[[final-cause|end]]ed the Mosaic law. (066-011)
