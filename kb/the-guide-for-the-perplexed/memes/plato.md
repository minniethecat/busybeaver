## Overview

Plato (Aflatoun, in Arabic philosophical tradition) is the preeminent Greek philosopher whose ideas permeated medieval Arabic and Jewish thought through translations and commentaries. In the Guide, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] engages with Plato selectively — primarily through the lens of [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s critique — and positions Platonic philosophy as a stage in the development of thought that [[aristotle|Aristotle]] both inherited and transc[[final-cause|end]]ed. Plato's theory of forms, his cosmological account in the Timaeus, and his method of enigmatic expression are each addressed in the Guide, always with an eye toward what they reveal about the relationship between reason and revelation. (026-005)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of Plato in the Guide is characteristically economical. In Part I, Chapter 26, during a discussion of anthropomorphic language in Scripture and philosophy, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] notes that "C'est ainsi que Platon et d'autres avant lui appelaient la matière la femelle, et la forme le mâle" (Thus Plato and others before him called matter the fe[[evil|mal]]e, and form the [[evil|mal]]e). This remark is part of a broader argument that philosophers, like prophets, employ figurative and enigmatic language when addressing non-specialist audiences. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] uses Plato's practice as a precedent to legitimize his own esoteric reading of biblical [[anthropomorphism]]s: if Plato resorted to metaphor when discussing metaphysical principles, it is no objection that Scripture does the same. (026-005)

> C'est ainsi que Platon et d'autres avant lui appelaient la matière la femelle, et la forme le mâle. (026-005)

In Part II, Chapter 13, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] engages with Platonic cosmology when enumerating the three opinions concerning the eternity or creation of the world. He presents Plato's view — that [[god|God]] created the world from pre-existing eternal matter — as an intermediate position between the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian doctrine of eternal existence and the Mosaic doctrine of creation ex nihilo. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that Plato's theory of an eternal, uncreated matter is incompatible with the biblical account, but he ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s it as a distinct philosophical position that avoids some of the difficulties facing both extremes. (071-010)

In Part I, Chapter 56, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] alludes to the Platonic-[[aristotle|Aristote]]lian debate over the nature of [[wisdom|knowledge]] when he notes that certain epistemological insights belong more to Plato than to [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. Specifically, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] credits Plato with recognizing that human [[wisdom|knowledge]] of particulars is more certain than [[wisdom|knowledge]] of universals, an epistemological priority that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself [[final-cause|end]]orses in his theory of divine [[wisdom|knowledge]]. (056-003)

## Significance

Plato's influence on [[maimonides|Maimonides]] is indirect but substantial. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian tradition in rejecting Platonic forms as indep[[final-cause|end]]ently existing entities, yet he adopts elements of Platonic political philosophy in his conception of the prophet as philosopher-king. The Guide's repeated emphasis on the limits of human [[wisdom|knowledge]] — that [[god|God]]'s essence cannot be known, only His actions — also echoes Platonic apophaticism. [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' relationship to Plato is thus one of critical engagement: he distinguishes his own position from Plato's where necessary (creation, forms) while quietly absorbing Platonic themes where they serve his theological and philosophical [[final-cause|purpose]]s.
