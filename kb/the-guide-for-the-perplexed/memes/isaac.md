# Isaac

**Isaac** (Hebrew: יִצְחָק, *Yitsḥaq*) is the second of the three biblical patriarchs, son of [[Abraham]] and father of [[Jacob]]. [[Maimonides]] invokes Isaac in the *Guide for the Perplexed* as part of his argument for the unique character of Mosaic [[Prophecy]].

## Isaac and the Uniqueness of Mosaic Prophecy

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s sole explicit reference to Isaac appears in Part I, Chapter 63, where he argues that before [[Moses]], no one had ever claimed to be sent by [[god|God]] as a prophet in the full sense:

> [066-011] ...de sorte qu'Abraham, ou Isaac, ou Jacob, ou ceux qui les précédèrent...

The pas[[wisdom|sage]] establishes that pre-Mosaic figures — including [[abraham|Abraham]], Isaac, and [[jacob|Jacob]] — may have received [[revelation|divine communication]]s, but these were of a fundamentally different order from the prophetic mission of [[moses|Moses]]. The patriarchs experienced divine guidance or revelation, but none claimed the status of a publicly commissioned prophet bearing a [[law|divine law]].

This distinction is central to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s doctrine of [[prophets|prophecy]]. By placing Isaac alongside [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[jacob|Jacob]] in the category of pre-Mosaic recipients of [[revelation|divine communication]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] affirms the patriarchs' genuine spiritual stature while simultaneously reserving a unique status for Mosaic [[prophets|prophecy]] — the only instance of direct, law-giving divine address to a prophet whose mission was publicly verified.

## Isaac in the Patriarchal Line

Isaac's role in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s thought is inseparable from his place in the patriarchal succession. The chain of transmission — [[Abraham]] → Isaac → [[Jacob]] — represents the continuity of monotheistic [[wisdom|knowledge]] before the [[revelation-at-sinai|giving of the Torah]] at Sinai. Each patriarch preserved and transmitted the [[wisdom|knowledge]] of the one [[God]] in an era dominated by [[Idolatry|idolatrous]] beliefs, as [[maimonides|Maimonides]] describes in the same chapter.
