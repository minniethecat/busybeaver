## Definition

Law (loi, Torah) in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' "Guide for the Perplexed" is the divinely revealed legal and moral code given through [[moses|Moses]]. Unlike human laws, which aim only at the welfare of the body (security, prosperity), the divine Law aims at the welfare of the soul as well — teaching true opinions about [[god|God]] and the universe. (P02-005)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] understands the Law as a comprehensive system comprising commandments (mitzvot), theological doctrines, historical narratives, and [[knowledge|wisdom]] literature — all of which serve the dual [[final-cause|purpose]] of establishing a just society and guiding the [[wisdom|intellect]] toward [[wisdom|knowledge]] of [[god|God]].

## In the Guide

The Guide devotes its entire Third Part to explaining the reasons for the commandments (ta'amei ha-mitzvot). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that every commandment has a rational [[final-cause|purpose]], even if that [[final-cause|purpose]] is not immediately apparent. The Law's ritual prescriptions, far from being arbitrary, serve to distance the [[israel|Israelites]] from [[idolatry|idolatrous]] practices — particularly those of the Sabians, an ancient pagan nation whose [[prayer|worship]] [[maimonides|Maimonides]] had studied extensively. (P02-006) (P03-024)

> The Law as a whole aims at two things: the welfare of the soul and the welfare of the body. The welfare of the soul consists in the multitude acquiring correct opinions; the welfare of the body comes through the governance of the city.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] contrasts the divine Law with human nomoi: the former teaches truth and promotes [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection, while the latter merely maintain order. This distinction undergirds his entire philosophy of Judaism as the perfect synthesis of reason and revelation.

## Interpretation

The rationalization of the commandments was one of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most controversial projects. Traditionalists objected that seeking reasons for divine commands implies they are subject to human judgment; rationalists praised it as making Judaism [[wisdom|intellect]]ually defensible. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]d that the specific details of certain commandments may ultimately be beyond human comprehension, while their general [[final-cause|purpose]]s are accessible to reason. (002-002)

The concept of Law in the Guide also carries political dimensions: [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on [[plato|Plato]]'s and al-Farabi's political philosophy, treating [[moses|Moses]] as the ideal philosopher-king who promulgates the perfect constitution.
