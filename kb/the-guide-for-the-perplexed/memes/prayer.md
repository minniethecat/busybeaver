## Definition

Prayer (French: *prière*, Hebrew: *tefillah*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the act of addressing [[god|God]] through formulated speech — an activity whose philosophical legitimacy he def[[final-cause|end]]s against the objection that it implies an anthropomorphic conception of the deity. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] approaches prayer not primarily as a ritual obligation but as a theological problem: if [[god|God]] is unchanging and omniscient, what is the point of telling Him things He already knows or asking Him to alter His decrees?

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' answer draws on his broader philosophy of language and divine attributes. Prayer does not change [[god|God]] but transforms the worshipper: it cultivates the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual and moral dispositions that bring the human soul into alignment with the divine. This is consistent with [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' interpretation of all the commandments as having rational [[final-cause|purpose]]s (*ta'amei ha-mitzvot*) directed at human perfection rather than divine need.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses prayer most directly in his discussion of the biblical terms that appear to attribute sensory perception to [[god|God]]. In Chapter 49, he notes that Scripture uses the verb "to hear" (*shamaʿ*) for [[god|God]]'s response to prayer — "[[god|Dieu]] a ou n'a pas exaucé la prière de celui qui priait" (049-005). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that divine "hearing" of prayer means simply that [[god|God]] knows the worshipper's intention and that the worshipper's state has changed through the act of prayer itself.

> Dieu a ou n'a pas exaucé la prière de celui qui priait.
> (049-005)

In Chapter 50, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s this analysis to the verb "to answer" (*ʿanah*) when applied to [[god|God]]'s response to prayer. He explains that "when it concerns a prayer, he explains the verb 'to hear' in the sense of acceptance" (050-013). The point is philosophical: [[god|God]]'s "answering" of prayer is not a temporal reaction but the eternal divine will that the prayer was always part of the causal order.

The most significant discussion of prayer in the Guide appears in Chapters 61–62, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] addresses the liturgical use of divine names. He notes that [[moses|Moses]], in his prayer, used the formula: "Ô [[god|Dieu]] grand, puissant, redoutable" (061-006). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that while the Sages did not sanction the multiplication of divine epithets in prayer — since each epithet risks implying a distinct attribute and thus compromising divine unity — the specific names fixed by the Men of the Great Assembly are permitted be[[causality|cause]] they were established with precise [[wisdom|understanding]] of their theological implications (061-007).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] further explains that the Sages did not themselves invent the liturgical formulas but "fixed" what had already come into practice: "les hommes du Grand Synode ont fixé dans les prières et les bénédictions" (062-003). This historical claim serves his larger argument that the received liturgy is theologically sound: it was shaped by men who understood the philosophical dangers of anthropomorphic language and selected only those formulations compatible with true monotheism (062-001).
