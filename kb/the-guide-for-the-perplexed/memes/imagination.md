## Definition

Imagination (French: *imagination*, Arabic: *al-mutakhayyila*, Hebrew: *dimyon*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the cognitive faculty responsible for receiving sensory forms, storing them, and combining them into novel representations that do not correspond to any real object. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws a sharp distinction between the imaginative faculty and the rational faculty: the [[wisdom|intellect]] appreh[[final-cause|end]]s universal and immaterial truths, while the imagination is bound to the particular and the sensory, incapable of grasping what lies beyond matter (040-003).

This distinction is central to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[knowledge|epistemology]]. The imagination can produce vivid and emotionally compelling images, but these images may bear no relation to reality. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] warns repeatedly against the error of taking imaginative constructs for [[wisdom|intellect]]ual [[wisdom|knowledge]] — a [[error|mistake]] he attributes to the ignorant, to those content with "false imaginations with which their soul finds rest" (040-003), and to those who conceive of [[God]] in anthropomorphic terms be[[causality|cause]] their imagination cannot escape sensory representation (009-003).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of the imaginative faculty appears most prominently in his theory of [[Prophecy]]. In Part II, Chapters 32–48, he argues that the imaginative faculty is one of the three human faculties whose perfection is prerequisite for [[prophets|prophecy]] — alongside the rational faculty and the moral faculty. The imaginative faculty receives the [[emanation|divine overflow]] and translates abstract [[wisdom|intellect]]ual truths into sensory images: dreams, visions, parables, and anthropomorphic descriptions. This is why prophetic texts speak in figurative language — the imagination must "clothe" immaterial truths in material form (036-001).

The key difference between Mosaic [[prophets|prophecy]] and all other prophetic experience lies precisely in the role of the imagination. [[Moses]] alone received [[prophets|prophecy]] without the mediation of the imaginative faculty — his [[prophets|prophecy]] was purely [[wisdom|intellect]]ual, which is why the [[law|Law]] he delivered needs no [[allegory|figurative interpretation]] at its core. All other prophets, from [[Prophets]] like Isaiah and Ezekiel to lesser figures, received [[prophets|prophecy]] through the combined action of the rational and imaginative faculties, and therefore their visions require interpretation to extract the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual kernel from the imaginative husk (047-003).

> Les hommes, je veux dire selon l'imagination populaire, ce serait attribuer à Dieu une forme corporelle.
> (032-002)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also discusses the imagination in a negative context: as a source of error when it overpowers the [[wisdom|intellect]]. He warns that those who allow the imagination to dominate their thinking will "take [[god|God]] to be a being of pure imagination or a non-being" (P02-005), confusing what is merely imagined with what is real. The person of perfected [[wisdom|intellect]], by contrast, judges all things "according to reality and not according to the imagination" (041-001). This theme appears throughout the *Guide*, especially in the discussion of [[Anthropomorphism]], where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that the error of attributing bodily qualities to [[god|God]] arises from the inability to transc[[final-cause|end]] imaginative thinking.

In the context of the commandments, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that certain ritual laws are designed precisely to restrain the imaginative faculty from running wild — to prevent the soul from "allowing the imaginations to gain the upper hand and being drawn into vice" (036-001). The [[law|Law]] trains the individual toward [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection by disciplining the imagination, not by indulging it.
