## Overview

The Creation of the World is the foundational event of biblical cosmology. In Genesis, [[god|God]] creates the heavens and the earth in six days. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets this account allegorically, identifying the "account of creation" (Maasé beréschîth) with [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian physics. (P01-021) (002-003)

For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], creation ex nihilo is a fundamental doctrine that every Jew must affirm. Together with [[incorporeality|divine incorporeality]], it forms the theological minimum without which one cannot be considered a member of the community of [[israel|Israel]]. (009-003)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] treats creation as a theological and philosophical watershed. The doctrine of creation ex nihilo distinguishes monotheism from [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian eternalism. Yet [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that creation cannot be demonstrated conclusively — it is accepted on the authority of revelation while being made philosophically plausible. (016-001) (016-002)

> The account of creation in Genesis, when properly interpreted, does not contradict Aristotelian physics — it encodes it in the language of allegory accessible to all readers.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes extensive chapters to arguing that [[aristotle|Aristotle]] himself did not claim to have demonstratively proved the eternity of the world — he merely considered it the most probable opinion. This leaves room for creation to be equally plausible on purely philosophical grounds. (027-001)

The Guide's reading of Genesis identifies the six days of creation with the hierarchical structure of the cosmos: day one corresponds to the sphere of the fixed stars, days two through four to the planetary spheres, and days five and six to the sublunar [[sublunar-world|world of generation and corruption]].
