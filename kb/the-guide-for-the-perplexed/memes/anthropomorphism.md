## Definition

Anthropomorphism (French: *anthropomorphisme*, Arabic: *tashbīh*, Hebrew: *hagshamah*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the error of attributing to [[God]] any quality, attribute, or description that properly belongs to created beings — including bodily form, motion, emotion, location, or any other positive attribute. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] treats anthropomorphism as the most fundamental theological error, more dangerous even than [[Idolatry]], be[[causality|cause]] while the idolater [[prayer|worship]]s a false god, the anthropomorphist corrupts the very concept of the true [[god|God]], effectively [[prayer|worship]]ing "a being of pure [[imagination]] or a non-being" (P02-005).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies the root of anthropomorphism in the human cognitive apparatus: the [[Imagination]] can only represent what it has received through the senses, and therefore it inevitably t[[final-cause|end]]s to picture [[god|God]] in sensory terms. The uneducated person, who has not trained the [[Intellect]] to transc[[final-cause|end]] sensory images, naturally conceives of [[god|God]] as a large human-like being located somewhere in space. Overcoming this t[[final-cause|end]]ency is the first and most essential step in the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual [[prayer|worship]] of [[god|God]] (029-005).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] wages a systematic campaign against anthropomorphism throughout the *Guide*. In Part I, he devotes extensive analysis to biblical terms that seem to attribute bodily qualities to [[god|God]] — sitting, standing, seeing, hearing, speaking — and demonstrates that each must be understood figuratively. He praises [[onkelos|Onkelos]], the Aramaic translator of the [[law|Torah]], for systematically rephrasing such expressions "in order to avoid anthropomorphisms" (*pour éviter les anthropomorphismes*) (P02-003), and identifies this practice as a model for all interpreters of Scripture.

> Les uns, poussant plus loin l'anthropomorphisme, le croyaient un corps ayant une figure.
> (052-003)

The danger of anthropomorphism is not merely theoretical. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that those who take Scripture's anthropomorphic language literally fall into one of two errors: either they believe [[god|God]] has a body (which is false and degrades the divine), or they recognize the absurdity of a bodily [[god|God]] and reject Scripture altogether (which is apostasy). The only correct path is to recognize that Scripture speaks in human language — using bodily terms metaphorically — and to extract the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual meaning from the imaginative form (070-015).

This principle of [[allegory|figurative interpretation]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s to the biblical descriptions of divine actions. When Scripture says [[god|God]] "desc[[final-cause|end]]ed" or "was angry," these are not descriptions of [[god|God]]'s essence (which is indivisible and changeless) but descriptions of effects in the world. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows [[onkelos|Onkelos]] in r[[final-cause|end]]ering such expressions through circumlocutions that preserve the theological truth while respecting the biblical text. He notes that [[onkelos|Onkelos]] "was always careful to remove anthropomorphisms from the Hebrew text" (068-003), even at the cost of departing from a literal r[[final-cause|end]]ering.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] connects the rejection of anthropomorphism directly to the doctrine of [[Incorporeality]]. Be[[causality|cause]] [[god|God]] has no body, no attribute that implies corporeality can be true of Him. This leads to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]: we can only describe [[god|God]] by negating attributes that imply imperfection — [[god|God]] is not corporeal, not multiple, not changeable, not limited. The positive content of these negations is that [[god|God]] is One, incorporeal, eternal, and infinite (052-003).

The elimination of anthropomorphism is presented not as an esoteric doctrine for the elite but as a fundamental obligation incumbent on every believer. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] states in his legal writings that anyone who believes [[god|God]] has a body is a heretic (*min*). In the *Guide*, he treats the matter with equal gravity: the anthropomorphist "does not [[prayer|worship]] the [[god|God]] of [[abraham|Abraham]], [[isaac|Isaac]], and [[jacob|Jacob]], but a phantom of his own [[imagination]]" (047-003).
