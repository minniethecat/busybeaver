## Definition

Divine Names (French: *noms divins*, Hebrew: *shemot*) are, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the various designations of [[God]] found in Scripture — terms that he subjects to rigorous philosophical classification in order to prevent the theological error of supposing that different names imply different attributes or a composite divine essence. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes Chapters 61–67 of Part I to this topic, making it one of the most concentrated discussions of any single subject in the Guide.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' fundamental distinction is between the tetragrammaton (YHWH, the four-letter name) and all other divine names. The tetragrammaton is a "proper name" (*schem meyuhad*) that designates [[god|God]]'s essence directly, without reference to any action or attribute. All other names — Elohim, Adonai, Shaddai, and the various epithets — are derived from divine actions and therefore designate [[god|God]] only indirectly and by homonymy (065-001).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] opens his treatment of divine names in Chapter 61 with the declaration that "tous les noms de [[god|Dieu]] qu'on trouve dans les livres sacrés sont généralement dérivés des actions" — all the names of God found in Scripture are generally derived from actions. The single exception is the tetragrammaton, which he describes as *schem mephorash*, the explicit name that "indique expressément l'essence de [[god|Dieu]]" (065-001). This name, written with the letters yod, hé, waw, hé, conveys the idea of necessary existence itself and is therefore incomparable to any other designation (065-004).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] then discusses the name Adonai (r[[final-cause|end]]ered in Scripture as "Lord"), explaining that it is used by substitution for the tetragrammaton in pronunciation. This name, like Elohim, is derived from a relational concept — lordship — and therefore does not express the divine essence. The distinction is crucial: calling [[god|God]] "Lord" says something about His relation to creatures, not about what He is in Himself (065-003).

> Tous les noms de Dieu qu'on trouve dans les livres sacrés sont généralement dérivés des actions. Un seul nom doit être excepté, c'est celui qui s'écrit par yod, hé, waw, hé; car c'est un nom improvisé pour désigner l'essence de Dieu.
> (065-001)

In Chapters 62–63, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] turns to the extra-scriptural divine names found in rabbinic tradition — the twelve-letter name, the forty-two-letter name, and other *schemot* used in Talmudic and mystical contexts. He takes a characteristically rationalist position: the twelve-letter name was probably not a single name but two or three names combined, and the elaborate names found in later mystical literature are "des fables" fabricated by the ignorant (066-002). [[maimonides|Maimonides]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that the Talmudic Sages guarded certain names and transmitted them only to worthy disciples, but he insists that these names functioned as pedagogical tools for teaching [[truth|philosophical truth]]s, not as magical formulas (066-007).

The deeper philosophical point of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' discussion emerges in Chapters 63–64, where he explains that the entire apparatus of divine names must be understood within the framework of [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]. Every name derived from action — "merciful," "powerful," "wise" — is equivocal when applied to [[god|God]], be[[causality|cause]] [[god|God]] does not have attributes in the human sense. The only name that escapes this limitation is the tetragrammaton, which signifies pure existence without qualification (063-004).
