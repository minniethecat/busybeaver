## Definition

Accident (Greek: *symbebēkos*, Arabic: *ʿaraḍ*) is the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian category for that which exists not in itself but in another — a property, quality, or state that inheres in a substance without constituting its essence. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] makes extensive use of the concept of accident in two major contexts: the analysis of divine attributes (where he argues that any attribute ascribed to [[god|God]] would be a disqualifying accident) and the critique of Kalām [[atoms|atom]]ism (where accidents play a central role in the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] theory of continual creation) (051-009).

The accident-substance distinction is foundational for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]. Since [[god|God]] is absolutely one and simple, He cannot be the subject of accidents. Every positive attribute — whether essential or accidental — would introduce multiplicity into the divine nature, which is incompatible with [[god|God]]'s unity (052-008).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] introduces the concept of accident in his discussion of [[providence|divine providence]] and human flourishing in Part III. He notes that "tout ce qui peut survenir par accident" (everything that can occur by accident) affects a person's happiness and well-being, but the truly wise person understands that accidental goods and evils are not the measure of a life (031-001). This ethical application of the accident concept reflects the Stoic and [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian traditions.

In his analysis of living beings in Part I, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes between essential and accidental properties: "le mouvement est un accident inhérent à l'être animé" (movement is an accident inherent to the living being). This distinction becomes crucial when discussing whether [[god|God]] can be said to have life or movement — such descriptions would treat [[god|God]] as having accidents, which is theologically unacceptable (032-003).

> La confusion des choses accidentelles avec les choses essentielles est une des causes principales de l'erreur.
> (041-002)

The most rigorous deployment of the accident concept comes in Part I, Chapters 51–52, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] analyzes the logical consequences of attributing any property to [[god|God]]. He argues that "quand l'attribut est l'essence même de la chose, ce n'est point un accident; mais quand il est autre que l'essence, c'est un accident" — when the attribute is the very essence of the thing, it is not an accident; but when it is other than the essence, it is an accident (051-009). Since [[god|God]]'s essence is unknowable and ineffable, any attribute we might ascribe would be other than His essence, and therefore an accident — which is impossible for a being of absolute unity.

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' treatment of accidents is philosophically rigorous and theologically radical. By applying the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian category system to the problem of divine attributes, he demonstrates that any positive theology commits a category error. The accident concept thus serves as the logical fulcrum of his [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]: [[god|God]] cannot have accidents, therefore [[god|God]] cannot have attributes, therefore all positive descriptions of [[god|God]] must be reinterpreted as either descriptions of divine actions or as negations of privations (052-009).
