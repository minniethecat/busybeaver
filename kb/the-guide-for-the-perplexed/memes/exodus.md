## Overview

The Exodus (Exode) is the liberation of the [[israel|Israelites]] from slavery in [[egypt|Egypt]] under the leadership of [[moses|Moses]], recounted in the biblical book of Exodus. For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], this event is not merely a historical memory but the empirical foundation of Jewish faith — it publicly demonstrated [[god|God]]'s power over nature, His providential care for [[israel|Israel]], and the authenticity of [[moses|Moses]]' prophetic mission. (Exodus, XXIV, 11) (P02-002)

The Exodus is referenced throughout the Guide as the p[[accident|arad]]igmatic example of divine intervention in history. The miracles performed in [[egypt|Egypt]] — the plagues, the splitting of the sea — serve as public evidence for doctrines that philosophy could only establish probabilistically.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] invokes the Exodus in multiple theological contexts. It serves as proof of creation ex nihilo: the miracles of [[egypt|Egypt]] demonstrate that [[god|God]] is not bound by the laws of nature, which He Himself established. (P03-020)

The [[revelation-at-sinai|Sinai revelation]] that followed the Exodus is the central theophany of the [[law|Torah]]. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] interprets the account of the elders of [[israel|Israel]] seeing [[god|God]] (Exodus, XXIV, 11) as an allegory for [[wisdom|intellect]]ual apprehension — those who "ate and drank" after the vision failed to achieve true [[wisdom|intellect]]ual comprehension. (019-004) (019-005)

> The public nature of the Sinai revelation — witnessed by an entire nation, not a lone mystic — is for Maimonides the decisive proof of the truth of Mosaic prophecy.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also notes that the commandment to remember the Exodus appears throughout the [[law|Law]], linking daily ritual observance to the historical consciousness of divine redemption. (024-001)
