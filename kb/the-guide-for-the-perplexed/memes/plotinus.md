# Plotinus

**Plotinus** (Greek: Πλωτῖνος, *Plōtinos*; 204–270 CE) was the founder of [[Neoplatonism]] and the author of the *Enneades*. [[Maimonides]] cites Plotinus twice in the *Guide for the Perplexed*, both times in the context of [[Negative Theology|negative theology]] — the doctrine that [[god|God]] can only be described through negation rather than affirmation.

## Negative Theology and the Inadequacy of Language

In his discussion of divine attributes (Part I, Chapter 58), [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on Plotinus's radical formulation of [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]:

> [059-003] ce que la doctrine des philosophes arabes a d'absolu à cet égard paraît être puisé dans les commentateurs néoplatoniciens et se rattacher aux doctrines de Plotin (cf. Ritter, Geschichte der Philosophie, t. IV, pag. 573 et suiv.).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] explicitly traces the Arab philosophers' uncompromising negation of divine attributes back through the Neoplatonic commentators to Plotinus. This genealogy establishes Plotinus as a key source for the theological method [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself adopts — the systematic denial of positive attributes to [[God]].

## God as Cause — the Enneades Citation

In [[069-013]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] directly quotes Plotinus's argument that calling [[god|God]] "[[causality|cause]]" says more about human dep[[final-cause|end]]ency than divine nature:

> [069-013] Ce raisonnement des Motécallemîn est à peu près le même que fait Plotin quand il dit qu'en appelant Dieu la cause, nous disons quelque chose qui ne convient pas à lui, mais à nous, parce que nous tenons quelque chose de lui, tandis que lui, il reste en lui-même (Ennéades, VI, 9, 3).

This is one of the rare direct citations of a Greek philosophical source in the *Guide*. Plotinus's insight — that even the apparently neutral term "[[causality|cause]]" is anthropocentric — reinforces [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s central argument that all [[Divine Attributes|divine attributes]] are ultimately descriptions of divine effects, not divine essence.

## Plotinus and the Kalām

The connection [[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws between Plotinian [[divine-attributes|negative theology]] and the [[Kalām]] tradition is significant. The Arab philosophers absorbed Plotinus's negative method through the Neoplatonic commentaries on [[Aristotle]], transmitting it into the Islamic philosophical tradition that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] both inherits and critiques. This makes Plotinus an indirect but important source for the theological framework of the *Guide*.
