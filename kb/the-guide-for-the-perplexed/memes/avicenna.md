## Overview

Avicenna (Ibn Sina) is, after [[aristotle|Aristotle]], the philosopher whose conceptual framework most deeply shapes [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide*. [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' French translator [[solomon|Salomon]] Munk identifies Avicenna as [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' "habitual guide" (guide habituel), and the *Guide*'s exposition of the Arabic Peripatetic system is drawn principally from Avicenna's works. (069-001) (P01-018)

Avicenna provided [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with two of his most fundamental metaphysical distinctions: the distinction between essence and existence (where existence is an accident superadded to essence in all created beings), and the distinction between necessary existence (that which exists through its own essence — [[god|God]] alone) and [[necessary-existence|contingent existence]] (that which requires an external [[causality|cause]]). (055-002) (058-003)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts the Avicennian essence/existence distinction without reservation, despite its rejection by Averroes and other philosophers. For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], this distinction is essential to [[divine-attributes|negative theology]]: be[[causality|cause]] [[god|God]]'s essence is identical with His existence, no predicate that applies to contingent beings — whose essence is distinct from their existence — can apply to [[god|God]] in the same sense. (058-003) (058-004)

The necessary/contingent distinction (wājib al-wujūd / mumkin al-wujūd) is equally central. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] deploys it to restructure the traditional proofs for [[god|God]]'s existence: [[god|God]] is the Necessary Being upon whom all contingent beings dep[[final-cause|end]], and whose existence can be demonstrated from the fact that contingent beings exist at all. This Avicennian framework replaces the older [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian proof from motion. (055-002)

> "Here, as elsewhere, the author follows in the footsteps of the Arabic Peripatetics, and notably of Ibn Sina, his habitual guide." — Salomon Munk (069-001)

Avicenna's influence ext[[final-cause|end]]s to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' cosmology (the hierarchy of separate Intelligences [[final-cause|end]]ing with the [[active-intellect|Active Intellect]]), his psychology (the tripartite division of the [[wisdom|intellect]] into material, in habitu, and acquired), and his doctrine that [[god|God]] is simultaneously [[intellect|Intellect]], Intelligent, and Intelligible. (069-001) [[maimonides|Maimonides]] is not, however, uncritical: the commentary tradition notes that he follows Avicenna's potentially problematic conflation of numerical unity with absolute unity, for which Averroes criticized them both. (058-005)

## Significance

Avicenna represents the high point of the Arabic philosophical synthesis that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] inherited. His conceptual innovations — particularly in modal metaphysics (necessity/contingency) and the analysis of being (essence/existence) — gave [[maimonides|Maimonides]] the tools to reconcile [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy with the biblical doctrine of creation. Without Avicenna's framework, the *Guide*'s central arguments about [[god|God]]'s unity, incorporeality, and absolute transc[[final-cause|end]]ence would lack their philosophical foundation.
