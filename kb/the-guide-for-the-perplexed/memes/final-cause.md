## Definition

The Final Cause (French: *cause finale*, Greek: *telos*, Arabic: *ghāya*) is, within [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s doctrine of the [[causality|four causes]], that for the sake of which a thing exists or an action is performed — its purpose, end, or goal. In the *Guide for the Perplexed*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] devotes a sustained discussion to the applicability of the [[causality|four causes]] to [[god|God]], arguing that [[god|God]] is simultaneously the efficient [[causality|cause]], the for[[evil|mal]] [[causality|cause]], and the final [[causality|cause]] of the universe.

The identification of [[god|God]] as the final [[causality|cause]] of the world means that the entire order of existence tends toward [[god|God]] as its ultimate purpose. This does not imply that [[god|God]] acts for an end external to Himself — a limitation incompatible with divine perfection — but rather that all things, in their being and activity, are oriented toward [[god|God]] as their highest good and ultimate intelligible object (069-012).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most concentrated discussion of the final [[causality|cause]] appears in Part I, Chapter 69. He begins by noting that the philosophers call [[god|God]] the "[[causality|first cause]]," while the [[kalam|Kalam]] theologians ([[kalam|Motécallemin]]) avoid this term, preferring "agent." [[maimonides|Maimonides]] rejects the [[kalam|Kalam]]'s linguistic scruple and affirms that all four [[causality|Aristotelian causes]] — efficient, material, for[[evil|mal]], and final — are properly predicated of [[god|God]], though in a sense appropriate to divine transcendence (069-015).

> Dieu est l'efficient du monde, sa forme et sa fin.
> (069-016)

The relationship between the final [[causality|cause]] and the other three [[causality|cause]]s is central to [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' argument. He follows [[aristotle|Aristotle]] in holding that the efficient, for[[evil|mal]], and final [[causality|cause]]s often coincide in a single entity — the form of a thing is both what makes it what it is (for[[evil|mal]] [[causality|cause]]) and the end toward which its development tends (final [[causality|cause]]). Applying this to [[god|God]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] argues that [[god|God]] is the form of the universe in the sense that His continuous overflow gives being to all things, and the final [[causality|cause]] in the sense that all things tend toward the perfection that consists in [[wisdom|knowledge]] of Him (069-020).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] extends the teleological framework beyond metaphysics into his treatment of providence and ethics. The purpose of human existence, he argues, is [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection — the actualization of the rational faculty through the acquisition of true [[wisdom|knowledge]]. The commandments of the [[law|Torah]] themselves serve a final [[causality|cause]]: they are means ordered toward the end of human perfection, guiding the individual toward the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual and [[ethics|moral virtues]] that constitute the ultimate human good.

## Interpretation

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' affirmation of final causality places him firmly in the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian tradition against both Epicurean [[atoms|atom]]ism and [[kalam|Kalam]] occasionalism, both of which deny that nature acts for ends. Yet his teleology is distinctively religious: the ultimate final [[causality|cause]] is not an impersonal prime mover but the [[god|God]] of [[abraham|Abraham]], whose [[knowledge|wisdom]] and governance are manifest in the order of creation. This synthesis of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian metaphysics and biblical theism is one of the defining achievements of the Guide.
