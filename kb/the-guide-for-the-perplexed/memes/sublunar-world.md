## Definition

The Sublunar World (French: *le monde sublunaire*) is the terrestrial realm situated beneath the sphere of the moon in the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian-Ptolemaic cosmology that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adopts as the framework for his discussion of physics and metaphysics. It is the domain of the four elements — earth, water, air, and fire — and of all composite bodies subject to generation and corruption. Everything below the lunar sphere is mutable; everything above it belongs to the celestial realm of eternal, unchanging circular motion.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] distinguishes the sublunar realm sharply from the celestial spheres. The spheres and the heavenly bodies are composed of a "fifth body" (*quintessence*), a substance not subject to the contraries that drive change in the lower world. The sublunar realm, by contrast, is characterized by the perpetual cycle of coming-to-be and passing-away — individuals perish, but species are preserved through the influence of the celestial motions upon terrestrial matter (071-020).

## In the Guide

The most detailed treatment of the sublunar world in the Guide appears in Part I, Chapter 72, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents a comprehensive analogy between the cosmos and a single living organism. The celestial spheres function as the vital organs, and their motions — particularly the daily rotation of the outermost sphere and the proper motions of the planets — transmit formative influences downward to the sublunar realm. These influences are mediated by light and darkness, heat and cold, producing the diverse mineral, vegetative, and animate forms of the terrestrial world (071-018).

> Dans le monde sublunaire, tout est soumis au changement perpétuel.
> (071-023)

The sublunar realm's mutability is not chaotic but ordered by the celestial influences. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] explains that different faculties emanate from the spheres: one faculty produces minerals, another gives the vegetative soul to plants, another the vital soul to ani[[evil|mal]]s, and another the rational faculty to human beings. All of this — generation, nourishment, growth, and rational thought — operates through the intermediary of the celestial bodies and their light (071-018).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also discusses the sublunar world in connection with the problem of matter. In the Introduction to the Guide, he compares matter to an unfaithful wife — beautiful and necessary for existence but always t[[final-cause|end]]ing toward corruption and infidelity to form. The sublunar realm is the domain where matter's recalcitrance is most evident, where the perpetual struggle between form and privation generates the [[final-cause|end]]less cycle of generation and destruction (006-002).
