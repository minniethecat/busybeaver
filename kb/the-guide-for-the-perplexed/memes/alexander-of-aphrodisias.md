## Overview

Alexander of Aphrodisias (Alexandre d'Aphrodise) was a Greek Peripatetic philosopher who held the imperial chair of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy at Athens around 200 CE. Known in both the Greek and Arabic traditions simply as "the Commentator," Alexander produced the most authoritative systematic commentaries on [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s works in antiquity. (034-005)

His interpretations shaped the entire subsequent tradition of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy, through Arabic translations into the [[arabs|Islamic world]] and through the Greek tradition in the East.

For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], Alexander was far more than a historical authority. In a letter to his translator [[samuel|Samuel]] Ibn Tibbon, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] specifically recomm[[final-cause|end]]ed the study of Alexander's commentaries as indispensable for [[wisdom|understanding]] [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. Within the *Guide*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] engages Alexander at three critical junctures, each touching on fundamental questions of [[knowledge|epistemology]] and philosophical psychology.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most sustained engagement with Alexander appears in the discussion of the [[wisdom|intellect]] spanning Part I, Chapters LXVIII–LXX. Here [[maimonides|Maimonides]] lays out the Arabic philosophers' theory of three stages of [[wisdom|intellect]]ual development: the material (or hylic) [[wisdom|intellect]], the [[wisdom|intellect]] in act, and the acquired [[wisdom|intellect]]. (069-005)

The central controversy concerns the nature of the material [[wisdom|intellect]] itself: is it a pure disposition inhering in the human soul and therefore perishable, or is it a separate, imperishable substance?

Alexander held that the hylic [[wisdom|intellect]] is nothing more than a pure disposition attached to the other faculties of the soul, perishable with them. [[themistius|Themistius]], by contrast, argued that since [[aristotle|Aristotle]] described the [[wisdom|intellect]] as "unmixed" with bodily faculties, its substrate must be a substance separate from those faculties. Ibn Rushd (Averroes) attempted a synthesis. (069-005)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] decisively sides with Alexander. In Chapter LXX, he states that "the soul born with man at his birth is merely a thing in potency and a disposition, while the thing that remains separately after death is what has become [[wisdom|intellect]] in act." The perishable disposition is not the same as the immortal acquired [[wisdom|intellect]]. (070-005)

Earlier, in Chapter XX, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites Alexander's analysis of the [[causality|cause]]s of philosophical disagreement. Alexander identified three sources: ambitious pretensions that prevent clear perception; the inherent difficulty of the subject matter; and the limitations of the inquirer. (034-005) This framework serves [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[final-cause|purpose]] of explaining why intelligent readers fall into confusion.

Alexander's authority also appears in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' discussion of the *[[kalam|Kalam]]* theologians. The [[kalam|Mutakallimun]] were aware of philosophical critiques that drew on commentators like Alexander, and developed their own counter-arguments accordingly.

## Significance

Alexander's influence on [[maimonides|Maimonides]] goes beyond explicit citations. His rigorous naturalism about the soul — the [[wisdom|intellect]] begins as a mere potentiality of the embodied human being — provides [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with a philosophical anthropology that is neither materialist nor mystically dualist.

The human being is born with the capacity for [[wisdom|intellect]], not with [[wisdom|intellect]] already formed. [[intellect|Intellect]]ual perfection must be earned through a lifetime of study and contemplation. This view undergirds the *Guide*'s entire pedagogical structure.

Alexander's authority also serves [[maimonides|Maimonides]] in negotiating between [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy and Jewish theology. By adopting Alexander's mortalist account of the hylic [[wisdom|intellect]], [[maimonides|Maimonides]] can affirm the biblical teaching that the soul is created with the body while preserving the philosophical teaching that [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection confers immortality.
