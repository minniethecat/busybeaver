## Definition

*Maasé Mercabâ* (Hebrew: מעשה מרכבה, "the Work of the Chariot") refers to the prophetic vision of the divine chariot (*merkabah*) described in Ezekiel 1 and 10. In rabbinic tradition, it is the most restricted of esoteric doctrines: the Mishnah prohibits teaching it even to a single individual unless that person is already wise (002-004).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies *Maasé Mercabâ* with **[[aristotle|Aristote]]lian metaphysics** — the [[knowledge|science]] of [[god|God]], the separate [[intellect|intelligence]]s, and that which exists beyond the physical world (002-003). Together with *[[maase-bereschith|Maasé Beréschîth]]* (physics), it forms the two branches of the esoteric [[law|Torah]].

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents his identification of *Maasé Mercabâ* with metaphysics in the Introduction and early chapters of Part I. He explains that the Account of the Chariot, as recorded by Ezekiel, is an allegorical r[[final-cause|end]]ering of metaphysical truths (001-003).

The chariot itself, with its wheels, living creatures (*hayyot*), and throne, symbolizes the cosmic order as understood by [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy, including the nature of [[god|God]] and the hierarchy of [[intellect|intelligence]]s.

The pedagogical progression that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] outlines is strict: one must first master *[[maase-bereschith|Maasé Beréschîth]]* (physics) before approaching *Maasé Mercabâ* (metaphysics). This mirrors the [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian curriculum in which the study of nature precedes the study of first principles (003-001).

The extreme restrictions on teaching *Maasé Mercabâ* reflect the greater danger of metaphysical error. While errors in physics lead to [[error|false belief]]s about the natural world, errors in metaphysics lead to [[error|false belief]]s about [[god|God]], which [[maimonides|Maimonides]] considers the gravest [[wisdom|intellect]]ual sin.

## Interpretation

Maimonidean treatment of *Maasé Mercabâ* illustrates the conviction that Jewish esotericism and Greek philosophy converge on the same truths. The "[[esoteric-interpretation|secrets of the Torah]]" are not gnostic mysteries but rational demonstrations.

By identifying the Chariot vision with metaphysics, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] legitimizes the philosophical enterprise within Jewish tradition while explaining why Scripture speaks in riddles: metaphysical truths, conveyed directly, would confuse the unprepared reader.
