# Solomon

**Solomon** (Hebrew: שְׁלֹמֹה, *Shlomo*) is the biblical king of [[israel|Israel]], son of [[David]], renowned for his [[knowledge|wisdom]]. [[Maimonides]] cites Solomon's writings — particularly Proverbs and Ecclesiastes — extensively throughout the *Guide for the Perplexed* as authoritative models of allegorical expression and philosophical [[knowledge|wisdom]].

## Solomon as Allegorist

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] presents Solomon as the p[[accident|arad]]igmatic author of allegorical discourse. In the introduction to the *Guide*, he cites Solomon's Proverbs as the key to [[wisdom|understanding]] prophetic allegories:

> [004-002] Tu sais enfin comment Salomon a commencé (son livre): Pour comprendre l'allégorie et le discours...

Solomon's opening of Proverbs is treated as a programmatic statement for the entire allegorical method that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] himself employs. The aesthetic dimension of Solomon's allegories is also emphasized:

> [005-001] Il dit donc: Comme une pomme d'or dans un filet d'argent à ouvertures très fines, telle est la parole dite selon ses deux faces.

The "apple of gold in a silver filigree" (Proverbs 25:11) becomes [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s central metaphor for the two-faced nature of prophetic discourse — beautiful on the surface, but containing deeper gold within.

## Solomon on Preparatory Studies

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] draws on Ecclesiastes to establish the necessity of preparatory studies before approaching metaphysical [[knowledge|wisdom]]:

> [042-001] Salomon déjà a déclaré que les études préparatoires sont absolument nécessaires, et qu'il est impossible de parvenir à la véritable sagesse, si ce n'est après s'être exercé; il a dit: Si le fer est émoussé et qu'il n'ait pas les faces polies, vaincra-t-il des armées? mais il faut encore plus de préparation pour (acquérir) la sagesse (Ecclésiaste, X, 10).

This pas[[wisdom|sage]] (Ecclesiastes 10:10) is used to argue that the [[wisdom|intellect]], like a dull blade, must be sharpened through preparatory disciplines before it can cut through difficult metaphysical problems.

## Solomon and Moses

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] compares Solomon with [[Moses]] in the context of divine perception:

> [060-006] ...ce qu'ont reçu de la science divine les prophètes, et ce que recevaient notre maître Moïse et Salomon est la même chose...

Both [[moses|Moses]] and Solomon are presented as recipients of divine [[wisdom|knowledge]], though their modes of reception differ according to their respective stations. Solomon's [[knowledge|wisdom]] represents a form of prophetic [[revelation|inspiration]] accessible through [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection rather than direct divine address.

## Elevation and Greatness

Solomon's elevation by [[god|God]] serves [[maimonides|Maimonides]] as a linguistic proof for the metaphorical use of height and ascent terminology:

> [023-001] Et l'Éternel éleva Salomon très haut (I Chron., XXIX, 25).

This verse supports [[maimonides|Maimonides]]'s argument that terms of spatial elevation, when applied to persons, denote greatness and honor rather than physical position — a key principle in his [[Negative Theology|negative theology]].

## Solomon on Restraint in Speech

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites Solomon's warnings about excessive speech as a model for the discipline required in metaphysical inquiry:

> [010-003] ...Salomon, n'est-il pas assez que tes paroles...

Solomon's proverbs about the dangers of loquacity are marshaled to support the rabbinic and philosophical principle that divine matters should be discussed only with appropriate restraint and preparation.
