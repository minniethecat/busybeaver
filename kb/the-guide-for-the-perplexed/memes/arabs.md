## Overview

The Arabs (Arabes), in the context of [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' Guide, refer not merely to an ethnic group but to the Arabic-Islamic civilization that dominated the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual landscape of the medieval Mediterranean. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] wrote the Guide in Judeo-Arabic and drew extensively on works of Arabic philosophy, [[knowledge|science]], and theology. (P01-017)

The Arabic philosophical tradition — particularly the works of al-Farabi, [[avicenna|Ibn Sina]] ([[avicenna|Avicenna]]), and Ibn Bajja (Avempace) — provided [[maimonides|Maimonides]] with the conceptual framework for synthesizing [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian philosophy with monotheistic theology. The Guide is unthinkable without this Arabic [[wisdom|intellect]]ual heritage. (P03-009)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] engages extensively with the [[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]], the scholastic theologians of Islam. He devotes a lengthy section (Part I, chapters 71–76) to expounding and refuting their system, which he considers philosophically inadequate but historically influential. (P01-019) (P03-011)

> The Arabic translations of Aristotle, produced in Baghdad under the Abbasid caliphate, were the conduit through which Greek philosophy reached Maimonides and the entire medieval Jewish philosophical tradition.

The Guide also discusses the Sabians (Sabiens), an ancient pagan nation whose practices [[maimonides|Maimonides]] researched through Arabic sources. His [[wisdom|knowledge]] of Sabian religion — the historical context for many biblical commandments — came entirely through Arabic-language texts. (001-001) (001-003)

Arabic literary forms also shaped the Guide: the use of Makāmāt (rhymed prose narratives) and the epistle format of addressing a disciple were conventions of Arabic belles-lettres. (001-002)
