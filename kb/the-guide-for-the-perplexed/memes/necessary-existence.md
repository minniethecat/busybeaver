## Definition

Necessary Existence (Arabic: *wājib al-wujūd*, French: *existence nécessaire*) is a central concept in medieval Islamic and Jewish metaphysics, originating with [[avicenna|Avicenna]] ([[avicenna|Ibn Sīnā]]) and adopted by [[maimonides|Maimonides]] in the *Guide for the Perplexed*. The distinction divides all beings into two fundamental categories: that which exists necessarily through its own essence (*necessary being*), and that which is merely possible — requiring an external [[causality|cause]] to bring it into existence and sustain it (*possible being*). [[god|God]] alone belongs to the first category; everything else in the created order belongs to the second.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] identifies the demonstration of [[god|God]]'s existence as the first of the fundamental dogmas of the [[law|Law]], paired inseparably with the doctrines of unity and incorporeality (P02-005). The necessary/possible distinction provides the metaphysical framework for this demonstration: the existence of possible beings requires an ultimate necessary being as their ground, since an infinite regress of contingent [[causality|cause]]s is unintelligible. This argument form — the proof from contingency — is distinct from the [[kalam|Kalam]]'s *kalām* cosmological argument and reflects [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' synthesis of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian and Avicennian metaphysics.

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most explicit engagement with the necessary/possible distinction appears in his discussion of the domains of the [[wisdom|intellect]]. In Chapter 16, he distinguishes between the "domain of the necessary" (*domaine du nécessaire*) and the "domain of the possible" (*domaine du possible*): what is necessary is determined by the nature of the thing itself, whereas what is possible admits of alternatives and is determined by will (016-002). This epistemological distinction has direct theological application: the question of whether the world was created necessarily or voluntarily is one of the great disputes between the philosophers, who affirm eternal necessity, and the theologians, who affirm free creation.

> Dans le (domaine du) nécessaire il n'y a pas du tout de bon choix.
> (016-003)

The Guide's table of contents repeatedly identifies [[god|God]]'s existence as a fundamental dogma that requires demonstration. The preface declares that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] undertook the work "notamment pour démontrer l'existence, l'unité et l'incorporalité de [[god|Dieu]]" (P01-021). The proof of [[god|God]]'s existence through necessary being draws on the Avicennian insight that essence and existence are distinct in all created things but identical in [[god|God]]: in [[god|God]] alone, existence belongs to the essence necessarily; in all other beings, existence is something added to essence from without.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] ext[[final-cause|end]]s the necessary/possible distinction to the question of divine attributes and divine naming. In Chapter 21, he discusses the metaphysical rank of [[god|God]]'s existence: "si ce n'est le rang de son existence, qui n'a pas de pareil ni de semblable" — the rank of His existence has no equal or similar (021-002). In Chapter 22, he explains that the Tetragrammaton itself indicates necessary existence: "il indique mon existence, ma grandeur et ma puissance" — it indicates a mode of being that is self-subsistent and un[[causality|cause]]d (022-001).

The distinction also underlies [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' critique of the [[kalam|Kalam]] theologians. The *[[mutakallimun|Mutakallimūn]]*, he argues, fail to grasp the necessary/possible distinction properly be[[causality|cause]] their [[atoms|atom]]istic occasionalism denies stable natures. Without the concept of a nature or essence that determines what a thing necessarily is, the [[kalam|Kalam]] cannot coherently distinguish between what belongs to a thing essentially and what belongs to it accidentally. This failure, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cont[[final-cause|end]]s, undermines their proofs for [[god|God]]'s existence (P01-020).
