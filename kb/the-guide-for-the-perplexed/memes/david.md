## Overview

David (Daoud, in Arabic; David in Hebrew) is the second king of [[israel|Israel]], the founder of the Davidic dynasty, and the traditional author of the Psalms. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] references David throughout the Guide, not as a biographical subject but as a source of authoritative scriptural precedent. The biblical narratives concerning David — his anointing, his words, and the events of his reign — serve [[maimonides|Maimonides]] as examples in his analyses of Hebrew language, divine will, and the reconciliation of apparent contradictions in the biblical text. [[maimonides|Maimonides]] follows the talmudic tradition of resolving contradictions between David's words and those of [[solomon|Solomon]], treating David's utterances as data points within the larger project of making Scripture philosophically coherent. (010-003)

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] invokes David most frequently in the context of linguistic analysis. In Part I, Chapter 67, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] examines the Hebrew verb amar (to say) and its metaphorical application to divine will. He cites the pas[[wisdom|sage]] "Et il dit de frapper David" (2 [[samuel|Samuel]] 21:16) to demonstrate that amar here does not denote verbal utterance but intention: the Philistine warrior wished or desired to strike David. This is one of several biblical examples [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adduces to show that when Scripture attributes "speech" to [[god|God]], it designates will rather than spoken words. (067-009)

> Les verbes amar (dire) et dibber (parler) sont des homonymes... Et il dit de frapper David (II Sam., XXI, 16), — ce qui veut dire, et il voulut le frapper, c'est-à-dire, il s'en préoccupait. (067-009)

Similarly, in Chapter 68, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] analyzes the verb nouah (to rest, be quiet) in the narrative of David's servants and Nabal: "Et ils dirent à Nabal, au nom de David, toutes ces paroles, puis ils se tinrent tranquilles" (1 [[samuel|Samuel]] 25:9). He argues that the verb here means not physical rest but the cessation of speech — an instance of how biblical Hebrew uses the language of rest and speech in ways that illuminate theological language about [[god|God]]. (068-005)

Beyond linguistic analysis, David's words in the Psalms provide support for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' philosophical positions. In Part I, Chapter 32, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites David's statement "Et je n'ai point pénétré dans des choses trop grandes et trop obscures pour moi" (Psalm 131:1) to [[final-cause|end]]orse the rabbinic injunction against studying matters beyond human [[wisdom|intellect]]ual capacity. This citation places David among those who recognized the proper limits of human [[wisdom|knowledge]] — a crucial principle for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' project of distinguishing what the [[wisdom|intellect]] can demonstrate from what must be accepted through tradition. (037-002)

In Part I, Chapter 46, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] also references David's anointing as king — "un seul cœur pour établir David roi" (1 Chronicles 12:8) — to illustrate the metaphorical use of lev (heart) to mean unified opinion or common sentiment. (046-002)
