## Overview

Themistius was a Greek philosopher, statesman, and orator of the fourth century CE who produced paraphrases and commentaries on [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s works. Unlike [[alexander-of-aphrodisias|Alexander of Aphrodisias]], whose method aimed at exhaustive philological analysis, Themistius composed elegant paraphrases that made [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s thought accessible to a broader public. (071-002)

This pedagogical clarity, combined with philosophical acuity, made Themistius one of the most widely read [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian commentators in the Arabic tradition.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]], like many Arabic philosophers, encountered [[aristotle|Aristotle]] largely through the mediation of commentators. Among these, Themistius held a place of particular authority. In the *Guide*, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] engages him at two critical junctures — first on the nature of the [[wisdom|intellect]], then on an epistemological principle central to his critique of the [[kalam|Mutakallimun]].

## In the Guide

Themistius appears in Part I, Chapter LXVIII, in the dispute over the hylic [[wisdom|intellect]]. Against [[alexander-of-aphrodisias|Alexander of Aphrodisias]], who held that the material [[wisdom|intellect]] is a mere disposition of the soul and perishable, Themistius argued that [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s description of the [[wisdom|intellect]] as "unmixed" with other faculties requires a separate substrate. (069-005)

On Themistius' reading, [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s characterization of the [[wisdom|intellect]] as "separable, impassible, and unmixed" means the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual principle must be ontologically indep[[final-cause|end]]ent. (069-005)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]], in Chapter LXX, ultimately sides with Alexander on the perishability of the hylic [[wisdom|intellect]]. But his presentation is characteristically even-handed: he presents Themistius' argument fairly and ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s its seriousness before explaining his reasons for preferring Alexander.

Themistius' most important contribution appears in Chapter LXXI, where [[maimonides|Maimonides]] critiques the *[[kalam|Kalam]]* theologians. These theologians, he argues, erect theological systems without regard for the actual nature of things. Their method begins with desired conclusions and invents premises to support them, treating [[imagination]] as reason. (071-001)

Against this, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] invokes Themistius' definitive principle: **"Being does not accommodate itself to opinions, but true opinions accommodate themselves to being."** (071-001) Reality has its own structure; rational inquiry discovers that structure rather than imposing one upon it.

The commentator [[moses|Moses]] Narboni notes that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] cites Themistius rather than [[aristotle|Aristotle]] himself, though [[aristotle|Aristotle]] made the same point in the *Metaphysics*. Narboni speculates that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] consulted the modern commentators. This confirms Themistius' status as a primary conduit of [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian [[knowledge|epistemology]] in the Arabic tradition. (071-002)

## Significance

Themistius' role in the *Guide* is structurally significant. His epistemological realism — truth is conformity to being, not to opinion — provides the philosophical foundation for [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' entire project.

If Scripture and reason appear to conflict, the resolution cannot lie in abandoning either. The interpreter must let the nature of things guide interpretation, trusting that divine and natural truth are ultimately one.

Themistius' method of paraphrase and clarification also shaped the *Guide*'s rhetorical strategy. [[maimonides|Maimonides]], like Themistius, addresses an educated but non-expert audience, employing clear exposition and strategic simplification to make difficult ideas accessible.
