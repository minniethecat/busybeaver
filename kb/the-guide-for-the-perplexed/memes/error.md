## Definition

Error (French: *erreur*, Arabic: *ghalaṭ*) is, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' *Guide for the Perplexed*, the failure of the [[wisdom|intellect]] to grasp truth as it really is — a failure that [[maimonides|Maimonides]] analyzes with systematic precision, identifying multiple distinct [[causality|cause]]s and treating the avoidance of error as a central methodological concern. For [[maimonides|Maimonides]], error is not merely a lack of [[wisdom|knowledge]] but a positive impediment to the [[wisdom|intellect]]ual perfection that constitutes the highest human good.

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' [[knowledge|epistemology]] of error is distinctive in combining [[aristotle|Aristote]]lian cognitive psychology with rabbinic pedagogical [[knowledge|wisdom]]. He identifies errors arising from the inherent limitations of the human [[wisdom|intellect]], errors [[causality|cause]]d by overhasty judgment before the mind is properly prepared, and — crucially — errors deliberately induced by Scripture's use of allegory, which is designed to conceal truth from the unworthy while revealing it to the prepared reader (019-003).

## In the Guide

[[maimonides|Maimonides]]' most structured treatment of error appears in Part I, Chapters 31–32, where he adapts a taxonomy from [[alexander-of-aphrodisias|Alexander of Aphrodisias]]. Alexander identified three [[causality|cause]]s of error: "the love of domination" (prejudice in favor of one's own opinions), "the subtlety of the object of perception itself," and "the ignorance of the perceiver and his incapacity." [[maimonides|Maimonides]] adds a fourth [[causality|cause]] specific to his own time: "habit and education" — the t[[final-cause|end]]ency to accept without questioning what one has been raised to believe (035-001).

> La manière de se préserver des erreurs de l'esprit: il ne devra rien trancher selon une première opinion qui lui viendrait, ni laisser aller ses pensées tout d'abord en les dirigeant résolument vers la connaissance de Dieu.
> (019-003)

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] emphasizes that [[wisdom|intellect]]ual haste is among the most dangerous sources of error. In Chapter 5, he warns the reader not to form a first opinion and pursue it resolutely, but to proceed with modesty and restraint, pausing to verify each step. He illustrates this with the biblical story of the [[israel|Israelites]] at Sinai, whose premature demand to see [[God]] directly was the "necessary result of the precipitation they had put into it before having perfected themselves" (019-004).

The *Guide* itself, [[maimonides|Maimonides]] insists, is deliberately structured to prevent error in unprepared readers. The famous rabbinic warning — "Do not study what is too obscure for you and do not scrutinize what is too hidden for you" (037-001) — becomes, in [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' hands, a methodological principle: the reader who forces his gaze on truths too subtle for his current capacity will not only fail to grasp them but will lose even what he previously understood. This is the cognitive equivalent of blinding oneself by staring at the sun (037-001).

[[maimonides|Maimonides]] also discusses error as it relates to the interpretation of Scripture. The literal sense of the "books of revelation" has led many to attribute corporeal qualities to [[god|God]] — an error that [[maimonides|Maimonides]]' entire project aims to correct. But he also ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that Scripture itself, through its use of [[Allegory]] and deliberate contradictions, is designed to produce a productive confusion that forces the serious reader to think philosophically (035-003).
