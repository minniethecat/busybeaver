## Definition

Renunciation (*Verzicht*, *Entsagung*) is the moral center of Goethe's "Elective Affinities" — not because the novel celebrates it, but because it places renunciation at the core of every ethical decision and then refuses to sentimentalize its cost. To renounce is to give up what one most desires, not because desire is wrong but because its fulfillment would injure others or violate the moral order. The novel traces renunciation across its characters with clinical precision, showing that the capacity to renounce is what distinguishes moral agents from mere desiring beings — and that this capacity exacts a price from which there is no recovery.

Goethe's treatment of renunciation draws on a long tradition — Christian asceticism, Stoic self-mastery, the Enlightenment's rational restraint of passion — but the novelist's contribution is to show that renunciation does not resolve desire but intensifies it. Those who renounce in "Elective Affinities" do not become serene; they become spectral. Ottilie after her renunciation is more powerful than Ottilie before it, and more dangerous. The novel's deepest insight is that renunciation, far from being the end of passion, is often passion's most potent form.

## In the Novel

Renunciation makes its first explicit appearance in the novel in a passage of extraordinary beauty and sadness. The narrator describes how Eduard, recognizing the impossibility of his love for Ottilie, must "renounce such a beautiful, noble inclination" (013-017) — *auf eine so schöne, edle Neigung Verzicht zu tun*. The phrasing is remarkable: renunciation is directed not at a vice but at something "beautiful" and "noble." The sentence captures the novel's moral complexity — what must be renounced is not base desire but genuine love, and the renunciation is therefore not a purification but a wound.

The language of renunciation returns with greater urgency in the novel's second part. The Major, speaking to Charlotte about Eduard and Ottilie's relationship, asks: "Do you believe you can renounce your rights, so directly renounce me?" (016-015) — *glaubst du denn wohl, auf deine Rechte, auf mich so geradehin Verzicht leisten zu sollen?* Here renunciation becomes a question of rights — legal, moral, relational. To renounce one's claim on a spouse is not merely a personal act but a legal one with consequences that radiate outward. The Major's question makes explicit what the novel's earlier treatment of renunciation had left implicit: that renunciation is never purely personal, that it always involves others who may or may not consent to being renounced.

In Chapter 19, the narrator describes how Ottilie and her companions "renounce the privilege of being buried in the churches" (019-014) — *auf das Vorrecht Verzicht tun, in den Kirchen persönlich begraben zu werden.* The passage extends renunciation from the domain of love to the domain of death, suggesting that the logic of giving up what one values applies to every dimension of human life, including its final arrangements. Renunciation is not an occasional moral exercise but a permanent posture toward existence.

The novel's most intimate account of renunciation comes in Chapter 30, when Charlotte reflects: "it remained impossible for me to completely renounce it" (030-005) — *es mir unmöglich blieb, völlig Verzicht darauf zu tun.* The confession is striking for its honesty: Charlotte acknowledges that even her best efforts at renunciation are incomplete. She cannot fully give up what she has resolved to give up. The sentence is the novel's most humane moment on the subject — a recognition that renunciation is never clean, never total, that something of the renounced object always remains.

The novel's ultimate renunciation, however, is Ottilie's. Her decision to withdraw into silence, to refuse food, to die — this is renunciation in its most extreme form, the renunciation not merely of a particular desire but of desire itself. It is also the novel's most troubling act, for Ottilie's self-renunciation does not restore order but sanctifies disorder. The lovers buried side by side in the chapel are a monument to renunciation's failure — not failure to renounce, but failure of renunciation to heal what it has sacrificed.

## Related Characters

- [[Ottilie]] — the novel's supreme renunciant; her self-denial in the final chapters is renunciation taken to its ultimate limit, becoming indistinguishable from self-destruction
- [[Charlotte]] — the character who most honestly confronts the incompleteness of renunciation; her confession that she "could not completely renounce" (030-005) is the novel's most truthful statement on the subject
- [[Eduard]] — the character who most strenuously resists renunciation; his speech against voluntary loss (035-012) is the novel's case against renunciation, and the novel's ending is its rebuttal
- [[the Major]] — the character who poses renunciation as a legal-moral question (016-015), forcing Charlotte to confront what she is being asked to give up
