## Definition

Pain (*Schmerz*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" designates the emotional suffering that accompanies the dissolution and reconfiguration of human bonds. It is not a mere negative sensation but a central affective state through which characters register the cost of their shifting affinities.

The novel treats pain as an index of attachment: the deeper the bond that has been formed or broken, the more acute the pain. In this sense, pain is the subjective measure of the chemical affinities operating between persons. It marks the point at which a relationship that had been sustaining becomes a source of torment.

## In the Novel

Pain permeates the narrative from the moment the characters' carefully balanced arrangement begins to unravel. Eduard, Charlotte, the Captain, and Ottilie each experience distinct forms of suffering as their elective affinities draw them toward new configurations and away from older commitments.

Charlotte's pain is the most stoically borne. After the death of the child, she "surrendered herself wholly to her grief, a passion, a suffering" (010-041), yet she maintains composure in the presence of others. The narrator observes her "wavering between hope and pain, between tears and cheerfulness" (018-005), capturing the oscillating quality of grief that the novel renders with clinical precision.

Eduard's pain is more demonstrative: at the novel's climax, "his present suffering seemed to make him indifferent to everything" (034-001), and later "he felt his pain no more, he was entirely beside himself" (034-002). His suffering transforms into a kind of self-annihilating devotion.

Ottilie's pain is the most interior and ultimately the most consequential. She endures what the narrator calls a "dull pain" — "rarely had a duller pain seized her than this clarity" (028-023). Her suffering drives her toward silence, renunciation, and finally self-starvation.
