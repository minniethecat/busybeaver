## Definition

Family (*Familie*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" designates the domestic household as a social unit — the primary sphere within which the novel's drama of shifting attachments unfolds. It is presented not as a fixed biological fact but as a constructed arrangement, constantly negotiated and vulnerable to dissolution.

The family circle is the container for the novel's chemical experiment: it is the "compound" into which new elements are introduced and within which affinities are tested. The narrative tracks the transformation of one family configuration (Eduard–Charlotte) into a threatened new one (the fourfold arrangement) and ultimately into the posthumous family constituted by Eduard and Ottilie lying side by side in the chapel.

## In the Novel

The family at the novel's center is Eduard and Charlotte's — a marriage reunited after youthful separation, now settled into the routines of estate management. Their household is initially described as an "unusual way of life for a family, composed of the given persons" (007-005), already hinting at the constructed and precarious character of the arrangement.

Family appears repeatedly as a locus of both comfort and constraint. The narrator observes that "in all families where she visited, she inquired..." (024-001), making the family the unit of social observation. A neighboring family serves as a moral foil, one that "conducted itself and its members on a certain footing of propriety" (019-007).

The novel's closing image is of a new kind of family — not the conventional marital household but the pair united in death: Eduard and Ottilie in the chapel, "from the family circle into a wilderness" (028-051). This final transformation suggests that the elective affinities that disrupted the biological family ultimately produce their own, stranger form of kinship.
