# der Major

## Overview

**Der Major** (the Major) is an old military friend of [[Eduard]] who reappears
in the novel's final movement (Chapters 30–31). His return, motivated by
affection and concern, inadvertently accelerates the catastrophe. As a man of
honor and practical judgment, the Major represents the world of duty and order
that the novel's central characters have left behind — and his attempts to
restore that order only deepen the disorder.

## Background

The Major's relationship with Eduard predates the events of the novel. They
share a military past, and the Major's affection for Eduard is genuine and
longstanding. When Eduard, in desperation over his separation from [[Ottilie]],
summons his old friend, the Major responds without hesitation:

> vor allen Dingen berief er den Major. Die Freude des Wiedersehens war groß.
> (030-001)

The reunion is joyful, but the context is desperate — Eduard has retreated from
the estate, living in isolation, and the Major arrives to find his friend
transformed by passion and suffering.

## Personality

The Major is characterized by clear-sightedness and directness. Where
[[der-graf|the Count]] offers worldly rationalizations and Mittler offers
impulsive advice, the Major offers honest assessment. He does not flatter Eduard
or indulge his fantasies:

## Key Events

### The Summons (Chapter 30)

Eduard, isolated and despairing, calls for the Major as a last resort. The
Major's arrival brings a temporary sense of hope — but also brings the outside
world's norms and judgments into Eduard's private world of passion.

> Du löschest mit wenig Zügen alles aus, was ich in dieser ganzen
> Zeit gelitten habe.
> (030-006)

Eduard's claim that the Major "extinguishes" his suffering with a few words is
both gratitude and delusion — the suffering is not extinguished, only briefly
distracted.

### The Embassy to Charlotte (Chapter 31)

The Major's central action is his embassy to [[Charlotte]] on Eduard's behalf.
He rides to the estate with the task of negotiating a reconciliation — or at
least an arrangement. The mission is doomed from the start, not because the
Major lacks skill, but because the forces he is attempting to negotiate with are
not susceptible to negotiation:

> der Major soll die Sache Charlotten dringend vorstellen
> (031-004)

His inability to change the situation is not a personal failure but a
confirmation of the novel's thesis: elective affinities operate beyond the reach
of friendly intervention.

## Thematic Significance

The Major embodies the novel's critique of practical reason. Like Mittler, he is
well-intentioned; unlike Mittler, he is competent. But competence is
insufficient when the problem is not logistical but existential. The Major can
arrange meetings, deliver messages, propose solutions — but he cannot alter the
chemical logic that has reorganized the characters' bonds.
