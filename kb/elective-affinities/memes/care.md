## Definition

**Care** (*Sorge*) in *Elective Affinities* operates on two distinct planes:
practical concern for the well-being of others and inner anxiety that isolates
the self. The German *Sorge* encompasses both — it is the work of maintenance
(estate, child, social order) and the private weight of worry.

Goethe distributes care unevenly among his characters, and this distribution is
itself a moral map of the novel. Those who bear care are those who bear
responsibility. Those who are "without care" (*ohne Sorge*) are those who evade
the novel's ethical demands.

## In the Novel

The novel's programmatic statement on care appears early, when Charlotte
articulates a wish to share burdens with Eduard:

> … tragen beiderseits dieselben Sorgen in einem treuen, freundschaf…
> (002-011)

To bear the same cares together — *dieselben Sorgen* — is the marital ideal.
The very next line introduces the contrary longing:

> „Wenn wir nur etwas, das uns Sorge macht, aus unserer Gegenwart …
> (002-012)

To banish care from the present — this wish contains the germ of the novel's
tragedy. Care, once banished, returns in more consequential forms.

Charlotte is the novel's primary bearer of care. She worries about the practical
infrastructure of life — the vessels, the estate improvements, the child:

> …Gefäße hatte ihr schon manche Sorge gemacht. Sie ließ sich hierüb…
> (004-012)

The vessels (*Gefäße*) are concrete objects, yet the care they occasion is real.
Charlotte's care attaches to things because things sustain people.

The Count, a character of aristocratic detachment, utters the novel's most
breezy dismissal of care:

> „Nur ohne Sorge!“ sagte der Graf; „die Barone…
> (011-010)

"Only without care!" — *Nur ohne Sorge!* — is the voice of privilege, the
assurance that social position can insulate against consequence. The novel
quietly contradicts this assurance.

After Ottilie's pregnancy is announced, the practical dimension of care sharpens.
Eduard entrusts Charlotte:

> …nicht unter fremden Menschen. Sorge für sie, behandle sie wie son…
> (016-025)

*Sorge für sie* — "care for her" — is the imperative of responsibility. Care
here is not abstract worry but concrete action: the provision of protection.

Following the child's death, care shifts from the practical to the existential:

> …r zu befürchten. Ihre nächste Sorge, nachdem das Bewußtsein wiede…
> (017-005)

Her next care after consciousness returned — *ihre nächste Sorge* — is the
care that succeeds catastrophe. Care does not end with tragedy; it is what
fills the space that tragedy leaves.

Ottilie, in her isolation, carries her cares in solitude:

> …en, um ihre Betrachtungen und Sorgen, die sie niemanden mitteilen…
> (021-004)

Cares that she shared with no one — *die sie niemanden mitteilen*. The
isolation of care is the novel's deepest grief. Care unshared becomes a
private burden, a weight carried in silence.

The assistant, by contrast, embodies care as joyful service:

> …ein. Freudig übernahm er jede Sorge und Bemühung, und weil er mit…
> (019-002)

Joyfully he undertook every care — *freudig übernahm er jede Sorge*. The
assistant's care is the novel's model of ethical action: care as willing
engagement rather than anxious weight.

The novel's final reflection on care comes through death. The care for the
beloved remains extends beyond the living:

> …r tränenvollen Nacht fiel die Sorge, die geliebten Reste zu besta…
> (036-020)

The care to bury the beloved remains — *die Sorge, die geliebten Reste zu
bestatten* — is the last care, the care that attends the dead. It is the
care that cannot be evaded.

## Related Characters

  narrative in material reality

  care into isolation
- [[Eduard]] — largely exempt from care; his passions displace worry
