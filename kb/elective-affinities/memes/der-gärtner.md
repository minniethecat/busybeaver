## Overview

Der Gärtner is the gardener of Eduard's estate, appearing from the opening chapter to the novel's conclusion. A devoted and skilled servant, he tends the gardens, orchards, and greenhouse, and serves as a quiet witness to the estate's changing fortunes.

## Biography

The gardener first appears in Chapter 1, joining Eduard in his garden work and discussing the new installations — the moss hut, the footbridge, and the expanding vistas (001-001, 001-003, 001-005). He shares Eduard's enthusiasm for the estate improvements and carries out his instructions with diligence.

In Part II, the gardener continues his work but feels the absence of the master. As the harvest promises abundance, he thinks constantly of Eduard, wishing for his return to see the fruits of the estate's labor (017-019). When a visitor remarks on the garden's flourishing state, the gardener replies cautiously, wishing only that the master were there to enjoy it (017-020).

During Luciane's tumultuous visit, the gardener, along with Ottilie, is distressed by the chaos that disrupts the carefully tended grounds and the social order of the estate (023-009). Charlotte later consoles him for the damage done (027-001). In the novel's later chapters, Ottilie visits him daily in the castle garden, finding solace in his quiet company and the ordered natural world he maintains (028-006).

## Relationships

- **[[Eduard]]** — The gardener is devoted to his master, thinking of him constantly during his absence and wishing for his return to appreciate the estate's beauty (017-019).
- **[[Charlotte]]** — She respects the gardener's work and consoles him after disruptions to the garden (027-001).
- **[[Ottilie]]** — She finds comfort in the gardener's company, visiting him daily in the garden during the novel's later chapters (028-006).
- **[[Luciane]]** — Her chaotic visit upsets the gardener's carefully maintained order (023-009).

## Role in the Novel

The gardener represents steadfastness and devotion to craft in a world of shifting human attachments. While the aristocratic characters grapple with passion, renunciation, and the rearrangement of bonds, the gardener simply continues his work — planting, tending, harvesting. His constancy and his loyalty to the absent Eduard provide a quiet counterpoint to the novel's central drama of infidelity and elective affinities.
