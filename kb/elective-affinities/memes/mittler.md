# Mittler

## Overview

**Mittler** (literally "the Mediator") is one of Goethe's most psychologically
acute comic creations in *Elective Affinities*. A self-styled resolver of
disputes, Mittler dashes about the countryside on horseback, inserting himself
into the lives of others with impulsive energy that makes situations worse.
His name compelled him, the narrator notes, to adopt
"this strangest of vocations."

Mittler functions as a living critique of the belief that human conflicts can be
resolved through rational intervention. Each time he attempts to fix something,
the novel's deeper forces — *[[Neigung]]*, *[[Leidenschaft]]*, the chemical
logic of elective affinity — reassert themselves. He is the anti-mediator:
the proof that some bonds cannot be negotiated.

## Background and Origins

Mittler is introduced in Chapter 2 as a figure of almost comical energy. His
arrival at the estate is announced by the sound of his horse in the courtyard,
and he immediately launches into a recitation of his recent good deeds — settling
disputes, reconciling neighbors, arranging marriages:

> Herr Mittler ist in den Schloßhof gesprengt. Er rief uns alle
> zusammen, wir sollen Sie aufsuchen, wir sollen Sie fragen, es sei
> gleichviel, was es sei, ob es geraten werden könne.
> (002-019)

He is an old acquaintance of [[Charlotte]] and [[Eduard]], part of the loose
network of gentry who circulate through the estate. His precise background is
never detailed — he simply exists as a function, a man whose identity has been
entirely absorbed by his self-chosen role.

The narrator's comment on his name is revealing:

> wenn man auf seine Art gelten lassen will, daß der Name des
> Menschen, wie an der Stirn, geschrieben stehe, so möchte wohl
> behaupten, der Name Mittler habe ihn genötigt, diese seltsamste
> aller Bestimmungen zu ergreifen.
> (002-032)

The idea that his name "compelled" him into his vocation suggests a man whose
character is an act of submission to an external label — a theme that resonates
with the novel's broader concern with forces that operate beyond individual
choice.

## Personality and Character

Mittler is defined by the tension between his self-image as a wise counselor and
the reality of his impulsive, often destructive interventions. Goethe renders
him with a mixture of affection and irony:

> Der wunderliche Mann, unser Mittler
> (002-038)

Charlotte's characterization — "the strange man, our Mittler" — captures the
affectionate exasperation he provokes. He is tolerated, even welcomed, but never
taken entirely seriously.

| Trait | Manifestation | PN Reference |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| Impulsive energy | Arrives unannounced, speaks rapidly, acts before thinking | 002-019 |
| Self-appointed authority | Takes on disputes without being asked | 002-030 |
| Comic seriousness | Utterly convinced of his own wisdom | 018-019 |
| Rash temperament | "sein rasches Naturell" — quick to judge, quick to act | 018-014 |

His most significant character note is his quickness — both a strength (he gets
things done) and a fatal flaw (he acts without understanding). The narrator
repeatedly uses *rasch* (quick, rash) to describe him, emphasizing that his
failures are not failures of intention but of pace. He moves too fast for the
slow, gravitational logic of human relationships.

## Key Relationships

### Eduard

Mittler's relationship with [[Eduard]] is perhaps his most consequential. When
Eduard, already drifting away from Charlotte and toward [[Ottilie]], seeks
Mittler's counsel, the mediator's response is characteristically blunt — and
characteristically unhelpful:

> Lächeln Sie nicht, lieber Mittler, oder lächeln Sie auch! O ich
> möchte nicht leben, wenn sie nicht mehr fortdauern könnte.
> (018-011)

Eduard's desperation meets Mittler's impatience, and the result is a
conversation that deepens rather than resolves the crisis. Mittler cannot
comprehend the force of Eduard's passion because his own emotional range does
not include such depths.

### Charlotte

[[Charlotte]], with her characteristic clarity, sees Mittler for what he is:
well-meaning but unreliable. She values his goodwill but does not depend on his
judgment:

> diesmal, wie schon öfters, über Mittlern unzufrieden
> (018-030)

Her dissatisfaction with him "this time, as often before" suggests a long
pattern of disappointment — he is a fixture of their social world, but not a
resource they can truly rely upon.

### The Wider Social World

Mittler's true function in the novel is to represent the inadequacy of social
convention in the face of passion. He embodies the world of practical
arrangements — marriages, inheritances, godparent letters — that the novel's
central characters are in the process of transcending (or destroying). His
busyness is the busyness of a world that no longer applies:

> Tauf- und Gevatterbriefe übernahm Mittler; sie sollten gleich
> ausgefertigt werden
> (026-017)

Even as the novel's central relationships collapse, Mittler busies himself with
baptismal certificates and godparent letters — the paperwork of a social order
that has already been rendered irrelevant by the passions it cannot contain.

## Key Events

### First Appearance (Chapter 2)

Mittler bursts into the novel like the force of nature he aspires to manage.
His first conversation with Charlotte and Eduard establishes his character:
voluble, self-satisfied, and entirely unaware of the gap between his intentions
and his effects. (002-019–002-038)

### The Intervention (Chapter 18)

Mittler's most significant narrative function occurs in Chapter 18, when Eduard,
in a state of desperation over his separation from Ottilie, seeks his advice.
The conversation is a masterpiece of dramatic irony: Eduard pours out his soul,
and Mittler — unable to grasp the depth of what he is hearing — responds with
bromides and practical suggestions that entirely miss the point. (018-005–018-030)

### Late-Novel Activities (Chapter 26)

In the novel's final section, Mittler reappears as a background functionary,
handling the bureaucratic aftermath of births and deaths. His presence at this
late stage underscores the novel's structure: the world of practical arrangements
continues its mechanical operation even as the human drama has passed beyond its
reach. (026-015–026-017)

## Thematic Significance

Mittler represents the novel's most sustained argument against Enlightenment
faith in reason. He is not a villain — he genuinely wants to help — but his
failure is structural, not personal. The forces that drive [[Eduard]] toward
[[Ottilie]], that dissolve the bond between [[Charlotte]] and Eduard, that lead
to [[the-childs-death|the child's death]], are not forces that can be "mediated."

They are, as the novel's chemical metaphor insists, natural laws — and one does
not negotiate with natural law.

| Dimension | Mittler | The Novel's Forces |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Mode | Rational intervention | Natural affinity |
| Pace | Quick, impulsive | Slow, gravitational |
| Understanding | Surface-level | Depth psychology |
| Outcome | Chaos despite good intentions | Order through natural logic |
