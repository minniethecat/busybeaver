# Order

## Definition

**Order** (*Ordnung*) in *Elective Affinities* is the external manifestation of
rational will. It is what Charlotte imposes on papers in drawers, on gardens
along sightlines, on the rhythms of domestic life — and what the novel's
chemistry lesson reveals to be far more fragile than it appears. Goethe presents
order as a genuine human achievement and a genuine human need, but also as
something that nature (including human nature) does not spontaneously produce.
Order must be actively maintained, and the novel measures the cost of that
maintenance with unsparing clarity.

## In the Novel

Charlotte is the novel's architect of order. From the opening chapters, she is
organizing, arranging, bringing chaos into form:

> der Wust in eine erfreuliche Ordnung gebracht, lag rubriziert in
> beschriebenen Kapseln
> (004-003)

The papers are "brought into a pleasing order," sorted into labeled boxes — a
small triumph of classification that prefigures the larger ordering of the
estate's grounds and the household's routines.

Ottilie proves to be Charlotte's equal in this domain, grasping the order of the
household with intuitive speed:

> Ottilie hatte schnell die ganze Ordnung eingesehen
> (006-006)

Her ability to internalize order without being explicitly taught positions her
as Charlotte's natural successor in the domestic sphere — and makes her eventual
departure from that order all the more devastating.

The novel connects order to cleanliness and moral rectitude through the motif of
the Swiss model village:

> sondern zur Schweizer Ordnung und Sauberkeit, welche die
> Bequemlichkeit andeutete
> (006-026)

"Swiss order and cleanliness" is a phrase that recurs, suggesting a cultural
ideal of ordered domesticity that the estate is meant to embody.

Sunday becomes the ritual day for renewing order:

> es ward ihnen angenehmen Pflicht gemacht, wenigstens jeden
> Sonntag, diese Reinlichkeit, diese Ordnung zu erneuern
> (009-025)

The weekly renewal of cleanliness and order — "this cleanliness, this order" —
is a small liturgy of domestic virtue. The repetition of "diese" gestures toward
an order so familiar that it has become a proper name.

Yet the novel also shows order breaking down. After the child's death,
Charlotte's insistence on order becomes strained:

> Ernst und Geduld und Ordnung mehr als sonst üben
> (013-004)

She must practice "earnestness and patience and order more than usual" — order
is no longer natural but an effort, something to be "practiced" (*üben*). This
is the moment when the novel's architecture of order begins to crack.

The garden, once the supreme expression of ordered will, descends into
randomness:

> einzelnen Partieen, ohne Rang und Ordnung, auf dem Platz
> gemächlich ein
> (015-002)

"Without rank and order" — the estate that was Charlotte's life's work reverts
to casual formlessness. The final planting follows "a certain order" but only
from the edge inward:

> Nach einer gewissen Ordnung sollten vom Ende heran die neuen
> Bäume gesetzt werden
> (019-005)

The order is now merely conventional, not vital — a gesture of form rather than
an expression of will.

## Chemical Metaphor

Order corresponds to the *crystalline state* — the regular, predictable
arrangement of elements in a stable lattice. But the chemical lesson also
teaches that every crystal can be dissolved. Introduce a new reagent with
sufficient affinity, and the ordered structure collapses into solution. The
novel's plot is precisely this dissolution: the ordered estate, the ordered
marriage, the ordered self — all dissolve when elective affinity is introduced
into the system.

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — order's primary representative; her need for order is genuine
  and her skill in creating it is admirable, but the novel asks whether order
  can survive the disruption of desire
- [[Ottilie]] — Charlotte's unexpected ally in order, then the agent of its
  dissolution; her tragedy is that her very virtue disrupts the order she was
  meant to sustain
- [[Eduard]] — order's antagonist; he is impatient with classification,
  restless in routine, and his pursuit of passion dismantles the structures
  others have built
- [[der Hauptmann|the Captain]] — a figure of professional order (military,
  engineering), but his orderly competence is no protection against the
  disorder of the heart
