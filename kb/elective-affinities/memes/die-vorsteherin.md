## Overview

Die Vorsteherin is the headmistress of the boarding school (Pension) where [[Ottilie]] was raised and educated. Though she never appears in person at the estate, her presence is felt throughout the novel through her letters to [[Charlotte]] and her role in shaping Ottilie's character.

## Biography

The headmistress first enters the narrative in Chapter 2, when Charlotte recalls how she presented the young Ottilie as a model pupil at school examinations (002-010). She maintains regular correspondence with Charlotte, providing detailed reports on Ottilie's progress and character.

In Chapter 3, she writes a lengthy letter explaining that Ottilie, despite her excellence as a student, has not performed well in examinations — a fact that concerns the headmistress and prompts her to suggest Ottilie's future path (003-029). She delegates the writing of letters to Ottilie as a form of practice, revealing her pedagogical approach (003-031).

When Ottilie's academic struggles become more apparent, the headmistress writes again in Chapter 5, comparing herself to a good shepherd who cares for each member of her flock individually (005-006). She takes Charlotte aside during a visit to discuss Ottilie's situation seriously, demonstrating her genuine concern for her pupil's welfare (005-010).

In Part II, the headmistress appears in person (or is at least physically present in the narrative world), visiting the area in connection with plans for Ottilie's return to the school (025-031). The assistant ([[der Gehülfe]]) later returns to her with reports and hopes for the future (026-011).

## Relationships

- **[[Ottilie]]** — The headmistress has raised and educated Ottilie, taking a maternal interest in her development. She recognizes Ottilie's unique character but is concerned about her academic performance (003-029, 005-006).
- **[[Charlotte]]** — Charlotte receives the headmistress's letters and shares responsibility for decisions about Ottilie's education and future (003-029).
- **[[der Gehülfe]]** — The assistant teacher works under the headmistress's authority and reports back to her from his travels (026-011).

## Role in the Novel

The headmistress serves as an offstage authority figure who represents the world of order, education, and social convention that exists beyond the estate. Her letters provide important exposition about Ottilie's character before the reader meets her directly. The headmistress's concern about Ottilie's academic performance — she is an excellent person but a poor examination-taker — foreshadows the central tension in Ottilie's character: her inner worth is not easily measured by external standards.
