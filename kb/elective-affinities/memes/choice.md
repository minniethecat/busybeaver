## Definition

**Choice** (*Wahl*) is the word at the center of Goethe's title — and the
concept the novel most thoroughly unsettles. The compound
*Wahlverwandtschaften* (elective affinities) promises that human bonds are a
matter of *Wahl*, of election. The novel spends its entire length testing that
promise and finding it false.

Goethe stages a debate between two models of human relationships: the chemical,
in which elements combine according to fixed laws of affinity, and the ethical,
in which persons choose their bonds freely. The novel's title sides
rhetorically with choice — *Wahl*-verwandtschaften — but its plot sides with
necessity. What the characters experience as choice is, the novel increasingly
suggests, something closer to chemical reaction.

## In the Novel

The concept enters the novel through the Captain's exposition of the chemical
theory that gives the work its name:

> …hr berechtigt, sogar das Wort Wahlverwandtschaft anzuwenden, wei…
> (004-044)

"So we may well be justified," said the Captain, "in applying the term elective
affinity…" The Captain presents the term as a scientific metaphor, a way of
describing how chemical substances "choose" their partners. He speaks with the
confidence of the natural scientist describing a law.

Charlotte immediately objects to the word *Wahl*:

> …r ich würde hier niemals eine Wahl, eher eine Naturnotwendigkeit…
> (004-045)

"Forgive me," said Charlotte, "but I would never call that a choice — rather a
natural necessity." Charlotte's objection is the novel's thesis in miniature.
She recognizes that what the Captain calls "choice" is in fact *Naturnotwendigkeit*
— natural necessity. The chemical substances do not choose; they follow their
natures. The question the novel poses is whether human beings are any different.

Charlotte presses her point with ethical force:

> …r hier mit den schönen Worten Wahl und Wahlverwandtschaft etwas …
> (004-049)

"If conscience," Charlotte replied, "prompts you to use such fine words as
choice and elective affinity…" She warns against the seduction of language
itself — the "fine words" (*schönen Worten*) that make chemical determinism
sound like freedom. The novel is suspicious of its own central metaphor.

The Captain defends the scientific usage:

> …en zu und hält das Kunstwort 'Wahlverwandtschaften' für vollkomm…
> (004-051)

The Captain conceded this and held the technical term "elective affinities"
to be perfectly appropriate. The word *Kunstwort* — technical term, art-word —
is significant. It is an artifice, a human construction laid over natural
processes. The novel's whole drama unfolds in the gap between the artifice
and the nature it purports to describe.

Charlotte gets the last word in this debate — and it is a word of caution:

> …ffen und daß diese Natur- und Wahlverwandtschaften unter uns ein…
> (004-058)

"If even the example," said Charlotte, "as it seems to me, does not quite fit
our case…" She insists on the difference between chemical substances and human
persons. But the novel that follows her statement will systematically erode
that distinction.

A second appearance of *Wahl* comes during Luciane's visit, in the context of
improvised entertainment:

> …sich unentschlossen, ließ die Wahl, bat wie ein Improvisator um …
> (022-012)

She hesitated, left the choice, asked like an improviser for a theme. Here
*Wahl* is trivialized — it is the choice of what game to play, what song to
sing. Luciane treats choice as entertainment, a freedom exercised in the
smallest possible sphere. The novel places this light usage against the
heavy choice — whom to love, whom to bind oneself to — that its central
characters face and fail to master.

The novel's final formulation of the problem of choice comes as an aphorism
about education and distraction:

> „Abwechselung ohne Zerstreuung wäre für Lehre und Leben der schönste
> Wahlspruch…
> (025-009)

"Variety without distraction would be for teaching and life the finest motto."
The word *Wahlspruch* — motto, literally "choice-saying" — condenses the
novel's ambivalence. A motto is a chosen principle, a freely adopted rule. But
the novel has spent hundreds of pages demonstrating that the most important
things in life are not chosen at all.

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — the novel's most articulate critic of the "choice" metaphor;
  she sees from the beginning that what is called choice may be natural
  necessity in disguise
- [[Eduard]] — insists he has chosen freely but acts with the predictability
  of a chemical reagent; the novel's demonstration that the feeling of choice
  is not the fact of it
- [[the Captain]] — introduces the chemical metaphor and defends its
  terminology; his scientific confidence masks the metaphor's dangerous
  implications
- [[Ottilie]] — the character for whom choice is most dramatically foreclosed;
  her love for Eduard feels to her not like a choice but like a fate
