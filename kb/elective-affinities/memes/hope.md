## Definition

**Hope** (*Hoffnung*) in *Elective Affinities* is the forward-looking emotion
that animates decision and sustains endurance. Unlike the static contentment of
[[Happiness|happiness]], hope is inherently temporal — it projects into a future
that may or may not arrive. Goethe treats hope with characteristic ambivalence:
it is the indispensable fuel of human action, without which no character would
move or change, yet it is also the source of the novel's deepest suffering when
its object proves impossible.

## In the Novel

Hope enters the novel early, in a domestic register. Eduard surveys his estate in
late winter and finds comfort in anticipation:

> Er freute sich daran in Hoffnung, daß der Frühling bald alles …
> (001-009)

This seasonal hope — the expectation that spring will restore the garden — is the
novel's gentlest form of the emotion, and perhaps the only one that is reliably
fulfilled.

When Charlotte and the Captain discuss the child, hope takes on a more charged
character. Charlotte is found to be in "good hope" — the German idiom for
pregnancy (*guter Hoffnung*):

> …nders sein, da Sie mich guter Hoffnung finden.
> (018-024)

The phrase is the novel's most concentrated double-meaning: "you find me in good
hope" means both "I am pregnant" and "I am hopeful" — the biological and the
emotional fused in a single expression. The narrator immediately reinforces the
resonance of this phrase:

> … Worte wirkt eine solche gute Hoffnung, die fürwahr die beste Hoffnu…
> (018-027)

Eduard, inflamed by wine and longing, embodies hope at its most reckless:

> …duard, aufgeregt von Wein und Hoffnung, mit Ottilien an einem Fenste…
> (010-051)

His hope is erotic and impatient — a demand rather than a patient expectation.

The novel's most sustained meditation on hope comes through the figure of the
assistant's account of Ottilie's early years. She describes a state of suspension
between hope and pain:

> …nem ewigen Schwanken zwischen Hoffnung und Schmerz, zwischen Tränen …
> (018-005)

This oscillation — *zwischen Hoffnung und Schmerz*, between hope and pain — is
the novel's definitive emotional rhythm. Hope and suffering are not opposites but
neighbors, and the novel's characters shuttle perpetually between them.

Eduard, contemplating divorce, nourishes hope even when circumstances argue
against it:

> …ne sich ausdachte, mancherlei Hoffnungen nährte. Er konnte sich nich…
> (018-003)

Ottilie, in her final state of renunciation, offers a hope that reaches beyond
death — the consoling prospect of being reunited with the beloved in the
afterlife:

> …er zu bringen, die tröstliche Hoffnung, dereinst unmittelbar neben i…
> (019-009)

The novel's darkest pronouncement on hope comes near its end, in the observation
that certain passions are diseases without cure:

> …schaften sind Krankheiten ohne Hoffnung. Was sie heilen könnte, macht…
> (022-057)

"Passions are illnesses without hope" — the statement is absolute. What could
heal them only makes them worse. It is the novel's diagnosis of its own
characters, and it lands with the finality of a medical verdict.

## Related Characters

- [[Eduard]] — hope's most reckless practitioner; his hopes are demands that the
  world refuses to satisfy
- [[Charlotte]] — her "good hope" (*guter Hoffnung*) is the novel's pivotal
  double-meaning, fusing biology and emotion
- [[Ottilie]] — her hope is transfigured into the transcendent; she hopes not for
  earthly union but for eternal proximity
- [[Mittler]] — the mediator who intervenes precisely when hope is most fragile,
  with results that foreclose it entirely
