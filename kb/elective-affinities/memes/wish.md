## Definition

The wish (*Wunsch*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" occupies a distinctive position in the spectrum of human desire. Unlike passion, which is overwhelming and involuntary, or inclination, which is a natural tendency, the wish is a conscious, articulated expression of longing.

Wishes in the novel function as narrative catalysts: once uttered, they set chains of events in motion, often with consequences that far exceed the original intention. The wish represents the threshold where private desire enters the social world through language.

## In the Novel

Wishes proliferate and frequently serve as turning points. Eduard's wish to invite the Captain to the estate — a seemingly innocent expression of friendship — initiates the entire plot (001-024). Charlotte's wish for a rural park life is another (006-026).

The wish carries an almost talismanic quality. Characters express wishes that in retrospect appear prophetic. The "pious wish" (025-028) and the "quite natural wish" (023-030) reveal characters' innermost orientations.

The wish differs from action: one may wish without acting, yet the mere expression of a wish can precipitate action in others. This gap is one of the novel's central moral problems — characters are judged by what they allow themselves to wish for as much as by what they do.

## Chemical Metaphor

In the novel's chemical framework, the wish corresponds to the initial condition that determines the direction of a reaction. The chemist's choice of which reagents to combine sets the terms for what follows. A wish is not the reaction itself but the act that brings elements into proximity, making certain outcomes inevitable.
