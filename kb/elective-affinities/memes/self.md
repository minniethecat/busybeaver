## Definition

The self (*Selbst*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" is rarely discussed in the abstract. Instead, Goethe approaches selfhood through a rich vocabulary of compounds — *Selbstliebe* (self-love), *Selbstbewußtsein* (self-awareness), *Selbstbeherrschung* (self-control), *Selbstbetrug* (self-deception), *Selbständigkeit* (independence), *Selbstbeschränkung* (self-limitation), and *Selbstgefühl* (self-feeling). This grammatical pattern is itself a thesis: the self is not a stable substance but a site of ongoing operations — one loves, deceives, controls, limits, feels *oneself* into being.

The novel's deepest contribution to the philosophy of selfhood is its demonstration that these self-operations are not purely internal. They are shaped, enabled, and disrupted by the presence of others. The self in "Elective Affinities" is a chemical compound, not an atom — its properties emerge from its affinities with other selves.

## In the Novel

The vocabulary of self-compounds maps directly onto the novel's central characters and their trajectories. Eduard is introduced through the lens of *Selbstliebe* (self-love): the narrator notes dryly that "he liked to believe it was the best, as his self-love would have it appear" (007-001). His self-love is the engine of his actions throughout — it is Eduard who most consistently treats his own desires as natural laws.

Charlotte embodies *Selbstbeherrschung* (self-control), described admiringly as possessing "this self-control in extraordinary cases" (010-045). Her self-mastery is the counterpoint to Eduard's impulsivity, but the novel is subtle: self-control is not an unqualified virtue. Charlotte's composure can shade into emotional withholding, and her mastery of self can become a barrier to intimacy.

Ottilie's trajectory traces *Selbständigkeit* (independence) — or rather, its difficult emergence. The narrator observes that when people "step over into the field of their own activity and independence" (025-028), they encounter both possibility and peril. Ottilie's growth into selfhood follows precisely this arc: her increasing independence from the social orbit of the estate is inseparable from her increasing vulnerability.

The critical negative terms are *Selbstbetrug* (self-deception) and *Selbstbeschränkung* (self-limitation). Charlotte warns against self-deception in the novel's most direct moral address: "Think honestly and without self-deception of Ottilie removed from our company" (016-012). And the narrator observes that "as soon as a true obstacle enters, self-limitation is immediately restored" (026-006). Self-deception and self-limitation are presented as paired dangers: the former is the refusal to see clearly, the latter the refusal to act fully. Between them lies the troubled territory of authentic selfhood.

The novel's most concentrated moment of self-address occurs in Eduard's desperate plea: "Unite her with me, with your beautiful self" (034-006). Here *Selbst* appears not as a compound but as a noun — *dein schönes Selbst*, "your beautiful self." The phrase is striking because it treats the self as something that can be *given*, a possession that can be shared. Eduard's language reveals his deepest assumption: that selves are not autonomous units but things that can be joined, exchanged, combined — chemical substances in the novel's governing metaphor.

In the final chapter, Ottilie's death is preceded by "a kind of serene self-satisfaction, a smile" (036-003), and followed by the suspicion of *Selbstmord* (suicide, literally "self-murder," 036-043). The arc from self-love to self-satisfaction to self-murder recapitulates the novel's tragic structure in the vocabulary of the self.
