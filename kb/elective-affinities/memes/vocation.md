## Definition

Vocation (*Bestimmung*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" refers to the inner determination that directs a person's life — a convergence of natural disposition, cultivated character, and chosen purpose.

It is distinct from fate (*Schicksal*), which is external and imposed, in that vocation arises from within: "character, individuality, inclination, direction, homeland, circumstances" (035-017). The concept bridges nature and freedom — one's *Bestimmung* is both given by innate disposition and chosen through decisions that shape a life.

## In the Novel

The question of vocation surfaces at critical moments. The earliest appearance concerns Eduard's "strangest of all determinations" (002-032), referring to the forces that shaped his marriage. The word recurs in discussions of Ottilie's education — what is her proper sphere?

The concept gains its deepest resonance in the final chapters. Charlotte recognizes Ottilie's "fortunate determination" to guide others while she herself has lost her way (033-014). The most significant articulation comes near the novel's end, enumerating all the elements constituting a person's vocation (035-017).

The novel's tragic dimension lies in misaligned vocations. Eduard pursues a vocation of passionate attachment that conflicts with duty. Ottilie's capacity for devotion finds no adequate social form. The Captain's vocation of rational improvement is subordinated to emotional chaos. Only Charlotte achieves a measure of equilibrium, and even this is fragile.

## Chemical Metaphor

In the novel's chemical framework, vocation corresponds to the specific affinity that determines which elements combine. Each chemical element has its characteristic bonding behavior — its "determination" toward certain partners.

Similarly, each person has a *Bestimmung* inclining them toward particular relationships. Tragedy occurs when different persons' determinations prove incompatible within the same social compound.
