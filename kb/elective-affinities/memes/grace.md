## Definition

Grace (*Anmut* in German) in *Elective Affinities* denotes a quality of spontaneous beauty in conduct — the unforced harmony of movement, speech, and disposition that signals an inner accord between inclination and moral goodness. *Anmut* occupies a privileged place in Goethe's aesthetic and ethical vocabulary: it is neither mere physical beauty (*Schönheit*) nor moral virtue (*Tugend*) alone, but the visible expression of their unity. A person who possesses *Anmut* acts beautifully without effort, and in that effortlessness, reveals that their moral nature and their natural impulse are not at war. The concept draws on Schiller's famous essay "On Grace and Dignity" (*Über Anmut und Würde*), in which grace is defined as beauty of movement under the sign of moral freedom.

## In the Novel

Grace is first attributed to Luciane in Chapter 2, where the narrator praises her "freedom of bearing, grace in dance, comfortable propriety" (002-010). This initial association of *Anmut* with social performance — dancing, bearing, ease among others — establishes the concept's worldly valence. Grace is what makes social being appear natural.

In Chapter 11, the concept takes on a paradoxical dynamic: "while stiff persons usually lose thereby in grace, those who are light and natural gain" (011-022). Grace is not a fixed possession but a quality that increases or diminishes depending on the situation and the inner state of the person. Ottilie is the novel's primary exemplar of this principle: her grace grows as she becomes more fully herself within the household.

The loss of grace marks crisis. In Chapter 13, the narrator notes that Eduard, consumed by passion, "lacked even his accustomed grace" (013-016) — the phrase registers that grace is fragile, dependent on inner composure, and lost when passion overwhelms equilibrium. Similarly, Chapter 14 insists that nothing disturbs "the grace of a celebration" more than the visible discomfort of its host (014-010).

Charlotte offers the novel's most explicit catalogue of graceful qualities in Chapter 17: "prudence, consideration for others, grace, and amiability themselves were most amiable" (017-008). Here *Anmut* is listed alongside practical wisdom and other-directed virtue, suggesting that grace is as much an ethical as an aesthetic quality — a mode of conduct that makes moral goodness attractive rather than forbidding.

Luciane embodies the instability of grace in the social world. Chapter 22 notes that "people were so accustomed to permitting her much on account of her grace" (022-030) — grace functions here as a social credit that can be drawn upon, and potentially overdrawn. In the novel's closing chapters, grace returns in its purest form: Ottilie, even in decline, appears "in youthful strength and grace, thrown back upon herself" (036-033), her *Anmut* now stripped of social performance and revealed as a quality of being rather than doing.

## Key Passages

> Sie gewann das Kind durch Freiheit des Betragens, Anmut im Tanze, schickliche Bequemlichkeit der Unterhaltung.
> (002-010)

> Wie steife Personen dadurch meist an Anmut verlieren, so gewinnen diejenigen, die von Natur leicht und bequem sind, unendlich dabei.
> (011-022)

> Eduard fühlte keine Neigung, sich im geringsten zu zerstreuen; er war in sich selbst genugsam beschäftigt, und der sonst so gesellige, mitteilende Mann ermangelte auch der gewohnten Anmut.
> (013-016)

> Klugheit, Schonung anderer, Anmut und Liebenswürdigkeit selbst waren liebenswürdig.
> (017-008)

> Man war so gewohnt, ihrer Anmut vieles zu erlauben, daß man zuletzt auch die Unart noch anmutig fand, womit sie dieselbe zu begleiten wußte.
> (022-030)

> Sie sahen das liebenswürdige Kind, in jugendlicher Kraft und Anmut, auf sich selbst zurückgewiesen.
> (036-033)
