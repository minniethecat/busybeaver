## Definition

Correspondence (*Briefwechsel*) in Goethe's *Elective Affinities* refers to the exchange of written letters between characters, serving as both a practical communication method and a significant narrative device. In the late 18th and early 19th centuries, letter-writing was the primary means of communication across distances, and Goethe employs it to reveal character psychology, advance the plot, and introduce dramatic irony.

## In the Novel

Letters permeate the narrative of *Elective Affinities* from the very first chapter. The novel opens with the correspondence between Eduard and the Captain (*der Hauptmann*), which catalyzes the central plot — the Captain's invitation to the estate. Charlotte's ongoing correspondence with the Captain concerning estate improvements provides a parallel epistolary thread that contrasts with the direct emotional exchanges among the four main characters.

The *Vorsteherin* (headmistress) of Ottilie's former boarding school contributes extended letters that function as character testimony, offering an external perspective on Ottilie's nature and development. These letters create a documentary layer within the novel, supplying biographical detail that the omniscient narrator withholds. Eduard's hurried letter to Charlotte after the Captain's departure marks a pivotal emotional turning point. The motif reaches its tragic culmination in Ottilie's silent period, during which written communication ceases entirely — a telling absence that underscores the breakdown of social convention and personal connection.

Letters in the novel are more than plot mechanics; they embody the tension between mediated and direct experience, between convention and genuine feeling. Eduard's reliance on letters reflects his preference for idealized, distant connection over the demands of present relationship, while Charlotte's practical correspondence mirrors her commitment to order and social form.
