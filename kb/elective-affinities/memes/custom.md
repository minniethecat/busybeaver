## Definition

**Custom** (*Sitte*, plural *Sitten*) in *Elective Affinities* refers to the
shared norms, manners, and conventions that govern social life. Goethe treats
custom with characteristic ambivalence: it is the civilizing achievement that
makes peaceful coexistence possible, but it is also the cage against which
passion presses.

The German *Sitte* carries a stronger ethical charge than the English "custom."
It encompasses not merely habitual behavior but morally freighted convention —
the borderland between law and habit, what one does because one *ought* to.
The novel repeatedly probes this borderland, asking whether customs are
guardrails or chains.

## In the Novel

The novel's early chemical conversations establish the framework. Eduard
compares social customs to the chemical "middle terms" that allow otherwise
incompatible substances to combine:

> …wie diese durch Sitten und Gesetze vereinbar sind, so gibt es auch in unserer chemischen Welt Mittelglieder, dasjenige zu verbinden, was sich einander abweist.
> (004-033)

This is a governing metaphor: customs are chemical mediators, making the
incompatible compatible — but also enabling combinations that might better
remain separate.

Midway through the novel, the narrator observes how thoroughly customs have
shifted for the household. The renovation of the church and its antiquities
makes the present feel strange:

> …ob es nicht ein Traum sei, daß man nunmehr in ganz andern Sitten, Gewohnheiten, Lebensweisen und Überzeugungen verweile.
> (020-005)

The arrival of the Count and Baroness in Chapter 23 makes custom visible as
performance. The men of rank and breeding cluster around the Count:

> Die Männer von Stand und Sitten umgaben den Grafen…
> (023-015)

The narrator then delivers one of the novel's most concentrated reflections on
custom, equating good manners with a substitute for violence:

> Durch das, was wir Betragen und gute Sitten mennen, soll das erreicht werden, was außerdem nur durch Gewalt oder auch nicht einmal durch Gewalt zu erreichen ist.
> (023-041)

This is a radical claim: customs achieve what force cannot. They are not mere
decoration but a technology of social peace.

The sentence that follows is equally compressed:

> Der Umgang mit Frauen ist das Element guter Sitten.
> (023-042)

Ottilie playfully proposes an addition to "the little book of good customs" —
a chapter on how to behave in art collections and museums:

> …wenn man künftig in das Büchlein von guten Sitten nach den Kapiteln, wie man sich in Gesellschaft beim Essen und Trinken benehmen soll, ein recht umständliches einschöbe…
> (024-013)

This moment of lightness reveals Ottilie's intimate familiarity with the
customs she quietly subverts.

In the novel's final movement, the Major appeals to Charlotte with an argument
that turns on whether the customs of the age permit what they contemplate:

> …aus irgendeiner Art von Bedenklichkeit dasjenige versagen, was uns die Sitten der Zeit nicht absprechen?
> (030-009)

The question is devastating: if *Sitten der Zeit* (the customs of the age) no
longer forbid divorce, on what grounds can Charlotte refuse? Custom, which
earlier functioned as a binding force, now becomes the justification for
unbinding.

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — the character most invested in the preservation of custom;
  her resistance to divorce is a defense of social order
- [[Eduard]] — treats custom as negotiable when it obstructs his desires; his
  chemical analogy of *Sitten* as "middle terms" justifies transgression
- [[Ottilie]] — outwardly conforms to every custom while inwardly dissolving
  their authority through her radical ethical stance
- [[Mittler]] — the mediator who embodies the customs he enforces, yet whose
  interventions repeatedly produce the opposite of good order
