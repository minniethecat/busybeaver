## Definition

The figure of the neighbor (*Nachbar*, *Nachbarschaft* in German) in *Elective Affinities* spans a wide semantic range — from literal geographic adjacency to the charged symbolic proximity of the inner novella "The Neighbor's Children." Goethe uses neighbor relations to explore the social fabric that both sustains and constrains the novel's central characters: the neighbor is at once an obligation (the duty of reciprocal visits), a measure of social standing, and — in the novella's case — the figure of desire itself, concealed beneath the guise of proximity. The term thus traces the arc from social convention to erotic passion, making it one of the novel's most layered conceptual threads.

## In the Novel

Neighbor relations first appear as part of the ordered social world of the estate. Charlotte, planning the couple's retired life together, warns against bringing "anything alien or obstructive" into their shared space — the neighbor is, from the outset, figured as a potentially destabilizing outside presence (001-034). Yet the estate cannot remain sealed. By Chapter 22, after Luciane's arrival, the social dynamic inverts: "she felt obliged to pay visits all around the neighborhood. Because one rode and drove very quickly, the neighborhood extended rather far" (022-007). What was once a boundary becomes a field of social performance; the neighbor is no longer kept at bay but actively pursued.

Luciane's relationship to neighbors is characteristically ambivalent. She cultivates the young man who has lost his right hand with "intrusive obligingness," reserving for him the place beside her at table — but only so long as no one of higher standing displaces him: "if older, more distinguished persons took his neighborhood from her, she extended her attentiveness across the whole table" (023-004). Neighborhood here is a trophy, a transaction, a unit of social capital. The same chapter reveals the corrosive underside: "no visit was paid in the neighborhood, nowhere were she and her company received in castles and residences, without her letting it be noticed on her return, in the most unrestrained manner, that she was inclined to take all human relations only from their ridiculous side" (023-006). The neighbor, in Luciane's world, exists to be mocked once the visit is over.

The concept reaches its deepest articulation in the inner novella "The Neighbor's Children" (*Die wunderlichen Nachbarskinder*), recounted in Chapter 28. Here the neighbor is not a social obligation but the object of childhood antagonism that conceals a deeper elective affinity. The young woman's "struggle against her young neighbor was her first passion, and this violent struggle was, under the form of resistance, only a violent, quasi-inborn inclination" (028-035). The novella reinterprets the entire motif: the neighbor is the figure one fights against in order not to recognize one's own desire. In retrospect, "it seemed to her, in memory, as though she had always loved him" (028-035).

In the novel's final chapters, the neighbor returns to its social register. The Major "would now pay a visit in the neighborhood, from which she thought she had observed something that gave her joy" (031-006), and Eduard, driven by "insurmountable impatience, crept out of his hiding place" near the lake's reeds in the neighborhood of the lake (031-007). The neighbor has become the space of concealment, watching, and final, desperate approach.

## Key Passages

> Kaum hatte sie das Haus und die Gegend erschöpft, als sie sich verpflichtet fühlte, rings in der Nachbarschaft Besuch abzulegen. Weil man sehr schnell ritt und fuhr, so reichte die Nachbarschaft ziemlich fern umher. Das Schloß ward mit Gegenbesuchen überschwemmt, und damit man sich ja nicht verfehlen möchte, wurden bald bestimmte Tage angesetzt.
> (022-007)

> So wurde kein Besuch in der Nachbarschaft abgelegt, nirgends sie und ihre Gesellschaft in Schlössern und Wohnungen freundlich aufgenommen, ohne daß sie bei der Rückkehr auf das ausgelassenste merken ließ, wie sie alle menschlichen Verhältnisse nur von der lächerlichen Seite zu nehmen geneigt sei.
> (023-006)

> Der Kampf gegen ihren jungen Nachbar war die erste Leidenschaft gewesen, und dieser heftige Kampf war doch nur, unter der Form des Widerstrebens, eine heftige, gleichsam angeborne Neigung. Auch kam es ihr in der Erinnerung nicht anders vor, als daß sie ihn immer geliebt habe.
> (028-035)

> Auf eine ganz natürliche, aber doch sonderbare Weise stand er seiner schönen Nachbarin abermals entgegen. Sie hatte in der letzten Zeit nur freundliche, bräutliche Familienempfindungen bei sich genährt, sie war mit allem, was sie umgab, in Übereinstimmung; sie glaubte glücklich zu sein und war es auch auf gewisse Weise. Aber nun stand ihr zum erstenmal seit langer Zeit wieder etwas entgegen.
> (028-033)

## Related Characters

- [[Luciane]] — the character most associated with *Nachbarschaft* as social theater; her visits structure the neighborhood as a field of display and derision
- [[Charlotte]] — her early wish to exclude neighbors ("nothing alien or obstructive") sets the baseline against which all subsequent neighbor relations are measured
- [[Eduard]] — in the final chapters, becomes a neighbor in hiding, watching from the margins
- [[Ottilie]] — the object of the kind of proximity that, in the novella's terms, makes neighbor into beloved
