## Definition

Judgment (*Gericht* in German) in *Elective Affinities* operates on multiple registers simultaneously: as divine tribunal, as social censure, and as the internal reckoning of conscience. The German word carries the double sense of a court of law and the Last Judgment, and Goethe exploits this semantic density to frame the novel's central moral crisis. When Mittler, the novel's self-appointed moral arbiter, condemns the public recitation of the Ten Commandments and proposes instead that transgressions be punished by a "secret tribunal," he inadvertently describes the novel's own narrative logic: the characters are judged not by ecclesiastical authority but by the inexorable unfolding of consequences that follows from their actions. The concept of *Gericht* thus names the moral architecture of a novel in which every transgression finds its retribution, not through divine intervention but through the internal logic of human relationships.

## In the Novel

The term *Gericht* emerges in Chapter 36 during one of Mittler's characteristic harangues. Waiting for Eduard on the eve of his birthday, Mittler holds forth on his favorite topic: the fatuity of conventional moral instruction. The Ten Commandments, he argues, are pedagogically disastrous — especially the sixth ("Thou shalt not commit adultery"), whose very prohibition excites the imagination of children toward the forbidden. Far better, Mittler proposes, that such matters "be arbitrarily punished by a secret tribunal than that one should babble about them before church and congregation" (036-009).

The irony is devastating, and it operates on multiple levels. Mittler does not know — cannot know — that the "adultery" he condemns has been the central fact of the lives of the very people he is addressing. Charlotte and the Major sit before him in the aftermath of the child's death, the novel's most terrible judgment on the transgression Mittler so blithely denounces. His proposal of a "secret tribunal" (*heimliches Gericht*) is exactly what the novel itself provides: a hidden court in which actions are judged not by public opinion but by their consequences. The child's drowning, Ottilie's self-starvation, Eduard's wasting death — these are not punishments imposed from without but judgments enacted from within the causal structure of the relationships themselves.

The motif of judgment also operates through the novel's social world. Characters are constantly judging one another: the Count and Baroness are judged by society for their irregular union; Luciane's theatrical cruelties subject the vulnerable to social judgment; and Eduard's final, devastating self-judgment ("my whole striving remains only ever an imitation") is the novel's most concentrated act of *Gericht*, performed not by any tribunal but by the guilty conscience itself.

## Key Passages

> Und nun gar das sechste, das finde ich ganz abscheulich! Was? Die Neugierde vorahnender Kinder auf gefährliche Mysterien reizen, ihre Einbildungskraft zu wunderlichen Bildern und Vorstellungen aufregen, die gerade das, was man entfernen will, mit Gewalt heranbringen! Weit besser wäre es, daß dergleichen von einem heimlichen Gericht willkürlich bestraft würde, als daß man vor Kirch und Gemeinde davon plappern läßt.
> (036-009)

> „Du sollst nicht ehebrechen“, fuhr Mittler fort. „Wie grob, wie unanständig! Klänge es nicht ganz anders, wenn es hieße: 'du sollst Ehrfurcht haben vor der ehelichen Verbindung'?“
> (036-010)

> Sollte sich irgend in ihrem Verhältnis etwas trüben, so sollst du suchen, es aufzuklären; du sollst suchen, sie zu begütigen, sie zu besänftigen, ihnen ihre wechselseitigen Vorteile deutlich zu machen.
> (036-011)

## Related Characters

- [[Mittler]] — the novel's self-appointed moral spokesman, whose advocacy of a "secret tribunal" inadvertently names the narrative logic by which the characters are judged; his hectoring moralism is at once ridiculous and, in its diagnosis of conventional piety, devastatingly acute
- [[Charlotte]] — the character most subject to the judgment of conscience; her forced smile at Mittler's harangue ("Sie erlassen uns wohl das siebente Gebot") is one of the novel's most painful moments of social performance under moral duress
- [[Eduard]] — the principal object of the novel's implicit judgment; his final self-condemnation represents the internalization of *Gericht*
- [[Ottilie]] — her self-starvation is the novel's most radical enactment of self-judgment, a tribunal more severe than any that society or religion could impose
