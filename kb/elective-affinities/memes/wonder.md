## Definition

Wonder (*Wunder*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" designates the intrusion of the extraordinary into ordinary life — events, persons, or phenomena that exceed rational expectation and provoke astonishment.

Goethe treats wonder not as a supernatural category but as an intensification of nature itself, a concentration of natural forces that appears miraculous because it follows laws we do not yet fully comprehend. The concept bridges the novel's scientific and aesthetic dimensions.

## In the Novel

Wonder manifests in several distinct registers. The most literal is the "miracle" of Ottilie herself — described as if she were "performing a miracle" (024-004) when she appears in costume for the tableaux vivants.

The child of Charlotte and Eduard, conceived in a moment of adulterous imagination, is called a "wonder-child" (029-015), capturing the uncanny way natural processes produce results that feel supernatural.

The novel deploys wonder as an aesthetic principle. The tableaux vivants, landscape transformations, and architectural projects aim at wondrous effects — "such wonders with other wonders in living" (025-048). The narrator remarks that "the wondrous eventually seems true" (025-015).

Yet wonder carries an ominous undertone. Charlotte warns against "what wondrous things we are beginning here" (029-009), recognizing that the pursuit of the wondrous can lead into dangerous territory.

## Chemical Metaphor

Wonder corresponds to the moment in a chemical experiment when an unexpected result emerges — a precipitate, a color change — that reveals a law not previously understood. The "miracle" is not a violation of chemical law but a demonstration of it in concentrated form. The novel's wondrous events are intensifications of natural laws, made visible by the concentration of passionate forces.
