## Overview

Der Bettler is a beggar who appears in several key scenes in Part I. Though unnamed and with few lines, he serves an important symbolic function: he is the figure whom [[Eduard]], trapped in the bonds of marriage and social convention, comes to envy as a model of freedom.

## Biography

The beggar first appears in Chapter 6, during a conversation in which Charlotte and Eduard discuss charity. Charlotte argues that excessive generosity only attracts more beggars rather than solving the problem, while Eduard defends the rights of the poor (006-032, 006-033).

In Chapter 15, the beggar's features are recalled by a character who compares his own situation to the beggar's persistence (015-020). The beggar reappears most significantly in Chapter 16, when Eduard, walking past his estate, sees the beggar sitting contentedly in the arbor. Overcome with envy at the beggar's apparent freedom from social bonds and obligations, Eduard exclaims: "O du Beneidenswerter!" — "O you enviable one!" (016-028, 016-030).

## Relationships

- **[[Eduard]]** — The central relationship. Eduard first gives the beggar charity, then comes to envy his freedom from the very social order that Eduard himself is trapped within (016-028, 016-030).

## Role in the Novel

The beggar functions as a mirror for Eduard's evolving psychological state. In Chapter 6, Eduard's charitable impulse toward the beggar reflects his comfortable position within the social order. By Chapter 16, after the "chemical" rearrangement of affections has begun, Eduard looks at the same beggar and sees not an object of pity but an object of envy — a man unburdened by property, marriage, and the complex web of obligations that now torment Eduard. The beggar's freedom is an illusion, of course, but the illusion itself reveals how desperate Eduard has become.
