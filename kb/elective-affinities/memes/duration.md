## Definition

Duration (*Dauer*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" names the quality of persisting through time. It stands in tension with transience, change, and decay — a tension that runs through the novel's treatment of architecture, landscape, art, and human relationships.

Goethe approaches duration not as a simple binary (permanent vs. temporary) but as a spectrum. Some things endure for years, others for a lifetime; some are built to last, others are inherently fleeting. The moral significance of duration lies in what humans choose to make durable and what they allow to pass.

## In the Novel

The theme of duration surfaces most explicitly in the novel's treatment of building projects. When the foundation stone is laid for the new house, the ceremony invokes "security and duration" (014-004) — architectural permanence as a symbol of domestic stability. This aspiration is undercut by the narrative's later events, which reveal how fragile human arrangements truly are.

The question of duration also pervades the novel's reflections on art. In a discussion of portraiture, the narrator observes that a portrait represents "a duration of one's existence" (019-014) captured by the artist's hand — a permanence wrested from the flux of lived time. The garden, meanwhile, embodies the interweaving of "the transient and the enduring" (027-014), a living testament to the inseparability of growth and decay.

Charlotte, contemplating her marriage, reflects that "only this decisive, eternal duration amidst so much movement" (010-010) gives meaning to the institution — a conviction the novel's plot will test to its limits.
