## Definition

Misfortune (*Unglück*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" is the dark twin of happiness (*Glück*), and the novel insists from the outset that the two are inseparable. The narrator's early observation — that "we are as fertile in fortune and misfortune without our merit or fault being attributable" (002-039) — establishes a cosmology in which *Unglück* is not punishment but a structural possibility built into the nature of things.

This is not the misfortune of Greek tragedy, brought about by *hamartia* or divine retribution. Goethe's *Unglück* is closer to a chemical precipitate: it falls out of the same reactions that produce happiness, governed by the same laws of affinity and separation. The novel's tragic architecture is thus not theological but naturalistic — misfortune is what happens when elective affinities run their course.

## In the Novel

The vocabulary of *Unglück* runs through the novel like a bass line, surfacing at every critical turn. Eduard speaks of it casually at first: "on my ride, some misfortune must have befallen me" (002-012), treating it as an external event. But the novel progressively reveals misfortune as an internal structure, not an external accident.

In the pivotal Chapter 9 conversation, the narrator delivers one of the novel's most quoted reflections: "All fortune, that every individual misfortune against it is not to be counted at all" (009-044). The context is Eduard and Charlotte's discussion of their marriage and its pressures. The sentence captures the novel's distinctive calculus: collective happiness absorbs individual misfortune into itself, making suffering disappear into a larger sum — until, catastrophically, it doesn't.

The misfortune that befalls the young couple introduced in Chapter 28 is treated with documentary immediacy: "The misfortune, the need spoke itself out quickly" (028-047). This episode, often read as a miniature of the novel's larger concerns, shows misfortune as a social fact — it becomes visible, demands response, and in doing so exposes the limits of sympathy.

Ottilie's trajectory is the novel's most concentrated study in misfortune. After the child's death, the narrator reflects on how "the indiscretion of people is against such unfortunate ones" (033-010), describing the peculiar social vulnerability of the *Unglückliche* — the unfortunates who become objects of curiosity and judgment rather than compassion. The observation is pointed: "They were called to stand by the unfortunate; and whoever can deny that he has done so?" (033-014). Misfortune, in this analysis, is not merely suffered; it is spectated.

The novel's final pages return to the theme with redoubled intensity. In Charlotte's meditation on Eduard's death, the misfortune is complete: "to snatch him away, but only to his misfortune; for it became clear to him…" (036-022). The verb *entreißen* (to snatch away) captures the passivity of the unfortunate — misfortune is something that happens to you, an event in the grammatical passive.
