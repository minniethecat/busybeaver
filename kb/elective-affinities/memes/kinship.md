## Definition

*Verwandtschaft* in *Elective Affinities* operates on a double register that is fundamental to the novel's conceptual architecture. In its ordinary sense, it means kinship or relatedness — the bonds of blood and marriage that structure family and society. But in the chemical discourse that the Captain introduces in Chapter 4, *Verwandtschaft* is the standard eighteenth-century German term for what we now call chemical affinity: the tendency of substances to combine, separate, and recombine according to elective laws. Goethe's genius is to let these two meanings contaminate each other throughout the novel: every reference to *Verwandtschaft* hovers ambiguously between the family tree and the chemical laboratory, between the given bonds of blood and the chosen bonds of elective attraction. Charlotte's involuntary slide from "Verwandtschaften" (chemical affinities) to "Verwandten" (relatives) in Chapter 4 is not a mistake but the novel's thesis in miniature.

## In the Novel

The conceptual slippage is established in Chapter 4, during the Captain's chemistry lesson. Charlotte interrupts with a confession: "I heard of *Verwandtschaften* being read, and at once I thought of my relatives, of a couple of cousins who are just now giving me trouble. My attention returns to your reading; I hear that the discussion is of wholly lifeless things, and I look into your book to reorient myself" (004-016). The confusion is productive: Charlotte's mind, habituated to social *Verwandtschaft*, cannot help but hear the social in the chemical. She then asks the question that sets the novel's intellectual machinery in motion: "How is it actually meant here with the *Verwandtschaften*?" (004-019).

The Captain obliges with a definition that, while chemically precise, is saturated with social implication: "Those natures which, on encountering one another, quickly seize hold of each other and mutually determine one another, we call related [*verwandt*]. In alkalis and acids, which, although opposed to one another and perhaps precisely for that reason because they are opposed to one another, most decisively seek and grasp each other, modify one another, and together form a new body — this *Verwandtschaft* is striking enough" (004-037). The language of "seizing," "grasping," and "forming a new body" carries unmistakable erotic resonance. Chemical *Verwandtschaft* is being described in terms that make it impossible not to think of human *Verwandtschaft* — and vice versa.

Eduard immediately escalates the metaphor: "the complicated cases are the most interesting. Only with these does one learn the degrees of affinities, the nearer, stronger, more distant, weaker relations; affinities only become interesting when they bring about separations" (004-039). The word *Scheidungen* (separations) is the same term used for divorce. Eduard has grasped, faster than anyone, that the chemical model licenses the dissolution of existing bonds in favor of newer, stronger ones. The chemical *Verwandtschaft* becomes a moral argument — or an alibi.

In Chapter 29, the term resurfaces in a new key. After the inner novella and the child's death, the narrator reflects on Ottilie's bond with the child: "Through this strange *Verwandtschaft* [affinity/kinship] and perhaps still more through the beautiful feeling of women, which embraces the child of a beloved man, even by another, with tender inclination, Ottilie became as much a mother to the growing creature, or rather another kind of mother" (029-016). Here *Verwandtschaft* has fully absorbed both meanings: it is a "strange" bond — neither blood nor chemistry but something between, a relatedness forged by love and proximity rather than biology. Ottilie is "another kind of mother" — a *Verwandte* by election, not by nature.

The novel thus traces an arc: from Charlotte's confusion between chemical and familial *Verwandtschaft* (Chapter 4), through Eduard's weaponization of the metaphor as justification for divorce (Chapter 4), to Ottilie's living out of a *Verwandtschaft* that is neither biological nor chemical but something third — a relatedness of the heart that the novel can name but not fully explain.

## Key Passages

> Ich hörte von Verwandtschaften lesen, und da dacht ich eben gleich an meine Verwandten, an ein paar Vettern, die mir gerade in diesem Augenblick zu schaffen machen. Meine Aufmerksamkeit kehrt zu deiner Vorlesung zurück; ich höre, daß von ganz leblosen Dingen die Rede ist, und blicke dir ins Buch, um mich wieder zurechtzufinden.
> (004-016)

> Möchtet ihr mich nur kürzlich belehren, wie es eigentlich hier mit den Verwandtschaften gemeint sei?
> (004-019)

> Die jenigen Naturen, die sich beim Zusammentreffen einander schnell ergreifen und wechselseitig bestimmen, nennen wir verwandt. An den Alkalien und Säuren, die, obgleich einander entgegengesetzt und vielleicht eben deswegen, weil sie einander entgegengesetzt sind, sich am entschiedensten suchen und fassen, sich modifizieren und zusammen einen neuen Körper bilden, ist diese Verwandtschaft auffallend genug.
> (004-037)

> Erst bei diesen lernt man die Grade der Verwandtschaften, die nähern, stärkern, entferntern, geringern Beziehungen kennen; die Verwandtschaften werden erst interessant, wenn sie Scheidungen bewirken.
> (004-039)

> Durch diese sonderbare Verwandtschaft und vielleicht noch mehr durch das schöne Gefühl der Frauen geleitet, welche das Kind eines geliebten Mannes, auch von einer andern, mit zärtlicher Neigung umfangen, ward Ottilie dem heranwachsenden Geschöpf soviel als eine Mutter oder vielmehr eine andre Art von Mutter.
> (029-016)
