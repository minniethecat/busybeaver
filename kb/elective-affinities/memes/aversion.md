## Definition

Aversion (*Abneigung*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" is the negative counterpart to inclination (*Neigung*). Just as chemical elements exhibit selective affinity — combining readily with some substances and repelling others — human beings experience spontaneous dislike and repulsion that govern social behavior.

Goethe treats aversion not as a moral failing but as a natural psychological phenomenon, as elemental and involuntary as attraction itself. In the novel's chemical metaphor, aversion corresponds to the absence of affinity between substances — the reason certain compounds cannot form.

## In the Novel

Eduard's aversion to social festivities and formal gatherings is established early in the narrative. The narrator notes that "it did not take much to revive Eduard's old aversion to such celebrations" (008-002), revealing a character trait that sets him at odds with social convention.

The concept extends beyond individual taste to shape the novel's entire relational geometry. Where Neigung draws characters together, Abneigung creates distance, suspicion, and friction. Charlotte observes that in the wider world "indifference and aversion are truly at home" (003-035), contrasting the polite surface of society with the colder realities beneath.

In a meta-fictional moment, the narrator reflects on portraiture, confessing "a kind of aversion to portraits" (019-018) because they always seem to carry a silent reproach. This observation links aversion to the novel's deeper concern with perception, representation, and the limits of human understanding.
