## Definition

**Silence** (*Schweigen*) in *Elective Affinities* is the novel's primary
alternative to speech — and, Goethe suggests, its superior. Where words fail,
deceive, or wound, silence carries meaning that language cannot. The novel
constructs a hierarchy of communication in which what is not said consistently
outweighs what is.

Silence in this work is not a single phenomenon. It is social tactic, moral
weapon, symptom of trauma, and — in its most radical form — a mode of being
that precedes and transcends language. The novel tracks silence across all these
registers, building toward the conclusion that silence is the only adequate
response to the deepest forms of suffering.

## In the Novel

The Count and Baroness bring worldly sophistication to the estate, but their
visit produces the novel's first significant silence. After dinner, a tension
manifests:

> Auch nach Tische fand sich ein Zwiespalt in der Gesellschaft. Der Graf…
> (010-051)

Also after the meal a discord appeared in the company. The Count, who had
spoken freely, now finds himself met with silence — a silence that is not
absence of response but response itself, the social group's collective judgment
rendered without a word.

The silence that surrounds Ottilie becomes, over the course of the novel, her
defining feature. After the realignment of affections, the narrator observes:

> Schweigend hält sie daher die Liebenden noch immer auseinander, und die Sache…
> (013-019)

Silently she therefore still holds the lovers apart. Ottilie's silence is
active — it *holds apart* (*hält auseinander*). It is not the passive silence
of someone who has nothing to say but the active silence of someone who is
doing something by not speaking.

In Part II, Ottilie has been transformed by her time in the Pension. She has
learned to listen rather than to speak:

> Sie hatte sich an Hören und Schweigen gewöhnt aber sie saß diesmal in der…
> (028-019)

She had grown accustomed to hearing and silence. The pairing *Hören und
Schweigen* — hearing and keeping silent — describes a mode of being in the
world that is receptive rather than assertive. Ottilie has trained herself in
the discipline of attention, and silence is its necessary condition.

After the child's death, silence becomes something more than social strategy.
It becomes a symptom of a wound that will not close:

> Glücklicherweise trennte sich die Gesellschaft für einige Zeit. Ottilie fand
> Raum, sich in der Einsamkeit auszuweinen.
> (028-023)

Fortunately the company separated for a time. Ottilie found space to weep
herself out in solitude. The silence here is the precondition for grief — only
when the social demand for speech is lifted can the real work of mourning
begin.

Eduard, returning to Ottilie after the catastrophe, confronts a silence that
terrifies him:

> „Ottilie“, rief er aus, „laß mich das furchtbare Schweigen brechen! Sind
> wir nur Schatten…"
> (034-009)

"Ottilie," he cried out, "let me break the terrible silence! Are we only
shadows…" The silence is *furchtbar* — terrible, fearsome. Eduard experiences
it as something that must be broken, as if it were a spell or a prison. But
his attempt to break it with speech only confirms the silence's power.

Ottilie's silence persists and intensifies. It becomes the defining fact of her
presence in the household:

> …indem sie immerfort bei ihrem Schweigen verharrt. Man redet ihr zu, s…
> (035-004)

While she continually persists in her silence. They speak to her, they entreat
her — and she remains silent. The verb *verharrt* (persists) makes silence an
active choice, a position one occupies and defends.

The Major and Mittler attempt to break through:

> …Erstarren wieder beleben, ihr Schweigen wieder auflösen wollte. Er se…
> (035-020)

To reanimate her petrification, to dissolve her silence again. The image is
physical: silence as a frozen state (*Erstarren*), as something solid that
must be *dissolved* (*auflösen*). Speech is figured as solvent, silence as
stone.

Mittler's response to Ottilie's silence reveals his fundamental
misunderstanding of it:

> …n zu schmieden ist. Ottiliens Schweigen sowie ihre Weigerung legte er…
> (036-004)

Ottilie's silence as well as her refusal he interpreted… Mittler reads silence
as he reads everything: as material for his own interpretation. He cannot grasp
that silence might not *mean* something else — that it might be itself the
meaning.

## Related Characters

- [[Ottilie]] — the novel's central figure of silence; her progression from
  quiet reserve through active withholding to the silence of trauma is the
  novel's most sustained study of what it means not to speak
- [[Eduard]] — experiences Ottilie's silence as a barrier that must be
  broken; his inability to accept silence as communication is a measure of
  his limitations
- [[Charlotte]] — practices silence as social discipline; her restraint is
  the adult counterpoint to Ottilie's more absolute silence
- [[Mittler]] — cannot abide silence; his compulsive speech is the novel's
  demonstration of what silence is not — and why silence is the wiser
  posture
