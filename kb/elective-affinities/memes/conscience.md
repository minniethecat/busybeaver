## Definition

Conscience (*Gewissen* in German) in *Elective Affinities* functions as an inner moral tribunal — the faculty by which characters measure their actions against an internalized standard of right and wrong. Unlike law, which is external and social, conscience speaks from within, though its voice proves to be intermittent, sometimes silent at crucial moments, and often overridden by passion or inclination. Goethe deploys *Gewissen* at pivotal narrative junctures, tracing its presence and absence as an index of each character's moral state. The term appears with particular force around Charlotte, the character most consistently attuned to its claims.

## In the Novel

Conscience first enters the novel during the chemical affinities debate in Chapter 4. After Charlotte expresses unease about the seductive power of analogies, she grounds her skepticism in conscience itself: "If conscience bids you to make such reflections, I can be without worry" (004-049). For Charlotte, conscience acts as a check against self-deception — the faculty that distinguishes genuine moral reasoning from sophistry dressed in scientific language.

The motif recurs in Chapter 9, where the narrator reflects that one can be "married to conscience" as much as to a spouse (009-045). This formulation introduces a key tension: conscience, like marriage, constitutes a binding force, one that characters may wish to escape but cannot fully elude. Eduard's flight from his marital obligation is simultaneously a flight from the voice of conscience.

In Chapter 13, conscience appears negatively — by its conspicuous silence. As Eduard drifts through the war zone, "no memory rises before him, no conscience speaks to him; everything within him that was not his passion has fallen silent" (013-003). The absence of conscience marks the depth of his moral dislocation. By contrast, Charlotte's conscience remains active: in Chapter 23, she feels the impulse to speak to her daughter's conscience about proper conduct (023-028), and in Chapter 25, the narrator describes characters who "make a matter of conscience" out of settling debts even when blessed with great wealth (025-036). Conscience in these passages is not merely private feeling but a social practice grounded in reciprocity.

In the penultimate chapter, as Ottilie's condition worsens, the narrator records that Eduard spoke to her "according to feeling and conscience of the moment" (035-009) — but remained silent when it mattered most, a silence that serves as the novel's final, damning verdict on his moral character.

## Key Passages

> Wenn das Gewissen dich solche Betrachtungen machen heißt, so kann ich ohne Sorge sein. Diese Gleichnisreden sind artig und unterhaltend, und wer spielt nicht gern mit Ähnlichkeiten! Aber der Mensch ist doch um so manche Stufe über jene Elemente erhöht.
> (004-049)

> Sind wir nicht auch mit dem Gewissen verheiratet, das wir oft gern los sein möchten, weil es uns beschwerlicher fällt, als uns je ein Mann oder eine Frau werden könnte?
> (009-045)

> Keine Erinnerung steigt vor ihm auf, kein Gewissen spricht ihm zu; alles, was in ihm nicht seine Leidenschaft war, ist verstummt.
> (013-003)

> Nach Gefühl und Gewissen des Augenblicks schwieg ich, verstummte ich, suchte ich zu unterhandeln, zu vermitteln.
> (035-009)

> bei dem größten Vermögen ein Gewissen daraus machen, denjenigen einigermaßen schuldig zu bleiben, der uns in unserm Leben viel Gutes erwiesen hat.
> (025-036)

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — the character most consistently guided by conscience; uses it as a yardstick against self-deception and warns against analogies that bypass moral judgment
- [[Eduard]] — the character whose conscience falls silent when passion overtakes him; his moral drift is measured by the progressive absence of *Gewissen*
- [[Ottilie]] — her silence in the novel's final crisis implicates a form of conscience that speaks too late; her self-starvation can be read as an extreme act of conscience
- [[the Captain]] — introduced the chemical framework that Eduard transforms into moral escape; his own conscience remains less explicitly examined
