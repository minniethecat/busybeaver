## Definition

Law (*Gesetz* in German) in *Elective Affinities* encompasses two distinct but interlocking domains: social law — the statutes, customs, and contractual bonds that govern marriage, property, and public conduct — and natural law — the deeper, often wordless regularities that govern human affinities, chemical bonds, and moral necessity. The novel's central conflict plays out precisely at the intersection of these two orders. Characters repeatedly invoke, propose, break, and suffer under laws, and the tragic arc of the narrative can be read as an inquiry into whether human inclination can be reconciled with legal-moral order — or whether the two stand in irreconcilable tension.

## In the Novel

The *Gesetz* motif is introduced early, during the chemical affinities conversation in Chapter 4. Eduard, explaining how chemical elements combine and separate, declares that just as "customs and laws make these things compatible, so there are also compounds in our chemical world" (004-033). The analogy already smuggles in a normative claim: human laws, like chemical ones, govern combination and dissolution — but unlike chemical laws, human laws can be contested, reinterpreted, or broken.

In Chapter 6, Charlotte's refusal to legislate for her daughter's conduct — "I will prescribe no laws to you" (006-021) — marks a pedagogical stance, one that trusts individual judgment over rigid prescription. The Captain's contribution in Chapter 9 deepens the metaphor: "just as law cements them together, so our substances will also hold together more firmly" (009-008). Law here is imagined as a binding agent, a social cement analogous to chemical affinity.

The novel's most radical engagement with law comes in Chapter 10, where a visitor proposes that marriage should last only five years, renewable by mutual consent (010-011, 010-014). This thought experiment — a *Gesetzvorschlag* or law-proposal — strikes at the heart of the novel's moral architecture, asking whether marriage law serves human flourishing or merely constrains it. Eduard's enthusiastic endorsement of the proposal foreshadows his own willingness to override existing marital law in favor of personal inclination.

In the second half of the novel, the language of law shifts toward transgression and consequence. Ottilie, reflecting on her own conduct, acknowledges: "I have stepped out of my orbit, I have broken my laws, I have even lost the feeling for them" (032-018, 032-019). This confession transforms law from an external social constraint into an internal moral order whose violation carries catastrophic personal cost. The final chapter brings the motif to its darkest formulation: the narrator speaks of "prohibitive laws and ordinances" (036-007), casting law not as a framework for freedom but as a bulwark against chaos — one that the characters have fatally dismantled.

## Key Passages

> Wie diese durch Sitten und Gesetze vereinbar sind, so gibt es auch in unserer chemischen Welt Mittelglieder, dasjenige zu verbinden, was sich einander abweist.
> (004-033)

> In diesen Dingen will ich dir darüber keine Gesetze vorschreiben. Du bist jung.
> (006-021)

> Jene Substanzen, sagte der Hauptmann, die sich beim Zusammentreffen schnell ergreifen und wechselseitig bestimmen, nennen wir verwandte. Wie das Gesetz sie verkittet, so werden auch unsere Substanzen fester zusammenhalten.
> (009-008)

> Ich bin aus meiner Bahn geschritten, ich habe meine Gesetze gebrochen, ich habe sogar das Gefühl dafür verloren.
> (032-019)

> Er sprach es aus, als Verbote, als verbietende Gesetze und Anordnungen. Der Mensch ist gar nicht frei, sich vor diesen Dingen zu hüten.
> (036-007)

## Related Characters

- [[Eduard]] — the character who most actively seeks to revise or escape existing laws, whether marital, social, or moral
- [[Ottilie]] — the character whose tragic arc turns on the recognition that she has broken her own inner laws
- [[Charlotte]] — consistently upholds law and order, yet her pedagogical approach refuses to impose law rigidly on others
- [[the Captain]] — frames law as a binding chemical analogy; his own conduct remains legally and morally circumspect
