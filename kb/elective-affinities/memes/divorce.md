## Definition

Divorce (*Scheidung*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" is the legal mechanism that would permit the dissolution of existing marriages and the formalization of new bonds according to the characters' shifted affinities. It is the juridical counterpart to the chemical principle of separation — the social procedure through which one compound may be dissolved so that its elements may recombine.

The concept carries enormous weight in the novel because it represents the point at which private feeling must confront public legality. Divorce is the gate through which the characters' elective affinities must pass if they are to achieve social recognition — and it is precisely this passage that the novel's moral logic puts into question.

## In the Novel

The possibility of divorce is first raised indirectly. "People only become interesting," a character remarks, "when they bring about divorces" (004-039). This aphorism — half-cynical, half-prophetic — introduces divorce as the social mechanism that translates private passion into public drama.

The question takes concrete form after the four characters have settled into their new configuration. The Baroness "had been thought to be on the point of divorce" (009-032), establishing her as a figure for whom marital dissolution is a live possibility rather than an abstraction. It is she who later urges Mittler: "bring about, dear sir, a divorce which is so necessary, which has already been arranged" (018-017).

Charlotte's progression toward accepting divorce is the novel's central moral arc. She moves from resistance to reluctant acknowledgment, finally declaring: "I consent to the divorce. I should have decided upon it earlier" (032-009). Her consent — when it comes — is the novel's most decisive ethical act: she releases Eduard, but in so doing takes upon herself the full moral weight of the dissolution.

Eduard's response to Charlotte's consent is telling: "when I learn that you have consented to the divorce, I atone in the same hour" (032-021). Divorce, for him, is not liberation but guilt — a recognition that even legally sanctioned separation carries a moral cost that cannot be discharged by legal procedure alone.

> (004-039)

> (009-032)

> (032-021)
