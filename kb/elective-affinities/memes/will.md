## Definition

Will (*Wille*) in *Elective Affinities* is a contested concept, constantly set against its shadow: *Willkür* (arbitrariness, caprice). The novel traces a trajectory from the confident assertion of will to its gradual dissolution under the pressure of circumstances, passions, and fate. Early in the novel, will appears as a mark of human agency — what distinguishes persons from mere elements. But as the plot unfolds, the characters discover that their wills are not sovereign: they are shaped by affinities they did not choose, overtaken by events they set in motion but cannot control, and ultimately forced to confront the limits of volition itself. Will in Goethe's treatment is not simply free or determined; it is entangled, compromised, and — in Ottilie's case — sublimated into renunciation.

## In the Novel

The novel's first explicit treatment of will comes in Chapter 4, during the chemical affinity discussion. The Captain observes that man "lends his wisdom as his folly, his will as his arbitrariness to the animals, the plants, the elements, and the gods" (004-018). This anthropomorphic projection — seeing will everywhere in nature — is precisely what makes the chemical metaphor so seductive and so dangerous. Eduard earlier draws a sharp distinction: "Business demands seriousness and strictness, life demands *Willkür*" (004-017 preamble). The opposition structures the novel's value system: will belongs to the realm of order and intention; arbitrariness to the realm of life, spontaneity, and desire.

Eduard is the novel's primary figure of will, and his arc traces will's progressive failure. Early on, "no one opposed his will" (018-033); he acts with the confidence of someone whose desires encounter no resistance. But this very confidence blinds him to the forces his actions unleash. By Chapter 31, the narrator observes that Eduard "believed he was meeting her decided wishes and hoped for her quick consent, because he could have no other will" (031-004). The phrase "could have no other will" is devastating: what Eduard experiences as free choice is revealed as compulsion. His will has become identical with his desire, and his desire has become necessity.

Ottilie presents the novel's alternative model of will. When Eduard begs her "to speak only a word, to declare her will" (034-013), she answers with silence and a gentle shake of the head. Her will is expressed not through assertion but through refusal, not through speech but through renunciation. In Chapter 35, she reveals to Charlotte: "My resolve was pure, to renounce Eduard, to remove myself from him" (035-008). This is will as self-limitation rather than self-assertion — a will that chooses not to choose, or rather, chooses what costs the self most.

The most philosophically compressed statement on will comes from Ottilie's diary: "We arrange ourselves domestically only to move out again, and if we do not do it with will and arbitrariness, then circumstances, passions, chances, necessity and what not all do it" (028-017). Will and *Willkür* are here placed on one side of a fundamental divide: on the other side stand the impersonal forces — circumstances, passions, chance, necessity — that accomplish what conscious volition cannot or will not. The novel's tragedy lies in the gap between these two realms.

## Key Passages

> So behandelt er alles, was er außer sich findet; seine Weisheit wie seine Torheit, seinen Willen wie seine Willkür leiht er den Tieren, den Pflanzen, den Elementen und den Göttern.
> (004-018)

> Niemand stellte seinem Willen ein Hindernis entgegen, da er einen weitern Sinn hatte als die andern.
> (018-033)

> Er bittet sie inständig, nur ein Wort zu reden, ihren Willen zu erklären. Er wolle allen ihren Willen, schwört er; aber sie schweigt.
> (034-013)

> Wir richten uns immer häuslich ein, um wieder auszuziehen, und wenn wir es nicht mit Willen und Willkür tun, so wirken Verhältnisse, Leidenschaften, Zufälle, Notwendigkeit und was nicht alles.
> (028-017)

> Eduard, der seine Wünsche auf sie übergetragen hatte, glaubte nicht anders, als daß er ihren entschiedenen Wünschen entgegenkomme, und hoffte eine so schnelle Einwilligung von ihr, weil er keinen andern Willen haben konnte.
> (031-004)

> Ganz rein war mein Vorsatz, Eduarden zu entsagen, mich von ihm zu entfernen.
> (035-008)

## Related Characters

- [[Ottilie]] — who embodies will as renunciation, expressing the purest volition through silence and refusal
- [[Charlotte]] — who navigates between Eduard's impulsive will and Ottilie's renunciatory will with practical reason
- [[the Captain]] — who first articulates the philosophical problem: that man projects his will onto a nature that has none
