## Definition

Imitation (*Nachahmung* in German) occupies a central and fraught position in Goethe's aesthetic thought, and *Elective Affinities* dramatizes this tension through Eduard's terminal self-assessment. The concept of *Nachahmung* — mimesis, imitation, the reproduction of models — was a foundational category of eighteenth-century aesthetics, from Winckelmann's injunction to imitate the ancients to the young Goethe's own essays on Shakespeare and Gothic architecture. But in the novel's final pages, *Nachahmung* appears not as an aesthetic program but as an existential verdict: Eduard, reflecting on his life from the desolation of Ottilie's death, concludes that "my whole striving remains only ever an imitation, a false endeavor" (036-041). The concept thus bridges aesthetic theory and moral psychology: the failure to create an authentic life is figured as a failure of mimesis.

## In the Novel

The term *Nachahmung* appears at the novel's emotional nadir. After Ottilie's death, Eduard withdraws into a state of suspended animation — "he seemed to have no more tears, to be capable of no further suffering" (036-040). But this numbness is punctuated by moments of terrible clarity, in which he speaks to the Major, his oldest friend and the unwitting agent of so much of the novel's tragedy. It is in one such moment that Eduard delivers his self-condemnation: "Ach! what am I unhappy, that my whole striving remains only ever an imitation, a false endeavor!" (036-041).

The charge is devastating because it is precise. Eduard's entire life, as the novel has revealed it, has been structured by imitation: he imitates the landed gentleman without possessing the gravity of that role; he imitates the passionate lover without accepting the costs of passion; he conducts landscape "improvements" that are really imitations of fashionable English gardens; his chemical metaphor for human relationships is itself an imitation — the application of a scientific model to a domain it cannot adequately describe. Even his love for Ottilie, authentic in its intensity, is shadowed by imitation: he desires her because she mirrors his own handwriting, his own mannerisms, a narcissistic mimesis that the novel diagnoses with clinical precision.

Goethe's deployment of *Nachahmung* here is philosophically loaded. In his later aesthetic writings, Goethe distinguished between *einfache Nachahmung* (simple imitation of nature), *Manier* (manner, the artist's subjective inflection), and *Stil* (style, the highest synthesis in which the particular reveals the universal). Eduard's tragedy is that he never progresses beyond the first stage: he imitates without transforming, copies without creating. His final, shattering insight is that a life lived in imitation is not really lived at all.

## Related Characters

- [[Eduard]] — the novel's central imitator; his terminal self-diagnosis of *Nachahmung* as the structure of his life is the novel's most concentrated moment of moral-aesthetic judgment
- [[the Captain]] — Eduard's foil; the man of action and competence against whom Eduard's imitative inadequacy is measured; his practical relation to the world stands in implicit contrast to Eduard's derivative postures
- [[Ottilie]] — the object of Eduard's mimetic desire; her handwriting mirrors his, and this mirroring is the novel's most troubling figuration of imitation as the ground of erotic attraction
