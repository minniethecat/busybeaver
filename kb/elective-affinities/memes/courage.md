## Definition

Courage (*Mut*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" denotes the inner fortitude required to confront uncomfortable truths, make difficult decisions, and endure the consequences of one's choices. It is less the battlefield bravery of epic tradition than the moral resolve needed to navigate intimate crises.

The novel distinguishes between authentic courage — the willingness to act on clear-sighted recognition of necessity — and its degenerate form, *Mutwille* (wantonness, reckless self-indulgence), which Luciane embodies. Genuine courage is grounded in self-knowledge; its opposite is the impulsiveness that refuses to reckon with consequences.

## In the Novel

Courage appears as a quality that several characters must summon at critical junctures. Charlotte is urged to find courage (*Mut einzusprechen*) in facing the dissolution of her marriage (025-032), a moment that tests her composure against the force of social expectation and personal grief.

Ottilie is the character who most needs courage — and who most conspicuously finds it, though in a form that leads toward renunciation rather than assertion. When encouraged, "she gave him courage that everything would soon be well again" (027-001), her courage takes the form of a quiet, steadfast faith that sustains others even as she herself diminishes.

The narrator comments on the "youthful courage and wantonness" (*jugendlichen Mut und Mutwillen*) of earlier, less burdened days (011-006), establishing a contrast between the carefree courage of youth and the heavier courage required by adult moral life. The distinction between *Mut* and *Mutwille* — between courage and self-indulgent recklessness — runs through the novel as a moral axis.
