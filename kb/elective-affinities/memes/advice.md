## Definition

Advice (*Rat*) in Goethe's "Elective Affinities" is a social practice under sustained skeptical scrutiny. The novel opens with a direct attack on the institution of advice-giving, and its subsequent action systematically demonstrates why advice fails: because the forces that determine human behavior — passion, affinity, fate — are not responsive to rational counsel. Advice assumes that understanding leads to action; the novel's chemical model of human relationships suggests otherwise.

Goethe's treatment of *Rat* is both comic and tragic. The comedy lies in the self-importance of advice-givers, the gap between the confidence with which counsel is offered and the certainty with which it is ignored. The tragedy lies in the moments when advice might genuinely have helped — and comes too late, or in the wrong form, or from the wrong source. The novel maps advice as a communication channel that is perpetually noisy, its signal degraded by the very passions it seeks to regulate.

## In the Novel

The novel's position on advice is announced in its second chapter, in an exchange between Eduard and the Captain that has the force of a thesis statement. "Do you believe," the Captain asks, "that I am in the world to give advice? That is the stupidest thing one can do" (002-034) — *daß ich in der Welt bin, um Rat zu geben? Das ist das dümmste*. The vehemence is startling and programmatic: the novel will not be a conduct book, will not dispense wisdom, will not pretend that human beings are improved by being told what to do. The Captain's self-mockery establishes advice as a suspect category from the outset.

Yet the novel does not simply abandon advice; it tracks its circulation through the social world with ethnographic precision. Charlotte, the character most identified with practical reason, responds to a problem with the characteristic phrase "advice can surely be found for that too" (003-016) — *läßt sich dazu wohl auch noch Rat finden*. Her confidence in the availability of advice is both her strength and her limitation: she believes that every problem has a counsel-shaped solution, a belief the novel's subsequent events will disprove.

The narrator's reflection on the school examination introduces a structural account of why advice fails: "the examiners conferred together and left us teachers little to say" (005-004) — *die Prüfenden zusammen Rat hielten und uns Lehrern wenig übrigließen*. The phrase *Rat halten* (to hold counsel, to deliberate) reveals the social dynamics of advice: it is produced by groups, distributed according to hierarchies, and its content is shaped more by the needs of the advisors than the needs of the advised. The teachers, who know the students best, are the ones excluded from the deliberation.

The novel's most concentrated meditation on the difficulty of advice appears in a narrator's aside: "that is why it is so hard to give advice, especially with the young" (006-031) — *Deswegen ist es so schwer, Rat zu pflegen, besonders mit der Jugend*. The verb *pflegen* (to cultivate, to practice) suggests that advice is not a one-time transaction but an ongoing practice — something that must be maintained, like a garden or a friendship. And the special difficulty with the young is not that they refuse advice but that they cannot hear it: their passions are too loud, their experience too thin.

The novel's second part introduces the motif of advice-as-plea. "Do not reject good advice," a character implores, "do not refuse the help that is offered to you" (016-015) — *den guten Rat nicht ab, nicht die Hülfe, die man dir bietet*. The pairing of advice with help (*Hülfe*) redefines counsel as a form of aid rather than instruction. Good advice is not about being right; it is about being present, offering resources the recipient may not know they need.

The social dimension of advice is most visible in the description of a character who "demanded their advice, their cooperation" (023-010) — *ihren Rat, ihre Mitwirkung verlangte*. Advice and cooperation are linked: to ask for counsel is to invite collaboration, to acknowledge that one's own judgment is insufficient. The character who demands advice is also the character who draws others into her orbit; advice-seeking is a form of social binding.

The novel's final treatment of *Rat* returns to the question of deliberation: "quickly consulting her feeling" (024-015) — *indem sie schnell ihr Gefühl zu Rate zog*. The phrase *zu Rate ziehen* (to consult, literally "to draw into counsel") is applied here not to another person but to one's own feeling. The inward turn is characteristic of the novel's second part: when external advice fails, characters consult themselves, and the results are no more reliable. Feeling, when called to counsel, gives advice as partial and as fallible as any human advisor.

## Related Characters

- [[the Captain]] — delivers the novel's definitive dismissal of advice-giving (002-034), yet continues to offer counsel throughout; the paradox of the advisor who knows advice is futile
- [[Charlotte]] — the character most identified with the belief that advice can solve problems; her faith in counsel is both her pragmatic strength and her epistemic limitation
- [[Eduard]] — notably immune to advice; his passions override counsel, demonstrating the novel's thesis that advice fails against affinity
- [[Ottilie]] — the character who seeks advice least and whose silent resistance to counsel is its own form of moral autonomy
