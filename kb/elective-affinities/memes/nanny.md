# Nanny

## Overview

**Nanny** is [[Ottilie]]'s servant and companion, a minor character whose
devotion to her mistress provides one of the novel's most emotionally direct
registers. Where the central characters' passions are mediated by reflection,
hesitation, and social constraint, Nanny's attachment to Ottilie is simple and
absolute. Her grief at the novel's conclusion strips away the philosophical
framing and confronts the reader with the raw fact of loss.

Nanny's name — diminutive, familiar — signals her role: she is not an equal but
an intimate, the one who sees Ottilie not as an object of desire or a moral
case study but as a person to be cared for.

## Role in the Novel

Nanny appears intermittently but crucially. She accompanies Ottilie when the
latter returns to the [[pensionat|boarding school]], serves her during her final
illness, and is present at the novel's catastrophic conclusion:

> Niemand als Nanny bediente sie.
> (035-016)

The isolation of Ottilie's final days — "no one but Nanny served her" —
establishes Nanny as the sole witness to her mistress's decline. She is the
character who sees what the others only hear about.

## Key Moments

### The Return to the Pension (Chapter 33)

When Ottilie returns to the boarding school, Nanny accompanies her as both
servant and guardian. The journey marks the beginning of Ottilie's withdrawal
from the world, and Nanny is her sole connection to ordinary life:

> Nanny sollte sie begleiten und ihre Wärterin sein.
> (033-026)

### The Final Illness (Chapter 35)

During Ottilie's self-imposed silence and fasting, Nanny is the only person
permitted to attend her. The intimacy of this arrangement — a servant who knows
her mistress's body and habits — contrasts with the abstract concern of the
other characters, who worry about Ottilie from a distance:

> Niemand als Nanny bediente sie. (035-016)

### The Death Scene (Chapter 36)

Nanny's most dramatic moment comes when she discovers Ottilie dead — or dying:

> mit einem Schrei hereinstürzend rief Nanny: „sie stirbt! Das
> Fräulein stirbt!"
> (036-014)

The raw panic of this cry — "she's dying! The young lady is dying!" — is the
novel's most unmediated expression of grief. Nanny's subsequent collapse and
revival, recounted in the novel's closing pages, forms a kind of coda:

> Nanny, von dem Arzt heftig gescholten, hatte alle Gebärden des
> Schmerzes, des Vorwurfs, der Verzweiflung.
> (036-021)

## Thematic Significance

Nanny serves a structural function in the novel's emotional economy. The main
characters — [[Eduard]], [[Charlotte]], the Captain — process their feelings
through reflection and rationalization. Nanny, by contrast, simply feels. Her
grief is the novel's reminder that beneath the chemical metaphor, the
philosophical conversations, and the careful symmetries of the plot, there is
simply the fact of human loss.
