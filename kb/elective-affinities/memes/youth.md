## Definition

Youth (*Jugend*) in *Elective Affinities* signifies both a biological stage of life and a formative period whose experiences and impressions indelibly shape adult character. Goethe, writing in the tradition of the *Bildungsroman*, treats youth not as a mere prelude to adulthood but as the foundation upon which all later psychological and emotional patterns are built.

## In the Novel

The theme of youth operates on multiple levels throughout the narrative. Eduard's character is explicitly traced to his upbringing as "the only spoiled child of a wealthy father" — his adult impulsiveness and self-centeredness are presented as direct consequences of a youth without restraint or hardship. His nostalgic attachment to the plane trees he planted in his youth reveals a man psychologically anchored to his younger self, unable to fully inhabit the present.

Ottilie embodies youth in its purest form. Her arrival at the estate as a young woman on the cusp of adulthood, her "childlike" quality that Charlotte notes, and her gradual awakening to love and its consequences all unfold within the frame of youthful development. The narrator repeatedly draws attention to her youth as both a source of her appeal and a vulnerability — she is still "at an age when one is shaped by one's surroundings."

The novel also explores youth through generational contrast. The discussions between Eduard, Charlotte, and the Count about education reform reflect larger Enlightenment-era debates about how youth should be shaped. The pension (boarding school) system that produced Ottilie serves as a case study in institutional approaches to youth development. Charlotte's concern for her daughter Luciane's education provides another angle on the same theme.
