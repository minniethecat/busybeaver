# Habit

## Definition

**Habit** (*Gewohnheit*) is the novel's most understated force — the accumulated
weight of repeated actions, settled routines, and unexamined attachments that
holds characters in place even as passion pulls them elsewhere. Goethe treats
habit not as the enemy of vitality (the Romantic cliché) but as a genuine moral
and psychological fact. Habit is what makes Charlotte's marriage durable when
inclination has faded; it is what makes Ottilie's departure from the estate
excruciating; it is what the novel's characters must reckon with when they
contemplate breaking old bonds and forming new ones. In the chemical analogy,
habit is the *activation energy* that must be overcome for any elective affinity
to assert itself.

## In the Novel

Habit appears early as a benign force, almost indistinguishable from
inclination:

> nach alter Gewohnheit und Neigung, in keinem Hause
> verweilen mochte
> (002-032)

The narrator notes that Eduard, "according to old habit and inclination," cannot
bear to linger in any house but his own. Habit here is simply the shape of a
life — neither virtue nor vice, but the given.

The novel deepens the concept when it turns to marriage. The Captain, observing
Charlotte, recognizes that habit has the power to bind where passion has
departed:

> eine unwiderstehliche Gewohnheit ihn an Charlotten zu
> fesseln drohte
> (008-010)

The word "irresistible" (*unwiderstehliche*) is telling — habit exerts a force
comparable to passion, but quieter and more persistent.

The novel's psychological realism is at its sharpest in passages that
acknowledge habit's hold on the reluctant:

> ungern gering scheinende Gewohnheiten, aber schmerzlich
> empfinden
> (017-001)

Characters suffer giving up "seemingly minor habits" — the small rituals and
comforts that constitute a shared life. This is Goethe's insight: that what
binds people is not only grand passion or solemn duty, but the daily texture of
familiarity.

The novel's final section returns to habit as a force that reasserts itself when
passion exhausts itself:

> im Gedränge treten zuletzt alte Gewohnheiten, alte Neigungen
> wieder hervor
> (018-033)

Old habits, old inclinations emerge again in the crush of events. This is not
redemption but entropy — the system returning to its lowest energy state.

Habit also has its dark side. Ottilie develops the "unhappy habit" of prefacing
her speech with gestures that make her harder to understand:

> die unglückliche Gewohnheit angenommen, das, was sie
> vortrug, durch eine Art von Vorspiel einzuleiten
> (023-019)

And in the novel's final pages, habit is named as a force that can dull
perception and moral sensitivity:

> auf uns macht, der nur durch Gewohnheit abgestumpft wird
> (025-047)

> die schleppende, träumerische Gewohnheit
> (028-035)

The "dragging, dreamy habit" becomes the medium of Ottilie's slow withdrawal
from life — habit as the opposite of vitality, the gradual dimming of presence.

## Chemical Metaphor

If elective affinity is the force that drives elements toward new combinations,
habit is the *resistance* that must be overcome for that force to act. In
chemistry, some compounds require heat or a catalyst to break apart — the bond
energy is too high for spontaneous dissociation. Habit is the bond energy of
human relations: the accumulated warmth of shared years that must be overcome
before any new connection can form.

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — habit's greatest ally and its most articulate defender; her
  commitment to the marriage is sustained by habit when inclination has
  migrated elsewhere
- [[Eduard]] — the character most impatient with habit; he treats it as an
  obstacle to be swept aside, and the novel judges him for this
- [[Ottilie]] — habit's most poignant victim; her "dragging, dreamy habit" in
  the novel's final pages is the physical manifestation of renunciation
- [[Mittler]] — a figure of rigid habit in the social sphere; his insistence
  on the indissolubility of marriage is a habit of mind rather than a living
  conviction
