## Definition

Honor (*Ehre*) in *Elective Affinities* encompasses both external social reputation and internal moral integrity. In the stratified society of early 19th-century Germany, honor functions as a regulatory principle governing conduct, particularly in matters of marriage, professional standing, and public perception. Goethe distinguishes between genuine moral honor and its superficial social counterpart, exploring the tension between reputation and authentic virtue.

## In the Novel

Honor appears as a structuring principle in the social world of the estate and its visitors. When the Count and Baroness visit, the narrative engages with questions of marital honor and social reputation — the Count's discussion of marriages that "make good sense" rather than good passion reflects an honor-based conception of matrimony. The Captain's professional honor as a military man is invoked to explain his restraint and discipline; his departure from the estate is partly motivated by the need to preserve his honor and career standing.

Charlotte is most explicitly concerned with honor in its social dimension. Her management of the estate, her careful attention to appearances, and her insistence on proper form all stem from a conception of honor rooted in *Sitte* (custom) and social expectation. Eduard, by contrast, repeatedly transgresses the boundaries of honorable conduct — his insistence on inviting the Captain despite Charlotte's reservations, his pursuit of Ottilie, and his decision to return to the estate after the tragedy all violate the honor-code of his social position.

The novel's most profound treatment of honor occurs in Ottilie's response to the child's death. Her refusal of food and speech represents a form of honor that transcends social convention — an absolute ethical stance that places moral integrity above survival. This radical honor, which the other characters observe with a mixture of awe and incomprehension, marks the limit of the novel's social world.
