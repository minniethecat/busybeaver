## Definition

Character (*Charakter* in German) in *Elective Affinities* denotes the stable moral and psychological disposition that constitutes a person's core identity — the enduring pattern of thought, feeling, and conduct that persists beneath shifting circumstances and competing inclinations. Goethe's treatment of *Charakter* is both descriptive and evaluative: characters in the novel are repeatedly assessed, by themselves and by the narrator, for whether they act "in character" or "out of character," and deviations from character are treated as morally significant events. The concept bridges the novel's psychological realism with its ethical inquiry, since acting against one's character is presented not merely as inconsistency but as a form of self-betrayal.

## In the Novel

Character is introduced early as a hereditary transmission. In Chapter 2, the narrator observes in Luciane "the whole character of her mother" — a remark that frames character as something recognizable, inherited, and legible in conduct (002-010). This theme of legibility recurs in Chapter 6, where the pedagogical question is posed: how quickly must one become acquainted with the character of others (006-010)? The speed at which character can (or cannot) be known becomes a narrative problem — characters misread one another, sometimes fatally.

Eduard's relationship to his own character is marked by self-conscious departure. In Chapter 9, he bursts out: "But why do I go out of my character! I should not have come here" (009-042). This exclamation reveals that Eduard knows his character but cannot consistently inhabit it; he experiences his own conduct as a deviation from a known baseline, yet he lacks the will to correct course.

The novel's most sustained theoretical engagement with *Charakter* occurs in Chapters 22 and 23. In Chapter 22, the narrator reflects that "one knows people more by what they find laughable than by their character" (022-046) — an inversion that suggests character may be less legible than the spontaneous responses it shapes. Chapter 23 then poses the question explicitly: "How can character, the peculiarity of a person, coexist with a complete deviation from it?" (023-043). The answer, developed across the chapter, is that character is not a rigid template but a ground against which deviations appear; even when someone acts out of character, "they do not entirely leave their character, for behind every mask there is ultimately something true and genuine" (023-047).

In the novel's final movement, character becomes juridical. The narrator, describing Charlotte's painful decision to consent to divorce, invokes "character, individuality, inclination, direction" as the determinants that give every action its specific moral coloring (035-017). Character here is not merely a psychological category but a moral one — the principle that makes each person's actions uniquely their own and uniquely answerable.

> Aber warum gehe ich aus meinem Charakter heraus! Ich hätte nicht kommen sollen.
> (009-042)

> Bezeichnen die Menschen mehr ihren Charakter als durch das, was sie lächerlich finden?
> (022-046)

> Wie kann der Charakter, die Eigentümlichkeit des Menschen mit einer völligen Abweichung von demselben bestehen?
> (023-043)

> Sie gehen wenigstens nicht aus ihrem Charakter, und weil doch meist hinter der Larve sogleich wieder etwas Wahres und Wirkliches hervorscheint.
> (023-047)

> Charakter, Individualität, Neigung, Richtung, Örtlichkeit, Umgebungen und Gewohnheiten bilden zusammen ein Ganzes, in welchem jeder Mensch, wie in einem Elemente, in einer Atmosphäre schwimmt, worin es ihm allein bequem und behaglich ist.
> (035-017)

## Related Characters

- [[Eduard]] — the character who most self-consciously experiences his actions as departures from his own character; his tragedy lies in knowing himself without mastering himself
- [[Charlotte]] — possesses the most stable and consistent character in the novel; her decisions rarely surprise either herself or the reader
- [[Ottilie]] — her character is presented as unusually unified: her silence, her devotion, her self-denial are all of a piece; when she breaks with this pattern, it is catastrophic
- [[Luciane]] — introduced as the inheritor of her mother's character, though she expresses it through volatile social performance rather than steady moral judgment
