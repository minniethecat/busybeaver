## Definition

**Force** (*Gewalt*) is the operative principle behind the novel's central
chemical metaphor. In the laboratory, elective affinities are demonstrated when
one element compels another to separate from its partner — a *Gewalt* that
overcomes existing bonds. Goethe extends this scientific concept into the human
sphere: the force of attraction, the force of self-restraint, the force of social
convention, and the force that shatters all restraint. The word *Gewalt* carries
a semantic range from "power/authority" to "violence/compulsion," and Goethe
exploits every register.

## In the Novel

The word appears early, in Eduard's opening declaration to Charlotte about the
self-restraint he has already exercised:

> …u und habe mir schon eben die Gewalt angetan, die ich dir nun über…
> (002-007)

"I have already done violence to myself" — Eduard frames restraint as
self-inflicted force, a temporary violence against his own inclinations. The
sentence reveals his moral logic: restraint is unnatural, force is the only
check, and force can be withdrawn.

The chemical lecture in Chapter 4 provides the theoretical foundation. The speaker
explains that certain compounds cannot be separated except through force:

> …lassen sie nicht, außer durch Gewalt oder sonstige Bestimmung. Ist…
> (004-026)

"Except through force or other determination" — the alternatives are explicit.
In the human sphere, "other determination" might be the elective affinity itself:
attraction as a force that dissolves prior bonds.

Charlotte's self-command is described as a force she exercises over herself. After
Eduard's departure, she believes she has mastered her own inclinations:

> …egen glaubte sie nun auch die Gewalt, die sie über sich selbst aus…
> (016-001)

"She believed she now had the force over herself" — the phrasing is significant.
Force over oneself (*Gewalt über sich selbst*) is the moral achievement that
distinguishes Charlotte and Ottilie from Eduard, who exercises force only over
others or in self-gratification.

The Captain observes that no one has more force over Eduard than a woman:

> …ich bemerkt, daß niemand mehr Gewalt über Sie hat als ein Frauenzi…
> (010-033)

The remark is diagnostic: Eduard is governed by external forces — specifically,
by women — rather than by internal discipline.

In the novel's most programmatic statement about self-governance, the narrator
observes:

> …uns geneigt, indem wir soviel Gewalt über uns selbst üben, unsre H…
> (010-045)

"In that we exercise so much force over ourselves" — self-governance is a
*Gewalt*, a force that must be actively exerted. The sentence is the novel's
ethical core: freedom is not the absence of force but the right internal force,
properly directed.

Ottilie's trajectory traces the transformation of *Gewalt* from external
compulsion to internal mastery. In the boating scene, physical force is required:

> …s Ruder und stößt ab. Sie muß Gewalt brauchen, sie wiederholt den …
> (031-021)

But in the novel's final movement, she no longer needs force over herself —
self-mastery has become her nature:

> …eschicks. Sie bedurfte keiner Gewalt mehr über sich selbst; sie ha…
> (033-005)

"She needed no more force over herself" — the ultimate moral state is one in
which discipline has been so fully internalized that it ceases to feel like
compulsion. This is the novel's answer to Eduard's opening self-violence: the
force that begins as restraint can, through Ottilie's path, become grace.

## Chemical Metaphor

In the novel's chemical analogy, *Gewalt* is the force that separates bound
elements and enables new combinations. The chemist applies heat or a reagent —
external force — to break existing bonds. Goethe's human translation of this
principle is precise: love, desire, and elective affinity itself are the
"chemical forces" that dissolve marriages and forge new unions. The moral
question the novel poses is whether these forces operate like physical laws —
inevitable and amoral — or whether self-governance (*Gewalt über sich selbst*)
can redirect them.
