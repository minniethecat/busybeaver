## Definition

Gratitude (*Dankbarkeit* in German) in *Elective Affinities* is a complex moral emotion that occupies the ambiguous territory between spontaneous feeling and social obligation. Goethe traces gratitude across the novel's emotional register: from Eduard's effusive thankfulness toward Charlotte for agreeing to invite the Captain, to the quiet, unspoken gratitude of Ottilie toward those who shelter her, to the novel's final scene in which the dying Ottilie's gestures express "love, gratitude, apology, and the heartiest farewell" (036-017). Gratitude in this world is never merely felt; it must be performed, and the performance — whether in writing, in speech, or in the silent language of the body — is always subject to misreading, excess, and the shadow of indebtedness.

## In the Novel

Gratitude's first major appearance is charged with dramatic irony. In Chapter 2, Eduard thanks Charlotte effusively for agreeing to invite the Captain: "Eduard assured his wife in the most charming manner of his liveliest gratitude. He hurried with a free, joyful spirit to put his proposals to his friend in writing. Charlotte was to add her approval in a postscript in her own hand" (002-040). The scene is domestic, even tender — but the gratitude Eduard expresses is for a decision that will unravel their marriage. Charlotte's pen produces "a blot of ink, which made her cross and only grew larger as she tried to wipe it away" (002-040). The inkblot is a classic Goethean omen: gratitude performed in writing already bears the stain of what it will bring about.

In Chapter 17, after Eduard has departed, Charlotte articulates a philosophy of gratitude that doubles as a strategy for managing grief: "how lively is the gratitude of those whom we help, with calm, beyond passionate embarrassments! Let us gladly and cheerfully intervene in what the men have left unfinished; thus we prepare for ourselves the most beautiful prospect of their return, by preserving and furthering through our moderation what their stormy, impatient nature would destroy" (017-006). Gratitude here is not a response to a gift received but an anticipated return on emotional labor. Charlotte proposes to earn gratitude by tending the garden the men abandoned — making gratitude a form of moral investment.

The gardener's repeated questions about Eduard's return press on Ottilie with the weight of unexpressed gratitude: "the good man let her notice, not without quiet sorrow, that he believed she did not trust him, and painful to her was the feeling of ignorance that was in this way fairly forced upon her" (017-021). Gratitude here is what cannot be given — because Ottilie herself does not know when Eduard will return. The debt of gratitude becomes indistinguishable from the debt of love, and both remain unpaid.

The Architect's gratitude in Chapter 24 introduces a different register. Having been excluded from the tableau vivant performances, and having witnessed Charlotte's ill health, "he did not want to leave without proving his gratitude also by arranging a far more beautiful presentation than the previous ones had been, in honor of the one and for the entertainment of the other. Perhaps there came to this, unbeknownst to himself, another secret impulse: it was so hard for him to leave this house, this family, indeed it seemed impossible to him to part from Ottilie's eyes" (024-016). Gratitude is the socially acceptable pretext; beneath it runs the current of erotic fascination. The Architect's gratitude-performance — the living nativity scene — is the novel's most exquisite example of a moral emotion serving as cover for a more dangerous feeling.

The novel's final invocation of gratitude comes at the moment of Ottilie's death: "her gestures expressed to those standing around the tenderest attachment, love, gratitude, apology, and the heartiest farewell" (036-017). In this terminal compression of emotional expression — where gratitude stands between love and apology — Goethe suggests that *Dankbarkeit* is the emotion that binds the living to the dying, the obliged to the obliger. It is, in the end, what remains when speech fails.

## Key Passages

> Eduard versicherte seine Gattin auf die anmutigste Weise der lebhaftesten Dankbarkeit. Er eilte mit freiem, frohem Gemüt, seinem Freunde Vorschläge schriftlich zu tun. Charlotte mußte in einer Nachschrift ihren Beifall eigenhändig hinzufügen... und was ihr nicht leicht begegnete, sie verunstaltete das Papier zuletzt mit einem Tintenfleck, der sie ärgerlich machte und nur größer wurde, indem sie ihn wegwischen wollte.
> (002-040)

> Wie lebhaft ist die Dankbarkeit derjenigen, denen wir mit Ruhe über leidenschaftliche Verlegenheiten hinaushelfen! Laß uns freudig und munter in das eingreifen, was die Männer unvollendet zurückgelassen haben; so bereiten wir uns die schönste Aussicht auf ihre Rückkehr, indem wir das, was ihr stürmendes, ungeduldiges Wesen zerstören möchte, durch unsre Mäßigung erhalten und fördern.
> (017-006)

> Nun wollte er sich nicht entfernen, ohne seine Dankbarkeit auch dadurch zu beweisen, daß er zur Ehre der einen und zur Unterhaltung der andern eine weit schönere Darstellung veranstaltete, als die bisherigen gewesen waren. Vielleicht kam hierzu, ihm selbst unbewußt, ein andrer geheimer Antrieb: es ward ihm so schwer, dieses Haus, diese Familie zu verlassen, ja es schien ihm unmöglich, von Ottiliens Augen zu scheiden.
> (024-016)

> Sie scheint Abschied nehmen zu wollen, ihre Gebärden drücken den Umstehenden die zarteste Anhänglichkeit aus, Liebe, Dankbarkeit, Abbitte und das herzlichste Lebewohl.
> (036-017)
