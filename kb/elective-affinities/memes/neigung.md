# Neigung

## Definition

**Neigung** (inclination) is the novel's master concept — the psychological
equivalent of the chemical "elective affinity" (*Wahlverwandtschaft*) that gives
the work its title. In Goethe's usage, *Neigung* denotes a natural, almost
involuntary attraction between persons: a gravitational pull that operates
beneath rational choice and social convention. Unlike the English "inclination,"
which suggests mere preference, Goethe's *Neigung* carries the weight of natural
law — it is to human relationships what chemical affinity is to elements — a [[Leidenschaft|passion]] that, given the right conditions, will inevitably reorder existing bonds — a
force that will, given the right conditions, inevitably reorder existing bonds.

The concept is introduced through the novel's famous chemical analogy, in which
[[Charlotte]], [[Eduard]], and the Captain discuss how limestone and sulfuric
acid, once combined, form gypsum — each element "choosing" a new partner and
releasing the old one. (004-037) This laboratory observation becomes the
novel's structural metaphor: human beings, like chemical substances, are
governed by affinities that can dissolve marriages and form new unions.

## In the Novel

*Neigung* operates at every level of the narrative. It is first presented as a
theoretical idea — discussed over dinner in Chapter 4 — but rapidly becomes the
engine of the plot. Each of the four central characters is seized by a *Neigung*
that draws them away from their existing attachments:

| Character | Object of Neigung | PN Evidence |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| [[Eduard]] | [[Ottilie]] | "Eduards Neigung war aber grenzenlos" (014-008) |
| [[Charlotte]] | [[the-captain|the Captain]] | "Charlottens Neigung zum Hauptmann" (013-019) |
| [[Ottilie]] | [[Eduard]] | "Ottiliens stille Neigung" (008-006) |
| [[the-captain|the Captain]] | [[Charlotte]] | implied through parallel structure |

Goethe traces *Neigung* from its earliest, most innocent stages — Eduard's
boyhood affection for Charlotte — through its mature and dangerous
intensification:

> bis in die ersten Epochen der Neigung Eduards zu Charlotten. Ottilie wird
> uns bald eine Menge Vorteile gewähren.
> (007-002)

The narrator repeatedly emphasizes that *Neigung* is not a moral failing but a
natural phenomenon. Charlotte herself, reflecting on Ottilie's situation with
the young Architect, acknowledges:

> wie hoch jede wahre Neigung zu schätzen sei in einer Welt, wo
> Gleichgültigkeit und Abneigung eigentlich recht zu Hause sind.
> (003-035)

This validation of *Neigung* as something "true" (*wahr*) and precious — even in
a world of indifference — is the novel's most subversive move. It grants
dignity to the very force that will destroy the characters' lives.

## Chemical Metaphor

The novel's central metaphor — *Wahlverwandtschaft*, or elective affinity — is
the chemical counterpart of *Neigung*. In Chapter 4, the Captain explains:

> Was ihr Wahlverwandtschaften nennt, sind eigentlich Verwandtschaften nach
> entgegengesetzten Polen. Jenes berühmte Beispiel des Kalksteins und der
> Schwefelsäure...
> (004-037)

The chemical logic is precise: substance A is bonded to substance B, and
substance C to D. When all four are brought together, A abandons B for D, and C
abandons D for B. The "choice" (*Wahl*) is not free but determined by innate
"relationship" (*Verwandtschaft*). The human parallel is explicit: bring
together a married couple and two new acquaintances, and the same rearrangement
will occur — not through vice, but through the nature of affinity itself.

The chemical metaphor serves as both explanation and exoneration. If human
*Neigung* follows the same laws as chemical affinity, then Eduard's passion for
Ottilie is not a moral choice but a natural inevitability. The novel never fully
endorses this view — the tragic ending argues against it — but neither does it
refute the metaphor. The ambiguity is the point.

## Key Passages

> Denn eigentlich war die Neigung dieser beiden ebensogut im Wachsen, und die
> Gefahr war so groß, als beide unter einem Dache vereint waren.
> (008-009)

> sogleich behauptete die innre Neigung, behauptete die Einbildungskraft ihre
> Rechte über das Wirkliche: Eduard sah Ottilien nur in Charlotten.
> (011-023)

> Sie dachten nicht daran, auf eine so schöne, edle Neigung Verzicht zu tun.
> (013-017)

> Neigung und Leidenschaft entspringt und nährt sich auch wohl an wenigem.
> (016-008)

> Freundschaft, Neigung, Entsagen gingen vor ihr in heitern Bildern vorüber.
> (012-023)

## Related Characters

- **[[Eduard]]** — the character most completely governed by *Neigung*; his
  "grenzenlos" inclination toward Ottilie drives the novel's central tragedy
- **[[Ottilie]]** — her "stille Neigung" (quiet inclination) toward Eduard is
  the novel's purest and most destructive form of the concept
- **[[Charlotte]]** — represents the counterforce of *Sitte* (custom, order);
  her own *Neigung* toward the Captain is more measured but equally real
- **[[the-captain|the Captain]]** — the rational man whose scientific
  explanation of elective affinity sets the novel's conceptual framework
- **[[der-graf|der Graf]]** — the Count whose worldly philosophy provides an
  intellectual rationalization for following *Neigung* wherever it leads
