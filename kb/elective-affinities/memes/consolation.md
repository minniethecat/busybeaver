## Definition

**Consolation** (*Trost*) in *Elective Affinities* is a failed art. The novel
systematically tests the proposition that one person can ease another's
suffering through words, presence, or shared feeling — and almost every test
returns a negative result.

Goethe does not treat consolation as a simple comfort that characters either
give or withhold. He treats it as a test of the relationship between speech and
suffering. The question the novel poses is whether words can reach the place
where suffering lives — and its answer, across multiple scenes and characters,
is that they cannot.

## In the Novel

The novel's first mention of consolation is already couched in irony. Eduard
receives the conventional comfort after a loss:

> …wöhnlich pflegt, den leidigen Trost, daß auch solche Schmerzen du…
> (011-014)

The "wretched consolation" (*leidigen Trost*) that such pains are temporary —
the novel's first treatment of consolation marks it as something offered by
rote, a formula that the sufferer is expected to accept but cannot feel.

For Ottilie's companion, the arrival of a confidante is a genuine comfort:

> …o war es für diese ein großer Trost, als jene gelegentlich mit Be…
> (017-006)

It was a great consolation (*ein großer Trost*) when the other occasionally
shared her thoughts. Consolation here takes the form of presence and attention
rather than speech. The comfort lies in being accompanied, not in being advised.

The novel's most radical statement on consolation comes from Eduard in a moment
of desperate self-justification:

> …, ja, es gibt deren! Wo jeder Trost niederträchtig und Verzweiflu…
> (018-015)

"Where every consolation is base and despair is duty" — *Wo jeder Trost
niederträchtig*. This is the novel's anti-consolation manifesto. There are
situations, Eduard insists, where accepting comfort would be a moral failure.
Consolation is not always a good; it can be a betrayal of the truth of
suffering.

After the child's death, Eduard's peculiar consolation is the thought of his own
impending dissolution:

> …den drohte; ja es war ihm ein Trost zu denken, daß er nicht mehr …
> (018-033)

It was a consolation to him to think that he would not live much longer. This is
consolation as negation — the comfort of extinction, the relief of imagining an
end to consciousness.

The funeral scene introduces the minimal consolation of material memorial:

> … begräbt, ist es eine Art von Trost, ein schwaches hölzernes Kreu…
> (019-008)

When one buries someone beloved, it is a kind of consolation (*eine Art von
Trost*) to set a weak wooden cross on the grave. The consolation is in the
gesture, not in the effect — the cross does nothing for the dead, but the act of
placing it is what the living need.

The novel's most cynical line about consolation comes as an aphorism:

> Es gibt keinen größern Trost für die Mittelmäßigkeit, als …
> (023-061)

"There is no greater consolation for mediocrity than that genius is not
immortal." The consolation is not for the suffering but for the mediocre — a
cold comfort that depends on another's limitation rather than on one's own
worth. Goethe places this observation in the voice of the narrator, giving it
the weight of authorial judgment.

After Ottilie's death, the supernatural enters the discourse of consolation:

> … nächtliche Erscheinungen zum Trost gegeben, die ihr das Dasein d…
> (026-025)

Nocturnal apparitions were given to her as consolation, assuring her of the
existence of the beloved. Consolation here takes a visionary form — not words
but images, not argument but apparition. It is the novel's only instance of
consolation that actually works, and it requires the supernatural to do so.

The final mention of consolation names the grounds that fail to persuade:

> …einem Handwerk, als daß diese Trostgründe viel bei ihm hätten fru…
> (027-001)

These grounds for consolation (*Trostgründe*) could not take hold. The compound
*Trostgründe* — consolation-grounds, reasons-for-comfort — marks the
rationalist's approach to consolation: give reasons, and the feeling will
follow. The novel's judgment is that reasons do not work.

## Related Characters

- [[Eduard]] — the novel's most radical critic of consolation; his insistence
  that "every consolation is base" marks the ethical limit of the concept
- [[Ottilie]] — the primary object of others' consolatory efforts; neither
  music nor words can reach her after the child's death; only supernatural
  vision provides what human consolation cannot
- [[Charlotte]] — offers the conventional consolations of shared feeling and
  rational perspective, both of which the novel shows to be insufficient
- [[Luciane]] — incapable of genuine consolation; her social performances
  around suffering are the novel's demonstration of what consolation is not

## See Also

- [[Guilt]] — the condition that makes consolation feel "base"; where guilt
  is real, comfort can feel like evasion
- [[Death]] — the event that most demands consolation and most resists it
- [[Grief]] — the state for which consolation is offered but from which no
  speech can deliver
- [[Suffering]] — the broader category of experience that consolation
  promises to ease but the novel suggests is inaccessible to language
- [[Speech]] — the medium of most consolation and the reason most
  consolation fails; the novel consistently opposes speech-based comfort to
  the wordless presence that sometimes helps
