## Definition

Error (*Irrtum* in German) in *Elective Affinities* is more than simple factual mistake: it names a cognitive and moral condition that pervades the novel's tragic structure. Goethe deploys *Irrtum* at a pivotal moment of psychological insight, when Charlotte warns Ottilie against the delusive effect of distance from the beloved. The concept encompasses both self-deception (the belief that separation strengthens resolve) and the catastrophic moment of disillusionment (the sudden recognition that what one thought dispensable has become indispensable). Error, in this sense, is not a punctual failure of knowledge but a structural feature of passionate consciousness: desire distorts perception, and the correction of error arrives too late to prevent tragedy.

## In the Novel

The term *Irrtum* surfaces at a crucial turning point in Chapter 33, during Charlotte's conversation with Ottilie about the latter's resolution to leave the estate and renounce Eduard forever. Charlotte, acting with characteristic ethical seriousness, does not simply accept Ottilie's decision but interrogates it. She warns that the resolve formed in absence may be an illusion: "When we are distant from the beloved object, we seem, the livelier our inclination is, to become all the more master of ourselves, since we turn the whole force of passion, as it extended outward, inward; but how soon, how swiftly are we torn from this error when that which we believed we could do without suddenly stands before our eyes again as indispensable" (033-021).

Charlotte's analysis is both psychologically acute and structurally prophetic. The "error" she describes is the novel's fundamental mechanism: characters believe they have mastered their passions precisely when those passions have merely gone underground. Ottilie's vow of renunciation, Eduard's resolution to stay away, the Captain's strategic withdrawal — all are forms of the same *Irrtum*, the delusion that geographical separation can neutralize emotional entanglement. The novel's catastrophe unfolds from the rupture of exactly this error: when absence collapses and the beloved object reappears, the carefully constructed edifice of renunciation disintegrates.

The concept of error also operates at the level of social judgment. Throughout the novel, characters misread one another: Eduard mistakes Charlotte's reasonableness for consent; the company mistakes Ottilie's silence for simplicity; Mittler mistakes his own moral certainty for wisdom. These cascading errors of interpretation form the epistemic substrate of the tragedy — a world in which no one truly understands anyone else, and every action is undertaken on the basis of a misreading.

## Key Passages

> „Wenn dein Entschluß“, entgegnete ihr Charlotte, „Eduarden zu entsagen, so fest und unveränderlich ist, so hüte dich nur vor der Gefahr des Wiedersehens. In der Entfernung von dem geliebten Gegenstande scheinen wir, je lebhafter unsere Neigung ist, desto mehr Herr von uns selbst zu werden, indem wir die ganze Gewalt der Leidenschaft, wie sie sich nach außen erstreckte, nach innen wenden; aber wie bald, wie geschwind sind wir aus diesem Irrtum gerissen, wenn dasjenige, was wir entbehren zu können glaubten, auf einmal wieder als unentbehrlich vor unsern Augen steht. Tue jetzt, was du deinen Zuständen am gemäßesten hältst; prüfe dich, ja verändre lieber deinen gegenwärtigen Entschluß: aber aus dir selbst, aus freiem, wollendem Herzen. Laß dich nicht zufällig, nicht durch Überraschung in die vorigen Verhältnisse wieder hineinziehen; dann gibt es erst einen Zwiespalt im Gemüt, der unerträglich ist.“
> (033-021)

> Wie gesagt, ehe du diesen Schritt tust, ehe du dich von mir entfernst und ein neues Leben anfängst, das dich wer weiß auf welche Wege leitet, so bedenke noch einmal, ob du denn wirklich für alle Zukunft Eduarden entsagen kannst.
> (033-022)

> „Ich müßte mich sehr irren“, versetzte Charlotte, „wenn deine Neigung dich nicht zur Pension zurückzöge.“
> (033-013)

## Related Characters

- [[Charlotte]] — the novel's most penetrating analyst of error; her warning to Ottilie about the delusive effect of distance is the novel's most concentrated statement of its tragic epistemology
- [[Ottilie]] — the character whose resolution, formed in the false security of Eduard's absence, proves catastrophically vulnerable to the "error" Charlotte diagnoses
- [[Eduard]] — the character most thoroughly in the grip of error; his belief that passion exempts him from obligation is the novel's central epistemic and moral mistake
- [[the Captain]] — his strategic withdrawal enacts the very error Charlotte describes: physical absence does not extinguish emotional presence
