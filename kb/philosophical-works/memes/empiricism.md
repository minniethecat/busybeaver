# Empiricism

**Empiricism** is the epistemological tradition that developed in dialogue with — and often in opposition to — Cartesian [[rationalism]]. Where [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] grounded [[knowledge]] in innate ideas and deductive reason, the empiricists — [[locke|John Locke]], [[berkeley|George Berkeley]], and [[hume|David Hume]] — argued that all ideas derive from sensory experience. The mind at birth is a *tabula rasa*, a blank slate; all our concepts, from "[[substance]]" to "[[god]]," must be traced back to the impressions and reflections that furnished them.

## The Three Great Empiricists

**John Locke** (1632–1704): The founder of British empiricism. Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) explicitly targets Descartes' doctrine of innate ideas, arguing that if there were innate principles, children and "idiots" would be aware of them. All knowledge comes from sensation (external experience) and reflection (internal experience of our own mental operations).

**George Berkeley** (1685–1753): The idealist empiricist. Berkeley agreed with Locke that all ideas come from experience but drew a radical conclusion: if we only know ideas, and ideas exist only in the mind, then material [[substance]] — Descartes' extended stuff — does not exist. *Esse est percipi*: to be is to be perceived.

**David Hume** (1711–1776): The sceptical empiricist. Hume pushed empiricism to its limit, arguing that neither the self (as a permanent [[substance]]), nor [[causality]] (as necessary connection), nor the external world (as independent reality) can be known through experience. Hume's critique "awakened [[kant]] from his dogmatic slumber."

## Empiricism and Descartes

Descartes was the primary target of Locke's *Essay*. Descartes' claim that the [[cogito]] reveals an innate idea of the self as thinking substance, and that the idea of [[god]] is implanted in the mind at creation, were the doctrines Locke set out to refute.

Yet empiricism is not simply anti-Cartesian. Descartes' own [[method]] — rejecting all received opinion and starting from what is immediately given — has empiricist overtones. And his mechanistic physics, by reducing nature to matter in [[motion]] knowable through observation and mathematics, contributed to the empirical-scientific worldview the empiricists championed.
