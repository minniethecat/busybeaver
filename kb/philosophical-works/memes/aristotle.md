# Aristotle

**Aristotle** (384–322 BCE) was the dominant philosophical authority of the medieval and early modern periods. For Descartes, Aristotle represents both the tradition to be overthrown and — more subtly — a source of principles that Descartes claims to preserve. Descartes' relationship to Aristotle is one of the defining tensions of his philosophy: he rejects Aristotelian [[substance|substantial forms]] and final [[cause|causes]] while simultaneously insisting that his own mechanistic physics "made use of no principle which was not received and approved by Aristotle" (058-002). Understanding this relationship is essential for grasping the full scope of Descartes' philosophical project and its place in the history of Western thought.

## Biography

Aristotle was born in Stagira (Macedonia) in 384 BCE. He studied at [[plato|Plato's]] Academy in Athens for twenty years, tutored Alexander the Great, and founded his own school, the Lyceum. His surviving works — compiled lecture notes rather than polished dialogues — cover logic, metaphysics, natural philosophy, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics.

Aristotle's natural philosophy, as transmitted and developed by the scholastics (particularly **Thomas Aquinas**), was the dominant philosophical framework of the universities Descartes attended. At **La Flèche**, the Jesuit college where Descartes was educated (1607–1615), Aristotle was taught through the lens of **Thomas Aquinas** and **Francisco Suárez**.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes' relationship to Aristotle is complex and often misunderstood. On the one hand, Descartes explicitly rejects Aristotelianism as the philosophy of the schools. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]*, he describes how "the majority of those who in these later ages aspired to be philosophers, blindly followed Aristotle, so that they frequently corrupted the sense of his writings, and attributed to him various opinions which he would not recognise as his own" (022-007). The scholastic Aristotelians are Descartes' primary polemical target.

On the other hand, Descartes claims that his own philosophy is compatible with Aristotle's genuine principles. In the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*, he writes: "I have nevertheless made use of no principle which was not received and approved by Aristotle, and by the other philosophers of all ages; so that this philosophy, so far from being new, is of all others the most ancient and common: for I have in truth only considered the figure, motion, and magnitude of bodies" (058-002). This is a strategic claim — Descartes presents mechanism not as a rejection of Aristotle but as a return to his authentic principles, purged of scholastic distortions.

Descartes also acknowledges Aristotle's [[method]]ology: "Aristotle himself expressly says... that, with regard to things which are not manifest to the senses, he thinks to adduce sufficient reasons and demonstrations of them, if he only shows that they may be such as he explains them" (062-003). Descartes cites this as precedent for his own hypothetical method in physics.

## Key Ideas

Aristotle's philosophy that is most relevant to Descartes includes:

**Substance and Accident**: Aristotle's *Categories* distinguishes primary substances (individual things) from their accidents (properties). Descartes' own substance-accident distinction, while differently grounded (extension/thought vs. matter/form), preserves this categorical framework. Descartes' tripartite division of substance ([[god|God]], [[mind]], [[body]]) replaces Aristotle's multiplicity of substantial forms.

**The Four Causes**: Aristotle's explanatory framework — material, formal, efficient, and final causes — is dramatically simplified by Descartes. Descartes retains only **efficient causation** (mechanistic push-pull interaction) and rejects **final causes** (purposes in nature) as unknowable by finite minds. This rejection is one of Descartes' most consequential breaks with Aristotelianism.

**Hylomorphism**: Aristotle's doctrine that natural substances are composites of matter (*hyle*) and form (*morphe*) is the primary target of Descartes' mechanism. Descartes argues that all natural phenomena can be explained by matter (extension) and motion alone, without substantial forms. "The nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing" (032-002) — this is the anti-hylomorphic principle.

**Physics and Cosmology**: Aristotle's physics — with its distinction between sublunary and superlunary realms, its four elements, and its natural places — is fully rejected by Descartes. Descartes' unified physics, applying the same laws to earth and heavens, is a direct repudiation of Aristotelian cosmology.

## Descartes' Break with Aristotle: A Summary

| Aristotelian Doctrine | Cartesian Replacement | Key Text |
|----------------------|----------------------|----------|
| Substantial forms | Matter as pure extension (*res extensa*) | *Principles* II.4 (032-002) |
| Final causes | Efficient causation only; purpose unknowable | *Meditations* IV |
| Four elements (earth/air/fire/water) | Three elements (fire/air/earth), corpuscular | *Principles* III |
| Sublunary/superlunary divide | Unified physics for all bodies | *Principles* II.22 |
| Natural places and motions | Laws of motion (inertia, collision) | *Principles* II.37–39 |
| Hylomorphic soul | Soul as thinking substance (*res cogitans*) | *Meditations* VI |

Despite these radical breaks, Descartes' system retains key Aristotelian structural features: the priority of substance over modes, the role of first principles, and the ideal of a deductively organized science.
