# Socrates

**Socrates** (c. 470–399 BCE) is the archetypal philosopher — the figure who, through Plato's dialogues, established rational inquiry as the path to wisdom. Descartes invokes Socrates in the *Discourse on the Method* as the model of intellectual honesty: the willingness to confess what one does not know.

## Biography

Socrates wrote nothing; he is known through the dialogues of his student **Plato**, the memoirs of **Xenophon**, and the satire of **Aristophanes**. He was executed by Athens in 399 BCE on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth. His method — the *elenchus* (cross-examination) — exposed contradictions in his interlocutors' beliefs, forcing them to acknowledge their ignorance.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes mentions Socrates in his discussion of ancient philosophy. In the *Discourse*, he notes that Plato, "following in the footsteps of his master, Socrates, ingenuously confessed that he had never yet been able to find anything certain" (022-005). This Socratic confession of ignorance is the ancient precedent for Descartes' methodical doubt — both begin by acknowledging the inadequacy of received opinion.

However, Descartes goes beyond Socrates. Socrates exposed ignorance but did not claim to have found certain knowledge. Descartes' ambition is greater: to move through doubt to certainty. The *cogito* does what Socrates never claimed to do — it establishes an indubitable first truth.

## Key Ideas

**Socratic Method**: The practice of questioning beliefs to expose contradictions — an ancestor of Descartes' methodical doubt. Both are critical methods that clear away false opinion. But Descartes' method is constructive: it leads to positive knowledge, while Socratic *elenchus* was primarily destructive.

**Intellectual Honesty**: Socrates' willingness to admit ignorance — "I know that I know nothing" — is the virtue Descartes recommends to his readers. In the *Principles* Appendix, Descartes asks readers to "consider how feeble are the reasons that have hitherto led them to repose faith in their senses" (153-015) — a Socratic exercise in epistemic humility.
