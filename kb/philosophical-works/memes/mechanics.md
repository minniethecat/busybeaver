In Descartes' philosophy, **mechanics** is the science of bodies in motion, governed by the laws of nature that God established at creation. Descartes reduced all physical explanation to the principles of mechanics: matter (*res extensa*) moving according to deterministic laws, with no appeal to occult qualities, substantial forms, or final causes.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes presents his mechanical physics most systematically in the *Principles of Philosophy*. He holds that the laws of mechanics "are the same with those of nature" — that is, there is no distinction between mechanical laws and physical laws (061-002). All natural phenomena, from planetary orbits to biological processes, are to be explained by the size, shape, and motion of material particles.

This is a radical departure from Aristotelian natural philosophy, which explained change in terms of form, matter, privation, and the four causes. Descartes substitutes a purely quantitative and geometrical physics: the essence of matter is extension, and all change is local motion. The three fundamental laws of nature — concerning the conservation of motion, the tendency of bodies to move in straight lines, and the transfer of motion in collisions — form the axiomatic core of his system.

>

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' mechanical philosophy has far-reaching consequences. By eliminating final causes from physics, it clears the way for a purely predictive-mathematical science of nature. The identification of matter with extension also leads Descartes to deny the possibility of a vacuum — since extension is the essence of body, wherever there is extension there must be body.

The mechanistic program also shapes Descartes' biology. He treats the human body as a machine in which "animal spirits" — fine material particles — flow through the nerves like hydraulic fluid, producing all involuntary motions (008-008). This mechanical physiology, while crude by modern standards, was a pioneering attempt to explain biological phenomena without vital forces.

## Historical Context

Descartes' mechanics synthesizes several intellectual currents of the early seventeenth century: Galileo's mathematical physics, the atomism revived by Gassendi, and the mechanism of clockwork automata. It also reacts against Renaissance naturalism and the hermetic tradition.

The *Principles of Philosophy* (1644) was Descartes' attempt to present a complete alternative to the Aristotelian physics still dominant in the universities. Despite the Condemnation of Galileo in 1633 — which led Descartes to suppress his own treatise *The World* — the mechanical philosophy gained wide acceptance. By the end of the seventeenth century, through the work of Huygens, Boyle, and Newton, mechanism had become the default framework for natural philosophy.

## Criticisms and Responses

The most significant challenge to Cartesian mechanics came from Newton, whose law of universal gravitation seemed to require action at a distance — precisely the kind of occult quality Descartes had banished. Newton himself was uncomfortable with this implication and famously declared "I frame no hypotheses."

Cartesian mechanics also struggled to explain cohesion, elasticity, and magnetism with only matter-in-motion resources. Leibniz developed an alternative mechanical philosophy based on force rather than extension, which proved more fruitful for eighteenth-century dynamics.
