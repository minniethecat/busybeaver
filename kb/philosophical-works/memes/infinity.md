# Infinity

In Descartes' philosophy, **infinity** is a perfection belonging properly to God alone. The human mind possesses an innate idea of the infinite, and this idea serves as the cornerstone of Descartes' first causal proof of God's existence in the Third Meditation. Descartes holds that the idea of the infinite is *positive* — not merely the negation of the finite — and that the finite mind could not have generated it from its own resources.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces the idea of the infinite in the Third Meditation as part of his cosmological argument for God's existence. He reasons that he has within himself an idea of an infinite, eternal, immutable, omniscient, omnipotent substance — God — and asks how such an idea could have arisen in him, a finite being. The finite cannot produce the idea of the infinite from itself, for "there must at least be as much reality in the efficient and total cause as in its effect" (153-028). Since the objective reality of the idea of an infinite substance exceeds what could be produced by a finite substance, God must exist as the cause of this idea.

Descartes insists that our idea of the infinite is not merely the negation of the finite, but a positive conception: "I clearly understand that there is more reality in the infinite substance than in the finite, and therefore that in some way I have in me the perception of the infinite before that of the finite — that is, the perception of God before that of myself" (077-002). This is a striking claim: the idea of the infinite is prior to the idea of the finite, meaning my very awareness of my own finitude presupposes an idea of infinity against which I measure my limitations.

The presence of infinite perfections in the idea of God — "infinite, eternal, immutable, omniscient, omnipotent" (007-004) — is what distinguishes the idea of God from all other ideas. In the *Discourse*, Descartes describes how "reflecting on the fact that I doubted, and that consequently my being was not quite perfect (for I clearly saw that it was a greater perfection to know than to doubt), I was led to inquire whence I had learned to think of something more perfect than myself" (007-004). The answer is that the idea of a more perfect being — God — could only come from a being that actually possesses those perfections.

Knowledge of God's infinity has a methodical function. In the Preface to the French edition of the *Principles*, Descartes writes that "those who... accustom themselves to contemplate the idea of God, and to turn their thoughts to his infinite perfections... will be induced to acknowledge that he is above all things, and will conceive for him a much greater veneration" (077-002). Reflection on divine infinity thus adds to the knowledge of both God and self: "for by the light of nature, the contemplation of all these perfections, of which we recognize in ourselves only some germs, cannot but elevate us to the knowledge of a much more perfect being" (078-002).

## Arguments and Implications

The idea of infinity grounds Descartes' first proof of God's existence. The argument turns on the causal adequacy principle: the cause of an idea must contain at least as much formal reality as the idea has objective reality. Since the idea of an infinite substance has more objective reality than any finite idea, only an actually infinite being could cause it (080-002). This argument underwrites the entire edifice of Cartesian metaphysics — without God as guarantor, clear and distinct ideas could not be trusted, and the project of the *Meditations* would fail.

For Descartes, human finitude is not simply a limitation but is understood through the idea of infinity: "passing from the knowledge of God to the knowledge of the creatures, we should bear in mind the infinity of the power and goodness of God, that we may not be afraid of falling into error by imagining that his works are too vast, or too beautiful, or too perfect" (040-002). The infinite power of God also grounds the doctrine of the creation of the eternal truths: God could have made the truths of mathematics otherwise, because his power is literally infinite and unbounded by any standard external to his will.

> By the name God I understand a substance that is infinite, eternal, immutable, independent, omniscient, omnipotent, and by which I myself, and every other thing that exists (if any such there be) were created. Now these properties are so great and excellent that the more attentively I consider them, the less I am persuaded that the idea I have of them can owe its origin to myself alone.
>
> — Meditations, III (080-002)

## Historical Context

Descartes' treatment of infinity marks a departure from the scholastic tradition. For Aristotle, the infinite (*apeiron*) was potential, never actual, and associated with imperfection — matter without form. Aquinas followed Aristotle in holding that only God is actually infinite, but he treated infinity primarily as a negative attribute: God is infinite because he is not limited by any genus or form, not because "infinity" names a positive perfection. Descartes radicalizes this by treating the idea of the infinite as a *positive* idea, one we possess innately and which is epistemically *prior* to the idea of the finite. This reversal — the infinite before the finite — would become a major theme in later philosophy, particularly in **Levinas**'s ethics of the Other.

## Criticisms and Responses

The most famous objection to Descartes' use of infinity is the "Cartesian Circle" criticism, raised by **Arnauld** in the Fourth Objections. If clear and distinct ideas require God's guarantee, but God's existence is proved using clear and distinct ideas (including the idea of the infinite), the reasoning is circular. A related objection concerns whether the idea of the infinite is genuinely positive: empiricist critics like **Hume** argued that our idea of infinity is merely the negation or indefinite extension of finite experience — we never actually perceive the infinite. Descartes would reply that the very ability to recognize our finitude proves we possess a positive standard of the infinite against which we measure ourselves.
