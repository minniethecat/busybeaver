# Demonstration

In Descartes' philosophy, **demonstration** (*demonstratio*) is the method of deriving certain knowledge by deducing conclusions from self-evident first principles through a continuous chain of clear and distinct inferences. Descartes models his conception of demonstration on **geometry**: just as geometers proceed from axioms and definitions to theorems by step-by-step reasoning, so philosophy should proceed from indubitable first principles (the *cogito*, the existence of God) to conclusions about the nature of mind, body, and the physical world. "Those long chains of reasoning, quite simple and easy, which geometers use to reach their most difficult demonstrations, gave me reason to imagine that all things which can be known by man are mutually connected in the same way" (005-011).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' method is fundamentally demonstrative. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he describes the "long chains of simple and easy reasonings by means of which geometers are accustomed to reach the conclusions of their most difficult demonstrations" (005-013), and resolves to apply this method to all domains of knowledge. The unity of knowledge — the doctrine that all truths form a single interconnected system — is what makes universal demonstration possible: "all things, to the knowledge of which man is competent, are mutually connected in the same way" (005-011).

The *Meditations on First Philosophy* is designed as a demonstrative work. Descartes presents it as an exercise in which each conclusion follows with necessity from what has been established before. The *Principles of Philosophy* extends this demonstrative structure to the whole of natural philosophy.

In the *Reply to the Second Objections*, Descartes provides a **geometrical exposition** of the *Meditations*, laying out definitions, postulates, and axioms (common notions) followed by demonstrated propositions — explicitly modeling philosophical reasoning on Euclid's *Elements*.

The mind's capacity for demonstration depends on **common notions** — innate axioms that are "born with us" (068-001). "The mind also discovers certain common notions out of which it frames various demonstrations that carry conviction to such a degree as to render doubt of their truth impossible, so long as we give attention to them" (071-002).

## Arguments and Implications

**The Unity of Science**: If all truths are connected in a single demonstrative chain, then philosophy can be unified. Metaphysics provides the roots (the first principles), physics the trunk (general laws of nature), and the special sciences the branches (medicine, mechanics, ethics) (022-012). Demonstration is the method that connects all levels of this tree.

**Analysis vs. Synthesis**: Descartes distinguishes two methods of demonstration. **Analysis** (or resolution) proceeds from effects to causes — it is the "true way" by which a thing is discovered and is the method of the *Meditations*. **Synthesis** (or composition) proceeds from causes to effects — it is the method of geometry, beginning with definitions and axioms and deducing theorems. Descartes prefers analysis for metaphysical inquiry because it follows the natural order of discovery, but he provides a synthetic exposition in the *Second Replies* to satisfy readers accustomed to geometrical rigor.

**The Role of God**: Demonstrative certainty ultimately requires God. Even the clearest demonstration is subject to doubt under the deceiver hypothesis: we might be so constituted as to err in what seems most evident (030-002). Only after proving God's existence and veracity can we trust that "the light of nature, or faculty of knowledge given us by God, can never compass any object which is not true" (088-002). God guarantees the reliability of demonstrative reasoning beyond the moment of attention.

## Historical Context

The ideal of demonstrative science descends from **Aristotle**'s *Posterior Analytics*, which defined scientific knowledge (*epistēmē*) as knowledge through demonstration from first principles. Aristotle held that different sciences have different first principles and that the highest science (metaphysics) provides the principles for the others.

Descartes radicalizes this ideal in two ways. First, he makes **mathematical** rather than syllogistic reasoning the model for all demonstration. Syllogisms, he argues, only make explicit what is already contained in the premises — they do not generate new knowledge. The Cartesian method, by contrast, is one of **discovery**. Second, Descartes insists that all sciences share a single set of first principles grounded in metaphysics. The tree of knowledge metaphor (022-012) expresses this ambition: a single demonstrative system extending from the *cogito* to the cure of disease.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The Problem of First Principles**: How do we know the first principles themselves? If all knowledge is demonstrative, the first principles cannot be demonstrated without circularity. Descartes' answer is that first principles are known by **intuition** — the clear and distinct perception of the mind — not by demonstration. Intuition, not deduction, is the ultimate ground of certainty.

**The Limits of Deduction**: Pascal, a younger contemporary, argued that geometrical method cannot capture all reasoning — there are truths of "the heart" and of "subtlety" (*esprit de finesse*) that escape demonstration. Descartes would agree that demonstration is not the only form of knowledge but would insist that whatever can be known with certainty must be demonstrable from clear and distinct principles.
