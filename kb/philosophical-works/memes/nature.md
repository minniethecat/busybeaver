# Nature

In Descartes' philosophy, **nature** (*natura*) is a multi-layered concept. Most fundamentally, it denotes the essence or principal attribute of a substance — what makes a thing what it is. Thus "the nature of mind" is thought, and "the nature of body" is extension. But "nature" also refers to the created corporeal world as a whole, to the innate cognitive faculty ("the light of nature"), and to the constitution of the human being as a mind-body composite. These meanings are systematically connected throughout Descartes' works.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

The primary use of "nature" in Descartes is ontological: a thing's nature is its essence, its principal attribute. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes states this with clarity: "extension in length, breadth, and depth, constitutes the nature of corporeal substance; and thought the nature of thinking substance" (111-002). These natures are known through clear and distinct perception: "Thought and extension may be regarded as constituting the natures of intelligent and corporeal substance" (121-002). Each nature grounds all the properties of its substance — "every other thing that can be attributed to body, presupposes extension, and is only some mode of an extended thing; as all the properties we discover in the mind are only diverse modes of thinking" (111-002).

Descartes devotes considerable argumentation to establishing the true nature of body. Against the scholastic view that bodily nature includes sensible qualities like weight, hardness, and colour, Descartes argues that these are inessential. "We will discern that the nature of matter or body, considered in general, does not consist in its being hard, or ponderous, or coloured, or that which affects our senses in any other way, but simply in its being a substance extended in length, breadth, and depth" (139-002). He proves this by showing that hardness can be removed from the concept of body without the body being destroyed — if bodies receded at our touch, "we should never feel hardness; and yet we have no reason to believe that bodies which might thus recede would on this account lose that which makes them bodies" (139-002). The same argument applies to weight, colour, and all sensible qualities.

Between these two created natures — mind and body — Descartes establishes not merely a conceptual distinction but an ontological one. They are "the nature of mind" and "the nature of body" (153-018), and "all those objects which are clearly and distinctly conceived to be diverse substances, as mind and body, are substances really reciprocally distinct" (013-002). The nature of mind includes not only pure intellection but also the fact that "we exist in so far as it is our nature to think" (133-002).

The "light of nature" is a distinct usage, denoting the innate cognitive faculty by which the mind grasps first principles. In the *Principles*, Descartes declares that "the light of nature, or faculty of knowledge given us by God, can never compass any object which is not true, in as far as it attains to a knowledge of it, that is, in as far as the object is clearly and distinctly apprehended" (088-002). The light of nature is the guarantee of our capacity for truth — "God would have merited the appellation of a deceiver if he had given us this faculty perverted" (088-002). Through the light of nature we see that "nothing cannot be the cause of anything" and other eternal truths (133-002).

The nature of God occupies a special place in Descartes' system. "Although we may not comprehend the nature of God, there is yet nothing which we know so clearly as his perfections" (077-001). God's nature is infinite perfection: necessary existence "is contained in the nature or in the concept of God" (153-037). And unlike created natures, God's nature is the cause of his own existence — "the very immensity of his nature is the cause or reason why there is no need of any cause of his existence" (153-023).

Finally, Descartes speaks of human nature in the compound sense, as the union of mind and body. The opening paragraph of the *Discourse* declares that reason "is by nature equal in all men" (004-001), and the Meditations explore "the intellectual nature" as distinct from "what is to be referred to the body" (013-002). The epistemological project itself relies on knowing "whether perhaps our nature was such that we might be deceived even in those things that appear to us the most evident" (088-002) — a doubt resolved by the proof of God's veracity.

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of nature marks a radical departure from the Aristotelian-scholastic understanding. For Aristotle, a thing's nature (*physis*) was an internal principle of motion and rest, and each kind of thing had its own substantial form, which constituted its nature. The natural world was populated with qualitatively distinct natures — the nature of fire was to rise, the nature of stones was to fall — each defined by its form, its end (*telos*), and its active powers.

Descartes eliminates substantial forms and final causes. The nature of body is a single uniform essence — extension — and all physical phenomena are explained mechanically by the motions and collisions of extended particles. The nature of mind is likewise a single uniform essence — thought. This simplification makes possible the mathematical physics that **Newton** would later systematize, but it also creates the central problem of Cartesian metaphysics: how two substances with wholly different natures (extension and thought) can interact in the human being.

The concept of the "light of nature" has roots in Augustinian illumination theory, but Descartes naturalizes it: it is not a participation in divine light but a created cognitive faculty that operates reliably because God is not a deceiver. This move is foundational for Enlightenment epistemology, establishing the autonomy of human reason.

A further dimension of Descartes' concept of nature is its role in his natural philosophy. Having identified the nature of body with extension, Descartes deduces the laws of nature — the laws of motion — from God's immutability. "God preserves the whole of matter in the same way and by the same process as he originally created it," and from this divine constancy, Descartes derives his three laws of nature (conservation of motion, rectilinear tendency, and transfer of motion in collision) (139-002). These laws constitute the foundation of Cartesian physics: all corporeal phenomena, from planetary motion to the circulation of the blood, are to be explained mechanically through the configurations and motions of extended matter governed by these laws. This unification — one nature, one set of laws — is the hallmark of the modern scientific worldview that Descartes helped to found.
