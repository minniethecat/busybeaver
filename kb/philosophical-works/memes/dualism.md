# Dualism

**Dualism**, in Descartes' philosophy, is the doctrine that [[mind]] and [[body]] are two really distinct substances: mind is a thinking, non-extended substance (*res cogitans*), and body is an extended, non-thinking substance (*res extensa*). "Two substances are said to be really distinct, when each of them may exist without the other" (153-013). Descartes argues that mind and body satisfy this definition: we can clearly and distinctly conceive of mind without body and body without mind, and God can bring about whatever we clearly and distinctly conceive. Dualism is thus not merely an epistemological distinction but an ontological one — mind and body are different kinds of thing.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' dualism is systematically developed across his works. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]*, the *cogito* reveals the thinking self as independent of body: "I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing" (007-002).

In the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]*, the real distinction argument receives its fullest treatment. The Sixth Meditation argues: (1) I have a clear and distinct idea of myself as a thinking, unextended thing; (2) I have a clear and distinct idea of body as an extended, unthinking thing; (3) God can make whatever I clearly and distinctly perceive to be the case; (4) therefore, mind and body are really distinct substances.

The *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]* Appendix codifies the definitions: "The substance in which thought immediately resides is called *Mind*" (153-009); "The substance which is the immediate subject of local extension... is called *Body*" (153-010). The real distinction follows: "The mind and body are really distinct" (153-066); "the mind can exist without the body, and the body without the mind" (153-069).

## Arguments and Implications

**The Argument from Clear and Distinct Conception**: I can clearly and distinctly conceive of myself as a thinking, unextended thing. I can clearly and distinctly conceive of body as an extended, unthinking thing. Whatever I clearly and distinctly conceive, God can bring about. Therefore, mind and body can exist separately — they are really distinct substances.

**The Argument from Divisibility**: Body is by nature divisible (any extended thing can be divided in thought or in reality). Mind is by nature indivisible (when I think, I am aware of myself as a single, unified subject). Therefore, mind and body have different natures and must be distinct substances.

**Implications for Human Nature**: The human being is a union of mind and body — not a single substance but a composite. The mind is not in the body as a pilot in a ship but is "intermingled" with it, forming a substantial union. This creates the central problem of Cartesian philosophy: how do two really distinct substances interact? Descartes located the point of interaction at the pineal gland, but the problem of mind-body interaction became the most persistent objection to Cartesian dualism.

**Implications for Immortality**: Dualism provides a philosophical ground for the [[immortality]] of the soul. If mind can exist without body, the death of the body does not entail the death of the mind.

## Historical Context

Descartes' dualism radicalizes a distinction present in [[plato|Plato]] (the soul as distinct from the body) and [[augustine|Augustine]] (the inner man vs. the outer man). But Descartes' version is novel in two respects: (1) he identifies body with pure extension, stripping it of all psychic qualities; (2) he makes the real distinction the cornerstone of a systematic metaphysics that grounds both physics (the study of body) and epistemology (the study of mind).

The scholastics (following **[[aristotle|Aristotle]]**) held that the soul is the form of the body — not a separate substance but the organizing principle of a living being. Descartes' dualism breaks decisively with this hylomorphic tradition.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Princess [[princess-elisabeth|Elisabeth of Bohemia]]** posed the most famous objection: if mind is unextended and body is extended, how can they interact? An unextended thing cannot make contact with an extended thing at any point in space. Descartes' appeal to the pineal gland and the "primitive notion" of union did not satisfy her — or subsequent philosophers.

**[[spinoza|Spinoza]]** rejected dualism by making thought and extension two attributes of a single substance (God or Nature). Mind and body are not two substances but two ways of conceiving the same reality.

**[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** proposed pre-established harmony: mind and body do not interact but run in parallel, synchronized by God at creation.

**Contemporary physicalism** denies that mental states require a non-physical substance. The "hard problem of consciousness" is arguably Descartes' problem in modern form.
