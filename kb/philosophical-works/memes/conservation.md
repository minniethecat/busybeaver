# Conservation (Self-Conservation)

In Cartesian metaphysics, **conservation** (or continuous creation) is the doctrine that finite substances do not possess the power to sustain their own existence from moment to moment. Descartes argues that the same infinite power required for creation *ex nihilo* is equally required for conservation — and since only [[god]] possesses infinite power, all finite things depend on God's continuous creative act for their continued existence.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes develops the conservation doctrine most directly in the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* and its geometrical restatement. In the Third Meditation, he argues that "the conservation of a substance at each moment of its duration requires the same power and action as would be necessary to produce and create it anew" (153-031). The reasoning is that time is composed of independent moments — the fact that I existed a moment ago does not logically entail that I exist now. Something must cause my continued existence at each instant.

This leads Descartes to a crucial conclusion about human finitude: "I have not the power of self-conservation" (153-051). If I had the power to sustain my own existence, I would also have the power to give myself all the perfections I lack — since conservation and creation require the same infinite power. That I lack many perfections proves I lack the power of self-conservation.

The conservation doctrine plays a central role in Descartes' second proof of God's existence. Since I exist as a thinking thing but lack the power of self-conservation, my continued existence must be caused by something else. Tracing this causal chain leads ultimately to a being that possesses the power of self-conservation — that is, God.

Descartes also distinguishes between the conservation of the soul and of the body: "the conservation of the body depends on the union of its parts and on the disposition of the organs" (047-002), while the soul's conservation depends directly on God's continuous creative act.

## Key Passages

> The conservation of a substance at each moment of its duration requires the same power and action as would be necessary to produce and create it anew, supposing it did not yet exist.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Geometrical Appendix: Axioms (153-031)

> From the fact that I have recently existed, it does not follow that I must exist now, unless some cause, as it were, creates me again at this moment, that is to say, conserves me.
>
> — Meditations on First Philosophy, III (153-053)

> I have not the power of self-conservation, as I have recently proved. Hence I am conserved by another.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Geometrical Appendix (153-051)

## Arguments and Implications

**The Argument from Temporal Atomism**: Descartes conceives of time as composed of discrete, independent moments. My existence at time *t1* does not cause my existence at *t2* — nothing within me bridges the temporal gap. An external conserving cause must operate at every instant. This view contrasts sharply with Aristotle's concept of an internal principle of change and persistence.

**Implications for the Proof of God**: The conservation doctrine provides the crucial premise in Descartes' causal proof. If I cannot conserve myself, and my parents or other finite causes cannot conserve me (since conservation requires infinite power), then only an infinitely powerful being — God — can be the cause of my continued existence.

**Implications for Occasionalism**: Descartes' conservation doctrine influenced later Cartesians, particularly [[malebranche|Malebranche]], who extended it into occasionalism — the view that God is the sole true cause of all events, with finite things serving merely as "occasions" for divine action.

## Historical Context

The conservation doctrine has roots in medieval scholasticism. **[[aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]]** argued that all created beings depend on God not only for their coming-into-being but for their continued existence, since essence and existence are distinct in creatures but identical in God. Descartes radicalized this view by making it a premise in a rational proof of God's existence, detaching it from its theological context.

The doctrine also engages with ancient debates: the pre-Socratic question of why anything persists rather than perishing receives a distinctively Cartesian answer — persistence is continuous re-creation.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The Temporal Atomism Objection**: Critics argue that Descartes' view of time as composed of independent instants is philosophically question-begging. If time is continuous rather than atomic, the argument for the necessity of a conserving cause loses its force. **[[spinoza|Spinoza]]** would later reject temporal atomism entirely, arguing that substance exists necessarily and eternally.

**[[hume|Hume]]'s Causal Challenge**: Hume would question the causal principle itself — why must every moment of existence have a cause? The intuition that "nothing comes from nothing" may not be rationally demonstrable.

**The Problem of Interaction**: If God continuously creates both mind and body, what role remains for created causes? Descartes himself resisted occasionalist conclusions, maintaining that bodies genuinely interact through contact mechanics, but later critics found this position unstable.
