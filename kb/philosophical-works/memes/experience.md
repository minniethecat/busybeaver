# Experience

In Descartes' methodology, **experience** (*experientia*) serves reason as a source of data but cannot by itself yield certain knowledge. Descartes values observation and experiment — as his anatomical dissections and optical investigations attest — while insisting that experience must be guided and judged by reason.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes describes in the *Discourse on the Method* how he "spent the remainder of my youth in traveling, in visiting courts and armies, in holding intercourse with men of different dispositions and ranks, in collecting varied experience" (004-005). This was not idle wandering but a deliberate project: "I should find much more truth in the reasonings of each individual with reference to the affairs in which he is personally interested... than in those conducted by a man of letters in his study" (004-005).

Yet experience alone is insufficient. In the *Meditations*, Descartes demonstrates that the senses can deceive and that empirical knowledge requires a rational foundation. The wax argument shows that what we know of bodies — their extension, flexibility, mutability — is grasped by the intellect, not the senses (013-011).

In Part VI of the *Discourse*, Descartes outlines how experience and reason work together in scientific inquiry: reason deduces effects from first principles, and experience confirms (or disconfirms) these deductions. This is not pure empiricism but a rationalist experimentalism.

## Experience and the Senses

Descartes treats sensory experience as a starting point for knowledge but not its terminus. He acknowledges that "our senses sometimes deceive us" (007-001), which motivates the methodological doubt of the First Meditation. However, he does not dismiss sensory experience entirely. Once God's veracity is established, Descartes holds that "the truth of the thoughts we experience when awake, ought not in the slightest degree to be called in question on account of the illusions of our dreams" (007-008).

Experience thus plays a crucial validating role after the metaphysical foundations are secured. Descartes's rationalism is not a rejection of experience but a doctrine about its proper ordering: metaphysical certainty must precede empirical investigation.

## The Role of Experiments in Natural Philosophy

In Part VI of the *Discourse*, Descartes develops a sophisticated account of the relationship between experiment and theory. He observes that "experiments become always more necessary the more one is advanced in knowledge" (009-003). At the outset of inquiry, one may rely on ordinary sensory experience; but as science progresses, increasingly specialized and controlled experiments are required.

Descartes is candid about the practical challenges of experimental science. He acknowledges that "neither my hands nor my income, though it were a thousand times larger than it is, would be sufficient" to perform all the necessary experiments (009-005). This leads him to appeal for collaborative assistance: he calls on "all who have the common good of man at heart" to "communicate to me the experiments they had already made, as to assist me in those that remain to be made" (009-006).

Crucially, Descartes insists that experiment and theory are mutually confirming. He explicitly denies committing "the fallacy which the logicians call a circle; for since experience renders the majority of these effects most certain, the causes from which I deduce them do not serve so much to establish their reality as to explain their existence; but on the contrary, the reality of the causes is established by the reality of the effects" (009-009).

## Experience as a Degree of Wisdom

In the *Letter of the Author* prefacing the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes systematizes the role of experience within a hierarchy of wisdom. He identifies four degrees: the first contains "only notions so clear of themselves that they can be acquired without meditation"; the second comprehends "all that the experience of the senses dictates"; the third, "that which the conversation of other men teaches us"; and the fourth, "the reading, not of all books, but especially of such as have been written by persons capable of conveying proper instruction" (022-002). Experience of the senses occupies the second degree — foundational but not sufficient for the highest wisdom, which requires rational meditation on first principles.

## Experience as a Criterion of Philosophical Truth

Descartes repeatedly appeals to experience as a practical test of his philosophical system. He writes: "it appears to me that I cannot better establish [my principles] than by the test of experience: in other words, by inviting readers to peruse the following work" (022-004). The proof of first principles lies in the coherent system of truths they generate — a system whose fruits can be verified in practice.

He also wields experience as a critical tool against rival philosophies. He observes that "experience shows that they who make pretensions to philosophy are often less wise and reasonable than others who never applied themselves to the study" (022-001), using this empirical observation to argue for the need to rebuild philosophy on new foundations.
