# Motion

In Descartes' natural philosophy, **motion** (*motus*) is defined strictly as "the transference of one part of matter, or one body, from the vicinity of those bodies that are in immediate contact with it... into the vicinity of others" (153-025). This purely mechanistic concept replaces the scholastic notions of potentiality and actuality with a physics grounded in extension and divine immutability.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes derives his laws of motion not from observation but from the divine nature. In the *Discourse on the Method* Part V, he explains "the motion of the heart and of some other difficulties pertaining to Medicine" (008-001) in purely mechanical terms — the heart as a heat-engine driving circulation, without any vegetative or sensitive soul.

The foundational principle is the **conservation of motion**: God, being immutable, conserves the same quantity of motion in the universe that he put there at creation. "God preserves the whole of matter in the same way and by the same process as he originally created it," Descartes writes, and from this divine constancy flows the entire system of physical law (153-025). This is not an empirical discovery but a metaphysical deduction: because God's action is perfectly constant, the total "quantity of motion" (*quantitas motus*) in the universe — measured as the product of a body's size and speed — remains invariant through all collisions and interactions.

From this principle Descartes derives **three laws of nature** (139-002). The first law: "each individual thing, in so far as it is simple and undivided, remains in the same state as far as it can, and never changes except through external causes" (139-002). This is the principle of inertia — a body at rest stays at rest, a body in motion stays in motion, unless something acts on it. The second law: "each part of matter, considered separately, never tends to continue moving in any curved line, but only in a straight line" (139-002). This rectilinear tendency is why stones fly off tangentially from a sling. The third law governs collisions: when a moving body strikes another, if the struck body offers less resistance, the moving body transfers motion; if more, it rebounds.

Descartes also offers a detailed theory of the heart's motion as a purely mechanical heat-engine. "The motion of the heart," he writes, depends on "the heat of the blood which rarefies and expands it" (008-002) — no need for a vegetative soul or mysterious vital force. The heart is a furnace, and circulation is hydraulics. This mechanistic physiology was one of Descartes' most influential contributions, directly inspiring **Harvey**'s work on circulation even as Descartes disagreed with Harvey on the mechanism of cardiac contraction.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' theory of motion has far-reaching implications. First, by grounding the laws of motion in God's immutability rather than in empirical generalization, Descartes makes physics a deductive science — a branch of metaphysics. The laws of nature are not contingencies but consequences of divine perfection. Second, the principle of inertia eliminates the need for angels, intelligences, or substantial forms to explain planetary motion and free fall — a single principle governs all corporeal change. Third, the conservation of the quantity of motion provides the first formulation of a conservation law in physics, anticipating the modern principle of the conservation of momentum (though Descartes' scalar "quantity of motion" differs from Newton's vector momentum).

However, Descartes' account has a significant limitation: he has no concept of force as distinct from motion. Collisions are explained solely by the transfer of motion from one body to another, without any notion of acceleration or impressed force. This accounts for several well-known empirical failures of Cartesian collision rules (which predict that a small body can never move a large one, regardless of speed). **Leibniz** would correct this by distinguishing *vis viva* (living force, roughly kinetic energy) from quantity of motion, and **Newton** would introduce the concept of impressed force in his second law. Nevertheless, Descartes' program — reduce all physical explanation to matter in motion — set the agenda for mechanistic physics for two centuries.

## Historical Context

Descartes' rejection of Aristotelian motion was revolutionary. Aristotelian physics held that terrestrial motion required a continuing mover in contact with the moved object, and that celestial motion was circular because the heavens were made of a fifth element. Descartes' law of inertia — that a body continues in its state of motion unless disturbed — made continued motion natural rather than requiring explanation, fundamentally changing the questions physics asks.

The shift from qualitative to quantitative physics owes much to Descartes' insistence that the essence of body is extension alone. If body is just *res extensa*, then its only measurable properties are size, shape, and motion — all amenable to mathematical treatment. **Galileo** had already mathematized terrestrial motion; Descartes extended the program to all of nature, including the heavens, which he brought under the same physical laws as the earth — a decisive break with the Aristotelian two-realm cosmology.
