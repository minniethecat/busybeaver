# Extension

In Descartes' philosophy, **extension** (*extensio*) is the principal attribute of corporeal substance — that by which body occupies space, and the one property without which body cannot be conceived. Descartes' identification of matter with extension is the foundational move of his mechanistic physics and the key premise of his mind-body dualism.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes argues that extension is the essence of body. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he writes that "the nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing, and that its extension is not different from that which we attribute to space, however empty" (032-002). This is a radical simplification: all the properties of physical objects — weight, hardness, colour — are merely modes of extension, reducible to the size, shape, and motion of extended parts.

The identification of extension with corporeal substance means that empty space is impossible. Descartes argues: "it is not more contradictory to conceive a mountain without a valley than such a cavity without the extension it contains, or this extension apart from an extended substance, for, as we have often said, of nothing there can be no extension" (031-002). Space is not a container; it is identical with extended substance itself. If God removed all body from a vessel, "the sides of the vessel would thus come into proximity with each other" (031-002).

This plenism extends to the entire universe. Descartes concludes that "a plurality of worlds is impossible, because we clearly conceive that the matter whose nature consists only in its being an extended substance, already wholly occupies all the imaginable spaces where these other worlds could alone be" (035-002).

## Arguments and Implications

Extension serves as Descartes' criterion for distinguishing mind from body. Mind is defined by thought, body by extension — and since we can clearly conceive each without the other, they are distinct substances. This argument, developed in the *Meditations* and formalized in the *Principles* Appendix, is the logical core of Cartesian dualism.

In physics, the reduction of body to extension eliminates the need for substantial forms, occult qualities, and all Aristotelian explanatory apparatus. All physical change is local motion of extended parts. Descartes derives his laws of nature — the conservation of motion, the rectilinear tendency of motion — from the immutability of God, but the material substrate to which these laws apply is simply extension.

The indefinite extension of the universe follows from the nature of extension itself: wherever we can imagine space, there must be extended substance, because extension and space are identical. The universe has no boundary because we can always imagine space beyond any boundary — and imagining space is imagining extension, which is imagining matter.

## Historical Context

Descartes' theory of extension transforms the Aristotelian concept of matter. For **Aristotle**, matter (*hyle*) was a principle of potentiality, requiring form to become an actual substance. Matter had no independent existence and no positive attributes of its own. Descartes inverts this: extension is a positive attribute, the very essence of corporeal substance, and it is fully actual.

The Greek atomists — **Democritus** and **Epicurus** — had already identified matter with space-filling solidity, but they combined this with the void, which Descartes rejects. **Galileo** distinguished primary qualities (figure, size, motion) from secondary qualities (colour, taste), but his primary qualities still presupposed matter as something distinct from mere extension. Descartes' identification of matter with extension is the most radical reduction of the mechanical philosophy.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Newton** rejected Descartes' identification of extension with matter. In Newtonian physics, space is absolute — it exists independently of matter, and matter is distinguished by mass and impenetrability, not mere extension. Newton's gravitational force, acting across empty space, was inconceivable in Cartesian physics.

**Leibniz** also criticized Cartesian extension: if substance is mere extension, it lacks any active principle. Leibniz argued that substance must include force or power (*vis viva*), and that extension is a phenomenon — an appearance — not the essence of substance. This critique anticipated the concept of energy.
