# Science

For Descartes, **science** (*scientia*) means certain and evident knowledge, not merely probable opinion. "All science is certain and evident cognition," he writes in the *Rules for the Direction of the Mind*. The model for all science is mathematics: "the long chains of simple and easy reasonings by means of which geometers are accustomed to reach the conclusions of their most difficult demonstrations" (005-003).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' scientific program is outlined in Part VI of the *Discourse on the Method*. He explains that his method "has satisfied me in regard to all the difficulties pertaining to the speculative sciences" and that he has "essayed to discover the true principles of material things" (009-001). His physics — covering light, heavens, planets, comets, and the earth — is derived from these principles.

The practical aim of Cartesian science distinguishes it from contemplative philosophy: "these notions made me see that it is possible to attain knowledge that is very useful in life, and that, instead of the speculative philosophy taught in the schools, we may find a practical philosophy by means of which... we might render ourselves the masters and possessors of nature" (009-003). This ambition to master nature through knowledge is one of the founding statements of the modern technological project.

Descartes' physics covers all of nature: "I have essayed to give a general explanation of the nature of the heavens and of the earth, of the planets, of comets, and generally of all the phenomena of nature" (009-001). His ambition is nothing less than a complete mechanistic account of the physical universe, covering cosmology (the vortex theory), optics (the *Dioptrics*), meteorology (the *Meteors*), and physiology (the *Treatise on Man*).

The famous **tree of philosophy** metaphor from the Preface to the *Principles* places science within the broader Cartesian system: "The whole of philosophy is like a tree, of which the roots are metaphysics, the trunk is physics, and the branches that spring from this trunk are all the other sciences, which may be reduced to three principal ones, namely medicine, mechanics, and morals" (078-002). Science (physics) is the trunk connecting metaphysical foundations to practical applications. The highest and most perfect moral science, Descartes adds, "presupposes an entire knowledge of the other sciences, and is the last degree of wisdom" (078-002).

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' vision of unified science — all knowledge connected by chains of reasoning from first principles — is one of the most influential ideas in modern intellectual history. If metaphysics provides the roots (God, the soul, the nature of substance) and physics provides the trunk (the laws of matter in motion), then every science from medicine to mechanics to ethics can be derived deductively. The certainty of geometry becomes the standard for all knowledge.

This deductive ideal has important epistemological implications. For Descartes, "science" excludes merely probable opinion — it requires certainty. History, jurisprudence, and empirical natural history, which depend on testimony and contingent fact, do not qualify as *scientia* in the strict sense. This high bar distinguishes Cartesian science from the Baconian empirical program: for **Bacon**, science advances by induction from observed particulars; for Descartes, it advances by deduction from evident principles, with experiment serving only to determine which of several possible mechanisms nature actually employs.

The practical payoff is medicine. "The preservation of health," Descartes writes, "is without doubt the first good and the foundation of all the other goods of this life" (009-004). His confidence that mechanistic physiology would lead to medical breakthroughs — extending human life, curing disease — reflects both the ambition and the hubris of the early modern scientific revolution. He reports spending time on dissections and anatomical study, convinced that "even the mind depends so much on the temperament and disposition of the bodily organs" (009-004) that advances in physiology would directly improve human well-being.

## Historical Context

Descartes' conception of science emerged at a moment of extraordinary intellectual ferment. **Galileo** had already demonstrated the power of mathematical physics; **Bacon** had called for a new empirical method; **Harvey** had discovered the circulation of the blood through careful experiment. Descartes synthesized these currents into a unified vision: a deductive science of nature, grounded in clear metaphysical principles, confirmed by experiment, and directed toward practical benefit. His *Principles of Philosophy* (1644) was the most ambitious attempt of its era to provide a complete mechanistic account of the physical universe — a work that **Newton** would both build upon and supersede.
