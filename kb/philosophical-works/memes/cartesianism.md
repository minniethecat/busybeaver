# Cartesianism

**Cartesianism** is the philosophical movement that arose from the work of [[rene-descartes|René Descartes]] and dominated European intellectual life for over a century. More than simply "Descartes' philosophy," Cartesianism was a living tradition — debated, modified, and extended by followers and critics across France, the Netherlands, England, and Germany. Its characteristic doctrines — [[dualism]], [[mechanism]], the [[cogito]], clear and distinct ideas, the ontological argument for [[god]]'s existence — became the framework within which early modern [[philosophy]] developed, even for those who ultimately rejected them.

## Core Doctrines

Cartesianism is defined by several interlocking commitments:

1. **The Cogito as Foundation**: All [[knowledge]] begins with the indubitable certainty of one's own existence as a thinking thing. This is the Archimedean point from which the entire system is derived.

2. **Mind-Body Dualism**: [[Mind]] and [[body]] are distinct substances — mind is thinking, unextended substance; body is extended, unthinking substance. This distinction grounds both the immortality of the [[soul]] and the mechanistic account of nature.

3. **Mechanism**: The physical world is a machine — matter in [[motion]] governed by mathematical laws. No substantial forms, final causes, or occult qualities are needed to explain natural phenomena.

4. **Innate Ideas**: The mind possesses innate ideas — of God, of perfection, of mathematical truths — that are not derived from sensory experience. The clarity and distinctness of these ideas guarantees their truth.

5. **Occasionalism**: Developed by [[malebranche|Malebranche]], occasionalism is the Cartesian solution to the mind-body problem: God is the only true cause, and created substances are merely "occasions" for divine action.

## Major Figures

- **Nicolas Malebranche** (1638–1715): The most important Cartesian after Descartes, Malebranche developed occasionalism and the "vision in God" — the doctrine that we see all things in God's ideas.
- **Antoine Arnauld** (1612–1694): Theologian and co-author of the Port-Royal Logic, Arnauld defended Cartesianism against theological critics while rejecting Malebranche's occasionalism.
- **Pierre-Sylvain Régis** (1632–1707): The leading French Cartesian of the late 17th century, Régis systematized Descartes' philosophy for public instruction.

## Opposition and Decline

Cartesianism faced fierce opposition from the Catholic Church (Descartes' works were placed on the Index in 1663), from scholastic Aristotelians, and from empiricists like [[locke]] and [[hume]]. By the mid-18th century, [[newton|Newtonian]] physics had largely replaced Cartesian mechanism, and [[kant|Kant's]] critical philosophy claimed to synthesize rationalism and [[empiricism]] into a new framework.
