## Definition

Eternal truths (*veritates aeternae*) in Descartes' philosophy are necessary, immutable propositions — such as the truths of mathematics and logic — that God has freely established. Unlike the medieval view that eternal truths exist independently in the divine intellect, Descartes holds that God freely created them by an act of will, just as he freely created the world.

## Created by Divine Will

Descartes' most distinctive metaphysical claim is that eternal truths depend on God's free decree. Mathematical truths like "2 + 2 = 4" are true because God willed them so, not because God's intellect recognizes an independent necessity. This voluntarist doctrine of eternal truths makes God the sole source of all truth and being.

> In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes enumerates eternal truths as one of two categories: "affections of things, or as eternal truths possessing no existence outside our thought" (106-002). They are "eternally true" propositions (133-002).

The standard list of eternal truths includes: "nothing comes from nothing," "it is impossible for the same thing to be and not to be," and the axioms of mathematics (106-001, 107-001).

## Distinction from Things

Descartes carefully distinguishes eternal truths from actually existing entities. They "possess no existence outside our thought" (106-002) — they are not Platonic forms or divine ideas with independent being. They are common notions (*notiones communes*) that our mind finds within itself and applies to things.

This is why eternal truths "cannot be enumerated" in the same way as categories of being (107-001): they are principles of judgment, not kinds of substance.

## Relation to God's Nature

In the *Meditations*, Descartes argues from the idea of God: "I myself have become infinite, eternal, immutable, omniscient, all-powerful" — these are perfections belonging to God alone, not to finite minds (007-004). Eternity as a divine attribute is distinct from the created eternal truths: God is eternal in being, while eternal truths are eternal only in their necessity.

Descartes also connects the idea of God to the eternal truths: "we perceive that he is eternal, omniscient, omnipotent, the source of all goodness and truth" (080-002). God's eternal nature grounds the immutability of the truths he freely created.

## Theological Implications

The doctrine of created eternal truths has significant consequences. If God had willed otherwise, even the law of non-contradiction could have been different. This radical claim — which Descartes' contemporaries found troubling — maximizes divine freedom and power while placing all necessity within the scope of divine will.
