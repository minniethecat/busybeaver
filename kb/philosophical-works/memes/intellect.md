# Intellect

In Descartes' philosophy, the **intellect** (*intellectus*, *entendement*) is the faculty by which the mind perceives ideas — the power of cognition that receives, inspects, and understands. It is one of two fundamental operations of the thinking substance, the other being the will. "All the operations of the will, intellect, imagination, and senses, are thoughts" (153-004), but among these the intellect is special: it is the seat of clear and distinct perception, the faculty through which truth is apprehended.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes defines the intellect as the faculty of perception — the mind's capacity to receive and contemplate ideas. Unlike the will, which is active and infinite in scope, the intellect is passive and finite: "the perception of the intellect extends only to the few things presented to it" (093-002). The intellect does not create its objects; it receives them, either from the senses, the imagination, or the mind's own innate store.

The limitation of the intellect is central to Descartes' theodicy of error. "It is of the nature of created intellect to be finite, and of finite intellect not to embrace all things" (094-002). Error does not arise from the intellect — which merely perceives what is presented — but from the will, which assents or dissents beyond what the intellect clearly perceives. If the will withheld judgment whenever the intellect's perception was obscure, error would be impossible. "God has not given to us such perspicacity of intellect that we should never have erred" (096-002); rather, he has given us the means to avoid error — the discipline of suspending judgment when perception is not clear and distinct.

Descartes distinguishes *intellection* (*intellectio*) — the act of pure understanding — from imagination and sensation. "The act of the understanding is distinguished from that of the imagination" (013-006): intellection deals with purely intellectual natures (God, the soul, mathematical truths), while imagination deals with corporeal images. This distinction grounds Descartes' argument for the real distinction between mind and body: I can clearly and distinctly understand myself as a thinking thing without including extension, therefore mind and body are distinct substances.

The intellect also plays a role in Descartes' account of the passions. In the *Passions of the Soul*, he speaks of "intellectual joy" (*gaudium intellectuale*) — "when the soul rejoices, it rejoices with that intellectual joy which arises from the contemplation of things intelligible" (047-002). The intellect is not merely a cold cognitive faculty; it is a source of the highest form of pleasure — the contemplation of truth.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' theory of intellect has three major implications for his system:

**The Ground of Error**: Because the intellect is finite and the will infinite, error occurs when the will affirms or denies what the intellect has not yet clearly perceived. "Nor have we any reason to complain that God has not given to us a more ample intellect... since created intellect is by its nature finite" (096-002). The remedy is not to expand the intellect — which is impossible — but to discipline the will.

**The Real Distinction**: The intellect can conceive itself without body. "I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). Because intellection is a mode of thought, not of extension, the thinking substance can be clearly and distinctly conceived apart from corporeal substance. This is the epistemological basis of [[dualism]].

**The Hierarchy of Ideas**: The intellect perceives ideas of three kinds: adventitious (from the senses), factitious (constructed by the mind), and innate (implanted by God). The intellect's proper object is innate ideas — the ideas of God, of the self, of mathematical truths — which are "clear and distinct" by nature and require only attention to be recognized.

## Historical Context

Descartes inherits the scholastic distinction between *intellectus* and *ratio* — intellect as the faculty of direct intuition, reason as the faculty of discursive inference. But Descartes reshapes this distinction: for him, *intellectus* is not a higher faculty reserved for the beatific vision but the ordinary power of clear and distinct perception available to every attentive mind. "Even men of the lowest grade of intellect can do" what the method requires (008-008).

This democratization of intellect is characteristically Cartesian. The *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]* opens with the remark that "good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed" (001-001). What distinguishes successful from unsuccessful thinkers is not native intellectual power but the discipline of method — the careful use of the intellect everyone possesses.

[[spinoza|Spinoza]] would later collapse the distinction between intellect and will entirely, treating them as one and the same faculty under different descriptions. For Spinoza, a clear idea carries its own affirmation — there is no separate act of will. Descartes' distinction between the two faculties is essential to his account of error and freedom.

## Criticisms and Responses

**[[spinoza|Spinoza]]** rejected the distinction between intellect and will in the *Ethics*: "The will and the intellect are one and the same." An idea, for Spinoza, is not a passive picture contemplated by a separate faculty of judgment; it is an active affirmation. There is no act of will beyond the idea itself. Descartes would reply that we experience ourselves as free to withhold assent even from a clear idea — though in practice, a perfectly clear and distinct perception compels assent.

**Empiricists** ([[locke|Locke]], [[hume|Hume]]) denied that the intellect possesses innate ideas. For Locke, the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth; all ideas derive from sensation and reflection. Descartes' innatism — the claim that the intellect contains ideas of God, substance, and mathematical truths prior to all experience — was the principal target of Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*.

**[[kant|Kant]]** transformed the Cartesian intellect from a passive receiver of ideas into an active faculty that structures experience through categories. For Kant, the intellect does not merely perceive; it legislates. Descartes' passive intellect becomes, in Kant, the "understanding" (*Verstand*) that synthesizes the manifold of intuition under concepts.
