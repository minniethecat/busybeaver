# Objective Reality

In Descartes' philosophy, **objective reality** (*realitas objectiva*) is the reality that belongs to an idea *by virtue of what it represents*. It is distinct from **formal reality** (*realitas formalis*), which is the reality a thing possesses in itself — the degree of being or perfection it actually has. A stone has a certain formal reality as an existing substance; my idea of a stone has the same degree of objective reality by representing a stone. Descartes deploys this distinction in the *Meditations* to construct the causal argument for God's existence: the objective reality of the idea of God (which is infinite) requires a cause with at least as much formal reality — which can only be God himself.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

The distinction between objective and formal reality is inherited from scholastic philosophy but radically transformed by Descartes. In the scholastic tradition, *esse objectivum* meant the being a thing has as an object of thought. Descartes gives this distinction a new, causal role in his metaphysics.

The key principle is that **there must be at least as much reality in the efficient and total cause as in its effect** (the causal adequacy principle). When applied to ideas, this means: the cause of an idea must have at least as much formal reality as the idea has objective reality. An idea representing a finite substance could in principle be caused by my own mind (which is itself a finite substance). But the idea of God — representing an infinite, independent, supremely perfect substance — has more objective reality than my finite mind has formal reality. Therefore, only God himself can be the cause of this idea.

Descartes also recognizes **eminent reality** (*realitas eminens*): when a cause contains the reality of its effect in a higher or more perfect form, rather than formally (in the same form). For example, God contains the reality of bodies not formally (God is not extended) but eminently (God's infinite perfection includes whatever perfection is found in extension).

## Key Passages

> There are diverse degrees of reality, that is, of entity [or perfection]: for substance has more reality than accident or mode, and infinite substance than finite.
>
> — Reply to the Second Objections, Axioms (153-028)

> It is a greater and more difficult thing to create or conserve a substance than to create or conserve its attributes or properties.
>
> — Reply to the Second Objections, Axioms (153-031)

> Thus, the idea of the mind, for example, possesses no less objective reality because it is the idea of a substance than the idea of body: but the mind itself — the formal reality — is a substance as really as body is one.
>
> These axioms and definitions contain the foundations of Descartes' causal proof for God's existence in Meditation III.

## Arguments and Implications

**The Causal Argument for God**: The objective reality of the idea of God is the lynchpin of Descartes' first proof of God's existence (Meditation III). I find within myself the idea of a supremely perfect, infinite being. This idea has *infinite* objective reality. By the causal adequacy principle, its cause must have at least as much formal reality. I am finite, so I cannot be the cause. Only an actually existing infinite being — God — can be the adequate cause of this idea. The idea of God in my mind is thus the "mark of the workman" — the signature God has impressed upon his creation.

**The Hierarchy of Reality**: Descartes posits a hierarchy: infinite substance (God) has the most reality, finite substances (minds, bodies) have less, and modes/accidents have the least. An idea's objective reality follows this same hierarchy — an idea of a substance has more objective reality than an idea of a mode. This hierarchy makes the causal argument possible.

**Ideas vs. Judgments**: Falsehood, strictly speaking, belongs only to judgments, not to ideas. An idea considered in itself — simply as a representation — is never false. Error arises only when I *judge* that the object represented by the idea exists outside my mind in a way corresponding to the representation. This distinction protects Descartes from the charge that he is positing a realm of "objective beings" in the mind.

## Historical Context

The terminology of "objective" and "formal" comes from **Duns Scotus** and the later scholastic tradition, where *esse objectivum* meant the mode of being something has as known — the being of the known *in* the knower. Francisco **Suárez**, the Jesuit philosopher whose *Disputationes Metaphysicae* Descartes knew well, used the distinction extensively.

Descartes' innovation is to connect this epistemological distinction to a **causal principle**: the degree of objective reality of an idea determines what kind of cause is required to produce it. This transforms a theory of intentionality into an argument for God's existence — one of the most ambitious moves in early modern philosophy.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Caterus** (in the First Objections) argued that the objective reality of an idea does not require a formal cause, because objective reality is merely a "label" or denomination — it is not a real entity needing a cause. Descartes' reply: objective reality is not merely a label; it is a genuine mode of being that requires a cause. An idea could not represent a stone unless something with the reality of a stone had produced it.

**Hobbes** and later empiricists argued that the idea of God can be constructed by the mind from finite materials — by amplifying our ideas of power, knowledge, and goodness and removing their limits. Descartes responds that the idea of the *infinite* is prior to the idea of the *finite*: I recognize my own finitude only because I already possess the idea of the infinite against which to measure it.
