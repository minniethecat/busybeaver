## Definition and Classification

In Cartesian philosophy, an **innate idea** (*idea innata*) is an idea present in the mind from birth, implanted by God, and not acquired through sensory experience or constructed by the mind. In the Third Meditation, Descartes classifies all ideas into three categories: *innate*, *adventitious* (coming from external objects via the senses), and *factitious* (constructed by the mind itself, such as mythical creatures).

> But among these ideas, some appear to me to be innate, others adventitious, and others to be made by myself (factitious).
>
> (Meditations III)

The distinction is fundamental to Descartes' rationalism: knowledge derived from innate ideas is more certain than knowledge derived from the senses, because innate ideas are placed in us by a perfect and non-deceiving God.

## Innate Ideas as the Foundation of Knowledge

For Descartes, certain ideas could not possibly come from the senses because they represent perfections or infinities that sensory experience cannot provide. The most important innate idea is the **idea of God** — the concept of an infinite, perfect being. Descartes argues that since he himself is finite and imperfect, he could not have generated the idea of an infinite and perfect being; therefore, God must exist as the cause of this idea.

> There remains only the inquiry as to the way in which I received this idea from God; for I have not drawn it from the senses... there but remains the alternative that it is innate, in the same way as is the idea of myself.
>
> (Meditations III)

Other innate ideas include the idea of the self (the *cogito*), mathematical and geometrical truths, and the fundamental laws of logic and causality. In the Appendix to the Second Objections (the "Geometrical Exposition"), Descartes provides formal definitions of "idea" and "thought" (153-005)(153-006), and argues that the objective reality of our ideas requires a cause with at least as much formal reality (153-027).

## Historical Significance

The doctrine of innate ideas became one of the central points of contention between 17th-century [[rationalism]] and [[empiricism]]. [[John Locke|Locke]], in his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, devoted Book I to a systematic refutation of innate ideas, arguing the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth. [[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Leibniz]] defended innate ideas in his *New Essays on Human Understanding*, countering Locke's arguments and refining the doctrine: innate ideas are not fully formed at birth but exist as "dispositions" or "tendencies" of the mind, like veins in a block of marble that guide the sculptor's chisel.

[[Baruch Spinoza|Spinoza]] radicalized the doctrine, collapsing Descartes' threefold classification into a monistic framework where all adequate ideas are, in a sense, innate to the one substance.

## In Descartes' System

Innate ideas serve a dual role in Descartes' philosophy:

1. **Epistemological role**: They provide the foundation for certain knowledge (*scientia*). Since God is no deceiver (089-002), whatever we clearly and distinctly perceive through our innate cognitive faculties must be true (013-004).

2. **Theological role**: The innate idea of God is the bridge from the *cogito* to the external world. Without it, Descartes cannot escape the solipsism of the Second Meditation or the specter of the [[evil-demon]].

## 相关Influences

The concept has ancient roots. [[Plato]]'s theory of anamnesis (recollection) in the *Meno* and *Phaedo* argues that all learning is the recollection of knowledge the soul possessed before birth. [[Augustine]]'s doctrine of divine illumination — that God illuminates the mind, enabling it to perceive eternal truths — is a major influence on Descartes' version.
