# Cogito

The **_cogito_** — from the Latin *cogito ergo sum*, "I think, therefore I am" — is Descartes' first indubitable principle. After subjecting all his beliefs to methodical [[doubt]], Descartes discovers that the very act of doubting proves his own existence: "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The *cogito* is the Archimedean point of Cartesian philosophy — the one certainty immune to even the most radical doubt, from which all other [[knowledge]] must be reconstructed.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

The *cogito* appears in two major formulations. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]* (Part IV), Descartes presents it as the outcome of his methodical doubt: after rejecting "as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt," he finds that "this truth, *I think, therefore I am*, was so certain and so assured that all the most extravagant suppositions brought forward by the sceptics were incapable of shaking it" (004-003).

In the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]*, the argument receives fuller treatment. Even if an evil demon is deceiving him about everything, Descartes must exist in order to be deceived. "Let him deceive me as much as he will, he can never cause me to be nothing so long as I think that I am something." The *cogito* is not a syllogism but a direct intuition — the act of thinking immediately reveals the thinker's existence.

Descartes elaborates: "I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing" (007-002). The *cogito* thus establishes not only that I exist, but *what* I am: a thinking thing (*res cogitans*).

The *cogito* grounds Descartes' criterion of truth: whatever is perceived clearly and distinctly is true. For the *cogito* itself is perceived clearly and distinctly, and there is no mark by which the true could be distinguished from the false if this perception were deceptive.

## Arguments and Implications

**The Argument from Doubt**: I am doubting. If I am doubting, I am thinking. If I am thinking, I exist. Therefore, I exist. This argument cannot be refuted by any sceptical hypothesis because the premise (I am doubting) is self-verifying — to doubt that I am doubting is to engage in the very activity being doubted.

**Implications for the Mind-Body Distinction**: The *cogito* reveals that I am essentially a thinking thing. My existence as a thinker does not depend on having a body. This is the foundation of Cartesian [[dualism]]: "We clearly conceive mind, that is, a substance which thinks, without body" (153-068).

**Implications for the Criterion of Truth**: The *cogito* is the paradigm case of clear and distinct [[perception]]. Whatever I perceive with equal clarity and distinctness must also be true — this becomes Descartes' rule for acquiring certain knowledge, though it requires a divine guarantee (see [[god]]) to extend beyond immediate intuition.

## Historical Context

Descartes was not the first to formulate a version of the *cogito*. **[[augustine|Augustine]]**, in *The City of God* (Book XI, Chapter 26), wrote: "If I am mistaken, I am" (*Si fallor, sum*). Descartes was likely aware of this precedent. However, Descartes' use of the *cogito* differs fundamentally: where Augustine used it as a refutation of Academic scepticism within a theological framework, Descartes deploys it as the foundational principle of a wholly new rationalist epistemology, independent of revelation.

**[[hobbes|Hobbes]]**, in the Third Objections to the *Meditations*, objected that "I am thinking" does not entail "I am a thinking thing" — a walking person is not essentially a walker. Descartes replied that thinking is an attribute, not an action — it reveals the nature of the substance.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The "Cartesian Circle"**: Critics charge that Descartes uses clear and distinct perception to prove God's existence, then uses God to guarantee clear and distinct perception. Descartes' defenders distinguish between immediate clear and distinct perceptions (like the *cogito*, which are self-verifying) and remembered perceptions (which require the divine guarantee).

**[[kant|Kant]]** critiqued the *cogito* as an analytic judgment that smuggles in a substantialist metaphysics. "I think" accompanies all my representations, but this does not prove that I am a substance.

**[[hume|Hume]]** argued that introspection never reveals a "self" — only a bundle of perceptions. The *cogito* assumes precisely what Hume denies: that there is a unified thinking subject behind the thoughts.

**Lichtenberg's objection**: "It thinks" rather than "I think" — the *cogito* posits an "I" that may be an unwarranted inference from the occurrence of thinking.
