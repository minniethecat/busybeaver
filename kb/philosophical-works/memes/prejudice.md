The concept of **prejudice** (from Latin *praejudicium*, "prior judgment") occupies a central role in Descartes' epistemology as the principal impediment to clear and distinct knowledge. For Descartes, prejudices are opinions formed in childhood and reinforced by sensory experience, which must be systematically dismantled through the method of doubt before any certain foundation for knowledge can be established.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes identifies prejudice as the root cause of philosophical error throughout his corpus. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he presents the first rule of his method as the resolve "to avoid precipitancy and prejudice" (*éviter la précipitation et la prévention*), placing the elimination of prejudice at the foundation of his entire philosophical program (005-007). The *Meditations* opens with the recognition that the meditator must "deliver himself from all prejudice" acquired since infancy, for these ingrained opinions prevent the intellect from apprehending truth with clarity (011-010). In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes devotes an entire article to the proposition that "prejudices hinder many from clearly knowing the truth," arguing that the habit of trusting the senses from childhood forms a barrier to rational understanding (074-001). The methodical doubt of the First Meditation is therefore, at its core, a systematic procedure for freeing the mind from prejudice, enabling the exercise of "unprejudiced natural reason" (009-011).

## Key Passages

> The first [rule] was never to accept anything as true that I did not plainly know to be such — that is to say, carefully to avoid precipitancy and prejudice, and to comprise nothing more in my judgments than what presented itself so clearly and distinctly to my mind that I had no occasion to doubt it.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part II (005-007)

> For in order to be able to apprehend the truth, we must first of all be wholly free from prejudices; but as we have been accustomed from our earliest years to judge of things by means of the senses, it is scarcely possible for us to arrive at the truth by any other path than that which delivers us from all prejudice.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part I, LXXIV (074-001–074-002)

## Arguments and Implications

The Cartesian attack on prejudice has far-reaching consequences for his system. First, it underpins the methodical doubt: because the senses have implanted prejudices from childhood, everything acquired through sensory experience is epistemically suspect (022-011). Second, it supports Descartes' rejection of scholastic philosophy, which he regards as perpetuating rather than correcting inherited prejudices. Third, the concept of prejudice is closely tied to his theory of error in the Fourth Meditation, where false judgments arise when the will assents to ideas that the intellect does not perceive clearly — a pattern Descartes traces to the "prejudices of our early years" that must be corrected through disciplined attention (105-001). The elimination of prejudice thus serves both a negative function (clearing the ground) and a positive one (enabling the natural light of reason to operate without obstruction).

## Historical Context

The concept of *praejudicium* had a long history in scholastic philosophy before Descartes, where it typically referred to a preliminary judgment that could be revised upon fuller inquiry. Descartes radicalizes this notion: for him, prejudices are not tentative starting points but systematic distortions requiring wholesale elimination. This move reflects the broader early modern break with Aristotelianism, in which thinkers like Francis Bacon (with his "Idols of the Mind") similarly diagnosed inherited opinion as an obstacle to scientific knowledge. Descartes' innovation lies in transforming the critique of prejudice from a rhetorical gesture into a rigorous method — the methodical doubt — that proceduralizes the process of intellectual liberation.

## Criticisms and Responses

Critics have charged that Descartes' project of eliminating all prejudice is self-defeating, since the very act of doubting presupposes linguistic and conceptual frameworks acquired through social experience. Gassendi, in the Fifth Objections, questioned whether it is genuinely possible for an embodied mind to free itself entirely from the prejudices of sensory life. Descartes' response in the *Replies* emphasizes that the methodical doubt is a *methodological* rather than *psychological* project: the aim is not to erase all prior beliefs from memory but to suspend assent to any proposition that admits of the slightest doubt, thereby creating the conditions for certain knowledge. Contemporary epistemologists have also noted that the concept of prejudice in Descartes anticipates themes in later hermeneutic philosophy, where "prejudice" (in Gadamer's sense) is rehabilitated as a condition of understanding rather than an obstacle to it.
