# Platonism

**Platonism** is the philosophical tradition that descends from [[plato|Plato]] (427–347 BCE) — the tradition that, more than any other ancient school, shaped the intellectual world into which [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] was born. Though Descartes rarely cites Plato directly (022-005), the Platonic currents in his thought are unmistakable: the distinction between the intelligible and sensible realms, the priority of [[mind]] over [[body]], the doctrine that [[knowledge]] is recollection of truths already present in the [[soul]], and the vision of [[philosophy]] as a turning away from the senses toward pure intellectual apprehension.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Theory of Forms**: Plato held that the visible world is a shadow of a higher reality — the eternal, unchanging Forms accessible only to the intellect. Descartes' distinction between the "clear and distinct" ideas of the intellect and the confused deliverances of the senses echoes this Platonic dualism of worlds.

2. **Innate Knowledge**: In the *Meno*, Plato demonstrates that an untutored slave boy "recollects" geometric truths — knowledge that was always in his [[soul]], awaiting elicitation. Descartes' doctrine of innate ideas — that the mind possesses the idea of [[god]], perfection, and mathematical truths independently of experience — is a direct descendant of this Platonic doctrine.

3. **The Soul's Ascent**: For Plato, philosophy is a *turning* (*periagoge*) of the [[soul]] from the shadows of the cave toward the light of the Forms. Descartes' *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* narrates a similar journey: the meditator turns away from the senses, undergoes systematic [[doubt]], and ascends to certain knowledge of self, God, and world.

## Platonism and Descartes

Descartes' Platonism was mediated through **Augustine** (354–430 CE), who baptized Plato for the Christian tradition. Augustine's argument that the certainty of one's own existence refutes scepticism (*si fallor, sum* — "if I am deceived, I exist") anticipates Descartes' [[cogito]]. Descartes acknowledged the parallel but insisted he arrived at it independently.

The Platonic-Augustinian tradition also shaped Descartes' conception of [[god]] — not as Aristotle's Unmoved Mover but as the source of all being, truth, and goodness, whose existence is more certain than the existence of bodies.
