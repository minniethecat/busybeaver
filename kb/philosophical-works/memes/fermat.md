# Pierre de Fermat

**Pierre de Fermat** (1607–1665) was the most formidable mathematical contemporary of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] — a jurist in the Parlement de Toulouse whose spare-time mathematical discoveries made him one of the founders of modern number theory, probability theory, and analytic geometry. Descartes and Fermat, linked through the correspondence network of [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]], clashed over the method of tangents in 1637–38: Descartes dismissed Fermat's approach as unsystematic, while Fermat exposed flaws in Descartes' own method. Their rivalry was the first great mathematical controversy of the modern era.

## Biography

Born in Beaumont-de-Lomagne, Fermat studied law at the University of Orléans and became a councillor in the Parlement de Toulouse in 1631. He lived a quiet, provincial life, publishing almost nothing during his lifetime — his mathematical work circulated through letters to [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]], [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]], and others. His famous "Last Theorem" was scribbled in the margin of his copy of Diophantus' *Arithmetica* in 1637, with the note: "I have discovered a truly remarkable proof, but this margin is too narrow to contain it."

Fermat's mathematical achievements were extraordinary: he independently invented analytic geometry (the representation of curves by algebraic equations) before Descartes published his *Géométrie*; he founded the theory of probability in correspondence with Pascal; and he made fundamental contributions to number theory, including what became Fermat's Little Theorem. He died in 1665 at Castres, his work largely unpublished.

## Relationship to Descartes

Fermat's relationship with Descartes was one of rivalry, mutual suspicion, and reluctant respect:

1. **The Tangent Dispute (1637–38)**: When Descartes' *Géométrie* appeared in 1637, Fermat — through Mersenne — sent his own method for finding tangents to curves. Descartes, who prided himself on the superiority of his algebraic geometry, attacked Fermat's method as logically unsound. The ensuing controversy consumed the Republic of Letters for months. Fermat responded by demonstrating that Descartes' own method failed for certain curves.

2. **Priority of Analytic Geometry**: Fermat's *Introduction to the Theory of Plane and Solid Loci* (written circa 1629 but unpublished until 1679) anticipated Descartes' analytic geometry by several years. When Descartes learned of this, he accused Fermat — privately — of plagiarism, though the charge was unfounded. Both had independently discovered the fundamental insight: geometric curves could be expressed as algebraic equations.

3. **Mediation of Mersenne**: [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]] played the crucial role of mediator — forwarding challenges, moderating egos, and ultimately persuading both men to acknowledge each other's contributions. Without Mersenne, the dispute might have degenerated into permanent enmity.

4. **Reconciliation**: By 1638, Descartes conceded: "I do not wish to have any dispute with M. de Fermat… those who love the truth, as I hope M. de Fermat does, are not capable of taking sides." The reconciliation was incomplete but genuine — a recognition that they were fellow seekers.

## Key Ideas

**Method of Maxima and Minima**: Fermat's method for finding the maxima and minima of curves — a precursor to the differential calculus — used a technique of "adequality" (approximate equality) that anticipated the concept of the derivative. Descartes criticized it for lacking the deductive rigor of his own geometric method, but Fermat's approach proved more computationally powerful.

**Independent Discovery of Analytic Geometry**: Fermat's algebraic representation of curves (using coordinates) was developed independently of and possibly before Descartes. While Descartes' version was more systematic and better publicized, Fermat's was arguably more elegant in its algebraic manipulations.

**Probabilistic Reasoning**: With [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]], Fermat founded the mathematical theory of probability — a contribution that, ironically, Descartes' deductive-rationalist [[method]] could not have produced, since Descartes believed probability was no guide to truth.
