# Cause

In Descartes' philosophy, **cause** (*causa*) is governed by a fundamental metaphysical principle: the cause must contain at least as much reality (or perfection) as the effect. This **causal adequacy principle** drives Descartes' argument for God's existence in the Third Meditation and underpins his commitment to mechanistic explanation in physics.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes formulates the causal adequacy principle explicitly in the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix: "It is a greater and more difficult thing to create or conserve a substance than to create or conserve its attributes or properties" (153-031). The principle is presented as a self-evident axiom: what is more perfect cannot arise from what is less perfect.

This principle applies to both **formal reality** (the reality a thing has in itself) and **objective reality** (the reality an idea has in virtue of what it represents). "There is more objective reality in the idea of substance than in that of accident, and in the idea of infinite than in the idea of finite substance" (153-028). The causal adequacy principle applied to ideas yields the core of the Third Meditation argument: the idea of God — which has infinite objective reality — cannot have a finite cause; it must come from God himself.

Descartes' causal principle also governs his physics. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he describes how he derived his laws of nature not from empirical observation but from the divine attributes: God's immutability entails the conservation of motion, and God's simplicity entails the rectilinear tendency of motion. Causality in the physical world is a reflection of divine causality.

## Arguments and Implications

The causal adequacy principle has two primary applications in Descartes' system. First, it generates the **causal argument for God's existence**: (1) I have an idea of an infinite, perfect being; (2) this idea has infinite objective reality; (3) the cause of an idea must have at least as much formal reality as the idea has objective reality; (4) only an infinite being has infinite formal reality; therefore (5) God exists as the cause of my idea of God.

Second, it grounds the rejection of **occult qualities** in physics. Cartesian physics explains all phenomena in terms of matter (extension) and motion — properties whose reality is known clearly and distinctly. Explanations that appeal to unknown causal powers ("dormitive virtue," "sympathies," "substantial forms") violate the causal principle because they attempt to explain effects by causes whose reality is less known than the effects.

Descartes also develops an important identity between **creation and conservation**. "This creation of a thing is not greater or more difficult than its conservation" (153-031). In every moment, God must conserve the world in existence — which is equivalent to creating it anew. This doctrine, sometimes called **continuous creation**, makes divine causality pervasive: every instant of the world's existence depends on God's sustaining power.

## Historical Context

The principle that the cause must be proportionate to the effect has deep roots. **Aristotle** argued that actuality is prior to potentiality — nothing can be reduced from potentiality to actuality except by something already actual. **Thomas Aquinas** used this principle in his Five Ways, arguing that a chain of causes cannot go to infinity and must terminate in a first cause (God).

Descartes' innovation is to formulate the principle in terms of **reality** (or perfection) rather than actuality or being. This allows him to apply it to ideas: an idea's objective reality can demand a cause with proportionate formal reality. The scholastics did not apply causal principles to ideas in this way — Descartes' move from things to ideas is the hallmark of his epistemological turn.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Hume** launched the most influential critique of the causal principle. For Hume, the causal relation is not a necessary connection discoverable by reason but a constant conjunction observed in experience. The idea that the cause must contain the reality of the effect is a metaphysical prejudice, not a self-evident truth.

**Kant** accepted causality as a necessary condition of experience but denied that it applies beyond the bounds of possible experience. The causal argument for God's existence, Kant argued, illegitimately extends the category of causality from phenomena to noumena — from the world of experience to the transcendent.
