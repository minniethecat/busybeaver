## Definition

In Descartes' epistemology, **intuition** (*intuitus* in Latin, *inspectio mentis* — "inspection of the mind") is the direct, immediate, and indubitable apprehension of a truth by the pure intellect, without the mediation of the senses, imagination, or discursive reasoning. It is, alongside deduction, one of the two fundamental operations of the mind by which certain knowledge (*scientia*) is attained.

In the *Rules for the Direction of the Mind* (Rule III), Descartes defines intuition as "the conception of a pure and attentive mind, which is so easy and distinct that there can be no doubt about what we are understanding" — a direct intellectual "seeing" analogous to sensory vision but operating at the level of pure thought.

## The Wax Argument

The most celebrated illustration of intellectual intuition in Descartes' work appears in the Second Meditation (013-002), where he examines a piece of wax. As the wax is brought near the fire, all of its sensory qualities — its hardness, coldness, shape, color, fragrance — change. Yet we know it remains the same wax. Descartes concludes that our knowledge of the wax does not come from the senses or imagination:

> The perception of it is neither an act of sight, of touch, nor of imagination... but is simply an intuition (*inspectio*) of the mind, which may be imperfect and confused, as it formerly was, or very clear and distinct, as it is at present.
>
> (Meditations II)

This passage demonstrates that even our knowledge of material objects ultimately rests on intellectual intuition, not sensation. The mind "sees" the essence of the wax — that it is an extended, flexible, and changeable thing — through an act of pure intellection. The Second Meditation establishes that the mind knows itself more certainly and directly than it knows bodies (013-002), laying the foundation for Descartes' [[dualism]].

## Intuition and Deduction

For Descartes, intuition provides the indubitable starting points of knowledge: simple, self-evident truths that the mind grasps directly. Deduction then chains these intuitions together into longer chains of reasoning. The model is Euclidean geometry: intuitions are the axioms and definitions; deduction is the step-by-step proof.

> We say, for example, that we see the same wax when it is before us, and not that we judge it to be the same from its retaining the same color and figure: whence I should forthwith be disposed to conclude that the wax is known by the act of sight, and not by the intuition of the mind alone.
>
> (Meditations II)

The *cogito* itself — "I think, therefore I am" — is the paradigmatic intuition in Descartes' system (013-002): an indubitable truth grasped in a single, instantaneous act of the mind.

## Intuition in Rationalism and Empiricism

The concept of intellectual intuition distinguishes [[rationalism]] sharply from [[empiricism]]. For rationalists like Descartes, [[Baruch Spinoza|Spinoza]], and [[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Leibniz]], intuition is the highest form of cognition, providing direct access to necessary truths. Spinoza's *Ethics* identifies *scientia intuitiva* (intuitive knowledge) as the third and highest kind of knowledge, surpassing both opinion and reason — it is the intellectual love of God, seeing things *sub specie aeternitatis*.

Empiricists like [[John Locke|Locke]] and [[David Hume|Hume]] rejected the notion of intellectual intuition, arguing that all knowledge derives ultimately from sensory experience. [[Immanuel Kant|Kant]] partly revived the concept in his notion of "pure intuitions" of space and time, though he restricted intuition to the sensory (*Anschauung*), denying that humans possess intellectual intuition — a capacity he reserved for a divine intellect.
