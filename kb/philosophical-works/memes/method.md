# Method

In Descartes' philosophy, the **method** (*methodus*) is a systematic procedure for directing reason toward the discovery of [[truth]]. Descartes presents his method in the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]* as the instrument that enabled all his discoveries in mathematics, physics, and metaphysics. It is not merely a set of rules but a discipline of the [[mind]] — a habit of orderly thinking that begins with radical [[doubt]] and ascends through analysis and synthesis to certain [[knowledge]]. The method unifies Descartes' contributions across disciplines and stands as one of the most influential accounts of scientific reasoning in the history of philosophy.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces his method in Part II of the *Discourse* as consisting of four principal rules: "the first was never to accept anything as true that I did not evidently know to be such" (the rule of evidence); "the second, to divide each of the difficulties under examination into as many parts as possible" (the rule of analysis); "the third, to conduct my thoughts in order, beginning with objects the simplest and easiest to know, and ascending little by little to knowledge of the most complex" (the rule of synthesis); and the fourth, "to make enumerations so complete and reviews so general that I might be assured that nothing was omitted" (the rule of enumeration).

Descartes presents his method as superior to traditional logic. He writes: "I was induced to seek some other method which would comprise the advantages of the three [logic, geometric analysis, and algebra] and yet be exempt from their defects" (005-006). The method combines the certainty of geometry with the generality of algebra.

In the *Discourse*, Descartes emphasizes that his aim "is not to teach the method which each ought to follow for the right conduct of his reason, but solely to describe the way in which I have endeavored to conduct my own" (004-005). This modesty is strategic — the method is presented as a personal discovery rather than a universal prescription, though Descartes clearly intends it as the latter.

## The Four Rules

Descartes' method is defined by four precepts, each addressing a distinct phase of inquiry:

| Rule | Name | Function | Application |
|------|------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Evidence | Accept only what is clearly and distinctly perceived as true | Reject all merely probable opinions; the foundation of methodical doubt |
| 2 | Analysis | Divide difficulties into their simplest parts | Break complex problems into elementary, self-evident components |
| 3 | Synthesis | Proceed from simple to complex in orderly fashion | Reconstruct knowledge by logical deduction, as in geometry |
| 4 | Enumeration | Review completely to ensure nothing omitted | Verify that all links in the chain of reasoning are secure |

Each rule presupposes and reinforces the others. The **rule of evidence** (Rule 1) provides the epistemic standard that governs the entire procedure: accept nothing that can be doubted. The **rule of analysis** (Rule 2) implements the Cartesian conviction that complex truths are composed of simple, intuitively known elements. The **rule of synthesis** (Rule 3) guarantees that the reconstructed knowledge is deductively sound. The **rule of enumeration** (Rule 4) serves as a completeness check — it is Descartes' answer to the problem of ensuring that analysis has not omitted any relevant factor.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' method is founded on two core principles: **methodical doubt** (the rule of evidence) and **orderly progression** (analysis and synthesis). The rule of evidence requires that the mind accept only what is "so clearly and distinctly presented to my mind that I could have no occasion to doubt it" — this is the epistemic standard that drives the *Meditations*.

The analysis-synthesis structure mirrors the method of geometers: reduce complex problems to simple elements (analysis), then reconstruct knowledge from those elements in logical order (synthesis). Descartes claims that "no other advantage from the method I employ beyond satisfying my own mind on certain difficulties" (009-002), but his actual aim is far more ambitious: to provide a unified method for all the sciences.

Descartes' method is also a method of **discovery**, not merely of proof. Unlike [[aristotle|Aristotle's]] syllogistic, which Descartes dismisses as useful only for "explaining to another what one already knows," his method is supposed to generate new [[knowledge]]. This distinguishes the Cartesian method from both scholastic logic and the empirical induction advocated by his contemporary [[francis-bacon|Bacon]].

## Historical Context

Descartes' method emerged from his dissatisfaction with the education he received at **La Flèche**, the Jesuit college. He found scholastic philosophy full of disputes but empty of certain knowledge. Mathematics offered [[certainty]], but its methods were not applied outside geometry and arithmetic.

The *Discourse* was published in 1637 as the preface to three scientific essays (the *Dioptrics*, *Meteorology*, and *[[geometry-descartes|Geometry]]*). The method was presented as having been developed in 1619–1620, when Descartes experienced his famous "vision" in the stove-heated room at Ulm. Earlier, unpublished versions appear in the *Rules for the Direction of the Mind* (*Regulae ad Directionem Ingenii*, c. 1628), which contains 21 rules rather than the *Discourse's* four.

**Francis Bacon** had published his *Novum Organum* (1620) proposing an inductive method based on systematic observation. Descartes' method, by contrast, is rationalist and deductive — it proceeds from clear intellectual intuitions rather than sensory data. This contrast defines the rationalist-empiricist divide that structures early modern philosophy.

## Criticisms and Responses

Critics have noted that Descartes' method, despite its claims to universality, works best in mathematics and mathematical physics. Its applicability to ethics, politics, and the humanities is questionable. **Vico** argued that the Cartesian method is inappropriate for historical knowledge, which requires a different kind of understanding (*verum factum*).

The rule of evidence — accept only what is clear and distinct — is also circular: it presupposes the very criterion it is supposed to establish. Descartes' response, developed in the *Meditations*, is that [[god|God]] guarantees clear and distinct [[perception]]. This answer depends on proofs that themselves rely on the method, generating the familiar "Cartesian circle."
