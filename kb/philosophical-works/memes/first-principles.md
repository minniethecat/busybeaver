## Definition

In Descartes' philosophy, **first principles** (*prima principia*) are the foundational truths that serve as the indubitable starting points for all certain knowledge. Just as Euclidean geometry rests on axioms and definitions from which all theorems are deduced, Descartes' philosophical system rests on first principles known with absolute certainty through intellectual intuition.

> I am in doubt as to the propriety of making my first meditations in the place above mentioned matter of discourse... (007-001)

The search for first principles is the central project of the *Meditations on First Philosophy* — the very title signals Descartes' intention to establish the foundations (*principia*) upon which all knowledge of God, the mind, and the material world must rest.

## The Method for Discovering First Principles

Descartes' method for arriving at first principles proceeds through three stages:

1. **Methodological doubt**: All beliefs that can be doubted — even slightly — are set aside. This includes sensory evidence (which can deceive), the existence of the external world (which could be a dream), and even mathematical truths (which could be the work of an [[evil-demon]]).

2. **The *cogito***: The act of doubting itself reveals an indubitable truth — I exist as a thinking thing. "I think, therefore I am" (*cogito, ergo sum*) is the first and most certain first principle (013-002).

3. **The criterion of truth**: From the *cogito*, Descartes derives a general rule: whatever is perceived clearly and distinctly must be true (013-004). This criterion itself becomes a first principle, guaranteed by the veracity of God.

## The Three Fundamental First Principles

Descartes' mature system rests on three interconnected first principles:

### 1. The *Cogito*
The certainty of one's own existence as a thinking substance. This is known through a direct act of intellectual [[intuition]] — no deduction is required.

### 2. The Existence of God
God's existence is demonstrated in the Third and Fifth Meditations through multiple arguments: the causal argument (the idea of an infinite, perfect being requires an infinite, perfect cause) and the ontological argument (existence is a perfection, and God, as the supremely perfect being, must possess it).

### 3. The Reliability of Clear and Distinct Perception
Since God exists and is not a deceiver (089-002), the cognitive faculties He gave us — when used properly — must be reliable. Whatever we perceive clearly and distinctly must be true. This principle closes the skeptical loop opened by the evil demon hypothesis and vindicates the use of reason.

## First Principles and the Tree of Knowledge

In the Preface to the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes famously compares philosophy to a tree:

> Thus the whole of philosophy is like a tree: the roots are metaphysics, the trunk is physics, and the branches emerging from the trunk are all the other sciences, which may be reduced to three principal ones, namely medicine, mechanics, and morals.

The "roots" — metaphysics — contain the first principles: the nature of God, the immateriality of the soul, and the foundations of clear and distinct knowledge. From these, the "trunk" of physics (including the [[laws-of-nature]], the nature of [[body]], and the mechanics of [[motion]]) can be deduced. The "branches" — applied sciences — grow from physics.

## Critiques and Legacy

Descartes' first principles have faced sustained criticism:

- **The Cartesian Circle**: Critics charge that Descartes' proof of God's existence relies on clear and distinct perception, while the reliability of clear and distinct perception depends on God's existence — a circular argument (see [[cartesian-circle-synthesis]]).

- **The *cogito* as inference**: Some argue that "I think, therefore I am" is not a genuine first principle but a syllogism with a suppressed premise ("whatever thinks, exists").

- **Foundationalism**: Descartes' project of grounding all knowledge in certain first principles — **[[foundationalism]]** — became the dominant epistemology of modern philosophy but was challenged by coherentism, pragmatism, and later by [[skepticism-as-method|skeptical]] critiques.

Despite these challenges, Descartes' insistence that philosophy must begin with methodically established first principles remains one of his most enduring contributions to Western thought.
