# Meditations on First Philosophy

The **Meditations on First Philosophy** (*Meditationes de Prima Philosophia*) was published in 1641 in Latin, accompanied by six sets of **Objections and Replies** from leading intellectuals of the day (including **Hobbes**, **Gassendi**, **Arnauld**, and **Mersenne**). It is Descartes' most rigorous and influential philosophical work — the text in which he systematically dismantles all [[knowledge]] through methodical [[doubt]] and rebuilds it on the foundation of the *cogito*. The *Meditations* represents Descartes' mature philosophical [[method]] at full strength: beginning from radical scepticism, it advances through the discovery of the [[mind|self]], the demonstration of [[god|God's]] existence, and the proof of the real distinction between [[mind]] and [[body]], establishing the metaphysical framework on which all modern philosophy would build.

## Overview

The *Meditations* is structured as a first-person spiritual exercise. Descartes asks the reader to perform the meditations with him — to set aside all preconceptions, enter into radical doubt, and discover for themselves the truths he unfolds. This is not a textbook to be read but a discipline to be practiced.

Unlike the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]*, which was written in French for a general audience, the *Meditations* was written in Latin for the scholarly community. Its rigorous structure — six meditations, each building on the previous — reflects Descartes' commitment to the method of analysis and synthesis. The accompanying Objections and Replies are a landmark: the first systematic peer review in the history of philosophy, with contributions from figures including [[hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]], [[gassendi|Pierre Gassendi]], [[antoine-arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]], and Marin Mersenne.

## Structure: The Six Meditations

| Meditation | Title/Theme | Key Discovery | Methodological Role |
|------------|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| I | Of the Things of Which We May Doubt | All sensory and mathematical beliefs can be doubted; the evil demon hypothesis | Destructive: clears the ground of all uncertain beliefs |
| II | Of the Nature of the Human Mind | *Cogito ergo sum*; the wax argument; mind known better than body | Constructive: discovers the first indubitable truth |
| III | Of God: That He Exists | Causal argument: the idea of the infinite requires an infinite cause | Theological: establishes God as guarantor |
| IV | Of Truth and Error | Error arises from will outrunning intellect; God is not a deceiver | Epistemological: explains how error is possible in a world created by a veracious God |
| V | Of the Essence of Material Things | Ontological argument for God; essence of body is extension | Ontological: secures the intelligibility of corporeal nature |
| VI | Of the Existence of Material Things | Real distinction of mind and body; proof of the external world | Conclusive: establishes dualism and the existence of body |

## Central Arguments

The *Meditations* systematizes the arguments sketched in the *Discourse*. The **method of doubt** is developed through three increasingly radical waves: sensory unreliability, the dream argument, the evil demon. The ***cogito*** is refined from the "I think, therefore I am" to "I am, I exist, is necessarily true each time I pronounce it or mentally conceive it."

The **two proofs of God** — causal (Third Meditation) and ontological (Fifth Meditation) — are the theological core. The causal argument holds that the [[idea]] of an infinite, perfect being contains more objective reality than any finite idea and therefore requires an actually infinite cause. The ontological argument holds that existence is a perfection, and the idea of a supremely perfect being necessarily includes existence.

The **mind-body distinction** (Sixth Meditation) is the metaphysical conclusion toward which the entire work aims: "the mind is thus enabled easily to distinguish what pertains to itself, that is, to the intellectual nature, from what is to be referred to the body" (013-002). Because mind is essentially thinking and body is essentially extended, and these attributes are conceptually independent, mind and body are really distinct substances — a conclusion that defined the mind-body problem for subsequent philosophy.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- [[cogito]] — the indubitable foundation: "I am, I exist"
- [[god]] — proved by causal (III) and ontological (V) arguments
- [[doubt]] — methodical, hyperbolic, deployed in three waves
- [[mind]] — thinking substance (*res cogitans*), really distinct from body
- [[body]] — extended substance (*res extensa*), known through intellect not senses
- [[dualism]] — the real distinction established in the Sixth Meditation
- [[error]] — explained in the Fourth Meditation as will outrunning intellect
- [[idea]] — the medium through which reality is represented to the mind

## Descartes on This Work

> The mind which, in the exercise of the freedom peculiar to itself, supposes that no object is, of the existence of which it has even the slightest doubt, finds that, meanwhile, it must itself exist.
>
> — Meditations, Synopsis (013-002)

## Historical Significance

The *Meditations* is the central text of Cartesian philosophy and one of the most studied works in the Western canon. Its arguments for dualism, its proofs of God, and its foundationalist epistemology have generated continuous debate from 1641 to the present. The work's influence extends far beyond philosophy: its model of the mind as a private, self-transparent theatre has shaped modern psychology, and its radical separation of mind from body has been both foundational and controversial for the cognitive sciences.
