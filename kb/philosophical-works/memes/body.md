# Body

In Descartes' philosophy, **body** (*corpus*) is extended substance — *res extensa* — whose essential nature consists solely in extension in three dimensions. As the complement to mind (thinking substance), body is the subject of all corporeal properties: local motion, figure, size, and the accidents that presuppose extension. Descartes' identification of body with extension is the cornerstone of his mechanistic physics.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes defines body in the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix as "the substance which is the immediate subject of local extension, and of the accidents that presuppose this extension, as figure, situation, local motion, etc." (153-010). He immediately raises the question that drives his dualism: "whether the substance which is called mind be the same with that which is called body, or whether they are two diverse substances" (153-010).

In the *Principles of Philosophy* proper, Descartes argues that "the nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing" (032-002). This identification of body with extension is a revolutionary simplification. It means that all physical properties — weight, hardness, colour, heat — are reducible to modes of extension and motion. "The quantity of the parts of which a body is composed does not depend on their weight or hardness, but only on the extension, which is always equal in the same vase" (032-002).

Descartes further argues that body — as extended substance — is indefinitely extended. "We further discover that this world or the whole (universitas) of corporeal substance, is extended without limit, for wherever we fix a limit, we still not only imagine beyond it spaces indefinitely extended, but perceive these to be truly imaginable" (034-002). This leads to the remarkable conclusion that "a plurality of worlds is impossible, because we clearly conceive that the matter whose nature consists only in its being an extended substance, already wholly occupies all the imaginable spaces" (035-002).

## Key Passages

> VII. The substance which is the immediate subject of local extension, and of the accidents that presuppose this extension, as figure, situation, local motion, etc., is called *body*.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Appendix: Definitions (153-010)

> The nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing, and its extension is not different from that which we attribute to space, however empty.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (032-002)

> It is not more contradictory to conceive a mountain without a valley than such a cavity without the extension it contains, or this extension apart from an extended substance, for, as we have often said, of nothing there can be no extension.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (031-002)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' conception of body has radical implications for physics. By reducing body to extension, he eliminates the need for substantial forms, occult qualities, and all the Aristotelian apparatus for explaining physical phenomena. Matter is just extension; all change is local motion. This is the foundation of **Cartesian mechanism**.

The rejection of the void follows directly: "it is not more contradictory to conceive a mountain without a valley than such a cavity without the extension it contains" (031-002). Space is not a container that can be empty — it is identical with extended substance itself. If God were to remove all body from a vessel, "the sides of the vessel would thus come into proximity with each other" (031-002). This plenism stands in stark contrast to the atomism of **Democritus** and **Epicurus**, which Descartes rejects.

Descartes also argues that all bodies — celestial and terrestrial — are made of the same matter: "the earth and heavens are made of the same matter" (035-002). This unification of physics under a single material principle was a major advance over the Aristotelian division between sublunary and superlunary realms.

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of body radically simplifies the Aristotelian-scholastic tradition. **Aristotle** held that bodies are composed of matter (*hyle*) and form (*morphe*), with different kinds of bodies having different substantial forms. The scholastics multiplied these forms to explain every observable property: heaviness was explained by the form of gravity, heat by the form of heat, and so on.

Descartes sweeps all of this away. There is only one kind of matter — extension — and all differences between bodies are differences in the size, shape, and motion of their parts. This was profoundly influential: **Galileo** had already distinguished primary qualities (size, shape, motion) from secondary qualities (colour, taste, smell), but Descartes went further by identifying matter itself with extension. Later mechanical philosophers, including **Boyle** and **Locke**, developed their own versions of the primary-secondary quality distinction, all indebted to Descartes' breakthrough.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Newton** would later challenge Descartes' identification of body with extension. For Newton, extension is space, not matter — space can be empty, and matter is distinguished by its mass and impenetrability, not mere extension. Newton's physics, with its forces acting at a distance, replaced Cartesian mechanism.

**Leibniz** also objected: if body is mere extension, then it lacks any active principle — any force or power. Leibniz proposed that substance must include an active force (*vis viva*), anticipating the concept of energy. The Cartesian reduction of body to passive extension could not, Leibniz argued, explain the activity we observe in nature.
