# Queen Christina of Sweden

**Queen Christina of Sweden** (1626–1689) was the philosopher-queen who brought [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] to Stockholm and inadvertently became the agent of his death. Christina ascended the Swedish throne at age six (on her father Gustavus Adolphus' death in battle in 1632) and began ruling in her own right at eighteen. Her determination to make Stockholm a "new Athens" — a center of learning to rival Paris and Amsterdam — led her to summon Descartes from the Netherlands in 1649. Descartes died four months later, a victim of the Swedish winter and the Queen's requirement that he tutor her at five o'clock in the morning.

## Biography

Christina was born in Stockholm, the only surviving legitimate child of Gustavus Adolphus. Educated as a prince rather than a princess, she mastered Latin, Greek, French, German, and Italian; studied theology, philosophy, and statecraft; and was declared of age to rule in 1644. Her court became a magnet for European intellectuals: [[hugo-grotius|Grotius]], the philologist Isaac Vossius, and the physician Pierre Bourdelot all spent time in Stockholm.

Christina was deeply interested in [[philosophy]], corresponding with [[gassendi|Gassendi]] and reading [[descartes|Descartes]]' *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*. Through the French ambassador Pierre Chanut — a friend of Descartes — she invited the philosopher to Sweden. Descartes arrived in October 1649; by February 1650 he was dead. Five years later, Christina abdicated, converted to Catholicism, and moved to Rome, where she became a patron of the arts and lived until her death.

## Relationship to Descartes

Christina's connection to Descartes was brief but transformative — for both:

1. **The Summons**: Through Chanut, Christina began a correspondence with Descartes in 1646, discussing the nature of love, the supreme good, and the passions. Impressed by his intellect, she invited him to Stockholm to organize a new Swedish academy of sciences.

2. **The Tutorials**: Descartes arrived in October 1649. Christina — an early riser — required him to give philosophy lessons at 5:00 a.m. in her unheated library, in the middle of the Swedish winter. Descartes, who habitually rose at 11:00 a.m. and spent his mornings in bed thinking, was constitutionally unsuited to this schedule.

3. **The Death**: In February 1650, Descartes contracted pneumonia and died on 11 February at age 53. His last words, according to Chanut, were "Ça, mon âme, il faut partir" ("So, my soul, it is time to depart"). The morning tutorials, the cold, and perhaps the sudden change in lifestyle had killed one of Europe's greatest minds.

4. **Posthumous Influence**: After Descartes' death, Christina preserved his manuscripts — including unpublished mathematical papers — and arranged for their eventual transmission. Her later conversion to Catholicism (1655) was influenced by the theological discussions she had hosted, including those with Descartes.

## Key Ideas

**Philosophical Patronage**: Christina's court represented a model of enlightened patronage — the sovereign as intellectual, actively engaging with and supporting philosophical work rather than merely funding it. Her letters to Descartes show genuine philosophical acumen, particularly on the nature of the passions and the highest good.

**The Academy Project**: Christina's vision for a Swedish academy — with Descartes as its intellectual centerpiece — anticipated the later scientific academies of the Enlightenment (the Royal Society, the Académie des Sciences). Though unrealized, it marks an important moment in the institutionalization of [[knowledge]].

> — Letter to Queen Christina, 20 April 1647
