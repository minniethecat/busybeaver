# Subject

In Descartes' philosophy, the **subject** (or "thinking thing," *res cogitans*) is the individual thinking self — the "I" that constitutes the first certainty of his philosophical system. Unlike the Aristotelian-scholastic notion of *subjectum* as the underlying substratum of attributes, Descartes radically reconceives the subject as the self-conscious thinker whose existence is grasped directly in the act of thinking.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' discovery of the subject occurs in the *Discourse on the Method* Part IV and the *Meditations*, through the method of radical doubt. Having resolved to reject everything that could be doubted, he arrives at the one indubitable truth: the existence of the thinking self. "Whilst I thus wished to think that all was false, it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be somewhat" (007-001). This yields the famous formula: "I think, hence I am" (*je pense, donc je suis* / *cogito, ergo sum*).

The subject Descartes discovers is defined solely by thought. "I attentively examined what I was... I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The "I" is therefore a **thinking substance**: "from the very circumstance that I thought to doubt of the truth of other things, it most clearly and certainly followed that I was; while, on the other hand, if I had only ceased to think, although all the other objects which I had ever imagined had been in reality existent, I would have had no reason to believe that I existed" (007-002).

In the *Principles of Philosophy* (Part I, LIII), Descartes provides a technical definition: "The substance in which thought immediately resides is here called mind" (153-009). Thought is the **principal attribute** of this substance: "thought constitutes the nature of thinking substance" (111-002). "Every other thing that can be attributed to body, presupposes extension, and is only some mode of an extended thing; as all the properties we discover in the mind are only diverse modes of thinking" (111-002).

## The Subject as Substance

In the scholastic Aristotelian tradition, *subjectum* (ὑποκείμενον) referred to the underlying bearer of properties — that of which things are predicated but which is not itself predicated of anything else. Descartes retains this formal definition — "Everything in which there immediately resides, as in a subject, or by which there exists any object we perceive... is called substance" (153-008) — but fills it with a new content. The thinking subject is no longer an abstract logical substratum; it is the concrete self known through the cogito.

Critically, the Cartesian subject is **distinct from the body**. "Those who have not philosophized in order have had other opinions on this subject, because they never distinguished with sufficient care the mind from the body" (070-002). Though the human being is a union of mind and body, the subject as thinking substance is fundamentally independent of extension.

## Historical Context

Descartes' conception of the subject represents a decisive break with both Aristotelian scholasticism and Renaissance naturalism. In Aristotle's *Categories*, the primary substance (*protē ousia*) is the individual concrete thing — "this man," "this horse" — and its status as subject is inseparable from its material composition. The scholastics (Aquinas, Scotus, Suárez) elaborated this into a complex metaphysics of form and matter.

Descartes reverses this priority. By the method of doubt, the subject loses its body, its world, even its own past — and discovers that what remains is pure thought. The "I" is no longer a composite of form and matter; it is a *thinking substance* whose essence is consciousness alone. This move inaugurates what later philosophers would call the "philosophy of the subject" — a tradition running from Descartes through Kant's transcendental unity of apperception to Husserl's transcendental ego.
