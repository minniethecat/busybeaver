# Eternal Truths

In Descartes' philosophy, **eternal truths** (*aeternae veritates*, *vérités éternelles*) are self-evident propositions that are not "things" or "affections of things" but truths that "possess no existence beyond our thought" — yet they are not arbitrary mental constructs, for they are grounded in God, who freely established them. Descartes distinguishes eternal truths from the objects of knowledge that are "things or the affections of things" (106-002). Eternal truths are what he also calls **common notions** or **axioms**: propositions so fundamental that they cannot be doubted when attentively considered.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes classifies all objects of human knowledge into two categories. The first comprises "things or the affections of things" — substances and their modes. The second comprises eternal truths, which "possess no existence beyond our thought" (106-002). Examples include: *ex nihilo nihil fit* (nothing comes from nothing), the principle of non-contradiction ("the same thing cannot at once be and not be"), and the principle that "what is done cannot be undone" (107-002).

Crucially, Descartes holds that "he who thinks must exist while he thinks" is also among these eternal truths (107-002) — a point that connects the doctrine of eternal truths directly to the *cogito*. This means the *cogito* is not merely a psychological experience but an instance of an eternal truth made manifest in the act of thinking.

Eternal truths are the foundation of all demonstration. "The mind also discovers certain common notions out of which it frames various demonstrations that carry conviction to such a degree as to render doubt of their truth impossible, so long as we give attention to them" (071-002). Mathematical proofs, for example, depend on these axioms — but their certainty ultimately requires knowledge of God's veracity.

## Key Passages

> Whatever objects fall under our knowledge we consider either as things or the affections of things, or as eternal truths possessing no existence beyond our thought. Of the first class the most general are substance, duration, order, number... I do not, however, recognise more than two highest kinds (*summa genera*) of things; the first of intellectual things... the second, of material things...
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part I, §48 (106-002)

> When we apprehend that it is impossible a thing can arise from nothing, this proposition, *ex nihilo nihil fit*, is not considered as somewhat existing, or as the mode of a thing, but as an eternal truth having its seat in our mind, and is called a common notion or axiom.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part I, §49 (107-002)

> We will likewise find that we possess the knowledge of many propositions which are eternally true, as, for example, that nothing cannot be the cause of anything, etc.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part I, §75 (133-002)

## Arguments and Implications

**The Status of Eternal Truths**: Are eternal truths independent of God, or created by God? Descartes' most radical claim — expressed in his 1630 letters to Mersenne — is that eternal truths are **freely created by God's will**. God did not find the truths of mathematics already true; he made them true by an act of will. Just as a king establishes the laws of his kingdom, God establishes the laws of truth. This makes Descartes a **theological voluntarist** about truth — mathematical and logical truths are contingent on divine will.

**The Seat in the Mind**: Although eternal truths are grounded in God, they have their "seat in our mind" (107-002). They are innate — "born with us" — and "if blinded by no prejudices, we cannot fail to know them when the occasion of thinking them occurs" (107-002). This is part of Descartes' broader doctrine of [[innate-idea|innate ideas]].

**Epistemological Role**: Eternal truths are the axioms from which all certain knowledge is deduced. But Descartes introduces a crucial qualification: even these truths can be doubted under the hypothesis of a deceiving God (030-002). Only after proving God's existence and veracity can we trust that our clear and distinct perception of eternal truths is reliable.

## Historical Context

Descartes' doctrine of freely created eternal truths was **controversial even in his own time**. The scholastic tradition, following Thomas Aquinas, held that eternal truths exist in the divine intellect independently of God's will — God knows them because they are true, they are not true because God wills them. Descartes reversed this: God's will is the efficient cause of truth itself.

**Leibniz** strongly objected to this doctrine. For Leibniz, eternal truths are necessary in all possible worlds — even God could not have made 2 + 2 = 5. To say otherwise, Leibniz argued, is to undermine the very possibility of rational knowledge.

**Spinoza** took the opposite approach: he identified eternal truths with God's essence itself, making them necessary in the strongest possible sense. For Spinoza, things could not have been produced by God in any other way than they are — eternal truths flow from the divine nature with mathematical necessity.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The Problem of Divine Arbitrariness**: If God freely created the eternal truths, could he have made contradictions true? Descartes would respond that our finite minds cannot comprehend God's infinite power — what appears impossible to us may not be impossible for God. But critics charge that this makes rational theology impossible, since we can never be sure that God's nature conforms to our logic.

**The Circularity Problem**: Descartes uses eternal truths (like *ex nihilo nihil fit*) in his proofs of God's existence, but he also claims that the certainty of eternal truths depends on God's veracity. This appears circular. Descartes' response is that eternal truths are self-evident *while we attend to them* — the doubt only arises when we turn our attention away, and God's veracity guarantees that what was once clearly perceived remains true.
