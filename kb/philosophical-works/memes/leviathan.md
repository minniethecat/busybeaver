# Leviathan

**Leviathan** (1651) is [[hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]]' masterwork and one of the foundational texts of modern political [[philosophy]] — a systematic attempt to derive the necessity of absolute sovereignty from a materialist account of human nature. Hobbes was one of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' most astute critics: he was among the objectors to the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]*, collected by [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]], where he challenged Descartes' [[dualism]] and argued that thinking is a bodily process. *Leviathan* develops this materialist philosophy into a complete system encompassing [[body]], man, and citizen.

## Overview

Published in 1651, *Leviathan* is divided into four parts. **Part I: Of Man** presents a materialist psychology: all mental phenomena — sensation, imagination, reasoning, the passions — are motions in the body caused by external objects. **Part II: Of Commonwealth** derives political authority from the social contract: individuals in the state of nature, fearing violent death, surrender their natural rights to a sovereign who guarantees peace. **Part III: Of a Christian Commonwealth** argues that ecclesiastical authority must be subordinated to civil sovereignty. **Part IV: Of the Kingdom of Darkness** attacks scholastic philosophy and Catholic theology as instruments of deception.

## Hobbes and Descartes

Hobbes' relationship with Descartes was one of critical engagement:

1. **Materialism vs. Dualism**: Where Descartes argued that the [[mind]] is an immaterial substance distinct from [[body]], Hobbes insisted that "a thinking thing is something corporeal." His objection to the *Meditations* — that Descartes had not proven that the "I" that thinks is not a body — anticipated the central problem of Cartesian dualism.

2. **Shared Mechanism**: Despite their metaphysical disagreement, Hobbes and Descartes shared a commitment to mechanistic explanation. For both, the physical world — including the human body — is to be explained by matter in [[motion]] according to mathematical laws.

3. **Divergent Politics**: Descartes never developed a systematic political philosophy; Hobbes did. The *Leviathan* represents what a Cartesian political philosophy grounded in materialism rather than dualism might look like.

## Key Themes

**The State of Nature**: Hobbes' famous account of human life without government — "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — grounds political obligation in rational self-interest rather than divine right or natural sociability.

**The Social Contract**: Individuals covenant together to transfer their natural rights to a sovereign, creating an "artificial man" — the Leviathan — whose will represents the will of all. This is a mechanistic model of the state: sovereignty as the soul, magistrates as the joints, rewards and punishments as the nerves.

**Materialist Epistemology**: Hobbes' theory of knowledge is empiricist and nominalist: all ideas derive from sensation; universal terms are names, not real essences. This directly opposes Descartes' doctrine of innate ideas and the [[cogito]].
