# Principles of Philosophy

The **Principles of Philosophy** (*Principia Philosophiae*) was published in 1644 in Latin, with a French translation (supervised by Descartes) appearing in 1647. It is Descartes' most comprehensive work — a systematic presentation of his entire philosophical system, from the first principles of metaphysics to the specific mechanisms of physics and cosmology. Descartes intended it as a university textbook to replace the Aristotelian manuals then in use.

## Overview

The *Principles* is structured as a deductive system. Part I establishes metaphysical foundations: the nature of knowledge, clear and distinct perception, the existence of God, and the nature of substance. Parts II–IV develop the physics that follows from these foundations: the laws of motion, the nature of body as extension, the rejection of the void, the vortex theory of planetary motion, and explanations of terrestrial phenomena (magnetism, fire, glass).

In this Standard Ebooks edition, the *Principles* includes the **Appendix containing the Definitions and Axioms** — Descartes' attempt to present his philosophy "in geometrical order" (*more geometrico*), after the model of Spinoza's later *Ethics*. The Appendix defines substance, mind, body, and God (153-008 through 153-011), and presents the axioms from which the mind-body distinction can be demonstrated.

## Structure

**Part I**: Metaphysical principles — the foundations of knowledge, the existence of the self, God, the nature of truth and error.

**Part II**: The principles of material things — extension as the essence of body, the rejection of the void, the indefinite extension of the universe, the laws of motion.

**Part III**: The visible world — celestial phenomena, the vortex theory, the nature of the sun and fixed stars.

**Part IV**: The earth — terrestrial phenomena including gravity, magnetism, fire, glass, and the magnet.

**Appendix**: Definitions, postulates, and axioms arranged in geometrical order, demonstrating the existence of God and the real distinction between mind and body.

## Central Arguments

The *Principles* systematizes Descartes' identification of **matter with extension**: "the nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing" (032-002). This is the foundational principle of Cartesian physics — all corporeal phenomena are explicable in terms of the size, shape, and motion of extended parts.

Descartes derives his **laws of motion** from the immutability of God. The total quantity of motion is conserved; each body tends to continue in its state; all motion is rectilinear in tendency. These are the first systematic laws of nature in modern physics, anticipating Newton's first law.

The *Principles* also contains Descartes' most explicit discussion of **Aristotle**. Descartes claims that his philosophy "made use of no principle which was not received and approved by Aristotle" (058-002) — a strategic claim that mechanism is the true Aristotelian physics, purged of scholastic corruptions.

## Descartes on This Work

> I have nevertheless made use of no principle which was not received and approved by Aristotle, and by the other philosophers of all ages; so that this philosophy, so far from being new, is of all others the most ancient and common.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part IV (058-002)

## Historical Significance

The *Principles* was Descartes' most ambitious work — an attempted replacement for the Aristotelian corpus that had dominated university education for centuries. While it did not succeed in displacing Aristotle, it established the framework for Cartesian physics that would dominate European science until Newton's *Principia* (1687).
