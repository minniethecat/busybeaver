Matter, in Descartes' philosophy, is corporeal or extended substance (*res extensa*) — the fundamental constituent of the physical world whose sole essential attribute is extension in length, breadth, and depth. Together with mind (*res cogitans*, thinking substance), it forms one half of Descartes' substance dualism. Unlike scholastic Aristotelian physics, which attributed various "forms" and "qualities" to matter, Descartes radically reduced matter to pure geometric extension, making all physical phenomena explicable in terms of size, shape, and motion (008-002).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces his conception of matter most systematically in *The World* (summarized in Discourse Part V) and *Principles of Philosophy* Part II. Breaking with scholastic tradition, he expressly supposes that matter "possessed none of those forms or qualities which are so debated in the schools" (008-002). For Descartes, the nature of corporeal substance "consists only in its being an extended thing" (032-002). This identification of matter with extension has profound consequences: there can be no empty space (vacuum), since wherever there is extension there is matter, and matter is infinitely divisible — there are no atoms (033-002).

Central to Descartes' dualism is the claim that the rational soul "could by no means be educed from the power of matter" but "must be expressly created" (008-009). Matter and mind are "substances really reciprocally distinct" — we cannot conceive "the half of a mind, as we can of any body, however small, so that the natures of these two substances are to be held, not only as diverse, but even in some measure as contraries" (013-002).

In Descartes' physics, a body is defined as "a part of matter" — "all that which is transferred together, although it be perhaps composed of several parts, which in themselves have other motions" (038-002). All physical change reduces to the motion of extended parts.

>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part V (008-002)

> After we have thus remarked that the nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing, and that its extension is not different from that which we attribute to space, however empty, it is easy to discover the impossibility of any one of its parts in any way whatsoever occupying more space at one time than at another, and thus of being otherwise than rarefied.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (032-002)

> I had after this described the reasonable soul, and shown that it could by no means be educed from the power of matter, as the other things of which I had spoken, but that it must be expressly created.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part V (008-009)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' identification of matter with extension has far-reaching implications for his philosophical system. In **metaphysics**, it establishes the real distinction between mind and body: the essence of matter is extension, the essence of mind is thought, and neither can be reduced to the other (013-002). This dualism grounds the argument for the immortality of the soul — the destruction of the body does not entail the destruction of the mind.

In **physics**, the reduction of matter to extension enables a purely mechanistic science. All natural phenomena — light, heat, gravity, magnetism — can be explained by the motion and arrangement of extended parts (008-002). There is no need for occult qualities, substantial forms, or final causes.

In **theology**, created matter depends continuously on God's concurrence. Descartes held that God, having created matter and established the laws of nature, sustains the physical world at every moment through the same power by which He created it (008-002).

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of matter represents a decisive break with Aristotelian-Scholastic physics, which explained physical change through matter-form composition and four causes. By reducing matter to pure extension, Descartes joined Galileo in mathematizing nature — making geometry the language of physics. This mechanization of the world picture was enormously influential: Newton, while rejecting Descartes' identification of extension with matter, inherited the project of explaining physical phenomena through mathematical laws.

The concept also has roots in ancient atomism (Democritus, Epicurus), but Descartes rejected the void and indivisible atoms, arguing that "the smallest extended particle is always divisible, since it is such of its very nature" (033-002).

## Criticisms and Responses

**The Vacuum Problem**: If matter is extension, then empty space is impossible — wherever there is extension, there is matter. This plenum theory was empirically challenged by Torricelli's barometer experiments. Descartes responded by arguing that what appears to be empty space is filled with "subtle matter" imperceptible to the senses.

**The Mind-Body Problem**: If matter and mind are entirely distinct substances, how do they interact? Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia pressed this objection. Descartes located the point of interaction at the pineal gland but never fully resolved the difficulty — this remains the canonical mind-body problem in philosophy.

**Newton's Critique**: Newton demonstrated that Descartes' identification of matter with extension cannot account for gravity, which acts at a distance through empty space. Newton's *Principia* effectively replaced Cartesian physics, though the mechanistic ambition Descartes championed endured.
