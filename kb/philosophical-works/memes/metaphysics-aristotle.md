# Metaphysics (Aristotle)

**Aristotle's *Metaphysics*** is the foundational text of the tradition that [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] set out to dismantle. For two millennia, Aristotle's doctrine of substance, form, and matter provided the framework for Western [[philosophy]], elaborated by medieval scholastics like [[aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]] into a comprehensive system. Descartes' rejection of Aristotelian metaphysics — his replacement of substantial forms with mechanical laws (022-005), his relocation of certainty from the external world to the [[cogito|cogito, ergo sum]] — marks the decisive break between ancient and modern philosophy (022-007).

## Overview

The *Metaphysics* is a collection of fourteen books (organized by later editors) that Aristotle described as the science of "being *qua* being" — the study of what exists in the most fundamental sense. Its central books examine: the nature of **substance** (*ousia*) as the primary mode of being; the doctrine of **form and matter** (hylomorphism); the four **causes** (material, formal, efficient, final); and the existence of the **Unmoved Mover**, the divine intellect that is pure actuality and the ultimate explanation of all motion.

## Descartes' Critique

Descartes' rejection of Aristotelian metaphysics was systematic and radical:

1. **Against Substantial Forms**: Descartes dismissed Aristotelian forms as occult qualities that "explain" nothing. In their place, he proposed a mechanistic physics in which all natural phenomena result from matter in [[motion]] — the sizes, shapes, and movements of corpuscles, governed by mathematical laws.

2. **Against Final Causes**: Aristotle's teleology — that things have intrinsic purposes or ends — was rejected by Descartes as presumptuous. We cannot know God's purposes, and natural phenomena should be explained by efficient causes alone.

3. **Against the Senses**: Aristotle grounded knowledge in sensory experience; Descartes grounded it in the clear and distinct ideas of the intellect. The *Meditations* systematically undermines sensory knowledge to establish the [[cogito]] as the new foundation.

4. **A New First Philosophy**: Descartes' *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* was explicitly intended to replace Aristotelian metaphysics as "first philosophy." Instead of beginning with being, Descartes begins with thinking — with the indubitable certainty of one's own existence as a thinking thing.

## Legacy

Despite Descartes' critique, Aristotelian concepts persisted in transformed ways. Descartes' [[substance]] theory — though radically different — still uses the language of substance and attribute. The Cartesian distinction between [[mind]] and [[body]] can be seen as a transformation of Aristotle's distinction between form and matter. The *Meditations* is, in a deep sense, an anti-*Metaphysics* — but its questions are the same: What truly exists? What is the nature of substance? What can we know with certainty?
