# Ethics (Spinoza)

**Spinoza's *Ethics*** (*Ethica, Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata*, published posthumously in 1677) is the most rigorous and uncompromising expression of Cartesian rationalism — and simultaneously its most radical departure. [[spinoza|Baruch Spinoza]] took [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' vision of a philosophy modeled on [[geometry-descartes|geometry]] — proceeding from self-evident axioms through necessary deductions — and applied it more systematically than Descartes ever did. The result is a metaphysical system that begins with [[god|God]] and ends with human blessedness, collapsing Descartes' [[dualism]] into a monism of a single infinite [[substance]].

## Overview

The *Ethics* is structured in five parts on the model of Euclidean geometry: definitions, axioms, propositions, demonstrations, and scholia. **Part I: Of God** argues that there is only one substance — God, or Nature (*Deus sive Natura*) — which is infinite, self-caused, and necessarily existing. **Part II: Of the Nature and Origin of the Mind** shows that [[mind]] and [[body]] are not separate substances but two attributes of the same substance, perceived under the attributes of thought and extension respectively. **Part III: Of the Origin and Nature of the Affects** treats human emotions with geometric precision as necessary consequences of our nature. **Part IV: Of Human Bondage** analyzes the power of the passions over reason. **Part V: Of the Power of the Intellect, or Of Human Freedom** culminates in the intellectual love of God — the highest form of [[knowledge]] and the source of human blessedness.

## Spinoza and Descartes

Spinoza's relationship to Descartes is one of radicalization and transformation:

1. **Method**: Spinoza adopts Descartes' geometric ideal but executes it more rigorously. While Descartes' *Meditations* uses the analytic method, Spinoza's *Ethics* proceeds synthetically from first principles — "in geometrical order," as the subtitle declares.

2. **Substance**: Descartes recognized two created substances (mind and body) and one uncreated substance (God). Spinoza argues that there can only be **one** substance — God — of which mind and body are attributes. Cartesian dualism is absorbed into Spinozist monism.

3. **Freedom**: For Descartes, freedom lies in the will's ability to affirm or deny. For Spinoza, freedom is not free will but self-determination — acting from one's own nature according to adequate ideas, liberated from the bondage of the passions.

4. **God**: Descartes' God is a transcendent creator; Spinoza's God is immanent — the infinite substance of which everything that exists is a mode. This led to Spinoza being denounced as an atheist, though he called it the intellectual love of God.

## Key Themes

**Conatus**: Each thing, insofar as it is in itself, strives to persevere in its being (*conatus in suo esse perseverandi*). This is the fundamental law of nature and the foundation of Spinoza's ethics — virtue IS power, and acting virtuously IS acting from one's own nature.

**Three Kinds of Knowledge**: Spinoza distinguishes imagination (inadequate ideas from sensory encounter), reason (adequate ideas of common notions), and intuition (the direct intellectual grasp of things in their dependence on God). The highest blessedness consists in the third kind of knowledge.
