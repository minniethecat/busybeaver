## Definition

Power (*potentia*) in Descartes' philosophy operates at multiple levels: God's infinite power of creation and conservation; the mind's power of judgment, will, and self-determination; and the mechanical powers of bodies in motion. The concept is central to Descartes' causal arguments for God's existence, his account of error, and his physics.

## God's Power

The most fundamental use of "power" in Descartes' system is divine. In the *Meditations*, Descartes argues from the idea of God within him: "we have in us the idea of a power so great, that by the being alone of this power ... all other perfections ... follow" (153-063). God's power is infinite, self-originating, and the source of all finite existence.

The causal argument for God's existence turns on the notion of power. Descartes reasons: if he had the power of self-conservation, he would also have the power to confer all perfections on himself (153-049). Since he lacks self-creating power — "I have not the power of conferring on myself these perfections" (153-051) — his existence must be sustained by a being with greater power.

> "If I possessed the power of conserving myself, I should also possess the power of conferring on myself all the perfections I lack" (153-049).

The argument extends: "Hence, I have not the power of self-conservation" (153-051). This leads to the conclusion that a being of infinite power — God — must exist as the conserving cause of the meditator's continued existence (153-052, 153-053, 153-056).

## Power of the Mind

In the *Discourse on Method*, Descartes asserts the universal human capacity for right judgment: "the power of judging aright and of distinguishing truth from error ... is naturally equal in all men" (004-001). This egalitarian claim about the power of reason — *bon sens* — grounds Descartes' method as universally accessible.

Descartes also warns against "projects that exceed their powers" (004-008), advocating methodological humility. The power of the mind, while equal in principle, requires proper direction through method to be effective.

## Power of Self-Determination

In "power" contexts, Descartes touches on the mind's capacity to direct itself: if things "are in its power," the will can command attention and assent (153-029). This connects power to the Cartesian doctrine of judgment — the will's infinite power combined with the intellect's finite capacity is what makes error possible.
