## Biography

Titus Lucretius Carus (c. 99 – c. 55 BCE) was a Roman poet and philosopher. Almost nothing is known about his life with certainty — the major biographical source is a brief and possibly unreliable account by St. Jerome, who claims Lucretius was driven mad by a love potion and composed his poem in lucid intervals before committing suicide. Modern scholars regard this story as likely legendary.

Lucretius was a devoted follower of [[epicurus]] and his philosophical poem _De Rerum Natura_ ("On the Nature of Things") is the most comprehensive surviving exposition of Epicurean physics, ethics, and epistemology. The work had a profound influence on the development of modern philosophy, particularly through its rediscovery in the Renaissance. Its atomistic cosmology directly inspired early modern mechanical philosophy, including the work of [[galileo]] and [[newton]].

## Key Philosophical Ideas

### Atomism and Mechanism

Lucretius expounded Epicurean [[atomism]]: the thesis that the universe consists exclusively of atoms (indivisible material particles) and void (empty space), and that all natural phenomena — including mind, perception, and life itself — can be explained through the motions and combinations of atoms. This eliminative materialism stands in stark contrast to the substance dualism of [[rene-descartes]], who posited an immaterial thinking substance alongside extended material substance.

Lucretius argued that the atoms, though eternal and indestructible, are in constant motion and naturally "swerve" (clinamen) unpredictably. This swerve introduces indeterminacy into an otherwise deterministic cosmos, providing a physical basis for free will — a problem that Descartes would later confront in his own terms.

### Mortality of the Soul

A central thesis of _De Rerum Natura_ is that the soul (anima) is material — a fine atomic body dispersed throughout the physical organism — and therefore perishes with the body. Lucretius deployed a battery of arguments against the fear of death: the dead do not exist to experience suffering; the symmetry between pre-natal and post-mortem non-existence; and the vanity of grieving over one's future absence. These arguments later influenced the [[immortality]] debates of the early modern period and shaped Descartes' own attempt to prove the soul's immateriality (and hence its natural immortality) in the _[[meditations-on-first-philosophy]]_.

### Anti-Teleology

Lucretius rejected final causes in nature, arguing that the apparent design of living things results from the blind trial-and-error of atomic combinations over vast time scales — an anticipation of natural selection. This anti-teleological stance contrasts with Descartes' own rejection of final causes (on different grounds — the inscrutability of divine purposes) and anticipates the mechanistic biology of the Scientific Revolution.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes never directly engaged with Lucretius in his published works, but Lucretian atomism forms the background against which Cartesian physics developed. Descartes rejected atomism — he denied both the void and indivisible particles — but shared Lucretius' commitment to mechanistic explanation in natural philosophy. Both sought to eliminate occult qualities and teleological explanations from physics.

The key differences are instructive. Where Lucretius saw the universe as the product of blind atomic motion, Descartes attributed the laws of motion and the conservation of [[motion]] to God's continuous creative action. Where Lucretius treated the soul as a material organ, Descartes made its immateriality the cornerstone of his metaphysics. The Cartesian system can be read as an attempt to preserve the mechanistic program while avoiding the theological consequences — atheism, materialism, mortalism — associated with Epicurean-Lucretian philosophy.
