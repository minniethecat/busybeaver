# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (1646–1716) was the third great rationalist of the early modern period, following **[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]** and **[[spinoza|Spinoza]]**. A universal genius — mathematician (co-inventor of calculus), logician, diplomat, historian, and mining engineer — Leibniz developed a systematic metaphysics that both critiqued and preserved central Cartesian insights. His doctrine of monads, principle of sufficient reason, and thesis that this is "the best of all possible worlds" constitute one of the most ambitious philosophical systems ever attempted.

## Biography

Leibniz was born in Leipzig in 1646, the son of a professor of moral philosophy. A prodigy, he taught himself Latin by age 8 and entered the University of Leipzig at 14. He earned a doctorate in law from the University of Altdorf (1666) but declined an academic career, entering the service of the Elector of Mainz as a diplomat.

Leibniz spent most of his career (1676–1716) in the service of the **House of Hanover**, where he served as court librarian, historian, and advisor. His diplomatic missions took him to Paris (1672–1676), where he met leading mathematicians and philosophers, and to London. He founded the **Berlin Academy of Sciences** (1700) and corresponded with over 1,000 intellectuals across Europe.

Leibniz died in Hanover at age 70, largely neglected by the court he had served for forty years. His vast Nachlass — over 50,000 manuscript pages — was not fully catalogued until the 20th century.

## Relationship to Descartes

Leibniz's relationship to Descartes is one of critical engagement. He studied Cartesian philosophy intensively in his youth and was briefly drawn to mechanism, but he came to reject key Cartesian doctrines:

1. **Critique of Cartesian Physics**: Leibniz argued that Descartes' laws of motion were incorrect. Descartes held that the quantity of motion (mass × speed) is conserved; Leibniz demonstrated that what is conserved is *vis viva* (mass × velocity², later kinetic energy). This was a major correction to Cartesian physics.

2. **Critique of Extension as Essence**: Descartes identified body with extension. Leibniz argued that extension alone cannot account for the resistance, inertia, and activity of bodies. The essence of substance must include an active principle — *force* or *entelechy*.

3. **From Mechanism to Organism**: Descartes' world is a machine; Leibniz's world is a hierarchy of living mirrors (monads), each representing the entire universe from its own point of view. This is a fundamentally organic, rather than mechanical, conception of nature.

Despite these critiques, Leibniz preserved Cartesian rationalism's core commitments: the primacy of reason, the search for first principles, and the ideal of a demonstrative metaphysics.

## Key Ideas

**Monads**: Leibniz's ultimate entities are monads — simple, immaterial, mind-like substances with no parts, no extension, and no causal interaction. Each monad represents the entire universe with varying degrees of clarity. God is the supreme monad. This is Leibniz's solution to the problems he found in Cartesian [[substance]] theory.

**Pre-established Harmony**: Since monads have "no windows" through which anything could enter, their apparent causal interaction is explained by God's pre-established harmony — God created each monad's internal program so that their states correspond perfectly. This replaces Descartes' problematic interactionism and [[malebranche|Malebranche's]] occasionalism.

**Principle of Sufficient Reason**: Nothing happens without a sufficient reason why it is so and not otherwise. God chose to create this world because it is the best possible — the one that maximizes perfection while minimizing evil. This principle grounds Leibniz's theodicy against the problem of evil.

**The Best of All Possible Worlds**: God, being perfectly good and wise, chose to create the best possible world from among the infinite possible worlds. The existence of evil does not disprove God's goodness, because evil may be a necessary component of the optimal whole — a view famously satirized by Voltaire in *Candide*.

## Criticisms of Cartesianism

Leibniz's objections to Descartes center on the nature of substance:

- Descartes' identification of material substance with extension makes matter inert and incapable of action. For Leibniz, true substances must be active sources of their own modifications — hence the need for an active principle (force or perception) in every monad.
- Descartes' interactionist dualism is unintelligible: how can an immaterial mind move a material body if the essence of body is pure extension? Pre-established harmony avoids this problem while preserving dualism.
- Descartes' criterion of clear and distinct [[perception]] is too subjective. Leibniz demands formal criteria for truth — the principle of contradiction (for necessary truths) and the principle of sufficient reason (for contingent truths).
