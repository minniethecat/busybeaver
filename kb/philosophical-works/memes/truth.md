# Truth

In Descartes' philosophy, **truth** (*veritas*) is the correspondence of clear and distinct perception with reality, guaranteed by the veracity of God. Descartes' epistemology is built around the question: how can we distinguish truth from error? His answer — that whatever we clearly and distinctly perceive is true — depends on his prior proof of God's existence and goodness.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' quest for truth begins with the method of radical doubt. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he resolves "to reject as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt, in order to see if there did not remain, after this, something in my belief that was wholly indubitable" (004-006). This method leads him to the *cogito* — the first indubitable truth.

The criterion of truth, once established, is clarity and distinctness. In the *Meditations*, Descartes argues that God is not a deceiver, so whatever we clearly and distinctly perceive must be true. "It may with truth be affirmed that all these things are in objects, which we clearly conceive to be contained in them" (153-018). This is the foundational rule of Cartesian epistemology.

Descartes asks his readers to cultivate the habit of recognizing truth. In the *Principles* Appendix, he recommends that readers "examine the ideas of those natures which contain in them an assemblage of several attributes" (153-018) — such as the nature of a triangle, of mind, of body, and above all of God. This examination trains the mind to recognize truth in purely intellectual perception, independent of the senses.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes distinguishes between formal truth (truth of things) and the truth of judgments. Formal truth belongs to ideas considered in themselves — every idea, as a mode of thought, is something real and true. The truth (or falsity) of judgments, however, depends on whether we affirm of things what is actually contained in our clear and distinct ideas of them.

The rule of truth — that clear and distinct perception is reliable — has both an epistemic and a metaphysical dimension. Epistemically, it provides a criterion: if an idea is clear and distinct, it can be trusted. Metaphysically, it depends on God: God would be a deceiver if he had created us such that our clearest perceptions were systematically false. Since God is perfect and not a deceiver, the rule holds.

Error, for Descartes, arises not from the intellect (which merely perceives ideas) but from the will (which affirms or denies beyond what the intellect clearly perceives). This analysis of error preserves God's goodness while explaining why we so often fall into falsehood.

## Historical Context

Descartes' conception of truth marks a decisive shift from medieval to modern epistemology. For the scholastics, following **Thomas Aquinas**, truth was *adaequatio rei et intellectus* — the adequation of the intellect to the thing. This correspondence theory assumed that the mind naturally grasps things as they are.

Descartes' method of doubt destabilizes this assumption. By bracketing all sensory and received knowledge, he requires truth to be established from within the mind itself. The criterion of clarity and distinctness replaces external correspondence as the working standard of truth. This "way of ideas" — making ideas, not things, the immediate objects of knowledge — sets the agenda for modern epistemology from **Locke** through **Kant**.

## Criticisms and Responses

The most famous objection is the **Cartesian circle**: Descartes uses clear and distinct perception to prove God's existence, then uses God's veracity to guarantee clear and distinct perception. Descartes' defenders distinguish between the *cogito* (self-verifying, needs no guarantee) and memory-dependent reasoning (requires God's guarantee). **Arnauld** raised this objection in the Fourth Objections.

A further criticism: the criterion of clarity and distinctness is subjective. What seems clear and distinct to one person may not to another. Descartes' test — that clarity resists methodical doubt — requires the meditator to actually perform the meditations, not merely read about them.
