In Descartes' philosophy, **love** (*amour*) is one of the six primitive passions, analyzed in his final work *The Passions of the Soul* (1649). Love is "an emotion of the soul caused by the movement of the spirits, which incites the soul to join itself in volition to objects that appear to be agreeable to it."

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes classifies love as one of the six primitive passions, alongside wonder, hatred, desire, joy, and sadness. He defines it physiologically: love is produced when the animal spirits convey to the brain an impression of an object as suitable or agreeable, which in turn causes the soul to feel itself drawn toward union with that object (047-002).

Descartes distinguishes several varieties of love. The most basic is *affection* — love for a thing valued less than oneself. *Friendship* is love for an equal. *Devotion* is love for something valued above oneself. The highest form is the *intellectual love of God*, which Descartes describes as a rational recognition that God is the source of all perfection and goodness (043-002). This intellectual love, unlike the passions, does not depend on bodily movements and is thus attainable even by the disembodied soul.

## Key Passages

> After the error of those who deny God... there is none that more effectually estranges men from the right path of virtue than the notion that the passions of animals and men are alike; for from this it is inferred that we, like the brutes, have nothing to hope or to fear after this life.
>
> — The Passions of the Soul, Article V (047-001)

> All the good and evil of this life depend on the passions alone; of which, however, some are better than others...
>
> — The Passions of the Soul, Article CCXII (055-002)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' account of love is part of his broader project of providing a mechanistic-physiological account of the passions, which had traditionally been studied under moral philosophy. By grounding the passions in the movements of animal spirits and the pineal gland, Descartes brings them within the scope of his mechanical physics (008-008).

At the same time, the analysis of love reveals the limits of purely mechanical explanation for Descartes. The *intellectual* love of God — a love unaccompanied by any bodily agitation — points toward a realm of purely rational affect that belongs to the soul alone. This anticipates Spinoza's more systematic treatment of intellectual love in the *Ethics*.

## Historical Context

Descartes' analysis of love belongs to a long tradition of philosophical reflection on the passions, from Aristotle's *Rhetoric* through Aquinas' *Summa Theologiae*. The *Passions of the Soul* was written in response to questions from Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia, who pressed Descartes on the interaction between mind and body. The work represents Descartes' most sustained engagement with moral psychology.

The distinction between bodily passion and intellectual love would prove highly influential. Malebranche developed it into a full theory of the love of God as the only motive not corrupted by self-interest. Spinoza reconceived intellectual love (*amor Dei intellectualis*) as the mind's participation in God's infinite self-knowledge.

## Criticisms and Responses

Descartes' physiological account of the passions, while pioneering, relies on a now-obsolete model of animal spirits and the pineal gland. His claim that love diminishes digestion (by diverting heat from the stomach to the heart) exemplifies the speculative character of much seventeenth-century psychophysiology.

The more philosophical challenge concerns whether "intellectual love" is genuinely a form of love or merely a rational appreciation. If love is essentially a bodily passion for Descartes, the notion of a purely intellectual love seems to strain the definition. His successors — particularly Spinoza — addressed this by reconceiving the mind-body relation more fundamentally.
