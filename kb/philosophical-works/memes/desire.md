# Desire

In Descartes' moral psychology, **desire** (*désir*) is a fundamental passion of the soul that directs the will toward goods perceived as future and attainable. It occupies a central place in both his provisional moral code in the *Discourse on the Method* and his systematic taxonomy of the passions in *The Passions of the Soul*, where it is classified as one of the six primitive passions from which all others derive.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes treats desire under two distinct but connected aspects: as a practical problem for moral philosophy (how should we regulate our desires to achieve contentment?) and as a psychophysical phenomenon (what bodily mechanism produces the passion of desire?).

The practical treatment appears most prominently in the *Discourse on the Method* Part III, where Descartes formulates his third provisional maxim: "to endeavor always to conquer myself rather than fortune, and change my desires rather than the order of the world" (006-004). This maxim is explicitly Stoic in inspiration — Descartes argues that "except our own thoughts, there is nothing absolutely in our power," and that recognizing this limitation "seemed to me sufficient to prevent me from desiring for the future anything I could not obtain, and thus to render me contented" (006-004). The ancient philosophers who grasped this truth "acquired a sway so absolute over their thoughts, that they had some ground on this account for esteeming themselves more rich and more powerful, more free and more happy, than other men" (006-004).

In the *Passions of the Soul*, Descartes provides a physiological account: desire is "an agitation of the soul caused by the animal spirits, which disposes the soul to will, for the future, the things which it represents to itself as suitable" (Passions, art. 86). Together with wonder, love, hatred, joy, and sadness, desire is one of the six primitive passions. Descartes distinguishes desire from volition proper — desire is a passion (something the soul *suffers*), whereas the act of willing is an action of the soul. The passion of desire, however, directly inclines the will toward its object.

Desire is future-oriented by its nature: "the passion of desire is always for the future" (Passions, art. 57). This distinguishes it from joy and sadness, which concern present goods and evils. Descartes notes that desire can be excessive and harmful when its object is unattainable, which is why the regulation of desire through reason is a central task of ethics. His correspondence with Princess Elisabeth and Queen Christina develops these themes, exploring how the passions — desire chief among them — can be mastered through the proper use of free will and the cultivation of "generosity" (*générosité*).

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' account of desire supports three interconnected arguments in his philosophical system. First, it underwrites his ethics of self-mastery: because the objects of desire are represented by the mind as future goods, and because the mind's judgments about what is attainable are corrigible by reason, the regulation of desire becomes a project of cognitive discipline rather than of ascetic renunciation (006-004). The third maxim of the provisional morality is thus not merely prudential advice but follows from Descartes' metaphysical commitments about the scope of human power.

Second, desire illuminates the mind-body union in Descartes' dualism. The appetites — hunger, thirst, and the bodily passions — belong "neither to the mind of itself, nor to the body alone, but to the close and intimate union between them" (106-002). This classification challenges readings of Descartes as a pure substance dualist who neglects psychophysical interaction. The passions, including desire, are the phenomena that most clearly resist reduction to either substance alone, and Descartes' physiology of the animal spirits (047-002) is his attempt to give a mechanistic account of this union without abandoning dualism.

Third, desire plays an epistemological role. In the *Discourse*, Descartes recounts that "I had always a most earnest desire to know how to distinguish the true from the false" (004-014). The properly directed desire for knowledge — what Descartes calls the search for "so necessary a science" (009-002) — is the motivational engine of the method. Without the passion of wonder (*admiration*) and the desire to understand, the method would remain an inert set of rules. Descartes' autobiographical narrative in the *Discourse* is thus structured around the transformation of a precritical desire for knowledge into a methodically disciplined inquiry.

## Historical Context

Descartes' treatment of desire draws on both Stoic and Augustinian sources. The maxim of conforming desire to what is within one's power echoes **Epictetus** and **Seneca**, whom Descartes had encountered through his Jesuit education at La Flèche. The Stoics taught that the passions are cognitive judgments that can be corrected by reason — a view Descartes largely adopts, though he adds a mechanistic physiology the Stoics lacked.

In the Augustinian tradition, desire (*cupiditas*) is the misdirection of love toward created goods rather than the Creator. Descartes secularizes this framework: the problem of desire is not its object (God vs. creatures) but its proportionality. Desiring what exceeds our power produces misery; desiring only what reason judges attainable produces contentment. This is a characteristically modern shift — the ethical standard is internal (the will's self-governance) rather than external (the divine law).

**Spinoza** would later radicalize Descartes' analysis: in the *Ethics*, desire (*cupiditas*) becomes the very essence of man — "appetite with consciousness of itself." **Hobbes**, writing contemporaneously with Descartes, similarly treats desire as the fundamental motive of human action, though with a more pessimistic anthropology. Descartes occupies a middle position: desire is natural and not inherently corrupt, but it requires rational discipline to prevent it from becoming a source of unhappiness.

## Criticisms and Responses

Descartes' treatment of desire has drawn several lines of criticism. One objection concerns the coherence of his classification of the passions as both bodily events and modes of thought. If desire is "an agitation of the soul caused by the animal spirits" (Passions, art. 86), it seems to be causally determined by a physical mechanism; yet Descartes also insists that the will can resist any passion through the proper use of free judgment. Critics in the Aristotelian tradition argued that Descartes had not adequately explained how a purely spiritual will could intervene in a mechanically determined chain of animal-spirit motions without violating the conservation of motion that he himself posited in the *Principles*.

A second criticism concerns the scope of Descartes' ethics. His prescription to "change my desires rather than the order of the world" (006-004) has been read as quietist — an accommodation to the status quo that precludes social and political reform. The Stoic inheritance of this maxim reinforces the concern: if the sage's freedom consists solely in adjusting internal attitudes to external necessity, then injustice in the "order of the world" is met with inner resignation rather than outward resistance. Descartes' defenders note that his provisional morality was explicitly a temporary code for practical action while the foundations of knowledge were being rebuilt, and that his later correspondence with Princess Elisabeth develops a more active conception of virtue as the rational pursuit of the common good.

A third line of criticism, advanced by **Malebranche** and later Cartesians, concerned the status of natural appetites. If hunger, thirst, and sexual desire are "sensations excited in the mind by means of the nerves" (047-002), then they are, strictly speaking, modes of thought caused by bodily states. But Descartes also maintains that we have "clear knowledge" of our appetites "if we take care to comprehend in the judgments we form of them only that which is precisely contained in our perception of them" (124-002). Malebranche objected that this epistemic optimism about bodily passions was unwarranted — that the senses and appetites systematically mislead us about the true nature of the good, and that only the "intellectual joy" (*gaudium intellectuale*) that Descartes himself distinguishes from bodily passion (047-002) can serve as a reliable guide.
