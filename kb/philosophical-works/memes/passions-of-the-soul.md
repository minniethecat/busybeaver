# Passions of the Soul

**Passions of the Soul** (*Les Passions de l'âme*, 1649) is [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' final major work and the capstone of his philosophical system — a treatise on the emotions written for his most perceptive correspondent, [[princess-elisabeth|Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia]]. Where the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* established the distinction between [[mind]] and [[body]], the *Passions* explores their **union** — how bodily states cause mental feelings, how the will can master them, and how wisdom consists in governing the passions rather than eliminating them.

## Overview

The treatise is divided into three parts. Part I surveys the nature of the passions in general, explaining them as "perceptions, feelings, or emotions of the [[soul]]" caused by the movement of animal spirits in the body (008-008). Descartes defines the passions as "the affections of the mind" — internal sense perceptions that arise from the union of mind and body (047-001). Part II provides a taxonomy of the six primitive passions — **wonder, love, hatred, desire, joy, and sadness** (047-002) — from which all others are composed. Part III treats specific passions and the art of governing them.

Descartes wrote the work during 1645–46 in response to Elisabeth's probing questions about the nature of happiness and the control of the emotions. It was published in French (not Latin) in 1649, the year before his death in Stockholm, and dedicated to Elisabeth.

## Key Themes

**The Mind-Body Union**: The *Passions* develops Descartes' most sustained account of how [[mind]] and [[body]] interact. The pineal gland serves as the "principal seat of the soul," where animal spirits cause passions and the will can redirect them. This is not dualism but a theory of psycho-physical integration.

**Generosity**: Descartes' central ethical virtue is *générosité* — the recognition of one's own free will and the resolve to use it well. A generous person knows that nothing truly belongs to them except their free use of their will, and therefore neither envies nor despises others.

**Mastery, Not Extirpation**: Against the Stoic ideal of *apatheia* (freedom from passion), Descartes argues that the passions are **all good by nature** — they are the body's way of directing the mind toward what is beneficial. The goal is not to eliminate them but to understand them and redirect them through habituation and judgment.

## In Descartes' System

The *Passions* completes the Cartesian project. The *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]* gave the [[method]]; the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* established metaphysics; the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles]]* developed physics; and the *Passions* provides the ethics. Together they form a unified tree of [[knowledge]] — metaphysics as roots, physics as trunk, and medicine, mechanics, and morals as branches.
