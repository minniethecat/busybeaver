# David Hume

**David Hume** (1711–1776) was the most radical of the British empiricists and, arguably, Descartes' most consequential critic. Where Descartes sought to ground all [[knowledge]] in the certainty of self-evident [[idea|ideas]], Hume demonstrated that the most basic operations of the human [[mind]] — causal reasoning, belief in an external world, the very idea of a unified self — rest not on rational demonstration but on custom, habit, and the "gentle force" of association. Hume's skeptical naturalism represents the logical terminus of the empiricist tradition that Descartes' rationalism defined itself against, and his critique of causation directly targets the Cartesian conception of [[substance]] and divine causation.

## Biography

Hume was born in Edinburgh, Scotland, and studied at the University of Edinburgh from age 12. He spent three years in France (1734–1737), where he wrote his masterpiece, *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–1740), at La Flèche — the very Jesuit college where [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had been educated a century earlier. The *Treatise* "fell dead-born from the press," in Hume's own words, though it is now regarded as one of the greatest works of Western philosophy.

Denied academic posts at Edinburgh and Glasgow because of his reputation for atheism, Hume worked as a tutor, librarian, and diplomat. His later works — the *Enquiries* and the *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (published posthumously) — refined the *Treatise*'s arguments in more accessible prose. He died in Edinburgh, cheerful and unrepentant, prompting [[kant|Kant]]'s famous tribute: Hume "interrupted my dogmatic slumber."

## Relationship to Descartes

Hume's philosophy is best understood as a systematic inversion of the Cartesian project:

1. **Impressions vs. Innate Ideas**: Descartes traced knowledge to innate [[idea|ideas]] and the natural light of [[reason]]. Hume divided all perceptions into *impressions* (vivid, immediate sensations and feelings) and *ideas* (faint copies of impressions). Every idea must ultimately derive from some prior impression — there are no innate ideas. The Cartesian *[[cogito]]* is not a rational intuition but a "bundle or collection of different perceptions."

2. **The Rejection of Substance**: Descartes posited three [[substance|substances]] — God, [[mind]], and [[body]] (extension). Hume argued that "substance" is a fiction: we have no impression of any substance, only of particular qualities. The self is not a simple, enduring thing but "nothing but a bundle or collection of different perceptions, which succeed each other with an inconceivable rapidity."

3. **Causation as Custom, Not Reason**: Descartes held that the connection between cause and effect can be known by the light of reason. Hume's most famous argument: we never perceive a "necessary connection" between cause and effect — only constant conjunction. Our belief in causation is a habit of the mind, not a rational insight. This undermines the Cartesian proof of [[god|God]] as the cause of our idea of the infinite.

4. **Skepticism About the External World**: Descartes attempted to prove the existence of the external world through the veracity of God. Hume concluded that our belief in an external world is the product of the imagination's "unreasoning" tendency to project constancy onto our perceptions.

## Key Ideas

**The Problem of Induction**: Hume's most enduring contribution. No inference from past experience to the future can be rationally justified without circularity — we assume nature is uniform because it has been uniform in the past, which is itself an inductive inference. This challenges the very project of Cartesian science, which sought necessary, deductive [[knowledge]] of nature.

**The Copy Principle**: All simple ideas are copies of prior impressions. This is the empiricist criterion of meaning — any term that cannot be traced to an impression is "without meaning." Cartesian innate ideas, substance, and the self all fail this test.

**Naturalism as an Alternative to Rationalism**: Hume did not conclude that we should abandon causal reasoning or belief in the external world — only that these are grounded in human nature, not in reason. The "gentle force" of custom is sufficient for life, if not for Cartesian certainty.

## In Descartes' Legacy

> Hume's skepticism about Cartesian reason is itself the most powerful argument for the limits of rationalist metaphysics. By demonstrating that the [[mind]] is governed by association and habit rather than the natural light of reason, Hume forced subsequent philosophers — most notably [[kant|Kant]] — to reconstruct the relationship between thought and [[experience]] on entirely new foundations.
