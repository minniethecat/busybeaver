# Error

In Descartes' philosophy, **error** (*error*) is not a positive creation of God but a **privation** — an absence of knowledge where knowledge ought to be (089-001). Descartes' analysis of error, developed in the Fourth Meditation, is a crucial piece of his theodicy: it explains how human fallibility is compatible with divine perfection, and it prescribes a methodological remedy.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes confronts the problem of error directly: if God is supremely perfect and not a deceiver, why do we make mistakes? His solution is that error arises from the **disproportion** between two faculties: the intellect (which merely perceives ideas and is finite) and the will (which affirms or denies and is "infinite" in scope). Error occurs when the will "extends itself to things I do not understand" — affirming or denying beyond what the intellect clearly perceives (089-002).

Crucially, error is a privation, not a positive reality. Just as darkness is the absence of light, error is the absence of knowledge — specifically, the absence of the restraint that would prevent the will from judging beyond the intellect's clear perception. Since privations do not require a positive cause, God is not responsible for error. Responsibility lies entirely with the individual who misuses the perfect faculty of will.

In the *Discourse on the Method*, Descartes describes his own encounters with error: "I found myself involved in so many doubts and errors, that I was convinced I had advanced no farther in my attempt at learning, than the discovery at every turn of my own ignorance" (004-006). The method of doubt is the remedy: by withholding judgment whenever the intellect is not clear, one avoids error.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' analysis of error serves a **theodicy** — a justification of God's goodness in the face of human fallibility. If God could have created us incapable of error, why didn't He? Descartes' answer: error is not a created thing but a privation, so God is not its cause. Moreover, the will's infinite scope is a perfection — it makes us "in a certain manner like God" — and it is better to have this perfection and occasionally misuse it than to lack it.

The practical remedy is the **methodological rule**: "abstain from judging" whenever the intellect lacks clear and distinct perception. This rule transforms error from a theological problem into a methodological one. If we follow the method, we will never err — we will simply remain in a state of suspended judgment when certainty is unavailable.

Descartes also distinguishes intellectual error from moral error. Moral error involves the will's choice of a lesser good over a greater, and it arises from the passions rather than the intellect. The *Passions of the Soul* develops this account of moral error, but it is less central to Descartes' epistemology.

## Historical Context

The problem of error was central to the **Augustinian** tradition. **Augustine** located the origin of error in original sin — the fall of Adam corrupted human nature, and error (like death and suffering) is a consequence of that corruption. Descartes' account is notably different: error is not the result of a corrupted nature but of a structural feature of the human mind (the intellect-will disproportion).

This shift from theological to structural explanation is characteristic of Descartes' modernizing impulse. He does not deny original sin, but his explanation of error makes no use of it. The cause of error is in the mind's architecture, not its history.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Spinoza** rejected the faculty psychology underlying Descartes' account. For Spinoza, will and intellect are not distinct faculties — every idea involves an affirmation, and the will is simply the conatus (striving) inherent in every idea. Error is not a failure of restraint but the presence of inadequate ideas.

**Malebranche**, Descartes' most important French follower, developed a different account: error arises not from the will's excess but from the senses and imagination, which present confused perceptions that we mistake for clear ideas. Malebranche's occasionalism — the doctrine that God is the only true cause — made the will's activity itself a form of error.
