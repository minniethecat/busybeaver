# Scepticism

**Scepticism** (or Skepticism) is the ancient philosophical tradition that advocated **suspension of judgment** (*epoché*) about all matters beyond immediate appearance, aiming at tranquillity (*ataraxia*). Descartes' methodical doubt is deeply indebted to the sceptical tradition — but he transforms scepticism from a destination into a method.

## Core Doctrine

Ancient scepticism took two main forms. **Pyrrhonian scepticism** (named after **Pyrrho of Elis**, c. 360–270 BCE) advocated suspension of judgment on all matters, including the claim that nothing can be known. **Academic scepticism** (the later Platonic Academy under **Arcesilaus** and **Carneades**) held the weaker view that no knowledge is possible, but that some beliefs are more probable than others.

The sceptics developed a battery of arguments — the **modes of suspension** (*tropoi*) — to show that for any claim, an equally compelling counter-claim can be produced. The senses conflict with each other and with reason; customs and laws vary across cultures; appearances differ with distance, perspective, and the state of the perceiver. The only rational response is to suspend judgment entirely.

## Key Figures

- **Pyrrho of Elis** — founder of Pyrrhonian scepticism, advocated total suspension of judgment
- **Sextus Empiricus** — (c. 160–210 CE) author of *Outlines of Pyrrhonism*, the principal surviving sceptical text
- **Arcesilaus** — head of the Platonic Academy who turned it toward scepticism
- **Carneades** — developed Academic probabilism: we cannot know, but some beliefs are more plausible

## Descartes' Engagement

Descartes' relationship to scepticism is complex and transformative. He does not name the ancient sceptics directly in the surviving English corpus but his methodical doubt is clearly modelled on sceptical argumentation. The three waves of doubt in the First Meditation — sensory unreliability, the dream argument, the evil demon hypothesis — are sceptical arguments of increasing radicality.

However, Descartes does not endorse scepticism. His doubt is **methodical**, not **sceptical**: it is a tool for discovering certainty, not a permanent stance. The *cogito* breaks through scepticism: "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body... but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The very act of doubting proves the doubter's existence.

Descartes transforms the sceptical challenge into a **foundationalist** program. Where the sceptics concluded that nothing can be known, Descartes concludes that everything the sceptics doubted can be rebuilt on the foundation of clear and distinct perception, guaranteed by God's veracity.

## Descartes' Criticisms

> I thought that I ought to do precisely the contrary [of the sceptics], and to reject as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt, in order to see if there did not remain, after this, something in my belief that was wholly indubitable.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (004-006)

## Historical Influence

Descartes' engagement with scepticism transformed both traditions. The sceptical challenge became a permanent feature of epistemology: every theory of knowledge must now explain how knowledge is possible in the face of the sceptical arguments Descartes deployed. The rediscovery of Sextus Empiricus in the sixteenth century and Descartes' response to it set the agenda for modern epistemology from **Locke** through **Kant** to the present.
