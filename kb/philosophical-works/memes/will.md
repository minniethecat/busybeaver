# Will

In Descartes' philosophy, the **will** (*voluntas*) is the faculty of affirming or denying — of giving or withholding **assent** to what the intellect presents. The will is "infinite" in its scope and constitutes the essence of human freedom (093-002). Descartes' analysis of the will is central to his theodicy: error arises not from God but from the misuse of a perfect faculty (089-002).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes develops his theory of the will most fully in the Fourth Meditation. Having established the existence of God and the criterion of clear and distinct perception, he confronts the problem of error: if God is perfect and not a deceiver, why do we so often err?

His answer is that error arises from the **disproportion** between intellect and will. The intellect is finite — it perceives only a limited range of ideas clearly and distinctly. The will, however, is infinite — "I experience it to be limited by no bounds whatever" — and can affirm or deny anything the intellect presents, including obscure and confused ideas. "Whence, then, spring my errors? From this cause alone, that the will being much more ample and extended than the understanding, I do not restrain it within the same limits, but extend it also to things I do not understand."

The will is the seat of human freedom. Descartes argues that "the will consists alone in our being unable to do or not to do a thing (that is, to affirm or deny, to pursue or shun it), or rather in this alone, that we are carried toward what the intellect proposes as to be affirmed, or denied, sought or shunned, in such a way that we feel we are not determined by any external force." This is the highest perfection in human nature — it makes us "in a certain manner like God."

## Arguments and Implications

The will's infinitude is crucial to Descartes' **theodicy**. God cannot be blamed for error because error is a privation — a misuse of a perfect faculty — not a positive creation. The will itself is perfect (it is God's greatest gift to us); error occurs only when we fail to restrain it within the bounds of clear and distinct perception. The remedy is methodological: "abstain" from judging when the intellect is not clear.

This analysis also grounds Descartes' account of **freedom**. True freedom is not indifference (the ability to choose arbitrarily) but **spontaneity** — the will's self-determination in accordance with clear and distinct perception. When the intellect perceives a truth clearly, the will irresistibly assents — yet this is the highest freedom, because the will is "carried toward" the truth by its own nature, not coerced by anything external.

Descartes further connects the will to the *cogito*: "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body... but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The act of doubting — which is an act of will (withholding assent) — proves the self's existence.

## Historical Context

The problem of free will and God's foreknowledge was a central topic in medieval theology. **Augustine** had argued that God's foreknowledge is compatible with human free will because God's knowledge is outside time. **Boethius** developed this into the doctrine of divine eternity: God sees all times in a single eternal present.

Descartes' innovation is to frame the problem epistemologically rather than theologically. Error is explained not by reference to original sin (Augustine) or the influence of the body (Platonism) but by the structural relationship between two mental faculties — intellect and will. This "intellectualist" account of error anticipates **Spinoza's** more radical determinism.

## Criticisms and Responses

Descartes' claim that the will is infinite has puzzled commentators. In what sense is a finite creature's will infinite? The answer seems to be: in scope. The will can affirm or deny anything — even what it does not understand — without limit. But this "infinity" is purely negative (absence of external constraint) rather than positive (actual power to accomplish what one wills).

**Spinoza** rejected Descartes' account of free will entirely, arguing that the will is not a distinct faculty but simply the affirmation or negation inherent in every idea. For Spinoza, all things follow necessarily from God's nature, and the experience of free will is an illusion born of ignorance of causes.
