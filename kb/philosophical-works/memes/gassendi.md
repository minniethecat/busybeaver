## Biography

Pierre Gassendi (1592–1655) was born in Champtercier, Provence. Ordained a priest in 1616, he pursued a career that combined ecclesiastical duties with intense philosophical and scientific activity. He was appointed professor of philosophy at Aix-en-Provence (1617–1622), then provost of the cathedral of Digne, and finally professor of mathematics at the Collège Royal in Paris (1645).

Gassendi was a central figure in the European republic of letters. He corresponded with [[galileo]], Hobbes, and Mersenne, and was closely associated with the circle of libertins érudits (learned freethinkers) in Paris. His philosophical project — the revival of Epicurean philosophy in a form compatible with Christian theology — was both audacious and influential, shaping the development of early modern empiricism and mechanical philosophy. He died in Paris in 1655.

## Key Philosophical Ideas

### Christianized Epicureanism

Gassendi's major philosophical achievement was the reconstruction of Epicurean philosophy for a Christian audience. He argued, against centuries of polemic, that [[epicurus]]' philosophy, properly understood, was not atheistic or hedonistic in the vulgar sense. Gassendi retained the Epicurean [[atomism]] — the theory that the natural world consists of atoms moving in void space — while rejecting Epicurus' denial of divine providence and the mortality of the soul. Atoms, for Gassendi, were created by [[god]] and set in motion according to divinely ordained laws, making atomism compatible with Christian creation theology.

This Christianized atomism provided a powerful alternative to Aristotelian natural philosophy and to the more radically rationalist Cartesian physics. It directly influenced [[locke]], [[newton]], and the development of British empiricism.

### Empiricist Epistemology

Gassendi defended an empiricist theory of knowledge against the Cartesian appeal to innate ideas and pure intellectual intuition. In his _Fifth Objections_ to the _Meditations_, he challenged [[rene-descartes]]' claim that the mind can know itself as a thinking substance independently of the body and the senses. Gassendi insisted that all our ideas derive ultimately from sensory experience, and that the Cartesian cogito establishes only that thinking occurs, not that there exists a substantial "I" distinct from the body.

This critique anticipated the empiricism of [[locke]] and [[hume]]. Hume's analysis of the self as a bundle of perceptions rather than a substantial soul echoes Gassendi's objection that Descartes had illegitimately inferred a substantial mind from the mere fact of thinking.

### The Theory of Space and Time

Gassendi developed an absolute theory of space and time as infinite, unchanging frameworks existing independently of the objects and events within them. This conception, elaborated in the _Syntagma Philosophicum_ (published posthumously in 1658), anticipated [[newton]]'s absolutist conception of space and time and provided a metaphysical framework for the new mathematical physics. Descartes, by contrast, identified matter with extension, denying the existence of void space — a position Gassendi's atomistic physics directly challenged.

## Relationship to Descartes

Gassendi was the author of the _Fifth Objections_ to the _[[meditations-on-first-philosophy]]_ — one of the most penetrating and thorough critiques Descartes received. Gassendi challenged nearly every step of the Cartesian argument: the inference from "I think" to "I am a thinking substance"; the criterion of clear and distinct ideas; the ontological argument for God's existence; the real distinction between mind and body; and the theory of innate ideas.

Descartes responded dismissively, addressing Gassendi with the mocking epithet "O Flesh" (Caro — a pun on the name Gassendi, which Descartes Latinized as Gassendus and then associated with the flesh/caro that philosophers should transcend). Despite Descartes' contempt, Gassendi's objections proved more enduring than many of Descartes' replies. The empiricist tradition that Gassendi helped launch would become the dominant epistemological framework of the eighteenth century.
