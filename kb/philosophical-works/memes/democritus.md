# Democritus

**Democritus** (c. 460–370 BCE) was the principal ancient exponent of **atomism**. Descartes engages with Democritus directly in the *Principles of Philosophy*, acknowledging the surface similarity between atomism and mechanism while carefully distinguishing his own physics.

## Biography

Democritus was born in Abdera (Thrace) and travelled extensively. His writings survive only in fragments. His atomism was developed in response to Parmenides' denial of change and was later adopted by Epicurus and Lucretius.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes addresses the comparison directly: "It may be said that Democritus also supposed certain corpuscles that were of various figures, sizes, and motions, from the heaping together and mutual concourse of which all sensible bodies arose" (060-002). He acknowledges the parallel but insists on two crucial differences.

First, Democritus assumed **indivisible atoms**; Descartes holds that matter (extension) is infinitely divisible. Second, Democritus assumed the **void**; Descartes, identifying matter with extension, denies that empty space is possible: "it is not more contradictory to conceive a mountain without a valley than such a cavity without the extension it contains" (031-002). Descartes also insists his mechanism is grounded in God's immutability, not random atomic motion.

## Key Ideas

**Atomism**: Reality consists of indivisible particles in empty space. All perceptible qualities are byproducts of atomic arrangements. This reduction anticipates Descartes' mechanism, though Descartes grounds primary qualities in extension rather than solidity.

**The Void**: Democritus' most important difference from Descartes. For Descartes, space is identical with extended substance; the void is impossible.

## In Descartes' Texts

> It may be said that Democritus also supposed certain corpuscles that were of various figures, sizes, and motions, from the heaping together and mutual concourse of which all sensible bodies arose; and, nevertheless, his mode of philosophizing is commonly rejected by all.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part IV (060-002)
