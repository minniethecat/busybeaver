# Blaise Pascal

**Blaise Pascal** (1623–1662) was the thinker who most powerfully challenged Descartes' project of grounding [[certainty]] — and religious faith — in human [[reason]]. A mathematical prodigy, Pascal invented the mechanical calculator (the Pascaline) at age 19, founded [[probability]] theory (with [[fermat]]), and made fundamental contributions to hydrostatics and the theory of conic sections. Yet his *Pensées* — fragments toward a defense of Christianity, published posthumously — contains the most devastating critique of Cartesian rationalism ever written: "Descartes useless and uncertain." For Pascal, the God of the philosophers — Descartes' God, demonstrated by clear and distinct [[idea|ideas]] — is not the living God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

## Biography

Pascal was born in Clermont-Ferrand, the son of a tax judge and amateur mathematician. His mother died when he was three; his father, Étienne Pascal, educated him at home, initially withholding mathematics for fear it would distract him. The boy responded by independently deriving the first 32 propositions of Euclid. The family moved to Paris in 1631, where Pascal was introduced to [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne's]] circle and, at age 16, presented a paper on conic sections that Descartes initially refused to believe was the work of a teenager.

In 1646, Pascal's family encountered Jansenism — the austere Augustinian movement centered at **Port-Royal** — and Pascal underwent a "first conversion." A second, mystical conversion on the night of November 23, 1654 (recorded in his "Memorial," sewn into his coat and discovered after his death) transformed him from a philosopher-scientist into a religious apologist. The *Lettres Provinciales* (1656–57), his satirical defense of Jansenism against Jesuit attacks, is a masterpiece of French prose — and prompted [[antoine-arnauld|Arnauld's]] expulsion from the Sorbonne. Pascal died at 39, leaving the *Pensées* unfinished.

## Relationship to Descartes

Pascal and Descartes met at least twice (in 1647, at Port-Royal), and their relationship was one of mutual respect mixed with fundamental disagreement:

**On the Void**: Descartes denied the possibility of a vacuum — extension is the essence of [[body]], and where there is extension, there is body. Pascal's experiments with the barometer and the Puy-de-Dôme (proving that atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude) demonstrated the reality of the vacuum to the satisfaction of the scientific community. Descartes visited Pascal during his illness in 1647 and, according to Pascal's sister, they argued about the vacuum.

**On Reason and Faith**: This is the fundamental disagreement. Descartes believed that the existence of [[god|God]] and the immortality of the [[soul]] could be demonstrated by natural reason — this was the promise of the *Meditations*. Pascal held that the God of pure reason is an idol: "The God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — not of the philosophers and scholars." Faith is a wager, a leap, a gift of grace — not a conclusion of [[geometry-descartes|geometry]].

**On the Self**: Descartes found the [[mind|self]] in the act of thinking — the *cogito*. Pascal found the self dispersed, hidden, insaisissable: "The self is hateful" (*le moi est haïssable*). Where Descartes built a philosophy on self-certainty, Pascal saw self-deception and the flight from our own mortality — *divertissement* (distraction) as the fundamental human condition.

## Key Ideas

**Pascal's Wager**: The most famous argument in the *Pensées*: if God exists and you believe, you gain infinite happiness; if God does not exist and you believe, you lose little. Therefore it is rational to believe. This probabilistic argument is rigorously Cartesian in form — yet its conclusion points beyond Cartesian reason toward lived faith.

**The Orders of Knowledge**: Pascal distinguished three orders — the body (material power, the order of kings), the mind (intellectual genius, the order of Descartes and scientists), and charity (the order of saints, holiness). The third infinitely transcends the first two. Descartes' entire project operates in the second order — and Pascal insists it is not the highest.

**The Heart Has Its Reasons**: "The heart has its reasons which reason does not know." This is Pascal's central epistemological claim against Cartesianism: there are truths — about God, about love, about value — that are known not by deductive reasoning but by the immediate intuition of the heart. Descartes' dream of a purely rational foundation for all knowledge is, from Pascal's perspective, a philosophical blindness to the most important dimensions of human existence.
