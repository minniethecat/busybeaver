Axioms, also called common notions or eternal truths, are self-evident propositions that serve as the foundation of all demonstrative knowledge in Descartes' philosophical system. Descartes distinguishes two related but distinct concepts: axioms in the strictly logical sense (propositions that cannot be denied without contradiction) and common notions as innate ideas implanted by God in the human mind that ground our capacity for certain knowledge.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' most sustained treatment of axioms appears in the Appendix to the Second Objections, where he presents his metaphysical arguments arranged in geometrical order. There he lists ten axioms, including: "Nothing exists of which it cannot be asked what is the cause of its existing" (Axiom I, 153-027), "The objective reality of our ideas requires a cause in which this same reality is contained" (Axiom V, 153-043), and "There are various degrees of reality or entity" (Axiom VI). These axioms function as premises in the deductive proofs of God's existence and the distinction of mind and body.

In the Principles of Philosophy, Descartes explains that axioms are not arbitrary propositions but have a specific metaphysical status. When we apprehend that "it is impossible a thing can arise from nothing," this proposition, "ex nihilo nihil fit, is not considered as somewhat existing, or as the mode of a thing, but as an eternal truth having its seat in our mind, and is called a common notion or axiom" (107-002). Axioms are thus eternal truths — they do not refer to any existing thing but are the fundamental principles according to which all reasoning must proceed.

Descartes instructs his readers to "diligently examine such propositions as are self-evident, which they will find within themselves, as the following: — That the same thing cannot at once be and not be; that nothing cannot be the efficient cause of anything, and the like" (153-017). This introspective verification is important: while axioms cannot be proved (they are the starting points of proof), their self-evidence can and must be recognized by anyone who attends to them carefully.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' use of axioms embodies his conviction that metaphysics can achieve the same certainty as geometry. By beginning with self-evident axioms, proceeding through rigorous deduction, and concluding with demonstrated theorems, Descartes aims to make philosophical argument as compelling as mathematical proof.

The claim that axioms are "eternal truths having their seat in our mind" (107-002) has deep implications for Descartes' theory of innate ideas. Axioms are not learned from experience — no empirical observation could establish the necessity of "ex nihilo nihil fit." Instead, they are part of the native endowment of the human mind, placed there by God. Recognizing an axiom is a matter of intellectual intuition, not sensory perception.

Descartes' axiomatic method also reveals his rationalism. Since all knowledge worthy of the name must be deduced from self-evident first principles, the empirical sciences must be reconstructed on a deductive foundation. Descartes' physics — deriving laws of nature from the axioms of extension and motion — is the practical expression of this commitment.

The function of axioms in Descartes' causal arguments for God's existence is particularly revealing. Axioms I through X in the Geometrical Appendix form the logical scaffolding for the proofs: "Necessary existence is contained in the nature or in the concept of God (by Axiom X)" (153-037). The proofs' validity depends entirely on the self-evidence of these axioms.

## Historical Context

The concept of axioms (axiomata) originates with Aristotle's Posterior Analytics, where they are the indemonstrable first principles from which all demonstration proceeds. Euclid's Elements gave this concept its canonical expression in geometry. Descartes inherits both traditions but transforms them by grounding axioms not in the nature of being (as Aristotle did) but in the nature of the human mind as created by God.

Descartes was also influenced by the scholastic concept of "common notions" (notiones communes), propositions so fundamental that they are presupposed by all reasoning. But whereas the scholastics typically grounded common notions in the nature of being, Descartes grounds them in the nature of mind — a characteristically modern, epistemological turn.

## Criticisms and Responses

A persistent criticism concerns Descartes' argument that God's existence can be proven from axioms. Leibniz objected that Descartes' axioms were not sufficiently analyzed — that the ontological argument, for instance, presupposes that the concept of a supremely perfect being is coherent, which Descartes merely assumes. Kant would later radicalize this criticism, arguing that "existence is not a predicate" and that no existential claim can be deduced from concepts alone.

A related criticism concerns the status of the axioms themselves. If axioms are eternal truths implanted by God, then their certainty depends on God's existence and veracity. But the proof of God's existence depends on these same axioms. Descartes' response, through the distinction between what is clearly perceived at the moment of attention and what is remembered, attempts to break this circle but has not satisfied all critics.
