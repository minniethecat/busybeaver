# Philosophy

Descartes reconceived **philosophy** as a unified system of certain knowledge grounded in indubitable first principles. In the famous tree-of-knowledge metaphor from the *Principles of Philosophy* Preface, "the whole of philosophy is like a tree: the roots are metaphysics, the trunk is physics, and the branches that come out of the trunk are all the other sciences — medicine, mechanics, and morals" (153-014). This image captures Descartes' ambition to replace the fragmentary and disputatious philosophy of the schools with an integrated body of demonstrated truths.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' rejection of traditional philosophy was sweeping. In the *Discourse on the Method* Part I, he recounts: "I was especially delighted with the mathematics, on account of the certitude and evidence of their reasonings... On the other hand, I compared the disquisitions of the ancient moralists to very towering and magnificent palaces with no better foundation than sand and mud" (004-004). Philosophy, as taught in the schools, produced only probable opinions and endless disputation.

Descartes' remedy was to rebuild philosophy on a foundation of certainty. "I thought it necessary first of all to endeavor to establish its principles" (004-014). The result was a complete reconstruction: from the *cogito* as first principle, through proofs of God's existence, to a physics grounded in extension and divine immutability.

The goal of philosophy, for Descartes, is not merely contemplative but practical: "it is possible to attain knowledge that is very useful in life... and thus render ourselves the masters and possessors of nature" (009-003). Philosophy serves human welfare through medicine, mechanics, and moral guidance.

## What Philosophy Is

In the letter-preface to the French edition of the *Principles*, Descartes offers his clearest definition: "the word 'philosophy' signifies the study of wisdom, and that by wisdom is to be understood not merely prudence in the management of affairs, but a perfect knowledge of all that man can know, as well for the conduct of his life as for the preservation of his health and the discovery of all the arts" (022-001). Philosophy, so understood, is the most comprehensive human endeavor — it "embraces all that the human mind can know" (022-002).

Descartes draws a striking analogy: "to live without philosophizing is in truth the same as keeping the eyes closed without attempting to open them; and the pleasure of seeing all that sight discloses is not to be compared with the satisfaction afforded by the discoveries of philosophy" (022-003). The pursuit of wisdom is not a luxury but constitutive of a fully human life.

## The Critique of Scholastic Philosophy

Descartes' critique of the scholastic tradition is both methodological and historical. He observes that "experience shows that they who make pretensions to philosophy are often less wise and reasonable than others who never applied themselves to the study" (022-001). The root problem, he argues, is that "they all laid down as a principle what they did not perfectly know" (022-007). The scholastics built elaborate systems on unexamined premises — and "just as in travelling, when we turn our back upon the place to which we were going, we recede the farther from it in proportion as we proceed in the new direction for a greater length of time" (022-008).

The "majority of those who in these later ages aspired to be philosophers, blindly followed Aristotle, so that they frequently corrupted the sense of his writings, and attributed to him various opinions which he would not recognise as his own were he now to return to the world" (022-006). Descartes' criticism is not directed at Aristotle himself but at the sterile Aristotelianism of the schools, which had substituted textual authority for rational inquiry.

## Philosophy as a Unified System

Descartes insists that all philosophical knowledge forms a single connected whole. Though he published separate treatises — the *Dioptrics*, the *Meteors*, the *Geometry*, and the *Principles* — he conceived them as parts of one continuous exposition. "In the Dioptrics, I designed to show that we might proceed far enough in philosophy as to arrive, by its means, at the knowledge of the arts that are useful to life" (022-015). In the *Meteors*, he "desired to exhibit the difference that subsists between the philosophy I cultivate and that taught in the schools" (022-016). Each work occupies a determinate place in the systematic unfolding of knowledge from first principles.

## The Moral Fruit of Philosophy

Descartes emphasizes that the ultimate value of philosophy is not speculative but practical: "as it is not from the roots or the trunks of trees that we gather the fruit, but only from the extremities of their branches, so the principal utility of philosophy depends on the separate uses of its parts, which we can only learn last of all" (022-014). Metaphysics and physics are necessary foundations, but medicine, mechanics, and ethics — the three branches — are where philosophy bears directly on human life. "The supreme good, considered by natural reason without the light of faith, is nothing more than the knowledge of truth through its first causes, in other words, the wisdom of which philosophy is the study" (022-004).
