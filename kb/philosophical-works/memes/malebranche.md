## Biography

Nicolas Malebranche (1638–1715) was born in Paris. Frail and malformed in childhood, he was educated at the Collège de la Marche and the Sorbonne before entering the Oratory — a congregation of priests devoted to scholarship and teaching — in 1660. His philosophical vocation was determined by a chance encounter with Descartes' _Treatise on Man_ in a bookshop in 1664; he reportedly experienced such excitement reading it that he was forced to pause to catch his breath.

Malebranche became the most important Cartesian philosopher of the generation after [[rene-descartes]]. His major work, _The Search After Truth_ (1674–1675), went through six editions in his lifetime and established him as a central figure in European philosophy. He engaged in notable controversies with [[antoine-arnauld]] (over theology and grace), with Leibniz (over dynamics and the laws of motion), and with the Jesuit _philosophes_ (over the proper interpretation of Descartes). He died in 1715 at the Oratory in Paris.

## Key Philosophical Ideas

### Occasionalism

Malebranche is best known for developing occasionalism — the doctrine that created substances have no genuine causal powers. When one billiard ball strikes another and the second moves, the impact is merely the "occasion" on which [[god]] exercises divine causal power to produce the motion in the second ball, in accordance with the general laws of nature. True causation belongs to God alone.

Malebranche drew this conclusion from two Cartesian premises pushed to their logical limit: (1) the distinction between extended substance (matter, which is passive) and thinking substance (mind, which is active only in thinking, not in causing motion); and (2) the doctrine of continuous creation, according to which God must conserve the world in being at every instant. If God's creative power is required at each moment, then created things cannot genuinely produce effects themselves.

This doctrine directly addresses the problem raised by Princess Elisabeth: how can an immaterial mind cause motion in an extended body? Malebranche's answer is that it cannot — and neither can bodies cause motions in each other. Only God is a true [[cause]].

### The Vision in God

Malebranche argued that we see all things in God — the "Vision in God." Since ideas cannot be produced by material objects (which have no causal power) or by the mind itself (which cannot generate the universal, necessary truths of mathematics and ethics), they must be seen in God, who contains the archetypes or "intelligible extension" of all created things. When we perceive a geometrical truth, we are not constructing it in our own minds but participating in God's own understanding of the intelligible world.

### Theodicy

In the _Treatise on Nature and Grace_ (1680), Malebranche developed a distinctive theodicy. God could have created a more perfect world, but only at the cost of employing more complex laws of nature. Since God acts by the simplest means (simplicity of ways being a divine attribute), the actual world — with all its apparent imperfections — achieves the optimal balance between the perfection of the effect and the simplicity of the divine means. This theodicy was developed in parallel with and in contrast to [[leibniz]]'s more famous argument that this is the best of all possible worlds.

## Relationship to Descartes

Malebranche both extended and transformed Cartesianism. His occasionalism radicalizes the Cartesian thesis that matter is passive extension by denying causal power even to bodies in collision. His theory of ideas — the Vision in God — departs from Descartes' innatism by locating eternal truths not in the human mind but in God's own intellect. And his theodicy introduces a dimension largely absent from Descartes' work: the problem of evil and divine justice.

Malebranche represents the metaphysical high point of the Cartesian tradition — the most systematic attempt to construct a complete philosophy on Cartesian foundations. His influence extended to [[berkeley]] (whose immaterialism can be read as occasionalism without the material world) and [[hume]] (whose analysis of causation echoes Malebranche's argument that we perceive only constant conjunction, not genuine causal power).
