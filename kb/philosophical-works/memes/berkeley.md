# George Berkeley

**George Berkeley** (1685–1753) was the philosopher who drew the most radical conclusion from Cartesian premises about [[idea|ideas]] and perception: if all we directly perceive are ideas, and ideas exist only in minds, then the material world — the world of extended [[substance]] independent of mind — is a gratuitous and incoherent hypothesis. Berkeley's **immaterialism** (or subjective idealism) eliminates [[body|matter]] entirely: to be is to be perceived (*esse est percipi*), and the only substances are minds and their ideas. His philosophy represents both a fulfillment of Cartesian representationalism and a *reductio ad absurdum* that later philosophers (including [[kant|Kant]]) would spend careers trying to escape.

## Biography

Berkeley was born in County Kilkenny, Ireland, and educated at Kilkenny College and Trinity College, Dublin, where he was elected a Fellow in 1707. His major philosophical works were all published before he turned 30: *An Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision* (1709), *A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge* (1710), and *Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous* (1713).

After a period in London (where he met [[swift]], Pope, and Addison) and two extended tours of Europe as tutor, Berkeley conceived an ambitious project: a college in Bermuda to educate both colonists and Native Americans. He spent three years in Rhode Island (1728–31) waiting for funding that never arrived. Returning to Ireland, he was appointed Bishop of Cloyne in 1734, where he spent his remaining years addressing the social problems of his diocese (his *Siris*, 1744, proposes tar-water as a universal medicine). He died in Oxford in 1753.

## Relationship to Descartes

Berkeley's immaterialism is a direct development — and critique — of Cartesian representationalism:

**The Way of Ideas**: Descartes held that the mind directly perceives only its own ideas — immaterial representations of things. The existence of an external, material world must be inferred (and Descartes attempted to prove it via God's veracity). Berkeley simply denied the inference. If ideas are all we perceive, and ideas are mind-dependent, then the notion of **mind-independent matter** is both unverifiable and incoherent.

**Against Abstract Ideas**: Descartes' clear and distinct perception of [[body]] as pure extension presupposes that we can abstract extension from all sensible qualities — color, texture, temperature — and conceive it alone. Berkeley denied that we can form such abstract ideas. We can conceive only particular, concrete ideas. The notion of "matter in general" or "extension in itself" is an illusion of language.

**God as the Cause of Ideas**: In Descartes' system, [[god|God]] guarantees the veracity of clear and distinct ideas about the external world. In Berkeley's system, God is the direct cause of all our ideas — there is no external world to mediate. The regular order of our ideas (what we call "nature") is the language in which God speaks to finite minds.

## Key Ideas

**Esse est Percipi**: The cornerstone of Berkeley's philosophy: the being of sensible objects is their being perceived. A table exists only insofar as it is perceived by some mind. When no finite mind perceives it, it subsists in the mind of God — the eternal perceiver.

**Elimination of Matter**: Berkeley argued that matter is not merely unknowable (as [[locke|Locke]] had suggested about substance) but strictly meaningless. The concept of an unperceived, unthinking substance "supporting" sensible qualities is an instance of philosophers' language running beyond experience.

**Continuity with [[malebranche]]**: Berkeley's system can be read as Malebranche's occasionalism without the material world. Malebranche held that bodies exist but cannot causally interact with minds — God is the sole causal agent. Berkeley held that "bodies" are simply collections of ideas in minds, and God causes them directly. The step from occasionalism to immaterialism is shorter than it appears.
