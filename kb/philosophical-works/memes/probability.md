In Descartes' epistemology, **probability** marks the boundary between genuine knowledge (*scientia*) and mere opinion (*opinio*). Throughout his methodological writings, Descartes insists that probable reasoning, however compelling, cannot serve as the foundation for certain knowledge. The concept is central to his critique of scholastic dialectic and to his distinction between theoretical and practical reasoning.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes draws a sharp distinction between probability and certainty in the *Discourse on the Method*. The first rule of his method requires that he "never accept anything as true" unless it presents itself with clarity and distinctness, deliberately excluding propositions that appear merely probable (006-003). This epistemic austerity is reinforced in Part II, where Descartes observes that upon examining the opinions of others, "they see how little probability there is in the reasons that have satisfied" even the most learned men (022-009). In Part IV of the *Discourse*, he extends this principle to metaphysics, arguing that even when "we may not perceive more probability in the one than in the other," we must suspend judgment rather than assent to the merely probable (028-002). The Cartesian rejection of probability as a standard of truth represents a decisive break with the scholastic and Renaissance traditions that treated probable reasoning as a legitimate mode of philosophical argument.

## Key Passages

> I formed a provisional code of morals, which consisted of but three or four maxims ... following opinions that I knew to be very uncertain, just as if they were indubitable ... in imitation of travelers who, when they have lost their way in a forest, ought not to wander turning now this way, now that ... but to walk as straight as they can in one direction.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part III (023-001)

> The first [rule] was never to accept anything as true that I did not plainly know to be such.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part II (006-003)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' rejection of probability as an epistemic standard has several important implications for his philosophical system. First, it drives his quest for absolute foundations: if probable knowledge is insufficient, then only indubitable first principles — the *cogito* and the existence of God — can ground the sciences. Second, it creates a sharp division between theoretical and practical reasoning. In the *Discourse*, Descartes famously adopts a "provisional morality" (*morale par provision*) that allows him to act on probable opinions in practical life while suspending judgment in theoretical inquiry. This dual standard — certainty for science, probability for conduct — becomes a hallmark of Cartesian thought and influences the later development of probabilistic reasoning in the 17th century, particularly in Pascal and the Port-Royal Logic.

## Historical Context

Before Descartes, the concept of probability (*probabilitas*) was central to scholastic and Renaissance dialectic, where arguments were evaluated according to their degree of probability rather than their certainty. The revival of ancient skepticism in the 16th century, particularly through the works of Montaigne, had already challenged the pretensions of probable reasoning. Descartes inherits this skeptical critique but transforms it: rather than resting content with probability (as the ancient skeptics recommended), he demands certainty as the only adequate ground for knowledge, thereby inaugurating the modern epistemological project.

## Criticisms and Responses

The Cartesian dismissal of probability has been criticized on several grounds. Pascal, in his *Pensées*, argued that Descartes' quest for certainty was both impractical and hubristic, since most of human life — including religious faith — operates in the domain of probability rather than demonstrative certainty. Later pragmatist philosophers, including Peirce and James, rejected the Cartesian demand for absolute foundations, arguing that fallible, probable knowledge is both sufficient and inescapable for finite inquirers. Within Descartes' own framework, however, the exclusion of probability from *scientia* is defended by the argument that genuine knowledge requires the impossibility of error — a standard that probable reasoning can never meet.
