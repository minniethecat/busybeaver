# Mind

In Descartes' philosophy, the **mind** (*mens*, *esprit*) is the thinking substance — *res cogitans* — whose essential nature is thought. As distinct from body (extended substance), the mind is unextended, indivisible, and known with greater certainty than any material object. Descartes establishes the mind as the foundation of his epistemology through the *cogito* argument and as one pole of his mind-body dualism.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes formally defines mind in the Appendix to the *Principles of Philosophy* as "the substance in which thought immediately resides" (153-009). He deliberately chooses *mens* over *anima* (soul), noting that the latter "is equivocal, being frequently applied to denote a corporeal object" (153-009). This terminological choice reflects Descartes' determination to separate thinking substance cleanly from anything corporeal.

The discovery of the mind as thinking substance occurs in Part IV of the *Discourse on the Method*, through the famous *cogito* argument. Descartes writes: "I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing" (007-002). The "I" — the mind — is "wholly distinct from the body, and is even more easily known than the latter" (007-002).

In the Synopsis to the *Meditations*, Descartes explains why the Second Meditation is so important: "the mind which, in the exercise of the freedom peculiar to itself, supposes that no object is, of the existence of which it has even the slightest doubt, finds that, meanwhile, it must itself exist" (013-002). This establishes that the mind knows itself before it knows any body — a foundational epistemological priority.

The mind's nature as unextended substance is further developed in the *Principles*. Descartes argues that the mind is indivisible: "we are not able to conceive the half of a mind, as we can of any body, however small" (013-002). This is a key argument for dualism — the natures of mind and body are "not only as diverse, but even in some measure as contraries" (013-002).

## Key Passages

> VI. The substance in which thought immediately resides is here called *mind* (*mens*, *esprit*). I here speak, however, of *mens* rather than of *anima*, for the latter is equivocal, being frequently applied to denote a corporeal object.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Appendix: Definitions (153-009)

> I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing; so that "I," that is to say, the mind by which I am what I am, is wholly distinct from the body, and is even more easily known than the latter.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-002)

> The absolute distinction of mind and body is, besides, confirmed in this Second Meditation, by showing that we cannot conceive body unless as divisible; while, on the other hand, mind cannot be conceived unless as indivisible.
>
> — Meditations, Synopsis (013-002)

## Arguments and Implications

The mind-body distinction is the foundation of Cartesian dualism. In the *Principles* Appendix, Descartes argues: "All that we clearly conceive can be made by God in the manner in which we conceive it. But we clearly conceive mind, that is, a substance which thinks, without body" (153-068). Since we can conceive each without the other, they are distinct substances capable of independent existence.

Descartes also asks readers to perform a cognitive exercise: "That they consider their own mind, and all those of its attributes of which they shall find they cannot doubt, though they may have supposed that all they ever received by the senses was entirely false, and that they do not leave off considering it until they have acquired the habit of conceiving it distinctly, and of believing that it is more easy to know than any corporeal object" (153-016). This is the methodical turn inward that defines Cartesian epistemology — the mind is better known than body.

The immortality argument depends on the mind being a pure substance, not a configuration of accidents. Descartes explains: "the human mind is not made up of accidents, but is a pure substance. For although all the accidents of the mind be changed — although, for example, it think certain things, will others, and perceive others, the mind itself does not vary with these changes" (013-002). Unlike the body, which changes identity when its parts change, the mind is "in its own nature immortal" (013-002).

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of mind as thinking substance represents a radical break with the Aristotelian-scholastic tradition. For **Aristotle**, the soul (*psyche*) was the form of a living body — inseparable from it, as the shape of wax is inseparable from the wax. The scholastics, following **Thomas Aquinas**, held that the rational soul could survive death but remained the "form of the body" during life.

Descartes rejects this hylomorphic framework entirely. The mind is not the form of the body; it is a separate substance defined solely by thought. This is a more radical dualism than anything in the ancient or medieval traditions. It was criticized by contemporaries including **Hobbes**, who argued that thinking is a material process, and **Gassendi**, who objected that Descartes had not proven the mind can exist without the body.

## Criticisms and Responses

The central criticism of Descartes' concept of mind is the **interaction problem**: if mind and body are wholly distinct substances with nothing in common, how can they causally interact? **Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia** pressed this objection in their correspondence (1643), asking how an unextended thinking substance could move an extended body. Descartes' reply — appealing to the pineal gland and the primitive notion of mind-body union — was widely regarded as unsatisfactory. **Spinoza** solved the problem by denying dualism entirely: thought and extension are attributes of a single substance. **Leibniz** proposed pre-established harmony. The modern philosophy of mind — from **Ryle's** "ghost in the machine" to contemporary debates about consciousness — continues to grapple with the legacy of Descartes' concept of mind.
