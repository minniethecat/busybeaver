# Reason

In Descartes' philosophy, **reason** (*ratio*, *bon sens*) is the natural human faculty of correct judgment — "the power of judging aright and of distinguishing truth from error" (004-001). Unlike the scholastics who distinguished multiple intellectual faculties, Descartes identifies reason as the single universal instrument of knowledge, equally distributed among all human beings.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes opens the *Discourse on the Method* by declaring that "good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed" (004-001). Reason, or good sense, is what distinguishes humans from animals: "as to the reason or sense, inasmuch as it is that alone which constitutes us men, and distinguishes us from the brutes, I am disposed to believe that it is to be found complete in each individual" (004-001).

The equality of reason does not guarantee equality of opinion. "The diversity of our opinions... does not arise from some being endowed with a larger share of reason than others, but solely from this, that we conduct our thoughts along different ways, and do not fix our attention on the same objects" (004-001). The problem is not the faculty itself but its *application*: "to be possessed of a vigorous mind is not enough; the prime requisite is rightly to apply it" (004-001).

This conviction drives Descartes' entire philosophical project. If reason is naturally perfect and universal, then error must result from faulty method, not faulty faculty. The solution is to develop a **method** that guides reason correctly — the famous four rules of the *Discourse* Part II.

## Reason and the Mathematical Model

Descartes' vision of reason is deeply shaped by his admiration for mathematics. He was "especially delighted with the mathematics, on account of the certitude and evidence of their reasonings" (004-004). This mathematical ideal — clear definitions, stepwise deduction, indubitable conclusions — became the template for all rational inquiry. "The long chains of simple and easy reasonings by means of which geometers are accustomed to reach the conclusions of their most difficult demonstrations, had led me to imagine that all things, to the knowledge of which man is competent, are mutually connected in the same way" (005-011). The universality of reason thus entails a unity of the sciences: all knowledge forms a single connected system, accessible to a properly guided intellect.

## Reason as Self-Direction

A recurring theme in the *Discourse* is Descartes' determination to "use my own reason in the conduct of my life" (005-006). After surveying the conflicting opinions of philosophers, the customs of different nations, and the limitations of book-learning, he concludes that no external authority can substitute for the autonomous exercise of one's own rational faculty. This is not intellectual arrogance but a methodological commitment: "the greatest advantage I derived from the study consisted in this, that, observing many things which, however extravagant and ridiculous to our apprehension, are yet by common consent received and approved by other great nations, I learned to entertain too decided a belief in regard to nothing of the truth of which I had been persuaded merely by example and custom" (004-009).

## Reason and Its Limits

Despite his confidence in reason, Descartes acknowledges its proper limits. In his approach to theology, he writes: "being given assuredly to understand that the way is not less open to the most ignorant than to the most learned, and that the revealed truths which lead to heaven are above our comprehension, I did not presume to subject them to the impotency of my reason" (004-005). This demarcation — reason governs natural knowledge, faith governs revealed truth — is characteristic of Descartes' careful navigation of theological constraints while radically expanding the scope of rational inquiry in philosophy and science.

## The Cultivation of Reason

Descartes emphasizes that reason must be cultivated through practice. He describes his method as a means of "exercising my reason in all matters, if not with absolute perfection, at least with the greatest attainable by me" and notes that "by its use my mind was becoming gradually habituated to clearer and more distinct conceptions of its objects" (005-014). Reason is not a static possession but a capacity that improves through disciplined exercise. The *Meditations* themselves are presented as a spiritual exercise in which the meditator progressively strengthens their rational capacities by repeatedly confronting the most fundamental questions.

## Historical Context

Descartes' conception of reason as a universal natural light breaks with the scholastic tradition. The medieval schools distinguished between the active intellect, passive intellect, and various internal senses — a complex psychology derived from Aristotle via Aquinas and Averroes. Descartes radically simplifies this picture: there is one faculty, reason, and it operates by intuition (grasping simple natures directly) and deduction (moving through chains of clear and distinct ideas).

This simplification has consequences. If reason is a single universal faculty, then any human being can discover truth by applying it correctly — no special training, authority, or revelation is required. Descartes' *Discourse* is written in French rather than Latin precisely to make it accessible to those "who use only their natural reason in its purity" (004-003).
