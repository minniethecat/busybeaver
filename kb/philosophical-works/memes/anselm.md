## Biography

Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) was born in Aosta, in what is now northern Italy. He entered the Benedictine monastery at Bec in Normandy in 1059, where he studied under Lanfranc and eventually became prior and then abbot. In 1093, he was appointed Archbishop of Canterbury, a position he held through periods of conflict with Kings William II and Henry I over the rights of the Church.

Anselm is often described as the father of Scholasticism — the tradition of philosophical theology that reached its zenith in [[aquinas]]. His works are characterized by the motto "faith seeking understanding" (fides quaerens intellectum): the conviction that rational argument can illuminate and defend Christian doctrine without supplanting faith. He was canonized in 1494 and declared a Doctor of the Church in 1720.

## Key Philosophical Ideas

### The Ontological Argument

Anselm's most celebrated and contested contribution to philosophy is the ontological argument for the existence of [[god]], presented in the _Proslogion_ (1077–1078). The argument proceeds from the very definition of God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" (id quo maius cogitari non potest). Anselm reasoned that if God exists only in the understanding, we can conceive of something greater — a God that exists both in the understanding and in reality. Therefore, the greatest conceivable being must exist in reality, not only in thought.

This argument became one of the most debated proofs in the history of philosophy. [[aquinas]] rejected it, but [[rene-descartes]] offered his own version in the Fifth Meditation, reasoning that existence belongs to God's essence as necessarily as the property of having three angles belongs to a triangle. [[kant]] famously criticized all versions of the argument on the grounds that existence is not a predicate. [[leibniz]] supplemented the Cartesian argument by first proving that God (the most perfect being) is possible.

### Satisfaction Theory of Atonement

In _Cur Deus Homo_ ("Why God Became Man"), Anselm developed the satisfaction theory of the atonement. He argued that sin constitutes an infinite offense against divine honor, requiring satisfaction proportionate to the offense — a debt humanity cannot pay. Only a being who is both fully divine (to offer infinite satisfaction) and fully human (to represent humanity) can make this satisfaction: hence the Incarnation. This juridical framework for understanding Christ's death became the dominant model in Western theology, though it was later challenged by [[aquinas]] and others.

### Divine Attributes and Perfect Being Theology

Anselm's method of reasoning from the concept of a perfect being to conclusions about God's attributes — simplicity, eternity, omnipresence, omnipotence, justice, and mercy — established the framework for "perfect being theology." Descartes adopted and extended this method in the _[[meditations-on-first-philosophy]]_, deriving the divine attributes from the idea of a supremely perfect being. For both Anselm and Descartes, the concept of God as the most perfect conceivable being is the starting point for rational theology.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes' debt to Anselm is most direct in the ontological argument. Descartes presents his version in the Fifth Meditation, though he never explicitly cites Anselm. The Cartesian argument shares the core Anselmian insight — that the concept of a supremely perfect being entails its existence — but Descartes frames it in terms of clear and distinct perception rather than Anselm's conceptual comparison between existence-in-the-understanding and existence-in-reality.

Beyond the ontological argument, Anselm's project of "faith seeking understanding" mirrors Descartes' own ambition in the _Meditations_: to provide rational foundations for belief in God and the soul. Both thinkers believed that reason, properly employed, leads to the truths of natural theology. Both were also criticized for allegedly offering arguments that presuppose what they seek to prove — a charge leveled against Anselm by his contemporary Gaunilo (the "perfect island" objection) and against Descartes by [[gassendi]], [[hobbes]], and later empiricists like [[hume]].
