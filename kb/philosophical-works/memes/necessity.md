# Necessity

In Descartes' philosophy, **necessity** applies primarily in three domains: the necessary existence of God, the necessity of eternal truths, and the necessity of logical deduction. Most distinctively, Descartes holds that all necessity — including logical and mathematical necessity — depends on God's free will, a doctrine known as the "creation of the eternal truths."

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes identifies necessary existence as a divine attribute. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he argues that "from this alone, that we cannot conceive God except as existing, it follows that existence is inseparable from him, and therefore that he really exists" (153-019). God's existence is not merely actual but necessary: "necessary existence is comprised in the idea of a being of the highest power" (153-038). This grounds a version of the ontological argument — unlike finite things, whose existence is contingent, God contains existence in his very essence.

Beyond God's necessary existence, Descartes makes the striking claim that even the eternal truths — the laws of logic, mathematics, and morality — are necessary only because God willed them to be so. In the *Principles*, he writes: "We now come to speak of eternal truths. When we apprehend that it is impossible that anything can proceed from nothing, the proposition *ex nihilo nihil fit* is not considered as a thing which really exists... but merely as an eternal truth which has its seat in our mind, and is called a common notion or axiom" (107-002). These truths are "eternal" in the sense that they are innate to the human mind and unchanging, but they are not independent of God — they depend on his creative will.

The creation of the eternal truths has a radical implication: God could have made it false that 2 + 2 = 4, or that a contradiction cannot be true. Necessity, in Descartes' system, is not an independent Platonic realm but a feature of created reality, as contingent on God's will as the existence of the physical universe. This doctrine sets Descartes apart from the mainstream rationalist tradition and has been a source of ongoing philosophical controversy.

In the order of human knowledge, necessity also characterizes valid deduction. Descartes' method requires that we "preserve in our thoughts the order necessary for the deduction of one truth from another" (005-011). Here necessity is epistemic: we perceive that a conclusion follows necessarily from its premises with the same clarity and distinctness as a mathematical demonstration. The necessity of deduction is what distinguishes *scientia* (certain knowledge) from mere opinion.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' doctrine of necessary existence underwrites his ontological argument. If God is the supremely perfect being, and existence is a perfection, then God must exist — just as the idea of a triangle necessarily includes the sum of its angles equaling two right angles. The necessity here is conceptual: we cannot conceive God without conceiving him as existing.

The creation of the eternal truths has far-reaching implications. It makes Descartes a theological voluntarist: God's will, not God's intellect, is primary. This contrasts sharply with **Aquinas**, for whom God's will follows his intellect — God wills the good because he knows it. For Descartes, the good is good because God wills it. This voluntarism extends to mathematics and logic, making Descartes a precursor to the radical divine omnipotence tradition that runs through **Ockham** to contemporary discussions of divine command ethics.

The epistemic necessity of deduction is the foundation of Cartesian science. If we begin with clear and distinct first principles and proceed by necessary steps, we build an edifice of knowledge as secure as geometry. This model of science — axiomatic, deductive, certain — shaped the early modern conception of what knowledge should be.

## Historical Context

Descartes' doctrine of the creation of the eternal truths emerged in a series of letters to **Mersenne** in 1630, before the *Discourse* or *Meditations* were composed. The intellectual background includes the medieval debate over divine omnipotence: could God make a stone so heavy he could not lift it? Could God make the past not have happened? Descartes pushes this tradition to its limit by extending divine power over the truths of reason themselves. **Leibniz** would later reject this doctrine forcefully, insisting that eternal truths are necessary in all possible worlds and independent of God's will — God's intellect, not his will, grounds necessity. **Spinoza** would take the opposite extreme, identifying God with Nature and making all things follow with geometric necessity from the divine nature.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Leibniz** argued that Descartes' creation of eternal truths makes necessity arbitrary and destroys the foundation of reason. If God could have made contradictions true, then no argument — not even Descartes' own proofs of God's existence — is absolutely reliable. **Arnauld** worried that the doctrine undermines the certainty of mathematics. Descartes' response, implicit in his letters, is that God's immutability guarantees the stability of eternal truths: once God has willed them, he does not change them, so we can rely on them. The doctrine's purpose is theological — to preserve divine transcendence by ensuring nothing, not even the laws of logic, is independent of God.
