# Order

In Descartes' philosophy, **order** (*ordo*) is a methodological and metaphysical principle. As a rule of method, it requires proceeding from simple to complex objects of knowledge. As a feature of his philosophical exposition, the "order of reasons" organizes his arguments so that each step follows necessarily from what precedes it. Order is thus both how we think correctly and how reality is structured.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes makes order foundational to his method. In the *Discourse on the Method*, the second rule of method is "to conduct my thoughts in such order that, by commencing with objects the simplest and easiest to know, I might ascend by little and little, and, as it were, step by step, to the knowledge of the more complex" (005-009). This rule appears deceptively simple, but it has profound implications: all knowledge must be organized as a deductive chain, where each link is clearly seen to follow from what precedes it. The *Meditations* are written precisely in this synthetic order — from the *cogito* as the first indubitable truth, through proofs of God's existence, to the existence of the external world.

Order also structures Descartes' exposition of his physics. The *Principles of Philosophy* is organized by an "order of reasons": "In the first part of Philosophy, the principles of knowledge... in the second, the principles of material things... in the third, the visible world... in the fourth, the Earth... in the fifth, the order of Physical questions" (002-001). This hierarchical ordering from metaphysics through physics to particular sciences embodies Descartes' conviction that knowledge must be built on secure foundations.

The requirement of order grounds Descartes' criticism of traditional philosophy. Scholastic disputations proceed by topics and authorities rather than by methodical deduction from first principles. Descartes' "order of reasons" replaces the scholastic *ordo disciplinae* with a rational, geometric order: "those who seek the right road to truth ought not to occupy themselves with any object about which they cannot have a certainty equal to that of the demonstrations of arithmetic and geometry" (005-011).

Order is also essential to the deduction of one truth from another. Descartes writes that we must "preserve in our thoughts the order necessary for the deduction of one truth from another" (005-011). Without ordered deduction, isolated truths — even if individually certain — would not constitute *science* in Descartes' sense. Science requires systematic unity, and order is the principle of that unity.

## Key Passages

> The second [rule of method], to conduct my thoughts in such order that, by commencing with objects the simplest and easiest to know, I might ascend by little and little, and, as it were, step by step, to the knowledge of the more complex; assigning in thought a certain order even to those objects which in their own nature do not stand in a relation of antecedence and sequence.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part II (005-009)

> To preserve in our thoughts the order necessary for the deduction of one truth from another, that they may be able to follow the whole series of my arguments.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part II (005-011)

## Arguments and Implications

Order has both epistemological and metaphysical significance. Epistemologically, it provides the structure of deduction — without order, there is no passage from premises to conclusions. Metaphysically, it grounds Descartes' hierarchical picture of reality: God is the supreme substance, created minds and bodies depend on God, and modes depend on substances. This ontological hierarchy is mirrored by the epistemological hierarchy of the order of reasons.

Descartes' insistence on order has a polemical edge: he explicitly rejects the disorderly accumulation of opinions that characterizes scholastic education. "As to the opinions to which I had been hitherto accustomed," he writes, "I could not do better than undertake to rid myself of them all at once" (005-007). The method of doubt is itself an ordered procedure — a systematic demolition prior to systematic reconstruction.

The geometrization of order is perhaps Descartes' most lasting contribution. By modeling philosophical reasoning on mathematical demonstration, he inaugurated the rationalist tradition's quest for a *mos geometricus* — a geometric method — that would culminate in **Spinoza**'s *Ethics*, written *ordine geometrico*.

## Historical Context

Descartes' emphasis on order echoes the Renaissance revival of the Euclidean *Elements*, widely studied in Jesuit colleges like La Flèche where Descartes was educated. The geometric method — axioms, definitions, postulates, theorems — provided the model for ordered knowledge. Descartes adapted this model from mathematics to metaphysics in a way his predecessors had not. The "order of reasons" also owes something to the Stoic concept of *ordo* as the rational structure of the cosmos, though Descartes' order is primarily epistemic rather than cosmic. Later, **Leibniz** would generalize Descartes' demand for order into the Principle of Sufficient Reason — nothing happens without a reason why it is thus and not otherwise — which requires that even contingent truths be embedded in a rational order.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Vico** and the rhetorical tradition objected that Descartes' geometric order is inappropriate for human affairs — history, law, and politics cannot be organized *more geometrico*. **Pascal** distinguished the *esprit de géométrie* (geometrical mind) from the *esprit de finesse* (intuitive mind), implying that Descartes' exclusive focus on geometric order misses a whole dimension of human knowing. **Giambattista Vico** argued that the Cartesian method, by privileging clear and distinct ideas, makes us strangers to the historical and cultural world we have made. Descartes would likely respond that these critics conflate the order of exposition with the order of discovery: his method is for arriving at certain knowledge, not for navigating every domain of human life — hence his provisional moral code in the *Discourse*, which operates without geometric certainty.
