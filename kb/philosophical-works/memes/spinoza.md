# Baruch Spinoza

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677) was the most radical and systematic of Descartes' philosophical successors. He adopted Cartesian [[method]], the geometric mode of demonstration, and the substance-mode ontology, but transformed them into a radically monistic system: there is only one [[substance]] — [[god|God]] or Nature (*Deus sive Natura*) — of which [[mind]] and [[body]] are merely two attributes. Spinoza's *Ethics* (1677) represents both the fulfillment and the subversion of Cartesian rationalism.

## Biography

Spinoza was born in Amsterdam to a family of Portuguese-Jewish merchants who had fled the Inquisition. He received a traditional Jewish education but was drawn to the new philosophy and science. In **1656**, at age 23, he was excommunicated (*cherem*) by the Amsterdam synagogue for his "abominable heresies." He spent the rest of his life as a lens-grinder, living modestly in Rijnsburg, Voorburg, and finally The Hague, where he died of a lung ailment (likely aggravated by glass dust) at age 44.

Spinoza corresponded with leading intellectuals including **[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** and **Henry Oldenburg** (secretary of the Royal Society). He declined a professorship at Heidelberg in 1673, preferring to preserve his "philosophic quiet." His major work, the *Ethics*, was published posthumously in 1677.

## Relationship to Descartes

Spinoza's philosophical starting point is explicitly Cartesian. His first published work was *Principles of Cartesian Philosophy* (1663), a geometric exposition of Descartes' *Principles*. He adopted Descartes' ideal of a deductive science modeled on geometry, the method of clear and distinct [[idea|ideas]], and the substance-mode categorical framework.

However, Spinoza radicalized Cartesianism in ways that Descartes would have rejected:

1. **From Dualism to Monism**: Descartes posited two created substances ([[mind]] and [[body]]) plus the divine substance. Spinoza argued that there can be only one substance, since substance is "that which is in itself and conceived through itself." God alone satisfies this definition. Mind and body are not substances but *attributes* of the one substance.

2. **From Interaction to Parallelism**: Descartes struggled to explain how mind and body interact. Spinoza eliminated the problem: mind and body are two aspects of the same reality. "The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things" (*Ethics* II, Prop. 7).

3. **From Voluntarism to Necessitarianism**: Descartes held that God freely created the eternal truths. Spinoza held that everything follows necessarily from the divine nature — God could not have created a different world.

## Key Ideas

**Substance Monism**: There is exactly one substance — God (or Nature). Everything that exists is in God. Individual things are *modes* of the divine substance, not independent entities. This is Spinoza's most radical departure from Cartesianism.

**The Conatus Principle**: "Each thing, in so far as it is in itself, strives to persevere in its being" (*Ethics* III, Prop. 6). This *conatus* is the foundation of Spinoza's ethics and psychology — all human actions are expressions of the drive for self-preservation.

**Intellectual Love of God**: The highest form of [[knowledge]] is the intellectual love of God (*amor Dei intellectualis*) — the intuitive understanding of oneself and all things as necessary consequences of the divine nature. This is Spinoza's answer to Descartes' quest for certain knowledge: it culminates not in a proof but in a form of blessedness.

## Criticisms of Cartesianism

Spinoza's system implicitly critiques Descartes at several key points:

- **The Problem of Interaction**: Descartes' account of mind-body interaction through the pineal gland is philosophically unsatisfying. Spinoza's attribute theory dissolves the problem entirely.
- **The Status of Finite Substances**: If substance is "that which needs nothing else in order to exist" (Descartes' definition), then strictly only God qualifies. Descartes' created substances are, by this definition, not substances at all.
- **Divine Freedom**: Descartes' voluntarism about eternal truths makes God's nature arbitrary. Spinoza's God acts from the necessity of its own nature — a more perfect form of freedom, not a lesser one.
