## Definition

Consciousness (*conscientia*) in Descartes' philosophy denotes the mind's immediate, indubitable awareness of its own thoughts (012-003). It is not a separate faculty but the intrinsic character of all thinking: every act of thinking — doubting, understanding, affirming, willing, sensing — carries with it the consciousness that one is thinking. This immediate self-awareness forms the epistemic foundation of the cogito and the entire Cartesian system.

## Role in the Cogito

Descartes' famous argument "I think, therefore I am" depends on consciousness. The certainty of one's own existence is not inferred from prior premises but directly experienced in the act of thinking itself. In the *Meditations*, Descartes shows that even while doubting everything — the existence of the external world, the reliability of the senses, the truths of mathematics — the very act of doubting reveals consciousness of thinking as an indubitable fact (012-003).

> Descartes writes that from "the consciousness that nothing besides thinking belongs to the essence of the mind," we can demonstrate the real distinction between mind and body (012-003).

The cogito is therefore not a syllogism but an immediate intuition grounded in consciousness.

## Consciousness and Sensation

Descartes distinguishes the physiological process of sensation from the conscious experience of sensing. When we see or walk, there is a bodily mechanism, but the sensation itself — "the consciousness of seeing or walking" — is a mode of thought (067-002). This distinction is crucial: even if the external objects causing sensation do not exist, the consciousness of sensing remains indubitable as a mental act.

## Consciousness of Freedom

In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes appeals to consciousness as evidence for human freedom: "we have such consciousness of the liberty and indifference which exists in us" that nothing compels us more clearly than our awareness of free will (099-002). This internal consciousness of freedom provides a direct, experiential basis for the doctrine of free will, independent of theological arguments.
