# Idea

In Descartes' philosophy, an **idea** (*idea*) is "that form of any thought, by the immediate perception of which I am conscious of that same thought" (153-005). Ideas are the fundamental units of mental content — the medium through which the mind represents objects to itself. Descartes' theory of ideas provides the foundation for his epistemology, his arguments for God's existence, and his theory of error.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes defines idea with precision in the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix: "By the word 'idea' I understand that form of any thought, by the immediate perception of which I am conscious of that same thought; so that I can express nothing in words, when I understand what I say, without making it certain, by this alone, that I possess the idea of the thing that is signified by these words" (153-005). He clarifies that he does not restrict "idea" to images in the corporeal imagination — ideas in the strict sense belong to the intellect alone.

Descartes classifies ideas into three kinds by their origin. **Innate ideas** arise from the mind's own nature — the ideas of thought, substance, God, and the simple natures. **Adventitious ideas** seem to come from external objects through the senses — the idea of the sun, of heat, of colour. **Factitious ideas** are constructed by the mind itself — the idea of a chimera, a hippogriff, or a perfect triangle.

In the Third Meditation, Descartes develops a theory of **objective reality**: ideas have formal reality as modes of thought (they are real mental acts), and objective reality in virtue of what they represent. "There is more objective reality in the idea of substance than in that of accident, and in the idea of infinite than in the idea of finite substance" (153-028). This hierarchy of objective reality underwrites the causal argument for God's existence — the idea of an infinite substance must have an infinite substance as its cause.

## Arguments and Implications

The theory of ideas is central to Descartes' break with scholastic epistemology. For the scholastics, knowledge was *adaequatio rei et intellectus* — the conformity of intellect to thing — and the mind grasped things directly through intelligible species. Descartes replaces direct realism with **representationalism**: we know things only through our ideas of them, and the question is whether our ideas correspond to reality.

This raises the problem of the **external world**. If we know only our ideas, how do we know they correspond to anything outside the mind? Descartes' answer depends on God's veracity: since God is not a deceiver, and we have a strong natural inclination to believe that our adventitious ideas come from bodies, bodies must exist. This argument, developed in the Sixth Meditation, has been widely criticized as circular or insufficient.

The classification of ideas also underpins Descartes' **innatism**. Ideas like that of God, of perfection, and of geometrical truths cannot come from the senses (which deliver only particular, imperfect data) or from the imagination (which can only recombine sensory material). They must be innate — planted in the mind by God. This innatism was the central target of **Locke's** *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1690).

## Historical Context

Descartes' use of "idea" is a semantic innovation. In **Plato**, *idea* (*eidos*) refers to the eternal Forms — the transcendent archetypes of which sensible things are copies. In Descartes, ideas are mental entities — modes of thought — not transcendent realities. This shift from metaphysical to psychological usage is characteristic of modern philosophy.

The scholastics had used the term loosely, sometimes for Platonic exemplars in the divine mind, sometimes for the intelligible species by which the intellect knows things. Descartes' precise definition — idea as a form of thought of which we are immediately conscious — eliminates this ambiguity and makes "idea" a technical term of epistemology.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Arnauld**, in the Fourth Objections, raised a powerful objection: if ideas are mental modifications, how can they represent objects distinct from the mind? The "objective reality" of ideas seems to require that ideas have a representational content that is not reducible to their formal reality as mental acts. This problem — the intentionality of mental states — remains central in philosophy of mind.

**Locke** rejected innate ideas, arguing that the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth and that all ideas derive from sensation and reflection. **Leibniz** defended innatism in his *New Essays on Human Understanding*, replying to Locke that the mind is like a block of marble with veins that predispose it to certain shapes — ideas are innate as dispositions, not as actual conscious contents.
