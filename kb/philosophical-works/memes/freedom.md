# Freedom

In Descartes' philosophy, **freedom** (*libertas*, *liberum arbitrium*) is the fundamental power of the will. It is the faculty by which we affirm or deny propositions in judgment, and pursue or avoid goods in action. For Descartes, the will is "in a certain sense infinite" (013-002) — more God-like than any other human faculty — and freedom of the will is the supreme perfection of human nature. Descartes makes this point forcefully in the Fourth Meditation: "it is free-will especially that renders me a certain image and similitude of Deity" (155-001).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes locates freedom in the will, which he distinguishes sharply from the intellect. In the Fourth Meditation, he argues that the will is "the power of choosing" and is unlimited in scope: "it is only the will, or freedom of choice, which I experience to be so great that I cannot conceive the idea of any greater; so that it is chiefly this will that causes me to know that I bear a certain image and similitude of God" (013-002). Unlike the intellect, which is finite and grasps only some things clearly, the will extends to everything — even what is not clearly understood (155-001).

This asymmetry between infinite will and finite intellect explains human error. Descartes writes that "the cause of error... is to be found in the circumstance that the faculty of the will being much wider in its range and compass than the understanding, I do not restrain it within the same limits but extend it even to things which I do not understand" (098-002). Error occurs when the will affirms or denies what the intellect has not yet clearly perceived. The remedy is not to limit freedom but to use it correctly: suspend judgment when evidence is insufficient. Descartes identifies two distinct grades of freedom: the freedom of indifference (choosing between contraries without decisive reason) and the higher freedom of spontaneity (being so clearly convinced by truth that assent is necessary yet fully voluntary) (155-001).

Descartes considers the reconciliation of divine foreknowledge with human freedom. In the *Principles*, he states that after proving God's existence, "we will be free from these embarrassments if we recollect that our thought is finite, and that the omnipotence of God... is infinite" (099-002). However, he insists that "we have so clear an internal consciousness of the freedom and spontaneity which exists in ourselves, that there is nothing we comprehend more evidently or perfectly" (099-001). The experience of freedom is a primitive datum that no theological speculation can undermine.

Freedom also has an ethical dimension. In the *Discourse*, Descartes describes his provisional moral code, beginning with "to obey the laws and customs of my country" — but this obedience is a free choice of the will, not a renunciation of freedom (006-004). He emphasizes contentment through adjusting desire rather than fortune: "if we consider all external goods as equally beyond our power, we shall not regret their absence" (006-004). This Stoic self-mastery is itself an exercise of freedom: the will controls what it pursues. In a letter to Princess Elizabeth, Descartes further distinguishes between external freedom (freedom of action, which may be constrained) and internal freedom (freedom of judgment, which is always within our power) (155-001).

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' doctrine of freedom has significant implications. First, it underwrites his epistemology: the method of doubt is itself an act of free will — we *choose* to suspend judgment on dubitable matters. The entire *Meditations* project depends on this capacity for voluntary doubt. Second, freedom grounds moral responsibility: we can be blamed for error because we freely assent beyond the limits of clear perception. Third, the infinity of the will makes humans *imago Dei* — bearers of the divine image — more through will than through reason.

The reconciliation problem (how can a free human will coexist with divine predestination?) is addressed not by solving it but by delimiting its scope. Descartes regards both divine power and human freedom as clear starting points, and warns against trying to "comprehend" how they fit together — such comprehension would require an infinite intellect, which humans lack (098-002). This is a characteristic Cartesian move: demarcate the limits of finite understanding and accept that some truths exceed our grasp (099-002).

The distinction between two grades of freedom is especially important for understanding Descartes' ethics and epistemology together. The freedom of indifference — choosing without a decisive reason — is the lowest grade, characteristic of ignorance. The freedom of spontaneity — assenting because one clearly sees the truth — is the highest grade, characteristic of wisdom. In both cases the act is free, but only in the latter is it guided by knowledge. This allows Descartes to maintain that the clear perception of truth does not *compel* assent in a way that destroys freedom; rather, it *perfects* freedom by orienting the will toward its proper object (155-001).

## Historical Context

The problem of free will was central to late medieval and early modern philosophy. The Reformation debates between **Luther** and **Erasmus** over the bondage of the will set the stage. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) affirmed free will against Protestant denials, making the topic ecclesiastically sensitive. Descartes navigates this by affirming both divine omnipotence and human freedom without reducing one to the other. His position influenced **Malebranche**, who radicalized the divine causality side, and **Leibniz**, who sought a systematic reconciliation through pre-established harmony. Descartes' analysis of error as a product of will outstripping intellect also anticipates themes in **Spinoza**, though Spinoza would ultimately deny libertarian freedom altogether. The two-grades doctrine also had medieval precedent in the distinction between *libertas indifferentiae* and *libertas spontaneitatis* found in **Aquinas** and the scholastic tradition, though Descartes gave it a distinctly epistemological emphasis (155-001).

## Criticisms and Responses

**Hobbes** objected that Descartes' notion of freedom as indifference (the ability to choose either way) is incoherent — freedom is simply the absence of external impediments to action. Descartes replied that indifference is the lowest grade of freedom; true freedom consists in being so clearly persuaded of the truth that one cannot but affirm it, and this necessity of assent is the highest freedom, not its negation. **Spinoza** took this line further and concluded that freedom is not libertarian choice but acting from one's own nature under the guidance of adequate ideas. **Arnauld** questioned whether Descartes' infinite will makes God the author of sin; Descartes maintained that error is a privation, not a positive reality, and thus requires no divine cause.
