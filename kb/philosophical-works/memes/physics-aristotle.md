# Physics (Aristotle)

Aristotle's **Physics** is the foundational text of Western natural philosophy. Descartes engages with Aristotelian physics as both a target of critique and — he claims — a precedent for his own mechanistic principles. Descartes insists that his physics "made use of no principle which was not received and approved by Aristotle, and by the other philosophers of all ages" (058-002).

## Overview

The *Physics* develops Aristotle's core doctrines: the four causes (material, formal, efficient, final), the distinction between potentiality and actuality, the nature of change and motion, the concepts of place and time, and the argument for an unmoved mover. It was the standard physics textbook of the medieval and Renaissance universities Descartes attended.

## Descartes' Engagement

Descartes rejects Aristotle's **final causes**: "I think it proper here to make such aware, that it was my aim to write nothing of which I could not give exact demonstration" (013-002). Teleological explanation — explaining things by their purposes — is unknowable by finite minds. Descartes retains only efficient causation.

Descartes also rejects Aristotle's **hylomorphism** — the doctrine that natural substances are composites of matter and form. For Descartes, matter is extension, and all change is local motion. No substantial forms, no occult qualities.

Yet Descartes claims continuity: "I have in truth only considered the figure, motion, and magnitude of bodies" (058-002) — principles he finds in Aristotle himself. This strategic claim positions Cartesian mechanism as reformed Aristotelianism rather than a radical break.
