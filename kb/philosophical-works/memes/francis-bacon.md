# Francis Bacon

**Francis Bacon** (1561–1626) is the founding figure of the empiricist tradition — the philosophical counterpart and rival to [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' rationalism. While Descartes sought to rebuild [[knowledge]] on the foundation of clear and distinct [[idea|ideas]] intuited by the intellect, Bacon proposed a method of systematic observation, experiment, and inductive generalization. His *Novum Organum* (1620) — the "New Instrument" of knowledge, as opposed to [[aristotle|Aristotle's]] *Organon* — argued that the mind must be cleansed of "idols" (systematic errors) before it can read the book of nature. The contrast between Baconian induction and Cartesian deduction structures the **rationalist-empiricist divide** that runs through the entire subsequent history of Western [[philosophy]].

## Biography

Bacon was born in London, the son of Sir Nicholas Bacon (Lord Keeper of the Seal under Elizabeth I). Educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, and Gray's Inn, he entered Parliament at 23 and rose through the ranks of the English legal and political establishment: Solicitor General (1607), Attorney General (1613), Lord Keeper (1617), and finally Lord Chancellor (1618). He was created Baron Verulam in 1618 and Viscount St. Albans in 1621.

His political career ended in disgrace: in 1621 he was convicted of accepting bribes (though he claimed the gifts did not influence his judgments), fined, imprisoned briefly in the Tower, and banned from office. He spent his final years writing philosophy at his estate in Gorhambury. He died in 1626 after contracting pneumonia while stuffing a chicken with snow — an experiment to test whether cold could preserve flesh.

## Relationship to Descartes

Bacon and Descartes never corresponded directly, and Descartes' engagement with Bacon's work was limited. Yet the two stand as the symbolic founders of the two great methodological traditions:

- **Baconian Induction**: Knowledge begins with the senses. The mind collects particulars, eliminates accidental correlations through controlled experiment, and ascends by gradual steps to general principles. Truth is discovered, not deduced.

- **Cartesian Deduction**: Knowledge begins with intellectual intuition — clear and distinct ideas that the mind apprehends directly. From these first principles, the mind deduces consequences by a chain of necessary reasoning. Truth is demonstrated, not gathered.

Descartes briefly discusses Baconian induction in the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]*, dismissing the syllogism and the empiricist alternative together: syllogisms are useful only for explaining what one already knows, and mere collection of observations without a guiding [[method]] leads nowhere.

## Key Ideas

**The Four Idols**: Bacon's theory of systematic error — the obstacles that prevent the mind from seeing nature clearly. The *Idols of the Tribe* (errors from human nature), *Idols of the Cave* (individual biases), *Idols of the Marketplace* (confusions of language), and *Idols of the Theatre* (dogmas of philosophical systems) must be cleared away before genuine inquiry can begin. This anticipates Descartes' project of clearing away [[prejudice]] through methodical [[doubt]] — though Bacon's cleansing is empirical, not metaphysical.

**Induction by Elimination**: Bacon's method proceeds by compiling tables of presence, absence, and variation, then eliminating hypotheses inconsistent with the data. The form (essence) of a phenomenon — heat, for example — is discovered by identifying what is always present when it occurs, always absent when it does not, and varies in proportion with it. This is the ancestor of Mill's Methods and modern experimental design.

**Knowledge Is Power**: Bacon's most famous dictum — *scientia potentia est* — expresses the pragmatic criterion of knowledge. True knowledge is productive: it enables the control and transformation of nature for human benefit. Descartes shared this practical ambition (the *Discourse* promises a "practical philosophy" that makes us "masters and possessors of nature"), but insisted that power must be built on [[certainty|certain]] foundations.
