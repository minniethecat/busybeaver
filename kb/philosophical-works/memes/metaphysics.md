Metaphysics, or "first philosophy," is for Descartes the foundational discipline of all knowledge. It establishes the existence of God and the nature of the human soul, and from these it derives the principles — clarity and distinctness as the criterion of truth, the veracity of God as warrant for clear ideas — that underwrite the entire edifice of science. Without metaphysical foundations, Descartes argues, physics would rest on uncertain principles; with them, knowledge becomes systematic and certain.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes announces the primacy of metaphysics in the *Discourse on Method*, where he describes his first meditations as establishing "the principles of the philosophy of which I make use" (009-006). His famous tree-of-knowledge metaphor in the Preface to the *Principles* elaborates the point: philosophy as a whole is a tree whose roots are metaphysics, whose trunk is physics, and whose branches are the other sciences — medicine, mechanics, and morals.

The content of Cartesian metaphysics is developed most fully in the *Meditations on First Philosophy*. The work proceeds through radical doubt to the discovery of the cogito ("I think, therefore I am") as an indubitable foundation (007-001). From the cogito, Descartes derives the criterion of truth — whatever is clearly and distinctly perceived is true — and then establishes the existence of God as the guarantee that this criterion is reliable. With God's veracity secured, the existence of the external world and the principles of physics follow.

In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes reorganizes metaphysical truths into a systematic exposition, beginning with the principles of human knowledge, proceeding to the principles of material things, and culminating in the visible world. The work is designed to replace "the speculative philosophy usually taught in the schools" with a practical philosophy grounded in certain first principles (009-002).

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' conception of metaphysics as foundation has far-reaching implications. First, it inverts the traditional Aristotelian ordering in which physics is the most fundamental science. For Aristotle, "first philosophy" was the study of being qua being, posterior to physics in the order of learning. For Descartes, metaphysics must come first epistemologically — until God and the soul are secured, no other knowledge can count as certain.

Second, the foundational role of metaphysics gives Descartes' arguments for God's existence an urgency they would not have in a purely theological context. God is not merely an object of religious devotion but the epistemological guarantor of science itself. Without God, we could never be certain that clear and distinct perceptions correspond to reality — the evil genius hypothesis would remain an ineliminable possibility.

Third, the Cartesian conception of metaphysics as foundation for physics was enormously influential. Spinoza, Leibniz, and Malebranche all accepted the basic structure — though each modified the content — and the project of grounding natural science in first philosophy remained a dominant ambition through Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason*.

## Historical Context

Descartes' redefinition of metaphysics is a response to the crisis of scepticism in early modern philosophy. The rediscovery of Sextus Empiricus in the sixteenth century had made radical sceptical arguments newly available, and the collapse of Aristotelian scholasticism after the Scientific Revolution left philosophy without agreed-upon first principles. Descartes' project is to reconstruct metaphysics on a foundation that even the most radical sceptic cannot doubt.

The term "first philosophy" (*prima philosophia*) comes from Aristotle, for whom it is the science of being qua being. Descartes appropriates the term but gives it a distinctly epistemological cast: first philosophy is first in the order of knowledge, not in the order of being. This epistemological turn is characteristic of early modern philosophy and distinguishes Cartesian metaphysics from both ancient and medieval versions.

## Criticisms and Responses

The *Objections and Replies* to the *Meditations* contain extensive criticisms of Cartesian metaphysics. Hobbes and Gassendi challenged the inference from "I think" to "I am a thinking substance," arguing that all Descartes has shown is that thinking occurs, not that there is a substantial self. Arnauld pointed out the circularity in Descartes' reasoning — the notorious "Cartesian circle": Descartes uses clear and distinct perception to prove God's existence, but then appeals to God's veracity to guarantee clear and distinct perception.

Later critics have questioned whether Descartes' foundationalist ambition is coherent. Can any set of principles be so certain that they provide an unshakeable foundation for all knowledge? Hegel argued that philosophy cannot begin with abstract first principles but must instead trace the development of thought through its concrete historical unfolding. Heidegger saw Descartes as the origin of modern subjectivity — the reduction of being to what is represented by a subject — and thus as a fateful wrong turn in the history of philosophy.
