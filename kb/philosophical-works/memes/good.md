# The Good

In Descartes' philosophy, **the good** (*bonum*) has both metaphysical and ethical dimensions. Metaphysically, goodness is a perfection, and God is the supreme good — the source and standard of all goodness. Ethically, the will is drawn toward what the intellect presents as good, and the highest human good is attained through the proper exercise of the will, guided by clear and distinct knowledge.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes makes goodness central to his theory of judgment and error. In the Fourth Meditation, he explains error not as a positive faculty but as a privation: "error is not a pure negation, but a privation or want of some knowledge which somehow or other I ought to possess" (013-004). The will, which is infinite, is drawn toward the good — but when the intellect presents something as good without sufficient clarity, the will may assent to a falsehood, mistaking an apparent good for a real one. Judgment, for Descartes, is an act of the will responding to what intellect presents: "the will of a thinking being is carried, voluntarily indeed and freely (for that is the essence of will), but nevertheless infallibly, to the good that is clearly known to it" (153-029).

This account has an important implication: the will cannot err when it assents only to what is clearly and distinctly perceived. Error occurs when the will overextends itself, judging matters that exceed the intellect's clear grasp. This is why Descartes insists on suspending judgment in cases of obscurity — the wise person assents only to clear goods, and the highest wisdom lies in knowing the limits of one's understanding.

God's goodness is foundational to Descartes' system. In proving that God is not a deceiver, Descartes appeals to divine goodness: "the source of all goodness and truth, creator of all things... though possessing also some perfection; and it is accordingly impossible that he should be a deceiver" (080-002). God's goodness guarantees the reliability of clear and distinct ideas — a deceiving God would be an imperfect, and therefore impossible, being. The goodness of God thus underwrites the entire Cartesian epistemological project.

In the *Discourse*, Descartes sketches a provisional ethics. He resolves "to seek the largest amount of what is truly good which I could ever hope to secure" (006-005). His maxims — obedience to law and custom, firmness in action, self-mastery over fortune — are means to the supreme good, which he identifies with the cultivation of reason and the pursuit of truth. Descartes never developed a systematic ethics, but his scattered remarks suggest that the highest human good is knowledge of God, self, and world — the fruit of the philosophical life.

>
> — Principles of Philosophy, I.XXXII (153-029)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' analysis of the good has several significant implications. His claim that the will is infallibly carried to the clearly-known good echoes the Socratic and Platonic doctrine that no one errs knowingly — vice is ignorance. Yet Descartes adds a distinctively modern element: error is not just ignorance but a misuse of free will. We are responsible for our errors because we freely choose to judge beyond the limits of clear perception.

The identification of being, goodness, and perfection is a theme inherited from medieval metaphysics — the *transcendentals* (*ens*, *bonum*, *verum*) are convertible. But Descartes gives this a new epistemological twist: goodness is not just a metaphysical attribute of being but a regulative principle for judgment. We should assent only to what we clearly perceive as good (i.e., as true), and suspend judgment elsewhere.

The supreme good for humans is not pleasure or honor but wisdom — the perfection of the mind through knowledge. This aligns Descartes with the ancient eudaimonistic tradition, but with a distinctively modern, epistemological emphasis: the *summum bonum* is attained through method, not through habituation in virtue.

> For, from the sole fact that God is supremely good, it follows that he cannot be a deceiver; since in all fraud and deception there is some imperfection to be found, and though the power to deceive may seem to argue subtlety of mind, the will to deceive testifies undoubtedly to malice or weakness, and thus is incompatible with the nature of a being supremely perfect.
>
> — Meditations, IV (096-002)

The identification of the good with the clearly perceived true has a further consequence for Descartes' ethics of belief. In the *Meditations*, he resolves "to withhold my assent from matters which are not entirely certain and indubitable" (080-001) — this withholding is itself a moral act, a refusal to assent to apparent goods that are not clearly perceived. The ethics of belief and the metaphysics of goodness thus converge: to judge well is to will well, and to will well is to pursue the true good rather than its simulacra.

Descartes' account also illuminates why he considered the philosophical life the highest form of human existence. "The greatest good that can belong to man, so far as reason is concerned, is to have the firm and constant will to do that which he judges to be the best" (006-005). This is not a utilitarian calculus but a perfectionist ideal: the supreme good consists in the perfection of our highest faculty — the will, guided by clear knowledge — and this perfection is attained through the practice of philosophy itself.

## Historical Context

Descartes' treatment of the good draws on several traditions. From **Augustine**, he inherits the privation theory of evil — evil has no positive reality but is the absence of good, just as error is the absence of knowledge where knowledge ought to be. From **Aquinas** and the scholastic tradition, he inherits the convertibility of being and goodness, and the principle that the will is drawn to the good under the aspect of the good (*sub ratione boni*). From the Stoics, he borrows the ideal of self-mastery and the identification of the highest good with virtue — though he replaces virtue with knowledge. Descartes' provisional morality in the *Discourse* is explicitly Stoic in its emphasis on controlling desire rather than fortune (006-004).

## Criticisms and Responses

The most persistent criticism of Descartes' treatment of the good is that he never developed a systematic ethics. **Spinoza**, **Malebranche**, and **Leibniz** all attempted to fill this gap, each in different ways. Spinoza's *Ethics* extends Descartes' rationalist method to the domain of human flourishing, identifying the intellectual love of God as the highest good. **Hobbes** would reject the entire framework — for Hobbes, "good" is simply what we desire, and there is no objective *summum bonum*. Descartes would likely reply that the very possibility of error in judgment proves that there is a distinction between real and apparent goods, and that only clear and distinct perception can bridge the gap.
