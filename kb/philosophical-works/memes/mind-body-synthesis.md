The mind-body problem — how mental states relate to physical states — originates in its modern form with Descartes' radical separation of mind and body into two distinct substances. This wiki's coverage, spanning 85 concepts and 33 philosophers, provides a rich map of the problem's development from its Cartesian formulation through early modern critiques and beyond. This synthesis traces three key threads: Descartes' dualist argument, the interaction problem it generated, and the alternative frameworks — Aristotelian hylomorphism and modern physicalism — that emerged in response.

## Descartes' Dualism

Descartes argues in the *Meditations* that mind and body are "substances really reciprocally distinct" (013-002). The argument rests on the conceivability of the mind existing without the body: because we can clearly and distinctly conceive the mind as a thinking, unextended thing, and the body as an extended, unthinking thing, God could create them separately, and therefore they are distinct substances (013-002).

The metaphysical foundation is Descartes' substance dualism: the essence of [[mind]] is thought (*cogitatio*), while the essence of [[body]] is extension. These natures are "not only diverse, but even in some measure contraries" (013-002) — the mind is indivisible (we cannot conceive "half of a mind"), while the body is infinitely divisible.

Descartes deploys this dualism to establish the immortality of the soul: since the mind is a substance distinct from the body, the destruction of the body does not entail the destruction of the mind (008-009, 013-002). The rational soul "could by no means be educed from the power of matter" but "must be expressly created" (008-009).

## The Interaction Problem

The canonical difficulty with Cartesian dualism is the interaction problem: if mind and body are entirely distinct substances with nothing in common, how can they causally interact? Princess [[elisabeth-of-bohemia]] pressed this objection in her correspondence with Descartes.

Descartes located the point of interaction at the pineal gland, where the animal spirits (subtle bodily fluids) interface with the rational soul. But this merely relocates the problem — it does not explain how an unextended thinking substance can move extended matter, or how corporeal motions can produce ideas in an immaterial mind.

The wiki's coverage of [[dualism]], [[interactionism]], and [[pineal-gland]] documents Descartes' attempts to address this difficulty and the persistent philosophical dissatisfaction with his solution.

## Alternative Frameworks

### Aristotelian Hylomorphism

Before Descartes, the dominant framework was [[aristotle]]'s hylomorphism: the soul is the form of the body, not a separate substance. On this view, mind and body are not two things but two aspects of one unified living being. The wiki's coverage of [[aristotle]], [[soul]], and [[substance]] explores how Descartes' dualism represented a decisive break from this tradition — and why some philosophers have argued that the Aristotelian framework avoids the interaction problem altogether.

### Modern Physicalism

The scientific revolution that Descartes helped launch ultimately undermined his dualism. If all physical phenomena reduce to matter in motion (008-002), and if mental states correlate systematically with brain states, the postulation of an immaterial thinking substance appears increasingly unnecessary. The wiki's treatment of [[mind]], [[body]], [[matter]], and [[consciousness]] traces how the mechanistic worldview Descartes championed eventually rendered his own dualism problematic.

Philosophers from [[spinoza]] (who proposed a double-aspect theory) to [[hume]] (who challenged the very notion of mental substance) to modern physicalists have developed alternatives that avoid Cartesian dualism's difficulties while preserving the reality of mental phenomena.

## Synthesis

The mind-body problem persists as a living philosophical issue precisely because Descartes' formulation, while arguably untenable in its original form, identified a genuine explanatory gap. The wiki's coverage illustrates a trajectory: from Aristotelian unity, through Cartesian separation, toward modern attempts to reconcile mental and physical within a single ontological framework. Each stage in this trajectory is represented in the wiki's concept, person, and work pages, providing a multi-perspective view of one of philosophy's most enduring problems.
