# Rationalism

**Rationalism** is the epistemological tradition that [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] inaugurated and that [[spinoza]] and [[leibniz]] brought to its highest systematic expression. Its core commitment is that [[reason]] — not sensory experience — is the primary source of [[knowledge]]. The paradigm of knowledge is mathematical demonstration: starting from self-evident axioms (innate ideas), the rationalist deduces necessary truths through chains of clear reasoning. This tradition dominated Continental [[philosophy]] for two centuries and, through its dialogue with British [[empiricism]], set the terms for modern epistemology.

## The Three Great Rationalists

**René Descartes** (1596–1650): The founder. Descartes' [[method]] — systematic [[doubt]], the [[cogito]] as first principle, clear and distinct ideas as criterion of truth — established the rationalist program. The *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* demonstrates that certain knowledge of [[god]], the [[soul]], and the material world can be achieved through reason alone.

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677): The radical. Spinoza took Descartes' geometric ideal further than Descartes himself, writing his *[[ethics-spinoza|Ethics]]* in Euclidean form — definitions, axioms, propositions, demonstrations. But Spinoza's conclusions explode Cartesian [[dualism]]: there is only one [[substance]] (God-or-Nature), of which [[mind]] and [[body]] are attributes.

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (1646–1716): The synthesizer. Leibniz defended rationalism against [[locke]]'s empiricism, arguing that the mind is not a blank slate but contains innate dispositions toward necessary truths. His doctrine of pre-established harmony — that [[god]] created mind and body to run in parallel without interaction — was an alternative to both Cartesian interactionism and Spinozist monism.

## Core Commitments

1. **Innate Ideas**: The mind possesses ideas (of God, perfection, mathematical truths) that are not derived from experience. These are the foundation of certain knowledge.

2. **Deductive Method**: Knowledge proceeds from self-evident axioms through necessary deductions. The model is geometry.

3. **Reason over Sense**: The senses are unreliable and provide only confused representations. True knowledge is intellectual, not sensory.

4. **Substance Metaphysics**: Reality is understood in terms of [[substance]] and its attributes — thinking substance (mind), extended substance (body), and infinite substance (God).

## Rationalism vs. Empiricism

The rationalist-empiricist distinction (which [[kant]] later attempted to synthesize) structured early modern philosophy. Where [[locke]], [[berkeley]], and [[hume]] argued that all ideas derive from experience, the rationalists insisted that experience alone cannot yield necessary truths. Mathematics — a priori, certain, independent of sensory confirmation — was the rationalists' Exhibit A.
