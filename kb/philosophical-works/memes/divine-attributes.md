# Divine Attributes

In Descartes' philosophy, the **divine attributes** are the perfections that constitute God's nature as an infinitely perfect being. Descartes holds that these attributes can be known by the natural light of reason, independently of divine revelation, and that knowledge of them is inseparable from knowledge of God's existence. The divine attributes play a foundational role in Cartesian metaphysics, grounding both the causal proof of God's existence and the distinction between infinite and finite substance.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes maintains that knowledge of God's attributes follows directly from knowledge of God's existence. "In knowing the existence of God, in the manner here explained, we likewise know all his attributes, as far as they can be known by the natural light alone" (080-001). This is a striking claim: the natural light of reason — not revelation or faith — is sufficient to grasp the divine perfections.

The core divine attributes for Descartes include infinity, eternity, immutability, omniscience, omnipotence, and supreme perfection. In the Geometrical Appendix, he defines God as "the substance which we understand to be supremely perfect, and in which we conceive nothing that involves any defect, or limitation of perfection" (153-011). Each attribute is understood as a perfection without limit — God is not merely very powerful but omnipotent, not merely long-lasting but eternal.

Descartes draws an important distinction between how attributes apply to God and how they apply to created substances. In created things, what is always present in the same mode — such as existence and duration — ought to be called an attribute rather than a mode or quality (114-002). But for God, there is a stricter rule: "since God must be conceived as superior to change, it is not proper to say that there are modes or qualities in him, but simply attributes" (114-002). God's perfections are not variable states but immutable features of his essence.

The concept of attributes is central to Descartes' causal proof of God. He argues that perfections are attributes of substance, and that if he possessed the power to conserve his own existence, he would also have the power to confer all missing perfections upon himself (153-049). Since he lacks these perfections, he cannot be the cause of his own existence. The causal adequacy principle — "all the reality or perfection which is in a thing is found formally or eminently in its first and total cause" (153-026) — requires that the idea of an infinitely perfect being can only have an infinitely perfect cause.

## Arguments and Implications

The divine attributes underpin several key Cartesian arguments. In the causal proof of God's existence, the idea of an infinite substance possessing all perfections serves as the premise: since this idea has more objective reality than any finite idea, and since the finite mind cannot generate it from its own resources, God must exist as its cause (080-001, 153-026).

The divine attributes also serve a methodological function in natural philosophy. Descartes instructs that we should consider God "as the efficient cause of all things" and endeavour to discover "by the natural light which he has planted in us, applied to those of his attributes of which he has been willing we should have some knowledge, what must be concluded regarding those effects we perceive by our senses" (086-002). This means that knowledge of God's attributes — particularly his immutability — directly informs Descartes' laws of nature: because God is unchanging, the quantity of motion he conserves in the universe remains constant.

For epistemology, the divine attribute of veracity is critical. Descartes identifies it as "the first attribute of God which here falls to be considered, that he is absolutely veracious and the source of all light" — since a perfect being cannot deceive, clear and distinct ideas can be trusted. This bridges the metaphysical knowledge of God's attributes and the epistemic reliability of human reason.

## Historical Context

Descartes' treatment of the divine attributes inherits from the medieval scholastic tradition — particularly Aquinas, who developed an extensive doctrine of the divine attributes (omnipotence, omniscience, goodness, eternity) in the *Summa Theologiae*. However, Descartes departs from scholasticism in two significant ways. First, he insists that the divine attributes are accessible to natural reason without the aid of revelation, aligning with the rationalist project of grounding theology in philosophy. Second, he treats infinity not as a negative attribute (the absence of limit) but as a positive perfection — a reversal that would profoundly influence Spinoza and later rationalist metaphysics.
