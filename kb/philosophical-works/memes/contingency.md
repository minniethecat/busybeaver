# Contingency

In Descartes' philosophy, **contingency** (*contingentia*) refers to the mode of existence proper to finite and limited things: existence that is possible but not necessary. The contingent/necessary distinction is central to Descartes' ontological argument for God's existence and his metaphysics of substance. For Descartes, God alone possesses necessary existence — existence that belongs to his essence — while all created things exist only contingently, depending on God's continuous conservation.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes draws the distinction between contingent and necessary existence most clearly in the Geometrical Appendix to the *Meditations*. He states that "in the concept of a limited thing, possible or contingent existence is alone contained, and in the concept of a being sovereignly perfect, perfect and necessary existence is comprised" (153-032). This means that when we examine the idea of any finite thing — a triangle, a mind, a body — we find that its existence is merely possible, not guaranteed by its essence. But when we examine the idea of God, we find that necessary existence is contained within it.

The distinction is restated in the Fifth Meditation: the mind discovers that in the idea of a supremely perfect being "there is contained not only possible and contingent existence, as in the ideas of all other things which it clearly perceives, but existence absolutely necessary and eternal" (072-002). From this Descartes concludes that existence belongs to God's essence just as necessarily as the property of having angles equal to two right angles belongs to a triangle.

Descartes explicitly marks the asymmetry: "necessary existence is not in the same way comprised in the notions which we have of other things, but merely contingent existence" (073-001). Created substances — minds and bodies — exist contingently: they require God's continuous creation to persist, and their non-existence is logically possible. God's existence, by contrast, is necessary — God cannot not exist.

The contingent/necessary distinction also grounds Descartes' cosmological argument. In the Geometrical Appendix, he argues that if a finite being had the power to conserve its own existence, it would have the power to confer all missing perfections upon itself (153-049). The fact that finite beings lack these perfections demonstrates their contingent status — they do not exist *a se* (from themselves) but depend on a necessary being.

## Arguments and Implications

The contingency of finite existence has profound implications for Descartes' physics and metaphysics. All created substances depend moment-by-moment on God's continuous conservation: "it is a greater and more difficult thing to create or conserve a substance than to create or conserve its attributes or properties; but this creation of a thing is not greater or more difficult than its conservation" (153-031). This doctrine of continuous creation means that the distinction between creation and conservation collapses — God sustains the world in existence at every instant, and without this sustaining power, contingent things would cease to be.

The contingency of the natural world also grounds Descartes' doctrine of the creation of the eternal truths: because God's will is absolutely free and his power infinite, the laws of nature and even the truths of mathematics are contingent upon God's will. They are necessary *for us* (we cannot conceive them otherwise) but contingent *in themselves* (God could have made them otherwise). This radical contingency of all created reality — including logical and mathematical truths — distinguishes Descartes from later rationalists like Leibniz, who held that eternal truths are necessary in all possible worlds.

## Historical Context

The distinction between necessary and contingent being has medieval roots in **Aquinas**'s *Third Way*, which argues from the existence of contingent beings to the existence of a necessary being. Descartes inherits this distinction but transforms it: whereas Aquinas's argument proceeds from the observed contingency of the world to God as first cause, Descartes locates the necessary/contingent distinction within the *ideas* themselves, making it an *a priori* rather than *a posteriori* argument. This shift exemplifies Descartes' rationalist turn — the move from sense-based to idea-based metaphysics.
