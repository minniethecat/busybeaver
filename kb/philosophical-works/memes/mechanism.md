# Mechanism

In Descartes' philosophy, **mechanism** is the doctrine that all natural phenomena — celestial, terrestrial, and biological alike — can be explained by the size, shape, and motion of material parts. Descartes rejects the Aristotelian substantial forms and final causes of the scholastics, holding instead that "the explication of the motion of the heart and of some other things" is derivable from "the mere disposition of the organs as wholly as the motion of a clock, or other automaton, from that of its counterpoise and wheels" (008-002). Mechanism is the positive programme of Cartesian natural philosophy: the replacement of qualitative physics with quantitative, geometrical physics grounded in the single principle of extension.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' mechanism rests on the identification of body with extension. "The substance which is the immediate subject of local extension, and of the accidents that presuppose extension, as figure, situation, local motion, etc., is called *body*" (153-010). Since body is nothing but extension, all bodily change is nothing but change of figure and local motion. There is no need for substantial forms, real qualities, or final causes — these are explanatory fictions of the schools.

Descartes makes the machine analogy explicit throughout his works. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]*, he compares the human body to "different automata, or moving machines fabricated by human industry" (008-008). A clock exhibits complex behaviour — the movement of its hands, the striking of its hours — yet its entire operation is explained by the arrangement of its gears and springs. So too, Descartes argues, the body of an animal or a human being can be fully understood through the disposition of its material parts.

In the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*, Descartes extends this reasoning to the cosmos: he speaks of "this world, as if it were merely a machine in which there was nothing at all to consider but the figures and motions of its parts" (045-002). The same mechanical laws that govern terrestrial bodies govern celestial ones — there is no distinction between sublunary and superlunary physics.

Descartes' mechanism differs from ancient atomism in one crucial respect: Descartes denies the void and holds that matter is infinitely divisible. Mechanism without atomism — extended substance is a plenum, and all motion is transmitted through contact.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes advances three principal arguments for mechanism:

**The Argument from Intelligibility**: "The effects of machines depend for the most part on the agency of certain instruments, which, as they must needs be proportioned to the hands of the workmen, are always so simple and large that their figure and motion can be seen" (061-002). Mechanical explanations are perspicuous — the parts, their sizes, and their motions can be clearly conceived. Substantial forms, by contrast, are occult — no one can say what a "form" is or how it produces effects.

**The Argument from Divine Craftsmanship**: "When we have an idea of any machine in which great skill is displayed, we are wont to pay a higher tribute of admiration to the skill of the maker" (078-002). If human machines display the intelligence of their makers, how much more does the human body — "a machine constructed by the hands of God" — display divine wisdom? Mechanism is a theologically motivated position: the more law-like and machine-like nature is, the more it reflects God's constancy and perfection.

**The Argument from Parsimony**: Mechanism requires only extension and motion — two clear and distinct ideas. The scholastics required substantial forms, real qualities, final causes, and a host of other obscure notions. Descartes' razor: do not multiply entities beyond what is clearly and distinctly perceived.

## Historical Context

Mechanism did not appear from nowhere. Descartes drew on the mechanical tradition of **Archimedes** (the science of simple machines), the corpuscularianism of **[[galileo|Galileo]]**, and the atomism of **[[lucretius|Lucretius]]** and **Democritus**. But Descartes' mechanism is more radical than its predecessors: where atomists allowed only atoms and void, Descartes eliminates void entirely. Where Galileo restricted mechanism to terrestrial physics, Descartes universalizes it.

Descartes' mechanism became the dominant paradigm of the Scientific Revolution. **[[newton|Newton]]** would later modify it (adding forces acting at a distance), and **[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** would critique its passive-matter assumption (proposing active force instead). But the core commitment — that nature operates by law-like mechanical principles, not by purposes or sympathies — was Descartes' decisive contribution.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Leibniz** objected that Descartes' matter is purely passive — mere extension cannot account for the activity we observe in bodies. Leibniz proposed *vis viva* (living force) as a supplement. Descartes would respond that motion is imparted by God at creation and conserved thereafter — bodies need no internal active principle.

**[[newton|Newton]]** showed that gravity appears to act at a distance, contradicting Descartes' contact-mechanics requirement. Descartes' vortex theory of planetary motion was empirically refuted by Newton's *Principia*. This was the most consequential failure of Cartesian mechanism.

**Vitalists** (including the Montpellier school) argued that living organisms cannot be explained mechanically — life requires a *vis vitalis* or vital principle. Descartes' response was the beast-machine hypothesis: animals are machines, and the apparent purposiveness of their behaviour is no different in kind from the apparent purposiveness of a clock.
