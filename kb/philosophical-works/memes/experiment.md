## Definition

**Experiment** is the systematic observation and controlled testing of natural phenomena. In Descartes' philosophy of science, experiment plays an essential but secondary role to rational deduction from first principles, becoming increasingly necessary as knowledge advances into particular domains.

## Role in Cartesian Method

Descartes acknowledged that experiment becomes "always more necessary the more one is advanced in knowledge; for, at the commencement, it is better to make use only of what is spontaneously presented to our senses" (009-003). He recognized the practical limits of individual experimentation: "one man is not equal to the task of making them all" (009-007), suggesting that scientific progress requires collaborative effort.

Despite his rationalist commitments, Descartes devoted considerable time to experiments, particularly in anatomy and optics. He noted that "the principal utility of philosophy depends on the separate uses of its parts" (022-013), implying that experiment is necessary to determine how general principles apply to specific natural phenomena.

## Limitations

Descartes was acutely aware that his philosophical project required "the infinity of experiments I require, and which it is impossible for me to make without the help of others" (009-008). This recognition partly motivated his decision to publish his work — to enlist others in the experimental verification and extension of his principles.

## Experiment vs. Rationalism

Descartes' approach to experiment illustrates the tension in his methodology: while he insisted on deduction from clear and distinct first principles, he conceded that experiment was indispensable for determining the particular mechanisms of the physical world. This contrasts with [[francis-bacon|Francis Bacon's]] more thoroughly empiricist program.
