# Certainty

In Descartes' philosophy, **certainty** (*certitudo*) is the state of mind achieved when the intellect perceives an idea so clearly and distinctly that the will cannot withhold assent. Certainty is the subjective correlate of truth — the psychological sign that knowledge has been attained. Descartes' project is to establish not just true beliefs but *certain* knowledge, grounded in indubitable foundations.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes pursues certainty through the method of doubt. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he resolves "to reject as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt, in order to see if there did not remain, after this, something in my belief that was wholly indubitable" (004-006). Certainty is whatever survives this radical scrutiny.

The *cogito* is the paradigm of certainty. "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not; and that, on the contrary, from the very circumstance that I thought to doubt of the truth of other things, it most clearly and certainly followed that I was" (007-002). The certainty of the *cogito* is immediate and self-verifying — it requires no external guarantee.

Descartes contrasts his standard of certainty with the "feeble" certainty of sensory knowledge. Readers must learn "no longer trusting so confidently in their senses" and instead acquire "the habit of conceiving [the mind] distinctly, and of believing that it is more easy to know than any corporeal object" (153-016). This is a deliberate **cognitive transformation** — a reorientation from sensory to intellectual certainty.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes distinguishes two kinds of certainty. **Metaphysical certainty** is absolute — the impossibility of being mistaken, guaranteed by God's veracity. **Moral certainty** is sufficient for practical life — what is "as certain as we judge anything in morals to be" — even though it falls short of the metaphysical standard. The results of scientific inquiry may achieve only moral certainty, but this is sufficient for action.

Certainty also has a **temporal dimension**. The *cogito* is certain in the moment of perception, but when I turn my attention away, doubt can creep back in. The proof of God's existence eliminates this temporal instability: if God exists and is not a deceiver, then whatever I clearly and distinctly perceive *was* true even when I am not currently attending to it. This is the solution to the "Cartesian circle" — God guarantees the reliability of memory and the stability of knowledge across time.

The quest for certainty also reshapes the **order of knowing**. Sensory knowledge, which common sense treats as the most certain, turns out to be the least certain. Intellectual knowledge of the self (the *cogito*) and of God is more certain than knowledge of the external world. This reversal of common-sense priorities is one of Descartes' most radical philosophical moves.

## Historical Context

The search for certainty in the early modern period was motivated by the collapse of traditional authority. The Reformation challenged the certainty of religious doctrine; the scientific revolution challenged the certainty of Aristotelian physics; the rediscovery of ancient scepticism challenged the very possibility of knowledge. Descartes' foundationalism — rebuilding knowledge from indubitable first principles — is a response to this crisis.

Descartes' standard of certainty is notably higher than the **Aristotelian** standard. Aristotle allowed that different subjects admit of different degrees of precision — ethics cannot achieve the certainty of mathematics, but it can achieve the certainty appropriate to its subject matter. Descartes, by contrast, makes mathematics the model for all knowledge.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Peirce** and the pragmatists argued that Descartes' standard of certainty is impossible and unnecessary. Genuine inquiry does not begin with universal doubt — it begins with real, specific doubts that arise in the course of inquiry. Cartesian "paper doubt" does not actually undermine the beliefs it targets.

**Wittgenstein** argued in *On Certainty* that certain propositions — "Here is a hand," "The world has existed for more than five minutes" — function not as empirical claims but as the **scaffolding** of our entire system of beliefs. They cannot be doubted because doubt presupposes them. Cartesian doubt is incoherent not because it fails to find certainty but because it tries to doubt what makes doubting possible.
