## Overview

Time (*tempus*) in Descartes' philosophy is not a substance or an independent reality but a mode of thinking — a way the mind measures duration. This places time squarely in the domain of thought rather than in things themselves, marking a significant departure from both Scholastic realism about time and the Newtonian absolute time that would follow.

## Time as a Mode of Thought

Descartes' canonical definition appears in the *Principles of Philosophy*: "Time, which we distinguish from duration taken in its generality, and call the measure of motion, is only a certain mode under which we think duration itself" (115-002). Duration belongs to things as an attribute; time is the mind's way of measuring that duration through comparison with regular motion.

> Of these attributes or modes there are some which exist in the things themselves, and others that have only an existence in our thought; thus, for example, time, which we distinguish from duration taken in its generality, and call the measure of motion, is only a certain mode under which we think duration itself. (115-002)

This distinction is crucial: the duration of a thing at rest is no different from the duration of a thing in motion. "If two bodies are in motion for an hour, the one moving quickly and the other slowly, we do not reckon more time in the one than in the other" (115-002). Time is the measure, not the measured.

## Continuous Creation and Temporal Atomism

One of Descartes' most striking doctrines is that the present moment is ontologically independent of the past: "The present time is not dependent on that which immediately preceded it; for this reason, there is not need of a less cause for conserving a thing than for at first producing it" (153-024). This leads to the doctrine of continuous creation — God must sustain the world at each instant, since no moment contains within itself the power to produce the next.

## Time and the Method

In the practical context of the method, Descartes emphasizes patience and deliberation over time. "I did not even choose to dismiss summarily any of the opinions that had crept into my belief without having been introduced by reason, but first of all took sufficient time carefully to satisfy myself of the general nature of the task I was setting myself" (005-005). The method requires temporal discipline — not rushing to judgment.

## Time in Physics

In Descartes' mechanistic physics, time plays a fundamental role as the parameter against which motion is measured. Descartes describes light traversing "in an instant of time the immense spaces of the heavens" (008-002), and the heart's valves opening and closing in precise temporal sequence: "causing in this way all the branches of the arterial vein and of the grand artery to expand almost simultaneously with the heart which immediately thereafter begins to contract" (008-005).

He also uses the clock as a telling analogy: "A clock composed only of wheels and weights can number the hours and measure time more exactly than we with all our skill" (008-008). The clock analogy serves dual purposes — illustrating mechanistic explanation while also highlighting the distinction between mere mechanism and rational mind.

## Time and the Eternal Truths

Duration and time are among Descartes' "eternal truths" — common notions implanted in the mind: "We will also have most distinct conceptions of duration, order, and number, if, in place of mixing up with our notions of them that which properly belongs to the concept of substance, we merely think that the duration of a thing is a mode under which we conceive this thing, in so far as it continues to exist" (113-002).
