Emotion, or passion (*passio*), in Descartes' philosophy refers to a class of perceptions that originate in the body and are experienced by the soul as its own states. Unlike purely intellectual ideas, passions are psychophysical phenomena — they arise from the interaction of body and mind through the animal spirits and the pineal gland, and they constitute the foundation of Descartes' moral psychology.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' most systematic treatment of the passions appears in the *Passions of the Soul* (1649), though the topic is discussed throughout his corpus. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he defines passions as "the emotions or passions of the mind which are not exclusively attributed to the soul or the body" (106-002). They are perceptions that the soul feels as happening within itself, caused by movements of the animal spirits — fine material particles coursing through the nerves and brain.

In the *Discourse*, Descartes already sketches the mechanistic basis of the passions: the movements which indicate the passions "can be imitated by machines" (008-008), suggesting that the bodily component of emotion operates according to purely mechanical principles. This is consistent with his broader project of explaining all bodily functions — including those underlying emotion — in terms of matter in motion, reserving conscious thought and volition for the immaterial soul alone.

Descartes identifies six primitive passions — wonder, love, hatred, desire, joy, and sadness — from which all other passions are compounded. Each has a specific physiological signature and a specific function for the mind-body composite. For instance, joy arises from the perception of present good, while sadness arises from the perception of present evil (047-002). The passions serve the body's self-preservation by inclining the soul to will what is beneficial and avoid what is harmful.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' theory of the passions bridges his metaphysics of mind-body dualism and his practical ethics. Because passions are bodily in origin but mental in experience, they are the primary site of mind-body interaction — and therefore the primary locus of moral struggle. The wise person does not seek to eliminate the passions, which are naturally good as signals from the body, but to master them through reason and generous judgment.

The doctrine also has important implications for Descartes' account of error and sin. Unlike intellectual error, which results from the will outstripping the understanding, passionate error involves the body's influence on the will. Passions incline the will without necessitating it — freedom consists in the power to resist passionate inclination through the correct use of reason.

## Historical Context

Descartes' theory of the passions draws on a long tradition stretching from Plato and Aristotle through the Stoics and the medieval scholastics. The Stoics had argued that passions are fundamentally cognitive — judgments about what is good or bad — and therefore the sage can eliminate them entirely. Aquinas classified the passions into concupiscible and irascible types, following Aristotle.

Descartes breaks with this tradition in key ways. First, he grounds the passions in mechanistic physiology rather than in the faculties of the soul. Second, he argues that passions are inherently good as natural signals, even if they sometimes mislead — a partial rehabilitation of emotion against Stoic apathy. Third, his account of the six primitive passions and their combinations represents a new taxonomy that influenced Spinoza's more systematic treatment in the *Ethics*.

## Criticisms and Responses

Spinoza, though deeply influenced by Descartes' taxonomy, rejected the dualistic framework that makes passions a problem of mind-body interaction. For Spinoza, mind and body are two attributes of one substance, so passions are simply ideas of bodily affections — no causal interaction across substances is required.

Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia, in her correspondence with Descartes, pressed the fundamental difficulty: how can an immaterial soul move the animal spirits to produce or resist a passion? Descartes' appeal to the pineal gland and the union of mind and body was widely seen as unsatisfactory, and the problem of mind-body interaction remains one of the most persistent criticisms of Cartesian philosophy.
