# Stoicism

**Stoicism** was founded by **Zeno of Citium** (c. 334–262 BCE) and developed by **Chrysippus**, **Seneca**, **Epictetus**, and **Marcus Aurelius**. The Stoics taught that virtue is the only good, that the passions are errors of [[judgment]], and that the wise person lives in accordance with the rational order of nature. Descartes engages with Stoicism primarily in his ethics — his theory of the passions and his provisional moral code show clear Stoic influence — while his [[method]] and physics break decisively with Stoic natural philosophy. Descartes acknowledges the Stoics directly in the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*, noting their view that "the wise man is not deprived of passions" (047-002), a position he partly endorses against the caricature of Stoic *apatheia*.

## Core Doctrine

Stoicism is a systematic philosophy with three branches: **logic** (theory of knowledge and reasoning), **physics** (the cosmos as a living, rational organism pervaded by divine reason — *logos*), and **ethics** (virtue is living in accordance with nature/reason). The Stoics held that the passions — grief, fear, desire, pleasure — are false judgments about what is good or bad. The sage eliminates the passions and achieves *apatheia* (freedom from passion).

## Key Figures

| Figure | Role | Key Contribution |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BCE) | Founder | Taught in the *Stoa Poikile* (Painted Porch) in Athens; established the tripartite division of philosophy |
| Chrysippus (c. 279–206 BCE) | Systematizer | Developed Stoic logic and physics into a comprehensive system; said to have written over 700 works |
| Seneca (c. 4 BCE–65 CE) | Roman Stoic | *Letters to Lucilius*; focused on practical ethics and the therapy of the passions |
| Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) | Teacher | Former slave whose *Encheiridion* (Handbook) emphasizes that we control only our judgments, not external events |
| Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) | Emperor-Philosopher | *Meditations* — a Stoic spiritual exercise written as a Roman emperor, focused on duty and acceptance |

## Descartes' Engagement

Descartes' relationship to Stoicism is visible in his **theory of the passions**. In *The Passions of the Soul* (1649), Descartes analyzes the passions as bodily-mental events and advocates their regulation through reason — a project with clear Stoic antecedents. However, Descartes rejects the Stoic ideal of *apatheia*: the passions are natural and beneficial when properly directed, not errors to be eliminated. As he notes, the Stoics themselves "did not deny to their wise man" that he experiences bodily passions (047-002) — Descartes recovers this nuance against the popular caricature of Stoic emotionlessness.

Descartes' **provisional morality** in the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]* also has Stoic resonances. The maxim "to endeavour always to conquer myself rather than fortune, and change my desires rather than the order of the world" echoes Epictetus: "Do not seek to have events happen as you want them to, but want them to happen as they do happen."

Descartes' physics, however, breaks decisively with Stoic physics. The Stoic cosmos is a living organism pervaded by divine *pneuma*; Descartes' cosmos is inert extension governed by mechanical laws. The Stoic [[god|God]] is immanent in nature; Descartes' God is transcendent and creates the laws of nature by fiat.
