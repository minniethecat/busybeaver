Skepticism in philosophy takes two fundamentally different forms: as a conclusion (the view that knowledge is impossible) and as a method (a tool for testing beliefs and discovering certainties). Descartes' most original contribution to the skeptical tradition was transforming skepticism from a destination into a vehicle — using systematic doubt not to end in suspension of judgment, but to arrive at indubitable truth. This synthesis traces the method of skepticism through three pivotal moments in the wiki's coverage: ancient skepticism, Cartesian doubt, and Humean empiricism.

## Ancient Roots: Pyrrho and the Academy

Ancient skepticism, as represented in the wiki's coverage of [[pyrrho]], [[sextus-empiricus]], and [[skepticism]], aimed at *ataraxia* (tranquility) through suspension of judgment (*epoché*). For Pyrrho, the recognition that opposing arguments carry equal weight leads to abandoning the search for truth altogether — skepticism as a way of life, not a methodological tool.

Academic skepticism, associated with [[cicero]]'s account of the Platonic Academy, adopted a more moderate stance: knowledge is unattainable, but probable opinion can guide action. The wiki's coverage of these ancient traditions establishes the intellectual context Descartes inherited and transformed.

## Cartesian Innovation: Doubt as Method

Descartes' genius was to weaponize skepticism against itself. In the *Discourse on the Method* and *Meditations*, he deploys hyperbolic [[doubt]] — doubting not only what is probably false, but everything that can be doubted at all — as a systematic procedure for identifying what cannot be doubted (004-007)(007-001).

The method proceeds through three waves:
1. **Sensory doubt**: The senses sometimes deceive, so sensory beliefs are dubitable
2. **The dream argument**: No certain marks distinguish waking from dreaming experience
3. **The evil demon**: An all-powerful deceiver could make even mathematical truths seem certain while being false

At the foundation of this demolition lies the *cogito*: "I think, therefore I am" (007-002) — the one proposition that survives even the most radical doubt. The wiki's coverage of [[doubt]], [[certainty]], [[cogito]], and [[evil-demon]] traces each stage of this argument.

Crucially, Descartes does not end in skepticism. The *cogito* provides a starting point for reconstructing knowledge: from the *cogito* to God's existence, from God's veracity to the reliability of clear and distinct ideas, and from there to the existence of the external world. The method of doubt is not the conclusion but the instrument of this reconstruction.

## Hume's Empiricist Skepticism

[[hume]] radicalized the skeptical method in a different direction. Where Descartes used doubt to reach rationalist certainty, Hume applied skeptical scrutiny to empiricism itself — and found that key concepts (causation, the self, induction) lack rational justification.

Hume's skepticism about [[cause|causation]] is particularly significant: we never perceive necessary connection between events, only constant conjunction. Our belief in causation is a habit of the mind, not a deliverance of reason. Similarly, Hume's critique of the [[self]] — that introspection reveals only a bundle of perceptions, never a unified substance — undermines the Cartesian *cogito* itself.

The wiki's coverage of [[hume]], [[cause]], [[skepticism]], and [[habit]] connects these Humean arguments to the Cartesian framework they challenge.

## The Trajectory of Methodological Skepticism

The arc from ancient *epoché* through Cartesian method to Humean empiricism reveals skepticism as philosophy's most productive destructive force. Each iteration uses doubt to clear the ground — but what gets built on that ground depends on broader philosophical commitments (rationalist, empiricist, or skeptical). The wiki's multi-perspective coverage of this trajectory, across concept, person, and work pages, illustrates how skepticism as method has been philosophy's engine of conceptual progress.

> From my childhood, I have been familiar with letters; and as I was given to believe that by their help a clear and certain knowledge of all that is useful in life might be acquired, I was ardently desirous of instruction. But as soon as I had finished the entire course of study, at the close of which it is customary to be admitted into the order of the learned, I completely changed my opinion. For I found myself involved in so many doubts and errors, that I was convinced I had advanced no farther in all my attempts at learning, than the discovery at every turn of my own ignorance. And yet I was studying in one of the most celebrated schools in Europe, in which I thought there must be learned men, if such were anywhere to be found. I had been taught all that others learned there; and not contented with the sciences actually taught us, I had, in addition, read all the books that had fallen into my hands, treating of such branches as are esteemed the most curious and rare. I knew the judgment which others had formed of me; and I did not find that I was considered inferior to my fellows, although there were among them some who were already marked out to fill the places of our instructors. And, in fine, our age appeared to me as flourishing, and as fertile in powerful minds as any preceding one. I was thus led to take the liberty of judging of all other men by myself, and of concluding that there was no science in existence that was of such a nature as I had previously been given to believe.
