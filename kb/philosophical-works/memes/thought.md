# Thought (Cogitatio)

**Thought** (*cogitatio*, *pensée*) is Descartes' most comprehensive category of mental activity. In the geometrical appendix to the Second Objections, he defines it as "all that is in us, so that we are immediately conscious of it. Thus, all the operations of the will, intellect, imagination, and senses, are thoughts" (153-004). This definition is foundational to Cartesian metaphysics: thought is not merely ratiocination but the entire field of conscious experience, and it constitutes the essence of the thinking substance ([[mind]]).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes provides his most precise definition of thought in the *Principles of Philosophy* (Geometrical Appendix, Definition I): "By the term *thought* (*cogitatio*, *pensée*), I comprehend all that is in us, so that we are immediately conscious of it" (153-004). The qualifier "immediately" is crucial — it excludes effects that follow from thought, such as voluntary bodily motion. Descartes notes: "voluntary motion has, in truth, thought for its source (principle), but yet it is not itself thought" (153-004).

Thought is the defining attribute of [[mind]]: "The substance in which thought immediately resides is here called *mind* (*mens*, *esprit*)" (153-009). This means that thinking is not something the mind *does* occasionally — it is what the mind *is*. A mind that ceases to think ceases to exist as mind.

In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]*, Descartes reflects on how thought distinguishes humans: "Good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed" (004-001), noting that differences in opinion arise not from some possessing more reason, but from conducting thoughts "along different ways" (004-001). The proper method for directing thought is thus the central project of the *Discourse*.

The relationship between thought and [[idea]] is one of form to content: "By the word *idea* I understand that form of any thought, by the immediate perception of which I am conscious of that same thought" (153-005). Every thought has an ideational content — what the thought is *about* — and it is through ideas that thought represents objects to the mind.

## Arguments and Implications

**Thought as the Essence of Mind**: Descartes' identification of mind with thinking substance has a radical implication: the mind is always thinking. If thought is the essential attribute of mental substance, then the mind cannot exist without thinking any more than body can exist without extension. This leads to Descartes' controversial claim that the soul always thinks, even in deep sleep or in the womb.

**The Criterion of Immediate Consciousness**: By defining thought through immediate consciousness, Descartes establishes a form of privileged access. I cannot be wrong about *that* I am thinking, even if I can be wrong about *what* my thoughts represent. This is the basis of the [[cogito]]: if I am conscious of doubting, I must exist as a thinking thing.

**The Scope of Thought**: Descartes' definition is remarkably broad. It includes not only intellectual acts but sensory perceptions, emotions, and volitions — everything of which we are immediately aware. This means that sensing, imagining, and willing are all modes of thought, and the distinction between them lies in their ideational content, not in their fundamental nature.

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of *cogitatio* draws on the scholastic tradition while transforming it. The scholastics distinguished between the intellective soul (unique to humans) and the sensitive soul (shared with animals). Descartes collapses this distinction: all conscious states are thoughts, and thought belongs to mind alone. This is why Descartes denies that animals think — they lack the immaterial substance in which thought inheres.

The breadth of Descartes' definition also responds to the sceptical challenge: if even sensory experience counts as thought, then the sceptic who claims to be deceived by the senses is already thinking, and therefore already exists.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The "Always Thinking" Problem**: Critics including [[antoine-arnauld|Arnauld]] objected that we are not conscious of thinking during dreamless sleep. Descartes' claim that the soul always thinks seems empirically false. Cartesian defenders distinguish between thinking and *remembering* that one thought — the soul may think during sleep but retain no memory of those thoughts upon waking.

**[[hume|Hume]]'s Bundle Theory**: Hume denied that we ever introspect a unified thinking substance. We perceive only a flux of particular thoughts, not a "mind" that has them. From a Cartesian perspective, this misses the point: the very fact that thoughts occur *to* a single consciousness is what it means to be a thinking substance.

**The Exclusion of Unconscious Mentality**: Modern psychology reveals extensive unconscious mental processing. Descartes' definition makes consciousness essential to thought, which creates difficulties for explaining phenomena like implicit memory or unconscious perception.
