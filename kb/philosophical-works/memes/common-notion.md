# Common Notion

In Descartes' philosophy, a **common notion** (*notio communis*, *communes notiones*) is a self-evident proposition that is innate to the mind and serves as the foundational axiom of all reasoning. Common notions include the principle of non-contradiction ("the same thing cannot at once be and not be"), the causal principle ("nothing comes from nothing"), and mathematical axioms ("if equals be added to equals the wholes will be equal") (107-002). They are called "common" because they are shared by all human minds and apply universally to all subject matters. Descartes also identifies them with **eternal truths** — truths that "have their seat in our mind" and "possess no existence beyond our thought" (106-002).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes treats common notions as the building blocks of all demonstrative knowledge. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he explains that "the mind also discovers certain common notions out of which it frames various demonstrations that carry conviction to such a degree as to render doubt of their truth impossible, so long as we give attention to them" (071-002). These notions are not learned through experience — they are innate, "born with us" (068-001), and "if blinded by no prejudices, we cannot fail to know them when the occasion of thinking them occurs" (107-002).

However, Descartes warns that common notions are not equally admitted by everyone. Some "are not, with respect to all men, equally deserving of the name, because they are not equally admitted by all" (108-002). Prejudice, the dominance of sensory images, and the habit of thinking only of corporeal things can obscure even the most self-evident truths.

In the *Second Replies*, Descartes provides a formal list of "Axioms or Common Notions" (153-022) as part of his geometrical exposition of the *Meditations*. These include propositions such as: "there are diverse degrees of reality" (substance has more reality than mode), "it is a greater thing to create or conserve a substance than its attributes," and "the objective reality of our ideas requires a cause which contains this reality not only objectively but formally or eminently."

## Arguments and Implications

**The Foundations of Demonstration**: Common notions are what make deductive science possible. Mathematics proceeds by combining common notions with definitions to derive theorems. Descartes' ambition was to extend this axiomatic method to all of philosophy — to build a system where every conclusion is demonstrated from self-evident first principles. The *Meditations* and the *Principles of Philosophy* are attempts to execute this program.

**The Certainty Problem**: Common notions are self-evident *while we attend to them*. But "as the mind cannot always think of these with attention... it perceives that there is just ground to distrust the truth of such conclusions, and that it cannot possess any certain knowledge until it has discovered its author" (071-002). This is why the proof of God's existence is essential: even the most self-evident common notions remain vulnerable to the deceiver hypothesis until God's veracity is established.

**Innateness and the Mind's Nature**: Descartes holds that common notions are innate — they are built into the structure of the mind itself. "The notions which are simplest and self-evident, are obscured by logical definitions; and such are not to be reckoned among the cognitions acquired by study, but as born with us" (068-001). This is part of Descartes' broader rationalism: the mind is not a blank slate but comes equipped with the fundamental principles of reasoning.

## Historical Context

The concept of "common notions" (*koinai ennoiai*) originates in **Stoic** philosophy and was transmitted to the early modern period through Cicero, Seneca, and the scholastic tradition. For the Stoics, common notions were the spontaneous, universal conceptions that arise in all human minds through the natural operation of reason.

**Euclid**'s *Elements* provided the model: the axioms (*koinai ennoiai*) of geometry — "things equal to the same thing are equal to each other," "the whole is greater than the part" — are common notions applicable to all quantities. Descartes' innovation was to expand the scope of common notions beyond mathematics to include metaphysical principles, and to ground them in innate ideas rather than empirical abstraction.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Locke** argued that there are no innate principles — even the most self-evident propositions ("whatever is, is") are not known by children or "idiots." Descartes' response (anticipated in 108-002) is that common notions are not always consciously entertained — they require the "occasion of thinking them." The fact that a child does not articulate the principle of non-contradiction does not mean the child's mind lacks it; the principle is the innate structure of all coherent thought.

**Leibniz** modified Descartes' position: common notions are innate not as fully formed propositions but as **dispositions** or tendencies of the mind — "inclinations, dispositions, habits, or natural potentialities." This is a more nuanced version of innatism that avoids Locke's objections.
