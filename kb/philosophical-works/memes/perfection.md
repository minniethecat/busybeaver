# Perfection

In Descartes' philosophy, **perfection** (*perfectio*) is the degree of reality or entity possessed by a thing. Perfection is not merely a moral or aesthetic quality but a fundamental **ontological measure**: God, as infinite substance, possesses supreme perfection; finite substances possess limited perfection; and accidents or modes possess the least reality. This hierarchy underwrites Descartes' causal argument for God's existence.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes establishes the perfection hierarchy in the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix: "There are diverse degrees of reality, that is, of entity [or perfection]: for substance has more reality than accident or mode, and infinite substance than finite" (153-028). Perfection is thus equivalent to ontological status — what has more perfection has more being.

The perfection hierarchy grounds the **causal adequacy principle**: "It is a greater and more difficult thing to create or conserve a substance than to create or conserve its attributes or properties" (153-031). The cause must have at least as much perfection (reality) as the effect. This principle is the engine of Descartes' causal argument for God's existence in the Third Meditation: the idea of an infinite, perfect being cannot originate from a finite, imperfect mind — it must come from God himself.

Perfection also defines God's essence. God is "the substance which we understand to be supremely perfect, and in which we conceive nothing that involves any defect, or limitation of perfection" (153-011). All perfections belong to God eminently; creatures possess them only in limited, participated form. In the *Meditations*, Descartes argues that "If I possessed the power of conserving myself, I should likewise have the power of conferring, *a fortiori*, on myself, all the perfections that are awanting to me" (153-049). Since he lacks perfections, he is not self-caused.

## Arguments and Implications

The perfection hierarchy is not merely metaphysical furniture — it does heavy argumentative work. In the Third Meditation's causal argument, Descartes reasons: (1) the cause must have at least as much reality as the effect; (2) my idea of God has infinite objective reality (it represents an infinite being); (3) I, a finite being, cannot be the cause of an idea with infinite objective reality; therefore (4) God exists as the cause of this idea.

The perfection hierarchy also explains why error is possible. Error is a privation — a lack of perfection in judgment. The intellect, considered in itself, is limited but not erroneous; it simply perceives ideas. The will, however, is infinite in scope and can affirm or deny beyond what the intellect clearly perceives. Error arises when the will exceeds the intellect — a privation, not a positive reality, and therefore consistent with God's goodness.

In Descartes' physics, perfection also plays a role. God's perfection entails immutability, which in turn entails the conservation of the total quantity of motion. The laws of nature are consequences of divine perfection.

## Historical Context

Descartes' equation of perfection with ontological degree has roots in **Plato** and **Neoplatonism**, where being and goodness are convertible — the more real something is, the better it is. **Augustine** Christianized this framework, identifying God as *ipsum esse subsistens* — being itself subsisting, the source of all being and goodness.

The scholastics, particularly **Thomas Aquinas**, developed the doctrine of the *transcendentals* — being, truth, goodness, unity — which are convertible with one another. Descartes preserves the connection between being and perfection but strips it of scholastic metaphysical baggage, using it as a functional principle for causal reasoning rather than a comprehensive metaphysics of participation.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Hume** would later reject the causal adequacy principle entirely. For Hume, the idea of God could easily be constructed by augmenting finite qualities — we take our ideas of power, knowledge, and goodness and increase them without limit. No actually infinite cause is required.

**Kant** criticized the ontological argument (which depends on the concept of perfection as a predicate included in God's essence) by arguing that existence is not a predicate — it does not add to the concept of a thing. The most perfect being must exist only if existence is a perfection, which Kant denies.
