# Substance

In Descartes' philosophy, **substance** (*substantia*) is the fundamental ontological category — a thing that exists in such a way that it needs no other thing in order to exist. Descartes develops this concept primarily in the *Principles of Philosophy*, where he defines substance rigorously and distinguishes three kinds: infinite substance (God), thinking substance (mind), and extended substance (body).

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces his formal definition of substance in the *Principles of Philosophy*. In the Appendix to the *Principles*, arranged in geometrical order, he defines substance as "everything in which there immediately resides, as in a subject, or by which there exists any object we perceive, that is, any property, or quality, or attribute of which we have in us a real idea" (153-008). He emphasizes that we have no direct idea of substance itself, but only of its attributes — the substance is known through the property or quality that inheres in it, since "we are taught by the natural light that nothing can have no real attribute" (153-008).

From this foundational definition, Descartes proceeds to distinguish three kinds of substance. The first is **mind** (*mens*): "the substance in which thought immediately resides" (153-009). The second is **body**: "the substance which is the immediate subject of local extension, and of the accidents that presuppose this extension, as figure, situation, local motion, etc." (153-010). The third is **God**: "the substance which we understand to be supremely perfect, and in which we conceive nothing that involves any defect, or limitation of perfection" (153-011). This tripartite division is central to the entire Cartesian system.

In the *Discourse on the Method*, Descartes had arrived at the concept of thinking substance through the *cogito*: "I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing" (007-002). This is the famous conclusion that the "I" — the mind — is "wholly distinct from the body, and is even more easily known than the latter" (007-002). The *Discourse* thus anticipates the mind-body dualism that the *Principles* would later systematize.

Descartes also develops his theory of corporeal substance across the *Meditations* and *Principles*. In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he argues that "the nature of corporeal substance consists only in its being an extended thing" (032-002). This identification of body with extension means that all properties of physical objects — weight, hardness, colour — are reducible to modes of extension. Space itself cannot be empty, for extension necessarily requires an extended substance: "it is not more contradictory to conceive a mountain without a valley than such a cavity without the extension it contains, or this extension apart from an extended substance" (031-002).

> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-002)

> All those objects which are clearly and distinctly conceived to be diverse substances, as mind and body, are substances really reciprocally distinct.
>

## Arguments and Implications

The concept of substance underpins Descartes' most famous philosophical doctrine: **mind-body dualism**. Two substances are "really distinct, when each of them may exist without the other" (153-013). Since mind is defined by thought and body by extension, and we can clearly conceive each without the other, they must be distinct substances. The argument reaches its conclusion in the *Principles* Appendix: "The mind and the body are substances which can exist independently of each other" (153-071), therefore they are really distinct (153-070).

This dualism has profound implications. For epistemology, it means the mind is far better known than the body — "the mind is thus enabled easily to distinguish what pertains to itself, that is, to the intellectual nature, from what is to be referred to the body" (013-002). For physics, it means that all corporeal phenomena can be explained purely in terms of extension and motion, without appeal to substantial forms or occult qualities. For theology, it provides a rational basis for the immortality of the soul: "the human mind is not made up of accidents, but is a pure substance" (013-002), and unlike the body — which changes with every alteration of its parts — the mind "does not vary with these changes" and is "in its own nature immortal" (013-002).

Descartes also establishes an ontological hierarchy through substance. "There are diverse degrees of reality, that is, of entity [or perfection]: for substance has more reality than accident or mode, and infinite substance than finite" (153-028). This hierarchy underpins his causal argument for God's existence in the Third Meditation: the idea of an infinite substance must have an infinite substance as its cause, since there must be at least as much reality in the cause as in the effect.

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of substance draws on a long tradition. **Aristotle** defined substance (*ousia*) as the primary category of being — that which underlies all other categories. **Scholastic** philosophy, particularly through **Thomas Aquinas**, developed Aristotle's framework into a sophisticated metaphysics of substance and accident.

Descartes radically simplifies this tradition. Where scholastics recognized many kinds of substances (human beings, animals, plants, inanimate objects, angels), Descartes reduces created substances to just two: mind and body. He eliminates the scholastic notion of "substantial forms" — the immaterial principles that supposedly give each kind of thing its nature — and replaces them with mechanical explanation. The only "forms" left are thought (the principal attribute of mind) and extension (the principal attribute of body).

This simplification was revolutionary but generated immediate objections. **Hobbes** and **Gassendi** challenged whether we can legitimately infer the existence of substance from the presence of attributes. Princess **Elisabeth of Bohemia** pressed Descartes on the interaction problem: if mind and body are wholly distinct substances, how can they causally interact? Descartes' responses, recorded in his correspondence, never fully satisfied his critics, and the "mind-body problem" remains a central topic in philosophy of mind.

## Criticisms and Responses

The most enduring criticism of Descartes' substance dualism is the **interaction problem**: how can an unextended thinking substance causally interact with an extended material substance? Descartes' suggestion that this occurs through the pineal gland raised more questions than it answered. **Spinoza** would later reject Cartesian dualism entirely, arguing in his *Ethics* (1677) that there can be only one substance — God or Nature — and that thought and extension are merely two attributes of this single substance. **Leibniz** proposed his own theory of pre-established harmony to avoid the interaction problem. Later empiricists, particularly **Locke**, criticized the very idea of substance as an "I know not what" — a bare substratum of which we can have no clear idea.
