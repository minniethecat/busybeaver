# Ethics

In Descartes' philosophy, **ethics** (*la morale*, moral philosophy) occupies the highest place in the tree of knowledge. "By the science of Morals, I understand the highest and most perfect which, presupposing an entire knowledge of the other sciences, is the last degree of wisdom" (022-012). Ethics is the ultimate fruit of the philosophical tree: its roots are metaphysics, its trunk is physics, and its three principal branches are medicine, mechanics, and ethics. But unlike the other branches, ethics cannot be fully developed until the entire system is complete — it requires knowledge of human nature, the passions, the body, and the external world.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes approaches ethics from two directions. On the one hand, he envisions a **perfect moral science** — a demonstrative ethics grounded in metaphysics and physics, deducing the rules of right conduct from knowledge of human nature. This science remains a distant goal, the "last degree of wisdom" attained only after all other knowledge is secure.

On the other hand, Descartes recognizes that we cannot suspend action while awaiting perfect knowledge. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he famously proposes a **provisional morality** (*une morale par provision*) — "a code of morals sufficient to regulate the actions of my life" while the foundations of knowledge are being rebuilt (004-009). This provisional code consists of three (or four) maxims: obey the laws and customs of one's country, be firm and resolute in action once a course is chosen, seek to master oneself rather than fortune, and devote one's life to the cultivation of reason.

The provisional morality is not a placeholder for a future demonstrative ethics but a practical framework that remains valuable even after scientific ethics is achieved. It reflects Descartes' recognition that ethics is not purely theoretical — it is an art of living that must guide action under conditions of uncertainty.

## Arguments and Implications

**The Place of Ethics in the System**: Ethics is both the last and the most valuable part of philosophy. It is last in the order of discovery — it presupposes physics (which explains the passions and the body-mind union) and metaphysics (which establishes the nature of the soul, free will, and God). But it is first in the order of value — it is "the principal utility of philosophy" (022-013). The whole edifice of Cartesian science is ultimately justified by its contribution to human well-being.

**Provisional vs. Perfect Morality**: Descartes' distinction between provisional and perfect morality anticipates a tension that runs through modern ethics. The provisional morality is **stoic** in character — emphasizing self-mastery, the distinction between what is in our power and what is not, and the importance of resolute action. The perfect morality would be **scientific** — grounded in knowledge of the passions, the body, and the mind-body union that Descartes develops in *The Passions of the Soul* and the correspondence with Princess Elisabeth.

**Generosity as the Master Virtue**: In *The Passions of the Soul*, Descartes identifies **generosity** (*générosité*) as the foundation of all virtue. Generosity is the justified esteem one has for oneself based on the recognition that one possesses free will and the resolve to use it well. This is not the provisional morality of the *Discourse* but a step toward the scientific ethics grounded in Descartes' theory of the passions.

## Historical Context

Descartes' approach to ethics reflects the intellectual landscape of the early 17th century. The **Aristotelian** tradition placed ethics (along with politics) among the practical sciences, governed by *phronēsis* (practical wisdom) rather than *epistēmē* (scientific knowledge). The **Stoic** revival — represented by Justus Lipsius and Guillaume du Vair — emphasized self-mastery and the distinction between what depends on us and what does not, themes that appear prominently in Descartes' provisional morality.

Descartes' innovation is to envision ethics as a **science** — a body of certain knowledge deduced from first principles, on the model of geometry. This ambition would be taken up by **Spinoza**, whose *Ethics* (1677) is the most systematic attempt to realize Descartes' vision of a demonstrative moral philosophy.

## Criticisms and Responses

**The Gap between Theory and Practice**: Descartes acknowledges that moral life cannot wait for the completion of science. The provisional morality is a concession to this fact. But critics have argued that the gap between provisional and perfect morality is never closed — Descartes never produced the scientific ethics he envisioned. His followers (Spinoza, Malebranche) each developed their own ethical systems, suggesting that the Cartesian framework does not determine a unique ethics.

**The Problem of Moral Knowledge**: If all knowledge is grounded in clear and distinct ideas, can ethics be a genuine science? Descartes' own theory of the passions suggests that moral judgment involves the body and the emotions in ways that resist purely intellectual analysis. The interaction between reason and passion — central to Cartesian ethics — may require a form of knowledge that is not purely demonstrative.
