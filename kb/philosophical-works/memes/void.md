# Void

In Descartes' philosophy, a **void** or vacuum — space containing no body or substance whatsoever — is "repugnant to reason" (151-001). Because Descartes identifies body with extension (length, breadth, and depth), the notion of extended space without bodily substance is a contradiction: "it is absolutely contradictory that nothing should possess extension" (151-002). The denial of the void is a cornerstone of Cartesian physics and distinguishes Descartes' plenism from ancient atomism.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' rejection of the void follows directly from his identification of body with extension. "The same extension in length, breadth, and depth, which constitutes space, constitutes body" (145-002). Wherever there is extension — wherever we can conceive length, breadth, and depth — there is necessarily body. The difference between space and body is "merely in the mode in which they are wont to be conceived by us" (145-002), not in reality.

This position has dramatic consequences for physics. Descartes must explain phenomena that his predecessors attributed to the void — the motion of bodies, rarefaction and condensation — without admitting any empty space. Rarefaction, he argues, is not the creation of void between particles but the introduction of smaller particles into the pores of a body, like "a sponge swells with water" (140-002). All space is a plenum: every region of extension is filled with some body, however subtle.

Descartes extends this reasoning to celestial physics. The ancient atomists — [[lucretius|Lucretius]], Democritus, and Epicurus — had posited atoms moving through infinite void. Descartes eliminates both atoms (matter is infinitely divisible) and void (extension is always substantial). The heavens are not empty but filled with a subtle fluid matter — the vortex — whose circular motion carries the planets. "The matter of the heaven, like that which constitutes the sun and the fixed stars, is liquid" (035-002), and this fluid fills "all the imaginable spaces where these other worlds could be conceived to exist" (035-002).

## Key Passages

For since from this alone, that a body has extension in length, breadth, and depth, we have reason to conclude that it is a substance, it being absolutely contradictory that nothing should possess extension, we ought similarly to conclude that the same holds of the space which is supposed void, viz., that since there is extension in it, there is necessarily also substance.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (151-002)

> Space or internal place, and the corporeal substance which is comprised in it, are not different in reality, but merely in the mode in which they are wont to be conceived by us. For, in truth, the same extension in length, breadth, and depth, which constitutes space, constitutes body.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (145-002)

> It will be easy to discern that it is the same extension which constitutes the nature of body as of space, and that these two things are mutually diverse only as the nature of the genus and species differs from that of the individual.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, Part II (146-002)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes advances two principal arguments against the void:

**The Argument from Extension**: Whatever is extended is a substance. Space is extended. Therefore, space is a substance — and not merely an empty container. "If we remove a stone from the space or place in which it was, we conceive that its extension also is taken away, because we judge this to be particular and inseparable from the stone" (147-002). But the extension of the place remains — it is simply filled by another body.

**The Argument from Intelligibility**: A void — extension without substance — is as unintelligible as a mode without a subject. Just as there cannot be shape without something shaped, or motion without something moving, there cannot be extension without something extended. To posit a vacuum is to posit a property without a bearer, which Descartes regards as a conceptual confusion.

The denial of the void has far-reaching implications. It eliminates action at a distance: all physical causation must be by contact, through the plenum of corporeal substance. It grounds Descartes' vortex cosmology: planetary motion is carried by contiguous fluid matter, not by gravitational attraction across empty space. And it underwrites Descartes' conservation laws: since there is no void, the total quantity of motion in the universe — imparted by God at creation — is conserved as motion is transferred from body to body by collision.

## Historical Context

Descartes' denial of the void stands in a long tradition. **Aristotle** had also rejected the void, arguing in the *Physics* that motion in a vacuum would be impossible (there would be no medium to differentiate directions). The scholastics preserved Aristotle's horror vacui but grounded it in final causes — nature abhors a vacuum because it aims at the good of the whole.

Descartes replaces the scholastic "abhorrence of a vacuum" with a purely geometrical argument: void is impossible not because nature abhors it but because extension *just is* substance. This is a characteristically Cartesian move — replacing teleological explanation with clear and distinct geometrical intuition.

The atomist tradition — **Democritus**, **Epicurus**, **[[lucretius|Lucretius]]** — had embraced the void as the necessary condition of motion: atoms move through empty space. Descartes' plenism is a direct rejection of this tradition. For Descartes, motion does not require empty space; it requires only the displacement of contiguous bodies in a closed circuit — a vortex.

## Criticisms and Responses

**[[newton|Newton]]** and the experimental tradition delivered the decisive refutation of Descartes' plenism. Newton's *Principia* (1687) showed that planetary motion is better explained by universal gravitation acting across empty space than by Descartes' vortices. The Torricellian vacuum (1643) — the space above the mercury column in a barometer — appeared to be a directly observed void, though Descartes and his followers argued it was filled with subtle matter.

**[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** accepted Descartes' argument against atoms and void but criticized the details of Cartesian plenism. Leibniz held that space is relational — an order of coexistences — rather than a substance or a property of substance. This relational theory of space, developed further by **[[kant|Kant]]**, offered a third way between Descartes' plenism and Newton's absolute space.

**Gassendi** revived Epicurean atomism in the seventeenth century, defending both atoms and the void against Cartesian objections. Descartes engaged with Gassendi's arguments in the *Objections and Replies* to the Meditations, though the debate was never settled on Descartes' terms — the experimental successes of Newtonian physics ultimately made plenism untenable.
