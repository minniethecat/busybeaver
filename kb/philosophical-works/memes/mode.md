## Overview

Mode (*modus*) is one of the three fundamental categories in Descartes' substance-attribute-mode ontology. While substance is that which exists independently, and attribute is the essential nature constituting a substance, mode is a determinate way in which an attribute is expressed. Every particular property of a created thing is a mode.

## Definition in the Principles

Descartes provides a precise taxonomy in the *Principles of Philosophy*: "We here understand by modes the same with what we elsewhere designate attributes or qualities. But when we consider substance as affected or varied by them, we use the term modes; when from this variation it may be denominated of such a kind, we adopt the term qualities; and, finally, when we simply regard these modes as in the substance, we call them attributes" (114-002).

This terminological flexibility has a systematic basis: the same feature can be called a mode, quality, or attribute depending on the perspective from which it is considered. However, a strict distinction applies to God: "Since God must be conceived as superior to change, it is not proper to say that there are modes or qualities in him, but simply attributes" (114-002).

## Modes of Thinking and Extension

The two principal attributes each have their characteristic modes. "Perception, volition, and all modes as well of knowing as of willing, are related to thinking substance; on the other hand, to extended substance we refer magnitude, or extension in length, breadth, and depth, figure, motion, situation, divisibility of parts themselves, and the like" (106-002).

Descartes further classifies the modes of thinking: "All the modes of thinking of which we are conscious may be referred to two general classes, the one of which is the perception or operation of the understanding, and the other the volition or operation of the will" (090-002). Sensory perception, imagination, and pure intellection are all modes of perceiving; desire, aversion, affirmation, denial, and doubt are modes of willing.

## Mode vs. Substance

A critical feature of modes is that they cannot exist independently. "For two bodies must touch each other when there is nothing between them, and it is manifestly contradictory for two bodies to be apart, in other words, that there should be a distance between them, and this distance yet be nothing; for all distance is a mode of extension, and cannot therefore exist without an extended substance" (031-002). Modes depend ontologically on the substance they modify.

Similarly, Descartes clarifies that motion is a mode, not a substance: "I understand that it is a mode of the movable thing, and not a substance, just as figure is a property of the thing figured, and repose of that which is at rest" (038-002).

## Imagination as a Mode

Descartes identifies imagination as a mode of thinking specifically tied to material objects: "They never raise their thoughts above sensible objects, and are so accustomed to consider nothing except by way of imagination, which is a mode of thinking limited to material objects, that all that is not imaginable seems to them not intelligible" (007-006). This limitation of imagination demonstrates why modes of pure thought — like the idea of God or the soul — cannot be grasped through imaginative modes.

## Modal Distinction

The distinction between a mode and its substance is called a *modal distinction*. Descartes explains: "Thought and extension may be also considered as modes of substance; in as far, namely, as the same mind may have many different thoughts, and the same body, with its size unchanged, may be extended in several diverse ways" (122-002). A modal distinction is less fundamental than a real distinction (between substances) but more than a distinction of reason.

## Error as a Defect in Mode of Acting

Descartes applies the concept of mode to moral philosophy as well: "Error is a defect in our mode of acting, not in our nature; and that the faults of their subjects may be frequently attributed to other masters, but never to God" (096-001). Error arises not from a defect in our faculty of judgment itself but from our mode of using it — specifically, from allowing the will to outrun the understanding.
