# God

In Descartes' philosophy, **God** (*Deus*) is the infinite [[substance]] — "supremely perfect, and in which we conceive nothing that involves any defect, or limitation of perfection" (153-011). God occupies a uniquely central role in the Cartesian system: as the causal source of the [[idea]] of the infinite, as the guarantor of clear and distinct [[perception]], and as the ultimate foundation of all [[existence]]. Descartes' arguments for God's existence appear across his major works, including the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* and the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*, and are foundational to his broader metaphysical system.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes defines God in the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix as "the substance which we understand to be supremely perfect, and in which we conceive nothing that involves any defect, or limitation of perfection" (153-011). This definition is crucial: God is not merely one substance among others but is the only substance in the strict sense — the only being that truly needs nothing else in order to exist.

The existence of God is demonstrated through multiple arguments across Descartes' works. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]*, Part IV, Descartes presents a version of the ontological argument: existence belongs to God's essence as necessarily as the property of having three angles equal to two right angles belongs to a triangle. In the *Meditations*, the Third Meditation develops the causal argument: the idea of an infinite, perfect being cannot originate from a finite, imperfect being; therefore, God must exist as its cause.

Descartes establishes an ontological hierarchy grounded in God. "There are diverse degrees of reality, that is, of entity [or perfection]: for substance has more reality than accident or mode, and infinite substance than finite" (153-028). God, as infinite substance, stands at the summit of this hierarchy. "It is for this reason also that there is more objective reality in the idea of substance than in that of accident, and in the idea of infinite than in the idea of finite substance" (153-028).

## Descartes' Arguments for God

Descartes presents three principal arguments for God's existence, each operating at a different level of his philosophical system:

| Argument | Source | Type | Key Premise |
|----------|--------|------|-------------|
| Causal Argument | *Meditations* III | A posteriori | The idea of an infinite perfect being requires an infinite perfect cause |
| Ontological Argument | *Meditations* V; *Principles* I.14 | A priori | Existence is a perfection; God's essence entails existence |
| Conservation Argument | *Meditations* III; *Principles* I.21 | A posteriori | My continued existence requires a sustaining cause, which must be God |

The **causal argument** proceeds from the fact that Descartes possesses the idea of God as an infinite, perfect being. Since "there must be at least as much reality in the efficient and total cause as in its effect" (153-026), and the idea of the infinite has more objective reality than any finite idea, only an actually infinite being — God — could be its cause.

The **ontological argument** holds that "necessary existence is contained in the nature or in the concept of God" (153-037). Just as the idea of a triangle necessarily includes the property that its three angles equal two right angles, the idea of a supremely perfect being necessarily includes existence, for a being that lacked existence would lack a perfection.

The **conservation argument** draws on the nature of time: "The whole time of my life may be divided into an infinity of parts, each of which is in no way dependent on the others" (153-049). My existence at one moment does not entail my existence at the next; therefore, I require a continuously acting cause to conserve me — and this cause must be God.

## Arguments and Implications

God serves as the epistemological guarantor in Descartes' system. Clear and distinct [[perception]] is reliable only because a veracious God — a God who is not a deceiver — exists and would not allow us to be systematically deceived about what we clearly and distinctly perceive. Without God, the evil demon hypothesis of the First Meditation remains a live possibility, and all [[knowledge]] beyond the *cogito* is suspended.

God also plays a crucial role in Descartes' physics. Because God is immutable, the total quantity of motion in the universe is conserved. The laws of nature are expressions of God's constancy. Descartes' physics is thus theological physics — the laws of motion are derived from the divine attributes.

The causal argument for God's existence also establishes the framework for Descartes' theory of ideas. In the Third Meditation, Descartes argues that "If I possessed the power of conserving myself, I should likewise have the power of conferring, *a fortiori*, on myself, all the perfections that are awanting to me" (153-049). Since he lacks those perfections, he cannot be self-caused — he must depend on God for his continued [[existence]].

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of God draws on the medieval tradition of **[[anselm|Anselm]]** (the ontological argument) and **Thomas [[aquinas|Aquinas]]** (cosmological arguments) but transforms both. Unlike Aquinas, who began from sensory experience of motion and causation, Descartes begins from ideas in the [[mind]] — his arguments are thoroughly epistemological.

Descartes' claim that God's existence is more certain than the existence of the external world was bold and controversial. His contemporary **Blaise Pascal** famously wrote in the *Pensées*: "I cannot forgive Descartes. In all his philosophy he would have been quite willing to dispense with God. But he had to make Him give a fillip to set the world in motion; after this he has no further use for God."

## Criticisms and Responses

**[[kant|Kant]]** later critiqued the ontological argument by arguing that existence is not a predicate — it does not add to the concept of a thing. **Hume** rejected the causal argument, holding that the idea of God could be constructed by augmenting finite qualities without requiring an actually infinite cause. The "Cartesian circle" — the apparent circularity of proving God's existence using clear and distinct ideas, then using God to guarantee clear and distinct ideas — has been debated extensively. Descartes' defenders, including **[[antoine-arnauld|Arnauld]]**, argue that the *cogito* is self-verifying and does not depend on God; the guarantee applies only to memory-dependent reasoning.
