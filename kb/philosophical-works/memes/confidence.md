## Definition

**Confidence** in the Cartesian context refers to the subjective attitude of certainty or trust that accompanies belief. Descartes distinguished between warranted confidence grounded in clear and distinct perception and unwarranted confidence stemming from prejudice or pride.

## Legitimate Confidence

Having developed his method, Descartes expressed a measured confidence in its power: "I nevertheless derive the highest satisfaction from the progress I conceive myself to have already made in the search after truth, and cannot help entertaining such expectations of the future as to believe that if, among the occupations of men as men, there is any one really excellent and important, it is that which I have chosen" (004-014). This confidence is grounded in the actual fruits of his method, not in self-flattery.

## Excessive Confidence

Descartes was critical of philosophers who speak "with as much confidence as if they really knew them" about matters they do not understand (009-006). He similarly disapproved of "those who with more than a due confidence in their own powers, are precipitate in their judgments" (005-003). This critique extends to the scholastic tradition, whose elaborate systems Descartes saw as built on unwarranted confidence in unclear principles.

## Confidence in Others

Descartes also addressed interpersonal confidence, noting that he had "such confidence in [another's] genius as to believe there was nothing he did not know" (022-015). Such confidence in authority, while sometimes warranted, must ultimately give way to the individual's own rational examination.
