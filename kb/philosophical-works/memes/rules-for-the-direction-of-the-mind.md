# Rules for the Direction of the Mind

**Rules for the Direction of the Mind** (*Regulae ad Directionem Ingenii*, composed c. 1628, published posthumously in 1701) is [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' earliest major philosophical treatise — the work in which the [[method]] that would later be presented in the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]* receives its most detailed and technical formulation. Abandoned unfinished at Rule 21 (of a projected 36), the *Regulae* reveals the young Descartes working out the epistemological program that would define his mature philosophy: the reduction of all [[knowledge]] to clear and distinct intuition, the analysis of complex problems into simple elements, and the systematic enumeration of all relevant factors (005-010).

## Overview

The *Regulae* proposes a universal method for directing the mind toward certain knowledge. Unlike the scholastic logic it replaces, Descartes' method is not a set of formal rules for argumentation but a **discipline of attention** — a way of training the mind to perceive truth directly through intellectual intuition.

The work's structure is programmatic: Rules 1–12 establish the nature of knowledge and the basic operations of the mind (intuition and deduction); Rules 13–24 (of which only 13–21 survive) apply the method to "perfectly understood problems," primarily mathematical; the projected Rules 25–36 would have extended it to "imperfectly understood problems" in natural philosophy.

## Key Themes

**Intuition and Deduction**: Descartes distinguishes two indubitable acts of the intellect — *intuitus* (the immediate, clear apprehension of a simple truth) and *deductio* (the necessary movement from one intuition to another). All certain knowledge is built from these two operations (005-011).

**Mathesis Universalis**: The *Regulae* proposes a "universal mathematics" — a general science of order and measure that underlies arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, and all other disciplines concerned with proportion. This is the seed of Descartes' analytic geometry and his vision of a unified science.

**Enumeration**: Descartes insists on complete enumeration — the systematic review of all elements relevant to a problem — as essential to certainty. Incompleteness is the primary source of error.

## In Descartes' Development

The *Regulae* marks the transition from Descartes' early mathematical work (inspired by [[isaac-beeckman|Beeckman]]) to his mature philosophical project. The rules for directing the mind become, in the later works, the four precepts of the *Discourse on Method*: accept nothing as true that is not clearly known to be such; divide problems into parts; proceed from simple to complex; and make complete enumerations.
