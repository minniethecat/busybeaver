# Galileo Galilei

**Galileo Galilei** (1564–1642) was the most influential natural philosopher of the generation before [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]. His fusion of mathematical analysis with experimental observation, his defense of the Copernican system, and his dramatic conflict with the Roman Inquisition defined the intellectual landscape into which Descartes published his own works. Descartes admired Galileo's [[method]] but carefully distanced himself from the cosmological conclusions that had brought Galileo to trial in 1633.

## Biography

Galileo was born in Pisa and initially studied medicine before turning to mathematics. He held professorships at Pisa and Padua before accepting a position as court mathematician to the Medici in Florence in 1610 — the year he turned his newly improved telescope to the heavens and discovered the moons of Jupiter, the phases of Venus, and the mountains of the Moon.

His *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632) — comparing the Ptolemaic and Copernican cosmologies — led to his trial before the Inquisition in 1633. Found "vehemently suspect of heresy," he was forced to abjure Copernicanism and spent the remainder of his life under house arrest, where he wrote his greatest scientific work, the *Two New Sciences* (1638). He died in 1642, the year of [[newton|Newton]]'s birth.

## Relationship to Descartes

Galileo's influence on Descartes was profound, but complex and ambivalent:

1. **Mathematical Physics**: Galileo's principle that "the book of nature is written in the language of mathematics" was the founding charter of mathematical physics. Descartes' own project — to reduce physics to geometry, explaining all natural phenomena in terms of extension and motion — was a direct extension of Galileo's mathematization of nature.

2. **The Condemnation and Its Effect**: Descartes learned of Galileo's condemnation in 1633 while preparing his own cosmological treatise, *The World* (*Le Monde*). He suppressed the work. "I inquired about the condemnation of Galileo," he wrote to Mersenne, "and I was told that his view about the motion of the earth had been condemned. This so astonished me that I almost decided to burn all my papers." The *World* was not published until after Descartes' death.

3. **Methodological Differences**: Despite his admiration, Descartes criticized Galileo's approach as unsystematic. Galileo, he wrote in a 1638 letter to Mersenne, "has examined only a few questions without having considered the first causes of nature, and has thus built without a foundation." Descartes' *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]* and *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* were intended to provide the metaphysical foundation that Descartes believed Galileo's physics lacked.

4. **Shared Mechanical Vision**: Both Galileo and Descartes rejected Aristotelian substantial forms and final causes. Both sought to explain natural phenomena in terms of matter in motion. Galileo's law of falling bodies and principle of inertia (in its Galilean form) became cornerstones of Cartesian physics, though Descartes would give them a different theoretical grounding.

## Key Ideas

**The Mathematization of Nature**: Galileo's methodological revolution — "measure what is measurable, and make measurable what is not" — opened the path to modern mathematical physics. This Cartesian conviction that physics must be grounded in mathematics is Galileo's most enduring contribution to the new philosophy.

**Primary and Secondary Qualities**: Galileo distinguished between "primary affections" of bodies (figure, size, motion — real properties) and "secondary qualities" (tastes, odors, colors — residing only in the perceiver). This distinction was adopted and refined by Descartes and later [[locke|Locke]], becoming a cornerstone of early modern epistemology.

**The Telescope and the Senses**: Galileo's telescopic discoveries — mountains on the Moon, sunspots, the moons of Jupiter — demonstrated that the unaided senses are an unreliable guide to nature. This lent powerful empirical support to Descartes' methodological skepticism about sensory [[knowledge]].

## In Descartes' Legacy

> Galileo's condemnation cast a long shadow over Descartes' career. While Descartes never publicly endorsed Copernicanism after 1633, his physics — the vortex theory, the identification of [[body]] with extension, the rejection of absolute [[space]] — was shaped in constant dialogue with Galileo's achievements and in constant awareness of Galileo's fate. The methodological caution of the *Discourse* — its famous "provisional morality" — is, in part, the caution of a philosopher who had seen what happened to natural philosophers who challenged Church doctrine too openly.
