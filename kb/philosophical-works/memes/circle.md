The **Cartesian circle** is a classic objection to René Descartes' epistemology. The charge is that Descartes argues in a circle: he proves God's existence and veracity using clear and distinct perception, but then claims that clear and distinct perception is trustworthy only because a veracious God guarantees it.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes establishes the criterion of truth in the *Discourse on Method*: whatever is perceived "clearly and distinctly" must be true (004-002). In the *Meditations*, he uses this criterion to prove that God exists and is not a deceiver. He then argues that because God is veracious, whatever is clearly and distinctly perceived is true — which is the very criterion he employed to prove God's existence in the first place.

Descartes himself anticipated this objection. In the *Replies to the Second Set of Objections*, he acknowledges "that fallacy which the logicians call a circle" (009-010). His response distinguishes between individual clear and distinct perceptions — which depend on memory and can be doubted when not directly attended to — and the general principle that what is clearly and distinctly perceived is true, which is secured by God's veracity once established.

## Key Passages

> I observed that, whilst I thus wished to think that all was false, it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be something; and as I observed that this truth, *I think, therefore I am*, was so certain and of such evidence that no ground of doubt, however extravagant, could be alleged by the skeptics capable of shaking it, I concluded that I might, without scruple, accept it as the first principle of the philosophy of which I was in search.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-003)

>

## Arguments and Implications

The Cartesian circle has generated an extensive scholarly literature. Major interpretative strategies include: (1) the "memory defense" — that God guarantees only remembered clear and distinct perceptions, not presently intuited ones; (2) the "psychological certainty" reading — that the Meditations aims only at subjective conviction, not logical demonstration; and (3) the "general rule" reading — that Descartes never needs to prove the general rule, only to remove the specific doubt about a deceiving God.

If the charge of circularity stands, Descartes' entire foundationalist project is threatened. Without an independent warrant for clear and distinct perception, neither the *cogito*, nor the proofs of God, nor the existence of the external world can be securely established.

## Historical Context

The circle objection was raised almost immediately by Descartes' contemporaries. Antoine Arnauld, in the Fourth Set of Objections, formulated the classic version of the charge. Marin Mersenne raised a related concern in the Second Objections. The debate continued through the correspondence with Princess Elisabeth and into later Cartesian literature.

The circle is structurally similar to problems faced by any foundationalist epistemology that attempts to justify its own criterion of justification. It anticipates later debates about epistemic circularity and the possibility of non-question-begging foundations for knowledge.

## Criticisms and Responses

Descartes' own response — distinguishing present intuition from remembered demonstration — has been the starting point for most later defenses. Interpreters such as Martial Gueroult and Harry Frankfurt have developed sophisticated versions of the distinction.

Critics respond that the distinction is ad hoc: if God's veracity is needed to trust remembered clear perceptions, then the steps of the proof of God's existence — which span multiple meditations and require memory — cannot be trusted either. More recent scholarship by Margaret Wilson, Edwin Curley, and others treats the circle as a genuine vulnerability in Descartes' system rather than a solved problem.
