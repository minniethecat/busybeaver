# Judgment

In Descartes' philosophy, **judgment** (*judicium*) is the act by which the mind affirms or denies something of an idea. Judgment is not a pure intellectual operation — it involves both the **intellect** (which perceives the idea) and the **will** (which affirms or denies). Because judgment engages the will, it is the locus of both truth and error, and of human freedom.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' account of judgment is developed in the Fourth Meditation. The intellect, considered alone, never errs — it merely perceives ideas, and every idea, as a mode of thought, is real and true in its formal reality. Error enters at the point of judgment, when the will affirms or denies something that the intellect does not clearly and distinctly perceive.

The remedy is methodological: "abstain from judging" when ideas are not clear and distinct. This is the practical application of methodical doubt — not to remain in doubt permanently, but to suspend judgment until the intellect achieves clarity. As Descartes advises his readers in the *Principles* Appendix: they must "consider how feeble are the reasons that have hitherto led them to repose faith in their senses, and how uncertain are all the judgments which they afterwards founded on them" (153-015).

Judgment is also where human dignity resides. The will's capacity to affirm or deny freely — "in such a way that we feel we are not determined by any external force" — is what makes humans images of God. In judgment, we exercise our highest perfection.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' analysis of judgment has important consequences for **epistemology**. It means that knowledge requires not just having a correct idea but *affirming it on the right grounds* — namely, clear and distinct perception. A true belief held on the basis of authority or sensory appearance may be correct, but it does not count as knowledge (*scientia*) because it was not formed by the proper exercise of judgment.

The analysis also has consequences for **ethics**. Since judgment involves the will, we are responsible for our errors. We cannot blame the senses (they merely present ideas) or the intellect (it merely perceives them). Responsibility falls on the agent who *judges* — who affirms or denies. This is Descartes' version of the Stoic insight that our judgments, not external things, are the source of our peace or disturbance.

For **philosophy of mind**, Descartes' account of judgment establishes the mind as active, not merely passive. The mind does not simply receive ideas from the world; it *engages* with them through acts of affirmation, denial, and doubt. This active conception of mind is a defining feature of modern philosophy.

## Historical Context

The scholastics had a sophisticated theory of judgment, distinguishing the **simple apprehension** (the intellect's grasp of a concept), the **composition or division** (the intellect's combination or separation of concepts), and **assent** (the will's acceptance of the resulting proposition). Descartes simplifies this: judgment is the act of the will affirming or denying an idea. The intellect perceives, the will judges.

This simplification relocates judgment from the intellect to the will. For **Thomas Aquinas**, judgment was primarily an act of the intellect — the intellect composes and divides, and the will commands the intellect to do so. For Descartes, judgment just is the will's act. This makes error a moral failure (misuse of will) rather than an intellectual one.

## Criticisms and Responses

**Spinoza** rejected the distinction between intellect and will that Descartes' account requires. For Spinoza, "the will and the intellect are one and the same" — every idea involves an affirmation, and the experience of free judgment is an illusion born of ignorance of the causes that determine us. There is no faculty of will distinct from the ideas that constitute the mind.

**Hume** also challenged Descartes' account, arguing that belief (assent) is not a voluntary act but a "manner of conception" — a vividness or force that attaches to certain ideas automatically, not through an act of will. We cannot will ourselves to believe what we do not find convincing.
