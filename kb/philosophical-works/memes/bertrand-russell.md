# Bertrand Russell

**Bertrand Russell** (1872–1970) was not a contemporary or interlocutor of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] — he lived three centuries later — but he shaped the modern reception of Descartes more than almost any other figure. Russell's *A History of Western Philosophy* (1945), one of the best-selling philosophy books ever written, canonized Descartes as the founding figure of modern [[philosophy]], treating the [[cogito]] as the decisive break between medieval scholasticism and the modern era. For millions of readers, Russell's Descartes is *the* Descartes.

## Biography

Russell was born into the British aristocracy (his grandfather had been Prime Minister) and educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he became a fellow in 1895. His early work with Alfred North Whitehead on the logical foundations of mathematics — *Principia Mathematica* (1910–1913) — attempted to reduce all of mathematics to logic, a project that, though ultimately unsuccessful, established the framework for analytic philosophy.

Russell was also a public intellectual and political activist: he was imprisoned for opposing the First World War (1918), campaigned for nuclear disarmament, and won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1950. He died in 1970 at age 97, having been one of the most recognizable philosophers — and public faces of philosophy — in the modern world.

## Relationship to Descartes

Russell's relationship to Descartes is that of a critical historian and methodological successor:

1. **The *History of Western Philosophy***: Russell's chapter on Descartes made the "father of modern philosophy" narrative a staple of undergraduate education. For Russell, Descartes' importance lay in his attempt to ground [[knowledge]] in the certainty of self-consciousness — the [[cogito]] — rather than in authority or tradition.

2. **Critique of Cartesian Doubt**: Russell admired Descartes' ambition but criticized the cogito as a logical error — what later philosophers called the "Cartesian Circle" (Descartes uses reason to prove that reason is trustworthy). Russell wrote: "Descartes' philosophy is important, not because it is true, but because it is the first systematic attempt to build a philosophy on the basis of reason alone."

3. **Methodological Parallel**: Russell's own philosophical method — begin with what is indubitable (sense-data, logical atoms), rebuild knowledge from simple elements — is a direct descendant of Descartes' methodological doubt. Both philosophers sought a foundation of certainty amid the ruins of received opinion.

## Key Ideas

**Logical Atomism**: Russell's doctrine that the world consists of logically independent atomic facts, knowable through analysis, mirrors in a 20th-century idiom the Cartesian project of reducing complex knowledge to simple, clear, and distinct elements.

**The Analytic Tradition**: Russell, with [[wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]] (his student) and G.E. Moore, founded analytic philosophy — a tradition that, like Descartes, privileges clarity, logical rigor, and the critique of unexamined presuppositions.

**History as Narrative**: Russell's historical writings — accessible, opinionated, and stylistically brilliant — created the popular genre of philosophy-for-the-general-reader. The Cartesian narrative that dominates introductory philosophy courses owes much to Russell's storytelling.

## In Descartes' Texts

Bertrand Russell does not appear in Descartes' texts — he was born 222 years after Descartes' death. But Russell's own judgment of Descartes captures the relationship:

> He is the first philosopher of the modern age. His work is of the utmost importance; it has had a greater influence on the subsequent course of [[philosophy]] than any other writer since Aristotle.

> — Bertrand Russell, *A History of Western Philosophy* (1945)
