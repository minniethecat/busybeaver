# Ludwig Wittgenstein

**Ludwig Wittgenstein** (1889–1951) was the most important philosopher of the 20th century and, from a Cartesian perspective, the most radical critic of the conception of [[mind]] as a private, inner theatre accessible only to its owner. Descartes' *[[cogito]]* established the thinking self as the foundation of [[knowledge]] — a self known directly and privately, whose mental contents ([[idea|ideas]], sensations, thoughts) are transparent to it alone. Wittgenstein's "private language argument" in the *Philosophical Investigations* (1953) challenges the very intelligibility of this picture: if there could be a language that only its speaker could understand, the Cartesian conception of mind would be vindicated. Wittgenstein argues there cannot be.

## Biography

Wittgenstein was born in Vienna, the youngest of eight children in one of Europe's wealthiest industrial families. He studied engineering at Berlin and Manchester before turning to the foundations of mathematics under [[bertrand-russell|Bertrand Russell]] at Cambridge. His *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921), completed while a prisoner of war in Italy, attempted to map the limits of language and thought: "What can be said at all can be said clearly, and what we cannot talk about we must pass over in silence."

Believing he had solved all philosophical problems, Wittgenstein left philosophy to work as a schoolteacher in rural Austria. He returned to Cambridge in 1929, where he repudiated much of his earlier work and developed the radically different philosophy of the *Investigations*. He held the Chair of Philosophy at Cambridge from 1939 to 1947, resigning to write in isolation in Ireland. He died of cancer in 1951; his last words — "Tell them I've had a wonderful life" — close one of philosophy's most singular biographies.

## Relationship to Descartes

Wittgenstein's relationship to Descartes is one of fundamental opposition — a systematic dismantling of the picture of mind, meaning, and knowledge that Descartes inaugurated.

**The Private Language Argument**: Descartes held that mental states are *private* in a strong sense: I have direct, incorrigible access to my own thoughts and sensations, while others can only infer them from my behavior. If this were correct, it should be possible (in principle) to name one's sensations and talk about them in a language only one could understand. Wittgenstein argues this is impossible: for a word to have meaning, there must be public criteria for its correct application. A "private" rule that only I can check is no rule at all — whatever seems right to me would be right, "and that only means that here we can't talk about 'right'."

**Against the *Cogito***: Descartes' [[method]] of doubt strips away all that can be doubted until it arrives at the *cogito* — the thinking self, certain of its own existence. Wittgenstein does not deny that one can say "I am thinking," but he denies that this reports a discovery about a private, inner substance. First-person psychological utterances are not descriptions of an inner realm but expressions — "avowals" — whose grammar is fundamentally different from that of third-person reports. "I know I am in pain" is not a statement of private [[certainty]]; it is a grammatical confusion.

**Meaning as Use, Not Mental Representation**: Descartes' theory of [[idea|ideas]] treats thinking as the inspection of mental representations — ideas are the immediate objects of thought. Wittgenstein replaces the representational model of meaning with the concept of *use*: the meaning of a word is its use in the language. Thinking is not an occult mental process but a form of activity embedded in social practices — "language games" that are part of "forms of life." The Cartesian picture of thought as an inner representation inspected by the mind's eye is "a picture that held us captive."

**Skepticism and Certainty**: In his final notebooks (*On Certainty*), Wittgenstein examined G. E. Moore's "proof of an external world" — itself a response to Cartesian [[skepticism]]. Wittgenstein argued that Cartesian [[doubt]] is not a genuine doubt but a philosophical construction that misuses the grammar of "know" and "doubt." Some propositions — "Here is a hand," "The world has existed for more than five minutes" — are not things we *know* but the scaffolding of our language games, the "hinges" on which doubt turns. The Cartesian dream of finding unshakeable foundations for knowledge misunderstands the logic of our epistemic practices.
