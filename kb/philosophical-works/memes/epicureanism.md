# Epicureanism

**Epicureanism** was founded by **[[epicurus|Epicurus]]** (341–270 BCE) and is known primarily through **Lucretius'** poetic masterpiece *De Rerum Natura* (On the Nature of Things). Epicureanism combined Democritean **atomism** with a distinctive **ethics** (moderate hedonism), **theology** (gods exist but are indifferent to humans), and **epistemology** (all sensations are true; error arises in [[judgment]]). Descartes engages with Epicureanism primarily through his critique of [[democritus|Democritean]] atomism in the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]* — acknowledging the surface similarity between mechanism and atomism while distinguishing his physics on fundamental grounds. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse]]*, Descartes notes how far Epicurus "ventured" in his philosophical daring (022-005), a remark that captures the ambiguous status of Epicureanism: both an inspiration for mechanistic [[method]] and a cautionary example of philosophy without theological foundation.

## Core Doctrine

Epicurean physics explains all phenomena through atoms moving in the void. The **swerve** (*clinamen*) — a minimal random deviation of atoms from their downward path — explains free will and the formation of worlds. Epicurean ethics identifies pleasure (*hedone*) as the highest good, but pleasure means the absence of bodily pain (*aponia*) and mental disturbance (*ataraxia*), not indulgence. The gods exist in the spaces between worlds (*intermundia*) but are blissfully indifferent to human affairs.

## Key Figures

| Figure | Role | Key Contribution |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Epicurus (341–270 BCE) | Founder | Taught in the Garden in Athens; wrote over 300 works (mostly lost); established atomism + hedonism as a unified system |
| Lucretius (c. 99–55 BCE) | Poet-Philosopher | *De Rerum Natura* — the principal surviving exposition of Epicurean physics, ethics, and epistemology in six books of Latin hexameter |
| Philodemus (c. 110–40 BCE) | Scholar | Epicurean philosopher whose works survive in the Herculaneum papyri; focused on ethics, rhetoric, and the theory of the passions |

## Descartes' Engagement

Descartes does not name Epicureanism directly but engages with its atomistic physics through his critique of **Democritus**. In the *Principles*, he acknowledges the surface similarity between atomism and mechanism — "Democritus also supposed certain corpuscles that were of various figures, sizes, and motions" (060-002) — but distinguishes his physics on three grounds: (1) Descartes rejects indivisible atoms (matter is infinitely divisible); (2) Descartes rejects the void (extension is identical with space); (3) Descartes grounds physical law in [[god|God's]] immutability, not random atomic motion.

The Epicurean **swerve** is fundamentally incompatible with Cartesian physics. Descartes' laws of motion are deterministic, flowing necessarily from God's immutability. The randomness of the *clinamen* has no place in a physics grounded in divine reason.

| Aspect | Epicurean Atomism | Cartesian Mechanism |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| Fundamental entities | Indivisible atoms + void | Infinitely divisible extension (no void) |
| Motion source | Natural downward motion + random swerve | Laws of motion grounded in God's immutability |
| God's role | Gods are remote, indifferent, in *intermundia* | God continuously creates and conserves the world |
| Determinism | Swerve introduces indeterminacy | Strict determinism from divine laws |
| Teleology | None — atoms and void explain everything | None in physics, but God's purposes are inscrutable |

## Descartes' Criticisms

Descartes' mechanism shares the Epicurean ambition to explain all natural phenomena through matter and motion without divine intervention or final [[cause|causes]]. But Descartes' God is continuously active — creating and conserving the world at every moment — while the Epicurean gods are remote and indifferent. Descartes' physics is theistic mechanism; Epicurean physics is atheistic atomism.
