# Discourse on the Method

The **Discourse on the Method of Rightly Conducting the Reason and Seeking Truth in the Sciences** (*Discours de la méthode pour bien conduire sa raison et chercher la vérité dans les sciences*) was published in 1637 as the preface to three scientific essays: the *Dioptrics*, the *Meteorology*, and the *[[geometry-descartes|Geometry]]*. It is Descartes' methodological manifesto and intellectual autobiography — the work in which he introduces the [[method]] of [[doubt]], the four rules of method, the provisional moral code, and the *cogito* argument. As the founding text of modern Western philosophy, the *Discourse* sets the agenda for the next three centuries of philosophical inquiry: the autonomy of reason, the [[mind]]-[[body]] distinction, and the quest for certain [[knowledge]] grounded in indubitable first principles.

## Overview

The *Discourse* is deliberately accessible: Descartes wrote it in French (not Latin) to reach an audience beyond the universities. It presents his philosophical discoveries as the story of his intellectual development — from his disillusionment with scholastic education to his development of a new method, from the *cogito* to the proof of [[god|God's]] existence, from the foundations of physics to the ethics of scientific inquiry.

Descartes describes his aim modestly: "My design, then, is not to teach the method which each ought to follow for the right conduct of his reason, but solely to describe the way in which I have endeavored to conduct my own" (004-005). This modesty is strategic — the *Discourse* presents itself as a personal narrative while advancing a universal philosophical program.

## Structure

The *Discourse* is organized in six parts:

**Part I**: Critique of scholastic education and the value of the "natural light" of reason. "Good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed" — the famous opening line. Descartes recounts his education at La Flèche and his growing dissatisfaction with the uncertainty of the learning he received there.

**Part II**: The four rules of method and the decision to apply them. Descartes describes discovering his method while serving in the army during the Thirty Years' War, meditating in a "stove-heated room" at Ulm in the winter of 1619.

**Part III**: The provisional moral code — rules for living while methodically doubting everything. "Obey the laws and customs of my country, adhere firmly to the religion in which, by God's grace, I had been instructed from my childhood." These maxims ensure that action is possible even while theoretical knowledge is suspended.

**Part IV**: The metaphysical foundations — the method of doubt, the *cogito* ("I think, therefore I am"), the distinction between mind and body, the proof of God's existence. "I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking" (007-002).

**Part V**: Physics and biology — the circulation of blood, the distinction between human and animal souls, a summary of Descartes' concealed treatise *[[the-world|The World]]*.

**Part VI**: The ethics of publication — why Descartes published the *Discourse* despite the Galileo affair, the benefits of scientific progress, and a defence of philosophical inquiry against the charge of impiety.

## Central Arguments

The *Discourse* introduces the **method of doubt**: "to reject as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt, in order to see if there did not remain, after this, something in my belief that was wholly indubitable" (004-006). This is the engine of Cartesian epistemology.

From doubt emerges the ***cogito***: "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world... but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The *cogito* is the Archimedean point — the indubitable foundation on which all knowledge must be rebuilt.

The *Discourse* also presents Descartes' **provisional morality**: a set of maxims for acting while knowledge is uncertain. This addresses the practical challenge of radical doubt — one cannot suspend all action while seeking metaphysical [[certainty]].

## Historical Significance

The *Discourse* is arguably the founding text of modern Western philosophy. Its emphases — the autonomy of reason, the method of systematic doubt, the *cogito* as first principle, the mind-body distinction — set the agenda for the next three centuries of philosophical inquiry. Its publication in French rather than Latin was a deliberate act of intellectual democratization.

The *Discourse* is inseparable from the three scientific essays it prefaces. The *Dioptrics* presents Descartes' theory of light and vision; the *Meteorology* explains natural phenomena (rainbows, winds, storms) by mechanistic principles; the *Geometry* founds analytic geometry. Together, the *Discourse* and its essays demonstrate the unity of Descartes' philosophical method and scientific practice.
