# Sensation

In Descartes' philosophy, **sensation** (*sensus*, *sentire*) is a mode of thinking produced by the body's interaction with the mind through the nervous system. Sensations are "perceptions of the senses (sensuum perceptiones)" — the diverse affections of the mind that arise from motions in the brain caused by the nerves (046-002). Unlike pure intellection, sensation is inseparable from the body, and it reveals not the essence of external objects but rather their utility or harmfulness to the mind-body composite.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes classifies sensation as a mode of perception — one of "the two modes of thinking in us" identified in the *Principles* (090-001). Sensation is a species of the understanding's operation: "to perceive by the senses (sentire), to imagine, and to conceive things purely intelligible, are only different modes of perceiving" (090-002). All modes of thinking, sensation included, inhere in a thinking substance: "we cannot conceive... sensation, or will, unless in a thinking thing" (111-002). But sensation is not essential to mind, since "we can conceive... thought without imagination or sensation" (111-002).

Descartes gives sensation a mechanical explanation in the *Passions of the Soul* and the later *Principles*. Sensation occurs "by the medium of the nerves, which are extended like threads from the brain to all the other members" (046-002). Motions in the external world stimulate the nerves, which transmit the motion to the brain, where they "variously affect the soul or mind, which is intimately conjoined with the brain, according to the diversity of the motions themselves" (046-002). The mental states directly arising from these brain motions are sensations.

The epistemological status of sensation is complex. Descartes insists that sensations are not inherently deceptive — "we may also have a clear knowledge" of our sensations "if we take care to comprehend in the judgments we form of them only that which is precisely contained in our perception of them" (124-002). The problem is that "we have all, without exception, from our youth judged that all the things we perceived by our senses had an existence beyond our thought, and that they were entirely similar to the sensations... we had of them" (124-002). This prejudice — that sensations resemble their external causes — is the root of sensory error.

The practical value of sensation is defended against the hyperbolic doubt. The senses serve to preserve the mind-body union: sensations of pain signal bodily damage, and sensations of pleasure signal what is beneficial. Descartes explains in the *Passions* how "corporeal pleasure and pain, although sensations of quite an opposite character, arise nevertheless from causes nearly alike" (048-002) — both are the body's way of communicating its state to the mind. Sensations are not meant to reveal the essence of things but to guide the embodied mind in navigating the world.

Descartes also carefully distinguishes sensation from imagination and pure understanding. Whereas imagination can produce images without external stimuli (as in dreams), sensation requires actual bodily causation. And whereas pure understanding grasps essences, sensation presents only confused appearances. "To say we perceive colours in objects is in reality equivalent to saying we perceive something in objects and are yet ignorant of what it is, except as that which determines in us a certain highly vivid and clear sensation" (128-002). The sensation of color is vivid and clear *as a sensation*, but it gives no knowledge of the nature of the object causing it.

## Key Passages

> And the diverse affections of the mind or thoughts that immediately arise from these motions, are called perceptions of the senses (sensuum perceptiones), or, as we commonly speak, sensations (sensus).
>
> — Passions of the Soul, Art. XII-XIII (046-002)

> There remain our sensations, affections, and appetites, of which we may also have a clear knowledge, if we take care to comprehend in the judgments we form of them only that which is precisely contained in our perception of them, and of which we are immediately conscious.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, I.LXVI (124-002)

> We may remark that the pain which is excited in the eye by a light too strong is nothing else than a sensation caused by a very violent motion of the nerves of sight, and the pleasure which a moderate light excites is caused by a moderate motion.
>
> — Principles of Philosophy, IV (153-025)


Descartes' mechanistic account of sensation has profound implications for the philosophy of perception. If sensations are "confused modes of thinking" (090-002) caused by bodily motions, then the qualitative character of experience — the redness of red, the painfulness of pain — is not a property of external objects but a modification of the mind. This is the origin of the primary/secondary quality distinction that would become central to **Locke** and early modern philosophy. Extension, figure, and motion are "in the objects"; color, taste, smell, and sound are "in the mind" as confused sensations.

The mechanistic theory also suggests a solution to the mind-body problem at the level of sensation: the relation between nerve motion and felt quality is instituted by nature (i.e., by God) rather than by any intelligible connection. "Nature teaches me," Descartes writes in the Sixth Meditation, "that there is a great difference between mind and body, inasmuch as body is by nature always divisible, and the mind is entirely indivisible" (013-006). The causal relation between a specific nerve motion and a specific sensation is a contingent psycho-physical law established by divine ordinance — not a logical necessity. This allows Descartes to maintain substance dualism while acknowledging the intimate union of mind and body in sensation.


## Historical Context

Descartes' account of sensation represents a decisive break with the Aristotelian-scholastic theory of perception. For Aristotle and Aquinas, sensation involved the reception of the "sensible form" of an object without its matter — a process of formal assimilation. The senses were reliable because they received the very form of the thing itself. Descartes replaces this with a mechanistic theory: sensation is simply the mind's confused response to motions in the nerves and brain. There is no resemblance between the sensation and its external cause — the sensation of red no more resembles red than a word resembles the thing it signifies.

This revision had profound consequences. It made possible a mathematical physics in which the only real properties of bodies are extension, figure, and motion (the primary qualities), while colour, sound, taste, and other sensory qualities are relegated to the mind. Galileo and **John Locke** would develop similar distinctions, forming the basis of modern scientific realism. But Descartes' theory also raises the skeptical problem he confronts in the *Meditations*: if sensations do not resemble their causes, how can we be sure that material objects exist at all? His answer — that God's veracity guarantees that our natural inclination to believe in bodies is not deceptive — would be challenged by subsequent philosophers from **Berkeley** to **Hume**.
