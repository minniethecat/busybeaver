# Hope

In Descartes' moral psychology, **hope** (*espoir*) is a derivative passion composed of desire and a partial judgment of attainability. Unlike simple desire, which inclines the will toward any represented future good, hope arises specifically when "the probability of obtaining what is desired is such that the soul persuades itself it will not fail" (Passions, art. 58). Its contrary, **fear** (*crainte*), is the anticipation of a future evil considered equally possible. Hope and fear are thus symmetrical — twin passions that stake the soul's affective life on an uncertain future.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' most sustained reflection on hope appears in *The Passions of the Soul*. He classifies it as a particular passion (not one of the six primitives) formed from the combination of desire and a judgment about likelihood. Hope "disposes the soul to regard as easy of attainment the good it desires" (Passions, art. 165). When the grounds for this confidence are strong, hope becomes **confidence** (*assurance*); when they are weak, it shades into **despair** (*désespoir*). Hope is thus an intrinsically unstable passion — it exists on a spectrum between the extremes of certainty (which would eliminate the passion altogether, since what is certain ceases to be hoped for) and impossibility (which produces despair).

The usefulness of hope is practical: it sustains action in the face of uncertainty. Descartes' own intellectual career was propelled by hope — the hope "that by its use my mind was becoming gradually habituated to clearer and more distinct conceptions of its objects" (005-013). In the *Discourse*, he describes his method as a source not of certainty from the start but of justified hope — the expectation that following the right procedure will, over time, yield results. This is the characteristic temper of early modern science: not dogmatic certitude but methodical optimism.

Hope also plays a significant role in Descartes' natural theology. In the Synopsis to the *Meditations*, he states that his arguments "are sufficient to show that the destruction of the mind does not follow from the corruption of the body, and thus to afford to men the hope of a future life" (013-002). Hope, in this context, is the appropriate affective response to rational demonstration that falls short of absolute proof. Descartes does not claim to *prove* the immortality of the soul in the way he proves the *cogito*; he claims to provide grounds sufficient for rational hope.

This distinction between proof and hope-giving argument is methodologically important. Descartes recognizes that different domains admit different degrees of certainty. The truths of geometry admit of demonstration; the immortality of the soul admits of "moral certainty" — certainty sufficient for the conduct of life and for grounding a reasonable hope. The wise person "hopes for success" in the search for wisdom (022-003) without claiming to have already attained it.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' treatment of hope generates several important implications for his philosophical system. First, hope provides the affective bridge between theoretical reason and practical action. Pure cognition yields no motivation; desire alone, unguided by judgment, yields caprice. Hope is the passion proper to rational agency under uncertainty — it combines the cognitive element (a judgment of probability) with the conative element (the desire for a good), and in doing so it renders action possible even where knowledge is incomplete (005-013). This is essential to Descartes' method, which requires the inquirer to act on provisional certainties while foundational knowledge is being pursued.

Second, hope illuminates Descartes' conception of the relationship between philosophy and theology. By grounding the hope of a future life in philosophical argument rather than revelation (013-002), Descartes claims a role for natural reason in establishing the rational credibility of doctrines that had traditionally been the province of faith alone. This is a carefully calibrated position: he does not claim to make theology redundant (indeed, he submits his opinions to the authority of the Church), but he does claim that philosophy can "afford hope" where theology commands faith. The distinction is one of epistemic modality — hope, unlike belief or knowledge, is compatible with the absence of demonstrative certainty.

Third, hope has a social dimension. Descartes acknowledges that the pursuit of knowledge in its fullness "would demand a great expenditure, to which the resources of a private individual like myself would not be adequate, unless aided by the public" (022-013). The individual inquirer's hope for progress must be supplemented by the hope that others will contribute — that the advancement of learning is a collective enterprise spanning generations. This recognition anticipates the Enlightenment model of scientific progress as a cooperative undertaking.

## Historical Context

Descartes' analysis of hope draws on two classical sources. **Aristotle**, in the *Rhetoric*, treats hope (*elpis*) as central to the psychology of action: "hope is the belief that something good is attainable, and it is always accompanied by pleasure." The Aristotelian tradition emphasized hope's motivational character. The Stoics, by contrast, were suspicious of hope: as an attachment to external goods, it was a passion to be extirpated. The sage, being self-sufficient, neither hopes nor fears. Descartes charts a middle course: hope is a natural passion that serves genuine goods, but it must be regulated by reason lest it become the source of self-deception.

In the Christian tradition, hope is a theological virtue alongside faith and charity. **Aquinas** defines hope as the expectation of future beatitude, made possible by God's grace. Descartes does not deny this theological sense, but he focuses on hope as a natural passion accessible to philosophical analysis. His hope of a future life, grounded in the argument for the real distinction of mind and body, is a *rational* hope — not a theological one dependent on revelation.

**Hume** would later reduce hope and fear to the "mixture of contrary passions" arising from probability judgments. **Spinoza** would treat hope (*spes*) as an inconstant pleasure arising from the image of a future thing whose outcome we doubt — and, like the Stoics, would argue that the free man "thinks of nothing less than of death, and his wisdom is a meditation not of death, but of life." Descartes' hope is more modest: it is the passion appropriate to finite minds pursuing an infinite task — the perfection of knowledge — within the limits of a single lifetime.

## Criticisms and Responses

Descartes' concept of hope faces several criticisms. The first concerns its epistemological basis. If hope is rational only when grounded in a sound judgment of probability, then the rationality of hope collapses into the rationality of the underlying judgment. But Descartes' own arguments for the hope of a future life — the real distinction of mind and body — have been widely contested. If the argument fails, does the hope become irrational? Descartes' response, implicit in his practice, is that the standard for hope-giving argument is lower than the standard for demonstrative proof; one may reasonably hope on the basis of arguments that are merely probable, provided one recognizes their probabilistic character.

A second criticism, pressed by **Pascal** and other Augustinian thinkers, is that Descartes' rational hope is spiritually insufficient. For Pascal, the human condition is one of radical uncertainty that no natural reason can resolve — only the "wager" of faith, which is a different kind of commitment from the probabilistic calculation Descartes offers. The hope Descartes derives from the real-distinction argument is, from Pascal's perspective, a philosophical consolation that falls short of the genuine hope born of religious conviction.

A third line of objection concerns the social conditions of hope. Descartes acknowledges that his own intellectual hopes were sustained by privileged circumstances — leisure, freedom from financial necessity, access to books and correspondence (006-006). Hope, as a passion, is not equally available to all; it depends on the contingent availability of grounds for confidence. This raises the question whether Descartes' ethics of hope — his prescription that we should adjust our desires to what is within our power (006-004) — is genuinely universalizable or merely reflects the perspective of a leisured gentleman-scholar. The objection is not fatal to Descartes' account, but it points to a tension between the formal universality of his method and the material particularity of the conditions required to practice it.
