Descartes' relationship with logic is one of studied ambivalence: he acknowledges its role as an art of reasoning but dismisses scholastic syllogistic logic as incapable of discovering new truths. In its place he proposes his own method — grounded in intuition, deduction, and the natural light of reason — which he presents as a more reliable path to certain knowledge.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes received formal training in scholastic logic at La Flèche, and he acknowledges having "given some attention to logic" alongside geometrical analysis and algebra — three arts he considered potentially useful to his philosophical project (005-006). Yet he consistently distances himself from the Aristotelian syllogistic tradition. His critique targets not reasoning itself but the claim that formal logic can generate new knowledge: syllogisms, he argues, merely rearrange what is already known without producing discoveries.

In the *Discourse on Method*, Descartes explains that he seeks "the true method of arriving at the knowledge of everything my mind was capable of grasping" — a method distinct from the formal logic taught in the schools (022-012). His alternative is a set of rules modeled on geometrical reasoning: accept only clear and distinct ideas, divide problems into parts, proceed from simple to complex, and review completely. This method, he insists, is what enabled his discoveries in optics, meteorology, and geometry.

In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes goes further, claiming that "the notions which are simplest and self-evident are obscured by logical definitions" — that the scholastic practice of defining everything in technical terms actually clouds rather than clarifies foundational truths (068-001). He thus places intuitive grasp above formal definition, a reversal of scholastic priorities that grounds his entire epistemology.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' rejection of syllogistic logic has far-reaching consequences for his system. First, it elevates intuition — the immediate, indubitable apprehension of clear and distinct ideas — above discursive reasoning, making the *cogito* and the existence of God accessible without formal logical apparatus (009-010). Second, it aligns his method with mathematical rather than dialectical reasoning, explaining why his *Meditations* are presented as a sequence of intuitions rather than as formal proofs. Third, it contributes to his break with scholastic education: if logic cannot discover truth, then the entire curriculum built around it requires reform.

The concept of "logical distinction" (*distinctio rationis*) also appears in Descartes' metaphysics. In the *Principles*, he distinguishes between real distinctions (between substances), modal distinctions (between a substance and its modes), and distinctions of reason — the last being a conceptual separation "made by the thought" without any corresponding division in reality (120-001). This preserves the unity of substance while allowing the intellect to consider its attributes separately.

## Historical Context

Descartes' critique of logic continues a tradition of Renaissance and early modern dissatisfaction with scholasticism. Humanists like Vives and Ramus had already attacked Aristotelian logic as pedantic and sterile. Descartes radicalizes this critique by replacing logic not with a reformed version but with a method grounded in the natural light of reason. His approach influenced the Port-Royal Logic (1662), which attempted to reconcile Cartesian method with a reformed Aristotelian framework, and anticipated later debates about whether logic is a tool of discovery or merely of justification.

## Criticisms and Responses

Critics objected that Descartes' method, for all its claims to certainty, contained its own logical flaw: the famous "Cartesian circle." In proving God's existence through clear and distinct ideas, and then relying on God to guarantee the reliability of clear and distinct ideas, Descartes appeared to commit exactly the circular reasoning he had criticized in the scholastics (009-010). Descartes responded that the memory of a proof, as opposed to its immediate intuition, requires divine guarantee — a distinction many commentators have found unsatisfying. Later logicians from Leibniz to Frege would develop formal systems far more rigorous than scholastic logic, raising the question of whether Descartes' dismissal of formal logic was premature.
