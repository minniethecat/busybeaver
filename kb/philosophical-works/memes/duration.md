## Definition

Duration (*duratio*) in Descartes' philosophy is the persistence or continuance of a thing in existence. It is a mode of the thing that endures — not a separate entity — and is distinguished from time (*tempus*), which is a mode of thought by which we measure and compare durations.

## Duration and Substance

In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes makes a strong identity claim about duration: "duration is not distinct from substance except in thought" (120-002). Whatever endures is a substance or a mode of substance; duration has no independent being. This is because if something ceases to exist, its duration ceases with it — duration inheres entirely in the enduring thing.

> "Duration, order, and number" are listed among "the most general" affections of things (106-002). They apply across all categories of being.

## Duration Distinguished from Time

Descartes draws a careful distinction between duration and time. Duration belongs to things themselves — it is their persistence in being. Time, by contrast, is "a certain way of considering duration" (113-001): we measure one duration against another (e.g., the motion of the sun against the duration of a human life) and call that comparison "time."

> "We distinguish duration taken in its generality, and call it time" only when we compare durations to "the greatest and most regular motions" (115-002).

Time is therefore a mode of thought — a way the mind measures — while duration is a mode of the enduring thing (113-002).

## Duration and God's Conservation

Descartes' doctrine of continuous creation gives duration a theological dimension. God conserves the world at every instant, meaning duration depends moment-by-moment on divine power. "The duration alone of our life is sufficient to demonstrate the existence of God" (079-001) — the fact that the self persists from one moment to the next requires a conserving cause.

The nature of time is "of such a kind that its parts do not depend on one another" (079-002): each moment of duration is independent, requiring a continuous act of divine conservation.

## Duration in Physics

In Descartes' physics, duration applies to bodies as well as minds. The "duration of things" is a general feature of created being (079-002). The "situation of parts, duration, number, and those other properties" that we clearly perceive in bodies are modes of extended substance (127-002).
