# John Locke

**John Locke** (1632–1704) was the founding figure of British empiricism and Descartes' most systematic early critic. Born the same year as [[spinoza|Spinoza]], Locke came of age in the intellectual world Descartes had transformed. His *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) opens with a direct assault on the doctrine of innate [[idea|ideas]] — the cornerstone of Cartesian epistemology — and proposes instead that the [[mind]] is a *tabula rasa*, a blank slate furnished entirely by [[experience]]. Locke's influence extends from epistemology through political philosophy to theories of personal identity, making him one of the most consequential thinkers of the early modern period.

## Biography

Locke was born in Wrington, Somerset, and educated at Westminster School and Christ Church, Oxford, where he studied the new mechanical philosophy of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[newton|Newton]], and Boyle alongside traditional scholastic curriculum. He served as personal physician and secretary to Anthony Ashley Cooper, the first Earl of Shaftesbury, a connection that drew him into the political conflicts of Restoration England.

In 1683, Locke fled to the Netherlands to escape political persecution, where he completed the *Essay* and the *Two Treatises of Government*. He returned to England after the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and published his major works in rapid succession. His final years were spent at Oates, the country estate of Lady Damaris Masham, where he hosted intellectual luminaries including [[newton|Isaac Newton]]. He died in 1704.

## Relationship to Descartes

Locke's philosophy is a sustained and detailed response to Cartesianism:

1. **Against Innate Ideas**: The entirety of Book I of the *Essay* is devoted to refuting the doctrine of innate ideas. Descartes had argued that certain [[idea|ideas]] — of [[god|God]], of the self, of mathematical truths — are innate, stamped on the soul by God. Locke countered: if ideas were innate, they would be universally assented to, yet "children and idiots" have no notion of them. All ideas derive from sensation (experience of external objects) or reflection (experience of our own mental operations).

2. **Simple and Complex Ideas**: Locke adopted but transformed Descartes' distinction between ideas and their objects. Simple ideas (of color, solidity, extension) are passively received; complex ideas (of [[substance|substances]], modes, and relations) are actively constructed by the mind. This account preserves the empiricist principle — all materials of [[knowledge]] come from experience — while acknowledging the mind's active role.

3. **Substance as "Something I Know Not What"**: Descartes' [[substance]]-attribute ontology posited substances as the bearers of properties. Locke argued that while we must suppose a substratum supporting observable qualities, we have no clear idea of what this substratum is — it is a "something I know not what." This critique opened the door to [[hume|Hume]]'s more radical rejection of substance.

4. **Personal Identity**: Locke's famous account of personal identity — that the self is constituted by continuity of consciousness and memory, not by an immaterial substance — directly challenges the Cartesian identification of the self with a thinking substance. "[The self] is a thinking intelligent being, that has reason and reflection, and can consider itself as itself, the same thinking thing, in different times and places" (*Essay* II.xxvii.9).

## Key Ideas

**The Tabula Rasa**: The mind at birth is "white paper, void of all characters, without any ideas." All [[knowledge]] is built from experience — sensation provides ideas of external objects; reflection provides ideas of the mind's own operations.

**Primary and Secondary Qualities**: Following the mechanical philosophy Descartes helped establish, Locke distinguished between primary qualities (solidity, extension, figure, motion — actually in objects) and secondary qualities (color, taste, sound — powers to produce ideas in us, not resemblances of anything in objects).

**The Labor Theory of Property**: In the *Second Treatise*, Locke argued that property rights arise from mixing one's labor with nature — a theory that would profoundly influence modern political thought.

## In Descartes' Legacy

> Locke's *Essay* transformed Cartesian epistemology by redirecting it from the certainty of innate ideas to the patient investigation of how ideas are actually acquired. While Descartes had asked "What can I know with certainty?", Locke asked "What are the origins, limits, and extent of human understanding?" This shift from rational introspection to empirical psychology marks the birth of a distinctly modern approach to the [[mind]].
