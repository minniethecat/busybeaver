## Definition

In Descartes' natural philosophy, the **laws of nature** are the immutable rules established by God that govern all physical change and motion in the material universe. They are not inherent in matter itself but are imposed by God's continuous creative act. Since God is immutable, the laws by which He conserves the world are likewise eternal and unchanging.

> I have also observed certain laws established in nature by God in such a manner, and of which he has impressed on our minds such notions, that after we have reflected sufficiently upon these, we cannot doubt that they are accurately observed in all that exists or takes place in the world. (008-001)

Descartes presents his laws of nature most fully in Part V of the *Discourse on the Method*, where he outlines a hypothetical cosmogony: even if God had initially created only a chaos of matter in motion, the laws of nature alone would suffice to produce the ordered cosmos we observe (008-002).

## The Three Laws of Motion

In the *Principles of Philosophy* (Part II, §§37–40), Descartes formulates three fundamental laws of nature governing all corporeal motion:

1. **The Law of Inertia** (first law): Each thing, insofar as it is simple and undivided, remains in the same state — at rest or in uniform rectilinear motion — unless acted upon by an external cause. This is the first clear statement of the principle of inertia in modern physics.

2. **The Law of Rectilinear Motion** (second law): All motion is, of itself, in a straight line. Bodies moving in a circle always tend to recede from the center. This breaks with the Aristotelian-Scholastic view that circular motion is the most perfect and natural.

3. **The Law of Conservation of Quantity of Motion** (third law): When a moving body collides with another, the total quantity of motion (*mv*, where *m* is the body's magnitude and *v* its speed) is conserved. Though incorrect in detail (Descartes treated speed as a scalar, missing the vector nature of momentum), this was a landmark attempt at a quantitative conservation law.

## Theological Foundation

Crucially, Descartes grounds his laws of nature not in empirical observation but in the nature of God. Because God is immutable in His essence and operation, His conservation of the world must follow constant, unchanging rules:

> With no other principle upon which to found my reasonings except the infinite perfection of God, I endeavored to demonstrate all those about which there could be any room for doubt, and to prove that they are such, that even if God had created more worlds, there could have been none in which these laws were not observed. (008-002)

This theological grounding sets Descartes' physics apart from both Aristotelian natural philosophy (which explained motion in terms of inherent tendencies toward natural places) and later Newtonian physics (which treated laws as empirically discovered regularities).

## Influence and Legacy

Descartes' conception of laws of nature was revolutionary. It established the framework for modern physics: nature as a law-governed machine, intelligible through mathematics. [[Isaac Newton|Newton]] adopted and refined the concept in his *Principia Mathematica*, though he rejected Descartes' purely mechanistic vortex theory of planetary motion in favor of universal gravitation.

The idea that the laws of nature are discoverable by reason (they are "impressed on our minds" by God, per 008-001) makes physics an extension of metaphysics in the Cartesian system — a view that would later be challenged by [[empiricism]] but which proved enormously productive in the Scientific Revolution.
