## Definition

The **evil demon** (*malin génie* in French, sometimes translated "evil genius" or "malicious demon") is a radical skeptical hypothesis introduced by Descartes in the First Meditation. After systematically doubting the evidence of the senses, the existence of the external world, and even mathematical truths — all of which could, he argues, be the work of a deceiving God — Descartes posits the possibility of an all-powerful, malevolent being whose sole purpose is to deceive him about everything:

> I will suppose... that some malignant demon, who is at once exceedingly potent and deceitful, has employed all his artifice to deceive me; I will suppose that the sky, the air, the earth, colors, figures, sounds, and all external things, are nothing better than the illusions of dreams, by means of which this being has laid snares for my credulity.
>
> (Meditations I)

## Role in the Method of Doubt

The evil demon hypothesis is the culminating stage of Descartes' method of hyperbolic doubt. It follows a progression of increasingly radical skeptical arguments:

1. **Sensory deception**: The senses sometimes deceive (e.g., optical illusions), so they cannot be trusted absolutely.
2. **The dream argument**: There are no certain marks distinguishing waking from dreaming; all present experience could be a dream.
3. **The deceiving God**: An omnipotent God *could* deceive us even about mathematical truths (2+2=4), though Descartes' religious convictions make it implausible that a perfect God *would* do so.
4. **The evil demon**: To avoid any temptation to exempt God, Descartes substitutes the fiction of an evil demon — a being of maximal power and malevolence, dedicated wholly to deception.

This final hypothesis strips away *all* certainty, reducing the meditator to a state of radical doubt from which the only escape is the discovery of the indubitable *cogito* in the Second Meditation.

## The Cogito as Refutation

The evil demon hypothesis is ultimately refuted by the *[[cogito]]* — "I think, therefore I am." Even if an evil demon is deceiving me about everything, I must exist in order to be deceived. The very act of doubting confirms my existence as a thinking thing:

> But there is I know not what being, who is possessed at once of the highest power and the deepest cunning, who is constantly employing all his ingenuity in deceiving me. Doubtless, then, I exist, since I am deceived; and let him deceive me as he may, he can never bring it about that I am nothing, so long as I shall be conscious that I am something.
>
> (Meditations II)

The *cogito* thus emerges as the first certainty — the Archimedean point from which Descartes will rebuild knowledge.

## Philosophical Significance

The evil demon hypothesis represents one of the most powerful skeptical scenarios in the history of philosophy. It anticipates:

- The **brain in a vat** thought experiment in contemporary epistemology
- The **simulation hypothesis**: the idea that all experience could be a computer-generated illusion
- **Global skepticism** as a methodological tool rather than a final position

Crucially, Descartes does not *endorse* skepticism; he uses it as a purgative to clear away uncertain beliefs and establish a firm foundation. The evil demon is a device within a larger constructive project — methodological, not dogmatic, skepticism.

## 相关Influences

The hypothesis also connects to the theological problem of deception. Descartes later proves that God exists and is not a deceiver (089-002), which guarantees that clear and distinct perceptions are reliable (013-004). The evil demon is thus a philosophical foil: by driving doubt to its extreme, Descartes reveals the necessity of a veracious God as the guarantor of knowledge.
