# Esteem

In Descartes' moral psychology, **esteem** (*estime*) is the passion by which the soul judges the worth of an object — including itself. When directed at oneself and properly regulated by reason, esteem becomes **generosity** (*générosité*), which Descartes identifies as the highest moral virtue: the firm and constant resolution to use one's free will well, grounded in the recognition that "nothing truly belongs to us except the free disposition of our will" (Passions, art. 153). Esteem is thus the affective foundation of Cartesian ethics — the passion whose right ordering constitutes the well-lived life.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' treatment of esteem unfolds primarily in *The Passions of the Soul* (1649). In Articles 149–161, he develops a taxonomy of self-regarding passions. There are two kinds of esteem: the esteem we have for ourselves, and the esteem we have for others. These are intimately connected: "we cannot often esteem others without also esteeming ourselves" (Passions, art. 154), because recognizing excellence in another requires that we possess the same standard of judgment within ourselves.

Esteem becomes a vice when it is ill-founded. **Pride** (*orgueil*) is the excess of self-esteem — "a species of joy, founded on the love we have for ourselves, and arising from the opinion or hope we entertain of being praised by other men" (Passions, art. 160). **Self-abasement** or servility (*bassesse*) is its defect — the failure to esteem oneself as one ought. Between these extremes lies generosity, the virtuous mean: "true generosity, which causes a man to esteem himself as highly as he legitimately can" (Passions, art. 153).

The foundation of legitimate self-esteem is Descartes' most distinctive ethical claim: the sole ground of human dignity is the possession of free will. "I see nothing else in us that can reasonably give us cause to esteem ourselves" (Passions, art. 152). All other goods — intelligence, beauty, wealth, social rank — are either gifts of nature or fortune, not achievements of the will. Only the resolve to use freedom rightly is genuinely ours. The person who recognizes this truth "is naturally led to do great things, and at the same time to undertake nothing of which he does not feel himself capable" (Passions, art. 153). Generosity makes one simultaneously ambitious and realistic — aspiring to great deeds without overreaching.

Descartes' discussion of esteem in non-moral contexts also illuminates his epistemology. In the *Discourse*, he describes the attitude appropriate to received opinions: "I esteemed them as so certain that I cannot change our opinions regarding them" (022-006) — a provisional certainty required for practical life but always subject to revision by reason. In the *Principles*, he notes that sensory experience "we esteemed it certain and indubitable" (124-002) until philosophical reflection reveals the gap between sensation and reality. Esteem, in these contexts, is a judgment of reliability — an epistemological as well as a moral category.

## Historical Context

Descartes' concept of generosity/esteem represents a creative synthesis of classical and Christian sources. The Aristotelian virtue of *megalopsychia* (greatness of soul) is the man who "thinks himself worthy of great things and is worthy of them." Descartes transforms this: the greatness worthy of esteem is not in achievements but in the resolution of the will itself. The Stoic teaching that only what is in our power has moral value — "everything else is indifferent" — is given a new metaphysical foundation in the Cartesian theory of free will.

The Christian tradition of humility receives, in Descartes' hands, a rationalist reconstruction. Humility is not self-abasement before God (as in the *Imitatio Christi*) but the accurate recognition that one's will, though free, is finite. "Those who have this knowledge and feeling of themselves persuade themselves readily that other men may have the same" (Passions, art. 155). Generosity thus has a horizontal, egalitarian dimension absent from the hierarchical virtues of the medieval tradition.

**Spinoza** would later reject the idea that free will can ground self-esteem — since all things are determined — but he retains generosity (*generositas*) as an affect of active joy directed at aiding others (Ethics III, Prop. 59, Schol.). **Kant** would transform the Cartesian insight into the doctrine that the good will is the only thing good without qualification. Descartes' discussion of esteem, though fragmentary compared to his epistemology and metaphysics, is one of the most original contributions of his later philosophy.
