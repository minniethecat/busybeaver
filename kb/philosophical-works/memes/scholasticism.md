# Scholasticism

**Scholasticism** was the dominant intellectual tradition of medieval Europe — the system Descartes was trained in and against which he defined his [[philosophy]]. At the Jesuit college of La Flèche, the young [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] absorbed the scholastic curriculum: [[aristotle|Aristotelian]] logic, metaphysics, and natural philosophy, interpreted through the commentaries of [[aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]] and the Jesuit tradition. Descartes' lifelong project — to replace scholastic [[substance]] theory with mechanistic physics, final causes with mathematical laws, and authority with the [[cogito]] — can only be understood as a response to the scholasticism he was taught to revere.

## Historical Context

Scholasticism emerged in the 12th century as European universities rediscovered Aristotle's works through Arabic and Latin translations. Its characteristic method — the *quaestio* — posed a question, marshaled authorities on both sides, and resolved the dispute through dialectical reasoning. The great syntheses of the 13th century (Aquinas, Bonaventure, Albertus Magnus) integrated Aristotle's philosophy with Christian theology, creating a comprehensive worldview in which everything from the motion of the planets to the nature of the [[soul]] had its proper place in a hierarchy of being.

By Descartes' time (early 17th century), scholasticism had become sclerotic — a system of rote learning in which disputes about substantial forms, prime matter, and the distinctions among kinds of [[soul]] had replaced genuine inquiry into nature.

## Descartes' Critique

Descartes rejected scholasticism on three fronts:

1. **Method**: Against the scholastic *quaestio* — which weighed conflicting authorities — Descartes proposed the geometric [[method]]: begin with self-evident axioms, deduce consequences, accept nothing on authority.

2. **Physics**: Against substantial forms, real qualities, and final causes, Descartes proposed mechanistic explanation: all natural phenomena result from matter in [[motion]], governed by mathematical laws.

3. **Foundation**: Against the scholastic synthesis of faith and reason, Descartes sought an indubitable foundation — the [[cogito]] — independent of both tradition and revelation.

## Legacy

Scholasticism did not disappear after Descartes. Leibniz was deeply influenced by scholastic logic; [[spinoza]]'s geometric method is in some ways a scholastic form pressed into Cartesian service; and the problem of the relationship between [[mind]] and [[body]] — which Descartes made central — was itself inherited from scholastic debates about the soul. Descartes' revolution was less an absolute break than a transformation of the questions scholasticism had asked for centuries.
