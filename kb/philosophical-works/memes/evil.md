In Descartes' philosophy, evil is understood primarily through the lens of privation theory — evil is not a positive reality but the absence or negation of being. This doctrine, inherited from Augustine and scholasticism, serves Descartes' broader project of reconciling the existence of a perfect, omnipotent God with the reality of human error and moral failing. Descartes treats evil principally in two registers: error (intellectual evil) as treated in the Fourth Meditation, and sin (moral evil) as discussed in the Principles.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes addresses the problem of evil most directly in the context of human error. In the Fourth Meditation, he argues that error arises not from God but from the disproportion between the human will (which is infinite and God-like) and the human intellect (which is finite). God is not culpable for our errors because error is a privation — a lack of knowledge — rather than something positively created (013-004). This framework extends to moral evil as well: Descartes maintains that God "does not will the evil of sin, seeing this is but the negation of being" (081-001). Evil has no efficient cause in God; it arises solely from the misuse of free will by finite creatures.

Descartes explicitly distinguishes his treatment of intellectual error from that of sin. He notes that his analysis in the Meditations concerns "not at all... Sin, that is, of error committed in the pursuit of good and evil, but of that sort alone which arises in the determination of the true and the false" (013-004). Nevertheless, the underlying metaphysical principle remains consistent across both domains: evil is always a defect, never a substance.

Descartes further argues that it is unreasonable to hold God responsible for our errors. While humans may hold one another accountable for failing to prevent evil, God's dominion over the universe "is perfectly absolute and free" (096-002). The freedom God granted to humanity includes the possibility of error, but God's omnipotence means he bears no obligation to prevent it.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' privation theory of evil serves a strategic theological function: it shields God from responsibility for error and sin while preserving divine omnipotence. By locating the cause of error in the human will's overreach beyond the finite intellect, Descartes makes human freedom — not divine will — the source of evil (013-004).

The implication for Descartes' epistemology is significant. Since error is a correctable defect rather than an inescapable condition, the remedy lies in methodological discipline: withholding judgment when ideas are not clear and distinct. This directly connects the problem of evil to Descartes' broader method of doubt and his criterion of truth as clear and distinct perception.

The metaphysical implication is equally important: by defining evil as non-being, Descartes preserves the perfection of creation. Everything that God creates, insofar as it has being, is good. Evil enters only through the privation of being, which requires no separate creative act.

## Historical Context

The privation theory of evil traces back to Augustine, who formulated it in response to Manichaean dualism, and was refined by Thomas Aquinas in the thirteenth century. Descartes inherits this tradition but adapts it to his own philosophical system, which has no room for Aristotelian teleology. For Descartes, the natural world operates by mechanical laws, not final causes, so evil cannot be explained in terms of purposes gone awry. Instead, it must be explained entirely through the metaphysical structure of the mind — the interplay of infinite will and finite intellect — and the absolute freedom of God's creative act.

## Criticisms and Responses

A standard objection to the privation theory, raised by Descartes' contemporaries and by later philosophers, is that it fails to account for the experiential reality of suffering. If evil is mere non-being, why does it feel so positive? Descartes' response is implicit in his treatment: what feels positive is not evil itself but the *awareness* of privation, which is a mode of thought — something real, though its content is a negation.

A further objection concerns the compatibility of human free will with divine foreknowledge and omnipotence. Descartes does not fully resolve this tension in his published works, though his insistence on the absolute freedom of God's will suggests a form of voluntarism that may undercut the very theodicy the privation theory is meant to support.
