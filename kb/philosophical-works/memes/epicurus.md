# Epicurus

**Epicurus** (341–270 BCE) developed Democritus' atomism into a comprehensive philosophical system encompassing physics, epistemology, and ethics. Descartes is aware of Epicurean atomism primarily through **Lucretius'** *De Rerum Natura*, and his own mechanism is partly a response to the Epicurean tradition (022-005).

## Biography

Epicurus founded his school, the Garden, in Athens around 307 BCE. Unlike the Academy or Lyceum, the Garden admitted women and slaves. Epicurus wrote prolifically, but most of his works are lost; his philosophy is known through Lucretius, Diogenes Laërtius, and the Herculaneum papyri.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes does not name Epicurus directly in the surviving English corpus, but his engagement with atomism — through his critique of Democritus — applies equally to Epicurean atomism. The Epicurean "swerve" (*clinamen*) — the random deviation of atoms from their downward path, introduced to explain free will — is incompatible with Descartes' physics, where all motion follows deterministic laws grounded in God's immutability.

Descartes' rejection of the void, his insistence on the infinite divisibility of matter, and his grounding of physical law in divine attributes all distinguish his mechanism from Epicurean atomism. However, the broader Epicurean project — explaining all natural phenomena through matter and motion without appeal to divine intervention or final causes — is shared by Descartes.

## Key Ideas

**Atomism and the Swerve**: Epicurus added to Democritus' atomism the doctrine of the *clinamen* — a minimal, random swerve in atomic motion. Descartes rejects this: randomness has no place in a physics grounded in God's immutable nature.

**Theology**: Epicurus held that the gods exist but are indifferent to human affairs, living in the *intermundia* between worlds. Descartes' God, by contrast, is the continuously acting cause of all existence.

**Ethics**: Epicurus' ethical hedonism — identifying pleasure as the highest good — is distant from Descartes' ethics, though Descartes' *Passions of the Soul* shares the Epicurean interest in understanding and moderating the emotions.
