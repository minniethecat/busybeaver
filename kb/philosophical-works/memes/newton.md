# Isaac Newton

**Isaac Newton** (1643–1727) was the dominant figure of the Scientific Revolution and the philosopher whose work finally displaced Cartesian physics. Descartes' *Principles of Philosophy* (1644) had proposed a comprehensive mechanical world-system based on [[extension]], [[matter]] in motion, and vortex theory. Newton's *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) replaced this qualitative mechanical model with a mathematical system of universal laws. Though Newton's physics triumphed, his conception of absolute [[space]] and time, his theory of [[gravity]] as action at a distance, and his methodological principle *hypotheses non fingo* ("I feign no hypotheses") were direct responses to Cartesian positions that had dominated natural philosophy for half a century.

## Biography

Newton was born in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, on Christmas Day 1642 (Old Style) — the year [[galileo|Galileo]] died. He entered Trinity College, Cambridge in 1661, where he encountered Descartes' *Principles* and *Geometry*, along with the works of [[hobbes|Hobbes]], Boyle, and Gassendi. During the plague years 1665–1666, he returned to Woolsthorpe and made his foundational discoveries in calculus (his "method of fluxions"), optics, and gravitation.

In 1669, Newton succeeded Isaac Barrow as Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge. His *Principia* (1687), written at the urging of Edmond Halley, established the three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation. From 1703 until his death, he served as President of the Royal Society. Knighted in 1705, he died in 1727 and was buried in Westminster Abbey.

## Relationship to Descartes

Newton's relationship to Descartes was one of deep engagement, critique, and ultimate displacement. He studied the *Principles of Philosophy* intensively as a student and built his own system in explicit opposition to key Cartesian doctrines.

**Against the Vortex Theory**: Descartes explained planetary motion through a system of fluid vortices — the planets are carried around the sun by swirling celestial matter. Newton demonstrated mathematically that vortices could not reproduce Kepler's laws of planetary motion. The *Principia* replaced this qualitative mechanism with a single mathematical law: every particle of matter attracts every other with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance.

**Absolute Space and Time**: Descartes defined [[space]] (extension) as identical with body — there is no empty space, no vacuum. He also held that motion is purely relative: a body's place is defined only by its relation to immediately contiguous bodies. Newton countered with absolute space: "absolute space, in its own nature, without relation to anything external, remains always similar and immovable." This was a philosophical choice driven by physics: absolute space was needed to define absolute motion (acceleration and rotation produce observable effects that relative motion cannot explain).

**Method: Mathematics vs. Mechanism**: Descartes sought to explain physical phenomena by deducing them from the nature of bodies — extension, figure, and motion. Newton rejected this approach: "I do not feign hypotheses." In place of Descartes' deductive mechanical explanations, Newton offered mathematical laws describing how bodies move, without claiming to know the underlying nature of [[substance]]. This shift from mechanism to mathematical description was a turning point in the history of [[science]].

**Optics**: Descartes' *Dioptrics* had explained light as a pressure transmitted instantaneously through the plenum. Newton's *Opticks* (1704) demonstrated experimentally that white light is composed of colored rays with different refrangibilities — a corpuscular theory of light inconsistent with Cartesian principles.

## Legacy

Newton's triumph over Cartesian physics was decisive but not total. [[leibniz|Leibniz]] defended a relativistic conception of space and time against Newton's absolutism, and Cartesian conservation principles (the conservation of *mv*, later transformed into *mv²*) continued to shape debates in dynamics. The Newtonian-Cartesian divide structured 18th-century natural philosophy: French Cartesians like Fontenelle resisted Newtonian attraction as a return to occult qualities, while Voltaire's *Eléments de la philosophie de Newton* (1738) popularized Newtonianism in France. Kant's early work attempted a synthesis, reconciling Newtonian physics with a modified Cartesian metaphysics — a project that culminated in the *Critique of Pure Reason*.
