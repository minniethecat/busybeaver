## Overview

Sense perception (Latin *sensus*, French *sens*) occupies an ambivalent position in Descartes' philosophy. It is indispensable for navigating practical life and provides the raw material for scientific inquiry, yet it is also the primary source of error and prejudice. Descartes' methodological revolution begins with a systematic withdrawal from the senses, clearing the ground for rational intuition.

## Senses as Source of Error

The first step of Cartesian method is to recognize that the senses deceive. "Seeing that our senses sometimes deceive us, I was willing to suppose that there existed nothing really such as they presented to us" (007-001). This is not merely occasional error — it is systematic. The senses present qualities (color, heat, taste) as belonging to objects, when in reality bodies possess only extension, figure, and motion.

Descartes identifies sensory prejudice as a fundamental obstacle: "As we were at one time children, and as we formed various judgments regarding the objects presented to our senses, when as yet we had not the entire use of our reason, numerous prejudices stand in the way of our arriving at the knowledge of truth" (026-002).

## The Withdrawal from the Senses

The method of doubt requires detaching the mind from sensory commerce: "I would advise none to read this work, unless such as are able and willing to meditate with me in earnest, to detach their minds from commerce with the senses, and likewise to deliver themselves from all prejudice" (012-006). This withdrawal is the "easiest pathway by which the mind may withdraw itself from the senses" (013-001).

This is not a permanent rejection — Descartes does not deny that the senses have a practical function. Rather, it is a methodological suspension: "We will doubt, first, whether of all the things that have ever fallen under our senses, or which we have ever imagined, any one really exist; in the first place, because we know by experience that the senses sometimes err" (029-002).

## Sensation as a Mode of Thought

In Descartes' mature taxonomy, sensation is classified as a mode of thinking: "Thus, to perceive by the senses (*sentire*), to imagine, and to conceive things purely intelligible, are only different modes of perceiving" (090-002). This is a revolutionary claim — sensation is not a purely bodily process but a mode of the mind's perceptual activity.

## Mechanistic Theory of Sensation

Descartes provides a detailed physiological account of how sensation works: the nerves terminate in the skin and are moved by external objects in diverse ways — "in one way by their hardness, in another by their gravity, in a third by their heat, in a fourth by their humidity" (048-002). These motions are transmitted to the brain, where they "variously affect the soul or mind, which is intimately conjoined with the brain, according to the diversity of the motions themselves" (046-002).

> The nerves terminating in the skin of the whole body can be touched through this medium by any terrene objects whatever, and moved by these wholes, in one way by their hardness, in another by their gravity, in a third by their heat, in a fourth by their humidity, etc. — and in as many diverse modes as they are either moved or hindered from their ordinary motion, to that extent are diverse sensations excited in the mind. (048-002)

## Senses and the Knowledge of God

A crucial Cartesian argument concerns the limits of sensory knowledge: "The philosophers of the schools accept as a maxim that there is nothing in the understanding which was not previously in the senses, in which however it is certain that the ideas of God and of the soul have never been" (007-006). The existence of innate ideas — particularly of God and the thinking self — demonstrates that sensory experience does not exhaust the sources of knowledge.

## Role in Scientific Inquiry

Despite their limitations, the senses are essential to natural philosophy. Descartes notes that experiments become "always more necessary the more one is advanced in knowledge; for, at the commencement, it is better to make use only of what is spontaneously presented to our senses" (009-003). The empirical data provided by the senses serve as the starting point for rational deduction from first principles.
