# Plato

**Plato** (428/427–348/347 BCE) is the foundational figure of Western philosophy, the student of [[socrates|Socrates]] and the teacher of [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. Descartes' relationship to Plato is one of affinity rather than direct citation — Descartes rarely names Plato, but his philosophy shares deep structural features with Platonism: the priority of intellect over sense, the doctrine of innate [[idea|ideas]], the immortality of the [[mind|soul]], and the conviction that true [[knowledge]] is of intelligible rather than sensible reality. The Platonic inheritance in Cartesian thought is most visible in the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]*, whose meditative structure and arguments for the soul's independence from body recall the *Phaedo*.

## Biography

Plato was born into an aristocratic Athenian family. After Socrates' execution (399 BCE), he travelled extensively before founding the Academy in Athens, the first institution of higher learning in the Western world. His dialogues feature Socrates as the principal speaker and cover ethics, politics, metaphysics, epistemology, and cosmology. The *Timaeus*, with its account of the cosmos as the product of a divine craftsman, was particularly influential on medieval and Renaissance thought.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes acknowledges Plato alongside Aristotle. In the *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]*, he notes that "the most remarkable writings we possess are Plato and Aristotle, between whom there was no difference, save that the former, following in the footsteps of his master, Socrates, ingenuously confessed that he had never yet been able to find anything certain" (022-005). Descartes aligns himself with this Platonic-Socratic honesty about the difficulty of attaining certainty.

The Platonic affinities in Descartes' thought are substantial: **innatism** echoes Plato's *anamnesis*; **dualism** echoes the *Phaedo's* separation of soul from body; **mathematics as paradigm of knowledge** is shared. However, Descartes naturalizes Plato: innate ideas are built into the mind's nature, not recollected from a prior existence.

## Key Ideas

**Theory of Forms**: Plato held that the objects of genuine knowledge are eternal, unchanging Forms — the Form of the Good, of Justice, of Equality — of which sensible things are imperfect copies. Descartes replaces transcendent Forms with innate ideas in the mind. Both philosophers locate genuine knowledge in an intelligible realm distinct from the sensible world.

**Dualism**: In the *Phaedo*, Plato argues that the soul is distinct from the body, that true knowledge requires separation from bodily influences, and that the soul is immortal. Descartes' Meditations follow the same arc: the mind is distinct from [[body]], and the mind is "in its own nature immortal" (013-002).

**The Cave**: Plato's image of prisoners mistaking shadows for reality is an ancestor of Descartes' methodical [[doubt]]. Both argue that ordinary experience is systematically misleading.

## Plato and Descartes: Structural Parallels

| Theme | Platonic Expression | Cartesian Expression | Key Difference |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Knowledge of reality | Forms known by intellect | Clear and distinct ideas in the mind | Forms are transcendent; ideas are innate mental contents |
| Soul-body relation | Soul imprisoned in body (*Phaedo*) | Real distinction of mind and body (*Meditations* VI) | Descartes proves distinction; Plato asserts it |
| Source of error | Sensory illusion (Cave) | Precipitate judgment (Fourth Meditation) | Plato: senses deceive; Descartes: will outruns intellect |
| Method of ascent | Dialectic (Socratic elenchus) | Methodical doubt + analysis/synthesis | Plato: interrogation of interlocutors; Descartes: solitary meditation |
| The Good/God | Form of the Good illuminates intelligibles | God guarantees clear and distinct perception | Plato: impersonal principle; Descartes: personal, veracious deity |
| Immortality | Soul's pre-existence and reincarnation | Mind's natural immortality (synopsis) | Descartes: natural, not transmigratory; no memory of prior existence |
