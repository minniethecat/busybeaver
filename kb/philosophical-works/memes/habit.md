## Definition

Habit (*consuetudo*, *habitude*) in Descartes' philosophy refers to an acquired disposition of the mind: a pattern of thinking, judging, or perceiving that becomes automatic through repetition. Descartes treats habit as a double-edged phenomenon — habitual beliefs inherited from the senses and childhood are the primary obstacle to philosophical knowledge, but carefully cultivated habits of clear and distinct perception are the goal of his method.

## Habit as an Epistemological Obstacle

In the *Meditations*, Descartes identifies deeply ingrained habits of judgment as the main reason we err. From childhood, we form the habit of judging that ideas resembling external objects are caused by those objects. This "habit of judging in this way" leads to the prejudice that material things exist as they appear to the senses (124-002).

The skeptical method of the First Meditation is designed precisely to break these habits: "they may acquire the habit of no longer trusting so confidently in the senses" (153-015). Descartes describes the need to "turn the mind away from the senses" — a deliberate counter-habitual practice — before genuine philosophical knowledge is possible.

> Descartes advises that readers should spend "months, or at least weeks" meditating on the First Meditation doubts so they may "acquire the habit of conceiving [the mind] distinctly" apart from the body (153-016).

## Habit as a Methodological Tool

Descartes views his method as a form of mental training. In the *Discourse*, he notes: "my mind was becoming gradually habituated to clearer and more distinct conceptions" (005-013). The mathematical exercises of the method — analysis and synthesis — cultivate the habit of clear perception.

Similarly, he writes that those who practice his method "will acquire the habit" of reasoning geometrically (009-006). Habit formation through practice is the pedagogical mechanism by which the method transforms the mind.

## Habit in Ordinary Life

Descartes distinguishes the habits of philosophical inquiry from those of ordinary speech. Many people "are in the habit of spontaneously inventing certain ideas" when they hear words, confusing linguistic facility with genuine understanding (008-008). The habit of study must be carefully distinguished from mere loquaciousness: Descartes addresses those "who unite good sense with habits of study" (009-011).

## Habit and the Body

Descartes also recognizes bodily habits. The mind-body union produces habitual associations: "we are not in the habit of believing that there exists anything incorporeal" (125-002), and the embodied mind "is not in the habit of referring all things" to pure intellect (130-002). Breaking these corporeal habits of thought is a central task of the *Meditations*.
