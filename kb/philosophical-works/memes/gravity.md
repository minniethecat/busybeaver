**Gravity** (*gravitas*) serves as a key example in Descartes' critique of scholastic physics and his advocacy for a mechanistic account of natural phenomena. Rather than treating gravity as an inherent "quality" or "form" of heavy bodies — as the scholastics did — Descartes explains it in terms of the motion and impact of imperceptible particles, consistent with his broader project of reducing all physical phenomena to matter in motion.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes addresses gravity in several contexts, most notably as an illustration of the kind of explanation rejected by his mechanistic physics. In the *Discourse on the Method*, he presents gravity as a phenomenon whose explanation must be sought not in "forms" or "real qualities" but in the mechanical interactions of bodies: "I did not suppose that there was gravity in terrestrial bodies; but although I did not as yet know what it was, I thought that it was much better to reason in this way" — that is, mechanistically (022-007). In the *Principles of Philosophy*, he groups gravity with other sensible qualities — "light, heat, gravity, and the like" — that traditional philosophy had treated as real properties inhering in bodies but that mechanistic physics must explain through the configuration and motion of particles (022-013, 048-002). Descartes further distances himself from the scholastic attribution of gravity as an intrinsic property, noting that his own physics does not assume bodies to possess such occult qualities (060-002). The Cartesian treatment of gravity thus exemplifies the transition from qualitative to quantitative physics in the 17th century.

>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part VI (022-007)

> — Discourse on the Method, Part VI (022-013)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' rejection of gravity as a real quality has important implications for his physics and metaphysics. First, it exemplifies his commitment to the principle that all physical explanation must appeal only to the "modes" of extension — size, shape, motion, and the laws governing their interaction — excluding all occult qualities and forms. Second, it contributes to his argument that physics can be deduced from first principles (metaphysics) rather than accumulated through empirical observation alone. Third, Descartes' own vortex theory of gravity, according to which terrestrial bodies are driven toward the center of the Earth by the motion of subtle celestial matter, was an influential (though ultimately unsuccessful) attempt at a unified mechanistic cosmology. The theory was later superseded by Newton's law of universal gravitation, which introduced a concept — action at a distance — that Cartesians regarded as a regression to occult qualities.

## Historical Context

Before Descartes, the standard scholastic account treated gravity (*gravitas*) as a real quality inherent in heavy bodies, causing them to move toward their "natural place" at the center of the universe. This teleological explanation — bodies fall because they seek their natural place — was explicitly rejected by early modern mechanists. Galileo, whom Descartes admired, had already begun to mathematize the study of falling bodies without speculating about the "cause" of gravity. Descartes' contribution was to integrate the rejection of real qualities into a comprehensive mechanistic physics grounded in his metaphysics of extended substance. The debate over the nature of gravity became a central fault line between Cartesian and Newtonian physics in the late 17th and early 18th centuries.

## Criticisms and Responses

Newton's demonstration that gravity operates universally and according to precise mathematical laws posed the most significant challenge to the Cartesian account. In the *General Scholium* to the *Principia* (1687), Newton famously declared "I frame no hypotheses" (*Hypotheses non fingo*) regarding the cause of gravity, while insisting on its reality as a force. Cartesians, including Huygens and Leibniz, objected that Newton's gravitational force was a reversion to the very occult qualities that the mechanical philosophy had sought to eliminate. Descartes himself, having died before the publication of the *Principia*, could not respond to this objection, but his methodological principle — that genuine physical explanation must be mechanical — remained influential even among those who rejected his specific vortex theory.
