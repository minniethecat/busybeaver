# René Descartes

**René Descartes** (31 March 1596 – 11 February 1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and natural philosopher whose work marks the transition from medieval scholasticism to modern philosophy. His *[[discourse-on-the-method|Discourse on the Method]]* (1637), *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations on First Philosophy]]* (1641), and *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]* (1644) constitute one of the most ambitious philosophical projects ever attempted: the complete reconstruction of human [[knowledge]] from indubitable first principles. Descartes' [[cogito]] ("I think, therefore I am"), his proof of [[god|God's]] existence, his [[dualism|mind-body dualism]], and his mechanistic physics shaped the course of Western thought for the next three centuries.

## Biography

Descartes was born in **La Haye en Touraine** (now Descartes, France) in 1596 to a family of the minor nobility. His mother died shortly after his birth. He was educated at the **Jesuit College of La Flèche** (1607–1615), where he received a thorough grounding in Aristotelian scholasticism, mathematics, and classical literature — an education he would later subject to radical critique in the *Discourse*. He studied law at the University of Poitiers (1616) but never practiced.

In 1618, Descartes enlisted in the Dutch States Army under **[[maurice-of-nassau|Maurice of Nassau]]**, where he met the Dutch mathematician and philosopher **[[isaac-beeckman|Isaac Beeckman]]**, who rekindled his interest in mathematics and natural philosophy. The following year he joined the Bavarian army, and on the night of **10–11 November 1619**, while quartered near Ulm, Descartes experienced a series of dreams that he interpreted as a divine calling to establish a universal science based on mathematical [[method]].

From 1628 to 1649, Descartes lived primarily in the **Dutch Republic**, where relative religious tolerance and intellectual freedom allowed him to develop his philosophical system. He never married but had a daughter, **Francine** (1635–1640), with a servant; her death at age five was, he wrote, the greatest sorrow of his life.

In **1649**, Descartes accepted an invitation from **[[queen-christina-of-sweden|Queen Christina of Sweden]]** to Stockholm. Required to tutor the queen at 5 a.m. in the bitter Swedish winter, Descartes contracted pneumonia and died on 11 February 1650, aged 53.

## Relationship to [This Corpus]

This wiki is built from Descartes' own writings — the **Veitch translation** (1901) of the *Discourse on the Method*, *Meditations on First Philosophy* (with Objections and Replies), and *Principles of Philosophy*. Every page is anchored in Descartes' own words through the PN (paragraph number) citation system, spanning 156 chapters across three major works.

Descartes' philosophy, as presented in this corpus, can be organized into four major projects:

1. **The Method** (*Discourse* Parts I–VI): The rejection of scholastic education, the four rules of method, and the provisional moral code
2. **The Metaphysics** (*Meditations* I–VI): Methodical [[doubt]], the *cogito*, the proofs of [[god|God]], the real distinction of [[mind]] and [[body]], and the foundation of certain [[knowledge]]
3. **The Physics** (*Principles* Parts II–IV, *The World*): The laws of motion, the vortex theory, mechanistic cosmology, and the explanation of all natural phenomena by extension and motion alone
4. **The Anthropology** (*The Passions of the Soul*): The union of mind and body, the physiology of the passions, and the foundations of ethics

## Key Ideas

**Methodical Doubt and the *Cogito***: Descartes' most famous contribution is his method of radical [[doubt]] — the systematic rejection of any belief that can be doubted — leading to the one indubitable truth: "I think, therefore I am" (*cogito ergo sum*). This is not a logical deduction but a direct intuition of the thinking self's existence. The *cogito* establishes that I am essentially a thinking thing ([[mind]]), setting the stage for all subsequent philosophy.

**Clear and Distinct Perception as Criterion of Truth**: From the *cogito*, Descartes derives his criterion: whatever I [[perception|perceive]] clearly and distinctly must be true. This criterion requires a divine guarantee — God, being perfect, cannot be a deceiver — which Descartes provides through his proofs of God's existence (the trademark argument and the ontological argument).

**Mind-Body Dualism**: Descartes argues that [[mind]] and [[body]] are distinct substances. Mind is essentially thinking (*res cogitans*), body is essentially extended (*res extensa*). They can exist independently, though in human beings they are "substantially united." This [[dualism]] grounds both the [[immortality]] of the soul and mechanistic physics.

**Mechanistic Physics**: Descartes rejects Aristotelian substantial forms and final [[cause|causes]] in favor of a physics of matter in motion. All natural phenomena — from planetary orbits to animal bodies — are explained by the size, shape, and motion of corpuscles. This is the founding vision of the mechanical philosophy that dominated 17th-century science.

**The Tree of Philosophy**: Descartes compares philosophy to a tree: "The roots are metaphysics, the trunk is physics, and the branches that spring from the trunk are all the other sciences, which may be reduced to three principal ones, namely, medicine, mechanics, and morals."

## In Descartes' Texts

> I observed that I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not; on the contrary, from the very circumstance that I thought of doubting the truth of other things, it most clearly and certainly followed that I was.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-002)

> I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing; so that this "me," that is to say, the soul by which I am what I am, is wholly distinct from the body.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-002)

> Good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed; for every one thinks himself so abundantly provided with it, that those even who are the most difficult to satisfy in everything else, do not usually desire a larger measure of this quality than they already possess.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part I (004-001)
