Descartes held a distinctive view of **definition** that departs sharply from the Aristotelian-scholastic tradition. He rejected the method of defining terms through genus and differentia, arguing that the most fundamental concepts — being, thought, certainty — are *self-evident* and cannot be made clearer by logical definition. Attempting to define them only obscures what is already known by the natural light of reason.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' position on definition appears most clearly in the *Search after Truth*, where he argues that "truths which are of themselves evident are obscured by logical definitions" (068-001). The scholastic practice of defining every term by genus and specific difference, Descartes claims, is not only unnecessary but harmful — it makes simple things complex and confuses what should be intuitively grasped.

This stance follows directly from Descartes' criterion of clear and distinct perception. A clear idea is one that is "present and apparent to an attentive mind"; a distinct idea is one that is "so precise and different from all other objects that it contains within itself nothing but what is clear." When an idea is already clear and distinct, definition adds nothing. Worse, substituting a verbal definition for the direct intuition of a simple nature risks introducing error by replacing the thing itself with a conventional label.

## Key Passages

> I remark that there are many truths which, though of themselves sufficiently evident, are obscured by logical definitions; and that such are not to be reckoned among acquired knowledge, but as things which we know from our birth.
>
> — Search after Truth (068-001)

> I observed that, whilst I thus wished to think that all was false, it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be something; and as I observed that this truth, *I think, therefore I am*, was so certain and of such evidence that no ground of doubt, however extravagant, could be alleged by the skeptics capable of shaking it, I concluded that I might, without scruple, accept it as the first principle of the philosophy of which I was in search.
>
> — Discourse on the Method, Part IV (007-003)

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' rejection of scholastic definition supports his broader methodology. The *cogito* is not the conclusion of a syllogism (it is not deduced from "all thinking things exist" plus "I think") but a direct intellectual intuition. Similarly, the existence of God is demonstrated not through formal logic alone but through the innate idea of an infinite and perfect being.

This has implications for Descartes' theory of innate ideas. If the most fundamental concepts — being, unity, duration, thought — are innate, then their content is known through the mind's direct acquaintance with itself, not through verbal definitions learned from authority. Descartes' anti-definitionalism is thus part of his broader rejection of book learning in favor of the "book of the world" and introspection.

## Historical Context

The distrust of logical definition has precedents in the skeptical tradition — Montaigne's *Apology for Raymond Sebond* mocks the endless definitions of the schools — and in the mathematical turn of early modern science. Descartes was influenced by the method of analysis used by ancient geometers, where first principles are exhibited rather than defined.

Pascal, a younger contemporary and admirer of Descartes, similarly held that certain primitive terms — space, time, number — cannot be defined without circularity. This shared anti-definitionalism reflects a broader seventeenth-century shift away from verbal-logical methods toward intuitive-mathematical modes of knowledge.

## Criticisms and Responses

Leibniz, while sharing much of Descartes' rationalism, criticized the neglect of definition. He sought to construct a *characteristica universalis* — a universal symbolic language in which all concepts could be defined from primitive simples. For Leibniz, the Cartesian claim that some ideas are simply "clear and distinct" amounts to an abandonment of rigor.

Later empiricists, particularly Locke, though critical of innate ideas, retained Descartes' suspicion of purely verbal definition by grounding all ideas in experience. The debate over the role and limits of definition continues in contemporary philosophy, especially in discussions of conceptual analysis.
