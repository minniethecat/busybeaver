Sadness (*tristitia*) is one of the six primitive passions in Descartes' taxonomy, along with wonder, love, hatred, desire, and joy. It is defined as the soul's perception of a present evil — something harmful or disagreeable to the mind-body composite. As a primitive passion, sadness cannot be reduced to any combination of more basic emotions; instead, it serves as a component from which complex emotional states are built.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

In the *Principles of Philosophy*, Descartes lists sadness among the principal passions: "the emotions of anger, joy, sadness, love, etc." (106-002), treating them as a distinct class of mental states that arise from the union of mind and body. Sadness is the counterpart to joy: where joy is the perception of a present good, sadness is the perception of a present evil (047-002).

The mechanistic basis of sadness mirrors that of the other passions. Bodily states — changes in the animal spirits, heart rate, and other physiological processes — cause the soul to experience sadness. Descartes' mechanistic account means that sadness, like all passions, has a bodily component that operates according to physical laws. Yet the experience of sadness is irreducibly mental: it is the soul, not the body, that feels sad.

In the *Discourse on Method*, Descartes makes a striking metaphysical claim about sadness: "doubt, inconstancy, sadness, and suchlike, could not be found in God" (007-004). This is part of his argument that the idea of God as perfect being requires that God be conceived as pure actuality without any privation — and sadness, like all passions, involves privation or lack. This theological point underscores the dual nature of sadness: it belongs to the human composite, not to pure intellect or to God.

## Arguments and Implications

The status of sadness as a primitive passion has important implications for Descartes' moral philosophy. Because sadness is a natural signal of present evil, it serves a protective function — it alerts the soul to what is harmful and inclines it toward avoidance. The wise person does not seek to eliminate sadness but to ensure that it is proportionate to its object. Excessive or misplaced sadness is a pathology of the passions, and the cultivation of wisdom involves learning to feel sadness only at genuine evils.

The connection between sadness and the body also illuminates Descartes' account of the mind-body union. Sadness is not a pure intellectual judgment (as the Stoics held) but a psycho-physical state that we experience passively. This passivity distinguishes passions from actions of the will and is key to Descartes' theory of human freedom: we are free precisely because we can resist or redirect the inclinations that passions like sadness produce.

## Historical Context

Before Descartes, the dominant philosophical accounts of sadness came from the Stoics and the scholastics. The Stoics classified sadness (*lupe*) as one of the four primary passions (along with fear, desire, and pleasure) and argued that it is always irrational — the sage, who judges only what is within his control to be good or evil, never experiences sadness. Aquinas, following Aristotle, gave a more nuanced account in which sadness is a passion of the concupiscible appetite responding to present evil.

Descartes' innovation is to combine a mechanistic physiology with a taxonomy of six primitive passions that differs from both the Stoic and Thomistic schemes. By making joy and sadness the two polar passions responding to good and evil respectively, Descartes provides a simplified framework that Spinoza would later systematize into the three primary affects of joy, sadness, and desire.

## Criticisms and Responses

One difficulty for Descartes' account is the relationship between physical pain and the passion of sadness. Physical pain is a bodily sensation, while sadness is a passion of the soul — yet they are deeply intertwined. Descartes addresses this by distinguishing sensory perceptions (like pain) from the passions proper, while acknowledging that the former typically trigger the latter. The close connection between sadness and bodily states also raises the mind-body interaction problem that Elisabeth pressed Descartes on.

Spinoza radicalized the Cartesian taxonomy by arguing that all emotions reduce to combinations of three fundamental affects: joy (*laetitia*), sadness (*tristitia*), and desire (*cupiditas*). In Spinoza's system, sadness is the transition to a lesser state of perfection — a purely immanent dynamic within the individual, without reference to external goods or evils. This internalization of sadness represents both a development of and a departure from Descartes' more embodied account.
