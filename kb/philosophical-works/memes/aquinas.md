# Thomas Aquinas

**Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274) was the towering figure of medieval scholasticism and the philosopher whose system [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' generation was trained to think within — and against. Aquinas's grand synthesis of [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and Christian theology, articulated in the monumental *Summa Theologica*, defined the intellectual orthodoxy of European universities for four centuries. Descartes' project of radical doubt, his rejection of substantial forms, and his demand for [[method|methodological]] certainty are best understood as a deliberate repudiation of the Thomistic-Aristotelian framework.

## Biography

Thomas was born at Roccasecca, near Naples, to a noble family. Against his family's wishes, he joined the Dominican Order in 1244. He studied under Albertus Magnus at Cologne and later taught at the University of Paris, where he defended the newly recovered works of [[aristotle|Aristotle]] against conservative theologians who viewed them as incompatible with Christian doctrine.

Aquinas produced an astonishing body of work before his death at age 49: the *Summa Theologica*, the *Summa Contra Gentiles*, commentaries on Aristotle and Scripture, and numerous disputed questions. He was canonized in 1323 and declared a Doctor of the Church in 1567. His thought — Thomism — became the official philosophy of the Catholic Church.

## Relationship to Descartes

Descartes' relationship to Aquinas is one of systematic opposition:

1. **Rejection of Substantial Forms**: Aquinas, following Aristotle, explained natural phenomena in terms of substantial forms — immaterial principles that give things their nature. Descartes reduced all [[body|bodies]] to extension and motion, eliminating substantial forms entirely. The Cartesian physical world is a machine; Aquinas's is a hierarchy of beings defined by essences.

2. **From Final to Efficient Causation**: Aquinas's physics was teleological — things act toward ends determined by their natures. Descartes restricted [[causation]] to efficient causes, eliminating final causes from natural philosophy. No purpose, only mechanism.

3. **The Role of the Senses**: Aquinas held that all [[knowledge]] begins in the senses — *nihil est in intellectu quod non prius fuerit in sensu* (nothing is in the intellect that was not first in the senses). Descartes' [[method]] of doubt systematically calls sensory evidence into question, grounding certainty instead in the [[cogito]] and clear and distinct [[idea|ideas]].

4. **Proofs of God**: Aquinas's "Five Ways" argue from features of the world (motion, causation, contingency) to the existence of [[god|God]]. Descartes' arguments for God's existence — in the *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* — proceed from the [[idea]] of God within the [[mind]], not from external evidence. The direction of argument is reversed entirely.

## Key Ideas

**The Five Ways**: Aquinas's five arguments for God's existence — from motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and teleology — were the standard proofs Descartes' readers would have known. Descartes' decision to replace them with arguments from the [[idea]] of God was a deliberate methodological break.

**Essence and Existence**: Aquinas distinguished between essence (what a thing is) and existence (that it is). In God alone are essence and existence identical. This distinction shaped later scholastic metaphysics, which Descartes rejected with his doctrine that essence and existence are inseparable in all [[substance|substances]].

**Natural Law**: Aquinas's theory that moral principles are accessible to [[reason]] without revelation — they are "promulgated" by God through human nature — influenced early modern natural law theory, though Descartes himself showed little interest in moral philosophy.

## In Descartes' Works

> Descartes rarely names Aquinas directly, but scholastic philosophy — overwhelmingly Thomistic in its vocabulary and structure — is the unnamed adversary throughout his corpus. When Descartes insists on beginning from doubt rather than authority, on clear and distinct [[perception]] rather than syllogism, and on mechanism rather than substantial forms, he is dismantling the Thomistic edifice his Jesuit teachers had built at La Flèche.
