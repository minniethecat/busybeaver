# Existence

In Descartes' philosophy, **existence** (*existentia*) is the actuality of a thing — the fact that it is, as opposed to what it is (its essence). Descartes distinguishes between **possible existence** (contained in the idea of finite things), **actual existence** (the fact that a finite thing exists now), and **necessary existence** (the mode of existence belonging uniquely to [[god|God]], whose essence entails existence). "In the idea or concept of every other thing, possible existence is indeed contained, but in the concept of God, necessary existence is contained" (153-019). The concept of existence is foundational to Descartes' [[substance]] ontology, his argument for God, and his account of the self.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' treatment of existence is inseparable from his theory of substance. A substance is "every thing in which there exists any object we perceive, that is, any property, or quality, or attribute" (153-008). Existence is not itself a property but the actuality of a substance and its attributes.

The most philosophically significant distinction is between possible and necessary existence. For finite substances, "possible existence is indeed contained" in their idea (153-019) — there is no contradiction in conceiving of a finite thing as existing or not existing. But for God, existence belongs to the very concept. "The existence of God is known from the consideration of his nature alone, and from the fact that his essence involves existence" (153-034). This is the ontological argument in its Cartesian form.

Existence also plays a crucial role in Descartes' causal principles. "Nothing exists of which it cannot be inquired what is the cause of its existing" (153-023). And: "The perfection of a thing actually existent cannot have *nothing*, or a thing not existing, as the cause of its existence" (153-025). These principles ground the cosmological argument in the Third Meditation.

## Arguments and Implications

**The Ontological Argument**: Existence is a perfection. God, by definition, possesses all perfections. Therefore, God possesses existence — God necessarily exists. Just as the idea of a triangle necessarily includes the property that its angles equal two right angles, "necessary existence is contained in the nature or in the concept of God" (153-037).

**The Causal Argument**: My existence at this moment does not explain my existence at the next moment. "The whole time of my life may be divided into an infinity of parts, each of which is in no way dependent on the others" (153-049). My continued existence requires a continuously acting cause, which must be God — a being whose essence includes existence and therefore needs no external cause.

**The *Cogito* and Self-Existence**: The *cogito* establishes that I exist ("I think, therefore I am"), but it does not establish *what* I am or *why* I continue to exist. A full account of my existence requires ascending to God as the sustaining cause.

**Degrees of Reality**: Descartes' causal principle about existence is hierarchical: "There are diverse degrees of reality, that is, of entity [or perfection]: for substance has more reality than accident or mode, and infinite substance than finite" (153-028). The cause must have at least as much reality (perfection) as the effect — a finite thing cannot be the cause of the idea of the infinite.

## Historical Context

Descartes' ontological argument revives and transforms **[[anselm|Anselm's]]** argument from the *Proslogion*: God is "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," and a God that exists is greater than one that does not. Descartes simplifies the argument by treating existence as a perfection directly contained in the concept of a supremely perfect being.

**[[aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]]** had rejected the ontological argument, holding that we cannot know God's essence and therefore cannot deduce God's existence from it. Descartes bypasses this objection: we have a clear and distinct idea of God as a supremely perfect being, and this idea suffices for the argument.

## Criticisms and Responses

**[[kant|Kant]]** famously objected that existence is not a predicate — "being is obviously not a real predicate; that is, it is not a concept of something which could be added to the concept of a thing." To say that a triangle exists is not to add a property to the concept of a triangle; it is to posit an instance of the concept. If existence is not a predicate, the ontological argument collapses.

**[[hume|Hume]]** rejected the causal argument by questioning whether the idea of God requires an actually infinite cause. We can, Hume argued, construct the idea of the infinite by augmenting finite qualities — no actual infinite being is required.

**Gassendi** objected that existence is not a perfection in the same sense as omniscience or omnipotence. A non-existent perfect being is a contradiction not because existence is a property but because a "non-existent being" is incoherent.
