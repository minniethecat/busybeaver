# Michel de Montaigne

**Michel de Montaigne** (1533–1592) was the most influential sceptical voice of the Renaissance — the thinker whose *Essais* (1580–88) popularized Pyrrhonian arguments and created the intellectual environment that made Descartes' project simultaneously urgent and possible. Descartes never names Montaigne directly in the *Meditations*, but the sceptical crisis to which the *[[doubt|method of doubt]]* responds was largely of Montaigne's making. The *[[meditations-on-first-philosophy|Meditations]]* can be read as Descartes' attempt to answer the question Montaigne posed: if the senses deceive, reason falters, and custom governs belief, what remains?

## Biography

Montaigne was born at the Château de Montaigne in Périgord, the son of a wealthy merchant family elevated to the nobility. He was raised speaking Latin as his first language (his father hired a German tutor who spoke no French), educated at the Collège de Guyenne in Bordeaux, and studied law — probably at Toulouse. He served as a magistrate in the Bordeaux Parlement, where he formed his deep friendship with Étienne de La Boétie (the subject of his famous essay "On Friendship").

In 1571, at age 38, Montaigne retired from public life to his library tower, where he began writing the *Essais* — a genre he invented. The *Essais* are not systematic philosophy but a sustained experiment in self-examination: "I am myself the matter of my book." He traveled through Switzerland, Germany, and Italy (1580–81), served two terms as Mayor of Bordeaux (1581–85), and continued revising and expanding the *Essais* until his death in 1592.

## Relationship to Descartes

Montaigne's relationship to Descartes is one of **problem and response**:

**The Sceptical Crisis**: Montaigne's longest essay, the "Apology for Raymond Sebond" (Book II, Chapter 12 of the *Essais*), is an encyclopedia of sceptical arguments drawn from Sextus Empiricus' *Outlines of Pyrrhonism*. Montaigne argues: the senses are unreliable, reason is self-refuting, customs vary infinitely across cultures, animals may be wiser than humans, and no criterion of [[truth]] can be defended without circularity. The famous motto "Que sçay-je?" ("What do I know?") captures his conclusion — a learned ignorance that humbles human pretension to knowledge.

**Descartes' Response**: Descartes' method of [[doubt]] in the First Meditation is a direct answer to Montaigne's scepticism. Where Montaigne deployed sceptical arguments to induce humility and fideism (since reason fails, one must rely on divine revelation), Descartes deployed them to find an unshakeable foundation. The *[[cogito]]* — "I think, therefore I am" — is the Archimedean point that Montaigne claimed could not be found. If Montaigne is the prosecutor of reason, Descartes is its defense attorney, determined to show that [[certainty]] is possible after all.

## Key Ideas

**The Essay as Method**: Montaigne invented the essay form — a tentative, personal, digressive mode of writing that embodies his philosophical commitments. The essay does not prove or demonstrate; it tries, tests, weighs. This is philosophy as *inquiry* rather than *system* — an approach fundamentally opposed to Descartes' geometric ideal of philosophical exposition.

**The Fluidity of the Self**: "I cannot fix my object," Montaigne writes. "I paint the passage." The self is not a stable [[substance]] but a flux of perceptions, moods, and judgments. Descartes' discovery of a fixed, thinking substance — the *res cogitans* — is precisely the kind of claim Montaigne's self-observation undermines.

**Fideism**: Montaigne's scepticism serves a religious purpose: by demonstrating reason's impotence, it makes room for faith. Descartes reverses this: reason, properly employed, can demonstrate the existence of [[god|God]] and the immortality of the [[soul]] — faith need not rest on sceptical despair.
