# Immanuel Kant

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) was the central figure of the German Enlightenment and arguably the most important philosopher since [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. His "critical philosophy" — articulated in the three *Critiques* — took Descartes' turn to the [[subject]] as its starting point but subjected the capacities and limits of [[reason]] itself to systematic scrutiny. Where Descartes sought to ground knowledge in the certainty of the *[[cogito]]*, Kant asked: what must the subject be like for such experience to be possible at all? This "Copernican revolution" in philosophy shifted the focus from objects of knowledge to the conditions of knowing.

## Biography

Kant was born in Königsberg, East Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia), where he spent his entire life. He studied at the University of Königsberg and served as a private tutor before becoming a professor of logic and metaphysics in 1770. His early work was in the Leibnizian-Wolffian rationalist tradition, but he later credited **[[hume|David Hume]]** with awakening him from his "dogmatic slumber," prompting the critical turn that produced the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781, revised 1787).

Kant never married and lived a famously regimented life — his daily walks were said to be so regular that the citizens of Königsberg set their clocks by them. He died in 1804, his last words reportedly being "Es ist gut" (It is good).

## Relationship to Descartes

Kant's relationship to Descartes is one of deep engagement and fundamental transformation. He adopted Descartes' starting point — the thinking subject as the foundation of philosophical inquiry — but rejected the metaphysical conclusions Descartes drew from it.

1. **From the Cogito to Transcendental Apperception**: Descartes' *cogito* establishes the existence of the thinking self as a [[substance]]. Kant argued that the "I think" is not knowledge of a substantial soul but the formal condition of all experience — the transcendental unity of apperception.

2. **From Innate Ideas to Categories**: Descartes explained certain [[idea|ideas]] (God, the self, mathematical truths) as innate. Kant replaced innateness with the *a priori* categories of the understanding — pure concepts that structure experience rather than being derived from it.

3. **From God as Guarantor to Reason's Self-Critique**: Descartes needed [[god|God]] to guarantee the truth of clear and distinct [[perception|perceptions]]. Kant argued that reason must instead determine its own limits — the project of critique — without appeal to divine veracity.

4. **From Substance Dualism to Phenomenal/Noumenal Distinction**: Descartes' [[dualism]] of [[mind]] and [[body]] becomes, in Kant, the distinction between phenomena (things as they appear to us) and noumena (things as they are in themselves).

## Key Ideas

**The Synthetic A Priori**: Kant's central innovation. Unlike analytic judgments (predicate contained in subject), synthetic judgments add new information. Unlike a posteriori judgments (derived from experience), a priori judgments are independent of experience. Kant argued that mathematics, the principles of natural science, and metaphysics itself consist of synthetic a priori judgments — a claim Descartes would have recognized from his own geometrical method.

**The Categorical Imperative**: In ethics, Kant rejected all consequentialist and theological foundations. The moral law is a command of pure practical reason: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law" (first formulation, *Groundwork* 4:421). This marks a decisive break with Descartes' voluntarist ethics grounded in divine will.

**The Limits of Reason**: Kant's critique of traditional metaphysics — including Cartesian rational psychology and the ontological argument — demonstrated that [[reason]] oversteps its bounds when it claims knowledge of God, the soul, or the world as a whole. These are regulative [[idea|ideas]], not objects of theoretical knowledge.

## In Descartes' Legacy

> Kant did not engage Descartes' texts directly as a commentator. Rather, Descartes' philosophy had become the common inheritance of European rationalism by Kant's time. The *Critique of Pure Reason* is, among other things, a reckoning with the Cartesian tradition — from Descartes through [[spinoza|Spinoza]] and [[leibniz|Leibniz]] to Christian Wolff — that Kant inherited and transformed.
