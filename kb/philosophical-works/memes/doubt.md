# Doubt

In Descartes' philosophy, **doubt** (*dubitatio*) is not a psychological state but a **methodological instrument** — a systematic procedure for testing beliefs by withholding assent from anything that can be called into question. Descartes deploys methodical doubt in the *Discourse on the Method* and *Meditations on First Philosophy* to clear the ground of uncertain opinions and discover indubitable foundations for knowledge.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces methodical doubt in Part IV of the *Discourse on the Method*: "I had long remarked that in relation to practice it is sometimes necessary to follow opinions which we know to be very uncertain... But as I then wished to occupy myself solely in the search after truth, I thought that I ought to do precisely the contrary, and to reject as absolutely false everything in which I could imagine the least doubt" (004-006). This is doubt as a deliberate strategy, not a spontaneous feeling.

The *Meditations* develops doubt through increasingly radical stages. The First Meditation deploys three waves of doubt: the unreliability of the senses, the dream argument (we cannot distinguish waking from dreaming with certainty), and the evil demon hypothesis (a powerful deceiver might make us err even about mathematics). Each wave sweeps away a broader class of beliefs.

In the *Principles of Philosophy* Appendix, Descartes provides a pedagogical guide for readers to cultivate this doubting habit: "I request that my readers consider how feeble are the reasons that have hitherto led them to repose faith in their senses, and how uncertain are all the judgments which they afterwards founded on them; and that they will revolve this consideration in their mind so long and so frequently, that, in fine, they may acquire the habit of no longer trusting so confidently in their senses" (153-015). Doubt is thus a skill to be practiced, not merely a proposition to be entertained.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' doubt is distinct from **scepticism**. The ancient sceptics (Pyrrhonists, Academics) doubted in order to achieve tranquillity (*ataraxia*) by suspending judgment permanently. Descartes doubts in order to overcome doubt — to find something indubitable that can serve as a foundation. His doubt is **methodical**, not **sceptical**: it is a tool for construction, not a destination.

The *cogito* — "I think, therefore I am" — is the indubitable truth that survives even the most radical doubt: "I observed that I could suppose that I had no body, and that there was no world nor any place in which I might be; but that I could not therefore suppose that I was not" (007-002). The very act of doubting proves the doubter's existence.

Descartes warns that doubt is not a one-time exercise but a habit that must be cultivated. Readers must "not leave off considering it until they have acquired the habit of conceiving it distinctly" (153-016). Those who attempt to doubt casually — "call in doubt what is clearly and distinctly conceived" (153-021) without proper preparation — misunderstand the method.

## Historical Context

Descartes' methodical doubt responds to the **sceptical crisis** of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The rediscovery of Sextus Empiricus' *Outlines of Pyrrhonism* and the religious wars following the Reformation had eroded confidence in traditional authorities. **Montaigne's** essays popularized sceptical arguments, and the **libertins érudits** used scepticism to challenge religious orthodoxy.

Descartes' innovation was to turn scepticism against itself. By pushing doubt to its absolute limit, he discovered that the sceptic cannot doubt his own existence. Scepticism thus becomes a ladder that can be kicked away once climbed — a propaedeutic to certainty, not its enemy.

## Criticisms and Responses

Critics have questioned whether Descartes' doubt can be as radical as he claims. **Peirce** argued that genuine doubt is involuntary — we cannot will ourselves to doubt what we actually believe. Descartes' "pretend" doubt may not genuinely undermine the beliefs it targets.

**Wittgenstein**, in *On Certainty*, argued that doubt presupposes a framework of unquestioned certainties — language, logic, a shared world. The attempt to doubt everything simultaneously is incoherent because doubt only makes sense against a background of what is not doubted.

Descartes' response, implicit in his method, is that the meditator need only withhold **assent** from uncertain beliefs — not actively disbelieve them. The doubt is methodological, not psychological: it concerns the justification of beliefs, not the psychology of believing.
