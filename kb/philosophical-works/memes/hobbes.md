# Thomas Hobbes

**Thomas Hobbes** (1588–1679) was Descartes' most formidable contemporary critic. In 1641, [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]] circulated Descartes' *Meditations* to leading philosophers for objections, and Hobbes' "Third Set of Objections" cut to the heart of the Cartesian project: even if the *[[cogito]]* establishes that I think, it does not establish that I am an immaterial [[substance]]. "I am thinking" may be an activity of a material body. Hobbes' challenge forced Descartes to clarify the relationship between thinking and extension — and the exchange between the rationalist and the materialist set the terms for the [[mind-body problem]] that would dominate early modern philosophy.

## Biography

Hobbes was born prematurely in Westport, Wiltshire — his mother went into labor upon hearing news of the approaching Spanish Armada; he later joked that "fear and I were born twins." Educated at Oxford, he spent most of his career as tutor, secretary, and intellectual companion to the Cavendish family, Earls of Devonshire. This patronage gave him access to the intellectual circles of both England and continental Europe.

During his extended stays in Paris, Hobbes met [[marin-mersenne|Mersenne]], [[gassendi|Gassendi]], and through them became one of the philosophers asked to respond to Descartes' *Meditations*. His *Leviathan* (1651) — written during the English Civil War while Hobbes was in exile in Paris — is one of the founding texts of modern political philosophy. He returned to England under Cromwell, survived the Restoration (Charles II had been his mathematics pupil), and continued writing philosophy, mathematics, and translations until his death at age 91.

## Relationship to Descartes

Hobbes' engagement with Descartes was early, direct, and philosophically profound. Their exchange on the *Meditations* is among the most important philosophical correspondences of the 17th century.

**The Cogito Does Not Prove an Immaterial Soul**: Descartes argued from "I think" to "I am a thinking thing" — a substance whose essence is thought alone. Hobbes' objection is devastating in its simplicity: "It may be that a thinking thing is the *subject* of mind, reason, or intellect, and this subject may be corporeal." The inference from "I am thinking" to "I am an immaterial substance" is a non sequitur. Descartes' reply — that Hobbes simply does not understand the distinction between the *act* of thinking and the *substance* that thinks — was widely regarded as inadequate.

**Ideas as Images**: Descartes' theory of [[idea|ideas]] treats them as the immediate objects of thought — immaterial representations present to the mind. Hobbes, a materialist, insisted that all ideas derive from sensation: "there is no conception in a man's mind which has not first been begotten upon the organs of sense." Thinking is the manipulation of "phantasms" — decaying sense-impressions — and "understanding" is nothing but "conception caused by speech." There are no innate ideas.

**Reason as Computation**: For Descartes, [[reason]] is the natural light that intuits first principles and deduces consequences. For Hobbes, "reasoning is but reckoning" — the addition and subtraction of names according to conventional definitions. [[truth]] is not correspondence to eternal essences but the correct joining of names in propositions. This nominalist, computational account of reason anticipates key themes in 20th-century philosophy of language.

**Against the Ontological Argument**: Hobbes also objected to Descartes' proof of [[god|God]]'s existence in the Fifth Meditation. The argument from the idea of a supremely perfect being to the existence of that being assumes, Hobbes charged, that "existence is a perfection" — a claim he rejected. This objection was repeated by [[kant|Kant]] a century and a half later.

**Political Philosophy**: Though the exchange focused on metaphysics, Hobbes' *Leviathan* and Descartes' *Discourse on the Method* offer competing accounts of the relationship between [[knowledge]] and political order. Descartes sought to rebuild knowledge from [[certainty|certain]] foundations; Hobbes sought to rebuild political order from the science of human nature. Both were responding to the collapse of traditional authority — one epistemologically, the other politically.
