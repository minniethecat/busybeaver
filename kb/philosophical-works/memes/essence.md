Essence is a foundational concept in Descartes' metaphysics, designating what a thing most fundamentally is. In a decisive break with scholastic Aristotelianism, Descartes identifies a thing's essence not with a substantial form but with its principal attribute — the property from which all its other properties flow and without which the thing cannot be conceived.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes introduces his conception of essence through the *cogito* argument. In the *Discourse on Method*, he discovers that he is "a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking" — a being that requires no place and depends on no material thing (007-002). This insight establishes the pattern for his entire ontology: a substance is defined by a single principal attribute that constitutes its essence, and all other properties are modes of that attribute.

The doctrine receives systematic treatment in the *Principles of Philosophy*, where Descartes states that "there is... one principal property of every substance, which constitutes its nature or essence, and upon which all the others depend. Thus, extension in length, breadth, and depth, constitutes the nature of corporeal substance; and thought the nature of thinking substance" (111-002). This yields a clear dualism: mind and body have entirely distinct essences — thought and extension respectively — and neither can be understood in terms of the other.

Descartes also addresses the relationship between essence and existence. In created things, essence and existence are conceptually distinct: we can "distinguish, in all other things, essence from existence, and to imagine at will many ideas of things which neither are nor have been" (074-002). In God alone are essence and existence inseparable, since God's essence includes necessary existence — a cornerstone of the ontological argument.

Descartes' treatment of the will reveals an important nuance in his essence concept. The will, he says, is carried "voluntarily and freely, for that is of the essence of will" (153-029). Freedom is not an accidental property of will but belongs to its very nature — a claim with significant implications for his theodicy and account of error.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' essence doctrine has wide-ranging consequences. First, it underwrites his mind-body dualism: if mind and body have radically different essences, they must be distinct substances capable of existing independently. Second, it provides a criterion for clear and distinct perception: we grasp something clearly when we grasp its essence. Third, the essence-existence distinction in creatures supports the cosmological argument: if the essence of a finite thing does not include existence, its existence must be caused by something else.

The doctrine also shapes Descartes' physics. By identifying the essence of body with extension alone, he eliminates all occult qualities and substantial forms from physical explanation. All corporeal phenomena are reducible to modifications of extension: size, shape, position, motion. This methodological reduction is the foundation of his mechanistic natural philosophy.

## Historical Context

Descartes' essentialism transforms the Aristotelian-scholastic theory of essence. For Aristotle, essence (*to ti ên einai*) was what a thing is said to be in itself, articulated through genus and specific difference. For Aquinas, essence (*essentia*) was the "whatness" of a thing, distinct from its act of existing (*esse*). Descartes strips away the apparatus of substantial forms, matter-form composition, and the act-potency distinction, replacing it with a simpler schema: one substance, one principal attribute, many modes. This simplification was both a strength — it enabled a unified mathematical physics — and a source of difficulty, particularly in explaining the unity of human nature, where thought and extension must somehow interact.

## Criticisms and Responses

The most persistent criticism concerns the real distinction argument: does grasping the essence of mind as thinking and body as extended actually prove they can exist apart? Arnauld, in the Fourth Objections, pointed out that one might equally know a right triangle's essence without knowing the Pythagorean theorem, yet the theorem follows necessarily from that essence. Descartes replied that the real distinction requires a "complete" idea of each substance, but the criteria for completeness remain contested. Later rationalists, particularly Spinoza, rejected Descartes' substance dualism entirely, arguing that thought and extension are not essences of distinct substances but attributes of a single substance: God or Nature.
