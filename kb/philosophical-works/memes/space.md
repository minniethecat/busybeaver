# Space

In Descartes' philosophy, **space** is not an empty container or an absolute framework independent of bodies. Space is identical with corporeal extension: "the same extension in length, breadth, and depth, which constitutes space, constitutes body" (145-002). What we call "space" and what we call "body" are the same thing, distinguished only by a modal distinction of reason — we consider extension as particular and changeable when we call it body, and as generic and repeatable when we call it space.

## In Descartes' Philosophy

Descartes' theory of space follows from his identification of body with extension. In the *[[principles-of-philosophy|Principles of Philosophy]]*, he devotes an entire section (Part II, §§10–15) to establishing that space and body are not distinct realities. "It will be easy to discern that it is the same extension which constitutes the nature of body as of space, and that these two things are mutually diverse only as the nature of the genus and species differs from that of the individual" (146-002).

The argument proceeds by elimination. What do we think constitutes body? Hardness? "If the stone were liquefied or reduced to powder, it would no longer possess hardness, and yet would not cease to be a body" (146-002). Colour? Weight? Heat? All are accidents that can be removed while the body remains. The only thing that cannot be removed without destroying the body is extension. "After this examination we will find that nothing remains in the idea of body, except that it is something extended in length, breadth, and depth; and this something is comprised in our idea of space" (146-002).

The difference between space and body is therefore not in reality but in our mode of conception. "If we remove a stone from the space or place in which it was, we conceive that its extension also is taken away, because we judge this to be particular and inseparable from the stone" (147-002). But we also conceive that the extension of the place remains — it is now occupied by another body (air, subtle matter). The extension itself persists because extension is not a property of *this* body but the common nature of all body.

This doctrine has two important corollaries. First, there can be no vacuum: "a vacuum or space in which there is absolutely no body is repugnant to reason" (151-001), because wherever there is extension there is substance. Second, there can be no space beyond the world: the world is "indefinitely extended" — we cannot conceive a limit to extension, but neither can we affirm an actual infinite. "We...imagine beyond it spaces indefinitely extended" (034-002), but these spaces are not empty; they are filled with the same subtle matter that constitutes the heavens.

## Arguments and Implications

Descartes' identification of space with extension has three significant implications:

**The Rejection of Absolute Space**: For Descartes, space is not a pre-existing container in which bodies are placed. There is no "space itself" independent of the bodies that fill it. This positions Descartes against [[newton|Newton]]'s later doctrine of absolute space and in partial alignment with [[leibniz|Leibniz]]'s relational theory — though for Descartes, space is not a relation among bodies but the very substance of body considered generically.

**The Indefinite Universe**: Because space is extension and extension has no intrinsic limit, the universe is indefinite — not infinite in the strict sense (which is a property only of God) but unbounded. "We...imagine beyond it spaces indefinitely extended" (034-002). The idea of a finite world bounded by nothing is incoherent: the boundary itself would be extended, and hence corporeal. Descartes' indefinite universe replaces the closed medieval cosmos with an open Cartesian one.

**The Ground of Geometry**: If space is extension, and extension is the essence of body, then geometry — the science of extended magnitude — is the science of body. Descartes' analytic geometry (the union of algebra and geometry) becomes the universal language of physics. "The object of the geometers, which I conceived to be a continuous body or a space indefinitely extended in length, breadth, and height or depth" (007-005) is not an abstraction — it is physical reality itself.

## Historical Context

Descartes' theory of space is a response to two competing traditions. The **Aristotelian** tradition treated space as the innermost motionless boundary of the containing body — place (*topos*), not space in the abstract. The **atomist** tradition treated space as infinite void — the empty theatre in which atoms move. Descartes rejects both: against Aristotle, he holds that space is extension itself, not a relation of containment; against the atomists, he holds that space is always filled, never void.

Descartes' theory was enormously influential. [[spinoza|Spinoza]] adopted it wholesale: extension is an attribute of God, and space is the infinite mode of extension. [[leibniz|Leibniz]] rejected it, developing a relational theory in which space is the order of coexisting things. [[newton|Newton]] rejected it from the opposite direction, positing absolute space as the sensorium of God. The Leibniz-Clarke correspondence (1715–1716) — with Clarke defending Newton's absolute space against Leibniz's relationalism — was fought on ground Descartes had cleared.

## Criticisms and Responses

**[[newton|Newton]]** argued that Descartes' identification of space with body cannot account for inertial effects. In Newton's bucket experiment, the water rises up the sides of the rotating bucket — but relative to what? Not relative to the bucket (it rotates with the water), and not relative to the surrounding bodies (they could be absent). Newton concluded that rotation must be relative to absolute space — a real entity independent of bodies. Descartes' plenist physics had no answer to this argument.

**[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** objected that if space *is* body, then the motion of a body is the motion of space. But if space moves, we need a further space in which to locate that motion — an infinite regress. Descartes' response is that motion is the translation of a body from the vicinity of contiguous bodies to the vicinity of others; "space" does not move because space is not a thing.

**[[kant|Kant]]** transcendentalized the Cartesian thesis: space is not a property of things in themselves but the *a priori* form of outer intuition. What Descartes took to be a metaphysical identity (space = extension = body) becomes, in Kant, a transcendental condition of experience. We do not discover that space is extension; rather, extension is the form under which we necessarily represent outer objects.
