## Overview

Doctor McDermott is Brightwood Hospital's eye-ear-nose-and-throat specialist, a member of the senior staff who participates in the Christmas Eve intervention to address Dr. Hudson's deteriorating health. Described as uneasy and perspiring under Pyle's interrogation, McDermott embodies the staff's collective anxiety — loyal to Hudson but frightened enough by his decline to support a confrontation none of them wants to initiate.

## Background

McDermott is identified by his specialty — "eye-ear-nose-and-throat McDermott" (001-001) — in the novel's catalogue of Brightwood's surgical staff. His meticulous personal habits are captured in a single vivid image: during the tense wait for Pyle, McDermott occupies himself by "meticulously pecking at diminutive bits of lint on his coat sleeve" (001-005), a nervous tic that speaks to his discomfort with the impending confrontation.

## Role in the Narrative

McDermott's primary narrative function is as a voice of the staff's unease. When Jennings and Carter first relay news of the meeting, McDermott responds with reluctance: "Oh, yes, I'll come, but I don't relish the idea of a staff meeting without the chief. Looks like treason" (001-003). This instinctive loyalty — the sense that meeting without Hudson is a betrayal — articulates the ethical discomfort that all the staff feel but few voice directly.

During the meeting, McDermott serves as a foil for Pyle's gruff authority. When Pyle challenges the staff to say whether any of them have ever dared to advise Hudson about his personal affairs, McDermott's silent, perspiring discomfort confirms the point: no one has (001-019). It is McDermott who cautiously raises the question of Hudson's psychological state — "Do you mean that — literally?" (001-021) — seeking clarity about the gravity of Hudson's condition. And when the question of who should confront Hudson arises, McDermott "again nominated Pyle" (001-031), the repetition of "again" suggesting his persistent anxiety to delegate the unpleasant task to someone — anyone — else.

Late in the novel's opening chapter, Hudson names McDermott among those Pyle should inform of his travel plans: "Report to Aldrich and Carter and the others that I'm off on a vacation" (001-053).

## Relationships

McDermott's most important professional relationship is with **[[malcolm-pyle|Malcolm Pyle]]**, whom he repeatedly nominates as the staff's emissary to Hudson — a role Pyle reluctantly accepts. With **[[doctor-carter|Doctor Carter]]**, McDermott shares the initial moment of receiving the summons to the staff meeting, and the two represent the staff's internal-medicine and surgical-specialty wings respectively. His relationships with **[[wayne-hudson|Wayne Hudson]]**, **[[doctor-jennings|Doctor Jennings]]**, and **[[nancy-ashford|Nancy Ashford]]** are defined by the shared institutional context of Brightwood and the collective concern for Hudson that drives the novel's opening crisis.

## Thematic Significance

McDermott represents the institutional conscience at its most anxious — the staff member who knows something is wrong, knows something must be done, but feels acutely uncomfortable about overstepping professional boundaries. His reluctance to meet without the chief ("Looks like treason") and his perspiring silence under Pyle's interrogation capture the hierarchical deference that makes institutional intervention so difficult. In a novel about the courage required to speak hard truths, McDermott embodies the ordinary reluctance that must be overcome for collective action to succeed.
