# Paris

## Overview

Paris is the city where [[maxine-merrick|Maxine Merrick]], [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]]'s mother, resides after the death of her husband Clifford. It represents the old world of European sophistication and emotional distance from which Bobby is disconnected throughout Lloyd C. Douglas's 1929 novel *Magnificent Obsession*. Paris appears in the narrative primarily as an absence — the place Bobby is not, the home of a mother who is not present.

## Symbolic Function

Paris functions as a symbol of detachment. Maxine's residence there after her husband's death signals her withdrawal from Bobby's life. "Shortly after Maxine had been assured that henceforth she would know exactly where Clif was, the big house on Piedmont Square was sold, and Bobby was taken to Europe where his mother rapidly improved" (002-055). The move is presented not as a new beginning but as an escape — one that leaves Bobby severed from his American roots.

The city also represents the gilded privilege that nearly ruins Bobby. As [[wayne-hudson|Dr. Hudson]] observes: "from my own observation of the type of young cub whose father is dead, whose mother lives in Paris, whose doting grandfather is a retired millionaire, and who gets into the papers with a story like this" (001-157) — the portrait of the idle rich, disconnected from meaningful work.

## In the Narrative

**Bobby's Atlantic crossing**: After the boating accident that sets the plot in motion, "Robert Merrick, the paper continued to explain, was the only son of the late Clifford Merrick and Mrs. Maxine Merrick, now resident in Paris" (001-153). His return from Europe marks the beginning of his moral transformation.

**The climax**: Paris reappears at the novel's climax. When Maxine learns of the accident, "Immediately upon reading the news of the accident, she had been fortunate in making connections with the best westbound train of the day" (021-040). The transatlantic journey, reversing the earlier departure, signals Maxine's return to maternal engagement.

**Bobby's pursuit**: In the final act, Bobby chases his destiny across Europe. "He engaged an aeroplane and flew to Paris. Between the racket of the triple engines and the furious pounding of his heart, it was a noisy trip" (022-018). The flight to Paris is his final crossing — not away from responsibility but toward it.

## Key Passages

> "from my own observation of the type of young cub whose father is dead, whose mother lives in Paris, whose doting grandfather is a retired millionaire" (001-157)

Dr. Hudson's wry taxonomy of privilege captures the novel's critique of inherited wealth and absent parenting.

> "Shortly after Maxine had been assured that henceforth she would know exactly where Clif was, the big house on Piedmont Square was sold, and Bobby was taken to Europe" (002-055)

The passive construction — "was sold," "was taken" — underscores Bobby's lack of agency in his early life.

## Related Places

Paris is paired in narrative opposition with [[detroit|Detroit]] — the European city of distant elegance versus the American city of professional purpose. It is also linked to [[piedmont-square|Piedmont Square]] (the Merrick family home, sold after Clifford's death) and to the European settings Bobby traverses in his final journey.
