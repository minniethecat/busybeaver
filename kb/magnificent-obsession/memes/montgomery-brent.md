## Overview

Montgomery Brent is the older cousin of Helen Brent, whom she affectionately calls "Brother Monty" and idealizes far beyond his actual merits. Five years Helen's senior, Monty left college after his sophomore year to play saxophone in dance orchestras before finding a position at a brokerage firm. Wayne Hudson describes him as "a bit of a rake" (001-092), though Helen — who habitually champions underdogs and misunderstood figures — sees him quite differently.

## Background

Montgomery Brent is the son of Helen's uncle, "an irascible, penurious, not very successful old lawyer" (001-092) who raised Helen after she was orphaned. Monty left college early, bringing "his saxophone to the jazz-market" (006-004), and spent several years performing nightly in dance orchestras — "bouncing and writhing and puffing himself purple every night from eight until two" (006-004). Eventually, through connections made among the young clerks at a brokerage office, he secured a desk job and a small salary, moving from the nightlife of jazz into the more conventional world of finance.

There are no women in the Brent household, which may partly explain Monty's undisciplined character and Helen's fierce loyalty to him despite his flaws.

## Role in the Narrative

Montgomery Brent appears primarily through others' descriptions rather than direct scenes. Wayne Hudson, explaining Helen's background to Malcolm Pyle, offers a frank assessment: Monty is "a bit of a rake, though she has idealized him out of all proportion; calls him 'Brother Monty,' thinks him vastly misunderstood by his father and everybody else" (001-092). Hudson's characterization of Helen as someone who "espouses the cause of homeless cats, underdogs, misunderstood cousins" (001-092) suggests that Monty represents one of Helen's lost causes — someone she loves in spite of, or perhaps because of, his failures.

After Hudson's death, Monty writes his "little sister" a brief, brotherly letter offering his counsel on business matters, now that her "responsibilities were increased" (006-001). The letter reflects a shift in their relationship — with Hudson gone and Helen newly responsible for the Hudson estate, Monty positions himself as a potential advisor, though the brevity of his letter and Hudson's earlier characterization suggest the offer may be less substantial than it appears.

Later in the novel, Montgomery becomes the recipient of Bobby Merrick's strange story about the sculptor Randolph (013-002), and at the end of that conversation, Brent asks Bobby if he may write to her (013-020), suggesting a personal interest that extends beyond brotherly concern.

## Relationships

Montgomery is the cousin of [[helen-brent]], who idealizes him despite his flaws. He is the son of Helen's uncle, the lawyer who raised her. He later meets [[bobby-merrick]] and expresses interest in corresponding with her. Hudson's characterization of Monty as "a bit of a rake" hints at a less-than-virtuous character that contrasts with Helen's idealized image of him.

## Thematic Significance

Montgomery Brent serves as an example of the gap between idealized perception and reality — a recurring theme in the novel. Helen's devotion to "Brother Monty" mirrors other characters' tendencies to see people not as they are but as they wish them to be. Monty's trajectory from college dropout to jazz musician to brokerage clerk also reflects the novel's interest in social mobility and the varied paths through which young men of the era sought their fortunes.
