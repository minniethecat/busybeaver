# Nicholas Merrick

## Overview

Nicholas Merrick is [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]]'s grandfather in Lloyd C. Douglas's *Magnificent Obsession*. A retired industrialist of formidable presence and old-fashioned values, "old Nick Merrick" represents the established order that Bobby's generation must either inherit and honor or squander. His relationship with Bobby evolves from distant disapproval to genuine respect as Bobby's transformation proves itself through action rather than words. The grandfather's role in the novel is that of witness — his growing belief in Bobby's change validates the philosophy for both the reader and Bobby himself.

## Background

Nicholas Merrick is introduced as a retired millionaire who lives at "[[windymere|Windymere]]," his estate near [[the-lake|the lake]] where the novel's opening crisis occurs. [[malcolm-pyle|Pyle]] describes Bobby as "old Nick Merrick's carousing grandson" (001-043), establishing both the grandfather's social standing and his grandson's reputation. Nicholas's own life — building a fortune through industry — represents the old model of masculine achievement: wealth creation through work. Bobby must demonstrate that his new model — wealth deployed through secret service — is equally valid.

## Role in the Narrative

Nicholas functions as Bobby's most important audience. When Bobby announces his intention to study medicine, his grandfather's reaction is characteristically gruff: "I cannot remember when any member of my tribe has made a decision of greater moment than yours!" (004-013). This moment of approval — hard-won and sincere — marks Bobby's first departure from the pattern of dissipation and disappointment.

Their most significant conversation occurs late in the novel, when Bobby explains the philosophy to his grandfather. Bobby's account of the "Major Personality" and the practice of secret philanthropy meets with Nicholas's cautious but genuine interest. The grandfather's question — "Bobby — are you a Christian?" (014-049) — crystallizes the novel's theological tension.

## Relationships

Nicholas's relationship with **[[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]]** forms the emotional core of his presence in the novel, evolving from disappointment to pride. His relationship with his daughter-in-law **[[maxine-merrick|Maxine Merrick]]**, Bobby's absent mother, is mentioned only in passing — she "lives in [[paris|Paris]]" (001-157), disconnected from both her son and her father-in-law. The grandfather-grandson bond thus carries the weight of the entire Merrick family legacy.

## Thematic Significance

Nicholas represents the old order that the new philosophy must engage with, not reject. His acceptance of Bobby's transformation validates the philosophy's claim that genuine change is self-evident — even a skeptical old industrialist can recognize it. His question about Christianity also marks the novel's engagement with institutional religion: the grandfather represents traditional faith, while Bobby represents experiential spirituality.

## Key Passages

> "I cannot remember when any member of my tribe has made a decision of greater moment than yours! May God — bless you!" (004-013)

Nicholas's emotional response to Bobby's decision to study medicine is the novel's first affirmation of Bobby's transformation.
