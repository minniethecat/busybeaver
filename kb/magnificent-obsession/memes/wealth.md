# Wealth

**Wealth** appears in *Magnificent Obsession* as both a social fact and a moral problem.
The novel treats material affluence not as a neutral condition but as a charged circumstance
that demands something of its possessor.

## Early Characterization

Wealth is first introduced in a dismissive, even satirical register.
A character is described in terms that mock the equation of wealth with authority:
it is said that from hearing his self-important talk, one "would have sworn... that he was
a wealthy and neurotic old lady trying to run the world" (001-027).
This framing establishes wealth as potentially ridiculous — a source of unearned
self-importance rather than genuine standing.

## Bobby Merrick's Wealth

The novel's protagonist, [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]], is himself a figure of significant
wealth. At medical school, it is known "that there was a wealthy student in the class" (011-050) —
a fact that colors others' perceptions of him.
Crucially, another character "had known nothing of Bobby's wealth before he had interested himself
in her" (011-084), indicating that Bobby's wealth is not immediately visible in his person
and that authentic relationships in the novel are those formed without knowledge of financial status.

## Thematic Role

Wealth in *Magnificent Obsession* functions as a test of character.
The novel distinguishes between wealth as a source of vanity (the neurotic old lady)
and wealth as a resource whose proper use is demanded by a moral imperative —
a theme that connects directly to Doctor Hudson's philosophy of obligation and the
novel's broader argument about the responsibilities of privilege.
