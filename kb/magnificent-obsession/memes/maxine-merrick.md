# Maxine Merrick

## Overview

Maxine Merrick is [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]]'s mother — a vain, self-absorbed expatriate living in [[paris|Paris]] whose neglect of her son contributes to his early dissolution. She appears only briefly in Lloyd C. Douglas's *Magnificent Obsession*, but her character serves as a foil to the novel's ideal of selfless [[service]].

## Background

Maxine Merrick's defining trait is her relentless self-absorption. "Petulant, selfish, suspicious — Maxine Merrick was no end difficult". Her only proficiency was her skill as a pianist, "and aware that it was the sole endowment she was in a position to transmit, she had begun to teach the child piano technique almost before he knew the letters on his building-blocks".

Her life in Paris revolves around a cycle of vanity and hypochondria: "She vibrated between institutions for the care of the body and the cure of the soul. Having spent a busy season of assiduous devotion to the business of being plucked, picked, dyed, frescoed, and massaged...Maxine would suddenly experience an unaccountable surge of disgust, and make off hurriedly to some sanitarium de luxe". She filled her apartment "with wigged and bangled old harridans, who swapped dull prattle about their aristocratic relatives for caviar and champagne".

## Role in the Narrative

After her husband Clifford Merrick's death, Maxine moved to Paris and placed young Bobby in "a school for rich waifs at Versailles, where he fraternized with youngsters who had become an embarrassing liability to divorced parents". On brief vacations, Bobby would visit her in Paris, where "she chattered baby talk to him, to which he made sour replies in a voice that frequently skidded off the treble clef".

Maxine reappears toward the novel's end, when she learns from a magazine that her son has become a famous surgeon. "Maxine Merrick, pouring their coffee in the sunny breakfast room of her over-Louised apartment in Boulevard Haussmann near the Etoile, glanced up shyly to note the effect" as she boasts about her son's achievements to her friends. When Bobby visits her in Paris, their encounter is brief and strained — he flees "frantically" after a few minutes of her company.

## Relationships

- **Bobby Merrick**: Her son — and the primary victim of her neglect. Maxine represents everything the novel's philosophy stands against: self-absorption, vanity, and the failure to invest in others.
- **Clifford Merrick**: Her late husband. Their marriage was characterized by mutual contempt that Bobby witnessed from childhood.
- **Nicholas Merrick**: Her father-in-law, who took over Bobby's upbringing after Clifford's death and Maxine's departure to Paris.

## Thematic Significance

Maxine Merrick serves as a negative exemplum — the antithesis of the "Magnificent Obsession" philosophy. Where the novel champions secret generosity and personality expansion through service to others, Maxine embodies its opposite: a life spent on self-beautification and social performance that yields only emptiness. Her character demonstrates what Bobby might have become had he not been transformed by Dr. Hudson's philosophy.
