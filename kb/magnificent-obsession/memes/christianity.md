# Christianity

## Overview

Christianity in Lloyd C. Douglas's *Magnificent Obsession* undergoes a radical reinterpretation. The novel does not reject Christianity but rescues it from what it sees as institutional decay — the reduction of a practical "science" of personality expansion to ritual, music, and moral platitudes. Through Dr. McLaren, the progressive clergyman, and Bobby Merrick's own reading of the Gospels, the novel argues for a Christianity stripped of supernatural claims and focused entirely on the example of Christ as a man who achieved perfect alignment with the divine source of power.

## Context

Douglas was himself an ordained Lutheran minister who left the pulpit to write fiction. The novel's treatment of Christianity reflects this biography — it is the work of an insider critiquing the institution from within. Published in 1929, the novel engaged with the modernist-fundamentalist controversy then dividing American Protestantism, offering a "third way" that retained Christian identity while abandoning doctrinal literalism.

## In the Novel

Christianity enters the narrative primarily through two channels. First, Bobby's direct engagement with the Gospels, which he reads as "the actual textbook of a science relating to the expansion and development of the human personality" (011-094). His shock at discovering that this manual had been "set to music" and ritualized is the novel's most pointed critique of institutional religion.

Second, Dr. McLaren provides the voice of progressive Christianity attempting to remain relevant. His church preaches a rational, ethical faith focused on "a Christ who is not a mere prestidigitator — a magician who feeds an acre of people from a boy's lunch-basket — but a great prophet and an understanding friend" (018-061). Yet McLaren himself recognizes the limitation of his approach when confronted with Bobby's experiential spirituality: ethics can inspire, but it cannot deliver the transformative power that Bobby describes.

The novel's pivotal theological question comes from Bobby's grandfather: "Bobby — are you a Christian?" (014-049). Bobby cannot answer simply, because the term carries institutional baggage that his experiential practice has transcended. His faith is in the method, not the label.

## Key Passages

> "Bobby — are you a Christian?" (014-049)

The question that crystallizes the novel's theological tension — Bobby's practice is Christian in source but not in institutional form.

> "In our church, we're trying to offer a Christ who is not a mere prestidigitator — a magician who feeds an acre of people from a boy's lunch-basket — but a great prophet and an understanding friend!" (018-061)

Dr. McLaren articulates progressive Christianity's project — retaining Christ while jettisoning miracle claims — which the novel both sympathizes with and finds insufficient.

## Legacy / Significance

The novel's reinterpretation of Christianity as a practical "science" of personality expansion was enormously influential, offering readers a way to retain spiritual identity while embracing modern rationalism. Douglas's approach — separating the historical Jesus from the institutional church, and treating his teachings as a technology rather than a theology — anticipated later developments in American spirituality, from the "power of positive thinking" to the contemporary "spiritual but not religious" movement. The novel remains a fascinating document of early twentieth-century efforts to reconcile Christianity with modernity.
