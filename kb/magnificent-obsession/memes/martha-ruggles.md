## Overview

Martha Ruggles is the housekeeper at Flintridge, Wayne Hudson's remote country retreat, where she lives and works alongside her twin brother Perry. Described as "buxom" and possessing a deep, unfulfilled need for affection, Martha channels her love into domestic acts — quilting, baking pies, and tending to the cottage with devoted anticipation of Hudson's visits. Her quiet, yearning devotion to the doctor and her unease around his beautiful young wife make her one of the novel's most poignantly drawn minor characters.

## Background

Martha Ruggles and her twin brother Perry serve as the caretakers of Flintridge. While Perry handles the outdoor work, Martha manages the household with meticulous care. Her life is defined by routine: she makes quilts, conceals the damage caused by a pet fawn, dotes on a pair of tame pheasants, and gathers wild berries for pies (001-110). Her world is small — bounded by the cottage and the ridge road — and her emotional life revolves almost entirely around the anticipation of Hudson's visits.

The narrator reveals a childhood memory that shapes Martha's adult sensibility: at the age of nine, on a pre-Christmas shopping trip, she saw a French doll "so beautiful it had made Martha's little throat ache with longing" (001-112). She reached out tentatively but could not have it. This early experience of unattainable beauty echoes throughout her adult life, where she yearns for connection while knowing her place at the margins.

## Role in the Narrative

Martha's role is revealed through her rituals of anticipation. On Saturdays, she prepares Hudson's room — laying out his pyjamas, adjusting the roses on his chiffonier — and then takes her position at the sun-parlour window, "her knuckles pressed hard against her pretty teeth, devoutly praying for a swirl of yellow dust and a flash of glittering nickel at the bend of the ridge road" (001-111). She always hopes, and hates herself for hoping, that Hudson will come alone.

Young Mrs. Hudson's beauty stirs complicated emotions in Martha — she is "uneasy, abashed, and awkward" in Joyce Hudson's presence (001-112), feeling the same ache of longing and exclusion she felt as a child before the unattainable doll. This parallel suggests Martha's unspoken, perhaps unconscious, romantic attachment to Hudson.

After Hudson's death, Martha is "dreadfully broken up" (003-077). Helen Brent, now Hudson's widow, travels to the country to visit Martha, acknowledging the depth of the housekeeper's grief and the importance of her connection to Hudson.

## Relationships

Martha is the devoted twin sister of [[perry-ruggles]], though they keep secrets from one another — Perry never tells Martha about the pier incident, and Martha conceals the fawn's mischief from Perry. Her devotion to [[wayne-hudson]] is the defining feature of her life; she lives for his visits and is shattered by his death. She feels intimidated by [[joyce-hudson]]'s beauty and social grace. [[helen-brent]] reaches out to Martha after Hudson's death, recognizing the depth of her grief.

## Thematic Significance

Martha represents the theme of invisible devotion — the love and care given by those whose service goes largely unnoticed. Her parallel with the childhood doll crystallizes the novel's meditation on longing, beauty, and the pain of desiring what one cannot possess. Martha's grief after Hudson's death validates the importance of her role: she is not merely a servant but someone whose life was meaningfully shaped by her devotion to the doctor. The fact that Helen Brent, Hudson's widow, makes a special journey to comfort Martha affirms the dignity of her love and loss.
