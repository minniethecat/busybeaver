# Herman Bliss

**Doctor Herman Bliss** is a physician in Seattle who appears briefly in the novel's opening chapters,
reacting to news of the boating accident on the lake.

## Role in the Story

Doctor Herman Bliss read the "shocking headlines" about the accident from his home in Seattle (001-135).
He and his wife discussed the circumstances of the tragedy, including the question of why a young man
who reportedly could not swim would risk himself on the water.

Mrs. Bliss conjectured about the psychology of the accident, wondering whether fear itself might have
been a factor: "Do you suppose... that there could have lurked..." (001-138).
She pressed her questions further, asking why someone would go out swimming "if he was afraid of it" (001-142),
and applying her own logic to the situation (001-144).

Doctor Bliss himself responded with professional indignation to the suggestion that the tragedy
was simply a matter of chance: "Things like that don't often..." (001-148).

## Function in the Narrative

The Bliss household serves as a kind of Greek chorus in the novel's opening — an ordinary, distant
couple who read about the tragedy in the newspaper and discuss it with the mix of curiosity,
speculation, and moral judgment that characterizes public reactions to private catastrophe.
Their conversation establishes the accident as news that traveled beyond the immediate community,
and their questions anticipate themes the novel will explore at greater depth.
