## Overview

Perry Ruggles is the devoted caretaker of Flintridge, Doctor Wayne Hudson's isolated lakeside retreat. A middle-aged man with a stiff knee, hairy throat, and what the narrator calls an "Airedale disposition" (001-109), Perry lives at Flintridge with his twin sister Martha. He becomes a tragic figure when he attempts unsuccessfully to save Hudson from drowning — an event that leaves him weeping hysterically over Hudson's lifeless body.

## Background

Perry Ruggles and his twin sister Martha serve as the caretakers of Flintridge, Hudson's remote cottage. Perry is described as a man of few words — "the taciturn Perry" (001-110) — who occupies himself with maintaining the boat engine, fishing for bass, and tending the gardens. His physical limitations include a stiff knee, which gives him a distinctive limp, though he compensates with a determined work ethic and fierce loyalty to his employer.

On Saturdays, Perry would put on his better coat, limp down to the gate, and prepare the driveway for Hudson's arrival — flicking small stones off the path with his good foot (001-109). This ritual reflects his deep devotion to Hudson and his pride in maintaining Flintridge to the highest standard.

## Role in the Narrative

Perry Ruggles plays a crucial role in the backstory of Hudson's death. He is burdened with a terrible secret: there had once been "a very anxious hour at Flintridge, down on the narrow pier" (001-131), an incident that left Hudson needing to bring an inhalator to the cottage. Hudson taught Perry how to use the device, but the responsibility terrified Perry — it became "a grim spectre that haunted his life" (001-131), keeping him awake at night with the dread that someday he would need to use it.

Perry's worst fear is realized when Hudson drowns in the lake. Perry rows out to him, dives to retrieve his body, and rows desperately toward shore until his strength gives out. When rescuers arrive, they find him "huddled over the lifeless body of Doctor Hudson, weeping hysterically" (001-151). The tragedy is compounded by the knowledge that Hudson's inhalator might have saved him had it been immediately available.

Perry also shares a moment of unusual candour with Hudson, having once called him "a fool to his face" (001-131) — a rare instance of anyone speaking bluntly to the great surgeon. Hudson accepted the rebuke with dignity.

## Relationships

Perry is deeply devoted to [[wayne-hudson]], for whom he serves as caretaker at Flintridge. His relationship with his twin sister [[martha-ruggles]] is close but marked by secrecy — Perry keeps the pier incident hidden even from her. His connection to Hudson is one of the novel's most poignant examples of loyal service, described as "such doglike devotion as old Perry's" (001-152).

## Thematic Significance

Perry Ruggles embodies the theme of loyal devotion and the burden of responsibility. His terror of the inhalator — and the tragic irony that he could not reach it in time to save Hudson — speaks to the novel's broader meditation on duty, preparedness, and the gap between intention and outcome. Perry's helpless grief after Hudson's death underscores the depth of the bond between the surgeon and those who served him, suggesting that Hudson's greatness inspired extraordinary loyalty in people of all stations.
