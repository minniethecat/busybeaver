## Overview

Doctor Aldrich is a staff physician at the hospital where Wayne Hudson serves as chief of surgery. He appears in the opening chapter among the group of doctors who gather to discuss their concerns about Hudson's failing health. Aldrich is portrayed as a serious, pragmatic member of the staff — he rattles the pages of his engagement book while waiting and, when the discussion turns facetious, reminds his colleagues that "it was no time for kidding" (001-030).

## Background

Aldrich is introduced in the novel's opening scene, where he waits impatiently for Malcolm Pyle to arrive at a staff meeting. He is one of several doctors — alongside Carter, McDermott, Watson, Gram, and others — who have grown alarmed by Hudson's deteriorating condition. Aldrich's nervous habit of rattling his engagement book pages (001-002) suggests a man who values order and schedules, perhaps reflecting a methodical temperament befitting a physician.

## Role in the Narrative

Aldrich participates in the Chapter I staff meeting where Hudson's colleagues debate how to address their chief's health crisis. When Carter suggests deputizing Jennings — known for his impertinence — to confront Hudson, Aldrich firmly rejects the levity, stating "it was no time for kidding" (001-030). This serious-minded intervention helps steer the conversation back toward action, culminating in Pyle's reluctant agreement to speak with Hudson directly.

Later, when Hudson decides to leave for Europe with his daughter, he instructs that the news be relayed to Aldrich and Carter specifically: "Report to Aldrich and Carter and the others that I'm off on a vacation" (001-095). Hudson's singling out of Aldrich by name suggests that Aldrich holds a position of some seniority or trust within the hospital hierarchy, making him one of the key figures to be informed of the chief's plans.

## Relationships

Aldrich works alongside [[doctor-carter]], [[doctor-mcdermott]], [[doctor-watson]], [[doctor-gram]], and [[doctor-jennings]] as a colleague of [[malcolm-pyle]], who is designated to confront Hudson. His professional relationship with Wayne Hudson is inferred from Hudson's instruction to inform Aldrich personally of his departure. The mention of Aldrich alongside Carter in Hudson's directive (001-095) suggests they may hold comparable standing among the staff.

## Thematic Significance

Aldrich represents the voice of professional seriousness within the collective anxiety of Hudson's staff. While other doctors crack nervous jokes or fidget impatiently, Aldrich insists on treating the situation with the gravity it deserves. His inclusion among those Hudson specifically names when announcing his departure hints at the web of professional relationships and loyalties that surround the central figure of the novel — men who care about their chief but are ultimately powerless to prevent the tragedy that unfolds.
