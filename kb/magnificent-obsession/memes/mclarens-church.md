# Dr. McLaren's Progressive Church

Dr. [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s progressive church — referred to in the novel as **Grace Church** — is the congregation led by [[doctor-mclaren|the Rev. Bruce McLaren, Ph.D.]], a young, scholarly preacher who represents [[christianity|institutional religion]]'s most earnest attempt to remain relevant in a scientific age. The church occupies a pivotal position in Lloyd C. Douglas's *Magnificent Obsession* (1929), serving as the setting for the novel's most sustained theological debate between modernist [[christianity|Christianity]] and the experiential spirituality practiced by [[bobby-merrick|Dr. Bobby Merrick]]. Congregants include [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s wife [[betty-sealback|Betty McLaren]], the prominent [[the-social-congress|Social Congress]] patroness [[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]], and an unusually youthful congregation — fully half under forty — that the [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]s cite as proof that "the intellectual approach to [[faith|religion]]" can attract a generation otherwise indifferent to [[christianity|organized faith]].

## Overview

Grace Church is characterized in the novel as a self-consciously modern institution, one that prides itself on shedding the "old stock phrases of theology" (018-058) in favor of a vocabulary compatible with science and rational inquiry. It is, as [[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]] famously declared, "the most forward-looking — indeed the only forward-looking church in town" (018-056). Yet the novel subjects this claim to sustained scrutiny. Over the course of a single Sunday in Chapter XVIII, the church hosts a scholarly sermon, a post-service social hour, and a private luncheon conversation that together expose both the genuine value and the inherent limitation of [[christianity|institutional religion]] in the novel's spiritual economy. The church's pastor himself voices the culminating admission: "We've been trying to teach religion without — without knowing what it's about!" (018-077).

## Leadership

The church is led by the Rev. Bruce [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]], Ph.D., whose credentials and character set him apart from the caricatured clergyman of popular imagination. Originally from [[brightwood-hospital-institution|Brightwood]] — where he later recovers from a near-fatal brain injury treated by Dr. Merrick at [[brightwood-hospital-institution|Brightwood Hospital]] — [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] is described as "by no means an intellectual coxcomb or a solemn blatherskite with a fondness for big words and an itch to achieve the reputation of a savant" (018-060). His scholarship is genuine, his personality warm, and his congregation genuinely respects him.

[[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s approach to [[service|ministry]] is defined by three commitments:

- **Intellectual accessibility**: He deliberately avoids "old stock phrases of theology" (018-058), believing that "we'll have to evolve a new vocabulary for religion, to make it rank with other subjects of interest" (018-061). His sermons treat the congregation as intellectual peers — so much so that he must self-censor, making "last-minute substitutions for certain erudite terms he feared might overshoot his congregation" (018-058).
- **Youth engagement**: The [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]s measure their success by demographics, noting with pride that "the students and the young business and professional people have outgrown the old traditions and are eager for an intellectual approach to religion" (018-058).
- **Constructive modernism**: [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] is not merely a critic of tradition; he is trying to build something new. His Christ is "not a mere prestidigitator — a magician who feeds an acre of people from a boy's lunch-basket — but a great prophet and an understanding friend" (018-061).

Despite these virtues, [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] himself is the first to recognize the limits of his program when confronted by [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]]'s account of [[wayne-hudson|Dr. Hudson]]'s experiential spirituality. "How utterly trivial," [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] says afterward, "my whole programme of preaching seems in the face of such astounding possibilities!" (018-077).

## Role in Narrative

Grace Church appears principally in Chapter XVIII, but its significance radiates through the novel's entire theological architecture.

**The Sunday service (XVIII)**. [[bobby-merrick|Bobby Merrick]], having recovered from the press frenzy surrounding his surgical invention, cautiously re-enters public life by attending church — his first such attendance in the novel. The sermon is crafted with [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by in mind: "What Dr. [[bobby-merrick|Robert Merrick]] was going to think of that sermon had loomed so large in the popular young preacher's mind" (018-058) that [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] had to restrain his erudition. The congregation's pride in their pastor is genuine but compromised — they are "at once flattered and befuddled by its charming inexplicability" (018-058), suggesting that the intellectual approach may achieve less real comprehension than it appears to.

**The luncheon debate (XVIII)**. After the service, [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by joins the [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]s for luncheon at their apartment adjacent to the church. What begins as polite small talk about the sermon escalates into a profound theological conversation in which [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by challenges [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s modernist framework point by point: [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] proposes that God is "an hypothesis" (018-069); [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by responds that he has "proofs" (018-071). [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] argues that the old terminology is "obsolete" and "misleading" (018-061); [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by observes that "electricity" still means "amber" — the name was never changed because people were "too busy trying to make it work" (018-062). The conversation pivots when [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] delivers his impassioned critique of supernatural Christology and [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by counters with the suggestion that religion should be presented not as ethics but as dynamics — "power, energy, dynamics — the kind you read about, not in a book, but on a meter!" (018-063).

**The resolution (XVIII)**. By the luncheon's end, [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] has undergone a kind of conversion — not to [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's specific [[faith|belief]]s, but to the recognition that his own program is incomplete. His parting question to [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by crystallizes the church's new agenda: "We modernists have been trying to show how religion is not at odds with science. What we've got to do now is to show how religion *is a science*!" (018-079). [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's reply — "Exactly! Nothing less or else than that! You have it!" — is both an endorsement and a commission.

**The pre-history (XV)**. Although Grace Church itself does not appear until Chapter XVIII, [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s churchmanship is discussed earlier. In Chapter XV, [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] tells [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by that "this oncoming generation … is not in the least way concerned about organized religion. Religious enough, instinctively, I dare say; but out of sorts with the sects; weary of their bad-mannered yammering at one another over matters in which one man's guess is as good as another's, and no outcome promised either in faith or conduct, no matter whose guess is right!" (015-043). This critique establishes the church's purpose — to be the exception to an institution in decline.

## Theological Position

The church's theological stance is the novel's case [[education|study]] in the strengths and weaknesses of liberal Protestant modernism circa 1929. Its positions can be analyzed across several axes:

### Positions held by Grace Church (McLaren's modernism)

| Issue | Grace Church Position | Novel's Assessment |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Scripture** | A valuable text but not inerrant; miracles can be de-emphasized | Insufficient — the Bible is a "textbook of a science" (011-094) |
| **Christology** | A human teacher and prophet, not a miracle-worker | Insufficient — Christ demonstrated access to power that is available to all |
| **Language** | Must be modernized to stay relevant | Partially correct, but secondary to actually *demonstrating* the power |
| **God** | An "hypothesis" — [[faith|belief]] held tentatively | Contradicted by [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's claim of experiential "proofs" (018-071) |
| **Evolution** | Embraced as explaining "everything we have and are" (018-078) | Challenged: biology cannot explain personality, [[hope|aspiration]], or penitence |
| **Ethics** | The church's primary product — inspiring self-examination | Necessary but insufficient without access to transforming power |
| **Young people** | The metric of success; retention of youth validates the approach | A demographic metric, not a spiritual one |

The fundamental limitation of Grace Church's theology, from the novel's perspective, is that it operates entirely within the domain of ethics — what [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by calls "inspiring people to take stock of themselves" (018-077) — while the philosophy Hudson discovered belongs to "the field of science" (018-077). Ethics can refine conduct; the philosophy can transform personality. The two are complementary but not interchangeable.

### The "amber" argument

[[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's most telling critique of [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s project is his analogy of electricity:

> "It just occurs to me — casting about at random for a parallel case — that the word 'electricity' means 'amber.' All that the ancients knew about electricity was that a chunk of amber, when rubbed with silk, would pick up a feather. Now that it has been developed until it will pick up a locomotive, electricity still means amber. They never went to the bother to change the name of it. Maybe they thought there was at least a pleasant sentiment in retaining the name. More likely, they never thought about it, at all. Too busy trying to make it work, I suppose." (018-062)

The implication is clear: [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s preoccupation with modernizing the vocabulary of faith is a symptom of not knowing how to *make it work*. Those who can access the power are too busy using it to worry about what it is called.

## The Congregation

Grace Church's congregation embodies the contradictions of progressive religion in the novel:

**[[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]] and the Social Congress elite**. [[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]] is the church's most prominent lay advocate, the woman who declared it "the only forward-looking church in town" (018-056) while recommending [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] to invoke divine blessing at the Social Congress. Yet her advocacy collapses under questioning from Mrs. Cordelia Kunz of Grand Rapids, who asks pointedly: "Forward-looking on economic questions, social problems, political issues — or merely posing as the last outpost of orthodoxy?" (018-056). [[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]]'s inability to answer — she "snapped her purse several times, quite noisily" (018-056) — suggests that the church's progressive reputation may owe more to social positioning than theological substance.

**Deacon Chester**. The prosperous cookie-baker who declares [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s sermon "the most profound … ever delivered in Grace Church" (018-060) represents the well-meaning but intellectually unqualified laity. The narrator's observation — that Chester's use of the word "guess" was "meticulously adequate" because "had a photographic plate been exposed to Mr. Chester's knowledge of the subject … it could have been used again, quite unimpaired, for other purposes" (018-060) — gently satirizes the gap between the sermon's erudition and its actual comprehension.

**The youth demographic**. The [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]s take particular pride in their young congregation — "fully half of whom were under forty" (018-057). [[betty-sealback|Betty]] [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] asks [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by, "wasn't he surprised to see so many young people in the congregation?" (018-058). [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's dry reply — "was it anything to be surprised at that young people went to church?" (018-058) — undercuts the pride without denying the achievement. The youth are present, but the novel leaves open the question of whether they are being fed or merely entertained by intellectual novelty.

## Significance

Grace Church functions in *Magnificent Obsession* as more than a setting — it is the institutional embodiment of the novel's central theological argument. Douglas, himself a former Lutheran minister, uses the church to dramatize both the nobility and the inadequacy of the modernist project:

**A genuine achievement**. The novel does not mock [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] or his church. [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] is portrayed as intelligent, sincere, and [[influence|effect]]ive within his frame of reference. His congregation is "flattered" by his scholarship, and "they had every right to be proud" (018-060). The intellectual approach is a real advance over the alternatives — the "dull, monotonous chant" of the preacher Hudson heard, who read scripture without comprehension (015-037), or the "bad-mannered yammering" of sectarian controversy (015-043).

**An inherent limit**. The advance, however, is from worst to better — not from better to best. The church offers a refined ethics, an intellectualized faith, a rational Christ. What it does not offer — what it *cannot* offer within its theological framework — is access to transforming power. [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's account of Hudson's experience reveals that the progressive church has been "trying to teach religion without knowing what it's about" (018-077) — because religion, properly understood, is not a subject to be taught but a force to be channeled.

**A redeemed future**. Significantly, the novel does not leave [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] in despair. [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's final en[[fear|courage]]ment is genuine: [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s work clearing away "the old superstitions; the old irrelevancies" is "not labour lost" (018-078). The task ahead — showing how religion *is a science* — is the logical next step, and [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] accepts it. The parting exchange at the curb, with [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] gripping [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's hand and resolving on a new direction, suggests that the progressive church may yet evolve into something more powerful than a lecture hall for ethical uplift.

## Key Passages

> "Really, the most forward-looking — indeed the only forward-looking church in town!" (018-056)

[[betty-sealback|Mrs. Sealback]]'s claim for Grace Church — a boast that proves more fragile than its confidence suggests.

> "Obsolete! Misleading! We'll have to evolve a new vocabulary for religion, to make it rank with other subjects of interest. We've got to phrase it in modern terms; don't you think so?" (018-061)

[[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]] articulates the modernist program in a single sentence — and invites [[bobby-merrick|Bob]]by's quiet dissent.

> "In our church, we're trying to offer a Christ who is not a mere prestidigitator — a magician who feeds an acre of people from a boy's lunch-basket — but a great prophet and an understanding friend!" (018-061)

The positive content of [[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s progressive Christology — sympathetic but, the novel suggests, insufficient.

> "How utterly trivial my whole programme of preaching seems in the face of such astounding possibilities! Why — we've been trying to teach religion without — without knowing what it's about!" (018-077)

[[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s moment of honest reckoning — the novel's verdict on [[christianity|institutional religion]] delivered by its own most attractive representative.

> "We modernists have been trying to show how religion is not at odds with science. What we've got to do now is to show how religion *is a science*!" (018-079)

[[doctor-mclaren|McLaren]]'s parting resolution on the curb outside the church — the prospect of a transformed mission.
