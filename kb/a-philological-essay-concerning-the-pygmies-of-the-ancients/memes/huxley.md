Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895) was a British biologist and anatomist, known as "Darwin's Bulldog" for his vigorous public defence of evolutionary theory. In Tyson's essay, Huxley's authority enters through a single but significant osteological reference: his report on the Keiss skeletons from Caithness, Scotland, which documented a population of notably short-statured females inhabiting the northern British Isles in antiquity.

## The Keiss Skeletons and Stature Evidence

In Section 8 of the essay, Tyson marshals archaeological and osteological evidence for the physical characteristics of the ancient inhabitants of the British Isles. He cites Huxley's examination of the Keiss skeletons to illuminate the stature profile of a northern population:

> Speaking of the Keiss skeletons, Professor Huxley says that the males are, the one somewhat above, and the other probably about the average stature; while the females are short, none exceeding five feet two inches or three inches in height. (008-110)

The finding that the female skeletons from Keiss did not exceed five feet two or three inches — approximately 157–160 cm — placed them firmly below modern British female averages. Huxley's observation carried particular weight given his reputation as the pre-eminent British anatomist of the Victorian era.

## Interpretive Context in Tyson's Essay

Tyson invokes Huxley's Keiss data as part of a broader argument about short-statured populations in ancient Britain. The passage in which Huxley appears is embedded in a discussion of skeletal remains from the Shetland and Orkney islands, with Carter Blake's Muckle Heog excavation and Dr. Garson's Orkney osteology providing complementary evidence of populations with reduced stature in the northern extremities of Scotland.

This collective evidence served Tyson's larger thesis that traditions of diminutive beings — fairies, pygmies, and dwarfs — might be rooted in the historical presence of physically small populations in various regions of the ancient and medieval world. Huxley's Keiss analysis, however, was deployed cautiously: the Keiss males were of average or above-average stature, and only the females were notably short. This nuanced presentation reflects the care with which Tyson handled his osteological sources.

## Scientific Career and Authority

Huxley's towering scientific reputation — as President of the Royal Society (1883–1885), President of the Geological Society, and the leading comparative anatomist of his generation — made his Keiss report a weighty citation for Tyson. Huxley had pioneered the application of rigorous anatomical method to questions of human variation and evolution. His *Evidence as to Man's Place in Nature* (1863) had established the anatomical continuity between humans and the great apes, and his contributions to vertebrate palaeontology and ethnology were internationally recognised.

By the time Tyson wrote in the 1890s, Huxley's name on any osteological question carried near-definitive authority. The citation of Huxley alongside Carter Blake, Garson, and Sir William Turner in Section 8 created a constellation of the most respected anatomical voices of the period, all testifying to the reality of short-statured populations in ancient Britain.
