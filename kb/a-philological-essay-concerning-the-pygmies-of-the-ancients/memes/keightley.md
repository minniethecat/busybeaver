Thomas Keightley (1789–1872) was an Irish writer, historian, and folklorist, best known for his pioneering work *The Fairy Mythology* (1828), which compiled and classified fairy traditions from across Europe and beyond. His work was among the first systematic treatments of comparative folklore in English.

Tyson cites Keightley more than any other modern (post-classical) authority on folklore in his essay, drawing on Keightley's collections of fairy traditions to support his argument that the "little folk" of European legend preserve dim memories of ancient pygmy races.

## Tyson's Use of Keightley

Tyson invokes Keightley in Section 5 as a methodological model. Having discussed the fairy traditions of England, Tyson notes that "after the example of Keightley," he will "treat the little folk of each country separately" (005-087). This organizational approach — surveying pygmy-like traditions nation by nation — structures much of Tyson's argument in Sections 5 through 9.

Tyson describes Keightley's work as a rich repository of comparative material:

> "The pages of Keightley's work contain instances of hill-inhabiting fairies in Scandinavia, Denmark, the Isle of Rugen, Iceland, Germany, and Switzerland."
> (005-121)

This geographical breadth serves Tyson's thesis that diminutive supernatural beings — whom he identifies with the Pygmies of the ancients — are a near-universal feature of human folklore.

## Keightley as Source for Specific Traditions

Throughout Sections 5–9, Tyson draws on specific tales and citations from Keightley's collection:

- A Danish account of a hill-dwelling dwarf who invites a peasant woman into his underground home, quoted "from Thiele, i. 118" and cited via Keightley (p. 9) (006-043).
- Stories collected by Keightley that were "made use of in Cruikshank's picture, which forms the frontispiece to that volume" (006-035).
- Multiple citations of Grimm's *Teutonic Mythology* relayed through Keightley's work, including Grimm's accounts of underground dwellings (008-032), dwarf beliefs (009-021), and the widespread motif of subterranean little people (007-145).

Tyson's reliance on Keightley as a secondary source for Grimm's material illustrates the interconnected nature of 19th-century folklore scholarship.

## Significance in Tyson's Argument

Keightley's *Fairy Mythology* functions in Tyson's essay as both a methodological model and an evidentiary source. By citing Keightley, Tyson connects his own philological investigation of ancient pygmy accounts to the emerging discipline of comparative folklore studies. The fairy traditions that Keightley documented — underground dwellings, diminutive stature, magical crafts, ambivalent relations with humans — provide Tyson with modern analogues that he argues corroborate the historical reality behind ancient pygmy legends.

## Key Work Cited by Tyson

- **_The Fairy Mythology_ (1828)** — Tyson cites specific page numbers (pp. 9, 62, 70, 130, 178, 230, 440, 441, 488), indicating direct use of Keightley's compilation as a source for European fairy traditions and their parallels to pygmy lore.
