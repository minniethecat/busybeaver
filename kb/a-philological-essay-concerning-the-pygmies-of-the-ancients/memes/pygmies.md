# Pygmies

## Overview

The Pygmies (Ancient Greek: Πυγμαῖοι, Latin: _Pygmaei_) are a legendary race of diminutive people who appear throughout classical Greek and Roman literature. They are the central subject of Edward Tyson's 1699 essay _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_. (001-026)

Tyson's central argument was that the ancient accounts of Pygmies were not pure fabrications. "The Pygmy described in this work was, as a matter of fact, a chimpanzee." (001-043) He proposed that [[homer|Homer]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], and [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]] had described real creatures — great apes — through the lens of their own cultural frameworks.

This argument placed Tyson at the intersection of [[philology]], [[natural-history|natural history]], and [[comparative-anatomy|comparative anatomy]]. His essay marks the transition from classical textual authority to empirical natural science.

## Ancient Sources

The pygmy legend appears in some of the earliest extant Greek literature and persists through late Roman sources. Tyson surveyed this tradition systematically, from Homer to the Church Fathers.

### Homer and the Geranomachia

The earliest and most influential pygmy account appears in [[homer|Homer]]'s _Iliad_, where the poet describes Pygmies engaged in an annual battle with migrating cranes — the [[geranomachia|geranomachia]] (crane-war).

> "So when inclement winters vex the plain
> With piercing frosts, or thick-descending rain,
> To warmer seas the cranes embodied fly,
> With noise and order, through the midway sky;
> To Pigmy nations wounds and death they bring,
> And all the war descends upon the wing."
> (005-001)

Tyson notes that Homer is "generally owned as the first who made mention of the Pygmies" (010-086). This Homeric simile became the template for nearly every subsequent classical reference.

### Aristotle and Natural History

[[aristotle|Aristotle]] treated the Pygmies not as myth but as a subject for natural-historical inquiry. In his _Historia Animalium_, he discussed the crane-migration and noted that the Pygmies dwelled in caves. (011-010) Tyson found this detail significant, as cave-dwelling matched the behavior of apes.

### Herodotus and African Exploration

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] provided an account independent of the Homeric tradition. He recorded a report of Nasamonian travelers who encountered small-statured people in the African interior. Tyson connected this to accounts of [[africa-aethiopia|African]] pygmy populations. Unlike Homer's mythical battle, Herodotus presented his account as empirical geography.

### Later Classical Sources

Pliny the Elder compiled and systematized earlier pygmy accounts in his _Natural History_. He placed them within his catalog of [[monstrous-races|monstrous races]]. [[strabo|Strabo]] debated the veracity of pygmy accounts. [[ctesias|Ctesias]] wrote of Pygmies in his _Indica_, a lost work partially preserved through [[photius|Photius]]'s summary.

## Tyson's Philological Method

Tyson's approach to the pygmy question was methodologically sophisticated for 1699. Rather than accepting or rejecting the ancient accounts wholesale, he subjected each source to three tests:

1. **Textual criticism**: Comparing variant readings and manuscript traditions
2. **Geographic correlation**: Mapping ancient descriptions against known ape distributions in [[africa-aethiopia|Africa]] and [[india|India]]
3. **Anatomical comparison**: Using his chimpanzee dissection to evaluate ancient physical descriptions

This triangulation of textual, geographic, and anatomical evidence anticipated methods that would become standard in 19th-century [[philology]] and [[natural-history|natural history]].

## The Stature Question

Central to Tyson's argument is the question of [[stature]]. Ancient sources attributed wildly varying heights to Pygmies — from Homer's warriors small enough to fight cranes to Pliny's beings of 27 inches.

Tyson recognized that "the stature must fall" within certain bounds for a being to be considered human. (001-102) By comparing ancient descriptions with known human stature ranges and ape measurements, he demonstrated that many classical "Pygmies" fell below the minimum plausible human height.

## Pygmies and Apes

Tyson's most original argument was the identification of ancient Pygmies with great apes, particularly the chimpanzee ("Orang-Outang"). He noted that despite widespread dismissal of the pygmy story as fable, "there is something of Truth, they think, must have given the first Rise thereto." (017-001)

Tyson enumerated correspondences between classical descriptions and known ape traits. These included cave-dwelling habits, small stature, bipedal posture, and geographic overlap with ancient accounts. His argument was not that every story was literally true. Rather, consistency across independent sources pointed to a real but misunderstood natural phenomenon.

## Legacy

Tyson's philological essay influenced later discussions of human variety and the human-ape relationship.

His method of collating classical texts with anatomical observation bridged Renaissance humanism and Enlightenment natural science.

The essay remains a notable example of early interdisciplinary research, employing [[philology]], [[comparative-anatomy|comparative anatomy]], and [[natural-history|natural history]] to address a question that had puzzled scholars since antiquity.
