# Dr. Rahon

Dr. Rahon was a late nineteenth-century French anthropologist and osteologist who conducted detailed studies of prehistoric and proto-historic human skeletal remains, with particular attention to stature. His research on the physical dimensions of ancient populations was cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in Section 4 of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* as part of the scientific evidence for the existence of diminutive human races in prehistoric Europe.

## Stature of Prehistoric Races

In Section 4, which surveys the osteological and anatomical evidence for pygmy populations, Tyson invokes Rahon's analysis of Neolithic skeletal material to establish that below-average stature was characteristic of Europe's earliest inhabitants:

> Dr. Rahon, who has recently made a careful study of the bones of pre-historic and proto-historic races, with special reference to their stature, states that the skeletons attributed to the most ancient and to the Neolithic races are of a stature below the middle height, the average being about five feet three inches (1.60 m). (004-009)

Rahon's finding — that the mean stature of Neolithic Europeans fell below the modern middle height — provided Tyson with physical-anthropological corroboration for the thesis that the prehistoric inhabitants of Europe were, by modern standards, a diminutive race. This osteological evidence complemented the literary and folkloric testimonies assembled throughout the essay.

## Significance in Tyson's Argument

Rahon's work belongs to the scientific arm of Tyson's comprehensive argument. While earlier sections of the essay accumulate testimony from classical authors, medieval travelers, and modern ethnographers, Section 4 turns to measurable anatomical data. Rahon's stature measurements of Neolithic skeletons offered precisely the kind of quantitative evidence that nineteenth-century anthropology demanded, bridging the gap between folkloric tradition and empirical science.

Tyson deploys Rahon's finding alongside measurements from [[garson|Dr. Garson]] on Orkney skeletons and broader surveys of stature in ancient populations. Together, these osteological witnesses form a coherent case that humans of below-average height were not anomalous exceptions but rather represented the normal condition of Europe's most ancient inhabitants.
