Juan Luis Vives (Latin: Ludovicus Vives) was a Spanish humanist scholar
and philosopher of the Renaissance period. In his _Scholiast_—a
commentary on Augustine of Hippo's _City of God_—Vives examined
classical and patristic sources concerning the nature of pygmies,
a discussion that Edward Tyson engages with directly in his
_Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_. (014-152)

## Vives and the Pygmy Question

Tyson cites Vives in the context of a long-running scholarly debate
over whether the pygmies described by ancient authors were human beings
or a species of ape. Vives's _Scholiast_ compiled quotations from
various ancient authorities that might illuminate the question, though
Tyson notes that Vives himself did not argue that pygmies were apes.
Rather, Vives "only quotes from other Authors, what might illustrate
the" topic, without taking a definitive position on the matter.
(016-055)

The passage appears in Tyson's Section 16, where he systematically
reviews the arguments of scholars who either identified pygmies with
apes or insisted on their human status. Vives is presented as a neutral
compiler rather than an advocate for either side, reflecting the
humanist scholarly method of gathering testimonia without necessarily
adjudicating between them. (016-055)

## Scholarly Context

Tyson groups Vives alongside Jo. Laurentius Anania, who drew upon
merchants' reports in his own treatment of pygmies. (014-152)
This pairing reflects the early modern practice of combining classical
philology with contemporary travel accounts—a methodology that Tyson
himself would employ throughout his essay. Vives's work on Augustine
placed him within the broader Renaissance project of reconciling
classical and patristic learning with new empirical observations,
including reports of diminutive peoples encountered by European
travellers in Africa and Asia.
