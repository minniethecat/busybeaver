## Overview

Prehistoric Pygmies denotes the archaeological, geological, and palaeontological evidence for diminutive human populations in periods before written record. The concept appears throughout Tyson's essay in connection with Neolithic barrows, cromlechs, dolmens, and underground dwellings that nineteenth-century scholars — notably Professor Boyd Dawkins and De Quatrefages — interpreted as the material remains of aboriginal small-statured races. Tyson treats this evidence as a crucial complement to the textual record, arguing that "the most ancient of which may be referred back to prehistoric times" — a claim that anchors the pygmy question in deep human history rather than in classical literature alone (001-126).

## Ancient Sources

The ancient world had no concept of prehistory as modern archaeology understands it. However, classical authors recorded traditions that later scholars recognized as folk memories of prehistoric populations. Pliny's description of troglodytes who "live in caves" and eat serpents, and his account of pygmies who dwell "in the part of India where the sun rises," were read by nineteenth-century archaeologists not as mythology but as distorted recollections of Neolithic cave-dwellers and diminutive aboriginal populations. The "monstrous races" catalogued by Pliny and repeated by medieval bestiaries — Cynocephali, Blemmyae, Sciopodes — were reinterpreted through the new discipline of prehistoric archaeology as garbled descriptions of actual ethnic groups encountered at the margins of the known world.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson draws extensively on the prehistoric evidence assembled by his contemporaries. He notes that Neolithic remains throughout Europe and Asia point to populations of smaller stature than their modern successors: "traces of these prehistoric connections between the indigenous Negrito" populations and the pygmy races of classical tradition suggest a once-continuous distribution of diminutive peoples across the Old World (002-099). The material evidence falls into several categories. First, the physical remains: skeletons and skulls from Neolithic sites in Switzerland, France, and Britain show average statures consistently below modern European norms, with the Schaffhausen group described as "a group of Neolithic pigmies" by contemporary anatomists (004-001).

Second, the architectural evidence: the chambered mounds, dolmens, and cromlechs that litter the European landscape from Scandinavia to the Mediterranean — structures that "prehistoric peoples" built as dwellings and sepulchres (006-018). Tyson follows Boyd Dawkins in identifying these "fortified or not" structures with the underground dwellings that fairy traditions associate with little people. Third, the stratigraphic evidence: "still in the Stone Age" describes the technological level of the populations that Tyson's contemporaries encountered in parts of Africa and Southeast Asia, suggesting cultural continuity from the Neolithic period (002-040).

Tyson notes that "the Neolithic races are of a stature below the middle height, the average" being lower than modern European means, a finding that he treats as consistent with the pygmy hypothesis without overclaiming its significance (004-012). The crucial point for Tyson's argument is not that any single Neolithic skeleton proves the existence of Homer's Pygmies, but that the cumulative prehistoric evidence establishes the *plausibility* of diminutive human races — what the classical texts asserted, the spade now confirmed.

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's integration of prehistoric archaeology into the pygmy question was remarkably forward-looking for 1699. The Neolithic "pygmy" hypothesis that he reported from Boyd Dawkins and De Quatrefages has been substantially revised: modern palaeoanthropology recognizes Homo floresiensis (the "hobbit" of Flores Island, discovered 2003) as a genuinely diminutive hominin species, while African rainforest foragers such as the Aka and Mbuti represent small-statured populations adapted to tropical forest environments. The European Neolithic "pygmies" that Tyson discussed are now understood as normal-variation gracile populations rather than a distinct dwarf species. Nevertheless, the methodological principle that Tyson championed — that the pygmy question must be investigated through material evidence, not textual exegesis alone — remains a foundation of palaeoanthropology.
