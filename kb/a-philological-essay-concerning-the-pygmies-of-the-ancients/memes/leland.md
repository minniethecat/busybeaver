Charles Godfrey Leland (1824–1903) was an American folklorist, humorist, and writer whose studies of Native American and European folk traditions provided comparative material for late-nineteenth-century folklore scholarship. Educated at Princeton and later at universities in Heidelberg and Munich, Leland developed a particular interest in the fairy lore and spirit beliefs of the Algonquin peoples. His collection *The Algonquin Legends of New England* (1884) documented oral traditions of the Wabanaki tribes, including tales of diminutive spirit beings that resonated with European fairy motifs.

## Testimony on Little People

Leland's work is cited in Tyson's essay principally for the Native American parallels it offers to European traditions of diminutive supernatural beings. In Section 9, Tyson notes that "Mr. Leland, speaking of the Un-a-games-suk, or Indian spirits of the rocks and streams, says that these beings enter far more largely, deeply, and socially into the life and faith of the Indians than elves or" comparable beings do among European peoples (009-035). This observation supports the wider argument that beliefs in "little people" are not confined to European folklore but represent a near-universal motif.

In Section 6, Tyson quotes Leland directly: "deep in them, in caves or among old ruins and rocks," as Mr. Leland, who gives a tale respecting these little people, tells us (006-048). The passage occurs within a broader discussion of subterranean and cave-dwelling traditions associated with diminutive races, a theme that connects Leland's Native American materials to the mound-dweller theories Tyson is evaluating.

## Norse Influence Theory

Leland proposed a specific diffusionist hypothesis regarding certain Algonquin elf traditions. Tyson records that "Mr. Leland states that the red-capped, scanty-shirted elf of the Algonquins was obtained from the Norsemen" (009-056). Tyson adds the qualification that "if, as he says, the idea of little people has sunk so deeply" into Native American traditions, this borrowing must have occurred at a very early period (009-056). This Norse-contact theory represents one of several diffusionist models that the essay considers alongside MacRitchie's mound-dweller hypothesis.

## Contribution to the Essay's Argument

Leland's material serves a specific function within Tyson's argumentative structure. By providing well-documented Native American instances, Leland's collections strengthen the essay's contention that pygmy-like spirit beliefs are globally distributed and therefore unlikely to derive exclusively from a single prehistoric race. Leland's authority as a first-hand collector of oral traditions — rather than an armchair compiler — lent additional weight to the American evidence within the essay's comparative framework.
