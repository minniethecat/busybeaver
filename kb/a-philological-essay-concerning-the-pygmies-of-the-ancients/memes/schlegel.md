Gustaaf Schlegel (1840–1903) was a Dutch sinologist who held the chair of Chinese at Leiden University. He co-founded the journal *T'oung Pao*, one of the leading sinological periodicals of the era.

Having spent his early career in China as an interpreter, Schlegel brought direct experience of East Asian textual traditions to his scholarly work. His publications covered Chinese linguistics, ethnography, and the interpretation of classical Chinese geographical texts.

## Chinese Evidence for a "Land of Little Men"

Schlegel's principal contribution to Tyson's essay is his analysis of the *Shan-hai-king* (Classic of Mountains and Seas), an ancient Chinese geographical text. In Section 2, Tyson records that "as Professor Schlegel points out, their country is spoken of as the Siao-jin-Kouo, or land of little men" (002-071).

The *Shan-hai-king* describes this diminutive people "in distinction, be it noted, to the Peh-min-Kouo, or land of white people, identified by him" with a specific region (002-071). This Chinese testimony provides independent comparative evidence for the global distribution of pygmy-like accounts.

The distinction drawn between the "land of little men" and the "land of white people" is methodologically important (002-071). It indicates the Chinese text treats the diminutive people as a distinct ethnic category, paralleling the essay's effort to separate historical reports from fabulous accretions.

## Etymological Preference

In a discussion of competing etymologies for a key term, Tyson notes that "Schlegel and Mr. Savage Landor both seem to prefer the former etymology" (002-085).

This attribution indicates Schlegel's authority extended to philological questions on East Asian source material. The alignment of his view with that of Arnold Henry Savage Landor suggests a convergence of sinological and ethnographic opinion.

## Prefatory Acknowledgment

Schlegel receives acknowledgment in the essay's prefatory note, where he is named alongside other scholars whom "I have to thank" (P03-012).

This formal gratitude, extended to a scholar at a major European university, reflects the essay's effort to ground its arguments in recognized expert authority. Schlegel's inclusion signals the essay's ambition to be geographically comprehensive, drawing on Chinese sources alongside the classical Mediterranean tradition.
