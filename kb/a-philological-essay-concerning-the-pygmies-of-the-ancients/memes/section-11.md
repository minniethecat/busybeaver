# Section 11

[Footnote A: _Plinij. Hist. Nat._ lib. 7. cap. 2. p.m. 13.]

 
[Footnote B: _Strab. Geograph_. lib. 15. pag. 489.]

[011-001]-----_Si videas hoc

[011-002]Gentibus in nostris, risu quatiere: sed illic,

[011-003]Quanquam eadem assiduè spectantur Prælia, ridet

[011-004]Nemo, ubi tota cohors pede non est altior uno_.[A]

 
[Footnote A: _[[juvenal|Juvenal]]. Satyr_. 13 vers. 170.]

[011-005]This Account therefore of these Campaigns renewed every year on this

[011-006]Provocation between the _Cranes_ and the _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_, contains nothing but

[011-007]what a cautious Man may believe; and _[[homer|Homer]]'s Simile_ in likening the

[011-008]great shouts of the _Trojans_ to the Noise of the _Cranes_, and the

[011-009]Silence of the _Greeks_ to that of the _Pygmies_, is very admirable and

[011-010]delightful. For _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_[B] tells us, That the _Cranes_, to avoid the

[011-011]hardships of the Winter, take a Flight out of _[[scythia|Scythia]]_ to the _Lakes_

[011-012]about the _Nile_, where the _Pygmies_ live, and where 'tis very likely the

[011-013]_Cranes_ may lay their Eggs and breed, before they return. But these rude

[011-014]_Pygmies_ making too bold with them, what could the _Cranes_ do less for

[011-015]preserving their Off-spring than fight them; or at least by their mighty

[011-016]Noise, make a shew as if they would. This is but what we may observe in

[011-017]all other Birds. And thus far I think our _Geranomachia_ or _Pygmæomachia_

[011-018]looks like a true Story; and there is nothing in _Homer_ about it, but

[011-019]what is credible. He only expresses himself, as a _Poet_ should do; and if

[011-020]Readers will mistake his meaning, 'tis not his fault.

 
[Footnote B: _Aristotle. Hist. Animal_. lib. 8. cap. 15. Edit. Scalig.]

[011-021]'Tis not therefore the _Poet_ that is to be blamed, tho' they would father

[011-022]it all on him; but the fabulous _Historians_ in after Ages, who have so

[011-023]odly drest up this Story by their fantastical Inventions, that there is no

[011-024]knowing the truth, till one hath pull'd off those Masks and Visages,

[011-025]wherewith they have disguised it. For tho' I can believe _Homer_, that

[011-026]there is a fight between the _Cranes_ and _Pygmies_, yet I think I am no

[011-027]ways obliged to imagine, that when the _Pygmies_ go to these Campaigns to

[011-028]fight the _Cranes_, that they ride upon _Partridges_, as _Athenæas_ from

[011-029]_Basilis_ an _Indian Historian_ tells us; for, saith he,[A] [Greek:

[011-030]Basilis de en toi deuteroi ton Indikon, oi mikroi, phaesin, andres oi tais

[011-031]Geranois diapolemountes Perdixin ochaemati chrontai;]. For presently

[011-032]afterwards he tells us from _Menecles_, that the _Pygmies_ not only fight

[011-033]the _Cranes_, but the _Partridges_ too, [Greek: Meneklaes de en protae

[011-034]taes synagogaes oi pygmaioi, phaesi, tois perdixi, kai tais Geranois

[011-035]polemousi]. This I could more readily agree to, because _Onesicritus_, as

[011-036]I have quoted him already confirms it; and gives us the same reason for

[011-037]this as for fighting the _Cranes_, because they rob their Nests. But

[011-038]whether these _Partridges_ are as big as _Geese_, I leave as a _Quære_.

 
[Footnote A: _Athenæi Deipnesoph_. lib. p. 9. m. 390.]

[011-039]_Megasthenes_ methinks in _Pliny_ mounts the _Pygmies_ for this expedition

[011-040]much better, for he sets them not on a _Pegasus_ or _Partridges_, but on

[011-041]_Rams_ and _Goats_: _Fama est_ (saith _Pliny[A]) insedentes Arietum

[011-042]Caprarumque dorsis, armatis sagittis, veris tempore universo agmine ad

[011-043]mare descendere_. And _Onesicritus_ in [[strabo|Strabo]] tells us, That a _Crane_ has

[011-044]been often observed to fly from those parts with a brass Sword fixt in

[011-045]him, [Greek: pleistakis d' ekpiptein geranon chalkaen echousan akida apo

[011-046]ton ekeithen plaegmaton.][B] But whether the _Pygmies_ do wear Swords, may

[011-047]be doubted. 'Tis true, _Ctesias_ tells us,[C] That the _King_ of _[[india|India]]_

[011-048]every fifth year sends fifty Thousand Swords, besides abundance of other

[011-049]Weapons, to the Nation of the _Cynocephali_, (a fort of _Monkeys_, as I

[011-050]shall shew) that live in those Countreys, but higher up in the Mountains:

[011-051]But he makes no mention of any such Presents to the poor _Pygmies_; tho'

[011-052]he assures us, that no less than three Thousand of these _Pygmies_ are the

[011-053]_Kings_ constant Guards: But withal tells us, that they are excellent

[011-054]_Archers_, and so perhaps by dispatching their Enemies at a distance, they

[011-055]may have no need of such Weapons to lye dangling by their sides. I may

[011-056]therefore be mistaken in rendering [Greek: akida] a Sword; it may be any

[011-057]other sharp pointed Instrument or Weapon, and upon second Thoughts, shall

[011-058]suppose it a sort of Arrow these cunning _Archers_ use in these

[011-059]Engagements.

 
[Footnote A: _Plinij. Nat. Hist._ lib. 7. cap. 2. p. 13.]

 
[Footnote B: _Strabo Geograph._ lib. 15. p. 489.]

 
[Footnote C: _Vide Photij. Biblioth._]

[011-060]These, and a hundred such ridiculous _[[fable|Fables]]_, have the _Historians_

[011-061]invented of the _Pygmies_, that I can't but be of _Strabo_'s mind,[A]

[011-062][Greek: Rhadion d' an tis Haesiodio, kai Homaeroi pisteuseien

[011-063]haeroologousi, kai tois tragikois poiaetais, hae Ktaesiai te kai

[011-064]Haerodotoi, kai Hellanikoi, kai allois toioutois;] i.e. _That one may

[011-065]sooner believe_ [[hesiod|Hesiod]], _and_ Homer, _and the_ Tragick Poets _speaking of

[011-066]their_ Hero's, _than_ Ctesias _and_ [[herodotus|Herodotus]] _and_ Hellanicus _and such

[011-067]like_. So ill an Opinion had _Strabo_ of the _Indian Historians_ in

[011-068]general, that he censures them _all_ as fabulous;[B] [Greek: Hapantes men

[011-069]toinun hoi peri taes Indikaes grapsantes hos epi to poly pseudologoi

[011-070]gegonasi kath' hyperbolaen de Daeimachos; ta de deutera legei

[011-071]Megasthenaes, Onaesikritos te kai Nearchos, kai alloi toioutoi;] i.e. _All

[011-072]who have wrote of_ India _for the most part, are fabulous, but in the

[011-073]highest degree_ Daimachus; _then_ Megasthenes, Onesicritus, _and_

[011-074]Nearchus, _and such like_. And as if it had been their greatest Ambition

[011-075]to excel herein, _Strabo_[C] brings in _Theopompus_, as bragging, [Greek:

[011-076]Hoti kai mythous en tais Historiais erei kreitton, ae hos Haerodotos, kai

[011-077]Ktaesias, kai Hellanikos, kai hoi ta Hindika syngrapsantes;] _That he

[011-078]could foist in Fables into History, better than_ Herodotus _and_ Ctesias

[011-079]_and_ Hellanicus, _and all that have wrote of_ India. The _Satyrist_

[011-080]therefore had reason to say,

[011-081]-----_Et quicquid Græcia mendax

[011-082]Audet in Historia._[D]

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph._ lib. 11. p.m. 350.]

 
[Footnote B: _Strabo ibid._ lib. 2. p.m. 48.]

 
[Footnote C: _Strabo ibid._ lib. 1 p.m. 29.]

 
[Footnote D: _Juvenal._ _Satyr._ X. _vers._ 174.]

[011-083]_Aristotle_,[A] 'tis true, tells us, [Greek: Holos de ta men agria

[011-084]agriotera en tae [[asia|Asia]], andreiotera de panta ta en taei Europaei,

[011-085]polymorphotata de ta en taei libyaei; kai legetai de tis paroimia, hoti

[011-086]aei pherei ti libyae kainon;] i.e. _That generally the Beasts are wilder

[011-087]in_ Asia, _stronger in_ [[europe|Europe]], _and of greater variety of shapes in_

[011-088]Africa; _for as the_ Proverb _saith_, Africa _always produces something

[011-089]new_. _Pliny_[B] indeed ascribes it to the Heat of the _Climate,

[011-090]Animalium, Hominumque effigies monstriferas, circa extremitates ejus

[011-091]gigni, minimè mirum, artifici ad formanda Corpora, effigiesque cælandas

[011-092]mobilitate igneâ_. But _Nature_ never formed a whole _Species_ of

[011-093]_Monsters_; and 'tis not the _heat_ of the Country, but the warm and

[011-094]fertile Imagination of these _Historians_, that has been more productive

[011-095]of them, than _Africa_ it self; as will farther appear by what I shall

[011-096]produce out of them, and particularly from the Relation that _Ctesias_

[011-097]makes of the _Pygmies_.

 
[Footnote A: _Aristotle Hist. Animal_, lib. 8. cap. 28.]

 
[Footnote B: _Plin. Nat. Hist._ lib. 6. cap. 30. p.m. 741.]

[011-098]I am the more willing to instance in _Ctesias_, because he tells his Story

[011-099]roundly; he no ways minces it; his Invention is strong and fruitful; and

[011-100]that you may not in the least mistrust him, he pawns his word, that all

[011-101]that he writes, is certainly true: And so successful he has been, how

[011-102]Romantick soever his Stories may appear, that they have been handed down

[011-103]to us by a great many other Authors, and of Note too; tho' some at the

[011-104]same time have looked upon them as mere Fables. So that for the present,

[011-105]till I am better informed, and I am not over curious in it, I shall make

[011-106]_Ctesias_, and the other _Indian Historians_, the _Inventors_ of the

[011-107]extravagant Relations we at present have of the _Pygmies_, and not old

[011-108]_Homer_. He calls them, 'tis true, from something of Resemblance of their

[011-109]shape, [Greek: andres]: But these _Historians_ make them to speak the

[011-110]_Indian Language_; to use the same _Laws_; and to be so considerable a

[011-111]Nation, and so valiant, as that the _King_ of _India_ makes choice of them

[011-112]for his _Corps de Guards_; which utterly spoils _Homer's Simile_, in

[011-113]making them so little, as only to fight _Cranes_.

[011-114]_Ctesias_'s Account therefore of the _Pygmies_ (as I find it in

[011-115]_[[photius|Photius]]_'s _Bibliotheca_,[A] and at the latter end of some Editions of

[011-116]_Herodotus_) is this:

 
[Footnote A: _Photij. Bibliothec. Cod._ 72. p.m. 145.]

[011-117][Greek: Hoti en mesae tae Indikae anthropoi eisi melanes, kai kalountai

[011-118]pygmaioi, tois allois homoglossoi Indois. mikroi de eisi lian; hoi

[011-119]makrotatoi auton paecheon duo, hoi de pleistoi, henos haemiseos paecheos,

[011-120]komaen de echousi makrotataen, mechri kai hepi ta gonata, kai eti

[011-121]katoteron, kai pogona megiston panton anthropon; epeidan oun ton pogona

[011-122]mega physosin, ouketi amphiennyntai ouden emation: alla tas trichas, tas

[011-123]men ek taes kephalaes, opisthen kathientai poly kato ton gonaton; tas de

[011-124]ek tou po gonos, emprosthen mechri podon elkomenas. Hepeita

[011-125]peripykasamenoi tas trichas peri apan to soma, zonnyntai, chromenoi autais

[011-126]anti himatiou, aidoion de mega echousin, hoste psauein ton sphyron auton,

[011-127]kai pachy. autoite simoi te kai aischroi. ta de probata auton, hos andres.

[011-128]kai hai boes kai hoi onoi, schedon hoson krioi? kai hoi hippoi auton kai

[011-129]hoi aemionoi, kai ta alla panta zoa, ouden maezo krion; hepontai de toi

[011-130]basilei ton Indon, touton ton pygmaion andres trischilioi. sphodra gar

[011-131]eisi toxotai; dikaiotatoi de eisi kai nomoisi chrontai osper kai hoi

[011-132]Indoi. Dagoous te kai alopekas thaereuousin, ou tois kysin, alla koraxi

[011-133]kai iktisi kai koronais kai aetois.]

[011-134]_Narrat præter ista, in media India homines reperiri nigros, qui Pygmæi

[011-135]appellentur. Eadem hos, qua Inda reliqui, lingua uti, sed valde esse

[011-136]parvos, ut maximi duorum cubitorum, & plerique unius duntaxat cubiti cum

[011-137]dimidio altitudinem non excedant. Comam alere longissimam, ad ipsa usque

[011-138]genua demissam, atque etiam infra, cum barba longiore, quàm, apud ullos

[011-139]hominum. Quæ quidem ubi illis promissior esse cæperit, nulla deinceps

[011-140]veste uti: sed capillos multò infra genua à tergo demissos, barbámque

[011-141]præter pectus ad pedes usque defluentem, per totum corpus in orbem

[011-142]constipare & cingere, atque ita pilos ipsis suos vestimenti loco esse.

[011-143]Veretrum illis esse crassum ac longum, quod ad ipsos quoque pedum

[011-144]malleolos pertingat. Pygmeos hosce simis esse naribus, & deformes. Ipsorum

[011-145]item oves agnorem nostrotum instar esse; boves & asinos, arietum fere

[011-146]magnitudine, equos item multosque & cætera jumenta omnia nihilo esse

[011-147]nostris arietibus majora. Tria horum Pygmæorum millia Indorum regem in suo

[011-148]comitatu habere, quod sagittarij sint peritissimi. Summos esse justitiæ

[011-149]cultores iisdemque quibus Indi reliqui, legibus parere. Venari quoque

[011-150]lepores vulpesque, non canibus, sed corvis, milvis, cornicibus, aquilis

[011-151]adhibitis._

[011-152]In the middle of _India_ (saith _Ctesias_) there are black Men, they are

[011-153]call'd _Pygmies_, using the same Language, as the other _Indians_; they

[011-154]are very little, the tallest of them being but two Cubits, and most of

[011-155]them but a Cubit and a half high. They have very long hair, reaching down

[011-156]to their Knees and lower; and a Beard larger than any Man's. After their

[011-157]Beards are grown long, they wear no Cloaths, but the Hair of their Head

[011-158]falls behind a great deal below their Hams; and that of their Beards

[011-159]before comes down to their Feet: then laying their Hair thick all about

[011-160]their Body, they afterwards gird themselves, making use of their Hair for

[011-161]Cloaths. They have a _Penis_ so long, that it reaches to the Ancle, and

[011-162]the thickness is proportionable. They are flat nosed and ill favoured.

[011-163]Their Sheep are like Lambs; and their Oxen and Asses scarce as big as Rams;

[011-164]and their Horses and Mules, and all their other Cattle not bigger. Three

[011-165]thousand Men of these _Pygmies_ do attend the _King_ of _India_. They are

[011-166]good _Archers_; they are very just, and use the same _Laws_ as the

[011-167]_Indians_ do. They kill Hares and Foxes, not with Dogs, but with Ravens,

[011-168]Kites, Crows, and Eagles.'

[011-169]Well, if they are so good Sports-men, as to kill Hares and Foxes with

[011-170]Ravens, Kites, Crows and Eagles, I can't see how I can bring off _Homer_,

[011-171]for making them fight the _Cranes_ themselves. Why did they not fly their

[011-172]_Eagles_ against them? these would make greater Slaughter and Execution,

[011-173]without hazarding themselves. The only excuse I have is, that _Homer_'s

[011-174]_Pygmies_ were real _[[apes|Apes]]_ like _Men_; but those of _Ctesias_ were neither

[011-175]_Men_ nor _Pygmies_; only a Creature begot in his own Brain, and to be

[011-176]found no where else.
