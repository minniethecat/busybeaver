Edward Burnett Tylor (1832–1917) was a British anthropologist and the founding figure of cultural anthropology in the English-speaking world. His major work, *Primitive Culture* (1871), established the doctrine of "survivals" — the idea that customs, beliefs, and practices persisting in modern societies preserve traces of earlier stages of cultural development. Tylor's concept of animism as the foundation of religious belief was particularly influential.

Tyson's essay draws on Tylor's theoretical framework at critical junctures, positioning the folkloric evidence within the anthropological discourse of the late nineteenth century.

## Tylor in Tyson's Argument

In Section 8, Tyson invokes Tylor to frame a methodological problem. Describing a particular interpretive difficulty, he notes "one in which, as Mr. Tylor" has shown, scholarly caution is warranted when drawing conclusions from folkloric parallels (008-101). This reference establishes Tylor as an authority whose methodological standards the essay aspires to meet.

In Section 9, Tylor is cited more substantively. Tyson notes that "Tylor, as I have already mentioned," has addressed the question of how beliefs in diminutive supernatural beings relate to broader patterns of primitive thought (009-068). The essay draws on Tylor's demonstration of "which idea is shown by Dr. Tylor" — that certain folkloric motifs persist across widely separated cultures — to support its comparative survey of pygmy-like traditions (009-127).

Section 10 synthesizes the Tylorean framework most explicitly. Tyson observes that the traditions he has surveyed "are fully dealt with in Dr. Tylor's pages, and must not be forgotten" when evaluating the evidence for historical pygmy races (010-005). This final invocation positions Tylor's anthropological framework as an essential interpretive lens for the essay's conclusions.

## Significance

Tylor functions in Tyson's essay as a theoretical anchor. While MacRitchie provides the specific mound-dweller hypothesis and Hartland the comparative folklore data, Tylor supplies the overarching anthropological theory — survival, animism, and the comparative method — within which the pygmy question is framed. His presence in the essay reflects the late-Victorian moment when folklore studies was consolidating as a scientific discipline under anthropology's umbrella.

## Key Work Cited

- **_Primitive Culture_ (1871)** — Tylor's foundational work of cultural anthropology, cited across Sections 8–10 as the authoritative framework for understanding the survival of primitive beliefs in modern folklore.
