## Overview

Pygmy Language addresses one of the most contentious sub-questions in the pygmy debate: whether the diminutive races of ancient and modern report possess genuine articulate language, and whether the character of their speech — its presence, absence, or distinctiveness — bears on their classification as fully human. The question matters because language has served, from antiquity to the present, as the diagnostic criterion of human status; to deny a people language is to place them outside the category of the fully human. Tyson examines the evidence from both ancient and modern sources, concluding that the linguistic testimony, while fragmentary, consistently supports the fully human status of pygmy populations.

## Ancient Sources

The most important ancient testimony on pygmy language comes from Ctesias of Cnidus (c. 400 BC), whose *Indica* — a notoriously fabulous account of India — includes the report that the Pygmies use a unique language unintelligible to their Indian neighbours. Tyson records that "Ctesias's speaking Pygmies" possessed "a Language or Speech" distinct from the surrounding populations, a claim that places them within the human category of language-users while marking their speech as exotic and isolated (012-150). The claim is extraordinary precisely because it is so specific: Ctesias does not merely assert that Pygmies exist but attributes to them a particular linguistic characteristic that, in principle, could be verified or falsified by subsequent observers.

The linguistic testimony of other ancient authors is more ambiguous. Homer's Pygmies, famously, do not speak — they fight, they die, they are compared to cranes, but no words are attributed to them. Aristotle's discussion of pygmy vocalizations in the *History of Animals* distinguishes between "voice" (*phone*) and "speech" (*logos*), a distinction that later commentators would invoke to argue that pygmies possess the former but not the latter. Pliny, who reports that Indian pygmies "using the same language as the other Indians," provides the counter-testimony: far from possessing a unique tongue, these pygmies are fully integrated into the regional linguistic community (011-153).

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson's analysis of the pygmy language question is characteristically cautious and cumulative. He begins by noting the intrinsic difficulty of the evidence: "tho' I cannot believe his speaking Pygmies, yet what he" reports about their distinctive tongue must be evaluated against the general credibility of Ctesias as a source (012-039). The fact that Ctesias's account of a pygmy language is "confirm'd by" other ancient testimony — including the Aristotelian distinction between mere voice and genuine language — strengthens the case that ancient observers consistently attributed linguistic capacity to pygmy populations (012-045).

The most probative modern evidence comes from the Akka reports. Stanley and Schweinfurth both noted that the Central African pygmies spoke "the Indian Language as well as the Pygmies" — that is, they were bilingual in their own tongue and that of their taller neighbours (012-101). This observation is doubly significant: it confirms that pygmies possess genuine articulate language, and it provides a mechanism (bilingualism) by which pygmy languages could have been preserved alongside the dominant regional languages.

Tyson addresses the objection that pygmy language might represent mere "human voice" rather than true articulate speech. He observes that "since they were all little black Men, and their Language could not be" dismissed as sub-human vocalization, the linguistic evidence reinforces the anatomical and ethnographic case for their fully human status (012-145). The critical passage from Ctesias — "had the Pygmies ever spoke any Language intelligible by Mankind" — is read by Tyson not as scepticism about pygmy linguistic capacity but as evidence that their language, while genuine, was unfamiliar to outsiders (012-154).

The linguistic evidence also bears on the broader question of pygmy distribution and antiquity. The fact that pygmy populations "speak the Indian Language" suggests long-term contact with neighbouring groups, contradicting the stereotype of pygmies as isolated troglodytes with no social organization (013-149). Aristotle's observation that "speaking of the Pygmies" involves distinguishing them from other small-statured creatures reinforces the taxonomic point: language is the criterion that separates human pygmies from merely small animals (018-050).

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's treatment of the pygmy language question anticipated several themes of modern linguistic anthropology. The distinction he draws between genuine language and mere "voice" parallels the modern concept of duality of patterning — the hierarchical organization of meaningless phonemes into meaningful morphemes that distinguishes human language from animal communication systems. Modern field studies of Central African pygmy languages (Mbuti, Efe, Aka) have confirmed that these are fully developed human languages with complex tonal systems, rich vocabularies for forest ecology, and distinctive registers for hunting, gathering, and ritual contexts. The ancient anxiety about whether pygmies "really" speak — an anxiety rooted in the unwillingness to grant full humanity to physically different populations — has been resolved by the empirical fact that all known pygmy populations speak fully human languages.
