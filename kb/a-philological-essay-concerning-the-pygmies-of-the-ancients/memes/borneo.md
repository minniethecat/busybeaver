## Overview

Borneo, the large Southeast Asian island shared by modern Indonesia, Malaysia, and Brunei, enters Tyson's essay through two distinct lines of evidence: the survival of Negrito populations in the island's interior, and early modern accounts of a "wild man" whose description intersects with classical pygmy traditions. (003-004)

The island's Negrito inhabitants represent, for Tyson, the same pattern of autochthonous diminutive races displaced by larger invading populations that he traces across the globe. The Dyak practice of hunting these forest-dwellers echoes the classical trope of pygmies as hunted peoples.

## Negrito Inhabitants

Tyson cites Borneo as a concrete example of how indigenous diminutive populations have been "split up, amalgamated with, or driven to the islands by the conquering invaders." (003-002) The Negritos of central Borneo survive in the island's interior forests, where "the Dyaks chase them like wild beasts, and shoot down the children, who take refuge in the trees." (003-005)

This pattern — an older, smaller race driven to marginal habitats by larger newcomers — is presented as a universal dynamic: "It is the same story that has been told of the Anglo-Saxon race in its dealings with aborigines in America, and notably, in our case, in Tasmania." (003-009)

Tyson uses the Borneo case to draw a parallel with the fate of diminutive races across continents, positioning it alongside African pygmy populations in his survey of global evidence.

## The "Wild Man" of Borneo

Tyson reproduces at length the account of the Jesuit missionary Le Comte concerning a "wild or savage Man in Borneo" (012-121):

> _That in_ Borneo _this_ wild _or_ savage Man _is indued with extraordinary strength; and not withstanding he walks but upon two Legs, yet he is so swift of foot, that they have much ado to outrun him. People of Quality course him, as we do Stags here: and this sort of hunting is the King's usual divertisement._ (012-122)

This description of a bipedal, extraordinarily swift creature hunted for sport is framed by Tyson within his broader discussion of whether Herodotus's Troglodyte Æthiopians might be "no other than our _Orang-Outang_ or _wild Man_." (012-129) The Borneo wild man thus sits at the boundary between human pygmy populations and the great apes — a taxonomic ambiguity that runs through early modern natural history.

Gassendus, in the Life of Peiresky, confirms that similar hunting practices occur in Angola in Africa (012-127), suggesting a parallel tradition across continents.

## Geographic and Ethnographic Significance

For Tyson, Borneo serves as an Asian parallel to the African pygmy evidence. Just as the Akka and Batwa inhabit the central African forests, the Negritos of Borneo represent a relict diminutive population pushed to the margins by later migrations. The island's geographic isolation — separated from the Asian mainland — made it a refuge for these earlier populations, preserving evidence that had been erased on the continent itself.
