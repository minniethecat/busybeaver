## Biography

Paul of Venice (Latin: _Paulus Venetus_, Italian: _Paolo Veneto_, c. 1369–1429) was a Venetian author known for his geographical treatise _De Regionibus Orientalibus_ (On the Oriental Regions). The work compiled medieval European knowledge of Asia, drawing on earlier travel accounts including those of [[marco-polo|Marco Polo]] and other eastern travelers. It circulated in manuscript and early print editions among European scholars interested in Oriental geography.

## Simians mistaken for pygmies

In [[section-18|Section 18]], Tyson examines reports of pygmy-like creatures in Java. He cites Paul of Venice's account of the island of Basman (in Java Major), where the author describes an abundance of large and small simians that closely resemble humans. Tyson notes that these simians may have been mistaken for pygmy peoples by earlier travelers.

> This _M.P. Venetus_ assures us to have been done; and 'tis not unlikely: For, saith he, _Abundat quoque Regio ipsa_ (sc. Basman in Java majori) _diversis Simiis magnis & parvis, hominibus simillimis..._ (018-116)

Tyson uses this passage to illustrate the long history of confusion between non-human primates and pygmy peoples in travel literature—a theme central to his philological method of separating fact from fable in ancient accounts.
