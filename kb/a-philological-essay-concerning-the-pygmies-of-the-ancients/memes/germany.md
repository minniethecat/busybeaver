## Overview

Germany enters Tyson's essay through its rich fairy-lore traditions, which he marshals as European evidence for the prehistoric existence of diminutive races. German folklore preserves three distinct types of small supernatural beings — hill-dwelling fairies, forest-dwelling Wood and Moss People, and aquatic Water-Dwarfs — each of which Tyson interprets through the lens of MacRitchie's pygmy-fairy theory. (005-122, 007-151)

The German material is particularly valuable because it bridges the Scandinavian and Celtic fairy traditions, showing a continuous distribution of diminutive-race folklore across Northern and Central Europe.

## Hill-Inhabiting Fairies

In his survey of fairy habitation patterns, Tyson notes that "the pages of Keightley's work contain instances of hill-inhabiting fairies in Scandinavia, Denmark, the Isle of Rugen, Iceland, Germany, and Switzerland." (005-122) This distribution of mound-dwelling fairy beliefs across Germanic Europe parallels the Celtic fairy-mound traditions of Britain and Ireland, reinforcing the pan-European character of these beliefs.

The German hill-fairy traditions are presented as part of a continuous belt of similar folklore stretching from Iceland to the Alps, which Tyson interprets as the cultural memory of an aboriginal diminutive population that once inhabited the whole of Europe.

## Wood and Moss People of South Germany

The forest-dwelling beings of South Germany receive detailed description: "the beings in South Germany who resemble the dwarfs, and are called Wild, Wood, Timber and Moss People." (007-099) Tyson quotes the account that:

> _These generally live together in society, but they sometimes appear singly. They are small in stature, yet somewhat larger than the Elf, being the size of children of three years, grey and old-looking, hairy and clad in moss. Their lives are attached, like those of the Hamadryads, to the trees, and if any one causes by friction the inner bark to loosen, a_ [the being dies]. (007-101)

This description — small stature, communal living, hairy bodies, and clothing of moss — closely matches accounts of diminutive forest-dwelling peoples from other regions. The Hamadryad-like attachment to trees suggests an identification with the forest itself characteristic of autochthonous peoples.

The existence of four distinct German names for these beings (Wild, Wood, Timber, and Moss People) indicates the depth and regional variation of the tradition.

## Water-Dwarfs

The aquatic variant of German dwarf-lore is equally well attested: "In Germany the Water-Dwarfs are also known. At Seewenheiher, in the Black Forest, a little water-man (_Seemännlein_) used to come and join the people, work the whole day along with them, and in the evening go back into the lakes." (007-151)

This image of helpful water-dwarfs who work alongside humans during the day and return to their aquatic homes at night parallels the Scandinavian Neck and the Breton Korrigan traditions. The Black Forest setting anchors the tradition in a specific, identifiable German landscape.

Tyson distinguishes these Water-Dwarfs from the Scandinavian Necks by noting that the latter "are, however, not always small, but may be of diverse statures" (007-150), suggesting that the German tradition preserves a more consistent diminutive characterization.

## Pygmy-Fairy Theory

All three German traditions — hill-fairies, Wood-People, and Water-Dwarfs — are interpreted within MacRitchie's framework that European fairy lore preserves the cultural memory of a prehistoric diminutive race. Germany's preservation of multiple distinct types of small-being folklore, each associated with a different habitat (mounds, forests, water), suggests to Tyson a rich and multi-layered tradition that cannot be dismissed as mere fancy.
