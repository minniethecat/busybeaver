Arnold Henry Savage Landor (1865–1924) was an English explorer, writer, and painter who travelled extensively in Asia. He is best known for his first-hand accounts of the Ainu people of northern Japan. Tyson's editor, Bertram C.A. Windle, cites Landor in the appendix (Section 2) of the 1894 edition as a modern ethnographic witness relevant to the question of ancient "little men."

## The Koro-pok-guru

Windle introduces Landor's evidence in connection with the Ainu legend of the Koro-pok-guru — a race of small-statured pit-dwellers who, according to Ainu tradition, inhabited the Japanese islands before the Ainu themselves. Windle writes that the remains of these people — "pit-dwellings, stone arrow-heads, pottery, and other implements" — "will be found fully described by Mr. Savage Landor in a recent most interesting work" (002-070), specifically his 1893 book *Alone with the Hairy Ainu*.

The Koro-pok-guru are spoken of in the Chinese *Shan-hai-king* as the Siao-jin-Kouo, or "land of little men" — a designation carefully distinguished from the Peh-min-Kouo, or "land of white people," identified by Professor Schlegel with the Ainu (002-070).

## The Etymology Debate

Windle notes a scholarly disagreement about the meaning of the name Koro-pok-guru. According to Milne, the term means "men occupying excavations" or pit-dwellers. According to Chamberlain, however, the name means "dwellers under burdocks" and is associated with an Ainu legend in which the little people, before being displaced, "raised their eyes to heaven, and, weeping, cried aloud to the gods, 'Why were we made so small?'" Windle records that "Professor Schlegel and Mr. Savage Landor both seem to prefer the former etymology" (002-085) — that of pit-dwellers.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Landor's account of the Koro-pok-guru serves Windle's broader purpose: connecting the classical Pygmy tradition to modern ethnographic reports of diminutive peoples in Asia. Unlike the fabulous elements of ancient accounts, Landor's description rests on archaeological remains and living Ainu oral tradition, offering a potential real-world parallel to the Pygmies of the ancients.
