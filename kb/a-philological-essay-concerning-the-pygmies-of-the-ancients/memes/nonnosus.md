Nonnosus (fl. 6th century AD) was a Byzantine diplomat who served as ambassador for Emperor Justinian I to Aethiopia (East Africa/Arabia). His account of his travels, though lost as an independent work, survives in a summary preserved by Photius in his *Bibliotheca*. Tyson devotes considerable attention to Nonnosus's narrative because it provides what appears to be an eyewitness account of people resembling the ancient Pygmies.

## The Encounter with the Small People

The central passage, which Tyson quotes from Photius at length (005-057, 012-073), describes Nonnosus's voyage: "sailing from Pharsa, when he came to the farthermost of the Islands, a thing, very strange to be heard of, happened to him" (012-073). Nonnosus encountered people of small stature who "had no Courage; for seeing our Men, they were frighted, as we are at the sight of the greatest and most terrible Beast" (012-082).

Tyson records the details of their way of life: "They liv'd upon Sea Oysters, and Fish that were cast out of the Sea, upon the Island" (012-082). Crucially, these people "did not speak the Indian" language (012-102), leading Tyson to doubt they possessed articulated language at all — a point he uses to distinguish them from the speaking Pygmies described by Ctesias.

## Tyson's Analysis and Vossius's Interpretation

Nonnosus's account became a contested piece of evidence in the scholarly debate Tyson engages. Isaac Vossius interpreted the people Nonnosus encountered as "a Colony of the Nubæ" (016-070), attempting to connect them to the ancient Pygmies. Tyson rejects this: "Vossius's opinion of their being Men, had so possessed him, that it put him upon fancying they must be the Nubæ; but 'tis plain that those in Nonnosus could not be a Colony of the Nubæ" (016-078).

Tyson's reasoning is linguistic: if they were Nubæ colonists, "the Nubæ must have understood their Language" (016-079) — but Nonnosus's party could not communicate with them at all. For Tyson, this linguistic barrier undermines any direct identification of Nonnosus's islanders with the ancient Pygmies of Homeric and Aristotelian tradition.

## Significance to Tyson's Philological Method

Nonnosus represents a critical test case in Tyson's method. The account is relatively late (6th century AD) but purports to be eyewitness testimony rather than literary transmission. Tyson treats it seriously, quoting it at length, but applies the same critical scrutiny he brings to classical authorities. His rejection of Vossius's interpretation demonstrates the core of his philological approach: evaluating each witness on its own terms rather than forcing convergence toward a predetermined conclusion.
