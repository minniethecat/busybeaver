# Section 18

[018-001]I cannot see therefore any thing that will so fairly solve this doubt,

[018-002]that will reconcile all, that will so easily and plainly make out this

[018-003]Story, as by making the _[[apes|Orang-Outang]]_ to be the _Pygmie_ of the Ancients;

[018-004]for 'tis the same Name that Antiquity gave them. For _[[herodotus|Herodotus]]_'s [Greek:

[018-005]andres agrioi], what can they be else, than _Homines Sylvestres_, or _wild

[018-006]Men_? as they are now called. And _[[homer|Homer]]_'s [Greek: andres pygmaioi], are

[018-007]no more an Humane Kind, or Men, then _Herodotus_'s [Greek: andres agrioi],

[018-008]which he makes to be [Greek: theria], or _wild Beasts_: And the [Greek:

[018-009]andres mikroi] or [Greek: melanes] (as they are often called) were just

[018-010]the same. Because this sort of _Apes_ had so great a resemblance to Men,

[018-011]more than other _Apes_ or _Monkeys_; and they going naturally erect, and

[018-012]being designed by Nature to go so, (as I have shewn in the _Anatomy_) the

[018-013]Ancients had a very plausible ground for giving them this denomination of

[018-014][Greek: andres] or [Greek: anthropoi], but commonly they added an Epithet;

[018-015]as [Greek: agrioi, mikroi, pygmaioi, melanes], or some such like. Now the

[018-016]Ancient _Greek_ and _Indian Historians_, tho' they might know these

[018-017]_[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_ to be only _Apes_ like _Men_, and not to be real _Men_, yet

[018-018]being so extremely addicted to _Mythology_, or making [[fable|Fables]], and finding

[018-019]this so fit a Subject to engraft upon, and invent Stories about, they have

[018-020]not been wanting in furnishing us with a great many very Romantick ones on

[018-021]this occasion. And the Moderns being imposed upon by them, and misguided

[018-022]by the Name of [Greek: andres] or [Greek: anthropoi], as if thereby must

[018-023]be always understood an _Humane Kind_, or _real Men_, they have altogether

[018-024]mistaken the Truth of the Story, and have either wholly denied it, or

[018-025]rendered it as improbable by their own Conjectures.

[018-026]This difficulty therefore of their being called _Men_, I think, may fairly

[018-027]enough be accounted by what I have said. But it may be objected that the

[018-028]_Orang-Outang_, or these _wild_ or _savage Men_ are not [Greek: pygmaioi],

[018-029]or _Trispithami_, that is, but two Foot and a quarter high, because by

[018-030]some Relations that have been given, it appears they have been observed to

[018-031]be of a higher stature, and as tall as ordinary Men. Now tho' this may be

[018-032]allowed as to these _wild Men_ that are bred in other places; and probably

[018-033]enough like wise, there are such in some Parts of the Continent of

[018-034]_Africa_; yet 'tis sufficient to our business if there are any there, that

[018-035]will come within our Dimensions; for our Scene lies in _Africa_; where

[018-036]_[[strabo|Strabo]]_ observes, that generally the Beasts are of a less size than

[018-037]ordinary; and this he thinks might give rise to the Story of the

[018-038]_Pygmies_. For, saith he[A] [Greek: Ta de boskaemata autois esti mikra,

[018-039]probata kai aiges, kai kynes mikroi, tracheis de kai machimoi (oikountes

[018-040]mikroi ontes) tacha de kai tous pygmaious apo tes touton mikrophyias

[018-041]epenoaesan, kai aneplasan.] i.e. _That their Beasts are small, as their

[018-042]Sheep, Goats and Oxen, and their Dogs are small, but hairy and fierce: and

[018-043]it may be_ (saith he) _from the [Greek: mikrophyia] or littleness of the

[018-044]stature of these Animals, they have invented and imposed on us the_

[018-045]Pygmies. And then adds, _That no body fit to be believed ever saw them_;

[018-046]because he fancied, as a great many others have done, that these _Pygmies_

[018-047]must be _real Men_, and not a sort of _Brutes_. Now since the other

[018-048]_Brutes_ in this Country are generally of a less size than in other Parts,

[018-049]why may not this sort of _Ape_, the _Orang-Outang_, or _wild Man_, be so

[018-050]likewise. _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_ speaking of the _Pygmies_, saith, [Greek: genos

[018-051]mikron men kai autoi, kai oi hippoi.] _That both they and the Horses there

[018-052]are but small_. He does not say _their_ Horses, for they were never

[018-053]mounted upon _Horses_, but only upon _Partridges, Goats_ and _Rams_. And

[018-054]as the _Horses_, and other _Beasts_ are naturally less in _Africa_ than in

[018-055]other Parts, so likewise may the _Orang-Outang_ be. This that I dissected,

[018-056]which was brought from _Angola_ (as I have often mentioned) wanted

[018-057]something of the just stature of the _Pygmies_; but it was young, and I am

[018-058]therefore uncertain to what tallness it might grow, when at full Age: And

[018-059]neither _Tulpius_, nor _Gassendus_, nor any that I have hitherto met with,

[018-060]have adjusted the full stature of this _Animal_ that is found in those

[018-061]parts from whence ours was brought: But 'tis most certain, that there are

[018-062]sorts of _Apes_ that are much less than the _Pygmies_ are described to be.

[018-063]And, as other _Brutes_, so the _Ape-kind_, in different Climates, may be

[018-064]of different Dimensions; and because the other _Brutes_ here are generally

[018-065]small, why may not _they_ be so likewise. Or if the difference should be

[018-066]but little, I see no great reason in this case, why we should be

[018-067]over-nice, or scrupulous.

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph_. lib. 17. p.m. 565.]

[018-068]As to our _Ape Pygmies_ or _Orang-Outang_ fighting the _Cranes_, this, I

[018-069]think, may be easily enough made out, by what I have already observed; for

[018-070]this _wild Man_ I dissected was Carnivorous, and it may be Omnivorous, at

[018-071]least as much as _Man_ is; for it would eat any thing that was brought to

[018-072]the Table. And if it was not their Hunger that drove them to it, their

[018-073]Wantonness, it may be, would make them apt enough to rob the _Cranes_

[018-074]Nests; and if they did so, no doubt but the _Cranes_ would noise enough

[018-075]about it, and endeavour what they could to beat them off, which a Poet

[018-076]might easily make a Fight: Tho' _Homer_ only makes use of it as a

[018-077]_Simile_, in comparing the great Shouts of the _Trojans_ to the Noise of

[018-078]the _Cranes_, and the Silence of the _Greeks_ to that of the _Pygmies_

[018-079]when they are going to Engage, which is natural enough, and very just, and

[018-080]contains nothing, but what may easily be believed; tho' upon this account

[018-081]he is commonly exposed, and derided, as the Inventor of this Fable; and

[018-082]that there was nothing of Truth in it, but that 'twas wholly a Fiction of

[018-083]his own.

[018-084]Those _Pygmies_ that _Paulus Jovius_[A] describes, tho' they dwell at a

[018-085]great distance from _Africa_, and he calls them _Men_, yet are so like

[018-086]_Apes_, that I cannot think them any thing else. I will give you his own

[018-087]words: _Ultra Lapones_ (saith he) _in Regione inter Corum & Aquilonem

[018-088]perpetua oppressa Caligine_ Pygmæos _reperiri, aliqui eximiæ fidei testes

[018-089]retulerunt; qui postquam ad summum adoleverint, nostratis Pueri denum

[018-090]annorum Mensuram vix excedunt. Meticulosum genus hominum, & garritu

[018-091]Sermonem exprimens, adeo ut tam Simiæ propinqui, quam Statura ac sensibus

[018-092]ab justæ Proceritatis homine remoti videantur_. Now there is this

[018-093]Advantage in our _Hypothesis_, it will take in all the _Pygmies_, in any

[018-094]part of the World; or wherever they are to be met with, without supposing,

[018-095]as some have done, that 'twas the _Cranes_ that forced them to quit their

[018-096]Quarters; and upon this account several Authors have described them in

[018-097]different places: For unless we suppose the _Cranes_ so kind to them, as

[018-098]to waft them over, how came we to find them often in Islands? But this is

[018-099]more than can be reasonably expected from so great Enemies.

 
[Footnote A: _Paul. Jovij de Legatione Muschovitar_. lib. p.m. 489.]

[018-100]I shall conclude by observing to you, that this having been the Common

[018-101]Error of the Age, in believing the _Pygmies_ to be a sort of _little Men_,

[018-102]and it having been handed down from so great Antiquity, what might

[018-103]contribute farther to the confirming of this Mistake, might be, the

[018-104]Imposture of the Navigators, who failing to Parts where these _Apes_ are,

[018-105]they have embalmed their Bodies, and brought them home, and then made the

[018-106]People believe that they were the _Men_ of those Countries from whence

[018-107]they came. This _M.P. Venetus_ assures us to have been done; and 'tis not

[018-108]unlikely: For, saith he,[A] _Abundat quoque Regio ipsa_ (sc. Basman in

[018-109]Java majori) _diversis Simiis magnis & parvis, hominibus simillimis, hos

[018-110]capiunt Venatores & totos depilant, nisi quod, in barba & in loco secreto

[018-111]Pilos relinquunt, & occisos speciebus Aromaticis condiunt, & postea

[018-112]desiccant, venduntque Negociatoribus, qui per diversas Orbis Partes

[018-113]Corpora illa deferentes, homines persuadent Tales Homunciones in Maris

[018-114]Insulis reperiri. Joh. Jonston_[B] relates the same thing, but without

[018-115]quoting the Author; and as he is very apt to do, commits a great mistake,

[018-116]in telling us, _pro Homunculis marinis venditant_.

 
[Footnote A: _M. Pauli Veneti de Regionibus Oriental_. lib. 3. cap. 15. p.

[018-117]m. 390.]

 
[Footnote B: _Jo. Jonston. Hist. Nat. de Quadruped_. p.m. 139.]

[018-118]I shall only add, That the Servile Offices that these Creatures are

[018-119]observed to perform, might formerly, as it does to this very day, impose

[018-120]upon Mankind to believe, that they were of the same _Species_ with

[018-121]themselves; but that only out of Sullenness or cunning, they think they

[018-122]will not _speak_, for fear of being made Slaves. _[[philostratus|Philostratus]]_[A] tells

[018-123]us, That the _Indians_ make use of the _Apes_ in gathering the Pepper; and

[018-124]for this Reason they do defend and preserve them from the _Lions_, who are

[018-125]very greedy of preying upon them: And altho' he calls them _Apes_, yet he

[018-126]speaks of them as _Men_, and as if they were the Husbandmen of the _Pepper

[018-127]Trees_, [Greek: kai ta dendra oi piperides, on georgoi pithekoi]. And he

[018-128]calls them the People of _Apes_; [Greek: ou legetai pithekon oikein demos

[018-129]en mychois tou orous]. _Dapper_[B] tells us, _That the Indians take the_

[018-130]Baris _when young, and make them so tame, that they will do almost the

[018-131]work of a Slave; for they commonly go erect as Men do. They will beat Rice

[018-132]in a Mortar, carry Water in a Pitcher_, &c. And Gassendus[C] in the Life

[018-133]of _Pieresky_, tells us, us, _That they will play upon a Pipe or Cittern,

[018-134]or the like Musick, they will sweep the House, turn the Spit, beat in a

[018-135]Mortar, and do other Offices in a Family_. And _Acosta_, as I find him

[018-136]quoted by _Garcilasso de la Vega_[D] tells us of a _Monkey_ he saw at the

[018-137]Governour's House at _Cartagena_, 'whom they fent often to the Tavern for

[018-138]Wine, with Money in one hand, and a Bottle in the other; and that when he

[018-139]came to the Tavern, he would not deliver his Money, until he had received

[018-140]his Wine. If the Boys met with him by the way, or made a houting or noise

[018-141]after him, he would set down his Bottle, and throw Stones at them; and

[018-142]having cleared the way he would take up his Bottle, and hasten home, And

[018-143]tho' he loved Wine excessively, yet he would not dare to touch it, unless

[018-144]his Master gave him License.' A great many Instances of this Nature might

[018-145]be given that are very surprising. And in another place he tells us, That

[018-146]the Natives think that they can speak, but will not, for fear of being

[018-147]made to work. And _Bontius_[E] mentions that the _Javans_ had the same

[018-148]Opinion concerning the _Orang-Outang_, _Loqui vero eos, easque Javani

[018-149]aiunt, sed non velle, ne ad labores cogerentur_.

 
[Footnote A: _Philostratus in vita Apollonij Tyanæi_, lib. 3. cap. I. p.

[018-150]m. 110, & 111.]

 
[Footnote B: _Dapper Description de l'Afrique_, p.m. 249.]

 
[Footnote C: _Gassendus in vita Pierskij_, lib. 5. p.m. 169.]

 
[Footnote D: _Garcilasso de la Vega Royal Commentaries of Peru_, lib. 8.

[018-151]cap. 18. p. 1333.]

 
[Footnote E: _Jac. Bontij Hist. Nat. & Med_. lib. 5. cap. 32. p.m. 85.]
