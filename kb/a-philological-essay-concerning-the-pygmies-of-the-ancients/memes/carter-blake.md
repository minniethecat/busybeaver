# Carter Blake

Carter Blake was a nineteenth-century British anthropologist and archaeologist who conducted excavations of prehistoric burials in the Shetland Islands. His work on skeletal remains from the Muckle Heog of Unst contributed to the Victorian debate over the stature of early British populations — a question that [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] addresses in his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*.

## The Muckle Heog Excavation

Tyson cites Blake's findings in Section 8 of his essay, during a discussion of whether the earliest inhabitants of Britain were of diminutive stature. Surveying the scanty archaeological evidence then available, Tyson reports:

> The scanty evidence which we have on this point, so far as it goes, proves the truth of this assertion. Mr. Carter Blake found in the Muckle Heog of the Island of Unst, one of the Shetlands, together with stone vessels, human interments of persons of considerable stature and of great muscular strength. (008-104–008-113)

Blake's findings from this Shetland chambered tomb were significant because they contradicted the hypothesis that Britain's prehistoric populations were pygmy-sized. The discovery of robust, tall skeletons alongside stone vessels pointed instead to a population of substantial physical development.

## Context in Tyson's Argument

Tyson deploys Blake's evidence to counter claims made by earlier antiquarian writers who had suggested that the rude stone monuments of Britain were the work of a vanished dwarf race. The Shetland interments, as documented by Blake, demonstrated the opposite: individuals of "considerable stature" associated with megalithic constructions. This finding aligned with other archaeological evidence, including the [[keiss-skeletons|Keiss skeletons]] examined by [[huxley|Professor Huxley]], which Tyson also cites in the same passage.

## Significance

Blake's contribution to the pygmy question is indirect but important: his excavation data helped establish that the prehistoric megalith-builders of northern Europe were not a race of dwarfs — a hypothesis that had been entertained by some antiquarian writers seeking universal pygmy origins for megalithic monuments. His work thus represents the emerging archaeological method that would eventually supplant speculative ethnology in discussions of prehistoric stature.
