Professor Alexander Macalister (1844–1919) was a British anatomist and anthropologist who served as Professor of Anatomy at the University of Cambridge from 1883 until his death, and as President of the Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland. In the prefatory note to his essay, Tyson acknowledges Macalister as a reviewer who read through portions of the manuscript and provided "valuable suggestions."

## Role as Reviewer of Tyson's Manuscript

Tyson's acknowledgment of Macalister appears prominently in the closing paragraph of the prefatory note:

> Finally, I have to acknowledge my indebtedness to Professor Alexander Macalister, President of the Anthropological Institute, and to Mr. E. Sidney Hartland, for their kindness in reading through, the former the first two sections, and the latter the last two sections of the Introduction, and for the valuable suggestions which both have made. (P03-015)

Macalister's role as a reader of the first two sections of the Introduction indicates that he exercised a form of scholarly peer review over the opening portions of Tyson's essay — the sections that established the philological and anthropological framework for the entire work. As President of the Anthropological Institute, Macalister represented the institutional voice of British anthropology at the time of the essay's composition. His imprimatur, however informal, would have lent the work credibility within the scientific establishment.

The division of labour between Macalister (who read the first two sections) and the folklorist E. Sidney Hartland (who read the last two) is suggestive of Tyson's careful targeting of his reviewers: Macalister's anatomical and anthropological expertise was best suited to the opening sections dealing with physical evidence, while Hartland's folkloric knowledge aligned with the later sections' focus on tradition and legend.

## Scientific Career

Macalister was one of the most distinguished British anatomists of his generation. Born in Dublin and educated at Trinity College, he held chairs of anatomy at the University of Dublin (1877–1883) before succeeding Sir George Murray Humphry as Professor of Anatomy at Cambridge, a position he held for thirty-six years.

His anatomical work was exceptionally varied. He published extensively on human and comparative anatomy, including a widely used *Text-Book of Human Anatomy* (1889) that went through multiple editions. His morphological work extended to invertebrate anatomy and the systematic description of muscle variations in humans. Beyond anatomy, Macalister was a serious contributor to Egyptian archaeology and palaeography, serving as a member of the Egypt Exploration Fund and publishing on Egyptian mummies and skeletal remains — an intersection of anatomical expertise and archaeological interest that paralleled the concerns of Tyson's essay.

As President of the Anthropological Institute in the mid-1890s, Macalister occupied the leading institutional position in British anthropology. The Institute, founded in 1871, was the primary professional body for anthropologists in Britain and the publisher of the *Journal of the Anthropological Institute*, the field's most important periodical. Macalister's presidency during the period when Tyson was composing his essay placed him at the institutional centre of the discipline.

## Significance for Tyson's Essay

Macalister's willingness to read and comment upon Tyson's draft carries several implications. It suggests that the essay, while the work of an independent scholar rather than an academic professional, was taken seriously by the anthropological establishment. Macalister's suggestions, which Tyson describes as "valuable" without specifying their content, may have shaped the final form of the Introduction's opening sections — the portions of the essay most concerned with the anatomical and physical-anthropological evidence for pygmy populations.

The acknowledgment also reveals the collaborative nature of late-Victorian scholarly production and the role of informal networks in manuscript preparation before formal peer review became standardised in scientific publishing.

## Key Works Cited

- **Text-Book of Human Anatomy** (1889) — Macalister's comprehensive anatomical textbook, the standard English-language reference of its period.
- **Introduction to Animal Morphology** (1876) — A foundational work in comparative anatomy.
- **Contributions to the Egypt Exploration Fund** — Reports on Egyptian mummies and skeletal remains.
