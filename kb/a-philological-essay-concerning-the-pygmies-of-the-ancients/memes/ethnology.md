## Overview

The Ethnological Method is the comparative study of human races through the converging evidence of physical anthropology, linguistics, archaeology, geography, and textual criticism. Although the term "ethnology" was only coming into currency in Tyson's intellectual milieu (the essay cites the *American Bureau of Ethnology* among its sources), the method itself is fully deployed throughout the essay. Tyson approaches the pygmy question not as a single problem soluble by any one type of evidence but as a nexus of converging and conflicting data streams that must be weighed against one another. This synthetic approach — treating the pygmy question as simultaneously a philological, anatomical, archaeological, and ethnographic problem — is what distinguishes the essay from earlier treatments that relied on classical authority alone.

## Ancient Sources

The ancient world possessed no formal discipline of ethnology, but the empirical impulse that would later develop into comparative ethnography is visible in several classical authors. Herodotus's systematic comparison of Egyptian, Scythian, and Greek customs; Aristotle's classification of animal species by their physical characteristics; and Strabo's geographical surveys all represent partial anticipations of the comparative method. Tyson's own practice — moving freely between Homeric poetry, Aristotelian zoology, and nineteenth-century craniometry — would have been unintelligible to the ancients, but it is the logical extension of their empirical curiosity into a domain of systematic comparison.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson's ethnological method proceeds by triangulation. No single line of evidence — not the classical texts, not the travellers' reports, not the archaeological remains — is treated as decisive in isolation. Instead, Tyson weighs each body of evidence against the others and seeks convergence. When Homer, Aristotle, Pliny, Ctesias, and Stanley all describe small-statured populations in roughly similar terms, the convergence itself becomes evidence — not that any single account is accurate but that the phenomenon they describe is unlikely to be pure fabrication.

The method is clearest in Tyson's treatment of stature. Rather than accepting the classical claim that pygmies are three spans (*trispithameis*) tall, he compiles anthropometric data from modern explorers and anatomists: the Negritos of the Andaman Islands, the Akka of Central Africa, the Samang of the Malay Peninsula, the Aeta of the Philippines. By comparing these measured populations against the classical descriptions, he establishes a range of plausible pygmy stature — approximately four to five feet — that neither dismisses the ancient testimony nor accepts it uncritically.

The comparative method also operates diachronically. Tyson identifies a historical trajectory from the Homeric simile — a poetic image embedded in a naturalistic observation of crane migration — through the accumulation of geographical and ethnographic detail in the Hellenistic and Roman periods, to the modern convergence of archaeological, anatomical, and ethnographic evidence. This historicization of the pygmy tradition is itself an ethnological move: it treats the textual tradition not as a static deposit of facts but as a dynamic process of interpretation and reinterpretation.

The limitations of the method are equally instructive. The comparative approach depends on the assumption that similar physical characteristics (short stature, dark skin, distinctive hair form) indicate genetic relationship — an assumption that modern genetics has partly confirmed and partly falsified. Tyson himself recognized the danger: "these ethnological equations we need not much concern ourselves" with, he cautions, acknowledging that racial classification based on superficial traits may obscure more than it reveals.

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's triangulation method remains the gold standard for interdisciplinary research on questions that span the humanities and sciences. Modern anthropologists, geneticists, and linguists studying the origins and relationships of "pygmy" populations in Africa and Southeast Asia deploy a method that Tyson would recognize: comparing genetic markers against linguistic data against ethnographic observations against archaeological evidence, with no single line of evidence treated as decisive. The major advance since Tyson's day is the availability of genomic data, which has revealed that African and Southeast Asian "pygmy" populations represent independent evolutionary convergences rather than descent from a single ancestral pygmy race — a finding that vindicates Tyson's caution about "ethnological equations" and his preference for multiple independent lines of evidence.
