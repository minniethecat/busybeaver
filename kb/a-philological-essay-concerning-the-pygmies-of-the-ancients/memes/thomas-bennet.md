Thomas Bennet (fl. 1690–1706) was a London bookseller and publisher operating from the Half-Moon in St. Paul's Church-yard, one of the principal centres of the English book trade in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries. His name appears on the title page of Edward Tyson's essay as the publisher who brought the work to print in 1699.

## The Publication of Tyson's Essay

The title page of Tyson's essay records the publication details that establish Bennet's role:

> Printed for Thomas Bennet at the Half-Moon in St. Paul's Church-yard; and Daniel Brown at the Black Swan and Bible without Temple-Bar (001-034)

This imprint follows the standard formula of the period, in which a bookseller-publisher ("printed for") rather than a printer took the financial risk of publication and offered the work for sale at their shop. The Half-Moon was one of the well-known bookselling addresses in St. Paul's Church-yard, a precinct crowded with booksellers' shops that served as the hub of the London book trade from the sixteenth century until the Great Fire and its subsequent rebuilding.

The co-publication arrangement with Daniel Brown — who operated from the Black Swan and Bible without Temple-Bar — was also typical of the period. Joint publication spread the financial risk of a scholarly work with a limited readership between two stationers, each offering the book from their respective shops.

## Bennet's Place in the London Book Trade

Thomas Bennet was a significant figure in the late-Stuart book trade. He is recorded as having published works by John Locke, the philosopher, and Samuel Clarke, the theologian and Boyle Lecturer — associations that place him within the圈子 of serious philosophical and theological publishing rather than the more ephemeral popular press. His shop at the Half-Moon was located in the heart of the bookselling district, a location that marked him as an established rather than marginal trader.

The decision to publish Tyson's philological essay — a learned work on a recondite subject, drawing upon Greek, Latin, and Hebrew sources — suggests Bennet's willingness to take on scholarly projects with limited commercial appeal. The essay was likely printed in a small edition aimed at the Republic of Letters rather than a broad readership, a publishing model that required the publisher to balance scholarly prestige against commercial viability.

## The Half-Moon in St. Paul's Church-yard

The sign of the Half-Moon was one of dozens of distinctive shop signs that identified booksellers' premises in St. Paul's Church-yard before the numbering of streets became standard. These signs functioned as addresses and were prominently displayed on the shop front. Bennet's Half-Moon would have been a familiar landmark to London's literate public, who navigated the city by such visual markers.

St. Paul's Church-yard in the 1690s was home to many of the leading booksellers of the day, including Richard Chiswell, Awnsham and John Churchill, and Thomas Newborough. Bennet's presence among them marks him as a member of the established book trade rather than a peripheral figure.
