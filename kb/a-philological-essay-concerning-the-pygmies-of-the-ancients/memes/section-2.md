# Section 2

[002-001]"Their list of forbidden degrees

[002-002]An extensive morality shows,"

[002-003]since even the marriage of cousins-german is considered highly immoral.

[002-004]"Men and women," says Man, "are models of constancy." They believe in a

[002-005]Supreme Deity, respecting whom they say, that "although He resembles fire,

[002-006]He is invisible; that He was never born, and is immortal; that He created

[002-007]the world and all animate and inanimate objects, save only the powers of

[002-008]evil. During the day He knows everything, even the thoughts of the mind;

[002-009]He is angry when certain sins are committed, and full of pity for the

[002-010]unfortunate and miserable, whom He sometimes condescends to assist. He

[002-011]judges souls after death, and pronounces on each a sentence which sends

[002-012]them to paradise or condemns them to a kind of purgatory. The hope of

[002-013]escaping the torments of this latter place influences their conduct.

[002-014]Puluga, this Deity, inhabits a house of stone; when it rains, He descends

[002-015]upon the earth in search of food; during the dry weather He is asleep."

[002-016]Besides this Deity, they believe in numerous evil spirits, the chief of

[002-017]whom is the Demon of the Woods. These spirits have created themselves, and

[002-018]have existed _ab immemorabili_. The sun, which is a female, and the moon,

[002-019]her husband, are secondary deities.

 
[Footnote A: The quotations from this author are taken from his work _Les

[002-020]Pygmées_. Paris, J.B. Baillière et Fils, 1887.]

 
[Footnote B: _Jour. Anthrop. Inst_., vii.]

 
[Footnote C: _Ibid_., iv.]

[002-021]South of the Andaman Islands are the Nicobars, the aborigines of which,

[002-022]the Shom Pen,[A] now inhabit the mountains, where, like so many of their

[002-023]brethren, they have been driven by the Malays. They are of small, but not

[002-024]pigmy stature (five feet two inches), a fact which may be due to crossing.

 
[Footnote A: Man, _Jour. Anthrop. Inst._, xviii. p. 354.]

[002-025]Following the Negritos east amongst the islands, we find in Luzon the

[002-026]Aetas or Inagtas, a group of which is known in Mindanao as Manamouas. The

[002-027]Aetas live side by side with the Tagals, who are of Malay origin. They

[002-028]were called Negritos del Monte by the Spaniards who first colonised these

[002-029]islands. Their average stature, according to Wallace, ranges from four

[002-030]feet six inches to four feet eight inches. In New Guinea, the Karons, a

[002-031]similar race, occupy a chain of mountains parallel to the north coast of

[002-032]the great north-western peninsula. At Port Moresby, in the same island,

[002-033]the Koiari appear to represent the most south-easterly group; but my

[002-034]friend Professor Haddon, who has investigated this district, tells me that

[002-035]he finds traces of a former existence of Negritos at Torres Straits and in

[002-036]North Queensland, as shown by the shape of the skulls of the inhabitants

[002-037]of these regions.

[002-038]The Malay Peninsula contains in Perak hill tribes called "savages" by the

[002-039]Sakays. These tribes have not been seen by Europeans, but are stated to be

[002-040]pigmy in stature, troglodytic, and still in the Stone Age. Farther south

[002-041]are the Semangs of Kedah, with an average stature of four feet ten inches,

[002-042]and the Jakuns of Singapore, rising to five feet. The Annamites admit that

[002-043]they are not autochthonous, a distinction which they confer upon the Moïs,

[002-044]of whom little is known, but whose existence and pigmy Negrito

[002-045]characteristics are considered by De Quatrefages as established.

[002-046]China no longer, so far as we know, contains any representatives of this

[002-047]type, but Professor Lacouperie[A] has recently shown that they formerly

[002-048]existed in that part of [[asia|Asia]]. According to the annals of the Bamboo Books,

[002-049]"In the twenty-ninth year of the Emperor Yao, in spring, the chief of the

[002-050]Tsiao-Yao, or dark pigmies, came to court and offered as tribute feathers

[002-051]from the Mot." The Professor continues, "As shown by this entry, we begin

[002-052]with the semi-historic times as recorded in the 'Annals of the Bamboo

[002-053]Books,' and the date about 2048 B.C. The so-called feathers were simply

[002-054]some sort of marine plant or seaweed with which the immigrant Chinese,

[002-055]still an inland people, were yet unacquainted. The Mot water or river,

[002-056]says the Shan-hai-king, or canonical book of hills and seas, was situated

[002-057]in the south-east of the Tai-shan in Shan-tung. This gives a clue to the

[002-058]localisation of the pigmies, and this localisation agrees with the

[002-059]positive knowledge we possess of the small area which the Chinese dominion

[002-060]covered at this time. Thus the Negritos were part of the native population

[002-061]of China when, in the twenty-third century B.C., the civilised Bak tribes

[002-062]came into the land." In Japan we have also evidence of their existence.

[002-063]This country, now inhabited by the Niphonians, or Japanese, as we have

[002-064]come to call them, was previously the home of the Ainu, a white, hairy

[002-065]under-sized race, possibly, even probably, emigrants from [[europe|Europe]], and now

[002-066]gradually dying out in Yezo and the Kurile Islands. Prior to the Ainu was

[002-067]a Negrito race, whose connection with the former is a matter of much

[002-068]dispute, whose remains in the shape of pit-dwellings, stone arrow-heads,

[002-069]pottery, and other implements still exist, and will be found fully

[002-070]described by Mr. Savage Landor in a recent most interesting work.[B] In

[002-071]the Shan-hai-king, as Professor Schlegel[C] points out, their country is

[002-072]spoken of as the Siao-jin-Kouo, or land of little men, in distinction, be

[002-073]it noted, to the Peh-min-Kouo, or land of white people, identified by him

[002-074]with the Ainu. These little men are spoken of by the Ainu as

[002-075]Koro-puk-guru, _i.e._, according to Milne, men occupying excavations, or

[002-076]pit-dwellers. According to Chamberlain, the name means dwellers under

[002-077]burdocks, and is associated with the following legend. Before the time of

[002-078]the Ainu, Yezo was inhabited by a race of dwarfs, said by some to be two

[002-079]to three feet, by others only one inch in height. When an enemy

[002-080]approached, they hid themselves under the great leaves of the burdock

[002-081](_koro_), for which reason they are called Koro-puk-guru, i.e., the men

[002-082]under the burdocks. When they were exterminated by the wooden clubs of the

[002-083]Ainu, they raised their eyes to heaven, and, weeping, cried aloud to the

[002-084]gods, "Why were we made so small?" It should be said that Professor

[002-085]Schlegel and Mr. Savage Landor both seem to prefer the former etymology.

 
[Footnote A: Babylonian and Oriental Record, vol. v.]

 
[Footnote B: Alone with the Hairy Ainu.]

 
[Footnote C: _Problèmes Géographiques. Les Peuples Etrangers chez les

[002-086]Historiens Chinois_. Extrait du T'oung-pao, vol. _iv_. No. 4. Leide, E.J.

[002-087]Brill.]

[002-088]Passing to the north-west of the Andamans, we find in [[india|India]] a problem of

[002-089]considerable difficulty. That there were at one period numerous Negrito

[002-090]tribes inhabiting that part of Asia is indubitable; that some of them

[002-091]persist to this day in a state of approximate purity is no less true, but

[002-092]the influence of crossing has here been most potent. Races of lighter hue

[002-093]and taller stature have invaded the territory of the Negritos, to a

[002-094]certain extent intermarried with them, and thus have originated the

[002-095]various Dravidian tribes. These tribes, therefore, afford us a valuable

[002-096]clue as to the position occupied in former days by their ancestors, the

[002-097]Negritos.

[002-098]In some of the early Indian legends, De Quatrefages thinks that he finds

[002-099]traces of these prehistoric connections between the indigenous Negrito

[002-100]tribes and their invaders. The account of the services rendered to Rama by

[002-101]Hânuman and his monkey-people may, he thinks, easily be explained by

[002-102]supposing the latter to be a Negrito tribe. Another tale points to unions

[002-103]of a closer nature between the alien races. Bhimasena, after having

[002-104]conquered and slain Hidimba, at first resisted the solicitations of the

[002-105]sister of this monster, who, having become enamoured of him, presented

[002-106]herself under the guise of a lovely woman. But at the wish of his elder

[002-107]brother, Youdhichshira, the king of justice, and with the consent of his

[002-108]mother, he yielded, and passed some time in the dwelling of this Negrito

[002-109]or Dravidian Armida.

[002-110]It will now be necessary to consider some of these races more or less

[002-111]crossed with alien blood.

[002-112]In the centre of India, amongst the Vindyah Mountains, live the Djangals

[002-113]or Bandra-Lokhs, the latter name signifying man-monkey, and thus

[002-114]associating itself with the tale of Rama, above alluded to. Like most of

[002-115]the Dravidian tribes, they live in great misery, and show every sign of

[002-116]their condition in their attenuated figures. One of this tribe measured by

[002-117]Rousselet was five feet in height. It may here be remarked that the

[002-118]stature of the Dravidian races exceeds that of the purer Negritos, a fact

[002-119]due, no doubt, to the influence of crossing. Farther south, in the

[002-120]Nilgherry Hills, and in the neighbourhood of the Todas and Badagas, dwell

[002-121]the Kurumbas. and Irulas (children of darkness). Both are weak and

[002-122]dwarfish, the latter especially so. They inhabit, says Walhouse,[A] the

[002-123]most secluded, densely wooded fastnesses of the mountain slopes. They are

[002-124]by popular tradition connected with the aboriginal builders of the rude

[002-125]stone monuments of the district, though, according to the above-mentioned

[002-126]authority, without any claim to such distinction. They, however, worship

[002-127]at these cromlechs from time to time, and are associated with them in

[002-128]another interesting manner. "The Kurumbas of Nulli," says Walhouse, "one

[002-129]of the wildest Nilgherry declivities, come up annually to worship at one

[002-130]of the dolmens on the table-land above, in which they say one of their old

[002-131]gods resides. Though they are regarded with fear and hatred as sorcerers

[002-132]by the agricultural B[)a]d[)a]gas of the table-land, one of them must,

[002-133]nevertheless, at sowing-time be called to guide the first plough for two

[002-134]or three yards, and go through a mystic pantomime of propitiation to the

[002-135]earth deity, without which the crop would certainly fail. When so

[002-136]summoned, the Kurumba must pass the night by the dolmens alone, and I have

[002-137]seen one who had been called from his present dwelling for the morning

[002-138]ceremony, sitting after dark on the capstone of a dolmen, with heels and

[002-139]hams drawn together and chin on knees, looking like some huge ghostly fowl

[002-140]perched on the mysterious stone." Mr. Gomme has drawn attention to this

[002-141]and other similar customs in the interesting remarks which he makes upon

[002-142]the influence of conquered non-Aryan races upon their Aryan subduers.[B]

 
[Footnote A: _Jour. Anthrop. Inst._, vii. 21.]

 
[Footnote B: Ethnology and Folk-Lore, p. 46; The Village Community, p.

[002-143]105.]

[002-144]Farther south, in Ceylon, the Veddahs live, whom Bailey[A] considers to be

[002-145]identical with the hill-tribes of the mainland, though, if this be true,

[002-146]some at least must have undergone a large amount of crossing, judging from

[002-147]the wavy nature of their hair. The author just quoted says, "The tallest

[002-148]Veddah I ever saw, a man so towering above his fellows that, till I

[002-149]measured him, I believed him to be not merely comparatively a tall man,

[002-150]was only five feet three inches in height. The shortest man I have

[002-151]measured was four feet one inch. I should say that of males the ordinary

[002-152]height is from four feet six inches to five feet one inch, and of females

[002-153]from four feet four inches to four feet eight inches."

 
[Footnote A: _Trans. Ethn. Soc._, ii. 278.]

[002-154]In the east the Santals inhabit the basin of the Ganges, and in the west

[002-155]the Jats belong to the Punjab, and especially to the district of the

[002-156]Indus. The Kols inhabit the delta of the Indus and the neighbourhood of

[002-157]Gujerat, and stretch almost across Central India into Behar and the

[002-158]eastern extremities of the Vindhya Mountains. Other Dravidian tribes are

[002-159]the Oraons, Jouangs, Buihers, and Gounds. All these races have a stature

[002-160]of about five feet, and, though much crossed, present more or less marked

[002-161]Negrito characteristics. Passing farther west, the Brahouis of

[002-162]Beluchistan, a Dravidian race, who regard themselves as the aboriginal

[002-163]inhabitants, live side by side with the Belutchis. Finally, in this

[002-164]direction, there seem to have been near Lake Zerrah, in [[persia|Persia]], Negrito

[002-165]tribes who are probably aboriginal, and may have formed the historic black

[002-166]guard of the ancient kings of Susiana.
