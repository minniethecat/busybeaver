# Theopompus

**Theopompus** (c. 380–315 BC) was a Greek historian from Chios, a pupil of [[isocrates]] and a prolific writer whose major works—the _Hellenica_ (a continuation of [[thucydides]]) and the _Philippica_ (a history centered on [[philip-of-macedon]])—survive only in fragments. In Tyson's [[A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients]], Theopompus appears as an example of a classical historian who openly boasted of embellishing his narratives with fabulous material (011-075).

## Strabo's Critique

The key passage appears in [[section-11]], where Tyson quotes [[strabo]] to illustrate the classical world's awareness of unreliable historiography:

> "[011-075]to excel herein, Strabo brings in Theopompus, as bragging, that he could foist in Fables into History, better than Herodotus and Ctesias and Hellanicus, and all that have wrote of India." (011-075–011-079)

Strabo's criticism places Theopompus in a tradition of historians who deliberately mixed fact with fiction. Theopompus's boast—that he could insert fables (_mythoi_) into his histories more skillfully than his predecessors—reveals the competitive culture of Greek historiography, where rhetorical embellishment was often prized alongside factual accuracy.

## Life and Works

Born on the island of Chios, Theopompus studied rhetoric under Isocrates and became one of the most celebrated historians of the 4th century BC. His _Hellenica_ covered Greek history from 411 to 394 BC, continuing Thucydides's unfinished narrative. His magnum opus, the _Philippica_ in 58 books, used the career of Philip II of Macedon as an organizing frame for a wide-ranging history of the Greek world and its neighbors.

Ancient critics praised Theopompus's diligence and learning while faulting his taste for digression, sensationalism, and moral censure. His fragments reveal an interest in marvels, distant peoples, and paradoxical customs—topics that overlapped with the traditions about [[pygmies]] and other fabulous races.

## Significance for Tyson's Essay

Theopompus serves Tyson's argument in two ways:

1. **Classical self-awareness**: The passage demonstrates that ancient critics themselves recognized the problem of fabulous history-writing. Strabo's condemnation of Theopompus shows that not all classical testimony about distant peoples was accepted uncritically—a point Tyson uses to strengthen his own methodology of sifting reliable from unreliable sources.

2. **The Herodotus comparison**: By positioning Theopompus as claiming superiority over [[herodotus]] in the art of inserting fables, the passage indirectly illuminates Herodotus's reputation in antiquity as a historian who included marvels in his work. This matters for Tyson because Herodotus is a key source on African [[pygmies]] (the Nasamonian expedition account).

Tyson thus deploys Theopompus not as a direct witness to pygmy traditions but as part of the methodological framework that justifies his critical approach to classical sources.
