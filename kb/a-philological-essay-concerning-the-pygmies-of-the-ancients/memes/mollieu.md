# Mollieu

Mollieu (Gaspard-Théodore Mollien, 1796–1872) was a French explorer and travel writer whose account of the interior of West Africa provided one of the earliest nineteenth-century European descriptions of a pygmy group in the region of the upper Rio Grande. His observations were cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in Section 3 of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* as part of the growing corpus of modern evidence for diminutive African populations.

## The Pygmy Group at Tenda-Maié

During his travels in what is now Guinea-Bissau and the Senegambia region, Mollieu encountered and documented a population living near Tenda-Maié, close to the Rio Grande. Tyson, surveying the various West African pygmy groups reported by nineteenth-century travelers, records:

> Another group was described by Mollieu in 1818 as inhabiting Tenda-Maié, near the Rio Grande, but very little is known about them. (003-111–003-112)

Tyson's caveat — "very little is known about them" — is characteristic of his scholarly caution. He includes Mollieu's report because it adds geographic breadth to the evidence for pygmy populations in West Africa, even while acknowledging the limitations of the data. The group at Tenda-Maié joined a growing catalogue of diminutive peoples reported from the region, including the Babonko described by [[battell|Andrew Battell]], the Obongo documented by [[du-chaillu|Paul Du Chaillu]], and various others mentioned by later travelers.

## Significance

Mollieu's brief mention in Tyson's essay exemplifies a broader historiographic pattern: the accumulation of fragmentary reports that, taken individually, were often thin in detail, but collectively established the reality of pygmy or pygmoid populations across a wide belt of equatorial Africa. His 1818 observation predated by several decades the more systematic accounts produced by [[stanley|Stanley]] and [[schweinfurth|Schweinfurth]] in the later nineteenth century, making him part of the earliest wave of modern European documentation of African pygmy peoples.
