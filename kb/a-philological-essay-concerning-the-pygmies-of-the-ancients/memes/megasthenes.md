Megasthenes (c. 350–290 BC) was a Greek ethnographer and diplomat who served as ambassador to the court of Chandragupta Maurya in India. His work *Indica*, now lost but extensively quoted by later writers, was the most comprehensive Greek account of India and its peoples. Tyson cites Megasthenes three times in his essay, always through the filter of later authorities — principally Pliny and Strabo.

## Megasthenes on Indian Pygmies

Tyson records that "Pliny out of Megasthenes, and Strabo from Onesicritus" provide an account of Pygmies in India (010-169). This makes Megasthenes a key link in the chain of Greek ethnographic writing about eastern Pygmies. Pliny's version, drawing on Megasthenes, describes the Pygmies mounted on rams and goats for their seasonal expeditions against the cranes — an account that Tyson treats as one of the more plausible renditions, noting that Megasthenes "methinks in Pliny mounts the Pygmies for this expedition much better, for he sets them not on a Pegasus or Partridges, but on Rams and He-Goats" (011-039).

## Reliability Assessment

Tyson acknowledges that Megasthenes, like other writers on India, was not always reliable. Strabo judged that "those who have wrote of India for the most part, are fabulous, but in the highest degree Daimachus; then Megasthenes, Onesicritus, and Nearchus" (011-073). Tyson's inclusion of this critical assessment shows his awareness that Megasthenes's credibility was contested even in antiquity. Nevertheless, Megasthenes's account — filtered through Pliny — remained important enough to be weighed seriously in Tyson's comprehensive survey of ancient testimony on the Pygmies.
