David MacRitchie (1851–1925) was a Scottish folklorist, antiquarian, and founding member of the Gypsy Lore Society. He is the theorist most extensively engaged with in Tyson's essay, his work appearing across six sections (005–010). MacRitchie's central thesis — that the "little folk" of European folklore represent folk memories of an actual prehistoric race of small-statured mound-dwellers — serves as one of the principal modern frameworks that Tyson evaluates against the ancient pygmy accounts.

## MacRitchie's Theory

MacRitchie's theory, as summarized in Tyson's essay, holds that "the elves, trolls, and fairies represented in popular tradition are really the mound-dwellers, whose remains have been discovered in some abundance in the form of green hillocks, which have been artificially raised over a long and low passage leading to a central chamber open to the sky" (008-054). He identifies these mound-dwelling peoples with the Picts of Scottish history — noting that fairies are called "Pechs" in Scotland — and with other early races (008-054).

Tyson traces the lineage of this theory to an earlier paper by J.F. Campbell, who observed that Scottish Highland traditions about fairies "closely resemble the outside of Lapp huts" and that the fairies of tradition "appear as small cunning people, eating and drinking, living close at hand in their green mound" (008-039). MacRitchie developed this observation into a systematic theory linking folklore to archaeological remains.

## Engagement with Irish and Scottish Sites

Throughout Section 5, Tyson tests MacRitchie's theory against specific archaeological sites. At Inverness, Tyson notes that MacRitchie "seems to think that an investigation" of certain mounds would reveal subterranean dwellings (005-149). At the Brugh of the Boyne (Newgrange), MacRitchie "seems to regard it as having been a dwelling-place," an interpretation Tyson weighs against the competing view put forward by George Coffey (005-167). Tyson also discusses the stone basins at these sites, "whose nature Mr. MacRitchie regards as doubtful" (005-171).

## Tyson's Critical Assessment

Tyson engages substantively with MacRitchie's theory across multiple sections. In Section 8, he states that "it is permitted to me to inquire how far Mr. MacRitchie's views tally with the evidence which lies before me" (008-097). He finds that while "many of these habitations fall into line with Mr. MacRitchie's theory" (008-150), significant problems remain.

In Section 9, Tyson enumerates his objections, concluding that he is "unable to accept Mr. MacRitchie's" identification of the mound-dwellers with the pygmies of ancient tradition (009-065). Nevertheless, he grants that "Mr. MacRitchie has" made a valid contribution by demonstrating the widespread distribution of mound-dwelling traditions (009-073).

In Section 10, Tyson qualifies MacRitchie's influence, observing that certain archaeological features may not be "so important as Mr. MacRitchie would have us believe" (010-022). Despite his reservations, MacRitchie's work provides the most thoroughly examined modern parallel to the ancient pygmy accounts in the essay.

## Key Works Cited

- **_The Testimony of Tradition_ (1890)** — MacRitchie's principal work, arguing for the historical reality behind fairy folklore, extensively cited by Windle throughout the essay
- **Various papers** — Tyson references "another paper" in which MacRitchie "collected a number of instances" of mound-dwelling traditions from diverse countries (008-087)
