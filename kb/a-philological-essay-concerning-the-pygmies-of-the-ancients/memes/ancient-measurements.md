## Overview

Ancient Measurements encompasses the units of length — cubits, spans, digits, and inches — that classical authors used to describe pygmy stature. The seemingly dry question of ancient metrology is in fact central to the pygmy debate, because the quantitative claims about pygmy height (three spans, two cubits, one cubit and a half) cannot be evaluated without knowing what these units meant to their original users. A "three-span" pygmy is either impossibly small or perfectly plausible depending on whether a span is measured from thumb to little finger (about 9 inches, yielding a pygmy 27 inches tall — improbable) or from thumb to index finger (about 6 inches, yielding a pygmy 18 inches tall — still unlikely but less absurd). Tyson devotes sustained attention to these metrological questions because the entire quantitative case for pygmy races rests on them.

## Ancient Sources

The principal ancient metrological data come from Pliny and Ctesias. Pliny reports that Indian pygmies "do not exceed the height of two cubits, and the most part only of one cubit and a half" (004-070, 004-071). Ctesias gives the more famous measurement of three spans (*trispithameis*). A cubit (*cubitus* in Latin, *pechys* in Greek) is the distance from elbow to fingertip, standardized in the Roman period at approximately 18 inches (one and a half Roman feet); two cubits would thus be about 36 inches (three feet), and one cubit and a half about 27 inches. These figures are at the extreme lower bound of human stature — plausible for a pathological dwarf but extraordinary as a population average.

The span (*spithame* in Greek) is the distance from thumb-tip to little-finger-tip with the hand fully extended, approximately 9 inches. Three spans would be 27 inches — consistent with Pliny's lower estimate of one cubit and a half. The digit (*dactylos*) is a finger-breadth, approximately 0.75 inches, sixteen of which make a foot. These small units matter because ancient descriptions sometimes specify pygmy stature to the digit, implying a precision of measurement that the sources almost certainly do not possess.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson approaches the metrological question with his characteristic blend of philological precision and empirical scepticism. He recognizes that "Readers will mistake his meaning" if they take ancient measurements at face value without considering the variability of ancient units — a cubit might be anywhere from 17 to 21 inches depending on the standard in use (011-020). The philological dimension is crucial: "therefore be mistaken in rendering" a Greek measurement into English without specifying which standard is being used (011-056).

The substantive question is whether the reported measurements are compatible with known human stature ranges. Tyson notes that even the most generous interpretation of the ancient figures — using the shortest possible cubit — yields pygmy heights well below the range of documented human populations. The two-cubit pygmy at 34–36 inches is shorter than any recorded adult human population; the one-and-a-half-cubit pygmy at 25–27 inches is at the extreme margin of dwarfism. These figures, Tyson concludes, cannot represent population averages but might represent either extreme individuals or — more likely — rhetorical exaggeration.

The dwarf-race tradition provides a partial check: "been built by a dwarf race, a cubit high, who" constructed the megalithic monuments of Europe (007-006). Here the measurement is explicitly fabulous — no human population one cubit (18 inches) tall has ever existed — and Tyson uses this example to illustrate the rhetorical function of small measurements in ancient ethnography. To say that a people is "a cubit high" is not to provide a measurement but to assert their extreme diminutiveness in the strongest available terms.

## Modern Perspective

The metrological question that Tyson investigated has been largely settled by modern anthropometry. The shortest documented human populations — Central African pygmies and Southeast Asian Negritos — average approximately four feet six inches to five feet in adult male stature, well above the ancient figures even on the most generous interpretation. The ancient measurements are now understood as rhetorical tropes rather than empirical data: "three spans" and "two cubits" are ways of saying "very small" in a literary culture that valued quantitative precision as a marker of authority. Nevertheless, Tyson's metrological analysis remains valuable as a case study in how philological precision can expose the gap between ancient rhetoric and empirical reality.
