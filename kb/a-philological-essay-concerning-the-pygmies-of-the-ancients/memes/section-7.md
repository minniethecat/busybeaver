# Section 7

[Footnote A: Keightley, 440.]

[007-001]In [[india|India]] megalithic remains are also associated with little people.

[007-002]"[[dwarfs|Dwarfs]] hold a distinct place in Hindu mythology; they appear sculptured

[007-003]on all temples. Siva is accompanied by a body-guard of dwarfs, one of

[007-004]whom, the three-legged Bhringi, dances nimbly. But coming nearer to

[007-005]Northern legend, the cromlechs and kistvaens which abound over Southern

[007-006]India are believed to have been built by a dwarf race, a cubit high, who

[007-007]could, nevertheless, move and handle the huge stones easily. The villagers

[007-008]call them Pandayar."[A]

 
[Footnote A: _Folk Lore_, iv. 401.]

[007-009]Mr. Meadows Taylor, speaking of cromlechs in India, says, "Wherever I

[007-010]found them, the same tradition was attached to them, that they were Morie

[007-011]humu, or Mories' houses; these Mories having been dwarfs who inhabited the

[007-012]country before the present race of men." Again, speaking of the cromlechs

[007-013]of Koodilghee, he states, "Tradition says that former Governments caused

[007-014]dwellings of the description alluded to to be erected for a species of

[007-015]human beings called 'Mohories,' whose dwarfish stature is said not to have

[007-016]exceeded a span when standing, and a fist high when in a sitting posture,

[007-017]who were endowed with strength sufficient to roll off large stones with a

[007-018]touch of their thumb." There are, he also tells us, similar traditions

[007-019]attaching to other places, where the dwarfs are sometimes spoken of as

[007-020]Gujaries.[A]

 
[Footnote A: _Jour. Ethnol. Soc_., 1868-69, p. 157.]

[007-021]Of stone structures built by fairies or little people for the use of

[007-022]others, may be mentioned the churches built by dwarfs in Scotland and

[007-023]Brittany, and described by Mr. MacRitchie, as also the two following

[007-024]instances, taken from widely distant parts of the globe. In Brittany, the

[007-025]dolmen of Manné-er Hrock (Montaigne de la Fee), at Locmariaquer, is said

[007-026]to have been built by a fairy, in order that a mother might stand upon it

[007-027]and look out for her son's ship.[A] In Fiji the following tale is told

[007-028]about the Nanga or sacred stone enclosure:--"This is the word of our

[007-029]fathers concerning the Nanga. Long ago their fathers were ignorant of it;

[007-030]but one day two strangers were found sitting in the Rara (public square),

[007-031]and they said they had come up from the sea to give them the Nanga. They

[007-032]were little men, and very dark-skinned, and one of them had his face and

[007-033]bust painted red, while the other was painted black. Whether these were

[007-034]gods or men our fathers did not tell us, but it was they who taught our

[007-035]people the Nanga. This was in the old times, when our fathers were living

[007-036]in another land--not in this place, for we are strangers here."[B] It is

[007-037]worthy of note that the term "Nanga" applies not merely to the enclosure,

[007-038]but also to the secret society which held its meetings therein.[C]

 
[Footnote A: _Flint Chips_, p. 104.]

 
[Footnote B: Fison, _Journ. Anthrop. Inst._, xiv, 14.]

 
[Footnote C: Joske, _Internat. Arch. f. Ethnographie_, viii. 254.]

[007-039]5. The little people make their dwellings either in the interior of a

[007-040]stone or amongst stones. I am not here alluding to the stones on the sides

[007-041]of mountains which are the doorways to fairy dwellings, but to a closer

[007-042]connection, which will be better understood from some of the following

[007-043]instances than from any lengthy explanation. The Duergas of the

[007-044]Scandinavian Eddas had their dwelling-places in stones, as we are told in

[007-045]the story of Thorston, who "came one day to an open part of the wood,

[007-046]where he saw a great rock, and out a little way from it a dwarf, who was

[007-047]horridly ugly."[A] In Ireland, in Innisbofin, co. Galway, Professor Haddon

[007-048]relates that the men who were quarrying a rock in the neighbourhood of the

[007-049]harbour refused to work at it any longer, as it was so full of "good

[007-050]people" as to be hot.[B] In England the Pixy-house of Devon is in a stone,

[007-051]and a large stone is also connected with the story of the Frensham

[007-052]caldron, though it is not clear that the fairies lived in the rock

[007-053]itself.[C] Oseberrow or Osebury (_vulgo_ Rosebury) Rock, in Lulsey,

[007-054]Worcestershire, was, according to tradition, a favourite haunt of the

[007-055]fairies.[D] In another part of Worcestershire, on the side of the

[007-056]Cotswolds, there is, in a little spinney, a large flat stone, much worn on

[007-057]its under surface, which is called the White Lady's Table. This personage

[007-058]is supposed to take her meals with the fairies at this rock, but what the

[007-059]exact relation of the little people to it as a dwelling-place may be, I

[007-060]have not been able to learn.

 
[Footnote A: Keightley, 70.]

 
[Footnote B: _Folklore_, iv. 49.]

 
[Footnote C: Ritson, 106, quoting Aubrey's _Natural History of Surrey_,

[007-061]iii. 366.]

 
[Footnote D: Allies, _Antiquities and Folk-Lore of Worcestershire_,

[007-062]p.443.]

[007-063]There is an Iroquois tale of dwarfs, in which the summons to the Pigmies

[007-064]was given by knocking upon a large stone.[A] The little people of

[007-065]Melanesia seem also to be associated in some measure with stones. Speaking

[007-066]of these beings, Mr. Codrington says,[B] "There are certain Vuis having

[007-067]rather the nature of fairies. The accounts of them are vague, but it is

[007-068]argued that they had never left the islands before the introduction of

[007-069]Christianity, and indeed have been seen since. Not long ago there was a

[007-070]woman living at Mota who was the child of one, and a very few years ago a

[007-071]female Vui with a child was seen in Saddle Island. Some of these were

[007-072]called Nopitu, which come invisibly, or possess those with whom they

[007-073]associate themselves. The possessed are called Nopitu. Such persons would

[007-074]lift a cocoa-nut to drink, and native shell money would run out instead of

[007-075]the juice and rattle against their teeth; they would vomit up money, or

[007-076]scratch and shake themselves on a mat, when money would pour from their

[007-077]fingers. This was often seen, and believed to be the doing of a Nopitu. In

[007-078]another manner of manifestation, a Nopitu would make himself known as a

[007-079]party were sitting round an evening fire. A man would hear a voice in his

[007-080]thigh, 'Here am I, give me food.' He would roast a little red yam, and

[007-081]fold it in the corner of his mat. He would soon find it gone, and the

[007-082]Nopitu would begin a song. Its voice was so small and clear and sweet,

[007-083]that once heard it never could be forgotten; but it sang the ordinary Mota

[007-084]songs. Such spirits as these, if seen or found, would disappear beside a

[007-085]stone; they were smaller than the native people, darker, and with long

[007-086]straight hair. But they were mostly unseen, or seen only by those to whom

[007-087]they took a fancy. They were the friendly Trolls or Robin Goodfellows of

[007-088]the islands; a man would find a fine red yam put for him on the seat

[007-089]beside the door, or the money which he paid away returned within his

[007-090]purse. A woman working in her garden heard a voice from the fruit of a

[007-091]gourd asking for some food, and when she pulled up an arum or dug out a

[007-092]yam, another still remained; but when she listened to another spirit's

[007-093]panpipes, the first in his jealousy conveyed away garden and all." Amongst

[007-094]the Australians also supernatural beings dwell amongst the rocks, and the

[007-095]Annamites and Arabians know of fairies living amongst the rocks and

[007-096]hills.[C]

 
[Footnote A: Smith, _Myths of Iroquois, ut supra._]

 
[Footnote B: _Journ. Anthrop. Inst._, x. 261.]

 
[Footnote C: Hartland, _Science of Fairy Tales_, p. 351.]

[007-097]6. The little people may have their habitation in forests or trees. Such

[007-098]were the Skovtrolde, or Wood-Trolls of Thorlacius,[A] who made their home

[007-099]on the earth in great thick woods, and the beings in South Germany who

[007-100]resemble the dwarfs, and are called Wild, Wood, Timber and Moss People.[B]

[007-101]"These generally live together in society, but they sometimes appear

[007-102]singly. They are small in stature, yet somewhat larger than the Elf, being

[007-103]the size of children of three years, grey and old-looking, hairy and clad

[007-104]in moss. Their lives are attached, like those of the Hamadryads, to the

[007-105]trees, and if any one causes by friction the inner bark to loosen, a

[007-106]Wood-woman dies." In Scandinavia there is also a similarity between

[007-107]certain of the Elves and Hamadryads. The Elves "not only frequent trees,

[007-108]but they make an interchange of form with them. In the churchyard of Store

[007-109]Heddinge, in Zeeland, there are the remains of an oak-wood. These, say the

[007-110]common people, are the Elle King's soldiers; by day they are trees, by

[007-111]night valiant soldiers. In the wood of Rugaard, in the same island, is a

[007-112]tree which by night becomes a whole Elle-people, and goes about all alive.

[007-113]It has no leaves upon it, yet it would be very unsafe to go to break or

[007-114]fell it, for the underground people frequently hold their meetings under

[007-115]its branches. There is, in another place, an elder-tree growing in a

[007-116]farmyard, which frequently takes a walk in the twilight about the yard,

[007-117]and peeps in through the window at the children when they are alone. The

[007-118]linden or lime-tree is the favourite haunt of the Elves and cognate

[007-119]beings, and it is not safe to be near it after sunset."[C] In England, the

[007-120]fairies also in some cases frequent the woods, as is their custom in the

[007-121]Isle of Man, and in Wales, where there was formerly, in the park of Sir

[007-122]Robert Vaughan, a celebrated old oak-tree, named Crwben-yr-Ellyl, or the

[007-123]Elf's Hollow Tree. In Formosa[D] there is also a tale of little people

[007-124]inhabiting a wood. "A young Botan became too ardent in his devotion to a

[007-125]young lady of the tribe, and was slain by her relatives, while, as a

[007-126]warning as to the necessity for love's fervour being kept within bounds,

[007-127]his seven brothers were banished by the chief. The exiles went forth into

[007-128]the depths of the forest, and in their wanderings after a new land they

[007-129]crossed a small clearing, in which a little girl, about a span in height,

[007-130]was seated peeling potatoes. 'Little sister,' they queried, 'how come you

[007-131]here? where is your home?' 'I am not of homes nor parents,' she replied.

[007-132]Leaving her, they went still farther into the forest, and had not gone far

[007-133]when they saw a little man cutting canes, and farther on to the right a

[007-134]curious-looking house, in front of which sat two diminutive women combing

[007-135]their hair. Things looked so queer that the travellers hesitated about

[007-136]approaching nearer, but, eager to find a way out of the forest, they

[007-137]determined in their extremity to question the strange people. The two

[007-138]women, when interrogated, turned sharply round, showing eyes of a flashing

[007-139]red; then looking upward, their eyes became dull and white, and they

[007-140]immediately ran into the house, the doors and windows of which at once

[007-141]vanished, the whole taking the form and appearance of an isolated

[007-142]boulder." Amongst the Maories also we have "te tini ote hakuturi," or "the

[007-143]multitude of the wood-elves," the little people who put the chips all back

[007-144]into the tree Rata had felled and stood it up again, because he had not

[007-145]paid tribute to Tane.[E]

 
[Footnote A: Quoted by Keightley, p. 62.]

 
[Footnote B: Grimm ap. Keightley, p. 230.]

 
[Footnote C: Keightley, p. 92, quoting from Thiele.]

 
[Footnote D: _Folk Lore Journal_, v. 143.]

 
[Footnote E: Tregear, _Journ. Anth. Inst._, xix. 121.]

[007-146]7. The association of little people with water as a home is a widespread

[007-147]notion. The Sea-Trows of the Shetlanders inhabit a region of their own at

[007-148]the bottom of the sea. They here respire a peculiar atmosphere, and live

[007-149]in habitations constructed of the choicest submarine productions. They

[007-150]are, however, not always small, but may be of diverse statures, like the

[007-151]Scandinavian Necks. In Germany the Water-Dwarfs are also known. At

[007-152]Seewenheiher, in the Black Forest, a little water-man (_Seemännlein_) used

[007-153]to come and join the people, work the whole day along with them, and in

[007-154]the evening go back into the lakes.[A] The size of the Breton Korrigs or

[007-155]Korrigan, if we may believe Villemarqué in his account of this folk, does

[007-156]not exceed two feet, but their proportions are most exact, and they have

[007-157]long flowing hair, which they comb out with great care. Their only dress

[007-158]is a long white veil, which they wind round their body. Seen at night or

[007-159]in the dusk of the evening, their beauty is great; but in the daylight

[007-160]their eyes appear red, their hair is white, and their faces wrinkled;

[007-161]hence they rarely let themselves be seen by day. They are fond of music,

[007-162]and have fine voices, but are not much given to dancing. Their favourite

[007-163]haunts are the springs, by which they sit and comb their hair.[B] The

[007-164]Maories also have their Water-Pigmies, the Ponaturi, who are, according to

[007-165]Mr. Tregear, elves, little tiny people, mostly dwellers in water, coming

[007-166]ashore to sleep.[C] "The spirits most commonly met with in African

[007-167]mythology," says Mr. Macdonald, "are water or river spirits, inhabiting

[007-168]deep pools where there are strong eddies and under-currents. Whether they

[007-169]are all even seen now-a-days it is difficult to determine, but they must

[007-170]at one time have either shown themselves willingly, or been dragged from

[007-171]their hiding-places by some powerful magician, for they are one and all

[007-172]described. They are dwarfs, and correspond to the Scottish conception of

[007-173]kelpies or fairies. They are wicked and malevolent beings, and are never

[007-174]credited with a good or generous action. Whatever they possess they keep,

[007-175]and greedily seize upon any one who comes within their reach. 'One of

[007-176]them, the Incanti, corresponds to the Greek Python, and another, called

[007-177]Hiti, appears in the form of a small and very ugly man, and is exceedingly

[007-178]malevolent' (Brownlee). It is certain death to see an Incanti, and no one

[007-179]but the magicians sees them except in dreams, and in that case the

[007-180]magicians are consulted, and advise and direct what is to be done."[D]
