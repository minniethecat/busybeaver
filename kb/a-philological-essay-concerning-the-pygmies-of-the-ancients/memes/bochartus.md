Samuel Bochart (1599–1667), known in Latin as Samuel Bochartus, was a French Protestant scholar whose expertise spanned biblical exegesis, geography, philology, and natural history. His major works — *Hierozoicon* (*On the Animals of Scripture*) and *Geographia Sacra* (*Sacred Geography*) — are among the most frequently cited modern authorities in Tyson's essay. Bochart is Tyson's primary source for the theory identifying the ancient Pygmies with the Nubæ people of East Africa.

## Bochart's Nubæ Theory

The central argument that Tyson examines from Bochart is the identification of the Pygmies with the Nubæ (or Nobæ). Tyson summarizes: "I therefore next enquire into Bochartus's Opinion, who would have the Nubæ here to be the ancient Pygmies" (016-058). Bochart located the Nubæ near the sources of the Nile, drawing on both classical sources and Arabic geographical traditions.

Tyson records Bochart's linguistic evidence with care: "Arabes idem (saith Bochartus) referunt ex cujusdam Græci" (016-109), and provides an extensive footnote citing "*Bocharti Hierozoici pars Posterior*" (016-103). The philological dimension is key — Bochart attempts to connect the name *Nubæ* with the Greek and Latin traditions about Pygmies through comparative etymology.

## Tyson's Critical Assessment

While Tyson treats Bochart with considerable respect — calling him "the Learned Bochartus" (017-155) and "the same learned Bochartus" (016-107) — he does not accept the Nubæ identification uncritically. Tyson questions whether Bochart's translation is accurate: "Bochart's Translation, as well as Gesner's" (016-095). He faults Bochart for not considering a specific grammatical point about Aristotle's text: "which had Bochartus considered he would not have" drawn the conclusions he did (016-097).

Tyson's treatment of Bochart exemplifies his balanced approach: he engages deeply with Bochart's evidence, acknowledges his erudition, but ultimately applies independent philological judgment. "Bochartus tho' he gives Aristotle's Opinion" (013-110) — the concessive "tho'" signals Tyson's characteristic mode of respectful disagreement.

## The Nubæ-Pygmy Equation

The Bochartian thesis that the Nubæ were the historical Pygmies is a key thread running through Sections 16 and 17 of Tyson's essay. Bochartus's argument draws on multiple lines of evidence: geographical proximity to the Nile sources, Arabic testimonia, and the dwarf-like stature attributed to the Nubæ in various accounts. Tyson notes that Bochartus "esteems the Pygmies" to have been real (017-155), placing him in the camp of those who accepted the essential historicity of the ancient accounts while seeking to identify them with a known African people.
