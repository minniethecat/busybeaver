## Biography

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913) was a British naturalist, biologist, and
anthropologist best known for independently conceiving the theory of evolution
through natural selection alongside Charles Darwin. Wallace's extensive travels
in the Malay Archipelago (1854–1862) yielded not only the zoological and
botanical discoveries that underpinned his evolutionary thinking, but also
detailed observations of the indigenous peoples of Southeast Asia and Oceania.

Wallace's anthropological interests extended to the physical characteristics
of the various ethnic groups he encountered, particularly the Negrito
populations scattered across the islands of Southeast Asia. His measurements
and descriptions of these peoples became important primary data for
nineteenth-century physical anthropology and the study of human variation.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson invokes Wallace's observations in Section 2 of the essay as part of a
wider survey of the Negrito peoples of the Malay Archipelago and their
relationship to the pygmy question. Following the Negrito populations
eastward across the islands, Tyson notes that in Luzon the Aetas (also known
as Inagtas) live alongside the Tagals of Malay origin and were called
"Negritos del Monte" by the Spanish colonizers (002-025–002-028).

Wallace's key contribution is his anthropometric data: according to his
measurements, the average stature of these Negrito peoples "ranges from
four feet six inches to four feet eight inches" (002-029). This places the
Aetas and related groups firmly within the pygmy stature range as defined
by Tyson's essay. The discussion continues with reference to similar races
in New Guinea — the Karons, who occupy a chain of mountains parallel to
the north coast of the great north-western peninsula (002-030–002-031).

Wallace's stature data is particularly valuable because it comes from a
rigorous scientific observer whose methods of measurement and recording
were among the most reliable of the nineteenth century. His figures of
four feet six to eight inches align closely with other accounts of pygmy
peoples from Africa and Asia, strengthening the comparative framework of
Tyson's argument.

## Key Works Cited by Tyson

- **The Malay Archipelago** (1869) — Wallace's account of his eight-year
  expedition through the Malay Archipelago, containing his observations of
  the Negrito peoples and their physical characteristics. The stature data
  cited by Tyson derives from this work (002-029).
