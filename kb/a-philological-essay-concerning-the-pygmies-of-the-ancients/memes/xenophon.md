Xenophon of Athens (c. 430–354 BC) was a Greek historian, soldier, and philosopher. A student of Socrates, he is best known for the *Anabasis*, his account of the march of the Ten Thousand, and the *Hellenica*, a history of Greece from 411 to 362 BC. His writings span history, philosophy, and practical treatises on horsemanship and estate management.

## Role in Tyson's Essay

Tyson invokes Xenophon as a chronological reference point in Section 12, where he evaluates the testimony of Ctesias of Cnidus regarding the Pygmies. Ctesias was a physician to the Persian king Artaxerxes Mnemon, and his account of a diminutive race in India is one of the earliest Greek prose treatments of the pygmy subject:

> "Ctesias was Physician to Artaxerxes Mnemon as Diodorus Siculus and Strabo inform us. He was contemporary with Xenophon, a little later than Herodotus."
> (012-001, 012-002)

Tyson uses Xenophon's well-established dates to fix Ctesias in the late fifth to early fourth century BC. This chronological anchor is important for Tyson's historical argument: it establishes that the pygmy account was current in Greek literature before Aristotle and is not a late invention (012-003).

Helvicus, Tyson notes, places Ctesias "three hundred eighty three years before Christ," further confirming the antiquity of the Ctesian account (012-004). This dating matters because it makes Ctesias one of the earliest prose authorities on the Pygmies, predating even Aristotle's treatment of the subject.

## Tyson's Assessment of Ctesias

Tyson acknowledges Ctesias's antiquity but remains cautious about his reliability. Xenophon's importance here is as a chronological benchmark — his well-documented career provides a fixed point around which the less certain dates of Ctesias can be anchored:

> "He is an ancient Author, 'tis true, and it may be upon that score valued by some. We are beholden to him, not only for his Improvements on the Story of the Pygmies, but for his Remarks likewise on several other parts of Natural History."
> (012-005, 012-006)

Tyson's ambivalent assessment of Ctesias — ancient but not always reliable — reflects the broader historiographical challenge facing any scholar who attempts to disentangle authentic observation from marvel-mongering in classical accounts of distant peoples.

## Relation to Other Classical Sources

Although Xenophon did not contribute directly to the pygmy discourse, his historical writings provide valuable context for the intellectual world in which the pygmy accounts circulated.

As a contemporary of both Ctesias and the later years of Herodotus, Xenophon occupies a pivotal position in the transition between the Herodotean tradition of ethnography and the more systematic historical writing of the fourth century BC.

His silence on the pygmy subject is itself noteworthy — it suggests that the pygmy legend was not universally discussed among Greek prose writers of the classical period.

## Key Works

- **Anabasis** — Account of the Greek mercenary expedition to Persia and the march of the Ten Thousand
- **Hellenica** — History of Greece from 411 to 362 BC, continuing Thucydides' unfinished work
- **Cyropaedia** — A fictionalized biography of Cyrus the Great of Persia
- **Memorabilia** — Recollections of Socrates and his teachings
