# Edward Tyson

## Biography

Edward Tyson (1651 – 1 August 1708) was an English physician and anatomist. He was admitted as a candidate of the Royal College of Physicians and practiced in London. (001-010) He died on 1 August 1708. (001-019)

Tyson was born, according to some accounts, at Bristol, and was educated at Oxford. He became a fellow of the Royal Society and was known for his anatomical studies, particularly his work on the anatomy of the chimpanzee, which he described in his 1699 work _Orang-Outang, sive Homo Sylvestris_.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson is the author of _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699), a systematic examination of ancient Greek and Roman accounts of pygmy-like peoples. His thesis was that the "Pygmies" described by ancient authors were not a race of miniature humans but were actually apes—specifically, the chimpanzee. (001-002)

The full title page records the work as authored by _Edvardo Tyson_, M.D., Fellow of the Royal Society. (001-041) Tyson's granddaughter later married into a medical family connected to South Kensington. (001-045)

## Key Works Cited by Tyson

The _Philological Essay_ draws extensively on classical sources, including Homer, Aristotle, Herodotus, Ctesias, Strabo, Pliny the Elder, and numerous other ancient and medieval authors. Tyson's methodology combines philological analysis of ancient texts with anatomical knowledge derived from his own dissection work.
