## Biography

Paul Belloni Du Chaillu (c. 1835–1903) was a French-American explorer and anthropologist
known for his travels in Gabon and Central Africa during the mid-nineteenth century.
His observations of the indigenous peoples of West Africa provided nineteenth-century
anthropologists with some of the earliest modern accounts of African populations of
unusually short stature.

Du Chaillu's expeditions took him into the interior regions between the Gaboon (Gabon)
River and the Congo River, where he documented the physical characteristics of various
tribal groups. His work was part of the broader nineteenth-century European exploration
of Africa that brought new empirical data to longstanding debates about the existence
and nature of pygmy peoples.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson cites Du Chaillu's observations as modern evidence for the existence of
peoples of diminutive stature in Africa. In the region of Ashangoland, situated
between the Gaboon and the Congo, Du Chaillu described a male subject who measured
only four feet six inches in height (003-087). This account is part of a broader
discussion in Section 3 of the essay, where Tyson surveys reports of African
peoples of short stature, including the Bakke-Bakke and the Babonko of Loango
(003-088).

Du Chaillu's testimony belongs to the modern (nineteenth-century) strand of
evidence in Tyson's essay, complementing the ancient and medieval sources.
Tyson groups Du Chaillu's account with other modern observers who reported
on the physical anthropology of African populations, reinforcing the argument
that pygmy-sized peoples were not merely legendary but continued to exist
into the modern era (003-083–003-087).
