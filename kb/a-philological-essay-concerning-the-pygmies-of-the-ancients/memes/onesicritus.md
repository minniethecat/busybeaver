Onesicritus (c. 360–290 BC) was a Greek historian and navigator who accompanied Alexander the Great on his Indian campaign. His lost work on India was among the primary Greek sources on the geography and peoples of the East. Tyson encounters Onesicritus alongside [[megasthenes|Megasthenes]] as a key Greek witness to Indian Pygmies, transmitted through the filter of later authorities — principally [[strabo|Strabo]] and [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny the Elder]].

## Onesicritus on Indian Pygmies

Tyson first mentions Onesicritus in Section 10, noting that "Pliny out of Megasthenes, and Strabo from Onesicritus" provide the principal Greek accounts of Pygmies in India (010-169). Onesicritus's version, as preserved by Strabo, described Pygmies as a people who waged war against cranes — the same legendary conflict that appears in the Homeric tradition, but relocated to the Indian subcontinent.

The most striking detail from Onesicritus concerns the Pygmies' stature. Strabo reports Onesicritus's account of Pygmies "three spans high" ([Greek: Pros de tous trispithamous]) (010-175). This measurement — approximately 27 inches or 69 cm — became a standard datum in classical discussions of Pygmy height. Tyson, for his part, was "more readily agree to" Onesicritus's account through Strabo (011-035), finding it more plausible than some of the more extravagant Greek descriptions of Indian marvels.

## Reliability and Classical Reception

Tyson acknowledges the critical tradition surrounding Onesicritus. Through Strabo, he reports that the Indian historians were ranked by reliability, with "Daimachus" judged the most fabulous, "then Megasthenes, Onesicritus, and Nearchus" following in descending order of credibility (011-073). Strabo judged that "those who have wrote of India for the most part, are fabulous" — a sweeping dismissal that Tyson records but does not fully endorse, since he finds in Onesicritus's account a kernel of ethnographic truth.

Additional classical references place Onesicritus in the broader tradition of writers on Pygmies. Tyson lists him among the authorities who described diminutive peoples: "Proconnesius, & Isagonus, & Nicæensis, & Ctesias, & Onesicritus" (012-037). This catalogue positions Onesicritus within a continuous chain of Greek ethnographic writing that Tyson methodically surveys.

## Geographical Significance

Onesicritus in Strabo tells us "that a Crane has been seen to fall from a mountain" (011-043) — a passage that Tyson interprets as evidence for the physical setting of the Pygmy-crane conflict. The Indian location, transmitted through Onesicritus and paralleled in Megasthenes's account, represented an important alternative to the African setting championed by other authorities.
