# Rochon

Alexis-Marie de Rochon (1741–1817), commonly known as the **Abbé Rochon**, was a French astronomer, physicist, and traveler. Best known for his optical inventions, Rochon also undertook voyages to the Indian Ocean region, during which he documented the peoples of Madagascar. His description of a dwarf population in the island's central highlands was cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] as part of the accumulating evidence for the wide geographic distribution of diminutive human races.

## The Kunios of Ankova

Tyson introduces Rochon's observations in Section 3 of his essay during a survey of modern reports of pygmy populations. Drawing on earlier scholarship, Tyson summarizes the traditional account of Madagascar's first inhabitants and Rochon's description of them:

> Oliver states that "the Vazimbas are supposed to have been the first occupants of Ankova. They are described by Rochon, under the name of Kunios, as a nation of dwarfs averaging three feet six inches in stature, of a lighter colour than the Negroes, with very long arms and woolly hair." (003-124–003-127)

The reported stature of three feet six inches (approximately 107 cm) placed the Kunios firmly within the pygmy range and made them among the shortest populations documented in the ethnographic literature available to Tyson. The physical description — lighter skin, long arms, and woolly hair — aligned with other nineteenth-century descriptions of African pygmy groups, including those described by [[du-chaillu|Paul Du Chaillu]] in West Africa.

## Context and Credibility

Rochon was primarily a scientist rather than an adventurer, which lent credibility to his ethnographic observations. His training as an astronomer gave his geographical descriptions unusual precision for the period. Tyson, who was careful to distinguish reliable observers from credulous travelers, appears to have accepted Rochon's testimony at face value, incorporating it without the skepticism he reserved for less verifiable claims.

The Vazimba-Kunios identification reported by Rochon remained a subject of anthropological debate well into the twentieth century, with some scholars arguing that the aboriginal population of Madagascar included a distinct pygmy or pygmoid component, while others attributed the short stature to nutritional or environmental factors.
