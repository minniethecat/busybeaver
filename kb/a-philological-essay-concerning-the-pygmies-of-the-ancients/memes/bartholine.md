Caspar Bartholine (1585–1629) was a Danish physician and polymath whose *Opusculum de Pygmæis* (A Little Work on the Pygmies) stands as the most thorough early modern treatise devoted exclusively to the Pygmy question. Tyson subjects Bartholine's work to an exhaustive chapter-by-chapter review across Sections 14 and 15 of the essay, making Bartholine, along with [[vossius|Isaac Vossius]], one of Tyson's two most important modern predecessors in the debate.

## Bartholine's Defense of the Pygmies

Tyson introduces Bartholine early in his survey of modern authorities: "Old Caspar Bartholine tells us" of earlier writers on the subject (014-129). Bartholine compiled an impressive roster of classical and modern witnesses, including "Cassanius, Joh. Talentonius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela, and Olaus Magnus" (014-150). His method was cumulative — assembling every ancient and contemporary testimony to build a case for the reality of Pygmies.

Tyson reports Bartholine's core claim: *eos (sc. Pygmæos) in relatione tradit* — that Pygmies are attested by reliable tradition (014-153). In summarizing Bartholine's position, Tyson writes: *Tandem neque ideo Pygmæi sunt, si fortè sagittis &c.* — that they are Pygmies not merely because they use bows and arrows, but because of their distinctive diminutive stature (015-008).

## Tyson's Chapter-by-Chapter Review

Tyson structures his engagement with Bartholine as a systematic review of the *Opusculum*'s eight chapters. The "probable Reasons that Bartholine gives in the fifth Chapter" (015-025) receive careful attention. Bartholine's fifth chapter appears to have contained the core positive evidence, and Tyson notes how Bartholine "concludes this Chapter" with a transition to the next (015-036).

The sixth chapter bore the title *Pygmæos esse aut fuisse* — that Pygmies are or were real (015-048). Here Bartholine addressed objections, declaring *Autorum hic dissensus nullus est* — that there is no real disagreement among the authorities (015-052). Tyson also notes that Bartholine drew on the testimony of Adrian Spigelius regarding a passage about Pygmies (015-069).

The seventh chapter had a "promising Title" concerning whether Pygmies might be apes rather than men (015-090). The eighth and final chapter rounded out Bartholine's defense (015-115). Tyson's final verdict is measured: he asks "how far Bartholine in his Treatise has made out" the case (015-157), and notes that "Bartholine and others insist upon that" — the argument from written authorities (015-161).
