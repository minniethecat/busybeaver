# INTRODUCTION

[001-001]I.

[001-002][[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]], the author of the Essay with which this book is concerned,

[001-003]was, on the authority of Monk's Roll of the Royal College of Physicians,

[001-004]born, according to some accounts, at Bristol, according to others, at

[001-005]Clevedon, co. Somerset, but was descended from a family which had long

[001-006]settled in Cumberland. He was educated at Magdalene Hall, Oxford, as a

[001-007]member of which he proceeded Bachelor of Arts on the 8th of February 1670,

[001-008]and Master of Arts on the 4th of November 1673. His degree of Doctor of

[001-009]Medicine he took at Cambridge in 1678 as a member of Corpus Christi

[001-010]College. Dr. Tyson was admitted a candidate of the College of Physicians

[001-011]on the 30th of September 1680, and a Fellow in April 1683. He was Censor

[001-012]of the College in 1694, and held the appointments of Physician to the

[001-013]Hospitals of Bridewell and Bethlem, and of Anatomical Reader at Surgeons'

[001-014]Hall. He was a Fellow of the Royal Society, and contributed several papers

[001-015]to the "Philosophical Transactions." Besides a number of anatomical works,

[001-016]he published in 1699 "A Philosophical Essay concerning the Rhymes of the

[001-017]Ancients," and in the same year the work by which his name is still known,

[001-018]in which the Philological Essay which is here reprinted finds a place.

[001-019]Tyson died on the 1st of August 1708, in the fifty-eighth year of his age,

[001-020]and is buried at St. Dionis Backchurch. He was the original of the Carus

[001-021]not very flatteringly described in Garth's "Dispensary."

[001-022]The title-page of the work above alluded to runs as follows:--

[001-023]_[[apes|Orang-Outang]], sive Homo Sylvestris_:

[001-024]OR, THE ANATOMY OF A PYGMIE

[001-025]Compared with that of a _Monkey_, an _Ape_, and a _Man_.

[001-026]To which is added, A PHILOLOGICAL ESSAY Concerning the _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_, the

[001-027]_Cynocephali_, the _Satyrs_, and _Sphinges_ of the ANCIENTS.

[001-028]Wherein it will appear that they are all either _APES_ or _MONKEYS_, and

[001-029]not _MEN_, as formerly pretended.

[001-030]By _EDWARD TYSON_ M.D.

[001-031]Fellow of the Colledge of Physicians, and the Royal Society: Physician to

[001-032]the Hospital of _Bethlem_, and Reader of Anatomy at _Chirurgeons-Hall_.

[001-033]_LONDON_:

[001-034]Printed for _Thomas Bennet_ at the _Half-Moon in St. Paul's_ Church-yard;

[001-035]and _Daniel Brown_ at the _Black Swan_ and _Bible_ without _Temple-Bar_

[001-036]and are to be had of Mr. _Hunt_ at the _Repository_ in _Gresham-Colledge_.

[001-037]M DC XCIX.

[001-038]It bears the authority of the Royal Society:--

[001-039]17° _Die Maij_, 1699.

[001-040]Imprimatur Liber cui Titulus, _Orang-Outang, sive Homo Sylvestris,_ &c.

[001-041]Authore _Edvardo Tyson_, M.D. R.S.S.

[001-042]JOHN HOSKINS, _V.P.R.S_.

[001-043]The Pygmy described in this work was, as a matter of fact, a chimpanzee,

[001-044]and its skeleton is at this present moment in the Natural History Museum

[001-045]at South Kensington. Tyson's granddaughter married a Dr. Allardyce, who

[001-046]was a physician of good standing in Cheltenham. The "Pygmie" formed a

[001-047]somewhat remarkable item of her dowry. Her husband presented it to the

[001-048]Cheltenham Museum, where it was fortunately carefully preserved until,

[001-049]quite recently, it was transferred to its present position.

[001-050]At the conclusion of the purely scientific part of the work the author

[001-051]added four Philological Essays, as will have appeared from his title-page.

[001-052]The first of these is both the longest and the most interesting, and has

[001-053]alone been selected for republication in this volume.

[001-054]This is not the place to deal with the scientific merit of the main body

[001-055]of Tyson's work, but it may at least be said that it was the first attempt

[001-056]which had been made to deal with the anatomy of any of the anthropoid

[001-057]apes, and that its execution shows very conspicuous ability on the part of

[001-058]its author.

[001-059]Tyson, however, was not satisfied with the honour of being the author of

[001-060]an important morphological work; he desired to round off his subject by

[001-061]considering its bearing upon the, to him, wild and fabulous tales

[001-062]concerning pigmy races. The various allusions to these races met with in

[001-063]the pages of the older writers, and discussed in his, were to him what

[001-064]fairy tales are to us. Like modern folk-lorists, he wished to explain,

[001-065]even to euhemerise them, and bring them into line with the science of his

[001-066]day. Hence the "Philological Essay" with which this book is concerned.

[001-067]There are no pigmy races, he says; "the most diligent enquiries of late

[001-068]into all the parts of the inhabited world could never discover any such

[001-069]puny diminutive race of mankind." But there are tales about them, "fables

[001-070]and wonderful and merry relations, that are transmitted down to us

[001-071]concerning them," which surely require explanation. That explanation he

[001-072]found in his theory that all the accounts of pigmy tribes were based upon

[001-073]the mistakes of travellers who had taken apes for men. Nor was he without

[001-074]followers in his opinion; amongst whom here need only be mentioned Buffon,

[001-075]who in his _Histoire des Oiseaux_ explains the Homeric tale much as Tyson

[001-076]had done. The discoveries, however, of this century have, as all know,

[001-077]re-established in their essential details the accounts of the older

[001-078]writers, and in doing so have demolished the theories of Tyson and Buffon.

[001-079]We now know, not merely that there are pigmy races in existence, but that

[001-080]the area which they occupy is an extensive one, and in the remote past has

[001-081]without doubt been more extensive still. Moreover, certain of these races

[001-082]have been, at least tentatively, identified with the pigmy tribes of

[001-083]Pliny, [[herodotus|Herodotus]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], and other writers. It will be well, before

[001-084]considering this question, and before entering into any consideration of

[001-085]the legends and myths which may possibly be associated with dwarf races,

[001-086]to sketch briefly their distribution throughout the continents of the

[001-087]globe. It is necessary to keep clearly in view the upper limit which can

[001-088]justly be assigned to dwarfishness, and with this object it may be

[001-089]advisable to commence with a statement as to the average heights reached

[001-090]by various representative peoples. According to Topinard, the races of the

[001-091]world may be classified, in respect to their stature, in the following

[001-092]manner:--

[001-093]Tall 5 ft. 8 in. and upwards.

[001-094]Above the average 5 ft. 6 in. to 5 ft. 8 in.

[001-095]Below the average 5 ft. 4 in. to 5 ft. 6 in.

[001-096]Short Below 5 ft. 4 in.

[001-097]Thus amongst ordinary peoples there is no very striking difference of

[001-098]height, so far as the average is concerned. It would, however, be a great

[001-099]mistake to suppose that all races reaching a lower average height than

[001-100]five feet four inches are, in any accurate sense of the word, to be looked

[001-101]upon as pigmies. We have to descend to a considerably lower figure before

[001-102]that appellation can be correctly employed. The stature must fall

[001-103]considerably below five feet before we can speak of the race as one of

[001-104]dwarfs or pigmies. Anthropometrical authorities have not as yet agreed

[001-105]upon any upward limit for such a class, but for our present purposes it

[001-106]may be convenient to say that any race in which the average male stature

[001-107]does not exceed four feet nine inches--that is, the average height of a

[001-108]boy of about twelve years of age--may fairly be described as pigmy. It is

[001-109]most important to bear this matter of inches in mind in connection with

[001-110]points which will have to be considered in a later section.

[001-111]Pigmy races still exist in considerable numbers in [[asia|Asia]] and the adjacent

[001-112]islands, and as it was in that continent that, so far as our present

[001-113]knowledge goes, they had in former days their greatest extension, and, if

[001-114]De Quatrefages be correct, their place of origin, it will be well to deal

[001-115]first with the tribes of that quarter of the globe. "The Negrito" (_i.e._,

[001-116]pigmy black) "type," says the authority whom I have just quoted, and to

[001-117]whom I shall have to be still further indebted,[A] "was first placed in

[001-118]South Asia, which it without doubt occupied alone during an indeterminate

[001-119]period. It is thence that its diverse representatives have radiated, and,

[001-120]some going east, some west, have given rise to the black populations of

[001-121]Melanesia and Africa. In particular, [[india|India]] and Indo-China first belonged

[001-122]to the blacks. Invasions and infiltrations of different yellow or white

[001-123]races have split up these Negrito populations, which formerly occupied a

[001-124]continuous area, and mixing with them, have profoundly altered them. The

[001-125]present condition of things is the final result of strifes and mixtures,

[001-126]the most ancient of which may be referred back to prehistoric times." The

[001-127]invasions above mentioned having in the past driven many of the races from

[001-128]the mainland to the islands, and those which remained on the continent

[001-129]having undergone greater modification by crossing with taller and alien

[001-130]races, we may expect to find the purest Negritos amongst the tribes

[001-131]inhabiting the various archipelagoes situated south and east of the

[001-132]mainland. Amongst these, the Mincopies of the Andaman Islands offer a

[001-133]convenient starting-point. The knowledge which we possess of these little

[001-134]blacks is extensive, thanks to the labours in particular of Mr. Man[B] and

[001-135]Dr. Dobson,[C] which may be found in the Journal of the Anthropological

[001-136]Institute, and summarised in De Quatrefages' work. The average stature of

[001-137]the males of this race is four feet six inches, the height of a boy of ten

[001-138]years of age. Like children, the head is relatively large in comparison

[001-139]with the stature, since it is contained seven times therein, instead of

[001-140]seven and a half times, as is the rule amongst most average-sized peoples.

[001-141]Whilst speaking of the head, it may be well to mention that these

[001-142]Negritos, and in greater or less measure other Negritos and Negrillos

[001-143](_i.e._, pigmy blacks, Asiatic or African), differ in this part of the

[001-144]body in a most important respect from the ordinary African negro. Like

[001-145]him, they are black, often intensely so: like him, too, they have woolly

[001-146]hair arranged in tufts, but, unlike him, they have round (brachycephalic)

[001-147]heads instead of long (dolichocephalic); and the purer the race, the more

[001-148]marked is this distinction. The Mincopie has a singularly short life; for

[001-149]though he attains puberty at much the same age as ourselves, the

[001-150]twenty-second year brings him to middle life, and the fiftieth, if

[001-151]reached, is a period of extreme senility. Pure in race, ancient in

[001-152]history, and carefully studied, this race deserves some further attention

[001-153]here than can be extended to others with which I have to deal. The moral

[001-154]side of the Mincopies seems to be highly developed; the modesty of the

[001-155]young girls is most strict; monogamy is the rule, and--
