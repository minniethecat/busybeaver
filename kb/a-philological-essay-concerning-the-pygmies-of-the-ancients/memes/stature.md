# Stature and Anthropometry

## Overview

Stature — the measurement of human height — serves as a critical analytical tool in Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699). Tyson classified human varieties by stature, establishing a comparative framework that distinguished between normal human variation and the extraordinary statures attributed to [[pygmies|Pygmies]] and [[giants]].

Tyson recognized that peoples could be "classified, in respect to their stature, in the following" manner, anticipating the systematic anthropometry of the 19th century. (001-091)

## Tyson's Anthropometrical Framework

For Tyson, stature was not merely descriptive but diagnostic. He argued that certain stature ranges could classify peoples and, crucially, distinguish humans from apes.

"The stature must fall" within certain bounds for a being to be considered human. (001-102) This quantitative approach was remarkable for a work of classical philology. Rather than relying solely on textual authority, Tyson introduced measurable physical criteria.

Tyson drew on contemporary anthropometric data. He noted the "average male stature" of various populations, building a comparative database against which ancient descriptions could be evaluated. (001-106) He also referenced de Quatrefages' work on human variety, noting systematic variation in "the average stature of" different peoples across geographic regions. (001-136)

## The Pygmy Stature Question

The question of what constitutes "pygmy stature" is central to Tyson's argument. He defines pygmy stature as approximately five feet two inches — a height below the European average of his time but well within the normal human range. (002-024) This definition allows Tyson to distinguish between the merely short-statured (human pygmies) and the impossibly small (mythical pygmies).

Tyson also reports Wallace's anthropometric data from Southeast Asia, noting that "their average stature, according to Wallace, ranges" below European norms but far above what would be expected of the mythical pygmy. (002-029)

## Implications for the Pygmy Question

Tyson's focus on stature leads to a key insight: the ancient descriptions of pygmies as small and cave-dwelling could describe either a pygmy human population or apes. As he noted, a being might be "pygmy in stature, troglodytic, and still in the" primitive state — the ambiguity was precisely what Tyson exploited to argue that ancient accounts described chimpanzees. (002-040)

The stature framework demonstrates Tyson's methodological sophistication. He did not simply assert that ancients saw apes. He built a quantitative argument showing that the numbers, when examined closely, pointed away from humans.
