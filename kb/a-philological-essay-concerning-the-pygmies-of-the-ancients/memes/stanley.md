Sir Henry Morton Stanley (born John Rowlands, 1841–1904) was a Welsh-American explorer and journalist, best known for his expeditions in Central Africa. His exploration of the Congo region and his detailed descriptions of African pygmy populations provided nineteenth-century scholarship — and Edward Tyson in particular — with the first substantial modern eyewitness evidence for the existence of pygmy peoples in Africa.

## Stanley's African Discoveries

Stanley's expeditions into the Central African forests brought him into contact with pygmy populations inhabiting the region between the Ngaiyu and Ituri rivers. His account identified these people by several names: "the Wambutti, variously called Batwa, Akka, and Bazungu" (003-020). These forest-dwelling populations were described as nomadic hunters who "live in the uncleared virgin forest, and support themselves on game, which they are very expert in catching," using poisoned arrows to kill elephants and trapping other game (003-023).

Stanley documented their physical characteristics with unusual precision for the period. His measurements record a height range "from three feet to four feet six inches," with a full-grown adult weighing approximately ninety pounds (003-027). This data aligned remarkably well with classical descriptions of pygmy stature, lending empirical weight to ancient accounts.

## Two Tribes: Batwa and Wambutti

Stanley distinguished two separate pygmy groups within the region. Tyson's essay summarizes Stanley's findings: "The former have longish heads, long narrow faces, and small reddish eyes set close together, whilst the latter have round faces and open foreheads, gazelle-like eyes, set far apart, and rich yellow ivory complexion" (003-036). The physical differences between these groups were pronounced enough that Stanley "says they are as unlike as a Scandinavian is to a Turk" (003-021).

## The Pygmy Queen

One of Stanley's most vivid passages, quoted by Tyson at length, describes a pygmy queen brought before the explorer: "She was brought in to see me, with three rings of polished iron around her neck, the ends of which were coiled like a watch-spring. Three iron rings were suspended to each ear. She is of a light-brown complexion with broad round face, large eyes, and small but full lips" (003-041). Stanley estimated her height at about four feet four inches. His description of her "quiet modest demeanour" and "pleasing little creature" characterisation reflects both his ethnographic curiosity and the paternalistic attitudes of the period (003-042).

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Stanley's discoveries provided Tyson with what he regarded as conclusive modern evidence for the reality of the ancient pygmy accounts. Tyson notes that the Akkas described by Stanley could "from their situation, be fairly identified with the Akkas described by Stanley and others" in relation to Aristotle's pygmy-race (004-158). The essay further records that "Stanley believed that he had discovered the representatives of the Pigmies mentioned in this history" — a reference to the Herodotean account of the Nasamonian explorers' encounter with pygmies along the Niger (005-021). Stanley himself drew this connection explicitly: "Twenty-six centuries ago his ancestors captured the five young Nasamonian explorers, and made merry with them at their villages on the banks of the Niger" (005-024).

## Key Work Cited

- **In Darkest Africa** (1890) — Stanley's account of his Emin Pasha Relief Expedition, containing his most detailed observations of Central African pygmy populations. Tyson quotes extensively from Volume II of this work.
