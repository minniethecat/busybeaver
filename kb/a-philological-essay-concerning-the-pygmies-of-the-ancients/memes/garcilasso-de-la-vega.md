# Inca Garcilaso de la Vega

Inca Garcilaso de la Vega (1539–1616), born Gómez Suárez de Figueroa, was a Peruvian historian and writer of mixed Spanish and Inca descent. His *Royal Commentaries of Peru* (1609, 1617) stands as one of the foundational texts of Latin American literature and historiography. In Edward Tyson's *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*, Garcilaso serves as a source for ethnographic accounts of South American primates that bore upon the question of whether pygmies were human or simian.

## Garcilaso in Tyson's Essay

Tyson cites Garcilaso de la Vega in two distinct contexts within his essay, both relating to the behaviour and capacities of large primates.

### The Predatory Monkey of Peru

In his discussion of the physical strength and predatory behaviour of great apes, Tyson records Garcilaso's account of a monkey seizing a child:

> And Garcilasso de la Vega relates that they will seize and fly away with a Child ten or twelve years old. (017-028–017-029)

This testimony, drawn from the *Royal Commentaries of Peru* (017 Footnote B), forms part of Tyson's broader survey of ape behaviour across global sources. By juxtaposing Garcilaso's Peruvian account with the classical satire of [[juvenal|Juvenal]] on the pygmy-crane battle (017-029–017-034), Tyson implicitly contrasts empirical reportage with literary embellishment.

### The Tavern-Going Monkey of Cartagena

Garcilaso's most vivid contribution to Tyson's essay appears in a passage quoted from [[acosta|José de Acosta]], describing a monkey at the Governor's House in Cartagena:

> Acosta, as I find him quoted by Garcilasso de la Vega tells us of a Monkey he saw at the Governour's House at Cartagena, whom they sent often to the Tavern for Wine, with Money in one hand, and a Bottle in the other; and that when he came to the Tavern, he would not deliver his Money, until he had received his Wine. (018-136–018-140)

The monkey's remarkable behaviour — throwing stones at harassing boys, refusing to part with money before receiving wine, and resisting the temptation to drink without his master's permission (018-140–018-144) — illustrated for Tyson the near-human intelligence of certain primates. This supported his central argument: that the pygmies of ancient tradition were not men but apes, whose human-like behaviours had deceived credulous observers.

## The Question of Simian Speech

Tyson further cites Garcilaso's report that "the Natives think that they can speak, but will not, for fear of being made to work" (018-146–018-147). This native belief, paralleled by [[bontius|Jacobus Bontius]]'s report of similar Javanese opinions concerning the [[orang-outang|Orang-Outang]] (018-147–018-149), reinforced Tyson's thesis that the boundary between human and simian was far more ambiguous than commonly supposed — and that this ambiguity explained the ancient belief in diminutive human races.

## Legacy

Garcilaso de la Vega's *Royal Commentaries* represents the first major literary work by an author born in the Americas to achieve European recognition. His inclusion in Tyson's essay illustrates the global reach of early modern ethnographic writing and its role in shaping European debates about human diversity.
