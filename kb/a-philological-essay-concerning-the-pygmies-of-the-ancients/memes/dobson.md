# Dr. Dobson

Dr. Dobson was a late nineteenth-century British anthropologist whose anthropometric research on the stature of the Andaman Islanders contributed to the scientific literature on diminutive human populations. His measurements, published in the *Journal of the Anthropological Institute*, were cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in the prefatory matter of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* as part of the modern anthropometric evidence establishing the existence of pygmy-sized human groups.

## Andaman Islander Stature

In the Introduction to his essay, Tyson surveys the modern scientific evidence for the existence of diminutive races. He references Dobson's measurements of the Andaman Islanders, a Negrito population of the Bay of Bengal whose notably short stature had attracted the attention of British anthropologists:

> Dr. Dobson, which may be found in the Journal of the Anthropological Institute, and summarised in De Quatrefages' work. The average stature of the males of this race is four feet six inches, the height of a boy of ten years of age. (001-135)

The Andaman Islanders' mean male stature of four feet six inches — equivalent to a ten-year-old European child — represented one of the most striking modern data points in Tyson's catalogue of diminutive populations. Dobson's measurements provided empirical, institutionally-published evidence that adult human populations could indeed fall within the size range traditionally ascribed to the pygmies of ancient legend.

## Significance in Tyson's Preface

Tyson places Dobson's Andaman data prominently in his introductory survey of the evidence, alongside the work of [[de-quatrefages|Jean Louis Armand de Quatrefages]] who had summarized and contextualized these findings. The Andaman Islanders, along with the [[akka|Akka]] of Central Africa and the Negritos of the Philippines, formed the modern anthropometric baseline against which Tyson measured the classical and medieval testimonies that occupy the bulk of his essay. Dobson's precise, peer-reviewed stature figures lent scientific credibility to the entire enterprise.
